B-17 (Amygdalin/Laetrile) and Cancer Treatment

A Comprehensive Scientific Dashboard Examining All Evidence

Overview & Background

B-17 (Amygdalin/Laetrile) is a cyanogenic glycoside naturally occurring in apricot kernels and other Prunus species. It has been promoted as an alternative cancer treatment since the 1950s, though it was never classified as a vitamin.

Key Scientific Concepts

  • Cyanogenic Glycoside: A compound that releases hydrogen cyanide when metabolized
  • Laetrile: Semi-synthetic form of amygdalin developed for medical use
  • Vitamin B-17 Myth: Amygdalin is NOT a vitamin (vitamins cannot be synthesized by the body); this designation was marketing terminology

Scientific Studies & Research Findings

Study 1: Amygdalin Induces Apoptosis in Cancer Cells (2018) In Vitro Review

PMID:29308747
Journal:Anticancer Agents Med Chem. 2018;18(12):1650-1655
DOI:10.2174/1871520618666180105161136

Methodology: Comprehensive review of published literature on amygdalin's anti-cancer mechanisms from PubMed, ScienceDirect, Google Scholar, Scopus, and Medline databases.

Key Findings:

  • Amygdalin activates apoptosis (programmed cell death) in cancer cells
  • Upregulates Bax protein (pro-apoptotic)
  • Increases caspase-3 expression
  • Downregulates Bcl-2 protein (anti-apoptotic)
  • Promotes cell cycle arrest in G0/G1 phase
  • Decreases cells entering S and G2/M phases

Limitations:

"Well-planned clinical trials are still needed to be conducted to prove effectiveness of this substance in vivo and to get approval for human use."

Study 2: Molecular Mechanisms of Amygdalin Action (2018) In Vitro Review

PMID:29486614
Journal:Immunopharmacol Immunotoxicol. 2018 Jun;40(3):212-218
DOI:10.1080/08923973.2018.1441301

Methodology: Review of latest research on amygdalin's molecular mechanisms in vitro.

Key Findings:

  • Induces apoptosis via Bax and caspase-3 upregulation
  • Reduces anti-apoptotic Bcl-2 protein expression
  • Inhibits adhesion of breast, lung, and bladder cancer cells through decreased integrin expression
  • Blocks Akt-mTOR pathway (may inhibit metastases)
  • In renal cancer cells: increases p19 protein expression, inhibiting G1 to S-phase transition
  • Anti-inflammatory effects via NF-kβ and NLRP3 pathway inhibition

Critical Safety Warning:

"Current studies have shown that HCN [hydrogen cyanide] is also released in normal cells, therefore it may not be safe for human organism."

Study 3: Amygdalin with Zinc on Hepatocellular Carcinoma (2020) In Vitro

PMID:31958042
Journal:Anticancer Agents Med Chem. 2020;20(4):486-494
DOI:10.2174/18715206206662001200955525

Methodology: In vitro study on HepG2 hepatocellular carcinoma cell line using MTT assay, flow cytometry, and Annexin V-FITC/PI staining.

Key Findings:

  • Amygdalin without zinc showed strong anti-HepG2 activity
  • Amygdalin + 20μmol zinc and amygdalin + 800μmol zinc showed highly significant apoptotic effects compared to amygdalin alone
  • Induced cell cycle arrest at G2/M phase
  • Increased p53, Bax, cytochrome c, and caspase-3 levels significantly
  • Decreased anti-apoptotic Bcl2 levels

Conclusion:

"Amygdalin is a natural anti-cancer agent, which can be used for the treatment of hepatocellular carcinoma. The potency increased significantly by addition of zinc."

Study 4: Hepatic and Renal Toxicity Evaluation (2021) Animal Study

PMID:34799332
Journal:Pak J Pharm Sci. 2021 Jul;34(4(Suppl)):1555-1560

Methodology: In vivo study on albino Wistar rats (n=6 per group) administered aqueous extract of Prunus armeniaca seeds at doses of 1000, 1500, and 2000 mg/kg body weight for 42 days.

Key Findings:

  • No significant increment in liver enzymes (AST, ALT, ALP, bilirubin)
  • Reduced MDA levels in liver and kidney
  • Dose-dependent increased antioxidant activity
  • Improved antioxidant markers: catalase, superoxide dismutase, glutathione reductase

Authors' Conclusion:

"The extract did not show any remarkable hepatotoxicity or renal toxicity and improved antioxidant activity. Histology supported that selected doses are safe for consumption."

Critical Counterarguments & Safety Concerns

🔴 Severe Cyanide Toxicity Cases

Case Report 1 (2005): Severe cyanide poisoning in adult woman

PMID:16175068

Details: 32-year-old woman with breast cancer and hepatic metastases used 'vitamin B-17' (amygdalin) purchased online. Presented unresponsive, shocked, fixed dilated pupils, hypothermic.

Clinical Course: Required mechanical ventilation, increasing inotropes, developed diabetes insipidus over 6 hours. Serum thiocyanate level markedly elevated.

Outcome:

"Full recovery over 8 h with supportive therapy alone. Internet search identified this as a debunked cancer remedy and cyanogen."


Case Report 2 (2015): Severe cyanide poisoning in child

PMID:25605411

Details: 4-year-old boy with metastatic ependymoma received CAM including intravenous and oral "vitamin B-17" (amygdalin) and oral apricot kernels.

Clinical Presentation: Severely agitated, encephalopathic, unresponsive (Glasgow Coma Scale of 3). Severe metabolic/lactic acidosis on initial blood gas analysis.

Outcome:

"After administering sodium thiosulfate, rapid improvement with complete recovery without need for further intensive care treatment. Serum cyanide level markedly elevated."

🔬 Scientific Criticisms

  • Theoretical Flaw: Amygdalin was claimed to be hydrolyzed ONLY in cancer cells releasing cyanide, killing cancer selectively. However, studies show HCN is released in normal cells as well, negating the proposed selective toxicity mechanism.
  • No Human Clinical Evidence: Despite decades of use and promotion, no rigorous clinical trials have demonstrated efficacy in humans. The conclusion from multiple reviews: "well-planned clinical trials are still needed."
  • Cyanide Release Mechanism: Amygdalin metabolism releases hydrogen cyanide (HCN), a potent cellular toxin that inhibits cytochrome c oxidase, disrupting ATP production and potentially causing death of healthy cells.
  • Historical Clinical Trials (1980s): Multiple clinical trials conducted by the U.S. National Cancer Institute found:
    • No anti-tumor effects in cancer patients
    • Significant risk of cyanide poisoning, particularly when combined with vitamin C or when taken orally (gut bacteria can release cyanide)

🚫 The "Wellness" Sphere Arguments

Claims Made by Wellness Advocates:

  • Amygdalin is a "vitamin B-17" essential for health (scientifically false - it's not a vitamin)
  • Cancer cells contain higher levels of enzyme beta-glucosidase that activates amygdalin to release cyanide specifically in cancer tissue
  • Conventional medicine suppresses amygdalin due to pharmaceutical interests
  • Natural forms (apricot kernels) are safer than synthetic laetrile

Scientific Counter-Arguments:

  • "Vitamin B-17" designation is not recognized by any scientific or nutritional authority. Amygdalin cannot be classified as a vitamin.
  • The theory that cancer cells selectively activate amygdalin has been disproven - cyanide release occurs in all cells, creating systemic toxicity risk.
  • Natural apricot kernels contain highly variable amounts of amygdalin (can range from 3% to 15%), making dosing unpredictable and dangerous.
  • Dog and cat studies also showed no therapeutic benefit, suggesting the mechanism doesn't work across species.

📊 Scientific Consensus & Conclusion

In Vitro Evidence: Amygdalin demonstrates apoptotic effects on various cancer cell lines through multiple mechanisms including Bax/caspase-3 upregulation, Bcl-2 downregulation, and cell cycle arrest.

Clinical Evidence in Humans: Zero rigorous clinical trials have demonstrated efficacy. Multiple historical clinical trials found no anti-tumor effects.

Safety Concerns: Documented cases of severe cyanide poisoning, including one adult and one child requiring intensive care. The theoretical advantage of selective cancer cell toxicity has been disproven - cyanide release occurs in normal cells as well.

Current Scientific Position: Amygdalin remains an experimental compound with promising in vitro mechanisms but no proven clinical efficacy and significant safety risks. Further rigorous clinical trials would be required to establish any therapeutic role, though the cyanide toxicity risk presents a major barrier.

Clinical Recommendation: Patients should NOT use amygdalin as a cancer treatment outside of controlled clinical trial settings due to proven risks and unproven benefits. Conventional evidence-based treatments remain the standard of care.
