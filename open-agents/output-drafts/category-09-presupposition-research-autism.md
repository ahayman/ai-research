# Category 09: Presupposition -- Research on Autism and Presupposition Processing

## Research Overview

This document synthesizes research on how autistic individuals process, produce, and interact with linguistic presuppositions. Presuppositions are implicit assumptions embedded in language that speakers take for granted as shared knowledge. They sit at the intersection of linguistic semantics, pragmatics, and Theory of Mind -- making them a particularly rich and complex domain for understanding autistic communication patterns.

The research reveals a nuanced picture: autistic individuals may process certain presupposition triggers with difficulty (particularly those requiring Theory of Mind), while simultaneously possessing cognitive advantages that make them *better* at detecting hidden or manipulative presuppositions. This dual profile -- vulnerability to missing presuppositions combined with strength in questioning them -- is central to understanding presupposition in the context of autism.

---

## 1. Research on Autistic Processing of Presuppositions

### 1.1 Direct Studies of Presupposition Comprehension in Autism

The most systematic research on presupposition comprehension in autism comes from Cheung and colleagues, who conducted multiple studies with Cantonese-speaking children.

**Cheung et al. (2017)** investigated four types of presupposition -- existential, factive, lexical, and structural -- in school-age children with and without ASD (n=21 ASD, n=106 typically developing, mean age 8.8). Children with ASD showed a deficit in presupposition comprehension even after controlling for general language ability and non-verbal intelligence. This was one of the first studies to systematically test multiple presupposition types in an autistic population.

**Cheung et al. (2020)** expanded the investigation to seven presupposition trigger types: definite descriptions, factive predicates, change-of-state verbs, implicative verbs, iteratives, temporal clauses, and counterfactual conditionals. Twenty-seven children with ASD (mean age 9.07) were compared with age-matched and language-matched typically developing peers. The key finding: children with ASD performed significantly worse than age-matched TD peers, but *similarly* to language-matched TD peers -- except for temporal clauses, where ASD children showed unique difficulty even after controlling for age, language, and non-verbal intelligence. This suggests that much of the presupposition difficulty in autism tracks with overall language development rather than being a specific pragmatic deficit, with temporal presuppositions as a notable exception.

**Chanchaochai (2021, Experiments in Linguistic Meaning)** studied Thai-speaking children with ASD (n=32), TD children (n=70), and adults (n=40), testing scalar implicatures, lexical presuppositions, and implicated presuppositions using the negated universal quantifier "not every." Children with ASD were equally likely as TD children to derive lexical presuppositions, though they showed more literal response tendencies overall. No additive effects of implicated presuppositions were found in any group.

**Chen et al. (2020, Frontiers in Psychology)** investigated comprehension of the presupposition trigger "ye" (also) by Mandarin-speaking preschoolers with and without ASD, further contributing to cross-linguistic evidence that presupposition processing in autism varies by trigger type and language context.

### 1.2 Factive Verbs and Cognitive Verb Comprehension

Factive verbs (e.g., "know," "realize," "regret") trigger presuppositions about the truth of their complement clauses. Understanding factivity requires grasping that "She knows he left" presupposes "he left" while "She thinks he left" does not.

A 2025 study on the comprehension of cognitive verbs among autistic children (published in the Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders) tested 59 autistic and 62 TD children aged 3-10 on two tasks: understanding relative certainty (that "know" conveys greater certainty than "think" or "guess") and understanding factivity (that the complement of "know" is presupposed true). **Autistic children had significantly more difficulty than TD peers on both tasks, and Theory of Mind was the exclusive predictor of factivity scores for the autistic group.** This directly links presupposition processing to ToM abilities.

Related work by Cheung et al. found that for Mandarin-speaking autistic children aged 4-7, weaker ToM correlated with poorer understanding of verb factuality, and language development facilitated ToM improvement. This bidirectional relationship between ToM and factive presupposition comprehension is important: improving one may improve the other.

### 1.3 The Presupposition-Theory of Mind Connection

Presuppositions are fundamentally about what speakers assume their listeners already know or accept. This makes them inherently dependent on modeling other minds -- a speaker who says "Leo is eating *again*" presupposes that the listener knows Leo ate before, and expects the listener to take this as given background.

The connection is well-established in the theoretical literature. Psycholinguists have argued that no account can be given of key pragmatic notions like presuppositions without the involvement of Theory of Mind. Baron-Cohen's early work on ToM in autism (1985) laid groundwork that later researchers connected to presupposition processing.

However, the ToM account of presupposition difficulties in autism has become more nuanced:

- **Empirical challenges to the ToM deficit view:** A 2019 paper in Psychological Bulletin ("Empirical Failures of the Claim That Autistic People Lack a Theory of Mind," PMC6959478) marshaled evidence that multiple ToM tasks fail to relate to each other and fail to account for autistic traits, social interaction, and empathy. The simple "autistic people lack ToM" narrative is increasingly questioned.

- **The double empathy problem (Milton, 2012):** Communication breakdowns between autistic and non-autistic people may reflect mutual misunderstanding rather than a one-sided deficit. When presuppositions fail between autistic and non-autistic speakers, both parties may be misjudging shared knowledge.

- **Kissine's ego-centric anchoring proposal:** Kissine (2016) argued that "ego-centrically anchored pragmatic phenomena" -- including presupposition processing -- do not necessarily cause difficulties for autistic people, because processing presuppositions can often be done without modeling the other person's mental state. This proposal has been supported by some findings (e.g., preserved scalar implicature and lexical presupposition) while being challenged by others (e.g., difficulties with ad-hoc implicatures requiring social reasoning).

### 1.4 The Linguistic vs. Social Pragmatics Distinction

A crucial research distinction has emerged between **linguistic pragmatics** (inferences based on linguistic form and convention) and **social pragmatics** (inferences requiring perspective-taking and contextual reasoning):

- Autistic individuals often show **relative strength** in linguistic pragmatic reasoning (e.g., deriving scalar implicatures from "some" meaning "not all," processing lexical presupposition triggers)
- Autistic individuals often show **greater difficulty** with social pragmatic inference (e.g., understanding what a specific speaker would presuppose given their particular knowledge state)

**Petit, Geoffray, Cassar & Baltazar (2025)**, in "Some (but not all) Pragmatic Inferences are Difficult for Autistic Children" (Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders), compared 23 autistic children with 237 neurotypical children. Autistic children showed poorer scalar implicature but similar metaphor comprehension -- the reverse of what many theories predicted. The authors proposed that a dimension opposing "guided" versus "spontaneous" pragmatic processes may explain when autistic individuals struggle.

This distinction matters enormously for presupposition: **presuppositions triggered by lexical items (stop, again, too, know) may be processed relatively well, while presuppositions requiring assessment of shared knowledge (what the speaker assumes the listener knows) may be more challenging.**

## 2. Common Ground and Presupposition

### 2.1 Presuppositions as the Assumed Common Ground

In Stalnaker's influential pragmatic account, presuppositions are propositions that are "on record as belonging to the common ground between speakers." Common ground is the set of propositions that conversational participants mutually accept as true, known, or taken for granted. Every presupposition is, in effect, an assumption about what the common ground contains.

This means that presupposition use and comprehension require a working model of shared knowledge -- knowing what your listener knows, what they don't know, what you can take for granted, and what needs to be explicitly stated.

### 2.2 Research on Common Ground in Autism

**Karin et al. (2025, "Paths to Common Ground in ASD," Autism Research)** conducted one of the most comprehensive studies to date, examining common ground generation in ASD (N=84) versus control (N=56) children and adolescents aged 6-16. Both groups generated common ground spontaneously, but the control group achieved common conceptualization faster and with fewer words. Path analysis revealed independent contributions from both Vocabulary and Theory of Mind to common ground efficiency.

**Hyter et al. (2017, "The Art of Common Ground," Journal of Child Language)** found that typically developing participants produced shorter narrations when they shared knowledge with an interlocutor -- an effect not observed at the group level in ASD. However, older and less severely affected participants with ASD did shorten their narratives, suggesting the ability develops with age.

**De Marchena & Eigsti (2018)** found that 5-to-7-year-olds with ASD were able to consider what they had previously shared with a conversation partner, suggesting basic common ground tracking is present but may develop on a delayed trajectory.

### 2.3 Over-Presupposing and Under-Presupposing

When speakers calibrate presuppositions incorrectly, two patterns emerge:

**Under-presupposing (providing too much background):** This manifests as over-informativeness -- explaining things the listener already knows, providing excessive context, restating shared information. Research on referential communication in autism has found that autistic speakers sometimes provide more information than necessary, not shortening descriptions when the referent is already established in common ground.

**Over-presupposing (assuming the listener knows things they don't):** This manifests as under-informativeness -- jumping into topics without sufficient background, assuming shared knowledge that isn't shared, using references the listener can't resolve. This can happen when an autistic speaker is deeply immersed in a topic and doesn't calibrate how much the listener knows.

**Geelhand, Papastamou, Jaspard & Kissine (2025, Autism)** found that autistic adults displayed different verbal behavior only in mixed-neurotype interactions (autistic-to-neurotypical) rather than in same-neurotype interactions. This supports the double empathy framing: presupposition calibration breaks down specifically when communicative styles differ, not as an inherent deficit.

Research on reference production (Arnold et al., 2013, PMC3668432) found that all participants, including those with ASD, showed "fine-grained sensitivity to the discourse context" in their referential choices, modulated by cognitive effort. This suggests the underlying sensitivity to discourse context (and thus to what can be presupposed) is present, even when its deployment is inconsistent.

### 2.4 Working Memory and Common Ground

Discourse comprehension research has found that working memory capacity constrains common ground representation and impacts pragmatic skills in ASD. Tracking what has been presupposed, what has been explicitly stated, and what remains unshared requires maintaining a dynamic model of the conversation -- a task that is computationally expensive. For autistic individuals who may be simultaneously processing sensory input, managing social performance, and tracking linguistic content, the working memory demands of presupposition management can be overwhelming.

## 3. The Autistic Advantage: Detecting Hidden Presuppositions

### 3.1 Enhanced Rationality and Resistance to Cognitive Biases

**Rozenkrantz, D'Mello & Gabrieli (2021, Trends in Cognitive Sciences)** published a landmark review titled "Enhanced Rationality in Autism Spectrum Disorder" documenting that autistic individuals display more objective judgments and less biased decision-making across multiple domains:

- **Reduced framing effect:** Autistic individuals tend to remain objective regardless of how information is presented, making them less susceptible to the way choices are framed.
- **Resistance to the sunk cost fallacy:** Autistic individuals are less likely to continue investing in a losing proposition based on past investment.
- **Reduced conjunction fallacy:** Autistic individuals are more likely to recognize that a specific scenario cannot be more probable than a general one that includes it.
- **Reduced decoy effect:** In consumer choice experiments, neurotypical individuals are typically distracted by decoy products that shift preferences; autistic individuals are less susceptible to this manipulation.
- **Lower susceptibility to the attraction effect:** Autistic individuals make more rational and less intuitive choice selections when emotions and reward are not heavily involved.

Rozenkrantz's explanations for this enhanced rationality include: (1) reduced interference of emotions and rewards on reasoning (reduced amygdala activation), (2) reduced influence of context on judgment, and (3) enhanced attention to detail.

### 3.2 Literal-Mindedness as a Presupposition Detection Tool

The autistic tendency toward literal interpretation -- often framed as a deficit -- can function as a *strength* when it comes to detecting hidden presuppositions:

- **Automatic acceptance vs. questioning:** Neurotypical listeners typically *accommodate* presuppositions automatically -- when someone says "Have you stopped procrastinating?" a neurotypical listener is likely to simply accept the presupposition (that they were procrastinating) and process the question. An autistic listener may be more likely to pause at the presupposition itself: "Wait -- I wasn't procrastinating."

- **Pattern recognition applied to language structure:** The autistic cognitive style characterized by "hyper-systemizing" (Baron-Cohen) may lead to noticing structural features of language that carry presuppositions, rather than just flowing past them.

- **Vincente (2023, Mind & Language)** discussed accounting for the preference for literal meanings in autism, noting that the tendency to process language at face value means implicit content -- including presuppositions -- is not automatically absorbed.

- **Sperber & Wilson's predictive processing account** (discussed in Springer, 2023): Literalism in autism may be understood through a predictive processing framework where the autistic brain generates fewer top-down predictions about likely intended meanings, leading to more bottom-up processing of actual linguistic content -- including its presuppositional structure.

### 3.3 Resistance to Manipulation via Presupposition

The implications for manipulation detection are significant:

- **Advertising resistance:** Research from the British Psychological Society reports that autistic people may be less susceptible to marketing tricks. A Psychology Today article documented that advertising "falls flat" for many autistic individuals because ads rely heavily on emotional appeals and social scenarios that don't resonate with detail-focused, fact-oriented processing. When advertising uses presupposition-heavy language ("Discover why millions trust Brand X" presupposes that millions trust it), autistic consumers may be more likely to question rather than accept the embedded claim.

- **Political rhetoric:** The reduced susceptibility to framing effects has implications for how autistic individuals process politically loaded language, which frequently relies on embedded presuppositions (e.g., "How do we fix our broken immigration system?" presupposes the system is broken).

- **Social conformity resistance:** Research by Yafai, Verrier & Reidy (2014, Autism) found that children with autism were much less likely to conform in misleading conditions than TD children. A later study (2019, Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, PMC6394587) provided further nuance, but the general finding of reduced social conformity holds across multiple studies.

### 3.4 Important Caveats and the Vulnerability Paradox

While autistic individuals may be better at detecting *structural* manipulation via presupposition, they face a paradox regarding *social* manipulation:

- **Honest communication norms:** Autistic communication tends to be direct and honest. As the Organization for Autism Research noted, autistic people "are honest, up front and do not often do things like manipulation and deceit," which can make it harder to recognize when others are being manipulative -- not because of cognitive inability, but because of different baseline assumptions about communicative intent.

- **Processing delays:** By the time an autistic person recognizes a manipulative presupposition in real-time conversation, the conversational moment may have passed. The advantage in detecting presuppositions may be more accessible in written text or with time to reflect.

- **Support needs variation:** A 2025 ACM study on chatbot persuasion found that autistic young adults with substantial support needs were *more* susceptible to persuasion than the general population, while those without substantial support needs showed typical or enhanced resistance. This highlights that the "autistic advantage" in detecting manipulation is not universal across the spectrum.

- **Gaslighting vulnerability:** Despite cognitive advantages in detecting logical manipulation, autistic people are particularly vulnerable to gaslighting -- a form of manipulation that targets self-trust and reality-testing. When an autistic person *does* detect that something is wrong (including a false presupposition), they may be talked out of their correct assessment by someone who exploits social authority or the autistic person's self-doubt about social perception.

## 4. Loaded Questions and Autism

### 4.1 The Mechanics of Loaded Questions

A loaded question embeds a controversial or unwarranted presupposition, rendering direct answers implicitly concessive to an unproven claim. The classic example -- "Have you stopped beating your wife?" -- presupposes that the person was beating their wife. Whether answered "yes" or "no," the answerer implicitly accepts the presupposition.

Loaded questions work through presupposition triggers:
- **Change-of-state verbs:** "When did you *stop* caring?" presupposes you once cared and then stopped.
- **Factive verbs:** "Do you *realize* how wrong you are?" presupposes that you are wrong.
- **Temporal clauses:** "*Since* you agree, can we move on?" presupposes that you agree.
- **Definite descriptions:** "What's your excuse *this time*?" presupposes you are making an excuse and have done so before.

### 4.2 Autistic Processing of Loaded Questions

No studies have directly tested autistic responses to loaded questions. However, research convergences suggest a complex picture:

**Potential advantages:**
- The tendency toward literal processing means an autistic person may be more likely to notice the embedded presupposition rather than automatically accepting it.
- Enhanced analytical thinking may help identify the logical structure of the trap.
- Research on the Cognitive Reflection Test shows autistic individuals produce more deliberative responses, suggesting they may be more likely to stop and analyze a question before responding.

**Potential challenges:**
- The social pressure to provide a direct answer may conflict with the need to challenge the presupposition.
- Real-time processing demands may prevent the recognition from occurring quickly enough.
- Challenging a presupposition requires sophisticated social navigation -- knowing when and how to push back without appearing confrontational.

### 4.3 The Response Dilemma

For everyone, but particularly for autistic individuals, loaded questions create a difficult pragmatic situation. The question "Why are you always late?" contains the presupposition that you are always late. The challenge is:

1. **Answering the question directly** (giving a reason) accepts the presupposition
2. **Saying "I'm not always late"** challenges the presupposition but may seem evasive
3. **Explaining the presupposition structure** ("That question assumes I'm always late, which isn't accurate") requires metalinguistic awareness and social courage

For autistic individuals, option 3 may be the most natural (given enhanced analytical tendencies) but the most socially costly (perceived as pedantic or oppositional). This creates a real-world dilemma that practical strategies need to address.

## 5. Presupposition in Social Communication

### 5.1 Shared Presuppositions and In-Group Effects

Conversational presuppositions create powerful in-group/out-group dynamics. When a group shares presuppositions (background knowledge, cultural references, inside jokes, shared history), communication becomes efficient and bonding. When someone doesn't share those presuppositions, they experience exclusion -- not through overt rejection but through the invisible barrier of assumed knowledge.

Examples of presupposition-based exclusion:
- "Obviously, you know how this works" (presupposes shared procedural knowledge)
- "As we discussed last time..." (presupposes shared conversational history the person may not have)
- "Well, you know how she is" (presupposes shared social knowledge about a third party)
- Cultural references that presuppose shared media consumption, shared class background, shared generational experience

### 5.2 Missing Shared Presuppositions as Social Exclusion

For autistic individuals, missing shared presuppositions can be a significant source of social difficulty:

- **Cultural common ground gaps:** Autistic individuals may not share the same cultural consumption patterns (popular TV shows, social media trends, celebrity gossip) that form much of neurotypical small talk's presuppositional base.
- **Social rule presuppositions:** Neurotypical social interaction presupposes knowledge of "unwritten rules." As research documents, for autistic individuals, implicit expectations "can be invisible unless stated directly." The presupposition that "everyone knows you should offer guests a drink" is only visible when you're the person who doesn't share that knowledge.
- **Experiential presuppositions:** Much social bonding relies on presuppositions about shared experiences (dating, parties, workplace politics). Autistic individuals whose life experiences differ may lack the presuppositional base for these conversations.

Research on social exclusion shows stark numbers: 60% of autistic students report being bullied compared to 18% of the general population. While presupposition-based exclusion is subtler than overt bullying, it contributes to the sense of being on the outside of social groups.

### 5.3 The Knowledge vs. Performance Distinction

A critical review (PMC10613329) challenged the assumption that autistic social challenges stem from lacking social knowledge. Evidence suggests autistic people often *have* the relevant social knowledge (and thus the presuppositional base) but struggle with real-time social *performance* -- deploying that knowledge fluidly in dynamic interaction. This has important implications:

- An autistic person may *know* the social rules being presupposed but struggle to access them in real time
- They may understand a cultural reference but not signal that understanding in the expected way
- They may share the common ground but fail to demonstrate that sharing through expected social cues

This means the problem isn't always one of missing presuppositions but of failed *signaling* of shared presuppositions.

### 5.4 The Double Empathy Perspective

Milton's double empathy problem (2012) reframes presupposition failures as bidirectional. When an autistic person and a non-autistic person interact:

- The non-autistic speaker may presuppose shared social norms the autistic person doesn't share
- The autistic speaker may presuppose shared factual knowledge or logical reasoning the non-autistic person doesn't share
- Both may presuppose that the other's communication style matches their own

Research shows autistic people often establish better rapport and connection with other autistic people -- in part because shared presuppositions align more naturally within neurotype. The presupposition failures are specifically concentrated in cross-neurotype interactions.

## 6. Accommodation and Autism

### 6.1 Lewis's Concept of Presupposition Accommodation

David Lewis (1979) introduced the concept of *accommodation* in pragmatics: when a speaker makes an utterance whose presupposition is not part of the common ground, the listener typically adjusts their model of the common ground to include the presupposed content, as long as doing so doesn't create contradictions with established beliefs.

For example, if someone says "My sister called me yesterday" and the listener didn't know the speaker had a sister, accommodation is the process of simply adding "the speaker has a sister" to the common ground without questioning it. This happens automatically, seamlessly, and without conscious effort in most neurotypical communication.

Stalnaker's pragmatic account emphasizes that presuppositions are maintained in the "context set" -- the set of possible worlds compatible with what is mutually assumed. Accommodation updates this context set.

### 6.2 Autistic Accommodation Patterns

While no studies have directly investigated presupposition accommodation in autism, converging evidence suggests autistic listeners may accommodate differently:

**Reduced automatic accommodation:**
- The literal processing tendency suggests autistic listeners may be less likely to automatically add unstated information to their model of shared knowledge.
- Where a neurotypical listener hears "John stopped smoking" and automatically accommodates "John used to smoke," an autistic listener may process the explicit assertion ("John stopped smoking") without automatically updating the background model.
- The enhanced analytical thinking documented by Rozenkrantz et al. suggests presupposed content may be treated as a *claim to be evaluated* rather than background to be accepted.

**More deliberate/questioning accommodation:**
- The Cognitive Reflection Test research showing more deliberative responses in autism suggests that when accommodation does occur, it may be more effortful and conscious.
- This creates both an advantage (catching false presuppositions) and a cost (slower, more effortful processing of routine communication).

**Dichotomized responses:**
- Research on scalar implicatures (Frontiers in Psychology, 2018, PMC6064869) found that autistic children showed dichotomized attitudes -- tending to either fully agree or fully disagree with underinformative statements, rather than using a middle ground. This pattern may extend to accommodation: either fully accepting a presupposition or fully rejecting it, with less gradient processing.

### 6.3 When Questioning Is Appropriate vs. When Accommodation Is Appropriate

This is a critical practical distinction:

**Accommodation is usually appropriate when:**
- The presupposed information is plausible and non-controversial ("My dog loves the park" -- accommodate that the speaker has a dog)
- The conversation would be disrupted by questioning ("Since we're all tired, let's wrap up" -- even if you're not tired, accommodation keeps things moving)
- The social cost of questioning exceeds the cost of accepting the presupposition

**Questioning is appropriate when:**
- The presupposition contains a factual error ("Since you agree with our proposal..." -- if you don't agree)
- The presupposition is being used manipulatively ("When did you start cheating on your diet?" -- if you haven't been cheating)
- The presupposition would commit you to something you don't accept ("Given that we all want to proceed with this plan..." -- if you don't want to proceed)
- The presupposition significantly misrepresents reality

For autistic individuals, the challenge is calibrating *when* to accommodate and *when* to question -- and the answer requires social context assessment that can be difficult in real time.

## 7. Practical Strategies

### 7.1 Detecting Presuppositions in Real-Time Conversation

Based on the research, presuppositions can be identified by attending to specific linguistic triggers:

**High-frequency triggers to watch for:**
- **Change-of-state verbs:** stop, start, begin, continue, resume, finish ("You've stopped trying" presupposes you were trying)
- **Factive verbs:** know, realize, discover, notice, regret, remember ("You realize this is wrong" presupposes it is wrong)
- **Iteratives:** again, another, also, too, back ("Here we go again" presupposes it happened before)
- **Temporal clauses:** before, after, since, while, when ("Before you object..." presupposes you will object)
- **Definite descriptions:** the, this, that + noun ("The problem with your approach..." presupposes there is a problem)
- **Cleft constructions:** "It was YOU who..." (presupposes someone did it)
- **Counterfactuals:** "If you had listened..." (presupposes you didn't listen)

**A practical detection heuristic:** If you notice yourself about to answer a question and feeling uncomfortable, pause and ask: "What does this question *assume* to be true?" The discomfort may be a signal that you've detected a presupposition you don't accept.

### 7.2 When and How to Challenge Hidden Presuppositions

**Graduated response strategies:**

1. **Silent non-accommodation:** Simply don't accept the presupposition internally. Useful when the stakes are low and challenging would be socially costly.

2. **Gentle redirect:** "I'm not sure I agree with the premise -- can we back up?" This challenges without naming the specific presupposition.

3. **Explicit identification:** "That question assumes X, and I'm not sure X is true." This is direct and clear but may be perceived as confrontational in some contexts.

4. **Question-based challenge:** "What makes you think X?" This turns the presupposition back into a claim that needs support.

5. **Reframing:** Instead of answering a loaded question, answer the question you wish had been asked. "Have you stopped procrastinating?" -> "I've been managing my time differently this week."

### 7.3 Using Presupposition Awareness as a Critical Thinking Tool

The research on enhanced rationality in autism suggests that presupposition detection can be developed into a powerful critical thinking skill:

- **In media literacy:** News headlines and political discourse are heavily presupposition-laden. "How to fix our broken education system" presupposes it's broken. "The threat of immigration" presupposes immigration is a threat. Detecting these presuppositions enables more critical engagement with public discourse.

- **In advertising:** "Discover why millions trust Brand X" presupposes that millions trust it. "Get the results you deserve" presupposes you deserve specific results. Recognizing these embedded claims reduces susceptibility to marketing manipulation.

- **In interpersonal conflict:** "Why are you always so difficult?" presupposes difficulty is a stable trait. Detecting the presupposition allows responding to the actual content rather than the embedded judgment.

- **In professional settings:** "Since we've all agreed to move forward..." in a meeting uses presupposition to manufacture consensus. Detecting this allows for timely correction.

### 7.4 Handling Situations Where You Don't Share the Common Ground

When you realize you're missing presupposed knowledge:

1. **Normalize the gap:** "I missed that context -- can you fill me in?" is a socially acceptable way to surface a missing presupposition without drawing attention to it as a deficit.

2. **Use questions strategically:** "What's the background on this?" lets you gather presupposed information without admitting you "should" have known it.

3. **Pattern recognition:** Track which types of presuppositions you commonly miss. If it's cultural references, pop culture knowledge, or workplace gossip, those are domains where intentional information-gathering can help build the presuppositional base.

4. **Reframe the situation:** You're not "failing" to know something everyone else knows. The speaker *assumed* you knew it. That assumption may have been wrong -- and that's on the speaker as much as on you.

## 8. Key Research Findings Summary

### What the Research Converges On:

1. **Presupposition processing in autism is not uniformly impaired.** Lexical presuppositions (triggered by words like "stop," "again," "too") are often processed at age-appropriate levels when matched for language ability. Presuppositions requiring Theory of Mind or social reasoning show more difficulty.

2. **Common ground management develops on a delayed trajectory in autism** but does develop. Older, more linguistically able autistic individuals show more sensitivity to shared knowledge in calibrating their communication.

3. **Autistic individuals show enhanced rationality** in multiple domains, including reduced susceptibility to framing effects, sunk cost fallacy, conjunction fallacy, and social conformity -- all of which involve resisting embedded assumptions similar to presuppositions.

4. **The literal processing tendency is a double-edged sword:** it can cause difficulty when presuppositions need to be accommodated smoothly in conversation, but it can be an advantage when presuppositions need to be detected and questioned.

5. **The distinction between linguistic and social pragmatics is critical.** Presuppositions triggered by linguistic form may be processed adequately; presuppositions that require modeling another person's knowledge state may be more challenging.

6. **Cross-neurotype communication is where presupposition problems concentrate.** Autistic people communicating with other autistic people face fewer presupposition mismatches, supporting the double empathy framework.

7. **Vulnerability to manipulation via presupposition is complex.** Autistic individuals may resist structural manipulation (loaded questions, misleading advertising) while being vulnerable to social manipulation (gaslighting, authority-based pressure to accept false presuppositions).

### What Remains Under-Researched:

- Direct studies of presupposition accommodation in autistic adults
- Real-time processing of loaded questions across the autism spectrum
- The interaction between presupposition detection and executive function demands
- How masking affects presupposition management (does performing neurotypical social behavior consume cognitive resources that would otherwise detect presuppositions?)
- Cross-cultural variation in presupposition-related challenges for autistic individuals
- Interventions specifically targeting presupposition awareness as a pragmatic skill
