# Iran's Axis of Resistance: Proxy Network & Regional Influence

## 1. Strategic Doctrine: "Forward Defense"

### Origins and Evolution

Iran's "forward defense" doctrine -- known in Persian as "defa-e pishro" -- is the strategic framework that underpins the entire Axis of Resistance. The doctrine emerged from the existential trauma of the Iran-Iraq War (1980-1988), during which Iran faced invasion, chemical weapons attacks, and near-total international isolation. The lesson Iran's military and political leadership drew was stark: Iran must never again be caught fighting a defensive war on its own territory with no allies.

The doctrine holds that Iran should project its power and fight its enemies on foreign soil through a network of allied and proxy armed forces, creating strategic depth and deterrence without relying on conventional military parity with adversaries like the United States or Israel, which Iran could never achieve.

Initially shaped during and after the Iran-Iraq War, the forward defense concept gained coherence in the early 2000s, and was fully operationalized by 2016 under the leadership of IRGC Quds Force commander Qasem Soleimani. The doctrine serves multiple strategic purposes:

1. **Asymmetric deterrence**: Iran cannot match American or Israeli conventional military power. The proxy network provides the ability to threaten retaliation across multiple fronts simultaneously, creating costs that deter direct military action against Iran.
2. **Strategic depth**: By maintaining armed allies in Lebanon, Syria, Iraq, Yemen, and the Palestinian territories, Iran extends the geographical space over which any adversary must operate to threaten Iran.
3. **Power projection without attribution**: Proxy forces allow Iran to exert influence, conduct operations, and impose costs on adversaries while maintaining plausible deniability and avoiding direct confrontation.
4. **Ideological legitimacy**: Support for the "oppressed" -- particularly Palestinians -- reinforces Iran's claim to moral leadership in the Muslim world, transcending the Sunni-Shia divide.

### The Orchestrating Body: IRGC Quds Force

The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps' Quds Force (sometimes transliterated as "Qods Force") is the external operations arm of the IRGC, responsible for managing Iran's entire proxy network. With an estimated 5,000-15,000 personnel, the Quds Force handles intelligence operations, unconventional warfare, arms transfers, financial support, and strategic coordination with allied forces across the region.

The Quds Force operates with significant autonomy within the Iranian system, reporting directly to the Supreme Leader rather than through the regular military chain of command or the civilian government. Its budget is largely opaque, though estimates suggest Iran spent approximately $700 million annually on the Axis of Resistance before sanctions began constricting resources after 2019.

## 2. Hezbollah (Lebanon)

### Origins: Born from Israeli Invasion

Hezbollah ("Party of God") was founded in 1982 in direct response to Israel's invasion of Lebanon. The invasion, which aimed to destroy the Palestine Liberation Organization's presence in Lebanon, displaced hundreds of thousands of Lebanese Shia in the south and the Bekaa Valley, creating a deep well of resentment and a ready base for recruitment.

Iran's revolutionary government, led by Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini, saw an opportunity to export its Islamic revolution and project power into the Levant. Approximately 1,500 IRGC trainers and advisers were dispatched to Lebanon's eastern Bekaa Valley in 1982. They did not fight Israeli forces directly but instead mobilized, trained, funded, and equipped a new underground militia drawn from various Lebanese Shia factions, including elements of the Amal movement and Islamic Dawa Party.

The IRGC provided the organizational template, military training, weapons, and ideology. Hezbollah's founding manifesto (1985) called for the establishment of an Islamic republic in Lebanon modeled on Iran, the destruction of Israel, and the expulsion of Western forces from Lebanon. Over time, the organization moderated some of these positions, but the core alliance with Iran remained its defining feature.

### Dual Identity: Militant Group and Political Party

Hezbollah evolved into a unique hybrid entity -- simultaneously a political party, a social services provider, and a military force that in many respects rivaled or exceeded the capabilities of the Lebanese state:

- **Political party**: Hezbollah has participated in Lebanese parliamentary elections since 1992, winning seats and participating in governing coalitions. It holds significant political influence, particularly within the Shia community but also through alliances with Christian and Druze factions.
- **Social services**: The organization runs an extensive network of hospitals, schools, agricultural cooperatives, and reconstruction programs, providing services to Lebanon's Shia community that the weak Lebanese state could not. This social infrastructure is a critical source of legitimacy and loyalty.
- **Media**: Hezbollah operates Al-Manar television and various media outlets that broadcast its messaging across the Arab world.
- **Military force**: Hezbollah's military wing, the Islamic Resistance, developed into the most capable non-state armed force in the world, with capabilities exceeding those of many national armies.

### The 2006 Lebanon War

The July-August 2006 war between Hezbollah and Israel was a defining moment. Triggered by Hezbollah's cross-border raid and capture of two Israeli soldiers, the 34-day conflict saw Israel launch a massive air and ground campaign in southern Lebanon.

Hezbollah's performance surprised observers:
- The group fired approximately 4,000 rockets at Israeli territory (from a pre-war arsenal of roughly 15,000), striking deep into northern Israel and disrupting daily life for millions.
- Hezbollah's fighters demonstrated sophisticated infantry tactics, effective use of anti-tank missiles (particularly the Russian-made Kornet), and pre-prepared defensive positions.
- Israel's air power and ground forces inflicted significant damage but failed to destroy Hezbollah's military capabilities or stop the rocket fire.

The war ended in a UN-brokered ceasefire (UNSCR 1701). Israel withdrew, and the UN Interim Force in Lebanon (UNIFIL) was expanded. Hezbollah declared a "divine victory" -- a characterization that, while contested, reflected the reality that a non-state actor had fought the region's strongest conventional military to a standstill.

### The Arsenal Buildup

After 2006, Hezbollah dramatically expanded its arsenal with Iranian and Syrian assistance:
- **Pre-2006**: Approximately 15,000 rockets, mostly short-range Katyushas.
- **By 2023-2024**: Estimated 150,000+ rockets, missiles, and mortars, including precision-guided munitions capable of striking anywhere in Israel.
- The arsenal included short-range rockets (Katyusha, Grad), medium-range rockets (Fajr-3, Fajr-5, Raad-2, Raad-3, Khaibar-1), and long-range precision missiles (Fateh-110 variants, Scud derivatives).
- Hezbollah also possessed anti-ship missiles, advanced anti-tank weapons, surface-to-air missiles, and armed drones.
- The Radwan Force, Hezbollah's elite special operations unit, was trained for cross-border raids into Israeli territory.

Israeli estimates suggested Hezbollah could launch at least 2,500 rockets per day for eight weeks -- a bombardment that would overwhelm Israel's air defense systems, including Iron Dome.

### Involvement in the Syrian Civil War

Beginning in 2012 and escalating dramatically in 2013, Hezbollah intervened militarily in the Syrian civil war to save the Assad regime -- Iran's most important state ally and the critical land bridge connecting Iran to Hezbollah through Iraq and Syria.

Hassan Nasrallah initially denied Hezbollah's involvement, but by April 2013 publicly acknowledged it, framing the intervention as defending Lebanese Shia communities on the Syrian border and preventing the spread of takfiri (extremist Sunni) groups into Lebanon.

At its peak, Hezbollah had thousands of fighters deployed across Syria, participating in major battles including the recapture of Qusayr (June 2013), the siege of Aleppo, operations around Damascus, and campaigns in southern Syria near the Israeli-occupied Golan Heights. The Syrian deployment gave Hezbollah's fighters extensive combat experience but also cost the organization significant casualties -- estimated at 1,500-2,000 killed.

### Devastation in 2024-2026

The October 7, 2023 Hamas attack on Israel triggered a cascade of events that devastated Hezbollah:

- **October 8, 2023**: Hezbollah began firing rockets at Israeli positions in the Shebaa Farms area in solidarity with Palestinians, initiating a low-intensity cross-border conflict.
- **September 17-18, 2024**: Israel conducted the "pager attack" -- a sophisticated operation that detonated explosive-laden pagers and walkie-talkies distributed to Hezbollah operatives, wounding thousands.
- **September 27, 2024**: Israel assassinated Hassan Nasrallah, Hezbollah's Secretary-General since 1992, in a massive airstrike on Beirut's southern suburbs (Dahieh). His deputy, Hashem Safieddine, was also killed.
- **September-November 2024**: Israel launched a full-scale military campaign against Hezbollah in southern Lebanon, systematically destroying tunnel networks, weapons depots, and command infrastructure. Israel's ground incursion and air campaign eliminated most of Hezbollah's senior leadership.
- **November 27, 2024**: A ceasefire agreement was signed, requiring Israeli withdrawal from southern Lebanon and Hezbollah's withdrawal north of the Litani River.

Hezbollah's losses were staggering: possibly up to 4,000 members killed -- over 10 times its losses in the 2006 war. Israel claimed to have confiscated over 155,000 weapons and pieces of military equipment, including around 12,000 explosive devices, drones, more than 13,000 anti-tank missile launchers and rockets, and anti-aircraft missiles. The organization's entire command structure was decapitated, and a significant portion of its arsenal was destroyed.

On March 2, 2026, the ceasefire collapsed when Hezbollah launched rocket salvos at Israel in retaliation for the killing of Iran's Supreme Leader Khamenei, triggering another round of Israeli strikes in Lebanon.

## 3. Hamas (Gaza)

### The Pragmatic Alliance: Shia Iran and Sunni Hamas

Iran's relationship with Hamas represents one of the most strategically significant cross-sectarian alliances in the Middle East. Hamas, founded in 1987 as an offshoot of the Egyptian Muslim Brotherhood, is a Sunni Islamist movement. Its alliance with Shia Iran defies the simplistic Sunni-Shia framework often applied to Middle Eastern politics.

The relationship has always been, as the European Council on Foreign Relations characterized it, "a marriage of convenience produced by shared interests on the ground rather than ideological affinity." The shared interests are straightforward: opposition to Israel's existence, resistance to American hegemony in the region, and the Palestinian cause, which Iran has championed since the 1979 revolution as a core pillar of its foreign policy identity.

### Iranian Support

Iran's support for Hamas has encompassed:

- **Financial support**: By 2023, Iran reportedly provided Hamas with up to $350 million per year, making it one of the group's primary funding sources. Earlier estimates put the figure at around $30 million annually, suggesting a dramatic increase over the years.
- **Weapons and military technology**: Iran smuggled weapons to Hamas via multiple routes -- through Yemen and Sudan, across the Egyptian Sinai via Bedouin smuggler networks, and through tunnels into Gaza. Critically, Iran also trained Palestinian engineers to manufacture weapons locally, including rockets (Qassam series, later longer-range variants), anti-tank missiles, and drones, enabling Hamas to produce weapons indigenously even when smuggling routes were disrupted.
- **Training**: Thousands of Hamas fighters received military training at IRGC bases in Iran and Lebanon, learning infantry tactics, weapons handling, explosives, and tunneling techniques.
- **Intelligence**: Iran provided intelligence support and strategic guidance through the Quds Force.

### October 7, 2023: Iran's Role

The question of Iran's involvement in Hamas's October 7 attack on Israel remains one of the most debated intelligence questions:

**Evidence suggesting Iranian involvement**:
- Reports (including from the Wall Street Journal, citing senior Hamas and Hezbollah members) indicated that IRGC officials helped plan the attack and gave the green light at a meeting in Beirut on October 2, 2023.
- Captured documents reportedly showed Hamas coordinated with Iran for approximately two years in preparation for a "strategic operation" against Israel.
- In the weeks before the attack, approximately 500 fighters from Hamas and Palestinian Islamic Jihad reportedly received training in Iran under Quds Force supervision.
- Iran's decades of weapons, training, and funding support made the attack's sophistication possible.

**Evidence against direct Iranian planning**:
- U.S. intelligence assessments suggested Hamas's attack surprised Iran, indicating Tehran was not involved in the specific operational planning.
- Iran denied any role in the attack.
- Hamas itself reportedly maintained strict operational security, limiting knowledge of the attack's details to a small circle.

The most likely assessment, held by many analysts, is that Iran provided the long-term enabling support -- weapons, training, funding, and strategic encouragement -- that made the attack possible, while Hamas retained operational independence in planning the specific date, targets, and tactics. As one analyst summarized: "It is unlikely that the October 7 attacks could have happened without decades-long Iranian support," even if Iran did not direct the specific operation.

## 4. Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ)

Palestinian Islamic Jihad is Iran's closest Palestinian ally, with a relationship that is deeper and more ideologically aligned than the Iran-Hamas connection. Unlike Hamas, which has sometimes distanced itself from Iran (notably during the Syrian civil war, when Hamas sided with the Sunni opposition against Iran's ally Assad), PIJ has been consistently loyal to Tehran.

Iran is virtually PIJ's sole state sponsor, providing:
- Financial support: Over $100 million annually to Hamas and PIJ combined, according to U.S. State Department estimates in 2020. The IRGC pledged $70 million to PIJ in May 2016 alone.
- Weapons, training, and intelligence support mirroring the Hamas relationship.
- PIJ operatives have explicitly acknowledged: "We buy our weapons with Iranian money" and "An important part of our activity is under the supervision of Iranian experts."

PIJ is smaller than Hamas but more directly oriented toward armed struggle, with less emphasis on social services or governance. It has participated in most rounds of conflict with Israel from Gaza and has conducted attacks in the West Bank.

## 5. The Houthi Movement (Ansar Allah, Yemen)

### Building the Relationship

The Houthis -- officially known as Ansar Allah ("Supporters of God") -- are a Zaydi Shia movement from northern Yemen. While Zaydi Shiism differs significantly from Iran's Twelver Shiism, religious connections provided the foundation for a strategic relationship that has grown dramatically since 2015.

The relationship has deep roots: the Houthi family's religious scholars, including Badr al-Din al-Houthi (father of the movement's namesake, Hussein al-Houthi, and the current leader, Abdul-Malik al-Houthi), studied at Iranian religious institutions in Qom. Iran provided modest support to the Houthis during their intermittent conflicts with the Yemeni government from 2004 onward.

However, the relationship intensified after the 2014-2015 Houthi takeover of Sanaa and the subsequent Saudi-led military intervention in March 2015. The Saudi campaign, supported by a coalition including Bahrain, Egypt, Jordan, Kuwait, Morocco, Qatar, Sudan, the UAE, and later Eritrea and Pakistan, with U.S. logistical and intelligence support, aimed to restore the government of President Abdrabbuh Mansur Hadi.

### The Saudi-Led Coalition War

The intervention, which began on March 26, 2015, became one of the world's worst humanitarian catastrophes:
- The coalition conducted over 25,000 airstrikes, causing over 19,000 civilian casualties.
- More than 377,000 people have died since the intervention began, with approximately 60% of deaths resulting from indirect causes such as food insecurity and lack of healthcare.
- 21.6 million people (78% of Yemen's population) required humanitarian aid, including 11 million children. Over 4.5 million were displaced.
- A de facto blockade dramatically worsened conditions, restricting food, fuel, and medical supply imports.

The war was widely criticized internationally, with human rights organizations documenting numerous coalition airstrikes on hospitals, schools, markets, and wedding parties. U.S. and British arms sales to Saudi Arabia became a major political controversy.

### Iran's Support for the Houthis

Iran's support for the Houthis has been significant but modest compared to its investment in Hezbollah or Iraqi militias:

- **Financial support**: Estimated in the low hundreds of millions of dollars per year since 2015 -- a fraction of what Iran provides to other proxies.
- **Weapons**: Iran provided small arms, ammunition, and components for more advanced weapons systems including ballistic missiles, cruise missiles, and drones. Weapons were smuggled via maritime routes, often in small vessels making the journey from Iran or Oman.
- **Technology transfer**: Critically, Iran transferred the knowledge and components for indigenous weapon production, enabling the Houthis to manufacture increasingly sophisticated weapons locally.
- **Training and intelligence**: IRGC advisers and Hezbollah trainers provided military instruction.

However, characterizing the Houthis as simple "Iranian proxies" overstates Iran's control. The Houthis have their own political agenda, leadership structure, and domestic constituency. Their primary grievances -- Zaydi marginalization, northern underdevelopment, corruption -- are indigenous to Yemen. Iran has leveraged these grievances rather than created them.

### Drone and Missile Capabilities

By 2024-2025, the Houthis possessed an arsenal of remarkable sophistication for a non-state actor:
- **Ballistic missiles**: Including the Toufan (range: 1,800 km) and Quds-2 (range: 1,350 km).
- **Cruise missiles**: From the Iranian Soumar family, with strike ranges of approximately 2,000 km.
- **Drones/loitering munitions**: Samad-3 and Samad-4 UAVs (range: 1,800 km), Wa'id drones (similar to Iran's Shahed-136, range: 2,500 km).
- **Naval drones**: 7-meter unmanned surface vessels laden with explosives for maritime attacks.

### Red Sea Operations (2023-2025)

Beginning October 19, 2023, the Houthis launched missiles and drones at Israel and began attacking commercial shipping in the Red Sea and the Bab el-Mandeb Strait, the southern maritime gateway to the Suez Canal. They declared these attacks were in solidarity with Palestinians in Gaza and demanded an end to Israel's military operations.

The Houthi campaign achieved what no non-state actor had before: effectively disrupting one of the world's most critical shipping lanes. Major shipping companies rerouted vessels around the Cape of Good Hope, adding weeks and significant costs to journeys. The economic impact rippled through global supply chains.

A U.S.-led multinational naval coalition (Operation Prosperity Guardian) attempted to protect shipping and conducted airstrikes on Houthi positions in Yemen, but the Houthis proved resilient, continuing attacks despite the military response. The campaign demonstrated that a relatively low-cost investment by Iran had created a strategic actor capable of challenging the combined naval power of the United States, United Kingdom, and allied nations for 18 months.

The Red Sea attacks were suspended around October 2025 following a Gaza peace plan proposal, but resumed in March 2026 after the U.S.-Israeli strikes on Iran.

## 6. Iraqi Shia Militias (Popular Mobilization Forces)

### Formation and Rise

The Popular Mobilization Forces (PMF, Arabic: al-Hashd al-Shaabi) emerged in June 2014 in response to the Islamic State's dramatic conquest of Mosul and much of northern and western Iraq. Grand Ayatollah Ali Sistani, Iraq's most revered Shia cleric, issued a fatwa calling on all able-bodied men to take up arms against ISIS -- a call that reflected the catastrophic failure of Iraq's U.S.-trained conventional military, which had collapsed in the face of the ISIS offensive.

The PMF became an umbrella organization encompassing dozens of militias, ranging from genuinely nationalist volunteer groups to long-established Iranian-backed factions. However, the Iran-aligned militias quickly dominated the PMF's organizational structure, leveraging Iranian weapons, training, and funding.

### Key Iran-Backed Militia Groups

**Kata'ib Hezbollah (KH)**: Considered the most powerful Iran-backed militia in Iraq. Founded around 2007 by Abu Mahdi al-Muhandis (who was killed alongside Soleimani in January 2020), KH received extensive training, funding, logistical support, weapons, and intelligence from the IRGC Quds Force. It was designated a Foreign Terrorist Organization by the United States in 2009 and was responsible for numerous attacks on U.S. forces in Iraq.

**Asa'ib Ahl al-Haq (AAH, "League of the Righteous")**: Led by Qais al-Khazali, AAH broke away from Muqtada al-Sadr's Mahdi Army around 2006 with IRGC support. Designated a Foreign Terrorist Organization by the U.S. in 2020, it has also entered Iraqi politics, winning parliamentary seats.

**Badr Organization**: One of the oldest Iran-backed groups, originally formed as the military wing of the Supreme Council for the Islamic Revolution in Iraq (SCIRI) in Iran during the Iran-Iraq War. Led by Hadi al-Amiri, the Badr Organization is deeply integrated into Iraq's political system and has held ministerial positions.

**Kata'ib Sayyid al-Shuhada**: Another IRGC-supported militia active in both Iraq and Syria.

### Role in Defeating ISIS

The Iran-backed PMF groups played a crucial military role in the campaign to defeat ISIS in Iraq. They fought in nearly every major battle during the 2014-2017 war, including:
- The defense of Baghdad's approaches
- Operations to recapture Tikrit (March 2015)
- The Battle of Fallujah (May-June 2016)
- The Battle of Mosul (October 2016 - July 2017)

Soleimani personally directed many of these operations, often photographed at the front lines alongside militia commanders. The anti-ISIS campaign gave the PMF both military credibility and political legitimacy, which the Iran-backed factions leveraged to entrench their power within the Iraqi state.

### Political Power in Iraq

After defeating ISIS, the PMF transitioned from an emergency military formation to a permanent feature of Iraqi political life:
- The PMF was officially incorporated into Iraq's security forces by parliamentary vote in 2016.
- PMF-affiliated political parties (particularly the Fatah Alliance) won significant seats in the 2018 and 2021 parliamentary elections.
- PMF leaders held ministerial and senior government positions.
- Iran-backed militias operated extensive economic networks, controlling border crossings, ports, and businesses.

Critics described this as the "Hezbollahization" of Iraq -- the creation of a state-within-a-state modeled on Hezbollah's dual political-military structure in Lebanon. The PMF's integration into the state gave Iran influence over Iraqi government policy while maintaining armed forces that operated outside normal military command structures.

### Attacks on U.S. Forces

Iran-backed Iraqi militias have conducted numerous attacks on U.S. military personnel and diplomatic facilities:
- During the 2003-2011 U.S. occupation, Iranian-supplied explosively formed penetrators (EFPs) killed hundreds of American soldiers.
- After October 2023, militia attacks on U.S. bases in Iraq and Syria intensified dramatically, with over 150 attacks in the following months, including a drone strike on Tower 22 in Jordan that killed three U.S. soldiers in January 2024.
- These attacks were directed by Soleimani and later Qaani as part of the "unity of fronts" strategy -- pressuring U.S. forces across the region to support Hamas during the Gaza conflict.

## 7. Syrian Involvement

### The Strategic Imperative

Syria under the Assad dynasty was Iran's most important state ally -- the only Arab country that had consistently aligned with Iran since the 1979 revolution. Syria served as the critical land bridge connecting Iran to Hezbollah in Lebanon, enabling the transfer of weapons and personnel through Iraqi and Syrian territory.

When the Syrian civil war erupted in 2011, Iran viewed Assad's survival as an existential strategic interest. The loss of Syria would sever Iran's connection to Hezbollah, eliminate a key staging area for projecting power in the Levant, and demonstrate that the Axis of Resistance could be broken.

### Scale of Involvement

Iran's intervention in Syria was massive and multifaceted:
- **Military advisers**: IRGC and Quds Force officers provided strategic guidance, command and control, and tactical advice to Syrian forces.
- **Ground forces**: Iran deployed its own military personnel, including IRGC ground forces and Basij militia members.
- **Proxy mobilization**: Iran organized, funded, and deployed Shia fighters from across the region: Hezbollah from Lebanon (the largest contingent), Afghan Shia refugees organized into the Fatemiyoun Division, Pakistani Shia in the Zeinabiyoun Brigade, and Iraqi Shia militias.
- **Financial support**: Iran provided billions of dollars in credit lines, fuel shipments, and direct financial support to the Assad government.
- **Military bases**: By 2023, Iran maintained 55 military bases in Syria and 515 other military points, predominantly in Aleppo and Deir Ezzor governorates and the Damascus suburbs. These constituted approximately 70% of foreign military sites in the country.

### Israeli Strikes on Iranian Assets

Israel conducted a sustained campaign of airstrikes against Iranian military assets in Syria, targeting:
- IRGC formations and commanders
- Iranian missile storage and transfer facilities
- Hezbollah operatives and weapons shipments
- Arms convoys destined for Hezbollah in Lebanon
- Iranian military bases near the Israeli-occupied Golan Heights

Israel carried out hundreds of strikes over the course of the Syrian conflict, operating with near-impunity in Syrian airspace. Several senior IRGC commanders were killed, including Brigadier General Mohammad Reza Zahedi, killed in an Israeli airstrike on the Iranian consulate in Damascus in April 2024 -- an attack that triggered Iran's first direct missile and drone attack on Israeli territory.

### The Loss of Syria (December 2024)

On December 8, 2024, the Assad regime collapsed after a lightning rebel offensive led by Hayat Tahrir al-Sham (HTS). The offensive, which began in late November and swept through Aleppo, Hama, and Homs before reaching Damascus in less than two weeks, caught Iran and its allies off guard.

Iran's forces in Syria -- already weakened by years of Israeli strikes and the devastation of Hezbollah in the September-November 2024 conflict with Israel -- were unable to mount effective resistance. Iran began withdrawing its personnel on December 6, evacuating Quds Force commanders, embassy staff, and IRGC personnel. Approximately 4,000 Iranian citizens were evacuated on flights organized by Mahan Air.

Assad fled to Moscow, where Russia granted him asylum. The loss of Syria was a catastrophic strategic blow to Iran: it severed the land bridge to Hezbollah, eliminated years of military infrastructure investment, and demonstrated the limits of forward defense when the home base (Assad's regime) collapsed.

## 8. The IRGC Quds Force: Orchestrator of the Network

### Soleimani's Legacy

Qasem Soleimani, who commanded the Quds Force from 1998 until his assassination on January 3, 2020, was the architect of Iran's modern proxy network. Under his leadership, the Quds Force transformed from a relatively modest external operations unit into the driving force behind Iran's geopolitical influence, extending Iranian power from Iraq across the Levant to Yemen.

Soleimani's approach was characterized by:
- **Personal relationships**: He maintained direct personal relationships with proxy leaders, from Nasrallah to Iraqi militia commanders to Houthi leadership.
- **Strategic vision**: He integrated disparate proxy forces into a coherent strategic framework, coordinating their activities to serve Iran's broader objectives.
- **Battlefield presence**: Unlike most intelligence chiefs, Soleimani frequently appeared at the front lines, directing operations personally in Iraq and Syria during the anti-ISIS campaign.
- **Political acumen**: He navigated complex local politics in each theater, managing the tensions between Iran's interests and those of its proxies.

Soleimani's assassination was intended to decapitate the Axis of Resistance. While it removed a uniquely talented commander, the organizational structures and relationships he built proved more durable than a single individual.

### Esmail Qaani: The Successor

Brigadier General Esmail Qaani succeeded Soleimani as Quds Force commander. Qaani's background was primarily in the eastern theater (Afghanistan, Pakistan) rather than the Arab world that was Soleimani's domain, and his leadership style was more bureaucratic and less charismatic than his predecessor's.

Since October 7, 2023, Qaani adopted a strategy of closely controlling all fronts, ordering proxy forces to "bother" American troops in Iraq, Syria, and Jordan and pressure the IDF, giving Hamas some "breathing space" -- but while being careful to stop short of escalation that would trigger a full-scale war. He continued Soleimani's pattern of visiting allies and proxies in Syria, Lebanon, Iraq, and Yemen, supplying arms, munitions, and money.

However, Qaani's tenure was marked by controversy. In October 2024, reports emerged that he was being questioned over security breaches, with suspicions he may have been connected to an intelligence leak that enabled Israel's devastating campaign against Hezbollah. By early 2026, reports suggested he may have been detained or executed by Iranian authorities on suspicion of being an Israeli spy -- allegations that, whether true or not, reflected the deep institutional crisis within the Quds Force after the repeated penetration of Iran's proxy networks by Israeli intelligence.

## 9. How the Network Serves Iran's Strategic Interests

### Deterrence

The Axis of Resistance serves as Iran's primary deterrent against attack. The implicit threat is that any strike on Iran would trigger retaliation not just from Iran's own military but from Hezbollah against Israel, from Iraqi militias against U.S. bases, from Houthis against Gulf shipping, and potentially from all fronts simultaneously. This "ring of fire" strategy was designed to make the cost of attacking Iran prohibitively high.

### Regional Influence

Through its proxy network, Iran has maintained influence over political outcomes in Lebanon, Iraq, Syria, Yemen, and the Palestinian territories -- a reach that far exceeds what Iran's conventional diplomatic or military power could achieve alone. This influence has allowed Iran to:
- Block the emergence of a unified anti-Iranian coalition in the Arab world
- Maintain leverage in negotiations on the nuclear file and other issues
- Project itself as a champion of the Palestinian cause and resistance to Western hegemony
- Prevent the permanent deployment of hostile military forces near its borders

### Strategic Depth

By maintaining armed allies on multiple borders of its adversaries, Iran forces potential attackers to consider threats from multiple directions. Israel must worry about Hezbollah in the north, Hamas in the south, and Iranian forces in Syria. The United States must consider the vulnerability of its troops and bases across the Middle East. Saudi Arabia and the Gulf states face Houthi missiles and drones.

## 10. Cost to Iran

### Financial Costs

Estimates of Iran's spending on the Axis of Resistance vary, but the Quds Force was spending roughly $700 million annually before 2019. This figure encompasses:
- Hezbollah: Estimated $700 million per year (the largest single recipient, and some estimates place this figure alone at $700 million, with total network spending significantly higher)
- Hamas and PIJ: $100-350 million annually
- Iraqi militias: Hundreds of millions in various forms of support
- Houthis: Low hundreds of millions annually
- Syrian intervention: Billions in credit lines, fuel, and military support over the course of the war

After 2019, U.S. sanctions significantly constricted Iran's ability to fund its proxies, though money continued to flow through smuggling networks, cryptocurrency, and oil sales to China.

### Political Costs

Iran's proxy activities have:
- Deepened its international isolation and provided justification for escalating sanctions
- Contributed to the collapse of the JCPOA, as critics cited Iran's regional behavior as evidence the deal was insufficient
- Damaged relations with Sunni-majority countries who view Iran's proxies as instruments of sectarian expansionism
- Generated domestic criticism from Iranians who question why resources are spent on foreign adventures while the economy deteriorates (the slogan "Not Gaza, not Lebanon, my life only for Iran" has been a recurring theme in protests)

### Military Costs

- Thousands of Iranian military personnel and IRGC members killed in Syria and Iraq
- Israeli strikes killed numerous senior IRGC officers
- The 2020 assassination of Soleimani was both a personal and institutional blow
- The 2024-2026 conflicts devastated Hezbollah and effectively destroyed Iran's position in Syria

## 11. Vulnerabilities of the Proxy Network

### Intelligence Penetration

The most devastating vulnerability exposed in 2024 was the depth of Israeli intelligence penetration of the Axis of Resistance. The pager attack on Hezbollah, the systematic assassination of Hezbollah's entire command structure, and the precision strikes on IRGC commanders all demonstrated that Israel had achieved extraordinary intelligence access to organizations that prided themselves on secrecy.

### Dependency on the Land Bridge

The Syria-Lebanon corridor was the critical infrastructure of the network. The fall of Assad in December 2024 severed this corridor, making it vastly more difficult to resupply Hezbollah and potentially cutting off the most important node in the Axis.

### Local Legitimacy Deficits

In Lebanon, Iraq, and Yemen, Iran's proxies face significant domestic opposition:
- Lebanon's 2019-2020 protests targeted the entire political establishment, including Hezbollah.
- Iraqi protests in October 2019 explicitly denounced Iranian influence, with protesters burning Iranian consulates and flags.
- In Yemen, the Houthi movement rules through coercion in many areas.

### Conventional Military Inferiority

When confronted with the full weight of Israeli or American conventional military power, Iran's proxies cannot ultimately prevail. The 2024 Hezbollah-Israel conflict demonstrated that Israel, when fully committed, could systematically dismantle Hezbollah's military infrastructure despite decades of buildup.

### Over-extension

Iran's network stretched across multiple countries and theaters, each with its own local dynamics, factions, and challenges. Managing this diverse portfolio required significant resources and attention, and setbacks in one theater (such as Syria) could cascade to others.

## 12. How October 7, 2023 Changed Everything

### The Intended Effect

From Iran's perspective, the October 7 attack was designed to derail the Abraham Accords normalization process between Israel and Saudi Arabia, demonstrate the continuing relevance of the Palestinian cause, and prove the potency of the Axis of Resistance. The "unity of fronts" strategy envisioned Hezbollah, the Houthis, and Iraqi militias all applying pressure simultaneously to prevent Israel from focusing solely on Gaza.

### The Unintended Consequences

The aftermath proved catastrophic for the Axis of Resistance, producing outcomes that many analysts described as "boomeranging" on Iran in spectacular fashion:

1. **Hezbollah devastated**: Rather than deterring Israel, Hezbollah's cross-border attacks invited a full-scale Israeli military response that decimated the organization. The elimination of Nasrallah and the senior leadership, the destruction of a major portion of the arsenal, and the loss of thousands of fighters represented the worst setback in Hezbollah's 42-year history.

2. **Syria lost**: The weakening of Hezbollah and Iran's forces in Syria created the vacuum that enabled the rebel offensive of December 2024. The loss of Assad -- Iran's most important state ally -- was a geopolitical disaster that few would have predicted in October 2023.

3. **Iran directly exposed**: The cycle of escalation eventually led to direct Iranian-Israeli confrontation. Iran's April and October 2024 missile and drone attacks on Israel, while politically necessary for deterrence credibility, demonstrated the limitations of Iran's own conventional capabilities, with most missiles intercepted by Israeli and allied air defenses.

4. **The Houthis as sole beneficiary**: The Houthis emerged as the one element of the Axis that strengthened its position. Their Red Sea campaign elevated them from a peripheral actor to a major force in the Middle Eastern strategic landscape, demonstrating that Iran's decentralized proxy model could succeed even as centralized nodes like Hezbollah were degraded.

5. **Iran's credibility questioned**: Iran's proxies and allies began questioning Tehran's reliability. When Hezbollah was being systematically dismantled, Iran's response was limited. When Assad fell, Iran evacuated rather than fought. The perception grew that Iran was willing to "fight to the last Arab" while protecting its own territory.

### The 2026 War

The culmination came in February-March 2026, when the United States and Israel launched joint strikes on Iran itself, killing the Supreme Leader and targeting military and government infrastructure across the country. The Axis of Resistance -- weakened by two years of attrition -- was unable to prevent the strikes, though Hezbollah launched a new barrage at Israel and the Houthis resumed Red Sea threats.

The network that Soleimani built over two decades, designed to protect Iran from exactly this scenario, had instead contributed to an escalation cycle that brought war to Iran's doorstep.

### Adaptation and Reconstitution

Despite these setbacks, analysts caution against declaring the Axis of Resistance dead. After Israel's 12-day war against Iran in June 2025, the network entered a phase of "strategic dormancy," evolving into a decentralized structure sustained by gray-zone economic activities including oil smuggling, cryptocurrency, and reconstruction contracts.

Hezbollah has historically demonstrated remarkable resilience, rebuilding after the 2006 war stronger than before. Iraqi militias retain deep roots in Iraqi society and politics. The Houthis control most of Yemen's populated territory. The organizational knowledge, ideological commitment, and strategic rationale for the network persist even as its capabilities have been severely degraded.
