# Concept Research: Semasiographic vs. Glottographic Systems

## Overview

Every writing system in human history can be classified along a fundamental axis: does it represent meaning directly (semasiographic), or does it encode the sounds of a specific spoken language (glottographic)? This distinction, first systematized by Ignace Gelb in his 1952 *A Study of Writing*, defines the theoretical framework for the entire visual language project. A metaphor-based visual language that encodes image schemas and conceptual structures rather than the phonemes of English or Mandarin is, by definition, a semasiographic system. The question is whether a semasiographic system can achieve the full expressive power of natural language — something that has never been accomplished.

The term "semasiographic" derives from the Greek *semasia* (meaning) and *graphe* (writing): writing that represents meaning directly without relying on phonetic encoding of any spoken language. Mathematical notation (2 + 3 = 5), road signs (red octagon = stop), musical notation (quarter note on the third line of a treble staff), and circuit diagrams are all functioning semasiographic systems — readable by speakers of any language without translation. But all are domain-restricted. No semasiographic system has ever achieved the open-ended expressiveness of natural language: the ability to say anything that can be thought, including novel propositions never before expressed.

Glottographic systems, by contrast, represent elements of a specific spoken language — whether at the level of words/morphemes (logographic systems like Chinese), syllables (syllabaries like Japanese kana), or phonemes (alphabets like Latin, abjads like Arabic). The overwhelming historical tendency has been toward glottographic representation. Gelb argued this was teleological — that writing systems naturally evolve from semasiographic origins toward ever more precise phonetic encoding. Modern scholars debate whether this "evolution" is inevitable or merely one historical trajectory among several possible paths.

## Core Components

### Component 1: Gelb's Taxonomy (1952)

- **Purpose**: Provide a theoretical framework for classifying all writing systems based on what their symbols represent
- **Classification**:
  - **Semasiography** (forerunner stage): Symbols represent meanings directly without phonetic specification. Includes pictographs, ideographs, and "thought writing"
  - **Word-syllabic** (logographic): Symbols represent words or morphemes, with syllabic components
  - **Syllabic**: Symbols represent syllables
  - **Alphabetic**: Symbols represent individual phonemes
- **The "Teleological" Claim**: Gelb argued that writing systems naturally evolve from semasiography toward phonographic representation — from meaning-based to sound-based. He positioned semasiography as a primitive "forerunner" phase, not as a viable endpoint.
- **Critical Assessment**: Gelb's teleological view is now contested. His claim that semasiographic systems are inherently limited and must evolve toward phonographic representation has been challenged by scholars who note that mathematical notation, musical notation, and sign language writing systems demonstrate viable alternatives to phonetic encoding. The teleological view also reflects a Western alphabetic bias that privileged the Greek alphabet as the "culmination" of writing development.

### Component 2: The Glottographic Spectrum

- **Purpose**: Understand the range of ways writing systems encode spoken language
- **Types**:
  - **Logographic**: Each symbol represents a word or morpheme (Chinese characters, Sumerian cuneiform, Egyptian hieroglyphs — though all three are actually mixed systems)
  - **Syllabic**: Each symbol represents a syllable (Japanese kana, Cherokee syllabary, Linear B)
  - **Alphabetic**: Each symbol represents a phoneme (Latin, Greek, Cyrillic, Hangul)
  - **Abjad**: Consonant-only alphabet with optional vowel marking (Arabic, Hebrew)
  - **Abugida**: Consonant-vowel unit with systematic vowel modification (Devanagari, Ethiopic)
- **Key Insight**: All glottographic systems identify phonetic values. This means they are inherently tied to a specific spoken language (or family of languages). A text in the Latin alphabet can only be "read" by someone who knows which spoken language it represents. But a semasiographic symbol (like ÷ or ♭) can be "read" in any language.

### Component 3: Functioning Semasiographic Systems

- **Purpose**: Identify existing semasiographic systems that succeed within restricted domains, analyzing why they work
- **Mathematical Notation**:
  - Fully semasiographic: 2 + 3 = 5 is read identically in English, Mandarin, Arabic, Swahili
  - Achieves precision, compositionality, and productivity within its domain
  - Shares meaning without encoding any language's phonology
  - But restricted to quantitative and logical relationships
- **Musical Notation**:
  - A quarter note on the G line of a treble staff represents the same pitch and duration regardless of the reader's language
  - Compositional: notes combine into chords, measures, phrases
  - But restricted to sound specification — cannot express "this passage should feel nostalgic" without verbal annotation
- **Chemical Formulas**:
  - H₂O means the same thing in every language
  - Compositional: elements combine into compounds following bonding rules
  - But restricted to molecular structure
- **Road Signs**:
  - Red octagon, triangular yield, circular prohibition — meaning encoded through shape, color, pictographic content
  - Internationally standardized (Vienna Convention on Road Signs, 1968)
  - But restricted to a fixed, closed vocabulary of traffic situations
- **Circuit Diagrams / Engineering Notation**:
  - Resistors, capacitors, transistors represented by universal symbols
  - Compositional: components combine into circuits following electrical rules
  - But restricted to electrical/electronic systems
- **Why These Systems Succeed**: All functioning semasiographic systems share four properties: (1) restricted domain with clear boundaries, (2) shared context among users, (3) finite or slowly growing vocabulary, (4) precise, unambiguous semantics. The question is whether these properties can be extended to general-purpose communication.

### Component 4: The Generality Problem

- **Purpose**: Explain why no semasiographic system has achieved open-ended expressiveness
- **The Core Challenge**: Natural language can express anything that can be thought — novel propositions, counterfactual conditions, nested hypotheticals, emotional nuance, irony, poetry, metaphor, lies. This open-ended expressiveness depends on several properties that domain-restricted semasiographic systems lack:
  - **Productivity**: The ability to create an unlimited number of novel expressions from a finite set of elements (compositionality + recursion)
  - **Displacement**: The ability to refer to things not present — past events, future possibilities, hypothetical scenarios, abstract concepts
  - **Metalinguistic capacity**: The ability to talk about the language itself ("I didn't mean that literally")
  - **Pragmatic flexibility**: The ability to mean different things with the same expression depending on context (irony, sarcasm, implicature)
- **Why Domain Restriction Helps**: Mathematical notation achieves compositionality and productivity within its domain because the domain itself constrains interpretation. "2 + 3" can only mean arithmetic addition because the domain excludes other interpretations. General-purpose communication lacks this constraint — the same visual symbol must function in contexts ranging from cooking instructions to philosophical arguments to love letters.
- **The Historical Record**: Otto Neurath's ISOTYPE was explicitly an attempt at general visual communication. "Isotype never came close to matching the expressivity of spoken languages." No semasiographic system with coverage as broad as a spoken language has ever existed.

### Component 5: Mixed Systems and the Semasiographic-Glottographic Continuum

- **Purpose**: Recognize that real writing systems often blend semasiographic and glottographic elements
- **Chinese Characters**: The most important example of a mixed system. Approximately 80%+ of Chinese characters are semantic-phonetic compounds — a semantic radical (semasiographic component) combined with a phonetic radical (glottographic component). The semantic radical places the character in a meaning domain (氵= water-related, 心 = heart/emotion-related), while the phonetic radical provides a pronunciation hint. Chinese is therefore partially semasiographic and partially glottographic.
- **Egyptian Hieroglyphs**: Combined logographic, phonetic (uniliteral, biliteral, triliteral), and determinative elements. Determinatives are purely semasiographic — they specify meaning domain without contributing to pronunciation. Example: the "walking legs" determinative after a verb indicates physical movement.
- **Aztec and Maya Notation**: Pre-Columbian Mesoamerican writing systems blended semasiographic notation (pictographic scenes, calendric symbols) with glottographic elements (phonetic signs representing syllables of Nahuatl or Mayan languages). Researchers studying Teotihuacan notation have analyzed these as occupying a middle ground between pure semasiography and glottography.
- **Modern Emoji**: Function as a semasiographic overlay on glottographic text. "I'm going to the 🏖️ tomorrow" mixes alphabetic text with a pictographic semasiograph.
- **Key Insight**: The semasiographic-glottographic distinction is a spectrum, not a binary. Most successful writing systems in history have been mixed. A viable visual language may need to be primarily semasiographic with a carefully designed glottographic subsystem (for proper names, borrowed terms, etc.) — exactly the design pattern Chinese evolved over millennia.

### Component 6: The Sign Language Counter-Argument

- **Purpose**: Address whether sign languages prove that full-expressiveness visual-spatial communication is possible without phonetics
- **The Argument**: Sign languages (ASL, BSL, JSL, LSF, and hundreds more) are full natural languages with complete grammars — syntax, morphology, pragmatics, poetry — all encoded in the visual-spatial modality through handshape, movement, location, and facial expression. They achieve full displacement, productivity, metalinguistic capacity, and pragmatic flexibility. They prove that the visual-spatial modality can support full linguistic expressiveness.
- **The Qualification**: Sign languages are not semasiographic systems. They are full natural languages that happen to use the visual-spatial modality instead of the vocal-auditory modality. They have phonology (formational parameters that function like phonemes), they are language-specific (ASL and BSL are mutually unintelligible), and they are tied to communities rather than universally transparent. A semasiographic visual writing system would need to be readable without prior knowledge of a specific sign language.
- **What Sign Languages Demonstrate**: That the visual-spatial modality has the bandwidth and structural resources for full linguistic expressiveness. The limitation of semasiographic systems is not a modality limitation — it is a design limitation. The question is whether those visual-spatial resources can be harnessed in a written (rather than gestural) system.

### Component 7: Implications for Design

- **Purpose**: Define what a general-purpose semasiographic system would need to succeed
- **Requirements Derived from Analysis**:
  1. **Compositionality**: Must combine primitives to produce unlimited novel expressions (like mathematics does for quantities)
  2. **Grammar**: Must encode relationships between symbols — agent, patient, action, time, condition, negation, scope — not just list symbols
  3. **Abstractability**: Must represent abstract concepts (justice, possibility, irony) not just concrete objects (house, tree, person)
  4. **Displacement**: Must reference past, future, hypothetical, and counterfactual situations
  5. **Productivity**: Must create new meanings for new situations without requiring new symbols for each concept
  6. **Phonetic Bridge**: Must handle proper names, borrowed words, and technical terminology through some subsystem (the phoneme representation problem)
  7. **Learnability**: Must be acquirable in reasonable time — not requiring the years of study that Chinese characters demand
- **The Design Hypothesis**: If image schemas (CONTAINER, PATH, FORCE, etc.) are indeed the pre-linguistic structures that ground all conceptual thought, and if primary metaphors systematically map abstract domains onto these spatial-bodily structures, then a system that visually represents image schemas and their metaphorical extensions could potentially be both semasiographic (not tied to any spoken language) and expressively general (not domain-restricted). This is the central hypothesis that the visual language project must test.

## How It Works

### Step 1: Classification of a Writing System
- **Trigger**: Encountering any system of visual marks used for communication
- **Action**: Determine whether the symbols encode (a) meaning directly (semasiographic), (b) speech sounds (glottographic), or (c) a combination of both
- **Result**: Placement on the semasiographic-glottographic continuum

### Step 2: Assessing Expressive Power
- **Trigger**: Evaluating a semasiographic system for general-purpose viability
- **Action**: Test whether it can express: (a) novel propositions, (b) counterfactual conditions, (c) temporal displacement, (d) negation, (e) quantification, (f) recursion/embedding, (g) metalinguistic reference
- **Result**: Identification of expressive gaps

### Step 3: Identifying the Phonetic Bridge Requirement
- **Trigger**: Attempting to express a proper name, borrowed word, or language-specific term in a semasiographic system
- **Action**: Determine what phonetic subsystem (if any) the system uses — fingerspelling, phonetic radicals, transliteration conventions
- **Result**: Assessment of whether the system can interface with the world of spoken language

- **Type**: Three-column comparison

- **Notes**: Argue that experiential grounding (the image schema approach) combines the language-independence of semasiography with the expressiveness potential of natural language

## Terminology Glossary

| Term | Definition | Also Known As |
|------|------------|---------------|
| Semasiography | Writing that represents meaning directly without encoding the sounds of a spoken language | Thought writing, ideographic writing |
| Glottography | Writing that represents elements of a specific spoken language (words, syllables, phonemes) | Phonographic writing |
| Logographic | Glottographic writing where symbols represent words or morphemes | Morphographic |
| Syllabic | Glottographic writing where symbols represent syllables | Syllabary |
| Alphabetic | Glottographic writing where symbols represent phonemes | Segmental writing |
| Abjad | Consonant-only alphabet with optional vowel marking | Consonantary |
| Abugida | Writing system where consonant-vowel units are the basic symbols, with vowels shown as modifications | Alphasyllabary |
| Determinative | A semasiographic element within a glottographic system that specifies meaning domain without contributing to pronunciation | Semantic classifier (in Chinese) |
| Gelb's teleology | The (now contested) claim that writing systems inevitably evolve from semasiographic to phonographic | Unilinear evolution of writing |
| Productivity | The ability of a language system to create unlimited novel expressions from finite elements | Creative capacity |
| Displacement | The ability to refer to things not present in the immediate context | Temporal/spatial displacement |

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: Mathematical Notation as Successful Semasiography
- **Scenario**: A Japanese mathematician, a Brazilian engineer, and a Nigerian accountant all read "∫₀¹ x² dx = 1/3"
- **Illustration**: All three understand the expression identically, despite speaking different languages. No translation is needed. The notation is fully compositional — novel expressions can be constructed freely. But none of them can use this notation to say "I'm feeling anxious about tomorrow's meeting."
- **Key Insight**: Mathematical notation proves semasiographic compositionality works. Its limitation is domain restriction, not inherent impossibility.

### Example 2: Chinese as a Semasiographic-Glottographic Hybrid
- **Scenario**: The character 洋 (yáng) means "ocean/foreign." Its semantic radical 氵(three water drops) says "this relates to water" — semasiographic, transparent to anyone. Its phonetic radical 羊 (yáng, "sheep") says "pronounce it like 'sheep'" — glottographic, meaningful only to Mandarin speakers.
- **Illustration**: A Korean reader familiar with Chinese characters (hanja) can extract the semantic meaning from the radical but pronounces the character completely differently. The semasiographic element travels across languages; the glottographic element does not.
- **Key Insight**: Chinese demonstrates that semasiographic and glottographic elements can coexist within a single symbol. This mixed strategy may be the only viable path for a visual language that must interface with the phonetic world.

### Example 3: The Counterfactual Test
- **Scenario**: Attempt to express "If it hadn't rained yesterday, we would have gone hiking" in various semasiographic systems.
- **Illustration**: Mathematical notation: impossible (not in domain). Road signs: impossible (not in domain). Emoji: 🌧️❌🥾🏔️? (hopelessly ambiguous — could mean "no rain, go hiking" or "rain stopped the hike" or "don't hike in rain"). Blissymbolics: possible but extremely cumbersome, requiring indicator stacking for counterfactual past conditional.
- **Key Insight**: Counterfactual conditionals are the acid test for semasiographic expressiveness. They require temporal displacement (yesterday), negation (hadn't), conditionality (if), and irrealis mood (would have). Any system that cannot express these is not a general-purpose language.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: Chinese characters are a semasiographic system (writing meaning, not sound).
  - **Reality**: This is the single most common misconception about writing systems. Over 80% of Chinese characters are semantic-phonetic compounds that include a phonetic element. Chinese encodes the sounds and morphemes of spoken Chinese — it is a glottographic system with significant semasiographic elements, not the reverse.

- **Misconception**: Semasiographic systems failed because humans "need" phonetic writing.
  - **Reality**: Sign languages demonstrate that full linguistic expressiveness is achievable without phonetics. The failure of semasiographic systems to achieve general-purpose expressiveness is a design failure (insufficient grammar and compositionality), not a modality limitation. The visual-spatial channel has the bandwidth; no one has yet built the right grammar to use it.

- **Misconception**: Emoji are the future of semasiographic communication.
  - **Reality**: Emoji are a vocabulary without grammar. They cannot express propositions, only supplement them. The sequence 😀🏠🌧️ could mean "I'm happy at home in the rain" or "Happy house rain" or "Home is happiness despite storms" — there is no compositional rule to determine the reading. Emoji illustrate the ceiling of ungrammatical semasiography.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **Aztec/Maya mixed notation**: Pre-Columbian Mesoamerican writing systems combined semasiographic elements (pictographic scenes, calendric notation, directional symbols) with glottographic syllabic signs. Recent research on Teotihuacan notation has analyzed this as a stable mixed system that deliberately maintained both channels rather than "evolving" toward pure phonography. This challenges Gelb's teleology.

- **Computer programming languages**: Partially semasiographic (the symbol `+` means addition in C, Python, Java, Ruby — language-independent) and partially arbitrary/conventional (variable names, keywords). Programming languages achieve enormous expressiveness within their domain through formal grammar and compositionality. They may be the closest existing model for what a general-purpose semasiographic system would need.

- **Emojis in legal/institutional contexts**: Courts and employers increasingly treat emoji as communicative acts with real semantic content ("that thumbs-up emoji constituted agreement to the contract"). This suggests that even a limited semasiographic system can acquire pragmatic force in context — but the meaning is always context-dependent and disputable, precisely because emoji lack grammar.

- **Historical Visual Language Systems** (Category 1): Each historical system can be classified as semasiographic, glottographic, or mixed

## Research Gaps

- Is there a formal proof (or strong argument) that general-purpose semasiography is impossible? Or is the absence of such a system merely an empirical gap?
- How do Teotihuacan and other Mesoamerican mixed systems handle the transition between semasiographic and glottographic elements within a single text? What are their compositional rules?
- Has anyone formally analyzed programming languages as semasiographic systems? The parallel to visual language design seems strong but underexplored.
- What is the minimum set of grammatical operations (negation, quantification, temporal reference, conditionality, recursion) that a semasiographic system must support to count as "expressively general"?
- Can sign language writing systems (SignWriting) serve as a bridge model — they notate a visual-spatial language in writing, which is close to what a semasiographic visual language would do?
