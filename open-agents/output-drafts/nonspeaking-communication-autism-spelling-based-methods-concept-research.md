# Concept Research: Spelling-Based Methods — S2C, RPM, and Letterboard Communication

## Overview

Spelling-based communication methods occupy the most fiercely contested ground in the landscape of non-speaking autism communication. These methods — the Rapid Prompting Method (RPM), Spelling to Communicate (S2C), and various letterboard approaches — claim to enable non-speaking autistic individuals to communicate by pointing to letters on a board held by a trained communication partner. Proponents describe a population of non-speaking autistic people who possess age-appropriate or above-average literacy, intelligence, and complex inner lives that can be accessed through spelling, if the right motor support and prompting strategies are provided. Critics argue that these methods are variants of facilitated communication under new names, carrying the same risks of facilitator influence and the same absence of controlled evidence for communicator authorship.

The Rapid Prompting Method was developed by Soma Mukhopadhyay around 1991 in India to teach her son Tito Rajarshi Mukhopadhyay, who was diagnosed with severe autism. RPM uses a "Teach-Ask" paradigm with intensive multisensory prompts — auditory, visual, and tactile — to engage the learner's attention and elicit responses through pointing to letters on a held-up letterboard, writing, or typing. Soma Mukhopadhyay moved to the United States and established the HALO (Helping Autism through Learning and Outreach) clinic in Austin, Texas. Spelling to Communicate (S2C) was developed by Elizabeth Vosseller beginning around 2013, with formalized protocols published in 2015. Vosseller founded I-ASC (International Association for Spelling as Communication) in 2019 to promote the method. S2C describes itself as having "clear protocols, processes, and procedures for spelled communication that is replicable, reliable, responsible, and researchable." Both methods involve a facilitator/communication partner holding a letterboard while the individual points to letters.

The evidence situation is stark. A 2019 systematic review of RPM published in the Review Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders found no experimental studies of sufficient rigor to demonstrate a link between RPM and communication improvements in the person with a disability, no studies testing authorship of messages produced using RPM, and no experimental studies addressing RPM's effectiveness. ASHA has explicitly warned against both RPM and S2C, stating "there is no evidence that messages produced using RPM reflect the communication of the person with a disability." In 2025, a presentation at ASHA titled "The Empty Review" highlighted that systematic reviews of FC/RPM/S2C consistently come up empty — there are no qualifying studies to review. The Jaswal et al. 2020 eye-tracking study is the most frequently cited research by proponents, but it has received substantial published criticism that the study fails to control for facilitator influence, uses a sample too small (N=9) to draw generalizable conclusions, and rests on assumptions about eye-gaze that the critics consider unfounded. The proponent argument centers on the claim that motor planning support does not equal authorship influence, and that the scientific establishment's refusal to study these methods in context-appropriate ways constitutes a form of ableism.

## Core Components

### Component 1: Rapid Prompting Method (RPM)
- **Developer**: Soma Mukhopadhyay (born 1963), who holds a master's degree in chemistry and a bachelor's in education; developed RPM around 1991 for her son Tito
- **Organization**: HALO (Helping Autism through Learning and Outreach), based in Austin, Texas; Soma serves as Executive Director of Education
- **Method description**: The "Teach-Ask" paradigm involves the instructor delivering academic content through rapid, multisensory presentation (reading aloud, writing, drawing, using manipulatives) and then immediately asking questions that the learner answers by pointing to letters on a letterboard held in the air by the instructor. The instructor provides intensive verbal, auditory, visual, and/or tactile prompts throughout.
- **Physical contact**: The instructor holds the letterboard in the air (not on a table) while the learner points. This is described by proponents as less physical contact than classic FC (no hand-over-hand support). However, the instructor controls the position, angle, and distance of the letterboard, and observers note that Mukhopadhyay gives a high rate of verbal, gestural, and physical prompts even to the most independent students.
- **Tito Mukhopadhyay**: The original RPM student, born 1989, has published multiple books including "The Mind Tree" (2003) and "How Can I Talk If My Lips Don't Move?" (2011). He uses handwriting as his preferred communication mode. His case is cited as evidence that RPM can enable complex communication, though his communication has not been subjected to formal controlled authorship testing.

### Component 2: Spelling to Communicate (S2C)
- **Developer**: Elizabeth Vosseller, SLP; began using assisted communication techniques around 2013; formalized S2C protocols in 2015
- **Organization**: I-ASC (International Association for Spelling as Communication), founded 2019; trains practitioners, promotes the method, and advocates for research
- **Method description**: S2C describes a progression from stencil boards (large motor pointing with full arm movement) to laminated letterboards to keyboards. The communication partner holds the board and provides verbal prompts and direction. S2C claims to have "clear protocols" that distinguish it from FC, emphasizing that practitioners provide "motor support" rather than "hand-over-hand support."
- **Claimed distinctions from FC**: Proponents emphasize that S2C involves no hand-over-hand contact; the board is held rather than the person's hand; the method teaches motor planning skills progressively; and the goal is independence. Critics argue these distinctions are superficial — the facilitator still controls the communication display, provides high levels of prompting, and there is no controlled evidence that the claimed differences produce different outcomes.
- **The Spellers documentary (2023)**: Released March 31, 2023, executive produced by Jenny McCarthy-Wahlberg and Donnie Wahlberg. Won awards at Phoenix Film Festival. Promotes S2C without mentioning facilitated communication or the controlled studies that debunked FC. Critical reviewers noted scenes suggesting facilitator influence, including a young man pointing while looking in the opposite direction and a facilitator pulling the board away after nonsense letter sequences.

### Component 3: The Evidence Gap
- **Systematic reviews finding no evidence**:
  - **Tostanoski et al. (2019)**: Systematic review of RPM in the Review Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders found zero experimental studies meeting inclusion criteria; concluded "no studies have tested authorship of messages produced using RPM"
  - **Schlosser et al. (2014)**: Found "unequivocal evidence for facilitator control" in FC; implications for S2C/RPM as related methods
  - **ASHA 2025 "Empty Review"**: Presentation documenting that systematic reviews of FC/RPM/S2C consistently find zero qualifying studies to include
- **ASHA position**: "There is no evidence that messages produced using RPM reflect the communication of the person with a disability." ASHA warns that "RPM and S2C raise significant questions about author validity" and that "many experts argue that the apparent success of RPM and S2C may be due to facilitator influence."
- **The research standoff**: Proponents argue that the absence of evidence is not evidence of absence — that researchers have not conducted appropriate studies. Critics respond that the burden of proof lies with those making extraordinary claims, and that proponent organizations have actively resisted controlled testing.

### Component 4: The Jaswal 2020 Eye-Tracking Study
- **Study**: Jaswal, Wayne & Golino (2020), "Eye-tracking reveals agency in assisted autistic communication," published in Nature Scientific Reports
- **Method**: Used head-mounted eye-tracking with 9 non-speaking autistic letterboard users to examine whether they looked at and pointed to letters in patterns consistent with communicative agency
- **Findings**: Participants looked at and pointed to letters quickly, with patterns the authors interpreted as consistent with planning and production processes
- **Published critiques**:
  - **Catherine & Katharine (2021)**: Published in Evidence-Based Communication Assessment and Intervention; argued the study rests on "faulty assumptions" about test performance, prerequisites for linguistic skills, the disconnect between speech and typing abilities, and what eye-gaze can prove about agency; noted "there is no officially recognized language disorder that combines extant oral skills with pragmatic skills that only emerge during hunt and peck typing"
  - **Skeptical Inquirer (Vyse)**: Detailed methodological concerns including small sample size (N=9), no control condition without a facilitator present, and failure to rule out subtle cueing
  - **facilitatedcommunication.org analysis**: Argued that the study "fails to establish the authenticity of S2C for non-speakers"; noted that looking at letters is a necessary but not sufficient condition for authorship
- **Key limitation**: The study demonstrated that participants looked at letters before pointing to them, but critics argue this does not establish that the participants chose which letters to point to. A person following the lead of a facilitator who positions the board would also look at the target letter.

### Component 5: The Jaswal 2024 Literacy Study
- **Study**: Jaswal, Lampi & Stockwell (2024), "Literacy in nonspeaking autistic people," published in the journal Autism
- **Method**: Used an iPad tapping task where participants tapped sequentially pulsing targets; the researchers looked for reaction-time signatures consistent with literacy (faster responses to targets forming real words vs. nonwords)
- **Findings**: About half (17 of 31) of non-speaking autistic participants showed patterns the authors interpreted as consistent with foundational literacy skills, despite most reportedly never receiving formal literacy instruction
- **Published rebuttal**: "Illusions of literacy in nonspeaking autistic people: a response to Jaswal et al. 2024" (Evidence-Based Communication Assessment and Intervention, 2024) argued:
  - Participants were recruited from RPM/S2C programs, meaning they regularly used letterboards — this creates practice effects with letter sequences
  - No comparison condition without the experimenter reading the sentence aloud
  - The task measured reaction times to letter sequences, not actual reading comprehension
  - The assistant's presence and behavior during the task were not controlled
  - The study assessed "knowledge of orthographic conventions" rather than literacy per se
- **Current status**: The study and its rebuttal represent the ongoing scientific debate; neither settles the question of whether S2C/RPM users are the authors of their messages

### Component 6: The Motor Support vs. Authorship Influence Argument
- **Proponent claim**: Motor support is distinct from authorship influence. Just as a physical therapist can support someone's arm during a reaching exercise without choosing the direction of the reach, an S2C practitioner can provide motor support (holding the board, verbal prompts, sensory regulation) without influencing which letters the individual selects. The individual has the intention and linguistic competence; they need motor scaffolding to execute the pointing.
- **Critic response**: Unlike physical therapy where the goal direction is set by the patient's own visible reaching behavior, in letterboard spelling the "goal" is the next letter in a message whose content is unknown. The facilitator controls the position, angle, and distance of the letterboard; provides verbal cues about the pace and correctness of spelling; and makes decisions about which letter was selected when pointing is ambiguous. All of these represent channels through which the facilitator can influence the output. Without controlled testing, the claim that motor support does not equal authorship influence is unfalsifiable.
- **The prompt dependency question**: Even methods claiming to reduce physical contact still involve high levels of verbal prompting, pacing cues, and facilitator decision-making about ambiguous pointing. Critics argue that these non-physical prompts can guide letter selection just as effectively as physical hand support.

## How It Works (The RPM/S2C Method)

### Step 1: Engagement through Rapid Multisensory Input
- **Trigger**: Instructional session begins
- **Action**: In RPM, the instructor rapidly presents academic content — reading aloud, writing on paper, drawing diagrams, manipulating objects — while providing continuous verbal narration. The pace is deliberately fast to "keep the learner's attention channel open" (proponent framing) or to prevent the learner from disengaging (neutral framing).
- **Result**: The learner is exposed to content and engaged through multiple sensory channels

### Step 2: Question Posing and Letterboard Presentation
- **Trigger**: Content has been presented
- **Action**: The instructor asks a question about the content (e.g., "What did Columbus discover?") and holds up a letterboard in the air in front of the learner. The learner is expected to point to letters spelling an answer.
- **Result**: The learner begins pointing to letters while the instructor holds the board
- **Critical variables**: The instructor determines the board's position, angle, height, and distance; provides verbal cues ("keep going," "almost there," "try again"); and decides which letter the learner's pointing finger has selected when pointing is ambiguous

### Step 3: Response Construction and Interpretation
- **Trigger**: The learner points to letters
- **Action**: The instructor announces each letter as it is selected, building a word or message. If the sequence appears to be misspelling, the instructor may prompt the learner to "try again" or may pull the board away and reposition it.
- **Result**: A letter sequence is constructed and interpreted as a message
- **Authorship question**: At each step, the instructor makes interpretive decisions — was that an "E" or an "F"? Was that an intentional letter or a motor error? Should the board be moved? These decisions all represent potential channels for facilitator influence.

### Step 4: Claimed Progression Toward Independence
- **Trigger**: Extended practice (proponent framing)
- **Action**: Proponents describe a progression from stencil boards (large targets) to laminated letterboards to keyboards, with decreasing facilitator involvement. Some individuals are reported to transition to typing on keyboards or iPads without a facilitator holding a board.
- **Result**: Claimed independent communication
- **Critical assessment**: The transition from facilitated to independent communication is the critical claim. When it occurs and can be verified, it provides the strongest evidence for the method. However, systematic documentation of this transition with controlled verification at each stage has never been published. Some individuals described as "independent" still have a communication partner present who provides verbal cues and environmental support.

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: Ido Kedar — RPM to Independent Typing
- **Scenario**: Ido Kedar, born in 2000, was diagnosed with severe autism and assessed as intellectually disabled. At approximately age 7, he began using RPM through Soma Mukhopadhyay. He published "Ido in Autismland" at age 15 (2012), documenting the experience of being trapped in a body that would not obey his mind. He now communicates by typing on an iPad and describes himself as independently communicating.
- **Illustration**: Represents the strongest type of case for spelling-based methods — an individual who began with supported methods and transitioned to what is described as independent communication
- **Key Insight**: Kedar's case is compelling but has not been formally verified through controlled authorship testing. He is critical of ABA and has become an advocate for non-speaking autistic people. His Wikipedia article notes Soma Mukhopadhyay "was involved with subsequent facilitated communication interventions." The distinction between "types independently in some observers' judgment" and "independently verified through controlled testing" is crucial.

### Example 2: The Empty Systematic Review
- **Scenario**: Tostanoski et al. (2019) conducted a systematic review of RPM following standard Cochrane-style methodology. After searching multiple databases, screening hundreds of papers, and applying inclusion criteria for experimental studies with adequate methodological rigor, they found zero studies that met inclusion criteria. Their review was published as a paper documenting the absence of qualifying evidence.
- **Illustration**: The most rigorous form of evidence review found literally nothing to review — no controlled studies, no authorship tests, no experimental evidence for effectiveness
- **Key Insight**: Proponents argue this reflects researchers' failure to study the method, not the method's failure to work. Critics argue that after 30+ years of claims, the complete absence of controlled evidence is itself damning — especially when proponent organizations have resources to fund research but have not produced controlled authorship studies.

### Example 3: A Typical S2C Session
- **Scenario**: A non-speaking autistic teenager sits across from an S2C practitioner (Communication Regulation Partner). The practitioner presents a lesson about photosynthesis, reading aloud and drawing diagrams. Then the practitioner holds up a letterboard and asks, "What gas do plants absorb?" The teenager extends their arm and points to letters: C-O-2. The practitioner says "C" — "O" — "2" — "CO2, that's right!" The practitioner repositions the board: "What do they release?" The teenager points: O-X-Y-G-E-N.
- **Illustration**: Shows the observable process and the ambiguity at its heart — is the teenager demonstrating knowledge of photosynthesis, or is the practitioner (who just taught the content and knows the answers) unconsciously guiding the pointing?
- **Key Insight**: This scenario cannot be resolved by observation alone. The only way to determine authorship would be to test with information the practitioner does not have — which is exactly the controlled testing that proponents argue is inappropriate.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: S2C and RPM are completely different from facilitated communication
  - **Reality**: There are described procedural differences (board holding vs. hand-over-hand support), but the fundamental structure is the same: a facilitator controls the communication display and provides prompts while a non-speaking individual points to letters. Whether these procedural differences eliminate the risk of facilitator influence has not been tested in controlled studies.

- **Misconception**: Eye-tracking studies have proven that S2C users are the authors of their messages
  - **Reality**: The Jaswal 2020 study showed that 9 S2C users looked at letters before pointing to them, but multiple published critiques argue this does not establish authorship. Looking at a letter on a board someone is holding in front of you is expected behavior regardless of who is determining which letter is next.

- **Misconception**: All professionals oppose spelling-based methods
  - **Reality**: While every major professional scientific and medical organization has warned against these methods, many individual SLPs, educators, and therapists continue to use or support them. The gap between organizational positions and individual practice is significant.

- **Misconception**: Critics of S2C/RPM believe non-speaking autistic people cannot communicate
  - **Reality**: Critics strongly support evidence-based AAC and advocate for communication access through methods where authorship is not in question. The critique targets the specific method, not the population's communicative potential.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **Verified independent typers**: Some individuals who reportedly began with RPM or S2C are described as now typing independently on keyboards or iPads without a communication partner holding a board or providing prompts. If these transitions are genuine, they represent the strongest possible evidence for the methods. However, systematic controlled documentation of such transitions has not been published.
- **The university student**: The AAC literature includes case studies of individuals who transitioned from letterboard to iPad typing and went on to academic success. These cases are important but do not resolve the general question of whether the method reliably produces independent communicators.
- **Partial independence**: Some individuals may generate some genuine communication while also being subject to facilitator influence for other portions — a mixed authorship scenario that is particularly difficult to detect and study.

## Related Concepts

- **Facilitated Communication Controversy**: The historical predecessor and ongoing context for S2C/RPM; understanding FC is essential for evaluating spelling-based methods
- **Motor-Language Dissociation**: The scientific hypothesis invoked to explain why motor support might be needed and why it would not compromise authorship — but the hypothesis can be addressed through evidence-based AAC rather than methods requiring facilitator involvement
- **AAC Technology**: Evidence-based alternatives that provide communication access without the authorship concerns associated with spelling-based methods
- **Research Methodology**: The debate about what constitutes appropriate evidence for communication methods — controlled studies vs. ecological approaches

## Research Gaps

- **Controlled authorship studies of S2C/RPM**: The most fundamental gap — after 30+ years of RPM and nearly a decade of S2C, no controlled authorship test has been published. Either proponent organizations should fund such studies, or independent researchers should conduct them.
- **Transition documentation**: No systematic, controlled documentation of individuals transitioning from supported to independent communication through these methods exists. Case studies with video documentation and independent verification would be valuable.
- **Facilitator behavior analysis**: Detailed behavioral coding of what facilitators/CRPs actually do during S2C sessions — how often do they reposition the board? How are ambiguous points resolved? What verbal prompts are given? — would clarify the degree of facilitator involvement.
- **Comparison studies**: No studies comparing outcomes for non-speaking individuals receiving S2C/RPM vs. evidence-based AAC have been conducted. Such studies would help determine whether one approach produces better communication outcomes.
- **The motor planning rationale**: The claim that S2C/RPM provides needed motor support rests on the motor-language dissociation hypothesis, but no studies have specifically tested whether the motor support provided in S2C sessions is actually targeting motor planning deficits vs. serving as a channel for facilitator influence.
