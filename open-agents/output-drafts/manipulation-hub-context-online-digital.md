# Online and Digital Settings

## Pragmatic Manipulation and Self-Protection Hub — Context Page

> **Safety frame — read first**
>
> Online and digital contexts are not usually physically lethal in the way intimate partner contexts are, but they can be: cyberstalking has driven survivors to physical harm and to suicide; doxxing has produced real-world violence; coordinated harassment campaigns have produced documented suicides among targets, including disabled targets; and image-based sexual abuse (sometimes called revenge porn or non-consensual intimate imagery) has catastrophic effects that have been linked to suicide in multiple cases. The cumulative effects on mental health, employment, housing, and legal standing are also serious.
>
> If you are reading this page because:
>
> - You are being actively stalked online (someone is monitoring your accounts, following you across platforms, finding personal information about you);
> - You are being doxxed or threatened with doxxing (your personal information is being published or threatened to be published);
> - You have been the target of image-based sexual abuse (intimate images of you have been or are being shared without consent, including deepfakes);
> - You are being pile-on harassed (a coordinated or semi-coordinated group is targeting you across platforms);
> - You are being financially exploited online (romance scam, crypto scam, fake support relationship, fraudulent fundraiser using your situation);
> - You believe spyware or stalkerware is on your devices;
> - You are facing a legal or employment consequence from online activity;
>
> ...then the immediate priority is finding outside support, not the recognition framework on this page. The [Crisis Resources Directory](manipulation-hub-crisis-resources.md) lists context-specific options. Cyber Civil Rights Initiative (CCRI) handles non-consensual intimate imagery cases in the US. The Revenge Porn Helpline operates in the UK. SWGfL's Report Harmful Content service handles UK reports. National DV hotlines often have technology safety expertise (Safety Net at NNEDV). Cyberstalking should generally be reported through the same DV hotlines or through specialized cybercrime units in police departments where they exist. **For active stalking with credible threats, the Safety Assessment lethality indicators apply** — the IPV literature on lethality risk generalizes to stalking by current and former partners.
>
> The rest of this page is built on top of this safety frame.

**Read first:**
- [Safety Assessment](manipulation-hub-safety-assessment.md) — the foundational filter, particularly for situations involving stalking, threats, or coordinated harassment.
- [Crisis Resources Directory](manipulation-hub-crisis-resources.md) — for technology safety hotlines, image-based abuse helplines, and cybercrime reporting resources.
- [Intimate Partner Relationships](manipulation-hub-context-intimate-partner-relationships.md) — for the device monitoring and stalking dimensions when the harm-causing party is a current or former partner.

## 1. Why This Page Exists

Online and digital contexts deserve their own page in this sub-hub for two structurally distinct reasons that pull in different directions:

**The first reason:** for many autistic adults, online and digital communication is one of the few contexts in which the patterns this hub treats can be substantially mitigated. Written communication removes prosody from the attack surface. Asynchronous messaging gives time to compose, revise, and check. Sensory environments are largely under the user's control (lighting, noise, pacing). Autistic peer communities exist online in ways they often do not exist locally. Disclosure decisions can be calibrated by audience in ways that face-to-face contexts make harder. Documentation is often automatic (chat logs, email threads, social media posts are preserved by the platforms themselves). Many autistic adults explicitly report that online relationships and online communities are the contexts in which they have experienced the most genuine connection of their lives, sometimes for the first time.

**The second reason:** online and digital contexts have produced their own specific forms of harm that affect autistic adults disproportionately and that the hub has to address explicitly. Cyberstalking, coordinated harassment, doxxing, image-based sexual abuse, online grooming, romance scams, parasocial exploitation, online community manipulation, platform-mediated abuse, and the surveillance dimensions of intimate partner control are all documented in the broader cyberharassment literature and have specific autism-relevant dimensions. The same features that make digital communication accessible for autistic adults also produce specific vulnerabilities that exploiters can target.

This page treats both dimensions honestly. The reframe in one sentence: **online and digital contexts are simultaneously protective and dangerous for autistic adults — protective because they remove some of the friction that other contexts impose, dangerous because they enable specific forms of harm that the hub has to recognize — and the protective work is the work of accessing the protective dimensions while reducing exposure to the dangerous ones.**

The page does not pretend that "stay offline" is viable advice. For many autistic adults, the online dimension is the only context in which they have access to autistic peer support, autism-affirming community, professional networks calibrated for their needs, or the kind of asynchronous communication their cognition works best with. Telling them to disengage from the internet is telling them to give up significant resources that the rest of this hub recommends building. The page is calibrated for the recognition that the protective relationship with digital contexts is "engagement with care" rather than "avoidance."

## 2. Lethality, Cumulative Stakes, and Immediate Safety

### 2.1 Cyberstalking and the connection to physical lethality

Cyberstalking — the use of digital tools to monitor, track, harass, or control another person — is one of the documented pathways to physical violence in intimate partner contexts. The empirical literature on stalking generally (Logan & Walker; Spitzberg & Cupach) documents that stalking is often a precursor to physical assault, and that cyberstalking often co-occurs with physical stalking and physical violence.

**Key findings from the stalking literature:**
- **Approximately 61% of female stalking victims and 44% of male stalking victims** report that the stalker is a current or former intimate partner (NISVS data treated on the [Intimate Partner Relationships](manipulation-hub-context-intimate-partner-relationships.md) page).
- **Cyberstalking specifically** has been documented to predict physical confrontation and physical violence in significant fractions of cases.
- **The lethality indicators from Campbell's Danger Assessment** (treated on the IPR page) apply to stalking contexts as well — strangulation history, weapon access, separation contemplation, escalating threats, and other indicators warrant the same elevated-risk response in stalking contexts.
- **Stalking by ex-partners is particularly dangerous** in the period after separation, which is also the period that the femicide literature identifies as the highest-risk.

**Evidence quality:** [VALIDATED] for the broader stalking literature; [PRELIMINARY-to-MODERATE] for autism-specific cyberstalking prevalence and outcomes.

**What this means in practice:**
- **If you are being cyberstalked by a current or former partner**, the IPR page applies and the Safety Assessment is the foundational filter. Cyberstalking is not a separate, lower-stakes phenomenon; it is often the digital dimension of the same coercive control pattern.
- **DV hotlines have specific technology safety expertise.** The Safety Net project at the National Network to End Domestic Violence (NNEDV) in the US is the most-developed technology safety resource for DV survivors. Equivalents exist in other countries.
- **Document everything.** Screenshots, URLs, timestamps, account information, the full content of messages. The documentation supports both safety planning and (when warranted) legal action.

### 2.2 Image-based sexual abuse

Image-based sexual abuse (IBSA), also called non-consensual intimate imagery (NCII) and sometimes called "revenge porn" in older literature, is the non-consensual creation, distribution, or threatened distribution of intimate images. The category includes:

- **Distribution of images that were created consensually** but shared without consent.
- **Distribution of images that were created non-consensually** (covertly, under coercion, during sexual violence).
- **Threats to distribute such images** as a coercion mechanism.
- **Deepfake imagery** that places the target's face onto sexual content they did not participate in.
- **AI-generated imagery** that creates realistic fake intimate content of the target.

**Key sources:**
- McGlynn, C., Rackley, E., et al. Series of papers on image-based sexual abuse, including the legal frameworks and survivor experiences.
- Cyber Civil Rights Initiative (CCRI) in the US. cybercivilrights.org.
- Revenge Porn Helpline (UK).
- Citron, D. K. (2014). *Hate Crimes in Cyberspace.* Harvard University Press.
- Powell, A., & Henry, N. Series of papers on technology-facilitated sexual violence.

**The autism-specific dimensions:**
- **The credibility discount applies to IBSA reporting** — autistic survivors report being disbelieved or dismissed when they report intimate image abuse, sometimes with the framing that they "should have known better" than to share images in the first place.
- **The grooming-style exploitation** documented in the Friendships and IPR contexts often produces the original images that are later weaponized.
- **The legal landscape is uneven.** IBSA is criminalized in many US states and in the UK, but enforcement is inconsistent and the laws often have gaps that affect specific cases.
- **The mental health impact is severe.** IBSA has been documented to produce trauma responses comparable to other sexual violence and has been linked to suicide in multiple cases.

**What this means in practice:**
- **Specialized helplines exist.** CCRI in the US, Revenge Porn Helpline in the UK, and equivalents elsewhere. These are usually free and confidential.
- **The Take It Down service** (operated by NCMEC for under-18 imagery) and the StopNCII.org service (for adult imagery) provide hash-matching technology that can sometimes prevent distribution.
- **Legal pathways exist** in many jurisdictions, both criminal and civil. Specialized attorneys handle these cases.
- **Recovery includes trauma-informed therapy** with autism awareness, recognition that the harm is real and not the survivor's fault, and the slow rebuilding of safety in digital contexts.

### 2.3 Doxxing and coordinated harassment

Doxxing — the publication of personal information (real name, address, employer, family connections, medical information) intended to expose, intimidate, or enable harm — is a specific form of online harm that has produced documented physical violence and that disproportionately targets disabled, marginalized, and visible community members.

**Coordinated harassment** is the related pattern in which multiple people, sometimes coordinated through specific platforms, target an individual with a sustained campaign of messages, complaints, false reports, public shaming, and other tactics. The campaigns can extend across weeks, months, or years, and can involve hundreds or thousands of participants.

**Sources:**
- Citron, D. K. (2014). *Hate Crimes in Cyberspace.* Harvard University Press.
- Pew Research Center. Multiple surveys on online harassment, including disability-specific findings.
- Plan International, ADL, and other organizations have documented patterns of coordinated harassment against marginalized targets.

**The autism-specific dimensions:**
- **Autistic creators and advocates** are documented targets of coordinated harassment, particularly those who are visible in autism community advocacy, who challenge ableist content, or who participate in autism community discourse on contested topics.
- **The "anti-self-diagnosis" harassment movements** that have emerged in some online autism-related communities specifically target self-identified autistic adults with sustained campaigns.
- **The credibility discount in platform reporting** means that autistic targets of harassment often have their reports dismissed by platform moderation while the harassers' reports against the targets are sometimes acted on.
- **The cumulative cost** of sustained harassment is severe and intersects with the autistic burnout and trauma literatures.

**What this means in practice:**
- **Doxxing is a crime in many jurisdictions** when it involves intent to harm or stalking. Reporting pathways exist, though enforcement is uneven.
- **Tactical Tech, Access Now, and the Electronic Frontier Foundation** have resources on protecting personal information online and on responding to doxxing.
- **Platform reporting is often inadequate** but is sometimes effective. Documenting harassment for both platform reports and legal action is part of the protective work.
- **Block, mute, and disengage strategies** are sometimes the most effective immediate response, even when they feel insufficient against the scale of the harassment.

### 2.4 Romance scams and online financial exploitation

A specific form of online harm that disproportionately affects autistic adults: romance scams and adjacent online financial exploitation patterns. The pattern is documented in consumer protection literature and increasingly in disability-specific advocacy.

**The pattern:**
- A scammer establishes contact with the target through dating apps, social media, or community platforms.
- The relationship develops over weeks or months, with extensive emotional content and the apparent development of trust.
- The scammer introduces financial requests — initially small, gradually escalating — framed as emergencies, business opportunities, or expressions of love.
- The target sends money, sometimes substantial sums, sometimes draining savings.
- The scammer disappears, or continues extracting until the target recognizes the scam.
- The target faces both financial loss and emotional trauma.

**The autism-specific dimensions:**
- **The good-faith assumption** (treated elsewhere in this hub) makes autistic adults more vulnerable to accepting emotional content at face value.
- **The literal interpretation pattern** can lead autistic adults to believe statements that a more skeptical observer would question.
- **The desire for connection** that drives many autistic adults to use online dating and friendship platforms is the same desire the scammer exploits.
- **The credibility discount** in subsequent reporting — to police, family, or banks — sometimes leads autistic survivors to be blamed for "should have known better" rather than supported as victims of crime.
- **Crypto scams and "support" scams** that target the autistic community specifically have been documented.

**Evidence quality:** [VALIDATED] for romance scam prevalence and patterns generally; [PRELIMINARY] for the autism-specific overrepresentation, with documentation in disability advocacy and consumer protection sources.

**What this means in practice:**
- **Romance scams are crimes**, and reporting pathways exist (FBI IC3 in the US, Action Fraud in the UK, equivalents elsewhere).
- **Banks may sometimes recover funds** if the scam is reported quickly, particularly for wire transfers.
- **The shame around being scammed is real and is weaponized** by both the scammer (during the scam) and by the social environment afterward. Recognition that romance scams are sophisticated crimes targeting features the autistic adult cannot easily change is part of the protective frame.

### 2.5 Stalkerware and intimate partner surveillance

A specific intersection with the IPR page: stalkerware and other surveillance tools enable intimate partners (and others) to monitor the autistic adult's digital communications, location, online activity, and device usage. The tools include:

- **Commercial stalkerware applications** marketed for "parental monitoring" or "employee monitoring" but routinely used in intimate partner abuse contexts.
- **Built-in family-sharing features** of operating systems and platforms that can be exploited for surveillance.
- **Account access** (the partner knows passwords or has account credentials).
- **Device access** (the partner has physical access to the autistic adult's phone or computer).
- **Smart home devices** (cameras, voice assistants, smart locks) that the partner controls or accesses.
- **GPS tracking** through phones, vehicles, AirTags, and similar devices.

**Sources:**
- Coalition Against Stalkerware (stopstalkerware.org). Multi-organization initiative documenting stalkerware and providing detection resources.
- Safety Net project at NNEDV in the US.
- Citizen Lab at the University of Toronto, which has published technical analyses of stalkerware.

**The autism-specific dimensions:**
- **The reliance on digital tools** that many autistic adults have for executive function support, communication, calendar management, and daily life logistics means that compromised devices are particularly damaging.
- **The credibility discount** means autistic survivors who report surveillance are sometimes dismissed as paranoid.
- **The technical complexity** of detecting and removing surveillance tools is a barrier for many autistic adults, particularly those without technical support resources.

**What this means in practice:**
- **DV hotlines with technology safety expertise** can sometimes help. NNEDV's Safety Net project is one resource.
- **Citizen Lab and the Coalition Against Stalkerware** have technical resources for detection.
- **Reset and protect approaches** (factory reset, new accounts, new devices, new passwords) are sometimes the most reliable response to confirmed compromise, though they have their own costs.
- **In acute IPV contexts**, removing surveillance tools without leaving evidence of removal can sometimes trigger escalation. Coordinating with a DV advocate before removal is sometimes the protective sequence.

## 3. Why Online and Digital Contexts Are Different for Autistic Readers

### 3.1 The protective dimensions: written, asynchronous, sensory-controllable

Many autistic adults explicitly report that online and digital communication is **easier** than in-person communication. The reasons are structural:

- **Written communication removes prosody from the attack surface.** The Tone Policing and Emotional Invalidation Double Bind patterns rely heavily on prosodic and affect features that do not exist in text. The autistic writer can deliver substantive content without being judged on vocal tone, eye contact, facial expression, or body language.
- **Asynchronous communication gives processing time.** The autistic adult can read, consider, draft, revise, and send without the real-time pressure that face-to-face communication imposes. The time matters substantially for many autistic processing styles.
- **Sensory environments are user-controlled.** The autistic adult can communicate from a quiet room with controlled lighting, comfortable temperature, no perfumed strangers, and the ability to take breaks. The sensory environment of communication, which is one of the largest costs of in-person communication, is largely solved in digital contexts.
- **Editing and revision are possible.** Verbal communication is one-shot; written communication can be reviewed before sending, which catches the kinds of misstatements that the autistic adult might make under cognitive load.
- **The credibility discount is partially reduced.** The features that drive the discount in face-to-face contexts (prosody, eye contact, affect) are absent in text. The discount still operates in some forms (writing style, response patterns) but is generally weaker in text than in voice or video.
- **Documentation is automatic.** Chat logs, email threads, forum posts, and social media interactions are preserved by the platforms themselves. The Documentation strategy that this hub treats as foundational is structurally easier in digital contexts than in face-to-face ones.
- **Disclosure decisions are calibratable.** The autistic adult can control who sees what about them in digital contexts in ways that face-to-face contexts make harder.
- **Autistic peer communities exist online** in ways they often do not exist locally. For autistic adults in geographic areas without local autism community, online community is sometimes the only access to autistic peers.

**Evidence quality:** [PRELIMINARY-to-MODERATE]. The qualitative literature on autistic adults and online communication is convergent in documenting the protective dimensions. The quantitative literature is smaller but consistent.

**The implication:** the autistic adult's preference for digital communication is empirically grounded and is often a protective feature rather than an avoidance pattern. The hub's recommendation throughout (use written communication where possible, build reality-check support, document, calibrate disclosure) is structurally easier in digital contexts.

### 3.2 The dangerous dimensions: context collapse, audience uncertainty, scale

The same features that make digital communication accessible also produce specific vulnerabilities:

- **Context collapse.** Communication that was calibrated for one audience is seen by other audiences with different expectations. A post written for autistic peers can be read by allistic strangers, family members, employers, or harassers. The autistic adult often cannot control which audiences will see what, particularly on public platforms.
- **Audience uncertainty.** The autistic adult often does not know who is reading their content, what their intentions are, or how they will respond. The good-faith assumption that supports many autistic interactions becomes a vulnerability when the audience is unknown.
- **Scale.** Online harassment, doxxing, and pile-on campaigns can involve hundreds or thousands of participants in ways that face-to-face contexts cannot easily produce. The cost of being targeted scales with the platform.
- **Permanence.** Digital content tends to persist. Things written years ago can be discovered, taken out of context, and weaponized in ways that verbal communication is not.
- **Algorithmic amplification.** Platform algorithms sometimes amplify divisive or controversial content, including content targeted at marginalized communities. The autistic adult who becomes the subject of algorithmic amplification can face cumulative attention that is structurally different from the attention they would receive in non-algorithmic contexts.
- **Platform power.** The platforms themselves have substantial power over content visibility, account access, and dispute resolution. Platform decisions are often opaque and unappealable.
- **The intersection with surveillance.** Devices, accounts, and platforms can be monitored by third parties (intimate partners, family members, employers, governments) in ways that face-to-face conversation cannot easily be.

**The implication:** the protective dimensions of digital communication coexist with these dangerous dimensions, and the autistic adult navigating digital contexts needs to be aware of both. The strategies in section 7 are calibrated for the simultaneity.

### 3.3 The autistic community's online concentration and its consequences

A specific structural feature: the autistic adult community is heavily concentrated online. Major autistic organizations have substantial online presences, autistic-led media is largely online, autistic peer support is largely online, and autistic identity-based community formation has happened largely online for the past 15-20 years. The concentration matters in several ways:

- **The protective resource of "autistic peer reality-check" that this hub recommends throughout is often only available online for autistic adults without local autism community.** Disengaging from online contexts means losing access to one of the protective resources the hub has recommended building.
- **The autistic community discourse spaces** (Twitter/X, Reddit, Discord, Mastodon, Bluesky, Tumblr, specific forums and platforms) are where many autistic adults form their understanding of autism, get exposure to autism research and self-advocacy, and develop their community identity. These spaces are valuable and are also subject to the patterns in this hub.
- **Online autism community has its own internal dynamics**, including disputes, hierarchies, popularity dynamics, and (in some cases) the patterns this hub treats operating between autistic community members. The "autistic community as a refuge" frame is mostly true and is also incomplete; some online autism community is itself a context where the patterns operate.
- **The targeted harassment of autistic creators and advocates** (section 2.3) is partly enabled by the concentration — when the community is online, the harassers know where to find their targets.

The implication: the autistic adult's relationship with online autism community is one of the more consequential digital relationships they have. Building protective practices for online community participation is part of the broader protective work this hub treats.

### 3.4 The masking-online-vs-offline question

A specific autism-relevant complication: many autistic adults experience digital communication as a context in which they can mask less than in face-to-face contexts. The reasons are the protective dimensions in section 3.1 — written communication removes the features that masking is most expensive to perform (prosody, eye contact, affect, real-time pacing).

**The implication for protective strategy:** digital contexts are sometimes the venue in which autistic adults can practice unmasking, can experience their communication being received accurately, and can build relationships that do not depend on masking. The protective recommendation throughout this hub to "find environments where your autism is not being weaponized against you" is sometimes most easily met in digital contexts.

**The complication:** the unmasking that is possible in some digital contexts is not possible in all of them, and the same digital context that allows unmasking in one community can require masking in another. The autistic adult navigating multiple digital contexts often manages multiple presentations across them, with the cognitive cost that the masking literature documents.

**The protective strategy:** find the digital contexts in which unmasking is possible and protective, and treat them as recovery resources. Recognize that other digital contexts (professional, family, public) may still require management of presentation. Calibrate your participation accordingly.

### 3.5 The platform-as-institution dimension

Online platforms function as institutions in many of the same ways that workplaces, schools, and social services agencies do. They have:

- **Terms of service** that function as rules of engagement.
- **Moderation systems** that adjudicate disputes between users.
- **Reporting and complaint mechanisms** that sometimes work and often don't.
- **Account access controls** that can be revoked at the platform's discretion.
- **Algorithmic systems** that determine visibility and reach.
- **Data collection and surveillance** capacities that the user has limited control over.
- **Customer service and dispute resolution** that is often inadequate, particularly for marginalized users.

**The autism-specific dimensions:**
- **Platform moderation often applies the credibility discount** to autistic users in disputes. Autistic users' communication features can be misread as harassment, hostility, or rule violation, leading to account suspension or content removal that does not happen to the people they were responding to.
- **Algorithmic systems do not understand autism** and sometimes amplify content in ways that disadvantage autistic creators or that target autistic communities.
- **Reporting mechanisms are often inadequate** for the kinds of harm autistic users disproportionately face — coordinated harassment, doxxing, intimate image abuse, and exploitation patterns.

The implication: the protective response to online manipulation often involves both individual strategies and institutional strategies (platform reporting, escalation to platform leadership, public advocacy, legal action). Disability rights and digital rights organizations sometimes advocate at the platform level on behalf of autistic and other disabled users.

### 3.6 The intersection with all other context pages

A structural feature of digital contexts that distinguishes them from most other context pages: **online and digital contexts intersect with every other context page in this hub.** Specifically:

- **Intimate partner violence** intersects through cyberstalking, stalkerware, monitoring, and the IBSA dimension.
- **Family of origin** intersects through family-shared accounts, family group chats, and family monitoring of autistic adults' online presence.
- **Medical** intersects through patient portals, telehealth, online medical records, and online support communities.
- **Workplace** intersects through workplace digital communication, social media policies, and online presence affecting employment.
- **Educational** intersects through online learning, learning management systems, and digital records.
- **Friendships** intersects through online friendships and the digital dimensions of in-person friendships.
- **Legal** intersects through online evidence in legal proceedings and through cybercrime as a criminal context.
- **Social services** intersects through digital benefits administration and online case management.
- **Religious/community** (the next context page) intersects through online religious communities and digital community management.

The implication: many of the strategies on this page are not novel — they are applications of strategies developed in other context pages to the digital dimension of those contexts. The protective work in digital contexts often borrows directly from the protective work in the underlying context.

## 4. The Patterns Most Common in Online and Digital Contexts

### 4.1 Bandwagon isolation in coordinated harassment

The [Bandwagon Isolation](manipulation-hub-pattern-bandwagon-isolation.md) page treats the broader pattern. In online contexts, the bandwagon is sometimes literal: a coordinated or semi-coordinated group of people targets the autistic person with a sustained campaign that explicitly cites the chorus ("everyone agrees you're wrong," "the entire community sees through you," "look at how many people are calling you out").

**The online-specific feature:** the chorus is sometimes verifiable in ways that offline bandwagon claims are not — the autistic target can see the dozens or hundreds of people participating in the campaign. The verifiability does not make the chorus correct; it makes the experience of being targeted more material and more disorienting.

**The protective response:** disengage from the platform where the harassment is occurring when feasible. Block aggressively. Avoid responding to harassers. Document for legal and platform-reporting purposes. Build reality-check support outside the targeting platform with people who can hold the perception that the harassment is wrong even when it is loud.

### 4.2 DARVO in online dispute resolution

The [DARVO](manipulation-hub-pattern-darvo.md) page treats the broader pattern. In online contexts, DARVO frequently appears in disputes between users, in platform reports, and in public arguments. The harm-causing party Reverses the role: "I'm the one being harassed by this autistic person who can't take a joke."

**The online-specific feature:** platform reporting systems often credit the DARVO Reverse, particularly when the harm-causing party files reports first or in greater volume. Autistic targets of DARVO in online contexts often find their accounts suspended while the harm-causing party's accounts remain active.

**The protective response:** documentation of the original dispute, escalation to higher levels of platform moderation when possible, and recognition that platform decisions are sometimes wrong and not necessarily reflective of which party was actually in the right.

### 4.3 Diagnostic weaponization in autism community discourse

The [Diagnostic Weaponization](manipulation-hub-pattern-diagnostic-weaponization.md) page treats the broader pattern. In online autism community contexts, diagnostic weaponization can appear from multiple directions: from non-autistic harassers ("you're not really autistic, you're just being difficult"), from autism community members in disputes ("you're using your autism as an excuse"), and from "anti-self-diagnosis" voices who target self-identified autistic adults specifically.

**The online-specific feature:** the diagnostic weaponization can be public in ways that offline contexts are not — the autistic adult is sometimes called out publicly for their autism, with the framing distributed to audiences who do not know them.

**The protective response:** treat the disclosure decision in online contexts as audience-dependent (the [Disclosure Decisions](manipulation-hub-strategy-disclosure-decisions.md) page applies), recognize that some online communities are not safe spaces for autism disclosure even when they appear to be, and build reality-check support with autistic peers who do not run the diagnostic-discount script.

### 4.4 Information asymmetry in platform terms and surveillance

The [Information Asymmetry Exploitation](manipulation-hub-pattern-information-asymmetry-exploitation.md) page treats the broader pattern. In online contexts, the information asymmetry includes:

- **Platform terms of service** that the user has agreed to but rarely read.
- **Data collection practices** that the user is largely unaware of.
- **Algorithmic systems** that affect what the user sees and what others see of the user.
- **Surveillance capabilities** that intimate partners, family members, employers, or governments may exercise without the user's knowledge.

**The protective response:** privacy hygiene practices, explicit attention to terms of service for high-stakes platforms, use of privacy-respecting alternatives where feasible, and recognition that complete privacy is not generally available in mainstream online contexts.

### 4.5 The compliance trap in online community moderation

The [Compliance Trap](manipulation-hub-pattern-compliance-trap.md) page treats the broader pattern. In online community contexts, the trap can appear when community moderators or community leaders demand specific kinds of behavior from members that the autistic member cannot easily produce — particular communication norms, particular emotional expression, particular conformity to community discourse styles. The compliance demand is enforced through warnings, content removal, and account actions.

**The protective response:** find online communities where the communication norms match the autistic adult's natural style, recognize that community membership in non-fitting communities has costs, and build participation strategies that protect the autistic adult's resources rather than depleting them.

### 4.6 Concern trolling in online interactions

The [Concern Trolling](manipulation-hub-pattern-concern-trolling.md) page treats the broader pattern. In online contexts, concern trolling is one of the most prevalent forms of disguised harassment. "I'm worried about your mental health" delivered to an autistic creator by a stranger in a social media reply is rarely sincere concern; it is often a passive-aggressive harassment tactic.

**The online-specific feature:** concern trolling is easier to perform online than offline because the troll does not have to maintain the performance face-to-face and can pivot to escalation if the target responds.

**The protective response:** recognize the pattern, do not engage with strangers offering "concern" as the substance of an interaction, and use the unfalsifiability test from the Concern Trolling page.

### 4.7 Other patterns in online contexts

Briefly:
- **[Gaslighting](manipulation-hub-pattern-gaslighting.md)** — particularly common in long-running online disputes where the harm-causing party rewrites the history of the interaction.
- **[Memory Gaslighting](manipulation-hub-pattern-memory-gaslighting.md)** — counter-balanced in online contexts by the automatic documentation that platforms provide. Memory gaslighting in online disputes can sometimes be refuted with the actual chat or post log.
- **[Tone Policing](manipulation-hub-pattern-tone-policing.md)** — pervasive in online discourse, particularly when autistic users are direct or analytical in their communication style.
- **[Pragmatic Packaging Invalidation](manipulation-hub-pattern-pragmatic-packaging-invalidation.md)** — applies to email and platform communication; the institutional version applies to platform reporting.
- **[Performative Allyship](manipulation-hub-pattern-performative-allyship.md)** — particularly common in online identity-based community contexts where allyship credentials can be displayed publicly.
- **[Sensory Gaslighting](manipulation-hub-pattern-sensory-gaslighting.md)** — less directly applicable in digital contexts (the sensory environment is largely user-controlled), but applies to discussions of sensory needs that autistic users have online.
- **[Post-Meltdown Shaming](manipulation-hub-pattern-post-meltdown-shaming.md)** — public meltdowns posted online can be weaponized indefinitely in ways that offline meltdowns sometimes are not.
- **[Weaponized Accommodations](manipulation-hub-pattern-weaponized-accommodations.md)** — community moderation decisions and platform features that were granted as accommodations being cited as the basis for ongoing compliance demands.

## 5. The Most Dangerous Configurations

### 5.1 The cyberstalking + intimate partner + escalation configuration

**Components:** treated in section 2.1 above and on the IPR page. A current or former partner uses digital tools to monitor, track, harass, or control the autistic adult. The cyberstalking co-occurs with physical stalking, threats, and (in worst cases) physical violence. The lethality indicators apply.

**The protective response:** DV hotlines with technology safety expertise; DV advocates trained in stalking cases; legal action including protective orders; coordination of digital safety with the broader safety planning treated on the IPR page.

### 5.2 The doxxing + coordinated harassment + escalation configuration

**Components:** treated in section 2.3. Personal information is published or threatened. A coordinated or semi-coordinated group launches sustained harassment. The harassment extends to the autistic adult's employer, family, friends, and associates. Real-world consequences (job loss, housing harassment, threats to family) follow.

**The protective response:** digital security hardening (account protections, removal of personal information from public sources, privacy services); legal action where the doxxing meets criminal thresholds; platform escalation; documentation; in severe cases, physical safety planning that treats the digital harassment as a precursor to physical risk.

### 5.3 The image-based sexual abuse + distribution + recovery configuration

**Components:** treated in section 2.2. Intimate images of the autistic adult (real or deepfaked) are distributed without consent. The distribution may be to specific audiences (family, employer, social network) or broadly online. The harm is severe and immediate.

**The protective response:** specialized helplines (CCRI in the US, Revenge Porn Helpline in the UK, equivalents elsewhere); hash-matching services (StopNCII.org); legal action (criminal and civil); trauma-informed therapy with autism awareness; recognition that the harm is real and is not the survivor's fault.

### 5.4 The romance scam + financial loss + isolation configuration

**Components:** treated in section 2.4. The autistic adult is targeted by a romance scammer, develops emotional investment in the relationship, sends money over time, and faces both financial loss and emotional trauma when the scam is recognized.

**The protective response:** report to law enforcement (FBI IC3, Action Fraud, equivalent); contact bank to attempt fund recovery; support from victim services; trauma-informed therapy that does not blame the survivor for being scammed; reality-check support that does not include shame or "you should have known better" framings.

## 6. Distinguishing Productive Online Engagement from Manipulation

A specific distinguishing question for online contexts: many of the patterns this hub treats appear in online interactions, but online interactions are also where many autistic adults find their most productive relationships and most valuable communities. The question is not "is this online, therefore suspicious?" but "is this specific online relationship or interaction productive or exploitative?"

The general distinguishing tools from elsewhere in this hub apply:

- The patterns are consistent across multiple interactions, not isolated to a bad day.
- The relationship is asymmetric, with resources flowing in one direction.
- The reality-check from autistic peers reads it as the pattern.
- The substance of concerns is consistently displaced rather than engaged with.
- The pattern co-occurs with other manipulation patterns.
- You feel dread rather than ordinary social friction.
- Your wellbeing is deteriorating in patterns connected to the online relationship.

The signals that shift the probability away from manipulation include:
- The relationships and communities support your wellbeing rather than corrode it.
- You can disagree without losing the relationship.
- You receive accurate reality-check support.
- Your communication features are received with curiosity and care rather than dismissed.

The protective frame: **online engagement is not categorically suspect, and the autistic adult who has built protective relationships online has built something genuinely valuable. The protective work is to distinguish productive from exploitative engagement, not to disengage from digital contexts entirely.**

## 7. Strategies, Sequenced by Priority

### 7.1 First: Digital security hygiene as foundational practice

For all autistic adults using online and digital tools, basic security hygiene reduces risk substantially:

- **Strong, unique passwords** for each account, managed through a password manager.
- **Two-factor authentication** on all important accounts, preferably through an authenticator app rather than SMS.
- **Privacy settings reviewed and configured** on each platform.
- **Personal information minimized** in public profiles.
- **Old accounts deleted** when no longer needed.
- **Email aliases** or separate email addresses for different purposes.
- **Regular backups** of important data.
- **Recognition that no security is perfect**, but reasonable practice substantially reduces casual exploitation.

The Electronic Frontier Foundation's Surveillance Self-Defense guides (ssd.eff.org) are accessible introductions to digital security hygiene calibrated for general users.

### 7.2 Second: For acute online harms, contact specialized helplines

For active cyberstalking, doxxing, image-based sexual abuse, romance scam losses, and similar acute online harms, the priority is contacting specialized resources:

- **NNEDV Safety Net** for technology safety in DV contexts (US).
- **CCRI** for image-based sexual abuse (US).
- **Revenge Porn Helpline** (UK).
- **National DV hotlines** that handle cyberstalking.
- **Cybercrime reporting** through national pathways (FBI IC3 in US, Action Fraud in UK, etc.).
- **Legal aid** for cases that warrant civil or criminal action.

These resources are usually free and confidential and have specific expertise that this page cannot provide.

### 7.3 Third: Document and preserve evidence

For any online harm, documentation is foundational:

- **Screenshots** with timestamps visible and URLs included.
- **Full thread captures** rather than isolated messages.
- **Account information** for the harm-causing party.
- **Platform reports filed** and platform responses recorded.
- **Storage of evidence** in a location the harm-causing party cannot access.
- **Chain of custody** considerations for evidence that may be used in legal proceedings.

The Documentation strategy page applies, with the digital-specific calibration that platforms often preserve evidence automatically and that the autistic adult's job is to capture and back up the evidence before the harm-causing party can delete or alter it.

### 7.4 Fourth: Use platform reporting with appropriate expectations

Platform reporting is sometimes effective and often inadequate. The strategic considerations:

- **File reports promptly** while the harmful content is still active.
- **File complete reports** with all relevant context, even when the platform's reporting form does not seem to require it.
- **Document the platform response** for possible future escalation or legal action.
- **Escalate when the initial response is inadequate.** Many platforms have escalation pathways for serious harms; some can be reached through digital rights organizations.
- **Recognize that platform reporting is not the only response** and is sometimes the least effective response. Use it as one tool among several.

### 7.5 Fifth: Build digital reality-check networks

[Reality-Check Networks](manipulation-hub-strategy-reality-check-networks.md) applies in digital contexts the same way it applies elsewhere, with the digital-specific recognition that online autism community is often the most accessible reality-check resource for autistic adults without local autism community.

Practical components:
- **Identify autistic peers online** whose readings of digital interactions you trust.
- **Build relationships in low-stakes digital contexts** before high-stakes situations arise.
- **Use private messaging or smaller platforms** for reality-check conversations rather than public ones, to reduce exposure to harassment.
- **Recognize that online autistic community has its own internal dynamics**, and that not all online autism community members are appropriate reality-check partners for any given situation.

### 7.6 Sixth: Privacy hygiene against doxxing and surveillance

For autistic adults at elevated risk of doxxing or surveillance — visible advocates, creators, people in active conflict with abusive partners or family, marginalized community members in hostile environments — additional practices reduce risk:

- **Removing personal information from public sources.** Data broker opt-outs, search result removal where feasible, and review of public profiles for information that could enable identification.
- **Address protection.** USPS PO boxes, virtual mailboxes, address confidentiality programs in some US states for DV survivors.
- **Phone protection.** Separate phone numbers for different purposes; voice-over-IP services that do not require disclosing real numbers.
- **Email separation.** Different email addresses for different purposes, with the addresses not linked.
- **Browser hygiene.** Tracker blocking, separate browser profiles for different purposes, or privacy-focused browsers.
- **Device hygiene.** Recognition of the risk of stalkerware on shared devices; periodic security audits; in acute situations, factory reset and new accounts.

The Tactical Tech Holistic Security guide and the Access Now Digital Security Helpline are resources for more advanced security needs.

### 7.7 Seventh: Strategic use of autistic-friendly platforms and communities

Not all platforms are equally autism-friendly. Some are dramatically better than others. Strategic platform selection is part of the protective work:

- **Platforms with robust moderation** that addresses harassment effectively.
- **Smaller, more curated communities** rather than mass-scale public platforms.
- **Mastodon, Bluesky, and other federated or alternative platforms** that often have different community norms.
- **Discord servers and other invite-only spaces** for autistic peer support.
- **Forums and dedicated platforms** for specific autistic interests or identities.

The autistic community has developed specific online spaces that work better than mainstream platforms for many autistic users. Finding them and participating in them is part of the protective work.

### 7.8 Eighth: Recovery from digital trauma

[Recovery and Post-Traumatic Growth](manipulation-hub-strategy-recovery.md) addresses the broader recovery work. Digital trauma is real and is documented in the cyberharassment literature. Recovery includes:

- **Recognition that the harm was real**, even when the platform or the social environment dismissed it.
- **Trauma-informed therapy** with awareness of digital-context trauma, where accessible.
- **Recovery of digital agency** — sometimes through changed practices, sometimes through choosing different platforms, sometimes through periods of reduced engagement.
- **Rebuilding online community** on different terms, when feasible.

The standing recovery frame applies: **the goal is not to disengage from digital contexts. The goal is to engage with digital contexts on terms that protect rather than corrode you.**

## 8. What This Page Cannot Do

- **It cannot provide complete digital security advice.** Security is technical, contextual, and changing. The page provides orientation; the EFF, Tactical Tech, and Access Now provide more comprehensive resources.
- **It cannot guarantee platform reporting will work.** Platforms vary widely and inconsistently in how they respond to reports.
- **It cannot recover lost money from scams.** Some recovery is possible through banks and law enforcement; much is not.
- **It cannot remove all online content about you.** The internet has substantial persistence; even successful removal efforts often leave residual content.
- **It cannot replace specialized helplines.** For active acute harms, the helplines listed in section 7.2 have expertise this page cannot provide.
- **It cannot make harassment campaigns stop.** Sustained harassment is a documented harm whose end depends on factors outside the target's control.
- **It cannot reverse digital trauma quickly.** Recovery is the long arc.
- **It cannot make all platforms autism-friendly.** Platform design and moderation are structural features that individual users cannot change alone.
- **It cannot substitute for legal counsel** in cases involving criminal conduct, civil claims, or threats requiring legal response.

## 9. Key Takeaways

1. **Online and digital contexts are simultaneously protective and dangerous for autistic adults.** Protective because they remove some friction (prosody, real-time pacing, sensory environment, in-person credibility discount); dangerous because they enable specific forms of harm (cyberstalking, IBSA, doxxing, coordinated harassment, romance scams, surveillance).

2. **For many autistic adults, online community is the most accessible source of autistic peer reality-check.** The protective resource the rest of this hub recommends building is often only available digitally. Disengaging from online contexts means losing access to a valuable protective resource.

3. **Cyberstalking is connected to physical lethality** when the stalker is a current or former intimate partner. The lethality indicators from the IPV literature apply. DV hotlines with technology safety expertise are the foundational resource.

4. **Image-based sexual abuse is a specific severe harm** with its own helplines (CCRI, Revenge Porn Helpline) and legal frameworks. The harm has been linked to suicide in multiple cases and warrants treatment as serious sexual violence.

5. **Doxxing and coordinated harassment** are documented forms of harm that disproportionately target marginalized and visible community members, including autistic creators and advocates. Documentation, platform reporting, legal action, and reduced exposure are the protective tools.

6. **Romance scams disproportionately target autistic adults** through the good-faith assumption and the desire for connection. Recognition of the scam patterns is part of the protective work; recovery includes not blaming the survivor for being scammed.

7. **The four most dangerous configurations** are cyberstalking + intimate partner + escalation; doxxing + coordinated harassment + escalation; image-based sexual abuse + distribution + recovery; and romance scam + financial loss + isolation. Each warrants specialized resources.

8. **Digital security hygiene reduces risk substantially.** Strong passwords, two-factor authentication, privacy settings, and basic precautions are accessible and effective. The Electronic Frontier Foundation's Surveillance Self-Defense guides are a good starting resource.

9. **Online engagement is not categorically suspect.** The protective work is to distinguish productive from exploitative engagement, not to disengage from digital contexts entirely. Online communities can be where autistic adults experience their most genuine connection.

10. **The recovery frame is not "go offline."** It is "engage with digital contexts on terms that protect rather than corrode you." Some autistic adults need extended periods of reduced engagement; some need to change platforms or communities; some can engage robustly with appropriate practices; some choose to remain visible despite the costs because the visible work is part of the broader autistic community's reform efforts.

**One last thing.** If you have read all the way through this page and you recognize what the page describes, the most important thing the page can tell you is this: **the protective dimensions of digital contexts are real, and the dangerous dimensions are also real, and your job is not to choose between them but to navigate both**. For many autistic adults, online community is the most genuine connection they have ever experienced. For many autistic adults, online harassment, stalking, and exploitation have caused real harm. Both are true, and neither cancels the other. Practice the digital security hygiene. Use the specialized resources for acute harms. Build the communities that nourish you. Disengage from the platforms that corrode you. The protective work is engagement with care, not avoidance, and the engagement is part of how you live in the world that exists.
