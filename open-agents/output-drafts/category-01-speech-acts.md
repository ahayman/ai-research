## How to Read This Document

This is not a list of rules to memorize. It is a description of a **system** -- one with consistent mechanics, identifiable patterns, and underlying logic. The system governs how English speakers use sentences to *do things*, not just say things. Understanding the system means you can predict what people are doing with their words, even in situations you've never encountered before.

Each section builds on the previous one. If you find a section dense, that's normal -- come back to it after reading the later sections, which often make the earlier concepts click.

## 1. The Core Insight: Every Sentence Does Something

### 1.1 The Revolution in Thinking About Language

For most of the history of philosophy and linguistics, language was treated as a tool for **describing reality**. Sentences were "true" or "false," and that was basically all there was to say about them. "The cat is on the mat" -- true or false? That was the question.

In 1955, the British philosopher J.L. Austin delivered a series of lectures at Harvard that overturned this entire framework. His insight was deceptively simple:

**When you say something, you don't just describe the world. You *do* something in the world.**

When a bride says "I do" at a wedding ceremony, she isn't describing an internal state -- she is *performing the act of marrying*. When a judge says "I sentence you to five years," they aren't reporting a fact -- they are *changing someone's legal status*. When you say "I promise I'll be there," you aren't merely predicting your own behavior -- you are *creating an obligation that didn't exist before*.

Austin called these **speech acts**. His book, published posthumously in 1962, was titled *How to Do Things with Words*. The title says it all.

### 1.2 Why This Matters for Understanding Communication

Here's the key implication: **every time someone speaks to you, they are doing something**. Not just conveying information -- *acting*. They might be:

- Requesting something from you
- Warning you about a danger
- Committing to a future action
- Expressing an emotion
- Changing a social reality
- Some combination of the above

If you only attend to the **information content** of what someone says ("What facts are they communicating?"), you will miss what they are actually **doing**. And what they are doing is often more important than what they are saying.

This is the form-function mismatch that lies at the heart of many pragmatic difficulties. We'll explore it in depth in Section 5.

### 1.3 Austin's Journey: How He Got Here

Austin didn't start with this conclusion. He started by noticing something specific: some sentences can't be true or false at all.

He called these **performatives**:
- "I name this ship the *Queen Elizabeth*." (Not true or false -- it performs the christening.)
- "I bet you sixpence it will rain tomorrow." (Not a description of betting -- it IS the bet.)
- "I give and bequeath my watch to my brother." (In a will -- not reporting a bequest, but making one.)

He contrasted these with **constatives** -- ordinary descriptive sentences that *can* be true or false:
- "The cat is on the mat." (True or false.)
- "It is raining." (True or false.)

But then Austin did something remarkable: he systematically **demolished his own distinction**. He showed that:

1. There's no grammatical test that reliably separates performatives from constatives.
2. Constatives have "success conditions" too -- "All of John's children are bald" fails if John has no children, not by being false, but by presupposing something untrue.
3. Some performatives can be true or false -- "I warn you the bull is about to charge" is a false warning if there's no bull.
4. **Every utterance performs an action.** Even "The cat is on the mat" performs the act of *asserting*, *informing*, or *stating*.

Having proved that the distinction doesn't hold, Austin proposed that we need a completely new framework -- one that treats **all** utterances as actions. That framework is the three-layer architecture.

## 2. The Three Layers of Every Utterance

Austin proposed that when anyone says anything, they simultaneously perform **three** distinct but related acts:

### 2.1 The Locutionary Act: The Act of Saying

The **locutionary act** is simply the act of producing a meaningful sentence. It has three sub-components:

- **Phonetic act**: Making sounds (a parrot can do this)
- **Phatic act**: Making sounds that constitute words in a language (requires grammar)
- **Rhetic act**: Using those words with definite sense and reference (requires meaning)

The locutionary act is what you'd report with: **"She said that..."**

### 2.2 The Illocutionary Act: The Act In Saying

The **illocutionary act** is what the speaker is *doing* by producing the utterance -- its **force** or **function**. This is the core concept of speech act theory.

The same locutionary act (the same words) can carry completely different illocutionary force depending on context:

| Words | Possible Illocutionary Force |
|-------|------------------------------|
| "You will leave this room." | Prediction, threat, promise, order, or statement of fact |
| "It's cold in here." | Observation, complaint, request (to close a window), or warning |
| "I'll be there at five." | Promise, prediction, information, or reassurance |

You report illocutionary acts with: **"She warned / promised / requested / ordered..."**

The illocutionary act is:
- **Conventional**: It depends on recognized social conventions
- **Under the speaker's control**: If conditions are right, performing the act is within the speaker's power
- **Can be made explicit**: "I promise...", "I warn you...", "I order you..."

### 2.3 The Perlocutionary Act: The Act By Saying

The **perlocutionary act** is the **actual effect** on the listener -- what happens *as a result of* the utterance.

The perlocutionary act is:
- **NOT conventional**: It depends on the listener's psychology, the relationship, the circumstances
- **NOT under the speaker's control**: You can try to persuade, but you can't guarantee persuasion
- **NOT performable by formula**: You cannot say "I hereby convince you" -- convincing must actually happen

**The "I hereby" test** cleanly separates illocutionary from perlocutionary:
- "I hereby **promise**..." -- Works. Promise is illocutionary.
- "I hereby **warn** you..." -- Works. Warning is illocutionary.
- "I hereby **persuade** you..." -- Doesn't work. Persuasion is perlocutionary.
- "I hereby **intimidate** you..." -- Doesn't work. Intimidation is perlocutionary.

### 2.4 Worked Examples: All Three Layers

**"There is a bull in the field."**

| Layer | What Happens |
|-------|-------------|
| Locutionary | Speaker produces a meaningful English sentence about a bull's location. |
| Illocutionary | Speaker performs the act of **warning** the listener about danger. |
| Perlocutionary | Listener is **alarmed** and decides not to enter the field. |

**"Can you pass the salt?"**

| Layer | What Happens |
|-------|-------------|
| Locutionary | Speaker produces an interrogative sentence about the listener's ability. |
| Illocutionary | Speaker performs the act of **requesting** the salt. (NOT asking about ability.) |
| Perlocutionary | Listener **passes the salt**. |

**"I promise I'll pay you back on Friday."**

| Layer | What Happens |
|-------|-------------|
| Locutionary | Speaker produces a sentence about future repayment. |
| Illocutionary | Speaker performs the act of **promising** -- creating an obligation. |
| Perlocutionary | Listener is **reassured** about the money (or **skeptical**, based on experience). |

Note that the perlocutionary effect is the only layer that can "fail" -- the promise is made regardless of whether the listener believes it. This distinction matters enormously for understanding social dynamics.

## 3. The Five Types of Speech Acts

John Searle, Austin's student and intellectual successor, systematized and refined Austin's work into a rigorous taxonomy. He identified five fundamental categories of illocutionary acts, distinguished by three primary criteria:

1. **Illocutionary point**: What is the act fundamentally trying to accomplish?
2. **Direction of fit**: What is the relationship between the words and the world?
3. **Sincerity condition**: What psychological state must the speaker have?

### 3.1 Direction of Fit: The Organizing Principle

Before we look at the five types, we need to understand Searle's most important concept -- **direction of fit**. This describes whether the words are supposed to match the world, or the world is supposed to match the words.

| Direction | Meaning | Analogy | If They Don't Match... |
|-----------|---------|---------|----------------------|
| **Word-to-world** | Words must match reality | A map must match the terrain | The words are wrong (false) |
| **World-to-word** | Reality must come to match the words | A shopping list tells you what to buy | The world hasn't caught up yet |
| **Null** | No fit required | A sigh of relief | The state of affairs is presupposed |
| **Double** | Both simultaneously | A referee's call | The words create the reality they describe |

### 3.2 Assertives: Committing to Truth

**Point**: To represent the world as being a certain way.
**Direction of fit**: Word-to-world (the words must match reality).
**Sincerity condition**: The speaker **believes** the proposition is true.

Assertives are the backbone of information exchange. When you perform an assertive, you put yourself "on record" as believing something. You can be held accountable for its truth.

**Examples (from weakest to strongest commitment):**
- Guessing: "I'd guess it's about 3 PM."
- Suggesting: "Maybe the problem is in the wiring."
- Hypothesizing: "The virus may have originated in a wet market."
- Stating: "The meeting starts at 3 PM."
- Claiming: "This land belongs to my family."
- Insisting: "I'm telling you, he was there at eight."
- Swearing: "I swear I saw it happen."

**Social function**: Assertives build shared knowledge. The social cost of failed assertives is loss of credibility. Lies are violations of the sincerity condition -- the speaker doesn't believe what they're asserting.

**Pattern to recognize**: Assertives answer the question "What is the case?" They describe, report, explain, predict, or claim.

### 3.3 Directives: Getting Someone to Do Something

**Point**: To get the listener to do something.
**Direction of fit**: World-to-word (the world must change to match the words).
**Sincerity condition**: The speaker **wants** the listener to do the action.

Directives are the speech act category most sensitive to social relationships. The same illocutionary point (getting someone to close a window) takes radically different forms depending on the social context:

**Examples (from weakest to strongest pressure):**
- Hinting: "It's a bit drafty in here, isn't it?"
- Suggesting: "Maybe we could try the Italian place."
- Requesting: "Could you please pass the salt?"
- Asking: "What time does the train leave?"
- Advising: "You should see a doctor about that."
- Urging: "I really think you need to talk to her."
- Warning: "Don't touch that -- it's hot."
- Insisting: "You need to leave now."
- Commanding: "Stand at attention!"
- Ordering: "Report to my office immediately."

**Important**: Searle classified **questions** as a type of directive. This is initially counterintuitive but logically consistent: a question is an attempt to get the listener to *do something* -- specifically, to provide information. "What time is it?" is a directive whose desired action is an assertive (a statement of the time).

**Social function**: Directives coordinate human action. They are where **power dynamics** (see Category 22) become most visible. Using too strong a directive with an equal or superior is perceived as rude or domineering; too weak a directive with a subordinate may seem uncertain or unclear.

**Pattern to recognize**: Directives answer the question "What should happen?" They request, command, ask, invite, advise, suggest, warn, or dare.

### 3.4 Commissives: Committing to Future Action

**Point**: To commit the speaker to a future course of action.
**Direction of fit**: World-to-word (the world must change via the speaker's future actions).
**Sincerity condition**: The speaker **intends** to do the action.

Commissives and directives share the same direction of fit (world-to-word), but the critical difference is **who acts**: directives put the burden on the listener; commissives put it on the speaker.

**Examples:**
- Promising: "I promise I'll pick you up at the airport."
- Threatening: "If you don't stop, I'll call the police."
- Offering: "I can drive you to the station if you like."
- Vowing: "I vow to protect and defend the Constitution."
- Guaranteeing: "I guarantee it will last five years."
- Refusing: "No, I won't attend the meeting." (A negative commissive -- committing to NOT doing something.)
- Volunteering: "I'll take care of the dishes."
- Betting: "I bet you ten dollars it rains."

**Social function**: Commissives are the foundation of trust. Promises create expectations; fulfilling them builds reputation; breaking them destroys it. Human societies invest enormous effort in enforcing commissives, from informal social pressure ("You said you would!") to contract law.

**The promise-threat distinction**: Both are commissives (speaker commits to future action). The difference is whose interest the action serves. Promises benefit the listener; threats harm them. Both create social obligations, but of different kinds.

**Pattern to recognize**: Commissives answer the question "What will I do?" They promise, threaten, offer, guarantee, vow, pledge, refuse, or volunteer.

### 3.5 Expressives: Expressing Psychological States

**Point**: To express the speaker's feelings about a state of affairs.
**Direction of fit**: Null (no fit -- the state of affairs is presupposed true by both parties).
**Sincerity condition**: The speaker **feels** the specified emotion (gratitude, regret, pleasure, etc.).

Expressives are unique: they don't try to describe the world (like assertives) or change it (like directives and commissives). They express an attitude about something both parties already accept as true.

**Examples:**
- Thanking: "Thank you for helping me move."
- Apologizing: "I'm sorry I forgot your birthday."
- Congratulating: "Congratulations on the promotion!"
- Welcoming: "Welcome to our home."
- Condoling: "I'm so sorry for your loss."
- Complaining: "This traffic is unbearable."
- Praising: "You did an excellent job on this."
- Deploring: "It's terrible what happened."

**Social function**: Expressives maintain social relationships. They signal that you share the appropriate emotional response to events. Failing to produce expected expressives (not saying "thank you," not congratulating, not expressing sympathy) is a significant social breach -- not because information is lost, but because the social bond is weakened.

**Why this can be confusing**: Expressives are often **expected** by social convention even when the sincerity condition isn't fully met. You're expected to say "I'm sorry for your loss" even if you didn't know the person who died. You're expected to say "thank you" even for minor things. The performative function (maintaining social bonds) can be more important than the sincerity condition (actually feeling the emotion). This is one of the areas where the autistic preference for authenticity collides with neurotypical social convention.

**Pattern to recognize**: Expressives answer the question "How do I feel about this?" They thank, apologize, congratulate, welcome, condole, complain, or praise.

### 3.6 Declarations: Changing Reality by Speaking

**Point**: To bring about a change in the world simply by saying so.
**Direction of fit**: Double (the words both create and describe the new reality simultaneously).
**Sincerity condition**: None required (a judge doesn't need to "feel" a sentence to impose it).

Declarations are the most remarkable speech acts. They are the only ones that **create the very reality they describe**. But they only work if the speaker has the **institutional authority** to make them:

**Examples:**
- "I now pronounce you married." (Requires officiant authority)
- "You're fired." (Requires employer authority)
- "I sentence you to ten years." (Requires judicial authority)
- "I declare this meeting adjourned." (Requires chairperson authority)
- "I resign." (Requires being the person in the role)
- "War is hereby declared." (Requires governmental authority)
- "I christen this ship the *Adventurer*." (Requires the designated role)
- "You're out!" (Requires umpire authority)

**The institutional requirement**: This is what makes declarations unique. A random person on the street saying "I pronounce you married" to a passing couple changes nothing. The same words spoken by an authorized officiant during a ceremony change legal reality. The words are identical; the institutional framework makes the difference.

**Assertive declarations**: Some speech acts combine assertive and declarative properties. A referee calling "You're out!" is simultaneously asserting a fact about the play AND creating a new game state. A jury foreperson saying "We find the defendant guilty" is both reporting the jury's conclusion AND changing the defendant's legal status.

**Pattern to recognize**: Declarations answer the question "What is now the case because I said so?" They require institutional authority and bring about the state of affairs they name.

### 3.7 Summary: The Five Types at a Glance

| Type | Point | Direction of Fit | Sincerity | Key Question | Examples |
|------|-------|-----------------|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Assertive** | Represent the world | Word → World | Belief | "What is the case?" | State, claim, predict, report |
| **Directive** | Get listener to act | World → Word | Want | "What should happen?" | Request, command, ask, advise |
| **Commissive** | Commit speaker to act | World → Word | Intention | "What will I do?" | Promise, threaten, offer, vow |
| **Expressive** | Express feelings | Null | Feeling | "How do I feel?" | Thank, apologize, congratulate |
| **Declaration** | Change reality | Double | None required | "What is now true?" | Fire, sentence, declare, resign |

## 4. When Speech Acts Succeed or Fail: Felicity Conditions

Speech acts aren't evaluated as "true" or "false." They're evaluated as **felicitous** (successful) or **infelicitous** (failed). Austin introduced the concept of **felicity conditions** -- the conditions that must hold for a speech act to come off successfully.

### 4.1 The Four Types of Conditions (Searle's Framework)

For any given speech act, four types of conditions must be met:

**1. Propositional Content Condition** -- What the words must be *about*:
- A promise must refer to a **future act by the speaker** (not a past act, not someone else's act)
- An apology must refer to a **past act by the speaker**
- A warning must refer to a **future event not in the listener's interest**

**2. Preparatory Conditions** -- What must be true about the situation:
- A command requires the speaker to have **authority** over the listener
- A question requires that the speaker **doesn't already know** the answer
- A promise requires that the listener would **prefer** the act to its absence

**3. Sincerity Condition** -- What the speaker must actually feel/believe/intend:
- A promise requires the speaker to **intend** to follow through
- An assertion requires the speaker to **believe** the proposition
- An apology requires the speaker to **feel regret**

**4. Essential Condition** -- What the act **counts as**:
- A promise counts as **undertaking an obligation**
- A request counts as **an attempt to get the listener to act**
- An assertion counts as **committing to truth**

### 4.2 Worked Example: Felicity Conditions for a Promise

**"I promise I'll pick you up from the airport on Tuesday."**

| Condition | What Must Hold | Violation Example |
|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Propositional content** | Refers to a future act by the speaker | "I promise I picked you up yesterday" -- nonsensical |
| **Preparatory** | Listener prefers being picked up; it's not obvious speaker would do it anyway | Listener has said they don't want a ride -- promise is unwanted |
| **Sincerity** | Speaker genuinely intends to show up | Speaker has no intention of coming -- insincere promise |
| **Essential** | Counts as undertaking an obligation | This is what makes it a promise, not just a prediction |

### 4.3 What Happens When Conditions Are Violated

The social consequences of violating felicity conditions differ dramatically depending on **which type** is violated:

**Procedure violations (preparatory/propositional) = the act NEVER HAPPENED:**
- A random person says "You're fired!" to someone in a coffee shop. Nothing happens. There's no employment relationship. The speech act misfires -- it's null and void. Reactions range from confusion to amusement.
- A child says "I sentence you to prison!" during play. No one goes to prison. The institutional conditions aren't met.

**Sincerity violations = the act HAPPENED but is HOLLOW:**
- Someone says "I promise I'll help you move" with no intention of showing up. The promise IS made -- the speaker is now socially accountable for it. But it's an insincere promise. If discovered, this produces feelings of **betrayal** and **broken trust**, which are far more socially damaging than a misfired act.
- Someone says "I'm sorry" without feeling any regret. The apology is issued, but it's hollow. The listener may sense the insincerity.

**This distinction matters enormously:** Misfires produce confusion or amusement. Insincerities produce feelings of betrayal. Understanding the difference helps explain why people react so differently to different types of speech act failure.

### 4.4 Austin's Taxonomy of Failure

Austin mapped out the full landscape of how speech acts can go wrong:

```
INFELICITIES
|
|-- MISFIRES (the act is void -- it never happened)
|   |-- Misinvocations (wrong procedure)
|   |   |-- Non-plays: No such procedure exists
|   |   |-- Misapplications: Procedure exists but wrong persons/circumstances
|   |-- Misexecutions (procedure botched)
|       |-- Flaws: Executed incorrectly
|       |-- Hitches: Not completed
|
|-- ABUSES (the act happened but is defective)
    |-- Insincerities: Required feelings/intentions are absent
    |-- Non-fulfillments: Required follow-through doesn't happen
```

**Examples:**
- **Non-play**: "I hereby divorce you" spoken in a jurisdiction where verbal divorce has no legal standing. No procedure exists for this.
- **Misapplication**: A bystander at a wedding says "I now pronounce you married." The procedure exists, but this person can't invoke it.
- **Flaw**: A judge says "I sentence you to five years in the federal penitentiary" for a misdemeanor with a maximum sentence of one year. The sentencing procedure exists and the judge has authority, but the execution is incorrect.
- **Hitch**: A witness takes an oath but is interrupted before completing it. The oath is incomplete.
- **Insincerity**: "I promise to return your book tomorrow" -- the speaker has no intention of returning it.
- **Non-fulfillment**: "I promise to return your book tomorrow" -- the speaker sincerely intended to but simply doesn't do it.

## 5. The Form-Function Mismatch: Why the Same Words Do Different Things

This section addresses the single most consequential aspect of speech act theory for autistic learners.

### 5.1 The Core Problem

English has three basic sentence types, each with a "default" function:

| Sentence Form | Default Function |
|---------------|-----------------|
| Declarative ("The door is open.") | Making a statement |
| Interrogative ("Is the door open?") | Asking a question |
| Imperative ("Open the door.") | Issuing a command |

If language worked straightforwardly, form would always match function. Declaratives would always be statements. Interrogatives would always be questions. Imperatives would always be commands.

**Language does not work this way.**

In actual use, the form of a sentence frequently does not match its function:

| What's Said (Form) | What's Done (Function) | The Mismatch |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| "Can you pass the salt?" (interrogative) | Requesting the salt (directive) | Question form, request function |
| "I notice the report isn't finished." (declarative) | Criticizing/requesting completion (directive) | Statement form, directive function |
| "Would you like to close the door?" (interrogative) | Telling you to close the door (directive) | Question form, command function |
| "That's an interesting choice." (declarative) | Expressing disapproval (expressive) | Statement form, criticism function |
| "How kind of you to finally join us." (exclamative) | Expressing irritation at lateness (expressive) | Praise form, criticism function |
| "I was wondering if you might possibly..." (declarative) | Making a request (directive) | Self-report form, request function |

### 5.2 Why This Mismatch Exists

The form-function mismatch isn't random. It exists for specific, systematic reasons:

**Reason 1: Politeness and face-saving.**
Direct speech acts ("Close the door") can threaten the listener's autonomy (what politeness theory calls "negative face"). Dressing up a directive as a question ("Could you close the door?") makes it easier for the listener to refuse without either party losing face. The question form provides a social escape hatch. (See Category 3: Politeness Theory.)

**Reason 2: Plausible deniability.**
If you say "It's cold in here" and your host takes offense at the implication that they should turn up the heat, you can retreat behind the literal meaning: "I was just commenting on the temperature." Indirect speech acts create a layer of **deniability** that protects both parties.

**Reason 3: Power dynamics.**
Lower-status speakers are expected to use more indirect forms when addressing higher-status people. "I was wondering if perhaps you might consider possibly extending the deadline" is a request from a subordinate. "Extend the deadline" is a request from a superior. Same illocutionary point; different forms, determined by hierarchy. (See Category 22: Social Hierarchy.)

**Reason 4: Social convention has frozen certain patterns.**
"Can you pass the salt?" is technically a question about ability, but it has been used as a request so many millions of times that it has essentially become an **idiom for requesting**. The literal meaning is still there, but the conventional indirect meaning is primary.

### 5.3 Two Types of Indirect Speech Acts

**Conventional indirect speech acts** are formulaic patterns that have become so common they function almost like idioms. They don't require much (or any) social inference to interpret correctly:

| Pattern | Form | Function |
|---------|------|----------|
| "Can you...?" / "Could you...?" | Ability question | Request |
| "Would you mind...?" | Willingness question | Request |
| "I was wondering if..." | Self-report | Request |
| "Do you have the time?" | Yes/no question | Request for information |
| "Why don't you...?" | Negative question | Suggestion |
| "I'd like to..." | Want statement | Request/order |
| "Would it be possible to...?" | Possibility question | Request |
| "Do you think you could...?" | Opinion question | Request |
| "If you could just..." | Conditional | Polite command |
| "I don't suppose you could...?" | Negative supposition | Very polite request |

**This is the good news for autistic learners**: Research consistently shows that autistic individuals process conventional indirect speech acts **relatively normally**. These are learnable patterns -- once you know that "Can you...?" means "Please do...," you can apply the pattern reliably. The conventional forms are essentially vocabulary to be learned, not social puzzles to be solved.

**Non-conventional indirect speech acts** (hints, insinuations) are where the difficulty increases dramatically. These require inferring the speaker's intention from context:

| What's Said | What's Meant | How You'd Know |
|-------------|-------------|----------------|
| "It's cold in here." | "Close the window / turn up the heat." | Context: window is open, speaker seems uncomfortable |
| "That's an interesting choice." | "I disapprove of your choice." | Tone, context, the word "interesting" used where a positive adjective was expected |
| "I notice the dishes are still in the sink." | "Do the dishes." | Context: it's your turn, the speaker has noted this before |
| "Nice car. Would be a shame if something happened to it." | Threat | Context: speaker has power over you, menacing tone |
| "Well, you certainly tried your best." | "You failed, but I'm trying to be kind." | Emphasis on "tried," damning with faint praise |

**This is where the research shows the greatest difficulty for autistic individuals**, and the difficulty maps specifically to the requirement for **speaker intention modeling** (figuring out what the speaker *believes* and *wants*, not just what they *said*).

### 5.4 How Listeners Derive Indirect Meaning

Searle proposed a step-by-step inferential chain:

1. Listener hears the literal meaning
2. Listener notices the literal meaning seems **irrelevant or trivially obvious** in context (at dinner, everyone can obviously pass the salt -- why ask about ability?)
3. Listener assumes the speaker is **cooperating** (Grice's Cooperative Principle -- see Category 2)
4. Listener recognizes that the utterance references a **felicity condition** of a related speech act (the ability to pass the salt is a preparatory condition for requesting someone to pass it)
5. Listener infers the **intended primary speech act** (a request)

There's a debate about whether listeners actually go through these steps or jump directly to the intended meaning. Research suggests that for **conventional** indirect speech acts, listeners access the indirect meaning **directly** (no literal-first processing). For **novel or non-conventional** indirect speech acts, some literal processing occurs.

**For autistic learners**: The five-step process isn't just an academic model -- it can be used as a **conscious strategy**. When someone says something that seems irrelevant or obviously true, ask: "Why would a cooperating person say something so obvious? What could they actually be trying to accomplish?" This converts social intuition into a systematic reasoning process.

## 6. Speech Act Sequences: How Conversations Are Built

Speech acts rarely occur in isolation. They chain together in predictable sequences with specific expectations at each step.

### 6.1 Adjacency Pairs and Conditional Relevance

Conversations are built from **adjacency pairs** -- two-part exchanges where the first part creates an expectation for the second:

| First Pair Part | Expected Second Pair Part |
|----------------|--------------------------|
| Question | Answer |
| Greeting | Greeting |
| Offer | Accept / Decline |
| Request | Grant / Refuse |
| Invitation | Accept / Decline |
| Complaint | Apology / Justification |
| Compliment | Acceptance / Deflection |

When someone produces the first pair part, the second part becomes **conditionally relevant** -- it is expected. If it doesn't come, its absence is **noticeable and meaningful**. Silence after "How are you?" is not neutral -- it communicates something (reluctance, distraction, or social rupture).

### 6.2 Preferred and Dispreferred Responses

Every first pair part has a **preferred** response and a **dispreferred** response. This is not about what the speaker wants to hear -- it's a structural property of conversation.

**Preferred responses** are:
- Quick (less than 200ms gap)
- Simple and direct
- Short
- No hedging or preface markers

**Dispreferred responses** are:
- Delayed (median ~561ms -- noticeably longer)
- Preceded by markers: "well...", "uh...", "um...", in-breath
- Padded with hedging, appreciation, apology
- Accompanied by an account (reason/excuse)
- Longer and more complex

**This is one of the most reliable signals in English conversation**: If you hear a pause, an in-breath, "well...", or "uh..." before someone responds, a dispreferred response is coming -- refusal, disagreement, bad news, or decline.

**Example of preferred (acceptance):**
> "Want to come to dinner?" → "Sure!" (immediate, simple, no hedging)

**Example of dispreferred (refusal):**
> "Want to come to dinner?" → "Oh... [pause] I'd love to, but [appreciation] I actually have my sister visiting [account/excuse]. I'm really sorry [apology]. Maybe next week? [alternative]"

**Why this matters for autistic learners**: When you need to give a dispreferred response (saying no, disagreeing), the expected pattern is: **delay + appreciation + account + apology + alternative**. Giving a bare "No" without this padding is interpreted as hostile or rude -- not because "no" is wrong, but because the expected structural padding is missing.

Conversely, when **receiving** a dispreferred response, recognizing the pattern helps you understand what's happening. If someone says "Well... that's an interesting idea, but..." you can recognize the dispreferred structure and know that a "no" is coming, even before they get to it.

### 6.3 Pre-Sequences

Before performing major speech acts, speakers often use **pre-sequences** -- preliminary moves that test the waters:

| Pre-Sequence | Purpose | What It Sounds Like |
|-------------|---------|-------------------|
| **Pre-request** | Check availability before requesting | "Are you busy right now?" |
| **Pre-invitation** | Check availability before inviting | "Are you free Saturday?" |
| **Pre-announcement** | Prepare listener for news | "Guess what?" / "You won't believe this." |
| **Pre-closing** | Signal conversation is ending | "Well, I should probably get going..." |
| **Pre-offer** | Test whether help is needed | "Do you need a hand with that?" |

**Why pre-sequences exist**: They protect face. If you ask "Are you free Saturday?" and the person says "No, I'm busy," you can simply not issue the invitation. Neither party has to deal with the social discomfort of an explicit refusal. The pre-sequence lets the listener head off a dispreferred response before it's needed.

**Responding to pre-sequences**: The listener has three options:
1. **Go-ahead**: "Yeah, I'm free!" (Signals: proceed with your invitation)
2. **Hedge**: "It depends... why?" (Signals: tell me more before I commit)
3. **Block**: "Actually, I'm packed this weekend." (Signals: don't bother asking)

### 6.4 Common Sequences Mapped Out

**The Request Sequence:**
```
Pre-request ("Are you busy?")
  → Go-ahead ("No, what's up?")
    → Request ("Could you help me move this weekend?")
      → [PREFERRED] Grant ("Sure, what time?")
        → Thanks ("Thanks, I really appreciate it.")
      → [DISPREFERRED] Refuse
        → Delay + Appreciation + Account + Apology + Alternative
          ("Oh... I would, but I have my daughter's tournament.
           I'm really sorry. Maybe Sunday morning?")
        → Acceptance of refusal ("No worries, I understand.")
```

**The Invitation Sequence:**
```
Pre-invitation ("Are you free Saturday?")
  → Go-ahead ("I think so, why?")
    → Invitation ("We're having a barbecue, want to come?")
      → [PREFERRED] Accept ("Sounds great! What can I bring?")
      → [DISPREFERRED] Decline
        → Appreciation + Account + Reciprocal Interest
          ("That sounds lovely, but we have family in town.
           Another time for sure!")
```

**The Complaint-Apology Sequence:**
```
Complaint ("You said you'd be here at three. It's four thirty.")
  → [PREFERRED] Apology + Account + Remedy
    ("I'm so sorry. Traffic was a nightmare. Let me make it
     up to you -- dinner's on me.")
  → [DISPREFERRED] Justification / Denial
    ("Well, I said 'around' three. And you know the highway
     has been terrible lately.")
    → Escalation or Resolution
```

### 6.5 Speech Act Rituals

Some speech act sequences have become so ritualized that deviation is noticed:

**The "Thank You" Ritual:**

| Era/Group | Response to "Thank you" | Connotation |
|-----------|------------------------|-------------|
| Traditional | "You're welcome" | Formal, complete |
| Younger generations | "No problem" | Casual, implies ease |
| Service contexts | "Of course" | Implies natural obligation |
| Close friends | "Don't mention it" | Minimizing |
| Very casual | "No worries" / "Sure thing" | Low-key |

**"Sorry" as Phatic vs. Real Apology:**

There are at least two distinct speech acts both performed by the word "sorry":

| Context | Function | Example |
|---------|----------|---------|
| Bumping into a stranger | Phatic -- social lubricant, not real apology | "Oh, sorry!" (said quickly while moving) |
| Forgetting someone's birthday | Real apology -- expressing genuine regret | "I'm sorry I forgot. I feel terrible about it." |
| Hearing bad news | Expressive -- conveying sympathy | "I'm sorry to hear about your father." |
| Getting someone's attention | Pre-sequence marker | "Sorry, do you know where the station is?" |

Autistic individuals sometimes struggle with the first type -- using "sorry" as social lubrication when no actual fault exists feels inauthentic. Understanding that this "sorry" is performing a *different speech act* (phatic/ritual, not apology) can help: it's not claiming fault, it's maintaining the social channel.

## 7. Everyday Scripts: Speech Acts in Common Scenarios

This section maps the expected speech act sequences in everyday situations. These are not rigid scripts -- real conversations are more flexible -- but they represent the **default expectations** that neurotypical speakers carry. Deviating from these patterns without signaling why creates friction.

### 7.1 Ordering at a Restaurant

```
Phase 1: Arrival
  Host: "Hi, how many?" (Question/directive)
  You: "Two, please." (Answer + politeness marker)
  Host: "Right this way." (Offer)

Phase 2: Seating
  Server: "Hi, I'm Alex. I'll be taking care of you today.
           Can I start you off with something to drink?"
           (Greeting + self-introduction + offer)
  You: "Could I get a water, please?" (Indirect request)
  Server: "Of course." (Grant)

Phase 3: Ordering
  Server: "Ready to order?" (Pre-request)
  You: "Yes, I'll have the salmon." (Assertive + directive)
  Server: "Great choice." (Expressive -- may be formulaic)

Phase 4: Check
  You: "Could we get the check?" (Indirect request)
  Server: "Sure thing." (Grant)

Phase 5: Departure
  Server: "Have a great evening!" (Expressive)
  You: "Thanks, you too!" (Expressive + reciprocal)
```

### 7.2 Asking a Stranger for Help

```
Phase 1: Attention-getting + Apology for intrusion
  "Excuse me, sorry to bother you..." (Pre-request + politeness)

Phase 2: Establish shared ground
  "Do you know this area?" (Pre-request -- testing capability)

Phase 3: Actual request
  "I'm looking for the train station. Could you point me
   in the right direction?" (Account + indirect request)

Phase 4: Response
  "Sure, it's two blocks that way." (Grant + information)

Phase 5: Thanks
  "Thank you so much, I appreciate it." (Expressive)
  "No problem!" (Minimizing ritual response)
```

### 7.3 Running Into an Acquaintance

```
Phase 1: Recognition
  "Oh hey! [Name]! How are you?" (Greeting + phatic question)
  "Good, good! What are you up to?" (Phatic response + phatic question)

Phase 2: Brief exchange
  [1-3 minutes of catching up -- weather, work, family]
  (Mixed assertives and phatic exchanges)

Phase 3: Pre-closing
  "Well, it was great running into you!" (Pre-closing signal)
  "Yeah, we should catch up sometime." (Formulaic commissive --
   may or may not be sincere)

Phase 4: Closing
  "Definitely! Take care." (Agreement ritual + farewell)
  "You too! See you around." (Reciprocal farewell)
```

**Note**: "We should catch up sometime" is one of the most confusing speech acts for autistic individuals. It LOOKS like a commissive (commitment to future action) but often functions as a **phatic closing ritual** -- a warm way to end the encounter without any actual commitment. Whether it's sincere depends on tone, relationship history, and whether either party follows up. There is no reliable way to tell in the moment.

## 8. Speech Acts and the Autistic Experience

### 8.1 What the Research Actually Shows

The standard narrative -- "autistic people can't understand speech acts" -- is wrong. The research reveals a much more nuanced picture:

**What autistic individuals generally handle well:**
- Conventional indirect speech acts ("Can you...?" as request) -- these are **learned patterns**, not social puzzles
- Scalar implicatures ("some" meaning "not all") -- recent research finds minimal differences
- Direct speech acts of all types
- Understanding explicit performatives ("I promise," "I apologize")

**Where genuine difficulty exists:**
- Non-conventional indirect speech acts (hints, insinuations) -- these require **modeling the speaker's mental state**
- Irony and sarcasm -- these require understanding that the speaker **believes the opposite** of what they're saying
- Phatic and ritual speech acts -- these seem "empty" when you don't understand their social function
- Dispreferred response structure -- the expected padding (delay, hedge, account) is a learned convention

**The critical finding (Frost et al., 2024):** In autistic adults, **autism diagnosis itself did not predict indirect request interpretation**. What predicted performance was working memory and emotion recognition. This suggests the difficulty is with **processing demands**, not a categorical inability.

**The conventional/non-conventional distinction (Kissine et al., 2012, 2015; Deliens et al., 2018):** Autistic individuals correctly interpret conventionalized indirect speech acts. The difficulty is specifically with non-conventionalized forms that require Theory of Mind. This means the gap is **narrower and more specific** than the stereotype suggests.

### 8.2 The Double Empathy Perspective

Damian Milton's double empathy problem (2012) fundamentally reframes speech act difficulties:

**The old frame**: Autistic people fail to produce and understand normal speech acts.
**The new frame**: Autistic and neurotypical people use **different speech act norms**, and misunderstanding is **bidirectional**.

Evidence:
- **Crompton et al. (2020)**: Autistic people share information with other autistic people **as effectively as** neurotypical people do with each other. In mixed groups, transfer drops. The communication system works; the **mismatch** is the problem.
- **Nature Human Behaviour (2025)**: Large-scale replication confirming bidirectional communication advantage with same-neurotype partners.
- **Geelhand et al. (2025)**: Autistic adults change their verbal behavior specifically in mixed-neurotype interactions, not in autistic-autistic conversations.

### 8.3 Autistic Communication as a Valid Speech Act System

Research on autistic communication values (Sutherland et al., 2024) reveals a coherent system with its own norms:

| Autistic Communication Value | Corresponding Speech Act Norm |
|-----|------|
| **Directness** | Using direct speech acts (assertives, directives) without hedging or indirection |
| **Honesty/Authenticity** | Sincerity conditions are treated as non-negotiable -- if you don't feel sorry, don't say "sorry" |
| **Information focus** | Prioritizing assertives (sharing accurate information) over phatic communication |
| **Purposeful interaction** | Each exchange should accomplish something concrete |
| **Respect through clarity** | Showing respect by being clear, not by being indirect |

From this perspective, autistic directness is not a "failed attempt at indirect speech" -- it's a **different set of pragmatic priorities**. The goal of this learning hub is not to replace autistic communication norms but to make the neurotypical system **legible** so that autistic individuals can navigate it when they choose to.

### 8.4 Echolalia as Speech Act Production

A crucial insight: echolalia (repeating heard language), present in 75-80% of verbal autistic individuals, often **performs speech act functions**.

Prizant & Duchan (1981) analyzed over 1,000 echolalic utterances and found seven functional categories, including turn-taking, declarative statements, yes/no answers, requests, labeling, and directives. What looks like "meaningless repetition" from outside may be **performing speech acts using stored linguistic units**.

This connects to **gestalt language processing** -- the idea that some individuals acquire language as whole phrases first, then break them into parts. A child who says "comesitdownatthetable" as a single unit may be performing the speech act of directing/requesting using a stored formula. The speech act function is present; the surface form is non-standard.

### 8.5 The Masking Cost

Research on camouflaging (Hull et al., 2017, PMC5509825) documents the cognitive cost of performing neurotypical speech acts:

- Autistic individuals report **consciously applying learned rules** for speech act production
- This includes monitoring indirect speech act conventions, producing expected expressive speech acts (thanking, congratulating), and managing dispreferred response structures
- The cognitive load of this conscious monitoring is significant and contributes to burnout
- This is why the goal should be **understanding** rather than **performance** -- giving autistic individuals the knowledge to choose when and how to deploy these patterns, not the obligation to always use them.

## 9. Pattern Recognition: A Practical Guide

This section offers practical tools for identifying what speech act someone is performing. These are **pattern recognition heuristics**, not rules -- they work most of the time but have exceptions.

### 9.1 Identifying Speech Act Type by Form

| If You Hear... | It's Likely... | Unless... |
|----------------|---------------|-----------|
| "I promise / I swear / I guarantee..." | Commissive | It's said sarcastically ("I promise I don't care") |
| "Could you / Would you / Can you..." | Directive (request) | It's genuinely asking about ability |
| "I'm sorry / I apologize..." | Expressive (apology) | It's phatic ("Sorry, what was that?") |
| "Thank you / I appreciate..." | Expressive (thanks) | It's sarcastic ("Thanks a lot!") |
| "You need to / You have to / You must..." | Directive (command/insistence) | It's advice ("You have to try this restaurant") |
| "I think / I believe / It seems..." | Assertive (hedged) | It's a polite directive ("I think you should leave") |
| "I'll / I will / I'm going to..." | Commissive | It's a prediction about the future |
| "Congratulations / Well done / Good job..." | Expressive (praise) | It's sarcastic |

### 9.2 Recognizing Indirect Speech Acts

**The Relevance Test**: If someone says something that seems **obviously true** or **irrelevant** in context, they're probably performing an indirect speech act.

- At dinner, "Can you pass the salt?" -- Obviously you can. So it must be a request.
- In a cold room, "It's cold in here." -- Everyone can feel it. So it must be a request or complaint.
- After a mistake, "Well, that was clever." -- It obviously wasn't. So it must be sarcasm.

**The Felicity Condition Test**: Indirect speech acts often work by **referencing the felicity conditions** of the real intended act:

| What They Say | Which Felicity Condition It References | What They Mean |
|---------------|---------------------------------------|---------------|
| "Can you close the door?" | Preparatory condition (ability) for requesting | "Please close the door." |
| "I want you to close the door." | Sincerity condition (desire) for requesting | "Close the door." |
| "Would you close the door?" | Propositional content of a request | "Close the door." |
| "The door is open." | Reason condition for a request | "Close the door." |

### 9.3 Recognizing Dispreferred Responses

**Signals that a "no" is coming:**
1. Pause before responding (> 0.5 seconds)
2. In-breath
3. "Well..." / "Uh..." / "Um..."
4. Token agreement: "Yeah, but..."
5. Appreciation: "That's a great idea, but..."
6. Account/excuse: "The thing is..."

If you hear these signals, prepare for a refusal, disagreement, or decline -- even before the person gets to the actual "no."

### 9.4 When You're Unsure

When you genuinely can't tell what speech act someone is performing, you have several options:

1. **Ask for clarification**: "Sorry, are you asking me to do something or just letting me know?" (This is direct but functional.)
2. **Respond to both interpretations**: "Yeah, it is cold in here. Want me to close the window?" (Treats the utterance as both an assertion and a possible request.)
3. **Wait for more context**: Sometimes the next utterance clarifies the first.
4. **Default to the most likely interpretation given the context**: At dinner, treat ambiguous utterances as requests. In a meeting, treat ambiguous utterances as assertions.

## 10. Key Takeaways

1. **Every utterance is an action.** Words don't just describe -- they request, promise, warn, apologize, declare, and more.

2. **Form doesn't always match function.** "Can you pass the salt?" is a request, not a question. Learning the common form-function mismatches is the most practical thing you can do.

3. **Conventional indirect speech acts are learnable patterns.** "Can you...?" = request. "Would you mind...?" = request. "I was wondering if..." = request. These are essentially vocabulary items.

4. **Non-conventional indirect speech acts (hints) require context.** These are harder, but the Relevance Test helps: if something seems obviously true or irrelevant, look for the hidden speech act.

5. **Conversations have structure.** Adjacency pairs, preferred/dispreferred responses, pre-sequences -- these are predictable patterns you can learn to recognize.

6. **Your directness is a valid communication style.** Autistic communication norms prioritize clarity, honesty, and purpose. The neurotypical system prioritizes face-saving, indirectness, and social bonding. Neither is wrong. But knowing both gives you choices.

7. **When in doubt, look at what the words are trying to accomplish** -- not what they literally say. The question "What is this person trying to DO?" will almost always point you toward the right interpretation.
