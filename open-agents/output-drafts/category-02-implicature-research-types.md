## 1. What Implicature IS: The Said/Implicated Distinction

### 1.1 The Core Insight

Human communication routinely conveys more than what words literally mean. The philosopher H. P. Grice (1975), in his seminal paper "Logic and Conversation," formalized this observation by distinguishing between **what is said** (the semantic content of an utterance—what the words literally mean) and **what is implicated** (the additional meaning the speaker intends to convey beyond the literal content).

Grice's key insight: the **total signification** of an utterance—everything the speaker means—is not exhausted by what is said. It includes what is implicated as well. According to Grice, what a speaker means by an utterance can be divided into what the speaker "says" and what the speaker thereby "implicates."

**Example:**
- A says: "Is there anywhere I can get some gas?"
- B says: "There's a gas station around the corner."
- **What B said** (literal): There exists a gas station located around the corner.
- **What B implicated**: The gas station is open, has gas available, and you can get gas there.

B did not *say* the station was open. But the only way B's response is cooperative (relevant to A's need) is if B believes the station can actually provide gas. That additional meaning is an **implicature**.

### 1.2 Grice's Technical Terminology: Saying, Implying, and Implicating

Grice was deliberately precise about his terminology. He introduced the technical term **"implicature"** and its cognates **"to implicate"** and **"implicatum"** (the thing implicated) because everyday English was too vague. As he put it, "'Implicature' is a blanket word to avoid having to make choices between words like 'imply', 'suggest', 'indicate', and 'mean'."

The three key terms:

1. **Saying** — What is said is closely tied to the conventional (dictionary) meaning of the words uttered. Grice used "say" in a somewhat technical sense: what you *say* is roughly the truth-conditional content of your utterance—the proposition that determines whether your sentence is true or false. Grice described saying as performing a "central" or "direct" speech act.

2. **Implicating** — What is implicated goes beyond what is said. It is meaning that the speaker intends to convey and that the hearer can work out, but that is not part of the literal content. Grice described implicating as performing a "non-central" or "indirect" speech act.

3. **Implying** (everyday English) — Grice avoided this everyday term precisely because it is ambiguous. In ordinary English, "imply" can mean many things: suggest, hint, insinuate, indicate, entail. Grice replaced it with "implicate" to create a cleaner theoretical framework.

**Critical distinction:** You can *say* something true while *implicating* something false, or *say* something false while *implicating* something true (as in irony). What is said and what is implicated are independent dimensions of meaning.

### 1.3 The Structure of Speaker Meaning

Grice's framework divides the total meaning of an utterance hierarchically:

```
SPEAKER MEANING (total signification)
├── WHAT IS SAID (truth-conditional content / semantic meaning)
│   └── Determined by: conventional word meaning + context (reference, disambiguation)
│
└── WHAT IS IMPLICATED (non-truth-conditional meaning)
    ├── CONVENTIONAL IMPLICATURE (attached to specific words)
    └── CONVERSATIONAL IMPLICATURE (derived from context + Cooperative Principle)
        ├── Generalized (arise without special context)
        └── Particularized (require specific context)
```

This taxonomy is the backbone of implicature theory. Everything that follows in this document elaborates on these categories.

## 2. The Taxonomy of Implicature

### 2.1 Conventional Implicature

#### 2.1.1 Definition

Conventional implicatures are meanings that are attached to specific words or constructions as part of their conventional (dictionary-level) meaning, but that do not contribute to the truth conditions of the sentence. They are implicated—not said—but they are triggered by the words themselves, not by the conversational context.

Grice introduced conventional implicature with his classic example: **"He is an Englishman; he is, therefore, brave."** The word *therefore* implicates that his bravery follows from (is a consequence of) his being English. But this causal claim is not part of what is strictly *said*. The sentence would be true if the man were both English and brave, even if his bravery had nothing to do with his nationality. Using this sentence while disbelieving that bravery follows from Englishness would be misleading, but not technically a *lie*—because the causal connection is implicated, not said.

#### 2.1.2 Key Properties of Conventional Implicature

Conventional implicatures differ from conversational implicatures in several critical ways:

| Property | Conventional Implicature | Conversational Implicature |
|---|---|---|
| **Source** | Attached to specific words/constructions | Derived from context + Cooperative Principle |
| **Cancellable?** | **No** — Cannot be cancelled without contradiction | **Yes** — Can be explicitly cancelled |
| **Calculable?** | **No** — Must be learned word-by-word | **Yes** — Can be worked out step by step |
| **Context-dependent?** | **No** — Arises regardless of context | **Yes** — Depends on conversational situation |
| **Detachable?** | **Yes** — Can be removed by choosing a different word | **No** — Same content in different words generates same implicature |
| **Truth-conditional?** | **No** — Does not affect whether the sentence is true or false | **No** — Does not affect truth conditions |

The non-cancellability is the most important diagnostic. You cannot say "She is poor but happy, and there's no contrast between those ideas" without creating a contradiction. The word *but* **commits** you to the contrast. Conversational implicatures, by contrast, can always be explicitly cancelled.

#### 2.1.3 Conventional Implicature Trigger Words: Detailed Examples

**"But" — Implicates contrast/unexpectedness:**

- "Donovan is poor **but** happy."
  - **What is said**: Donovan is poor AND happy. (Logically equivalent to "Donovan is poor and happy.")
  - **What is implicated**: There is a contrast between being poor and being happy—being happy is unexpected or surprising given the poverty.
  - **Test**: "Donovan is poor but happy, and there's nothing surprising about that combination" → sounds contradictory. The contrast is part of what *but* means.

- "She studied hard **but** failed the exam."
  - **What is said**: She studied hard AND failed.
  - **What is implicated**: Failing was unexpected given the hard study. The *but* signals that these two facts are in tension.

- "He's a politician **but** he's honest."
  - **What is said**: He's a politician AND he's honest.
  - **What is implicated**: Honesty is surprising or unexpected in a politician.

**"Even" — Implicates unexpectedness/extreme case on a scale:**

- "**Even** John passed the exam."
  - **What is said**: John passed the exam.
  - **What is implicated**: (1) John was the *least likely* person to pass. (2) Other people also passed. (3) John's passing is surprising.
  - The word *even* ranks John at the bottom of an implicit scale of likelihood.

- "**Even** Ken knows it's unethical."
  - **What is said**: Ken knows it's unethical.
  - **What is implicated**: Ken is the least likely person to know this. If *even Ken* knows, then surely everyone else does too.

- "She can solve **even** the hardest problems."
  - **What is said**: She can solve the hardest problems.
  - **What is implicated**: The hardest problems represent the extreme end of a difficulty scale. She can solve everything up to and including the most difficult.

**"Therefore" / "so" — Implicates causal or logical connection:**

- "He is an Englishman; he is, **therefore**, brave."
  - **What is said**: He is English. He is brave.
  - **What is implicated**: His bravery is a consequence of his Englishness.

- "It rained; **therefore**, the match was cancelled."
  - **What is said**: It rained. The match was cancelled.
  - **What is implicated**: The rain caused the cancellation.

**"Yet" / "still" — Implicates persistence despite expectation of change:**

- "She's 90 years old and **still** runs marathons."
  - **What is said**: She's 90 and runs marathons.
  - **What is implicated**: Running marathons at 90 is contrary to what would be expected—the expectation would be that she would have stopped.

- "He hasn't finished **yet**."
  - **What is said**: He has not finished.
  - **What is implicated**: There is an expectation that he will finish (eventually). The non-completion is temporary.

**"Although" / "however" / "nevertheless" — Implicates concession:**

- "**Although** she was tired, she finished the race."
  - **What is said**: She was tired. She finished the race.
  - **What is implicated**: Her tiredness was a reason NOT to finish, yet she finished despite it. There is a concessive relationship.

**"Moreover" / "furthermore" — Implicates additive strengthening:**

- "The restaurant was expensive. **Moreover**, the food was terrible."
  - **What is said**: The restaurant was expensive. The food was terrible.
  - **What is implicated**: The second point adds to and reinforces the first. The complaint is building.

**Other conventional implicature triggers:** Words like *too*, *also*, *again*, *already*, *stop*, *start*, *manage to*, *know*, and *regret* also carry conventional implicatures (e.g., *regret* implicates that the regretted thing actually happened; *stop* implicates that the activity was previously occurring).

### 2.2 Conversational Implicature

Conversational implicatures are meanings that arise not from the words themselves but from the interaction between what is said, the context of the conversation, and the assumption that speakers are being cooperative. They are the central innovation of Grice's framework.

#### 2.2.1 Generalized Conversational Implicature (GCI)

**Definition:** Generalized conversational implicatures arise by default, without needing any special or particular context. They are associated with certain linguistic forms and arise in *most* normal contexts. They are "presumptive meanings" (Levinson's term)—default interpretations that hold unless something in the context cancels them.

##### Scalar Implicature: The Core Mechanism

The most important and best-studied type of generalized conversational implicature is **scalar implicature**. The mechanism works as follows:

**The Principle:** If a speaker uses a *weaker* term from an ordered scale of alternatives, the hearer infers that the speaker was not in a position to use any *stronger* term from the same scale. Using the weaker term implicates the negation of all stronger terms.

**Why it works:** The Maxim of Quantity says "Make your contribution as informative as is required." If a stronger, more informative term were true and available, the speaker should have used it. The fact that they used the weaker term implicates that the stronger term does not hold (as far as the speaker knows).

##### Horn Scales: The Framework

**Horn scales** (named after Laurence Horn, who first defined them in 1972) are conventionalized scales of lexical items ordered by informativity/strength. They are written with the weaker term first and the stronger term last, enclosed in angle brackets.

The fundamental principle: Using the weaker (left) member of a Horn scale implicates the negation of the stronger (right) member.

**Major Horn Scales with Detailed Examples:**

**Quantifiers: ⟨some, most, all⟩**
- "**Some** students passed the exam."
  - **What is said**: At least some students passed (logically compatible with *all* having passed).
  - **What is implicated**: Not all students passed. (If all had passed, the speaker should have said "all"—using "some" implicates the stronger term doesn't apply.)
  - **Cancellation test**: "Some students passed—in fact, **all** of them did." → Perfectly coherent. This proves it's an implicature, not part of the literal meaning.

- "**Most** of the work is done."
  - **What is implicated**: Not all of the work is done.

**Logical connectives: ⟨or, and⟩**
- "You can have cake **or** ice cream."
  - **What is said**: You can have cake or ice cream (logically, inclusive *or* allows both).
  - **What is implicated**: Not both. You can have one or the other, but not cake AND ice cream.
  - This is why "or" is typically interpreted as exclusive or in everyday conversation, even though logical *or* is inclusive.

**Modals: ⟨possible/may, necessary/must⟩**
- "It's **possible** that she'll come."
  - **What is said**: Her coming is possible.
  - **What is implicated**: It's not certain/necessary that she'll come. (If the speaker knew she was definitely coming, they should have said "she will come" or "she must come.")

- "You **may** leave."
  - **What is implicated**: You are not required to leave (you don't *have to*).

**Adjectives (degree): ⟨warm, hot⟩; ⟨good, excellent⟩; ⟨like, love⟩**
- "The soup is **warm**."
  - **What is implicated**: The soup is not hot. (If it were hot, the speaker should have said so—"hot" is more informative on the temperature scale.)

- "The essay was **good**."
  - **What is implicated**: The essay was not excellent. It was good but not outstanding.

- "I **like** her."
  - **What is implicated**: I don't love her. "Like" is the weaker term on the affection scale.

**Verbs of cognition: ⟨believe, know⟩; ⟨think, be certain⟩**
- "I **believe** she's coming."
  - **What is implicated**: I don't *know* for certain she's coming. (If I knew, I should have said "I know.")

- "I **think** he's right."
  - **What is implicated**: I'm not certain he's right.

**Frequency: ⟨sometimes, often, always⟩**
- "He **sometimes** helps with the dishes."
  - **What is implicated**: He doesn't always help. (If he always did, the speaker should have said "always.")

- "She **often** goes running."
  - **What is implicated**: She doesn't always go running.

**Cardinals/Numbers: ⟨1, 2, 3, ...⟩**
- "She has **three** children."
  - **What is implicated**: She has exactly three—not four, not five. (If she had more, the speaker should have given the higher number.)

##### Other Generalized Conversational Implicatures

**Indefinite articles and "not known to the speaker":**
- "Peter is meeting **a woman** this evening."
  - **What is said**: Peter is meeting some woman (female person).
  - **What is implicated**: The woman is not Peter's wife, girlfriend, mother, or someone the speaker could more specifically identify. The indefinite article "a" generates a default implicature that the referent is not closely related to or identifiable by the speaker.
  - If Peter were meeting his wife, the speaker would normally say "Peter is meeting **his wife** this evening"—the Maxim of Quantity demands sufficient informativeness.

- "I saw **a man** leaving the building."
  - **What is implicated**: The man is not someone known to or identifiable by the speaker. (If it were the speaker's neighbor or friend, they would normally have said so.)

**Possessive constructions:**
- "I broke **a finger** yesterday."
  - **What is implicated (generalized)**: It was *my* finger. (Default interpretation in most contexts.)

#### 2.2.2 Particularized Conversational Implicature (PCI)

**Definition:** Particularized conversational implicatures depend heavily on the specific context of the utterance. Unlike generalized implicatures, they do not arise by default—they require specific background knowledge, the particular situation, and the specific conversational purpose to be derived. Particularized implicatures are by far the more common type of conversational implicature.

**Key characteristic:** The same sentence could have completely different implicatures (or none at all) in different contexts. The implicature is tied to *this particular* conversation.

##### Worked Examples

**Example 1: The Movie Review (flouting Relation/Relevance)**
- A: "How was the movie?"
- B: "Well, the popcorn was good."
- **What B said**: The popcorn was good.
- **What B implicated**: The movie was NOT good. B avoided commenting on the movie itself and instead commented on something peripheral. If the movie had been good, B would have said so (Maxim of Quantity). The fact that B changed the subject to popcorn (apparently violating Relevance) implicates that B cannot say anything positive about the movie.
- **Why it's particularized**: This implicature only works in the context of A having asked about the movie. In a different context ("How was the snack bar?"), the same utterance would carry no negative implicature about the movie.

**Example 2: The Letter of Recommendation (flouting Quantity)**
- A philosophy professor writes a recommendation letter for a job candidate that says, in its entirety: "Dear Sir, Mr. X's command of English is excellent, and his attendance at tutorials has been regular. Yours, etc."
- **What is said**: Mr. X writes good English and attends tutorials regularly.
- **What is implicated**: Mr. X is NOT a good philosophy candidate. The letter is devastatingly negative without saying anything negative. A recommendation letter for a philosophy position is *expected* to discuss philosophical ability, research quality, originality of thought, etc. By saying only that the candidate writes well and shows up, the writer ostentatiously says TOO LITTLE (flouting Quantity), implicating that there is nothing positive to say about the candidate's philosophical abilities.
- This is the classic case of **"damning with faint praise."**

**Example 3: The Gas Station (exploiting Relation)**
- A: "I'm out of gas."
- B: "There's a gas station around the corner."
- **What B said**: A gas station exists around the corner.
- **What B implicated**: The gas station is open, operational, and has gas available. B's contribution is only relevant to A's problem if the gas station can actually help.
- **Why it's particularized**: If A had said "I'm looking for an example of commercial architecture," B's same utterance would implicate nothing about whether the station has gas.

**Example 4: The Dog and the Roast Beef (exploiting Relation)**
- A: "Where's the roast beef I left on the counter?"
- B: "The dog is looking very happy."
- **What B said**: The dog appears happy.
- **What B implicated**: The dog ate the roast beef. B's utterance seems irrelevant to A's question unless there is a connection between the missing roast beef and the dog's happiness.

**Example 5: The Rain Response (exploiting Relation)**
- A: "Want to play tennis?"
- B: "It's raining."
- **What B said**: Rain is occurring.
- **What B implicated**: No, I don't want to play (or: we can't play), because tennis is an outdoor activity and rain prevents outdoor activities. The utterance appears irrelevant unless the hearer connects rain → can't play outdoors → declining the invitation.

**Example 6: The Shellfish Response**
- A: "Want to grab sushi tonight?"
- B: "I'm allergic to shellfish."
- **What B said**: I have a shellfish allergy.
- **What B implicated**: No, I'd rather not go to a sushi restaurant, because shellfish is likely present and could affect me. The relevance of the allergy to the dinner invitation is what creates the implicature.

**Example 7: War is War (context-dependent meaning)**
- "War is war."
- **What is said**: A tautology—trivially true, completely uninformative.
- **What is implicated**: Depends entirely on context. Could mean "terrible things happen in war and we shouldn't be surprised" or "we must accept the harsh realities" or "there's no point in complaining about wartime conditions." The same tautological sentence can carry very different particularized implicatures depending on the conversational situation.

**Example 8: The Birthday Cake**
- A: "Did you bake the cake yourself?"
- B: "I had to work late."
- **What B said**: B worked late.
- **What B implicated**: No, B did not bake the cake. Working late prevented it. The relevance of working late to the question about baking is what generates the implicature.

## 3. Properties of Conversational Implicature

### How to Tell Something Is an Implicature (Not Literal Meaning)

Grice identified several distinguishing properties of conversational implicatures. These serve as **diagnostic tests**—if a meaning has these properties, it is likely an implicature rather than part of what is said.

### 3.1 Calculable

**Definition:** A conversational implicature must be capable of being *worked out* through a chain of reasoning. It is not a mysterious intuition—there must be a step-by-step rational path from what was said to what was implicated.

**What the hearer uses to calculate an implicature:**
1. The conventional (literal) meaning of the words used
2. The Cooperative Principle and its maxims
3. The context of the utterance (who is speaking, where, when, why)
4. Background knowledge shared by speaker and hearer
5. The assumption that all of the above are available to both participants

**Example calculation:**
- A asks: "Can you tell me the time?"
- B responds: "Well, the milkman has come."
- **Calculation:** B's response appears to violate Relevance (it doesn't directly answer the question about time). But I assume B is being cooperative. B's response can only be relevant if there is a connection between the milkman's arrival and the time. I know that the milkman comes at a regular time (say, around 8 AM). Therefore, B is implicating that it is approximately 8 AM or shortly after.

**Why this matters for autistic individuals:** Calculability means implicatures are NOT random or arbitrary. They follow a logical process. While neurotypical individuals often perform this calculation automatically and unconsciously, it can be made explicit and systematic. The reasoning is predictable once you know the principles.

### 3.2 Cancellable (Defeasible)

**Definition:** A conversational implicature can be explicitly cancelled without creating a logical contradiction. The speaker can add a clause that negates the implicature, and the result is coherent (even if slightly unusual).

This is perhaps the single most important diagnostic test for implicature.

**Explicit cancellation examples:**
- "**Some** of the students passed the exam—in fact, **all** of them did."
  → The scalar implicature (not all) is cancelled. Perfectly coherent.

- "I **sometimes** lie. In fact, I **always** do."
  → The scalar implicature (not always) is cancelled.

- "**Some** Dutch men are above average height for Dutch men. In fact, **all** of them are."
  → Scalar implicature cancelled. Coherent.

- "Daniel was born, lived a long and happy life, and died—but not in that order."
  → The temporal ordering implicature (generated by Manner: be orderly) is cancelled.

- "I **believe** she's right—in fact, I **know** she is."
  → The scalar implicature (I don't know for certain) is cancelled.

**Contextual cancellation:** An implicature can also be cancelled by the context rather than by an explicit statement. For example, "Some students passed" normally implicates "not all," but in a context where the speaker is listing minimum requirements ("At least some students must pass for the program to continue—and some students passed"), the "not all" implicature may not arise at all.

**Why cancellability matters:** If a meaning CANNOT be cancelled, it is probably not an implicature—it is either an entailment or a conventional implicature.

- "She managed to finish" → implicates difficulty. Try cancelling: "She managed to finish, and it wasn't difficult at all" → sounds contradictory. This suggests the "difficulty" component might be a conventional implicature or an entailment of *manage*, not a conversational implicature.

### 3.3 Non-Detachable

**Definition:** Conversational implicatures are attached to the *content* (what is said), not to the *specific words* used to say it. If you express the same content using different words, the same implicature arises.

**Example:**
- "There is a gas station around the corner" → implicates: it's open/operational.
- "A fuel station is located nearby" → **same implicature**: it's open/operational.
- "You can buy gas just around the bend" → **same implicature**: it's available.

The wording changed, but the implicature persists, because it derives from the *content* of what was said in context, not from any particular word.

**Contrast with conventional implicature:** Conventional implicatures ARE detachable. You can remove them by choosing different words:
- "She is poor **but** happy" → implicates contrast.
- "She is poor **and** happy" → NO contrast implicature. Same content, different word, implicature disappears.

**Important exception:** Implicatures arising from flouting the Maxim of **Manner** (which concerns *how* something is said, not *what* is said) can be detachable, because they depend on the form of expression rather than the content.

### 3.4 Non-Conventional

**Definition:** Conversational implicatures are not part of the conventional (dictionary) meaning of any word. They arise from the USE of language in context, not from the MEANING of the words themselves.

**Example:** The word "some" does not *mean* "not all." Its literal meaning is "at least one" or "a portion of." The "not all" inference is a conversational implicature that arises from the pragmatic reasoning about why the speaker didn't use "all." This is why the implicature can be cancelled ("Some, in fact all...").

**Contrast:** The contrast meaning of "but" IS conventional—it's part of what the word "but" means. You learn it when you learn the word.

### 3.5 Indeterminate

**Definition:** The specific content of a conversational implicature may be somewhat vague or uncertain. There may be multiple possible implicatures, and the exact intended meaning may not be fully precise.

**Example:**
- A: "How's the new job?"
- B: "Well, the commute is short."

B is probably implicating something negative about the job itself (flouting Quantity by saying too little about the actual job). But what exactly? That the work is boring? The boss is terrible? The pay is low? The implicature is that the job is bad in SOME way, but the exact nature of the badness is indeterminate.

**Why this matters for autistic individuals:** Indeterminacy is a genuine feature of implicature, not a failure of understanding. Even neurotypical speakers sometimes disagree about the exact implicature of an utterance. It is reasonable to ask for clarification when the implicature is ambiguous.

## 4. How Implicature Is Generated: The Step-by-Step Reasoning Process

### 4.1 The General Schema

Grice provided a general schema for how hearers calculate (work out) conversational implicatures. The process follows a logical chain:

**Step 1: The speaker says something (p).**
The hearer processes the literal meaning.

**Step 2: The hearer notices an apparent problem.**
What was said appears to violate one of the conversational maxims—it seems too little, too much, irrelevant, unclear, or false.

**Step 3: The hearer assumes the speaker is still cooperating.**
Despite the apparent violation, the hearer does not abandon the assumption that the speaker is following the Cooperative Principle. The hearer assumes the speaker is a rational, cooperative conversational partner.

**Step 4: The hearer searches for an interpretation that restores cooperation.**
The hearer asks: "What would the speaker have to be thinking/meaning in order for this utterance to be consistent with the Cooperative Principle?" The hearer looks for an additional proposition (q) that, if assumed to be part of what the speaker meant, would make the utterance cooperative after all.

**Step 5: The interpretation that preserves cooperation IS the implicature.**
The proposition q—the thing the speaker must be thinking/meaning to be cooperative—is the conversational implicature.

**Step 6: The speaker knows (and knows the hearer knows) that the hearer can work this out.**
The implicature depends on mutual knowledge: the speaker intended the hearer to derive this interpretation, and the speaker knows the hearer is capable of doing so.

### 4.2 Worked Example of the Full Process

**Situation:** A and B are coworkers. A job applicant has just left an interview.
- A: "What did you think of that candidate?"
- B: "She had a very firm handshake."

**Step-by-step calculation:**

1. **B said:** The candidate had a firm handshake.

2. **Problem detected:** A asked for B's overall opinion of the candidate. B's response addresses only the handshake—a minor detail. This appears to violate the **Maxim of Quantity** (not enough information) and possibly **Relation** (not directly relevant to the evaluation).

3. **Assume cooperation:** B is still trying to be a helpful conversational partner. B is not randomly commenting about handshakes.

4. **Search for interpretation:** Why would B mention ONLY the handshake? In a context where B is expected to evaluate the candidate's qualifications, B's failure to mention anything substantive implicates that B has nothing positive to say about the candidate's actual qualifications. If the candidate had been impressive, B would have said so—the Maxim of Quantity demands it. The only interpretation consistent with cooperation is that B is tactfully indicating a negative assessment without saying anything overtly negative.

5. **The implicature:** B thinks the candidate was poor. The only positive thing B could find to mention was the handshake.

6. **Mutual knowledge:** Both A and B understand the conventions of job candidate evaluation. Both know that a recommendation focusing only on peripheral qualities is a negative signal.

### 4.3 The Different Triggers for Implicature

Implicatures can be triggered by apparent violations of ANY of the four maxims:

**Apparent violation of Quantity (too much or too little):**
- Saying too little → implicates that there's nothing more positive to say
- Saying too much → implicates emphasis, concern, or defensiveness

**Apparent violation of Quality (saying something false or unsupported):**
- Triggers irony, sarcasm, metaphor, hyperbole, understatement

**Apparent violation of Relation (saying something seemingly irrelevant):**
- Triggers the hearer to search for relevance, finding the implicit connection

**Apparent violation of Manner (being obscure, ambiguous, or disorderly):**
- Triggers the hearer to look for a reason behind the unusual expression

## 5. Flouting vs. Violating: The Critical Distinction

### 5.1 The Core Difference

This is one of the most important distinctions in all of pragmatics:

**FLOUTING:** The speaker **obviously and deliberately** breaks a maxim. Both speaker and hearer recognize the breach. The hearer, assuming the speaker is still cooperative, searches for an implicature. Flouting **generates** implicature. It is communicatively productive—it is how speakers say more than their words literally express.

**VIOLATING:** The speaker **secretly and covertly** breaks a maxim. The hearer is NOT meant to recognize the breach. The speaker intends to deceive or mislead. Violating does **NOT generate** implicature (because the listener doesn't know the maxim was broken). It generates **misinformation** instead.

**The crucial test:** Did the speaker *intend* the hearer to recognize the maxim breach? If yes → flouting (implicature). If no → violating (deception).

As Grice put it, the violation must be "quiet and unostentatious" to be deception; if it is "blatant," it is a flout that triggers implicature generation.

### 5.2 Additional Categories of Non-Observance

Grice and subsequent scholars identified five types of maxim non-observance:

1. **Flouting** — Blatant, obvious breach intended to generate implicature. Communicatively productive.

2. **Violating** — Covert breach intended to mislead. Deceptive. The speaker wants the hearer to think the maxim is being observed when it isn't.

3. **Opting out** — The speaker explicitly signals unwillingness to cooperate in the expected way, often for legitimate reasons (legal obligation, ethical constraint, confidentiality). Example: "I'm afraid I can't comment on that" or "No comment." The speaker doesn't generate a false implicature—they signal that they're choosing not to cooperate on this point.

4. **Infringing** — The speaker unintentionally fails to observe a maxim—not through deception or communicative strategy, but through imperfect command of the language, nervousness, cognitive impairment, drunkenness, excitement, etc. No implicature is generated because there is no communicative intention behind the breach.

5. **Suspending** — Cultural, social, or situational norms override the maxim. No participant expects the maxim to apply. Examples: funeral orations suspend Quality (you don't speak ill of the dead); poetry suspends Manner (clarity, brevity are not expected); telegrams suspend Quantity (brevity is functional, not implicature-bearing).

### 5.3 Flouting: Detailed Examples by Maxim

#### 5.3.1 Flouting the Maxim of QUALITY (Do not say what you believe to be false)

Flouting Quality produces some of the most recognizable figures of speech. The speaker says something OBVIOUSLY untrue, and the hearer is meant to recognize the falsehood and derive the intended meaning.

**Irony / Sarcasm:**
The speaker says the opposite of what they mean. The utterance is blatantly false in context, signaling that the opposite is intended.

- After a terrible meal: "Well, THAT was a culinary masterpiece."
  - **What is said** (literally false): The meal was excellent.
  - **What is implicated**: The meal was terrible. The obvious falsity signals ironic intent.

- To someone who has made a huge mess: "Oh, your gracefulness is impressive."
  - **What is implicated**: You are clumsy. The obvious falsity of praising the "gracefulness" of someone who just knocked everything over triggers the ironic reading.

- "I'm SO excited to go to the dentist."
  - **What is implicated**: The speaker dreads going to the dentist. Both speaker and listener know this is false, so the listener recovers the real meaning.

- After receiving terrible advice: "Thanks for the GREAT advice."
  - **What is implicated**: The advice was terrible. The exaggerated praise is recognizably false.

- On a bitterly cold day: "Lovely weather we're having."
  - **What is implicated**: The weather is awful. The contrast between the statement and the obvious reality triggers the implicature.

**Metaphor:**
The speaker says something categorically false (a person is not literally a type of object or animal), and the hearer derives the intended figurative meaning.

- "You are the sunshine of my life."
  - **What is said** (literally false): You are a celestial body emitting light and heat.
  - **What is implicated**: You bring warmth, happiness, and brightness to my life.

- "He's a real bulldozer in meetings."
  - **What is implicated**: He is forceful, aggressive, and pushes through opposition without regard for others' input.

- "I married a rat."
  - **What is implicated**: My spouse is deceitful, untrustworthy, or contemptible—the hearer must look for which traits of rats map onto the spouse.

- "She has a heart of stone."
  - **What is implicated**: She is emotionally cold, unfeeling, unmoved by sympathy.

**Hyperbole (overstatement):**
The speaker exaggerates beyond what is literally true.

- "I've told you a MILLION times."
  - **What is said** (literally false): The number of times is one million.
  - **What is implicated**: I've told you many times—more than enough for you to have gotten the message.

- "This bag weighs a ton."
  - **What is implicated**: The bag is very heavy (but not literally 2,000 pounds).

- "Everyone was at the party."
  - **What is implicated**: Very many people were at the party (but obviously not literally every person on Earth).

**Meiosis / Understatement (litotes):**
The speaker deliberately says LESS than what is true.

- After winning the lottery: "I suppose I'll be able to pay my bills."
  - **What is implicated**: This is a massive financial windfall that will change my life—far more than just bill-paying money.

- Describing a hurricane: "It's a bit windy outside."
  - **What is implicated**: The weather is extremely severe. The understatement makes the severity MORE vivid by contrast.

- "He's not the friendliest person" (about someone who is actively hostile).
  - **What is implicated**: He's very unfriendly, rude, or hostile. The understated negation communicates a stronger negative than a direct statement would.

**Rhetorical questions:**
The speaker asks a question to which no answer is expected, flouting the expectation that questions seek information.

- "Who cares?"
  - **What is implicated**: Nobody cares. (Or: the speaker certainly doesn't care.)

- "Is the Pope Catholic?"
  - **What is implicated**: The answer is obviously yes—used to mean "of course" in response to a question with an obvious answer.

- "How many times do I have to tell you?"
  - **What is implicated**: This is not a request for a number. The speaker is frustrated at having to repeat themselves.

#### 5.3.2 Flouting the Maxim of QUANTITY (Be as informative as required / Don't say more than required)

**Saying too little (under-informativeness):**

- The recommendation letter example (Section 2.2.2): Praising only a candidate's handwriting when a full evaluation of philosophical ability is expected.
  - **What is implicated**: The candidate has no notable philosophical abilities.

- A: "What do you think of my new painting?"
  B: "It's certainly... colorful."
  - **What is implicated**: The painting is not good. B can only find one weak positive to mention.

- A: "Would you recommend this restaurant?"
  B: "It's conveniently located."
  - **What is implicated**: The food, service, or ambiance is not good enough to mention.

**Saying too much (over-informativeness):**

- A: "Did you finish the report?"
  B: "Well, I got up early, made myself a coffee, sat down at my desk, opened my computer, looked at the files, organized my notes, reviewed the requirements..."
  - **What is implicated**: B is stalling. The excessive detail about preparation implicates that the answer is "no" and B is trying to soften the blow by showing effort.

- When declining an invitation, providing elaborate detail: "I'd love to come, but I have to pick up my sister from the airport, and then I need to stop by the vet to get my dog's medication, and then my landlord is coming by to fix the plumbing..."
  - **What is implicated**: The speaker is establishing that the decline is genuine, not a brush-off. The extra detail is a politeness strategy showing respect.

#### 5.3.3 Flouting the Maxim of RELATION (Be relevant)

Flouting relevance means giving a response that *seems* off-topic but actually communicates through the apparent irrelevance.

- A: "How's your new job?"
  B: "The commute is short."
  - **What is implicated**: The job itself is not good. The only positive B can identify is the commute, which is peripheral.

- A: "Did you like the play?"
  B: "The theater had very comfortable seats."
  - **What is implicated**: The play was not good. B is commenting on the venue instead of the performance.

- A: "Are you interested in a game of tennis?"
  B: "It's raining."
  - **What is implicated**: No—we can't play tennis in the rain. The apparent irrelevance is actually highly relevant once the hearer makes the connection.

- A: "Do you want sushi tonight?"
  B: "I'm allergic to shellfish."
  - **What is implicated**: No—a sushi restaurant would be problematic for me. The relevance of the allergy to the dinner invitation creates the implicature.

- A: "Where's the roast beef?"
  B: "The dog is looking very happy."
  - **What is implicated**: The dog ate the roast beef. The connection between the dog's suspicious happiness and the missing food is the bridge the hearer must construct.

#### 5.3.4 Flouting the Maxim of MANNER (Be clear; avoid obscurity, ambiguity, prolixity; be orderly)

Flouting manner means being deliberately unclear, wordy, ambiguous, or disorderly to communicate something beyond the literal content.

**Deliberate ambiguity:**
- A: "Did you put the clothes away properly?"
  B: "I didn't NOT put the clothes away."
  - **What is implicated**: B did put the clothes away, but not well. The convoluted double negative (instead of simply "Yes") implicates that the answer is not a clean "yes."

**Deliberate prolixity (wordiness):**
- Instead of "They got married," a speaker says: "They went through a prolonged legal process of officially joining their lives together in the eyes of the state."
  - **What is implicated**: The speaker has a detached, possibly cynical, attitude toward the marriage.

**Deliberately obscure or formal language:**
- After a child asks what "someone" (a neighbor) does for work: "He engages in a particular professional enterprise related to controlled substances."
  - **What is implicated**: The parent does not want to explicitly say what the person does in front of the child. The obscurity is itself the message: "I'll tell you later" or "this is not for present company."

**Departure from expected order:**
- "She got married and got pregnant." vs. "She got pregnant and got married."
  - The Manner maxim (be orderly) creates a default implicature that events are narrated in chronological order. The second version implicates that the pregnancy came first, which may carry additional social implicature.

### 5.4 Violating: Detailed Examples by Maxim

Remember: When a maxim is **violated**, the speaker breaks it **covertly**—the hearer is NOT supposed to notice. The result is deception or misleading, NOT implicature.

#### 5.4.1 Violating the Maxim of QUALITY

This is straightforward **lying**—saying something the speaker believes to be false, intending the hearer to believe it is true.

- A child who ate the cookies: "I didn't eat any cookies."
  - **Violation**: The child says something false, intending the parent to believe it. No implicature is generated—just misinformation.

- A job candidate: "I have five years of experience in project management."
  - (When they actually have two years.) The violation is covert; the interviewer is meant to take the statement at face value.

- "I was at the library all evening." (When actually at a party.)
  - The speaker intends the hearer to believe the false statement. If the hearer detected the falsehood, no deception would occur—it would become either a recognized lie or an attempted flout.

#### 5.4.2 Violating the Maxim of QUANTITY

Misleading by strategically providing too much or too little information.

**Too little information (lies of omission):**
- "There's a gas station around the corner." (The speaker knows it's been abandoned for years.)
  - **Violation**: The speaker withholds crucial information (that it's closed), knowing the hearer will assume it's operational.

- A car seller: "The car was serviced last month."
  - (Omitting that the service revealed major engine problems.) The statement is true but deliberately insufficient.

**Too much information (distraction/obfuscation):**
- Including irrelevant but true details to distract from what has been omitted. Insurance fine print is a common example: burying important exclusions in walls of legal text.

#### 5.4.3 Violating the Maxim of RELATION

Misleading by implying relevance where there is none.

- "I should mention that it takes a long time to heat the oven." (When the recipe doesn't involve baking.)
  - The speaker implies that preheating is relevant (and therefore that baking is required) when it isn't. The hearer is misled into thinking this information matters.

- A politician asked about a scandal: "What I want to talk about is the economy..."
  - If the shift in topic is designed to make the hearer forget the original question (not openly opt out), it's a covert violation.

#### 5.4.4 Violating the Maxim of MANNER

Misleading through deliberate but covert lack of clarity.

- Using technical jargon or complex language intentionally to prevent the hearer from understanding unfavorable information (e.g., financial documents, contracts, medical consent forms designed to obscure rather than inform).

- Burying a crucial clause in a long sentence to make it less noticeable: "The subscription auto-renews annually at the standard rate, which may be adjusted, and cancellation must be submitted in writing 60 days prior to renewal."

### 5.5 Summary: Flouting vs. Violating at a Glance

| | **Flouting** | **Violating** |
|---|---|---|
| **The breach is...** | Obvious, blatant, recognizable | Covert, hidden, sneaky |
| **Both parties know?** | Yes — that's the point | No — only the speaker knows |
| **Intent** | Communicate additional meaning | Deceive or mislead |
| **Result** | Implicature (cooperative enrichment of meaning) | Misinformation (damage to communication) |
| **Examples** | Irony, sarcasm, metaphor, understatement, rhetorical questions, tact | Lying, omission, misdirection, obfuscation |
| **Trust** | Maintained (both parties are "in on it") | Eroded (one party is being manipulated) |
| **Listener experience** | Listener works out the real meaning | Listener is deceived into a wrong meaning |

## 6. Additional Considerations

### 6.1 Why Implicature Matters for Autistic Individuals

Research on pragmatic inference in autism reveals a nuanced picture:

- **Scalar implicatures** (generalized conversational implicatures like "some → not all") may actually be a relatively **spared** domain for autistic individuals. Some studies suggest that autistic individuals process these similarly to neurotypical peers, likely because scalar implicatures follow a more rule-like, logical pattern.

- **Particularized conversational implicatures** (context-dependent, requiring "mind-reading" or theory of mind) tend to be more challenging, particularly when they require:
  - Reasoning about the speaker's knowledge and intentions
  - Heavy context utilization
  - Rapid integration of social cues with linguistic content

- **Ad-hoc implicatures** (novel, context-bound inferences that don't follow established patterns) tend to be the most difficult.

- The **calculability** property of implicature is genuinely useful: because implicatures can be worked out step by step using explicit principles, they are learnable even when the automatic/intuitive processing is not available.

- **Indeterminacy** is a real property—it is not a failure on the listener's part when the exact implicature is unclear. Asking for clarification is a legitimate and often appreciated response.

### 6.2 The Relationship Between Implicature Types

The types of implicature exist on a continuum from most to least dependent on specific words:

1. **Conventional implicature** — Completely word-dependent. Changes if you change the word. Non-cancellable.
2. **Generalized conversational implicature** — Pattern-dependent. Arises from general linguistic patterns (scales, indefinites). Cancellable but default.
3. **Particularized conversational implicature** — Context-dependent. Arises from the specific conversational situation. Cancellable and non-default.

### 6.3 Implicature vs. Other Types of Non-Literal Meaning

Implicature should be distinguished from:

- **Entailment**: If A entails B, then B must be true whenever A is true. Entailments cannot be cancelled. ("She is a widow" entails "Her husband has died.")
- **Presupposition**: Background assumptions taken for granted by the speaker. ("Have you stopped smoking?" presupposes you used to smoke.) Presuppositions survive negation; implicatures typically do not.
- **Speech acts**: Implicature is about what is communicated beyond what is said; speech acts are about what ACTION is performed by speaking (requesting, promising, warning, etc.). These can overlap—an indirect speech act often works through implicature.
