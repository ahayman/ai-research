## 1. What Discourse Markers Are

### 1.1 Definition

Discourse markers are small words and phrases -- such as *well*, *so*, *but*, *you know*, *I mean*, *oh*, *okay*, *right*, *now*, *anyway*, *like*, and *actually* -- that serve pragmatic rather than semantic functions in communication. They organize discourse, manage turn-taking, signal speaker attitudes, hedge assertions, mark topic shifts, and maintain social relationships between interlocutors.

Deborah Schiffrin (1987), in the foundational work *Discourse Markers*, defined them as **"sequentially dependent elements which bracket units of talk"** -- that is, nonobligatory utterance-initial items that function in relation to ongoing talk and text. They provide "contextual coordinates for utterances," signaling relationships between immediately adjacent units of talk and thus building coherence at a local level (Schiffrin, 1987).

Bruce Fraser (1999) refined the definition: discourse markers are **"a class of lexical expressions drawn primarily from the syntactic classes of conjunctions, adverbs, and prepositional phrases"** that **"signal a relationship between the interpretation of the segment they introduce, S2, and the prior segment, S1."** They have a core meaning that is **procedural, not conceptual**, and their more specific interpretation is "negotiated" by context, both linguistic and conceptual (Fraser, 1999).

A discourse marker is thus a word or phrase that plays a role in managing the flow and structure of discourse. Since their main function operates at the level of discourse (sequences of utterances) rather than at the level of individual sentences, discourse markers are relatively syntax-independent and usually do not change the truth-conditional meaning of the sentence they appear in.

### 1.2 The Critical Pragmatic Work They Do

Discourse markers are often dismissed as "meaningless filler" -- verbal tics, hesitation noise, or sloppy speech habits. This perception is fundamentally wrong. Discourse markers do critical pragmatic work that content words cannot:

- **Organizing discourse**: They signal how utterances relate to each other -- as elaborations, contrasts, consequences, or topic shifts. Without them, a listener must infer all connective structure from context alone.
- **Managing turn-taking**: They signal when a speaker is beginning, continuing, or yielding a turn. Words like *well*, *so*, *okay*, and *right* often mark turn boundaries. Research shows discourse markers in utterance-final position function as turn-transition mechanisms, signaling that the turn is over and the floor is open to the hearer.
- **Signaling attitudes and stances**: They communicate the speaker's epistemic stance (how certain they are), their emotional response, or their social positioning relative to the listener. *I think* and *I guess* serve as epistemic stance markers; *oh* signals changes in information state; *well* often signals that a dispreferred or unexpected response is coming.
- **Hedging and face-saving**: They soften the force of assertions, requests, and disagreements. *Sort of*, *kind of*, *like*, *you know*, and *I mean* reduce the directness of statements, protecting both the speaker's and listener's "face" in the politeness-theory sense (Brown & Levinson, 1987).
- **Maintaining social relationships**: They signal solidarity, shared knowledge, attention, and engagement. *You know* invites the listener into shared understanding. Backchannels like *yeah*, *uh-huh*, *right*, and *mm-hm* signal active listening without claiming the conversational floor.
- **Self-repair and reformulation**: *I mean* and *well* serve as self-repair markers -- signals that the speaker is about to correct, clarify, or reformulate something they just said. Research identifies *well* as a "claiming editing marker" and *I mean* as a "mistake editing marker."

### 1.3 Key Researchers and Foundational Works

The modern study of discourse markers emerges from three major theoretical traditions, each offering a distinct analytical lens:

**Deborah Schiffrin (1987)** -- *Discourse Markers* (Cambridge University Press). The foundational work. Schiffrin analyzed 11 English discourse markers (*oh, well, and, but, or, so, because, now, then, I mean, y'know*) from sociolinguistic interviews with seven Jewish Philadelphians. She developed a multi-plane model of discourse showing that markers operate simultaneously across five different levels of discourse organization. Her key insight: markers are multifunctional -- they don't do just one thing, and understanding them requires looking at multiple dimensions of discourse simultaneously.

**Bruce Fraser (1990, 1996, 1999)** -- "An Approach to Discourse Markers" and "What Are Discourse Markers?" Fraser took a more taxonomic, sentence-level approach, classifying discourse markers by the type of relationship they signal between adjacent discourse segments: contrastive, elaborative, inferential, and topic-change markers. He emphasized that discourse markers form a "pragmatic class" whose elements "contribute to the interpretation of an utterance rather than to its propositional content."

**Diane Blakemore (1987, 2002)** -- *Relevance and Linguistic Meaning: The Semantics and Pragmatics of Discourse Markers* (Cambridge University Press). Working within Sperber and Wilson's Relevance Theory, Blakemore introduced the crucial distinction between **conceptual** and **procedural** meaning. Her key argument: discourse markers don't encode concepts (like nouns and verbs do); they encode **instructions** for how to process an utterance -- they tell the listener what kind of inferential work to do. This was a paradigm-shifting insight.

**Other important contributors:**
- **John Heritage (1984)** -- Analyzed *oh* as a "change-of-state token" in conversation analysis, showing it marks a shift in the speaker's knowledge, information, orientation, or awareness.
- **Gisela Redeker (1991)** -- Proposed a refined tripartite classification of discourse operators: ideational (connecting real-world events), rhetorical (connecting assumptions or speech acts), and sequential (connecting larger discourse units such as turns or topics). This condensed Schiffrin's five-plane model into a more streamlined framework.
- **Andreas Jucker and Yael Ziv (1998)** -- Edited *Discourse Markers: Descriptions and Theory*, which brought together thirteen papers ranging from functional descriptions to theoretical issues, using data from English, Finnish, Hebrew, Korean, and Japanese. They treated "discourse marker" as an umbrella term with "discourse particles" as a subclass.

### 1.4 The Terminological Confusion

One of the most striking features of this research area is the lack of terminological consensus. Different researchers use different terms for overlapping (but not identical) phenomena:

- **Discourse markers** (Schiffrin 1987, Fraser 1999) -- the most widely used term
- **Pragmatic markers** (broader term, includes attitudinal and interpersonal markers beyond discourse-connective functions)
- **Discourse particles** (often restricted to uninflected single words, e.g., *oh*, *well*, *so*)
- **Discourse connectives** (narrower term, emphasizing the connecting function between discourse segments)
- **Discourse operators** (Redeker 1991)
- **Fillers** (pejorative folk term, usually for *um*, *uh*, *like*, *you know*)
- **Hedges** (Lakoff 1973; overlapping category for markers that reduce commitment, like *sort of*, *kind of*)
- **Pragmatic particles** and **modal particles** (especially in Germanic linguistics)

The key distinction in the literature is between narrower and broader definitions:
- **Narrow definition** (Fraser): Discourse markers are specifically items that signal relationships between discourse segments. This excludes interjections (*oh*), fillers (*um*), and hedges (*sort of*).
- **Broad definition** (Schiffrin; most sociolinguistic approaches): Discourse markers include any element that functions to manage discourse coherence, speaker-hearer relations, and information flow, including interjections, fillers, and epistemic markers.

Rather than viewing discourse markers and pragmatic markers as entirely separate categories, recent research suggests they are better understood as occupying positions along a continuum. The degree to which a given marker fulfills a discourse-management function (text-organizing) versus a stance-marking function (interpersonal/attitudinal) determines whether it falls closer to the "discourse marker" or "pragmatic marker" pole. Most markers do both to some degree.

**Why this matters for learners**: The terminological chaos in the research literature reflects genuine complexity in the phenomenon. These words resist neat categorization because they are inherently multifunctional -- a single word like *well* can organize discourse, manage turns, signal stance, hedge assertions, and maintain relationships, all at the same time or in different contexts.

## 2. How Discourse Markers Differ from Content Words

### 2.1 The Truth-Conditional Distinction

The single most important theoretical distinction between discourse markers and content words is this: **discourse markers do not contribute to the truth-conditional meaning of the sentence.** Truth-conditional meaning is the dimension of meaning that determines whether a sentence is true or false. Content words like nouns, verbs, adjectives, and adverbs contribute to truth conditions -- they describe states of affairs in the world.

Compare:

> (a) "Well, I think we should go."
> (b) "I think we should go."

These two sentences have **identical truth conditions**. If it's true that the speaker thinks they should go, then both (a) and (b) are true. If it's false, both are false. Removing *well* does not change what the sentence literally says.

But removing *well* **does** change what the sentence pragmatically communicates. Sentence (a) -- with *well* -- signals something additional: hesitation, a dispreferred response, a topic shift, or a considered opinion that may contrast with what was previously said. Without *well*, the statement is more direct and carries none of these pragmatic implications.

More examples:

> (c) "So, you're coming to the party?" -- *so* signals that this is an inference drawn from prior information
> (d) "You're coming to the party?" -- neutral question without inferential framing

> (e) "He's, like, really tall." -- *like* hedges the description, making it approximate
> (f) "He's really tall." -- unhedged assertion

> (g) "I mean, that's not what I expected." -- *I mean* signals reformulation or emphasis
> (h) "That's not what I expected." -- bare statement of the proposition

In every case, the truth conditions are unchanged. The propositional content -- the factual claim being made -- remains the same. What changes is the **pragmatic frame**: how the listener is being instructed to process and contextualize the information.

### 2.2 Syntactic Properties That Set Discourse Markers Apart

Discourse markers share a cluster of syntactic properties that distinguish them from content words:

**Optionality**: Discourse markers are syntactically optional. Removing them does not make the sentence ungrammatical. *"Well, I don't know"* is grammatically equivalent to *"I don't know."* However -- and this is crucial -- their optionality is purely syntactic. In pragmatic terms, optionality is "nonsensical," because discourse markers are essential for organizing discourse and marking the speaker's attitudes toward the proposition. Removing them fundamentally changes what is being communicated, even if it doesn't change what is being said.

**Positional flexibility with initial preference**: Discourse markers typically occur in initial position within an utterance or turn (turn-initial), though they can also appear medially or finally. Turn-initial position is the most common and unmarked position. When they appear in utterance-final position (e.g., *"That's interesting, you know"*), they often function as turn-transition mechanisms or appeals for listener engagement.

**Prosodic detachability**: Discourse markers are typically set off from the rest of the utterance by intonation -- they have their own prosodic contour, often separated by a slight pause. They are prosodically "outside" the sentence they accompany.

**Syntactic independence**: They do not participate in the syntactic structure of the sentence. They cannot be modified, negated, or focused in the same way content words can. You cannot say *"Very well, I think we should go"* (with *very* modifying the discourse marker *well*) or *"Not well, I think we should go"* (with *not* negating the discourse marker *well*) in the same way you could modify or negate the adjective *well*.

**Multi-categoriality**: Discourse markers are drawn from diverse grammatical sources -- conjunctions (*but, and, or, so*), adverbs (*now, then, anyway, actually*), prepositional phrases (*by the way, in addition, on the other hand*), verb phrases (*you know, I mean, you see, look, listen*), and interjections (*oh, well*). They do not form a unified syntactic class; they form a unified functional class.

### 2.3 The Discourse Level vs. the Sentence Level

Content words operate at the sentence level -- they contribute to the meaning of individual propositions. Discourse markers operate at the **discourse level** -- they signal relationships between utterances, manage the interaction between speakers, and organize the flow of conversation.

This is a crucial distinction. A sentence can be grammatically complete and semantically interpretable without any discourse markers. But a conversation -- an actual exchange between real speakers -- cannot function smoothly without them. They are the connective tissue of spoken interaction.

Consider this stripped-down exchange:

> A: "Do you want to go to the movie tonight?"
> B: "I have a lot of work to do."

Without discourse markers, B's response is technically just a statement of fact. The listener must infer (through pragmatic reasoning) that it's an indirect refusal. Now add discourse markers:

> A: "Do you want to go to the movie tonight?"
> B: "Well... I mean, I have a lot of work to do, you know?"

The discourse markers make the pragmatic function explicit:
- *Well* signals that a dispreferred response is coming (not a straightforward "yes")
- *I mean* signals that the speaker is about to provide an explanation or justification
- *you know* appeals to shared understanding and softens the refusal

The propositional content is the same. The discourse-level communication is entirely different.

## 3. Schiffrin's (1987) Framework: Five Planes of Discourse

### 3.1 The Multi-Plane Model

Schiffrin's central insight was that discourse is not a single linear stream of propositions. It is a complex event operating simultaneously on multiple levels, and discourse markers operate on -- and help integrate -- these multiple levels. She identified five simultaneous planes (or structures) of discourse:

#### 3.1.1 Exchange Structure
Exchange structure reflects the mechanics of the conversational interchange -- the turn-taking system. It shows how speaker turns alternate and how these alternations are related to each other. Discourse markers operating on this plane manage who speaks when, signal turn boundaries, and organize the sequential flow of conversation.

**Markers primarily operating here**: *well* (marks a new turn, especially a responsive turn), *okay* and *right* (can mark acknowledgment of a prior turn or transition to a new one), *so* (can mark return to the main business after a digression)

**Example**: When someone begins a response with *"Well..."*, they are performing exchange-structure work -- they are signaling that they are taking their turn but that their response will not be a straightforward, expected one. The *well* tells the listener: "I'm responding, but brace yourself."

#### 3.1.2 Action Structure
Action structure reflects the sequence of speech acts within the discourse -- not just what is said but what is *done* with language (requesting, promising, refusing, complimenting, etc.). Discourse markers on this plane signal the relationships between speech acts: is this next act a response to the previous one? A new initiation? A side comment?

**Markers primarily operating here**: *so* (can mark a consequential speech act), *well* (marks dispreferred speech acts like refusals and disagreements), *but* (marks a speech act that contrasts with or overrides a prior one)

**Example**: In adjacency pairs (question-answer, invitation-acceptance/refusal), *well* before a response is a strong signal of a dispreferred second -- a response that departs from what was expected or hoped for. "Would you like to come to dinner?" "Well..." -- the *well* alone communicates that a refusal or complication is coming, even before any content words are spoken.

#### 3.1.3 Ideational Structure
Ideational structure reflects relationships between the ideas and propositions in the discourse -- how pieces of content connect to each other logically. Are they additive? Contrastive? Causal? Temporal?

**Markers primarily operating here**: *and* (additive connection), *but* (contrastive connection), *or* (alternative), *so* (consequential/causal), *because* (causal), *then* (temporal sequence)

**Example**: "I was tired, *so* I went home" -- *so* marks a causal-consequential relationship between the two propositions. "I was tired, *but* I stayed anyway" -- *but* marks contrast between what would be expected (going home) and what actually happened (staying).

#### 3.1.4 Participation Framework
The participation framework refers to the different ways speakers and hearers can relate to each other -- their roles, their alignment, their relationship. Discourse markers on this plane manage interpersonal dynamics: solidarity, distance, shared knowledge, epistemic authority.

**Markers primarily operating here**: *you know* (marks shared knowledge, invites solidarity, appeals to common ground), *I mean* (marks the speaker's effort to be understood, a kind of interpersonal repair), *well* (can mark the speaker's negotiation of their position relative to the hearer)

**Example**: "It was, *you know*, one of those awkward situations" -- *you know* invites the listener into a shared frame of reference, assuming common experience. It builds solidarity. Its removal makes the statement more neutral and less interpersonally engaged.

#### 3.1.5 Information State
Information state reflects the ongoing organization and management of knowledge as it evolves over the course of conversation -- what is known, what is new, what has been established, what is being revised.

**Markers primarily operating here**: *oh* (marks a change in information state -- "I just learned something" or "I just realized something"), *well* (can mark a reassessment of the information state), *so* (can mark a conclusion drawn from accumulated information)

**Example**: Heritage (1984) showed that *oh* functions as a "change-of-state token" -- it signals that the speaker has undergone "some kind of change in his or her locally current state of knowledge, information, orientation, or awareness." When someone says *"Oh, I didn't know that,"* the *oh* is doing work beyond the content words: it marks the moment of cognitive transition from not-knowing to knowing.

### 3.2 Multifunctionality Across Planes

Schiffrin's crucial finding was that **although markers have primary functions on particular planes, their use is multifunctional.** The same marker simultaneously operates on multiple planes of discourse. It is this multifunctionality that helps integrate the many different simultaneous processes underlying the construction of discourse, and thus helps create coherence.

For example, *well* has its primary function in the participation framework (managing the speaker-hearer relationship), but it simultaneously does work on the exchange structure (marking a turn), the action structure (signaling a dispreferred response), and the information state (marking a reassessment). A single instance of *well* can be doing all of these things at once.

This multifunctionality is both what makes discourse markers powerful and what makes them difficult to analyze -- or to learn. They don't map one-to-one onto functions; they operate across the full complexity of discourse simultaneously.

### 3.3 Schiffrin's Eleven Markers -- Summary of Primary Functions

| Marker | Primary Plane(s) | Core Function |
|--------|------------------|---------------|
| **oh** | Information state | Marks change in speaker's knowledge or awareness |
| **well** | Participation framework | Manages speaker-hearer alignment; signals face-threatening or dispreferred responses |
| **and** | Ideational | Coordinates ideas; marks continuation |
| **but** | Ideational | Marks contrast between propositions |
| **or** | Ideational | Marks alternatives |
| **so** | Ideational + Action | Marks consequence or result; marks return to main topic |
| **because** | Ideational | Marks cause or reason |
| **now** | Information state + Exchange | Marks a shift in topic or orientation |
| **then** | Ideational + Information state | Marks temporal sequence or conditional consequence |
| **I mean** | Participation framework | Marks self-repair, clarification, or emphasis of speaker's intention |
| **y'know** | Participation framework + Information state | Appeals to shared knowledge; invites listener engagement |

## 4. Fraser's (1999) Classification

### 4.1 Overview

Where Schiffrin took a multi-dimensional, ethnographic approach, Bruce Fraser developed a more formal, taxonomic classification. For Fraser, discourse markers are a "pragmatic class" that contributes to the interpretation of utterances rather than to their propositional content. He classified discourse markers by the type of relationship they signal between two adjacent discourse segments (S1 and S2).

Fraser's definition emphasizes that discourse markers have procedural rather than conceptual meaning, and their specific interpretation is context-dependent -- "negotiated" by the linguistic and conceptual context.

### 4.2 The Four Categories

#### 4.2.1 Contrastive Discourse Markers (CDMs)
Contrastive markers signal that the message conveyed by S2 contrasts -- directly or indirectly -- with the message of S1.

**Examples**: *but, however, although, whereas, nevertheless, on the contrary, on the other hand, in contrast, instead, despite this, in spite of this, yet, still, nonetheless, rather, conversely, notwithstanding*

**How they work**:
> S1: "The movie got terrible reviews."
> S2: "*But* we enjoyed it anyway."

The marker *but* signals: "What follows will contrast with or qualify what came before." The listener is instructed to look for a contrast relationship between the two segments.

#### 4.2.2 Elaborative Discourse Markers (EDMs)
Elaborative markers signal that S2 provides an elaboration, extension, or refinement of the information in S1 -- it adds to or develops the same thread.

**Examples**: *and, moreover, in addition, furthermore, also, besides, above all, what's more, equally, likewise, similarly, in particular, for example, for instance, that is, in other words, namely, specifically*

**How they work**:
> S1: "The restaurant has great food."
> S2: "*Moreover*, the service is excellent."

The marker *moreover* signals: "What follows adds to and reinforces what was just said."

#### 4.2.3 Inferential Discourse Markers (IDMs)
Inferential markers signal that S1 provides a basis for inferring S2 -- the listener should understand S2 as a conclusion, consequence, or result drawn from S1.

**Examples**: *so, therefore, thus, consequently, hence, as a result, accordingly, then, in that case, it follows that, for this reason*

**How they work**:
> S1: "It's been raining all day."
> S2: "*So* the picnic is probably cancelled."

The marker *so* signals: "What follows is a conclusion you should draw from what was just said."

#### 4.2.4 Topic Change Markers
Topic change markers signal a shift from the current topic to a new or different one, or a return to a previously abandoned topic.

**Examples**: *by the way, incidentally, speaking of which, anyway, in any case, returning to, as I was saying, on another note, parenthetically*

**How they work**:
> S1: "So we're agreed on the budget."
> S2: "*By the way*, did you hear about the new policy?"

The marker *by the way* signals: "What follows is a digression or shift to a different topic." *Incidentally* and *parenthetically* function similarly, often indicating that the new information is less central than what preceded it. *Anyway* can signal a return to a main topic after a digression -- a "reshifting" function.

### 4.3 Strengths and Limitations of Fraser's System

**Strengths**: Fraser's taxonomy is clear, systematic, and relatively easy to apply, making it useful for teaching and for formal linguistic analysis. It captures a large portion of discourse marker functions, particularly those that connect written discourse segments.

**Limitations**: The system is primarily oriented toward written and monologic discourse -- it captures how ideas connect to ideas well, but doesn't fully account for the interactional, interpersonal, and turn-management functions that are central in spoken conversation. Markers like *well*, *oh*, *you know*, and *I mean* -- which are critical in conversation -- don't fit neatly into contrastive/elaborative/inferential/topic-change categories because their primary functions are interpersonal, not ideational. This is precisely the territory Schiffrin's multi-plane model captures better.

## 5. The Relevance Theory Approach (Blakemore)

### 5.1 The Conceptual/Procedural Distinction

Diane Blakemore's work, grounded in the Relevance Theory of Dan Sperber and Deirdre Wilson (1986/1995), introduced what is arguably the most theoretically important distinction in discourse marker research: the difference between **conceptual** and **procedural** meaning.

Sperber and Wilson's key insight was that inferential comprehension involves constructing and manipulating conceptual representations, and linguistic decoding feeds this inferential comprehension. Therefore, linguistic constructions can encode two basic types of information:

1. **Conceptual encoding**: Words that encode concepts or conceptual representations -- the building blocks of mental models. Most content words do this. *Dog* encodes the concept DOG. *Run* encodes the concept RUN. *Beautiful* encodes the concept BEAUTIFUL.

2. **Procedural encoding**: Words that encode instructions for how to manipulate conceptual representations -- they don't contribute concepts to the mental model but rather guide the inferential processes that determine how the model is assembled and interpreted. Discourse markers are the prime example.

This is a profound distinction. Most words give you **things to think about** (concepts). Discourse markers give you **instructions for how to think** (procedures).

### 5.2 Discourse Markers as Processing Instructions

Under Blakemore's analysis, discourse markers do not encode meanings in the way content words do. Instead, they encode **constraints on the inferential process** -- they tell the listener what kind of cognitive operation to perform on the information being presented.

**"But" does not mean "contrast"** -- rather, it encodes a procedure: "Look for a way in which what follows contradicts, qualifies, or is unexpected in light of what came before." The contrast itself is not encoded in the word *but*; the word *but* instructs the listener to construct a contrast from the available information.

**"So" does not mean "therefore"** -- rather, it encodes a procedure: "Treat what follows as a conclusion or consequence that can be inferred from what came before." The inferential relationship itself is not encoded in *so*; the word *so* tells the listener to perform an inferential derivation.

**"After all" does not mean "here is supporting evidence"** -- rather, it instructs the listener to treat what follows as evidence that strengthens or confirms an existing assumption.

Blakemore held that discourse markers help make discourse coherent in three specific ways:
1. **Introducing contextual implications** -- guiding the listener to derive new conclusions from combining what was said with what they already know
2. **Strengthening existing assumptions** -- providing further evidence for something already believed
3. **Denying or contradicting assumptions** -- signaling that a prior expectation should be revised

### 5.3 Why This Matters: The Instruction Metaphor

The procedural account offers a powerful metaphor for understanding discourse markers: they are **instruction words**, not **content words**. They tell you *how to process* what someone is saying, not *what* they are saying.

Consider this analogy: if content words are the ingredients of a recipe (flour, eggs, butter), then discourse markers are the instructions (mix, fold, bake). The instructions don't add any new ingredients, but without them, you can't turn ingredients into a coherent dish. Similarly, discourse markers don't add propositional content, but without them, you can't turn propositions into coherent discourse.

This is captured in formal terms: **discourse markers encode procedural meanings that guide interpretation rather than contributing to truth conditions.** They constrain the hearer's choice of context under which the utterance is relevant -- they narrow down the space of possible interpretations.

### 5.4 Blakemore's Critique of Classification Exercises

Blakemore argued that analyzing discourse markers as expressions linking discourse segments -- the approach taken by Fraser and others -- leads to "unproductive classification exercises." The problem with taxonomic approaches is that the same marker can signal different relationships in different contexts (multifunctionality), and different markers can signal the same relationship (redundancy). The procedural approach is more explanatory because it describes what markers *do cognitively* rather than trying to slot them into fixed categories.

For example, *but* can signal direct contrast, denial of expectation, concession, or correction -- these are not four different meanings of *but* but rather four different outcomes of applying the same procedural instruction ("look for contradiction/qualification") in different contexts.

## 6. Specific Discourse Markers: A Functional Inventory

### 6.1 "Oh" -- The Information State Marker

*Oh* marks a change in the speaker's cognitive state -- a transition in knowledge, awareness, or orientation. Heritage (1984) coined the term **"change-of-state token"** for this function. When a speaker says *oh*, they are displaying that they have "undergone some kind of change in his or her locally current state of knowledge, information, orientation, or awareness."

**Functions of *oh***:
- **Response to new information**: "Oh, I didn't know that" (marks the moment of learning)
- **Sudden realization**: "Oh! That's what you meant" (marks a shift in understanding)
- **Shift of attention**: "Oh, speaking of which..." (marks reorientation to a new topic)
- **Preface to a revised assessment**: "Oh, it's not as bad as I thought" (marks a shift in evaluation)
- **Recognition**: "Oh, it's you!" (marks identification)

The meaning depends on prosodic delivery -- "Oh" with falling intonation typically marks simple receipt of information; "Oh!" with rising-falling intonation marks surprise; "Oh..." with extended, falling intonation may mark disappointment. These "cognitive-affective laminations" (Heritage) are conveyed through prosody, not through the word itself.

### 6.2 "Well" -- The Multifunctional Pragmatic Pivot

*Well* is perhaps the most studied and most multifunctional discourse marker in English. Its primary home, per Schiffrin, is the participation framework -- managing the relationship between speaker and hearer. But it operates across virtually all planes.

**Key functions of *well***:
- **Dispreferred response marker**: The most studied function. When *well* prefixes a response, it signals that what follows is not the expected, straightforward, or preferred response. "Would you like to come?" "Well..." = a refusal or complication is coming. This is a critical signal for conversation participants to recalibrate their expectations.
- **Face-threat mitigator**: Related to the dispreferred response function -- *well* softens face-threatening acts (disagreements, refusals, criticisms, corrections) by signaling that the speaker is aware the response may be unwelcome.
- **Topic shift marker**: *Well* can signal a transition to a new topic or a return to a previous one: "Well, anyway, what I wanted to ask you was..."
- **Delay and thinking marker**: *Well* buys time while the speaker formulates a response, particularly when the response requires consideration: "That's a good question. Well, I'd say..."
- **Self-repair marker**: *Well* can signal that the speaker is about to correct or revise something: "It was Tuesday -- well, actually, Wednesday."
- **Narrative transition**: In storytelling, *well* marks transitions between episodes or signals that the story is about to take an unexpected turn.

### 6.3 "You Know" and "I Mean" -- Participation Framework Markers

**"You know"** has two core discoursal functions identified in research:
1. **Marker of shared meta-knowledge**: Appeals to what speakers and hearers share -- "It was, you know, one of those situations" (invites the listener to access shared experience)
2. **Marker of general knowledge**: Appeals to what is generally known -- "Well, you know, that's just how it goes" (presents the claim as common sense)

Additional pragmatic functions:
- **Attitude marker** (about 20% of uses in corpus studies): The discourse after *you know* typically expresses the speaker's genuine attitudes and feelings
- **Mitigator** (about 15% of uses): Applied in face-threatening situations to soften interpersonal friction
- **Engagement device**: Checks whether the listener is following; invites verbal or nonverbal acknowledgment

**"I mean"** functions as:
- **Clarification marker**: "I mean, what I'm trying to say is..."
- **Correction marker**: "It was Tuesday -- I mean, Wednesday"
- **Emphasis marker**: "That was I mean really impressive"
- **Politeness moderator**: Softens the force of an assertion by framing it as a personal interpretation rather than an absolute claim

### 6.4 "So" -- The Versatile Connector

*So* operates across ideational structure (marking consequence), action structure (returning to main business), and information state (marking a conclusion drawn from accumulated evidence):

- **Inferential/consequential**: "It's raining, so we should take umbrellas" (marks S2 as a consequence of S1)
- **Summarizing/concluding**: "So, what you're saying is..." (draws together prior information)
- **Topic launching/returning**: "So, the reason I called..." (marks the beginning of the main business, often after small talk or digression)
- **Turn-taking**: "So..." at the beginning of a turn can claim the floor and signal the speaker is about to make a substantive contribution

### 6.5 Backchannels -- Listener Response Markers

Though not always classified as discourse markers in the narrow sense, backchannels are functionally essential to discourse management:

- **"Yeah"**, **"uh-huh"**, **"mm-hm"** -- signal continued attention and encourage the speaker to continue
- **"Right"**, **"okay"** -- signal understanding and acknowledgment
- **"Really?"** -- signals interest and can prompt elaboration
- **"I see"** -- signals processing of new information

Backchannels are **phatic expressions** -- they primarily serve social and meta-conversational purposes (signifying attention, understanding, sympathy, or agreement) rather than conveying propositional information. They contribute to the flow, efficiency, and coherence of dialogue and play a central role in **grounding** -- the ongoing effort by which speakers and listeners establish shared understanding.

## 7. Why Discourse Markers Are Especially Important for Autistic Learners

### 7.1 The Filtering Problem

For autistic individuals who process language with heightened attention to literal meaning and semantic content, discourse markers present a specific challenge: **they seem semantically empty, and therefore may be filtered out as meaningless noise.** If you are listening for *what someone is saying* (propositional content), discourse markers literally do not contribute anything. They don't add information about the world. They don't change whether the sentence is true or false.

But they carry critical pragmatic information about **how to interpret what follows**. Missing a discourse marker means missing a processing instruction -- and the consequences cascade:

- **Missing "well"** before a response = missing the signal that a dispreferred response is coming. Without registering the *well*, the listener has no advance warning that the next utterance will be a refusal, disagreement, or complication. The refusal then arrives without the cushioning and framing that *well* provided, and it may be experienced as more abrupt or confusing than it was intended to be.

- **Missing "oh"** = missing the signal that the speaker's information state has changed. This means missing the emotional/cognitive subtext: surprise, realization, disappointment, recognition.

- **Missing "you know"** = missing an invitation to shared understanding, or an appeal for engagement. This can result in missing social bids and opportunities for connection.

- **Missing "I mean"** = missing the signal that a self-repair or clarification is coming, potentially leading to confusion about which version of the utterance is the intended one.

- **Missing "so" as a topic-return marker** = losing track of the discourse structure and not understanding why the speaker suddenly returned to an earlier topic.

### 7.2 The Research Evidence

Research confirms that autistic individuals use discourse markers differently from neurotypical peers, and that pragmatic language differences are among the most reliable diagnostic markers for ASD:

- **Reduced frequency**: Previous research shows that autistic individuals use discourse markers significantly less frequently than non-autistic peers. However, this is not universal -- research on the specific marker *like* found that older autistic children used it as often as their peers and in functionally similar ways.

- **Pragmatic inference difficulty**: Autistic individuals are classically associated with difficulties in pragmatic inferences, often resulting in over-literal interpretation of language. Autistic participants were over twice as likely to endorse a non-normative interpretation of an implied meaning and over five times as likely to select "do not know" when asked about the presence of an implied meaning.

- **Preference for certainty**: An important finding reveals that autistic adults were much more likely to select "I don't know" when asked about implied meanings. When the "I don't know" option was removed, they performed more similarly to non-autistic adults. This suggests that pragmatic "deficits" may partly reflect a preference for certainty and explicit information over uncertain inferences.

- **Context dependence**: Pragmatic differences are not fixed or consistent across all situations -- they vary depending on the open-ended nature of the task, the roles involved, and the general context. Structured contexts with clear expectations reduce pragmatic difficulties; unstructured, open-ended conversations amplify them.

- **Reformulation patterns**: Autistic individuals tend to reformulate and revise their speech more frequently than neurotypical individuals, possibly due to challenges with executive functioning. This suggests a potentially higher reliance on self-repair markers like *I mean* and *well*, even if other discourse markers are used less frequently.

### 7.3 Why Explicit Teaching of Discourse Markers Matters

Research on pragmatic instruction -- primarily from second-language acquisition, but directly applicable to pragmatic learning in autism -- demonstrates that **explicit instruction in discourse markers significantly improves pragmatic competence**, outperforming implicit or unguided exposure.

Key findings:
- Explicit instruction in pragmatic markers consistently leads to greater improvement than implicit instruction
- Learners exposed to explicit instruction developed awareness of differences between their current pragmatic repertoire and target pragmatic patterns
- Corpus-based studies highlight the need for explicit instruction on pragmatic markers, emphasizing the importance of raising learners' awareness of their functions and contextual use
- Authentic language input (conversation recordings) combined with analysis and practice activities (role plays, simulations) provides effective learning contexts

This is directly relevant for autistic learners because:

1. **Pattern recognition strength**: Autistic individuals often excel at pattern recognition and systematic learning. Discourse markers, once made explicit and systematic, can be learned as a decodable system rather than absorbed implicitly through social osmosis.

2. **Rule-based processing**: The theoretical frameworks presented here -- Schiffrin's five planes, Fraser's four categories, Blakemore's procedural instructions -- provide explicit, systematic rules for what discourse markers do. These rules can serve as a parsing toolkit for real-time conversation.

3. **Reducing ambiguity**: For individuals who prefer certainty and explicit information, understanding discourse markers reduces the ambiguity of conversation. When you know that *well* before a response signals a dispreferred action, you are not left guessing -- you have a decoding rule.

4. **Compensating for implicit learning gaps**: Neurotypical speakers typically acquire discourse marker competence through implicit socialization -- thousands of hours of conversation from early childhood. For autistic individuals who may not absorb these patterns implicitly, explicit teaching provides an alternative pathway to the same competence.

### 7.4 Discourse Markers as a Parsing Tool for Conversation

Understanding discourse markers provides something that is extremely valuable for autistic conversation participants: **real-time parsing tools**. Rather than trying to intuit the overall "vibe" of a conversation (a process that may feel opaque or unreliable), an autistic listener who understands discourse markers can use them as explicit signals:

- **"Well..."** = "A dispreferred or complicated response is coming. Prepare for a refusal, disagreement, or qualification."
- **"Oh!"** = "The speaker just learned or realized something. Their information state changed."
- **"So..."** = "The speaker is drawing a conclusion, or returning to the main topic."
- **"You know..."** = "The speaker is appealing to shared experience or softening what they're about to say."
- **"I mean..."** = "The speaker is about to clarify, correct, or reframe something."
- **"But..."** = "What follows will contrast with or qualify what came before."
- **"By the way..."** = "Topic change incoming -- this will be about something different."
- **"Anyway..."** = "The speaker is returning to a main topic after a digression."
- **"Right?"/"Okay?"** = "The speaker is checking if you're following. A brief acknowledgment is expected."
- **"Actually..."** = "The speaker is about to correct a prior assumption or provide unexpected information."

These are not vague social intuitions -- they are **decodable signals** with reliable (though probabilistic) meanings. Learning them is analogous to learning traffic signals: each marker tells you something specific about what's coming next in the conversational flow.

## 8. Theoretical Synthesis: Three Complementary Lenses

The three major theoretical approaches to discourse markers are not competing accounts -- they are complementary lenses that illuminate different aspects of the same phenomenon:

### 8.1 Schiffrin's Approach: Discourse Coherence
**Question it answers**: How do discourse markers create and maintain the coherence of conversation across multiple simultaneous dimensions?
**Strength**: Captures the full complexity and multifunctionality of discourse markers in natural conversation. Shows that markers integrate exchange structure, action structure, ideational structure, participation framework, and information state.
**Best for understanding**: Why a single marker like *well* can be doing five different things at once; how conversation stays coherent despite its surface messiness.

### 8.2 Fraser's Approach: Discourse Relations
**Question it answers**: What specific relationship does a discourse marker signal between adjacent discourse segments?
**Strength**: Provides clear, teachable categories (contrastive, elaborative, inferential, topic change) that are immediately applicable, especially in writing and formal discourse.
**Best for understanding**: The logical/rhetorical structure of how ideas connect to each other.

### 8.3 Blakemore's Approach: Processing Instructions
**Question it answers**: What kind of cognitive processing does a discourse marker instruct the listener to perform?
**Strength**: Explains *why* markers behave the way they do at a deeper cognitive level -- they are procedural, not conceptual, and they constrain the inferential process.
**Best for understanding**: Why discourse markers "don't mean anything" in the traditional sense but are nonetheless essential; the difference between giving someone content and giving someone instructions.

### 8.4 Integration for Autistic Learners

For the purpose of teaching pragmatics to autistic learners, all three frameworks contribute:

- **Schiffrin's planes** provide the analytical framework: when you encounter a discourse marker, ask "what is it doing on each plane?" This makes the multifunctionality systematic rather than bewildering.
- **Fraser's categories** provide the most immediately learnable taxonomy: learn to recognize contrastive, elaborative, inferential, and topic-change markers as a first-order classification system.
- **Blakemore's processing instructions** provide the deepest conceptual understanding: discourse markers are not meaningless filler -- they are instructions for how to process what you're hearing. This reframes them as something cognitively important that deserves attention, not as noise to be filtered out.

## 9. Properties of Discourse Markers: Summary

Drawing together findings from across the literature, discourse markers share these defining properties:

1. **Non-propositionality**: They do not contribute to the truth-conditional content of utterances
2. **Optionality** (syntactic): They can be removed without making the sentence ungrammatical
3. **Non-optionality** (pragmatic): Their removal changes what is communicated, even when it doesn't change what is said
4. **Positional preference for initiality**: They typically occur at the beginning of utterances or turns
5. **Prosodic independence**: They are set off from the rest of the utterance by intonation or pause
6. **Multifunctionality**: They typically serve multiple functions simultaneously
7. **Context dependence**: Their specific interpretation is shaped by the linguistic and situational context
8. **Multi-categoriality**: They are drawn from diverse grammatical sources (conjunctions, adverbs, prepositional phrases, verb phrases, interjections)
9. **Procedural meaning**: They encode instructions for processing rather than concepts for representation
10. **Connectivity**: They signal relationships between discourse units, creating coherence

## 10. Grammaticalization and the Origins of Discourse Markers

Discourse markers typically develop through a process of **grammaticalization** (or, as some researchers prefer, **pragmaticalization**) -- the historical process by which content words lose their original semantic meaning and acquire new grammatical or pragmatic functions.

- *Well* originates from the adverb/adjective meaning "in a good manner" -- over time, its discourse-marker use became decoupled from this original meaning
- *You know* originates from the literal second-person verb phrase "you know (this fact)" -- it has been bleached of its literal meaning and now functions as a solidarity marker and engagement device
- *I mean* originates from the literal first-person expression of intention -- it has been grammaticalized into a repair and clarification marker
- *Like* originates from the adjective/preposition of comparison -- it has developed into a hedge, quotative, and general discourse marker
- *So* retains its causal/consequential meaning in some uses but has developed additional discourse-organizing functions (topic launcher, summarizer, turn-claimer)

There is active debate about whether this process is best described as grammaticalization (the standard term for historical semantic bleaching and functional shift) or pragmaticalization (a separate process specific to the development of pragmatic functions). Some researchers view discourse markers as a "joint product" of grammaticalization and cooption. The theoretical terminology is unsettled, but the process itself is well-documented: content words lose semantic weight and gain pragmatic function.
