# Research Supplement 4: Demographic Updates, Intersectional Evidence, and Recent Replications

This supplement addresses seven areas identified by validators as needing updated data, corrected statistics, or new evidence: (1) racial/ethnic diagnostic disparities, (2) LGBTQ+ and autism statistics, (3) sex-stratified suicide risk, (4) children and masking, (5) late diagnosis research, (6) racial/cultural intersections with masking, and (7) the Crompton et al. 2025 replication study.

---

## 1. Updated Diagnostic Disparities Data

### 1.1 The Mandell 2007 Finding: Has It Been Superseded?

**Original Claim**: Mandell et al. (2007) found Black children were 2.6x less likely to receive an autism diagnosis than White children.

**Original Study Details**:
- Mandell, D. S., et al. (2007). "Disparities in diagnoses received prior to a diagnosis of autism spectrum disorder." *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 37(9), 1795-1802.
- Sample: 406 Medicaid-eligible children
- Methodology: Medicaid claims data analysis, 1993-1999
- Key findings: African-American children were 2.6x less likely than White children to receive an ASD diagnosis on their first specialty visit. Black children were 5.1x more likely to be diagnosed with adjustment disorder and 2.4x more likely to be diagnosed with conduct disorder prior to ASD diagnosis.
- DOI: 10.1007/s10803-006-0314-8
- [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17160456/)

**Current Status (2020-2025): The Disparity Has Reversed in Prevalence but Persists in Other Forms**

The CDC ADDM Network data from 2020 and 2022 show a dramatic shift. For the first time, autism prevalence is now *higher* among Black, Hispanic, and Asian/Pacific Islander children than among White children, reversing the historical pattern documented by Mandell.

**CDC ADDM 2020 Data (published March 2023)**:
- Overall U.S. prevalence: 1 in 36 (2.8%) among 8-year-olds
- Asian/Pacific Islander: 3.3%
- Hispanic: 3.2%
- Black: 2.9%
- White: 2.4%
- Source: Maenner, M. J., et al. (2023). "Prevalence and characteristics of autism spectrum disorder among children aged 8 years." *MMWR Surveillance Summaries*, 72(SS-2), 1-14.
- [CDC MMWR](https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/volumes/72/ss/ss7202a1.htm)

**CDC ADDM 2022 Data (published April 2025)**:
- Overall U.S. prevalence: 1 in 31 (3.2%) among 8-year-olds
- Asian: 3.82%
- Black: 3.66%
- Hispanic: 3.30%
- White: 2.77%
- Source: CDC Community Report on Autism 2025. [CDC PDF](https://www.cdc.gov/autism/media/pdfs/2025/04/ADDM-Community-Report-SY2022.pdf)
- [Johns Hopkins Press Release](https://publichealth.jhu.edu/2025/new-cdc-report-shows-increase-in-autism-in-2022-with-notable-shifts-in-race-ethnicity-and-sex)

**Interpretation**: The reversal in prevalence rates is generally interpreted as progress toward more equitable identification, NOT as an increase in actual autism prevalence among minority groups. However, significant disparities persist in other domains:

### 1.2 Persistent Disparities Despite Prevalence Equalization

**Diagnostic Age Disparities**:
- Black children have historically been diagnosed later than White children, though the gap is narrowing
- Median age at diagnosis across racial groups ranges from 4.4-4.9 years (as of most recent data)
- Asian children have the youngest mean age of diagnosis (4.49 years); White children the oldest (4.97 years)
- Source: Constantino, J. N., et al. (2022). "Racial and ethnic differences in rates and age of diagnosis of autism spectrum disorder." *JAMA Network Open*, 5(10), e2239604.
- DOI: 10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2022.39604
- [JAMA Network Open](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2797991)

**Intellectual Disability Co-Diagnosis Disparities**:
- Black children with ASD: 50.8% also have co-occurring intellectual disability
- Hispanic children with ASD: 34.9%
- White children with ASD: 31.8%
- This disparity suggests that Black children may still be preferentially identified only when their autism is accompanied by more "visible" cognitive impairment.
- Source: CDC ADDM Community Report 2025

**Misdiagnosis Patterns**:
- Black children continue to be more likely to receive an initial diagnosis of conduct disorder or ADHD before receiving an accurate ASD diagnosis
- In a study of >260,000 children under 8, up to one-quarter were undiagnosed, with most being Black or Latinx
- Source: Constantino et al. (2022), JAMA Network Open (above); [Child Mind Institute](https://childmind.org/article/why-do-black-children-with-autism-get-diagnosed-late/)

**Service Access Disparities**:
- Black children receive lower rates of outpatient services (5.2%) than White children, despite higher prevalence
- Higher median household income (MHI) is still associated with higher ASD prevalence among Asian/PI, Black, and Hispanic children (but not White children), suggesting SES-mediated access barriers persist
- Source: CDC ADDM 2020 data stratified by race and MHI

### 1.3 Socioeconomic Status and Diagnosis

**Historical Pattern**: From 2002-2010, robust associations existed between higher SES and higher autism prevalence (reflecting diagnostic access, not true prevalence differences).

**Current Pattern (2020-2025)**: The SES-prevalence association has become "much more variable" and is no longer consistently observed in the most recent ADDM data, suggesting improved but incomplete equalization of diagnostic access.

- Source: Durkin, M. S., et al. (2021). "Racial, ethnic, and sociodemographic disparities in diagnosis of children with autism spectrum disorder." *Journal of Developmental and Behavioral Pediatrics*, 42(8), 682-689. DOI: 10.1097/DBP.0000000000000996
- [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8500365/)

### 1.4 Commentary on Systemic Issues

Jones & Mandell (2020) argued that addressing racial disparities in autism research requires recruiting, developing, and supporting Black students and trainees to create the next generation of scientists and practitioners. They noted that the dearth of Black scientists and clinicians likely has contributed to health disparities among Black autistic people.

- Jones, D. R., & Mandell, D. S. (2020). "To address racial disparities in autism research, we must think globally, act locally." *Autism*, 24(7), 1587-1589. DOI: 10.1177/1362361320948313
- [SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/1362361320948313)

## 2. LGBTQ+ and Autism -- Corrected Statistics

### 2.1 Warrier et al. (2020) -- What It Actually Found

**Study Details**:
- Warrier, V., et al. (2020). "Elevated rates of autism, other neurodevelopmental and psychiatric diagnoses, and autistic traits in transgender and gender-diverse individuals." *Nature Communications*, 11, 3959.
- DOI: 10.1038/s41467-020-17794-1
- Sample: Five datasets totaling 641,860 people (mostly adults); 30,892 had autism; 3,777 identified as gender-diverse. Majority of data (~514,000) came from a 2017 British television documentary online survey.
- [Nature Communications](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-020-17794-1)

**Key Findings**:
- Autistic people were more likely to be gender-diverse; gender-diverse people were more likely to be autistic
- Transgender adults were 3.03-6.36x more likely to be autistic than cisgender people (depending on dataset)
- Gender-diverse people showed lower empathizing and higher systemizing compared to cisgender people
- Gender-diverse people had higher rates of all six examined conditions (autism, ADHD, depression, schizophrenia, and two others), with the highest association for autism and depression

**Important Limitation**: This study focused on gender diversity, not sexual orientation broadly. It should not be cited as a source for LGBTQ+ prevalence rates among autistic people as a whole.

### 2.2 George & Stokes (2018) -- Sexual Orientation Study

**Study Details**:
- George, R., & Stokes, M. A. (2018). "Sexual orientation in autism spectrum disorder." *Autism Research*, 11(1), 133-141.
- DOI: 10.1002/aur.1892
- Sample: 309 individuals with ASD (mean age 32.3) and 310 typically developing controls (mean age 29.8), international online sample
- Instrument: Sell Scale of Sexual Orientation
- [Wiley](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/aur.1892)

**Key Findings**:
- ASD group: 69.7% reported non-heterosexual orientation
- TD group: 30.3% reported non-heterosexual orientation
- Higher rates of homosexuality, bisexuality, and asexuality in ASD group
- Finding was statistically significant (P < 0.001)

**Critical Limitation**: The unusually high non-heterosexual rate in the TD control group (30.3% vs. ~7% in general population surveys) suggests significant self-selection bias in this online convenience sample. The absolute percentages should not be treated as population prevalence estimates.

### 2.3 Best Available Prevalence Estimates

**SPARK Cohort Studies (Largest Population-Based Data)**:
- 41.2% of 651 autistic adults identified as sexual minority (LGBQ+) in one SPARK cohort
- 40% of 980 autistic adults aged 18-46 identified as LGBTQ+ in a second SPARK cohort
- General population comparison: ~7% (2022 Gallup)
- Source: [SPARK for Autism](https://sparkforautism.org/discover_article/autism-lgbtq-identity/)

**Weir et al. (2021) -- Largest Sexual Orientation Study**:
- Weir, E., et al. (2021). "The sexual health, orientation, and activity of autistic adolescents and adults." *Autism Research*, 14(11), 2342-2354.
- DOI: 10.1002/aur.2604
- Sample: 2,386 adults (1,183 autistic), aged 16-90
- Key findings: Autistic individuals less likely to report heterosexuality; autistic males 3.5x more likely to identify as bisexual; autistic females 3x more likely to identify as homosexual; autistic individuals 8.1x more likely to identify as asexual
- [Wiley](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/aur.2604)

**Corrected Statement**: Replace "52% of autistic women are LGBTQ+" with: "Large cohort studies (SPARK) find approximately 40-41% of autistic adults identify as LGBTQ+, compared to ~7% of the general population. Autistic individuals are 3-8x more likely to identify as bisexual, homosexual, or asexual compared to non-autistic peers (Weir et al., 2021)."

### 2.4 Trans/Nonbinary Autism Prevalence -- Current Best Estimates

**Meta-Analytic Data (2024)**:
- A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis examining studies published 2013-2023 (24 studies, 14 with usable data from 3,894 autistic participants) found approximately 7.37% of autistic people identify as gender-diverse (95% CI: 4.45%-11.98%)
- Conversely, the rate of autism in transgender individuals is estimated at 6-26%, compared to ~1.85% in the general population
- Source: "Gender on the Spectrum: Prevalence of Gender Diversity in Autism Spectrum Disorder -- A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis." *Autism in Adulthood*, 2024. DOI: 10.1089/aut.2024.0202
- [Liebert Pub](https://www.liebertpub.com/doi/10.1089/aut.2024.0202)

**NPR/Transmitter Summary**: Transgender and nonbinary people are up to 6x more likely to have autism than cisgender people.
- [NPR](https://www.npr.org/2023/01/15/1149318664/transgender-and-non-binary-people-are-up-to-six-times-more-likely-to-have-autism)
- [The Transmitter](https://www.thetransmitter.org/spectrum/largest-study-to-date-confirms-overlap-between-autism-and-gender-diversity/)

### 2.5 McQuaid et al. (2025) -- Sexual Minority Masking

**Study Details**:
- McQuaid, G. A., Lee, N. R., & Wallace, G. L. (2025). "Self-reported masking in sexual minority and heterosexual autistic adults." *Autism*. DOI: 10.1177/13623613251335738
- Sample: N=462 autistic adults (ages 18-50) from SPARK, without intellectual disability
- Instrument: Camouflaging Autistic Traits Questionnaire (CAT-Q)
- [SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/13623613251335738)
- [SPARK Summary](https://sparkforautism.org/discover_article/rm-identity-masking/)

**Key Findings**:
- Sexual minority autistic adults reported significantly higher levels of masking than heterosexual autistic adults
- Adults assigned female at birth masked significantly more than those assigned male at birth
- This is believed to be the first study to compare masking by sexual identity in autistic adults

**Theoretical Significance**: The study applies an intersectional framework, examining how persons who are "multiply marginalized" (e.g., autistic + female-assigned + bisexual) may face compounding pressures to conceal authentic identity across multiple dimensions simultaneously.

## 3. Sex-Stratified Suicide Risk -- Corrected Figures

### 3.1 Hirvikoski et al. (2016) -- Actual Standardized Mortality Data

**Study Details**:
- Hirvikoski, T., et al. (2016). "Premature mortality in autism spectrum disorder." *British Journal of Psychiatry*, 208(3), 232-238.
- DOI: 10.1192/bjp.bp.114.160192
- Sample: 27,122 autistic individuals matched with 2,672,185 general population controls, Swedish national registers
- [Cambridge Core](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/the-british-journal-of-psychiatry/article/premature-mortality-in-autism-spectrum-disorder/4C9260DB64DFC29AF945D32D1C15E8F2)

**Overall Mortality**: During the observation period, 706 (2.60%) of ASD individuals died vs. 24,358 (0.91%) of controls. Overall mortality OR = 2.56 (95% CI: 2.38-2.76).

**Suicide-Specific Findings**:
- Overall ASD suicide mortality OR = 7.55 (compared to matched general population)
- ASD *without* co-occurring intellectual disability: OR = 9.40 (95% CI: 7.43-11.9)
- ASD *with* co-occurring intellectual disability: OR = 2.41 (95% CI: 1.14-5.11)

**Sex-Stratified Mortality Data**:
- Raw suicide rates: 0.32% (27) of autistic women died by suicide vs. 0.03% (213) of non-autistic women; 0.30% (56) of autistic men died by suicide vs. 0.05% (881) of non-autistic men
- Overall mortality ORs by sex and ID status:
  - Females with ASD + ID: OR = 8.52 (all-cause mortality)
  - Males with ASD + ID: OR = 4.88 (all-cause mortality)
  - Females with ASD only (no ID): OR = 1.88 (all-cause mortality)
  - Males with ASD only (no ID): OR = 2.49 (all-cause mortality)
- For suicide specifically, females with ASD had higher *relative* mortality risk compared to same-sex controls than males with ASD had compared to same-sex controls

**Corrected Statement**: Replace "8x for women" with: "Hirvikoski et al. (2016) found an overall suicide mortality OR of 7.55 for autistic people. When stratified by intellectual disability, autistic people without ID were 9.40x more likely to die by suicide. The relative increase in suicide risk was proportionally greater for autistic females compared to non-autistic females than for autistic males compared to non-autistic males."

### 3.2 Kirby et al. (2019) -- U.S. Population-Based Study

**Study Details**:
- Kirby, A. V., et al. (2019). "A 20-year study of suicide death in a statewide autism population." *Autism Research*, 12(4), 658-666.
- DOI: 10.1002/aur.2076
- Sample: Statewide Utah population, 1998-2017, observed in 5-year intervals
- First U.S. population-based study of suicide in autism
- [Wiley](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/aur.2076)

**Key Findings**:
- In the final study period, cumulative suicide incidence among ASD individuals was significantly higher (0.17%) than non-ASD peers (0.11%)
- Female-specific risk: RR = 3.42 (P < 0.01) -- females with ASD were 3.42x more likely to die by suicide than females without ASD
- ASD individuals less likely to use firearms (adjusted OR = 0.33, P < 0.001)
- Age at death ranged from 14-70 years (M = 32.41, SD = 15.98)

### 3.3 Kirby et al. (2024) -- The Call for Clarity

**Study Details**:
- Kirby, A. V., et al. (2024). "Are autistic females at greater risk of suicide? A call for clarity to advance suicide prevention for the whole community." *Autism Research*, 17(4), 707-711.
- DOI: 10.1002/aur.3120
- [Wiley](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/aur.3120)

**Critical Clarification**:
This paper addresses widespread miscommunication about sex differences in autistic suicide risk. The key points:

1. Five population-based studies consistently show that autistic people (both male and female) are at greater risk of suicide than non-autistic counterparts
2. These same studies consistently show that within the autistic population, **autistic males and females have similar rates of dying by suicide** -- unlike the general population where males die by suicide at higher rates
3. The confusion arises from comparing autistic females to non-autistic females (large relative increase) vs. comparing autistic females to autistic males (similar rates)
4. The "greater risk for autistic women" framing is based on the relative comparison to non-autistic same-sex peers, NOT a comparison between autistic men and autistic women

**Correct Framing**: "Both autistic males and females die by suicide at elevated rates compared to non-autistic same-sex peers. The relative increase is proportionally greater for females (because the female baseline rate is much lower in the general population), but absolute suicide rates among autistic males and females are similar."

### 3.4 Citation Context Analysis -- Misrepresentation of Hirvikoski

A 2024 JAMA Network Open analysis documented widespread misrepresentation of the Hirvikoski (2016) findings:
- High-profile publications and government reports claimed "people with autism die 16 years earlier" or have a "54-year life expectancy," citing Hirvikoski et al.
- However, less than 3% of the autistic cohort died during the observation period, making life expectancy calculations impossible from these data
- The misrepresentation has potential repercussions: psychological distress, altered retirement planning, insurance denials, and exacerbated health disparities
- Source: "Citation context analysis of autism mortality and suicide findings from Hirvikoski's landmark study." *JAMA Network Open*, 2024.
- [JAMA Network Open](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2830153)

### 3.5 Recent Updates (2023-2025)

**2023 Meta-Analysis**:
- Estimated the relative risk of suicide death among autistic people at 2.85x the population baseline
- Found no consistent evidence for sex differences in risk of suicide death within the autistic population
- Source: "A systematic review and meta-analysis of suicidality in autistic and possibly autistic people without co-occurring intellectual disability." *Molecular Autism*, 2023.
- [Springer Nature](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13229-023-00544-7)

**2025 Research on Midlife/Older Adults**:
- Depression, anxiety, PTSD, loneliness, and social isolation all significantly mediate the relationship between autistic traits and suicidal ideation in middle-aged and older adults
- Source: "Autistic traits and suicidality in midlife and old age." *Nature Mental Health*, 2025.
- [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44220-025-00579-0)

## 4. Children and Masking

### 4.1 Dean et al. (2017) -- Verified and Expanded

**Study Details**:
- Dean, M., Harwood, R., & Kasari, C. (2017). "The art of camouflage: Gender differences in the social behaviors of girls and boys with autism spectrum disorder." *Autism*, 21(6), 678-689.
- DOI: 10.1177/1362361316671845
- Methodology: Observational study of playground behavior in 7-year-old children
- [SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1362361316671845)

**Key Findings (Verified)**:
- Autistic girls tended to stay near peers without fully engaging, weaving in and out of activities -- appearing similar to neurotypical girls
- Autistic boys spent most time alone, while neurotypical boys played games together
- Girls (not boys) with autism appeared similarly to neurotypical counterparts, providing evidence of greater behavioral camouflaging in autistic girls
- If practitioners look for social isolation on playgrounds to identify social challenges, autistic girls will continue to be missed

**Significance**: This remains one of the earliest and most cited demonstrations that sex differences in masking are observable in children as young as 7.

### 4.2 Newer Studies on Masking in Autistic Children

**Jorgenson et al. (2020) -- Adolescents**:
- "Social camouflaging in autistic and neurotypical adolescents: A pilot study of differences by sex and diagnosis." *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 50, 4344-4355.
- DOI: 10.1007/s10803-020-04491-7
- Found females reported higher overall camouflaging levels; an age-by-diagnosis interaction emerged when controlling for age
- Differences emerged on masking and assimilation subscales specifically
- [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10803-020-04491-7)

**Halsall, Clarke, & Crane (2021) -- Adolescent Girls**:
- Examined camouflaging in adolescent autistic girls in mainstream and specialist school settings
- Girls reported that they stopped camouflaging when returning home from school
- Camouflaging was more prevalent in mainstream than specialist settings
- Source: "'Camouflaging' by adolescent autistic girls who attend both mainstream and specialist resource classes." *Autism*, 25(7), 2074-2086. DOI: 10.1177/13623613211012819
- [SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/13623613211012819)

**Lei, Leigh, Charman, Russell, & Hollocks (2024)**:
- Explored the association between camouflaging and self-vs. caregiver-report discrepancies in anxiety and depression in autistic and non-autistic socially anxious adolescents
- Found camouflaging may explain why caregiver reports underestimate internalizing symptoms in autistic adolescents
- Source: *Autism*, 2024. DOI: 10.1177/13623613241238251
- [SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/13623613241238251)

### 4.3 Developmental Trajectory of Masking

**When Does Masking Begin?**
- No study has identified a precise developmental onset age for masking
- Dean et al. (2017) documented masking-like behaviors at age 7, the youngest age with observational evidence
- The CAT-Q has not been validated for children under 15, limiting quantitative measurement in younger populations
- Qualitative research suggests children, adolescents, and adults all engage in camouflaging "perhaps to varying degrees"

**Does It Increase or Decrease Over Time?**
- Age-related fluctuations in camouflaging likely relate to changes in cognitive development, social demands, and mental health across the lifespan
- Individuals diagnosed in adulthood report higher levels of assimilation and compensation compared to those diagnosed in childhood/adolescence, suggesting later diagnosis may intensify camouflaging trajectories
- Van der Putten et al. (2025) found that in older autistic adults, camouflaging levels did NOT predict worsening mental health over a 2-year period, and higher baseline camouflaging was associated with a small *decrease* in mental health difficulties over time
- Source: van der Putten, W. J., et al. (2025). "Camouflaging in autism: A cause or a consequence of mental health difficulties?" *Autism*. DOI: 10.1177/13623613251347104
- [SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/13623613251347104)

**Research Gaps**: There are currently no published studies exploring camouflaging with interview data from children aged 10 and younger, representing a critical gap. Posserud et al. (2021) documented that the male-to-female autism diagnosis ratio shifts from 1:4 in 4-10-year-olds to 1:2 in adults, which may reflect increasing masking effectiveness in females across development.

## 5. Late Diagnosis Research

### 5.1 Bargiela et al. (2016) -- Contextualized with Larger Studies

**Original Study**:
- Bargiela, S., Steward, R., & Mandy, W. (2016). "The experiences of late-diagnosed women with autism spectrum conditions: An investigation of the female autism phenotype." *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 46(10), 3281-3294.
- DOI: 10.1007/s10803-016-2872-8
- Sample: n=14 women aged 22-30, diagnosed in late adolescence or adulthood
- Key finding: 9 of 14 (64%) reported rape by romantic partner (n=7) and/or strangers (n=3)
- [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10803-016-2872-8)

**Limitations**: The n=14 sample is acknowledged as very small. This was a qualitative IPA (Interpretative Phenomenological Analysis) study -- appropriate for deep qualitative exploration but not for prevalence estimation.

### 5.2 Larger Studies on Sexual Violence and Autism

**Cazalis et al. (2022) -- Largest Direct Study**:
- Cazalis, F., Reyes, E., Leduc, S., & Gourion, D. (2022). "Evidence that nine autistic women out of ten have been victims of sexual violence." *Frontiers in Behavioral Neuroscience*, 16, 852203.
- DOI: 10.3389/fnbeh.2022.852203
- Sample: N=225 women from the French autistic community
- Methodology: Online survey with both open-ended and standardized questionnaire items
- Key findings: Open question identified victimization in 68.9%; standardized questionnaire identified 88.4%; 75% had been assaulted multiple times; two-thirds were under 18 at first assault
- [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/behavioral-neuroscience/articles/10.3389/fnbeh.2022.852203/full)

**Systematic Review Evidence**:
- A 2022 meta-analysis across 34 studies estimated ~40% of autistic adults have experienced sexual violence in their lifetime
- General population comparison: ~30% of women experience sexual violence
- Autistic women face a 2-3x increased rate of victimization
- Source: "A systematic review of sexual violence among autistic individuals." *Review Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 2022.
- [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40489-022-00310-0)

**Corrected Statement**: Rather than citing the Bargiela n=14 study for prevalence, use: "The largest direct study (Cazalis et al., 2022, n=225) found that 69-88% of autistic women report experiencing sexual violence (depending on measurement method), with 75% assaulted multiple times. A meta-analysis of 34 studies estimated ~40% lifetime prevalence of sexual violence among autistic adults overall, representing a 2-3x increase over general population rates."

### 5.3 Mental Health Consequences of Late Diagnosis

**Key Findings from Recent Research**:
- Women diagnosed with autism in adulthood are more likely to experience anxiety, depression, eating disorders, and substance use issues than those diagnosed in childhood
- Many women spend years masking symptoms, leading to identity confusion, chronic stress, and burnout
- Without understanding their underlying autistic profile, women often receive fragmented care treating individual symptoms rather than the root condition ("diagnostic overshadowing")
- Source: [Simply Psychology](https://www.simplypsychology.org/autistic-women-diagnosed-in-adulthood-face-higher-mental-health-challenges.html)

**Large-Scale Systematic Review**:
- A 2025 review of studies from CINAHL and PsycInfo (2004-2024) screened 8,420 records with 218 meeting inclusion criteria
- Found higher rates of mental health conditions in autistic females than males for anxiety, mood, eating, obsessive-compulsive, psychotic, and personality disorders
- Source: "Disproportionate mental health risks in autistic females." *MedRxiv*, 2025.
- [MedRxiv](https://www.medrxiv.org/content/10.1101/2025.09.12.25335643v1.full.pdf)

### 5.4 Russell et al. (2025) -- Systematic Review of "Late Diagnosis"

**Study Details**:
- Russell, A. S., et al. (2025). "Who, when, where, and why: A systematic review of 'late diagnosis' in autism." *Autism Research*, 18(1), e3278.
- DOI: 10.1002/aur.3278
- Scope: 420 studies spanning 35 years (1989-2024), participants from every continent except Antarctica
- [Wiley](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/aur.3278)

**Key Findings**:
- No consensus exists on what constitutes "late diagnosis" -- cutoffs ranged from 2 to 55 years
- Average late diagnosis cutoff: 11.5 years; most common cutoffs: 3 and 18 years (tied)
- Only 34.7% of studies provided a clear threshold for "late diagnosis"
- Threshold varied by participant location and sample age

### 5.5 Lai & Baron-Cohen (2015) -- The "Lost Generation"

**Study Details**:
- Lai, M.-C., & Baron-Cohen, S. (2015). "Identifying the lost generation of adults with autism spectrum conditions." *The Lancet Psychiatry*, 2(11), 1013-1027.
- Proposed the concept of a "female autism phenotype" -- female-specific manifestation of autism that fits imperfectly with male-based conceptualizations
- Argued that women are particularly vulnerable to late/missed diagnosis due to better-than-expected social functioning, reduced externalizing behaviors, and greater internalization
- Proposed that camouflaging strategies (suppressing physical characteristics, giving scripted responses, imitating facial expressions) contribute to diagnostic overshadowing

## 6. Racial and Cultural Intersections with Masking

### 6.1 Do Masking Patterns Differ Across Racial/Cultural Groups?

**Current Evidence**: Research on this topic is extremely limited, but emerging evidence suggests important differences:

**Black Autistic Girls in UK Education (2025)**:
- Using IPA, intersectionality theory, and Disability Critical Race Theory (DisCrit), researchers found that for Black autistic girls, masking involves layers of complexity shaped by the simultaneous intersection of autism, race, and gender
- Participants reported: "Before people see the autism, they see my race"
- Source: "The lived experiences of masking black Autistic girls in UK education." *Educational Psychology in Practice*, 2025.
- [Taylor & Francis](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/02667363.2025.2541211)

**African American/Black Young Adults (2023)**:
- Intersectionality was reflected in 12-30% of reported reasons for discrimination
- Stressful and potentially traumatic events were nuanced for autistic AA/B people based on the co-occurrence of racial and disability experiences
- Source: "Examination of race and autism intersectionality among African American/Black young adults." *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 2023.
- [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9908282/)

**Racial Disparities in Depression and Mental Health Service Use (2025)**:
- Study published in Scientific Reports examined differences in depression and mental health service access between Black and White autistic adults
- Source: [Nature Scientific Reports](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-34696-8)

### 6.2 Code-Switching and Double Masking

Black autistic individuals often engage in both **code-switching** (adjusting behaviors to mirror dominant racial norms) and **masking** of autistic characteristics -- what some researchers describe as "double masking." Both forms of concealment are associated with increased mental distress and depressive symptoms.

The concept of "neurotypical privilege" (Botha, 2022) intersects with racial privilege in complex ways. White-passing autistic people are cautioned to "think more carefully about telling people of color to #TakeTheMaskOff," as unmasking advice differs across racial contexts. For autistic people of color, being recognized as visibly neurodivergent may carry additional safety risks beyond those faced by White autistic people.

- Source: Botha, M. (2022). "Conceptualising autistic masking, camouflaging, and neurotypical privilege: Towards a minority group model of neurodiversity." *Human Development*, 66(2), 113-127. DOI: 10.1159/000524122
- [Karger](https://karger.com/hde/article/66/2/113/828423/Conceptualising-Autistic-Masking-Camouflaging-and)

### 6.3 Masking in Non-Western Cultural Contexts

**Japan-UK Cross-Cultural Study (2023)**:
- Atherton, G., et al. (2023). "The association between social camouflage and mental health among autistic people in Japan and the UK: A cross-cultural study." *Molecular Autism*, 14, 47.
- DOI: 10.1186/s13229-023-00579-w
- Sample: Japan N=210 (123 men, 87 women); UK N=305 (181 women, 104 men, 18 nonbinary)
- [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13229-023-00579-w)

**Key Findings**:
- UK sample: Linear relationship -- more camouflaging = poorer mental health
- Japan sample: Nonlinear (U-shaped) -- both too little AND too much camouflaging were associated with poorer mental health; moderate camouflaging was associated with *better* mental health
- Japanese autistic people have fewer opportunities to learn about autistic traits and feel they "do not fit in with society"
- Japanese culture's emphasis on "reading between the lines" (implicit communication) creates particular challenges for autistic people who struggle with decoding implicit social rules

**Eight-Country Study (2024)**:
- Registered report comparing autism acceptance, camouflaging, and mental health across Australia, Belgium, Canada, Japan, New Zealand, South Africa, UK, and USA
- Japanese participants were most likely to conceal autistic traits, connected to collectivist cultural norms
- South African participants showed highest mental health difficulties, possibly due to barriers in accessing support
- Lack of acceptance linked to depression; camouflaging linked to depression, anxiety, and stress across all countries
- Source: "Cross-cultural variation in experiences of acceptance, camouflaging and mental health difficulties in autism: A registered report." *PLOS ONE*, 2024. DOI: 10.1371/journal.pone.0299824
- [PLOS ONE](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0299824)

**Cultural Diagnostic Bias**:
- Autism could be both overdiagnosed AND underdiagnosed in Asian patients based on DSM-5 criteria: traits normal in Asian culture (e.g., avoiding eye contact) could be misread as impairments, while actual autism indicators might be dismissed as "being Asian"
- In many Asian cultures, direct eye contact is considered disrespectful rather than a social necessity, potentially masking a diagnostic indicator or creating false positives
- Source: [Scientific American Blog](https://blogs.scientificamerican.com/guest-blog/how-cultural-differences-affect-autism-diagnoses/)

### 6.4 Minority Stress Models Applied to Autism

**Botha & Frost (2020) -- Foundational Study**:
- Botha, M., & Frost, D. M. (2020). "Extending the minority stress model to understand mental health problems experienced by the autistic population." *Society and Mental Health*, 10(1), 20-34.
- DOI: 10.1177/2156869318804297
- Sample: N=111 high-functioning autistic individuals
- Key finding: Minority stressors (everyday discrimination, internalized stigma, concealment) significantly predicted poorer mental health, even controlling for general stress
- [SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2156869318804297)

**Perry et al. (2022) -- Camouflaging as Stigma Response**:
- Reframed camouflaging through a Social Identity Theory lens
- Autistic people viewed camouflaging as a defensive strategy protecting from discrimination
- Being recognized as a visible neurominority described as a risk factor for physical violence
- Source: "Understanding camouflaging as a response to autism-related stigma: A social identity theory approach." *JADD*, 52, 800-810. DOI: 10.1007/s10803-021-04987-w
- [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8813820/)

**Trans/Nonbinary Autistic Adults -- Intersecting Minority Stresses**:
- Minority stress, camouflaging, and mental health outcomes interact in complex ways for transgender/nonbinary autistic adults
- Source: "Minority stress, camouflaging, and mental health outcomes in transgender and/or non-binary autistic adults." *Autism in Adulthood*, 2024. DOI: 10.1089/aut.2023.0151
- [Liebert Pub](https://www.liebertpub.com/doi/10.1089/aut.2023.0151)

### 6.5 Research Gaps in Racial/Cultural Masking

Critical gaps identified across sources:
- Black autistic individuals have been largely absent from masking research
- Most camouflaging research lacks racial/ethnic diversity in samples
- No large-scale quantitative studies specifically compare masking levels across racial groups
- Intersection of racial code-switching and autistic masking is theoretically discussed but empirically understudied
- Non-Western masking research exists for Japan only; data from Africa, Latin America, Middle East, South/Southeast Asia is absent
- Source: "The scholarly neglect of Black autistic adults in autism research." *JADD*, 2023. [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9908289/)

## 7. Crompton et al. (2025) -- Registered Replication

### 7.1 The Original Study (2020)

**Study Details**:
- Crompton, C. J., Ropar, D., Evans-Williams, C. V. M., Flynn, E. G., & Fletcher-Watson, S. (2020). "Autistic peer-to-peer information transfer is highly effective." *Autism*, 24(7), 1704-1712.
- DOI: 10.1177/1362361320919286
- Sample: N=72, 9 diffusion chains of 8 people each, single geographic location (Scotland)
- [SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/1362361320919286)

**Original Findings**:
- Autistic chains did not significantly differ from non-autistic chains in information retention
- Mixed (autistic + non-autistic) chains showed significantly steeper decline in information fidelity
- Rapport ratings were significantly lower in mixed chains
- Supported the "double empathy problem" (Milton, 2012)

**Original Limitations**: Modest sample (n=72), single geographic location, unbalanced gender distribution

### 7.2 The 2025 Registered Replication

**Study Details**:
- Crompton, C. J., et al. (2025). "Information transfer within and between autistic and non-autistic people." *Nature Human Behaviour*, 9, 1488-1500.
- DOI: 10.1038/s41562-025-02163-z
- Published online: May 14, 2025; print: July 2025
- Sample: N=311, data collected across three independent sites (Scotland, England, USA)
- Pre-registered as a Registered Report
- [Nature Human Behaviour](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41562-025-02163-z)

**Methodology**:
- Diffusion chain technique with both fictional story and factual information passage
- Some participants told whether partner was autistic/non-autistic; others not told
- Substantially increased sample size across geographically diverse sites (2 of 3 independent from original)

### 7.3 Key Results -- Partial Replication with Important Differences

**Information Transfer** (DID NOT REPLICATE ORIGINAL):
- **No difference** was found in information transfer between single-neurotype and mixed-neurotype chains
- Autistic groups, non-autistic groups, and mixed groups all shared information equally well
- No difference in performance when sharing fictional vs. factual information
- Knowing whether partner was autistic or not did NOT change information transfer performance

**Rapport** (PARTIALLY REPLICATED):
- Non-autistic chains showed higher rapport than other chain types (partially replicating original)
- Disclosing diagnosis improved rapport (new finding)
- The pattern was more nuanced than the original binary finding

### 7.4 Why Results Differed

The authors attribute the differences to the replication's greater sample heterogeneity:
- Wider range of ages, genders, and ethnicities
- Autistic participants had wide range of IQ scores and different diagnosis ages
- Participants drawn from multiple sites across three nations
- The original sample's relative homogeneity may have amplified the mixed-chain effect

### 7.5 Implications for Double Empathy Theory

The replication both supports and complicates the double empathy problem:
- **Supports**: Communication differences between autistic and non-autistic people are real and measurable (rapport findings)
- **Complicates**: Information transfer itself may not be as impaired in mixed-neurotype interactions as originally suggested; the barrier may be more about rapport and social connection than about information exchange per se
- The finding that disclosure of diagnosis improved rapport suggests that awareness and accommodation, not neurotype matching, may be the key variable
