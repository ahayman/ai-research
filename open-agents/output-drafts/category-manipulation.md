## How to Read This Document

Every other category in this hub teaches you how the pragmatic system works. This one teaches you what to do when someone uses it against you.

The central problem is not that autistic people struggle to communicate. The central problem is that those struggles are **weaponized**. Your directness is called rudeness -- and then your message is dismissed. Your flat affect is called coldness -- and then your pain is denied. Your diagnosis is called a credibility deficit -- and then your perceptions are overridden. The pragmatic gap between your communication style and neurotypical expectations isn't just a gap. It's an attack surface.

Three things before we begin:

1. **Not every negative interaction is manipulation.** Genuine miscommunication between autistic and non-autistic people is common, bidirectional, and often nobody's fault (Milton, 2012). This document includes a framework for telling the difference.

2. **You are not naive.** The research on autistic vulnerability doesn't mean you lack intelligence. It means the detection systems most people rely on -- gut feelings, tone reading, facial microexpressions -- operate differently in autistic cognition. This document provides alternative detection systems that work with your strengths: pattern recognition, evidence tracking, logical analysis.

3. **Knowledge is armor, not anxiety.** Some of these patterns will be familiar. That recognition is data, not damage.

## 1. Why Autistic People Are Targeted

### 1.1 The Scale of the Problem

The research is unambiguous:

| Finding | Source |
|---------|--------|
| Up to 88.4% of autistic women report sexual violence | Cazalis et al., 2022, *Frontiers in Behavioral Neuroscience* |
| 84% of autistic adults report victimization across multiple categories | Cooke et al., 2025, meta-analysis |
| ~50% of autistic adults report being tricked into giving away money or possessions | VEQ study, Griffiths et al., 2019 |
| 60-70% of autistic employees experience workplace bullying (vs. 15-20% general population) | Specialisterne; systematic reviews |
| Autistic adults have 4x the PTSD rate of the general population | Multiple studies |

These are not statistics about deficiency. These are statistics about targeting.

### 1.2 The Pragmatic Attack Surface

Every pragmatic system this hub covers creates a vulnerability when someone acts in bad faith:

| System | How It's Weaponized |
|--------|-------------------|
| **Literal processing (Cat 2)** | "I never said that" -- denying the implicature you correctly identified |
| **Tone/prosody (Cat 14-15)** | "You don't even sound like you care" -- using your delivery to dismiss your content |
| **Politeness norms (Cat 3)** | "That was so rude" -- framing directness as rudeness to avoid your point |
| **Common ground (Cat 16)** | "We all agreed to this" -- claiming shared understanding that never existed |
| **Hierarchy (Cat 22)** | Exploiting your difficulty reading implicit power dynamics |
| **Register (Cat 7)** | "They can't even talk to clients properly" -- using register mismatch as evidence of incompetence |
| **Implicature (Cat 2)** | You miss an implied commitment, then are told you "agreed" to something never made explicit |

### 1.3 Epistemic Injustice: The Credibility Discount

Philosopher Miranda Fricker (2007) identified two forms of injustice that are directly relevant:

**Testimonial injustice:** Your credibility is deflated because of who you are. Research confirms this: autistic adults are perceived as more deceptive and less credible than neurotypical adults *when telling the truth* (Lim, Young & Brewer, 2022). Your different eye contact, prosody, and body language trigger neurotypical "deception detectors" that were calibrated for neurotypical behavior. You are telling the truth and being read as lying.

**Hermeneutical injustice:** The concepts needed to describe your experience don't exist in mainstream frameworks. When you explain sensory overload to a doctor who has no framework for it, or autistic burnout to an employer who thinks you're "not trying," you face hermeneutical injustice.

Your diagnosis can function as a credibility discount in every context where it's known. This is not a reason to hide it. It's a reason to understand the landscape and plan accordingly.

## 2. Six Vectors of Pragmatic Weaponization

### 2.1 "You Said It Wrong, So It Doesn't Count"

The most pervasive form. Your content is valid, but your pragmatic packaging doesn't meet NT expectations -- so the content is dismissed.

| What You Did | What They Say | What's Actually Happening |
|-------------|--------------|--------------------------|
| Stated a concern directly | "That was really aggressive" | Your lack of hedging is read as hostility |
| Gave honest feedback | "You need to work on your delivery" | Your content is never addressed |
| Identified a problem | "That's not how we raise issues here" | Register/process violation used to avoid substance |
| Expressed disagreement | "Everyone else is fine with this" | Your dissent is framed as the problem, not the issue |

The pragmatic trap: if you adjust your packaging (more hedging, softer tone), the content may land -- but you've accepted the frame that your natural communication is the problem. And the adjustment is never enough.

### 2.2 Diagnostic Weaponization

"That's just your autism talking."

Using the diagnosis itself to invalidate perceptions, opinions, and experiences:
- "You're overthinking it" (penalizing systematic analysis)
- "You're being too literal" (denying that what was said is what was meant)
- "That's your autism, not reality" (pathologizing accurate perception)
- "You're not able to understand social situations" (invoking diagnosis to override correct social observation)

This is Stark's (2019) "sidestepping and displacing" in action: sidestepping the evidence (ignoring what you actually said) and displacing (attributing a cognitive defect as the reason your testimony should be dismissed).

### 2.3 Sensory Gaslighting

Denying the autistic person's sensory reality:
- "It's not that loud"
- "The lights aren't that bright"
- "Stop being so sensitive"

This is often the **first form of reality-denial** autistic people experience, beginning in childhood. When a child is repeatedly told their direct sensory experience is wrong, they learn:
1. My perception of reality is unreliable
2. Other people's perceptions override mine
3. I should defer to others' version of reality

This establishes the template that makes all other gaslighting possible.

### 2.4 The Emotional Expression Double Bind

| How You Express Emotion | How It's Weaponized |
|------------------------|-------------------|
| Flat affect / verbal statement | "You don't really feel that way" / "You don't seem upset" |
| Intense expression (meltdown) | "You're overreacting" / "You're being dramatic" |
| Masking / performing NT expression | Unsustainable; eventually breaks down |

There is no available emotional expression mode that is consistently accepted as authentic. Each mode is penalized differently, but all are penalized.

### 2.5 The Compliance Trap

**"If you could just learn to [mask/perform/comply], people would treat you better."**

This framing:
- Makes pragmatic conformity your **responsibility**
- Frames mistreatment as your **fault** for failing to perform
- Positions more masking as the **solution** rather than changing how others respond
- Creates an unfalsifiable standard: if masking doesn't work, you're not masking hard enough

The compliance trap has institutional roots. Compliance-focused interventions can train patterns that increase vulnerability: suppressing "no" responses, prioritizing others' comfort, learning that acceptance is conditional on performance. This creates adults who default to compliance when challenged -- exactly the psychological condition abusers exploit.

### 2.6 Institutional Pragmatic Invalidation

Not just individual abusers. Systems do this:

| Institution | How It Invalidates |
|------------|-------------------|
| **Medical** | Symptom dismissal because you "don't look sick"; diagnostic overshadowing ("it's probably just anxiety"); denying support because you're "too articulate to be autistic" |
| **Workplace** | Performance reviews based on "communication style" not work quality; framing accommodation requests as "difficult behavior"; excluding from informal networks then blaming for being "out of the loop" |
| **Education** | Participation grades that penalize different conversational styles; group work penalties; "professional communication" requirements that pathologize directness |
| **Criminal justice** | Gaze aversion read as deception; flat affect read as lack of remorse; literal answers to questions read as evasion |
| **Social services** | Masking during assessments hides actual support needs; then support is denied because "you don't seem like you need it" |

## 3. Compound Manipulation Patterns

### 3.1 DARVO

**Deny, Attack, Reverse Victim and Offender** (Freyd, 1997). Each step exploits a pragmatic system:

| Step | What Happens | Pragmatic Mechanism |
|------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Deny** | "That didn't happen" / "That's not what I said" | Common ground corruption (Cat 16) |
| **Attack** | "You're being oversensitive" / "That's your autism" | Ad hominem targeting autistic traits (Cat 3) |
| **Reverse** | "Actually, I'M the one being hurt here" | Tu quoque + appeal to emotion (Fallacies category) |

For autistic people: the denial exploits self-doubt ("Maybe I did misremember"), the attack targets autistic traits, and the reversal exploits justice sensitivity ("If they're the real victim, I need to help").

### 3.2 Gaslighting as Sustained Pragmatic Corruption

Gaslighting is not a single tactic. It's the systematic corruption of shared reality over time -- an assault on common ground itself. It operates through:
- Repeated presupposition manipulation (inserting false premises)
- Reality rewriting ("That never happened")
- Diagnostic weaponization ("You're just being autistic")
- Social isolation ("Nobody else has this problem with me")
- Evidence destruction ("You're reading too much into it")

### 3.3 Post-Meltdown Shaming

A devastating documented pattern (Douglas & Sedgewick, 2024): abusers deliberately create conditions that trigger meltdowns, then use the meltdown as evidence that the autistic person is irrational, unstable, or dangerous. This undermines the victim's credibility if they tell anyone what's happening -- people already think they're an "unreliable narrator."

## 4. Is This Manipulation or Miscommunication?

This is THE critical practical skill. Not every negative interaction is manipulation.

### Four Possibilities

| Category | What's Happening | Response |
|----------|-----------------|----------|
| **Miscommunication** | Genuine double empathy mismatch | Clarify, explain, try again |
| **Ignorance** | They don't understand autism | Educate if you have the energy |
| **Carelessness** | They know better but aren't trying | Set a boundary |
| **Manipulation** | They're exploiting the pragmatic gap deliberately or habitually | Protect yourself |

### Indicators of Miscommunication (Not Manipulation)

- They're willing to rephrase or clarify
- They acknowledge your perspective even when disagreeing
- The pattern doesn't consistently advantage them
- They adjust their behavior when you explain the impact
- They engage with your content, not just your delivery

### Indicators of Manipulation

- The pattern is consistent and always benefits them
- Raising the concern makes things worse, not better
- They DARVO (deny, attack, reverse)
- You consistently leave feeling like the problem is you
- Documentation reveals a pattern they deny exists
- They treat your diagnosis as a credibility discount
- They escalate when challenged rather than engaging

### The Intentionality Bypass

You don't need to prove someone is deliberately manipulating you. **Patterns matter more than intentions.** If someone consistently benefits from a pattern that consistently harms you, and they don't change when you point it out, the effect is the same regardless of intent.

## 5. Self-Protection Strategies

### 5.1 The Documentation Habit

**Why:** Documentation anchors reality. When someone says "that didn't happen," your notes say otherwise. Over time, documentation reveals patterns that individual incidents obscure.

**What to document:** Date, time, what was said/done, who was present, your experience, the outcome.

**The follow-up email:** After verbal agreements, send an email: "Per our conversation, we agreed to [X]. Let me know if I've captured this correctly." This creates a record AND gives them a chance to correct genuine misunderstandings.

### 5.2 The Reality-Check Network

Identify trusted people (friends, autistic peers, therapist) who can serve as external validators: "Can I describe a situation and get your honest take?"

**Red flag:** If someone tries to isolate you from your reality-check network ("They don't understand our relationship" / "Your friends are a bad influence"), that itself is a manipulation indicator.

### 5.3 Scripts for Self-Protection

| Situation | Script |
|-----------|--------|
| Content dismissed because of delivery | "I hear your feedback about my tone. I'd also like you to respond to what I said." |
| "That's not what I meant" | "I understand that wasn't your intention. The impact was [X]. Can we address that?" |
| Diagnostic weaponization | "My being autistic doesn't make my observation wrong. Can we discuss the substance?" |
| "You're overreacting" | "I trust my experience. I'd like us to focus on solving [the specific issue]." |
| Memory gaslighting | "My recollection is different. Let me check my notes." |
| Bandwagon ("everyone agrees") | "I'd like to understand the specific reasoning, not just the headcount." |
| Tone policing | "I hear that my delivery didn't land well. Can we also address the content?" |
| Weaponized accommodations | "Accommodations aren't favors. They're what allows me to function. I appreciate them, but they don't create a debt." |

### 5.4 The Energy Calculation

Before engaging:
1. Does this matter enough to spend energy on?
2. Will engaging change anything?
3. Is this person capable of hearing me?
4. Do I have the resources right now?
5. Is there a lower-cost alternative (later, different channel, different person)?

Choosing not to engage is not defeat. It's resource management.

## 6. The Meta-Lesson: Learning Pragmatics vs. Being Coerced

This is the most important section in the document, and possibly in the entire hub.

This project teaches pragmatics as a learnable system. But there's a line between:

| Learning (Empowering) | Coercion (Harmful) |
|-----------------------|-------------------|
| "I'm choosing to hedge here because it will help me be heard" | "I HAVE to hedge or I'll be punished" |
| "Understanding politeness norms gives me options" | "I must perform politeness or I'm a bad person" |
| "I can code-switch when I choose to" | "My natural communication is inherently wrong" |
| "I know the rules so I can decide when to follow them" | "The rules are non-negotiable and failure is my fault" |

The right to choose is non-negotiable. Pragmatic knowledge should expand your options, not narrow them. If someone uses your pragmatic learning against you -- "See, you CAN do it, so you should ALWAYS do it" -- that is the compliance trap in action.

There must be spaces where you can communicate naturally: with autistic friends, in autistic community, in safe relationships. The ability to mask is not an obligation to mask.

## 7. Key Takeaways

1. **Your struggles are weaponized.** The pragmatic gap isn't just a communication challenge -- it's an attack surface. Understanding this is the first step to defending against it.

2. **Epistemic injustice is real and documented.** Autistic people are perceived as less credible when telling the truth. Your diagnosis can function as a credibility discount. This is injustice, not deficiency.

3. **Sensory gaslighting is foundational.** Being told your sensory experience isn't real -- starting in childhood -- establishes the template for all later gaslighting. Your senses are telling the truth.

4. **Not everything is manipulation.** The miscommunication-to-manipulation spectrum is real. Use the indicators to distinguish. Document to reveal patterns over time.

5. **Detection works differently for you.** Standard gut-feeling detection may not be your strength. Pattern recognition, documentation, and logical analysis ARE your strengths. Use them.

6. **The compliance trap is the deepest danger.** "Perform better and people will treat you better" makes your mistreatment your fault. Reject this frame.

7. **Learning pragmatics should empower choice, not enforce compliance.** The entire hub exists to give you options. Anyone who uses your pragmatic learning to demand permanent performance is weaponizing the education itself.
