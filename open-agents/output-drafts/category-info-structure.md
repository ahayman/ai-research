# Category: Information Structure (Given vs. New, Topic vs. Focus)

## The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

---

## How to Read This Document

This category covers the **packaging system** of language. Every sentence you produce contains two things: the content (what you're saying) and the packaging (how you've arranged it for the listener). Information structure is the packaging.

Think of it this way: if you order something online, the product matters -- but so does the box it arrives in. If the product is buried under layers of unnecessary packing material, or if fragile items arrive without any cushioning, or if the shipping label is stuck on the wrong side, the product is still the same but the experience of receiving it is frustrating. Information structure is the shipping and packaging of your message. Same content, different packaging, radically different reception.

This category connects to almost everything else in the hub:
- **Category 15 (Prosodic Pragmatics)** covers the primary *signal* for focus in spoken English -- stress placement. This category covers the *system* that stress serves.
- **Category 12 (Narrative)** covers information packaging across entire stories. This category covers the sentence-level principles.
- **Category 16 (Common Ground)** determines what counts as "given." This category covers what you do with that determination.
- **Category 5 (Discourse Markers)** provides the transition signals ("Speaking of...", "By the way...") that mark information structure shifts.
- **Category 13 (Topic Management)** covers conversational topic. This category covers sentence-level topic, a related but distinct concept.

For autistic individuals, this category is exceptionally practical. Information structure is **highly rule-governed, systematic, and transferable to writing** -- where you have time to plan and revise. It may be the single highest-leverage pragmatic skill for written communication.

## 1. The Problem Information Structure Solves

Consider these seven sentences -- they all describe the same event:

1. "John bought the car."
2. "The car was bought by John."
3. "It was John who bought the car."
4. "It was the car that John bought."
5. "What John bought was the car."
6. "The car, John bought."
7. "There was this car that John bought."

Same facts. Same truth. But they are not interchangeable. Each answers a different implicit question, fits a different moment in a conversation, and creates different expectations about what comes next.

If someone asks "Who bought the car?" and you answer "The car was bought by John," the information is correct but the packaging is wrong. You've put "the car" in the spotlight position when the question was about the buyer. The answer "JOHN bought the car" (with stress on John) or "It was JOHN who bought the car" packages the same content to directly address the question.

This mismatch between question and answer packaging is a significant source of communicative friction -- and it happens not because someone said something wrong, but because they packaged it wrong.

Wallace Chafe (1976) called this **information packaging**: the choice speakers make not about *what* to say but about *how to arrange* what they say for maximum processability. As Chafe put it, packaging affects communication "in partial independence of the quality of the [content] inside" -- just as toothpaste packaging affects sales regardless of the toothpaste quality.

## 2. The Given-New Distinction

### 2.1 The Core Contract

Clark and Haviland (1977) formalized the **Given-New Contract**: an implicit agreement between speakers and listeners.

**The speaker's obligation:** Mark information the listener already knows as "given" and information the listener doesn't yet know as "new."

**The listener's strategy:** Use given information as an anchor to integrate new information into their mental model.

**The practical rule:** Given before new. Start with what the listener already knows, then deliver the new information.

### 2.2 What Counts as "Given"?

"Given" is not a simple category. Ellen Prince (1981) identified a taxonomy of information statuses:

| Status | Definition | Example | Linguistic Signal |
|--------|-----------|---------|-------------------|
| **Brand new** | Never mentioned, not inferrable | "I met a woman at the conference." | Indefinite article "a/an" |
| **Brand new anchored** | New but attached to something known | "I met a friend of Sarah's." | Indefinite + known anchor |
| **Unused** | In the listener's long-term knowledge but not recently activated | "You know Sarah from accounting? She called me." | Proper name + reactivation |
| **Inferable** | Not mentioned but derivable from context | "I took the car to a mechanic. The engine needed work." | Definite article "the" (bridging) |
| **Textually evoked** | Mentioned earlier in the conversation | "A woman walked in. She sat down." | Pronoun |
| **Situationally evoked** | Present in the physical context | "Can you hand me that?" | Demonstrative |

Chafe (1987, 1994) added the concept of **activation states**: information in the listener's mind can be **active** (currently in focus), **accessible** (retrievable with a small cue), or **inactive** (stored but dormant). The speaker's job is to calibrate referential choice to the listener's activation state.

### 2.3 The Given-New Contract in Action

**A correctly packaged narrative:**

> "I went to a conference last week. [introduces: conference]
> The keynote speaker was incredible. [bridges from conference to speaker]
> She talked about AI in education. [tracks speaker with pronoun]
> After her talk, I met a woman in the hallway. [introduces: woman]
> She runs a startup in Berlin. [tracks woman with pronoun]
> I emailed her yesterday and she wants to collaborate." [continues tracking]

Each new referent is introduced with an indefinite form ("a conference," "a woman") and then tracked with pronouns. The listener builds a mental model step by step.

**The same narrative with Given-New violations:**

> "She wants to collaborate with me. [WHO?]
> The conference was last week. [What conference?]
> Her startup is based in Berlin. [Whose startup?]
> I met her in the hallway after the keynote. [Who is 'her'?]"

Same facts. Dramatically harder to follow. New information arrives without anchors.

### 2.4 Two Failure Modes

| Failure | What It Looks Like | The Listener's Experience | Common Cause |
|---------|-------------------|--------------------------|--------------|
| **Too much given, not enough new** | Long preamble restating what's already known before reaching the point | Impatience: "Get to the point!" | Speaker unsure the listener remembers the context |
| **Too much new, not enough given** | Jumping straight to new information without establishing context | Confusion: "Wait, what?" | Speaker assumes more shared knowledge than exists |

Both failures are **information structure calibration errors**. The first treats everything as new when it's already given. The second treats everything as given when it's actually new.

## 3. Focus: The Point of the Sentence

### 3.1 What Focus Is

Every sentence has a **focus** -- the element that carries the main informational payload. It's the part that answers the implicit or explicit question behind the sentence.

| Question | Answer | Focus (in caps) |
|----------|--------|-----------------|
| "Who bought the car?" | "JOHN bought the car." | John |
| "What did John buy?" | "John bought THE CAR." | The car |
| "Did John sell the car?" | "John BOUGHT the car." | Bought |
| "What happened?" | "JOHN BOUGHT THE CAR." | Whole sentence (broad focus) |

In spoken English, focus is marked primarily by **prosodic stress** -- the focused element receives the main pitch accent in the intonation phrase. (See Category 15 for full treatment of prosodic focus marking.)

### 3.2 Three Types of Focus

| Type | Function | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| **Presentational focus** | Introduces new information | "There's a CAT on the roof." |
| **Contrastive focus** | Selects one option and rejects alternatives | "I don't want COFFEE, I want TEA." |
| **Exhaustive focus** | Identifies the unique element (and no other) | "It was JOHN who called." (John and only John) |

### 3.3 The Question Under Discussion (QUD)

Roberts (1996) proposed that every utterance in discourse implicitly answers a **Question Under Discussion** (QUD). The focus of the sentence should match the questioned element of the QUD.

When focus and QUD mismatch, communication feels off:

| QUD | Matching Answer | Mismatching Answer |
|-----|----------------|-------------------|
| "When is the meeting?" | "The meeting is at THREE." | "THE MEETING is at three." |
| "Who organized the event?" | "SARAH organized the event." | "Sarah organized THE EVENT." |

The mismatching answers aren't wrong -- they're just packaged for a different question than the one that was asked. The listener has to do extra work to extract the relevant information.

## 4. Topic and Comment

### 4.1 What Topic Is

The **topic** is what the sentence is about. The **comment** is what's said about it.

In "As for John, he passed the exam," "John" is the topic and "he passed the exam" is the comment.

English typically puts the topic in subject position (sentence-initial), but it can be marked explicitly through:

| Construction | Example | Effect |
|-------------|---------|--------|
| **As for X** | "As for the budget, we need to talk." | Explicitly sets X as the topic |
| **Topicalization** | "That book, I really enjoyed." | Fronts X to topic position |
| **Left-dislocation** | "The report, I sent it yesterday." | Sets topic with resumptive pronoun |

### 4.2 Marked vs. Unmarked Topic

| Type | What It Is | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Unmarked** | Topic is the grammatical subject (default in English) | "John bought the car." (John = topic) |
| **Marked** | Topic is moved to a non-default position for discourse reasons | "The car, John bought." (car = topic) |

Marked topics signal that the speaker is deliberately choosing what the sentence is about -- usually to shift attention, contrast with a previous topic, or resume a dropped topic.

## 5. English's Information Structure Toolkit

English has a set of syntactic constructions, each serving a specific information-structural function:

| Construction | Form | Information Function | When to Use It |
|-------------|------|---------------------|----------------|
| **Active voice** | "John broke the window." | Agent as topic | Default; when you're talking about what John did |
| **Passive voice** | "The window was broken." | Patient as topic; agent backgrounded | When you're talking about the window, or the agent is unknown/irrelevant |
| **It-cleft** | "It was John who broke it." | Exhaustive focus on one element | When you need to pinpoint exactly who/what/when |
| **Pseudo-cleft** | "What I need is more time." | Frames the question, then answers it | When you want to set up context before delivering the point |
| **Topicalization** | "That movie, I loved." | Explicitly marks the topic | When shifting to discuss a specific entity |
| **Left-dislocation** | "The report, I sent it yesterday." | Reactivates a referent | When resuming discussion of something from earlier |
| **Right-dislocation** | "She's amazing, your sister." | Clarifies/specifies after the main clause | When you realize the listener needs more specificity |
| **Existential there** | "There's a problem." | Introduces a brand-new entity | When presenting something entirely new |
| **Fronted adverbial** | "Yesterday, I went to the store." | Sets the temporal/spatial frame | When time or place is important context for what follows |

These are not random stylistic choices. Each one is a tool for packaging information differently. Learning when to use which tool is a learnable skill.

## 6. Thematic Progression: Information Structure Across Sentences

Individual sentences chain together through **thematic progression** -- the pattern of how topics and new information flow across a text. Danes (1974) identified three main patterns:

### Constant Theme (Same Topic, New Comments)

> "**The new policy** was announced yesterday. **It** affects all departments. **The policy** requires monthly reporting. **It** takes effect in June."

Topic stays the same; each sentence adds new information about it. Good for sustained focus on one entity.

### Linear Progression (New Becomes Given)

> "I met **a researcher**. **The researcher** told me about **a new study**. **The study** found that **sleep deprivation** affects memory. **Memory loss** from sleep deprivation..."

Each sentence's new information becomes the next sentence's given information. Good for building chains of reasoning or narrative.

### Split Theme (One Topic Splits Into Multiple)

> "**The project** has two phases. **Phase one** involves research. **Phase two** involves implementation."

A single topic is divided into subtopics, each getting its own comment. Good for organized exposition.

Understanding these patterns helps with both **producing** well-organized text and **recognizing** when a text loses its thread (usually because the thematic progression breaks down -- the listener can't connect the new topic to anything previously established).

## 7. The Autistic Experience with Information Structure

### 7.1 The Core Finding: Competence Is There, Implementation Is Challenging

Research consistently shows that autistic individuals have the **underlying linguistic competence** for information structure. The difficulties emerge at the interface between knowing the system and implementing it in real time, under processing pressure, while modeling the listener's knowledge state.

Key findings:

| Study | Finding |
|-------|---------|
| Paul et al. (2005) | Two distinct autistic subgroups: one uses wide pitch range but fails to mark information-structural focus; the other uses narrow pitch range but marks focus functionally. The system exists but is unevenly deployed. |
| Patel et al. (2023) | Autistic adults produce contrastive stress correctly 73.6% of the time (vs. 89% controls). Comprehend it 80.6% (vs. 93.1%). The gap is real but not catastrophic. |
| Arnold et al. (2009) | Autistic children over-specify referents (use full names/descriptions instead of pronouns), but this decreases with age (r=.60). It's developmental, not fixed. |
| Nadig et al. (2009) | Referential shortening for common ground (using shorter descriptions when the listener already knows the referent) was absent in younger autistic participants but emerged in older ones. |
| Geelhand et al. (2025) | Autistic adults are more verbose specifically in mixed-neurotype interactions -- they use more words when communicating across neurotypes than within their own. |

### 7.2 Why Information Structure Is Challenging

| Challenge | What It Means | Connection |
|-----------|-------------- |------------|
| **Audience modeling** | Packaging information requires knowing what the listener already knows. This is common ground tracking in real time. | Category 16 |
| **Prosodic focus marking** | Placing stress on the right word to signal "the point" requires real-time pragmatic prosody processing. | Category 15 |
| **Processing speed** | Su et al. (2025) found autistic children show a processing speed penalty for contrastive stress -- they CAN process it but it takes longer. Real-time conversation doesn't wait. | -- |
| **Working memory load** | Schuh et al. (2016) found that working memory constraints mediate common ground effects -- when WM is loaded, common ground tracking suffers, affecting information packaging. | -- |
| **Referential calibration** | Choosing between "John," "he," "my colleague," "the tall guy" requires assessing how accessible the referent is in the listener's mind. | Category 8 |

### 7.3 The Over-Specification Pattern

One of the most documented autistic information-structure patterns: using more explicit referential forms than the context requires.

| Situation | Neurotypical | Autistic (tendency) |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Already-established referent | "He called me." | "John called me." |
| Obvious from context | "Can you pass it?" | "Can you pass the salt?" |
| Shared knowledge | "The meeting" | "The Tuesday 3pm project meeting" |

**Why this happens:** It's a rational strategy. Over-specification eliminates ambiguity. If you're not confident in your ability to track what the listener has activated, providing the full form is the safe choice.

**The double empathy trap:** Neurotypical listeners can read over-specification as condescending ("Does she think I don't know who John is?") or as indicating the speaker thinks they're not paying attention. The autistic speaker is being precise; the neurotypical listener reads it as a social signal it was never intended to be.

### 7.4 The Under-Specification Pattern

The opposite also occurs: assuming shared knowledge that isn't shared.

| Situation | What Happens | Listener's Experience |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------|
| Starting with a pronoun | "She told me the project's delayed." | "Who is 'she'?" |
| Skipping background | "The algorithm needs refactoring." | "What algorithm? What's the context?" |
| Assumed shared interest | Jumping into detailed content without establishing relevance | "Why are you telling me this?" |

This pattern is connected to **monotropism** (Murray et al., 2005): when deeply focused on a topic, the autistic speaker may treat their own rich mental model as shared, forgetting that the listener doesn't have the same depth of context.

### 7.5 Autistic Strengths

| Strength | How It Helps |
|----------|-------------|
| **Explicit reference** | Over-specification, while sometimes socially penalized, prevents ambiguity. In technical, legal, and written contexts, this is an asset. |
| **Logical ordering** | Autistic preference for systematic presentation maps well to given-before-new principles. |
| **Pattern recognition** | Information structure rules are patterns. Autistic pattern recognition can quickly internalize them once they're made explicit. |
| **Written communication** | Writing allows deliberate information packaging without real-time pressure. Research suggests autistic individuals often communicate more effectively in writing. |

## 8. Practical Strategies

### 8.1 The Five Core Principles

| Principle | Practical Rule |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Given before new** | Start sentences with known entities; introduce new ones with "a" or a descriptive phrase |
| **One new thing per sentence** | Split complex sentences; let each new element have its own sentence |
| **Focus goes at the end** (or is marked by stress/cleft) | Put the point at the end of the sentence, or use a cleft to isolate it |
| **Signal your transitions** | Use discourse markers, topic sentences, paragraph breaks when the topic shifts |
| **Calibrate to your audience** | Assess what they already know; don't over- or under-explain |

### 8.2 The Knowledge Check Protocol

Before constructing a message (especially in writing), ask:

1. **What does the listener already know?** (This is your "given" pool)
2. **What is new to them?** (This is your payload)
3. **What is the one thing I most want them to take away?** (This is your focus)
4. **What's the topic?** (Start there)
5. **Am I introducing too many new things at once?** (If yes, split)

### 8.3 Self-Check for Messages

Before sending a written message:

1. Is my main point identifiable within the first few sentences?
2. Does every pronoun have a clear referent?
3. Does any sentence introduce more than one new person, concept, or event?
4. Does each sentence connect to the previous one?
5. Have I signaled topic shifts with transition phrases?
6. Am I calibrated to my audience's knowledge? (Not over-explaining what they know, not under-explaining what they don't)

### 8.4 The BLUF Strategy

**Bottom Line Up Front**: State the main point first, then provide supporting context.

Instead of:
> "So I've been looking at the quarterly data and I noticed some trends in the eastern region and I talked to the team leads and they mentioned some supply chain issues and after looking into it further..."

Try:
> "Eastern region revenue dropped 12% this quarter due to supply chain delays. Here's what I found..."

BLUF works because it establishes the focus immediately and provides the listener's anchor for everything that follows.

## 9. Key Takeaways

1. **Information structure is packaging, not content.** The same proposition can be packaged in many ways. The choice of packaging depends on what the listener knows, what question is being answered, and what the speaker wants to emphasize.

2. **Given before new is the fundamental rule.** Anchor new information to what the listener already knows. Violations in both directions -- too much given (get to the point) and too much new (wait, what?) -- cause friction.

3. **Focus marks the point.** Every sentence has a focus -- the element that carries the new, important information. In spoken English, stress marks it. In written English, sentence position and syntactic constructions (clefts, passives) mark it.

4. **English has a toolkit of constructions.** Passives, clefts, pseudo-clefts, topicalization, and existential "there" are not stylistic flourishes -- they're information-packaging tools, each with a specific function.

5. **Autistic over-specification is rational, not deficient.** Using full names instead of pronouns, providing extra context, and being explicit are strategies that eliminate ambiguity -- valued in many contexts even if socially penalized in others.

6. **This system is highly learnable.** Unlike many pragmatic domains that depend on real-time social inference, information structure follows explicit rules, can be practiced in writing, and rewards systematic thinking. It may be the most accessible pragmatic skill for autistic learners.

7. **Writing is the practice ground.** In conversation, information packaging happens in real time under processing pressure. In writing, you can plan, revise, and deliberately apply the principles. Master it in writing, then transfer what you can to speech.
