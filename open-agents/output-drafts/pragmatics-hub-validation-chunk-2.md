## Executive Summary

Categories 05 (Discourse Markers), 06 (Hedging), and 07 (Register and Code-Switching) are in generally strong shape. The theoretical backbone — Schiffrin's 1987 five-plane model, Fraser's 1999 four-category taxonomy, Blakemore's procedural/conceptual distinction, Heritage's 1984 "change-of-state token," Lakoff's 1973 hedge paper, Prince/Frader/Bosk's 1982 approximator/shield taxonomy, Halliday's field/tenor/mode, Joos's five styles, and Giles's Communication Accommodation Theory — is summarized accurately and in the right historical order. The major autism-specific studies (Gorman et al. 2016, Jones/Zane/Grossman 2022, Crompton et al. 2020, Cola et al. 2022, Malkin & Abbot-Smith 2021, Hull et al. 2019, Vermeulen 2012/2015, Wilson & Bishop 2022, Milton 2012, Kissine et al. 2019, Abalos et al. 2025) exist and largely say what the hub claims they say.

**The most important problem in this chunk is a citation error that recurs in at least four files.** The hub consistently credits the "Uh, Um, and Autism: Filler Disfluencies as Pragmatic Markers" study to "Lake et al. (2011)," and in one place cites "Lake et al., ACL 2021" for a different computational paper. The actual paper by that title is **Irvine, Eigsti, & Fein (2016)**, *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders* 46(3): 1061–1070 (online 2015). Lake is not an author. There is a separate, earlier 2011 paper by Jennifer Lake et al. ("Listener versus speaker-oriented aspects of speech…" *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review* 18(1): 135–140) that did find reduced filler use in adults with HFA, but it did **not** have this title and did not separately analyze um vs. uh. The um/uh dissociation finding the hub attributes to "Lake et al., 2011" is actually the Irvine/Eigsti/Fein 2016 result (and independently the Gorman et al. 2016 result in younger children). This error appears in at least six places across category-05 and category-06 files, and propagates into the hub's short "Key Sources" list and the "Tier 1" practical tier.

Second-most significant: the hub cites "Bohn et al., 2022" for the PeerJ registered report on preference for certainty. The actual authors are **Wilson & Bishop (2022)** — correctly cited elsewhere in the hub but misattributed in `category-06-hedging-research-autism.md` §2.2. These two authors are already cited side-by-side on other pages, so this is a slippage rather than a genuine unknown source.

Third: the "Butterbrod (Tilburg University)" source — relied on in `category-06-hedging.md` and `category-06-hedging-research-autism.md` for the flagship claim that "individuals with ASD used significantly lower overall politeness markers compared to non-autistic individuals, with differences primarily driven by lower use of negative politeness markers" — is a Bachelor's thesis that used **fictive scenarios** with **typically-developed participants who self-reported autistic traits**, not clinically diagnosed autistic individuals. The hub in several places describes it as if it measured ASD speech directly. The finding itself (autistic traits correlate with less negative politeness) may survive, but the wording needs to be loosened so it does not sound like a diagnosed-group study.

No Section 12 forbidden claims (triune brain, polyvagal literalism, "trauma stored in body," universal PTG, forgiveness requirement, pop-gaslighting overreach, narcissistic-abuse clinical category, boundaries-as-slogan) appear in any file in this chunk. No mention of "Rozenkrantz et al. 2021" was found (the known citation gotcha is clean here). The Double Empathy framing cites Milton (2012) in every `*-research-autism.md` file, as required.

Deficit-language slippage is minimal and mostly flagged already by the hub itself; a handful of unhedged "deficit" uses remain (notably around "pedantic speech" and "pragmatic impairment") and are noted below.

## Systemic Issues

### S1. "Lake et al. 2011 — Uh, Um, and Autism" (INVALID — recurring)

Files affected:
- `category-05-discourse-markers.md` §1.3 and Key Sources list
- `category-05-discourse-markers-research-autism.md` §1.2 and Sources list
- `category-06-hedging.md` Key Sources list
- `category-06-hedging-research-autism.md` §1.3 ("Um is listener-oriented… Lake et al., 2011; Gorman et al., 2016")
- Category 5 "Tier 1" priority table (attributing the core um/uh dissociation)

**The actual publication:** Irvine, C.A., Eigsti, I.-M., & Fein, D.A. (2016). "Uh, Um, and Autism: Filler Disfluencies as Pragmatic Markers in Adolescents with Optimal Outcomes from Autism Spectrum Disorder." *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders* 46(3): 1061–1070. DOI 10.1007/s10803-015-2651-y. First published online 19 November 2015; in print March 2016. PMC7536647. PubMed 26586555.

**The actual Lake 2011 paper:** Lake, J.K., Humphreys, K.R., & Cardy, S. (2011). "Listener vs. speaker-oriented aspects of speech: Studying the disfluencies of individuals with autism spectrum disorders." *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review* 18(1): 135–140. This paper found that adults with HFA produced fewer fillers overall than TD adults but did **not** analyze um and uh separately and did **not** establish the listener-oriented vs. speaker-oriented dissociation — that specific finding belongs to Irvine/Eigsti/Fein 2016 in adolescents and Gorman et al. 2016 in 4–8-year-olds.

**Severity:** High. This is the single most empirically important claim in Category 05 — the foundation of the "um vs. uh = listener-oriented vs. speaker-oriented" reframing that the hub uses to motivate its entire marker-specific approach. The finding is real and well-replicated; the authorship attribution is simply wrong. Every downstream file, the hub index, and any webpage derived from this chunk will propagate the wrong citation.

**Recommended fix:** Replace all "Lake et al., 2011" citations for this title with "Irvine, Eigsti, & Fein (2016)." Retain a separate Lake et al. (2011) citation for the *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review* paper as corroborating evidence, but do not attribute the um/uh split to it.

**Secondary confusion:** `category-05-discourse-markers-research-autism.md` §1.6 cites "Lake et al., ACL 2021" for a computational pragmatic discourse analysis (politeness, uncertainty, informativeness, BERT embeddings, F1 > 0.82) linked to PMC9633181. PMC9633181 is actually a different paper titled "Predicting pragmatic discourse features in the language of adults with ASD" — which is cited correctly elsewhere on the same page under "Lake, J.K., Bhullar, S., & Bhattacharyya, O. (2022)." The "ACL 2021" attribution appears to be a confusion between ACL venue proceedings and the JADD/PMC paper. Needs disambiguation — either pull the correct ACL reference or consolidate under the 2022 PMC9633181 citation.

### S3. Butterbrod (Tilburg) — overstated methodologically (UNCERTAIN / partly INVALID)

Files affected:
- `category-06-hedging.md` §7.1 and Key Sources ("Butterbrod, T. The Effect of Autistic Traits on Politeness Strategies, Tilburg University")
- `category-06-hedging-research-autism.md` §1.1 ("The critical finding: individuals with ASD used significantly lower overall politeness markers…")

**The source:** Tim Butterbrod, undergraduate (Bachelor's) thesis, Tilburg University, Faculty of Humanities, Communication and Information Sciences (arno.uvt.nl/show.cgi?fid=133361). The thesis measured **the effect of autistic traits** on politeness strategy use in **typically-developed participants** (not clinically diagnosed ASD) using **fictive scenarios** (not naturalistic speech).

**What the hub claims it shows:** "individuals with ASD used significantly lower overall politeness markers… driven by lower use of negative politeness markers." This phrasing implies a diagnosed-group comparison. The actual study operates on an autistic-traits continuum in non-diagnosed speakers.

**Severity:** Medium. The direction of the finding (higher autistic traits → fewer negative politeness markers) is defensible, but the hub is using this source to anchor a claim about diagnosed autistic speakers, and is leaning hard on it because it is the most specific study available on hedging per se. Recommend (a) softening to "individuals higher in self-reported autistic traits" rather than "individuals with ASD," (b) flagging methodological limits (undergraduate thesis, fictive scenarios, non-diagnosed sample), and (c) triangulating the hedging/negative-politeness claim against a peer-reviewed source (e.g., the VET "voicemail elicitation task" study in JINS, or the Lake/Bhullar/Bhattacharyya computational politeness work in PMC9633181) wherever it is load-bearing.
