# Category 21: Pragmatic Competence in Specific Contexts

## The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

---

## How to Read This Document

Every previous category taught a pragmatic **mechanism** -- speech acts, hedging, turn-taking, register. This category is where they all come together. Each real-world context activates a specific combination of mechanisms with its own rules, stakes, and conventions.

This document provides a **map** of six major life contexts, showing how pragmatic principles apply differently in each. The supplemental research documents contain detailed scripts and deep dives for each context. This main document gives you the overview and the key principles.

---

## 1. The Workplace

### 1.1 Why It Matters Most

The workplace is where pragmatic competence has the most **material consequences**. Research consistently identifies social-communicative challenges -- not technical skill deficits -- as the primary employment barrier for autistic adults (40-85% unemployment/underemployment depending on methodology).

Four features make workplace communication unique:

| Feature | Implication |
|---------|-----------|
| **Involuntary proximity** | You must maintain relationships with people you didn't choose |
| **Dual hierarchy** | Formal (org chart) + informal (social capital) operate simultaneously |
| **Persistent consequences** | What you say accumulates into a reputation |
| **Instrumental relationships** | Communication must accomplish tasks THROUGH relationships |

### 1.2 Key Workplace Pragmatic Skills

| Skill | What It Requires | Categories Involved |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Meeting participation** | Turn-taking, knowing when to speak, how to disagree | Cat 4, 18 |
| **Email/Slack** | Register calibration, hedging by recipient | Cat 7, 6, 20 |
| **Giving/receiving feedback** | Face-management, hedging, disagreement | Cat 3, 6, 18 |
| **Small talk** | Phatic exchange, safe topics, bridge strategies | Cat 19 |
| **Hierarchy navigation** | Register, hedging level, deference | Cat 7, 6, 22 |
| **Self-advocacy** | Requests, framing, accommodation language | Cat 1, 3, 18 |

### 1.3 The Autistic Workplace Advantage

| Context | Why Autistic Communication Wins |
|---------|-------------------------------|
| Technical documentation | Precision and clarity prevent errors |
| Code review / bug reports | Detailed, systematic description |
| Written communication | More advanced writing skills (Gillespie-Lynch et al.) |
| Process improvement | Pattern recognition identifies inefficiencies |
| Quality assurance | Attention to detail catches what others miss |

JPMorgan's Autism at Work program reported autistic employees were **48% faster** and **92% more productive** in certain roles.

### 1.4 The Core Workplace Rule

**The relationship is not the goal -- it's the means.** At work, you communicate to accomplish goals THROUGH relationships. Neglecting the relationship destroys the means, which destroys the goal. This is why small talk, hedging, and face-management matter at work even when they feel inefficient -- they maintain the infrastructure through which work gets done.

## 2. Medical/Healthcare Encounters

### 2.1 The Power Asymmetry

Doctors control the agenda, time, space, specialized knowledge, and treatment decisions. Research shows doctors interrupt patients within 11-18 seconds of their opening statement. Despite this, **you have the right** to ask questions, disagree, request tests, and seek second opinions.

### 2.2 The OLDCARTS Framework for Describing Symptoms

| Element | What to Describe |
|---------|-----------------|
| **O**nset | When did it start? |
| **L**ocation | Where exactly? |
| **D**uration | How long does it last? |
| **C**haracter | What does it feel like? (sharp, dull, throbbing) |
| **A**ggravating factors | What makes it worse? |
| **R**elieving factors | What makes it better? |
| **T**iming | When does it happen? (morning, after eating, during exercise) |
| **S**everity | Scale of 1-10 |

### 2.3 Autistic-Specific Challenges

| Challenge | Strategy |
|-----------|---------|
| **Masking hides symptoms** | Write symptoms down BEFORE the appointment when you're not performing |
| **Alexithymia** (~50% prevalence) | Use the OLDCARTS framework as a checklist instead of describing feelings |
| **Sensory overload** in medical settings | Request accommodations: dim lights, quiet room, written instructions |
| **Appearing "fine"** | Explicitly state: "I tend to look calmer than I feel. The pain/anxiety is actually [level]." |

### 2.4 Key Scripts

**Requesting accommodation**: "I'm autistic and I communicate best when I can [read instructions / have extra processing time / have a quiet room]. Would that be possible?"

**Requesting a test**: "I've been reading about [condition] and my symptoms seem to match. Would it be appropriate to test for that?"

**When you don't understand**: "Can you explain that in simpler terms?" / "Can you write that down for me?"

## 3. Educational Settings

### 3.1 Key Pragmatic Demands

| Demand | What's Expected |
|--------|----------------|
| **Classroom participation** | Raise hand, wait to be called on, keep contributions concise and relevant |
| **Student-professor email** | Formal greeting, clear purpose, respectful tone, sign with full name |
| **Group work** | Negotiate tasks, give feedback diplomatically, handle unequal contribution |
| **Academic register** | Hedged claims ("The data suggest..." not "The data prove...") |

### 3.2 The High School → College Shift

| High School | College |
|------------|---------|
| Teachers manage you | You manage yourself |
| "You must do X" | "You are expected to do X" (implicit) |
| Participation = attendance | Participation = verbal contribution |
| Simple email OK | Formal email expected |

### 3.3 Accommodation Script

**With disclosure**: "I'm registered with disability services. My accommodations include [X]. How would you like me to coordinate those for your class?"

**Without disclosure**: "I learn best when I can [read instructions / have extra time / record lectures]. Is that something you'd be open to?"

## 4. Service Encounters

### 4.1 The Restaurant Script (Abbreviated)

```
Arrival:  Host asks party size → You answer → Seated
Drinks:   Server introduces self + asks for drinks → You order
Ordering: Server asks if ready → You order (can ask questions)
Meal:     Server checks in → "Everything's great, thanks"
Check:    "Could we get the check?" → Pay → Tip 18-20%
Leaving:  "Thank you!" → Server: "Have a great evening!"
```

### 4.2 Common Service Situations

| Situation | What to Say |
|-----------|------------|
| **Declining help in a store** | "I'm just browsing, thanks!" |
| **Returning an item** | "I'd like to return this. I have the receipt." |
| **Complaining about food** | "Excuse me, I think my order might be wrong -- I ordered the [X]." |
| **Phone call to business** | State name → state purpose → ask question. "Hi, my name is [X]. I'm calling about [Y]. Could you help me with [Z]?" |

## 5. Friendship and Relationships

### 5.1 Friendship Pragmatics

**Initiation requires time**: Research shows 40-60 hours of shared time for casual friendship, 200+ hours for close friendship. It's a gradual process.

**Maintenance requires contact**: The medium matters less than the consistency. Regular brief contact (a text, a meme, a "thinking of you") maintains friendships more than occasional long conversations.

**The invitation system**: Invite → accept/decline (with the expected padding from Category 18). If declined, the DECLINER is expected to suggest an alternative. If they don't suggest an alternative after 2-3 declines, they may not want to maintain the friendship.

### 5.2 Romantic Communication

| Challenge | Strategy |
|-----------|---------|
| **Reading interest** | Look for: repeated contact initiation, physical proximity, personal questions, laughter at your jokes, finding excuses to interact |
| **Expressing interest** | Be more direct than feels necessary -- subtle hints may not register in either direction |
| **The "DTR" (Define the Relationship)** | "I want to make sure we're on the same page about us. How would you describe what this is?" |
| **Boundaries** | "I need [X]. It's important to me. Can we figure out how to make that work?" |

### 5.3 Autistic Friendship Strengths

- **Loyalty and reliability** -- once a friendship is established, autistic individuals tend to be deeply committed
- **Honesty** -- friends can trust your feedback is genuine
- **Shared interest bonding** -- autistic friendships built on shared passions can be intensely fulfilling
- **Low-maintenance option** -- some autistic friendships thrive on parallel activity with minimal phatic demand

## 6. Authority and Institutional Interactions

### 6.1 Police Encounters -- Safety First

Research shows 80% of officers cannot identify autism. Autistic behaviors (gaze aversion, echolalia, flat affect, not following commands immediately) can be misinterpreted as non-compliance, deception, or threat.

**Safety strategies:**
- Stay calm, keep hands visible
- Say: "I am autistic. I may need extra time to respond."
- Carry an autism awareness/alert card
- If possible, have a support person present
- You have the right to remain silent (but in the US, you must explicitly invoke it -- Salinas v. Texas)

### 6.2 Emergency Communication

**Calling 911:**
```
1. State the emergency type: "I need [police / ambulance / fire]."
2. State the location: "[Address]."
3. State what happened: "[Brief description]."
4. Answer their questions.
5. Stay on the line until told to hang up.
```

## 7. Burnout and Energy Management

### 7.1 The Burnout Connection

Research (Raymaker et al., 2020) established that autistic burnout involves chronic exhaustion, loss of previously acquired skills, and reduced tolerance for stimulation -- driven by cumulative life stressors and masking demands. Pragmatic performance across ALL these contexts draws from the same cognitive-social energy pool.

### 7.2 The Energy Triage

| Priority | Investment | Context |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| **High** | Full pragmatic engagement | Job interviews, key meetings, doctor appointments, safety situations |
| **Medium** | Standard pragmatic effort | Regular workplace interactions, acquaintance encounters |
| **Low** | Minimal pragmatic investment | Passing encounters, low-stakes service encounters |
| **None** | Conservation mode | When overwhelmed; it's OK to not perform |

### 7.3 The Right to Not Perform

Reduced social engagement is sometimes **self-care**, not failure. When the social battery is depleted:
- It's OK to skip optional social events
- It's OK to keep interactions brief
- It's OK to say "I need some quiet time"
- It's OK to choose text over voice, email over meeting
- It's OK to leave early

## 8. Key Takeaways

1. **Each context is a complete pragmatic system.** The workplace, healthcare, education, service encounters, relationships, and institutional interactions each have their own combination of pragmatic rules. Learning the context-specific patterns is as important as learning the general mechanisms.

2. **The workplace has the highest stakes.** Pragmatic failures at work have career consequences. Investing in workplace pragmatic skills yields the most material return.

3. **Preparation is the universal strategy.** Across ALL contexts, researching norms before entering, preparing scripts, and knowing what to expect reduces the cognitive load of real-time pragmatic processing.

4. **Your directness is an asset in the right contexts.** Technical, professional, written, and safety contexts all value autistic communication strengths. The key is matching your natural style to the contexts that reward it.

5. **Energy management is pragmatic competence.** Knowing WHEN to invest in social performance and when to conserve is a skill. Not every context deserves full pragmatic engagement.

6. **Self-advocacy is a speech act.** Requesting accommodations, describing needs, and setting boundaries are learnable pragmatic skills -- not inherent personality traits.
