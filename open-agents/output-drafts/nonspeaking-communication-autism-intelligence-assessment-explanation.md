# Intelligence, Assessment, and Measuring Non-Speaking Minds

---

## 1. Overview

The IQ score assigned to a non-speaking autistic person is not a neutral data point. It cascades through every domain of their life: educational placement, access to communication technology, eligibility for services, and whether anyone attempts to teach them to read, write, or participate in their own care decisions. For decades, non-speaking autistic individuals have been assessed using instruments that require the very skills they lack -- verbal responses, reliable motor output, sustained attention to examiner-directed tasks -- producing scores that confirm the prior assumption of intellectual disability in a self-reinforcing cycle.

This document examines why standard tests fail, what alternatives exist, the philosophical frameworks that guide assumptions about competence, the contested "profound autism" label, and the legal consequences of assessment decisions. It draws on validated research and incorporates corrections identified through independent validation.

### Validation Summary

This document draws on concept research and independent validation. Of the major claims, the Dawson/Mottron Raven's finding, Donnellan's least dangerous assumption principle, the Jaswal 2024 study and its rebuttal, the "profound autism" debate, alternative assessment tools, and Jordyn Zimmerman's case details were all independently confirmed. One critical correction was applied: the "stretch boundaries" blog post was misattributed to John Elder Robison in the original research; it is actually by Peter Lloyd-Thomas (Epiphany ASD blog, 2025), a parent of an autistic son. This changes the framing -- the position comes from a parent perspective, not an autistic adult. One minor correction: the 2025 Cued Looking Paradigm is a bioRxiv preprint, not a peer-reviewed publication.

### Key Takeaways

1. **Standard IQ tests systematically underestimate autistic cognitive ability.** The Dawson-Mottron finding (2007) demonstrated a 30-percentile-point average discrepancy between Wechsler scores and Raven's Progressive Matrices scores for the same autistic children. One-third scored in the "intellectual disability" range on the Wechsler; only 5% scored that low on the Raven's.

2. **The assessment creates the conditions that justify its own findings.** Low scores lead to low-expectation placements, which deny cognitive stimulation, which ensures continued low scores on re-assessment. The cycle is self-reinforcing.

3. **No current test fully solves the problem.** Even "nonverbal" tests require motor responses, and truly motor-free assessment remains an unsolved problem. Eye-tracking approaches are promising but not yet standardized.

4. **The consequences of underestimation are catastrophic and potentially irreversible.** Jordyn Zimmerman received no communication tool until age 18. She subsequently earned two degrees, was named to the Forbes Accessibility 100, and became Board Chair of CommunicationFIRST. Her intelligence did not change -- only her access.

## 2. Why Standard IQ Tests Fail

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-3-what-iq-tests-measure.html -->

The Wechsler Intelligence Scales (WISC for children, WAIS for adults) are the most widely used IQ tests worldwide. They include a Verbal Comprehension Index that requires spoken responses, a Working Memory Index that relies on auditory verbal processing, and a Processing Speed Index that requires rapid motor responses. Every one of these systematically disadvantages non-speaking individuals.

But the problem runs deeper than verbal demands. Even the "nonverbal" Performance IQ subtests require pointing, placing blocks, assembling puzzles, or other fine motor actions. For individuals with motor planning difficulties -- apraxia and dyspraxia, which are common in non-speaking autism -- these demands create a barrier unrelated to the cognitive skill being tested. The test-taker understands the pattern but cannot make their hand point to the correct answer.

Then there are the compliance demands. Standard IQ tests require sitting at a table with an examiner, attending to examiner-directed stimuli, and responding within time limits. These demands presuppose behavioral compliance, joint attention, and social engagement -- all of which may be atypical in autism for reasons that have nothing to do with intelligence.

Finally, tests are administered through verbal instructions. A non-speaking person who cannot comprehend spoken instructions, or who comprehends them perfectly but cannot demonstrate comprehension through the expected motor response, receives a score reflecting the instruction barrier rather than their cognitive capacity.

The result is that when you give a standard IQ test to a non-speaking autistic person, you are measuring the sum of barriers -- verbal expression demands, motor response demands, compliance demands, instruction comprehension demands, and test-taking convention knowledge -- rather than the cognitive ability you are trying to assess. For a neurotypical test-taker, these barriers are minimal, and the score primarily reflects cognitive ability. For a non-speaking autistic test-taker, the barriers dominate, and the score tells you almost nothing about the brain behind them.

## 3. The Dawson-Mottron Finding: A 30-Percentile Gap

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-2-wechsler-vs-ravens.html -->

In 2007, Michelle Dawson, Isabelle Soulieres, Morton Ann Gernsbacher, and Laurent Mottron published a landmark paper in Psychological Science: "The Level and Nature of Autistic Intelligence." Their finding was staggering in its implications.

They administered both the Wechsler Intelligence Scales and Raven's Progressive Matrices (a nonverbal test of abstract reasoning using visual patterns) to 38 autistic children, 26 autistic adults, and matched non-autistic controls. The results:

- Autistic children scored an average of **30 percentile points higher** on Raven's than on the Wechsler.
- Individual discrepancies exceeded **70 percentile points** in some cases.
- **Zero** autistic children scored in the "high intelligence" range on the Wechsler. **One-third** scored "high intelligence" on the Raven's.
- **One-third** of autistic children scored in the "intellectual disability" range on the Wechsler. Only **5%** (1 in 20) scored that low on the Raven's.
- Autistic individuals completed Raven's items **23-42% faster** than non-autistic participants -- and the speed advantage was greater for more complex items.

These are the same children. The difference is the test, not the child.

Raven's Progressive Matrices taps visual-spatial reasoning without requiring language. It presents a matrix of visual patterns with a missing piece, and the test-taker identifies which of several options completes the pattern. It measures what psychometricians call fluid intelligence -- the ability to reason and solve novel problems independent of prior knowledge. The Wechsler, by contrast, heavily weights crystallized intelligence (accumulated knowledge) and verbal ability.

The study has been cited over 1,000 times. A follow-up fMRI study (Soulieres et al., 2009) found that autistic participants used distinctive perceptual processing strategies during Raven's, engaging posterior brain regions more heavily than controls.

**Important caveat**: The original study included speaking autistic individuals, not non-speaking participants specifically. Whether the Raven's advantage extends to non-speaking individuals has not been directly tested -- because even Raven's requires a motor response (pointing to the correct answer), which may be difficult for individuals with severe motor planning difficulties. The finding demonstrates systematic underestimation of autistic intelligence by standard tests, but the precise magnitude of this underestimation in non-speaking populations remains unknown.

## 4. The Assessment-Placement-Confirmation Loop

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-1-assessment-loop.html -->

The most insidious aspect of assessment failure is its self-reinforcing nature.

A non-speaking autistic child reaches school age and is assessed. On the Wechsler, the child scores IQ below 70 -- the "intellectual disability" range. This score enters the child's Individualized Education Program (IEP). The IEP team uses it to determine educational placement: a segregated "life skills" classroom where goals focus on self-care, basic safety, and behavioral compliance rather than academic content. Literacy instruction is not provided because "the data show" the child cannot learn to read.

Two years later, the child is re-evaluated. The same standardized tests are administered. The child, who has received no academic instruction, no cognitive stimulation beyond basic life skills, and no opportunity to develop test-taking conventions, performs at the same or lower level. The original placement is confirmed. The cycle continues.

Each re-assessment reinforces the previous one because the educational environment has been shaped by the assessment rather than challenging it. The assessment does not merely measure current ability -- it shapes future ability by determining the educational environment. This makes the first assessment a high-stakes, potentially self-fulfilling prophecy.

The cycle can be broken. When a clinician uses an adapted assessment -- Raven's, Leiter-3, or emerging eye-tracking-based methods -- the results may reveal abilities the standard test missed. Or a clinician may invoke the least dangerous assumption and provide AAC and literacy instruction without waiting for assessment confirmation. When either pathway is taken, and when the child demonstrates previously unsuspected abilities, the IEP goals are revised upward, academic instruction begins, and a different trajectory becomes possible.

Jordyn Zimmerman's story is the most dramatic illustration: 18 years in the loop, then Proloquo2Go, then two degrees and national leadership. The person who spent 18 years assessed as incapable turned out to be an exceptional scholar and advocate. The circle can be broken -- but only if someone breaks it.

## 5. The Contested Jaswal Literacy Study

In 2024, Vikram Jaswal, Ariel Lampi, and Allison Stockwell published "Literacy in nonspeaking autistic people" in the journal Autism. The study enrolled 31 non-speaking autistic participants, all with at least one year of experience using letterboard communication through RPM or S2C methods. Participants played a tapping game on an iPad, and 17 of 31 (55%) showed a reaction-time pattern consistent with knowledge of English orthographic conventions -- a "literacy signature" -- despite most never having received formal literacy instruction.

The study generated immediate controversy. A published rebuttal, "Illusions of literacy in nonspeaking autistic people" (Evidence-Based Communication Assessment and Intervention, 2024), raised several substantial criticisms: all participants were recruited from RPM/S2C programs and regularly used letter boards, so their familiarity with letters could explain the results without literacy; there was no comparison condition without the experimenter reading sentences aloud; the study did not control for potential facilitated influence in participants' communication histories; and the "literacy signature" could reflect letter familiarity from extensive letterboard use rather than actual literacy.

The Jaswal lab published detailed FAQs addressing these criticisms, arguing that letterboard familiarity alone cannot explain the specific orthographic patterns observed.

The honest assessment: the study reveals something about non-speaking participants' letter processing, but whether it constitutes evidence of "literacy" is genuinely disputed. It must be presented alongside the published rebuttal. Neither uncritical acceptance nor reflexive dismissal is warranted.

## 6. The Presuming Competence Spectrum

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-4-presuming-competence-spectrum.html -->

How should clinicians, educators, and families act when they do not know what a non-speaking person understands?

In 1984, Anne M. Donnellan (1943-2024) published "The Criterion of the Least Dangerous Assumption" in Behavioral Disorders. The core principle: "In the absence of conclusive data, educational decisions should be based on assumptions which, if incorrect, will have the least dangerous effect on the student."

Applied to non-speaking autism: if you do not know whether a non-speaking student can learn to read, it is less dangerous to assume they can (and provide literacy instruction that may be unnecessary) than to assume they cannot (and withhold literacy instruction they may have needed). The consequences of wrongly presuming incompetence -- denying education, communication, autonomy, for an entire lifetime -- are far more severe and far less reversible than the consequences of wrongly presuming competence -- providing supports that turn out to be unnecessary.

Donnellan's principle has become foundational in inclusive education and disability rights advocacy. It is central to the Autistic Self Advocacy Network's position on communication access.

But it has critiques. Some argue the principle has been stretched beyond Donnellan's original intent -- from a guide for educational decisions to a blanket presumption of high cognitive competence in all non-speaking individuals regardless of assessment data. In the facilitated communication context, critics argue that "presuming competence" can be misapplied to justify accepting facilitated communication output as authentic without verification, which may itself be the "more dangerous assumption" -- attributing sophisticated thoughts to someone who did not produce them.

Peter Lloyd-Thomas, a parent of an autistic son, proposed a middle position on his Epiphany ASD blog in 2025: neither "presume competence" nor "presume incompetence," but "stretch boundaries" -- continuously testing and expanding what each individual can do without defaulting to either extreme. This perspective comes from a parent, not from an autistic adult, which changes the framing somewhat.

The spectrum of positions looks like this: at one end, presume incompetence (the historical default, with devastating consequences). Near the center, test empirically using adapted assessments. Slightly right of center, stretch boundaries. Further right, Donnellan's least dangerous assumption. At the far right, full presumption of competence (the disability rights position). Every position on this spectrum carries risks. The argument from Donnellan and her successors is that the risks of underestimation are categorically worse than the risks of overestimation -- with the specific caveat that in the FC context, overestimation introduces its own set of harms.

## 7. Alternative Assessment Methods

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-5-alternative-assessments.html -->

If standard IQ tests are the wrong instrument, what are the alternatives?

**Raven's Progressive Matrices (RPM)**: The nonverbal test that produced the 30-percentile-point advantage. No language demands, low motor demands (pointing). Demonstrated validity for autistic populations. Limitation: still requires pointing, which may be impossible for individuals with severe motor difficulties.

**Leiter International Performance Scale (Leiter-3)**: Entirely nonverbal. Instructions are given through gestures and demonstration rather than spoken language. Can be administered with adapted response methods. Described by its publisher as "ideal for use with those who are on the autism spectrum."

**Test of Nonverbal Intelligence (TONI-4)**: Minimizes both language and cultural demands. Uses visual patterns requiring pointing or gesturing to indicate the correct answer.

**Naglieri Nonverbal Ability Test (NNAT)**: Uses geometric shapes and patterns. No reading, writing, or speaking required.

**Eye-tracking-based assessment**: The most promising emerging approach. A pilot study developed video tasks including deductive reasoning, working memory, and attention, finding that cognitive scores based on eye tracking were closely related to scores from standardized neuropsychological tests. The critical advantage: eye tracking can assess individuals who cannot point, select, or produce any voluntary motor response. A 2025 Cued Looking Paradigm (currently a bioRxiv preprint, not yet peer-reviewed) showed that 83% of minimally verbal participants demonstrated hidden language abilities through involuntary gaze patterns.

**Curriculum-based measurement**: Assessing what a person can do in naturalistic contexts rather than decontextualized test settings. Less standardized but potentially more valid for individuals whose behavior differs dramatically across contexts.

The unsolved problem: even "nonverbal" tests typically require motor responses, sustained attention to examiner-directed tasks, and understanding of implicit test-taking conventions. Truly motor-free, instruction-free assessment remains the holy grail. Eye-tracking approaches this ideal but is not yet standardized or widely available.

A key challenge that crosses all alternative assessment methods is the "best day" problem. Non-speaking individuals' performance can vary dramatically across days, settings, emotional states, and the presence or absence of familiar people. A single assessment session captures one snapshot. An individual who performs well in a calm, familiar environment with a trusted examiner may perform entirely differently in an unfamiliar clinic under fluorescent lights with a stranger asking questions. This variability is not a sign of inconsistent ability -- it reflects the sensitivity of performance to context, a well-documented feature of autism. Assessment methods that rely on a single session risk under- or over-estimating ability depending on which snapshot they capture. Repeated assessment across contexts would be more valid but is rarely practical within existing educational and clinical systems.

## 8. The "Profound Autism" Label Debate

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-6-profound-autism-debate.html -->

In 2021, the Lancet Commission on the Future of Care and Clinical Research of Autism proposed the administrative term "profound autism" for autistic individuals who cannot live independently and require 24-hour care. A 2025 INSAR consensus definition established specific criteria through a multi-stakeholder Delphi process.

The arguments for the label are sympathetic. Parents of high-support-needs children argue that their children's needs are invisible when grouped with the entire autism spectrum. They argue a distinct category could drive targeted research funding and service provision. They describe children who fall "off the cliff" of services at age 21.

The arguments against are also compelling. Research has found the label "does not predict strengths or help plan supports" (PMC10576471, 2023). It has been called "isolating, dehumanizing, and misleading" for professionals making prognoses (MDPI, 2023). Autistic self-advocates have resisted functioning labels for over 30 years as misleading and reductive. ASAN and other organizations oppose the label, arguing it revives discredited functioning categories under a new name.

The label conflates current behavior with immutable capacity. A person labeled "profound" at age 5 based on lack of speech may develop robust communication through AAC at age 18 -- as Zimmerman's trajectory demonstrates. The label risks creating a formal category of people presumed incompetent, undermining the very principle that changed Zimmerman's life.

There is a meta-irony at the center of this debate. Non-speaking autistic individuals -- the people most directly affected by the label -- are the least represented in the decision-making process about whether and how it should be applied. A label partly justified by lack of communication was developed without full communication access for those it labels.

## 9. Legal and Educational Consequences

The stakes of assessment are not abstract. They are codified in law and operationalized in IEP meetings.

The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) guarantees a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE) for all children with disabilities, delivered through Individualized Education Programs (IEPs). Assessment determines what those IEPs contain and where the child is placed.

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) requires "appropriate auxiliary aids and services where necessary to ensure effective communication." Assessment determines what accommodations are deemed "necessary."

The placement consequences are stark:
- IQ below 70 on a standard test leads to placement in a "life skills" or segregated classroom, focus on self-care rather than academics, no literacy instruction, and perpetuation of measured disability.
- IQ in the normal or above-average range (via Raven's or adapted assessment) leads to inclusive classroom access, academic instruction, communication tool provision, and opportunity to demonstrate and develop abilities.

Adults assessed as having intellectual disability may be placed under guardianship, losing the legal right to make decisions about their own lives, medical care, and communication methods. Non-speaking individuals in the legal system face additional barriers: their communication forms "may be viewed as eccentric or suspicious, leading to misunderstandings and negative outcomes."

## 10. The Zimmerman Case Study

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-7-zimmerman-timeline.html -->

Jordyn Zimmerman's trajectory is the single most important illustration in this entire domain -- not because it is typical, but because it demonstrates definitively what becomes possible when the assessment barrier is removed.

For 18 years, Zimmerman was placed in segregated "life skills" classrooms. No one gave her a communication tool of any kind. She received no literacy instruction. Her assessments presumably classified her as having significant intellectual disability. She was presumed incompetent.

At age 18, she received Proloquo2Go -- a standard AAC app on an iPad. She used it independently from the start, with no facilitator involvement. This is not a contested communication method. It is the same technology endorsed by ASHA, AAP, and every professional organization.

After receiving communication access:
- Bachelor's degree in Education Policy from Ohio University
- Master's of Education from Boston College
- Board Chair of CommunicationFIRST
- Apple Distinguished Educator
- Two-time appointee to the U.S. President's Committee for People with Intellectual Disabilities
- Named to the inaugural 2025 Forbes Accessibility 100
- Keynote speaker worldwide
- Published in The New York Times and The Boston Globe

Her intelligence did not change at age 18. Her brain at 17 was the same brain at 19. What changed was access. The assessment system that classified her as intellectually disabled was catastrophically wrong, and the consequences of that error spanned 18 years.

Zimmerman's case does not prove all non-speaking individuals have hidden academic ability. Non-speaking autism encompasses individuals with a wide range of cognitive profiles, and some genuinely have co-occurring intellectual disabilities. But her case proves that the current assessment system can be catastrophically wrong, that the consequences of underestimation are devastating and irreversible, and that the ceiling of possibility is far, far higher than standard assessment suggests.

## 11. The Unresolved Question

The tension at the heart of this domain remains unresolved.

The "presume competence" position demands that every non-speaking individual receive communication access and academic instruction regardless of assessment scores. The evidence strongly supports this: standard assessments are unreliable, the consequences of underestimation are devastating, and individual cases demonstrate extraordinary untapped potential.

The "measure accurately" position demands better tools and more rigorous evidence before making population-level claims about hidden competence. Critics note that individual success stories cannot establish what proportion of non-speaking autistic people have dramatically underestimated abilities, and that some individuals may genuinely have intellectual disabilities.

Both positions contain truth. The answer is not to choose between them but to pursue both simultaneously: presume competence and act accordingly (provide communication, provide education, expect growth), while simultaneously developing better assessment tools that can measure abilities without barriers. The worst response -- and the historical default -- is to do neither: to withhold communication access because assessments say a person cannot learn, while relying on assessments that cannot measure what they claim to measure.

The evidence is clear on one point: the current system has been wrong more often and more catastrophically than its defenders have acknowledged. Until assessment tools exist that can measure non-speaking minds without requiring those minds to speak, point, sit still, and follow verbal instructions, every IQ score assigned to a non-speaking autistic person should be treated as provisional at best and misleading at worst.

## 12. Edge Cases and Honest Complications

Intellectual honesty requires acknowledging several complications that resist easy narrative.

**Some non-speaking autistic individuals genuinely have co-occurring intellectual disability.** Not everyone whose abilities are hard to measure has hidden competence. Some individuals have cognitive limitations that affect their capacity across domains. The challenge is that current assessment tools cannot reliably distinguish between genuine intellectual disability and test-induced underestimation. Until they can, the least dangerous assumption applies -- but it is important not to stretch the Dawson-Mottron finding into a claim that all non-speaking autistic people have normal or high intelligence. The finding demonstrates systematic underestimation, not universal hidden genius. Even on the Raven's, 5% of autistic children in the sample scored in the intellectual disability range.

**A high Raven's score does not guarantee functional abilities.** Intelligence is not a single dimension. A person who demonstrates strong visual-spatial reasoning on the Raven's may still struggle with language processing, executive function, or daily living skills. A strength in one domain does not guarantee strengths in others. Assessment should identify specific abilities and needs, not produce a single number that determines a person's entire educational trajectory.

**Late-life cognitive changes.** Some non-speaking autistic individuals experience cognitive changes in adulthood, including regression, catatonia, and skills loss. Assessment methods designed for stable ability levels may not capture dynamic trajectories. A person who demonstrates high ability at one point may not maintain that level, and a person who appears low-functioning at one point may improve dramatically with appropriate support.

**The intersection with poverty, race, and culture.** Non-speaking autistic individuals in low-resource settings may never receive any form of adapted assessment. The children most likely to be trapped in the assessment-placement loop are those whose families have the fewest resources to challenge it. The intersection of poverty, race, and disability in assessment outcomes is virtually unstudied, but the consequences are likely severe: children of color and children in poverty are already more likely to be placed in restrictive educational settings, and the additional barrier of non-speaking autism compounds this disparity.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Raven's Progressive Matrices (RPM)** | Nonverbal test of abstract reasoning using visual patterns; requires identifying the missing piece from a matrix of designs |
| **Wechsler Intelligence Scales** | Most widely used IQ tests; WISC for children, WAIS for adults; include verbal and performance components |
| **Least dangerous assumption** | Donnellan's (1984) principle that when data are inconclusive, one should assume competence rather than incompetence |
| **Presuming competence** | The practice of assuming a person understands and can learn until proven otherwise |
| **Profound autism** | Proposed administrative term (Lancet Commission, 2021) for autistic individuals requiring 24-hour care who cannot live independently; 2025 INSAR consensus criteria established |
| **IDEA** | Individuals with Disabilities Education Act; guarantees FAPE for children with disabilities |
| **FAPE** | Free Appropriate Public Education; the IDEA mandate for education suited to each child's needs |
| **IEP** | Individualized Education Program; specifies educational goals and services for a student with disabilities |
| **ADA** | Americans with Disabilities Act; prohibits disability discrimination and mandates communication accommodations |
| **Leiter-3** | Entirely nonverbal intelligence test using gestures and demonstration rather than verbal instructions |
| **TONI-4** | Test of Nonverbal Intelligence; minimizes language and cultural demands |
| **Fluid intelligence (Gf)** | The ability to reason and solve novel problems independent of prior knowledge; what Raven's measures |
| **Crystallized intelligence (Gc)** | Accumulated knowledge and skills; what verbal IQ subtests heavily weight |
| **Apraxia** | Difficulty planning and executing voluntary motor movements despite intact motor function |
