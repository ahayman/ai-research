# Voice Identity, Ownership, and Agency in AAC

## Research Notes

---

## 1. "This Is My Voice" — AAC Identity Research

### 1.1 Wickenden's Ethnographic Study: "Whose Voice Is That?"

Mary Wickenden (2011) conducted an 18-month ethnographic study with disabled teenagers aged 10-19 who use AAC. The study, published in *Disability Studies Quarterly*, involved participant observation and extended narrative conversations with 9 key young people in a variety of contexts, yielding rich data about how they see themselves and how they are seen by others. Additional input came from other teenage AAC users, parents, school staff, and three adult AAC users who served as research advisors. The paper focuses on how the teenagers perceive that their use of AAC impacts on their identities, how individuals' voices emerge in co-constructed conversations, and issues around representation. The study was informed by theory from childhood studies, disability studies, and anthropology.

**Key finding:** The study explored the fundamental distinction between "using a voice" (a tool for communication) and "having MY voice" (an extension of identity). AAC users construct their identities through and around their communication systems, but the synthetic voice of the device raises persistent questions about authenticity, ownership, and representation.

- **Source:** Wickenden, M. (2011). "Whose Voice is That?: Issues of Identity, Voice and Representation Arising in an Ethnographic Study of the Lives of Disabled Teenagers who use Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC)." *Disability Studies Quarterly*, 31(4). [Link](https://dsq-sds.org/article/id/1011/)

### 1.2 The Stephen Hawking Case: Voice as Adopted Identity

Stephen Hawking provides perhaps the most iconic illustration of synthetic voice becoming personal identity. Since first adopting the CallText5010 with its "Perfect Paul" voice (based on MIT scientist Dennis Klatt's voice) in the mid-1980s, Hawking had many opportunities to upgrade components of his communication system but refused. In 2006, Hawking explained: **"I keep it because I have not heard a voice I like better and because I have identified with it."**

He used this same voice for 32 years until his death in 2018, even as the original hardware became increasingly problematic. A team from Cambridge and Palo Alto eventually emulated the workings of the CallText 5010 on a Raspberry Pi, which Hawking used from January 2018 until his death in March. The voice had become so deeply associated with Hawking's identity that it was inseparable from public perception of him.

**Key insight:** This demonstrates that voice identity in AAC is not necessarily about replicating one's biological voice — it can be about the voice one *chooses* and *identifies with* over time.

- **Sources:**
  - [Stephen Hawking's Eternal Voice | MIT Press Reader](https://thereader.mitpress.mit.edu/stephen-hawkings-eternal-voice/)
  - [Stephen Hawking's Voice, Made By A Man Who Lost His Own](https://beyondwords.io/blog/stephen-hawkings-voice/)

### 1.3 Voice Rejection and Device Abandonment

Research shows that AAC users sometimes reject their speech-generating devices specifically due to the identity or pitch of the voice. Synthetic voices are often perceived as impersonal and fail to capture the unique voice and identity of the user. Communication is a crucial aspect of personal identity and social context, and its impairment can devastate a person's role in their environment and negatively affect their emotional balance.

- **Source:** [NCBI Bookshelf: AAC and Voice Products and Technologies](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK453284/)

### 1.4 Psychological Impact of Voice Personalization

The recognition of familiar voice qualities — whether one's own banked voice or a chosen voice — can significantly enhance well-being. Voice banking has been introduced as a strategy enabling individuals to "speak" in a voice that reflects their identity rather than a generic synthetic tone, providing both emotional and psychological benefits.

## 2. Voice and Self-Determination

### 2.1 Self-Determination Theory Applied to AAC

Self-Determination Theory (SDT), developed by Deci and Ryan, identifies three psychological needs that drive human growth: **autonomy**, **competence**, and **relatedness**. Applied to AAC:

- **Autonomy** connects directly to competence, relationships, and overall well-being. For people who communicate via AAC, autonomy goes far deeper than independence — it is about whether choices are respected, whether voices are recognized, and whether people are able to direct their own lives.
- **Competence** in AAC involves the ability to effectively use one's communication system.
- **Relatedness** requires the ability to connect authentically with others, which is mediated by voice quality and identity.

Consideration of an individual's preference for communication modality — including the type of speech output (digitized, synthetic), the particular synthesized voice, and/or the speaker from whom digitized speech is drawn — has been identified as a way to promote individual autonomy by allowing people to determine the methods they prefer when communicating.

- **Sources:**
  - [Autonomy and AAC Through the Lens of Self-Determination | AAC Language Lab](https://aaclanguagelab.com/articles/myfilesMH2Prof?mode=view)
  - [PrAACtical AAC: Self-Determination](https://praacticalaac.org/tag/self-determination/)

### 2.2 Communication Autonomy vs. Independence

Communication autonomy for AAC users means the ability to express themselves freely — the user gets to dictate when and what they choose to communicate, with the liberty to initiate communication or respond on their own accord. Voice selection is a key component of this autonomy.

- **Source:** [Avaz: Autonomous Communication & AAC](https://www.avazapp.com/blog/autonomous-communication-aac/)

### 2.3 Impact on Communication Confidence and Willingness to Communicate

The willingness to communicate model identifies psychosocial factors that influence communicative competence: **motivation**, **confidence**, **attitude**, and **resilience**. Confidence determines the individual's propensity to act — to attempt to communicate in a given situation. Confidence is largely affected by the specific situation rather than underlying personality alone.

**Key psychosocial factors for AAC success:**
- **Motivation** — the individual's drive to communicate with others
- **Attitude** — willingness to use AAC
- **Confidence** — propensity to actually attempt communication
- **Resilience** — persistence in the face of communication failures

Despite the importance of these psychosocial factors in determining outcomes, there has been limited attention to them in AAC research and practice. Only a very small percentage of intervention studies published in *Augmentative and Alternative Communication* over the past decade focused on psychosocial factors.

- **Sources:**
  - [USSAAC: Confidence, Willingness to Communicate, and AAC](https://ussaac.org/speakup/articles/confidence-willingness-to-communicate-and-aac/)
  - [NWACS: Psychosocial Competence](https://www.nwacs.info/blog/2021/8/communicative-competence-psychosocial)

### 2.4 Adolescent AAC Users and Identity Expression

Adolescent AAC users experience a heightened need for positive self-image and participation in conversation. Considering the preoccupation with appearance at this age, the stigma of a disability can be especially difficult. A limited range of voice choices raises questions about the role of voice in reflecting the user's communication identity. If AAC systems are attractive, adolescent AAC users may be motivated to use their device, serving to enhance self-esteem and facilitate interaction. Poor voice intelligibility and naturalness have negative implications for self-expression.

- **Source:** [RESNA: Adolescent Preferences and Priorities for AAC Devices](https://www.resna.org/sites/default/files/legacy/conference/proceedings/2009/CAC/Student%20Papers/Dowling.html)

## 3. Populations with Specific Voice Identity Needs

### 3.1 ALS/MND: Progressive Speech Loss and Voice Banking

#### The Psychological Experience of Losing One's Voice

For people living with ALS (amyotrophic lateral sclerosis) / MND (motor neurone disease), the progressive loss of speech is one of the most devastating aspects of the condition. While communication devices support the functional side of speaking, losing one's natural voice is a profoundly emotional experience. Voice banking offers emotional and psychological benefits by enabling individuals to "speak" in a voice that reflects their identity.

#### Timeline Pressures

Research on speech decline in ALS reveals critical timelines:

- Most people with **bulbar-onset ALS** experience significant reductions in speaking rate approximately **23 months** after first symptoms
- Speech becomes somewhat unintelligible at approximately **32 months**
- Speech remains adequate on average for **18 months** from the first bulbar symptom
- Speaking rate below **100-120 words per minute** marks the initiation of accelerated decline in speech intelligibility (below 85%)
- Changes in lip and jaw movements anticipate the drop in speech intelligibility by approximately **3 months**

**Critical clinical finding:** The onset of bulbar symptoms indicated the time to loss of speech better than ALS diagnosis or the first speech therapy evaluation. People with bulbar-onset ALS are more susceptible to delayed evaluation and AAC intervention.

- **Sources:**
  - [Rate of speech decline in ALS | Scientific Reports](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-022-19651-1)
  - [ALS News Today: Speech Problems](https://alsnewstoday.com/speech-problems/)
  - [Predicting Early Bulbar Decline in ALS | PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4468279/)

#### Voice Banking Methods

**Voice Banking:** The process of creating a personal synthetic voice by recording hundreds to thousands of phrases and sentences. Current technology requires 600-3,500 sentences (at least 8 hours of recording) for a good quality English synthetic voice (e.g., ModelTalker). Newer AI-based services (e.g., Acapela My-Own-Voice) have reduced this to as few as 30-50 phrases.

**Message Banking:** Recording specific words, phrases, stories, or sounds in one's own natural voice with personal inflection and intonation. The BCH Message Banking Process supports people to authentically record and store words, phrases, sentences, and personally meaningful sounds/stories. Unlike voice banking, playback is limited to what has been recorded — messages cannot be used for generating novel speech by spelling.

**Key distinction:** Voice banking creates a synthetic version of one's voice for open-ended communication; message banking preserves the authentic emotional quality of specific important phrases (e.g., "I love you," a laugh, a catchphrase).

- **Sources:**
  - [ALS Association: Voice Preservation](https://www.als.org/navigating-als/resources/fyi-voice-preservation)
  - [Boston Children's Hospital: Voice Banking](https://www.childrenshospital.org/programs/als-augmentative-communication-program/protocol-assessment-considerations/voice-preservation)
  - [Talk To Me Technologies: Voice and Message Banking](https://www.talktometechnologies.com/pages/voice-and-message-banking)

#### "I Will Always Be Me" — Transforming Voice Banking

"I Will Always Be Me" is a first-of-its-kind book created by Dell Technologies and Intel in partnership with the MND Association and Rolls-Royce. Written by New York Times bestselling author Jill Twiss from the perspective of a person living with MND, it integrates voice banking into the reading experience. People make a recording of themselves reading the short, illustrated storybook aloud, which is processed digitally and expanded for later use.

**Impact:** The project streamlined voice banking from an average of **3 months** to **20-25 minutes**. A documentary was created showing people with MND and their families experiencing the book for the first time. More than **72%** of those recently diagnosed with MND are now using "I Will Always Be Me" to bank their voice.

- **Sources:**
  - [I Will Always Be Me](https://www.iwillalwaysbeme.com/)
  - [MND Association: I Will Always Be Me Launch](https://www.mndassociation.org/media/latest-news/i-will-always-be-me-worlds-first-e-book-banks-your-voice-launched)
  - [ALS News Today: I Will Always Be Me](https://alsnewstoday.com/news/i-wll-always-be-me-lets-als-patients-voice-bank-with-feeling/)

### 3.2 Head/Neck Cancer: Post-Laryngectomy Voice Changes

#### "Not Sounding Like Myself Anymore"

Loss of voice following total laryngectomy poses a fundamental threat to self-image and identity. Since voice is essential to psychological identity, laryngectomy may pose significant adjustment problems. Loss of vocal identity is particularly distressing because voice plays a central role in personal expression and social interaction.

#### Psychological Impact

- **16-53%** of laryngectomized patients experience depression or anxiety (range across studies; up to 53% on BDI-II screening), often due to stigmatization, changes in self-identity, and difficulty maintaining social relationships
- Patients experience higher levels of social isolation, frustration, and reduced self-esteem due to difficulties in verbal expression
- Social withdrawal and reduced participation in professional activities are commonly reported
- Even after successful voice restoration, voice changes carry stigma due to communication challenges, potentially limiting overall improvement in quality of life

#### Identity and Adaptation

The personal conviction associated with "communicating with one's own voice" is integral to the adaptation process. Elements of adaptation include orthotic management, emotional management, and coping with changes in speech production methods — all of which are crucial for continuity of voice use.

- **Sources:**
  - [PMC: Reacquiring New Vocalization in Laryngectomy Patients](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10904916/)
  - [ASCO Post: A Laryngectomy Altered How I See Myself](https://ascopost.com/issues/november-25-2022/a-laryngectomy-altered-how-i-see-myself-in-the-world/)
  - [PMC: Psychological Distress and Quality of Life in Laryngeal Cancer](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12250225/)

### 3.3 Children: Growing Up with a Synthetic Voice

#### Historical Limitations

Historically, there were very limited child voice options available in AAC devices — typically only one higher-pitched variation of adult voices. When the Speak for Yourself app was created, there were initially no child voice options from text-to-speech providers. A few years later, Acapela and AssistiveWare created the Josh and Ella child voices, which were then licensed by other apps. Speech synthesis remains expensive to create and incorporate.

#### Age-Appropriate Voice Needs

By adolescence, communication is not just about words — it is about identity. Teens using AAC want to be understood, respected, and accepted — not treated differently. AAC systems should be regularly reassessed as the child grows and their needs evolve. Longitudinal research has followed AAC users from early intervention through high school (ages 2-15), documenting how changes in AAC technology and contextual factors contribute to outcomes.

#### VocaliD Voice Blending for Children

VocaliD's technology blends brief samples of a recipient's residual vocalization with a comprehensive speech database recorded by a matched donor, resulting in a voice that sounds like the recipient in age, personality, and vocal identity but is as clear and understandable as the donor. More than 10,000 donors have signed up. Family members and users attribute increased AAC device usage in educational and social settings, as well as improved self-esteem and quality of life, to this innovation.

- **Sources:**
  - [Speak For Yourself: The Ever-Evolving AAC Voice Options](https://speakforyourself.org/the-ever-evolving-aac-voice-options/)
  - [PMC: Growing up with AAC in the Digital Age](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8281386/)
  - [NIDCD: Voice Matters! Rupal Patel Builds Personalized Voices](https://www.nidcd.nih.gov/newsletter/2014/spring/voice-matters-speech-researcher-rupal-patel-builds-personalized-voices-people)

### 3.4 Transgender/Non-Binary AAC Users

#### The Vocabulary Gap

Words describing gender and sexuality are not included in symbol-based AAC apps. While some have the word "gay," none allow users to describe identities like being nonbinary, trans, or genderqueer. AAC users need access to words to describe themselves and their identities — these words should not be denied because of assumptions they cannot understand.

One CommunicationFIRST contributor describes specific identity terms they cannot express in existing AAC apps, including terms for neurogender, gendervague, asexual, demiromantic, grey-aromantic, polyamorous, and m-spec identities.

#### Healthcare Access Barriers

Many trans part-time AAC users decide to **force themselves to speak** for care related to gender transition out of fear that using AAC will lead to being deemed incompetent to know their own genders and consent to transition. This represents a devastating intersection of ableism and transphobia.

#### Voice Identity and Gender Expression

Gender is one of the most salient identity markers conveyed through the human voice. For gender non-conforming speakers such as transgender and non-binary individuals, vocal characteristics may diverge from binary norms, altering both their self-perception of voice gender and how others perceive it.

Current standards for "voice feminization" rely on gender binary norms and may not represent the goals of transgender women, nonbinary clients, or transfeminine clients. Clinical guidance for voice and communication therapy in gender-affirming care is very limited, and professionals providing these services are not always integrated into gender clinics.

- **Sources:**
  - [CommunicationFIRST: What Does Gender Have To Do with Presuming Competence?](https://communicationfirst.org/what-does-gender-have-to-do-with-presuming-competence/)
  - [Springer: Harmonizing Identities Scoping Review](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10803-025-06768-1)
  - [ASHA: Gender Affirming Voice and Communication](https://www.asha.org/practice-portal/professional-issues/gender-affirming-voice-and-communication/)

### 3.5 Cultural/Linguistic Identity

#### Accent and Dialect Representation

Accent helps individuals express themselves and voice their identity. Language is intrinsically tied to cultural identity. For children in the Black community, this connection plays a crucial role in communication and should be reflected in AAC systems. African American English is a rich, valid form of expression, and AAC systems should be adjusted to reflect this linguistic variation.

#### Creating Diverse Voices

AAC partners including AssistiveWare, PRC-Saltillo, and Tobii Dynavox collaborated to create the first African American English digital voices. Acapela's repertoire includes voices in 30+ languages, with male and children's voices in the African American English family under development.

#### Language Coverage Gaps

Only 7 languages — English, French, German, Italian, Japanese, Portuguese, and Spanish — are covered by the voice assistants of the three main technology companies (Google, Amazon, Apple). Nearly half of the world's languages are endangered, and most have limited digital presence with very few supported by commercial voice tools.

#### Bias in Speech Technology

Black, brown, and Indigenous speakers often see far worse recognition rates than white counterparts. The average word error rate for white subjects was **19%** compared to **35%** for Black subjects speaking in African-American Vernacular English. Members speaking Spanglish and Chicano English had the least accurate transcriptions.

- **Sources:**
  - [PrAACtical AAC: Cultural-Linguistic AAC Intervention](https://praacticalaac.org/praactical/cultural-linguistic-aac-intervention-a-framework-for-consideration/)
  - [Acapela Group: African American English Digital Voice](https://www.acapela-group.com/news/african-american-english-digital-voice/)
  - [Murf AI: Eliminating Lack of Diversity in TTS](https://murf.ai/blog/eliminating-lack-of-diversity-in-text-to-speech)

### 3.6 Autistic Individuals

#### Mixed Preferences

Research presents nuanced findings on voice preferences among autistic individuals. Some studies report that autistic children exhibit a preference for mechanic voices, potentially because a social robot's controlled and consistent voice may aid perception and facilitate entrainment. However, other research shows convergence toward naturally recorded words over synthetic voice, suggesting many prefer more natural-sounding voices.

#### Personalization and Identity

Some autistic individuals find generic synthesized voices to be robotic-sounding and of the wrong pitch, while having a more natural voice can support independence and self-esteem by providing "an actual natural extension of yourself." Generic synthetic voices can feel unsatisfying to children and young adults who want their identity expressed in a voice that is all their own.

#### Speaking Autistic Adults Who Use AAC

The "Everyone Deserves AAC" study (Donaldson, corbin, & McCoy, 2021) examined experiences of six speaking autistic adults who use AAC. Key findings:
- Participants identified AAC as a communication choice as adults, after using speech during childhood
- Forced communication choices have greatly impacted their lives
- Different communication methods meet needs at different times — the fluidity of autistic traits may lead a speaking AAC user to choose one method over another depending on situation, environment, and communication partner
- Replacing autistic language with nonautistic language may lead to repeated mis- or non-recognition of autistic identity

- **Sources:**
  - [ASHA Perspectives: "Everyone Deserves AAC"](https://pubs.asha.org/doi/10.1044/2021_PERSP-20-00220)
  - [PMC: Including Speaking and Nonspeaking Autistic Voice in Research](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8992839/)

## 4. "Aging Up AAC" Research

### 4.1 Study Overview

"Aging Up AAC: An Introspection on Augmentative and Alternative Communication Applications for Autistic Adults" conducted in-depth interviews with **12 autistic adults** to find pain points in current AAC and determine what technological advances they might find helpful. Published on arXiv (2024).

### 4.2 Key Findings on Voice Identity

- Participants want **inclusive and diverse TTS voices** so they can find a voice that matches their identity, not necessarily their body's voice
- One of the most common complaints was that there are **not enough nonbinary or middle-pitch voice options**, which becomes even more limiting when apps prohibit basic customizations such as adjusting pitch
- There is a **large overlap between transgender and autistic populations**, making gender-diverse speech synthesis particularly valuable to the autistic community
- One participant using he/him pronouns dislikes men's voices and prefers to pitch down child/teen TTS

### 4.3 Direct Participant Quotes

A transgender participant stated: **"I am trans, but I deal with age dysphoria as well, which is part of why I like [this voice]. Having a voice that sounds right is, therefore really, really important."**

### 4.4 Recommendations from the Research

- Research should continue to look at **creating voices not based on voice cloning** and understanding how trans-feminine, trans-masculine, nonbinary, and genderqueer people sound and/or want to sound with TTS
- Future research needs to look at **how people feel when using these voices** for daily AAC and how to create TTS voices customizable by trans users
- **Adult-centered AAC** might include: no recording of button presses, "adult" vocabulary, more expressive adult TTS voices, and symbol libraries that feel more mature

- **Source:** [Aging Up AAC | arXiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.17730v3)

### 4.5 Harmonizing Identities: Autistic Trans and Gender Diverse Individuals

A 2025 scoping review, "Harmonizing Identities," examined how autistic trans and gender diverse (TGD) individuals face unique voice and communication challenges compounded by minority stressors. Drawing on **40 sources** (29 peer-reviewed articles, 8 clinical guidelines, 2 books, 1 position statement):

- **96.8%** of identified challenges pertained to communication, with fewer (3.2%) focused on voice-specific issues
- Significant gaps remain in addressing the unique voice and communication needs of this population regarding voice dysphoria, camouflaging versus identity disclosure, and communication within healthcare settings
- The review calls for improved clinical guidelines and specialized training for speech pathologists

- **Source:** [Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders: Harmonizing Identities](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10803-025-06768-1)

## 5. Psychological Research

### 5.1 Voice as Social Signal

Voices are idiosyncratic representations of individuals, with anatomical, physiological, and psychological characteristics unique to each person. The voice is not only a means to deliver language but also signals paralinguistic information: prosody, emotional expressiveness, and self-identifying characteristics such as **age**, **gender**, **ethnicity**, **geographical identity**, and **health status**.

When perceiving an unfamiliar voice, listeners recognize one or more physical, psychological, and/or social attributes, which may give rise to a complex percept (e.g., "middle-aged, tall, and confident").

- **Source:** [PMC: Flexible Voices: Identity Perception from Variable Vocal Signals](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6425070/)

### 5.2 Body Schema and Voice Ownership

Bodies of research address self-perception and voice separately, and very little attention has been given to the relationship between these two constructs — or how alteration of one may affect the other. If one's voice changes, does this disrupt self-concept and self-identity? Body awareness is closely linked to self-identity and is linked to the constructs of interoception and multisensory integration.

Research on "vocal congruence" — the alignment between one's voice and sense of self — measured through interoceptive awareness represents an emerging field connecting voice to embodied identity.

- **Source:** [ScienceDirect: Vocal Congruence — The Voice and the Self](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0892199719302930)

### 5.3 Voice Variability and Identity Perception

Human voices are extremely variable — the same person can sound very different when speaking, laughing, shouting, or whispering. To recognize someone from their voice, a listener must generalize across these different vocal signals. Within-person variability is a key factor in identity perception that approaches emphasizing only between-person variability fail to capture.

- **Source:** [Springer: Flexible Voices — Identity Perception from Variable Vocal Signals](https://link.springer.com/article/10.3758/s13423-018-1497-7)

### 5.4 The "Uncanny Valley" of Voice

#### Research Findings

The "uncanny valley" concept, originally proposed by Masahiro Mori for visual humanoid design, applies to synthetic voices:

- Results indicate a **vocal uncanny valley driven by deviations from typical human voices**. While voices can fall into an uncanny valley, successfully synthesized voices may escape it
- A robot with a human voice, or a human being with a synthetic voice, is perceived as **eerier** than a robot with a synthetic voice or a human with a human voice — the **mismatch** creates eeriness
- When the ratings of human-likeness for both voice and speaker characteristics were higher, they seemed **less eerie** to participants
- Voices perceived as more humanlike were more liked by participants

**Key implication for AAC:** A perfectly natural-sounding synthetic voice may not trigger uncanny valley effects. However, a voice that is *almost* right — particularly when paired with a human user — can create an unsettling mismatch. This suggests voice banking and cloning should aim for either clearly synthetic or very high-quality naturalistic output, avoiding the "almost-right" middle ground.

- **Sources:**
  - [ScienceDirect: Deviation from Typical Organic Voices](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2451958824000630)
  - [PMC: Mismatch in Human Realism of Face and Voice](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3485769/)
  - [PMC: Humanlike Synthesized Voices Perceived as Less Eerie](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7772241/)

### 5.5 Communication Partner Perceptions of AAC Voice Quality

#### Stigma and Device Use

Parents initially worry that AAC devices will single their children out to peers as being different. There is a contrast in the experience of societal stigma: parent-carers acknowledge a real impact, whereas clinicians attribute it to perception. The discrepancy is largely context-dependent, with limited knowledge of and exposure to AACs in the broader community compared to structured environments like schools or clinics.

#### Synthetic Voice Perception

Attitudes toward users of synthetic speech are **positively related to how easy the synthetic speech is to listen to**. Synthetic voices produced by AAC devices are perceived as impersonal and lacking in expressivity and emotion. Partner reauditorization (repeated exposure) may improve attitudes and contribute to ease of understanding aided messages and increased likelihood of peer interactions.

#### Impact of Voice Output on Conversations

Research on voice output in AAC-supported conversations (e.g., for persons with Alzheimer's disease) demonstrates that voice output quality directly affects communication dynamics and partner engagement.

- **Sources:**
  - [ASHA: Effect of Partner Reauditorization on Attitudes](https://pubs.asha.org/doi/10.1044/2017_AJSLP-16-0242)
  - [PMC: Barriers in AAC Use](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11197385/)

### 5.6 Impact of Mismatched Voice on Perception — Infantilization

People use loud, higher-pitched, sing-songy voices to talk to people with disabilities. Staff remove swear words from an adult's AAC device because they do not think the words are appropriate. This pattern — **treating disabled teens and adults as children** — is called infantilization.

Studies show that an individual, when infantilized, is overwhelmingly likely to feel disrespected, and such individuals may report a sense of transgression akin to **dehumanization**. From a patient's perspective, this is seen as disrespectful and patronizing. The choice of an age-appropriate, dignity-preserving voice in AAC is directly tied to how communication partners treat the AAC user.

- **Source:** [NWACS: AAC and Consent, Safety, & Dignity](https://nwacs.info/aac-and-consent-safety-dignity)

## 6. Clinical Guidelines

### 6.1 SLP Best Practices for Voice Selection

Speech-language pathologists (SLPs) should conduct a culturally and linguistically relevant, comprehensive assessment of the individual's speech, language, and overall communication ability. Device selection depends entirely on individual client needs rather than universal "best" options. SLPs develop familiarity with multiple systems to match recommendations to specific client profiles, motor abilities, cognitive levels, and communication goals.

**Key assessment factors (ranked by SLP importance):**
1. Child expressive and receptive language abilities
2. Cognitive ability
3. Diagnosis
4. Chronological age

**Barriers identified:** SLPs identified caregiver buy-in and carryover across providers as the most significant barriers to AAC implementation.

- **Source:** [ASHA: Augmentative and Alternative Communication](https://www.asha.org/practice-portal/professional-issues/augmentative-and-alternative-communication/)

### 6.2 Voice Banking Timing for Degenerative Conditions

#### ALS Association Recommendations

- Voice and message banking should begin **as soon as possible** while speech is still clear and intelligible
- Even if someone has waited, voice banking can begin after mild changes to speech
- Voice banking is recommended at time of diagnosis, not waiting for speech decline
- Team Gleason covers the cost for anyone in the USA officially diagnosed with ALS

#### MND Association (UK) Recommendations

- Early intervention is advantageous to ensure equipment is accepted and available when needed
- The MND Association covers costs (reduced from normal fee of £175-£300) for patients in England, Wales, and Northern Ireland
- Applications should be made before paying, as retrospective funding is not available

#### Clinical Timing Guidelines

- Speaking rate of approximately **120 words per minute** marks the initiation of accelerated decline — voice banking should ideally be completed before reaching this threshold
- Articulatory and phonatory predictors are most responsive to early bulbar deterioration and can detect decline before speech intelligibility drops
- For bulbar-onset ALS, onset of bulbar symptoms is a better predictor of time to speech loss than diagnosis date

- **Sources:**
  - [ALS Association: Voice Preservation](https://www.als.org/navigating-als/resources/fyi-voice-preservation)
  - [MND Association: Voice Banking](https://www.mndassociation.org/sites/default/files/public/2025-01/P10-Voice-banking.pdf)
  - [ALS Association: Voice Banking Program](https://www.als.org/support/programs/voice-banking)

### 6.3 Insurance and Funding

- **Voice banking is not covered by insurance** in the United States

## 7. Emotional Expression Through Voice (Prosody)

### 7.1 The Prosody Gap in AAC

Emotional prosody — the paralinguistic aspects of language that convey emotion through pitch, loudness, timbre, speech rate, and pauses — is critical to human communication. Key acoustic features:

- **Pitch** is the most prominent feature: wider pitch range and higher average pitch are associated with positive emotions (joy, excitement), while flatter pitch contour is linked to sadness or anger
- **Rhythm and tempo** are key components of emotional prosody
- **Voice quality, shimmer, and mel-frequency cepstrum** all contribute to emotional perception

Over time, humans become experienced at using prosody to form and maintain social positions, which influences communication partner behavior. The human voice compared to TTS recordings shows higher accuracy for comprehension and is rated as more pleasant and easier to understand.

### 7.2 Limitations of Synthetic Emotional Expression

**Expressivity remains one of the major challenges** in achieving variability in TTS systems. While intelligibility is generally accomplished, voices do not always sound natural and lack "humanity." The role of expression authenticity in the acoustics-emotion mapping is of great interest as humans become surrounded by synthetic speech.

This has direct implications for AAC: flat-affect patients — who struggle not with emotional vocabulary but with the **inability to express emotions nonverbally** — demonstrate reduced level and variability of voice intensity in emotional statements. Synthetic voices that lack prosodic range impose a similar "digital flat affect" on AAC users, limiting their ability to express genuine emotion.

## 8. Intersection with Communication Struggles Taxonomy

### 8.1 Domain 6 (Psychosocial): Voice, Confidence, Identity, and Stigma

Voice choice directly impacts:
- **Self-concept and identity** — a mismatched voice can undermine self-perception
- **Communication confidence** — poor voice quality reduces willingness to communicate
- **Stigma experiences** — synthetic-sounding voices trigger social stigma and infantilization
- **Social participation** — voice quality affects whether communication partners engage naturally
- **Emotional well-being** — 16-53% of laryngectomy patients experience depression/anxiety related to voice loss (range across studies)

### 8.2 Domain 2.5 (Emotional Expression): Voice Prosody

- Current TTS systems impose a **"digital flat affect"** that limits emotional expression
- Pitch, tempo, and voice quality are the primary acoustic channels for emotional communication
- Message banking partially addresses this by preserving authentic emotional recordings
- Research on expressive TTS synthesis is advancing but remains a major unsolved challenge

### 8.3 Domain 7 (Rate/Efficiency): Voice Synthesis Latency

- Typical speaking rates range from **150-250 words per minute** in conversation
- The **300ms latency target** is based on natural human conversation timing — average human reaction time is approximately 220ms
- Delays beyond this window are misinterpreted as hesitation, disrupting conversational flow
- AAC communication rate is already significantly slower than natural speech; synthesis latency compounds this
- Digitized speech is associated with greater intelligibility relative to synthesized speech, but both impact rate

## 9. Technology Landscape: Voice Banking and Personalization Services

### 9.1 ModelTalker

Developed by the Nemours Speech Research Laboratory. Creates personal synthetic voices for SGD users. Current technology requires recording 600-3,500 sentences. Now testing hybrid Deep Neural Network (hDNN) voices using machine learning. Compatible with Grid 3, Communicator 5, MindExpress, and Tobii Dynavox TD Talk for iPad. Free for people with ALS.

### 9.2 Acapela My-Own-Voice

Allows preservation of one's own voice as synthetic speech from as few as 30-50 phrases. Recording is free; download fee applies. Web-based recording process. Includes child voices (Josh and Ella) and African American English voices.

### 9.3 Apple Personal Voice

Introduced in iOS 17 (September 2023). Requires only 15 minutes of audio recording. Uses on-device machine learning for privacy. Integrates with Live Speech for FaceTime, phone calls, and assistive apps. Designed specifically for users at risk of losing speech ability (e.g., ALS).

### 9.4 VocaliD

Founded in 2014 by Rupal Patel. Blends recipient's residual vocalizations with matched donor recordings. Over 10,000 voice donors signed up. Creates voice that sounds like the recipient in age, personality, and vocal identity but is as clear as the donor. Family members report increased device usage and improved self-esteem.

### 9.5 ElevenLabs

AI-powered voice cloning. Offers free Professional Voice Clones for ALS patients through Impact Program. Requires brief audio samples for cloning.

### 9.6 SpeakUnique

Creates custom synthesized voices from high-quality message banking recordings. Offers personalized voice design based on existing recordings.

## 10. Key Themes and Synthesis

### 10.1 Voice Is Not Just Functional — It Is Existential

Across all populations studied, voice serves as far more than a communication tool. It is a marker of age, gender, ethnicity, personality, health, social status, and emotional state. When someone loses their voice or must use a voice that does not represent them, the impact extends to their fundamental sense of self.

### 10.2 The Ownership Spectrum

Voice identity in AAC exists on a spectrum:
1. **No ownership** — generic synthetic voice that does not represent the user
2. **Tolerated voice** — acceptable but not identified with
3. **Chosen voice** — deliberately selected and identified with (e.g., Hawking)
4. **Preserved voice** — banked from one's own speech
5. **Blended voice** — combines one's own vocal characteristics with donor speech
6. **Full voice clone** — AI-generated replica of one's natural voice

### 10.3 Intersectionality Amplifies Voice Identity Needs

The most underserved populations face compounded challenges:
- **Autistic trans/nonbinary individuals** face limited voice options that match neither their neurotype nor gender identity
- **Multilingual/minority language speakers** lack TTS options in their languages
- **Black AAC users** face both limited culturally relevant voice options and bias in speech recognition
- **Adults who grew up with AAC** may be stuck with childish voices or symbols that infantilize them

### 10.4 Timing Is Critical for Degenerative Conditions

For ALS/MND and similar conditions:
- Voice banking should begin at diagnosis, not at speech decline
- The window for quality voice banking narrows rapidly — speaking rate below 120 wpm signals accelerated decline
- New technology (Apple Personal Voice, I Will Always Be Me) has dramatically reduced the effort required
- Both voice banking (for generative speech) and message banking (for emotional authenticity) should be pursued

### 10.5 The Research Gap

Despite the clear importance of voice identity in AAC:
- Psychosocial factors receive limited research attention in AAC studies
- Very few studies examine voice choice preferences systematically
- Transgender and nonbinary AAC users are almost entirely absent from the literature
- Cultural and linguistic diversity in TTS remains a significant gap
- The relationship between voice identity and long-term AAC outcomes is understudied
