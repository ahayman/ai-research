# Category 12: Narrative and Discourse Coherence

## The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

---

## How to Read This Document

Every conversation includes some form of narrative -- telling someone what happened, explaining how something works, describing what you saw, or making a case for your point of view. Narrative is where all the pragmatic skills from the previous categories come together: speech acts, implicature, politeness, turn-taking, hedging, register, and deixis all operate simultaneously when you tell a story or follow someone else's.

This category teaches the **architecture** of extended discourse -- how stories are built, what makes them work, what makes them fail, and how the autistic experience of narrative differs from (and sometimes exceeds) neurotypical norms.

---

## 1. Why Narrative Is Pragmatic

### 1.1 The Core Insight

Stringing sentences together in order is grammar. Making those sentences into a **story that serves a purpose** is pragmatics. Narrative is pragmatic because it requires:

- **Audience design**: Adjusting your story to what the listener knows and cares about
- **Perspective management**: Representing multiple viewpoints (your own, characters', the listener's)
- **Information packaging**: Deciding what to say first, what to reveal later, what to leave out
- **Relevance calibration**: Every element must justify its presence -- if it doesn't contribute to the point, it shouldn't be there
- **Tellability judgment**: Before starting, you must judge whether the story is worth telling to THIS person at THIS time

Jerome Bruner (1986) argued that narrative is a fundamental mode of human thought -- not just a communication skill but a way of **organizing experience**. We don't just tell stories; we think in stories.

### 1.2 Every Story Must Answer Two Questions

This is the single most practical insight about narrative:

**"And then what happened?"** → This is the **events** -- the sequence of what occurred.

**"So what?"** → This is the **point** -- why the events matter, why they're worth telling.

A story that answers only the first question is a list of events. A story that answers only the second is an opinion. A good story answers both -- and the "so what?" is what makes it satisfying.

## 2. The Structure of a Story: Labov's Six-Part Model

William Labov (1967) analyzed how real people tell stories in real conversations and identified six structural components:

| Component | Function | Example | What Happens If Missing |
|-----------|----------|---------|------------------------|
| **Abstract** | Announces the story; tells the listener what kind of story to expect | "Something crazy happened at work today." | Listener doesn't know why you're talking |
| **Orientation** | Sets the scene: who, what, where, when | "So I was in the break room, around 2 PM, and my manager walks in..." | Listener is lost -- no context |
| **Complicating Action** | The events -- what happened, in sequence | "...and she starts opening every cabinet, slamming them shut..." | No story -- just a setup with no payoff |
| **Evaluation** | WHY this story matters -- the point, the emotional weight | "I had *never* seen her that angry. It was actually terrifying." | The "so what?" question goes unanswered |
| **Resolution** | How it ended | "Turns out someone had eaten her lunch." | Story feels incomplete |
| **Coda** | Bridges back to the present; signals the story is over | "So yeah, that's why I don't use the break room fridge anymore." | Listener doesn't know the story is done |

### 2.1 Evaluation Is the Most Important Element

Evaluation is what separates a **story** from a **report**. It's the narrator's attitude toward the events -- what makes them interesting, funny, shocking, or meaningful. Without evaluation, the listener thinks: "Why are you telling me this?"

**Evaluation devices** include:
- **Intensifiers**: "I had *never* seen..." / "It was *absolutely*..."
- **Comparators**: "It was like..." / "I thought it was going to be X, but..."
- **Emotional language**: "I was terrified" / "It was hilarious"
- **Direct speech**: Quoting what people said (makes the story vivid)
- **Repetition for emphasis**: "She just kept slamming. Slamming and slamming."
- **Rhetorical questions**: "Can you believe that?"

### 2.2 The Story Preface: Getting Permission to Tell

In normal conversation, turns are short. Stories need extended turns. Before telling a story, you need a **preface** -- a short announcement that secures the floor:

| Preface Type | Example | What It Signals |
|-------------|---------|-----------------|
| Dramatic | "Oh my god, you'll never guess what happened." | Something exciting |
| Topic link | "That reminds me of this time when..." | Story connects to current topic |
| News | "Did you hear about...?" | New information |
| Warning | "OK so this is kind of a long story, but..." | Requesting patience |
| Assessment | "The weirdest thing happened to me." | Pre-evaluating the story |

The listener responds with a **go-ahead** ("What?" / "No, tell me!" / "Oh really?"), which gives you permission to hold the floor.

**Without a preface**: Listeners don't know you're telling a story, may try to take their turn after your first sentence, and don't know what kind of response to give.

## 3. Coherence vs. Cohesion

These sound similar but describe different things:

### 3.1 Cohesion: Surface-Level Links

**Cohesion** is the linguistic glue that connects sentences to each other:

| Type | What It Does | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Reference** | Pronouns point back to earlier nouns | "John arrived. **He** was tired." |
| **Conjunction** | Linking words connect ideas | "She left **because** it was late." |
| **Lexical cohesion** | Repeating or related words create continuity | "The **dog** barked. The **animal** was hungry." |
| **Ellipsis** | Leaving out words that are understood | "Can you help?" "I **can**." (= I can help) |

### 3.2 Coherence: Deep-Level Unity

**Coherence** is whether the discourse makes sense as a whole -- whether the ideas connect logically:

| Dimension | What It Means | Example |
|-----------|--------------|---------|
| **Causal** | Events connected by cause and effect | "It rained, so the game was cancelled." |
| **Temporal** | Events in time sequence | "First we ate, then we left." |
| **Thematic** | Everything relates to a central topic | Every sentence contributes to the same story |
| **Relevance** | Each element earns its place | No random tangents that go nowhere |

**The critical distinction**: You can have **cohesion without coherence** (sentences that are grammatically linked but make no sense together) and **coherence without cohesion** (ideas that make sense together but lack explicit linking words).

**Example of cohesion without coherence:**
> "John went to the store. The store was near a park. Parks have trees. Trees produce oxygen."

Each sentence connects to the previous one, but the passage as a whole has no point.

**Example of coherence without cohesion:**
> "She walked in. Silence. Every head turned. She sat down. Meeting resumed."

No connecting words, but the scene is perfectly coherent -- you can follow what happened.

## 4. Coherence Markers: The Signpost System

Specific words and phrases signal the logical structure of your discourse:

| Marker Type | Examples | What They Signal |
|------------|---------|-----------------|
| **Temporal** | "First," "then," "next," "after that," "finally," "meanwhile" | Time sequence |
| **Causal** | "Because," "so," "therefore," "as a result," "that's why" | Cause and effect |
| **Contrastive** | "But," "however," "although," "on the other hand" | Contrast or exception |
| **Additive** | "And," "also," "moreover," "in addition" | Adding to what was said |
| **Evaluative** | "The best part was," "what really got me was," "the thing is" | Signaling the point |
| **Topic management** | "Anyway," "so," "speaking of which," "going back to" | Navigating between topics |

**The impact of markers on perception**: Research shows that adding coherence markers significantly improves how well a narrative is understood and how "organized" it sounds. A story without markers sounds choppy and disconnected, even if the same events are present.

## 5. Beyond Narrative: Other Discourse Types

Narrative isn't the only extended discourse type. Each type has its own coherence requirements:

| Type | Purpose | Key Structural Requirement |
|------|---------|--------------------------|
| **Narrative** | Tell what happened | Temporal + causal ordering; evaluation |
| **Description** | What something is like | Spatial organization; salient features first |
| **Explanation** | How/why something works | Known-to-unknown; cause-to-effect |
| **Argumentation** | Why a claim is true | Claim → evidence → warrant → conclusion |
| **Instruction** | How to do something | Step-by-step ordering; completeness |

**Argumentation structure (Toulmin model)**:

```
CLAIM: "We should change our approach."
  ↑
GROUNDS: "The current approach has failed three times."
  ↑
WARRANT: "Repeated failure indicates a fundamental problem."
  ↑
BACKING: "Research shows that after three failures, the root cause is structural."
  ↑
QUALIFIER: "Almost certainly" / "In most cases"
  ↑
REBUTTAL: "Unless the failures had external causes."
```

## 6. Common Narrative Failures

These are the specific ways stories go wrong and create social friction:

| Failure | What Happens | Social Impact | Fix |
|---------|-------------|--------------|-----|
| **Missing evaluation** | Events without a point | "Why are you telling me this?" | Add evaluation: "The crazy part was..." |
| **Too much orientation** | Excessive scene-setting | Listener loses interest before the story starts | Trim to essentials; get to the action faster |
| **Too little orientation** | Jumping into events without context | Listener is confused about who/what/where | Add a brief orientation: "So I was at work, and..." |
| **Chronological confusion** | Events out of order | Listener can't follow the timeline | Use temporal markers: "first," "then," "after that" |
| **Losing the thread** | Digressing without returning | Listener forgets the main story | Use "anyway" to return: "Anyway, so then..." |
| **Anticlimactic ending** | Build-up to nothing remarkable | Listener feels let down | Either add evaluation to make the ending significant, or reconsider whether the story is tellable |
| **Wrong audience** | Story inappropriate for the listener | Discomfort, offense, or boredom | Assess: does this person want/need to hear this? |
| **"Had to be there"** | Story depends on context the listener doesn't share | Listener can't appreciate it | Either provide the missing context or choose a different story |

## 7. The Autistic Experience with Narrative

### 7.1 What the Research Shows

**The macrostructure/microstructure distinction is key**: Autistic individuals' greatest narrative challenges are at the **macrostructure** level (global organization, evaluation, causal connections), while **microstructure** (syntax, sentence complexity, productivity) is often comparable to NT peers when matched for language ability (Baixauli et al., 2016 meta-analysis).

**Specific findings:**

| Element | Finding |
|---------|---------|
| **Global coherence** | Reduced -- individual sentences are well-formed but don't always cohere as a whole |
| **Evaluation** | Consistently the hardest element -- fewer evaluative devices, less signaling of "why this matters" |
| **Causal connections** | Fewer causal links between events; temporal sequencing may be preserved while causal linking is weaker |
| **Internal state language** | Fewer references to characters' emotions and thoughts |
| **Character introduction** | Higher rates of ambiguous character establishment (assuming the listener already knows who's being discussed) |
| **Detail orientation** | More local details, fewer global "big picture" elements |
| **Context dependence** | Performance improves dramatically with scaffolding (visual supports, story frameworks) |

### 7.2 What May Be Strengths

| Strength | Where It Excels |
|----------|----------------|
| **Detail and precision** | Technical, scientific, and professional narratives |
| **Factual accuracy** | Retelling events accurately |
| **Logical/sequential ordering** | Step-by-step instructions, procedures, timelines |
| **Specialized knowledge narratives** | Deep-topic storytelling within areas of expertise |
| **Written narrative** | Some autistic individuals excel in written form, where real-time processing pressure is removed |

### 7.3 The "Infodump" as Narrative

The extended, detailed, single-topic monologue sometimes called "infodumping" is a form of narrative that doesn't follow standard story structure but serves real communicative purposes:
- Sharing knowledge that genuinely excites the speaker
- Connecting with others through shared interests
- Processing and organizing complex information aloud

In autistic-autistic communication, infodumping is often valued and reciprocated. In mixed-neurotype communication, it can create friction when the listener expects standard narrative structure (orientation → action → evaluation → resolution) and instead receives an encyclopedic knowledge transfer.

**The reframe**: Infodumping is not "failed narrative." It's a different discourse type -- more like **explanation** or **description** than **narrative**. Understanding the difference helps calibrate when each type is appropriate.

### 7.4 The Double Empathy Perspective

Research shows that NT listeners evaluate autistic narratives as "less coherent" -- but this evaluation may be driven by **presentation style** (prosody, facial expression, eye contact) more than **content quality**. Thin-slice judgment studies find that NT listeners form negative impressions of autistic speakers within seconds, before content has been evaluated.

Autistic narratives may be differently organized rather than deficiently organized:
- More detail-oriented (strength in technical contexts)
- Less evaluation-heavy (which reads as "no point" to NT listeners expecting evaluation)
- More factual/accurate (strength in reporting contexts)
- Less audience-adapted (the same story regardless of listener -- consistency vs. flexibility)

## 8. Practical Toolkit

### 8.1 The Story Structure Template

When you need to tell a story:

```
1. PREFACE:    "Something [funny/weird/important] happened..."
                → Wait for go-ahead ("What?")
2. ORIENTATION: Who + Where + When (1-2 sentences max)
                "So I was at [place], and [person] was there..."
3. ACTION:      What happened (in chronological order)
                "And then... and then... and then..."
4. EVALUATION:  Why it matters (THE MOST IMPORTANT PART)
                "The [crazy/funny/scary] part was..."
5. RESOLUTION:  How it ended
                "So eventually..."
6. CODA:        Bridge back
                "And that's why I [current situation]."
```

### 8.2 The Evaluation Toolkit

When you're not sure how to signal "why this matters":

| Device | How to Use It |
|--------|-------------|
| **Direct statement of significance** | "The important thing was..." / "What made this so [adjective] was..." |
| **Emotional language** | "I was shocked" / "It was hilarious" / "I couldn't believe it" |
| **Intensifiers** | "I had *never*..." / "It was *the most*..." |
| **Quoted speech** | "And she said, '[exact words]'" (makes it vivid) |
| **Comparison** | "I thought it was going to be X, but it was actually Y" |
| **Rhetorical question** | "Can you believe that?" / "How is that even possible?" |

### 8.3 The Audience Design Checklist

Before telling a story, ask:
1. **Does this person know the background?** If not → add more orientation
2. **Will they care about this topic?** If not → reconsider telling it, or connect it to something they care about
3. **Am I the right person to tell this story?** (Is it my story to tell?)
4. **Is this the right time and place?** (Register check -- Category 7)
5. **What response am I hoping for?** (Laughter? Sympathy? Agreement?) → This helps calibrate your evaluation

### 8.4 When Detail Is a Strength (Not a Problem)

| Context | Detailed narrative is VALUED |
|---------|------------------------------|
| Technical/professional briefings | Precision matters |
| Written reports | Thoroughness expected |
| Academic presentations | Evidence requires detail |
| Expert explanations | Deep knowledge is the point |
| Instruction/tutorial | Completeness prevents errors |
| Legal/medical contexts | Accuracy is essential |

In these contexts, the autistic preference for detailed, precise, comprehensive narrative is not a liability -- it's exactly what's needed.

## 9. Key Takeaways

1. **Every story answers two questions**: "What happened?" and "So what?" Both are essential. If you only provide events without evaluation, listeners wonder why you're telling them.

2. **Evaluation is the hardest but most important element.** It's the "so what?" signal. Autistic narrators consistently under-produce evaluation -- making it the highest-priority skill to develop.

3. **Use a story preface.** Before launching into a story, announce it: "Something crazy happened." Wait for the go-ahead. This secures the floor and sets expectations.

4. **Coherence markers are signposts.** "First," "then," "because," "but," "the thing is" -- these words tell the listener how your ideas connect. Adding them makes your discourse sound more organized with minimal effort.

5. **Macrostructure is the challenge; microstructure is often fine.** The difficulty is in global organization (the big picture), not in sentence-level grammar. Story structure templates help bridge this gap.

6. **Infodumping is valid -- it's a different discourse type.** It's explanation or description, not failed narrative. Knowing the difference helps you choose the right type for the right context.

7. **Your detail and precision are strengths in the right contexts.** Technical, professional, academic, and instructional contexts value exactly what autistic narrative tends to provide.

8. **The "so what?" test is your best friend.** After building your story, ask yourself: "Would someone who heard this understand why I told them?" If not, add evaluation.
