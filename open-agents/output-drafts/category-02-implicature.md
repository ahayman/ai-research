# Category 2: The Cooperative Principle and Conversational Implicature

## The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

---

## How to Read This Document

Category 1 (Speech Acts) covered what sentences **do**. This category covers what sentences **mean beyond what they say** -- the vast layer of implied meaning that English speakers generate and interpret constantly, usually without conscious awareness.

This is arguably the most important category in the entire learning hub. The gap between what people *say* and what they *mean* is the single largest source of pragmatic misunderstanding. Understanding the **system** that governs this gap -- not memorizing examples, but grasping the underlying mechanics -- is the most powerful tool available for navigating English communication.

---

## 1. The Core Problem: What Is Said vs. What Is Meant

### 1.1 The Gap

Every day, in virtually every conversation, people communicate meaning that is **not contained in the literal words they use**:

| What Is Said | What Is Meant | The Gap |
|-------------|--------------|---------|
| "How was the movie?" → "The popcorn was good." | The movie was bad. | The speaker never said the movie was bad. |
| "Some students passed the exam." | Not all of them passed. | The speaker never said "not all." |
| "Can you come Saturday?" → "I have an early flight Sunday." | No, I can't come. | The speaker never said no. |
| "She's not exactly the brightest." | She's quite dumb. | The speaker said the opposite of what they meant. |
| "That's an... interesting approach." | I disapprove of the approach. | The speaker literally praised it. |

This gap between what is said and what is meant is called **implicature**. The philosopher H.P. Grice gave this phenomenon its name and its theory in 1967, and his framework remains the foundation of how linguists understand implied meaning.

### 1.2 Why the Gap Exists

People don't communicate indirectly because they're trying to be confusing. The gap exists because it serves multiple social functions simultaneously:

1. **Efficiency**: "The popcorn was good" communicates "the movie was bad" with fewer words and less confrontation than "I thought the movie was terrible." The listener does the inferential work.
2. **Politeness**: Indirectness protects face. Saying "She's not exactly the brightest" is kinder than "She's dumb."
3. **Plausible deniability**: If challenged, the speaker can retreat to the literal meaning. "I just said the popcorn was good!"
4. **Social bonding**: Successfully navigating implicature signals group membership. You and the listener share a code.
5. **Richness**: Indirect communication conveys not just information but attitude, evaluation, and social positioning -- all at once.

### 1.3 Grice's Achievement

Before Grice, linguists and philosophers knew this gap existed but had no systematic way to explain it. Grice's insight was that the gap is bridged by **rational inference**. Listeners don't just decode words -- they **reason** about what a cooperating speaker would mean by saying those particular words in that particular context.

This means the system is not arbitrary. It follows rules. It generates predictions. It can be learned.

## 2. The Cooperative Principle: The Foundation

### 2.1 What It Is

In 1967, Grice delivered the William James Lectures at Harvard, where he proposed that conversation rests on an unspoken agreement:

> **"Make your conversational contribution such as is required, at the stage at which it occurs, by the accepted purpose or direction of the talk exchange in which you are engaged."**

He called this the **Cooperative Principle**.

### 2.2 What "Cooperative" Actually Means (This Is Critical)

**"Cooperative" does NOT mean nice, polite, friendly, or helpful.**

This is the single most common misunderstanding of Grice's framework. He was not describing an ideal of pleasant conversation. He was describing a **rational expectation** that makes communication possible: both parties are contributing to a recognizable exchange with some shared direction.

Two people in a bitter argument are "cooperative" in Grice's sense. A hostile cross-examination in court is "cooperative." A sarcastic put-down is "cooperative" -- because the speaker expects the listener to recognize the sarcasm and derive the intended meaning.

Grice illustrated with an analogy: "If you are assisting me to mend a car, I expect your contribution to be neither more nor less than is required. If I need four screws, I expect you to hand me four, rather than two or six."

The maxims are not:
- Rules of etiquette
- Moral prescriptions
- Descriptions of how people actually speak

They ARE:
- Rational expectations that make inferring meaning possible
- A framework for explaining how listeners bridge the said/meant gap

### 2.3 The Mechanism: How Implicature Is Generated

Here's the engine that drives the entire system:

1. Speaker appears to break a conversational expectation (a maxim)
2. Listener assumes the speaker is still cooperating (still participating rationally)
3. Listener searches for an interpretation that **preserves the assumption of cooperation**
4. That interpretation IS the implicature

This is why the system works: listeners assume cooperation and use apparent violations as **signals** that extra meaning is present. The violations aren't failures -- they're **triggers for inference**.

## 3. The Four Maxims: The Rules of the Game

Grice identified four categories of conversational expectations. He borrowed the category names from Kant: Quantity, Quality, Relation, and Manner.

### 3.1 QUANTITY: Say Enough, But Not Too Much

**Sub-maxim 1**: Make your contribution as informative as is required (for the current purposes of the exchange).
**Sub-maxim 2**: Do not make your contribution more informative than is required.

**What this means in practice**: Give the right amount of information -- enough to serve the conversation's purpose, but not so much that you create noise or raise irrelevant issues. What counts as "enough" depends entirely on context.

**Following the maxim:**
- Q: "How do I get to the library?"
- A: "Go straight two blocks, then turn right. It's on the left."
  (Exactly the needed information.)

**Too little (violating sub-maxim 1):**
- Q: "How do I get to the library?"
- A: "It's not far."
  (Uninformative -- you haven't actually helped.)

**Too much (violating sub-maxim 2):**
- Q: "How do I get to the library?"
- A: "Go straight two blocks -- past the Italian restaurant that used to be a laundromat, my grandmother loved that laundromat -- then turn right at the intersection where that car accident happened last week, did you hear about that? Anyway, the library is on the left, it was built in 1956 originally..."
  (Information overload. The core answer is buried.)

**Why this matters for autistic learners**: Research consistently identifies **Relevance** (closely related to Quantity) as the maxim autistic speakers most frequently appear to violate. Providing too much detail about a special interest, not calibrating information to the listener's needs, or giving technically complete but contextually inappropriate responses can all be perceived as Quantity violations. Understanding that "as informative as required" is context-dependent -- not an absolute standard -- is key.

### 3.2 QUALITY: Be Truthful

**Supermaxim**: Try to make your contribution one that is true.
**Sub-maxim 1**: Do not say what you believe to be false.
**Sub-maxim 2**: Do not say that for which you lack adequate evidence.

**What this means in practice**: Quality is the foundation of the entire system. If listeners couldn't generally assume speakers are being truthful, communication would collapse. Every other maxim depends on Quality.

**Following the maxim:**
- "It's 3:15." (You checked your watch and it says 3:15.)
- "I think there's a gas station on Elm Street, but I'm not 100% sure." (Hedging signals you're following sub-maxim 2 -- you don't have sufficient evidence for a stronger claim.)

**Violating (secretly breaking -- this is lying):**
- "I was stuck in traffic." (Said by someone who overslept. They secretly break the Quality maxim -- the listener is deceived.)

**Flouting (obviously breaking -- generating implicature):**
- "What lovely weather!" (Said during a downpour. Both parties know this is false. The obvious falsehood generates an implicature: the speaker means the opposite. This is irony.)

**The Quality-trust connection**: Quality is special because violating it **covertly** (lying) undermines the entire cooperative system. Flouting it **overtly** (irony, sarcasm, hyperbole) works precisely because the listener trusts the speaker enough to know they can't literally mean what they said.

### 3.3 RELATION: Be Relevant

**The maxim**: Be relevant.

This is the shortest and most powerful of the four maxims. Grice himself acknowledged its apparent simplicity concealed enormous complexity -- he noted that questions about what "relevant" means and how relevance shifts during conversation were "too important to be discussed here."

**What this means in practice**: Each contribution should connect to the current topic and purpose of the conversation. When a response seems irrelevant, the listener doesn't conclude that the speaker has lost track -- the listener searches for the **hidden relevance**.

**Following the maxim:**
- Q: "What time does the meeting start?"
- A: "Three o'clock."

**Apparently violating (but actually generating an implicature):**
- Q: "Can you come to the party Saturday?"
- A: "I have an early flight on Sunday."
  
  The response doesn't literally answer the question. But the listener assumes the speaker is being relevant and infers: an early Sunday flight means they can't stay out late Saturday, so the answer is "no" (or "probably not"). **The inferential bridge from the stated fact to the implied answer IS the implicature.**

**The classic example (Grice's own):**
- A: "I'm out of gas."
- B: "There's a gas station around the corner."
  
  B's response is only relevant if the gas station is open and has gas. B didn't say either of those things. But the listener infers them because that's the only way B's response is relevant to A's problem.

**Why Relation is the most common source of implicature**: People routinely "answer" questions by stating facts that are *related to* the answer rather than *being* the answer. The listener must construct the bridge. This is where a huge amount of everyday implied meaning lives.

### 3.4 MANNER: Be Clear

**Supermaxim**: Be perspicuous.
**Sub-maxim 1**: Avoid obscurity of expression.
**Sub-maxim 2**: Avoid ambiguity.
**Sub-maxim 3**: Be brief (avoid unnecessary prolixity).
**Sub-maxim 4**: Be orderly.

**How Manner differs from the others**: Quantity, Quality, and Relation concern **what** you say. Manner concerns **how** you say it. Two speakers can communicate the same content but trigger different implicatures through their choice of expression.

**Following the maxim:**
- "She sang 'Home Sweet Home.'" (Clear, simple, unambiguous.)

**Flouting the maxim (Grice's own example):**
- "She produced a series of sounds that corresponded closely with the score of 'Home Sweet Home.'"
  
  This says the same thing as "She sang 'Home Sweet Home'" but in an elaborately circumlocutory way. The listener reasons: why would a cooperating speaker avoid the simple description? Because the simple description ("she sang") would implicate that the performance was adequate. The elaborate phrasing implicates that **it was so bad that calling it "singing" would be too generous.**

**Common Manner implicatures:**

| Unusual Phrasing | What It Implicates |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| "She... passed away" (pause, euphemism) | Avoiding the direct word "died" signals emotional weight or sensitivity |
| "He was *let go*" instead of "fired" | Softening suggests the speaker wants to minimize the event's severity |
| Overly formal language in an informal setting | Distance, anger, or sarcasm |
| "The company *restructured*" | Layoffs. The corporate euphemism implicates something worse than the literal meaning. |
| Describing events out of order | The non-standard order implicates a causal connection: "He tripped and fell" ≠ "He fell and tripped" |

## 4. The Five Ways Speakers Relate to Maxims

This is where the system becomes powerful. Speakers don't just follow or break maxims -- they relate to them in five distinct ways, each with different consequences:

### 4.1 Observing (Following)

The speaker simply follows the maxim. No implicature is generated. This is the "default" mode of communication.

"The meeting is at 3 PM." (Truthful, informative, relevant, clear.)

### 4.2 Violating (Secretly Breaking)

The speaker **quietly** breaks a maxim without the listener knowing. This is **deception**. No implicature is generated because the listener doesn't know a maxim has been broken.

"I was at the library all evening." (Said by someone who was actually elsewhere. The listener, unaware of the violation, takes it at face value.)

### 4.3 Flouting (Obviously Breaking)

The speaker **blatantly** breaks a maxim in a way that is **obvious to both parties**. Because the listener assumes the speaker is still cooperating, the listener searches for a reason behind the obvious violation. That reason is the **implicature**.

**This is the primary engine of conversational implicature.**

Flouting by maxim:

**Flouting Quality** (saying something obviously false):
- Irony: "What a beautiful day!" (during a storm)
- Sarcasm: "Oh great, another meeting. Just what I needed."
- Metaphor: "He's a real snake."
- Hyperbole: "I've told you a million times."
- Understatement: "He's not exactly the brightest."
- Rhetorical questions: "Do I look like I care?"

**Flouting Quantity** (saying obviously too little or too much):
- "How was the movie?" → "The popcorn was good." (Too little -- omits evaluation of the movie)
- A suspiciously brief recommendation letter (too little for the context)
- An elaborate, overwrought excuse (too much -- the excessive detail implicates that the speaker is compensating for a weak reason)

**Flouting Relation** (saying something apparently irrelevant):
- "Is John a good cook?" → "He makes a great cup of coffee." (The irrelevance IS the message: he's not a good cook)
- "Nice weather, isn't it?" said while walking past a sensitive topic (the irrelevance signals a desire to change subjects)

**Flouting Manner** (saying something in an unusual way):
- "She produced a series of sounds..." (avoiding the word "singing" implicates it wasn't singing)
- Using someone's full name ("William") when they usually go by "Will" (formality implicates distance or anger)

### 4.4 Opting Out (Explicitly Refusing)

The speaker explicitly signals that they can't or won't follow a maxim. No implicature is generated -- the listener understands the speaker is not being uncooperative, just constrained.

- "I can't say anything -- client confidentiality." (Opting out of Quantity)
- "I'm not sure, but..." (Opting out of full Quality commitment)
- "No comment." (Opting out of informativeness)

### 4.5 Clashing (Following One Maxim Forces Breaking Another)

Sometimes following one maxim makes it impossible to follow another. The speaker must choose which to prioritize.

- Q: "Where does John live?"
- A: "Somewhere in the south of France."
  
  The speaker gives less information than the question requires (potential Quantity violation), but they don't know the exact location and don't want to guess (following Quality). Quality wins, and the listener recognizes the Quantity shortfall is due to a Quality constraint, not uncooperativeness.

## 5. Types of Implicature

### 5.1 The Taxonomy

Grice divided implicature into a hierarchy:

```
WHAT THE SPEAKER MEANS
├── What is SAID (literal content)
└── What is IMPLICATED
    ├── CONVENTIONAL implicature (attached to specific words)
    └── CONVERSATIONAL implicature (derived from context + cooperation)
        ├── Generalized (arises without special context)
        └── Particularized (requires specific context)
```

### 5.2 Conventional Implicature: Meaning Built Into Words

Some words carry implications as part of their **dictionary meaning**, independent of any conversational context. These are conventional implicatures -- you don't need to calculate them from context; you just need to know what the word means.

**"But" -- implicates contrast/unexpectedness:**
- "She's poor **but** happy."
  - What is said: She's poor AND happy. (Logically identical to "She's poor and happy.")
  - What is implicated: There's a contrast -- happiness is unexpected given poverty.
  - You CANNOT cancel this: "She's poor but happy, and there's nothing surprising about that" sounds contradictory.

**"Even" -- implicates extreme case on a scale:**
- "**Even** John passed."
  - What is said: John passed.
  - What is implicated: John was the *least likely* person to pass. Others passed too.

**"Yet/still" -- implicates expectation of change:**
- "She **still** hasn't replied."
  - What is implicated: A reply was expected by now.

**"Therefore/so" -- implicates causal connection:**
- "It rained; **therefore**, the match was cancelled."
  - What is implicated: The rain caused the cancellation.

**Why conventional implicature matters**: These words are **reliable** -- they always carry their implication regardless of context. For autistic learners building a system of pragmatic knowledge, conventional implicatures are solid ground: learn what "but" does, and it does it every time.

### 5.3 Generalized Conversational Implicature: Defaults That Don't Need Context

Some implicatures arise so regularly that they function almost like defaults -- they don't require any special context to emerge. The most important of these are **scalar implicatures**.

**How scalar implicatures work:**

Certain words form natural **scales** from weak to strong:

| Scale (weak → strong) |
|----------------------|
| some → most → all |
| sometimes → often → always |
| possible → probable → certain |
| warm → hot → scalding |
| like → love → adore |
| or → and |
| good → excellent → perfect |

**The rule**: Using a weaker term on a scale **implicates the negation of all stronger terms**.

- "**Some** students passed." → Implicates: **not all** of them.
  (If all had passed, the speaker would have said "all" -- using the stronger term. Choosing the weaker term implicates the stronger one doesn't apply.)

- "It's **possible** she'll come." → Implicates: it's **not certain**.

- "I **like** her." → Implicates: I don't **love** her.

- "The exam was **good**." → Implicates: it wasn't **excellent** or **perfect**.

**The cancellation test**: Real implicatures can be explicitly cancelled without contradiction:
- "Some students passed -- in fact, **all** of them did." (Perfectly coherent. The implicature is cancelled.)
- "I like her -- actually, I **love** her." (Also coherent.)

This test distinguishes implicature from literal meaning. You can't cancel literal meaning: "No students passed -- in fact, some did" is contradictory.

**Why this matters for autistic learners**: Research consistently shows that autistic individuals handle scalar implicatures **relatively well** (Pijnacker et al., 2009; Andrés-Roqueta et al., 2025). These are linguistic-pragmatic inferences -- they depend on language knowledge (knowing the scales) rather than social inference (reading mental states). This is a strength to build on.

### 5.4 Particularized Conversational Implicature: Context-Dependent Inference

These are one-off implicatures that arise only in specific contexts. They can't be predicted from the words alone -- you need the situation.

**Example 1: The movie review**
- Q: "How was the movie?"
- A: "The popcorn was good."
- Implicature: The movie was not good.
- Mechanism: Relevance -- the listener asked about the movie; the speaker talked about popcorn. The only way this is relevant is if the speaker is *avoiding* evaluating the movie, which implicates the evaluation would be negative.

**Example 2: The indirect decline**
- Q: "Can you come to the party Saturday?"
- A: "I have an early flight on Sunday."
- Implicature: No, I can't come (or: I probably can't stay late).
- Mechanism: Relevance -- the flight information is only relevant if it explains why the speaker can't attend.

**Example 3: The recommendation letter (Grice's classic)**
- A philosophy professor writes about a job candidate: "Mr. X's command of English is excellent, and his attendance at tutorials has been regular."
- Implicature: Mr. X is not a good philosopher.
- Mechanism: Quantity -- in a recommendation for a philosophy position, the professor says nothing about philosophical ability. The omission of the relevant information IS the message.

**Example 4: The faint praise**
- Q: "Do you like my painting?"
- A: "It's very colorful!"
- Implicature: The speaker doesn't think the painting is good, but found something technically positive to say.
- Mechanism: Quantity -- praising an incidental quality (color) while conspicuously not evaluating the thing that was actually asked about (quality/appeal).

### 5.5 The Step-by-Step Reasoning Process

When someone generates a particularized implicature, the listener's reasoning follows this chain:

1. The speaker said X.
2. X appears to violate a maxim (it's irrelevant, or uninformative, or obviously false, or oddly phrased).
3. But I assume the speaker is cooperating.
4. The speaker knows I'll notice the apparent violation.
5. The only way to reconcile the apparent violation with cooperation is to assume the speaker means Y (beyond X).
6. The speaker knows I can work out Y.
7. **Therefore, the speaker has implicated Y.**

**Worked example:**
- Context: Dinner party. Host asks: "Would you like some more casserole?"
- Guest: "I'm absolutely stuffed."
- Step-by-step:
  1. The guest said they're stuffed (an assertion about their physical state).
  2. This doesn't directly answer the yes/no question (apparent Relation violation).
  3. But I assume the guest is cooperating.
  4. Being stuffed is relevant to the question if it explains why the answer is "no."
  5. Therefore, the guest means: "No thank you, I've had enough."

## 6. The Implicature Detection Toolkit

This section provides practical tools for identifying implied meaning in real conversations.

### 6.1 The Enthusiasm Test (The Single Most Reliable Detector)

**If someone could show more enthusiasm and doesn't, the lack of enthusiasm IS the message.**

- Compare what was said to what **could** have been said.
- If the response is the minimum positive assessment, the real assessment is probably below positive.
- If the response avoids specifics when specifics would be easy, the specifics are probably negative.

| What Could Have Been Said | What Was Actually Said | What's Implicated |
|---------------------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| "I loved it!" / "It was amazing!" | "It was fine." | It was mediocre at best. |
| "She's incredibly talented." | "She tries hard." | She's not very talented. |
| "Absolutely! I'd love to!" | "Sure." (flat tone) | Reluctant compliance. |
| "He's a fantastic cook." | "He makes a good cup of coffee." | He's not a good cook. |

### 6.2 The Four-Step Decision Tree

For any utterance that feels "off":

**Step 1: Is the response surprisingly incomplete?**
- Does it answer a narrower question than was asked?
- Does it praise something peripheral while ignoring the central topic?
- Does it use a weaker word than expected?
→ **Quantity implicature.** The missing information is the message.

**Step 2: Is the statement obviously false?**
- Would both parties know it isn't literally true?
- Is the tone mismatched with the content?
- Is the claim impossibly extreme?
→ **Quality implicature.** Look for irony, sarcasm, hyperbole, or understatement.

**Step 3: Does the response seem off-topic?**
- Does it not directly answer the question?
- Can you find a logical bridge between the response and the question?
→ **Relevance implicature.** The bridge IS the implicated meaning.

**Step 4: Is the response oddly phrased?**
- Is the language more formal, vague, or elaborate than the situation calls for?
- Did the speaker conspicuously avoid a direct word?
→ **Manner implicature.** The unusual phrasing itself carries meaning.

### 6.3 Common English Phrases That Are Almost Entirely Implicature

These phrases are so conventionalized that their implicated meaning has almost entirely replaced their literal meaning. Learning these as a vocabulary set is highly practical.

**Phrases that signal disagreement without saying "I disagree":**

| Phrase | What It Literally Says | What It Actually Means |
|--------|----------------------|----------------------|
| "That's interesting." | That's interesting. | I disagree or disapprove. |
| "That's one way to look at it." | There's more than one perspective. | I think your perspective is wrong. |
| "With all due respect..." | I respect you. | I'm about to disrespect your position. |
| "I hear what you say." | I'm listening. | I disagree and don't want to discuss it further. |
| "Fair enough." | That's fair. | I disagree but I'm dropping it. |
| "If you say so." | I accept your claim. | I don't believe you. |
| "I see what you mean, but..." | I understand you. | I disagree. The "but" is the real message. |

**Phrases that signal "no" without saying "no":**

| Phrase | What It Actually Means |
|--------|----------------------|
| "We'll see." | Probably no. |
| "I'll think about it." | No, but I'm deferring the rejection. |
| "Let me get back to you." | I might not respond. Buying time. |
| "Maybe next time." | There probably won't be a next time. |
| "I wish I could, but..." | No. The clause after "but" is the real message. |

**Phrases that signal emotional states:**

| Phrase | What It Actually Means |
|--------|----------------------|
| "I'm fine." (flat tone) | I'm not fine, but I don't want to discuss it. |
| "It's fine." / "It doesn't matter." | It does matter. I'm upset but suppressing it. |
| "Do what you want." | I disagree with your choice but I'm withdrawing. |
| "I'm not mad." (unsolicited) | I am probably mad. Unsolicited denial often confirms. |

**Phrases that are disclaimers followed by violations:**

| Phrase | What Follows |
|--------|-------------|
| "No offense, but..." | Something offensive. |
| "I don't want to gossip, but..." | Gossip. |
| "I hate to ask, but..." | A request. |
| "I'm not racist, but..." | Something that sounds racist. |
| "Not to be rude, but..." | Something rude. |
| "I'm just saying..." | A strong opinion disguised as observation. |

**The pattern**: When someone disclaims the very thing they're about to do, they know what they're about to do is socially problematic. The disclaimer is a face-saving move, not a genuine prevention.

### 6.4 The British English Implicature Spectrum

British English uses particularly heavy implicature, especially understatement. This table (widely cited in cross-cultural pragmatics) shows the gap:

| What a British person says | What they mean | What others think they mean |
|---------------------------|---------------|---------------------------|
| "I hear what you say" | I disagree completely | They accepted my point |
| "With the greatest respect" | I think you're wrong | They respect my opinion |
| "That's not bad" | That's good | That's not good |
| "Quite good" | A bit disappointing | Very good |
| "That is a very brave proposal" | You're out of your mind | They think I'm courageous |
| "I would suggest..." | Do it or face consequences | Think about it, your call |
| "Oh, by the way..." | This is the main point | It wasn't important |
| "I was a bit disappointed that..." | I'm furious that... | It barely matters |
| "Very interesting" | That's nonsense | They're impressed |
| "I'll bear it in mind" | I've already forgotten it | They'll probably do it |
| "I'm sure it's my fault" | It's your fault | It was their fault |
| "I only have a few minor comments" | I'm going to rewrite everything | Just a few small edits |

## 7. An Alternative Framework: Relevance Theory

Grice's four-maxim framework is the standard, but there's an alternative that may be more useful for pattern-thinking minds: **Relevance Theory**, developed by Dan Sperber and Deirdre Wilson (1986/1995).

### 7.1 The Core Idea

Relevance Theory replaces Grice's four maxims with a single cognitive principle:

> **Human cognition tends to be geared to the maximization of relevance.**

And a single communicative principle:

> **Every act of communication conveys a presumption of its own optimal relevance.**

"Relevance" is defined as a ratio: **maximum cognitive effects for minimum processing effort**. An utterance is relevant to the degree that it gives you useful new information (effects) without requiring too much mental work (effort).

### 7.2 The Comprehension Procedure: An Algorithm

This is what makes Relevance Theory potentially powerful for autistic learners. Instead of four maxims and five ways of relating to them, it offers a **procedure** -- a step-by-step algorithm:

1. **Decode** the linguistic signal (get the literal meaning).
2. **Enrich** the meaning by resolving ambiguities, assigning references, narrowing/broadening word meanings.
3. **Derive implicatures** by accessing contextual assumptions and drawing conclusions.
4. **Check**: Does this interpretation yield enough cognitive effects to be worth the processing effort?
   - **If yes**: Stop. You've found the intended meaning.
   - **If no**: Try the next most accessible interpretation.

**Key feature**: You **stop at the first satisfactory interpretation**. You don't need to find the *best* interpretation -- just the first *good enough* one.

### 7.3 Worked Example

- You ask a colleague: "Are you coming to the meeting?"
- They reply: "I have a dentist appointment."

The procedure:
1. **Decode**: They have a dentist appointment.
2. **Enrich**: The appointment is today/at the meeting time. (Contextual enrichment.)
3. **Derive implicature**: People can't be in two places at once. The appointment conflicts with the meeting. → They're not coming.
4. **Check relevance**: This interpretation answers your question (high effects) and was reached with minimal effort. → Satisfied. **Stop.**

### 7.4 Why Relevance Theory May Suit Autistic Minds

| Feature | Grice's Framework | Relevance Theory |
|---------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Number of rules | 4 maxims + 1 principle | 1 principle |
| Approach | Detect which maxim is being violated | Follow a procedure |
| Foundation | Social cooperation | Cognitive processing |
| Figurative language | Requires identifying maxim "violations" | No violations -- just different routes to relevance |
| Output | Must identify violation type, then calculate | Single question: "What interpretation is most relevant?" |

The single-question approach ("What interpretation of this utterance would be most relevant given what I know?") is a more tractable algorithm than "Which of four social norms is being broken, and how?" Relevance Theory treats communication as a **cognitive optimization problem** rather than a social norm game.

### 7.5 How RT Handles Irony and Metaphor

Grice treats irony as a Quality violation (saying something obviously false) and metaphor as a different Quality violation (saying something categorically false).

Relevance Theory offers a more elegant account:

- **Metaphor** is **loose use** of language. "He's a bulldozer" isn't a lie -- it's an approximation. The word "bulldozer" is used loosely to communicate a cluster of properties (forceful, relentless, flattens opposition). The listener adjusts the concept to maximize relevance.

- **Irony** is **echoic use** of language. The speaker echoes a thought or expectation (their own or someone else's) and expresses **dissociation** from it. "What lovely weather!" echoes an expectation of nice weather and dissociates from it. The listener detects the dissociation and infers the opposite.

This matters because the echoic account explains something Grice's framework can't: why sarcasm often **echoes specific prior statements** ("Oh sure, 'it'll be done by Friday,' just like you said") rather than just saying the opposite of reality.

## 8. Implicature and the Autistic Experience

### 8.1 What the Research Actually Shows

The standard narrative -- "autistic people can't understand implied meaning" -- is substantially wrong. The research reveals a far more nuanced picture:

**What autistic individuals handle well:**
- **Scalar implicatures** ("some" means "not all"): Multiple studies (Pijnacker et al., 2009; Andrés-Roqueta & Katsos, 2020; Andrés-Roqueta et al., 2025) show performance at or near neurotypical levels.
- **Material particularized implicatures** (where the inference can be worked out from the propositional content alone): Jary et al. (2025) found autistic adults mirrored neurotypicals.
- **Conventional implicatures** ("but" means contrast): These are word-level knowledge and don't require social inference.

**Where genuine difficulty exists:**
- **Irony and sarcasm**: Consistently the hardest category. Requires understanding that the speaker believes the **opposite** of what they said -- a second-order mental state attribution.
- **Behavioural particularized implicatures** (requiring reasoning about why the speaker chose those words): Somewhat harder, though Jary et al. (2025) found smaller differences than expected.
- **Real-time processing**: Many studies find that autistic adults perform well on implicature tasks in controlled settings but struggle more in live, time-pressured conversation. This suggests a **processing demand** issue, not a competence deficit.

### 8.2 The Linguistic vs. Social Pragmatics Distinction

The most influential framework for understanding autistic implicature processing comes from Andrés-Roqueta & Katsos (2020):

- **Linguistic-pragmatic** inferences (scalar implicatures, some conventional implicatures): Depend on language knowledge and pragmatic scales. **Relatively preserved** in autism.
- **Social-pragmatic** inferences (irony, sarcasm, speaker-attitude reasoning): Additionally require modeling the speaker's mental states. **More challenging** in autism.

This distinction means the learning hub should approach different types of implicature differently:
- Scalar implicatures → Teach as learnable scales (autistic strength in systemizing)
- Conventional implicatures → Teach as vocabulary (word-level knowledge)
- Particularized implicatures → Teach the reasoning procedure (step-by-step inference)
- Irony/sarcasm → Teach recognition patterns (prosodic cues, context flags, common formulas)

### 8.3 The Predictive Processing Account

Vicente et al. (2023) proposed that autistic "literalism" is not a deficit in pragmatic reasoning but a consequence of **reduced confidence in top-down predictions**. In the predictive processing framework:

- Neurotypical listeners generate strong predictions about what a speaker probably means based on context, social cues, and prior experience. These top-down predictions guide rapid interpretation.
- Autistic listeners may have lower confidence in these predictions, leading them to **default to literal interpretation** as the safest, most reliable option.
- This isn't an inability to do pragmatic inference -- it's a rational strategy given uncertainty about top-down models.

**Implication**: Building confidence in predictions (through explicit learning, pattern catalogs, and practice) should improve implicature processing. The more patterns you've explicitly learned, the stronger your top-down predictions become.

### 8.4 The Double Empathy Angle

The inference failure isn't one-directional. Neurotypical people also fail to draw correct implicatures from autistic speech:

- Crompton et al. (2020) showed that information transfer is equally effective in autistic-autistic and NT-NT pairs, but drops in mixed pairs.
- Geelhand et al. (2025) found autistic adults change their verbal behavior specifically in mixed-neurotype interactions.
- Neurotypical listeners may misinterpret autistic directness (no hedging, no softening) as rudeness, when it's actually a different pragmatic system that prioritizes clarity over face-management.

### 8.5 Gricean Violations in Autistic Speech

Research (Wahyunianto et al.) found that the **maxim of Relevance** is the most frequently violated in autistic speech, followed by Quantity. However, what looks like a "violation" may reflect **different conversational priorities**:

- Providing extensive detail about a topic of interest (apparent Quantity violation) may reflect a priority on thoroughness and accuracy over brevity.
- Topic shifts that seem irrelevant (apparent Relevance violation) may reflect genuine interest connections that are not obvious to the listener.
- Using precise, technical language (apparent Manner violation) may reflect a priority on accuracy over social accessibility.

Understanding Grice's maxims gives autistic speakers a framework for recognizing how their natural communication style may be perceived -- not to suppress it, but to make informed choices about when to adjust.

## 9. Key Takeaways

1. **The said/meant gap is systematic.** People don't communicate indirectly randomly. They do it through specific mechanisms (Quantity, Quality, Relation, Manner) that generate specific types of inference.

2. **Flouting ≠ violating.** When a maxim is *obviously* broken (flouting), it generates implicature -- the listener searches for hidden meaning. When it's *secretly* broken (violating), it's deception.

3. **Scalar implicatures are your friend.** "Some" means "not all." "Good" means "not excellent." These are learnable patterns that don't require social intuition. Build your vocabulary of scales.

4. **The Enthusiasm Test is the single most useful detector.** When someone could say something more positive and doesn't, the gap between what they said and what they *could* have said IS the message.

5. **The four-step decision tree works.** Incomplete? → Quantity. Obviously false? → Quality. Off-topic? → Relevance. Oddly phrased? → Manner.

6. **Relevance Theory offers an algorithm.** Instead of four maxims, one question: "What interpretation of this utterance would be most relevant given what I know?" Follow the path of least effort to the first satisfactory interpretation.

7. **Conventional phrases are vocabulary.** "We'll see" = probably no. "I'm fine" = I'm not fine. "With all due respect" = I'm about to be disrespectful. Learn these as you'd learn any vocabulary set.

8. **Your directness is not a deficit.** Autistic communication prioritizes clarity. The neurotypical system prioritizes indirectness. Neither is objectively superior. But knowing both systems gives you choices.
