## 1. Autistic Facial Expression Differences

### Core Finding: Autistic people DO express emotions — just differently

The body of research consistently shows that autistic facial expressions are **different, not absent**. When feeling sad, an autistic person might move their face into an expression that is not the downturned mouth expression most neurotypical individuals would adopt — but they ARE expressing emotion. The problem is not that autistic people lack expression; it is that neurotypical observers lack the ability to read those expressions.

---

### Study: Brewer et al. (2016) — Neurotypical People Struggle to Read Autistic Expressions

**Full Citation:** Brewer, R., Biotti, F., Catmur, C., Press, C., Happe, F., Cook, R., & Bird, G. (2016). Can neurotypical individuals read autistic facial expressions? Atypical production of emotional facial expressions in autism spectrum disorders. *Autism Research*, 9(2), 262–271.

**DOI:** [10.1002/aur.1508](https://doi.org/10.1002/aur.1508)

**Link:** [Wiley Online Library](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/aur.1508) | [PMC Full Text](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4975602/)

**Methodology:** Investigated ASD and neurotypical (NT) adults' ability to recognize emotional expressions produced by ASD and NT models. This was the first study to use raters with ASD in addition to NT raters.

**Key Findings:**
- ASD expressions were **more poorly recognized** than NT expressions **regardless of recognizer group** (both NT and ASD raters found ASD expressions harder to read)
- NT individuals find it difficult to recognize autistic emotional expressions
- This suggests the "readability" problem is about cross-neurotype communication differences, not an absence of expression in autistic people

**Critical Implication:** If even autistic raters find other autistic expressions somewhat harder to decode (though less so than NT raters), this reflects genuine *difference* in expressive style rather than deficit. Training autistic children to mimic NT expressions would be like forcing someone to speak a second language while forbidding their native tongue.

---

### Study: Brewer et al. (2016) — Alexithymia, Not Autism, Drives Expression Differences

**Full Citation:** Brewer, R., Biotti, F., Catmur, C., Press, C., Happe, F., Cook, R., & Bird, G. (2016). Alexithymia, but not autism spectrum disorder, may be related to the production of emotional facial expressions. *Molecular Autism*, 7, 41.

**DOI:** [10.1186/s13229-016-0108-6](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13229-016-0108-6)

**Link:** [Molecular Autism Full Text](https://molecularautism.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s13229-016-0108-6)

**Key Findings:**
- **Alexithymic traits — but NOT ASD traits, IQ, or sex** — were associated with quantity of facial expression production
- This means reduced facial expression in autism may be better explained by **co-occurring alexithymia** rather than autism itself
- Many autistic people without alexithymia produce facial expressions at typical rates

**Critical Implication:** Any technology that assumes autistic people have "deficient" facial expressions is conflating autism with alexithymia — a scientifically inaccurate premise.

---

### Study: Keating, Sowden-Carvalho, O'Donoghue, & Cook (2026) — Autistic Expressions Are "Different Languages"

**Full Citation:** Keating, C.T., Sowden-Carvalho, S., O'Donoghue, H., & Cook, J.L. (2026). Mismatching expressions: Spatiotemporal and kinematic differences in autistic and non-autistic facial expressions. *Autism Research*.

**DOI:** [10.1002/aur.70157](https://doi.org/10.1002/aur.70157)

**Link:** [Wiley Online Library](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/aur.70157)

**Sample:** 25 autistic and 26 non-autistic adults; nearly 5,000 expressions recorded; 265+ million data points from facial motion tracking.

**Key Findings:**
- For **anger**: autistic participants relied more on the mouth and less on eyebrows than non-autistic peers
- For **happiness**: autistic participants showed a less exaggerated smile that did not "reach the eyes"
- For **sadness**: autistic participants created a downturned expression by raising their upper lip more
- Autistic and non-autistic people differ not only in the **appearance** of facial expressions but also in **how smoothly** expressions are formed (kinematic differences)
- Alexithymia (not autism per se) was linked to less clearly distinct angry and happy expressions

**Researcher Conclusion:** "Autistic and non-autistic people may express emotions in ways that are different but equally meaningful — almost like speaking different languages. What has sometimes been interpreted as difficulties for autistic people might instead reflect a **two-way challenge** in understanding each other's expressions."

**Press Coverage:**
- [ScienceDaily: New research shows emotional expressions work differently in autism](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/01/260118233549.htm)
- [University of Birmingham press release](https://www.birmingham.ac.uk/news/2026/autistic-and-non-autistic-faces-may-speak-a-different-language-when-expressing-emotion)
- [SciTechDaily: Autistic Faces Show Emotion Differently And That's Not a Deficit](https://scitechdaily.com/autistic-faces-show-emotion-differently-and-thats-not-a-deficit/)

### Study: Sheppard, Pillai, Wong, Ropar, & Mitchell (2016) — Different but Not Less Expressive

**Full Citation:** Sheppard, E., Pillai, D., Wong, G.T.L., Ropar, D., & Mitchell, P. (2016). How easy is it to read the minds of people with autism spectrum disorder? *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 46, 1247–1254.

**DOI:** [10.1007/s10803-015-2662-8](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10803-015-2662-8)

**Link:** [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26603886/) | [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10803-015-2662-8)

**Methodology:** "Targets" (ASD and NT) had their reactions to four events video-recorded. NT participants then attempted to identify which event each target had experienced.

**Key Findings:**
- In Study 1: participants were more successful for NT than ASD targets
- In Study 2: participants rated ASD targets as **equally expressive** as NT targets for 3 of 4 events
- In Study 3: participants gave **different verbal descriptions** of ASD vs. NT target reactions
- **Conclusion: People with ASD react differently but NOT LESS EXPRESSIVELY to events**

**Critical Implication:** Autistic people are not under-expressing; they are expressing differently. Neurotypical people's inability to read those expressions is an NT comprehension problem, not an autistic production problem.

### Study: Faso, Sasson, & Pinkham (2015) — Posed vs. Evoked Expressions

**Full Citation:** Faso, D.J., Sasson, N.J., & Pinkham, A.E. (2015). Evaluating posed and evoked facial expressions of emotion from adults with autism spectrum disorder. *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 45, 75–89.

**DOI:** [10.1007/s10803-014-2194-7](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10803-014-2194-7)

**Link:** [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25037584/) | [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10803-014-2194-7)

**Sample:** 6 ASD adults and 6 TD adults produced expressions; 38 naive female observers judged them.

**Methodology:** Photographed participants' posed expressions (asked to make a face) and evoked expressions (coached to re-experience emotional stories). Naive raters then identified emotions and rated intensity/naturalness.

**Key Findings:**
- ASD expressions were rated as **more intense** and **less natural** than TD expressions
- However, ASD expressions were **identified with GREATER accuracy** than TD expressions (an effect driven by angry expressions)
- Faso noted: "If the expressivity of a person with ASD is perceived as atypical, that can present challenges beyond their difficulties processing social information. If they are viewed as awkward, unnatural and overly intense, then people may respond inappropriately."

**Critical Implication:** Autistic expressions are MORE intense and MORE recognizable — they are just rated as "unnatural" by neurotypical standards. The problem is neurotypical aesthetic bias, not autistic expressive deficit.

---

### Meta-Analysis: Trevisan, Hoskyn, & Birmingham (2018) — Comprehensive Review of Expression Production

**Full Citation:** Trevisan, D.A., Hoskyn, M., & Birmingham, E. (2018). Facial expression production in autism: A meta-analysis. *Autism Research*, 11(12), 1586–1601.

**DOI:** [10.1002/aur.2037](https://doi.org/10.1002/aur.2037)

**Link:** [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30393953/) | [Wiley](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/aur.2037)

**Sample:** 67 effect sizes aggregated across multiple studies.

**Key Findings:**
- Overall moderate effect size (d = -0.481) indicating autistic people DO produce expressions differently
- Autistic people display expressions less frequently, for shorter durations, and are less likely to share expressions with others
- Expressions are judged as "lower in quality" by NT raters — but this is a subjective judgment of style, not substance
- **Critical finding:** Group differences were stronger for "covertly elicited" than "explicitly elicited" expressions — meaning autistic people naturally express differently but can produce "typical" expressions when prompted, suggesting the difference is one of **natural communication style**, not incapacity
- Effect sizes were moderated by age, intellectual functioning, and measurement methods

---

### Study: Edey et al. (2016) — Neurotypical "Mind-Blindness" Toward Autistic People

**Full Citation:** Edey, R., Cook, J., Brewer, R., Johnson, M.H., Bird, G., & Press, C. (2016). Interaction takes two: Typical adults exhibit mind-blindness towards those with autism spectrum disorder. *Journal of Abnormal Psychology*, 125(7), 879–885.

**DOI:** [10.1037/abn0000199](https://doi.org/10.1037/abn0000199)

**Key Findings:**
- Non-autistic people struggle to judge the emotional expressions of autistic people
- Non-autistic individuals have difficulty understanding autistic facial expressions and body movements
- This turns the traditional "mind-blindness" narrative on its head: NT people show their own form of mind-blindness when interacting with autistic people

**Critical Implication:** The very concept of using facial recognition to "teach" autistic children "normal" expressions presumes autistic people are the ones who are broken. This study shows neurotypical people are equally "broken" at reading autistic expressions — the problem is bidirectional.

## 2. The Double Empathy Problem

### Foundational Paper: Milton (2012) — The Original Formulation

**Full Citation:** Milton, D.E.M. (2012). On the ontological status of autism: The 'double empathy problem.' *Disability & Society*, 27(6), 883–887.

**DOI:** [10.1080/09687599.2012.710008](https://doi.org/10.1080/09687599.2012.710008)

**Link:** [Taylor & Francis](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/09687599.2012.710008) | [Kent Academic Repository PDF](https://kar.kent.ac.uk/62639/1/Double%20empathy%20problem.pdf)

**Core Argument:**
- Communication difficulties between autistic and non-autistic people are **bidirectional** — both parties struggle to understand each other
- The traditional framing of autism as a "social communication disorder" places the locus of the problem in the autistic person's brain. Milton argues this is a **mutual and interpersonal** issue
- The "double empathy problem" refers to a breakdown in mutual understanding that is more likely when people of very different dispositions attempt to interact
- This critique challenges the dominance of "Theory of Mind" deficit models that claim autistic people lack empathy or social understanding

**Critical Implication for the AAC app:** Using facial recognition to normalize autistic expressions is a product of the *deficit model* that Milton critiques. It assumes the communication failure belongs to the autistic person alone. The double empathy problem shows it belongs to both parties equally.

---

### 10-Year Follow-Up: Milton, Gurbuz, & Lopez (2022)

**Full Citation:** Milton, D., Gurbuz, E., & Lopez, B. (2022). The 'double empathy problem': Ten years on. *Autism*, 26(8), 1901–1903.

**DOI:** [10.1177/13623613221129123](https://doi.org/10.1177/13623613221129123)

**Link:** [SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/13623613221129123)

**Key Update:** Reviews the decade of empirical evidence that has accumulated supporting the double empathy framework since 2012, confirming that the theory has been robustly validated.

### Empirical Validation: Crompton et al. (2020) — Autistic Peer Communication Works

**Full Citation:** Crompton, C.J., Ropar, D., Evans-Williams, C.V.M., Flynn, E.G., & Fletcher-Watson, S. (2020). Autistic peer-to-peer information transfer is highly effective. *Autism*, 24(7), 1704–1712.

**DOI:** [10.1177/1362361320919286](https://doi.org/10.1177/1362361320919286)

**Link:** [SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1362361320919286) | [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32431157/)

**Sample:** 72 participants divided into 9 diffusion chains of 8 people each (3 all-autistic chains, 3 all-non-autistic chains, 3 mixed chains).

**Methodology:** A "telephone game" (diffusion chain) paradigm. Person 1 is told a 30-point story about a bear on a surreal adventure, then recounts it to Person 2, who recounts it to Person 3, and so on through 8 people.

**Key Findings:**
- **Autistic-autistic chains retained information just as well as non-autistic chains**
- **Mixed chains (autistic + non-autistic) showed significantly steeper decline in detail retention**
- Rapport ratings were **significantly lower** in mixed chains
- Autistic people share information with other autistic people **as effectively** as non-autistic people do with each other

**Critical Implication:** Autistic communication is not deficient — it is different. When autistic people communicate with each other, information transfer is highly effective. The breakdown occurs only at the cross-neurotype boundary, supporting Milton's double empathy theory. Training autistic children to emulate neurotypical expressions does NOT fix the underlying bidirectional communication mismatch — it just forces autistic people to bear the entire burden of adaptation.

---

### Empirical Validation: Morrison et al. (2019) — The Problem Is in the Observer

**Full Citation:** Morrison, K.E., DeBrabander, K.M., Faso, D.J., & Sasson, N.J. (2019). Variability in first impressions of autistic adults made by neurotypical raters is driven more by characteristics of the rater than by characteristics of autistic adults. *Autism*, 23(6), 1518–1529.

**DOI:** [10.1177/1362361318824104](https://doi.org/10.1177/1362361318824104)

**Link:** [SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1362361318824104) | [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30848682/)

**Sample:** 20 autistic adults rated by 505 neurotypical raters.

**Key Findings:**
- Variability in first impression ratings was driven **more by characteristics of the raters** than by characteristics of the autistic adults
- Only ratings of "awkwardness" were driven more by autistic adult characteristics than rater characteristics
- When raters were informed of autism status, impressions generally improved — EXCEPT among raters with high stigma toward autism, where disclosure **worsened** impressions
- Rater stigma and autism knowledge contributed significantly to first impression ratings

**Critical Implication:** The "problem" with how autistic people are perceived is largely a problem with the perceiver. Technology that tries to change the autistic person's face is targeting the wrong variable.

---

### Empirical Validation: Sasson et al. (2017) — Thin-Slice Judgments

**Full Citation:** Sasson, N.J., Faso, D.J., Nugent, J., Lovell, S., Kennedy, D.P., & Grossman, R.B. (2017). Neurotypical peers are less willing to interact with those with autism based on thin slice judgments. *Scientific Reports*, 7, 40700.

**DOI:** [10.1038/srep40700](https://doi.org/10.1038/srep40700)

**Link:** [Nature Scientific Reports](https://www.nature.com/articles/srep40700) | [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28145411/)

**Key Findings:**
- First impressions of autistic individuals were **far less favorable** across multiple trait judgments and were associated with **reduced willingness to pursue social interaction**
- These negative patterns were "remarkably robust, occurred within seconds, did not change with increased exposure, and persisted across both child and adult age groups"
- Autistic people were rated similarly to non-autistic adults on **trustworthiness and intelligence** but less favorably on **likeability and awkwardness**
- **CRITICAL FINDING: The biases disappeared when impressions were based on conversational content lacking audio-visual cues** — meaning the substance of what autistic people said was fine; it was only the *style* of presentation that triggered negative judgments

**Critical Implication:** Neurotypical people reject autistic people based on superficial presentation style, not content. A facial recognition system teaching "normal" expressions is literally training autistic children to perform for an audience whose negative judgments are based on surface-level bias, not genuine incompatibility.

---

### Heasman & Gillespie (2019) — Autistic Intersubjectivity

**Full Citation:** Heasman, B. & Gillespie, A. (2019). Neurodivergent intersubjectivity: Distinctive features of how autistic people create shared understanding. *Autism*, 23(4), 910–921.

**DOI:** [10.1177/1362361318785172](https://doi.org/10.1177/1362361318785172)

**Link:** [SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1362361318785172) | [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30073872/)

**Sample:** 30 autistic adults video-recorded during collaborative video gaming sessions.

**Key Findings:**
- Autistic social interaction incorporates distinctive features that promote social understanding between autistic people — termed "neurodivergent intersubjectivity"
- Identified features like "generous assumption of common ground" and "low demand for coordination"
- Neurodivergent interactions provide opportunities for rich intersubjectivity even when faced with fragmentation
- **Neurotypical norms potentially limit this possibility because they interpret such fragmentation as failures needing to be addressed**

**Critical Implication:** Autistic people have their own effective social communication system. Neurotypical norms are not the only valid framework. Imposing those norms through facial recognition technology destroys autistic social strengths.

## 3. Why Training "Normal" Expressions Is Harmful

### It Is a Form of Forced Masking

Research on autistic masking/camouflaging consistently demonstrates that the pressure to appear "normal" is one of the most psychologically damaging aspects of living as an autistic person in a neurotypical world.

---

### Study: Hull et al. (2017) — "Putting on My Best Normal"

**Full Citation:** Hull, L., Petrides, K.V., Allison, C., Smith, P., Baron-Cohen, S., Lai, M.C., & Mandy, W. (2017). "Putting on my best normal": Social camouflaging in adults with autism spectrum conditions. *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 47(8), 2519–2534.

**DOI:** [10.1007/s10803-017-3166-5](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10803-017-3166-5)

**Link:** [PMC Full Text](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5509825/) | [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28527095/)

**Sample:** 92 adults with autism spectrum conditions.

**Methodology:** Qualitative analysis developing a three-stage model of the camouflaging process.

**Key Findings — Three-Stage Model:**
1. **Motivations:** Fitting in and increasing connections with others
2. **Process:** A combination of *masking* (suppressing autistic behaviors) and *compensation* (developing alternative strategies)
3. **Consequences:** Exhaustion, threats to self-perception, identity confusion

**Specific to facial expressions:** Participants described rehearsing facial expressions, consciously controlling their facial muscles, and monitoring their face to ensure it displayed "appropriate" emotions — a process that was exhausting and disconnected them from their actual emotional experience.

---

### Study: Pearson & Rose (2021) — Masking, Stigma, and the "Illusion of Choice"

**Full Citation:** Pearson, A. & Rose, K. (2021). A conceptual analysis of autistic masking: Understanding the narrative of stigma and the illusion of choice. *Autism in Adulthood*, 3(1), 52–60.

**DOI:** [10.1089/aut.2020.0043](https://doi.org/10.1089/aut.2020.0043)

**Link:** [SAGE/Liebert Full Text](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1089/aut.2020.0043) | [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8992880/)

**Key Arguments:**
- Masking is an unsurprising response to the **deficit narrative** and accompanying **stigma** that has developed around autism
- Masking is framed as a "choice" but is actually coerced by an intolerant social environment — hence the "illusion of choice"
- Receiving a diagnosis later in life can lead to profound difficulty in disentangling which parts of one's identity are "real" versus products of masking
- The paper situates masking within social identity theory, showing how stigma drives conformity

**Critical Implication:** Teaching autistic children to make "normal" faces is not giving them a tool — it is training them in the very masking behavior that research shows leads to identity loss, burnout, and mental health crises. The technology is not neutral; it is an instrument of forced normalization.

---

### Surface Acting and Emotional Labor Theory Applied to Autism

**Foundational Theory:** Hochschild, A.R. (1983). *The Managed Heart: Commercialization of Human Feeling.* University of California Press.

Hochschild identified two forms of emotional labor:
- **Surface acting:** Displaying emotions you do not feel while suppressing real emotions (e.g., smiling when sad)
- **Deep acting:** Working on your emotions to actually feel what you are supposed to display

**Research on Surface Acting and Burnout:**
- Surface acting leads to **emotional dissonance** — a discrepancy between felt and expressed emotions ([PMC review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5823819/))
- Emotional dissonance leads to emotional exhaustion, burnout, decreased satisfaction, anxiety, and depression
- Surface acting is consistently associated with burnout, whereas deep acting is not

**Application to Autism:**
Teaching autistic children to produce "normal" facial expressions is training them in chronic **surface acting**. They are being taught to display emotions they may not feel, or to display emotions in a way that conflicts with their natural expression. This is the exact mechanism Hochschild identified as leading to burnout — except autistic children are being asked to do it **all day, every day, for their entire lives**, not just during work hours.

---

### Study: Livingston et al. (2019) — Compensation vs. Masking Distinction

**Full Citation:** Livingston, L.A., Shah, P., & Happe, F. (2019). Compensatory strategies below the behavioural surface in autism: A qualitative study. *The Lancet Psychiatry*, 6(9), 766–777.

**DOI:** [10.1016/S2215-0366(19)30224-X](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2215-0366(19)30224-X)

**Link:** [The Lancet](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanpsy/article/PIIS2215-0366(19)30224-X/fulltext) | [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31350208/)

**Sample:** 136 adults (58 clinically diagnosed autistic, 19 self-identified, 59 with social difficulties).

**Key Findings:**
- Distinguished between **compensation** (adaptive, generating new behaviors to navigate situations) and **masking** (suppressing or hiding behaviors deemed undesirable)
- "Deep compensation" involved complex, flexible strategies that genuinely improved social cognition (e.g., pattern detection: gesture + facial expression + context = particular mental state)
- "Shallow strategies" transferred poorly to new contexts and seldom reduced social difficulties
- Masking is fundamentally different from compensation — it is about **hiding**, not **learning**

**Critical Implication:** A facial recognition app teaching "normal" faces is training masking, not compensation. It does not help autistic children understand social situations — it trains them to hide their authentic responses. Genuine compensation would involve helping autistic children understand what emotions others are expressing AND helping neurotypical people understand what autistic expressions mean — a bidirectional approach.

---

### Editorial: Mandy (2019) — "Is It Time to Lose the Mask?"

**Full Citation:** Mandy, W. (2019). Social camouflaging in autism: Is it time to lose the mask? *Autism*, 23(8), 1879–1881.

**DOI:** [10.1177/1362361319878559](https://doi.org/10.1177/1362361319878559)

**Link:** [SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1362361319878559) | [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31552745/)

**Key Argument:**
- Attempts to change only the individual through intervention, without modifying the environment, **run the risk of being ineffective or harmful**
- Interventions should emphasize improving **person-environment fit** by modifying the environment rather than the person
- Autistic people commonly state they would like to camouflage less but fear consequences of being more openly autistic
- The #TakeTheMaskOff movement reflects autistic community resistance to forced normalization

## 4. Impact on Emotional Authenticity and Wellbeing

### Masking and Mental Health: The Research Is Unambiguous

---

### Study: Cage, Di Monaco, & Newell (2018) — Acceptance Predicts Mental Health

**Full Citation:** Cage, E., Di Monaco, J., & Newell, V. (2018). Experiences of autism acceptance and mental health in autistic adults. *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 48, 473–484.

**DOI:** [10.1007/s10803-017-3342-7](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10803-017-3342-7)

**Link:** [PMC Full Text](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5807490/) | [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29071566/)

**Sample:** 111 autistic adults.

**Key Findings:**
- Autism acceptance from external sources and personal acceptance significantly predicted depression
- Acceptance from others also significantly predicted stress
- A subset of participants (n=27) spontaneously reported camouflaging experiences
- Camouflaging was associated with **higher rates of depression**
- Participants reported camouflaging had a detrimental effect on psychological wellbeing

---

### Study: Cassidy et al. (2018) — Camouflaging as Suicide Risk Factor

**Full Citation:** Cassidy, S., Bradley, L., Shaw, R., & Baron-Cohen, S. (2018). Risk markers for suicidality in autistic adults. *Molecular Autism*, 9, 42.

**DOI:** [10.1186/s13229-018-0226-4](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13229-018-0226-4)

**Link:** [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13229-018-0226-4) | [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30083306/)

**Key Findings:**
- **Camouflaging** was identified as a unique risk marker for suicidality in autistic adults
- Non-suicidal self-injury, camouflaging, and number of unmet support needs significantly predicted suicidality
- Camouflaging explained small but significant **additional variance** in suicidality above known risk factors common in the general population

---

### Study: Cassidy et al. (2020) — Camouflaging Linked to Suicidal Thoughts

**Full Citation:** Cassidy, S.A., Gould, K., Townsend, E., Pelton, M., Robertson, A.E., & Rodgers, J. (2020). Is camouflaging autistic traits associated with suicidal thoughts and behaviours? Expanding the Interpersonal Psychological Theory of Suicide in an undergraduate student sample. *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 50(10), 3638–3648.

**DOI:** [10.1007/s10803-019-04323-3](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10803-019-04323-3)

**Link:** [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31820344/) | [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7502035/)

**Key Findings:**
- Camouflaging autistic traits is associated with increased risk of experiencing **thwarted belongingness** and **lifetime suicidality**
- Camouflaging is a **transdiagnostic risk factor** for lifetime suicidality
- Relevant to both the Interpersonal Psychological Theory of Suicide and the Integrated Motivational Volitional model

---

### Study: Cassidy et al. (2023) — Further Confirmation

**Full Citation:** Cassidy, S.A., et al. (2023). Is camouflaging autistic traits associated with defeat, entrapment, and lifetime suicidal thoughts? Expanding the Integrated Motivational Volitional Model of Suicide. *Suicide and Life-Threatening Behavior*, 53(5), 812–824.

**DOI:** [10.1111/sltb.12965](https://doi.org/10.1111/sltb.12965)

**Link:** [Wiley](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/sltb.12965) | [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37129164/)

**Key Finding:** Camouflaging autistic traits is a transdiagnostic risk factor for lifetime suicidality, relevant to the defeat and entrapment constructs of the Integrated Motivational Volitional model.

### Study: Raymaker et al. (2020) — Defining Autistic Burnout

**Full Citation:** Raymaker, D.M., Teo, A.R., Steckler, N.A., Lentz, B., Scharer, M., Delos Santos, A., Kapp, S.K., Hunter, M., Joyce, A., & Nicolaidis, C. (2020). "Having all of your internal resources exhausted beyond measure and being left with no clean-up crew": Defining autistic burnout. *Autism in Adulthood*, 2(2), 132–143.

**DOI:** [10.1089/aut.2019.0079](https://doi.org/10.1089/aut.2019.0079)

**Link:** [PMC Full Text](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7313636/) | [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32851204/)

**Methodology:** Community-based participatory research; thematic analysis of 19 interviews and 19 public internet sources.

**Key Findings:**
- Autistic burnout is characterized by: **chronic exhaustion, loss of skills, and reduced tolerance to stimulus**
- Top contributing factor: **Masking autistic traits** — suppressing autistic behaviors, pretending to be non-autistic, working hard to act in a non-autistic way
- Other factors: unreachable expectations from society, stress from living in a world not set up for autistic people
- Negative impacts: mental health difficulties, struggling with independent living, loss of self-belief, fear that skill loss might be permanent, **increased suicidal ideation and behavior**

**Critical Implication:** Autistic burnout is directly caused by masking. A facial recognition app that trains children to mask their expressions is engineering burnout in children. The scientific literature could not be more clear on this point.

### Systematic Reviews Confirming the Pattern

**Systematic Review:** Cook, J., Hull, L., Crane, L., & Mandy, W. (2021). Camouflaging in autism: A systematic review. *Clinical Psychology Review*, 89, 102080.

**Link:** [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0272735821001239)

**Mixed-Methods Review:** Bradley, L., Shaw, R., Baron-Cohen, S., & Cassidy, S. (2021). Autistic adults' experiences of camouflaging and its perceived impact on mental health. *Autism in Adulthood*, 3(4), 320–329.

**Link:** [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8992917/)

**Key Findings Across Reviews:**
- Up to 79% of autistic adults meet criteria for a co-occurring psychiatric condition
- Camouflaging is described as "highly stressful, exhausting, and anxiety provoking"
- Camouflaging increases risk of depression, suicidal thoughts, and self-harm
- Fourteen studies described pressure and expectations to conform to non-autistic norms as a reason for camouflaging
- Participants reported camouflaging to avoid attention and to fit in with society's expectations of "normal"
- Camouflaging led to "mental health breakdowns," "suicidal thoughts," and "self-injury"

## 5. Neurodiversity-Affirming Alternatives for Communication Technology

### The Right Approach: Translate, Don't Normalize

The research literature points toward a clear alternative to normalization-based technology: **bidirectional translation** and **authentic expression support**.

---

### Paper: van Grunsven & Roeser (2021) — AAC Technology and the Empathic Turn

**Full Citation:** van Grunsven, J. & Roeser, S. (2021). AAC technology, autism, and the empathic turn. *Social Epistemology*, 35(5), 475–489.

**DOI:** [10.1080/02691728.2021.1897189](https://doi.org/10.1080/02691728.2021.1897189)

**Link:** [Taylor & Francis Full Text](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/02691728.2021.1897189)

**Key Arguments:**
- AAC technology should promote an "empathic turn" — recognizing non-speaking autistic people as persons whose ways of engaging the world are "indicative of psychologically rich and intrinsically meaningful experiential lives"
- AAC technology can simultaneously **undermine** genuine empathic communication if it imposes neurotypical frameworks
- The field should incorporate insights from Design for Emotions and enactive embodied cognitive science
- **Stimming** should be recognized as an autistic form of bodily expressivity that plays an important role in empathic communicative exchanges — not something to suppress

---

### Neurodiversity-Affirming Principles for Communication Technology

Based on the research literature, the following principles should guide AAC and communication technology development:

1. **Validate all communication methods:** A neurodiversity-affirming perspective values all forms of expression — scripting, AAC, stimming, echolalia — as meaningful and valid forms of communication.

2. **Focus on authentic expression, not conformity:** True progress lies in nurturing each person's natural communication preferences, with a focus on authenticity rather than compliance.

3. **Bridge, don't normalize:** Technology should help neurotypical people understand autistic expressions AND help autistic people understand neurotypical expressions — **bidirectional translation**, not one-way normalization.

4. **Support, don't suppress:** AI-powered AAC devices should analyze input and provide contextual suggestions to support meaningful conversation without requiring conformity to neurotypical norms.

5. **Teach the environment to adapt:** Per Mandy (2019), interventions should modify the environment, not force the individual to conform.

### What Technology SHOULD Do Instead

| Instead of... | Technology should... |
|---|---|
| Teaching autistic children to make "normal" faces | Help neurotypical people understand autistic expressions |
| Facial recognition to detect "wrong" expressions | Emotion boards/symbols that let children communicate feelings in their own way |
| Training compliance with NT expression norms | Support authentic self-expression through multiple modalities |
| Treating autistic expression as deficient | Provide bidirectional translation tools |
| Measuring success by proximity to NT behavior | Measure success by communicative effectiveness and wellbeing |

## 6. Summary Argument Against Facial Normalization Technology

### The Scientific Case

1. **Autistic people express emotions — differently, not deficiently.** (Brewer et al. 2016; Sheppard et al. 2016; Keating et al. 2026; Faso et al. 2015; Trevisan et al. 2018 meta-analysis)

2. **The "problem" is bidirectional.** Neurotypical people are bad at reading autistic expressions, just as autistic people may struggle with NT expressions. The Double Empathy Problem (Milton, 2012) and the Crompton et al. (2020) diffusion chain study prove that autistic-autistic communication works perfectly well — the breakdown happens at the cross-neurotype boundary.

3. **Negative impressions are driven by neurotypical bias, not autistic deficit.** Sasson et al. (2017) showed negative judgments disappear when visual cues are removed. Morrison et al. (2019) showed impression variability is driven more by rater characteristics than autistic characteristics. The problem is in the observer, not the observed.

4. **Training "normal" expressions IS masking.** Hull et al. (2017), Pearson & Rose (2021), and Livingston et al. (2019) document that rehearsing facial expressions is a core component of camouflaging/masking behavior.

5. **Masking causes severe harm.** Masking is linked to:
   - Depression and anxiety (Cage et al. 2018)
   - Suicidal ideation and behavior (Cassidy et al. 2018, 2020, 2023)
   - Autistic burnout (Raymaker et al. 2020)
   - Loss of identity (Pearson & Rose 2021)
   - Emotional exhaustion (Hull et al. 2017)

6. **Masking in children is even more concerning.** Teaching masking to children — whose identities are still forming — risks permanent identity confusion, early-onset burnout, and a lifetime of emotional dissonance.

7. **Hochschild's emotional labor theory predicts the harm.** Chronic surface acting (displaying unfelt emotions) leads to emotional dissonance, exhaustion, and burnout. Teaching children to produce "correct" expressions when they feel differently is training them in lifelong surface acting.

### The Ethical Case

Using facial recognition technology to teach autistic children to "be normal" is:

- **Scientifically unsupported:** Based on outdated deficit models that ignore 15+ years of research on bidirectional communication differences
- **Actively harmful:** Training the exact masking behaviors that research links to depression, burnout, and suicidality
- **Ethically problematic:** Assumes autistic expression is wrong and needs correction rather than understanding
- **Unidirectional when the problem is bidirectional:** Places all burden of adaptation on autistic children while doing nothing to educate neurotypical observers
- **A form of normalization, not communication support:** Prioritizes "looking normal" over actually connecting

### What the Evidence Supports Instead

Technology that:
- Translates between neurotypes rather than normalizing one to match the other
- Supports authentic expression through multiple modalities (symbols, text, gesture, voice)
- Educates neurotypical users about autistic expression styles
- Measures success by wellbeing and communicative effectiveness, not conformity
- Follows neurodiversity-affirming principles (van Grunsven & Roeser, 2021)
