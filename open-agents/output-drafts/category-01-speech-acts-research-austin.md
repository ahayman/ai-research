# Speech Act Theory: J.L. Austin's Foundational Framework

## Research Summary

This document covers J.L. Austin's foundational theory of speech acts as presented in *How to Do Things with Words* (1962), including the three-layer architecture of speech acts, the performative hypothesis and its evolution, felicity conditions, and Austin's taxonomy of how speech acts fail. This research is intended for a knowledge base teaching English pragmatics to autistic individuals, where precision and structural clarity are paramount.

---

## 1. Historical Context: How Austin Developed Speech Act Theory

### 1.1 Austin the Person

John Langshaw Austin (26 March 1911 -- 8 February 1960) was a British philosopher of language at Oxford University and a leading figure in what became known as **ordinary language philosophy**. He held the position of White's Professor of Moral Philosophy from 1952 until his death from lung cancer at age 48. He published remarkably little during his lifetime; his influence spread primarily through lectures, tutorials, and his famous "Saturday morning meetings" at Oxford.

### 1.2 The William James Lectures

Austin first began formulating the central ideas of speech act theory around **1939**. He refined them through Oxford lectures in the early 1950s under the working title "Words and Deeds." In **1955**, Austin traveled to Harvard University, where he delivered the **William James Lectures** -- a prestigious twelve-lecture series. These lectures became the basis for *How to Do Things with Words*, which was published posthumously in **1962**, edited by J.O. Urmson. A corrected second edition followed in **1975**, co-edited by Urmson and Marina Sbisa, incorporating additional marginal notes from Austin's papers.

The text reads as a lightly edited set of lecture notes rather than a polished monograph -- Austin died before he could prepare them for publication. This explains the informal, example-driven style and certain tensions and unfinished lines of thought within the work.

### 1.3 The Core Insight

Austin challenged what he called the **"descriptive fallacy"** -- the dominant philosophical assumption that the primary purpose of language is to describe reality and make statements that are either true or false. Austin argued that many utterances do not describe anything at all. Instead, **to say something is often to do something**. When a bride says "I do" at a wedding ceremony, she is not describing an internal state; she is *performing* the act of marrying.

## 2. The Performative Hypothesis: Austin's Starting Point

### 2.1 Performative vs. Constative Utterances

Austin began his lectures by drawing a sharp distinction between two types of utterance:

**Constative utterances** are statements that describe states of affairs and can be evaluated as **true or false**:
- "The cat is on the mat."
- "It is raining."
- "Paris is the capital of France."

**Performative utterances** do not describe anything and are neither true nor false. Instead, they **perform an action** simply by being spoken in the right circumstances:
- **"I do"** -- uttered by a bride or groom during a wedding ceremony (performs the act of marrying)
- **"I name this ship the *Queen Elizabeth*"** -- uttered while smashing a bottle of champagne against the bow (performs the act of christening)
- **"I give and bequeath my watch to my brother"** -- as occurring in a will (performs the act of bequeathing)
- **"I bet you sixpence it will rain tomorrow"** -- (performs the act of betting)

Austin's key observation: "To utter the sentence (in, of course, the appropriate circumstances) is not to describe my doing of what I should be said in so uttering to be doing or to state that I am doing it: **it is to do it.**"

### 2.2 Why Austin Abandoned the Performative/Constative Distinction

This is one of the most important structural moves in the book. Austin spent the first several lectures carefully building the performative/constative distinction, then systematically **demolished it**. Marina Sbisa has interpreted this as a deliberate proof by contradiction -- Austin showing that the distinction fails in order to establish that **all speech is action**.

Austin found the distinction collapsed for four interconnected reasons:

#### Reason 1: No Clear Grammatical Boundary
Austin explored whether performatives could be identified by grammatical or lexical criteria (e.g., first person, present tense, active voice, presence of "hereby"). But he found these criteria were neither necessary nor sufficient. Many performatives lack these features, and many non-performatives have them.

#### Reason 2: Constatives Have Felicity Conditions Too
Austin initially claimed that performatives are assessed by happiness/unhappiness (felicity/infelicity), while constatives are assessed by truth/falsity. But he discovered that **constatives also have felicity conditions**. The truth of "I am stating that John is running" depends on the felicity of the speaker actually saying "John is running." Statements can "misfire" -- for example, if someone says "All of John's children are bald" when John has no children, the statement is neither straightforwardly true nor false; it presupposes something that does not hold.

#### Reason 3: Performatives Can Be True or False
Austin found that some performatives seem evaluable as true or false. The warning "I warn you that the bull is about to charge" is a **false warning** if the bull is not about to charge. The assessment "I estimate the population at 35 million" can turn out to be accurate or inaccurate.

#### Reason 4: Every Utterance Performs an Action
The deepest reason: Austin realized that even the most ordinary-seeming "constative" statement performs an action -- the act of **stating**, **asserting**, **claiming**, or **reporting**. When you say "The window is open," you are not merely describing reality; you are performing the act of informing, or requesting (that someone close it), or warning, or complaining. The apparently pure constative is actually just another kind of speech act.

Austin summarized: **"The traditional 'statement' is an abstraction, an ideal."**

### 2.3 The Fresh Start

Having shown that the performative/constative distinction does not hold, Austin announced a **"fresh start"** in Lecture VII. Rather than dividing utterances into two types, he proposed that **every utterance** simultaneously performs multiple acts. This is the three-layer architecture.

## 3. Austin's Three-Layer Architecture: Locutionary, Illocutionary, and Perlocutionary Acts

### 3.1 Overview

Austin proposed that in making any utterance, a speaker typically performs **at least three distinct but related acts simultaneously**:

| Layer | Definition | Key Question | Reported As |
|-------|-----------|--------------|-------------|
| **Locutionary act** | The act of **saying something** meaningful | What did the speaker say? | "She said that..." |
| **Illocutionary act** | The act performed **in saying** something | What did the speaker do by saying it? | "She warned / promised / ordered..." |
| **Perlocutionary act** | The act performed **by saying** something -- its effects | What happened as a result? | "She convinced / frightened / amused..." |

The prepositions are critical and were deliberately chosen by Austin:
- Locutionary = the act **of** saying
- Illocutionary = the act **in** saying
- Perlocutionary = the act **by** saying

### 3.2 The Locutionary Act (The Act of Saying Something)

The locutionary act is the production of a meaningful linguistic expression. Austin further analyzed it into **three sub-acts**:

#### Phonetic Act
The act of uttering certain **sounds**. A parrot can perform a phonetic act -- it makes the right noises -- but it is not performing a phatic or rhetic act.

#### Phatic Act
The act of uttering **words** -- sounds that belong to a recognized vocabulary and follow grammatical rules of a language. This goes beyond mere noise; it requires that the sounds conform to a language's structure. A phatic act is reported by **direct quotation**: *He said, "The cat is on the mat."*

#### Rhetic Act
The act of using those words with a **definite sense and reference** -- that is, with meaning. The speaker refers to particular things and predicates something of them. A rhetic act is reported by **indirect quotation**: *He said that the cat was on the mat.*

**The difference matters:** Someone reporting a phatic act claims to offer a verbatim account of the speaker's words, without commitment to whether the words successfully referred to anything. Someone reporting a rhetic act commits to the proposition that the speaker's words had definite sense and reference, but does not claim to reproduce the exact words. The speaker might have said, "The feline pet is lying upon the fabric used for protecting the floor" -- the rhetic act report would be the same: "He said that the cat was on the mat."

### 3.3 The Illocutionary Act (The Act In Saying Something)

The illocutionary act is the **conventional force** of the utterance -- what the speaker is doing *in* producing the utterance. This is the core concept of speech act theory and corresponds to what speakers commonly mean when they talk about "what someone meant" by saying something.

Illocutionary acts include: **asserting, questioning, commanding, requesting, promising, warning, apologizing, congratulating, declaring, threatening, advising, inviting, refusing, suggesting**, and many more.

Key properties of illocutionary acts:
- They are **conventional**: their force depends on recognized social conventions and procedures
- They are typically **under the speaker's control**: if the conditions are right, performing the act is within the speaker's power
- They can be made **explicit** using performative verbs: "I promise...", "I warn you...", "I order you..."
- They are the layer at which **illocutionary force** operates -- the same propositional content can carry different forces

**Same content, different illocutionary force:**
- "You will leave this room." (assertion/prediction)
- "Will you leave this room?" (question)
- "Leave this room!" (order)
- "I advise you to leave this room." (advice)
- "I promise you will leave this room." (threat/promise)

All five utterances share the same **propositional content** (the addressee's departure from the room) but carry entirely different **illocutionary forces**.

### 3.4 The Perlocutionary Act (The Act By Saying Something)

The perlocutionary act is the **effect** the utterance actually produces on the audience's feelings, thoughts, or actions. Unlike illocutionary acts, perlocutionary effects are:
- **Not conventional**: they depend on the specific circumstances, the audience's psychology, the relationship between speaker and hearer
- **Not fully under the speaker's control**: you can *try* to persuade someone, but you cannot guarantee persuasion
- **Not performable by formula**: you cannot successfully say "I hereby persuade you" or "I hereby intimidate you" -- the effect must actually occur in the hearer

Examples of perlocutionary effects: **persuading, convincing, scaring, frightening, amusing, inspiring, annoying, surprising, deceiving, embarrassing, intimidating, comforting, offending, boring, confusing**.

### 3.5 Worked Examples: All Three Layers in the Same Utterance

#### Example 1: "There is a bull in the field."

| Layer | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| **Locutionary** | The speaker produced a grammatical English sentence referring to a bull and predicating its presence in a particular field. |
| **Illocutionary** | The speaker performed the act of **warning** the hearer about a danger. |
| **Perlocutionary** | The hearer was **alarmed** and decided not to enter the field. |

#### Example 2: "I'll be there at five o'clock."

| Layer | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| **Locutionary** | The speaker produced a meaningful English sentence about arriving at a location at a specified time. |
| **Illocutionary** | Depending on context, the speaker performed the act of **promising** (committing to be there), **predicting** (stating an expectation), or **informing** (sharing a plan). The force depends on context. |
| **Perlocutionary** | The hearer was **reassured** and made plans accordingly, or the hearer was **satisfied** that the arrangement was confirmed. |

#### Example 3: "It's cold in here."

| Layer | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| **Locutionary** | The speaker produced a sentence describing the temperature of the current location. |
| **Illocutionary** | The speaker performed the act of **requesting** (that someone close the window or turn up the heat), or **complaining**, or merely **asserting** a fact. The illocutionary force is underdetermined by the words alone. |
| **Perlocutionary** | The hearer **closed the window** (or felt guilty for not doing so, or ignored the remark). |

#### Example 4: "Can you pass the salt?"

| Layer | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| **Locutionary** | The speaker produced an English interrogative sentence about the hearer's ability to pass a condiment. |
| **Illocutionary** | The speaker performed the act of **requesting** -- not literally asking about ability. |
| **Perlocutionary** | The hearer **passed the salt**. |

#### Example 5: "I promise I'll pay you back on Friday."

| Layer | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| **Locutionary** | The speaker produced a meaningful sentence about a future repayment event. |
| **Illocutionary** | The speaker performed the act of **promising** -- undertaking an obligation. This is an explicit performative; the word "promise" names the illocutionary force. |
| **Perlocutionary** | The hearer was **reassured** about receiving the money, or perhaps **skeptical** based on past experience (perlocutionary effects are not guaranteed). |

### 3.6 The Critical Distinction: Illocutionary vs. Perlocutionary

This is one of the trickiest but most important distinctions in pragmatics:

- **Illocutionary acts are conventional and successful upon utterance** (given proper conditions). If I say "I promise to pay you back" and the conditions are right, I *have* promised -- whether or not you believe me.
- **Perlocutionary acts require an actual effect on the hearer** and are not guaranteed. I can *try* to persuade you, but if you remain unpersuaded, no perlocutionary act of persuasion has occurred.

Test: Can you say "I hereby [verb]" and have the act succeed?
- "I hereby promise..." -- YES. The promise is made. (Illocutionary)
- "I hereby warn you..." -- YES. The warning is issued. (Illocutionary)
- "I hereby persuade you..." -- NO. Persuasion requires the hearer's actual change of mind. (Perlocutionary)
- "I hereby intimidate you..." -- NO. Intimidation requires the hearer to actually feel intimidated. (Perlocutionary)

## 4. Explicit vs. Implicit (Primary) Performatives

### 4.1 Explicit Performatives

An explicit performative is an utterance that **names the illocutionary act it performs** using a **performative verb** in the first person singular, present tense, indicative mood, active voice. The word **"hereby"** can typically be inserted before the performative verb as a diagnostic test.

Examples:
- "I **promise** to return the book tomorrow." (= "I **hereby promise** to return the book tomorrow.")
- "I **apologize** for being late." (= "I **hereby apologize** for being late.")
- "I **order** you to leave the premises." (= "I **hereby order** you to leave the premises.")
- "I **declare** this meeting adjourned." (= "I **hereby declare** this meeting adjourned.")
- "I **name** this ship the *Adventurer*." (= "I **hereby name** this ship the *Adventurer*.")
- "I **warn** you that the road is flooded." (= "I **hereby warn** you that the road is flooded.")
- "I **bet** you five dollars it will rain." (= "I **hereby bet** you five dollars it will rain.")
- "I **sentence** you to ten years' imprisonment."
- "I **pronounce** you husband and wife."

The performative verb makes the illocutionary force **unambiguous**. There is no question about what act the speaker is performing.

### 4.2 Implicit (Primary) Performatives

An implicit performative (Austin also called these **primary performatives**) performs the same illocutionary act but **without** using a performative verb. The illocutionary force must be inferred from context, tone of voice, grammatical form, and social situation.

| Explicit Performative | Implicit (Primary) Performative |
|-----------------------|-------------------------------|
| "I order you to shut the door." | "Shut the door!" |
| "I promise I'll be there." | "I'll be there." |
| "I warn you that the dog bites." | "The dog bites." / "Careful -- the dog bites." |
| "I request that you pass the salt." | "Could you pass the salt?" |
| "I advise you to see a doctor." | "You should see a doctor." |
| "I apologize for stepping on your foot." | "Sorry about your foot." |
| "I declare that the meeting is over." | "The meeting is over." |

### 4.3 Differences Between Explicit and Implicit Performatives

The explicit and implicit forms are **not socially equivalent**, even when they perform the same illocutionary act:

1. **Authority and formality**: Explicit performatives sound more **formal, authoritative, and institutional**. Saying "I order you to leave" implies a relationship of power that "Get out!" does not necessarily carry.

2. **Social weight**: Uttering an explicit performative version of a command has **much more serious social impact** than the implicit version. People therefore often **avoid** explicit performatives in everyday conversation because they can imply an unequal power relationship or assert particular rights over the hearer.

3. **Deniability**: Implicit performatives offer the speaker more **plausible deniability**. If you say "It's getting late" (implicitly requesting that guests leave), and the guests take offense, you can claim you were merely commenting on the time. If you say "I request that you leave my house," there is no such ambiguity to retreat behind.

4. **Historical development**: Austin hypothesized that explicit performatives represent a later, more sophisticated development in the history of language. Primitive communication would have relied entirely on implicit performatives -- tone of voice, facial expression, and situational context to convey illocutionary force. The evolution of explicit performative verbs represents language becoming more precise about what actions speakers intend to perform.

### 4.4 The "Hereby" Test

Austin proposed the **"hereby" insertion test** as a diagnostic for identifying explicit performative utterances. If inserting "hereby" before the verb produces a felicitous utterance that preserves the performative character, the original is likely an explicit performative:

- "I **hereby promise** to pay you back." -- Felicitous. This is a performative.
- "I **hereby run** down the street." -- Infelicitous. Running is a physical action, not a speech act.
- "I **hereby know** the answer." -- Infelicitous. Knowing is a cognitive state, not something enacted by declaration.
- "I **hereby believe** you." -- Infelicitous. Belief is not under performative control.

## 5. Felicity Conditions: What Must Be True for a Speech Act to Succeed

### 5.1 Austin's Original Formulation

Austin introduced the term **"felicity conditions"** (also called "happiness conditions") to describe the conditions that must be met for a performative utterance to succeed -- to be "happy" or "felicitous." If any condition is not met, the utterance is **"infelicitous"** or **"unhappy."**

Austin formulated these conditions in *How to Do Things with Words* (pp. 14-15) as a set of rules:

#### A-Conditions (Conventional Procedure)
- **A.1**: There must exist an **accepted conventional procedure** having a certain conventional effect, that procedure to include the uttering of certain words by certain persons in certain circumstances.
- **A.2**: The **particular persons and circumstances** in a given case must be **appropriate** for the invocation of the particular procedure invoked.

#### B-Conditions (Proper Execution)
- **B.1**: The procedure must be executed by all participants **correctly**.
- **B.2**: The procedure must be executed by all participants **completely**.

#### Gamma-Conditions (Sincerity and Follow-Through)
- **Gamma.1**: Where the procedure requires certain **thoughts, feelings, or intentions**, the participants must actually **have** them.
- **Gamma.2**: Where subsequent **conduct** is specified, the participants must **so conduct themselves** afterward.

### 5.2 Searle's Refinement: Four Types of Felicity Conditions

John Searle, Austin's most important successor, reorganized and refined the felicity conditions into a clearer four-part taxonomy. While Searle's system is technically his own contribution, it builds directly on Austin's framework and is now the standard way felicity conditions are presented. For each type of illocutionary act, Searle distinguishes:

#### 1. Propositional Content Condition
Restricts **what can be said** -- the semantic content that is appropriate for this type of speech act.

| Speech Act | Propositional Content Condition |
|-----------|-------------------------------|
| Promise | Must refer to a **future act by the speaker** (you cannot promise something already done; you cannot promise someone else's action) |
| Warning | Must refer to a **future event or state** that is not in the hearer's interest |
| Apology | Must refer to a **past act done by the speaker** |
| Request | Must refer to a **future act by the hearer** |
| Congratulation | Must refer to an **event or accomplishment related to the hearer** |

#### 2. Preparatory Conditions
Describe the **background circumstances** that must hold for the speech act to make sense. These concern the relationship between speaker, hearer, and situation.

| Speech Act | Preparatory Conditions |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Promise | The hearer would **prefer** the speaker's doing the act to not doing it; it is **not obvious** that the speaker would do it in the normal course of events anyway |
| Warning | The speaker has **reason to believe** the event will occur and that it is **not in the hearer's interest** |
| Order/Command | The speaker must have **authority** over the hearer |
| Question | The speaker **does not already know** the answer |
| Apology | The speaker believes the act **warrants** an apology (the speaker did something the hearer might consider harmful or wrong) |

#### 3. Sincerity Condition
Requires the speaker to have the **psychological state** that matches the speech act -- the appropriate thoughts, feelings, or intentions.

| Speech Act | Sincerity Condition |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Promise | The speaker **intends** to carry out the promised act |
| Assertion | The speaker **believes** the proposition to be true |
| Apology | The speaker **feels regret** for the act |
| Warning | The speaker **believes** the event will be harmful to the hearer |
| Request | The speaker **wants** the hearer to do the act |
| Congratulation | The speaker is **pleased** about the hearer's accomplishment |
| Thank | The speaker **feels grateful** for the act |

#### 4. Essential Condition
Defines what the utterance **counts as** -- the illocutionary point of the act. This is the condition that captures the essential nature of the speech act.

| Speech Act | Essential Condition |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Promise | Counts as the speaker **undertaking an obligation** to perform the act |
| Assertion | Counts as the speaker **committing to the truth** of the proposition |
| Request | Counts as an **attempt to get the hearer to do** the act |
| Apology | Counts as an **expression of regret** for the act |
| Order | Counts as an **attempt to get the hearer to do** the act, backed by authority |
| Declaration | Counts as **bringing about the state of affairs** named in the proposition |

### 5.3 Complete Worked Example: Felicity Conditions for a Promise

**Utterance**: "I promise I'll pick you up from the airport on Tuesday."

| Condition Type | What Must Hold | How It Could Be Violated |
|---------------|---------------|------------------------|
| **Propositional content** | The content refers to a future act (picking up) by the speaker | Violated if: "I promise I picked you up yesterday" (past act) or "I promise John will pick you up" (another person's act) |
| **Preparatory** | The hearer would prefer being picked up to not being picked up; it is not obvious the speaker would do it anyway | Violated if: the hearer has explicitly said they do not want to be picked up, or the speaker always picks the hearer up anyway (making the promise vacuous) |
| **Sincerity** | The speaker genuinely intends to pick the hearer up | Violated if: the speaker has no intention of showing up and is making an empty promise |
| **Essential** | The utterance counts as the speaker undertaking an obligation to pick the hearer up | This is what makes the utterance a promise rather than a prediction or expression of intent |

### 5.4 Complete Worked Example: Felicity Conditions for an Apology

**Utterance**: "I apologize for forgetting your birthday."

| Condition Type | What Must Hold | How It Could Be Violated |
|---------------|---------------|------------------------|
| **Propositional content** | Refers to a past act by the speaker (forgetting the birthday) | Violated if: the speaker is apologizing for something they didn't do, or for a future event |
| **Preparatory** | The speaker believes the act (forgetting) warrants an apology -- that it caused harm or offense to the hearer | Violated if: the speaker doesn't think forgetting was wrong (e.g., they don't consider birthdays important and are only saying the words under social pressure) |
| **Sincerity** | The speaker feels genuine regret about forgetting | Violated if: the speaker feels no regret and is apologizing insincerely to manage social appearances |
| **Essential** | The utterance counts as an expression of regret for the act | This is what makes it an apology rather than an acknowledgment or excuse |

### 5.5 What Happens Socially When Felicity Conditions Are Violated

The social consequences of violating felicity conditions differ dramatically depending on **which type** of condition is violated:

**When A-conditions (conventional procedure) are violated -- the act simply does not happen:**
- A random person on the street says "I pronounce you husband and wife" to a passing couple. **Nothing happens.** No marriage is contracted. People may be confused or amused.
- A child says to their parent's colleague, "You're fired!" **Nothing happens.** The person is not fired. The utterance is treated as a child's game or imitation, not a genuine speech act.

**When B-conditions (proper execution) are violated -- the act is defective or incomplete:**
- A judge in a trial says "I find the defendant..." but is interrupted before saying "guilty" or "not guilty." **The verdict was not delivered.** The procedure is incomplete.
- A couple goes through a wedding ceremony but the officiant forgets to have them sign the marriage certificate. **The marriage may be legally invalid** depending on jurisdiction.

**When Gamma-conditions (sincerity/follow-through) are violated -- the act goes through but is "hollow":**
- Someone says "I promise to help you move on Saturday" with no intention of showing up. **The promise is made** -- the speaker is now socially accountable for it -- but it is an **insincere promise**. If discovered, this damages trust and the speaker's social reputation.
- Someone says "I apologize for what I said" without feeling any regret. **The apology is issued** but is **hollow**. The hearer may sense the insincerity, leading to further social damage rather than repair.

**Key insight for pragmatic understanding:** Misfires (A and B violations) mean the social act **never existed**. Abuses (Gamma violations) mean the social act **did happen** but the speaker's inner state was misaligned with it. The social consequences are completely different: misfires produce confusion or amusement; abuses produce feelings of betrayal, deception, and broken trust.

## 6. Misfires and Abuses: Austin's Taxonomy of How Speech Acts Fail

### 6.1 The Master Classification

Austin developed a detailed taxonomy of **infelicities** -- ways that speech acts can go wrong. The taxonomy has a branching tree structure:

```
INFELICITIES (unhappy performatives)
|
|-- A. MISFIRES (act purported but void -- the act does not come off)
|   |
|   |-- A.1 MISINVOCATIONS (the procedure is wrongly invoked)
|   |   |-- Non-plays: No such conventional procedure exists at all
|   |   |-- Misapplications: The procedure exists but cannot be applied
|   |       to these persons or circumstances
|   |
|   |-- A.2 MISEXECUTIONS (the procedure is botched)
|       |-- Flaws: The procedure is executed incorrectly (errors)
|       |-- Hitches: The procedure is not completed (omissions)
|
|-- B. ABUSES (act professed but hollow -- the act goes through
        but is defective)
    |-- Insincerities: Required thoughts, feelings, or intentions
    |   are absent
    |-- Non-fulfillments (Infractions/Breaches): Required
        subsequent conduct does not follow
```

### 6.2 Misfires: The Act Does Not Come Off

In cases of misfire, the purported speech act **does not happen at all**. Although words are spoken, no marriage is contracted, no bet is laid, no christening succeeds, no verdict is delivered. The result is **null and void**.

#### A.1 Misinvocations

The conventional procedure is **wrongly invoked** -- either because no such procedure exists or because it cannot apply to the current situation.

##### Non-plays: No Such Procedure Exists
The speaker attempts to invoke a conventional procedure that simply **does not exist** in the relevant social or institutional framework.

*Examples:*
- In a culture with no concept of "dibs," someone shouts "I call dibs on that parking space!" The utterance performs nothing because the procedure has no conventional recognition in that context.
- Someone says "I hereby un-marry you" to their spouse. Divorce does not work this way; there is no conventional procedure of "un-marrying" by declaration in most legal systems.
- Someone picks up a rock and says, "I hereby baptize this rock in the name of the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit." There is no recognized conventional procedure for baptizing rocks.

##### Misapplications: Wrong Persons or Circumstances
The procedure exists but **does not apply** to the particular persons or circumstances at hand.

*Examples:*
- A random person on the street says to a couple, "I now pronounce you husband and wife." The procedure of marrying exists, but this person **lacks the authority** to perform it. No marriage occurs.
- Someone already married attempts to go through a second wedding ceremony (bigamy). The procedure exists but **cannot be invoked** because a precondition (being unmarried) is not met. Austin noted: "If we are not in a position to do the act because we are married already, then the act in question, marrying, is not successfully performed at all."
- A five-year-old child walks up to their parent's employee and says, "You're fired!" The procedure of dismissal exists, but the child has no standing to invoke it.
- Someone tries to bet on a horse race after the race has already been run. The procedure exists but the circumstantial precondition (the event being in the future) is not met.

#### A.2 Misexecutions

The right procedure is invoked by the right person in the right circumstances, but the execution goes wrong.

##### Flaws: Procedure Executed Incorrectly
The procedure is carried out but with **errors** in the execution.

*Examples:*
- A wedding officiant says, "Do you, Margaret, take this man to be your husband?" but the bride's name is actually Martha. The ceremony contains an error; depending on jurisdictional rules, this may invalidate the marriage.
- A judge says, "I find the defendant guilty of the charge of battery" when the actual charge was burglary. The verdict may be void because the wrong charge was named.
- Someone swearing an oath of office uses the wrong words or the wrong hand.

##### Hitches: Procedure Not Completed
The procedure is started but **never finished**.

*Examples:*
- A wedding ceremony proceeds normally, but the officiant collapses from illness before pronouncing the couple married. The marriage is not complete.
- A bet is proposed ("I bet you $20 the Lakers will win") but the other party never accepts. A bet requires two parties; without acceptance, no bet exists.
- A judge begins to deliver a sentence but is interrupted by a fire alarm and the courtroom is evacuated before the sentence is pronounced. No sentence was passed.
- In an auction, the auctioneer says "Sold to the woman in the blue hat for $500" but the woman shakes her head -- she had not actually bid. The sale did not complete.

### 6.3 Abuses: The Act Goes Through but Is Hollow

In cases of abuse, the speech act **is successfully performed** -- the conventional procedure is properly invoked and correctly executed. But the act is **defective** because the participant's internal states do not match what the procedure requires. The act is "professed but hollow."

This is a crucial distinction: with misfires, the act **fails to exist**. With abuses, the act **exists but is flawed**.

#### Insincerities: Required Thoughts or Feelings Are Absent

The speaker performs the act but does **not have** the psychological states (intentions, beliefs, feelings) that the act conventionally requires.

*Examples:*
- **Insincere promise**: "I promise I'll meet you for lunch tomorrow" -- but the speaker has **no intention** of keeping the promise. The promise is made (it exists as a social commitment), but it is an abuse of the promising procedure.
- **Insincere apology**: "I'm sorry for what I said" -- but the speaker feels **no regret** whatsoever and is apologizing only because social pressure demands it. The apology is issued, but it is hollow.
- **Insincere congratulation**: "Congratulations on your promotion!" -- but the speaker is actually **jealous and resentful**. The congratulation is performed, but the required feeling of pleasure at the hearer's success is absent.
- **Insincere welcome**: "Welcome to our home!" -- but the speaker wishes the guest had not come. The welcoming act occurs but is insincere.
- **Insincere bet**: "I bet you $100 it will rain" -- but the speaker has **no intention of paying** if they lose. The bet is made, but the commitment to honor it is absent.

#### Non-fulfillments (Infractions/Breaches): Required Follow-Through Does Not Occur

The speaker had (or seemed to have) the right intentions at the time of the speech act but **fails to follow through** with the conduct that the act requires.

*Examples:*
- A person sincerely promises to help their friend move on Saturday, but when Saturday comes, they sleep in and never show up. The promise was made sincerely (not an insincerity) but was **not fulfilled** (a breach).
- A country's representative signs a treaty committing to certain actions, then the country fails to carry them out. The treaty was entered into validly but was breached.
- A person declares "I swear to tell the truth, the whole truth, and nothing but the truth" and then proceeds to lie on the witness stand. The oath was taken but subsequently violated.

### 6.4 Social Consequences of Different Types of Infelicity

| Type of Failure | What Happened? | Social Consequence |
|----------------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Non-play** | Attempted an act with no recognized procedure | **Confusion**, bewilderment, social awkwardness, possibly humor |
| **Misapplication** | Right procedure, wrong person/circumstances | **Embarrassment**, possibly anger or indignation. "Who does she think she is?" |
| **Flaw** | Right procedure, executed with errors | **Uncertainty** about whether the act counts; may require repetition or legal clarification |
| **Hitch** | Right procedure, never completed | **Frustration**, need to start over or reschedule |
| **Insincerity** | Act performed, but speaker did not mean it | **Betrayal of trust** when discovered; damage to speaker's reputation and social relationships |
| **Non-fulfillment** | Act performed sincerely but not followed through | **Disappointment**, erosion of trust, potential conflict; social accountability ("But you promised!") |

## 7. Austin's Five Categories of Illocutionary Acts

In his final lecture (Lecture XII), Austin tentatively proposed a classification of illocutionary acts into **five categories**, using lists of explicit performative verbs as his starting point. Austin himself acknowledged this classification was preliminary and imperfect.

### 7.1 Verdictives
**Acts of giving a verdict, estimate, or appraisal.** These involve making a judgment about something, whether formally (as in legal proceedings) or informally.

*Examples*: acquitting, convicting, finding (as in "I find the defendant not guilty"), ruling, estimating, reckoning, appraising, assessing, diagnosing, grading, ranking, rating, classifying, describing, characterizing, valuing.

*Key feature*: The speaker renders a judgment that may be contested but that carries some authority or weight.

### 7.2 Exercitives
**Acts of exercising powers, rights, or influence.** These involve the speaker using some form of authority to bring about a state of affairs.

*Examples*: appointing, dismissing, excommunicating, nominating, ordering, commanding, sentencing, fining, granting, pardoning, resigning, vetoing, voting, declaring (war, an emergency), warning, advising, recommending, proclaiming, enacting, repealing.

*Key feature*: The speaker has (or claims to have) authority, and the act exercises that authority.

### 7.3 Commissives
**Acts that commit the speaker to a future course of action.** The speaker binds themselves to doing (or not doing) something.

*Examples*: promising, vowing, pledging, swearing, undertaking, committing, guaranteeing, betting, consenting, agreeing, opposing, refusing, offering, contracting, planning, intending (when stated).

*Key feature*: The speaker takes on an obligation or commitment.

### 7.4 Behabitives
**Acts concerned with attitudes and social behavior.** This was Austin's most problematic and heterogeneous category -- he himself called it a "shocker." These are reactions to other people's behavior, expressions of attitudes toward events, or acts of social ritual.

*Examples*: apologizing, thanking, congratulating, sympathizing, condoling, greeting, welcoming, toasting, cursing, challenging, daring, protesting, deploring, praising, complimenting, criticizing, blaming, blessing.

*Key feature*: The speaker expresses an attitude toward a situation, event, or person, often fulfilling a social expectation.

### 7.5 Expositives
**Acts that clarify how utterances fit into the flow of reasoning or conversation.** These are acts of exposition -- making plain how one's words relate to other words, arguments, or the conversational context.

*Examples*: stating, affirming, denying, asserting, describing, reporting, informing, testifying, admitting, conceding, illustrating, answering, replying, objecting, agreeing (with a claim), stipulating, defining, postulating, remarking, mentioning, beginning ("I begin by..."), turning to ("Let me now turn to...").

*Key feature*: The speaker positions their utterance within a larger discourse or line of reasoning.

### 7.6 Note on Austin's Classification

Austin himself was dissatisfied with this taxonomy and considered it tentative. He found the categories overlapping (many verbs seem to fit multiple categories) and the boundaries unclear. John Searle later proposed an alternative five-part taxonomy (Representatives/Assertives, Directives, Commissives, Expressives, Declarations) that is now more widely used. But Austin's original categories remain historically important and illuminate the sheer variety of things speakers do with words.

## 8. Key Terminology Reference

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Speech act** | An action performed by means of language (e.g., promising, warning, requesting) |
| **Locutionary act** | The act of producing a meaningful linguistic expression |
| **Illocutionary act** | The conventional action performed in saying something (the speech act proper) |
| **Illocutionary force** | The type of action an utterance performs (e.g., the force of a promise, a warning, a question) |
| **Perlocutionary act** | The actual effects of an utterance on the hearer |
| **Performative utterance** | An utterance that performs an action rather than describing one |
| **Constative utterance** | An utterance that describes a state of affairs (Austin's early term, later abandoned) |
| **Explicit performative** | A performative that names its illocutionary force using a performative verb ("I promise...") |
| **Implicit/primary performative** | A performative whose force must be inferred from context ("I'll be there.") |
| **Performative verb** | A verb that names an illocutionary act and can be used to perform it (promise, warn, order, etc.) |
| **Felicity conditions** | The conditions that must be met for a speech act to succeed |
| **Infelicity** | Any failure of felicity conditions -- an "unhappy" speech act |
| **Misfire** | An infelicity where the act does not come off at all (null and void) |
| **Abuse** | An infelicity where the act occurs but is hollow (insincere or not followed through) |
| **Misinvocation** | A misfire caused by invoking the wrong procedure or lacking authority |
| **Misexecution** | A misfire caused by errors or incompleteness in executing the procedure |
| **Flaw** | A misexecution involving errors in the procedure |
| **Hitch** | A misexecution involving failure to complete the procedure |
| **Insincerity** | An abuse where the speaker lacks the required thoughts, feelings, or intentions |
| **Non-fulfillment/Breach** | An abuse where the speaker fails to carry out required subsequent conduct |
