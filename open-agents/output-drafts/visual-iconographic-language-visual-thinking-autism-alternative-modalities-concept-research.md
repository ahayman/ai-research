# Concept Research: Visual Thinking, Autism, and Alternative Communication Modalities

## Overview

This category is the "why" behind the entire visual iconographic language project. Not "why is this intellectually interesting?" but "why does this matter for real human beings?" The answer lies in a fundamental mismatch: current communication systems overwhelmingly privilege the verbal-phonetic modality, while a significant population of human beings—particularly but not exclusively autistic individuals—process information most effectively through visual-spatial channels. This is not a deficit to be remediated. It is a cognitive architecture to be served.

Temple Grandin's taxonomy of thinking styles in autism—object visualizers (photorealistic mental imagery), spatial visualizers (pattern and system thinkers), and verbal thinkers—has been supported and refined by subsequent research. But the story goes deeper than Grandin's categories. Laurent Mottron's Enhanced Perceptual Functioning (EPF) model, the most empirically validated account of autistic cognition, characterizes autistic perception as genuinely enhanced, not disordered. Autistic individuals show stronger engagement of the visual system as a physiological feature of neural organization. They outperform neurotypical peers on tasks requiring abstract spatial reasoning (Raven's Progressive Matrices), local detail detection (Embedded Figures Test), and pattern recognition (Block Design). In Michelle Dawson and Mottron's landmark finding, autistic children scored 30–70 percentile points higher on Raven's Matrices than on the Wechsler Intelligence Scales—meaning their intelligence was systematically underestimated by verbal measures and better captured by visual-spatial ones.

The communication implications are profound. An estimated 25–30% of the autistic population is minimally verbal or nonspeaking. But even many verbal autistic individuals experience significant processing costs when using spoken language—difficulties that manifest not as inability to produce speech but as exhaustion, mismatch between thought complexity and verbal expression, and preference for written or visual communication. Research confirms a modality-specific effect: autistic individuals show superior performance on semantic tasks when matching pictures to words rather than words to words, suggesting that semantic processing itself is not impaired but that the verbal channel adds friction. Autistic participants' thought modality is more stable and less context-variable than non-autistic participants', suggesting a more consistent reliance on visual processing that does not shift as readily between modalities.

A language designed around image schemas and primary metaphors—the conceptual structures that precede and ground verbal language—could potentially offer a communication modality that works with rather than against visual-spatial cognition. This is not "accommodating a disability" but recognizing that the current communication infrastructure has a design flaw: it assumes everyone thinks in words.

## Core Components

### Component 1: Temple Grandin's Visual Thinking Taxonomy

- **Purpose**: Provides the first systematic account of different types of visual thinking in autism, distinguishing between object visualization and spatial visualization.
- **The Three Types**:
  1. **Object Visualizers (Photo-Realistic Thinkers)**: Think in vivid, detailed mental images. Grandin describes her own mind as "working like an Internet search engine set to access only images." Stored pictures serve as templates for how to act in new situations. Strong in: art, design, animal behavior, concrete problem-solving.
  2. **Spatial Visualizers (Pattern Thinkers)**: Think in abstract patterns, systems, and spatial relationships rather than photographic images. Strong in: mathematics, engineering, music, programming, systems thinking.
  3. **Verbal Thinkers**: Process primarily through words and language. Present in some autistic individuals, though less commonly discussed as an "autistic strength."
- **Mixed Profiles**: Most individuals have mixtures of these thinking styles, not pure types.
- **Research Support**: Studies on cognitive styles in autistic adults confirm the distinction between object and spatial visualization as meaningful dimensions, though the boundary between them is graded rather than categorical.
- **Relevance to Visual Language**: A visual language based on image schemas and spatial relationships would naturally serve spatial visualizers. Object visualizers might benefit from more pictorial/iconic elements. The design challenge is to accommodate both—perhaps through a system where base symbols are spatially/schematically organized but can include iconic detail for concrete referents.

### Component 2: Enhanced Perceptual Functioning (EPF) Model

- **Purpose**: Laurent Mottron's model, the most empirically validated account of autistic cognition, reframes autistic perception as enhanced rather than disordered.
- **Core Claims**:
  - Autistic individuals show enhanced low-level perceptual processing—superior detection of visual details, better discrimination of similar stimuli, faster visual search for targets.
  - This enhancement is physiologically grounded: stronger engagement of the visual system is a core feature of neural organization in autistic populations.
  - The EPF model is supported by meta-analysis across multiple perceptual domains (vision, audition, tactile).
  - Autistic perception is characterized by: locally oriented processing, greater perceptual discrimination, enhanced pattern detection, and potentially greater independence of perceptual processing from top-down conceptual influence.
- **Eight Principles of Autistic Perception** (Mottron, 2006):
  1. Enhanced perception of visual patterns and details
  2. Greater perceptual discrimination
  3. Locally oriented default processing
  4. Enhanced detection of first-order patterns
  5. Possible increased autonomy of perception from top-down influence
  6. Superiority on certain spatial tasks
  7. Enhanced auditory perceptual processing
  8. Possible enhanced tactile perceptual processing
- **Relevance to Visual Language**: If autistic perception is genuinely enhanced for visual patterns and spatial relationships, then a communication system built on visual patterns and spatial relationships is not merely "accessible"—it is aligned with cognitive strengths. The EPF model suggests that autistic individuals might process a well-designed visual symbol system more efficiently than neurotypical individuals do, not less.

### Component 3: The Raven's Matrices Finding and Spatial Reasoning Advantage

- **Purpose**: Demonstrates that autistic intelligence is systematically underestimated by verbal measures and better captured by visual-spatial reasoning tasks.
- **Key Findings**:
  - Dawson, Soulieres, Gernsbacher, and Mottron (2007): Autistic children's scores on Raven's Progressive Matrices were 30–70 percentile points higher than on the Wechsler Intelligence Scales.
  - Not a single autistic child scored in the "high intelligence" range of the Wechsler, yet fully one-third scored in the "high intelligence" range on Raven's.
  - One-third of autistic children scored in the "intellectually disabled" range on the Wechsler, but only 1 in 20 scored that low on Raven's.
  - Neuroimaging: For Raven's tasks, autistic individuals showed increased activity in extrastriate visual areas (BA18) and decreased activity in prefrontal cortex (BA9)—visual processing playing a more prominent role in reasoning.
  - Constructive Matching Strategy: Autistic individuals showed a stronger preference for constructive matching (building the answer from components) rather than response elimination.
- **Implications**:
  - Verbal-based assessments of autistic intelligence are systematically biased downward.
  - Visual-spatial reasoning is a genuine cognitive strength, not merely a "splinter skill."
  - Communication systems that rely on verbal-phonetic encoding may similarly underestimate what autistic individuals can express.
  - A visual-spatial communication system could reveal communicative competence that is hidden by the verbal channel.

### Component 4: Monotropism and Focused Attention

- **Purpose**: Describes the autistic attention profile—intense focus on specific stimuli—and its implications for learning and processing visual symbols.
- **Theory**: Monotropism (Murray, Lesser, and Lawson, 2005) proposes that autistic cognition is characterized by a tendency to focus attention intensely on a smaller number of interests or stimuli at any given time, rather than distributing attention broadly.
- **Characteristics**:
  - Information related to current focus is prioritized and stored more effectively in long-term memory.
  - High focus enables detailed pattern recognition and deep logical problem-solving within the area of interest.
  - Transitions between foci can be difficult—"attention tunneling."
  - The flip side of monotropism is that it enables extraordinary depth of knowledge and skill in areas of interest.
- **Relevance to Visual Language**:
  - Monotropic attention could be an advantage for learning a visual symbol system: deep, focused engagement with the symbol set during the learning phase.
  - Symbol design should support monotropic processing—each symbol should reward close inspection with meaningful detail, not punish deep focus with overwhelming complexity.
  - The attentional profile suggests that a visual language might be learned more effectively through interest-driven exploration than through distributed practice across all symbols simultaneously.

### Component 5: Neural Connectivity Differences

- **Purpose**: Describes the atypical neural wiring patterns associated with autism and their implications for visual processing.
- **Key Findings**:
  - Autistic brains show increased short-range connectivity within certain regions and decreased long-range connectivity between distant brain areas.
  - Enhanced visual processing ability is linked to alterations in wiring of the visual cortex—denser local connections may support superior local detail detection.
  - The pattern of connectivity may facilitate bottom-up, detail-driven processing at the expense of top-down, context-driven processing.
  - Visual cortex in autistic individuals may process information with greater fidelity and less top-down "smoothing."
- **Implications for Visual Language**:
  - Symbols with rich local detail may be processed particularly well by autistic users.
  - Compositional rules that are bottom-up (build meaning from visible components) rather than top-down (require context to disambiguate) may better align with autistic neural architecture.
  - The system should not require extensive context-dependent interpretation—each symbol and composition should carry its meaning as transparently as possible.

### Component 6: The Modality Mismatch Hypothesis

- **Purpose**: Frames the communication challenges of autistic individuals not as language deficits but as a mismatch between cognitive architecture and communication modality.
- **Evidence**:
  - Autistic individuals show better performance on semantic tasks when matching pictures to words (visual access) rather than words to words (verbal access). This suggests that semantic processing is intact but verbal access adds processing cost.
  - Visuospatial expression may represent a person's knowledge and skills more accurately than spoken language—suggesting that the communication modality, not the underlying cognition, is the bottleneck.
  - Autistic participants' thought modality is more stable and less variable across contexts—more consistently visual, less likely to shift to verbal mode even when tasks demand it.
  - Difficulties in translating mental images into words create a communication cost that may appear as "language difficulty" but is actually a modality translation cost.
- **The Hypothesis**: Many autistic individuals do not have a language deficit. They have a modality mismatch: their cognitive architecture processes and represents information primarily in visual-spatial format, but available communication systems require translation into verbal-phonetic format. The translation cost—from visual thought to verbal expression—creates the appearance of communication difficulty.
- **Implications**: A communication system that does not require this translation—one that allows visual-spatial thought to be expressed directly in visual-spatial format—could reduce or eliminate the modality translation cost. This is the fundamental rationale for a visual iconographic language.

### Component 7: Current Alternatives and the Gap

- **Purpose**: Maps the current communication options available to autistic individuals and identifies what is missing.
- **For Nonspeaking/Minimally Verbal Individuals**:
  - PECS, PCS-based AAC, Makaton, Blissymbolics—all described in Category 10.
  - These systems are augmentative (supplementing speech) or alternative (replacing it), but most are linguistically limited—they support basic requesting and labeling, not full expressive communication.
  - Spelling-based AAC (typing + speech synthesis) provides full expressiveness but requires literacy.
- **For Verbal Autistic Individuals**:
  - Visual supports (schedules, social stories, visual timers) help with daily functioning but are not communication languages.
  - Written language provides full expressiveness but still requires verbal-phonetic encoding (spelling represents speech sounds).
  - No existing system offers a non-phonetic, visual-spatial alternative for extended communicative expression.
- **The Gap**: Between the limited expressiveness of current AAC symbol systems and the full expressiveness of written language lies a vast space—the space for a visual communication modality that is:
  - More expressive than PECS/PCS/Makaton
  - Not dependent on phonetic encoding (unlike written language)
  - Designed for visual-spatial cognitive processing
  - Usable as a primary communication medium, not just a supplement

### Component 8: Neurodiversity-Affirming Design Principles

- **Purpose**: Establishes the ethical framework for designing a visual language for autistic users—centering agency, self-determination, and cognitive difference (not deficit).
- **Key Principles**:
  1. **Design for cognitive difference, not deficit**: The visual language is not "fixing" a communication problem but providing an alternative modality that aligns with visual-spatial cognition.
  2. **Center user agency and self-determination**: Autistic individuals should be co-designers, testers, and decision-makers in the language design process, not just recipients of a system designed "for" them.
  3. **Avoid the "cure" framing**: The language is not meant to replace speech or "normalize" autistic communication. It is an additional modality for those who find it useful.
  4. **Recognize existing competence**: Autistic individuals who struggle with verbal communication are not cognitively deficient—they may be using a suboptimal channel for their cognitive architecture. The visual language recognizes and serves their existing capabilities.
  5. **Affirm autistic communication styles**: Direct, pattern-based, systemized communication is not "wrong"—it may simply need a communication medium that matches its structure.
  6. **Broader applicability**: While designed with autistic visual-spatial thinkers in mind, the language should be usable by anyone—individuals with aphasia, multilingual contexts, anyone who processes information visually.
- **Research Support**: Recent frameworks for neurodiversity-affirming interventions emphasize empowering individuals to actively participate in decision-making and assert their preferences, with a focus on changing environments rather than changing people. Until recently, nearly no research sought input from autistic people in designing interventions or prioritizing goals.

## How It Works

### Step 1: Identify the Cognitive Profile

The user is an autistic individual (or any visual-spatial thinker) who:
- Processes information primarily through visual-spatial channels
- Experiences friction, fatigue, or reduced expressiveness when communicating verbally
- Demonstrates strong pattern recognition, spatial reasoning, and visual memory
- May or may not produce speech (the language serves both nonspeaking and verbal individuals)

### Step 2: Present the Visual Communication Modality

Rather than requiring translation from visual thought → verbal encoding → speech/writing:
- The visual language allows expression directly in visual-spatial format
- Image schema-based symbols map to the pre-linguistic conceptual structures the user already thinks in
- Spatial composition rules mirror the spatial reasoning the user already excels at
- The system reduces or eliminates the modality translation cost

### Step 3: Leverage Cognitive Strengths

The visual language is designed to align with autistic processing strengths:
- **Pattern regularity**: Consistent compositional rules that reward pattern detection
- **Local detail**: Symbols that carry meaningful information at the component level
- **Spatial relationships**: Grammar encoded through position, proximity, and enclosure
- **Systematic structure**: Rules that are logical and predictable, not arbitrary and exception-laden
- **Deep focus compatibility**: Symbol sets that reward sustained, focused engagement

### Step 4: Build Communicative Competence

Following principles from AAC research (especially PECS and Makaton):
- Start with core vocabulary (the most functional 200–400 concepts)
- Expand through interest-driven exploration (leveraging monotropism)
- Develop compositional skills gradually (from single symbols to compositions to extended expression)
- Maintain multimodal options (the visual language alongside speech, gesture, and written language—not replacing them)

### Diagram 6: Monotropism and Learning Design

- **Type**: Focus/funnel diagram
- **Purpose**: Shows how monotropic attention (deep focus on specific interests) can be leveraged for visual language learning, contrasting with distributed practice models.
- **Elements**:
  - Monotropic attention pattern: intense focus on narrow domain, deep learning within it
  - Distributed attention pattern: broad, shallow engagement across many domains
  - Learning pathway: interest-driven symbol acquisition → expanding through related symbols → building compositional skill within interest domain → generalizing
  - Symbol design features that reward deep focus: meaningful sub-components, pattern regularity, composable detail
- **Connections**:
  - Attention pattern → Learning strategy → Skill development
- **Notes**: Frame as designing with monotropism, not against it.

### Diagram 7: Neurodiversity-Affirming Design Process

- **Type**: Participatory design flow diagram
- **Purpose**: Maps the design process for a visual language that centers autistic agency—from co-design through testing to community governance.
- **Elements**:
  - Phases: Co-design (autistic designers as equal partners) → Prototype → User testing (with autistic testers) → Iterative refinement → Community governance
  - Principles at each phase: agency, self-determination, cognitive respect, avoiding cure framing
  - Feedback loops: user input shapes every phase
  - Broader applicability: aphasia, multilingual, visual thinkers generally
- **Connections**:
  - Phase → Phase with feedback loops
  - Principles → Design decisions
- **Notes**: Not an afterthought—participatory design is a requirement, not a nice-to-have.

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: The Raven's Test Reveals Hidden Competence

- **Scenario**: A 10-year-old autistic child is assessed using both the Wechsler Intelligence Scale for Children and Raven's Progressive Matrices.
- **Illustration**: On the Wechsler, the child scores in the "intellectually disabled" range—struggling with verbal comprehension, sequential working memory for digit sequences, and timed verbal responses. On Raven's, the same child scores in the "high intelligence" range—rapidly identifying abstract spatial patterns, completing matrix sequences, and demonstrating sophisticated logical reasoning.
- **Key Insight**: The child is not "low-functioning" or "intellectually disabled." The child is being measured with a tool that does not match their cognitive architecture. Change the measurement tool—change the modality—and competence becomes visible. A visual communication system could do for expression what Raven's does for assessment.

### Example 2: The Translation Cost in Daily Communication

- **Scenario**: An autistic adult who speaks fluently describes the experience of verbal communication.
- **Illustration**: "I can see the entire concept in my mind as a spatial structure—all the pieces, how they connect, the relationships between them. But to tell you about it, I have to disassemble that structure, serialize it into a linear sequence of words, find the right words for each piece, and speak them one at a time. By the time I have said the third sentence, I have forgotten what I was going to say in the seventh, because the verbal serialization process has consumed the working memory I need to hold the overall structure. The thought was complete and coherent in visual-spatial form; it only became fragmented when I had to push it through the verbal channel."
- **Key Insight**: This is not a language deficit. This is a bandwidth mismatch. The visual-spatial representation is rich and complete; the verbal channel is the bottleneck. A visual communication system would allow the spatial structure to be expressed as a spatial structure.

### Example 3: Interest-Driven Symbol Learning via Monotropism

- **Scenario**: An autistic teenager with intense interest in marine biology is learning a visual symbol system.
- **Illustration**: Rather than learning symbols in the order prescribed by a curriculum (greetings, daily routines, food vocabulary), the teenager begins with marine biology symbols—water, creature, deep, surface, predator, prey, ecosystem, adaptation. From this interest domain, they expand outward: "water" connects to "flow" and "container"; "predator" connects to "force" and "path"; "adaptation" connects to "change" and "time." The monotropic attention that drives deep learning within the interest domain naturally extends to foundational symbols through semantic connections.
- **Key Insight**: Monotropism is not an obstacle to learning—it is a learning strategy. A visual language should be designed to support interest-driven acquisition paths, not to require sequential mastery of an imposed curriculum.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: "Thinking in pictures" means all autistic people have photographic memory or think in literal photographs.
  - **Reality**: Grandin's taxonomy distinguishes object visualizers (photorealistic) from spatial visualizers (pattern/system thinkers). Many autistic individuals think in abstract spatial patterns, not concrete pictures. And many have mixed profiles.

- **Misconception**: Nonspeaking autistic people have nothing to say.
  - **Reality**: The inability to produce speech does not indicate absence of thought, emotion, or communicative intent. Many nonspeaking autistic individuals, when given appropriate communication tools (AAC, spelling boards, typing), demonstrate rich inner lives and sophisticated cognitive abilities.

- **Misconception**: Visual supports are "baby stuff"—appropriate for young children but not for adults.
  - **Reality**: Visual processing preferences are lifelong. Many autistic adults prefer visual information presentation, use visual organizational tools, and process written/visual information more efficiently than spoken instructions. The infantilization of visual communication tools is a design failure, not a reflection of user needs.

- **Misconception**: If an autistic person can speak, they don't need alternative communication modes.
  - **Reality**: Many verbal autistic individuals experience significant processing costs when using speech—fatigue, reduced expressiveness, difficulty translating complex spatial thoughts into linear verbal output. Access to a visual communication modality could be valuable for verbal individuals, not just nonspeaking ones.

- **Misconception**: A visual language designed "for autism" would be patronizing or limiting.
  - **Reality**: A well-designed visual-spatial communication system would be useful for anyone who processes information visually—including individuals with aphasia, multilingual contexts, and anyone who finds visual-spatial representation intuitive. Designing for autistic visual-spatial thinkers is designing for the most demanding use case, not the most limited one.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **Autistic verbal thinkers**: Not all autistic people are visual thinkers. Some process primarily through language. A visual language would be one option among several, not a mandate.
- **Acquired communication disorders**: Individuals who lose speech through stroke, TBI, or progressive conditions may benefit from a visual communication system even without pre-existing visual processing preferences. Their learning pathway would differ from lifelong visual thinkers.
- **Alexithymia and emotion vocabulary**: Many autistic individuals experience alexithymia (difficulty identifying and describing emotions verbally). A visual language with grounded emotion representations (image schema-based, like Blissymbolics' heart + direction) could provide alternative access to emotional vocabulary.
- **Intersecting disabilities**: Visual impairment, motor impairment, and intellectual disability can co-occur with autism. A visual language must be usable through multiple input methods (touch, gaze, switch) and potentially adaptable to tactile representation (as sign language adapts to tactile sign for deafblind users).
- **Cultural variation in visual processing**: While enhanced perceptual functioning appears to be a universal feature of autistic cognition, specific visual preferences and symbol interpretations may vary across cultures.

## Related Concepts

- **Cognitive Science of Visual Processing** (Category 9): The EPF model, Raven's Matrices findings, and dual coding theory provide the neuroscientific evidence base for this category's claims about visual-spatial cognitive advantages.
- **Existing AAC Systems** (Category 10): Current AAC systems serve some of the population described here, but with significant limitations in expressiveness and visual-spatial alignment.
- **Image Schemas as Visual Building Blocks** (Category 4): Image schemas are the theoretical foundation for why a visual language could work for visual-spatial thinkers—they are the pre-linguistic structures that ground visual thought.
- **Semantic Primitives / NSM** (Category 3): Core vocabulary research (200–400 essential words) aligns with Makaton's core vocabulary and provides the starting vocabulary for a visual language.
- **Requirements Engineering** (Category 12): The needs identified in this category—modality alignment, expressiveness, learnability, neurodiversity-affirming design—are direct inputs to the requirements analysis.
- **Compositionality** (Category 5): The compositional rules of the visual language must exploit visual-spatial processing strengths—spatial arrangement, pattern regularity, systematic structure.

## Research Gaps

- **Quantifying the modality mismatch**: No study has directly measured the "translation cost" of converting visual-spatial thought to verbal expression in autistic individuals. Indirect evidence (modality-specific performance differences) supports the hypothesis, but direct measurement is needed.
- **Visual language pilot studies**: No visual language based on image schemas and primary metaphors has been tested with autistic users. The closest data comes from Blissymbolics use in AAC, but Blissymbolics uses geometric primitives rather than experientially grounded image schemas.
- **Longitudinal effects**: How would extended use of a visual communication system affect cognitive development, literacy, and social communication in autistic individuals? No data exists because no such system has been available.
- **Spatial visualizer vs. object visualizer preferences**: Would the two types of visual thinkers prefer different features in a visual language? No study has tested this.
- **Autistic co-design research**: While neurodiversity-affirming design is increasingly discussed in the literature, concrete examples of autistic-led communication system design are extremely rare. Participatory design research specifically for visual language development is needed.
