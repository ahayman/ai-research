# Concept Research: Self-Led IFS, Journaling, and Adaptations for Externalized Processing

## Overview

Self-led Internal Family Systems work refers to the practice of using IFS principles and procedures outside of, or alongside, formal therapy — usually mediated by a book, workbook, app, or written exercise rather than a clinician's voice. The category exists because IFS has, more than most contemporary trauma therapies, produced a parallel self-help literature in which the founder (Richard Schwartz) explicitly invites readers to "do the work" on themselves. *No Bad Parts* (Sounds True, 2021) is the flagship; the *Internal Family Systems Workbook* (Schwartz, Sounds True 2023) is its companion; Jay Earley's *Self-Therapy* (multiple editions, 2009 onward) is the longest-running guided-self-led IFS manual; Bonnie Weiss's *Self-Therapy Workbook* (2013) is its exercise companion. A growing online layer — the IFS Journal (theifsjournal.com), Insight Timer's IFS guided meditations, the Sounds True online courses, the IFS Institute's Online Circle, blog-driven journaling guides like Propagate Hope's and Casually Luxe's — extends the model into mostly-written, mostly-asynchronous practice.

For a low-interoception adult autistic with complex trauma working with a therapist, this category is the most directly actionable in the deep dive. Two facts shape it. First, journaling-led parts work externalises the inner work onto a page that holds material outside the body — exactly the substitution channel Category 6 (alexithymia) argues is required when interoception is unreliable. Second, complex trauma is the population for which solo IFS carries the highest documented risks (Schwartz himself warns of "premature unburdening"; Joanne Twombly's *Trauma and Dissociation Informed IFS* describes decompensation in dissociative-disorder clients; Frank Anderson notes the model's safeguards work only when the protector-first sequence is followed). The honest framing is therefore not "self-led vs. therapist-led" but "what survives translation into solo written practice without therapist support, given this profile."

This research distinguishes throughout among (a) what Schwartz and the IFS Institute teach as the canon for self-led work, (b) what specific exercises exist in the published self-led literature, (c) what risks the literature flags, and (d) what externalised/written adaptations are documented for low-interoception readers. It does not invent prompts; every concrete prompt cited has a published source.

## Core Components

### Component 1: The Self-Led IFS Book Layer

- **Purpose**: Books that explicitly position the reader as the practitioner, not the patient
- **Key titles**:
  - *No Bad Parts: Healing Trauma and Restoring Wholeness with the Internal Family Systems Model* (Schwartz, Sounds True, 2021). Chapter exercises include: Getting to Know a Protector, Mapping Your Parts, Unblending and Embodying, Daily IFS Meditation, Working with a Challenging Protector, Advanced Parts Mapping, Working with Triggers, Body Meditation. Mixes accessible explication of the model with experiential exercises. Foreword by Alanis Morissette in audiobook.
  - *The Internal Family Systems Workbook: A Guide to Discover Your Self and Heal Your Parts* (Richard Schwartz, Sounds True Inner Workbooks, 2024, ISBN 9781649633118). Solo-authored Schwartz workbook with **50+ practices, exercises, and meditations**, plus QR codes pointing to guided audio practices. Companion to *No Bad Parts*. Note on the brief: this is Schwartz-authored, not Schwartz/Sweezy co-authored, and the publisher is Sounds True, not PESI.
  - *You Are the One You've Been Waiting For: Applying Internal Family Systems to Intimate Relationships* (Schwartz). Original 2008 self-published edition; substantially revised and re-released by Sounds True in 2023 (ISBN 9781683643623). Couples-relationship-focused but contains substantial self-led parts-work content (the "courageous love" / "internal mate" framework).
  - *Self-Therapy: A Step-By-Step Guide to Creating Wholeness Using IFS* (Jay Earley). First edition 2009, second 2012, third 2022 (with Karen Donnely). Pre-dates *No Bad Parts* by a decade. Treats IFS as a complete self-help method; includes session transcripts, help sheets, structured exercises. Foreword by Schwartz himself in some editions.
  - *Self-Therapy Workbook: An Exercise Book for the IFS Process* (Bonnie J. Weiss LCSW, 2013). Companion exercise workbook to Earley's *Self-Therapy*; "designed for people using IFS to do personal work on themselves" with "written exercises that give readers a chance to process their experience and track their internal work" — explicitly journal-style parts work. Sold in the IFS Institute store.
  - *Self-Therapy for Your Inner Critic* (Weiss); *Activating Your Inner Champion* (Weiss); *Letting Go of Perfectionism* (Weiss). Topic-focused workbooks in the same lineage.
  - *Transcending Trauma: Healing Complex PTSD with Internal Family Systems* (Frank Anderson, PESI, 2021). Primarily a clinician-facing book but with substantial self-applicable material; Anderson's own meditations and worksheets are downloadable companion content.
  - *Internal Family Systems Skills Training Manual* (Anderson, Schwartz, Sweezy, PESI, 2017). Clinician training manual; commonly used by IFS-Informed practitioners outside the IFS Institute training pipeline.
  - *Embodying Autism: Wired to Feel — Autism as a Condition of Sensory Surplus* (Bergenfield, Sweezy, Malow, New Harbinger, 2026). The first IFS-grounded book written specifically for autistic readers, by an autistic IFS Level 3 practitioner co-authoring with Sweezy and a sleep-medicine MD. (Listings vary: New Harbinger uses *Wired to Feel*; some retailer listings front *Embodying Autism* — ISBN 9781648486357 in both cases.)
- **Inputs**: A reader willing to do exercises in writing
- **Outputs**: Structured self-led practice between sessions, or as primary practice
- **Relationships**: Books connect to apps and online courses as parallel/supplementary channels

### Component 3: Journaling-Led Parts Work — Specific Techniques

The published journaling-led IFS literature converges on a small set of repeatable structures. Each is documented in at least two independent sources.

- **Letter to a part**. Write directly to the part by name, in second person. The model template (per Casually Luxe and the IFS Journal): "Dear [part name], I'm noticing you today because…" continue with what the part has been doing, what you appreciate, what you'd like to ask. Schwartz uses this in *No Bad Parts* under "Working with a Challenging Protector."
- **Letter from a part to Self**. Reverse direction: write *as the part* speaking to the Self. Allow first-person voice of the part. Preserve any defensive, hostile, or scared tone; do not edit. Schwartz frames this as direct access in writing — the equivalent of the therapist asking the part to speak directly when in-sight access is unavailable.
- **Two-pass dialogue**. Sequence: (1) write as the part, uninterrupted, until it has said everything; (2) re-read what was written; (3) write back as Self, responding to what was said. The IFS Journal site's "rule that everyone gets to finish their sentence" is the protocol's explicit norm. The two-pass structure externalises the unblending/Self-energy move: pause and re-read forces the reader out of the part's stance into the observer position.
- **Three-pass dialogue (the unblending pass added)**. Insert a pass between the part's voice and the Self response: a pass where the writer writes about *how they feel toward what the part said*. This is the "Feel toward" 6-F move written down. If the answer is anything other than 8-Cs (curious, calm, compassionate, etc.), there is another part in the way; that part gets its own letter before continuing.
- **Parts mapping (Schwartz, *No Bad Parts*)**. Procedure: notice a part calling for attention; locate it in body or mind; on paper, draw or label the part. Hold the page at arm's length and ask: how does this part relate to other parts? Draw lines or circles to show alliances, polarisations, protections. Practice being an observer of the system. *No Bad Parts* includes both a basic and "Advanced Parts Mapping" version. Tim Fortescue's Insight Timer meditations guide the same process auditorily.
- **Burden inventory**. List for each part: what beliefs is it carrying ("I am a burden," "I am unsafe," "I have to be perfect"), what feelings ("terror," "shame," "helplessness"), what was the formative scene if known. The burden inventory is a Schwartz move from clinical IFS adapted to writing; Bonnie Weiss's *Self-Therapy Workbook* contains the most structured written burden-inventory exercises.
- **Polarisation tracking**. For each named part, identify its polarisation partner (the part that takes the opposite stance). E.g., perfectionist manager polarised with rebellious firefighter. Write what each says about the other. Earley's *Self-Therapy* includes a chapter on polarisations specifically; the Weiss workbook adds the structured version.
- **Trigger journal**. Longitudinal log: date, situation, which part was activated, what the part felt or said, how the situation resolved. *No Bad Parts* "Working with Triggers" exercise; Propagate Hope's clinical-techniques guide turns this into a multi-week structured tracking practice. This is the externalised data layer — exactly the modality the user describes as their best processing channel.
- **Daily IFS meditation as written micro-practice**. Schwartz's "Daily IFS Meditation" in *No Bad Parts* is auditory-led but commonly transcribed: a 5–10 minute morning practice asking each part if it needs anything today, written as a dialogue.
- **Inner Critic protocol** (Bonnie Weiss). The most-cited IFS application of journaling — *Self-Therapy for Your Inner Critic* and *The Illustrated Workbook for Self-Therapy for Your Inner Critic* provide a specific written protocol for inner-critic parts: identify, name, ask its concern, identify what exile it protects, dialogue with both.

### Component 4: Other Externalisation Techniques (Beyond Writing)

These appear in clinical practice writing and self-led books, named here so the reader knows the full menu.

- **Index cards**. One part per card; physically arrange the cards on a table to show relationships. Card moves represent shifts in the system. Used by autistic IFS practitioners (Bergenfield references variants of this) and in some IFS Institute training material.
- **Stones, figurines, sand tray**. Object-representation: a bowl of stones or small figures; choose one for each part; arrange on a board or tray. Originates partially in Jungian sand tray and family-systems Constellation work; adopted by IFS-Informed practitioners.
- **Drawings and visual mapping**. Schwartz's parts-mapping in *No Bad Parts* is fundamentally a drawing exercise; can be elaborated into formal portraits of each part, shapes, colours. Important note: drawing works for aphantasic readers because the visual happens on the page, not in the mind.
- **Spreadsheet/database structures**. Less canonical but well-suited to systems-thinker users: each part as a row; columns for role, age, primary feeling, what it protects against, polarisation partner, trigger pattern, current burden, target unburdening goal. Allows longitudinal data tracking and pattern recognition across months. No published authoritative source; this is an extrapolation from the trigger-journal idea.
- **Voice memos**. For users who process in spoken language better than written; record yourself speaking as a part, then listen back and respond as Self. The auditory parallel to the two-pass written method.
- **Multi-chair work** (Gestalt-derived). Sit in one chair as a part, move to another chair as Self, alternate. The IFS Institute teaches this in trainings as a body-anchored alternative to in-sight work; the self-led version is documented in *Self-Therapy* and the workbooks. Not strictly externalisation (the parts are still inside) but the chair-change forces a physical pattern interrupt that the body registers.

### Component 5: The IFS-Informed / IFS-Trained / IFS-Certified Distinction

The IFS Institute uses three labels for practitioners. The distinctions are official but readily blurred in the marketplace.

- **IFS-Informed**. The Institute's own definition (per "Learning Streams" page): "Those who take Learning Hub courses, attend annual conferences, or participate in workshops from IFS Trainers may represent this level of education by saying they are IFS Informed." The label covers anyone who uses an IFS lens without completing Level 1. The range is enormous: it includes practitioners who have done extensive Sounds True coursework, attended several IFS conferences, and completed the IFS Online Circle on one end, and on the other end a practitioner who has read *No Bad Parts* once and is using the language. The label does not certify clinical competence in IFS or any formal supervision.
- **IFS-Trained**. Awarded by the IFS Institute upon completion of Level 1 training: ~100 hours of cohort-based training, currently $3,990–$5,300, organised around a curriculum developed and gate-kept by the Institute. Three-level pathway exists: Level 1 (Foundations), Level 2 (Deepening — topic-specific advanced trainings), Level 3 (Mastery). Training does not include a clinical-competence examination; completion is mainly attendance-based and consultation-based with assistant trainers.
- **IFS-Certified**. A separate, additional process layered on top of training. Requires Level 1 (and Level 2 or 3 in some cases), supervised consultation hours, case presentations, formal evaluation by senior IFS Institute trainers, and is renewed every two years. Currently a separate $200 application fee plus $150 every two years for recertification. As of 2024–2025, certified practitioners are a fraction of trained practitioners.
- **Where the labels blur**: Psychology Today and most therapist directories accept self-reported IFS designation. A profile saying "IFS" or "IFS-Informed" tells the reader very little about actual training depth. The IFS Institute Provider Directory (ifs-institute.com/practitioners) lists only practitioners who have completed Institute training, which is the strongest filter. Asking a practitioner directly: (1) what level (Informed / Trained / Certified)? (2) when and through whom did you train? (3) are you in current consultation? (4) how do you adapt for [your profile]? — surfaces the actual depth.

### Component 6: When Self-Led IFS Is Appropriate vs. Risky

Drawn from Schwartz's own framing in *No Bad Parts* (which warns repeatedly against rushing protectors), Earley's *Self-Therapy* (which assumes peer or solo practice on relatively well-resourced clients), and the trauma-informed IFS literature (Twombly, Anderson, Fisher).

- **Appropriate territory for solo practice (per multiple sources)**:
  - Mild to moderate concerns; everyday-life manager parts (perfectionism, procrastination, inner critic)
  - Established Self-access most of the time; can step back from protectors at will
  - Stable life context: housing, employment, relationships are not in crisis
  - With-therapist supplementation: the solo work feeds session material rather than replacing the session
  - **Parts identification, naming, and mapping** — generally low-risk; produces useful data for therapy
  - **Trigger-tracking and longitudinal observation** — generally low-risk; externalised data
  - **Letters to and from protectors** — moderate-risk; usually fine, occasionally activates an exile underneath
  - **Daily-life unblending micro-practice** — generally low-risk; the "step back" move
- **Risky or contraindicated for solo practice**:
  - Active complex PTSD or severe dissociation; the trauma-and-dissociation-informed IFS literature is consistent that these populations need a clinician who can phase the work and titrate exposure
  - Severe ongoing dysregulation (active panic attacks, dissociative episodes, suicidal ideation)
  - Active eating-disorder or substance-use crisis (pre-stabilisation)
  - "Going to your most painful exile" without support — Schwartz himself names this as a frequent failure mode; *No Bad Parts* repeatedly warns readers to back off if a part feels too young, too overwhelmed, or asks them to slow down
  - Premature unburdening — releasing burdens before protectors consent, before witnessing is complete, or in the absence of Self-energy. This is the most-cited solo failure mode in the clinical literature.
  - Working with parts when not in Self — a key indicator: another part is doing the work and calling itself Self. The two-pass written method partly mitigates this (the re-read produces a step-back) but does not eliminate the risk
  - Rumination loops — same parts, same content, weeks running; the writing is not producing change, it is rehearsing the burden
- **The user's case is "alongside therapy"**. This is the most common modern configuration and is explicitly supported by Schwartz's work. What it looks like in practice: weekly therapy session as the primary container; between-session journaling as data-gathering and observation, not as deep exile work; bringing the journal (or its summary) into session when material has built up.

### Component 7: Why Writing-Led Parts Work May Fit Low-Interoception Readers

This is a structural argument, not a marketing claim. Pulled forward from Cat 6 (alexithymia) and made specific to the journaling/writing modality.

- **The page is an external surface**. It holds material outside the body, where it can be read again. For readers whose access to internal sensation is unreliable, the page substitutes for the felt-sense channel as a place to put parts material.
- **Writing forces a slower pace**. The 6-F protocol live runs at the speed of a session. Writing slows it. Each step takes as long as it takes. There is no clinician waiting; there is no felt-sense-snapping-shut.
- **Re-reading creates externalised step-back**. The unblending move in clinical IFS is internal: notice you are blended, separate. In writing, the equivalent is finishing a paragraph and re-reading it. The page does the unblending move *for* you. This is a real structural translation, not a workaround.
- **Multi-pass methods externalise the Self-energy move**. The three-pass dialogue (write-as-part, write-as-feel-toward, write-as-Self) externalises the diagnostic question "Am I in Self?" into a written check.
- **Patterns become visible over weeks**. Real-time experience cannot hold the longitudinal pattern. The journal can. For a systems-thinker who notices structure best in aggregated data, this is the channel.
- **Granularity-building works better in writing**. Lisa Feldman Barrett's emotional granularity argument: putting feelings into words trains the system. Writing is the granularity-training modality. Even the act of having to choose between "frustrated" and "resentful" does developmental work.
- **What is lost in the translation**:
  - **The unburdening event**. IFS frames unburdening as a somatic-experiential release with a felt shift. Writing can approximate it (a do-over scene written in detail) but the felt shift may not arrive. A reasonable position: writing-led parts work may produce a *different* therapeutic event than full IFS — possibly a slower one, possibly a less-bodily one, possibly equally lasting. Claim made honestly: this is a trade-off, not a shortfall.
  - **Corrective experience in imagination**. Schwartz's "do-over" and "retrieval" rituals (taking the exile out of the past scene; bringing it to a safe inner location) depend on visualisation-with-feel. Aphantasic readers and low-interoception readers can simulate these in writing, but the body-shift may not occur.
  - **In-session emotional release work**. Crying, shaking, somatic discharge — all of these happen in clinical sessions sometimes. They are unlikely in solo writing.
  - **Live witnessing**. The therapist as witness is a real ingredient; their presence is part of why exile material can be held without overwhelm. The page does not witness in the same way.

## How It Works

### Step 1: Choose your container

- Trigger: deciding to work between sessions
- Action: pick the primary modality (notebook, structured workbook, app, drawings, cards). One per session is enough.
- Result: a clear surface to hold the work

### Step 2: Identify which part is calling

- Trigger: a feeling, behaviour, or recurrent thought has surfaced
- Action: write the part's name (descriptive: "the part that wants to skip the call"). Note where, if anywhere, you feel it. If nowhere, write "no body location" — that is data.
- Result: the part is named on the page

### Step 3: Write as the part (uninterrupted)

- Trigger: the part has been named
- Action: speak in the part's voice. Don't edit. Let it be defensive, scared, hostile, or vague.
- Result: the part has had its first turn; you have its actual stance in writing

### Step 4: Re-read and check stance toward the part

- Trigger: the part has finished
- Action: re-read what was written. Notice your stance: curious? annoyed? scared? frozen? Note it on the page. If the answer is anything other than 8-Cs, name the second part that is reacting and give it its own letter.
- Result: either Self-energy is present and the work continues, or another part has been identified and gets its own brief turn first

### Step 5: Respond as Self

- Trigger: Self-energy is present (curious, calm, compassionate, etc.)
- Action: write back to the original part. Acknowledge what it said. Ask if there's more. Ask what it wants you to know.
- Result: a written exchange has happened; the relationship has been built one increment

### Step 6: Note and close

- Trigger: the part has been heard for one session
- Action: write a single closing line — what you noticed about the part, what it might need next time. Date the entry.
- Result: an externalised record that builds across weeks

### Step 7 (longitudinal): Bring patterns to therapy

- Trigger: enough entries have accumulated (commonly 1–2 weeks)
- Action: re-read recent entries. Note: which parts recur? what triggers them? where is movement happening, where is rumination? Bring the summary or the journal itself to a session.
- Result: the solo work feeds the therapeutic work; the therapist sees the externalised data the user has been gathering

  - User profile marker: complex trauma, low interoception, with therapist → middle/yellow zone, journaling-as-data into therapy

- **Notes**: Honest framing — the user is not in the green zone of pure solo practice; the user is in the alongside-therapy zone

### Diagram 5: A Weekly Structure Example

- **Type**: Weekly schedule / calendar diagram
- **Purpose**: Concrete example of journaling-with-therapy structure (not prescriptive)
- **Elements** (for illustration; user adapts):
  - **Mon**: brief check-in entry (5 min, what part is loud today)
  - **Tue**: longer letter to a manager part (15–25 min)
  - **Wed**: trigger log (5 min each evening across the week)
  - **Thu**: re-read recent entries; note patterns (10 min)
  - **Fri**: prep for therapy — pull 1–2 entries to bring (5–10 min)
  - **Sat**: therapy session (or whichever day)
  - **Sun**: post-session integration — write what arose; note follow-up parts (15 min)
- **Connections**: Days as a flow; therapy as pivot point; weekend as integration
- **Notes**: One concrete worked example; user can scale up or down

| 8 Cs | The qualities of Self: curiosity, calm, compassion, clarity, courage, confidence, creativity, connectedness | Self-energy markers |
| IFS-Informed | Any IFS exposure short of formal Level 1 training | IFS lens |
| IFS-Trained | Level 1 IFS Institute training completed | IFS Level 1 |
| IFS-Certified | Level 1 + supervised consultation + formal evaluation + biennial renewal | Certified IFS Therapist |
| IFS Provider Directory | The Institute's vetted listing (ifs-institute.com/practitioners) | IFS Directory |
| Aphantasia | Absence of voluntary mental imagery | Visualisation-absence |
| Externalisation | Placing parts on an outside surface (paper, cards, objects) | External representation, parts-on-the-page |

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: A first-letter exchange

- **Scenario**: A reader notices a procrastinating part has been loud all morning.
- **Letter to the part**: "Dear procrastinator, I'm noticing you've been keeping me away from the email all morning. I want to know what you're worried about."
- **Letter from the part**: "If you start the email you'll write the wrong thing and it'll be public and you'll have to fix it forever. Don't open it."
- **Stance check (re-read)**: "Curious about why this is so big. Slightly impatient — that's another part. Mostly curious. Self-energy is here-ish."
- **Self response**: "I hear you. The email feels really high-stakes. Can you tell me more about why? Is there a time this happened before?"
- **Note and close**: "The procrastinator is worried about a public mistake. Probably tied to [specific past event]. Bring up next session."

### Example 2: A parts-mapping page

- **Scenario**: After a difficult workday, the reader sits down to map.
- **Process**: On a sheet of paper, drawn in different colours: a circle labelled "Pleaser," a square labelled "Critic," a small triangle labelled "Tired Kid." Lines from Pleaser to Critic ("they argue at night"), from Critic to Tired Kid ("Critic blames Kid for being tired"), from Pleaser to Tired Kid ("Pleaser tries to hide Kid").
- **Insight**: The Pleaser and Critic are polarised; both protect the Tired Kid. Bring this map to therapy to discuss what the Tired Kid is carrying.

### Example 3: A trigger journal across two weeks

- **Scenario**: User logs each evening, three sentences max.
- **Sample entries**:
  - Mon: "Late email from manager. Pleaser took over. Re-wrote the reply five times."
  - Tue: "Sensory overload at the store. Numbing kicked in. Stayed numb until bedtime."
  - Wed: "Phone call I didn't want. Procrastinator delayed it. When I finally did it, fine."
  - …
- **Pattern review**: Pleaser activates around manager-figure; numbing activates around sensory load; procrastinator activates around uncertain phone calls. Three different protectors; three different triggers; useful map for the next session.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: "Self-led IFS means doing IFS alone instead of with a therapist."
  - **Reality**: For complex-trauma profiles, the strongly-recommended frame is "alongside therapy." Self-led practices that pair well with weekly therapy are the appropriate target; pure-solo unburdening is the high-risk move.
- **Misconception**: "If I journal correctly I'll get the same result as a session."
  - **Reality**: Writing-led parts work likely produces a different therapeutic event — possibly slower, possibly less bodily, possibly more durable as data — but not interchangeable with a session. The honest claim is "useful and complementary," not "equivalent."
- **Misconception**: "An IFS-Informed therapist is basically the same as an IFS-Trained therapist."
  - **Reality**: IFS-Informed ranges from "read the book" to "extensive coursework." Ask the practitioner directly about training depth.
- **Misconception**: "If I can't feel into my body, I can't do IFS."
  - **Reality**: Cognitive, written, and externalised access channels are documented; the model has more flexibility than its standard 6-F prompt implies. (Cat 6 establishes this in detail.)
- **Misconception**: "If a part feels too young or overwhelming I should push through."
  - **Reality**: Schwartz himself repeatedly warns to back off. *No Bad Parts* and Earley's *Self-Therapy* both treat "too much, too fast" as the signal to stop and bring the material to a clinician.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **Aphantasic readers**. Mental visualisation is unavailable. Drawing on paper substitutes; the visual happens on the page. Multi-chair work substitutes; the part has a literal location.
- **Readers with severe alexithymia + complex trauma + no current therapist**. The literature is consistent: this is not the right time for unsupported solo unburdening work. Parts naming, mapping, and trigger journaling are still useful and low-risk; the deeper work waits for a therapist.
- **Hypergraphic / very-prolific writers**. Writing more is not always doing more parts work. The signal is whether the journal is producing change or rehearsing the same content. Schwartz uses "rumination loop" for the latter.
- **Readers whose therapist is not IFS-Trained or IFS-Informed**. The user's solo IFS reading may diverge from the therapist's frame. Worth surfacing in session: "I've been doing some IFS-style journaling between sessions; here's what came up." The therapist's response will tell you whether to integrate or compartmentalise.

## Related Concepts

- **Pennebaker's expressive writing tradition**. Pennebaker and Beall (1986) and follow-ups; writing about traumatic events for 15–20 minutes/day across several days produces measurable health outcomes. Pasting IFS structure onto Pennebaker's evidence base is not formally tested but is plausible: the structured-dialogue methods may compound some of the same mechanisms (cognitive integration, narrative coherence) with the IFS-specific compassionate-stance ingredient.
- **Gendlin's Focusing tradition**. Independent self-led practice tradition that has wrestled longer with the felt-sense problem; some Focusing teachers explicitly position writing as a parallel-channel substitute. Cat 6 covers this in depth.
- **IFIO (Toni Herbine-Blank's couples-IFS)**. *You Are the One You've Been Waiting For* approaches IFS in a relationship context; IFIO is the formal sub-modality for couples. Mostly clinician-led but the book is self-led companion content.
- **Cat 6 (alexithymia)**: structural argument that externalised channels substitute for impaired interoception. Cat 10 is the practical application of that argument.
- **Cat 9 (failure modes)**: catalogue of what goes wrong; gates which solo work is safe.

## Research Gaps

- **No peer-reviewed empirical study of journaling-led IFS** specifically. The closest analog is Pennebaker's expressive-writing literature (which is rigorous but not IFS-structured) plus uncontrolled clinician case writing in IFS workbooks. The research base for "writing IFS works" is the writing-and-feelings literature plus IFS-internal practice writing — the formal cross.
- **No peer-reviewed study of self-led IFS in any population**, much less in complex trauma. The recommendations against solo unburdening rest on clinical experience and case-report consensus, not RCT evidence.
- **The IFS-Informed label has no quality floor**. The IFS Institute's own definition is the broadest possible. There is no published study of variability in actual practice depth among self-described IFS-Informed practitioners.
- **The IFS Journal app has no published independent evaluation**. It is a high-quality user-friendly product but the claims about parts-mapping efficacy are not empirically tested.
- **Aphantasia + IFS** is barely engaged in writing or empirically. Cat 7 noted the same gap.
- **The Bergenfield et al. *Wired to Feel / Embodying Autism* (2026)** book is fresh; the field has not yet metabolised it. Some retailer listings refer to it as *Embodying Autism* — same book, same authors, same publisher; the New Harbinger title is *Wired to Feel*.
- **What proportion of "IFS-Informed" therapists on Psychology Today have done substantive coursework vs. read one book** is unknown and not measurable from listings.
