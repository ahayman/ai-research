# Category 08: Deixis -- Research on Autism and Deictic Language

## Research Summary

This document covers the intersection of deixis (context-dependent reference) and autism through a double-empathy, non-deficit lens. Deixis is the family of linguistic devices whose reference shifts depending on who is speaking, where they are, when they are speaking, who they are addressing, and what part of the discourse they are pointing at. Every deictic expression -- "I," "you," "here," "there," "now," "then," "this argument," "that point," "sir," "ma'am" -- demands that interlocutors maintain a shared, constantly updating coordinate system.

The literature on autism and deixis has historically been narrow. It has focused almost exclusively on **person deixis** (pronoun reversal in young children) and has often framed autistic patterns as failures of perspective-taking. This document preserves the genuine empirical findings from that literature (Kanner's original observation, Tager-Flusberg's developmental trajectories, Mizuno et al.'s neuroimaging, Shield et al.'s sign-language evidence, Hobson et al.'s person-centred expressions study, Oakley & Vidanović's fluidity-to-inelasticity hypothesis) while reframing them along Milton's (2012) double-empathy lines: deictic coordination is a **bidirectional** problem, carried out at **different cognitive costs** by different minds, rather than a unilateral autistic deficit.

It also expands coverage to the four deictic domains that earlier reviews neglected: **spatial deixis**, **temporal deixis**, **social deixis** (honorifics, address terms, register-encoded hierarchy), and **discourse deixis** (anaphora, cataphora, reference to stretches of ongoing talk). Across all five domains, a consistent picture emerges. Autistic minds are not blind to deictic reference; they pay a higher **real-time processing cost** for the same deictic computation, they often develop **explicit compensations** (names over pronouns, dates over "tomorrow," landmarks over "there"), and those compensations turn out to be communicatively robust -- often more precise than neurotypical deictic shortcuts.

## 1. Pronoun Reversal: The Classic Finding, Reframed

### 1.1 Historical Context

Leo Kanner first documented pronoun reversal in his landmark 1943 paper describing 11 children with "autistic disturbances of affective contact." He observed that children would refer to themselves as "you" and to others as "I," and he initially explained this as echolalia: "Personal pronouns are repeated just as heard, with no change to suit the altered situation" (Kanner, 1943). This observation became one of the defining features associated with early autism descriptions, and it has cast a long shadow over how deixis in autism is framed.

Kanner's framing was that something was "missing" from the child's perspective-taking. The modern literature, taken as a whole, supports a different reading: pronoun reversal is **effortful deictic work made visible**. It is the sound of a young child doing the translation labour of turning heard speech into spoken self-reference, and leaving the seams showing.

**Sources:**
- [The Diagnosis of Autism: From Kanner to DSM-III to DSM-5 and Beyond (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8531066/)
- [Perspectives on Personal Pronoun Reversal in Children with ASD: A Critical Review](https://jeps.efpsa.org/articles/10.5334/jeps.br)

### 1.2 What Pronoun Reversal Looks Like

Pronoun reversal (also called pronominal reversal) occurs when a speaker uses "you" where they mean themselves, or "I" where they mean someone else. Common examples:

- A child says **"You want a cookie"** when they mean **"I want a cookie"**
- A child says **"Pick you up"** when they mean **"Pick me up"** (echoing what a caregiver says before lifting them)
- A child refers to themselves by name or as "he/she" rather than using "I/me"

That last pattern -- using proper names or third-person pronouns for oneself -- is sometimes called **pronoun avoidance**. It is related to but distinct from pronoun reversal, and it is important to note that it often **persists into adulthood as a working solution** rather than as a residual symptom. Many autistic adults prefer proper names over pronouns in writing and in speech because names remove ambiguity. That preference is not an unresolved childhood error. It is a communication strategy that happens to be more precise than the pronoun system it replaces.

**Sources:**
- [Teaching Pronouns to Kids with Autism & Avoiding Pronoun Reversal](https://marybarbera.com/teaching-pronouns-kids-with-autism-and-avoiding-pronoun-reversal/)
- [Pronoun Reversal and Confusion | Musings of an Aspie](https://musingsofanaspie.com/2014/03/14/pronoun-reversal-and-confusion/)

### 1.3 Prevalence: Less Common Than Kanner Implied

Modern research has substantially revised Kanner's impression that pronoun reversal is rampant in autism:

- Pronoun reversals occur in **less than 10% of the pronoun use** of young autistic children aged 2-5
- In the Naigles et al. (2016) corpus study, autistic children reversed pronouns in about **13% of all pronouns produced**
- Typically developing children reverse pronouns about **1% of the time** at similar ages
- Both groups produce reversals at low absolute rates -- the difference is of degree, not kind

Pronoun misuse in autism is **less common than Kanner's 1943 impression suggested**, but it is still more frequent in early childhood than in typical development, and it tends to persist longer before resolving.

**Sources:**
- ["You're telling me!" The Prevalence and Predictors of Pronoun Reversals in Children with ASD and Typical Development (Naigles et al. 2016, PMC4834724)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4834724/)
- [Questions for Letitia Naigles: Parsing pronoun confusion | The Transmitter](https://www.thetransmitter.org/spectrum/questions-for-letitia-naigles-parsing-pronoun-confusion/)

### 1.4 Developmental Trajectory and Resolution

- In typical development, most children consolidate personal pronouns between **ages 2.5 and 3**, coming to treat them as referential (shifting with the speaker) rather than absolute (fixed to a particular person)
- Barokova & Tager-Flusberg (2020) traced person reference longitudinally in autistic children and found a different developmental trajectory: autistic children showed slower change over age, and older autistic children in the sample were still producing more reversals than their typically developing peers
- Resolution timing is highly individual. For some autistic children the reversal phase ends around age 3; for others it extends into age 5 or beyond; some never reverse pronouns at all
- Many autistic adults retain a preference for proper names over pronouns. This is a **durable adaptation, not a residual symptom**.

The important point: **adult autistic people, as a general matter, do not reverse pronouns.** The pattern resolves. What may persist is a preference for more explicit reference, and that preference is communicatively sound.

**Sources:**
- [Person-Reference in Autism Spectrum Disorder: Developmental Trends (Barokova & Tager-Flusberg, 2020)](https://www.bu.edu/autism/files/2020/08/Barokova-Tager-Flusberg-Person-Reference-in-ASD-1.pdf)
- [Children with autism spectrum disorder show pronoun reversals in interpretation](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29528676/)

### 1.5 Theoretical Accounts, Reframed

Three main accounts attempt to explain pronoun reversal. All three, when read carefully, describe **cognitive load** rather than absence of perspective-taking.

#### A. The Echolalia / Gestalt Language Processing Account
- The child echoes heard speech without re-indexing the pronouns: a caregiver says "Do you want a cookie?" and the child repeats "You want a cookie" as a request
- This is closely linked to **gestalt language processing (GLP)**: gestalt processors acquire language in whole chunks rather than individual words, so the pronouns within an echoed phrase are carried along unchanged
- Under this reading, "pronoun reversal is not an error but rather a common characteristic of early stage gestalt language development"
- Reframed: the child is producing the **most faithful possible echo** of the input. They are doing more work than a child who replaces "you" with "I," not less, because they are preserving the exact surface form. The translation step is what has not yet been automated.

#### B. The Perspective-Shifting Account
- Pronouns are **inherently deictic**: "I" means "the person currently speaking" and "you" means "the person currently being addressed." These references flip with every conversational turn.
- Correctly producing "I" and "you" requires the speaker to perform a **deictic shift**: to identify the speaker role, map themselves into it, and index accordingly
- Research consistently finds that pronoun reversals are associated with the cost of this shift. Theory of Mind (ToM) performance, working memory, IQ, and verbal ability all predict pronoun use in autistic samples
- Reframed: this is a **processing-cost** finding, not a perspective-taking failure. Shifting deictic centres is cognitively demanding in any mind. Autistic minds appear to pay more of that cost in real time, especially at ages when the computation has not yet been automated.

#### C. The Self-Other Neural Account
- fMRI research by Mizuno et al. (2011) found **reduced functional connectivity** between the right anterior insula and the precuneus during deictic shifting in autistic adults
- The right anterior insula is involved in self-awareness and the precuneus in self-other representation
- The original paper framed this as "disordered neural representation of the self." It is more accurate to say: **these two regions talk to each other less tightly during the specific task of re-anchoring reference to self when prompted by someone else's "you."**
- The effect was specific to the "you" condition. Reference to someone else's view did not produce the same connectivity reduction. This is a task-specific load signature, not a global self-representation problem.

#### D. Multi-Factor Reality
Modern work recognises that multiple factors contribute. In the ASD group in Naigles et al. (2016), early language and joint attention scores independently predicted reversal frequency. Linguistic and social factors are both implicated, and reversals seem to surface when a child's language and social development are unfolding asynchronously -- one ahead of the other.

None of these accounts requires, or even supports, a "missing perspective-taking module" reading. They describe a cognitive operation with a cost, and developmental variation in when and how that cost gets absorbed.

### 1.6 The Sign Language Evidence: Specificity, Not Deficit

A particularly illuminating line of evidence comes from deaf autistic children using sign language. Shield, Meier & Tager-Flusberg (2015) studied native-signing autistic children whose input language was American Sign Language (ASL):

- In ASL, the pronouns corresponding to "I" and "you" are **literally pointing gestures** (point to self = "I", point to addressee = "you"). They are far more transparent than spoken pronouns.
- Despite this transparency, deaf autistic children showed **palm orientation reversals**: signs typically produced with the palm facing outward were produced with the palm facing the signer -- mirroring the handshape as the addressee would see it, not as the signer would produce it.
- Crucially, **true pronoun reversals (pointing to the wrong person) have not been documented** in deaf autistic children's signing. They point at who they mean.
- The pattern is therefore one of **surface-form mirroring**: the child reproduces the exact handshape they see, palm-facing-them, rather than performing the spatial transformation required to reproduce the sign from the signer's own perspective.
- This is strong evidence for a **specific perspective-shifting mechanism** being costly, rather than a global perspective-taking deficit. Deaf autistic children know who they mean; what they do is reproduce the visible surface of the input with maximum fidelity.

Shield and colleagues' follow-up fingerspelling work (PMC9614108) extended the finding: reversed palm orientation in fingerspelled letters parallels the pronoun-reversal pattern in hearing autistic children. Both phenomena plausibly share a single underlying pattern -- **gestalt reproduction of linguistic form without performing the deictic transformation** that would flip it for the addressee.

The sign-language evidence matters because it localises the phenomenon. It is not about self-knowledge, theory of mind, or social interest. It is about **the cost of transforming the form of a linguistic signal so that it is produced from the self's perspective rather than the addressee's.**

**Sources:**
- [Shield, Meier & Tager-Flusberg (2015) The Use of Sign Language Pronouns by Native-Signing Children with Autism (PMC4474758)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4474758/)
- [Are palm reversals the pronoun reversals of sign language? Evidence from a fingerspelling task (PMC9614108)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9614108/)
- [Sign language study solves autism's pronoun mystery | The Transmitter](https://www.thetransmitter.org/spectrum/sign-language-study-solves-autisms-pronoun-mystery/)

### 1.7 Is Pronoun Reversal Unique to Autism?

No. Pronoun reversal is not autism-specific:

- Typically developing children also produce pronoun reversals, particularly between ages 2 and 3
- Children with other language delays also show reversal patterns
- The difference in autism is one of **frequency, persistence, and the combination of reversal with pronoun avoidance** (using names instead of pronouns)
- Reversal is a **graded** phenomenon across child populations, not a categorical marker

**Sources:**
- [Naigles et al. (2016) "You're telling me!" (PMC4834724)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4834724/)
- [Individual differences in pronoun reversal: Evidence from two longitudinal case studies](https://www.mq.edu.au/__data/assets/pdf_file/0008/910088/Individual-differences-in-pronoun-reversal.pdf)

## 2. Deictic Shifting and Perspective-Taking

### 2.1 What Deictic Shifting Is

Every utterance is anchored to a **deictic centre** -- the speaker's "I-here-now." When someone else speaks, the entire deictic centre shifts: their "I" is now the reference point, their "here" is the spatial anchor, their "now" is the temporal anchor. This constant re-anchoring is called **deictic shifting**, and every conversational turn requires it.

Deictic shifting is cognitively demanding because it requires, simultaneously:

1. **Tracking who is currently speaking** (to interpret "I" and "you")
2. **Maintaining awareness of multiple spatial reference points** (to interpret "here" and "there")
3. **Updating temporal anchors** (to interpret "now" and "then")
4. **Suppressing the hearer's own default perspective** in order to adopt the speaker's

This is not a trivial operation for any mind. Neurotypical adults do it automatically because it has been heavily over-practised since infancy; the cost has been pushed out of conscious awareness. For many autistic people, more of the cost remains visible.

### 2.2 Mizuno et al. (2011): What the fMRI Actually Showed

The Mizuno et al. (2011) fMRI study (PMC3155703) remains the landmark neuroimaging investigation:

- Adults with autism and neurotypical controls performed a linguistic perspective-taking task requiring deictic shifting
- Autistic participants showed **slower and less accurate behavioural responses** on the task
- The key neural finding: **reduced functional connectivity between the right anterior insula and precuneus** during deictic shifting
- The reduction was specific to the "you" condition, where "you" referred to the participant and required mapping the external deictic term back onto self
- The right anterior insula supports attention to internal states; the precuneus supports self-other representation; their coordination appears to be part of the machinery of deictic shifting

The study concluded that "deictic shifting constitutes a challenge for adults with high-functioning autism, particularly when reference to one's self is involved." **A non-deficit reading of the same result:** the real-time coupling between self-monitoring regions and self-other mapping regions is less tight in autism during this specific operation, which is consistent with the behavioural picture of higher processing load. It is a load finding, not a capacity finding. Accuracy was lower, but it was not floor; autistic participants performed the task.

**Sources:**
- [Mizuno et al. (2011) (PMC3155703)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3155703/)
- [ScienceDaily summary of Mizuno et al. 2011](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2011/08/110801122956.htm)

### 2.3 Deictic Shifting in Narrative

Retelling a story from multiple characters' perspectives requires extensive deictic shifting -- the narrator has to adopt and release each character's "I-here-now." Research findings:

- Fictional narrative generation places heavy deictic demands because the narrator has to hold characters' motivations, outlooks, and spatial anchors in mind simultaneously
- Autistic narrators show lower **causal-motivational coherence** in life narratives, and this appears related to the cost of holding multiple shifting deictic centres rather than to indifference to the characters
- A pilot training study found that when autistic adults practised retelling stories from the original storyteller's perspective, attending explicitly to embedded thoughts and emotions, they showed significant improvements in mentalising about others' beliefs and in narrative coherence
- This is consistent with the broader reading: explicit, effortful practice can absorb some of the real-time deictic cost

**Sources:**
- [A narrative sequencing and mentalising training for adults with autism: A pilot study (PMC9433774)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9433774/)
- [Narrative identity differences in autism | Scientific Reports](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-00178-0)
- [Narrative production abilities of children with autism](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/23969415251321824)

### 2.4 Comprehension vs. Production

An important distinction emerges from the research:

- **Production** (generating the right deictic term in real time) tends to be more costly than **comprehension** (interpreting someone else's deictic term)
- Eye-tracking reveals processing differences even when behavioural accuracy matches: during pronoun resolution, autistic participants often fixate on the subject referent more than neurotypical participants, suggesting a different but successful search strategy
- The conclusion: autistic participants generally interpret pronouns as accurately as neurotypical peers; divergences tend to show up in **how** the reference is computed, and in the **timing** of when the answer becomes available
- Many autistic individuals comprehend deictic terms well but find the **rapid online production** demands of conversational deictic use much more expensive

**Sources:**
- [Structural and Contextual Cues in Third-person Pronoun Interpretation by Children with ASD (PMC7878583)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7878583/)
- [Reference Production in Young Speakers with and without Autism (PMC3668432)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3668432/)

### 2.5 The Theory of Mind Connection

Deictic processing and Theory of Mind are intertwined but not identical:

- Deictic frames "have been found to underlie complex behavior, including perspective-taking"
- False-belief task performance is predicted heavily by vocabulary and grammar, suggesting that ToM tasks partly index language ability
- However, training on deictic relations (I-you, here-there, now-then) using the Barnes-Holmes protocol **does not automatically transfer** to broader measures of perspective-taking or ToM in autistic participants
- This suggests that deictic processing and ToM share mechanisms but are not equivalent. Fluent deictic use may be a **component** of perspective-taking rather than a **proxy** for it.

**Sources:**
- [Deictic Relational Responding and Perspective-Taking in Autistic Individuals: A Scoping Review (PMC11035524)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11035524/)
- [Examining the Impact of Deictic Relational Responding on Advanced Theory of Mind and Pretense in Children with Autism](https://digitalcommons.usf.edu/etd/6185/)

## 3. Spatial Deixis in Autism

### 3.1 Here/There, This/That, and the Body as Origin

Spatial deixis covers the words that anchor reference to the speaker's physical position: **here/there, this/that, come/go, left/right (of me), in front of/behind (me)**. All of these assume that speaker and hearer can jointly recover where the speaker's body currently is.

The most direct investigation of spatial deixis in autism remains Hobson, García-Pérez & Lee (2010), whose two-part study set out the pattern that later work has largely confirmed.

**Study 1 (Production):**
- Most autistic children spontaneously produced deictic terms, often with pointing
- A subset of autistic children in the sample referred to **distal locations using proximal terms** ("this" or "here" for things that were far away) -- a pattern not seen in the comparison group
- Autistic children also made **unusually precise points**, sometimes lining the finger up with one eye, suggesting attention to the act of pointing itself rather than to its function as a direction-of-gaze signal for a partner

**Study 2 (Comprehension):**
- Autistic children were less accurate in responding to **contrastive** deictic instructions ("put it here, not there"), where the contrast is the whole point of the utterance
- Fewer autistic participants responded accurately to **indicative head-nods** used as deictic gestures

These findings are often summarised as "difficulty with spatial deixis." A more precise reading: **the speaker's body is a less default anchor** for some autistic children. Spatial deixis works by treating the speaker's body as the origin of a coordinate system ("here" = at me, "there" = away from me, "come" = toward me, "go" = away from me). If the child is attending more to the object in the world than to the partner's body-as-origin, proximal/distal terms will get mapped onto the object's salience rather than onto distance-from-speaker. That is a different attentional setting, not a perspective-taking failure.

**Sources:**
- [Hobson, García-Pérez & Lee (2010) Person-Centred (Deictic) Expressions and Autism | Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10803-009-0882-5)
- [Deictic Terms | Springer Nature Link](https://link.springer.com/rwe/10.1007/978-3-319-91280-6_966)

### 3.2 Deictic Verbs: Come and Go

"Come" and "go" are deictic verbs. "Come" points movement toward the speaker's location; "go" points it away. The reference shifts with the speaker's position, which means the listener has to track not just what moved, but which origin it moved with respect to.

- Autistic children often find the **come/go** contrast costly in both comprehension and production, particularly when the speaker and hearer are at different physical locations
- Intervention work in Japanese-speaking autistic children showed that using **multiple spatial configurations** (face-to-face vs. side-by-side) together with deictic gestures improved appropriate use of these verbs in training and generalised to daily life
- The therapeutic ingredient was **making the two origins concretely visible** rather than assumed. Once the child could see both bodies as candidate anchors, the come/go distinction followed.

**Sources:**
- [Improving the Use of Deictic Verbs in Children with Autism Spectrum Disorder](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/17518423.2021.1964004)

### 3.3 Pointing and Deictic Gesture

Pointing is the most fundamental deictic gesture, and differences in pointing appear early in autistic development.

- Autistic children often produce fewer pointing gestures overall and fewer index-finger points without contact with the referent
- They respond less reliably to pointing when it is the only cue available, and often discriminate intentional pointing from incidental gestures less sharply
- When multiple cues are combined (looking **and** pointing, or looking **and** naming), autistic children respond more reliably. **Redundant cues help.** This is not a workaround for a deficit; it is how any communication system should work when the signal is noisy.
- In practice, most autistic communicators settle into a preference for **explicit, redundant reference** (naming + locating + sometimes gesturing), which is communicatively more robust than a bare point.

**Sources:**
- [Different Ways of Making a Point: A Study of Gestural Communication in Typical and Atypical Early Development (PMC8246876)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8246876/)
- [A scoping review of deictic gesture use in toddlers with or at-risk for autism spectrum disorder](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/322908243_A_scoping_review_of_deictic_gesture_use_in_toddlers_with_or_at-risk_for_autism_spectrum_disorder)
- [Attention to intentional versus incidental pointing gestures in young autistic children](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0022096521001235)

### 3.4 Egocentric vs. Allocentric Reference Frames

Research on spatial cognition in autism reveals a pattern that matters directly for spatial deixis:

- **Egocentric** reference (anchored to the self's body: "to my left") tends to be intact in autism and sometimes enhanced
- **Allocentric** reference (anchored to external landmarks or to another person's position: "to his left") is often more costly
- Autistic participants show particular difficulty in viewpoint-independent (allocentric) navigation tasks, while viewpoint-dependent (egocentric) navigation remains robust
- Abstract spatial reasoning is often a strength in autism; **relative spatial language** -- "in front of," "to the left of" -- is where the specific cost surfaces, because relative spatial language forces a dynamic choice of origin

This maps cleanly onto spatial deixis: "here/there" and "this/that" require constantly deciding **whose** "here" is in play. Autistic cognition can handle the geometry; what is expensive is the rapid switching of which body counts as origin.

**Sources:**
- [Allocentric Versus Egocentric Spatial Memory in Adults with Autism Spectrum Disorder (PMC5948263)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5948263/)
- [Spatial language difficulties reflect the structure of intact spatial representation: Evidence from high-functioning autism](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0010028519302397)
- [Tell me where it is: Selective difficulties in spatial language on the autism spectrum (PMC7545647)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7545647/)

### 3.5 Spatial Cognition Strengths and Mental Rotation

The deficit framing of the older autism literature rarely mentions the other half of the picture: autistic spatial cognition is often **unusually strong** on the dimensions that matter for geometry, mental rotation, and perceptual detail.

- The enhanced perceptual functioning model (Mottron, Dawson and colleagues) describes a systematic autistic advantage in locally-driven perceptual processing, including mental rotation, embedded-figures detection, and block design
- Mental rotation tasks -- imagining what an object looks like from another viewpoint -- are frequently performed at or above neurotypical level in autistic participants
- This is important because it separates **spatial transformation as a geometric operation** (often a strength) from **spatial deixis as a real-time social coordination task** (often costly). They are not the same thing.
- The autistic pattern is: strong on spatial geometry, costly on rapid switching of deictic origin. This is consistent with a cost-of-shifting account, and inconsistent with a "can't take perspectives" account.

### 3.6 Digital Deixis: Here Has Become Plural

Video calls, shared screens, and remote work have fractured "here" into several simultaneous locations:

- **The speaker's here** -- where their body actually is
- **The listener's here** -- where the listener's body is
- **The shared screen's here** -- the cursor, the slide, the currently-focused document, which is neither participant's physical location
- **The camera's here** -- the direction the speaker is looking, which on a video call is usually **not** the direction of the listener's face

A sentence like "can you see that?" on a video call is now deictically under-specified by default. Neurotypical speakers usually manage the ambiguity implicitly. Autistic communicators, who tend toward explicit reference anyway, often disambiguate automatically: "on my screen, top right" or "in the slide we're looking at now." That is a **superior adaptation** to a fractured deictic environment, not a clumsy workaround.

### 3.7 Practical Implications

- Use named landmarks instead of "here" or "there" when you can. ("In the kitchen, on the second shelf" beats "over there.")
- When you receive a spatial deictic instruction you cannot resolve, ask for a landmark. ("When you say 'next to it,' do you mean next to the phone or next to the laptop?")
- On video calls, name the shared artefact explicitly. "On the slide" or "in the shared doc" is clearer than "here."
- When giving directions, consider giving both egocentric ("my left") and allocentric ("north" or "toward the window") versions. The redundancy costs little and removes a common source of failure.
- Remember that your preference for explicit spatial reference is not a deficit. In technical writing, legal documents, and safety-critical contexts, it is the professional standard.

## 4. Temporal Deixis in Autism

### 4.1 The Challenge of Shifting Time Words

Temporal deictic terms -- "now," "then," "yesterday," "today," "tomorrow," "last week," "next month" -- anchor to the moment of speaking. "Yesterday" has no fixed date; it means "the day before the day this utterance is produced." This makes temporal deixis doubly costly because:

1. The reference shifts with every utterance (the deictic shift)
2. The underlying temporal categories are **transitory**: today's "tomorrow" becomes tomorrow's "today" and the next day's "yesterday"

### 4.2 Time Perception Research in Autism

There is a substantial literature on time perception in autism. The key points:

- Allman & Falter (2015), in their review *Abnormal Timing and Time Perception in Autism Spectrum Disorder? A Review of the Evidence*, proposed that temporal processing differences in autism extend across duration perception, relative timing of sensory signals, and temporal cognition
- Casassus et al. (2019) conducted a systematic review of time perception studies and found that divergent performance is most consistent on tasks that place **additional non-timing cognitive demands** on participants, such as working memory and attention management
- Poole et al. (2021) interviewed parents of autistic children and reported qualitative themes of "no idea of time" in some children, alongside heightened interest in clocks and schedules in others -- the picture is heterogeneous, not uniformly delayed
- Time-based prospective memory (remembering to do X at time Y without an external cue) tends to be more costly in autism than event-based prospective memory (remembering to do X when Y happens). The difference is cognitive load, not indifference to the task.

The important framing: autistic time perception differences are about **the cost of internally maintained temporal tracking when there is no external anchor**, not about inability to use time concepts.

**Sources:**
- [Casassus et al. (2019) Time perception and autistic spectrum condition: A systematic review (PMC6852160)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6852160/)
- [Allman & Falter (2015) Abnormal Timing and Time Perception in Autism Spectrum Disorder? A Review of the Evidence](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/281742173_Abnormal_Timing_and_Time_Perception_in_Autism_Spectrum_Disorder_A_Review_of_the_Evidence)
- [Poole et al. (2021) "No idea of time": Parents report differences in autistic children's behaviour relating to time](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/13623613211010014)

### 4.3 Episodic Future Thinking

Episodic future thinking is the ability to **pre-experience** a future scenario -- to mentally project yourself into "tomorrow at 3pm when I am at the dentist" with enough specificity to notice how you'll feel, what you'll see, and what you'll need. It is the temporal equivalent of episodic memory.

- Lind, Williams and colleagues have shown that autistic participants recall or imagine fewer specific past and future events than matched comparison participants
- The underlying cost appears to be **scene construction**, not self-projection. When participants imagine fictitious scenes that do not involve themselves, the same cost appears, which suggests that the difficulty is in assembling the rich imagined scenario rather than in projecting the self
- Episodic future thinking is thought to feed flexible planning, anticipatory pleasure, and the ability to update behaviour in light of imagined consequences. Where it is more costly, autistic people often rely on **external scaffolds** -- calendars, routines, written plans -- which again are working solutions, not deficits
- Faustmann et al. (2024) showed that **enactment encoding** (physically rehearsing an intended future action) and episodic future thinking both improved prospective memory in autistic adults, suggesting that the route to robust temporal planning often runs through concrete, externalised representations rather than abstract mental time-travel

**Sources:**
- [Lind & Bowler (2010) Episodic memory and episodic future thinking in adults with autism](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20853917/)
- [Lind, Williams et al. Episodic Memory and Episodic Future Thinking Impairments in High-Functioning Autism Spectrum Disorder (PMC3906795)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3906795/)
- [Faustmann et al. (2024) Practice is the best of all instructors: enactment encoding and episodic future thinking on prospective memory in autism](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/aur.3165)

### 4.4 Yesterday, Today, Tomorrow: The Shifting-Word Problem

The specific temporal deictic words are often the most effortful part:

- Autistic children often find "yesterday" and "tomorrow" harder than fixed time expressions like "Tuesday" or "September 14"
- The autism intervention literature consistently finds that **concretising** temporal reference helps: pointing at a calendar, using a visual schedule, attaching an event to a fixed date rather than a shifting deictic label
- In Relational Frame Theory terms, the "now-then" deictic relation is one of three core deictic frames (alongside I-you and here-there). Training on now-then relations has been shown to be possible in autism interventions using the Barnes-Holmes protocol, though, as noted earlier, these gains do not always transfer automatically to broader perspective-taking
- Many autistic adults convert "tomorrow" to an explicit date in their own heads before storing it. This is not an oddity. It is a more reliable encoding.

**Sources:**
- [Teaching the Concept of Time - Autism Awareness Centre](https://autismawarenesscentre.com/teaching-the-concept-of-time/)
- [Teaching "Then-Later" and "Here-There" Relations to Children with Autism (PMC6411558)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6411558/)

### 4.5 Asynchronous Communication: When Now Splits in Two

Asynchronous communication -- email, text, voicemail, recorded audio, forum posts -- fractures the temporal deictic anchor in ways that matter for autistic communicators.

- In face-to-face speech, the speaker's "now" and the hearer's "now" are the same. "I'll call you in ten minutes" is unambiguous.
- In email, the writer's "now" (when composing) is not the reader's "now" (when reading). "I'll call you in ten minutes" is undefined unless it includes a timestamp or an absolute time.
- Autistic communicators tend to write in absolute time by default -- "I'll call you at 3:15pm today (April 13)" -- and this is pragmatically superior in asynchronous contexts
- The deictic fragility of words like "now," "today," and "recently" in email is not an autism problem. It is a property of the medium. Autistic writers simply tend to notice it earlier and compensate by default.

### 4.6 Practical Implications

- Translate deictic time expressions to absolute dates and times in your own notes: when someone says "next Friday," write down the actual date
- In email and text, state absolute times rather than "now," "today," or "later"
- External temporal scaffolds (calendars, alarms, visual schedules, shared timelines) are not crutches. They are the medium in which reliable temporal planning lives for most people, autistic or not
- If an incoming message uses an ambiguous temporal deictic ("let's do it soon"), it is legitimate to ask for a concrete anchor ("does 'soon' mean this week or this month?")
- For long-term projects, converting "next sprint" or "later this quarter" into explicit week numbers or dates protects against rolling-deadline drift -- a productivity practice that coincidentally maps onto autistic temporal preferences

## 5. Social Deixis in Autism

### 5.1 What Social Deixis Is

Social deixis covers the linguistic devices whose meaning depends on the **social relationship** between speaker and addressee, or between speaker and referent. Examples:

- Address terms: **sir**, **ma'am**, **doctor**, **professor**, **officer**, **your honour**, first-name vs. surname
- Pronouns of formality: **T/V** distinctions (French tu/vous, German du/Sie, Spanish tú/usted) -- absent from modern English as a grammatical contrast but encoded indirectly through choice of address term, title, and register
- Honorifics: **Dr.**, **Rev.**, **Ms.**, **Mrs.**, **Mr.**, **Mx.**
- Register-encoded social deixis: the choice between "please could you," "could you," "would you mind," and "hey, grab that" encodes different assumed social relationships

Social deixis overlaps heavily with Category 03 (Politeness) and Category 22 (Social Hierarchy) -- it is the part of the deictic system where grammatical reference and social coordinates collide.

### 5.2 The "Context Blindness" Framing and Why It Needs Reframing

Vermeulen's (2012) book *Autism as Context Blindness* proposes that reduced spontaneous use of context is a common pathway in autistic cognition. The book has been influential and contains some accurate empirical observations, particularly about the real-time cost of switching interpretive contexts. However, the **framing** -- that autistic people are "blind" to context -- is misleading and has been legitimately criticised.

A more accurate reading:

- Autistic people are not context-blind. Many autistic people are **extraordinarily sensitive** to contextual detail, often more so than neurotypical peers
- What appears to be costly is **rapid automatic switching** between multiple overlapping interpretive frames under time pressure, especially when the cues are subtle, non-verbal, or culturally-encoded
- Social deixis is exactly the kind of domain where this cost is most visible: the address-term system encodes hierarchy, familiarity, and local custom simultaneously, and the "right" choice shifts continuously with context
- The "blindness" metaphor implies absence of perception. The evidence supports a **different distribution of where attention lands** under time pressure, not absence of contextual sensitivity

The honest summary: social deixis is cognitively expensive for many autistic people **not because they cannot perceive the social context, but because the context-to-form mapping is densely culturally coded, heavily automated in neurotypical speakers, and rarely made explicit in language instruction.**

**Sources:**
- [Vermeulen (2012) Autism as Context Blindness (AAPC)](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/249970775_Autism_as_Context_Blindness)

### 5.3 Address Terms and Register in Practice

English no longer has a grammatical T/V contrast, but it has an elaborate, mostly unwritten system of social-deictic register:

- **First name** ("Sarah"): assumed peer or solidarity relationship
- **Title plus surname** ("Dr. Miller"): assumed formal or hierarchical distance
- **Honorific alone** ("doctor," "professor," "ma'am," "sir"): marks deference without personal familiarity
- **Nickname or diminutive** ("Sar"): marks intimacy or claimed intimacy
- **No address term at all**: the most common choice in ambiguous situations -- English speakers often just avoid the issue

For autistic speakers, the expensive part is typically not learning which form is which. It is knowing **which is expected in which situation**, especially when the situation gives mixed signals: a senior colleague on a video call who is also a personal friend, a professor who has said "call me Jane" in office but is addressed formally in a meeting, a customer-service interaction where the customer's age is uncertain.

The research literature on autism and address-term use is thinner than it should be. What exists mostly describes the surface pattern -- autistic speakers often default to one register (usually the more formal one) across situations, or avoid address terms altogether -- without framing it as a rational response to a high-cost, low-information task. Many autistic speakers adopt a **"safe formal" default** in unfamiliar settings and rely on explicit invitations to downshift ("please call me Jane"). That is a working strategy. It optimises for avoiding offence when the cost of getting it wrong is borne largely by the speaker.

### 5.4 The Asymmetry of Cost

An important double-empathy point: when an autistic speaker gets a social-deictic form wrong -- uses "sir" to someone who prefers "Professor," uses a first name too early, uses a title that has been declined -- **the pragmatic cost is almost always borne by the autistic speaker, not by the hearer.** The hearer may be briefly confused or mildly irritated; the speaker may be judged as impolite, unprofessional, or socially incompetent.

This asymmetry is rarely acknowledged in the literature. When autistic people are described as "missing" social-deictic cues, the framing hides the reality that the social-deictic system is a one-sided enforcement mechanism: the hearer is rarely penalised for failing to signal clearly how they want to be addressed. Autistic speakers who explicitly **ask** ("how would you like me to address you?") are performing the exact repair work the system was built to avoid, and they are usually treated as slightly odd for asking. That is not a deficit in the asker. It is an unresolved ambiguity in the system.

### 5.5 Register-Encoded Power and Milton's Double Empathy

Milton's (2012) *On the ontological status of autism: the 'double empathy problem'* (*Disability & Society* 27(6):883-887) makes the decisive move here. Milton reframes what the older literature calls autistic "deficits" in social communication as a **mismatch** between two communicative styles, neither of which has intrinsic priority. Non-autistic interlocutors also fail to read autistic social signals, but the literature has historically counted those failures as autistic problems.

For social deixis specifically:

- Neurotypical speakers encode social status through dense, fast, implicit register choices
- Autistic speakers often prefer explicit, stable, consistent register choices
- Both are legitimate communicative systems
- When they interact, coordination failures occur in **both directions**. The neurotypical speaker fails to notice that their register shift is opaque to the autistic interlocutor; the autistic speaker fails to match the implicit signalling. These are symmetrical failures, but only one of them is named as a "social impairment."
- The double empathy framing does not require anyone to change how they speak. It requires that both sides acknowledge the cost of translation.

**Sources:**
- [Milton, D. E. M. (2012) On the ontological status of autism: the 'double empathy problem'. *Disability & Society* 27(6):883-887](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/09687599.2012.710008)
- [Milton 2012 open-access version (Kent Academic Repository)](https://kar.kent.ac.uk/62639/1/Double%20empathy%20problem.pdf)
- [The double empathy problem | National Autistic Society](https://www.autism.org.uk/advice-and-guidance/professional-practice/double-empathy)

### 5.6 Practical Implications

- **Safe formal default.** In unfamiliar settings, the more formal register is usually the safer choice. You can downshift later if invited.
- **Explicit asking is legitimate.** "What's the best way to address you?" is a professional question, and it removes the ambiguity at source. The social penalty for asking is almost always smaller than the penalty for guessing wrong.
- **Mirroring works.** If you are unsure of the register, use the form your interlocutor used first. If they said "hi, I'm Jane," you can say "hi Jane." If they said "Dr Miller," you can say "Dr Miller."
- **Register inconsistency is informative.** If a person uses formal register in one context and informal in another, that difference is usually carrying meaning about the situation, not about you.
- **In written communication, explicit address is easier.** Email signatures and professional titles are externally visible anchors. Use them.

## 6. Discourse Deixis in Autism

### 6.1 What Discourse Deixis Is

Discourse deixis is the use of linguistic forms to point at **the discourse itself** rather than at the world. Examples:

- "**This** argument is weak" (pointing back at an argument the speaker has just made)
- "**That** point is worth considering" (pointing at something someone else said earlier)
- "**The following** example" (pointing forward at something about to come)
- "**The above** discussion" (pointing back at preceding text)
- "**As I said earlier**" (explicit recall of earlier discourse material)
- "**Which reminds me**" (announcing an anaphoric jump)

Discourse deixis sits at the border with **anaphora** (backward-pointing reference) and **cataphora** (forward-pointing reference). All three are ways of linking parts of a text or conversation together so that the listener can reconstruct "which bit of the discourse are we talking about now?"

Kleiber and others have argued that deixis and anaphora are best understood as **the same underlying targeting operation** with different sources: deixis points to the extralinguistic situation; anaphora points to something already in the discourse. The cognitive machinery is shared.

**Sources:**
- [Targeting in Language: Unifying Deixis and Anaphora](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/538289)

### 6.2 Reference Establishment vs. Cohesion

A consistent finding in the autism discourse literature:

- Autistic speakers show differences in **referential establishment** (introducing new referents clearly to a listener who does not yet know them) more than in **referential cohesion** (maintaining reference to an already-established entity)
- In the Arnold et al. and related corpus studies, autistic children produced significantly higher rates of **ambiguous first mentions**: using "he" in a story with multiple male characters without pre-introducing which one, or using "this" without a clear antecedent
- This is specifically a **social-pragmatic** pattern: once the referent is in play for both participants, autistic speakers maintain it well; the cost lies in **modelling what the listener does not yet know**
- Reframed: this is not a failure of discourse tracking. It is a consequence of the fact that autistic speakers often hold the referent vividly in their own attention, and the labour of modelling the listener's ignorance is the specific step that is effortful

The practical upshot: establishing reference explicitly up front ("I was reading an article -- it's by Sarah Miller -- and she argues that...") solves the pattern. The difficulty is the **inferring-the-listener-doesn't-know-yet** step, not the referring itself.

**Sources:**
- [Analysis of Noun Phrase Ambiguity in Narratives Reveals Differences in Referential Establishment But Not Cohesion for Older Autistic Children (PMC10555458)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10555458/)
- [Reference Production in Young Speakers with and without Autism (PMC3668432)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3668432/)

### 6.3 Comprehension of Discourse-Level Reference

- Eye-tracking research shows that when autistic adults resolve pronouns in discourse, the resolution itself is often accurate; the **search strategy** differs, with autistic readers sometimes spending longer fixating candidate antecedents
- Autistic participants show fine-grained sensitivity to discourse context, and their referential choices are modulated by cognitive effort in largely the same way as neurotypical participants
- The difficulty is more pronounced when resolution requires **pragmatic inference** (working out the most likely referent from social context) than when it can be handled by **structural cues** (grammar rules that constrain which entities a pronoun can refer to)
- Some autistic individuals describe using **extra working memory** to track "which 'it' is which" in dense discourse, and the cost is real but is not the same thing as a comprehension failure

**Sources:**
- [Structural and Contextual Cues in Third-person Pronoun Interpretation by Children with ASD (PMC7878583)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7878583/)
- [Is verbal reference impaired in autism spectrum disorder? A systematic review](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2396941518763166)
- [Production and comprehension of pronouns in individuals with autism: A meta-analysis and systematic review](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/1362361320949103)

### 6.4 Episodic Memory, Pattern Memory, and Discourse Deixis

Discourse deixis depends on holding the structure of the unfolding discourse in memory. This is where the Boucher & Mayes line of work becomes relevant.

- Boucher & Mayes (2012) *Memory in ASD: have we been barking up the wrong tree?* (*Autism* 16(5)) proposed that episodic memory differences in autism are better explained by posterior parietal dysfunction and associated differences in **free recall** than by a wholesale hippocampal story
- The pattern that emerges: autistic memory is often **weaker on free recall of rich autobiographical episodes** and **stronger on cued recall, semantic memory, and pattern-based retrieval**
- For discourse deixis, this means: holding a dense, multi-character, rapidly-unfolding episode in episodic memory long enough to resolve "which 'he' is which" is the costly mode; retrieving a well-indexed fact or pattern is often the strong mode
- Autistic **pattern memory** and long-term semantic memory are frequently assets. When a discourse provides clear structural cues -- headings, numbered points, explicit "first... second... third" scaffolding -- reference resolution is often easier than in free-running conversation
- The overlap with Category 12 (Narrative) and Category 16 (Common Ground) matters here: narrative coherence and common-ground tracking both lean on the same machinery. A preference for explicit, structured discourse is not an idiosyncrasy -- it is a rational allocation of cognitive resources to the mode where autistic processing is strongest.

**Sources:**
- [Boucher & Mayes (2012) Memory in ASD: have we been barking up the wrong tree?](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/1362361311417738)
- [Boucher & Anns (2018) Memory, learning and language in autism spectrum disorder](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2396941517742078)
- [Cooper et al. Exploring the neurocognitive basis of episodic recollection in autism (PMC6424931)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6424931/)

### 6.5 Cataphora and Forward-Pointing Reference

Cataphora -- "the following example," "what I'm about to say," "consider this: ..." -- is the mirror of anaphora and is often overlooked in the autism discourse literature. A few practical observations:

- Cataphoric reference places an open referent in working memory that has to be **resolved later**, which increases short-term cognitive load
- Autistic writers and speakers often prefer **explicit signposting** ("I'll make three points: first, ...; second, ...; third, ...") over dense cataphoric structure
- This is a stylistic choice that trades memory load for linear clarity. It is the structure of technical documentation, legal briefs, and many forms of professional writing -- contexts where explicit discourse deixis is already the norm

### 6.6 Practical Implications

- **Introduce referents explicitly** before using pronouns. "Sarah -- the friend I was telling you about -- called yesterday, and she..."
- **Use named structure** in longer explanations. "Three things I want to cover: A, B, C." Then explicitly mark each one.
- **Request re-establishment when you lose the thread.** "When you say 'it,' which thing are you pointing at?" is a legitimate, professional repair move.
- **Prefer written communication for complex multi-referent discussions.** Writing leaves the referents visible on the page, which removes most of the tracking cost.
- **Use back-references explicitly in your own writing.** "As noted in section 3" is more robust than "as mentioned earlier."
- **Leverage pattern memory.** If a conversation is structured around a familiar pattern or template, the cost of reference tracking drops substantially. Seeking out conversational patterns you already know is not rigidity; it is efficient cognitive use.

## 7. Cross-Deictic Integration: The Effort Cost

### 7.1 Deixis Is Costly in Any Mind

The cumulative picture across the five deictic domains is not that autistic minds are blind to reference. It is that **deictic processing is cognitively expensive in any mind**, and that autistic minds carry more of that cost in real time rather than absorbing it into unconscious automatic processing.

- Every deictic expression requires: (a) identifying the current deictic centre, (b) applying the context-to-form mapping, (c) suppressing competing candidate referents, (d) updating the representation when the centre shifts
- Neurotypical adults do this mostly outside awareness because decades of dense input have pushed the cost below threshold. This does not mean the cost is zero; it means it has been absorbed
- Autistic adults often do the same work with more of it visible. The accuracy is generally comparable (as comprehension studies repeatedly show); the difference is in **when** the answer becomes available and in **how much** cognitive resource the computation used
- Where the autistic pattern shows up as "slower" or "more effortful," that is a real-time load finding, not a capacity finding

### 7.2 The Compensations Are Communicatively Sound

The adaptations autistic people tend to develop for deictic processing are often **objectively more precise** than the shortcuts they replace:

- Names instead of pronouns -- fewer ambiguous referents
- Absolute dates instead of "tomorrow" -- no resolution cost
- Named locations instead of "here" -- independent of the speaker's body
- Explicit titles and honorifics by default -- less risk of offence
- Signposted discourse structure ("first... second... third") -- lower listener memory load

In technical, legal, scientific, and safety-critical writing, these compensations are **the professional standard**. The autistic preference for explicit reference is not an oddity; it is the communicative style that high-stakes domains have independently converged on, because it minimises errors when the cost of failure is high.

### 7.3 The Double Empathy Asymmetry

Milton's (2012) double empathy framing is decisive here. When two speakers with different deictic conventions interact, coordination failures happen **in both directions**:

- The neurotypical speaker fails to notice that their deictic shortcuts are opaque to the autistic interlocutor
- The autistic speaker fails to match the neurotypical speaker's implicit, rapidly-shifting reference system

Both are failures. Only one has traditionally been named as a clinical feature. The literature on "autistic deictic difficulties" would look very different if it had been written with equal attention to the **non-autistic interlocutors who fail to track autistic deictic anchors** when the autistic speaker is the primary source.

Some observations in that direction:

- Non-autistic listeners often lose track of autistic speakers' explicit referent chains because they expect pronouns and are disoriented by full names
- Non-autistic listeners often treat autistic absolute-time expressions ("I'll call you at 3:15pm") as over-specification rather than as a helpful convention
- Non-autistic listeners often read "safe formal" register as distant or cold rather than as a rational default under uncertainty
- Non-autistic listeners often read explicit discourse signposting ("first... second... third") as pedantic rather than as structural support
- Autistic-to-autistic communication, by contrast, is often reported as **more efficient and more satisfying**, partly because both parties are running the same high-explicitness deictic protocol

### 7.4 What the Literature Should Be Saying

A fair summary of the evidence across the five deictic domains:

- Autistic deictic processing is not absent, impaired, or blind. It is **expensive in real time** and tends to resolve into **explicit, durable compensations**
- Those compensations are **communicatively robust** and often professionally valued
- The asymmetry in who pays the coordination cost between autistic and non-autistic interlocutors is **bidirectional**, but has historically been counted one-sidedly
- Pronoun reversal in childhood is a **developmental signature** of deictic translation labour, not a symptom of missing self-knowledge
- Adult autistic people generally do not reverse pronouns; what persists is a preference for explicit reference that is **a strength, not a remnant**

## 8. Practical Strategies for Autistic Readers

### 8.1 For Clear Deictic Reference

1. **Use names when pronouns would be ambiguous.** In a conversation with multiple people of the same gender, "Sarah took the folder to Mark" is objectively clearer than "She took it to him." This is not a deficit. It is communicatively superior.

2. **Ask for clarification when deictic reference is unclear.** "When you say 'there,' do you mean [specific place]?" or "Who does 'he' refer to?" are legitimate, professional moves.

3. **Translate deictic time expressions to concrete dates.** "Do you mean Tuesday the 8th?" Calendars and planners serve as concrete anchoring tools and are not crutches.

4. **Ask for or combine multiple cues.** Research consistently shows that redundant deictic cues (pointing **and** naming, looking **and** verbalising) aid comprehension. Requesting this from others is reasonable.

5. **Restate your understanding when references multiply.** "So when you said 'she should do it,' you meant [Name], right?" verifies reference resolution without requiring real-time deictic processing under pressure.

### 8.2 Digital Communication Changes the Deictic Landscape

Digital communication alters the deictic environment in ways that often favour autistic preferences:

- **Person deixis is often simpler:** in most text channels the writer is named, and many conversations are one-to-one
- **Spatial deixis is largely eliminated:** "here" and "there" rarely arise in text because participants are not co-located; locations must be named explicitly
- **Temporal deixis can be concretised:** timestamps provide absolute temporal anchoring
- **Processing time is available:** text-based communication eliminates the pressure to interpret and respond in real time
- **Non-deictic cues are reduced:** the absence of pointing, head-nods, and gaze direction in text forces explicit reference

Research confirms that many autistic adults prefer written communication over verbal, partly because the reduced deictic demands leave more cognitive room for the content of the message itself.

**Challenges that remain in digital communication:**

- Group chats make "you" ambiguous
- Emoji and tone indicators are still partially deictic
- Video calls re-introduce fractured "here" (see section 3.6)
- Shared documents create cataphoric reference ("see the comment below") that is slightly more fragile than in-person reference

**Sources:**
- ['Anything but the phone!': Communication mode preferences in the autism community](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/13623613211014995)
- [Autism Texting Habits: Communication in the Digital Age](https://neurolaunch.com/autism-texting-habits/)

### 8.3 When to Ask for Clarification

Situations where deictic reference is especially likely to be ambiguous, and where asking for clarification is appropriate:

- **Multiple people of the same gender in a story** -- "He did X" -- which "he"?
- **"This" or "that" without a clear antecedent** -- "We need to fix this" -- fix what?
- **Temporal reference without context** -- "Let's do it tomorrow" -- which day?
- **Spatial reference without shared location** -- "over there" -- relative to whom?
- **Reported speech and nested quotation** -- "she said he told her that she..." -- the deictic centres multiply at each layer
- **Anaphoric reference after topic change** -- "it" after switching topics -- which "it"?
- **Address-term uncertainty** -- new colleague, unfamiliar register, mixed formal/informal signals

## 9. Strengths and Reframing

### 9.1 Explicit Reference as Communicative Strength

Using names instead of pronouns, specific dates instead of "tomorrow," and explicit locations instead of "here/there" is often framed in the clinical literature as a deficit or oddity. It is more accurately described as a **precision strategy**:

- Names eliminate pronoun ambiguity. In any context with multiple possible referents, "Sarah took the folder to Mark" beats "She took it to him."
- Explicit temporal reference prevents misunderstanding. "Tuesday at 3pm" cannot be misinterpreted the way "the day after tomorrow in the afternoon" can.
- Concrete spatial reference is more informative. "In the kitchen cabinet" communicates more than "over there."
- Precision reduces communication breakdown.

Technical, legal, scientific, and safety-critical writing all prize this kind of explicit reference over deictic shortcuts. The autistic tendency toward precision is an asset in these domains and tracks the evolution of professional writing in general.

### 9.2 The Fluidity-to-Inelasticity Hypothesis (Oakley & Vidanović, 2014)

Oakley & Vidanović (2014), in *Deixis and personhood: Autism, intersubjectivity, and the fluidity-to-inelasticity hypothesis* (*Cognitive Semiotics* 7(2):191-225), proposed a theoretical framework that fits cleanly into the non-deficit reading.

- Typical deictic processing requires **fluid** shifting of reference points -- the ability to rapidly and flexibly update who the "I-here-now" centre is
- In autism, there is often **inelasticity** in this shifting: deictic reference points are less fluid, harder to update, and more likely to "stick" to a particular configuration
- Oakley & Vidanović specifically note that "verbal autists possess many of the intersubjectivity skills required for normal conversation but lack the ability to deploy stable reference points when using the deictic scheme to communicate about situations in which either the topic or the audience are displaced in time and space"
- This is not an all-or-nothing deficit. It is a **flexibility** continuum. The challenge increases when topics or audiences are displaced from the immediate context.

Reframed in double-empathy terms: the fluidity-to-inelasticity hypothesis describes an **elasticity cost**, not a missing faculty. Autistic deictic reference is more stable, less rapidly reconfigurable, and more durable. In most domains, that is a feature, not a bug. The cost appears at the specific moment of rapid reconfiguration under time pressure, which is the part of conversational deixis that is most taxing for any mind.

**Sources:**
- [Oakley & Vidanović (2014) Deixis and personhood: Autism, intersubjectivity, and the fluidity-to-inelasticity hypothesis. *Cognitive Semiotics* 7(2):191-225](https://www.degruyterbrill.com/document/doi/10.1515/cogsem-2014-0013/html?lang=en)

### 9.3 Relational Frame Theory (RFT): What It Gets Right and Wrong

Relational Frame Theory offers a systematic framework for deictic skills and has contributed useful empirical work:

- Three core deictic frames: **I-You**, **Here-There**, **Now-Then**
- Three levels of complexity:
  - **Simple:** "I have a red brick, you have a green brick. Which brick do I have?"
  - **Reversed:** "If I was you and you were me, which brick would I have?"
  - **Double-reversed:** "If I was you and you were me, and if here was there and there was here, where would I be sitting?"
- The Barnes-Holmes Perspective Taking Protocol has been used to assess and train these skills in autism
- Training can successfully establish deictic relational responding in autistic participants
- **Important caveat:** training on deictic relations does not automatically transfer to broader Theory of Mind or general social perspective-taking. The relation between deictic language use and social perspective-taking is not a simple identity.

The double-empathy reading of this finding: mastering the **linguistic form** of perspective-shifting is not the same thing as bridging the social gap between different minds. Both are valuable. Neither is the same as the other. The literature sometimes confuses them.

### 9.4 The Double Empathy Angle in Full

Milton's (2012) double empathy problem reframes communication difficulties as **bidirectional** rather than as unilateral autistic impairment:

- Non-autistic speakers often **over-rely on deictic expressions** that assume shared context, shared spatial reference, and shared ability to track shifting pronouns
- When a non-autistic speaker says "can you hand me that thing over there?" to someone who does not share their spatial perspective or has not been tracking the same referents, the communication failure is on **both sides**
- Many of the difficulties autistic individuals face when talking with non-autistic individuals are due in part to mismatch, and in part to a lack of mutual understanding between the two groups
- Autistic people often communicate more efficiently with other autistic people than with non-autistic people, which is consistent with the hypothesis that autistic communicators tend to share a more explicit, less deictic-shortcut-dependent protocol
- The so-called "deficit" in autistic deictic processing can be redescribed as: non-autistic speakers under-specify their references, relying on assumptions about shared context that are not always warranted. Neither side is wrong. Both pay a cost.

### 9.5 A Corpus-Based Perspective

One corpus study comparing autistic and typically developing children's deictic use found that "the frequency and type of deictic words uttered was relatively similar" between the groups. This challenges the notion of a categorical deictic deficit and supports the view that differences are better described as:

- **Processing speed** (more time to compute deictic shifts) rather than inability
- **Specific contexts** (displaced, complex, or multi-referent situations) rather than all deictic use
- **Production strategies** (preferring explicit reference) rather than comprehension failure

**Sources:**

## 11. Summary of Key Findings

1. **Pronoun reversal** is more common in young autistic children than in typical development (~13% vs. ~1% of pronoun use in the Naigles et al. sample), but less common than Kanner's original 1943 framing implied. It is best described as effortful deictic translation work made visible, not as missing self-knowledge. Adult autistic people generally do not reverse pronouns; what persists is a preference for proper names over pronouns as a working communicative strategy.

2. **Deictic shifting** -- the constant re-anchoring of I/here/now with every speaker change -- is measurably more costly for autistic minds in real time. Mizuno et al.'s neuroimaging showed reduced task-specific functional coupling between the right anterior insula and precuneus during the specific operation of mapping an external "you" back to self. This is a load finding, not a capacity finding.

3. **Sign-language evidence** (Shield, Meier & Tager-Flusberg, 2015) localises the phenomenon. Deaf autistic children produce palm-orientation reversals (surface-form mirroring) but not pointing-direction errors (they point at who they mean). The pattern is about the cost of transforming the form of a linguistic signal for the self's perspective, not about not knowing who is who.

4. **The fluidity-to-inelasticity hypothesis** (Oakley & Vidanović, 2014, *Cognitive Semiotics* 7(2):191-225) describes deictic reference points in autism as less rapidly reconfigurable but more durable. This is an elasticity cost, not a missing faculty.

5. **Spatial deixis** shows a specific pattern: costly rapid switching of deictic origin (which body is "here"?), paired with often-strong spatial geometry, mental rotation, and egocentric navigation. Digital environments (video calls, shared screens) fracture "here" for everyone, and autistic communicators' explicit habits are an ergonomic fit to the fractured environment.

6. **Temporal deixis** research (Allman & Falter 2015; Casassus et al. 2019; Lind et al. on episodic future thinking) shows that the cost lives in internally-maintained temporal tracking without external anchors. External scaffolds -- calendars, timestamps, written plans -- are working solutions, not crutches. Asynchronous communication (email, text) fractures "now" for everyone, and autistic writers' default preference for absolute dates/times is communicatively superior in that environment.

7. **Social deixis** (address terms, titles, formal/informal register) is costly because the context-to-form mapping is culturally dense, implicit, and rarely taught explicitly. Vermeulen's (2012) "context blindness" framing is reframed here: autistic attention to context is often high, but rapid automatic context-to-form mapping under time pressure is expensive. A "safe formal" default and explicit asking ("how would you like me to address you?") are rational, effective strategies. The pragmatic cost of getting social deixis wrong is almost always borne by the speaker, not the hearer.

8. **Discourse deixis** -- reference to the ongoing discourse itself, and to anaphoric/cataphoric structure -- shows the pattern that autistic speakers' cost lies more in **referent establishment** (modelling what the listener does not yet know) than in **referent maintenance** once established. Pattern memory and structured signposting are often strengths, and they map directly onto the discourse conventions of technical and professional writing.

9. **Training deictic relations** (I-you, here-there, now-then) can succeed in controlled protocols but does not automatically transfer to broader Theory of Mind or social perspective-taking. Mastering the linguistic form of perspective is not the same thing as bridging the social gap between different minds.

10. **The autistic preference for explicit reference** -- names over pronouns, dates over "tomorrow," landmarks over "there," titles over first-names-by-default, structured signposting over dense anaphora -- is a set of durable compensations that are **communicatively robust** and that coincide with the independent conventions of technical, legal, scientific, and safety-critical writing.

11. **Digital communication** reduces the real-time deictic burden in ways that often favour autistic processing (timestamps, named locations, available processing time, no pressure to decode non-verbal gesture). This is consistent with the well-documented autistic preference for written over spoken communication.

12. **Milton's (2012) double empathy framing** is decisive. The asymmetry in who bears the coordination cost between autistic and non-autistic interlocutors is bidirectional, but has historically been counted one-sidedly. Autistic deictic processing is not blind, impaired, or missing. It is expensive in real time and resolves into explicit, durable compensations -- compensations that are frequently the conventions of high-precision communication in any community.
