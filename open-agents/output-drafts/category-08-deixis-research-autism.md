# Category 08: Deixis -- Research on Autism and Deictic Language

## Research Summary

This document covers the intersection of deixis (context-dependent reference) and autism, focusing on the well-documented challenges autistic individuals face with deictic expressions -- words whose meaning shifts depending on the speaker, location, and time of utterance. The research spans from Kanner's 1943 observations of pronoun reversal through modern neuroimaging studies, with practical implications for autistic adults navigating deictic reference in everyday communication.

---

## 1. Pronoun Reversal: The Classic Finding

### 1.1 Historical Context

Leo Kanner first documented pronoun reversal in his landmark 1943 paper describing 11 children with "autistic disturbances of affective contact." He observed that children would refer to themselves as "you" and to others as "I," and he initially explained this as echolalia: "Personal pronouns are repeated just as heard, with no change to suit the altered situation" (Kanner, 1943). This observation became one of the defining features associated with early autism descriptions.

**Sources:**
- [The Diagnosis of Autism: From Kanner to DSM-III to DSM-5 and Beyond (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8531066/)
- [Perspectives on Personal Pronoun Reversal in Children with ASD: A Critical Review](https://jeps.efpsa.org/articles/10.5334/jeps.br)

### 1.2 What Pronoun Reversal Looks Like

Pronoun reversal (also called pronominal reversal) occurs when an individual uses "you" to refer to themselves or "I" to refer to someone else. Common examples:

- A child says **"You want a cookie"** when they mean **"I want a cookie"**
- A child says **"Pick you up"** when they mean **"Pick me up"** (echoing what a caregiver says before lifting them)
- A child refers to themselves by name or as "he/she" rather than using "I/me"

This last pattern -- using proper names or third-person pronouns to refer to oneself -- is sometimes called **pronoun avoidance** and is distinct from but related to pronoun reversal. Children with ASD are more likely to use a name than a pronoun when identifying people.

**Sources:**
- [Teaching Pronouns to Kids with Autism & Avoiding Pronoun Reversal](https://marybarbera.com/teaching-pronouns-kids-with-autism-and-avoiding-pronoun-reversal/)
- [Pronoun Reversal and Confusion | Musings of an Aspie](https://musingsofanaspie.com/2014/03/14/pronoun-reversal-and-confusion/)

### 1.3 Prevalence: Less Common Than Once Believed

Modern research has revised Kanner's impression that pronoun reversal is rampant in autism:

- Pronoun reversals occur in **less than 10% of the speech** of children with ASD aged 2-5 years
- One study found that while all children with ASD in the sample produced reversed pronouns, they did so in only **~13% of all pronouns produced**
- Typically developing children reverse pronouns about **1% of the time** at similar ages
- The ASD group produces "somewhat more reversals than the TD group, overall and at multiple visits"
- **Both groups** produce reversals at **low absolute rates** -- the difference is in degree, not kind

Key finding: Pronoun misuse in autism is **less common than originally assumed** by early autism research, but it is still significantly more frequent than in typical development and tends to persist longer.

**Sources:**
- ["You're telling me!" The Prevalence and Predictors of Pronoun Reversals in Children with ASD and Typical Development (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4834724/)
- [Questions for Letitia Naigles: Parsing pronoun confusion | The Transmitter](https://www.thetransmitter.org/spectrum/questions-for-letitia-naigles-parsing-pronoun-confusion/)

### 1.4 Developmental Trajectory and Resolution

- In typical development, most children gain a firm grasp of personal pronouns between **ages 2.5 and 3**, understanding that pronouns are referential (shifting) rather than absolute (fixed) words
- In autism, pronoun reversal follows a **different developmental trajectory**: "Children with ASD hardly improved with age. Older children with ASD thus showed more pronoun reversals than did their TD peers"
- Resolution timing varies enormously: "For some, this happens at age 3. For others, it's age 5. Other children with autism might never reverse pronouns"
- The key trigger for resolution: "Once social perspective-taking catches up with language in these children, pronoun reversals stop"
- Many autistic individuals resolve overt reversal but **retain a preference for using proper names** over pronouns, which may persist into adulthood

**Sources:**
- [Person-Reference in Autism Spectrum Disorder: Developmental Trends (Barokova & Tager-Flusberg, 2020)](https://www.bu.edu/autism/files/2020/08/Barokova-Tager-Flusberg-Person-Reference-in-ASD-1.pdf)
- [Children with autism spectrum disorder show pronoun reversals in interpretation](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29528676/)

### 1.5 Theoretical Explanations

Three major accounts attempt to explain pronoun reversal:

#### A. The Echolalia Account
- The child echoes heard speech without adjusting pronouns: a caregiver says "Do you want a cookie?" and the child repeats "You want a cookie" as a request
- Closely linked to **gestalt language processing (GLP)**: gestalt processors acquire language in whole chunks rather than individual words, so the pronouns within an echoed phrase come along unchanged
- Under this view, "pronoun reversal is not an error but rather a common characteristic of early stage gestalt language development"
- **Limitation:** This account cannot fully explain cases where reversal occurs in novel (non-echoed) sentences

#### B. The Perspective-Shifting Account
- Pronouns are **inherently deictic**: "I" means "the person currently speaking" and "you" means "the person currently being addressed" -- these references **flip with every conversational turn**
- Correctly using "I" and "you" requires the speaker to perform a **deictic shift**: to adopt the perspective of the speaker role and map themselves into it
- This is fundamentally a **perspective-taking** operation, and research consistently finds: "Pronoun reversals most likely result from perspective-shifting difficulties"
- Theory of Mind (ToM) understanding, working memory, IQ, and verbal ability are all associated with pronoun interpretation, and ToM is specifically associated with correct pronoun use
- **This is the dominant modern account**

#### C. The Self-Other Distinction Account (Neural)
- fMRI research (Mizuno et al., 2011) found **diminished functional connectivity** between the right anterior insula and precuneus during deictic shifting in autism
- The right anterior insula is involved in **self-awareness** and the precuneus in **self-other distinction**
- "Errors in choosing a self-referring pronoun reflect a disordered neural representation of the self"
- The connectivity reduction was specific to answering questions containing "you" (requiring mapping to self) but not when answering about someone else's view

#### D. Multi-Factor Reality
Modern research recognizes that multiple factors contribute: "In the ASD group, early language and joint attention scores contributed significantly and independently to the incidence of reversal. Both linguistic and social factors seem implicated; moreover, reversals seem to occur when children's language and social abilities develop asynchronously."

**Sources:**
- [Pronoun Reversal & Gestalt Language Processing](https://www.meaningfulspeech.com/blog/pronoun-reversal)
- [The neural basis of deictic shifting in linguistic perspective-taking in high-functioning autism (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3155703/)
- [New brain imaging research reveals why autistic individuals confuse pronouns | ScienceDaily](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2011/08/110801122956.htm)
- [Perspectives on Personal Pronoun Reversal in Children with ASD: A Critical Review](https://jeps.efpsa.org/articles/10.5334/jeps.br)

### 1.6 The Sign Language Evidence

A particularly illuminating line of evidence comes from deaf autistic children who use sign language:

- In sign languages, pronouns like "I" and "you" are literally **pointing gestures** (point to self = "I", point to addressee = "you") -- making them far more transparent than spoken pronouns
- Despite this transparency, deaf autistic children show **palm orientation reversals**: signs typically produced with an outward-facing palm are produced with the palm toward the signer
- However, true pronoun reversals (pointing to the wrong person) have **not** been documented in deaf autistic children's signing
- This supports the perspective-shifting account: "Both phenomena could be grounded in the same underlying difference -- a tendency to reproduce linguistic forms in a gestalt fashion without undergoing a shift"
- The absence of pointing-direction errors but presence of palm-orientation errors suggests the difficulty is in **transforming the form of the linguistic signal**, not in understanding who is meant

**Sources:**
- [The Use of Sign Language Pronouns by Native-Signing Children with Autism (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4474758/)
- [Are palm reversals the pronoun reversals of sign language? Evidence from a fingerspelling task (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9614108/)
- [Sign language study solves autism's pronoun mystery | The Transmitter](https://www.thetransmitter.org/spectrum/sign-language-study-solves-autisms-pronoun-mystery/)

### 1.7 Is Pronoun Reversal Specific to Autism?

**No.** Pronoun reversal is not unique to autism:

- Typically developing children also produce pronoun reversals, particularly between ages 2-3
- Children with other language delays may also show pronoun reversal
- The difference in autism is one of **degree and persistence**: reversals are more frequent, occur beyond early development, and require more cognitive resources to resolve
- What may be more specific to autism is the **combination** of pronoun reversal with pronoun avoidance (using names instead of pronouns) and with broader deictic difficulties

**Sources:**
- ["You're telling me!" (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4834724/)
- [Individual differences in pronoun reversal: Evidence from two longitudinal case studies](https://www.mq.edu.au/__data/assets/pdf_file/0008/910088/Individual-differences-in-pronoun-reversal.pdf)

## 2. Deictic Shifting and Perspective-Taking

### 2.1 What is Deictic Shifting?

Every utterance is anchored to a **deictic center** -- the speaker's "I-here-now." When someone else speaks, the entire deictic center shifts: their "I" is now the reference point, their "here" is the spatial anchor, their "now" is the temporal anchor. This constant re-anchoring is called **deictic shifting** and is required for every conversational turn.

Deictic shifting is cognitively demanding because it requires:
1. **Tracking who is currently speaking** (to interpret "I" and "you")
2. **Maintaining awareness of multiple spatial reference points** (to interpret "here" and "there")
3. **Updating temporal anchors** (to interpret "now" and "then")
4. **Suppressing one's own default perspective** when taking someone else's viewpoint

### 2.2 Research on Deictic Shifting in Autism

The Mizuno et al. (2011) fMRI study is the landmark investigation:

- Adults with high-functioning autism and neurotypical controls performed a linguistic perspective-taking task requiring deictic shifting
- Autism participants showed **reliably slower and less accurate behavioral responses**
- The key neural finding: **diminished functional connectivity between the right anterior insula and precuneus** during deictic shifting
- This connectivity reduction was specific to the "you" condition (where "you" referred to the participant and required self-reference) -- **reference to one's own perspective was specifically harder when invoked by someone else's deictic expression**
- The right anterior insula supports **attention to internal states** while the precuneus supports **self-other representation**, and their collaboration appears critical for deictic shifting

The study demonstrated that "deictic shifting constitutes a challenge for adults with high-functioning autism, particularly when reference to one's self is involved."

**Sources:**
- [The neural basis of deictic shifting in linguistic perspective-taking in high-functioning autism (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3155703/)
- [New brain imaging research reveals why autistic individuals confuse pronouns | ScienceDaily](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2011/08/110801122956.htm)

### 2.3 Deictic Shifting in Narrative

Retelling a story from different characters' perspectives requires extensive deictic shifting -- the narrator must adopt each character's I-here-now center. Research findings:

- Fictional narratives are especially challenging for autistic individuals because they require "generating fictional scenarios, understanding characters' motivations, and predicting outcomes"
- Autistic narrators show lower **causal-motivational coherence** in life narratives, which may relate to difficulties tracking perspective shifts
- A pilot training study found that when autistic adults practiced retelling stories from the original storyteller's perspective while attending to embedded thoughts and emotions, they showed "significant improvements in mentalizing about others' beliefs and in narrative coherence"
- This suggests that explicit practice with deictic perspective-taking can improve both narrative and social understanding

**Sources:**
- [A narrative sequencing and mentalizing training for adults with autism: A pilot study (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9433774/)
- [Narrative identity differences in autism | Scientific Reports](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-00178-0)
- [Narrative production abilities of children with autism](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/23969415251321824)

### 2.4 Comprehension vs. Production

An important distinction emerges in the research:

- **Production** (generating the right deictic term) appears more challenging than **comprehension** (understanding what someone else's deictic term refers to)
- Eye-tracking studies reveal processing differences even when behavioral accuracy is similar: "The ASD group had a tendency to look at the subject referent to a greater extent than the NT group" during pronoun resolution, suggesting different processing strategies even when arriving at correct answers
- "Although the ASD group is capable of interpreting pronouns like the NT group, processing differences between the two groups may lead to divergences in production"
- Some autistic individuals can comprehend deictic terms but struggle more with the rapid, online production demands of conversation

**Sources:**
- [Structural and Contextual Cues in Third-person Pronoun Interpretation by Children with ASD and their Neurotypical Peers (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7878583/)
- [Reference Production in Young Speakers with and without Autism (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3668432/)

### 2.5 The Connection to Theory of Mind

Deictic processing and Theory of Mind are deeply intertwined but not identical:

- Deictic frames "have been found to underlie complex behavior, including perspective-taking"
- ToM tasks rely heavily on spoken language, "with nearly three-quarters of variance in False Belief task performance predictable by vocabulary facility and grammar appreciation"
- **However:** Training on deictic relations (I-you, here-there, now-then) using the Barnes-Holmes protocol "did not lead to increases in measures of perspective-taking (e.g., ToM) in autistic individuals" in several studies
- This suggests that deictic language processing and Theory of Mind may share some underlying mechanisms but improving one does not automatically improve the other
- The relationship may be that deictic language **indexes** perspective-taking ability rather than **constituting** it

**Sources:**
- [Deictic Relational Responding and Perspective-Taking in Autistic Individuals: A Scoping Review (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11035524/)
- [Examining the Impact of Deictic Relational Responding on Advanced Theory of Mind and Pretense in Children with Autism](https://digitalcommons.usf.edu/etd/6185/)

## 3. Spatial Deixis in Autism

### 3.1 "Here/There" and "This/That"

The Hobson, Garcia-Perez, and Lee (2010) study is the most direct investigation of spatial deixis in autism:

**Study 1 (Production):**
- Most autistic children spontaneously produced deictic terms, often with pointing
- **Key finding:** Only among children with autism were there participants who **referred to distal locations using proximal terms** (saying "this" or "here" for things that were far away)
- Autistic children also made **atypical points with unusual precision**, "often lining-up with an eye" -- suggesting a focus on the object itself rather than the communicative act of directing attention

**Study 2 (Comprehension):**
- Children with autism were **less accurate in responding to instructions involving contrastive deictic terms** (e.g., "put it here, not there")
- Fewer autistic participants responded accurately to **indicative head nods** used as deictic gestures

The distal-as-proximal confusion is particularly interesting: it suggests that some autistic children may not consistently anchor spatial deixis to their own body as the reference point, instead focusing on the object being referred to.

**Sources:**
- [Person-Centred (Deictic) Expressions and Autism | Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10803-009-0882-5)
- [Deictic Terms | Springer Nature Link](https://link.springer.com/rwe/10.1007/978-3-319-91280-6_966)

### 3.2 Deictic Verbs: "Come" and "Go"

"Come" and "go" are deictic verbs -- "come" means movement toward the speaker's location, "go" means movement away from it. Their reference shifts with the speaker's position:

- Children with ASD show "difficulty in comprehension and production of the deictic verbs 'come/go'"
- A Japanese study found that intervention using multiple spatial configurations (face-to-face vs. side-by-side) with deictic gestures improved appropriate use of these verbs both in training and daily life
- The spatial relation between the two parties (who is where) and the accompaniment of gestures (moving arm toward/away from body) both aided comprehension

**Sources:**
- [Improving the Use of Deictic Verbs in Children with Autism Spectrum Disorder](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/17518423.2021.1964004)

### 3.3 Gesture and Deictic Reference

Pointing is the most fundamental deictic gesture, and differences in pointing are among the earliest signs of autism:

- **Deictic pointing is among the most impaired gestures in children with autism**
- Children with ASD produce **fewer pointing gestures overall** and fewer index finger points without contact with the referent
- Autistic children "pay little attention to pointing when no other social cues are available and fail to discriminate intentional pointing gestures from incidental ones"
- They "seem to grasp neither the spatial nor the social value of pointing" in some contexts
- However, when multiple deictic cues are combined (looking AND pointing, or looking AND verbalizing), autistic children show better response -- **redundant cues help**
- Pointing by an examiner "acts as a facilitator for gaze following and helps to attract attention to the face and referent object for a longer period of time"

**Sources:**
- [Different Ways of Making a Point: A Study of Gestural Communication in Typical and Atypical Early Development (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8246876/)
- [A scoping review of deictic gesture use in toddlers with or at-risk for autism spectrum disorder](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/322908243_A_scoping_review_of_deictic_gesture_use_in_toddlers_with_or_at-risk_for_autism_spectrum_disorder)
- [Attention to intentional versus incidental pointing gestures in young autistic children](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0022096521001235)

### 3.4 Egocentric vs. Allocentric Reference Frames

Research on spatial cognition in autism reveals a pattern relevant to spatial deixis:

- **Egocentric** reference (anchored to the self's body: "to my left") -- generally intact in autism, sometimes enhanced
- **Allocentric** reference (anchored to external landmarks or other people's positions: "to his left") -- more challenging
- ASD participants show "particular difficulties in performing viewpoint independent (allocentric) navigation, leaving viewpoint dependent navigation (egocentric) intact"
- Paradoxically, some evidence suggests "particular difficulty with using the self as a reference frame" when the self-reference must be dynamically updated (as in deictic shifting)
- Abstract spatial reasoning may be a strength, but **relative spatial language** ("in front of", "to the left of") shows selective impairment

The implication for deictic processing: spatial deixis requires constantly switching between what is "here" from your perspective versus someone else's perspective -- precisely the kind of dynamic self-other spatial mapping that is challenging.

**Sources:**
- [Allocentric Versus Egocentric Spatial Memory in Adults with Autism Spectrum Disorder (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5948263/)
- [Spatial language difficulties reflect the structure of intact spatial representation: Evidence from high-functioning autism](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0010028519302397)
- [Tell me where it is: Selective difficulties in spatial language on the autism spectrum (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7545647/)

## 4. Temporal Deixis in Autism

### 4.1 The Challenge of Shifting Time Words

Temporal deictic terms ("now," "then," "yesterday," "today," "tomorrow," "last week," "next month") anchor to the **moment of speaking**. "Yesterday" has no fixed date -- it means "the day before the day this utterance is produced." This makes temporal deixis doubly challenging because:

1. The reference shifts with every utterance
2. The temporal concepts themselves require understanding that **categories like "tomorrow" are transitory** -- today's "tomorrow" becomes tomorrow's "today" and the next day's "yesterday"

Research on autism and temporal understanding shows:
- Autistic children "find it hard to understand what 'yesterday' or 'tomorrow' means"
- "The position of events in the overall time continuum -- their inclusion in the before or after; or past, present and future categories of experience is transitory... This type of hierarchical temporal coding is likely deficient in autism"
- Autistic individuals may score significantly lower on assessments of thinking about future events ("How often does your child talk about or seem to think about what he/she will be doing tomorrow?")
- Concrete anchoring helps: "They need to be concretized by showing them the days on a calendar"

### 4.2 The "Now-Then" Deictic Frame

In Relational Frame Theory research, the now-then deictic relation is one of three core deictic frames (alongside I-you and here-there):

- "Now-then" requires understanding that a temporal reference point shifts depending on when the statement is made
- Training on now-then relations has been studied in autism interventions using the Barnes-Holmes protocol
- Teaching "then-later" and "here-there" relations to children with autism showed that these deictic temporal frames can be established through systematic instruction

**Sources:**
- [Teaching the Concept of Time - Autism Awareness](https://autismawarenesscentre.com/teaching-the-concept-of-time/)
- [Time perception and autistic spectrum condition: A systematic review (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6852160/)
- [Teaching "Then-Later" and "Here-There" Relations to Children with Autism (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6411558/)

## 5. Reference Tracking in Discourse

### 5.1 Following Pronoun Reference Across Extended Text

Tracking who "he" or "she" refers to across multiple sentences requires maintaining a mental model of the discourse -- who has been mentioned, who is currently in focus, and who the speaker most likely intends to reference. Research findings:

- Autistic individuals show differences in **referential establishment** (introducing new referents clearly) but not necessarily in **referential cohesion** (maintaining reference once established)
- "Autistic children produced significantly higher rates of ambiguous establishment than non-autistic peers" -- using "he" in a story with multiple male characters without clarifying which one
- This is specifically a **social pragmatic** issue: "They specifically struggle to clarify reference to someone who does not share their background knowledge"
- The difficulty is not with pronouns per se but with **modeling what the listener knows** -- understanding that if you introduce two male characters and then say "he," the listener doesn't know which one you mean

**Sources:**
- [Analysis of Noun Phrase Ambiguity in Narratives Reveals Differences in Referential Establishment But Not Cohesion for Older Autistic Children (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10555458/)
- [Reference Production in Young Speakers with and without Autism (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3668432/)

### 5.2 Comprehension of Pronoun Reference

- Eye-tracking data reveals **processing differences** even when behavioral accuracy matches: autistic readers/listeners may use different strategies for resolving who a pronoun refers to
- Autistic individuals show sensitivity to discourse context: "all participants exhibit fine-grained sensitivity to discourse context, and referential choices for all participants are modulated by factors related to the cognitive effort of language production"
- The challenge is more pronounced when resolution requires **pragmatic inference** (figuring out the most likely referent based on context and social reasoning) versus **structural cues** (grammar rules that constrain who a pronoun can refer to)
- Some autistic individuals "have a decreased ability to use context to process ambiguity and polysemy"

**Sources:**
- [Structural and Contextual Cues in Third-person Pronoun Interpretation by Children with ASD (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7878583/)
- [Is verbal reference impaired in autism spectrum disorder? A systematic review](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2396941518763166)
- [Production and comprehension of pronouns in individuals with autism: A meta-analysis and systematic review](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/1362361320949103)

### 5.3 Social vs. Linguistic Pragmatics in Reference

A crucial distinction:

- **Linguistic pragmatics** (context-independent rules): autistic children perform similarly to non-autistic peers
- **Social pragmatics** (listener/context-specific rules): autistic children show differences
- "Autistic children show differences in their application of social pragmatic principles, whereas their use of linguistic pragmatics is similar to that of non-autistic peers"
- For deictic reference, this means: the **grammatical rules** about what pronouns can refer to are often intact, but the **social awareness** of when to use a pronoun vs. a name (based on what the listener can be expected to track) is more challenging

**Sources:**
- [Some (but not all) Pragmatic Inferences are Difficult for Autistic Children](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10803-025-06838-4)

## 6. The Broader Perspective-Taking Connection

### 6.1 Deixis as Fundamentally Perspectival

All deictic reference is perspective-dependent:

- **Person deixis** (I/you): requires knowing who is speaking
- **Spatial deixis** (here/there, this/that): requires knowing where the speaker is
- **Temporal deixis** (now/then, today/tomorrow): requires knowing when the speaker is speaking
- **Social deixis** (formal/informal address): requires knowing the speaker's relationship to the addressee

Every deictic expression requires the listener to **reconstruct the speaker's perspective** to interpret it. This makes deixis one of the most perspective-demanding aspects of language.

### 6.2 The Fluidity-to-Inelasticity Hypothesis

Oakley & Vidanović (2014) in *Cognitive Semiotics* 7(2):191-225 proposed a theoretical framework specifically for autism and deixis:

- Typical deictic processing requires **fluid** shifting of reference points -- the ability to rapidly and flexibly update who the "I-here-now" center is
- In autism, there may be **inelasticity** in this shifting -- the deictic reference points are less fluid, harder to update, and more likely to "stick" to a particular configuration
- "Verbal autists possess many of the intersubjectivity skills required for normal conversation but lack the ability to deploy stable reference points when using the deictic scheme to communicate about situations in which either the topic or the audience are displaced in time and space"
- This is not an all-or-nothing deficit but a matter of **flexibility** -- the challenge increases when topics or audiences are displaced from the immediate context

**Sources:**
- [Deixis and personhood: Autism, intersubjectivity, and the fluidity-to-inelasticity hypothesis](https://www.degruyterbrill.com/document/doi/10.1515/cogsem-2014-0013/html?lang=en)

### 6.3 The Relational Frame Theory (RFT) Approach

Relational Frame Theory offers a systematic framework for understanding deictic skills:

- Three core deictic frames: **I-You**, **Here-There**, **Now-Then**
- Three levels of complexity:
  - **Simple:** "I have a red brick, you have a green brick. Which brick do I have?" (straightforward perspective identification)
  - **Reversed:** "If I was you and you were me, which brick would I have?" (single perspective swap)
  - **Double-reversed:** "If I was you and you were me, and if here was there and there was here, where would I be sitting?" (two simultaneous perspective swaps)
- The Barnes-Holmes Perspective Taking Protocol (BH-PTP) has been used to both assess and train these abilities in autism
- Training can successfully establish deictic relational responding in autistic individuals
- **However:** "Training on deictic relations did not lead to increases in measures of perspective-taking (e.g., ToM) in autistic individuals" -- suggesting that mastering the language of perspective does not automatically transfer to social perspective-taking

**Sources:**
- [Deictic Relational Responding and Perspective-Taking in Autistic Individuals: A Scoping Review (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11035524/)
- [Teaching a Deictic Relational Repertoire to Children with Autism](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40732-014-0078-z)
- [Establishing deictic repertoires in autism](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1750946715000288)

## 7. Practical Implications

### 7.1 Strategies for Clear Deictic Reference

**For autistic individuals navigating deictic language:**

1. **Use names when pronouns are ambiguous.** If a conversation involves multiple people of the same gender, using "Sarah" instead of "she" is clearer. This is not a deficit -- it is communicatively superior in many contexts.

2. **Ask for clarification when deictic reference is unclear.** Phrases like "When you say 'there,' do you mean [specific place]?" or "Who does 'he' refer to?" are entirely legitimate.

3. **Anchor temporal deixis to concrete markers.** Instead of relying on "yesterday" or "next week," you can translate to specific dates: "Do you mean Tuesday the 8th?" Calendars and planners can serve as concrete anchoring tools.

4. **Combine deictic gestures with verbal reference.** Research shows that redundant cues (pointing PLUS saying the name or location) aid comprehension. You can request this from others: "Can you point to which one you mean?"

5. **In ambiguous conversations, restate your understanding.** "So when you said 'she should do it,' you meant [Name], right?" This verifies your reference resolution without requiring real-time deictic processing under pressure.

### 7.2 How Digital Communication Changes Deictic Demands

Digital communication (text, email, messaging) alters the deictic landscape in ways that may be advantageous:

- **Person deixis is reduced:** In text, you generally know who is writing (the name is displayed), and many conversations are one-on-one, reducing ambiguity about who "I" and "you" refer to
- **Spatial deixis is largely eliminated:** "Here" and "there" rarely arise in text because participants are not co-located -- locations must be named explicitly
- **Temporal deixis persists but can be concretized:** Timestamps on messages provide concrete temporal anchoring; "today" in a message has a visible date
- **Processing time is available:** Text-based communication "eliminates the need to interpret nonverbal cues and allows for more time to process information and formulate responses"
- **Non-deictic cues are reduced:** The absence of pointing, head-nods, and gaze direction in text means that all reference must be made explicit -- which can actually be clearer

Research confirms: "Adults with autism spectrum disorder prefer written over verbal communication" and text-based formats reduce social anxiety while offering "predictability and control, allowing individuals time to organize thoughts, choose words, and reconsider meaning."

**Challenges that remain in digital communication:**
- Sarcasm and implied meaning can still be ambiguous
- Group chats can make it unclear who "you" addresses
- Emoji and tone indicators are still partially deictic (they modify meaning based on context)

**Sources:**
- ['Anything but the phone!': Communication mode preferences in the autism community](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/13623613211014995)
- [Autism Texting Habits: Communication in the Digital Age](https://neurolaunch.com/autism-texting-habits/)

### 7.3 When to Ask for Clarification

Situations where deictic reference is particularly likely to be ambiguous (and where clarification requests are appropriate):

- **Multiple people of the same gender in a story:** "He did X" -- which "he"?
- **"This" or "that" without a clear antecedent:** "We need to fix this" -- fix what specifically?
- **Temporal reference without context:** "Let's do it tomorrow" -- which day is being called "tomorrow"?
- **Spatial reference without shared location:** "Over there" -- relative to whom?
- **Reported speech or nested quotation:** "She said he told her that she..." -- the deictic centers multiply with each layer
- **Anaphoric reference after topic change:** When someone uses "it" after switching topics -- which "it"?

## 8. Strengths and Reframe

### 8.1 Autistic Preference for Explicit Reference as a Communicative Strength

The tendency to use names instead of pronouns, specific dates instead of "tomorrow," and explicit locations instead of "here/there" is often framed as a deficit or oddity. In reality:

- **Using names instead of pronouns eliminates ambiguity.** In any context with multiple possible referents, "Sarah took the folder to Mark" is objectively clearer than "She took it to him."
- **Explicit temporal reference prevents misunderstanding.** "Tuesday at 3pm" cannot be misinterpreted the way "the day after tomorrow in the afternoon" can.
- **Concrete spatial reference is more informative.** "In the kitchen cabinet" communicates more than "over there."
- **Precision in language reduces communication breakdown.** Research confirms that "literal communication reduces misunderstandings by ensuring that instructions and conversations are interpreted exactly as they are meant."

Technical, legal, and scientific writing all prize this kind of explicit reference over deictic shortcuts. The autistic tendency toward precision is an asset in these domains.

### 8.2 The Double Empathy Angle

The double empathy problem (Milton, 2012) reframes communication difficulties as bidirectional:

- NT speakers may **over-rely on deictic expressions** that assume shared context, shared spatial reference, and shared ability to track shifting pronouns
- When an NT speaker says "Can you hand me that thing over there?" to someone who doesn't share their spatial perspective or hasn't been tracking the same referents, the communication failure is on **both sides**
- "Many of the difficulties autistic individuals face when socializing with non-autistic individuals are due, in part, to mismatch and a lack of mutual understanding between the two groups"
- Autistic people "communicate more efficiently with other autistic people than they do with non-autistic people" -- possibly in part because autistic communicators tend to be more explicit and less deictic
- The so-called "deficit" in deictic processing could equally be described as: NT speakers under-specify their references, relying on assumptions about shared context that are not always warranted

**Sources:**
- [The double empathy problem | National Autistic Society](https://www.autism.org.uk/advice-and-guidance/professional-practice/double-empathy)
- [Double empathy, explained | The Transmitter](https://www.thetransmitter.org/spectrum/double-empathy-explained/)
- [Autism, Human Connection and the 'Double Empathy' Problem | Scientific American](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/autism-human-connection-and-the-double-empathy-problem/)

### 8.3 A Corpus-Based Perspective

One corpus study comparing the use of deixis in autistic vs. typically developing children found that "the frequency and type of deictic words uttered was relatively similar" between the groups. This challenges the notion of a categorical deictic deficit and supports the idea that differences may be more about:
- **Processing speed** (needing more time to compute deictic shifts) rather than inability
- **Specific contexts** (displaced/complex situations) rather than all deictic use
- **Production strategies** (preferring explicit reference) rather than comprehension failure

## 10. Summary of Key Findings

1. **Pronoun reversal** is more common and persistent in autism than typical development, but less common than historically assumed (~10% of pronoun use in children with ASD vs. ~1% in TD). It is best explained by the perspective-shifting account, with gestalt processing/echolalia contributing.

2. **Deictic shifting** -- the constant re-anchoring of I/here/now with every speaker change -- is measurably harder for autistic individuals, with neuroimaging showing reduced connectivity between self-awareness and self-other distinction brain regions.

3. **Spatial deixis** shows specific patterns: some autistic children use proximal terms for distal referents, are less accurate with contrastive deictic terms, and show reduced response to non-verbal deictic gestures like head-nods.

4. **Temporal deixis** is challenging because shifting time words ("yesterday," "tomorrow") require understanding that temporal categories are transitory and context-dependent.

5. **Reference tracking** in discourse shows differences in establishing clear reference (introducing who "he" is) more than in maintaining coherence once established. The challenge is specifically in modeling what the listener knows.

6. **Sign language evidence** from deaf autistic children supports the perspective-shifting account over the echolalia account: palm reversals occur (gestalt reproduction) but actual pointing-direction errors do not.

7. **Training deictic relations** (I-you, here-there, now-then) can be successful, but does not automatically transfer to broader Theory of Mind or social perspective-taking.

8. **The autistic preference for explicit reference** (names over pronouns, specific dates over temporal deictics, named locations over here/there) is a communicative **strength** that reduces ambiguity.

9. **Digital communication** reduces deictic demands in several ways (spatial deixis eliminated, temporal deixis concretized by timestamps, processing time available) and is preferred by many autistic adults.

10. **The double empathy perspective** suggests that NT over-reliance on deictic shortcuts that assume shared context is itself a communication weakness -- not just an autistic "deficit" in processing those shortcuts.
