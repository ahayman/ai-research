# Friendships and Mate Crime

## Pragmatic Manipulation and Self-Protection Hub — Context Page

> **Safety frame — read first**
>
> Friendship-based exploitation of autistic and other disabled adults has killed people. The case of **Steven Hoskin**, a 39-year-old man with learning disability murdered in 2006 by people who had befriended and exploited him, is one of the most cited cases in the UK disability hate crime literature. The Hoskin case is not isolated — it sits within a broader pattern that UK disability advocacy has named *mate crime*, in which a disabled adult is befriended by people who then exploit them financially, sexually, emotionally, and sometimes physically, often while presenting the relationship to outsiders as a normal friendship. The pattern has been documented in cases involving autism, intellectual disability, mental illness, and other disability categories, and the lethal endpoint is rare but real.
>
> If you are reading this page because:
>
> - A "friend" is currently living in your home in conditions you did not freely choose, or is using your housing, finances, vehicle, or benefits;
> - A friendship has become physically violent or has involved sexual coercion;
> - A friendship has produced threats — to harm you, to expose you, to harm your family or pets, to take things from you;
> - You have been groomed (over weeks, months, or years) into a relationship that now feels structurally inescapable;
> - A friend or group of friends is exploiting you in a context where law enforcement involvement may be needed;
>
> ...then the immediate priority is finding outside support, not the recognition framework on this page. The [Crisis Resources Directory](manipulation-hub-crisis-resources.md) lists context-specific options. Mate crime falls within disability hate crime in many jurisdictions and can be reported through the same channels as other hate crimes; in the UK, third-party reporting through disability advocacy organizations is one pathway when direct contact with police feels unsafe. Adult Protective Services (US) or Safeguarding Adults (UK) handle exploitation of vulnerable adults, with the same caveats about guardianship treated on the [Family of Origin](manipulation-hub-context-family-of-origin.md) page. Disability rights organizations (ASAN, AWN, NDRN in the US; Mencap, Dimensions, and the Ann Craft Trust in the UK; equivalents elsewhere) can sometimes help.
>
> The rest of this page is built on top of this safety frame.

## 1. Why This Page Exists

The cultural script of "friendship" treats friendships as categorically positive relationships chosen freely by both parties for mutual benefit. The script is so deeply held that the possibility of friendship-based exploitation is often invisible to outside observers, sometimes invisible to the person being exploited, and structurally hard to name even when it is recognized. Friendships do not have the legal frameworks that surround intimate partner relationships and family-of-origin relationships. They do not have the protective infrastructure (DV hotlines, domestic abuse shelters, custody courts) that even imperfectly serves intimate partner survivors. The patterns this hub treats can operate through friendship-based relationships with relatively few institutional brakes, and the autistic adult who is being exploited by a "friend" often arrives at the recognition late and with fewer resources to act on it.

This page treats friendship contexts as a category that warrants its own page because:

1. **The empirical literature documents disproportionate friendship-based victimization of autistic adults.** The Vulnerability Experiences Quotient research (Griffiths et al. 2019), the Pearson research program (particularly Pearson, Rees & Forster 2022, "'This Was Just How This Friendship Worked'"), and the broader autism-IPV literature all include friendship-based exploitation as a documented pattern.
2. **The UK disability advocacy concept of "mate crime"** names a specific recognizable pattern that the broader IPV literature does not capture — the slow grooming of a disabled adult into a friendship that is then used as cover for financial, sexual, physical, and emotional exploitation, sometimes culminating in physical injury or death. The mate crime concept has been adopted into UK police practice and disability hate crime legislation, but the framework is much less developed in other jurisdictions.
3. **The cultural script of friendship makes recognition particularly hard.** The autistic adult who is being exploited by a friend often cannot easily recognize the exploitation because the relationship is, by definition, "just a friendship," and friendship does not have the recognized red flags that intimate partnerships have.
4. **The autistic-autistic friendship dimension complicates the picture.** Crompton et al. (2020) on autistic peer-to-peer communication, and the broader autistic community literature on autistic friendship, document that autistic-autistic friendships can be highly valuable. They are also not categorically safer than autistic-allistic friendships, and the assumption that autistic peers are inherently safe is itself a vulnerability the patterns can exploit.
5. **Friendships are often the protective resource the rest of the hub recommends building.** The reality-check network strategy depends on having trustworthy relationships outside the harm-causing system. When the harm-causing system is itself a friendship, the protective recommendation has to be calibrated around the recognition that not all friendships are safe.

The reframe this page offers, in one sentence: **friendship is not a category that confers safety. Friendships can be exploitative, manipulative, and dangerous, and the protective work is the work of distinguishing the friendships that are protective from the friendships that are not — without collapsing into either the "all friends are safe" cultural script or the over-correction that treats all friendships as suspect.**

## 2. Lethality, Mate Crime, and Immediate Safety

This section treats the safety dimension first.

### 2.1 Mate crime: the UK disability advocacy framework

**Mate crime** is the term developed in UK disability advocacy contexts to describe a specific pattern of exploitation in which a disabled adult is befriended by people who then exploit them. The term originated in the broader disability hate crime literature and was developed through case analysis, advocacy work, and police training in the UK.

**Key sources:**
- **Thomas, P. (2011).** "'Mate crime': Ridicule, hostility and targeted attacks against disabled people." *Disability & Society* 26(1): 107–111.
- **Doherty, P. (2015), Quarmby, K., and other UK disability rights advocates** have written extensively on the mate crime pattern.
- **Mencap and Dimensions** (UK learning disability and autism advocacy organizations) have published guidance and case studies on mate crime.
- **The Ann Craft Trust** specializes in safeguarding adults at risk and has published on disability-targeted exploitation including mate crime.
- **The Hoskin case** (2006): Steven Hoskin, a 39-year-old man with learning disability in Cornwall, was befriended and then exploited by people who eventually killed him. The case prompted significant reform of UK safeguarding practice and is one of the most-cited mate crime cases.

**The pattern, as documented in the case literature:**

1. **Identification.** The perpetrators identify a disabled adult as a target — often someone who is socially isolated, who lives alone, who has access to housing or benefits, who shows signs of wanting friendship.
2. **Befriending.** The perpetrators offer friendship — sometimes intense, sometimes warm, often filling a real need the target had for social connection. The early stages of the relationship feel like a long-overdue welcome into a peer group.
3. **Gradual exploitation.** The perpetrators begin extracting resources — money, food, housing access, possessions, services, sometimes sexual contact. Each individual extraction is small enough to be plausibly framed as "what friends do for each other."
4. **Escalation.** The exploitation increases. The target's home may be taken over (a pattern sometimes called *cuckooing*, particularly when associated with drug operations). The target may be physically assaulted, sometimes repeatedly. Threats prevent the target from disclosing.
5. **Isolation.** The target's other relationships — family, support workers, prior friends — are severed or undermined. The mate crime perpetrators become the sole social context.
6. **In severe cases**, the target is killed. The Hoskin case and others document this endpoint.

**Evidence quality:** [VALIDATED] within the UK case and policy literature. The pattern is documented in case files, Serious Case Reviews, police reports, and academic disability studies work. The empirical generalizability outside the UK is less established because the framework is less developed in other countries, but the same pattern appears in disability rights literature globally under different names.

**What this means for safety:**

- **Mate crime is reportable as a hate crime** in the UK and in jurisdictions with disability hate crime legislation. Third-party reporting through disability organizations is available when direct police contact feels unsafe.
- **Safeguarding Adults processes (UK) and equivalent processes elsewhere** can sometimes provide support, with the caveats about institutional safeguarding processes that the Family of Origin page treats.
- **Mencap, Dimensions, the Ann Craft Trust, and similar organizations** have specific expertise in mate crime cases and can sometimes intervene or refer.
- **In the US**, similar patterns exist but the legal framework is less developed. APS (Adult Protective Services) is the formal pathway for vulnerable adult exploitation, with the same caveats about guardianship triggering treated elsewhere in this hub.

### 2.2 Cuckooing and home takeover

A specific severe form of mate crime that warrants its own treatment: *cuckooing* refers to the pattern in which exploiters take over a vulnerable adult's home, often using it as a base for drug operations, sex work, or other illegal activity. The disabled tenant is sometimes treated as a hostage in their own home, sometimes treated as a participant in the activity, sometimes physically harmed, and almost always unable to leave the situation safely.

**The pattern:**
- The perpetrators move into the disabled adult's home, sometimes invited (as friends or romantic partners) and sometimes by intimidation.
- The home becomes the staging ground for the perpetrators' activities.
- The disabled tenant loses control over who enters, what happens, when they can sleep, what they can eat.
- The tenant is sometimes assaulted, sometimes coerced into illegal activity, sometimes documented in police records as a co-perpetrator rather than as a victim.
- Reporting is dangerous because the perpetrators are physically present and because the disabled tenant may be implicated in the activity occurring in the home.

**Evidence quality:** [VALIDATED] in the UK case literature and police practice; [PRELIMINARY] for autism-specific prevalence but documented in disability advocacy sources.

**What this means for safety:** if cuckooing is occurring or has occurred, the protective response involves coordinated work with police, housing authorities, and disability safeguarding services. The disabled tenant is often treated as a victim under modern UK police practice (the "vulnerable adult" framing has been formalized in some forces), but historic practice frequently treated cuckooing victims as criminals. Outside support — disability rights organizations, victim advocacy services, sometimes legal representation — is generally needed.

### 2.3 Sexual exploitation in friendship contexts

A specific form of friendship-based exploitation that warrants explicit treatment. The autism-IPV literature documents disproportionate rates of sexual victimization of autistic adults, including by people the survivor considered friends. The Cazalis et al. (2022) finding that nine in ten autistic women in their sample reported sexual violence (PMC9087551) includes friendship contexts in the sample.

**The patterns documented:**
- **Grooming-style exploitation** in which a friendship is established over time and sexual contact is introduced gradually, sometimes under the framing that the contact is "what friends do" or "what people who really care about each other do."
- **Coerced sexual contact** during friendships, sometimes framed as obligation, sometimes as the price of continued friendship, sometimes as the friend "doing you a favor" or "being patient with you."
- **Sexual exploitation of autistic adults who have not had prior consensual sexual experience** and who have been told that this is what consensual sex looks like.
- **Sexual assault followed by social-circle isolation**, in which the survivor's friend group sides with the perpetrator and the survivor loses access to the social network they had.
- **Trafficking patterns** in which the friendship is the entry point to commercial sexual exploitation. The autistic community is at elevated risk for trafficking patterns because of the social isolation and the desire for connection.

**Evidence quality:** [VALIDATED] for the broader autistic sexual victimization rates; [PRELIMINARY] for the specific friendship-context patterns, with strong qualitative support from the Pearson research program and from autistic survivor accounts.

**What this means for safety:** sexual violence in friendship contexts has the same lethality and trauma implications as sexual violence in other contexts, and the same disclosure decisions apply (Ullman 2010). Sexual assault hotlines and victim advocacy services treat friendship-context sexual violence as sexual violence, and the legal frameworks generally apply regardless of the relationship between the parties at the time of the assault.

### 2.4 Financial exploitation in friendship contexts

Friendship-based financial exploitation is the most prevalent form of mate crime and one of the most documented forms of vulnerable-adult abuse generally. The patterns include:

- **Loans that are never repaid**, requested repeatedly, sometimes draining the autistic person's savings or putting them into debt.
- **Cohabitation arrangements** in which the friend lives in the autistic person's home without contributing fairly to expenses, sometimes consuming food, utilities, and other resources beyond their share.
- **Joint accounts and access to finances** that the friend then uses to extract money.
- **Coerced purchases** — being pressured to buy things for the friend, to give the friend access to credit cards, to take out loans on the friend's behalf.
- **Benefits fraud** in which the friend uses the autistic person's identity or benefit eligibility for the friend's gain.
- **Will and estate manipulation** — the friend pressuring the autistic person to change their will, name the friend as beneficiary, or transfer assets.
- **Inheritance theft** — when the autistic person inherits from a family member, the "friend" appears to help manage the inheritance and gradually extracts it.

**The protective implication:** financial exploitation in friendship contexts is sometimes the entry point to recognizing the broader exploitation pattern. When you notice that you have lost track of your money, that you cannot account for what was spent, that you have been asked for "loans" repeatedly without repayment, the financial pattern is information about the relationship even if other dimensions of the relationship still feel ambiguous.

## 3. Why Friendship Contexts Are Different for Autistic Readers

This section names the dimensions in which friendship contexts differ from other contexts in this hub.

### 3.1 The cultural script of friendship as categorically positive

The strongest structural feature of friendship contexts: the cultural default treats friendships as inherently good relationships chosen by both parties for mutual benefit. The script is so deeply held that:

- **The possibility of exploitative friendship is invisible** to most outside observers. When the autistic adult tries to disclose that a friend is harming them, the listener's default reading is "you must be misunderstanding the friendship" or "real friends sometimes have hard moments" or "you're lucky to have any friends, why are you being so critical?"
- **The autistic adult often cannot recognize the exploitation** because the relationship is, by definition, "just a friendship," and friendship does not have the institutional definitions or red flags that intimate partnerships have.
- **The cultural script provides cover** for the exploiter, who can present the relationship to third parties, to police, and to courts as a normal friendship that the disabled person is misinterpreting.
- **Sweet's (2019) sociology of gaslighting framework applies** — the cultural script is the structural scaffolding that makes the manipulation effective. The exploiter does not have to argue that the relationship is benign; the cultural default already says so.

The protective implication: **recognizing that friendships can be exploitative is itself a protective move**, because the cultural default does not include this recognition. The recognition does not mean treating all friendships as suspect; it means having the category "exploitative friendship" available as a possibility that can be applied when warranted.

### 3.2 Smaller social networks raise the stakes of any individual friendship

The Vulnerability Experiences Quotient research (Griffiths et al. 2019, PMC6851759) and the broader social-isolation literature document that autistic adults often have smaller social networks than non-autistic adults. The implication for friendship-based exploitation:

- **Each individual friendship matters more** when there are fewer of them. The cost of losing a friend is higher for someone with two friends than for someone with twenty.
- **The threat of friendship withdrawal is a more effective leverage tool** when alternative friendships are not readily available. "If you don't do this, I'll stop being your friend" is a much more serious threat in a small social world.
- **Reality-check resources are reduced.** The autistic adult who has fewer friends has fewer outside perspectives on the friendships they have, which means exploitative patterns have fewer external observers to interrupt them.
- **Building new friendships is structurally harder** for many autistic adults due to communication differences, sensory tolerance for typical friendship-building environments (bars, parties, group activities), and the credibility discount in initial encounters. The autistic adult who loses friends through exit from an exploitative friendship sometimes cannot easily replace them.

The protective implication: the autistic adult navigating friendship exploitation faces a structural challenge that the standard advice ("you can always make new friends") does not address. Building reality-check support outside the friendship system is often the prior step before the exploitative friendship can be safely addressed.

### 3.3 The autistic-autistic friendship dimension

A specific dimension that warrants its own treatment because it is sometimes assumed away by autistic community discourse: **autistic-autistic friendships are not categorically safer than autistic-allistic friendships.** The Crompton et al. (2020) finding that autistic-to-autistic communication is highly effective is empirically valid and is one of the most useful findings for autistic peer support. It does not mean that autistic peers are categorically safe.

**The patterns:**
- Autistic adults can exploit other autistic adults in all the ways non-autistic adults can.
- The "shared identity" of autism can become the trust mechanism that the exploiter uses — "we autistic people have to stick together" or "you can trust me, I get it."
- The desire for autistic community can make the autistic adult vulnerable to being targeted by people who present as autistic peers but who do not behave as good-faith community members.
- Some self-identified autistic peers in friendship contexts are not actually autistic, are not fully integrated into autistic community norms, or are running patterns that the autistic community has named but that the individual target may not yet recognize.
- The cultural discourse of "the autistic community as a refuge" can produce defensive responses to recognition that some autistic-autistic friendships are exploitative — the recognition feels like betraying the community.

**Evidence quality:** [COMMUNITY KNOWLEDGE] strongly supported by autistic survivor accounts; [PRELIMINARY] for any specific empirical study of autistic-autistic friendship exploitation, which has not been a research priority.

**The protective implication:** the autistic peer reality-check strategy that this hub recommends throughout is still the best available — but the recommendation is for **autistic peers in general**, not for any specific autistic person. A specific autistic friend who is exhibiting the patterns in this page is not a protective resource just because they are autistic. Distinguishing protective autistic peer support from exploitative autistic friendship is part of the same diagnostic work as distinguishing supportive non-autistic relationships from exploitative ones.

### 3.4 The grooming dynamic and the slow build

A specific structural feature of mate crime and friendship-based exploitation: the relationship usually develops slowly over time, in a pattern that is recognized in the broader grooming literature. The slow build matters because:

- **Each individual step seems benign at the time it happens.** The exploiter does not arrive at the relationship asking for the autistic adult's house keys; they arrive offering to help with a small task and gradually expand the relationship over weeks or months or years.
- **The autistic adult's recognition lags the relationship's actual nature.** By the time the exploitation is severe enough to be obviously exploitative, the autistic adult has already invested time, trust, and emotional connection, and the relationship feels like an established part of their life.
- **The cultural script of "real friendship grows over time"** legitimizes the slow build. The autistic adult cannot easily distinguish "this friendship is becoming closer" (a normal friendship trajectory) from "this friendship is gradually consuming more of my resources, time, and autonomy" (the grooming trajectory).
- **The exit cost increases as the relationship deepens.** Stepping back from a relationship that has been "growing" for a year is much harder than stepping back from a relationship that just started.

**The protective implication:** noticing the trajectory matters. *"How is this relationship different from how it was six months ago? Are the changes things I would have agreed to if they had been proposed all at once? What am I getting from this relationship that I wasn't getting before, and what am I giving that I wasn't giving before?"* The trajectory questions can sometimes reveal the grooming pattern in time.

### 3.5 The "this is just how this friendship worked" naming

Pearson, Rees, & Forster (2022) titled their qualitative study **"'This Was Just How This Friendship Worked': Experiences of Interpersonal Victimization Among Autistic Adults"** because the phrase recurred in their interviews. The phrase captures a specific autism-relevant pattern: the autistic adult, looking back on a friendship that was clearly exploitative in retrospect, describes their experience inside the friendship as treating the exploitative dynamics as normal — "this was just how this friendship worked," with the implicit understanding that the autistic person had assumed all friendships might work this way and therefore did not question it.

**The pattern:**
- The autistic adult enters the friendship without strong baseline expectations about what friendships should be like.
- The friendship establishes a particular dynamic — sometimes from the start, sometimes through gradual normalization.
- The autistic adult adapts to the dynamic, sometimes uncomfortably, but does not have a strong reference point for "this is wrong" because the comparison group of healthy friendships is not available.
- The recognition that the dynamic was wrong often comes later, after the friendship ends or after the autistic adult has access to other reference points.

**The implication:** the lack of healthy friendship reference points is itself a vulnerability. Many autistic adults benefit from explicit articulation of what healthy friendships look like — not as a rigid template but as a set of features that can be checked against current relationships. *Reciprocity over time. Mutual investment. The freedom to disagree without losing the friendship. Respect for each other's other relationships. The absence of leverage and threat. Explicit care for each other's wellbeing without conditions. Joy that is not transactional.* These features are not all present in every healthy friendship, but their consistent absence in a friendship over time is information.

### 3.6 The friendship-to-romantic-relationship boundary blur

A specific autism-relevant complication: many autistic adults report that they have difficulty distinguishing friendship from romantic relationship, particularly when the other person is sending mixed signals or when the cultural scripts for the two are unclear. The complication matters for friendship-based exploitation because:

- **Sexual contact in a friendship** can be framed by the perpetrator as "what friends sometimes do" or as "moving the friendship to the next level" or as "you wanted this." The autistic adult who was uncertain about the nature of the relationship may not recognize the contact as boundary-crossing.
- **Romantic attachment in a one-sided friendship** can leave the autistic adult emotionally invested in someone who is exploiting them and who has not reciprocated the romantic interest.
- **The "are we dating?" question** is often unanswered in autistic-allistic relationships because the allistic side is not communicating the answer clearly and the autistic side is not pressing for clarity.

**The protective implication:** explicit communication about the nature of the relationship is one of the protective moves. *"Are we friends, or are we dating?"* is a sentence that some autistic adults find uncomfortable to ask but that resolves a specific kind of ambiguity that exploitative relationships sometimes use. The right to ask the question and to receive a direct answer is part of the protective work.

### 3.7 The autism-specific friendship failure modes

A more general category of autism-specific friendship dynamics that are not always exploitation but that can shade into it under certain conditions:

- **The "fixer" friend** who has decided that they will help the autistic adult become more "normal" and who treats the friendship as a project of self-improvement for the autistic person. The fixer may be sincere, and the relationship may not be exploitative in the formal sense, but the underlying dynamic can be the compliance trap (see [The Compliance Trap](manipulation-hub-pattern-compliance-trap.md), section 3.7 — the "concerned friend" subtype).
- **The "savior" friend** who positions themselves as the autistic adult's protector, often in ways that produce resource dependency the autistic adult did not choose.
- **The "I'm one of the good ones" friend** who positions their autism allyship as a credential and uses the credential to override the autistic adult's account of their relationship dynamics. (See [Performative Allyship](manipulation-hub-pattern-performative-allyship.md).)
- **The "high-investment" friend** who pours intense attention and care into the friendship in the early stages, creating an emotional dependency that the autistic adult is then loath to lose. The early investment can be sincere or can be a grooming pattern; distinguishing them sometimes takes time.
- **The friend group dynamics** in which the autistic adult is included but never quite equal — the butt of jokes, the person whose contributions are dismissed, the person whose preferences are overruled. The friend group can be the social context the autistic adult depends on while also being the context that is harming them.

These are not always exploitation. Some are the normal friction of cross-neurotype friendship. Some are misguided but well-meaning. Some are the early stages of the patterns the rest of this page treats. The distinguishing tools in section 6 apply.

### 3.8 The disclosure asymmetry in friendship contexts

Ullman (2010) on disclosure asymmetry — negative responses to disclosure harm more than positive responses help — applies to friendship contexts as much as to sexual assault disclosure contexts. The friendship-specific dimension:

- **Friends are often the audience the autistic adult would naturally turn to for reality-check** about a problem in another friendship. When the friends are part of a shared friend group with the harm-causing friend, the disclosure to one friend can trigger the entire group's response.
- **The friend group's response is often defensive of the harm-causing friend** when the autistic adult discloses, particularly when the harm-causing friend has been distributing their own version of events over time (the bandwagon isolation pattern; see [Bandwagon Isolation](manipulation-hub-pattern-bandwagon-isolation.md)).
- **The autistic adult disclosing exploitative friendship to friends who do not believe them** loses both the harm-causing friend and the friends they disclosed to. The cost of disclosure can be the entire social network at once.

**The protective implication:** disclosure decisions in friendship contexts should be made strategically, with particular attention to whether the audience is likely to side with the harm-causing friend. The Disclosure Decisions strategy page applies; the friendship-specific calibration is that disclosure to mutual friends is often the highest-risk audience and is sometimes worth deferring until reality-check support outside the friend group is established.

## 4. The Patterns Most Common in Friendship Contexts

### 4.1 Weaponized accommodations and resource extraction

The [Weaponized Accommodations](manipulation-hub-pattern-weaponized-accommodations.md) page treats the broader pattern. In friendship contexts, the pattern often takes the form of *the friend providing accommodations that are then cited as the basis for ongoing extraction*.

**Examples:**
- The friend drives the autistic adult to medical appointments and then cites the driving as a basis for asking for money, time, or other resources.
- The friend helps the autistic adult navigate a bureaucratic process and then leverages the help into ongoing access.
- The friend includes the autistic adult in social activities and then uses the inclusion as the implied debt that justifies later extraction.
- The friend offers emotional support during a difficult time and then references the support indefinitely as the reason the autistic adult owes them.

**The friendship-specific feature:** unlike intimate partner contexts where the accommodations are formally part of the relationship, friendship accommodations are often informal favors that are then converted into a debt structure. The conversion is the manipulation; the original favor may have been benign.

### 4.2 Bandwagon isolation through friend groups

The [Bandwagon Isolation](manipulation-hub-pattern-bandwagon-isolation.md) page treats the broader pattern. In friendship contexts, the chorus is often the friend group itself — the friends who can be marshaled to back the harm-causing friend's version of events.

**The friendship-specific feature:** the friend group sometimes pre-exists the harm-causing friendship and sometimes is built around it. In either case, the autistic adult who wants to push back finds themselves facing not one friend but a coordinated social network. The cost of dispute is the entire network.

### 4.3 Concern trolling and the "good friend" frame

The [Concern Trolling](manipulation-hub-pattern-concern-trolling.md) page treats the broader pattern. In friendship contexts, concern trolling often takes the form of *concerned friends* who are very worried about the autistic adult's life choices, mental health, relationships, career, or independence — and whose concern is structurally indistinguishable from criticism wrapped in care.

**The friendship-specific feature:** the "good friend" frame is one of the highest-status social positions, and friends who cast themselves as "the only one who really cares about you" often acquire significant leverage over the autistic adult's decisions. The leverage is then used to direct the autistic adult's life in ways that serve the friend rather than the autistic adult.

### 4.4 Diagnostic weaponization in friendship contexts

The [Diagnostic Weaponization](manipulation-hub-pattern-diagnostic-weaponization.md) page treats the broader pattern. In friendship contexts, particularly in autistic-autistic friendships, diagnostic weaponization can take the form of one friend claiming superior insight into the other friend's autism — "you're just doing the autistic thing where you misread my intention" or "your autism is making this harder than it needs to be" or "I know how to handle your autism better than you do."

**The friendship-specific feature:** the claim is hard to refute when both parties are autistic and one party has constructed themselves as the more "self-aware" or "experienced" autistic. The autistic-autistic dynamic does not protect against this form.

### 4.5 The compliance trap in friendship contexts

The [Compliance Trap](manipulation-hub-pattern-compliance-trap.md) page treats the broader pattern. In friendship contexts, the trap appears when a friend repeatedly tells the autistic adult that they should mask better, fit in more, stop stimming around others, work harder on their social skills — with the implicit promise that successful changes will produce better friendship outcomes.

**The friendship-specific feature:** the friend is often using the friendship as the venue for the compliance demand — *"if you really wanted to be good friends with me, you'd work on this"* — and the friendship is held as the reward conditional on compliance.

### 4.6 Gaslighting in friendship contexts

The [Gaslighting](manipulation-hub-pattern-gaslighting.md) page treats the broader pattern. In friendship contexts, gaslighting often operates through small, accumulated rewrites of shared history and shared agreements. *"That's not what we agreed at lunch."* *"You were the one who suggested that, not me."* *"You're remembering it wrong, again."*

**The friendship-specific feature:** the autistic adult's memory of the friendship is often the only record of what happened. There is rarely contemporaneous written documentation of friendship interactions in the way there is for workplace or institutional contexts. The protective move of documentation is structurally harder.

### 4.7 Other patterns in friendship contexts

Briefly:
- **[DARVO](manipulation-hub-pattern-darvo.md)** — when the autistic adult raises a concern about the friend, the response often follows the deny-attack-reverse pattern.
- **[Tone Policing](manipulation-hub-pattern-tone-policing.md)** — friends who critique how the autistic adult communicates rather than what they are communicating about.
- **[Sensory Gaslighting](manipulation-hub-pattern-sensory-gaslighting.md)** — friends who deny the autistic adult's sensory needs in friendship environments (loud restaurants, bright clubs, crowded events).
- **[Information Asymmetry Exploitation](manipulation-hub-pattern-information-asymmetry-exploitation.md)** — when the friend manages logistics or finances for the autistic adult and uses the information as leverage.
- **[Memory Gaslighting](manipulation-hub-pattern-memory-gaslighting.md)** — particularly damaging in friendship contexts because of the lack of contemporaneous documentation.
- **[Performative Allyship](manipulation-hub-pattern-performative-allyship.md)** — friends who position their autism allyship credentials as the proof they could not be doing harm.

## 5. The Most Dangerous Configurations

### 5.1 The mate crime + cuckooing configuration

**Components:** described in section 2.1 and 2.2 above. Befriending leads to gradual exploitation, exploitation escalates to home takeover, the autistic adult is trapped in their own home with people who are exploiting them physically, financially, and sometimes sexually.

**The protective response:** disability hate crime reporting, safeguarding adults processes, disability rights legal support, and (in immediate danger) emergency services. The Crisis Resources Directory lists context-specific options.

### 5.2 The "best friend" + isolation + financial extraction configuration

**Components:**
- A single friend establishes themselves as the autistic adult's closest friend.
- The friend gradually positions themselves as the only person who really understands or accepts the autistic adult.
- Other relationships in the autistic adult's life are framed as inadequate, problematic, or unsafe.
- The autistic adult's social world contracts to the single friend.
- The friend extracts money, time, attention, and resources from the autistic adult under the framing of close friendship.
- When the autistic adult tries to push back, the threat is the loss of the only friendship they have.

**Why this configuration is dangerous:** the structural dependency is total. The autistic adult cannot leave the friendship without losing the entire social world, and the single friend has unilateral control over the friendship's terms.

**The protective response:** building reality-check support outside the single friendship is the first move, and is often the longest-arc move because the friend may resist it. The strategies on the Reality-Check Networks page apply, with the recognition that the building has to be done carefully because the friend is likely to interfere with new relationships.

### 5.3 The friend group + mockery + scapegoating configuration

**Components:**
- The autistic adult is part of a friend group that includes them but treats them as a lower-status member.
- The autistic adult is the butt of jokes, often delivered with "we're just teasing" framings.
- The autistic adult's contributions to the group are dismissed, mocked, or appropriated.
- When the autistic adult expresses discomfort, the response is "you're being too sensitive, we love you, that's how we are with everyone."
- The autistic adult continues participating because the alternative is total isolation.
- The cumulative effect on the autistic adult's self-image is severe and is invisible to the friend group, who continue to think of themselves as inclusive friends.

**Why this configuration is dangerous:** it is structurally invisible from outside, the autistic adult has the framing "I have friends" that is technically true, and the cumulative emotional damage is not always apparent until much later. It is also one of the most common configurations and is often present alongside the more acute patterns elsewhere on this page.

**The protective response:** recognition is the first move. Naming the dynamic as the pattern it is, even just internally, interrupts the framing that this is just how friend groups work. Building friendships outside the group is often the longer-arc move. Sometimes leaving the group is the right call, and sometimes the right call is to maintain reduced engagement while building alternatives.

### 5.4 The grooming + sexual exploitation configuration

**Components:**
- A friend establishes a close friendship over weeks or months.
- The friendship gradually introduces physical contact that the autistic adult is uncertain about but does not refuse.
- Sexual contact is introduced in conditions that are framed as natural or expected.
- The autistic adult, who may not have prior consensual sexual experience, accepts the framing that this is normal.
- The friendship continues, with the sexual contact becoming a feature of it.
- Over time, the autistic adult recognizes that the contact was not consensual in the way the friend framed it.
- Disclosure is complicated by the friendship framing, by the friend's social network, and by the autistic adult's uncertainty about whether what happened "counted" as assault.

**Why this configuration is dangerous:** sexual violence in friendship contexts is one of the most common forms of sexual victimization of autistic adults, and the framing makes recognition and disclosure both harder than for stranger or formal-relationship contexts. The Cazalis et al. (2022) finding that nine in ten autistic women in their sample reported sexual violence (PMC9087551) includes friendship contexts in the sample.

**The protective response:** sexual violence in friendship contexts is sexual violence. The same crisis resources, the same legal frameworks, the same trauma-informed supports apply. The disclosure decision is audience-dependent (as for all disclosure decisions in this hub), and the friend group's likely response is one of the factors to weigh.

## 6. Distinguishing Exploitative Friendship from Difficult Friendship

Friendships are difficult sometimes. People in friendships make mistakes. The threshold between "difficult friendship" and "exploitative friendship" matters because the cost of error in either direction is high — over-correction risks discarding genuinely good friends; under-correction risks remaining in friendships that are causing harm.

### Signals that shift the probability *toward* exploitative friendship

- **Resources flow in one direction over time.** Money, time, energy, emotional labor, and attention move from you to the friend without comparable return. The asymmetry is stable, not occasional.
- **The patterns from the Tier 3 pattern pages are recognizable in the friendship.**
- **The friendship has changed in ways you would not have agreed to if proposed all at once.** The grooming trajectory is identifiable in retrospect.
- **You have lost other relationships during the course of the friendship**, particularly relationships the friend characterized as problematic.
- **The friendship's terms are set unilaterally by the other person.** When they want closeness, you provide it. When they want distance, you accept it. The reverse rarely happens.
- **You feel obligated to maintain the friendship** even when it is making you worse, because of what they have done for you, because of how lonely you would be without them, because of what they would say about you, or because of what they have on you.
- **You feel relief when they are unavailable** and apprehension when they want to see you.
- **The friendship has involved threats** — to harm you, to expose you, to take things from you, to harm people you care about, to spread information.
- **You have started lying to other people about the friendship**, downplaying its costs or its content, hiding the dynamic from other friends or family.
- **Your physical, mental, or financial health is deteriorating in patterns that track the friendship.**

### Signals that shift the probability *away* from exploitative friendship

- **Resources flow in both directions** over time, even if not always equally in any single moment.
- **You can disagree without losing the friendship.** Both of you can raise concerns and have them heard.
- **The friendship has not produced the loss of other relationships.** Your other friends and family are still in your life.
- **You feel warmth, joy, and ease in the friendship**, not primarily obligation, dread, or relief at distance.
- **The friend respects your other relationships and is glad for them**, rather than threatened or critical of them.
- **The friend has invested in your wellbeing in ways that cost them something**, not just symbolically.
- **The friendship has improved over time as you have both learned each other**, not deteriorated.

### When you are uncertain

- **Apply the trajectory question.** How is this friendship different from how it was a year ago? Are the changes things you would have agreed to if proposed all at once?
- **Apply the resources question.** Track, for a month, what flows in each direction. Money, time, energy, attention, emotional labor. Honestly. Not to argue with the friend about the asymmetry but to see it for yourself.
- **Get an outside read** from someone outside the friendship. An autistic peer who has navigated similar dynamics, a trauma-informed therapist, a trusted family member who knew you before the friendship.
- **Apply the response-to-naming test, carefully.** Try, in a moment of relative safety, to name a small concern about the friendship dynamic. The friend's response is informative. A real friend can hear "I'd like to talk about something that's bothering me" and engage with it; an exploitative one cannot.
- **Trust the cumulative weight of small signals.** No single signal is proof; the cluster across time is.

## 7. Strategies, Sequenced by Priority

### 7.1 First: Run the Safety Assessment

The Safety Assessment is the foundational filter. Friendships involving physical violence, sexual coercion, weapons, financial control combined with the inability to remove the friend from your home, or threats to harm you or your dependents are in safety territory and should be treated as such. Section 2 of this page treats the immediate-safety dimensions; the Safety Assessment is the broader framework.

### 7.2 Second: Build reality-check support outside the friendship

[Reality-Check Networks](manipulation-hub-strategy-reality-check-networks.md) is the foundational social strategy for friendship contexts. In particular:

- **Identify or build relationships outside the friend group** that contains the harm-causing friend. The relationships do not need to be deep; they need to be independent.
- **Prioritize autistic peer relationships** when possible, with the recognition that "autistic peer" does not categorically mean "safe peer" (see section 3.3).
- **Use online autistic communities** when local autistic peer relationships are not available.
- **Consider therapy** as one form of reality-check, with the autism-aware criterion.
- **Recognize that building reality-check support is itself a longer-arc strategy**, particularly if your social network has been shrunk by the friend over time.

### 7.3 Third: Document the patterns

[Documentation](manipulation-hub-strategy-documentation.md) applies. For friendship contexts, the documentation is often retrospective rather than contemporaneous because friendship interactions are not usually documented in real time. The retrospective documentation focuses on:

- **The trajectory.** How has the friendship changed over time? When did each major shift happen? What resources have moved in each direction?
- **Specific incidents that reflected the patterns.** Direct quotes if you remember them. Dates if you can reconstruct them. Witnesses if any were present.
- **Financial flows.** This is one of the few friendship dimensions that is easier to document because banks have records. Going through the financial trail of a friendship can be revealing and is sometimes the basis for legal action if exploitation is severe.
- **Patterns observed by other people.** Notes from conversations with friends or family who saw something concerning. Their observations are part of the record.

The documentation is for your own clarity, for reality-check support, for trauma-informed therapy, and (when warranted) for formal action through safeguarding, hate crime, or legal channels.

### 7.4 Fourth: Map the dependencies

[Power Differential and Resource Analysis](manipulation-hub-power-differential.md) provides the framework. For friendship contexts, the dependencies often include:

- **Social network dependency** — what social relationships does this friendship represent?
- **Practical dependency** — does this friend provide transportation, help with bureaucracy, emotional support, or other practical resources you depend on?
- **Housing dependency** — does the friend live with you, or do you live with them, or do you depend on their lease or housing arrangements?
- **Financial dependency** — does the friend manage shared resources or have access to your finances?
- **Identity dependency** — has this friendship become part of how you understand yourself, such that losing it would feel like losing part of who you are?

The map reveals which protective moves are feasible now and which require building alternatives first.

### 7.5 Fifth: Consider the disengagement spectrum

The [Family of Origin](manipulation-hub-context-family-of-origin.md) page (section 7) named the disengagement spectrum for family contexts: full engagement with new tools, modified engagement, reduced contact, no contact, selective contact, the "estranged but on speaking terms" middle position. The same spectrum applies to friendship contexts, with friendship-specific calibrations:

- Friendships are technically easier to end than family relationships, but the social and emotional costs can be high.
- The disengagement spectrum gives options between "full engagement" and "complete severance."
- The right position on the spectrum depends on the specific configuration, the resources available, and the stage of the autistic adult's recognition.

### 7.6 Sixth: For mate crime, use the safeguarding and hate crime frameworks

If the friendship configuration meets the criteria of mate crime (section 2.1), the protective response involves the formal frameworks designed for it:

- **Disability hate crime reporting** in the UK and other jurisdictions with relevant legislation. Third-party reporting through disability organizations is available.
- **Safeguarding Adults processes** in the UK; APS in the US; equivalents elsewhere. The caveats about institutional safeguarding processes apply (see Family of Origin page section 2.3).
- **Disability rights organizations** (Mencap, Dimensions, Ann Craft Trust in UK; ASAN, AWN, NDRN in US; equivalents elsewhere) for case advocacy and referral.
- **Police involvement** when the situation is acute and when the police are equipped to handle disability hate crime cases. UK police forces have varying levels of training in this area; some have dedicated hate crime units.
- **Legal representation** when the situation involves criminal matters or when civil action may be warranted (recovery of stolen property, eviction of a cuckooing perpetrator, restraining orders).

### 7.7 Seventh: Recovery is the long arc

Recovery from friendship-based exploitation often involves:

- **Grief for the friendship that was not what it was framed as.** The grief is real and is part of recovery; suppressing it is part of how the patterns continue to operate.
- **Recovery of trust in your own capacity to evaluate friendships**, which the patterns often corrode. The work is slow.
- **Building new friendships on different terms**, which is structurally hard for autistic adults and which often requires both skill development and patient willingness to begin again with new people.
- **Recognition that the exploitation was real**, even when the friend was sincere or when the exploitation was unintentional. The both-can-be-true principle from the Family of Origin page applies.
- **Trauma-informed therapy** with autism-awareness, when accessible, particularly for severe cases involving sexual or physical violence.

The standing recovery frame applies: **the goal is not to become a person who does not need friends. The goal is to be in friendships that are mutual, that respect your autonomy, and that do not weaponize the things that make you vulnerable. Some autistic adults find these friendships in autistic community, some in long-standing relationships from earlier in life, some in carefully chosen new relationships, and some never quite find them in the form they wanted. The page does not pretend that friendship recovery has a single endpoint.**

## 8. What This Page Cannot Do

- **It cannot tell you whether a specific friendship is exploitative.** The distinguishing tools support the recognition; the recognition is yours to make, often slowly, with reality-check support.
- **It cannot replace legal advocacy in mate crime cases.** Cases involving cuckooing, financial exploitation, sexual violence, or physical violence require legal and safeguarding support that this page cannot provide.
- **It cannot create friendships where social isolation is the larger problem.** Many autistic adults are reading this page in conditions where the friendships available to them are limited, and "find better friends" is structurally hard advice. The page acknowledges this and does not pretend otherwise.
- **It cannot reverse the cultural script of friendship.** Most people the autistic adult interacts with will continue to default to the "friendships are categorically positive" framing. The recognition of exploitative friendships is sometimes unsupported even by close family and other friends.
- **It cannot make autistic-autistic friendships categorically safe.** The autistic peer relationship is empirically more communicatively effective on average (Crompton et al. 2020), but it is not exempt from exploitation. The page treats this honestly.
- **It cannot guarantee that disclosure will be received well.** Friend groups often side with the harm-causing friend, particularly if the harm-causing friend has been distributing their narrative. The disclosure decision is audience-dependent.
- **It cannot reverse the trauma of friendship-based sexual violence.** The trauma is real, the recovery is long, and the page can only orient toward the work, not perform it.
- **It cannot substitute for the broader social infrastructure** that would make safer friendships easier to build. The structural dimensions of autistic social isolation are not solvable at the individual level.

## 9. Key Takeaways

1. **Friendship is not a category that confers safety.** Friendships can be exploitative, manipulative, and dangerous. The cultural script that treats friendships as categorically positive is itself a vulnerability that the patterns exploit.

2. **Mate crime is a documented pattern with lethal endpoints.** UK disability advocacy has named and worked to address mate crime; the case literature includes documented child and adult deaths. Disability hate crime reporting and safeguarding processes are available.

3. **Friendship-based sexual violence is one of the most common forms of sexual victimization of autistic adults.** Cazalis et al. (2022) and the broader autism-IPV literature document the pattern. Friendship-context sexual violence is sexual violence and warrants the same crisis resources and supports.

4. **Smaller social networks raise the stakes of any individual friendship.** The cost of losing a friend is higher when there are fewer of them, and the threat of friendship withdrawal is more effective leverage.

5. **Autistic-autistic friendships are not categorically safer than autistic-allistic friendships.** The autistic peer reality-check strategy still applies, but the recommendation is for autistic peers in general, not for any specific autistic person who is exhibiting the patterns this page treats.

6. **Pearson, Rees, & Forster (2022) — "'This Was Just How This Friendship Worked'"** captures the autism-specific recognition pattern: many autistic adults report treating exploitative friendship dynamics as normal because they did not have healthy reference points to compare them to. The protective implication is that explicit articulation of healthy friendship features is itself a protective tool.

7. **The grooming trajectory is recognizable in retrospect** but often invisible in real time. The trajectory questions — how is this friendship different from a year ago, are the changes things you would have agreed to if proposed all at once — can sometimes reveal the pattern in time.

8. **The disclosure decision in friendship contexts is high-risk.** Friend groups often side with the harm-causing friend. Reality-check support outside the friend group is generally needed before disclosure to mutual friends.

9. **The four most dangerous configurations** are mate crime + cuckooing; "best friend" + isolation + financial extraction; friend group + mockery + scapegoating; and grooming + sexual exploitation. Each warrants the protective response calibrated to its specific dynamics, and each can warrant outside legal or safeguarding support.

10. **The recovery frame is not "become a person who does not need friends."** It is "be in friendships that are mutual, that respect your autonomy, and that do not weaponize the things that make you vulnerable." Some autistic adults find these friendships; some struggle to; the page does not pretend that recovery has a single endpoint.
