# Category 9: Presupposition -- What's Assumed Before a Word Is Spoken

## The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

---

## How to Read This Document

"Have you stopped procrastinating?"

If you answer "yes," you've admitted you were procrastinating. If you answer "no," you've admitted you're *still* procrastinating. There's no way to answer the question without accepting the hidden premise: that you were procrastinating in the first place.

That hidden premise is a **presupposition**. And presuppositions are everywhere -- in questions, statements, compliments, advertisements, political speeches, and everyday conversation. They are the background assumptions baked into language that speakers take for granted without ever stating them directly.

This category teaches you to **see** them. That skill alone is one of the most powerful analytical tools in this entire learning hub.

---

## 1. What Presupposition Is

### 1.1 Definition

A **presupposition** is a background assumption that must be true for an utterance to make sense. It's what a sentence **takes for granted** rather than what it **states**.

Every utterance has two layers:

| Layer | What It Does | Example: "John stopped smoking" |
|-------|-------------|-------------------------------|
| **Asserted** (foreground) | The new information; the point of the sentence | John no longer smokes |
| **Presupposed** (background) | What's assumed already true; not argued for | John WAS smoking before |

The asserted content is "up for debate" -- you can agree or disagree with it. The presupposed content is presented as already established. You're expected to accept it without question.

### 1.2 The Negation Test: How to Detect Presuppositions

The single most useful diagnostic: **negate the sentence and see what survives.**

| Positive | Negative | What Survives (= presupposition) |
|----------|----------|-------------------------------|
| "She stopped smoking." | "She didn't stop smoking." | She WAS smoking (survives both) |
| "He regrets eating the cake." | "He doesn't regret eating the cake." | He ATE the cake (survives both) |
| "She's late again." | "She's not late again." | She was late BEFORE (survives both) |

If negating the sentence doesn't remove the assumption, the assumption is a presupposition. If it does remove it, it's an entailment (a different thing -- see Section 5).

### 1.3 Why This Matters

Presuppositions matter because they are the primary mechanism by which information is **smuggled into conversation without being explicitly stated**. When you hear a presupposition-carrying sentence, your brain automatically processes the presupposed content as established fact -- usually without critical evaluation.

This makes presuppositions a tool for:
- **Efficient communication** (not having to state everything explicitly)
- **Shared understanding** (signaling what's common ground)
- **Manipulation** (inserting claims as if they're already accepted)
- **Framing** (shaping how a topic is understood before discussion begins)

## 2. Presupposition Triggers: The Complete Catalog

Presuppositions don't arise from thin air -- they are triggered by specific words and grammatical constructions. Learning these triggers gives you a checklist for detecting presuppositions in real-time.

### 2.1 Change-of-State Verbs

Words that describe starting, stopping, or changing a state presuppose the prior state.

| Trigger | Example | Presupposes |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **stop** | "She stopped smoking." | She WAS smoking |
| **start/begin** | "He started running." | He WASN'T running before |
| **continue** | "They continued arguing." | They WERE arguing already |
| **finish** | "She finished the project." | She WAS working on the project |

**The loaded question version**: "Have you stopped procrastinating?" presupposes you WERE procrastinating -- and there's no yes/no answer that escapes it.

### 2.2 Factive Verbs

Verbs that presuppose the truth of their complement clause.

| Trigger | Example | Presupposes |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **know** | "She knows you lied." | You DID lie |
| **realize** | "When did you realize you were wrong?" | You WERE wrong |
| **regret** | "He regrets eating the cake." | He DID eat the cake |
| **discover** | "They discovered the error." | There WAS an error |
| **be aware** | "Is she aware of the problem?" | There IS a problem |

**Key distinction**: "know" is factive (presupposes truth); "think" and "believe" are NOT factive (don't presuppose truth). "She knows he left" presupposes he left. "She thinks he left" does NOT presuppose he left.

### 2.3 Iterative Expressions

Words that presuppose something happened before.

| Trigger | Example | Presupposes |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **again** | "He's late again." | He was late BEFORE |
| **another** | "She bought another car." | She already HAD a car |
| **return** | "She returned to Paris." | She WAS in Paris before |
| **restore** | "We need to restore order." | There WAS order before (and it was lost) |
| **re-** prefix | "We need to reconsider." | We considered it before |

### 2.4 Definite Descriptions

"The [X]" presupposes that X exists and is uniquely identifiable.

| Trigger | Example | Presupposes |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **the** + noun | "The manager left early." | There IS a manager |
| **'s** possessive | "John's sister called." | John HAS a sister |

### 2.5 Temporal Clauses

| Trigger | Example | Presupposes |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **before** | "Before she left, she locked up." | She LEFT |
| **after** | "After the meeting, we'll talk." | There WILL BE a meeting |
| **since** | "Since you arrived, things improved." | You DID arrive |
| **while** | "While you were sleeping..." | You WERE sleeping |

### 2.6 Cleft Constructions

"It was X who..." presupposes someone did the thing -- the assertion is about WHO.

| Construction | Example | Presupposes |
|-------------|---------|-------------|
| **It-cleft** | "It was John who broke the window." | Someone broke the window |
| **Wh-cleft** | "What she wants is a raise." | She wants something |

### 2.7 Comparative Constructions

| Trigger | Example | Presupposes |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **more/less/better** | "Carol is a better linguist than Barbara." | Barbara IS a linguist |
| **as...as** | "He's as tall as his father." | His father has a specific height |

### 2.8 Counterfactual Conditionals

| Trigger | Example | Presupposes |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **If...had** | "If I had known, I would have helped." | I did NOT know |
| **I wish** | "I wish I had studied harder." | I did NOT study hard enough |

### 2.9 Implicative Verbs

| Trigger | Example | Presupposes |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **manage** | "She managed to finish." | Finishing was DIFFICULT |
| **fail** | "He failed to appear." | He was EXPECTED to appear |
| **happen to** | "I happened to see him." | The seeing was UNPLANNED |
| **avoid** | "She avoided answering." | Answering was a possibility she CHOSE against |

### 2.10 Questions (Especially Loaded)

Every wh-question presupposes an answer exists:

| Question | Presupposes |
|----------|-------------|
| "Where were you last night?" | You WERE somewhere relevant |
| "Why did you do that?" | You DID do it |
| "When did you stop caring?" | You DID stop caring (AND you used to care) |
| "How could you be so careless?" | You WERE careless |

## 3. Loaded Questions: Presupposition as a Trap

The most practically important application of presupposition theory is understanding **loaded questions** -- questions that smuggle in assumptions you may not accept.

### 3.1 How They Work

A loaded question embeds a presupposition in such a way that **any direct answer accepts the hidden premise**:

| Loaded Question | Hidden Presupposition | Why It's a Trap |
|----------------|----------------------|----------------|
| "Have you stopped cheating?" | You WERE cheating | "Yes" = I was cheating but stopped. "No" = I'm still cheating. |
| "When will you stop wasting time?" | You ARE wasting time | Any time-based answer accepts the premise |
| "Why did you lie to me?" | You DID lie | Explaining "why" accepts that lying happened |
| "What's your biggest weakness?" | You HAVE a weakness you know about | Standard but still loaded |

### 3.2 How to Respond to Loaded Questions

**Strategy 1: Direct challenge**
"That question assumes I was procrastinating. I wasn't."

**Strategy 2: Meta-level challenge**
"I'd push back on the premise of that question."

**Strategy 3: Decompose**
"There are two things embedded in that question. Let me address them separately."

**Strategy 4: Reframe**
"I think the better question is..."

**Strategy 5: Gentle correction**
"Actually, I don't think that's quite what happened. Here's what I see..."

### 3.3 Where Loaded Questions Appear

| Context | Example | Strategy |
|---------|---------|---------|
| **Political debates** | "When will you stop wasting taxpayer money?" | Challenge the premise |
| **Job interviews** | "What's your biggest weakness?" | Accept the frame (it's expected) |
| **Relationship conflicts** | "Why don't you ever listen to me?" | Address the presupposition ("I do listen -- can you help me understand what you're experiencing?") |
| **Sales** | "When would you like to schedule installation?" | Recognize the premature close (you haven't agreed to buy) |
| **Court** | "Where did you hide the evidence?" | "Objection -- assumes facts not in evidence" |

## 4. Presupposition as a Tool of Persuasion

### 4.1 In Advertising

Advertisers use presupposition to bypass critical evaluation:

| Ad Language | Presupposition | What It Bypasses |
|------------|---------------|-----------------|
| "Aren't you glad you use Dial?" | You use Dial | Whether you use Dial is never questioned |
| "Get even MORE miles per gallon." | You already get good mileage | The "even more" presupposes a baseline |
| "Discover the secret to..." | There IS a secret | Whether a "secret" exists is smuggled in |
| "When you're ready to upgrade..." | You WILL upgrade | The temporal clause treats upgrading as inevitable |
| "Because you're worth it." | You ARE worth it | Flattery embedded as presupposition |

### 4.2 In Political Rhetoric

| Rhetoric | Presupposition | Effect |
|----------|---------------|--------|
| "We must restore our nation's greatness." | The nation WAS great and ISN'T now | Frames decline as established fact |
| "As everyone knows..." | The claim is common knowledge | Discourages dissent (if "everyone" knows, disagreeing marks you as ignorant) |
| "The obvious solution is..." | There IS an obvious solution | Forecloses consideration of alternatives |
| "When we win back the Senate..." | We WILL win | Treats outcome as established |

### 4.3 In Everyday Persuasion

| Context | Presupposition Technique |
|---------|------------------------|
| **Sales** | "Would you like that in blue or red?" (presupposes you're buying) |
| **Parenting** | "What would you like for dinner, chicken or pasta?" (presupposes you'll eat) |
| **Management** | "How should we implement this?" (presupposes implementation is decided) |

## 5. How Presupposition Differs from Related Phenomena

| Feature | Presupposition | Entailment | Implicature |
|---------|---------------|------------|-------------|
| **Survives negation?** | YES | NO | Often yes |
| **Cancellable?** | Difficult | No | Yes (conversational) |
| **Source** | Specific trigger words | Logical meaning | Context + cooperation |
| **If false** | Sentence is odd/infelicitous | Sentence is false | Sentence is misleading |
| **Background or foreground?** | Background (taken for granted) | Foreground (part of what's said) | Foreground (inferred addition) |

**Example showing all three:**

"John stopped smoking."
- **Presupposition**: John WAS smoking. (Survives negation: "John didn't stop smoking" still presupposes he was smoking.)
- **Entailment**: John no longer smokes. (Does NOT survive negation: "John didn't stop smoking" means he DOES still smoke.)
- **Implicature**: Perhaps he quit for health reasons. (Cancellable: "John stopped smoking -- not for health reasons, he just couldn't afford it.")

## 6. Accommodation: What Happens When Presuppositions Are New

The philosopher David Lewis (1979) identified a crucial mechanism: **accommodation**. When a speaker presupposes something that ISN'T actually in the common ground, the listener typically doesn't object -- they simply **add it to their model** and continue.

**Example**: A stranger says "My sister lives in Boston." You didn't know they had a sister. But you don't say "Wait -- you have a sister?" You just... accept it. You **accommodate** the presupposition.

Accommodation is so automatic that it's the **primary way** presuppositions function in real communication. Most presuppositions in conversation are NOT things already in the common ground -- they're new information smuggled in as if they were already established.

This is why presuppositions are so powerful as a persuasion tool: listeners accommodate them by default rather than questioning them.

## 7. Presupposition and the Autistic Experience

### 7.1 What the Research Shows

**Processing presupposition triggers**: Cheung et al. (2017, 2020) found that autistic children had difficulty with presupposition comprehension, but most of the difficulty tracked with language development rather than being a specific pragmatic deficit. The exception was **temporal clauses** and **factivity**, which were uniquely linked to Theory of Mind.

**The linguistic vs. social split applies here too**: Presuppositions triggered by specific words (stop, again, know) are processed relatively well. Presuppositions that require assessing **what the speaker assumes the listener knows** (shared knowledge/common ground) are harder, because they involve perspective-taking.

### 7.2 The Autistic Advantage: Detecting Hidden Presuppositions

Here's the reframe -- and it's backed by research:

**Autistic individuals may be BETTER at detecting presuppositions** precisely because they are less likely to automatically accommodate them.

Rozenkrantz et al. (2021) demonstrated that autistic individuals show **enhanced rationality** in domains that typically trip up neurotypical reasoners:
- Reduced susceptibility to the **framing effect** (how a choice is presented changes NT decisions but not autistic ones)
- Reduced **sunk cost fallacy** (throwing good money after bad)
- Reduced **conjunction fallacy** (judging a specific scenario as more likely than a general one)

These are all cases where NT cognitive shortcuts create vulnerability to manipulation. The same cognitive style that resists framing effects should resist presupposition-based manipulation: if you don't automatically accommodate hidden premises, you're more likely to **notice** them.

**The literal-processing advantage**: The tendency to process language more literally means presuppositions are more likely to be **parsed rather than absorbed**. Where an NT listener hears "Have you stopped procrastinating?" and automatically accommodates the presupposition, an autistic listener may pause at the "stopped" trigger and think: "Wait, was I procrastinating?"

### 7.3 The Vulnerability Paradox

Research reveals a paradox: autistic individuals may be **resistant to structural manipulation** (advertising, rhetorical framing, loaded questions) but **vulnerable to social manipulation** (gaslighting, repeated assertions by trusted individuals, social pressure to accept group premises).

The difference: structural manipulation relies on linguistic shortcuts (presupposition triggers, framing effects) that literal processing resists. Social manipulation relies on interpersonal trust and common ground -- areas where autistic individuals may be more vulnerable because of difficulty calibrating who to trust and what constitutes genuine shared knowledge.

### 7.4 Common Ground Challenges

Presuppositions require speakers to model what listeners already know. Research (Karin et al., 2025) found that both autistic and NT speakers generate common ground spontaneously, but NT speakers do it more efficiently. This means autistic speakers may:

- **Under-presuppose**: Providing too much background information because they're unsure what the listener already knows (explaining things the listener already understands)
- **Over-presuppose**: Assuming the listener shares knowledge they don't (assuming everyone knows the details of a topic the speaker is deeply knowledgeable about)

Both patterns create communication friction, but they stem from difficulty with common ground estimation, not from a presupposition processing deficit per se.

## 8. Practical Toolkit

### 8.1 The Presupposition Detection Checklist

When you hear a sentence and something feels "off" or "loaded," scan for these triggers:

| Trigger Type | Key Words | Ask Yourself |
|-------------|-----------|-------------|
| Change-of-state | stop, start, continue, finish | "Was the prior state actually true?" |
| Factive | know, realize, regret, discover | "Is the embedded claim actually true?" |
| Iterative | again, another, return, re- | "Did this actually happen before?" |
| Definite | the [X], [X]'s [Y] | "Does X actually exist?" |
| Temporal | before, after, since, while | "Did the assumed event actually happen?" |
| Comparative | more, better, as...as | "Is the baseline comparison actually valid?" |
| Counterfactual | if...had, I wish | "Is the implied reality actually the case?" |
| Implicative | manage, fail, avoid | "Was the implied difficulty/expectation real?" |
| Questions | when, why, how (loaded) | "Am I accepting hidden claims by answering directly?" |

### 8.2 The Negation Test (Quick Method)

To check if something is a presupposition:
1. Negate the sentence
2. Does the assumption still hold?
3. If yes → it's a presupposition
4. If no → it's an entailment or assertion

### 8.3 When to Challenge vs. When to Accommodate

| Situation | Action | Why |
|-----------|--------|-----|
| Casual conversation, benign presupposition | Accommodate (accept silently) | Social efficiency; no harm in accepting "My sister lives in Boston" |
| Loaded question in an argument | Challenge the presupposition | Answering directly traps you |
| Advertising / sales pressure | Recognize but don't need to challenge | Awareness protects you from the manipulation |
| Professional setting, factual error | Gently correct | "Actually, I think the timeline might be different..." |
| Political rhetoric / media | Identify for yourself | Critical thinking; decide if the framing is valid |

### 8.4 Your Detection Ability Is a Strength

If you find yourself noticing assumptions that others accept automatically, that's not pedantry -- it's **analytical precision**. The ability to detect hidden presuppositions is a genuine cognitive strength that most critical thinking programs try to teach and most people never fully develop.

The key is calibrating when to use it: challenge manipulative presuppositions, accommodate benign ones, and recognize the difference.

---

## 9. Key Takeaways

1. **Presuppositions are hidden premises.** They're the assumptions baked into language that speakers take for granted. They're background, not foreground -- which means they bypass normal critical evaluation.

2. **The negation test detects them.** Negate the sentence. If the assumption survives, it's a presupposition.

3. **Specific words trigger them.** "Stop," "again," "know," "regret," "before," "the," "manage" -- each has a specific presupposition pattern. Learning the triggers gives you a detection checklist.

4. **Loaded questions are the most practically dangerous application.** Any question that embeds a presupposition you don't accept creates a trap. The skill is recognizing the trap and challenging the premise rather than answering the question directly.

5. **Advertising, politics, and persuasion rely heavily on presupposition.** "Restore greatness" presupposes lost greatness. "Get even more" presupposes you already have some. Recognizing these patterns makes you resistant to manipulation.

6. **Accommodation is the default.** Listeners usually just accept presuppositions rather than questioning them. This is why presuppositions are so powerful -- and why the ability to NOT automatically accommodate is genuinely valuable.

7. **Your literal processing is an asset here.** The autistic tendency to parse language rather than absorb it makes you naturally better at detecting presuppositions. This is a genuine cognitive strength.
