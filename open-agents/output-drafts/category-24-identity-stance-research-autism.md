# Category 24 Research: Autism-Specific Stance and Identity

## Companion Research File to Category 24: Identity and Stance in Interaction

This file covers the autism-specific research base for Category 24. It begins from the double empathy framing (Milton 2012) as the default assumption of this project, and then works through the literature on masking as sustained stance performance, autistic burnout as the cost of that performance, credibility discount as a consequential stance assignment, autistic-autistic interaction as a counter-case, weaponized features, disclosure decisions, and claimed autistic identity.

This file is not a clinical overview of autism. It is a focused research synthesis on the specific intersection between autism and the stance/identity framework. The work it draws on is recent, partly empirical and partly qualitative, and in some places thin. Where the evidence base is thin, the file says so.

## 1. The Double Empathy Starting Point

### 1.1 Milton 2012

Damian Milton's 1200-word paper "On the ontological status of autism: The 'double empathy problem'" (Milton 2012) is one of the most-cited papers in autism studies from the last fifteen years. Its argument is narrow and consequential: what has traditionally been described as an autistic deficit in social understanding is better described as a **mutual** breakdown of understanding between parties who bring incompatible interactional expectations to the same encounter. Neither party has full access to the other's meaning-making. Both are working without the shared background that would let ordinary conversational inference proceed.

The reframe is load-bearing for this category. If the breakdown is mutual, then the autistic speaker's stance moves are not deficient -- they are native to a different interactional ecology. The stance misreadings are not failures of the autistic speaker to perform correctly; they are mismatches between two sets of defaults, each of which is internally coherent.

### 1.2 Empirical Extensions

Milton's argument was initially philosophical. Subsequent empirical work has supported the core claim:

- **Sheppard et al. (2016)** found that non-autistic participants had difficulty reading the mental states of autistic targets -- that is, the inference difficulty runs in both directions, not only from autistic to non-autistic.
- **Edey et al. (2016)** found that non-autistic observers exhibited mind-blindness when reading mental states from autistic targets' movement animations, supporting the view that the cross-group difficulty is shared. (For explicit social-competence ratings, see Alkhaldi et al. 2019, cited below.)
- **Alkhaldi et al. (2019)** found that neurotypical observers judged autistic individuals more negatively after only brief exposure, and that these judgments were made using thin-slice social information.
- **Mitchell, Sheppard, & Cassidy (2021)** extended the line of work, arguing that the phenomena traditionally called "autistic social impairment" are better characterized as an interaction-level mismatch.

The double empathy framing is now widely taken as the default framing in autism research, including in this project. It does not mean that autism imposes no distinctive demands; it means that whatever demands autism imposes are symmetric in a way the older framing did not recognize.

The empirical work since Milton's original paper has generally not tried to replace the framework; it has tried to specify the mechanism. The question is no longer whether the breakdown is mutual but what specifically about the cross-neurotype encounter produces it. Recent candidate mechanisms include: mismatched prosodic reading (autistic and non-autistic listeners use different cues to decode affect); mismatched timing expectations (what counts as a natural pause for one party is a marked silence for the other); mismatched topic-management defaults (autistic speakers often prefer deeper engagement with fewer topics, non-autistic speakers often prefer lighter engagement with more topics); and mismatched alignment markers (what counts as listening for one party reads as inattention to the other). Each of these is an active area of research, and each is a stance-level mismatch rather than a content-level deficit.

### 1.3 Why This Matters for Stance

The double empathy framing matters for stance theory because it explains why autistic stance defaults get **systematically** misread. The misreading is not random, and it is not a failure of the autistic speaker. It is the predictable output of two sets of stance defaults meeting without a shared translation layer. The autistic speaker's "I disagree" uses flatter prosody because that is the default for the speaker; the non-autistic listener parses it as marked because the listener expects a softer default for "I disagree." Both parties are doing ordinary stance work in their own ecology. The mismatch is structural.

This is why the practical research file's detection heuristics do not treat stance misreadings as the autistic speaker's fault. They are produced by the interaction, not by one party's deficit.

## 2. Masking as Sustained Stance Performance

### 2.1 What Masking Is

The empirical literature on autistic masking (also called camouflaging, compensation, or social camouflaging) has grown rapidly since the mid-2010s. The foundational papers include:

- **Hull et al. (2017)** "'Putting on my best normal': Social camouflaging in adults with autism spectrum conditions" -- a qualitative study of the strategies autistic adults use to conceal autistic traits in social interaction.
- **Hull et al. (2019)** -- development of the Camouflaging Autistic Traits Questionnaire (CAT-Q).
- **Cage & Troxell-Whitman (2019)** -- on the contexts and costs of camouflaging.
- **Cook et al. (2021)** -- systematic review of the camouflaging literature.
- **Pearson & Rose (2021)** -- critical conceptual review of camouflaging research and its relationship to masking.
- **Miller, Rees & Pearson (2021)** -- qualitative comparison of masking in autistic and non-autistic adults.

The descriptive core of this literature is consistent across studies: autistic adults report a sustained, effortful performance of non-autistic interactional defaults, including prosody, facial expression, eye contact, topic selection, and pacing of speech.

### 2.1.a Hull et al. 2017 in Detail

The Hull et al. (2017) paper is worth expanding on because it is the qualitative study most often cited as the descriptive foundation of masking research.

Using semi-structured interviews with 55 autistic adults (analyzed thematically), the authors identified a cluster of specific stance moves that participants reported performing:

- **Suppression of stimming.** Participants reported actively inhibiting repetitive movements (hand flapping, rocking, finger-tapping) in non-autistic settings, even when stimming would have supported their self-regulation. The suppression is a stance move because stimming is, among other things, a visible affective signal; suppressing it performs the positioning "I am not dysregulated."
- **Controlled facial expression.** Participants described consciously producing smiles, nodding, and matched affect at moments when their own affect did not match. This is stance work at the evaluation layer of the stance triangle: the face is producing an evaluative signal that is disconnected from the speaker's internal evaluation.
- **Scripted conversational moves.** Participants reported preparing in advance for predictable social encounters, rehearsing openings, transitions, and closings, and treating spontaneous small-talk as a scripted performance. The preparation is stance work at the positioning layer: the speaker is pre-producing the stance moves of "a socially confident person who knows what to say."
- **Effortful eye contact.** Participants described forcing gaze patterns that did not come naturally, sometimes by focusing on the bridge of the nose or the interlocutor's eyebrow rather than the eyes themselves, to simulate an expected alignment signal.
- **Managed prosody.** Participants reported monitoring their pitch range, speech rate, and volume to match non-autistic defaults, often in real time and with cognitive load that interfered with content production.

Each of these moves is a stance-level act, not a content-level act. None of them is about *what* the autistic speaker is saying; all of them are about *how* the autistic speaker is performing the saying. This is what Category 24 means by treating masking as sustained stance performance: the layer at which masking operates is the stance layer, not the propositional content layer.

### 2.1.b The CAT-Q Three-Factor Structure

Hull et al. (2019) developed the Camouflaging Autistic Traits Questionnaire (CAT-Q) through exploratory and confirmatory factor analysis with 354 autistic and 478 non-autistic adults. The final 25-item instrument has three factors, each of which corresponds to a distinct kind of stance work:

- **Compensation.** Behaviors aimed at overcoming social challenges by substituting an external strategy for an intuitive one — for example, using a learned script, imitating an observed conversational move, or pre-rehearsing a topic. In stance-theoretic terms, compensation is the production of stance moves through explicit computation rather than through default generation. The speaker is still taking the stance, but is building it from parts rather than enacting it natively.
- **Masking** (in the narrower CAT-Q sense). Active efforts to present a non-autistic persona — to hide autistic traits and display non-autistic ones. This is stance performance in the strongest sense: the speaker is producing a stance that is not their own, sustained across the interaction.
- **Assimilation.** Attempts to fit into the social situation by suppressing one's own discomfort and modifying one's behavior to blend in. This is stance work at the alignment layer: the speaker is performing alignment with the group even when the internal state is one of misalignment.

Reading the three factors together, the CAT-Q can be understood as measuring three distinct forms of non-native stance labor:

- Building stance moves from parts (compensation).
- Performing a non-native stance persona (masking).
- Performing alignment with a group that does not in fact feel aligned with (assimilation).

Each is a stance-level phenomenon; the questionnaire's three-factor structure is, from Category 24's vantage, a three-factor decomposition of stance performance itself.

The CAT-Q's internal consistency (alpha = 0.94) and test-retest reliability (r = 0.77) are acceptable, and the factor structure has been shown to be invariant across gender and diagnostic group. The instrument is now widely used in camouflaging research, including in Cook et al. (2021) and in subsequent work linking camouflaging to mental health outcomes.

### 2.1.c Cage & Troxell-Whitman 2019: Why People Mask

Cage and Troxell-Whitman's (2019) study of 262 autistic adults asked directly about the reasons for masking. The findings are pragmatically consequential: masking is not motivated by the autistic person's enjoyment of the performance. The most frequently cited reasons were stigma avoidance, employment protection, fear of judgment, and the practical need to access non-autistic settings safely. A much smaller number of participants described masking as a neutral or positive practice.

This matters for the Category 24 framework because it rules out a framing in which masking could be treated as a voluntary style choice. The stance performance is structurally required by the environment; the autistic person is not choosing to perform it in the way one might choose a vocabulary register. The performance is the cost of admission to settings the autistic person needs to enter. Cage and Troxell-Whitman also found that higher masking was associated with worse mental health outcomes, consistent with the later systematic review findings of Cook et al. (2021).

### 2.1.d Pearson & Rose 2021 as the Foundational Reframe

Pearson and Rose's (2021) conceptual analysis is the foundational reframe paper for the whole area. Before their analysis, the camouflaging literature was largely framed in clinical terms: masking as an observable phenomenon to be measured, explained by gender differences, late diagnosis, or cognitive compensation. Pearson and Rose argue that this framing misses the mechanism. Masking, they argue, is a stigma-driven adaptation to an environment that penalizes autistic defaults, not a cognitive strategy that autistic people happen to deploy.

Their argument has three moves:

**First: the narrative of stigma.** Autistic people grow up in an environment saturated with implicit and explicit messages that autistic features are undesirable. The adaptation to those messages is not a free choice; it is shaped before the person has the conceptual vocabulary to resist it.

**Second: the illusion of choice.** Framing masking as a choice the autistic person makes obscures the structural coercion that produces it. The person "choosing" to mask is choosing under conditions in which non-masking carries consequences the person cannot afford.

**Third: the reframe.** Masking is better understood as a stigma-management strategy comparable to the stigma-management strategies documented in the wider sociological literature on minority identity (Goffman; social identity theory). It is a stigma-level phenomenon with a clinical-looking surface.

For Category 24, Pearson and Rose's reframe is load-bearing.

It is what licenses the treatment of masking as stance performance rather than as trait expression. The stance-theoretic analysis of masking depends on the prior move of treating masking as a stigma-driven adaptation, because that is what makes it a performance at all.

A trait that the person cannot help expressing is not a stance move; a stigma-managed adaptation that the person enacts at cost is.

### 2.1.e Cook et al. 2021 Systematic Review

Cook, Hull, Crane, and Mandy's (2021) systematic review in *Clinical Psychology Review* 89:102080 synthesized 29 studies of camouflaging in autistic adults and children. Three preliminary findings emerged:

1. Higher self-reported autistic traits are associated with higher self-reported camouflaging.
2. Sex and gender differences exist in camouflaging, with autistic women generally reporting higher camouflaging than autistic men, though the effect is smaller than often assumed in the popular literature.
3. Higher camouflaging is associated with worse mental health outcomes, including anxiety, depression, and suicidality.

The review also identified two methodological approaches that have not yet been fully reconciled: the internal-external discrepancy approach (comparing observer ratings to self-reports) and the self-report approach (asking autistic people directly about their camouflaging). Each approach captures different aspects of the phenomenon, and each misses something the other catches. From Category 24's vantage, the two approaches correspond to two different stance-theoretic questions: the discrepancy approach measures the gap between the stance the speaker is performing and the stance an outside observer would read from the speaker's behavior; the self-report approach measures the speaker's own awareness of doing stance work. Both are real, and neither is the whole story.

### 2.2 Masking as Stance Work

Masking is, in the vocabulary of Category 24, **sustained stance performance**. What the autistic person is performing is a non-native stance: the stance of someone whose defaults are those of the non-autistic interactional ecology. The performance covers all three parts of Du Bois's stance triangle:

- **Evaluation**: softening evaluations to match non-autistic expectations of affective packaging.
- **Positioning**: presenting as calmer, more confident, more interested, or otherwise more socially legible than the autistic person's native stance would suggest.
- **Alignment**: performing alignment markers (mm-hmm, nodding, "right, right") at the rate and in the places expected by non-autistic interlocutors.

None of this is faking. It is real stance work, at real pragmatic cost, aimed at producing a communicative output that non-autistic interlocutors can read without effort. The cost falls entirely on the autistic party because the non-autistic interlocutor's defaults are the unmarked ones in the dominant ecology.

### 2.2.a The Specific Positioning Moves of Masking

It is useful to be explicit about the positioning moves that masking typically produces. Each masking act can be described as a specific claim the speaker is making about themselves:

- **"I am relaxed"** — performed through controlled prosody, unhurried pacing, a neutral facial expression, and body posture consistent with the surrounding group. Held against internal states that may include overload, anxiety, or sensory distress.
- **"I am engaged"** — performed through gaze patterns that simulate ordinary attention, alignment tokens ("mm-hmm," nodding, "right"), and facial responses that match the speaker's content. Held against internal states that may involve genuine engagement processed on a different timescale, or attention that has been diverted by sensory load the non-autistic speaker is not tracking.
- **"I am following"** — performed through rapid alignment responses and topical uptake at the pace non-autistic interlocutors expect. Held against an internal processing tempo that may require more time to produce a considered response, where the considered response is the one the autistic speaker would actually endorse.
- **"I am appropriate"** — performed through the suppression of stims, the modulation of affect intensity, the softening of direct evaluations, and the avoidance of monologuing on topics the speaker is competent in. Held against a native stance vocabulary in which these suppressed behaviors are not in fact inappropriate but are marked as such by the dominant ecology.
- **"I am one of you"** — performed through assimilation-layer moves: group-matched laughter, group-matched affect, group-matched silence, group-matched turn-taking cadence. Held against the internal experience of not being one of them.

Each of these is a stance claim. Each claim is made through observable behavior. And each claim, when performed sustainably, requires the speaker to monitor their output in real time, compare it to an expected norm, adjust when it deviates, and carry the cognitive and affective load of the whole process while also doing the ordinary work of the conversation.

The cumulative cost is not just cognitive load. It is also the identity-level damage of sustained public self-misrepresentation. A stance claim held repeatedly, over years, in front of the people with whom the speaker is in their most consequential relationships, is not cost-neutral even when it is not detected. The Miller, Rees, and Pearson (2021) participants describe this vividly: the cost is the accumulating sense that the person the others know is not the same person the speaker actually is, and the related sense that repairing the gap would require disclosing material the speaker does not yet feel safe to disclose.

### 2.2.b The Social-Identity-Theoretic Reframe

Pearson and Rose (2021) ground their reframe in social identity theory (Tajfel and Turner) and in Goffman's sociology of stigma. This grounding matters because it provides a vocabulary for the autistic person's experience that is not clinical. In social identity theory, members of stigmatized groups engage in predictable stigma-management strategies: concealment, selective disclosure, in-group affiliation, and the cultivation of positive group identity as a counter to external devaluation. Each of these strategies has been documented in minority communities of many different kinds. The reframe locates autistic masking inside this general pattern rather than treating it as an autism-specific peculiarity.

The practical payoff: if masking is a stigma-management strategy, then the autistic person who masks is not exhibiting a clinical deficit but is doing something that other stigmatized groups also do, for recognizable reasons, with recognizable costs. The person is not broken. The environment is hostile, and the person is navigating it with the tools available.

### 2.3 What Masking Is Not

Some framings in the popular literature treat masking as a form of deception or inauthenticity. The qualitative literature pushes back on this framing (Pearson & Rose 2021; Miller et al. 2021). Masking is better understood as a structurally required survival strategy in a stance environment that penalizes autistic defaults, not as a moral failure of authenticity. The autistic person performing a masked stance is not lying about who they are; they are performing a non-native stance in an environment where performing their native stance carries consequences.

This distinction matters because the framing of masking as deception becomes itself a stance assignment -- one that positions the autistic speaker as inauthentic or manipulative. Several manipulation patterns documented in the sub-hub (performative allyship, DARVO, diagnostic weaponization) rely on exactly this move: reading the autistic person's masked stance performance as evidence that they are lying about who they really are.

## 3. Cost: Autistic Burnout

### 3.1 Raymaker et al. 2020

Dora Raymaker and colleagues' 2020 paper "'Having all of your internal resources exhausted beyond measure and being left with no clean-up crew': Defining autistic burnout" (Raymaker et al. 2020) is the first community-led empirical study to name and define autistic burnout. The paper's definition:

> Autistic burnout is a syndrome conceptualized as resulting from chronic life stress and a mismatch of expectations and abilities without adequate supports. It is characterized by pervasive, long-term (typically 3+ months) exhaustion, loss of function, and reduced tolerance to stimulus.

### 3.2 Burnout as the Cost of Non-Native Stance Work

From the Category 24 vantage point, autistic burnout can be described as the cumulative cost of sustained non-native stance performance under conditions where that performance is required across many settings over long periods. The "chronic life stress" Raymaker et al. describe is, in part, the stance-work budget of masking. The "mismatch of expectations and abilities" is, in part, the mismatch between what the dominant interactional ecology requires and what autistic stance defaults produce.

This reframing has a specific theoretical payoff. Du Bois (2007), the central theorist of stance in pragmatics, describes stance as "a public, observable, consequential act" — a move made in the open, with effects the speaker cannot unmake. When the public act is incompatible with the internal state, the gap is not free. Every masked stance move is a moment in which the speaker's public, observable, consequential self diverges from the speaker's actual self. Ordinary conversation accumulates stance records in the memories of the participants; masked conversation accumulates stance records that misrepresent the masked speaker. Over long periods, across many settings, the accumulated misrepresentation becomes the record by which the masked speaker is known. Burnout is, in part, the physiological and psychological cost of living inside that record.

Subsequent work has refined this picture:

- **Mantzalas, Richdale, & Dissanayake (2022)** "A conceptual model of risk and protective factors for autistic burnout" *Autism Research* 15(6):976-987 proposes a conceptual model mapping measurable variables that can act as risk or protective factors depending on context. Their model explicitly names camouflaging under "personal demands" alongside executive function load and life stress, and it names community acceptance, self-awareness, and stimming under "personal resources." The model's key feature is that the same variable can be a risk factor in one context and a protective factor in another — for example, camouflaging at work may be a survival strategy that reduces immediate risk while simultaneously raising the long-term risk of burnout. For the Category 24 framework, this is the explicit statement of what the stance-performance cost looks like when mapped as a longitudinal risk structure.
- **Higgins et al. (2021)** used a grounded Delphi method with autistic experts-by-experience to define autistic burnout, documenting the role of masking in precipitating burnout episodes.
- **Phung et al. (2021)** describe autistic burnout alongside inertia, meltdown, and shutdown from the perspective of autistic youth.
- **Arnold, Higgins, Weise, Desai, Pellicano, & Trollor (2023)** have developed an initial assessment instrument for autistic burnout, the AASPIRE Autistic Burnout Measure.

The empirical base is growing but still thin. What is available supports the claim that sustained stance performance has substantial costs for autistic adults, and that those costs accumulate in ways that are not captured by ordinary models of workplace or relational stress. The Mantzalas et al. model is particularly useful for Category 24 because it makes explicit the bidirectional structure of the cost: the same stance-performance labor that protects the autistic person from immediate social consequences is what generates the cumulative cost that produces burnout. There is no version of the environment in which the autistic person can avoid both the immediate stance cost of being read as inappropriate and the long-term stance cost of performing non-native stance.

### 3.3 Implications

If masking is stance performance and burnout is its cost, then the Category 24 framework has a direct clinical implication: reducing stance-performance load is a real intervention target. The interventions that reduce stance load -- spending time in autistic community spaces, reducing the frequency of masked interactions, choosing work environments with different stance expectations -- are identifiable interventions on a specific pragmatic variable, not general "self-care."

This reframe changes how several widely recommended interventions should be understood. "Take breaks" is useful because it reduces stance-performance load, not because it is restorative in some general sense. "Spend time with other autistic people" is useful because the stance ecology of those spaces does not require non-native performance, not because of a vague social-support mechanism. "Find a job that fits your profile" is useful because stance load varies enormously by workplace, and a workplace that does not penalize autistic stance defaults is one in which the cumulative budget stays manageable. Each of these is a specific intervention on the stance-work variable, and each can be evaluated by asking whether, for this particular person in this particular week, it is reducing the stance-performance load they are carrying.

### 3.4 What the Literature Does Not Yet Tell Us

The empirical literature on masking and burnout has moved quickly, but several important questions remain unresolved. The practical research file's recommendations should be read as extrapolations from the available literature, not as findings:

- The precise quantitative relationship between masking intensity and burnout severity has not yet been measured with validated instruments in large samples.
- The directionality of the masking-burnout relationship is not fully established. Most of the literature treats masking as causally upstream of burnout, but the reverse direction (burnout making masking harder, which in turn increases the stance-reading consequences the person experiences) is also possible and not yet disentangled from the forward direction.
- The effects of reducing masking on burnout recovery have not been tested in a controlled design. The clinical recommendation to "unmask more" is based on qualitative reports from autistic adults who found it helpful, not on intervention studies.
- The protective effect of autistic community spaces is widely reported in qualitative work but has not been measured with standard social-support instruments. Crompton, Hallett, et al. (2020) is one of the closer approximations, but it measures rapport and friendship satisfaction rather than burnout outcomes directly.

These gaps do not undermine the theoretical framework of Category 24 — the framework is internally coherent and is consistent with the available evidence — but they should shape the confidence with which specific recommendations are offered to individual readers. The framework is load-bearing; the dosing is not yet calibrated.

## 4. Crompton et al. and Autistic-Autistic Interaction

### 4.1 The Peer Transfer Study

Catherine Crompton and colleagues' 2020 paper "Autistic peer-to-peer information transfer is highly effective" (Crompton et al. 2020) is the most-cited empirical study of autistic-autistic interaction. Using a diffusion-chain design, the researchers found that information transfer rates in autistic-to-autistic chains were equivalent to those in non-autistic-to-non-autistic chains and substantially higher than in mixed chains.

The finding is consistent with the double empathy prediction: the breakdown is not in autistic interaction per se but in interaction between parties with mismatched defaults.

### 4.2 Follow-Up Work

Subsequent studies have extended the finding:

- **Crompton, Sharp, Axbey et al. (2020, "Neurotype-Matching, but Not Being Autistic, Influences Self and Observer Ratings of Interpersonal Rapport," *Frontiers in Psychology* 11:586171)** on rapport in autistic-autistic versus mixed interactions. This study found that autistic adults reported significantly higher rapport, ease, and enjoyment when interacting with other autistic adults than when interacting in mixed pairs. The rapport finding is particularly important for stance theory because rapport is precisely the interactional outcome that stance alignment is supposed to produce. When rapport is high in autistic-autistic pairs, it means that the stance alignment work is landing — that each speaker's stance moves are being read the way they were intended.
- **Crompton, Hallett, et al. (2020, "I never realised everybody felt as happy as I do when I am around autistic people," *Autism* 24(6):1438-1448)** (the thematic-analysis companion paper to the diffusion-chain study) on the specific conversational features that support autistic-autistic rapport.
- **Morrison et al. (2020)** on first impressions between autistic and non-autistic participants.
- **Heasman & Gillespie (2018)** qualitative work on perspective-taking in autistic family interactions (*Autism* 22(6):740-750).
- **Heasman & Gillespie (2019)** on "neurodivergent intersubjectivity," arguing that autistic interactants develop specific coordinated strategies for shared understanding that look different from non-autistic strategies but are functionally equivalent (*Autism* 23(4):910-921).

The cumulative picture: autistic interactants have shared stance defaults with each other that non-autistic interactants do not share with them. The stance ecology of autistic-to-autistic interaction is internally coherent and functional.

Heasman and Gillespie's "neurodivergent intersubjectivity" concept deserves particular attention because it names a positive phenomenon rather than the absence of a deficit. Their qualitative work identified several specific features of autistic-autistic interaction that support shared understanding: direct topic transitions without the non-autistic "padding," explicit rather than implicit negotiation of meaning, extended engagement on topics of mutual interest, and less pressure on alignment markers. Each of these is a departure from non-autistic defaults, and each is functional within the autistic stance ecology. The authors' framing is important for Category 24: autistic interaction is not "non-autistic interaction minus some features." It is a different system with its own positive features, and those features support coordinated meaning-making when the system is interacting with itself.

### 4.3 What This Means for Stance Theory

The peer-transfer work is theoretically important because it establishes that autistic stance defaults are **a stance system**, not a collection of broken pieces. The system works, when it is interacting with itself. What looks like breakdown from outside the system is mismatch at the boundary, not failure within the system.

This is the empirical underpinning for the theoretical claim in Category 24's main document that autistic defaults are different defaults, not deficient ones.

### 4.4 Double Empathy as a Stance-Reading Problem

The double empathy problem (Milton 2012; Mitchell, Sheppard & Cassidy 2021) is often described as a problem of empathy or theory of mind. At the level of mechanism, however, it is best understood as a stance-reading problem. Each party to a cross-neurotype interaction is reading the other's stance moves through their own native stance vocabulary and missing the intended meaning.

Consider what happens during a concrete exchange. An autistic speaker says "I think that's a bad idea" with flat prosody, a steady gaze, and no softening hedge. The speaker's native stance system treats this output as ordinary disagreement — the content is the content, and the absence of softening is not an absence of regard, it is simply the default mode of assertion in the speaker's stance ecology. The non-autistic listener, however, parses the output through a different expected default. In the non-autistic listener's stance vocabulary, "I think that's a bad idea" said without softening carries an additional stance layer: a claim of confrontation, of anger, of personal attack, or of social insensitivity. The listener responds to the stance layer the speaker did not intend, not because the listener is failing but because the listener is reading the speaker's output with the listener's own reading grammar.

The reverse direction is exactly symmetric. A non-autistic speaker says "I wonder if that approach might run into some issues down the line," softening an actual rejection with hedges, a rising contour, and an implicit "we." The speaker's native stance system treats this as a clear rejection — the hedges are obligatory packaging, not the substance. The autistic listener, however, parses the output literally: "You wondered about possible issues. I will address the issues." The autistic listener responds to what the speaker said, not to what the speaker meant, not because the autistic listener lacks theory of mind but because the autistic listener is reading the speaker's output with the autistic listener's reading grammar.

Neither party is doing anything wrong. Both parties are doing ordinary stance-reading work. The mismatch is at the boundary between two stance systems. This is what makes the double empathy problem "double": it is not a deficit of either party; it is a mismatch between two native grammars, each of which is internally coherent and each of which fails at the boundary in the predictable direction.

### 4.5 Empirical Extensions of the Stance-Reading Frame

Several empirical studies support the stance-reading frame:

- **Edey et al. (2016)** "Interaction takes two" *Journal of Abnormal Psychology* 125(7):879-885 showed that non-autistic observers had systematic difficulty reading the mental states of autistic targets, demonstrating that the reading difficulty runs in both directions.
- **Sheppard et al. (2016)** showed that non-autistic observers had difficulty inferring the mental states of autistic targets from short vignettes describing the target's behavior — the inference difficulty runs in both directions, not only from autistic to non-autistic. (For non-autistic observers' difficulty reading autistic *facial* emotion displays specifically, see Brewer et al., 2016.)
- **Alkhaldi, Sheppard & Mitchell (2019)** showed that non-autistic observers formed negative impressions of autistic targets from thin-slice exposure, and these impressions were driven by the observer's reading rather than by the target's content.
- **Morrison et al. (2020)** and **DeBrabander et al. (2019)** showed that autistic-autistic pairs produced outcomes different from mixed pairs even on the same task content, supporting the boundary-mismatch interpretation.

Taken together, these studies establish that the cross-neurotype reading difficulty is a robust, symmetric phenomenon and not a deficit of either party.

### 4.6 Crompton et al. 2020 and Native-Like Stance Coordination

Crompton and colleagues' (2020) peer-transfer finding is especially important because it establishes what autistic-autistic interaction looks like *when it is not being read through non-autistic stance grammar*. In their diffusion-chain design, nine groups of eight participants each (three all-autistic, three all-non-autistic, three mixed) passed a story along the chain. The information retained at the end of the chain was essentially equivalent for the all-autistic and all-non-autistic chains. The mixed chains lost significantly more information. The all-autistic chains also reported rapport at levels comparable to the all-non-autistic chains and higher than the mixed chains.

For stance theory, this is direct evidence that autistic-autistic interaction involves functional stance coordination. The participants in the all-autistic chains were not producing stance-impaired output that happened to work because nobody noticed; they were producing stance output that their conversational partners could read, and the reading supported effective information transfer and rapport. The information-transfer failure is not a property of autistic speakers. It is a property of mixed-neurotype interactions.

This finding also dissolves a long-standing confusion in the popular literature. If autistic communication were genuinely impaired, autistic-autistic interaction should be worse than autistic-non-autistic interaction. If autistic communication were merely unusual but compatible with non-autistic communication, autistic-non-autistic interaction should be equivalent to the other conditions. Neither prediction matches the data. What matches the data is the stance-system mismatch prediction: each system works with itself, the mismatch is at the boundary.

## 5. Credibility Discount: Pearson, Lim, and Colleagues

### 5.0 Why the Credibility Discount Is a Core Category 24 Topic

Among the several autism-specific phenomena this file covers, the credibility discount is arguably the most consequential for the Category 24 framework. Masking is a cost borne by the autistic person; burnout is a downstream health outcome; disclosure is a decision the autistic person makes. The credibility discount is different in kind: it is a stance assignment made by others, without the autistic person's consent, often without the autistic person's awareness, that directly shapes what happens to them in institutional settings where the consequences are largest. It is where the pragmatic theory of stance assignment meets material outcomes — care received, jobs kept, custody decisions, criminal justice outcomes, service eligibility. When the theoretical framework of Category 24 cashes out into real-world consequences, it often cashes out through this mechanism.

### 5.1 The Empirical Base

The empirical literature on autistic credibility and interpersonal victimization has grown since around 2020. Key papers:

- **Pearson, Rees, & Forster (2022)** "'This was just how this friendship worked': Experiences of interpersonal victimization among autistic adults." *Autism in Adulthood*, 4(2), 141-150. Qualitative documentation of how autistic adults describe being taken advantage of, and of having their accounts discounted when they tried to report them.
- **Pearson et al. (2023)** on autism, trauma, and victimization.
- **Lim, Young, & Brewer (2022)** "Autistic Adults May Be Erroneously Perceived as Deceptive and Lacking Credibility." *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 52(2), 490-507. PMC8813809. Direct documentation of the credibility discount.
- **Brown-Lavoie, Viecili, & Weiss (2014)** earlier work on sexual victimization.
- **Griffiths et al. (2019)** on the Vulnerability Experiences Quotient in autistic adults.

The consistent finding: autistic adults are disproportionately likely to experience interpersonal victimization across domains (relational, financial, sexual, institutional), and their reports of such experiences are disproportionately likely to be discounted by the people and institutions to whom they report.

### 5.1.a The Lim, Young & Brewer Study in Detail

Lim, Young, and Brewer's (2022) study is the most direct empirical demonstration of the credibility discount. The design is important for understanding what the result actually shows. The researchers recorded 30 autistic and 29 neurotypical adults in video interviews in which each participant was asked to tell both a truthful account and a deceptive account on matched topics. The videos were coded for a set of candidate autistic behavioral features: gaze aversion, repetitive body movements, literal interpretation, poor reciprocity, and flat affect. A separate set of 1,410 participants then viewed a single video each and rated the interviewee's truthfulness and credibility.

The headline finding: autistic interviewees were rated as more deceptive and less credible than non-autistic interviewees even when the autistic interviewees were telling the truth. The effect was driven not by content — the content of truthful statements was the same for both groups — but by the interviewee's overall presentation. What observers were reacting to was a set of stance-level features that differed across groups: prosody, gaze, facial affect, and micro-expression patterns that observers read as "off" without being able to say what was off.

A critical secondary finding: the effect was not cleanly reducible to any one of the individual coded behaviors. Observers' truth-credibility ratings did not vary systematically with the presence or absence of gaze aversion, or flat affect, or any other single feature. What drove the effect was the overall impression — the gestalt read of the interviewee as "not quite right." This is what makes the finding pragmatically consequential. The feature the observer is reading is not a single voluntary behavior that the autistic speaker could modify; it is the cumulative signal of the speaker's native stance output, which the speaker cannot dismantle piece by piece without losing the ability to communicate at all.

### 5.1.b The Features Are Stance Features

The features that drove the credibility discount in Lim et al. were not content features. They were not errors in the facts being reported, or slips in the reported sequence, or implausibilities in the narrative. They were:

- **Prosodic features.** Pitch range, speech rate, intonation contour, stress placement. In non-autistic stance work, these features serve as an unmarked channel for affect and alignment; in autistic speech, they often carry a different default range that observers read as "flat" or "robotic" even when the speaker's affective state is perfectly matched to the content.
- **Gaze features.** Frequency, duration, and timing of eye contact relative to the rhythm of the exchange. Observers expect gaze to modulate in a particular pattern during truth-telling; when the pattern is different, observers read the difference as evidence of deception rather than as evidence of a different stance system.
- **Facial features.** The timing and amplitude of smiles, brow movements, and micro-expressions. Ekman's older work on micro-expression reading, still widely used in training materials for interviewers and clinicians, does not correct for the autistic baseline. The result is that trained observers are trained to read the autistic baseline as suspicious.
- **Reciprocity features.** The pacing of turn-taking, the timing of back-channels, the calibration of response length to question length. Again, these are stance-level features, and again, the autistic default differs from the non-autistic default in ways that observers read as marked without being able to say why.

These are exactly the features that the stance literature treats as the channel for stance work. What the Lim et al. finding tells us is that the channel is being read — by observers making consequential judgments about the speaker's credibility — in ways that systematically disadvantage autistic speakers even when the content is truthful. The stance is being installed by features the speaker cannot voluntarily control.

### 5.2 Credibility Discount as Stance Assignment

In Fricker's (2007) vocabulary, these are instances of testimonial injustice. In Category 24's vocabulary, they are instances of a specific stance assignment: being placed into the position of unreliable narrator. The pragmatic mechanism is consistent with what the theoretical file describes: the speaker is assigned a stance without explicit discussion, the assignment is hard to refuse once installed, and the assignment carries consequences that the assignor does not have to defend.

The autistic-specific version of this mechanism has a particular structure. The autistic person is being stance-assigned the position of unreliable narrator *before* any content is evaluated. The assignment happens in the first few seconds of the encounter, on the basis of features that precede and pre-shape the reading of whatever content comes next. A clinician who has already, unconsciously, read the patient as "flat and slightly off" is not then going to apply neutral weight to the patient's symptom report. A performance reviewer who has already read the employee's affective output as "odd" is not then going to read the employee's technical contribution with neutral weight. A judge who has already read the witness's delivery as "evasive" is not then going to read the witness's testimony with neutral weight.

This pre-reading is what makes the credibility discount particularly hard for autistic adults to counter. By the time the autistic person gets to the content layer, the stance layer has already been committed to. The person they are talking to is not evaluating them fresh. They are evaluating them against a stance reading that was installed before the content started.

The specific form the assignment takes in autistic contexts often involves the following pragmatic moves:

- The autistic speaker's flat affect is read as indicating that the content is not serious.
- The autistic speaker's detailed factual recall is read as either "too much information" or as evidence that the speaker is fabricating.
- The autistic speaker's atypical reactions to interpersonal harm (delayed distress, unusual processing) are read as evidence that no harm occurred.
- The autistic speaker's directness about what happened is read as "coming on too strong" and therefore as exaggerating.

Each of these moves is a stance read that goes wrong because the reader is using non-autistic defaults to interpret autistic stance output.

### 5.3 Institutional Consequences

The credibility discount is especially consequential in institutional settings where reports are formally recorded. The practical research file (Section 2) covers this in more detail. What this autism-specific file adds is the empirical point that the discount is not a one-off misunderstanding -- it is a pattern with a documented distribution and documented consequences for autistic adults' ability to access support, protection, and resources.

When a credibility discount is formalized into an institutional record — a medical chart note, an educational evaluation, a performance review, a court transcript, a case worker's assessment — the stance assignment becomes durable in a way conversational stance assignments are not. The chart note does not need to be re-performed at each subsequent encounter; it is available, pre-loaded, to the next reader of the chart. The performance review is available to the next reviewer. The evaluation is available to the next teacher. Each subsequent reader picks up the stance assignment already installed and tends to read the autistic person through its lens.

This is what makes the institutional version of the credibility discount pragmatically different from the conversational version. The conversational version can, in principle, be refused or repaired in a subsequent conversation. The institutional version often cannot, because it has left the conversational domain entirely and entered the record-keeping domain, where the autistic person has limited ability to speak.

### 5.4 Cross-Links to the Manipulation Sub-Hub

The credibility-discount mechanism described here is the pragmatic foundation of several manipulation patterns documented in the manipulation sub-hub. In each case, the manipulator does not need to invent the stance assignment; they can rely on the stance assignment being installed automatically by the autistic person's stance features, and then weaponize the installed assignment.

- **`manipulation-hub-pattern-diagnostic-weaponization.md`** — the manipulator uses the autism label, or the autistic features without naming them, to position the autistic person as an unreliable reporter. The Lim et al. mechanism is the pragmatic basis for why this weaponization often succeeds: the stance assignment is already installed by default.
- **`manipulation-hub-pattern-gaslighting.md`** — the manipulator rewrites shared events and counts on the autistic person's credibility deficit to protect the rewrite. Outside observers are pre-primed to trust the manipulator over the autistic target, not because they have examined the evidence but because the autistic target's stance output has already been read as unreliable.
- **`manipulation-hub-pattern-emotional-invalidation-double-bind.md`** — the autistic person's affective output is read as either too flat to be trusted or too intense to be trusted, depending on what serves the manipulator. The Lim et al. mechanism shows that both readings are available because neither is sensitive to the actual content of the autistic person's internal state.
- **`manipulation-hub-pattern-memory-gaslighting.md`** — the autistic person's detailed recall, which would normally be evidence of accuracy, is re-read as evidence of obsession, rumination, or fabrication. This too is a stance read: the same behavior that would be credibility-enhancing for a non-autistic speaker is credibility-discounting for an autistic one.

## 6. Douglas & Sedgewick and Weaponized Features

### 6.1 The Weaponization Pattern

Recent work by Sarah Douglas and Felicity Sedgewick (Douglas & Sedgewick, 2024, "Experiences of interpersonal victimization and abuse among autistic people," *Autism* 28(7):1732-1745) has documented a pattern they describe as the weaponization of autistic features: the deliberate use by abusive partners of features known to be associated with autism in order to destabilize the autistic target. The pattern includes:

- Exploiting literal interpretation by making statements that will be taken at face value and then denying the face-value meaning.
- Using sensory environments that will overload the target, then framing the target's withdrawal as evidence of emotional instability.
- Timing demanding interactions to moments of low executive-function availability.
- Using the target's autistic features to build a case for the target's unreliability.

This is a specific case of the general pattern Category 24 describes: the manipulator uses stance assignments to position the autistic target in ways that carry consequences, and the stance assignments exploit features the target has limited ability to hide or modify.

### 6.2 Why This Is a Stance Story

Weaponized features sit at the intersection of two stance moves. First, the manipulator positions themselves as reasonable, the target as unreasonable, in a way that is backed by the target's observable behavior. Second, the manipulator invokes the autism label -- or does not, using the target's autistic features without naming them -- to recruit third-party readers into the same stance assignment. The combined effect is that the target's autistic features become evidence against them in a conversation that was engineered to produce exactly that evidence.

The Category 24 framework gives this move a name: it is intentional stance assignment disguised as tacit positioning, using the interactional asymmetry created by the target's autistic features as leverage.

### 6.3 The Pragmatic Asymmetry

The thing that makes weaponized features a particularly consequential stance attack is the structural asymmetry it exploits. The target's features are durable and at least partially involuntary — the autistic person cannot easily stop responding to sensory overload, cannot easily produce on-demand affect, cannot easily suppress the processing delay that gives the attacker time to escalate. The manipulator's positioning moves are flexible — they can be tuned to the audience, softened, denied, or withdrawn. The result is that every round of the interaction produces more evidence for the manipulator's position and no new evidence for the target's.

This asymmetry matters for the way Category 24 frames stance theory in general: stance work is not equally available to all speakers. Speakers with features that read as marked by the dominant stance ecology have less maneuvering room than speakers with features that read as unmarked. When a manipulator exploits this difference deliberately, they are leveraging a structural inequality that the target did not create and cannot easily remove.

The Douglas and Sedgewick (2024) paper is important precisely because it documents the pattern in detail with first-person data from autistic people who experienced it. It rules out the naive framing in which "communication problems in the relationship" are mutual and symmetric. They were not. They were structured.

### 6.4 Related Work: Shaw et al. and the Triple Empathy Problem

Shaw et al. (2024) "Barriers to healthcare and a 'triple empathy problem' may lead to adverse outcomes for autistic adults: A qualitative study" *Autism* 28(7):1746-1757 extends the double empathy framing to healthcare settings with a specific proposal: the "triple empathy problem" in which autistic patients face not only the ordinary double empathy mismatch with a non-autistic clinician, but also the additional mismatch that comes from medical training's formal stance conventions. Medical interviews have their own stance grammar — they expect a particular prosody for symptom reports, a particular affect for pain descriptions, a particular pacing for history-taking. Autistic patients are mismatched with the general non-autistic stance grammar and additionally mismatched with the medical-interview-specific stance grammar, and the combined mismatch produces the disproportionate adverse outcomes documented in Doherty et al. (2022) and elsewhere. For Category 24, the triple empathy frame is a reminder that stance ecologies are domain-specific: a person's stance-reading grammar is not a single thing, it is a set of grammars tuned to specific institutional contexts, and mismatches can stack.

## 7. Disclosure: Cage, Howes, and Identity Decisions

### 7.1 The Disclosure Literature

Whether, when, and how to disclose autism is a persistent practical question for autistic adults. The empirical literature includes:

- **Cage & Howes (2020)** on autistic experiences of identity and disclosure.
- **Romualdez et al. (2021)** on workplace diagnostic disclosure.
- **Sarrett (2018)** on disclosure in higher education.
- **Thompson-Hodgetts et al. (2020)** scoping review on disclosure decisions.
- **Doherty et al. (2022)** on disclosure in healthcare settings, documenting that autistic adults often cannot safely disclose their diagnosis to clinicians without triggering diagnostic overshadowing — a direct pragmatic illustration of disclosure-as-stance-assignment.

The consistent picture: disclosure is not a single decision but a repeated one, made context by context, with consequences that vary substantially by setting. In some settings disclosure is protective; in others, disclosure itself becomes a stance assignment -- the autistic person is re-read through the lens of the label in ways that close off other stance positions they might otherwise have occupied.

The Doherty et al. (2022) finding is worth emphasizing because it illustrates the paradox at the heart of the disclosure decision. Disclosing autism to a clinician should, in principle, give the clinician the information needed to provide appropriate care. In practice, the disclosure often triggers the clinician to attribute subsequent symptoms to autism rather than to investigate them — the positioning move that Shaw et al. (2024) documents as a direct contributor to adverse outcomes. The information that should have helped the patient becomes the evidence that is used to discount them. This is not a failure of individual clinicians; it is a feature of how the autism label is currently read in medical stance grammar, and it means that the decision of whether to disclose autism in a medical setting is not a question of helpfulness but of tactical positioning.

### 7.2 Disclosure as a Stance Move

From Category 24's vantage point, disclosure is a stance claim: the speaker is claiming the identity "autistic" in a particular conversation. Like any stance claim, it can be taken up by the audience or not. When it is taken up sympathetically, the disclosure becomes part of the speaker's self-positioning. When it is taken up unsympathetically, the disclosure becomes a resource the audience can use for their own positioning of the speaker.

This is why the disclosure decision is hard. It is not just a question of whether to share information. It is a question of what stance claim to make, to what audience, with what expected response, and with what fallback plan if the audience responds badly.

### 7.3 The Real Decision Tree

The ordinary framing of disclosure treats it as a single binary: will the person receiving the disclosure react well or badly? The pragmatic framing is more complicated. The question is not "will they react well" but "what stance will they assign me if I disclose, and is that stance better or worse than the stance I'm currently being assigned without disclosure?"

This reframing produces a real decision tree:

1. **What stance am I currently being assigned in this setting?** Before disclosure, the autistic person is usually already being read through a stance assignment, often one that includes "odd," "difficult," "rigid," "cold," or "unreliable." The fact that the person does not know they are being assigned these stances does not change the fact that they are operating inside them.
2. **What stance would I be assigned if I disclosed?** Disclosure would change the stance from "odd" to "autistic." Depending on the audience, that change is a reduction in cost (the audience has a category that makes sense of the perceived oddness) or an increase in cost (the audience now reads the person through a pathologizing lens). Neither outcome is automatic.
3. **Which stance is more survivable?** The decision is not between "no stance assignment" and "autistic stance assignment." It is between two stance assignments, one of which is in place whether the person speaks or not. The pragmatic question is which of the two the person can live inside.
4. **What is the reversibility of the disclosure?** Disclosure is asymmetric: once made, it cannot be unmade. The person cannot take back the claim "I am autistic" if the audience responds badly. The pragmatic cost of a bad outcome is therefore higher than the pragmatic cost of a bad outcome in conversations where the stance claim can be revised.
5. **What is the audience's own stance vocabulary for autism?** If the audience has a stance vocabulary in which "autistic" maps to "neurodivergent adult with a different communication style," disclosure is likely to be a reduction in cost. If the audience has a stance vocabulary in which "autistic" maps to "childlike, deficient, or unpredictable," disclosure will not fix the problem and may worsen it.
6. **What fallback options exist if disclosure goes badly?** If the person can exit the setting, the cost of a bad outcome is capped. If they cannot (family, employer with no alternative, clinician who controls a diagnosis they need), the cost is open-ended.

This framing is consistent with the empirical literature. Romualdez, Walker, and Remington (2021) found that autistic adults' experiences of workplace disclosure varied enormously depending on the specific workplace culture; there was no single "disclose or don't disclose" answer. Cage and Howes (2020) found similar context-dependence in higher education settings. Thompson-Hodgetts et al. (2020) in their scoping review concluded that the outcomes of disclosure are heterogeneous and that the decision cannot be simplified to a universal recommendation. All three findings are consistent with the stance-theoretic framing above: disclosure is a specific pragmatic move with audience-dependent consequences, not a universal good or universal risk.

## 8. Botha and Claimed Autistic Identity

### 8.1 "Autism Is Me"

Monique Botha, Bridget Dibb, and David Frost's 2022 paper "'Autism is me': An investigation of how autistic individuals make sense of autism and stigma" (Botha, Dibb, & Frost 2022) is a qualitative study of how autistic adults construct a positive autistic identity under conditions of social stigma. The paper's central finding is that claimed autistic identity -- the identity the person actively asserts and maintains -- is accomplished through ongoing stance work, not through a single act of self-labeling.

The study used critical grounded theory with twenty autistic participants. Participants discussed autism as central to their identity and integral to who they were, while also describing autism itself as a value-neutral characteristic onto which society confers negative meanings. Participants reported constant exposure to stigma and a range of strategies for managing it: reframing toward more positive understandings of autism, reclaiming language ("autistic," "neurodivergent"), and using concealment and disclosure strategically depending on the setting. The study is one of the first to document, in careful qualitative detail, how autistic adults construct a positive identity not by denying stigma but by actively working alongside it.

The relevance to Category 24 is direct. Claimed identity is built the same way any identity is built: through accumulated stance moves, ratified by a community of interlocutors who take the stance moves seriously. For autistic adults, the community of interlocutors is often the autistic community itself, because non-autistic settings often resist the stance moves through which autistic identity is built.

### 8.2 Identity Against Assignment

Botha and colleagues document a specific pragmatic tension: the stance moves autistic adults use to claim autistic identity are often the same stance moves that non-autistic interlocutors read as evidence of the pathologized identity. Talking about autistic experience in autistic community spaces is identity-building; the same speech in non-autistic spaces can be read as pathology-performing. The speaker's stance work is identical; the audience uptake is reversed.

This is the mechanism behind the community knowledge that identity work is easier in autistic community spaces. The spaces provide a set of interlocutors who ratify the stance moves as claimed identity rather than re-reading them as pathological.

### 8.2.a Claimed Autistic Identity vs. Assigned Autistic Stance

A distinction that is pragmatically useful and often collapsed in popular discussion:

- **Claimed autistic identity** is a first-person positioning act. The autistic person says, in effect, "I am autistic, and I take that position; I am claiming it; I am asking you to recognize me as I am claiming to be." The stance move is the autistic person's own; it is ratified (or not) by the audience, but the speaker is the author of it.
- **Assigned autistic stance** is what happens when non-autistic interlocutors use autism as a position they place the autistic person in. The stance move is the non-autistic interlocutor's own; the autistic person is the object of it, not the author. The assigned stance may or may not correspond to any claim the autistic person has made; it is installed without the autistic person's consent and is often installed without the word "autism" being used at all.

The distinction matters because the word "autism" is the same in both cases, but the pragmatic work is not. Claimed autistic identity is a self-advocacy move with all of the attendant costs and benefits of any claimed minority identity. Assigned autistic stance is a positioning move that the autistic person often has no awareness is being made against them, because the assigning party rarely names it.

In practice, the two often interact in hostile ways. An autistic person who has claimed autistic identity as a positive self-description may then encounter an interlocutor who uses "but you're autistic" as a stance assignment: "you're autistic, so of course you can't read the situation"; "you're autistic, so your reaction is disproportionate"; "you're autistic, so your report isn't reliable." The same word that the autistic person uses to claim their identity is used to assign them to an unreliable-narrator position. The autistic person cannot easily refuse the assignment without appearing to refuse the identity, and cannot easily refuse the identity without giving up the positive self-description. This is a double bind at the category level, and it is pragmatically distinct from the ordinary double bind because it operates on the meaning of a category rather than on a single speech act.

### 8.2.b Implications for Self-Advocacy

The claimed-versus-assigned distinction has practical implications for self-advocacy:

- **The goal of self-advocacy is to build an audience that ratifies the claimed identity.** It is not primarily to change the minds of assigners. Assigners are often not available to be changed within the timeframe in which the autistic person needs to operate. Building audiences — peer networks, community spaces, specific colleagues, specific family members — is the pragmatically more achievable move.
- **Claimed identity is cumulative.** Each stance move that claims identity, if ratified, adds to the record of the identity being claimed. The identity is not a one-time announcement; it is built through repetition and ratification over time.
- **Assigned stance can sometimes be refused without refusing the identity.** The move is to separate the two: "Yes, I am autistic. That is not what's happening here. What's happening here is that you are assigning me a stance I don't accept, and the fact that I'm autistic is not evidence for your assignment." This is hard, but it is sometimes available, especially in writing and with a trusted witness.
- **Cross-links to the manipulation sub-hub.** The claimed-versus-assigned distinction is the pragmatic foundation of the `manipulation-hub-pattern-diagnostic-weaponization.md` page and is directly relevant to `manipulation-hub-pattern-compliance-trap.md` (in which the autistic person is set up to "prove" that their assigned stance is wrong through compliance that only reinforces the assignment). Category 24 is the place where this distinction is stated at the theoretical level; the manipulation sub-hub is where its tactical consequences are worked out.

### 8.3 Other Claimed-Identity Work

Adjacent empirical work supports and extends Botha et al.:

- **Kapp et al. (2013)** on autistic identity and neurodiversity.
- **Cooper, Smith, & Russell (2017)** on social identity and wellbeing in autistic adults.
- **Bagatell (2010)** on identity construction in an autistic advocacy community.
- **Leadbitter et al. (2021)** on autism and neurodiversity.

The consistent picture: autistic identity, when it is experienced as affirming, is the product of repeated stance work in supportive interactional environments. It is not an inherent property waiting to be expressed, and it is not a label passively accepted. It is a claim maintained through interaction.

## 9. Community Knowledge: The Felt Sense

Several observations repeatedly surface in autistic community discussion, first-person memoir, and qualitative research, but the empirical base is thin. They are worth including here with an explicit label.

### 9.1 "Too Much" as a Stance Assignment

Many autistic adults report the experience of being repeatedly stance-assigned as "too much" -- too intense, too direct, too detailed, too emotional, too literal, too anything. The assignment is almost never explicit. It is performed by tone shifts, by subject changes, by withdrawn attention. The result is an accumulated sense of having to modulate nearly every stance move to avoid being placed into the "too much" position.

The empirical literature on masking supports this indirectly (Hull et al. 2017; Cage & Troxell-Whitman 2019), but the specific "too much" characterization is community knowledge rather than empirical finding. It is included because the language is widely used and because naming it as a stance assignment is useful for people who have the experience but have not had words for it.

The pragmatic structure of "too much" is worth noting. Its power lies in its unfalsifiability: there is no specific behavior the autistic person can stop doing that will make the "too much" judgment go away, because "too much" is not about a single behavior. It is about the cumulative read of the speaker's stance output against a threshold that the listener has set and the speaker cannot see. The speaker who tries to reduce the "too much" by being quieter is then too quiet. The speaker who tries to reduce it by being warmer is then fake. The speaker who tries to reduce it by being more interested is then obsessive. Each of these corrections is met with a new stance assignment at the opposite pole, producing a double-bind structure in which the speaker cannot navigate out of the marked position without entering a different marked position.

This double-bind structure is one of the pragmatic signatures of hostile stance environments. It is a useful detection heuristic: if your efforts to correct a stance assignment keep producing new assignments at the opposite extreme, the underlying problem is that the environment is treating you as marked regardless of what you do. The correct move in such environments is not to keep correcting; it is to reduce exposure to the environment.

### 9.2 The Literalness Trap

A related community observation: autistic speakers report being positioned as "too literal," where "too literal" is itself a stance assignment that casts the autistic reading of a non-literal utterance as the speaker's error rather than as a mismatch between two legitimate readings. The assignment is especially consequential in legal and clinical settings, where the literal reading is often the defensible one but the non-literal reading is the expected one.

The literalness trap is pragmatically distinctive because it treats the autistic speaker's reading as evidence of a general mental limitation even when the reading is, on the surface, the correct one. If someone says "I'm dying of hunger" and the autistic listener responds "let's get you food," the response is appropriate to the literal content and appropriate to the underlying need. But if the autistic listener asks "are you actually dying? should I call a doctor?" — which is the reading produced by parsing the utterance as literal — the response is held up as evidence that the autistic listener does not understand how language works. The asymmetry is that the non-autistic speaker is not held to the same standard in reverse: a non-autistic speaker who misreads an autistic literal utterance as non-literal is not described as having a deficit in pragmatic comprehension. They are described as having misunderstood.

### 9.3 The Inauthenticity Accusation

A further community observation: autistic adults who have learned to mask effectively are sometimes stance-assigned as inauthentic when the mask is noticed, and as deficient when the mask drops. The stance environment is structured so that both states carry a cost. The pragmatic vocabulary of Category 24 lets us name this as a double bind at the stance level: there is no stance position available to the autistic speaker that is both low-cost and socially acceptable.

### 9.4 "You Didn't Seem Autistic"

A related community observation: autistic adults who disclose their diagnosis are often met with some version of "but you don't seem autistic" or "you didn't seem autistic to me." The comment presents itself as a compliment but operates as a stance move. What it does, pragmatically, is mark the speaker's masked presentation as the criterion for "seeming autistic" and simultaneously erase the cost the autistic person has paid to produce that presentation. The comment also implicitly questions the disclosure itself, positioning the autistic person as either exaggerating their diagnosis or, more subtly, as someone whose diagnosis is performative rather than descriptive.

There is no clean pragmatic refusal of "you don't seem autistic" that does not sound defensive. The autistic person is offered two positions: accept the compliment (implicitly confirming that masking successfully hid the thing that was supposed to be hidden) or reject it (and sound like they are insisting on a diagnosis the audience wants to minimize). Both positions reinforce the background assumption that autism is something that should ideally be invisible.

This observation is widely shared in autistic community writing and first-person memoir. It is included here not as an empirical finding but as descriptive language for a pragmatic pattern that many autistic adults will recognize.

### 9.5 The Exhaustion That Has No Name Outside the Community

A further observation: autistic adults in autistic community spaces routinely describe a kind of exhaustion that they have not been able to explain to non-autistic people in their lives. The exhaustion is not ordinary tiredness and is not ordinary depression. It is the specific feeling of having spent the day performing stance. Non-autistic colleagues, friends, or partners who have not done this kind of work often hear the description as "I'm just tired" or "you're overreacting" or "everyone has stressful days." The community vocabulary for this exhaustion — "masking hangover," "social hangover," "people hangover" — is attempting to name a phenomenon for which the dominant language does not yet have a word.

The Raymaker et al. (2020) burnout literature and the Mantzalas et al. (2022) conceptual model both touch on this exhaustion, but they describe it at a longer timescale (weeks to months) than the daily scale at which the community uses the word "hangover." The daily-scale phenomenon is not yet well covered in the empirical literature, but it is the scale at which the stance-performance cost is most directly felt, and naming it is useful even if the research base is still thin.

These observations are presented as community knowledge. Where empirical research supports them, it is cited. Where it does not, the observations are offered as useful descriptive language rather than as validated findings.

## 9A. The Intersection With Other Marginalized Identities

Most of the literature discussed in this file treats "autistic" as the relevant identity dimension without fully engaging with the intersectional reality that autistic adults also hold other identities — gender, race, sexuality, disability status beyond autism, socioeconomic position — that shape how their stance output is read. A growing body of qualitative work is beginning to address this:

- Qualitative work on autistic women of color has documented a distinct intersectional pattern of stance misreading, in which the participant's stance output is read through stereotypes of both race and autism simultaneously, often in ways that make each reading reinforce the other. Botha, Dibb, & Frost (2022) touch on this in their stigma-management discussion, and subsequent work has extended it.
- Work on late-diagnosed autistic women, including the broader literature cited in Cook et al. (2021), has documented the specific pattern in which gendered expectations of emotional expressiveness interact with autistic stance defaults to produce different stance assignments than autistic men receive. The flat affect that is read as "odd" in a man is often read as "cold" or "ungrateful" in a woman, with different downstream consequences.
- Work on autistic LGBTQ+ adults has documented the parallel pattern in which the autistic person is navigating multiple stigma-management demands simultaneously, with different costs in different settings. The specific literature on this intersection is still developing.

For Category 24, the intersectional finding is important because it shows that the stance-reading mechanism is not applied uniformly. Different readers, reading the same autistic stance output, may install different stance assignments depending on what other categories they also place the speaker in. The stance attack is not a single attack; it is a configuration that can be tuned to the target's full profile.

This has practical implications for detection and refusal. An autistic adult who is also a member of other marginalized groups may need to audit not just the autism-specific stance assignments but the intersectional ones: how the assignment deploys race or gender or sexuality to do additional positioning work. The manipulation sub-hub's context pages (family-of-origin, workplace, medical, legal) each discuss the intersectional dimensions at the level of the specific setting.

## 11. What the Literature Does and Does Not Support

### 11.1 Well-Supported Claims

- The double empathy framing is supported by multiple studies and has become the default framing in contemporary autism research.
- Masking is a real, effortful, sustained activity with documented costs.
- Autistic-autistic interaction works well when both parties are autistic.
- Autistic adults experience disproportionate rates of interpersonal victimization and disproportionate credibility discount.
- Claimed autistic identity is built through stance work in supportive community contexts.

### 11.2 Less Well-Supported Claims

- Specific mechanisms linking particular stance misreads to particular institutional outcomes are often described through qualitative data only.
- The quantitative relationship between masking load and burnout severity is not yet precisely measured.
- The "weaponized features" pattern is described in emerging literature but has not yet been systematically replicated.
- The community observations about "too much," "too literal," and the inauthenticity bind are descriptive language widely used in autistic communities, but the specific framing has not been operationalized in quantitative research.

### 11.3 Where Evidence Is Absent

- There is no empirical research, as of writing, on specific stance-refusal strategies and their effectiveness for autistic adults in institutional settings. The practical research file's recommendations are extrapolated from positioning theory and conversation-analytic repair, not from intervention studies.
- There is no evidence base for specific training programs aimed at non-autistic interlocutors to improve their stance reading of autistic speakers. The double empathy framing implies such training should be useful in principle, but the specific interventions have not been tested.
- The effectiveness of specific disclosure scripts in specific settings has not been evaluated in controlled designs. The decision-tree in section 7.3 is derived from qualitative literature plus stance theory, not from outcome studies.
- The relationship between stance-level masking and physical health outcomes (chronic fatigue, sleep disturbance, autonomic dysregulation) is an active area of investigation but not yet well characterized.
- The long-term effects of autistic community participation on stance-performance load are supported by qualitative reports but have not been studied longitudinally with validated instruments.

These gaps are worth naming because they shape the confidence with which Category 24's practical advice can be offered. The theoretical framework is well-grounded. The detection heuristics are operationally useful but have not been formally validated. The refusal strategies are drawn from adjacent literatures and may or may not transfer in specific institutional settings.

### 11.4 The Confidence Structure of This File

For readers who want to know how much weight to put on each part of this file, the confidence structure is approximately as follows:

- **High confidence.** The double empathy framing (Milton 2012 and its empirical extensions); the existence, effortfulness, and cost of masking (Hull et al. 2017, 2019; Cook et al. 2021); autistic-autistic interaction effectiveness (Crompton et al. 2020); the existence and characterization of autistic burnout (Raymaker et al. 2020; Mantzalas et al. 2022); the credibility discount (Lim, Young & Brewer 2022; Pearson, Rees & Forster 2022); and disproportionate rates of interpersonal victimization (Griffiths et al. 2019; Douglas & Sedgewick 2024).
- **Moderate confidence.** The specific stance-theoretic reframe of the above — that masking is sustained stance performance, that burnout is the cost of that performance, that the credibility discount is a form of stance assignment. These framings are internally coherent and consistent with the evidence but are not themselves empirically tested; they are theoretical moves that organize the findings.
- **Lower confidence.** The specific community observations in section 9 (the "too much" pattern, the literalness trap, the inauthenticity accusation, the "you didn't seem autistic" pattern, the daily-scale exhaustion). These are widely reported in community discussion but have limited empirical backing as described. They are included because the language is useful, not because it has been validated in controlled research.
- **Theoretical only.** The specific mechanisms linking individual stance features (prosody, gaze, affect) to specific institutional outcomes (chart notes, performance reviews, legal decisions) in individual autistic adults' lives. The general pattern is documented; the mechanism at the individual-case level is usually described through qualitative data and has not been operationalized quantitatively.

This structure is offered so that readers can calibrate their use of the file. Material in the high-confidence category can be cited and relied on; material in the lower categories should be used descriptively and checked against the reader's own experience.
