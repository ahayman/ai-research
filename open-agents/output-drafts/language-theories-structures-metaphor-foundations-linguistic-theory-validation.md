## Issues Requiring Correction

### 1. Chomsky Review Page Count (Minor)

**Claim**: "Chomsky's review of *Verbal Behavior* ran to 33 pages"

**Finding**: The most common scholarly citation is *Language* 35, pp. 26-57, which is 32 pages. Some sources cite pp. 26-58 (33 pages), and at least one secondary source (Palmer, 2006) explicitly says "33 pages." The discrepancy likely arises from how the final page is counted (if it includes notes or bibliography extending to p. 58).

**Recommendation**: The claim is defensible and originates from a reputable secondary source. No correction strictly required, but could be softened to "approximately 33 pages" or cited as "pages 26-58 in *Language*."

**Rating**: UNCERTAIN (7/10)

### 2. Langacker's Cognitive Grammar Start Date (Very Minor)

**Claim**: "Developed Cognitive Grammar... beginning in the late 1970s"

**Finding**: Langacker dates the formulation of Cognitive Grammar to 1976, and his first published description appeared in 1982. Saying "late 1970s" is approximately correct but 1976 is technically mid-1970s.

**Recommendation**: Change "late 1970s" to "mid-to-late 1970s" or simply "the 1970s."

**Rating**: UNCERTAIN (6/10)

### 3. Albert Riedlinger Omission (Minor)

**Claim**: *Course in General Linguistics* "published 1916 by his students Charles Bally and Albert Sechehaye from lecture notes"

**Finding**: Albert Riedlinger collaborated on the edition and is credited on the title page. He was not a co-editor at the same level as Bally and Sechehaye but is consistently named as a collaborator.

**Recommendation**: Add "with the collaboration of Albert Riedlinger" for completeness.

**Rating**: UNCERTAIN (8/10 -- the claim is technically incomplete rather than wrong)

### 4. Prague School Dating (Non-Issue Upon Review)

**Claim**: "The Prague School (1920s-1930s)"

**Finding**: Founded 1926, active through 1939 (and beyond in various forms). The "1920s-1930s" label is accurate.

**Rating**: CONFIRMED

## Missing Perspectives and Research Opportunities

### 1. Halliday's Systemic Functional Linguistics (SFL) -- SIGNIFICANT OMISSION

The research covers Saussurean structuralism, Chomskyan generativism, cognitive linguistics, and connectionism, but entirely omits **M.A.K. Halliday's Systemic Functional Linguistics (SFL)**. This is arguably the most significant missing perspective. Halliday (1925-2018) developed a comprehensive functional theory of language starting in the 1960s that:

- Treats language as a social semiotic system (language as resource for meaning-making)
- Identifies three metafunctions: ideational, interpersonal, and textual
- Emphasizes the relationship between language and social context
- Has had enormous influence in applied linguistics, discourse analysis, education, and computational linguistics
- Represents a major alternative to both Chomskyan and cognitive linguistic approaches

SFL is particularly important in Australia, the UK, and much of the non-American linguistic world. Its omission leaves a gap in the theoretical landscape being presented.

**Recommendation**: Add a subsection on Halliday's SFL, even if brief, to acknowledge this major tradition.

### 2. Lexical-Functional Grammar (LFG) and HPSG -- Minor Omission

The research mentions HPSG in passing (as a descendant of generative semantics) but does not discuss it or **Lexical-Functional Grammar (LFG, Bresnan 1982+)** as distinct theoretical frameworks. Both are important alternatives within the formal/generative tradition that reject transformations while maintaining mathematical rigor. Their absence is understandable given scope constraints but worth noting.

### 3. Role and Reference Grammar (RRG) -- Minor Omission

**Robert Van Valin's Role and Reference Grammar** is a functionalist-formal hybrid framework that bridges generative and functional traditions. Its omission is understandable given scope but represents another perspective on the landscape.

### 4. Corpus Linguistics and Empirical Turn

The research could benefit from a brief note on how **corpus linguistics** has reshaped the field since the 1990s. The availability of large electronic corpora has provided empirical grounding for usage-based claims and has challenged both generative and cognitive models with actual frequency data. Joan Bybee's work on frequency effects in grammar would be a natural addition.

### 5. Deep Learning and Large Language Models

The connectionism section ends with the rules-vs-statistics debate and a brief note about modern deep learning. Given the transformative impact of transformer-based large language models (since 2017-2018) on debates about linguistic competence, statistical learning, and the poverty of the stimulus, this could be expanded. The success of LLMs has reinvigorated connectionist/emergentist arguments in new ways and raised fresh questions about what linguistic knowledge consists of.
