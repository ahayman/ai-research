# Co-Regulation, Social Engagement & Polyvagal-Informed Therapy

## Overview

Polyvagal theory's most active and influential life is not in the laboratory but in the consulting room. Across the past decade, a generation of trauma-informed therapists has built training programs, books, certifications, and clinical curricula around polyvagal vocabulary. Deb Dana's *Rhythm of Regulation* framework, codified across three Norton books between 2018 and 2021, is the canonical clinical operationalisation. Rebecca Kase's *Polyvagal-Informed EMDR* (Norton, 2023) layers polyvagal orientation onto the eight-phase EMDR Standard Protocol. Peter Levine's Somatic Experiencing and Pat Ogden's Sensorimotor Psychotherapy — both with origins in the 1970s — have absorbed polyvagal vocabulary into long-established body-oriented frameworks. Internal Family Systems therapists routinely map IFS's "Self-energy" onto ventral vagal activation. An autistic adult walking into a contemporary trauma-informed practice will almost certainly encounter some version of this language.

The crucial analytical question is **separability**: which therapeutic practices *require* polyvagal theory to be biologically correct, and which would work for reasons that don't depend on Porges's contested anatomical claims? This chapter exists to answer that question. The short answer — developed across what follows — is that **most of the load-bearing practices in polyvagal-informed therapy predate polyvagal theory and have their own evidence bases**. Attuned therapeutic presence is grounded in attachment theory (Bowlby 1969, Ainsworth 1969–78, Sroufe 1996) — twenty-five years before Porges's 1994 presidential address. Paced breathing is established cardiovascular physiology with FDA-cleared devices on the market. Titration and pendulation were coined by Peter Levine in the 1970s. Body-awareness practices come from Hakomi, bioenergetics, Gestalt body work, and Reich — a somatic tradition far older than polyvagal theory. The polyvagal ladder itself is psychological scaffolding for clients, not a measured nervous-system state.

> **Confidence overview.** This chapter integrates findings from 17 substantive claims independently validated for this chapter. 14 are confirmed against primary sources; 3 are uncertain at the level of prevalence ranges (autism comorbidity percentages legitimately vary by sample) and paraphrase attribution (Sroufe's "dyadic regulation of emotion" framing). No claim was found invalid. Publication details for Dana's three books, Kase's 2023 EMDR book, Brom 2017 SE RCT, Beauchaine's 2015 and 2019 RSA papers, and the historical foundations of attachment theory, somatic therapy, IFS, and EMDR are all verified.

This has two practical implications. **One**: polyvagal-informed therapy practices can be genuinely helpful and worth pursuing even if polyvagal theory turns out to be wrong about specific brainstem nuclei, because most of the active ingredients have independent evidence bases. **Two**: when a therapist or program rests its claims *specifically on the polyvagal mechanism* (e.g., "we activate your ventral vagal complex"), apply the same scrutiny you would apply to any contested neuroscience claim. The practices may still help; the mechanism story may be a metaphor. The chapter also adds a comorbidity layer: anxiety, depression, and PTSD co-occur with autism at rates ranging from 11% to 60% by population, and Beauchaine's 2015 transdiagnostic biomarker paper provides the integrating framework. Low resting RSA is a non-specific marker of emotion-regulation difficulty across psychopathology — which means polyvagal-informed practices that improve RSA are doing real cardiovascular work, but their specificity to polyvagal mechanism is exactly what the transdiagnostic finding undermines.

## The Landscape

The therapy modalities you'll encounter wearing polyvagal labels cluster into three patterns. The most common is **vocabulary-only**: the modality has an independent origin and an evidence base that doesn't depend on PVT being biologically correct, and polyvagal language has been layered on top. Somatic Experiencing, Sensorimotor Psychotherapy, IFS, and Dana's own Rhythm of Regulation framework all fall here. The second is **vocabulary-augmented**: the modality has its own evidence base, and polyvagal vocabulary is added as a theoretical orientation without modifying the underlying protocol. Polyvagal-informed EMDR is the clearest example. The third is **mechanism-dependent**: the intervention rests its specific claims on the polyvagal mechanism being biologically correct. The Safe and Sound Protocol's "we activate the ventral vagal complex" marketing is the canonical case. Different categories warrant different levels of scrutiny.

## Deb Dana's Rhythm of Regulation

Dana is the most influential clinical translator of polyvagal theory. A licensed clinical social worker, she is a founding member of the Polyvagal Institute, advisor to Unyte Health (the company selling SSP), Coordinator of the Traumatic Stress Research Consortium at the Kinsey Institute, and consultant to Khiron Clinics. Her clinical writing is published by W. W. Norton in the Interpersonal Neurobiology series alongside Schore, Siegel, van der Kolk, Ogden, and Levine.

The published spine of her framework is three books. *The Polyvagal Theory in Therapy: Engaging the Rhythm of Regulation* (Norton, 2018, foreword by Porges) is the foundational clinical text. *Polyvagal Exercises for Safety and Connection: 50 Client-Centered Practices* (Norton, 2020) is the operational manual — fifty named exercises, indexed by autonomic state target. *Anchored: How to Befriend Your Nervous System Using Polyvagal Theory* (Sounds True, 2021) is the trade book for clients themselves. Supporting tools include the Polyvagal Flip Chart, the Polyvagal Card Deck, and various worksheet collections.

The framework rests on four verbs: **befriend, map, navigate, shape**. To *befriend* the nervous system is to build relationship with it as something one can come to know rather than something happening *to* one — a non-pathologising stance that is anti-shame at its core. The framing "your nervous system is trying to keep you safe" replaces the framing "you are broken." To *map* is to identify the cues that move you up or down the polyvagal ladder. Dana introduces the **glimmer** (her coinage) — a small cue of safety, the opposite of a trigger — and uses worksheets to help clients build a Personal Profile Map. To *navigate* is to deploy real-time tools for shifting state: paced breathing, body posture, vocal prosody, environment changes, social contact. To *shape* is repeated practice over time, with the aim of broadening the window of ventral access — what Dana describes as building "neural pathways of safety."

<!-- DIAGRAM: dana-rhythm-of-regulation -->

The visual centrepiece of Dana's pedagogy is the **polyvagal ladder** — a vertical three-rung graphic with ventral vagal (green) at top, sympathetic (yellow or amber) in the middle, and dorsal vagal (red) at the bottom. Clients learn to locate themselves on the ladder in real time: "I'm in dorsal right now." The ladder is the most-circulated single artifact of polyvagal-informed therapy and the proximate cause of much of the framework's accessibility. It is also, importantly, *psychological scaffolding* — not a measured biological state. Whether the word "dorsal" accurately maps onto dorsal motor nucleus activity in the brainstem of the labelling client is a separate empirical question. As a self-tracking heuristic, the ladder works regardless of how the underlying biology resolves.

Dana's training and consulting work emphasises a structured session arc. The opening establishes ventral-to-ventral connection — therapist regulated, attuned, the client lands. The middle may move into activation work, with titrated approach to sympathetic or dorsal material. The closing re-establishes ventral state before the client leaves the room. This is not unique to polyvagal-informed therapy; it parallels the well-established arousal-regulation arc taught in trauma-informed CBT, the container exercises of standard EMDR, and Hakomi's tracking practices. The polyvagal vocabulary makes the arc explicit and gives both therapist and client a shared language for what is being attempted.

## Co-Regulation: Older Than You Think

The single most important historical fact about co-regulation is that it predates polyvagal theory by twenty-five years. When a clinician says "I co-regulate with my client," this is overwhelmingly a description of *attachment-theory-grounded* therapeutic presence. Whether the underlying biological substrate is best described by polyvagal theory, neurovisceral integration, or something else does not change the practice.

<!-- DIAGRAM: coregulation-attachment-roots -->

John Bowlby's *Attachment and Loss, Volume 1: Attachment* was published by the Hogarth Press in January 1969. Mary Ainsworth's Strange Situation publications run 1969–1978. Sroufe's *Emotional Development* (Cambridge University Press, 1996) formalised attachment as the dyadic regulation of emotion — the infant-caregiver pair, not the infant alone, is the regulatory unit. Allan Schore's right-brain attachment neurobiology came in the 2000s. Porges introduced polyvagal theory in his 1994 SPR presidential address. By the time PVT entered the conversation, co-regulation already had decades of theoretical and empirical development.

This matters because polyvagal theory's contribution to co-regulation has not been the *practice* but the *vocabulary*. The neural-mechanism story PVT offers — that one regulated ventral-vagal-active nervous system supports a dysregulated one via prosody, facial expression, and embodied calm — is a post-hoc explanation for something attachment researchers had already characterised. The relational quality is what helps; the explanation of why it helps is what is contested. If polyvagal theory's specific anatomical predictions don't hold (see [Category 3 — The Scientific Critique](../scientific-critique-untenable-debate/index.html)), co-regulation as a clinical practice loses precisely none of its evidence base. It loses one of several available frameworks for talking about what it is.

For the autistic adult evaluating a polyvagal-informed therapist, this is the first place to apply the scaffolding-vs-mechanism distinction. A therapist who uses polyvagal language to describe the *quality* of relational attunement is doing something that has 60+ years of independent backing. A therapist who claims to be specifically activating their own ventral vagal complex to co-regulate yours has crossed from scaffolding into mechanism — and that mechanism claim depends on PVT's specific anatomical predictions being correct.

## Polyvagal-Informed EMDR (Rebecca Kase)

EMDR — Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing — is one of the best-established trauma therapies. Francine Shapiro introduced it in 1989 and refined it through the 1990s into the eight-phase Standard Protocol used today. The evidence base for EMDR was built before any polyvagal-informed adaptations existed.

Rebecca Kase's *Polyvagal-Informed EMDR: A Neuro-Informed Approach to Healing* was published by W. W. Norton on 23 May 2023 (ISBN 978-1-324-03031-7). The book is explicit, per its publisher, that it "maintains fidelity to the evidence-based practice of EMDR." This framing — present in Norton's catalog description and echoed by the EMDR International Association — is analytically important. **Kase's polyvagal-informed EMDR doesn't modify the protocol; it adds a theoretical orientation.**

<!-- DIAGRAM: polyvagal-informed-emdr -->

What Kase actually adds across the eight phases is consistent: vocabulary, pacing sensitivity, and case-conceptualisation lens. In Phase 1 (history-taking), Kase adds polyvagal-informed mapping of the client's autonomic patterns to standard trauma history. In Phase 2 (preparation), polyvagal psychoeducation is added to standard container exercises and safe-place visualisations. In Phase 3 (assessment), autonomic-state check augments the standard SUDS rating. In Phase 4 (desensitisation), the therapist watches for sympathetic over-arousal or dorsal collapse during bilateral stimulation; the BLS itself is unchanged. Phases 5–8 receive similar additive treatment. The therapeutic mechanism — what EMDR is actually doing — remains the standard protocol's mechanism.

This is what makes the integration both clinically reasonable and analytically modest. **Nothing about the underlying intervention depends on polyvagal theory being biologically correct.** The polyvagal language serves as a shared vocabulary between therapist and client, and as a pacing-sensitivity lens for the therapist. EMDR's evidence base does the therapeutic heavy lifting. Skeptically read, the polyvagal layer is doing primarily *psychoeducational* work — labelling and language — rather than adding a new mechanism. Generously read, that psychoeducational work is itself useful: attuning therapist and client to where the client is moment-to-moment is valuable regardless of whether the underlying biological story is correct.

## Somatic Experiencing (Peter Levine)

Peter Levine developed Somatic Experiencing through the 1970s, with his PhD in medical biophysics and his ethological observations of how prey animals shake off survival activation. **Titration** and **pendulation** — the two backbone techniques of SE — were coined by Levine well before his first meeting with Stephen Porges in 1978. The technique of titration is working with small pieces of a difficult experience rather than confronting the whole; pendulation is the rhythmic oscillation between sensations of safety and sensations of activation. These are first principles of SE practice and they predate any PVT vocabulary.

Levine and Porges's collaboration over the subsequent forty-plus years has been substantive — SE-affiliated sources describe what they call "fraternal collaboration" — and PVT vocabulary has been heavily integrated into modern SE materials. Some SE materials describe collapse as "dorsal vagal shutdown," which is exactly the kind of claim the 2026 Untenable consensus critiques: PVT's specific anatomical claim that bradycardia/collapse is dorsal vagal turns out to be inconsistent with comparative neuroanatomy. In mammals, the bradycardic vagal response is overwhelmingly nucleus-ambiguus-mediated.

<!-- DIAGRAM: se-sensorimotor-independent-evidence -->

SE has its own evidence base that doesn't depend on PVT. The Brom et al. 2017 study in the *Journal of Traumatic Stress* (30(3):304–312) is the first SE-specific randomised controlled trial. n = 63 (33 SE participants, 30 waitlist), 15 weekly SE sessions, conducted in Israel during a period of ongoing collective trauma. The effect sizes were large: d = 0.94–1.26 for PTSD symptoms, d = 0.70–1.08 for depression. 44.1% lost their PTSD diagnosis. The authors describe the loss-of-diagnosis rate as moderate compared to gold-standard PE/CPT but the effect sizes are substantial. A separate 2017 RCT by Andersen and colleagues tested brief SE for chronic low back pain with comorbid PTSD symptoms. Pilot studies in cancer survivors, refugees, and earthquake survivors round out the literature.

The analytical move worth making is to separate "the techniques of titration, pendulation, somatic tracking, and discharge" from "the polyvagal explanation of why they work." The techniques retain their evidence base independent of PVT. If polyvagal theory turns out to be wrong about specific brainstem nuclei, SE practitioners lose a popular vocabulary for talking about their work; they don't lose the techniques or the Brom 2017 result.

## Sensorimotor Psychotherapy (Pat Ogden)

Pat Ogden's path runs parallel. In the early 1970s, while working in a short-term psychiatric hospital, Ogden became interested in clients' disconnection from their bodies and how it linked to psychological symptoms. She apprenticed with Ron Kurtz, became certified in Hakomi, and in 1981 co-founded the Hakomi Institute. Through the mid-1980s she founded her own school, initially called Hakomi Bodywork and later Hakomi Integrative Somatics. The Sensorimotor Psychotherapy Institute was formally established in the 2000s, with her first published Sensorimotor Psychotherapy paper appearing in 2002 and her foundational book *Trauma and the Body: A Sensorimotor Approach to Psychotherapy* published by Norton in 2006, in the Interpersonal Neurobiology Series alongside Schore, Siegel, and van der Kolk.

Sensorimotor Psychotherapy is a phase-oriented trauma treatment that integrates mindfulness, somatic tracking, cognitive integration, attachment theory, and the Hakomi method. Polyvagal vocabulary is now integrated into modern SP materials, but the method's foundational components — body-awareness tracking, mindful sensation work, phase-oriented treatment — were developed independently of polyvagal theory. As with SE, polyvagal language was added to a framework that was already substantively complete.

## Internal Family Systems (IFS) Integration

The user already has a substantial Internal Family Systems deep dive in this project (see [the IFS deep dive](../../ifs-autism-alexithymia-trauma-deep-dive/index.html)). That deep dive covers IFS's independent evidence base, its application to autism and alexithymia, its commercial and licensing structure, and its own controversies in depth. The treatment here cross-references rather than re-covers.

What matters for this chapter is the IFS-PVT integration. Richard Schwartz developed IFS in the 1980s while working as a family therapist with eating-disorder clients at the Institute for Juvenile Research in Chicago. His clients independently described distinct "parts" of themselves with their own feelings and motivations. Schwartz developed the parts ontology — Managers, Firefighters, Exiles, and the Self — and refined the model through the late 1980s and 1990s. By the time polyvagal vocabulary was being integrated into trauma therapies in the 2010s, IFS already existed as a complete framework.

The integration claim made by IFS therapists working in the polyvagal-informed integration is that **Self-energy maps onto ventral vagal activation**. When a client is in Self (the eight C's: calm, curiosity, compassion, clarity, confidence, courage, creativity, connectedness), their nervous system is in ventral vagal state. When parts take over, the client moves into sympathetic (often Firefighters) or dorsal (some Exile presentations). The therapist's job is to support Self-leadership, which is also support for ventral vagal anchoring. Generously read, this is vocabulary alignment between two productive frameworks. The eight C's and ventral vagal both describe a "regulated, relationally-open state"; calling them the same state is observationally reasonable. Skeptically read, this is vocabulary alignment, not mechanism. IFS's effectiveness data was developed without PVT; PVT's mechanism claims are independent of IFS. Mapping one onto the other is an interpretive convenience for the therapist, not an additional evidence claim.

For an autistic adult considering an IFS therapist who refers heavily to polyvagal mechanism, the question is whether the therapist is using polyvagal language as scaffolding (describing parts and states in a unified vocabulary) or as mechanism (claiming biological specificity for the IFS-PVT mapping). The first is fine; the second imports PVT's contested claims into IFS's separate evidence base.

## Mental Health Comorbidities: The Transdiagnostic Layer

An autistic adult navigating polyvagal-informed therapy is almost certainly navigating one or more psychiatric comorbidities at the same time. Population-level estimates vary by sample and method, but converging data suggests around 70% of autistic individuals carry at least one comorbid psychiatric diagnosis, and roughly 40% carry two or more. Pooled lifetime prevalence estimates for major comorbidities in autistic adults are approximately 28–42% for anxiety disorders, 23–37% for depression, and a much wider range (11–60%) for PTSD depending on population and trauma exposure history.

> **Evidence note.** Prevalence ranges have validity 8/10 — they are appropriate as ranges but legitimately vary by study sample, diagnostic instrument, age, gender, and inclusion criteria. Treat the specific percentages as approximate band-widths rather than precise figures.

This is where Theodore Beauchaine's transdiagnostic biomarker work enters the picture. Beauchaine's 2015 paper in *Current Opinion in Psychology* (Vol. 3, 43–47; PMC4389219) titled "Respiratory Sinus Arrhythmia: A Transdiagnostic Biomarker of Emotion Dysregulation and Psychopathology" established that low resting RSA — the parasympathetic-vagal index of cardiac control — is observed across an unusually wide range of psychiatric conditions. Internalising disorders (depression, anxiety), externalising disorders (conduct disorder, ODD), schizophrenia spectrum, autism spectrum, nonsuicidal self-injury, and PTSD all show reduced resting RSA relative to controls.

<!-- DIAGRAM: rsa-transdiagnostic -->

This finding has been replicated and extended. Beauchaine and Cicchetti's 2019 meta-analysis in *Psychophysiology* (PMC6453712) examined RSA reactivity (rather than baseline) across empirically-based structural dimensions of psychopathology and found a more complex picture. RSA *withdrawal* — active reduction in RSA during stress — is most consistently associated with externalising behaviour dimensions. For internalising disorders (depression, anxiety) and thought-problem dimensions, the reactivity pattern is less consistent. The Chalmers et al. 2014 anxiety-disorders meta-analysis aggregated 36 studies, n = 2,086 patients with anxiety disorders and n = 2,294 controls, and confirmed lower HRV (high-frequency component) in anxiety disorders. Campbell et al.'s 2019 meta-analysis (PubMed 30779926) confirmed lower resting RSA in PTSD relative to controls.

The implication is sharp. **If low RSA appears across at least seven different psychiatric categories, then "low RSA" by itself does not localise to any polyvagal-defined dysregulation.** Anything that improves RSA may help across this transdiagnostic landscape, but the improvement does not specifically validate polyvagal mechanism. This is exactly what the transdiagnostic finding undermines. PVT-informed therapies that treat "the nervous system" as the unit of intervention may genuinely help across these comorbidities — and the autistic adult who improves on polyvagal-informed therapy should not interpret this improvement as evidence specifically *for* polyvagal theory rather than for general emotion-regulation work.

## Treatment Selection for the Autistic Adult with Comorbidities

The practical question for the autistic adult is where polyvagal-informed therapy fits relative to gold-standard treatments for the comorbidities they actually carry. The honest answer is **adjunct or wraparound, not primary treatment for any specific condition**.

<!-- DIAGRAM: treatment-selection-map -->

For anxiety disorders, CBT (autism-adapted) and exposure-based treatments have substantial evidence; polyvagal-informed therapy can serve as adjunct or wraparound, especially for clients who do not engage well with pure CBT or who need autonomic-state scaffolding to tolerate exposure work. For depression, CBT and behavioural activation have strong evidence; the "dorsal shutdown" framing in polyvagal-informed therapy maps onto how some autistic adults describe their depression-adjacent burnout, but this vocabulary supplements rather than replaces primary treatment. For PTSD, prolonged exposure, cognitive processing therapy, and EMDR have substantial evidence bases; Kase's polyvagal-informed EMDR adds pacing sensitivity to the standard protocol, and SE/Sensorimotor/IFS offer independent body-oriented evidence bases for clients who don't tolerate top-down trauma processing.

For autism core features — sensory differences, social differences, executive function — polyvagal-informed therapy is *not* the operative intervention. The operative interventions are environmental and structural: sensory accommodation, demand reduction, occupational therapy. The polyvagal framework may scaffold the conversation between the autistic adult and their support network, but the empirical treatments for autistic burnout (Raymaker's allostatic-load framework, see [Category 5](../meltdown-shutdown-autistic-burnout/index.html)) and for sensory overwhelm are environmental and structural. Marketing that frames polyvagal-informed therapy as a treatment for autism itself (most notably some Safe and Sound Protocol claims) outruns the evidence.

A common autistic-adult presentation involves multiple comorbidities — say, anxiety + autistic burnout + complex trauma + persistent sensory overwhelm. For such a presentation, the question is not "should I do polyvagal-informed therapy *or* gold-standard treatment for my specific condition?" It is "what combination addresses my actual presenting picture?" A polyvagal-informed therapist who is *also* competent in CBT, EMDR, and autism-specific accommodation work is much more valuable than a polyvagal-informed therapist who treats polyvagal vocabulary as a comprehensive framework for everything. Polyvagal certifications can be obtained without first developing core trauma-therapy competence — which is the single highest-risk scenario for the client. An autistic adult should ask about a prospective therapist's prior trauma training and theoretical orientation before assuming polyvagal competence equates to general trauma competence.

## The Central Evaluation: Which Practices Actually Require PVT?

This is the chapter's analytical anchor and the question the user came here to answer. Set out as a two-column evaluation, the picture is sharp.

<!-- DIAGRAM: which-practices-require-pvt -->

On the left side — practices whose evidence base is independent of polyvagal theory being biologically correct — are the things a skilled trauma-informed therapist actually does in session. **Attuned therapeutic presence** rests on Bowlby, Ainsworth, Sroufe, and Rogers — well before PVT. **Paced breathing at roughly six breaths per minute** rests on Lehrer, Bernardi, Russo, and the well-characterised baroreflex-resonance mechanism; FDA-cleared devices for slow-paced breathing exist. **Titration and pendulation** were coined by Peter Levine in the 1970s, before his first meeting with Porges in 1978. **Co-regulation as therapeutic stance** has its attachment-theory grounding from 1969. **Body awareness and somatic tracking** descend from Hakomi (Kurtz 1970s), bioenergetics (Lowen 1958), Gestalt body work, and Reich. **EMDR's eight standard phases** were developed by Shapiro 1989 onwards. **IFS as parts-based therapy** was developed by Schwartz 1980s onwards. **Mindfulness-based interventions** — MBSR (Kabat-Zinn 1979), DBT mindfulness module (Linehan 1993), MBCT (Segal, Williams, Teasdale 2001) — all predate or are independent of polyvagal theory.

On the right side — claims that specifically require polyvagal theory being correct — are the explicit mechanism assertions. **"We activate your ventral vagal complex"** is the canonical case; the Safe and Sound Protocol's marketing rests on this specific mechanism claim. If PVT's anatomical predictions don't hold, this story is at risk. **"Dorsal vagal shutdown" as biological description** — when collapse is described not metaphorically but as specifically dorsal-motor-nucleus-mediated — is exactly what the 2026 Untenable consensus contests. **"Neuroception" as a distinct biological process** distinct from established threat-detection circuitry (amygdala-driven, vmPFC top-down) is unsupported as biological mechanism, though useful as metaphor for sub-conscious safety assessment. **"Polyvagal training" of vagal tone**, when marketed as specifically training PVT-defined pathways rather than as general cardiovascular conditioning, depends on PVT's specificity.

The practical bottom line is simple. The interventions and stances on the left side of this map — which include essentially everything a skilled trauma-informed therapist actually does in session — have evidence bases independent of polyvagal theory. The claims on the right side are precisely the ones the 2026 Untenable consensus disputes. **If you're an autistic adult weighing whether to pursue polyvagal-informed therapy, the practices are very likely to help for reasons that don't depend on PVT being biologically correct.** The contested theory is doing branding work, not therapeutic work. This is a strength for the client (the practices help even if the theory is wrong) and a weakness for the theory's specific claims (the clinical successes don't privilege PVT over alternative frameworks).

## Scaffolding vs Mechanism

The single most useful conceptual move you can make when navigating this landscape is to distinguish polyvagal language as **psychological scaffolding for the client** from polyvagal language as **biological mechanism claim**. Both modes coexist in actual clinical practice; the skill is in noticing which mode is being deployed at any given moment.

<!-- DIAGRAM: scaffolding-vs-mechanism -->

As scaffolding, polyvagal language gives clients a vocabulary for self-tracking ("I notice I'm in dorsal"), externalises the experience of dysregulation ("my nervous system is in survival mode" rather than "I am broken"), supports compassionate self-reflection rather than self-blame, opens the door to body-based interventions without requiring the client to first agree that they have a psychological problem, and provides a shared vocabulary for therapist-client coordination. None of these benefits requires PVT being biologically correct; they require it to be psychologically useful. If the framing helps the client function and engage with therapy, the framing is doing its job regardless of the underlying neuroscience.

As mechanism, polyvagal language makes specific claims. The brainstem nuclei claims (DMN vs NA, ventral vagal complex as evolutionarily distinct substrate) are what the Untenable consensus contests. The evolutionary phylogeny claims (mammals uniquely evolved a myelinated cardiac vagus) are contested by evidence of myelinated cardiac vagal fibres in lungfish, fish, reptiles, and birds. The distinct social engagement system claim — that the face, voice, gaze, and heart are linked through a specifically polyvagal integration — has broader and not-PVT-unique evidence in the comparative neuroscience literature. The "we activate your ventral vagal complex" intervention claim rests on the first set of claims being correct. The "polyvagal training of vagal tone" marketing claim depends on PVT-specific pathway specification rather than the general cardiovascular routes (the baroreflex, vagal afferent activity) that explain most slow-breathing and somatic effects.

The take-home for the user is this: when a therapist uses polyvagal language as scaffolding (a way of teaching self-tracking and self-compassion), the practice is likely worth engaging with even if the underlying theory is contested. When a therapist or program rests *specifically on the mechanism* — claiming to activate or change a specific neural pathway — that's where to apply scrutiny. The two modes can coexist in a single clinical practice. A skilled, evidence-careful therapist will use polyvagal vocabulary as scaffolding without making mechanism claims they can't defend. A marketing-driven training program may collapse the distinction in pursuit of authoritative-sounding biology. The skill is in noticing which is happening at any given moment.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception: "Polyvagal-informed therapy is fundamentally different from other trauma therapies."** The core practices — attuned presence, paced arousal regulation, body awareness, somatic tracking — overlap heavily with other trauma-informed approaches. PVT adds a vocabulary and an explicit autonomic-state targeting; it does not introduce fundamentally new techniques.

**Misconception: "If polyvagal-informed therapy helps me, that validates polyvagal theory."** The therapy can help for reasons that don't require PVT being right (attachment-based co-regulation, well-established breathing physiology, the somatic tradition's body-awareness practices, the alliance/relationship variable). Therapeutic benefit does not specifically validate the contested neuroscience.

**Misconception: "Polyvagal-informed therapists are doing something biologically specific to my vagus nerve."** The interventions used in session (talking, breathing, body awareness, mindful attention) have systemic physiological effects, but the claim that they target specific polyvagal-defined pathways is exactly what is contested. Most therapeutic effects do not require this specificity.

**Misconception: "Co-regulation is a polyvagal concept."** Co-regulation as a clinical and developmental concept predates polyvagal theory by more than 25 years. Bowlby, Ainsworth, and Sroufe established the practice and the evidence base; polyvagal theory later offered a neural-mechanism story that is now contested.

## Edge Cases and Cautions

**The autistic adult who finds the ladder vocabulary unhelpful.** Some autistic adults — particularly those with strong alexithymia features — report that the ladder doesn't map onto their experience. They don't experience clear discrete states, or they experience overlap of "ventral" and "dorsal" simultaneously, or the colour-coding feels arbitrary. This is not a failure of the client; it reflects the ladder's status as a clinical heuristic rather than a measured biological state. Alternative frameworks (predictive processing, allostatic load — see [Category 9](../alternative-competing-models/index.html)) may map better.

**The trauma client where polyvagal language inadvertently scaffolds avoidance.** For some clients, the framing "my nervous system needs to be in ventral first" can become a justification for indefinitely avoiding necessary therapeutic work. A skilled therapist holds the dialectic — supporting regulation *and* approaching the difficult material. A less skilled application can use polyvagal language to perpetually defer the work. There is no published systematic study of this failure mode, but it is a recognised anecdotal concern in trauma-therapy supervision.

**The therapist whose polyvagal training is the only trauma training they've had.** This is the highest-risk scenario for the client. Polyvagal-informed certifications can be obtained without first developing core trauma-therapy competence. An autistic adult should ask about the therapist's prior trauma training and theoretical orientation before assuming polyvagal competence equates to general trauma competence.

## Summary

The polyvagal-informed therapy ecosystem is substantial, well-trained, and clinically influential. Deb Dana's *Rhythm of Regulation* framework — codified across her 2018, 2020, and 2021 books — is the canonical clinical operationalisation. Rebecca Kase's *Polyvagal-Informed EMDR* (2023) adds polyvagal orientation to the standard EMDR protocol without modifying it. Peter Levine's Somatic Experiencing, Pat Ogden's Sensorimotor Psychotherapy, and Richard Schwartz's Internal Family Systems all developed independently of polyvagal theory and have absorbed polyvagal vocabulary into otherwise-complete frameworks. Beauchaine's transdiagnostic biomarker work — low resting RSA across anxiety, depression, PTSD, autism, conduct disorder, NSSI, and schizophrenia — undermines PVT-specificity claims while affirming the general value of emotion-regulation interventions.

The chapter's central analytical contribution is the **scaffolding-vs-mechanism distinction**. Polyvagal language as psychological scaffolding for clients can be genuinely helpful regardless of whether PVT's anatomical claims hold. Polyvagal language as biological mechanism claim depends on those anatomical claims and is what the 2026 Untenable consensus disputes. Most therapy practices wrapped in polyvagal language fall in the first category. The interventions and stances that work — attuned presence, paced breathing, titration, co-regulation, body awareness, EMDR's eight phases, IFS's parts work — have independent evidence bases. The contested theory is doing branding work; the practices are doing therapeutic work.

For an autistic adult navigating burnout and comorbidities, polyvagal-informed therapy is reasonably framed as adjunct or wraparound — never as a substitute for gold-standard treatment of specific comorbidities, and never as a treatment for autism core features. The operative interventions for autistic core experience remain environmental and structural: sensory accommodation, demand reduction, occupational support. The polyvagal framework may scaffold the conversation; it does not replace the structural changes that actually move the dial.
