# Avaz AAC: India's Voice for the Voiceless

In a world where approximately 97% of people who need augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) tools live in developing countries, access to affordable speech technology is not merely a convenience -- it is a fundamental human right. When Ajit Narayanan, a young engineer from IIT Madras, walked into his first special education classroom in India, he encountered children with autism and cerebral palsy who had no way to express their thoughts, needs, or emotions. The AAC devices available at the time cost between $5,000 and $10,000 and supported only English. For Indian families, these tools were simply out of reach. Narayanan set out to change that, and the result was Avaz -- a word meaning "Voice" in Hindi -- which would become India's first AAC intervention and one of the most innovative communication apps in the world.

Today, Avaz AAC stands as a unique force in the AAC landscape: the only major communication app available on both iOS and Android, supporting over 13 languages including 8 Indian languages, packed with 40,000+ SymbolStix pictures, and priced at a fraction of its competitors. Now backed by PRC-Saltillo, the world's leading AAC company, Avaz is poised to bring communication to millions of people who have never had a voice.

## The Origin Story: From Chennai Classrooms to MIT Recognition

### The Problem That Sparked a Revolution

The story of Avaz begins in 2009 in the special education classrooms of Chennai, India. Ajit Narayanan, an electrical engineering graduate from the prestigious Indian Institute of Technology Madras, had been working with schools serving children with autism, cerebral palsy, and other complex communication needs. What he found was a vast, unmet need.

In India alone, an estimated 2-3 million children have autism spectrum disorder, and millions more have conditions that affect speech. Yet the AAC devices available in the market were designed for Western users, priced for Western incomes, and programmed in Western languages. A single dedicated AAC device from companies like DynaVox (now Tobii Dynavox) cost upwards of $8,000. For a country where the average annual household income at the time was around $1,500, these devices existed in a parallel universe.

Narayanan's insight was both simple and radical: what if AAC technology could be built on affordable consumer hardware, using languages that Indian children actually spoke, with vocabulary that reflected Indian culture -- the foods, festivals, family structures, and daily routines of South Asian life?

### Building India's First AAC Device

Working closely with speech therapists, special educators, and families across India, Narayanan's team developed the first Avaz device in 2010. By partnering with Asian hardware manufacturers, they produced a dedicated tablet device that cost approximately $800 -- still significant, but a fraction of the $5,000-$10,000 price tag of American equivalents. More importantly, it supported Indian languages with culturally appropriate vocabulary.

The impact was immediate. Special educators and therapists reported that children who had never communicated independently were suddenly able to express wants, make choices, and participate in classroom activities. The device saw incredible adoption across India's network of special education schools.

### MIT Technology Review Recognition

The innovation did not go unnoticed. In 2010, Avaz won the National Award for Empowerment of People with Disabilities from the President of India -- the country's highest honor in the disability sector. Then, in 2011, came the recognition that would put Avaz on the global map: Ajit Narayanan was named "Innovator of the Year" by MIT Technology Review as part of their prestigious TR35 list of innovators under 35. Avaz was the first product in the disability category to ever receive this recognition.

In 2013, Narayanan took the TED stage in Long Beach, California, delivering a talk that would accumulate over one million views. He demonstrated not just Avaz but also FreeSpeech, a revolutionary visual language tool that maps relationships between words spatially rather than linearly -- an approach that resonated deeply with how many people with autism process information.

## How Avaz Works: Two Modes, One Voice

At its core, Avaz AAC operates through two complementary communication modes that allow users to grow with the app as their skills develop.

### Picture Mode: Visual Communication

Picture Mode is Avaz's symbol-based communication system, designed for users who communicate primarily through images. The app comes pre-loaded with over 15,000 SymbolStix pictures from a library of more than 40,000, covering an enormous range of words, concepts, and ideas.

Symbols are organized in a grid layout using the **Fitzgerald Key color coding system**, a decades-old educational convention that color-codes words by their grammatical function:

- **Yellow**: People and pronouns
- **Green**: Verbs and actions
- **Orange**: Adjectives and descriptors
- **Blue**: Nouns and objects
- **Pink**: Social phrases and interjections
- **White/Gray**: Miscellaneous and function words

This color coding is not arbitrary -- it is consistent with materials used across special education classrooms worldwide, allowing children to transfer their understanding between the app and other learning environments.

Users build sentences by tapping symbols in sequence. A sentence strip at the top of the screen shows the constructed message, which can then be spoken aloud using high-quality text-to-speech voices. The system uses **consistent motor planning**, meaning that core words appear in the same position across all vocabulary levels, so users develop muscle memory that accelerates communication speed over time.

### Keyboard Mode: Text-Based Communication

For users who can read and type, Keyboard Mode provides a full QWERTY keyboard with predictive text. This mode is essential for older users, adults with acquired communication disorders (such as aphasia from stroke), and children who have progressed beyond symbol-based communication.

The seamless **Quick Toggle** feature allows users to switch between Picture and Keyboard modes instantly within the same conversation, accommodating the reality that many AAC users benefit from both approaches depending on the context.

### The Core Word Philosophy

Avaz's vocabulary is organized around a research-backed principle: core words -- the small set of words that make up approximately 80% of everything we say -- should be the foundation of AAC communication. Words like "want," "go," "more," "help," and "like" are used constantly across every conversation context, from ordering food to playing with friends to expressing emotions.

Avaz organizes these core words into three graded vocabulary levels based on generative language development principles. The **Frozen Row** feature ensures that the most critical core words remain visible and accessible at all times, regardless of which category folder the user is browsing. This means a child can always reach "I want" or "help" without navigating away from their current screen.

## What Makes Avaz Unique

### Cross-Platform: iOS AND Android

Perhaps Avaz's single most distinctive feature in the AAC market is its availability on both iOS and Android. This might sound unremarkable in consumer software, but in the AAC world, it is revolutionary.

The dominant AAC apps -- Proloquo2Go ($249.99), TouchChat HD ($299.99), and LAMP Words for Life ($299.99) -- are all available exclusively on Apple's iOS platform. This means that families must purchase an iPad or iPhone before they can even begin using these tools. In the United States, where iOS market share hovers around 55%, this is a significant but manageable limitation. In India, where Android commands over 95% of the smartphone market, iOS exclusivity effectively locks out the vast majority of families.

Avaz's Android support is not an afterthought -- it is a core design principle rooted in the company's mission to make AAC accessible in developing countries where Android devices are far more affordable and prevalent.

### 8+ Indian Language Support

Avaz supports more Indian languages than any other AAC app in the world:

- **Hindi** -- India's most widely spoken language (over 500 million speakers)
- **Tamil** -- Primary language of Tamil Nadu and Sri Lankan Tamils
- **Kannada** -- Official language of Karnataka state
- **Malayalam** -- Language of Kerala
- **Telugu** -- Spoken by over 80 million people in Andhra Pradesh and Telangana
- **Marathi** -- Official language of Maharashtra
- **Gujarati** -- Language of Gujarat (personalizable vocabulary)
- **Bengali** -- Spoken by over 230 million people across India and Bangladesh (personalizable vocabulary)

Each language version includes culturally relevant vocabulary -- not just translated English words, but terms specific to regional foods, festivals, currencies, clothing, and family structures. A child in Tamil Nadu can talk about idli and dosa at breakfast; a child in Gujarat can reference Navratri celebrations. This cultural specificity makes communication feel natural and authentic rather than foreign.

In addition to Indian languages, Avaz also supports English (multiple regional variants), Spanish, French, Portuguese, and Arabic, bringing the total to over 13 languages.

### AI-Powered Expressive Tones

One of Avaz's most innovative recent features is Expressive Tones, which uses AI technology to infuse emotional nuance into the synthesized speech output. Users can select from tones including excitement, frustration, sarcasm, sadness, and curiosity, allowing their AAC voice to convey not just words but feelings.

For AAC users, this is profoundly meaningful. Traditional text-to-speech produces flat, monotone output that strips away the emotional content of communication. When a child says "I want to go to the park" with excitement versus frustration, the meaning changes entirely. Expressive Tones bridges that gap, giving users a more authentic voice.

### Media-Rich Communication

Avaz goes beyond traditional symbol-and-text AAC with several media features that feel genuinely modern:

- **YouTube Integration**: A built-in YouTube search and player with custom start/stop times and no ads or autoplay. Users can share videos as part of their communication.
- **GIFs**: Animated GIFs for expressing humor, emotions, and reactions, with adjustable loop counts to manage sensory input for users who are sensitive to repetitive visual stimulation.
- **Animation**: Visual reinforcement through animation when words are selected -- a feature unique among AAC apps that provides satisfying feedback and engagement.

### Built-In Caregiver Training: The Avaz Dashboard

One of the most common barriers to successful AAC use is not the technology itself but the lack of training for the adults supporting the AAC user. Parents, teachers, and therapists often receive an app and are left to figure it out on their own.

Avaz addresses this directly with the **Avaz Dashboard**, a built-in tutorial system that provides hands-on guidance for AAC strategies across more than 10 real-world scenarios: mealtimes, circle time, reading activities, playground interactions, and more. Caregivers receive elaborate tips and strategy guidance without ever leaving the app, dramatically lowering the learning curve.

### Progress Tracking and Analytics

Avaz includes a comprehensive analytics system that tracks communication development through rich graphs and charts. Therapists and parents can monitor word usage patterns, communication frequency, vocabulary growth, and session engagement over time. Email notifications keep stakeholders informed about therapy progress, creating accountability and visibility into the user's communication journey.

## The PRC-Saltillo Acquisition: A New Chapter

In October 2025, Avaz became a wholly owned subsidiary of PRC-Saltillo, the global leader in AAC technology based in Wooster, Ohio. PRC-Saltillo's portfolio includes some of the most recognized names in AAC: TouchChat, LAMP Words for Life, Dialogue AAC, Unity AAC, and dedicated hardware devices including Accent, NovaChat, and Via.

The acquisition was structured to preserve Avaz's identity and mission:
- All Avaz employees were retained in their roles
- No plans to change pricing, availability, or content
- A newly incorporated entity in India strengthens PRC-Saltillo's presence in India and Southeast Asia

PRC-Saltillo CEO Sarah Wilds framed the acquisition in terms of the company's core philosophy: "AAC is a right, not a privilege." The combination positions Avaz to leverage PRC-Saltillo's resources -- research capabilities, clinical partnerships, distribution networks -- while continuing to serve the markets and languages that make it unique.

## Strengths and Limitations

### Strengths

1. **True Cross-Platform Support**: The only major AAC app on both iOS and Android, critical for the 95%+ of users in developing countries on Android devices
2. **Unmatched Multilingual Coverage**: 8+ Indian languages with culturally authentic vocabulary -- no competitor comes close
3. **Most Affordable Premium AAC**: $199.99 lifetime vs. $249-$299 for competitors, with flexible subscription options
4. **Built-In Training**: Avaz Dashboard eliminates the need for external training resources
5. **Research-Based Design**: Core word vocabulary with consistent motor planning and generative language principles
6. **Modern Media Features**: YouTube, GIFs, and animation make communication feel contemporary
7. **AI Expressive Tones**: Emotional voice output that gives users authentic self-expression
8. **Comprehensive Analytics**: Progress tracking with graphs and email notifications
9. **Low Barrier to Entry**: 14-day free trial with no credit card required
10. **World-Class Backing**: PRC-Saltillo acquisition brings global AAC leadership resources

### Limitations

1. **Speech Controls**: Voice speed and pitch adjustment could be more granular; some users report the range is insufficient
2. **US Market Presence**: Smaller user community and fewer SLP resources compared to Proloquo2Go in North America
3. **Subscription Friction**: Some users are frustrated that voice output requires a paid subscription, even though a free trial is available
4. **Prediction Engine**: Sentence and grammar prediction is less sophisticated than some competitors like Proloquo2Go
5. **Navigation Density**: The grid layout can feel overwhelming for users with motor planning difficulties such as apraxia
6. **Ecosystem Size**: Fewer third-party resources, training videos, and community materials compared to established US-market apps

## The Road Ahead

With PRC-Saltillo's backing, Avaz is positioned for significant growth. The acquisition brings access to PRC-Saltillo's clinical research partnerships, hardware integration possibilities, and global distribution channels. For Avaz, this could mean deeper integration with eye-tracking technology, expanded language support, and enhanced prediction algorithms.

The broader AAC market is evolving rapidly, driven by advances in AI, natural language processing, and mobile device capabilities. Avaz's existing investment in AI-powered expressive tones suggests the company is well-positioned to leverage these trends. The combination of affordable pricing, cross-platform availability, and unmatched multilingual support gives Avaz a unique competitive position -- particularly in the Global South, where the vast majority of people who need AAC live but have historically been underserved.

For millions of children and adults around the world who cannot speak, Avaz represents something more than an app. It represents the fundamental promise that technology can be a great equalizer -- that a child in a village in Tamil Nadu deserves the same right to express themselves as a child in a suburb of Boston. In Hindi, Avaz means "Voice." For its users, it is exactly that.
