## 1. Why This Page Exists

This page integrates the framework, strategies, and recognition patterns of the entire sub-hub for the specific context of intimate partner relationships. Intimate partner contexts are the most lethal context in the sub-hub. They are also the context where autistic adults are most disproportionately targeted, where the cultural script of "communication problems in relationships" most aggressively obscures coercive control, and where the failure to recognize a manipulation pattern in time can have the highest material consequences — including death.

The sub-hub user has said, of this entire project: *"Of all that this project will accomplish, if it can help autistics combat this effectively, I'll have considered it a resounding success."* This page is the page where that goal is most directly tested. Every choice in writing it has been calibrated for the stakes.

The page does five things, in order of safety priority:

1. **Front-loads lethality and immediate safety information** so that a reader in crisis reaches the protective information before reaching anything else.
2. **Names why intimate partner contexts are specifically more dangerous for autistic adults**, drawing on the convergent autism-IPV research literature.
3. **Integrates the Tier 3 pattern pages** to show how the patterns cluster in intimate partner contexts and which configurations are most dangerous.
4. **Provides strategies sequenced by safety priority**, with the explicit framing that some strategies are contraindicated in high-risk contexts and that the responsible move is sometimes "consult an advocate first."
5. **Names what this page cannot do**, honestly and prominently, because the gap between recognition and protection is real and the page should not pretend otherwise.

The reframe this page offers, in one sentence: **intimate partner manipulation of autistic adults is not a "communication problem in a difficult relationship"; it is a recognizable pattern of coercive control in which the autistic person's neurology is being weaponized against them, and the appropriate response is the response the DV literature has developed over forty years — safety planning, advocacy, documentation, exit support — adapted for the autism-specific context.** Everything else on the page elaborates that reframe and provides the tools to act on it.

## 2. Lethality and Immediate Safety

This section is the highest-priority section on the page. It is the section a reader in crisis needs to find before any other content.

### 2.1 The strangulation finding

**If your partner has ever strangled you, choked you, or restricted your breathing — even once, even briefly, even "playfully" — your risk of being killed by this partner is dramatically elevated above the baseline risk for abused women.** This is not metaphor. This is the strongest single lethality predictor in the empirical IPV literature.

**The primary source:** Glass, N., Laughon, K., Campbell, J., Block, C. R., Hanson, G., Sharps, P. W., & Taliaferro, E. (2008). "Non-Fatal Strangulation Is an Important Risk Factor for Homicide of Women." *Journal of Emergency Medicine* 35(3): 329–335. PMC2573025.

**The findings:**
- Non-fatal strangulation was reported in 10% of abused controls, **45% of attempted homicides, and 43% of completed homicides.**
- Prior non-fatal strangulation was associated with **>6× odds (OR 6.70, 95% CI 3.91–11.49)** of becoming an attempted homicide case.
- Prior non-fatal strangulation was associated with **>7× odds (OR 7.48, 95% CI 4.53–12.35)** of becoming a completed homicide case.

**Evidence quality: [VALIDATED] across multiple studies and replicated globally.** The finding was strong enough that, as of 2023, all 50 US states have criminalized non-fatal strangulation as a felony or aggravated assault, largely on the basis of this research. International criminalization has followed in many jurisdictions.

**What this means for you:**

1. **Strangulation is a medical emergency**, not just a "fight that got physical." Carotid artery damage, brain injury, and delayed mortality are documented consequences of non-fatal strangulation, sometimes occurring days or weeks after the event with no immediate symptoms. If you have been strangled, seek medical evaluation. The Training Institute on Strangulation Prevention (strangulationtraininginstitute.com) maintains clinical protocols.
2. **Strangulation history is itself sufficient grounds for advocate contact**, regardless of how the incident was framed at the time, regardless of whether you stayed afterward, regardless of whether your partner apologized, regardless of how long ago it was. Advocates trained in lethality assessment will treat any history of strangulation as an elevated-risk indicator and will adjust safety planning accordingly.
3. **The risk does not go down if you stay quiet.** The OR 7.48 finding describes the relationship between the underlying pattern and the eventual outcome, not between disclosure and outcome. Quiet does not protect you from the risk; it only protects the abuser from accountability.

If you have ever been strangled by your partner, **stop reading this page and contact an advocate**. The Crisis Resources Directory lists hotlines you can call from a device your partner does not control. This is more important than anything else this page can teach you.

### 2.2 The separation finding

**The single most dangerous time in an abusive relationship is the period when the survivor is leaving.** Approximately 55.2% of women murdered by an intimate partner had separated or were in the process of leaving (Campbell et al., 2003). The post-separation period is also associated with stalking, legal-system abuse, and post-separation control patterns documented in the more recent literature (Spearman, Hardesty, & Campbell, 2023; PMC11114442).

**The primary source:** Campbell, J. C., Webster, D., Koziol-McLain, J., Block, C. R., Campbell, D., Curry, M. A., Gary, F., Glass, N., McFarlane, J., Sachs, C., Sharps, P., Ulrich, Y., Wilt, S. A., Manganello, J., Xu, X., Schollenberger, J., Frye, V., & Laughon, K. (2003). "Risk Factors for Femicide in Abusive Relationships: Results from a Multisite Case Control Study." *American Journal of Public Health* 93(7): 1089–1097.

**Adjusted odds ratio of 4.07** for femicide under the combination of high control × separation. The risk is not additive; it is multiplicative.

**Evidence quality: [VALIDATED]** across the Campbell research program, replicated in multiple national contexts, and used as the empirical foundation for Campbell's Danger Assessment instrument.

**What this means for you:**

1. **"Just leave" is empirically unsafe advice.** It is one of the most common recommendations from people who do not know the literature, and it is one of the most dangerous. Leaving requires safety planning *before* the separation begins, ideally coordinated with a trained advocate.
2. **If you are considering leaving, the priority is to plan the leaving safely**, not to leave faster. Sullivan & Bybee (1999), in a randomized controlled trial of advocacy plus safety planning, found significant reductions in revictimization among women who received the intervention compared with controls. The safety-planning component is the part that has empirical support; freelance leaving without planning is the dangerous part.
3. **The Crisis Resources Directory lists hotlines that can connect you to safety planners.** Most major DV hotlines (NDVH in the US, Women's Aid in the UK, 1800RESPECT in Australia, others listed in the Crisis Resources page) have trained staff who do safety planning over the phone. Free. Confidential (with mandatory-reporting caveats noted on the Crisis Resources page).
4. **Post-separation safety is part of the planning.** Stalking, legal-system abuse, custody disputes, financial sabotage, harassment of new partners and friends, and violations of restraining orders are documented features of the post-separation period and need to be planned for as part of the leaving, not after.

The Safety Planning strategy page treats the planning process in detail, including the eight autism-specific adaptations from the 2024 Autism Adapted Safety Plans pilot RCT. The point of this section is to flag the structural fact: **leaving is when the danger spikes, and the response to that fact is planning, not staying.**

### 2.3 Other validated lethality indicators

In addition to strangulation and separation contemplation, the Campbell research program and related work (Hilton et al. on ODARA; Kropp et al. on SARA; Messing et al. on the Lethality Screen) have established a set of validated lethality indicators. The presence of any of these in your relationship is grounds for advocate contact, not for waiting:

- **Access to firearms.** Single strongest gun-specific predictor of lethal outcome.
- **Prior threats with a weapon.** Even if no use occurred.
- **Threats to kill.** Even if framed as "in the heat of the moment."
- **Escalating frequency or severity of violence.** Trajectory matters.
- **Violent jealousy.** Particularly jealousy combined with controlling behaviors.
- **Forced sex / sexual coercion.**
- **Abuser threats of suicide or attempts.** Counterintuitively, abuser suicidality is associated with elevated victim homicide risk (murder-suicide patterns).
- **Unemployment of the abuser.** Particularly recent job loss.
- **Abuse during pregnancy.**
- **A child in the home who is not biologically the abuser's.**
- **Controlling most of the survivor's daily activities.** This is the coercive control pattern Stark named, and it has been quantitatively associated with elevated lethality.

**Validated risk-assessment instruments** for use by trained advocates and clinicians:
- **Danger Assessment (DA)** — Campbell. The gold standard for IPV femicide risk prediction. Available at dangerassessment.org. Should be administered by a trained advocate, not by the survivor alone.
- **Ontario Domestic Assault Risk Assessment (ODARA)** — Hilton et al. Brief, actuarial.
- **Spousal Assault Risk Assessment (SARA)** — Kropp, Hart, Webster, Eaves. Structured professional judgment.
- **Lethality Screen (LS)** — Messing et al. Derived from the Danger Assessment for use by first responders.

**What this section is not:** a self-administered diagnostic tool. The Danger Assessment in particular is not a DIY instrument. Its proper use requires a trained advocate. The point of listing these indicators is to give you a vocabulary for what counts as elevated risk and to make explicit that the appropriate response to any of them is to contact an advocate, not to wait until the situation gets worse.

### 2.4 The autism-specific lethality picture

The general DV lethality literature is well-validated. The autism-specific extension is more recent and more limited. The convergent finding from the Pearson research program, Douglas & Sedgewick (2024), and Cooke et al. (2025) is that **autistic adults experience IPV at substantially higher rates than non-autistic adults**, that the gender protective effect that exists in the general population is **attenuated or absent** in autistic samples (Douglas & Sedgewick found no gender protection — autistic men experienced IPV and sexual violence at rates similar to autistic women and non-binary participants), and that the patterns include specific autism-targeted forms (the weaponized features framework).

**The most direct prevalence estimate:** Cazalis, F., Reyes, E., Leduc, S., & Gourion, D. (2022). "Evidence That Nine Autistic Women Out of Ten Have Been Victims of Sexual Violence." *Frontiers in Behavioral Neuroscience.* PMC9087551. The 88.4% figure should not be generalized uncritically — the sample is self-selected — but the finding converges with other qualitative work suggesting that autistic women, in particular, experience sexual violence at rates that are very high relative to the general population baseline.

**The mixed-methods systematic review:** Cooke, K., Ridgway, K., Pecora, L., Westrupp, E., Hedley, D., Hooley, M., & Stokes, M. A. (2025). "Outcomes of Experiencing Interpersonal Violence in Autism: A Mixed Methods Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis." *Trauma, Violence & Abuse.* This is currently the most authoritative single source on the topic.

**The qualitative depth:**
- Pearson, A., Rose, K., & Rees, J. (2023). "'I felt like I deserved it because I was autistic': Understanding the impact of interpersonal victimisation in the lives of autistic people." *Autism.*
- Pearson, A., Rose, K., Mitchell, A., Joseph, W., Douglas, S., Sedgewick, F., & Botha, M. (2024). "'It's Not a Physical Prison but You Can't Get Out.' How Autistic Adults Make Sense of the Experience of Intimate Violence and Abuse." *Autism in Adulthood.*
- Douglas, S., & Sedgewick, F. (2024). "Experiences of interpersonal victimization and abuse among autistic people." *Autism* 28(7): 1732–1745. PMC11191373.

**Evidence quality: [PRELIMINARY-to-MODERATE].** The qualitative findings are converging across multiple research groups and across multiple countries. The prevalence estimates have wider uncertainty due to sampling limitations. The "weaponized features" framing has strong qualitative support and is increasingly accepted in the autistic-led research literature, but has not been operationalized in standardized assessment instruments.

**What this means for you:** The intimate partner risk you face as an autistic adult is, on average, higher than the risk faced by the general population, and the patterns include specific forms calibrated to your neurology. Any framing that treats your relationship difficulties as "just communication problems" or "not as bad as real abuse" is operating against this evidence. The autism-specific elevation is real, the autism-specific weaponization is real, and the appropriate response is to take the warning signs seriously even when other people in your life are telling you not to.

## 3. Why Intimate Partner Contexts Are Different for Autistic Readers

The general DV literature applies to autistic IPV survivors. Everything in Sections 2.1–2.3 above is as true for you as for anyone else. The strangulation finding, the separation finding, the validated lethality indicators — all generalize. The autism-specific dimension is *additive*: it adds risks and complications on top of the general picture, not in place of it.

This section names the additional dimensions.

### 3.1 The weaponized features framework

Douglas & Sedgewick (2024) introduced the *weaponized features* framing as a conceptual contribution from autistic-led qualitative research. The argument: perpetrators of abuse against autistic people often appear to **deliberately** exploit autism-related features as components of their tactics. The features are not the cause of the abuse; the *exploitation* of the features is the tactic.

**Examples documented in the qualitative literature** (and which appear repeatedly across the Pearson, Douglas & Sedgewick, and broader autistic survivor accounts):

- **Sensory triggering.** The abuser learns the autistic partner's specific sensory triggers and engineers environments designed to provoke meltdowns. The meltdowns are then reframed as the autistic partner's instability or violence. (See [Sensory Gaslighting](manipulation-hub-pattern-sensory-gaslighting.md) and [Post-Meltdown Shaming](manipulation-hub-pattern-post-meltdown-shaming.md).)
- **Alexithymia exploitation.** The abuser confidently labels the autistic partner's emotional states for them ("you're not angry, you're just tired"), exploiting the autistic partner's reduced confidence in their own emotional self-reporting. (See [Emotional Invalidation Double Bind](manipulation-hub-pattern-emotional-invalidation-double-bind.md) and [Gaslighting](manipulation-hub-pattern-gaslighting.md), section 3.4.)
- **Trust and literal interpretation.** The abuser makes promises that will be denied later, using language the autistic partner takes at face value when the abuser did not intend it that way. (See [Memory Gaslighting](manipulation-hub-pattern-memory-gaslighting.md).)
- **Diagnostic vocabulary as discrediting frame.** "That's your autism talking." (See [Diagnostic Weaponization](manipulation-hub-pattern-diagnostic-weaponization.md).)
- **Routine and predictability needs as control mechanisms.** The abuser disrupts routines as punishment, knowing the autistic partner depends on predictability to function.
- **Masking demands as compliance pressure.** "If you would just act normal, none of this would happen." (See [The Compliance Trap](manipulation-hub-pattern-compliance-trap.md).)
- **Accommodation debt as leverage.** The accommodations the autistic partner needs are reframed as gifts the abuser is giving and can withdraw. (See [Weaponized Accommodations](manipulation-hub-pattern-weaponized-accommodations.md).)
- **Information asymmetry exploitation.** The abuser manages logistics, finances, schedules, and bureaucratic tasks the autistic partner cannot reliably handle, and uses control of the resulting information as leverage. (See [Information Asymmetry Exploitation](manipulation-hub-pattern-information-asymmetry-exploitation.md).)

The weaponized features framing is the most important conceptual contribution the autism-IPV literature has made to date, and it is the framing this page is built on.

### 3.2 The "I am too much" internalization

Pearson, Rose & Rees (2023) titled their qualitative study "'I felt like I deserved it because I was autistic'" because that phrase, or close variants, recurred across their interview participants. The phrase is the endpoint of a successful internalization: the autistic person no longer needs the abuser to discount them, because they are doing it themselves. The diagnostic frame has become the operating premise.

This internalization is the *substrate* on which intimate partner manipulation operates against autistic adults. By the time an adult abuser arrives, the conviction that the autistic person is *too much*, *too difficult*, *too sensitive*, *too needy*, *too much trouble for anyone to put up with* is often already installed by years of childhood and adolescent messaging. The abuser does not have to construct the discrediting frame; the cultural and developmental context has constructed it for them. The abuser only has to activate it.

The implication: the protective work of recognizing and exiting an abusive intimate partner relationship is, for many autistic adults, also the work of unbinding the internalization that the abuse is deserved. This is one of the longer arcs in autism-specific IPV recovery, and it is one of the dimensions where trauma-informed therapy paired with autistic peer reality-checking is most consequential. (See [Recovery and Post-Traumatic Growth](manipulation-hub-strategy-recovery.md) and Section 8.10 of this page.)

### 3.3 The credibility discount, applied to IPV reporting

Lim, Young & Brewer (2022, PMC8813809) demonstrated that autistic adults are perceived as more deceptive and less credible than non-autistic adults *when telling the truth*. The effect is driven by communication features (gaze, prosody, affect) that observers read as "off" without being able to articulate what they noticed. The effect operates below conscious awareness.

**For IPV reporting, this finding is structural and consequential.** When an autistic survivor discloses abuse — to a friend, a family member, a doctor, a therapist, a police officer, a child welfare worker, a judge, a custody evaluator — the listener defaults to reading the survivor as less credible than they would read a non-autistic survivor saying the same words. The abuser, who is more likely to be non-autistic, is correspondingly read as more credible. The credibility differential is pre-loaded before any specific facts of the case are considered.

The implication: external evidence is not optional for autistic IPV survivors. Verbal testimony alone is structurally undervalued. This is one reason the Documentation, Building External Evidence, and Reality-Check Networks strategy pages are foundational rather than supplementary on this hub. The credibility gap has to be closed by evidence that does not depend on the survivor's verbal account.

### 3.4 The double empathy problem in intimate partner contexts

Milton (2012) introduced the *double empathy problem*: the difficulty in autistic-allistic communication is bidirectional, not a one-sided autistic deficit. Crompton et al. (2020) provided the empirical anchor: autistic-to-autistic information transfer is highly effective, more effective than mixed-neurotype transfer.

The implication for intimate partner contexts is twofold. First, **autistic-allistic intimate partnerships face a structural communication challenge that is bidirectional**, and partnerships that try to address that challenge through "the autistic person needs to communicate better" framings are loading the entire correction onto one side of a two-sided problem. This is sometimes the kernel of a difficult-but-good-faith relationship; it is also sometimes the kernel of the compliance trap as it operates in intimate partner contexts. The distinction matters.

Second, **autistic-autistic partnerships have a different baseline calibration**, and the patterns this page treats can occur in autistic-autistic partnerships as well as in autistic-allistic ones. Manipulation is not exclusive to mixed-neurotype contexts. An autistic-autistic relationship can be coercive, controlling, and lethal. The double empathy framing is not a guarantee of safety in autistic-autistic contexts; it is a corrective to the assumption that all autistic-allistic friction is the autistic partner's fault.

### 3.5 The smaller-network problem

The Vulnerability Experiences Quotient research (Griffiths et al., 2019, PMC6851759) and the broader social-isolation literature establish that autistic adults, on average, have smaller, denser social networks than non-autistic adults. They also have higher rates of social isolation overall.

For intimate partner contexts, this matters because **the standard DV protective factors include access to multiple, independent reality-check sources** — friends, family, colleagues, neighbors, community members who can independently notice that something is wrong and offer alternative perspectives. When the social network is small and dense, several things follow:

- The reality-check sources may overlap with the abuser's network, which makes them less independent.
- Losing one or two reality-check relationships (which abusers often engineer through isolation tactics) can collapse the entire network.
- The autistic person may have fewer alternative relationships to draw on after exit, which makes the post-separation period harder and the emotional cost of leaving higher.
- Autistic peer networks, when they exist, are particularly valuable (Crompton et al., 2020) but are not always present, and many autistic adults arrive at adulthood without a strong autistic peer community to draw on.

The implication: building the reality-check network is itself a strategic priority for autistic adults in or considering exit from intimate partner manipulation, and the building cannot be done quickly. (See Section 8.6.)

### 3.6 The autism-specific safety planning gap (with one promising development)

Until very recently, no formal safety planning protocol existed that was specifically calibrated for autistic survivors. The standard NDVH framework, the Sullivan & Bybee advocacy model, the Glass MyPlan tool, and Campbell's Danger Assessment were all developed in samples that were not autistic-specific.

**The promising development:** A 2024 pilot RCT of Autism Adapted Safety Plans, with associated health economic evaluation in 2025 (BMC Health Services Research, PMC reference in the foundational autism research document), tested an autism-adapted version of standard safety planning protocols. The pilot identified eight specific adaptations needed for autistic survivors:

1. **Concrete, specific language** rather than abstract or metaphorical phrasing.
2. **Visual or written formats** in addition to verbal discussion.
3. **Sensory accommodations** in the safety planning environment itself.
4. **Extra time** for processing.
5. **Routines and predictability** built into the plan.
6. **Recognition of meltdowns** as legitimate features of the survivor's experience that the plan needs to accommodate, not as evidence of instability.
7. **Acknowledgment of executive function differences** — plans that require complex sequencing under stress will not work.
8. **Recognition that masking depletes resources** — the plan cannot assume the survivor will be able to mask their way through a high-stakes interaction with an advocate, an officer, or a court.

**Evidence quality: [PRELIMINARY].** This is a pilot RCT with a small sample. The results are encouraging but the protocol is not yet widely available, and most DV advocates are not yet trained in the autism adaptations. **The implication is not "wait until your local advocate is trained" — it is "ask for these adaptations explicitly and use them as a starting point if your advocate is not familiar with them."** Many advocates, given the explicit list, can apply the adaptations in real time.

The Safety Planning strategy page treats these adaptations in detail. They are flagged here because they are one of the few autism-specific protective developments from the empirical DV literature.

## 4. The Patterns Most Common in Intimate Partner Contexts

This section integrates the Tier 3 pattern pages by showing which patterns are most common in intimate partner contexts and how they typically present. Each subsection cross-references the dedicated page for the pattern; the treatment here is brief and oriented toward the IPV-specific manifestation.

### 4.1 Gaslighting in intimate partner contexts

Gaslighting is the most prevalent pattern in intimate partner abuse. In intimate partner contexts, all six channels from the [Gaslighting](manipulation-hub-pattern-gaslighting.md) page are typically active over time: memory, sensory, diagnostic, emotional, social, and institutional. The relationship becomes a multi-channel gaslighting environment in which the autistic partner is gradually corroded across every dimension of self-knowledge at once.

**The IPV-specific feature:** intimate partners have the most leverage of any relationship for sustained gaslighting because they share the autistic person's daily life. The partner sees the events, was usually present for them, and can rewrite them with the apparent authority of a co-witness. The autistic partner's every disputed memory is being disputed by the only other person who was there.

**The protective implication:** documentation in intimate partner contexts must be calibrated for safety (see Section 8.5). The partner who has access to your physical space and your devices can discover documentation, and discovery can trigger escalation. Safe storage, ideally outside the home and outside any device the partner can access, is the precondition for documentation in this context.

### 4.2 DARVO in intimate partner contexts

DARVO (Deny, Attack, Reverse Victim and Offender) is one of the most reliable patterns in intimate partner abuse. The Freyd, Harsey, and Zurbriggen research has documented it consistently in survivor accounts of confronting abusive partners. (See [DARVO](manipulation-hub-pattern-darvo.md).)

**The IPV-specific feature:** in intimate partner contexts, DARVO frequently culminates in the partner positioning themselves as the *real* victim of the relationship, building a narrative they distribute to mutual friends, family, and (in worst cases) custody evaluators or courts. The Reverse phase becomes the official narrative the abuser presents to the world. The autistic survivor, who is more vulnerable to the credibility discount, is in a structurally weaker position to refute the narrative.

**The protective implication:** the Harsey & Freyd (2020) inoculation effect — naming DARVO to your reality-check network in advance reduces the persuasive impact of the eventual DARVO narrative — is one of the few empirically-supported educational interventions in this hub. Use it. Your reality-check network should know what DARVO is before the abuser's narrative reaches them.

### 4.3 The compliance trap in intimate partner contexts

The compliance trap ("if you would just mask better, this wouldn't be happening") is one of the most damaging long-run patterns in intimate partner contexts because it produces the burnout that makes everything else worse. Hull et al. (2017) on masking and Raymaker et al. (2020) on burnout together establish the cost trajectory: sustained masking produces burnout, burnout reduces capacity, reduced capacity makes the autistic partner more vulnerable to all the other patterns, and the abuser then frames the burnout as the autistic partner's failure to keep up.

**The IPV-specific feature:** in intimate partnerships, the compliance trap often appears as the *masking-as-loyalty* frame — the partner reframes masking as the proof of love. "If you really loved me, you wouldn't need to stim around me." "I'm not asking you to be inauthentic, just to love me enough to be the version of yourself that doesn't embarrass me." This frame routes the compliance demand through the attachment system and asks the autistic partner to choose between being known and being loved. (See [The Compliance Trap](manipulation-hub-pattern-compliance-trap.md), section 3.4.)

**The protective implication:** burnout recovery is part of exit recovery, not separate from it. Many autistic survivors leave a compliance-trap relationship in a state of advanced burnout and then experience the burnout for months or years afterward. The recovery work is slow. (See Section 8.10 and [Recovery and Post-Traumatic Growth](manipulation-hub-strategy-recovery.md).)

### 4.4 Diagnostic weaponization in intimate partner contexts

"That's your autism talking" is one of the most prevalent IPV patterns against autistic adults. Pearson et al. (2022, 2023, 2024) and Douglas & Sedgewick (2024) document it consistently in their qualitative work. (See [Diagnostic Weaponization](manipulation-hub-pattern-diagnostic-weaponization.md).)

**The IPV-specific feature:** in intimate partnerships, diagnostic weaponization often becomes the *bundling layer* that attaches every other manipulation pattern to the autistic partner's neurology. Gaslighting becomes "you remember it wrong because of your autism." DARVO becomes "you're being aggressive because you can't read social cues." Tone policing becomes "you don't realize how harsh you sound because of your autism." Refusals become "rigid thinking." Boundaries become "executive dysfunction." The diagnosis is the frame that makes all the other moves harder to refuse.

**The protective implication:** disclosure of autism in an intimate partnership is a high-stakes decision. Disclosure to a partner who will weaponize the diagnosis is worse than no disclosure. The decision tree on the [Disclosure Decisions](manipulation-hub-strategy-disclosure-decisions.md) page applies, and the autism disclosure decision should be made with the audience-dependence in mind.

### 4.5 Sensory gaslighting and post-meltdown shaming in intimate partner contexts

These two patterns are treated together because they so frequently co-occur in intimate partnerships in a specific configuration: the partner denies the sensory input that is overwhelming the autistic person (sensory gaslighting), the autistic person, unable to escape the input and unable to be heard, has a meltdown, and the partner then frames the meltdown as evidence of instability (post-meltdown shaming). Sometimes the partner deliberately *engineers* the sensory environment that produces the meltdown — the deliberate-triggering form. (See [Sensory Gaslighting](manipulation-hub-pattern-sensory-gaslighting.md) and [Post-Meltdown Shaming](manipulation-hub-pattern-post-meltdown-shaming.md), sections 3.6 and 2.3 respectively.)

**The IPV-specific feature:** the deliberate-triggering form is particularly damaging in intimate partnerships because the partner has continuous access to the autistic survivor's sensory environment, knows the triggers intimately, and can engineer them invisibly. The cycle is largely invisible from outside the relationship, and the meltdowns become the official record that gets shared with mutual contacts, custody evaluators, and clinicians.

**The protective implication:** documenting the *full configuration* (conditions → meltdown → response) is the diagnostic move. A record that shows the sensory environment that preceded the meltdown is much more powerful than a record that shows only the meltdown itself. The Post-Meltdown Shaming page treats documentation in detail.

### 4.6 Weaponized accommodations and information asymmetry in intimate partner contexts

These two patterns also frequently co-occur in intimate partnerships, and they are the patterns that produce *material entrapment*. Pearson et al. (2024) — "It's Not a Physical Prison but You Can't Get Out" — documented that autistic adults in abusive relationships are often trapped by the resource architecture of the relationship: the partner manages the finances, schedules, transportation, medical communication, and bureaucratic tasks the autistic survivor cannot reliably handle alone, and the management becomes the leash. (See [Weaponized Accommodations](manipulation-hub-pattern-weaponized-accommodations.md) and [Information Asymmetry Exploitation](manipulation-hub-pattern-information-asymmetry-exploitation.md).)

**The IPV-specific feature:** the resource and information architecture of an intimate partnership is *the* central material substrate of coercive control in Stark's framework. When the abuser controls the resources and information the survivor needs to function, leaving means losing the substrate of daily functioning, and the threat of losing the substrate is the leverage that keeps the survivor in place. *"Other people wouldn't put up with you"* is not just a cruel line — it is, for many autistic survivors, an empirically plausible threat given the scarcity of accommodating environments.

**The protective implication:** mapping the resource and information dependencies is the precursor to safety planning. The Power Differential and Resource Analysis framework page does this mapping; the Safety Planning strategy page translates it into action. Reducing dependency, where reduction is feasible, is one of the longer-arc protective moves and is treated in Section 8.7.

### 4.7 Emotional invalidation, tone policing, and pragmatic packaging invalidation in intimate partner contexts

These three patterns operate at the level of moment-to-moment communication and are present in nearly every coercive control intimate partnership. They are the granular mechanism by which the autistic partner's voice is gradually erased in the relationship. Each conversation becomes a contest the autistic partner cannot win, regardless of how they communicate.

**The IPV-specific feature:** the cumulative effect over years is the loss of the autistic partner's ability to speak about their own experience in the relationship. By the time they consider exit, they have often stopped trying to communicate about anything important, because every previous attempt has been invalidated on different grounds. The silence is not consent; it is the result of a sustained pattern of pragmatic suppression.

**The protective implication:** writing — to a journal, to a reality-check person, to an advocate, in formal documentation — is the channel that bypasses most of the in-relationship invalidation surface. The autistic partner who has stopped trying to communicate verbally with the abuser may still be able to write, and the writing is usable for safety planning, advocate communication, and (eventually) legal proceedings.

### 4.8 Other patterns that appear in intimate partner contexts

The remaining Tier 3 patterns also appear in intimate partner contexts and are cross-linked here without the same length of treatment:

- **[Memory Gaslighting](manipulation-hub-pattern-memory-gaslighting.md)** is present in nearly all coercive control intimate partnerships. The contemporaneous-documentation strategy is the primary defense.
- **[Bandwagon Isolation](manipulation-hub-pattern-bandwagon-isolation.md)** is one of the structural mechanisms by which the autistic partner's reality-check network is shrunk over time. The "everyone agrees with me" frame is particularly damaging when it includes claimed agreement from the autistic partner's autistic peers ("your autistic friends are enabling you").
- **[Concern Trolling](manipulation-hub-pattern-concern-trolling.md)** in intimate partnerships often takes the form of "I'm worried about your mental health" deployed as cover for control of treatment, medication, or therapy choices. The autism-aware therapist criterion becomes essential.
- **[Performative Allyship](manipulation-hub-pattern-performative-allyship.md)** appears when the abusive partner has built an autism-affirming public identity that complicates the autistic survivor's later attempts to disclose. *"But they read all the autism books — they wouldn't actually be ableist."*

The full Tier 3 pattern list applies to intimate partner contexts. The patterns above are highlighted because they are the ones most commonly *foregrounded* in IPV configurations.

## 5. The Most Dangerous Configurations

Patterns rarely appear singly. In intimate partner contexts, they cluster, and certain clusters are more dangerous than others. This section names four configurations that recur in the autistic IPV literature and that warrant immediate advocate contact when recognized.

### 5.1 The deliberate-triggering + post-meltdown shaming + isolation configuration

**Components:**
- The partner has identified the autistic survivor's sensory and cognitive triggers and engineers environments designed to provoke meltdowns.
- The meltdowns are framed as evidence of the survivor's instability or violence.
- The framing is distributed to mutual friends, family, and (worst case) institutional figures (clinicians, custody evaluators, social workers, police).
- The survivor's social network is shrunk through isolation tactics, often with the framing that the network was "enabling" or "not really friends."
- Over time, the survivor's reputation is rewritten and their reality-check resources are removed.

**Why this configuration is dangerous:** it produces the conditions under which the abuser can later weaponize institutional systems against the survivor. By the time custody, criminal proceedings, or protective orders are at issue, the survivor's documented "history" of meltdowns, behavioral incidents, and "instability" is in place, and the survivor is structurally positioned as the unstable party. The configuration is one of the routes by which autistic IPV survivors lose custody, lose jobs, lose housing, and lose their freedom.

**The protective response:** advocate contact, documentation of the *full configuration* (conditions and meltdown together, not just the meltdown), and external reality-check from autistic peers and from autism-aware clinicians who can later serve as character witnesses or expert sources.

### 5.2 The weaponized accommodations + DARVO + financial control configuration

**Components:**
- The partner manages the finances, transportation, medical communication, bureaucratic tasks, and other resources the autistic survivor depends on.
- The management is reframed as exceptional generosity, with frequent reminders of how much the partner is doing.
- When the survivor raises any concern about the partner's behavior, the response is the Reverse phase of DARVO: "after everything I do for you, you're attacking me?"
- The financial control specifically prevents the survivor from accessing money to leave, hire a lawyer, see a therapist of their own choice, or travel to support networks.
- The pattern is publicly invisible because the abuser is the one talking to the bank, the doctor, the landlord, the school.

**Why this configuration is dangerous:** it is the specific mechanism by which Pearson et al.'s "prison without walls" forms. The survivor cannot leave because leaving means losing the substrate of daily functioning, and they cannot challenge the substrate without triggering the DARVO Reverse and the accusation of ingratitude.

**The protective response:** mapping the dependencies (Power Differential page), documenting the financial pattern (Documentation strategy), advocating for access to resources outside the partner's control (often via legal aid, disability rights organizations, or DV advocates with financial-abuse expertise), and building an exit fund through whatever means are safe. Some DV organizations operate confidential exit funds for survivors who need to leave; the Crisis Resources Directory lists them.

### 5.3 The diagnostic weaponization + couples therapy entrapment configuration

**Components:**
- The partner has cast the relationship's difficulties as products of the survivor's autism rather than as products of the partner's behavior.
- The partner suggests couples therapy as the way to "work things out."
- The couples therapist, who is not autism-trained, accepts the framing that the difficulties are autism-related and structures the therapy around helping the survivor "communicate better" or "manage their reactions."
- The couples therapy becomes a venue for further diagnostic weaponization with professional credibility.
- Sessions provide the abuser with new information about what the survivor has shared, which is then used outside the session room.
- The therapist's notes become part of the official record about the survivor's "communication issues."

**Why this configuration is dangerous:** it converts a manipulation pattern into a clinical record. By the time the survivor recognizes what is happening, there is a paper trail in clinical notes that supports the abuser's framing and that later providers, courts, and custody evaluators may rely on. The Bograd & Mederos (1999) contraindication is precisely about this risk.

**The protective response:** if your partner is suggesting couples therapy and any of the patterns on this hub are present, **do not enter couples therapy**. The contraindication is one of the strongest research consensus positions in the DV literature. If you are already in couples therapy, the protective move is to stop, with an advocate's support, and to seek individual trauma-informed therapy (with the autism-aware criterion) instead. The [Finding Professional Support](manipulation-hub-strategy-professional-support.md) page treats the selection in detail.

### 5.4 The bandwagon isolation + meltdown weaponization + custody threats configuration

**Components:**
- The partner has been distributing the "instability" narrative to mutual contacts and to professionals.
- Meltdowns are documented (by the partner, in their own framing) and used as evidence of the survivor's unfitness.
- If children are involved, the partner threatens custody — explicitly or by implication — based on the documented "instability."
- The threat of losing the children is the leverage that keeps the survivor in the relationship.
- The actual lethality risk is elevated by the separation contemplation, the controlling-pattern history, and (often) by additional Campbell indicators.

**Why this configuration is dangerous:** child custody is one of the most material consequences of autistic IPV survivor disclosure, and the family court system is one of the institutional contexts where the credibility discount and the cultural script of "autistic parents are unfit" are most operative. Many autistic IPV survivors with children describe the custody threat as the single largest barrier to leaving, and the threat is empirically credible given how custody decisions actually go in many jurisdictions.

**The protective response:** family law advocacy. This configuration warrants legal advocacy from the start, ideally from a family lawyer who has experience with disability and IPV cases. The Crisis Resources Directory lists legal aid options. The strategies on this page support recognition; they cannot substitute for legal representation in custody contexts. (The Tier 4 Legal and Criminal Justice context page, will treat the institutional dynamics in more detail.)

## 6. Distinguishing Intimate Terrorism from Situational Couple Violence

Stark (2007), building on Michael Johnson's typology (Johnson 2008), draws a critical distinction that is one of the most useful conceptual tools for autistic readers trying to understand what is happening in their relationship. The distinction is between two qualitatively different patterns:

- **Situational couple violence** is conflict-driven. It involves discrete incidents of conflict, often bidirectional, often without the surrounding architecture of control. Both parties may sometimes act badly. The incidents are not part of a broader pattern of one partner systematically constraining the other's autonomy. Situational couple violence is not safe and can still cause harm, but it is structurally different from the second type, and the protective responses differ.
- **Intimate terrorism** (or coercive control) is control-driven. It involves a sustained pattern in which one partner systematically constrains the other's autonomy, resources, identity, and decision-making space. The fights, when they occur, are one feature of a much larger architecture of constraint. The architecture is what the survivor lives inside, not the discrete incidents.

The distinction matters for several reasons:

1. **The lethality literature is largely about intimate terrorism, not situational couple violence.** The Campbell findings, the strangulation findings, the separation findings — these describe the dynamics of intimate terrorism. Situational couple violence has its own risks, but the lethality elevation is far smaller.
2. **Couples therapy is contraindicated for intimate terrorism. It is sometimes appropriate for situational couple violence**, with carefully screened protocols and trained therapists (Stith et al. 2012; Karakurt et al. 2016). Treating an intimate terrorism case as if it were situational couple violence is one of the routes by which couples therapy becomes the configuration in Section 5.3.
3. **The autistic experience often gets misclassified as situational couple violence when it is actually intimate terrorism.** This is the distinction the foundational autism research document identifies as "the single most useful concept for autistic readers who have been told that their difficulties are 'mutual communication problems.'" The intimate terrorism pattern, applied to an autistic partner, will frequently *look* like situational couple violence to outside observers — including therapists, friends, and the autistic survivor themselves — because the autistic partner's reactions to coercive control can look like "fights" while the surrounding control architecture is invisible.

**The diagnostic question is not "are there fights?" The diagnostic question is "is there a sustained pattern of one partner constraining the other's autonomy, resources, identity, and decision-making space?"** When the answer to the second question is yes, you are in intimate terrorism, regardless of how many or how few fights have occurred. When the answer is no, you may be in a difficult situational dynamic that responds to different tools.

**Stark's "fight vs control" distinction**, applied to your relationship: ask not how many incidents have occurred but whether the *architecture* of constraint is present. The architecture has features:

- You no longer raise concerns because you have learned that raising them is too costly.
- You modify your behavior, schedule, friendships, finances, communication, or self-presentation to manage your partner's reactions.
- You feel watched, monitored, or surveilled in ways that did not exist at the start of the relationship.
- You have lost access to friends, family, or community that your partner has framed as problematic.
- Your sense of who you are has been gradually replaced by your partner's framing of who you are.
- You feel relief when your partner is away and apprehension when they return.
- The cost of refusing your partner is high enough that you have stopped refusing.

When several of these features are present, the architecture is present, and the appropriate framing is intimate terrorism. The strategies on this page are calibrated for that case.

If you are uncertain whether your situation is intimate terrorism or situational couple violence, **the safest move is to consult an advocate**. Advocates trained in lethality assessment can help you make the distinction without you having to make it alone. The Crisis Resources Directory lists hotlines that can connect you to one.

## 7. The Patterns of Leaving

This section is included because the leaving process has its own structure that is empirically documented and that reduces the chance of catastrophe when followed.

### 7.1 The Burke et al. stages-of-change finding

Burke et al. (2004), applying the Prochaska transtheoretical model to leaving abuse, found that approximately **95% of women in their abused-women sample were in the precontemplation or contemplation stage at study entry** — meaning they were not yet ready to leave, even though they were in measurably abusive relationships. The implication: most survivors of intimate terrorism are not in a position to "just leave," even when they recognize what is happening, and the appropriate intervention has to match the stage they are actually in.

The stages, applied to leaving:

1. **Precontemplation** — not currently considering leaving. The intervention here is information, validation that the situation is harmful, and the planting of the recognition that an alternative exists.
2. **Contemplation** — considering leaving. The intervention is exploring the costs and benefits, identifying barriers, and building the foundations of a plan without committing to action.
3. **Preparation** — actively planning. The intervention is safety planning, advocate contact, resource gathering, exit fund building, documentation, and building the reality-check network.
4. **Action** — leaving. The intervention is the specific safety planning around the separation moment itself, which is the most dangerous time.
5. **Maintenance** — staying gone. The intervention is post-separation safety planning, stalking protection, legal advocacy, financial recovery, and the longer arc of trauma recovery.
6. **Relapse** — returning. The literature treats relapse as a normal part of the cycle, not as failure. Khaw & Hardesty (2007) found that 66.3% of women in shelter samples reported at least one prior separation-and-return, and 97.1% of those reported multiple. The implication: returning does not mean the survivor failed; it means the conditions for sustained leaving were not yet in place, and the next round can build on what was learned.

**The "7 attempts to leave" figure** that circulates in DV practitioner literature is attributed to NDVH practitioner data and should be cited with care; the underlying phenomenon (multiple attempts before final separation) is robustly documented even though the specific number is approximate.

**Evidence quality: [MODERATE].** The transtheoretical model has face validity and some quantitative support for IPV applications, but the model was originally designed for individual health behaviors and its fit with a dyadic decision involving safety, finances, children, and ongoing risk is debated. Treat the stages as a useful organizing heuristic, not as a clinical diagnosis.

### 7.2 The Sullivan & Bybee evidence base

The empirical anchor for "advocacy plus safety planning works":

**Sullivan, C. M., & Bybee, D. I. (1999).** "Reducing Violence Using Community-Based Advocacy for Women With Abusive Partners." *Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology* 67(1): 43–53.

This is one of the few randomized controlled trials of a DV intervention. Women received either standard services or standard services plus 4–6 hours per week of community-based advocacy for ten weeks. The advocacy intervention included safety planning, resource access, and emotional support. The findings:

- Women in the advocacy condition reported less abuse over the two-year follow-up period.
- They reported better access to resources.
- They reported less depression and higher quality of life.
- The effects persisted at follow-up beyond the intervention period.

**Evidence quality: [VALIDATED]** within the limits of a single RCT. Subsequent observational and quasi-experimental work has supported the framework. The Sullivan & Bybee model is one of the strongest empirical supports for the recommendation that survivors should be paired with trained advocates rather than left to navigate the process alone.

**The implication for autistic survivors:** the strategies on this page that involve safety planning, exit support, and recovery are not DIY substitutes for advocacy. They are orientation tools that pair with advocacy. The advocate is the load-bearing element; the page is the orientation framework that helps you understand what the advocate is doing and why.

### 7.3 The autism-specific safety planning adaptations

Section 3.6 above named the eight adaptations from the 2024 pilot RCT. They are repeated here in the leaving context because they apply most directly to the safety planning that happens during the preparation and action stages:

1. Concrete, specific language.
2. Visual or written formats.
3. Sensory accommodations in the planning environment.
4. Extra time for processing.
5. Routines and predictability built into the plan.
6. Recognition of meltdowns as legitimate features.
7. Acknowledgment of executive function differences.
8. Recognition that masking depletes resources.

If you are working with an advocate who is not autism-trained, **ask for these adaptations explicitly**. Many advocates can apply them in real time once they are flagged. The Safety Planning strategy page treats each adaptation in detail.

### 7.4 What leaving looks like in practice

The actual structure of a safety plan, in the standard NDVH framework, includes the following components:

- **Code words** with trusted friends or family that can be used to signal danger without alerting the abuser.
- **A bag of essentials** stored somewhere outside the home, containing identification documents, money, medications, copies of important papers, a change of clothes, and anything else needed for an emergency departure.
- **A list of safe locations** — friends' houses, shelters, hotels, family — and the routes to each.
- **A communication plan** — how you will contact help, what device you will use, what your abuser monitors.
- **A plan for children, pets, and other dependents.**
- **A plan for the moment of separation itself**, often the highest-risk moment.
- **A plan for the days, weeks, and months after separation**, when stalking and post-separation control are most likely.
- **An understanding of the legal landscape** — restraining orders, custody, divorce, immigration status — relevant to your specific situation.
- **A plan for finances** — access to money the abuser does not control, exit funds, replacement of joint accounts.
- **A plan for the abuser discovering the plan**, because discovery is one of the riskiest possibilities.

**This list is not a substitute for actual planning with an advocate.** It is an orientation. The plan has to be specific to your situation, your abuser, your resources, and your children. An advocate can produce the specific plan in a single conversation; this list is what to expect.

The autism-specific adaptations layer on top of every component. A safety plan that is written in concrete, specific language with visual formats, that accounts for sensory needs in the safe location, that builds in routines and predictability where possible, and that does not assume executive function under stress is more usable for an autistic survivor than a generic plan.

## 8. Strategies, Sequenced by Safety Priority

This section gives the strategies for intimate partner contexts in the order they should be considered, with the explicit framing that some strategies are contraindicated in some configurations. The ordering matters: safety strategies come before recognition strategies, recognition strategies come before recovery strategies.

### 8.1 First: Run the Safety Assessment with current information

Before applying any other strategy, run the [Safety Assessment](manipulation-hub-safety-assessment.md). This is the foundational filter for the entire sub-hub. The Safety Assessment establishes whether you are in the "engage" or "exit" zone, identifies immediate-risk indicators, and orients the rest of the response.

**Apply when:** any time you suspect that the patterns on this hub are present in your relationship. Apply repeatedly if the situation changes; the Safety Assessment is not a one-time evaluation.

**Do not apply when:** you are in immediate physical danger right now. In immediate danger, call the emergency line and an advocate first; the Safety Assessment can come after immediate safety is established.

### 8.2 Second: If lethality indicators are present, contact an advocate before doing anything else

Lethality indicators from Section 2.3 — strangulation history, separation contemplation under coercive control, weapon access, escalating violence, threats to kill, abuser suicidality, controlling daily activities — warrant immediate advocate contact. The advocate has training in risk assessment, has access to local resources, and can coordinate the next steps in a way that this page cannot.

**Apply when:** any lethality indicator is present. Especially: any history of strangulation, ever, even once.

**Do not apply when:** the contact itself would be discovered by the abuser and would trigger escalation. In that case, the prior step is finding a safe device or location from which to make the contact. Public phones, library computers, friends' phones, and burner phones are options. The Crisis Resources Directory lists device-safety resources for this situation specifically.

### 8.3 Third: Run the patterns recognition

Once safety is the front of the conversation and (if needed) an advocate is engaged, the recognition work can proceed. Use the Tier 3 pattern pages to identify which patterns are present in your relationship. Section 4 of this page lists the patterns most commonly present in intimate partner contexts.

**Apply when:** safety has been addressed at least to the point of advocate contact, and the recognition work would inform decisions about engage, exit, documentation, or disclosure.

**Do not apply when:** the recognition work is being substituted for safety work. Recognizing the patterns intellectually is not the same as protecting yourself from them. Both are needed; safety is first.

### 8.4 Fourth: Refuse couples therapy

If your partner is suggesting couples therapy, and any of the patterns on this hub are present, **the answer is no**. This is one of the most consequential single recommendations on this page. The reasons:

- Couples therapy assumes equal power and equal safety to speak honestly. Coercive control violates both assumptions.
- The victim cannot truthfully disclose in session without later retaliation.
- The therapeutic framing of "communication problems" or "both contribute" colludes with the abuser's minimization tactics (Duluth wedge category 5: minimizing, denying, blaming).
- Sessions provide the abuser with new information about what the survivor has shared with the therapist, which is then weaponized outside the session.
- The therapist's notes become a clinical record that may later be used in custody, criminal, or other proceedings.

**Primary source for the contraindication:** Bograd, M., & Mederos, F. (1999). "Battering and Couples Therapy: Universal Screening and Selection of Treatment Modality." *Journal of Marital and Family Therapy* 25(3): 291–312.

**The exception (with caveats):** specialized, screened, safety-focused conjoint approaches exist for situational couple violence cases (Stith et al. 2012). They require advanced training and explicit screening protocols. Most therapists offering "couples therapy" are not using these specialized protocols, and if your situation is intimate terrorism rather than situational couple violence, even the specialized protocols are contraindicated.

**What to do instead:** individual trauma-informed therapy for you, with the autism-aware criterion. The [Finding Professional Support](manipulation-hub-strategy-professional-support.md) page treats the selection process in detail. Your partner should pursue their own help separately, if they choose to; that is not your responsibility to arrange.

**If you are already in couples therapy** and any of the patterns on this hub are present, the protective move is to stop, ideally with an advocate's support. Stopping itself can be a high-risk move because the therapy was often part of the relationship's official structure, and your partner may treat your withdrawal as abandonment. Plan the stopping carefully.

**Apply when:** couples therapy is being suggested, considered, or already underway in a relationship where any of the patterns on this hub are present.

**Do not apply when:** you are in a relationship that is genuinely situational and not coercive, the therapist is specifically trained in DV-aware conjoint protocols, screening has occurred, and you can speak honestly in the session room without later retaliation. The conditions are restrictive for a reason.

### 8.5 Fifth: Document, with safety calibration

[Documentation](manipulation-hub-strategy-documentation.md) is the primary written strategy for almost every pattern on this hub. In intimate partner contexts, documentation is **safety-calibrated**: it must account for the risk of discovery.

The principles, from the Documentation page, apply here with the additional layer:

- **Storage outside the home and outside any device the partner can access.** Personal email opened from a library computer, a notebook stored at a trusted friend's house, a cloud account the partner does not know about. The partner who finds your documentation can use it as the trigger for escalation.
- **Document the full configuration of patterns**, not just events. The Sensory Gaslighting + Post-Meltdown Shaming combination is most diagnostic when the conditions and the meltdown are documented together, not when the meltdown alone is documented.
- **The follow-up email technique** (verbal conversations summarized in writing afterward) is one of the most useful single moves, both for documentation and as a diagnostic test of how the partner responds to written records of what was said.
- **Pattern documentation matters more than incident documentation** in intimate partner contexts because most legal and advocacy proceedings rely on patterns.

**Apply when:** you have a safe storage option, have recognized patterns you want to track, and have not been told by an advocate to wait.

**Do not apply when:** you do not yet have a safe storage option AND lethality indicators are present. In that case, advocate contact comes first; the advocate can help you set up safe storage as part of safety planning.

### 8.6 Sixth: Build a reality-check network, with autistic peers prioritized

[Reality-Check Networks](manipulation-hub-strategy-reality-check-networks.md) is the primary social strategy. In intimate partner contexts, the network is the social complement to documentation: it is the people who can hold the perception that the patterns are real, against the abuser's sustained pressure to corrode that perception.

The autistic peer priority is empirically grounded (Crompton et al. 2020) and particularly important in intimate partner contexts because:

- Autistic peers can recognize the patterns from their own experience without needing extensive explanation.
- They are not subject to the credibility discount in the same direction as allistic friends.
- They can validate your perception of meltdowns, sensory needs, and communication style in a way allistic listeners often cannot.
- They are less likely to be co-opted by the abuser's social-distribution campaigns because they share your calibration and know what the patterns look like from inside.

Building autistic peer relationships when you do not have them is itself a strategic priority. Online autistic communities, local autistic adult groups, autism-affirming organizations, and reading autistic-authored writing are starting points. The relationships do not need to be deep to be useful; even a few people who can read your account and respond from a shared calibration is protective.

**Apply when:** you have any access to trustworthy people outside the abuser's social circle. Apply also when you do not — the building of new reality-check relationships is itself one of the protective moves.

**Do not apply when:** your reality-check candidate is in the abuser's social network and would disclose the contact. In that case, the prior step is to find or build relationships outside that network.

### 8.7 Seventh: Map and reduce resource and information dependency

The [Power Differential and Resource Analysis](manipulation-hub-power-differential.md) framework page maps the dimensions of power and resource. The [Weaponized Accommodations](manipulation-hub-pattern-weaponized-accommodations.md) and [Information Asymmetry Exploitation](manipulation-hub-pattern-information-asymmetry-exploitation.md) pattern pages name the specific ways resources and information become leverage in intimate partner contexts.

Reducing dependency is a longer-arc strategy. It involves:

- **Re-establishing access to financial information** (bank accounts, credit reports, household income/expense data) that the partner may control. Legal aid organizations and DV financial-abuse specialists can help.
- **Re-establishing access to medical records** (HIPAA in the US, equivalent rights in other jurisdictions) so the partner is not the only one talking to your doctors.
- **Re-establishing legal access** (knowing your legal status, your rights, what documents have been filed, what proceedings are active or pending).
- **Building independent income** where feasible, even small amounts. An exit fund need not be large to be useful.
- **Identifying alternative sources** for the executive function, scheduling, transportation, and care coordination tasks the partner has been managing. Some tasks can be reassigned to other people or to paid services; others require new skills the survivor may need to develop over time.

**Apply when:** lethality indicators are not actively elevated by the dependency-reduction work, AND you have advocate guidance, AND you have time horizon long enough for the work.

**Do not apply when:** the dependency-reduction work itself would trigger discovery and escalation. In high-risk contexts, dependency reduction has to be coordinated with safety planning, not pursued independently.

### 8.8 Eighth: Plan for the institutional dimensions

Intimate partner contexts often involve multiple institutional dimensions: medical, legal, custody, employment, housing, immigration. Each of these institutional dimensions carries its own version of the credibility discount and its own risks of misclassification.

The relevant strategy and pattern pages:

- [Building External Evidence Beyond Documentation](manipulation-hub-strategy-external-evidence.md) for the broader evidence apparatus.
- [Disclosure Decisions](manipulation-hub-strategy-disclosure-decisions.md) for the audience-by-audience decision about what to disclose.
- [Diagnostic Weaponization](manipulation-hub-pattern-diagnostic-weaponization.md), section 6.7, for the institutional version of the autism discount.
- The forthcoming Tier 4 context pages on Medical Settings, Legal and Criminal Justice, Workplace, Educational Settings, and Social Services.

**For custody specifically:** if children are involved and custody is or may become an issue, family law advocacy is essential from the start. Many jurisdictions have legal aid programs that prioritize DV cases. Disability rights organizations may also be able to help with the intersection of autism and parental fitness questions. The Crisis Resources Directory lists context-specific options.

### 8.9 Ninth: Find autism-aware trauma-informed therapy

[Finding Professional Support](manipulation-hub-strategy-professional-support.md) treats the selection process. For intimate partner contexts, the criteria are:

- **Trauma-informed**, with experience in IPV cases, including coercive control specifically (not just incident-based violence).
- **Autism-aware**, with willingness to learn from you about your specific experience and not to substitute clinical assumptions for your self-report.
- **Not the couples therapist**. If you have been in couples therapy with your abuser, the individual therapist should not be the same person.
- **Not autism-uninformed in ways that replicate the patterns**. The red flags from the Professional Support page apply with extra weight here.

**The authority vacuum:** trauma-informed *and* autism-aware clinicians are scarce. You may need to settle for the closest available approximation, ask your therapist to learn alongside you, or use autistic peer communities as a partial substitute when no suitable clinician is available. The Professional Support page treats the gap explicitly.

### 8.10 Tenth: Recovery is the long arc

[Recovery and Post-Traumatic Growth](manipulation-hub-strategy-recovery.md) addresses the broader recovery work. For intimate partner contexts specifically, recovery typically includes:

- **Burnout recovery** from the sustained masking, sensory absorption, and cognitive depletion of the relationship. Months at minimum, often years (Raymaker et al. 2020).
- **Trauma recovery** from the specific traumatic events of the relationship and from the cumulative effects of sustained coercive control. Rumball et al. (2020) on autistic PTSD applies here; the broader range of trauma triggers means autistic survivors often have PTSD-like presentations from events that mainstream clinicians may not code as traumatic.
- **Unbinding the "I felt like I deserved it because I was autistic" internalization** (Pearson et al. 2023). This is the deepest layer and the longest arc. It is generally trauma-informed therapy work, not amenable to self-help shortcuts.
- **Rebuilding self-knowledge** that the relationship corroded. Many survivors emerge from coercive control intimate partner relationships with reduced access to their own preferences, opinions, and judgments. The rebuilding is slow.
- **Rebuilding the social network** that the relationship shrunk. Often easier when autistic peer relationships are among the rebuilding targets.
- **Post-separation safety planning** as a sustained practice, particularly for the first one to two years after separation, when stalking and post-separation control are most likely.

The standing recovery frame for the entire sub-hub applies here: **the goal is not to become less autistic. The goal is to live in environments where your autism is not being weaponized against you.** Recovery from intimate partner manipulation does not require you to become a different kind of person. It requires you to be in a different kind of relationship — or no intimate partner relationship at all, if that is what is safest for you in this period of your life. There is no single right answer about whether or when to enter a new relationship after exit, and the page does not pretend to know it. The right answer is the one you arrive at with reality-check support, with trauma-informed therapy, and with time.

## 9. What This Page Cannot Do

The honest limits of this page, named explicitly because the gap between recognition and protection is real and the page should not pretend otherwise.

- **It cannot replace an advocate.** Trained DV advocates have information, training, local knowledge, and resources this page does not. The strategies here are calibrated to pair with advocacy, not to substitute for it. Any reader in a high-risk context should be in contact with an advocate first; the page is the orientation framework that helps you understand what the advocate is doing.
- **It cannot guarantee your safety.** The lethality risks named in Section 2 are statistical patterns, not predictions about any individual relationship. Some survivors who do everything "right" are still killed by their abusers. Some survivors who do nothing "right" survive. The page describes what reduces risk on average; it does not promise outcomes in any particular case.
- **It cannot tell you whether to leave.** The decision to leave or stay is yours. It depends on facts the page cannot see — your specific abuser, your specific resources, your specific children, your specific support system, your specific stage of change. The page can help you understand the risks of each option; it cannot make the decision for you, and it should not.
- **It cannot adjudicate whether your specific relationship is intimate terrorism or situational couple violence.** Section 6 provides the conceptual distinction. The application to your specific situation depends on facts the page cannot see and is best done with an advocate or autism-aware clinician who can hold both possibilities while you work through the assessment.
- **It cannot create resources where none exist.** The recommendations on advocacy, autism-aware therapy, family law representation, exit funds, and dependency reduction depend on resources that many autistic survivors do not have equal access to. The page can name the resources; it cannot conjure them. Some readers will recognize the patterns and the strategies and also recognize that they cannot currently act on the recognition. That is a real situation, and it is not a failure of the reader. Do what you can; maintain what reality-check support you have; hold the recognition itself as protective even when external action is not yet possible.
- **It cannot reverse the long-term effects of intimate partner manipulation on its own.** The trauma, the burnout, the internalization, the corroded self-knowledge — these dissolve slowly and usually require external support. Reading the page is not equivalent to the recovery work. The page can name what is true; the work itself happens over years.
- **It cannot guarantee that institutional systems will treat you fairly.** Police, courts, custody evaluators, child welfare workers, housing authorities, medical providers, and benefits systems all carry the credibility discount and the cultural script of "autistic adults are unreliable narrators of their own lives." Documentation strengthens your position; advocacy strengthens your position; external evidence strengthens your position. None of these guarantees that the institutional system will produce a just outcome. Many autistic IPV survivors lose custody, lose jobs, lose housing, and lose freedom despite doing everything within their capacity to protect themselves. The institutional failure is not the reader's failure; it is a structural feature of how these systems treat autistic adults.
- **It cannot replace the love and care of people who are not in the manipulation pattern.** Recovery from intimate partner manipulation requires being known and being received, by people who are not running the patterns the abuser ran. The page cannot provide the relationships; it can only orient the reader toward where they might be found.
- **It cannot promise that the patterns will not recur in your next relationship.** Many autistic survivors of one abusive relationship find themselves in another later, partly because the same vulnerabilities that made them targets the first time are still present and partly because the work of unbinding the internalizations has not yet been done. Recurrence is not failure; it is part of how the patterns operate, and recognizing it is part of the longer recovery. The strategies on this page apply equally to the second relationship as to the first.

## 10. Key Takeaways

1. **Strangulation is the highest single lethality predictor known.** If you have ever been strangled by your partner, even once, even briefly, your risk of being killed by this partner is approximately 7.5× the baseline (Glass et al. 2008). Advocate contact is not optional.

2. **Leaving is the most dangerous time.** Approximately 55.2% of women murdered by an intimate partner had separated or were in the process of leaving (Campbell et al. 2003). "Just leave" is empirically unsafe advice. Leaving requires safety planning before the separation begins, ideally with an advocate.

3. **Couples therapy is contraindicated when coercive control is present.** Bograd & Mederos (1999) is the foundational reference. If your partner is suggesting couples therapy and any of the hub patterns are present, the answer is no.

4. **Autistic adults experience IPV at substantially elevated rates**, with the gender protective effect attenuated or absent in autistic samples (Douglas & Sedgewick 2024; Cazalis et al. 2022; Cooke et al. 2025). The autism-specific risk is real, the autism-specific weaponization is real, and the cultural script of "communication problems in difficult relationships" is operating against the evidence.

5. **The weaponized features framework names the autism-specific dimension.** Perpetrators deliberately exploit autism-related features as components of their tactics — sensory triggering, alexithymia exploitation, diagnostic weaponization, masking demands, accommodation debt, information control. Recognizing the exploitation as a tactic is the foundational move.

6. **Stark's "fight vs control" distinction is the single most useful concept** for autistic readers told their difficulties are "mutual communication problems." The diagnostic question is not whether there are fights but whether there is a sustained pattern of one partner constraining the other's autonomy, resources, identity, and decision-making space.

7. **The four most dangerous configurations** are deliberate-triggering + post-meltdown shaming + isolation; weaponized accommodations + DARVO + financial control; diagnostic weaponization + couples therapy entrapment; and bandwagon isolation + meltdown weaponization + custody threats. Each warrants immediate advocate contact when recognized.

8. **The advocacy + safety planning evidence base is the empirical anchor.** Sullivan & Bybee (1999) is the foundational RCT. The strategies on this page are not DIY substitutes for advocacy; they are orientation tools that pair with it. The advocate is the load-bearing element.

9. **Documentation in intimate partner contexts must be safety-calibrated.** Safe storage outside the home and outside any device the partner can access is the precondition. The full configuration of patterns (conditions + behavior + response) is more diagnostic than individual incidents.

10. **The recovery frame is not "become less autistic."** It is "live in an environment where your autism is not being weaponized against you." Recovery from intimate partner manipulation is a long arc — months to years — and includes burnout recovery, trauma recovery, unbinding the internalization, rebuilding self-knowledge, and rebuilding the social network. The page can name the work; the work itself happens with external support, over time.
