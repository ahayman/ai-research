# Concept Research: French for Pattern Learners — Decision Trees, Systematic Rules, and the Meta-Pattern

## Overview

Every preceding category in this deep dive has documented a specific structural domain of French --- phonology, morphology, syntax, verbs, gender, semantics, pragmatics, history, writing system, typological comparison. This capstone category does something different: it synthesizes all of those domains into a unified, pattern-based learning framework designed specifically for the kind of learner who thrives on explicit rules, systematic patterns, decision trees, and structural insight. If French grammar initially appears to be a chaotic collection of arbitrary rules, endings, agreements, and exceptions, this category reveals the underlying architecture: a small number of systematic principles that, once internalized, predict the vast majority of French grammatical behavior.

The single most powerful insight for a pattern learner approaching French is the meta-pattern: French is more morphologically synthetic than English, and this single fact predicts dozens of specific differences. French packs more grammatical information into individual words (through verb conjugation, gender agreement, mood marking) while English distributes the same information across multiple words (through auxiliaries, prepositions, word order). Understanding this meta-pattern transforms French learning from "memorize thousands of rules" to "learn one principle and derive its consequences." When you encounter grammatical gender, it is predicted by the meta-pattern (more synthesis = more morphological categories). When you encounter rich verb conjugation, it is predicted. When you encounter adjective agreement, past participle agreement, clitic pronouns, conditional as a mood, productive subjunctive --- all predicted. The meta-pattern does not eliminate the need to learn specific rules, but it provides a coherent structural framework within which those rules make sense.

This category provides eight concrete decision trees and systematic rule systems that transform the most challenging aspects of French grammar into learnable algorithms. For an autistic learner whose cognitive style favors explicit pattern extraction over implicit acquisition, these decision trees provide exactly the kind of transparent, rule-based framework that makes complex systems manageable. Each decision tree is designed to be complete (covering all cases), systematic (following consistent logic), and predictive (allowing the learner to handle new examples without memorization). Together, they constitute a pattern-learner's toolkit for French.

## Core Components

### Component 1: Gender Prediction from Noun Endings — The 80% Rule

- **Purpose**: Transforming gender from a memorization task into a pattern-recognition task
- **The research basis**: A 2005 corpus study by Roy Lyster (McGill University) demonstrated that 80% of masculine nouns and 81% of feminine nouns have endings that systematically predict their gender. Gender is not random --- it is approximately 80% rule-governed based on morphological form.
- **Feminine-predicting endings** (combined accuracy ~95%+):
  - -tion / -sion / -aison / -ation: nation, passion, raison, éducation (~99% feminine)
  - -té / -ité: liberté, société, beauté, université (~95% feminine)
  - -ure: nature, culture, voiture, aventure (~90% feminine)
  - -ence / -ance: science, chance, puissance, patience (~95% feminine)
  - -ie / -rie: vie, philosophie, boulangerie (~95% feminine)
  - -ette / -elle: assiette, fourchette, chapelle (~98% feminine)
  - -ade / -ude / -ase / -ise / -ose / -use: parade, attitude, base, surprise, chose, excuse (~95% feminine)
  - -ée: idée, journée, année, entrée (~95% feminine; major exception: musée = masculine)
  - General -e: words ending in silent -e are ~65% feminine (weak predictor but directional)
- **Masculine-predicting endings** (combined accuracy ~90%+):
  - -ment: moment, gouvernement, changement (~95% masculine)
  - -age: voyage, fromage, village, ménage (~90% masculine; exception: image, page, plage = feminine)
  - -eau: bureau, château, tableau, gâteau (~95% masculine)
  - -isme: capitalisme, réalisme, tourisme (~100% masculine)
  - -eur: honneur, bonheur, moteur (~85% masculine; exceptions: fleur, couleur, peur, douleur, humeur = feminine)
  - -al / -ail / -eil: journal, travail, soleil (~95% masculine)
  - -ier / -er: papier, dossier, danger (~95% masculine)
  - -in / -ain / -ein: vin, pain, plein (~90% masculine)
  - -oir: miroir, espoir, pouvoir (~95% masculine)
  - -et / -at / -ot: objet, chocolat, mot (~95% masculine)
  - Consonant endings (other than -tion/-sion): words ending in a pronounced consonant are ~80% masculine
- **The decision tree algorithm**:
  1. Does the word end in -tion/-sion/-aison? → Feminine (99%)
  2. Does the word end in -té/-ité? → Feminine (95%)
  3. Does the word end in -ment? → Masculine (95%)
  4. Does the word end in -age? → Masculine (90%, check exceptions)
  5. Does the word end in -ure? → Feminine (90%)
  6. Does the word end in -isme? → Masculine (100%)
  7. Does the word end in -eau? → Masculine (95%)
  8. Does the word end in -eur? → Masculine (85%, check exceptions)
  9. Does the word end in -ence/-ance? → Feminine (95%)
  10. Does the word end in other -e? → Feminine (65%, weak predictor)
  11. Does the word end in a consonant? → Masculine (80%)
  12. Not covered? → Learn individually
- **English comparison**: English has no grammatical gender, so English speakers have no prior pattern-recognition framework for gender. But the ending-based system provides exactly the kind of systematic, rule-based approach that replaces rote memorization with structural analysis.

### Component 2: Verb Conjugation Patterns — The Three-Group System

- **Purpose**: Reducing 50+ verb forms per verb to a small number of learnable patterns
- **The 90/10 principle**: Group 1 (-er verbs) accounts for approximately 90% of all French verbs and is completely regular. Group 2 (-ir with -issant present participle) accounts for ~300 verbs and is regular. Group 3 (everything else) accounts for ~350 verbs but contains identifiable sub-patterns. Learning to conjugate is primarily about mastering Group 1 and then handling exceptions.
- **Group 1: -er verbs (the universal pattern)**:
  - Present: stem + -e, -es, -e, -ons, -ez, -ent (parler → parl- → je parle, tu parles, il parle, nous parlons, vous parlez, ils parlent)
  - Spoken reality: -e, -es, -e, -ent are ALL silent → 4 of 6 forms sound identical (/paʁl/). Only -ons (/ɔ̃/) and -ez (/e/) are audibly distinct.
  - Imparfait: stem + -ais, -ais, -ait, -ions, -iez, -aient (ALL regular for all verbs, even irregular ones --- the imparfait is the most regular French tense)
  - Future: infinitive + -ai, -as, -a, -ons, -ez, -ont (parler + ai = parlerai)
  - Conditional: infinitive + -ais, -ais, -ait, -ions, -iez, -aient (parler + ais = parlerais --- future stem + imparfait endings)
  - Subjunctive: 3rd person plural present stem (ils parlent → parl-) + -e, -es, -e, -ions, -iez, -ent
  - Pattern insight: The conditional is literally future stem + imparfait endings. Once you know the future and the imparfait, the conditional is free.
- **Group 2: -ir verbs with -issant**:
  - Same endings as Group 1 but with -iss- extension in plural forms: je finis, tu finis, il finit, nous finissons, vous finissez, ils finissent
  - The -iss- extension is the diagnostic feature: if the present participle is -issant (finissant), it is Group 2; if -ant (partant), it is Group 3
- **Group 3: the "irregular" verbs (with hidden patterns)**:
  - Sub-pattern: -oir verbs (vouloir, pouvoir, devoir, savoir, voir) share stem-change patterns
  - Sub-pattern: -dre verbs (prendre, vendre, attendre) share conjugation patterns (-ds, -ds, -d, -dons, -dez, -dent)
  - Sub-pattern: -aindre/-eindre/-oindre (craindre, peindre, joindre) share stem-change + nasal patterns
  - The "big four" truly irregular verbs: être, avoir, aller, faire --- these must be memorized but are the four most frequent verbs in French
- **The meta-rule**: In any French tense, the stem determines WHAT you are saying, and the ending simultaneously encodes WHO is saying it (person), HOW MANY are saying it (number), WHEN it happened (tense), and HOW CERTAIN it is (mood). One ending, four pieces of information. English requires separate words for each.

### Component 3: BAGS Adjective Placement — The Prenominal Pattern

- **Purpose**: Providing a clear rule for the most common question English speakers have about French adjectives
- **The default rule**: French adjectives follow the noun they modify (un livre intéressant, une maison blanche). This is the opposite of English (an interesting book, a white house). Approximately 85% of French adjectives are postnominal.
- **The BAGS exception**: A specific set of short, common, subjective adjectives precede the noun. BAGS = Beauty, Age, Goodness, Size:
  - **B**eauty: beau/belle (beautiful), joli/jolie (pretty)
  - **A**ge: jeune (young), vieux/vieille (old), nouveau/nouvelle (new), ancien (former, when prenominal)
  - **G**oodness: bon/bonne (good), mauvais/mauvaise (bad), meilleur/meilleure (better/best)
  - **S**ize: grand/grande (big/tall), petit/petite (small), gros/grosse (big/fat), long/longue (long), court/courte (short)
  - Also: autre (other), même (same), tel/telle (such), tout/toute (all) --- function-like adjectives that precede
- **The deeper pattern**: Prenominal adjectives tend to be:
  - Short (one or two syllables)
  - High-frequency (among the most common adjectives)
  - Subjective/evaluative (expressing the speaker's judgment rather than classifying the noun objectively)
  - Postnominal adjectives tend to be classifying, technical, or specific: une voiture française (a French car), une porte métallique (a metal door), un problème économique (an economic problem)
- **Position-dependent meaning change**: Some adjectives change meaning based on position:
  - un ancien collègue (a former colleague) vs. un château ancien (an ancient castle)
  - un grand homme (a great man) vs. un homme grand (a tall man)
  - un pauvre homme (a poor/pitiable man) vs. un homme pauvre (a financially poor man)
  - un propre cahier (one's own notebook) vs. un cahier propre (a clean notebook)
  - un certain charme (a certain/particular charm) vs. une chose certaine (a certain/sure thing)
  - un cher ami (a dear friend) vs. un restaurant cher (an expensive restaurant)
  - un seul enfant (a single/only child) vs. un enfant seul (a lonely child)
- **The decision tree**:
  1. Is the adjective in the BAGS list? → Prenominal (before the noun)
  2. Is the adjective classifying, technical, or multi-syllabic? → Postnominal (after the noun)
  3. Is the adjective position-dependent (ancien, grand, pauvre, propre, certain, cher, seul, etc.)? → Check desired meaning
  4. Default (when unsure): postnominal --- you will be correct ~85% of the time
- **Special forms before vowel-initial masculine nouns**: beau → bel (un bel homme), nouveau → nouvel (un nouvel ami), vieux → vieil (un vieil arbre). These forms exist to avoid vowel-vowel hiatus and apply only to prenominal masculine adjectives before vowels.
- **English comparison**: English adjectives always precede the noun (with rare literary exceptions: "the body politic," "court martial"). English speakers must learn to reverse their default adjective position for French, then learn the BAGS exceptions that reverse back to prenominal.

### Component 4: Liaison Rules — The Systematic Sound Connection

- **Purpose**: Making French connected speech predictable through explicit rules
- **What liaison is**: A silent final consonant is pronounced when the following word begins with a vowel or mute h. The consonant "links" the two words: les amis = /le.z‿a.mi/, not /le a.mi/.
- **The three-category system**:
  
  **Obligatory liaison** (omission = error):
  - Determiner + noun: les‿amis, un‿ami, des‿enfants, mon‿ami
  - Pronoun + verb: ils‿ont, nous‿avons, vous‿êtes, on‿a, elles‿aiment
  - Adjective + noun (prenominal): un petit‿ami, les grands‿arbres, un bon‿exemple
  - After très, plus, moins, bien, trop: très‿intéressant, plus‿ou moins, bien‿entendu
  - Fixed expressions: les États-Unis, de temps‿en temps, comment‿allez-vous, c'est-à-dire
  - Rule summary: Obligatory within a syntactic unit where the two words form a tight grammatical bond (determiner-noun, pronoun-verb, modifier-head)
  
  **Forbidden liaison** (production = error):
  - After et (and): NEVER. "Pain et // eau" (/pɛ̃ e o/), not *"pain et‿eau"
  - Before aspirate h words: le // héros, les // haricots (NOT *les‿haricots, despite what some dictionaries allow)
  - After singular nouns: un étudiant // intelligent (NOT *un étudiant‿intelligent)
  - Before onze and oui: le // onze mars, mais // oui
  - After interrogative adverbs (when, comment, combien + inversion): Comment // allez-vous? (the liaison here is within the inversion, not after comment)
  - Rule summary: Forbidden across syntactic boundaries, after certain specific words, and before certain specific words
  
  **Optional liaison** (register-dependent):
  - After plural nouns: les étudiants (‿)intelligents --- liaison here is formal/careful; omission is normal in casual speech
  - After verbs (except être and avoir): ils parlent (‿)anglais --- liaison here signals careful/formal speech
  - After prepositions: dans (‿)un an --- more common but not obligatory in all registers
  - Rule: Optional liaisons are register markers --- more liaison = more formal. Using optional liaisons in casual speech sounds overly careful; omitting them in formal speech sounds sloppy.
  
- **Which consonants surface in liaison**:
  - -s → /z/: les‿amis /le.z‿a.mi/
  - -x → /z/: deux‿amis /dø.z‿a.mi/
  - -t → /t/: petit‿ami /pə.ti.t‿a.mi/
  - -d → /t/ (voicing change): grand‿homme /ɡʁɑ̃.t‿ɔm/
  - -n → /n/: un‿ami /ɛ̃.n‿a.mi/ (note: the nasal vowel may denasalize)
  - -f → /v/ (voicing change, rare): neuf‿ans /nœ.v‿ɑ̃/, neuf‿heures /nœ.v‿œʁ/

- **English comparison**: English has no liaison system. Final consonants in English are always pronounced or always silent regardless of what follows. The closest English phenomenon is linking r (in non-rhotic dialects: "far away" /fɑː.ɹ‿ə.weɪ/), but this is a single, limited phenomenon compared to French's systematic three-category system. The absence of liaison in English means that word boundaries are more acoustically clear in English than in French.

### Component 5: Passé Composé vs. Imparfait — The Aspect Decision Tree

- **Purpose**: Providing a systematic algorithm for choosing between French past tenses
- **The fundamental distinction**: This is an aspectual distinction, not a temporal one. Both tenses refer to the past, but they encode different perspectives on past events:
  - **Passé composé**: The event is viewed as completed, bounded, having a definite beginning and/or end, constituting a single "packet" of action. Answers: "What happened?"
  - **Imparfait**: The event is viewed as ongoing, unbounded, habitual, or providing background context. Answers: "What was the situation? What was happening? What used to happen?"

- **The decision tree**:
  1. Did the action happen a specific, countable number of times? → **Passé composé** ("J'ai mangé trois fois" = I ate three times)
  2. Was the action habitual or repeated without a specific count? → **Imparfait** ("Je mangeais souvent là-bas" = I used to eat there often)
  3. Did the action have a clear beginning, end, or both? → **Passé composé** ("J'ai travaillé de 9h à 17h" = I worked from 9 to 5)
  4. Was the action ongoing with no specified endpoint? → **Imparfait** ("Je travaillais quand il est arrivé" = I was working when he arrived)
  5. Is this a narrated event that advances the story? → **Passé composé** ("Il est entré, il a vu, il a conquis" = He came, he saw, he conquered)
  6. Is this background description, scene-setting, or simultaneous context? → **Imparfait** ("Il faisait beau, les oiseaux chantaient" = It was beautiful, the birds were singing)
  7. Is this a state or condition in the past? → Usually **imparfait** ("J'avais faim" = I was hungry; "Il était grand" = He was tall) --- UNLESS the state changed at a specific moment → then **passé composé** ("J'ai eu peur" = I became afraid [at that moment])
  8. Was it a single completed action that interrupted ongoing background? → Background = **imparfait**, interruption = **passé composé** ("Je dormais [imparfait] quand le téléphone a sonné [passé composé]")

- **Trigger words as clues**:
  - Imparfait clues: souvent (often), toujours (always), d'habitude (usually), chaque jour (every day), quand j'étais petit (when I was little), tous les matins (every morning), en général (in general)
  - Passé composé clues: hier (yesterday), soudain (suddenly), tout à coup (all of a sudden), un jour (one day), une fois (once), l'année dernière (last year), à ce moment-là (at that moment)

- **The metaphor**: Think of past narration as a movie. The imparfait is the background --- the scenery, the weather, the ambient sound, what people looked like and felt. The passé composé is the action --- the plot events, the changes, the things that happened. You need both to tell a complete story: "Il faisait nuit [imparfait, background]. Soudain, un bruit a retenti [passé composé, event]."

- **English comparison**: English CAN express this distinction ("I was eating" vs. "I ate"; "I used to eat" vs. "I ate") but does not REQUIRE it grammatically. English "I ate" is ambiguous between completed action, habitual action, and background state; context disambiguates. French REQUIRES the speaker to choose between passé composé and imparfait on every past-tense verb, making the aspectual distinction grammatically obligatory rather than optional.

### Component 6: Subjunctive Triggers — The WEIRDO System

- **Purpose**: Providing a systematic taxonomy of when the subjunctive is required
- **When to use subjunctive**: The subjunctive is used in subordinate clauses (after "que") when the main clause expresses something other than factual assertion. The WEIRDO acronym captures the categories:

  - **W - Wishes/Wants/Will**: vouloir que (want that), souhaiter que (wish that), désirer que (desire that), préférer que (prefer that), aimer que (like that --- in conditional: j'aimerais que)
    - "Je veux que tu viennes" (I want you to come)
  
  - **E - Emotions**: être content que (be happy that), être triste que (be sad that), avoir peur que (be afraid that), regretter que (regret that), être surpris que (be surprised that), être furieux que (be furious that)
    - "Je suis content qu'elle soit là" (I am happy that she is here)
  
  - **I - Impersonal expressions**: il faut que (it is necessary that), il est possible que (it is possible that), il est important que (it is important that), il est normal que (it is normal that), il vaut mieux que (it is better that), il se peut que (it may be that)
    - "Il faut que nous partions" (It is necessary that we leave)
  
  - **R - Recommendations/Requests/Requirements**: demander que (ask that), exiger que (demand that), ordonner que (order that), suggérer que (suggest that), proposer que (propose that), permettre que (allow that), interdire que (forbid that)
    - "Je demande qu'il vienne" (I ask that he come)
  
  - **D - Doubt/Denial**: douter que (doubt that), ne pas croire que (not believe that), ne pas penser que (not think that), nier que (deny that), il est douteux que (it is doubtful that), il n'est pas certain que (it is not certain that)
    - "Je doute qu'il sache la réponse" (I doubt that he knows the answer)
    - Note: Croire que and penser que in the AFFIRMATIVE take indicative: "Je crois qu'il sait" (I believe he knows). Only in the negative/interrogative do they trigger subjunctive.
  
  - **O - Opinions (subjective judgments)**: il est bon que (it is good that), il est dommage que (it is a pity that), c'est étrange que (it is strange that), il est rare que (it is rare that), il est temps que (it is time that)
    - "Il est dommage que tu ne puisses pas venir" (It is a pity that you cannot come)

- **Conjunctions requiring subjunctive** (independent of WEIRDO):
  - avant que (before), pour que / afin que (in order that), bien que / quoique (although), sans que (without), à moins que (unless), jusqu'à ce que (until), de peur que / de crainte que (for fear that), pourvu que (provided that)
  - Contrast: après que takes indicative (after); avant que takes subjunctive (before). The distinction: what happened after is factual; what will happen before is anticipated/uncertain.

- **The two-subject rule**: Subjunctive requires TWO DIFFERENT subjects (main clause subject ≠ subordinate clause subject). If the subjects are the same, use infinitive instead:
  - Different subjects: "Je veux que tu viennes" (I want [that] you come --- subjunctive)
  - Same subject: "Je veux venir" (I want to come --- infinitive, NOT *"Je veux que je vienne")

- **English comparison**: English subjunctive is vestigial, surviving only in:
  - Formulaic: "If I were you" (not "was"), "God save the Queen," "Be that as it may"
  - Mandative: "I suggest that he go" (not "goes") --- and even this is often replaced by "I suggest that he should go" or "I suggest he goes"
  - French subjunctive is alive, productive, and grammatically obligatory in all WEIRDO contexts. This is one of the starkest French-English contrasts and a direct consequence of French's greater morphological synthesis.

### Component 7: Determiner Selection — The Article Decision Tree

- **Purpose**: Providing a systematic algorithm for choosing between French articles
- **The decision tree**:
  1. Is the noun specific and identified? → **Definite article**: le (m.sg), la (f.sg), l' (before vowel), les (pl)
     - "Le chat dort" (The cat is sleeping --- a specific, known cat)
  2. Is the noun generic (the category as a whole)? → **Definite article** (French uses definite for generic; English uses bare nouns)
     - "J'aime le chocolat" (I like chocolate [in general]) --- compare English: no article
     - "Les chiens sont fidèles" (Dogs are loyal) --- compare English: bare plural
  3. Is the noun an indefinite countable thing? → **Indefinite article**: un (m.sg), une (f.sg), des (pl)
     - "J'ai un chat" (I have a cat)
     - "J'ai des amis" (I have [some] friends)
  4. Is the noun an uncountable substance or abstract concept in indefinite quantity? → **Partitive article**: du (m.sg), de la (f.sg), de l' (before vowel)
     - "Je bois du café" (I drink [some] coffee)
     - "J'ai de la patience" (I have [some] patience)
  5. Is the sentence negative? → Indefinite and partitive articles become **de/d'**:
     - "Je n'ai pas de chat" (I don't have a cat) --- un → de
     - "Je ne bois pas de café" (I don't drink coffee) --- du → de
     - EXCEPTION: Definite articles do NOT change in negation: "Je n'aime pas le café" (I don't like coffee)
     - EXCEPTION: After être, articles don't change: "Ce n'est pas un problème" (It's not a problem)
  6. Is there an adjective before a plural noun? → des → de/d':
     - "J'ai de bons amis" (I have good friends) --- not *"des bons amis" (though this "rule" is increasingly violated in spoken French)
  7. After expressions of quantity? → Use **de** alone (no article):
     - beaucoup de livres (many books), un peu de sucre (a little sugar), assez de temps (enough time), trop de problèmes (too many problems)
     - EXCEPTION: la plupart des (most of the) retains the article

- **English comparison**: English has a simpler decision: "the" (specific) or "a/an" (indefinite singular) or nothing (generic, plural indefinite, uncountable). French fills every position with an article --- there are no bare nouns in French. The partitive article (du, de la) is the most foreign concept for English speakers because English simply uses nothing where French uses a specific grammatical form.

### Component 8: Clitic Pronoun Ordering — The Template

- **Purpose**: Providing the fixed ordering template for French preverbal clitic pronouns
- **The problem**: When a French sentence has multiple pronoun objects, they must all appear before the verb in a strict fixed order. English places pronouns after the verb in flexible order ("I give it to him" / "I give him it"). French requires: "Je le lui donne" (I it to-him give).
- **The standard template** (declarative/negative sentences):

  ```
  Subject + (ne) + [me/te/se/nous/vous] + [le/la/les] + [lui/leur] + [y] + [en] + verb + (pas)
  ```

  - Column 1: me, te, se, nous, vous (reflexive or 1st/2nd person object)
  - Column 2: le, la, les (3rd person direct object)
  - Column 3: lui, leur (3rd person indirect object)
  - Column 4: y (replaces à + noun/place)
  - Column 5: en (replaces de + noun/quantity)
  - Maximum two pronouns from this template per verb
  - Rule: Select from left to right. If you have a Column 1 pronoun, it comes first. If you have Column 2 and Column 3, Column 2 comes first.

- **Examples**:
  - "Je le donne" (I give it) = Column 2 only
  - "Je lui donne le livre" (I give him the book) = Column 3 + full noun
  - "Je le lui donne" (I give it to him) = Column 2 + Column 3
  - "Il me le donne" (He gives it to me) = Column 1 + Column 2
  - "Je m'en souviens" (I remember it) = Column 1 (reflexive) + Column 5
  - "Il y en a" (There are some) = Column 4 + Column 5
  - "Je te les donne" (I give them to you) = Column 1 + Column 2

- **The imperative exception**: In affirmative imperatives, pronouns follow the verb with hyphens, and the order changes:
  - Verb + le/la/les + moi/toi/lui/nous/vous/leur + y + en
  - "Donne-le-moi" (Give it to me) --- not *"Donne-moi-le"
  - "Donnez-les-leur" (Give them to them)
  - But in NEGATIVE imperatives, the standard preverbal order returns: "Ne me le donne pas" (Don't give it to me)

- **English comparison**: English has no clitic ordering template. "I give it to him" and "I give him it" are both grammatical with the same meaning. English pronouns always follow the verb. The French clitic system is one of the most distinctive syntactic features of the language and has no English parallel.

## How It Works

### The Meta-Pattern: "More Synthetic" Predicts Differences

The single most powerful pattern for a French learner is: **French is more morphologically synthetic than English**. This means French packs more grammatical information into individual word forms through inflection. From this single principle, the following specific predictions can be derived:

| Because French is more synthetic... | English compensates by... |
|---------------------------------------|---------------------------|
| French has 50+ verb forms per verb | English has 5 forms + auxiliary stacking |
| French has grammatical gender (2 genders) | English has no grammatical gender |
| French has rich agreement (5+ agreement types) | English has minimal agreement (3rd person -s) |
| French has conditional as a morphological mood | English uses "would" (analytic) |
| French has productive subjunctive | English subjunctive is vestigial |
| French has 3 article types (definite, indefinite, partitive) | English has 2 types + bare nouns |
| French has preverbal clitic pronouns with fixed order | English has postverbal full pronouns with flexible order |
| French has bipartite negation (ne...pas) | English uses do-support + not |
| French marks gender/number on adjectives (4 forms) | English adjectives are invariable |
| French past participle agrees with subject/object | English past participle is invariable |

**The practical implication**: When encountering a new French grammatical feature, ask: "Is this a consequence of French being more synthetic?" If yes, the feature will involve more morphological complexity per word but fewer words overall, and the English equivalent will involve more separate function words with simpler forms. This prediction is correct approximately 80% of the time across all French-English grammatical differences.

### Step 1: Identify the Pattern Category
When encountering a French grammatical challenge, categorize it:
- **Gender**: Use the noun-ending decision tree (Component 1)
- **Verb form**: Identify the conjugation group (Component 2) and apply the regular pattern
- **Adjective position**: Apply the BAGS rule (Component 3)
- **Connected speech**: Apply the three-category liaison system (Component 4)
- **Past tense choice**: Apply the passé composé vs. imparfait decision tree (Component 5)
- **Mood choice**: Check WEIRDO triggers (Component 6)
- **Article selection**: Apply the article decision tree (Component 7)
- **Pronoun placement**: Apply the clitic ordering template (Component 8)

### Step 2: Apply the Decision Tree
Each component provides a systematic algorithm. Follow the decision tree step by step. The trees are designed to be exhaustive --- every French sentence should be handleable by the appropriate tree.

### Step 3: Check for Exceptions
Each decision tree has a known set of exceptions. These exceptions are themselves systematic (e.g., feminine -eur words cluster around abstract qualities: fleur, couleur, peur, douleur, humeur). Learn the exception patterns as secondary patterns, not individual items.

### Step 4: Cross-Reference with the Meta-Pattern
If a rule seems arbitrary, check whether it follows from the meta-pattern (more synthetic). If it does, the rule becomes predictable rather than arbitrary. If it does not (e.g., vigesimal numbers, aspirate-h distinction), it is a historical contingency that must be learned independently.

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: The Meta-Pattern in Action
- **Scenario**: A learner encounters the French sentence "Je lui en ai parlé" (I spoke to him/her about it) and is confused by the pronoun ordering
- **Illustration**: Apply the meta-pattern: French is more synthetic, so it integrates pronouns into the verbal complex as clitics. The ordering template (Column 3: lui + Column 5: en) predicts the sequence. English equivalent "I spoke to him about it" uses postverbal pronouns in flexible order. The "confusion" resolves into a systematic pattern.
- **Key Insight**: The clitic ordering is not arbitrary --- it follows from French's synthetic character and is governed by a learnable template

### Example 2: Gender Prediction Success
- **Scenario**: A learner encounters the unfamiliar word "organisation" and needs to determine its gender
- **Illustration**: Decision tree step 1: Does it end in -tion? YES → Feminine (99% accuracy). "L'organisation." Confirmed. The learner did not need to look up the gender --- the ending predicted it.
- **Key Insight**: The noun-ending system transforms gender from memorization (English speaker approach: learn each noun's gender individually) to prediction (pattern learner approach: apply ending rules and learn exceptions)

### Example 3: The Passé Composé / Imparfait Decision in Context
- **Scenario**: Narrating a past event: "I was sleeping when the phone rang"
- **Illustration**: Decision tree: "I was sleeping" = ongoing, unbounded, background → imparfait (je dormais). "The phone rang" = single completed event that interrupted the background → passé composé (le téléphone a sonné). "Je dormais quand le téléphone a sonné." The movie metaphor: sleeping = background scenery (imparfait), phone ring = plot event (passé composé).
- **Key Insight**: The imparfait/passé composé choice maps onto the principled distinction between background and event, which English can express ("was sleeping" vs. "rang") but does not grammatically require

### Example 4: WEIRDO Subjunctive Check
- **Scenario**: Translating "I want you to come"
- **Illustration**: Main verb: vouloir (want) = WEIRDO category W (Wishes). Two different subjects (I vs. you). Therefore: subjunctive required. "Je veux que tu viennes" (not *"tu viens"). If the subjects were the same: "Je veux venir" (infinitive, not *"Je veux que je vienne").
- **Key Insight**: The WEIRDO system converts subjunctive usage from a vague "feeling" into a checkable list: identify the main verb, check if it falls into any WEIRDO category, confirm two different subjects, and apply subjunctive. Entirely algorithmic.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: French grammar is too complex to learn systematically; you just have to "absorb" it through immersion
  - **Reality**: Every major aspect of French grammar follows identifiable patterns that can be learned through explicit rule systems. Gender is ~80% predictable from endings. Verb conjugation is ~90% regular. Adjective placement follows the BAGS rule. Liaison has three systematic categories. The subjunctive has the WEIRDO trigger list. The passé composé/imparfait choice follows an aspectual decision tree. Pattern learners can achieve high accuracy through systematic rule application.

- **Misconception**: French verb tables need to be memorized individually for each verb
  - **Reality**: 90% of French verbs are Group 1 (-er) and follow a single, completely regular paradigm. The conditional = future stem + imparfait endings. The imparfait is regular for ALL verbs including irregulars. Once you know the pattern, you can conjugate thousands of verbs. Only ~350 Group 3 verbs (and even these have sub-patterns) and the "big four" (être, avoir, aller, faire) require individual attention.

- **Misconception**: You need to "feel" whether to use passé composé or imparfait
  - **Reality**: The distinction is systematic and can be determined through a decision tree. If the action is completed, bounded, countable, or advances the narrative → passé composé. If it is ongoing, habitual, descriptive, or provides background → imparfait. Trigger words provide additional clues. The decision is algorithmic, not intuitive.

- **Misconception**: French exceptions make rules useless
  - **Reality**: French "exceptions" are themselves often patterned. Feminine -eur words (fleur, couleur, peur, douleur, humeur) cluster around abstract/emotional qualities. Masculine -e words often end in specific patterns (-age, -isme, -ège). Learning the exception patterns as secondary rules reduces the truly unpredictable items to a small set.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **Gender prediction failures**: The ~20% of nouns whose gender is not predicted by endings. Some are genuinely unpredictable (la main = feminine despite -ain ending being typically masculine). These must be learned individually, but they are the minority.
- **BAGS vs. meaning-change adjectives**: Some adjectives appear in the BAGS list but change meaning with position (ancien, grand). The BAGS rule gives the default; the meaning-change rule overrides when the non-default meaning is intended.
- **Passé composé for states**: While states default to imparfait, some state changes at a specific moment use passé composé: "J'ai eu peur quand j'ai vu le serpent" (I became afraid [at that specific moment] when I saw the snake). The key is whether the state has an identifiable onset moment.
- **Subjunctive after espérer**: Despite being a wish verb, "espérer que" (to hope that) takes indicative, NOT subjunctive, in the affirmative: "J'espère qu'il viendra" (indicative). This is a well-known exception to the WEIRDO W category. In the negative, it takes subjunctive: "Je n'espère pas qu'il vienne."
- **Clitic ordering edge cases**: The combination of y + en is rare and sometimes avoided in careful speech. "Il y en a" (there are some) is standard, but stacking y + en with other clitics is generally avoided by restructuring the sentence.
