# Category 23: Framing and Frame Analysis -- Practical Applications

## Overview

The theory file covered what frames are, where the concept comes from, and how the major thinkers in the frame-analysis tradition have formalized it. This file takes the theory and applies it. The goal here is practical recognition: given an actual situation, how do you tell what frame is operating, how do you tell when a frame is being installed on you, how do you tell whether a frame is serving or harming the people inside it, and what are the specific moves you can make to contest, substitute, or refuse a frame.

Framing is not an abstract skill. It is something you do dozens of times a day, mostly without noticing. This file walks through the cases where noticing matters: ordinary conversational frames, institutional frames, record-based frames, frame installations in the manipulation patterns catalogued in the Pragmatic Manipulation Sub-Hub, and the protective moves of refusal, substitution, and reframing.

Throughout, the treatment is evidence-honest. Where the research is strong (Tannen and Wallat's interactional findings, Kahneman and Tversky's decision-framing effects, Iyengar's episodic/thematic framing), that is flagged. Where the material is extrapolation from theoretical work or synthesis from community knowledge, that is also flagged. Nothing in this file is recipe-ification of clinical practice; the recipes are for *recognition* and for *orientation*, not for handling specific life-stakes situations alone.

## 1. HOW FRAMES SHOW UP IN ORDINARY CONVERSATION

### 1.1 The Cues That Install Frames

Frames get installed through several kinds of cues, which speakers deploy rapidly and receivers interpret largely without conscious attention. Tannen (1993) and Tannen and Wallat (1987) document the main categories:

- **Lexical choice.** A single word can install a frame (Fillmore, 1982). *Meeting* installs one frame; *chat* installs another; *intervention* installs a third. The word *concerns* installs a concern frame; *complaint* installs a grievance frame; *feedback* installs an evaluative frame. Same event, different frames.
- **Prosody and tone.** The same sentence read in a playful tone and a serious tone installs different frames. Prosody is one of the fastest frame-cues, which is why phone and text communication are more frame-ambiguous than face-to-face talk.
- **Pronoun use.** *We* installs a collaborative frame; *you* installs an address-to-other frame; *one* installs a generic frame. Shifts among pronouns signal frame shifts.
- **Tense.** Present tense installs immediacy; past tense installs distance or reflection; conditional installs hypothesis. Pronounced tense shifts often mark frame shifts.
- **Reported speech and laminations.** Quoting someone else installs a different frame than speaking in one's own voice. Imitations, hypothetical dialogues, and voiced examples all layer frames (see the theory file, Section 2.4).
- **Physical setting.** A room, a piece of furniture, a costume, a prop -- each pre-loads a frame. A desk in front of an interviewer installs a formal frame; a couch installs a casual one; a doctor's coat installs a clinical frame; an ID badge installs an institutional one.
- **Opening moves.** The first few turns of an interaction often settle the frame for the rest. "I want to talk to you about something" opens a serious frame; "guess what happened today" opens a narrative frame; "can I ask you a question?" opens a request frame.

### 1.2 Frame Shifts Mid-Conversation

Most conversations are not held in a single frame. They shift. A work meeting may include a serious-business frame, a brief personal-catch-up frame, a problem-solving frame, and a closing-ritual frame, all in twenty minutes. Shifts are signalled by changes in the cues above: a change of vocabulary, a tone shift, a new physical orientation, a key phrase like "on another note" or "getting back to business."

**For autistic readers: frame shifts are one of the highest-density demands in ordinary conversation.** The cues are often multiple and simultaneous, the shifts are often fast, and the content of the conversation continues as if the frame had always been in place. If you find frame shifts particularly effortful, this is documented in the autism research file (Section 2 on Pellicano & Burr's attenuated-priors account and Section 3 on Vermeulen's context-sensitivity account). The effort is not a deficit; it is a structural consequence of processing that weights bottom-up detail and top-down context differently.

### 1.3 When Frames Are Ambiguous

Sometimes frames are genuinely unclear, even to fluent non-autistic participants. A joke can be ambiguous between teasing and mockery; a request can be ambiguous between a serious ask and a polite formality; a conversation can be ambiguous between a first-date flirtation and a professional networking meeting. Ambiguous frames are a normal feature of social life, and conversationalists often negotiate the frame explicitly when the stakes of getting it wrong are high ("is this a meeting or are we just catching up?").

The protective move in a genuinely ambiguous situation is to ask. "I want to make sure I'm reading this right -- are we talking as colleagues or as friends?" This is not a deficit adaptation; it is what competent speakers do when the ambiguity matters. The only adaptation that matters for autistic readers is that the threshold for "when to ask" may be different -- it is often worth asking in situations where non-autistic speakers would have settled the frame by inference. This is a legitimate calibration choice, not a failure.

## 2. RECOGNIZING FRAME INSTALLATION

### 2.1 The Installation Signals

A frame is being installed on you or on the situation when you notice one or more of the following:

- **A situation that was previously open suddenly has a confident name attached to it.** "This is a performance issue." "This is an argument." "This is a boundary violation." "This is just banter." The name is the frame.
- **The vocabulary around a topic shifts in a way that reassigns responsibility.** *Your outbursts* replaces *your reactions*. *Your refusal to cooperate* replaces *your disagreement*. *Your tone* replaces *what you said*. Each substitution installs a frame in which you look more at-fault than in the earlier frame.
- **A mutual situation is being described as asymmetric.** A conflict you perceived as two-sided is being described as one-sided, with you as the difficult one, the unstable one, the problematic one. The frame has reassigned the asymmetry.
- **You are being asked to agree to characterizations in passing.** *"You know how you get when you're tired."* *"You always do this."* *"We both know you have a problem with authority."* These are prep-work for frames you would not have chosen if asked directly. Assenting to them installs the frame.
- **Your objections are being interpreted as evidence for the frame.** If you push back and the pushback is read as *proving* the frame ("see, you're being defensive, which just shows I'm right"), the frame is self-sealing. Self-sealing frames are a diagnostic sign of a frame attack.
- **You feel a sudden disorientation.** A frame break or frame swap has probably just happened. The disorientation is information -- take it seriously rather than dismissing it as confusion on your part.

### 2.2 Worked Example: A Workplace Meeting

A manager opens a meeting with: "I wanted to touch base about a few concerns that have come up. A couple of people on the team have mentioned that they find your communication style a bit challenging, and I wanted to get your perspective on that."

Apply Entman's four functions (see the theory file, Section 5):

- **Problem definition.** "Your communication style." The problem is being defined as something about you, not something about the interaction between you and specific colleagues about specific events.
- **Causal diagnosis.** Implied: a trait of yours produces difficulty for others.
- **Moral evaluation.** Implied: the difficulty is yours to fix.
- **Treatment recommendation.** Implied: you should adjust your style.

Now notice the framing moves packed into two sentences:

- The indefinite subjects ("a couple of people," "the team") install a bandwagon frame -- an unverifiable chorus (see the sub-hub page on bandwagon isolation).
- The word *concerns* installs a concerns frame that is hard to refuse without looking defensive.
- *Challenging* installs a frame of you as the asymmetric cause, not of a mutual mismatch.
- The offer to "get your perspective" installs a collaborative frame on top of the already-installed concerns frame, making it feel ungenerous to refuse the whole setup.

A protective response does not argue with the content inside the installed frame. It contests the frame itself:

"I'm happy to talk about specific situations with specific people. I don't think it's productive to respond to generalizations about my 'style' without knowing who said what, when, and in what context. Can you tell me the specific incidents?"

This response refuses the unverified-chorus frame and the trait-attribution frame, and substitutes a specific-incidents frame in which the conversation can actually be productive.

### 2.3 Worked Example: A Family Dinner

A parent says: "I was just worried when you didn't call -- I don't know why you have to make it so hard for me."

Entman's four:

- **Problem definition.** Problem = you not calling. But also: you *making it hard*.
- **Causal diagnosis.** Your disposition.
- **Moral evaluation.** You are at fault for the parent's worry.
- **Treatment recommendation.** You should call more, be more considerate, stop making it hard.

The frame moves:

- The subordinate clause "I was just worried" installs a concern frame, which pre-legitimates the critique.
- "I don't know why" installs a bafflement frame, which pre-exempts the parent from being asked to explain their own reaction.
- "You have to make it" installs a frame of deliberate action -- you are doing something, not just failing to do something.
- "So hard for me" installs a frame of you as the asymmetric cause of the parent's distress.

A protective response, again, does not argue inside the frame. It refuses it:

"I understand you were worried. I'm not making it hard -- I didn't call because I was busy at work, which is a normal reason not to call. I don't want to be in a conversation where my not calling is framed as me deliberately causing you distress."

This is not a denial of the facts (you didn't call; the parent did worry); it is a refusal of the frame that has been placed around the facts. Substitute a more accurate frame -- ordinary adult life with ordinary commitments -- and the situation becomes tractable in a way that arguing inside the original frame could not have made it.

### 2.4 A Note on Proportionality

Not every framing move is a frame attack. Most framing is ordinary conversational work, done in good faith, and does not need to be contested. The diagnostic questions are:

1. Is the frame being installed one you would have chosen if asked explicitly?
2. Does refusing the frame appear to be a transgression in itself?
3. Is the frame doing interpretive work that reassigns responsibility away from accurate attribution?
4. Does the frame hide aspects of the situation that you think are actually relevant?
5. Is the frame self-sealing (does it read your objections as confirmation)?

If the answers are no, yes, yes, yes, yes -- you are looking at a frame attack, and contesting it is appropriate. If the answers are mostly the other direction, you are looking at ordinary framing, which is fine.

## 3. FRAME ATTACKS: THE MANIPULATION-SUB-HUB CATALOG

This section maps the major manipulation patterns from the Pragmatic Manipulation Sub-Hub onto frame-analytic terms. Each pattern can be described as a frame move, and frame analysis gives the technical vocabulary for recognizing and refusing the move.

### 3.1 DARVO as Frame Swap

DARVO (Deny, Attack, Reverse Victim and Offender) is the paradigm frame swap. The initial frame is *we are discussing X's behavior*. The DARVO move substitutes the frame *we are discussing how I have been mistreated*. The swap is accomplished in three micro-moves (Deny, Attack, Reverse) but the structural effect is a single frame substitution.

After the swap, Entman's four functions have all been reassigned:

- Problem definition: no longer X's behavior; now the accusation itself.
- Causal diagnosis: no longer X's choice to act; now the accuser's motivation.
- Moral evaluation: the accuser is the wrongdoer.
- Treatment recommendation: the accuser should apologize, retract, or back off.

The protective move is not to argue inside the new frame (that is, not to spend time defending yourself against the new accusation). It is to name the swap as a swap and reassert the original frame.

*"You just changed the subject. I'm not going to talk about my motivations for raising this. I'm talking about [the specific behavior], and I'd like to go back to that."*

This is frame refusal plus frame reassertion. It works because the DARVO move depends on the target accepting the new frame; if the target refuses, the move fails. (Refusal does not mean the situation becomes tractable -- the manipulator may continue to push the new frame -- but refusal does keep you out of the inside-the-frame defensive posture that the move is designed to produce.)

See the sub-hub page on DARVO for the full treatment, including practical handling, documentation strategies, and the intersection with autistic masking dynamics.

### 3.2 Gaslighting as Frame Attack on Perception

Gaslighting is a frame attack on the target's *perceptual frame*. The frame being installed is: *the target's perception is unreliable; mine is reliable*. Once that frame is in place, every subsequent disagreement between perceptions is resolved in the manipulator's favor by default, because the frame has pre-decided whose account is credible.

Gaslighting works through repetition more than through any single move. Each individual instance may be small; the cumulative installation of the perception-unreliable frame is the damage. The Sweet (2019) sociological analysis frames gaslighting as a structural move that uses gender inequality as scaffolding; the same scaffolding operates when the inequality is disability-based rather than gender-based (or when both are present).

The protective move is documentation -- an external, unfalsifiable record that the frame *your perception is unreliable* cannot revise after the fact. See the sub-hub strategy page on documentation. The documentation does not have to convince the gaslighter; it exists so that *you* can consult it when the frame starts installing itself internally.

Caution: not every casual disagreement is gaslighting. The sub-hub's guidance on distinguishing manipulation from miscommunication applies here -- a one-time memory mismatch is not a frame attack, but a *pattern* of perception-reassignment that produces sustained self-doubt is.

### 3.3 Sensory Gaslighting as Frame Swap

A specific variant. The initial frame is *this environment is overstimulating for me, and I need an accommodation*. The gaslighting move substitutes the frame *you are being difficult, oversensitive, or dramatic*. The substitution reassigns the problem from the environment to a trait of the target.

The protective reframe is: *sensory differences in autism are empirically well-established, documented in DSM-5 diagnostic criteria, and not a matter of temperament*. The reframe does not argue with the individual sensation; it substitutes a frame in which the sensation is read as a neurological fact rather than a personality flaw. See the sub-hub's sensory-gaslighting page.

### 3.4 Post-Meltdown Shaming as Frame Installation

The frame being installed is: *the meltdown was a behavior, a choice, or an attack on the audience*. The accurate frame is: *the meltdown was a neurological response to overload, not a directed action*. The frame installed by post-meltdown shaming turns Entman's four functions against the autistic person:

- Problem: meltdowns (as behavior).
- Cause: autistic person's instability.
- Moral: autistic person is at fault.
- Treatment: suppress meltdowns.

The accurate frame assigns the functions differently:

- Problem: the overloading conditions that produced the meltdown.
- Cause: environmental inputs exceeding processing capacity.
- Moral: nobody at fault; the audience may need to adjust conditions.
- Treatment: remove or reduce the overloading conditions.

The sub-hub page on post-meltdown shaming offers the protective reframe directly: *a meltdown is a neurological response to overload, not a behavior, a choice, an attack, or a manipulation; the framing of meltdowns as any of these things is the manipulation, not the meltdown.* Notice the structural form: the protective reframe refuses the frame that has been installed and substitutes one that the evidence supports.

### 3.5 Diagnostic Weaponization as Frame Over-Application

The diagnosis is a legitimate frame in clinical settings. Diagnostic weaponization is the over-application of that frame to contexts where it is not accurate or not useful, with the effect of reassigning responsibility away from the actual situation. *"This is just your autism talking"* applies the diagnostic frame to an ordinary disagreement in a way that discounts the disagreement. *"You can't expect [reasonable thing] because of your autism"* uses the diagnostic frame to justify withdrawal of reasonable treatment.

Frame analysis lets you see the move: the diagnostic frame is being installed in a place where it does not accurately describe what is happening, and the installation is doing interpretive work that benefits the person doing the installing. See the sub-hub's diagnostic weaponization page.

### 3.6 Concern Trolling as Frame Wrapping

The frame being installed is: *I am speaking from concern for you*. The wrapping pre-legitimates criticism and makes it hard to refuse, because refusing the criticism appears to refuse the care. The frame analysis insight is that the concern frame is structurally separate from the content: concern-wrapping can be applied to any content, legitimate or not, and the wrapping itself is the move, not the content inside it.

The protective move is to distinguish concern (which can be acknowledged without being agreed to) from the content (which can be refused regardless of the wrapping). *"I hear that you're worried. That doesn't change what I'm telling you about the situation."*

### 3.7 Performative Allyship as Frame Installation

The frame being installed is: *I am your ally*. The installation pre-legitimates a position of interpretive authority -- the ally is supposed to know what is good for you and to have your interests at heart. Like the concern frame, the allyship frame is structurally separate from behavior: the frame can be installed without the accompanying actions that the frame implies. The protective move is to check whether the behavior matches the frame, and to refuse to grant frame-level authority that the behavior does not earn.

### 3.8 Tone Policing as Frame Reassignment

The frame being installed is: *the problem is how you said it, not what you said*. The move reassigns attention from the content to the delivery, which allows the content to be set aside. The autism intersection is particularly sharp here, because autistic communication often differs from non-autistic communication in delivery features (prosody, pacing, emphasis) in ways that are socially read as "wrong tone" regardless of content. See the sub-hub page on tone policing and the autism research file's treatment of masking.

### 3.9 Pragmatic Packaging Invalidation as Frame Overwriting

The frame being installed is: *the way you communicate is the problem; if you communicated normally, there would be no issue*. The move overwrites the content layer with the packaging layer. Like tone policing, this is especially load-bearing in autistic contexts where the packaging layer differs from non-autistic norms. The protective move is to distinguish content from packaging and to insist that the content be addressed regardless of the packaging.

### 3.10 Bandwagon Isolation as Chorus Frame

The frame being installed is: *everyone agrees with me; you are the outlier*. The chorus may be real, partially real, or fabricated; frame analysis shows that the move is the same in each case, because what is being installed is the *frame of consensus*, not the specific content. The protective move is refusing to grant frame-level authority to an unverifiable chorus, and insisting on speaking with the specific people who are supposedly part of the chorus. See the sub-hub's bandwagon isolation page for the full treatment.

## 4. INSTITUTIONAL AND RECORD-BASED FRAMES

### 4.1 Records as Pre-Installed Frames

Records are frames that have been written in advance and are running when the next interaction begins. The mechanism is simple: a reader of a record reads the subject of the record *through* the record. Whatever the record says about the subject is already present in the reader's mind before the subject walks into the room. This is not a bug; it is the purpose of records. Records exist to let institutions extend memory and judgment across staff transitions. But the same mechanism has consequences that need frame-analytic recognition.

For autistic readers, the consequential records are usually:

- **Educational records** -- IEPs, behavioral incident reports, teacher notes, special-education files.
- **Medical records** -- clinical notes, psychiatric notes, diagnostic impressions, medication histories.
- **Social services files** -- eligibility assessments, home visits, safeguarding reports.
- **HR files** -- performance reviews, disciplinary records, accommodation requests.
- **Legal records** -- court filings, custody reports, probation reports.

Each of these is a written frame that travels with the subject through subsequent encounters. A child with a long behavioral-incident file arrives at a new school already framed. An adult with a psychiatric history arrives at a new clinician already framed. An autistic adult who has had a social-services assessment arrives at subsequent benefit decisions already framed.

### 4.2 What Records Encode

Records do not encode raw facts. They encode facts-inside-a-frame. The same event can be written three different ways in three different records, and each write-up installs a different frame on the subsequent reader.

- "Student had a meltdown during transition." -- Installs a clinical-observation frame.
- "Student was disruptive during transition." -- Installs a behavioral-problem frame.
- "Student demonstrated dysregulated behavior when asked to comply with classroom routine." -- Installs a pathology frame with causal implication.

The underlying event may be identical. The subsequent reader, seeing the third version, will form impressions that differ substantially from what they would form from the first version. Frame analysis is the tool for recognizing that the record is doing interpretive work, not reporting.

### 4.3 Contesting Records

Contesting a record is structurally harder than contesting a live interaction, because the record has the authority of paperwork and the reader often trusts it more than the subject. But the contest is possible:

- **Supplementary records.** You can produce your own documentation that offers a different frame for the same events. The sub-hub strategy page on documentation covers this in detail.
- **Formal corrections.** Many record systems have formal processes for requesting amendment, correction, or supplementation. These processes are slow and imperfect but they exist.
- **Frame-naming in subsequent encounters.** When a new reader encounters you through a record, you can name the frame the record has installed and offer a different one. "I understand that the file probably describes me as [X]. I'd describe myself differently -- [Y]."
- **Legal protections.** In some jurisdictions, records are subject to data-protection law, and subjects have rights to access, correct, and annotate records about themselves. The specific rights vary by jurisdiction and record type.

See the sub-hub page on context-educational-settings and the foundation document on DV frameworks for the record-handling sections that apply in specific contexts.

### 4.4 Assessment Situations as Real-Time Frame Attacks

An assessment -- clinical, capacity, eligibility, fitness, benefits -- is a situation in which someone with institutional authority is actively installing a frame on you that will then become a record. Frame analysis is the technical tool for recognizing what is happening and for understanding what you can and cannot do about it during the assessment itself.

What you can do during an assessment:

- **Recognize the frame as a frame.** The assessment is not a neutral observation. The assessor is working through a protocol that is a structured frame, and every answer you give will be interpreted inside that frame.
- **Use the assessment's own vocabulary.** Inside the protocol's frame, your answers are being coded. Using the protocol's words (when you honestly can) makes the coding align with what you intended.
- **Do not assume the assessor knows what the words mean to you.** If a question uses a word that has a specific meaning to you different from its everyday meaning, clarify the meaning before answering. "By X, do you mean Y or Z?"
- **Bring someone.** If allowed, bring a supporter. A second witness to the assessment produces an independent memory that can contest the record if needed.
- **Request the record.** Ask for a copy of the assessment when it is complete. Read it. If it contains errors or framing you dispute, start the formal correction process.

What you cannot reliably do during an assessment:

- **Argue the frame down.** Assessors are trained to treat frame-level objections as non-cooperation or as symptoms. Arguing the frame during the assessment often backfires.
- **Prevent the record from being written.** The record will be written regardless of what happens in the assessment.
- **Undo the installation after the fact, easily.** Corrections are possible but slow.

This is not advice for handling a specific assessment -- that depends on the specific jurisdiction, institution, and context. This is the frame-analytic orientation that should inform whatever specific advice you get from advocates, lawyers, or autistic peers who know the specific system you are in.

### 4.5 The Social-Services Assessment Case

The Pragmatic Manipulation Sub-Hub flags social-services assessments as one of the most important pragmatics cross-links, and the reason is frame analysis. A social-services assessment of an autistic adult installs a frame that subsequent decisions operate within. The decisions that follow -- about eligibility, about guardianship, about benefits, about child custody, about housing -- read the autistic adult through the assessment's frame. Contesting the decisions without contesting the frame is very hard; contesting the frame requires recognizing the frame *as* a frame rather than as the objective truth about the autistic adult's situation.

Frame analysis does not by itself win a contested social-services case. What it does is let you see what is happening at the level where the battle is actually occurring. The battle is not about the specific facts; the battle is about which frame is going to be in effect when the facts are read.

## 5. THE PROTECTIVE TOOLKIT

This section collects the practical moves for refusing, substituting, and reframing at the frame level. None of these is a cure. Each of them is a tool that can be used in appropriate situations.

### 5.1 Naming the Frame Out Loud

The most basic move. When you notice that a frame has been installed, name it. "I think we're reading this through different frames. You're treating this as [X]. I'm treating it as [Y]." Naming the frame accomplishes several things simultaneously:

- It makes explicit what has been implicit, which removes the frame's main source of power (frames work best when invisible).
- It puts a verbal marker on the frame that can be referred back to later.
- It opens the possibility of negotiating the frame rather than being operated on by it.

Naming a frame does not always work. Some interlocutors will refuse to discuss the frame level and will continue to operate in the frame regardless. But naming is almost always the first move, because it is a precondition for any of the later moves.

### 5.2 Refusing Implicit Uptake

Every conversation includes many small characterizations that you are invited to accept by not objecting. "You know how you get..." "We both know that..." "You always..." Each of these is a frame-installation prep-move. Not objecting is tacit acceptance.

Refusing uptake does not require confrontation. It can be as small as "Actually, I'd describe that differently" or "Wait, I don't agree with that characterization" or even a simple "hmm, that's not how I'd put it." The function is to prevent the frame from being installed by default while the rest of the conversation moves on.

This is especially important in conversations with people who are practiced at framing. Skilled manipulators install frames through many small uptakes rather than through one big move. Refusing the small uptakes is how you protect yourself from the installation.

### 5.3 Substituting a Better Frame

The Lakoff insight: you cannot defeat a frame by negating it. "I'm not being difficult" runs the *being difficult* frame. The defeat requires substitution: "What I'm doing is [alternative frame]." Substitution has three parts:

1. **Name the alternative.** Have an explicit alternative frame ready, not just a rejection of the current one.
2. **Ground the alternative in evidence.** A substituted frame that is more accurate than the installed one is defensible; one that is merely preferred is not.
3. **Offer the alternative as description, not as argument.** *"Here's how I see it -- [alternative]."* This is harder to refuse than *"You're wrong, it's actually [alternative]"*, even though the content is the same.

The sub-hub's opening "The reframe this page offers" sentences are worked examples of frame substitution. Each one has the structure: *here is a more accurate frame than the one the manipulation installs*. The reframe is not a denial; it is a substitution grounded in evidence.

### 5.4 Documentation as Frame-Preservation

Written records do frame work in your favor as well as against you. A written account of what happened, written close to the event, preserves the frame you understood the event through -- before subsequent reinterpretation has a chance to install a different one. This is why the sub-hub's documentation strategy page emphasizes contemporaneous, specific, context-preserving records. The record is a frame that you are installing in advance for yourself.

Documentation also lets you compare the frame you held at the time of the event to the frame that has been installed on you subsequently. When gaslighting is operating, the comparison is the main protective tool -- your past self's frame is the evidence against the currently-installed frame.

### 5.5 Reality-Check Networks

Someone outside a frame can often see the frame more clearly than someone inside it. This is why autistic peer networks have a unique diagnostic value: peers who are not being operated on by the same frame can tell you what the frame looks like from outside it. Crompton et al. (2020) demonstrated that autistic-to-autistic information transfer is unusually effective, and the sub-hub's reality-check-networks strategy page treats peer networks as the load-bearing resource for frame diagnosis. See also the bandwagon isolation page for the specific threat that manipulators often try to sever the peer network first, precisely because it is the most valuable external frame-check.

### 5.6 The Limits of the Toolkit

Frame analysis is a tool, not a cure. There are situations in which naming the frame, refusing uptake, substituting an alternative, documenting, and checking with peers are all *insufficient* to protect you -- because the institutional power, the relational asymmetry, or the immediate danger is too great. The sub-hub's safety assessment page is clear that cognitive-reframing tools alone are not adequate in situations of physical danger, coercive control with active lethality indicators, or institutional contexts where framing is backed by enforcement power.

Frame analysis gives you clearer vision into what is happening. Clearer vision is almost always useful. But clearer vision is not the same as leverage, and the practical work of responding to a frame attack often requires external resources (legal, advocacy, safety-planning) that frame analysis alone does not provide. Treat this category as one tool in a toolkit, not as the toolkit.

## 6. DIAGNOSTIC RECIPES

### 6.1 The Five-Question Frame Audit

For any situation you suspect is being framed in ways you want to examine:

1. **What frame is currently in effect?** Name it in a sentence.
2. **Who installed it?** You? Someone else? An institution? A record?
3. **Does the frame fit the facts you actually have?** What does the frame predict, and is that what you are seeing?
4. **What does the frame hide?** Frames both reveal and conceal. Which aspects of the situation is this frame making invisible or irrelevant?
5. **Is there a more accurate frame available?** Can you describe one?

### 6.2 The Entman Four-Function Check

For any frame you suspect is being installed:

1. **Problem definition.** What is this frame saying the problem is?
2. **Causal diagnosis.** What is this frame saying caused it?
3. **Moral evaluation.** Who is this frame saying is at fault or in the right?
4. **Treatment recommendation.** What is this frame saying should be done next?

If any answer feels wrong under explicit examination, the frame is probably misaligned. If all four feel wrong, the frame is a frame attack and needs to be refused and replaced.

### 6.3 The Self-Sealing Test

A frame is **self-sealing** if it interprets disagreement with the frame as evidence for the frame. The classic form: *"If you weren't crazy, you wouldn't be denying that you're crazy -- so the fact that you're denying it proves you're crazy."* Self-sealing frames are a warning sign. No frame that accurately describes a situation needs to seal itself against disagreement -- accurate frames can tolerate objection. Self-sealing is a structural feature of frame attacks specifically.

### 6.4 The Opposite-Person Test

Ask: *would this frame read differently if it were being applied to someone else?* If the same event would be described with different vocabulary if the person being described were someone the framer liked more, the frame is partly about the relationship and not entirely about the event. This does not automatically invalidate the frame -- sometimes different people genuinely are different -- but it flags that the frame is doing interpretive work beyond what the facts alone would require.

### 6.5 The Three-Conversation Test

A pattern recommended in the sub-hub's miscommunication-vs-manipulation decision framework. If a frame that is being installed on you would be corroborated by three different people who know the situation and have independent vantage points, it is probably approximately accurate. If not, you are probably looking at a frame that exists mainly in the mind of the person installing it. This is not a certain test -- sometimes three people can all be wrong -- but it is a reasonable first-pass check that prevents both false negatives (dismissing real problems as manipulation) and false positives (treating ordinary framing as attack).

## 7. VOCABULARY OF FRAME MOVES

A reference list of frame moves that appear frequently enough to have names. This vocabulary helps you describe what is happening when you suspect frame-level work is underway.

- **Frame installation.** Putting a frame in place, usually through vocabulary, setting, or opening moves.
- **Frame maintenance.** The ongoing work of keeping a frame in effect after it has been installed.
- **Frame break.** A sudden failure of the current frame, producing disorientation.
- **Frame shift.** A signaled, collaborative move from one frame to another.
- **Frame swap.** An unannounced replacement of one frame with another, usually by one party without the consent of the other. (DARVO is the paradigm.)
- **Frame dispute.** A sustained disagreement about which frame is in effect.
- **Frame attack.** A deliberate installation of a misleading frame that harms the target.
- **Frame refusal.** Declining to adopt a frame that has been proposed or imposed.
- **Frame substitution.** Replacing an installed frame with a different one, usually with justification.
- **Reframing.** Substituting a more accurate or more tractable frame for a current one.
- **Lamination.** Layering one frame on top of another (reported speech, imitation, hypotheticals).
- **Fabrication.** A frame transformation in which one or more participants are intentionally kept in the wrong frame (Goffman's term).
- **Keying.** An open transformation of a primary frame that all participants recognize as such (play, rehearsal, ceremony).
- **Self-sealing frame.** A frame that interprets objection to it as confirmation of it.

## 8. FRAMES AND THE OTHER PRAGMATICS CATEGORIES: PRACTICAL BRIDGES

Frame analysis is the infrastructure beneath most other pragmatic systems. The practical bridges:

- **Implicature (Category 2).** Implicatures are computed relative to a frame. To understand what someone is implying, you have to know what frame they think is in effect. A question that would be implicature-rich inside a flirtation frame may be literal inside a task frame. When implicature feels confusing, checking the frame is often the first move.
- **Politeness (Category 3).** Brown and Levinson's politeness strategies are calibrated to the frame. The politeness level appropriate to a *formal-business* frame is wrong in an *intimate-conversation* frame, and vice versa. If politeness feels mismatched, the frame may be mismatched.
- **Register (Category 7).** Register is the linguistic surface of a frame. The frame selects the register; using the register maintains the frame. Register mismatches often reveal frame mismatches.
- **Common Ground (Category 16).** Frames specify what should be mutually known. Installing a frame installs a whole schema of assumed-shared assumptions. Disagreements about common ground are often frame-level disagreements in disguise.
- **Narrative (Category 12).** Narratives install frames over extended stretches of time. Who controls the story controls the frame. The family's narrative about your childhood is a frame; contesting the narrative is contesting the frame.
- **Social Hierarchy (Category 22).** Framing power tracks social power. Who gets to install a frame is not distributed equally, and the frames installed by institutions, professionals, parents, employers, and dominant culture-members are harder to refuse than those installed by peers. This is not an incidental fact; it is load-bearing for everything in the sub-hub.

## 9. COMMUNITY KNOWLEDGE AND EVIDENCE QUALITY FLAGS

Most of this file is grounded in the theoretical literature (Goffman, Fillmore, Lakoff, Entman, Tannen, Watzlawick et al., Kahneman and Tversky) that the theory file treats in depth. Specific claims marked throughout include:

- **[VALIDATED]** -- claims for which the theoretical framework and empirical support are well-established. Examples: the framing effect (Kahneman and Tversky, 1981); the interactive frame shifts documented by Tannen and Wallat (1987); the episodic/thematic framing distinction (Iyengar, 1991); the lexical-frame mechanism (Fillmore, 1982).

- **[EXTRAPOLATED]** -- claims that combine established theory with reasonable application to autism contexts that have not been directly studied at the same rigor. The mapping of DARVO, gaslighting, and the other sub-hub patterns onto Goffman's frame-attack and frame-swap vocabulary is extrapolation of this kind. The extrapolation is direct, but the specific studies of "DARVO-as-frame-swap" do not exist.

- **[COMMUNITY KNOWLEDGE]** -- claims grounded primarily in autistic community accounts rather than peer-reviewed research. The specific protective moves recommended in Section 5, the diagnostic recipes in Section 6, and several of the worked examples are synthesized from autistic community experience and are consistent with the theoretical literature but have not been formally tested.

Readers should hold the claims here at the level of confidence appropriate to each flag. Where the underlying theory is strong and the application is direct, the confidence is high. Where the application is more novel or the evidence base is thinner, the appropriate stance is "this is a useful framework that is worth using and that has not been fully validated for this specific use."
