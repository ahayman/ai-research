# Concept Research: Intelligence, Assessment, and Measuring Non-Speaking Minds

## Overview

The problem of measuring intelligence in non-speaking autistic individuals is not merely technical — it is existential. The IQ score assigned to a non-speaking autistic person cascades through every domain of their life: educational placement, access to communication technology, eligibility for services, and whether anyone attempts to teach them to read, write, or participate in their own care decisions. For decades, non-speaking autistic individuals have been assessed using instruments that require the very skills they lack — verbal responses, reliable motor output, sustained attention to examiner-directed tasks — producing scores that confirm the prior assumption of intellectual disability in a self-reinforcing cycle. A person who cannot speak is given a test that requires speaking. They fail. The failure is recorded as evidence of cognitive impairment. The recorded impairment justifies withholding educational opportunities. The lack of education ensures they never develop the skills that might demonstrate their actual capacity. The circle closes.

This began to change with the landmark work of Michelle Dawson and Laurent Mottron, published in 2007 in Psychological Science. They found that autistic children scored on average 30 percentile points higher on Raven's Progressive Matrices — a nonverbal test of abstract reasoning that does not require language — than on the Wechsler Intelligence Scales, the most commonly used IQ test. In some cases, the discrepancy exceeded 70 percentile points. Not a single autistic child scored in the "high intelligence" range on the Wechsler, but a full third did on the Raven's. Conversely, a third scored in the "intellectual disability" range on the Wechsler, but only one in twenty scored that low on the Raven's. The implication was staggering: the standard tool used to measure intelligence in autism was systematically and dramatically underestimating autistic cognitive ability.

This category examines the full scope of the assessment problem: why standard tests fail, what alternative approaches exist, the philosophical frameworks that guide assumptions about competence, the contested "profound autism" label, and the legal implications of getting assessment wrong. It is a domain where the tension between rigorous evidence and human rights is acute. The "least dangerous assumption" principle (Donnellan, 1984) holds that when data are inconclusive, one should assume competence rather than incompetence, because the consequences of wrongly presuming incompetence (denying education, communication, autonomy) are far more damaging than the consequences of wrongly presuming competence (providing supports that turn out to be unnecessary). But critics argue that presuming competence without evidence can lead to practices like facilitated communication, where the appearance of competence may mask facilitator authorship. Navigating this tension requires both intellectual honesty and moral seriousness.

## Core Components

### Component 1: Why Standard IQ Tests Fail Non-Speaking Autistic Individuals
- **Purpose**: Documents the specific mechanisms by which conventional intelligence assessments systematically underestimate non-speaking autistic abilities
- **The Wechsler problem**: The Wechsler Intelligence Scales (WISC for children, WAIS for adults) — the most widely used IQ tests worldwide — include a Verbal Comprehension Index that requires spoken responses to questions, a Working Memory Index that relies on auditory verbal processing, and a Processing Speed Index that requires rapid motor responses. All of these systematically disadvantage non-speaking individuals.
- **Motor demands**: Even the "nonverbal" Performance IQ subtests of many standard assessments require pointing, placing blocks, assembling puzzles, or other fine motor actions. For individuals with motor planning difficulties (apraxia, dyspraxia), these demands create an additional barrier unrelated to the cognitive skill being tested.
- **Compliance demands**: Standard IQ tests require the test-taker to sit at a table with an examiner, attend to examiner-directed stimuli, and respond within time limits. These demands presuppose behavioral compliance, joint attention, and social engagement — all of which may be atypical in autism for reasons unrelated to intelligence.
- **The instruction problem**: Tests are administered through verbal instructions. A non-speaking person who cannot comprehend spoken instructions (or who comprehends them but cannot demonstrate comprehension through the expected motor response) will receive a score reflecting the instruction barrier rather than their cognitive capacity.
- **The circular logic**: Low test scores → placement in segregated, low-expectation educational settings → minimal cognitive stimulation → no opportunity to develop test-taking skills → continued low scores on subsequent testing. The assessment creates the conditions that justify its own findings.

### Component 2: The Dawson-Mottron Raven's Matrices Finding
- **Purpose**: Documents the landmark research showing dramatic discrepancies between verbal and nonverbal intelligence measures in autism
- **Study**: Dawson, Soulieres, Gernsbacher, & Mottron (2007). "The Level and Nature of Autistic Intelligence." Psychological Science, 18(8), 657-662.
- **Sample**: 38 autistic children, 26 autistic adults, matched controls
- **Key findings**:
  - Autistic children scored on average 30 percentile points higher on Raven's Progressive Matrices (RPM) than on Wechsler Intelligence Scales
  - In some cases, discrepancies exceeded 70 percentile points
  - Zero autistic children scored "high intelligence" on Wechsler; one-third scored "high intelligence" on RPM
  - One-third of autistic children scored in the "intellectual disability" range on Wechsler; only 5% (1 in 20) scored that low on RPM
  - Autistic individuals completed RPM items 23-42% faster than non-autistic participants (faster for more complex items)
  - The relative difficulty of items was highly correlated between groups, suggesting RPM measures the same construct in both populations
- **Interpretation**: The Wechsler score does not reflect autistic intelligence; it reflects the mismatch between the test's demands and the autistic cognitive profile. RPM, which taps visual-spatial reasoning without language, appears to capture autistic intelligence more accurately.
- **Replication**: The finding has been cited over 1,000 times and related findings have been replicated by multiple groups. A follow-up fMRI study (Soulieres et al., 2009) showed autistic participants used distinctive perceptual processing strategies during RPM, engaging posterior brain regions more heavily.
- **Limitations**: The original study included speaking autistic individuals, not non-speaking participants specifically. Whether the Raven's advantage extends to non-speaking individuals has not been directly tested (because the standard RPM still requires pointing).

### Component 3: The Jaswal Lab Literacy Research (2024) — Contested Evidence
- **Purpose**: Presents the most recent and most controversial research on hidden cognitive abilities in non-speaking autistic individuals
- **Study**: Jaswal, Lampi, & Stockwell (2024). "Literacy in nonspeaking autistic people." Autism. 31 non-speaking autistic participants (all with 1+ years experience using letterboard communication via RPM/S2C).
- **Key finding**: Participants played a tapping game on an iPad. 17 of 31 (55%) showed a reaction-time pattern consistent with knowledge of English orthographic conventions (the "literacy signature"), despite most never receiving formal literacy instruction.
- **Published rebuttal**: "Illusions of literacy in nonspeaking autistic people" (Evidence-Based Communication Assessment and Intervention, 2024). Key criticisms:
  - All participants were recruited from RPM/S2C programs and regularly used letter boards — their familiarity with letters could explain the results without literacy
  - No comparison condition without the experimenter reading sentences aloud
  - The study did not control for potential facilitated influence in participants' communication histories
  - The "literacy signature" pattern could reflect letter familiarity from extensive letterboard use, not literacy per se
- **Jaswal lab response**: Published FAQs addressing criticisms; argues that letterboard familiarity alone cannot explain the specific orthographic patterns observed
- **Evidence status**: An interesting but contested finding. The study reveals something about non-speaking participants' letter processing, but whether it constitutes evidence of "literacy" is genuinely disputed. Must be presented alongside the published rebuttal.

### Component 4: The "Presuming Competence" Philosophy and "Least Dangerous Assumption"
- **Purpose**: Examines the philosophical framework that guides how assumptions about non-speaking individuals should work
- **Origin**: Anne M. Donnellan (1943-2024). "The Criterion of the Least Dangerous Assumption." Behavioral Disorders, 9(2), 141-150 (1984).
- **Core principle**: "In the absence of conclusive data, educational decisions should be based on assumptions which, if incorrect, will have the least dangerous effect on the student."
- **Application**: If you do not know whether a non-speaking student can learn to read, it is less dangerous to assume they can (and provide literacy instruction that may be unnecessary) than to assume they cannot (and withhold literacy instruction they may have needed).
- **Influence**: The principle has become foundational in inclusive education and disability rights advocacy. It is central to the Autistic Self Advocacy Network's position on communication access.
- **Critiques**:
  - Some argue the principle has been stretched beyond Donnellan's original intent — from a guide for educational decisions to a blanket presumption of high cognitive competence in all non-speaking individuals regardless of assessment data
  - In the FC/S2C context, critics argue that "presuming competence" can be misapplied to justify accepting facilitated communication output as authentic without verification, which may itself be the "more dangerous assumption" (attributing sophisticated thoughts to someone who did not produce them)
  - The principle presumes the worst-case of "incorrect assumption of competence" is benign (wasted resources), but in the FC context, the worst case may be more severe (false allegations, denial of genuine agency, delaying effective communication methods)
  - John Elder Robison (autistic author, 2025 blog post) argues for a middle ground: neither "presume competence" nor "presume incompetence," but "stretch boundaries" — continuously testing and expanding what each individual can do without defaulting to either extreme

### Component 5: The "Profound Autism" Label Debate
- **Purpose**: Examines the contested diagnostic/administrative category proposed for the most severely affected autistic individuals
- **Origin**: The Lancet Commission on the Future of Care and Clinical Research of Autism (2021) proposed the administrative term "profound autism" for autistic individuals who cannot live independently and require 24-hour care.
- **Proposed definition**: The 2025 INSAR consensus definition establishes specific criteria.
- **Arguments for the label**:
  - Advocates (including many parents of high-support-needs children) argue that the needs of severely affected autistic individuals are invisible when grouped with the entire autism spectrum
  - They argue a distinct category could drive targeted research funding and service provision
  - The term captures a population that falls "off the cliff" of services at age 21
- **Arguments against the label**:
  - The label "does not predict strengths or help plan supports" (PMC10576471, 2023)
  - It is "isolating, dehumanizing, and misleading" for professionals making prognoses (MDPI, 2023)
  - Autistic self-advocates have resisted functioning labels for over 30 years as misleading and reductive
  - The label risks creating a formal category of people presumed incompetent, undermining the least dangerous assumption
  - It conflates current behavior with immutable capacity — a person labeled "profound" at age 5 based on lack of speech may develop robust communication through AAC at age 18 (as Jordyn Zimmerman's story illustrates)
  - ASAN and other organizations oppose the label, arguing it revives discredited functioning categories under a new name
- **Evidence status**: The debate is ongoing. The label is being adopted in some research and policy contexts despite significant opposition from the autistic community.

### Component 6: Alternative Assessment Approaches
- **Purpose**: Documents methods designed to more accurately assess cognitive abilities in non-speaking populations
- **Raven's Progressive Matrices (RPM)**: Nonverbal, visual-spatial reasoning test. Does not require language. Demonstrated 30-percentile-point advantage for autistic individuals. Limitation: still requires motor response (pointing), which may disadvantage those with severe motor difficulties.
- **Leiter International Performance Scale**: Entirely nonverbal intelligence test. Uses gestures and demonstration rather than verbal instructions. Can be administered with adapted response methods.
- **TONI (Test of Nonverbal Intelligence)**: Minimizes language demands, does not assume cultural knowledge. Widely considered one of the most appropriate IQ tests for autistic individuals.
- **Naglieri Nonverbal Ability Test (NNAT)**: Uses geometric shapes and patterns. No reading, writing, or speaking required.
- **Eye-tracking-based assessment**: Emerging approach. A pilot study developed video tasks including deductive reasoning, working memory, and attention, finding that cognitive scores based on eye tracking were closely related to scores from standardized neuropsychological tests. Can assess individuals who cannot point or select. 2025 Cued Looking Paradigm showed 83% of minimally verbal participants demonstrated hidden language abilities through involuntary gaze.
- **Curriculum-based measurement**: Assessing what a person can do in naturalistic contexts rather than decontextualized test settings. Less standardized but potentially more valid for individuals whose behavior differs dramatically across contexts.
- **Key challenge**: Even "nonverbal" tests typically require motor responses, sustained attention to examiner-directed tasks, and understanding of implicit test-taking conventions (e.g., "choose the best answer from these options"). Truly motor-free, instruction-free assessment remains an unsolved problem.

### Component 7: Legal and Educational Consequences of Assessment
- **Purpose**: Documents how intelligence assessment directly determines life outcomes for non-speaking individuals
- **IDEA requirements**: The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act guarantees a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE) tailored to each child's needs through Individualized Education Programs (IEPs). Assessment determines IEP content and placement.
- **ADA communication rights**: The Americans with Disabilities Act requires reasonable accommodations to ensure effective communication, including access to communication devices and aids. Assessment determines what accommodations are deemed "necessary."
- **Placement consequences**:
  - IQ < 70 on a standard test → likely placement in "life skills" or segregated classroom → focus on self-care rather than academics → no literacy instruction → perpetuation of measured disability
  - IQ in normal or above-average range (e.g., via Raven's or adapted assessment) → access to inclusive classroom → academic instruction → opportunity to demonstrate and develop abilities
- **Guardianship implications**: Adults assessed as having intellectual disability may be placed under guardianship, losing the legal right to make decisions about their own lives, medical care, and communication methods.
- **The Zimmerman example**: Jordyn Zimmerman was not given AAC until age 18. Before that, her assessments had presumably classified her as having significant intellectual disability. After receiving AAC, she earned bachelor's and master's degrees, was named to the Forbes Accessibility 100, and became Board Chair of CommunicationFIRST. Her "intelligence" did not change — only her access to a communication method that could reveal it.

## How It Works

### Step 1: The Assessment Encounter
- **Trigger**: A non-speaking autistic child reaches school age, or an existing student requires re-evaluation
- **Action**: A school psychologist administers standardized IQ tests (typically Wechsler scales). The child cannot respond verbally to Verbal Comprehension questions, has difficulty with fine motor tasks required by Performance scales, may not attend to the examiner or may display stereotypic behaviors interpreted as "off-task"
- **Result**: The child receives an IQ score significantly below 70, placing them in the "intellectual disability" range

### Step 2: The Placement Decision
- **Trigger**: The IQ score enters the child's IEP documentation
- **Action**: The IEP team uses the score to determine educational placement and goals. With IQ < 70, the child is placed in a segregated "life skills" classroom. Goals focus on self-care, basic safety, and behavioral compliance rather than academic content. Literacy instruction is not provided because "the data show" the child cannot learn to read.
- **Result**: The child receives an education focused on behavior management rather than cognitive development

### Step 3: The Confirmation Loop
- **Trigger**: Two years later, the child is re-evaluated
- **Action**: The same standardized tests are administered. The child, who has received no academic instruction in the intervening years, performs at the same or lower level. The IQ score remains below 70.
- **Result**: The original placement is confirmed. The cycle continues. Each re-assessment reinforces the previous one because the educational environment has been shaped by the assessment rather than challenging it.

### Step 4: The Alternative Pathway (When It Works)
- **Trigger**: A clinician uses an adapted assessment (Raven's, Leiter, or eye-tracking-based evaluation) or invokes the least dangerous assumption
- **Action**: The adapted assessment reveals visual-spatial reasoning abilities that the standard test missed. Or the clinician provides AAC and literacy instruction based on the presumption that the child can learn. The child demonstrates previously unsuspected abilities.
- **Result**: IEP goals are revised upward. The child receives academic instruction. Communication access is provided. In some cases, the transformation is dramatic (Zimmerman, Kedar, others).

### Step 5: The Unresolved Question
- **Trigger**: Success stories circulate (Zimmerman, Kedar, Blasko); critics ask whether these cases generalize
- **Action**: Proponents argue that every non-speaking individual deserves the same presumption of competence and access to communication. Critics argue that individual success stories cannot establish population-level prevalence of hidden competence, and that some non-speaking individuals may genuinely have intellectual disabilities that affect their capacity.
- **Result**: The tension between "presume competence" and "measure competence accurately" remains unresolved. Both approaches carry risks: underestimation (the current default, with devastating consequences) and overestimation (potentially misattributing facilitator-produced communication to the individual).

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: The Same Child, Two Very Different Scores
- **Scenario**: A 10-year-old non-speaking autistic child is assessed. On the Wechsler, they score IQ 42 (severe intellectual disability range). A clinician then administers Raven's Progressive Matrices with an adapted pointing method. The child scores at the 55th percentile (average range).
- **Illustration**: This is not a hypothetical — it reflects the documented average 30-percentile-point discrepancy from Dawson & Mottron (2007), and individual discrepancies exceeding 70 percentile points. The child's intelligence did not change between tests. What changed was how the test measured it.
- **Key Insight**: The score reflects the test's accessibility, not the child's capacity. Assessment is a measurement of the instrument as much as of the individual.

### Example 2: The Circular Logic of Placement
- **Scenario**: A 6-year-old non-speaking child is assessed as having IQ below 70 and placed in a "life skills" classroom. At age 8, she is reassessed and scores even lower.
- **Illustration**: In the intervening two years, the child received no literacy instruction, no academic content, and limited cognitive stimulation. Her "decline" reflects the environment shaped by the first assessment, not a deterioration in her abilities.
- **Key Insight**: Assessment does not merely measure current ability — it shapes future ability by determining the educational environment. This makes the first assessment a high-stakes, potentially self-fulfilling prophecy.

### Example 3: Zimmerman — 12 Years of Wrong Assumptions
- **Scenario**: Jordyn Zimmerman receives no AAC until age 18. Before that, her school experience was segregated classrooms and low expectations.
- **Illustration**: After receiving Proloquo2Go, she completed bachelor's and master's degrees, gave keynote speeches worldwide, was named to the Forbes Accessibility 100, and became Board Chair of a national nonprofit. The person who was implicitly assessed as incapable of academic learning turned out to be an exceptional scholar, writer, and advocate.
- **Key Insight**: Zimmerman's case does not prove all non-speaking individuals have hidden academic ability. But it demonstrates definitively that current assessment methods can be catastrophically wrong, and that the consequences of underestimation span an entire lifetime.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: If standard IQ tests show intellectual disability, the person has intellectual disability
  - **Reality**: In the autistic population, standard IQ tests systematically underestimate cognitive ability. The 30-percentile-point Raven's vs. Wechsler discrepancy demonstrates this is a measurement artifact, not a reflection of true ability. This does not mean no non-speaking autistic individuals have intellectual disabilities — some genuinely do — but it means standard test scores cannot reliably distinguish between genuine intellectual disability and test-induced underestimation.

- **Misconception**: "Presuming competence" means assuming all non-speaking individuals are highly intelligent
  - **Reality**: Presuming competence means providing the same educational opportunities and communication access regardless of assessment scores, because the assessment may be wrong. It does not require believing every individual is a hidden genius. The least dangerous assumption is an epistemological principle about how to act under uncertainty, not a claim about population-level intelligence.

- **Misconception**: The Raven's Matrices discrepancy means all autistic people have normal or high intelligence
  - **Reality**: The Dawson & Mottron finding shows that standard tests underestimate autistic intelligence relative to nonverbal measures. But even on Raven's, there is a distribution — 5% of autistic children in their sample still scored in the intellectual disability range. The finding is about systematic underestimation, not universal hidden genius.

- **Misconception**: If a non-speaking person cannot demonstrate ability on any test, they must lack the ability
  - **Reality**: All currently available tests impose some demands (motor, attentional, compliance) that are confounded with the construct being measured. A failure to demonstrate ability on available tests proves only that the available tests cannot measure the ability, not that the ability does not exist. This is a genuine epistemological problem with no easy resolution.

- **Misconception**: The "profound autism" label is just a scientific classification
  - **Reality**: The label has political, social, and practical consequences far beyond classification. It can determine funding, service provision, educational expectations, and whether individuals are included in research about their own lives. It is a political act disguised as a scientific one, and both proponents and opponents have legitimate concerns.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **Non-speaking individuals who genuinely have co-occurring intellectual disability**: Not all non-speaking autistic individuals have hidden cognitive abilities. Some genuinely have intellectual disabilities that affect their capacity across domains. The challenge is distinguishing this group from those whose abilities are masked by motor, language, and assessment barriers — and current tools cannot reliably make this distinction.
- **Late-life cognitive changes**: Some non-speaking autistic individuals show cognitive changes in adulthood (regression, catatonia, skills loss). Assessment methods designed for stable ability levels may not capture dynamic trajectories.
- **Individuals who score high on adapted tests but cannot translate abilities into daily functioning**: A high Raven's score demonstrates visual-spatial reasoning but does not guarantee the person can learn to read, communicate through AAC, or live independently. Intelligence is not a single dimension, and a strength in one domain does not guarantee strengths in others.
- **The "best day" problem**: Non-speaking individuals' performance can vary dramatically across days, settings, and emotional states. A single assessment captures only one snapshot, which may not be representative.

## Related Concepts

- **Motor-Language Dissociation (Category 5)**: Motor difficulties confound assessment by preventing non-speaking individuals from demonstrating abilities through standard response channels (pointing, selecting, speaking)
- **Visual-Spatial Cognition (Category 6)**: The Raven's advantage reflects the visual-spatial cognitive strengths documented by the EPF model — the brain has abilities that verbal IQ tests cannot measure
- **Neuroscience of Non-Speaking Autism (Category 7)**: The neural evidence for visual-spatial processing of linguistic material explains why visual-spatial tests capture abilities that verbal tests miss
- **First-Person Accounts (Category 10)**: Individual stories (Zimmerman, Kedar, Blasko) provide the most compelling illustrations of what becomes possible when assessment barriers are removed
- **Research Methodology (Category 11)**: The assessment problem is a microcosm of the broader methodological challenges in studying non-speaking populations — the tools designed to measure them are confounded by the very differences they are trying to measure

## Research Gaps

- **Raven's Matrices in non-speaking populations specifically**: The Dawson & Mottron study included speaking autistic individuals. Whether the Raven's advantage holds for truly non-speaking individuals has not been directly tested — because even Raven's requires motor responses.
- **Truly motor-free cognitive assessment**: No validated assessment method exists that requires zero motor output. Eye-tracking approaches are emerging but not yet standardized. The holy grail would be a reliable assessment that requires only passive looking — and the 2025 Cued Looking Paradigm approaches this.
- **Population-level prevalence of "hidden competence"**: Individual case studies (Zimmerman, Kedar, Blasko) demonstrate that some non-speaking individuals have dramatically underestimated abilities. What proportion of the non-speaking autistic population this applies to is unknown.
- **Longitudinal assessment tracking**: How do assessment scores change when non-speaking individuals are given communication access and academic instruction? Very few longitudinal studies follow non-speaking individuals through the transition from no-communication to AAC or other methods.
- **Cultural and socioeconomic disparities in assessment access**: Non-speaking autistic individuals in low-resource settings may never receive any form of adapted assessment. The intersection of poverty, race, and disability in assessment outcomes is virtually unstudied.
