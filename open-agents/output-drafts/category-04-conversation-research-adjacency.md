# Adjacency Pairs and Preference Organization

## Research for English Pragmatics Knowledge Base (Category 4: Conversation Structure)

---

## 1. What Adjacency Pairs Are

### 1.1 Definition

An adjacency pair is the most basic unit of sequence organization in conversation. It consists of two utterances produced by two different speakers, placed one after the other, where the first utterance (the **first pair part**, or FPP) creates a normative expectation for a specific type of responding utterance (the **second pair part**, or SPP). The concept was developed within the tradition of Conversation Analysis (CA), pioneered by Harvey Sacks, Emanuel Schegloff, and Gail Jefferson in the 1960s-70s.

Schegloff and Sacks (1973) identified the defining properties of adjacency pairs:
1. They consist of **two turns** (utterances)
2. The turns are produced by **different speakers**
3. The turns are **adjacently placed** (one follows the other)
4. The turns are **relatively ordered** -- a first pair part comes before a second pair part
5. The turns are **pair-type related** -- the type of first pair part determines what type of second pair part is relevant (a question makes an answer relevant, not a greeting)

These are not just statistical regularities about what tends to follow what. They are **normative structures** -- participants orient to them as obligations. When someone asks you a question, you don't just happen to answer; you are *expected* to answer, and your failure to do so is *noticeable* and *accountable*.

### 1.2 Conditional Relevance

The concept of **conditional relevance** was introduced by Schegloff (1968) in his paper "Sequencing in Conversational Openings" and is central to understanding how adjacency pairs organize interaction.

**Conditional relevance** means this: when a first pair part is produced, the second pair part becomes *conditionally relevant* -- that is, it is now expected, and its absence is not merely nothing happening but is something *noticeably absent*. The non-occurrence of the second pair part is treated as a meaningful event, not simply as silence.

Schegloff (1968, p. 1083) described this as an item being "officially absent" -- missing after being made relevant by a prior item. This is a crucial distinction:

- **Before** a question is asked: silence is just silence
- **After** a question is asked: silence is the *noticeable absence of an answer*

This is what gives adjacency pairs their organizing power. The first pair part doesn't just precede the second -- it makes the second *due*. When it doesn't appear, participants treat this as requiring explanation.

**What happens when the expected SPP is absent:**

When the second pair part fails to materialize, several things can happen:

1. **The first speaker repeats or reformulates the FPP** -- treating the silence as indicating the recipient may not have heard or understood:
   - A: "What time is it?" [silence] A: "I said what time is it?"
   
2. **The first speaker pursues a response** -- using various techniques to elicit the missing SPP:
   - A: "Did you hear me?"
   - A: "Hello?"
   - A: "Well?"

3. **The silence itself is interpreted as meaningful** -- often as a harbinger of a dispreferred response:
   - When someone asks "Can you come to my party?" and receives silence, both parties understand this silence as projecting a likely refusal
   - The silence does interactional work: it communicates reluctance, difficulty, or unwillingness

4. **The absence is treated as accountable** -- the person who failed to produce the SPP may be sanctioned, questioned, or treated as having done something interactionally problematic:
   - A: "I asked you a question."
   - A: "Are you ignoring me?"
   - The absent SPP can be treated as rude, evasive, or hostile

### 1.3 First Pair Parts (FPPs) and Second Pair Parts (SPPs)

**First Pair Parts (FPPs)** are actions that initiate a sequence. They create a slot for a responding action and constrain what can relevantly appear in that slot. Examples: questions, greetings, requests, offers, invitations, complaints, compliments, accusations, summonses, and assessments.

**Second Pair Parts (SPPs)** are actions that respond to and are constrained by the FPP. They complete the sequence initiated by the FPP. Most adjacency pair types have **alternative SPPs** -- one or more different ways to respond. For example, a request can be met with a grant or a refusal; an invitation with acceptance or decline.

These alternative SPPs are not equal in their interactional status. This asymmetry is the domain of **preference organization** (covered in detail in Section 3).

### 1.4 How Adjacency Pairs Organize Conversation at the Most Basic Level

Adjacency pairs are the fundamental building blocks of conversational sequence. They organize conversation in several key ways:

1. **They create local order**: In the otherwise open, unscripted flow of conversation, adjacency pairs create predictable two-turn sequences. Each FPP projects what should come next.

2. **They enable mutual understanding**: The SPP demonstrates the second speaker's understanding of the FPP. If someone asks "Do you have the time?" and the respondent says "It's 3:30," the response shows the question was understood as a request for the time (not a philosophical inquiry about the nature of time). This is what Heritage (1984) called the **next-turn proof procedure** -- each turn displays an analysis of the prior turn.

3. **They create accountability**: Because second pair parts are conditionally relevant, their absence or deviation is noticeable and sanctionable. This keeps conversations on track.

4. **They provide for action sequences**: Complex conversational activities (like making arrangements, telling stories, ending phone calls) are built up out of chains and expansions of adjacency pairs.

5. **They coordinate transition**: Adjacency pairs help manage the transition between speakers. The FPP selects the next speaker and constrains what they should do.

## 2. Major Types of Adjacency Pairs

### 2.1 Question → Answer

**Pair structure**: Speaker A asks a question (FPP) → Speaker B provides an answer (SPP)

This is perhaps the most common and most studied adjacency pair type. Questions can be yes/no questions, wh-questions (who, what, where, when, why, how), or alternative questions.

**Examples:**

1. A: "What time does the meeting start?"
   B: "Ten o'clock."

2. A: "Have you eaten yet?"
   B: "Yeah, I grabbed something earlier."

3. A: "Who's picking up the kids today?"
   B: "I think it's Sarah's turn."

4. A: "Do you want coffee or tea?"
   B: "Coffee, please."

**When the expected SPP doesn't come:**
When someone asks a question and receives no answer, the questioner typically treats this as problematic. They may repeat the question, reformulate it, or explicitly call attention to the absence: "I asked you a question." Sustained failure to answer a question can be interpreted as evasiveness, hostility, or deliberate rudeness. In institutional settings (courtrooms, interviews), the obligation to answer is even stronger, and non-answers may be explicitly sanctioned.

The absence of an answer can also be used strategically -- a politician who doesn't answer a journalist's question is noticeably *not answering*, which itself communicates something (often that the answer would be unfavorable).

### 2.2 Greeting → Greeting

**Pair structure**: Speaker A produces a greeting (FPP) → Speaker B returns a greeting (SPP)

Greetings are among the most tightly paired adjacency pairs. The obligation to return a greeting is very strong across cultures.

**Examples:**

1. A: "Hi!"
   B: "Hey!"

2. A: "Good morning."
   B: "Good morning."

3. A: "How are you?"
   B: "Good, how are you?"

4. A: "Hey, what's up?"
   B: "Not much, you?"

**When the expected SPP doesn't come:**
Failing to return a greeting is one of the most socially conspicuous absences. If you say "Hello" to someone and they say nothing, this is immediately noticeable and typically interpreted as a deliberate snub, extreme distraction, or failure to hear. People will often pursue a response ("Hello? Did you hear me?") or feel slighted. The strength of this norm is visible in how uncomfortable people feel when they greet someone and are ignored -- it creates immediate social awkwardness. Children are explicitly taught this norm ("Say hello to Mrs. Johnson").

### 2.3 Offer → Accept/Decline

**Pair structure**: Speaker A makes an offer (FPP) → Speaker B accepts or declines (SPP)

Offers involve one person proposing to provide something of value (an object, service, help, food, etc.) to another.

**Examples:**

1. A: "Would you like some coffee?"
   B: "Yes, please." (acceptance)

2. A: "I can give you a ride to the airport."
   B: "Oh, that would be great, thanks!" (acceptance)

3. A: "Can I help you carry those bags?"
   B: "No, I'm fine, but thanks." (decline)

4. A: "Want me to proofread that for you?"
   B: "Uhh... actually, I think I'm good. Thanks though." (decline -- note the delay, hedge, and account)

**When the expected SPP doesn't come:**
When someone makes an offer and gets no response, the offerer may repeat or reformulate the offer ("I said, would you like some coffee?"). Silence after an offer tends to project a decline, because acceptances are typically produced immediately. The offerer may also withdraw or modify the offer if they sense reluctance.

### 2.4 Request → Grant/Refuse

**Pair structure**: Speaker A makes a request (FPP) → Speaker B grants or refuses (SPP)

Requests involve one person asking another to do something for them. Unlike offers (where the offerer proposes giving), requests involve the requester trying to get something.

**Examples:**

1. A: "Can you pass the salt?"
   B: "Sure." [passes salt] (grant)

2. A: "Could you cover my shift on Friday?"
   B: "Yeah, no problem." (grant)

3. A: "Would you mind turning down the music?"
   B: "Oh, sorry! Of course." (grant)

4. A: "Can you lend me fifty dollars?"
   B: "Uh... I'm kind of tight right now. Maybe next week?" (refusal -- note delay, account, and alternative offer)

**When the expected SPP doesn't come:**
Silence after a request is socially uncomfortable and is typically interpreted as reluctance to grant it. The requester may restate the request, provide additional justification for it, or explicitly address the silence: "Is that a no?" or "Is there a problem?" Because requests are face-threatening acts (they impose on the recipient), the absence of a response creates significant social tension.

### 2.5 Invitation → Accept/Decline

**Pair structure**: Speaker A issues an invitation (FPP) → Speaker B accepts or declines (SPP)

Invitations propose that the recipient participate in a future activity.

**Examples:**

1. A: "We're having a barbecue Saturday. Want to come?"
   B: "I'd love to! What should I bring?" (acceptance)

2. A: "There's a concert Friday night. You interested?"
   B: "Absolutely! What time?" (acceptance)

3. A: "Want to grab lunch tomorrow?"
   B: "Oh, I'd love to, but I've got a meeting at noon. How about Thursday?" (decline with account and alternative)

4. A: "We're going hiking this weekend if you want to join."
   B: "Hmm... that sounds fun, but I think I'm going to be pretty wiped out from the week. Thanks for thinking of me though." (decline with appreciation, hedge, and account)

**When the expected SPP doesn't come:**
Silence after an invitation strongly projects a decline. The inviter may pursue: "So... what do you think?" or "No pressure, but let me know." The absence of a response to an invitation is face-threatening to the inviter (it suggests their invitation isn't worth responding to) and can damage the relationship if unresolved.

### 2.6 Complaint → Apology/Justification/Denial

**Pair structure**: Speaker A produces a complaint (FPP) → Speaker B responds with an apology, justification, or denial (SPP)

Complaints are face-threatening to the recipient and create a complex response space.

**Examples:**

1. A: "You're late again."
   B: "I know, I'm sorry. Traffic was terrible." (apology + account)

2. A: "This room is a mess!"
   B: "I was out all day! I haven't had time to clean up." (justification/account)

3. A: "You never listen to me."
   B: "That's not true. I was listening, I just didn't agree." (denial + justification)

4. A: "You said you'd call me back and you didn't."
   B: "Oh no, you're right. I completely forgot. I'm really sorry." (admission + apology)

**When the expected SPP doesn't come:**
Silence after a complaint is particularly loaded. It can be interpreted as:
- Guilty acknowledgment (the person knows the complaint is valid and has no defense)
- Dismissiveness (the person doesn't think the complaint warrants a response)
- Passive aggression or stonewalling

The complainer will typically escalate: "Well? Don't you have anything to say?" or "Are you even listening to me?" Unaddressed complaints tend to fester and damage relationships.

### 2.7 Compliment → Accept/Deflect/Return

**Pair structure**: Speaker A produces a compliment (FPP) → Speaker B responds (SPP)

Compliment responses are unusually complex because of what Pomerantz (1978) identified as **conflicting constraints**: recipients simultaneously face pressure to (a) agree with the compliment (a general preference for agreement) and (b) avoid self-praise (a norm against appearing conceited). This creates what she called an "interactional bind."

**Response strategies** (following Pomerantz 1978):

- **Acceptance/appreciation token**: "Thanks!" / "Oh, thank you!"
- **Praise downgrade**: "It's nothing special really" / "It was on sale"
- **Credit shift**: "My mom actually picked it out" / "The whole team deserves credit"
- **Return compliment**: "You look great too!" / "Well, yours is even better"
- **Questioning**: "Really? You think so?"
- **Disagreement**: "Oh, no, I look terrible today"

**Examples:**

1. A: "That's a beautiful dress!"
   B: "Oh, thanks! I found it at a thrift store." (acceptance + downgrade)

2. A: "You did an amazing job on that presentation."
   B: "Thanks, but honestly the data spoke for itself." (acceptance + credit shift)

3. A: "Your garden looks incredible."
   B: "Ha, you should see it when the weeds take over." (deflection through downgrade)

4. A: "I love your haircut!"
   B: "Oh really? I'm still getting used to it." (questioning + mild deflection)

**When the expected SPP doesn't come:**
Silence after a compliment is awkward and may be interpreted as either (a) the recipient not hearing, (b) the recipient being uncomfortable, or (c) the compliment being perceived as insincere or inappropriate. The complimenter may pursue: "I mean it, you really do look great." Culturally, failing to acknowledge a compliment at all is relatively rare and is treated as interactionally problematic.

### 2.8 Accusation → Denial/Admission/Justification

**Pair structure**: Speaker A produces an accusation (FPP) → Speaker B denies, admits, or justifies (SPP)

Accusations are high-stakes adjacency pairs because they directly threaten the recipient's face and moral standing.

**Examples:**

1. A: "Did you eat my leftover pizza?"
   B: "No, I didn't touch it." (denial)

2. A: "You copied your homework from the internet."
   B: "Okay, yeah, I did. I was running out of time." (admission + account)

3. A: "You told Sarah what I said, didn't you?"
   B: "I may have mentioned it, but she promised not to say anything." (partial admission + justification)

4. A: "You're the one who broke the vase."
   B: "It was an accident! I was trying to clean behind it and it slipped." (admission + account/justification)

**When the expected SPP doesn't come:**
Silence after an accusation is one of the most heavily interpreted absences in conversation. It is very commonly treated as tantamount to admission -- the folk wisdom "silence implies guilt" has a basis in conversational structure. The accuser will typically press: "Well? Did you or didn't you?" The accused person's failure to deny is treated as especially damning because denial is so strongly expected.

### 2.9 Farewell → Farewell

**Pair structure**: Speaker A produces a farewell (FPP) → Speaker B returns a farewell (SPP)

Farewells constitute the **terminal exchange** of a conversation -- the final adjacency pair that accomplishes the actual leave-taking (after pre-closing sequences have wound the conversation down).

**Examples:**

1. A: "Bye!"
   B: "Bye!"

2. A: "See you later."
   B: "See you."

3. A: "Have a good night."
   B: "You too. Take care."

4. A: "Talk to you soon."
   B: "Sounds good. Bye."

**When the expected SPP doesn't come:**
Like greetings, farewells have a very strong norm of reciprocity. Failing to return a farewell is conspicuously rude -- if someone says "Goodbye" and the other person says nothing, this is a strong social signal (of anger, hurt, or deliberate rudeness). People may pursue: "I said goodbye..." The matched structure of farewells (both parties say goodbye) is what allows participants to actually disengage from the conversation without anyone being "left holding the floor."

### 2.10 Summons → Answer

**Pair structure**: Speaker A produces a summons (FPP) → Speaker B answers (SPP)

A summons is an action designed to get the attention of another person and establish a channel for further talk. This is a special type of adjacency pair because the SPP (the answer) doesn't complete a conversational action but rather *opens the way* for one -- it's a pre-sequence (see Section 5).

**Examples:**

1. A: "Hey, Sarah?"
   B: "Yeah?"

2. A: "Mom!"
   B: "What is it?"

3. [Phone rings -- this is the summons]
   B: "Hello?" [this is the answer]

4. A: "Excuse me?"
   B: "Yes? Can I help you?"

**When the expected SPP doesn't come:**
When a summons goes unanswered, the summoner almost always repeats with increased intensity:
- "Sarah?" [silence] "SARAH!" [silence] "SARAH, I'M TALKING TO YOU!"
- Phone rings... rings... rings... (the repeated ringing is the mechanism pursuing an answer)

Schegloff noted that the summons-answer sequence has a special property: **non-terminality**. A summons is incomplete without an answer, and an answer typically requires some further talk. You can't just summon someone and then say nothing; equally, you can't just answer a summons without the summoner then saying why they summoned you.

### 2.11 Assessment → Agreement/Disagreement

**Pair structure**: Speaker A produces an assessment (FPP) → Speaker B agrees or disagrees (SPP)

Assessments are evaluative statements about events, objects, people, or experiences. They make agreement or disagreement relevant as responses.

**Examples:**

1. A: "Nice weather today!"
   B: "Yeah, gorgeous!" (agreement -- note the upgrade)

2. A: "That movie was terrible."
   B: "Oh, I know. I couldn't even finish it." (agreement)

3. A: "The food here is really good."
   B: "Mm, yeah, it's not bad." (weak agreement / mild downgrade)

4. A: "I think the new policy is great."
   B: "Well... I can see some benefits, but I'm not sure about the implementation." (disagreement -- note the "well" preface, the partial agreement before the disagreement)

**When the expected SPP doesn't come:**
Silence after an assessment is uncomfortable because assessments invite alignment. The assessor may pursue: "Don't you think?" or "Right?" If someone says "Beautiful sunset, isn't it?" and gets no response, they may feel their attempt at shared experience has been rebuffed. In social situations, the pressure to produce at least minimal agreement with assessments is quite strong.

Pomerantz (1984) demonstrated that agreement with assessments is the preferred SPP and is produced in specific ways:
- **Upgraded agreement**: A: "It's a nice day." B: "It's gorgeous!" (stronger evaluation = emphatic agreement)
- **Same-level agreement**: A: "It's nice." B: "Yeah, it is." (matching evaluation)
- Disagreement, in contrast, is characteristically delayed, hedged, and prefaced

## 3. Preference Organization

### 3.1 Definition and Core Concept

**Preference organization** is one of the most important concepts in Conversation Analysis, and also one of the most commonly misunderstood. The term "preference" here does **NOT** refer to what individual speakers personally want or psychologically prefer. It refers to a **structural asymmetry** between alternative second pair parts.

When an FPP makes alternative SPPs possible (e.g., a request can get a grant or a refusal), these alternatives are not structurally equivalent. One is **preferred** and one (or more) is **dispreferred**. This is a property of the conversational system, not of individual psychology.

The key insight: **the conversational system is organized to promote social affiliation and minimize social conflict**. Actions that maintain social bonds (accepting, agreeing, granting) are structurally facilitated; actions that threaten social bonds (refusing, disagreeing, denying) are structurally marked as problematic.

Heritage (1984) and subsequent scholars have emphasized that this is not about individual desires but about the observable, systematic design features of turns. You can tell whether a response is preferred or dispreferred from its *form* -- how it is delivered -- not from whether the speaker wanted to give that response.

### 3.2 Characteristics of Preferred Responses

Preferred second pair parts are characteristically:

- **Produced without delay** -- they come immediately, often with minimal or no gap after the FPP
- **Structurally simple** -- they are brief and straightforward
- **Direct** -- they are produced without hedging, mitigation, or qualification
- **Unaccompanied by accounts** -- no explanation is needed for why you're giving this response
- **Unmarked** in their design -- they don't draw attention to themselves as a special type of response

Example of a preferred response (accepting an invitation):
> A: "Want to come to dinner Saturday?"  
> B: "Sure! What time?"

Notice: immediate, simple, direct, no explanation needed, no hedging.

### 3.3 Characteristics of Dispreferred Responses

Dispreferred second pair parts are characteristically:

- **Delayed** -- there is often a noticeable gap (silence) before the response
- **Prefaced** -- they begin with turn-initial markers like "well," "uh," "um," "so"
- **Include appreciation or partial agreement** -- "That's really nice of you..." / "I'd love to, but..."
- **Mitigated** -- the dispreferred action is softened, hedged, or qualified
- **Accompanied by an account** -- an explanation is provided for *why* the dispreferred action is being performed
- **Include the dispreferred component in a non-initial position** -- the actual refusal/disagreement is pushed later in the turn
- **May offer an alternative** -- "I can't Saturday, but how about Sunday?"
- **Structurally complex** -- they are longer and more elaborate than preferred responses

The canonical structure of a dispreferred response includes some or all of these components **in roughly this order**:

1. **Delay/silence** -- a gap before speaking
2. **Preface marker** -- "Well..." / "Uh..." / "Um..."
3. **Appreciation/partial agreement** -- "That's really kind of you..." / "I see what you mean..."
4. **Account/justification** -- "The thing is, I have a work commitment..." / "It's just that I'm so busy this week..."
5. **The dispreferred component itself** -- "...so I can't make it." / "...so I'll have to pass."
6. **Mitigating offer/alternative** -- "But maybe next time?" / "How about the week after?"

Example of a dispreferred response (declining an invitation):
> A: "Want to come to dinner Saturday?"  
> B: [0.8 second pause] "Oh, um... that sounds really great, and I'd love to, but I actually have my parents coming in from out of town this weekend, so I don't think I can swing it. Could we do it the following weekend maybe?"

Notice: delay, "oh" and "um" prefaces, appreciation ("sounds really great"), expression of desire to accept ("I'd love to"), account (parents visiting), the decline itself ("I don't think I can swing it"), and an alternative offer (following weekend).

### 3.4 Timing Data

Quantitative research has provided precise measurements of the timing differences between preferred and dispreferred responses:

**Stivers et al. (2009)** examined turn-timing across 10 languages and found:
- The modal gap between a question and its answer is approximately **0 to 200 milliseconds** across languages
- Confirmations (preferred) are produced faster than disconfirmations (dispreferred)
- This pattern holds across all 10 languages studied, suggesting it is a universal feature of human interaction

**Kendrick and Torreira (2015)** conducted a detailed quantitative study ("The Timing and Construction of Preference") and found:
- Gaps of **300 milliseconds or more** decrease the likelihood of receiving an unqualified acceptance and increase the likelihood of a response with dispreferred turn format
- Gaps of **700 milliseconds or more** are strongly associated with dispreferred actions
- The difference is not simply that dispreferred responses take longer to produce; the delay itself is a *signal* -- it communicates that a dispreferred response is coming

**Roberts, Torreira, and Levinson (2015)** reported:
- A mean gap duration of **around 200 milliseconds** in English telephone conversations from the Switchboard Corpus for typical turn transitions

**What this means in practice:**
When someone pauses for even half a second before responding to a request or invitation, both parties can sense that a refusal is likely coming. This is why people often say things like "uh oh" or prepare themselves when they hear hesitation. The timing information is unconsciously but powerfully processed.

### 3.5 Preference Organization for Each Major Adjacency Pair Type

| First Pair Part | Preferred SPP | Dispreferred SPP |
|---|---|---|
| **Request** | Grant | Refuse |
| **Offer** | Accept | Decline |
| **Invitation** | Accept | Decline |
| **Assessment** | Agree | Disagree |
| **Question** | Expected answer (answer that aligns with question design) | Unexpected/non-answer |
| **Proposal/Suggestion** | Accept | Reject |
| **Blame/Accusation** | Denial | Admission |
| **Compliment** | *Complex* (see below) | *Complex* (see below) |
| **Greeting** | Return greeting | [Non-return is deviant, not simply dispreferred] |
| **Farewell** | Return farewell | [Non-return is deviant, not simply dispreferred] |
| **Self-deprecation** | Disagreement ("No, you're great!") | Agreement ("Yeah, you are bad at that") |

**Detailed preference patterns for each:**

#### Request → Grant (preferred) / Refuse (dispreferred)

**Preferred (grant):**
> A: "Can you help me move on Saturday?"  
> B: "Sure, what time?"

Notice: immediate, simple, no account needed.

**Dispreferred (refusal):**
> A: "Can you help me move on Saturday?"  
> B: [pause] "Oh man... I would, but I actually already promised my mom I'd help her with her garden this weekend. I'm really sorry. Is there anyone else who could help?"

Notice: delay, preface ("oh man"), expression of willingness ("I would"), account (prior commitment), apology, alternative suggestion.

#### Offer → Accept (preferred) / Decline (dispreferred)

**Preferred (acceptance):**
> A: "Want me to give you a ride?"  
> B: "Oh, that would be great!"

**Dispreferred (decline):**
> A: "Want me to give you a ride?"  
> B: [pause] "That's really sweet of you to offer, but I actually need to make a stop on the way, so I'll just drive myself. Thanks though."

Notice: appreciation ("that's really sweet"), account (need to make a stop), the decline, thanks.

#### Invitation → Accept (preferred) / Decline (dispreferred)

**Preferred (acceptance):**
> A: "We're having people over Friday. Want to come?"  
> B: "I'd love to! Can I bring anything?"

**Dispreferred (decline):**
> A: "We're having people over Friday. Want to come?"  
> B: [pause] "Oh... that sounds really fun. I wish I could, but we've actually got tickets to a show that night. Maybe next time?"

#### Assessment → Agreement (preferred) / Disagreement (dispreferred)

**Preferred (agreement):**
> A: "That was a great movie."  
> B: "Yeah, I loved it!" (note the upgrade: "great" → "loved")

Pomerantz (1984) showed that agreed-with assessments are characteristically **upgraded** -- the second speaker produces a stronger version of the assessment.

**Dispreferred (disagreement):**
> A: "That was a great movie."  
> B: "Hmm... I don't know. I thought the ending was kind of weak. I mean, the acting was good, but..."

Notice: "hmm," "I don't know" (hedging), partial agreement before disagreement, qualification.

Pomerantz (1984) showed that disagreements are characteristically **delayed and downgraded**, often preceded by partial or token agreement.

#### Blame/Accusation → Denial (preferred) / Admission (dispreferred)

**Preferred (denial):**
> A: "You left the door unlocked again."  
> B: "No I didn't -- I definitely locked it."

**Dispreferred (admission):**
> A: "You left the door unlocked again."  
> B: [pause] "Uh... yeah, I think I might have. I was in such a rush this morning. I'm sorry, I'll be more careful."

Note: Some CA scholars describe the preference organization for blame/accusation as complex. The structural preference is for denial (which aligns with self-defense and face preservation), but the social situation may make admission the more appropriate response. This is an area where preference structure and social expectations can pull in different directions.

#### Compliment → Complex Preference Structure

Compliment responses have a **uniquely complex** preference organization, first described by Pomerantz (1978). The complexity arises from two conflicting constraints:

**Constraint A: Agree with the compliment** (general preference for agreement)
**Constraint B: Avoid self-praise** (norm against boasting)

If you fully accept a compliment ("Yes, I am very talented!"), you satisfy Constraint A but violate Constraint B. If you reject the compliment ("No, I'm really not good at all"), you satisfy Constraint B but violate Constraint A.

The result is that speakers employ a range of **compromise strategies**:

| Strategy | Example | How It Resolves the Conflict |
|---|---|---|
| **Appreciation token** | "Thanks!" | Acknowledges without explicitly agreeing |
| **Praise downgrade** | "It's nothing fancy" | Partially accepts but reduces the evaluated quality |
| **Credit shift** | "My teacher deserves all the credit" | Accepts the evaluation but deflects it from self |
| **Return compliment** | "Well, you look great too!" | Redirects attention to the other person |
| **Questioning** | "Really? You think so?" | Neither accepts nor rejects; seeks confirmation |
| **Disagreement** | "Oh, I look terrible today" | Rejects the compliment (risky -- can seem like fishing) |

#### Self-Deprecation → Disagreement (preferred) / Agreement (dispreferred)

This is a case where the preference structure is **reversed** from what you might expect. When someone puts themselves down, the preferred response is to disagree (to reassure them):

**Preferred (disagreement):**
> A: "I'm such a terrible cook."  
> B: "What are you talking about? That lasagna you made was amazing."

**Dispreferred (agreement):**
> A: "I'm such a terrible cook."  
> B: [pause] "Well... you're not the strongest in the kitchen, maybe."

Notice that agreement with self-deprecation is dispreferred even though agreement is normally preferred. This shows that preference is about social affiliation, not simply about matching polarity.

## 4. Insertion Sequences

### 4.1 What Insertion Sequences Are

Adjacency pairs don't always proceed directly from FPP to SPP. Often, another complete adjacency pair is inserted *between* the first pair part and its second pair part. This is called an **insertion sequence** (or **insert expansion**).

The inserted pair is understood as being done in the service of the still-pending base pair -- typically to clarify, establish preconditions, or address a problem that would prevent the production of an appropriate SPP.

### 4.2 How They Work

**Basic pattern:**

> FPP (base pair): "Can you give me a ride to the airport?"  
> → FPP (insert): "When is your flight?"  
> → SPP (insert): "Saturday at noon."  
> SPP (base pair): "Sure, I can do that."

The insert pair (Q: "When?" / A: "Saturday") is understood as being produced in service of answering the base request. The responder needs this information to determine whether they can grant the request.

### 4.3 Types of Insert Expansions

Schegloff (2007) distinguished between two types:

**Post-first insert sequences** occur after the base FPP and are oriented toward it. They address some problem or insufficiency with the FPP that prevents the immediate production of an SPP:

> A: "Can you pick up some milk?" (base FPP)  
> B: "Regular or oat?" (post-first insert FPP -- seeks clarification)  
> A: "Oat." (insert SPP)  
> B: "Sure." (base SPP)

**Pre-second insert sequences** are oriented toward the upcoming SPP. They establish the conditions needed to produce an appropriate SPP:

> A: "Want to go to that new Thai place?" (base FPP)  
> B: "Is it the one on Main Street?" (pre-second insert FPP)  
> A: "Yeah, near the bookstore." (insert SPP)  
> B: "Oh, I've been wanting to try that! Let's go." (base SPP)

### 4.4 Multiple and Nested Insertions

Insertion sequences can be **nested** -- an insert pair can have its own insert pair:

> A: "Can you drive me to the dentist?" (base FPP)  
> B: "Which dentist?" (insert 1 FPP)  
> A: "Dr. Martinez." (insert 1 SPP... almost)  
> B: "The one on Oak Street?" (insert 2 FPP -- inserted into insert 1)  
> A: "Yeah." (insert 2 SPP)  
> B: "That's only ten minutes away. Sure, no problem." (base SPP)

Despite this complexity, participants track which pair is being addressed and can "pop back" to the base pair to deliver the still-pending SPP. This demonstrates remarkable organizational competence on the part of ordinary conversationalists.

### 4.5 The Three Positions of Expansion

Schegloff (2007) organized all sequence expansions into three positions relative to the base adjacency pair:

**1. Pre-expansion** (before the base FPP):
An adjacency pair that comes before the main action to prepare for it. See Section 5 for detailed treatment.

**2. Insert expansion** (between the base FPP and SPP):
As described above -- a pair inserted between the first and second parts of the base pair.

**3. Post-expansion** (after the base SPP):
An adjacency pair that comes after the base pair's SPP. Post-expansions come in two varieties:

**Minimal post-expansion (sequence-closing thirds):**
These are single turns that close the sequence without projecting further talk:
> A: "What time is the meeting?" (FPP)  
> B: "Three o'clock." (SPP)  
> A: "Okay." (minimal post-expansion -- sequence-closing third)

Common sequence-closing thirds include "oh" (marking receipt of new information), "okay" (accepting/acknowledging), "right" (confirming), and "great" (evaluating positively).

Heritage (1984) identified the "oh" token specifically as a **change-of-state token** -- it marks that the speaker has moved from a state of not-knowing to knowing.

**Non-minimal post-expansion:**
These project additional turns and can launch new sequences:
> A: "Did you call the plumber?" (FPP)  
> B: "Yeah, this morning." (SPP)  
> A: "What did they say?" (non-minimal post-expansion -- launches new sequence)

**Third-position repair** is a special type of post-expansion where the first speaker, in third position, addresses a problem with the second speaker's understanding:
> A: "Is Dana coming tonight?" (FPP)  
> B: "She said she's busy." (SPP)  
> A: "No, I mean Dana from work, not your sister." (third-position repair)

## 5. Pre-Sequences in Detail

### 5.1 What Pre-Sequences Are

Pre-sequences are preliminary adjacency pairs that occur *before* a projected main action. They serve to **test the waters** -- checking whether conditions are favorable for the upcoming FPP before actually producing it. If conditions aren't favorable, the speaker can abandon the projected action without ever having explicitly performed it, thereby avoiding the face-threat of an explicit rejection.

Pre-sequences are themselves adjacency pairs (they have their own FPP and SPP), and they are oriented toward a *future* action that has not yet been performed.

### 5.2 Types of Pre-Sequences

#### Pre-Requests

Pre-requests check whether the conditions for a successful request are in place:

> A: "Are you busy this Saturday?" (pre-request)  
> B: "Not really, why?" (go-ahead)  
> A: "Could you help me move some furniture?" (the actual request)

> A: "Do you have a car?" (pre-request)  
> B: "Yeah..." (go-ahead, with some wariness)  
> A: "Any chance you could give me a ride to the airport?" (request)

> A: "Are you busy Saturday?" (pre-request)  
> B: "Yeah, I've got plans all day." (blocking response)  
> A: [abandons the request without ever making it]

#### Pre-Invitations

Pre-invitations check the recipient's availability or interest:

> A: "Are you doing anything Friday night?" (pre-invitation)  
> B: "I don't think so. What's up?" (go-ahead)  
> A: "We're having a dinner party. Want to come?" (invitation)

> A: "Do you like Thai food?" (pre-invitation)  
> B: "I love it!" (go-ahead)  
> A: "There's a great new place downtown. Want to check it out?" (invitation)

> A: "Are you free this weekend?" (pre-invitation)  
> B: "I'm actually pretty slammed." (blocking response)  
> A: [can abandon the invitation without losing face]

#### Pre-Announcements

Pre-announcements prepare the recipient for upcoming news:

> A: "Guess what!" (pre-announcement)  
> B: "What?" (go-ahead)  
> A: "I got the job!" (announcement)

> A: "You'll never believe what happened." (pre-announcement)  
> B: "What happened?" (go-ahead)  
> A: "I ran into our old teacher at the grocery store!" (announcement)

Terasaki (1976/2004) studied pre-announcement sequences in detail, showing how "guess what" / "what" functions as a preliminary pair that opens space for a telling.

#### Pre-Tellings

Pre-tellings prepare the way for an extended telling (story, report, piece of news):

> A: "Did I tell you about what happened at work?" (pre-telling)  
> B: "No, what?" (go-ahead)  
> A: [launches into the story]

> A: "Something funny happened this morning." (pre-telling)  
> B: "Oh yeah?" (go-ahead)  
> A: [tells the story]

#### Pre-Closings

Pre-closings signal that the speaker is ready to begin winding down the conversation (see Section 6 for detailed treatment):

> A: "Well, I should probably let you go..." (pre-closing)  
> B: "Yeah, I should get going too." (acceptance of pre-closing)  
> [Terminal exchange follows]

### 5.3 The Three Possible Responses to Pre-Sequences

When a pre-sequence is produced, the recipient can respond in three ways:

**1. Go-ahead**: The recipient signals that conditions are favorable, inviting the speaker to proceed with their projected action.
> A: "Are you busy Saturday?" → B: "Nope, I'm free. What's up?"

The go-ahead typically includes a question or prompt that explicitly invites the projected action ("What's up?" / "Why?" / "What?"). This gives the speaker the floor to produce their request, invitation, or announcement.

**2. Blocking response**: The recipient signals that conditions are NOT favorable, allowing the speaker to abort the projected action.
> A: "Are you busy Saturday?" → B: "Yeah, I've got my sister's wedding."

A blocking response effectively performs a preemptive rejection of the projected action without the action ever being explicitly stated. This is **face-saving for both parties**: the requester/inviter doesn't have to be explicitly rejected, and the recipient doesn't have to explicitly refuse.

**3. Hedging response**: The recipient signals ambivalence or caution, often by asking for more information before committing.
> A: "Are you busy Saturday?" → B: "Depends... why?" / "I might have something. What were you thinking?"

A hedging response keeps options open. The recipient wants to know what's coming before committing to availability or unavailability. This allows for what Schegloff calls **"contingent commitments"** -- the answer depends on what the question is leading to.

### 5.4 Why Pre-Sequences Exist

Pre-sequences exist primarily for **face management**. They allow speakers to:

1. **Test the waters** before committing to a potentially face-threatening action
2. **Avoid explicit rejection** -- if the pre-sequence gets a blocking response, the main action never happens, so neither party loses face from an explicit refusal
3. **Gauge receptivity** -- the response to the pre-sequence provides information about how the main action will be received
4. **Provide an account in advance** -- pre-sequences can lay groundwork that makes the upcoming request/invitation more reasonable
5. **Give the recipient an easy way out** -- a blocking response to a pre-sequence is much less face-threatening than an explicit refusal of a direct request

This is particularly important for actions that carry significant face risk. You're more likely to use a pre-sequence before asking someone to lend you money than before asking someone to pass the salt. The greater the imposition, the more likely a pre-sequence.

## 6. Closing Sequences

### 6.1 The Closing Problem

One of the foundational insights of Conversation Analysis is that **ending a conversation is not simple**. Schegloff and Sacks (1973) identified what they called the "closing problem":

Because the turn-taking system continuously allocates turns -- each turn makes a next turn relevant -- there is no natural stopping point built into the system. Any turn completion is a place where someone else could (and normatively should) speak. So how do participants stop talking without someone being left "holding the floor"?

You can't just stop talking -- that would leave the other person in a position where they might have more to say. You can't just say "bye" at any random point -- that would be abrupt and potentially rude. Even a terminal exchange ("bye" / "bye") only works if both participants have agreed that the conversation is over. The problem is: **how do you get to the point where both participants agree that a terminal exchange is appropriate?**

### 6.2 The Structure of Conversation Closing

Schegloff and Sacks (1973) showed that conversation closings have a systematic, multi-stage structure:

#### Stage 1: Topic Exhaustion or Winding Down

Before closing can begin, participants need to have exhausted (or be ready to suspend) their topical talk. This doesn't mean everything has been said; it means both parties signal willingness to stop introducing new topics.

#### Stage 2: Pre-Closing Sequence

The pre-closing is a crucial mechanism. It is a turn that occupies the floor **without introducing new topical talk**, thereby signaling readiness to close. Common pre-closing tokens include:

- **"Well..."** -- perhaps the most common pre-closing marker
- **"So..."** -- signals wrapping up
- **"Okay..."** -- suggests business is concluded  
- **"Anyway..."** -- signals departure from current topic toward closing
- **"Right..."** -- suggests confirmation of what's been discussed
- **"I should probably get going..."** -- explicit warrant for closing
- **"I'll let you go..."** -- frames the closing as benefiting the other person (a politeness strategy)
- **"I should let you get back to..."** -- similar politeness frame

These pre-closing tokens are what Schegloff and Sacks called **"possible pre-closings"** -- they are *possible* because they can be either accepted (moving toward closing) or rejected (by introducing a new topic).

#### Response to Pre-Closing

When a pre-closing is produced, the other participant can:

**Accept the pre-closing** (and proceed to terminal exchange):
> A: "Well, I should probably get going."  
> B: "Yeah, me too. It was great talking to you."  
> A: "You too. Talk soon."  
> B: "Bye."  
> A: "Bye."

**Reject the pre-closing** (by introducing something new):
> A: "Well..."  
> B: "Oh, one more thing -- did you hear about the meeting on Thursday?"  
> [conversation continues with new topic]

This is what Schegloff and Sacks meant by "opening up closings" -- a possible pre-closing can be the site where new topics are introduced, precisely because it signals that closing is imminent (which creates urgency for any remaining business).

#### Stage 3: Closing-Relevant Activities

Before the final terminal exchange, several activities commonly occur:

**Arrangement-making (planning future contact):**
> "Let's do lunch next week."  
> "I'll call you tomorrow about the details."  
> "See you at the meeting on Thursday."

Arrangements serve a dual function: they provide substance for the closing section and they affirm the continuing relationship (we're ending this conversation, but not the relationship).

**Warrants (reasons for ending):**
> "I need to get to the store before it closes."  
> "I've got to get back to work."  
> "I should let you get back to your dinner."

Warrants account for the closing -- they explain *why* the conversation is ending, which makes the closing seem motivated by external circumstances rather than disinterest.

**Summaries and back-references:**
> "Well, it was great catching up."  
> "Thanks for the advice on the car thing."  
> "Good luck with the interview!"

These demonstrate that the conversation was valued and provide a final collaborative moment.

**Appreciations:**
> "Thanks for calling."  
> "This was really helpful."  
> "I appreciate you taking the time."

**Well-wishes:**
> "Take care."  
> "Have a good night."  
> "Good luck tomorrow."

#### Stage 4: Terminal Exchange

The terminal exchange is the final adjacency pair that accomplishes the actual goodbye:

> A: "Bye."  
> B: "Bye."

Or:
> A: "See you later."  
> B: "See you."

Or:
> A: "Talk to you soon."  
> B: "Sounds good. Bye."  
> A: "Bye."

The terminal exchange is itself an adjacency pair (farewell-farewell), and it requires matched participation -- both parties must produce a farewell for the conversation to be properly closed.

### 6.3 Pre-Closing Devices in Detail

Schegloff and Sacks (1973) identified specific properties of pre-closing turns:

1. They **occupy a turn without introducing new topical talk** -- they take up the floor but don't say anything that requires a substantive response
2. They are **recognizably possible pre-closings** -- both participants can identify them as signals of readiness to close
3. They are **passable** -- if neither participant has more to say, the pre-closing can lead to the terminal exchange
4. They are **rejectable** -- the other participant can introduce a new topic, which "opens up" the closing and returns to topical talk

Common pre-closing formats:

| Pre-Closing Type | Examples | How It Works |
|---|---|---|
| **Discourse markers** | "Well..." "So..." "Anyway..." "Okay then..." | Signal topic transition toward closing |
| **Explicit warrants** | "I should get going" / "I need to run" | Provide external reason for ending |
| **Other-attentive warrants** | "I'll let you go" / "I don't want to keep you" | Frame closing as considerate of other person |
| **Summary** | "Well, it was great catching up" | Characterize the conversation as complete |
| **Arrangement** | "Let's talk again soon" | Plan future interaction to soften departure |
| **Appreciation** | "Thanks so much for your help" | Express gratitude that frames conversation as concluded |

### 6.4 Why Closing Is Systematically Organized

The systematic organization of closings reflects several interactional needs:

1. **Coordination**: Both participants must agree that the conversation is over. Unilateral departure is possible (hanging up the phone, walking away) but is treated as rude or aggressive.

2. **Face protection**: Abrupt closing threatens the other person's face (implying their company isn't valued). The pre-closing machinery provides a graceful transition.

3. **Business completion**: The pre-closing sequence provides a last opportunity for any remaining business ("oh, one more thing...").

4. **Relationship maintenance**: Closing-section activities (arrangements, well-wishes, appreciations) affirm the relationship extends beyond this conversation.

5. **Mutual release**: Both parties must be released from the obligation to speak. The terminal exchange accomplishes this -- after both parties say "bye," neither is obligated to take another turn.

### 6.5 The Closing Problem in Practice

Research has shown that the closing problem is genuinely difficult for people. A Harvard study (Mastroianni et al., 2021) found that conversations almost never ended when both participants wanted them to, and rarely ended when even one partner desired it. People systematically misjudge when their conversational partners want to stop talking.

This difficulty is amplified for individuals who may not automatically process the subtle pre-closing signals (discourse markers, topic winding-down, warrant-giving). Recognizing these patterns as systematic structures -- rather than trying to intuit the "right moment" to end a conversation -- provides an alternative pathway for understanding conversation closing.
