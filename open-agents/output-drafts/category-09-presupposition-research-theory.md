# Presupposition: Comprehensive Research for English Pragmatics Knowledge Base

## Research Purpose
This document provides comprehensive theoretical and practical coverage of presupposition in linguistics and pragmatics, intended for a knowledge base teaching English pragmatics to autistic individuals. The emphasis is on clear definitions, systematic categorization of triggers, explicit diagnostic tests, and precise distinctions from related phenomena.

## 1. What Presupposition IS

### 1.1 Core Definition

A **presupposition** is a background assumption that must be true for an utterance to make sense -- to be "felicitous" in pragmatic terms. Presuppositions are not what a sentence directly states; they are what a sentence *takes for granted* as already being the case.

More formally: a presupposition is an implicit assumption about the world or background belief relating to an utterance, whose truth is taken for granted in discourse. When a speaker produces an utterance, the presupposition is treated as shared knowledge -- something both speaker and listener are expected to already accept.

**The key distinction: ASSERTED vs. PRESUPPOSED**

Every utterance has two layers of meaning:

- **Asserted content (foreground, new information):** What the sentence directly claims, questions, or commands. This is the "point" of the utterance -- what the speaker is putting forward for consideration.
- **Presupposed content (background, taken-for-granted information):** What the sentence assumes to be already established and true. This is the foundation the utterance is built on.

**Example:**
- Sentence: *"John stopped smoking."*
- **Asserted:** John no longer smokes (this is the new information, the point of the sentence).
- **Presupposed:** John was smoking before (this is the background assumption -- it is taken for granted, not argued for).

The presupposed content is not "up for debate" -- it is presented as something everyone already knows and accepts. The asserted content is the new contribution to the conversation.

### 1.2 Foundational Philosophers

#### Gottlob Frege (1892)

The study of presupposition begins with Frege's seminal paper *"Über Sinn und Bedeutung"* ("On Sense and Reference"), published in 1892 in *Zeitschrift für Philosophie und philosophische Kritik*. Frege observed that when we use a definite description like "the present King of France," we are not *asserting* that such a person exists -- rather, existence is a *precondition* for the sentence to have a denotation (a truth value) at all.

On the Fregean analysis:
- Existence and uniqueness are understood as a **presupposition** of a sentence containing a definite description, rather than part of the content **asserted** by such a sentence.
- The sentence *"The present King of France is bald"* is not used to claim that there exists a unique present King of France who is bald. Instead, that there *is* a unique present King of France is part of what this sentence **presupposes**, and what it **says** (asserts) is that this individual is bald.
- If the presupposition fails (there is no King of France), then according to Frege, the sentence lacks a truth value entirely -- it is neither true nor false.

Frege's notion of presupposition (*Voraussetzung*) was relatively narrow, tied primarily to referring expressions (names and definite descriptions). But his insight -- that some aspects of meaning serve as preconditions rather than assertions -- became the foundation for all subsequent work.

#### P. F. Strawson (1950)

Strawson's paper *"On Referring"* (1950) directly challenged Bertrand Russell's theory of definite descriptions and brought presupposition into the center of philosophical and linguistic debate.

**The Russell-Strawson debate:**
- **Russell (1905)** had argued in "On Denoting" that *"The King of France is bald"* is simply **false**, because Russell's logical analysis of definite descriptions treats the existence claim as part of what the sentence asserts. On Russell's view, the sentence says: "There is one and only one King of France, and he is bald." Since the first conjunct is false, the whole sentence is false.
- **Strawson (1950)** objected: definite descriptions do not assert existence; they **presuppose** it. If there is no King of France, the sentence is not false -- it fails to make any statement at all. The question of whether it is true or false "does not arise." This creates what philosophers call a **truth-value gap**: the sentence is neither true nor false.

Strawson's contribution was to argue that presupposition failure does not produce falsehood but rather a breakdown of the conditions under which the sentence can be meaningfully evaluated. This is a fundamentally different picture from Russell's.

### 1.3 The Classic Example

*"The King of France is bald."*

This sentence has been the central example in presupposition theory since Frege and Russell:

- **What it asserts:** The King of France has the property of being bald.
- **What it presupposes:** There exists a (unique) King of France.

Since France is currently a republic and has no king, the presupposition is false. The question then becomes: is the sentence false (Russell's view) or does it fail to have a truth value (Frege/Strawson's view)?

This debate remains one of the foundational puzzles of philosophy of language and has driven much of the theoretical development of presupposition theory over the past century.

### 1.4 How Presuppositions Differ from Entailments and Implicatures

Presuppositions must be carefully distinguished from two other types of meaning inference: **entailments** and **implicatures**. These three phenomena are often confused but have fundamentally different properties.

**Quick comparison:**

| Property | Presupposition | Entailment | Implicature |
|----------|---------------|------------|-------------|
| Survives negation? | YES | NO | Depends (often yes for conversational) |
| Cancellable? | Not easily | No | Yes (conversational) |
| Part of what is said? | No (background) | Yes (logical consequence) | No (inferred) |
| Source | Specific trigger words/constructions | Logical/semantic meaning | Conversational context + maxims |
| If false, the sentence is... | Infelicitous / truth-value gap | Still has a truth value | Still has a truth value |

These distinctions are elaborated in detail in Section 6 below.

## 2. The Projection Problem

### 2.1 Overview

The **projection problem** is the central theoretical puzzle of presupposition theory. It asks: when a presupposition-triggering expression is embedded inside a larger sentence (under negation, in a question, in a conditional, etc.), does the presupposition of the embedded part become a presupposition of the whole sentence?

The answer, discovered through decades of research most notably by Lauri Karttunen (1973), is: **sometimes yes, sometimes no, and the rules are complex**. This inconsistency is what makes it a "problem."

### 2.2 Presuppositions Survive Negation

The most important and well-known property of presuppositions is that they **survive under negation**. This is also the primary diagnostic test for identifying presuppositions.

**The test:** Take a sentence. Negate it. If an inference still holds after negation, it is a presupposition. If it does not survive negation, it is an entailment.

**Example with presupposition:**
- *"John stopped smoking."* → Presupposes: John was smoking before.
- *"John did NOT stop smoking."* → STILL presupposes: John was smoking before.
- The presupposition survives. Whether John stopped or didn't stop, both versions take for granted that he was a smoker.

**Example with entailment (for contrast):**
- *"John is a bachelor."* → Entails: John is unmarried.
- *"John is NOT a bachelor."* → Does NOT entail John is unmarried. (He might be married, widowed, etc.)
- The entailment does not survive negation.

This is the **key diagnostic test**: if you negate a sentence and the background assumption still holds, that assumption is a presupposition. If negating the sentence removes the inference, it is an entailment.

### 2.3 Presuppositions Survive Questioning

Presuppositions also project (survive) when the sentence is turned into a question:

- *"Is the King of France bald?"* → Still presupposes there IS a King of France.
- *"Has John stopped smoking?"* → Still presupposes John was smoking.
- *"Did she realize she was wrong?"* → Still presupposes she was wrong.

The question form does not challenge the presupposition; it only questions the asserted content.

### 2.4 Presuppositions Survive Conditionalization

Presuppositions typically survive when placed in the antecedent or consequent of a conditional:

- *"If the King of France is bald, he needs a hat."* → Still presupposes a King of France exists.
- *"If John stopped smoking, his health will improve."* → Still presupposes John was smoking.

### 2.5 Presuppositions Survive Modal Embedding

Presuppositions survive under possibility and necessity modals:

- *"John might have stopped smoking."* → Still presupposes John was smoking.
- *"It's possible that she regrets leaving."* → Still presupposes she left.

### 2.6 The "Family of Sentences" Test

Linguists use a systematic **family of sentences test** to diagnose presuppositions. Given a target sentence S that seems to carry an inference P, construct four variants:

1. **Negation:** "It is not the case that S" -- does P still hold?
2. **Question:** "Is it the case that S?" -- does P still hold?
3. **Conditional antecedent:** "If S, then Q" -- does P still hold?
4. **Modal:** "It is possible that S" -- does P still hold?

If P survives all four embeddings, it is almost certainly a presupposition. If it fails to survive negation, it is an entailment. This is the standard diagnostic toolkit.

### 2.7 Karttunen's Plugs, Holes, and Filters

Lauri Karttunen (1973) systematically investigated what happens to presuppositions inside complex sentences and developed a three-way taxonomy:

- **Holes:** Operators that allow presuppositions to project freely (pass through unchanged). These include negation, yes/no questions, possibility modals ("perhaps," "might"), and factive verbs like "know." When a presupposition-triggering expression is embedded under a hole, the presupposition of the embedded part becomes a presupposition of the whole sentence.

- **Plugs:** Operators that block presupposition projection. These include verbs of saying like "say that," "tell," "mention," and "claim." When a presupposition trigger is embedded under a plug, the presupposition does NOT project to the whole sentence. For example: *"John said that the King of France is bald"* does not presuppose that there is a King of France -- it only reports what John said.

- **Filters:** Operators with intermediate behavior -- they allow some presuppositions through but block others. The most important filters are conjunction ("and"), conditionals ("if...then"), and disjunction ("or"). The filtering behavior depends on the logical relationship between the clauses. In a conjunction like "P and Q," presuppositions of Q that are entailed by P get filtered out (they don't project), while others do project.

Karttunen's filtering condition for conjunctions: if the first conjunct entails the presupposition of the second conjunct, the presupposition is "satisfied" locally and does not project to the whole sentence.

**Example of filtering:**
- *"John is a smoker and he has stopped smoking."* -- The first clause ("John is a smoker") satisfies the presupposition of "stopped smoking" (that John was a smoker), so the whole sentence does NOT presuppose that John was a smoker. The presupposition is filtered out.
- Compare: *"It's raining and John has stopped smoking."* -- The first clause says nothing about smoking, so the presupposition of "stopped smoking" projects: the whole sentence presupposes John was a smoker.

## 3. Presupposition Triggers

Presupposition triggers are the specific linguistic constructions -- words, phrases, and syntactic structures -- that generate presuppositions. Levinson (1983, pp. 181-184) identified thirteen major types. The following is a comprehensive catalog with extensive examples.

### 3a. Definite Descriptions

**Trigger:** The use of "the [X]" (the definite article followed by a noun phrase)

**What it presupposes:** X exists (and is unique in context)

**Mechanism:** When a speaker uses a definite description, they are taking for granted that the referent exists. The existence is not asserted; it is assumed as background.

**Examples:**
- *"The teacher left early."* → Presupposes: There is a (contextually identifiable) teacher.
- *"The dog is barking."* → Presupposes: There is a (specific, identifiable) dog.
- *"Please close the door."* → Presupposes: There is a door (in the relevant context).
- *"The winner will receive a prize."* → Presupposes: There will be a winner.
- *"The President of France visited Berlin."* → Presupposes: France has a president.

**Negation test:**
- *"The teacher did NOT leave early."* → Still presupposes there is a teacher.

**Theoretical note:** Definite descriptions carry both an **existence presupposition** (the referent exists) and a **uniqueness presupposition** (there is exactly one such referent in the context). Russell (1905) treated these as part of the assertion; Frege (1892) and Strawson (1950) treated them as presuppositions. Modern accounts (Heim 1991, Abbott 2008, Schoubye 2013) generally side with the presuppositional analysis.

Possessive noun phrases work similarly: *"John's car broke down"* presupposes John has a car.

### 3b. Factive Verbs

**Trigger:** Verbs that presuppose the truth of their complement clause: *know, realize, regret, discover, be aware, be sorry, be proud, be glad, be sad, be indifferent, notice, remember, forget, deplore, agree*

**What it presupposes:** The complement clause (the embedded "that..." clause) is TRUE.

**Mechanism:** Kiparsky and Kiparsky (1970) made the foundational distinction between **factive** and **non-factive** verbs. Factive verbs presuppose the truth of their sentential complements; non-factive verbs (believe, think, assume, imagine, claim) do not.

**Examples:**
- *"She realizes she was wrong."* → Presupposes: She WAS wrong.
- *"He regrets eating the cake."* → Presupposes: He DID eat the cake.
- *"I discovered that the door was unlocked."* → Presupposes: The door WAS unlocked.
- *"They know that the earth is round."* → Presupposes: The earth IS round.
- *"She was sorry that she missed the meeting."* → Presupposes: She DID miss the meeting.
- *"He's aware that the deadline has passed."* → Presupposes: The deadline HAS passed.
- *"I forgot that we had a meeting."* → Presupposes: We DID have a meeting.
- *"She noticed that the window was open."* → Presupposes: The window WAS open.

**Negation test:**
- *"She does NOT realize she was wrong."* → Still presupposes: She was wrong.
- *"He does NOT regret eating the cake."* → Still presupposes: He did eat the cake.

**Subclasses of factive verbs:**
- **Epistemic factives** (know, realize, discover, be aware, notice): presuppose the truth of the complement and relate to knowledge/awareness. These are sometimes called "soft" triggers because they can more easily shed their factivity in certain contexts.
- **Emotive factives** (regret, be sorry, be proud, be glad, be sad, deplore, hate): presuppose the truth of the complement AND add that the subject has an emotional attitude toward that fact. These are generally "harder" triggers -- more resistant to cancellation.

**Contrast with non-factive verbs:**
- *"She thinks she was wrong."* → Does NOT presuppose she was wrong. (Non-factive verb.)
- *"He believes the door was unlocked."* → Does NOT presuppose the door was unlocked.

### 3c. Change-of-State Verbs (Aspectual Verbs)

**Trigger:** Verbs that denote a change in state or the continuation/cessation of a process: *stop, start, begin, continue, finish, cease, resume, keep on, carry on, complete*

**What it presupposes:** The existence of a prior state (or the absence of one, depending on the verb).

**Examples:**
- *"Have you stopped smoking?"* → Presupposes: You WERE smoking.
- *"She started crying."* → Presupposes: She was NOT crying before.
- *"He continued running."* → Presupposes: He WAS already running.
- *"They finished building the house."* → Presupposes: They WERE building the house.
- *"The engine ceased making noise."* → Presupposes: The engine WAS making noise.
- *"She began learning German."* → Presupposes: She was NOT learning German before.
- *"He resumed his studies."* → Presupposes: He HAD been studying before (and had stopped).

**Negation test:**
- *"She has NOT stopped smoking."* → Still presupposes: She was smoking.
- *"He didn't start running."* → Still presupposes: He was not running before.

**Why this matters pragmatically:** Change-of-state verbs are a common source of manipulative presupposition. The classic "trick question" -- *"Have you stopped beating your wife?"* -- exploits this trigger. Whether you answer "yes" or "no," you appear to accept the presupposition that you were beating your wife.

### 3d. Iterative Expressions

**Trigger:** Words and morphemes that indicate repetition or return: *again, another, return, restore, re- (prefix), repeat, too, also, back, more, still*

**What it presupposes:** The relevant action or state has occurred before.

**Examples:**
- *"He's late again."* → Presupposes: He was late before (at least once).
- *"She returned to Paris."* → Presupposes: She was in Paris before.
- *"They rebuilt the bridge."* → Presupposes: The bridge existed before (and was destroyed/removed).
- *"Can I have another cookie?"* → Presupposes: I already had at least one cookie.
- *"The government restored order."* → Presupposes: There was order before (which had been disrupted).
- *"He came back."* → Presupposes: He was here before and had left.
- *"She's still working."* → Presupposes: She was working before (and has continued).
- *"They reopened the investigation."* → Presupposes: There was an investigation before that had been closed.

**Negation test:**
- *"He's NOT late again."* → Still presupposes: He was late before.
- *"She did NOT return to Paris."* → Still presupposes: She was in Paris before.

### 3e. Cleft Constructions

**Trigger:** Syntactic structures that split a simple sentence into two clauses, backgrounding part of the information:
- *It-clefts:* "It was X who/that..."
- *Pseudo-clefts:* "What X did was..."

**What it presupposes:** The backgrounded clause is true; only the focused element is asserted.

**Examples:**
- *"It was JOHN who broke the window."* → Presupposes: Someone broke the window. Asserts: That someone was John.
- *"It was IN PARIS that they met."* → Presupposes: They met somewhere. Asserts: The place was Paris.
- *"What John lost was his wallet."* → Presupposes: John lost something. Asserts: It was his wallet.
- *"It's the NOISE that bothers me."* → Presupposes: Something bothers me. Asserts: It's the noise.
- *"What she needs is a vacation."* → Presupposes: She needs something. Asserts: It's a vacation.

**Negation test:**
- *"It was NOT John who broke the window."* → Still presupposes: Someone broke the window.

**Why clefts matter:** Cleft constructions are one of the clearest examples of the foreground/background split. The cleft takes information that could have been fully asserted ("John broke the window") and restructures it so that part (someone broke the window) is presupposed and only the focused element (John) is asserted. Clefts are considered "hard" presupposition triggers -- their presuppositions are very resistant to cancellation.

### 3f. Temporal Clauses

**Trigger:** Subordinating conjunctions that locate events in time: *before, after, during, while, since, when, until, as soon as, once*

**What it presupposes:** The event described in the temporal clause occurred (or will occur).

**Examples:**
- *"Before she left, she locked the door."* → Presupposes: She left.
- *"After the war ended, they rebuilt the city."* → Presupposes: The war ended.
- *"While he was sleeping, the phone rang."* → Presupposes: He was sleeping.
- *"Since you arrived, everything has been better."* → Presupposes: You arrived.
- *"I'll call you when I get home."* → Presupposes: I will get home.
- *"Until the police arrived, there was chaos."* → Presupposes: The police arrived.

**Negation test:**
- *"Before she left, she did NOT lock the door."* → Still presupposes: She left.

**Important note:** The temporal clause information is presented as background context, not as the main point of the sentence. It sets the temporal frame within which the asserted event occurs.

### 3g. Comparative and Contrastive Constructions

**Trigger:** Comparative adjectives and adverbs, and constructions that compare or contrast: *more, less, better, worse, as X as, rather than, compared to*

**What it presupposes:** The basis of comparison is true.

**Examples:**
- *"Carol is a better linguist than Barbara."* → Presupposes: Barbara IS a linguist.
- *"This cake is tastier than the last one."* → Presupposes: There was a last one (a previous cake) and it had some degree of tastiness.
- *"He runs faster than his brother."* → Presupposes: His brother runs.
- *"She's as tall as her mother."* → Presupposes: Her mother has some height (exists).
- *"This year's profits are lower than last year's."* → Presupposes: There were profits last year.

**Negation test:**
- *"Carol is NOT a better linguist than Barbara."* → Still presupposes: Barbara is a linguist.

### 3h. Counterfactual Conditionals

**Trigger:** Conditional sentences that describe hypothetical, non-actual situations, typically using past subjunctive or past perfect: *"If X had happened...", "If X were the case..."*

**What it presupposes:** The antecedent (the "if" clause) is FALSE -- it did not happen.

**Examples:**
- *"If I had known, I would have helped."* → Presupposes: I did NOT know.
- *"If she were here, things would be different."* → Presupposes: She is NOT here.
- *"If he had studied harder, he would have passed."* → Presupposes: He did NOT study hard enough.
- *"If we had left earlier, we wouldn't have missed the train."* → Presupposes: We did NOT leave earlier.
- *"Had I been taller, I might have played basketball."* → Presupposes: I am NOT tall (enough).

**Negation test:**
- The counterfactual form itself embeds the presupposition so deeply that negation applies to the consequent, not the presupposition. The falsity of the antecedent is baked into the grammatical structure.

### 3i. Questions (Especially Loaded Questions)

**Trigger:** Questions, particularly *wh-questions* (who, what, when, where, why, how) and loaded questions.

**What it presupposes:** The proposition embedded in the question is (at least partially) true.

**Examples:**
- *"When did you stop beating your wife?"* → Presupposes: You WERE beating your wife. (The classic loaded question.)
- *"Why did you do that?"* → Presupposes: You DID do it.
- *"Who told you that?"* → Presupposes: Someone told you.
- *"Where did you put my keys?"* → Presupposes: You put my keys somewhere.
- *"How did you break the vase?"* → Presupposes: You broke the vase.
- *"What time did the meeting end?"* → Presupposes: The meeting ended.
- *"Why are you angry?"* → Presupposes: You ARE angry.

**Loaded questions as manipulation:**
A loaded question is a question that contains a presupposition the respondent has not accepted. It forces the respondent into a dilemma: answering either "yes" or "no" appears to accept the built-in presupposition. The respondent must first challenge the presupposition itself before they can meaningfully answer.

- *"When are you going to stop being lazy?"* -- Presupposes: You are lazy. Any answer to "when" implicitly accepts this.
- *"Have you told your boss about your mistakes?"* -- Presupposes: You made mistakes.

**Yes/no questions preserve presuppositions:**
- *"Is the King of France bald?"* → Still presupposes: There is a King of France.
- *"Did John stop smoking?"* → Still presupposes: John was smoking.

### 3j. Implicative Verbs

**Trigger:** Verbs that entail or presuppose something about the success, difficulty, or expectation associated with the action: *manage, fail, happen to, avoid, bother, remember (to), forget (to), dare*

**What it presupposes:** Varies by verb, but generally relates to effort, expectation, or attempt.

**Examples:**
- *"She managed to finish."* → Presupposes: Finishing was difficult / required effort / she tried.
- *"He failed to appear."* → Presupposes: He was expected to appear.
- *"I happened to see her."* → Presupposes: I didn't plan or intend to see her.
- *"She avoided answering the question."* → Presupposes: Answering the question was a possibility / was expected.
- *"He didn't bother to call."* → Presupposes: Calling was expected or would have been appropriate.
- *"She remembered to lock the door."* → Presupposes: She was supposed to / intended to lock the door.
- *"He forgot to buy milk."* → Presupposes: He was supposed to buy milk.

**Negation test:**
- *"She did NOT manage to finish."* → Still presupposes: Finishing was difficult / she tried.
- *"He did NOT fail to appear."* → Still presupposes: He was expected to appear.

**Important theoretical note:** Implicative verbs are classified as **"soft" triggers** in the distinction made by researchers like Abrusan (2011, 2016). Their presuppositions are more context-dependent and defeasible (cancellable) than those of "hard" triggers like cleft constructions or factive verbs. Experimental research shows that presuppositions from implicative verbs are less accessible in processing and elicit longer response times than presuppositions from hard triggers.

### 3k. Additional Trigger Types

Beyond the major categories above, several additional trigger types deserve mention:

#### Verbs of Judging
**Trigger:** *accuse, criticize, blame, praise, forgive, excuse, credit, justify, glorify*

- *"She criticized him for being late."* → Presupposes: He was late.
- *"He accused her of stealing."* → Presupposes: Theft occurred (or is alleged to have occurred).
- *"They blamed him for the accident."* → Presupposes: An accident happened.
- *"She forgave him for lying."* → Presupposes: He lied.

#### Non-Restrictive Relative Clauses
**Trigger:** Clauses set off by commas that provide additional information: *"..., who...", "..., which..."*

- *"My brother, who lives in Berlin, called me."* → Presupposes: My brother lives in Berlin. (The relative clause is backgrounded, not asserted.)
- *"The report, which was published yesterday, contains errors."* → Presupposes: The report was published yesterday.

#### Stressed / Focused Constituents (Implicit Clefts)
**Trigger:** Contrastive or emphatic stress on a particular word.

- *"JOHN broke the window."* (with heavy stress on "John") → Presupposes: Someone broke the window. Asserts: It was John.
- *"She went to BERLIN."* (stress on "Berlin") → Presupposes: She went somewhere. Asserts: The destination was Berlin.

#### Additive Particles
**Trigger:** *too, also, even, either*

- *"John came too."* → Presupposes: Someone else (besides John) came.
- *"She also speaks German."* → Presupposes: She speaks at least one other language.
- *"Even BILL passed the exam."* → Presupposes: Others passed the exam AND it was unexpected for Bill to pass.

## 4. Semantic vs. Pragmatic Presupposition

### 4.1 The Semantic View (Frege, Strawson)

On the semantic view, presupposition is a **relation between sentences** (or propositions). It is defined purely in terms of truth conditions:

**Definition:** Sentence A semantically presupposes sentence B if and only if:
- Whenever A is true, B is true.
- Whenever A is false, B is true.
- In other words, B must be true for A to have a truth value at all.

This means presupposition is a property of the language itself -- it follows from the meanings of words and constructions, regardless of who is speaking or what they intend. If a sentence has a presupposition, that presupposition holds no matter the context.

The semantic view treats presupposition failure (when B is false) as resulting in a **truth-value gap**: the sentence A is neither true nor false.

**Strengths:** Clean, formal, testable definition. Explains the negation test straightforwardly.
**Weaknesses:** Difficulty explaining context-dependent behavior; difficulty explaining accommodation and cancellation.

### 4.2 The Pragmatic View (Stalnaker 1973, 1974)

On the pragmatic view, presupposition is a **relation between a speaker and a proposition** -- it is about what the speaker **takes for granted**, not about sentence meaning per se.

**Stalnaker's definition (1974):** A proposition P is a pragmatic presupposition of a speaker S if and only if:
1. S assumes or believes that P.
2. S assumes or believes that the addressee assumes or believes that P.
3. S assumes or believes that the addressee recognizes that S is making these assumptions.

In simpler terms: presuppositions are the propositions whose truth the speaker **takes for granted** -- the background beliefs that the speaker treats as shared between speaker and listener.

This shifts the focus from sentence properties to **speaker behavior** and **discourse context**. A presupposition is not a feature of a sentence in isolation but a feature of how a speaker uses that sentence in a particular conversational context.

### 4.3 The Common Ground

The pragmatic view is intimately connected to the concept of **common ground** -- the set of propositions that all participants in a conversation mutually assume to be true.

**Stalnaker's Common Ground framework:**
- The common ground is the shared set of background assumptions in a conversation.
- Every assertion updates the common ground by adding new propositions.
- Presuppositions are propositions that the speaker assumes are **already in** the common ground.
- For an utterance to be felicitous, its presuppositions should already be part of the common ground.

**Example:**
If I say *"John stopped smoking,"* I am presupposing that "John was smoking" is already part of our shared knowledge -- it's already in the common ground. The new information I'm adding to the common ground is that John no longer smokes.

If the presupposition is NOT already in the common ground (perhaps the listener didn't know John smoked), then we have a problem -- which is addressed by **accommodation** (see below).

### 4.4 The Relationship Between Semantic and Pragmatic Presupposition

The two views are not entirely incompatible. Semantic presuppositions of sentences can become pragmatic presuppositions of speakers: speakers should believe that the context satisfies the conditions required by their utterances. But the pragmatic view allows for additional presuppositions that have non-semantic sources -- presuppositions that arise from context, social norms, or conversational expectations rather than from specific trigger words.

Modern presupposition theory generally incorporates elements of both views, with the pragmatic/common-ground framework being dominant in contemporary work.

### 4.5 Accommodation (Lewis 1979)

**The problem:** What happens when a speaker produces an utterance whose presupposition is NOT already in the common ground? In theory, the utterance should be infelicitous. But in practice, communication usually proceeds smoothly.

**Lewis's solution:** In his influential paper *"Scorekeeping in a Language Game"* (1979), David Lewis introduced the concept of **accommodation**. He compared conversation to a game with a scoreboard that tracks the evolving state of the discourse.

**How accommodation works:**
- If an utterance requires a presupposition that is not currently on the conversational "scoreboard" (not in the common ground), the score **automatically adjusts** to include that presupposition -- provided nobody objects.
- The listener **accommodates** the presupposition by silently adding it to the common ground.
- This happens routinely and usually unconsciously.

**Example:**
Imagine someone says to you: *"I have to pick up my sister from the airport."* You may not have known this person had a sister. But you don't stop and say, "Wait, you have a sister?" Instead, you silently accommodate -- you add "this person has a sister" to your mental model of the common ground, and the conversation continues.

**Types of accommodation:**
- **Global accommodation:** The presupposition is added to the top-level common ground of the entire conversation.
- **Local accommodation:** The presupposition is added only within the scope of the embedding operator (e.g., only within the "if" clause of a conditional).
- **Intermediate accommodation:** The presupposition is added at some intermediate level of embedding.

**Limits on accommodation:** Accommodation is not unlimited. Presuppositions that are too surprising, too controversial, or too consequential may resist accommodation. A listener might object: *"Wait -- who said you have a sister?"* or *"Hold on -- I never agreed that John was smoking."* This is where the manipulative potential of presupposition becomes relevant.

## 5. Presupposition Failure

### 5.1 What Happens When a Presupposition Is False?

When a presupposition is false -- when the background assumption does not hold -- the result is called **presupposition failure**. This is a distinct phenomenon from mere falsehood.

### 5.2 The Russell vs. Strawson Debate

This is the foundational philosophical disagreement:

**Russell's view:** The sentence is simply **false**. Russell's logical analysis treats the presupposed content as part of the assertion, so if any part of the assertion is false, the whole sentence is false. *"The King of France is bald"* is false because there is no King of France.

**Strawson's view:** The sentence is **neither true nor false** -- it suffers from a truth-value gap. The presupposed content is not part of the assertion but a precondition for the sentence to be evaluable. If the precondition fails, the sentence simply "doesn't get off the ground." The question of truth or falsity "does not arise."

**Strawson's later revision:** In 1964, Strawson came to acknowledge that the matter might not be decidable by formal argument alone. He suggested that each view might be reasonable depending on one's interests and perspective -- a concession to the complexity of the phenomenon.

### 5.3 Practical Consequences of Presupposition Failure

When presupposition failure occurs in actual conversation, several things can happen:

**Confusion:** The listener may be confused because the sentence doesn't make sense given what they know. *"I need to pick up my unicorn from the vet"* has a presupposition failure (presupposes you have a unicorn) that causes confusion.

**Reframing / Correction:** The listener may challenge the presupposition: *"Wait -- you don't have a unicorn"* or *"Hold on, John was never smoking."* This is the listener refusing to accommodate.

**Manipulation:** Speakers can deliberately use false presuppositions to smuggle claims into the common ground. If the listener accommodates without noticing, the false presupposition becomes part of the shared conversational background. This is the basis for manipulative loaded questions and persuasive rhetoric (see Section 3i on loaded questions).

**Accommodation of new information:** Sometimes presupposition "failure" is entirely routine -- the speaker uses a presupposition to introduce new information efficiently. *"My sister called"* (said to someone who didn't know you had a sister) is technically a presupposition failure, but the listener simply accommodates and moves on. This is **informative presupposition** -- using the presupposition mechanism to introduce new content without asserting it directly.

## 6. How Presupposition Relates to Other Pragmatic Phenomena

### 6.1 Presupposition vs. Entailment

**Entailment** is a logical relationship between sentences: sentence A entails sentence B if B must be true whenever A is true. Entailment is a truth-conditional relationship -- it follows from the semantic content of the sentence.

**The critical difference: behavior under negation.**

- **Entailments do NOT survive negation:**
  - *"John is a bachelor"* entails *"John is unmarried."*
  - *"John is NOT a bachelor"* does NOT entail *"John is unmarried."* (He could be married.)
  - The entailment is cancelled by negation.

- **Presuppositions DO survive negation:**
  - *"John stopped smoking"* presupposes *"John was smoking."*
  - *"John did NOT stop smoking"* STILL presupposes *"John was smoking."*
  - The presupposition survives negation.

**Another key difference: what happens when the inference is false.**
- If an entailment is false, the original sentence is straightforwardly false. (*"John is a bachelor"* is false if John is married -- no truth-value gap.)
- If a presupposition is false, there may be a truth-value gap (Strawson) or confusion/infelicity.

**Formal summary:**
- A entails B: if A is true, then B must be true; if A is false, B can be either true or false.
- A presupposes B: if A is true, B is true; if A is false, B is STILL true.

### 6.2 Presupposition vs. Implicature

**Conversational implicature** (in the Gricean sense) is an inference that arises not from what is said but from the way it is said, governed by conversational maxims (quantity, quality, relevance, manner).

**The critical difference: cancellability.**

- **Implicatures ARE cancellable.** A speaker can explicitly cancel an implicature without contradiction:
  - *"Some of the students passed"* implicates: Not all students passed.
  - But: *"Some of the students passed -- in fact, all of them did."* This cancellation is perfectly coherent.

- **Presuppositions are NOT easily cancellable.** Attempting to cancel a presupposition creates oddness or contradiction:
  - *"John stopped smoking"* presupposes: John was smoking.
  - *"John stopped smoking, but he never smoked."* This is contradictory/incoherent.

**Other differences:**

| Feature | Presupposition | Conversational Implicature |
|---------|---------------|---------------------------|
| Source | Specific trigger words/constructions | General conversational principles |
| Cancellable? | Not easily (creates contradiction) | Yes (can be explicitly cancelled) |
| Calculable? | Identified by trigger type | Calculated via maxims |
| Conventional? | Tied to specific lexical items | Arises from context/reasoning |
| Information status | Old/background/given | Can be new |
| Survives negation? | Yes | Often yes, but varies |
| Survives embedding? | Yes (projects) | Often does not project |

**The boundary cases:** Some phenomena straddle the presupposition/implicature boundary. For example, *"even"* and *"too"* have been analyzed as presupposition triggers by some theorists and as conventional implicature triggers by others (following Grice's notion of conventional implicature for words like "but" and "therefore"). The debate continues in the literature.

### 6.3 Presupposition vs. Assertion

**Assertion** is the act of putting forward a proposition as true -- it is the primary communicative purpose of a declarative sentence. The assertion is the "new information," the contribution the speaker is making to the discourse.

**Presupposition** is the background against which the assertion is made -- the "old information" or "given information" that frames the assertion.

**Comparison:**
- *"John STOPPED smoking."*
  - **Assertion:** John no longer smokes. (New information, the point of the utterance.)
  - **Presupposition:** John was smoking before. (Old information, taken for granted.)

- *"It was JOHN who broke the window."*
  - **Assertion:** The person who broke the window was John. (New information.)
  - **Presupposition:** Someone broke the window. (Background information.)

**Key properties of assertions vs. presuppositions:**
- Assertions are **challengeable**: the listener can say *"No, that's not true."*
- Presuppositions are **harder to challenge**: they must be explicitly identified and rejected.
- Assertions **add** to the common ground.
- Presuppositions are **assumed to already be** in the common ground.
- Assertions are **at issue**: they are the focus of the conversational exchange.
- Presuppositions are **not at issue**: they provide the frame, not the content being discussed.

**The manipulative asymmetry:** Because presuppositions are harder to challenge than assertions, information encoded in presuppositions can bypass critical scrutiny. If someone asserts *"John is lazy,"* you can directly disagree. But if someone says *"When are you going to deal with John's laziness?"* the claim that John is lazy is presupposed -- it's much harder to challenge because it's not the "topic" of the sentence. The presupposed content slips in through the "back door."

### 6.4 Presupposition vs. Conventional Implicature

**Conventional implicature** (Grice's term) refers to non-truth-conditional meaning that is conventionally associated with specific words, but which is not part of what is asserted:

- *"He is poor but honest."* -- "But" conventionally implicates that poverty and honesty are in contrast/unexpected together. This is not part of the truth conditions (the sentence is true if he is poor and honest, regardless of whether the contrast is real).

**How this relates to presupposition:**
- Both conventional implicatures and presuppositions are non-truth-conditional: they don't affect whether the sentence is true or false.
- Both are tied to specific lexical items (conventionalized).
- Both are non-cancellable.
- However, presuppositions are **backgrounded** (taken for granted), while conventional implicatures are **speaker-oriented** (they express the speaker's attitude or perspective).
- Presuppositions have a clear projection behavior (survive negation, questioning, etc.); conventional implicatures project too, but the theoretical treatment is different.

The boundary between presupposition and conventional implicature is debated, particularly for particles like "too," "also," "even," and "still."

## 7. The Hard/Soft Trigger Distinction

An important refinement in modern presupposition theory is the distinction between **hard** and **soft** presupposition triggers.

### 7.1 Hard Triggers

Hard triggers produce presuppositions that are very resistant to cancellation, even in embedded contexts. They are considered "genuinely semantic" presuppositions.

**Examples of hard triggers:**
- Cleft constructions: *"It was John who..."*
- Factive emotive verbs: *"regret," "be sorry that"*
- Definite descriptions: *"the X"*
- Change-of-state verbs: *"stop," "start"*

**Properties:**
- Presuppositions are strongly maintained across contexts.
- Violations of backgroundedness are judged as clearly deviant.
- Resistant to contextual override.

### 7.2 Soft Triggers

Soft triggers produce presuppositions that are more easily cancelled or overridden by context. They are sometimes argued to arise pragmatically rather than semantically.

**Examples of soft triggers:**
- Implicative verbs: *"manage," "fail"*
- Epistemic factive verbs in some contexts: *"know," "discover"*
- Focus-sensitive triggers

**Properties:**
- Presuppositions are context-dependent and defeasible.
- Can be cancelled without strong infelicity in certain discourse contexts.
- Experimental evidence shows they are processed differently: longer response times, less automatic activation.

### 7.3 Theoretical Implications

The hard/soft distinction challenges the idea that all presuppositions are the same kind of phenomenon. One school of thought (Abrusan 2011, 2016) argues the distinction can be derived from how different triggers interact with context -- their focus sensitivity, anaphoricity, and discourse congruence properties -- rather than from a fundamental split between two types of presupposition.

## 8. Dynamic Approaches to Presupposition

### 8.1 Context Change Potentials (Heim 1983)

A major advance in presupposition theory came from **dynamic semantics**, particularly Irene Heim's *File Change Semantics* (1983), developed simultaneously and independently with Hans Kamp's *Discourse Representation Theory*.

**Core idea:** The meaning of a sentence is not a truth value but a **Context Change Potential (CCP)** -- a function from contexts to contexts. A sentence is "instructions for how to update the current context."

**Application to presupposition:** A sentence's presuppositions are **definedness conditions** on its CCP. The CCP is defined (the update can proceed) only if the input context already satisfies the presupposition. If the presupposition is not met, the update is undefined -- this is presupposition failure.

### 8.2 Local Contexts

Following Karttunen (1973), Heim distinguishes between:
- **Global context:** The full common ground of the conversation.
- **Local context:** The context relative to which a particular embedded clause is evaluated.

In a conjunction "P and Q," the local context for Q is the global context updated with P. This explains filtering: if P entails the presupposition of Q, then the local context for Q already satisfies Q's presupposition, and it does not project to the global context.

## 9. Summary of Key Concepts

### Essential Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Presupposition** | A background assumption that must be true for an utterance to be felicitous |
| **Assertion** | The new information a sentence puts forward -- its main communicative purpose |
| **Entailment** | A logical consequence that follows from truth conditions (fails under negation) |
| **Implicature** | An inference arising from conversational context and maxims (cancellable) |
| **Common Ground** | The shared set of background assumptions in a conversation |
| **Accommodation** | The process by which listeners add unfamiliar presuppositions to the common ground |
| **Projection** | The behavior of presuppositions "surviving" embedding under operators |
| **Presupposition Trigger** | A word, phrase, or construction that generates a presupposition |
| **Presupposition Failure** | What happens when a presupposition turns out to be false |
| **Truth-Value Gap** | The Strawsonian view that presupposition failure renders a sentence neither true nor false |

### The Negation Test (Quick Reference)

To determine whether an inference is a presupposition or an entailment:

1. Take the original sentence and identify the inference.
2. Negate the sentence.
3. Check: does the inference still hold?
   - **YES → Presupposition**
   - **NO → Entailment**

### Complete Trigger Quick-Reference

| Trigger Type | Example Construction | What Is Presupposed |
|-------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Definite descriptions | "the X" | X exists |
| Factive verbs | "know/realize/regret that P" | P is true |
| Change-of-state verbs | "stop/start/continue V-ing" | Prior state existed |
| Iterative expressions | "again / return / re-" | Previous occurrence |
| Cleft constructions | "It was X who..." | Someone did the action |
| Temporal clauses | "before/after/while P" | P occurred |
| Comparatives | "more/better/as X as" | Basis of comparison is true |
| Counterfactuals | "If X had happened..." | X did NOT happen |
| Loaded questions | "Why did you X?" / "When did you stop X-ing?" | You did X / You were X-ing |
| Implicative verbs | "manage/fail/happen to" | Effort/expectation existed |
| Verbs of judging | "accuse/criticize/blame" | The action occurred |
| Additive particles | "too/also/even" | Others share the property |
| Non-restrictive relatives | "..., who/which..." | The relative clause content is true |
