# Validation: English Semantics — How English Encodes Meaning

## Validation Summary

The English Semantics research document is largely accurate with strong support for its core claims. The three-layer register system (Germanic/French/Latin), polysemy patterns, conceptual metaphor framework, collocational patterns, and construction grammar claims are all well-supported. However, there are a few claims that require nuance or correction: the OED vocabulary figure of ~171,476 refers specifically to the Second Edition (1989) and is now outdated (the current OED has 520,779+ entries); the claim about "set" having "430+ senses" is correct for the OED2 but "run" has since surpassed it with 645 senses; and the specific percentage breakdown of English etymology (29% Latin, 29% French, 26% Germanic) is a commonly cited approximation that varies by methodology.

## Claim-by-Claim Validation

### Claim 1: OED records approximately 171,476 words in current use, plus 47,000 obsolete words
- **Rating**: UNCERTAIN (6)
- **Evidence**: The 171,476 figure is accurate for the OED Second Edition (1989), which listed 171,476 words in current use and 47,156 obsolete words. However, this figure is significantly outdated. As of January 2026, the OED contains 520,779 entries and 888,251 meanings. The document should note that this is the OED2 figure, not the current total. The broader claim about English having "one of the largest vocabularies of any language" remains valid regardless of the exact count.
- **Correction needed**: The document should either specify "OED Second Edition (1989)" or update to the current figure of 520,779+ entries.
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia - Oxford English Dictionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary), [OED - About](https://languages.oup.com/research/oxford-english-dictionary/)

### Claim 2: "Set" has 430+ senses in the OED (most polysemous word); "run" has 645 uses
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED with nuance
- **Evidence**: "Set" had 430 senses listed in the OED Second Edition (1989), confirmed by Guinness World Records as the English word with the most meanings at that time. "Run" has since surpassed it with 645 senses for the verb form alone in the OED's third edition revision. The document accurately presents both figures. However, the document says "set" is "the most polysemous word" and separately notes "run" at 645 -- it should clarify that "run" has now overtaken "set" as the word with the most definitions.
- **Sources**: [NPR - Has 'Run' Run Amok?](https://www.npr.org/2011/05/30/136796448/has-run-run-amok-it-has-645-meanings-so-far), [Guinness World Records - English word with most meanings](https://www.guinnessworldrecords.com/world-records/english-word-with-the-most-meanings)

### Claim 3: The three-layer register system (Germanic everyday, French formal, Latin-Greek technical)
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: The three-layer lexical register system is well-documented in linguistic literature. The characterization (Germanic = everyday/informal, French = formal/institutional, Latin-Greek = technical/academic) is confirmed across multiple academic sources. The Nature article "German in childhood and Latin in adolescence" specifically documents the bidialectal nature of English lexical access along these etymological strata. The specific triplet examples (ask/question/interrogate, begin/commence/initiate, etc.) are standard illustrations found across multiple sources.
- **Sources**: [Nature - German in childhood and Latin in adolescence](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-021-00836-4), [ResearchGate - Developing register differentiation](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/339939313)

### Claim 4: Etymology distribution -- Latin ~29%, French ~29%, Germanic ~26%, Greek ~6%
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED with caveat
- **Evidence**: The figures 29% Latin, 29% French, 26% Germanic, 6% Greek are widely cited and appear in multiple sources including Wikipedia's article on foreign-language influences in English. However, these are approximations that vary depending on methodology (dictionary headword count vs. running text frequency). The document correctly notes the critical distinction: by TOKEN frequency, Germanic words dominate overwhelmingly (the 100 most frequent words are almost entirely Germanic). This dual perspective (type vs. token) is well-supported.
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia - Foreign-language influences in English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign-language_influences_in_English)

### Claim 5: The animal-meat split (cow/beef, pig/pork, sheep/mutton, calf/veal, deer/venison)
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: The animal-meat split is one of the most well-known examples of the Norman Conquest's effect on English vocabulary. The explanation (English-speaking peasants tended animals with Germanic names; French-speaking nobility ate the meat with French names) is a standard account in the history of English. All five pairs listed are accurate.
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia - List of English words of French origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_English_words_of_French_origin)

### Claim 6: Semantic prosody -- "cause" has ~90% negative collocates; "commit" strongly negative
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Corpus linguistics research extensively documents the negative semantic prosody of "cause." Research confirms that "cause" predominantly occurs in "contexts where cause and effect are unpleasant," with main collocates being "problems, trouble and damage, death, pain and disease." The claim about "commit" having strongly negative prosody (commit murder, fraud, suicide, crime, atrocities) is also well-documented. The specific "~90%" figure for "cause" is an approximation consistent with the research, though exact percentages vary by corpus.
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia - Semantic prosody](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semantic_prosody), [USC Dornsife - Semantic Prosody](https://dornsife.usc.edu/norbert-schwarz/wp-content/uploads/sites/231/2023/12/23_CDPS_Hauser_Schwarz_Semantic_prosody.pdf)

### Claim 7: Conceptual metaphors -- UNDERSTANDING IS SEEING, TIME IS MONEY, ARGUMENT IS WAR, etc.
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: These are the canonical examples from Lakoff and Johnson's conceptual metaphor theory (1980, "Metaphors We Live By"). The mappings listed (UNDERSTANDING IS SEEING, UNDERSTANDING IS GRASPING, IDEAS ARE OBJECTS, ARGUMENT IS WAR, TIME IS MONEY, MORE IS UP, GOOD IS UP, LIFE IS A JOURNEY, ANGER IS HEAT) are standard in the cognitive linguistics literature. The English examples provided for each metaphor are accurate.
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia - Conceptual metaphor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conceptual_metaphor), [Lakoff - Contemporary Theory of Metaphor](https://terpconnect.umd.edu/~israel/lakoff-ConTheorMetaphor.pdf)

### Claim 8: Construction Grammar -- constructions carry meaning independently of words (Goldberg 1995)
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Goldberg's 1995 "Constructions: A Construction Grammar Approach to Argument Structure" is accurately cited. The famous example "She sneezed the napkin off the table" is Goldberg's own canonical illustration of the caused-motion construction. The ditransitive construction ([Subject V Object1 Object2] = "X causes Y to receive Z") and the claim that constructions impose meaning beyond lexical semantics are core tenets of Construction Grammar. The resultative and way-construction examples are also standard.
- **Sources**: [University of Chicago Press - Constructions](https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/C/bo3683810.html), [ResearchGate - Goldberg's construction grammar](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/320856777)

### Claim 9: Collocations -- "strong tea" but not *"powerful tea"; "heavy rain" but not *"strong rain"
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: These are textbook examples of collocational restrictions in English. The partial semantic explanation (strong = intensity of quality, powerful = capacity for action) is standard in the literature. The claim that collocational knowledge is one of the last things L2 learners master is also well-supported.
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia - Collocation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collocation)

### Claim 10: Berlin & Kay (1969) -- English has 11 basic color terms
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Berlin and Kay's 1969 study "Basic Color Terms: Their Universality and Evolution" identified 11 basic color terms in English: black, white, red, green, yellow, blue, brown, orange, pink, purple, gray. The document correctly identifies this as matching the endpoint of the universal hierarchy. The claim that "this matches the endpoint of Berlin & Kay's (1969) universal color term hierarchy" is accurate.
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia - Basic Color Terms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basic_Color_Terms)

### Claim 11: Legal doublets and triplets -- "aid and abet," "will and testament," "cease and desist"
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Legal doublets (and triplets) in English are well-documented as a consequence of the bilingual Norman-English legal system. The examples listed (acknowledge and confess, aid and abet, breaking and entering, cease and desist, goods and chattels, have and hold, will and testament, null and void) are standard. The triplets (right, title, and interest; give, devise, and bequeath) are also accurately cited.
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia - Doublet (linguistics)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doublet_(linguistics))

### Claim 12: Active vocabulary of educated adult ~20,000-35,000 word families; passive ~40,000-70,000
- **Rating**: UNCERTAIN (7)
- **Evidence**: These ranges are commonly cited but vary significantly across studies depending on methodology (what counts as a "word family," how vocabulary is tested, etc.). Some studies put active vocabulary higher (up to 40,000 word families for well-read adults) and some put it lower. The ranges given are reasonable approximations but should be understood as rough estimates.
- **Sources**: Various lexical studies; the specific ranges are widely cited in EFL/ESL literature.

## Issues Requiring Correction

### Issue 1: OED vocabulary count is outdated
- **Severity**: Moderate
- **Current text**: "~171,476 words in current use"
- **Correction**: Should specify "OED Second Edition (1989)" or note that the current OED has expanded to 520,779+ entries. The 171,476 figure is historically accurate but may mislead readers into thinking it represents the current state of the dictionary.

### Issue 2: "Set" described as most polysemous vs. "run" at 645
- **Severity**: Minor
- **Current text**: The document lists "set" at 430+ senses as "the most polysemous word" and separately lists "run" at 645 senses
- **Correction**: The document should clarify that "run" has overtaken "set" as the word with the most OED definitions. "Set" held the record in the OED2 (1989) but "run" surpassed it in the OED3 revision with 645 senses for the verb alone.

## Overall Assessment

**Confidence: 8/10**

The English Semantics research is thorough and accurate on its core claims. The three-layer lexicon, conceptual metaphor framework, construction grammar, collocational patterns, and semantic prosody are all well-supported by independent research. The main deductions are for the outdated OED vocabulary count (which uses the 1989 figure without noting it has been superseded) and the slight inconsistency in identifying which word is "most polysemous." These are factual details that are easy to correct without affecting the document's analytical framework. The theoretical content -- radial category structure, conceptual metaphor mappings, constructional meaning, and register-etymology correlations -- is excellent.
