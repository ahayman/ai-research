# Turn-Taking in Conversation: Research Document

## Purpose
Comprehensive research on the turn-taking system in conversation for a knowledge base teaching English pragmatics to autistic individuals. This document covers the historical development of conversation analysis, the mechanical details of the turn-taking system, overlap and interruption, backchannels, silence, multi-party dynamics, and floor-management signals.

---

## 1. Historical Context: The Birth of Conversation Analysis

### The Founders

Three researchers fundamentally transformed the study of how people talk to each other. Their 1974 paper, "A Simplest Systematics for the Organization of Turn-Taking for Conversation," published in the journal *Language* (vol. 50, pp. 696-735), remains the single most cited article ever published in that journal -- the flagship publication of the Linguistic Society of America.

**Harvey Sacks (1935-1975)**
Born July 19, 1935. Educated at Columbia College (BA, 1955), Yale Law School (LLB, 1959), and UC Berkeley (PhD in Sociology, 1966). He studied under Harold Garfinkel, the founder of ethnomethodology, and also with Erving Goffman. Sacks lectured at UCLA and UC Irvine from 1964 until his death in a car accident on November 14, 1975, at age 40.

Despite his early death and limited publications during his lifetime, Sacks is recognized as the founder of conversation analysis. His major posthumous work, *Lectures on Conversation* (1992), comprises approximately 1,200 pages of edited transcriptions from lectures delivered between 1964 and 1972.

**The origin story:** Sacks became interested in the structure of conversation while working at the Center for the Scientific Study of Suicide in Los Angeles in the early 1960s. He began analyzing audio recordings of telephone calls to the Suicide Prevention Center -- calls by or about suicidal persons. He noticed a practical problem the center staff faced: callers would often not give their names, though the center needed names for follow-up. Sacks observed that very early in each conversation, there were systematic, recurrent patterns -- indications of possible difficulties in obtaining the caller's name. This seemingly mundane observation opened up an entirely new field: the study of how ordinary conversation is organized.

**Emanuel Schegloff (1937-2024)**
Born July 24, 1937, in Brooklyn, New York. Educated at Harvard (BA, 1958, under Talcott Parsons and Barrington Moore) and UC Berkeley (PhD, 1967, under Erving Goffman). He taught at Columbia University's Teachers College before moving to UCLA in 1972, where he became Distinguished Professor of Sociology.

Schegloff's 1968 paper "Sequencing in Conversational Openings" was the first published expression of conversation analysis -- a field later described as "perhaps the only completely new sociological research methodology developed in the United States since World War II." Over a career spanning six decades, Schegloff produced foundational work on turn-taking, sequence organization, repair, person reference, word selection, turn design, and the overall structural organization of interaction. His findings influenced anthropology, linguistics, psychology, cognitive science, communication studies, and found practical applications in medicine, neuroscience, and human-computer interaction. He died May 23, 2024, at age 86.

**Gail Jefferson (1938-2008)**
Born April 22, 1938, in Iowa City, Iowa. She received a BA in Dance from UCLA (1965) and a PhD in Social Sciences from UC Irvine (1972). Her work in conversation analysis began as coursework in a class taught by Harvey Sacks in spring 1965, and she subsequently did graduate work under his supervision.

Jefferson held positions at various universities, including a research position at the University of Manchester (1978-1981) and an honorary position at the University of York. She moved to the Netherlands in 1987 and remained there until her death on February 21, 2008, at age 69.

Jefferson is remembered for two major contributions: (1) co-founding conversation analysis with Sacks and Schegloff, and (2) developing the Jefferson Transcription System -- a detailed notation system for transcribing speech that captures not just words but pauses, overlaps, emphasis, breathing, laughter, and other features of talk. This system became the standard transcription method in conversation analysis worldwide. Her specific research focused on laughter in conversation and on moment-by-moment repair processes. During the last decade of her life, she worked on transcribing the Watergate tapes.

### What Was Conversation Analysis?

Conversation analysis (CA) emerged from ethnomethodology, a sociological approach developed by Harold Garfinkel beginning in the 1940s. Ethnomethodology studied the practical methods of common-sense reasoning that people use in everyday life -- how ordinary people produce and maintain social order without consciously thinking about it. Garfinkel drew on Talcott Parsons' theory of action and Alfred Schutz's phenomenological analysis of the "taken-for-granted" aspects of social life.

Sacks, Schegloff, and Jefferson took ethnomethodology's core insight -- that mundane social activities have a discoverable, orderly structure -- and applied it to the most mundane social activity of all: ordinary conversation. Their approach was radical in several ways:

1. **They used naturally occurring data.** Rather than experiments, surveys, or invented examples, they analyzed audio (and later video) recordings of real conversations as they actually happened.

2. **They treated conversation as a system.** Rather than studying individual utterances for their meaning, they studied how utterances were organized into sequences, how speakers coordinated their actions, and what structural mechanisms enabled this coordination.

3. **They discovered that conversation has rules.** Not rules imposed from outside, but systematic practices that participants orient to and use. The turn-taking system was the most fundamental of these.

4. **They showed that nothing in conversation is random.** Even seemingly trivial details -- the length of a pause, the placement of an "mm-hmm," a speaker's gaze direction -- could be shown to be systematic, meaningful, and consequential for how the interaction unfolds.

### How the 1974 Paper Revolutionized the Study of Talk

Before Sacks, Schegloff, and Jefferson, linguists studied language primarily as a system of grammar and semantics. Sociologists studied social structure through surveys and statistics. Neither discipline had a systematic way to study how people actually talk to each other in real time.

The 1974 paper proposed a model for the organization of turn-taking that was:
- **Locally managed:** Turn-taking is organized moment by moment, not planned in advance
- **Party-administered:** The participants themselves manage who speaks when, without an external moderator
- **Interactionally controlled:** The system operates through the interaction itself
- **Sensitive to recipient design:** Speakers shape their turns with specific recipients in mind

The paper identified **14 observable features** of ordinary conversation that any adequate model must account for. These include: speaker-change recurs; overwhelmingly, one party talks at a time; occurrences of more than one speaker at a time are common but brief; transitions from one turn to the next with no gap and no overlap are common, together with transitions characterized by slight gap or slight overlap; turn order is not fixed but varies; turn size is not fixed but varies; length of conversation is not fixed; what parties say is not specified in advance; the relative distribution of turns is not fixed; number of parties can change; talk can be continuous or discontinuous; turn-allocation techniques are used; various turn-constructional units are employed; and repair mechanisms exist for dealing with turn-taking errors and violations.

The paper went through extensive development, evolving from an initial draft of thirteen pages to a final draft of over one hundred pages, with changes in authorship and extended correspondence with the journal editor.

## 2. The Turn-Taking Mechanism in Detail

### 2.1 Turn Constructional Units (TCUs): What Counts as a "Turn"

A **turn constructional unit (TCU)** is the fundamental building block of a conversational turn. It is the smallest unit of speech that could constitute a complete turn -- that is, a point at which it would be appropriate for another speaker to begin talking.

The crucial insight is that turns are not just "sentences." A turn can be constructed from any of four types of unit:

**Lexical (single-word) TCU:**
- "Yes."
- "There."
- "No."
- "Tuesday."
- "Really?"

A single word can be a complete turn. When someone asks "When should we meet?" and you answer "Tuesday," that single word is a syntactically, prosodically, and pragmatically complete unit.

**Phrasal TCU:**
- "In the basket."
- "Out of here."
- "Around three o'clock."
- "The tall one."

A phrase -- not a full sentence, but a unit that makes sense in context and could constitute a complete response.

**Clausal TCU:**
- "When I am free."
- "If I got the job."
- "Because she told me."

A dependent clause that, in context, constitutes a complete action (e.g., answering a question).

**Sentential TCU:**
- "I am working on my thesis."
- "He has got my car."
- "We should probably leave now."

A full sentence, which is what many people imagine when they think of a "turn" -- but it is only one of four possible types.

**Key principle:** The type of TCU is not determined by grammar alone. What counts as a "complete" unit depends on the interaction -- on what action the speaker is performing and what the recipient needs in order to respond. A one-word answer to a question is complete; a one-word beginning of a story is not.

**Multi-unit turns:** A single turn can contain multiple TCUs. A speaker telling a story may produce many sentences in sequence. The turn-taking system handles this through mechanisms that allow a speaker to project that they are not yet done (discussed in Section 7 on floor-holding cues).

### 2.2 Transition Relevance Places (TRPs): Where Speaker Change Becomes Possible

A **transition relevance place (TRP)** is a point of possible completion of a TCU -- the moment where it would be legitimate for another speaker to begin. It is not the only point where speaker change *can* occur, but it is the point where the turn-taking system makes speaker change *relevant*.

**How do listeners know when a TRP is approaching?**

This is one of the most remarkable features of the system. Listeners don't wait for a speaker to finish and then start planning what to say. They *predict* where the turn will end, in advance, using multiple types of information simultaneously. Research by Levinson and Torreira (2015) showed that the average gap between turns in conversation is only about 200 milliseconds -- roughly the time it takes to pronounce a single syllable. But the cognitive processes required to plan and produce a response take *over 600 milliseconds*. This means listeners must begin planning their response while the current speaker is still talking.

Four types of cues help listeners project TRPs:

**1. Syntactic (grammatical) cues:**
Listeners track the grammatical structure of an utterance as it unfolds. If someone begins "I went to the store and bought...", a listener knows from grammar that an object noun is coming, and can project approximately when the sentence will be syntactically complete. English is an SVO language with relatively rigid word order, which makes syntactic projection especially useful.

**2. Prosodic (intonation/rhythm) cues:**
Falling pitch at the end of a declarative, rising pitch at the end of a question, final lengthening of the last syllable, decrease in volume -- these are prosodic signals that a turn is coming to completion. Research has shown that the vast majority of listeners' predictions about turn endings occur at or near intonational boundaries. Prosodic cues are particularly important in the final few hundred milliseconds of a turn.

**3. Pragmatic cues:**
Listeners assess whether the speaker's action is "pragmatically complete" -- whether enough has been said for the speaker's apparent communicative purpose. If someone has asked you a question and you've provided an answer, the action is pragmatically complete even if the sentence could grammatically continue.

**4. Visual/gestural cues:**
In face-to-face conversation, speakers' gaze direction, hand gestures, and body position all contribute to TRP projection. Kendon (1967) showed that speakers tend to gaze away at the beginning of their turns and look toward the listener as they approach the end -- this gaze shift signals the approaching TRP. Gestures that are in progress signal the turn is continuing; gestures that complete their trajectory signal the turn is ending.

**The Complex TRP (Ford & Thompson, 1996):**
Ford and Thompson introduced the concept of the "complex transition relevance place" -- the point where syntactic completion, prosodic completion, and pragmatic completion all converge simultaneously. When all three types of completion align, speaker change is most likely. When only one or two align, the current speaker is more likely to continue.

### 2.3 The Three-Part Turn-Allocation System

At each TRP, the turn-taking system applies a set of ordered rules to determine who speaks next. These rules apply in sequence -- later rules only apply if earlier ones have not been satisfied:

**Rule 1: Current speaker selects next speaker.**
The person currently speaking can select who should speak next. This is accomplished through:
- **Gaze:** Looking directly at a specific person as the turn nears completion
- **Naming:** Using someone's name ("What do you think, Sarah?")
- **Directing a question or action to a specific person:** Asking a question, making an accusation, or performing any action that makes a response from a particular person relevant
- **Gesture:** Pointing, nodding toward, or otherwise physically indicating the intended next speaker

When current speaker selects next, that selected person both *may* and *should* take the next turn. They have the right to speak, and there is a social expectation that they will.

**Rule 2: If no next speaker has been selected, any participant may self-select.**
If the current speaker's turn reaches a TRP and they have not selected a next speaker, any participant can begin speaking. In this case, the first person to start gains the right to the turn. This creates a competitive element: if multiple people want to speak, the one who starts first gets the floor. This is why "first starter" advantage matters -- and why people sometimes begin speaking at the very first moment a TRP becomes available.

**Rule 3: If no one self-selects, the current speaker may continue.**
If the current speaker finishes a TCU, has not selected anyone, and no one has self-selected, the current speaker may (but is not obligated to) continue with another TCU. This is recursive: after this next TCU reaches completion, the same set of rules applies again at the new TRP.

**These rules cycle:** At every TRP, the system resets and applies again. This means the turn-taking system is *locally managed* -- it operates on a turn-by-turn, moment-by-moment basis, not according to any predetermined plan.

### 2.4 How the System Minimizes Both Gaps and Overlaps

The system produces a striking empirical result: in ordinary conversation, the vast majority of turn transitions involve either no gap and no overlap, or only very brief gap or overlap.

**Empirical evidence from the landmark cross-linguistic study (Stivers, Enfield, et al., 2009):**
A team of researchers analyzed natural conversations in 10 typologically diverse languages from five continents. They found:
- The most common turn-transition times across all 10 languages fell between 0 and 200 milliseconds (a barely perceptible gap)
- The great majority of transitions fell between -100ms (slight overlap) and +500ms (brief gap)
- The cross-language average gap between question and response was +208 milliseconds
- All languages showed evidence of both overlap avoidance and silence minimization
- Variation between languages was quantitative (how long the average gap was), not qualitative (whether the system operated at all)

This pattern is explained by the turn-taking system itself:
- **Gaps are minimized** because the rules encourage prompt transition: at each TRP, either current speaker selects next (who should respond promptly), or self-selectors compete to start first
- **Overlaps are minimized** because the system provides for *one speaker at a time*: the rules allocate the turn to one speaker, not to multiple speakers simultaneously
- **When overlaps do occur**, they are typically brief because an overlap resolution device (Schegloff, 2000) leads speakers to quickly resolve who will continue

The functional basis for this tight timing: a timely response makes clear its connection to the prior speaker's utterance, showing how that utterance was understood and allowing rapid correction if necessary. Gaps and overlaps are not random noise -- they are meaningful deviations from the system's default operation.

## 3. Overlap vs. Interruption: The Critical Distinction

### 3.1 Defining the Terms

**Overlap:** Two (or more) speakers talking simultaneously. This is a structural description -- it refers to the observable phenomenon of simultaneous speech, regardless of intent or social meaning.

**Interruption:** One speaker cutting off another before a transition relevance place -- before the current speaker's turn has reached a possible completion point. This is a social and interactional description -- it carries implications about intent, power, and propriety.

The distinction matters enormously: overlap is common, often benign, and frequently cooperative. Interruption is socially marked and carries specific interactional meaning.

### 3.2 Types of Non-Problematic Overlap

Schegloff (2000), in his detailed analysis of overlapping talk, identified four categories of overlap that participants treat as unproblematic:

1. **Backchannel overlaps:** "Mm-hmm," "yeah," "right" produced while the speaker continues talking. These are listener responses, not turn-taking attempts.

2. **Terminal overlaps:** A next speaker begins at what they project as the very end of the current speaker's turn, slightly too early, resulting in a brief overlap with the last word or syllable. This represents accurate but slightly early prediction of the TRP.

3. **Choral productions:** Simultaneous laughter, greetings ("Hi!" "Hi!"), congratulations, or other actions that are properly done collectively.

4. **Conditional entry:** A listener offers a word or completion at a moment of word-search by the speaker (when the speaker visibly or audibly hesitates, looking for a word, and the listener supplies it).

### 3.3 Cooperative Overlap vs. Competitive Interruption

**Cooperative overlap** (term coined by Deborah Tannen, 1984):
A listener begins talking along with the speaker not to take the floor but to show enthusiastic engagement. Key features:
- Typically occurs at or near TRPs
- Often brief
- Does not attempt to change the topic
- Functions include: showing agreement, completing the speaker's thought, adding supporting evidence, matching the speaker's emotional intensity
- The original speaker typically continues unperturbed
- Both speakers are "working on a similar goal" -- bonding, problem-solving, or building shared understanding

Tannen associated cooperative overlap with a "high-involvement" conversational style, contrasting it with "high-considerateness" style (which favors longer pauses and avoidance of simultaneous speech). Among people who share a high-involvement style, cooperative overlap "greases rather than gums up the conversational wheels." When styles are mismatched, however, high-involvement speakers are perceived as rude and domineering, while high-considerateness speakers are perceived as cold or disengaged.

**Competitive interruption:**
A listener begins speaking before the current speaker's TRP in order to take the floor, change the topic, or prevent the current speaker from completing their point. Key features:
- Occurs away from TRPs (mid-utterance, mid-phrase)
- Speaker attempts to redirect or control the conversation
- The interrupted speaker is forced to stop or is talked over
- May be associated with power assertion, disagreement, or topic control
- The original speaker's contribution is cut short or abandoned

### 3.4 The Overlap Resolution Device

When overlap does occur, speakers deploy an organized set of practices to resolve it -- the **overlap resolution device** (Schegloff, 2000). This device operates independently of how the overlap started. Features include:
- Speakers may increase volume, speed, or pitch to "compete" for the floor
- One speaker typically drops out, yielding to the other
- The device pushes toward resolution -- a return to one-speaker-at-a-time -- regardless of who "should" have the floor

### 3.5 Power Dynamics and Interruption

Research consistently shows that interruption patterns reflect and reinforce social power hierarchies:
- People with higher perceived social status interrupt more often and are interrupted less
- Interruption can function as a dominance move -- a way to control the conversational floor and, by extension, the interaction
- The right to interrupt (and the expectation of not being interrupted) is asymmetrically distributed along lines of status, authority, and institutional role

### 3.6 Gender Research on Interruption Patterns

**The Zimmerman and West (1975) study:**
Don Zimmerman and Candace West conducted one of the first systematic studies of gender and interruption, examining same-sex and mixed-sex pairs in casual conversation. Their key finding: in mixed-gender pairs, men interrupted women far more than the reverse. They argued that interruptions function as conversational control devices, disproportionately used by men to assert dominance.

Their study made a precise distinction:
- **Overlap:** Slight over-anticipation by the next speaker -- beginning to speak at the very end of the current speaker's turn, overlapping the last word or part of it. Overlap is a "by-product" of the turn-taking system.
- **Interruption:** Starting to speak when there is no indication the current speaker was going to stop. This is a "purposeful violation" of the turn-taking system.

**Subsequent research and complications:**
- Meta-analyses show the gender effect on interruption is real but moderated by many factors: relationship type, setting, topic, relative status, cultural context
- Among peers of equal status, gender differences in interruption frequency are smaller or absent
- Context matters: men interrupt just as much as women in some peer settings
- However, in professional and institutional settings, the pattern of men interrupting women more frequently is robust
- West's later research on doctor-patient interactions found that gender could override professional status: female doctors were interrupted by male patients more than male doctors were
- The type of interruption matters: women may use more "supportive" or cooperative interruptions, while men may use more "competitive" or floor-taking interruptions

**Key nuance:** Whether simultaneous speech is perceived as cooperative overlap or rude interruption depends heavily on the relationship between speakers and the social context. The same behavior may be welcomed between close friends but perceived as aggression between strangers or across a power differential.

## 4. Backchannels and Listener Responses

### 4.1 What Are Backchannels?

The term "backchannel" was coined by Victor Yngve in 1970. He defined it as "the back channel, over which the person who has the turn receives short messages such as 'yes' and 'uh-huh' without relinquishing the turn."

Backchannels are the sounds and words a listener produces while someone else is talking. They are NOT turn-taking attempts. They operate on a separate "channel" from the primary speaking turn.

### 4.2 Common English Backchannels

**Verbal backchannels:**
- "Mm-hmm" / "Mm" / "Mhm"
- "Uh-huh"
- "Yeah"
- "Right"
- "OK"
- "I see"
- "Sure"
- "Really?"
- "Wow"
- "Oh"
- Brief laughter
- Sighing or other vocalizations

**Non-verbal backchannels:**
- Head nodding
- Eyebrow raising
- Facial expressions (smiling, frowning, widening eyes)
- Forward body lean
- Eye contact maintenance

**Variation within verbal backchannels:**
Research shows that specific backchannel choices correlate with conversational dynamics. "Mm-hm" and "uh-huh" are used more during lively, animated interactions. "OK" and "yeah" are used more during calmer, less animated exchanges. English allows reduplication of syllables in non-lexical backchannels (uh-huh, mm-hm, um-hm), as well as single-syllable forms (mm, hm).

### 4.3 What Backchannels Do

Backchannels serve multiple functions simultaneously:

1. **Continuers:** They signal that the listener understands the current speaker has not finished and consents to their continuation. "Mm-hmm" during a story means "I'm following, keep going."

2. **Attention signals:** They demonstrate that the listener is paying attention to what is being said.

3. **Understanding displays:** They show the listener is tracking the content. "Right" or "I see" signals comprehension.

4. **Agreement/alignment signals:** "Yeah" or nodding during an argument signals alignment with the speaker's position.

5. **Encouragement:** They encourage the speaker to continue, especially during narratives or extended explanations.

6. **Repair waivers:** By producing a backchannel, the listener signals that they have no problem with what was just said -- they are waiving their opportunity to initiate repair (ask for clarification, signal misunderstanding, or disagree).

**Critical distinction:** Backchannels are *phatic* expressions -- they primarily serve a social/relational function rather than conveying new information. They maintain the interactive channel rather than transmitting content through it.

### 4.4 When Backchannels Occur

Backchannels are not randomly distributed. Research shows they are coordinated with the speaker's behavior:
- They often occur at or near syntactic boundaries in the speaker's turn
- They are frequently triggered by the speaker's gaze: Bavelas et al. (2002) found that listeners tend to produce backchannels during moments of mutual gaze -- when the speaker looks at them. The speaker tends to look away soon after the backchannel, creating a rhythmic "gaze window" that coordinates backchannel production.
- They occur after information that is particularly new, surprising, or emotionally charged

### 4.5 The Meaning of Backchannel Absence

**This is one of the most important points about backchannels: their absence is as meaningful as their presence.**

When a listener stops providing backchannels, it communicates:
- **Disengagement:** The listener is no longer paying attention
- **Disagreement:** The listener does not align with what is being said
- **Confusion:** The listener has lost the thread of the conversation
- **Disapproval:** The listener disapproves of the content or the speaker's behavior
- **Discomfort:** The listener is uncomfortable with the topic

The absence of any encouraging response, even the minimal backchannel, signals to the speaker that something has gone wrong. Speakers are highly attuned to this absence -- when backchannels stop, speakers often pause, check in ("Are you still there?" / "Does that make sense?"), or modify their talk.

When a listener's participation is absent, a conversation ceases to be a conversation and becomes a monologue. This highlights how fundamental listener responsiveness is to the interactive nature of talk.

## 5. Silence and Pauses

### 5.1 Types of Silence in Conversation

Conversation analysts distinguish three types of silence, based on their position within the turn-taking system:

**Pause:** A silence within a single speaker's turn. The speaker has not reached a TRP; they are in the middle of their TCU. This might indicate thinking, word-searching, or emotional processing. The turn still "belongs" to the current speaker.

**Gap:** A silence between turns -- after one speaker has reached a TRP and before the next speaker begins. A gap is what happens when speaker change is relevant but has not yet occurred.

**Lapse:** A silence that occurs when no sequence or structured activity is in progress -- a moment when no one is specifically expected to speak. Lapses can occur between topics or during transitions in the activity.

### 5.2 How Long Is a "Normal" Pause in English?

Research provides precise measurements:

- **Typical turn transitions:** The most common gap between turns in English conversation is approximately 200-300 milliseconds -- about a quarter of a second. This is roughly the time it takes to inhale.
- **Cross-linguistic average:** Stivers, Enfield, et al. (2009) found a cross-language mean gap of about 208 milliseconds across 10 languages.
- **Short pauses dominate:** Inter-turn silences tend to be short (less than 1 second), with a large proportion being less than half a second.
- **Very brief silences:** A PLOS ONE study found that over 90% of silence intervals in English speech are shorter than 0.1 seconds.
- **The perception threshold:** Negative perceptions often emerge after just 600 milliseconds to 1 second of silence. At this point, the silence begins to feel "long" or "awkward."
- **The 0.7-second benchmark:** In conversation analysis, a silence of 0.7 seconds is often cited as a notable gap. This is at the longer end of typical inter-turn silence and approaches the threshold where it begins to carry interactional meaning (see below).

### 5.3 What Silence Communicates

Silence in conversation is never simply "nothing happening." The turn-taking system makes silence meaningful because it creates expectations about *when* things should happen:

**Silence as thinking/processing:**
Brief pauses (under ~500ms) within a turn typically indicate normal cognitive processing -- the speaker is formulating the next part of their utterance. These are generally unmarked and unproblematic.

**Silence as a signal of upcoming disagreement or refusal:**
This is one of the most robust findings in conversation analysis. When a response is delayed, it signals that a **dispreferred response** is coming. Research on preference organization shows:

- Preferred responses (accepting an invitation, agreeing with an assessment, granting a request) are delivered quickly, without delay or mitigation. Median response time: ~269 milliseconds.
- Dispreferred responses (declining an invitation, disagreeing, refusing a request) are systematically delayed. Median response time: ~561 milliseconds.
- Recipients are sensitive to this pattern: they expect preferred responses after short gaps and dispreferred responses after longer gaps (roughly after 300ms).
- Dispreferred responses are additionally marked by audible breathing, prefacing particles like "well" or "uh," appreciations ("That's really kind of you, but..."), apologies ("I'm sorry, but..."), and accounts/justifications.

**The predictive power of "well":** Research shows that 72% of dispreferred responses to yes/no questions were prefaced with "well," while 74% of preferred responses were NOT prefaced with "well." When a response begins with turn-initial "well," the probability that it will be a dispreferred action is 0.78.

**Silence as discomfort:**
Extended silence in contexts where speech is expected creates social discomfort for most English speakers. This discomfort is itself interactionally consequential -- it motivates speakers to fill the silence, often with concessions or additional information they might not otherwise have provided.

### 5.4 Cultural Variation in Silence Tolerance

Different cultures have markedly different norms for silence in conversation:

- **English-speaking cultures (US, UK, Australia):** Relatively low tolerance for silence. Gaps beyond about 1 second are often perceived as awkward or meaningful.
- **Finnish culture:** Silence is valued as a sign of thoughtfulness and good listening. Less speaking and more thinking signals engagement rather than disengagement.
- **Japanese culture:** Silence is valued as a sign of respect and contemplation. However, a counterintuitive finding from cross-linguistic research: despite the cultural association of Japanese conversation with silence, Japanese speakers in the Stivers et al. (2009) study were actually the *fastest* to respond of all 10 languages tested (mean transition time of ~7ms). The cultural reputation for silence may relate more to specific contexts (formal, hierarchical situations) than to ordinary conversation.
- **Nordic cultures (Finland, Norway, Sweden):** Practice "the art of listening" -- silence signals thoughtful engagement.
- **Danish conversation:** Showed the *slowest* average response times in the Stivers et al. study (~469ms).

**Key finding:** Despite cultural variation in *attitudes* toward silence, the underlying turn-taking system appears to be universal. All languages tested show evidence of both overlap avoidance and silence minimization. Cultural variation exists in the *degree* of gap tolerance, not in the *existence* of the turn-taking system.

### 5.5 The 0.7-Second Threshold

The figure of 0.7 seconds appears frequently in conversation analysis literature as a boundary. A gap of 0.7 seconds occurs during a turn transition when no new speaker has been selected by the previous speaker, and it typically lasts only until a new speaker takes the floor.

At ~0.7 seconds, a gap is long enough to:
- Be clearly perceptible to all participants
- Signal that something is "off" -- that the expected response has not come
- Trigger the current speaker to add more talk (self-selecting under Rule 3)
- Signal to the recipient that their silence is being noticed

Beyond 0.7-1.0 seconds, silence increasingly communicates disagreement, reluctance, confusion, or some other interactional trouble.

## 6. Turn-Taking in Multi-Party Conversation

### 6.1 How Turn Allocation Changes with 3+ Participants

The Sacks, Schegloff, and Jefferson turn-taking model was designed primarily around dyadic (two-person) conversation, but its principles extend to multi-party interaction with important modifications.

**In dyadic conversation:** Turn allocation is structurally simple. If the current speaker selects next, it can only be the other person. If no one is selected, there is only one other person who can self-select. These structural constraints eliminate much ambiguity.

**In triadic (three-party) conversation:** The system becomes more complex. When the current speaker selects next, they must somehow differentiate between two possible recipients. When self-selection operates, two people might compete to speak first. The structural constraints that simplify dyadic interaction are relaxed.

**In larger groups:** Turn allocation faces even greater challenges:
- Multiple participants are potential next speakers
- The current speaker must use more explicit techniques to select a specific next speaker (naming, gaze direction, question design)
- Self-selection becomes more competitive, as more participants may want to speak
- The one-at-a-time norm comes under greater pressure

### 6.2 Gaze as a Turn-Allocation Tool in Groups

In multi-party settings, gaze becomes a primary turn-allocation mechanism (Lerner, 2003; Auer, 2018, 2021):

- **Gazing at a specific co-participant when approaching a TRP** is "very frequently used as a current-selects-next technique" in multi-party face-to-face interaction
- There is an important distinction between **addressee selection** (who the talk is directed to) and **next-speaker selection** (who should speak next). In multi-party conversation, a speaker may address everyone but select only one person as next speaker through gaze.
- Listeners in multi-party conversation track the speaker's gaze to determine whether they are being selected as next speaker

### 6.3 The Schism Phenomenon

When a group of four or more people is conversing, the conversation may **schism** -- split into two or more separate sub-conversations running simultaneously. This is a systematic, recognizable phenomenon, not a breakdown in the system.

Key features of schisming:
- It is a "methodical accomplishment" -- participants orient to it and produce it in orderly ways
- A single mechanism -- the dynamics of conversational fitness as related to group size -- controls when schisming occurs
- Certain types of turn (called "toss-out" turns) often result in schisming: these are turns that are not clearly directed to any specific participant and that fail to secure uptake from the group as a whole
- Schisming typically occurs when a conversational group grows large enough that not all members can meaningfully participate in a single thread
- Within each resulting sub-conversation, the standard turn-taking system operates normally

### 6.4 Managing Turn-Taking in Larger Groups

Several strategies help manage turn-taking in multi-party settings:

- **Addressing by name:** Becomes more necessary as group size increases, since gaze alone may be ambiguous
- **Self-selection competition:** Multiple speakers may start simultaneously at a TRP, requiring overlap resolution
- **Pre-allocation:** In some institutional settings (meetings, classrooms), turn-taking is pre-allocated by a designated moderator, reducing the competitive self-selection problem
- **Physical positioning:** Participants may orient their bodies to create sub-groups or signal who they are addressing
- **Turn-constructional design:** Speakers may design their turns to make it clear who is being addressed (e.g., "Sarah, what do you think about that?")

## 7. Turn-Taking Signals: How Speakers Signal Their Intentions

### 7.1 Floor-Yielding Cues (Signals That the Speaker Is Ready to Give Up the Turn)

When a speaker approaches the end of their turn and wants to transfer the floor, they produce combinations of the following cues:

**Prosodic cues:**
- **Falling intonation** at the end of a declarative statement (the pitch drops)
- **Rising intonation** at the end of a question (the pitch rises, signaling that a response is now due)
- **Final lengthening:** The last syllable of the turn is stretched slightly
- **Decrease in volume:** The speaker's voice gets quieter as the turn approaches completion -- speakers often "trail off"
- **Decrease in tempo:** Speech slows down toward the end

**Syntactic cues:**
- **Syntactic completion:** The sentence reaches a point where it is grammatically complete
- **Tag questions:** "...right?" / "...don't you think?" / "...you know?" -- these add a syntactic element that explicitly invites response

**Visual cues:**
- **Gaze shift to the listener:** Kendon (1967) showed that speakers tend to look away from listeners while speaking but shift their gaze back to the listener as they approach the end of their turn. This gaze shift is one of the strongest floor-yielding cues.
- **Gesture completion:** If the speaker has been gesturing, the gesture returns to a resting position
- **Body relaxation:** The speaker's posture may shift from "active speaker" orientation to a more relaxed position

**Pragmatic cues:**
- **Action completion:** The speaker has done what they set out to do -- asked a question, made a point, finished a story
- **Explicit invitations to respond:** "What do you think?" / "So..." / trailing off expectantly

### 7.2 Floor-Holding Cues (Signals That the Speaker Wants to Continue)

When a speaker reaches a TRP but wants to continue rather than yield the floor, they deploy techniques to prevent speaker change. These are crucial for producing multi-unit turns (stories, explanations, arguments):

**Filled pauses ("uh" and "um"):**
Clark and Fox Tree (2002) showed that these are not mere verbal tics but communicative signals:
- **"Uh"** signals an expected *minor* delay in speaking. It is more self-directive and inward-oriented.
- **"Um"** signals an expected *major* delay. It is more communicative and listener-oriented.
- Both can function as floor-holding devices -- by producing "um," a speaker signals "I am not done; I am planning what to say next." However, Clark and Fox Tree argue these are not *primarily* floor-holding devices (people use them in monologues too, where there is no floor to hold); rather, they announce upcoming delays, and listeners interpret the announcement as a signal to wait.

**Gaze aversion:**
Speakers tend to look away from listeners at the beginning of a new TCU and when they want to hold the floor. By avoiding the listener's gaze, the speaker avoids triggering the gaze-based next-speaker selection mechanism.

**Rush-throughs:**
A speaker approaching a TRP speeds up at the end of one TCU and latches immediately into the next, leaving no gap in which another speaker could self-select. The prosodic acceleration signals "I'm continuing."

**Latching:**
Contiguous talk that eliminates the usual "beat of silence" between TCUs. The speaker produces the next unit so quickly that it is attached to the prior unit with no gap. Latching and rush-through designs "minimize potential interruption to, or derailing of, talk through suspending the regular turn taking." They feature detailed phonetic design including pitch, intensity, and speaking rate.

**Sound stretching:**
Elongating a vowel sound ("I was thinking that theeeee... the best option is...") keeps sound flowing through a point where silence might invite a transition.

**Self-interruption and restart:**
Breaking off one syntactic structure and starting another ("I went to the -- actually what happened was...") resets the syntactic projection, pushing the TRP further into the future.

**Phrasal coordination ("and," "but," "so"):**
Starting a new clause with a conjunction projects syntactic continuation, signaling that the turn is not complete.

**Listing/enumeration:**
Beginning a list ("There are three reasons. First,...") projects a multi-component turn, signaling to listeners that the speaker will need the floor for an extended period.

**Pre-announcements and story prefaces:**
"Let me tell you what happened" or "I have to tell you something" -- these announce that an extended turn is coming, securing the floor in advance.

### 7.3 Floor-Seeking Cues (Signals That a Listener Wants to Speak)

When a listener wants to take the floor, they produce signals that can be detected by the current speaker and other participants:

**Audible in-breath:**
Research shows that audible inhalation can signal an intention to speak. Speakers make their inbreaths perceptually prominent by increasing loudness, signaling readiness to take the floor. Pre-turn inbreaths typically begin briefly after the end of the current speaker's turn unit. The presence of a pre-answer inbreath co-occurs with substantially delayed answers (modal latency of 576ms vs. 100ms for answers without a pre-answer inbreath).

**Body lean forward:**
Physical movement toward the center of the interaction space signals engagement and readiness to speak.

**Raised hand or finger:**
A physical gesture indicating the desire to speak. More common in institutional settings (classrooms, meetings) but also occurs in casual conversation as a subtle finger raise or hand movement.

**Repeated eye contact attempts:**
A listener who wants to speak will repeatedly try to catch the speaker's eye and maintain eye contact. This is an attempt to activate the gaze-based next-speaker selection mechanism.

**Increased backchannel frequency:**
Paradoxically, a listener who wants to speak may increase their backchannel production -- giving more "yeah"s, "mm-hmm"s, and nods. This signals heightened engagement and can function as a pre-turn-taking display.

**False starts:**
Brief, aborted attempts to begin speaking -- starting a word and then pulling back. These signal desire to speak without fully committing to a turn-taking attempt.

**Pre-beginning elements:**
- "Well..." -- signals that a substantive turn is about to follow
- "So..." -- can signal a desire to introduce a new topic or return to a prior one
- "I mean..." -- can preface a new turn
- Throat clearing or other preparatory sounds

**Overlap at TRP:**
Beginning to speak at the very first moment a TRP becomes available -- even if this creates brief overlap with the current speaker's final syllables. This is the most direct floor-seeking technique and represents legitimate self-selection under Rule 2 of the turn-allocation system.

## 8. Additional Key Concepts

### 8.1 Adjacency Pairs

The turn-taking system operates in conjunction with **adjacency pairs** -- paired actions where the first action (first pair part) makes a specific second action (second pair part) conditionally relevant:

- Question -> Answer
- Greeting -> Greeting
- Request -> Acceptance/Refusal
- Invitation -> Acceptance/Decline
- Compliment -> Thanks/Downplay
- Accusation -> Denial/Admission

Adjacency pairs create expectations. When someone asks a question, an answer is not just possible but *expected*. The absence of the expected second pair part is noticeable and meaningful -- it is accountable. This is why silence after a question feels different from silence during a lapse: the question creates an obligation that makes the silence interpretable as reluctance, disagreement, or evasion.

### 8.2 Preference Organization

Adjacency pairs typically have **preferred** and **dispreferred** second pair parts:

| First Pair Part | Preferred Response | Dispreferred Response |
|---|---|---|
| Invitation | Accept | Decline |
| Request | Grant | Refuse |
| Assessment | Agree | Disagree |
| Offer | Accept | Decline |
| Proposal | Accept | Reject |
| Compliment | Accept/Downplay | Reject |

**"Preferred" does not mean "personally desired."** It means structurally unmarked -- delivered quickly, without delay, qualification, or account. A "dispreferred" response is structurally marked -- delivered with delay, hedging, accounts, and mitigation.

The turn shapes are predictable:
- **Preferred response:** Quick onset, no delay, no hedging, direct. "Would you like to come to dinner?" -> "Sure, I'd love to!"
- **Dispreferred response:** Delayed onset, prefaced with "well" or "uh," includes appreciation or apology, provides an account. "Would you like to come to dinner?" -> (pause) "Well... that's really nice of you, but I actually have plans that evening, I'm sorry."

This pattern is universal across languages and is directly tied to the turn-taking system: the delay and mitigation in dispreferred responses occupy turn space, extending the gap between the first and second pair parts, and providing advance warning to the questioner that a non-desired answer is coming.

### 8.3 Repair

The turn-taking system includes built-in **repair mechanisms** for dealing with problems in speaking, hearing, and understanding. Schegloff, Jefferson, and Sacks (1977) described an "organization of repair" with a strong preference for self-repair over other-repair:

- **Self-initiated self-repair:** The speaker identifies and corrects their own problem ("I went to the store -- I mean the bank")
- **Other-initiated self-repair:** The listener signals a problem ("Huh?" "What do you mean?"), and the original speaker provides the repair
- **Self-initiated other-repair:** The speaker signals they need help ("What's the word for...?"), and the listener provides it
- **Other-initiated other-repair:** The listener both identifies and corrects the problem ("You mean Tuesday, not Thursday")

The preference hierarchy places self-repair before other-repair. The structural positions for self-repair (within the current turn, in the transition space, in the third turn) precede the positions for other-repair (in the next turn), giving speakers the first opportunity to fix their own problems. This preference reflects a broader social norm: correcting someone else is a face-threatening act, so the system provides maximum opportunity for the speaker to correct themselves before someone else does.

### 8.4 Turn-Taking Timing and Cognitive Processing

The Levinson and Torreira (2015) model resolves a cognitive puzzle: if language production takes over 600ms but turn gaps average only 200ms, how do speakers respond so quickly?

The answer: **parallel processing.** While listening to the current speaker, the next speaker simultaneously:
1. **Identifies the speech act** -- what kind of action the current speaker is performing (asking a question, making a request, telling a story)
2. **Grasps the gist** -- understands the content well enough to formulate a response
3. **Begins planning their response** -- selecting words, building syntactic structure, preparing articulation
4. **Monitors for turn-end** -- tracking the current speaker's syntax, prosody, and pragmatics for signals that the turn is about to conclude
5. **Launches their planned response** when the turn-end is imminent

This means speakers begin planning their response *before* the current speaker has finished -- sometimes well before. The "head start" in production planning is what allows the remarkably short gaps that characterize conversation.

This has implications for understanding conversational difficulty: anyone who processes language more slowly, who has difficulty with parallel processing, or who needs more time to formulate responses will find the standard 200ms transition window challenging. The system is designed for extremely rapid alternation, and any additional processing time will produce longer-than-typical gaps.

### 8.5 Autistic Turn-Taking: Research Findings

Research on turn-taking in autistic individuals provides important context for a pragmatics knowledge base:

- **Limited evidence suggests longer gaps** in conversations involving autistic participants, though recent analysis found no clear overall difference between autistic and non-autistic groups. Both groups showed the same *preference* for very short silent-gap transitions.
- **Longer gaps in initial stages:** Autistic dyads produced longer gaps specifically in the earliest stages of dialogue, suggesting that the challenge may be related to establishing conversational rhythm rather than a fundamental difference in the turn-taking system.
- **Relationship to autism severity:** Studies have found a relationship between autism severity and prolonged turn-taking gaps for both autistic children and adults, suggesting language processing speed may play a role.
- **Cooperative overlap is common:** Research indicates that approximately 80% of autistic individuals use cooperative overlap -- enthusiastic "interrupting" that signals engagement and connection rather than floor-taking. When autistic speakers interrupt in this manner, something said has triggered a thought; these interruptions are signs of connections and rapport, viewed by autistic speakers as encouragement and agreement.
- **Autistic conversation between autistic peers** may be experienced differently: "It's easier to converse with other neurodivergent people because you interrupt each other all the time and it's absolutely fine." Research on neurodivergent intersubjectivity identifies features like a generous assumption of common ground that leads to rapid rapport, and a low demand for coordination.
- **Important framing:** Autistic dialogue strategies should not simply be considered "wrong" or "inappropriate." They may diverge from non-autistic peers in ways that are most appropriate and functional for autistic individuals. The conversation skills that are characteristically challenging for autistic children are also challenging for many neurotypical children, apparently for similar reasons.
