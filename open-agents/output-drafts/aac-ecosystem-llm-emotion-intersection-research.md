# TOPIC 1: Ecosystem & Infrastructure

*Companion apps, parent/teacher portals, SLP tools, school management, cloud services*

## 1A. Intersection with Communication Struggles Taxonomy

### Domain 1: Receptive Struggles (Understanding Incoming Communication)

**Intersection:** An ecosystem approach addresses receptive struggles by ensuring that communication partners across ALL environments have access to the same strategies and supports for helping users understand incoming communication.

- **Parent/teacher portals** can share "communication passports" that document how a specific user best receives information (e.g., "Use 3-word sentences," "Pair speech with visuals," "Allow 10-second processing time"). Currently, this information lives in the SLP's head or in scattered IEP documents and rarely transfers consistently across environments.
- **Cloud-synced visual supports** ensure that the same visual schedules, social stories, and simplified-language aids are available at home, school, therapy, and in the community. Research shows AAC devices must be available across settings per IEP mandate, yet school-home coordination remains a primary barrier (ASHA Leader, 2025; SLP NerdCast).
- **Communication partner training modules** embedded in companion apps can teach parents, teachers, and aides how to modify their own language for users with receptive processing challenges. Research from 2025 confirms that asynchronous online training is effective for caregivers of children who use AAC (Tandfonline, 2025), and the POWR method (Prepare, Offer, Wait, Respond) is gaining adoption.

**Gap addressed:** No current AAC app provides a structured ecosystem for ensuring receptive support strategies are consistently applied across environments.

### Domain 2: Expressive Struggles (Motor Speech, Language Formulation, Emotional Expression)

**Intersection:** Ecosystem infrastructure supports expressive communication by ensuring:

- **Vocabulary consistency across environments:** SLP tools that push curated vocabulary sets to both school and home devices, so the user doesn't face different symbol layouts in different settings. Research identifies vocabulary inconsistency between environments as a key abandonment factor.
- **SLP remote configuration:** Telepractice integration allows SLPs to modify AAC layouts, add vocabulary, and adjust settings remotely without requiring in-person visits. This is critical for rural areas and for the 115+ student caseloads common in school settings (ASHA, 2025 state caseload guidance).
- **Data logging and sharing:** Automatic tracking of which vocabulary is used, how frequently, and in what contexts, shared across the care team. Current AAC data collection capabilities exist but are underutilized (PrAACtical AAC; AAC Community evaluation tools).
- **Voice banking coordination** for progressive conditions: An ecosystem that prompts users to bank their voice at the right time (while speech is still intelligible) and integrates the banked voice seamlessly across all devices. Currently, 80% of eligible patients do NOT bank their voice (existing research).

**Gap addressed:** No AAC product offers an integrated SLP dashboard with remote configuration, usage analytics, and care team coordination.

### Domain 3: Interactive/Pragmatic Struggles (Conversation, Social Cognition)

**Intersection:** Ecosystem tools can address pragmatic struggles by connecting communication partners:

- **"I'm composing" companion app feature:** A companion app for conversation partners that signals when the AAC user is preparing a response, reducing social pressure and awkward silences. This addresses the fundamental timing problem (8-15 WPM vs. 150-250 WPM).
- **Social script sharing:** SLPs, teachers, and parents can collaboratively create and share social scripts for specific recurring situations (ordering food, answering teacher questions, playground interactions), synced to the user's device.
- **Peer interaction tools:** Companion apps that help neurotypical peers understand AAC, reducing stigma and improving interaction quality. Schools report that a culture of AAC acceptance accelerates adoption (ASHA AJSLP, 2023).
- **Conversational context sharing:** When a user transitions between environments (school to home), the system can share a conversational summary ("Today we talked about volcanoes in science class") so parents can continue the conversation, reducing the re-initiation burden.

**Gap addressed:** Zero current AAC products offer companion apps for communication partners that facilitate real-time conversational coordination.

### Domain 4: Physical/Motor Access Barriers

**Intersection:** Ecosystem infrastructure supports motor access through:

- **Multi-device sync:** The same AAC configuration works across tablet, phone, dedicated device, and computer, allowing seamless transitions. When a powered wheelchair-mounted device is unavailable, the user can access the same vocabulary on a phone.
- **Smart home integration:** For users with severe motor impairment (ALS, locked-in, MD/SMA), the AAC ecosystem extends to environmental control -- lights, doors, thermostats, entertainment. An IoT-based assistive system for SMA patients demonstrated practical daily activity improvements (Journal of Neuroengineering and Rehabilitation, 2024).
- **Switch/access method profiles synced across devices:** A user's specific switch configuration (timing, sensitivity, scanning patterns) follows them across all devices in the ecosystem.
- **Progressive adaptation management:** For ALS/MND, the ecosystem tracks declining motor function and prepares the next access method transition BEFORE the current one fails. The SLP and care team receive alerts about declining performance metrics.

**Gap addressed:** No AAC ecosystem offers proactive access method transition planning for progressive conditions.

### Domain 5: Cognitive-Communication Barriers (Attention, Memory, Executive Function)

**Intersection:** Ecosystem infrastructure reduces cognitive load by:

- **Simplified, consistent interfaces:** When the same layout appears everywhere, users with memory deficits don't have to re-learn the system in each environment.
- **Caregiver-managed complexity:** Parent/teacher portals allow non-technical caregivers to manage the cognitive complexity of the AAC interface without requiring SLP visits. SLPs set guardrails; caregivers make day-to-day adjustments.
- **External memory support:** Cloud-synced communication logs serve as memory aids. For dementia, caregivers can review what was communicated earlier; for TBI, users can review their own conversation history.
- **Routine and schedule integration:** The AAC ecosystem connects to the user's daily schedule (school timetable, therapy schedule, meal times) and surfaces relevant vocabulary automatically.

**Gap addressed:** No current AAC system integrates with external scheduling systems to reduce the cognitive load of vocabulary navigation.

### Domain 6: Psychosocial Barriers (Confidence, Identity, Stigma)

**Intersection:** The ecosystem approach has profound psychosocial implications:

- **Family engagement reduces isolation:** Parent portals that show a child's communication progress create a positive feedback loop. Research consistently shows that family buy-in is the single strongest predictor of AAC success, yet schools report limited time to communicate with families (SLP caseloads of 115+).
- **Peer inclusion tools:** Companion apps that help classmates interact with AAC users normalize the technology and reduce stigma. Research shows a school culture endorsing AAC accelerates adoption (ASHA AJSLP, 2023).
- **Progress visualization:** Dashboards that show communication growth over time help users, families, and educators see the value of AAC, countering the "this isn't working" narrative that drives the 30-50% abandonment rate.
- **Community connection:** Cloud-based features that connect AAC user families (with privacy controls) reduce the profound isolation families report.
- **Identity preservation:** For progressive conditions, the ecosystem preserves voice recordings, personal vocabulary, communication style, and interaction history -- maintaining identity even as physical abilities decline.

**Gap addressed:** No AAC product offers family-facing progress dashboards or peer inclusion companion tools.

### Domain 7: Rate/Efficiency Barriers

**Intersection:** Ecosystem infrastructure improves rate through:

- **Cross-environment learning:** Prediction models that learn from usage across ALL environments produce better predictions than single-context models. Cloud sync enables this.
- **SLP-optimized vocabulary:** Remote analytics let SLPs identify rate bottlenecks (e.g., "User takes 45 seconds to navigate to food vocabulary at lunchtime") and push targeted optimizations.
- **Pre-loaded context:** When transitioning environments (arriving at school, entering doctor's office via geofence), the ecosystem pre-loads relevant vocabulary, reducing navigation time.
- **Quick-phrase sharing:** SLPs, teachers, and parents can all contribute quick-access phrases for situations they observe, crowdsourcing efficiency improvements.

**Gap addressed:** No AAC system uses cross-environment usage analytics to optimize prediction and vocabulary placement.

## 1B. Condition-Specific Applications

### ASD (Autism Spectrum Disorder)

**Primary ecosystem needs: School-home coordination, visual schedule integration, sensory-aware configuration, social skills carryover**

- **School-home data bridge:** ASD is the condition where school-home coordination is most critical because (a) communication behaviors often differ dramatically between environments, (b) IEP goals require documentation across settings, and (c) generalization of communication skills is the primary therapeutic challenge. Literature review confirms aided AAC systems enhance social communication of autistic elementary students (Evidence-Based Communication Assessment, Jan 2026). The school-home data gap is a recognized barrier (PAAutism.org; Undivided.io).
- **Visual schedule ecosystem:** ASD users rely heavily on visual schedules for transition support, routine predictability, and anxiety reduction. An ecosystem that syncs visual schedules across school, home, and therapy -- and connects them to AAC vocabulary -- addresses a need currently met by 3-5 separate apps that don't talk to each other.
- **Sensory profile management:** ASD users may need different AAC configurations based on sensory state (low-stimulation mode when overwhelmed, full-feature mode when regulated). An ecosystem that allows caregivers to switch profiles remotely or automatically based on sensory indicators is unique to ASD.
- **Social skills carryover tracking:** SLP works on social scripts in therapy; teacher monitors use in classroom; parent reports on use at home. Currently this coordination happens via paper data sheets or not at all.

### Cerebral Palsy

**Primary ecosystem needs: Multi-device access continuity, seating/positioning-aware configuration, therapy integration**

- **Multi-device continuity:** CP users frequently switch between powered wheelchair-mounted devices and handheld devices. The ecosystem must maintain identical vocabulary and configuration across all form factors.
- **Positioning-aware access:** Switch/touch accuracy changes with positioning (sitting in chair vs. reclined vs. side-lying). The ecosystem should store position-specific access profiles that activate automatically or with caregiver input.
- **Therapy goal tracking:** CP often involves intensive physical and occupational therapy alongside speech therapy. An integrated ecosystem that connects AAC usage data with PT/OT milestones creates a complete picture.

### ALS/MND

**Primary ecosystem needs: Progressive adaptation planning, voice banking timing, care team coordination, ventilator integration**

- **Staged transition planning:** ALS requires planned transitions through multiple access methods (touch -> switch -> eye gaze -> BCI) as motor function declines. The ecosystem must proactively prepare each next stage. Stage-based communication rehabilitation review (Fortune Journals, 2025) emphasizes the need for systematic transition planning.
- **Voice banking timing ecosystem:** The system monitors speech intelligibility metrics and alerts the care team when voice banking should begin -- currently, 80% of patients miss this window. AI-empowered voice generation research (Scientific Reports, 2024) shows feasibility of creating synthetic voices even from limited samples.
- **Multidisciplinary team coordination:** ALS clinics typically involve neurologists, SLPs, PTs, OTs, respiratory therapists, and social workers. The AAC ecosystem must share relevant data with all team members. Many multidisciplinary ALS clinics already provide referrals (Les Turner ALS Foundation).
- **Ventilator integration:** As ALS progresses to respiratory involvement, the AAC system must work alongside ventilator equipment, coordinating voice output timing with respiratory cycles.

### Aphasia

**Primary ecosystem needs: Communication partner training, therapy exercise integration, cross-setting vocabulary**

- **Partner training portal:** Aphasia communication is fundamentally a dyadic challenge -- the communication partner's behavior determines success as much as the user's AAC skills. A companion app that trains partners in supported conversation techniques (SCA, Aphasia Institute) is critical.
- **Therapy-device integration:** Lingraphica's TalkPath offers 13,500+ exercises. An ecosystem that connects therapeutic exercises directly to the AAC device's vocabulary means practice words become functional words seamlessly.
- **Life participation approach (LPAA):** The ecosystem supports LPAA by connecting AAC use to real activities (community participation, work tasks, social events) rather than isolating communication to therapy contexts.

### Dementia/Alzheimer's

**Primary ecosystem needs: Caregiver management tools, progressive simplification, memory aid integration, identity preservation**

- **Caregiver-centered management:** As dementia progresses, the USER cannot manage their own AAC system. The ecosystem must be designed so caregivers (family, staff) can manage everything. This is fundamentally different from all other conditions where user autonomy is prioritized.
- **Progressive simplification engine:** The ecosystem tracks declining cognitive metrics and automatically simplifies the interface over time (fewer choices, larger targets, more recognition-based than recall-based). No current product does this.
- **Memory book integration:** Digital memory books (photos, names, personal history) integrated into the AAC system serve both communicative and cognitive support functions. The ecosystem syncs memory book content across family members who can add/update entries.
- **Care facility integration:** For residential care, the ecosystem must work within facility infrastructure (WiFi, shared devices, staff turnover). Currently, AAC in dementia care is near-zero adoption despite 57M people affected globally.

### TBI (Traumatic Brain Injury)

**Primary ecosystem needs: Recovery-tracking adaptation, cognitive scaffolding, therapy coordination, return-to-work/school transition**

- **Recovery trajectory tracking:** TBI recovery is non-linear. The ecosystem must track cognitive-communication improvements and increase AAC complexity as the user recovers, while also detecting setbacks.
- **Multi-therapy coordination:** TBI rehabilitation involves speech therapy, cognitive rehabilitation, occupational therapy, psychology, and neuropsychology. The AAC ecosystem should share communication data across all these disciplines.
- **Return-to-participation planning:** The ecosystem supports gradual transition back to school, work, and social participation by providing environment-specific communication supports that can be faded as recovery progresses.

### Parkinson's Disease

**Primary ecosystem needs: Speech monitoring, medication cycle awareness, voice amplification integration**

- **Speech intelligibility monitoring:** The ecosystem tracks speech intelligibility over time, detecting decline patterns that may not be apparent to the user (Parkinson's "scaling problem" -- patients perceive they are speaking louder/clearer than they are).
- **Medication cycle integration:** Speech fluctuates with medication cycles (on-phase vs. off-phase). The ecosystem can time communication demands and access method profiles to medication schedules.
- **LSVT (Lee Silverman Voice Treatment) integration:** The most evidence-based speech therapy for Parkinson's. The ecosystem connects LSVT LOUD practice sessions with daily AAC use tracking.

### Down Syndrome

**Primary ecosystem needs: School inclusion support, literacy scaffolding, peer interaction facilitation**

- **Inclusive education tools:** Companion tools for general education teachers who have limited AAC training but have students with Down Syndrome in their classrooms.
- **Literacy-AAC connection:** Down Syndrome involves persistent literacy challenges. The ecosystem should bridge AAC symbol use with literacy instruction, supporting the transition from symbol-based to text-based communication where possible.
- **Peer modeling tools:** Apps that allow neurotypical peers to explore and learn the AAC system, promoting inclusive interaction.

### Head/Neck Cancer

**Primary ecosystem needs: Rapid onboarding, support community access, voice prosthesis management**

- **Rapid deployment:** Unlike developmental conditions, head/neck cancer AAC needs arise suddenly post-surgery. The ecosystem must support extremely fast onboarding with minimal learning curve.
- **Support community integration:** Connecting newly laryngectomized patients with experienced users provides critical psychosocial support. The Provox voice prosthesis community shows high satisfaction with peer support (85% satisfied with hospital help).
- **Multi-modality management:** Users may use a combination of TEP voice, electrolarynx, AAC app, and text-to-speech. The ecosystem manages and coordinates all these modalities.

### MS (Multiple Sclerosis)

**Primary ecosystem needs: Fatigue-aware scheduling, relapse tracking, access method flexibility**

- **Energy budgeting tools:** The ecosystem helps MS users plan their communication energy across the day, suggesting lower-effort communication methods during high-fatigue periods.
- **Relapse-sensitive adaptation:** During MS relapses, communication ability may drop suddenly. The ecosystem detects performance changes and automatically simplifies the interface.
- **Flexible access method profiles:** MS users may need to switch between touch, voice, switch, and eye gaze within a single day depending on fatigue and symptom fluctuation.

### Rett Syndrome

**Primary ecosystem needs: Eye-gaze optimization, caregiver training, educational integration**

- **Eye-gaze calibration sharing:** Calibration profiles synced across devices reduce the daily frustration of re-calibration. Eye tracking is one modality that individuals with Rett can use successfully (PubMed 29703090).
- **Intensive caregiver training:** Rett syndrome caregivers need specialized training in aided language modeling (ALM) combined with eye-gaze AAC. The ecosystem delivers this training in-context.
- **Educational participation tools:** Tools that allow Rett syndrome students to participate in educational activities through eye-gaze AAC, with interfaces adapted for their unique visual processing patterns (fewer/longer fixations, less scanning, per research on scanpath atypicalities).

### Locked-In Syndrome

**Primary ecosystem needs: BCI/eye-tracking integration, emergency communication, caregiver coordination**

- **Multi-modality failsafe:** When eye tracking becomes unreliable (as in progression to complete locked-in state), the ecosystem must seamlessly transition to BCI or other input methods. A 2025 pilot study demonstrated a hybrid ET-BCI fusion algorithm for maintaining communication continuity (arXiv, 2025).
- **Emergency communication priority:** The ecosystem must provide immediate access to emergency/urgent communication at all times, regardless of which access method is in use.
- **Caregiver shift management:** Locked-in syndrome requires 24/7 care. The ecosystem must support caregiver handoffs with communication context maintained.

### Muscular Dystrophy / SMA

**Primary ecosystem needs: Smart home integration, energy conservation, progressive adaptation**

- **Environmental control integration:** AAC should be the hub for all device control -- communication, computer access, home automation, entertainment, mobility device control. IoT assistive systems for SMA have demonstrated practical benefits (Springer, 2024).
- **Energy budget management:** SMA/MD users must conserve energy across ALL daily activities. The MDA recommends thinking of energy as "a tank with a certain amount" -- the ecosystem should help users prioritize communication within their total energy budget.
- **Respiratory-aware communication:** For ventilator-dependent users, the ecosystem coordinates voice output and typing activity with respiratory support cycles.

### Childhood Apraxia of Speech (CAS)

**Primary ecosystem needs: Practice tracking, therapy-AAC bridge, motor learning support**

- **Practice data integration:** CAS therapy requires high-repetition practice. The ecosystem tracks practice frequency across therapy, school, and home settings.
- **Gradually fading AAC support:** As speech improves with therapy, the AAC system should gradually reduce support level -- this requires tracking speech production data from therapy sessions.
- **Motor planning support:** The ecosystem provides consistent motor plans (same physical movements for same messages) across all devices, supporting the motor learning process.

# TOPIC 2: LLM Usage

*Large language models for prediction, sentence generation, conversation support*

## 2A. Intersection with Communication Struggles Taxonomy

### Domain 1: Receptive Struggles (Understanding Incoming Communication)

**Intersection:** LLMs can transform receptive support in ways impossible before 2023:

- **Real-time speech simplification:** An on-device LLM can intercept incoming speech (via speech-to-text) and simplify it to the user's comprehension level. Research confirms LLM-based text simplification improves comprehension by up to 14.6% (arXiv 2505.01980, 2025), with reduced perceived cognitive load measured via NASA Task Load Index. This is validated across 4,563 participants.
- **Figurative language translation:** LLMs can detect idioms, sarcasm, metaphor, and figurative speech and provide literal alternatives in real time. Meta-analytic evidence confirms significant figurative language comprehension deficits in ASD (PMC5843023). An LLM could transform "Break a leg!" to "This means 'good luck.'"
- **Vocabulary simplification by level:** LLMs can dynamically adjust incoming text complexity to match user's receptive level -- paragraph simplification for mild impairment, single-word simplification for severe impairment. Research on Easy-to-Read generation using LLMs shows feasibility (Frontiers in Computer Science, 2024; arXiv 2510.00662).
- **Summarization of conversations:** For users with memory/attention deficits, LLMs can maintain a rolling summary of conversation topics and key points. LLMs deployed in dementia care have shown ability to engage users in meaningful conversations and maintain memory of previous interactions (CLOVA CareCall, deployed across 140 institutions, 30,000 users as of March 2025).
- **Explanation of social context:** For users with pragmatic comprehension deficits (ASD, TBI), LLMs can provide brief social context annotations ("She seems frustrated because..." or "This is a joke").

**Research basis:** Adapting LLMs for character-based AAC has been validated (arXiv 2501.10582, January 2025). "The less I type, the better" (CHI 2023) established that AI language models can both enhance AND impede AAC communication.

### Domain 2: Expressive Struggles (Motor Speech, Language Formulation, Emotional Expression)

**Intersection:** This is the domain where LLM impact on AAC has been most studied:

- **Symbol-to-sentence generation:** LLMs can transform selected symbol sequences into grammatically complete sentences. Research at CSUN (2024) demonstrated automatic sentence generation using GPT-4 from AAC symbol combinations. This addresses the agrammatism problem in AAC -- users select 2-3 symbols, and the LLM generates a full sentence. Critical for users with cerebral palsy, ASD, and developmental conditions where morphological/syntactic production is impaired.
- **Abbreviation expansion:** SpeakFaster (Nature Communications, 2024) demonstrated 57% motor action savings via LLM-powered abbreviation expansion, with 29-60% rate improvements in field testing with ALS eye-gaze users. "gm hw r u" becomes "Good morning, how are you?"
- **Word retrieval support:** For aphasia, LLMs can provide word-finding support during natural conversation. Research proposes language-assistive solutions embedded in dialogue systems that detect and correct aphasic speech errors in real-time (Scientific Reports, 2025). A self-administered protocol targeting word-finding difficulties in chronic aphasia using ASR technology has shown clinical effectiveness (Aphasiology, 2025).
- **Sentence completion and expansion:** Users type or select a fragment; the LLM generates multiple possible complete messages. The PrAACT system (Expert Systems with Applications, 2024) uses transformer-based prediction specifically for AAC communication card prediction.
- **Emotional vocabulary surfacing:** LLMs can detect the emotional context of a conversation and surface relevant emotional vocabulary that the user might want to express but cannot independently retrieve.

### Domain 3: Interactive/Pragmatic Struggles (Conversation, Social Cognition)

**Intersection:** LLMs can serve as real-time social coaches and conversation facilitators:

- **Conversational repair suggestions:** When communication breakdown is detected (e.g., partner confusion), the LLM can suggest alternative phrasings or clarifications.
- **Social skills coaching:** Stanford's Noora chatbot (Stanford HAI, August 2025) provides individualized guidance on social communication scenarios for people with ASD, helping users learn to ask questions, give compliments, and respond empathetically. LLMs matched human experts in evaluating Theory of Mind tasks (GPT-4o, no statistically significant differences; PMC12501279, 2025).
- **Register adaptation:** LLMs can automatically adjust vocabulary, formality, and tone based on conversation context -- formal with a doctor, casual with a friend, respectful with a teacher.
- **Topic suggestion and maintenance:** For users who struggle with topic maintenance (dementia, TBI), LLMs can suggest relevant responses and topic continuations.
- **Turn-taking support:** LLMs can generate quick acknowledgments ("I see," "That's interesting," "Tell me more") to maintain conversational flow while the user composes a longer response.

**Research basis:** "LLMs for Autism Treatment: Current Trends and Emerging Strategies" (IEEE, 2025) reviews applications in communication, social skills, and emotional learning. LLMs that achieve adult human performance on higher-order Theory of Mind tasks (Frontiers in Human Neuroscience, 2025) demonstrate the foundation for pragmatic support.

### Domain 4: Physical/Motor Access Barriers

**Intersection:** LLMs reduce the motor demands of communication:

- **Keystroke reduction:** Every word the LLM accurately predicts is a keystroke (or scan cycle, or eye fixation) saved. SpeakFaster demonstrated 57% motor action savings. For users with severe motor impairment (ALS, locked-in, MD/SMA), this translates directly to reduced fatigue and faster communication.
- **Abbreviated input expansion:** Users with limited motor range can type minimal input and have LLMs expand it to full sentences. This is especially valuable for switch and eye-gaze users where each selection has high motor cost.
- **Context-aware vocabulary reduction:** LLMs that understand conversational context can reduce the number of options displayed, meaning fewer navigation steps (fewer motor actions) to reach the target message.
- **Multi-word and full-sentence prediction:** Instead of predicting one word at a time, LLMs can predict entire phrases or sentences, dramatically reducing the total number of selections needed.

### Domain 5: Cognitive-Communication Barriers (Attention, Memory, Executive Function)

**Intersection:** LLMs can serve as cognitive prosthetics:

- **Conversation memory:** LLMs maintain context across a conversation, compensating for working memory deficits. For TBI users who lose track of what was said 2 minutes ago, the LLM can surface "You were just talking about..." prompts.
- **Executive function scaffolding:** LLMs can break complex communication tasks into steps ("First, let's greet them. Then you can ask about..."), supporting users with executive function deficits.
- **Attention-guided interfaces:** LLMs can prioritize the most relevant vocabulary/options at any moment, reducing the attention demands of searching through large vocabulary sets.
- **Cognitive assistance for TBI:** Research presents ontologies for tailoring cognitive assistance during TBI interventions that could be integrated into LLM-powered AAC (Disability and Rehabilitation: Assistive Technology, 2025).
- **Dementia support:** LLM-powered socially assistive robots improved cognitive task performance in older adults after just 3 sessions -- fewer word repetitions, more detailed descriptions, reduced need for hints (CHI 2025). This principle translates to AAC: LLMs can scaffold communication tasks for users with dementia.

### Domain 6: Psychosocial Barriers (Confidence, Identity, Stigma)

**Intersection:** LLMs can transform the social experience of AAC use:

- **Voice and style personalization:** LLMs can be fine-tuned on a user's communication history to generate suggestions that sound like THEM, not like a generic system. This preserves communicative identity.
- **Competence projection:** When an LLM helps generate grammatically correct, contextually appropriate, age-appropriate messages, it reduces the "I sound like a child" stigma many adult AAC users report.
- **Reduced visible effort:** Less visible effort (fewer keystrokes, faster output) means the conversation feels more natural to both the user and partner, reducing the stigma of slow, labored communication.
- **Autistic worker support:** Research on LLM use by autistic workers for communication assistance (arXiv 2403.03297) found LLMs were perceived as trustworthy tools for drafting professional communications -- "It's the only thing I can trust."
- **Negative self-talk support:** Research explores autistic individuals' visions for AI in coping with negative self-talk (arXiv 2503.17504), indicating demand for LLM-based emotional support tools.

### Domain 7: Rate/Efficiency Barriers

**Intersection:** Rate improvement is the most studied and most immediately impactful LLM application:

- **29-60% rate improvement validated:** SpeakFaster field testing demonstrated 29-60% above baseline text-entry rates for ALS eye-gaze users (Nature Communications, 2024).
- **57% motor action savings:** Offline simulation of abbreviated input showed 57% keystroke savings.
- **Context-aware prediction:** LLMs that use conversation history, time of day, location, and communication partner identity can achieve dramatically higher prediction accuracy than current word-prediction systems.
- **Character-level prediction improvement:** An algorithm for producing character predictions from subword LLMs provides more accurate predictions than byte-level LLMs, n-gram models, or classification layers (arXiv 2501.10582, January 2025).
- **Closing the rate gap:** Current AAC: 8-15 WPM. Natural speech: 150-250 WPM. LLM-augmented AAC has a plausible path to 30-60 WPM, transforming the social dynamics of AAC conversation.

## 2B. Condition-Specific Applications

### ASD

**LLM needs: Figurative language translation, social coaching, register adaptation, conversational support**

- **Figurative language:** ASD users show significant deficits in understanding metaphor, idiom, sarcasm, and irony (PMC5843023). LLMs can provide real-time literal translations of figurative speech encountered in conversation.
- **Social scenario practice:** Stanford's Noora chatbot demonstrates LLMs providing individualized social coaching (Stanford HAI, August 2025). Scalable, always-available social training is uniquely valuable for ASD.
- **Communication drafting for work/school:** Autistic workers report LLMs as trusted tools for drafting professional communications (arXiv 2403.03297). An AAC-integrated LLM could help adult ASD users compose emails, messages, and formal communications.
- **Theory of Mind support:** LLMs performing at adult human levels on ToM tasks (Frontiers in Human Neuroscience, 2025) could power real-time "perspective-taking" annotations during conversations.
- **Echolalia-to-generative bridge:** For ASD users at Natural Language Acquisition stages who use gestalt language, LLMs could help therapists and caregivers understand echolalic utterances and support the transition to self-generated language.

### Cerebral Palsy

**LLM needs: Maximum keystroke reduction, symbol-to-sentence expansion, motor-efficient communication**

- **Keystroke savings are life-changing:** For CP users with severe motor impairment, each keystroke saved through LLM prediction translates to less fatigue, less pain, and more communication. The 57% motor action savings from SpeakFaster would transform daily communication for this population.
- **Symbol-to-sentence generation:** CP users who rely on symbol-based AAC (often pre-literate or with co-occurring intellectual disability) benefit from LLMs that generate full sentences from symbol selections, producing grammatically correct output that projects competence.
- **Dual access-method optimization:** LLMs can adapt prediction strategies based on whether the user is currently using touch, switch, or eye-gaze, as each method has different optimal prediction behaviors (more aggressive for high-cost methods like switch scanning).

### ALS/MND

**LLM needs: Maximum rate enhancement, progressive input adaptation, conversation maintenance during decline**

- **Rate is survival:** As ALS progresses, every keystroke becomes more expensive. LLM prediction directly extends the period during which meaningful communication is possible. The 29-60% improvement demonstrated in SpeakFaster was specifically validated with ALS eye-gaze users (Nature Communications, 2024).
- **Abbreviated input is critical:** As motor function declines, abbreviated input with LLM expansion allows users to continue communicating with minimal physical effort.
- **Voice persona maintenance:** LLMs fine-tuned on pre-decline communication can maintain the user's communication style even after they lose the ability to type freely, generating suggestions that "sound like them."
- **BCI-LLM integration:** As ALS progresses to BCI access, LLMs become even more critical because BCI signal rates are extremely slow. LLM prediction multiplies the information rate of any BCI system.

### Aphasia

**LLM needs: Word finding support, sentence reconstruction, speech error correction**

- **Real-time word retrieval:** LLMs can provide real-time word suggestions based on partial descriptions, circumlocutions, or semantic associations. User says "the thing you write with" -- LLM suggests "pen, pencil, marker."
- **Aphasic speech reconstruction:** Research demonstrates LLMs "reconstructing impaired language using generative AI for people with aphasia" (Scientific Reports, 2025). The LLM takes garbled or incomplete output and reconstructs the intended message.
- **Error detection and correction:** LLM-based systems can detect paraphasic errors (semantic: "pen" for "pencil"; phonemic: "fen" for "pen") and suggest corrections in real-time during conversation.
- **Linguistic fingerprint analysis:** LLMs can analyze speech patterns to provide clinicians with detailed aphasia classification information (PMC12437502), enabling more targeted AAC configuration.
- **Multilingual support:** For multilingual aphasia patients, LLMs offer particular promise -- though equity concerns about underrepresented languages remain (Frontiers in Psychology, 2025).

### Dementia/Alzheimer's

**LLM needs: Memory scaffolding, progressive simplification of language output, recognition-based interfaces**

- **Conversation memory prosthetic:** LLMs maintain conversation context when the user cannot. "You were just telling me about your grandchildren" helps re-anchor someone who has lost the conversational thread.
- **Progressive language simplification:** As dementia advances, the LLM progressively simplifies its own generated suggestions, matching the user's declining language capacity.
- **Cognitive stimulation integration:** LLM-powered cognitive exercises integrated into daily communication can maintain cognitive function longer. CHI 2025 research on LLM-powered socially assistive robots showed measurable cognitive improvements after 3 sessions.
- **AI care call model:** South Korea's CLOVA CareCall (deployed to 30,000 users) demonstrates feasibility of LLM-based conversation systems that remember previous interactions and maintain longitudinal relationships with users experiencing cognitive decline.
- **Cognitive decline detection:** LLMs can analyze speech patterns to detect cognitive decline (Springer Applied Intelligence, 2024), potentially alerting caregivers to worsening before it's clinically apparent.

### TBI

**LLM needs: Conversation tracking, executive function scaffolding, discourse organization support**

- **Conversation thread maintenance:** TBI users with working memory deficits lose track of conversations. LLMs can provide real-time "conversation tracker" summaries showing what's been discussed and current topic.
- **Discourse organization:** TBI hallmark is rambling, disorganized speech. LLMs can help users organize their thoughts before expressing them -- "It sounds like you want to say three things: [1]..., [2]..., [3]..."
- **Cognitive assistance ontologies:** Research presents ontologies reflecting clinical reasoning used by occupational therapists to tailor cognitive assistance for TBI (Disability and Rehabilitation: Assistive Technology, 2025). These ontologies could structure LLM scaffolding.
- **Recovery-adaptive complexity:** As TBI users recover, the LLM gradually offers less scaffolding, challenging the user to be more independent -- a therapeutic progression built into daily communication.

### Parkinson's Disease

**LLM needs: Rate enhancement to compensate for reduced speech, medication-aware prediction, LSVT integration**

- **Speech compensation:** With 89-90% of Parkinson's patients developing dysarthria, many will eventually need AAC. LLM prediction makes the transition to AAC less drastic by enabling faster communication.
- **On/off phase awareness:** LLM prediction can be more or less aggressive based on the user's medication phase (more aggressive during off-periods when motor function is worse).
- **Voice clarity assessment:** LLMs could analyze the user's speech output to provide feedback on loudness and clarity, supporting LSVT LOUD therapy goals.

### Down Syndrome

**LLM needs: Simplified language generation, literacy scaffolding, age-appropriate output**

- **Age-appropriate language:** Down Syndrome users often have AAC output that sounds much younger than their chronological age. LLMs can generate age-appropriate language from simplified input, projecting competence.
- **Literacy bridge:** LLMs can generate symbol+text output that supports literacy development, gradually increasing text complexity as literacy improves.
- **Simplified sentence construction:** For users with ~300-400 word vocabulary at age 6, LLMs can expand limited input into complete sentences using their existing vocabulary.
- **Stereotype-aware generation:** Research highlights that LLMs can perpetuate stereotypes about Down Syndrome (arXiv 2512.02275). AAC LLMs must be trained to generate output that reflects the individual's actual communication intent without stereotypical assumptions.

### Head/Neck Cancer

**LLM needs: Rapid text-to-speech with personality, professional communication support, voice reconstruction**

- **Professional communication preservation:** Many head/neck cancer patients are working adults who need to maintain professional communication. LLMs can help generate professional emails, texts, and spoken output that maintains their pre-surgery communication style.
- **Voice reconstruction:** AI-empowered voice generation for laryngectomy patients uses LLM-adjacent technology to reconstruct natural-sounding voice from limited input (Scientific Reports, 2024). Laronix's Bionic Voice combines hardware and AI voice-cloning.
- **Rapid deployment language models:** Unlike developmental conditions, these users have extensive language models. LLMs can quickly personalize to an adult user's existing communication patterns.

### MS

**LLM needs: Fatigue-responsive prediction aggressiveness, cognitive support during flares, flexible rate optimization**

- **Fatigue-scaled prediction:** During high-fatigue periods, the LLM becomes more aggressive (offering complete sentences from minimal input) to conserve user energy. During good periods, it can pull back.
- **Cognitive-communication support:** MS cognitive-communication difficulties (processing speed, working memory) benefit from the same LLM scaffolding as TBI -- conversation tracking, topic maintenance, discourse organization.
- **Relapse-aware adaptation:** During MS relapses, the LLM should dramatically increase prediction aggressiveness and offer more scaffolding.

### Rett Syndrome

**LLM needs: Eye-gaze prediction optimization, extremely efficient communication from minimal input**

- **Maximum prediction per gaze fixation:** Rett syndrome users communicate via eye-gaze with severe motor apraxia. Each gaze fixation is effortful and fatiguing. LLMs that maximize information per selection are critical.
- **Aided language modeling support:** LLMs can generate aided language modeling prompts for caregivers, showing them how to model language on the eye-gaze device in real-time conversation.

### Locked-In Syndrome

**LLM needs: Maximum information per minimal input, BCI signal amplification, predictive communication**

- **BCI signal multiplication:** BCI provides extremely low-bandwidth input (bits per minute). LLMs multiply this bandwidth by predicting intended messages from minimal signals.
- **Binary choice optimization:** For yes/no communicators, LLMs can generate optimal yes/no question sequences to efficiently determine the user's intended message.
- **Anticipatory communication:** Based on context (time of day, caregiver present, routine), LLMs can proactively suggest likely messages, reducing the need for laborious construction.

### Muscular Dystrophy / SMA

**LLM needs: Energy-efficient communication, environmental control via language, fatigue-responsive prediction**

- **Maximum keystroke reduction:** Every motor action costs energy from a limited supply. LLM prediction is directly life-quality-improving.
- **Natural language environmental control:** Instead of navigating through menus to control smart home devices, users can type/say natural language ("Turn off the bedroom light") and the LLM translates to device commands.
- **Communication-within-energy-budget:** LLMs can suggest more efficient phrasings when the user has been communicating for a long time, helping conserve energy.

### Childhood Apraxia of Speech

**LLM needs: Speech practice support, transitional AAC-to-speech bridge, motor learning reinforcement**

- **Therapeutic vocabulary targeting:** LLMs can ensure AAC predictions prioritize words the child is currently working on in speech therapy, reinforcing motor learning through AAC use.
- **Gradual AAC fading support:** As speech improves, LLMs can suggest that the user try saying the word before using AAC, supporting the transition from AAC to speech.

# TOPIC 3: Emotional Tone Detection & Adaptation

*Using AR/camera/health data/usage patterns to detect emotional state and adapt the AAC interface*

## 3A. Intersection with Communication Struggles Taxonomy

### Domain 1: Receptive Struggles (Understanding Incoming Communication)

**Intersection:** Emotional detection helps users understand the emotional dimensions of incoming communication:

- **Emotional tone annotation of partner speech:** For users who cannot interpret prosody (ASD, TBI), the system can analyze the communication partner's voice and display emotional tone indicators ("They sound frustrated," "This is a joke/sarcasm"). Research shows autistic traits are the strongest predictors of poorer emotion recognition (ScienceDirect, 2025).
- **Facial expression translation:** Camera-based analysis of the partner's facial expression, translated into text or emoji annotations that the user can understand. ERAnet (ScienceDirect, 2025) demonstrated that autistic children could match emojis to emotions using an emotion-recognition-to-emoji system, with adults adapting to the system in ~19 minutes.
- **Real-time environmental emotional translator:** A real-time environmental translator for emotion recognition in ASD (Scientific Reports, 2024) demonstrates feasibility of live emotional context annotation.
- **Receptive emotional calibration:** New research (ScienceDaily, January 2026) reveals autistic and non-autistic people express emotions through their faces in distinct ways, which may explain cross-group misunderstandings. Detection systems must be calibrated for these differences.

### Domain 2: Expressive Struggles (Motor Speech, Language Formulation, Emotional Expression)

**Intersection:** This is where emotional detection has perhaps its most profound impact -- helping users EXPRESS emotions they cannot otherwise communicate:

- **Automatic emotional vocabulary surfacing:** The system detects the user's emotional state (via physiological signals, facial analysis, or usage patterns) and automatically surfaces relevant emotional vocabulary. A user who is physiologically distressed but unable to express it sees "I'm upset," "I need a break," "This is frustrating" prominently displayed.
- **Emotional prosody injection into synthesized speech:** When the system detects the user's emotional state, it can inject appropriate prosody (pitch, rate, emphasis) into the AAC device's synthesized speech output. Currently, AAC speech is monotone, which communication partners interpret as emotional flatness even when the user is deeply engaged.
- **Facial masking compensation:** For Parkinson's (up to 70% hypomimia) and ALS (facial muscle weakness), the system detects internal emotional state through physiological signals and expresses it through the AAC device, compensating for the facial expression deficit. Machine learning for facial expression analysis in Parkinson's has been reviewed (ACM Computing Surveys, 2025).
- **Nonverbal expression enhancement:** For users who cannot gesture, make facial expressions, or use body language, the AAC system becomes their emotional expression channel. Emotional detection ensures the system accurately represents their internal state.

### Domain 3: Interactive/Pragmatic Struggles (Conversation, Social Cognition)

**Intersection:** Emotional detection transforms social interaction for AAC users:

- **Real-time social emotional feedback:** For ASD users with social cognition deficits, the system provides subtle cues about the conversation partner's emotional state, helping navigate social situations. Wristband users showed 63% faster response to peer expressions during group activities (EditVerse, 2025).
- **Conversational repair triggered by emotional signals:** If the system detects confusion or frustration in the communication partner (via their facial expression or vocal tone), it can prompt the AAC user to clarify or rephrase.
- **Emotional reciprocity support:** The system can suggest emotionally appropriate responses based on the partner's detected emotional state -- empathetic responses to sadness, enthusiastic responses to excitement.
- **Social cognition coaching:** Employees using color-coded emotional cues during meetings showed 41% improvement in empathetic responses over 6 weeks (EditVerse, 2025).

### Domain 4: Physical/Motor Access Barriers

**Intersection:** Emotional and physiological detection directly informs motor access adaptation:

- **Fatigue detection from physiological signals:** Wearable sensors monitoring heart rate, skin conductance, and acceleration can detect fatigue BEFORE it results in communication failure. Digital biomarkers for fatigue monitoring in neurological disorders provide continuous, objective monitoring vs. subjective, episodic self-report (NPJ Digital Medicine, 2025; PMC12110069).
- **Stress-responsive interface simplification:** When physiological stress is detected, the interface simplifies -- larger buttons, fewer options, more pre-stored phrases -- reducing the motor demand during high-stress periods.
- **Motor accuracy prediction:** Usage pattern analysis (increasing error rates, slower selection times, more corrections) predicts declining motor performance, allowing proactive interface adjustment.
- **Respiratory monitoring integration:** For ventilator-dependent users (ALS, MD/SMA), respiratory monitoring can time interface responsiveness to breathing cycles.

### Domain 5: Cognitive-Communication Barriers (Attention, Memory, Executive Function)

**Intersection:** Emotional state profoundly affects cognitive function, and detecting this interaction improves AAC support:

- **Cognitive load inference from emotional/physiological signals:** High arousal, stress, or frustration correlate with reduced cognitive capacity. Detecting these states allows the system to reduce cognitive demands (simplify interface, reduce choices, increase scaffolding).
- **Agitation detection for dementia:** A 2025 study (JMIR Aging) achieved 99% accuracy predicting agitation episodes in dementia at least 6 minutes in advance using wearable data. A novel multimodal system combines wearable sensors and privacy-preserving video analytics (arXiv 2411.08882). Real-time alerts empower caregivers to intervene before behavioral escalation.
- **Attention state monitoring:** Eye-tracking patterns and interaction metrics can detect attention lapses, prompting the system to draw the user back to the communication task or simplify the interface.
- **Arousal regulation support:** For ASD users, detecting dysregulation BEFORE meltdown allows the system to proactively offer calming supports, regulation vocabulary, or simplified interfaces. Gaia Wearable uses embedded sensors to detect "behavioral meltdown" signals and alert caregivers; MOXO Wearable uses skin conductance to predict emotions.

### Domain 6: Psychosocial Barriers (Confidence, Identity, Stigma)

**Intersection:** Emotional detection and adaptation can address deep psychosocial needs:

- **Frustration detection and encouragement:** The system detects frustration (through physiological signals or usage patterns like repeated corrections, abandoned messages) and responds with encouragement or suggests easier communication strategies, preventing abandonment.
- **Confidence building through emotional success tracking:** The system tracks emotional communication successes (user expressed an emotion and was understood) and surfaces this progress to the user, building confidence.
- **Emotional authenticity:** When the AAC device accurately represents the user's emotional state (through prosody, vocabulary, or explicit emotional indicators), it enhances the user's sense of being truly understood, countering the emotional flatness that drives social isolation.
- **Stigma reduction through natural interaction:** When AAC output includes appropriate emotional tone, conversations feel more natural to partners, reducing the "talking to a robot" perception.

### Domain 7: Rate/Efficiency Barriers

**Intersection:** Emotional context is a powerful prediction signal:

- **Emotion-informed prediction:** If the system detects the user is happy/excited, prediction models can prioritize positive vocabulary; if frustrated, prioritize complaint/request vocabulary. This emotional context improves prediction accuracy, reducing navigation time.
- **Energy-state-aware rate optimization:** Physiological fatigue detection triggers more aggressive prediction, compensating for declining motor efficiency with increased AI assistance.
- **Context-switching from emotional detection:** Detecting a shift in emotional state can trigger proactive vocabulary context switches (from casual conversation vocabulary to "I need help" vocabulary) faster than the user could navigate manually.
- **Communication priority during distress:** When distress is detected, the system can immediately surface emergency/urgent phrases, bypassing normal navigation entirely.

## 3B. Condition-Specific Applications

### ASD

**Emotional detection challenges: Alexithymia, atypical expression, sensory processing differences**

ASD represents the most complex and most impactful target for emotional detection in AAC:

- **Alexithymia prevalence:** Approximately 50% of autistic individuals experience alexithymia -- difficulty identifying and describing their own emotions. Emotional detection can externalize what the user cannot introspectively access. The system detects physiological arousal and helps the user label it ("Your body signals suggest you might be feeling anxious").
- **Atypical emotional expression:** January 2026 research (ScienceDaily) confirms autistic people express emotions through their faces in distinct ways. Standard facial emotion recognition trained on neurotypical expressions will FAIL for autistic users. The system must be calibrated for autistic expression patterns.
- **Sensory overload prediction:** Wearable physiological monitoring can detect rising arousal that precedes sensory meltdowns. Reveal device integrates an "Anxiety Meter"; Gaia Wearable notifies caregivers of impending meltdowns. For AAC users, the system can proactively simplify the interface and surface regulation vocabulary ("I need to leave," "Too loud," "I need a break").
- **Meltdown prevention through early warning:** A 6-month study using Empatica E4 with an autistic child demonstrated XGBoost-based prediction of agitated behaviors from BVP, EDA, and acceleration data (Springer, 2023). Integrating this prediction into AAC enables preemptive communication support.
- **Bidirectional translation:** The system must BOTH help the autistic user understand others' emotions (partner facial expression -> emoji/text) AND help others understand the autistic user's emotions (physiological data -> emotional label shared with partners).
- **Preference for predictability:** Emotional detection that enables predictable AAC adaptations (always simplifies when stress rises) aligns with the autistic preference for predictable systems.

### Cerebral Palsy

**Emotional detection challenges: Motor-emotional interaction, pain detection, effort vs. emotion differentiation**

- **Pain detection:** CP users experience chronic pain that affects communication. Physiological detection of pain states can trigger comfort-seeking vocabulary and alert caregivers.
- **Effort vs. emotion differentiation:** Motor effort (physiological arousal from physical exertion of using the device) must be distinguished from emotional arousal. CP users show elevated heart rate and skin conductance simply from the physical effort of communication.
- **Emotional vocabulary access:** When the system detects emotional states, it surfaces relevant vocabulary, compensating for the motor difficulty of navigating to emotional vocabulary manually.

### ALS/MND

**Emotional detection challenges: Progressive loss of all expression channels, pseudobulbar affect, caregiver burnout detection**

- **Emotional expression lifeline:** As ALS strips away speech, facial expression, gesture, and body language, the AAC device becomes the ONLY channel for emotional expression. Accurate emotional detection is literally the user's last connection to emotional communication.
- **Pseudobulbar affect (PBA) compensation:** ~50% of ALS patients experience PBA (involuntary laughing or crying unrelated to actual emotion). The system must distinguish PBA from genuine emotion, preventing misinterpretation. The AAC system could display "I'm not actually sad/happy -- this is involuntary" when PBA is detected.
- **Fatigue-responsive adaptation:** Digital biomarker research shows continuous fatigue monitoring is feasible for neurodegenerative diseases (NPJ Digital Medicine, 2025). The AAC interface simplifies proactively as fatigue increases.
- **Caregiver emotional state monitoring (with consent):** The ecosystem could monitor caregiver stress levels and suggest respite or support resources, addressing the burnout crisis in ALS caregiving.

### Aphasia

**Emotional detection challenges: Frustration-accuracy spiral, catastrophic reaction detection, emotional word retrieval**

- **Frustration spiral detection:** Aphasia users experience a vicious cycle: communication difficulty -> frustration -> worse communication -> more frustration. Detecting the onset of this spiral allows the system to intervene early (simplify interface, offer encouragement, suggest a break).
- **Catastrophic reaction detection:** Some aphasia patients experience "catastrophic reactions" -- sudden, overwhelming emotional breakdowns triggered by communication failure. Physiological detection could provide early warning.
- **Emotional word retrieval support:** Anomia affects emotional vocabulary. When the system detects emotion but the user cannot retrieve the relevant word, it proactively surfaces emotional vocabulary options.

### Dementia/Alzheimer's

**Emotional detection challenges: Agitation prediction, sundowning detection, caregiver alert systems**

- **Agitation prediction (validated):** 2025 research (JMIR Aging) achieved 99% accuracy in personalized agitation prediction using wristband data, predicting episodes at least 6 minutes in advance. The multimodal system combining wearable data and video analytics (arXiv 2411.08882) represents the current state of the art.
- **Sundowning detection:** Late-afternoon/evening agitation patterns (sundowning) can be detected through longitudinal physiological monitoring, triggering environmental and communication adjustments.
- **Pain communication:** Dementia patients often cannot verbalize pain. Physiological detection (elevated heart rate, skin conductance, behavioral changes) combined with pain-communication vocabulary surfacing addresses a critical unmet need.
- **Real-time caregiver alerts:** Wearable technologies provide real-time alerts enabling caregivers to intervene promptly, reducing severity and duration of behavioral episodes (Texas A&M, 2025).
- **Behavioral and psychological symptom detection:** A scoping review of AI technologies for early detection of BPSD (behavioral and psychological symptoms of dementia) confirms feasibility (JMIR Aging, 2025).

### TBI

**Emotional detection challenges: Emotional dysregulation, impaired self-awareness, irritability management**

- **Emotional dysregulation detection:** TBI frequently causes emotional dysregulation. Detecting rising agitation or emotional overwhelm allows the AAC system to simplify and provide calming supports before the user loses communicative function.
- **Self-awareness compensation:** Many TBI patients have impaired awareness of their own emotional state (anosognosia). External emotional detection provides objective information about emotional state that the user cannot self-assess.
- **Irritability management:** TBI-related irritability is a common barrier to social participation. Detecting escalating irritability allows preemptive communication support ("I'm getting frustrated and may need a break").

### Parkinson's Disease

**Emotional detection challenges: Facial masking, voice masking, medication state interaction**

- **Facial masking (hypomimia) compensation:** Up to 70% of Parkinson's patients have reduced facial expression. Machine learning analysis of facial expressions in Parkinson's is actively researched (ACM Computing Surveys, 2025). The AAC system can detect internal emotion through other channels (physiological, vocal analysis, usage patterns) and express it explicitly, compensating for the "masked" face.
- **Voice masking compensation:** Parkinson's produces monotone speech that sounds emotionless. The AAC system can inject emotional prosody into synthesized speech based on detected emotional state.
- **Medication-emotional interaction:** Emotion detection contextualizes medication effects -- some Parkinson's medications affect mood. Tracking emotional patterns relative to medication timing provides clinical insight.

### Down Syndrome

**Emotional detection challenges: Emotional expression may be more transparent, behavioral vs. emotional state**

- **Building on emotional strengths:** Individuals with Down Syndrome often have relatively strong emotional expression compared to language skills. Detection systems can leverage this by amplifying emotional communication through the AAC device.
- **Behavioral state monitoring in educational settings:** Detecting frustration or confusion during educational activities allows the AAC system to adapt, potentially preventing behavioral escalation.
- **Emotional vocabulary expansion:** The system uses detected emotions as teaching moments, expanding emotional vocabulary by labeling detected states.

### Head/Neck Cancer

**Emotional detection challenges: Depression screening, loss/grief detection, social reintegration monitoring**

- **Depression screening:** Head/neck cancer patients have among the highest depression rates of any cancer population. Continuous emotional monitoring through usage patterns and physiological signals can support depression screening.
- **Grief and adjustment monitoring:** The sudden loss of voice is a profound grief experience. Emotional detection can track adjustment over time and alert the care team to prolonged difficulty.
- **Social participation tracking:** Monitoring emotional state during social interactions can indicate successful reintegration or persistent social avoidance.

### MS

**Emotional detection challenges: Fatigue-emotion differentiation, relapse detection, pseudobulbar affect**

- **Fatigue vs. depression differentiation:** MS fatigue and MS depression are frequently comorbid and difficult to distinguish. Physiological detection can help differentiate, allowing appropriate AAC adaptation for each.
- **Relapse early detection:** Changes in emotional patterns, fatigue profiles, and communication performance may serve as early relapse indicators. Digital remote monitoring research (Frontiers in Immunology, 2025) supports this approach.
- **PBA management:** Like ALS, MS can cause pseudobulbar affect. The same PBA detection and compensation strategies apply.

### Rett Syndrome

**Emotional detection challenges: Limited expression channels, eye-gaze emotional patterns, happiness detection**

- **Eye-gaze emotional analysis:** For Rett syndrome, where eye-gaze is the primary communication channel, analyzing gaze patterns for emotional content (gaze aversion = discomfort, sustained gaze = interest/happiness) provides emotional detection without additional sensors.
- **Happiness and engagement detection:** VR research (Applied Sciences, 2026) shows eye-tracking-based assessment of happiness and engagement in Rett syndrome is feasible, with VR improving attentional efficiency.
- **Caregiver emotional communication support:** Since Rett syndrome caregivers must interpret very subtle emotional cues, detection systems can amplify these cues for caregivers.

### Locked-In Syndrome

**Emotional detection challenges: No conventional expression channels, physiological signals only, quality-of-life monitoring**

- **Physiological signals as the only emotional channel:** With no facial expression, voice, gesture, or body language, physiological signals (heart rate, skin conductance, EEG patterns from BCI) are the ONLY source of emotional information. This makes emotional detection not merely helpful but essential.
- **Quality of life assessment:** Locked-in syndrome patients report quality of life that surprises clinicians. Continuous emotional monitoring provides objective quality-of-life data, countering assumptions about suffering.
- **Desire to communicate urgency detection:** Physiological arousal when the user has something urgent to communicate can trigger priority communication mode.

### Muscular Dystrophy / SMA

**Emotional detection challenges: Pain monitoring, respiratory-emotional interaction, energy-emotional tracking**

- **Pain communication:** MD/SMA patients experience progressive pain that is difficult to communicate. Physiological detection supports pain communication, especially for users with limited motor ability.
- **Respiratory-emotional interaction:** Respiratory distress affects emotional state and vice versa. Monitoring both simultaneously allows appropriate AAC adaptation.
- **Energy-emotional integrated tracking:** Low energy causes frustration which causes more energy expenditure in a vicious cycle. Detecting this cycle early allows intervention.

### Childhood Apraxia of Speech

**Emotional detection challenges: Frustration during practice, confidence monitoring, therapeutic engagement**

- **Practice frustration detection:** CAS therapy requires high-repetition practice that is inherently frustrating. Detecting frustration allows the system to adjust difficulty, offer encouragement, or suggest breaks at optimal times.
- **Therapeutic engagement monitoring:** Detecting waning attention or rising frustration during therapy exercises allows real-time session adjustment.

## Cross-Cutting Opportunity 2: Bidirectional Emotional Communication Bridge

**Topics spanned:** Emotional Detection (core) + LLM (vocabulary generation) + Ecosystem (sharing with partners/care team)

**Taxonomy domains spanned:** Domain 1 (Receptive -- understanding partner emotions), Domain 2 (Expressive -- expressing own emotions), Domain 3 (Pragmatic -- emotional reciprocity), Domain 6 (Psychosocial -- emotional authenticity)

**Conditions served:** ASD (alexithymia, atypical expression), Parkinson's (facial masking), ALS (progressive expression loss), Locked-In (no expression channels), Rett Syndrome (limited expression), CP (motor expression barriers), Dementia (emotional communication decline)

**Description:** A system that:
1. **DETECTS** the user's emotional state through multimodal sensing (physiological, facial, vocal, usage patterns) with condition-specific calibration
2. **DETECTS** the partner's emotional state through camera/microphone analysis
3. **TRANSLATES** the partner's emotions into accessible formats for the user (emoji, text labels, color codes)
4. **EXPRESSES** the user's detected emotions through the AAC output (emotional prosody, vocabulary, explicit emotional labels)
5. **SHARES** emotional data with the care team for longitudinal tracking (depression screening, QoL monitoring, medication effects)

**Key technical challenge:** Condition-specific calibration. Autistic emotional expression differs from neurotypical (ScienceDaily, January 2026). Parkinson's facial masking confounds facial emotion detection. PBA in ALS/MS produces false emotional signals. Each condition requires specific calibration models.

**Research support:** ERAnet emotion recognition to emoji (ScienceDirect, 2025). Reveal/Gaia/MOXO wearable emotion detection. Multimodal agitation detection (JMIR Aging, 2025). Stanford Noora social coaching (Stanford HAI, 2025).

**Grant relevance:** VERY HIGH -- Emotional communication is core to ASD. The bidirectional aspect (both understanding others' emotions AND expressing own emotions) addresses two of the most impactful ASD communication struggles.

## Cross-Cutting Opportunity 3: Intelligent Communication Partner Ecosystem

**Topics spanned:** Ecosystem (core) + LLM (partner coaching content generation) + Emotional Detection (partner frustration/confusion detection)

**Taxonomy domains spanned:** Domain 3 (Pragmatic -- conversation quality), Domain 6 (Psychosocial -- inclusion, stigma), Domain 7 (Rate -- conversational timing), Domain 1 (Receptive -- partner language adaptation)

**Conditions served:** ALL conditions -- communication partner behavior is the single strongest modifiable predictor of AAC success across all conditions

**Description:** A companion app/system for communication PARTNERS that:
1. **COACHES** partners on communication strategies specific to the user's condition and abilities (generated/personalized by LLM)
2. **DETECTS** partner confusion, frustration, or impatience through emotional analysis and suggests specific strategies
3. **SIGNALS** when the AAC user is composing a message (reducing partner anxiety about silence)
4. **PROVIDES** real-time suggestions to the partner ("Try asking a yes/no question," "Give them more time," "Simplify your sentence")
5. **TRACKS** partner interaction quality over time, providing feedback and training recommendations

**Research support:** Asynchronous caregiver training effective (Tandfonline, 2025). POWR method gaining adoption. Peer AAC culture accelerates adoption (ASHA AJSLP, 2023). SLP caseloads of 115+ limit direct training time (ASHA, 2025).

**Grant relevance:** VERY HIGH -- For ASD specifically, communication partner behavior (parents, teachers, peers) is the primary determinant of communication outcome. An intelligent partner ecosystem directly addresses the school-home generalization challenge.

## Cross-Cutting Opportunity 4: LLM-Powered Receptive Communication Support

**Topics spanned:** LLM (core) + Ecosystem (delivery across environments) + Emotional Detection (emotional annotation of incoming speech)

**Taxonomy domains spanned:** Domain 1 (Receptive -- all sub-domains), Domain 3 (Pragmatic -- figurative language, social context), Domain 5 (Cognitive -- processing support)

**Conditions served:** ASD (figurative language, pragmatic comprehension), Aphasia (language simplification), Dementia (progressive comprehension decline), TBI (cognitive processing), Down Syndrome (vocabulary simplification)

**Description:** A system that transforms incoming communication for the user:
1. **SIMPLIFIES** partner speech to match user's comprehension level (LLM text simplification, validated at 14.6% comprehension improvement)
2. **TRANSLATES** figurative language to literal meaning (critical for ASD)
3. **ANNOTATES** social/emotional context of partner communication (emotional detection)
4. **SUMMARIZES** conversations for users with memory challenges (LLM)
5. **DELIVERS** the same receptive support across all environments via the ecosystem

**Technical innovation:** No AAC product does ANY of this. The entire AAC market is built around expressive support. Receptive support represents an unoccupied, unpatented, high-impact space.

**Grant relevance:** VERY HIGH -- Receptive struggles are among the most impactful for ASD (figurative language, pragmatic comprehension, social context reading) and are COMPLETELY unaddressed by every AAC product on the market.

## Cross-Cutting Opportunity 5: Progressive Condition Adaptation Engine

**Topics spanned:** All three (Ecosystem for care team coordination; LLM for adaptive prediction; Emotional Detection for state monitoring)

**Taxonomy domains spanned:** Domain 4 (Motor -- progressive decline), Domain 5 (Cognitive -- progressive decline), Domain 7 (Rate -- progressive slowdown), Domain 6 (Psychosocial -- identity preservation during decline)

**Conditions served:** ALS, Dementia, MS, Parkinson's, MD/SMA, Rett Syndrome

**Description:** A system specifically designed for progressive conditions that:
1. **MONITORS** trajectory of decline across motor, cognitive, and communication dimensions
2. **PREDICTS** when transitions will be needed (access method change, vocabulary simplification, care team alerts)
3. **PROACTIVELY PREPARES** the next stage (pre-configures eye-gaze before touch fails; pre-simplifies vocabulary before cognitive decline makes navigation impossible)
4. **PRESERVES IDENTITY** throughout decline (maintains voice, vocabulary, communication style, interaction history)
5. **COORDINATES** the care team through proactive alerts and shared data

**Grant relevance:** MODERATE for ASD (not typically progressive), but HIGH for demonstrating cross-condition value and for late-diagnosed ASD populations aging into other conditions.

## Cross-Cutting Opportunity 6: Contextual Prediction Engine with Multimodal Input

**Topics spanned:** LLM (core prediction) + Ecosystem (cross-environment context) + Emotional Detection (emotional context signal)

**Taxonomy domains spanned:** Domain 7 (Rate -- primary), Domain 2 (Expressive -- formulation support), Domain 5 (Cognitive -- reduced decision load)

**Conditions served:** ALL conditions -- rate improvement is universally beneficial

**Description:** An LLM prediction engine that uses:
1. **Conversational context** (what has been said in this conversation)
2. **Environmental context** (location, time, activity, communication partner -- from ecosystem)
3. **Emotional context** (user's emotional state, partner's emotional state -- from detection)
4. **Historical context** (this user's communication patterns, vocabulary preferences -- from cloud)
5. **Medical context** (medication timing, fatigue level, recent relapse -- from ecosystem)

To produce dramatically more accurate predictions than any single-context prediction system.

**Target:** Closing the rate gap from 8-15 WPM toward 40-60 WPM through multimodal context-aware prediction.

**Grant relevance:** HIGH -- Rate improvement is the most immediately measurable outcome for grant reporting, and the multimodal approach demonstrates technical sophistication appropriate for a $4M investment.
