## How to Read This Document

The main Cat 11 document (`category-11-nonliteral-language.md`) introduces conceptual metaphor as one mechanism among many. This document treats it the way cognitive linguistics has treated it since 1980: **as the backbone of abstract thought**, not as a rhetorical garnish. It also adds metonymy, which the main document does not cover at all.

Two core claims organize the material:

1. **Metonymy is reference-shifting within a single domain.** It is fundamentally different from metaphor in its cognitive mechanism, and the difference matters.
2. **Conceptual metaphor is a system of thought.** The way English speakers reason about time, causation, emotion, morality, understanding, and argument is structured by a relatively small inventory of cross-domain mappings — most of them unconscious, most of them learnable as patterns.

If you are an autistic reader who has been told you "can't understand metaphor," this document is likely to be useful because it reframes the object of study. Metaphor-as-system is studiable. Metaphor-as-mystery is not.

## PART 1: METONYMY

### 1.1 What Metonymy Is

Metonymy is reference-shifting: using an expression that conventionally picks out one thing to stand for a related thing within the same conceptual domain.

- "The **White House** announced new tariffs." (The building did not announce anything. *White House* stands for the executive branch of the US government.)
- "I'm reading **Shakespeare**." (Not the man, who is dead. His works.)
- "The **kettle** is boiling." (The water in the kettle.)
- "She's got a good **head** on her shoulders." (Her cognitive capacities.)
- "Nice **wheels**." (Car.)
- "The **ham sandwich** at table five wants his check." (The customer who ordered it — a classic example from Nunberg 1979 and Lakoff & Johnson 1980.)

None of these are "figures of speech" in the sense of ornamental language. They are the ordinary, default way of saying certain things in English. Most speakers use dozens of metonymies per conversation without noticing.

### 1.2 Metaphor vs. Metonymy: The Core Contrast

The cleanest distinction comes from Lakoff & Johnson (1980) *Metaphors We Live By* and has been refined in Radden & Kövecses (1999):

| Feature | Metaphor | Metonymy |
|---|---|---|
| **Structure** | A IS B (two domains) | A FOR B (one domain) |
| **Mechanism** | **Mapping** across domains | **Reference shift** within a domain |
| **Example** | TIME IS MONEY ("spend time") | PLACE FOR INSTITUTION ("the White House announced") |
| **Cognitive function** | Understanding one thing in terms of another | Providing mental access to one thing via another |
| **Relation** | Similarity (or structured correspondence) | Contiguity, association, part-whole |
| **Domains** | Two conceptual domains | One conceptual domain / ICM |

The technical term for the single-domain structure metonymy operates within is **Idealized Cognitive Model** (ICM), from Lakoff (1987) *Women, Fire, and Dangerous Things*. An ICM is a structured mental representation of a familiar situation — "restaurant," "government building," "author-and-works." Metonymy works by exploiting the internal structure of an ICM: any salient element in an ICM can, under the right conditions, stand for another element or for the whole.

#### Why the distinction matters

- **Metaphor requires cross-domain inference.** "She's a bulldozer" requires constructing an ad hoc concept BULLDOZER* that captures the relevant properties (forceful, flattens opposition) without the literal machinery. Cross-domain work.
- **Metonymy requires within-domain lookup.** "The White House announced" requires knowing that government buildings in the White-House ICM are associated with the executive branch that works there. Within-domain work.

For autistic readers with strong rule-extraction profiles, this distinction is load-bearing. Metonymy is **more rule-like** than metaphor. Once you have the pattern "PLACE FOR INSTITUTION," dozens of expressions open up ("Downing Street denied," "the Pentagon confirmed," "Wall Street reacted," "Brussels warned," "the Vatican said").

### 1.3 A Typology of Metonymy

Radden & Kövecses (1999) offer the most cited taxonomy. The typology is not exhaustive — metonymy is productive and new patterns are created constantly — but it covers the bulk of everyday English. I list patterns with examples.

#### Part-Whole metonymies

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| **PART FOR WHOLE** | "We need more **hands**" (workers) |
| **PART FOR WHOLE** | "Nice **wheels**" (car) |
| **PART FOR WHOLE** | "There are too many **mouths** to feed" (people) |
| **WHOLE FOR PART** | "I'm going to **fill up the car**" (the gas tank) |
| **WHOLE FOR PART** | "**America** won the gold" (the American athlete) |
| **MATERIAL FOR OBJECT** | "She put on her **furs**" (fur garments) |

**Synecdoche**, in traditional rhetoric, is the name for Part-Whole and Whole-Part metonymies specifically. Contemporary cognitive linguistics mostly absorbs synecdoche into the broader metonymy category; there is no sharp theoretical line.

#### Container and location metonymies

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| **CONTAINER FOR CONTENT** | "The **kettle** is boiling" |
| **CONTAINER FOR CONTENT** | "I drank **the whole bottle**" |
| **CONTAINER FOR CONTENT** | "**The glass** spilled" |
| **PLACE FOR INSTITUTION** | "The **White House** announced" |
| **PLACE FOR INSTITUTION** | "**Downing Street** denied" |
| **PLACE FOR INSTITUTION** | "**Brussels** is concerned" |
| **PLACE FOR EVENT** | "**Vietnam** changed America" (the Vietnam War) |
| **PLACE FOR EVENT** | "Remember **Pearl Harbor**" |
| **PLACE FOR INHABITANTS** | "The **village** objected" |
| **PLACE FOR INHABITANTS** | "The whole **stadium** stood up" |

Place-based metonymies are extraordinarily common in news language. If you learn one rule — *locations associated with institutions stand for those institutions* — you unlock a very large class of otherwise puzzling expressions.

#### Producer-product and author-work

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| **PRODUCER FOR PRODUCT** | "I'm reading **Shakespeare**" |
| **PRODUCER FOR PRODUCT** | "She drove a **Ford**" |
| **PRODUCER FOR PRODUCT** | "I own two **Picassos**" |
| **AUTHOR FOR WORK** | "**Dickens** is on the top shelf" |
| **BRAND FOR PRODUCT** | "Pass me a **Kleenex**" |

#### Controller-controlled

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| **CONTROLLER FOR CONTROLLED** | "**Nixon** bombed Hanoi" (not personally) |
| **CONTROLLER FOR CONTROLLED** | "**Napoleon** lost at Waterloo" |
| **CONTROLLER FOR CONTROLLED** | "**Schwarzkopf** defeated Iraq" |

This pattern is a common source of what looks like lying in political discourse: attributing actions to individuals that were actually performed by armies, bureaucracies, or institutions they command. Grammatically fine; ethically complex.

#### Cause-effect

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| **CAUSE FOR EFFECT** | "His **native tongue**" (the language his tongue produces) |
| **CAUSE FOR EFFECT** | "A **good hand**" (what the hand produces — handwriting) |
| **EFFECT FOR CAUSE** | "**Healthy** complexion" (a complexion that indicates health) |
| **EFFECT FOR CAUSE** | "**Slow** road" (a road that makes driving slow) |

#### Action-based

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| **OBJECT FOR ACTION** | "Let me **google** that" |
| **OBJECT FOR ACTION** | "To **Xerox** a document" |
| **INSTRUMENT FOR ACTION** | "He has a sharp **tongue**" |
| **BODY PART FOR PERSON** | "I need another **pair of hands**" |

#### Role and social

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| **OBJECT FOR USER** | "**The ham sandwich** wants his check" |
| **OBJECT FOR USER** | "**The saxophone** has a cold tonight" |
| **UNIFORM FOR ROLE** | "**The suits** decided" |
| **SIGN FOR REFERENT** | "**The flag** fell" (a defeat) |

#### Temporal and event

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| **TIME FOR EVENT** | "**9/11**" (the attacks of September 11, 2001) |
| **TIME FOR EVENT** | "Since **the pandemic**" |
| **DATE FOR EVENT** | "Remember **1968**" |

This typology is useful the way a periodic table is useful: you are not expected to memorize every instance, but you should be able to recognize when an expression fits a pattern. Once the pattern is named, novel tokens become transparent.

### 1.4 The Reference-Point Model

Langacker (1993) proposes that metonymy works via a **reference point construction**: the explicitly mentioned entity is a mental access point (the "vehicle") that gives cognitive access to the intended target. The vehicle is chosen because it is more salient, more concrete, or more conventionalized than the target.

- "I'm parked **out back**." → "out back" is the salient vehicle; "the car I am currently driving" is the target.
- "**Washington** is worried." → "Washington" is salient; "the US political establishment" is the target.

This model explains two things at once:

1. **Why metonymy is productive.** Any sufficiently salient element in an ICM can, in principle, serve as a reference point for any other element. New metonymies can be created on the fly and are usually understood without effort.
2. **Why metonymy is usually easy to parse.** Within-domain lookup is cheap. You do not need cross-domain imagination — only knowledge of the associations inside a single conceptual structure.

### 1.5 Metonymy and Autism: What the Research Shows

The empirical literature on metonymy comprehension in autism is much thinner than the metaphor literature. This section reports what exists, clearly and without overclaiming.

#### Rundblad & Annaz (2010)

Rundblad & Annaz (2010), *Autism* 14(1):29–46, is the canonical comparison of metaphor and metonymy comprehension in autistic children. Using a child-friendly story-picture task, they compared autistic children to typically developing controls across chronological age, mental age, and receptive vocabulary.

**Findings:**

- Autistic children were **impaired on metaphor** relative to both chronological and mental age — that is, their metaphor comprehension lagged behind what either age-match would predict.
- Autistic children were **delayed but not impaired on metonymy relative to receptive vocabulary** — that is, their metonymy comprehension tracked their vocabulary rather than lagging behind it.
- In typical development, metonymy comprehension emerges earlier than metaphor comprehension, and metonyms are generally easier.
- The authors interpret the asymmetry as evidence that metaphor and metonymy recruit partially distinct mechanisms, with metaphor being the more language-dependent and metonymy the more experience-dependent.

**How to read this in autism-affirming terms:** The study's own data say that autistic children's metonymy performance was in line with their receptive vocabulary. That is a very different claim from "autistic children cannot understand metonymy." It says, effectively, that once you know the relevant vocabulary, metonymy works — which is exactly what the reference-point model would predict.

A caveat: Rundblad & Annaz's sample was small (11 autistic children, 17 typically developing). This is a starting point, not a settled picture.

#### Rundblad & Annaz's follow-up work

Rundblad (2010) *British Journal of Developmental Psychology* 28(3):547–563 examined metaphor and metonymy comprehension in typical development, confirming the typical-development finding that metonymy emerges earlier and is easier than metaphor. This is the baseline against which the autism findings should be read.

#### Later cross-sectional and longitudinal work

A 2018 cross-sectional and longitudinal study (published in *Frontiers in Psychology*) examined novel metaphor and metonymy comprehension across children, adolescents, and adults with autism. The longitudinal design was designed to disentangle age, language level, and the autism-specific contribution. The general finding across this line of work is that metonymy comprehension in autism is **less impaired than metaphor comprehension** and tracks language ability closely.

#### Framework for autistic readers

Putting the pieces together:

- Metonymy is **pattern-dense** and operates within a single conceptual domain, which is close to a best-case scenario for systematic, rule-based learning.
- The research that exists does not support a strong "autistic people can't do metonymy" claim. What it supports is: metonymy comprehension tracks vocabulary.
- Metonymy typology is **learnable as a closed-ish inventory of patterns**. Once you know the dozen or so major patterns (PLACE FOR INSTITUTION, CONTAINER FOR CONTENT, AUTHOR FOR WORK, etc.), most everyday tokens slot in.

**[COMMUNITY KNOWLEDGE / PRELIMINARY]**: The empirical base is thin. Most of what is known about metonymy-in-autism comes from a small handful of studies. Claims stronger than "metonymy comprehension seems to track vocabulary" should be treated as working hypotheses, not established findings.

### 1.6 Metonymy as a Rhetorical and Manipulative Move

Reference-shifting is not politically neutral. The choice of which vehicle to use for which target is a choice about **where to locate agency**.

Consider the difference between:

- **"The company denies wrongdoing."** (INSTITUTION — diffuse agency)
- **"The CEO denies wrongdoing."** (INDIVIDUAL — concentrated agency)
- **"Their lawyers deny wrongdoing."** (ROLE — buffered agency)

All three can be reporting the same underlying event. They imply very different things about who is responsible.

Common manipulative uses of metonymy (cross-link to the manipulation sub-hub — see `manipulation-hub-pattern-pragmatic-packaging-invalidation.md`):

- **Institution-for-person to deflect accountability.** "The department made a decision" rather than "Susan made a decision." Useful when Susan wants to avoid being named; deceptive when Susan wants credit for the parts that worked and anonymity for the parts that did not.
- **Place-for-institution to depersonalize conflict.** "Washington is broken" is cleaner-sounding than "specific senators, lobbyists, and executive officials are doing specific things I disapprove of."
- **Family-as-unit to override individuals.** "The family will never recover" treats a family as a single experiencing subject when the actual dispute is between two of its members, and often between the abuser and the person naming the abuse.
- **Nation-as-actor in war coverage.** "Vietnam bombed Cambodia" is a metonymy that hides the individuals and institutions making decisions. Lakoff (2003) "Metaphor and War, Again" argues that this class of metonymies does substantial political work.

**For autistic readers:** metonymy is also a **protective tool**. Saying "management decided to end the project" is often safer, in a professional context, than saying "Tom decided to end the project," even when Tom is the only decider. The institutional framing is conventionally accepted as discreet. You are not lying when you use it; you are using a conventional reference device. Knowing when the community accepts which framing is a learnable pragmatic skill.

See also: Cat 23 Framing (Lakoff's political framing work) for how metonymy and metaphor combine in discourse-level framing effects.

## PART 2: CONCEPTUAL METAPHOR THEORY, IN DEPTH

### 2.1 The Core Claim

Lakoff & Johnson (1980) *Metaphors We Live By* (University of Chicago Press) made a claim that, at the time, was radical: **metaphor is primarily a matter of thought, not of language.** Linguistic metaphors are surface evidence of underlying conceptual mappings. We reason about abstract domains by systematically mapping them onto concrete source domains we already understand.

Four sub-claims follow:

1. **Metaphor is conceptual, not linguistic.** When you say "our relationship is at a crossroads," the word "crossroads" is a symptom. The underlying conceptual mapping is LOVE IS A JOURNEY. That mapping is what produced the linguistic expression, and it can produce many more ("we've come a long way," "we're stuck," "we need to go our separate ways," "we're spinning our wheels").
2. **Metaphor is systematic.** A single conceptual mapping licenses a large family of expressions. You do not learn each expression separately; you learn the mapping and the expressions fall out.
3. **Metaphor is largely unconscious.** Native speakers use dozens of conceptual metaphors per conversation without noticing. The mapping is automatic; only the linguistic output reaches awareness.
4. **Metaphor is often embodied.** Many conceptual mappings have concrete experiential roots. UP is associated with GOOD partly because standing upright is associated with health, alertness, and power, while lying down is associated with sleep, illness, and death. The metaphor is not arbitrary — it is grounded in bodily experience.

This was a break from the older view that metaphor is a rhetorical ornament applied to otherwise literal thought. If Lakoff & Johnson are right, abstract thought itself runs on metaphor, and the question "what is the literal meaning?" is the wrong question to ask for large swaths of everyday language.

### 2.2 Canonical Examples

Below is an inventory of the conceptual metaphors most commonly discussed in the Lakoff-Johnson literature, with surface linguistic evidence for each. The point of the inventory is to make the systematicity visible: each mapping produces many expressions, and knowing the mapping lets you predict new ones.

#### TIME IS MONEY / TIME IS A RESOURCE

*"You're wasting my time." "This will save you time." "I've invested a lot of time in her." "You're running out of time." "You need to budget your time." "He's living on borrowed time." "Is that worth your while?" "Do you have enough time to spare?" "I don't have the time to give you." "That cost me an hour."*

The linguistic evidence is overwhelming and systematic: time is treated as a limited commodity that can be spent, wasted, saved, invested, given, stolen, and run out of. The mapping is so entrenched in modern English that it feels literal.

#### ARGUMENT IS WAR

*"Your claims are indefensible." "He attacked every weak point in my argument." "Her criticisms were right on target." "I demolished his argument." "I've never won an argument with him." "You disagree? OK, shoot." "If you use that strategy, he'll wipe you out." "He shot down all of my arguments."*

Lakoff & Johnson's famous observation: we do not just talk about arguments in terms of war. We **experience** arguments as war. We win and lose. We see our interlocutors as opponents. We plan strategies, attack positions, defend our own. The mapping organizes the activity itself, not just how we describe it.

#### LIFE IS A JOURNEY

*"He's gone through a lot in life." "I'm at a crossroads." "He's at a dead end." "She's on her way up." "He's taking a detour." "You're going nowhere." "She's lost her way." "I'm sure he'll find his path." "He's lost." "This is a milestone."*

LIFE IS A JOURNEY is a complex mapping: the traveler is the person, the destination is the life goal, the obstacles are difficulties, the path is the course of life, the crossroads are major decisions. Once you know the mapping, a sentence like "I'm at a crossroads" immediately decodes as "I am facing a decision whose alternatives lead to significantly different life courses."

#### LOVE IS A JOURNEY

*"Look how far we've come." "We're at a crossroads." "We'll just have to go our separate ways." "We can't turn back now." "I don't think this relationship is going anywhere." "Where are we?" "We're stuck." "It's been a long, bumpy road." "This relationship is off the track." "The marriage is on the rocks." "We may have to bail out of this relationship."*

This is a **specialization** of LIFE IS A JOURNEY — a subcase where the travelers are two people, the vehicle is the relationship, the destination is shared goals, and stuckness is a stalled relationship. Once the mapping is named, the expressions are predictable and generative.

#### UNDERSTANDING IS SEEING (and KNOWING IS SEEING)

*"I see what you mean." "That's a clear explanation." "The idea finally clicked into focus." "It's murky to me." "He gave me a clearer picture." "I'm in the dark about this." "Can you shed some light on the problem?" "That argument doesn't hold water — oh wait, wrong metaphor — that argument is not transparent." "It's opaque to me." "I've been enlightened."*

UNDERSTANDING IS SEEING is a near-universal mapping (Sweetser 1990 *From Etymology to Pragmatics*) — across very many unrelated languages, the vocabulary for mental understanding comes from the vocabulary for vision. The cross-linguistic regularity is strong evidence that this is not a quirk of English.

#### IMPORTANT IS BIG / MORE IS UP / GOOD IS UP

*"A **big** deal." "A **huge** problem." "A **massive** achievement." "The **weight** of responsibility." "She's a **heavyweight** in the field."*

*"**Rising** crime rates." "My income **went up**." "The temperature is **falling**." "The number of books I read **keeps going up**." "**More** is **up**."*

*"I'm feeling **up** today." "I'm feeling **down**." "My spirits **rose**." "She's **high** on life." "He's at the **peak** of his career." "She **fell** into a depression." "That was an **uplifting** experience."*

Orientational mappings of this kind are the clearest case for embodiment. MORE IS UP corresponds to the physical fact that adding more of a substance to a container raises its level. HAPPY IS UP corresponds to the postural fact that happy people stand tall and sad people slump. These correlations, repeated many times in daily experience, appear to ground the conceptual mapping.

#### THEORIES ARE BUILDINGS

*"Is that the **foundation** for your theory?" "The theory needs more **support**." "The argument is **shaky**." "We need to **buttress** the theory with solid arguments." "The theory will **stand or fall** on the strength of that argument." "We need to **construct** a strong argument for that." "I haven't figured out yet what the **form** of the argument will be." "Here are some more facts to **shore up** the theory." "We need to **buttress** the theory with solid arguments." "The theory will **collapse** under the weight of counterexamples."*

THEORIES ARE BUILDINGS is partial — you map foundations, support, strength, and structural integrity, but not (say) windows or porches. Lakoff & Johnson call this partial mapping; only parts of the source domain are used, and which parts depend on the target domain's own structure.

#### IDEAS ARE FOOD

*"**Food** for thought." "I can't **digest** that argument." "I'm **hungry** for knowledge." "He **devoured** the book." "That's **half-baked**." "Let me **chew on** that for a while." "She **spoon-fed** us the information." "That's a **raw** idea." "This is a **meaty** paper." "Let me **stew** on it."*

IDEAS ARE FOOD is productive in English, less so in some other languages. Kövecses (2005) *Metaphor in Culture* argues that such variation is real and patterned: the primary mappings tend to be stable across cultures, but which source domains get elaborated into rich systems varies.

### 2.3 Three Families: Structural, Orientational, Ontological

Lakoff & Johnson (1980) distinguish three types of conceptual metaphor. The distinction is heuristic, not absolute — most real metaphors blend types — but it is a useful organizing frame.

#### Structural metaphors

One concept is **structured** in terms of another. The source domain's internal organization is projected onto the target.

- ARGUMENT IS WAR (attackers, defenders, strategies, positions)
- TIME IS MONEY (quantifiable, spendable, savable)
- LIFE IS A JOURNEY (travelers, paths, destinations, obstacles)
- THEORIES ARE BUILDINGS (foundations, support, structure, collapse)
- IDEAS ARE FOOD (raw, cooked, digestible, nourishing)

Structural metaphors are the richest, the most productive, and the ones that do the most cognitive work. They license whole inferential patterns. Because ARGUMENT IS WAR is active in English, a speaker who is told they have made an argument can be told "I'll destroy you in the debate" and the inference is automatic.

#### Orientational metaphors

Concepts are organized along **spatial schemas**. These tend to come in clusters and to be highly embodied.

- HAPPY IS UP, SAD IS DOWN ("I'm feeling up / down")
- CONSCIOUS IS UP, UNCONSCIOUS IS DOWN ("wake up / fall asleep")
- HEALTH IS UP, SICKNESS IS DOWN ("in top shape / she came down with the flu")
- HAVING CONTROL IS UP ("I'm on top of the situation / she's under my control")
- MORE IS UP, LESS IS DOWN ("prices rose / fell")
- GOOD IS UP, BAD IS DOWN ("things are looking up / hit rock bottom")
- VIRTUE IS UP, DEPRAVITY IS DOWN ("high-minded / low-down trick")
- RATIONAL IS UP, EMOTIONAL IS DOWN ("rise above your emotions / he couldn't rise above his own pettiness")
- FUTURE IS AHEAD, PAST IS BEHIND ("looking forward / that's behind us")

Orientational metaphors tend to be consistent: UP is consistently the positive pole across almost all of these, which is why they cluster so neatly. The claim is that this consistency is not an accident. Lakoff & Johnson argue that it reflects a coherent embodied experience in which uprightness and verticality are correlated with a stable set of positive outcomes.

#### Ontological metaphors

Abstract entities are treated as **substances, objects, or persons**. This is the most basic and probably the most ubiquitous kind of metaphor; it tends to go unnoticed precisely because it is so fundamental.

- **Events as objects**: "That was a great **game**." (The game is treated as a container or object.)
- **Activities as substances**: "There is **a lot of running** in that game."
- **States as containers**: "He is **in love**." "She is **out of trouble**."
- **The mind as a machine**: "My mind just isn't **operating** today." "I'm a little **rusty** today." "We've been **working** on this problem all day and now we're **running out of steam**."
- **The mind as a brittle object**: "He **cracked** under pressure." "She is easily **crushed**." "His ego is very **fragile**."
- **Inflation as an entity**: "Inflation is **lowering** our standard of living." "We need to **combat** inflation." "Inflation **backed us into a corner**."

**Personification** is the limiting case of ontological metaphor, where an abstract entity is treated specifically as a person: "Inflation has robbed me of my savings." "Cancer took my father." "Life has been kind to her."

### 2.4 Mappings and the Invariance Principle

A conceptual metaphor is a **mapping** — a structured correspondence between elements of the source domain and elements of the target domain. For LOVE IS A JOURNEY:

| Source: Journey | Target: Love |
|---|---|
| Travelers | The lovers |
| Vehicle | The relationship |
| Route / path | Shared life trajectory |
| Destination | Shared goals |
| Obstacles | Difficulties in the relationship |
| Crossroads | Decision points |
| Dead end | Irresolvable difficulty |
| Going separate ways | Breaking up |

This is not a list of associated expressions. It is a **correspondence**: each element on the left corresponds to a specific element on the right. Once the correspondence is in place, linguistic expressions are derivative. "We're at a crossroads" is not a separately learned idiom; it is the automatic output of the mapping applied to a particular journey-element.

#### The invariance principle

Lakoff (1990, 1993) proposed the **invariance principle**: the topology — the image-schematic structure — of the source domain is preserved in the mapping onto the target, as long as the target's own structure is consistent with it. That is, source-domain structure projects onto the target, but not in a way that overrides the target's intrinsic structure.

Why this matters:

- **Predictive power.** Once you know a conceptual metaphor is operating, you can predict linguistic expressions that have not been said yet, and those predictions will usually be accepted as natural. For autistic readers with strong pattern-recognition profiles, this is a major advantage: metaphor becomes generative rather than memorizable.
- **Constraints on interpretation.** The mapping is not "anything goes." If someone says "our relationship is at a crossroads," the listener knows the expression is about the shared trajectory, not about literal intersections. The mapping constrains the search space.
- **Systematic variation.** Different source domains map onto the same target with different consequences. LOVE IS A JOURNEY and LOVE IS WAR are both available in English, and which one a speaker activates frames the relationship differently (cooperative travelers vs. opposing forces).

### 2.5 Primary Metaphors and the Neural Theory

Grady (1997) *Foundations of Meaning: Primary Metaphors and Primary Scenes* (UC Berkeley PhD dissertation, published and summarized in subsequent work) proposed the concept of **primary metaphor**: a minimal conceptual mapping that directly pairs a subjective experience with a sensorimotor experience that tends to co-occur with it.

Grady's primary metaphors are small, atomic, and often near-universal across languages. They are the building blocks out of which the larger, culturally elaborated metaphors of Lakoff-Johnson are assembled.

| Primary metaphor | Subjective experience | Sensorimotor source |
|---|---|---|
| **AFFECTION IS WARMTH** | Feeling affection | Being held |
| **IMPORTANT IS BIG** | Judging importance | Children's experience of large things being significant |
| **MORE IS UP** | Having more | Rising levels in containers |
| **KNOWING IS SEEING** | Knowing | Getting visual information |
| **UNDERSTANDING IS GRASPING** | Understanding | Manipulating objects |
| **DIFFICULTIES ARE BURDENS** | Experiencing difficulty | Carrying heavy objects |
| **CATEGORIES ARE CONTAINERS** | Category membership | Containment in a space |
| **LINEAR SCALES ARE PATHS** | Progression on a scale | Physical motion along a path |
| **BAD IS STINKY** | Disgust/disapproval | Smelling something foul |
| **HAPPY IS UP** | Feeling good | Upright posture when healthy |
| **TIME IS MOTION** | Time passing | Moving through space |
| **SIMILARITY IS CLOSENESS** | Finding things alike | Spatial proximity |
| **CHANGE IS MOTION** | State change | Physical motion |

Each primary metaphor is hypothesized to arise during early childhood through the **repeated co-occurrence** of the two experiences. A child who is held experiences warmth and affection simultaneously, many times a day, for years. On Lakoff's Neural Theory of Metaphor (Lakoff 2008, 2014), this co-activation is hypothesized to become encoded as a stable neural association. When the child later reasons about affection, the warmth network is activated alongside; when the child later reasons about warmth, the affection network is activated alongside.

The claim that primary metaphors are near-universal has empirical support: Kövecses (2005) and others have shown that a core set of primary metaphors recurs across unrelated languages, while the elaborated structural metaphors (TIME IS MONEY, ARGUMENT IS WAR) are more culturally variable. See §2.6.

**For autistic readers**: the primary-metaphor framework is particularly useful because it makes explicit the experiential grounds of mappings that would otherwise seem arbitrary. If someone says "that was a weighty issue," the mapping DIFFICULTIES ARE BURDENS makes the expression predictable rather than mysterious. The metaphor is not a decorative choice; it is a direct projection of a physical experience you have had many times.

### 2.6 Cultural Variation

Kövecses (2005) *Metaphor in Culture: Universality and Variation* (Cambridge University Press) is the standard treatment of how conceptual metaphor varies across cultures. The core findings:

1. **Primary metaphors are largely universal**, or at least very widely shared, because they are grounded in universal embodied experience.
2. **Elaborated structural metaphors are culturally variable.** TIME IS MONEY is intelligible in most modern industrial societies but organizes time concepts much more thoroughly in English-speaking corporate cultures than in, say, traditional Hopi (as Whorf observed, though with caveats that have been debated since).
3. **The same source domain can map onto different targets in different cultures.** The HEART is a source for emotion in English and many European languages; in Chinese, the 心 xīn (heart-mind) is a source for both emotion and thought. In some cultures, other organs (liver, gut, kidneys) are the seat of specific emotions. See Yu (1995) for Chinese.
4. **The same target can draw on different source domains.** ANGER is conceptualized as HEAT IN A CONTAINER in English ("he boiled over," "I blew my top") but with different salient sources in Japanese (Matsuki 1995) and Zulu (Taylor & Mbense 1998).

#### Autism-relevant implication

For an autistic reader navigating cross-cultural or cross-neurotype communication, the **universal vs. culture-specific** distinction is a usable diagnostic. Primary metaphors are more likely to land. Culturally elaborated metaphors may not. When a metaphor feels confusing in a cross-cultural context, the question to ask is: "Is this a primary mapping I would expect anywhere, or is it a cultural elaboration that may not be portable?"

This generalizes to the double-empathy problem (Milton 2012, *Disability & Society* 27(6):883–887). If metaphors — especially the elaborated, pragmatically loaded ones — are not universal even across cultures, the idea that autistic and non-autistic speakers should automatically share all the same conceptual metaphors is already shaky. Mismatches are a form of cross-cultural communication, not a deficit.

### 2.7 Conceptual Metaphor and Abstract Reasoning

If conceptual metaphor is really foundational to abstract thought, we should find that changing the metaphor changes the thought. Two lines of experimental work bear on this.

#### Boroditsky on time and space

Lera Boroditsky and colleagues have run a long series of experiments showing that the mappings people use for TIME carry over into how they reason about temporal situations. A representative finding: if you prime English speakers with a spatial schema of "ego moving forward through time" (the metaphor implicit in "we're approaching the deadline"), they give different answers to ambiguous temporal questions than if you prime them with "time moving toward ego" ("the deadline is approaching us"). See Boroditsky (2000) *Cognition* and subsequent work.

Across languages: speakers of Mandarin Chinese tend to represent time vertically more than English speakers do, consistent with the way Mandarin uses vertical spatial terms for time (shàng yuè "last/upper month," xià yuè "next/lower month"). The linguistic metaphor and the cognitive representation appear to track each other.

#### Thibodeau & Boroditsky on crime framing

Thibodeau & Boroditsky (2011) *PLOS ONE* "Metaphors We Think With: The Role of Metaphor in Reasoning" ran a now-famous series of experiments on policy judgment. Participants read a brief passage about rising crime in a fictional city. In one condition, crime was described as "a beast preying on the city." In the other, crime was described as "a virus infecting the city." Everything else was held constant. Participants were then asked what the city should do about the problem.

**Findings:**

- The BEAST condition produced systematically more enforcement-oriented proposals: catching, caging, and punishing criminals.
- The VIRUS condition produced systematically more social-reform proposals: investigating root causes, treating the community, reducing poverty.
- The effect was about **20 percentage points** — larger than the effect of participants' own political affiliation.
- Participants did **not notice** that they were being influenced by the metaphor. When asked to justify their policy preferences, they pointed to facts and numbers, not to the framing.
- Follow-up work (Thibodeau & Boroditsky 2013) found the effect was robust when the metaphor was introduced early and was load-bearing in the passage, and less so when the metaphor was a late, decorative addition.

The implication for Cat 11 is striking: conceptual metaphors are not decoration. They are load-bearing for abstract thought. The metaphor you activate in someone's head measurably changes their policy conclusions, and they do not realize it is happening. This is one reason conceptual metaphor is also a node in the manipulation sub-hub.

### 2.8 Conceptual Metaphor in Autism: Beyond the Deficit Frame

The older literature on "autism and metaphor" tends to approach metaphor as a single skill that autistic people either have or lack. This framing has been substantially undercut by more recent work.

#### Kalandadze et al. (2018): the language-matching finding

As summarized in the main Cat 11 document, Kalandadze et al. (2018) *Autism* meta-analyzed 41 studies and found that when groups are matched for language ability, figurative language differences between autistic and typically developing groups shrink dramatically. Metaphor-specific findings within this meta-analysis were moderated by the same factor: language ability, not autism status, did most of the work. See `category-11-nonliteral-research-autism.md` for the detailed numbers.

#### Kasirer & Mashal (2014): autistic people generate MORE novel metaphors

Kasirer & Mashal (2014) "Verbal creativity in autism: comprehension and generation of metaphoric language in high-functioning autism spectrum disorder and typical development," *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience* 8:615, is the canonical study challenging the "literal-minded" stereotype in the generation direction.

Design: 17 adults with ASD (mean age 21.06) and 17 typically developing adults (mean age 22.71). A multiple-choice questionnaire tested comprehension of conventional and novel metaphors. A sentence-completion questionnaire tested **generation** of metaphoric language.

**Findings:**

- Comprehension of conventional and novel metaphors was **similar** in both groups. The "autistic people can't understand metaphor" claim simply failed to replicate in this adult, high-functioning sample.
- Generation differed in the **opposite** direction from the stereotype: adults with ASD **generated more creative metaphors** than typically developing controls.

**How to read this in terms of conceptual metaphor theory**: if metaphor is a systematic mapping, then generating novel metaphors requires the ability to construct new cross-domain correspondences. Kasirer & Mashal's result says autistic adults do this at least as well as non-autistic adults, and in some conditions better. That is more consistent with the "metaphor as systematic conceptual mapping" framework than with the "metaphor as social inference" framework.

#### Morsanyi, Stamenković & Holyoak (2020)

Morsanyi, Stamenković & Holyoak (2020) conducted two meta-analyses that, together, clarify the picture considerably:

1. *Analogical reasoning in autism: A systematic review and meta-analysis*. Finding: autistic people perform at the level of age- and IQ-matched controls on nonverbal analogy tasks, and autistic people with learning difficulties **outperform** matched controls in some studies. Analogical reasoning, as measured by nonverbal tasks, appears to be spared in autism.
2. *Metaphor processing in autism: A systematic review and meta-analysis* (published in *Developmental Review*). Finding: overall reduced accuracy and slower responding on metaphor tasks for autistic vs. TD groups matched for verbal IQ and chronological age, with effect sizes that were substantially moderated by task properties and verbal IQ.

The **dissociation** matters: nonverbal analogy is spared, but linguistic metaphor tasks show reduced accuracy. Morsanyi et al. argue this suggests that the difficulty is in the **language system** engaged by the task, not in the underlying analogical-mapping machinery. If that is right, then the "autistic people can't think in metaphor" claim is clearly wrong — what is harder is performing well on specific verbal tasks, not constructing cross-domain mappings as such.

#### Pragmatic implication: metaphor as process, not possession

The literal/metaphorical dichotomy has been criticized (Gibbs 1994 *The Poetics of Mind*; Giora 1997 on graded salience) as too coarse. Comprehension is graded, effortful, and depends on context and familiarity. Autistic readers may be better characterized as processing metaphor **more effortfully** and **more systematically**, not as failing to process it.

The practical upshot: if you can learn which conceptual metaphors are operating in your linguistic community, you can **decode** them deliberately, **predict** new expressions, and **generate** your own. This is pattern recognition applied to abstract thought. For pattern-rich autistic cognitive profiles, it is closer to a strength than a weakness, once the framing is right.

#### Connecting to the double-empathy frame

Milton (2012) "On the ontological status of autism: the 'double empathy problem'," *Disability & Society* 27(6):883–887, argues that difficulties between autistic and non-autistic speakers are mutual — a mismatch of conventions, not a deficit in one party. Conceptual metaphor provides a good test case. If autistic and non-autistic speakers sometimes draw on partly different elaborated metaphor systems (while sharing the primary ones), then miscommunication is structurally analogous to the miscommunication between speakers of different cultural variants of the same language. Neither side is "wrong." Both are working from locally coherent systems. The repair strategy is translation, not remediation.

## PART 3: METAPHOR-METONYMY INTERACTION (METAPHTONYMY)

### 3.1 Why the Distinction Blurs in Practice

In principle, metaphor and metonymy are distinct: metaphor is cross-domain mapping, metonymy is within-domain reference-shifting. In practice, many real expressions involve both, and the distinction is a matter of analysis rather than a matter of which cognitive operation the speaker performed.

Goossens (1990) *Cognitive Linguistics* 1(3):323–342, "Metaphtonymy: The interaction of metaphor and metonymy in expressions for linguistic action," is the foundational analysis of these mixed cases. He coined the term **metaphtonymy** and identified several patterns:

- **Metonymy within metaphor.** A metonymic component is embedded inside a metaphorical mapping.
- **Metaphor from metonymy.** A metaphor originates historically from a metonymy that has become entrenched and then extended.
- **Metaphor within metonymy** and **metonymy from metaphor** (less common, sometimes disputed).

### 3.2 Worked Examples

#### "Lend me your ears" (Mark Antony, *Julius Caesar*)

- **Metonym layer:** EAR FOR HEARING / EAR FOR ATTENTION (part-for-whole, or organ-for-function).
- **Metaphor layer:** ATTENTION IS A LENDABLE OBJECT (ontological metaphor treating attention as a physical thing that can be transferred).

The sentence is simultaneously metonymic (ears stand for attention) and metaphorical (attention is treated as a lendable object). Neither layer alone gives the full interpretation.

#### "She shouted her head off"

- **Metonym layer:** HEAD FOR LIFE / HEAD FOR VITALITY (a classical part-for-whole).
- **Metaphor layer:** INTENSE EMOTIONAL ACTIVITY IS PHYSICAL DAMAGE (ontological/structural).

The expression "shout one's head off" is a paradigm case of Goossens's metaphtonymy: the head is metonymically engaged as the organ of speech and life, and the metaphor of losing the head encodes intensity.

#### "Get a grip"

- **Metonym layer:** GRIP FOR PHYSICAL CONTROL (instrument for action).
- **Metaphor layer:** UNDERSTANDING IS GRASPING / EMOTIONAL CONTROL IS PHYSICAL CONTROL (primary metaphor extended to the emotional domain).

"Get a grip" works because both layers are active: a grip is the physical metonym for control, and emotional control is mapped from physical control via a primary metaphor.

#### "The White House denied the allegations"

- **Metonym layer:** PLACE FOR INSTITUTION (the building stands for the executive branch).
- **Metaphor layer:** INSTITUTIONS ARE PEOPLE (ontological metaphor — institutions are personified as agents who deny, announce, warn).

Nearly every institutional-agency news sentence in English is a metaphtonymic stack: metonymy delivers the vehicle for the institution; personification metaphor delivers the action.

#### "She has a sharp tongue"

- **Metonym layer:** TONGUE FOR SPEECH (instrument for action).
- **Metaphor layer:** WORDS ARE WEAPONS / HURTFUL SPEECH IS SHARPNESS (structural metaphor for verbal hostility).

### 3.3 Radden (2000)

Radden (2000) "How metonymic are metaphors?" (in Barcelona ed., *Metaphor and Metonymy at the Crossroads*) argues that many conventional conceptual metaphors have metonymic roots. HAPPY IS UP, for example, can be analyzed as originating in a metonymy in which upright posture (a part of the experiential situation of being healthy/happy) stands for the whole happy state, and then becoming generalized into a cross-domain mapping. On this view, the line between metaphor and metonymy is not a sharp category boundary but a cline from tightly-contiguous mappings (classic metonymy) to loosely-contiguous mappings (classic metaphor), with lots of mixed cases in between.

### 3.4 Implications for Autistic Readers

Two practical points:

1. **Many opaque expressions become transparent once both layers are named.** "Shout one's head off" is weird if you process only the metaphor layer. It becomes parseable once you notice the metonym (HEAD FOR LIFE/VITALITY) sitting beneath the metaphor.
2. **The distinction matters for which decoding move to reach for.** If the core is metonymic, ask "what is the associated item in the same ICM?" If the core is metaphorical, ask "what is the source domain being mapped from?" If both, do both.

## PART 4: APPLICATION FOR THE AUTISTIC READER

### 4.1 Reading Conceptual Metaphor in Everyday English

A simple procedure for noticing conceptual metaphors in real speech:

1. **Spot a surface expression that is not literally true.** "I'm at a crossroads." "Their argument collapsed." "We're running out of time."
2. **Ask what conceptual mapping would make it sensible.** For "crossroads," the mapping is LIFE IS A JOURNEY. For "collapsed argument," the mapping is THEORIES ARE BUILDINGS. For "running out of time," the mapping is TIME IS A RESOURCE.
3. **Check whether other expressions from the same mapping are present in the surrounding text.** If LIFE IS A JOURNEY is active, expect path, direction, detour, stuck, destination. If you see clusters, the mapping is live in this discourse.
4. **Decode the inference, not just the word.** "At a crossroads" is not just "facing a choice." It is "facing a choice where the alternatives lead to significantly different continuations, and where going back is not easy." The inference structure comes from the source domain (journeys).
5. **Be alert to mixed mappings.** Discourse often shifts metaphors without marking the shift. "We've hit a wall, but we'll find another path" mixes THEORIES ARE BUILDINGS with LIFE IS A JOURNEY. Speakers usually do not notice the mix.

### 4.2 When Surface Metaphors Conceal Concept Attacks

Conceptual metaphors can be weaponized. Once a metaphor is in place, its inferential structure is imported whether or not the speaker notices. Some examples:

- **"The welfare state is a disease that needs to be cured."** The DISEASE IS ENEMY mapping smuggles in a set of inferences: diseases are bad, diseases must be eradicated, diseases spread, people who defend a disease are irrational. If you accept the metaphor you are halfway to accepting a policy conclusion.
- **"Illegal immigration is a flood."** The FLOOD metaphor imports uncontrollability, damage, emergency response, and the need for barriers. Compare "migration is a flow" (neutral) or "migration is a human movement" (foregrounds people). Each metaphor pre-loads different policy inferences.
- **"Your body is a temple."** The TEMPLE metaphor imports reverence, purity, moral contamination. It is used to shame specific food, sexual, or drug choices. The alternative — "your body is yours" — imports autonomy instead.
- **"The market is a living thing."** The MARKET IS AN ORGANISM metaphor makes markets sound deserving of protection from "interference" (a word that already presupposes the metaphor). A deliberate counter-metaphor might be "the market is a tool that we designed."
- **"Crime is a beast."** See §2.7. This metaphor alone, introduced early and repeated, measurably shifted policy preferences toward enforcement in Thibodeau & Boroditsky's experiments.

The autism-relevant skill is not "resist all metaphor" (that is impossible; everyone reasons in metaphors). It is "notice which metaphor is active, notice what it smuggles in, and ask whether a different metaphor would change the conclusion." If a policy argument depends on the metaphor, the policy argument is weaker than it looks.

This connects directly to Lakoff's political framing work. See Cat 23 Framing (`category-23-framing.md` or the relevant hub) for the extended treatment. Lakoff (2004) *Don't Think of an Elephant!* is the accessible introduction; Lakoff (1996/2002) *Moral Politics* is the full theoretical treatment.

### 4.3 Metonymy as a Protective Tool

Earlier sections described how metonymy can deflect accountability. The other side of the coin: **metonymy gives you socially accepted ways to name practices without naming individuals**. In many professional, family, and community contexts, this is a protective resource, not a dishonest one.

- **"The department decided."** Lets you report a decision without identifying the decider, which can be safer when the decider has more institutional power than you do.
- **"Management has concerns."** Conventionally understood as "someone in a supervisory position has said something you should take seriously," without pinning the concern to a specific supervisor. This is often the register the supervisor themselves prefers.
- **"Someone in the family is struggling."** Lets you reference a family member's difficulty without disclosing who, which can preserve both the referent's privacy and your own safety.
- **"Old friends have mentioned..."** Lets you report information without committing the source.

These are metonymic moves (INSTITUTION FOR PERSON, ROLE FOR PERSON, GROUP FOR MEMBER). They are conventional, accepted, and often expected in professional and family discourse. Knowing when to reach for them is a pragmatic skill, not a form of lying.

**Caveat.** The same move that protects you can also be used to manipulate you. "The family is worried about you" may be a metonymic softening of "I am worried about you," or it may be a way to present one person's anxiety as a collective verdict when it is not. Ask: "Who specifically is worried?" If the answer is "well, I am" — you have a single-person concern. If the answer is "your uncle and your cousin and your mother all told me separately," you have an actual collective. The metonymy does not tell you which.

See `manipulation-hub-pattern-pragmatic-packaging-invalidation.md` for how institution-metonymies can be used to launder individual judgments into collective verdicts.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Metonymy and metaphor are different cognitive operations.** Metonymy is within-domain reference-shifting; metaphor is cross-domain mapping. Both are systematic, both are pervasive, neither is decorative.

2. **Metonymy is rule-rich and pattern-learnable.** The major patterns (PLACE FOR INSTITUTION, CONTAINER FOR CONTENT, AUTHOR FOR WORK, etc.) form a small inventory that covers most everyday tokens. For pattern-oriented readers this is close to a best-case domain.

3. **Conceptual metaphor is a system of thought, not a figure of speech.** Lakoff & Johnson's (1980) core claim is that we reason about abstract domains by mapping them onto concrete source domains. The linguistic metaphors are surface evidence; the mappings are doing the work.

4. **Metaphors come in three structural families.** Structural (ARGUMENT IS WAR), orientational (HAPPY IS UP), ontological (INFLATION IS AN ENTITY). Each does different conceptual work; most real thought combines all three.

5. **Primary metaphors are the atomic building blocks.** Grady (1997) proposed that basic, experientially grounded mappings (AFFECTION IS WARMTH, MORE IS UP, KNOWING IS SEEING, IMPORTANT IS BIG) are near-universal. Elaborated metaphors are culturally variable compositions built from primary parts.

6. **Conceptual metaphors are load-bearing for reasoning.** Thibodeau & Boroditsky (2011) showed that crime framed as a beast vs. a virus produced measurably different policy preferences, independent of political affiliation and without participants noticing the effect.

7. **Autism research does not support a deficit view of metaphor as a system.** Kalandadze et al. (2018) showed that language-matched groups show shrunk or absent differences. Kasirer & Mashal (2014) showed that autistic adults generate more creative novel metaphors than non-autistic adults. Morsanyi, Stamenković & Holyoak (2020) showed that nonverbal analogy is spared. The difficulty, where it exists, is in specific verbal task performance, not in cross-domain conceptual mapping.

8. **Metonymy in autism tracks vocabulary.** Rundblad & Annaz (2010) found that autistic children's metonymy comprehension was delayed but not impaired relative to receptive vocabulary. This is a much weaker claim than "autistic people can't do metonymy," and it is compatible with a pattern-learning approach.

9. **Metaphor and metonymy interact (metaphtonymy).** Many real expressions involve both layers. Goossens (1990) showed that recognizing the layers clarifies otherwise opaque expressions. "Lend me your ears" is EAR FOR ATTENTION (metonymy) plus ATTENTION IS A LENDABLE OBJECT (metaphor).

10. **Conceptual metaphors can be weaponized — and named.** "Crime is a beast," "immigration is a flood," "the welfare state is a disease" are concept attacks that smuggle policy conclusions into apparently neutral descriptions. Naming the metaphor defuses it. This is a core skill for pragmatic self-defense and cross-links to the manipulation sub-hub.

11. **Metonymy can be a protective tool.** Institutional and role-based metonymies let you report information without pinning it to individuals, which is often the register-appropriate, safety-preserving move. The same tool can also be used to launder individual judgments into collective verdicts — noticing the difference is the skill.

12. **Double empathy applies here too.** Conceptual metaphor is culturally (and plausibly neurotype-) variable. When communication breaks down, the repair strategy is translation between locally coherent systems, not remediation of a deficit (Milton 2012).
