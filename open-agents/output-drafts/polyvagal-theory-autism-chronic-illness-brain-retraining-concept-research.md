# Concept Research: Polyvagal Theory in Chronic Illness & "Brain Retraining"

## Overview

The vocabulary of polyvagal theory (PVT) has migrated into one of the most consequential venues in 21st-century medicine: the chronic-illness landscape — long COVID, myalgic encephalomyelitis/chronic fatigue syndrome (ME/CFS), postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome (POTS), and the broader dysautonomia spectrum. Two things are simultaneously true and must be held together. First, **autonomic dysfunction in these conditions is real, documented, and measurable**, and there is a legitimate scientific overlap with the autonomic-physiology layer of PVT (the parts of PVT that don't depend on its contested phylogenetic claims). Second, **a commercial "brain retraining" industry has built large paying programs on top of polyvagal/neuroplasticity language** — the Gupta Program, Dynamic Neural Retraining System (DNRS), Primal Trust, and the Lightning Process — claiming to cure or substantially improve ME/CFS, long COVID, fibromyalgia, mast cell activation syndrome (MCAS), multiple chemical sensitivities, and other complex chronic illnesses, on an evidence base that is generally thin, methodologically weak, and developed largely by the program developers themselves.

This category sits at a particularly important intersection for an autistic adult with burnout or chronic-illness overlap features. Autism, joint hypermobility, hEDS (hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos syndrome), POTS, and ME/CFS-spectrum fatigue cluster together in well-documented ways. Csecs et al. 2022 found 51% generalized joint hypermobility in a neurodivergent sample versus 20% in the general population (OR 4.51, 95% CI 2.17–9.37), and the same population reported nearly 5× the orthostatic intolerance symptoms (mean 24.2 vs 5.1). Many autistic adults will encounter brain-retraining programs through their chronic-illness comorbidities, often via TikTok, Instagram, podcast wellness content, or other neurodivergent users who have tried them. The user's question — does this work, is it safe, is it for me? — needs an honest answer that neither dismisses the patient-benefit reports nor whitewashes the methodological problems and the iatrogenic-harm history.

The chapter is structured around four moves. First, establish that autonomic dysfunction in chronic illness is genuinely documented (the scientific floor). Second, identify the polyvagal-informed clinical adaptations that have independent merit (pacing, slow-paced breathing, HRV biofeedback). Third, examine the brain-retraining industry — its claims, mechanisms, evidence base, and the specific methodological problems with the studies it cites. Fourth, situate the patient-benefit reports honestly alongside the advocacy critique and historical iatrogenic-harm record (PACE trial, SMILE trial, GET surveys). The chapter closes with a concrete evaluation framework for brain-retraining claims that generalizes beyond polyvagal-branded programs.

## How It Works — The Layered Argument

### Layer 1: Autonomic dysfunction in chronic illness is documented

**Long COVID**. Multiple systematic reviews (Marques et al. 2023, Long COVID HRV review 2024) and primary studies confirm that long COVID is associated with measurable HRV abnormalities — chiefly reduced high-frequency power (parasympathetic), elevated heart rate, and increased sympathetic-vagal imbalance. The RECOVER initiative, the largest US long COVID research effort, includes a RECOVER-AUTONOMIC platform protocol (NCT06305806, NCT06305780, NCT06305793) explicitly studying autonomic dysfunction in approximately 380 long COVID participants with POTS symptoms. A 2025 JACC paper (Bisaccia et al., though more commonly the Levine group / RECOVER consortia) using quantitative autonomic testing found that long COVID patients showed abnormalities including greater heart-rate increase during tilt-table testing and more pronounced blood pressure changes than controls. A 2024 follow-up study reported persistent autonomic dysfunction in 71.9% of long COVID participants up to 3.5 years after initial SARS-CoV-2 infection, with POTS the most common diagnosis. Ståhlberg et al. (Am J Med 2021;134:1451–1456) characterized "post-COVID-19 tachycardia syndrome" as a distinct phenotype of post-acute COVID-19 syndrome. Carod-Artal (Rev Neurol 2021;72(11):384–396) reviewed dysautonomia in long COVID and noted symptoms most commonly in gastrointestinal, secretomotor, and orthostatic-intolerance domains.

**ME/CFS**. Reduced HRV in ME/CFS is a robust finding (Escorihuela et al. 2020, J Transl Med — reduced HRV predicts fatigue severity). Autonomic dysregulation is a core diagnostic feature in the International Consensus Criteria (ICC, Carruthers et al. 2011) and is included in the IOM/NAM criteria (2015) under "orthostatic intolerance." Post-exertional malaise (PEM) — the cardinal ME/CFS feature — has an autonomic signature: HRV remains depressed for 24+ hours after exertion in long COVID patients with PEM (medRxiv 2025, wearable HRV study), and the ventilatory threshold (VT1) appears to function practically as a PEM threshold. Pacing within the energy envelope (Jason et al.; American ME and CFS Society) is the consensus clinical recommendation.

**POTS**. Defined by a heart rate increase of ≥30 bpm (≥40 bpm in adolescents) within 10 minutes of standing without orthostatic hypotension. Dysautonomia International estimates 3–6 million Americans affected, 80–85% female, most commonly women of childbearing age. POTS is a heterogeneous syndrome with neuropathic, hyperadrenergic, and hypovolemic subtypes; long COVID has dramatically expanded the post-viral POTS population.

**Dysautonomia broadly**. Includes POTS, neurocardiogenic syncope, autoimmune autonomic ganglionopathy, multiple system atrophy, and other autonomic disorders. The American Autonomic Society and Dysautonomia International maintain disease registries; a 2025 Australian registry study documented substantial diagnostic delay (years) and quality-of-life impairment.

### Layer 2: The autism × chronic illness overlap is real

This is increasingly mainstream in autonomic medicine, not fringe.

- **Csecs et al. 2022** (*Frontiers in Psychiatry* 12:786916): n=109 neurodivergent (autism, ADHD, Tourette) + 57 controls. 51% of neurodivergent participants met generalized joint hypermobility criteria (Beighton ≥4) vs. ~20% general population (OR 4.51, 95% CI 2.17–9.37). Orthostatic-intolerance symptoms were nearly 5× higher in the neurodivergent group (mean 24.2 vs 5.1). The study explicitly demonstrated that joint hypermobility mediates the relationship between neurodivergence and dysautonomic symptoms.

- **Eccles et al. 2014** ("Joint Hypermobility and autonomic hyperactivity: Relevance to neurodevelopmental disorders", *Journal of Neurology, Neurosurgery & Psychiatry*): foundational paper establishing that hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos syndrome appears overrepresented in autism spectrum disorder and may predispose to orthostatic intolerance / POTS.

- **Casanova et al. 2020** (and follow-up systematic reviews 2023–2024): autism and EDS/HSD prevalence overlap at 27.9% (broad screening) to 39% (clinical assessment) in autistic samples. This is one of the most consistent comorbidity findings in autism somatic-health research.

- **Mechanistic hypotheses**: Connective-tissue variation, autonomic-nervous-system development differences, and neuroimmune factors have all been proposed. None is settled. The overlap is empirically robust; the mechanism is not.

For an autistic adult, this means: it is statistically plausible that you have or will develop overlap features. Many autistic adults discover POTS, hEDS, or ME/CFS-spectrum fatigue mid-life. The chronic-illness landscape is therefore not a separate concern from autism — it is often the same concern, in another modality.

### Layer 3: Reasonable polyvagal-informed clinical adaptations

These practices align with polyvagal framing but have independent evidence bases. The framework explaining them may be contested. The practices have merit regardless.

- **Pacing / Energy Envelope (Jason et al.)** — the consensus ME/CFS approach. The polyvagal framing of pacing as autonomic-regulation makes intuitive sense and aligns with the IACFS/ME and BACME clinical wisdom. Pacing has a strong evidence base of its own (Workwell Foundation HR-based pacing protocols, ME Association pacing guidance, NICE 2021 ME/CFS guideline).

- **Slow-paced breathing / coherence breathing (~6 breaths/min, 0.1 Hz)** — well-established to produce maximal HRV at the resonant frequency. Lehrer & Gevirtz's body of work; HRVB systematic reviews (Goessl et al. 2017, Lehrer et al. 2020) document moderate-to-strong evidence in cardiovascular disease, hypertension, pain, and PTSD; moderate in cancer; mixed in fibromyalgia. The Long COVID HEARTLOC feasibility study (Corrado et al., *J Patient Exp* 2024) and Corrado et al. *Am J Med* 2024 case-control study suggest slow-paced breathing may help long COVID dysautonomia.

- **HRV biofeedback** — independent commercial therapeutic protocol (HeartMath, Inner Balance) and the academic Lehrer/Vaschillo protocols. Has stronger evidence in pain/anxiety/PTSD than in ME/CFS specifically; in ME/CFS the evidence is weaker (mostly small unblinded trials) but the technique is low-harm.

- **Autonomic-aware activity pacing** — using HR ceiling, percent of HR-max thresholds, ventilatory threshold estimates, or PEM-window monitoring. Workwell Foundation has developed practical protocols.

- **Safety-first trauma-aware orientation** in chronic-illness care — protecting patients from medical gaslighting, recognizing the psychological cost of years of dismissal, validating symptom reports. The polyvagal vocabulary is one way to frame this, but the underlying practice has wider justification (trauma-informed medicine, patient-centered care).

The crucial distinction: these practices are reasonable in their own right. **The polyvagal framing is scaffolding, not mechanism.** This is the same distinction Cat 6 made for the broader intervention landscape.

### Layer 4: The brain-retraining industry

Four major programs are commonly encountered.

#### The Gupta Program (Amygdala and Insula Retraining)

- **Founder**: Ashok Gupta. UK-based. Self-described former ME sufferer.
- **Premise**: Chronic illness symptoms persist due to a maladaptive amygdala/insula response — an over-active "fight, flight, freeze" loop. Retraining via thought-stopping, visualization, and behavior modification interrupts the loop and resolves symptoms.
- **Cost**: Approximately $399–$599 USD for online course access. Sometimes promoted with payment plans or sponsorships.
- **Evidence cited**:
  - Gupta 2010 (*Journal of Holistic Healthcare*): small, uncontrolled, no randomization, no blinding, no comparator. Widely critiqued as inadequate.
  - **Toussaint & Bratty 2023** (*Evidence-Based Complementary and Alternative Medicine* 2023:7068326): "Amygdala and Insula Retraining (AIR) Significantly Reduces Fatigue and Increases Energy in People with Long COVID." RCT, 100 participants randomized, **40 completed all measures (23 AIR, 17 control)** — 60% dropout rate. Active control was a structurally equivalent "12 weeks to wellness" general health-education intervention. **No blinding** specified. Primary outcomes were self-reported fatigue (Multidimensional Fatigue Inventory) and energy (SF-36 vitality subscale). The AIR group's fatigue reduction effect size was ~4× the control's; energy effect ~2× control's.
  - **Critical caveat**: Alexandra J. Bratty is CEO of AB Research Consulting, which provides consulting services to The Gupta Program; her company was compensated by "independent donors" for this work. This is a significant COI that should be disclosed in any honest discussion of the trial.
  - Bratty 2024 (PubMed 38404605): an "Improvement Across Various Chronic Conditions" paper — broader, retrospective design.
- **Regulatory action**: UK Advertising Standards Agency (ASA) in 2018 upheld three complaints filed by the ME Association against the Gupta Program for unsubstantiated claims that the program could treat fibromyalgia, ME/CFS, and electrical sensitivities. Gupta was instructed not to make those claims.
- **NICE 2021 ME/CFS guideline**: Explicitly recommends not offering "therapies derived from osteopathy, life coaching or neurolinguistic programming" — language widely understood to target Lightning Process and adjacent retraining approaches.
- **Independent researcher concern**: Dr. Juan Luciano, whose research the Gupta Program had cited, stated his team had "no idea" the program was using their work commercially and that he had no personal relationship with or endorsement of Gupta.

#### Dynamic Neural Retraining System (DNRS)

- **Founder**: Annie Hopper (Canada). Self-described former multiple-chemical-sensitivities patient. No medical credentials; describes herself as a "limbic system rehabilitation specialist."
- **Premise**: "Limbic system impairment" causes chronic illness symptoms (multiple chemical sensitivity, ME/CFS, fibromyalgia, long COVID, mold illness). Self-administered neuroplasticity training (incantational thought-stopping, visualization, behavioral routines) rewires the limbic system.
- **Cost**: Online program ~$300–$500; 5-day in-person seminars more; retreats higher still. Combined total can reach $1,500+.
- **Evidence cited**:
  - McMaster University retrospective study (2016, **unpublished**): 102 attendees of a DNRS seminar self-reported outcomes at 0/3/6/12 months; response rate fell to ~64. Significant self-reported improvements. The study is observational, uncontrolled, self-selected, with high dropout. As of 2025 it remains submitted but unpublished in peer-reviewed literature.
  - **David Tuller's Virology Blog critique (2020)** documented these limitations in detail and noted that DNRS has been promoted with overstated claims about evidence.
- **Critique highlights**:
  - Founder is not a clinician.
  - The "limbic system" framing is not consistent with current cognitive neuroscience (the limbic system is not a unified functional unit; "limbic retraining" is a marketing phrase).
  - Treats highly diverse conditions (MCS, ME/CFS, fibro, long COVID) with the same protocol despite very different documented pathophysiologies.
  - Patient harm reports: "crash" testimonials from people who pushed past PEM thresholds while following program encouragement.

#### Primal Trust (Dr. Cathleen King, DPT)

- **Founder**: Dr. Cathleen King, Doctor of Physical Therapy; identifies as recovered from chronic Lyme. Previously associated with DNRS.
- **Premise**: Combines "brain retraining + polyvagal theory + somatics + attachment repair + cell danger response" into a tiered curriculum (Regulate / Integrate / Liberate).
- **Cost**: Subscription membership; per published page, ~$60–$100/month tiers plus higher-tier offerings.
- **Evidence cited**: As of 2026, **no peer-reviewed RCTs published on Primal Trust specifically.** The program leans on testimonials and reported user counts (13,000+ members claimed on their website).
- **Distinctive feature**: Explicit polyvagal framing (more so than Gupta or DNRS). Uses Naviaux's "cell danger response" theory as an additional mechanism — itself a contested model.

#### The Lightning Process (Phil Parker)

- **Founder**: Phil Parker, UK osteopath. Background in NLP and life coaching.
- **Premise**: A 3-day training program teaches participants to interrupt their physiological/emotional response patterns via specific mental and verbal techniques drawn from NLP, osteopathy, and life coaching.
- **Cost**: GBP £700–£900+ for the 3-day course.
- **Evidence cited**:
  - **SMILE trial** (Crawley et al., *Archives of Disease in Childhood* 2018) — pediatric CFS, n=100 (planned), open-label, no blinding, subjective primary outcomes. **Major controversy**: the primary outcome was changed from school attendance (objective) to SF-36 physical function (subjective self-report) **after data collection had begun**, an outcome-switching pattern that has been the subject of David Tuller's open letters and a 2019 editorial correction in *Archives of Disease in Childhood* by Nick Brown. The journal corrected the paper to acknowledge it was "not fully ICMJE compliant" with respect to the outcome change.
  - **Ethical concerns**: ME Association, Young ME Sufferers Trust, Invest in ME raised concerns about trial design, consent, and study population (housebound children were excluded, biasing the sample toward less-severe cases). Ethical approval was delayed to 2011 after prolonged advocacy protest.
- **NICE 2021 ME/CFS guideline**: Explicitly recommends against the Lightning Process for ME/CFS. This is one of the strongest official UK-NHS-endorsed positions on a brain-retraining program.

#### Cross-cutting structural features of all four programs

| Feature | Pattern |
|---------|---------|
| Founder credentials | Mixed — Gupta and Hopper are not clinicians; King is DPT; Parker is osteopath. Limited expertise in viral pathophysiology, immunology, or autonomic medicine. |
| Funding | Direct-to-consumer paid courses; no insurance reimbursement |
| Mechanism specified | Generally vague — "amygdala," "limbic system," "neuroplasticity" without specific neural-circuit hypotheses |
| RCT with sham control | None of the four has an RCT with proper sham control; AIR (Gupta) has an *active control* but no blinding |
| Primary endpoint pre-specified | SMILE switched primary endpoint mid-trial; AIR's outcomes are subjective self-report |
| Harms reported | Systematically under-reported in program-developer-led studies; documented in patient survey/testimonial channels |
| Control for natural recovery | Generally not — ME/CFS has substantial natural fluctuation (CDC: 31.4% cumulative recovery probability over 5 years; 48.1% over 10 years) and post-viral fatigue often resolves over months to years |
| Selection bias | Strong — self-paying, motivated, less-severely-affected patients self-select into programs |
| Independent evaluation | Limited; most studies are program-developer-led or close-collaboration |
| Patient testimonials | Heavily featured in marketing; survivorship bias and confirmation bias acknowledged in critical reviews |

### Layer 5: The PACE/SMILE iatrogenic-harm history

To understand why ME/CFS advocacy is sharply critical of brain-retraining programs, you have to understand the PACE trial.

**PACE trial** (White, Sharpe, Chalder et al., *Lancet* 2011): £5 million UK study (funded partly by the Department for Work and Pensions, which itself raised eyebrows), n=641, comparing CBT, graded exercise therapy (GET), adaptive pacing, and specialist medical care for ME/CFS.

- **The outcome-switching**: The researchers changed the scoring of the primary outcome measures after data collection in ways that lowered the bar for "improvement" and "recovery." Under the original protocol, improvement rates were approximately 20%. Under the revised scoring, they reported approximately 60%. Recovery thresholds were similarly relaxed (the physical functioning score for "recovery" was lowered from 85 to 60, a level lower than the trial's *entry* threshold for being sick enough to qualify).
- **The data release**: Patient advocate Alem Matthees filed a Freedom of Information request; after a tribunal ordered data release (and the tribunal dismissed the PACE investigators' harassment claims as "grossly exaggerated"), independent researchers including Carolyn Wilshire reanalyzed the data and concluded the treatments were "not effective and unsafe."
- **Geraghty 2017** (*J Health Psychol* — special PACE issue): "Further commentary on the PACE trial: Biased methods and unreliable outcomes" documented the systemic methodological problems.
- **Outcome by 2026**: NICE (UK 2021 ME/CFS guideline) and CDC (US) no longer recommend CBT or GET for ME/CFS. The Cochrane GET review has been the subject of ongoing controversy and revision. Long-term follow-up at 2.5 years showed treatment effects had disappeared.
- **Patient-harm record**: Multiple patient surveys (Emerge Australia 2018: 89% of 610 respondents reported exercise/activity increase worsened their symptoms; Geraghty 2017 review: 54–74% reported GET harm) document substantial iatrogenic harm.

**SMILE trial** (Crawley et al. — see above): pediatric CFS, Lightning Process, outcome-switching documented, editorial correction issued by *Archives of Disease in Childhood*. David Tuller's open letters and Virology Blog reporting was central to surfacing the issues.

The pattern: psychotherapy/behavioral interventions for chronic illness, marketed as benign, validated in unblinded trials with subjective outcomes and undisclosed protocol changes, taken up by clinicians and policy bodies, ultimately rejected after independent reanalysis revealed methodological failures and patient-harm reports. The brain-retraining industry has not been through the same level of scrutiny but uses many of the same methodological shortcuts.

### Layer 6: The patient-benefit vs advocacy-critique tension

This is where careful honesty matters.

**The patient-benefit side**: Some patients DO report substantial benefit from brain retraining. Testimonials are not nothing. People describe recovery from years of debilitating symptoms after Gupta, DNRS, or Primal Trust programs. This deserves acknowledgment, not dismissal. Possible explanations:
- Genuine therapeutic benefit from the practices themselves (slow breathing, paced behavior, attention regulation) regardless of the framework
- Natural history (ME/CFS, long COVID, post-viral fatigue all have spontaneous remission rates that confound program-developer claims)
- Placebo / expectancy effects (real, especially in conditions with strong stress modulation)
- Selection effects (people who complete a paid program may be a different population than program dropouts; survivor reports dominate)
- Diagnostic heterogeneity (some "ME/CFS" cases are actually other conditions where these techniques happen to help)
- Concurrent improvements (pacing, sleep, nutrition often improve alongside) that get attributed to the program

**The advocacy-critique side**:
- **MEAction** (the largest international ME patient advocacy organization), **Action for ME**, **Long Covid Advocacy** (UK Substack), **Science for ME** forums, **Health Rising**, and **MEpedia** have published critical analyses of brain-retraining programs. Concerns include:
  - Programs frame illness as psychogenic/maladaptive-stress-response, recapitulating the biopsychosocial (BPS) framework that produced PACE/GET harm
  - Patient-blaming risk: when the program "doesn't work," the implicit narrative is that the patient didn't try hard enough, didn't believe enough, didn't follow the protocol correctly
  - PEM-disregard risk: program encouragement to push through, "graduate" from rest, or "expand the safety zone" can precipitate ME/CFS crashes
  - "Symptoms but no disease" framing: pathologizes patient experience while denying underlying organic pathophysiology
  - Diversion of research funding from biological mechanisms (immunology, neurology, viral persistence, mitochondrial dysfunction) toward psychological interventions
  - Gender/race dimensions: women and people of color disproportionately get the "it's in your head" treatment; brain retraining can echo this even when not intended

**The synthesis**: Honest treatment holds both. Some patients benefit. Some patients are harmed. The methodological weaknesses of the program-developer-led studies make it impossible to know the population-level effect with any precision. The historical record of PACE and SMILE makes due skepticism reasonable. The autistic adult considering these programs needs both: the patient-benefit acknowledgment and the methodological/historical context.

### Layer 7: Autistic-specific considerations

Several factors make brain-retraining decisions different for autistic people specifically:

- **Interoceptive differences**. The "feel your way to wellness" framing common in polyvagal-informed brain retraining presupposes typical interoceptive access — the ability to feel and label bodily states. Many autistic people have atypical interoception (under- or over-detection of internal signals). "Notice the safety in your body" instructions can be confusing, frustrating, or genuinely unworkable.

- **Sensory issues with retreat formats**. Multi-day retreats, group "neuro-conditioning" sessions, and the often-intense visualization/affirmation work can be sensory overload. Programs are not autism-adapted by default.

- **Mask-removal and compliance dynamics**. The "vulnerability" and "letting go of defenses" framing common in somatic programs can collide with autistic experiences of masking as protective coping. Telling an autistic adult to "drop the mask" without recognizing the social cost is not therapeutic.

- **PDA-profile (Pathological Demand Avoidance / Persistent Drive for Autonomy) autistics**. The compliance-based structure of many brain-retraining programs (do the practices exactly, daily, for 6+ months) can specifically trigger adverse PDA responses. The harder the program insists, the worse the result.

- **Autism + ME/CFS overlap risk**. Pushing past PEM thresholds is especially dangerous in this population, and the brain-retraining "expand your safety zone" language is the exact wrong framing if PEM is the active risk.

- **Autistic plain-language preferences**. Polyvagal-branded language (ventral vagal, neuroception, etc.) is itself a sensory/cognitive load. For an autistic adult who already finds verbal-emotional translation effortful, layering polyvagal metaphors on top of chronic-illness self-management adds cost without obvious benefit.

- **The "rewire your nervous system in 8 weeks" timeline problem**. For autistic adults with stable lifelong sensory and processing differences, the framing that 8 weeks of practice "rewires" a nervous system that has been the way it is for decades is implausible at face value. The framing itself may be doing harm by setting up failure-experience.

### Layer 8: The reusable evaluation framework

Drawn from Cat 6 (evidence-tier framework) + Cat 8 (red-flag patterns) + chronic-illness-specific questions:

1. **Mechanism specified?** Does the program offer a specific testable mechanism, or vague "neuroplasticity"/"limbic system"/"vagal tone" language?
2. **RCT with sham control?** Has the program been tested against a credible sham — not just an "active control" of general health education?
3. **Primary endpoint pre-specified?** Was the primary outcome registered before data collection, and not switched mid-trial?
4. **Outcomes objective or subjective?** Self-report fatigue questionnaires are vulnerable to expectancy effects; objective measures (HR/HRV, accelerometry, return to work) are more robust.
5. **Harms reported?** Does the program/study honestly report adverse events including crashes, deterioration, financial-harm?
6. **Control for natural recovery?** Did the study account for ME/CFS's substantial natural-recovery rate (31–48% over 5–10 years per CDC)?
7. **Selection bias addressed?** Did the study account for the self-selected, self-paying, motivated, often-less-severe population that signs up?
8. **Independent evaluation?** Has the program been evaluated by researchers without commercial ties to its sale?
9. **Conflict-of-interest disclosure clear?** Are researcher-program financial relationships transparently disclosed?
10. **Founder credentials and scope?** Is the founder qualified to make claims about the conditions the program treats? Are they making claims about diseases (long COVID, ME/CFS) without the relevant training?

A "no" or "vague" answer on multiple questions is a strong signal that the evidence claim deserves skepticism, regardless of how many positive testimonials are featured in marketing.

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: An autistic adult with long-COVID-related POTS considering brain retraining

**Scenario**: An autistic adult, two years into long COVID with diagnosed POTS, exhausted by years of symptoms and dismissive medical care, sees a wellness influencer praising the Gupta Program. The program is $499. They are wondering whether to try.

**Applying the chapter's framework**:
- Is autonomic dysfunction real here? Yes — POTS is documented.
- Are there polyvagal-informed adaptations with independent evidence? Yes — pacing, slow-paced breathing, HRV biofeedback. They can do these without paying $499.
- Does the Gupta Program have strong evidence? The 2023 Toussaint & Bratty RCT exists but has 60% dropout, active-control-not-sham design, no blinding, COI undisclosed in marketing, and the funded coordinator has a paid relationship with Gupta. NICE 2021 recommends against NLP-derived programs.
- PEM risk? POTS doesn't always involve PEM, but long COVID frequently does. Program encouragement to push through is a known risk.
- Autistic-specific factors? Interoception load, mask-removal framing, PDA dynamics all relevant.

**Conclusion**: A reasonable approach is to start with the free/low-cost practices (pacing, slow-paced breathing, HRV biofeedback via a $50 finger sensor and free app) and reserve $499 for something with better-grade evidence. If they later choose to try Gupta, do so with clear pre-set endpoints, PEM-monitoring vigilance, and an explicit non-judgmental exit plan if it doesn't work.

### Example 2: The "I tried DNRS and recovered" testimonial

**Scenario**: A user reads a testimonial on Instagram from someone who attributes their ME/CFS recovery to DNRS.

**Applying the chapter's framework**:
- Could this be real benefit? Possibly. DNRS practices include behavioral modification and stress reduction; some patients may genuinely improve.
- Could this be confounded? CDC data shows 31–48% natural recovery from ME/CFS over 5–10 years. The testifier may also have made concurrent changes (sleep, diet, pacing, work reduction) that they attribute to DNRS.
- Could this be survivorship bias? Yes — people who tried DNRS and got worse or didn't improve are systematically less visible in marketing channels and Instagram testimonials.
- Could this be a different condition? "ME/CFS" gets applied to a heterogeneous range of fatigue presentations; some may respond better to behavioral intervention than the prototypical ME/CFS phenotype.

**Conclusion**: The testimonial isn't dismissable, but it also isn't generalizable. A single testimonial doesn't tell you whether the program works for *you*, especially given your specific phenotype.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: "Autonomic dysfunction in chronic illness means polyvagal theory is right."
  - **Reality**: Autonomic dysfunction is documented by mainstream autonomic-medicine literature, independent of PVT. PVT's contested claims (the phylogenetic story, the unique role of myelinated vagus, the three hierarchical states) don't need to be true for the autonomic-data to be true. Multiple alternative frameworks (neurovisceral integration model, allostatic load model, predictive processing) explain the same data.

- **Misconception**: "Brain retraining programs have RCT evidence so they must work."
  - **Reality**: The Toussaint & Bratty 2023 RCT (the most-cited brain-retraining RCT) has 60% dropout, no sham control, no blinding, and is co-authored by a program consultant. This is not strong evidence. SMILE had outcome-switching and a journal correction. Gupta's 2010 study had no controls. The collective brain-retraining evidence base is weak.

- **Misconception**: "If patients report benefit, the program works."
  - **Reality**: Testimonials are real but confounded by selection bias, natural recovery, placebo/expectancy effects, and survivorship bias. They are useful as hypothesis-generators but not as program-effectiveness evidence.

- **Misconception**: "ME/CFS is psychogenic so brain retraining is the right treatment."
  - **Reality**: ME/CFS is not psychogenic. The PACE trial, which tried to operationalize the biopsychosocial model, has been substantively discredited. NICE 2021 and CDC no longer recommend CBT/GET. Treating ME/CFS as psychogenic produces documented iatrogenic harm.

- **Misconception**: "Polyvagal-informed pacing only works because of polyvagal theory."
  - **Reality**: Pacing has an independent evidence base (Workwell Foundation, Jason et al., NICE 2021 guideline). It works regardless of which theoretical framework you use to explain it. Calling it "polyvagal-informed" is scaffolding, not mechanism.

- **Misconception**: "All autistic people should try brain retraining because we have HRV differences."
  - **Reality**: HRV differences in autism are real (see Cat 4) but small in effect size and highly heterogeneous. They don't generate a treatment indication for an expensive commercial program. They certainly don't override autistic-specific considerations (PDA, interoception, sensory issues with retreat formats).

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **Patients with confirmed psychogenic non-epileptic seizures, functional neurological disorder (FND), or somatic symptom disorder** are a distinct population where behavioral interventions have a different evidence base. Brain retraining is more plausible for some FND presentations than for ME/CFS specifically. The error to avoid is conflating these populations.

- **Children with PVFS (post-viral fatigue syndrome)** have different natural-recovery trajectories than adults with ME/CFS, which makes interpretation of pediatric brain-retraining trials especially difficult. The SMILE trial controversy partly reflects this.

- **Patients without PEM** (some fatigue presentations don't involve PEM) have a different risk profile for behavioral interventions than patients with strong PEM. The chapter's PEM caution applies most strongly to patients with PEM.

- **Patients who have already tried pacing, slow breathing, and HRV biofeedback** without benefit may consider higher-cost programs with clearer endpoints and exit plans. The chapter doesn't say "never try"; it says "try cheaper, safer, more evidence-based interventions first, with clear evaluation."

## Research Gaps

- MEAction's official position on brain retraining is referenced in advocacy literature but a definitive policy statement was not located on their primary website. The critique is documented through their forums, blog posts, and aligned organizations (Long Covid Advocacy UK, Action for ME, Science for ME) more than through a single MEAction document.
- The autism × long COVID specifically (as distinct from autism × ME/CFS) has limited dedicated literature as of 2026; most data is by extrapolation from the broader autism × dysautonomia and autism × hEDS literature.
- Primal Trust does not appear to have any published RCT evidence as of 2026; its evidence claims rest on testimonials and member counts.
- The full RECOVER-AUTONOMIC trial results are pending; this section will need updating as the trial completes.
