# Evidence Base: What Has Actually Been Validated

## What "evidence-based" really means — and why it matters here

"Evidence-based" is one of the most overworked phrases in modern psychotherapy marketing, and IFS sits in the middle of the linguistic mess. In contemporary clinical research, the phrase is a spectrum, not a binary. At the **minimal threshold**, "evidence-based" can mean: at least one peer-reviewed trial showing that the intervention does something on at least one outcome in at least one population. At the **strong threshold**, it means: replicated randomized controlled trials, in independent labs, showing between-arm superiority over credible active comparators across multiple conditions, ideally backed by meta-analysis.

These are different planets. CBT for depression sits at the strong end — hundreds of RCTs over decades, multiple meta-analyses, replicated findings. EMDR for PTSD sits there too. IFS, as of April 2026, sits at the minimal end — and only for one or two conditions.

That's not an attack. It's a calibration. Some genuinely useful clinical models start out with thin evidence and accumulate it as they age. Some never do. IFS is currently somewhere in the middle of that arc: the model has been around for roughly 40 years, has been adopted at industrial scale (45,764 therapists self-listed it on Psychology Today as of April 2024), and has produced two RCTs and a handful of pilot studies. That ratio of adoption to evidence is the central thing this page exists to make visible.

The reader's job here isn't to decide whether IFS "works." It's to know, study by study, what each piece of evidence actually does and does not establish, so that downstream marketing claims (and downstream critiques) can be weighed against the actual data. By the end of this page, you should be able to say sentences like: "IFS has a single proof-of-concept RCT for rheumatoid arthritis with sustained 1-year benefits"; "IFS has shown rough equivalence to CBT and IPT in one underpowered pilot RCT for depression"; "the most-marketed PTSD numbers come from an uncontrolled pilot with n=17 and an effect size that would not survive a real RCT"; and "the SAMHSA listing is a 2015 evaluation hosted on a private mirror of a registry shut down in 2018."

Let's go study by study.

## Shadick et al. 2013 — the rheumatoid-arthritis RCT (the strongest single trial)

The 2013 Shadick et al. paper in the *Journal of Rheumatology* remains the most-cited and methodologically strongest IFS trial. It was a two-arm randomized controlled trial conducted at Brigham and Women's Hospital / Harvard Medical School. Seventy-nine patients with rheumatoid arthritis (RA) were randomized to either a 9-month IFS group intervention (n=39) or an education control (n=40) that received mailed materials about RA symptoms and management. Sixty-eight completed assessments. The authors describe the study explicitly as "proof-of-concept."

At posttreatment, the IFS arm showed greater improvement than the education arm in overall pain (mean change −14.9 vs. control, p=0.04) and physical function (+14.6, p=0.04). At 1-year follow-up — the more impressive number — the IFS arm showed sustained benefits in self-assessed joint pain (−0.6, p=0.04), self-compassion (+1.8, p=0.01), and depressive symptoms (−3.2, p=0.01). What didn't change: anxiety, self-efficacy, and the objective biological markers of RA disease activity (DAS-CRP4) measured by blinded rheumatologists. So the IFS effect, in this trial, was on patient-reported psychological and pain-related outcomes — not on the disease itself.

What does this trial establish? That IFS produces clinically meaningful, sustained benefits on patient-reported pain and depression in a chronic-illness population, when compared to a deliberately weak control (mailed information). What does it not establish? That IFS works for any other condition — RA is not depression and not PTSD. That IFS outperforms an active psychotherapy comparator — the comparator was education, not therapy. That the effects generalize to populations less like the trial sample. And the conflict-of-interest pattern that runs through this entire literature starts here: Schwartz, the IFS founder, is the senior author; Sweezy, a senior IFS-Institute author, is in the byline. The Harvard infrastructure and the rheumatology team appear independent, which is why this trial holds up better than later IFS-Foundation-only-funded work — but the COI is non-zero.

The honest sentence: "IFS has one proof-of-concept RCT showing sustained 1-year benefits in pain, depression, and self-compassion in rheumatoid arthritis, with weak-comparator caveats and partial IFS-Institute authorship."

## Haddock et al. 2016/2017 — the depression pilot RCT (equivalence, not superiority)

The 2017 Haddock paper in the *Journal of Marital and Family Therapy* is the second of IFS's two RCTs. It was a small pilot study of female college students with depression, randomized to IFS (n=17) or treatment-as-usual (TAU, which meant CBT or IPT) (n=15). The analytic sample was n≈32; some sources cite 37 enrolled. Each arm was supposed to receive 16 sessions; many participants completed 11–15.

The headline finding: both arms showed similar declines in depressive symptoms. The authors found no significant between-group differences in either the magnitude or rate of change. Their conclusion: "preliminary evidence for the efficacy of IFS in the treatment of depressive symptoms."

This is where you have to be careful with the language. "Preliminary evidence for efficacy" and "no significant difference between IFS and CBT/IPT" are both technically true sentences about the same data. But the second sentence is what statistical inference actually supports; the first is a softer framing that invites the reader to round up. In trial-design terms, the Haddock study was an **underpowered superiority trial that failed to find a difference**. With n=32, the trial cannot confidently distinguish "the two treatments are equivalent" from "the trial was too small to detect a difference if one exists." A real **equivalence trial** would have pre-specified an equivalence margin and would have required substantially more power than even a superiority test of the same size — equivalence is harder to demonstrate than superiority, not easier.

There are also imbalances worth tracking. Four of the five IFS therapists in the study had less than one year of experience; the IFS group was effectively learning on the job. More than half of the TAU participants were on antidepressants concurrently; none of the IFS participants were. These imbalances cut both ways and are themselves reasons to read the result as preliminary.

What does this trial establish? That short-course IFS produces depression reductions roughly comparable to short-course CBT or IPT in a small sample of mildly-to-moderately depressed female college students, in a single underpowered pilot, with imbalances on therapist experience and concurrent medication. What does it not establish? That IFS is superior to CBT or IPT for depression. That the effect generalizes outside the demographic studied. That experienced IFS therapists in a larger sample would replicate the result.

The honest sentence: "A small pilot RCT (n≈32) of depressed female college students found no significant difference between IFS and TAU (CBT or IPT). The trial was too small to confirm equivalence; it found no detectable difference."

## Hodgdon et al. 2021 — the PTSD pilot (the marketing number — and the methodological asterisks)

The 2021 Hodgdon et al. paper in the *Journal of Aggression, Maltreatment & Trauma* is the source of the most-marketed IFS statistic. It's also the one that, when you read it carefully, requires the most calibration.

The design first. This was an **uncontrolled, open-label, single-arm pilot effectiveness study with within-subject (pre/post) design**. There was no control group. There was no randomization. Seventeen adult survivors of multiple childhood traumas with current PTSD diagnoses received 16 weekly 90-minute IFS sessions. Of the 17, 13 were completers in some analyses. Funding came from the Foundation for Self Leadership — the IFS Institute's research-funding nonprofit arm. The lead author, Hilary Hodgdon, was at the Trauma Center / Justice Resource Institute; co-authors include Frank Anderson and Schwartz, both senior IFS-Institute figures.

The headline numbers: at 1-month follow-up, **92% of completers (12 of 13) no longer met criteria for PTSD**. The within-subject Cohen's d for the Clinician-Administered PTSD Scale (CAPS) was reported as **d = −4.46**. The companion Davidson Trauma Scale d was −3.05. There were also significant within-subject improvements in depression, dissociation, somatization, affect dysregulation, and disrupted self-perception.

Now the asterisks, in plain English.

A within-subject Cohen's d of −4.46 means the post-treatment mean is roughly four-and-a-half standard deviations below the pre-treatment mean. In the credible PTSD treatment literature — including for trauma-focused therapies that are the field's gold standards (Prolonged Exposure, Cognitive Processing Therapy, EMDR) — within-group effect sizes in well-conducted RCTs typically land in the d ≈ 0.8–1.5 range. Between-group effects (vs. waitlist) usually fall in the d ≈ 0.5–1.0 range. A within-group d of −4.46 is several times larger than anything credible the field has produced. It is not a defensible point estimate of IFS's effect on PTSD; it is closer to the upper bound of what an uncontrolled pilot with n=17 can produce when expectancy, regression to the mean, selection effects, and the absence of a comparator all push in the same direction.

The authors themselves acknowledge this in the limitations section. They write that "this was an open, uncontrolled trial using a within subjects design, which likely inflated the effect size to some degree" and that "these effect sizes are larger than what has been observed in the PTSD treatment literature." That admission, buried in limitations, doesn't usually make it into IFS marketing materials, where "92% no longer met PTSD criteria" travels alone.

What does this study actually establish? Preliminary signal that IFS is feasible and acceptable for adult survivors of multiple childhood trauma, with promising within-subject improvement worth a real RCT. What does it not establish? Anything about between-group efficacy. Anything about how IFS compares to PE, CPT, or EMDR. Confirmation that the d = −4.46 figure represents a real treatment effect rather than a combination of regression, expectancy, and small-sample variance.

The honest sentence: "An uncontrolled pilot of 17 adult survivors of childhood trauma with PTSD found that 92% of completers no longer met PTSD criteria at 1-month follow-up after 16 sessions of IFS. The reported within-group effect size (d = −4.46) is several times larger than anything in the credible PTSD treatment literature; the authors themselves acknowledge the design 'likely inflated the effect size to some degree.' Treat as a feasibility signal pending a real RCT."

## Comeau et al. 2024 — the second PTSD pilot (more credible numbers, same single-arm caveat)

In 2024, a different research group published a second IFS PTSD pilot in *Psychological Trauma* — the PARTS (Program for Alleviating and Resolving Trauma and Stress) study, led by Comeau et al. at Cambridge Health Alliance / Harvard. This one had n=15 (13 responders), was also single-arm and Foundation-funded, and tested an online group format (16 weekly group sessions). Attendance was 73% completion of 12 or more sessions; 92% of responders said they would recommend it; 100% found it helpful.

The clinically interesting numbers: CAPS-5 PTSD severity reduction d = −0.7 at week 16, d = −0.9 at week 24. 53% achieved a clinically meaningful response (defined as ≥10-point CAPS-5 reduction). Secondary improvements in decentering, self-compassion, and emotion regulation.

These effect sizes are within the credible range for PTSD psychotherapy outcomes. Putting Hodgdon 2021 (d = −4.46) and Comeau 2024 (d ≈ 0.7–0.9) side by side is itself informative: the smaller, more measured Comeau numbers are probably closer to what IFS actually does for PTSD, and the Hodgdon number is likely an artifact of the design. Both are single-arm and Foundation-funded; neither is a real RCT. But the Comeau pilot suggests that when IFS for PTSD is studied carefully, the effects look like real psychotherapy effects rather than miracles. The authors of Comeau themselves note: "well-controlled efficacy research is needed."

The honest sentence: "Two single-arm Foundation-funded PTSD pilots have been published; the more carefully measured one (Comeau 2024, online group, n=15) reports d ≈ 0.7–0.9 — within the credible range for PTSD psychotherapy outcomes. A real RCT is still missing."

## Ally et al. 2025 — the PARTS-SUD pilot (substance use, the newest data point)

The 2025 Frontiers in Psychiatry paper by Ally et al. extended the PARTS framework to comorbid PTSD and substance use disorder (PARTS-SUD). It's a single-arm pilot with n=10. Twelve weekly 90-minute group sessions plus six individual 50-minute sessions over 12 weeks; online/telehealth format. The sample was diverse (40% White, 40% Hispanic/Latinx, 20% Asian; 40% male, 40% female, 20% non-binary). Foundation-funded, with the Richard C. Schwartz Research Fellowship.

Acceptability metrics were strong: 86% general acceptability, 92% willingness to recommend, 70% retention at 12 weeks. The exploratory clinical outcomes: PCL-5 PTSD severity dropped 1.7 points/week (95% CI −2.45 to −0.93, p=0.002), and craving dropped 0.25 points/week (95% CI −0.45 to −0.06, p=0.014). 54% achieved a clinically meaningful difference on the PCL-5.

What it establishes: feasibility and acceptability of an online group IFS intervention for the PTSD-SUD comorbidity, with preliminary within-subject signal of reductions in both PTSD severity and craving. What it does not establish: efficacy compared to any active control, or to no treatment. n=10 is too small to rule out non-specific effects, expectancy, or regression to the mean. The authors are honest about this in their limitations section.

The honest sentence: "A 2025 single-arm Foundation-funded pilot of online group IFS for n=10 adults with comorbid PTSD and substance use found high acceptability and within-subject reductions in PTSD severity and craving. Preliminary; not efficacy."

## Buys 2025 — the scoping review (the most calibrating single source)

If you read only one paper on IFS evidence, read the 2025 Buys scoping review in *Clinical Psychologist*. It searched Medline, Scopus, PsycINFO, and Google Scholar for peer-reviewed IFS research and applied scoping-review inclusion criteria (broader than systematic-review criteria). The result: **27 studies** met inclusion. The breakdown:

- 2 RCTs (~7%): Shadick 2013 RA, Haddock 2016/2017 depression
- 5 quasi-experimental studies (~19%)
- 3 qualitative exploratory studies (~11%)
- 17 case studies (~63%)

That ratio is the single most useful fact you can carry away from this page. After roughly 40 years of model development, two-thirds of the IFS-research literature is case studies — the lowest tier of clinical evidence. Seven percent is RCT. Buys's synthesized conclusion is that the existing evidence is "promising" for chronic pain, depression, PTSD, and self-compassion development, but that "larger fully-powered clinical trials are needed for each clinical indication" and — importantly — that adverse-event monitoring is largely absent across the IFS evidence base. Most published IFS studies don't systematically report harms.

This isn't unique to IFS — psychotherapy trials in general are bad at reporting harms — but it's a real gap. For a complex-trauma reader, the absence of adverse-event monitoring matters: premature unburdening, over-flooding, and retraumatization are documented failure modes of IFS in clinical practice (see Category 9), and a research base that doesn't monitor for them gives you no data on how often they happen.

## SAMHSA NREPP — the correction that matters

This is the single most-misleading line in IFS marketing, and it deserves slow handling.

In November 2015, IFS was added to SAMHSA's National Registry for Evidence-Based Programs and Practices (NREPP), based on an application submitted in April 2014. The NREPP rating, after independent SAMHSA review, was: **"effective" for general functioning and well-being**, and **"promising"** for depression, anxiety, physical health, and self-concept. The single underlying study was Shadick 2013. Note carefully: the rating was for "general functioning and well-being." It was NOT for PTSD, was NOT for any specific clinical condition, and was based on one trial in rheumatoid arthritis.

That much is the historical record. Here's the part that gets stripped out of marketing materials.

NREPP no longer exists as a federal site. The administering contract was terminated for "the convenience of the government" on December 28, 2017. The website was shuttered in early 2018. SAMHSA Assistant Secretary Elizabeth McCance-Katz publicly characterized NREPP's standards as inadequate and the registry's coverage as "skewed" because it relied on voluntary self-submission from intervention developers. In April 2018, SAMHSA launched a different "Evidence-Based Practices Resource Center" that explicitly **does not replace NREPP** and does not contain the previous resources.

The "samhsa-nrepp.foundationifs.org" URL that IFS marketing materials currently link to is a private archive maintained by the IFS Foundation. It mirrors the 2015 NREPP entry so that the rating can continue to be displayed. It is not a federal site. It is not a current endorsement. It is a frozen mirror of an evaluation done on a since-discontinued registry, of a single 2013 trial, for a category ("general functioning and well-being") that doesn't correspond to any specific clinical condition.

When IFS marketing copy says "IFS is on the SAMHSA registry," translate that as: "IFS was added to a SAMHSA registry in 2015 for general functioning and well-being. That registry was discontinued by SAMHSA in 2018. The current archive is hosted by an IFS-affiliated foundation, not by the federal government."

Treat the NREPP claim, in 2026, as a historical fact about a registry that no longer exists — not as a current institutional credential.

## What's missing from the evidence base

The published-RCT and pilot picture above is striking less for what's there than for what isn't. Major gaps:

- **Anxiety disorders.** No targeted RCT. NREPP rated IFS "promising" for anxiety on the basis of a Shadick 2013 secondary outcome. There is no GAD, OCD, panic, social anxiety, or phobia trial.
- **Eating disorders.** This is the single most striking gap. Schwartz developed IFS in the 1980s working with bulimic patients at the Institute for Juvenile Research. Forty-plus years later, there is no published RCT for eating disorders. The model was born from this clinical territory and the empirical record on it is essentially blank. (Schwartz, Sykes, and Sweezy published a 2023 IFS-for-addictions book that touches eating-disorder territory, but a book is not a trial.)
- **Dissociative disorders.** Despite IFS being widely promoted as a fit for dissociative work, there is no published RCT or controlled trial in DID, OSDD, or partial DID.
- **Autism, alexithymia, neurodivergent populations.** Zero. There is no peer-reviewed clinical trial of IFS in autistic adults, in alexithymic adults, or in any documented neurodivergent population. All of the writing on IFS adaptations for autism and alexithymia exists in clinician blogs, Substack posts, and one recently published book (*Wired to Feel: Autism as a Condition of Sensory Surplus*, Bergenfield/Sweezy/Malow 2025). Useful, but not a clinical-trial evidence base.
- **Bipolar, serious mental illness, psychosis.** The Society for the Advancement of Psychotherapy 2024 critique specifically flags that IFS marketing has begun to extend into populations (active psychosis, severe SMI) where the model has neither evidence nor a clear safety record.
- **Children and adolescents.** Sparse. A few case reports.

For a complex-trauma reader specifically: the conditions you most likely care about — C-PTSD, dissociative spectrum presentations, comorbid trauma + alexithymia, comorbid trauma + autism — have either preliminary signals or no evidence at all.

## The funding-source pattern

A separate concern, related but distinct: as of April 2026, **every IFS clinical trial and pilot in the published peer-reviewed literature is either funded by the Foundation for Self Leadership (the IFS Institute's research nonprofit arm) or has authorship including the IFS founder or other senior IFS-Institute figures.** This is a pattern across:

- Shadick 2013 — Schwartz senior author, Sweezy co-author; Harvard infrastructure mitigates partially
- Haddock 2016/2017 — Foundation-supported per Foundation press materials
- Hodgdon 2021 — Foundation funded
- Comeau 2024 — Foundation funded
- Ally 2025 — Foundation funded plus the Richard C. Schwartz Research Fellowship

The Foundation publicly states it operates at arm's length, has independent review processes, and "will not attempt in any way to influence the results of the research." That is the standard COI mitigation language. It is not the same as evidence produced by funders with no stake in the outcome.

This concentration is not, by itself, a verdict. Foundation funding has produced what we have, and what we have isn't nothing. But it is the reason why the most-needed missing piece in the IFS evidence base is **independent, non-IFS-affiliated replication** of any of the existing signals. Until that happens, "evidence-based IFS" will keep meaning, in practice, "evidence produced or funded by the people who teach and certify and sell IFS." The empirical question — do Foundation-funded trials systematically report larger effects than non-Foundation-funded ones? — cannot be answered, because there are essentially no non-Foundation-funded IFS trials to compare against.

Allegiance effects in psychotherapy research are well-documented across modalities (researchers' allegiance to a treatment correlates with the size of the effects they report). That's a general background fact, not an IFS-specific accusation. It just argues for putting IFS effect-size estimates in a slightly tighter prior than their face-value numbers would suggest, and weighting the most credible-looking non-extreme estimates (Comeau d ≈ 0.7–0.9) more than the implausible-looking ones (Hodgdon d = −4.46).

## Where IFS sits on a strength-of-evidence scale

Putting this all together, with full hedges: here is roughly where IFS sits, condition by condition, compared to credible benchmarks.

- **IFS for rheumatoid arthritis**: One proof-of-concept RCT, sustained 1-year benefits in pain/depression/self-compassion, weak-comparator caveat. Roughly equivalent to "single-RCT evidence-based for one condition." If you have RA and depression, and your therapist offers IFS as an adjunct to medical care, the empirical case is fair.
- **IFS for depression**: One small pilot RCT showing rough equivalence to TAU (CBT or IPT), with imbalances and underpowering. Roughly equivalent to "one underpowered pilot suggesting parity with an established treatment." Compare to CBT for depression, which has hundreds of RCTs, multiple meta-analyses, and replicated superiority and equivalence findings.
- **IFS for PTSD**: Two single-arm Foundation-funded pilots (Hodgdon 2021, Comeau 2024). Best estimate of the effect size: somewhere between Comeau's credible d ≈ 0.7–0.9 and somewhere short of Hodgdon's implausible d = −4.46. Roughly equivalent to "promising preliminary signal pending a real RCT." Compare to PE, CPT, and EMDR for PTSD, which have dozens of RCTs and APA / WHO guideline endorsements.
- **IFS for substance use comorbid with PTSD**: One single-arm pilot (Ally 2025, n=10). Acceptability and feasibility signal, exploratory clinical signal. Compare to MDMA-AT, CBT-for-SUD, and contingency-management which have substantially deeper evidence bases.
- **IFS for autism, alexithymia, eating disorders, dissociative disorders, anxiety disorders, serious mental illness**: No peer-reviewed clinical-trial evidence base.

For a calibrated user — particularly one with a complex-trauma history, suspected alexithymia, and an autistic neurotype — the cleanest read is this: IFS has real preliminary evidence in chronic-illness adjunctive therapy (RA) and in trauma populations (small pilots). It has effectively no evidence in your specific intersectional population. Whether that means "use cautiously, with adaptations, knowing the data is thin" or "wait for evidence before committing" is a judgment call that depends on what alternatives are available and what your therapist thinks. The honest framing — neither "IFS is unsupported quackery" nor "IFS is evidence-based and proven" — is somewhere in the middle, and exactly *where* in the middle is what this page is for.
