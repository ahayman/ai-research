# Concept Research: Vagus Nerve Anatomy & Physiology

## Overview

This research note covers the factual neuroscience of the vagus nerve as established by neuroanatomy and clinical physiology, deliberately separated from polyvagal-theory interpretation. The aim is to lay the ground floor: anatomy that everyone (Porges, his critics, and neutral neuroanatomists) agrees on. Anything contested goes into Categories 1 (Foundations) or 3 (Critique). This page is the textbook chapter.

The vagus nerve (cranial nerve X, "the tenth") is the longest and most branched cranial nerve. It is a mixed nerve — carrying both sensory (afferent) signals up to the brainstem and motor (efferent) signals out to viscera. Two brainstem nuclei give rise to its motor output: the **dorsal motor nucleus of the vagus (DMV/DMN)** and the **nucleus ambiguus (NA)**. A third, the **nucleus tractus solitarius (NTS)**, receives most of its sensory inflow. The vast majority of vagal fibers — by classical count, roughly four out of every five — are *afferent*, carrying interoceptive information from heart, lungs, gut, larynx and other viscera back up to the brainstem, where that signal feeds forward into the insula, hypothalamus, amygdala, and prefrontal regulatory networks.

Two facts shape almost everything popular polyvagal writing gets wrong by omission. First: most vagal fibers are **unmyelinated** small-diameter C-fibers, not the larger myelinated fibers that produce the famous "vagal brake" on the heart. Second: most vagal fibers are **afferent**, not efferent. The myelinated cardio-inhibitory efferents from the nucleus ambiguus that polyvagal theory treats as the centerpiece of a "social engagement system" are a real and important subset of vagal output, but they are a minority subset of the nerve as a whole. Understanding that minority subset on its own terms is fine; mistaking it for "the" vagus is the most common error in lay polyvagal writing.

Respiratory sinus arrhythmia (RSA) — the rhythmic speeding up of heart rate on inhalation and slowing on exhalation — is the most-cited noninvasive index of cardiac vagal control. It is real and informative. It is also confounded by breathing rate, tidal volume, body position, fitness, age, and several medications, in ways that make naive "vagal tone" readings unreliable. This page covers what RSA actually measures, alongside the broader family of HRV metrics (RMSSD, SDNN, HF, LF, LF/HF), and the practical limits of using any of them as a biomarker.

## Core Components

### Component 1: The Brainstem Nuclei

The vagus nerve has four associated nuclei in the medulla oblongata. Three are the operational ones, plus a small sensory-only nucleus (the spinal trigeminal nucleus relay) most popular treatments ignore.

#### Dorsal Motor Nucleus of the Vagus (DMN / DMV / DVN)
- **Location**: Dorsal medulla oblongata, beneath the floor of the fourth ventricle. Long column running roughly parallel to the midline.
- **Function**: Provides the bulk of *parasympathetic visceromotor* output of the vagus. Preganglionic parasympathetic neurons.
- **Fiber type**: Predominantly **unmyelinated** efferents (slower-conducting C-fibers).
- **Projection targets**: Primarily **subdiaphragmatic** viscera — esophagus, stomach (gastric motility, acid secretion), small intestine, proximal colon (up to the splenic flexure), liver, pancreas. Also contributes some unmyelinated efferents to lungs and a smaller projection to the heart (typically rate-modulating rather than dominant).
- **Functional shorthand**: "Vegetative" parasympathetic output to viscera below the diaphragm. Gut work.
- **Source**: [StatPearls — Vagal Nerve Nuclei](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK545209/), [ScienceDirect — Vagus Nerve overview](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/neuroscience/vagus-nerve)

#### Nucleus Ambiguus (NA / NAmb)
- **Location**: Ventrolateral medulla oblongata, deeper and more ventral than the DMN. A column of neurons running through the reticular formation.
- **Function**: Mixed. Two distinct populations:
  - **Branchial motor neurons** — innervate striated (voluntary-like) muscles of soft palate, pharynx, larynx, and upper esophagus via the vagus and contributions from the cranial root of CN XI.
  - **Cardio-inhibitory parasympathetic neurons** — located in the "external formation" of the NA. These are the predominant source of cardiac vagal output in mammals, including humans.
- **Fiber type**: Both **myelinated B-fibers** (the cardio-inhibitory output and motor to laryngeal/pharyngeal muscle) and unmyelinated C-fibers.
- **Projection targets**: **Supradiaphragmatic** — heart (rate control via the sinoatrial node), larynx (via recurrent laryngeal nerve), pharynx, soft palate, upper esophagus.
- **Functional shorthand**: "Faster" parasympathetic output, including the moment-to-moment cardiac brake and voice/swallow control.
- **Source**: [StatPearls — Nucleus Ambiguus](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK547744/)

#### Nucleus Tractus Solitarius (NTS / NST)
- **Location**: Dorsomedial medulla, adjacent to DMN. Receives the central termination of the *tractus solitarius* — the bundle carrying visceral sensory afferents from CN VII, IX, and X.
- **Function**: **Sensory** processing. The first central relay for interoceptive information from heart (baroreceptors, chemoreceptors), lungs (stretch receptors), gut (mucosal mechanoreceptors, chemoreceptors), and special sense of taste.
- **Projection targets**: Onward — to parabrachial nucleus, hypothalamus, amygdala, thalamus, and via thalamus to the insular cortex (the central interoceptive cortex).
- **Importance**: This is the entry point for the ~80% of vagal fibers that are *afferent*. It's where bodily signals first become available to the central nervous system.
- **Source**: [Wikipedia — Vagus Nerve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vagus_nerve), [StatPearls — Vagal Nerve Nuclei](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK545209/)

#### Spinal Trigeminal Nucleus (relay)
- **Function**: Receives general somatic afferents from a small skin patch of the external ear served by the auricular branch of the vagus (Arnold's nerve). This is the anatomical basis for transcutaneous auricular vagus nerve stimulation (taVNS).
- **Note**: Mentioned for completeness because of taVNS relevance (covered in the Interventions category).

### Component 2: Fiber Composition (A, B, C fibers)

Classical nerve-fiber classification by Erlanger and Gasser sorts axons by diameter, myelination, and conduction velocity. The vagus contains all three classes.

| Fiber Type | Myelination | Diameter | Conduction Velocity | What It Carries (in vagus) |
|------------|-------------|----------|---------------------|----------------------------|
| **A-fibers** (Aα, Aβ, Aδ) | Myelinated | Largest (~5–20 μm) | Fastest (15–120 m/s) | Sensory visceral afferents; some somatomotor input |
| **B-fibers** | Lightly myelinated | Small (~3 μm) | Moderate (3–15 m/s) | **Preganglionic parasympathetic efferents** — the famous "vagal brake" |
| **C-fibers** | Unmyelinated | Smallest (~0.5–1.5 μm) | Slowest (~0.5–2.5 m/s) | Most vagal afferents (visceral sensation); also unmyelinated efferents from DMN |

**Quantitative composition (textbook approximation, originally from Foley & DuBois 1937 in cats)**:
- Approximately **80% of vagal fibers are afferent** (sensory, traveling toward the brain).
- The remaining ~20% are efferent.
- Of the afferents, about **90% are unmyelinated C-fibers**.
- Of the efferents, about **70% are unmyelinated C-fibers** (most from the DMN), with the remaining ~30% being myelinated B-fibers (most from the nucleus ambiguus).
- **Total unmyelinated**: ~80% of all vagal fibers in human and most mammalian cervical vagus.

**Caveats** (important to state explicitly):
- These percentages are **textbook approximations** rooted in older morphometric studies (Foley & DuBois 1937; Hoffman & Schnitzlein 1961). They are accurate within a tolerance of roughly ±5–10% but are not measured constants.
- Modern morphometry (e.g., **Settell et al. 2020** in domestic pigs in the *Journal of Neural Engineering*, [PMC7306215](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7306215/)) finds that the cervical vagus contains roughly **47 ± 7% myelinated fibers in pigs**, with the myelinated fraction containing ~80% small-diameter, ~18% medium, and ~2% large-diameter fibers. The unmyelinated count is harder to pin down with light microscopy.
- The fiber breakdown varies by **species, cervical level**, and possibly by individual. The classic 90%/70% breakdown is a *teaching shorthand*, not a precision figure.
- The substantive, robust point — vagus is **afferent-dominant and predominantly unmyelinated** — survives despite the imprecision in the specific percentages.

### Component 3: Afferent Dominance

- About **80% of vagal fibers are afferent** — sensory traffic from viscera to brainstem.
- They carry **interoceptive** information: heart rate, blood pressure, blood gas levels, gut distension, gut chemistry, gastric volume, pulmonary stretch, vocal cord position, taste (via CN VII and IX into the same NTS).
- Their first central termination is the **nucleus tractus solitarius (NTS)**, from which signals project to parabrachial nucleus, hypothalamus, amygdala, thalamus, and eventually the insular cortex (especially right anterior insula, which is heavily implicated in interoceptive awareness).
- These afferents do *not* directly cause motor output in the conventional sense. They feed prediction and regulation upstream. They are what your nervous system "knows" about your body.
- The clinical implication: when a popular polyvagal writer says "stimulating the vagus calms you down," roughly 80% of what they're stimulating is *sensory input to the brain*. Whatever calming effects occur are mostly mediated upstream — through brainstem and forebrain regulatory circuits acting on the body — not by direct efferent action of the stimulated fibers.
- **Source**: [ScienceDirect — Vagus Nerve overview](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/neuroscience/vagus-nerve)

### Component 4: Where the Vagus Actually Goes

The vagus nerve emerges from the medulla through the jugular foramen and descends through the neck, thorax, and abdomen. Its branches innervate:

**Cervical region**:
- Pharynx (pharyngeal branches — swallowing)
- Larynx (superior laryngeal and recurrent laryngeal branches — voice, cough, airway protection)
- Carotid body and carotid sinus (chemoreceptors, baroreceptors via the carotid sinus nerve interface)
- Tiny somatic afferents to the external ear via the auricular branch (Arnold's nerve)

**Thoracic region**:
- Heart (cardiac branches — primary parasympathetic supply, slowing the sinoatrial node)
- Lungs (pulmonary branches — bronchoconstriction, airway secretions, pulmonary stretch sensing)
- Esophagus (esophageal plexus — peristalsis, lower esophageal sphincter)

**Abdominal region** (subdiaphragmatic — most fibers here are DMN-derived):
- Stomach (gastric branches — motility, acid secretion)
- Liver (hepatic branch)
- Pancreas
- Small intestine (entire length)
- Proximal half of large intestine (up to the splenic flexure of the colon)
- Spleen and kidneys (some innervation)

**Important boundary**: Below the splenic flexure of the colon — descending colon, sigmoid, rectum — parasympathetic innervation comes from *sacral* spinal segments (S2–S4) via the pelvic splanchnic nerves, not the vagus.

**Source**: [StatPearls — Anterior Vagus Nerve](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK547696/), [Kenhub — Vagus Nerve](https://www.kenhub.com/en/library/anatomy/the-vagus-nerve), [TeachMeAnatomy — CN X](https://teachmeanatomy.info/head/cranial-nerves/vagus-nerve-cn-x/)

### Component 5: The Face-Heart Cranial Nerve Circuit

A useful and uncontested observation: several cranial nerves emerging from the brainstem share overlapping nuclei and innervate structures involved in expressive communication (face, voice, ear) and visceral control (heart). This functional clustering is real anatomy. It does not require any specific polyvagal interpretation to describe.

The cranial nerves participating in this circuit:

| CN | Name | Origin (nuclei) | Innervates (functional summary) |
|----|------|-----------------|--------------------------------|
| **V** | Trigeminal | Pons (motor + sensory nuclei) | Sensation across face; motor to muscles of mastication; **tensor tympani** muscle of the middle ear (acoustic gain control) |
| **VII** | Facial | Pons (facial nucleus, superior salivatory) | Muscles of facial expression; **stapedius** muscle of middle ear (acoustic gain control); taste anterior tongue; parasympathetic to lacrimal/submandibular glands |
| **IX** | Glossopharyngeal | Medulla (nucleus ambiguus, NTS, inferior salivatory) | Stylopharyngeus (swallowing); taste posterior tongue; sensory carotid sinus/body; parasympathetic to parotid |
| **X** | Vagus | Medulla (DMN, NA, NTS) | Larynx, pharynx, heart, lungs, gut (as above) |
| **XI** | Accessory (spinal) | Spinal cord C1–C5 (anterior horn) | Sternocleidomastoid, trapezius (head/shoulder orientation toward sound and gaze) |

**The anatomical observation**: the same brainstem region (medulla and adjacent pons) produces motor control of the face, ears, voice, and heart. Some of these nuclei are physically close (the nucleus ambiguus serves both laryngeal/pharyngeal muscle and cardiac parasympathetic output). This is real, established anatomy.

**The interpretive layer**: Polyvagal theory packages this cluster as the "social engagement system" and treats it as a coordinated functional unit. That packaging is one interpretation. Mainstream neuroanatomy notes the proximity and overlap but does not necessarily endorse the unitary functional construct. Whether the cluster behaves as a coordinated system in vivo is an empirical question separately from the anatomical observation.

**Source**: [Polyvagal Theory in Practice — face/vagus](https://www.polyvagaltheoryinpractice.com/vagus-nerve-face), [Kenhub — Glossopharyngeal Nerve](https://www.kenhub.com/en/library/anatomy/the-glossopharyngeal-nerve), [TeachMeAnatomy — Cranial Nerves](https://teachmeanatomy.info/head/cranial-nerves/summary/)

### Component 6: Respiratory Sinus Arrhythmia (RSA)

#### What RSA is
Heart rate is not constant. In a healthy resting adult, it speeds up slightly during inhalation and slows during exhalation. This **respiratory-coupled fluctuation in heart rate** is RSA. It is most pronounced at slow breathing rates (around 0.1 Hz, or six breaths per minute) and diminishes with faster breathing.

#### Why it happens (mechanism)
The dominant model attributes RSA to **rhythmic gating of cardiac vagal output** from the nucleus ambiguus by the brainstem respiratory rhythm generator. Inspiration inhibits cardiac vagal tone (heart speeds up); expiration restores it (heart slows). A secondary contribution comes from baroreceptor reflex modulation. There is ongoing debate about the precise weighting of feedforward (central) vs. feedback (peripheral) contributions — see Yasuma & Hayano (2004) and Eckberg's work — but the core picture (vagally-mediated, respiratory-coupled) is uncontested.

#### How RSA is measured
- **Spectral analysis** of the inter-beat interval (RR) time series from an ECG. Isolate the high-frequency band (HF: 0.15–0.40 Hz in adults) — this is RSA power.
- **Peak-to-trough method**: difference between maximum and minimum heart rate within each respiratory cycle.
- **Porges's "Porges-Bohrer method"**: band-pass filter centered on respiratory frequency.

#### Known confounds
RSA is the most-used noninvasive vagal index. It is also surprisingly easy to mislead. The major confounds:

| Confound | Direction of effect | Why it matters |
|----------|--------------------|----------------|
| **Breathing rate** | Slower breathing → larger RSA; faster → smaller. Even voluntary changes in pacing dramatically affect amplitude. | "Vagal tone" appears to improve when you ask someone to breathe at six breaths/min — even if nothing about their underlying parasympathetic system changed. |
| **Tidal volume** | Larger breaths → larger RSA. | Same as above; deep breathing alone can roughly double RSA amplitude in supine adults. |
| **Body position** | Supine RSA > standing RSA. Head-down tilt produces extreme RSA. | Posture matters; RSA measured sitting vs. lying are not directly comparable. |
| **Age** | RSA declines monotonically with age across the lifespan. | A 65-year-old's "low vagal tone" may be perfectly normal for age. |
| **Fitness** | Endurance-trained adults have higher resting RSA. | Confounds health/lifestyle with neural construct. |
| **Sex/menstrual cycle** | Modest effects; women generally higher than men in young adulthood. | |
| **Medications** | Beta-blockers, atropine, anticholinergics, SSRIs all affect RSA. | Common psychiatric meds can shift readings substantially. |
| **Voluntary respiratory control** | If subject knows their breathing is being measured, they may breathe deliberately. | Critical confound for any single-session "vagal tone" assessment. |
| **Coughing, talking, swallowing** | Disrupt the rhythm transiently. | Artifacts in short recordings. |

**Beauchaine 2015** ([PMC4389219](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4389219/)) reviews RSA as a transdiagnostic biomarker of emotion dysregulation. He notes that **RSA is most informative in terms of *reactivity* (how much it withdraws during emotion evocation) rather than baseline values**, because the latter are heavily contaminated by the confounds above while task-related changes within a single individual control for many of them.

**Practical implication**: a single "low vagal tone" reading from a wearable device sitting on the couch is not a reliable diagnostic. Trends over time, with controlled breathing protocols and consistent body position, are the only way to get meaningful longitudinal data. The cleanest research designs measure RSA *change* during a defined task (e.g., emotional reactivity paradigm) against the same person's baseline.

**Source**: [Beauchaine 2015 / PMC4389219](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4389219/), [Pinna et al. 2001 — RSA tidal volume](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11577909/), [Hirsch & Bishop 1981 — breathing pattern modulates HR](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7315987/), [Hayano et al. 2003 — RSA and daily activity](https://journals.physiology.org/doi/full/10.1152/ajpheart.00825.2003)

### Component 7: Other HRV Metrics (Beyond RSA)

RSA is one window onto heart rate variability; there are several others. Each captures a slightly different aspect of autonomic modulation. The Task Force of the European Society of Cardiology (1996) standardized the major metrics:

#### Time-domain metrics (computed directly from inter-beat intervals)

- **RMSSD** — Root Mean Square of Successive Differences. Captures short-term, beat-to-beat changes. Primarily reflects **parasympathetic/vagal** activity. Closely related to RSA and HF. Often considered the most reliable single index for vagal control in short recordings.
- **SDNN** — Standard Deviation of Normal-to-Normal RR intervals. Captures overall variability across the recording. Includes both sympathetic and parasympathetic contributions. Often used for 24-hour ambulatory recordings.
- **SDANN** — Standard Deviation of the Averages of NN intervals over 5-minute segments. Captures long-period rhythms (circadian, ultradian) within a 24-hour recording.
- **pNN50** — percentage of successive RR intervals differing by >50 ms. Vagal index, similar interpretation to RMSSD.

#### Frequency-domain metrics (power spectral density of the RR series)

- **HF (high frequency)** — 0.15 to 0.40 Hz. Reflects **respiratory sinus arrhythmia** and is generally considered a marker of cardiac vagal control. The frequency window corresponds to typical adult breathing rates.
- **LF (low frequency)** — 0.04 to 0.15 Hz. Often interpreted as a mixed sympathetic + parasympathetic + baroreflex band. The pure-sympathetic interpretation is **contested** in the contemporary literature; most experts now treat LF as reflecting baroreflex modulation.
- **VLF (very low frequency)** — 0.0033 to 0.04 Hz. Reflects thermoregulation, hormonal, and slow autonomic rhythms.
- **LF/HF ratio** — Popularly framed as the "sympathetic/parasympathetic balance ratio." This interpretation is **largely abandoned in mainstream HRV research** because LF is not a clean sympathetic index. Wearables that report it should be read with caution.

#### Practical relationships
- **RMSSD and HF** correlate strongly (r ≈ 0.85–0.95) and both index cardiac vagal control.
- **SDNN** correlates with both LF and HF in mixed proportions.
- For **short-term recordings (5 minutes or less)**, RMSSD is generally preferred over time-domain alternatives.
- For **long-term recordings (24 hours)**, SDNN captures overall variability including circadian rhythm.

**Source**: [Task Force 1996 / Circulation](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/01.cir.93.5.1043), [Wikipedia — HRV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_rate_variability), [Wang 2012 — SDNN/RMSSD as surrogate for LF/HF](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1155/2012/931943)

## How It Works (Functional Picture)

### Step 1: Visceral signal is generated
Stretch in the lung, distension in the gut, change in blood gas chemistry, baroreceptor stretch in the carotid sinus or aortic arch — these mechanical and chemical events trigger receptor potentials in vagal afferent terminals.

### Step 2: Afferent traffic ascends
The signal travels up unmyelinated C-fibers (slow, the majority) or myelinated A-fibers (fast, the minority). Cell bodies sit in the **nodose** and **jugular** ganglia just outside the skull. Central processes enter the medulla via the jugular foramen and terminate in the **nucleus tractus solitarius**.

### Step 3: Brainstem and forebrain processing
From the NTS, the signal projects to:
- **Parabrachial nucleus** (pons) — further integration with respiratory and cardiovascular control circuits.
- **Hypothalamus** — endocrine and homeostatic adjustments.
- **Amygdala** — affective evaluation.
- **Thalamus → insular cortex** — conscious interoceptive perception, especially in the right anterior insula. This is the cortical floor of "feeling your body" — sensing heartbeat, hunger, breath.

### Step 4: Top-down regulation
The same forebrain regions, plus medial prefrontal cortex, can modulate brainstem vagal preganglionic neurons. This is the Thayer-Lane "neurovisceral integration" picture: vmPFC → amygdala → brainstem vagal control.

### Step 5: Efferent output
Two main populations:
- **From DMN** (unmyelinated, slower): tonic parasympathetic input to gut, lungs, etc. — slow modulation of digestive and bronchial function.
- **From NA** (myelinated, faster): moment-to-moment cardiac modulation (the "vagal brake" — pulling heart rate back from sympathetic acceleration), plus voluntary-style motor control of larynx, pharynx, palate.

### Step 6: Body responds; afferents update
The loop closes. Effector tissues (heart, gut, larynx) change state; visceral receptors register the change; afferents send updated information back upstream.

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: Why slow breathing increases "vagal tone" readings
You sit down with a chest-strap heart rate monitor. You're breathing about 16 breaths/min naturally. Your RMSSD reads 35 ms. You're told to breathe at six breaths/min for two minutes; your RMSSD jumps to 85 ms. Has your parasympathetic system changed? Not necessarily — slow breathing shifts the respiratory rhythm into the HF band where RSA amplitude is maximal, and increases tidal volume, which mechanically magnifies the rhythmic modulation of heart rate. The reading is real; the inference "I have higher vagal tone now" is not straightforward.

### Example 2: A meal versus an emotional event
You eat a large meal. Your gastric mechanoreceptors fire, sending afferent signals via unmyelinated C-fibers up through DMN to NTS. Your NTS signals parabrachial and hypothalamus, which trigger postprandial responses — altered heart rate, blood flow redistribution, gut motility increase. Half an hour later, an emotionally evocative interaction occurs. Your NA cardiac neurons rapidly disinhibit; heart rate jumps. Then NA reapplies the brake within seconds. Two different vagal subsystems, two different speeds, both real.

### Example 3: Why a wearable's "vagal tone" reading on the couch isn't diagnostic
A wearable reports your RMSSD overnight as 28 ms. The same person measured at the same time using a research-grade ECG with controlled body position, no medications, and standardized breathing pacing would likely read differently. Wearable-grade measurements vary with sleep position, snoring, alcohol, late meal, stress about the wearable itself. The metric *is real*; the *single reading interpretation* often isn't.

## Common Misconceptions

### Misconception: "Stimulating the vagus directly relaxes you because the vagus is the parasympathetic nerve"
**Reality**: The vagus is mostly *afferent*. Stimulating it sends signals up to the brainstem and onward to higher regulatory centers. Any calming effect is the result of those upstream circuits acting downward on the body — not direct efferent action of the stimulated fibers in most cases. Stimulation can also activate efferent fibers, but they are the minority. The mechanism is more "uploading interoceptive signal to regulatory networks" than "pressing a relaxation button."

### Misconception: "The vagus nerve is the 'vagal brake' — its main job is slowing heart rate"
**Reality**: The cardiac branch from nucleus ambiguus does brake the heart, and that's important. But it's a small fraction of vagal output and an even smaller fraction of total vagal fibers. Most of what the vagus does is sensory (the ~80%) plus gut/digestive parasympathetic regulation (most of the DMN efferent output).

### Misconception: "LF/HF ratio measures your sympathetic-to-parasympathetic balance"
**Reality**: This interpretation has been largely abandoned in serious HRV research. LF power includes baroreflex contributions and is not a clean sympathetic index. Consumer wearables and many older clinical writeups still use the ratio, but the literature is much more cautious now.

### Misconception: "RSA is a stable trait — your 'vagal tone' is a fixed number"
**Reality**: RSA is highly state-dependent. Breathing rate, position, fitness, medications, time of day, recent meals, and emotional state all shift it. Reactivity (change during task) is generally more informative than baseline values, and even reactivity needs controlled measurement conditions.

### Misconception: "The dorsal motor nucleus is mainly for 'shutdown' / 'freeze' states"
**Reality**: This is a polyvagal-specific interpretation that the broader neuroanatomy literature does not endorse. Mainstream physiology characterizes the DMN as the primary source of parasympathetic visceromotor output to subdiaphragmatic organs — gut, GI tract, etc. The behavioral interpretation as a "freeze nucleus" is contested (see Neuhuber & Berthoud 2022; covered in Category 3 — the Critique). For purposes of this anatomy chapter, the DMN is best described by what it innervates (gut/GI) rather than by a behavioral label.

### Misconception: "Mammals have a uniquely myelinated cardiac vagus from nucleus ambiguus"
**Reality**: This is a polyvagal-specific claim that has been challenged by comparative neuroanatomy. Lungfish, sharks, bony fish, amphibians, reptiles, and birds also show myelinated cardiac vagal projections from ventrolateral brainstem sources. This is covered in Category 3 (the Critique). For purposes of this chapter: yes, in mammals, the nucleus ambiguus is the dominant cardiac parasympathetic source; no, this is not unique to mammals as a feature of vertebrate cardiac control.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **Bilateral vagotomy** (transection of both vagal trunks below the diaphragm) — used historically for peptic ulcer disease. Profound effects on gastric motility; modest effect on resting heart rate (because cardiac vagal supply is above the level of cut and partially preserved). Illustrates how regional the effects are.
- **Bezold-Jarisch reflex** — vagal afferent stimulation from cardiac chemoreceptors triggers profound bradycardia and hypotension. Sometimes invoked clinically in fainting episodes. A pure vagal-reflex example without need for any polyvagal interpretation.
- **Diving reflex** — facial cold exposure → trigeminal afferents → brainstem → vagal cardiac efferents → bradycardia. Real, robust, present even in some non-mammalian vertebrates. Often cited as a "vagus hack" but is a specific reflex, not a generic relaxation tool.
- **Vagal cardiac dominance varies during development** — fetal and neonatal heart rate control is less vagally dominant than adult; this is why baseline RSA is lower in infancy and rises through childhood.
- **Implanted cervical VNS** (left-sided, ~30 Hz, FDA-approved for refractory epilepsy and treatment-resistant depression) — primarily activates afferent A and B fibers because of their lower stimulation threshold. The reflex modulation upstream is presumed mechanism. Notably it has *not* been shown effective for autism in adequately powered RCTs.

## Research Gaps

- **Exact modern fiber count in human cervical vagus**: Modern human morphometric data using electron microscopy is sparser than the classic cat data; updated human-specific numbers would clarify how generalizable the textbook 80/90/70 approximations are.
- **Individual variability**: Most quoted ratios are population averages. Inter-individual variation in vagal fiber composition is plausibly substantial (factor-of-two or more) but not extensively characterized.
- **Functional consequences of the afferent dominance**: It is well-established that vagus is ~80% afferent, but what those afferents actually do (in terms of behavioral and cognitive consequences) is an active area where interoception research, predictive processing models, and traditional autonomic neuroscience are still working out the integration.
- **RSA-vagal-tone linkage strength**: RSA is the standard proxy for cardiac vagal tone, but pharmacological blockade studies show the link is good but not perfect (~70–80% shared variance). Other (e.g., respiratory feedforward) contributions exist.
