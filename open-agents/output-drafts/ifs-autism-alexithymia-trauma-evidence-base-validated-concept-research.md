# Concept Research: IFS Evidence Base — What Has Actually Been Validated

## Overview

"Evidence-based" is doing heavy lifting in IFS marketing. In contemporary clinical psychology, the phrase is a spectrum: at the minimal end, it means "at least one published trial showing benefit on at least one outcome"; at the strong end, it means "multiple replicated randomized trials, in independent labs, showing between-arm superiority over credible comparators across multiple conditions." For IFS as of 2026, the picture is roughly this: two published RCTs (rheumatoid arthritis 2013, depression 2016/2017), one widely-cited but uncontrolled PTSD pilot (2021), one 2025 substance-use pilot, a 2025 scoping review counting 27 studies total with only ~7% RCT-quality, and a now-defunct 2015 federal registry listing whose continued citation by IFS marketing is materially misleading.

This page distinguishes IFS-Foundation-funded from independent work, equivalence findings from superiority findings, pilot from confirmatory work, and live federal endorsement from archived private mirrors of a registry that no longer exists. The purpose is to give a calibrated reader something to anchor to — not "IFS is debunked" and not "IFS is proven," but a clear-eyed map of what each piece of evidence does and does not establish.

The reader should leave this page able to say sentences like: "IFS has a single proof-of-concept RCT for rheumatoid arthritis with sustained 1-year benefits"; "IFS has shown equivalence to CBT and IPT in one small pilot RCT for depression in college women"; "the most-marketed PTSD numbers come from an uncontrolled pilot with n=17 and an effect size that would not survive a real RCT"; and "the SAMHSA listing is a 2015 evaluation of a registry shut down in 2018, archived on a private IFS-affiliated mirror."

## Core Components

### Component 1: Shadick et al. 2013 — Rheumatoid Arthritis RCT
- **Citation**: Shadick NA, Sowell NF, Frits ML, Hoffman SM, Hartz SA, Booth FD, Sweezy M, Rogers PR, Dubuis RL, Atkinson J, Friedman P, Augusto F, Iannaccone CK, Fossel AH, Quinn G, Cui J, Losina E, Schwartz RC. *A Randomized Controlled Trial of an Internal Family Systems-Based Psychotherapeutic Intervention on Outcomes in Rheumatoid Arthritis: A Proof-of-Concept Study.* Journal of Rheumatology, 40(11): 1831–1841, 2013. DOI: 10.3899/jrheum.121465. PubMed: 23950186.
- **Design**: Two-arm RCT. IFS group intervention 9 months (n=39) vs. mailed RA-education control (n=40). Total n=79 enrolled, 68 completed assessments, 82% IFS protocol completion. Authors describe explicitly as "proof-of-concept."
- **Outcome measures**: Self-assessed joint pain (RA Disease Activity Index), SF-12 physical function, VAS overall pain, Beck Depression Inventory, State-Trait Anxiety Inventory, self-compassion. Disease activity (DAS-CRP4) by blinded rheumatologist.
- **Posttreatment findings (favoring IFS)**: Overall pain mean change −14.9 (SD 29.1; p=0.04); physical function +14.6 (SD 25.3; p=0.04).
- **1-year follow-up findings (favoring IFS, sustained)**: Self-assessed joint pain −0.6 (SD 1.1; p=0.04); self-compassion +1.8 (SD 2.8; p=0.01); depressive symptoms −3.2 (SD 5.0; p=0.01).
- **Null findings**: No sustained improvements in anxiety, self-efficacy, or objective disease activity.
- **Funding/affiliations**: Last author Schwartz RC is the IFS founder; Sweezy is an IFS Institute author. The trial was conducted at Brigham and Women's Hospital / Harvard Medical School; the senior medical/biostat team appears independent of IFS Institute. Conflict-of-interest disclosure is partial: trial leadership includes IFS-affiliated authors, which is a non-trivial COI in interpreting the headline results.
- **What this study establishes**: IFS produces clinically meaningful, sustained benefits on patient-reported pain, depression, and self-compassion in a single chronic-illness population, vs. a deliberately weak (mailed-information) control.
- **What this study does NOT establish**: That IFS works for any other condition. That IFS outperforms an active psychotherapy comparator (the comparator was education, not therapy). That biological RA disease activity changes (it didn't). That the effects generalize to populations less like the trial sample (mostly white, mostly female, U.S. urban academic-medical-center patients).

### Component 2: Haddock et al. 2016/2017 — Depression Pilot RCT
- **Citation**: Haddock SA, Weiler LM, Trump LJ, Henry KL. *The Efficacy of Internal Family Systems Therapy in the Treatment of Depression Among Female College Students: A Pilot Study.* Journal of Marital and Family Therapy, 43(1): 131–144, 2017 (online 2016). PubMed: 27500908.
- **Design**: Pilot RCT. Female university students with depression randomized to IFS (n=17) or treatment-as-usual (TAU; CBT or IPT, n=15). Total n=32 (some sources cite 37 enrolled; 32 in the analytic sample). 16-session protocol; many participants completed 11–15.
- **Outcome measures**: Depression severity (BDI-II and similar instruments).
- **Findings**: Both arms showed clinically meaningful decline in depressive symptoms. **No significant between-group differences** in magnitude or rate of change. Authors describe as "preliminary evidence for the efficacy of IFS in the treatment of depressive symptoms."
- **What this is — and is not**: This is an **equivalence-suggestive pilot**, NOT a superiority trial. "Both groups improved similarly" is the actual finding. It is also explicitly underpowered (n=32 for a between-arm comparison is too small to confidently detect superiority OR equivalence; it is closer to "non-rejection of the null" than affirmative equivalence).
- **Important methodological notes**: 4 of 5 IFS therapists had less than 1 year of experience; >50% of TAU participants used antidepressant medication concurrently while no IFS participants did. These imbalances cut both ways and are themselves reasons to treat the result as preliminary.
- **What this study establishes**: That short-course IFS produces depression reductions comparable to short-course CBT or IPT in a small sample of mildly-to-moderately depressed female college students.
- **What this study does NOT establish**: That IFS is superior to CBT or IPT for depression. That the effect generalizes beyond the demographic studied. That the result would replicate with adequately experienced therapists in a larger sample.
- **Equivalence vs. superiority — the key distinction**: A superiority trial asks "Does A beat B?" An equivalence (or non-inferiority) trial asks "Does A perform within a pre-specified margin of B, with adequate power?" The Haddock pilot is neither: it is an underpowered superiority test that failed to find a difference. Reporting it as "IFS works as well as CBT/IPT" overstates what the design supports.

### Component 3: Hodgdon et al. 2021 — PTSD Pilot Effectiveness Study
- **Citation**: Hodgdon HB, Anderson FG, Southwell E, Hrubec W, Schwartz RC. *Internal Family Systems (IFS) Therapy for Posttraumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) Among Survivors of Multiple Childhood Trauma: A Pilot Effectiveness Study.* Journal of Aggression, Maltreatment & Trauma, 31(1): 22–43, 2022 (online December 2021). DOI: 10.1080/10926771.2021.2013375.
- **Design**: **Uncontrolled, open-label, single-arm pilot effectiveness study with within-subject (pre/post) design.** No control group. No randomization.
- **Sample**: n=17 adult survivors of multiple childhood traumas with current PTSD diagnosis. 13 completers in some analyses.
- **Intervention**: 16 weekly 90-minute IFS sessions over ~16 weeks.
- **Headline findings**: At 1-month follow-up, **92% of completers no longer met PTSD criteria.** Within-subject Cohen's d for CAPS PTSD severity was reported as **d = −4.46**. Significant within-subject reductions in depression, dissociation, somatization, affect dysregulation, and disrupted self-perception; improvements in self-compassion and interoceptive awareness.
- **Funding**: Foundation for Self Leadership (the IFS Institute's research-funding arm). Lead author at Trauma Center / Justice Resource Institute; co-author Schwartz is the IFS founder; co-author Anderson is a senior IFS-Institute-affiliated trauma clinician.
- **Why d = −4.46 is implausibly large**: For context, established trauma-focused therapies (PE, CPT, EMDR) typically produce within-group effect sizes in the d ≈ 0.8–1.5 range in large RCTs and between-group effects (vs. waitlist) in the d ≈ 0.5–1.0 range. A within-group d of −4.46 means the post-treatment mean is 4.46 standard deviations below the pre-treatment mean — a magnitude not seen anywhere in the credible PTSD treatment literature. The authors themselves acknowledge in the limitations section that "these effect sizes are larger than what has been observed in the PTSD treatment literature" and that "this was an open, uncontrolled trial using a within subjects design, which likely inflated the effect size to some degree."
- **What this study establishes**: Preliminary signal that IFS is feasible and acceptable for adult survivors of multiple childhood trauma, with promising within-subject improvement.
- **What this study does NOT establish**: Anything about between-group efficacy. Anything about how IFS compares to CPT, PE, EMDR. The d = −4.46 figure is not a defensible point estimate of IFS's effect on PTSD; it is the upper bound of what an uncontrolled pilot with n=17 can produce when expectancy, regression to the mean, selection effects, and assessor non-blinding all push in the same direction.
- **How this number gets used**: "92% no longer met PTSD criteria" is widely cited in IFS marketing. The asterisks (uncontrolled, n=17, IFS-Foundation-funded, effect size implausible) are usually stripped off. A calibrated reader treats this as a positive feasibility signal that warrants a real RCT — which, as of 2026, has not been published.

### Component 4: Ally et al. 2025 — PARTS-SUD Online Group Pilot
- **Citation**: Ally D, Tobiasz-Veltz L, Tu K, et al. *A pilot study of an online group-based Internal Family Systems intervention for comorbid posttraumatic stress disorder and substance use.* Frontiers in Psychiatry, 16: 1544435, 2025. DOI: 10.3389/fpsyt.2025.1544435.
- **Design**: Single-arm pilot (no control). 12-week intervention: 12 weekly 90-minute group sessions + 6 individual 50-minute counseling sessions. Online/telehealth format.
- **Sample**: n=10 adults with comorbid PTSD and substance use disorder. Mean age 44.5 (SD 16.8). 40% male, 40% female, 20% non-binary. 40% White, 40% Hispanic/Latinx, 20% Asian.
- **Primary findings (acceptability/feasibility)**: Acceptability 86% (TFA general acceptability 4.3/5); willingness to refer 92% (4.6/5); 70% retention at 12 weeks (7/10 completers).
- **Exploratory clinical outcomes**: PTSD severity (PCL-5) reduced 1.7 points/week (95% CI: −2.45, −0.93; p=0.002); craving reduced 0.25 points/week (95% CI: −0.45, −0.06; p=0.014); 54% achieved a minimally important difference on PCL-5.
- **Funding**: Richard C. Schwartz Research Fellowship; Foundation for Self Leadership. Authors at Cambridge Health Alliance / Harvard Medical School.
- **What this establishes**: PARTS-SUD is feasible and acceptable as an online group intervention for PTSD-SUD comorbidity. Preliminary signal of within-subject reductions in PTSD severity and craving.
- **What this does NOT establish**: Efficacy compared to any active control (or to no treatment). The exploratory clinical outcomes are within-subject in a single-arm pilot; they cannot rule out non-specific effects, regression to the mean, or expectancy.
- **Why it matters for this category**: Substance use is a non-trivial application area for IFS (firefighter parts often involve substance involvement), and this is the first published pilot specifically designed for the comorbidity. It also fills a gap the 2025 scoping review noted.

### Component 5: Buys 2025 — Scoping Review of IFS Evidence
- **Citation**: Buys ME. *Exploring the evidence for Internal Family Systems therapy: a scoping review of current research, gaps, and future directions.* Clinical Psychologist, 2025. DOI: 10.1080/13284207.2025.2533127.
- **Method**: Scoping review (broader inclusion criteria than systematic review). Searched Medline, Scopus, PsycINFO, Google Scholar.
- **Counts**: **27 studies met inclusion criteria.** Breakdown:
  - 2 RCTs (~7%): Haddock 2016/2017 depression; Shadick 2013 RA
  - 5 quasi-experimental studies
  - 3 qualitative exploratory studies
  - 17 case studies (~63%)
- **Synthesized conclusion**: Existing evidence is "promising" particularly for chronic pain, depression, PTSD, and self-compassion. Larger fully-powered clinical trials needed for each indication. **Adverse-event monitoring is lacking** across the evidence base.
- **Why this is the most important single source on this page**: It is the most recent and most independent map of the IFS evidence base. The ~7% RCT rate after roughly 40 years of model development is the single most calibrating fact a reader can take away. Most modalities of IFS's age and adoption level have a deeper RCT literature; IFS does not.

### Component 6: SAMHSA NREPP — The Corrected Framing
- **Background**: NREPP (National Registry of Evidence-Based Programs and Practices) was a SAMHSA-administered public registry that vetted mental health and substance use interventions. It originated in 1997, was redesigned and re-launched in 2007, and accepted submissions from outside developers on a voluntary basis.
- **IFS listing**: IFS was added to NREPP on **November 23, 2015**, following a comprehensive application submitted in April 2014 and an independent SAMHSA scientific review. NREPP rated IFS as having "effective" evidence for "general functioning and well-being" and "promising" evidence for anxiety, depression, physical health, and self-concept outcomes. **Critically: the rating was for general functioning and well-being, NOT for PTSD or any other specific clinical condition.** The single source study underlying the listing was Shadick et al. 2013.
- **NREPP discontinuation**: The federal contract administering NREPP was terminated for "the convenience of the government" on **December 28, 2017**. The website was effectively shuttered and phased out in early **2018**. SAMHSA Assistant Secretary Elizabeth McCance-Katz explicitly characterized NREPP's standards as inadequate and the registry's coverage as "skewed" because it relied on self-submission. In April 2018, SAMHSA launched a different "Evidence-Based Practices Resource Center" that explicitly "does not replace NREPP and does not contain all of the resources that were previously available."
- **The current "samhsa-nrepp.foundationifs.org" URL**: This is a **private archive maintained by the IFS Foundation**, not a current federal site. It mirrors the 2015 NREPP rating page so that IFS marketing can continue to display it. This is not a federal endorsement.
- **Why this matters**: When IFS marketing copy says "IFS is on the SAMHSA registry," it is referring to a registry that has not existed as a live federal site for over eight years, evaluating IFS on a single 2013 trial in rheumatoid arthritis, for "general functioning and well-being" rather than for PTSD or any specific clinical condition. The honest sentence is: "IFS was added to a SAMHSA registry in November 2015 for general functioning and well-being; that registry was discontinued in 2018; the current archive is maintained by an IFS-affiliated foundation."

## How It Works — How to Read the IFS Evidence Base

### Step 1: Distinguish what type of trial you're looking at
- **RCT (randomized controlled trial)**: Random assignment to intervention vs. comparator. Different comparators (waitlist, education, active treatment) produce different conclusions. IFS has 2 published RCTs.
- **Pilot study / open trial**: Often single-arm, no randomization, no control. Cannot distinguish treatment effect from non-specific effects, regression to the mean, expectancy, or selection. IFS has multiple of these (Hodgdon 2021, Ally 2025, others).
- **Case study / case series**: One or a few clients described. Useful for hypothesis generation, not for efficacy claims. ~63% of IFS-published research falls in this category.

### Step 2: Distinguish equivalence from superiority
- **Superiority**: A is better than B by some pre-specified margin. Requires adequate power and a credible comparator.
- **Equivalence**: A is within a pre-specified margin of B, both directions. Requires *more* power than superiority.
- **Underpowered superiority that fails to find a difference**: Cannot conclude equivalence. The Haddock 2016/2017 pilot is in this category.

### Step 3: Distinguish within-group from between-group effect sizes
- **Within-group (pre-post)**: How much did the treated group change from baseline? Includes treatment effect *plus* expectancy, attention, regression to the mean, natural recovery.
- **Between-group (vs. comparator)**: How much more did treatment improve than the comparator? Subtracts the non-specific effects.
- A within-group d of −4.46 is not the same as a between-group d of −4.46. The Hodgdon 2021 figure is within-group; there is no between-group estimate because there was no comparator.

### Step 4: Note the funding and affiliations
- Most cited IFS trials are funded by the Foundation for Self Leadership (the IFS Institute's research arm) and/or have authorship including Schwartz, Sweezy, Anderson, or other senior IFS-Institute figures.
- This does not invalidate the work, but it is a non-trivial conflict of interest that the reader should track. Independent (non-IFS-affiliated) replication is what would change the picture most.

### Step 5: Map studies to conditions
- **Rheumatoid arthritis**: 1 RCT (Shadick 2013). Sustained benefits at 1 year.
- **Depression**: 1 pilot RCT (Haddock 2016/2017). Equivalence-suggestive.
- **PTSD**: 1 uncontrolled pilot (Hodgdon 2021). Preliminary signal.
- **Substance use comorbid with PTSD**: 1 single-arm pilot (Ally 2025). Acceptability/feasibility.
- **Anxiety**: No targeted RCT. NREPP-listed as "promising" on Shadick 2013 secondary outcome only.
- **Eating disorders**: No published RCT, despite IFS's origin in Schwartz's 1980s bulimia work.
- **Dissociative disorders**: No published RCT or controlled trial.
- **Autism, alexithymia, neurodivergent populations**: No published peer-reviewed studies of any kind.

### Step 6: Place IFS on the strength-of-evidence scale
- **Minimal threshold for "evidence-based"** (one or more peer-reviewed RCTs for one condition): IFS meets this for rheumatoid arthritis. Arguably meets it for depression with the equivalence-pilot caveat.
- **Strong threshold** (replicated RCTs across multiple conditions, with active comparators, ideally including non-IFS-affiliated independent labs): IFS does NOT meet this for any condition.
- For comparison: CBT for depression has hundreds of RCTs across decades, multiple meta-analyses, and replicated superiority over waitlist and active comparators. EMDR for PTSD has dozens of RCTs and APA / WHO guideline endorsement. IFS has 1 RCT in RA, 1 underpowered pilot RCT in depression, and 1 uncontrolled pilot in PTSD. These are different orders of magnitude.

## Terminology Glossary

| Term | Definition | Notes |
|------|------------|-------|
| RCT | Randomized controlled trial — random assignment to intervention vs. comparator | The minimum standard for efficacy claims |
| Effect size (Cohen's d) | Standardized mean difference; ~0.2 small, ~0.5 medium, ~0.8 large | Within-group d ≠ between-group d |
| Pilot study | Small-scale preliminary trial, often without full controls | Establishes feasibility, not efficacy |
| Effectiveness vs. efficacy | Effectiveness = real-world impact; efficacy = controlled-conditions impact | Both terms used in IFS literature; not interchangeable |
| Equivalence trial | Pre-specified margin design to test "as good as" comparator | Requires more power than superiority |
| Superiority trial | Tests whether A beats B | What people usually mean by "RCT" |
| Underpowered superiority | Sample too small to detect a difference if it exists | Failure to find a difference is not evidence of equivalence |
| NREPP | National Registry of Evidence-Based Programs and Practices | Discontinued 2018 |
| Foundation for Self Leadership | IFS Institute's research-funding nonprofit arm | Funded most cited IFS trials |
| Within-group effect size | How much the treated group changed pre-post | Includes non-specific effects |
| Between-group effect size | Treatment-vs-comparator difference | Subtracts non-specific effects |
| CAPS | Clinician-Administered PTSD Scale | Gold-standard PTSD measure |
| PCL-5 | PTSD Checklist for DSM-5 | Self-report PTSD measure |
| Treatment-as-usual (TAU) | Whatever active care the comparator group would normally get | Used in Haddock 2016/2017 (CBT or IPT) |

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: How "92% no longer meet PTSD criteria" gets used vs. what it actually says
- **Marketing-style sentence**: "IFS is so effective that 92% of patients no longer meet criteria for PTSD."
- **What the study actually showed**: Of n=17 enrolled, 13 completers, in an uncontrolled single-arm pilot funded by the IFS Foundation, 12 of 13 (92% of completers, ~71% of intent-to-treat) no longer met PTSD criteria at 1-month follow-up after 16 sessions.
- **What's missing from the marketing sentence**: No control group, n=17, IFS-Foundation-funded, completer-only analysis, no comparison to PE/CPT/EMDR (which have similar or better headline numbers in proper RCTs), no follow-up beyond 1 month.
- **Calibrated sentence**: "An uncontrolled pilot of 17 adults with childhood trauma–related PTSD found that 92% of completers no longer met PTSD criteria at 1-month follow-up after 16 sessions of IFS. Effect sizes from this design routinely shrink in subsequent RCTs."

### Example 2: How "IFS is on the SAMHSA registry" gets used vs. what it actually says
- **Marketing-style sentence**: "IFS is listed on SAMHSA's National Registry for Evidence-Based Programs and Practices."
- **What is actually true**: IFS was added to NREPP on November 23, 2015. The federal contract for NREPP was terminated on December 28, 2017, and the registry was effectively shuttered in early 2018. The current "samhsa-nrepp.foundationifs.org" URL is a private archive maintained by the IFS Foundation, not a federal site. The 2015 rating was for "general functioning and well-being," based on a single trial (Shadick 2013 in RA), and was rated only "promising" for depression, anxiety, physical health, and self-concept — not "effective" for any specific clinical condition.
- **Calibrated sentence**: "IFS was added to a SAMHSA registry in 2015 for general functioning and well-being. That registry was discontinued by SAMHSA in 2018. The 'SAMHSA listing' that current IFS marketing references is hosted on a private IFS-affiliated mirror and reflects a 2015 evaluation of a single 2013 trial."

### Example 3: How equivalence gets read as efficacy
- **Common claim**: "IFS works as well as CBT and IPT for depression."
- **What Haddock 2016/2017 actually showed**: In an underpowered pilot RCT (n=32) of female college students, both arms showed similar declines in depression. The study did not detect a difference between IFS and CBT/IPT. The TAU arm had >50% on antidepressants concurrently; the IFS arm had none.
- **Why "as well as" is a stretch**: The study was not powered to demonstrate equivalence. "Did not detect a difference" with n=32 is not the same as "is equivalent." With more power, a difference might emerge in either direction.
- **Calibrated sentence**: "A small pilot RCT (n=32) of depressed female college students found similar declines in depression for IFS and treatment-as-usual (CBT or IPT). The trial was too small to confirm equivalence; it found no significant difference, with caveats about therapist experience and concurrent medication use."

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: IFS is on the SAMHSA registry today.
  - **Reality**: NREPP was discontinued in 2018. IFS was listed in 2015 for general functioning/well-being. The current "SAMHSA-NREPP" page on the IFS Foundation site is a private archive.

- **Misconception**: "92% PTSD remission" is a robust finding.
  - **Reality**: It comes from an uncontrolled n=17 pilot with implausibly large effect sizes. It is a feasibility signal, not a confirmatory result.

- **Misconception**: IFS has been shown to work as well as CBT for depression.
  - **Reality**: One small pilot RCT failed to detect a difference. That is not the same as demonstrated equivalence.

- **Misconception**: IFS is "evidence-based" in the strong sense.
  - **Reality**: It is "evidence-based" only in the minimal sense (one or two peer-reviewed RCTs, mostly on a narrow set of conditions, with significant IFS-Foundation funding involvement).

- **Misconception**: The lack of evidence in autism, alexithymia, or eating disorders is just a matter of time.
  - **Reality**: It also reflects that IFS Institute training and writing has expanded into these populations far faster than research funding has prioritized rigorous studies in them. Eating disorders is particularly striking — Schwartz developed IFS with bulimic patients in the 1980s, and there is still no eating-disorder RCT 40 years later.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **The 2025 PARTS-SUD pilot** is recent and partially fills the substance use gap, but it is single-arm with n=10. Treat as feasibility/acceptability evidence, not efficacy.
- **There may be in-progress RCTs** (e.g., ongoing PTSD trials registered on clinicaltrials.gov, ongoing IFS-for-eating-disorders work) that have not yet published results. The picture above is the *published* evidence as of April 2026.
- **Some IFS-aligned chronic-pain work** exists (Frank Anderson, Schwartz collaborations) but is largely uncontrolled outcome reporting, not RCT-quality evidence.
- **TIST (Trauma-Informed Stabilization Treatment)**, Janina Fisher's IFS-plus-structural-dissociation integration, has clinical reception and clinician training infrastructure but not peer-reviewed RCT evidence as a packaged intervention.

## Related Concepts

- **Conflict of interest in psychotherapy research**: When trial funders and authors are also the developers/IP-holders of the modality being studied. A long-recognized issue across psychotherapy research, not unique to IFS, but particularly tractable in IFS's case because the funding pipeline is so concentrated.
- **Allegiance effect**: A meta-analytic finding that researchers' allegiance to a treatment correlates with the size of the effects they report. IFS-affiliated researchers reporting large IFS effects is consistent with this pattern (it does not invalidate the findings, but it should temper effect-size estimates).
- **Common factors vs. specific factors**: Some psychotherapy researchers argue that most of the effect of any therapy is non-specific (alliance, expectancy, hope, attention) and that "specific" mechanisms account for less of the variance than treatment-marketing implies. IFS's pilot effect sizes are vulnerable to this critique because the controls (when present) are weak.
- **Jonathan Shedler's critique of "evidence-based"**: Shedler and others argue that "evidence-based" has become a marketing term that obscures the gap between minimal and strong evidence. The IFS case is an example.

## Research Gaps

- Independent (non-IFS-Foundation-funded) replication of the Hodgdon PTSD pilot has not yet been published.
- No published RCT comparing IFS to PE, CPT, or EMDR for PTSD as of April 2026.
- No published RCT for eating disorders, despite IFS's bulimia origin.
- No published peer-reviewed studies of IFS in autistic, alexithymic, or generally neurodivergent populations.
- Adverse-event monitoring across IFS studies is inconsistent or absent — Buys 2025 explicitly flags this.
- No head-to-head comparison of IFS as a standalone modality vs. IFS as embedded within trauma-informed integrative approaches (TIST, sensorimotor + IFS, etc.).
