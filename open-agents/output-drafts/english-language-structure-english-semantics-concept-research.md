# Concept Research: English Semantics — How English Encodes Meaning

## Overview

English semantics — the study of how meaning is structured in the language — reveals a system of extraordinary depth and complexity. The Oxford English Dictionary records approximately 171,476 words in current use, plus 47,000 obsolete words, making English one of the largest vocabularies of any language. But the true semantic richness of English lies not in sheer quantity but in the structural organization of that vocabulary: its systematic stratification into three etymological-register layers (Germanic everyday, French formal, Latin-Greek technical), its massive polysemy (the word "set" has 430+ senses in the OED; "run" has 645 uses; "get" has 150+ senses), and the pervasive metaphorical extension that continuously generates new meanings from old ones by mapping spatial and physical experience onto abstract domains.

The three-layer lexicon — English's signature semantic feature — is not merely an accident of history but a functioning register system that native speakers deploy unconsciously to signal formality, social context, and domain. The Germanic base provides the everyday, concrete, emotionally direct vocabulary: "gut," "hearty," "begin," "ask," "help," "fire," "fear." The French overlay (post-1066 Norman Conquest) supplies the formal, social, and administrative vocabulary: "stomach," "cordial," "commence," "question," "aid," "flame," "terror." The Latin-Greek learned layer (Renaissance borrowing) furnishes the technical, abstract, and scientific vocabulary: "gastric," "cardiac," "initiate," "interrogate," "assist," "conflagration," "trepidation." These layers do not just provide synonyms — they provide register-calibrated alternatives that carry different connotations, collocational preferences, and social signals. The Latinate-to-Germanic ratio in a text is a reliable quantitative measure of its formality level.

For a pattern-oriented learner, English semantics offers particularly satisfying structural analysis. Polysemy — the phenomenon of one word having many related meanings — follows systematic radial category structure, where a core (usually spatial or physical) meaning extends outward to increasingly abstract domains through metaphorical mapping. The word "grasp" extends from physical grasping → understanding; "see" from visual perception → comprehension; "weigh" from physical measurement → cognitive evaluation. These are not random associations but instances of the same cross-domain mapping patterns that Lakoff and Johnson identified in conceptual metaphor theory, operating at the level of individual English words. Similarly, collocational patterns (why "strong tea" but not *"powerful tea," why "heavy rain" but not *"strong rain"), semantic prosody (why "cause" attracts negative events), and the meaning differences between syntactic constructions all follow systematic rules that become visible once the structural framework is understood.

## Core Components

### Component 1: Vocabulary Size and Structure

- **Purpose**: Provide the raw material — the word stock — from which English builds meaning
- **Structure**:
  - **Total vocabulary**: ~171,476 current words in the OED, plus ~47,000 obsolete words, plus ~9,500 derivative words
  - **Active vocabulary of an educated adult**: ~20,000-35,000 word families (a "word family" includes the base word and all its inflected and derived forms)
  - **Passive vocabulary**: ~40,000-70,000 word families (words understood but not actively used)
  - **New words per year**: Approximately 1,000+ new entries added to major dictionaries annually

  **Etymology distribution** (approximate):
  - Latin (including via French): ~29%
  - French: ~29%
  - Germanic (Anglo-Saxon + Old Norse): ~26%
  - Greek: ~6%
  - Other languages: ~10%
  - Note: by TOKEN frequency (how often words appear in text), Germanic words dominate overwhelmingly — the 100 most frequent English words are almost entirely Germanic (the, of, and, to, a, in, is, it, you, that...)

  **Why English vocabulary is so large**:
  - Three major source languages (Germanic, French, Latin) contributed overlapping vocabulary rather than replacing each other
  - Open borrowing policy — English borrows freely from any language (sushi, kindergarten, algebra, tsunami, safari, karma)
  - Productive word-formation processes generate new words continuously
  - Low morphological marking means distinctions encoded by inflection in other languages require separate words in English

- **Key insight**: English vocabulary is simultaneously redundant (multiple words for the same concept from different etymological layers) and precise (each layer carries different register connotations, allowing fine-grained control of formality and domain)

### Component 2: The Three-Layer Register System

- **Purpose**: Provide systematically organized register alternatives for the same concept at different formality levels
- **Structure**:

  **Layer 1 — Germanic (Anglo-Saxon and Old Norse):**
  - Characteristics: short (1-2 syllables), concrete, emotionally direct, initial stress
  - Register: everyday, informal, intimate, child-directed
  - Word types: body parts (head, hand, heart, gut), basic actions (eat, drink, walk, sleep, run), core emotions (love, hate, fear, anger), kinship (mother, father, brother, sister), nature (sun, moon, water, earth, fire)
  - Percentage of running text: ~60-70% (Germanic words are the functional backbone)

  **Layer 2 — French (post-1066 Norman Conquest):**
  - Characteristics: medium length (2-3 syllables), social/administrative, elegant
  - Register: formal, literary, institutional, professional
  - Domains especially enriched: law (justice, court, judge, jury, prison, crime), government (parliament, authority, sovereign, minister), cuisine (beef, pork, veal, mutton, sauce, roast), fashion (garment, costume, tailor), art (beauty, design, portrait)
  - Historical reason: French was the language of the ruling class for ~300 years after 1066; English-speaking peasants tended the animals (cow, sheep, pig — Germanic), while French-speaking nobles ate the meat (beef, mutton, pork — French)

  **Layer 3 — Latin and Greek (Renaissance scholarly borrowing):**
  - Characteristics: long (3+ syllables), abstract, technical, stress-shifting suffixes
  - Register: academic, scientific, medical, legal-philosophical
  - Domains: medicine (cardiac, gastric, renal, pulmonary), science (hypothesis, phenomenon, taxonomy), philosophy (epistemology, ontology, teleology), law (jurisprudence, litigation, adjudication)
  - Historical reason: Latin was the language of scholarship; Greek was the language of science. When English began replacing Latin in academic writing (16th-17th centuries), it borrowed massively from both to fill the technical vocabulary gap

  **Systematic triplets** (same concept, three registers):

  | Concept | Germanic | French | Latin/Greek |
  |---------|----------|--------|-------------|
  | "ask" | ask | question | interrogate |
  | "begin" | begin | commence | initiate |
  | "end" | end | finish | conclude / terminate |
  | "help" | help | aid | assist |
  | "rise" | rise | mount | ascend |
  | "hide" | hide | conceal | secrete |
  | "kingly" | kingly | royal | regal |
  | "fatherly" | fatherly | paternal | patriarchal |
  | "fear" | fear | terror | trepidation |
  | "holy" | holy | sacred | consecrated |
  | "sight" | sight | vision | optical |
  | "heart-" | hearty | cordial | cardiac |
  | "buy" | buy | purchase | acquire |
  | "fast" | fast | firm | secure |
  | "fire" | fire | flame | conflagration |
  | "inner" | inner | interior | internal |
  | "book" | handbook | manual | compendium |
  | "freedom" | freedom | liberty | autonomy |
  | "people" | folk | people | populace |

  **The animal-meat split** (the most famous doublet pattern):
  - cow → beef (Anglo-Saxon peasant → Norman French table)
  - pig → pork
  - sheep → mutton
  - calf → veal
  - deer → venison
  - The peasant tended the animal (Germanic word); the lord ate the meat (French word)

- **Diagnostic power**: The Latinate-to-Germanic ratio in a text reliably predicts its formality level. Child-directed speech: overwhelmingly Germanic. Casual conversation: mostly Germanic. Academic writing: heavily Latinate. Legal prose: extremely Latinate

### Component 3: Polysemy and Semantic Extension

- **Purpose**: Enable a finite vocabulary to express infinite meanings through systematic meaning extension
- **Structure**:

  **Scale of polysemy in English**:
  - "Set" — 430+ senses in the OED (the most polysemous word)
  - "Run" — 645 senses in some counts (including phrasal verbs)
  - "Get" — 150+ senses
  - "Take" — 120+ senses
  - "Turn" — 100+ senses
  - Short, frequent, Germanic-origin words tend to be the most polysemous
  - Long, rare, Latinate words tend to be monosemous (one meaning) or oligosemous (few meanings)

  **Radial category structure** (how polysemy is organized):
  - Each polysemous word has a core (prototypical) meaning, usually the most concrete/spatial
  - Extended meanings radiate outward through metaphorical and metonymic mappings
  - Example — "head":
    - Core: body part (the head on a body)
    - Extension 1 (metonymy): person ("counting heads," "per head")
    - Extension 2 (metaphor): top/leading part ("head of the table," "head of the class")
    - Extension 3 (metaphor): leader ("head of department," "head of state")
    - Extension 4 (metaphor): foam on beer ("the head on a pint")
    - Extension 5 (metaphor): crucial point ("come to a head")
    - Extension 6 (metaphor): mental faculty ("use your head," "can't get it into my head")
    - All extensions connect to the prototype through some shared schematic structure (the head is the topmost, leading, most important part of the body)

  **Frequency-polysemy correlation**: The more frequently a word is used, the more senses it tends to acquire. High-frequency words serve as semantic workhorses, extending into new domains rather than being replaced by specialized terms

  **Homonymy vs. polysemy**:
  - Polysemy: one word, multiple RELATED meanings (head of body / head of department — connected through metaphor)
  - Homonymy: two words that happen to sound/look the same but have UNRELATED meanings (bank of a river / bank for money — historically different words)
  - The boundary between polysemy and homonymy is fuzzy and debated

### Component 4: Sense Relations

- **Purpose**: Organize vocabulary into structured networks of meaning relationships
- **Structure**:

  **Synonymy** (same or similar meaning):
  - True synonyms (interchangeable in all contexts) are extremely rare in English
  - Near-synonyms differ in register (begin/commence/initiate), connotation (slender/thin/skinny), collocation (strong tea/*powerful tea), or scope (broad/wide)
  - The three-layer system provides register-differentiated near-synonyms as a core feature

  **Antonymy** (opposite meaning):
  - Gradable antonyms: hot/cold, big/small, tall/short (allow degrees: "very hot," "somewhat cold")
  - Complementary antonyms: dead/alive, open/closed, true/false (binary: no middle ground)
  - Relational (converse) antonyms: buy/sell, teacher/student, parent/child, above/below (imply each other)
  - Directional antonyms: up/down, left/right, in/out, come/go (spatial opposites)

  **Hyponymy** (IS-A relationship / taxonomy):
  - Dog IS-A animal; rose IS-A flower; chair IS-A furniture
  - Creates hierarchical taxonomies: entity → living thing → animal → mammal → dog → poodle
  - Superordinate: the higher category (animal)
  - Hyponym: the lower category (dog)
  - Co-hyponyms: members at the same level (dog, cat, horse are co-hyponyms of animal)

  **Meronymy** (PART-OF relationship):
  - Finger PART-OF hand; wheel PART-OF car; chapter PART-OF book
  - Not transitive in the same way as hyponymy: finger is part of hand, hand is part of body, but "finger is part of body" feels less natural than "poodle is a dog, dog is an animal, poodle is an animal"

  **Semantic fields** (domains of related vocabulary):
  - Color: red, blue, green, yellow, purple, orange, pink, black, white, gray, brown...
  - Kinship: mother, father, sister, brother, aunt, uncle, cousin, niece, nephew, grandmother...
  - Cooking: boil, fry, bake, roast, grill, steam, simmer, sautee, poach, braise...
  - Emotion: happy, sad, angry, afraid, surprised, disgusted, contemptuous, jealous, ashamed...
  - Each field has internal structure (hyponymic hierarchies, antonymic pairs, graded scales)

### Component 5: Metaphorical Extension in English

- **Purpose**: Systematically generate abstract meanings from concrete, spatial, and bodily experience
- **Structure**:

  **Core principle** (Lakoff & Johnson): Abstract concepts are understood in terms of concrete experience. The mapping is systematically unidirectional — from more concrete (source domain) to more abstract (target domain).

  **Major conceptual metaphors in English**:

  | Metaphor | Source → Target | English Examples |
  |----------|----------------|-----------------|
  | UNDERSTANDING IS SEEING | Vision → Cognition | "I see what you mean," "a clear explanation," "shed light on," "in view of," "an illuminating idea" |
  | UNDERSTANDING IS GRASPING | Physical holding → Comprehension | "grasp the concept," "get a handle on," "hold that thought," "can't get a grip on this" |
  | IDEAS ARE OBJECTS | Physical objects → Thoughts | "turn the idea over," "pick up an idea," "throw out a suggestion," "a half-baked idea" |
  | ARGUMENT IS WAR | Combat → Debate | "attack a position," "defend a thesis," "shoot down an argument," "a weak point" |
  | TIME IS MONEY | Economic resource → Time | "spend time," "waste time," "save time," "invest time," "budget your time," "a costly delay" |
  | MORE IS UP | Vertical space → Quantity | "prices are rising," "high numbers," "her spirits soared," "he fell into depression" |
  | GOOD IS UP | Vertical space → Evaluation | "high quality," "top-notch," "feeling up," "things are looking up," "low-quality," "feeling down" |
  | LIFE IS A JOURNEY | Travel → Life | "a crossroads in life," "a dead end," "making progress," "going in circles," "moving forward" |
  | LOVE IS A JOURNEY | Travel → Romance | "we're going nowhere," "a bumpy road," "we've come a long way," "at a crossroads" |
  | ANGER IS HEAT | Temperature → Emotion | "boiling with rage," "a heated argument," "cool down," "hot-tempered," "simmer with resentment" |

  **Dead metaphors** (metaphors so conventional they are no longer perceived as metaphorical):
  - "The leg of a table" — FURNITURE HAS BODY PARTS (no one pictures an actual leg)
  - "The foot of a mountain" — LANDSCAPE IS A BODY
  - "The head of a department" — ORGANIZATIONS ARE BODIES
  - "A branch of government" — INSTITUTIONS ARE TREES
  - "Falling in love" — LOVE IS A PHYSICAL DESCENT/LOSS OF CONTROL
  - "Time flies" — TIME IS A MOVING OBJECT
  - "Grasping an idea" — UNDERSTANDING IS PHYSICAL HOLDING
  - These are not "dead" in the sense of inactive — they continue to structure thought and generate further extensions

  **Body-to-mind mapping** (a major systematic pattern):
  - Physical perception → Intellectual perception: "I see" (understand), "That sounds right" (seems correct), "I feel that..." (believe), "That stinks" (is bad), "A tasteful design" (aesthetically pleasing)
  - Physical force → Social force: "pressure someone," "push an agenda," "pull strings," "throw one's weight around"
  - Physical movement → Abstract progress: "move forward," "take steps," "make strides," "get ahead," "fall behind"

### Component 6: The Germanic-Latinate Doublet System

- **Purpose**: Provide register-differentiated pairs of near-synonyms that allow fine-grained control of formality and style
- **Structure**:

  **Systematic doublets** (Germanic / Latinate pair for the same concept):

  | Germanic | Latinate | Register difference |
  |----------|----------|-------------------|
  | begin | commence | neutral → formal |
  | end | terminate | neutral → technical |
  | ask | inquire | neutral → formal |
  | help | assist | neutral → professional |
  | buy | purchase | neutral → commercial |
  | hide | conceal | neutral → formal |
  | understand | comprehend | neutral → academic |
  | empty | vacant | everyday → official |
  | answer | respond | neutral → formal |
  | think | cogitate | neutral → pretentious |
  | eat | consume | neutral → technical/formal |
  | go up | ascend | everyday → literary |
  | give up | relinquish | casual → formal |
  | look into | investigate | casual → professional |
  | put up with | tolerate | informal → neutral |
  | spit out | expectorate | vulgar → medical |

  **Semantic nuances between doublet members**:
  - Not always perfect synonyms — the Latinate word often has a narrower, more precise, or more abstract meaning
  - "Think" (Germanic): general cognitive activity. "Cogitate" (Latin): deliberate, effortful reasoning
  - "Understand" (Germanic): general comprehension. "Comprehend" (Latin): complete, thorough understanding
  - "Eat" (Germanic): any ingestion. "Consume" (Latin): systematic or complete ingestion (also extends to non-food: "consumed by fire")
  - Choosing the wrong register level produces comedy (using "masticate" instead of "chew" in casual speech) or confusion (using "commence" in a children's story)

  **Legal doublets and triplets** (a historical artifact):
  - English law, developed under bilingual Norman-English conditions, paired Germanic and French terms to ensure comprehension by both language communities:
  - "Acknowledge and confess," "aid and abet," "breaking and entering," "cease and desist," "goods and chattels," "have and hold," "will and testament," "null and void," "part and parcel," "peace and quiet"
  - Some triplets: "right, title, and interest" / "give, devise, and bequeath" / "rest, residue, and remainder"

### Component 7: Collocations and Semantic Prosody

- **Purpose**: Govern which words habitually co-occur and the evaluative aura that frequent collocation creates
- **Structure**:

  **Collocations** (words that habitually co-occur beyond statistical chance):
  - **Adjective + Noun**: strong tea (*powerful tea), heavy rain (*strong rain), fast food (*quick food), broad daylight (*wide daylight), bitter cold (*sour cold)
  - **Verb + Noun**: make a decision (*do a decision), do homework (*make homework), take a shower (*make a shower), commit a crime (*do a crime, though shifting)
  - **Adverb + Adjective**: deeply concerned (*highly concerned), highly unlikely (*deeply unlikely), utterly ridiculous (*completely ridiculous — though "completely" is more flexible)
  - Collocational knowledge is one of the last things L2 learners master — it requires massive exposure rather than rule-learning

  **Why "strong tea" but not *"powerful tea"?**:
  - "Strong" and "powerful" are near-synonyms, but they have different collocational ranges
  - "Strong" collocates with: tea, coffee, wind, accent, feelings, smell, argument
  - "Powerful" collocates with: engine, computer, speech, weapon, nation, emotion
  - The distinction is partly semantic (strong = intensity of quality; powerful = capacity for action) and partly conventional (historical usage patterns)

  **Semantic prosody** (evaluative aura from habitual collocations):
  - **"Cause"** has negative semantic prosody: cause damage, cause problems, cause suffering, cause death, cause delays — approximately 90% of collocates are negative. Even "cause happiness" feels slightly marked because of this association
  - **"Commit"** has strongly negative semantic prosody: commit murder, commit fraud, commit suicide, commit a crime, commit atrocities — the verb is almost exclusively associated with negative actions
  - **"Provide"** has positive/neutral semantic prosody: provide support, provide assistance, provide evidence, provide information, provide resources
  - **"Utterly"** has negative semantic prosody: utterly ridiculous, utterly destroyed, utterly wrong, utterly failed — overwhelmingly collocates with negative adjectives
  - **"Set in"** has negative semantic prosody: decay set in, rot set in, panic set in, disillusion set in

  **Semantic preference** (the semantic category a word tends to collocate with):
  - "Naked" prefers body parts and abstracted truth concepts: naked eye, naked truth, naked ambition, naked aggression
  - "Rampant" prefers negative unchecked growth: rampant corruption, rampant inflation, rampant disease, rampant crime

### Component 8: Constructional Meaning

- **Purpose**: Show that syntactic constructions carry meaning independently of the words that fill them
- **Structure** (following Goldberg's Construction Grammar):

  **The ditransitive construction**: [Subject V Object1 Object2]
  - Meaning: "X CAUSES Y to RECEIVE Z"
  - "She gave him the book" — transfer of possession
  - "She baked him a cake" — intended transfer (she didn't literally transfer baking)
  - "She denied him the promotion" — NEGATIVE transfer (the construction imposes transfer semantics even on verbs that don't inherently mean "give")
  - The construction itself means "transfer" — the verb only specifies the manner

  **Ditransitive alternation**:
  - "She gave him the book" (double object — implies successful transfer)
  - "She gave the book to him" (prepositional — does not necessarily imply success)
  - Difference: "She taught him French" (implies he learned) vs. "She taught French to him" (no guarantee he learned)

  **The caused-motion construction**: [Subject V Object PP-direction]
  - Meaning: "X CAUSES Y to MOVE to Z"
  - "She pushed him into the pool" — physical caused motion
  - "She sneezed the napkin off the table" — "sneeze" is not normally transitive, but the construction forces a caused-motion reading
  - "He laughed her out of the room" — again, "laugh" is intransitive, but the construction creates caused-motion meaning

  **The resultative construction**: [Subject V Object AdjP]
  - Meaning: "X causes Y to BECOME Z"
  - "She hammered the metal flat"
  - "She painted the wall red"
  - "She ran her shoes ragged"
  - "She drank herself silly"
  - The adjective describes the END STATE caused by the action

  **The way-construction**: [Subject V possessive way PP]
  - Meaning: "X moves along a path by means of V-ing"
  - "She elbowed her way through the crowd"
  - "He talked his way into the job"
  - "She fought her way to the top"
  - Creates a motion reading from virtually any verb

- **Key insight**: Constructions are not just empty templates filled by words — they CONTRIBUTE meaning. You can understand "She sneezed the napkin off the table" even though "sneeze" is normally intransitive, because the caused-motion construction imposes its own meaning on the verb. This is a core claim of Construction Grammar (Goldberg, 1995)

### Component 9: Idioms and Fixed Expressions

- **Purpose**: Store conventionalized multi-word expressions with meanings that range from partly to wholly non-compositional
- **Structure**:

  **Compositionality spectrum**:
  - Fully compositional: "red car" (meaning = sum of parts)
  - Semi-compositional: "spill the beans" (partially interpretable — spilling = revealing, beans = secrets)
  - Non-compositional: "kick the bucket" (= die — no connection between kicking, buckets, and death)

  **Idiom categories**:
  - **Body-part idioms**: "break a leg" (good luck), "get cold feet" (become nervous), "turn a blind eye" (ignore), "bite the bullet" (endure), "give the cold shoulder" (snub), "keep your chin up" (stay positive)
  - **Animal idioms**: "let the cat out of the bag" (reveal a secret), "wild goose chase" (futile pursuit), "the elephant in the room" (obvious unaddressed issue), "raining cats and dogs" (heavy rain)
  - **Color idioms**: "seeing red" (angry), "green with envy," "feeling blue" (sad), "a white lie" (harmless lie), "black sheep" (outcast), "caught red-handed" (in the act)
  - **Dead metaphor idioms**: "the tip of the iceberg" (small visible part of larger problem), "on the same page" (in agreement), "a stumbling block" (obstacle)

  **Frozen syntax in idioms**: Many idioms preserve archaic syntactic patterns:
  - "By and large" (original nautical terms)
  - "To and fro" (archaic "fro" = from)
  - "Willy-nilly" (will I, nill I = whether I want to or not)
  - "Helter-skelter" (reduplicative with no independent meaning)

  **Idiom processing**: Despite being stored as units, idioms are not fully "frozen" — speakers can often modify them productively: "She didn't just spill the beans — she upended the entire pot" (creative extension preserving the metaphorical frame)

### Component 10: Semantic Fields and Domain-Specific Vocabulary

- **Purpose**: Organize vocabulary into structured clusters covering specific experiential domains
- **Structure**:

  **Color terminology** (a well-studied semantic field):
  - English has 11 basic color terms: black, white, red, green, yellow, blue, brown, orange, pink, purple, gray
  - This matches the endpoint of Berlin & Kay's (1969) universal color term hierarchy
  - Non-basic: turquoise, magenta, crimson, scarlet, teal, indigo, beige, maroon, navy, olive, coral, lavender...
  - Modifier patterns: "light blue," "dark red," "bright green," "pale yellow" — systematic modification

  **Kinship terminology**:
  - English has a relatively simple kinship system (compared to, say, Chinese, which distinguishes paternal/maternal aunts and uncles)
  - Core terms: mother, father, sister, brother, son, daughter, aunt, uncle, cousin, niece, nephew, grandmother, grandfather
  - Notable gaps: no single word for "parent's sibling" (need "aunt" or "uncle"), no distinction between "mother's brother" and "father's brother" (both "uncle")

  **Emotion vocabulary**:
  - Core emotions (universal): happy, sad, angry, afraid, disgusted, surprised
  - English-specific elaborations: anxious, embarrassed, ashamed, guilty, jealous, envious, nostalgic, wistful, melancholy, bittersweet, schadenfreude (borrowed), ennui (borrowed)
  - Emotion metaphors: "boiling with anger," "sinking feeling," "soaring spirits," "crushed by grief," "frozen with fear"

  **Cooking and food vocabulary** (illustrating the Germanic-French split):
  - Cooking methods: bake (Germanic), boil (French), fry (French), roast (French), grill (French), steam (Germanic), stew (French), sautee (French), braise (French)
  - The kitchen is one of the domains most heavily Frenchified — reflecting Norman influence on courtly dining

## How It Works

### Step 1: Lexical Access
The speaker retrieves candidate words from the mental lexicon based on the intended meaning. Multiple alternatives may be activated simultaneously from different etymological layers.
- Trigger: Intended meaning (concept to express)
- Action: Mental lexicon searched; all words matching the target concept activated
- Result: Candidate set (e.g., for "begin": begin, start, commence, initiate)

### Step 2: Register/Context Filtering
The speaker selects from the candidate set based on the register, social context, and audience. The etymological layer (Germanic/French/Latin) provides the primary register signal.
- Trigger: Social context, formality level, domain
- Action: Inappropriate register candidates suppressed; appropriate layer selected
- Result: Single word chosen (e.g., "begin" for casual speech, "commence" for formal ceremony)

### Step 3: Collocational Checking
The selected word is checked against collocational patterns stored in memory. If the collocation is marked or unusual, an alternative may be selected.
- Trigger: Word + its syntactic neighbors
- Action: Collocational fit evaluated (strong tea = good; *powerful tea = bad)
- Result: Word confirmed or replaced with a better collocational match

### Step 4: Constructional Integration
The word is inserted into the syntactic construction, which may impose additional meaning. The construction and the word interact to produce the final interpretation.
- Trigger: Syntactic context (ditransitive, resultative, caused-motion, etc.)
- Action: Construction imposes its schematic meaning; verb provides manner specification
- Result: Composite meaning = constructional meaning + lexical meaning

### Step 5: Metaphorical Extension (If Needed)
If no existing word covers the intended meaning, the speaker may extend an existing word through metaphorical mapping — using a concrete-domain word in an abstract domain.
- Trigger: Semantic gap — no literal word available
- Action: Concrete-domain word mapped to abstract domain (physical → cognitive, spatial → temporal)
- Result: Novel or conventionalized metaphorical expression (e.g., "grasp the idea")

## Terminology Glossary

| Term | Definition | Also Known As |
|------|------------|---------------|
| Polysemy | One word having multiple related meanings | Multiple senses |
| Homonymy | Two unrelated words with the same form | Accidental similarity |
| Synonymy | Words with the same or very similar meaning | Sameness of meaning |
| Antonymy | Words with opposite meanings | Oppositeness |
| Hyponymy | IS-A relationship: "dog" is a hyponym of "animal" | Taxonomic relation |
| Meronymy | PART-OF relationship: "finger" is a meronym of "hand" | Part-whole relation |
| Semantic field | A cluster of vocabulary items sharing a common domain | Lexical field |
| Semantic prosody | The evaluative aura a word acquires from habitual collocation | Attitudinal meaning |
| Collocation | A habitual word combination (strong tea, heavy rain) | Co-occurrence pattern |
| Dead metaphor | A metaphor so conventional it is no longer perceived as figurative | Conventional metaphor |
| Conceptual metaphor | A systematic mapping from a concrete source domain to an abstract target domain | Cognitive metaphor |
| Register | Level of formality in language use | Style level |
| Construction | A form-meaning pairing at the syntactic level | Grammatical construction |
| Constructional meaning | Meaning contributed by a syntactic pattern itself, not just the words in it | Schematic meaning |
| Radial category | A polysemous word's senses organized around a core with extensions | Prototype structure |
| Doublet | Two words from the same historical root that entered English by different paths | Etymological twin |
| Triplet | Three words for the same concept from Germanic, French, and Latin sources | Register triplet |

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: Register Selection in Real Time
- **Scenario**: Three versions of the same message at different formality levels
- **Illustration**:
  - Casual (Germanic-dominant): "We need to start right now, or we'll run out of time and have to give up"
  - Formal (French-dominant): "We should commence immediately, or we risk exhausting our schedule and abandoning the effort"
  - Academic (Latinate-dominant): "It is imperative to initiate proceedings without delay, lest temporal resources be depleted and the enterprise necessitate termination"
  - All three express the same proposition. The vocabulary layer selected determines the register perceived by the audience. A skilled English speaker shifts between layers fluidly depending on context
- **Key Insight**: Register is not about "big words" vs. "small words" — it is about systematic selection from the correct etymological layer for the social context

### Example 2: Semantic Prosody of "Cause"
- **Scenario**: A student writes "The new policy caused happiness among employees"
- **Illustration**: While grammatically correct, this sentence feels slightly "off" because "cause" has acquired negative semantic prosody through overwhelming collocation with negative outcomes (cause damage, cause problems, cause suffering, cause death). A more natural phrasing would be "The new policy brought happiness" or "created happiness" or "led to happiness." The negative aura of "cause" is not a rule but a statistical tendency built up from millions of encounters — and native speakers "feel" it even without being able to articulate why
- **Key Insight**: Semantic prosody is invisible grammar — it governs word choice through associative patterns rather than explicit rules

### Example 3: Construction Grammar — "She Sneezed the Napkin Off the Table"
- **Scenario**: A sentence where the verb "sneeze" is used transitively, which its lexical entry does not permit
- **Illustration**: "Sneeze" is intransitive — you sneeze, you don't sneeze SOMETHING. But in the caused-motion construction [Subject V Object PP-direction], the construction imposes its own meaning ("X causes Y to move to Z"), and the verb provides the manner. So "She sneezed the napkin off the table" means "She caused the napkin to move off the table by sneezing." The construction, not the verb, contributes the transitive/causative meaning. This shows that syntactic patterns carry meaning independently of lexical items
- **Key Insight**: Grammar is not just a frame for words — it MEANS things. Constructions are form-meaning pairs at the phrase/sentence level

### Example 4: The Polysemy of "Run"
- **Scenario**: The word "run" appears in dozens of apparently unrelated contexts
- **Illustration**: Core: "She runs every morning" (physical locomotion). Extended: "The engine is running" (operating — MACHINES ARE BODIES). "The river runs south" (flowing — WATER MOVES LIKE A RUNNER). "She runs the company" (manages — MANAGING IS DIRECTING MOVEMENT). "The color ran in the wash" (spreading — DYE MOVES LIKE LIQUID). "Her nose is running" (discharging — FLUID MOVES). "She ran up a debt" (accumulated — ABSTRACT INCREASE). "The play ran for six months" (continued — DURATION IS DISTANCE). All connected to the core through metaphorical extension from physical running to other types of movement and continuation
- **Key Insight**: Polysemy is not random — it follows the same source-to-target mapping patterns that conceptual metaphor theory predicts

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: English has "too many words" and most are redundant
  - **Reality**: English vocabulary is structurally organized by register layers. The "redundancy" is functional — it provides systematic formality calibration. Germanic, French, and Latinate alternatives for the same concept serve different social purposes

- **Misconception**: Metaphors are literary devices used for decoration
  - **Reality**: Metaphor is the primary mechanism by which English (and all languages) extend meaning from concrete to abstract domains. Most metaphor in English is conventional and unconscious — "I see what you mean" is a metaphor (UNDERSTANDING IS SEEING) that speakers use without awareness

- **Misconception**: Collocations are random and must be individually memorized
  - **Reality**: While collocations lack the productivity of grammatical rules, they follow partial patterns. "Strong" collocates with intensity of quality (strong tea, strong feelings, strong wind), while "powerful" collocates with capacity for action (powerful engine, powerful speech). Understanding the semantic basis reduces the memorization burden

- **Misconception**: Word meaning is fixed and determined by the dictionary
  - **Reality**: Word meaning is flexible, context-dependent, and continuously evolving through metaphorical extension, narrowing, broadening, and amelioration/pejoration. Dictionaries record usage; they do not define boundaries

- **Misconception**: Idioms are completely opaque and unpredictable
  - **Reality**: Many idioms are partially compositional (you can detect the metaphorical basis: "spill the beans" = accidentally reveal, "kick the bucket" is genuinely opaque). And idiom comprehension is aided by the conceptual metaphor framework — if you know the metaphor, you can often guess the idiom

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **False friends across registers**: "Terrific" historically meant "terrifying" (from Latin terrificus) but underwent amelioration to mean "wonderful." "Awful" meant "awe-inspiring" but pejerated to "terrible." Register shifts can override etymological meaning
- **Semantic bleaching**: Some words have lost their original strong meaning through overuse: "very" originally meant "truly" (from French verrai); "really" meant "in reality"; "literally" is undergoing this process now ("I literally died" = I figuratively had a strong reaction)
- **Constructional coercion resistance**: Not all verbs accept all constructions — "She sneezed the napkin off the table" works, but "*She slept the napkin off the table" does not, because "sneeze" involves force that can be directed, while "sleep" does not
- **Collocation variation by dialect**: British and American English sometimes differ in collocations: "do an exam" (BrE) vs. "take an exam" (AmE); "have a bath" (BrE) vs. "take a bath" (AmE)
- **Polysemy vs. homonymy boundary disputes**: Is "bank" (river) related to "bank" (financial)? Most linguists say no (homonymy), but some argue for a distant connection through "edge/margin." The boundary is fuzzy

## Related Concepts

- **English Morphology**: The three-layer lexicon is created by derivational morphology from three etymological sources; the stratal affix system governs which affixes attach to which roots
- **English Syntax**: Constructional meaning (ditransitive, resultative, caused-motion) operates at the syntax-semantics interface
- **English Phonology**: Latinate words have different stress patterns than Germanic words; stress itself signals etymology and register
- **The English Verb System**: Phrasal verbs are a semantic system where particles carry metaphorical meaning extensions
- **English Pragmatics**: Register selection is a pragmatic act; semantic prosody contributes to pragmatic meaning
- **Conceptual Metaphor Theory** (Lakoff & Johnson): The theoretical framework underlying the metaphorical extension patterns in English
- **Construction Grammar** (Goldberg): The theoretical framework for understanding how syntactic patterns carry meaning

## Research Gaps

- **Computational polysemy networks**: While radial category structure is well-described qualitatively, computational models that predict which extensions a polysemous word will develop are still in early stages
- **Register perception thresholds**: At what Latinate-to-Germanic ratio does a text "feel" formal? The gradient is real but the perceptual boundaries have not been precisely measured experimentally
- **Semantic prosody awareness**: Whether native speakers are explicitly aware of semantic prosody (or only sensitive to it implicitly) has limited experimental evidence
- **Constructional productivity**: Which argument-structure constructions are fully productive (accept any verb) vs. limited to specific verb classes is still being mapped
- **Cross-dialectal semantic variation**: Systematic differences in polysemy patterns, collocations, and semantic prosody between British, American, Australian, Indian, and other varieties of English are under-studied compared to phonological and syntactic variation
