# Anxiety, Dissociation & the Episode Pattern

> Of everything duloxetine was prescribed to do for you, exactly one thing has been tested at 20mg and passed. It is not the pain. It is the anxiety.

That sentence is the least hedged thing in this entire deep dive, so it is worth being precise about what it does and does not buy you.

The trial is Nicolini 2009 `[primary, verified — PMID 18485261]`. It enrolled 581 adults with generalised anxiety disorder across four arms: placebo (n=170), **duloxetine 20mg as a genuinely fixed dose** (n=84), duloxetine 60–120mg flexible (n=158), and venlafaxine XR (n=169). Ten weeks, multi-centre, double-blind. The 20mg arm was not a titration step and it was not a waypoint on the way somewhere else. It existed as a fixed dose for the whole trial, because European regulators asked for it. On the HAM-A total score, that arm separated from placebo at p=0.01–0.001. On the HAM-A **psychic** factor, it separated again, at the same significance. On the HAM-A **somatic** factor, it did not (p=0.07) `[primary, verified — the abstract is explicit and unambiguous]`.

This is the only place in duloxetine's entire record where your exact dose has direct, positive, randomised evidence for anything. Low-dose duloxetine has been tested for pain three times, in roughly 350 patients, across two indications, and failed on pain every time, including twice with adequate power `[primary, verified]`. There is no other fixed 20mg arm anywhere in the anxiety literature, and none in depression either. One arm, 84 people, one trial, ten weeks, never replicated.

It is also worth resisting the story this invites. The temptation is to read "20mg works for anxiety" as "20mg is the sweet spot, and more would spoil it." Nicolini says the opposite. The higher-dose arm did slightly **better**, not worse: HAM-A fell 15.3 points versus 14.7, response was 65% versus 60%, remission 44% versus 42% `[secondary — unverified; these point estimates come from a review, not the primary paper, which is paywalled]`. Every one of those comparisons points the same direction, and it is not the direction that flatters your current dose. So the honest reading is not "20mg is best." It is "20mg cleared the bar, and 60mg cleared it by a little more."

Now the caveat that decides how much weight any of this can carry, and it is the reason a whole page is needed rather than a sentence. **Non-inferiority of 20mg to 60mg was never tested.** Not badly tested, not underpowered, not inconclusive. Never asked.

The proof is arithmetic, and it is exact. Duloxetine ran two nearly identical GAD registration trials: Hartford 2007 `[primary, verified — PMID 17414743]`, with 161 on placebo, 162 on duloxetine 60–120mg, and 164 on venlafaxine, and Nicolini 2009, with 170, 158, and 169 in the same three arms plus the 84 on 20mg. Allgulander 2008 `[primary, verified — PMID 18635722]` then pooled the two by prespecified plan to run the formal non-inferiority comparison, and reported 331 on placebo, 320 on duloxetine, 333 on venlafaxine. Add Hartford's arms to Nicolini's and you get 331, 320, and 333. Every arm reconciles to the digit. The 84 people on 20mg appear in none of the totals. The non-inferiority analysis pooled the 60–120mg arms and **excluded the 20mg arm entirely** `[primary, verified — the reconciliation is exact and the design is stated in the abstract]`.

The consequence is a distinction you should carry into the appointment, because it is the kind of thing that gets flattened in a ten-minute conversation. **"Beat placebo" and "as good as 60mg" are different claims, and only the first was ever asked.** With n=84 against n=158, and no comparison performed, "20mg is roughly as good as 60mg for anxiety" is not a finding that was made. It is a gap that has been mistaken for one.

Two more things belong in the narrative rather than the footnotes, because they cut against the reading you might prefer. The first is that the placebo hypothesis does not collapse here. Nicolini's placebo arm dropped **11.6 HAM-A points**, with a **42% response rate** and 20% remission `[secondary — unverified]`. The drug adds roughly three points and eighteen percentage points on top of a large placebo effect. That is the ordinary shape of GAD trial data, and an earlier pass of this project claimed that the 20mg evidence made the placebo explanation for your improvement collapse. It does not. What collapses is only the narrower claim that a 20mg anxiolytic benefit is pharmacologically unsupported. Those are different things, and running them together is how you end up more confident than the evidence permits.

The second is that the meta-analysis, which is the best evidence duloxetine has for anxiety, cannot help you at your dose. Li 2018 `[primary, verified — PMID 29558528]` pooled 8 RCTs and 2,399 participants and found response OR 2.22 (95% CI 1.88–2.62) and remission OR 1.99 (1.66–2.39). It is independently funded by the National Natural Science Foundation of China, with no competing interests declared, which makes it a rarity in a literature where Eli Lilly sponsored almost everything. But it ran **no dose-stratified analysis** `[primary, verified]`. So it establishes that duloxetine treats GAD. It does not establish that 20mg does. Nicolini remains the only thing in the world that speaks to your dose, and it is one Lilly trial in which six of the nine authors were Lilly employees or shareholders `[primary, verified]`.

## The guardrail, stated before you read another word about factor scores

Li 2018 found something else, and it is robust: duloxetine improves the HAM-A **psychic** anxiety factor and produces **no significant difference** on the HAM-A **somatic** factor, across all 8 trials, all doses, 2,399 patients `[primary, verified]`. Nicolini's 20mg arm shows the identical split. This is not a 20mg weakness. It is a dose-independent property of the drug, replicated across the whole literature.

**It is not about you, and it must not be handed to you as if it were.**

The HAM-A "somatic factor" is a list of physical symptom items: muscular tension, sensory symptoms, cardiovascular symptoms, respiratory symptoms, gastrointestinal symptoms, genitourinary symptoms, autonomic symptoms. Palpitations. Sweating. Churning stomach. The HAM-A "psychic factor" is anxious mood, tension, fears, insomnia, cognitive symptoms, depressed mood, and observed behaviour at interview.

Your phenomenology is **present anxiety with absent negative thoughts**. That is not "somatic anxiety." It is not the somatic axis at all. The HAM-A split is about *where the symptom shows up in the body*. Your description is about *whether the state has propositional content*. Those are two different axes, and a drug that moves one of them tells you nothing about the other. The words rhyme; the constructs do not.

This matters more than a definitional quibble, because this project already made exactly this mistake once. An earlier pass found "non-fearful panic disorder" in the literature, noticed that it sounded like your description, and handed it to you as a name for what you have. It was wrong, and it had to be retracted. Grabbing a superficially resonant label from an unrelated measurement literature and giving it to a real person as a description of themselves is a specific failure mode, and mapping you onto the HAM-A somatic factor would be the same failure with a different label. **Nobody has measured whether duloxetine's psychic/somatic split has anything to do with your episodes, and the honest answer is that there is no reason to think it does** `[no evidence exists]`.

## What your episodes actually are, and why twenty years of therapy missed them

Here is the fact that carries more diagnostic weight than anything else in this document: **your episodes have no negative cognitive content.** No negative thoughts. The anxiety is present and severe; the thinking that is supposed to accompany it is absent.

Two consequences follow from that, and both of them are things that already happened to you.

The first is that multiple therapists diagnosed PTSD or cPTSD and never found an index trauma. That is usually read as a failure of the search, and the conclusion drawn is that the trauma is there and buried. Consider the other reading. A trauma diagnosis predicts a memory with content: a proposition, an image, a narrative that the state is *about*. The search kept coming up empty because the state may not have the shape a trauma memory has. It is not that the content is hidden. It may be that there is no content. Those two situations look identical from the outside and they are completely different problems, and only one of them has a trauma in it `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

The second is that cognitive therapies never touched it, and the reason is mechanical rather than a matter of fit or effort or therapeutic alliance. **CBT's active ingredient is restructuring a thought.** You identify the automatic thought, you test it against evidence, you replace it with a more accurate one, and the feeling follows the thought down. Every step in that chain requires a thought to be there. If the state arrives without cognitive content, then the intervention's working end has nothing to make contact with. It is not that cognitive restructuring was applied poorly. It is that there was no proposition for it to restructure `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

That argument is worth keeping, and it is worth being clear about what it is not resting on. An earlier pass supported it by identifying your presentation as non-fearful panic disorder. **That diagnostic claim is dead**, and it died for three independent reasons, any one of which is sufficient. Panic attacks peak within minutes, and DSM-5 defines them that way; your episodes last one to two weeks, which is a thousand-fold difference in timescale and a different class of event entirely. NFPD is the absence of **fear** with somatic panic symptoms present; you report the presence of severe anxiety with the absence of **cognitions**, and "no fear" and "no negative thoughts" are not the same absence, because you have the affect and NFPD patients do not. And NFPD is defined and measured in cardiology and emergency-department chest-pain presenters, where the correct figure is 32–41% of panic disorder patients seeking treatment for chest pain `[primary, verified — Fleet 2000, PMID 10906353]`, a population with no established transfer to you.

**Drop the label. Keep the argument.** The CBT-mismatch insight never needed NFPD to work, because it follows directly from your own report rather than from anyone else's diagnostic category. If there is no negative cognition, cognitive restructuring has nothing to restructure. That conclusion stands on its own, and it is probably the most immediately useful paragraph in this deep dive, because it retires an explanation you have likely been carrying for years, which is that the therapies failed because you failed at them.

## The decoupling, and the tension underneath it

The central observation on duloxetine is not that your episodes stopped. It is stranger and more informative than that.

You report the acute sensory overwhelm as **worse** on the drug, and the one-to-two-week crash that normally follows as **absent**. The verified instance is five-plus hours at two crowded festivals, producing two panic attacks with heart rate above 140 bpm at rest. Historically that is a guaranteed crash trigger. There was no crash, and the absence was independently anticipated by your family, who braced for it and watched it not arrive.

Two effects moving in opposite directions, same drug, same person, same day. That shape is doing real work, because it rules things out. It rules out sedation, blunting, and global muting, all of which would move both effects the same way. It also rules out the simplest expectancy account, because expectancy predicts the acute overwhelm would feel *better*, and it was worse. And a family member's independent expectation of a crash that did not come is not a placebo response in you.

Now the part that is genuinely uncomfortable, and it belongs here rather than in a caveat box. **Four verified lines of evidence say that acute serotonin makes your exact problem worse.**

Escitalopram at 15mg significantly decreased P50 sensory gating and habituation in healthy volunteers, double-blind and placebo-controlled `[primary, verified — PMID 20971512]`. Escitalopram at 10mg significantly delayed habituation of the startle response in the same lab's earlier study `[primary, verified — PMID 17299503]`. Habituation is the exact function whose failure produces sensory overwhelm, and the drug in both studies is the one that made you dissociate and go non-functional. Separately, the serotonin agonist m-CPP induced depersonalisation significantly more than placebo in a 67-subject challenge study, with 18% experiencing depersonalisation or derealisation `[primary, verified — PMID 8570768]`. And in Maier and Watkins' work on stressor controllability, which is the most influential animal model of stress-induced collapse there is, the mechanism runs through serotonin as the **effector**: uncontrollable stress sensitises the dorsal raphe, the sensitised raphe releases exaggerated serotonin in response to subsequent input, and that exaggerated release is what produces the behavioural collapse `[primary, verified — PMID 15893820]`. The reader's intuitive question was whether serotonin sets a stress-resilience threshold. There is a real, well-developed story about serotonin and stress, and it runs the other way: serotonin is closer to the thing that causes the collapse than the thing that prevents it.

**Duloxetine is also serotonergic, and at 20mg it is predominantly serotonergic.** Its serotonin transporter affinity is nine times its noradrenaline transporter affinity `[primary, verified — PMID 11750180]`, and your dose sits high on the serotonergic occupancy curve and low on the noradrenergic one. So the four lines are not about a different drug class. They are about the arm of duloxetine that is doing most of the work at your dose.

There is exactly one account that reconciles your experience with that evidence, and it is reasoning rather than a finding. **If acute serotonin elevation is anxiogenic, dissociogenic and gating-disrupting, then the therapeutic effect cannot be the serotonin. It would have to be the adaptation to the serotonin: a receptor-level change that runs opposite to the drug's acute pharmacology** `[reasoning, not evidence]`. That account has genuine virtues. It explains your escitalopram failure and your duloxetine response with one mechanism rather than two. It explains why every antidepressant takes weeks rather than hours, which is otherwise a standing embarrassment. And it makes a falsifiable prediction, which is that raising the dose above the adapted set-point should transiently re-create the acute state.

It also has a problem that must travel with it, and I am flagging it against the account's own interest: **you are at two weeks, and two weeks is early for a chronic adaptation.** The account is in tension with your own timeline. Beyond that, the entire family of "receptors adapt over weeks and that adaptation is the therapeutic mechanism" explanations is under sustained attack, because the best-known version of it, built on 5-HT1A autoreceptors, has been failing its tests for two decades `[secondary — unverified]`. Holding the version built on 5-HT2C loosely is the appropriate response.

And underneath all of it sits the flattest fact available: **nobody has studied this decoupling in any human, for any drug** `[no evidence exists]`. Not weakly studied. Not studied in the wrong population. The human evidence explains the acute half. The animal evidence explains the aftermath half. Nothing has both. Your observation is, as far as this project could determine, unreported. That does not make it wrong. It makes it data.

## Does it keep working, and how would you know

You asked about durability directly, so here is the number everyone quotes and what it actually is.

The famous figure is that roughly **25%** of people on an antidepressant experience "poop-out." It comes from Solomon 2005 `[primary, verified — PMID 15766292]`, and the provenance is worth seeing in full. It is **observational**, not randomised. The cohort is the NIMH Collaborative Depression Study, **enrolled between 1978 and 1981**. The drugs are tricyclics and MAOIs, because that is what people took in 1980. And the operational definition of tachyphylaxis is *recurrence of depression while on maintenance pharmacotherapy*, which in an uncontrolled observational cohort **cannot distinguish tachyphylaxis from the natural course of a recurring illness, from non-adherence, or from a drug that never worked in the first place** `[reasoning, not evidence, though the reasoning follows directly from the design]`. Solomon 2005 does not measure how often antidepressants stop working. It measures how often depression comes back in people who are taking one.

The other figure in circulation is a range of **9–57%** `[primary, verified]`. A quantity whose credible range spans six-fold is not an estimate. It is a confession that the field has no measurement instrument.

The most informative single result points somewhere unexpected. Zimmerman and Thongy `[primary, verified — PMID 17854252]` found relapse during continuation in **24.1% of placebo responders** versus **7.4% of drug responders**. Read that carefully, because it inverts the usual story. If people who responded to a dummy pill lose their response three times as often as people who responded to the drug, then most of what gets called "poop-out" is **placebo-response decay**, and the phenomenon is real as an experience and largely illusory as a pharmacology. Note also that 24.1% sits remarkably close to Solomon's famous 25%.

The best number for you, and the one that survives the argument that antidepressant relapse trials are contaminated by withdrawal effects in the placebo arm, is this: **people who stayed on duloxetine relapsed at 13.7% by 26 weeks in GAD** `[primary, verified — PMID 18559291]` **and 14.4% by 52 weeks in depression** `[primary, verified — PMID 19552867]`. The withdrawal critique attacks the placebo arms of those trials, because those people were taken off a drug. It does not touch the duloxetine arms, because those people were not taken off anything.

That ~14% is an **upper bound on tachyphylaxis, not a measure of it.** It bundles genuine loss of drug effect together with natural illness course breaking through a partially effective drug and with people who stopped taking their tablets. Tachyphylaxis is at most 14% and probably well under it, because that figure is everyone who relapsed for any reason at all.

Two absences complete the picture and neither should be softened. **No placebo-controlled duloxetine data exists past 12 months, on anyone, for any indication** `[no evidence exists]`. And **duloxetine-specific tachyphylaxis has never been studied** `[no evidence exists]`. Not "studied and found reassuring." Never measured.

## The denominator is exposures, not weeks

The last piece is the one that turns your own knowledge of yourself into a study design, and it follows from something you already know.

**Your episodes are not spontaneous. They follow sensory overwhelm.** That single fact changes what counts as evidence, because it means the thing being tested is not the passage of time. It is the trigger.

Start with the time-based version, so you can see why it is weak. Treating episodes as a random process at your reported baseline of two to four per month, the probability of seeing zero episodes purely by chance is **13–37% at two weeks** `[reasoning, not evidence — standard Poisson arithmetic, and it can be checked]`. That is where you are now. Between one in three and one in seven people whose drug does absolutely nothing would be sitting exactly where you are sitting, seeing exactly what you are seeing, and drawing exactly your conclusion. This is not pessimism. It is the reason not to treat two weeks as informative. The odds fall below 5% at about six weeks and below one in four hundred by three months, so the chance explanation dies somewhere between six weeks and three months.

But notice precisely what that kills. **The passage of time kills random fluctuation. It does not touch placebo, regression to the mean, or natural course**, because those are not chance. They are systematic effects that produce the identical zero-episode observation. A three-month clean run proves the improvement is real. It says nothing about whether the drug caused it. Those are different questions and waiting only answers the first.

The trigger-conditional version answers the second. If p₀ is your baseline probability of an episode given a significant sensory overload, then surviving **k** comparable exposures with no episode has probability (1 − p₀)ᵏ under the null. If the trigger was near-deterministic for you, two clean exposures is already p<0.05. At p₀ = 0.8 it takes three. At p₀ = 0.5 it takes five. **Three to five comparable overloads survived cleanly is roughly a p<0.05 result** `[reasoning, not evidence]`, and it is a result about the thing that actually matters.

The reason this test is better rather than merely faster is that **regression to the mean cannot fake it.** Regression explains why a bad stretch is followed by a better stretch. It does not explain why a specific, repeated, previously reliable trigger stopped producing its effect. Two quiet months spent working from home and avoiding crowds is worth almost nothing, because the drug was never tested. One festival day with no crash afterwards is worth more than two uneventful months.

**You have one.** The festivals were a real exposure and it was survived clean. One is not three to five, and one data point does not settle anything. But it is the highest-value observation you have produced, it is worth strictly more than the two weeks of calendar time it sits inside, and it argues for recording exposures prospectively from now on rather than trying to reconstruct them later. The limits are worth stating at the claim: it requires you to characterise p₀ from memory, which is recall-biased; it requires exposures to be genuinely comparable, which is a judgement call; and deliberately seeking out overload to test a drug carries a real cost and a real risk, so nothing here is suggesting you go looking. The point is that when overloads happen anyway, those are the informative days.

So what you have, assembled, is one real trial at your exact dose that asked the smaller of the two available questions and never asked the larger one; a robust and replicated property of the drug's anxiolytic effect that is not about you and must not be handed to you as a description of yourself; a phenomenology with no cognitive content that plausibly explains two decades of therapeutic near-misses and that nobody has ever studied pharmacologically; a benefit that, if it is real and serotonergic, has to be running against the acute pharmacology of the drug producing it, on a timeline that does not comfortably fit; a durability estimate that is an upper bound on a phenomenon that may not exist as pharmacology at all; and one data point out of the three to five that would make the case. None of that tells you what to do about the dose, and it is not supposed to. What it does is let you walk into the appointment able to say precisely which of your questions the evidence has answered, which it has only appeared to answer, and which nobody has ever asked.

## Deep Dive

### The one trial at your dose

**Nicolini H, Bakish D, Duenas H, Spann M, Erickson J, Hallberg C, Ball S, Sagman D, Russell JM.** "Improvement of psychic and somatic symptoms in adult patients with generalized anxiety disorder: examination from a duloxetine, venlafaxine extended-release and placebo-controlled trial." *Psychological Medicine.* 2009 Feb;39(2):267–276. **PMID 18485261.** `[primary, verified — the PMID resolves to the paper claimed; the full text is paywalled and was not retrieved]`

| Arm | n | Nature |
|---|---|---|
| Placebo | 170 | — |
| **Duloxetine 20 mg** | **84** | **Genuinely fixed dose, whole trial, included to satisfy European regulatory requirements** |
| Duloxetine 60–120 mg | 158 | Flexible |
| Venlafaxine XR 75–225 mg | 169 | Active comparator |
| **Total** | **581** | 10 weeks, multi-centre, double-blind |

Adult outpatients with DSM-IV GAD, mean age 42.8, 57.1% women.

**The results, verbatim from the primary abstract** `[primary, verified]`:

> *"Each of the three active treatment groups had **significantly greater improvements on HAMA total score** from baseline to endpoint compared with placebo (**p=0.01–0.001**)."*

> *"For the **HAMA psychic factor score**, **both duloxetine treatment arms** and venlafaxine XR demonstrated **significantly greater improvement compared with placebo** (p=0.01–0.001)."*

> *"For the **HAMA somatic factor score**, the mean improvement in the duloxetine 60–120 mg and venlafaxine XR groups was significantly greater than placebo (p⩽0.05 and p⩽0.01 respectively), whose mean improvement **did not differ from the duloxetine 20 mg group (p=0.07)**."*

| Endpoint | 20mg vs placebo | Status |
|---|---|---|
| HAM-A total | p=0.01–0.001 | **Separated** |
| HAM-A psychic factor | p=0.01–0.001 | **Separated** |
| HAM-A somatic factor | **p=0.07** | **Did not separate** |
| **Non-inferiority to 60–120mg** | **—** | **NEVER TESTED** |

**The point estimates, and a warning about them** `[secondary — unverified]`:

| Arm | HAM-A change | Response | Remission |
|---|---|---|---|
| Duloxetine 20mg | **−14.7 ± 1.0 (SE)** | **60%** | **42%** |
| Duloxetine 60–120mg | **−15.3 ± 0.7 (SE)** | **65%** | **44%** |
| Placebo | **−11.6** | **42%** | **20%** |

These come from a secondary review, not from the primary paper. The review also reports the changes as "45%" and "55%" decreases respectively, and **those percentages are internally inconsistent with the absolute values and with each other**: if −14.7 is a 45% decrease the baseline HAM-A was 32.7, and if −15.3 is a 55% decrease the baseline was 27.8, and GAD registration trials typically enrol at baseline 24–26. Both implied baselines are too high and they disagree. **The percentages are not presented here as fact, and they should not be quoted.** The substantive claim survives without them: on the absolute change, on response, and on remission, all three comparisons put the higher dose slightly ahead.

**Derived, and labelled as derived** `[reasoning, not evidence]`: drug–placebo difference ≈ 3.1 HAM-A points at 20mg and ≈ 3.7 at 60–120mg; response NNT ≈ 5.6 at 20mg and ≈ 4.3 at 60–120mg; remission NNT ≈ 4.5 at 20mg.

**Funding and conflicts** `[primary, verified]`: Duenas, Spann, Ball, Sagman, Russell and Hallberg were **employees and shareholders of Eli Lilly**. Erickson was a former Lilly employee. Nicolini has received research funding from Lilly. Bakish has grant support from Lilly and sits on its speaker's bureau. **Six of nine authors are Lilly employees or shareholders**, and the summarising review states plainly that all five efficacy studies were sponsored by Eli Lilly. The single trial supporting your current dose was designed, run, analysed and written by the manufacturer.

**Uniqueness, confirmed by enumeration** `[primary, verified]`: Nicolini 2009 is the only duloxetine trial that ever contained a fixed 20mg GAD arm. Hartford 2007, Allgulander 2008, Koponen and Rynn have none. Li 2018's meta-analysis identifies only Nicolini as containing a 20mg fixed-dose arm. No fixed 20mg once-daily monotherapy efficacy trial exists in depression either; the depression programme used 40–120mg, and its common "20mg twice daily" is 40mg a day, which is not your dose. **20mg once daily is below the FDA-approved therapeutic minimum for every psychiatric indication** (GAD starts at 30mg; depression at 40mg). That does not mean it cannot work. It means you are outside the labelled range and should know it.

### The question nobody asked

**Allgulander C, Nutt D, Detke M, Erickson J, Spann M, Walker D, Ball SG, Russell JM.** "A non-inferiority comparison of duloxetine and venlafaxine in the treatment of adult patients with generalized anxiety disorder." *J Psychopharmacol.* 2008;22(4):417–425. **PMID 18635722.** `[primary, verified]`

Allgulander 2008 was a prespecified plan to pool data from two nearly identical 10-week, multi-centre, randomised, placebo-controlled, double-blind studies. The reconciliation:

| Arm | Hartford 2007 (PMID 17414743) | Nicolini 2009 (PMID 18485261) | Allgulander pooled |
|---|---|---|---|
| Placebo | 161 | 170 | **331** ✓ |
| Duloxetine 60–120mg | 162 | 158 | **320** ✓ |
| Venlafaxine XR | 164 | 169 | **333** ✓ |
| **Duloxetine 20mg** | **no such arm** | **84** | **EXCLUDED** |

Three arms reconcile exactly. One arm vanishes.

What this establishes `[primary, verified]`:

1. **Non-inferiority WAS formally tested**, but only for duloxetine 60–120mg against venlafaxine.
2. **The 20mg arm was excluded from the non-inferiority analysis entirely.**
3. **Therefore 20mg has never been tested for non-inferiority to 60–120mg.** Not against venlafaxine, not against the higher duloxetine arm, not at all.

An earlier pass of this project described 20mg as showing "a response in the same neighbourhood as 60–120mg" and "near-parity." Both overstate it, for three reasons `[reasoning, not evidence]`. The 60%-versus-65% comparison is a naive between-arm eyeball, not a statistical test. No test of 20mg against 60–120mg was performed at all, and with n=84 against n=158 the trial had no power to run one. And "beat placebo" and "as good as 60mg" are separate claims that must stay separate.

**The claim that survives, in full**: *20mg beat placebo on HAM-A total and the psychic factor in one 84-patient fixed-dose arm of one Lilly trial. It was never compared to 60mg. It failed on the somatic factor.*

### The split that is real and the mapping that is not

**Li X, Zhu L, Zhou C, Liu J, Du H, Wang C, Fang S.** "Efficacy and tolerability of short-term duloxetine treatment in adults with generalized anxiety disorder: A meta-analysis." *PLoS One.* 2018;13(3):e0194501. **PMID 29558528 / PMC5860757.** `[primary, verified — full text retrieved]`

- **8 RCTs, 2,399 adult patients** with moderate-intensity GAD (1,161 duloxetine / 1,238 placebo).
- **Response: OR 2.22 (95% CI 1.88–2.62), P<0.00001.**
- **Remission: OR 1.99 (95% CI 1.66–2.39).**
- **Funding**: National Natural Science Foundation of China (81671186). *"The authors have declared that no competing interests exist."* Independently funded and non-conflicted, which is rare in this literature and means it should carry more weight than the Lilly-authored reviews.
- **No dose-stratified analysis** `[primary, verified]`. This is why it cannot speak to your dose.
- **Harms**: discontinuation due to adverse events was significantly higher on duloxetine than placebo; treatment-emergent adverse event incidence was significantly increased.

### Four lines pointing the wrong way

The reader asked for mechanism. What the adjacent evidence supplies is a problem.

| Line | What it shows | Direction |
|---|---|---|
| **Oranje 2011** (PMID 20971512) `[primary, verified]` | Escitalopram **15mg**, n=20 healthy males, double-blind placebo-controlled crossover. Verbatim: *"Escitalopram significantly **decreased P50 suppression and habituation**, but had no effect on PPI."* Authors' conclusion: *"habituation and sensory gating are disrupted by increased serotonergic activity."* | **Against** |
| **Jensen 2007** (PMID 17299503) `[primary, verified]` | Escitalopram **10mg**, n=18 healthy males, same design. Verbatim: escitalopram *"significantly **delay[ed] habituation** of the ASR."* Conclusion: *"increased serotonergic activity **disrupts habituation**."* | **Against** |
| **Simeon 1995** (PMID 8570768) `[primary, verified]` | 67 subjects, double-blind placebo-controlled challenge with the partial serotonin agonist **m-CPP**. *"Challenge with m-CPP induced depersonalization significantly more than did placebo."* **18%** experienced depersonalisation or derealisation. | **Against** |
| **Maier & Watkins 2005** (PMID 15893820) `[primary, verified]` | Verbatim: *"uncontrollable stressors sensitize serotonergic neurons in the dorsal raphe... and the **consequent exaggerated release of serotonin** in response to subsequent input **is at least in part responsible for the behavioral changes that occur**."* | **Against** |

**Habituation is the exact function whose failure produces sensory overwhelm** `[reasoning, not evidence]`. **And the drug in the first two studies is escitalopram, the drug that made you dissociate and go non-functional** `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

**And there is a fifth line with the same sign, which is the one that matters most for the stress model.** In Maier and Watkins' framework, serotonin is closer to the **effector** of stress-induced collapse than to the buffer against it. The question "does serotonin set a stress-resilience threshold?" has a real, well-developed answer in the literature, and the answer runs the other way.

#### The honest limits, stated at the claim rather than below it

1. **m-CPP is a 5-HT2C-preferring agonist; duloxetine is a reuptake inhibitor.** An agonist slams a receptor. A reuptake inhibitor raises ambient transmitter and then the system adapts. Not the same pharmacology `[reasoning, not evidence]`.
2. **The m-CPP depersonalisation tracked panic, not serotonin per se.** The authors report *"a significant correlation between the induction of depersonalization and increase in **panic**, but not nervousness, anxiety, sadness, depression, or drowsiness."* m-CPP is a reliable panicogen, so the serotonergic step may be upstream of a non-specific route `[primary, verified on the correlation; reasoning on the implication]`.
3. **The gating studies are n=18 and n=20 healthy males, single acute doses, laboratory startle and EEG paradigms.** The leap from a P50 waveform to a crowded festival is enormous and unbridged `[reasoning, not evidence]`.
4. **There is a within-lab complication that cuts against a simple story.** Jensen 2008 (PMID 18000656), escitalopram **10mg**, n=21, found **P50 suppression intact**. Oranje 2011 at **15mg** found it **disrupted**. Same lab, same paradigm, same population, same design. A 1.5× dose step flipped the filter `[primary, verified ×2]`. That is a dose-graded ladder rather than a flat "serotonin breaks gating" claim, and it is a cross-study comparison rather than a within-study dose-response, which this project's own error log flags as its most repeated mistake. It is flagged here rather than buried.
5. **All four gating and MMN studies come from a single group** (Copenhagen/Glostrup). **No independent replication exists** `[primary, verified by enumeration]`. The one independent lab that touched the question ran the opposite manipulation: Mann 2008 (PMID 17895917) found that **serotonin depletion disrupted PPI but not P50 suppression**, which is the mirror image and refutes any simple monotonic story. An inverted-U with different measures on each limb is the more honest reading `[reasoning, not evidence]`.
6. **Maier & Watkins is rats and learned helplessness.** Not autism, not sensory overwhelm, not duloxetine, and the model's construct validity for human depression is itself contested `[reasoning, not evidence]`.
7. **Nobody has measured whether the acute gating cost persists chronically.** Every one of the escitalopram studies is a single acute dose in a drug-naive healthy volunteer. A targeted search for chronic-dosing sensory-gating studies returned nothing `[no evidence exists]`. So the question "am I permanently trading acute sensitivity for aftermath protection?" is not weakly answered. It is unanswered.

#### The only account that reconciles it, and why it is held loosely

`[reasoning, not evidence — this is a hypothesis, labelled as one]`

If acute serotonin elevation is anxiogenic, dissociogenic and gating-disrupting, and chronic treatment produces adaptation at the relevant receptors, then:

- **The acute phase looks like your escitalopram experience**: dissociation, no anxiolysis, non-functional.
- **The chronic phase looks like your current experience**: episodes suppressed.
- **The therapeutic effect is not "more serotonin." It is the adaptation to more serotonin**, a receptor-level change running opposite to the drug's acute pharmacology.

**Three virtues**: it explains the escitalopram failure and the duloxetine response with one mechanism; it explains the two-to-six-week onset delay every antidepressant shows; and it makes a falsifiable prediction, which is that a dose increase should re-create the acute state.

**Four problems, and they travel with it**:

1. **The desensitisation timeline in humans is not established** `[secondary — unverified]`.
2. **You are at two weeks, which is early for a chronic adaptation.** The account is in tension with your own timeline `[reasoning, not evidence]`. This is flagged against the account's own interest.
3. **The whole desensitisation family is under sustained attack.** The best-known version, built on 5-HT1A autoreceptors, has failed repeatedly: molecular imaging and postmortem work found no consistent 5-HT1A alterations in depression; 5-HT1A antagonists failed to produce consistent antidepressant efficacy, which the theory predicts they should; firing rate normalises *despite* ongoing inhibition rather than because inhibition was removed `[secondary — unverified]`. The critiques are specific to 5-HT1A and do not automatically transfer to a 5-HT2C version, but they should induce real humility about the entire family.
4. **Nobody has tested any of this in an autistic adult, in this phenotype, or with duloxetine** `[no evidence exists]`.

**Grade: the best available reasoning. Not a mechanism. Not evidence.**

There is also one human study pointing the other way on the adaptation half, and it belongs here. **Zohar 1988 (PMID 3276283)**: n=9 OCD patients given m-CPP challenge before and after four months of clomipramine, showing *"adaptive subsensitivity to the serotonergic agonist mCPP"* `[primary, verified]`. Read straight, that predicts the acute state should get **better** with chronic treatment, not that a dose step would re-create it. The objections are real (n=9, no control group, uncontrolled order effect, four months, clomipramine, OCD), but they are heterogeneity-grade objections rather than logic-grade ones, so the direction claim survives as weak evidence against the account.

**The observation** `[primary, verified — your own report, and the family's independent anticipation is a real corroborating detail]`:

> *"The acute sensory overwhelm **is** worse... **but** I'm not crashing after the incident, which I normally would."*

> *"Its lack was very stark, not just to me but to my whole family. We all braced for it, but it never came."*

**The instance**: 5+ hours at two crowded festivals. Two panic attacks with heart rate above 140 bpm at rest. Historically a guaranteed 1–2 week crash trigger. **No crash.**

**Two effects moving in opposite directions, same drug, same person, same day.** What that shape rules out `[reasoning, not evidence]`:

| Hypothesis | Why the shape defeats it |
|---|---|
| **Sedation** | Would blunt both. The acute got worse. |
| **Emotional blunting** | Same. Would move both the same way. |
| **Global muting** | Same. |
| **Simple expectancy** | Predicts the acute overwhelm would feel **better**. It was worse. |
| **Pure placebo response in you** | The absence of the crash was **independently anticipated by your family**, who braced for it. |

A targeted search from several angles (antidepressants and delayed stress sequelae; drugs decoupling acute reactivity from aftermath; prophylaxis of prolonged post-stress states) returned nothing. The human evidence explains the acute half. But it predicts acute-**intact**, not acute-**worse**, so even the best-fitting animal result does not produce your pattern.

**The absence is not a gap in the searching. It is a gap in the field**, and it sits on top of the three evidence-absences this deep dive already names as its most valuable claims. This is a fourth: *nobody has studied whether antidepressants decouple acute stress from its delayed sequelae in humans.*

### Does it keep working?

#### The famous number, dissected

**Solomon DA, Leon AC, Mueller TI, Coryell W, Teres JJ, Posternak MA, Judd LL, Endicott J, Keller MB.** "Tachyphylaxis in unipolar major depressive disorder." *J Clin Psychiatry.* 2005 Mar;66(3):283-90. **PMID 15766292.** `[primary abstract only]`

> Verbatim: *"For the 103 subjects, there were 171 maintenance treatment intervals... **Tachyphylaxis occurred during 43 (25%) of these 171 maintenance treatment intervals.**"*

| Feature | What Solomon 2005 actually is |
|---|---|
| Design | **Observational**, not randomised |
| Cohort | NIMH Collaborative Depression Study, **enrolled 1978–81** |
| Drugs | **Tricyclics and MAOIs** |
| Median maintenance interval | **20 weeks** |
| Definition of "tachyphylaxis" | **Recurrence of depression while on maintenance pharmacotherapy** |

**The fatal problem** `[reasoning, not evidence, though it follows directly from the design]`: in an uncontrolled observational cohort, "recurrence during maintenance" **cannot distinguish tachyphylaxis from the natural course of a recurring illness, from non-adherence, or from a drug that never worked in the first place.** Solomon 2005 does not measure how often antidepressants stop working. It measures how often depression comes back in people who are taking one. Those are different quantities and the design cannot separate them.

#### The range, which is a confession

**9–57%** `[primary, verified]`. Verbatim from the source review: *"Rates of tachyphylaxis varied from 9% to 57% depending on the patient population and duration of follow-up"*; *"studies of antidepressant tachyphylaxis are largely heterogeneous"*; *"few established treatment strategies exist to manage antidepressant tachyphylaxis."*

A quantity whose credible range spans six-fold is not an estimate.

#### The result that inverts the story

**Zimmerman M, Thongy T.** "How often do SSRIs and other new-generation antidepressants lose their effect during continuation treatment? Evidence suggesting the rate of true tachyphylaxis during continuation treatment is low." *J Clin Psychiatry.* 2007 Aug;68(8):1271-6. **PMID 17854252.** `[primary, verified]`

> **Relapse during continuation: 24.1% in PLACEBO responders vs 7.4% in DRUG responders.**

If people who responded to a dummy pill lose their response more than three times as often as people who responded to the drug, then **most of what gets called "poop-out" is placebo-response decay**. The phenomenon is real as an experience and largely illusory as a pharmacology `[reasoning, not evidence]`. Note the arithmetic: **24.1% is remarkably close to Solomon's 25%.** That may be coincidence. It may also be the whole story, which is that the famous 25% is the placebo-decay rate wearing a pharmacological costume. That cannot be established from here, and it is flagged as a pattern worth noticing rather than as a finding.

#### The best numbers available for duloxetine specifically

| Trial | Indication | Duration | **Relapse on duloxetine** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Davidson 2008** (PMID 18559291) `[primary, verified]` | **GAD** | **26 weeks** | **13.7%** (placebo 41.8%, P≤0.001) |
| **Perahia 2009** (PMID 19552867) `[primary, verified]` | MDD recurrence | **up to 52 weeks** | **14.4%** (placebo 33.1%, p<0.001) |

**Why these survive the withdrawal critique** `[reasoning, not evidence]`: the critique of antidepressant relapse-prevention trials is that the placebo arm's relapses are inflated by withdrawal effects, because those people were taken off a drug. That attacks the *contrast*, so "41.8% vs 13.7%" is confounded. **But the 13.7% itself is not confounded by withdrawal, because those people did not withdraw from anything.**

**What that ~14% is, precisely** `[reasoning, not evidence]`: an **upper bound on tachyphylaxis**, not a measure of it. It bundles (a) genuine loss of drug effect, (b) natural illness course breaking through a partially effective drug, and (c) non-adherence. **Tachyphylaxis is at most 14% and probably well under it**, because that figure is everyone who relapsed for any reason.

#### The two absences

- **No placebo-controlled duloxetine data exists past 12 months**, on anyone, for any indication `[no evidence exists]`.
- **Duloxetine-specific tachyphylaxis has never been studied.** A targeted search for duloxetine tachyphylaxis, duloxetine loss of efficacy, and SNRI poop-out found no study measuring it for duloxetine specifically, in any indication `[no evidence exists — negative search finding]`.

**One trap worth naming**, because it is the shape of source that has damaged this project before: an AI-generated medical answer site asserts that *"there is no documented evidence of tachyphylaxis with duloxetine"* and cites an open-label compassionate-use study. **That is not a source.** "No documented evidence of tachyphylaxis" in an open-label uncontrolled study that was not measuring tachyphylaxis is not evidence of absence. It is absence of measurement.

#### One counter-pattern worth knowing

Where recurrence has been examined closely, the second recurrences were **significantly less severe** than the first (25.1 → 21.6, P<0.0001) `[primary abstract only]`. **That is not the pattern tachyphylaxis predicts.** Pharmacological tolerance predicts escalating failure. Attenuating severity looks more like a drug still doing something partial against an illness running its own course `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

### Counting exposures, not weeks

#### The time-based test, and why it is weak

`[reasoning, not evidence — standard Poisson arithmetic for recurrent events; the calculation is reproducible]`

Model episodes as a Poisson process with baseline rate λ per month. Under the null that the drug does nothing, P(zero episodes over t months) = e^(−λt).

| Elapsed | λ=2/mo | λ=3/mo | λ=4/mo | Reading |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **2 weeks** ← **you are here** | **36.8%** | **22.3%** | **13.5%** | **Unremarkable. Would happen by chance often.** |
| 1 month | 13.5% | 5.0% | 1.8% | Suggestive at best |
| **6 weeks** | **5.0%** | **1.1%** | **0.25%** | **Crosses p<0.05 even at the lowest baseline** |
| 2 months | 1.8% | 0.25% | 0.03% | Chance implausible at every rate |
| **3 months** | **0.25%** | **0.012%** | **0.0006%** | **Chance is dead** |

**At two weeks, 13–37% of people whose drug does nothing at all would see exactly what you are seeing.** That is roughly a one-in-three to one-in-seven chance of a pure fluke. Not a reason for pessimism. A reason not to treat two weeks as informative.

> **⚠ An inconsistency in the input, flagged rather than smoothed over.** "Several times a month" combined with "episodes last 1–2 weeks" does not arithmetically fit: three episodes a month at 1.5 weeks each is 4.5 weeks of episode per 4.3-week month, which is continuous illness. Either the episodes usually run much shorter than the theme-park example, or the rate is an overestimate, or they overlap. **The theme-park-to-two-week episode may be an unusually severe instance rather than the modal one** `[reasoning, not evidence]`. This ambiguity propagates into every number in the table, and you are the only person who can resolve it. It is worth resolving before the appointment.

**And the crucial qualifier, which is where most n-of-1 reasoning goes wrong**: the math kills **random fluctuation**. It does **not** touch placebo, regression to the mean, or natural course, because those are not chance. They are systematic effects producing the identical zero-episode observation. **A three-month clean run proves the improvement is real. It says nothing about whether the drug caused it.**

#### The trigger-conditional test, which is the good one

`[reasoning, not evidence — a study-design argument, not a finding]`

**Your episodes are triggered, so the denominator is exposures, not time.**

> **Two months with no episodes while working from home, avoiding crowds, and having a quiet winter is worth almost nothing.** The drug was never tested.
>
> **One festival day with no crash afterwards is worth more than two uneventful months.**

Let p₀ be your baseline probability of an episode given a significant sensory overload. Surviving k comparable exposures clean has probability (1 − p₀)ᵏ under the null.

| Baseline P(episode \| overload) | Exposures needed for p<0.05 |
|---|---|
| **p₀ = 0.9** (near-deterministic) | **2** |
| **p₀ = 0.8** | **3** |
| **p₀ = 0.7** | **3–4** |
| **p₀ = 0.5** | **5** |

**Three to five comparable sensory overloads survived cleanly is roughly a p<0.05 result, about the thing that actually matters.**

**Why it beats waiting**: it is faster, better-powered, more specific, and it has a property the time-based test lacks. **Regression to the mean cannot fake it.** Regression explains why a bad stretch is followed by a better stretch. It does not explain why a specific, repeated, previously reliable trigger stopped producing its effect `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

**Where you are: one.** The festivals were a real exposure, and it was survived clean.

**The limits, at the claim**: it requires characterising p₀ retrospectively, which is recall-biased; it requires exposures to be genuinely comparable, which is a judgement call; and **deliberately seeking out overload to test a drug is a real cost with a real risk, and nothing here recommends it.** The point is that when overloads happen naturally, those are the informative data points, and they should be recorded prospectively, starting now.

#### What each hypothesis predicts

`[reasoning, not evidence throughout]`

| Observation | **Genuine drug effect predicts** | **Placebo / regression / natural course predicts** |
|---|---|---|
| **Trigger-conditional response** | Episodes stay absent **even after real sensory overloads** | Episodes absent while triggers absent; **return when a real overload happens** |
| **Time course** | Stable or consolidating over months | **Decay.** Placebo response decays; regression is a one-time reversion, not a plateau |
| **The escitalopram contrast** | Explained by drug-specific pharmacology | **Unexplained.** Placebo should have worked equally well for both drugs |
| **Other duloxetine-sensitive signs** | Sleep, vivid dreams, RLS worsening and the "floaty" feeling **track together** with the episode benefit | Episode benefit is **dissociated** from the pharmacological signs |
| **Direction of any breakthrough** | Breakthroughs **less severe** than baseline | Breakthroughs **as severe** as baseline |

**The three sharpest markers, ranked** `[reasoning, not evidence]`:

1. **The trigger test.** The only marker that separates drug from regression to the mean.
2. **The RLS, the vivid dreams, and the floaty feeling.** These are pharmacological signatures, and they are **not plausible placebo responses**, because nobody's expectation produces restless legs. They prove the drug is doing something neurologically real at 20mg. That does not prove the drug is causing the episode benefit, because two real effects of one drug need not be the same effect. **But it removes "the drug is inert at this dose" from the table**, and it supplies a covariate: if the dreams and RLS fade while the episode benefit persists, that dissociation is informative; if they fade together, that is a coherent drug story.
3. **The escitalopram contrast.** You have already run a within-subject control and it came out negative. A pure expectancy account has to explain why the first serotonergic drug produced dissociation and non-function while the second produced resolution. **Expectancy does not usually reverse sign between two drugs in the same class.** It is not conclusive, because the drugs differ pharmacologically, your circumstances differ, and time has passed. It is a genuine asymmetry that the deflationary account has to absorb.

`[reasoning, not evidence]`

| When | What is knowable |
