## Claim-by-Claim Validation

### 1. Lakoff & Johnson's Central Thesis (Section 1)

**Claim**: Lakoff and Johnson's central claim in *Metaphors We Live By* (1980) is that "our ordinary conceptual system, in terms of which we both think and act, is fundamentally metaphorical in nature."

**Rating**: CONFIRMED (10/10)

**Evidence**: This is a direct and accurate quote from the opening chapter of *Metaphors We Live By* (1980). Multiple independent sources confirm this as the exact wording. The three interlocking components described (metaphor is conceptual not linguistic; abstract concepts understood through bodily experience mappings; metaphorical thought is largely unconscious) are all accurate characterizations of Lakoff and Johnson's position.

### 2. The Invariance Principle (Section 1)

**Claim**: Lakoff's Invariance Principle (1990, 1993) states: "Metaphorical mappings preserve the cognitive topology (i.e., the image-schema structure) of the source domain, in a way consistent with the inherent structure of the target domain."

**Rating**: CONFIRMED (9/10)

**Evidence**: The Invariance Principle was formally introduced in Lakoff (1990) "The Invariance Hypothesis: is abstract reason based on Image-schemas?" in *Cognitive Linguistics* 1(1): 39-74, and further developed in Lakoff (1993) "The contemporary theory of metaphor." The formulation in the research document is accurate. The dates (1990, 1993) are correct. However, note that the 1993 formulation is slightly different in emphasis: "The image-schema structure of the source domain is projected onto the target domain in a way that is consistent with the inherent target domain structure" (Lakoff 1993: 245). The research document's wording synthesizes both formulations accurately.

Johnson may have presented this work or completed a dissertation earlier (1997 is widely cited in secondary literature as the date of his contribution), but the publication record shows the formal publication as 1999. The series is correctly identified as *Current Issues in Linguistic Theory*, but the research document calls it just that without noting the specific volume title.

### 11. fMRI Evidence and 200ms Timing (Section 5)

**Claim**: Sensory-motor regions are activated within 200 milliseconds of an action verb being presented, for both literal and metaphorical contexts.

**Rating**: CONFIRMED (9/10)

**Evidence**: Boulenger and colleagues found cross-talk between language processes and motor behavior within the first 200ms of processing. Pulvermuller and colleagues confirmed motor cortex activation occurs approximately 150-200ms after action verb onset. The 200ms timing figure is well-supported by EEG/MEG literature. The claim that "this speed rules out post-hoc imagery and indicates automatic, mandatory activation" is a reasonable interpretation, though it should be noted this is the CMT-favorable interpretation; critics might argue for alternative explanations of such rapid activation.

## Missing Perspectives and Critiques

### 1. Relevance Theory Alternative (MISSING)

**Importance**: HIGH

Sperber and Wilson's Relevance Theory provides a significant alternative account of metaphor processing that is not mentioned in the research document. Under Relevance Theory, metaphor is treated as "loose talk" -- a case of ad hoc concept construction where the encoded concept serves as a point of access to encyclopedic information. This framework offers a fundamentally different mechanism from CMT's pre-existing conceptual mappings and has generated substantial debate. The omission is significant given that the document covers Glucksberg's class-inclusion model but not this equally important alternative.

### 2. Gentner's Structure-Mapping Theory (MISSING)

**Importance**: MODERATE-HIGH

Dedre Gentner's Structure-Mapping Theory of analogy and metaphor (1983, 1988) is another major alternative not discussed. Gentner treats metaphor as analogical mapping where relational structure (not just features) is projected from source to target. Her "career of metaphor" theory (with Bowdle, 2005) proposes that novel metaphors are processed as comparisons while conventional metaphors are processed as categorizations. This theory bridges some of the gap between Lakoff's CMT and Glucksberg's class-inclusion model.

### 3. McGlone's Explanatory Value Critique (MISSING)

**Importance**: MODERATE

McGlone (2007) "What Is the Explanatory Value of a Conceptual Metaphor?" provides an important critique not covered in the document. McGlone argues that CMT functions more as a descriptive label than a tested causal mechanism, and that the theory's evidence base suffers from confirmation bias. This is a sharper version of the methodological critiques briefly mentioned in Section 7.

### 4. Corpus-Based Empirical Testing (MISSING)

**Importance**: MODERATE

The document does not discuss corpus-based approaches to testing CMT, such as Stefanowitsch and Gries's Metaphorical Pattern Analysis. These empirical methods have both supported and challenged CMT's claims by providing quantitative evidence about actual metaphor usage patterns, sometimes finding mappings not predicted by CMT and failing to find others that CMT predicts.

### 5. The Career of Metaphor Hypothesis (MISSING)

**Importance**: MODERATE

Bowdle and Gentner's (2005) "Career of Metaphor" hypothesis proposes that metaphors shift from comparison-based processing (when novel) to categorization-based processing (when conventionalized). This provides a developmental account of how metaphors change in cognitive processing over time, which complements the static mapping account in CMT.

### 6. Simulation Semantics (UNDERREPRESENTED)

**Importance**: MODERATE

While the document mentions Narayanan's computational model, it could more fully describe "simulation semantics" -- the hypothesis that understanding language involves running mental simulations using the same neural circuits involved in perception and action. This has become an important bridge concept between CMT, Barsalou's work, and the neuroscience evidence.
