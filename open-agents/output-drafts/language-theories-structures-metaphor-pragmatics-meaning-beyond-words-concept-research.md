# Pragmatics -- How Meaning Exceeds What Is Said

## Overview

Pragmatics is the study of how context, speaker intention, and shared knowledge combine to produce meaning that goes far beyond the literal content of an utterance. When someone says "It's cold in here," the words describe a temperature. But the utterance might function as a request to close a window, a complaint about the heating, or an explanation for wearing a coat -- and every competent speaker knows this without being told. This gap between what is said and what is communicated is not a flaw in language; it is language's most fundamental feature. Pragmatics investigates the mechanisms that bridge this gap: Grice's cooperative principle and conversational implicature, Sperber and Wilson's relevance theory, Austin and Searle's speech act theory, Wittgenstein's language games, the phenomena of presupposition and deixis, Brown and Levinson's politeness theory, and the pervasive role of non-literal meaning in everyday communication.

The central finding of pragmatics is radical: human communication is massively underdetermined by literal meaning. Every utterance requires inference about speaker intention, context, and shared knowledge. What we "say" is merely a skeleton that hearers flesh out using an elaborate apparatus of pragmatic reasoning.

## 1. Grice's Cooperative Principle and Conversational Implicature

### Historical Context

H. Paul Grice (1913-1988) was a British philosopher of language who delivered a series of lectures at Harvard in 1967 (the William James Lectures), later published as "Logic and Conversation" in 1975 and collected in *Studies in the Way of Words* (1989). Grice's work represents one of the most influential contributions to pragmatics and the philosophy of language, providing a systematic account of how speakers convey meaning beyond the literal content of their words.

Before Grice, philosophers and linguists had drawn a distinction between what a sentence means (its semantic content) and what a speaker might use it to convey. But Grice was the first to propose a principled, systematic mechanism for how this worked -- grounding it in rational cooperation between speakers.

### Natural vs. Non-Natural Meaning

In his 1957 paper "Meaning," Grice drew a foundational distinction:

- **Natural meaning (meaningN)**: Causal, law-like connections in the world. "Those spots mean measles." If the patient does not have measles, the spots do not mean measles. Natural meaning cannot be faked -- it rests on real-world causal relationships.
- **Non-natural meaning (meaningNN)**: Speaker-intended meaning that depends on the recognition of intentions. "Those three rings on the bell mean the bus is full." This meaning is conventional and intentional -- it holds even if the bus is not actually full (someone could ring three times by mistake). Non-natural meaning is what pragmatics studies.

Grice formulated non-natural meaning in terms of reflexive intentions: A speaker means something by an utterance if and only if they intend the utterance to produce a certain effect in the hearer, and they intend the hearer to recognize this intention, and they intend this recognition itself to be (at least partly) the reason the hearer is affected as intended.

### The Cooperative Principle

Grice proposed that conversation is governed by a general principle of cooperation:

> **"Make your conversational contribution such as is required, at the stage at which it occurs, by the accepted purpose or direction of the talk exchange in which you are engaged."**

This is not a moral injunction but a descriptive generalization about what speakers and hearers assume. Conversation works because participants tacitly agree to cooperate toward mutual understanding. Even when speakers violate this principle (through lies, evasions, or rudeness), the assumption of cooperation is what makes the violation meaningful.

### The Four Maxims

Under the Cooperative Principle, Grice identified four specific maxims:

**1. Maxim of Quantity** (Be informative)
- Make your contribution as informative as is required for the current purposes of the exchange.
- Do not make your contribution more informative than is required.

**2. Maxim of Quality** (Be truthful)
- Do not say what you believe to be false.
- Do not say that for which you lack adequate evidence.

**3. Maxim of Relation** (Be relevant)
- Be relevant. (Grice acknowledged this maxim was deceptively simple and that "relevance" itself needed further analysis -- a gap that Sperber and Wilson would later fill.)

**4. Maxim of Manner** (Be clear)
- Avoid obscurity of expression.
- Avoid ambiguity.
- Be brief (avoid unnecessary prolixity).
- Be orderly.

### Flouting Maxims: The Engine of Implicature

The real power of Grice's framework emerges not when the maxims are followed but when they are conspicuously violated -- or "flouted." When a speaker blatantly fails to observe a maxim, the hearer, still assuming cooperation, infers that the speaker must be communicating something beyond the literal content. This inferred meaning is a **conversational implicature**.

**Flouting Quantity** (saying too little or too much):
- Boss: "How is the new employee?"
- Colleague: "Well, he's always on time."
- The colleague says nothing about the employee's competence, creativity, or output -- conspicuously omitting the kind of information normally expected. The implicature: the employee is mediocre or incompetent, and the only positive thing the colleague can say is about punctuality.

**Flouting Quality** (saying something obviously false):
- "Oh, that's just great" (said when something terrible happens).
- The speaker says something patently untrue, generating the implicature of sarcasm or irony -- the speaker means the opposite of what they literally say.

**Flouting Relation** (saying something apparently irrelevant):
- A: "How did the exam go?"
- B: "Well, the weather was nice today."
- B's response seems irrelevant. The implicature: the exam went badly, and B is changing the subject rather than admitting it.

**Flouting Manner** (being deliberately obscure or prolix):
- "She did the thing with the stuff at the place" (when the speaker could be more specific).
- The deliberate vagueness implicates that the speaker does not want to be explicit -- perhaps because children are present or the topic is sensitive.

### Types of Conversational Implicature

**Particularized Conversational Implicature (PCI)**: Arises only in specific contexts and depends heavily on the particular situation. These are by far the more common kind. The "new employee" example above is particularized -- the implicature depends on the specific conversational context.

**Generalized Conversational Implicature (GCI)**: Arises regardless of the particular context, carried by certain words or constructions. For example:
- "Some students passed the exam" generally implicates "Not all students passed" (a scalar implicature, discussed below).
- "I saw a man in the garden" generally implicates "The man was not my husband/brother/someone I know well" -- otherwise I would have used a more informative description.

### Properties of Conversational Implicature

Grice identified several diagnostic properties that distinguish conversational implicatures from logical entailments and conventional meanings:

1. **Cancelability (Defeasibility)**: Implicatures can be explicitly canceled without contradiction. "Some students passed -- in fact, all of them did." The "not all" implicature is canceled, but there is no contradiction.

2. **Non-detachability**: The implicature is attached to the semantic content, not to the particular words used. If you paraphrase the utterance using different words with the same meaning, the implicature typically persists. (Exception: manner-based implicatures, which do depend on the form of expression.)

3. **Calculability**: Implicatures must be derivable through reasoning from the Cooperative Principle, the maxims, the literal meaning of what was said, and contextual knowledge. They are not arbitrary -- a competent hearer can work out why the implicature arises.

4. **Reinforceability**: An implicature can be made explicit without redundancy. "Some students passed, but not all" sounds natural rather than tautological, confirming that "not all" was an implicature rather than part of the literal meaning.

5. **Non-conventionality**: Unlike conventional meanings, implicatures are not part of the semantic content of the words themselves. They are generated by the interaction of what is said with the context and the maxims.

### Conventional Implicature

Grice also identified a distinct category: **conventional implicatures** are conveyed by particular words but are not part of the truth-conditional content. For example:
- "He is British, but brave" -- the word "but" conventionally implicates a contrast between being British and being brave, but the sentence is true if and only if he is both British and brave (the same truth conditions as "and").
- "Therefore," "even," "yet," and "still" all carry conventional implicatures.

Unlike conversational implicatures, conventional implicatures are not cancelable, not calculable from the Cooperative Principle, and are attached to specific linguistic forms.

## 2. Relevance Theory (Sperber & Wilson)

### Origins and Motivation

Dan Sperber (French cognitive scientist) and Deirdre Wilson (British linguist) developed Relevance Theory, first presented in their seminal book *Relevance: Communication and Cognition* (1986, revised 1995). They were motivated by what they saw as fundamental problems with Grice's framework:

1. The maxims are too numerous and sometimes conflict with each other.
2. "Relevance" (Grice's Maxim of Relation) is left unexplained -- but it arguably does most of the work.
3. The framework does not explain how hearers select from among many possible implicatures.
4. The distinction between "what is said" and "what is implicated" is too sharp -- there is a vast middle ground.

### The Code Model vs. the Inferential Model

Relevance Theory begins with a fundamental critique of what Sperber and Wilson call the **code model** of communication -- the idea that communication works like encoding and decoding messages (sender encodes thought into words; receiver decodes words back into thought). This model, inherited from information theory (Shannon & Weaver), is inadequate because:

- Sentences are ambiguous; words are polysemous.
- Context determines reference ("he," "she," "it," "here," "now").
- Much of what is communicated is never encoded in words at all.
- Understanding requires far more than linguistic decoding.

Instead, Sperber and Wilson propose the **inferential model**: communication is fundamentally a process of inferring speaker intentions from evidence (of which the utterance is only one piece). The hearer uses the utterance, together with contextual knowledge, to construct hypotheses about the speaker's intended meaning.

### Ostensive-Inferential Communication

Sperber and Wilson characterize verbal communication as **ostensive-inferential**:

- **Ostensive**: The speaker makes it manifest (obvious) that they intend to communicate something. The very act of speaking is an act of ostension -- it draws the hearer's attention to the fact that the speaker wants to convey information. This involves two layers of intention:
  - **Informative intention**: The intention to inform the audience of something.
  - **Communicative intention**: The intention to inform the audience of one's informative intention -- to make it mutually manifest that communication is being attempted.

- **Inferential**: The hearer infers what the speaker intended to communicate, using the utterance as evidence together with contextual assumptions.

This is fundamentally different from the code model. A coded message simply needs decoding; an ostensive-inferential act requires the hearer to construct hypotheses about intentions and evaluate them.

### The Cognitive Principle of Relevance

The theory rests on a bold claim about human cognition:

> **Cognitive Principle of Relevance**: Human cognition tends to be geared to the maximization of relevance.

Relevance is defined as a property of inputs to cognitive processes (utterances, observations, memories). An input is relevant to an individual when it connects with available background information to yield conclusions that matter -- **positive cognitive effects**:

1. **Contextual implications**: New conclusions derivable from the input together with existing assumptions, but not from either alone.
2. **Strengthening**: The input provides evidence that supports an existing assumption.
3. **Contradiction and elimination**: The input provides evidence against an existing assumption, leading to its revision.

Relevance is assessed along two dimensions:
- **Greater cognitive effects = greater relevance** (other things being equal)
- **Greater processing effort = lesser relevance** (other things being equal)

### The Communicative Principle of Relevance

> **Communicative Principle of Relevance**: Every act of ostensive communication conveys a presumption of its own optimal relevance.

An utterance is optimally relevant when:
1. It is relevant enough to be worth the hearer's processing effort.
2. It is the most relevant stimulus the speaker is willing and able to produce given their abilities, preferences, and goals.

This single principle replaces all four of Grice's maxims. The maxims of Quantity, Quality, Relation, and Manner are all subsumed: be informative (because uninformative utterances lack cognitive effects), be truthful (because false information leads to incorrect cognitive effects), be relevant (the core of the principle), and be clear (because obscurity increases processing effort for no gain).

### The Relevance-Theoretic Comprehension Procedure

The comprehension procedure is the mechanism by which hearers interpret utterances:

1. Follow a path of least effort in computing cognitive effects.
2. Test interpretive hypotheses (reference assignments, disambiguations, implicatures) in order of accessibility (i.e., the easiest to process come first).
3. Stop when the expected level of relevance is achieved.

This is not a conscious, deliberate procedure -- it is an automatic cognitive process. The hearer does not consider all possible interpretations and then select the best one; rather, they follow a fast, heuristic path and stop at the first interpretation that satisfies expectations of relevance.

### Explicature vs. Implicature

Relevance Theory draws a more nuanced distinction than Grice between explicit and implicit communication:

- **Explicature**: An explicitly communicated assumption. This is not simply "what is said" -- it is the result of decoding the linguistic form AND pragmatically enriching it through:
  - **Disambiguation**: Resolving lexical ambiguity ("bank" = financial institution or river bank?)
  - **Reference assignment**: Determining who "he," "she," or "they" refer to
  - **Saturation**: Filling in contextually required information ("She's tall" -- tall for what category?)
  - **Free enrichment**: Adding contextually implied content ("I've had breakfast" = today, not ever in my life)
  - **Ad hoc concept construction**: Narrowing or broadening word meanings ("The steak is raw" -- not literally uncooked, but undercooked for the speaker's taste)

- **Implicature**: An implicitly communicated assumption -- something the speaker intended to convey but did not make explicit in any way. These are derived wholly through inference.

The key insight is that even "what is said" requires substantial pragmatic work. There is no clean divide between a purely linguistic/semantic level and a pragmatic level -- pragmatics penetrates all the way down.

### Relevance Theory's Treatment of Metaphor and Irony

Relevance Theory treats metaphor and irony not as special linguistic mechanisms but as natural outcomes of the ordinary comprehension process:

- **Metaphor** is an instance of loose use -- the speaker communicates a concept that is an ad hoc broadening of the literal meaning. "She's a bulldozer" does not require a special metaphor module; the hearer simply constructs a concept BULLDOZER* (related to but broader than the literal concept) that yields adequate cognitive effects for minimal effort.

- **Irony** is an instance of echoic use -- the speaker echoes an attributed thought or utterance while expressing a dissociative (mocking, skeptical, critical) attitude toward it. This is discussed further below in Section 7.

## 3. Speech Act Theory (Austin and Searle)

### Austin's Revolution: Saying as Doing

J.L. Austin (1911-1960) was a British philosopher whose 1955 William James Lectures at Harvard, published posthumously as *How to Do Things with Words* (1962), fundamentally challenged the philosophical tradition's assumption that the primary function of language is to describe the world.

Austin began by observing that many utterances are not descriptions at all -- they are actions:

- "I name this ship the *Queen Elizabeth*."
- "I bet you sixpence it will rain tomorrow."
- "I promise to return the book."
- "I now pronounce you husband and wife."

These **performative utterances** do not describe a pre-existing state of affairs; they bring one into being. Saying "I promise" is not reporting on a promise -- it is the act of promising itself. The utterance is the action.

### Constatives vs. Performatives (and Their Collapse)

Austin initially distinguished:
- **Constatives**: Statements that describe the world and can be true or false. ("The cat is on the mat.")
- **Performatives**: Utterances that perform an action and cannot be true or false, but can succeed or fail. ("I promise to come.")

However, as Austin worked through the lectures, he found the distinction increasingly difficult to maintain:
- Constatives can also fail (a statement based on false presuppositions misfires just like a botched performative).
- Performatives can also have truth-like evaluations ("I promise to come" -- was it a sincere promise?).
- Many utterances are simultaneously descriptive and performative.

This led Austin to abandon the constative/performative distinction and replace it with a more general theory: **every utterance performs multiple acts simultaneously**.

### Austin's Three-Act Model

Austin proposed that any utterance involves three simultaneous acts:

**1. Locutionary Act** -- the act of saying something meaningful:
- **Phonetic act**: Producing certain sounds.
- **Phatic act**: Producing words conforming to a grammar.
- **Rhetic act**: Using those words with a definite sense and reference.

The locutionary act is "what is said" -- the production of a meaningful linguistic expression.

**2. Illocutionary Act** -- the act performed IN saying something:
- Asserting, questioning, commanding, promising, warning, apologizing, thanking, congratulating, declaring, etc.
- The illocutionary act is characterized by its **illocutionary force** -- the type of action the speaker is performing.
- "The door is open" has the same locutionary content whether it functions as an assertion (reporting a fact), a request (asking someone to close it), or a warning (don't go that way).

**3. Perlocutionary Act** -- the act performed BY saying something (the effects on the audience):
- Persuading, frightening, amusing, inspiring, annoying, informing, deceiving, etc.
- Perlocutionary effects may or may not be intended.
- A warning (illocutionary act) may frighten the hearer (perlocutionary effect).

### Felicity Conditions

Since performatives cannot be true or false, Austin asked: when do they succeed or fail? He identified **felicity conditions** -- the conditions that must be met for a speech act to succeed:

1. **A conventional procedure must exist**: There must be an accepted conventional procedure with a conventional effect (e.g., the institution of marriage must exist for "I pronounce you married" to work).
2. **Appropriate persons and circumstances**: The persons and circumstances must be appropriate (a random person on the street cannot pronounce you married -- only an authorized officiant can).
3. **Correct execution**: The procedure must be executed correctly and completely.
4. **Sincerity**: The participants must have the requisite thoughts, feelings, and intentions (a promise made with no intention of keeping it is defective -- Austin called this an "abuse").
5. **Subsequent compliance**: The participants must conduct themselves accordingly afterward.

When conditions 1-3 are violated, the act **misfires** (it fails to come off at all). When conditions 4-5 are violated, the act is **abused** (it goes through but is defective).

### Searle's Systematization

John Searle (b. 1932), Austin's student at Oxford, systematized and extended speech act theory in *Speech Acts: An Essay in the Philosophy of Language* (1969) and *Expression and Meaning* (1979).

**Searle's Taxonomy of Illocutionary Acts**:

Searle proposed five basic categories of illocutionary acts, classified by their **illocutionary point** (the purpose of the act) and **direction of fit** (the relationship between words and world):

| Category | Illocutionary Point | Direction of Fit | Examples |
|----------|-------------------|------------------|----------|
| **Assertives** (Representatives) | Commit speaker to truth of proposition | Words-to-world (words match the world) | Stating, claiming, reporting, asserting, concluding |
| **Directives** | Get hearer to do something | World-to-words (world is changed to match words) | Requesting, commanding, advising, inviting, begging |
| **Commissives** | Commit speaker to future action | World-to-words | Promising, threatening, offering, vowing, pledging |
| **Expressives** | Express psychological state | No direction of fit | Thanking, congratulating, apologizing, welcoming, condoling |
| **Declarations** | Change reality by utterance | Both directions simultaneously | Declaring war, firing someone, pronouncing married, christening |

### Indirect Speech Acts

Searle's most influential contribution may be his analysis of **indirect speech acts** -- cases where the illocutionary force of an utterance differs from what its grammatical form would suggest:

- "Can you pass the salt?" -- Grammatically a yes/no question about ability. Functionally a request (directive).
- "I'd like you to close the door." -- Grammatically a statement about the speaker's desires. Functionally a request.
- "It's cold in here." -- Grammatically an assertion about temperature. Functionally a request to close the window or turn up the heat.

How does the hearer get from the literal meaning to the intended speech act? Searle proposed a step-by-step inferential process:

1. The hearer recognizes the literal meaning (a question about ability).
2. The hearer recognizes that the literal meaning cannot be the primary purpose (the speaker obviously knows the hearer can pass salt).
3. The hearer applies the Cooperative Principle -- the speaker must be communicating something relevant.
4. The hearer recognizes that the literal question addresses a **preparatory condition** for a request (the hearer's ability to perform the action).
5. The hearer infers that a request is being made indirectly.

Indirect speech acts are pervasive in everyday language. We rarely issue bald commands ("Pass the salt"); we prefer indirect forms that leave the hearer's autonomy intact, connecting speech act theory to politeness theory (Section 6).

## 4. Wittgenstein's Language Games

### From the Tractatus to the Investigations

Ludwig Wittgenstein (1889-1951) is unique in the history of philosophy for having produced two major, fundamentally opposed philosophical systems. His early work, the *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus* (1921), presented a "picture theory" of language: propositions are logical pictures of facts in the world, and the meaning of a sentence is the state of affairs it depicts. Language has one essential function -- to represent reality.

In his later work, *Philosophical Investigations* (published posthumously in 1953), Wittgenstein repudiated almost everything in the *Tractatus*. He argued that the picture theory captures only one of the countless things we do with language, and a relatively rare one at that. The later Wittgenstein is centrally relevant to pragmatics because he argues that **meaning is use** -- understanding a word or sentence is understanding how it functions in practice, not knowing what object or state of affairs it corresponds to.

### Meaning as Use

> "For a large class of cases -- though not for all -- in which we employ the word 'meaning' it can be defined thus: the meaning of a word is its use in the language." (*Philosophical Investigations*, section 43)

This deceptively simple formulation has radical implications:

- Meaning is not a mental image, a logical form, or a reference to an object.
- Meaning is constituted by the patterns of use in which a word participates.
- To know the meaning of a word is to know how to use it correctly in the relevant social practices.
- Different contexts of use yield different "meanings" -- and there need not be a single common essence underlying them all.

### Language Games (Sprachspiele)

Wittgenstein introduced the concept of **language games** to capture the idea that language is embedded in activities:

> "I shall also call the whole, consisting of language and the activities into which it is woven, the 'language-game'." (*PI*, section 7)

Language games are the various activities in which language plays a role -- and there are innumerably many:

- Giving orders and obeying them
- Describing the appearance of an object or giving its measurements
- Constructing an object from a description or drawing
- Reporting an event
- Speculating about an event
- Forming and testing a hypothesis
- Making up a story and reading it
- Play-acting
- Singing catches (rounds)
- Guessing riddles
- Making a joke; telling it
- Solving a problem in practical arithmetic
- Translating from one language into another
- Asking, thanking, cursing, greeting, praying

Each language game has its own rules, its own criteria for success, its own context of application. "Is there some meaning?" is not answered by pointing to what a word refers to, but by examining how the word functions within the particular language game being played.

### Family Resemblance (Familienaehnlichkeit)

Wittgenstein used the concept of **family resemblance** to challenge the classical theory of categories (which holds that category membership is defined by necessary and sufficient conditions):

> "I can think of no better expression to characterize these similarities than 'family resemblances'; for the various resemblances between members of a family: build, features, colour of eyes, gait, temperament, etc. etc. overlap and criss-cross in the same way." (*PI*, section 67)

Consider the concept "game":
- Board games, card games, ball games, Olympic games, word games, children's ring-a-ring-a-roses...
- Is there something common to all? Competition? No -- solitaire. Amusement? Not always -- professional sports. Rules? Some games have explicit rules, others have vague guidelines. Winning and losing? Not in ring-a-ring-a-roses.
- There is no single feature shared by everything we call a "game." Instead, there is a complicated network of similarities, overlapping and criss-crossing.

This insight has profound implications for pragmatics and semantics: most of our concepts do not have sharp boundaries, and meaning cannot be captured by classical definitions. Categories are held together by family resemblance, not by essences.

### Forms of Life (Lebensformen)

> "To imagine a language means to imagine a form of life." (*PI*, section 19)

Language games are embedded in broader patterns of human activity -- **forms of life**. Language only works because we share a vast number of practices, reactions, and background assumptions:

- We laugh at the same kinds of jokes.
- We comfort people in similar ways.
- We follow directions.
- We respond to pain with sympathy.
- We trust assertions unless we have reason not to.

These shared practices are the "bedrock" on which language rests. Without them, the words would be meaningless. A Martian who could decode English grammar but shared none of our forms of life would understand nothing.

### The Private Language Argument

Wittgenstein's **private language argument** (sections 243-315 of the *Investigations*) reinforces the social nature of meaning. He argues that a language whose expressions are in principle understandable only by a single individual is impossible. The famous **beetle-in-the-box** thought experiment illustrates this:

Suppose everyone has a box with something called a "beetle" inside, but no one can look in anyone else's box. Whatever is in the box plays no role in the use of the word "beetle." If everyone had something different, or if some boxes were empty, it would make no difference to how the word is used. The "beetle" as a private object "drops out of consideration as irrelevant."

The lesson: meaning is not constituted by private mental states. It is constituted by public, shared practices of use. This has direct implications for pragmatics: communication works not because speakers and hearers have identical private experiences, but because they participate in shared forms of life and language games.

## 5. Presupposition and Deixis

### Presupposition: What Is Taken for Granted

A **presupposition** is something that is assumed or taken for granted in an utterance -- a background assumption that must be true for the utterance to make sense. Unlike entailments, presuppositions survive negation.

**The classic example**:
- "Have you stopped cheating on the exam?"
  - Whether you answer "yes" or "no," you are conceding that you were cheating. The question **presupposes** that you were cheating.
  - Even the negation preserves the presupposition: "I haven't stopped cheating" still presupposes that cheating was occurring.

**Test for presupposition: Constancy under negation**:
- "The King of France is bald" presupposes that there is a King of France.
- "The King of France is not bald" also presupposes that there is a King of France.
- The presupposition (existence of the King of France) survives negation, unlike an entailment ("X is bald" entails "X has a head," but "X is not bald" does not entail "X has a head" -- wait, it does. Better example: "John managed to escape" entails "John escaped." "John didn't manage to escape" does not entail "John escaped." But "John managed to escape" presupposes "John tried to escape," and "John didn't manage to escape" also presupposes "John tried to escape.")

### Presupposition Triggers

Certain linguistic constructions systematically trigger presuppositions:

| Trigger Type | Example | Presupposition |
|-------------|---------|---------------|
| **Definite descriptions** | "The professor is late" | There exists a professor (who is identifiable) |
| **Factive verbs** | "She regrets leaving" | She left |
| **Change-of-state verbs** | "He stopped smoking" | He was smoking before |
| **Iteratives** | "She returned to Paris" | She has been to Paris before |
| **Cleft sentences** | "It was John who broke the vase" | Someone broke the vase |
| **Temporal clauses** | "Before she left, she locked the door" | She left |
| **Counterfactual conditionals** | "If I were rich, I'd travel" | I am not rich |
| **Questions** | "When did you sell your car?" | You sold your car |
| **Implicative verbs** | "He managed to open the door" | He tried to open the door |
| **Non-restrictive relative clauses** | "My sister, who lives in London, called" | I have a sister; she lives in London |

### The Projection Problem

A central question in presupposition theory is the **projection problem**: how do presuppositions behave in complex sentences? When a presupposition-triggering expression is embedded in a larger sentence (under negation, in a conditional, in a question, etc.), does the presupposition "project" to the whole sentence or not?

- "If John was smoking, he has stopped" -- the presupposition that John was smoking does NOT project to the whole sentence (it is "filtered" by the conditional).
- "Either John never smoked, or he has stopped" -- again, the presupposition is filtered.
- "John believes that the King of France is bald" -- the presupposition that there is a King of France may or may not project (depending on whether it is attributed to John or to the speaker).

### Pragmatic vs. Semantic Presupposition

There is ongoing debate about whether presupposition is fundamentally a semantic phenomenon (part of the truth-conditional content of sentences) or a pragmatic phenomenon (part of what speakers take for granted in context):

- **Semantic view** (Frege, Strawson, early formal semantics): Presuppositions are conditions that must be met for a sentence to have a truth value at all. "The King of France is bald" is neither true nor false if there is no King of France.
- **Pragmatic view** (Stalnaker, Karttunen): Presuppositions are propositions that speakers take to be part of the common ground -- the shared background knowledge of the participants. Speakers can "accommodate" new presuppositions on the fly: if I say "My sister called," and you didn't know I had a sister, you simply add that information to the common ground.

### Deixis: Where Meaning Requires a Map

**Deixis** (from Greek *deiktikos*, "pointing" or "demonstrative") refers to expressions whose meaning depends on the context of utterance -- particularly the identity of the speaker, the time and place of speaking, and the social relationships between participants.

Deictic expressions are like indexical arrows that point outward from the utterance into the world. Without knowing who is speaking, when, where, and to whom, deictic expressions have no determinate reference.

### Types of Deixis

**1. Person Deixis**:
- First person: I, me, we, us (the speaker)
- Second person: you (the addressee)
- Third person: he, she, they, it (other)
- A note written by an unknown person saying "I'll be back" is meaningless without knowing who "I" is.

**2. Spatial Deixis**:
- Proximal: this, here, these (near the speaker)
- Distal: that, there, those (far from the speaker)
- Languages differ enormously in their spatial deictic systems. Japanese has a three-way distinction (near speaker, near addressee, far from both). Some languages have even more fine-grained spatial categories.

**3. Temporal Deixis**:
- Now, then, today, yesterday, tomorrow, ago, soon, last year, next week
- Tense systems are fundamentally deictic: past, present, and future are defined relative to the moment of speaking.
- "I'll do it tomorrow" -- said on different days, this refers to different days.

**4. Discourse Deixis (Text Deixis)**:
- Reference to parts of the ongoing discourse: "this," "that," "the former," "the latter," "as I mentioned above," "the following argument."
- "This is what I mean" -- "this" refers to something in the discourse, not the physical environment.

**5. Social Deixis**:
- Linguistic encoding of social relationships: T-V distinctions (French *tu* vs. *vous*; German *du* vs. *Sie*), honorifics (Japanese *-san*, *-sama*, *-sensei*), titles, terms of address.
- Social deixis encodes power, solidarity, respect, intimacy, and social distance directly into language.

### How Context Saturates Meaning

Deixis illustrates a fundamental principle: even the most basic aspects of linguistic meaning require contextual supplementation. Consider:

> "She put it there yesterday."

This sentence is perfectly grammatical and semantically well-formed, but it communicates almost nothing without context. Who is "she"? What is "it"? Where is "there"? When is "yesterday"? The sentence is a template that context must fill. This is not a failure of language -- it is language's design. Deictic expressions allow language to be maximally flexible, reusable across infinite situations, precisely because they derive their content from context rather than encoding it.

## 6. Politeness Theory (Brown & Levinson)

### Origins

Penelope Brown (American linguist) and Stephen Levinson (British linguist/anthropologist) published their influential theory in "Universals in Language Usage: Politeness Phenomena" (1978) and expanded it in *Politeness: Some Universals in Language Usage* (1987). Building on Erving Goffman's concept of "face" (the public self-image that every member of society wants to claim for themselves), they developed a comprehensive model of how and why speakers use indirect, softened, or elaborated language.

### Face and Its Two Dimensions

Brown and Levinson define **face** as the public self-image that every competent adult member of a society claims for themselves. Face has two components:

- **Positive face**: The desire to be liked, approved of, admired, and ratified by others. The want that one's own wants, personality, and values be desirable to others. Positive face is about belonging, connection, and being seen as a worthy member of the group.

- **Negative face**: The desire to be unimpeded, to have freedom of action, and to not be imposed upon. The want that one's actions not be constrained by others. Negative face is about autonomy, territory, and personal freedom.

These wants are universal (Brown and Levinson claim), though the relative importance of each and the strategies used to protect them vary across cultures.

### Face-Threatening Acts (FTAs)

Nearly every speech act threatens someone's face:

**Threats to hearer's positive face** (threats to being liked/approved of):
- Criticism, complaints, accusations, insults, disagreements, expressions of disapproval
- "That report was poorly written."

**Threats to hearer's negative face** (threats to autonomy/freedom):
- Requests, orders, suggestions, advice, reminders, threats, warnings
- "Could you rewrite that report by Friday?"

**Threats to speaker's positive face**:
- Apologies, confessions, admissions of guilt, self-humiliation
- "I'm sorry, I made a mistake in the report."

**Threats to speaker's negative face**:
- Accepting offers, accepting thanks, making excuses, making unwilling promises
- "Yes, I suppose I can rewrite the report."

### Politeness Strategies

Brown and Levinson identify five super-strategies for performing face-threatening acts, ranked from most to least face-threatening:

**Strategy 1: Bald on Record (without redress)**
- Perform the FTA directly, clearly, unambiguously, and without softening.
- "Close the window." / "You're wrong."
- Used when: efficiency is paramount, the threat is small, or the speaker has much greater power. Also in emergencies ("Watch out!").

**Strategy 2: Positive Politeness (on record with positive redress)**
- Attend to the hearer's positive face -- show interest, approval, solidarity.
- "That's a great shirt! Hey, could you close the window?"
- Strategies include: claim common ground, convey that S and H are cooperators, fulfill H's want for something.
- Markers: in-group identity markers, compliments, jokes, first names, expressions of interest.

**Strategy 3: Negative Politeness (on record with negative redress)**
- Attend to the hearer's negative face -- minimize imposition, show deference.
- "I'm sorry to bother you, but would you mind closing the window?"
- Strategies include: be indirect, question/hedge, be pessimistic, minimize imposition, give deference, apologize, impersonalize.
- This is the most elaborated and best-studied strategy in Western politeness.

**Strategy 4: Off Record (indirect)**
- Perform the FTA indirectly, using hints, understatement, irony, metaphor, rhetorical questions, ambiguity.
- "It's cold in here." (hint: close the window) / "Someone left the window open." (avoid naming the responsible party)
- The speaker can plausibly deny the intended interpretation.

**Strategy 5: Don't Do the FTA**
- Decide that the face threat is too great and simply do not perform the act.

### Factors Determining Strategy Choice

Brown and Levinson propose that speakers calculate the "weightiness" of an FTA based on three sociological variables:

- **D (social Distance)**: The degree of familiarity between speaker and hearer.
- **P (relative Power)**: The power of the hearer relative to the speaker.
- **R (Rank of imposition)**: The degree to which the act is considered an imposition in a particular culture.

**W(FTA) = D(S,H) + P(H,S) + R(x)**

Higher weightiness leads to more elaborate politeness strategies (or avoidance of the act altogether).

### Cross-Cultural Critiques

Brown and Levinson's theory has been influential but heavily criticized for Western bias:

- **Japanese scholars** (Matsumoto 1988, Ide 1989) argued that the concept of negative face (individual autonomy) does not apply well to Japanese culture, where politeness is more about maintaining proper social relations and group harmony than protecting individual freedom. Japanese honorific systems encode social hierarchy directly and are obligatory -- they do not represent "strategic" choices in the Brown-Levinson sense.

- **Chinese scholars** have pointed out that the concept of "face" (*mianzi* and *lian*) in Chinese culture operates differently -- *mianzi* is about social reputation and prestige (closer to positive face), while *lian* is about moral character. The two-dimensional model oversimplifies this.

- **Discursive politeness** approaches (Watts 2003, Locher & Watts 2005) reject the idea that politeness can be analyzed independently of interaction. What counts as "polite" is negotiated in real time between participants and cannot be predicted by formula.

- **Rapport Management** (Spencer-Oatey 2000, 2008) proposed an expanded model with multiple face dimensions (quality face, social identity face, equity rights, association rights) to better capture cross-cultural variation.

Despite these critiques, Brown and Levinson's model remains the most influential framework in politeness research and has generated an enormous body of cross-linguistic and cross-cultural empirical work.

### Politeness and Implicature

Politeness is one of the primary motivations for conversational implicature. Speakers routinely flout Grice's maxims for politeness reasons:

- Flouting Quantity: Giving less information than required to avoid giving bad news directly.
- Flouting Manner: Being deliberately vague or indirect to minimize face threat.
- Flouting Quality: Using ironic understatement or exaggeration to soften criticism.

The relationship between politeness and implicature explains why human language is so pervasively indirect. Directness is efficient but threatening; indirectness sacrifices efficiency for face-preservation. This trade-off between communicative efficiency and social harmony is one of the deepest tensions in human communication.

## 7. Irony, Sarcasm, and Non-Literal Meaning

### The Puzzle of Non-Literal Meaning

Non-literal meaning -- metaphor, irony, sarcasm, hyperbole, understatement, idiom -- poses a fundamental challenge to theories of communication. If the literal meaning of "What a lovely day" is a positive evaluation of the weather, how do speakers use it to communicate the opposite when it is raining?

The traditional Gricean account treats irony as a flouting of the Maxim of Quality: the speaker says something obviously false, and the hearer, assuming cooperation, infers that the speaker means the opposite. But this account has been criticized as too simple -- irony does not always involve saying the opposite of what is meant, and the emotional/attitudinal component of irony (mockery, bitterness, amusement) is left unexplained.

### The Echoic Account of Irony (Sperber & Wilson)

In Relevance Theory, irony is not a matter of saying the opposite of what is meant. It is a matter of **echoic use**: the speaker echoes an attributed thought, utterance, or expectation and expresses a **dissociative attitude** toward it.

**How it works**:
1. The speaker makes an utterance that echoes or alludes to a thought, expectation, norm, or previous utterance.
2. The utterance is presented not as the speaker's own view but as something attributed to someone else (or to a cultural norm, a general expectation, etc.).
3. The speaker expresses a range of dissociative attitudes toward the echoed content: mockery, skepticism, bitterness, wry amusement, scorn.

**Examples**:
- After a friend drops and breaks a vase: "What graceful handling." -- Echoes the expectation/hope that the friend would be careful. Dissociative attitude: mockery.
- After a terrible meal at an expensive restaurant: "Well, that was worth every penny." -- Echoes the restaurant's implicit promise of quality proportional to price. Dissociative attitude: bitter sarcasm.
- Weather forecast predicts sunshine; it pours: "What lovely weather we're having!" -- Echoes the forecast. Dissociative attitude: wry amusement.

**Why the echoic account is superior to the "opposite meaning" view**:
- Not all irony involves the opposite of what is meant. "That was a fine thing to do" (said of a social blunder) does not simply mean "That was a bad thing to do" -- it conveys mockery, reproach, and perhaps exasperation, none of which are captured by simple negation.
- The echoic account explains the emotional/attitudinal component: irony always conveys an attitude (not just a reversed proposition).
- It explains why irony requires an identifiable "target" -- something being echoed and mocked.

### The Pretense Account of Irony

An alternative to the echoic account, associated with Herbert Clark and Richard Gerrig (1984), treats irony as **pretense**: the speaker "makes as if" to perform a speech act (e.g., praising the weather), expecting the hearer to see through the pretense and recognize the mocking attitude behind it. The speaker does not actually assert that the weather is lovely -- they pretend to, and the audience is invited to recognize the pretense.

### Sarcasm

Sarcasm is often treated as a type of irony with a particularly sharp, often personal, mocking edge. While irony can be gentle or playful, sarcasm is typically directed at a person and is intended to wound or criticize. Some researchers distinguish sarcasm from irony by its intentional hostility, while others treat sarcasm as simply "bitter irony."

### Scalar Implicature

**Scalar implicature** is a specific type of generalized conversational implicature based on informational scales. When a speaker uses a weaker term from an ordered scale, the hearer infers that the speaker is not in a position to use a stronger term:

**Horn Scales** (named after Laurence Horn, who formalized the concept):
- \<all, most, many, some\> -- saying "some" implicates "not all"
- \<and, or\> -- saying "or" implicates "not and" (i.e., not both)
- \<always, often, sometimes\> -- saying "sometimes" implicates "not always"
- \<certain, probable, possible\> -- saying "possible" implicates "not certain"
- \<hot, warm\> -- saying "warm" implicates "not hot"
- \<love, like\> -- saying "I like him" may implicate "I don't love him"
- \<n, ..., 5, 4, 3, 2, 1\> -- saying "I ate three cookies" implicates "not more than three"

**The mechanism**: The Maxim of Quantity requires speakers to be as informative as required. If the speaker could have truthfully used a stronger term (e.g., "all" instead of "some"), and didn't, the hearer infers that the stronger term does not apply.

**Example**:
- "Some students passed the exam."
- Literal meaning: At least some students passed (compatible with all passing).
- Scalar implicature: Not all students passed.
- The speaker, being cooperative and informative, would have said "all" if all had passed. Since they didn't, the hearer infers that not all did.

**Key properties of scalar implicatures**:
- They are **cancelable**: "Some students passed -- in fact, all of them did."
- They are **reinforceable**: "Some, but not all, students passed."
- They arise from the Maxim of Quantity and general assumptions about cooperation.
- They are **generalized** (arising regardless of context, though context can cancel them).

### Other Non-Literal Language

**Hyperbole**: "I've told you a million times" -- exaggeration for emphasis. Not literally false in the way irony is; rather, the literal proposition is obviously too strong, and the hearer scales it down.

**Understatement (Litotes)**: "It's not the worst idea" -- saying less than is meant. "She's not unattractive" implicates she is quite attractive.

**Rhetorical questions**: "Who cares?" -- a question form used to make an assertion (nobody cares). "Isn't it obvious?" -- a question form used to assert that it is obvious.

## 8. The Fundamental Pattern: Communication as Inference

### The Underdetermination Thesis

The central insight of pragmatics can be stated as a thesis:

> **The linguistic meaning of an utterance massively underdetermines what the speaker communicates.**

Consider this simple exchange:

> A: "Are you going to the party tonight?"
> B: "I have to work."

To understand B's response, the hearer must:
1. **Resolve deixis**: Who is "I"? (B). When is "tonight"? (the evening of the day of utterance).
2. **Enrich the content**: "Have to work" means something like "am required by professional obligations to be at my place of employment performing work tasks during the time of the party."
3. **Derive the implicature**: B is not going to the party. The reason is that work conflicts with the party. B may also be implicating that they would go if they could (expressing regret), or that they would rather work than go (expressing disinterest), depending on tone and context.
4. **Assess illocutionary force**: B's utterance functions as a refusal of an implicit invitation, an explanation, and perhaps an apology -- none of which are encoded in the words "I have to work."

None of this is encoded in the literal meaning of "I have to work." The sentence, taken in isolation, says nothing about parties. Yet every competent speaker of English processes all of this effortlessly and instantly.

### The Architecture of Pragmatic Inference

Pragmatic meaning involves multiple interacting systems:

1. **Linguistic decoding**: Processing phonology, morphology, syntax, and compositional semantics to obtain a logical form (the "skeleton").
2. **Reference resolution**: Assigning referents to pronouns, demonstratives, definite descriptions.
3. **Disambiguation**: Selecting the relevant sense of ambiguous words.
4. **Enrichment**: Fleshing out underspecified content (domain restriction, temporal specification, etc.).
5. **Illocutionary force assignment**: Determining what speech act is being performed.
6. **Implicature derivation**: Inferring what is communicated beyond what is said.
7. **Presupposition accommodation**: Accepting or challenging background assumptions.
8. **Social/relational inference**: Calculating politeness, power dynamics, solidarity.

All of this happens in real time, typically in hundreds of milliseconds. Hearers are not conscious of most of this processing -- it feels effortless and automatic.

### Why Language Is Designed This Way

The massive gap between what is said and what is communicated is not a deficiency of language -- it is an extraordinarily efficient design:

- **Economy**: Speakers do not need to spell everything out. Shared knowledge, context, and inference do the heavy lifting, allowing utterances to be short and efficient.
- **Flexibility**: The same sentence can communicate different things in different contexts, making language infinitely reusable.
- **Social navigation**: Indirectness allows speakers to communicate sensitive content while maintaining deniability, preserving face, and managing social relationships.
- **Creativity**: The inferential nature of communication allows for humor, irony, metaphor, poetry, and all forms of creative language use.

### Computational Pragmatics and AI

Recent research (2024-2025) has explored how large language models (LLMs) handle pragmatic phenomena. Key findings include:

- LLMs demonstrate emerging capabilities in pragmatic tasks including implicature resolution and theory-of-mind reasoning, though they remain imperfect.
- Base models show sensitivity to pragmatic cues that improves with model scale.
- Supervised fine-tuning and reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) provide further gains in cognitive-pragmatic scenarios.
- Evaluating pragmatic competence in AI remains challenging because pragmatic evaluation requires rich, contextually grounded datasets that are difficult to construct at scale.
- The gap between human pragmatic competence and machine pragmatic competence illustrates just how sophisticated the human pragmatic system is -- we effortlessly perform inferences that remain difficult for the most powerful AI systems.

## Connections Across Pragmatic Theories

### The Common Thread

Despite their different frameworks and emphases, all pragmatic theories converge on a single fundamental insight: **what speakers communicate systematically exceeds what their words literally encode**. The theories differ in how they model this excess:

| Theory | How Meaning Exceeds Literal Content |
|--------|-------------------------------------|
| Grice | Flouting maxims generates calculable implicatures |
| Relevance Theory | Hearers seek optimal relevance, enriching beyond code |
| Speech Act Theory | Utterances perform actions beyond describing |
| Wittgenstein | Meaning is constituted by use in language games |
| Presupposition | Background assumptions are smuggled in with assertions |
| Deixis | Context saturates indexical expressions |
| Politeness Theory | Social face motivates systematic indirectness |
| Irony/Sarcasm | Echoing and attitude dissociation invert surface meaning |

### Points of Integration

1. **Grice and Relevance Theory**: Relevance Theory can be seen as a refinement of Grice -- replacing four maxims with one principle and providing a psychologically realistic processing model. But it also departs significantly: it treats the semantics/pragmatics boundary as more fluid (via explicatures) and gives a more prominent role to cognitive effort in interpretation.

2. **Speech Acts and Politeness**: Indirect speech acts are often motivated by politeness. "Can you pass the salt?" is a question form performing a request precisely because directness ("Pass the salt") threatens negative face. Brown and Levinson's off-record strategy is essentially a systematic deployment of indirect speech acts.

3. **Wittgenstein and Grice**: Wittgenstein's "meaning as use" and Grice's "meaning as intention" represent different (perhaps complementary) perspectives. Wittgenstein focuses on the social/institutional background that makes communication possible; Grice focuses on the inferential mechanisms that operate against this background.

4. **Presupposition and Politeness**: Presuppositions can be deployed strategically for politeness or manipulation. "When would you like to schedule the follow-up appointment?" presupposes that a follow-up is happening, making it harder to refuse.

5. **Scalar Implicature and Relevance**: The mechanism of scalar implicature (using a weaker term implicates the stronger doesn't hold) is naturally explained by Relevance Theory: the speaker would have used the stronger term if it were true, because it would be more relevant (more informative for the same effort).

## Key Terms Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Pragmatics** | The study of how context contributes to meaning beyond what is linguistically encoded |
| **Cooperative Principle** | Grice's principle that speakers cooperate by making contributions appropriate to the purpose of the conversation |
| **Conversational implicature** | Meaning that is inferred (not stated) through the interaction of what is said with the Cooperative Principle |
| **Flouting** | Conspicuously failing to observe a maxim in order to generate an implicature |
| **Violating** | Secretly failing to observe a maxim (e.g., lying -- violating Quality without the hearer realizing) |
| **Relevance** | A property of inputs defined by the ratio of cognitive effects to processing effort |
| **Explicature** | An explicitly communicated proposition, arrived at by decoding plus pragmatic enrichment |
| **Implicature** | An implicitly communicated proposition, derived wholly through inference |
| **Ostensive-inferential communication** | Communication through making intentions manifest (ostension) and having them inferred |
| **Locutionary act** | The act of saying something meaningful |
| **Illocutionary act** | The act performed IN saying something (asserting, requesting, promising, etc.) |
| **Perlocutionary act** | The effect achieved BY saying something (persuading, frightening, amusing) |
| **Performative** | An utterance that performs an action by being uttered ("I promise," "I apologize") |
| **Felicity conditions** | Conditions that must be met for a speech act to succeed |
| **Language game** | An activity in which language plays a role, governed by its own rules (Wittgenstein) |
| **Family resemblance** | A mode of categorization where members share overlapping similarities without any single common feature |
| **Form of life** | The broader pattern of human activity in which language games are embedded |
| **Presupposition** | A background assumption taken for granted in an utterance, surviving negation |
| **Presupposition trigger** | A linguistic construction that introduces a presupposition |
| **Accommodation** | The process by which hearers accept new presuppositions into the common ground |
| **Deixis** | Context-dependent reference (person, place, time, discourse, social) |
| **Positive face** | The desire to be liked, approved of, and ratified |
| **Negative face** | The desire not to be imposed upon, to have freedom of action |
| **Face-threatening act (FTA)** | A speech act that threatens the face of the speaker or hearer |
| **Scalar implicature** | An implicature arising from the use of a weaker term on an informational scale |
| **Horn scale** | An ordered set of linguistic expressions where use of a weaker member implicates the stronger does not apply |
| **Echoic use** | Using language to echo an attributed thought or utterance while expressing an attitude toward it |
| **Underdetermination** | The principle that linguistic meaning does not fully determine what is communicated |

- A: "Are you going to the party tonight?"
- B: "I have to work."

Steps:
1. Decode linguistic meaning: B is obligated to perform labor
2. Resolve deixis: "I" = B, "tonight" = evening of utterance date
3. Apply Cooperative Principle: B's response must be relevant to A's question
4. Derive explicature: B has a work obligation that overlaps with party time
5. Derive implicature 1: B will not go to the party (via Relation)
6. Derive implicature 2: Work prevents attendance (via Quantity -- this is the reason)

8. Assess illocutionary force: Indirect refusal + explanation + (possible apology)
