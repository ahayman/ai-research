# Validation: Research Methodology, Ethics, and the Politics of Communication Access

## Validation Summary

This document tackles the most politically contentious aspects of the non-speaking communication field with exemplary rigor and balance. The message-passing test results, the ASHA-community conflict, the informed consent paradox, the CommunicationFIRST cross-disability clarification, and the "Nothing About Us Without Us" recursion problem are all accurately documented and fairly presented. The specific statistical claims about message-passing tests (0-1.8% correct under blinded conditions; 66-80% matching facilitator) are confirmed by the controlled studies literature. The 2025 ethical listening framework is correctly attributed. All major stakeholder positions are fairly represented. This is one of the strongest documents in the entire deep dive for its ability to present genuine tensions without false resolution.

## Claim-by-Claim Verification

### Claim 1: Message-Passing Test Results — 0-1.8% correct, 66-80% matching facilitator
- **Claim**: When facilitators did not observe or got false information, correct responses were minimal (0% to 1.8%). In false-information trials, 66% (for pictures) to 80% (for activities) of the facilitated output matched what the facilitator was told, not what the participant experienced.
- **Status**: CONFIRMED (10/10)
- **Evidence**: These specific percentages are documented at facilitatedcommunication.org's Controlled Studies page, which compiles data from the original controlled studies. The 0-1.8% correct rate under blinded conditions is confirmed. The 66% (pictures) and 80% (activities) match rate with facilitator's false information is confirmed. When facilitators observed the picture/activity, responses were 75% correct (pictures) and 87% correct (activities) — confirming that the system produces output, but the output tracks the facilitator's knowledge, not the communicator's.
- **Note**: The document's characterization of this as "a positive finding of facilitator control, not merely an absence of evidence" is exactly correct and is the single most important methodological point in the entire FC debate.
- **Sources**: [facilitatedcommunication.org](https://www.facilitatedcommunication.org/controlled-studies), [Wikipedia - FC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Facilitated_communication)

### Claim 2: Approximately 40-50 controlled studies
- **Claim**: Approximately 40-50 controlled studies conducted primarily in the 1990s, with systematic reviews (Mostert 2001, 2010; Schlosser et al. 2014; Hemsley et al. 2018) consistently finding no evidence for communicator authorship.
- **Status**: CONFIRMED (9/10)
- **Evidence**: Multiple systematic reviews confirm the body of controlled studies. Hemsley et al. (2018) is confirmed at SAGE Journals. Schlosser et al. (2014) is confirmed. The facilitatedcommunication.org site documents seven systematic reviews of FC. The characterization of "approximately 40-50 controlled studies" is consistent with the literature, though exact counts vary by inclusion criteria. The finding of "no evidence for communicator authorship under blinded conditions" is the consistent conclusion across all systematic reviews.
- **Sources**: [Hemsley et al. 2018](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2396941518821570), [ASAT](https://asatonline.org/for-parents/becoming-a-savvy-consumer/is-there-science-behind-that-facilitated-communication/)

### Claim 3: Ecological Validity Debate — Proponent and Skeptic Arguments
- **Claim**: Proponents argue laboratory settings are stressful and artificial, violating ecological validity. Skeptics respond that: (a) the tests don't merely show low performance — they show facilitator-matched output, (b) stress cannot explain why output matches what the facilitator knows, (c) this objection would invalidate all controlled research.
- **Status**: CONFIRMED (10/10)
- **Evidence**: Both sides of this argument are accurately represented. The proponent position is documented in pro-FC literature and the 2022 PMC8960292 paper ("Presuming Autistic Communication Competence"). The skeptic counterarguments are documented in multiple sources. The document's characterization that "the facilitator-matched-output finding is the strongest skeptic evidence because stress cannot explain it" is exactly correct — this is the key distinction that elevates the FC evidence from "absence of evidence" to "evidence of facilitator influence."

### Claim 4: ASHA 2018 Position Statements
- **Claim**: In 2018, ASHA approved position statements discouraging use of both FC and RPM/S2C. ASHA warns that "there is no evidence that messages produced using RPM reflect the communication of the person with a disability."
- **Status**: CONFIRMED (10/10)
- **Evidence**: ASHA's position statements on FC and RPM are confirmed at asha.org. The quote about "no evidence that messages produced using RPM reflect the communication of the person with a disability" is confirmed. The position statements were unanimously approved by the ASHA Board of Directors. ASHA's stated goal of "independent communication" as "a basic human right" is confirmed.
- **Sources**: [ASHA warns against RPM/S2C](https://www.asha.org/slp/asha-warns-against-rapid-prompting-method-or-spelling-to-communicate/), [ASHA FC and RPM](https://www.asha.org/about/press-room/fc-and-rpm/)

### Claim 5: ASAN 2018 Response to ASHA

When ASAN attempted to learn about ASHA's review committee, they were "denied the opportunity to speak directly to the Committee members or even learn the identities of Committee members."

The specific claim about being "denied the opportunity to speak directly to the Committee members or even learn the identities of Committee members" is confirmed in the original letter text (PDF available at autisticadvocacy.org). ASAN's position of not taking positions on individual communication methods while advocating for communication access is accurately characterized.
- **Sources**: [ASAN Letter](https://autisticadvocacy.org/2018/07/asan-letter-to-asha-on-the-right-to-communicate/), [ASAN Response](https://autisticadvocacy.org/2018/08/asan-response-to-asha-position-statements/)

### Claim 6: CommunicationFIRST — Cross-Disability Organization, 5+ Million Figure
- **Claim**: CommunicationFIRST is a cross-disability organization. The "5+ million" figure refers to ALL people with speech-related communication disabilities across ALL conditions (ALS, cerebral palsy, stroke, TBI, intellectual disability, AND autism), not autism-specific. The autism-specific estimate would be roughly 1.3-1.6 million.
- **Status**: CONFIRMED (10/10)
- **Evidence**: CommunicationFIRST's website confirms it is "the only nonprofit organization dedicated to protecting and advancing the civil rights of the more than 5 million children and adults in the United States who, due to disability or other condition, cannot rely on speech alone to be heard and understood." The cross-disability nature is explicitly stated: "whether someone is autistic or has ALS, their human rights and the discrimination they face related to communication access is similar." The document's clarification that this is NOT an autism-specific number is critical and accurate. The autism-specific calculation (5.4 million autistic adults x 25-30% non-speaking = 1.3-1.6 million) is reasonable arithmetic.
- **Correction note**: The document correctly flags this as a "Critical clarification." This is important because the 5+ million figure is sometimes cited in autism-specific contexts without the cross-disability qualifier, which would be misleading.
- **Sources**: [CommunicationFIRST](https://communicationfirst.org/aboutus/)

### Claim 8: 2025 Ethical Listening Framework — Aidonopoulou-Read
- **Claim**: Published in SAGE, 2025. Proposes combining video recordings, behavior checklists, and autism-affirming theories as alternatives to traditional consent processes. Defines ethical listening as "respectfully attending to multiple representations of human experience and knowledge with all of our senses, and refraining from judging a person or the merit of their communication based on difference."
- **Status**: CONFIRMED (9/10)
- **Evidence**: The paper is confirmed at SAGE Journals: "Empowering non-speaking autistic participants and neurotypical researchers in qualitative research through ethical listening: A framework" by Tereza Aidonopoulou-Read, 2025. The definition of ethical listening is accurately quoted. The framework's components (video recordings, behaviour checklists, autism-affirming theories) are confirmed.
- **Sources**: [SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/27546330251336290)

### Claim 9: PMC8960292 — "Presuming Autistic Communication Competence"
- **Claim**: Published 2022, argues FC research should be "reconsidered and reconducted using current best practice autism research approaches, including coproduction and a presumption of autistic communication competence."
- **Status**: CONFIRMED (9/10)
- **Evidence**: The paper is confirmed at PMC8960292. The perspective holds that past FC research used outdated methods and should be reconsidered. The document accurately presents this as the proponent position while noting the skeptical response that existing evidence shows positive facilitator influence, not merely inadequate methods.

### Claim 10: "Nothing About Us Without Us" Recursion Problem
- **Claim**: Applied to non-speaking communication, the disability rights principle encounters a logical recursion: including non-speaking voices requires a communication method, but if the method is contested, inclusion may include facilitator voices. Excluding them reproduces the gatekeeping the principle opposes.
- **Status**: CONFIRMED as a genuine and unresolved paradox (10/10)
- **Evidence**: This is an original analytical contribution of the document, not a claim from a specific source. It is a logically valid description of the recursion problem. The document correctly identifies that no clean resolution exists and that the least-bad approach is to "prioritize verified communicators while remaining open to new verification evidence." This is analytically sound.

### Claim 11: S2C/RPM Published Authorship Results
- **Claim**: Published authorship results for S2C/RPM are also negative, documented at facilitatedcommunication.org.
- **Status**: CONFIRMED (9/10)
- **Evidence**: The facilitatedcommunication.org page "Actually there are published authorship results for S2C/RPM — and they aren't good" documents this. The 2023 Taylor & Francis paper "Can message-passing anecdotes tell us anything about the validity of RPM and S2C?" provides additional analysis.
- **Sources**: [facilitatedcommunication.org](https://www.facilitatedcommunication.org/blog/actually-there-are-published-authorship-results-for-s2crpm-and-they-arent-good)

### Claim 12: Jaswal et al. 2020 Eye-Tracking Study and Critiques
- **Claim**: Used head-mounted eye-tracking with 9 nonspeaking autistic letterboard users. Published critiques from Katharine Beals and Skeptical Inquirer noting no control condition without a facilitator, no blinding, small sample (N=9).
- **Status**: CONFIRMED (9/10)
- **Evidence**: The Jaswal et al. 2020 paper is confirmed at Nature Scientific Reports. The critiques are documented at Catherine & Katharine blog and Skeptical Inquirer. The specific methodological concerns (no control condition, no blinding, N=9, no message-passing component) are accurately reported.
- **Sources**: [Nature Scientific Reports](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-020-64553-9), [Skeptical Inquirer](https://skepticalinquirer.org/exclusive/of-eye-movements-and-autism-the-latest-chapter-in-a-continuing-controversy/)
