# Requirements Engineering and Feasibility Assessment: Can This Actually Work?

The Capstone

---

## 1. The Question That Matters

After eleven categories of research -- historical systems, theoretical frameworks, cognitive science, existing AAC deployment, sign language grammar, Chinese radical composition, image schemas, semantic primitives, and the neurological case for autistic visual-spatial processing -- the only question that matters is practical.

Can a visual iconographic language actually be built, learned, and used?

The answer is not yes or no. It is a map of what is achievable, what requires research, and what may be impossible. Honest feasibility assessment requires separating these three categories and resisting the temptation to collapse them into a single optimistic or pessimistic verdict.

This document is the capstone of the deep dive. It synthesizes everything. And its most important contribution is honesty about what may not work.

### Validation Summary

This section draws on validated research with an overall confidence rating of 8/10. The three-tier feasibility framework is confirmed as logically sound. Historical adoption precedents are accurately characterized. Learnability targets are reasonable extrapolations but explicitly flagged as empirically untested. The central limitation -- no semasiographic system has ever achieved full natural-language-level expressiveness -- is confirmed and acknowledged throughout.

## 2. The Theoretical Foundation: Stronger Than You Might Think

The project is not speculation from a single angle. It draws on multiple converging evidence bases:

**Natural Semantic Metalanguage** provides 65 empirically validated semantic primes -- concepts that appear universal across all known human languages. These are not hypothetical. They have been tested across dozens of typologically diverse languages. They provide a candidate foundation for the core symbol inventory: I, YOU, THIS, SOMETHING, WANT, DO, GOOD, BAD, BIG, SMALL, THINK, KNOW, MOVE, LIVE, and so on.

**Image schema theory** provides a vocabulary of spatial-relational primitives grounded in pre-linguistic embodied experience. CONTAINER (inside/outside, boundaries), PATH (source, trajectory, goal), FORCE (push, pull, resistance), BALANCE (equilibrium), LINK (connection), CENTER-PERIPHERY, CYCLE, UP-DOWN, NEAR-FAR. These are not abstract philosophical constructs. They arise from how human bodies interact with the physical world -- experiences shared by all humans regardless of language, culture, or neurological profile.

**Conceptual Metaphor Theory** maps how these primitives systematically structure abstract thought. MORE IS UP (prices rise, moods sink). KNOWING IS SEEING (I see what you mean). TIME IS MOTION (time flies, the deadline approaches). ARGUMENT IS WAR (she attacked his position). These mappings are not arbitrary -- they are grounded in correlations between bodily experience and abstract domains.

**The Diagrammatic Image Schema Language (DISL, 2024)** has begun formalizing visual representations of image schema primitives, providing a starting point for symbol design.

**Sign languages** prove that full linguistic expressiveness is achievable in the visual-spatial modality. Every concept expressible in spoken language is expressible in sign. This is an existence proof: visual-spatial encoding is not inherently limited.

**Chinese character composition** demonstrates that a finite set of visual building blocks (214 radicals) can generate an open-ended vocabulary (tens of thousands of characters). The mechanism -- semantic classification through shared visual components -- has sustained a writing system for over three millennia.

**Cognitive science** confirms that visual symbols enjoy memory advantages over verbal labels, that the Visual Word Form Area (VWFA) repurposes visual processing circuitry for reading, and that autistic visual-spatial thinkers -- the primary intended user population -- demonstrate enhanced perceptual processing.

None of these foundations is individually sufficient. Together, they provide a convergent theoretical case that is stronger than any previous visual language attempt has had.

## 3. The Three Tiers of Feasibility

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-1-feasibility-pyramid.html -->

### Tier 1: Achievable Now

**What**: Basic functional communication -- requests, refusals, object identification, simple actions, basic spatial and temporal relations, emotions, questions, social exchange. Approximately 200-400 concepts covering core vocabulary.

**Why it is achievable**: Every capability in Tier 1 has been demonstrated by existing systems. Makaton's 450 core concepts achieve this level. PECS enables basic requesting within days. Blissymbolics' compositional approach works for this range of expressiveness. The difference for a visual iconographic language would be in the grounding mechanism (image schemas and primary metaphors rather than arbitrary symbols), not in the expressiveness level.

**What it looks like**: A core symbol set of 65-100 base symbols (grounded in image schemas and NSM primes), combinable through spatial composition rules into 200-400+ composed concepts. A user could express: "I want to go home," "The food is too hot," "I feel sad," "Where is the book?" "Thank you," "Not that one."

**What it does not include**: Complex conditionals ("If it rains, I will stay home"), causal reasoning ("Because she was tired, she left early"), embedded clauses ("The person who called yesterday wants to visit"), counterfactuals ("If I had known, I would have come").

### Tier 2: Requires Research

**What**: Extended expressiveness -- negation of complex propositions, conditional statements, temporal complexity, causation, quantification, relative clauses, modality (can/must/might/should), reported speech.

**Why it requires research**: Each of these capabilities involves grammatical structures that natural languages encode through word order, morphology, or prosody -- mechanisms not directly available in a 2D static visual system. Sign languages achieve all of these, but they exploit temporal sequencing and 3D spatial arrangement that a screen-based system cannot directly replicate.

The central engineering challenge: adapting sign language's spatial grammar principles to 2D static form. Sign languages use spatial positioning (placing referents at different locations in signing space), spatial movement (showing relationships through motion), and non-manual markers (facial expressions, body posture) to encode grammar. A 2D visual language must achieve equivalent grammatical power through spatial arrangement on a flat surface, visual operators (overlays, enclosures, directional markers), and perhaps animation or interaction.

**Evidence that it is plausible**: Mathematical notation, circuit diagrams, musical score, and Chinese characters all achieve compositional expressiveness in 2D. None of these is a full natural language, but they demonstrate that 2D visual compositionality can encode complex structural relationships. The question is whether the techniques can scale to natural-language-level grammar.

**Estimated timeline**: 2-5 years of dedicated design research, user testing, and iterative refinement. The timeline is driven less by the design challenge (which is substantial but tractable) than by the testing requirement: each grammatical construction must be validated with real users to confirm that it is learnable, interpretable, and practically usable.

**The sign language bridge**: The most promising approach for Tier 2 is adapting sign language spatial grammar principles for 2D form. Sign languages handle conditionals (raised eyebrows for the "if" clause, neutral expression for the "then" clause), negation (headshake overlaid on the signed proposition), relative clauses (placing referents at specific locations in signing space, then pointing back to them), and quantification (sweeping movements across locations to indicate "all"). Each of these has a potential 2D analogue: visual markers for conditional scope, visual negation operators (strike-through, reversal), spatial reference zones, and visual quantifiers. Whether these 2D adaptations preserve the clarity and learnability of their 3D sources is the open research question.

### Tier 3: May Be Impossible

**What**: Full natural language equivalence -- recursive embedding, counterfactual conditionals, precise technical and legal language, literary and poetic expression, humor, irony, sarcasm.

**Why it may be impossible**: No semasiographic system -- no writing that represents meaning directly without encoding the sounds of a spoken language -- has ever achieved full natural language expressiveness. This is not for lack of trying. Blissymbolics attempted it. Philosophical languages from Wilkins (1668) to Leibniz to Ro attempted it. None succeeded.

Sign languages achieve full expressiveness, but they are not semasiographic -- they use the visual-spatial modality but encode specific spoken languages' grammar (ASL has its own grammar distinct from English; BSL has its own distinct from ASL). And critically, sign languages exploit the temporal dimension (signs unfold in time) and 3D space (referents are placed in space around the signer) -- affordances that a 2D static system does not have.

Recursive embedding -- "the dog that the cat that the boy owned chased ran away" -- may be fundamentally resistant to transparent visual representation. Humor and irony depend on violating expectations in ways that are culturally and linguistically specific. Literary expression depends on ambiguity, connotation, rhythm, and sound -- dimensions that a visual system represents differently, if at all.

**Honest assessment**: Tier 3 is an open question, not a design target. Partial achievement may be possible for some capabilities (limited recursive structures, some forms of abstraction). Full equivalence with natural language in a 2D semasiographic system is unproven and may be impossible. The design should aim for Tier 1 and 2, not Tier 3.

## 4. The Learnability-Expressiveness Trade-Off

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-2-learnability-expressiveness-tradeoff.html -->

This is the inescapable design tension. A system simple enough to learn in a week (like basic emoji) lacks the expressiveness for genuine communication. A system expressive enough for complex thought (like Chinese characters) takes years to learn. Every communication system in history occupies a specific position on this trade-off.

**Existing benchmarks**:
- Emoji: learnable in hours, expressiveness near zero for structured communication
- PECS Phase 1: achievable in hours, expressiveness limited to basic requesting
- Makaton core (450 concepts): weeks to months, functional daily communication
- Blissymbolics base (~120 elements): months, compositional use develops over years
- ASL conversational fluency: approximately 60-100 hours of instruction
- Chinese character literacy (3,000 characters): 4-5 years of dedicated study

**Target position**: The visual iconographic language aims for approximately 2-4 weeks to learn 65-100 base symbols to recognition level; 2-6 months for functional communication with 200-400 concepts; 6-12 months for basic compositional fluency.

**The compositionality hypothesis**: These targets assume that image-schema grounding makes base symbols more learnable than arbitrary shapes (because they map to pre-linguistic knowledge), and that systematic compositionality enables vocabulary expansion without proportional learning time (because compositional rules, once learned, generate new meanings from known parts).

**Honesty check**: These learnability targets are projections, not empirical data. No image-schema-based visual language has been tested with any learner population. All learning-time estimates are extrapolated from existing systems (Makaton, Blissymbolics, Chinese characters, ASL) and may not apply. The compositionality hypothesis -- that grounded primitives shift the trade-off curve -- is theoretically motivated but empirically untested.

There is a specific way this hypothesis could fail. If image-schema grounding does not actually make symbols more learnable -- if, for instance, the abstract nature of spatial-relational concepts (CONTAINMENT, PATH, FORCE) makes their visual representations as hard to learn as Blissymbolics' arbitrary geometric shapes -- then the visual language would land on the same trade-off curve as existing systems, not a shifted one. It would be "Blissymbolics with different base shapes" rather than a fundamentally more learnable compositional system. This is a real possibility, and the only way to rule it out is empirical testing.

Conversely, there are reasons for cautious optimism. Image schemas are pre-linguistic -- they arise from embodied experience before language acquisition. If the visual symbols activate the same cognitive structures that the schemas themselves describe (CONTAINER looks like containment; PATH looks like a trajectory from source to goal), then recognition should be facilitated by existing neural representations. This would not be learning new arbitrary associations but connecting visual symbols to pre-existing conceptual structures. But "should" is not "does," and until the symbols exist and are tested, this remains hypothesis.

This is the single most important research gap in the entire project. Until the core symbol set is designed and tested with actual learners, every timeline estimate is informed speculation. The honest path forward is: design the symbols, build the prototype, test with users, measure, and adjust expectations based on evidence rather than theory.

## 5. Compositionality: The Central Engineering Challenge

The visual language's value proposition depends on compositionality -- the ability to build complex meanings from simple visual atoms according to systematic rules. Without compositionality, the system is just another symbol set, requiring a new dedicated symbol for every new concept. With compositionality, a learner who masters 100 base elements can express thousands of concepts through combination.

**What compositionality must achieve**:

1. **Productive vocabulary**: Any concept should be expressible by composing known elements, even if no dedicated symbol exists for it. A user encountering the concept "justice" for the first time should be able to compose it from known elements (perhaps BALANCE + evaluation + social-group).

2. **Predictable meaning**: The meaning of a composition should be derivable from the meanings of its parts plus the rules for combining them. A reader encountering an unfamiliar composition should be able to work out what it means.

3. **Grammatical marking**: The system must distinguish statements from questions, assertions from negations, and encode tense/aspect/modality through visual means.

**Design approaches** (synthesized from Chinese, sign language, Blissymbolics, and visual programming):

- **Spatial positioning as syntax**: Agent/subject on the left; patient/object on the right; modifiers above; modified below; enclosure for scope/conditionality.
- **Visual operators as grammatical markers**: Strike-through or reversal for negation; question mark or distinctive enclosure for questions; directional flow for tense; size/emphasis for modality.
- **Radical-style semantic classification**: Base symbols organized into semantic domains (emotion, action, spatial, temporal, evaluative) with shared visual elements identifying the domain.
- **Metaphor-based extension**: Abstract concepts composed through primary metaphor mappings. "Understanding" = SEEING + GRASPING. "Progress" = PATH + forward.

**The cognitive load constraint**: Individual symbols should contain no more than 5-7 visually distinct sub-elements (applying Miller's Law to visual chunking; Cowan's more recent research suggests the limit may be closer to 4 chunks for novel items). Compositions should limit simultaneous novel elements to working memory capacity. This is not a fixed number -- it changes with expertise. A novice sees five separate elements; an expert sees two familiar chunks. The system must be processable at both levels.

Gestalt grouping principles (proximity, similarity, closure) should be exploited to reduce perceived complexity. Two adjacent elements that share a boundary are perceived as one unit, not two. This is free cognitive compression -- if the composition rules align with Gestalt principles, complex symbols will look simpler than their component count suggests.

There is also a specific autistic processing advantage to consider here. Enhanced perceptual functioning (EPF) includes superior local detail detection and pattern discrimination. If the primary user population can genuinely process visual detail more efficiently than neurotypical baselines, the effective working memory capacity for visual symbol processing may be higher for them than for the general population. This is speculative -- it has not been tested for visual symbol systems specifically -- but it is consistent with the EPF evidence and would mean that the cognitive load constraints are looser for the target users than for the general population.

## 6. The Phonetic Escape Hatch

A purely semasiographic system cannot represent "Tokyo," "Shakespeare," or "paracetamol" without some phonetic mechanism. This is not a theoretical objection -- it is a practical necessity. Proper names, place names, borrowed words, and novel technical terms require phonetic representation.

Every successful visual system has solved this differently:

- **Chinese**: Uses phonetic radicals and the mouth radical for transliterations
- **Sign languages**: Fingerspelling (alphabet-based)
- **Blissymbolics**: Struggles with this -- no systematic phonetic subsystem

Four design options exist for the visual language:

1. **Featural phonetic notation** (Hangul-inspired): Visual elements encode articulatory features (place, manner, voicing) rather than specific phonemes. Language-independent because based on universal articulatory properties. Theoretically elegant but complex to learn.

2. **IPA-based visual encoding**: Symbols for each IPA category. Cross-linguistically valid but requires phonetic knowledge.

3. **Alphabetic fingerspelling analogue**: Symbols corresponding to a Roman alphabet. Simple and fast but privileges European languages.

4. **Phono-semantic hybrid** (Chinese-inspired): New terms encoded with both a semantic composition (meaning approximation) and a phonetic approximation. Balanced but complex to implement.

No option is perfect. The phonetic subsystem is a design compromise, not a design triumph. A hybrid approach -- language-independent featural notation for formal use, shorthand alphabetic borrowing for casual use -- may be optimal. But this is an engineering decision, not a theoretical one, and it should be made by users through actual use, not by designers through theoretical analysis.

The deeper lesson here is about purity versus pragmatism. A "pure" semasiographic system -- one that never encodes phonetics at all -- would be unable to handle the daily reality of proper names, brand names, scientific terminology, and cultural references. The insistence on purity would make the system impractical. Every successful writing system has found a way to bridge between meaning representation and sound representation when needed. Chinese characters use phonetic radicals and phonetic borrowing. Even emoji users resort to spelling out names in text. The visual language needs the same pragmatic flexibility: a clean semasiographic core with a phonetic escape hatch that is available when needed but not required for normal use.

The phonetic subsystem also serves a bridging function. A visual expression that can be translated into spoken language (through a phonetic rendering of the composed meaning) allows communication with non-users. Without this bridge, the visual language is a closed system -- useful only among people who know it. With the bridge, it becomes one end of a bilingual channel: compose in visual symbols, output in spoken language. This is how modern AAC already works (compose with PCS symbols, output through speech synthesis), and the visual language must maintain this capability.

## 7. The Adoption Problem: The Hardest Obstacle

The adoption problem -- the chicken-and-egg challenge that nobody learns a language unless others use it, and nobody uses it unless others have learned it -- may be the greatest practical obstacle. Design quality does not determine adoption. History demonstrates this clearly.

**Esperanto**: Well-designed, regular, easy to learn. Has not achieved universal adoption despite more than a century of effort. Currently somewhere between 100,000 and 2 million speakers (depending on the definition and source), with approximately 2,000 native speakers. A proposed standard "will not flourish if it overlooks the practical issues inherent in real-life systems." Language adoption is driven by political, social, and cultural factors, not design quality alone.

**Blissymbolics**: Succeeded in AAC because it filled a genuine need for a specific population (people with motor impairments who needed compositional communication). Failed as a universal language because no broader community needed it urgently enough to learn it.

**Braille**: Succeeded through institutional mandate (adopted by schools for the blind), community advocacy (organizations of blind people), practical necessity (no alternative for non-visual reading), and standardization (Unified English Braille). Adoption took decades.

**Unicode**: Succeeded because it solved a real technical problem (encoding all writing systems digitally), had institutional support (W3C, major tech companies), and enabled rather than replaced existing systems.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-3-adoption-pathway.html -->

The lessons are consistent:

1. **Fill a genuine, unmet need.** The strongest adoption path is serving a population with no adequate existing solution. Autistic visual-spatial thinkers who find verbal communication costly are such a population.

2. **Niche first, expand later.** Start with a specific community where the language provides clear value. Expand as the community grows.

3. **Digital-first.** Physical artifacts are supplementary; digital implementation (apps, web, input methods) is essential.

4. **Open and community-governed.** The language should be open-source, not proprietary.

5. **Enable, don't replace.** Present as an additional modality, not a replacement for speech, sign, or text.

The proposed adoption pathway follows five stages, each providing standalone value:

**Stage 1 -- Personal notation**: A single user develops and refines the system for personal use. Achievable now. No community needed. Value: a thinking tool.

**Stage 2 -- Small community**: A small group (family, therapy setting, online community) uses and iterates. Requires a prototype. Value: communication between 2-10 people.

**Stage 3 -- AAC supplement**: Introduced as an option within existing AAC frameworks. Requires clinical validation. Value: additional modality for AAC users.

**Stage 4 -- Educational tool**: Used in schools for communication support or literacy bridging. Requires curriculum development.

**Stage 5 -- Broader modality**: Growing community for general-purpose communication. Requires community governance and significant user base.

Each stage provides value on its own. The system does not need to reach Stage 5 to justify its existence. A single user who finds it useful as a thinking tool has already achieved Stage 1.

## 8. What Is Feasible Now, What Needs Research, What May Be Impossible

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-4-feasibility-zones.html -->

**Feasible now (green zone)**:
- Core symbol set (65-100 base symbols grounded in image schemas)
- Basic spatial composition rules
- Digital SVG prototype with input method
- Tier 1 communication (requests, descriptions, emotions, questions)
- Personal and small-group use
- Core vocabulary coverage (200-400 concepts)
- Dual coding design (visual symbols with verbal names)

**Requires research (yellow zone)**:
- Complex grammar in 2D (conditionals, relative clauses, embedded structures)
- Cross-cultural symbol validation
- Optimal visual complexity thresholds (empirical, not theoretical)
- Tier 2 expressiveness (narrative, causation, quantification)
- AAC platform integration
- Learnability empirical testing
- Compositional fluency development timeline
- Cognitive fatigue in extended visual reading

**May be impossible (red zone)**:
- Full semasiographic expressiveness (Tier 3)
- Truly universal symbols (cultural neutrality at scale)
- Competing with natural language processing speed
- Cultural neutrality at scale
- Avoiding the adoption trap entirely
- Preventing compositional opacity over decades of community use

The design must prioritize the green zone, invest research effort in the yellow zone, and accept that the red zone may remain out of reach.

## 9. Digital Implementation: The Technical Stack

The visual language requires a complete technology stack:

**Symbol format**: SVG (Scalable Vector Graphics) with semantic metadata. Resolution-independent, renderable on any screen, searchable by meaning and component.

**Input method**: Multiple approaches serving different user populations and use cases:
- Component-based selection (choose base elements, system composes them -- analogous to Chinese pinyin input)
- Drawing/gesture input (sketch a symbol, system recognizes it)
- Keyboard mapping (base symbols mapped to keyboard shortcuts)
- Touch-based composition (drag and arrange on touchscreen)

**Search and indexing**: Symbols searchable by meaning, by component, and by semantic domain. "Show me all emotion symbols." "Show me symbols containing the CONTAINER element."

**Encoding standard**: Unique identifier for each symbol for digital storage, transmission, and interoperability. Unicode-compatible encoding (potentially using Private Use Area initially, with eventual proposal for standard inclusion).

**Speech synthesis bridge**: Composed visual expressions translatable to spoken language for communication with non-users. This requires maintained mapping from visual compositions to verbal equivalents in multiple languages.

**Accessibility**: Compatibility with screen magnification, high contrast modes, eye-tracking input, switch scanning, and screen readers (via semantic metadata).

## 10. Honest Failure Modes

Feasibility assessment requires identifying not just what could succeed but how it could fail.

**The opacity trap**: Over time, composed symbols may become conventionalized -- their meaning remembered as a unit rather than derived from components. This is exactly what happened to Chinese characters over three millennia: characters that were once transparently compositional are now opaque to modern readers. The same could happen to the visual language within decades. Mitigation: maintain an etymology database, design composition rules to maximize ongoing transparency, establish a governance body (like BCI for Blissymbolics) that reviews compositional logic.

**The learnability wall**: The image-schema grounding hypothesis may not hold. Base symbols grounded in image schemas may not actually be easier to learn than arbitrary shapes. If this is the case, the learning curve will be comparable to Blissymbolics', and the market will choose PCS-style pictorial approaches for the same reasons it already does. There is no mitigation for this except empirical testing -- and if the hypothesis fails, the project must adapt or be honest about the limitation.

**The adoption desert**: Even if the system works brilliantly for individuals, it may never achieve community scale. A communication system used by one person is a code, not a language. Without a community, there are no communication partners. The niche-first strategy addresses this, but there is no guarantee that the niche will grow.

**Cultural mismatch**: Image schemas are claimed to be universal, but visual representations of those schemas may carry cultural connotations. A CONTAINER symbol that reads as a bowl to one culture and a coffin to another would be disastrous. Cross-cultural testing is essential and has not been done.

**Cognitive fatigue**: Extended visual symbol processing -- reading the equivalent of a chapter of a book in composed visual symbols -- may be more cognitively demanding than reading text. This has never been tested because no comparable visual language exists. If extended reading proves exhausting, the system's utility may be limited to short exchanges rather than sustained discourse.

There is a reason to think this concern is real. Text reading benefits from thousands of years of optimization -- font design, spacing conventions, paragraph structure, typographic hierarchy -- all refined through centuries of use to minimize cognitive load during extended reading. A new visual language would start without any of this accumulated optimization. Early use would likely be more effortful than reading text, not less, regardless of how well the symbols are designed. Whether this improves with expertise and optimization, or remains a permanent limitation of compositional visual symbols, is an open question.

**Scope creep**: Perhaps the most insidious failure mode. A project that begins with the modest goal of "Tier 1 functional communication for visual-spatial thinkers" can easily expand its ambitions to "full natural language replacement" under the excitement of early success. If Tier 1 works, the temptation to immediately pursue Tier 3 -- rather than thoroughly validating and deploying Tier 1 -- could spread development effort across unsolvable problems at the expense of deliverable ones. The adoption pathway's stage-gate structure is designed to prevent this: you cannot advance to the next stage until the current one demonstrates value.

## 11. What Next: A Realistic Plan

Based on the evidence, the honest assessment, and the feasibility analysis, the next concrete steps are:

1. **Design the core symbol set**: 65-100 base symbols grounded in image schemas, NSM primes, and core vocabulary research. This is primarily a design task, achievable with current knowledge.

2. **Establish composition rules**: Spatial positioning syntax, visual grammatical operators, composition templates for common sentence types. Drawing on sign language spatial grammar, Chinese radical organization, and Blissymbolics' indicator system.

3. **Build a digital prototype**: SVG rendering, basic input method, searchable dictionary. Web-based for maximum portability.

4. **Test with users**: Begin with autistic visual-spatial thinkers. Measure recognition speed, composition accuracy, learning curve, subjective preference. Iterate based on feedback.

5. **Open-source everything**: Symbol set, composition rules, digital tools, documentation. Community governance from the earliest stage.

Each step is independently valuable. Step 1 produces a symbol set usable as personal notation. Step 3 produces a prototype usable for small-group communication. Step 4 produces empirical data that validates or invalidates the core hypothesis. Step 5 creates the conditions for community-driven evolution.

The order matters. It is tempting to jump to Step 4 (user testing) without completing Step 2 (composition rules). But a symbol set without composition rules is just another PCS -- a collection of pictures without productive power. The composition rules are what make it a language rather than a vocabulary. Testing must validate the compositional system, not just the individual symbols.

It is equally tempting to skip Step 5 (open-sourcing and community governance) in favor of maintaining designer control. This temptation should be resisted. Every successful communication system -- from natural languages to Braille to Unicode -- evolved through community use. A language that cannot evolve is a dead language. The designer's role is to establish the foundational architecture and then hand governance to the community of users.

The project does not require solving Tier 3 to begin. It does not require universal adoption to provide value. It does not require perfection to be useful. It requires honesty about what is achievable, rigor in testing, and willingness to adapt when evidence contradicts assumptions. The eleven categories of research behind this assessment do not prove the visual iconographic language will work. They prove that the attempt is grounded in evidence, that the potential benefits are real, and that the path forward is identifiable even if the destination is uncertain.

## 12. Final Assessment

The visual iconographic language is theoretically grounded, practically motivated, and partially feasible.

**Theoretically grounded**: The convergence of NSM semantic primes, image schema theory, conceptual metaphor theory, sign language existence proofs, Chinese radical composition models, and cognitive science of visual processing provides a richer theoretical foundation than any previous visual language attempt.

**Practically motivated**: The modality mismatch between autistic visual-spatial cognition and verbal communication infrastructure is real, documented, and underserved. A communication system designed for this cognitive architecture would serve a genuine need.

**Partially feasible**: Tier 1 is achievable now. Tier 2 is plausible with research. Tier 3 may be impossible. Learnability targets are reasonable but untested. Adoption requires community building, not just design quality.

The comparison with historical precedents is instructive. When Charles Bliss published Semantography in 1949, he had no empirical evidence that his system would work for communication. Twenty-two years later, Shirley McNaughton adapted it for AAC, and it is still used in 33+ countries. When Louis Braille developed his system in 1824, there was no guarantee it would become the standard for non-visual reading. It took decades of institutional advocacy and standardization. When the Unicode Consortium began in 1988, encoding all the world's writing systems in a single standard seemed impossibly ambitious. All of these succeeded not because they were perfect but because they filled genuine needs and iterated relentlessly.

The visual iconographic language has a stronger theoretical foundation than any of these had at their inception. It has the advantage of digital implementation (none of the historical systems did). It has a well-characterized target user population (autistic visual-spatial thinkers). It has a clear niche-first adoption strategy grounded in historical lessons. And it has something none of the universal language projects had: the explicit acknowledgment that it does not need to achieve universal adoption to be valuable.

The honest conclusion: this is worth attempting, with eyes open about the limits. Start small. Start practical. Build incrementally. Test everything. Let the evidence decide what works. And if certain elements do not work -- if Tier 2 grammar proves too complex for 2D, if learnability targets prove unrealistic, if the adoption problem proves intractable -- adapt, narrow scope, and deliver what is achievable rather than abandoning the project because perfection was not possible.
