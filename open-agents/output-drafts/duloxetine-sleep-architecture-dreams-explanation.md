# Sleep Architecture and Vivid Dreams

> You asked whether increasing the dose will ruin the sleep. The study that would answer that has never been run, and the evidence that does exist does not put the danger where you have put it.

Of everything duloxetine has done in two weeks, the sleep is the part you most want to keep. You said so directly: *"I don't want to increase my dose only to find my sleep ruined."* That is a specific, answerable question, and it deserves a specific answer rather than reassurance.

The answer has two halves, and they pull in different directions.

The first half is the one nobody will volunteer: **the evidence you would need does not exist.** Nobody has ever measured what duloxetine does to sleep architecture at two different doses in patients. Not at 20mg, which is where you are. Not at 30mg or 60mg, which is where you might go. Not in anyone with fibromyalgia, and not in anyone with ME/CFS. There is exactly one study in the world that compared two duloxetine doses on polysomnography, and it cannot answer your question for reasons that are structural rather than fixable `[primary, verified]`. This matters practically, because it means that anyone who tells you confidently what a dose increase will do to your sleep architecture is filling a gap with their intuition. Including, if I am not careful, this page.

The second half is the surprise, and it cuts against your fear. **The evidence that does exist, which is about reported side effects rather than measured architecture, locates the sleep risk at 120mg, not at the step you are considering.** In the one trial that ever tested 20mg, 60mg and 120mg head to head, insomnia was *lower* at 60mg than at 20mg, and it doubled at 120mg. That pattern holds across every sleep-related row in that trial, and it converges with two other independent trials. The defensible version of this is narrow but real: **there is no evidence that the 20 to 60mg step damages sleep.** Note what that sentence does not say. It does not say 60mg will help.

## The only dose comparison that exists, and why it cannot help you

The study is Chalon 2005 `[primary, verified]`. It compared duloxetine 80mg once daily against duloxetine 60mg twice daily, on polysomnography, in young healthy men. It found that the twice-daily arm reduced sleep continuity while the once-daily arm *improved* it, and concluded that 80mg once daily "appeared to exhibit less impact upon sleep quality than duloxetine 60 mg BID."

That reads like exactly the biphasic answer you are asking for. It is not, for three reasons, and any one of them is disqualifying.

There were **six people in each duloxetine arm**. Six.

More seriously, the comparison **confounds the dose with the schedule, irreducibly**. The 60mg twice-daily arm differs from the 80mg once-daily arm in total daily dose (120 versus 80), *and* in dosing schedule, *and* therefore in whether a dose gets swallowed in the evening. The arm that hurt sleep continuity is the only one with an evening dose. You cannot tell whether continuity fell because the dose was higher or because half of it was taken at night, and those two possibilities have completely different implications for you `[reasoning, not evidence]`. The regimen you would actually take, 30mg or 60mg once daily, is a regimen Chalon never tested.

And it was young healthy men, for seven days, at four to six times your dose.

So the honest position at the architecture level is that **nobody has measured this**, and the one attempt is unusable. That is a finding rather than a failure to find something, and it is worth holding onto, because the rest of this page is built on weaker material than the question deserves.

## What the trial evidence actually says, and where it puts the risk

Because architecture is unmeasured, the usable evidence is side-effect reporting: how many people in each dose arm told the investigators they had insomnia, or somnolence, or fatigue.

There is exactly one duloxetine trial with posted results anywhere that contains a **fixed 20mg arm**. It was found by scanning all 135 duloxetine trials with posted results on the ClinicalTrials.gov registry `[primary, verified]`. Your dose has been formally compared against 60mg and 120mg in a single registered trial, one time, ever. Here is what it found.

| | Placebo | **20mg** | **60mg** | **120mg** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Insomnia** | 2.56% | **10.17%** | **8.62%** | **18.75%** |
| **Somnolence** | 0% | **5.08%** | **4.31%** | **12.50%** |
| **Fatigue** | 0% | 0% | 6.03% | 8.93% |

Read the middle two columns. **20mg and 60mg are indistinguishable on every sleep measure in the table.** Insomnia goes slightly *down*. Somnolence is flat. Discontinuation for adverse events is flat, at 15.3% versus 14.7%. The inflection is at 120mg, where insomnia doubles and somnolence triples `[primary, verified]`.

The same trial ran the Athens Insomnia Scale, a validated instrument, by dose. It is the only by-dose validated sleep-scale data that exists for duloxetine. On it, 20mg was indistinguishable from placebo, **60mg beat placebo (p=0.045)**, and **120mg was significantly worse than 60mg (p=0.012)** `[primary, verified]`.

**Do not lean on that last paragraph, and I want to say why before you do.** It is a secondary endpoint of a trial that **failed its primary outcome**: no dose beat placebo on pain. It is uncorrected for multiplicity across six pairwise comparisons, so p=0.045 and p=0.012 would very likely not survive correction. The 20mg arm was n=59, roughly half the size of the others. It was chronic low back pain, not fibromyalgia and not ME/CFS. It was funded by the manufacturer. The 120mg arm lost 24.1% of its people to adverse events, which means the week-13 survivors there are enriched for people who tolerated it, which *flatters* 120mg rather than penalising it. **And decisively, it does not replicate**: two other trials measured the same Athens Insomnia Scale at 60mg and found nothing, at p=0.329 and p=0.590 `[primary, verified]`.

So the claim you can actually take to your appointment is the narrow one. **There is no evidence that going from 20mg to 60mg damages sleep, and the harm signal in the by-dose data sits at 120mg.** The tempting extra step, that 60mg will improve your sleep, rests on one uncorrected secondary endpoint in a failed trial that two other trials could not reproduce. It is not available to you.

## You are braced for the wrong thing

This is the single most useful reframe on this page.

**Insomnia does not rise from 20 to 60mg anywhere.** Not in the back pain trial (10.2% to 8.6%), not in the Korean adult trial at either food condition (12.7% to 13.6%, and 9.4% to 7.9%) `[primary, verified]`. It rises at 120mg.

**Somnolence is the effect that tracks the dose in this range.** At the 30 to 60mg step it roughly doubles to quadruples: 2.6% to 10.2% in adolescents, 0% to 6.8% and 7.8% to 14.3% in Korean adults `[primary, verified]`. In the back pain trial it triples at 120mg. Somnolence is also confirmed dose-dependent by the FDA label itself, at 10 to 11% versus 3% on placebo `[primary, verified]`. If you have seen a "1.5%" figure quoted for duloxetine somnolence, that is a **discontinuation rate**, not an incidence rate, and it is a different number answering a different question.

I owe you one honest complication here, because it runs mildly against the reframe. **In the one trial that actually has a 20mg arm, somnolence was flat from 20 to 60mg** (5.08% to 4.31%), and only rose at 120mg. The doubling-at-30-to-60 pattern comes from two other trials that had no 20mg arm. So the strongest form of this claim, that somnolence definitely rises at your specific step, is not supported. The supportable form is: **of the two effects, insomnia is the one that never rises across this range, and somnolence is the one that sometimes does** `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

Then there is the number that should hold your attention. **In the only trial of duloxetine in ME/CFS ever conducted, somnolence was 41%** at 60 to 120mg, against 10% on placebo, in 29 people `[primary, verified]`.

Here is why that lands where it does. Your gain from this drug, in your own description, is *waking less tired*. Somnolence is daytime sleepiness. It is the one adverse effect that arrives dressed as the thing you are trying to protect, and you have told us your discrimination on exactly this axis is poor: *"There are different types of exhaustion. Just feeling 'sleepy' isn't a bother. It's mostly that bone-weary, pain-laden exhaustion where my brain is filled with so much fog I can barely function."* That is a real distinction and it is a useful one, but it means the harm signal and the benefit signal arrive through a channel you have said is unreliable. **You are watching the insomnia door. The traffic is at the somnolence door** `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

One more thing about that ME/CFS trial deserves your attention, because it dismantles the folk model you are probably carrying. In those same 29 people, **insomnia was 34% and somnolence was 41%, simultaneously** `[primary, verified]`. Not at different doses. In the same population, at the same time. The FDA label lists **both** among the reactions with a significant dose-dependent relationship in fixed-dose studies `[primary, verified]`. So the popular story, that low doses sedate and high doses activate, is **not supported**. Insomnia and somnolence are not opposite ends of one dose axis. They are two different effects happening to different people on the same drug, and which one you get is not something anyone can currently predict for you.

This is also what the largest dataset says, and it is worth knowing it exists. A dose-effect network meta-analysis of **216 trials and 64,696 patients** examined exactly this question, independently of the manufacturer `[primary, verified]`. Its finding, verbatim, is that the dose-effect curves *"included linear shape, inverted U-shape, and other shapes"*, and that the risks *"were not always increased linearly along with the dose increase."* For duloxetine specifically it reports somnolence following a bell curve and insomnia following a plateau `[primary, partially verified]`. Weigh that carefully rather than gratefully: it is adverse-event reporting rather than measured sleep, it is depression trials only, and it is a model-based inference across trials rather than a comparison within one, so it inherits every difference between the trials that happened to use different doses. **It partly disagrees with the fixed-dose trial above**, which found insomnia doubling at 120mg in a genuine randomized within-trial comparison, and on that specific point the within-trial design is stronger.

## The vivid dreams: three explanations, all dead, and nobody knows

You have vivid dreams. They are common, they are expected, and they are not a warning sign. In the ME/CFS trial, **"vivid dreams" is a literal adverse-event term**, reported by 13.79% of patients versus 3.33% on placebo `[primary, verified]`. That is your exact symptom, reported as an adverse event, in your exact condition, at roughly four times the placebo rate. You are not unusual. You are in the 14%.

What you will be told about *why* is wrong, and I want to clear the ground rather than replace one tidy story with another.

**The REM rebound story is wrong for you.** You will read that antidepressants suppress REM, that REM pressure builds, and that the rebound produces vivid dreams. **REM rebound is a discontinuation phenomenon.** It happens when the drug comes off. You are two weeks into a steady dose and going up, not coming off `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

**The evidence points the opposite way from the folk account.** The decisive study gave healthy volunteers fluvoxamine or paroxetine for nineteen days and tracked their dreams. **Dream recall frequency went DOWN. Subjective dream intensity went UP** `[primary, verified]`. On a serotonergic drug you remember fewer dreams, and the ones you get feel more intense. That is the treatment phase of that study, and your report fits it precisely.

**And REM does not explain dream recall at all.** The 2020 review of this literature says it flatly: *"The impact of psychotropic drugs on rapid eye movement (REM) sleep does not explain DRF [dream recall frequency] modulations."* What explains it, on the best available account, is **awakenings**: *"The reduction of intra-sleep awakenings seems to be the parameter explaining best the modulation of DRF by psychotropic drugs"* `[primary, verified]`. The same review lists three drugs that make every possible combination: trimipramine does not reduce REM but reduces recall; clomipramine reduces REM but leaves recall alone; fluoxetine strongly inhibits REM and *increases* recall. A drug's REM effect predicts nothing about its dream effect. The relationship is not mechanistic.

So: **nobody knows why antidepressants intensify dreams.** You asked for mechanism, and the honest answer is that the mechanism is genuinely unidentified. The effect is real, expected, common at your dose, and unexplained. The tidy REM story is available everywhere and it is not true.

## Why the mornings are easier, and why the obvious answer is not usable

You wake less tired. The explanation you will find is that duloxetine suppresses REM, and that REM deprivation is itself antidepressant, which traces to Vogel's work in the 1970s.

**Do not use it.** It was never independently replicated. Modern reviews report a wide range of inconsistent effects of REM deprivation in animal models. And the confound is elegant and fatal: REM deprivation is followed by REM *rebound*, and it may be the rebound rather than the deprivation doing the work `[secondary — unverified]`. If that is right, the mechanism runs backwards from the version used to explain it. A fifty-year-old unreplicated finding with a possibly inverted mechanism cannot carry your morning fatigue.

There is one measured duloxetine finding that points the right way. Kluge 2007 found **stage 3 sleep up 78%**, from 21.0 to 37.4 minutes `[primary, verified]`. Deep sleep is the mechanistically obvious route to waking less tired, and it is the single most relevant sleep parameter for someone with ME/CFS. But it was ten people, with no control group and no placebo, in depressed patients whose slow-wave sleep recovers as their mood improves anyway, at a dose that appears nowhere in the paper's abstract and is known only from a secondary review `[secondary — unverified]`. And the same-class comparison points the other way: the venlafaxine polysomnography study found stages II and III **reduced** `[primary, verified]`. One small uncontrolled study says duloxetine protects deep sleep. A study of a same-class drug says the opposite. It is unresolved, and it is unresolved on the parameter that matters most to you.

So what is left is more parsimonious and less interesting. You say the effect is *"mostly psychological and with my sleep."* Your anxiety largely resolved. Anxiety reduction improves sleep quality and morning energy on its own, with no architecture story required. Fewer nocturnal awakenings would explain your dreams and your mornings with a single mechanism, and awakenings are precisely what the dream literature identifies as the operative variable. **The most parsimonious reading is that anxiolysis plus better sleep continuity are doing the work, and that REM architecture is not involved at all** `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

That reading has a practical consequence worth carrying to your appointment. If the benefit is anxiolytic and continuity-based rather than REM-based, then it is **not obviously dose-dependent in either direction**, and the relevant evidence becomes the 20mg anxiety trial rather than anything about REM.

## Two things you may have been told that are not true

**"More noradrenaline at 60mg means worse sleep."** The pharmacology is clean: noradrenaline is wake-promoting, and NET occupancy rises from about 30% at 20mg to about 40% at 60mg, so higher dose should mean more activation and worse sleep. **The outcome data contradicts it.** In the one fixed-dose trial, insomnia went slightly *down* from 20 to 60mg and the validated scale got *better*. Worse, the 60mg occupancy point is **n=2, with a confidence interval running from −20% to +100%** `[primary, verified]`, from a single laboratory that nobody has replicated, and there is **no NET occupancy threshold for any clinical effect, including sleep**. When a clean mechanism and messy outcome data disagree, and the mechanism rests on two people, trust the outcome data.

**"Duloxetine raises periodic limb movements."** You will find this stated with authority in a 2020 review. **It is extrapolated from venlafaxine and has no duloxetine primary behind it.** Duloxetine has **never been measured for periodic limb movements. At any dose. Ever.** Not by Kluge, who did not report them. Not by Chalon. The one duloxetine sleep case report explicitly found none `[primary, verified]`. Against the extrapolation stands a systematic review of 18 prospective studies which concluded that antidepressant-associated increases are **"uncommon"** and, for the drugs actually studied, produce movements **"that do not disrupt sleep and are thus unlikely to be clinically significant"** `[primary, verified]`. Duloxetine is not mentioned in it at all. Your restless legs did get worse, and that is real and it is yours. But it is a thing to **monitor**, not a thing to **predict**. The restless legs page carries the full treatment.

## The nap, which is the most interesting thing you own

You cannot nap. Ever. Even at maximum exhaustion you lie there for ten to twenty minutes and give up. You have wondered whether that is *"a fight/flight mechanism that won't turn off"*, and whether it might be causing your poor sleep. You have not tried since starting duloxetine.

I have to retract something an earlier pass of this project told you, because it was wrong.

**Your nap-failure is not the ME/CFS sleep finding experienced from the inside.** The study that was used to make that claim measured ME/CFS twins falling asleep in **8.9 minutes** `[primary, verified]`. They fell asleep, in under nine minutes, on a nap test. That is not nap failure. That is brisk sleep onset. The finding in that study was "feels sleepy, sleeps normally." Your report is "feels exhausted, cannot sleep at all." Those are different observations.

The wider claim was worse. The entire ME/CFS nap-test literature was enumerated, all fifteen papers, and the dissociation that was described to you as replicated is **not replicated, it is contradicted, in both directions** `[primary, verified]`. One study found ME/CFS patients fall asleep **significantly faster** than controls. Another found 80% abnormal in the sleepy direction. The field's own systematic review calls the nap-test findings *"inconsistent"* and *"non-replicated."* The CDC's paper says there are *"no reproducible perturbations of sleep architecture, multiple sleep latency times or Epworth Sleepiness Scores."* Nothing in the ME/CFS literature describes a group that cannot nap. **Where that literature is directional at all, it runs against you.**

What your nap-failure actually resembles is the **insomnia hyperarousal phenotype**, where the objectively verified inability to nap *is* the finding: those patients have measurably *longer* nap latencies `[primary, verified]`. **But that fit is imperfect and I will not oversell it either**, because you do not report insomnia. You report that your sleep improved and that it is the best thing about this drug. Daytime nap-failure with decent night sleep is not a described phenotype in either literature. You sit between them, and the field's own 2023 review names the cross-diagnostic comparison as an **open question** rather than a settled overlap `[primary, verified]`.

Two things follow, and they are both useful.

**First: you have been running an aborted test your whole life.** The formal nap test allows **twenty minutes** and scores a no-sleep trial as twenty. You quit at ten to twenty. Your lifelong "hard zero" may be a lifelong latency of eighteen minutes that you have never let run `[reasoning, not evidence]`. A "no" cannot move. **A number can.** Running the full twenty minutes converts a lifelong binary into a measurement, and that is the single highest-value change available to you here, and it is free.

**Second, and this is the part to understand before you try: the test is sound for a positive and nearly worthless for a negative.** A channel with a lifelong floor has no false-positive noise, so one clean, repeatable success would be genuinely surprising and genuinely informative. It would also be the first thing anyone has ever recorded about duloxetine and daytime sleep propensity, in any population, because **nobody has measured what duloxetine or any SNRI does to nap ability** `[no evidence exists]`.

But a null tells you nothing, because **the drug pushes the outcome both ways at once.** If duloxetine lowers your arousal, nap latency should fall. **But duloxetine improved your sleep, which reduces sleep drive, which makes napping harder.** This is not speculation about relative magnitudes. It was measured: sleep deprivation drove nap latency from 13.4 minutes down to 3.8, and a five-minute walk beforehand raised it from 6.9 to 11.9 `[primary, verified]`. **Sleep drive dominates arousal.** The conclusion of that work is explicit: measured sleep tendency is a *combination* of sleep drive and central arousal. So "still can't nap" is fully compatible with "my arousal did drop, but I am also less sleep-deprived than I have been in thirty years." **Know that before you run it, so that a null does not read to you as a refutation.**

If you do run it: four attempts rather than one, at a fixed early-afternoon time, no exertion for thirty minutes beforehand, no caffeine for eight hours, log the previous night's sleep, run the full twenty minutes, and write your prediction down first. And do it **now**, before the appointment, because the 20mg window closes the moment the dose moves and does not reopen.

## Your causal arrow may run backwards

This is the thing on this page I would most want you to see, because it goes against your own hypothesis and it is not obvious.

You have wondered whether your inability to nap is **causing** your poor sleep. The only dataset on napping in ME/CFS says the opposite.

**101 ME/CFS patients kept 14-day sleep diaries. 21 of them, 20.8%, did not nap at all across the whole fortnight** `[primary, verified]`. You are in that fifth. **Not napping is common in your condition, not anomalous.** And the study's conclusion, verbatim: *"Napping, particularly in the afternoon is associated with poorer cognitive functioning and more daytime sleepiness in CFS"* `[primary, verified]`.

In the only data that exists, **napping is associated with worse outcomes, not better.** It is cross-sectional, so the direction is unresolvable, and it is confounded by severity, and it measured *reported* napping rather than nap *ability*, which means it cannot distinguish "cannot nap" from "chooses not to nap." Your claim is about ability. This study cannot speak to it directly. But the sign is the opposite of your assumption, and it converges with ordinary sleep medicine, where daytime napping reduces nocturnal sleep pressure and degrades night sleep `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

**So it is a live possibility that not napping is protecting your night sleep rather than damaging it.** That is untested and it may be wrong. But you should know it exists before you treat "learning to nap" as a goal, because you may be trying to fix the mechanism that is currently working for you.

## The cortisol question is malformed, and that is the answer

You have wondered about *"excess cortisol in my body."* An earlier pass told you that you were backwards, because ME/CFS is associated with low cortisol rather than high. **That was retracted, and you are not backwards.**

The meta-analysis of unstimulated cortisol in ME/CFS found **no statistically significant difference on total cortisol output**. The only significant finding was an attenuated morning rise, at d=−0.34, which is small `[primary, verified]`. Meanwhile the famous study establishing that insomnia involves high cortisol, the one everyone cites, found **cortisol at P=0.07 in eleven people** `[primary, verified]`. It did not reach significance. Its ACTH did, at p=0.04, and the paper's title spent the certainty anyway.

So both candidate literatures say the same thing, and it is not what either of them is quoted as saying. **The absolute level is not the variable.** The meta-analysis is explicit that total cortisol output *"was rarely associated with fatigue in any population"* and that the within-day *change* is what relates to fatigue `[primary, verified]`. You asked "how much?" The evidence says **the shape, not the amount.** If you want this tested, the thing that would answer it is a diurnal salivary panel at waking, plus thirty minutes, and evening, so that the slope and the awakening response are visible rather than a single number. That is a conversation with your doctor, not a conclusion `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

Those absences are not gaps in this page. **They are the state of the field, and they are the most reliable things here.**

|---|---|
| n | **10** (7 male, mean age 39.9 ± 7.6) |
| Population | Patients with major depression, HAMD-21 23.6 ± 5.6 |
| Design | PSG before and after treatment. **Within-subject. No placebo. No control group.** |
| Duration | 7–14 days |
| Dose | **Not stated in the abstract.** A secondary review reports 60mg `[secondary — unverified]` |

**What Kluge does not report:** total sleep time, sleep efficiency, wake after sleep onset, arousals, sleep onset latency, **periodic limb movements**. The two parameters that matter most for this reader, sleep continuity and limb movements, are the two it did not measure or did not report.

Causation, because there is no placebo and no control, n=10, and depression itself shortens REM latency and reduces slow-wave sleep, both of which normalise as mood improves. |---|---|---|

The three disqualifying confounds are set out in the narrative. The important one to restate is the second: **the arm that reduced sleep continuity is the only arm with an evening dose.** That is consistent with "do not take the increment in the evening," and it is equally consistent with "120mg hurts continuity," and n=6 cannot separate them. No numerical PSG values appear in the abstract; the full text is behind a Springer authentication redirect and was not retrieved.

### The mechanism, and why it cannot bear weight

Raising synaptic serotonin and noradrenaline delays REM onset and reduces REM time. That empirical regularity is rock solid and holds across every antidepressant class `[HIGH]`. Duloxetine does it, desipramine does it, and venlafaxine does it so completely that **REM was abolished in all 8 volunteers by night 4** `[primary, verified]`.

The textbook explanation is the **reciprocal interaction model** (McCarley & Hobson, 1975): aminergic "REM-off" cells in the locus coeruleus and dorsal raphe tonically inhibit cholinergic "REM-on" cells; aminergic firing is high in wake, falls in NREM, and ceases in REM; reuptake inhibitors raise aminergic tone, so REM-off wins.

**That model is no longer the field's mechanism of record** `[secondary — unverified]`. It was revised into a limit-cycle version, and the field then moved to GABAergic/glutamatergic flip-flop models. The stated criticisms are that the majority of relevant pontine REM-on projecting neurons are not cholinergic, that carbachol has no effect on certain REM-on neurons, and that the monoaminergic predictions do not match experimental data.

> The "aminergic REM-off cells get louder" story still predicts the drug effect correctly, but it is a serviceable simplification rather than the current science. It should be presented as such and not as the explanation.

### The vivid-dreams graveyard

**Dead explanation 1: REM rebound.** Rebound is a **discontinuation** phenomenon. It explains vivid dreams during withdrawal or between doses. It does not explain vivid dreams on stable dosing, and this reader is two weeks into a steady dose `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

**Dead explanation 2: REM suppression drives dream intensity.**

**Pace-Schott EF, Gersh T, Silvestri R, Stickgold R, Salzman C, Hobson JA.** "SSRI treatment suppresses dream recall frequency but increases subjective dream intensity in normal subjects." *J Sleep Res.* 2001 Jun;10(2):129-42. **PMID 11422727** `[primary, verified]`

14 healthy volunteers, fluvoxamine 100mg (n=7) or paroxetine 20mg (n=7), 31-day home study: 7 days baseline, 19 days medication, 5 days discontinuation. **Dream recall frequency decreased during treatment. Subjective intensity increased during treatment and during acute discontinuation.** Authors: *"The decrease in dream frequency during SSRI treatment may reflect serotonergic REM suppression while the augmented report length and bizarreness during acute SSRI discontinuation may reflect cholinergic rebound."*

**Dead explanation 3: REM predicts dream recall.**

**Nicolas A, Ruby PM.** "Dreams, Sleep, and Psychotropic Drugs." *Front Neurol.* 2020;11:507495. **PMID 33224081** `[primary, verified]`

> *"The impact of psychotropic drugs on rapid eye movement (REM) sleep does not explain DRF [dream recall frequency] modulations."*
>
> *"The reduction of intra-sleep awakenings seems to be the parameter explaining best the modulation of DRF by psychotropic drugs."*
>
> *"Some antidepressants such as trimipramine do not reduce REM sleep but do reduce dream recall, and some such as clomipramine diminish REM sleep duration but have no effect on dream recall frequency, while others such as fluoxetine strongly inhibit REM sleep and increase dream recall frequency."*

Those three drugs exhaust the possible combinations of REM effect and recall effect. The relationship is not mechanistic.

> **Verdict.** The dreams are a real, common, expected serotonergic effect, at 13.79% versus 3.33% in the ME/CFS trial. The chain "REM suppression → REM pressure → denser REM → vivid dreams" is **not supported**. Dream intensity rises on serotonergic drugs by a mechanism nobody has identified. Dream recall tracks awakenings, not REM. **Nobody knows why antidepressants intensify dreams.**

**And the fatigue story that must not be used.** Vogel's 1970s REM-deprivation work (**PMID 165796**) found REM-deprived endogenously depressed patients improved comparably to imipramine. **It was never independently replicated** `[secondary — unverified]`. Modern reviews report inconsistent effects of REM deprivation in animal models. The confound is that REM deprivation is followed by REM rebound, and *"it might be the rebound sleep, and not REM sleep deprivation itself, that has an antidepressant effect"*, which inverts the mechanism relative to the version used to explain antidepressant action. Historically important, not established, and unusable here.

### The largest dataset: a dose-effect network meta-analysis

**Zhou S, Li P, Lv X, ... Sun F.** "Adverse effects of 21 antidepressants on sleep during acute-phase treatment in major depressive disorder: a systemic review and dose-effect network meta-analysis." *Sleep.* 2023 Oct 11;46(10):zsad177. **PMID 37422714** `[primary, verified]` (note: the published title reads "systemic," not "systematic")

**216 trials, 64,696 patients.** By a wide margin the largest relevant dataset available.

| Outcome | Duloxetine vs placebo, OR (95% CI) |
|---|---|
| **Somnolence** | **3.02 (2.31–3.95)** |
| **Insomnia** | **1.96 (1.60–2.42)** |

Abstract, verbatim: *"The dose-effect relationships curves between somnolence or insomnia and dose included linear shape, inverted U-shape, and other shapes."* Body: *"the risks of somnolence and insomnia were not always increased linearly along with the dose increase for 21 antidepressants."*

Duloxetine's curve shapes `[primary, partially verified — the per-drug sentences were truncated in retrieval and the dose-response figure was not read]`: **somnolence bell / inverted-U**, rising from low to moderate doses then declining at high doses; **insomnia plateau**, *"increased gently and then presented a flat trend."*

**Weight it carefully.** For: 64,696 patients, explicitly designed for the dose-effect question, independent of the manufacturer. Against: MDD trials only; **adverse-event reporting rather than measured sleep**; and it is a **model-based inference from aggregate across-trial data** rather than a within-trial dose comparison, so it inherits every confound between trials that happened to use different doses. The authors' own limitation: *"Some antidepressants only appeared in one dose in the constructed model."* **And it partly disagrees with NCT00408876**, which found insomnia doubling at 120mg in a within-trial randomized comparison, which on that specific point is the stronger design.

### Is the biphasic story real?

Partly, and not in the way the folk version says.

- **For somnolence**, an inverted-U is the finding of the largest dataset available, and is consistent in direction with Chalon.
- **For insomnia**, the best evidence says **plateau**, not biphasic, while the one within-trial fixed-dose comparison says flat 20→60 then a spike at 120.
- **The folk version, "low dose sedating, high dose activating," is NOT supported.** The actual finding is stranger and more important: **duloxetine causes insomnia AND somnolence in the same population at the same time.** In the ME/CFS trial, 34% insomnia and 41% somnolence, simultaneously, in 29 people. The FDA label lists **both** among the reactions with *"a significant dose-dependent relationship in fixed-dose studies"* `[primary, verified]`. They are not opposite ends of one dose axis. They are two different effects happening to different people on the same drug.

### Timing: morning versus evening

- **FDA label**: once-daily duloxetine may be taken **without regard to time of day** `[primary, verified]`.
- **FDA label PK**: an evening dose reportedly gives a ~3-hour delay in absorption and roughly one-third faster apparent clearance than a morning dose `[secondary — unverified]`.
- **There is no trial of morning versus evening duloxetine dosing with sleep outcomes.** The standard advice, "take it in the morning if it keeps you awake, at night if it makes you sleepy," is **convention, not evidence** `[reasoning, not evidence]`.
- ⚠ **`[NOT SOURCED — do not repeat]`**: the claim that *"morning dosing reduces insomnia risk by approximately 60%"* appears on commercial health-content sites with **no citation and no traceable primary.** It should be treated as fabricated until someone produces the study. It is flagged here because it is exactly the kind of specific-sounding number that gets laundered in by repetition.
- **The one real signal**: in the only dose-comparison PSG study, the arm that reduced sleep continuity, 60mg BID, is the only regimen with an evening dose. That is *consistent* with "do not take the increment in the evening," but n=6 and irreducibly confounded with total daily dose `[primary, verified — but the inference is reasoning, not evidence]`.

### Duloxetine and periodic limb movements

**Duloxetine has never been measured for PLMS. At any dose. Ever.** No polysomnography study of duloxetine has reported them: not Kluge (does not report them), not Chalon, and the RBD case report explicitly found none.

**The sentence that must be caught before it propagates.** Nicolas & Ruby 2020 states: *"Duloxetine demonstrates effectiveness in reducing PTSD nightmares and has a similar effect on sleep as venlafaxine: reduced length of REM sleep, fragmented sleep, and increase in periodic limb movement"* `[primary, verified that the review says this]`. **This is a review assertion with no duloxetine PLMS study behind it. It is extrapolated from venlafaxine.** A downstream reader of that review will find an authoritative-sounding sentence saying duloxetine increases PLM. It should not be repeated as a duloxetine finding.

**Against it stands the systematic review.** Kolla BP, Mansukhani MP, Bostwick JM. "The influence of antidepressants on restless legs syndrome and periodic limb movements: A systematic review." *Sleep Med Rev.* 2018 Apr;38:131-140. **PMID 28822709** `[primary, verified]`. **18 prospective studies.** Verbatim: *"Current data indicate that onset or exacerbation of restless legs syndrome and rise in frequency of periodic limb movements are **uncommon** following the initiation of an antidepressant."* And: *"Sertraline, fluoxetine, and amitriptyline appear to increase periodic limb movements that **do not disrupt sleep and are thus unlikely to be clinically significant**."* **Duloxetine is not mentioned.**

> **Verdict: monitor, do not predict.** The mechanism is coherent and the reader's own RLS did worsen at 20mg, which is the best evidence in the case because it is about him. But the chain from "SSRIs raise PLMS" to "duloxetine will fragment your sleep at 60mg" has four breaks: duloxetine has never been measured; PLMS dose-dependence is not established for any SNRI; the one systematic review of 18 prospective studies points the other way; and the only escalation study confounds dose with consecutive nights of exposure. **The restless legs page carries the full treatment**, including the pharmacovigilance data, the iron thresholds and the melatonin question.

### The nap test

**The retraction, stated plainly.** An earlier pass of this project told the reader that his nap-failure was the ME/CFS nap-test finding experienced from the inside. **That is wrong.**

**And the dissociation is not replicated. No MSLT study of any SNRI exists.** PubMed `duloxetine[tiab] AND (tonic[tiab] AND phasic[tiab])` returns exactly one record, and it is the RBD case report, where "tonic" and "phasic" refer to chin EMG rather than the locus coeruleus.

**The instrument verdict, and the asymmetry that is the whole point.**

**A positive is informative. A negative is not** `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

The reasoning that a lifelong hard-zero channel has no noise is **structurally sound**. If the probability of napping has been approximately zero across decades, one unambiguous success is genuinely surprising and therefore informative. Five things break it, in descending severity:

1. ⚠ **THE KILLER: duloxetine improved his sleep, which reduces sleep drive, which makes napping HARDER.** Bonnet & Arand measured it: sleep deprivation drove latency from 13.4 to 3.8 min. **Sleep drive dominates.** The drug pushes both directions at once, they cancel, and nobody knows by how much. **A null is therefore uninterpretable**: "still can't nap" is fully compatible with "tonic arousal reduced, but I am also less sleep-deprived than I have been in thirty years."
2. **He has been running an aborted test.** The MSLT protocol allows **20 minutes** and scores a no-sleep trial as 20. He quits at 10–20. **His lifelong hard zero may be a lifelong latency of 18 minutes he has never let run.**
3. **The effort paradox is his specific risk.** Trying to sleep prevents sleep, which is the defining mechanism of the very phenotype he most resembles. A deliberate test with a hypothesis attached maximises exactly the performance anxiety that raises latency.
4. **The denominator is unknown.** Rare, self-selected attempts under uncontrolled conditions make a lifelong zero a weaker prior than it feels.
5. **One nap is an event, not a rate.**

**The protocol, if he runs it** `[reasoning, not evidence — the design is inferred; the parameters come from the MSLT protocol and Bonnet & Arand's measured confounds]`:

| # | Rule | Why |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Four attempts, not one** | One trial is an anecdote. Four is a latency. |
| 2 | **Run the FULL 20 minutes** | Converts a lifelong binary into a number that can move. **Highest-value change.** |
| 3 | **Fixed time, ~13:00–15:00** | The circadian nap zone. The wrong hour guarantees a null. |
| 4 | **No exertion for 30+ min before** | A 5-minute walk raised latency 6.9 → 11.9 min, **larger than any plausible drug effect.** |
| 5 | **Fixed conditions**: same room, dark, quiet, lying down, no screen | Context moves sleepiness by 1–2 units either way. |
| 6 | **No caffeine 8h. Log it regardless.** | The covariate that actively masks the outcome. |
| 7 | **Log the previous night's sleep** | **The dominant covariate.** Eight hours stacks the test toward null. |
| 8 | **Log dose time**, keep the nap at a fixed interval from it | Distinguishes a plasma-tracking effect from a tonic one. |
| 9 | **Pre-register the prediction in writing** | Substitutes for the blinding he cannot have. |
| 10 | **Record three things**: minutes to sleep (or 20), fell asleep Y/N, and separately *"could I have?"* | The third is introspective and least trustworthy. Record it; do not let it outvote the first two. |

**Do it now.** The 20mg window closes the moment the dose moves and does not reopen.

**Benefit or adverse effect?** Partially distinguishable, on the **volitional/intrusive axis**, which requires no introspection and is therefore not routed through the compromised channel `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

| Discriminator | **Reduced arousal (benefit)** | **Somnolence (adverse)** | Strength |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Volitional vs intrusive** | Falls asleep **when he tries**, at a chosen time. Unintended dozes stay at zero. | Falls asleep **when he is not trying**: reading, in conversation, as a passenger. Unintended dozes rise. | **STRONGEST.** Opposite on "did I choose it?", and needs no introspection. |
| **MSLT/MWT dissociation** | Should move "can you sleep when you try" and leave "can you stay awake when you must" alone | Should move **both** | **Strong.** The two correlate at r=0.41, under 17% shared variance, two factors. |
| **Time-locking to dose** | Tonic: flat across the day | Should track the plasma curve | **Moderate.** Needs fixed-time sampling. |
| **Dose-dependence** | Unknown direction at 60mg | **Label-confirmed dose-dependent** | ⚠ **UNAVAILABLE BEFORE THE APPOINTMENT.** He is at one dose. |

> **The rule, and it is free: count two things separately and never merge them.** (1) Deliberate nap attempts that **succeeded**. (2) **Unintended dozes**, where he fell asleep without deciding to.
>
> **Benefit looks like: (1) rises, (2) stays at zero. Somnolence looks like: (2) rises**, whatever (1) does. **Both can happen at once.** They are not mutually exclusive and he should not expect a clean verdict.
>
> **The honest limit, which matters more than the taxonomy:** if he can nap, functions better, and nothing intrudes, then whether a physiologist would call it reduced arousal or mild somnolence does not change what he should do. **The question that bears on the dose decision is not "which is it?" but "does it cost me anything?"** That is answered by the unintended-doze count, the night-sleep log, and whether hours disappear. Those are countable. The mechanism label is not.

Conclusion, verbatim: *"**Napping, particularly in the afternoon is associated with poorer cognitive functioning and more daytime sleepiness in CFS.**"*

⚠ **The limit, stated at the claim** `[reasoning, not evidence]`: **Gotts measured reported napping, not nap ABILITY.** It cannot separate "cannot nap" from "chooses not to nap." **His claim is about ability. This study cannot speak to it.** It is the closest number that exists and it is not the number he needs. > **In the only dataset on napping in ME/CFS, napping is associated with WORSE outcomes, not better.** The sign is the opposite of his assumption, and it converges with standard sleep medicine, where daytime napping reduces nocturnal sleep pressure and degrades night sleep `[reasoning, not evidence]`.
>
> ### **His causal arrow may run backwards. Not napping may be PROTECTING his night sleep rather than damaging it.** That is a live possibility, it is untested, and he should know it before he treats "learning to nap" as a goal.

### Cortisol: the question is malformed

**Powell DJ, Liossi C, Moss-Morris R, Schlotz W.** "Unstimulated cortisol secretory activity in everyday life and its relationship with fatigue and chronic fatigue syndrome: a systematic review and subset meta-analysis." *Psychoneuroendocrinology.* 2013 Nov;38(11):2405-22. **PMID 23916911** `[primary, verified]`

19 studies reviewed; subset meta-analyses of case-control CFS studies:
- *"Meta-analyses revealed an **attenuation of the CAR increase** within CFS compared to controls (**d=−.34**) but **no statistically significant differences between groups for other markers**."*
- *"**total cortisol output (CAR or CP) was rarely associated with fatigue in any population**; CAR increase and DCS were most relevant."*
- *"**Outcomes reflecting within-day CHANGE in cortisol levels may be the most relevant to fatigue experience**."*
- *"Results should be considered with caution due to heterogeneity in one meta-analysis and the small number of studies."*

**Vgontzas AN, Bixler EO, Lin HM, Prolo P, Mastorakos G, Vela-Bueno A, Kales A, Chrousos GP.** "Chronic insomnia is associated with nyctohemeral activation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis." *J Clin Endocrinol Metab.* 2001 Aug;86(8):3787-94. **PMID 11502812** `[primary, verified]`

**n = 11 insomniacs vs 13 controls.** 24-h ACTH: 4.2 ± 0.3 vs 3.3 ± 0.3 pM, **P = 0.04**. 24-h cortisol: 218.0 ± 11.0 vs 190.4 ± 8.3 nM, **P = 0.07**. Cosinor analysis showed *"no differences in the temporal pattern."*

> ⚠ **The most-cited "insomnia has high cortisol" study did not reach significance on cortisol. P = 0.07. n = 11.** ACTH separated; cortisol did not. The paper's title says "activation of the HPA axis" and its conclusion says *"an overall increase of ACTH and cortisol secretion."* **The cortisol half of that sentence sits on p=0.07 in eleven people.**

**So is he backwards?** `[reasoning, not evidence — every premise is primary and verified]`

1. **Against the textbook ME/CFS claim: yes**, since the direction of that (weak) evidence is hypo, not hyper.
2. **But the textbook claim does not survive its own meta-analysis on the measure he means.** He is talking about how much cortisol is in his body. **Powell found no significant group difference on total cortisol output.** "ME/CFS = low cortisol" is not a fact he is contradicting. It is a claim that failed when meta-analysed on the variable he named.
3. **If he indexes to the insomnia phenotype instead**, which his nap-failure actually resembles, **he is arguably right in direction.** But that finding is p=0.07 in eleven people.
4. **The deepest answer: both literatures say the absolute level is not the variable.** Total output *"was rarely associated with fatigue in any population"*; the within-day change is what relates to fatigue.

> **Verdict: he is not backwards. He is asking a question the evidence in both candidate literatures cannot answer, about a variable the best meta-analysis says is the wrong one.** He asked "how much?"; the evidence says "the shape." If he wants it tested, the instrument is a **diurnal salivary panel** (waking, +30 min, evening) so the slope and awakening response are visible, not a single number. That is a conversation with his doctor, not a conclusion.

### Does the fibromyalgia and ME/CFS baseline change the answer?

**Alpha-delta sleep in fibromyalgia is real, replicated, and badly oversold.** Roizenblatt S, Moldofsky H, Benedito-Silva AA, Tufik S. "Alpha sleep characteristics in fibromyalgia." *Arthritis Rheum.* 2001 Jan;44(1):222-30. **PMID 11212164** `[primary, verified]`. In 40 female FM patients and 43 controls, three distinct alpha patterns appeared: **phasic alpha in 50%, tonic alpha in 20%, low alpha in 30%** (versus 83.7% of controls, P<0.01). **Only the phasic subtype tracks symptoms**: post-sleep pain worsening ran 100% in the phasic group versus 25.0% in the tonic group, and self-rated poor sleep 100% versus 12.5%.

> Alpha intrusion is real and replicated, and it is not a 1970s artifact. But **only half of FM patients have the symptom-linked phasic pattern**, and the tonic-alpha fifth report *better* sleep than the low-alpha group, meaning more alpha intrusion and fewer complaints. **The blanket claim "fibromyalgia is alpha-delta sleep" is false**, and any inference running "he has FM, therefore he has alpha-delta sleep, therefore X" is unsound.

⚠ **Two things that must not enter on their current provenance.** Doherty & Smith 1993, "Elusive 'alpha-delta' sleep in fibromyalgia and osteoarthritis" (**PMID 8484686**): **cited for existence only. Nobody has read it.** PMC holds only a scanned page image. Its title signals a skeptical result and it would be exactly the counter-evidence this project keeps missing, but inferring a conclusion from a title is the chain's signature sin. **Do not cite its result.** And the widely-repeated claim that inducing alpha intrusion with tones produces next-day fibromyalgia-like pain surfaced **only from a commercial mattress-retailer blog** `[NOT SOURCED — do not repeat]`.

**Does duloxetine improve sleep in fibromyalgia?** Marginally, on the weakest endpoint in the trial, and never by dose. NCT00673452, 12-week LS mean change `[primary, verified]`:

|---|---|---|---|
| Mood | −1.27 | −0.52 | **<0.001** |
| Stiffness | −2.54 | −1.67 | **<0.001** |

> **Sleep is the weakest of the five endpoints in duloxetine's own fibromyalgia trial**, significant only at the margin, where everything else clears 0.003. **No fibromyalgia trial reports sleep outcomes stratified by dose.** All 135 registry trials with posted results were checked: **the only dose-stratified sleep outcome in the entire registry is a chronic low back pain trial.**

**ME/CFS: the only trial, and what it did not measure.** Arnold LM, et al., NCT00375973, **PMID 25660434**, duloxetine 60–120 mg/day, n=29 vs placebo n=30, 12 weeks. Primary outcome MFI General Fatigue: **negative** `[primary, verified]`.

|---|---|---|

The trial's full outcome list: MFI General Fatigue (primary), BPI average pain, HADS depression, CGI-S, PGI-I, discontinuations.

> **There is NO sleep outcome measure in the only ME/CFS trial of duloxetine ever conducted.** Nobody measured sleep in the population he belongs to.
>
> **The number that should concern him most is 41% somnolence**, not the 34% insomnia. In an ME/CFS population at 60–120mg, two in five reported daytime sleepiness. **Somnolence is the adverse effect that lands directly on the thing he is trying to protect.** n=29, not stratified by dose, flexible 60–120mg, so it cannot tell him what 30mg or 60mg alone would do.

**Slow-wave sleep: the thing that matters most here, and the thing least known.** Kluge is the only duloxetine SWS measurement and it points the right way (stage 3 up 78%). But n=10, no control, depressed patients whose SWS recovers as mood improves, dose known only from a secondary source. The same-class comparison points the other way: the venlafaxine study found stages II and III **reduced** `[primary, verified]`. Chalon reports no SWS values for duloxetine at all. **Unresolved, on the single most relevant sleep parameter for an ME/CFS patient.**

### What to watch, and what to ask

**Six numbers, nightly, thirty seconds.** Each is falsifiable and maps to a specific pathway.

| # | Marker | Pathway it tests |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Minutes to fall asleep** | Continuity (Chalon's harm signal was continuity, not architecture) |
| 2 | **Number of awakenings** | Continuity. The variable that would explain losing the dreams AND the morning fatigue with one mechanism |
| 3 | **Morning fatigue on waking, 0–10** | **The outcome that matters.** The primary endpoint of his own experiment |
| 4 | **Daytime sleepiness, 0–10, scored SEPARATELY from fatigue** | Somnolence: the effect the trials say actually rises with dose |
| 5 | **Vivid dreams: present / absent** | Serotonergic tone. A free natural marker; if they vanish, something changed |
| 6 | **RLS severity at bedtime, 0–10** | The pathway most likely to degrade sleep silently |

**Numbers 3 and 4 must be scored separately.** For an ME/CFS patient they feel similar and mean opposite things: fatigue is the disease, somnolence would be the drug. Collapsing them makes the single most informative signal in this whole analysis unreadable `[reasoning, not evidence, but it follows directly from the AE data]`.

**Baseline these now, at 20mg, before the appointment.** Two weeks of baseline is achievable in the time available and is the highest-value thing available. **Without a baseline the dose test is uninterpretable**, because a change cannot be told from a mood.

**What is not useful: total sleep time from a consumer wearable** `[reasoning, not evidence]`. Wearables cannot measure PLMS or sleep continuity reliably. That is measuring noise and giving it authority.

**Time course: when would he know?** The duloxetine RLS case report had onset **the next night** after a 60→80mg increase `[primary, verified]`. Chalon's sleep-continuity effect was present at **day 6** `[primary, verified]`. Insomnia and somnolence as trial AEs emerge in the first one to two weeks after a dose change `[reasoning, not evidence — the trials do not report time-to-onset]`. **One to two weeks is enough to know**, which is what makes a trial period genuinely reasonable rather than a euphemism for a permanent change.

**Questions for the appointment.** Nothing here directs a dose; these are for the conversation.

1. *"The only trial that tested 20, 60 and 120mg head-to-head found insomnia was no worse at 60mg than at 20mg, and the jump was at 120mg. Does that match what you see?"*
2. *"Can we do 30mg rather than 60mg? What would we lose?"*
3. *"Can we agree in advance what result sends me back to 20mg, and write it in the notes?"*
4. *"The only ME/CFS trial of this drug found 41% somnolence at 60–120mg. My main disability is fatigue. How will we tell drug sleepiness apart from my ME/CFS?"*
5. *"My RLS got worse at 20mg. There's a published case of RLS appearing overnight after a 60→80mg increase, reversed by dropping the dose. What's our plan if that happens?"*
6. *"Should I take any increase in the morning rather than the evening?"* Flagging honestly: **this is convention, not evidence.** There is no trial. But the only regimen in the only dose-comparison sleep study that hurt sleep continuity was the one with an evening dose.
7. *"I started iron this week for ferritin 70. That takes about 3 months. Should we let it run before changing anything else?"*

### What nobody has studied

Each of these is an answer, not an absence of one. **Do not soften them.**

1. **Duloxetine's sleep architecture at two doses in patients.** One study exists (Chalon), n=6/arm, healthy young men, 80 vs 120mg, dose confounded with schedule. **Never at 20 / 30 / 60mg. Never in patients. Never with schedule held constant.**
2. **Duloxetine and periodic limb movements. At all, at any dose, ever.** The review claim that it raises PLM is extrapolated from venlafaxine.
3. **Sleep in ME/CFS on duloxetine.** The only CFS trial measured **no sleep outcome.**
4. **Sleep by dose in fibromyalgia.** No FM trial reports dose-stratified sleep. The registry's only dose-stratified sleep outcome is a **back pain** trial.
5. **Morning versus evening duloxetine dosing for sleep.** No trial. The advice is convention.
6. **What duloxetine, or any SNRI, does to daytime sleep propensity.** No MSLT study exists at any dose in any population.
7. **The mechanism of antidepressant dream intensification.** Recall and intensity dissociate; REM explains neither.
8. **Whether PLMS rises with dose for any SNRI.** The one escalation study confounds dose with night.
9. **Duloxetine in autistic adults.** Still true, and now also true specifically for sleep.
10. ⚠ **Kluge 2007's own full text.** Total sleep time, wake after sleep onset, sleep efficiency, arousals and PLMS are not in its abstract, and **the full text defeated this project across six paywall attempts.** ScienceDirect returned 403 to every route tried. **Those numbers remain unread by anyone in this chain.** If they exist, nobody here has seen them, and this page is written without them.
