## 1. Why This Page Exists (And The Specific Care Required)

This page is about what happens after. After you have recognized the situation. After you have begun to protect yourself. After (for some readers) you have left, or achieved sufficient separation, or the situation has otherwise changed. What does recovery actually look like? What is realistic? What is oversold? What works?

This page requires unusual care about evidence quality because **the popular literature on recovery from manipulation, trauma, and coercive control has made many claims that exceed the research base**, and some of those claims cause real harm when they fail to materialize for specific readers. The foundational trauma research document for this hub specifically catalogs these overreaches (see [manipulation-foundation-research-trauma-frameworks.md](manipulation-foundation-research-trauma-frameworks.md), Section 12). They include:

- **"Everyone experiences post-traumatic growth"** — not supported
- **"Trauma makes you stronger"** — not supported; average outcomes are not better than no-trauma baselines
- **"Time heals all wounds"** — not supported; untreated PTSD and complex PTSD often persist for decades
- **"You need to fully forgive your abuser to heal"** — not supported; recovery does not require forgiveness
- **"Confronting your abuser is a necessary step"** — not supported; can be actively unsafe
- **"The body keeps the score"** (as a neuroscience claim about literal tissue-level trauma storage) — not supported as stated in the popular book
- **"Narcissistic abuse" as a clinical trauma category** — not a recognized clinical category; the underlying phenomenon is real but the specific framing is pop-literature
- **"Everyone can reach acceptance"** — not supported; some people live with unresolved grief and anger as a permanent feature

This page will not repeat those claims. It will give you an honest picture of what the research actually supports about recovery, the limits of that research, and the specific considerations for autistic readers recovering from manipulation.

The core evidence base for this page:

- **Judith Herman's three-stage recovery framework** (Herman, 1992, 2015) — Safety → Remembrance and Mourning → Reconnection. Herman's framework is a clinical heuristic, not an RCT-validated protocol, but it has structured the field for over thirty years and has strong face validity. [ESTABLISHED CLINICAL PRACTICE]
- **Complex PTSD diagnosis in ICD-11** (Brewin et al., 2017; Cloitre et al., 2018) — the formal recognition of complex trauma as distinct from single-incident PTSD. [VALIDATED]
- **Tedeschi & Calhoun's post-traumatic growth framework** (1996, 2004) with the critical caveats from Frazier et al. (2009) — PTG is a real phenomenon for some survivors; the claim that everyone experiences it is not supported. [MIXED — real phenomenon, often overclaimed]
- **Zalta et al. (2021) meta-analysis on social support as a PTSD protective factor** — social support is one of the strongest predictors of trauma recovery. [VALIDATED]
- **Kristin Neff's self-compassion research** (Neff, 2003, 2023) — self-compassion is associated with better trauma recovery outcomes. [MODERATE EMPIRICAL]
- **Evidence-based trauma treatments** (CPT, PE, EMDR, TF-CBT, STAIR) — covered in the [Finding Professional Support page](manipulation-hub-strategy-professional-support.md). [VALIDATED for PTSD; LIMITED DATA for autistic adults specifically]
- **Autism-specific trauma research** (Rumball et al., 2020, 2024; Haruvi-Lamdan et al.; Kerns et al.) — elevated PTSD rates in autistic adults, broader range of events experienced as traumatic. [VALIDATED general findings; PRELIMINARY on treatment adaptations]

## 2. Herman's Three Stages (What the Framework Actually Says)

Judith Herman's three-stage recovery model, proposed in *Trauma and Recovery* (1992), remains the most widely-used framework for thinking about trauma recovery. It is important to understand what the framework actually claims and what it does not.

### Stage 1: Safety

Herman's central insight — and the one most directly relevant to readers of this hub — is that **recovery cannot meaningfully begin while the survivor is still in the situation.** This has two implications:

**First, if you are still in the situation:** the work available to you is Phase 1 work. Establishing safety. Reducing exposure to the harm. Building the resources you will need for whatever comes next. Protecting your capacity. This is not "pre-recovery"; it is the first stage of recovery itself. You are not failing to recover because you have not yet processed the trauma. You cannot process the trauma because you are still experiencing it.

**Second, if safety has been established:** the processing work that becomes possible is specific to post-safety conditions. Trying to do trauma-processing work in conditions that are not yet safe can be actively destabilizing. This is one of the reasons therapy sometimes makes things worse — when processing starts before safety has been established.

**What "safety" means in Herman's framework:**
- Physical safety from the person or situation causing harm
- Stability in basic needs (housing, food, income, medical care)
- Sufficient distance from the harmful dynamics that re-traumatization is not a daily event
- Basic self-care capacity (sleep, hygiene, medication, safe spaces)
- A baseline of trust with at least one other person

Herman's framework is clear that safety is rarely perfect and rarely permanent. It is a condition, not a destination. You may have more safety in some areas than others, and the safety may fluctuate over time. The framework does not require perfect safety before any other work can begin; it requires sufficient safety that further work does not immediately destabilize.

**Critical clinical nuance:** the phase-based assumption that full stabilization must precede any trauma processing has been challenged by research showing that direct trauma-focused therapies (PE, CPT) can be effective for complex trauma survivors without prolonged stabilization phases (Bisson et al., 2019; Melton et al. meta-analysis on stabilization-only treatment). The updated ISTSS PTSD Prevention and Treatment Guidelines (2019) do not explicitly mandate sequential phase-based treatment. Herman's stages remain useful as a heuristic; they should not be read as a rigid sequence.

### Stage 2: Remembrance and Mourning

When safety has been sufficiently established, the second stage involves **constructing a coherent narrative of what happened and grieving what was lost.** This is processing-phase work, and it is the stage most directly addressed by the evidence-based trauma treatments (CPT, PE, EMDR, TF-CBT, STAIR).

What this stage involves, in Herman's framing:
- **Reconstructing the trauma story** in a safe relational context. Not all at once, not without preparation, and not without support.
- **Putting words and affect to what happened.** Many survivors find that the event itself is available to them, but the emotional significance is not. Recovery involves reintegrating them.
- **Grieving what was lost.** This is important and often missed in recovery discussions. The trauma has cost you things — time, opportunities, relationships, parts of yourself, beliefs about the world. Grieving these losses is part of recovery, not separate from it.
- **Working through specific memories and their meanings.** Evidence-based trauma therapies have structured protocols for doing this work systematically.

**What this stage is not:**
- A complete re-experiencing of the trauma ("catharsis"). Cathartic approaches — re-living the trauma in intense emotional form — are not supported as a treatment approach and can be destabilizing.
- A requirement that you "relive" every incident in detail. Good trauma therapy is targeted, not exhaustive.
- A linear process. People often revisit this stage multiple times as new memories, insights, or triggers emerge.
- A guaranteed outcome. Some survivors do substantial work in this stage and still carry significant ongoing effects. The work reduces suffering; it does not always eliminate it.

### Stage 3: Reconnection

The third stage involves **rebuilding a sense of self, re-engaging with ordinary life, reconstructing meaning, and recommitting to relationships and purpose.** This is the "life after" phase, the longest stage in most recoveries, and the stage where much of what is valuable in the recovery actually lives.

What this stage involves:
- **Reclaiming an identity that is not defined by the trauma.** Survivors often find that early recovery is dominated by the trauma — it is the thing they think about, talk about, organize their life around. The third stage involves allowing the trauma to become one part of your identity rather than the central organizing principle.
- **Re-engaging with relationships** that were affected by the trauma, building new ones, and learning how to be in relationship again with the lessons from the experience.
- **Finding or rebuilding meaning.** This varies enormously by person. Some survivors find meaning in advocacy, in helping other survivors, in creative work. Others find meaning in quiet re-engagement with ordinary life. Both are legitimate.
- **Re-engaging with activities, interests, and pursuits** that may have been suspended during the survival phase.

**What this stage is not:**
- A return to "who you were before." That person is gone, and pretending otherwise usually produces disappointment. The goal is a post-trauma self, not a pre-trauma self.
- A fixed endpoint. People in Stage 3 sometimes cycle back to Stage 2 work when new material emerges. This is normal, not failure.
- A guarantee of happiness. You can be in Stage 3 and still have hard days, still grieve, still experience symptoms occasionally. Reconnection is a trajectory, not a state.

### How the stages are actually non-linear

Herman herself is clear that the stages are not a strict sequence. Real recovery cycles through them repeatedly:

- You can be doing Stage 3 reconnection work in one area of your life while Stage 1 safety work is still needed in another.
- New events can trigger the need for Stage 2 processing even after years of Stage 3 living.
- Building a new relationship might require working through Stage 2 material that did not come up when you were alone.
- Losses encountered in Stage 3 (a parent's death, a friend's betrayal) can trigger fresh Stage 2 work on earlier losses.

The framework is a heuristic, not a schedule. Use it to understand what kind of work is appropriate for your current situation, not to measure your progress against an imagined timeline.

## 3. Post-Traumatic Growth: What's Real and What's Oversold

**Post-traumatic growth (PTG)** is one of the most popular concepts in trauma literature and one of the most overclaimed. The honest framing matters, because overselling PTG to survivors who do not experience it can produce shame ("why am I not growing from this?") that is itself harmful.

### What PTG is

Tedeschi & Calhoun (1996, 2004) developed the concept of post-traumatic growth to capture a phenomenon they observed in some trauma survivors: positive psychological change following traumatic events. They identified five domains:

1. **Appreciation of life** — valuing things not previously valued
2. **Relationships with others** — deeper, more meaningful connections
3. **New possibilities** — new interests, new directions, new pursuits
4. **Personal strength** — greater confidence in one's ability to survive difficulty
5. **Spiritual/existential change** — shifts in how meaning is constructed

The research on PTG has grown substantially since the 1990s and includes measurement instruments (the Post-Traumatic Growth Inventory), cross-cultural validation, and investigation of predictors and mechanisms.

### What the research actually shows

- **PTG occurs in some trauma survivors.** Estimates vary, but the phenomenon is real for a subset of people.
- **It is not universal.** Many survivors do not experience PTG, and there is no evidence that they should or could. Absence of PTG is not a failure.
- **It is not predictable.** The research has not identified reliable predictors of who will experience PTG. It is not correlated with the specific type of trauma, the specific recovery approach, or specific personality traits in ways that let you engineer it.
- **Frazier et al. (2009)** critique: much of the PTG literature relies on retrospective self-report, which has known biases. When growth is measured prospectively (comparing before-trauma and after-trauma measures directly), the effect is much smaller than retrospective studies suggest. Some of what people report as "growth" may be reframing of how they remember their pre-trauma selves, not actual change.
- **The "trauma makes you stronger" claim is not supported.** Average post-trauma outcomes on standard wellbeing measures are not better than no-trauma baselines. Some individuals report growth; others experience persistent decline. The population average is not "better for having gone through it."
- **PTG and ongoing suffering can coexist.** Some survivors experience both growth in some domains and continued pain in others. This is not contradiction; it is complexity.

### The practical framing

**Do not organize your recovery around achieving PTG.** If it comes, it comes. If it does not, that does not mean you have failed at recovery. The goal of recovery is to reduce suffering and restore functioning; growth, when it happens, is a bonus that cannot be engineered.

**If others suggest you should be "growing" or "finding meaning" and you are not experiencing that, reject the framing without guilt.** Pressure to experience PTG you are not experiencing is a form of emotional labor imposed by people who want your suffering to resolve tidily. You do not owe them that resolution.

**Some survivors report growth in specific areas without overall wellbeing improvement.** You might feel deeper appreciation for specific relationships while still experiencing anxiety and hypervigilance in daily life. Neither of these invalidates the other.

*Evidence quality: [MIXED]. PTG as a phenomenon is [VALIDATED] for some survivors; PTG as a universal outcome or required goal is [NOT SUPPORTED].*

## 4. What the Research Says About Recovery (Honestly)

Setting aside both the pop-literature overclaims and the quickly-discarded old pessimism ("once damaged, always damaged"), what does the research actually support about what helps with recovery?

### Strong evidence

**Social support is protective.** Zalta et al. (2021) meta-analysis and subsequent research show that social support is one of the strongest predictors of PTSD recovery outcomes. This is consistent across trauma types and populations. Having people who believe you, who are stable in your life, and who can hold difficult material with you is reliably associated with better recovery trajectories.

**Evidence-based trauma treatments work for many people.** CPT, PE, EMDR, TF-CBT, and STAIR all have strong evidence for reducing PTSD symptoms. Effect sizes are moderate to large for these treatments relative to waitlist or non-specific controls. Not everyone responds, but many people do.

**Safety is prerequisite.** The clinical consensus (and Herman's foundational observation) that trauma processing requires safety is supported by practice observation, though not tested directly as an RCT variable. Processing trauma while still in an actively harmful situation is generally contraindicated.

**Self-compassion is associated with better outcomes.** Neff's self-compassion research (2003 and subsequent) shows moderate correlations between self-compassion practices and reduced trauma symptoms. The mechanism is plausible: self-blame is a major component of trauma-related suffering, and practices that reduce self-blame tend to reduce suffering.

**Meaning-making is protective for those who engage in it.** Park and others have researched the role of meaning-making in trauma recovery. The finding is nuanced: the ability to find some meaning in difficult experiences is protective, but not all attempts at meaning-making are helpful, and forced meaning-making can be worse than not doing it.

### Moderate evidence

**Expressive writing has small benefits.** Pennebaker's expressive writing research (multiple meta-analyses) shows small effect sizes (d ≈ 0.16) for processing difficult experiences through structured writing. Not a replacement for therapy but a legitimate low-cost supplement.

**Peer support has some benefits.** Research on peer support for trauma survivors is methodologically mixed, but the overall finding is modest benefit. For readers without access to traditional therapy, peer support can provide some of what therapy provides.

**Bibliotherapy has small benefits.** Cuijpers and others have reviewed bibliotherapy for mental health conditions. Effect sizes are modest but real. Well-chosen reading can support recovery, especially when combined with other resources.

**Mind-body practices (yoga, mindfulness) have moderate benefits.** Research on yoga and mindfulness for PTSD shows moderate effects, generally smaller than evidence-based trauma therapies but larger than no treatment. Trauma-sensitive yoga and mindfulness programs have better evidence than generic programs.

### Weak or mixed evidence

**Talking to friends (as opposed to structured support) has mixed outcomes.** The benefits depend heavily on who the friends are and how they respond. Negative reactions from friends harm more than positive reactions help (Ullman, 2010). Generic "talk to someone" advice oversimplifies this.

**Generic "self-care" practices** are not well-supported as trauma interventions. Bubble baths, treat yourself, etc. can be genuinely comforting and may support general wellbeing, but they are not trauma treatment.

**Exercise has modest benefits for general mental health** including trauma populations. The effect size is smaller than evidence-based trauma therapies but exercise is often part of overall recovery.

### Not well-supported or contraindicated

- **Critical incident stress debriefing** (single-session, shortly after trauma): no benefit, possible harm (Lilienfeld, 2007).
- **"Catharsis"-based approaches** (intense re-experiencing of the trauma for emotional release): not supported.
- **Recovered memory techniques**: risk of false memory, explicitly flagged as potentially harmful.
- **"Rapid" trauma protocols** claiming to heal trauma in a weekend, a single session, or a specific magic number of sessions: these are usually overselling, regardless of what they sound like.
- **Forgiveness-based recovery** as a requirement: not supported; recovery does not require forgiveness, and forcing forgiveness can produce worse outcomes.

## 5. What Recovery Looks Like in Practice

Recovery is not an event. It is a long trajectory that looks different for different people. Some things that are typically true about recovery:

### It is slower than you want

The common experience is that recovery takes longer than survivors expect. "I thought I'd be over this by now" is one of the most common statements in trauma recovery. Timelines of months to years are normal, not pathological.

For complex trauma — which is what most manipulation and coercive control produces — recovery is usually measured in years, not months. This is not because you are broken; it is because complex trauma is actually complex, and untangling it takes time. The research on C-PTSD treatment suggests that phased treatment often takes 12–18 months, and that is with active therapy.

### It is non-linear

Recovery often looks like this: good days, then a hard day, then good days, then a week of hard days, then good days again, with the overall trajectory gradually improving. Not steady improvement. The hard days after progress is not regression; it is part of the shape of recovery.

Triggers are common. A specific smell, a phrase, a date, a place can produce a sudden return of symptoms. This does not mean you have lost what you gained. It means trauma memory is associative and sometimes surfaces without warning.

### It happens in specific ways, not in the way you expected

Many survivors report that their recovery looked nothing like they thought it would. The things they expected to be hardest turned out to be manageable; the things they did not anticipate became the ongoing challenges. The areas where they expected growth did not grow; growth happened in unexpected domains.

This is another reason not to organize recovery around achieving specific milestones. Recovery is an emergent process, not a planned one.

### Symptoms often shift rather than disappear

For many survivors, full elimination of trauma symptoms is not the goal or the outcome. What changes is the relationship to the symptoms: they become less overwhelming, less frequent, more manageable, less controlling. A survivor who still has occasional hypervigilance but no longer lives in a state of constant alertness has recovered meaningfully, even though the symptom is not gone.

### It often involves grief you did not expect

Many survivors are surprised by the grief that emerges in recovery. Grief for time lost. Grief for the person you were before. Grief for relationships that ended. Grief for a world that turned out to be more dangerous than you had believed. Grief for the version of yourself that trusted and was wrong to trust.

Grief is a recovery process, not an interruption of it. Herman's Stage 2 is explicitly about mourning. Readers who are surprised by how much grief emerges are sometimes tempted to suppress it ("I should be over this"). The research suggests grief is part of the work, not a sign of failure.

### You will probably not "get closure"

The popular concept of "closure" — a specific resolution point where the trauma is definitively finished — is not supported by the research. Some survivors report a sense of completion; many do not. More often, recovery involves living with what happened in a way that allows you to function and find meaning, without ever closing the door completely.

This is not a failure of recovery. It is a realistic endpoint. The goal is a life in which the trauma is integrated, not a life in which the trauma has been erased.

## 6. Autism-Specific Recovery Considerations

Recovery research on autistic adults is sparse but growing. Several things are worth flagging specifically.

### Elevated PTSD rates, with a wider range of traumatizing events

Rumball et al. (2020, *Autism Research*) found that autistic adults exposed to trauma had PTSD rates around 40% current and 60% lifetime in their sample. These rates are significantly higher than in the general population. Subsequent research has replicated the general finding.

Moreover, Rumball and subsequent research found that autistic adults report a **wider range of events as traumatic** than the DSM-5 Criterion A stressor definition captures. Sensory overload, unexpected changes, being disbelieved, bullying that does not meet "threat to life" thresholds, and other events that would not qualify as traumatic in standard frameworks produce PTSD-like symptoms in autistic populations.

**Practical implication:** if your recovery is complicated by symptoms from events that mainstream clinicians do not code as traumatic, you are not wrong or dramatic. The research supports the validity of your experience even when clinicians do not. Pushing for trauma-informed care even when the specific events are outside the DSM-5 frame is legitimate.

### The autistic burnout overlap

Raymaker et al. (2020) defined autistic burnout as characterized by chronic exhaustion, loss of skills, and reduced sensory tolerance, attributed to chronic life stress and capacity mismatch. The overlap between autistic burnout and complex trauma is significant:

- Many symptoms overlap (exhaustion, sensory changes, cognitive slowing, emotional dysregulation)
- The triggering conditions overlap (prolonged stress, inescapable demands, chronic invalidation)
- The treatment literatures are mostly separate, but the conditions often co-occur

**Practical implication:** recovery from manipulation in autistic adults is often recovery from both trauma and burnout at the same time. What works for one may or may not work for the other. Rest, sensory recovery, and capacity-rebuilding are part of the work even if they are not usually framed as "trauma recovery."

### The mutual-masking problem

Late-diagnosed autistic adults especially often present to mental health services with complex trauma or personality disorder diagnoses that obscure the underlying autism. Conversely, trauma responses may be attributed to "autism" and not treated as trauma. Skylark Counselling and various case report literatures have documented this mutual-masking problem.

**Practical implications:**
- **If you have been diagnosed with BPD, CPTSD, or dissociative disorders** without autism being considered, it is worth asking whether autism is also part of the picture.
- **If you have been diagnosed as autistic without trauma being considered**, it is worth asking whether trauma is also part of the picture.
- **Both conditions can be present.** They are not mutually exclusive, and recovery often has to address both.

### The compliance training recovery problem

A specific recovery task for many autistic adults who grew up in compliance-focused environments (ABA, "social skills" training, or simply relentless pressure to mask): **recovering the capacity to have preferences, make decisions from self rather than imagined other-expectations, and trust your own responses.** This is not usually addressed in mainstream trauma recovery frameworks because it is not usually a major issue for non-autistic survivors.

What this work looks like:
- **Noticing when your internal response is "what do they want from me?" rather than "what do I want?"** — and working with the gap.
- **Practicing low-stakes preferences.** What do you actually want for dinner? Not "what would be convenient for others." What do you actually want? Reconnecting to desire is recovery work.
- **Distinguishing compliance from care.** Doing something for someone because you want to is different from doing it because you have been trained to. The felt experience is different; relearning the distinction takes time.
- **Unmasking in safe spaces.** The practice of being yourself without performance, in contexts where it is safe to do so, is recovery work that is rarely named as such in mainstream literature.
- **Working with the grief.** Many autistic adults who do this work encounter grief for the childhood and younger-adult years that were spent performing someone they were not. This grief is legitimate and often needs to be part of the recovery process.

*Evidence quality: [COMMUNITY KNOWLEDGE] with strong experiential support. The autism-specific compliance-recovery process has not been formally studied as a distinct component of trauma recovery, but it is widely described in autistic community writing and recognized by autism-informed clinicians.*

### Sensory recovery

For autistic readers, recovery from prolonged manipulation often includes recovery of sensory tolerance that has been exceeded. Burnout and chronic stress reduce sensory tolerance; recovery often involves gradual rebuilding.

Practical elements:
- **Respect reduced capacity.** During recovery, your sensory tolerance may be lower than it was before. Pushing through sensory demands "to prove you are recovering" usually produces setbacks.
- **Rebuild slowly.** Gradually increasing exposure to previously-tolerable sensory environments helps reshape capacity. This is similar in principle to how physical therapy rebuilds physical capacity after injury.
- **Protect sensory recovery spaces.** Quiet, dim, uncluttered spaces are not indulgences; they are recovery infrastructure.
- **Use sensory support tools.** Noise-canceling headphones, sunglasses, weighted items, stim tools. These are not regressing to childhood; they are accessing appropriate accommodations.

### The processing-time question

Autistic cognition often benefits from additional processing time. Recovery processing is no exception. You may need more time to work through specific events, specific insights, and specific emotional material than mainstream recovery timelines assume.

**Practical implication:** be skeptical of recovery timelines that assume neurotypical processing speeds. Your recovery is not on schedule or behind schedule; it is on your schedule, which is a different thing.

## 7. What You Can Actually Do

Moving from framework and research to practice: what are the concrete things a reader of this hub can do for their own recovery?

### If you are still in the situation

Recovery is Phase 1 work: establishing safety. See [Safety Planning](manipulation-hub-strategy-safety-planning.md), [Strategic Disengagement](manipulation-hub-strategy-strategic-disengagement.md), [Documentation](manipulation-hub-strategy-documentation.md), and [Reality-Check Networks](manipulation-hub-strategy-reality-check-networks.md). You are not "behind" on recovery work; you are doing the first stage.

### If you are newly out of the situation

The first months after exit are typically the hardest. Symptoms that were suppressed during the survival phase often become accessible for the first time. This is normal and expected.

- **Protect stability.** Routine, sleep, food, safety of space. These are not glamorous recovery work but they are foundational.
- **Expect the crash.** The post-exit period often involves exhaustion, grief, and symptom surges. This is your nervous system finally being able to acknowledge what you have been through. Let it happen.
- **Contact an advocate if you have not.** Even post-exit, advocates can help with the immediate practical and emotional work.
- **Do not make major decisions if you can avoid it.** The post-exit period is not the right time to decide about new relationships, career changes, or other major life moves. Stability first.
- **Start therapy if you can.** Evidence-based trauma therapy is most useful after safety has been established, and the early post-exit period is often when this becomes possible.

### If you have been out for a while and are in active recovery

The medium-term recovery period is where most of the processing work happens. It can last months to years.

- **Work with a trauma-informed therapist if available.** The evidence-based treatments are designed for this stage.
- **Build and maintain your reality-check network.** Connection is protective (Zalta et al., 2021).
- **Practice self-compassion deliberately.** Neff's work suggests this is protective. Specific practices include self-compassion meditation, compassionate letter-writing, and the "treat yourself like a friend" framing.
- **Process through expressive writing if therapy is not available.** Small effects, but real.
- **Find meaning where you can, without forcing it.** Some survivors find purpose in advocacy, creative work, or helping others. Others find meaning in quiet re-engagement. Both are legitimate.
- **Respect your grief.** Don't suppress it, don't perform it, don't rush it.
- **Move your body if you can.** Exercise has moderate benefits for general mental health. Gentle movement is recovery work.
- **Protect sleep.** Trauma affects sleep, and sleep affects trauma recovery. If sleep is consistently difficult, address it (this may require medical or therapeutic intervention).

### If you are in longer-term recovery

The longer-term phase is about integration and reconnection. This can extend for years.

- **Let the trauma become part of your story rather than the whole story.** Time helps with this, often without deliberate effort.
- **Rebuild or build new relationships.** With the lessons from the experience, not in denial of them.
- **Re-engage with interests and pursuits.** What brought you joy before? What new things might bring joy now?
- **Periodic check-ins with professional support.** Many survivors benefit from ongoing therapeutic contact even after active treatment ends. This is not dependence; it is maintenance.
- **Notice and address setbacks without self-blame.** Triggers, anniversaries, new losses can produce symptom return. This is recovery, not failure.

### If recovery is stuck

Sometimes recovery plateaus or reverses. Things that can help:

- **Reassess your professional support.** If you have been in therapy without progress for six months to a year, consider whether the therapist is the right fit or whether a different modality is needed.
- **Reassess safety.** If the original situation has changed (the person is back in your life, you are in a new similar situation), Phase 1 work may be needed again before Phase 2 work can continue.
- **Reassess capacity.** Burnout, new stressors, or major life changes can reduce the capacity available for recovery work. Pausing recovery work to rebuild capacity is sometimes the right move.
- **Consider what is missing.** Some survivors get stuck in Stage 2 (remembrance and mourning) and need help moving into Stage 3 (reconnection). Others are trying to do Stage 3 work prematurely and need to return to Stage 2.
- **Allow longer timelines.** Complex trauma recovery is slow. "Stuck" for three months may actually be "moving at an appropriate pace."

### If you cannot access professional support

- **Use the resources you have.** Crisis lines, reading, expressive writing, peer support, community connection.
- **Be deliberate about what you can do.** Self-compassion practice, grounding techniques, pattern recognition, incremental rebuilding of capacity. These have evidence and are accessible without therapy.
- **Do not push harder to compensate.** Self-directed recovery is slower than therapy-supported recovery, and pushing harder often backfires.
- **Focus on the foundations.** Stability, safety, sleep, basic care. These are recovery infrastructure that matters regardless of whether you have professional help.
- **Keep building access.** Your long-term recovery is supported by gradually increasing your access to support over time. Small steps toward that access are legitimate recovery work.

## 8. What Not To Do

Some things that the popular literature recommends that the research does not support, or that are actively counterproductive:

- **Do not force yourself to "move on."** Timelines cannot be engineered. Pressure to recover on someone else's schedule usually backfires.
- **Do not pursue forgiveness as a recovery requirement.** Forgiveness is one possible outcome among several, not a prerequisite for healing. Some survivors heal without forgiving; some never forgive and lead full lives anyway.
- **Do not confront your abuser as a recovery step.** This is not supported by research and is often unsafe.
- **Do not confuse catharsis with processing.** Intense re-experiencing of the trauma is not the same as processing it; done poorly, it is destabilizing.
- **Do not accept "everyone heals eventually" as a universal claim.** Untreated PTSD and C-PTSD can persist for decades. Waiting for time to heal may mean waiting indefinitely.
- **Do not suppress your grief.** Grief is part of the work, not an interruption of it.
- **Do not measure your recovery against others'.** Different people recover at different rates with different outcomes. Comparison produces shame, not insight.
- **Do not adopt pop-psychology frameworks without checking the evidence.** Some popular frameworks (especially "narcissistic abuse recovery" content) have no empirical basis and sometimes teach approaches that are counterproductive.
- **Do not pathologize yourself for the wrong things.** The symptoms you have are responses to what happened, not character flaws. Self-blame is itself a trauma symptom and not an accurate assessment.
- **Do not accept "you need to let it go" as helpful advice.** If you could let it go, you would have. The command to let it go is not a recovery strategy; it is an expression of the other person's discomfort with your continued suffering.

## 9. Autism-Specific Considerations (Revisited)

Several things bear repeating from earlier in the page because they are so central to autistic readers' recovery:

### The sensory dimension is real

Recovery for autistic adults is not just psychological and relational — it is sensory. Rebuilding sensory tolerance is part of the work, not a sideshow.

### The burnout-trauma overlap is your life, not a diagnostic puzzle

You do not need to resolve whether what you are experiencing is "really" burnout or "really" trauma. It is both, they are interacting, and the recovery work addresses both.

### The compliance recovery is its own project

Rebuilding your capacity to have preferences, make decisions from self, and trust your own responses is recovery work that mainstream frameworks rarely name. This is your work, and it is legitimate.

### Autistic peers are uniquely positioned to support this recovery

Other autistic adults who have done recovery work can understand specific dimensions that neurotypical people — even well-meaning clinicians — sometimes cannot. Crompton et al. (2020) on autistic peer-to-peer communication effectiveness supports the general finding that autistic peers can provide support other sources cannot.

### Your recovery may not be visible to others

Autistic expression of recovery may not match neurotypical expectations. Someone who thinks you are "still struggling" may be reading your flat affect or reduced social performance as lack of recovery when the actual recovery is happening in ways they cannot see. This is the credibility discount applied to your healing. Trust your own sense of your progress more than others' reading of it.

### Recovery does not require becoming less autistic

An important thing that is rarely said directly: the goal of recovery is not to become less autistic. Many things that are pathologized in neurotypical framings of recovery are autistic traits, not trauma symptoms. Your directness is not a trauma symptom. Your preference for solitude is not avoidance. Your systematic thinking is not obsessive. Recovery is about processing what happened to you, not about becoming more neurotypical.

If a therapist or recovery framework pushes you to become more neurotypical as a recovery goal, they are pathologizing autism, not supporting recovery.

## 10. What This Page Cannot Do

- **Replace therapy.** For moderate-to-severe trauma, professional support is substantially more effective than self-directed recovery. This page is a supplement, not a substitute.
- **Produce recovery on a specific timeline.** Recovery cannot be scheduled. What you can do is create conditions that support it.
- **Guarantee any outcome.** Some survivors recover fully, some recover partially, some live with significant ongoing effects. The variability is real and not always controllable.
- **Address every type of trauma.** The research base focuses heavily on intimate partner violence, sexual assault, and childhood abuse. Other trauma types (workplace harassment, family estrangement, medical trauma) have less dedicated research. The general principles still apply but with more caveats.
- **Tell you when you are "done."** Recovery does not have a clear endpoint for most people. The question is not "am I done yet" but "what is my life like now."

## 11. Key Takeaways

1. **Herman's three stages — Safety, Remembrance and Mourning, Reconnection — are a useful heuristic, not a rigid sequence.** Real recovery cycles through them non-linearly.

2. **Safety is prerequisite for processing.** If you are still in the situation, recovery work is Phase 1 work, and that is legitimate recovery work, not failure to recover.

3. **Post-traumatic growth happens for some survivors and not others.** It cannot be engineered, and absence of growth is not failure. Reject the pressure to grow on others' timelines.

4. **Social support is one of the strongest recovery predictors.** Reality-check networks and ongoing connection are protective in ways that are well-supported by research.

5. **Evidence-based trauma treatments work for many people.** CPT, PE, EMDR, TF-CBT, STAIR. These are the names to ask for.

6. **Many popular recovery claims are not supported.** Forgiveness as a requirement, confrontation as necessary, time as sufficient, "the body keeps the score" as literal — these are overclaimed. Do not adopt them without checking.

7. **Recovery is slower than you want.** Complex trauma recovery is usually measured in years, not months. This is not pathology; it is the shape of complex trauma.

8. **The autism-specific dimensions matter.** Elevated PTSD rates, broader trauma definitions, burnout overlap, sensory recovery, compliance recovery — your recovery has components that mainstream frameworks do not fully address.

9. **Grief is part of the work.** Losses from the trauma — time, opportunities, relationships, versions of yourself — need to be mourned, not suppressed.

10. **Recovery is not becoming less autistic.** Your autistic traits are not symptoms to resolve. The goal is processing what happened while remaining yourself.
