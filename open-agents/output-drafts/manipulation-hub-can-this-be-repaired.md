## 1. Why This Page Exists

At some point in a difficult situation, you will face the question: **should I try to fix this, or should I get out?**

The mainstream framings of this question are both harmful. One version says "leave if it's bad enough" — which implies that you should tolerate abuse until some threshold is crossed, and that leaving is always the right answer once you cross it. Neither is true. The other version says "don't give up on relationships" — which implies that all situations are repairable if you try hard enough. That is not true either. And it is particularly harmful when applied to manipulation, where "trying harder" is exactly the response the manipulator is counting on.

This page is the framework for thinking about the question without either framing. The real structure is:

- **Some situations are repairable.** Miscommunication responds to communication. Ignorance responds to education. These situations do not require exit and often benefit from engagement.
- **Some situations are not repairable.** Sustained coercive control does not respond to victim behavior change. Many workplace bullying situations do not respond to mediation. Exit is often the only path to safety in these cases.
- **Many situations are in between.** Carelessness may become repair if consequences appear; it may harden into manipulation if they do not. A situation that could be repaired at one point in time may not be repairable at another.
- **"Exit" is not a single thing.** Formal exit (ending a relationship, leaving a job) is one option. Gradual disengagement, structural distance, and strategic minimization are others. Which form of exit is viable depends on your power and resources.
- **The decision happens in stages, not all at once.** You do not have to decide today. You can observe, gather information, build resources, and decide when conditions support the decision.

**This page does not tell you what to do.** It gives you the structure to think about the question.

## 2. What "Repair" Actually Means

Before you can ask "can this be repaired?", you need to know what you are asking. Repair in a relationship is not a return to an imagined pre-conflict state. It is **a shift in the pattern of interaction such that the harm that was happening no longer happens, or happens less, or becomes recoverable when it does happen**.

Repair has three components, each of which can be present or absent independently:

1. **Acknowledgment.** The person causing the harm recognizes, in some form, that harm occurred. This does not require them to accept every word of your account, or to use the language you would use. It requires that they engage with the substance rather than deflecting it.
2. **Behavior change.** The pattern changes in observable ways. Not "promises to do better" — actual changed behavior over time. Promises are cheap; sustained change is expensive, and that is what distinguishes real repair from the reconciliation phase of an abuse cycle.
3. **Safety for you to be affected by what happened.** You can express that you were hurt without being punished for expressing it. You can ask for adjustments without those requests becoming ammunition. You can raise the topic again if it recurs.

**Relationships can have one or two of these without the third** — and that is often what pseudo-repair looks like. Someone can acknowledge the harm, promise change, but retaliate when you raise it later. Someone can change behavior without ever acknowledging what happened, leaving you with no way to discuss it. Someone can acknowledge and make you feel safe while the pattern continues unchanged. None of these is repair. Real repair has all three.

This matters for the question of whether something is repairable. If you ask "can this relationship be repaired?" without being specific about what repair means, you can end up accepting partial outcomes that look like repair but leave the core pattern intact.

## 3. Repairability Depends on the Category

The four-category framework from the [Manipulation vs. Miscommunication page](manipulation-hub-miscommunication-vs-manipulation.md) maps directly onto repairability:

| Category | Repairability | What repair looks like |
|----------|--------------|----------------------|
| **Miscommunication** | Usually repairable | Explicit communication, better channels (written over verbal, or verbal with check-ins), repeated repair cycles producing stable new patterns. Both parties adjust. |
| **Ignorance** | Often repairable *if the person is willing to learn* | Education, specific feedback, resources. Resolution is contingent on whether the person actually integrates what they learn. If they don't integrate, ignorance transforms into carelessness. |
| **Carelessness** | Sometimes repairable, requires consequences | Apologies alone are not repair. Repair requires consequences attached to the behavior that make the carelessness more costly than the adjustment. Without consequences, careless behavior continues. |
| **Manipulation** | Usually not repairable through victim effort | The research on coercive control is clear: victim behavior change does not end coercive control. The controller ends it (rarely), or the target exits it, or it continues. "Working on the relationship" is not effective when the relationship is the mechanism of harm. |

### Why manipulation is not repairable by victim effort

This is one of the most important things to take from this page. The common advice to victims of manipulation — "communicate better," "set boundaries," "explain how it makes you feel," "go to couples therapy" — does not work when the underlying dynamic is manipulation rather than miscommunication. The research bases for this include:

- **Couples therapy is contraindicated for coercive control.** This is a strong research consensus, not an opinion (Bograd & Mederos, 1999, *Journal of Marital and Family Therapy* 25(3); Stith et al., 2012, 38(1); Karakurt et al., 2016, meta-analysis, PMC5050084). Traditional couples therapy assumes approximately equal power and safety to speak honestly; both assumptions are violated by coercive control. In those situations, couples therapy can provide the manipulator with new information to use against the target and can collude with the minimization framing.
- **Mediation is contraindicated for workplace bullying.** The workplace bullying research finds that mediation between target and bully parallels the couples therapy contraindication — it assumes equal power that is not present (Namie & Namie, 2009; Salin, 2008).
- **Treatment-model effectiveness of batterer intervention is weak.** Even structured programs designed specifically to change manipulator behavior show small effect sizes (Babcock, Green & Robie, 2004, *Clinical Psychology Review*, meta-analysis). If professional intervention designed to change the abuser shows small effects, it is unrealistic to expect victim communication efforts to do better.
- **Pearson et al. (2022, 2024)** documented that autistic survivors frequently internalize "if I just try harder / explain better / don't provoke them, it will stop." The pattern does not respond to victim effort. The time spent trying is time taken from exit planning.

**Practical implication:** if your assessment points to manipulation (as distinct from miscommunication, ignorance, or carelessness), the question is not "how can I repair this?" It is "how can I protect myself while I figure out what exit option is viable given my resources?"

*Evidence quality: [VALIDATED] for the couples therapy contraindication. [STRONG EMPIRICAL CONSENSUS] for the limited effectiveness of victim behavior change against coercive control. The underlying mechanism — that manipulation is an active strategy rather than a reactive pattern — is well established in the coercive control literature.*

## 4. Factors That Shift Repairability

Even within the four categories, specific factors make repair more or less likely. These are not deterministic — they are probabilistic signals that can inform the decision.

### Factors making repair more likely

- **The person has a track record of learning from feedback in other contexts.** Not necessarily with you, but in general. People who learn from feedback are capable of repair; people who do not, generally are not.
- **The pattern is short in duration.** A pattern that has been going on for months is usually easier to change than one that has been going on for years. Compliance and expectation harden with time.
- **The person acknowledges, in some form, that something is wrong.** Not necessarily taking the blame, but recognizing that the pattern is a problem rather than defending it entirely.
- **Repair attempts are producing observable change, even if small.** Real repair is incremental. You should see something — not a perfect transformation, but movement.
- **The person accepts outside help.** Therapy (individual, not couples, for manipulation situations), coaching, mentorship. Openness to outside perspective is a repair signal.
- **Stakes are not escalating.** If each cycle of conflict gets worse — more intense, more consequential, more costly to you — repair is probably not happening.
- **The relationship has resources beyond the current conflict.** Shared history, mutual care, practical support, aligned goals. This does not guarantee repair, but it provides the substrate repair works on.

### Factors making repair less likely

- **The repair attempts make things worse, not better.** If raising concerns leads to retaliation, it is not a communication problem.
- **The person's self-concept is threatened by the acknowledgment repair requires.** Some people cannot admit fault without feeling their core identity is attacked. Real repair requires acknowledgment; if acknowledgment is impossible, repair is impossible.
- **Rotating explanations.** The Three-Conversation Test from the [Manipulation vs. Miscommunication page](manipulation-hub-miscommunication-vs-manipulation.md). Stable positions can be engaged with; rotating explanations indicate the function is defense, not communication.
- **Escalation when challenged.** A person who reacts to gentle feedback with disproportionate anger, shutdown, punishment, or withdrawal is not in a repair-capable state. Repair requires the ability to tolerate discomfort; escalation indicates that threshold has already been crossed.
- **The pattern has a payoff for them.** Manipulation that gains the manipulator something (control, labor, money, attention, status) is reinforced every time it works. People rarely abandon behaviors that are working for them.
- **Institutional backing.** In workplace and institutional contexts, if the person causing harm is protected by the structure, individual repair is not the appropriate frame. You are not trying to fix a relationship; you are trying to navigate an institution.
- **You are doing all the work.** If repair is a one-sided project — you raise concerns, you adjust, you initiate repair, you manage their emotional responses — the relationship is not repairing. It is being maintained through your labor, which is a different thing.

### The trajectory test, repeated

The single most reliable signal for repairability is the trajectory of the relationship over time:

- **Getting better over time:** repair is happening, even if imperfectly
- **Static:** repair is not happening, but harm is not escalating either. Can sometimes remain stable; sometimes precedes decline.
- **Getting worse over time:** repair is not happening. This is not a reversal that can be fixed by better communication; it is a warning.

Genuine miscommunications and good-faith repair efforts produce improvement over time. Manipulation tends to escalate, because the manipulator's tactics become more aggressive as you develop resistance to the earlier ones. If you have been trying to repair for months or years and the relationship is worse than when you started, that is load-bearing information.

## 5. "Exit" Is Not One Thing

If the assessment points away from repair, the next question is what exit looks like. The mainstream framing treats exit as binary: you stay or you leave. The reality is more structured.

### Five forms of exit

**1. Full formal exit.** Ending the relationship completely. Divorce, quitting the job, cutting off contact with the family member, moving away. This is the most definitive form of exit, and it usually requires the most resources.

**2. Structural distance while maintaining contact.** Reducing the role of the person in your life without formally ending it. Moving out without divorcing. Taking a different job within the same company. Reducing family visits from weekly to twice a year. This is sometimes the right move when full exit is impossible due to shared children, economic dependency, or other binding factors.

**3. Functional minimization.** The relationship continues but you engage with it minimally. You respond to texts but do not initiate. You attend the mandatory family events but not the optional ones. You do the work but skip the social lunches. This does not end the relationship but it reduces the surface area on which harm can occur.

**4. Gradual disengagement.** A slow withdrawal of emotional investment, access, and time, conducted in a way that avoids triggering escalation. This is often the approach when the person would retaliate against explicit exit but cannot effectively resist a slow fade. It takes longer but can be safer.

**5. Strategic non-disclosure.** Continuing the relationship but protecting yourself by withholding information, documentation, and vulnerability. Not a full exit — you are still in the relationship — but the emotional and informational channel is closed. This is sometimes the right move during a resource-building phase when you are preparing for a fuller exit later.

Each of these is a legitimate form of exit. The right choice depends on your power and resources (see the [Power Differential page](manipulation-hub-power-differential.md)) and on what the other person would do in response to each form.

### The separation paradox, revisited

From the [Safety Assessment page](manipulation-hub-safety-assessment.md): **leaving is the highest-risk period in intimate partner violence situations. 44% of women murdered by an intimate partner had separated or were in the process of leaving** (Campbell et al., 2003).

This finding applies specifically to IPV contexts with lethality indicators — but the broader principle matters more widely: **visible exit can trigger escalation in any situation where the other person is invested in maintaining control.** A manipulator who loses access to their target may retaliate more aggressively than they ever did while the target was present. A toxic employer may provide a bad reference or sabotage your reputation. A controlling family member may mobilize other family members against you.

**Practical implication for exit selection:** the form of exit that is "cleanest" morally (full formal exit with clear statements) is often not the form of exit that is safest practically. Gradual disengagement, structural distance, and strategic non-disclosure can be safer for vulnerable targets even though they feel more compromised. The safest exit is the one that achieves the actual separation you need without triggering maximum retaliation.

If lethality indicators are present, this is not a hub decision. Contact an advocate for safety planning. NDVH: 1-800-799-7233 in the US, or see the [Crisis Resources Directory](manipulation-hub-crisis-resources.md) for other regions.

## 6. The Stages of Change

One of the most useful frameworks for thinking about exit is the **stages of change model** (Prochaska & DiClemente, 1983, *Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology* 51(3)). Originally developed for health behavior change, it has been applied specifically to leaving abusive relationships (Burke et al., 2004, *American Journal of Health Behavior* 28(2); Brown, 1997, *Violence Against Women* 3(1)).

The five stages:

| Stage | What It Is | What Is Useful at This Stage |
|------|-----------|----------------------------|
| **Precontemplation** | Not yet considering change. The situation may feel normal, tolerable, or inevitable. | Awareness-building. Reading pages like this one. Noticing patterns. No action required; information is the work. |
| **Contemplation** | Considering change, weighing costs and benefits. May feel stuck between options. | Decision frameworks, reality-check networks, time to think, professional consultation. The work is clarifying what you want and what the tradeoffs are. |
| **Preparation** | Committed to change, building toward action. Acquiring resources, making plans. | Documentation, resource building, advocate contact, safety planning, specific decisions about timing and logistics. |
| **Action** | Implementing change. Leaving, ending contact, changing the situation. | Execution support. Advocate backing, practical help, safe logistics. |
| **Maintenance** | Sustaining the change. Avoiding return to the old pattern. | Sustained support, managing stalking/post-separation abuse, rebuilding life, trauma processing. |

### What Burke et al. (2004) found

Burke and colleagues applied this framework to a sample of abused women. Their key finding: **approximately 95% of the sample were in precontemplation or contemplation stages at study entry.** They were not actively preparing to leave. Interventions that tried to push someone from precontemplation straight to action tended to fail.

**This has two implications that matter for autistic readers:**

**First: if you are reading this page and thinking "I am not ready to do anything about this yet" — that is a real and valid stage, and most people in your situation are in it, most of the time.** Precontemplation and contemplation are not failure states. They are where the majority of people in difficult situations live for months or years. The right question is not "why haven't I left yet?" It is "what can I do at my current stage?"

**Second: stages are not linear.** You can move from contemplation to preparation and back to contemplation several times. You can get to action and then return to maintenance of an old pattern. Progress is not cumulative. The model is useful for describing where you are right now, not for producing a success metric.

### What is useful at each stage, in more detail

**Precontemplation work:** Reading, noticing, internal tracking. You are not trying to decide anything. You are building the informational base from which a decision might eventually become possible. This page, the pattern pages, the pragmatics categories that help you understand what is happening to you — all of these are precontemplation-stage resources. There is no time pressure. You can read the same page five times over six months.

**Contemplation work:** Decision framework application. The manipulation-vs-miscommunication decision, the power and resource analysis, the repair assessment on this page. You are trying to clarify what is true about your situation and what the tradeoffs of different actions are. Reality-check networks become more useful at this stage — someone who can help you think through the decision without pushing you toward their preferred outcome.

**Preparation work:** Concrete moves that make action possible. Opening a separate bank account. Storing documents in a safe location. Making an appointment with a lawyer or advocate. Identifying where you would go if you left. Telling one trusted person in detail. Building the infrastructure that action would require.

**Action work:** Execution, with support. The details vary enormously by situation. If lethality indicators are present, this is advocate territory. If the situation is a workplace exit, it is a different set of logistics. If it is a gradual disengagement from a family relationship, it is different again.

**Maintenance work:** Sustaining the change. Managing post-separation contact or lack of contact. Rebuilding social resources that were constrained. Processing the grief and trauma that often only becomes fully accessible after exit. This is where trauma-focused therapy can begin to be useful in ways it was not useful while you were still in the situation (Herman, 1992 — safety precedes processing).

**Practical implication:** figure out what stage you are in and work at that stage. Do not try to skip stages. Do not judge yourself for being at the stage you are at. The goal is to move through the stages as they become possible, not to arrive at any stage by a specific date.

*Evidence quality: [MODERATE]. The stages of change model has face validity and has been applied to IPV with some quantitative support, but it was designed for individual health behaviors and its fit with a dyadic, socially embedded decision is debated. The Burke et al. 95% finding on precontemplation prevalence is from a single study and may not generalize.*

## 7. Autism-Specific Considerations

### The compliance training problem affects the repair assessment

Many autistic adults, having been trained their whole lives to accommodate and defer, will genuinely believe that "trying harder" will solve the problem — because "trying harder" has been the instruction their entire lives. The assessment of repairability can be distorted by this in specific ways:

- You may overestimate how repairable a situation is because you are used to adjusting yourself until friction reduces, and you assume that friction reduction = repair.
- You may underestimate the trajectory signal (things getting worse over time) because you are used to attributing worsening to your own failure to adjust enough.
- You may resist the conclusion that a situation is not repairable because accepting that conclusion means facing that your training does not apply to this situation.

**Practical implication:** if your gut says "I just need to try harder," test that by asking what "trying harder" has produced so far. If you have been trying harder for months or years and the pattern has not improved, the hypothesis that trying harder is the answer has been falsified. Continuing to try harder is not evidence of dedication; it is a trained response producing no result.

### Your alternatives may be thinner than they appear

The [Power Differential page](manipulation-hub-power-differential.md) covered this in detail. For the repair-or-exit decision specifically:

- **Autistic adults have elevated rates of economic dependency.** Unemployment and underemployment are common; alternatives to a current job or relationship may be genuinely thin.
- **Social alternatives may be thin.** The person you would lean on if the current relationship ended may not exist, or may be someone you cannot actually lean on.
- **Housing alternatives may be thin.** Moving into a new living situation involves pragmatic and sensory challenges that are harder for autistic adults than mainstream advice acknowledges.

**These are not reasons to stay in unrepairable situations.** They are reasons to acknowledge that the resource thin zone is real, that exit from it is slow work, and that preparation matters more for you than for most.

### The autistic tendency to delay decisions until you are certain

Many autistic adults have a strong preference for certainty before acting. This is often a strength — careful decision-making produces fewer errors than impulsive decision-making. But it can become a trap in the repair-or-exit question, because **you will never have certainty** about whether this is repairable. You are operating under uncertainty. You will have to make decisions before you know the answer.

**Practical implication:** apply the frameworks on this page repeatedly over time. Gather information. Observe. Consult. But do not wait for certainty before acting when certainty is not available. The decision framework exists to help you make good decisions without certainty, not to produce certainty you can wait for.

### The autistic strength in pattern recognition

As noted throughout this hub: pattern recognition over time is a relative strength for autistic cognition (Williams et al., 2018; extrapolated from systemizing research). This is the core strength the repair assessment relies on. You are not trying to decide whether this specific incident is repairable; you are tracking the pattern across many incidents over time.

**Practical implication:** use your strength. Do not try to decide during any single conversation. Observe. Note. Compare across time. Your slower decision-making is appropriate caution in exactly this situation. The impulsive "leave today" or the impulsive "commit to working it out today" are both wrong for you. The slow, pattern-based assessment is right.

### "It's not bad enough"

A specific autistic experience: because autistic people often have higher baseline discomfort from sensory and social friction, they may not realize that a situation is unusually bad — they may think "everyone experiences this level of difficulty, I'm just complaining more." The calibration against "normal" is distorted because autistic baseline is already higher than neurotypical baseline.

**Practical implication:** do not calibrate against your felt sense of whether the situation is "normal difficulty" or "unusually bad." Your felt sense is calibrated to a life that has had more difficulty in it than average. Calibrate against the frameworks: is there a pattern, is it repairable, what are the alternatives. Let the frameworks do the calibration your felt sense cannot.

## 8. Strategies by Conditional

The engage/exit decision leads to different strategic postures. These are not rigid prescriptions; they are starting points.

### If the evidence points to repairable (miscommunication, ignorance, or early-stage carelessness with responsive other party)

- **Commit to repair, knowing repair is work.** Repair is not automatic. It requires clear communication, patience, and willingness to engage over time.
- **Shift channels if needed.** If verbal exchanges keep producing mismatches, try written. If one kind of conversation keeps going wrong, try a different kind.
- **Name what you need explicitly.** Double empathy problems often come from unstated assumptions. Surfacing them is usually sufficient.
- **Give it time and measure trajectory.** Genuine repair produces improvement over time. If you are not seeing improvement after a reasonable period, the category may need to be reassessed.
- **Don't rush to exit.** Miscommunications and ignorance-based situations can look like manipulation when you first try to repair them. Give the repair a chance before concluding it is not working.

### If the evidence points to carelessness that may or may not repair

- **Attach consequences.** Carelessness without consequence continues. Make the careless behavior actually cost something.
- **Set a mental timeline.** How long are you willing to continue at the current level before concluding the situation is not going to change? The specific duration is yours to set, but having one prevents the drift of "I will try a little longer" extending into years.
- **Document the pattern.** If carelessness does not improve, documentation will be needed later. Start now.
- **Know what exit looks like if needed.** You do not have to plan full exit, but you should know what options exist so that decision is available when needed.

### If the evidence points to manipulation

- **Stop trying to repair through communication.** The Repair Test has already shown you that explanation makes things worse. Continuing to explain is wasting energy and providing new ammunition.
- **Do not announce that you have concluded it is manipulation.** Telling the manipulator that you have figured out what they are doing often triggers retaliation.
- **Start preparation-stage work.** Documentation, resource building, quiet external support. You are building the conditions for exit, whatever form of exit is viable.
- **Consult an advocate.** The [Crisis Resources Directory](manipulation-hub-crisis-resources.md) has specific lines for this. An advocate can help you identify resources you do not know exist and strategies for building them.
- **Choose the exit form that fits your power and resources.** Full formal exit is one option; gradual disengagement, structural distance, and functional minimization are others. The "cleanest" exit is rarely the safest.

### If you are not ready to decide

- **This is a valid stage.** Precontemplation and contemplation are where most people in difficult situations are, most of the time. You do not have to be further along than you are.
- **Do precontemplation/contemplation work.** Read. Notice. Compare across time. Consult trusted people who will not pressure you. Build your internal understanding of what is happening without committing to action.
- **Take care of yourself.** Burnout is the primary enemy at this stage. You need to preserve enough capacity to eventually do the harder work that later stages require.
- **Know that you are not failing.** The timeline for this work is measured in months and years, not weeks. You are not behind schedule; there is no schedule.
- **Do not let anyone rush you.** People who genuinely want to help will respect your pace. People who pressure you toward a specific answer are not helping; they are centering their own anxiety about your situation. Notice the difference.

### If you cannot tell whether it is repairable

- **Default to more observation.** Extending the assessment period costs little; premature decisions can cost a lot.
- **Apply the trajectory test specifically.** Is it getting better, staying the same, or getting worse? Over what time period?
- **Consult your reality-check network.** External perspectives often see trajectories the person inside cannot see.
- **Assume it is less repairable than you think when in the resource thin zone.** When you have few resources, being wrong about repairability in the direction of "it's fine" costs much more than being wrong in the direction of "it's not fine."
- **Do not make the decision all at once.** Staging is legitimate. "I will decide in three months" is a decision. "I will spend this month building one specific resource and decide about the next step then" is a decision. Not every decision has to resolve the entire question.

## 9. What This Page Cannot Do

- **Tell you whether your specific situation is repairable.** The frameworks describe factors; only you can see how they apply to your situation.
- **Tell you whether to stay or leave.** This is your decision, and it depends on factors this page cannot know — your specific relationships, your specific resources, your specific risks, your specific values. The frameworks are aids to your decision, not replacements for it.
- **Make the decision less hard.** The decision is hard because it is genuinely hard, not because you are missing some insight that would make it easy. No framework makes this easy. The frameworks make it more structured, which is different from making it easier.
- **Account for the thing you have not told anyone.** Most people in difficult situations have information they have not disclosed to anyone, including this hub and including themselves. The decision is made with that information, which is beyond anything a framework can address.
- **Replace an advocate, therapist, or trusted friend.** The frameworks are tools for thinking; people are support for acting. You need both.
- **Work when you are in crisis.** Frameworks require cognitive resources to apply. If you are in crisis, come back when you have more capacity. Until then, the [Crisis Resources Directory](manipulation-hub-crisis-resources.md) is where to go.

## 10. Key Takeaways

1. **Repairability depends on category.** Miscommunication and ignorance are often repairable; carelessness is sometimes repairable with consequences; manipulation is usually not repairable through victim effort.

2. **Real repair has three components.** Acknowledgment, behavior change, and safety to be affected. Partial repair that lacks one of these is not repair.

3. **The trajectory test is the most reliable signal.** Genuine repair produces improvement over time. Static or worsening trajectories indicate the situation is not repairing.

4. **"Try harder" does not work against manipulation.** The research consensus is clear. Victim effort does not end coercive control; exit (in some form) is usually the path to safety.

5. **"Exit" is not one thing.** Full formal exit, structural distance, functional minimization, gradual disengagement, and strategic non-disclosure are all legitimate forms of exit. The right choice depends on your power, resources, and the other person's likely response.

6. **Visible exit can trigger escalation.** The cleanest exit is rarely the safest. In situations with lethality indicators, do not plan exit alone — contact an advocate.

7. **The decision has stages.** Precontemplation, contemplation, preparation, action, maintenance. Most people in difficult situations are in the early stages for long periods. That is normal, not failure.

8. **Use your pattern recognition strength.** Do not try to decide in any single conversation. Observe over time. Your slower decision-making is appropriate to this context.

9. **Compliance training distorts the assessment in a specific direction.** If your gut says "I just need to try harder," test that against what trying harder has actually produced. If it has produced nothing, the hypothesis is wrong.

10. **You are not obligated to be certain before you act.** You will never have certainty about the repair-or-exit question. You can only make the best decision available with the information you have, and revise as new information comes in.
