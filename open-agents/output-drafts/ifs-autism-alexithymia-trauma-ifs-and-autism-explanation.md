# IFS and Autism — Literal Thinking, Sensory Profile, Masking, and Adaptations

## A note on what this page is and isn't

This is the autism page of an eleven-category deep dive on IFS. The previous page (Cat 6) covered alexithymia and the interoception problem. This page handles what is left when you subtract that — and there is a lot left. Roughly half of autistic adults are also alexithymic, but the autism-specific issues exist independently and matter even when interoceptive access is intact. Where this page brushes up against the alexithymia material, it points across rather than re-litigating.

The literature this page draws on splits into two registers. On the empirical side, the citations are solid: monotropism is grounded in Murray, Lesser, and Lawson's 2005 paper in *Autism* (vol 9 issue 2, pages 139–156); aphantasia in Adam Zeman's group at Exeter and the Dance et al. 2021 paper in *Consciousness and Cognition* connecting it to autistic traits; masking in the Hull/Mandy CAT-Q research; autistic flow theory in Heasman et al.'s 2024 *Journal for the Theory of Social Behaviour* paper. On the clinical-adaptations side, the citations are blogs, Substacks, podcast episodes, conference presentations, and one published trade book (Bergenfield, Sweezy, Malow, *Wired to Feel*, New Harbinger 2025). Peer-reviewed validation of any of the proposed adaptations does not yet exist. The page treats the empirical claims as established and the clinical adaptations as the convergent reports of named autistic IFS practitioners — credible because they converge across independent sources, not because they have been formally tested.

## The metaphor problem

IFS hands the client a metaphor: imagine your inner life as a collection of "parts," each with a role, a fear, and a relationship to a core "Self." For some autistic readers this lands cleanly. The model speaks of internal-state patterns with enough specificity to be operationally useful, names them, and gives them roles. Pattern recognition is an autistic strength; once oriented, autistic clients often catch the patterns of protectors and exiles quickly.

For other autistic readers — and sometimes for the same reader on different days — the metaphor creates two distinct problems.

The first is **reification anxiety**. If parts are presented as actually existing entities with their own desires and intentions, the literal-thinking response is to ask whether they are real. They cannot be examined empirically; the model cannot say what they are made of. If treated as literal entities, "parts" cross into territory that mainstream clinical psychology does not endorse. If treated as merely metaphor, then the agentic language ("the part wants to..."), which is pervasive in IFS speech, begins to feel false. Schwartz himself has been ambivalent on this — sometimes calling parts neural networks, sometimes treating them as functionally autonomous, sometimes (in the spiritually-framed extensions of IFS that Cat 11 examines) suggesting they may be more than that. The ambivalence is therapeutically flexible. For systems thinkers, it is a problem, because a systems thinker wants to know what the model claims before deciding whether to use it.

The second is **imprecision rejection**. Even if the entity question is bracketed, the metaphor's looseness can grate. The model speaks of "parts" as if their boundaries were knowable and stable, when in fact what is being identified — patterns of internal experience that cluster around particular triggers and behaviors — is constructed in the moment, partly by the client, partly by the therapist's prompts. A literal-thinking client can find this looseness uncomfortable in a way that does not appear in the IFS literature, because the model has been written largely by clinicians whose default cognitive style works fine with metaphor.

This is where concretizing adaptations come in. The neurodiversity-affirming corner of the IFS world has converged on a set of techniques that translate the model's vocabulary into something more literal-thinker-friendly without losing the model's mechanics. The Self Leadership Journey Substack's piece "Making IFS Accessible to People Who Think Literally" is one of the most direct articulations: instead of "parts," use descriptive labels like "Anxious Mode" or "Focused Zone." Use color-coded cards representing different states. Use physical transition cues — clapping, tapping — to mark state shifts. Use brain-science framing: state shifts correspond to distinct prefrontal cortex activity, intentional state shifts engage the parasympathetic nervous system, neural pathways strengthen with practice. Use personalized metaphors matched to the client's special interests — train enthusiasts can think of states as "tracks," computer users as "switching programs."

Bergenfield's *Wired to Feel* extends this further. Index cards (one part per card) externalize the model. Spreadsheets — rows of parts, columns for triggers, behaviors, fears, origins — work for systems thinkers. Neural-network reframing recasts parts as patterns of brain activity rather than as little people inside, removing the reification problem at the cost of some of the relational warmth the model normally generates.

What gets preserved across all these adaptations: the parts taxonomy (managers, firefighters, exiles), the protector-first sequencing, the unburdening process for parts that need it, and the relational stance of Self toward parts. What changes: the language, the access channel, the ontological commitments. The work is recognizably IFS; the surface texture is not.

## Monotropism: an asset that the model wasn't designed for

Monotropism is the autistic attentional style that Dinah Murray articulated in 1992 and formalized with Mike Lesser and Wenn Lawson in 2005. The thesis: autistic minds tend to channel attentional resources into a small number of interests at a time, filtering out competing stimuli. Mono = single, tropism = channel. Within the channel, attention can be unusually sustained and deep; outside it, attention can feel costly to recruit and switching can feel jarring.

Canonical IFS technique was not designed with monotropic minds in mind. The 6 Fs (Find, Focus, Flesh out, Feel toward, beFriend, Fears) are usually taught as a sequence the therapist moves through within a single session. Sessions sometimes attend to multiple parts; the structure of "let's check in with that part now and this other one later" is built into how IFS is paced.

For a monotropic client, this can be exactly wrong, and exactly right.

Wrong, because attention-shifting itself costs energy. A monotropic client who has finally settled into deep contact with one part may experience the therapist's "let's check in with another part now" prompt as a small disorientation, paid for in cognitive resources that could otherwise have gone to the work. Multiplied across a session, the cost adds up.

Right, because once a monotropic client is in attention with a part, the depth available is unusual. The Focus and beFriend steps benefit directly. A protector that might take a non-monotropic client several sessions to build relationship with can sometimes be reached in one for a monotropic client willing to dwell.

The pacing implication: fewer parts per session, deeper relationship. Some autistic-affirming practitioners have described working a single part across ten or twelve sessions in a row, allowing the relationship to develop without forced switching. Bergenfield in particular has written about this in clinical terms.

There is a related question — whether monotropic immersion is itself a Self-access route. Csikszentmihalyi's flow construct describes a state of intense, undistracted absorption that overlaps phenomenologically with what autistic people often report during deep engagement with a special interest. Heasman, Williams, Charura, Hamilton, Milton, and Murray formalized this in 2024 as "autistic flow theory." The framing is provocative: when a monotropic client is in flow inside their special interest, they may be experiencing a state with substantial overlap to what canonical IFS calls Self-energy. The 8 Cs may be more accessible from inside that state than from the canonical "step back from parts" instruction. This is currently a clinical hypothesis, not a tested protocol, but it is mentioned by enough autistic IFS practitioners — and theoretically grounded enough through the Heasman work — to deserve naming.

> **Evidence note**: The "special-interest immersion as Self-access route" claim has a validity rating of 7/10. The Heasman et al. 2024 autistic-flow framework is theoretically grounded and the clinical reports are credible, but no published study has tested whether the 8 Cs are reliably present during autistic-flow states or whether parts work conducted from a flow state produces the same therapeutic effects as canonical Self-access. Treat as a working hypothesis with theoretical support, not as established protocol.

## Masking and the "this might not be a part" question

A lifetime of masking — of suppressing stims, forcing eye contact, scripting conversations, rehearsing responses to unwritten social rules — leaves an autistic adult with a problem that the IFS model is not built for. When the client and therapist start mapping the inner world, what is being mapped?

The straightforward IFS answer is: those masking behaviors are manager parts. They are protective patterns that formed in response to social pressure; they have a positive intention (keep me from being punished for being autistic); they can be befriended, their fears understood, and over time they can relax in safer contexts. This is a real and useful framing. Hull and Mandy's research program with the CAT-Q has documented that higher self-reported masking correlates with worse mental health — depression, anxiety, autistic burnout. Treating the masking patterns as parts that have been overworked, in a system that needs them less now, is a clinically meaningful move.

But there is a problem if the framing stops there. Some of what looks like a "part" is actually autism — the underlying processing style itself. A pattern-first, literal-direct, monotropic processing style is hardware. It is not a manager that developed under social pressure; it is how the brain works. If a therapist treats it as a part to befriend, hear the fears of, and over time release, the implicit goal is for the client to become less autistic. That goal is incompatible with neurodiversity-affirming practice, and it pathologizes the neurology.

The framework that has crystallized across the autistic-affirming IFS practitioners — Sanni Tsuruoka, Emma at IFS & Neurodiversity, Anchored in Self, Stroud Therapy, the IFS Telehealth Collective, and Bergenfield in *Wired to Feel* — is the **hardware/software distinction**. Autism is hardware: the underlying neurology, the operating system. Parts are software: patterns that formed in response to experience, including the experience of being autistic in a non-autistic world. Hardware is not unburdened. Software can be.

The boundary case is masking itself, because it is layered. The suppression rule (hide stims when colleagues are around) is software, a protector pattern, workable in IFS. The processing style underneath (literal-direct communication, pattern-first attention, monotropic engagement) is hardware. The work, when done well, befriends the masking-protector and over time relaxes it in safe contexts, while leaving the underlying autistic processing style alone.

The test: imagine the work fully complete, the protector at rest. Do you imagine being more comfortably autistic, or being less autistic? If the first, you've been working on software and the work was aimed correctly. If the second, you've been aimed at hardware and the work was wrong from the start.

This sounds clean but in practice the layers are tangled. A client who has masked for forty years may genuinely struggle to know where the suppression ends and the underlying processing style begins. Masking deconstruction can be its own slow process, separate from any specific parts work. Some autistic-affirming therapists frame this as an unmasking phase in its own right — not parts work in the strict sense, but a project of recognizing which patterns are doing protective work and which are simply the client's autism finally being allowed to show up.

The risk of getting this wrong cuts both ways. A canonical-IFS therapist without autism literacy may treat every autistic feature as a part, gradually sending the client the message that being autistic is the problem. An autism-only-affirming therapist may swing the other way and refuse to work with masking-protectors at all, leaving overworked managers in place because every behavior gets categorized as "just autism, don't touch it." Honest work threads the needle. Some patterns are software, some are hardware, some are both at different layers, and the test for which is which is whether the work points toward more comfort in autistic neurology or away from it.

## Sensory profile considerations

Sensory regulation is not really IFS's territory. It belongs more to occupational therapy and to sensory-integration approaches. But it shows up in IFS sessions because therapy happens in rooms and rooms have lighting, sound, temperature, and seating arrangements, and autistic clients often need different parameters than the rooms therapists work in were designed for.

The practical issues: cool-white or blue overhead fluorescents are particularly hard for many autistic adults — warmer 2200–3000K and dimmable lighting helps. HVAC hum, traffic noise, and fluorescent buzz are background noise for some clients and active dysregulators for autistic ones. Sitting still in a chair facing the therapist may not be the regulating posture; pacing, fidgeting, holding objects, and side-by-side seating sometimes work better. Sustained eye contact during emotionally-loaded conversation costs cognitive resources that could otherwise go to the actual parts work. Telehealth, despite IFS's traditional preference for in-person work, often turns out to be sensorily simpler — no commute, controlled environment, ability to pace the session physically.

The IFS-specific failure mode worth flagging: **sensory dysregulation framed as "a part is activated."** When a client becomes distressed mid-session, the IFS instinct is to ask what part is up. Sometimes that is the right question. Sometimes the client is being dysregulated by the room itself — a fluorescent flickering, the AC unit cycling on, ambient noise rising — and the cause is environmental, not internal. Treating sensory overload as a parts event misses the actual cause and adds confusion. The corrective is simple in principle: ask first whether the room is OK, before asking whether something inside is activating. Bergenfield, in *Wired to Feel*, frames sensory regulation as a precondition for the relational work IFS does, not as content of that work.

The genuinely interesting cases are when a part has been formed around a specific sensory pattern — a part that responds to a tone of voice that resembles a parent's, or a part that activates in fluorescent lighting that resembles a school the client was bullied in. Those are sensory triggers for parts and the parts work is appropriate. The distinction is whether the sensory event is the cause (regulate the room) or the trigger for an internal pattern (work with the part).

## Aphantasia and the visualization step

A subset of autistic clients — and a subset of clients in general — cannot form mental images. Aphantasia, in its complete form, is a lifelong absence of voluntary visual imagery; in its moderate form, the imagery is present but very weak. Adam Zeman's group coined "congenital aphantasia" in 2015. The estimated population prevalence is roughly 0.7% complete plus 2.6% moderate, so around 3% of the general population. Dance et al.'s 2021 study in *Consciousness and Cognition* matched 118 aphantasics to controls and found higher self-reported autistic traits in the aphantasics, particularly in imagination and social-skills subscales — suggesting (but not proving) that aphantasia is somewhat over-represented among autistic populations. Wei et al.'s 2025 *Autism Research* review of mental imagery in autism finds substantial heterogeneity: some autistic individuals have strong visual imagery, some have weak imagery, some are aphantasic, some are hyperphantasic.

The IFS problem: the third F, **Flesh out**, asks "what does this part look like?" What does it wear, how old does it seem, what is its expression. This presupposes mental imagery. For an aphantasic client, the question is unanswerable in its literal form.

The practical adaptations are scattered across clinician blogs (the IFS Telehealth Collective's "Supporting Your Neurotype with Internal Family Systems" piece is the closest thing to a canonical reference) and one widely-shared Quora thread by an aphantasic IFS practitioner. The available substitutions:

- **Felt-sense substitution.** "Where in the body do you notice this part? What is its texture, weight, temperature?" This works for aphantasic clients with intact interoception. It fails for the aphantasia + alexithymia intersection, which is exactly where this user sits.
- **Cognitive description.** "If you could describe this part in words without picturing it, what would you say about it?" This works for verbal-cognitive access — a written or spoken description that does not require imagery.
- **Auditory access.** Some aphantasic clients do have inner speech — the inner-voice channel is sometimes intact when the inner-picture channel is not. "What does this part sound like, what does it say, what tone does it speak in?"
- **Externalized representation.** Drawing the part on paper without "seeing it internally" is possible — many aphantasic people draw fine, they simply construct the drawing from concepts rather than copying an internal image. Choosing an object that represents the part. Writing about it in long-form journal-style prose. This is where this user's strength sits — written processing carries the work.
- **Symbolic placeholder.** Assigning the part a name, a category (manager, firefighter, exile), and a function description, without any sensory texture at all. The model's mechanics survive the substitution; what changes is the texture of access, not the procedural skeleton.

The hardest case is aphantasia + alexithymia + literal thinking together. No mental imagery, no reliable interoceptive read, no easy comfort with metaphor. The model's standard channels — visual, somatic, emotional — are all attenuated or blocked. What remains: cognitive description, written processing, externalized representation, and symbolic placeholder. This is unusual. The literature does not give it substantial treatment. Bergenfield's book covers the sensory-surplus side of autism in depth and the IFS Telehealth Collective post addresses neurotype adaptations briefly, but neither walks through the specific compound case.

> **Evidence note**: This is a research gap the validation explicitly flagged. Substantive treatment of aphantasia + IFS adaptation does not exist in peer-reviewed literature; the available material is in clinician blogs, a Telehealth Collective post, and scattered Quora-level personal reports. The substitutions named here are reasonable extrapolations from the available material, not validated protocols. A reader at this intersection has more honest options than the literature implies, but should expect to be doing some of this work without much formal guidance.

## The "Autistic Self" question

Canonical IFS makes a strong claim. Self is universal. It is undamaged by trauma. It is present in everyone. It is characterized by the eight Cs — curiosity, calm, compassion, clarity, courage, confidence, creativity, connectedness. Healing happens when Self builds relationships with parts. When a client cannot find Self, the model says it is because a part is in the way, and the work is to invite the part to step back.

The autistic-affirming corner of the IFS world has been the loudest critic of this universalist claim, and the most productive in proposing alternatives.

Sarah Bergenfield, who became the first openly autistic person to present at the IFS Conference in 2022, introduced the concept of the **Autistic Self**. The argument, summarized in the PARTS & SELF article that grew from her conference work and developed further in *Wired to Feel*: in an autistic system, **everyone is autistic** — parts and Self alike. Self is not a neurotypical visitor inside an autistic mind. It is autistic too. The 8 Cs are still available, but they are expressed through autistic neurology rather than around it.

Emma at the IFS & Neurodiversity Substack — an IFS therapist, clinical psychologist, and AuDHDer — takes a closely related but distinguishable position. In "Can Self Be Autistic?" she argues that Self is universal and shines through all neurotypes, but the system's neurology shapes how Self is expressed. She uses a prism metaphor: Self is white light, individual neurobiology is the prism, the spectrum that emerges is genuinely diverse without changing the source. The 8 Cs remain present in autistic people; what changes is their texture.

These are independent positions and worth examining separately. Bergenfield's "everyone in the system is autistic" goes further than Emma's "Self universal but expressed through neurotype." Bergenfield is making a stronger ontological claim about Self in autistic minds. Emma's position is less metaphysically ambitious — Self is what canonical IFS describes; what's autistic is the prism, not the light.

What both positions push back against is the idea that the 8 Cs as canonically described are neuro-neutral. Some autistic IFS practitioners argue the 8 Cs were articulated by a non-autistic clinician working with non-autistic clients, and that "calm" and "connectedness" in particular carry implicit neurotypical assumptions. Calm in a polytropic mind looks like spacious stillness, gentle breathing, soft attention. Calm in a monotropic mind may look like focused immersion in a single task, with the surrounding world tuned out — phenomenologically very different, possibly equally Self-energy in IFS terms. Connectedness in canonical Western therapeutic terms often implies eye contact, verbal exchange, and reciprocal small talk. Connectedness for an autistic person may happen through shared focus on an object, parallel play, rhythmic attunement, or extended deep conversation about a special interest.

The intermediate honest position: the 8 Cs are flexible enough to admit autistic expression. Curiosity, courage, creativity, and compassion translate cleanly across neurotypes; the texture of calm, clarity, and connectedness shifts more. Whether Self itself is autistic in autistic systems — Bergenfield's stronger claim — or whether Self is universal but expressed autistically — Emma's intermediate claim — is genuinely unsettled.

## Autistic IFS practitioners' adaptations: a practical inventory

The autistic-IFS adaptation literature does not yet exist as a formal canon. It exists as a set of converging articulations across a small number of practitioners writing in non-peer-reviewed venues. The convergence is itself meaningful — when Bergenfield, Sanni, Emma, the Self Leadership Journey author, the AutSide writer, and the IFS Telehealth Collective all arrive at similar conclusions independently, that suggests these adaptations are tracking real clinical work rather than a single idiosyncratic position.

**Sarah Bergenfield** (sarahbergenfield.com) — Certified Level 3 IFS Practitioner, Master's in Embodiment Studies, doctoral student in Somatic Psychology at California Institute of Integral Studies, assists in IFS Institute Level 1/2/3 trainings. Her central contributions: the Autistic Self concept (2022 IFS Conference, "Using the IFS Lens to Include the Autistic Experience in Therapeutic Spaces"); the autism-as-sensory-surplus reframe in *Wired to Feel* (2025, co-authored with Martha Sweezy PhD and Beth Ann Malow MD foreword, published by New Harbinger); the embodied / somatic-psychology-informed approach to working with autistic clients. Her position is the closest thing the autism + IFS adaptation literature has to a flagship articulation, and the *Wired to Feel* book gives that articulation formal IFS-Institute-adjacent legitimacy.

**Sanni Tsuruoka — IFS with Sanni** (ifswithsanni.com) — IFS therapist working in Europe and Asia. Her central contribution: the clearest articulation of the hardware/software distinction. Direct quote: "Autism is part of the hardware: the foundational wiring of your system, how your brain processes information, how you sense the world, how you relate to time, energy, and connection. It is damaging to neurodivergent folk to call neurology a part." Her practice frames neurodivergent-affirming IFS as a coherent approach, not just canonical IFS with adjustments.

**Emma at IFS & Neurodiversity** (ifsneurodiversity.substack.com) — IFS therapist, clinical psychologist, AuDHDer. Substantial Substack writing on IFS through a neurodiversity-affirming lens. Specific pieces include "Can Self Be Autistic?", "The Double Empathy Problem: When Understanding Goes Both Ways," "Parts that Feel It All: Living with Mirror-Touch Synaesthesia, Autism, and a Life of Sensory Overload," and "When Silence Speaks: Autism, IFS, and Other Ways of Being Heard." Her central contributions: the prism metaphor for Self; the Self-universal-but-expressed-autistically position; substantive engagement with the double-empathy problem (Damian Milton's framing) in IFS terms.

**The Journey to Self Leadership Substack** (selfleadershipjourney.substack.com) — "Making IFS Accessible to People Who Think Literally" is the most direct article-level guide for literal-thinking adaptations. Concrete suggestions include descriptive labels rather than abstract "parts," color-coded cards, physical transition cues, brain-science explanations, and personalized metaphors matched to special interests.

**The AutSide Substack** (autside.substack.com) — "Internal Architecture: Literal Thinking and the Missing Bridge" examines the relationship between gestalt processing in autism and how meaning organizes itself in autistic minds. The series treats the DSM not as a deficit inventory but as a record of how autistic cognition develops over time.

**IFS Telehealth Collective** (ifstherapyonline.com) — "Supporting Your Neurotype with Internal Family Systems" is the closest thing to a canonical reference for adapting IFS across multiple neurotypes (autism, ADHD, alexithymia, aphantasia, sensory sensitivities). Practical and pragmatic; lower theoretical commitment than Bergenfield or Emma.

**Anchored in Self** (anchoredinself.co.uk) — "IFS and the Ecology of Difference: Understanding Autism Beyond the DSM" articulates the hardware/neurology framing in similar terms to Sanni.

**Stroud Therapy / Natasha Wilson** — "IFS and Neurodifferences" similarly articulates the hardware/software distinction.

**Embrace Autism, Neurodivergent Insights** — broader autism resources that touch on IFS occasionally; not primary sources for IFS-specific adaptations but useful for broader autism context.

## Are these adaptations still IFS, or something else?

A reader who has tracked the modifications proposed across this page might reasonably ask whether what is being practiced is still IFS or whether it has become something different that uses IFS vocabulary.

The honest answer is that it is mostly still IFS, with substantive constraints added.

What gets preserved: the parts taxonomy (managers, firefighters, exiles), the protector-first / earn-permission-to-meet-exile sequencing, the unburdening process for parts that need it, the relational stance of Self toward parts, the no-bad-parts therapeutic frame, the witnessing-of-exiles work, the basic mechanics of how change happens.

What gets added or modified: the hardware/software distinction (not in canonical IFS literature); the "Self can be autistic" or "Autistic Self" framings (substantive modifications of the universalist Self claim); externalized practices alongside in-sight access; special-interest immersion as an alternative Self-access route; explicit "do not unburden autism" as a constraint; honoring monotropic pacing modifies the typical 6 Fs sequence; concretizing language adjustments for literal thinkers; sensory regulation as an explicit prerequisite rather than as an implicit assumption.

The accurate framing is **IFS with neurodiversity-affirming adaptations**. The work is sufficiently within the IFS frame to count as IFS — a Level 3 practitioner like Bergenfield is doing IFS, not something else. The constraints added are not in canonical Level 1/2/3 training material as of 2025, but they are also not in tension with most of the canonical material. The most significant tension is between the universal-Self claim and the Autistic Self / Self-universal-but-expressed-autistically positions, and even there the difference may be more about emphasis than incompatibility.

Whether the IFS Institute will absorb these adaptations into standard training over time is an open question. Bergenfield's role as a Level 1/2/3 trainer-assistant suggests some incorporation is happening informally. Whether it becomes formal — whether neurodiversity-affirming framing becomes part of the canonical model — is a question for the next several years of IFS Institute curriculum development.

## What the user, specifically, might find useful

The user this deep dive is for is autistic, suspected alexithymic, with a complex-trauma history. The user is a systems thinker. The user is working with a therapist. The user processes through writing. With those specifics in mind, the practical synthesis from the adaptation literature looks something like the following.

**Establish the hardware/software framing with the therapist explicitly.** Before any parts work, agree on which features are autism (hardware, off-limits) and which are protective patterns (software, workable). The test — "would resolution make me more comfortably autistic, or less autistic?" — is a usable lens. If a therapist treats every feature as a part to work with, raise the question. The neurodiversity-affirming framing is convergent across multiple autistic IFS practitioners but is not yet universal training content.

**Choose access channels that work, in combination.** The user's profile likely makes purely visual or purely somatic access difficult. Cognitive description, written processing, and externalized representation are likely to carry the work. Index cards or spreadsheets that map parts in tabular form align with systems-thinking strengths. The model's mechanics survive translation; what changes is the channel, not the procedural skeleton.

**Honor monotropic pacing.** Resist the urge to move quickly across multiple parts in a session. Sustained dwell-time on a single part across multiple sessions is often more productive. If transitions feel costly, name that to the therapist; pacing built around the user's attentional flow is a reasonable accommodation.

**Take aphantasia seriously if it applies.** If mental imagery is absent or weak, the visualization-dependent form of the Flesh out step is structurally blocked. Substitute: cognitive description, auditory access if inner voice is intact, externalized representation through writing or drawing, symbolic placeholder if everything else is attenuated.

**Attend to sensory regulation as a precondition.** Lighting, sound, posture, eye contact — get these working before doing parts work. If something goes wrong mid-session, ask whether the room is OK before asking what part is activated. The "is this sensory or is this internal" distinction is a real one and worth raising explicitly.

**Recognize special-interest immersion as a possible Self-access route.** Canonical IFS instructs the client to step back from parts to access Self. For a monotropic client, deep immersion in a special interest may produce a state with substantial overlap to what IFS calls Self-energy. This is not yet an established protocol, but it is a hypothesis with theoretical grounding (Heasman et al. 2024) and clinical reports — worth experimenting with.

**Lean on pattern recognition as the strength it is.** Autistic pattern recognition is an asset for protector mapping. Once oriented to the parts taxonomy, the user is likely to catch patterns of managers and firefighters quickly. Trust that.

**Read Bergenfield directly.** *Wired to Feel* is the most substantive published treatment of this intersection. Read it in addition to canonical IFS material. The Telehealth Collective's "Supporting Your Neurotype" piece is a useful pragmatic reference. Emma's IFS & Neurodiversity Substack is worth following for ongoing thinking.

**Notice when the literature is thin and don't pretend otherwise.** The aphantasia + alexithymia + literal thinking + complex trauma intersection is the hardest case. The available literature gives partial guidance, not full guidance. Some of the work the user does will be more original than the published material.

## Common misconceptions

**"Autism is a part to work with."** This is the most common and most damaging misframing. Autism is neurotype/hardware. Patterns that formed in response to being autistic in a non-autistic world are parts (software). Treating autism itself as a part to unburden pathologizes the neurology. The hardware/software distinction is not yet in canonical IFS Institute training material but is convergent across multiple autistic-affirming IFS practitioners.

**"Autistic clients can't do IFS because of metaphor difficulties."** Many autistic clients do IFS extremely well — pattern recognition is an asset for protector mapping, and many literal-thinking clients take to the model cleanly with minimal adaptation. Some need concretization, some don't. Heterogeneity is the rule.

**"Masking parts and autism are the same thing."** Masking is a software pattern (a protective rule that suppresses autistic expression in costly contexts). The autistic processing style underneath the mask is hardware. Befriending the masking-protector and leaving the underlying processing style alone is the honest move.

**"Aphantasia means IFS won't work."** Aphantasia blocks the visualization-dependent form of the Flesh out step. Substitutions exist (cognitive description, externalization, somatic mapping if interoception is intact). The model is more flexible than its standard presentation suggests, though the literature on this specific adaptation is thin.

**"Self looks the same in everyone."** This is the canonical IFS claim; it is contested by the autistic-IFS literature. The intermediate position — Self is universal but expressed through neurotype — is articulated by Emma at IFS & Neurodiversity. The stronger position — in an autistic system, Self too is autistic — is Bergenfield's. Both are worth holding in mind.

**"Sensory overload is just a part being activated."** Sometimes it is. Sometimes the room is the problem. Asking "is the room OK?" before "what part is activated?" is a low-cost diagnostic that prevents misattribution.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Monotropism | Tendency to channel attentional resources into a small number of interests at a time, filtering competing stimuli (Murray, Lesser, Lawson 2005) |
| Hardware/software distinction | Reframe in which autism = neurotype = hardware (not workable in IFS) and parts = patterns formed under pressure = software (workable) |
| Masking | Strategies autistic people develop to appear neurotypical (also called camouflaging) |
| Aphantasia | Inability to form voluntary mental imagery (Zeman 2015) |
| Autistic Self (Bergenfield) | Concept that in an autistic system, all parts and Self are autistic |
| Autistic flow | Heasman et al. (2024) reformulation of Csikszentmihalyi's flow grounded in monotropic immersion |
| Reification anxiety | Worry that parts are being treated as actual entities when they may not be |
| 6 Fs | Find, Focus, Flesh out, Feel toward, beFriend, Fears — IFS sequence for getting to know a protector |
| 8 Cs | Curiosity, calm, compassion, clarity, courage, confidence, creativity, connectedness — canonical Self qualities |
| CAT-Q | Camouflaging Autistic Traits Questionnaire (Hull et al.) |
| Double empathy problem | Damian Milton's framing of mutual misunderstanding between autistic and non-autistic people; not just a one-way deficit |

## Further reading

- Bergenfield, Sweezy, Malow — *Wired to Feel: Autism as a Condition of Sensory Surplus* (New Harbinger, 2025) — most substantive book-length treatment of autism + IFS adaptation
- IFS & Neurodiversity Substack (Emma) — ongoing writing on IFS through a neurodiversity-affirming lens
- IFS Telehealth Collective — "Supporting Your Neurotype with Internal Family Systems" — practical reference for adapting IFS across neurotypes
- Murray, Lesser, Lawson (2005) — original monotropism paper; foundational for understanding the attentional-style implications
- Heasman et al. (2024) — autistic flow theory; theoretical grounding for special-interest immersion as a Self-access route
- Hull and Mandy's CAT-Q research on masking — relevant for understanding masking-protectors and their mental-health correlates
