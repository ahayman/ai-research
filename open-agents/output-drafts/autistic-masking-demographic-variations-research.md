# Autistic Masking/Camouflaging: Demographic Variations

## Research Focus
How masking/camouflaging varies across demographics: gender, age/development, race/culture, late-diagnosed individuals, and LGBTQ+ autistic populations.

---

## 1. Gender Differences in Masking

### 1.1 Foundational Evidence: Women/AFAB Individuals Mask More Than Men/AMAB

**Lai, M.-C., Lombardo, M.V., Ruigrok, A.N.V., Chakrabarti, B., Auyeung, B., Szatmari, P., Happe, F., & Baron-Cohen, S. (2017). Quantifying and exploring camouflaging in men and women with autism. *Autism, 21*(6), 690-702.**
- DOI: 10.1177/1362361316671012
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27899710/
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5536256/
- **Methodology**: First study to operationally quantify camouflaging. Measured camouflaging as the discrepancy between "external" behavioral presentation (ADOS scores) and "internal" status (autistic traits via AQ + social cognition via Reading the Mind in the Eyes Test).
- **Sample**: Adults with autism (exact N available in full text; included both men and women with ASD diagnoses).
- **Key finding**: Women with autism had significantly higher camouflaging scores than men with autism, with a large effect size (Cohen's d = 0.98). Substantial variability existed in both groups.
- **Gender-specific associations**: Greater camouflaging was associated with more depressive symptoms in men but with better signal-detection sensitivity in women.
- **Significance**: Established the "discrepancy method" as a quantitative approach to measuring camouflaging and has been the methodological foundation for subsequent research.

**Hull, L., Lai, M.-C., Baron-Cohen, S., Allison, C., Smith, P., Petrides, K.V., & Mandy, W. (2020). Gender differences in self-reported camouflaging in autistic and non-autistic adults. *Autism, 24*(2), 352-363.**
- DOI: 10.1177/1362361319864804
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31319684/
- **Sample**: 306 autistic adults + 472 non-autistic adults.
- **Methodology**: Used the Camouflaging Autistic Traits Questionnaire (CAT-Q) to measure self-reported camouflaging.
- **Key findings**:
  - Autistic females demonstrated significantly higher total camouflaging scores than autistic males (partial eta-squared = 0.08).
  - Autistic females scored higher on Masking (partial eta-squared = 0.05) and Assimilation (partial eta-squared = 0.06) subscales, but NOT on the Compensation subscale.
  - No gender difference in camouflaging was found for non-autistic people.
  - Non-binary sample was underpowered, so no conclusions were drawn for that group.
- **Critical implication**: Gender differences in camouflaging are specific to the autistic population, not a general population phenomenon.

**Schuck, R.K., Flores, R.E., & Fung, L.K. (2019). Brief report: Sex/gender differences in symptomology and camouflaging in adults with autism spectrum disorder. *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 49*(6), 2597-2604.**
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30945091/
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6753236/
- **Sample**: 17 males and 11 females with ASD.
- **Methodology**: Replicated Lai et al. (2017) discrepancy approach; measured AQ scores and camouflaging levels.
- **Key findings**:
  - Camouflaging was more common in females with ASD than males.
  - Camouflaging correlated negatively with emotional expressivity in females but not males.
  - ASD symptoms (measured by AQ) trended higher in females than males, approaching significance.
  - Camouflaging was not associated with social phobia.

### 1.2 The "Female Autism Phenotype" and Camouflaging

**Hull, L., Petrides, K.V., & Mandy, W. (2020). The female autism phenotype and camouflaging: A narrative review. *Review Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 7*, 306-317.**
- DOI: 10.1007/s40489-020-00197-9
- Link: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40489-020-00197-9
- **Methodology**: Narrative review synthesizing existing literature on the female autism phenotype.
- **Key findings**:
  - Evidence supports the existence of a distinct female autism phenotype, including: differences in social difficulties (particularly in maintaining rather than initiating relationships); interests that tend to be more relational/social in nature (e.g., animals, fiction, celebrities rather than trains, numbers); and higher co-occurrence of internalizing disorders.
  - Females express autism in ways that do not meet current diagnostic criteria, which are based largely on male presentations.
  - Camouflaging may partially explain why the female phenotype is underrecognized.

**Tubio-Fungueirino, M., Cruz, S., Sampaio, A., Carracedo, A., & Fernandez-Prieto, M. (2021). Social camouflaging in females with autism spectrum disorder: A systematic review. *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 51*(7), 2190-2199.**
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32926304/
- Link: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10803-020-04695-x
- **Methodology**: Systematic review of 13 studies on camouflaging specifically in autistic females.
- **Key findings**:
  - Camouflaging abilities may be associated with self-control, empathy, and/or feedback abilities.
  - Motivators include societal expectations and feelings of loneliness or isolation.
  - Consequences include late diagnosis and negative emotions.
  - Camouflaging is an adaptive mechanism for females with ASD, despite negative implications.

### 1.3 How Masking Leads to Later/Missed Diagnosis in Women

**Diagnostic gender ratio**: Historically estimated at 4:1 (male:female); modern studies suggest an adjusted ratio closer to 3:1, with some estimates as low as 2:1 when camouflaging is accounted for.

**Key mechanisms of missed diagnosis**:
- Standardized instruments (e.g., ADOS) lack sensitivity to subtle female presentations, particularly in those with average or above-average IQ who engage in masking.
- Females' self-report of autistic traits is much higher than when measured by observational measures such as the ADOS, indicating the instruments fail to detect masked traits.
- Three out of four female participants in qualitative studies reported that camouflaging directly impacted their access to diagnosis, compared to only one out of four males.

**Misdiagnosis patterns in women**:
- Autistic women (31.7%) reported perceived misdiagnoses more frequently than men (16.7%).
- Women were specifically more likely to report perceived misdiagnoses of personality disorders (especially BPD), anxiety disorders, and mood disorders.
- 77% of autistic females receive at least one psychiatric diagnosis (vs. 62% of autistic males).
- BPD is a common misdiagnosis for autistic women; in most cases it was later felt to have been incorrect.
- Approximately 23% of people diagnosed with anorexia nervosa are autistic.

**Source**: PMC article on misdiagnosis - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11001629/

### 1.4 Dean et al. (2017) - Girls' Social Camouflaging on the Playground

**Dean, M., Harwood, R., & Kasari, C. (2017). The art of camouflage: Gender differences in the social behaviors of girls and boys with autism spectrum disorder. *Autism, 21*(6), 678-689.**
- DOI: 10.1177/1362361316671845
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27899709/
- **Methodology**: Observational study of children aged ~7 years on the playground, comparing social behaviors of autistic and neurotypical boys and girls.
- **Key findings**:
  - Girls with ASD used compensatory behaviors such as staying in close proximity to peers and weaving in and out of activities, which masked their social challenges.
  - Autistic boys, by contrast, played alone, making their social ostracism much easier to detect by observers.
  - Girls (but NOT boys) with autism appeared similarly to their neurotypical counterparts in terms of observable playground behavior, providing direct evidence of greater behavioral camouflaging in autistic girls.
- **Critical clinical implication**: "If practitioners look for social isolation on the playground when identifying children with social challenges, then our findings suggest that girls with ASD will continue to be left unidentified."

### 1.5 Bargiela et al. (2016) - Late-Diagnosed Women

**Bargiela, S., Steward, R., & Mandy, W. (2016). The experiences of late-diagnosed women with autism spectrum conditions: An investigation of the female autism phenotype. *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 46*(10), 3281-3294.**
- DOI: 10.1007/s10803-016-2872-8
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27457364/
- **Sample**: 14 women with ASC, aged 22-30, all diagnosed in late adolescence or adulthood.
- **Methodology**: Framework Analysis of semi-structured interviews.
- **Key themes identified**:
  1. **"Pretending to be normal"**: Women gave in-depth accounts of actively masking their autistic characteristics in social settings.
  2. **Gender and diagnostic barriers**: Participants described how their gender led various professionals to miss or dismiss their ASC.
  3. **Identity conflicts**: Women reported conflicts between ASC and traditional feminine identity expectations.
  4. **Sexual vulnerability**: 9 out of 14 participants reported rape by romantic partners and/or strangers, reflecting specific vulnerabilities from being female with undiagnosed ASC.
- **Conclusion**: Training would improve teachers' and clinicians' recognition of ASC in females, enabling timely identification to mitigate risks and promote wellbeing.

### 1.6 Suicidality Risk

**Cassidy, S.A., Bradley, L., Shaw, R., & Baron-Cohen, S. (2018). Risk markers for suicidality in autistic adults. *Molecular Autism, 9*, 42.**
- DOI: 10.1186/s13229-018-0226-4
- Link: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13229-018-0226-4
- **Sample**: 164 autistic adults.
- **Key finding**: Camouflaging was identified as a unique risk marker for suicidality in autistic adults. Additional risk markers included unmet support needs.
- **Gender disparity**: Autistic women are at more than 8x the risk of suicidality compared to non-autistic women, whereas autistic men are at less than 2x the risk compared to non-autistic men.

**Cassidy, S.A., Gould, K., Townsend, E., Pelton, M., Robertson, A.E., & Rodgers, J. (2020). Is camouflaging autistic traits associated with suicidal thoughts and behaviours? Expanding the interpersonal psychological theory of suicide in an undergraduate student sample. *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 50*(10), 3638-3648.**
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31820344/
- **Key finding**: Camouflaging autistic traits was associated with increased risk of experiencing thwarted belongingness and lifetime suicidality.

## 2. Children vs. Adults: Development of Masking

### 2.1 When Children Start Masking and Why

**Dean et al. (2017)** (see Section 1.4 above): Documented observable camouflaging in girls as young as 7 years old on the playground.

**Livingston, L.A., Shah, P., & Happe, F. (2019). Compensatory strategies below the behavioural surface in autism: A qualitative study. *The Lancet Psychiatry, 6*(9), 766-777.**
- DOI: 10.1016/S2215-0366(19)30224-X
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6706698/
- **Sample**: 136 adults (online survey, qualitative analysis).
- **Key findings on developmental onset**:
  - External pressures to compensate changed over the lifespan: higher in adolescence and lower in older age.
  - Compensation was necessary to avoid rejection and ostracism, and often stemmed from bullying or pressure to conform from a young age.
  - Compensatory strategies have both positive outcomes (social integration) and negative ones (poor mental health, barrier to diagnosis).
  - Distinguished compensation (generating new social behaviors) from masking (regulating/suppressing existing behaviors).

**General developmental timeline** (from clinical and research sources):
- Masking tends to become more evident in later elementary years (ages 8-10), middle school, and beyond, as social awareness and peer pressure increase.
- Between ages 2-9, if masking becomes a primary coping strategy, it can lead to anxiety, exhaustion, and low self-esteem.
- Children as young as 10 have been documented engaging in camouflaging behavior that is pervasive and automatic.
- In younger children (under ~6), what appears to be masking may simply be situational behavior differences between structured school settings and home.

### 2.2 Long-Term Consequences of Childhood Masking

**Raymaker, D.M., Teo, A.R., Steckler, N.A., Lentz, B., Scharer, M., Delos Santos, A., ... & Nicolaidis, C. (2020). "Having all of your internal resources exhausted beyond measure and being left with no clean-up crew": Defining autistic burnout. *Autism in Adulthood, 2*(2), 132-143.**
- DOI: 10.1089/aut.2019.0079
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32851204/
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7313636/
- **Methodology**: Community-based participatory research; thematic analysis of 19 interviews + 19 public internet sources.
- **Definition**: Autistic burnout is a syndrome resulting from chronic life stress and a mismatch of expectations and abilities without adequate supports.
- **Primary characteristics**: Chronic exhaustion, loss of skills, and reduced tolerance to stimulus.
- **Key developmental finding**: Adults said autistic burnout was often first experienced during adolescence, lasted months or years, and was hard to recover from.
- **Recovery strategies**: Acceptance and peer support, unmasking, social withdrawal, recognizing burnout onset symptoms, and benefits of early diagnosis.

**After-School Restraint Collapse (ASRC)**:
- Widely documented in clinical practice though limited formal academic research.
- Children spend all day "holding it together" at school through masking, then release built-up stress at home through meltdowns, emotional outbursts, irritability, or withdrawal.
- More intense and longer-lasting in neurodivergent children (autism, ADHD, sensory sensitivities) because their brains work harder to get through the school day.
- Term originally coined by psychotherapist Andrea Nair.

### 2.3 Consequences of Social Camouflaging: Systematic Reviews

**Systematic review and meta-analysis (2024) on mental health outcomes:**
- Published in Research in Autism Spectrum Disorders (2024).
- Link: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1750946724001673
- **Sample**: 16 studies eligible for meta-analysis + 6 for narrative review; 5,897 autistic participants total.
- **Key findings**:
  - Significant moderate positive relationships between camouflaging and anxiety/generalized anxiety, depression, and social anxiety.
  - Significant small negative relationship between camouflaging and mental wellbeing.
  - Relationships were unrelated to study quality, participant age, or share of female/formally diagnosed participants.
  - All studies were underpowered to detect small effects, and many used insufficiently validated measures.

**Cook, J., Hull, L., Crane, L., & Mandy, W. (2021). Camouflaging in autism: A systematic review. *Clinical Psychology Review, 89*, 102080.**
- DOI: 10.1016/j.cpr.2021.102080
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34563942/
- **Methodology**: Systematic search of 8 databases; 29 studies reviewed.
- **Three preliminary findings supported**:
  1. Adults with more self-reported autistic traits report greater camouflaging engagement.
  2. Sex and gender differences exist in camouflaging.
  3. Higher self-reported camouflaging is associated with worse mental health outcomes.
- **Measurement approaches**: Internal-external discrepancy and self-report methods appear to measure two distinct but connected elements of camouflaging.

### 2.4 Biological Stress Mechanisms

**Co-twin control study on camouflaging and physiological stress (2025):**
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12659362/
- **Key finding**: Camouflaging is associated with increased biological long-term stress at the population level.
- **Mechanism**: Consistent use of taxing camouflaging strategies may initially increase HPA (hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal) axis activity, then subsequently lead to suppressed cortisol levels, increasing risk for exhaustion, autistic burnout, and other mental health problems.

### 2.5 Longitudinal Evidence: Cause vs. Consequence

**van der Putten, W.J., Mol, A.J.J., Radhoe, T.A., Torenvliet, C., Agelink van Rentergem, J.A., Groenman, A.P., & Geurts, H.M. (2025). Camouflaging in autism: A cause or a consequence of mental health difficulties? *Autism.*
- DOI: 10.1177/13623613251347104
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12417600/
- **Sample**: 332 autistic adults in the Netherlands, aged 30-84, measured at two time points ~2 years apart.
- **Key findings**:
  - Cross-sectionally: Camouflaging was associated with greater mental health difficulties (consistent with prior literature).
  - Longitudinally: Camouflaging did NOT predict worsening mental health over time. Those who initially camouflaged more experienced a modest improvement in wellbeing; those who camouflaged less showed slight decline.
  - Effects were small and should be interpreted cautiously.
  - The association between camouflaging and mental health differs in strength across subgroups.
- **Implication**: Challenges the simple "camouflaging causes poor mental health" narrative; the relationship may be more complex and bidirectional.

## 3. Racial and Cultural Intersections

### 3.1 Diagnostic Disparities

**Mandell, D.S., Ittenbach, R.F., Levy, S.E., & Pinto-Martin, J.A. (2007). Disparities in diagnoses received prior to a diagnosis of autism spectrum disorder. *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 37*(9), 1795-1802.**
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2861330/
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17160456/
- **Sample**: 406 Medicaid-eligible children in Philadelphia.
- **Key findings**:
  - African American children were 2.6 times less likely than White children to receive an autism diagnosis on their first specialty care visit.
  - African American children were 5.1 times more likely to receive a diagnosis of adjustment disorder (before eventually receiving ASD diagnosis).
  - African American children were 2.4 times more likely to receive a diagnosis of conduct disorder.
  - ~15% of Black children received an incorrect conduct disorder diagnosis vs. ~6% of White children.

**Constantino, J.N., Abbacchi, A.M., Saulnier, C., Klaiman, C., Mandell, D.S., Zhang, Y., ... & Geschwind, D.H. (2020). Timing of the diagnosis of autism in African American children in a community-based sample. *Pediatrics.*
- Referenced in CDC ADDM data analyses.
- Black and Hispanic children have historically been diagnosed later than White children, though recent data suggest this gap is closing.

**CDC ADDM Network Data (2023-2024)**:
- Source: https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/volumes/72/ss/ss7202a1.htm
- For the first time, ASD prevalence among Asian/Pacific Islander (3.3%), Hispanic (3.2%), and Black (2.9%) 8-year-olds exceeded that of White children (2.4%).
- Previously, ASD prevalence was highest among White children in all birth cohorts prior to 2010.
- A higher percentage of Black children with autism were identified with co-occurring intellectual disability compared with White, Hispanic, or API children.
- Hispanic/Latino children historically diagnosed later, though age gaps are narrowing.

### 3.2 Double Marginalization: Race + Autism

**Perry, A.E., et al. (2022). Examination of race and autism intersectionality among African American/Black young adults. *Autism in Adulthood, 4*(4).**
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9908282/
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36777378/
- **Sample**: 32 autistic + 30 non-autistic African American/Black young adults.
- **Methodology**: Online survey examining risk factors of everyday discrimination, perceived stress, and protective factors of psychological empowerment and Black identity.
- **Key findings**:
  - Non-autistic AA/B participants endorsed race as their prime source of discrimination.
  - Autistic AA/B participants cited being autistic as a major additional contributor to discrimination.
  - Highlights intersection of dual marginalized identities.

**"I'm Kind of Stuck in the Middle" (2025)** - Race, autism, and intersectional stigma study:
- Link: https://www.liebertpub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/25739581251374440
- **Sample**: 32 autistic adults (16 Black, 16 White), semi-structured interviews.
- Explores how intersecting identities shape stigma experiences for Black vs. White autistic adults.

**The Scholarly Neglect of Black Autistic Adults in Autism Research (2022)**:
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9908289/
- Highlights the significant gap in autism research specifically examining Black autistic adults' experiences.

### 3.3 Cultural Expectations and Masking

**Atherton, G., Morimoto, Y., Nakashima, S., & Cross, L. (2023). Does the study of culture enrich our understanding of autism? A cross-cultural exploration of life on the spectrum in Japan and the West. *Journal of Cross-Cultural Psychology.*
- DOI: 10.1177/00220221231169945
- **Key findings**:
  - In Japan, the term "over-adaptation" describes the substantial efforts autistic people make to fit into social environments.
  - Autism was significantly more stigmatized in Japan than in the U.S. in university student samples.
  - Even after intervention, Japanese students still stigmatized autism more than U.S. students.

**Bernier, R. (2020). Psychopathology, families, and culture: Autism. AACAP curriculum.**
- Link: https://www.aacap.org/App_Themes/AACAP/Docs/resource_centers/cultural_diversity/competency_curriculum%20_cap_training/cases_supporting_materials/clinics/Bernier-psychopathology_families_and_culture-autism.pdf
- **Key cultural dynamics**:
  - American mothers promote autonomy, assertiveness, verbal competence; Japanese mothers promote emotional maturity, self-control, interdependence, social courtesy.
  - These parenting differences affect whether/when parents recognize atypical development.
  - In Asian cultures, reduced eye contact is normative respect for authority, but is also a diagnostic criterion for ASD -- creating cultural confounds.
  - Korean-American parents may express developmental concerns later due to different cultural expectations.
  - In collectivist cultures, acknowledging a child's disability may be viewed negatively, deterring families from accessing services.

**Tafla, T.L., Teixeira, M.C.T.V., Woodcock, K.A., & Sowden-Carvalho, S. (2024). Autism spectrum disorder diagnosis across cultures: Are diagnoses equivalent? *Autism.*
- DOI: 10.1177/27546330241226811
- **Key finding**: Current socio-cognitive assessments largely reflect Western norms, limiting applicability to non-Western populations.

**Global perspectives on autism acceptance and camouflaging (registered report protocol, 2021)**:
- PMC: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8718008/
- Notes that in collectivist cultures prioritizing community interdependence, there may be more pressure to camouflage autistic traits to minimize deviation from group norms, potentially leading people to alter behavior or hide traits to avoid shame.

### 3.4 Barriers to Diagnosis for Hispanic/Latino Communities

**Understanding barriers to autism diagnosis for Hispanic/Latino families (2022)**:
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9584143/
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35417307/
- **Key barriers**:
  - Limited English proficiency.
  - Noncitizenship and immigration status.
  - Low-income status limiting healthcare access.
  - Cultural barriers and poor knowledge about ASD.
  - Social stigma related to disabilities in many Latino cultures.
  - Hispanic/Latino children more likely to have severe symptoms and comorbidities when they are finally diagnosed (reflecting delayed identification).

## 4. Late-Diagnosed Individuals

### 4.1 Impact of Years/Decades of Unrecognized Masking

**Leedham, A., Thompson, A.R., Smith, R., & Freeth, M. (2020). 'I was exhausted trying to figure it out': The experiences of females receiving an autism diagnosis in middle to late adulthood. *Autism, 24*(1), 135-146.**
- DOI: 10.1177/1362361319853442
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31144507/
- **Sample**: 11 autistic females diagnosed after age 40.
- **Methodology**: Semi-structured interviews analyzed with Interpretative Phenomenological Analysis (IPA).
- **Key findings**:
  - Described an emotionally difficult adjustment period.
  - Diagnosis helped participants make sense of their identity.
  - Years of unrecognized masking had led to exhaustion, confusion, and accumulated mental health difficulties.

**Bargiela et al. (2016)** (see Section 1.5): 14 women aged 22-30 diagnosed in late adolescence/adulthood reported profound impacts of unrecognized masking, including sexual vulnerability and identity conflicts.

### 4.2 Mental Health at Time of Diagnosis

**Beck, J.S., Lundwall, R.A., Gabrielsen, T., Cox, J.C., & South, M. (2020). Looking good but feeling bad: "Camouflaging" behaviors and mental health in women with autistic traits. *Autism, 24*(4), 809-821.**
- DOI: 10.1177/1362361320912147
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32429817/
- **Sample**: 58 women (average age 25, average IQ 115) who reported finding social situations confusing and scored high on autistic traits.
- **Key findings**:
  - Majority reported significant levels of psychological distress, suicidal ideation, and daily functioning difficulties.
  - Camouflaging efforts and autistic traits modestly but significantly predicted psychological distress and functional challenges.
  - For women reporting above-average camouflaging, camouflaging was also associated with suicidal thoughts and struggles with everyday functioning.

**Bradley, L., Shaw, R., Baron-Cohen, S., & Cassidy, S. (2021). Is social camouflaging associated with anxiety and depression in autistic adults? *Molecular Autism, 12*(1), 13.**
- Link: https://molecularautism.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s13229-021-00421-1
- **Key finding**: Camouflaging was associated with greater symptoms of generalized anxiety, depression, and social anxiety. Camouflaging more strongly predicted generalized and social anxiety than depression.

**Systematic review on consequences of social camouflaging (2025)**:
- Link: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S3050656525000288
- **Key findings**: Social camouflaging came with long-term consequences including: impact on identity, delayed or missed diagnosis, and implications on mental health. Masking led to identity confusion and contributed to support needs being unrecognized or disbelieved.

### 4.3 Post-Diagnosis Identity Reconstruction

**Corden, K., Brewer, R., & Cage, E. (2021). Personal identity after an autism diagnosis: Relationships with self-esteem, mental wellbeing, and diagnostic timing. *Frontiers in Psychology, 12*, 699335.**
- DOI: 10.3389/fpsyg.2021.699335
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8360844/
- **Sample**: 151 autistic adults (online survey).
- **Key findings**:
  - Greater time elapsed since diagnosis related to less dissatisfaction with autistic personal identity.
  - More dissatisfaction with autistic identity predicted lower self-esteem; more autism pride predicted higher self-esteem.
  - Identity is an important variable in self-esteem and wellbeing regardless of when someone is diagnosed.

**Kiehl, I., Pease, R., & Hackmann, C. (2024). The adult experience of being diagnosed with autism spectrum disorder: A qualitative meta-synthesis. *Autism.*
- DOI: 10.1177/13623613231220419
- **Methodology**: Qualitative meta-synthesis of multiple studies.
- **Seven key emotional themes**: Frustration at the Barriers, Fear and Self-Doubt, The Grief of Revisiting the Past, Shock of the New Self, Anger at Being Dismissed, The Relief of Authenticity, and Pride and Belonging in Community.

**Systematic review on lived experiences of adult diagnosis (2024)**:
- Link: https://www.liebertpub.com/doi/10.1089/aut.2023.0152
- **Most reported category**: "Adjustment process" - participants discussed "making sense of it all over time," referring to re-evaluating their lives following diagnosis.

### 4.4 The Relief + Grief Cycle

Based on multiple qualitative studies, the post-diagnosis emotional trajectory commonly follows this pattern:

1. **Relief**: Finally having an explanation for lifelong difficulties. Understanding why things were so hard.
2. **Grief**: Mourning for the life that could have been with earlier support. Sadness about past misunderstandings, lost relationships, missed opportunities.
3. **Anger**: At professionals who missed the diagnosis. At systems that failed to recognize needs.
4. **Retrospective re-evaluation**: Reinterpreting past experiences through an autism lens. "Oh, THAT'S why that happened."
5. **Identity reconstruction**: Integrating autism into one's self-concept. Resolving the dilemma between self-acceptance and desire to change.
6. **Acceptance/Pride**: Over time, developing autism pride and community belonging, with decreasing dissatisfaction with autistic identity.

**Key quote from research**: For many older adults, the diagnosis "finally provided an explanation for past struggles, but at the same time, it could feel like a confronting judgment of the life they had lived before."

Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12526855/

## 5. LGBTQ+ Autistic Individuals

### 5.1 Higher Rates of LGBTQ+ Identity in Autistic Populations

**Warrier, V., Greenberg, D.M., Weir, E., Buckingham, C., Smith, P., Lai, M.-C., Allison, C., & Baron-Cohen, S. (2020). Elevated rates of autism, other neurodevelopmental and psychiatric diagnoses, and autistic traits in transgender and gender-diverse individuals. *Nature Communications, 11*, 3959.**
- DOI: 10.1038/s41467-020-17794-1
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7415151/
- **Sample**: Over 600,000 adults across five independently sourced datasets.
- **Key findings**:
  - Transgender and gender-diverse adults are 3-6x more likely than cisgender adults to be diagnosed as autistic.
  - Trans/gender-diverse individuals scored higher on autistic trait measures regardless of whether they had a diagnosis.
  - Trans/gender-diverse individuals also more likely to have diagnoses of depression (>2x cisgender rates).

**SPARK for Autism study data**:
- Source: https://sparkforautism.org/discover_article/rm-identity-masking/
- 52% of autistic women identified as LGBTQ+ compared to 15% of non-autistic women.
- 35% of autistic people overall identified as LGBTQ+ compared to 14% of non-autistic individuals.

### 5.2 Multiple Layers of Masking

**McQuaid, G.A., Lee, N.R., & Wallace, G.L. (2022). Camouflaging in autism spectrum disorder: Examining the roles of sex, gender identity, and diagnostic timing. *Autism, 26*(2), 552-564.**
- DOI: 10.1177/13623613211042131
- **Key findings**:
  - Autistic females reported more camouflaging across all CAT-Q subscales compared to males.
  - Gender diverse autistic adults reported elevated camouflaging on the Compensation subscale compared to cisgender adults.
  - Adulthood-diagnosed individuals reported elevated Assimilation and Compensation compared to those diagnosed in childhood/adolescence.

**McQuaid, G.A., Lee, N.R., & Wallace, G.L. (2025). Self-reported masking in sexual minority and heterosexual autistic adults. *Autism.*
- DOI: 10.1177/13623613251335738
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40304097/
- **Sample**: ~460 autistic adults from the SPARK study, ages 18-50 (no intellectual disability).
- **Key finding**: Sexual minority autistic adults masked their autistic traits more often than heterosexual autistic adults.

**George, R. & Stokes, M.A. (2018). A quantitative analysis of mental health among sexual and gender minority groups in ASD. *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 48*(6), 2052-2063.**
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29362955/
- **Key finding**: Autistic adults who were sexual minorities reported more symptoms of anxiety and depression, mental health conditions, or unmet healthcare needs than heterosexual autistic adults.

### 5.3 Community Barriers and Identity

**"My whole life has been a process of finding labels that fit" (2023)**: Thematic analysis of autistic LGBTQIA+ identity and inclusion.
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10280209/
- **Key findings**:
  - Community spaces for autistic LGBTQIA+ people are often inaccessible due to social, sensory, and identity-based barriers.
  - Autism understanding was highlighted as a barrier to coming out in both community and non-community settings.
  - Participants described a lifelong process of seeking identity labels that fit their experiences.

### 5.4 Intersectionality Framework

The intersectionality framework conceptualizes that those who hold multiple categories of marginalized identity experience a cumulative discriminatory impact which differs qualitatively from the prejudice experienced toward each identity individually.

For autistic LGBTQ+ individuals, this means:
- Multiple types of masking may occur simultaneously (masking autistic traits AND concealing sexual/gender identity).
- The cognitive load of managing multiple concealed identities compounds mental health risks.
- Social stress from different pressures means sexual minority autistic adults may feel more pressured to camouflage than heterosexual autistic adults.
- Emerging research suggests trans individuals may be even more likely to go undiagnosed, as autism may present in ways specific to the trans community that are poorly understood.

## 6. Key Measurement Tools Referenced

### 6.1 Camouflaging Autistic Traits Questionnaire (CAT-Q)

**Hull, L., Mandy, W., Lai, M.-C., Baron-Cohen, S., Allison, C., Smith, P., & Petrides, K.V. (2019). Development and validation of the Camouflaging Autistic Traits Questionnaire (CAT-Q). *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 49*(3), 819-833.**
- DOI: 10.1007/s10803-018-3792-6
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30361940/
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6394586/
- **Sample**: 354 autistic + 478 non-autistic adults.
- **Structure**: 25 items across 3 subscales:
  - **Compensation**: Behaviors aiming to compensate for autistic social/communication differences.
  - **Masking**: Efforts to present a non-autistic persona in social situations.
  - **Assimilation**: "Blending-in" and hiding one's discomfort in social situations.
- **Psychometrics**: Internal consistency alpha = 0.94; test-retest reliability r = 0.77; measurement invariance across gender and diagnostic group confirmed.
- **Status**: Most commonly used self-report measure of camouflaging in autism research.

### 6.2 The Discrepancy Method (Lai et al., 2017)

Operationalizes camouflaging as the gap between:
- **External presentation**: ADOS scores (clinician-observed behavior).
- **Internal status**: AQ scores (self-reported autistic traits) + RMET scores (social cognitive ability).

A larger discrepancy = more camouflaging.

## 7. Qualitative Landmark Study

**Hull, L., Petrides, K.V., Allison, C., Smith, P., Baron-Cohen, S., Lai, M.-C., & Mandy, W. (2017). "Putting on my best normal": Social camouflaging in adults with autism spectrum conditions. *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 47*(8), 2519-2534.**
- DOI: 10.1007/s10803-017-3166-5
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28527095/
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5509825/
- **Sample**: 92 adults with ASC, 15 different nationalities (55% British), age 16+, all with DSM-IV or DSM-5 diagnosis.
- **Methodology**: Internet-based survey with thematic analysis. Developed a three-stage model of camouflaging.
- **Three-stage model**:
  1. **Motivations**: Fitting in, increasing connections, avoiding bullying.
  2. **Camouflaging techniques**: Combination of masking (suppressing autistic behaviors) and compensation (creating new social behaviors).
  3. **Consequences**: Short-term (exhaustion) and long-term (threats to self-perception, challenging stereotypes).
