# Research: Narrative and Discourse Coherence in Autism

## Purpose
Comprehensive research on how autistic individuals produce, comprehend, and experience narrative discourse. This document supports a knowledge base teaching English pragmatics to autistic individuals, with the goal of making narrative structure explicit and learnable while respecting autistic communication styles as valid alternatives.

---

## 1. RESEARCH ON AUTISTIC NARRATIVE PRODUCTION

### 1.1 The Macrostructure/Microstructure Distinction

Narrative research distinguishes two levels of analysis:

**Macrostructure** refers to the overall content and organization of the narrative -- the "big picture" structure. This encompasses:
- Story grammar elements: orientation (setting, characters), complicating action (what happened), evaluation (why it matters), resolution (how it ended), and coda (return to present)
- The episodic structure: how events are organized into coherent episodes
- Global coherence: how the narrative holds together as a unified whole
- Causal connections between events: why one thing led to another

**Microstructure** refers to the sentence-level and local linguistic features:
- Cohesive devices: conjunctions, reference chains, lexical ties that connect sentences
- Syntactic complexity: sentence length, subordination, clause structure
- Referential expressions: how characters and objects are introduced and tracked
- Productivity: overall length and amount of language produced

This distinction is critical because research consistently shows that autistic individuals' greatest narrative challenges are at the macrostructure level, while microstructure often remains comparable to neurotypical peers when matched for language ability.

**Source:** Baixauli et al. (2016) meta-analysis of 24 studies confirmed that the most important discursive difficulties experienced by autistic individuals are situated at the level of macrostructure; autistic participants seem less capable of maintaining global coherence in their narratives relative to neurotypical peers, with a large effect size. At the level of microstructure, productivity and syntactic complexity were predominantly similar in autistic and language-matched children. ([Baixauli et al., 2016, meta-analysis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0378216620300977))

### 1.2 Key Findings on Autistic Macrostructure

Multiple studies converge on these macrostructure findings:

**Global coherence is reduced.** Autistic narrators produce stories that are less globally coherent -- meaning the overall narrative doesn't hold together as tightly as a unit. Individual sentences may be well-formed, but the connections between them, especially causal and motivational connections, are weaker. (Diehl, Bennetto, & Young, 2006; [Losh & Capps, 2003](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1023/A:1024446215446))

**Story grammar elements may be incomplete.** Autistic narrators may omit key story elements such as setting information, the central conflict, or resolution. The narrative may feel like a series of events without a clear "point" or dramatic arc. ([Naman et al., 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12163273/))

**Evaluation is particularly challenging.** The evaluation component -- signaling WHY the story is worth telling, what makes it interesting or important -- is consistently identified as an area of difficulty. Autistic narrators use fewer evaluative devices (causal language, emotional explanations, intensifiers, character perspective markers) than neurotypical peers. ([Losh & Capps, 2003](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1023/A:1024446215446); [Semantic-Pragmatic Impairment in Narratives](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6920157/))

**Context matters enormously.** Autistic individuals produce poorer quality narratives during less structured tasks but similar quality narratives during more structured tasks. When visual supports, picture sequences, or story frameworks are available, the gap narrows significantly. Free-form, unscaffolded narrative (like "tell me about something that happened to you") is the hardest context. ([Losh & Capps, 2003](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1023/A:1024446215446); [Context effects, Frontiers 2025](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2025.1588429/full))

**Detail orientation vs. big picture.** Autistic narrators tend to be biased toward providing local over global details -- giving more specific details about key components rather than the "big picture" that carries critical elements such as setting, character development, and resolution. ([Quantifying Narrative Ability, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4224608/))

### 1.3 Key Findings on Autistic Microstructure

**Syntactic complexity is often comparable.** When matched for language ability, autistic individuals' sentence-level grammar and syntax are often similar to neurotypical peers. The challenge is not usually at the sentence level but at the discourse level -- how sentences connect to form a coherent whole. ([Diehl et al., 2006](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16485176/))

**Cohesion can be affected.** While productivity and syntax may be similar, cohesive devices -- the words and structures that link sentences together (pronouns, conjunctions, temporal markers) -- may be used less effectively, contributing to the reduced global coherence. ([Geelhand et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0378216620300977))

**Reference tracking shows a specific pattern.** Research reveals that autistic children produce significantly higher rates of ambiguous character establishment (introducing characters in a way that assumes the listener already knows them) while showing comparable cohesion once characters are established. This means the initial introduction of "who's who" is the harder part, not the ongoing tracking. ([Analysis of Noun Phrase Ambiguity, ASHA 2023](https://pubs.asha.org/doi/10.1044/2023_JSLHR-22-00630))

**Pronoun use patterns are mixed.** Some studies find overproduction of ambiguous pronouns (using "he" when it's unclear which character is meant). Other studies find the opposite: overspecification, where autistic narrators use a full noun phrase ("the boy") when neurotypical speakers would use a pronoun ("he"). The underlying challenge is not pronouns per se but the pragmatic skill of selecting the appropriate referential form for the discourse context. ([Novogrodsky & Edelson, 2016](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0265659015602935); [PMC Reference Production](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10555458/))

**Speech patterns in adults.** Compared to neurotypical controls, autistic adults engage in more repetitions of speech but employ fewer revisions (self-corrections). Revisions are considered "listener-oriented" disfluencies that serve to clarify information for the audience. ([Geelhand et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0378216620300977))

### 1.4 Key Studies on Autistic Narrative Production

#### Geelhand, Papastamou, Deliens, & Kissine (2020)
**"Narrative production in autistic adults: A systematic analysis of the microstructure, macrostructure and internal state language"** -- Journal of Pragmatics, 164, 57-81.

This landmark study provided one of the first comprehensive analyses of narrative production in autistic adults (as opposed to children). Key findings:
- Autistic adults performed worse than neurotypical peers on ALL THREE dimensions: microstructure, macrostructure, and internal state language
- However, autistic adults did not lack the ability to create a story; they were less able to determine accurately how often to incorporate different features and how to exploit them together
- Narratives showed less coherent organization and fewer references to characters' internal states
- This confirmed that narrative difficulties documented in childhood persist into adulthood
- The study used pairwise matching on age, gender, and cognitive ability, strengthening the comparison

([Geelhand et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0378216620300977); [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/341931212_Narrative_production_in_autistic_adults_A_systematic_analysis_of_the_microstructure_macrostructure_and_internal_state_language))

#### Harvey, Spicer-Cain, Botting, & Henry (2025)
**"Narrative abilities of autistic and non-autistic adolescents: The role of mentalising and executive function"** -- Autism Research.

AND

**"Everyday narrative skills in autistic adolescents"** -- Child Language Teaching and Therapy (2025).

These two companion studies from the same research group examined narrative abilities in 44 autistic and 54 non-autistic adolescents (ages 11-15). Major findings:

- **Mentalizing was a stronger predictor of narrative ability than diagnostic group.** This is a crucial finding: it suggests that what matters most for storytelling is the ability to understand others' mental states, and this varies within both autistic and non-autistic groups.
- **Executive function did NOT predict narrative ability** in this sample, contrary to what some theories would predict.
- **Diagnostic group predicted story grammar scores but NOT coherence scores** -- meaning autistic adolescents showed structural differences in narrative organization but not necessarily in how "coherent" their stories were perceived to be.
- The study used a video-based spoken narrative task, scored for both overall structure and narrative coherence.

This research is important because it shifts the focus from "autism vs. not autism" to "mentalizing ability" as the key variable in narrative performance.

([Harvey et al., 2025 - Autism Research](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/aur.3272); [Harvey et al., 2025 - CLTT](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/01427237251315253); [PsyPost summary](https://www.psypost.org/mentalizing-skills-drive-teen-storytelling-ability-more-than-autism-diagnosis/))

#### Coburn & Williams (2023)
**"Quantitative Analysis of Narrative Discourse by Autistic Adults of Underrepresented Genders"** -- Autism in Adulthood, 5(2).

This study addressed a major gap: most narrative research has focused on cisgender boys and men. Twenty autistic adults of various genders told four narratives (two picture-based, two personal experiences). Findings:
- Autistic narrators of diverse genders showed more linguistic similarities than differences
- Autistic women may be more likely to talk about emotions and internal states than autistic men
- Gender-based expectations for spoken communication about internal states should be carefully reconsidered
- This has implications for diagnosis: adults of underrepresented genders seeking autism diagnosis may be misdiagnosed due to gender-biased expectations about autistic communication

([Coburn & Williams, 2023](https://www.liebertpub.com/doi/10.1089/aut.2021.0080); [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10280195/))

### 1.5 The Causal Coherence Finding

One of the most robust findings in the literature is that autistic narrators produce fewer causal connections between events. This has several dimensions:

**Temporal sequencing may be preserved, but causal linking is weaker.** Autistic narrators can often arrange events in the correct temporal order ("First X happened, then Y happened, then Z happened") but are less likely to explain WHY one event caused or led to another ("X happened, which made the character angry, so they did Y, which resulted in Z"). ([Sah & Torng, 2015](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0142723715584227))

**Causal-motivational coherence is particularly affected.** Research on life narratives found that global coherence, particularly causal-motivational coherence, was significantly lower in autistic individuals' stories. This means the connections between events and personal meaning -- why something matters, why a person did what they did -- are less explicitly stated. ([Narrative identity differences, Nature 2025](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-00178-0))

**Fewer causal relationships involving intentions.** Despite producing a comparable number of causal relationships overall, autistic children established significantly fewer causal relationships involving characters' intentions and key story events. The "mechanical" causation ("the ball hit the window and it broke") may be fine; the "intentional" causation ("she was jealous so she hid the toy") is harder. ([Comparing narrative coherence, Cambridge](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/applied-psycholinguistics/article/comparing-the-narrative-coherence-of-spanishspeaking-autistic-and-typically-developing-children-focus-on-causal-rhetorical-relations/75C677773D7606273A02755A1B92C724))

**This connects to the "and then... and then..." pattern.** When causal links are omitted, narratives can become temporal chains -- lists of events connected only by "and then" -- rather than causally structured stories. The events may all be accurate and in order, but the listener doesn't understand why they matter or how they connect.

### 1.6 The Internal State Language Finding

Internal State Language (ISL) refers to words and phrases that describe characters' thoughts, feelings, desires, intentions, and perceptions. This is a consistently documented area of difference in autistic narrative:

**Fewer references to characters' emotions.** The narratives of autistic individuals are less likely to reference characters' emotional states compared to neurotypical controls. ([Internal State Language, PMC 2014](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3988534/))

**Preference for reported speech over mental state description.** When autistic narrators DO reference mental states, they may prefer reported speech (especially direct quotation: "He said 'I want to go home'") over explicit mental state attribution ("He felt homesick" or "He wanted to leave"). ([Internal State Language, PMC 2014](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3988534/))

**Connection to Theory of Mind.** The use of emotion terms in narrative is specifically associated with performance on Theory of Mind tasks, suggesting that the reduction in internal state language reflects genuine difficulty in representing and communicating about others' mental states, not just a stylistic choice. ([ISL and ToM, PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24748899/))

**Gender differences.** Girls with ASD verbalize and motivate internal states more often than boys with ASD, though both groups fall behind typically developing children in production of affective words. This has implications for assessment and support. ([Narratives of Girls and Boys with ASD, PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26438638/))

**Fewer subordinate clauses about thoughts.** Compared with typically developing children, children with ASD used fewer subordinate clauses about characters' thoughts (e.g., "He thought that..." or "She realized that..."), which are grammatically complex structures that embed mental state content within the narrative. ([ISL research](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3988534/))

## 2. NARRATIVE COMPREHENSION IN AUTISM

### 2.1 Understanding Stories: The Big Picture

Narrative comprehension involves not just understanding individual sentences but integrating information across the story to build a coherent mental model of events, characters, and their motivations.

**Central Coherence Theory (Frith, 1989; Happe, 1999)** proposes that autistic individuals have a cognitive style biased toward local processing -- attending to details rather than integrating information into global patterns. Applied to narrative, this predicts difficulty with:
- Building a unified mental model of the story
- Connecting events across different parts of the narrative
- Using context to disambiguate meaning
- Drawing inferences that require integrating multiple story elements

([Central coherence and narrative comprehension](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20565231/); [Weak central coherence in adults with ASD](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35450487/))

### 2.2 Inference Generation

This is one of the most critical areas for narrative comprehension:

**Script-based inferences are harder.** Children with autism are less able to make inferences based on event scripts (common sequences of events, like what happens at a restaurant), but groups do not differ significantly on inferences requiring deductive logical reasoning. This suggests the difficulty is specifically with social/experiential knowledge, not with logical thinking per se. ([Narrative comprehension testing WCC](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20565231/))

**Irrelevant inferences.** Autistic children sometimes make inferences that are not relevant to the overall context of the story, which may result from failing to integrate their knowledge with the global context. They might make a locally logical inference that doesn't fit the bigger picture. ([Narrative comprehension research](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20565231/))

**Global vs. local inferences.** Autistic individuals show particular difficulty with global inferences (understanding the overall meaning or theme) compared to local inferences (understanding what a specific sentence means). ([Association of autistic traits with inference generation, Nature 2025](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-01252-3))

**Reading comprehension connection.** Narrative retelling and inference skills are important for successful reading comprehension for autistic individuals. Difficulties with narrative inference directly impact the ability to understand written texts. ([Growth in narrative retelling, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9620466/))

**Recent positive finding (2025).** A study by Coderre et al. found that autistic adults can anticipate both simple and complex narrative events, suggesting that some aspects of narrative prediction are preserved. This challenges overly negative characterizations of autistic narrative comprehension. ([Autistic Adults Anticipate Narrative Events, Springer 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10803-025-07037-x))

### 2.3 Understanding Characters' Motivations

Understanding WHY characters do what they do requires:
- Theory of Mind: representing characters' beliefs, desires, and intentions
- Emotional understanding: recognizing emotional states and their causes
- Social knowledge: understanding social norms, expectations, and conventions

Research shows that autistic individuals have particular difficulty with:
- Explaining the causes of characters' emotions ([Losh & Capps, 2006](https://faculty.weber.edu/eamsel/Classes/Projects%20and%20Research%20(4800)/Regret,%20Guilt,%20and%20Internalization%20of%20Standards/Losh%20&%20Capps%20(2006).pdf))
- Inferring characters' unstated motivations
- Understanding how characters' mental states drive the plot
- Recognizing emotional turning points in stories

### 2.4 The Role of Working Memory

Verbal working memory plays an important role in narrative comprehension for autistic individuals. Research shows that children with autism demonstrate verbal working memory limitations, with poorer performance on more complex verbal memory tasks. Vocabulary and language processing demands affect performance on tasks of verbal memory and story retelling. ([Verbal Working Memory and Story Retelling, ASHA](https://pubs.asha.org/doi/10.1044/0161-1461(2008/07-0023)))

This suggests that narrative comprehension difficulties may be partly attributable to the cognitive load of holding multiple story elements in mind simultaneously while also processing new incoming information.

## 3. WHAT SPECIFIC NARRATIVE ELEMENTS ARE CHALLENGING

### 3.1 Evaluation: The "So What?" Element

In Labov's (1972) influential model of narrative structure, every well-formed narrative contains:
1. **Abstract** -- what the story is about (preview)
2. **Orientation** -- who, when, where, what was happening
3. **Complicating Action** -- what happened (the events)
4. **Evaluation** -- WHY this story is worth telling (the "so what?")
5. **Resolution** -- how it turned out
6. **Coda** -- return to the present, wrap-up

The evaluation component is consistently the most challenging for autistic narrators. Evaluation involves:
- Signaling the emotional weight or significance of events
- Using devices like intensifiers ("it was INCREDIBLY loud"), comparators ("it was bigger than anything I'd ever seen"), causal explanations ("because she was terrified"), and character perspective ("he couldn't believe what he was seeing")
- Indicating to the listener why this particular sequence of events is reportable -- what makes it worth telling

Research consistently shows that autistic individuals use fewer evaluative devices, require more experimenter prompting for clarification, and produce narratives that listeners may perceive as lacking a clear "point." ([Losh & Capps, 2003](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1023/A:1024446215446); [What's the story? PMC 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5599333/); [Semantic-Pragmatic Impairment, Frontiers 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6920157/))

**Why this matters practically:** Without evaluation, a narrative sounds like a bare report of events. Compare:
- Without evaluation: "I went to the store. The store was closed. I went home."
- With evaluation: "I went to the store to get ingredients for my mom's birthday cake, but it was closed -- I couldn't believe it was closed on a Saturday! I had to go home empty-handed, which was really frustrating because the party was in three hours."

The evaluated version tells the listener: this matters because of the birthday, the surprise of the closure, the time pressure, and the emotional impact.

### 3.2 Perspective-Taking: Representing Characters' Inner Worlds

Narrative perspective-taking requires:
- Understanding what characters think and feel
- Choosing how much of this to share with the listener
- Using appropriate linguistic devices (mental state verbs, emotional vocabulary, subordinate clauses)
- Shifting between the narrator's perspective and characters' perspectives

This connects directly to Theory of Mind and is one of the core challenges documented across the literature. The difficulty is not just in knowing what characters might think but in the pragmatic skill of weaving this knowledge into the narrative in a way that helps the listener understand the story. ([Internal State Language research](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3988534/))

### 3.3 Audience Design: Adjusting for the Listener

Effective narrative requires constant audience modeling:
- What does the listener already know?
- What do they need to be told?
- What can be assumed vs. what must be stated explicitly?
- How much detail is appropriate?
- When does the listener need clarification?

Research indicates that autistic narrators may find it challenging to provide sufficient contextual information, as this involves judging what the audience already knows and what they need to be told. This can manifest as:
- Introducing characters as if the listener already knows them (e.g., "So he went to the thing" without establishing who "he" is or what "the thing" refers to)
- Providing too little background context for personal narratives
- Alternatively, providing excessive detail about aspects the listener doesn't need

([Audience design development in ASD](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26524381/); [Harvey et al., 2025](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/01427237251315253))

### 3.4 Causal Reasoning: Beyond "And Then"

As detailed in Section 1.5, the shift from temporal to causal narrative structure is a key challenge. Temporal narratives answer "what happened next?" while causal narratives answer "why did this happen? what did it lead to? what motivated this?"

The practical impact: narratives that are purely temporal ("and then... and then... and then...") can feel like lists of events to the listener, even when the events themselves are interesting. Adding causal language ("because," "so," "which meant that," "as a result") transforms a temporal chain into a story with explanatory power.

### 3.5 Reference Tracking: Keeping Characters Clear

The research on referential expression reveals a specific pattern:
- **Character introduction (establishment)** is more challenging -- autistic narrators may introduce characters in ambiguous ways
- **Character maintenance (ongoing tracking)** is relatively preserved once characters are established
- The pragmatic challenge is selecting the right level of specificity: full noun phrase vs. pronoun vs. zero reference, depending on what the listener needs at that point in the discourse

([Analysis of Noun Phrase Ambiguity, ASHA 2023](https://pubs.asha.org/doi/10.1044/2023_JSLHR-22-00630))

## 4. WHAT SPECIFIC NARRATIVE ELEMENTS MAY BE STRENGTHS

### 4.1 Detail and Precision in Description

Research consistently identifies attention to detail as a core autistic cognitive strength. In narrative contexts:
- Autistic individuals often provide rich, specific details about settings, objects, and events
- Descriptions can be precise and accurate in ways that add clarity and vividness
- Autistic children were found to be as adept as non-autistic children in providing detailed narratives about experienced events, with a particular strength in incorporating descriptive markers
- The detail-oriented cognitive style can produce narratives that are informationally rich, even when they may lack some conventional narrative structure

([Narrative Coherence of Autistic Children, Springer 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10803-024-06675-x); [Exploring Detail-Oriented Traits](https://rainbowtherapy.org/blogs-exploring-the-detail-oriented-traits-in-autism/))

### 4.2 Factual Accuracy in Retelling

Autistic memory skills can support narrative in specific ways:
- Remarkable ability to recall facts, figures, and experiences with precision
- Strong memory for specific details, dates, names, and technical specifications
- When retelling events that actually happened, autistic narrators may be more factually faithful to what occurred
- The literal, detail-oriented approach reflects a cognitive style that prioritizes accuracy and specificity

([Autistic Strengths](https://www.simplypsychology.org/autistic-strengths.html))

### 4.3 Logical/Sequential Ordering of Events

While causal linking may be challenging, temporal sequencing can be a relative strength:
- Events are often presented in clear chronological order
- The logical progression of steps or events may be well-organized
- Sequential, step-by-step narrative structure can be effective for certain types of communication (instructions, procedures, timelines, historical accounts)

([Narrative Skills and Temporal Sequencing, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7253523/))

### 4.4 Specialized Knowledge Narratives (Deep Expertise Storytelling)

Monotropism -- the tendency for attention to concentrate deeply on a small number of interests -- creates a unique narrative strength:
- Autistic individuals often develop profound expertise in their areas of interest
- This can produce narratives of extraordinary depth and detail about specialized topics
- "Infodumping" -- enthusiastically sharing a large quantity of detailed information about a highly-focused subject -- is a legitimate communication style that serves connection and knowledge transfer
- In professional, academic, and technical contexts, this depth of knowledge and precision in communication can be highly valued

The "infodump" represents a form of narrative that, while it may not follow conventional story structure (orientation-complication-resolution), serves important communicative functions: sharing knowledge, expressing passion, building connection through shared interest, and demonstrating expertise.

([Monotropism, Autism.org.uk](https://www.autism.org.uk/advice-and-guidance/professional-practice/what-is-monotropism); [Infodumping, Autism Awareness](https://autismawarenesscentre.com/what-is-autistic-info-dumping/); [Monotropism, Springer](https://link.springer.com/rwe/10.1007/978-1-4614-6435-8_102269-2))

### 4.5 Written Narrative vs. Oral Narrative

Research suggests a more nuanced picture than a simple "written is better" conclusion:
- Some studies found no differences between written syntax skills of autistic adults and vocabulary-matched neurotypical adults in narrative essays
- Written narrative may benefit from the reduced real-time processing demands: more time to plan, organize, and revise
- The removal of real-time social demands (reading the listener's face, managing turn-taking, monitoring comprehension) may free cognitive resources for narrative construction
- Some autistic individuals show notable strengths in written communication where they can carefully construct their message

However, results are task-dependent and individual variation is large. Written narrative is not universally easier for autistic individuals. ([Assessment of Personal Narrative Writing, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7453741/); [Preliminary Study of Writing Skills, Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10803-019-04254-z))

### 4.6 Contexts Where Autistic Narrative Strengths Shine

Professional and technical contexts often reward the very features that characterize autistic narrative style:
- **Quality Assurance and Testing:** meticulous attention to detail, precise description of processes and outcomes
- **Data Analysis and Research:** accurate reporting, systematic description, pattern recognition
- **Technical Documentation:** step-by-step logical structure, comprehensive detail, factual precision
- **Academic Writing:** depth of knowledge, thorough coverage, precise terminology
- **STEM Fields:** logical structure, accurate sequencing, systematic description

([Strengths and Abilities in the Workplace, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8992926/))

## 5. INTERVENTION RESEARCH

### 5.1 The Cognitive Pragmatic Treatment (CPT/A-CPT)

The Cognitive Pragmatic Treatment is a communicative-pragmatic training program developed in Italy. The adolescent version (A-CPT) has been specifically studied for its effects on narrative skills in autistic adolescents.

**Hilviu et al. (2023): "Can Narrative Skills Improve in Autism Spectrum Disorder? A Preliminary Study with Verbally Fluent Adolescents Receiving the Cognitive Pragmatic Treatment"** -- Journal of Psycholinguistic Research.

Study details:
- 16 verbally fluent adolescents with ASD received the A-CPT
- Pre- and post-training narrative production was assessed using both micro-linguistic measures (mean length of utterance, complete sentences, omissions) and macro-linguistic measures (cohesion, coherence errors, lexical informativeness)
- Results showed significant improvement in mean length of utterance and complete sentences, and a decrease in cohesion errors
- The findings suggest that pragmatically oriented training can improve grammatical efficiency in narrative production

This is important because it demonstrates that narrative skills in autism are not fixed -- they can improve with targeted intervention that addresses the pragmatic underpinnings of narrative discourse.

([Hilviu et al., 2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10936-023-09945-4); [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/370600014_Can_Narrative_Skills_Improve_in_Autism_Spectrum_Disorder_A_Preliminary_Study_with_Verbally_Fluent_Adolescents_Receiving_the_Cognitive_Pragmatic_Treatment))

### 5.2 Narrative Sequencing and Mentalizing Training

**Broekhof et al. (2022): "A narrative sequencing and mentalizing training for adults with autism: A pilot study"** -- Frontiers in Behavioral Neuroscience.

Study details:
- Participants with an official autism diagnosis were randomly assigned to Training (n=17) or waiting-list Control (n=15)
- Six weekly group sessions (groups of 3), approximately 60 minutes each
- Training involved retelling stories from the perspective of the original storyteller and answering mentalizing questions
- Significant improvements in mentalizing about others' beliefs and in narrative coherence
- All participants reported beneficial effects in their daily lives
- This is one of the few intervention studies specifically targeting autistic adults

([Broekhof et al., 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9433774/); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/behavioral-neuroscience/articles/10.3389/fnbeh.2022.941272/full))

### 5.3 Story Grammar Intervention with Visual Supports

Multiple studies have demonstrated the effectiveness of explicit story grammar instruction for autistic individuals:

**Key principles that work:**
- **Explicit instruction in story grammar elements:** Teaching the named parts of a story (setting, problem, plan, action, consequence, ending) using clear labels and definitions
- **Visual icons/supports:** Using visual representations of story grammar elements (e.g., a light bulb for "idea," a star for "feelings," a house for "setting") that make abstract discourse structure concrete and visible
- **Repeated practice with consistent structure:** Using the same framework across multiple stories so the pattern becomes automatic
- **Scaffolded progression:** Starting with supported retelling, moving to guided generation, then independent production

**Research findings:**
- All participating children demonstrated increased correct responding to both fact and inference questions following story grammar intervention
- Visual supports were essential: many children with autism required visuals to participate in and learn from the intervention
- By making the global structure of narrative discourse explicit and visually accessible, intervention helps autistic individuals organize information, identify temporal and causal relationships, and develop mental models of story content
- Teachers perceived the intervention as helpful and feasible

([Effects of adapted story grammar intervention, ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S089142221930174X); [Narrative Intervention with Strategy Instruction, PMC 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12382993/))

### 5.4 Other Narrative Intervention Approaches

**Readers Theater combined with story mapping and video self-modeling:** This multi-component approach was investigated for improving narrative reading comprehension in children with ASD. The combination of performance (Readers Theater), visual organization (story mapping), and self-observation (video self-modeling) addresses multiple pathways to narrative understanding. ([Combining Readers Theater, Springer 2021](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10803-021-04908-x))

**Personal narrative intervention:** Research has explored individualized narrative language intervention targeting personal narratives specifically, as these are the most common form of everyday storytelling and the most challenging context for autistic individuals. ([Systematic Individualized Narrative Language Intervention, ASHA](https://pubs.asha.org/doi/abs/10.1044/2013_LSHSS-12-0099))

**Autobiographical memory and social identity intervention:** Preliminary research has explored using cognitive intervention to support autobiographical memory and social positioning in autism, recognizing the connection between narrative ability and identity formation. ([Autobiographical Memory and Social Identity, PMC](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8009988/))

### 5.5 What Works: Summary of Intervention Principles

Across the intervention literature, several principles emerge consistently:

1. **Make structure explicit.** Don't assume narrative structure is implicitly learned. Name the parts, define them, show examples.
2. **Use visual supports.** Icons, graphic organizers, story maps, and visual sequences make abstract discourse structure concrete.
3. **Teach causal language explicitly.** Provide specific words and phrases for expressing causal connections: "because," "so," "which meant that," "as a result," "the reason was."
4. **Practice with feedback.** Multiple opportunities to produce narratives with specific, constructive feedback on both structure and content.
5. **Start structured, move toward spontaneous.** Begin with picture-supported, scaffolded tasks before moving to free narrative production.
6. **Address mentalizing alongside structure.** Narrative improvement benefits from concurrent work on understanding and representing others' mental states.
7. **Target evaluation explicitly.** Teach the "so what?" element as a named, learnable skill.

## 6. THE MONOLOGUE VS. DIALOGUE DISTINCTION

### 6.1 Monologic Narrative

Monologic narrative -- telling a story from start to finish without interruption -- places the full burden of narrative organization on the speaker. The narrator must:
- Select what to include and exclude
- Organize events into a coherent sequence
- Provide appropriate background and context
- Signal the narrative's significance
- Monitor the listener's comprehension (without explicit feedback)
- Manage the overall pacing and structure

Research shows that this is particularly demanding for autistic individuals because it requires simultaneous management of multiple cognitive and pragmatic demands without the scaffolding that interactive exchange provides. Monologic narrative during semi-structured conversation (where the listener is not actively scaffolding) shows the greatest difficulties. ([What's the story? PMC 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5599333/))

### 6.2 Dialogic Narrative

Dialogic or co-constructed narrative involves building a story interactively with a listener who asks questions, provides prompts, and fills in gaps. Research indicates that:
- Co-construction can scaffold narrative production for autistic individuals
- The listener's questions provide structure and signal what information is needed
- Turn-taking within narrative allows for smaller, more manageable chunks of narrative production
- However, dialogic narrative also requires reading social cues, managing turn-taking, and responding flexibly to the interlocutor's contributions

Digital storytelling approaches have been explored as a form of co-construction that may be more accessible: creating stories through images, sounds, and collaborative editing removes some real-time social demands while maintaining the co-constructive element. ([Co-constructing digital storytelling, SAGE](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/16094069221145286))

### 6.3 The "Infodump" as Narrative

The infodump -- extended, detailed, enthusiastic sharing about a topic of deep interest -- represents a distinctive autistic narrative form that deserves recognition as legitimate communication:

**Characteristics:**
- Typically involves one speaker sharing extensively about a topic of deep knowledge or passion
- Highly detailed, often with precise factual information
- May not follow conventional narrative structure (no "problem-resolution" arc)
- Often monologic in structure
- Driven by genuine enthusiasm and desire to share knowledge
- May continue at length, with the speaker not pausing for typical conversational turn-taking cues

**Communicative functions:**
- Knowledge transfer: sharing expertise and information
- Connection through shared interest: "I care about this and want to share it with you"
- Joy and enthusiasm: expressing positive affect through content
- Identity expression: "this is who I am and what matters to me"
- Trust signal: sharing deep interests is an act of vulnerability and trust

**The reframing:** Rather than viewing infodumping as a deficit in conversational skills, the neurodiversity perspective recognizes it as a valid communication style with its own pragmatic logic. Erika Sanborne describes it as "enthusiastically communicating a large quantity of words, with an inordinate amount of detail, about one super interesting category of information, all at once, interpersonally." It has been called "a love language" because it represents sharing what matters most with people who matter.

**The pragmatic challenge:** The challenge arises in mixed neurotype contexts where the infodump may violate NT conversational expectations (turn-taking, topic management, audience assessment). Understanding WHEN infodumping is welcomed and when a different narrative mode is needed is a pragmatic skill that can be explicitly taught without invalidating the infodump itself.

([Autistic infodumping, Autism Awareness Centre](https://autismawarenesscentre.com/what-is-autistic-info-dumping/); [HeyASD](https://www.heyasd.com/blogs/autism/infodumping); [1SpecialPlace](https://www.1specialplace.com/post/autistic-communication-and-infodumping))

## 7. THE DOUBLE EMPATHY PERSPECTIVE ON NARRATIVE

### 7.1 The Double Empathy Problem Applied to Narrative

Milton's (2012) double empathy problem proposes that communication difficulties between autistic and non-autistic individuals are not solely due to autistic deficits but arise from a bidirectional mismatch in dispositions, communication styles, and experiences.

Applied to narrative, this raises critical questions:
- When NT listeners judge autistic narratives as "less coherent," is this an objective measurement or a reflection of NT narrative expectations?
- Do autistic listeners perceive autistic narratives differently from how NT listeners perceive them?
- Are autistic narratives "disordered" or "differently ordered"?

([Milton, 2012 - Double Empathy Problem](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/254245139_On_the_ontological_status_of_autism_The_'double_empathy_problem'); [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_empathy_problem))

### 7.2 How NT Listeners Perceive Autistic Communication

Research on first impressions reveals significant bias:

**Thin-slice judgments:** NT peers are less willing to interact with autistic individuals based on brief exposure (thin-slice judgments), but this bias is driven by presentation style rather than content. When speech transcripts were presented without visual/auditory cues, first impressions were not negative. This means the CONTENT of autistic communication may be fine; it's the delivery that triggers bias. ([Sasson et al., 2017, Scientific Reports](https://www.nature.com/articles/srep40700))

**Rater characteristics matter more than speaker characteristics.** Variability in how autistic adults are rated was driven more by characteristics of the raters (their stigma toward autism, their autism knowledge) than by characteristics of the autistic adults themselves. ([Variability in first impressions, PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30848682/))

**Disclosure improves perception.** Impressions improve when NT adults are made aware of the autism diagnosis, suggesting that context and understanding reduce bias. ([First Impressions, Frontiers 2023](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2023.1241584/full))

**Autistic vs. NT raters may differ.** Some research has examined whether first impressions of autistic adults differ between autistic and non-autistic observers, with findings suggesting that the "deficit" in communication may be partly in the eye of the beholder. ([Do First Impressions Differ, Autism in Adulthood](https://www.liebertpub.com/doi/10.1089/aut.2019.0018))

### 7.3 One-Sided Research

A critical methodological point: previous research on discourse coherence in autism has been largely one-sided, only considering coherence from the standpoint of neurotypical individuals, without also considering the perspective of autistic individuals. As researchers at UBC noted, "social communication is a two-sided dynamic whereby both communication partners contribute to the coherence of discourse." ([UBC Library](https://open.library.ubc.ca/cIRcle/collections/ubctheses/24/items/1.0384568))

This means that much of the "deficit" literature may be measuring deviation from NT norms rather than genuine communicative failure. A narrative that is perfectly comprehensible to another autistic listener might be rated as "incoherent" by an NT rater using NT narrative expectations.

### 7.4 Cultural Dimensions of "What Counts as a Good Story"

The Labovian narrative model (abstract-orientation-complication-evaluation-resolution-coda) is itself a culturally specific framework, primarily derived from Western, oral storytelling traditions. Different cultures have different narrative expectations:
- Some cultures value elaborate, digressive storytelling; others value brevity
- Some prioritize emotional expression; others prioritize factual accuracy
- Some expect a clear moral or lesson; others value ambiguity
- The "point" of a story can be cultural as much as universal

Autistic narrative styles could be viewed as a form of cultural variation rather than deficit -- a different set of conventions for what constitutes effective communication.

### 7.5 Autistic Narrative Styles as Valid Alternatives

From a neurodiversity perspective, several autistic narrative characteristics can be reframed:

| "Deficit" framing | Neurodiversity reframing |
|---|---|
| "Lacks evaluation" | Prioritizes informational content over emotional framing |
| "Too much detail" | Provides comprehensive, accurate information |
| "Poor audience design" | Communicates with precision rather than assumption |
| "Infodumping" | Deep-knowledge sharing as connection |
| "Missing the point" | Organizing around different priorities |
| "Tangential" | Making connections that are not obvious to NT listeners |
| "Flat affect in narration" | Letting content speak rather than performing emotion |

This does NOT mean that learning NT narrative conventions is unnecessary -- navigating a predominantly NT world often requires this skill. But it means that autistic narrative is not broken; it's different. The goal of teaching narrative skills is to ADD to an autistic person's communicative repertoire, not to replace their natural communication style.

## 8. NARRATIVE IDENTITY AND AUTOBIOGRAPHICAL MEMORY

### 8.1 The Connection Between Narrative and Self

Narrative theories of identity propose that individuals understand their personal experiences by creating coherent stories of their lives, which enable them to construct a sense of who they are across time. The life story is considered the highest level of autobiographical memory organization.

Research reveals important differences in autistic narrative identity:
- Global coherence, particularly causal-motivational coherence, is lower in life narratives of autistic individuals
- Autistic individuals' narrative identity is less sustained by connections between biographical experiences and personality
- This may lower autistic individuals' sense of self-continuity -- the feeling of being the same person across time
- Important personal events are correctly identified but are less integrated into the self when narrated

([Narrative identity differences, Nature 2025](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-00178-0); [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12084585/))

### 8.2 Autobiographical Memory Characteristics

Both children and adults with ASD manifest autobiographical memory characteristics:
- Reduced specificity in autobiographical narratives
- Less elaborated and detailed personal narratives (contrasting with the detail strength seen in other contexts)
- Lower coherence in personal stories
- Increased reliance on scaffolding for retrieval

The distinction between general event memory (which may be strong) and personally integrated autobiographical memory (which may be atypical) is important for understanding narrative identity in autism. ([Nature and Effects of Autobiographical Memory, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9586886/))

### 8.3 Therapeutic Implications

Psychotherapeutic interventions could target narrative coherence and the causal relationship within life events and between life events and one's self, to enhance autistic individuals' self-understanding, self-esteem, and sense of self-continuity across time. This suggests that narrative skills are not just social tools but are connected to fundamental aspects of identity and wellbeing.

## 9. PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS FOR TEACHING NARRATIVE TO AUTISTIC INDIVIDUALS

### 9.1 How to Structure a Story for Maximum Clarity and Impact

Based on the research, explicit teaching should cover these named, learnable elements:

**The Story Grammar Framework (simplified for practical use):**
1. **Setting/Context** -- WHEN and WHERE (and establish WHO)
2. **Background** -- What was the normal situation before the story events
3. **Trigger/Problem** -- What happened to change the normal situation
4. **Response/Action** -- What the character(s) did about it
5. **Outcome** -- How it turned out
6. **Reaction/Evaluation** -- How the character(s) felt about it, and why it matters

**Key teaching point:** Each element can be explicitly named and practiced. Visual icons or checklists can serve as planning tools. The framework applies to both fictional stories and personal narratives.

### 9.2 When and How to Include Evaluation

Evaluation is the element that transforms a report of events into a story. Explicit strategies include:

**Before the story:** State why you're telling it.
- "Something really frustrating happened to me today..."
- "You won't believe what I saw at the park..."
- "I had an experience that changed how I think about..."

**During the story:** Signal emotional weight at key moments.
- Use intensifiers: "incredibly," "unbelievably," "the worst"
- Use comparators: "bigger than," "unlike anything," "the first time I'd ever"
- Use causal explanations: "because I was nervous," "since I didn't know anyone"
- Use character reactions: "I couldn't believe it," "she looked shocked"

**After the story:** State the takeaway or impact.
- "And that's why I never go there anymore"
- "It made me realize that..."
- "So now I always..."

### 9.3 Managing Audience Design

Practical strategies for calibrating what the listener needs:

**The "Reset" check:** Before starting a narrative, mentally check: Does the listener know the people, places, and contexts I'm about to mention? If not, establish them first.

**The "First mention" rule:** The first time you mention a person, place, or thing in a narrative, use a full description. After that, you can use pronouns or short references.
- First mention: "my colleague Sarah from the marketing team"
- Subsequent mentions: "she" or "Sarah"

**The "Why should they care?" check:** Before or early in the narrative, give the listener a reason to pay attention. What's at stake? What's surprising? What's the connection to them?

**Reading comprehension cues:** Watch for signs that the listener needs more context (confused expressions, clarifying questions) or less detail (checking phone, short responses, trying to change subject).

### 9.4 When Detail-Heavy Narrative Is Valued

Explicit teaching should acknowledge that different contexts call for different narrative styles:

**Detail is valued in:**
- Technical reports and documentation
- Academic writing and presentations
- Medical/legal/scientific contexts
- Instructional and procedural contexts
- Expertise-sharing with interested audiences
- Written communication generally

**Brevity is valued in:**
- Casual social conversation
- Brief workplace updates
- Social media posts
- Elevator pitches
- When the listener is clearly busy or distracted

The autistic tendency toward detail and precision is not inherently a problem -- it's a mismatch with certain contexts. Learning to calibrate detail level to context is a learnable skill.

### 9.5 The "Two Toolkits" Approach

Rather than teaching autistic individuals to abandon their natural narrative style, the research supports teaching a dual approach:

**Toolkit 1: Natural autistic narrative style**
- Deep, detailed, knowledge-rich communication
- Precise, factual, sequentially organized
- Used with: trusted people, fellow autistic individuals, professional/academic contexts, written communication, special interest discussions

**Toolkit 2: NT-conventional narrative style**
- Story grammar structure with explicit evaluation
- Audience-calibrated detail level
- Causal and emotional language woven in
- Used with: formal/social situations, job interviews, casual conversation with NT acquaintances, situations where "the point" needs to be quickly grasped

The goal is not to replace Toolkit 1 with Toolkit 2, but to have both available and the pragmatic awareness to choose between them based on context.

## 10. SIX DIMENSIONS OF COHERENT STORYTELLING: THE 6Cs FRAMEWORK

A scoping review of narrative coherence research identified six key dimensions of coherent storytelling, formalized as the "6Cs":

1. **Context** -- providing appropriate setting information (when, where, who)
2. **Characterisation** -- developing characters with motivations, reactions, and personality
3. **Chronology** -- temporal ordering of events in a logical sequence
4. **Causality** -- linking events through cause and effect
5. **Cohesion** -- using linguistic devices to connect sentences and paragraphs
6. **Congruence** -- overall consistency and plausibility of the narrative

This framework provides a useful checklist for both assessment and teaching, with each dimension representing a specific, learnable skill.

([Assessing coherence in spoken narrative accounts, ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1750946723000077))

## 11. NEURAL AND COGNITIVE UNDERPINNINGS

### 11.1 Mentalizing and Narrative Coherence: The Cerebellar Connection

Research by Broekhof et al. (2023) suggests that mentalizing and narrative coherence are related at both behavioral and neural levels, with the cerebellum serving as an important hub in shared cerebral networks. The cerebellum, traditionally associated with motor coordination, is increasingly recognized for its role in:
- Sequencing complex information (including narrative events)
- Prediction (anticipating what comes next in a story)
- Social cognition (understanding others' mental states)

This suggests that difficulties with narrative in autism may reflect underlying sequencing and prediction processes that are atypical, not just "pragmatic deficits." ([Mentalizing and narrative coherence, ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0149763423000143))

### 11.2 Executive Function and Narrative

Despite theoretical predictions, the Harvey et al. (2025) study found that executive function (working memory, inhibition, shifting, generativity) did NOT predict narrative ability in autistic adolescents. This is an important null finding: it suggests that narrative difficulties are not simply a consequence of general executive dysfunction but are more specifically related to mentalizing and pragmatic skills.

### 11.3 The Broader Autism Phenotype in Narrative

Subtle differences in narrative ability have been documented among parents and siblings of autistic individuals, suggesting a genetic influence on narrative abilities associated with autism. This "broader autism phenotype" effect means that narrative style may exist on a continuum in the general population, with autistic individuals at one end of a dimension that varies across all people.

([Narrative Ability in Autism and First-Degree Relatives, Springer 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10803-024-06424-0); [Context effects, Frontiers 2025](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2025.1588429/full))

## RESEARCH GAPS AND LIMITATIONS

1. **Most research focuses on children and adolescents.** Adult autistic narrative research is still relatively sparse, though growing (Geelhand et al., 2020; Coburn & Williams, 2023).

2. **Gender bias in samples.** Most studies have used predominantly male samples. Coburn & Williams (2023) specifically addressed this gap.

3. **One-sided coherence judgments.** Most studies use NT raters to judge coherence, without considering how autistic listeners perceive the same narratives.

4. **Limited cultural diversity.** Most studies are conducted in English-speaking, Western contexts. Some cross-linguistic work exists (Mandarin, Spanish, Persian, Tamil) but more is needed.

5. **Focus on deficits over strengths.** The literature is heavily weighted toward documenting what autistic narrators do "wrong" compared to NT norms, with less attention to autistic narrative strengths or the communicative effectiveness of autistic narrative styles in appropriate contexts.

6. **Limited ecological validity.** Many studies use artificial elicitation tasks (picture books, video retelling) rather than naturalistic narrative situations. Harvey et al. (2025) explicitly addressed "everyday narrative skills" as a move toward greater ecological validity.

7. **Intervention research is still preliminary.** Most intervention studies are small-scale, preliminary, or pilot studies. Large-scale randomized controlled trials of narrative interventions for autistic individuals are still needed.
