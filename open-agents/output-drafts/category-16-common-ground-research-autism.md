# Category 16: Common Ground and Grounding in Autism

## Research Document for English Pragmatics Knowledge Base

**Focus**: How autistic individuals establish, track, and deploy common ground during communication -- the research evidence, practical consequences, and strategies for improvement

**Scope Note**: Common ground is the shared knowledge, beliefs, and assumptions that conversation partners rely on to communicate efficiently. Grounding is the interactive process by which partners collaboratively confirm that understanding has been achieved. This document covers both the theoretical framework and the specific research on how autism affects these fundamental pragmatic processes.

---

## 1. THEORETICAL FOUNDATIONS: WHAT IS COMMON GROUND?

### 1.1 Definition and Core Concept

Common ground, as defined in the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, is "a central notion in pragmatics, and a fixture in discussions of reference, presupposition, speech acts, implicature, language conventions, fiction, and many other topics related to language use." Robert Stalnaker (2002, 2014) defines it as "a body of information that is presumed to be shared by the parties to a discourse."

Herbert Clark, the researcher most associated with the concept, distinguishes several sources of common ground:

- **Communal common ground**: Knowledge shared by virtue of membership in the same community (e.g., all English speakers know what "hello" means; all Americans know who the president is; all programmers know what a variable is)
- **Personal common ground**: Knowledge shared from direct interaction history (e.g., "that restaurant we went to last week"; "as I mentioned in my email")
- **Perceptual common ground**: What both partners can currently see, hear, or perceive in the immediate environment (e.g., "that thing over there"; "did you hear that?")

### 1.2 Why Common Ground Matters for Communication

Common ground enables efficiency. Without it, every conversation would have to start from scratch. Consider the difference:

- WITH common ground: "Can you grab the thing?" (both partners can see the object; both know what "grab" means)
- WITHOUT common ground: "Can you extend your arm toward the rectangular blue object sitting on the flat brown wooden surface approximately 60 centimeters to your left, close your fingers around it, and lift it?"

Every time you use a pronoun ("he," "it," "they"), a shortened reference ("the meeting," "that email"), or an assumption about shared knowledge ("you know how parking is downtown..."), you are relying on common ground. Getting this wrong in either direction causes problems: too much assumed common ground leaves the listener confused; too little wastes their time and can feel condescending.

### 1.3 Grounding: The Process of Building Common Ground

Clark and Brennan (1991) described conversation as a "collaborative process" in which grounding is the interactive mechanism by which partners "collaboratively establish and maintain mutual understanding -- or common ground -- sufficient for their purposes." Their model involves:

- **Presentation phase**: Speaker presents new information
- **Acceptance phase**: Listener provides evidence of understanding (nods, "uh-huh," "right," paraphrasing, relevant next turns)
- **Repair phase**: When evidence of understanding is absent or signals confusion, speakers and listeners engage in repair sequences

The **principle of least collaborative effort** governs this process: both partners try to minimize the total work needed to achieve mutual understanding. This means:
- Speakers try to gauge what the listener knows and calibrate accordingly
- Listeners provide efficient feedback signals (backchannels, acknowledgments)
- Both partners adjust continuously based on moment-to-moment evidence

### 1.4 Mutual Manifestness: The Relevance Theory Perspective

Sperber and Wilson's Relevance Theory offers a complementary framework. Rather than "mutual knowledge" (which requires certainty), they propose "mutual manifestness" -- a fact or assumption is mutually manifest when both interlocutors can perceive or infer it, and both recognize that the other can perceive or infer it.

This distinction matters because common ground is probabilistic, not absolute. We never truly *know* what another person knows -- we estimate it based on community membership, interaction history, and perceptual co-presence. Williams, Wharton, and Jagoe (2021) argue that this framework is particularly illuminating for understanding autistic communication, because "what is most salient to each interlocutor may be mismatched" when autistic and non-autistic people interact, and mutual manifestness is the mechanism by which this mismatch creates communication breakdowns.

## 2. RESEARCH ON AUTISTIC COMMON GROUND MANAGEMENT

### 2.1 The Karin et al. (2025) Study: Paths to Common Ground in ASD

This landmark study, published in *Autism Research*, examined common ground generation between peer dyads: 84 children and adolescents with ASD and 56 controls, aged 6-16, paired into peer dyads.

**Key findings:**
- Both ASD and control dyads **generated common ground spontaneously** -- autistic participants were not unable to establish shared understanding
- However, the control group demonstrated **greater efficiency**, "achieving common conceptualization faster and with fewer words"
- A new measure, "conceptualization efficacy," quantified how quickly partners reached shared understanding, "reflecting the dynamic nature of real-world communication"
- **Path analysis** revealed independent contributions from **vocabulary** and **Theory of Mind** to common ground efficiency
- Age and common ground measures were correlated, "outlining CG development" -- older participants were better
- The findings "highlight the potential for interventions targeting CG acquisition in ASD, which is crucial for daily life participation"

**Critical implication for autistic adults**: The ability to establish common ground is present. The challenge is efficiency -- doing it quickly enough and with few enough words to keep pace with real-time conversation. This maps onto a pattern seen across many pragmatic domains in autism: the capability exists, but the real-time deployment is slower or more effortful.

### 2.2 The de Marchena and Eigsti (2016) Study: The Art of Common Ground

Published in the *Journal of Child Language*, this study examined common ground in adolescents with ASD and typical development (TD) using an experimental narrative paradigm.

**Design**: Participants told a story to a listener. In one condition, the listener had already seen the story (shared knowledge); in another, they hadn't (no shared knowledge). Typically developing speakers shorten their narratives when the listener already knows the story -- they don't belabor shared information.

**Key findings:**
- TD participants produced **shorter narrations** when they shared knowledge with the listener -- the expected efficiency effect
- ASD participants **did not show this reduction at the group level** -- they told the story at similar length regardless of whether the listener already knew it
- However, within the ASD group, **older and less severely affected participants DID shorten** their narratives for informed listeners
- Critically, "several metrics suggested that, although adolescents with ASD did not show implicit reductions in their narrative length, **they were aware of common ground**"
- Evidence of awareness included explicit references to common ground ("as you already know..."), speech disfluencies at transition points (suggesting cognitive effort to adjust), and quality ratings from listeners

**The key distinction**: Autistic adolescents KNEW the listener had shared knowledge but did NOT automatically adjust their speech production accordingly. This is the difference between **explicit awareness** (knowing what you share) and **implicit deployment** (spontaneously using that knowledge to calibrate speech in real time).

### 2.3 The Nadig, Vivanti, and Ozonoff (2009) Study: Audience Design

Using a referential communication task that manipulated visually shared information, this study assessed audience design (adjusting speech based on what the listener can see or know) at increasing levels of complexity.

**Key findings:**
- Group differences at **every level of complexity**, with ASD participants providing "less efficient and less context-appropriate descriptions"
- BUT there was **enormous individual variation** within the ASD group: some showed no audience design at all, while others showed audience design as sophisticated as the comparison group
- **Higher structural language ability** (not symptom severity or social skills) was the best predictor of which autistic individuals showed typical audience design profiles
- A richer vocabulary was related to more adapted descriptions and linked to more efficient common ground establishment

### 2.4 The Cola et al. (2022) Study: Conversational Adaptation

Published in *Autism Research*, this study examined whether autistic children and teens adjust their behavior to conversation partners who act interested versus bored.

**Design**: 48 verbally fluent children and teens with ASC and 50 neurotypical peers completed the Contextual Assessment of Social Skills with two novel conversation partners. The first acted interested (more engaged, talked more); the second acted bored (less engaged, talked less).

**Key findings:**
- NT participants **adapted**: they were more talkative in the Interested condition and less talkative in the Bored condition (z = 9.92, p < 0.001)
- ASC participants **did not differentially adapt**: they remained consistently talkative in both conditions (p = 0.88)
- "Difficulty adapting to the changing needs of a conversation contributes to communicative breakdowns and poor social outcomes"

**What this means practically**: Common ground isn't static -- it evolves during a conversation. A bored listener is signaling that the current topic or level of detail exceeds their interest. Adjusting to that signal IS common ground management in action: you're updating your model of what the listener wants and calibrating accordingly. The failure to adjust isn't about not caring -- it's about not detecting or acting on the real-time social signal.

### 2.5 The Koch, van Langen, Basnakova, and Stolk (2026) Study: Partner-Dependent Communication Without Dynamic Adaptation

This very recent study, published in *Autism*, examined how autistic and non-autistic individuals adapted communication with partners who were ostensibly a child versus an adult (both actually portrayed by the same confederate).

**Key findings:**
- Autistic participants **DID adjust** their communication initially -- they used greater emphasis, played slower, and were calmer when they thought they were communicating with a child
- **However**, while non-autistic participants **dynamically updated** their adjustments throughout the interaction (relaxing child-directed modifications as they got evidence the partner was competent), autistic participants **maintained their initial stereotype-driven adjustments** throughout
- When an **explicit cue** appeared (like an error message), **both groups were equally sensitive** and adjusted immediately
- The difference was specifically in picking up on **subtle, implicit social cues** that would prompt dynamic updating

**Critical implication**: Autistic individuals CAN and DO make initial partner-dependent adjustments to communication -- they are not oblivious to different partners' needs. The difficulty is in **dynamically updating** those adjustments based on ongoing implicit social feedback. This is precisely the kind of grounding mechanism that Clark and Brennan described: the moment-to-moment updating based on listener evidence.

### 2.6 The Malkin and Abbot-Smith (2021) Study: Set-Switching and Context-Appropriate Language

Published in *Autism*, this study examined whether autistic children's difficulty with context-appropriate language intensifies when they need to cognitively switch how they describe a previously-described object.

**Design**: 30 autistic children and neuro-typical 5-7-year-olds. In the Switch condition, the same animal had to be re-described across trials to be informative (e.g., "dog" alone is sufficient when only one dog is present, but "spotty dog" becomes necessary when a second dog appears).

**Key findings:**
- Autistic children were **more over-informative than peers even BEFORE the switch requirement** -- they already defaulted to providing more information than strictly necessary
- The requirement to switch descriptions **further worsened** performance in the autism group
- "Switching a description hinders the ability of children to be appropriately informative, and as autistic children are generally less appropriately informative, the requirement to switch leads to particularly poor performance in autism"

**What this means**: Over-informativeness (giving too much information) appears to be a **default tendency** in autistic communication, not just a failure under complex conditions. When cognitive demands increase (needing to flexibly update a description), this tendency becomes more pronounced.

### 2.7 The Malkin, Abbot-Smith, and Williams (2018) Systematic Review

This systematic review of 19 studies on verbal reference in ASD found that **17 of 19 studies showed impairment** in at least one measure of the appropriateness of match between context and the form of verbal reference. This review established that difficulties with referential communication are one of the most consistently documented pragmatic challenges in autism.

### 2.8 The Geelhand et al. (2025) Study: Referential Communication in Mixed-Neurotype Dyads

Published in *Autism*, this study compared 23 autistic dyads, 23 non-autistic dyads, and 21 mixed-neurotype dyads on a referential communication task (guiding a partner to rearrange abstract images).

**Key findings:**
- Non-autistic dyads completed the task **faster** than autistic and mixed dyads
- In mixed dyads, **autistic Directors were more verbose** than non-autistic Directors across both written and oral communication modes
- Participants were **unaware of their partner's neurotype**, ruling out conscious behavioral adjustment
- The increased verbosity in mixed pairings suggests that "even in the absence of non-verbal cues, neurotype mismatch triggers autistic adults to display different verbal behavior"

**What this means for common ground**: When autistic speakers sense (perhaps unconsciously) that communication isn't flowing smoothly, their response is to provide MORE information -- more words, more detail, more specification. This is a logical compensatory strategy: if unsure whether the listener understands, give more. But it's inefficient compared to targeted repair or checking.

### 2.9 The Schuh, Eigsti, and Mirman (2016) Study: Working Memory and Common Ground

Published in *Autism Research*, this study investigated whether working memory (WM) constrains common ground representation in autism.

**Key findings:**
- Groups **did not differ in Theory of Mind** ability
- All participants were slower to fixate the target when considering privileged vs. shared information, with this effect greatest under **high working memory load**
- The ASD group was **more likely to fixate competing (non-target) shapes** -- suggesting difficulty filtering out information that was NOT in common ground
- Individuals with **better WM fixated the target more rapidly**, regardless of group
- "In addition to ToM knowledge, WM capacity constrains common ground representation and impacts pragmatic skills in ASD"

**Critical implication**: Common ground management is a cognitive load task. You must simultaneously: (1) track what you know, (2) model what the listener knows, (3) compute the overlap, (4) select language that fits the overlap, and (5) do all this in real time while also planning what to say next. Working memory capacity limits how well anyone can do this, and the additional processing demands associated with autism make it harder.

## 3. OVERSPECIFICATION AND UNDERSPECIFICATION

### 3.1 Defining the Problem

Common ground calibration errors come in two forms:

**Overspecification** (providing too much information for the listener's needs):
- Explaining things the listener already knows
- Giving extensive background before reaching the point
- Including every detail rather than summarizing
- Using full names/descriptions when a pronoun would suffice
- Re-explaining shared history to someone who was present

**Underspecification** (not providing enough information for the listener's needs):
- Using pronouns when the listener doesn't know who "he" or "she" refers to
- Assuming the listener shares context they don't have
- Jumping into the middle of a topic without setup
- Using jargon or specialized terms without defining them
- Referencing shared knowledge that isn't actually shared

### 3.2 Research on Over-informativeness in Autism

Research consistently finds that **overspecification is the more frequent pattern** in autism:

- Malkin and Abbot-Smith (2021) found autistic children were over-informative even in simple conditions, before any additional cognitive demands were introduced
- Malkin, Abbot-Smith, and Williams (2018), in their systematic review, documented widespread over-informativeness in referential communication tasks
- Drakopoulou Kalantzi (2024) found that older autistic children were more likely to be overinformative, providing unnecessary contrast where context made it redundant
- The Simply Psychology analysis of autistic overexplaining identifies several contributing factors:
  - **Detail-oriented thinking**: "Autistic cognition is often very detail-oriented... they tend to include every relevant detail when explaining, since all those details feel important"
  - **Theory of mind differences**: "It's harder to judge exactly how much the other person knows or wants to know, leading to giving more explanation just to be safe"
  - **History of being misunderstood**: "Autistic adults describe being 'accustomed to being misunderstood' throughout their lives... they learn to go to great lengths to communicate clearly"
  - **Anxiety-driven compensation**: "This can be a kind of anxiety or trauma response -- always feeling you must justify or explain yourself so you won't get in trouble or won't be ignored"

### 3.3 The "Info-Dumping" Phenomenon

Info-dumping represents an extreme form of overspecification particularly associated with special interests:

- "In info dumping, one person gives a lot of information in a long talk or monologue, and the speaker just keeps sharing what they know without looking for quick replies or help from the listener" (Autism Awareness Centre)
- "During info dumping, a person may have difficulty recognizing when their listener is overwhelmed, uninterested, or ready to change topics"
- "Autistic people share every detail rather than providing summaries"

Info-dumping combines multiple common ground failures:
1. **Failing to estimate what the listener already knows** (providing too much background or too much detail)
2. **Failing to track listener engagement signals** (not noticing boredom, confusion, or time pressure)
3. **Failing to calibrate level of detail to listener needs** (giving expert-level detail to a casual inquirer)

### 3.4 Underspecification Patterns

Underspecification is less studied but also occurs in autism, particularly:

- **Reference Production Study (PMC3668432)**: Both overspecific and underspecific references were produced by both autism and control participants. Younger autistic participants had "a higher threshold for deciding that a pronoun or zero is sufficient" -- they needed the referent to be MORE prominent before they felt confident using just a pronoun rather than a full description
- In typical language, underspecification is rare (0.5-6% of referring expressions), while overspecification is common (21-60%), suggesting that the overspecification pattern in autism is an exaggeration of a normal tendency, while underspecification reflects a qualitatively different pattern

Underspecification in autism tends to occur in specific contexts:
- **Deep expertise/special interest topics**: When speaking about topics of intense knowledge, autistic speakers may assume more shared knowledge than exists because the information feels "obvious" or foundational to them
- **Assumed shared context**: Beginning a conversation in the middle of a thought, assuming the listener knows what prompted it
- **Pronoun reference**: Using "he," "it," or "they" when the listener doesn't have enough context to identify the referent
- **The curse of knowledge**: Once you know something deeply, it becomes very difficult to model what it's like NOT to know it -- this affects everyone but may be intensified in autism around areas of special interest

### 3.5 The Social Consequences

**Of overspecification:**
- Perceived as condescending, patronizing, or "talking down to" the listener
- Perceived as "lecturing" -- treating a dialogue as a monologue
- Listener feels their own knowledge is not respected or recognized
- Wastes the listener's time and patience
- In professional settings, can undermine credibility ("they don't seem to know what level we're at")

**Of underspecification:**
- Listener is confused, cannot follow the conversation
- Listener must repeatedly ask for clarification, which can be frustrating
- Creates impression that the speaker doesn't care about being understood
- In professional settings, can lead to misunderstandings with real consequences
- Listener may disengage rather than keep asking for clarification

## 4. THE THEORY OF MIND CONNECTION

### 4.1 Common Ground Requires Modeling What the Listener Knows

To calibrate common ground, a speaker must construct and maintain a model of the listener's knowledge state. This requires:

1. **Initial estimation**: What does this person probably know? (Based on their profession, their previous statements, their apparent familiarity with the topic, their community memberships)
2. **Real-time tracking**: As the conversation progresses, what have I told them? What have they acknowledged understanding? What have they introduced that shows they already know?
3. **Dynamic updating**: As I learn more about what they know (from their questions, responses, facial expressions), how do I adjust my model?

All three of these steps involve Theory of Mind -- the ability to represent another person's mental states as different from one's own.

### 4.2 The Explicit vs. Implicit ToM Distinction

Research reveals a critical gap in autism between explicit and implicit Theory of Mind that maps directly onto common ground difficulties:

**Explicit ToM**: The ability to deliberately consider others' mental states. Many autistic adults pass explicit false-belief tasks -- when asked "Does Sally know the marble was moved?", they correctly answer "no." This is the slow, deliberate, conscious computation of another person's knowledge state.

**Implicit/Spontaneous ToM**: The automatic, rapid, unconscious tracking of others' mental states in real time. Research shows that "high-functioning adults with ASD, who can easily pass the false belief task when explicitly asked, do not show spontaneous false belief attribution" (Senju et al., 2009; PMC3796729).

**Applied ToM**: Defined as "the ability to deploy ToM knowledge to successfully address ToM dilemmas as they are presented in real-world samples of behavior." This applied dimension is where autistic individuals often struggle despite possessing explicit knowledge.

### 4.3 The Gap Between Knowing and Tracking

The de Marchena and Eigsti (2016) finding is the perfect illustration of this gap: autistic adolescents KNEW the listener had shared knowledge (explicit awareness), made explicit references to it ("as you know..."), but did NOT automatically shorten their narratives (implicit deployment).

This maps onto a common autistic experience:
- **In preparation** (before the conversation): "I know Maria is an expert in machine learning, so I don't need to explain basic concepts to her"
- **In the moment** (during the conversation): Starting to explain what neural networks are to a machine learning PhD, because in the rapid flow of conversation, the system that should automatically suppress unnecessary explanation doesn't fire efficiently
- **After the fact** (retrospective awareness): "Oh, I was explaining neural networks to Maria -- she literally wrote a paper on them, why did I do that?"

This is NOT a lack of understanding about other people's minds. It is a **processing speed and automaticity problem**: the knowledge is there, but deploying it in real time, under the cognitive load of ongoing conversation, is the challenge.

### 4.4 Working Memory as a Bottleneck

Schuh, Eigsti, and Mirman (2016) demonstrated that working memory capacity constrains common ground representation, independent of Theory of Mind. This makes sense: common ground tracking requires holding multiple representations simultaneously:

- What I know
- What I think they know
- The overlap (shared knowledge)
- The gap (what I know that they don't)
- What they've said that indicates understanding or confusion
- What I've already explained vs. what I haven't

This is a substantial working memory load. When working memory is taxed (by stress, fatigue, multitasking, sensory overload, or the general increased cognitive demands of social interaction in autism), common ground tracking is one of the first things to deteriorate.

## 5. GROUNDING BEHAVIORS IN AUTISM

### 5.1 What Grounding Behaviors Are

Grounding behaviors are the signals that conversation partners exchange to confirm mutual understanding. Clark and Brennan (1991) identified key grounding evidence:

- **Backchannels**: "Uh-huh," "yeah," "right," "okay," "mm-hmm" -- signals that the listener is following
- **Head nods**: Visual confirmation of understanding
- **Relevant next turns**: When a listener responds with something relevant, it implicitly confirms they understood
- **Paraphrasing**: "So what you're saying is..." -- explicit evidence of understanding
- **Clarification requests**: "Wait, what do you mean by...?" -- signals that understanding has NOT been achieved
- **Facial expressions**: Looks of confusion, understanding, surprise, etc.

### 5.2 Reduced Backchanneling in Autism

Research on backchanneling in autism (connecting to Category 14) reveals:

- **Frequency**: "ASD participants produced significantly fewer instances of listener feedback than neurotypical adolescents, which likely contributes to difficulties with social interactions" (PMC8854326)
- **Diversity**: "Backchannels in conversations between autistic adults are less frequent and less diverse prosodically and lexically" (Cambridge Core, Language and Cognition)
- **Autistic speakers produce fewer backchannels per minute** than matched controls, "suggesting they are overall less inclined to verbally support the ongoing turn of their interlocutor"

**Important nuance on autistic-autistic rapport**: "Although use of mutual gaze and backchanneling was lower in mixed pairs than in nonautistic pairs, less backchanneling in autistic pairs of both nonverbal and verbal subtypes **corresponded to higher ratings of rapport**" (PMC8992924). This means reduced backchanneling within autistic-autistic communication does NOT indicate reduced understanding or engagement -- it may simply be a different, equally valid interactional style.

### 5.3 How Reduced Backchanneling Affects Grounding

When a listener provides few or no backchannels, the speaker loses a crucial source of grounding evidence. This creates a cascade:

1. **Speaker uncertainty**: "Are they following? Did they understand? Are they engaged?"
2. **Compensatory overspecification**: Without feedback confirming understanding, speakers may repeat, elaborate, or add more detail
3. **Failed calibration**: Without signals of confusion or boredom, speakers can't adjust their level of detail
4. **Misattribution**: NT speakers may interpret lack of backchanneling as disinterest, disagreement, or hostility

This is bidirectional: when an autistic listener provides fewer backchannels, the NT speaker also loses grounding evidence and may miscalibrate. When an autistic speaker provides information without checking for listener feedback, the conversation becomes one-directional.

### 5.4 Repair as a Grounding Mechanism

Conversational repair (covered extensively in Category 4) IS a grounding mechanism. When someone says "Wait, I don't follow," they are providing grounding evidence -- specifically, evidence that grounding has FAILED, triggering a repair sequence.

Research on repair in autism (Volden, 2004) shows:
- Autistic speakers CAN respond to repair requests with various strategies
- They "recognized communicative breakdown and responded to requests for clarification with a wide variety of repair strategies"
- However, spontaneous self-monitoring and self-initiated repair may be reduced
- The ability to repair "requires syntactic, semantic, phonological, and lexical knowledge, as well as Theory of Mind, social awareness, nonverbal communication, emotion regulation, and persistence" -- a high cognitive load task

The connection is clear: if grounding evidence is sparse (reduced backchanneling from autistic listeners) AND monitoring for grounding failure is effortful (reduced self-monitoring from autistic speakers), AND repair initiation is reduced (both self-initiated and other-initiated), then the entire grounding system operates with less feedback than typical conversations provide.

### 5.5 Checking for Understanding: The Missing Explicit Strategy

One underexplored compensation strategy is **explicit grounding checks** -- meta-communicative moves that directly ask about the state of mutual understanding:

- "Does that make sense?"
- "Am I explaining too much?"
- "Should I give more context?"
- "Do you already know about X?"
- "Tell me if I'm going over things you already know"
- "Am I on the right track?"

These explicit checks substitute for the implicit grounding evidence that may not flow as freely in autistic conversations. They transform the automatic process into a deliberate one -- which matches the autistic cognitive profile of being stronger at explicit, deliberate processing than at automatic, implicit processing.

## 6. THE DOUBLE EMPATHY PERSPECTIVE ON COMMON GROUND

### 6.1 The Double Empathy Problem

Damian Milton (2012) proposed the double empathy problem: communication difficulties between autistic and non-autistic people are not solely the result of autistic deficits but reflect a **bidirectional mismatch** between differently disposed social actors. "Problems of interaction between autistic and neurotypical people isn't necessarily all down to a deficit on the part of the autistic person, but rather involves a mismatch in communication style and mismatch in background."

### 6.2 Williams, Wharton, and Jagoe (2021): Mutual (Mis)understanding

This influential paper, published in *Frontiers in Psychology*, reframes autistic pragmatic "impairments" using Relevance Theory:

**Key arguments:**
- Communication is "a two-way, intersubjective phenomenon -- as described by the double empathy problem"
- Relevance Theory's concept of "mutual manifestness" offers a model for understanding cross-neurotype communication breakdowns
- "Given that autistic and non-autistic people may have sometimes markedly different embodied experiences of the world, what is most salient to each interlocutor may be mismatched"
- "Humans are most successful at inferring the mental and affective states of those others who are most cognitively similar to themselves"
- Rather than seeing autistic language as a "test ground for pragmatic hypotheses based on assumed impaired mind-reading abilities," the authors reframe interactions as "mutually-mediated endeavours influenced by achieving mutual manifestness"

**The mutual manifestness model**: In Relevance Theory, a person's "cognitive environment" consists of all the assumptions manifest to them. For mutual communication to succeed, interlocutors need a shared cognitive environment -- they need enough overlap in what's manifest to them. When autistic and non-autistic people interact, their cognitive environments may overlap less than two people of the same neurotype, because:
- Different sensory experiences make different aspects of the environment salient
- Different interaction histories create different assumptions about "normal" behavior
- Different processing styles lead to different inferences from the same input
- Different cultural knowledge (autistic culture vs. NT culture) creates different background assumptions

### 6.3 Common Ground Within vs. Between Neurotypes: The Crompton Research

Catherine Crompton's diffusion chain studies provide the strongest evidence that common ground functions differently within vs. between neurotypes:

**Crompton et al. (2020)**: "Autistic peer-to-peer information transfer is highly effective"
- Participants told a story along a "diffusion chain" of eight people
- "Autistic people share information with other autistic people as well as non-autistic people do with other non-autistic people"
- "When there are mixed groups of autistic and non-autistic people, much less information is shared"
- "People in mixed groups also experienced lower rapport"

**Crompton et al. (2025, Nature Human Behaviour)**: A replication with larger samples found no difference in information transfer between single-neurotype and mixed-neurotype chains overall, BUT non-autistic chains indicated higher rapport, and disclosing diagnosis improved rapport across pairings.

**Neurotype-Matching and Rapport (Crompton et al., 2020, Frontiers in Psychology)**: "Participants, regardless of diagnostic status, give poorer ratings of rapport for mixed neurotype pairs than for matched neurotype pairs, suggesting that a mismatch between neurotypes results in lower ratings of rapport."

### 6.4 What the Double Empathy Perspective Means for Common Ground

The double empathy reframing transforms how we understand common ground challenges:

**Traditional framing** (deficit model): "Autistic people fail to establish common ground because they lack Theory of Mind."

**Double empathy framing**: Common ground mismatches occur bidirectionally:
- An NT speaker may assume common ground that the autistic listener doesn't share (e.g., assuming the autistic person picks up on facial expression changes, reads "between the lines," or interprets sarcasm as intended)
- An autistic speaker may assume common ground that the NT listener doesn't share (e.g., assuming the NT person cares about the detailed accuracy of a claim, or shares excitement about a special interest topic, or understands the literal meaning of their words without emotional loading)
- Non-autistic people struggle to read autistic emotional expressions and mental states just as autistic people struggle to read non-autistic ones -- "non-autistic people had trouble deciphering mental states autistic people portrayed through animations"

### 6.5 The Geelhand et al. (2025) Evidence: Neurotype Mismatch Triggers Behavioral Change

The finding that autistic adults become more verbose ONLY in mixed-neurotype dyads -- without even knowing their partner's neurotype -- suggests that something about the interaction itself signals mismatch. This could be:
- Reduced or absent implicit grounding signals from the NT partner (who is also struggling with the mismatch)
- Different timing, rhythm, or pacing expectations
- Subtle mismatches in backchanneling patterns or acknowledgment timing
- Different assumptions about how much explanation is needed

The autistic response to this sensed mismatch -- providing more information -- is a logical but inefficient compensatory strategy. More words may not solve the problem if the issue is a fundamental difference in communicative style or expectations.

## 7. COMMON GROUND IN DIFFERENT AUTISTIC CONTEXTS

### 7.1 Autistic-Autistic Common Ground

When two autistic people communicate, they share a layer of common ground that is absent in mixed-neurotype interactions:

**Shared cultural knowledge:**
- Understanding of sensory experiences and sensory overload
- Knowledge about stimming and self-regulation strategies
- Awareness of masking and its costs
- Shared vocabulary: "info-dumping," "special interest," "spoons," "meltdown vs. shutdown"
- Understanding that direct communication is a feature, not a bug
- Recognition that reduced eye contact or flat affect does not signal disinterest

**Shared communication norms:**
- Acceptance of longer pauses and different conversational rhythms
- Tolerance for (and even enjoyment of) info-dumps
- Less expectation of backchanneling
- Greater acceptance of topic shifts or deep dives
- Literal interpretation as default, with explicit marking of non-literal intent
- "While non-autistic people often prioritise social norms in an interaction, autistic people tend to value direct conversations and the ability to share ideas and information inherently interesting to them" (Reframing Autism)

**Research evidence**: Crompton's work demonstrates that autistic-autistic pairs achieve comparable information transfer to NT-NT pairs, and autistic pairs report higher rapport, flow, and intersubjective attunement than mixed pairs. This suggests that when the common ground foundation is solid (shared norms, shared experience, shared communication style), autistic communication works effectively.

### 7.2 Online Autistic Communities

Online spaces provide a unique common ground advantage for autistic communication:

- **Text-based interaction**: Removes need for real-time prosodic and facial expression processing, "bypassing non-verbal cues and emotional sharing that some autistics tend to have difficulty with" (Autism and communication online research)
- **Processing time**: "Text-based online communities provided opportunities for participants to process interactions on their own time"
- **Shared vocabulary**: Online autistic communities develop extensive shared terminology and reference points
- **Explicit communication norms**: Many autistic online spaces develop explicit rules about communication (content warnings, tone indicators like /s and /gen, explicit labeling of sarcasm)
- **Accumulated common ground**: Forum histories create a persistent, searchable shared knowledge base -- unlike spoken conversation, where common ground must be tracked in memory
- **Identity and culture building**: "Online blog spaces facilitated the emergence and construction of empowered individual and collective identities" (PMC8992807)

### 7.3 Professional and Expertise Contexts

Professional settings create an interesting common ground dynamic for autistic individuals:

**Where autistic expertise IS the common ground:**
- In technical or specialized fields, deep knowledge is valued and expected
- When the listener is there specifically TO learn (teaching, consulting, presenting at conferences), extensive explanation is appropriate rather than over-informative
- Domain-specific jargon creates a shared vocabulary that reduces ambiguity
- Written communication (emails, documentation, code reviews) allows for precise, detailed communication without real-time calibration demands

**Where professional common ground creates challenges:**
- Unwritten rules ("everyone knows you CC the manager on these emails")
- Assumed social conventions ("obviously you don't eat lunch alone in your office every day")
- Small talk and relationship-building expectations where the required common ground is social rather than technical
- Meetings where the "real" information is communicated through subtext, facial expressions, or political dynamics rather than explicit content

### 7.4 Mixed-Neurotype Settings

The most challenging common ground scenario occurs in mixed-neurotype groups where:

- **Different communication style defaults**: Autistic members may prioritize accuracy and completeness; NT members may prioritize brevity and social harmony
- **Different assumptions about "obvious" knowledge**: NT members may assume everyone reads the social subtext; autistic members may assume everyone values explicit statement
- **Different grounding evidence norms**: NT members may expect frequent backchannels and eye contact as engagement signals; autistic members may show engagement through questions and content-focused responses
- **Asymmetric accommodation**: Research shows NT individuals often expect autistic individuals to adapt to NT norms, while rarely adjusting their own communication style

## 8. PRACTICAL STRATEGIES FOR COMMON GROUND MANAGEMENT

### 8.1 Estimating Common Ground with a New Partner

Before and during early conversation, assess:

**Community membership clues:**
- What is this person's profession? (Tells you what technical knowledge to assume)
- What is the context of this interaction? (Work meeting? Casual party? Online forum?)
- What topic brought you together? (Conference session? Shared friend? Work project?)
- What have they said so far? (Their vocabulary and references reveal their knowledge level)

**Explicit information-gathering moves:**
- "Are you familiar with X?" (Directly asks about knowledge before explaining)
- "How much do you know about Y?" (Establishes a baseline)
- "I'm not sure if you've heard about Z..." (Hedged introduction that invites them to confirm or deny knowledge)
- "Just in case -- do you know what [term] means?" (Acknowledges uncertainty without assuming ignorance)

**The key principle**: When in doubt, ASK rather than guess. Explicit checking is more reliable than trying to intuit someone's knowledge state.

### 8.2 Monitoring for Over- and Underspecification

**Signs you may be overspecifying (providing too much):**
- The listener says "Yeah, I know" or "Right, right" repeatedly
- The listener finishes your sentences
- The listener looks away, checks their phone, shifts restlessly
- The listener says "You don't need to explain that part"
- You're explaining the basics to someone you know is an expert
- You've been talking for more than 2-3 minutes without the listener contributing

**Signs you may be underspecifying (not providing enough):**
- The listener asks "Wait, who?" or "What do you mean?"
- The listener has a confused expression (furrowed brow, tilted head)
- The listener asks you to back up or start over
- The listener says "I don't follow" or "You've lost me"
- You're using specialized jargon and the listener doesn't seem to be in that field
- You started a topic without context and the listener seems to be searching for the connection

### 8.3 Explicit Grounding Strategies

These meta-communicative moves can substitute for implicit grounding:

**Checking outgoing calibration:**
- "Am I explaining too much? I'm not sure how much background you need."
- "I tend to go into a lot of detail -- tell me if you want the short version."
- "Am I being clear enough, or should I give more context?"
- "I know you probably already know this, but just to make sure we're on the same page..."

**Checking incoming comprehension:**
- "Does that make sense so far?"
- "Any questions before I continue?"
- "What's your understanding of what we just discussed?"

**Signaling your own knowledge state:**
- "I'm not sure I follow -- can you back up to the part about X?"
- "I think I know what you mean, but just to confirm: you're saying [paraphrase]?"
- "I'm supposed to know about this, aren't I? I'm actually not familiar -- can you give me a quick overview?"
- "I know the basics of X but I'm not sure about the details of Y."

### 8.4 Managing the Curse of Knowledge with Special Interests

When discussing a topic you know deeply:

1. **Before speaking**: Ask yourself, "What is the minimum this person needs to know for this conversation?" Start there, not at the beginning of all knowledge on the topic.

2. **Start with the headline**: Give the conclusion or main point FIRST, then offer to elaborate. "The short version is [X]. Want me to explain how that works?"

3. **Create explicit opt-in points**: Rather than delivering a monologue, create natural stopping points where the listener can indicate whether they want more. "So that's the basic idea. Should I go into more detail about [specific aspect]?"

4. **Watch for the 2-minute rule**: If you've been speaking for more than about 2 minutes without the other person contributing, pause and check in. "Sorry, I'm going on -- is this what you wanted to know, or am I going off track?"

5. **Use the "elevator pitch" approach**: Practice summarizing your special interest in 30 seconds, 2 minutes, and 5 minutes. In most casual conversations, the 30-second version is appropriate. Save the longer versions for people who explicitly ask for more.

6. **Label your awareness**: "I know I tend to go really deep on this topic, so please stop me if I'm giving you too much." This explicit meta-communication lets the listener know you're aware of the tendency and gives them permission to redirect.

### 8.5 Handling Being Asked About Something You "Should" Know

This is a common and stressful common ground situation: someone assumes knowledge you don't have, and you feel pressure to pretend you know.

**Strategies:**
- **Direct acknowledgment**: "Actually, I'm not familiar with that -- can you fill me in?" (Works well in professional contexts where honesty is valued)
- **Partial acknowledgment**: "I know the name but not the details -- can you give me the quick version?" (Acknowledges some knowledge without pretending to full familiarity)
- **Redirect**: "I've heard different things about that -- what's your take?" (Buys time while getting the other person's perspective)
- **It's usually okay to not know**: Most people would rather explain something than discover later that you pretended to understand and got confused

### 8.6 Strategies Specific to Autistic-NT Interactions

**For the autistic speaker:**
- Be explicit about your communication style: "I tend to be very detailed -- feel free to tell me when you have enough"
- Ask about the listener's needs: "Do you want the full explanation or the summary?"
- Practice checking in: Set a mental timer to pause every minute or two and create space for the listener to respond
- When receiving listener signals you're not sure about (is that a bored face or a thinking face?), ask directly: "You look like you might want to say something?"

**For recognizing when an NT speaker is assuming common ground you don't share:**
- It's okay to say "I'm not picking up what you're implying -- can you be more direct?"
- "I think there's something between the lines that I'm missing -- can you spell it out?"
- "I want to make sure I understand: are you saying [literal interpretation] or [possible implied meaning]?"

## 9. REFERENTIAL PACTS AND PARTNER-SPECIFIC COMMUNICATION

### 9.1 What Are Referential Pacts?

When conversation partners repeatedly refer to the same thing, they tend to converge on a preferred term -- a "referential pact." For example, two coworkers might start calling a particular meeting room "the fishbowl" because it has glass walls. Once established, this pact becomes part of their personal common ground.

### 9.2 Research on Referential Pacts in Autism

The Frontiers in Psychology study (Brock, Norbury, Einav, & Nation, 2015) on partner-specific referential pacts in adults with ASD found:

- ASD and NT groups "did not differ with respect to the number of descriptors they provided or the number of exchanges needed for matchers to identify figures" -- basic referential communication was intact
- The ASD group was "slightly slower overall"
- NT directors maintained referential precedents with the same partner but NOT with a new partner -- showing partner-specific common ground tracking
- Directors with ASD "were qualitatively similar but displayed a less pronounced distinction between matchers"
- When speaking to a new partner, NT directors incorporated the new partner's contributions into descriptions, while "directors with ASD were less likely to do so"
- "A minority of directors with ASD continued to maintain referential precedents when speaking to a new matcher who did not share history" -- using terms from a previous partnership that the new partner wouldn't know
- "The majority made their descriptions more informative like neurotypical directors, but experienced delays in doing so"

**What this means practically**: Autistic adults CAN establish referential pacts and DO show sensitivity to partner-specific knowledge. But the distinction between "what I share with Partner A" and "what I share with Partner B" is less pronounced and slower to update. This could manifest as using inside jokes or shared references with someone who wasn't present when they were established, or failing to notice that a term that was clear to one colleague needs explanation for another.

## 10. THE MONOTROPISM CONNECTION

### 10.1 Monotropism and Attention Tunnels

Monotropism -- the tendency to allocate attention intensely to a small number of interests at any time -- has implications for common ground management:

- When deeply engaged with a topic (in the "attention tunnel"), monitoring the listener's state requires shifting attention AWAY from the content -- which monotropic processing resists
- The experience of "flow" in discussion of a special interest can override the social monitoring system that tracks listener engagement
- "In the monotropic mind, interests that are active at any given time tend to consume most of the available attention, causing difficulty with other tasks such as conventional social interaction"

### 10.2 Building Common Ground Through Interests

Monotropism also offers a pathway TO common ground:

- Shared interests create natural, powerful common ground -- two people with the same special interest have an enormous foundation of shared knowledge
- "Rather than trying to just reach in and pull the person out of the flow states, it's important to become part of the attention tunnels"
- When communication is ABOUT a deep interest, the autistic speaker's expertise provides genuinely useful common ground -- the challenge shifts from establishing shared knowledge to calibrating the level of detail

## 12. SYNTHESIS: THE COMMON GROUND PROFILE IN AUTISM

### 12.1 What Is Preserved

- The ABILITY to establish common ground
- AWARENESS that common ground exists and matters
- The ability to make INITIAL partner-dependent adjustments
- The ability to form referential pacts (shared terms)
- Effective common ground management within autistic-autistic pairs
- Response to EXPLICIT signals of misunderstanding
- The ability to use repair when breakdown is detected

### 12.2 What Is Challenged

- **Efficiency**: Getting to shared understanding takes more words and more time
- **Automaticity**: The implicit, spontaneous deployment of common ground knowledge
- **Dynamic updating**: Adjusting calibration based on ongoing subtle social signals
- **Monitoring**: Tracking listener engagement, boredom, confusion, or overwhelm in real time
- **Bidirectional calibration**: Managing both over- and underspecification simultaneously
- **Speed**: Processing common-ground-relevant information fast enough for real-time conversation
- **Cognitive load management**: Common ground tracking competes with content processing, especially under working memory load

### 12.3 The Teaching Opportunity

This profile suggests specific, concrete intervention targets:

1. **Make implicit processes explicit**: Teach the components of common ground estimation as a deliberate checklist rather than an automatic process
2. **Build meta-communicative skills**: Teach explicit grounding checks ("Am I explaining too much?") as a substitute for automatic calibration
3. **Develop calibration heuristics**: Rules of thumb for how much to explain based on context (e.g., "If they're an expert, start with the conclusion; if they're new to the topic, start with background")
4. **Practice headline-first communication**: Reducing the tendency toward chronological, comprehensive explanation by starting with the point and adding detail as requested
5. **Leverage written communication**: Text-based formats allow more time for common ground estimation and remove real-time monitoring demands
6. **Normalize explicit checking**: Frame "Am I going into too much detail?" not as a deficit but as a sophisticated communication strategy
