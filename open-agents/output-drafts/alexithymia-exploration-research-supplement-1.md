# Supplemental Exploration: Alexithymia — Round 1

## Purpose

This supplement addresses the seven Research Opportunities identified by the validator after the first exploration pass. It does **not** replace the parent exploration. The parent's 11-category taxonomy and 7-axis Heterogeneity Map remain intact; this document corrects specific factual errors, tightens contested claims, and substantially expands one section (alexithymia-as-adaptive-trait) that the user has elevated from a side question to a defining frame for the deep dive.

The most consequential correction concerns the Bird/Murphy interoception trajectory: the parent exploration's "Bird walked back the interoception-deficit hypothesis" framing is incorrect. The Bird group has not retreated from the interoception-deficit-in-alexithymia thesis; on the contrary, they have *strengthened* it (Murphy, Brewer, Catmur, Bird 2018; Brewer, Murphy, Bird 2021). What was walked back is the autism-specific claim — and that walk-back is asymmetric: the Bird group's position is precisely that interoceptive deficits track *alexithymia*, not autism. Downstream content must distinguish these.

The longest section below — Topic 3, the adaptive/functional analysis — gives the user a substantive evidence base for holding alexithymia as a trait with trade-offs rather than a pure deficit. Existing peer-reviewed work documents at least five domains in which alexithymia is associated with selectively *altered* (not always worse) cognition: framing-effect resistance, ambiguity-versus-risk decision asymmetry, emotion-attention deployment, divergent-thinking idea generation, and culturally-fitted emotional restraint. None of these are large bodies of evidence; together they warrant an honest "trade-off" framing rather than the "pure deficit" framing of most clinical literature.

## Topic 1: Bird/Murphy interoception trajectory — corrected account

### What the original exploration says (and what's wrong)

The parent exploration claims: "Bird/Murphy later studies have walked some of this back. The 'alexithymia is a general failure of interoception' claim is contested." This sentence conflates two different things and reads as if the *Bird group itself* retracted its own thesis. They have not.

### What the Bird/Murphy/Brewer lineage actually argues

**(a) Brewer, Cook, Bird (2016) — "Alexithymia: a general deficit of interoception"** ([Royal Society Open Science](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsos.150664), [PubMed 27853532](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27853532/)).
The foundational paper. Across 208 participants (105 typical adults, 103 reporting psychiatric diagnoses), self-reported alexithymia was associated with *poor non-affective interoception* and with *increased perceived similarity* between affective and non-affective body states. Conclusion: alexithymia is better characterised as a general failure of interoception than as a specifically emotional one. Crucially, this was a self-reported-interoception study — which becomes important for the 2018 fork.

**(b) Shah, Hall, Catmur, Bird (2016) — "Alexithymia, not autism, is associated with impaired interoception"** ([Cortex, PubMed 27253723](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27253723/)). Two experiments using *behavioural* (heartbeat-detection) interoception measures. The empirical claim was: across autistic and non-autistic samples, the variance in interoceptive accuracy is captured by alexithymia level, not by autism diagnosis. Interoceptive deficits are therefore a feature of *alexithymia*, not of autism per se. This is the "alexithymia hypothesis of autism" (Bird & Cook 2013) extended into the interoception literature.

**(c) Murphy, Brewer, Catmur, Bird (2018) — "Alexithymia is associated with a multidomain, multidimensional failure of interoception: Evidence from novel tests"** ([J Exp Psychol Gen, PMC5824617](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5824617/), [PubMed 29154612](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29154612/)). The key paper for this correction. Three experiments using novel non-cardiac interoception tasks (respiratory output, muscular effort, taste sensitivity). High-alexithymia individuals showed reduced interoceptive accuracy and reduced *use* of interoceptive cues across all three domains, even after controlling for co-occurring autism, depression, and anxiety. This is a *strengthening*, not a walk-back, of the multidomain interoception-deficit account.

**(d) Murphy, Brewer, Hobson, Catmur, Bird (2018) — "Is alexithymia characterised by impaired interoception? Further evidence, the importance of control variables, and the problems with the Heartbeat Counting Task"** ([Biol Psychol, PubMed 29803614](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29803614/), [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0301051118301066)). N=287. Initial bivariate analyses showed no relationship between alexithymia and HCT. *After controlling* for age, gender, time-estimation ability, BMI, knowledge of average HR, HRV, depression, anxiety, systolic BP, and average resting HR, the predicted negative relationship between alexithymia and HCT performance emerged. The paper has two findings: (i) the association is real once confounds are controlled, (ii) the HCT itself is methodologically problematic. The methodological critique is sometimes mistaken for a substantive walk-back; it is not.

**(e) Murphy, Catmur, Bird (2018) — "Interoceptive impairments do not lie at the heart of autism or alexithymia"** ([PMC6089261](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6089261/), [PubMed 30102067](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30102067/)). This is the paper most easily mistaken for a walk-back. Across multiple experiments using the *cardiac* HCT, Bayesian analyses supported the null hypothesis — neither autism nor alexithymia was associated with HCT performance. But notice the paper appears in the *same year* as (c) and (d), which both reach the opposite conclusion using different tasks. The authors' own resolution is that the deficit may lie in *interpretation* of physiological signals rather than detection per se, and that HCT-based detection measures may be insensitive to the actual locus of impairment. Read alongside (c) and (d), the picture is: the Bird group considers the cardiac HCT a poor measurement tool, but considers the underlying multidomain interoception deficit (especially in non-cardiac, sensibility-related, and labelling-related dimensions) well-supported.

**(f) Hobson, Brewer, Catmur, Bird (2019) — "The Role of Language in Alexithymia: Moving Towards a Multiroute Model"** ([Emotion Review, SAGE](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1754073919838528)). The Bird group introduces a multiroute account: alexithymia is the common downstream phenotype of multiple possible upstream impairments — interoception is one route, language/labelling is another, emotion-concept conceptualisation may be a third. This is the closest the lineage comes to qualifying its position. The qualification is that interoception failure is *one route* (still affirmed as a route) rather than *the* route.

**(g) Brewer, Murphy, Bird (2021) — "Atypical interoception as a common risk factor for psychopathology: A review"** ([Neurosci Biobehav Rev, PMC8522807](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8522807/), [PubMed 34358578](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34358578/)). The authoritative review of the Bird group's current synthesis. Direct quote from the review: "many individuals with alexithymia experience a generalised interoceptive impairment, rather than one specific to emotional processing." Impairment is hypothesised at multiple representational levels — conscious detection, recognition/labelling, or both — and individuals may differ in which level is affected. The review explicitly notes that "a recent meta-analysis noted no simple relationship between alexithymia and objectively measured interoceptive accuracy" — an honest qualification, but not a retraction. The general framework (multidomain failure, with heterogeneity across affected representational levels) is preserved.

### Bottom line for downstream content

The Bird/Murphy/Brewer lineage's published position in 2025 is:

1. **Alexithymia involves a multidomain, multidimensional interoception failure** — affirmed across non-cardiac modalities (respiration, muscular effort, taste, thermal). Not a walked-back claim.
2. **The cardiac heartbeat-counting task is methodologically problematic** — and effects emerge in HCT data only after careful control of confounds. This is a methodological clarification, not a substantive retreat.
3. **Autism per se is not associated with interoception deficits** — the variance loads on co-occurring alexithymia. *This* is the actual walk-back, and it is autism-specific.
4. **The deficit may be at multiple levels** — detection, recognition, labelling — and different alexithymics may sit at different levels. This was clarified, not retracted, between 2016 and 2021.
5. **Language/conceptualisation may be a parallel route to alexithymia** — Hobson et al. 2019's multiroute model qualifies but does not abandon the interoception-route account.

### Where the parsing-deficit reading actually comes from

The parent exploration's "parsing-deficit-not-detection-deficit" framing is *defensible* but its attribution is wrong. It is not Bird's revised position. The closest sources for the parsing-deficit reading are:

- **Garfinkel, Seth, Barrett, Suzuki, Critchley (2015) — "Knowing your own heart"** ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0301051114002294)). The three-dimensional model: interoceptive accuracy (objective performance), interoceptive sensibility (self-reported tendency to attend to body signals), and interoceptive awareness (metacognitive correspondence between accuracy and sensibility). These three dimensions are dissociable — a person can have high sensibility (lots of body attention) but low accuracy (poor metacognition about what the signals mean). This is the architectural basis for distinguishing *detection* from *labelling* deficits.

- **The quadratic-relationship study (Trevisan, Mehling, McPartland 2020)** ([Front Psychiatry, PMC7076086](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7076086/)). A specific empirical finding: alexithymia and interoceptive accuracy show a *U-shaped* relationship — both low *and* high IA can co-occur with high alexithymia. Alexithymia is best understood, on this account, as a problem of interpretation/labelling that can occur with either weak or hypervigilant body-signal detection. The same study ran a pilot mindfulness body-scan intervention and found this depended on baseline IA — an early signal that subtype-matched intervention may be the right framework.

- **Heartbeat-counting-task methodological critiques (Zamariola et al., Murphy et al. 2018b above, Desmedt et al.)**. These critiques are about *measurement*, not about whether interoception deficits exist in alexithymia.

- **Khalsa et al. interoception network and the Tulsa group's work** on interoceptive interventions (e.g., MABT-style work by Cynthia Price). This work assumes interoception heterogeneity in clinical populations and tries to design interventions accordingly, without committing to a single Bird-style global account.

### Recommended phrasing for downstream content

When the deep dive needs to discuss interoception in alexithymia, use phrasing along these lines:

> The Bird/Murphy/Brewer lineage continues to argue that alexithymia involves a multidomain failure of interoception that extends beyond emotion (Brewer, Cook & Bird 2016; Murphy et al. 2018; Brewer, Murphy & Bird 2021). The deficit may sit at any of three representational levels — detection, recognition, or labelling — and different alexithymics may be impaired at different levels. The cardiac heartbeat-counting task is, however, methodologically unreliable; clean signal in this group's data emerges only when confounds are controlled (Murphy et al. 2018b). Independently of the Bird group, Garfinkel's three-dimensional model and the quadratic-relationship findings (Trevisan et al. 2020) point in the same direction: that the practical alexithymic phenotype is more often a parsing/labelling failure than a pure detection failure, even though the deepest deficit may also include detection in some sub-profiles.

This phrasing preserves the parent exploration's parsing-deficit thesis while attributing it correctly.

## Topic 2: Parsing-deficit × therapy matching — empirical state

The validator's central concern: have any studies tested whether interoception-first vs. external/cognitive-first therapy works *differently* depending on alexithymia subtype or interoceptive baseline?

### Honest finding: this work is mostly theoretical

Direct searches for stratified RCTs — interoceptive accuracy quartile × therapy assignment — yield essentially nothing. The closest empirical material:

**(a) Trevisan, Mehling & McPartland (2020), [PMC7076086](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7076086/).** A small (N=76) pilot study that randomised young adults to a brief body-scan mindfulness intervention vs. control after a baseline heartbeat-detection task. The relationship between alexithymia and IA was quadratic at baseline, and "the literature suggests that reduced *or* elevated interoceptive accuracy or sensibility may be problematic in individuals characterized by difficulties with emotional awareness, suggesting the possibility of quadratic relationships." This *frames* subtype-matching as plausible but does not directly test interoception-first vs. cognitive-first.

**(b) Norman, Marzano, Coulson & Oskis (2019), [PMC10270453](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10270453/) — Effects of mindfulness-based interventions on alexithymia: systematic review.** The reviewable trials are very heterogeneous and underpowered for subtype matching. One signal: **Bornemann & Singer's data showed that decreases in TAS-20 were caused by the *Presence* and *Affect* training modules of the ReSource project — but not by the *Perspective* module.** This is the closest published evidence of within-mindfulness submodule × alexithymia-facet matching, but it is a single multi-component trial, not a head-to-head subtype-matched RCT. **Bornemann's data also showed greater reductions among participants with higher baseline alexithymia (TAS-20 above 51) than among those with low baseline alexithymia.** Suggestive but again not interoception-stratified.

**(c) Guendelman et al. (2024) RCT (S0165032723010704)** — randomised mindfulness vs. dyadic socio-emotional app practice over 10 weeks. Both arms improved interoceptive accuracy roughly equivalently; neither produced superior reductions in alexithymia. No subtype × therapy interaction was tested directly. The headline result — both interventions improved IA, neither was differentially superior on alexithymia outcomes — does not support the strong subtype-matching thesis but also does not refute it (the study was not designed to detect interaction effects).

**(d) MABT (Mindful Awareness in Body-oriented Therapy, Cynthia Price) — substance use disorder studies.** Improvements in interoceptive awareness, mindfulness, emotion dysregulation, depression, days abstinent ([Price & Hooven 2018, PMC5985305](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5985305/); [Price et al. 2019, PMC6775765](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6775765/)). MABT explicitly targets people who have difficulty attending to interoceptive awareness due to stress, chronic pain, or trauma — i.e. the population the parsing-deficit framework would consider risky for standard mindfulness. The MABT model is aware of this concern (it uses scaffolded touch-based and psychoeducational components rather than pure introspection) but does not stratify outcomes by alexithymia subtype.

**(e) Lumley's Emotional Awareness and Expression Therapy (EAET) for chronic pain.** Multiple RCTs (fibromyalgia, IBS, MUS, older veterans). Lumley's group has shown that **higher baseline depression, anxiety, and PTSD symptoms moderate *greater* pain-severity reduction after EAET but not after CBT** ([Carty et al. ~2023, see Lumley summaries](https://wayne.edu/people/aa5664)). This is the cleanest published example of *symptom severity × therapy assignment* moderation in the alexithymia-adjacent literature — but it is symptom-severity moderation, not interoceptive-subtype moderation. It also runs the *opposite* direction of the parsing-deficit caution: more emotionally dysregulated participants benefit *more* from the explicit-emotion therapy. The Lumley work is not, however, a clean test of parsing-deficit theory because EAET is not a pure interoception-first intervention.

**(f) Lumley's older research has also shown that the "Impoverished Emotional Experience" facet of the EPS-25 — overlapping with affective alexithymia — moderates outcomes of EAET for somatic symptom disorder** (PMC7928393 / [Frontiers Psychology 2021 mediation paper](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2021.712518/full)). The mediation finding: increased capacity to be aware of emotion (without intrusive flooding) was the active mechanism of somatic symptom reduction. This does *not* test interoception-first vs. cognitive-first; it shows that emotional-awareness-building is helpful in somatic disorders.

**(g) Oxytocin trials.** Personality-dependent effects: high-alexithymia scorers benefited more from intranasal oxytocin than low-alexithymia scorers on social-cognitive tasks ([Luminet et al. 2011, PubMed 21641964](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21641964/); [Quirin & Carter review, PMC4172094](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4172094/)). Recent large autism oxytocin trials have stratified by *baseline blood oxytocin* (Parker et al. 2017 PNAS; the Stanford trial reported in 2022 and 2023). This is a real example of biomarker stratification → differential response, but it is not interoception-stratified and it concerns oxytocin, not psychotherapy.

**(h) ADIE (Sussex group, Garfinkel)** — Interoceptive Training to Reduce Anxiety in Autistic Adults ([Lancet eClinicalMedicine 2021](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/eclinm/article/PIIS2589-5370(21)00322-9/fulltext)). Single-centre RCT, autistic adults randomised to interoceptive-accuracy-training (heartbeat-detection feedback) vs. control. The training improved IA and reduced anxiety. This is the closest existing test of interoception-first training in a population highly enriched for alexithymia — but it does not stratify outcomes by alexithymia subtype, and the headline finding (training improves anxiety) actually pushes back against the strong parsing-deficit caution. Counter-note: this group did not assess somatic-symptom amplification or dropout patterns by subtype.

### Bottom line for Opportunity 1

**No published RCT directly tests interoception-first vs. external/cognitive-first therapy stratified by alexithymia parsing-deficit subtype.** The closest empirical signals (Bornemann's submodule effects; Lumley's symptom-severity moderation; Trevisan's quadratic IA findings; oxytocin biomarker stratification; ADIE's positive results) collectively suggest that:

- Mindfulness-based interventions can reduce alexithymia, but probably not via pure interoceptive-detection training.
- Higher-baseline-distress patients can benefit *more* from explicit-emotion therapies, contrary to a strong parsing-deficit caution.
- ADIE's positive interoception-training results in autistic adults complicate the parsing-deficit theoretical claim that body-attention training is risky for parsing-deficit alexithymics.
- Oxytocin research demonstrates the *feasibility* of biomarker stratification but the relevant therapy literature has not yet adopted interoceptive-accuracy stratification.

**For downstream content**: the parsing-deficit-driven therapy-matching framework remains a *theoretical extrapolation*. The Bornemann submodule data and Lumley symptom-severity-moderation data are real signals worth citing, but the user should know the strong claim ("interoceptive training is risky for parsing-deficit alexithymics specifically") has not been directly tested. Treat as a reasonable clinical hypothesis informed by the meditation-related-adverse-effects literature, not as established subtype-matching evidence.

This is precisely the framing the validator recommended; the supplemental search confirms it.

## Topic 3: Alexithymia as adaptive trait — published evidence and trade-offs

**Promoted to high priority by user direction.** Below is the most substantive section of this supplement, covering five domains where peer-reviewed work documents alexithymia-associated *altered* (not necessarily worse) cognition or function. Each finding is qualified by what alexithymia costs in the same domain. The goal is to give downstream content material for an honest "trade-off" framing.

### 3.1 Resistance to the framing effect — reduced bias under emotional decision pressure

**The published finding**: Alexithymia is *negatively* associated with the framing effect — i.e., people scoring higher on alexithymia are *less* susceptible to whether a choice is presented in gain vs. loss terms. They make more logically consistent (rational, in the De Martino/Camerer sense) decisions across frame inversions ([Hsu, Wang, Lin 2021, PubMed 33482229](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33482229/), [ScienceDirect](https://sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0167876021000027)). The relationship is moderated by interoception — the bias-reduction effect emerges most clearly when interoception is high.

**Why this counts as adaptive in some contexts**: De Martino, Kumaran, Seymour & Dolan (2006, [PMC2631940](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2631940/)) showed that the framing effect is generated by amygdala activity and that decreased framing-effect susceptibility corresponds to greater orbital and medial PFC activation — i.e., to *more* "rational" decision-making in the technical sense. The alexithymia framing-effect-reduction finding maps directly onto this: alexithymics rely less on intuitive emotional appraisal, more on decision-frame-invariant logical structure. In contexts where consistent, frame-invariant decisions are valued — risk modelling, financial analysis, structured negotiation, rule-based work — this is a functional asset.

**The cost side**: The framing effect is also a heuristic that often tracks ecologically valid information; eliminating it can produce decisions that are technically rational but socially/contextually mismatched. There is no published evidence on whether real-world decision quality is *net* better in alexithymics — only that the lab-defined "rationality" measure goes up.

### 3.2 Decision under risk preserved; decision under ambiguity selectively impaired

**The published finding**: Across decision-making tasks, alexithymics show **selective impairment of decision-making under ambiguity** but **preserved performance under risk** ([Zhang et al. 2017, PMC5704455](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5704455/), [PubMed 29179707](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29179707/)). On the Iowa Gambling Task (where probabilities must be learned from feedback — i.e., where the somatic-marker pathway matters), high-alexithymia performance is poor. On the Game of Dice Task (where probabilities are explicit), performance is unimpaired.

**Why this is informative for trade-off framing**: The asymmetry maps directly onto Damasio's somatic-marker hypothesis. Alexithymics are not globally worse decision-makers; they are specifically less able to leverage *somatic-emotional learning* to navigate ambiguity. In decision contexts where probabilities are explicit and emotional learning is irrelevant or noise (formal verification, structured probability problems, explicit-payoff games), they perform comparably to non-alexithymic controls. **This means the decision-making "deficit" is task-conditional, not global.**

**Related finding**: [Bagnoli et al. 2010, PubMed 20078741](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20078741/) on cumulative-feedback IGT showed an "explore-learn-change-return" pattern in high-alexithymia participants — they learn the disadvantageous decks early but cannot consolidate over later trials. The pattern is consistent with reduced sensitivity to losses (i.e., reduced loss-aversion) — a cousin of the framing-effect finding.

**The cost side**: In many real-world domains decisions are made under ambiguity, not explicit risk. The selective preservation of risk-decision-making is a narrow advantage.

### 3.3 Emotional-attention bypass and faster non-emotional task processing

**The published finding**: Across attention paradigms, alexithymia is associated with **early avoidance of anxiety-evoking stimuli** and **reduced interference of negative affect with concurrent cognitive tasks** ([Lee & Lee 2024, *Psychological Reports*](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/pdf/10.1177/00332941221146917); see also Suslow & Junghanns review). On emotional-Stroop variants and dot-probe tasks, high-alexithymia individuals show *faster* RT for unpleasant-stimulus blocks than for neutral blocks — the literature describes this as "emotional bypass." Cognitive-style description: external attentional focus, little interest in introspection, weakly elaborated emotion schemas — together producing reduced emotional interference with executive function ([Cognitive-Emotional Processing in Alexithymia, Marquette](https://epublications.marquette.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1503&context=psych_fac)).

**Why this counts as adaptive in some contexts**: In high-affect-load environments where emotional intrusion would impair task performance — surgery, emergency response, crisis triage, certain forms of military operation, time-pressured technical work — the capacity to *bypass* emotional interference is valuable. The medical-education literature describes this explicitly as **"professional alexithymia"** — a learned/trained state of partial emotion-bypass that enables sustained cognitive performance under affectively demanding conditions ([Shapiro 2011, PubMed 21248595](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21248595/); [emotions-in-residency commentary](https://johannashapiro.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/EMOTIONS-IN-MEDICINE.EMOTIONAL-ALEXITHYMIA.RESIDENTS.pdf)). The literature is *critical* of medical education's role in inducing this state — but it acknowledges that the state itself is functional in the moment of acute clinical work.

**The cost side**: The same emotional-bypass that enables task focus carries downstream cost in three forms: (a) increased burnout and depersonalisation when the bypass becomes chronic ([healthcare detachment literature, PMC4917040](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4917040/)); (b) reduced empathic attunement to patients/colleagues; (c) impaired capacity to learn from emotional feedback. The medical-education literature now frames this as a maladaptive over-extension of an adaptive capacity, not as a pure deficit.

**Implication for trade-off framing**: Emotional bypass can be functional in time-bound, task-focused contexts and dysfunctional as a trait disposition. The same person can experience it both ways depending on context.

### 3.4 Divergent thinking — narrow positive correlation

**The published finding**: While alexithymia is *negatively* correlated with most measures of creativity (alexithymics score lower on Torrance-style creativity tests, on emotional creativity, on imaginative response patterns, on Rorschach Form%) — there is **one narrow finding** that higher alexithymia scores correlate *positively* with idea-generation in divergent thinking specifically ([emotional-creativity reviews](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10400419.2017.1263507); [Alexithymia–Imagination–Creativity, PII S019188690800189X](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S019188690800189X)). One mechanism proposed: reduced fantasy-life and affective-elaboration may free attentional resources for surface-level idea-generation, even as it impairs deeper imaginative integration.

**The cost side**: This is the only published positive-correlation finding in the creativity literature. The dominant pattern is impaired creativity. Take this as a narrow contextual finding, not as a general "alexithymia helps creativity" claim.

### 3.5 Culturally-embedded emotional restraint

**The published finding**: Alexithymia/emotional-restraint norms are systematically higher in interdependent cultural contexts (East Asian, some Middle Eastern) than in independent ones (Western/Anglosphere) — and **expressive suppression has different functional outcomes by cultural context**. In independent cultures, suppression predicts worse psychological adjustment, worse social support, lower likeability. In interdependent cultures, suppression is unrelated to or *positively* associated with relationship satisfaction and well-being ([Soto et al. 2011, PMC4341898](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4341898/); [Ryder et al. 2018 cultural-shaping chapter](https://spectrum.library.concordia.ca/id/eprint/987976/1/2018%20-%20Ryder,%20Sunohara,%20Dere,%20&%20Chentsova-Dutton.pdf); [English et al. 2020, Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2020.01048/full)).

**Why this matters for the deep dive**: The standard alexithymia construct is built on a Western-independent emotional-expressivity norm. Patterns that look like "alexithymia" against that norm may be *normatively-fitted emotional regulation* in interdependent contexts. The published cross-cultural data warrant a section in the deep dive cautioning that population-level alexithymia means and clinical claims do not transport cleanly across cultures.

**The cost side**: This applies to *expressive suppression* specifically, not to the deeper deficit of emotion-identification in alexithymia. A person can suppress fluent emotional self-knowledge functionally; a person who never built fluent emotional self-knowledge has a different kind of trait, and the cross-cultural-suppression literature does not directly speak to it. Distinguish the two carefully.

### 3.6 Stress reactivity — mixed but partially reduced

**The published finding**: HPA-axis findings in alexithymia are mixed. The cortisol-awakening response is *lower* in alexithymics than in controls ([Häusser et al. 2013, PubMed 23627992](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23627992/), [PMC review 9868825](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9868825/)). Salivary cortisol responses to social stress tests show inconsistent patterns — some studies report attenuated peak responses, others report increased anticipatory cortisol with normal peak. Autonomic reactivity to emotional stimuli is often (not always) attenuated.

**Why this is informative for trade-off framing**: Reduced anticipatory negative-emotion processing has been hypothesised as enabling more *effective* preparation for upcoming events because the person is less swamped by anticipatory affect ([Reduced anticipation of negative emotional events in alexithymia, Scientific Reports srep27664](https://www.nature.com/articles/srep27664)). In low-affect-load environments this can be functional. In high-affect-load environments it predicts increased allostatic load, somatic complaints, and reduced adaptive emotional learning.

**The cost side**: The same blunted reactivity that reduces anticipatory swamping also reduces the body's capacity to *use* affect as adaptive information. Across the long run, the literature is consistent: alexithymia → maladaptive emotion regulation strategies (suppression, behavioural withdrawal, ignoring) → increased risk of depression, anxiety, somatic illness, chronic pain ([Adaptive and maladaptive emotion processing review, PubMed 31556808](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31556808/)). The trade-off here is not balanced; the long-run cost is higher than the short-run benefit. But the short-run benefit is real.

### 3.7 First-responder and high-trauma-exposure occupations

**The published finding**: First responders (police, firefighters, paramedics) show **elevated alexithymia** alongside elevated trauma exposure, and the direction of causation is contested. Some research treats alexithymia as a risk factor for PTSD development; some treats it as a protective trait that allows the person to remain in trauma-exposed work without acute decompensation; some treats it as a trauma-induced consequence ([Riethof et al. 2020, female healthcare professionals SAGE](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0300060519887633); [How does it feel — police officers with/without PTSD, T&F 2023](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/20008066.2023.2281187); [October 7th attacks aftermath, ScienceDirect 2025](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0022395625002936)).

**Why this matters for trade-off framing**: There is genuine controversy about whether alexithymia in first responders is a (partly adaptive) coping response that lets the person stay in their job, vs. a (maladaptive) trauma sequela that interferes with recovery. The published literature has both framings. The downstream content can present this honestly as a contested adaptive/protective vs. costly/sequela question.

### 3.8 Empathic accuracy — selectively impaired, contextually preserved

**The published finding**: Alexithymia is associated with **reduced emotional empathy** but **preserved cognitive empathy** in many studies (cognitive empathy = perspective-taking, emotional empathy = affect-sharing). Lower scores on emotional contagion and skin-conductance responses to others' distress; comparable scores on perspective-taking and cognitive theory-of-mind tasks ([Grynberg et al. 2010, S019188691000365X](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S019188691000365X); [Pisani et al. 2020, PMC7517829](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7517829/); [Shaw et al. 2020, Nature Sci Reports srep35059](https://www.nature.com/articles/srep35059)). Some studies find no difference in prosody/semantic-cue accuracy.

**Why this matters for trade-off framing**: The dissociation has direct implications. Alexithymics are not globally worse at understanding other people — they are specifically less reactive to others' affect. In contexts where emotional contagion would be costly (sustained exposure to others' distress as in clinical work, advocacy, crisis response, court adjudication), reduced emotional empathy paired with intact cognitive empathy can function as professional emotional regulation. Cognitive empathy (the capacity to model what someone else is feeling without catching it) is preserved and can be deliberately developed.

**The cost side**: Reduced emotional empathy still tracks costs in close personal relationships and in caregiving contexts where emotional attunement matters more than analytic understanding. The published interpersonal-impact literature consistently shows alexithymia → poorer relationship satisfaction, more loneliness, more avoidant attachment.

### 3.9 Neurodiversity-affirming framings — modest academic uptake

The peer-reviewed literature on neurodiversity-affirming approaches to alexithymia is modest but growing ([Neurodiverse Connection alexithymia/autism guide, ndconnection.co.uk](https://ndconnection.co.uk/resources/p/alexithymia-autism-guide); [Alexithymia & Autism Spectrum Disorder, PMC6056680](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6056680/); [Framework for neurodiversity-affirming interventions, PMC10430771](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10430771/)). The academic uptake is currently dominated by:

- Reframing alexithymia as a "different emotional language" rather than as a deficit (autistic-affirming literature, Lawson, Knopf, McKenna).
- Mottron's autism-as-strength tradition ([Mottron 2011 in *Nature*](https://www.nature.com/articles/479033a) and subsequent work) provides a template — autism-related sensory and cognitive differences come with both costs and asymmetric advantages, and framing has clinical/identity consequences.
- Monotropism theory (Murray, Lawson, Lesser; later writers) — autistic attention focuses deeply on a small number of interests; this is associated with depth of expertise and flow-state capacity.

The closest analogy for alexithymia is: alexithymia may have similar dual-character. The peer-reviewed literature has not yet built a Mottron-equivalent for alexithymia, but the building blocks are present (decision-rationality findings; emotional-bypass functionality; cognitive-empathy preservation; cross-cultural normativity question). The deep dive could legitimately position itself as an early synthesis of these scattered signals into a coherent trade-off framing.

### 3.10 What's plausibly adaptive but not yet directly studied

For honesty: several plausible adaptive niches remain *unstudied* in published literature:

- **Engineering/CS/STEM/mathematics careers**: high autistic-trait prevalence in these fields is documented; high autistic-trait → high alexithymia rate is documented. But there is *no direct evidence* that alexithymia specifically predicts STEM career fit, performance, or satisfaction. The medical-students literature shows elevated alexithymia in that population (~26.9%, higher than general 10%) — this is partly a stress consequence and partly possibly a self-selection effect, but the self-selection direction has not been established empirically.
- **Programming, scientific research, structured analytical work**: theoretically plausible adaptive niches, but no direct alexithymia-task-fit data published.
- **Long-term meditation/contemplative practice in some traditions**: Theravada and Zen traditions explicitly cultivate decoupling from automatic emotional reactivity. This is not the same as alexithymia, but the functional output overlaps.
- **Strategic/competitive contexts under emotional pressure**: poker, negotiation, courtroom advocacy — contexts where emotional bypass under pressure is rewarded. No direct alexithymia studies in these populations.

The downstream content should be careful to mark these as *plausible-but-unstudied* rather than as established findings.

### 3.11 Summary of the trade-off frame

Alexithymia is *not* a uniformly adaptive trait, and the deep dive should not flatter the user by framing it as one. But it is also not a uniformly maladaptive trait. The published trade-off picture, conservatively read:

| Domain | Selective advantage | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Decision under explicit risk | Preserved or enhanced (less framing bias, faster non-emotional decisions) | Decision under ambiguity selectively impaired |
| High-affect-load task contexts | Emotional-bypass enables sustained focus | Chronic bypass → burnout, depersonalisation, downstream illness |
| Cognitive empathy | Preserved | Emotional empathy reduced; relationship costs |
| Anticipatory emotion | Reduced anticipatory swamping | Reduced adaptive use of affect signals |
| Cultural fit (interdependent contexts) | Suppression-based regulation can be functional | Western-independent contexts: regulation costs |
| Divergent-thinking idea generation | Narrow positive correlation | Most other creativity measures negatively correlated |
| First-responder occupational fit | Possibly protective for sustained trauma exposure | Possibly accelerates PTSD vulnerability — direction contested |

The user can hold their alexithymia as a trait with this trade-off structure. The deep dive can present this trade-off frame substantively, while noting that the dominant clinical literature still treats alexithymia as a deficit and that the trade-off case is built from *individual papers* rather than from a coherent published research program.

## Topic 4: Latent-profile subtypes — replication status

### Verdict: severity continuum is better-supported than discrete subtypes

Independent search confirms the validator's concern. The Chen et al. 2011/2017 four-cluster scheme has not been consistently replicated.

**Evidence against four discrete subtypes:**

- **Preece-affiliated 2021 LPA paper** ([Schaeuffele et al. 2021, S0165178121001372, PubMed 33667948](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33667948/)). LPA on the BVAQ found that solutions corresponded to "individuals with various degrees of alexithymia 'severity'" rather than supporting the four proposed BVAQ-derived subtypes. The authors explicitly question the implementation of subtype frameworks in research.

- **2025 Hogrefe factor-mixture-modelling paper** ([European Journal of Psychological Assessment, 41(1), Hogrefe DOI 10.1027/1015-5759/a000828](https://econtent.hogrefe.com/doi/10.1027/1015-5759/a000828)). Direct hit on the validator's prompt. FMM was used to test whether alexithymia heterogeneity is best modelled as discrete classes, continuous severity, or a hybrid. The paper finds that consensus has not been reached between cluster-analytic and latent-class approaches; cluster analysis (Chinese students) supports four subtypes, but LCA (German individuals) supports only three high-alexithymia subtypes ("low," "mixed," "high").

- **2025 Chinese nursing students LPA paper** ([BMC Medical Education 2025](https://bmcmededuc.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12909-025-08019-4)). Identifies *three* alexithymia categories (not four) — distinguished primarily by severity. The "high-risk group" shows greatest mental health symptoms.

- **2025 elderly LPA paper** ([Frontiers Psychology 2025](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2025.1701168/full)). Three-profile solution: pervasive alexithymia (21.15%), adaptive (72.81%), affective-cognitive dissociation (6.04%). Notice: the largest profile is labelled "adaptive" — non-alexithymic — and the two smaller profiles map onto severity-with-sub-pattern rather than onto Chen's four-class scheme.

**Evidence for four-class scheme**: limited beyond the original Chinese-student studies (Chen 2011, 2017; Wei 2013; Zhang 2020) and a few replications using cluster analysis specifically (which is methodologically inferior to LPA/FMM for this question).

### Implication for downstream content

The deep dive should frame heterogeneity primarily as **continuous severity along the TAS-20 facets and the BVAQ cognitive-affective dimensions**, with **subtype labels (general-high, introvert-high, extrovert-high, non-alexithymic) treated as descriptive rather than as established discrete classes**. The user's self-classification project is better served by "where on each axis am I?" than by "which of four boxes fits me?".

Reframe Axis 4 of the parent's Heterogeneity Map accordingly: keep the cluster scheme as a *descriptive* label set people sometimes find useful, but flag explicitly that the discrete-class structure has not consistently replicated in LPA/FMM work and that severity-based modelling fits better.

## Topic 5: Bermond Type II → psychosis vulnerability — strength of evidence

### Verdict: published, replicated within a narrow lineage, not yet prospectively tested

**The pattern is documented**: Type II alexithymia (high cognitive-dimension, normal/low affective-dimension) is found at elevated rates in psychosis-risk populations.

**Lineage evidence**:

- **Larøi et al. 2008/2010** ([as cited in Ozdemir 2025 and PMC4451258](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4451258/)). Initial finding linking BVAQ alexithymia dimensions to schizotypy in non-clinical samples — DDF and DIF specifically associated with negative schizotypy.

- **Cedro et al. 2001/2015** ([SAGE 10.2466](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.2466/pr0.2001.89.1.95) and follow-ups). Polish replication. Higher TAS-20 scores in paranoid schizophrenia patients than in healthy controls. Alexithymia associated with hallucinations specifically. Note: the SAGE paper is from 2001 not 2015 — citation in the parent exploration may need to be updated.

- **van der Velde et al. 2015** ([PLoS One 0124803, PMC4451258](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4451258/)). The strongest single piece of evidence. Prospective-cohort design with healthy controls (cog-alex score 48.8), siblings of schizophrenia patients (55.2), ultra-high-risk individuals (68.0), and patients with schizophrenia (64.6). Statistically significant parametric gradient on cognitive alexithymia from controls → siblings → UHR. The pattern: cognitive alexithymia rises with risk gradient; affective alexithymia does not. This *is* a strong piece of evidence — but it is one paper from one Dutch group.

- **Ozdemir et al. 2025** ([J Clin Psychol; PMC12050105](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12050105/)). Systematic review and meta-analysis. The review confirms moderate associations between DIF/DDF and subclinical negative symptoms in community samples. **However**, the review explicitly notes (a) limited number of studies reporting dimensional relationships, (b) no prospective ultra-high-risk cohort data discussed beyond van der Velde 2015, (c) ~60% of studies do not control for distress-related covariates, (d) cross-sectional designs predominate. The Ozdemir review's bottom line is conservative: the alexithymia-psychosis pattern is present, but the case for the *Type II distinction* specifically is weaker than the case for elevated alexithymia in psychosis broadly.

- **Network analysis papers (Ozdemir 2025 second paper, [Clin Psychol & Psychotherapy 70156](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/cpp.70156))**. Treats alexithymia as a bridge between negative affect and schizotypy. Provides indirect support for cognitive-alexithymia-as-vulnerability without strongly supporting the Type II categorical typology.

### What's missing

- **Prospective ultra-high-risk cohort data** that test whether the Type II pattern *predicts* transition to psychosis prospectively. None published.
- **Independent (non-Polish, non-Dutch) replications using BVAQ specifically** — limited.
- **Tests of the Type II distinction in ultra-high-risk samples beyond van der Velde 2015**.
- **Whether the Type II pattern replicates in non-Western psychosis-risk samples** — not yet established.

### Implication for downstream content

The Type II → psychosis vulnerability link is real-as-published but should be presented as:
- "Cognitive-alexithymia (high DIF/DDF) is elevated parametrically across the psychosis-risk spectrum (van der Velde 2015), suggesting a possible vulnerability marker."
- "The categorical Type I / Type II distinction itself is weakly supported in the broader BVAQ literature; the pattern is better summarised dimensionally."
- "Prospective evidence that the Type II pattern *predicts* transition to psychosis is not yet published."

This is consistent with how the parent exploration already frames this — the supplement just confirms it and adds the prospective-data caveat.

## Topic 6: PTSD + alexithymia — defensible prevalence range

### Verdict: 42-65% is the defensible range. The 90% should not anchor the upper bound.

**Frewen et al. 2008** (J Trauma Stress, [PubMed 18404647](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18404647/), [Wiley](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/jts.20320)). Original meta-analysis. 12 studies, N=1,095 PTSD individuals. The published abstract reports a *large effect size* but does not report a pooled prevalence percentage in the abstract. The often-quoted "53%" pooled prevalence appears to derive from the **2026 Frewen meta-analysis** and from secondary syntheses combining Frewen 2008 with later studies — not from Frewen 2008 directly.

**Recent meta-analysis** ([2026 systematic review, S0013700626000023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0013700626000023)). Pooled prevalence across 36 studies: **53% (95% CI: 42-65%)**. This is the most defensible single figure for downstream content.

**The 90% Mexican outpatient figure** ([Hernandez-Ramirez 2025, jogh-np 138201](https://joghnp.scholasticahq.com/article/138201-prevalence-of-alexithymia-in-patients-with-post-traumatic-stress-disorder-a-cross-sectional-study-in-mexican-outpatients)). Single cross-sectional Mexican outpatient study. Alexithymia rate ~90%. Should be cited as an outlier from a single Latin American clinical population, not as a representative upper bound. The likely explanation for the elevated rate in this cohort: clinical-treatment-seeking population with high comorbidity, possible cultural factors, possible measurement-norm differences.

**Complex PTSD vs. PTSD**: The literature does not currently provide a clean meta-analytic comparison of alexithymia prevalence in CPTSD vs. classic PTSD. CPTSD samples are typically more dissociation-symptom-loaded and typically show *higher* alexithymia rates than classic PTSD samples — but the published evidence is still sparse and the ICD-11 CPTSD literature is young. Best current statement: "Complex PTSD samples typically show alexithymia rates at the upper end or above the PTSD pooled range, consistent with their more pervasive emotion-regulation deficits, but a clean meta-analytic estimate is not yet available."

### Implication for downstream content

Present "approximately 53% (95% CI: 42-65%) in classic PTSD samples; rates appear higher in complex PTSD samples though pooled meta-analytic estimates are not yet available; outlier clinical-population studies report higher rates (up to ~90% in one Mexican outpatient sample)." This range matches what the validator suggested and is now confirmed by independent meta-analytic data.

## Topic 7: Alexithymia-specific iatrogenic harm — evidence audit

### Verdict: well-supported general meditation-related harm literature; thin alexithymia-specific harm literature

The validator's concern is correct. Here is what the evidence base actually contains.

**(a) General meditation-related-adverse-effects (MRAE) literature — well-established.**

- **Britton, Lindahl, Cooper, Canby, Palitsky 2021** ([PMC8845498](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8845498/)). Defined the MRAES-MBP measurement framework. **Important update on the parent exploration's "20-25%" claim**: that figure was the *a priori threshold* the field expected. Britton's measured rates *exceeded* it: 32.3% on single-occurrence items, 50% on broader MRAES-MBP scale, 37% reporting negative impact on functioning. Downstream content should update from "20-25%" to "32-50% depending on instrument and threshold."

- **Goldberg, Lam et al. 2022** ([PMC8636531](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8636531/)). Population-based US sample, N=434 with lifetime meditation exposure. **General-item MRAE rate: 32.3%**. **One-or-more-specific-item rate: 50.0%**. **MRAE duration ≥1 month: 10.4%**. **Functional impairment ≥1 month: 1.2%**. Anxiety, traumatic re-experiencing, and emotional sensitivity were the most common MRAE. Childhood adversity was a risk factor. *This is the primary updated source for downstream content's MRAE prevalence claims.*

- **Lindahl, Britton et al. 2017 — Varieties of Contemplative Experience study** ([PMC5443484](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5443484/), [PLOS One 0176239](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0176239)). 59-category taxonomy across 7 domains: cognitive, perceptual, affective, somatic, conative, sense of self, social. The 32 expert teachers in the sample reported 56 of 59 categories (95%); average 8.5 categories per expert. **The taxonomy does not specifically tag alexithymia subgroup data.** The Varieties data is qualitative and does not stratify by participant alexithymia level, so it cannot directly speak to "alexithymia-specific harm."

- **Treleaven, Britton 2018 — *Trauma-Sensitive Mindfulness*** ([wwnorton.com](https://wwnorton.com/books/9780393709780)). Foundational practical text. 36 specific modifications for trauma-survivor practitioners covering attention, arousal, relationship, dissociation, social context. **Alexithymia is not directly named as a contraindication or as a modifiable factor in Treleaven's framework**; the framework is trauma-focused, not alexithymia-focused. The downstream content's parsing-deficit-alexithymia caution is thus an *extrapolation* from Treleaven's trauma-focused framework, not a direct application of it.

- **Lanius et al. 2024 — Choice points for dissociative-subtype PTSD** ([PMC11257171](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11257171/)). Real published clinical literature on integrating mindfulness for dissociative-subtype PTSD. Again, not alexithymia-specific.

**(b) Alexithymia-specific iatrogenic-harm RCT data — essentially absent.**

Searches across "alexithymia mindfulness adverse effects," "alexithymia MBSR dropout," "alexithymia interoceptive training harm," "alexithymia somatosensory amplification" yield no identified RCTs that test (i) whether alexithymics specifically have higher dropout rates, (ii) whether alexithymics specifically experience worse outcomes, (iii) whether body-focused mindfulness specifically harms parsing-deficit alexithymics. The Norman et al. 2019 systematic review of mindfulness for alexithymia ([PMC10270453](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10270453/)) reviewed four trials and reports no adverse-event data from any of them — it explicitly notes this absence as a literature limitation.

**(c) Closest adjacent evidence.**

- **Bornemann & Singer (ReSource project)**: Within mindfulness, the *Presence* and *Affect* modules reduced TAS-20 scores; the *Perspective* module did not. This is a *positive-effect submodule analysis*, not a harm signal — but it is the cleanest existing data on within-mindfulness module × alexithymia interaction.

- **Quadratic-relationship study (Trevisan 2020)**: Suggests both low-IA and high-IA alexithymics may be problematic for body-scan interventions, consistent with the parsing-deficit caution. Pilot only.

- **Somatosensory-amplification literature** ([SSA framework, Barsky; A Neural Circuit Framework, Perez et al.](https://psychiatryonline.org/doi/full/10.1176/appi.neuropsych.13070170)). Strong evidence that *body-attention focus amplifies symptom intensity in somatic-symptom-disorder populations*. This is mechanistic support for the parsing-deficit caution but does not directly test alexithymia-subtype interactions.

- **Interoceptive-exposure literature in panic disorder** ([Pyschology Tools framework](https://www.psychologytools.com/resource/interoceptive-exposure); [PMC1559685](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1559685/); [PMC3727659](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3727659/)). Interoceptive exposure is *therapeutic* in panic disorder — it deliberately induces panic-related body sensations to break catastrophic-misinterpretation cycles. Sometimes-cited as a counter-example to the body-focus-is-harmful framing — but the interoceptive-exposure model is a structured exposure protocol, not an open-ended body-focused mindfulness practice, and the population (panic disorder) is different from somatic-symptom-disorder or alexithymia-with-trauma populations. The deep dive should note this asymmetry: not all interoceptive interventions are equivalent.

### Implication for downstream content

The downstream Category 8 ("When awareness training backfires") should distinguish three evidence tiers:

1. **Well-established**: Meditation-related adverse effects are common (Britton, Goldberg ~32-50% rates; childhood-adversity risk factor; trauma re-experiencing among the most common types).
2. **Theoretical extrapolation with adjacent evidence**: Body-focused interventions can amplify symptom intensity in somatic-symptom-disorder populations (mechanistic literature solid). It is *plausible* that this extends to parsing-deficit alexithymics with somatic-symptom or trauma comorbidity, but no direct alexithymia-subtype tests exist.
3. **Direct alexithymia-specific harm data**: Essentially absent. No RCT has tested whether alexithymia subtype × therapy assignment interacts to produce harm.

**Update the "20-25%" figure to "32-50% depending on measure"** with Goldberg 2022 as the primary citation. **Frame the parsing-deficit caution honestly as "theoretically motivated extrapolation"** rather than as established subtype-specific evidence. The clinical decision-relevance is real (a patient with somatic-symptom-disorder + alexithymia + trauma is a bad fit for unstructured body-scan practice on mechanistic grounds), but the dataset to support strong subtype-specific claims does not exist.

## Open Questions Remaining After Supplement

After this supplement's deeper search, several questions are *still* genuinely unresolved:

1. **Alexithymia-specific iatrogenic harm in RCT data**: still essentially absent. The mechanistic case is plausible; direct evidence does not exist. The deep dive cannot pretend otherwise.
2. **Subtype × therapy matching in stratified RCTs**: still untested. Bornemann's submodule data and Lumley's symptom-severity moderation are the closest signals; neither is interoception-stratified.
3. **Prospective psychosis prediction from Type II alexithymia**: still untested. Van der Velde 2015 is parametric-cross-sectional, not prospective.
4. **Whether elevated alexithymia in first responders is protective or sequela**: still contested. Both framings are defensible from published data.
5. **Whether alexithymia is over-represented in STEM, math, programming careers as a *self-selection* effect**: untested. The direction of inference is theoretically plausible but empirically uninvestigated.
6. **Cross-cultural construct validity of TAS-20 in non-Western populations**: continues unresolved. Mean differences may reflect construct-non-equivalence; the field has not produced a clean answer.
7. **Whether the multiroute model (Hobson 2019) admits a *third* route — autistic perceptual/interoceptive architecture — distinct from alexithymia-as-language-deficit and alexithymia-as-interoception-deficit**: not yet articulated cleanly in the literature.
8. **Whether the parsing-deficit framework actually carves alexithymia at a useful joint, or whether the more useful joint is the multiroute model's (interoception-route vs. language-route)**: an open theoretical-empirical question.
