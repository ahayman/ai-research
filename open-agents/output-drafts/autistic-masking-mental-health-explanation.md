# Autistic Masking (Camouflaging) and Mental Health Outcomes

A Comprehensive Evidence Review

---

## 1. Overview

Autistic masking -- also called camouflaging -- refers to the strategies autistic people use, consciously or unconsciously, to suppress autistic traits and perform neurotypical social behaviors. It is one of the most actively researched areas in autism science, with over 100 peer-reviewed studies published since 2017 examining its nature, measurement, and mental health associations.

This document presents the current state of the research -- including its limitations, contradictions, and open questions -- on masking, its relationship to mental health, and what the evidence means for interventions and technology design.

### Validation Summary

This document draws on 7 research files, 3 independent validation reports, and 4 supplemental research files produced through a rigorous validation process. Of 42 major claims examined across two rounds of validation, 30 were confirmed, 5 were confirmed with corrections, 5 were uncertain, and 2 were invalid. All corrections have been applied. Overall research reliability was rated HIGH.

### Key Takeaways

1. **Autistic masking is well-documented but causally complex.** Cross-sectional studies consistently show associations between masking and poorer mental health (depression r=0.36, anxiety r=0.27). However, the only longitudinal study found the opposite temporal direction, and the causal relationship remains unresolved.

2. **Autistic facial expressions are different, not deficient.** Multiple studies demonstrate that autistic people express emotions in ways that are meaningful but stylistically different from neurotypical norms. Neurotypical observers struggle to read autistic expressions, just as autistic people may struggle with neurotypical expressions -- this is a bidirectional problem.

3. **Training "normal" expressions is training masking.** The research literature identifies rehearsing and performing neurotypical facial expressions as a core component of masking behavior. Interventions that teach autistic people to produce "correct" expressions are, by definition, training masking.

4. **The evidence base for ABA shows both measurable benefits and significant gaps.** Meta-analyses demonstrate medium-to-large positive effects on communication and adaptive behavior, but 93% of intervention studies never mention adverse outcomes. The field cannot currently confirm safety because safety has never been systematically measured.

5. **Evidence-based alternatives exist.** Neurodiversity-affirming approaches, environmental accommodations, and acceptance-based therapies offer paths that the evidence suggests are less likely to cause harm and more likely to support wellbeing.

## 2. What Is Autistic Masking?

### Definition

[Hull et al. (2017)](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10803-017-3166-5), in the foundational qualitative study "Putting on My Best Normal," defined camouflaging as comprising two core behavioral processes: compensation (finding alternative ways around social and communication differences) and masking (hiding aspects of one's autistic presentation or presenting a non-autistic persona). This study of 92 autistic adults (55 female, 30 male, 7 other gender) identified a three-stage model: motivations for camouflaging (fitting in, forming connections, avoiding bullying), the camouflaging process itself, and its consequences (exhaustion, identity confusion, threats to self-perception).

[Cook et al. (2021)](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cpr.2021.102080), in a systematic review of 29 studies, offered a synthesized definition: "the employment of specific behavioural and cognitive strategies by autistic people to adapt to or cope within the predominately non-autistic social world."

[Pearson & Rose (2021)](https://doi.org/10.1089/aut.2020.0043) added the critical observation that masking is "an unsurprising response to the deficit narrative and accompanying stigma that has developed around autism" -- framing it as a survival strategy shaped by social pressure rather than a voluntary choice.

### Three Components: Compensation, Masking, Assimilation

The Camouflaging Autistic Traits Questionnaire (CAT-Q), the most widely used self-report measure, identifies three distinct components ([Hull et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10803-018-3792-6)):

**Compensation** (9 items): Using learned strategies and social rules to navigate interactions -- preparing conversation topics, monitoring social performance, applying explicit rules about turn-taking and appropriate responses. This corresponds roughly to what [Livingston et al. (2019)](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2215-0366(19)30224-X) called "shallow compensation" (simple rules that may not transfer across contexts) and "deep compensation" (flexible, context-sensitive strategies).

**Masking** (8 items): Presenting a non-autistic persona by suppressing autistic traits -- adjusting facial expressions, controlling body language, hiding natural reactions such as stimming. This is primarily passive suppression: reducing existing behavior rather than generating new behavior.

**Assimilation** (8 items): Blending in and hiding discomfort in social situations -- fitting in with groups, avoiding standing out, managing identity presentation across environments.

The CAT-Q shows good internal consistency (alpha = 0.94) and preliminary test-retest reliability (r = 0.77) ([Hull et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10803-018-3792-6)). However, construct validity concerns have been raised: a [2026 critical review of 389 studies (Arnold, Bitsika & Sharpley)](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10803-018-3792-6) found the CAT-Q may confound social anxiety with camouflaging, and the masking subscale specifically was unrelated to autistic traits. This means some portion of what the CAT-Q measures may reflect general social impression management rather than an autism-specific process.

### The Compensation Taxonomy

[Livingston & Happe (2017)](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neubiorev.2017.06.005) proposed a foundational framework distinguishing compensation from masking, defining compensation as "the processes contributing to improved behavioural presentation... despite persisting core differences at cognitive and/or neurobiological levels."

[Livingston et al. (2019)](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2215-0366(19)30224-X) then identified four strategy types through qualitative research with 136 adults:

| Strategy | Description | Generates New Behavior? |
|----------|-------------|------------------------|
| **Masking** | Suppressing or dampening pre-existing social behaviors to blend in | No -- reduces existing behavior |
| **Shallow Compensation** | Applying simple, learned social rules ("always ask how someone is doing") | Yes -- but context-limited |
| **Deep Compensation** | Flexible strategies that account for situational details in real time | Yes -- broadly applicable |
| **Accommodation** | Seeking environments that fit rather than changing oneself | No -- modifies context |

This taxonomy matters for technology design because it draws a clear line between strategies that suppress authentic behavior (masking) and those that build genuine communicative capacity (deep compensation) or modify the environment (accommodation). Technology can support the latter two without training the first.

### Measurement Approaches

Beyond the CAT-Q, researchers use two other approaches:

**The Discrepancy Method** ([Lai et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1177/1362361316671012)): Measures camouflaging as the gap between external behavioral presentation (ADOS scores) and internal status (autistic traits + social cognition). A larger gap suggests more camouflaging. This method captures observable masking efficacy rather than self-perceived effort.

**The Compensation Checklist** ([Livingston et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13229-019-0308-y)): A 31-item checklist quantifying self-reported compensatory strategies, aligned with the four-type taxonomy above.

Importantly, the self-report and discrepancy methods do not always correlate highly ([Hannon et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10099783/)), suggesting they capture different aspects of the phenomenon -- perceived effort versus observable outcome.

## 3. Masking and Mental Health Outcomes

### Meta-Analytic Associations

The most comprehensive meta-analysis to date ([Khudiakova et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.rasd.2024.102492)) analyzed 16 studies with 5,897 autistic participants and found the following pooled correlations between self-reported camouflaging and mental health outcomes:

| Outcome | Correlation (r) | 95% CI | Heterogeneity (I-squared) |
|---------|-----------------|--------|---------------------------|
| Depression | 0.36 | [0.27, 0.45] | 50% |
| Generalized Anxiety | 0.27 | [0.22, 0.33] | 32% |
| Social Anxiety | 0.24 | [0.19, 0.29] | 36% |
| Wellbeing (inverse) | -0.13 | [-0.19, -0.08] | 21% |

These are small to moderate correlations. They mean that people who report more camouflaging also tend to report more depression and anxiety, but there is substantial overlap between groups, and many individuals who camouflage heavily do not report poor mental health.

Critically, all 16 studies were cross-sectional. The meta-analysis itself noted that all studies were underpowered to detect small effects, and many used insufficiently validated measures.

### Suicidality Research

A series of studies by [Cassidy and colleagues](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13229-018-0226-4) has examined camouflaging as a risk factor for suicidal thoughts and behaviors:

- [Cassidy et al. (2018)](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13229-018-0226-4): In 164 autistic adults recruited online, 72% scored above the SBQ-R psychiatric cutoff for suicide risk (vs. 33.7% in a general population comparison). Camouflaging accounted for 3.5% additional variance in suicidality after controlling for age, sex, depression, anxiety, and other factors.

- [Cassidy et al. (2020)](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10803-019-04323-3): In 160 undergraduate students, the association between autistic traits and lifetime suicidality was significantly mediated by camouflaging and thwarted belongingness. The assimilation subscale specifically predicted thwarted belongingness (9.9% additional variance).

- [Cassidy et al. (2023)](https://doi.org/10.1111/sltb.12965): Camouflaging accounted for 3.2% additional variance in defeat and entrapment -- constructs from the Integrated Motivational-Volitional Model of Suicide.

**Important methodological caveats:** The Cassidy 2018 sample was self-selected, predominantly female, and predominantly late-diagnosed -- all factors that may inflate distress estimates. The SBQ-R cutoff of 8 was validated in psychiatric populations and its applicability to autistic populations has been questioned ([Pelton et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31820344/)). The variance explained by camouflaging (3.2-3.5%) is statistically significant but small in absolute terms. Nonetheless, a follow-up study ([Graham, Rodgers, & Cassidy, 2024](https://doi.org/10.1089/aut.2023.0039)) found that camouflaging alone was not able to predict suicidal thoughts -- entrapment mediated the relationship.

### Autistic Burnout

[Raymaker et al. (2020)](https://doi.org/10.1089/aut.2019.0079), using community-based participatory research with 19 autistic adults and 19 public internet sources, defined autistic burnout as "a syndrome conceptualized as resulting from chronic life stress and a mismatch of expectations and abilities without adequate supports," characterized by:

1. **Chronic exhaustion** -- depletion of physical, mental, emotional, or social energy
2. **Loss of skills** -- regression in executive function, self-care, vocational abilities
3. **Reduced tolerance to stimulus** -- heightened sensory sensitivity, increased meltdowns/shutdowns

Masking was identified as a primary contributing factor. Participants described burnout as typically lasting 3+ months, with consequences including loss of employment, deteriorated health, and suicidal ideation. A Grounded Delphi study ([Higgins et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1177/13623613211019858)) with 23 autistic adults as experts confirmed this definition and importantly noted that autistic burnout is distinct from clinical depression -- psychological treatments for depression could potentially make autistic burnout worse.

[Arnold et al. (2023)](https://doi.org/10.1177/13623613221147401) found that masking was a significant independent predictor of autistic burnout severity after controlling for alexithymia, interoception, repetitive behaviors, sensory sensitivities, and autism severity (N=141 autistic adults).

### Identity and Self-Concept

The qualitative literature consistently describes how sustained masking erodes identity. In [Miller, Rees & Pearson (2021)](https://doi.org/10.1089/aut.2020.0083), all three participant groups (144 autistic, 49 neurodivergent, 45 neurotypical adults) reported feeling disconnected from their true selves through masking. However, only autistic participants reported suicidal ideation connected to masking obligations.

A meta-ethnography by [Field et al. (2024)](https://doi.org/10.1177/13623613231223036), synthesizing 13 qualitative studies, found that camouflaging led to identity conflict, confusion, and feelings of inauthenticity across the included studies.

### The Causal Direction Question

**This is the single most important section of this document.** The research summarized above establishes that masking and poor mental health co-occur. But does masking cause poor mental health, or does poor mental health drive more masking, or are both driven by a third factor?

The dominant narrative in the masking literature -- that masking causes mental health harm -- has been significantly challenged by recent evidence.

#### Van der Putten et al. (2025): The First Longitudinal Study

[Van der Putten et al. (2025)](https://doi.org/10.1177/13623613251347104) conducted the first longitudinal study of camouflaging and mental health, following 332 autistic adults over approximately 2 years.

**The critical finding was the opposite of what was hypothesized:** Higher initial camouflaging predicted a small but significant *decrease* in mental health difficulties over time (beta = -0.12, adjusted p = 0.003; Bayes Factor = 3.51, moderate evidence). The authors stated directly: "We did not find strong evidence for camouflaging to be associated with development of mental health difficulties."

They offered possible explanations: camouflaging may reduce negative reactions from peers, paradoxically improving mental health; regression to the mean may contribute (people with high initial scores have more room to improve); and the relationship may differ across subgroups.

This study has important limitations: only 2 timepoints, an older sample (ages 30-84, mean 52), COVID-19 confound at T2, and small effect sizes. But as the only longitudinal evidence available, it carries substantial weight.

#### Khudiakova et al. (2025): Comprehensive Evidence Review

[Khudiakova et al. (2025)](https://doi.org/10.1002/aur.3299) explicitly concluded: "Quantitative research is yet to demonstrate clear causal links between camouflaging and mental health symptoms in autistic people, with some studies finding no significant correlations, and some finding relationships that differ by gender or sex, but with no discernible pattern."

#### Scheeren et al. (2025): Real-Time Daily Assessment

[Scheeren et al. (2025)](https://doi.org/10.1177/13623613251353358) used ecological momentary assessment (smartphone-based, 28 days) with 87 autistic adults. They found that day-to-day fluctuations in masking did **not** predict subsequent stress 4 hours later (b = 0.10, p = 0.253). Only average (trait-level) masking was associated with stress. This is consistent with a common cause model (people who mask more are the same people who have more stress) rather than a simple causal model (each masking episode causes stress).

#### Four Competing Causal Models

The current evidence is consistent with multiple models:

**Model A: Direct Harm.** Masking causes mental health problems through exhaustion, identity loss, and cognitive depletion. *Supporting evidence:* Qualitative studies consistently report this; cross-sectional associations exist; MZ co-twin QoL study ([Lundin Remnelius et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10803-024-06583-0)) found within-pair effects suggesting causal influence on quality of life. *Against:* The Van der Putten longitudinal finding; Scheeren EMA showing no within-person lagged effects; [Roisenberg et al. (2025)](https://doi.org/10.1002/aur.70199) finding camouflaging adds no predictive value beyond social anxiety.

**Model B: Reverse Causation.** Poor mental health drives more masking. People with greater anxiety may mask more as a coping mechanism. *Supporting evidence:* [Lei et al. (2024)](https://doi.org/10.1111/jcpp.13884) found masking was associated with social anxiety severity, not autism traits. Social anxiety reduction correlated with camouflaging reduction in a CBT study (r = 0.40-0.52; [Roisenberg et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1002/aur.70103)).

**Model C: Common Cause.** Shared underlying factors (hostile social environments, stigma, genetic/familial factors) independently drive both masking and poor mental health. *Supporting evidence:* [Zubizarreta et al. (2025)](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13229-025-00695-9) found that the between-individual association between camouflaging and cortisol disappeared or reversed when controlling for shared genetics and environment in a co-twin design (N=315, 91 MZ pairs). [Mandy (2019)](https://doi.org/10.1177/1362361319878559) explicitly suggested an "inhospitable environment" as an unmeasured third variable.

**Model D: Bidirectional Feedback Loop.** Masking and mental health influence each other cyclically: social pressure leads to masking leads to exhaustion leads to increased anxiety leads to more masking. *Supporting evidence:* Qualitative literature consistently describes escalating cycles. However, no quantitative study has tested a bidirectional model. This remains theoretical.

The honest summary: masking is consistently *associated* with poorer mental health, and the qualitative evidence for its toll on wellbeing and identity is compelling. But the causal mechanism remains unresolved, and the only longitudinal evidence points in the opposite direction from the dominant narrative. Clinicians are advised to take an individualized approach, exploring both the costs and benefits of masking for each person.

## 4. Comorbidities

Autism is associated with high rates of psychiatric and medical comorbidity. [Lever & Geurts (2016)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26861713/) found that 79% of autistic adults (N=344, ages 19-79) met criteria for a psychiatric disorder at least once in their lives. The following comorbidities interact with masking in clinically relevant ways.

### Alexithymia

Alexithymia -- difficulty identifying and describing one's own emotions -- affects approximately 50% of autistic adults compared to 10-13% of the general population ([Kinnaird, Stewart & Tchanturia, 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.eurpsy.2018.09.004); [Salminen et al., 1999](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0022-3999(98)00053-1)).

The "alexithymia hypothesis" ([Bird & Cook, 2013](https://doi.org/10.1038/tp.2013.61)) argues that emotional processing differences commonly attributed to autism are actually driven by co-occurring alexithymia. [Cook, Brewer, Shah & Bird (2013)](https://doi.org/10.1177/0956797612463582) demonstrated empirically that alexithymia, not autism severity, predicted poor recognition of emotional facial expressions. [Trevisan, Bowering & Birmingham (2016)](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13229-016-0108-6) found that alexithymic traits -- but not ASD traits, IQ, or sex -- predicted quantity of facial expression production.

This creates a particularly demanding situation for masking: approximately half of autistic people are socially pressured to perform "appropriate" emotions that they cannot reliably identify in themselves. The cognitive load of emotional performance without internal reference points is extremely high.

### PTSD

PTSD prevalence in autistic adults varies dramatically by measurement method. [Rumball et al. (2020)](https://doi.org/10.1002/aur.2306) found probable PTSD in 45% (current) and 61% (lifetime) of 59 trauma-exposed autistic adults using screening tools. However, [Mansour et al. (2025)](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cpr.2025.102621), in a meta-analysis of 13 studies (N=142,081), found clinical diagnosis point prevalence of only 2.06% (95% CI: 0.00-11.97%).

This enormous gap likely reflects both underdiagnosis (current PTSD criteria may not capture how autistic people experience and express trauma) and measurement inflation from screening tools in self-selected samples. Autistic adults are more than four times more likely to be diagnosed with PTSD than non-autistic adults ([Hoover & Kaufman, 2018](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK573608/)), and a broader range of events can be traumatic for them, including sensory overwhelm, social rejection, and forced masking.

### Eating Disorders

Approximately 20-30% of patients with anorexia nervosa meet diagnostic criteria for ASD ([Westwood & Tchanturia, 2017](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11920-017-0791-9); [Inal-Kaleli et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1002/eat.24307): 29% by ADOS in meta-analysis). An important caveat: starvation states can produce "pseudo-autistic" presentations, so the true rate of underlying ASD in anorexia may be closer to 10% by developmental history.

Critically, [Bradley et al. (2024)](https://doi.org/10.1177/13623613241245749) found that camouflaging -- not sensory processing or autistic identity -- significantly predicted eating disorder symptoms in 180 autistic participants. [Brede et al. (2020)](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10803-020-04479-3) found autistic women viewed anorexia as a downstream symptom of unaddressed autism-related needs.

### Substance Use

[Weir, Allison & Baron-Cohen (2021)](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2215-0366(21)00160-7), in the largest study of substance use in autism (N=2,386), found a paradox: autistic adults use substances *less* overall than non-autistic peers (16% vs. 22% regular alcohol use; 4% vs. 8% binge-drinking). However, those who do use substances are approximately 9 times more likely to report using drugs specifically to manage behavior (OR 8.89; 95% CI: 2.05-81.12). The very wide confidence interval reflects the small subsample endorsing this specific motivation.

Alcohol was often used to participate in social situations -- that is, to enable masking. Five participants specifically noted that receiving their autism diagnosis was crucial to discontinuing substance misuse.

### Overall Comorbidity Burden

The relationship between masking and these comorbidities is associational, not established as causal. However, the pattern is clinically relevant: masking exists within a web of psychiatric comorbidity that includes alexithymia (50%), anxiety, depression, PTSD, eating disorders, and substance use. These conditions may share underlying mechanisms (e.g., chronic stress from social marginalization) rather than being sequentially caused by masking.

## 5. Facial Expressions and the Double Empathy Problem

### Autistic Expressions Are Different, Not Deficient

Multiple studies demonstrate that autistic people produce emotional expressions that are genuine and meaningful but stylistically different from neurotypical norms:

[Keating et al. (2026)](https://doi.org/10.1002/aur.70157) used facial motion tracking to analyze nearly 5,000 expressions from 25 autistic and 26 non-autistic adults. For anger, autistic participants relied more on the mouth and less on eyebrows. For happiness, they showed a less exaggerated smile that did not "reach the eyes." For sadness, they created a downturned expression by raising their upper lip more. The researchers concluded: "Autistic and non-autistic people may express emotions in ways that are different but equally meaningful -- almost like speaking different languages."

[Faso, Sasson & Pinkham (2015)](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10803-014-2194-7) found that autistic expressions were rated as *more intense* and were identified with *greater accuracy* than neurotypical expressions, but were rated as "less natural" by neurotypical observers. The issue was neurotypical aesthetic bias, not autistic expressive deficiency.

[Trevisan, Hoskyn & Birmingham (2018)](https://doi.org/10.1002/aur.2037), in a meta-analysis of 67 effect sizes, found that autistic people naturally express differently but can produce "typical" expressions when explicitly prompted -- suggesting the difference is one of natural communication style, not incapacity.

### The Double Empathy Problem

[Milton (2012)](https://doi.org/10.1080/09687599.2012.710008) proposed the "double empathy problem": communication difficulties between autistic and non-autistic people are bidirectional. Both parties struggle to understand each other. This challenges the traditional framing that places the communication deficit solely in the autistic person.

[Edey et al. (2016)](https://doi.org/10.1037/abn0000199) demonstrated that non-autistic people exhibit their own form of "mind-blindness" when interacting with autistic people -- struggling to judge their emotional expressions and body movements.

### Crompton 2020 and the 2025 Replication

[Crompton et al. (2020)](https://doi.org/10.1177/1362361320919286), in a diffusion chain ("telephone game") study with 72 participants, found that autistic-autistic chains retained information just as well as non-autistic chains, while mixed chains showed significantly steeper decline. Rapport was lower in mixed chains.

The [2025 replication](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41562-025-02163-z) (N=311, pre-registered, multi-site) found an important nuance: **the information transfer finding did NOT replicate** -- no difference was found between single-neurotype and mixed-neurotype chains. However, **the rapport finding was confirmed**: rapport was higher with same-neurotype partners, and disclosure of diagnosis improved rapport.

This partial replication suggests that while the communication *efficiency* difference may have been overstated, the *social comfort* difference between matched and mismatched neurotypes is robust.

### Sasson et al. (2017): Style, Not Substance

[Sasson et al. (2017)](https://doi.org/10.1038/srep40700) found that neurotypical peers formed negative first impressions of autistic individuals within seconds, were less willing to pursue social interaction, and rated autistic people lower on likeability and attractiveness -- but not on trustworthiness or intelligence. The finding that is most consequential: **these biases disappeared when impressions were based on conversational content lacking audio-visual cues.** The substance of what autistic people communicated was fine; only the style of presentation triggered negative judgments.

### Why Training "Normal" Expressions Is Problematic

The research converges on a clear conclusion: teaching autistic children to produce neurotypical facial expressions is training them to mask. [Hull et al. (2017)](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10803-017-3166-5) documented that rehearsing facial expressions, consciously controlling facial muscles, and monitoring one's face for "appropriate" displays is a core component of camouflaging -- the very behavior that the masking literature associates with exhaustion, identity loss, and mental health risk.

Hochschild's (1983) emotional labor theory provides an additional framework: teaching children to display emotions they may not feel is training them in chronic "surface acting," which consistently predicts emotional dissonance and burnout in occupational research. Autistic children would be asked to do this not just during work hours, but throughout their waking lives.

### The Role of Alexithymia

As noted in Section 4, [Trevisan, Bowering & Birmingham (2016)](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13229-016-0108-6) found that alexithymic traits -- not autistic traits -- predicted reduced facial expression production. This means that any technology assuming autistic people have "deficient" facial expressions is conflating autism with alexithymia, a scientifically inaccurate premise. Many autistic people without alexithymia produce facial expressions at typical rates.

## 6. ABA and Masking

Applied Behavior Analysis is the most widely prescribed behavioral intervention for autistic children in the United States and also one of the most contested. An honest treatment of ABA requires presenting both the evidence for its benefits and the evidence for its harms. This section attempts to do that.

### Historical Context

ABA was founded in the 1960s by Ole Ivar Lovaas at UCLA. The original Young Autism Project employed aversive techniques -- electric shocks, slaps, food deprivation -- to suppress autistic behaviors and make children "indistinguishable from their peers" ([Lovaas, 1987](https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-006X.55.1.3)). Lovaas claimed 47% of children (9 of 19) achieved "normal intellectual and educational functioning" in a quasi-experimental (non-randomized) study that has never been fully replicated under controlled conditions.

Simultaneously, Lovaas was principal investigator of the "Feminine Boy Project" -- a conversion therapy program targeting gender-nonconforming boys using the same operant conditioning principles ([Gibson & Douglas, 2018](https://catalystjournal.org/index.php/catalyst/article/view/29579/23427)). One subject, Kirk Andrew Murphy, died by suicide at age 38.

### Modern ABA: What Has Changed and What Has Not

Modern ABA has evolved in meaningful ways. Aversive techniques are no longer sanctioned by the BACB. Many programs incorporate play-based, naturalistic methods. Assent-based practices are emerging. Some practitioners actively embrace neurodiversity-affirming principles ([2024 Behavior Analysis in Practice](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40617-024-00918-0)).

What has not changed is the fundamental operant conditioning framework: behaviors followed by reinforcement increase; those followed by extinction decrease. Critics argue that the target behaviors selected for increase (eye contact, scripted social responses, "appropriate" body language, neurotypical emotional displays) and for decrease (stimming, echolalia, intense interests, atypical sensory responses) continue to train masking even in "modern" ABA.

[Kapp et al. (2019)](https://doi.org/10.1177/1362361319829628) found that autistic adults viewed stimming as an important self-regulatory mechanism and objected to treatment aimed at eliminating it. Suppressing stimming was reported to increase anxiety and decrease self-regulation capacity.

The debate is genuine: a 2025 article in *Perspectives on Behavior Science* described ABA as being "at a crossroads," acknowledging both reforms and ongoing concerns. A 2024 study found significant barriers to implementing naturalistic developmental behavioral interventions (NDBIs) in ABA settings, with many providers still primarily trained in discrete trial training.

### Positive Evidence for ABA

Multiple meta-analyses show measurable benefits:

- [Virues-Ortega (2010)](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cpr.2010.01.008): Medium to large effect sizes for intellectual functioning, language development, daily living skills, and social functioning (22 studies). Effect sizes approached 1.5 for receptive and expressive language.

- [Rodgers et al. (2021)](https://doi.org/10.1002/aur.2447): Individual participant data meta-analysis (15 studies, 720 participants) found improvements in Vineland adaptive behavior scores (MD=7.00) and cognitive ability/IQ (MD=14.13) at 2 years. However, all studies were at high risk of bias with non-randomized designs.

- [2024 BMC Psychiatry meta-analysis](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12888-024-05911-x): Medium effects for intellectual functioning (SMD=0.51) and adaptive behavior (SMD=0.37) versus treatment as usual.

NDBIs -- naturalistic developmental behavioral interventions like ESDM, JASPER, and PRT -- show positive outcomes with fewer required hours and are generally better received by families.

### The Measurement Gap

The most significant criticism of the ABA evidence base is what it does not measure. [Bottema-Beutel et al. (2021)](https://doi.org/10.1177/1362361320965331) examined 150 group-design, non-pharmacological intervention studies for young autistic children and found that 93% (139 of 150) never mentioned adverse outcomes. Of the 11 that did, only 4 actually reported adverse effects. [Dawson & Fletcher-Watson (2022)](https://doi.org/10.1177/13623613211031403) wrote a commentary amplifying these findings.

This creates a fundamental evidentiary problem: the ABA literature can demonstrate benefits for communication and adaptive behavior, but it cannot confirm safety because safety has never been systematically measured. No study measures long-term wellbeing, quality of life, autonomy, or identity outcomes.

### Concerning Findings

[Aguirre Mtanous et al. (2025)](https://doi.org/10.1177/13623613251390604) analyzed a national database of 34,240 privately insured autistic youth (17,120 ABA recipients matched with 17,120 controls) and found ABA receipt was associated with 30% higher odds of mental health hospitalization (OR=1.30, p<0.001) and 32% higher incidence rate (IRR=1.32). No relationship was found between ABA dosing and PTSD or suicidality specifically. This is the largest and most methodologically rigorous study of ABA and mental health outcomes. As an observational study, it cannot establish causation -- children referred to ABA may have had more severe initial presentations.

[Kupferstein (2018)](https://doi.org/10.1108/AIA-08-2017-0016) found 46% of 460 ABA-exposed respondents met PTSD diagnostic thresholds, but this study had serious methodological problems: a self-designed, non-validated questionnaire; self-selected sample; and nearly 47% proxy reporting by caregivers. The finding should be treated with caution.

### Autistic Community Perspectives

The EUCAP 2022 survey found 92.2% of autistic respondents who had personal ABA experience opposed its use. [Anderson (2023)](https://doi.org/10.1177/13623613221082682) found 7 autistic adults described ABA as unethical despite acknowledging some skill benefits.

These surveys are subject to self-selection bias -- people with strong negative experiences are more motivated to participate, and EUCAP is an advocacy organization. They should not be treated as representative of all autistic ABA recipients. However, the consistency and intensity of the reported experiences warrant serious consideration.

### The Genuine Debate

The honest state of affairs is this: ABA may produce measurable developmental gains while simultaneously causing unmeasured harm to wellbeing, autonomy, and mental health. The evidence base cannot currently resolve this tension because the two sides of the debate measure different outcomes. Multiple meta-analyses show benefits for communication and adaptive behavior. Zero studies systematically measure adverse effects on mental health, identity, or quality of life.

The field's conflict-of-interest problem compounds the uncertainty: [Bottema-Beutel & Crowley (2021)](https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2021.676303) found 84% of ABA studies had at least one author with undisclosed conflicts of interest from clinical/training consultancy roles.

### Industry Context

The U.S. ABA therapy market was approximately $4-8 billion as of 2023-2025 (GM Insights, Credence Research). By 2019, all 50 states and Washington, D.C. had enacted mandates requiring insurance coverage for autism treatment including ABA -- Tennessee was the final state. The insurance funding model creates incentive structures that favor high-hour, long-duration treatment protocols.

## 7. Masking Across Demographics

### Gender

[Lai et al. (2017)](https://doi.org/10.1177/1362361316671012) found autistic women camouflaged significantly more than autistic men using the discrepancy method (Cohen's d = 0.98, a large effect), though this was in a small sample (30 per group). [Hull et al. (2020)](https://doi.org/10.1177/1362361319864804), using the CAT-Q with 306 autistic and 472 non-autistic adults, found autistic females scored higher on Masking and Assimilation subscales but *not* on Compensation -- meaning the gender difference is in trait suppression and blending in, not in learning social rules. No gender difference was found in non-autistic people.

[Dean et al. (2017)](https://doi.org/10.1177/1362361316671845) documented camouflaging in autistic girls as young as 7 on the playground: girls used compensatory behaviors such as staying near peers and weaving in and out of activities, masking their social challenges. Autistic boys, by contrast, played alone. The clinical implication: "If practitioners look for social isolation on the playground when identifying children with social challenges, then our findings suggest that girls with ASD will continue to be left unidentified."

Gender differences in masking contribute to diagnostic disparities: autistic women (31.7%) report perceived misdiagnoses more frequently than men (16.7%), particularly of personality disorders (especially BPD), anxiety, and mood disorders.

### Late Diagnosis

[Bargiela et al. (2016)](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10803-016-2872-8) interviewed 14 women diagnosed in late adolescence or adulthood and found profound impacts: years of unrecognized masking led to exhaustion, identity conflicts, and vulnerability -- 9 of 14 participants reported rape by romantic partners and/or strangers. [Cazalis et al. (2022)](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10803-022-05578-9), in a larger sample, confirmed that late diagnosis is associated with more masking and poorer outcomes.

Post-diagnosis, individuals commonly experience relief (finally having an explanation), grief (mourning what could have been), anger (at missed diagnosis), retrospective reinterpretation, and gradually developing identity reconstruction and autistic pride ([Kiehl et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1177/13623613231220419); [Corden et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2021.699335)).

### Racial and Ethnic Disparities

The landscape of racial disparities in autism diagnosis has changed dramatically. [Mandell et al. (2007)](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10803-006-0314-8) found Black children were 2.6x less likely than White children to receive an autism diagnosis. However, **CDC 2022 ADDM data show this prevalence disparity has reversed**: for the first time, ASD prevalence among Asian/Pacific Islander (3.3%), Hispanic (3.2%), and Black (2.9%) 8-year-olds exceeded that of White children (2.4%).

The disparities have not disappeared -- they have shifted from prevalence to services. Black, Hispanic, and lower-income children continue to face later diagnosis, fewer services, and less access to intervention despite similar or higher prevalence rates. A higher percentage of Black children with autism are identified with co-occurring intellectual disability compared to other groups.

Research on masking in non-white autistic populations is sparse, but theoretical work suggests autistic people of color face "double marginalization" -- managing both racial/ethnic identity and autistic identity simultaneously ([Perry et al., 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9908282/)).

### LGBTQ+ Intersection

Large cohort studies (SPARK) find approximately 40-41% of autistic adults identify as LGBTQ+, compared to approximately 7% of the general U.S. population. Transgender and gender-diverse adults are 3-6x more likely than cisgender adults to be diagnosed as autistic ([Warrier et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-020-17794-1)).

[McQuaid et al. (2025)](https://doi.org/10.1177/13623613251335738) found that sexual minority autistic adults mask their autistic traits more often than heterosexual autistic adults, consistent with the concept of compounded masking demands -- managing multiple concealed identities simultaneously.

### Cultural Context

[Atherton et al. (2023)](https://doi.org/10.1177/00220221231169945) found that in Japan, the concept of "over-adaptation" describes substantial masking efforts, and autism was significantly more stigmatized than in U.S. university student samples. In collectivist cultures prioritizing community interdependence, pressure to camouflage may be intensified.

### Suicide Risk

[Hirvikoski et al. (2016)](https://doi.org/10.1192/bjp.bp.114.160192) found the overall suicide mortality risk for autistic individuals was approximately 7.55 times higher than matched controls. When stratified by sex, **autistic women had an OR of 13.05** -- over 13 times the risk of women in the general population. Autistic individuals without intellectual disability had an OR of 9.40.

## 8. Alternative Approaches

The research literature offers several evidence-based alternative frameworks to normalization-based interventions.

### Neurodiversity-Affirming Interventions

[Leadbitter et al. (2021)](https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2021.635690) argued that all autism intervention stakeholders need to move away from a normative agenda, pay diligence to environmental goodness-of-fit, and redefine effectiveness by outcomes endorsed by the autistic community.

[Lerner & Gurba (2023)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10430771/) proposed a framework for neurodiversity-affirming interventions (NAIs) that reframes goals to support autistic strengths, creates environments that foster those strengths, and applies across ages and support needs. They specifically noted that "social skills training teaches autistic people how to mask their autism" with potential harms including exhaustion, anxiety, depression, and decreased self-esteem.

### ACT/NeuroACT

[Pahnke et al. (2023)](https://doi.org/10.1177/13623613221140749) conducted a randomized controlled pilot trial of NeuroACT, an Acceptance and Commitment Therapy program adapted for autistic adults (20 participants vs. 19 treatment-as-usual). Results showed statistically significant improvements in perceived stress and quality of life (effect sizes d = 0.70-0.90). However, no significant differences were found for depression, anxiety, sleep, or everyday functioning, and the sample was small. This is promising preliminary evidence that acceptance-based approaches can improve outcomes without requiring masking.

### Strengths-Based Approaches

[Taylor et al. (2023)](https://doi.org/10.1177/13623613221146440) found that strengths use strongly predicted better quality of life, subjective wellbeing, and lower levels of anxiety, depression, and stress in autistic people. Autistic individuals who frequently use their strengths reported significantly better outcomes than those who use them less -- suggesting that encouraging strengths usage could enhance wellbeing.

### Environmental Accommodations

[Mostafa (2014)](https://www.archnet.org/publications/9101) developed the ASPECTSS Autism Design Index -- seven evidence-based architectural design principles: Acoustics, Spatial Sequencing, Escape Space, Compartmentalization, Transition Spaces, Sensory Zoning, and Safety. These reduce the need for masking by modifying the environment to reduce sensory and social demands.

### Communicative Competence Research

[Light & McNaughton (2014)](https://doi.org/10.3109/07434618.2014.885080) defined communicative competence across four domains plus psychosocial factors:

1. **Linguistic competence** -- knowledge of the language code
2. **Operational competence** -- skills to operate communication systems
3. **Social competence** -- knowledge of social rules of interaction
4. **Strategic competence** -- compensatory strategies for communication breakdowns
5. **Psychosocial factors** -- motivation, attitude, confidence, resilience

Crucially, they argued that attainment of communicative competence is influenced not just by individual skills but by barriers and supports in the environment. Communication must be evaluated in terms of the whole communicative ecosystem, not just the individual's abilities.

### The Empathic Turn

[van Grunsven & Roeser (2021)](https://doi.org/10.1080/02691728.2021.1897189) argued that assistive technology should promote an "empathic turn" -- recognizing that autistic people's ways of engaging the world are "indicative of psychologically rich and intrinsically meaningful experiential lives." They specifically argued that stimming should be recognized as a form of bodily expressivity that plays an important role in empathic communicative exchanges, not something to suppress.

### Normalization vs. Accommodation

Based on the evidence reviewed across this document, the research literature draws a consistent distinction between two intervention approaches:

| Normalization-Based Approach | Accommodation-Based Approach |
|------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Training "correct" facial expressions | Helping observers understand diverse expression styles |
| Suppressing autistic behaviors | Supporting authentic self-expression |
| Compliance with neurotypical norms | Self-directed communication choices |
| Measuring success by proximity to NT behavior | Measuring success by communicative effectiveness and wellbeing |
| Changing the person to fit the environment | Changing the environment to fit the person |

## 9. Implications for Intervention and Technology Design

### A Framework for Evaluating Interventions

The research reviewed in this document suggests a set of questions for evaluating whether any autism intervention or technology promotes masking:

1. **Does this intervention aim to change how the person expresses themselves to match neurotypical norms?** If yes, the research identifies this as masking training, which is associated with negative mental health outcomes in cross-sectional studies.

2. **Does this intervention support the person in communicating what they want to say, in their own way?** If yes, this aligns with communicative competence frameworks and neurodiversity-affirming principles.

3. **Does this intervention modify the environment or educate communication partners?** If yes, this aligns with the double empathy research showing that communication difficulties are bidirectional.

4. **Is the intervention optional and self-directed?** The research on autonomy and self-determination suggests that interventions imposed without consent carry greater risk.

5. **What does the intervention measure as "success"?** If the metric is proximity to neurotypical behavior, the research literature classifies this as masking. If the metric is wellbeing and communicative effectiveness, it is more likely to be supportive.

### The Evidence Against Expression Normalization

The evidence against training autistic people to produce "normal" expressions converges from multiple directions:

1. **Autistic expressions are different, not deficient** -- the premise that expressions need "correcting" is scientifically inaccurate ([Keating et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1002/aur.70157); [Faso et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10803-014-2194-7)).

2. **The communication problem is bidirectional** -- placing all burden on the autistic person while doing nothing to educate neurotypical observers addresses only one side of a two-sided problem ([Milton, 2012](https://doi.org/10.1080/09687599.2012.710008); [Sasson et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1038/srep40700)).

3. **Training expressions is training masking** -- the research literature explicitly identifies rehearsing and performing neurotypical facial expressions as a core masking behavior ([Hull et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10803-017-3166-5); [Pearson & Rose, 2021](https://doi.org/10.1089/aut.2020.0043)).

4. **Masking in childhood is especially concerning** -- children's identities are still forming, and the literature documents that masking in childhood contributes to later burnout ([Raymaker et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1089/aut.2019.0079); [Dean et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1177/1362361316671845)).

5. **Alexithymia confounds the premise** -- approximately 50% of autistic people cannot reliably identify their own emotions, meaning expression training is fundamentally misdirected for half the population it targets ([Bird & Cook, 2013](https://doi.org/10.1038/tp.2013.61)).

## 10. Common Misconceptions

Based on the validation process, the following claims found in popular and clinical sources are either invalid, overstated, or require significant qualification:

| Misconception | Reality |
|--------------|---------|
| "Masking causes depression and burnout" | Masking is *associated* with depression and burnout in cross-sectional studies. The only longitudinal study found the opposite temporal direction. Causal mechanisms remain unresolved. |
| "Autistic people who mask heavily are 3x more likely to experience burnout" | No peer-reviewed study reports this specific statistic. Arnold et al. (2023) found masking significantly predicted burnout severity, but did not report a 3x odds ratio. |
| "Autistic people lack facial expressions" | Autistic people express emotions differently, not less -- and in some studies, more intensely. When expression differences exist, alexithymia (not autism) is the better predictor. |
| "ABA has no evidence of effectiveness" | Multiple meta-analyses show medium-to-large effects on communication, adaptive behavior, and cognitive outcomes. The issue is that 93% of studies never measured adverse effects. |
| "52% of autistic people are LGBTQ+" | Large cohort studies (SPARK) find approximately 40-41% of autistic adults identify as LGBTQ+, compared to ~7% of the general population. |
| "Autistic women face 8x the suicide risk" | Hirvikoski et al. (2016) found the overall autism suicide OR is 7.55; autistic women specifically have an OR of 13.05 (higher, not lower). |
| "Black children are underdiagnosed relative to White children" | CDC 2022 data show ASD prevalence in Black, Hispanic, and Asian/PI children now exceeds White children. Disparities persist in timing, services, and access. |
| "Cortisol elevation proves masking causes physiological damage" | No study directly measures cortisol in relation to masking behavior. The Gao (2022) meta-analysis found no significant overall cortisol difference in autism (g = -0.278, p = 0.467). The Zubizarreta (2025) co-twin study found between-individual cortisol associations disappeared when controlling for shared genetics. |
| "Autistic peer communication is more efficient than mixed-neurotype communication" | The Crompton (2020) information transfer finding did NOT replicate in the 2025 multi-site study (N=311). The rapport difference was confirmed. |

## 11. What We Do Not Yet Know

Despite the large and growing evidence base, fundamental questions remain unanswered:

**Causal direction.** Does masking cause poor mental health, does poor mental health drive masking, or are both caused by shared factors? The Van der Putten (2025) longitudinal finding, the Zubizarreta (2025) co-twin design, and the Scheeren (2025) EMA study all suggest the relationship is more complex than simple causation. Multi-timepoint longitudinal studies tracking both masking and mental health simultaneously are urgently needed.

**Long-term masking trajectories.** [Zhuang et al. (2025)](https://doi.org/10.1177/25739581251386126) found that camouflaging is highly stable over 2 years (ICCs = 0.80-0.86). If masking barely changes, what drives the fluctuations in mental health that are associated with it? The field lacks studies spanning decades that could track masking development from childhood into adulthood.

**Whether reducing masking improves mental health.** Unmasking is recommended in clinical contexts, but no controlled trial has tested whether reducing masking leads to improved mental health outcomes. The Roisenberg (2025) CBT study found that reducing social anxiety also reduced camouflaging, but the direction of influence is ambiguous.

**How masking differs across support needs.** Nearly all masking research involves autistic adults without intellectual disability who can complete online questionnaires. The experience of masking among autistic people with higher support needs is essentially unstudied. The CAT-Q is not validated for children under 15 or individuals with intellectual disability.

**Whether the CAT-Q actually measures masking.** The construct validity concerns raised by Arnold et al. (2026) and Lei et al. (2024) -- that the CAT-Q overlaps substantially with social anxiety measures -- mean the entire quantitative evidence base may be partially capturing anxiety-related social cognition rather than an autism-specific process.

**Cross-cultural variation.** Masking research is overwhelmingly Western, English-speaking, and white. How masking manifests in collectivist cultures, non-English-speaking populations, and communities with different autism attitudes remains largely unknown.

## 12. Summary

The evidence reviewed here tells a nuanced story:

**Masking is real and consequential.** Autistic people across studies and cultures report consciously and unconsciously suppressing autistic traits and performing neurotypical behaviors. The qualitative evidence for its toll on identity, energy, and wellbeing is consistent and compelling.

**The quantitative evidence is associational.** Cross-sectional studies show moderate correlations between masking and depression, anxiety, and reduced wellbeing. But the only longitudinal study found higher initial masking predicted *improved* mental health over time. The causal direction remains unresolved, and multiple causal models are plausible.

**Autistic expressions are a valid communication system.** Research consistently shows that autistic facial expressions are different, not deficient, and that communication difficulties between autistic and non-autistic people are bidirectional.

**Training normalization is training masking.** Any intervention that teaches autistic people to produce "correct" facial expressions is, by the definitions used in this literature, a masking-training tool. The qualitative evidence associating masking with exhaustion, identity loss, and burnout is extensive, even though the quantitative causal evidence is contested.

**Alternatives exist and are supported by evidence.** Neurodiversity-affirming approaches, environmental accommodations, acceptance-based therapies, and strengths-based interventions offer paths forward that align with both the evidence base and the expressed preferences of the autistic community.

The central question the research raises for any intervention is whether it supports people by changing the person to fit the environment, or by modifying the environment and communication practices to work for the person as they are. The evidence reviewed here, across all its complexity and uncertainty, points toward the latter.

## 13. Glossary

**ABA (Applied Behavior Analysis):** A behavioral intervention based on operant conditioning principles, widely used for autistic children. Uses reinforcement and extinction to increase desired behaviors and decrease undesired behaviors.

**Alexithymia:** A subclinical trait characterized by difficulty identifying and describing one's own emotions. Affects approximately 50% of autistic adults vs. 10-13% of the general population.

**Assimilation (CAT-Q):** The component of camouflaging involving efforts to blend in with groups and hide discomfort in social situations.

**Autistic Burnout:** A syndrome resulting from chronic life stress and expectation-ability mismatch, characterized by pervasive exhaustion, loss of skills, and reduced tolerance to stimulus. Distinct from occupational burnout or clinical depression.

**CAT-Q (Camouflaging Autistic Traits Questionnaire):** A 25-item self-report measure of camouflaging with three subscales: Compensation, Masking, and Assimilation. The most widely used measure of masking in autism research.

**Camouflaging:** Synonym for masking. The employment of behavioral and cognitive strategies by autistic people to adapt to or cope within the predominantly non-autistic social world.

**Compensation:** Using learned strategies and social rules to navigate social interactions. Generates new behavioral repertoire rather than suppressing existing behavior.

**Deep Compensation:** Sophisticated, flexible compensatory strategies that work across contexts by taking into account situational details.

**Discrepancy Method:** A measurement approach operationalizing camouflaging as the gap between observable behavior (ADOS scores) and underlying traits/cognition (AQ + RMET scores).

**Double Empathy Problem:** Milton's (2012) framework proposing that communication difficulties between autistic and non-autistic people are bidirectional -- both parties struggle to understand each other.

**Masking (behavior):** The suppression of autistic traits and performance of neurotypical behaviors to blend in. Includes suppressing stimming, forcing eye contact, rehearsing expressions, and scripting conversations.

**Masking (CAT-Q subscale):** The specific subscale measuring efforts to present a non-autistic persona by adjusting expressions, controlling body language, and hiding natural reactions.

**NDBI (Naturalistic Developmental Behavioral Intervention):** A class of interventions (including ESDM, JASPER, PRT) that combine behavioral and developmental approaches in naturalistic settings.

**Neurodiversity:** The diversity of human brains and minds -- the infinite variation in neurocognitive functioning. As a paradigm, it frames neurocognitive differences as part of normal human variation rather than pathology.

**Shallow Compensation:** Simple, learned social rules that may not generalize across contexts (e.g., "always ask how someone is doing").

**Stimming:** Self-stimulatory behavior such as hand-flapping, rocking, spinning, or fidgeting. Research identifies stimming as an important self-regulatory mechanism for autistic people.

**Surface Acting:** From Hochschild's emotional labor theory: displaying emotions one does not feel while suppressing actual emotions. Consistently associated with emotional dissonance and burnout.
