# Validation: The English Verb System — Tense, Aspect, Mood, and Modality

## Validation Summary

The English Verb System concept research is highly accurate and well-supported. All major structural claims -- the 12-cell tense-aspect matrix, the modal verb system with epistemic/deontic duality, the strong verb ablaut classes, phrasal verb particle semantics, and the do-periphrasis -- are confirmed by independent sources. Minor numerical discrepancies exist regarding how many strong verbs survive (the document gives two slightly different figures in different sections), but these reflect legitimate variation in how linguists count. The theoretical framework is sound and the examples are accurate.

## Claim-by-Claim Validation

### Claim 1: English has only two morphological tenses (past and non-past), with no future inflection
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Multiple grammar references confirm that English has only two inflectional tenses. The claim that futurity is expressed through modals (will/shall), semi-modals (be going to), present progressive, and present simple is standard in linguistics. The Grammarly verb tenses guide, Fiveable, and Wikipedia's Tense-aspect-mood article all describe the 12-cell system as built from 2 morphological tenses plus periphrastic future.
- **Sources**: [Grammarly - Verb Tenses Explained](https://www.grammarly.com/blog/parts-of-speech/verb-tenses/), [Wikipedia - Tense-aspect-mood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tense%E2%80%93aspect%E2%80%93mood)

### Claim 2: The 12-cell tense-aspect matrix (3 time references x 4 aspects)
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: The 12-cell matrix (present/past/future x simple/progressive/perfect/perfect progressive) is universally described in grammar references. The specific forms given in the document (e.g., "She has been reading" for present perfect progressive) are all correct. The fixed auxiliary stacking order (Modal -> Perfect -> Progressive -> Passive) is accurately described.
- **Sources**: [Fiveable - Tense-aspect combinations](https://fiveable.me/fundamentals-of-the-grammar-of-standard-english/unit-11/tense-aspect-combinations/study-guide/Pt2Gfj8OVqlxn8Yn), [Academic Marker - Aspects](https://academicmarker.com/grammar-practice/verb-functions/verb-aspect/what-are-simple-progressive-and-perfect-aspects/)

### Claim 3: Modal verbs form a closed class with unique syntactic properties (no -s, no infinitive, no past participle) and dual epistemic/deontic readings
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: The syntactic defectiveness of English modals (no third-person -s, no infinitive *to can, no past participle *have musted, followed by bare infinitive) is standard in the literature. The dual epistemic/deontic reading for each modal is well-documented. The specific examples in the table (e.g., "must" = logical necessity vs. obligation) are accurate. The semi-modals (have to, be able to, be going to) correctly identified as filling gaps left by defective modals.
- **Sources**: [MIT LSA 220 Handout - Modals](http://web.mit.edu/fintel/lsa220-class-2-handout.pdf), [Fiveable - Epistemic, deontic, and dynamic modality](https://fiveable.me/introduction-semantics-pragmatics/unit-6/epistemic-deontic-dynamic-modality/study-guide/Hl11CaNX7lzEFJVk)

### Claim 4: Historical development -- deontic meanings of modals came first, epistemic meanings developed later through metaphorical extension
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Research confirms that English modal verbs began as fully lexical stative verbs that became partially grammaticalized deontic modals in Old English, with more grammaticalized epistemic modals generally not appearing until Middle English. Of all modals, "magan" (can/may) appears to be the first to develop epistemic uses, though Goossens (1982) and Traugott (1992) consider epistemic uses rare in Old English.
- **Sources**: [GRIN - Development of English Modals](https://www.grin.com/document/163254), [UGA - Grammaticalization and semantic landscape](https://openscholar.uga.edu/record/7083)

### Claim 5: Approximately 177 strong verbs in Old English, about 98 survive in Modern English
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: A widely cited study on the evolutionary dynamics of language (Lieberman et al., published in Nature/PMC) tracked exactly these numbers: 177 Old English irregular verbs remained, of which 145 survived in Middle English and 98 remain irregular in Modern English. The document's figures match this source precisely.
- **Sources**: [PMC - Quantifying the evolutionary dynamics of language](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2460562/)

### Claim 6: Strong verb ablaut classes -- Class I (drive/drove/driven), Class III (sing/sang/sung), Class IV (bear/bore/born), Class V (give/gave/given)
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: The ablaut class groupings are accurately described and match standard Germanic linguistics references. The vowel alternation patterns are correctly identified. The document's class numbering follows the Proto-Germanic convention. One caveat: some verbs listed under "Class V" in the document (e.g., take/took/taken, shake/shook/shaken) show the /ʊ/ vowel in the past tense rather than the expected pattern, reflecting the historical merger and fragmentation of the original classes in Modern English. This is acknowledged by the document as "various" in the vowel pattern column.
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia - Germanic strong verb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germanic_strong_verb), [Wikipedia - English irregular verbs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_irregular_verbs)

### Claim 7: The -ed pattern is the only productive past tense pattern; all new verbs get -ed (googled, texted, Ubered)
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: This is universally accepted in linguistics. Almost all new verbs in Germanic languages are weak (take the dental suffix). The examples "googled," "texted" are standard illustrations. The regularization pressure from children's overgeneralization ("singed," "breaked") is a well-documented phenomenon in language acquisition research.
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia - Germanic weak verb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germanic_weak_verb)

### Claim 8: Phrasal verb particles carry systematic spatial-to-abstract metaphorical meanings (UP = completion/increase, OUT = exhaustion/distribution, OFF = separation, DOWN = decrease/cessation)
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Cognitive linguistic research extensively documents the systematic semantic extensions of phrasal verb particles from spatial to abstract domains. Multiple academic papers confirm that particles carry a number of metaphorical extensions from a central/prototypical locative meaning. The specific patterns (UP = completion, OUT = exhaustion/distribution, etc.) are well-supported.
- **Sources**: [IJIRSS - Understanding the semantics of phrasal verbs](https://www.ijirss.com/index.php/ijirss/article/download/6302/1197/9930), [ResearchGate - Verb-particle constructions in Cognitive Linguistics](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/368069945)

### Claim 9: The subjunctive survives in three contexts -- mandative, were-subjunctive, and formulaic
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: The three surviving contexts are accurately described. The mandative subjunctive ("I insist that she be present") is standard in formal American English; British English does often substitute "should + infinitive." The were-subjunctive in counterfactuals ("If I were you") and formulaic subjunctives ("God save the Queen," "Heaven forbid") are correctly identified as the remaining contexts. The historical note about the full Old English subjunctive paradigm eroding to these remnants is accurate.
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia - English subjunctive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_subjunctive)

### Claim 10: Do-support arose as a compensatory mechanism after V2 loss (14th-17th century timeline)
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: The historical timeline is broadly accurate. Do-support emerged beginning around 1500, with the development progressing through the stages described. The claim that it compensated for the loss of V2 word order (when main verbs could no longer invert for questions) is a standard analysis. The document's timeline (14th-15th century optional appearance -> 16th century peak -> 17th century modern pattern) aligns with the scholarly consensus.
- **Sources**: [ResearchGate - A multi-step analysis of do-support](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/304047210), [Wikipedia - Do-support](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Do-support)

### Claim 11: Voice system -- active, be-passive, get-passive, middle voice, causative constructions
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: The voice system description is accurate. The be-passive ("was eaten"), get-passive ("got fired"), and middle voice ("This bread cuts easily") distinctions are standard. The characterization of get-passive as more informal and implying adverse effect is well-documented. The causative hierarchy (make = coercion, have = authority, get = persuasion, let = permission) is accurately described.
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia - English passive voice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_passive_voice)

## Internal Consistency Check

### Minor Issue: Strong verb count discrepancy
- The document states "approximately 177 strong verbs in Old English, about 98 survive in Modern English" (Component 5) and separately mentions "approximately 200 irregular verbs" in the overview. These are not contradictory: the ~200 figure includes both strong verbs (98 surviving) and weak irregular verbs (kept, felt, brought, etc.) plus suppletive forms (go/went, be/was). The distinction is clearly drawn in the document itself.
- **Assessment**: No error; different counts for different categories are correctly distinguished.

## Overall Assessment

**Confidence: 9/10**

The English Verb System research document is exceptionally accurate. Every major claim is independently verified. The theoretical framework (periphrastic architecture, tense-aspect matrix, modal duality, ablaut systematicity, phrasal verb compositionality) is sound and reflects current linguistic understanding. The historical claims about do-support, subjunctive decline, and modal grammaticalization are well-supported. The only reason for not rating 10/10 is that some of the ablaut class assignments for specific verbs reflect the messiness of historical phonological change, where the original Proto-Germanic classes have fragmented in Modern English, making class boundaries somewhat debatable for edge cases.
