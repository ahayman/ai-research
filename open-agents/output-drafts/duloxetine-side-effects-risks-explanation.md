# Side Effects, Risks, and Long-Term Use

> Most of duloxetine's warning list is noise. Three items are not, and two of those will arrive from a doctor who does not know you take this drug.

The most useful thing on this page is not a warning. It is a sorting.

Duloxetine's interaction list, as a pharmacy checker or a patient leaflet presents it, runs to dozens of entries, and almost all of them are flagged at the same severity. That is functionally identical to flagging nothing, because a list on which everything is urgent teaches you to stop reading the list. The serotonin syndrome literature is genuinely bimodal: there are a handful of combinations that have killed people, and there is a long tail of reflexive warnings with no cases behind them. What follows separates the two, because the separation is the content. Under-warning you would be a failure. So would over-warning you, and it would be the more likely failure, because over-warning feels responsible while it is quietly training you to ignore the three things that matter.

Three things matter.

The first is a small group of drugs that are absolutely contraindicated: **MAOI antidepressants** (phenelzine, tranylcypromine, isocarboxazid, selegiline), the antibiotic **linezolid**, and intravenous **methylene blue** `[primary, verified]`. These are label contraindications with real deaths behind them, and the required gaps are specific: five days off duloxetine before starting an MAOI, and fourteen days off an MAOI before starting duloxetine `[primary, verified]`. But the reason this sits at the top of the page is not the pharmacology. It is the delivery route. You are not going to be prescribed linezolid by the doctor who prescribed your duloxetine. Linezolid comes from an infectious-disease team treating a resistant Gram-positive infection. Methylene blue comes from an anaesthetist, during surgery, often for parathyroid or urological localisation, at a moment when you are unconscious. Both are situations in which **you may be the only person in the room who knows you are taking a serotonergic drug**, and in one of them you will not be able to say so. This is the single item on the entire list most worth physically carrying: on a card, on a phone lock screen, on a bracelet, in a form you can hand over rather than recite. If methylene blue is genuinely unavoidable in an emergency, the guidance is to stop duloxetine immediately and resume no sooner than 24 hours after the last dose `[primary, verified]`.

The second is **tramadol**, and it matters for you specifically because tramadol is commonly offered for fibromyalgia. This is not a theoretical combination you might encounter; it is a plausible outcome of your next pain conversation. It carries three separate hazards, and the usual warning mentions only the first. It is serotonergic in its own right, because tramadol is a serotonin-noradrenaline reuptake inhibitor as well as an opioid, and serotonin syndrome on duloxetine plus tramadol is documented `[secondary — unverified]`. That much is standard. The part that is usually missed is pharmacokinetic, and it inverts the usual risk-benefit logic. Duloxetine is a moderate inhibitor of the liver enzyme CYP2D6 `[primary, verified]`. CYP2D6 is the enzyme that converts tramadol into O-desmethyltramadol, the metabolite that actually does the opioid pain relief. So duloxetine **blunts tramadol's analgesia while leaving the serotonergic parent compound circulating for longer** `[primary, verified]`. The combination is worse than additive: you get less of the benefit and more of the risk, from the same tablet. And there is a trap folded inside that, because a person whose tramadol "isn't working" will reasonably ask for a higher dose, which raises the serotonergic load while chasing an analgesic effect that is being blocked at the enzyme. The third hazard is separate from serotonin entirely: tramadol lowers the seizure threshold on its own, and CYP2D6-inhibiting antidepressants carry a modestly higher adjusted incidence rate ratio than non-inhibiting ones in the cohort data `[secondary — unverified]`. None of this means never. Tramadol and duloxetine are co-prescribed routinely. It means that if tramadol is offered, this is the conversation to have, and the sentence to say is that duloxetine inhibits the enzyme tramadol needs.

The third is a pair of drugs that change how much duloxetine you are actually exposed to. **Fluvoxamine** raises duloxetine's AUC roughly **six-fold** and cuts its apparent clearance by about 77% `[primary, verified]`, which means a 20mg dose would behave something like 120mg. It is the single largest interaction on the drug. **Ciprofloxacin** works through the same pathway, CYP1A2, which is duloxetine's primary metabolic route, and the label says to avoid it `[primary, verified]`. Ciprofloxacin is an extremely ordinary antibiotic. The practical consequence is one sentence: tell any prescriber writing you an antibiotic that you take duloxetine, and name ciprofloxacin.

Three things you have already told us are moot. You **do not smoke**, you **rarely drink**, and you **do not take St John's wort**. Padding this page with those warnings would be exactly the reflex it is trying to correct. But one of them inverts into something that does apply to you, and it is worth knowing. Smoking induces CYP1A2, so smokers run **38.4% lower serum duloxetine and 53.6% lower dose-adjusted concentrations** than non-smokers at the same dose `[primary, verified]`. The published figure is framed as smokers being lower. Read from where you stand, it means **you sit 38 to 54% higher in exposure than a smoker on an identical prescription**. That is not a hazard. It is a calibration: the population figures you will read for any given milligram dose are averages across a population that includes smokers, and you are systematically above that average before any other variable is considered. It is one more reason the population number does not describe you.

Now the ones that get flagged and should not. **Triptans** are the textbook case. The 2006 FDA alert rested on 29 case reports; when they were examined against the accepted diagnostic standard, **7 of 29 met the looser Sternbach criteria and 0 of 29 met the Hunter criteria** `[primary, verified]`. A 14-year cohort of 19,017 patients co-prescribed a triptan and an SSRI or SNRI, covering 30,928 person-years, found two definite cases and five possible ones, for an incidence of **0.6 to 2.3 per 10,000 person-years** `[primary, verified]`. The American Headache Society formally recommends against restricting the combination `[secondary — unverified]`. This warning has been known to be wrong for over a decade, and it does real harm, because untreated migraine is a genuine cost paid against a one-in-ten-thousand hazard. **5-HTP** has **zero documented human cases** of serotonin syndrome, either alone or combined with antidepressants, after decades of over-the-counter use `[primary, verified]`; the mechanism is not silly, since it is a direct serotonin precursor, but the case count is the case count. **Ondansetron** is a 5-HT3 *antagonist*, so despite an FDA warning the mechanism runs the wrong way. **Lithium** is routinely flagged and rarely a real problem. Saying this out loud is not permissiveness. It is the only way the first three items keep their force.

On the cardiovascular side, the measured effects are small and the tail is real, and you need both halves. Across 42 placebo-controlled studies and 8,504 patients, duloxetine produced **no significant difference in sustained blood pressure elevation versus placebo**, even in patients with raised baseline blood pressure `[primary, verified]`. The pooled heart rate effect is **+2.22 bpm** (95% CI 1.53 to 2.91) and the blood pressure effect is about **+0.5 systolic and +0.8 diastolic mmHg** `[primary, verified]`. Those are trivial numbers. But a population mean of +2 bpm is perfectly compatible with most people sitting at zero and a minority sitting at +40, and one case report is uncomfortably on point: a 26-year-old man on **duloxetine 20mg/day**, your exact dose, developed tachycardia of **110 to 120 bpm**, which resolved within a week of stopping and returned within two days of restarting at the same dose `[primary, verified]`. Positive dechallenge and positive rechallenge is the strongest form of single-case causal evidence there is, and the authors' own conclusion was that duloxetine can cause clinically significant tachycardia "even in low doses." The mean is small. The tail is real. Both are true, and the second one is why "monitor your blood pressure" is a reasonable instruction rather than an alarm.

The **POTS** question needs care, because it flip-flopped twice inside this project and the correct answer is narrower than either extreme. The claim that duloxetine can produce a POTS-like phenotype is **real, and it is in peer-reviewed primary literature** from the Vanderbilt Autonomic Dysfunction Center, not, as one research pass wrongly concluded, in AI-generated content farms `[primary, verified]`. A 2015 Heart Rhythm Society consensus statement carries a **Class III, Level B-R** recommendation that drugs blocking the norepinephrine transporter can worsen symptoms in POTS and should not be administered, and Class III is the "net harm, do not administer" class `[primary, verified]`. That is as formal as guidance gets. **But every duloxetine-specific source for that phenotype says high-dose and chronic, and traces to a single study using 80 to 240 mg/day** `[primary, verified]`, which is four to twelve times your dose. Worth noting for provenance: that underlying study could not be retrieved in full by this project, so the POTS-phenotype attribution rests on a later paper's characterisation of it `[secondary — unverified]`. This is not a 20mg problem. It is worth a conversation at 60mg. One adjacent claim that circulated in this project is simply wrong and should be discarded: **"SSRIs are preferred over SNRIs in POTS" is invalid**, because no guideline says it, and the one paper that speculated it published data in the same breath refuting it, finding sertraline no better than placebo on standing heart rate and *worse* than placebo on symptoms in 39 POTS patients `[primary, verified]`.

And the direction of this risk for you specifically is **genuinely unknown**, for two reasons that pull against each other. The first is that NET inhibition does two opposite things at once, and this is demonstrated inside single studies rather than across them: the same reboxetine experiment that manufactured a 35 bpm tilt tachycardia in healthy volunteers also cut vasovagal reactions from **9 of 18 on placebo to 1 of 18 on drug**, and a separate trial found NET inhibition extended tolerated tilt duration from 29 to 35 minutes and reduced the odds of early test termination with an **odds ratio of 0.22** (95% CI 0.09 to 0.55) `[primary, verified]`. So NET inhibition worsens orthostatic *tachycardia* and protects against vasovagal *pre-syncope*. The second reason is that **most ME/CFS orthostatic intolerance is not POTS**: in one carefully phenotyped cohort only 6 of 39 patients met POTS criteria while 72% had orthostatic symptoms, and the authors concluded that "orthostatic tachycardia did not account for OI symptoms in CFS" `[primary, verified]`. Which means "do you have POTS?" is the wrong question, and if your orthostatic intolerance is the neurally-mediated or hypotensive type rather than the tachycardic type, a NET inhibitor may be neutral or even helpful. Nobody has checked which you have. A ten-minute active stand test with a cheap home cuff would check it, and it can be done before your appointment.

There is one ME/CFS-specific signal, and it deserves both its billing and its correction. Arnold's 2015 trial is the only duloxetine trial ever run in CFS patients, and its adverse-event table records **dizziness in 31.0% on duloxetine versus 6.7% on placebo** `[primary, verified]`, roughly double the rate seen in fibromyalgia or depression populations, in a table that eight research passes had already cited for its negative primary endpoint without ever reading. That is worth knowing. It is also 9 events against 2, in arms of 29 and 30, with no statistical test attached, at 60 to 120mg, in a table where headache ran the *other* way (10.3% on duloxetine versus 40.0% on placebo) `[primary, verified]`. And the thing that deserves equal billing is what is *absent* from that same table: **no tachycardia, no palpitations, no syncope, and no orthostatic hypotension appear anywhere in it** `[primary, verified]`. There is no tachycardia signal in the only duloxetine trial ever run in your population. Reporting the dizziness row without the missing rows would be exactly the selective reading this project keeps catching itself in.

**Emotional blunting** is where a claim collapsed, and the collapse runs against caution, which is why it needs saying plainly. Both earlier passes of this project stated that antidepressant emotional blunting is dose-dependent, and a validation pass called that dose-dependence "one of the strongest arguments against a dose increase." It is not established, and no study has ever measured it. The chain is this: the 2021 review that asserts dose-relation cites a **qualitative interview study** whose only dose-relevant sentence is that some participants mentioned changes in relation to medication or dose, with no dose-response analysis performed and dose never quantitatively measured `[primary, verified]`. That review and a 2023 apathy review both root back to **the same 1990 case report** `[primary, verified]`, so two reviews asserting the same thing is one piece of evidence rather than two, and the appearance of consensus is manufactured by repetition. Meanwhile the closest thing to an actual test points the other way: in a follow-up cohort of 98 patients where **doses were systematically raised**, blunting scores **fell** significantly (P < 0.001) rather than rising, tracking remission instead of dose `[primary, verified]`. That does not refute dose-dependence, because it is confounded with time and with recovery. It does mean nobody has ever measured this, which is a reason for monitoring rather than for confident prediction in either direction. A caution built on an untested premise is still untested, and pointing in the safe direction does not make it sound.

What survives about blunting is worth having, and it is not comforting. It is **common**, at 46% in a survey of 669 treated depressed patients `[primary, verified]`, with a broader literature range of 40 to 60%. It is reversible, and dose reduction is the standard way it is managed, consistently supported by case reports `[secondary — unverified]`. And the finding that bears directly on you is that the same survey found **no difference in blunting according to any antidepressant agent**, at roughly 46% either way, with only bupropion appearing lower `[primary, verified]`. The implicit model, that duloxetine is a different kind of drug from escitalopram and therefore safer on this axis, does not hold here. Whatever made duloxetine different for you, it is **not** that duloxetine is a low-blunting drug.

Which leads to the question you actually have, and the honest answer is uncomfortable. **Does your escitalopram dissociation predict that it will happen again at a higher dose of duloxetine?** There is **no evidence**, in the strict sense: no study of whether prior antidepressant-induced dissociation predicts recurrence, none on a different serotonergic drug, none on dose-relation within an individual, and no validated risk prediction of any kind `[no evidence exists]`. Nobody has studied it, and nobody is going to, because that trial will not be run. The reasoning "I dissociated on one serotonergic drug, so I may dissociate on another, more so at a higher dose" is a perfectly sensible inference `[reasoning, not evidence]`, and it is consistent with a controlled challenge study in which a serotonin agonist induced depersonalization significantly more than placebo in 67 subjects `[primary, verified]`. But it is your own n-of-1 plus a mechanism, not literature. That is legitimate evidence about you, and it may be the best evidence that exists about you specifically. It should not be dressed up as something it is not, in either direction.

The rest of the profile is real but ordinary, and it is worth saying that this project verified it least. The common effects (nausea, headache, dry mouth, insomnia, constipation, fatigue, dizziness) mostly emerge early, run mild to moderate, and attenuate `[secondary — unverified]`. **Hepatotoxicity** is the serious rare one: hepatic failure, sometimes fatal, has been reported, and ALT elevations above three times the upper limit of normal occur in about 1% on duloxetine against 0.3% on placebo, with substantial alcohol use being the main amplifier `[secondary — unverified]`. Since you rarely drink, that amplifier is largely off the table for you. **Hyponatremia** occurs, occasionally severely, and is reversible on discontinuation `[secondary — unverified]`. **Sexual dysfunction** is classed as frequent and is genuinely under-quantified; this project tried to recover incidence figures and failed, so anyone quoting you a percentage is quoting something this chain could not find `[no evidence exists]`. Sweating and weight change are real and modest `[secondary — unverified]`. The **suicidality boxed warning** applies to adults up to age 25 and is actively contested rather than settled: the signal appears to reverse to protective after roughly age 30, and reduced prescribing following the warning was associated with *increased* suicidal events in severe depression `[secondary — unverified]`. Every claim in this paragraph comes from a single unvalidated research pass reading label-level sources, which is why they all carry that tag. They are probably right. They are not verified to the standard of everything above them.

On **long-term use**, the evidence is thinner than the drug's twenty-year track record implies, and the shape of the gap is specific. The FDA label states, verbatim and consistently across labels from 2008 through 2014, that **"the efficacy of Cymbalta in the management of fibromyalgia has been demonstrated in placebo-controlled studies up to 3 months. The efficacy of Cymbalta was not demonstrated in longer studies."** `[primary, verified]` That is not bureaucratic hedging. It is a description of a real failed trial: of the two six-month placebo-controlled fibromyalgia trials, one was positive and one failed both co-primary endpoints at P = 0.053 and P = 0.073 `[primary, verified]`. Beyond six months, the picture gets structurally worse rather than merely thinner. The one-year fibromyalgia study has **no placebo arm**: it randomises duloxetine against duloxetine, in a population already enriched by eight open-label weeks, so it can show that 60mg and 120mg behave alike over a year but cannot show that either beats placebo, and it cannot separate drug effect from natural history or from the 30 to 40% placebo response that fibromyalgia trials reliably produce `[primary, verified]`. **There is no placebo-controlled duloxetine data past 12 months in any indication** `[primary, verified]`. The asymmetry worth naming is that the safety database is large and long while the efficacy database is small and short. "Well tolerated long-term" is much better established than "still working long-term."

So the thing to carry out of this page is a shape rather than a list. Almost all of the risk that gets flagged at you is not risk; the triptan warning is a documented regulatory over-reach, 5-HTP has never caused a documented human case, and the ondansetron mechanism runs backwards. Almost all of the risk that is real arrives through a door this page cannot watch: an anaesthetist reaching for methylene blue, an infection team reaching for linezolid, a pain clinic reaching for tramadol, a walk-in centre reaching for ciprofloxacin. That is the asymmetry worth acting on, and the action is unglamorous, which is that the information needs to live somewhere outside your head, because the situations that matter most are the ones where you cannot explain yourself. On the questions that touch you most closely, the honest answers are smaller than they look: duloxetine's cardiovascular effect is small on average and occasionally not, and one documented case ran at your exact dose; the POTS mechanism is real but its evidence sits four to twelve times above where you are, and its direction for you depends on an orthostatic phenotype nobody has measured, which a ten-minute standing test would measure; emotional blunting is common and reversible but has never been shown to be dose-dependent, and duloxetine is not a low-blunting drug; and whether your escitalopram history predicts anything at all about a higher duloxetine dose is a question nobody has ever studied. None of that decides anything. It gives you the machinery, and it tells you which three sentences are worth saying out loud in a room where you may be the only one who knows.

## Deep Dive

### The interaction map, graded by real risk

The framing problem comes first, because it determines whether the map is usable. Interaction checkers assign a severity flag per pair of drugs, and they are built to minimise legal exposure rather than to inform a decision. The result is that a combination with documented fatalities and a combination with zero documented human cases can appear on the same screen at the same colour. Graded by what the evidence actually shows:

**Tier 1: absolute contraindications** `[primary, verified]`

| Agent | Status | Why it catches people |
|---|---|---|
| **MAOIs** (phenelzine, tranylcypromine, isocarboxazid, selegiline) | Contraindicated. 5 days off duloxetine before starting an MAOI; **14 days** off an MAOI before starting duloxetine | Rare in modern practice, but the washout is long and asymmetric |
| **Linezolid** | Reversible MAOI. Avoid | Given by infectious-disease teams for resistant Gram-positive infection, who are not thinking about your antidepressant |
| **Methylene blue** (IV) | Potent reversible MAO-A inhibitor. Avoid. FDA safety communication, 2011 | Given by anaesthetists intraoperatively, when you are unconscious and cannot tell them |

If emergency use is unavoidable: stop duloxetine immediately, resume no sooner than 24 hours after the last dose.

**Tier 2: real and situational**

- **Tramadol** `[primary, verified]` for the CYP2D6 mechanism, `[secondary — unverified]` for the case reports. See below; this is the one your diagnosis makes likely.
- **Another antidepressant**, particularly an SSRI. Genuine additive risk, and after MAOIs this is the most common real-world cause of serotonin toxicity. Relevant during cross-tapers `[secondary — unverified]`.
- **Dextromethorphan** at supratherapeutic doses. It is a serotonin reuptake inhibitor and an NMDA antagonist at high doses, with documented cases. A single over-the-counter cough syrup dose is a materially different exposure from recreational use, but it is in products bought without thinking `[secondary — unverified]`.
- **St John's wort** `[secondary — unverified]`. Moot for you, since you do not take it, but the reason it is a double hazard is worth knowing: it is additively serotonergic *and* it induces CYP1A2, which is duloxetine's primary metabolic pathway, so it would lower duloxetine levels and could produce withdrawal symptoms while you were still taking the drug.

**Tier 3: over-warned, and the case for saying so**

| Agent | The evidence | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| **Triptans** | 29 FDA case reports: **7/29** met Sternbach, **0/29** met Hunter criteria. Cohort of 19,017 co-prescribed patients over 30,928 person-years: 2 definite, 5 possible; **0.6 to 2.3 per 10,000 person-years**. American Headache Society recommends against restricting the combination | The warning is a documented over-reach `[primary, verified]` |
| **5-HTP** | **Zero documented human cases**, alone or in combination, after decades of OTC use. Mechanistically it is a direct serotonin precursor bypassing tryptophan hydroxylase, so the concern is coherent | Plausible mechanism, no cases `[primary, verified]` |
| **L-tryptophan** | Rate-limited by tryptophan hydroxylase; historically combined with TCAs deliberately and safely | Theoretical `[secondary — unverified]` |
| **SAMe** | Interaction literature explicitly characterised as "very limited" | Not established `[secondary — unverified]` |
| **Ondansetron** | A 5-HT3 **antagonist**. Despite an FDA warning, the mechanism runs the wrong way | Safely ignorable `[secondary — unverified]` |
| **Lithium** | "Unlikely to cause significant serotonin toxicity even when combined with other serotonergic medications" | Over-warned `[secondary — unverified]` |

**Serotonin syndrome on duloxetine monotherapy** is very rare. It exists as a case report, which is to say it is possible and vanishingly uncommon `[secondary — unverified]`.

### Tramadol: why the combination is worse than additive

This deserves its own section because the standard warning describes one hazard and there are three, and because the second one inverts the usual shape of a drug interaction.

**(a) Serotonergic.** Tramadol is a serotonin-noradrenaline reuptake inhibitor in addition to being an opioid. Serotonin syndrome on duloxetine plus tramadol is documented, including a 55-year-old with tachycardia, diaphoresis and psychomotor agitation whose duloxetine serum levels were unexpectedly elevated, with both symptoms and levels falling only after tramadol was stopped. CYP2D6 poor-metaboliser genotype was excluded in that case, so the raised levels were the interaction rather than a metabolic quirk `[secondary — unverified]`.

**(b) Pharmacokinetic, and this is the part that matters.** Duloxetine is a moderate CYP2D6 inhibitor `[primary, verified]`. CYP2D6 converts tramadol into O-desmethyltramadol (M1), the metabolite responsible for opioid analgesia. Duloxetine therefore reduces tramadol's pain relief while leaving the serotonergic parent compound circulating longer. The usual mental model of a drug interaction is "more of both, so more of everything." This one is: **less benefit, more risk, from the same tablet.** The failure mode follows directly: a patient whose tramadol is not working escalates the dose, which escalates serotonergic exposure in pursuit of an analgesic effect that is being blocked upstream.

**(c) Seizure threshold, independent of serotonin.** Tramadol lowers the seizure threshold on its own. A 2025 nursing-home cohort found seizure incidence of 16.10 per 100 patient-years when an antidepressant was added to tramadol and 20.17 per 100 patient-years when tramadol was added to an antidepressant, with CYP2D6-inhibiting antidepressants carrying an adjusted IRR of 1.09 (95% CI 1.02 to 1.18) versus non-inhibiting ones `[secondary — unverified]`. That population is elderly and institutionalised, so the absolute figures do not transfer to a younger adult. The direction and the mechanism do.

The same prodrug problem applies to **codeine**, which also requires CYP2D6 to become morphine `[secondary — unverified]`.

### What changes your drug level

Duloxetine is a substrate of **CYP1A2 (primary)** and CYP2D6, and a **moderate inhibitor of CYP2D6** `[primary, verified]`.

**Inbound, things that change duloxetine's level:**

| Agent | Effect | Grade |
|---|---|---|
| **Fluvoxamine** | AUC **up 6-fold**; apparent clearance down ~77%. A 20mg dose behaves like roughly 120mg | Avoid `[primary, verified]` |
| **Ciprofloxacin, enoxacin** | Potent CYP1A2 inhibitors; avoid per label | `[primary, verified]` |
| **Cimetidine** | CYP1A2 inhibitor | `[secondary — unverified]` |
| **Smoking** | Serum duloxetine **38.4% lower**; dose-adjusted concentration **53.6% lower** | `[primary, verified]` |
| **Fluoxetine, paroxetine** | Potent CYP2D6 inhibitors, so they raise duloxetine | Relevant only if a switch is contemplated `[secondary — unverified]` |
| **St John's wort** | CYP1A2 **inducer**, so it lowers duloxetine | `[secondary — unverified]` |

The smoking figure read in your direction: **as a non-smoker you run 38 to 54% higher exposure than a smoker on the same prescription** `[primary, verified]`. This is not a hazard and requires no action. It is a calibration on every population figure on every other page, because those populations contain smokers and you sit above their average before any other variable is considered. It also has a corollary that does not apply to you but is badly under-communicated generally: a person who quits smoking on a stable duloxetine dose receives a substantial spontaneous dose increase with no prescription change.

**Outbound, duloxetine changing other drugs:** the moderate CYP2D6 inhibition matters for CYP2D6 substrates, which includes tramadol, codeine, metoprolol, some antipsychotics, and TCAs `[secondary — unverified]`.

The combination named as genuinely dangerous is a **CYP2D6 poor metaboliser who is also given a CYP1A2 inhibitor**, because both clearance routes are blocked at once `[secondary — unverified]`.

**NSAIDs and bleeding:** SSRIs plus NSAIDs raise upper GI bleeding risk with an OR of about 1.75 (95% CI 1.32 to 2.33) `[secondary — unverified]`. The mechanism is real: platelets cannot synthesise serotonin and must take it up via SERT, so blocking SERT depletes platelet serotonin and impairs aggregation. But a 75% relative increase on a low baseline remains a low absolute risk in a younger adult with no ulcer history, no anticoagulant and no steroid. The relative risk is the number that gets quoted; the absolute risk is the one that matters. It becomes meaningful with regular high-dose NSAIDs, prior GI bleed, anticoagulants or antiplatelets, corticosteroids, or older age. Worth noting separately that NSAIDs have poor evidence in fibromyalgia specifically, so this risk may be being taken for very little benefit.

### Blood pressure, heart rate, and the shape of the tail

| Parameter | Effect | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Mean systolic BP vs placebo | **+0.5 mmHg** (placebo −0.6) | FDA label `[secondary — unverified]` |
| Mean diastolic BP vs placebo | **+0.8 mmHg** (placebo −0.3) | FDA label `[secondary — unverified]` |
| Heart rate | **+2.22 bpm** (95% CI 1.53 to 2.91), 17 trials | Park 2020 `[primary, verified]` |
| Diastolic BP (meta-analysis) | **+0.82 mmHg** (95% CI 0.17 to 1.47) | Park 2020 `[primary, verified]` |
| **Sustained** elevated BP (3 consecutive visits) | **No significant difference vs placebo**, 42 studies, 8,504 patients | Wernicke 2007 `[primary, verified]` |
| Supratherapeutic (up to 200mg BID) | +4.7 to 6.8 systolic / +4.5 to 7.0 diastolic | FDA label `[secondary — unverified]` |

Wernicke's pooled analysis concluded that "use of duloxetine does not appear to be associated with significant cardiovascular risks in patients with conditions for which the drug has been approved or studied," and found no increased risk of sustained BP elevation even in patients with elevated baseline blood pressure `[primary, verified]`.

**Against which sits Stevens 2008** `[primary, verified]`: a 26-year-old man presenting with fatigue, tachycardia, diaphoresis and chest pain about two months after starting **duloxetine 20 mg/day**. Cardiac workup including echocardiogram, exercise treadmill testing and Holter monitoring was negative except for tachycardia at 110 to 120 bpm. Duloxetine was withheld and heart rate normalised in under a week; duloxetine was restarted at the same dose and tachycardia returned within two days. Naranjo score: probable. The authors' conclusion was that duloxetine can produce clinically significant tachycardia "even in low doses."

The two facts are not in tension, and holding them together is the whole skill: **a population mean of +2.22 bpm is arithmetically compatible with most people at zero and a minority at +40.** The mean describes the population. It does not describe any individual in it. This is the same point that the 8.5-fold individual EC50 spread makes elsewhere in this deep dive, arriving from a different direction.

**The label's own risk amplifiers**, verbatim: "The risk of blood pressure decreases may be greater in patients taking concomitant medications that induce orthostatic hypotension (such as antihypertensives) or are potent CYP1A2 inhibitors... and in patients taking duloxetine at doses above 60 mg daily." A preceding sentence notes that syncope and orthostatic hypotension "tend to occur within the first week of therapy but can occur at any time during duloxetine treatment, particularly after dose increases" `[primary, verified]`.

Read precisely, that is a **timing** statement about dose increases and a **dose threshold of above 60mg**. The label does not support treating a 20 to 30mg or 20 to 60mg step as a high-risk event. That is a useful calibration in the cautious direction's disfavour, and it is worth having.

### Orthostatic intolerance, POTS, and the ME/CFS overlap

This item flip-flopped twice in this project, so the provenance is stated in full.

**The mechanism is real and cleanly demonstrated, at the class level** `[primary, verified]`. **Schroeder 2002** (PMID 11804991), reboxetine 8mg vs placebo, 18 healthy subjects, randomised double-blind crossover: at 75° head-up tilt, heart rate was 84±3 with placebo and **119±4 with reboxetine** (P<0.0001). The paper's own title calls it "a human model of orthostatic intolerance," which is to say a POTS phenotype manufactured in healthy people by NET blockade alone. **Green 2013** (PMID 24002370), atomoxetine 40mg vs placebo in **27 actual POTS patients**: standing HR **121±17 vs 105±15** (P=0.001), symptom scores worsened (+4.2 vs −3.5, P=0.028). This is about as clean as pharmacological causation gets, and it is coherent with the genetics, because a loss-of-function NET mutation causes familial orthostatic tachycardia.

**The duloxetine-specific claim is real, and it is dose-qualified** `[primary, verified]`:

- **Mar et al. 2014** (PMID 24227635, Vanderbilt Autonomic Dysfunction Center), verbatim: *"High-dose duloxetine can cause healthy volunteers to develop a POTS-like phenotype with increased orthostatic tachycardia after chronic administration that is likely related to norepinephrine transporter inhibition."*
- **Raj 2013** (PMID 23753844), verbatim: *"Pharmacological NET inhibition can recreate an orthostatic tachycardia phenotype in susceptible healthy volunteer subjects. POTS patients might have less 'tachycardia reserve' and be more susceptible to exaggerated tachycardia with these medications."* Its treatment table lists SNRI antidepressants among drugs to withdraw.
- **2015 Heart Rhythm Society consensus statement** (PMID 25980576), **Class III, Level B-R**: *"Drugs that block the norepinephrine reuptake transporter can worsen symptoms in patients with POTS and should not be administered."* Class III is the net-harm class. Corroborated by the 2020 Canadian Cardiovascular Society POTS position statement (PMID 32145864).

**An earlier pass in this project marked all of that INVALID and attributed it to AI content farms. That verdict was wrong and is overturned.** The narrower correction that does survive is the dose qualifier: both sources say *high-dose* and *chronic*, and the only duloxetine-specific study behind them used **80, 120 and 240 mg/day** in 15 healthy volunteers (Vincent 2004, PMID 15184278), which is **four to twelve times your dose**. Any text asserting a POTS-like phenotype at 20 to 60mg is overreaching its own source. **Provenance caveat that must not be dropped:** Vincent 2004 could not be retrieved in full by this project, so the "POTS-like phenotype" attribution rests on Mar 2014's characterisation of it rather than on the primary `[secondary — unverified]`. That is the single biggest remaining gap in this page.

**One adjacent claim is invalid and should be deleted on sight.** "SSRIs are preferred over SNRIs in POTS" appears in no guideline. The HRS statement never mentions SSRIs or SNRIs in its POTS section; Raj 2013 never uses the string "SSRI." Its only source is Mar 2014's own speculation that a more selective SSRI "might be better tolerated" with "further studies need to be conducted" — and **Mar 2014's own data refute it**: in 39 POTS patients, sertraline 50mg versus placebo in crossover produced standing HR and systolic BP that were *not significantly different*, and symptoms that were *worse* with sertraline than placebo `[primary, verified]`.

**A symmetry problem worth naming, and its defeater.** One pass argued that this deep dive cannot invoke low NET occupancy (~30% at 20mg) to dismiss noradrenergic *benefit* while ignoring the same 30% when asserting orthostatic *harm*. That catch is fair as a discipline point, and both claims should indeed be dose-qualified. But it is not a strict contradiction `[reasoning, not evidence]`, and the decisive reason is **compartments**: duloxetine's 30 to 40% is *brain PET occupancy*, whereas the orthostatic effect of NET inhibition is substantially *peripheral*, at cardiac sympathetic nerve terminals outside the blood-brain barrier. Brain occupancy is the wrong proxy for a cardiovascular effect, and Vincent 2004 measured *ex vivo plasma* NET binding inhibition, reporting a maximum of 60% at 240 mg/day, which is a different number in a different compartment. Two lesser reasons: analgesia and orthostatic tachycardia are different endpoints with no requirement to share a threshold, and a +2.22 bpm population mean is compatible with zero in most people and +40 in a few, which is precisely Raj's "less tachycardia reserve" point.

And the measured effects scale coherently with occupancy rather than contradicting it:

| Drug and dose | NET occupancy | Measured HR effect |
|---|---|---|
| Duloxetine 20mg | 29.7% (human PET) | — |
| Duloxetine 60mg | 40.0% (human PET) | — |
| Duloxetine 60–120mg | ~40–50% | **+2.22 bpm** (Park 2020, 17 trials) |
| Duloxetine 80–240mg | ~51–76% | "POTS-like phenotype" (Vincent 2004, **unverified**) |
| Atomoxetine 40mg | ~90% (derived estimate) | **+16 bpm standing, in POTS** (Green 2013) |
| Reboxetine 8mg | near-complete | **+35 bpm at 75° HUT** (Schroeder 2002) |

**The direction cuts both ways, and this is demonstrated within single studies rather than across them** `[primary, verified]`. Schroeder 2006 (PMID 16875978), 51 healthy subjects, double-blind randomised crossover: mean tolerated tilt duration was 29±2 min with placebo and **35±1 min with NET inhibition** (p=0.001), with an **odds ratio of 0.22 (95% CI 0.09 to 0.55)** for premature tilt termination. *(Note: an earlier pass restated that OR as a "78% reduction." An OR of 0.22 is not a 78% risk reduction. Quote the OR.)* And in the 2002 reboxetine study, **vasovagal reactions occurred in 9 of 18 subjects on placebo but only 1 of 18 on reboxetine.** Both of those studies also show the tachycardia. The duality is internal to each study, which strengthens rather than weakens the conclusion: **NET inhibition worsens orthostatic tachycardia and protects against vasovagal pre-syncope, simultaneously.**

**Which matters because most ME/CFS orthostatic intolerance is not POTS** `[primary, verified]`. Garner & Baraniuk 2019 (PMID 31159884), n=39: POTS on formal testing in **6 of 39** (~15%), yet 72% had orthostatic symptoms, and the authors' central conclusion was that *"orthostatic tachycardia did not account for OI symptoms in CFS."* Separately, van Campen's work finds ~90% of ME/CFS patients show an abnormal cerebral blood flow reduction on tilt, **including patients with normal heart rate and blood pressure responses** `[secondary — unverified]`. So a normal tilt-table heart rate does not rule out orthostatic intolerance in ME/CFS, and "do you have POTS?" is the wrong question.

**The honest net statement:** if your orthostatic intolerance, should you have any, is the tachycardic type, duloxetine's mechanism is unfavourable and a formal guideline says NET blockers should be withdrawn. If it is the neurally-mediated or hypotensive type, NET inhibition may be neutral or protective. **Nobody has characterised which you have, and the direction is genuinely unknown.**

**The protocol that would characterise it**, framed as what to understand rather than what to do: lie down 10 minutes and record HR and BP; stand, and record HR and BP at 1, 3, 5 and 10 minutes, noting symptoms at each point. An HR rise of ≥30 bpm sustained without a BP drop is the POTS pattern, the one where duloxetine's mechanism is unfavourable. A systolic drop ≥20 or diastolic drop ≥10 mmHg within 3 minutes is orthostatic hypotension. Symptoms with neither is the most common ME/CFS pattern, and the one where duloxetine's effect is unknown and possibly neutral or favourable. It takes ten minutes and a cheap home cuff, two weeks of baseline before the appointment is achievable, and repeating it after any dose change makes the drug's effect legible rather than inferable.

**The one ME/CFS-specific datum, with its correction** `[primary, verified]`. Arnold 2015 (PMID 25660434) is the only duloxetine trial ever run in CFS patients: n=60, duloxetine 60–120mg vs placebo, 12 weeks, negative on its primary endpoint. Its adverse-event table, retrieved from the ClinicalTrials.gov results posting for NCT00375973 where the numbers resolve exactly:

| Adverse event | Duloxetine | Placebo |
|---|---|---|
| **Dizziness** | **31.03%** (9/29) | 6.67% (2/30) |
| Lightheadedness | 17.24% (5/29) | 10.00% (3/30) |
| Nausea | 65.52% | 20.00% |
| Somnolence | 41.38% | 10.00% |
| Insomnia | 34.48% | 13.33% |
| **Headache** | **10.34%** | **40.00%** |

For comparison, dizziness runs ~11.0% in fibromyalgia, ~14.5% in breast cancer, and ~16.1% in depression at 60mg `[secondary — unverified]`, so the CFS figure is roughly double against a 6.7% placebo rate.

**Now the corrections, which an earlier pass omitted while putting substantial weight on the dizziness row.** There is **no statistical test attached to any row**; "31.03% vs 6.67%" is 9 events versus 2 on arms of 29 and 30, which is a wide and untested difference. Headache ran the opposite way, which is what a 30-person arm looks like. The dose was three to six times yours. And most importantly: **no tachycardia, no palpitations, no syncope and no orthostatic hypotension term appears anywhere in that table** at the 5% threshold. **There is no tachycardia signal in the only duloxetine trial ever run in this population.** The absence deserves equal billing with the dizziness.

### Emotional blunting: anatomy of a collapsed claim

**The claim:** antidepressant emotional blunting is dose-dependent, therefore a dose increase raises the risk, therefore this is an argument against going up. A validation pass in this project called it "one of the strongest arguments against a dose increase for this specific reader" and said "it should carry more weight, not less."

**It collapses.** Traced link by link `[primary, verified]`:

1. **Ma H, Cai M, Wang H.** *Front Psychiatry* 2021;12:792960, retrieved in full, states verbatim: *"Overall, the occurrence of emotion blunting seems to be dose-related, with lower doses being less likely to induce this symptom."* The review is explicitly self-described as "brief non-systematic."
2. **Its citation for that sentence is reference [25]**, which is **Price J, Cole V, Goodwin GM**, "Emotional side-effects of selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors: qualitative study," *Br J Psychiatry* 2009;195:211–7 (PMID 19721109). Design: 38 individual interviews, 8 in a group interview, 11 validation interviews, 272 web forum posts. Its only dose-relevant text is: *"Some participants made specific reference to changes in their emotional experiences in relation to changes in medication type or dose."* **No dose-response analysis was performed. Dose was never systematically measured or quantitatively analysed.**
3. *(An earlier pass in this project claimed the dose sentence cited a fluoxetine case report. That is factually wrong: the 1990 Hoehn-Saric case report is in the review, but it supports an adjacent sentence. Anyone repeating that phrasing would be making a checkable error.)*
4. **The 2023 apathy review** (Masdrakis, Markianos, Baldwin, *Acta Neuropsychiatr*, PMID 36644883) asserts apathy *"appears dose-dependent and reversible"* citing **only case reports**: Hoehn-Saric 1990/1991, George & Trimble 1992, Garland & Baerg 2001, Padala 2012, Sato & Asada 2011. It concedes that *"most studies are either case reports or internet/telephone surveys."*
5. **Ma 2021 and Masdrakis 2023 both root back to the same 1990 case report.** Two reviews asserting the same thing from the same source is **one piece of evidence, not two**. This is citation laundering: repetition across reviews manufacturing the appearance of consensus. Both the original research and the validator in this project consumed the laundered version.
6. **No dose-stratified or dose-response analysis of antidepressant-induced emotional blunting exists.** Searched across OQESA studies, ELEVATE/vortioxetine, PREDDICT, Goodwin, Price, Opbroek 2002, Sansone & Sansone, Fagiolini, and the Jawad scoping review.
7. **And the closest empirical test is adversarial to the claim.** Aşçibaşi K, et al., *J Clin Psychopharmacol* 2020 (PMID 33065720), n=98 MDD, doses **systematically escalated** (*"the antidepressant dose was increased in patients with less than a 50% reduction at each visit"*), OQESA at weeks 8 and 16: *"A significant difference is found in the OQESA score at the 8th-week visit compared with the 16th-week assessment (P < 0.001, t = 5.73)"* — **blunting scores fell.** Conclusion: *"As the rate of remitted patients is increased, OQESA scores get decreased."* This is confounded with time and remission, so it does not refute dose-dependence. It is the closest thing to a test that exists, and it does not support it.
8. **Compounding it:** Goodwin 2017 found blunting correlated with depression severity (higher HAD scores), raising the live possibility that blunting substantially *is* a symptom of depression rather than a dose effect, which would invert the clinical logic entirely. Relatedly, the two reviews contradict each other: Masdrakis says apathy emerges "independently of treatment outcome," while Aşçibaşi found blunting tracked remission. Both cannot be right.

**The honest position is not "blunting is dose-dependent, so don't go up." It is: nobody has ever measured whether blunting is dose-dependent.** That is a reason for monitoring, not for confident prediction in either direction.

**What survives** `[primary, verified]` unless marked:

- **Prevalence 46%** in Goodwin GM, Price J, De Bodinat C, Laredo J, *J Affect Disord* 2017;221:31–35 (PMID 28628765): 669 depressed patients on treatment plus 150 recovered controls, internet survey. Broader literature range for blunting: **40 to 60%**. *(Note: the "20 to 92%" range that circulates in this project's documents is a different construct. It refers to apathy specifically with SSRIs, from a different review whose overall antidepressant-induced apathy range is 5.8 to 50%. Merging blunting and apathy figures is a construct slip and should not be repeated.)*
- **Reversible**, and **dose reduction is the standard management**, consistently supported by case reports `[secondary — unverified]`. The clinical practice is real and widespread, which is at least indirect evidence that some dose relationship is genuine.
- **No difference by any agent.** Goodwin, verbatim: *"There was no difference according to antidepressant agent, though it appeared less frequent with bupropion."* SSRIs run 43 to 47%; bupropion 33% is the lowest `[secondary — unverified]` for the exact figures, direction confirmed. **This is the finding that matters for you and it is not comforting: on blunting, duloxetine ≈ escitalopram.** Whatever made duloxetine different for you, it is not that duloxetine is a low-blunting drug.
- Caveats on the prevalence figure: internet survey, self-selected, cross-sectional, no dose data, confounded by depression itself. Not a number to bet on. It is the best that exists.

### Dissociation, and the question nobody has studied

**Is depersonalization/derealization a recognised SSRI/SNRI adverse effect?** Recognised, yes. **Quantified, no** `[no evidence exists]` for incidence. It is not systematically collected in trials and is described as underreported relative to other adverse effects, so the honest answer to "how common is it?" is unknown and probably under-detected. Reversibility on discontinuation is case-report grade and consistently positive `[secondary — unverified]`.

**The mechanistic link is stronger than expected** `[primary, verified]`. Simeon D, Hollander E, Stein DJ, et al., *Psychiatry Res* 1995 (PMID 8570768): **67 subjects** received ratings of depersonalization after **double-blind, placebo-controlled** challenges with the partial serotonin agonist m-CPP. *"Challenge with m-CPP induced depersonalization significantly more than did placebo."* That is a real experimental demonstration that serotonergic agonism causes depersonalization in humans, and it is the best available support for the reading that your escitalopram dissociation was a drug effect rather than a coincidence. **Three caveats bound what it can bear:** m-CPP is a partial 5-HT agonist research probe rather than an antidepressant; the population was normal volunteers plus OCD, social phobia and BPD patients, not MDD patients on antidepressants; and **it says nothing about dose**, so it cannot be used to prop up a dose claim.

**And it cuts both ways.** There is a documented case of depersonalization-derealization syndrome induced by **reboxetine**, a selective *noradrenergic* reuptake inhibitor: Khazaal Y, Zullino DF, *Swiss Med Wkly* 2003;133(27–28):398–9 (PMID 12947529) `[primary, verified]`. That is a single case report against a 67-subject controlled challenge, so the serotonergic link remains much better evidenced, and the case report's own proposed mechanism (*"a noradrenaline-induced serotonin liberation of Raphe neurons"*) routes back through serotonin, so it does not demonstrate a purely noradrenergic route. But it is enough to block the clean story that "it was serotonin, duloxetine has proportionally less of it, therefore we are fine."

**Does prior serotonergic dissociation predict recurrence at higher doses?** `[no evidence exists]`. There is:

- **no** study of whether prior antidepressant-induced dissociation predicts recurrence,
- **no** study of whether it predicts recurrence on a *different* serotonergic drug,
- **no** study of whether it is dose-related *within* an individual,
- **no** validated risk prediction of any kind.

**Nobody has studied it, and nobody will.** That trial will not be run. The inference "I dissociated on one serotonergic drug, so I may dissociate on another, more so at a higher dose" is sensible `[reasoning, not evidence]`, consistent with the m-CPP data, with the reversibility case reports, and with ordinary pharmacological logic. It has never been tested. Which means your strongest personal argument for caution is an argument from **your own history and from mechanism, not from evidence**. That does not make it a bad argument. Personal history is legitimate evidence about a person, and here it is the only evidence that exists about this question at all. It should simply not be dressed up as literature.

### The "floaty" feeling: four candidates and a discriminator

This has no clean literature home. "Floaty" is not a clinical term, has no literature, and maps onto at least four distinct phenomena. It is not resolvable from the outside, and anyone who tells you otherwise is guessing `[reasoning, not evidence]`. But it is partly resolvable from the inside, and that is worth more.

| Candidate | Base rate | Distinguishing features |
|---|---|---|
| **Early orthostatic / dizziness effect** | Common: ~11 to 16% generally; **31% in the CFS trial** vs 6.7% placebo | Positional: worse on standing or rising. Time-locked to dosing. Physical rather than perceptual. **Attenuates over weeks** |
| **Mild emotional blunting** | Common: ~46% | Affective flattening, not spatial. Emotions muted rather than the self feeling unreal. Slow, gradual onset |
| **Early depersonalization** (the escitalopram thing, dimmer) | **Unknown, never quantified** | Perceptual and experiential. Self or world feels unreal, detached, dreamlike. **Not positional.** Persists regardless of posture |
| **Ordinary early adaptation** | Common | Non-specific, resolves in 2 to 4 weeks |

**What favours the benign readings:** dizziness is common, early, and attenuating, and it is *specifically elevated in CFS patients* at 31% versus 6.7%, which is your population. The single most likely explanation for a new floaty feeling in an ME/CFS patient two weeks into duloxetine is the most common early side effect of duloxetine in ME/CFS patients. That is a base rate, not a dismissal. Beyond that, you describe it as mild and non-impairing, whereas your escitalopram experience was dissociation plus non-functionality, which is a large qualitative gap rather than a small quantitative one, and your ability to tell those apart is itself data.

**What favours concern:** you have a documented prior serotonergic dissociation, and duloxetine at 20mg is a squarely serotonergic drug at roughly 75% SERT occupancy. The m-CPP data confirm serotonergic agonism can induce depersonalization. Onset is slow and gradual, so two weeks may be early in its course rather than past its window. And autistic adults with alexithymia may be systematically less able to detect early affective change in themselves, which is the exact population in which "mild and non-impairing" is least reliable as self-report `[reasoning, not evidence]`. That last point is inference from the alexithymia literature rather than a studied finding about this drug, and it is the reason not to lean too hard on the discrimination point above.

**The discriminator, which costs nothing** `[reasoning, not evidence]`. These candidates make different predictions about posture and timing:

- Does it get **worse on standing and better lying down**? That is orthostatic. Pair it with the standing test above: if floaty tracks a measurable BP drop or HR rise, it is very likely circulatory.
- Does it **track time since dose**, peaking around 6 hours at duloxetine's Tmax? That is pharmacokinetic, most likely dizziness.
- Is it **continuous and unrelated to posture**, a quality of experience rather than of the head? That is more concerning and more like the dissociative family.
- Is it **"the world feels unreal" or "I feel less"**? The first is depersonalization; the second is blunting. Different problems.
- Is it **attenuating week by week**? That strongly favours adaptation or dizziness. Stable or growing does not.

This is a reasonable inference from the known phenomenology of each candidate, not a validated test. No study has done it. But it uses observations you are already making, and it converts an unanswerable question into two weeks of data. The meta-point is the real answer: **you do not have to resolve this before the appointment. You have to notice which way it is moving.** If it is attenuating, you will know, and that is reassuring. If it is growing, that is the single most important thing to say out loud in that room, and it will be a fact rather than a worry.

### Long-term use: what the evidence covers, and where it stops

**The FDA label, verbatim and consistent across 2008, 2010 and 2014 labels** `[primary, verified]`:

> "The efficacy of Cymbalta in the management of fibromyalgia has been demonstrated in placebo-controlled studies up to 3 months. The efficacy of Cymbalta was not demonstrated in longer studies."

**That is not a bureaucratic technicality. It is a description of a specific failed trial.** There are two six-month placebo-controlled fibromyalgia trials of duloxetine, and they disagree `[primary, verified]`:

| Trial | Design | Result |
|---|---|---|
| **Russell 2008** | 6 months, 1:2:2:2, 20/60/120mg vs placebo | 120mg separated on both co-primaries; 60mg on BPI only |
| **Chappell AS, et al. 2008** (*Int J Gen Med* 1:91–102, PMID 20428412) | 27 weeks, duloxetine 60/120mg (n=162) vs placebo (n=168) | **Failed both co-primaries**: BPI average pain severity −1.62 vs −1.13, **P=0.053**; PGI-I 3.43 vs 3.72, **P=0.073** |

Chappell 2008 was sponsored by Eli Lilly and Boehringer Ingelheim, and three of its authors are Lilly employees and stockholders. Both trials were near-misses rather than flat nulls, and the secondaries were positive. But the dose commonly proposed as the target, 60mg, failed both co-primary endpoints in a 330-patient, six-month fibromyalgia trial. *(Note for anyone tracing citations: **Chappell 2008 and Chappell 2009 are different papers.** Chappell 2008 is the 6-month placebo-controlled trial. Chappell 2009, PMID 19454869, is the 1-year study below. Do not merge them.)*

**Beyond six months the evidence changes shape rather than merely thinning** `[primary, verified]`. The longest efficacy data is a one-year study (PMID 19454869): 8 weeks open-label duloxetine (30mg for 1 week, then 60mg for 7), then randomised **60mg vs 120mg, 1:2, for 52 weeks, double-blind**, n=350, 95.7% female. Pain reduction from the open-label phase continued through the 52-week phase in both groups.

**There is no placebo arm.** This trial randomises duloxetine against duloxetine. It can establish that 60mg and 120mg behave similarly over a year. It **cannot** establish that either beats placebo, and it cannot distinguish drug effect from natural history, regression to the mean, or the 30 to 40% placebo response that fibromyalgia trials reliably produce. Everyone enrolled had already responded well enough to continue through eight open-label weeks, so the population is selected and enriched.

**There is no placebo-controlled duloxetine data past 12 months in any indication** `[primary, verified]`.

**Does the benefit persist?** Unknown, and not answerable from the current evidence base. Cochrane records ≥50% pain response sustained at 28 weeks (RR 1.58, 95% CI 1.10 to 2.27), which is the best long-term controlled signal available, and it comes from the same six-month trials that split on their co-primaries `[secondary — unverified]`.

**Safety, unlike efficacy, does have a longer horizon** `[secondary — unverified]`. Lilly's five-trial pooled safety analysis (PMC2721139) concluded that duloxetine 20–120mg/day showed "a similar safety and tolerability profile in the treatment of fibromyalgia to that seen in the treatment of other indications." An open-label 52-week Japanese phase-III extension exists (PMID 27796152). But no years-scale controlled efficacy data exists for fibromyalgia, and safety follow-up beyond one year is open-label and sponsor-run.

**The asymmetry is the point: the safety database is large and long; the efficacy database is small and short.** "Well tolerated long-term" is much better established than "still working long-term." Those are different claims and they are routinely conflated.

### What to raise, and what to carry

Nothing on this page directs a dose. These are things to understand and things to raise.

**Worth carrying physically rather than remembering:** that you take duloxetine, in a form that survives you being unconscious or acutely unwell. The three drugs that matter most (linezolid, methylene blue, MAOIs) come from clinicians who did not prescribe your antidepressant and have no reason to know about it.

**Worth saying if tramadol is offered:** that duloxetine inhibits CYP2D6, which tramadol needs in order to work, so the combination gives less analgesia and more serotonergic exposure at once.

**Worth saying to any antibiotic prescriber:** that you take duloxetine, and that ciprofloxacin is the one to avoid.

**Worth asking at the appointment:**

- "Do I have orthostatic intolerance, and has anyone ever actually checked?" In ME/CFS it usually has not been.
- "Can we do a supine and standing BP and HR at this visit?" It takes three minutes and is rarely done.
- "The label says the orthostatic risk concentrates above 60mg and after dose increases. Does that change how we would step this?"
- "If blunting or the floaty feeling grows, what is the plan?" Dose reduction is the standard management, so the trigger is worth agreeing in advance rather than in the middle of it.

**Worth doing beforehand, because it is free and the window is open:** two weeks of active stand tests, and two weeks of tracking floaty on a 0 to 10 scale with three tags (positional yes/no, time since dose, and "unreal" versus "muted"). Neither resolves anything alone. Both convert a worry into a fact, and a fact is a much better thing to bring into a twelve-minute appointment.
