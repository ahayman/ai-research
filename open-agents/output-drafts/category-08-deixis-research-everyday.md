# Category 08: Deixis in Everyday English Communication

## Research Purpose
Map how deictic expressions function in real communicative situations, with extensive practical examples. Designed for a knowledge base teaching English pragmatics to autistic individuals. Special focus on where misunderstanding commonly occurs and why.

## Core Concept: What Deixis Is

Deixis (from Ancient Greek *deîxis*, meaning "display, demonstration, reference") refers to words and expressions whose meaning depends entirely on context -- specifically, on who is speaking, where they are, and when they are speaking. The word "I" has no fixed referent: it means whoever is currently talking. The word "here" has no fixed location: it means wherever the speaker currently is. The word "now" has no fixed time: it means whenever the speaker is speaking.

The **deictic center** (also called the *origo*) is the anchor point from which all deictic expressions are interpreted. In default face-to-face conversation, the deictic center is:
- **Person**: The current speaker
- **Place**: Where the speaker is located
- **Time**: The moment of speaking

This deictic center constantly shifts as speakers take turns in conversation. When you become the speaker, "I" now means you, "here" means where you are, and "now" means the current moment from your perspective.

**Why this matters for autistic individuals:** Deictic expressions require constant, real-time recalculation of reference points. Research has found that "deictic shifting" -- the cognitive process of remapping pronouns and spatial terms as perspectives change -- presents specific challenges for autistic individuals. Studies at PMC (PMC3155703) found that deictic shifting slowed response times in autistic participants more than in controls, supporting the hypothesis that this kind of perspective-dependent reference tracking is genuinely more effortful for autistic cognition. However, the challenge is not inability but rather processing cost -- and explicit awareness of how deixis works can significantly reduce that cost.

## 1. PERSON DEIXIS IN PRACTICE

Person deixis uses pronouns and related expressions to point to participants in a conversation. The first person ("I," "we") points to the speaker, the second person ("you") to the listener, and the third person ("he," "she," "they") to others. But in practice, each of these is far more complicated than it appears.

### 1.1 The "We" Problem

English has a single first-person plural pronoun: "we." Unlike many world languages (such as Mandarin Chinese, Malay, Tamil, and numerous Austronesian and Dravidian languages), English does not grammatically distinguish between **inclusive "we"** (you and I together) and **exclusive "we"** (I and some others, but not you). This creates systematic ambiguity.

**The "we" of shared action (inclusive):**
- "We're pregnant!" -- The speaker includes both partners in the experience. (Though some find this usage debatable, since only one person is literally pregnant.)
- "Shall we?" -- An invitation that includes the listener. Means "should you and I [do this together]?"
- "We had a great time at the party" (said to someone who was also at the party) -- Shared experience.

**The "we" of exclusion (exclusive, despite the word "we"):**
- "We need to talk." -- Despite using "we," this almost always means "I have a problem with something you did, and I want to discuss it." The "we" softens what is really a one-directional confrontation. Pragmatically, this phrase has become a fixed expression that triggers anxiety because its implicature is reliably negative.
- "We don't do that here." -- Means "You shouldn't do that." The "we" invokes a group norm that the listener is violating. The speaker positions themselves as spokesperson for the group, and the listener as the outsider who needs correction.
- "We've decided to go in a different direction." -- Corporate euphemism, typically meaning "You're fired" or "Your proposal is rejected." The "we" diffuses responsibility across an unnamed group.

**The institutional/corporate "we":**
- "We think this is a good approach." -- Who exactly is "we"? The speaker and their team? The entire department? The company? This is deliberately vague.
- "We are committed to customer satisfaction." -- Corporate "we" that represents the organization as a single entity.
- "We're going to need you to come in on Saturday." -- Often means "I, your boss, need you to," but "we" makes it sound like a collective decision, distributing the imposition.

**The patronizing "we" (sometimes called the "nurse's we"):**
- "How are we feeling today?" -- A doctor or nurse speaking to a patient. The speaker is clearly not feeling whatever the patient is feeling. This use of "we" is intended to express solidarity but is frequently received as condescending, particularly by elderly patients and people with disabilities. Research on "elderspeak" has documented that patients hear this as disrespectful and patronizing, even though the speaker intends to be supportive and caring.
- "Are we ready for our bath?" -- Same pattern. The "we" infantilizes the listener by pretending shared experience where none exists.

**The "royal we" and its modern echoes:**
The historical "royal we" (*pluralis majestatis*) dates to the late twelfth century in English, originally meaning "God and I" and invoking the divine right of kings. While the literal royal usage is essentially extinct in modern English (surviving mainly in historical fiction), echoes persist:
- Academics writing "we argue that..." (even in single-authored papers)
- Judges: "We find the defendant guilty" (the court as institution)
- Editorial "we": "We believe this policy is misguided" (the newspaper's editorial board)

**Common misunderstanding pattern:** When someone says "we" in a workplace context, an autistic listener may genuinely not know who is included. The statement "We need to finish this by Friday" could mean: (a) you and I together, (b) our team, (c) the department, or (d) just you -- phrased with "we" to sound collaborative. Neurotypical listeners often resolve this ambiguity through social context (tone, relationship dynamics, who has what responsibilities), but this resolution process may not be automatic for everyone.

**Strategy:** When "we" is ambiguous, it is entirely reasonable to ask: "When you say 'we,' who specifically do you mean?" This is not a strange question -- it is a precise question that prevents real miscommunication.

### 1.2 When "You" Is Generic vs. Specific

English uses "you" for both the specific listener and as a generic pronoun meaning "anyone" or "people in general." Research confirms that generic "you" serves to generalize the speaker's experience by attributing it to anyone who might be in a similar situation.

**Clearly generic "you":**
- "You never know what's going to happen." -- Not about the specific listener. Means "No one can predict the future."
- "You win some, you lose some." -- Generic life wisdom.
- "You can't make an omelette without breaking eggs." -- Proverb. The "you" means "anyone."
- "You just have to keep trying." -- Could be generic (life advice) or specific (said to someone struggling with a task). Context determines which.

**Clearly specific "you":**
- "You left the door open." -- Directed at the listener.
- "You owe me twenty dollars." -- Specific.
- "Can you pass the salt?" -- Directed at a particular person.

**Ambiguous "you" (the problem zone):**
- "You should eat more vegetables." -- Is this generic health advice ("one should eat more vegetables") or a directed comment about the listener's diet? The answer depends on context: a nutritionist in a lecture is generic; a partner at the dinner table is probably specific and may feel like a criticism.
- "You know what I mean?" -- Directed at the listener but so formulaic that it barely functions as a real question. It is more of a discourse marker checking for understanding or seeking agreement.
- "You have to be more careful." -- Specific (criticism of listener) or generic (general principle)? Often ambiguous enough to cause hurt feelings when generic was intended, or to let a specific criticism slide by when the listener interprets it as generic.

**Why this matters for autistic individuals:** Research from PNAS (2020) found that generic "you" creates a sense of personal resonance and connection even when listeners know they are not being addressed specifically. But this effect depends on intuitive pragmatic processing. An autistic listener who processes language more literally may either (a) take generic "you" as directed at them personally, creating unnecessary distress ("Why are they telling me to eat more vegetables? Am I doing something wrong?"), or (b) recognize the generic usage but miss the emotional solidarity it is meant to create.

### 1.3 "They" in Its Many Uses

"They" carries at least three distinct deictic functions in modern English:

**Specific singular "they" (known person, gender-neutral):**
- "Alex called. They want to meet tomorrow." -- "They" refers to Alex. Used when Alex's gender is unknown to the listener, when Alex uses they/them pronouns, or simply as a default.
- "Someone left their umbrella. I hope they come back for it." -- "They" refers to an unknown person.
- This usage has existed in English since the 14th century (about a century after the plural "they" emerged) and has been formally accepted by APA, AP, and Chicago style guides.

**Vague authority "they":**
- "They say it's going to rain." -- Who says? Meteorologists? People in general? The vague "they" invokes unspecified expertise or common wisdom.
- "They say breakfast is the most important meal of the day." -- Same pattern. "They" points to a non-specific authoritative source.
- "You know what they say..." -- Opens a claim by attributing it to unidentified general wisdom.
- The impersonal "they" functions to exclude the speaker from the statement and attribute it to an external authority, often one that cannot be questioned because it cannot be identified.

**Institutional "they":**
- "They changed the bus schedule." -- The transit authority.
- "They're raising tuition again." -- The university administration.
- "They won't let you park there." -- Some authority (police, property management, etc.).
- This "they" is similar to vague authority but refers to a more specific (though still unnamed) organizational entity.

**Common misunderstanding pattern:** In a conversation where multiple people have been mentioned, "they" can be genuinely ambiguous. "I talked to the Johnsons and the Petersons. They're coming to dinner." Which family? Both? In speech, this is usually resolved by context, emphasis, or gesture -- channels that may be less available to some autistic listeners.

## 2. SPATIAL DEIXIS IN PRACTICE

Spatial deixis orients communication in physical (and increasingly virtual) space. The basic system contrasts **proximal** (near the speaker) with **distal** (far from the speaker).

### 2.1 "This" vs. "That" in Conversation

**Physical proximity:**
- "This is my friend Sarah." -- Introducing someone who is present, near the speaker. You would not say "That is my friend Sarah" while Sarah is standing next to you (that would create a distancing effect and potentially sound dismissive or odd).
- "Can you hand me that book?" -- The book is away from the speaker, near or nearer to the listener.
- "What is this?" (holding an object) vs. "What is that?" (pointing to something across the room) -- Proximity determines the choice.

**Emotional or psychological proximity:**
- "This" can signal emotional closeness or current relevance: "This is what I've been talking about!" (= this is currently important to me)
- "That" can signal emotional distance: "That was a long time ago." (distancing from a past event)
- People sometimes switch from "this" to "that" when they want to distance themselves from a topic: "Yeah, that... that was a bad idea" (pushing the idea away).

**Introducing vs. referring back:**
- "I have this great idea..." -- "This" introduces something new, pulling it toward the listener.
- "Remember that trip we took?" -- "That" refers back to something already known, located in shared past experience.

### 2.2 "Here" vs. "There" -- Especially in Digital Communication

**In face-to-face interaction:**
- "Here" = where the speaker is. "Come here" = move toward me.
- "There" = somewhere the speaker is not. "Go there" = move to that other place.

**In text messages (the breakdown begins):**
- "I'm here." -- Where is "here"? The sender knows; the receiver may not. "Here" is the sender's physical location at the time of sending, which the receiver must infer or already know. If the context is clear (they agreed to meet at a restaurant), "I'm here" = "I'm at the restaurant." Without shared context, it communicates almost nothing.
- "Are you there?" -- In a text, this may mean "Are you at the location we discussed?" OR "Are you available / reading this message?" The spatial term has been repurposed for communicative availability.

**In email:**
- "Please find the document attached here." -- "Here" means "in this email." The spatial term has been abstracted to mean "in this digital artifact."
- "Click here for more information." -- "Here" is a hyperlink location on a screen. This is a spatial metaphor applied to a visual interface. For screen reader users who navigate by hearing link text read aloud, "click here" provides no useful information about what "here" refers to -- the spatial reference requires visual context that is absent.

**In video calls:**
- "I'm here" can mean the physical room the speaker is in OR the virtual meeting space.
- "Can everyone here see my screen?" -- "Here" = in this virtual meeting.
- "The weather here is terrible." -- "Here" = the speaker's physical location.
- The deictic center splits between physical and virtual space, and speakers switch between them constantly, often without explicit markers.

### 2.3 "Come" vs. "Go" -- Speaker-Centered Movement

English motion verbs "come" and "go" are inherently deictic:
- **"Come"** = movement toward the deictic center (usually the speaker)
- **"Go"** = movement away from the deictic center

**Straightforward cases:**
- "Come to my office." -- Movement toward the speaker.
- "Go to the conference room." -- Movement away from the speaker.
- "The bus is coming." -- The bus is approaching where the speaker is.
- "The bus has gone." -- The bus has departed from where the speaker is.

**The empathic deictic shift (where it gets interesting):**

English has a distinctive feature called "sympathetic deixis" or "deictic projection," where the speaker shifts the deictic center to the listener's location:
- "I'll come to you." -- The speaker is moving away from their own location, but says "come" (toward) because they have shifted the deictic center to the listener's position. They are imagining the movement from the listener's perspective.
- "I'm coming home tomorrow." (said on the phone to someone at home) -- The speaker shifts the center to "home" where the listener is.
- "I'll bring a bottle to John's party." -- The deictic center shifts to the party location. If the speaker said "take" instead of "bring," it would emphasize movement away from the current location rather than arrival at the destination.

**Cross-linguistic note:** This flexibility is language-specific. Many languages require the literal direction (equivalent of "I'll go to you"), making the English sympathetic shift a potential source of confusion for non-native speakers. The deictic projection signals empathy or alignment -- the speaker linguistically positions themselves at the listener's location, which carries a subtle social warmth.

## 3. TEMPORAL DEIXIS IN PRACTICE

Temporal deixis anchors language to the time of speaking. The deictic center for time is "now" -- the moment the speaker produces the utterance. Everything else is measured relative to this point.

### 3.1 The Asynchronous Communication Problem

In face-to-face conversation, temporal deixis works seamlessly because the speaker and listener share the same "now." In asynchronous communication (email, voicemail, text messages, letters), they do not -- and the system breaks.

**Email written Monday, read Wednesday:**
- "I'll send the report tomorrow." -- "Tomorrow" = Tuesday (the day after the email was written). But a reader on Wednesday might momentarily interpret "tomorrow" as Thursday. The deictic "tomorrow" is anchored to the time of writing, not reading.

**Voicemail left at 2pm, heard at 6pm:**
- "Call me back today." -- "Today" is the day of recording. If heard the same day, no problem. If heard the next morning? "Today" now refers to yesterday.

**Text message sent 10am, read 12:30am the next day:**
- "See you tonight." -- "Tonight" = the evening of the day the message was sent. If read after midnight, it refers to the previous evening -- which has already passed.

**The anchoring principle:** In written/recorded communication, temporal deictic expressions are anchored to the time of production, not reception. This is a convention, not a logical necessity -- and it is one that can cause genuine confusion.

**Strategy for avoiding confusion:** Use absolute time references in asynchronous communication. Instead of "I'll send it tomorrow," write "I'll send it by Tuesday, March 15th." Instead of "Call me today," say "Call me before 5pm on Wednesday."

### 3.2 "Now" in Different Contexts

"Now" is the canonical deictic time anchor, but it has developed multiple functions beyond pure temporal reference:

**Temporal "now" (the literal use):**
- "I'm leaving now." -- At this very moment.
- "She's sleeping now." -- At the present time.
- "The store is closed now." -- Currently, at this time.

**"Now" as a change marker:**
- "The meeting is now at 3." -- Does not mean "the meeting is currently happening at 3." It means "the meeting has been changed to 3" -- "now" marks a shift from previous information to new information. The temporal reference is not "this moment" but "as of this update."
- "Now accepting applications." -- Not temporal in the sense of "at this second" but rather "starting from this point forward, in contrast to previously not accepting them."

**"Now" as a discourse marker (not temporal at all):**
- "Now, where were we?" -- A topic-return marker. The speaker is not commenting on time; they are navigating back to a prior discussion thread.
- "Now, I want to be clear about something." -- A topic-shift marker. The speaker is redirecting attention, not making a temporal claim.
- "Now then, let's get started." -- Signals a transition, not a time reference.
- Research on "now" as a discourse marker shows it increasingly serves textual and affective functions like shifting topics, marking contrasts, and indicating stance. Its use as a discourse marker predominantly occurs in turn-initial or utterance-initial positions.

**Common misunderstanding pattern:** The sentence "Now, the company is doing well" is ambiguous: it could mean "at the present time, the company is doing well" (temporal) or "moving on to the next point: the company is doing well" (discourse marker). Only context disambiguates.

### 3.3 Relative Temporal Expressions

**"Next Friday" vs. "This Friday" -- perhaps the single most confusing deictic expression in English:**

Research has confirmed this is a genuine source of systematic miscommunication. The ambiguity depends on when in the week you are speaking:
- **Said on Monday:** "This Friday" = the Friday coming up this week (4 days away). "Next Friday" = Friday of next week (11 days away). This is the most common interpretation.
- **Said on Thursday:** "This Friday" = tomorrow. "Next Friday" = ? This is where it breaks down. Some speakers mean the very next Friday (tomorrow), while others mean the Friday after that.
- **Said on Saturday:** "Next Friday" = the Friday coming up in the next few days (6 days away)? Or the one after that (13 days away)? No consensus exists.

Regional variation compounds this: In British English, "next Friday" more often means the very next Friday to come, regardless of week boundaries. In American English, it typically means the Friday of the following week. Neither usage is "wrong" -- they are competing conventions.

**"Last week" / "this week" / "next week":**
The boundaries depend on whether the speaker conceptualizes weeks as starting Monday or Sunday, and whether "this week" includes only remaining days or the entire seven-day period containing the present moment.

**"In a minute" / "in a second" / "just a moment":**
These are rarely literal. Research on these expressions shows they function primarily as pragmatic markers rather than temporal measurements:
- "I'll be there in a minute" -- Could mean anywhere from 30 seconds to 15 minutes. Functions as "soon, but not this instant."
- "Just a second" -- Requests patience; signals brevity but does not promise literal brevity.
- "Give me a minute" -- Requests a short but unspecified delay.
- The semantic flexibility allows speakers to negotiate variable waiting periods without committing to precise durations, depending on contextual demands and social relationships.

**"Soon," "shortly," "in a bit," "later":**
All relative to the speaker's sense of scale. "Soon" after a doctor says "the results will be ready soon" could mean days; "soon" when a friend says "I'll be there soon" means minutes.

**Common misunderstanding pattern:** Autistic individuals who process temporal language more precisely may be frustrated or confused by the non-literal nature of these expressions. If someone says "I'll be there in a minute" and arrives in eight minutes, this is pragmatically normal but semantically wrong. The gap between what was said and what happened is a feature of how neurotypical speakers use temporal expressions, not a sign of deception.

## 4. SOCIAL DEIXIS IN PRACTICE

Social deixis refers to how language encodes information about social relationships -- relative status, power, intimacy, and group membership.

### 4.1 Address Forms as Social Pointers

The way someone is addressed is deictic in the sense that it points to the social relationship between speaker and addressee. This is not fixed -- it shifts as relationships evolve.

**The formality-to-familiarity progression:**
- "Dr. Williams" → "Professor Williams" → "David" → "Dave" -- This progression typically tracks the development of a relationship from formal/professional to familiar/intimate. Skipping stages (calling a new acquaintance "Dave" when they introduced themselves as "Dr. Williams") signals either deliberate informality, disrespect, or social miscalibration.

**How the wrong form feels wrong:**
- Being called by your first name by a stranger who should use your title can feel like a boundary violation -- they are claiming familiarity they have not earned.
- Being called "sir" or "ma'am" by a close friend can feel like they are creating deliberate distance -- it signals that something in the relationship has changed or that they are being ironic.
- Being called by your full name ("Jonathan!") by a parent who normally uses a nickname ("Jon") can signal that you are in trouble -- the shift in address form carries pragmatic meaning.

**Context-dependent shifting:**
- In a meeting, a boss might be "Ms. Chen." At a company social event, she might be "Linda." The shift in address form tracks the shift in social context.
- Using someone's title when angry ("That's quite enough, *Dr.* Harrison") versus their nickname when close ("Come on, Tom") -- the address form itself becomes a tool for expressing emotional stance.

### 4.2 Power-Encoding Deixis

**Titles and honorifics as status markers:**
- "Sir" / "Ma'am" -- Point to a perceived power or respect differential. Used upward (toward those of higher status) or by service workers toward customers.
- Military ranks, academic titles, professional titles (Judge, Professor, Officer) -- All encode hierarchical relationships. Using or omitting them is itself a social act.

**The asymmetry of address:**
A professor who calls students by first name but is addressed as "Professor Smith" is enacting a power asymmetry through deixis. The forms of address literally point to different positions in a social hierarchy. In more egalitarian settings, mutual first-name use signals symmetry.

**English compared to other languages:**
English lacks the grammaticalized T/V distinction found in many European languages (French *tu/vous*, German *du/Sie*, Spanish *tú/usted*). English lost its informal second person "thou" centuries ago, collapsing the distinction into a single "you." This means English speakers must encode social distance through other deictic means: address forms, tone, register, and lexical choice rather than pronoun selection.

### 4.3 Inclusive/Exclusive Social Deixis

**In-group language as inclusion:**
- Using slang, jargon, or in-group vocabulary signals membership: "That PR needs a rebase before we can merge" (software team); "He's been red-carded" (sports fans).
- Shared references function as social deixis: "Remember that time at the lake?" creates an in-group of people who share the memory and excludes those who do not.

**Formal language as exclusion:**
- Switching to unusually formal register can exclude: a group of friends where one suddenly begins speaking very formally may be signaling withdrawal, displeasure, or creating ironic distance.
- Institutional language ("Per our previous correspondence...") creates a professional barrier that excludes casual interaction.

## 5. DISCOURSE DEIXIS IN PRACTICE

Discourse deixis refers to expressions that point to parts of the ongoing discourse itself -- forward to what is about to be said, or backward to what has already been said.

### 5.1 How "This" and "That" Organize Arguments

In discourse, "this" and "that" stop pointing to physical objects and start pointing to ideas, propositions, and segments of speech:

**"This" for upcoming or currently-relevant information:**
- "This is important: always check the deadline." -- "This" points forward to what follows.
- "This is what I mean." -- "This" points to the current topic or to what the speaker is about to demonstrate.
- "I want to say this clearly:" -- Cataphoric; "this" previews what comes next.

**"That" for just-stated or receding information:**
- "That's exactly right." -- "That" points back to what the other person just said.
- "That was a good point." -- "That" refers to a previous utterance.
- "That said..." -- "That" encapsulates the preceding argument, then the speaker pivots.

**Organizing complex arguments:**
- "With this in mind, let's consider the next question." -- "This" = the point just established. The word bridges backward-looking reference with forward momentum.
- "Given all of that, what should we do?" -- "That" = everything previously discussed, packaged as a single reference.

### 5.2 Anaphora and Cataphora

These are the two directions of discourse reference:

**Anaphora (backward reference -- the common pattern):**
- "John arrived late. **He** was stuck in traffic." -- "He" refers back to "John." The pronoun follows its referent.
- "The cake was delicious. I want **another one**." -- "One" refers back to "cake."

**Cataphora (forward reference -- less common, more marked):**
- "When **he** arrived, John was tired." -- "He" comes before "John" in the sentence but refers to John. This is cataphoric.
- "If **she** wants to, Maria can join us." -- "She" precedes "Maria."
- "**This** is what happened: the power went out." -- "This" precedes the explanation.
- Cataphora creates a brief moment of suspended reference -- the listener encounters the pronoun before knowing what it refers to, creating a small forward tension. This is a stylistic device, but it can also be a processing challenge.

### 5.3 Pronoun Reference Tracking (And Where It Breaks Down)

In extended discourse, tracking who pronouns refer to is called **coreference resolution**. It usually works automatically, but it fails in predictable ways:

**Classic ambiguity examples:**
- "John told Mike that **he** should leave." -- Who should leave, John or Mike? Grammatically, "he" could refer to either. Neurotypical listeners often resolve this through context (e.g., if the conversation has been about Mike's bad behavior, "he" likely refers to Mike), but the sentence itself is genuinely ambiguous.
- "The manager told the employee that **she** needed more training." -- Who needs training? Pragmatic default is often that "she" refers to "the employee" (because training is more often directed downward in a hierarchy), but this is a contextual inference, not a grammatical rule.
- "Alice told Jane that **she** would win the prize." -- Same pattern. "She" could be Alice or Jane.

**Why these ambiguities persist in natural speech:**
Speakers often produce ambiguous pronouns because they have a clear referent in their own mind and do not realize the listener might parse it differently. The speaker's intention is determinate; the sentence is not.

**When ambiguity is genuinely unresolvable:**
- "She took her book." -- If "she" and "her" refer to the same person, this means the subject retrieved her own book. If they refer to different people, one person took another's book. Only context (and sometimes intonation/stress) disambiguates.

**Common misunderstanding pattern for autistic individuals:** Research suggests that autistic individuals may be more aware of pronoun ambiguity than neurotypical speakers -- that is, they may notice the ambiguity that others resolve automatically through contextual inference. This is not a deficit; it is a difference in processing strategy. The autistic approach (noticing that "he" could refer to either John or Mike) is logically correct. The neurotypical approach (automatically selecting the most contextually likely referent without noticing the ambiguity) is pragmatically efficient but sometimes wrong.

**Strategy:** When a pronoun is genuinely ambiguous, asking "When you say 'he,' do you mean John or Mike?" is clearer and more efficient than guessing. This is good communication practice for everyone, not an accommodation.

## 6. DEICTIC FAILURE -- Common Misunderstandings Caused by Deixis

Deictic failure occurs when a deictic expression fails to successfully point to its intended referent, leaving the listener unable to resolve who, where, when, or what is being referenced.

### 6.1 Classic Deictic Failures

**Person failure:**
- "She told me to bring it." -- Who told? Bring what? Two deictic failures in one sentence -- both the person reference and the object reference are unresolvable without context.
- "They changed the rules." -- Who changed them? Which rules? The institutional "they" may be clear to the speaker but opaque to the listener.

**Spatial failure:**
- "I'll meet you there." -- Where is "there"? If a meeting location was previously discussed, "there" anchors to it. If not, it points to nothing.
- "Can you hand me that?" (without gesture or clear referent) -- In face-to-face conversation, "that" usually accompanies a point, a gaze direction, or is otherwise anchored by the physical environment. Without these cues, it fails.
- "Put it over there." -- "There" requires a gesture or shared spatial orientation.

**Temporal failure:**
- "Let's do it later." -- How much later? This afternoon? This week? Next month? "Later" is maximally vague temporal deixis.
- "I sent that email recently." -- "Recently" could mean yesterday or three weeks ago, depending on the speaker's timescale.

**Multi-category failure (deictic overload):**
- "We should do this sometime." -- Person failure: who is "we"? Discourse failure: what is "this"? Temporal failure: when is "sometime"? Research on vague invitations (Isaacs & Clark, 1990) found that logistics were vague in 69% of insincere invitations but only 8% of genuine ones. When everything is deictic, nothing is specific -- and the expression often functions as a polite closing ritual rather than an actual plan.

### 6.2 Deictic Failure in Digital Contexts

**"Click here":**
"Here" is spatial deixis applied to a visual interface. For sighted users navigating with a mouse, "here" is the hyperlink on the screen. For screen reader users, "click here" is read aloud as link text without any visual context -- "here" points to nothing. Web accessibility guidelines specifically recommend against "click here" links because the spatial deixis fails when the visual context is unavailable.

**"I'm here" in a text message:**
Requires the receiver to know where the sender is. If they agreed to meet at the library, this works. If not, the spatial anchor is missing.

**"See below" in an email:**
"Below" is spatial deixis applied to the visual layout of a document. If the email is reformatted, forwarded, or read aloud, "below" may not correspond to anything the reader can identify.

### 6.3 How Autistic Individuals Navigate Deictic Challenges

Research has identified several patterns specific to autistic processing of deictic language:

**Pronoun reversal in early development:**
The best-known deictic challenge associated with autism is pronoun reversal -- using "you" to mean oneself or "I" to mean the listener. This occurs because personal pronouns require continuous remapping of their referent depending on who is speaking. Each time the speaker role changes, "I" and "you" swap referents. Research (Springer: "Deixis and personhood") proposes that this is related to the "fluidity-to-inelasticity" hypothesis -- the constant shifting of deictic reference requires a kind of cognitive flexibility that may be more effortful for some autistic individuals.

**Greater precision as a strength:**
Autistic individuals often produce more explicit reference than neurotypical speakers -- using names instead of pronouns, giving specific times instead of vague temporal expressions, providing exact locations instead of "there." This is frequently more communicatively effective, not less. The neurotypical norm of relying heavily on deictic inference creates ambiguity that explicit reference eliminates.

**The implicit resolution gap:**
Neurotypical listeners automatically resolve ambiguous deictic expressions using a combination of:
- Shared physical context (seeing what "that" points to)
- Shared knowledge (knowing where "there" means)
- Social inference (interpreting "we need to talk" as a negative)
- Pragmatic conventions (knowing "we should do this sometime" is often a polite brush-off, not a genuine plan)

Autistic individuals may process each of these channels with different efficiency. The physical context may be processed differently (if joint attention patterns differ). The shared knowledge may be present but not automatically activated. The social inference may require explicit reasoning rather than automatic processing. The pragmatic conventions may need to be learned as explicit rules rather than absorbed implicitly.

**Key distinction from research:** The challenge is not a failure of understanding but a difference in processing route. Research distinguishes between "linguistic pragmatics" (inferences from language structure) and "social pragmatics" (inferences from contextual and intentional reasoning). Studies consistently find that autistic individuals show relative strengths in linguistic pragmatic reasoning while exhibiting greater difficulty with social pragmatic inference. Deictic resolution often requires both.

### 6.4 Practical Strategies for Deictic Clarity

**For the autistic individual navigating deictic language:**
1. **Ask for clarification without apology.** "Which 'she' do you mean?" and "Where exactly is 'there'?" are good questions that improve communication for everyone.
2. **Request specific times over relative ones.** "Could you give me a specific date instead of 'soon'?" is a reasonable request.
3. **When "we" is ambiguous, name the participants.** "So when you say 'we,' you mean you, me, and Sarah?"
4. **Treat vague invitations as closings, not plans.** "We should get together sometime" usually = "goodbye." A genuine invitation includes specifics. (Research found only 8% of sincere invitations had vague logistics.)
5. **In asynchronous communication, mentally anchor temporal expressions to the time of writing**, not the time of reading.

**For communicating clearly with autistic individuals (or anyone):**
1. **Use names instead of pronouns** when multiple referents are in play.
2. **Use specific times and dates** instead of "tomorrow," "soon," "later."
3. **Use specific locations** instead of "there," "here," "that place."
4. **Make "we" explicit:** "You and I should..." or "The team needs to..."
5. **When giving deictic instructions in digital contexts**, provide redundant reference: "See the attached document (Project_Budget_v2.pdf)" rather than just "See attached."

## 7. THE DEICTIC CENTER: UNDERSTANDING THE HIDDEN ANCHOR

All deictic expressions point outward from an invisible center. Understanding this center -- and how it shifts -- is the key to parsing deictic language successfully.

### 7.1 Default Deictic Center

In ordinary face-to-face conversation:
- **I/me** = the person currently speaking
- **You** = the person being addressed
- **Here** = the speaker's location
- **Now** = the moment of speaking
- **This** = near the speaker
- **That** = far from the speaker
- **Come** = movement toward the speaker
- **Go** = movement away from the speaker

### 7.2 Deictic Center Shifts

The center moves in predictable (but not always obvious) ways:

**Turn-taking:** When your conversational partner starts speaking, the center shifts to them. Their "I" is now them, their "here" is their location, etc.

**Narrative:** "I went to the store. The clerk said, 'Can I help you?'" -- Inside the quoted speech, "I" = the clerk, "you" = the narrator. The center has jumped to the quoted speaker's perspective.

**Empathic projection:** "I'll come to you" -- The speaker shifts their deictic center to the listener's location, using "come" (toward center) rather than "go" (away from center).

**Temporal projection:** "By next year, we'll have finished." -- The speaker projects the deictic center to a future time, from which "have finished" is interpreted.

**Digital context splitting:** On a video call: "Here, let me show you" (points at something on their shared screen -- "here" = the virtual space) then "It's really cold here today" ("here" = the speaker's physical location). The center toggles between physical and virtual space within adjacent sentences.

### 7.3 Why Deictic Centers Matter for Autistic Processing

The constant, often unmarked shifting of deictic centers is one of the most cognitively demanding aspects of natural language. Each shift requires:
1. Recognizing that a shift has occurred
2. Identifying the new center
3. Reinterpreting all deictic expressions relative to the new center

For neurotypical speakers, this happens automatically and below conscious awareness. Research (PMC3155703) confirms that for autistic individuals, this process may require more explicit cognitive effort -- it is not that it cannot be done, but that it demands conscious attention that neurotypical speakers do not need to allocate.

**The practical implication:** When processing feels overwhelmed, the most productive strategy is to identify the deictic center first. Ask: Who is speaking? Where are they? When are they speaking? Once the center is established, all the deictic expressions radiating from it become interpretable.

## 8. CONNECTIONS TO OTHER PRAGMATIC CATEGORIES

### 8.1 Deixis and Implicature (Category 01)
"We need to talk" is a deictic expression ("we") combined with a conversational implicature (the negative meaning is implied, not stated). Many deictic failures are compounded by implicature -- "We should do this sometime" fails deictically (who? what? when?) and pragmatically (the surface meaning is an invitation; the implicature is often a closing ritual).

### 8.2 Deixis and Politeness (Category 03)
Social deixis is directly tied to politeness systems. The "patronizing we" is a deixis-politeness interaction. Address form choices are simultaneously deictic (pointing to a relationship) and face-managing (protecting or threatening face).

### 8.3 Deixis and Register (Category 04)
Register shifts often manifest through deictic changes: moving from "you" to "one," from first names to titles, from "here" to "at the aforementioned location." The deictic system and the register system are intertwined.

### 8.4 Deixis and Social/Relational Language (Category 07)
Address forms (Section 4.1 above) directly connect to Category 07's treatment of social relationships encoded in language. The formality-to-familiarity progression is both deictic (pointing to a relationship state) and socially performative (enacting that relationship).
