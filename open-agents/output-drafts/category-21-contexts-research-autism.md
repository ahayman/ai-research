## 1. THE EMPLOYMENT CONTEXT

### 1.1 Employment Statistics: The Scale of the Problem

The unemployment and underemployment of autistic adults remains one of the most striking disparities in the disability landscape. The frequently cited figure is that **approximately 85% of autistic adults are unemployed** in the United States (as of 2023), though this number requires nuance. Estimates actually range from 39% to 85% depending on the study methodology, sample, and how "employment" is defined (full-time, part-time, competitive, supported). In Europe, studies from 2014 show between 76% and 90% unemployment among autistic adults. In the United Kingdom, roughly 71% of autistic adults lack employment opportunities.

Even among those who are employed, many are **underemployed**---working in positions that do not match their qualifications, working fewer hours than desired, or earning significantly less than neurotypical peers with comparable education and skills.

### 1.2 Communication Factors That Drive Workplace Friction

Research consistently identifies several communication-related factors that create friction in the workplace for autistic employees:

**Social Communication Mismatches:**
- Written communication from autistic employees can be **misconstrued as impolite** due to directness, lack of softening language, or absence of social pleasantries that neurotypical colleagues expect
- Many autistic employees face challenges with **phone calls**, leading to avoidance behaviors that colleagues perceive as "weird" or "difficult"
- **Small talk**---the social lubricant of most workplaces---takes considerable social energy, and many autistic people do not consider the effort worth the return. Small talk is "quick, light, and full of unspoken rules" that autistic individuals must consciously decode rather than intuitively navigate
- **Nonverbal communication mismatches**: differences in eye contact, facial expression, gesture, and prosody can lead neurotypical colleagues to misjudge autistic employees' engagement, competence, or friendliness

**Structural Communication Barriers:**
- Verbal-only instructions that are ambiguous, implicit, or context-dependent
- Unwritten social rules about workplace behavior (when to speak in meetings, how to "read the room," office politics)
- Open-plan offices that create sensory overload, making social communication even more difficult
- Expectations of multitasking during social interactions (e.g., making small talk while also performing a task)

**Sources:**

### 1.3 The Szechy et al. Studies on Double Empathy in the Workplace

Two landmark studies by Kathryn Szechy and colleagues have directly applied the **double empathy problem** to workplace contexts:

**Szechy, Turk, & O'Donnell (2024):**
This study examined competing explanations for autistic social difficulties in primarily non-autistic workplaces. 81 autistic and 173 non-autistic participants read a story about an autistic employee and were asked to interpret the employee's behavior.

Key findings:
- **Autistic participants were significantly more likely to accurately interpret the autistic employee's behavior** (50.7% accuracy) compared with non-autistic participants (31.2% accuracy)
- Being autistic and having more autism knowledge were **significant predictors** of accurate behavior interpretation
- Results directly support the double empathy problem: the communication breakdown is mutual, not one-sided

**Szechy & O'Donnell (2025):**
A follow-up study found that **higher autism knowledge** was associated with:
- More positive attitudes toward the autistic employee's **competency**
- More positive views of the employee's **suitability for their job**
- Greater **social acceptability** as a coworker
- This was especially true when participants themselves demonstrated poor theory of mind toward the autistic employee

**Implications for autistic adults:** These studies show that the problem is not your communication---it is the knowledge gap in your workplace. When colleagues understand autism, they interpret the same behaviors more accurately and more positively.

**Sources:**
- [PMC - Autism and Employment Challenges: The Double Empathy Problem](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11317796/)
- [SAGE Journals - Szechy & O'Donnell 2025](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1089/aut.2024.0261)

### 1.4 Accommodations That Help

Research identifies several categories of accommodations that significantly improve autistic employees' workplace experience:

**Communication Accommodations:**
- **Written instructions** instead of or in addition to verbal communication of tasks and responsibilities
- **Email-based communication** preferred over face-to-face for task-related exchanges; asynchronous communication tools reduce social stress by allowing time to reflect before responding
- **Agendas for meetings** and standardized communication approaches
- **Explicit expectations**: clear, written checklists and deadlines for projects
- **Reduced small talk demands**: structuring interactions around tasks rather than social bonding

**Environmental Accommodations:**
- Noise-reducing workspaces (cubicles, quiet rooms, noise-canceling headphones)
- Controlled lighting (dimmer options, reduced fluorescent lighting)
- Permission to wear sunglasses or headphones
- Flexible scheduling to avoid peak sensory-overload periods

**Process Accommodations:**
- Providing interview questions in advance
- Alternative interview formats (work samples, trial periods, take-home assessments) instead of traditional face-to-face interviews
- Written job descriptions with explicit expectations
- Regular, structured check-ins rather than reliance on informal feedback
- Clear organizational charts and reporting structures

**Legal Framework:** Under the **Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)**, autistic employees have legal protection and the right to request reasonable accommodations. Similar protections exist under the Equality Act 2010 in the UK and equivalent legislation in other countries.

### 1.5 When to Disclose Autism at Work: Costs and Benefits

The decision to disclose an autism diagnosis at work is one of the most consequential pragmatic decisions autistic adults face. Research reveals a genuine double bind:

**Benefits of Disclosure:**
- Access to **legal accommodations** (cannot request ADA accommodations without disclosure)
- Greater **acceptance and inclusion** from informed colleagues
- Reduced need to **mask**, which conserves energy and reduces burnout risk
- Colleagues may **reinterpret past misunderstandings** more charitably
- Can increase **autism awareness** in the workplace
- First impressions of autistic adults improve when others know about the diagnosis

**Costs and Risks of Disclosure:**
- Exposure to **stigma and discrimination** (conscious or unconscious)
- Risk of being subject to **inaccurate stereotypes** ("Rain Man" assumptions, assumptions of low competence)
- Potential **career ceiling effects** (being passed over for promotion, excluded from client-facing roles)
- Information **cannot be un-disclosed**: once shared, you cannot control how it spreads or how it is interpreted

**Research-Based Decision Factors:**
- How **safe** the workplace culture is (is there a track record of supporting disabled employees?)
- The **potential benefit** of disclosure (will it give access to needed accommodations?)
- Whether the autistic individual has **support** in making the decision
- The 2024 **"DISCLOSURE" tool** pilot study (Tomas et al.) showed that a structured decision-aid helped autistic young adults make better-informed disclosure decisions, improving decision-making knowledge, skills, and confidence

**Practical Approach:**
- You do not have to disclose your specific diagnosis; you can frame accommodation requests around functional needs ("I work best with written instructions" rather than "I'm autistic and need written instructions")
- Disclosure can be **graduated**: disclosing to HR for accommodations but not to all colleagues, or disclosing to a trusted supervisor first
- Timing matters: disclosing during a crisis or conflict is less effective than disclosing proactively when things are going well

**Sources:**
- [Liebertpub - "People Might Understand Me Better": Diagnostic Disclosure Experiences](https://www.liebertpub.com/doi/10.1089/aut.2020.0063)
- [PMC - To tell or not to tell: disclosure of autism in the workplace](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10197981/)
- [SAGE Journals - Piloting a Workplace Autism Disclosure Decision-Aid Tool](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1089/aut.2023.0054)

### 1.6 How to Request Accommodations Effectively

Research and advocacy organizations suggest a structured approach:

1. **Know your rights**: Familiarize yourself with ADA protections (or equivalent in your country) before making a request
2. **Document your needs**: Write down specific accommodations you need and why (functional language, not diagnostic language)
3. **Use the interactive process**: Under the ADA, accommodations are determined collaboratively---you and your employer work together to find reasonable solutions
4. **Be specific and solution-oriented**: Instead of "I struggle with meetings," try "I would benefit from receiving the meeting agenda 24 hours in advance and being able to submit comments in writing afterward"
5. **Follow up in writing**: After any verbal conversation about accommodations, send a confirming email summarizing what was discussed and agreed
6. **Involve HR**: Going through formal channels creates a paper trail that protects you

**Sources:**
- [NeuroSpark Health - How to Request Workplace Accommodations for Autistic Adults](https://neurosparkhealth.com/accommodations/accommodations-for-autistic-adults)
- [Autism.org - Autism Accommodations and Self Advocacy at Work](https://autism.org/autism-workplace-accomodations/)

### 1.7 Industries and Roles Where Autistic Communication Is an Asset

Research identifies several domains where autistic cognitive and communication styles are not merely tolerated but actively valued:

**Technology and Software:**
- Software testing/QA (exceptional detail orientation, pattern recognition, systematic approach)
- Data analytics (finding patterns, comfort with structured data)
- Cybersecurity (vigilance, systematic thinking, sustained focus)
- Web development (logic combined with creativity, remote-work friendly)
- J.P. Morgan Chase discovered that their 300+ autistic employees were **48% faster and 92% more productive** than neurotypical peers in comparable roles

**Research and Science:**
- Academic research (deep specialization, commitment to accuracy, systematic methodology)
- Laboratory work (attention to protocol, precision, comfort with repetition)

**Detail-Oriented Professions:**
- Accounting and auditing (rule-based, pattern-focused)
- Legal research and document review (accuracy, thoroughness)
- Technical writing (precision, structured communication valued over social style)
- Library science and archival work (systematic organization)

**Key Insight:** The common thread is that these roles reward **precision, depth, systematicity, and honest communication** over social fluency, networking, and reading between the lines.

**The Interview Barrier:** Even for roles where autistic individuals excel, the traditional job interview remains a major barrier. Research describes the job interview as "probably the most difficult part of the job search for people with autism." Organizations like Specialisterne use alternative assessment methods (homework assignments, work samples, skill demonstrations) that better predict actual job performance.

**Sources:**

## 2. THE HEALTHCARE CONTEXT

### 2.1 Autistic Adults' Experiences with Healthcare

Research paints a concerning picture of autistic adults' healthcare experiences. A cross-sectional study found that the highest-rated barriers were:
- **Deciding if symptoms warrant a GP visit** (72% of autistic respondents)
- **Difficulty making appointments by telephone** (62%)
- **Not feeling understood** by healthcare providers (56%)
- **Difficulty communicating with their doctor** (53%)
- **The waiting room environment** (51%)

These barriers lead to serious adverse outcomes including **untreated physical and mental health conditions**, missed specialist referrals, delayed treatment requiring more extensive intervention or surgery, and in some cases, untreated **potentially life-threatening conditions**.

**Sources:**
- [PMC - Barriers to healthcare and self-reported adverse outcomes](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8883251/)
- [The Transmitter - Health-care barriers prevent many autistic people from seeking treatment](https://www.thetransmitter.org/spectrum/health-care-barriers-prevent-many-autistic-people-from-seeking-medical-treatment/)

### 2.2 The Triple Empathy Problem in Healthcare

Shaw, Doherty, and colleagues (2023/2024) expanded the double empathy problem into a **"triple empathy problem"** specific to healthcare settings. The three layers are:

1. **Doctor-patient gap**: Doctors have medical knowledge and perspective that patients lack, making it inherently difficult for doctors to see patients' perspectives
2. **Neurotype gap**: Autistic and non-autistic people struggle to understand each other's perspectives (the double empathy problem)
3. **Combined effect**: Autistic patients face a compounded barrier---they must bridge both the medical knowledge gap AND the neurotype communication gap simultaneously

Research themes from this study included:
- **Early barriers** (difficulty accessing appointments, navigating phone systems)
- **Communication mismatch** (literal vs. figurative language, processing speed differences)
- **Doubt---in oneself and from doctors** (autistic patients second-guessing their own symptoms; doctors doubting or dismissing autistic patients' reports)
- **Helplessness and fear** (feeling unable to advocate for oneself in medical settings)
- **Healthcare avoidance** (cumulative negative experiences leading to avoiding medical care entirely)

**Sources:**
- [PMC - Barriers to healthcare and a 'triple empathy problem'](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11191657/)
- [SAGE Journals - Shaw et al. 2024](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/13623613231205629)

### 2.3 Communication Barriers in Medical Settings

Specific communication barriers identified in research include:

**Language and Processing:**
- Healthcare providers often use **abstract, open-ended questions** ("How are you feeling?") that are difficult for autistic patients to parse
- **Idioms and metaphors** in medical communication can cause confusion
- Autistic patients may need **more time to process questions** and formulate answers, but appointments are typically time-pressured
- Questions like "On a scale of 1-10, how bad is your pain?" require interoceptive awareness that may be impaired

**Sensory Challenges:**
- Bright fluorescent lighting in examination rooms
- Unexpected physical touch during examinations
- Waiting room noise and unpredictability
- Strong smells (antiseptic, other patients' fragrances)

**Social Expectations:**
- Pressure to make eye contact, which may be read as engagement or credibility
- Expectation to engage in small talk with nurses, reception staff
- Assumption that patients will proactively volunteer information rather than responding only to direct questions
- Time pressure that does not accommodate different processing speeds

**Sources:**
- [PMC - Overcoming barriers to autistic health care](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9172244/)
- [Frontiers - Barriers and facilitators to primary healthcare encounters](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/medicine/articles/10.3389/fmed.2025.1481953/full)

### 2.4 The Alexithymia Challenge

**Alexithymia**---literally "no words for emotions"---is a condition characterized by difficulty identifying and describing one's own emotions. It is highly prevalent among autistic adults:
- Approximately **50% of autistic people** experience clinically significant alexithymia (compared to roughly 10% of the general population)
- Specifically: **47.3% of autistic females** and **21.0% of autistic males** meet the clinical cutoff
- The condition has three components (measured by the TAS-20 scale): difficulties in **identifying** feelings, difficulties in **describing** feelings, and **externally oriented thinking**

**Why This Matters for Healthcare:**
Alexithymia is closely linked to **interoception**---the ability to sense and interpret internal body signals (hunger, pain, heart rate, nausea, fatigue). When interoceptive awareness is impaired, an autistic patient may:
- Not recognize they are in pain until it becomes severe
- Struggle to localize where pain is occurring
- Be unable to describe the quality of a sensation (sharp vs. dull, burning vs. aching)
- Not connect emotional states to physical symptoms (not recognizing that their stomach pain is anxiety)
- Give answers to "How do you feel?" that seem evasive or contradictory when they are genuinely uncertain

**Crucially,** alexithymia is not the same as not having emotions---it is the inability to identify and articulate them. Autistic patients with alexithymia may have strong emotional and physical experiences but lack the internal vocabulary to report them in ways clinicians expect.

### 2.5 How Masking Interferes with Accurate Symptom Reporting

The healthcare context creates a particularly dangerous interaction with masking:
- Autistic patients who **mask** in medical settings may appear more "fine" than they are, leading clinicians to underestimate symptom severity
- Masking may suppress visible distress cues (flat affect, not crying, not grimacing) that clinicians rely on for assessment
- The effort of masking during appointments can make it **harder to access and report internal states** accurately---cognitive resources devoted to appearing "normal" are unavailable for introspection
- Autistic patients may **agree with leading questions** or give expected answers rather than accurate ones, particularly under time pressure
- After masking through a difficult appointment, patients may be **too exhausted to follow up** on instructions or fill prescriptions

**Sources:**
- [PMC - Barriers to healthcare and a 'triple empathy problem'](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11191657/)

### 2.6 Preparing for Medical Appointments: Evidence-Based Strategies

Research and advocacy organizations recommend specific preparation strategies:

**Before the Appointment:**
- **Write down symptoms** in advance, including when they started, how often they occur, what makes them better or worse
- **Prepare a list of questions** ranked by priority (most important first, in case time runs out)
- **Use the Autism Healthcare Accommodations Tool (AHAT)** developed by the AASPIRE collaborative to identify and communicate accommodation needs
- **Request the first or last appointment** of the day to minimize waiting room time
- **Ask about alternative booking methods**: many practices now offer online or text-based appointment booking
- **Email the reason for your visit in advance** if the practice allows it

**During the Appointment:**
- **Bring your written notes** and hand them to the clinician if speaking is difficult
- **Ask the clinician to use direct, specific questions** rather than open-ended ones ("Does the pain get worse when you eat?" rather than "Tell me about the pain")
- **Request that the clinician write down instructions** or send a follow-up summary email
- **Bring a support person** if helpful (someone who can advocate or take notes)
- **State your communication needs** at the outset: "I process things better when you speak slowly and use specific questions"

**After the Appointment:**
- **Review written instructions** in a calm environment
- **Follow up by email** if you have questions you forgot to ask
- **Set reminders** for follow-up appointments, prescriptions, or referrals

**Sources:**
- [AASPIRE Healthcare Toolkit](https://researchautism.org/healthcaretoolkit/)
- [PMC - "Respect the way I need to communicate with you"](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4841263/)
- [SAGE Journals - Adapting communication with autistic service users](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/27546330241266723)

### 2.7 Healthcare Provider Training

Research on healthcare provider training reveals both the need and the emerging solutions:

**The Problem:**
- Many providers feel **unprepared** to care for autistic patients, citing inadequate training and systemic constraints
- Providers may **assume incompetence** based on communication differences
- Medical education typically includes **minimal autism content**, and what exists tends to focus on children rather than adults

**Emerging Solutions:**
- The **ECHO Autism** training model connects autism experts with healthcare providers virtually for ongoing mentorship
- **Behavioral Skills Training (BST)** approaches have shown success: a recent study (2024) using video modules, guided notes, and the AHAT tool improved providers' communication skills, with improvements generalizing to new patients and maintaining over time
- **Co-produced adaptations** (Norris, Lei, & Maras, 2024) developed communication guidelines for medical services through collaboration between researchers, autistic adults, and healthcare providers
- Training that includes **contact with autistic adults** (not just lectures) is more effective at changing provider behavior

**What Providers Should Do:**
- Assume competence
- Use direct, literal language
- Avoid open-ended or vague questions
- Offer accommodations proactively (dim lights, reduce noise, provide written summaries)
- Allow extra time for processing
- Ask about communication preferences at the start of appointments

**Sources:**
- [Springer - Using an Instructional Package to Support Adults with Autism](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10803-024-06635-5)
- [UVA School of Education - Adults with Autism Don't Get Adequate Healthcare](https://education.virginia.edu/news-stories/adults-autism-dont-get-adequate-healthcare-training-providers-can-help)

## 3. THE RELATIONSHIP CONTEXT

### 3.1 Autistic Adults' Experiences with Friendship

Research consistently shows that autistic adults **desire friendships and social connections** at rates comparable to neurotypical adults, but face structural barriers to forming and maintaining them:

- One study found that **60% of autistic young adults wished they had more friends**, compared to only 18% of non-autistic participants
- A comprehensive scoping review of 22 empirical studies (2025) confirmed that autistic adults **actively desire and seek friendships**
- Autistic adults prioritize **understanding, caring, and acceptance** in friendships, but experience "social disorientation"---difficulty navigating the unwritten norms of neurotypical friendship culture
- A key concern: the **exhaustion of maintaining friendships** may be unsustainable, particularly when friendship maintenance requires ongoing social performance

**What Research Tells Us About Autistic Friendships:**
- Autistic friendships often form around **shared interests** (deep, passionate engagement with a topic as the basis for connection)
- **Quality over quantity**: autistic adults may prefer fewer, deeper friendships over large social networks
- **Online friendships** can be equally valid and meaningful, and may be easier to maintain due to the asynchronous nature of digital communication
- The PEERS(R) for Young Adults program has shown evidence-based success in teaching social skills for friendship initiation and maintenance, covering conversation skills, appropriate humor, organizing get-togethers, resolving conflicts, and navigating phone calls and social media

**Sources:**
- [PMC - Setbacks and Successes: How Young Adults on the Autism Spectrum Seek Friendship](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8992803/)
- [Frontiers - Experiences of friendship among autistic adults: a scoping review](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2025.1523506/full)
- [ScienceDirect - "Neurodivergent people just get each other"](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S3050656525001646)
- [PMC - Fostering Friendship and Dating Skills: PEERS(R) RCT](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10090714/)

### 3.2 Communication Patterns in Autistic-NT Relationships

Research on mixed-neurotype relationships reveals characteristic communication patterns:

**Common Friction Points:**
- **Directness vs. indirectness**: Autistic partners tend to say exactly what they mean; neurotypical partners may expect softening, hinting, or reading between the lines
- **Explicit vs. implicit communication**: Autistic partners often need things stated explicitly; neurotypical partners may feel that having to state everything explicitly means their partner "doesn't care enough to notice"
- **Emotional expression differences**: Flat affect or monotone voice can be misread as indifference; intense reactions to seemingly small things can be misread as overreacting
- **Sensory and touch negotiation**: Differences in sensory needs around physical intimacy, environmental preferences, and daily routines

**The Double Empathy Problem in Intimate Relationships:**
The double empathy problem is particularly impactful in close relationships because:
- Neurotypical partners may interpret autistic communication patterns through a **deficit lens** ("he doesn't care") rather than a **difference lens** ("he shows care differently")
- Autistic partners may not understand why their directness is experienced as hurtful
- Both partners may be simultaneously frustrated, each feeling that the other "doesn't get it"

**What Helps:**
- Both autistic and non-autistic partners shared a preference for **open and honest discussion**, but autistic participants emphasized the need for **explicit and clear communication**
- Successful relationships involve **mutual adaptation**: the neurotypical partner learning to be more explicit, the autistic partner learning what their NT partner needs emotionally
- Relationship satisfaction correlates with **both partners understanding and accepting** communication differences rather than trying to change each other

**Sources:**
- [PMC - Factors of relationship satisfaction for autistic and non-autistic partners](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10576901/)
- [Relational Psych - How Autistic People Experience Romantic Relationships Differently](https://www.relationalpsych.group/articles/how-autistic-people-experience-romantic-relationships-differently)
- [Attwood & Garnett Events - Bridging the Double Empathy Divide](https://www.attwoodandgarnettevents.com/blogs/news/bridging-the-double-empathy-divide-what-neurotypical-partners-can-learn)

### 3.3 Autistic Dating and Relationship Initiation

Research on autistic dating reveals specific pragmatic challenges:

**Initiation Challenges:**
- **Difficulty reading flirtation signals**: missing that someone is interested, or misinterpreting friendliness as romantic interest
- **Sensory challenges in typical dating environments**: loud bars, crowded restaurants, strong smells can be overwhelming, making it hard to focus on social interaction
- **Uncertainty about scripts**: dating involves a complex set of unwritten social scripts (who pays, when to text, how much to share) that can be opaque
- **Anxiety about disclosure timing**: when in the dating process to mention being autistic

**What Research Shows:**
- Most autistic adults report prior or current romantic relationship experience, and only a small minority report **no interest in dating** at all
- Online dating can be advantageous for autistic individuals because it allows time to compose messages, provides written communication, and allows early disclosure of communication preferences
- Activity-based dates (hiking, museums, cooking classes) tend to work better than conversation-focused settings because they provide shared focus and reduce the pressure of sustained face-to-face social performance

**Sources:**
- [Sagebrush Counseling - Autism and Romantic Relationships](https://www.sagebrushcounseling.com/blog/autism-and-romantic-relationships)
- [The Couples Center - Autism and Relationships](https://www.thecouplescenter.org/autism-and-relationships/)

### 3.4 Autistic-Autistic Relationships: Communication Compatibility

A growing body of research suggests that **autistic-autistic relationships** may have distinct communication advantages:

**Evidence for Communication Compatibility:**
- Studies show that **autistic-to-autistic communication can be exceptionally efficient and accurate**, challenging the assumption that autistic people are inherently poor communicators
- Observers rated **mixed (autistic-NT) conversations as the least smooth and enjoyable**, but did not observe any drop in quality when two autistic people chatted---suggesting the "problem" is in the mismatch, not in autistic communication itself
- The more similar two people are in their autistic traits (as measured by Autism Quotient scores), the **higher their perceived quality of friendship**---they feel closer, more accepted, and more supported
- Two autistic people may be **better attuned to each other** because their predictions about how social interactions will unfold are more likely to match the actual outcome

**Communication Strengths:**
- **Directness is mutual**: neither partner needs to decode hints or implied meanings
- **Shared understanding of sensory needs**: both partners understand the need for quiet, reduced stimulation, and recovery time
- **Less masking required**: reduced pressure to perform neurotypicality
- **Interest-based connection**: shared deep interests can form a strong relational foundation

**Challenges:**
- Both partners may struggle with **executive function** aspects of relationship maintenance (remembering dates, initiating contact)
- If both partners prefer solitude, **maintaining contact** may require deliberate scheduling
- Different sensory profiles can still create conflict (one partner needs silence while the other stims audibly)

**Sources:**
- [Embrace Autism - The AQ & relationship compatibility](https://embrace-autism.com/the-aq-and-relationship-compatibility/)
- [Scientific American - Autism, Human Connection and the 'Double Empathy' Problem](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/autism-human-connection-and-the-double-empathy-problem/)
- [PMC - Comparing Physical Intimacy and Romantic Relationships of Autistic and Non-autistic Adults](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11461547/)

## 4. THE SAFETY CONTEXT

### 4.1 Autistic Individuals and Law Enforcement Encounters

Research reveals alarming statistics about autistic individuals' interactions with police:

**Prevalence and Risk:**
- People with disabilities are **seven times more likely** to have police encounters than the general population
- A 2019 study found that **53% of autistic adults** with fewer support needs had four or more police interactions in their lifetime
- Nearly **20% of autistic individuals will be stopped and questioned** by police, and **5% will be arrested**, by age 21
- Many respondents reported **negative experiences**, primarily due to officers' lack of understanding of autism
- Less than **20% of law enforcement interactions** were rated as "satisfactory" by caregivers and autistic adults

**Sources:**
- [Autism Speaks - Better understanding for better police outcomes](https://www.autismspeaks.org/science-news/how-better-understanding-can-support-better-outcomes-police-interactions-autistics)

### 4.2 The Risk of Miscommunication

Several autistic communication traits can be dangerously misinterpreted by law enforcement:

**Behaviors Misread as Suspicious or Non-Compliant:**
- **Eye contact avoidance** → interpreted as deception, guilt, or evasiveness
- **Echolalia** (repeating the officer's words) → interpreted as mocking or not taking the situation seriously
- **Flat affect** → interpreted as coldness, lack of remorse, or psychopathy
- **Stimming** (hand-flapping, rocking) → interpreted as drug/alcohol intoxication or agitation
- **Literal interpretation** of questions → giving technically accurate but seemingly evasive answers
- **Delayed response time** → interpreted as non-compliance or resistance
- **Approach/avoidance behavior** (wanting to leave an uncomfortable situation) → interpreted as fleeing
- **Reaching for comfort objects or identification** → interpreted as reaching for a weapon

**Officer Knowledge Gaps:**
- Within a random sample of U.S. law enforcement officers, **80% were unable to accurately identify ASD characteristics**
- Officers recognized the **potential to misinterpret autistic behaviors** as drug or alcohol intoxication
- The majority of officers reported **no formal training in autism spectrum disorder**, despite working in states that mandate such training

### 4.3 Safety Strategies: Disclosure Cards, Practiced Scripts, and Proactive Measures

**Disclosure Cards and Identification:**
- **Medical alert bracelets or necklaces** that alert first responders to autism; can include basic information or reference a wallet card with details
- **Wallet cards** explaining that the carrier is autistic, describing possible behaviors (reduced eye contact, need for processing time, stimming), and providing emergency contact information
- The **Blue Envelope Program** (Massachusetts): drivers keep vehicle documents in a blue envelope along with an information sheet about autism, which is handed to officers during traffic stops
- The **Pennsylvania State Police informational card**: an officially distributed card designed to be presented during police encounters
- Programs like **The Wallet Card Project** (justdigit.org) provide free, professionally designed disclosure cards

**Critical Safety Protocol for Using Cards:**
- **ALWAYS tell the officer what you are reaching for BEFORE reaching for it** ("My autism information card is in my wallet. May I reach for it?")
- Police may misinterpret reaching motions as reaching for a weapon
- Do not reach for any identification or card until the officer gives permission

**Practiced Scripts for Police Encounters:**
- "I am autistic. I may not make eye contact, but I am listening."
- "I need a moment to process what you said."
- "I have an information card about autism. It is in my [location]. May I get it?"
- "I do not understand. Can you please say that in a different way?"
- Practice these scripts until they are automatic---during a high-stress encounter, scripted responses are more accessible than spontaneous speech

**Proactive Measures:**
- Visit your **local precinct** to introduce yourself to officers before a crisis situation occurs
- Register with local police **voluntary autism registries** if available
- Some communities offer **police ride-along** or meet-and-greet programs for autistic individuals and their families
- Keep your **hands visible** at all times during encounters
- If overwhelmed and unable to speak, **remain still** and wait for a support person

### 4.4 Police Training for Autism

**Current State of Training:**
- Most law enforcement training on autism, where it exists, is **lecture-based and brief** (often a single session during academy training)
- Training that emphasizes **active learning and direct interaction** between officers and autistic individuals is significantly more effective at changing behavior than lecture-only formats
- Despite mandates in some states, **most officers report receiving no formal autism training**

**What Effective Training Looks Like:**
- Direct contact with autistic adults (not just parents or professionals talking about autism)
- Scenario-based training with realistic simulations
- Emphasis on **de-escalation** techniques specific to sensory and communication differences
- Teaching officers to recognize that **non-compliance may be inability, not defiance**
- Information about how to access autism-specific resources during encounters

## 5. BURNOUT AND SOCIAL DEMANDS

### 5.1 Defining Autistic Burnout: The Raymaker et al. (2020) Study

The landmark study by **Dora M. Raymaker and colleagues (2020)**, published in *Autism in Adulthood*, used community-based participatory research to define autistic burnout through interviews with 19 autistic adults and analysis of 19 public internet sources.

**Definition:** Autistic burnout is characterized by three primary features:
1. **Chronic exhaustion** (physical, mental, and emotional)
2. **Loss of skills** (previously reliable abilities becoming temporarily unavailable---speech, executive function, self-care, social skills)
3. **Reduced tolerance to stimulus** (sensory thresholds dropping significantly)

**The Burnout Mechanism:**
The study identified a mechanism where **life stressors accumulate** (cumulative load) while **barriers to support** prevent relief from that load. When expectations outweigh abilities, burnout results. This is distinct from occupational burnout or clinical depression, though they can co-occur.

**Key Finding:** **Masking was identified as the most prominent life stressor** contributing to autistic burnout. The pressure to suppress autistic traits, pretend to be non-autistic, or perform social behaviors that do not come naturally creates an unsustainable cognitive and emotional load.

**Sources:**
- [PMC - "Having All of Your Internal Resources Exhausted Beyond Measure"](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7313636/)
- [PubMed - Raymaker et al. 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32851204/)
- [Liebertpub - Defining Autistic Burnout](https://www.liebertpub.com/doi/full/10.1089/aut.2019.0079)

### 5.2 How Cumulative Social/Pragmatic Demands Contribute to Burnout

Social and pragmatic demands are unique contributors to autistic burnout because they are **continuous, unavoidable, and often invisible** to others:

**The Cumulative Load:**
- Every social interaction requiring masking, script management, or neurotypical performance **depletes cognitive resources**
- Unlike physical tasks that have clear start and end points, social demands are **ambient and unpredictable** (a colleague stopping by your desk, an unexpected phone call, small talk in the elevator)
- Pragmatic processing that is automatic for neurotypical people requires **conscious effort** for autistic individuals: monitoring tone, managing eye contact, interpreting implied meaning, generating appropriate facial expressions
- The **gap between internal experience and external performance** is itself exhausting---knowing you feel overwhelmed but performing "fine" is cognitively expensive

**Workplace Social Demands as Burnout Drivers:**
- Mandatory social events (team lunches, happy hours, holiday parties)
- Open office plans with constant ambient social interaction
- Expectation of always being "on" and available for casual conversation
- Performance reviews that evaluate "teamwork" and "communication" based on neurotypical norms
- The accumulation of microaggressions and misunderstandings

**Sources:**
- [Autism.org.uk - Understanding autistic burnout](https://www.autism.org.uk/advice-and-guidance/professional-practice/autistic-burnout)
- [ResearchGate - A conceptual model of risk and protective factors for autistic burnout](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/359924348_A_conceptual_model_of_risk_and_protective_factors_for_autistic_burnout)

### 5.3 The Connection Between Masking and Burnout

Research has established increasingly clear links between camouflaging/masking and burnout:

**Psychological Evidence:**
- A systematic review (2025) found that camouflaging is **emotionally exhausting** for autistic adults and can result in burnout due to the cognitive demands required
- Interviewing 111 autistic adults, research found that those who reported masking had **symptoms of depression and felt unaccepted** by their social sphere
- Camouflaging was consistently associated with **anxiety, depression, suicidal ideation, and reduced quality of life** across multiple studies

**Biological Mechanisms:**
- Consistent camouflaging may initially **increase HPA axis activity** (stress hormone production) and subsequently lead to **suppressed cortisol levels** (hypocortisolism)
- This hypocortisolism has been linked to **chronic fatigue syndrome, PTSD, and burnout**, suggesting a physiological pathway from masking to autistic burnout
- Among adults, camouflaging was linked to both **stress-related symptoms and increased hair cortisol concentration**, with the burden possibly increasing with age as individuals invest more time and energy in masking

**The Vicious Cycle:**
Masking → exhaustion → reduced social capacity → increased pressure to mask (to compensate for visible decline) → deeper exhaustion → skill loss → burnout

### 5.4 Energy Management Strategies

The concept of **"spoon theory"** (originally from Christine Miserandino, 2003, for chronic illness) has been widely adopted by the autistic community to conceptualize limited energy:

**The Neurodivergent Spoon Drawer:**
A key refinement is that autistic energy is not only limited overall but **distributed unevenly across domains**:
- **Executive functioning spoons** (planning, organizing, task-switching)
- **Sensory spoons** (tolerance for noise, light, touch, crowds)
- **Social spoons** (capacity for conversation, masking, reading social cues)
- **Focus spoons** (ability to concentrate, resist distraction)
- **Language spoons** (verbal processing, word-finding, communication)

You might have full social spoons but depleted sensory spoons, or vice versa. Managing energy means tracking **which specific resources are depleted**, not just overall tiredness.

**Practical Energy Management Strategies:**

1. **Audit your energy expenditure**: Track what activities drain which types of energy. Identify your highest-cost social situations
2. **Set boundaries**: "I'm out of spoons today, I need some quiet time" / "I want to join, but I'll need to leave early"
3. **Schedule recovery time**: Build in decompression periods after high-demand social events---not as optional but as essential
4. **Strategic stimming**: Self-regulation through stimming, sensory breaks, or calming activities to replenish specific spoon types
5. **Batch social demands**: Group meetings or social obligations together to consolidate the masking cost, then have dedicated recovery time
6. **Minimize ambient social drain**: Use headphones, choose desk locations away from high-traffic areas, use "do not disturb" indicators
7. **Communicate energy levels**: Use shorthand with trusted people ("I'm at 2/10 social spoons right now")

**Sources:**
- [Neurodivergent Insights - Spoon Theory for Autism and ADHD](https://neurodivergentinsights.com/the-neurodivergent-spoon-drawer-spoon-theory-for-adhders-and-autists/)
- [Psychology Today - What Is the "Spoons Theory" of Autism?](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/women-autism-spectrum-disorder/202211/what-is-the-spoons-theory-autism)
- [Thrive Autism Coaching - How To Increase Your "Spoon Count"](https://www.thriveautismcoaching.com/post/how-to-increase-your-spoon-count-as-an-autistic-adult)

### 5.5 When to Invest in Social Performance vs. When to Conserve

Not all social demands are equal. A pragmatic approach involves **triaging social situations** based on cost-benefit analysis:

**High Investment, High Return (Worth the Spoons):**
- Job interviews and critical career meetings
- Medical appointments where accurate communication affects your health
- Relationships you genuinely value and want to maintain
- Safety situations (police encounters, emergencies)
- Advocating for accommodations you need

**Moderate Investment, Variable Return (Be Selective):**
- Work social events (attend some, skip others---establish a sustainable pattern)
- Acquaintance maintenance (periodic check-ins rather than constant availability)
- Extended family gatherings (negotiate duration, bring exit strategies)

**Low Return, High Cost (Permission to Skip):**
- Performative socialization (events you attend only because you "should")
- Small talk that serves no relationship or professional purpose
- Social situations that consistently leave you in burnout
- Events during already-depleted periods

**The Key Principle:** Social energy is finite and renewable---but only with adequate rest. Spending it wisely is not antisocial; it is necessary resource management.

### 5.6 The Right to NOT Perform

A critical message from the neurodiversity movement and autism self-advocacy research:

**What the Research Says:**
- Autistic self-advocacy and self-determination are associated with better outcomes across **educational, employment, social, identity, self-concept, and quality of life** domains
- The neurodiversity framework emphasizes that intervention should **"leave alone unconventional characteristics that cause no harm to self or other"**---such as monotone voice, preference for solitude, or reduced social engagement
- Research on 111 autistic adults found that those who masked had **worse depression and felt less accepted**, while the ability to be authentically autistic was associated with better mental health
- Early interventions that remove the right to say "no" can create lasting patterns of **compliance over self-advocacy**, which directly contributes to burnout

**What This Means Practically:**
- **Choosing not to attend a party is not a social failure**---it is accurate self-knowledge and responsible energy management
- **Speaking in a monotone is not something that needs to be "fixed"**---it is a valid way of communicating
- **Preferring written communication over phone calls is not avoidance**---it is choosing the medium where you communicate most effectively
- **Needing recovery time after socializing is not weakness**---it is a legitimate neurological need
- **Reduced social engagement during high-stress periods is self-care, not regression**

**The Cultural Shift:**
The autism self-advocacy movement argues that the burden of adaptation should not fall entirely on the autistic individual. Just as buildings must be wheelchair-accessible, social environments should accommodate different communication styles. The goal is not to make autistic people perform neurotypicality, but to create mutual understanding.

## 6. PRACTICAL CROSS-CONTEXT STRATEGIES

### 6.1 The Preparation Strategy: Researching Norms Before Entering New Contexts

Many autistic adults intuitively develop this strategy: **researching a new social environment before entering it**.

**How It Works:**
- Before a new job, research the company culture, dress code, meeting norms, and communication style (Glassdoor reviews, company blog posts, LinkedIn)
- Before a medical appointment, research the clinic layout, the doctor's communication style (patient reviews), and prepare questions
- Before a social event, learn the venue, expected duration, dress code, and who will attend
- Before a date, research the restaurant (menu, noise level, accessibility), look up common date conversation topics

**Why It Works for Autistic People:**
- Reduces uncertainty, which is a major anxiety driver
- Converts implicit social knowledge into explicit, learnable information
- Provides a framework for what to expect, reducing the cognitive load of real-time processing
- Allows preparation of scripts and exit strategies in advance

**Limitations:**
- Cannot cover every possibility (social situations are inherently unpredictable)
- Risk of over-preparation creating rigidity (struggling when reality deviates from research)
- Time-intensive; must be balanced against other demands

### 6.2 The Observation Strategy: Watching Before Participating

**How It Works:**
- In a new workplace, spend the first days/weeks **observing** social dynamics before actively participating
- At a party, find a position where you can **watch interactions** before joining conversations
- In a meeting, listen to several rounds of discussion to understand the rhythm before contributing
- Online, **lurk** in forums or groups before posting

**Why It Works:**
- Allows pattern recognition (a core autistic strength) to identify social norms
- Reduces the risk of early social missteps in a new environment
- Provides real examples of what communication is expected, rather than relying on abstract rules
- The observer role is itself a socially acceptable position ("I'm just getting settled in")

**Limitations:**
- Extended observation without participation can itself be noticed and misinterpreted
- Some environments expect immediate participation (sales roles, customer service)
- May reinforce anxiety-driven avoidance if used excessively

### 6.3 The Template Strategy: Having Scripts for Common Scenarios

**How It Works:**
Social scripts are **pre-prepared sets of words, phrases, or conversational sequences** that an autistic person can draw on in specific situations. They serve as a reliable framework when spontaneous conversation feels uncertain.

**Common Templates to Develop:**

**Workplace:**
- Greeting colleagues: "Morning! How's it going?" (with 2-3 follow-up responses ready)
- Ending a conversation: "I need to get back to [task], but nice chatting"
- Requesting clarification: "Could you put that in an email so I can refer back to it?"
- Declining invitations: "Thanks for the invite. I've got a lot on my plate this week, but I appreciate being included"

**Healthcare:**
- Opening: "I've written down my symptoms and questions---can I read from my notes?"
- Requesting accommodation: "I process information better when it's written down. Could you summarize the key points for me?"
- When confused: "I want to make sure I understand. Can you say that in a different way?"

**Social:**
- Joining a group conversation: (wait for a pause) "That's interesting---what do you mean by [specific thing someone said]?"
- Leaving a social event: "I've had a great time. I need to head out now, but let's do this again"
- When overwhelmed: "Excuse me for a moment---I need some air"

**Key Principles:**
- Scripts are a **toolkit, not a performance**---they should be adapted, not recited verbatim
- Build scripts from **observation** of what actually works in your specific context
- Have scripts for **exit** and **rescue** scenarios, not just openings
- Practice scripts until they are semi-automatic, freeing cognitive resources for other processing

**Sources:**
- [Tone2Emoji - Social Scripts for Autism](https://tone2emoji.com/blog/autism-social-scripts)
- [Neurolaunch - Social Scripts for Autism: Mastering Social Interactions](https://neurolaunch.com/social-scripts/)
- [Autism Advance - How To Use Social Scripts For Autism](https://autismadvance.com/how-to-use-social-scripts-for-autism/)

### 6.4 The Disclosure Strategy: When and How to Explain Your Communication Style

Disclosure is not binary---it exists on a spectrum from full diagnostic disclosure to explaining specific needs without mentioning autism.

**Levels of Disclosure:**

1. **No disclosure**: Managing differences privately. Appropriate when the environment is unsafe or the stakes of disclosure outweigh the benefits
2. **Functional disclosure**: Explaining specific needs without diagnostic labels. "I work best with written instructions" / "I need a few seconds to process what you said" / "Loud environments make it hard for me to focus on conversation"
3. **Partial disclosure**: Telling selected trusted individuals. "I'm neurodivergent, so I communicate a bit differently" / "I'm on the autism spectrum, which is why I might seem [specific trait]"
4. **Full disclosure**: Open about diagnosis. "I'm autistic. Here's what that means for how I communicate: [specifics]"

**When Each Level Is Appropriate:**

| Context | Recommended Level | Rationale |
|---------|------------------|-----------|
| Job interview | Functional or none | High stakes, low trust established |
| New workplace (first weeks) | Functional | Build credibility first |
| Trusted supervisor | Partial or full | Enables accommodation requests |
| HR (for accommodations) | Full | Legal protections require it |
| Medical appointments | Full | Directly relevant to care |
| Close friends | Full | Deepens understanding |
| First date | Varies | Context-dependent; no obligation |
| Police encounter | Full (via card) | Safety-critical |
| Casual acquaintances | Functional or none | Low stakes, minimal benefit |

**How to Disclose Effectively:**
- Lead with **strengths and specifics**, not limitations: "I'm really good at detailed analysis. I'm autistic, which means I communicate very directly and work best with clear written instructions."
- **Normalize**: "Autism is a neurological difference that affects how I process social information. About 1 in 36 people are autistic."
- **Be concrete**: Instead of "I'm socially awkward," try "I sometimes miss sarcasm and take things literally, so directness really helps me."
- **Set expectations**: "If I seem quiet in meetings, it doesn't mean I'm disengaged---I'm processing. I'll usually follow up with detailed written feedback."

### 6.5 The Exit Strategy: Knowing When and How to Leave

**Why Exit Strategies Matter:**
Autistic burnout often occurs because people **stay too long in overwhelming situations**. Having a pre-planned exit strategy reduces the anxiety of being "trapped" and the cognitive cost of making real-time decisions about when and how to leave.

**Components of an Exit Strategy:**

1. **Pre-commitment**: Before any social event, decide your **maximum duration**. Set a timer or alarm on your phone.
2. **Transport independence**: Always have your own way home (your car, a rideshare app ready, knowledge of public transit). Never be dependent on someone else's timeline.
3. **The social excuse**: Have a ready explanation that doesn't require disclosure. "I have an early morning" / "I promised my [pet/partner/roommate] I'd be back by [time]" / "I'm on a medication that makes me tire easily"
4. **The escape location**: Identify a quiet space you can retreat to temporarily (bathroom, outside, your car) for a sensory break before deciding whether to leave entirely
5. **The signal system**: If attending with a trusted person, establish a signal that means "I need to leave soon" (a code word, a specific text message)
6. **The graceful departure**: "I've really enjoyed this. I need to head out, but thank you for having me." Brief, warm, and non-negotiable.

**When to Use the Exit:**
- You notice signs of **sensory overload** (sound becoming painful, light too bright, skin crawling)
- You are **losing language** (word-finding becoming difficult, responses becoming shorter)
- You feel the **mask slipping** (unable to maintain expected social performance)
- You notice **emotional dysregulation** building (irritability, anxiety, desire to cry)
- You have been **performing at high intensity** for longer than your known threshold

### 6.6 The Debrief Strategy: Processing Social Encounters Afterward

Many autistic adults find that **social processing continues long after the interaction ends**. Rather than fighting this, the debrief strategy channels it productively.

**How It Works:**

1. **Scheduled decompression**: After a significant social event, build in 15-60 minutes of low-demand time. This is not optional---it is part of the event.
2. **Journaling or voice notes**: Record what happened, what went well, what felt difficult, and what you learned. This converts abstract social experience into concrete, reviewable data.
3. **Pattern identification**: Over time, review your debriefs for patterns. Do you consistently struggle with the same type of interaction? Do certain environments drain you more than others? Are there strategies that reliably work?
4. **Script refinement**: Use debrief insights to update your social scripts. "Next time someone asks about my weekend, I'll have 2-3 short anecdotes ready instead of blanking."
5. **Self-compassion check**: Debriefing is NOT rumination. The goal is not to catalog every mistake but to learn and move forward. Include what went **well** in every debrief.
6. **Trusted person processing**: If available, debrief with someone who understands autism. "How did that interaction seem from the outside?" can provide valuable perspective that you cannot access internally.

**Why It Works:**
- Channels post-event rumination into a structured, productive activity
- Builds a personal database of social knowledge
- Reduces anxiety about future events by creating a sense of growing competence
- Identifies patterns that pure in-the-moment processing misses

## 7. SERVICE ENCOUNTERS AND DAILY LIFE

### 7.1 Grocery Shopping

For many autistic adults, grocery shopping is not "just shopping"---it is a compound task involving **planning, sensory exposure, food uncertainty, public unpredictability, transitions, unpacking, and recovery**. Each of these components drains a different type of energy.

**Strategies:**
- **Shop at low-traffic times** (early morning, late evening)
- **Use a structured list** organized by store section to minimize backtracking and decision-making
- **Include "low-effort" reliable items** (foods that work on a tired day, freezer backups)
- **Shop with another person** for large runs to split the cognitive and sensory load
- **Use self-checkout** to avoid small talk at the register
- **Consider delivery services** when spoons are low

### 7.2 Restaurant Dining

**Strategies:**
- **Research the menu online** in advance to reduce decision pressure
- **Request a booth** to reduce ambient sensory load and create a sense of containment
- **Arrive early** to get settled before the restaurant becomes loud
- **Request the check early** to have an exit available when needed
- **Have a script for ordering**: practice or write out your order to reduce verbal demand under pressure
- **Noise-reducing earbuds** can take the edge off restaurant noise without fully blocking conversation

### 7.3 Phone Calls

Phone calls are a specific and well-documented challenge for many autistic adults, lacking the visual cues of in-person interaction and the reflection time of text communication.

**Strategies:**
- **Write out the purpose of the call** and key points before dialing
- **Script your opening**: "Hi, my name is [X]. I'm calling about [Y]."
- **Keep a notepad** to write down important information during the call
- **Request alternatives** when possible: email, online chat, or text-based customer service
- **Practice with trusted people** to build phone confidence incrementally

- **Triple Empathy Problem** (Shaw et al., 2024): In healthcare, the double empathy problem is compounded by the medical knowledge asymmetry between doctor and patient
- **Spoon Theory** (Miserandino, 2003; adapted for autism): Limited energy conceptualized as finite "spoons" allocated across different domains
- **Neurodivergent Spoon Drawer** (Neurodivergent Insights): Refinement of spoon theory recognizing that energy is distributed unevenly across specific domains (social, sensory, executive function, language, focus)
- **AASPIRE Healthcare Toolkit**: Evidence-based tools for autistic adults to prepare for and navigate healthcare encounters
- **PEERS(R) for Young Adults**: Evidence-based social skills program covering friendship and dating skills
