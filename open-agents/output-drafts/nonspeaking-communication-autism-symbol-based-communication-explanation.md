# Symbol-Based Communication: How It Works

## Overview

Symbol-based communication systems use pictures, symbols, signs, or visual supports as the primary medium of expression for individuals who cannot rely on speech. These systems are often the first form of augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) introduced to non-speaking autistic children, and they remain in widespread use across educational settings worldwide. The major systems -- PECS, Makaton, and Blissymbols -- differ in their theoretical foundations, teaching methods, modalities, and evidence bases, but they share a common premise: that visual representation can bridge the gap between communicative intent and expression.

**What is symbol-based communication?**
Any communication method that uses visual representations (pictures, line drawings, abstract symbols, or manual signs) rather than spoken or written words as the primary medium. Symbol-based systems range from simple picture cards for single requests to sophisticated compositional systems capable of generating novel meanings from base elements.

**Why does it exist?**
Many non-speaking autistic individuals have relative strengths in visual-spatial processing. Speech is transient -- once spoken, it disappears. Symbols are persistent -- a picture on a card or screen stays visible, can be examined, pointed to, and exchanged. Symbol-based communication capitalizes on this persistence and on documented autistic cognitive strengths in visual processing to create a communication channel that works with, rather than against, the individual's neurological profile.

**Key takeaways:**
- PECS is the most rigorously studied symbol-based system for autism, with multiple RCTs and meta-analyses
- Makaton is the most widely adopted system in the UK (100,000+ users) but lacks proportionate controlled evaluation
- Blissymbols offers the highest compositionality (building complex meanings from ~120 base elements) but has seen limited adoption in autism
- ARASAAC demonstrates the best iconicity across grammatical categories and is free and open-source
- Most programs stall at teaching requesting -- the full range of communicative functions (commenting, questioning, narrating) requires intentional programming beyond basic phases

### Confidence Overview

The research underpinning this explanation was independently validated with an overall confidence rating of 9/10. All major claims were confirmed. Two corrections from validation have been incorporated: Makaton was developed at Botleys Park Hospital (not the Royal Association for Deaf People), and the Blissymbols vocabulary count has been standardized to 6,183 from the 2025 BCI authorized vocabulary.

## Core Components

### PECS (Picture Exchange Communication System)

PECS was developed in 1985 by Andy Bondy, PhD, and Lori Frost, MS, CCC-SLP, at the Delaware Autism Program. It is grounded in applied behavior analysis (ABA) principles and B.F. Skinner's analysis of verbal behavior -- specifically, the concept of the "mand" (a verbal operant motivated by a need and reinforced by receiving what was requested).

PECS is not "pointing at pictures." It is a structured behavioral teaching program with six specific phases, each building a distinct communicative skill.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-1-pecs-phases -->

**Phase I: How to Communicate.** The learner picks up a picture of a desired item and physically hands it to a communication partner, who immediately provides the item. A physical prompter behind the learner guides the exchange initially (errorless learning). No verbal prompts are used ("What do you want?" is not asked). The critical skill is communicative initiation -- the learner must travel to the partner and make an exchange, not just point.

**Phase II: Distance and Persistence.** The learner learns to travel increasing distances to reach their communication partner and communication book. This teaches persistence and generalization -- communication works even when the partner is not directly in front of you.

**Phase III: Picture Discrimination.** The learner selects the correct picture from an array of multiple options. This requires discriminating between symbols -- a picture of crackers versus a picture of juice -- to request a specific item.

**Phase IV: Sentence Structure.** The learner constructs sentences on a sentence strip, combining an "I want" symbol with an item picture to form a two-part message. This introduces combinatorial communication -- the idea that symbols can be combined to create meaning that exceeds any single symbol.

**Phase V: Responding to Questions.** The learner uses pictures to answer "What do you want?" -- a shift from self-initiated requesting to responsive communication. This phase introduces intraverbal behavior (responding to verbal stimuli).

**Phase VI: Commenting.** The learner uses pictures to respond to "What do you see?" "What do you hear?" and to make spontaneous comments. This represents the transition from instrumental communication (getting needs met) to referential communication (sharing observations about the world). In Skinner's framework, this is the tact -- labeling or commenting on the environment.

**The Phase III stall.** A critical limitation of PECS in practice is that many programs stall at or before Phase III. Requesting is taught, but the higher communicative functions -- commenting, questioning, narrating, expressing emotions -- require intentional programming that many implementation settings do not provide. This means that many PECS users are taught to get their basic needs met but not to share observations, ask questions, tell jokes, or express how they feel.

**Evidence base.** A meta-analysis (Flippin et al., 2010, ASHA) found PECS effective for increasing communication frequency but noted that evidence for speech production gains was weaker. A 2025 systematic review of 37 RCTs in China showed significant improvements across cognitive function, social skills, and mental health. A systematic review analyzing 51 studies (2012-2024) found consistent positive outcomes. PECS has the strongest evidence base among dedicated symbol systems for autism, though controlled studies remain small in number relative to its widespread use.

### Makaton

Makaton was developed in the 1970s at Botleys Park Hospital in Chertsey, Surrey, UK, by Margaret Walker, Katherine Johnston, and Tony Cornforth (the name "Makaton" is derived from their first names: MArgaret, KAtherine, TONy).

> **Correction**: The original research attributed Makaton's development to "the Royal Association for Deaf People." Validation identified this as inaccurate. The correct institution is Botleys Park Hospital.

Makaton uses **key word signing** -- simplified signs from British Sign Language (or the national sign language of the country) used alongside speech to emphasize important words. It is not a full sign language. A Makaton user would sign the key words while speaking a full sentence: "Do you WANT [sign] JUICE [sign] or WATER [sign]?" This provides visual emphasis on critical vocabulary without requiring the child to process full signed sentences.

Makaton combines three modalities: manual signs, graphic symbols, and speech used simultaneously. Many individuals naturally drop signs as speech develops -- Makaton often functions as a bridge to spoken language rather than a permanent alternative.

**Adoption.** Over 100,000 children and adults use Makaton in the UK, with programs in more than 40 countries. It has been popularized through BBC's children's programming (Something Special, featuring Mr. Tumble), reaching millions of children beyond those with formal communication needs.

**Evidence base.** Makaton generally shows positive effects on communication and speech development. However, the program was adopted on a massive scale without the kind of rigorous controlled evaluation its reach warrants. Most evidence comes from UK clinical practice and observational studies rather than randomized controlled trials. This is a notable gap: a system used by over 100,000 people should have a stronger controlled evidence base than it does.

### Blissymbols (Blissymbolics)

Blissymbols is a compositional graphical language developed by Charles K. Bliss (1897-1985) between 1942 and 1949 while he was a refugee, first at the Shanghai Ghetto and then in Sydney. Inspired by Chinese characters, Bliss created a system of symbols built from approximately 100-120 base elements that can be combined according to systematic rules to construct complex meanings.

The current Blissymbolics Communication International (BCI) authorized vocabulary contains 6,183 approved symbols (2025 BCI-AV), all constructed from those base elements.

**How compositionality works.** Unlike picture systems where each concept requires a separate image, Blissymbols allow users to construct novel meanings by combining elements. The symbol for "house" combined with the symbol for "knowledge" produces "school." The symbol for "water" combined with "container" produces "cup" or "glass." A user who knows the base elements and the composition rules can construct meanings they have never been explicitly taught -- a generative capacity that no fixed-picture system provides.

**AAC adoption.** Blissymbols was pioneered for AAC use by Shirley McNaughton in 1971 at the Ontario Crippled Children's Centre (now Holland Bloorview) for children with cerebral palsy. It has been used primarily in physical disability communities and has seen limited adoption in autism specifically.

**Potential alignment with autistic cognition.** The compositional, rule-based, pattern-driven nature of Blissymbols may align well with the systematic cognitive style associated with autism -- but this hypothesis has not been formally tested. It represents a research opportunity.

**Limitations.** Blissymbols has a steeper learning curve than photographic or pictographic systems. The symbols are opaque (not guessable without instruction), which means communication partners must also learn the system. This limits spontaneous communication with unfamiliar partners.

### Symbol Sets vs. Symbol Systems

An important distinction: PCS (Picture Communication Symbols, by Mayer-Johnson/Tobii Dynavox) and Widgit Literacy Symbols are symbol **sets** -- libraries of visual representations. They are not communication **systems**. PCS provides the images used inside PECS books, AAC apps, and communication boards. Widgit is the dominant symbol set in UK education. They are the visual vocabulary, not the method.

ARASAAC (from the Aragonese Portal of AAC) deserves particular mention: it is free, open-source, and has demonstrated the best iconicity across grammatical categories (nouns, verbs, adjectives) in comparative research. For resource-limited settings, ARASAAC provides high-quality symbols at no cost.

## How Symbol Transparency Affects Learning

Symbol transparency -- how easily a symbol's meaning can be guessed without instruction -- directly affects learnability. Research distinguishes three levels:

**Transparent symbols** (high iconicity): The symbol visually resembles its referent so closely that meaning is immediately guessable. A photograph of a cup means "cup." No teaching needed.

**Translucent symbols**: The connection between symbol and meaning can be understood once explained but is not immediately obvious. A line drawing of a cup (PCS style) requires some inference.

**Opaque symbols**: The connection must be explicitly taught. Blissymbols for most abstract concepts are opaque -- you cannot guess their meaning from looking at them.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-3-transparency-spectrum -->

There is an inherent trade-off. Transparent symbols are easier to learn but represent fixed concepts -- each new meaning requires a new image. Opaque compositional systems like Blissymbols are harder to learn initially but offer greater expressive power -- the ability to construct novel meanings without needing a pre-made image for every concept. The choice between transparency and compositionality depends on the individual's cognitive profile, learning trajectory, and communication goals.

## The Communicative Functions Gap

One of the most significant limitations across all symbol-based systems is the gap between what is typically taught and the full range of human communication.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-4-communicative-functions -->

**Requesting** (Skinner's mand) is the most commonly taught function and has the strongest evidence base. A child learns to exchange a picture for a desired item, or sign "juice" to receive juice. This is functional and important -- getting basic needs met reduces frustration and challenging behavior.

But human communication extends far beyond requesting. **Rejecting** (saying no), **social routines** (greetings, turn-taking), **commenting** (sharing observations), **questioning** (requesting information), and **narrating** (telling stories, explaining, expressing emotions, humor) all represent communicative functions that most symbol-based programs do not systematically address.

The gap matters because a person who can only request is communicating instrumentally but not socially. They can get crackers but cannot say "I love you," cannot observe "that cloud looks like a dog," cannot ask "when is Dad coming home," and cannot express "I feel anxious." Advancing beyond requesting requires intentional, systematic programming -- and many implementation settings lack the resources, training, or mandate to provide it.

## Developmental Pathways

Symbol-based communication leads to different outcomes for different individuals, and all outcomes represent successful communication.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-5-development-pathways -->

**Path A: Bridge to speech.** For some individuals, symbols reduce communicative frustration, creating conditions favorable to speech emergence. As speech becomes more reliable, symbol use naturally decreases. Research across PECS and Makaton consistently shows that symbol and sign use does not inhibit speech development and may facilitate it.

**Path B: Bridge to literacy.** Symbol understanding can transfer to written word recognition. An individual who learns that a particular PCS symbol means "go" may subsequently learn that the written word "go" means the same thing. This pathway is particularly relevant for individuals who develop text-based AAC.

**Path C: Lifelong primary mode.** For some individuals, symbols remain the primary communication method throughout life, with vocabulary expanding and communicative sophistication increasing over time. This is a valid and successful outcome.

**Path D: Bridge to high-tech AAC.** Symbol skills learned through low-tech methods (PECS, communication boards) transfer to high-tech AAC apps and speech-generating devices. The same symbol vocabulary appears on the screen as appeared on the card.

## Visual Supports: The Broader Ecosystem

Beyond formal symbol systems, a broader ecosystem of visual supports structures the communicative environment for autistic individuals:

**Visual schedules** -- sequences of pictures or symbols showing the order of activities -- provide predictability and reduce transition anxiety. Strong evidence supports their effectiveness in reducing challenging behavior and increasing independence.

**Social stories** (Carol Gray, 1991) -- short narratives using text and pictures to explain social situations and expected behaviors -- have moderate evidence for effectiveness.

**Choice boards**, **first-then boards**, and **visual timers** provide concrete, persistent visual information that supports communication, decision-making, and self-regulation.

These supports work because autistic cognition often favors persistent visual information over transient auditory information. Speech disappears as soon as it is spoken. A visual schedule stays visible. This persistence provides the processing time that many autistic individuals need to understand and respond to communicative demands.

## Common Misconceptions

### Misconception: "PECS is just pointing at pictures"

**Reality**: PECS is a structured six-phase behavioral teaching program. The physical exchange mechanism -- picking up a picture and handing it to a partner -- teaches communicative initiation in a way that pointing does not. The exchange requires the learner to travel to the partner, gain attention, and complete a social transaction.

### Misconception: "Using signs or symbols will discourage speech"

**Reality**: Research across PECS and Makaton consistently shows that symbol and sign use does not inhibit speech and may facilitate it. The mechanism likely involves reduced communicative frustration and the provision of a visual scaffold for language.

### Misconception: "Symbol-based communication is only for young children or people with intellectual disability"

**Reality**: Symbol-based systems are used across the lifespan and across cognitive profiles. Many adults use symbol-based AAC as their primary communication mode. Visual supports benefit autistic individuals regardless of cognitive ability.

> **Correction**: The original research stated Blissymbols has "over 6,500 symbols." The verified count from the 2025 BCI authorized vocabulary is 6,183 approved symbols. This explanation uses the verified figure.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

**PECS and motor difficulties.** The physical exchange mechanism in PECS requires sufficient motor coordination to pick up a picture and hand it to a partner. Individuals with severe motor impairments may need adapted versions or alternative systems.

**Cultural variation in symbol transparency.** A symbol that is iconic in one cultural context may be opaque in another. Most commercially available symbol sets reflect Western cultural contexts, creating potential barriers for families from other cultural backgrounds.

**Digital-native symbols.** Younger AAC users may be more familiar with emojis and digital icons than traditional symbol sets, raising questions about whether standard AAC symbol libraries need updating for contemporary users.

## Summary

### Key Points

1. PECS, Makaton, and Blissymbols represent three distinct approaches to symbol-based communication, differing in theoretical foundation, modality, evidence base, and compositionality
2. Most programs stall at teaching requesting -- advancing to commenting, questioning, and narrating requires intentional systematic programming that many settings do not provide
3. Symbol-based communication leads to four equally valid developmental pathways: bridge to speech, bridge to literacy, lifelong primary mode, or bridge to high-tech AAC

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| PECS | Picture Exchange Communication System -- structured program teaching communication through physical picture exchange |
| Makaton | Communication system using simplified signs, symbols, and speech together |
| Blissymbols | Compositional graphical language of 6,183 symbols built from ~120 base elements (also: Blissymbolics, Semantography) |
| PCS | Picture Communication Symbols -- widely used symbol set by Mayer-Johnson/Tobii Dynavox (also: Boardmaker symbols) |
| ARASAAC | Free, open-source symbol set from the Aragonese Portal of AAC |
| Iconicity | The degree to which a symbol visually resembles its referent |
| Transparency | How easily a symbol's meaning can be guessed without instruction (guessability) |
| Translucency | How easily a symbol's meaning can be understood once the connection is explained |
| Mand | In Skinner's verbal behavior: a request motivated by a need and reinforced by receiving what was requested |
| Tact | In Skinner's verbal behavior: a label or comment on something in the environment |
| Visual schedule | Sequence of pictures/symbols showing activity order; provides predictability |
| Social story | Short narrative using text and pictures to explain social situations (Carol Gray, 1991) |
| Compositionality | The property of a system where complex meanings can be built from simpler elements |
