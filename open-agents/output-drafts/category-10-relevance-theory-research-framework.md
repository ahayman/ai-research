## 1. RT as a General-Purpose Pragmatic Framework

### 1.1 The Unifying Claim

Relevance Theory's most ambitious claim is that a **single cognitive principle** -- the pursuit of optimal relevance -- can account for ALL pragmatic phenomena. Where Grice needed four maxims (Quality, Quantity, Relation, Manner) and where Neo-Gricean theorists like Levinson proposed three heuristics (Q, I, M), Relevance Theory claims that one principle does the entire job and does it better.

This is not merely theoretical parsimony for its own sake. The claim is that a single principle reflects a deeper truth about human cognition: that our minds are designed to seek the best possible cognitive payoff for the least possible processing effort, and this single drive underlies all communicative interpretation.

Crucially, the principle of relevance is **not a maxim** that speakers can obey or violate. It is an exceptionless generalization about what happens when someone is addressed with an ostensive stimulus. The communicative principle of relevance states that every ostensive act of communication creates an expectation of optimal relevance -- this is automatic and cannot be "turned off." A speaker cannot "violate" relevance the way they can (in Grice's framework) violate the maxim of Quality by lying. Instead, relevance is always presumed; the question is only what interpretation satisfies that presumption.

**What RT replaces each Gricean maxim with:**

| Gricean Maxim | What It Governs | How RT Handles It |
|---|---|---|
| **Quality** (Be truthful) | Truthfulness | The speaker communicates what they believe to be true because asserting false information would reduce cognitive effects (false information produces misleading effects) and is therefore not optimally relevant, except when the speaker's "preferences" include deception -- which clause (b) of optimal relevance accommodates |
| **Quantity** (Be informative enough, not too informative) | Information amount | An optimally relevant utterance provides enough cognitive effects to justify processing. Too little information = insufficient effects. Too much = unnecessary processing effort. Both are captured by the single effects/effort ratio |
| **Relation** (Be relevant) | Topical relevance | This is the maxim that RT most directly absorbs -- the entire theory IS about relevance |
| **Manner** (Be clear, orderly, brief) | Form and style | Greater clarity reduces processing effort, making an utterance more relevant. But the speaker's "abilities and preferences" (clause b) may lead to less-than-maximally-clear expressions -- for reasons of politeness, delicacy, style, or cognitive limitations |

### 1.2 How RT Handles Speech Acts

Traditional speech act theory (Austin 1962, Searle 1969) posits a rich taxonomy of illocutionary acts -- assertions, promises, requests, warnings, threats, congratulations, apologies, and so on -- each with specific "felicity conditions." This taxonomy treats illocutionary force as something that needs to be separately specified for each utterance type.

**RT's radical simplification:** Relevance Theory recognizes only **three basic speech-act types**, which are loosely associated with the three basic sentence types:

1. **Saying that (p)** -- associated with declarative sentences ("The bus is leaving")
2. **Telling (someone) to (do X)** -- associated with imperative sentences ("Close the door")
3. **Asking whether (p)** -- associated with interrogative sentences ("Is it raining?")

These three speech-act descriptions function as **higher-level explicatures** (see Section 2). They are derived pragmatically, not encoded in the grammar. The sentence type provides a *cue* but does not *determine* the speech act. This is a crucial difference from Searle's approach.

**How the richer taxonomy is handled:** All the finer speech-act distinctions -- warning, threatening, promising, suggesting, advising, requesting, demanding, congratulating, and so on -- are treated as either:

- **Pragmatically derived** from the basic three types via the comprehension procedure (the same declarative "The bus is leaving" can function as an assertion, a warning, a promise, a report, etc., depending on which interpretation satisfies expectations of relevance in the specific context)
- **Culture-specific or institutional** rather than linguistic (bidding at auction, sentencing in court, pronouncing someone married -- these require specific institutional knowledge, not special pragmatic principles)

**Example -- "The boss is coming":**
- Basic explicature: The boss is coming [to this location, soon]
- Higher-level explicature (speech act): The speaker is **warning** that the boss is coming
- The "warning" interpretation is not linguistically encoded. It is pragmatically derived because the interpretation "the speaker is warning me" yields more cognitive effects (I should look busy, put away my phone, etc.) than "the speaker is merely informing me of a fact."

**Why this matters for autistic learners:** Rather than memorizing a taxonomy of speech acts and their felicity conditions, you can derive what speech act someone is performing by asking: "What interpretation of this utterance -- including what the speaker is *doing* by saying it -- gives me the most useful cognitive effects for the least effort?" The speech act emerges from the interpretation process rather than needing to be independently identified.

### 1.3 How RT Handles Presupposition

Traditional accounts treat presupposition as a semantic or conventional property of certain linguistic expressions. For example, "John stopped smoking" presupposes that John used to smoke. This presupposition is said to be "triggered" by the word "stopped" regardless of context.

**RT's approach:** Relevance Theory does not need a separate theory of presupposition. Instead, presuppositions are handled through the existing machinery of **manifest assumptions** and **cognitive effects**:

- A presupposition is an assumption that the speaker treats as **already manifest** in the hearer's cognitive environment (already part of what the hearer knows or can readily infer)
- When the speaker says "John stopped smoking," they treat it as manifest (taken for granted, backgrounded) that John used to smoke. This assumed-to-be-manifest content is not the **main point** of the utterance -- it is part of the contextual background against which the main point achieves its relevance
- The main point -- the **focus** of the utterance -- is what provides the new cognitive effects (namely, that the smoking has ceased)

**Mandy Simons' account (Presupposition and Relevance):** Simons proposes that presuppositions can be understood as a kind of **relevance implicature** -- propositions that the hearer must assume in order to find the utterance relevant. The presupposed content establishes the conditions under which the asserted content is relevant. For "John stopped smoking" to be relevant (to provide cognitive effects about John's current behavior), you need to assume that John previously smoked. The presupposition is the assumption that makes the main assertion relevant.

**Accommodation** (when presupposed content is not already manifest): If someone says "John stopped smoking" and you didn't know John smoked, you typically **accommodate** the presupposition -- you simply add "John used to smoke" to your cognitive environment because it is the most accessible assumption that makes the utterance relevant. This is not a separate mechanism; it falls out naturally from the comprehension procedure's search for an interpretation that satisfies expectations of relevance.

### 1.4 How RT Handles Politeness

Brown and Levinson's (1987) influential politeness theory is built on a Gricean foundation: politeness strategies are analyzed as systematic departures from Gricean maxims, motivated by the need to manage "face" (the public self-image). Indirect requests ("Could you possibly pass the salt?") are seen as face-saving deviations from the maxim of Manner (be brief, be direct).

**RT's alternative:** Politeness phenomena can be accounted for without a separate politeness theory, through **clause (b) of the presumption of optimal relevance**:

> The ostensive stimulus is the most relevant one compatible with **the communicator's abilities and preferences**.

The phrase "abilities and preferences" is doing crucial work here. A speaker's **preferences** include not just what they want to communicate but HOW they want to communicate it -- and these preferences include moral, prudential, and **stylistic** judgments, which encompass politeness considerations.

**How this works in practice:**
- A direct request ("Pass the salt") minimizes processing effort but may not be compatible with the speaker's preference to maintain social harmony
- An indirect request ("Could you possibly pass the salt?") requires more processing effort, but the additional effort is **justified** by additional cognitive effects: the hearer derives not just the request but also the information that the speaker is being considerate, respects the hearer's autonomy, and values the social relationship
- The extra processing effort is compensated by these additional social-relational effects, so the indirect utterance can still be optimally relevant

**Indirectness as a route to optimal relevance:** On this account, an indirect expression is not a "violation" of a maxim of brevity. It is the most relevant expression compatible with the speaker's preferences (including their preference to be polite). The extra effort creates a space for additional cognitive effects -- the hearer not only gets the literal request but also information about the speaker's attitude, the social relationship, and the appropriate register of interaction.

**Weak implicatures and politeness:** Polite indirectness often generates a range of **weak implicatures** -- loosely communicated suggestions about the speaker's attitude, deference, goodwill, and so on. These weak implicatures contribute to the overall cognitive effects of the utterance, even though no single one of them is essential. The resulting "warm glow" of politeness emerges from this range of weakly communicated attitudes, not from any specific conventionalized politeness strategy.

### 1.5 How RT Handles Register and Formality Variation

Register variation (formal vs. informal speech, technical vs. everyday language, written vs. spoken style) is handled in RT through the same clause (b) mechanism:

- **Formality as a preference:** A speaker in a formal context (job interview, academic paper, courtroom) has a preference for formal register. This preference constrains which utterance they choose -- they select the most relevant utterance compatible with this formality preference
- **Register as cognitive effects:** The choice of register itself communicates information. Using formal language in a job interview communicates "I take this seriously, I am professional, I understand the norms of this context." Using informal language with friends communicates solidarity, closeness, and relaxation. These register-related cognitive effects are part of what makes the utterance optimally relevant
- **Processing effort and register:** Unfamiliar register can increase processing effort. If someone uses extremely formal language in a casual context, the mismatch creates extra processing effort, which the hearer must then justify by finding additional cognitive effects (the speaker is being ironic, the speaker is signaling distance, the speaker doesn't understand the social context)

## 2. Explicature: The Most Important RT Concept Not Yet Fully Explored

### 2.1 What Explicature Is and Why It Matters

**Definition:** An explicature is a communicated assumption that is derived partly by **decoding** (extracting the linguistic meaning) and partly by **pragmatic inference** (enriching, adjusting, and completing that meaning). An explicature is a **development of the logical form** encoded by the utterance.

**The crucial insight:** What is "explicitly communicated" goes **far beyond** what is linguistically encoded. Even the "literal" meaning of an utterance requires massive pragmatic processing. The idea that there is a neat separation between "what was literally said" and "what was implied" is an illusion. Almost every utterance requires pragmatic work just to determine what was explicitly communicated.

### 2.2 The Semantic Underdeterminacy Thesis

This is one of the most important claims in Relevance Theory, developed most extensively by **Robyn Carston** in her 2002 book *Thoughts and Utterances: The Pragmatics of Explicit Communication*.

**The thesis:** The linguistic meaning of a sentence (what the words and grammar encode) **radically underdetermines** the proposition the speaker communicates. The decoded linguistic meaning -- the "logical form" -- is an incomplete, subpropositional schema that needs extensive pragmatic enrichment before it becomes a full proposition (a thought that can be true or false).

**What this means:** Natural language is not like a formal code where each sentence maps onto exactly one proposition. Instead, linguistic forms are more like **templates** or **blueprints** that need to be filled in, adjusted, and completed by the hearer's pragmatic processing. The gap between encoded meaning and communicated meaning is not occasional or marginal -- it is **systematic and pervasive**.

**Evidence for underdeterminacy -- consider the sentence "She gave him her key and he opened the door":**
- The linguistic form encodes something like: [FEMALE] gave [MALE] [FEMALE's] key and [MALE] opened the door
- But the communicated proposition involves far more: a specific woman gave a specific man a specific key (to a specific door), and he then (using that key) opened that (specific) door, and the opening happened AFTER the giving, and the giving was done so that the opening could happen
- None of the content in italics above is linguistically encoded. It is all pragmatically inferred as part of the explicature

### 2.3 Types of Enrichment That Produce Explicature

The process of constructing an explicature from the decoded logical form involves several distinct types of pragmatic processing:

#### 2.3.1 Disambiguation
Choosing among multiple linguistically possible meanings of a word or structure.

- "I went to the **bank**" -- financial institution or riverside?
- "He saw her **duck**" -- the bird, or the act of ducking?
- "Visiting relatives can be **boring**" -- relatives who visit, or the act of visiting relatives?

Disambiguation is guided by relevance: the hearer selects whichever meaning yields the most accessible interpretation that satisfies expectations of relevance. You don't consciously consider both meanings and choose -- the more relevant meaning is typically accessed first and the other meaning never even reaches consciousness.

#### 2.3.2 Reference Assignment
Determining what pronouns, demonstratives, and other referential expressions refer to.

- "**He** told **me** that **they** would arrive tomorrow" -- who is "he," who are "they," and when is "tomorrow"?
- "**That**'s amazing" -- what is being referred to?
- "**Everyone** had a good time" -- everyone in the world? Everyone at the party?

Reference assignment is not a purely semantic process. It requires the hearer to use contextual information to figure out who or what the speaker is talking about, guided by which referent yields the most relevant interpretation.

#### 2.3.3 Saturation (Filling in Linguistically Mandated Slots)
Some linguistic expressions have "gaps" or "variables" that **must** be contextually filled for the utterance to express a complete proposition. This is sometimes called a **mandatory** pragmatic process because the linguistic form itself signals that something needs to be supplied.

- "It's raining" -- raining WHERE? (The sentence is true or false only relative to a location)
- "It's too hot" -- too hot FOR WHAT? (For swimming? For sleeping? For the experiment?)
- "She's ready" -- ready FOR WHAT?
- "He's tall" -- tall relative to WHAT standard? (Tall for a basketball player? Tall for a 5-year-old?)

The term "saturation" (from Recanati) captures the idea that the linguistic form has a slot that needs to be filled (saturated) with contextual content. Without saturation, you don't have a full proposition.

#### 2.3.4 Free Enrichment (Adding Unarticulated Constituents)
This is the most radical type of explicature development. Free enrichment involves **adding conceptual material that is not mandated by anything in the linguistic form**. The enrichment is "free" in the sense that the grammar does not require it -- it is driven purely by pragmatic considerations (the search for optimal relevance).

Classic examples:

- **"I've had breakfast"** → I've had breakfast **this morning** (not: at some point in my life)
- **"I've got nothing to wear"** → I've got nothing **appropriate** to wear **to this event** (not: I literally own zero garments)
- **"Jane is a better candidate than John"** → Jane is a better candidate than John **for this specific job** (not: for all purposes)
- **"It will take time"** → It will take **a long** time (not: it will take some quantity of time, which is trivially true of everything)
- **"He hasn't eaten"** → He hasn't eaten **today/recently** (not: he has never eaten in his life)

In each case, the hearer adds content that is nowhere in the sentence. The concept of "unarticulated constituents" (a term from John Perry) captures this: there are constituents of the communicated proposition that have no corresponding element in the linguistic form.

**Why free enrichment is important:** It demonstrates that EVEN the proposition explicitly communicated by an utterance is partly a pragmatic construction. The explicit/implicit distinction is not a distinction between "what the words say" and "what is inferred beyond the words." Even "what the words say" involves inference.

**The debate:** Free enrichment is theoretically controversial. Some philosophers (including minimalists like Emma Borg) resist the idea that truth-conditional content can be affected by pragmatic processes that are not linguistically triggered. They argue that if enrichment is "free" (not mandated by the grammar), it should be treated as implicature, not explicature. Relevance theorists respond that the enriched content is intuitively part of "what the speaker said" and is a development of the logical form, hence explicature.

#### 2.3.5 Loosening / Broadening
The encoded concept is expanded to include items or properties that fall outside its literal denotation.

- **"The ATM swallowed my card"** → SWALLOWED* (took in and did not return -- not literal ingestion)
- **"The lake is round"** → ROUND* (roughly circular -- not geometrically perfect)
- **"The steak is raw"** → RAW* (very undercooked -- not literally uncooked)
- **"Holland is flat"** → FLAT* (relatively level -- not perfectly horizontal)
- **"I'm drowning in work"** → DROWNING* (overwhelmed -- not literal submersion)

The asterisk notation (SWALLOWED*, ROUND*, etc.) marks these as **ad hoc concepts** -- context-specific concepts constructed on the fly that differ from the encoded concept. (See Section 3 for detailed treatment.)

#### 2.3.6 Narrowing
The encoded concept is restricted to a proper subset of its literal denotation.

- **"I need a drink"** → DRINK* (alcoholic drink -- not just any liquid)
- **"I need to eat"** → EAT* (eat a proper meal -- not just consume any food item)
- **"She cut him"** → CUT* (cut him socially, snubbed him -- a specific kind of cutting)
- **"He's a bachelor"** → BACHELOR* (an eligible, unmarried man -- not any unmarried male, which would include the Pope and a 3-year-old boy)

Narrowing and broadening are not distinct mechanisms. They are different outcomes of a single process of ad hoc concept construction guided by relevance. Sometimes both happen simultaneously: "He drinks" can involve both narrowing (to alcoholic drinks) and broadening (to include beer, wine, spirits -- a specific range).

### 2.4 Higher-Level Explicatures

Beyond the basic explicature (the enriched propositional content), RT recognizes **higher-level explicatures** -- propositions in which the basic explicature is embedded under a description of:

1. **The speaker's propositional attitude** (what they believe, hope, fear, regret, etc.)
2. **The speech act being performed** (asserting, warning, promising, advising, etc.)

**Examples:**
- Basic explicature: The boss is coming [to this office, soon]
- Higher-level explicature (attitude): The speaker **believes** that the boss is coming to this office soon
- Higher-level explicature (speech act): The speaker is **warning** [me] that the boss is coming to this office soon

Higher-level explicatures are communicated by a combination of:
- **Linguistic cues**: sentence type (declarative, imperative, interrogative), mood markers, evidentials
- **Prosodic cues**: tone of voice, stress, intonation patterns
- **Contextual cues**: the situation, the relationship between speakers, what's at stake

The speech-act description embedded in a higher-level explicature is how RT handles the full range of illocutionary forces without needing a separate speech-act theory. "Warning," "threatening," "promising," "suggesting" -- these are all higher-level explicatures derived pragmatically.

### 2.5 The Explicature/Implicature Boundary

The boundary between explicature and implicature is defined by the **development-of-logical-form criterion** (Carston):

> A communicated proposition is an **explicature** if and only if it is a development of the logical form encoded by the utterance. Any other communicated proposition is an **implicature**.

In practice:
- **Explicatures** are derived by local processes that operate on subpropositional constituents -- modifying, completing, and enriching parts of the decoded linguistic form
- **Implicatures** are derived by global inference from fully propositional premises -- combining the explicature with contextual assumptions to derive new conclusions

**Why this distinction matters:**
1. It shows that "explicit" communication is not the same as "coded" communication. Much of what is explicitly communicated is inferred, not decoded
2. It reveals that pragmatic inference plays a role at every level of interpretation, not just at the level of "reading between the lines"
3. For autistic learners, it means that difficulty with pragmatics is not just about "missing the implication" -- pragmatic processing is needed even to understand the basic, explicit meaning of many utterances

## 3. Ad Hoc Concepts and Lexical Pragmatics

### 3.1 The Core Claim: Words Don't Have Fixed Meanings

One of the most radical insights from RT-based lexical pragmatics is that **the concept a word contributes to an utterance is frequently not the concept it encodes**. Words are not containers with fixed meanings that are simply unpacked in context. Instead, words are **pointers** toward conceptual regions, and the actual concept communicated is an **ad hoc concept** -- constructed on the fly, for that specific occasion, guided by the search for optimal relevance.

This theory was developed primarily by **Robyn Carston** (building on earlier work by Sperber and Wilson) and presented in detail in her 2002 book and subsequent papers.

### 3.2 The Notation

Ad hoc concepts are distinguished from encoded concepts using an **asterisk notation**:

- The encoded concept DRINK (the concept stored in your mental lexicon, associated with the word "drink") has a certain denotation: all drinkable liquids
- The ad hoc concept DRINK* (the concept actually communicated in "He drinks") has a different, narrower denotation: alcoholic beverages
- The ad hoc concept RAW* (communicated in "The steak is raw") has a different, broader denotation: undercooked (extending beyond literally uncooked)
- The ad hoc concept ROUND* (communicated in "The lake is round") has a broader denotation: roughly circular

The asterisk marks the concept as **occasion-specific**: it was constructed for this particular communicative event and may never be constructed in exactly the same way again.

### 3.3 Types of Ad Hoc Concept Construction

#### Narrowing
The ad hoc concept's denotation is a **proper subset** of the encoded concept's denotation.

| Utterance | Encoded Concept | Ad Hoc Concept | What Changes |
|---|---|---|---|
| "I need a drink" | DRINK (any potable liquid) | DRINK* (alcoholic beverage) | Denotation restricted |
| "She cut him" (social context) | CUT (divide with a sharp instrument) | CUT* (socially snubbed) | Denotation restricted to a metaphorical subset |
| "He's a good man" | GOOD (morally acceptable) | GOOD* (kind, generous, reliable in ways relevant to this context) | Denotation restricted to specific qualities |

#### Broadening (Loosening)
The ad hoc concept's denotation is a **superset** of (or extends beyond) the encoded concept's denotation.

| Utterance | Encoded Concept | Ad Hoc Concept | What Changes |
|---|---|---|---|
| "The steak is raw" | RAW (completely uncooked) | RAW* (very undercooked) | Denotation extended |
| "Holland is flat" | FLAT (perfectly horizontal) | FLAT* (relatively level) | Denotation extended |
| "The ATM swallowed my card" | SWALLOWED (ingested) | SWALLOWED* (took in and retained) | Denotation extended metaphorically |

#### Approximation
A special case of broadening where the communicated concept is "close to" but not identical with the encoded concept.

| Utterance | What's Happening |
|---|---|
| "The lake is round" | The lake is approximately circular -- ROUND* = roughly circular |
| "The table is square" | SQUARE* = roughly square-shaped |
| "The building is 100 meters tall" | 100* = approximately 100 (maybe 98 or 103) |

#### Metaphor as Extreme Broadening
Metaphor, in this framework, is not a special, distinct phenomenon. It is simply an **extreme case of broadening** -- the ad hoc concept extends so far beyond the encoded concept that the two barely overlap (or don't overlap at all in terms of literal denotation).

| Utterance | Encoded Concept | Ad Hoc Concept | Degree of Broadening |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Holland is flat" | FLAT | FLAT* (relatively level) | Slight broadening (approximation) |
| "I've told you a million times" | MILLION | MILLION* (very many) | Moderate broadening (hyperbole) |
| "She demolished his argument" | DEMOLISHED | DEMOLISHED* (thoroughly refuted) | Major broadening (metaphor) |
| "He's a bulldozer" | BULLDOZER | BULLDOZER* (forceful, insensitive person) | Extreme broadening (metaphor) |

### 3.4 The Continuum: A Unified Account

The crucial theoretical point is that there is **no qualitative distinction** between literal use, loose use, approximation, hyperbole, and metaphor. They are all points on a continuum, all produced by the same cognitive process (ad hoc concept construction guided by relevance), and all interpreted by the same comprehension procedure.

**The continuum:**

```
Literal use ←→ Slight loosening ←→ Approximation ←→ Hyperbole ←→ Metaphor
(encoded concept = communicated concept) ...................... (encoded concept ≠ communicated concept)
```

This means:
1. There is no "metaphor detection" mechanism. Hearers do not need to first try a literal interpretation, fail, and then "switch" to a figurative mode
2. ALL language use potentially involves ad hoc concept construction. Even "literal" uses may involve minor adjustments
3. The same pragmatic process (guided by the search for optimal relevance) handles ALL cases

### 3.5 Why This Matters: All Language Use Is Pragmatic

The traditional view splits language into two categories: "literal" language (where words mean what they encode) and "figurative" language (where special pragmatic processing is needed). RT's lexical pragmatics demolishes this split:

- **Even "literal" uses involve pragmatic processing:** When someone says "I need a drink" and means water, the hearer still needs to pragmatically identify DRINK* as contextually appropriate (maybe they're at a water fountain, not a bar)
- **Metaphorical uses involve no special mechanism:** The same relevance-guided concept-adjustment process that produces "literal" interpretations also produces metaphorical ones
- **The categories blur:** Is "He's cold" (meaning emotionally distant) a metaphor? A narrowing? A broadening? It doesn't matter -- the same process handles it regardless of classification

**For autistic learners:** This is potentially liberating. Instead of needing a separate set of rules for "figurative language" (which is often cited as difficult for autistic individuals), the RT account says that all language interpretation uses the same basic process. If you can learn the process (construct the ad hoc concept that makes the utterance optimally relevant), it works across the entire continuum.

## 4. The Comprehension Procedure in Full Detail

### 4.1 The Procedure Restated

The relevance-theoretic comprehension procedure (Wilson & Sperber 2004):

> **a.** Follow a path of least effort in computing cognitive effects: test interpretive hypotheses (disambiguations, reference resolutions, implicatures, etc.) in order of accessibility.
>
> **b.** Stop when your expectations of relevance are satisfied.

### 4.2 The Three Parallel Subtasks

When processing an utterance, the mind engages in three simultaneous subtasks:

1. **Constructing hypotheses about explicatures** (explicit content) -- via decoding, disambiguation, reference assignment, saturation, free enrichment, and ad hoc concept construction
2. **Constructing hypotheses about implicated premises** -- accessing relevant contextual assumptions from memory
3. **Constructing hypotheses about implicated conclusions** -- deriving conclusions from the combination of explicatures and implicated premises

### 4.3 Mutual Parallel Adjustment: The Key Innovation

**This is NOT a sequential process.** The hearer does NOT:
1. First fully decode the utterance
2. Then fully determine the explicature
3. Then search for implicated premises
4. Then derive implicated conclusions

Instead, **all three subtasks run in parallel and inform each other**. This is called **mutual parallel adjustment**:

- A tentative hypothesis about what the speaker **implicated** can help determine what they **explicitly said** (if you suspect the speaker is trying to warn you, this helps you narrow down the explicit meaning of their words)
- A tentative hypothesis about the **explicature** can suggest what **contextual assumptions** to search for
- Accessing a particular **contextual assumption** can suggest a particular **implicature**, which in turn can help refine the **explicature**

**Example -- "She handed him her key and he opened the door":**

The mutual adjustment process might work like this:
- DECODE: "She handed him her key and he opened the door"
- TENTATIVE EXPLICATURE begins forming: [A specific woman] handed [a specific man] her key and he opened [a specific] door
- IMPLICATED PREMISE accessed: Keys are used to open locked doors → this strengthens the hypothesis that "her key" refers to the key TO the door
- IMPLICATED CONCLUSION: She gave him the key SO THAT he could open the door → this strengthens the temporal enrichment (the giving happened BEFORE the opening)
- EXPLICATURE refined: She [= specific referent] handed him [= specific referent] her key [to that door] and [then, using that key] he opened the [specific] door

All of this happens essentially simultaneously, with each component informing the others.

### 4.4 The "First Satisfactory Interpretation" Principle

The procedure instructs the hearer to **stop at the first interpretation that satisfies expectations of relevance.** This is an efficiency mechanism:

- You do not search for the BEST interpretation -- just the first GOOD ENOUGH one
- "Good enough" means: it provides sufficient cognitive effects to justify the processing effort, and it is compatible with the speaker's abilities and preferences
- If the first accessible interpretation meets this bar, processing stops even if alternative interpretations exist

**Why this matters:**
- It explains why we interpret utterances so FAST -- we don't exhaustively evaluate all possible meanings
- It explains why misunderstandings occur -- sometimes the first satisfactory interpretation isn't what the speaker intended
- It explains why communication is generally efficient but imperfect

### 4.5 How the Procedure Handles Ambiguity

When a word or structure is ambiguous, the comprehension procedure does not compare all possible meanings and choose the best. Instead:

1. The **most accessible** meaning is tested first (accessibility depends on frequency, recency, contextual priming, and other factors)
2. If this meaning yields an interpretation that satisfies relevance expectations, processing stops -- the other meaning is never even considered
3. Only if the first meaning fails to yield a satisfactory interpretation does the processor move to the next most accessible meaning

**Example -- "bank":**
- Context: a conversation about finances → BANK (financial institution) is most accessible → tested first → satisfies relevance → processing stops. You never consciously consider "river bank"
- Context: a nature walk → BANK (riverside) is most accessible → tested first → satisfies relevance → processing stops

**For autistic learners:** The procedure is genuinely algorithmic. It has:
- An **input**: the decoded linguistic form plus contextual information
- A **process**: construct interpretive hypotheses in order of accessibility
- A **halting condition**: stop when relevance expectations are satisfied
- A **fallback**: if current hypothesis fails, try the next most accessible one

### 4.6 Worked Example: Full Procedure for a Complex Utterance

**Context:** Alice and Bob are colleagues. It's Monday morning. Alice looks tired. Bob says: "Looks like someone had a good weekend."

**Step-by-step parallel processing:**

**Decode:**
- Linguistic form: "looks like someone had a good weekend"
- Structural ambiguity: "someone" could refer to Alice or to an unspecified third party

**Construct explicature hypotheses (parallel with other subtasks):**
- Reference assignment: "someone" → most accessible referent in context = Alice (she is present and salient)
- Ad hoc concept construction: "good" → GOOD* -- in context, with Alice looking tired, GOOD* might need adjustment. Is it literal (pleasant, enjoyable)? Or is it ironic?
- Temporal enrichment: "weekend" → the most recent weekend (just passed)
- "Looks like" → the speaker is making an observation based on evidence (Alice's appearance)

**Access implicated premises (parallel):**
- People who have very active/late weekends look tired on Monday
- "Good weekend" in informal speech often means social activities, drinking, staying out late
- The speaker can see that Alice looks tired (this is mutually manifest)

**Construct implicated conclusions (parallel):**
- Alice had a very socially active weekend (staying out late, partying, etc.)
- Alice's tiredness is self-inflicted (from fun activities, not illness)
- The speaker is being playfully teasing

**Mutual adjustment:**
- The implicated conclusion (teasing about partying) adjusts the explicature: GOOD* → GOOD** (very socially active, perhaps excessively so, in a way that causes tiredness)
- This in turn confirms the reference assignment: "someone" = Alice (not a third party)
- And it suggests a higher-level explicature: the speaker is **teasing** Alice about her weekend

**Check against relevance expectations:**
- Does this interpretation provide adequate cognitive effects? YES -- it explains Alice's tiredness, creates social bonding through humor, and acknowledges Alice's state
- Is it accessible? YES -- it required minimal processing given the contextual cues
- Are relevance expectations satisfied? YES → **STOP**

**Final interpretation:**
- Explicature: It looks like Alice had a very active/indulgent weekend
- Higher-level explicature: Bob is teasingly observing this based on Alice's tired appearance
- Implicated premise: People who party hard look tired the next workday
- Implicated conclusion: Alice's tiredness is due to social activities over the weekend; Bob is amused by this

### 4.7 How the Procedure Handles Vagueness and Underdetermination

Vagueness is pervasive in natural language ("tall," "old," "near," "soon," "a lot"). The comprehension procedure handles vagueness by constructing an ad hoc concept that is precise ENOUGH to satisfy relevance expectations:

- "I'll be there soon" → SOON* = within a timeframe that is relevant given the current context (5 minutes if you're waiting at a restaurant; 20 minutes if you're waiting at home)
- The hearer doesn't need a precise value -- they need an ad hoc concept precise enough to yield adequate cognitive effects (knowing roughly when to expect the speaker)
- If the resulting imprecision is too great (the hearer really needs to know whether it's 5 or 20 minutes), the interpretation won't satisfy relevance expectations, and the hearer will seek clarification

## 5. Cognitive Environment and Mutual Manifestness (Deep Dive)

### 5.1 The Problem with "Shared Knowledge"

Traditional pragmatic theories (Grice, Stalnaker, Clark) rely on the concept of **common ground** or **mutual knowledge**: a body of information that both speaker and hearer know, and each knows the other knows, and each knows the other knows they know, and so on to infinity.

**The infinite regress problem (Sperber & Wilson 1986):** Mutual knowledge, strictly defined, generates an infinite recursion:
- A knows that p
- B knows that p
- A knows that B knows that p
- B knows that A knows that p
- A knows that B knows that A knows that p
- ... and so on, infinitely

This infinite chain can never be completed in a finite mind. You can never VERIFY that you have mutual knowledge, because doing so would require checking infinitely many levels of meta-knowledge. As Sperber and Wilson argue, the concept of mutual knowledge is psychologically unrealistic -- no one actually verifies mutual knowledge through such a chain.

### 5.2 The Solution: Mutual Manifestness

Sperber and Wilson replace "mutual knowledge" with the weaker, more psychologically realistic concept of **mutual manifestness**:

**Key definitions:**

| Concept | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Manifest** | A fact or assumption is manifest to an individual if they are **capable** of representing it mentally and accepting it as true or probably true at that time |
| **Cognitive environment** | The total set of assumptions manifest to an individual -- everything they know, perceive, or could infer given their current situation |
| **Mutual cognitive environment** | A set of assumptions that is manifest to two or more individuals, where the sharing itself is also manifest |
| **Mutually manifest** | An assumption is mutually manifest to A and B if it is manifest in a mutual cognitive environment they share |

**The critical difference from mutual knowledge:**
- **Knowledge** requires that you actually BELIEVE something to be true
- **Manifestness** requires only that you be CAPABLE of representing it as true
- You don't need to actually know what the other person knows -- you just need certain assumptions to be available (manifest) to both of you
- Manifestness comes in **degrees** (some assumptions are more manifest than others), whereas knowledge is typically treated as binary (you either know something or you don't)

### 5.3 How Manifestness Works

**Two properties of manifest assumptions:**

1. **Accessibility** -- how easily the assumption comes to mind. Highly accessible assumptions are those directly perceptible, recently mentioned, or strongly primed by context
2. **Strength** -- how confidently the assumption is held. Strong assumptions are those based on direct perception or reliable testimony; weak assumptions are those based on remote inference or uncertain sources

Together, accessibility and strength determine the **degree of manifestness** of an assumption.

**Example:** You and a colleague are standing at a window watching it rain.
- That it is raining is **highly manifest** to both of you (directly perceivable, strongly held)
- That the outdoor team lunch is probably cancelled is **moderately manifest** (requires a simple inference from the mutually manifest fact of rain)
- That the colleague who organized the lunch might be disappointed is **weakly manifest** (requires inference about someone else's mental state)

All three assumptions are manifest to both of you, but to different degrees. Communication can exploit any of these levels.

### 5.4 Why This Matters: You Don't Need to Read Minds

The shift from "shared knowledge" to "mutual manifestness" has a profound implication:

**You don't need to know what someone else KNOWS. You only need to identify what is MANIFEST to both of you.**

What is manifest is often directly observable:
- The physical environment (what you can both see, hear, feel)
- What has just been said in the conversation
- The social setting and its conventions
- Obvious facts about the situation

This is potentially much more tractable than trying to model another person's entire knowledge state.

### 5.5 Connection to Autistic Communication

This concept has been directly applied to autism by Williams, Wharton, and Jagoe (2021):

**The mutual manifestness mismatch:** Communication difficulties between autistic and non-autistic people may arise not because autistic individuals "lack" pragmatic competence but because the two parties have **different cognitive environments** -- different things are salient, accessible, and strongly held for each person.

What is manifest involves perception, attention, and inference:
- If autistic individuals have **different perceptual sensitivities** (e.g., heightened awareness of certain sensory inputs), their cognitive environments will contain different highly manifest assumptions
- If attention allocation differs (as monotropism theory suggests), different assumptions will be most accessible
- If inferential patterns differ, different conclusions will be derivable from the same perceptual input

The result: what is mutually manifest may be a smaller set, or a differently configured set, than either party realizes. Communication difficulty follows not from individual deficit but from **reduced overlap in cognitive environments**.

**The practical advantage:** "What is manifest to both of us in this situation?" is a more concrete, answerable question than "What does this person know?" You can look at the physical context, the conversation history, and the obvious features of the situation to identify mutually manifest assumptions. This is an analytical, observable process rather than a mind-reading exercise.

## 6. Procedural vs. Conceptual Meaning

### 6.1 The Distinction

Within RT, **Diane Blakemore** (1987, 2002) introduced a fundamental distinction between two types of encoded linguistic meaning:

1. **Conceptual meaning**: Words that encode CONCEPTS -- mental representations that contribute to the propositional content of an utterance. Most content words (nouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs) encode concepts.
   - "cat" encodes the concept CAT
   - "red" encodes the concept RED
   - "run" encodes the concept RUN

2. **Procedural meaning**: Words or expressions that encode PROCEDURES -- instructions about how to process the conceptual content. They do not contribute to propositional content but rather constrain and guide the inferential phase of interpretation.
   - "but" encodes a procedure: treat the following as contrasting with or contradicting what came before
   - "so" encodes a procedure: treat the following as a conclusion drawn from what came before
   - "however" encodes a procedure: treat the following as an unexpected contrast
   - "well" encodes a procedure: adjust your expectations about the upcoming response

### 6.2 Discourse Markers as Procedural Encodings

Many discourse markers and connectives have **procedural rather than conceptual meaning**. They don't add to what is said; they tell the hearer HOW to process what is said:

| Expression | Procedural Instruction |
|---|---|
| **but** | Process next segment as contrasting with or overriding the previous one |
| **so** | Process next segment as a conclusion from the previous one |
| **after all** | Process next segment as a premise supporting a conclusion already derived |
| **however** | Process next segment as an unexpected contrast |
| **moreover** | Process next segment as additional support for the same point |
| **well** | The upcoming response may not straightforwardly satisfy your expectations |
| **oh** | The speaker has just undergone a change in cognitive state (new realization) |

### 6.3 Why This Matters for Non-Verbal Communication

The conceptual/procedural distinction extends beyond words to non-verbal signals:

- **Facial expressions** (a frown, a smile, raised eyebrows) typically encode **procedural** meaning -- they provide instructions about the speaker's attitude or emotional state, constraining how the hearer should process the accompanying verbal content
- **Prosody** (stress, intonation, rhythm) provides **procedural** cues that guide interpretation -- contrastive stress tells the hearer which element is the focus; rising intonation may signal a question or uncertainty
- **Gestures** can encode either conceptual content (pointing to identify a referent) or procedural content (a dismissive wave indicating the previous topic should be abandoned)

## 7. RT and Communication Beyond Language

### 7.1 Ostensive-Inferential Communication Is Not Limited to Language

A central claim of RT is that the framework applies to **all ostensive-inferential communication**, not just verbal communication. Any behavior that is designed to attract the audience's attention and convey the communicator's intention to communicate falls within the scope of the theory.

This includes:
- Spoken and written language
- Gesture and pointing
- Facial expressions
- Tone of voice (prosody)
- Body language and posture
- Showing objects or evidence
- Demonstrative actions (showing someone how to do something)

### 7.2 The Showing/Meaning Distinction

**Tim Wharton** (2009, *Pragmatics and Non-Verbal Communication*), building on Sperber and Wilson's earlier work, developed a crucial distinction between **showing** and **meaning** (in Grice's sense of non-natural meaning):

**Showing:** Providing evidence from which the audience can draw conclusions. The communicator displays something (a facial expression, an empty glass, a wound) and the audience infers the relevant information from the evidence.

**Meaning:** Communicating through a code (natural language) where the connection between signal and message is conventional. The audience recognizes the communicator's intention to communicate a specific message.

**The continuum:** Sperber and Wilson argue that showing and meaning are not categorically distinct but form a **continuum**:

```
Pure showing ←────────────────────→ Pure meaning (coding)
(holding up an empty glass)         ("I'm thirsty")
        ↑                                    ↑
    Evidence-based                    Convention-based
    Less controlled                   More controlled
    More "natural"                    More "arbitrary"
```

**Examples along the continuum:**
- **Pure showing**: Holding up your empty glass at a bar (you're showing evidence that your glass is empty, from which the bartender can infer you want a refill)
- **Natural signal used ostensively**: Shivering dramatically when someone asks if you're cold (the shivering is a natural response to cold, but by performing it ostensively -- making sure the other person sees it -- you're using it communicatively)
- **Partly coded, partly shown**: Saying "I'm fine" in a tone of voice that shows you are clearly NOT fine (the words encode one meaning; the prosody shows the opposite)
- **Fully coded**: Saying "Could you please get me another drink?"

### 7.3 How RT Handles Prosody

Wilson and Wharton (2006, "Relevance and Prosody") analyzed how prosodic features (stress, intonation, rhythm, voice quality) contribute to utterance interpretation:

**Prosody provides both natural and coded input:**
- **Natural input**: Emotional states are naturally expressed through voice quality (a trembling voice shows fear; a raised voice shows anger). These are not coded but provide evidence from which the hearer infers the speaker's emotional state
- **Coded (procedural) input**: Contrastive stress, focus intonation, and question intonation encode procedural instructions about how to process the utterance

**Example -- "I didn't say he STOLE the money":**
The placement of contrastive stress radically changes the interpretation:
- "**I** didn't say he stole the money" (someone else said it)
- "I didn't **say** he stole the money" (I implied it)
- "I didn't say **he** stole the money" (I said someone else did)
- "I didn't say he **stole** the money" (I said he borrowed/found it)
- "I didn't say he stole the **money**" (I said he stole something else)

Each stress pattern provides a procedural instruction: "Focus on THIS element; it is the one that is being contrasted with alternatives."

### 7.4 How RT Handles Gesture and Facial Expression

Non-verbal signals contribute to interpretation in RT through the general comprehension procedure:

1. **They are ostensive stimuli**: A deliberate gesture or facial expression is designed to attract attention and convey information, so it triggers the presumption of optimal relevance
2. **They provide evidence**: The hearer processes them as evidence about the speaker's mental state, attitude, or intended meaning
3. **They constrain interpretation**: They narrow down which interpretation of the verbal content satisfies relevance expectations

**Example:** Someone says "That was a great movie" while making a face of disgust.
- Verbal content suggests a positive evaluation
- Facial expression (natural signal used ostensively) provides evidence of a negative evaluation
- The hearer must find an interpretation that reconciles both → irony/sarcasm (the speaker is being ironic about the movie's quality)
- The facial expression constrains the interpretation of the verbal content, guiding the hearer to the ironic reading

### 7.5 Why This Matters for Autistic Individuals

Non-verbal communication is often cited as particularly challenging for autistic individuals. RT provides a useful framework:

- **Non-verbal signals are NOT a separate communication system** -- they are part of the same ostensive-inferential process that handles verbal communication
- **They can be analyzed using the same procedure** -- what cognitive effects does this signal produce? What interpretation makes the overall communication optimally relevant?
- **The showing/meaning continuum** means that non-verbal signals range from highly transparent (holding up an empty glass) to highly coded (a subtle facial expression), and it's legitimate to find some points on the continuum easier than others
- **Procedural encoding** means that many non-verbal signals function as INSTRUCTIONS for processing, not as additional propositions to comprehend. Understanding "a raised eyebrow means to question or express surprise about what was just said" is a procedural instruction that can be learned explicitly

## 8. Strong vs. Weak Communication and Poetic Effects

### 8.1 The Strength Scale

RT recognizes that communicated assumptions vary in how **strongly** or **weakly** they are communicated:

**Strong communication**: The speaker makes a specific proposition so highly manifest that the hearer is expected to derive it. The speaker would consider the communication unsuccessful if the hearer failed to derive this assumption.

**Weak communication**: The speaker makes a range of assumptions slightly more manifest, without expecting the hearer to derive any specific one. The effect is a general impression, mood, or attitude rather than a specific proposition.

### 8.2 Weak Implicatures and Poetic Effects

An utterance achieves **poetic effects** when most of its relevance comes from a wide range of **weak implicatures** -- none of which is individually essential, but which collectively create a rich, evocative impression.

**Example -- "He has a square mind":**
This weakly implicates:
- He is rigid in his thinking
- He does not easily change his mind
- He follows rules strictly
- He sees things in black and white
- He is conventional, not creative
- He is systematic but inflexible

No single one of these is THE implicature. The speaker is not committed to any specific one. The richness of the metaphor lies in the array of weakly implicated properties, which together create a complex impression.

**For literary language:** Literature achieves much of its power through weak implicatures. A line of poetry may suggest dozens of loosely related implications, none of which is definitively intended. The reader's willingness to invest extra processing effort is rewarded by the richness and evocativeness of the resulting interpretation. The extra processing effort is justified by the additional (poetic) cognitive effects.

### 8.3 Implications for Communication Styles

This framework has implications for different communication styles:

| Communication Style | Strength | Processing Effort | Cognitive Effects |
|---|---|---|---|
| Direct, literal | Strong | Low | Specific, determinate |
| Formal, precise | Strong | Moderate | Specific, carefully qualified |
| Casual, colloquial | Mixed | Low | Mix of explicit and loosely communicated |
| Poetic, evocative | Weak | High | Rich, indeterminate, suggestive |
| Ironic, sarcastic | Varies | Higher | Includes attitude, dissociation |

Autistic individuals may find **strong communication** more comfortable (clear, specific, determinate) while finding **weak communication** more challenging (because it requires generating and evaluating a range of loosely communicated possibilities). RT provides a framework for understanding WHY this is the case (weak communication requires more inferential work across a wider set of possible interpretations) and for developing strategies for handling it.

## 10. Key Terminology (New Terms Not in Category 2 Document)

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Semantic underdeterminacy** | The thesis that linguistic meaning radically underdetermines the proposition communicated; extensive pragmatic enrichment is needed even for "literal" meaning |
| **Logical form** | The incomplete, subpropositional representation decoded from the linguistic signal, which needs pragmatic enrichment to become a full proposition |
| **Saturation** | Filling in contextually mandated variables (e.g., "It's raining" → WHERE?). A linguistically controlled pragmatic process |
| **Free enrichment** | Adding conceptual material not mandated by the linguistic form (e.g., "I've had breakfast" → THIS MORNING). A pragmatically driven process |
| **Unarticulated constituent** | A component of the communicated proposition that has no corresponding element in the linguistic form |
| **Higher-level explicature** | A proposition in which the basic explicature is embedded under a speech-act or propositional-attitude description (e.g., "The speaker is warning that...") |
| **Ad hoc concept** | A context-specific concept constructed on the fly by narrowing or broadening an encoded concept. Notated with an asterisk: DRINK*, RAW*, FLAT* |
| **Narrowing** | Restricting the denotation of an encoded concept to a subset (DRINK → DRINK* = alcoholic drinks) |
| **Broadening / Loosening** | Extending the denotation of an encoded concept beyond its literal range (RAW → RAW* = undercooked) |
| **Conceptual meaning** | Linguistic meaning that encodes concepts contributing to propositional content |
| **Procedural meaning** | Linguistic meaning that encodes instructions for processing conceptual content (Blakemore's contribution) |
| **Showing** | Communicating by providing evidence from which the audience infers the intended information (contrast with "meaning" in Grice's sense) |
| **Strong communication** | Communication where specific assumptions are made highly manifest |
| **Weak communication** | Communication where a range of assumptions are made slightly more manifest, creating impressions rather than determinate propositions |
| **Poetic effects** | Cognitive effects achieved through a wide range of weak implicatures |
| **Mutual parallel adjustment** | The process by which hypotheses about explicatures, implicated premises, and implicated conclusions are simultaneously constructed and refined |
| **Accommodation** | The process by which hearers adjust their cognitive environment to accept presupposed content they did not previously hold |
