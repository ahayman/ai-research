Validator: independent, no researcher context inherited. Method: read files; extract discrete high-stakes claims (citations, statistics, framework summaries, autism-specific empirical claims); verify each via WebSearch/WebFetch against primary sources or aggregators; rate as CONFIRMED / UNCERTAIN (X/10) / INVALID. Focus was on citation existence, framework accuracy, autism-specific empirical claims, and deficit-model language.

## Systemic issues

- **"High-functioning" usage.** The term appears 8 times across Chunk 1 files, mostly when reproducing paper titles (Sirota 2004, Mazzaggio et al. 2021, etc.) — that is legitimate citation fidelity. However, it is used in the hub's own editorial voice at least once: `category-02-implicature-research-autism.md` line 157 ("Many high-functioning autistic individuals understand..."). This is the kind of mild drift the instructions say to flag. Recommended remediation: reword as "many autistic individuals with fluent spoken language" or similar.
- **"Deficit" language.** 87 hits for deficit-family words across the chunk, but the overwhelming majority are (a) in quoted/cited material, (b) arguments AGAINST deficit framing (e.g., "not a deficit", "reframing deficit as difference"), or (c) direct quotes from older literature. I found no instance where the hub itself originates a deficit framing in its own voice. The framing is consistently neurodiversity-affirming; this is strong.
- **The Wahyunianto percentages (67.65% / 20.59% / 2.94%).** Cited as if authoritative but the underlying study is a Master's-level pragmatic analysis of **5 children** at a single special school (SLBN Surakarta, Indonesia), published in a regional Indonesian journal (Sosiohumaniora, 2020). The percentages are numerically correct but massively overweighted in how the hub presents them. Appearing in three files (`category-01-speech-acts-research-autism.md`, `category-02-implicature-research-autism.md`, `category-02-implicature-research-grice.md`) risks readers treating an n=5 single-site study as a population-level finding. Recommended remediation: keep the citation but add a caveat ("a small single-site Indonesian study of 5 children") and strip the table-of-percentages presentation from the main hub files.
- **Two citation-format drift items.** Sirota 2004 is cited as *Discourse Studies* in one file and *Discourse & Society* in another (`category-03-politeness-research-positive.md` line 974). The actual journal is *Discourse Studies* (SAGE). Minor inconsistency to normalize in cleanup.
- **The "Intercultural Cognitive Pragmatics (ICP) Framework (2026)"** in `category-03-politeness-research-autism.md` Section 6.5. Citation is real (Żuromski & Pacholik-Żuromska, Frontiers in Human Neuroscience, 2026, doi 10.3389/fnhum.2026.1729076) but this is a very recent theoretical paper and the hub leans on it heavily as the framing pivot for cross-cultural autism pragmatics. Not wrong, but worth hedging as "a recently proposed framework" rather than treating it as established.
