# Concept Research: The Wellness Industry, the Polyvagal Institute & Commercial Interests

## Overview

Polyvagal theory began as a 1994 academic address in a specialty psychophysiology journal. By 2026 it has migrated through four distinct registers of usage. A Google search for "polyvagal theory psychotherapy" returns roughly 120,000 results, dominated by popular self-help books, social-media practitioner accounts, course platforms, and direct-to-consumer device manufacturers. The same words — "ventral vagal," "social engagement system," "neuroception," "polyvagal-informed" — mean very different things depending on whether they appear in a peer-reviewed journal, a clinical handbook, an Instagram reel, or a supplement label.

The user's stated trust concern is the wellness-industry register. This chapter maps the institutional landscape (who controls the theory's framing, who teaches it, who certifies practitioners), traces the money flow (patents, royalties, course revenue, device sales), surveys the consumer-product ecosystem (devices, supplements, books, social media), and provides a reusable practitioner-evaluation checklist. The chapter does not impugn motives. Many people working in this space are sincerely trying to help people who have been failed by mainstream care. The issue is empirical and structural, not personal: a contested theory has been adopted as a marketing framework, and the framework's claims have run far ahead of the evidence.

The chapter's core analytical tool is **the three registers**: academic (contested), clinical (mixed evidence, some real value), and wellness/influencer (often pseudoscientific). Knowing which register a given claim comes from is the user's most useful filter when encountering polyvagal-branded content.

## Core Components

### Component 1: The Polyvagal Institute (PVI)

- **Status**: 501(c)(3) non-profit, EIN 84-5084757, headquartered in Denver, Colorado
- **Founded**: 2020 by Stephen Porges, Deb Dana, Karen Onderko, and Randall Redfield
- **Mission**: To advance the application of polyvagal theory through education, training, research support, and community building
- **Activities (from Form 990)**: Training (~80%), Facilitating research (~10%), Community building (~10%)
- **Revenue (2023 Form 990)**: $902,422 gross receipts; expenses $535,292; net income $367,130; net assets $450,531 at year-end
- **Revenue (2024 Form 990 filed)**: $806,914 gross receipts; expenses $751,767; net income $55,147; net assets $505,678 at year-end
- **Grants made (2024)**: $0
- **Offerings**:
  - **Polyvagal Certificate Course**: 6-month cohort-based program (35 CEs available for licensed professionals). Cohort 13 (April–November 2026) is the current advertised version. Tuition is in the four-figure range based on comparable cohort pricing.
  - **Polyvagal-Informed level credential**: Granted on certificate-course completion. Permits self-identification on CVs and professional materials.
  - **Polyvagal-Informed Certification for Organizations**: For schools, healthcare systems, workplaces.
  - **Topic-specific courses**: Including "Embodied Equity" (an 8-week certificate for educators/helping professionals, designed by Niki Elliott), Deb Dana's "Polyvagal Theory: Principles and Practice for Individuals," and others.
  - **Learning library**: Webinars, on-demand content.
  - **Partner courses**: With organizations including the University of San Diego Professional & Continuing Education.
- **Relationships**: Co-founded by people with commercial interests in the theory (Porges via Unyte royalties, Dana via the Rhythm of Regulation business and Unyte advisory role). Tax-exempt status does not preclude these structural relationships; it requires only that no individual receives improper private benefit from the non-profit's operations.

### Component 2: Unyte Health (formerly Integrated Listening Systems / ILS)

- **Status**: For-profit private company
- **History**: Founded as Integrated Listening Systems. Rebranded to Unyte Health after acquiring related listening-therapy assets. Has commercialized SSP since 2017.
- **Primary product**: The **Safe and Sound Protocol (SSP)** — a filtered-music intervention developed and patented by Stephen Porges. Subscription-based; sold through trained mental health professionals (the "Provider" pathway) to clients.
- **Other products**: Rest and Restore Protocol; Focus System; PhysioCam; Voice Pro.
- **Stephen Porges's role**: **Chief Scientific Advisor** at Unyte Health. He licenses SSP technology to Unyte (via Integrated Listening Systems originally) and **receives royalties** on SSP-related patents. He also holds licensed patent rights to NeuralSolution for PhysioCam and other Unyte-marketed product technologies.
- **Deb Dana's role**: **Advisor to Unyte-ILS**. Her clinical framework (Rhythm of Regulation) provides the implementation language that SSP providers use.
- **Pricing**: SSP is delivered via subscription. Therapist subscriptions and per-client access fees vary; the listing structure is on Unyte's site.

- **Royalties from W. W. Norton**: Author of *The Polyvagal Theory* (2011) and subsequent Norton titles. Norton publishes the trauma/interpersonal-neurobiology series that includes works by van der Kolk, Dana, Badenoch, Kase, and Inderbitzen — much of the popular-author chain shares a publisher.

### Component 4: Deb Dana's commercial profile

- **Rhythm of Regulation®**: Trademarked clinical framework. Multi-year program of trainings, workshops, and an on-demand digital course platform.
- **Polyvagal Institute**: Co-founder; teaches PVI courses.
- **Unyte Health**: Advisor.
- **Books (popular and clinical)**: *The Polyvagal Theory in Therapy* (Norton, 2018), *Polyvagal Exercises for Safety and Connection* (Norton, 2020), *Anchored* (Sounds True, 2021 — the consumer-facing self-help version), and parenting-applied work.
- **Reach**: Dana is the single most influential popularizer of polyvagal theory among practicing therapists. Her ladder graphic (ventral / sympathetic / dorsal) is the version most often shared on social media.

### Component 5: The popular-author chain (publishers and reach)

A handful of trade-publisher imprints have powered polyvagal theory's migration from neuroscience journal to bestseller list and from there into the wellness ecosystem.

- **Bessel van der Kolk**: *The Body Keeps the Score* (Viking, 2014). Has sold many millions of copies; weeks on the New York Times bestseller list measured in years, not months. Cites and popularizes polyvagal theory as an explanatory framework for trauma's bodily presentation. Critics (Kristen Martin / Washington Post; *Research on Social Work Practice* 2023 editorial) have specifically named polyvagal theory among the "uncertain science" the book promotes.
- **Deb Dana**: Series of Norton titles plus *Anchored* (Sounds True). See Component 4.
- **Holly Bridges**: *Reframe Your Thinking Around Autism: How the Polyvagal Theory and Brain Plasticity Help Us Make Sense of Autism*. First edition Jessica Kingsley Publishers (2015); second edition LicenseToThink Pty Ltd / Zebr (2022/2023). Develops the "Autism Reframe Technique" (A.R.T.). The book proposes that autism symptoms are a learned nervous-system response to prolonged flight/fight rather than a stable neurodevelopmental difference — a framing that the autistic community has heavily contested.
- **Sean M. Inderbitzen, DSW, LCSW**: *Autism in Polyvagal Terms: New Possibilities and Interventions* (W. W. Norton, 2024; ISBN 9781324053217). Norton Series on Interpersonal Neurobiology. Stephen Porges wrote the **foreword** (note: Porges is not a co-author of this title, despite some bibliographic listings using the foreword author as a co-author for catalog purposes). Inderbitzen is described as an autistic therapist; the book proposes polyvagal-framed interventions for individuals with autism.
- **Rebecca Kase, LCSW**: *Polyvagal-Informed EMDR: A Neuro-Informed Approach to Healing* (W. W. Norton, 2023). The first book integrating polyvagal theory with EMDR therapy. Kase runs Kase & CO Training and Consulting, an EMDRIA/APA/NBCC-approved continuing-education provider.
- **Bonnie Badenoch, PhD, LMFT**: *The Heart of Trauma: Healing the Embodied Brain in the Context of Relationships* (Norton, 2017); *Being a Brain-Wise Therapist* (Norton, 2008). Integrates interpersonal neurobiology, polyvagal theory, attachment, memory reconsolidation. Provides much of the bridge into trauma-informed school and parenting applications. Sounds True has published online course versions.

The publisher concentration matters: W. W. Norton (Norton Professional Books and the Interpersonal Neurobiology Series), Penguin/Viking, Sounds True, and Jessica Kingsley together carry most of the canonical polyvagal trade titles. Norton in particular has been the principal vehicle for the theory's transmission from clinical neuroscience to clinical practitioner readership.

### Component 6: Consumer "vagus nerve" devices

This is the most concrete site of overreach the user is asking about. As of 2026, five consumer devices commonly marketed as polyvagal- or vagus-nerve-related are widely available. **One is FDA-cleared (gammaCore, by prescription, for specific indications). The other four are sold under the FDA general wellness device framework, which does not require clinical efficacy demonstration.**

#### gammaCore (electroCore, Inc.)

- **FDA status**: 510(k) cleared. Multiple cleared indications: acute episodic cluster headache pain (2017); acute migraine pain in adults (2018); preventive treatment of cluster headache (2018, first device-or-drug); preventive treatment of adolescent migraine (ages 12–17); paroxysmal hemicrania and hemicrania continua.
- **Mechanism**: Non-invasive cervical vagus nerve stimulation. Handheld device delivering surface electrical stimulation to the cervical branch of the vagus nerve.
- **Cost**: Prescription-only. Insurance coverage varies.
- **Note**: gammaCore is the *only* consumer-accessible non-invasive VNS device with substantive FDA clearance. Clearance is limited to the cleared indications. Off-label use for autism, anxiety, or general "regulation" is not within the cleared indication set.

#### Truvaga (electroCore consumer line)

- **FDA status**: NOT FDA-cleared as a finished consumer wellness product. Uses related electroCore technology but Truvaga itself markets under the general wellness framework.
- **Mechanism claimed**: Non-invasive cervical vagus nerve stimulation.
- **Evidence**: No independent published sham-controlled RCTs of the consumer Truvaga product as marketed. Builds on the gammaCore evidence base but the consumer product is not the same regulatory entity.
- **Cost**: Approximately $499 for the Truvaga Plus device (subscription tier additionally available).

#### Pulsetto

- **FDA status**: NOT FDA-cleared. Sold as a general wellness device. The company notes its FCC certification (which it has) is not equivalent to FDA evaluation, clearance, or approval.
- **Mechanism claimed**: Surface electrical stimulation of the vagus nerve at the anterior neck.
- **Evidence**: No peer-reviewed sham-controlled RCTs as of validation date. Company-reported user counts (>100,000 worldwide in late 2025 per a company press release) are usage metrics, not efficacy data.
- **Cost**: In the $250–$500 range depending on the bundle.

#### Sensate

- **FDA status**: NOT FDA-cleared. Operates under the FDA's general wellness device framework.
- **Mechanism claimed**: Infrasonic resonance (chest-applied vibration) plus synchronized soundscape via headphones. Marketed for parasympathetic/vagal activation.
- **Evidence**: Some manufacturer-funded pilot work; no independent sham-controlled RCT establishing efficacy as marketed.
- **Cost**: Approximately $249–$299.

#### Apollo Neuro

- **FDA status**: NOT FDA-cleared. Manufactured under ISO 13485 quality-system standards (a manufacturing standard distinct from FDA medical-device clearance).
- **Mechanism claimed**: Low-frequency haptic vibration via wristband. The manufacturer cites a proposed pathway through touch-receptor afferents and sympathetic/parasympathetic balance; not direct vagal electrical stimulation.
- **Evidence**: More independent-looking pilot data than competitors. University of Pittsburgh trials and a 2025 *American Journal of Medicine* student RCT exist. Brand-reported clinical-trial count of seven (including double-blind placebo-controlled crossover designs) — independent replication and adequately-powered autism-specific endpoints remain limited. A small pediatric trial (15 children, 7–17, mental-health/ADHD/autism) reported "improvements within minutes" in mood/behavior/engagement; small open-label design.
- **Cost**: Approximately $249–$349.

#### Hapbee

- **FDA status**: NOT FDA-cleared. Company has stated its products are not medical devices and do not require clearance.
- **Mechanism claimed**: Ultra-low-radio-frequency-energy delivery of "magnetic signals" that emulate the molecular effects of substances (e.g., caffeine, melatonin) at the cellular level.
- **Evidence**: Sparse. The company's SEC filings reference plans for clinical trials and FDA approval pathways for some products in the future; current marketing operates under wellness framing.
- **Note**: Hapbee is *less* explicitly vagus-nerve-branded than the others but is frequently grouped with them in the "nervous system reset" wellness device category.
- **Cost**: Subscription-based.

### Component 7: "Vagus nerve" supplements

Supplements marketed with "vagal tone" or "vagus nerve" framing typically include omega-3 fatty acids, magnesium, B-complex vitamins, L-theanine, and probiotics.

The Massachusetts General Hospital position is direct: although omega-3 and probiotics have been described as "vagus nerve stimulators," **there is no research that points to them as having a specific or measurable impact on the function or tone of the vagus nerve**. Some HRV-mediated effects are documented (probiotics in a 2025 multi-species RCT, PMID 40298641, reported modest morning vagal-indices changes in depressed patients) but treating HRV proxy changes as "vagal tone" stimulation conflates measurement with mechanism.

The conceptual issue: HRV is downstream of many factors. Anything that reduces sympathetic load or improves baseline cardiovascular fitness will move HRV indices. Calling this "vagus nerve stimulation" obscures the actual pathway.

### Component 8: Gua sha and "vagus nerve massage" tools

The Instagram/TikTok adoption of gua sha as "vagus nerve stimulation" merges three separate ideas: (1) traditional Chinese practice of gua sha (vigorous scraping that produces visible petechiae); (2) social-media-friendly facial gua sha (gentle massage with a smooth stone or steel tool, no bruising); (3) claims that gentle neck massage targets the cervical vagus nerve.

The anatomical claim does not hold up: superficial neck massage cannot reach the deep cervical vagus fibers in a meaningful sense. As Northwell Health and the McGill Office for Science and Society have noted, the mechanism claimed (direct vagal stimulation via skin pressure) is inconsistent with established anatomy. Massage may produce relaxation effects via other mechanisms (general parasympathetic activation, perceived safety, attention to the body), but the "vagus nerve stimulation" branding is a specific, testable, and unsupported claim.

Cost of cheap brass or jade "vagus nerve massagers" is typically $10–$40 on consumer-marketplace platforms.

### Component 9: Social-media "polyvagal" content patterns

Documented patterns from Northwell Health, McGill, and The Conversation analyses:

- **Quantitative signal**: #vagusnerve has accumulated >185 million views on TikTok alone (as of 2024 reporting; growth continues).
- **Recurring content types**:
  - "Five-minute nervous system reset" reels
  - "You're stuck in dorsal vagal" diagnostic claims without measurement
  - Ear-clip device promotions
  - Neck-tapping routines
  - Cold-face-splash demonstrations (this one is genuinely supported by physiology — see Cat 6)
  - Hum-along videos
- **Vocabulary creep**: Polyvagal-specific terms ("ventral vagal," "neuroception," "social engagement system") appear in coaching and influencer posts without the academic context that grounds them.
- **Coaching credentials**: A growing category of "polyvagal-informed coaches" without therapy licenses. The Polyvagal Institute's own scope-of-practice language distinguishes "Applied Polyvagal Trauma Practitioner" (for licensed clinicians) from "I integrate applied polyvagal trauma interventions into my practice" (for non-licensed practitioners). The distinction is real and important; whether all non-licensed practitioners observe it is an empirical question.
- **Autism-specific concern**: The framing that autism is a deficit of social-engagement function and that vagus-nerve-targeted interventions can reduce or eliminate autistic traits has been contested by autistic writers (e.g., Trauma Geek's neurodiversity-paradigm critique) as fundamentally pathologizing. Cure claims for autism via polyvagal interventions are a clear red flag.

### Component 10: Parenting and education adoption

This is where the analysis requires the most care, because well-supported attachment and trauma-informed practices share considerable ground with polyvagal language, and conflating them gives the contested theory borrowed credibility.

#### What is genuinely well-supported (independent of polyvagal theory)

- **Attachment theory** (Bowlby, Ainsworth, et al.) — decades of evidence for the role of caregiver responsiveness in child socio-emotional development.
- **Trauma-informed practice in schools** — SAMHSA's framework, Centers for Disease Control adverse-childhood-experiences (ACE) literature, and adjacent applied work.
- **Co-regulation** as a developmental concept — well-established that adult emotional/physiological state shapes infant/child state.
- **Reflective parenting** — Mentalization-Based Treatment for parents (Fonagy and colleagues) is evidence-based.
- **Body-based emotional literacy education** — broadly supported by interoception-and-emotion-regulation research.

#### What is polyvagal-specific and contested

- The specific claim that the **ventral vagal complex** is a distinct mammalian system whose activation explains social engagement, and that calming "regulates the vagus nerve" in a literal anatomical sense.
- The mapping of parent-child interaction onto a three-state ladder (ventral / sympathetic / dorsal) as if these are discrete neural states with measurable boundaries.
- Specific intervention claims (e.g., SSP for school-aged children) based on the polyvagal mechanism.

#### Where these get conflated

In "polyvagal parenting" books and trauma-informed school curricula, the well-supported and the contested often appear in the same paragraph. A book may correctly describe attachment dynamics and the genuine importance of caregiver state, then attribute the mechanism to the dorsal/ventral/sympathetic polyvagal framework. The reader who finds the practical advice helpful naturally extends credibility to the framework. This is the conflation pattern the user is worried about.

A useful diagnostic question: **strip out the polyvagal vocabulary — does the advice still hold up?** For most well-functioning polyvagal-parenting recommendations, yes. The practices are good. The mechanism story is the part that depends on a contested theory.

Bonnie Badenoch's work is the clearest illustration: her relational practice and presence-over-technique framing draw on attachment, interpersonal neurobiology, mirror neurons, polyvagal theory, and memory reconsolidation. The first three of those have varying-but-respectable evidence bases. The fourth (mirror neurons in humans, especially as commonly invoked) and the fifth (memory reconsolidation as clinically deployed) are themselves contested. The integration may produce genuinely useful clinical practice even when individual theoretical inputs are uncertain.

### Component 11: The Polyvagal Equity Project and adjacent applications

A specific "Polyvagal Equity Project" as a stand-alone non-profit could not be located in independent records. What does exist:

- **Embodied Equity: A Polyvagal-Informed Approach for Educators & Helping Professionals** — a PVI 8-week certificate course designed by Niki Elliott, partnered with University of San Diego Professional & Continuing Education. Frames implicit bias and identity-threat through a nervous-system lens; uses mindfulness, breathwork, and somatic practices.
- **Mindful Leaders Project** — adjacent organization led by Niki Elliott.

The relevant question is the same diagnostic: are the recommended practices (somatic awareness, embodied work on bias) standalone-defensible, and is the polyvagal mechanism story being layered on top of them, or is the mechanism story doing load-bearing work that the practices themselves cannot bear?

## The Three Registers (The Key Analytical Tool)

The single most useful filter for the user when encountering any polyvagal-branded content is to ask: **which register is this claim coming from?**

### Register 1: Academic

- **Where you find it**: Peer-reviewed journals (psychophysiology, neuroscience, autonomic medicine).
- **Status as of 2026**: Actively contested. The 2023 Grossman critique in *Biological Psychology* argued all five core premises are inconsistent with comparative neuroanatomy. The 2026 multi-author consensus paper ("Why The Polyvagal Theory Is Untenable") with 38 international co-authors plus Grossman as first author argued the theory has been falsified. Porges has responded with a scholarly rebuttal arguing critics attack a reconstructed version of the theory.
- **What's claimed here**: Specific, falsifiable propositions about myelinated vs. unmyelinated vagal fibers, phylogeny, and the relationship between RSA and vagal tone.
- **What's not claimed here**: Sweeping life-applications. Academic papers say "in our sample, RSA correlated with X under conditions Y." They do not say "your dorsal vagal state is keeping you stuck."

### Register 2: Clinical

- **Where you find it**: Norton Professional Books, EMDRIA-approved continuing education, evidence-mixed clinical practice within licensed therapy. Deb Dana's clinical works, Bonnie Badenoch's interpersonal-neurobiology integration, the Polyvagal Institute's clinician courses.
- **Status as of 2026**: Mixed evidence; some real value as orientation. A 2023 *Journal of Psychiatry Reform* analysis was titled "Polyvagal Approaches: scientifically questionable but useful in practice" — and that title is a fair summary of the position. Clinical practitioners report that the language gives clients a useful narrative for autonomic experience, even if the underlying neuroanatomy claims are disputed.
- **What's claimed here**: That the framework provides useful orientation for therapy. Specific intervention claims (SSP, polyvagal-informed EMDR) sit at evidence Tier B–C per Cat 6 framework.
- **What's not claimed (in careful clinical practice)**: That the mechanism is settled, that the framework cures autism, or that practitioners without licenses can replace therapy.

### Register 3: Wellness / Influencer

- **Where you find it**: Instagram, TikTok, podcast wellness content, supplement marketing, consumer-device advertising, coaching certifications without clinical scope.
- **Status as of 2026**: Often pseudoscientific. Reliable scientific institutions (Massachusetts General Hospital, Northwell Health, McGill Office for Science and Society) have published explicit warnings.
- **What's claimed here**: "Reset your nervous system in 5 minutes." "You're stuck in dorsal vagal." "These supplements optimize your vagal tone." "This gua sha tool stimulates your vagus nerve." "Rewire your nervous system in 8 weeks." Autism "cure" or "reduction" via polyvagal interventions.
- **What's actually supported**: Some practices (slow breathing, cold face splash, humming) have real physiological effects through mechanisms that **predate and do not depend on polyvagal theory**. The branding is borrowed credibility.

**Knowing which register a claim comes from is the user's most useful filter.** A claim from Register 1 is hedged and narrow. A claim from Register 2 may be useful even if the underlying theory is contested. A claim from Register 3 is presumptively suspect until specific evidence is presented.

- **Rhythm of Regulation (Dana)**: Independent commercial training operation. Revenue not publicly disclosed (Dana operates as a private practitioner/business).
- **Kase & CO Training and Consulting (Kase)**: Independent commercial EMDR-and-polyvagal training. Revenue not publicly disclosed.
- **Many adjacent course providers**: PESI (continuing education), Embody Lab, the play-therapy and child-therapy training ecosystem, various "polyvagal therapy practitioner" certification businesses of varying credentialing rigor.

### Device and product sales

- **Unyte (SSP)**: Subscription pricing varies by therapist tier and client access. Not publicly disclosed at company-revenue level (Unyte is privately held).
- **Consumer device vendors**: Truvaga ($499), Pulsetto ($250–$500), Sensate (~$299), Apollo Neuro ($249–$349), Hapbee (subscription). Aggregate consumer "vagus nerve device" market is in the hundreds of millions of dollars annually based on company press releases and industry-tracking estimates.
- **Books**: Trade royalties to authors at standard rates. *The Body Keeps the Score* alone has generated tens of millions of dollars in trade-publishing revenue.
- **Supplements**: A separate, vastly larger market; "vagal tone" branding is one frame among many.

### What this means for the user

None of this is illegal or unusual; conflicts of interest in commercialized scientific frameworks are common. **The point is not that anyone is doing something wrong by selling things.** The point is that the people generating the public claims about how well the framework works are often the same people who profit when the framework remains popular — and that the consumer-facing communications about evidence often do not include the conflict-of-interest disclosures that the academic communications do.

## Concrete Red-Flag Examples (Things the User Should Recognize on Sight)

1. **Autism "cure" or "elimination" claims via polyvagal interventions.** Autism is a stable neurodevelopmental difference; the framing that autistic traits result from a "stuck" autonomic state that can be rewired contradicts the consensus view in autism research and is pathologizing in ways the autistic community has rejected. The autism-specific evidence for SSP (n=6 adult pilot, small uncontrolled child studies) does not support cure claims.

2. **"Rewire your nervous system in [short time]" content.** The nervous system does change; that change does not happen in 5 minutes, 8 weeks, or 30 days, and any product that promises such a timeline is making an unsupportable claim.

3. **"Polyvagal" branded supplements.** No supplement has been shown to specifically influence vagal nerve function. The branding is marketing.

4. **Practitioners diagnosing "dorsal vagal" or "ventral vagal" states without measurement.** The framework is meant to describe felt experience; clinical "diagnosis" of these states by an unlicensed practitioner is scope-of-practice creep at minimum.

5. **Devices marketed to "stimulate the vagus nerve" without FDA clearance and without published sham-controlled RCTs.** General wellness device framing is not a clinical efficacy claim. Pulsetto, Truvaga, Sensate, Apollo Neuro, and Hapbee all fall in this category.

6. **Coaches without clinical training offering polyvagal "therapy."** The PVI's own scope language is clear: non-licensed practitioners may integrate the framework into practice but cannot offer therapy. Practitioners who blur this line are operating outside both scope and PVI's own guidelines.

7. **Gua sha or "vagus nerve massager" tools claiming to stimulate the deep vagus nerve via surface skin contact.** The anatomical claim does not hold.

8. **Claims that omit the conflict-of-interest disclosure.** If a piece of SSP marketing does not mention that Porges holds patents and receives royalties on the product, the absence is a flag — not because the product is therefore useless, but because the consumer-facing communication is missing what the academic communications are required to include.

9. **Conflation of contested mechanism with well-supported practice.** A book that gives good parenting advice and attributes it to polyvagal theory should be evaluated on whether the advice stands without the framework.

10. **"Stuck in dorsal vagal" framings used to explain depression, fatigue, or autistic features.** These framings can feel validating; they are also unfalsifiable in everyday use and substitute a metaphor for diagnosis.

## The Practitioner-Evaluation Checklist (Reusable User Tool)

When encountering a polyvagal-branded practitioner, product, or claim, work through:

1. **Mechanism**: Does the practitioner claim a *mechanism* (a specific neural pathway) or an *experience* (a phenomenological felt sense)? Mechanism claims should be evaluated against anatomy. Experience claims are less falsifiable but also less load-bearing.
2. **Citations**: Are specific peer-reviewed studies cited, or only testimonials and case studies? Are the cited studies actually about what is being claimed?
3. **FDA status (for devices)**: Is the device FDA-cleared, and for what indication? "General wellness device" is not clinical efficacy.
4. **Scope of practice**: Is the practitioner a licensed clinician (therapist, MD, RN, OT, SLP) operating within license, or a coach/educator/influencer? Coaching is a legitimate role; coaching that does diagnosis or treatment is not.
5. **Register distinction**: Is the practitioner clear that "polyvagal-informed" is an orientation, or are they presenting the theory as established biological mechanism?
6. **Conflict of interest disclosure**: If the practitioner sells products, courses, or supplements, is the financial interest disclosed? Is there a referral arrangement?
7. **Falsifiability**: Could the practitioner state a condition under which their claim would be wrong? Unfalsifiable claims ("you're in dorsal vagal because you can't feel ventral") cannot be evaluated.
8. **Track record on uncertainty**: Does the practitioner acknowledge the 2023–2026 academic controversy about polyvagal theory, or does the controversy go unmentioned in their materials? Awareness of contest is itself a quality signal.

A "yes" or "good answer" on most of these does not mean the practitioner is effective — therapy is hard to evaluate from credentials alone. A "no" or "concerning answer" on most of these is grounds to look elsewhere.

## Concrete Examples for the Chapter

### Example 1: Walking through the Three Registers with one claim

Claim: *"Slow breathing activates your vagus nerve."*

- **Academic register**: "In healthy adults, paced breathing at ~0.1 Hz produces high-amplitude RSA via baroreflex coupling. The component of HRV captured by this protocol is vagally mediated through the nucleus ambiguus efferents." (Lehrer 2000; Russo 2017.) Specific, narrow, well-supported.
- **Clinical register**: "Slow breathing is a useful intervention for clients in elevated sympathetic arousal because it engages parasympathetic mechanisms via the vagus nerve." Reasonable, though the mechanism is more cardiovascular-physiology than uniquely polyvagal.
- **Wellness register**: "This 5-minute vagus nerve reset will activate your ventral vagal state and bring you back into social engagement." The practice may help (slow breathing is real). The framing converts a free, well-evidenced intervention into a polyvagal-branded product story that requires no patent.

Same practice, three different epistemological loads.

### Example 2: Evaluating a hypothetical consumer device claim

Marketing copy reads: *"Our device stimulates your vagus nerve, reducing anxiety in 5 minutes."*

Working the checklist:
- Mechanism: Stimulation claim. Anatomy: where on the body is the device applied, and does that location correspond to accessible vagal afferents? If neck or ear, *possibly*; if wrist (Apollo Neuro), the mechanism is haptic/touch not direct vagal.
- Citations: Are sham-controlled RCTs cited, or only manufacturer-funded pilots and testimonials?
- FDA status: Cleared? For what indication? Or "general wellness device"?
- Conflict of interest: Is the company's funding source disclosed? Are the study authors employees or consultants?
- Falsifiability: What would the company accept as a negative result?

If all answers are concerning, the device may still feel good to use — but the *specific causal claim* in the marketing copy is not supported.

### Example 3: A "polyvagal parenting" book audit

A typical "polyvagal parenting" book passage:

> *"When your child melts down at the grocery store, they are not being defiant — they are in a sympathetic state, and your calm, regulated presence can help bring them back to ventral safety. Your nervous system co-regulates theirs."*

- The practical advice (caregiver calm presence helps de-escalate a distressed child) is well-supported by attachment and co-regulation research, predating polyvagal theory.
- The mechanism story ("sympathetic state," "ventral safety," "your nervous system co-regulates theirs") attributes this to polyvagal-specific framing.
- The conflation is in the same paragraph. The reader cannot tell which part is doing the explanatory work.

The diagnostic question: "If you removed the polyvagal vocabulary, does the advice still hold?" — Here, yes. The vocabulary is decorative, not load-bearing. The advice is good independent of whether polyvagal theory survives the 2026 critique.

## Common Misconceptions

### Misconception: "If something is sold as 'polyvagal,' it must have a scientific basis."

**Reality**: "Polyvagal" is not a regulated term. Anyone can use it. The word's appearance on a product, course, or marketing page tells you about marketing positioning, not about evidence. Many products use the word with no published evidence at all.

### Misconception: "FDA clearance means the device works."

**Reality**: FDA clearance via 510(k) indicates substantial equivalence to a predicate device, for specified indications, with characterized safety profile. It does not certify efficacy for general "wellness." Many polyvagal-adjacent devices are sold under the FDA general wellness framework, which requires no FDA evaluation at all. (See Cat 6 for the full treatment.)

### Misconception: "Non-profit status means the organization is impartial."

**Reality**: A 501(c)(3) is a tax-status category. It implies non-distribution of profits to owners, but it does not imply the organization is impartial about the framework it teaches. PVI's mission is to advance polyvagal theory's application; that is its declared purpose. Non-profit status is compatible with that mission.

### Misconception: "If the practitioner has a license, the polyvagal advice is automatically reliable."

**Reality**: License indicates training in a regulated profession (therapist, OT, SLP, MD, RN). It does not certify expertise on any particular theoretical framework. A licensed therapist can adopt a contested theory; the license does not validate the theory.

### Misconception: "Wellness products are harmless even if they don't work."

**Reality**: Beyond financial cost, the user may forgo evidence-based interventions while pursuing low-evidence alternatives. For an autistic adult with burnout, time and energy are limited. The opportunity cost of wellness-product attention is genuine. Additionally, some products are not harmless: Wim Hof + water immersion has documented deaths (32+); brain-retraining programs have been associated with iatrogenic harm in ME/CFS populations (Cat 10).

### Misconception: "Critics of the wellness application are critics of the theory."

**Reality**: One can hold a calibrated view of polyvagal theory itself (e.g., "interesting framework, contested mechanism, some clinical utility") while also being skeptical of the wellness-industry deployment of polyvagal language. These are separate critiques.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **The legitimate-utility case**: A clinician using polyvagal vocabulary as patient-friendly language for autonomic dysregulation, with no theory-as-mechanism load-bearing, is doing useful translation work. Some patients find the language helpful even if the underlying neuroanatomy is wrong.
- **The self-directed-curious case**: A user reading widely in the trauma literature may encounter polyvagal-framed materials and benefit from the felt-experience descriptions while remaining agnostic about the mechanism. This is intellectually defensible.
- **The diagnostic-validation case**: For someone who has long felt that their autonomic state shifts in ways they could not name, encountering even a contested framework that names the experience can be transformatively useful — independent of whether the framework is biologically correct. This is real psychological value.
- **The lived-experience-from-an-autistic-author case**: Inderbitzen's autism-in-polyvagal-terms work comes from an autistic author. The framing the autistic community has criticized (autism as a deficit of social engagement) is not the only autistic engagement with polyvagal theory. Both critique and adoption from autistic writers exist; both are legitimate.

## Research Gaps

- The actual annual revenue of Unyte Health (private company) is not publicly disclosed. Estimates of SSP commercial penetration require triangulation from subscription pricing, advertised provider count, and inferred client volumes.
- The size of the "polyvagal-informed coach" market is hard to quantify. No central registry exists.
- Independent FTC or state-AG enforcement actions against specific "polyvagal" or "vagus nerve" marketing claims, if any, have not been systematically catalogued.
- Conflict-of-interest disclosure rates in the popular-author chain's *consumer-facing* materials (vs. *academic* publications) is a useful empirical question that would benefit from systematic content analysis.
- A separate "Polyvagal Equity Project" as a standalone non-profit could not be confirmed; PVI's "Embodied Equity" course is the most visible related offering.
