# Conceptual Metaphor Theory and Embodied Cognition -- How the Body Thinks in Patterns

The mind does not decorate thought with metaphor. Metaphor is the machinery of thought itself.

---

## 1. The Radical Claim You Already Suspected

Consider a few sentences you might hear on any given day:

- "I *see* what you mean."
- "We need to *weigh* the options."
- "Their relationship hit a *dead end*."
- "Prices are *rising*."
- "She *grasped* the concept."
- "He *fell into* depression."
- "That argument *collapsed*."

You process these without effort. They feel literal. But none of them are. Seeing has nothing to do with understanding. Options have no mass. Relationships do not travel roads. Prices do not move upward in physical space. Concepts cannot be held in the hand. Depression is not a pit. Arguments are not buildings.

These are all metaphors -- but not the decorative kind you learned about in school, where a poet says "the moon is a silver coin" and everyone nods approvingly. These are **structural metaphors**: systematic mappings between a concrete, bodily domain and an abstract domain. They are not ornaments you drape over pre-existing thoughts. They are the thoughts themselves.

This is the central claim of **Conceptual Metaphor Theory (CMT)**, first articulated by George Lakoff and Mark Johnson in *Metaphors We Live By* (1980) and developed across four decades of subsequent work. The claim has three interlocking components:

1. **Metaphor is conceptual, not linguistic.** The words "I see what you mean" are not the metaphor. They are surface evidence of a deeper conceptual mapping: KNOWING IS SEEING. The mapping lives in your cognitive architecture. Language merely reflects it.

2. **Abstract concepts are understood through systematic mappings from bodily experience.** The mapping goes from the concrete (source domain) to the abstract (target domain). We understand knowing through seeing, love through journeys, arguments through war, theories through buildings. The direction is always the same: from body to mind, from concrete to abstract. This is called **unidirectionality**.

3. **Metaphorical thought is largely unconscious and automatic.** You did not choose to think of understanding as seeing. You did not learn it as a figure of speech. The mapping is neurally entrenched -- it fires automatically, below the threshold of awareness, every time you process language or reason about abstract domains.

If this is correct, the implications are extraordinary. It means that the vast majority of abstract human thought -- about time, causation, morality, emotion, quantity, purpose, justice, mathematics -- is structured by a discoverable set of mappings from bodily experience. It means abstract thought is not free-floating and arbitrary but **patterned, systematic, and traceable to the body**.

For someone who thrives on patterns, this is the theory. This is pattern-recognition applied to cognition itself.

## 2. The Structure of Metaphorical Mapping

A conceptual metaphor is not a single word or phrase. It is a **systematic set of correspondences** between two conceptual domains. Every metaphor has:

- A **source domain**: concrete, bodily, directly experienced. This is where the structure comes from.
- A **target domain**: abstract, not directly accessible to the senses. This is what gets structured.

The mapping between them is not arbitrary. It follows precise structural principles:

**Ontological correspondences** map entities in the source to entities in the target. In LOVE IS A JOURNEY:

| Source: JOURNEY | Target: LOVE |
|---|---|
| Travelers | Lovers |
| Vehicle | The relationship |
| Destination | Shared life goals |
| Crossroads | Decision points |
| Dead end | Relationship that cannot continue |
| Impediments to travel | Relationship difficulties |
| Getting stuck | Stagnation |
| Smooth road | Easy progress |
| Going in different directions | Having incompatible goals |

**Epistemic correspondences** transfer inferential structure. If arriving at a crossroads forces choosing a direction, the mapping automatically generates the inference that being "at a crossroads" in a relationship means a decision must be made. The source domain hands you the reasoning.

**Partiality** means the mapping is selective. ARGUMENT IS WAR highlights adversarial aspects (attacks, defenses, winning) while hiding collaborative ones. A different metaphor would reveal different features. The metaphor does not describe the target -- it shapes what the target *is*.

### The Invariance Principle

Not anything can map onto anything. Lakoff's **Invariance Principle** (1990, 1993): metaphorical mappings preserve the image-schema structure of the source domain, consistent with the target domain's inherent structure. The skeleton of the source carries over, but cannot override the target's existing structure. Interiors map to interiors; paths to paths.

The deep consequence: if abstract reasoning preserves spatial/bodily topology, then abstract inferences may be **metaphorical versions of spatial inferences**. The logic of containment, paths, and forces may be the same logic -- carried by metaphor -- that structures reasoning about categories, purposes, and justice.

## 3. Primary Metaphors: The Atoms

If complex metaphors like LOVE IS A JOURNEY are molecules, what are the atoms?

Joseph Grady answered this in his 1997 doctoral dissertation at UC Berkeley, "Foundations of Meaning: Primary Metaphors and Primary Scenes." **Primary metaphors** are the simplest, most irreducible conceptual metaphors. They cannot be broken down into simpler metaphors. And they arise not from culture, convention, or literary tradition but from something far more basic: the structure of the human body interacting with the physical world.

### How Primary Metaphors Form: The Conflation Theory

The mechanism was clarified by Christopher Johnson's **Theory of Conflation** (presented in the late 1990s and published in 1999). During infancy, subjective experiences and sensorimotor experiences are not yet differentiated. They are **conflated** -- experienced as a single event. When an infant is held by a caregiver, the sensorimotor experience of warmth and the subjective experience of affection are fused. The child does not experience "warmth" and "affection" as separate things. It is one undifferentiated experience: warm-affection.

Later, during **differentiation**, the child learns that warmth and affection are distinct. You can be warm without being loved (sitting by a fire), and you can be loved without being warm (a phone call from a parent). But the neural connections formed during conflation persist. The cross-domain wiring remains. And it becomes the substrate of the primary metaphor AFFECTION IS WARMTH.

This formation process has four key properties:

- **Automatic**: The neural connections fire without conscious intention (you do not decide to link warmth and affection)
- **Universal**: Every human infant has a body that experiences warmth during caregiving, height during stacking, effort during carrying
- **Deeply entrenched**: Formed during the critical period of neural development, these connections are extraordinarily durable
- **Resistant to override**: Knowing that "warm greeting" is metaphorical does not stop you from processing it through the warmth-affection circuit

### The Catalog: A Small Set Generates Everything

Here is the key table -- the periodic table of abstract thought. Each primary metaphor maps a specific sensorimotor experience to a specific subjective judgment:

| Primary Metaphor | Sensorimotor Grounding | Example Expressions |
|---|---|---|
| **MORE IS UP** | Stacking increases both quantity and height | "Prices are *rising*," "Turn the volume *up*" |
| **AFFECTION IS WARMTH** | Caregiver's body heat + emotional bonding | "A *warm* greeting," "The *cold* shoulder" |
| **KNOWING IS SEEING** | Vision correlates with knowledge | "I *see* what you mean," "A *clear* explanation" |
| **INTIMACY IS CLOSENESS** | Physical proximity = emotional connection | "We're *close* friends," "They've *grown apart*" |
| **TIME IS MOTION** | Moving past landmarks = passage of time | "Time *flies*," "The deadline is *approaching*" |
| **IMPORTANCE IS SIZE** | Important entities (adults) are bigger | "Tomorrow's a *big* day," "Just a *small* matter" |
| **DIFFICULTIES ARE BURDENS** | Heavy objects are hard to carry | "A *heavy load*," "*Weighed down* by responsibility" |
| **PURPOSES ARE DESTINATIONS** | Reaching a destination = achieving a goal | "She *reached* her goal," "I'm not *there* yet" |
| **HAPPY IS UP / SAD IS DOWN** | Drooping posture = sadness; erect = positive | "I'm feeling *up*," "He's *down* in the dumps" |
| **STATES ARE LOCATIONS** | Being in a bounded space = being in a condition | "She's *in* trouble," "He fell *into* depression" |
| **CAUSES ARE FORCES** | Physical forces cause physical effects | "What *drove* you to do that?" "*Compelled* by circumstances" |
| **CHANGE IS MOTION** | Moving between locations = changing state | "Things are *moving* in the right direction" |
| **UNDERSTANDING IS GRASPING** | Hand manipulation = knowing objects | "I can't *grasp* the concept," "A *slippery* argument" |
| **SIMILARITY IS CLOSENESS** | Similar objects cluster together spatially | "These colors are *close*," "*Far* from what I had in mind" |
| **CONTROL IS UP** | Physical dominance = being above | "She's *on top of* things," "The *upper* hand" |

Count them. About fifteen here, and the complete inventory across the literature runs to roughly twenty-five to thirty. From this small set, the entire landscape of abstract human thought is generated.

### Universality and Cultural Variation

Primary metaphors are predicted to be universal because they arise from the universal structure of the human body in a physical world. Gravity makes MORE IS UP inescapable (stacking always goes up). Mammalian thermoregulation makes AFFECTION IS WARMTH universal (caregiver warmth is a biological constant). Visual dominance in human perception makes KNOWING IS SEEING universal.

Cross-linguistic research substantially confirms this prediction. But Zoltan Kovecses (2005) demonstrated an important nuance: while **generic-level** metaphors tend to be universal, **specific-level** elaborations are culturally shaped. The conceptual metaphor ANGER IS HEAT appears in virtually every language studied (grounded in the universal physiological experience of elevated body temperature during anger). But Tunisian Arabic extends this metaphor with culturally specific elaborations that go beyond universal physiology -- anger is not just heat but heat in culturally particular containers and contexts.

The pattern: universal body, universal primary metaphors, culturally variable elaborations. The atoms are shared; the molecules vary.

## 4. Complex Metaphors: From Atoms to Molecules

Complex metaphors are built by combining primary metaphors with cultural knowledge. They are compositional -- decomposable into primary components. This explains why metaphorical systems are systematic, why new expressions are immediately intelligible, and why certain mappings "work" while others do not.

### LOVE IS A JOURNEY: The Compositional Analysis

LOVE IS A JOURNEY is the canonical example of decomposition. It is composed from at least five primary metaphors:

- **PURPOSES ARE DESTINATIONS** -- the purpose of the relationship maps to the destination of the journey
- **INTIMACY IS CLOSENESS** -- the lovers' emotional closeness maps to physical proximity in the vehicle
- **DIFFICULTIES ARE BURDENS** -- relationship problems map to obstacles on the road
- **STATES ARE LOCATIONS** -- emotional states map to locations along the path
- **CHANGE IS MOTION** -- changes in the relationship map to movement along the path

These five atomic mappings snap together -- like molecular bonds -- to produce the rich, detailed complex metaphor with all its inferential structure. Once assembled, the metaphor generates linguistic expressions that feel natural and are immediately understood: "We're at a crossroads." "The marriage is on the rocks." "We're getting nowhere." "We've been going in different directions." "This relationship is a dead-end street."

No one designed this mapping. The compositional combination of independently grounded primary metaphors *automatically* produces the structure. The complex metaphor is emergent.

### ARGUMENT IS WAR

This was Lakoff and Johnson's opening example in *Metaphors We Live By*, and it remains one of the most revealing:

| Source: WAR | Target: ARGUMENT |
|---|---|
| Combatants | Arguers |
| Attacks | Criticisms |
| Defense | Justification and rebuttal |
| Strategy | Argumentative planning |
| Positions | Claims and stances |
| Ground gained/lost | Points won/conceded |
| Winning/losing | Persuading/being persuaded |
| Weapons | Rhetorical tools |

The linguistic evidence saturates everyday speech: "Your claims are *indefensible*." "He *attacked* every weak point." "I *demolished* his argument." "She *won* the argument."

But the deeper point is about cognition. Because we conceptualize argument as war, we *conduct* arguments as war -- attacking, defending, strategizing, trying to win, treating concession as defeat. The metaphor does not describe argument; it **constitutes** what argument is.

Lakoff and Johnson's thought experiment: imagine a culture where ARGUMENT IS A DANCE. Participants are performers, not combatants. The concept of "winning" an argument would be incoherent -- as incoherent as "winning" a waltz. The metaphor shapes the activity itself.

### Other Complex Metaphors

**THEORIES ARE BUILDINGS**: Foundation = assumptions. Framework = logical organization. Support = evidence. Collapse = refutation. "The theory has a solid *foundation*." "That *undermines* his theory."

**TIME IS MONEY**: "You're *wasting* my time." "This will *save* you hours." "I've *invested* a lot of time." This metaphor is culturally specific -- not all cultures treat time as a quantifiable, exchangeable resource. It reflects the commodification of time in societies where time has literal monetary value (hourly wages, billable hours).

**MORALITY IS PURITY**: "Pure motives," "dirty money," "clean conscience," "moral stain," "washing away sins." Lakoff extended this in *Moral Politics* (1996), arguing that conservative reasoning uses a STRICT FATHER metaphor for the state while progressive reasoning uses a NURTURANT PARENT metaphor -- both complex metaphors composed from primary metaphors about morality, authority, and care.

### The Compositional Principle

A finite inventory of primary metaphors combines to produce an unbounded range of complex metaphors -- the same design principle as phonology (finite phonemes, infinite words) and syntax (finite rules, infinite sentences). The power is in the combinatorics.

## 5. Image Schemas: The Skeletal Structures from the Body

Below primary metaphors lies an even more fundamental layer: **image schemas**. These are the deepest patterns -- the skeletal structures of bodily experience that provide the architecture for all metaphorical mapping.

Mark Johnson proposed image schemas in his 1987 book *The Body in the Mind: The Bodily Basis of Meaning, Imagination, and Reason*. They are:

- **Pre-linguistic**: They exist before and independently of language
- **Dynamic**: Not static templates but patterns of interaction and movement
- **Highly schematic**: They capture only the most fundamental structural features
- **Gestalt-like**: They function as integrated wholes
- **Embodied**: They arise directly from motor movement, object manipulation, and perceptual interaction

Image schemas are not images in the visual sense. They are the skeletal geometry of repeated physical experiences. A child who has been inside rooms, clothes, cribs, and a parent's arms extracts the abstract pattern: **CONTAINER** -- interior, boundary, exterior. This pattern then structures abstract thought.

### The Catalog of Image Schemas

#### CONTAINER (IN/OUT)

**Experiential basis**: From earliest infancy, you experience your body as a container (food goes in, waste comes out) and interact with containers (cups, rooms, buildings). You experience bounded regions -- inside versus outside.

**Structural elements**: Interior, boundary, exterior.

**Logic it provides**: Containers have an inside, an outside, and a boundary. If A is in the container and B is inside A, then B is in the container (transitivity). Something cannot be simultaneously inside and outside (excluded middle for containment).

**Metaphorical extensions**:
- STATES ARE CONTAINERS: "She's *in* love," "He fell *into* depression," "Get *out of* trouble"
- CATEGORIES ARE CONTAINERS: "That falls *within* the scope," "He's *outside* the mainstream"
- THE VISUAL FIELD IS A CONTAINER: "The ship came *into* view," "He's *out of* sight"
- THE MIND IS A CONTAINER: "I can't get that idea *out of* my head," "Let that sink *in*"

One schema. Four major metaphorical systems. Each inheriting the same interior/boundary/exterior logic.

#### SOURCE-PATH-GOAL

**Experiential basis**: From your first crawl to your first step, you experience purposeful movement -- starting somewhere, traversing a route, arriving somewhere else.

**Structural elements**: Source (starting point), path (trajectory), goal (endpoint), direction.

**Logic it provides**: If you start at A and follow a path to B, you pass through all intermediate points. You cannot arrive at the goal without traversing the path. You can be diverted, blocked, or turned back.

**Metaphorical extensions**:
- PURPOSES ARE DESTINATIONS: "She *reached* her goal," "We're getting *closer* to a solution"
- LIFE IS A JOURNEY: "He's at a *crossroads*," "She's come a long *way*"
- TIME IS MOTION: "Christmas is *approaching*," "The deadline has *passed*"
- ARGUMENTS ARE JOURNEYS: "We've *covered* a lot of ground," "Let me *go back* to my earlier point"

#### FORCE

**Experiential basis**: From birth, forces act on your body -- gravity, hands pushing and pulling, barriers blocking movement, momentum carrying you forward.

**Sub-schemas**: COMPULSION ("She was *driven* to succeed"), BLOCKAGE ("There's an *obstacle* in our way"), COUNTERFORCE ("Good and evil are in *conflict*"), DIVERSION ("He was *sidetracked*"), REMOVAL OF RESTRAINT ("That *frees* you to act"), ATTRACTION ("She was *drawn* to the idea"), RESISTANCE ("He *resisted* temptation").

**Metaphorical extensions**:
- CAUSES ARE FORCES: "What *drove* you to that conclusion?"
- EMOTIONS ARE FORCES: "She was *overwhelmed* by grief," "He was *moved* by the speech"
- SOCIAL PRESSURE IS FORCE: "*Peer pressure* made him conform"

#### BALANCE

**Experiential basis**: Maintaining physical equilibrium -- sitting upright, walking, standing -- is a constant background process involving gravity, vestibular processing, and proprioception.

**Metaphorical extensions**:
- JUSTICE IS BALANCE: "The *scales* of justice," "A *balanced* judgment"
- PSYCHOLOGICAL WELL-BEING IS BALANCE: "She's *well-balanced*," "He's become *unhinged*"
- FAIRNESS IS EQUAL DISTRIBUTION: "A *balanced* approach," "*Tilting* the playing field"

#### UP-DOWN (VERTICALITY)

**Experiential basis**: Gravity creates an absolute, inescapable asymmetry. Erect posture, stacking objects, rising liquids -- all create correlations between vertical position and other dimensions of experience.

**Metaphorical extensions** (this schema is extraordinarily productive): MORE IS UP ("Prices are *rising*"), HAPPY IS UP / SAD IS DOWN ("I'm feeling *up*," "He's *down* in the dumps"), GOOD IS UP / BAD IS DOWN ("Things are looking *up*," "He's hit *rock bottom*"), CONTROL IS UP ("She has the *upper* hand"), CONSCIOUS IS UP ("*Wake up*," "He *fell* asleep").

Any creature with a body like ours, in a gravitational field like ours, would develop some form of verticality schema.

#### Additional Schemas

- **CENTER-PERIPHERY**: Bodies have a center (vital organs) and a periphery (limbs, skin). "The *central* argument," "*peripheral* concerns," "*core* values," "*fringe* beliefs."
- **LINK**: Physical connections -- hands held, chains, ropes, bridges. "They're *tied* to each other," "These ideas are *linked*," "The *chain* of reasoning."
- **PART-WHOLE**: Your body is a whole with parts; the world follows suit. "A *member* of the team," "The *body* politic," "The *head* of the organization."

### Image Schemas as the Physics of Thought

Clausner and Croft (1999) demonstrated that all image schemas are embodied and all image schematic domains are basic domains. They function as the "physics" of the conceptual world -- providing the fundamental spatial, dynamic, and force-related structures that are then recruited for understanding abstract domains.

The analogy is precise: just as physical laws (gravity, electromagnetism, the strong and weak forces) generate all physical structure from a small set of principles, image schemas generate all conceptual structure from a small set of embodied patterns. About eight to twelve schemas, applied compositionally through metaphorical mapping, produce the entire architecture of abstract thought.

## 6. The Neural Theory of Metaphor

Everything described so far -- primary metaphors, complex metaphors, image schemas, compositional architecture -- is a cognitive theory. But in 1988, George Lakoff and computer scientist Jerome Feldman set out to ground it in neuroscience.

They established the **Neural Theory of Language (NTL) group** at the International Computer Science Institute (ICSI) at UC Berkeley. The fundamental question: how is metaphorical thought neurally instantiated in the brain? If metaphor is cognitive infrastructure, what does that infrastructure look like at the level of neurons?

### The Neural Mechanism: Conflation, Hebbian Learning, and Cascades

Srini Narayanan's 1997 doctoral dissertation at UC Berkeley provided the first neural computational theory of metaphor. The mechanism turns on a principle from neuroscience so fundamental it has its own slogan: **"Neurons that fire together wire together"** (Hebb's rule).

The process unfolds in four stages:

1. **Conflation (infancy)**: The infant simultaneously experiences warmth and affection. Neural circuits for thermal sensation and social bonding fire together, repeatedly.

2. **Hebbian learning**: Repeated co-activation strengthens synaptic connections between the two circuits. Cross-domain wiring forms.

3. **Differentiation (later childhood)**: The child learns warmth and affection are distinct. The domains separate, but the cross-domain connections persist.

4. **Metaphorical mapping (ongoing)**: Whenever the warmth circuit activates, activation cascades to the affection circuit. This cascade *is* the metaphor. AFFECTION IS WARMTH is not a stored rule -- it is a pattern of neural activation.

Narayanan and Lakoff formalized this as **Neural Cascade Theory**: bidirectional cascades of activation link linguistic form to metaphorical meaning via connections across brain regions. Metaphorical inferences arise through neural simulation -- the brain runs the source domain circuit and activation flows through mapping circuits into the target.

### The Evidence: What Neuroscience Shows

Empirical support for the neural theory has accumulated steadily:

**Motor cortex activation for action metaphors**: Both literal action sentences ("He kicked the ball") and metaphorical action sentences ("He kicked the habit") activate motor cortex regions. Crucially, the activation is somatotopic -- dorsal motor cortex for leg-related verbs, lateral motor cortex for arm-related verbs -- for both literal and figurative uses. This is not generic activation. The brain treats "kick" as involving the leg even when the sentence is about overcoming addiction.

**Speed of activation**: Sensorimotor regions activate within approximately **200 milliseconds** of an action verb being presented, for both literal and metaphorical contexts. This speed rules out the possibility that the activation is post-hoc imagery (consciously imagining kicking after understanding the sentence). Two hundred milliseconds is too fast for conscious processing. The activation is automatic and mandatory -- the hallmark of entrenched neural circuitry.

**The activation gradient**: Desai et al. (2011) demonstrated a gradient of sensorimotor activation across sentence types:

- **Literal sentences** ("He kicked the ball"): Strongest sensorimotor activation
- **Metaphorical sentences** ("He kicked the habit"): Significant activation
- **Idiomatic sentences** ("He kicked the bucket" = he died): Weaker activation
- **Abstract sentences** ("He considered the idea"): Weakest activation

This gradient suggests that as metaphorical expressions become more conventionalized and their sensorimotor origins more opaque, they retain some but progressively less sensorimotor grounding. Even highly conventionalized expressions are not fully "dead" -- they still partially activate the embodied circuits from which they originated. (Note: some research suggests even highly conventionalized "dead" metaphors like "breakthrough" may retain sensorimotor activation at very rapid timescales, though specific studies on this point require further verification.)

**Textural metaphors**: Lacey et al. (2012) found that comprehending textural metaphors ("She had a *rough* day") activates somatosensory cortex -- the brain region that processes touch. The texture word activates the touch circuit even when the sentence has nothing to do with physical texture.

### Lakoff and Narayanan's *The Neural Mind* (2025)

The culmination is Lakoff and Narayanan's *The Neural Mind: How Brains Think* (University of Chicago Press, 2025), unifying neuroscience, computation, and cognitive linguistics. Its core argument: every thought is physical, carried out by neural systems whose structure derives from circuits for vision, touch, and hearing. The "metaphors we live by" are patterns of activation in circuits evolved for sensing and moving.

## 7. Embodied Cognition: The Body Is Not a Vehicle for the Mind

The neural theory of metaphor is embedded within a larger philosophical revolution: **embodied cognition**. This is the rejection of the idea -- dominant in Western philosophy since Descartes -- that the mind is a disembodied rational substance that merely happens to be housed in a body.

### The Three Findings

Lakoff and Johnson laid out the philosophical implications in *Philosophy in the Flesh* (1999). Three findings from cognitive science demand a revision of Western philosophical foundations:

1. **The cognitive unconscious**: The vast majority of thought is unconscious -- not Freudian-unconscious but simply not available to introspection. Conceptual metaphors and image schemas operate below awareness.

2. **The embodied mind**: The mind is shaped and constrained by the body. Concepts are neural patterns grounded in sensorimotor experience, not abstract symbols manipulated by disembodied rules.

3. **Metaphorical thought**: Abstract concepts are structured by metaphorical mappings from bodily experience. Time, causation, morality, mathematics -- all are fundamentally metaphorical.

### Embodied Realism

Lakoff and Johnson propose **embodied realism** as a replacement, positioned between two extremes:

- **Objectivism**: one objective reality, accessible through disembodied reason. Rejected -- the mind is embodied; concepts are shaped by bodies, not by direct access to mind-independent truth.

- **Relativism**: no objective reality; all perspectives equally valid. Rejected -- embodied concepts are not arbitrary. They are constrained by real physical structure. Gravity is real. Spatial extension is real.

Embodied realism: we have access to reality, but always through embodied cognition. Our concepts pick out real patterns but are shaped by the particular body we have. A radically different body would produce a different conceptual system -- different image schemas, different primary metaphors -- but no less valid.

### The Challenge to Western Philosophy

- **Descartes**: The cogito assumes disembodied thinking. CMT says no thought without a body.
- **Kant**: Categories of understanding are supposed to be pure and a priori. CMT says they are embodied and metaphorically structured.
- **Formal logic**: Assumes literal propositions. CMT says most propositions are metaphorically structured, their "logic" inherited from spatial image schemas.
- **Moral philosophy**: Moral principles supposedly derived from pure reason. CMT says they are structured by metaphors (MORALITY IS PURITY, JUSTICE IS BALANCE) grounded in bodily experience.
- **Philosophy of mind**: The mind as abstract symbol-manipulator. CMT says the mind is a neural system grounded in, and constrained by, the body.

### Barsalou's Convergent Evidence

Lawrence Barsalou's (1999) **Perceptual Symbol Systems** theory provides independent support. Traditional cognitive science treats concepts as **amodal symbols** -- abstract tokens stripped of perceptual content. Barsalou proposed instead that concepts are **multimodal re-enactments** of perceptual experience: fragments of visual, auditory, motor, and proprioceptive experience are stored as perceptual symbols and recombined into **simulators** that function as concepts. Conceptualizing "chair" involves running a partial simulation of perceiving and interacting with chairs -- not retrieving an abstract definition.

This directly supports CMT: if concepts are simulations of embodied experience, then metaphorical mapping is running a simulation in one domain (bodily experience) to structure understanding in another (abstract thought).

## 8. Critiques and Limitations: What the Theory Does Not Explain

No theory that claims to explain the fundamental architecture of human thought will go unchallenged. CMT has faced sustained, serious criticism from multiple directions. Understanding these critiques is as important as understanding the theory itself.

### The Circularity Problem

Critics argue that CMT's methodology is potentially circular. The procedure is: (1) hypothesize a conceptual metaphor (e.g., ARGUMENT IS WAR), (2) look for linguistic expressions that confirm it ("He attacked my argument"), (3) claim the expressions prove the metaphor exists. But the theory determines what counts as evidence. If you are looking for war metaphors in argument language, you will find them -- because you defined "attack" as a war term rather than, say, a sports term or a predation term.

Matthew McGlone (2007) sharpened this critique, arguing that CMT functions more as a descriptive label than a tested causal mechanism. The theory can retrospectively organize linguistic data, but it is less clear that it generates testable, falsifiable predictions about what people will say or think.

### Glucksberg's Class-Inclusion Model

Sam Glucksberg and Boaz Keysar (1990) proposed an entirely different mechanism for metaphor comprehension. In their **class-inclusion model**, metaphors are not understood through cross-domain mappings at all. Instead, they are **class-inclusion statements**: the topic of a metaphor is assigned to an ad hoc category, and the vehicle serves as the prototype of that category.

"My surgeon is a butcher" does not map the domain of butchery onto surgery. Instead, it creates an ad hoc category -- roughly, "people who perform cutting tasks clumsily and with poor results" -- and includes the surgeon in that category, with "butcher" as the prototype. The process is online and context-dependent, not a retrieval of pre-existing conceptual structure.

This is a fundamentally different claim about the mechanism. CMT says metaphor understanding involves accessing **pre-existing conceptual mappings** stored in long-term memory. Glucksberg says it involves **real-time categorization** -- constructing ad hoc categories on the fly. The experimental evidence is mixed, and some researchers argue both mechanisms may operate depending on the type of metaphor (novel vs. conventional).

### Gentner's Structure-Mapping Theory

Dedre Gentner (1983, 1988) offers another alternative: metaphor as analogical mapping. In her **Structure-Mapping Theory**, what gets projected from source to target is relational structure -- not just features or entities but the pattern of relations among them. Gentner and Bowdle's (2005) **Career of Metaphor** hypothesis proposes that metaphors change processing mechanism as they conventionalize: novel metaphors are processed as comparisons (analogical mapping), while conventional metaphors are processed as categorizations (more like Glucksberg's model). This provides a developmental account that neither CMT nor the class-inclusion model alone captures.

### Relevance Theory: Metaphor as Loose Talk

Sperber and Wilson's **Relevance Theory** provides yet another alternative. Under this framework, metaphor is not a special cognitive mechanism at all. It is a case of "loose talk" -- ad hoc concept construction where the encoded word meaning serves as a point of access to encyclopedic information, and the hearer narrows or broadens the concept to achieve optimal relevance in context. There is no need for pre-existing mappings or special metaphor modules. The same pragmatic inference mechanism that handles literal language, approximation, and hyperbole also handles metaphor.

### Mahon and Caramazza: The Grounding Problem

Bradford Mahon and Alfonso Caramazza (2008) posed the most philosophically serious challenge to embodied cognition:

**The abstract concept problem**: Embodiment plausibly accounts for concrete concepts (chair, kick, red), but what about DEMOCRACY, ENTROPY, JUSTICE, or the NUMBER 7? These do not obviously reduce to sensorimotor patterns.

**The parsimony argument**: If embodied cognition must admit "disembodied" processes for abstract concepts anyway, why not use a single amodal system for everything?

**Grounding by interaction**: Their middle ground -- concepts may have an amodal core, but this core links to sensorimotor information through spreading activation. Motor cortex firing when you read "kick the habit" may reflect a secondary association, not constitutive embodiment.

This sharpens a crucial distinction:
- **Strong embodiment**: The concept IS the sensorimotor pattern. No body, no concept.
- **Weak embodiment**: The concept triggers sensorimotor associations but has an independent core. The body enriches but does not constitute understanding.

Current neuroscience data is consistent with both interpretations, and there is growing consensus that a hybrid account may be needed.

### The Scope and Variability Problems

**Scope**: If nearly all abstract thought is metaphorical, what is NOT metaphorical? Lakoff's answer -- basic-level concepts and image schemas are the literal foundation -- leaves critics unsatisfied, since the literal/metaphorical boundary can seem to shift to accommodate the theory.

**Embodiment variability**: CMT emphasizes universal embodiment, but bodies differ. If CONTROL IS UP depends on physical height dominance, what about wheelchair users' embodied experience? If UNDERSTANDING IS GRASPING depends on hand manipulation, what about people born without hands? The theory has not fully addressed how variation in embodiment maps to variation in conceptual metaphor.

## 9. The Pattern of Patterns: Why This Framework Matters for Understanding Language

Here is where the threads converge, and where this theory speaks directly to the project of understanding language as a system of patterns.

### The Generative Architecture

CMT reveals a **generative architecture** with a small number of levels, each built from the one below:

1. **Embodied Experience** -- sensorimotor interactions with the physical world
2. **Image Schemas** (~8-12) -- skeletal patterns abstracted from embodied experience
3. **Primary Metaphors** (~25-30) -- cross-domain mappings formed by childhood conflation
4. **Complex Metaphors** (hundreds) -- compositional combinations of primary metaphors + cultural knowledge
5. **Abstract Thought and Language** -- the full landscape of reasoning about time, causation, morality, emotion, mathematics

### Six Properties That Make This a Pattern-Lover's Framework

1. **Finite generates infinite.** ~12 image schemas and ~30 primary metaphors generate hundreds of complex metaphors, which structure all abstract thought. Same design principle as phonology and syntax.

2. **Mappings are systematic, not arbitrary.** If you know the source domain, you can *predict* the target domain structure. The Invariance Principle guarantees structural preservation.

3. **Massive pattern reuse.** The CONTAINER schema alone structures states, categories, the visual field, and the mind. One schema, four major conceptual systems.

4. **Entailments inherit automatically.** If ARGUMENT IS WAR, arguments get attacks, defenses, strategies, winners, and losers -- not by convention, but by automatic inferential transfer.

5. **Cross-domain consistency.** MORE IS UP appears across many complex metaphors and many languages, creating predictable regularity across unrelated domains.

6. **Hierarchical structure.** Image schemas --> primary metaphors --> complex metaphors --> domain-specific reasoning. Lower levels give systematic access to higher levels.

### What This Means for Language

- **Apparently arbitrary vocabulary becomes systematic.** "Grasping" a concept, "falling" in love, "rising" prices -- not arbitrary idioms to memorize one by one, but surface manifestations of systematic mappings. Learn the mapping, and dozens of expressions become transparent.

- **Idioms become decodable.** "On the rocks," "off the track," "at a crossroads" -- all transparent once you identify the underlying conceptual metaphor.

- **Cross-linguistic comparison becomes tractable.** Different languages elaborate the same primary metaphors differently, but the underlying mappings are shared. The atoms are universal; the molecules vary.

- **Grammar itself may be metaphorically structured.** The CONTAINER schema structures aspect markers; SOURCE-PATH-GOAL structures directional constructions.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Conceptual metaphor** | A systematic mapping between a source domain (concrete, embodied) and a target domain (abstract), structuring how we think about and reason within the abstract domain |
| **Source domain** | The concrete, experientially grounded domain from which structure is mapped (e.g., JOURNEY, WAR, BUILDING) |
| **Target domain** | The abstract domain that is understood through the mapping (e.g., LOVE, ARGUMENT, THEORY) |
| **Primary metaphor** | An irreducible metaphorical mapping arising from universal sensorimotor correlations during childhood; the "atom" of metaphorical thought |
| **Complex metaphor** | A metaphor composed from multiple primary metaphors plus cultural knowledge; the "molecule" of metaphorical thought |
| **Image schema** | A pre-linguistic, dynamic, schematic pattern arising from repeated sensorimotor experience (e.g., CONTAINER, PATH, FORCE); the deepest layer of embodied conceptual structure |
| **Conflation** | The early-childhood developmental phase in which source and target domain experiences are undifferentiated, creating cross-domain neural connections |
| **Differentiation** | The subsequent developmental phase in which conflated domains separate, leaving the cross-domain neural connections as the substrate for metaphorical mapping |
| **Invariance Principle** | The constraint that metaphorical mappings preserve the image-schema structure of the source domain, consistent with the inherent structure of the target domain |
| **Unidirectionality** | The principle that metaphorical mapping goes from concrete to abstract, not the reverse |
| **Ontological correspondences** | The specific entity-to-entity mappings within a conceptual metaphor (travelers = lovers, vehicle = relationship) |
| **Epistemic correspondences** | The knowledge and inference patterns that transfer from source to target domain via metaphorical mapping |
| **Neural cascade** | The bidirectional spreading of neural activation from linguistic form through metaphor mapping circuits to meaning |
| **Embodied realism** | The philosophical position that human concepts are shaped by embodiment but constrained by real physical structure; between objectivism and relativism |
| **Embodied cognition** | The broad framework holding that cognition is grounded in perception, action, and bodily states rather than being a disembodied symbol-manipulation system |
| **Grounded cognition** | Contemporary umbrella term for theories (CMT, perceptual symbols, simulation semantics) holding that cognition is grounded in sensorimotor experience |
| **Perceptual symbol systems** | Barsalou's theory that concepts are multimodal re-enactments of perceptual experience |
| **Class-inclusion model** | Glucksberg's alternative: metaphors are understood by assigning topics to ad hoc categories, with the vehicle as prototype |
| **Structure-Mapping Theory** | Gentner's theory that metaphor involves analogical projection of relational structure from source to target |
| **Somatotopy** | The mapping of body parts to specific brain regions; metaphorical language about leg actions activates leg motor cortex |
| **Hebbian learning** | "Neurons that fire together wire together" -- the mechanism underlying primary metaphor formation |
