# Where Alexithymia Comes From — Genes, Trauma, Attachment, Culture, Gender

## Why origins matter

The previous category mapped the brain. This one asks the harder personal question: where did your alexithymia come from, and what does that imply for what you can change? Behind the question are two sub-questions that the literature can answer with reasonable honesty.

The first is the modifiability question. A person who learns that 30 to 42 per cent of alexithymia is heritable, and the rest is environmental, holds a different relationship to their condition than a person who has been told it's all developmental and reversible — or all biological and fixed. Neither extreme is true. The honest framing is proportional. Some of what you experience as your alexithymic phenotype is constitutional and likely lifelong. A larger fraction is shaped by individual life experience and is, in principle, partly modifiable. The exact split for any one person is impossible to estimate from current science, but the population-level proportions are reasonably well-known.

The second is the identity question. If your alexithymia is largely constitutional — perhaps tightly bound up with autism, ADHD, or another neurodivergent profile — then the right relationship to it is closer to accommodation than reversal. You build vocabulary and external scaffolds that work with how your particular nervous system already works. If a substantial portion is developmental — derived from emotional neglect, dismissive attachment, or restrictive gender socialisation — then targeted therapeutic work has a more credible target. Most adults are some mix of both. Knowing the rough proportion changes which clinical promises sound credible.

This category sits between Cat 4, which mapped the brain mechanisms in their current adult form, and Cat 6, which will map the clinical populations where alexithymia commonly lives. Cat 5 is the etiological backstory — the *how* of arriving at the alexithymic phenotype. The throughline is dimensional and probabilistic: no single cause, no clean categorical types, but a set of partly overlapping pathways that combine differently in different lives.

The category also previews what's coming. The new Cats 7 and 8 in this deep dive will explore the bidirectional dance between trauma and alexithymia. This category presents maltreatment as a cause — a one-direction story. Cats 7 and 8 will pick up the more complex picture later: trauma can build alexithymia, and alexithymia in turn shapes how trauma is processed and recovered from. For now, hold this category as the developmental-origins half of that conversation.

## The genetic / heritability picture

The two largest twin studies in alexithymia agree on the qualitative picture and disagree on the precise number, in patterned and informative ways.

The Danish twin study by Jorgensen, Zachariae, Skytthe, and Kyvik ([2007, PubMed 17917473](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17917473/)) is the largest, with 8,785 twin pairs from the Danish Twin Registry. Across the TAS-20 total score and its facets, heritability lands in the 30–33 per cent range. The variance components are: heritable about 30 per cent, non-shared environment about 50 to 56 per cent, shared environment about 12 to 20 per cent. The dominant share is non-shared environment — meaning that what makes one identical twin more alexithymic than their co-twin is mostly each twin's own life history, not the family climate they grew up in together.

The Italian twin study by Picardi, Fagnani, Gigantesco, Toccaceli, Lega, and Stazi ([2011, PubMed 21911104](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21911104/)) ran a smaller sample of 729 twin pairs and found heritability of 42 per cent — drops to 33 per cent when depression is included as a covariate. The genetic correlation between alexithymia and depression in this sample was 0.65, meaning much of the heritable component is shared with the heritable component of depression. Picardi explicitly notes that the higher number reflects the younger sample. Younger samples tend to show higher heritability estimates because environmental accumulation has had less time to operate.

Combining the two studies: heritability lands in the **30 to 42 per cent range**, depending on sample age and depression handling. This is the behavioural-genetic profile typical of partly-heritable personality traits — comparable in size to neuroticism (40–50 per cent) and similar to most of the Big Five (30–50 per cent).

A few honest takeaways. There is a real constitutional component; it isn't negligible; it's unlikely to fully reverse with therapy. But the larger fraction at the population level is environmental — about 58 to 70 per cent of the variance, dominated by experiences each individual has rather than experiences shared with siblings. This is not a story of "mostly genetics" or "mostly experience." It's a story of partly-heritable, primarily individually-shaped trait. There is also no single-gene story to lean on. A few candidate-gene studies have looked at the serotonin transporter polymorphism, oxytocin receptor variants, and a handful of other targets; they tend to be underpowered and mostly unreplicated. The genetic architecture is polygenic and trait-level, in the same way most personality traits are. Don't expect a 23andMe panel to tell you much.

The practical reading: a non-trivial fraction of your alexithymia is likely constitutional. The rest was shaped by what happened to you. The next sections walk through what we know about the environmental side.

## Childhood maltreatment as risk factor

The single most studied environmental risk factor for adult alexithymia is childhood maltreatment. The 2023 meta-analysis by Aaron, Fisher, de la Vega, Lumley, and Palermo synthesised the available evidence and found a robust meta-analytic association between adverse childhood experiences and adult alexithymia, with **emotional neglect** emerging as the strongest single predictor among maltreatment subtypes. Physical and sexual maltreatment are also significantly predictive but operate at somewhat smaller average effect sizes.

Why emotional neglect specifically would be the strongest predictor makes sense developmentally. Emotional neglect is, by definition, the absence of co-regulation and emotion-coaching. A child whose distress is met with attuned naming — "you're feeling sad about that, aren't you" — is being given the vocabulary that will become the substrate for adult emotional self-knowledge. A child whose distress is dismissed, ignored, or punished has no source for that vocabulary to come from. Over development, the emotion-language scaffolding that secure caregiving builds simply doesn't get installed. By adulthood, the person presents as cognitively alexithymic — high difficulty identifying feelings, high difficulty describing feelings — not because something was destroyed but because something was never built.

Physical and sexual abuse correlate with adult alexithymia for additional reasons that overlap with but extend beyond the emotion-coaching mechanism. They commonly induce dissociation; they shape how the body holds and releases arousal; they often produce structural traumatic adaptations (the deeper splits explored in the trauma-focused literature on complex PTSD and dissociation). All of these can route through alexithymic phenotypes in adulthood.

A 2024 Frontiers in Psychiatry mediation meta-analysis takes the next step in the chain. Alexithymia tested as a mediator from ACEs to a range of adult psychopathology outcomes — depression, anxiety, PTSD, somatisation — produces significant indirect effects across multiple outcomes. This positions alexithymia as a transdiagnostic mediator: one of the routes by which adverse childhood experience becomes adult mental-health vulnerability is by failing to build the emotional self-regulation infrastructure that secure caregiving would have built. The route is not the only one, but it's one of the routes that consistently shows up.

This is the secondary alexithymia phenotype in the Messina, Beadle, Paradiso (2014) typology that we covered in Cat 2. Where primary alexithymia is constitutional and lifelong, secondary alexithymia is developmental — it emerged in response to specific life experiences, and is therefore at least theoretically more responsive to corrective experiences (relational, therapeutic, contextual). The two are not mutually exclusive. Most people with substantial alexithymia traceable to maltreatment also have some baseline constitutional contribution. The developmental piece sits on top of, and amplifies, whatever was already constitutionally there.

For the user trying to read their own etiology: if you have documented or remembered emotional neglect or maltreatment in childhood, a portion of your alexithymia is likely the developmental sequela of that history. That fraction is more theoretically modifiable than the constitutional piece — emotion-focused therapies, attachment-focused therapies, relational and trauma-informed approaches all target the developmental mechanism. The IFS deep dive that the user has already built covers many of these approaches. The more honest framing is rarely "this is all the trauma" or "this is all the constitutional pattern" — it's usually some of each, and treatment plans that address both work better than treatment plans that pick one.

## Attachment as developmental pathway

Bowlby's attachment theory — built across the volumes of his attachment-and-loss trilogy in the late 1960s and 1970s — is the foundational developmental frame for understanding how early caregiver–child relationships shape adult emotional self-regulation. The translation to alexithymia is theoretically straightforward, and the empirical literature broadly supports it.

Children in attuned, responsive caregiving contexts develop secure attachment. Their emotions are mirrored, named, validated, and modulated through co-regulation. Across childhood, this builds the internal capacity for emotional self-knowledge and self-regulation. Children in dismissive, inconsistent, or hostile caregiving contexts develop one of three insecure patterns: anxious-preoccupied (when caregiving is unpredictable), dismissive-avoidant (when caregiving minimises emotion), or fearful-disorganised (when the caregiver is themselves a source of threat).

The attachment style most consistently associated with elevated adult alexithymia is **dismissive-avoidant**. The mechanism is direct: caregivers who minimise or dismiss emotional expression don't coach it, don't mirror it, don't validate it. The child learns "feelings are not to be expressed or named." Across development, the emotion vocabulary that secure attachment would have built doesn't develop, and by adulthood the person presents as cognitively alexithymic — high DIF and DDF — with often a parallel high EOT (preferring practical over internal-emotional content).

Lyvers, Cotterell, and Thorberg ([2019, PubMed 30848836](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30848836/)) examined parental bonding, adult attachment, theory of mind, and alexithymia. They found insecure adult attachment and recalled low parental care / high parental overprotection associated with adult alexithymia. Theory-of-mind function partly mediated the relationship. The pattern across studies is consistent: dismissive-avoidant attachment is most consistently associated with elevated alexithymia; anxious-preoccupied less so; fearful-disorganised elevated but with more state variability and overlap with dissociation.

The "earned secure" question matters here. Attachment research has long argued that adult experience can shift attachment patterns toward security through corrective relational experience, including therapy. For alexithymia specifically, the mentalisation-based therapy literature (Bateman & Fonagy) has shown reductions in alexithymia in BPD populations, consistent with the idea that the developmental pathway that built insecure attachment + alexithymia is partly reversible through corrective relational work. Effect sizes are small to moderate, not transformative, and the evidence base in non-BPD populations is thinner. The pathway is real but not magical: it's "partial modification through committed relational work" rather than "reset to a securely-attached default."

A final caveat. The attachment frame is complicated by autism. An autistic child may experience relatively neutral caregiving as alexithymia-shaping if the cognitive-architectural starting point is different from a non-autistic child. The same caregiving environment that secured a non-autistic sibling may not have offered the same kind of co-regulation that an autistic child needed. Distinguishing constitutional autism+alexithymia from caregiving-derived attachment-alexithymia in autistic adults is harder than the attachment literature usually acknowledges. If you are autistic, both pathways may have contributed in your case, and the relative weights are difficult to estimate even retrospectively.

## Parental alexithymia as modeling effect

A separate but related environmental pathway from attachment is parental modeling. Parents who are alexithymic themselves tend to have alexithymic children at rates above what genetics alone would predict.

Some of this co-occurrence is genetic transmission — parents and children share DNA. The twin studies estimate that fraction. But on top of the genetic share is an environmental modeling pathway. Alexithymic parents may not coach emotion language in their children because they don't have it themselves; they may not engage in emotion-talk at the dinner table; they may not validate or mirror the child's emotional experience; they may model flat affective expression. None of this is necessarily neglect in the maltreatment sense — many alexithymic parents are responsive, present, and caring in non-emotional dimensions. But the specific coaching that builds emotion vocabulary may be absent.

The behavioural-genetic separation matters here. Twin studies allow statistical decomposition of the genetic share from the environmental share. The Danish and Italian studies' estimates are population-level summaries. They tell us, on average across the population, what proportion of variance is genetic. They do not tell any individual family how their particular case decomposes. In any one alexithymic family, both pathways may be operating: shared genetic load amplifying environmental modeling.

For a user trying to understand their etiology: "my parent was alexithymic too" is consistent with both genetic and environmental transmission. The intervention implications differ between the two. The environmental modeling part is theoretically modifiable through later exposure to emotion-rich relational contexts, therapy, and explicit emotion vocabulary work. The genetic transmission part is constitutional. Most adults from alexithymic families show some of both.

## Gender socialisation — Levant's normative male alexithymia and the cultural-context nuance

Levant and colleagues developed the construct of "Normative Male Alexithymia" and the **Normative Male Alexithymia Scale (NMAS)** to capture a specific developmental pathway: traditional masculine gender socialisation explicitly trains boys to restrict emotional expression. "Boys don't cry." "Don't be a sissy." "Be tough." Across childhood and adolescence, this restrictive socialisation pressure shapes the developmental window during which emotion vocabulary is built. The result is a population-level pattern in which men have systematically poorer emotional vocabulary and emotional expression than women, even after controlling for other factors.

Levant ([2009 ART manual](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/381169378)) developed the Alexithymia Reduction Treatment, a brief psychoeducational group intervention specifically designed to address normative male alexithymia. The components are familiar from broader emotional-skills literature: psychoeducation about emotion, vocabulary-building, structured emotion-identification exercises, group discussion. Effect sizes in pilot studies are small to moderate. The evidence base is not large; there are no large randomised trials. But the direction is consistent with the broader finding that secondary/developmental alexithymia is at least partially modifiable through targeted psychoeducational and relational work.

The 2024 meta-analysis of gender differences in alexithymia produces an effect size of **d ≈ 0.22** — men slightly higher than women, but small in absolute terms. Within-gender variance dwarfs the between-gender mean difference. Most alexithymic men are not more alexithymic than most non-alexithymic women. And the gap is heavily moderated by culture — larger in cultures with more rigid masculine gender socialisation, smaller in egalitarian cultures.

This is where the user's elevated adaptive-function frame matters most. The standard Levant story — gender socialisation as cost — is solidly built on Western-independent cultural data. The cost is documented: relationship dissatisfaction, failure to seek mental-health support, somatic-symptom expression of distress, downstream emotion-suppression costs. In the Western Anglosphere context where Levant's framework was developed, the cost framing is correct.

But Schouten, Boiger, Kirchner-Häusler, Uchida, and Mesquita ([2020, Frontiers in Psychology](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2020.01048/full)) studied Belgian and Japanese couples and found that *expressive suppression* has different functional outcomes by cultural context. In Belgian (Western-independent) couples, suppression correlated with lower relationship quality — the standard "suppression is costly" finding. In Japanese (interdependent) couples, the negative correlation was absent or weakly positive. Soto, Perez, Kim, Lee, and Minnick ([2011, PMC4341898](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4341898/)) showed the same pattern in Hong Kong Chinese versus European Americans: expressive suppression associated with adverse psychological functioning for European Americans but not Chinese.

The careful caveat — and this is important — is that these findings are about *expressive suppression* specifically, the outward control of emotional display. They do not directly speak to the deeper emotion-identification deficit that defines alexithymia. A person who has fluent emotional self-knowledge but suppresses outward expression is not the same as a person who lacks the self-knowledge in the first place. Both can score high on TAS-20-style measures, but they're different constructs.

The honest reading: Levant's normative male alexithymia is a documented cost in independent-cultural contexts. In interdependent-cultural contexts, the same emotional-restraint pattern may serve different social functions and the cost calculus is different. This is not a "male alexithymia is good in some cultures" finding. It is a "the cost-benefit calculus of emotional restraint is culture-dependent" finding. Both the cost side and the context-dependent benefit side need to be held simultaneously rather than collapsed into one direction.

The Shapiro 2011 paper on "professional alexithymia" in medical education sits alongside this nuance. It is critical of what medical training does to its students — the framing is "medical education promotes professional alexithymia, and we should attend to this" — not endorsing emotional bypass as functional adaptation. When this paper appears in adaptive-function discussions, it should be read as Shapiro's critique of medical training, not as a license for the bypass.

For the user practically: if you are male and culturally exposed to restrictive masculine emotional socialisation, that probably contributes to your alexithymia at the population level. The size of the gender effect (d ≈ 0.22) is small enough that it doesn't predict much for any one individual. Most of your profile is not from gender per se — it's from the larger constitutional + developmental pattern. If gender socialisation is a main contributor in your case, Levant's ART exists; the broader category of relational therapy (MBT, attachment-focused, EFT, IFS) addresses developmental etiology more generally.

## Cross-cultural patterns and the construct-equivalence question

The descriptive observation across cross-cultural alexithymia studies is consistent: East Asian samples (Chinese, Japanese, Korean) show systematically elevated TAS-20 means relative to Western Anglosphere samples. Some Middle Eastern samples show elevated rates. North American Aboriginal populations show elevated rates in available studies. The Mexican PTSD outpatient cohort in Hernandez-Ramirez et al. (2025) found ~90 per cent above the alexithymia cutoff — but that's a single clinical-treatment-seeking cohort and is best treated as an outlier, not a representative cross-cultural prevalence.

The harder question is whether these elevations reflect genuinely higher rates of alexithymia in those populations, or whether the **TAS-20 measures something different in interdependent cultural contexts**. Both readings have support, and the literature has not produced a clean answer.

The genuine-higher-rates reading argues that interdependent cultures emphasise emotional restraint as a relational virtue. Across development, this reduces emotional vocabulary practice and self-reflection on emotion. The result is real cognitive alexithymia at higher population rates — not artefactually-elevated scores, but actual fewer-emotion-words developmental outcomes. There's some plausibility to this; cultures that socialise emotional restraint over emotional expressivity could produce measurably different vocabulary and self-reflection patterns.

The construct-non-equivalence reading argues that the TAS-20 was developed in Western-independent cultural contexts where emotional expressivity is a presumed individual norm. Items like "I prefer to think about practical problems rather than discuss my feelings" and "I find it hard to describe how I feel about people" are scored as alexithymic in that frame. In interdependent cultural contexts where emotional restraint is a relational norm, the same item may indicate culturally-fitted self-presentation rather than a deficit in emotional self-knowledge. Mean differences may be partly artefactual — a measurement-tool issue rather than a substantive prevalence difference.

Ryder, Sunohara, Dere, and Chentsova-Dutton ([2018, "Cultural Shaping of Alexithymia"](https://spectrum.library.concordia.ca/id/eprint/987976/1/2018%20-%20Ryder,%20Sunohara,%20Dere,%20&%20Chentsova-Dutton.pdf)) develop the construct-non-equivalence argument carefully. The construct as currently measured is built on Western-independent assumptions. Cross-cultural comparisons of population means may reflect both real cultural differences in emotion-language and real construct non-equivalence in the TAS-20.

Most of the cross-cultural literature converges on the position that *both* readings are partly true. There are real cultural differences in emotion-vocabulary and self-reflection norms. There is also real construct non-equivalence in the TAS-20 across cultures. Disentangling them with current measurement tools is hard. New instruments — the PAQ, the BVAQ — may help in time, but the cross-cultural construct-validity work is ongoing.

For the user, the practical takeaway is this. If you grew up in or are embedded in an interdependent cultural context, some of what looks like alexithymia in your TAS-20 score may be culturally-fitted self-presentation rather than a deficit in emotional self-knowledge. If you grew up in or are embedded in a Western-independent context, the standard alexithymia construct probably maps more cleanly onto your phenotype. Either way, the construct should be held loosely and supplemented with behavioural evidence about how you actually navigate emotional content.

## Age effects and stability

Some studies find that alexithymia rises gradually with age in non-clinical populations. The interpretation is contested. Three explanations are plausible:

- **Cohort effects.** Older cohorts were socialised in more restrictive emotional contexts. The cross-sectional age difference may reflect generational shifts in emotion-socialisation rather than aging per se.
- **Age-related cognitive changes.** Emotion-language access may be more effortful with age, even when the underlying trait is stable.
- **Sample selection.** Who shows up for studies by age band varies systematically; older participants in alexithymia research may be a particular subset.

The within-individual longitudinal picture is clearer than the cross-sectional age picture. Tolmunen, Heliste, Lehto, Hintikka, Honkalampi, and Kauhanen ([2011, PubMed 21081227](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21081227/)) and the [2017 follow-up at PubMed 28314554](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28314554/) tracked 3,083 subjects in the Finnish Health 2000 / 2011 cohort over 11 years. The mean TAS-20 was approximately 44.2 in 2000 and approximately 44.2 in 2011 — no detectable group-level drift. Relative-rank stability was high: someone at the 70th percentile in 2000 was likely still at or near the 70th percentile in 2011. This is the classic signature of trait status. Combined with the Danish twin study showing ~30 per cent heritability, the trait component is solidly empirically grounded.

The two findings are compatible. An individual's alexithymia is reasonably trait-stable across adulthood; population-level cross-sectional age differences may reflect cohort effects rather than age-related change within individuals. Both can be true.

What does fluctuate is the state component. TAS-20 scores rise during depressive episodes and partially remit when depression remits. Acute distress and trauma flare-ups produce transient elevations. Therapy response demonstrates that the trait isn't fixed — inpatient psychotherapy and structured outpatient interventions produce measurable change. The picture, then, is: a substantial trait-like core that is stable across years, with a meaningful state component that fluctuates with mood and life events, with both shaped by the constitutional and developmental pathways covered above.

For self-classification: a single TAS-20 score taken during a difficult period probably overstates the trait. If you can, retake during a relatively stable time. Pay attention to which items feel like "this is always true of me" versus "this is true right now but wasn't last year." That distinction is a rough proxy for the trait-state split.

## Primary, secondary, organic — etiology mapping

The Messina, Beadle, and Paradiso (2014) etiology heuristic, introduced in Cat 2, maps onto what we've covered in this category:

- **Primary** is constitutional. The 30 to 42 per cent heritable fraction. Lifelong; less likely to remit; corresponds to the user's "what's likely lifelong" bucket. Often co-occurs with autism, ADHD, and other conditions where the cognitive-architectural starting point shapes the alexithymic phenotype.
- **Secondary** is developmental. Trauma, attachment, gender socialisation, cultural shaping. Emerges over development; potentially more modifiable. Corresponds to the user's "what's potentially modifiable" bucket.
- **Organic** is acquired. Post-lesion (anterior insula); covered fully in Cat 4. Rarely fully modifiable; neurorehabilitation framework.

The Messina heuristic is exactly that — a heuristic. It is not a validated discrete classification scheme, and most people don't fit cleanly into one box. The clinically common case is mixed, especially primary plus secondary: someone who is both constitutionally autistic and trauma-exposed does not fit cleanly as either "primary" or "secondary." Both contribute. Both implicate different therapy approaches. Both have to be held simultaneously.

What's useful about the heuristic is the directional question it raises: which routes contributed how much in this case? That question is more productive than "which type am I?" because it doesn't force a categorical answer where a proportional answer is more honest.

## What's modifiable versus what's constitutional — practical implications

Bringing the threads together for the user's central question:

**The constitutional component** is roughly 30 to 42 per cent of variance at the population level. It is likely lifelong in any given individual. It is not necessarily a deficit — in some occupational and cognitive contexts, the trait pattern has documented functional value (framing-effect resistance, decision-making under explicit-probability conditions, bypass capacity in high-affect-load environments — covered more in Cat 11 / adaptive-function content). The therapy implications for the constitutional fraction are accommodation more than reversal. Externalised approaches — granularity-building (Barrett), affect-labeling (Lieberman 2007), structured journaling, scaffolded vocabulary work — tend to fit this fraction better than insistence on internal-introspection-first protocols.

**The developmental component** is roughly 58 to 70 per cent of variance at the population level, dominated by individual life experience rather than shared family environment. Childhood maltreatment and emotional neglect (Aaron 2023) is meta-analytically a strong predictor; trauma-informed approaches and relational therapies (mentalisation-based therapy, attachment-focused work, Emotion-Focused Therapy, IFS) target this fraction. Insecure dismissive-avoidant attachment (Lyvers 2019) is the most consistently associated style; mentalisation-based therapy has shown alexithymia reductions in BPD populations. Cultural shaping operates more on expression and severity than on absolute presence; Schouten 2020 and Soto 2011 show the cost calculus differs by cultural context. Gender socialisation (Levant NMAS) is a well-defined developmental pathway in independent-cultural contexts; the Levant ART psychoeducational intervention has small-to-moderate effects.

**The lesion-derived component** is rare in the general alexithymic population (about 15 per cent of TBI patients meet alexithymia criteria — Hogeveen 2016) and rarely fully modifiable. Compensatory strategies, environmental scaffolding, and neurorehabilitation are the relevant frameworks. Insight-oriented therapy generally not first line. This is covered in Cat 4.

The composite take. For most alexithymic adults, the picture is mixed — some constitutional contribution, some developmental contribution, very rarely lesion-derived. Estimating the precise ratio for any individual is impossible from current science. But the *direction* of the question — "Is this likely lifelong, or can I work with it?" — is answerable in proportions. The larger fraction is environmental and theoretically more modifiable. The smaller but not negligible fraction is constitutional and probably accommodation-target.

The Cameron, Ogrodniczuk, and Hadjipavlou ([2024, PMC11673933](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11673933/)) systematic review reports medium effects on TAS-20 across 21 RCT arms (1,251 participants) for psychological treatments. Medium effects, not transformative. The therapy goal does not have to be — and probably should not be — "reverse all of it." It can be: build enough emotional vocabulary and self-regulation to function well in the contexts that matter to you, address the developmental fraction where you have appetite for that work, and accommodate the constitutional fraction rather than fight it. That framing is more honest than promises of full reversal — and more empowering than treating the whole thing as a fixed deficit.

## The trauma-cluster preview — looking ahead to Cats 7 and 8

This category has presented maltreatment as a cause of alexithymia — one direction. The trauma cluster in this deep dive (the new Cats 7 and 8) will pick up the more complex picture later: the bidirectional dance between trauma and alexithymia.

Briefly previewed: the secondary alexithymia phenotype after childhood maltreatment is genuinely a developmental sequela. That direction — trauma builds alexithymia — is what this category covered. But for adults already in the alexithymic phenotype encountering new trauma (e.g., adult-onset PTSD), the alexithymia mediates emotional processing of the trauma in complex ways. Sometimes alexithymia is protective in the short term, dampening the immediate affective load of overwhelming events. Sometimes it inhibits recovery in the long term, blocking the emotional processing that would allow integration. The two directions interact across the lifespan in ways that are hard to disentangle.

The full picture awaits Cats 7 and 8. For now, this category establishes the developmental-origins half: trauma in childhood, attachment disruption, parental modeling, gender socialisation, and cultural shaping all operate during developmental windows to build a portion of the adult alexithymic phenotype. The constitutional fraction sits underneath and is largely there from the start. The interaction between them and the rest of life — including adult trauma — is what the next categories will address.
