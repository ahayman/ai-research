# United States & Iran: Decades of Confrontation

## 1. The Forgotten Friendship: Pre-1953 US-Iran Relations

Before the fateful year of 1953, Iranians viewed the United States not as an imperial aggressor but as a beacon of anti-colonialism — a "third force" that could counterbalance the predatory designs of Britain and Russia. This perception was grounded in reality.

### American Missionaries and Institution-Building

American Presbyterian missionaries arrived in Persia in 1834, focusing not on conversion but on education and medicine. By 1895, they had established 117 schools around Urmia, along with hospitals and medical schools. Unlike their Russian, British, and French counterparts, American missionaries had no obvious connection to state-sponsored political interests. They built institutions that served Iranians, and in doing so, cultivated genuine goodwill that lasted for over a century.

### W. Morgan Shuster: The American Anti-Imperialist

The most emblematic figure of early US-Iran friendship was William Morgan Shuster, an American lawyer invited by the Persian government in 1911 to serve as treasurer-general. Shuster arrived with the spirit of American independence, determined to make Persia a sovereign state free from colonialism. His efforts to establish financial independence for Iran directly threatened British and Russian interests. When Russia issued an ultimatum demanding Shuster's removal, and Britain backed the demand, Shuster was forced out — but not before becoming a hero in Iranian national memory. His book, *The Strangling of Persia*, documented how European powers crushed Persian sovereignty.

### The Anti-Imperial Image

At the turn of the 20th century, anti-imperialist sentiment ran strong among Americans, and many Iranians sympathetic to the Persian Constitutional Revolution (1905-1911) saw the United States as a natural ally. America seemed to oppose both Russian and British imperialism — the twin forces that had carved up Iranian sovereignty through treaties like Turkmenchay (1828) and the Anglo-Russian Convention (1907). Howard Baskerville, an American teacher who died fighting alongside constitutional revolutionaries in 1909, became another symbol of American solidarity.

This context matters enormously: the depth of betrayal Iranians felt after 1953 was proportional to the trust they had placed in America as a different kind of power.

## 2. The 1953 Coup: The Original Sin

### Why Did the United States Do It?

On August 19, 1953, Prime Minister Mohammad Mosaddegh — Iran's democratically elected leader — was overthrown in a coup d'état orchestrated by Britain's MI6 (Operation Boot) and the CIA (Operation Ajax). Understanding why the United States made this decision requires examining the interplay of Cold War paranoia, oil interests, and the influence of key individuals.

**Cold War Context:** By 1953, the Korean War was raging, McCarthyism gripped Washington, and the Eisenhower administration saw Soviet expansionism everywhere. British intelligence, led by MI6, skillfully reframed what was fundamentally a commercial dispute over oil nationalization as a Cold War crisis. They argued — without strong evidence — that Mosaddegh had communist leanings and would move Iran into the Soviet orbit. The Tudeh (Communist) Party's vocal support for Mosaddegh, though not indicative of his actual policies, provided a convenient pretext.

**Oil Interests:** The deeper trigger was Mosaddegh's nationalization of the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company (AIOC, later BP). When Mosaddegh sought to audit AIOC's books to verify royalty payments, the company refused. The Iranian parliament then voted unanimously to nationalize the oil industry. Britain, furious at losing its most profitable overseas asset, imposed a devastating embargo and sought American help. After the coup, the Shah signed over 40 percent of Iran's oil fields to US companies — a fact not lost on Iranians.

**The Dulles Brothers:** Secretary of State John Foster Dulles and CIA Director Allen Dulles were both deeply connected to the corporate world and ideologically committed to aggressive anti-communism. Their influence was decisive in convincing Eisenhower to approve the operation.

**The Operation:** CIA officer Kermit Roosevelt Jr. (grandson of Theodore Roosevelt) orchestrated the coup over four days, using CIA-funded agents to foment street unrest, bribe military officers, hire mobs, and run a media disinformation campaign. The first attempt failed on August 15, and the Shah fled to Baghdad and then Rome. But on August 19, a second attempt succeeded, and Mosaddegh was arrested.

### Consequences

The crushing of Iran's first democratic government ushered in more than two decades of dictatorship under the Shah, who relied heavily on US aid, arms, and SAVAK — a brutal secret police force trained by the CIA and Mossad. The CIA acknowledged its role in the coup only in 2013, admitting for the first time that it was "undemocratic."

In Iran's national narrative, a straight line leads from the 1953 coup to the 1979 Islamic Revolution. The coup became the foundational grievance of Iranian anti-Americanism — proof that the United States, despite its rhetoric about democracy, would overthrow a democratic government to serve its economic and strategic interests.

## 3. The Shah Era: America's Policeman in the Gulf

### The Alliance

Following the coup, the Shah became America's most important Middle Eastern ally. The relationship deepened dramatically under the Nixon Doctrine (1969), which held that the United States would support allies' defense but would not deploy its own troops. When Britain withdrew from the Persian Gulf in 1971, the Nixon administration adopted the "Twin Pillar" policy, designating Iran and Saudi Arabia as the twin pillars of regional stability.

### Massive Arms Sales

After Nixon's meeting with the Shah in May 1972, he ordered the abandonment of all arms sales restraint toward Iran. From 1970 to 1974, US arms agreements with Iran increased twenty-three-fold. For the first time, Nixon agreed to sell F-14 and F-15 fighters, some of the most advanced weapons in the US arsenal, to a non-NATO country. Washington's arms exports surged from $1.4 billion in 1971 to $16 billion by 1975, roughly half going to Iran.

The strategic logic was circular: the US advocated raising international oil prices in the early 1970s to enable Iran and Saudi Arabia to afford these expensive weapons purchases, which in turn enriched American defense contractors.

### Seeds of Revolution

The massive arms buildup, combined with the Shah's authoritarian modernization (the "White Revolution"), rapid urbanization, and SAVAK's repression of dissent, created deep social dislocation. The flood of American military advisors, technicians, and their families — numbering in the tens of thousands — became a visible symbol of foreign domination. Ayatollah Khomeini, exiled in 1964 partly for opposing the Status of Forces Agreement that granted American military personnel legal immunity in Iran, built his revolutionary movement on opposition to this perceived American occupation.

## 4. Revolution and Hostage Crisis (1979-1981)

### The Shock

The 1979 Islamic Revolution stunned the American foreign policy establishment, which had viewed the Shah's regime as unshakeable. When President Carter admitted the Shah to the United States for cancer treatment in October 1979, revolutionary students, fearing a repeat of 1953, stormed the US Embassy on November 4, 1979, taking 66 Americans hostage. Fifty-two were held for 444 days.

The hostage-takers explicitly invoked the 1953 coup as justification, calling the embassy a "den of spies" — and indeed, the embassy had been used for intelligence operations. The crisis dominated Carter's presidency, contributed to his electoral defeat, and established the pattern of US-Iran mutual hostility that persists to this day.

### Operation Eagle Claw

Carter's attempted rescue mission in April 1980 ended in disaster in the Iranian desert, with eight American servicemembers killed when helicopters collided. The humiliation deepened American frustration and helped propel Ronald Reagan to the presidency.

---

## 5. Reagan Era: Iran-Contra and the Tanker War

### Iran-Contra Scandal

Despite publicly vowing never to negotiate with hostage-takers, the Reagan administration secretly sold weapons to Iran through Israeli intermediaries, hoping to secure the release of American hostages held by Hezbollah in Lebanon. The proceeds were illegally diverted to fund Contra rebels in Nicaragua. When exposed in 1986, the Iran-Contra affair revealed the cynical pragmatism beneath anti-Iran rhetoric — and confirmed Iranian suspicions about American duplicity.

### The Tanker War (1987-1988)

When the Iran-Iraq War's "Tanker War" phase threatened oil shipments through the Persian Gulf, the US intervened with Operation Earnest Will (1987-1988), escorting Kuwaiti tankers reflagged under the American flag. After the USS Samuel B. Roberts struck an Iranian mine in April 1988, the US launched Operation Praying Mantis — the largest American naval engagement since World War II — destroying two Iranian oil platforms, sinking two ships, and severely damaging another.

### The USS Vincennes Tragedy

On July 3, 1988, the USS Vincennes shot down Iran Air Flight 655, a commercial Airbus A300, killing all 290 passengers and crew, including 66 children. The United States never formally apologized. In 1990, the ship's captain received the Legion of Merit for "exceptionally meritorious conduct." For Iranians, this incident epitomized American callousness toward Iranian lives and remains one of the deepest wounds in the relationship.

---

## 6. George H.W. Bush: "Goodwill Begets Goodwill"

In his 1989 inaugural address, President Bush signaled an opening: "Good will begets good will. Good faith can be a spiral that endlessly moves on." He quietly worked through the United Nations to gain the release of remaining American hostages in Lebanon, and the last was freed in 1991.

But Iranian leaders were reportedly angry and frustrated that their efforts to facilitate hostage releases did not produce the promised American goodwill — specifically, a relaxation of sanctions or US acknowledgment that Iraq had initiated the Iran-Iraq War. The opportunity evaporated, becoming another in the long line of missed chances between Washington and Tehran.

---

## 7. Clinton: Dual Containment and Missed Opportunities

### Dual Containment Policy

The Clinton administration adopted "dual containment" in 1993-94, simultaneously containing both Iran and Iraq — a departure from the traditional balance-of-power approach of playing the two off against each other. The policy, articulated by Martin Indyk, treated both countries as threats to US interests.

### The Khobar Towers Bombing (1996)

On June 25, 1996, a truck bomb at the Khobar Towers barracks in Saudi Arabia killed 19 US Air Force personnel. Evidence increasingly pointed to Iranian Revolutionary Guard involvement. The Clinton administration seriously considered military retaliation but ultimately chose restraint, partly because the Saudis, who feared escalation, refused to share critical intelligence with the FBI.

### The Khatami Opening

The surprise 1997 election of reformist Mohammad Khatami offered the best opportunity for rapprochement in a generation. Khatami signaled openness in a widely publicized CNN interview, expressing desire to bring down the "wall of mistrust." Clinton responded with modest gestures, and the administration explored dialogue.

But the moment was lost. In Tehran, hardliners sabotaged Khatami's outreach. In Washington, Clinton was consumed by domestic scandals, and the specter of Khobar complicated any rapprochement. The millennium ended without a breakthrough — another missed opportunity that might have altered the trajectory of the relationship.

---

## 8. George W. Bush: "Axis of Evil" and Strategic Blunder

### Post-9/11 Cooperation — Then Betrayal

After September 11, 2001, Iran actually cooperated with the United States in Afghanistan. Iranian diplomats provided crucial intelligence, helped organize anti-Taliban Northern Alliance forces, and participated constructively in the Bonn Conference that established the post-Taliban Afghan government. Iranian Foreign Minister Kamal Kharrazi later said Tehran expected this cooperation to open a new chapter.

Instead, in his January 2002 State of the Union address, President Bush labeled Iran part of the "Axis of Evil" alongside Iraq and North Korea. The speech sparked outrage across Iran's political spectrum — reformists and hardliners alike felt betrayed. It effectively destroyed the reformist movement's argument that engagement with America was possible.

### The Rejected Grand Bargain (2003)

In May 2003, Iran transmitted what appeared to be a comprehensive proposal for a "grand bargain" through Swiss Ambassador Tim Guldimann. The proposal reportedly offered to put everything on the table: nuclear transparency, recognition of Israel's right to exist, termination of support for Hamas and Hezbollah's military activities, and cooperation on Iraq. In return, Iran sought security guarantees, normalization of relations, and the lifting of sanctions.

The Bush administration, riding high after the rapid fall of Baghdad, dismissed the offer. Officials later claimed the proposal lacked formal Iranian authorization, though Guldimann insisted it had high-level Iranian backing. Many analysts consider the rejection one of the most consequential missed opportunities in US foreign policy.

### Iraq Invasion Empowers Iran

The supreme irony of the Bush era is that the 2003 invasion of Iraq — partly justified by the "Axis of Evil" framework — dramatically empowered Iran. The removal of Saddam Hussein eliminated Iran's primary regional adversary, installed a Shia-majority government in Baghdad with deep ties to Tehran, and created a vacuum that Iranian influence rushed to fill. The $2 trillion war and occupation that cost over 4,400 American lives produced the opposite of its intended strategic effect.

## 9. Obama: Outreach and the JCPOA

### The Opening

Barack Obama entered office in 2009 committed to engagement. He sent two private letters to Supreme Leader Khamenei and, in a Nowruz (Persian New Year) message, addressed Iran's leaders and people with unprecedented respect. However, Iran's disputed 2009 election and brutal crackdown on the Green Movement complicated outreach.

### Secret Negotiations

Beginning in 2012, secret backchannel talks between US and Iranian officials in Oman laid the groundwork for formal negotiations. The election of Hassan Rouhani as Iran's president in 2013, on a platform of nuclear engagement, provided a willing partner.

### The JCPOA (2015)

After 687 days of negotiations and record-setting diplomatic marathon sessions in Vienna, the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action was finalized on July 14, 2015, between Iran and the P5+1 (US, UK, France, Russia, China, plus Germany). Under the deal:

- Iran agreed to reduce its enriched uranium stockpile by 98%, limit enrichment to 3.67%, reduce centrifuges from 19,000 to about 6,000, convert the Fordow enrichment facility to a research center, redesign the Arak heavy water reactor, and accept the most intrusive inspections regime in history.
- In return, nuclear-related sanctions were lifted, releasing approximately $100 billion in frozen assets.

The IAEA consistently confirmed Iran's compliance with the agreement. The deal represented the most significant US-Iran diplomatic achievement since the revolution.

## 10. Trump's First Term: Maximum Pressure and Soleimani

### JCPOA Withdrawal (2018)

On May 8, 2018, President Trump unilaterally withdrew the United States from the JCPOA, despite unanimous IAEA confirmation that Iran was in compliance. Trump argued the deal was "defective at its core," did not address Iran's missile program or regional activities, and had insufficient "sunset" provisions.

### Maximum Pressure Campaign

Trump reimposed and expanded sanctions — over 1,500 in total — targeting Iran's financial, oil, shipping, and petrochemical sectors. The campaign aimed to reduce Iran's oil exports to zero and crash its economy, theoretically forcing Tehran to negotiate a "better deal." Iran's currency lost roughly 70% of its value, inflation soared, and ordinary Iranians bore the brunt.

### The Soleimani Assassination (January 2020)

On January 3, 2020, a US drone strike near Baghdad International Airport killed Major General Qasem Soleimani, commander of the IRGC's Quds Force and widely considered the second most powerful person in Iran. Five Iraqi nationals and four other Iranians were also killed, including the deputy chairman of Iraq's Popular Mobilization Forces.

Iran retaliated on January 8 by launching rockets at Al-Asad Air Base in Iraq, causing concussive brain injuries to over 100 American service members but no fatalities — a calibrated response designed to demonstrate capability while avoiding escalation to full-scale war.

The assassination was a watershed moment. From the American perspective, Soleimani was responsible for the deaths of hundreds of US soldiers through the IEDs and militias he directed in Iraq. From the Iranian perspective, it was the assassination of a national hero — equivalent, in Iranian eyes, to a foreign power killing a top US military commander.

---

## 11. Biden: Failed JCPOA Revival

Joe Biden entered office pledging to return to the JCPOA if Iran came back into compliance. Negotiations resumed in Vienna in April 2021, but were plagued by complications: the election of hardliner Ebrahim Raisi as Iran's president, Russia's invasion of Ukraine (which complicated P5+1 unity), and mutual escalatory steps.

By late 2023, the talks had effectively collapsed. Iran had advanced its nuclear program far beyond JCPOA limits, accumulating enriched uranium at 60% purity — far beyond any civilian justification and just a short technical step from weapons-grade. JCPOA provisions began to expire. The window for diplomatic resolution appeared to be closing.

## 12. Trump 2.0 (2025-Present): From Diplomacy to War

### Renewed Maximum Pressure

On February 4, 2025, Trump signed a national security memorandum reimposing "maximum pressure" on Iran, aiming to deny Tehran all paths to a nuclear weapon and counter its regional influence. The administration proposed a 25% tariff on any country or company conducting business with Tehran, escalating from financial pressure to trade punishment.

### The Letter to Khamenei

On March 7, 2025, Trump announced he had written directly to Supreme Leader Khamenei, expressing desire for nuclear negotiations but warning that failure to engage would expose Iran to "serious military consequences." Iran cautiously agreed, and on April 12, 2025, negotiations began. Trump set a 60-day deadline.

### Operation Midnight Hammer (June 2025)

When negotiations stalled over Iran's refusal to halt all uranium enrichment, Trump ordered military action. On June 21, 2025, the US bombed the Fordow enrichment facility, the Natanz nuclear facility, and the Isfahan nuclear technology center. The White House called Operation Midnight Hammer an "overwhelmingly successful mission." This was followed by the 12-Day War (June 13-24, 2025), in which Israel conducted nearly 360 attacks across 27 Iranian provinces.

### The 2026 Escalation

Following the 2025-2026 Iranian protests — which began in December 2025 amid economic collapse and saw thousands killed in regime crackdowns — the United States and Israel launched a major joint operation in late February 2026. On February 28, 2026, Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei was assassinated in a strike on his compound, along with members of his family. Around 2,000 strikes were conducted across Iran, with over 600 civilian casualties reported by the Red Crescent.

Iran retaliated by launching over 500 ballistic and naval missiles and almost 2,000 drones at targets in Israel and US military bases across the region. As of early March 2026, Trump stated the war would last "4-5 weeks" and demanded Iran's "unconditional surrender."

## 13. Domestic Political Forces Shaping US Iran Policy

### AIPAC and the Israel Lobby

The American Israel Public Affairs Committee (AIPAC) has consistently advocated for a hardline approach to Iran, lobbying for "crippling" sanctions and opposing diplomatic engagement. AIPAC-organized trips to Israel have become a rite of passage for new members of Congress, and its network of donors provides substantial financial support to political campaigns. AIPAC actively opposed the JCPOA and supported its dissolution.

### Neoconservative Ideology

The neoconservative movement, prominent in the George W. Bush administration, advocated for regime change in Iran as part of a broader project to democratize the Middle East through American power. Think tanks associated with this ideology — the American Enterprise Institute, the Hudson Institute, the Washington Institute for Near East Policy — have consistently pushed for confrontational Iran policies.

### Evangelical Christian Zionism

Christians United for Israel, with over seven million members, is the largest pro-Israel lobbying group in America — larger than AIPAC itself. Many members of Congress are openly Christian Zionist, bringing theological convictions about Israel's biblical significance into their foreign policy positions. This creates a powerful constituency supporting hawkish positions toward Iran, which is perceived as Israel's primary threat.

### Oil and Energy Interests

American oil companies have had complex interests regarding Iran. While some would benefit from Iranian oil reaching global markets (reducing prices), others benefit from Iranian oil being sanctioned (reducing competition and keeping prices higher). The broader energy industry's influence on Middle East policy cannot be separated from Iran policy.

## 14. How Each Side Views the Other

### The Iranian Perspective: "The Great Satan"

The epithet "Great Satan" was coined by Ayatollah Khomeini on November 5, 1979, to characterize the United States as an imperialist power sponsoring corruption worldwide. The narrative resonates because it is anchored in genuine historical grievances:

- The 1953 coup that destroyed Iranian democracy
- Support for the Shah's authoritarian rule and SAVAK's repression
- The USS Vincennes shootdown of Flight 655 with no apology
- Decades of sanctions that punish ordinary Iranians
- Support for Saddam Hussein during the Iran-Iraq War (including tolerance of his chemical weapons use)
- Surrounding Iran with military bases

The regime has institutionalized anti-Americanism through textbooks, annual "Death to America" rallies, and state media. However, public opinion is more nuanced — many ordinary Iranians, particularly younger generations, distinguish between the American government and American people, and some polls suggest anti-Americanism is less pervasive among citizens than official narratives imply.

### The American Perspective: Terrorism, Nuclear Threat, and Hostages

For most Americans, Iran is associated with:

- The 1979 hostage crisis — the defining trauma that shaped a generation's view
- State sponsorship of terrorism (Hezbollah, Hamas, Islamic Jihad)
- Nuclear weapons proliferation
- The killing of American soldiers in Iraq through Iranian-backed militias
- "Death to America" chants
- Threats to Israel, America's closest Middle East ally

The American media narrative tends to present Iran as irrational and ideologically driven, with less attention to the historical context that produced Iranian grievances. The concept of Iranian strategic rationality — acting within comprehensible national interests — receives less public attention than the "mad mullah" caricature.

## 15. How US Decisions Created the Current Situation

A sober accounting reveals that many of the conditions the United States now finds most threatening about Iran were directly or indirectly produced by American policy choices:

1. **The nuclear program**: Iran's nuclear ambitions accelerated after the US withdrew from the JCPOA, removing the constraints that had frozen the program.
2. **Regional proxy networks**: Iran built its "Axis of Resistance" partly as a deterrent against perceived American (and Israeli) threats of regime change.
3. **Anti-American ideology**: The 1953 coup provided the founding narrative for revolutionary anti-Americanism.
4. **Iranian influence in Iraq**: The 2003 invasion created the vacuum Iran filled.
5. **Alliance with Russia and China**: US sanctions drove Iran toward alternative great-power patrons.
6. **Hardliner ascendancy**: Each cycle of American escalation (Axis of Evil speech, JCPOA withdrawal, maximum pressure, Soleimani assassination) strengthened hardliners who argued that engagement with America was futile.

This is not to absolve Iran's government of responsibility for its own choices — including domestic repression, support for violent proxies, and nuclear ambiguity — but to recognize that the US-Iran confrontation is a co-created tragedy in which each side's actions have consistently produced the opposite of their stated intentions.
