# Register, Code-Switching, and Autism: Research Synthesis

## Category 7: Register and Code-Switching

### Research Focus
The intersection of linguistic register, code-switching, and autism -- how autistic individuals navigate register shifting, the "pedantic speech" phenomenon, the cognitive costs of register management, and neurodiversity-affirming approaches to register awareness.

---

## 1. Autistic Register Use: Research Foundations

### 1.1 The Core Challenge: Context-Dependent Language Adaptation

Two key diagnostic criteria for autism are impairments in social communication and difficulties with adapting behavior to particular contexts. Register shifting -- the ability to adjust language formality, vocabulary, and style based on social context -- sits directly at this intersection (Malkin & Abbot-Smith, 2021).

**Key research findings on register adaptation:**

- Autistic individuals often have difficulty adapting language for particular listeners and contexts. This is not merely a vocabulary deficit but a pragmatic one: the challenge lies in *selecting* the appropriate register, not in *possessing* the linguistic resources (Malkin & Abbot-Smith, 2021).

- In a study comparing autistic and neurotypical children (Cola et al., 2022), neurotypical participants adjusted their talkativeness based on partner engagement cues (more talkative with interested partners, less with bored ones). The autistic group "did not differentially adapt their behavior to the Bored versus Interested context, instead remaining consistently talkative in both." This demonstrates a pattern of register consistency rather than register shifting.

- Malkin & Abbot-Smith (2021) found that autistic children were less likely to provide a context-appropriate amount of information overall than neurotypical peers. Critically, when object re-description was required (switching from calling something "the cup" to "the big cup" when context changed), autistic children only produced an appropriate description half the time. Without the need to re-describe, they could take listener needs into account.

- Research on cognitive flexibility (executive function) confirms that set-shifting -- the ability to switch from one learned rule to a new rule -- is a persistent difficulty across the autistic lifespan, from childhood into adulthood (meta-analysis findings). This cognitive flexibility deficit directly impacts the ability to shift registers fluidly.

### 1.2 Vermeulen's "Context Blindness" Framework

Peter Vermeulen's (2012, 2015) concept of "context blindness" provides a unifying theoretical framework for understanding register difficulties in autism. Context blindness is defined as "a reduced spontaneous use of context when giving meaning to a stimulus."

**Relevance to register:**
- Register selection depends heavily on reading contextual cues: Where am I? Who am I talking to? What is the social expectation here? What power dynamics are at play?
- Context blindness suggests that autistic individuals may not spontaneously process these contextual signals, leading to register selections that feel "off" to neurotypical listeners -- not because the autistic person lacks register knowledge, but because the context-to-register mapping doesn't happen automatically.
- Vermeulen argues that context blindness unifies existing cognitive models (theory of mind, executive functioning, central coherence) and explains why social situations become particularly challenging: the inability to read context and respond in expected ways can be disabling.

### 1.3 The Pragmatic Language Profile

Register management is part of a broader pragmatic language profile in autism. Key pragmatic skills include:
- Using language for different purposes (requesting, informing, joking, etc.)
- **Changing language according to the needs of a listener or situation** (this IS register)
- Understanding non-literal language (irony, sarcasm, implicature)
- Following rules for conversations (turn-taking, topic maintenance)

Research consistently finds that pragmatic language deficits are more common than structural language deficits in autism. An important distinction from the research: autistic individuals may show typical pragmatic abilities when measured with guided paradigms that provide scaffolded support, but struggle to recruit those skills spontaneously in unscaffolded, real-time social interaction. This suggests the knowledge may be present but the spontaneous, real-time deployment is the challenge.

## 2. The Pedantic/Formal Speech Pattern

### 2.1 Defining Pedantic Speech

The language of autistic individuals is frequently described by clinicians as having a "pedantic quality": overly formal, adult-like, and inappropriate for the conversational setting. The term does not have a standard clinical definition, but typically includes speech that is:
- Lengthy and containing too much detail for the context
- More similar to written language than spoken language
- Using rare or technical lexical items with formal phrasing ("bookish" quality)
- Including factual, accurate, specific, and/or technical information that is too detailed for a particular context

Luyster, Zane, & Wisman Weil (2022) updated the framework for unconventional spoken language in autism, categorizing pedantic language as a "generative" feature (created by the speaker, not merely repeated). They define pedantic language as involving "the combination of rare lexical items with formal phrasing, making the individual sound 'bookish'."

### 2.2 The "Little Professor" Phenomenon

Hans Asperger himself described children with what he called "autistic psychopathy" as having formal, adult-like speech. The colloquial term "little professor" emerged to describe autistic children whose:
- Voice pattern is more adult-like than child-like
- Vocabulary is sophisticated and formal for their age
- Speech includes "abnormalities such as verbosity, abrupt transitions, literal interpretations and miscomprehension of nuance, use of metaphor meaningful only to the speaker, and unusually pedantic, formal, or idiosyncratic speech"

Historically, a pedantic quality of speech was used to distinguish Asperger's syndrome from other autism presentations, though subsequent research has found this pattern across the autism spectrum more broadly.

### 2.3 Why Formal Register May Be the Default

Several explanations have been proposed for why autistic individuals may default to formal register:

**A. Language Acquired from Non-Social Sources**

A growing body of research documents atypical language acquisition in autism. Some autistic children learn language primarily from books, television, and other media rather than (or in addition to) social interaction:

- Kissine et al. (2019) documented spontaneous and productive use of Modern Standard Arabic (MSA) in Tunisian autistic boys, where television was the most important source. In a diglossic community where everyone speaks colloquial Arabic in daily life, these children used the *formal* television variety in everyday interactions.

- Francis et al. (2024) found that out of 5,314 Arabic-speaking children, only 0.46% used formal language in everyday contexts -- and 86% of those were diagnosed with ASD. The use of formal language did not correlate with parents' education or amount of exposure to MSA, but *did* correlate with ASD severity.

- Multiple case studies document autistic children acquiring productive mastery of languages they were exposed to only through screens, "attesting to the fact that autistic individuals may be interested in language in and of itself" (Kissine et al., 2023).

This has direct implications for register: if language is acquired primarily from books and media (which tend to use formal registers), the "default" register would naturally be more formal than if language were acquired primarily through casual social interaction.

**B. Preference for Precision**

Autistic individuals often value precision and clarity in communication. Formal register tends to be more precise, explicit, and unambiguous than informal register. The preference for formal language may reflect a genuine communicative value: saying exactly what you mean, with maximum clarity.

**C. Reduced Awareness of Register Norms**

Context blindness (Vermeulen) suggests that the social cues signaling "this is a casual situation" or "formal language is expected here" may not be spontaneously processed. Without these cues, the speaker may not perceive a reason to shift from their default register.

**D. Comfort with Rule-Governed Systems**

Formal language tends to follow more explicit, codified rules than informal language. Informal register involves more implicit knowledge, slang that changes rapidly, and social conventions that aren't written down. For individuals who prefer explicit rule systems, formal register may feel more navigable and predictable.

### 2.4 How Pedantic Speech Is Perceived

The reception of pedantic/formal speech depends heavily on context:

**Contexts where formal register is valued:**
- Academic settings, research environments, scientific communication
- Technical/engineering workplaces
- Written communication (formal register maps well onto writing)
- Professional presentations and lectures

**Contexts where formal register creates friction:**
- Casual conversations with peers
- Social gatherings, small talk
- Informal workplace interactions (break room, water cooler)
- Text messaging, casual online communication

The same speech pattern can be perceived as "impressive" or "intelligent" in academic contexts and as "weird," "cold," "robotic," or "condescending" in casual ones. This inconsistency in social reception is itself a source of confusion for autistic individuals who may not understand why the same communication style elicits such different responses.

## 3. Code-Switching, Masking, and Autistic Identity

### 3.1 Defining Code-Switching in the Autistic Context

Code-switching is adapting your communication style based on context -- switching between languages, dialects, registers, or behavioral presentations depending on who you're speaking with and where. For neurodivergent individuals, code-switching also includes adjusting or suppressing neurodivergent traits, behaviors, or ways of communicating in order to appear more "normal."

A critical distinction has emerged in the literature between:
- **Code-switching**: Adapting communication style while staying true to who you are (a skill that benefits the individual)
- **Masking/camouflaging**: Hiding your authentic self to fit in and survive (a survival strategy that depletes you)

However, this distinction is debated. Gerlach-Houck & DeThorne (2023) proposed that speech-language pathologists could offer "autistic code-switching" as a non-ableist social communication tool for high-stakes situations. Lipari (2023) responded critically, arguing that framing autistic masking as mere "code-switching" minimizes its harmful effects and that teaching autistic people to camouflage perpetuates ableism by placing the burden of adaptation entirely on the autistic individual.

### 3.2 Hull's Camouflaging Framework (CAT-Q)

Hull et al. (2017, 2019) developed the most widely used framework for understanding autistic camouflaging, categorized into three factors:

1. **Compensation** -- Strategies for overcoming social challenges, such as using learned social scripts, imitating observed behaviors, or preparing conversation topics in advance. *Register-relevant: learning to perform a casual register through deliberate study of how others speak informally.*

2. **Masking** -- Efforts to hide or suppress autistic traits to appear more neurotypical. *Register-relevant: suppressing the natural tendency toward formal/precise language and forcing a casual tone.*

3. **Assimilation** -- Attempts to fit into social situations by modifying behaviors to blend in. *Register-relevant: matching the register of the surrounding group even when it feels unnatural.*

The Camouflaging Autistic Traits Questionnaire (CAT-Q) was validated with 354 autistic and 478 non-autistic adults, showing high internal consistency (Cronbach's alpha = 0.94 total; compensation 0.91, masking 0.85, assimilation 0.92). Research identifies that autistic females camouflage more frequently across all three subscales and in more varied situations.

### 3.3 The Cognitive Cost of Conscious Register Management

For neurotypical individuals, register shifting is largely automatic -- context cues trigger appropriate register selection without much conscious effort. For many autistic individuals, register shifting requires conscious, deliberate cognitive work:

**The cognitive load includes:**
- Monitoring the social context for cues about expected register
- Suppressing the default/natural register
- Selecting and deploying the "appropriate" register
- Monitoring one's own output for register consistency
- Monitoring the listener's reaction for signs of register mismatch
- Adjusting in real-time based on feedback

This is functionally equivalent to a second language speaker constantly translating in their head -- possible but exhausting. Research consistently shows:

- Camouflaging is "emotionally exhausting for autistic adults and can result in burnout due to the cognitive demands required to engage in such behavior" (systematic review, 2025).
- The fatigue includes cognitive exhaustion from constant self-monitoring, emotional exhaustion from hiding natural responses, and physical exhaustion from suppressing natural behaviors.
- A 2024 validation study (AASPIRE Autistic Burnout Measure) found burnout includes "decreased cognitive abilities, decreased emotional regulation, increased sensitivity, decreased everyday abilities, increased avoidance or withdrawal, and increased exhaustion."
- Sustained masking has been shown to correlate with higher burnout severity across multiple studies.

### 3.4 The Identity Question: "Which Register Is the Real Me?"

Register management intersects deeply with questions of autistic identity:

- Autistic adults report that constant masking creates identity confusion: "I Like My Masked Self Better Than My Real Self -- Whoever That Even Is" (title of a widely-cited personal account from Reframing Autism).

- Research shows autistic people experience higher levels of identity distress and conflict, with identity distress mediating the relationship between masking and psychological distress.

- Higher self-reported masking behaviors are associated with lower self-esteem, lower authenticity, lower participation in the autistic community, and higher anxiety and depression symptoms (PMC, 2024).

- The goal for many autistic individuals is developing the choice to mask and unmask, and knowing "what is actually ME versus what is an intentional choice to act neurotypical because they have the capacity that day."

**Register-specific identity implications:**
- If someone's natural register is formal/precise, is that their "real" voice? Or is the casual register they've learned to perform also "real"?
- Some autistic adults report that learning to code-switch felt like gaining a useful tool -- it expanded their communicative range. Others report it felt like learning to be someone else.
- The neurodiversity-affirming approach suggests that the formal/precise register is not a deficit but a valid communication style, and the goal should be understanding when and why register shifts happen in society -- not performing them as a survival mechanism.

### 3.5 Autistic Burnout and Sustained Register Performance

The 2025 systematic review synthesizing 48 studies and approximately 4,000 autistic people established autistic burnout as a coherent, measurable construct distinct from occupational burnout or depression. Sustained masking -- including sustained register performance -- is consistently identified as a primary contributor.

Consequences of sustained register performance include:
- Mental exhaustion and cognitive depletion
- Deterioration of memory, speech, and executive function
- Increased sensory sensitivity
- Withdrawal from social interaction
- Depression, anxiety, and suicidal ideation
- Loss of skills that were previously reliable

The relationship is dose-dependent: more masking equals higher burnout risk. This has direct implications for register teaching: any approach that requires constant register performance is potentially harmful.

## 4. The "Flat Register" Hypothesis

### 4.1 Register Consistency Across Contexts

Research suggests that many autistic individuals use a more consistent register across contexts than neurotypical individuals. This manifests as:

- Using the same vocabulary, sentence structure, and formality level whether talking to a boss, a friend, or a stranger
- Not shifting into "small talk" mode in casual settings
- Maintaining the same level of precision and detail regardless of audience
- Not adopting the slang, idioms, or casual speech patterns of a peer group

The Cola et al. (2022) study provides direct evidence: autistic participants remained "consistently talkative" across interested and bored conversation partners, while neurotypical participants modulated their behavior. This is not a deficit in talkativeness per se -- it's a demonstration of register consistency.

### 4.2 Is It "Flat" or "Different Default"?

The term "flat register" may be misleading. Several considerations:

**It's not necessarily "flat":**
- The register may be rich, detailed, and expressive -- just consistently so across contexts
- Autistic speech may include significant variation in vocabulary, topic depth, and engagement -- the variation just doesn't correlate with social context in the expected way
- The "flat" perception may partly stem from prosodic differences (pitch, intonation) that are separate from register per se

**It may be a "different default":**
- The default register for many autistic individuals seems to be formal/precise rather than casual
- This default may have been learned from books, media, and formal educational contexts rather than from casual social interaction
- The register is functional and communicatively effective -- it just doesn't match NT expectations for casual contexts

**Prosodic factors compound the perception:**
- Research on prosody in autism shows complex patterns. Meta-analyses find that many autistic speakers actually produce *more melodic* intonation than controls, yet naive listeners perceive their speech as *more monotonous* -- suggesting that it's the *pattern* of prosodic variation, not its absence, that creates the "flat" perception.
- Decreased pitch excursion and higher pitch are associated with higher ASD symptom severity (Nature, 2021).
- Prosodic differences can "override" accurate register use, making speech that is correctly modulated for content sound flat or disengaged to listeners attuned to different prosodic cues.

### 4.3 Strengths of Register Consistency

Register consistency has genuine communicative strengths:
- **Authenticity and honesty**: What you see is what you get. The same person shows up in every context.
- **Reliability and predictability**: Communication partners know what to expect.
- **Clarity and precision**: Formal register tends to be less ambiguous.
- **Competence in formal contexts**: The default register is well-suited for academic, professional, and technical communication.
- **Resistance to social manipulation**: Consistent register makes it harder to be drawn into social games based on register matching.

### 4.4 Costs of Register Consistency

- **Social friction in casual contexts**: Formal language can be perceived as cold, condescending, or "showing off" in casual settings.
- **Difficulty building informal rapport**: Many social bonds form through casual register (banter, slang, shared informal language).
- **Workplace challenges**: The informal social layer of workplaces (break room chat, after-work socializing) often requires register shifting.
- **Perceived inaccessibility**: Others may assume the speaker is being deliberately distant or superior.

## 5. Autistic-Autistic Register Norms

### 5.1 The Double Empathy Problem and Communication

Milton's (2012) Double Empathy Problem fundamentally reframes autistic social difficulties. Rather than locating the problem within the autistic individual, it proposes that communication breakdowns arise from *mutual* misunderstanding between different neurotypes. This has direct implications for register:

- Communication difficulties are not inherent to autistic people but emerge from "bidirectional differences in dispositions (e.g., communication style, social-cognitive characteristics) and experiences between autistic and non-autistic individuals."
- When the register mismatch is removed (autistic-autistic interaction), communication may flow naturally.

### 5.2 Crompton's Information Transfer Research

Catherine Crompton and colleagues have produced the most rigorous empirical evidence on autistic-autistic communication:

- **Information transfer** (Crompton et al., 2020): Autistic people recall information shared by autistic peers as effectively as non-autistic people recall information shared by non-autistic peers. Information sharing is significantly poorer in chains of mixed neurotypes. "These results provide the first empirical evidence that difficulties in autistic communication are apparent only when interacting with non-autistic people."

- **Rapport** (Crompton et al., 2020; Foster et al., 2025): Rapport is higher with others of the same neurotype and when diagnosis is disclosed. Non-autistic pairings reported the highest rapport, autistic pairings the second-highest, and mixed neurotype pairings the lowest. Autistic individuals' rapport declined when in the company of non-autistic group members.

- A 2025 large-scale replication found some complexity in these findings, suggesting variability across methodologies and samples, but the overall direction of findings supports the double empathy framework.

### 5.3 Autistic Communication Norms

Qualitative research on autistic social communication reveals distinct register norms:

**Valued communication features in autistic-autistic interaction:**
- **Directness and literalness**: Words taken at face value; reduced need for subtext decoding
- **Infodumping**: Sharing detailed, passionate information about topics of interest -- "a way of bonding and showing affection" and "a love language for autistic people"
- **Precision**: Detailed, accurate language is appreciated rather than seen as "too much"
- **Asking clarifying questions**: Considered polite (though non-autistic people often misinterpret this as rude, challenging authority, or being difficult)
- **Reduced monitoring**: Participants "did not have to monitor themselves for monologuing, tangents, etc., or others for hidden agendas or non-literal language"
- **Explicit communication**: Intentions and meanings are stated directly rather than implied

**Research from "A Difference in Typical Values" (Scandinavian Journal of Disability Research, 2024):**
- Participants identified other autistic people as sharing social norms with them
- Technical features included interrupting (understood as engagement, not rudeness), monologuing about special interests, and tangent-oriented conversational style
- Differences in norms reflected "empathy, mutual accommodation, feelings of safety and acceptance"
- Participants did not have to perform neurotypical register norms, leading to greater comfort and authenticity

### 5.4 Online Autistic Community Register Norms

Online autistic communities have developed distinctive register patterns:
- Text-based communication (often preferred over face-to-face) allows more processing time and removes prosodic ambiguity
- Infodumping is explicitly welcomed and reciprocated
- Tone indicators (/s for sarcasm, /gen for genuine, /lh for light-hearted) serve as explicit register markers that replace implicit contextual cues
- Direct communication is the norm; indirectness may be seen as confusing rather than polite
- Content warnings and clear labeling of topic shifts are valued (explicit discourse management)
- "Interest-based socializing" replaces small talk: conversations center on shared topics rather than phatic communion

## 6. Register and Power Dynamics (Hierarchy Connection)

### 6.1 Autistic Relationship to Social Hierarchy

Register selection in NT communication is heavily determined by power dynamics: you speak differently to your boss than to your friend, to a judge than to a peer. This creates a specific challenge for autistic individuals:

- "The intuitive concept of social hierarchy that neurotypical people seem to learn and fall into as children doesn't appear to happen the same way for autistic folks. Instead of learning implicitly through their environment and social interactions, many autistic people seem to need to learn this concept explicitly."

- "For many autistic people, everyone is equal until they are told otherwise." This egalitarian default means register shifts based on social status feel arbitrary and unmotivated.

- Autistic people "tend to ignore dominance/authority structures unless they actually make sense to them. If the most qualified person is in charge, they will respect that without issue." Register deference based on position alone (rather than competence) may feel inauthentic.

### 6.2 Workplace Register Challenges

The workplace creates specific register challenges:
- Interpreting a "casual suggestion" from authority as a strict command (or vice versa)
- Not recognizing the weight of an important directive when it's communicated informally
- Being perceived as disrespectful for using the same register with a manager as with a peer
- "Group dynamics in workplaces can marginalize autistics who do not participate in the same way as their peers" -- informal register performance is often a prerequisite for social inclusion at work

## 7. Register and Politeness (Politeness Connection)

### 7.1 Politeness Strategies and Register

In Brown & Levinson's (1987) politeness theory, speakers manage face-threatening acts through specific linguistic strategies that are deeply register-dependent:

- **Bald-on-record**: Direct, unmitigated language (typical of very informal or very urgent registers)
- **Positive politeness**: Building solidarity, in-group language (informal register)
- **Negative politeness**: Deference, hedging, indirectness (formal register)
- **Off-record**: Hints, implicature (requires shared contextual knowledge)

Research on autism and politeness (Tikiz-Erturk & Demiroz, 2025) found that a 15-year-old autistic language learner showed "persistent challenges in comprehending abstract concepts, conversational implicature, and social role recognition" but could employ "politeness strategies, off-record communication, and explicit verbal markers" as adaptive strategies. This suggests register-dependent politeness can be learned, but the mapping between context and strategy selection remains challenging.

Autistic individuals may default to bald-on-record and negative politeness strategies (direct, formal) while finding positive politeness (casual solidarity-building) and off-record strategies (hints, implicature) more difficult. This aligns with the broader pattern of defaulting to formal register.

### 7.2 Hedging as a Register Variable

Hedging -- the use of qualifiers, softeners, and uncertainty markers -- varies dramatically across registers:
- Academic register: Heavy hedging ("It would appear that...", "One might argue...")
- Casual register: Moderate hedging ("I think maybe...", "It kinda seems like...")
- Direct register: Minimal hedging ("This is wrong", "I disagree")

Autistic speakers may have asymmetric hedging patterns: comfortable with academic hedging (rule-governed, explicit) but less comfortable with casual hedging (implicit, socially calibrated). This creates the paradox of sounding simultaneously too formal (academic hedging) and too blunt (insufficient casual hedging) in everyday conversation.

## 8. Register and Discourse Markers (Discourse Marker Connection)

### 8.1 Discourse Markers as Register Signals

Discourse markers (DMs) -- words like "you know," "like," "I mean," "so," "actually," "well" -- serve dual functions: they manage discourse flow AND they signal register. Their frequency and type shift dramatically between formal and informal registers:

- Informal register: High DM frequency ("Like, you know, I was just thinking, right, that maybe we should, like, go?")
- Formal register: Low DM frequency, different DMs ("Furthermore," "Moreover," "In other words")
- Academic register: Specialized DMs ("However," "Nevertheless," "That being said")

For autistic individuals:
- DMs that serve purely social/phatic functions (building rapport, signaling in-group membership) may be underused
- DMs that serve logical/organizational functions ("therefore," "however," "specifically") may be used at typical or elevated rates
- The mismatch between DM type and social context contributes to the perception of pedantic or formal speech
- Casual DMs like "like" and "you know" function as register softeners; their absence makes speech sound more formal even when the content is casual

## 9. Practical Approaches: Neurodiversity-Affirming Register Education

### 9.1 Core Principles

Any approach to teaching register awareness to autistic individuals should follow neurodiversity-affirming principles:

1. **Register awareness, not register performance**: The goal is understanding *why* registers shift and what different registers signal -- not requiring constant performance of a specific register.

2. **Respect the natural register**: The autistic individual's default register (often formal/precise) is valid and communicatively effective. It's not a problem to be fixed but a style to be understood.

3. **Explicit mapping, not implicit acquisition**: Since register norms are typically acquired implicitly, autistic individuals may benefit from explicit, systematic instruction about what register is expected in which contexts and why.

4. **Choice and agency**: The individual decides when register-shifting is worth the cognitive cost. Some situations genuinely benefit from register adaptation; others don't.

5. **Sustainable strategies**: Any register-shifting strategy must be sustainable and not lead to burnout. If maintaining a casual register for 8 hours is exhausting, that's not a viable expectation.

### 9.2 Teaching Strategies from the Research

**Explicit visual teaching methods:**
- Visual supports mapping contexts to expected registers (workplace = formal; friend's house = casual; etc.)
- Social narratives describing register expectations in specific situations
- Role-play activities with explicit register-switching practice in scaffolded settings

**Pragmatic language intervention approaches:**
- Experiential learning with reflection: practicing register shifts in functional activities, then discussing what happened
- Group-based intervention with discussion of register norms
- Real-world practice with debriefing (e.g., ordering food, making small talk, presenting at a meeting)

**Context cues for register selection:**
- Physical environment (office vs. park vs. classroom)
- Relationship to communication partner (boss vs. friend vs. stranger)
- Purpose of communication (making a request vs. socializing vs. giving a presentation)
- Communication channel (email vs. text vs. face-to-face vs. video call)
- Existing register of the conversation (if others are being casual, casual is expected)

### 9.3 When Register Consistency Is Appropriate and Valued

Teaching should include explicit discussion of when the natural formal/precise register is an asset:
- Academic and research contexts
- Technical documentation and code review
- Professional writing (reports, proposals, briefs)
- Medical, legal, and scientific communication
- Cross-cultural communication (formal register translates better)
- Written communication generally
- Presentations and public speaking
- Situations requiring clarity and precision

### 9.4 Managing the Cognitive Load of Register-Shifting

Practical strategies for sustainable register management:
- **Identify high-stakes vs. low-stakes situations**: Reserve conscious register-shifting for situations where it genuinely matters (job interviews, important meetings)
- **Develop register "scripts"**: Pre-prepared phrases and patterns for specific registers reduce real-time cognitive load
- **Find register-compatible environments**: Seek workplaces and social contexts where the natural register is valued
- **Schedule recovery time**: After sustained register performance (a full day of casual workplace interaction), plan for downtime
- **Use written communication**: Text-based communication allows more time to calibrate register
- **Disclose when appropriate**: Letting trusted people know about register differences can reduce the pressure to perform

## 10. Cross-References to Other Categories

### 10.1 Connection to Hierarchy (Category 22)
Register is partly determined by power dynamics. Autistic individuals who don't intuitively perceive social hierarchies may not make the register shifts that hierarchy-aware speakers make automatically. Understanding *why* people speak differently to bosses vs. peers requires understanding social hierarchy -- a concept that many autistic people need to learn explicitly rather than absorbing implicitly.

### 10.2 Connection to Hedging (Category 6)
Hedge level is a register variable. Academic register deploys heavy hedging; casual register uses lighter hedging; direct communication uses minimal hedging. Autistic speakers may show asymmetric hedging: comfortable with formal academic hedging but not with casual/social hedging. The absence of casual hedging can make direct statements sound harsh even when no criticism is intended.

### 10.3 Connection to Politeness (Category 3)
Politeness strategy choice is register-dependent. Formal register aligns with negative politeness (deference, indirectness). Casual register aligns with positive politeness (solidarity, in-group language). Autistic individuals may find negative politeness strategies more accessible (they're more rule-governed and explicit) while finding positive politeness strategies harder to deploy naturally.

### 10.4 Connection to Discourse Markers (Category 5)
Discourse marker frequency shifts with register. Casual register uses many DMs (especially phatic ones like "you know," "like," "right?"). Formal register uses fewer, more structured DMs. The type and frequency of DMs an autistic speaker uses may signal a register that doesn't match the social context, contributing to the perception of formality or awkwardness.

### 10.5 Connection to Turn-Taking and Conversation Structure
Register affects conversation structure: casual register allows more interruption, overlapping speech, and topic jumping. Formal register expects more orderly turn-taking. Autistic individuals may prefer the more structured turn-taking of formal register, contributing to a perception of formality in casual settings.

### 10.6 Connection to Indirect Speech and Implicature
Indirect speech is a register feature: casual register may use more indirectness for social lubrication ("It's kind of warm in here" = "Please open the window"), while formal register may be more explicit. However, this pattern reverses in some contexts (formal requests are often highly indirect: "I was wondering if you might possibly..."). The relationship between register and indirectness is complex, and autistic individuals may find the inconsistency challenging.

## 12. Key Takeaways for the Knowledge Base

1. **Register difficulty is real but nuanced**: Autistic individuals often have the *knowledge* to use different registers but struggle with *spontaneous, real-time* register selection based on social context cues.

2. **The formal default has explanations**: Pedantic/formal speech likely reflects language acquired from books/media, preference for precision, reduced sensitivity to register-context mapping, and comfort with rule-governed systems.

3. **Register consistency is not a deficit**: Using a consistent register across contexts reflects authenticity, precision, and reliability. It only becomes a "problem" when social norms demand register variation.

4. **Sustained register performance causes harm**: Teaching constant register performance leads to burnout, identity distress, and mental health consequences. Any pedagogical approach must prioritize sustainability.

5. **Autistic-autistic communication has its own register norms**: Direct, precise, infodumping-friendly, literal, and explicit communication is the norm in autistic communities -- and it works.

6. **The double empathy problem applies to register**: Register mismatch is bidirectional. Autistic people find NT casual register confusing; NT people find autistic formal register alienating. Both sides contribute to the mismatch.

7. **The goal is informed choice**: Understanding register mechanics empowers autistic individuals to make conscious decisions about when register-shifting serves their goals -- without requiring it as a default mode of operation.
