# Supplement 4: Will It Last, and Will More Milligrams Help?

## How to read this document

Every load-bearing claim carries an inline provenance tag. There are four:

- **`[primary, verified]`** — I retrieved the paper (full text or PubMed abstract) and the claim is stated in it.
- **`[primary abstract only]`** — I retrieved the abstract but not the full text. The claim is in the abstract; anything deeper is not checked.
- **`[secondary — unverified]`** — I could not reach a primary. Treat as a rumour with a citation attached.
- **`[reasoning, not evidence]`** — my inference. No study says this.

The tag sits **at the claim**, not in a footnote. This is deliberate. The standing adjudication (validation supplement 1, §"The single most important finding") diagnosed this chain's signature failure as *"the caveat is filed in an appendix while the headline spends the unearned certainty."* The only structural defence is to make it impossible to read a headline without its grade.

**A second discipline, stated up front:** the reader asked for mechanism. There is a strong temptation to supply one. Five prior passes established that **nobody has studied duloxetine in autistic adults, and nobody has studied the overwhelm→crash cycle pharmacologically.** That remains true after this pass. Section 4 does not fix it. What section 4 does is report what is known that *bears on* the question, and it reports something the reader will not expect: **four independent lines of evidence say that raising serotonin acutely makes dissociation, habituation, and sensory gating worse, not better.** That is not the answer anyone was looking for. It is the answer the evidence gives.

## 1. Tachyphylaxis — will the effect fade?

### 1.1 The ~25% figure is real. Its provenance makes it nearly useless for this reader.

The widely-quoted "about a quarter of people lose response" traces to one study, and it is worth naming precisely because almost everyone who cites it does not.

> **Solomon DA, Leon AC, Mueller TI, Coryell W, Teres JJ, Posternak MA, Judd LL, Endicott J, Keller MB.** "Tachyphylaxis in unipolar major depressive disorder." *J Clin Psychiatry.* 2005 Mar;66(3):283-90. **PMID 15766292.**

**What it found** `[primary abstract only]`, verbatim from the abstract:

> *"For the 103 subjects, there were 171 maintenance treatment intervals in which a subject received maintenance pharmacotherapy after having recovered from an episode of major depressive disorder. The median duration of maintenance treatment was 20 weeks. **Tachyphylaxis occurred during 43 (25%) of these 171 maintenance treatment intervals.** The subtype of melancholic (endogenous) major depressive disorder significantly elevated the risk of tachyphylaxis during the subsequent maintenance interval."*

**So the 25% is real, and correctly attributed.** Now the provenance, which is where it falls apart. All of the following is verbatim or directly stated in the same abstract `[primary abstract only]`:

| Feature | What Solomon 2005 actually is |
|---|---|
| Design | **Observational.** *"As an observational study, treatment was recorded but not controlled by anyone connected with the study."* |
| Enrolment era | **1978–1981**, followed up to 20 years |
| Control group | **None.** No placebo, no randomisation |
| Drugs | **Not specified in the abstract.** A cohort enrolled in 1978–81 was treated overwhelmingly with **TCAs and MAOIs** `[reasoning, not evidence]` |
| Adherence | Not verified |
| Median maintenance duration | **20 weeks** |
| Definition of "tachyphylaxis" | Operationally, **recurrence of depression while on maintenance pharmacotherapy** |

> **The decisive problem, and it is fatal to the figure's usual use:** Solomon's operational definition of tachyphylaxis is *"recurrence during maintenance."* In an uncontrolled observational cohort, **that definition cannot distinguish tachyphylaxis from the natural course of a recurring illness, from non-adherence, or from a drug that never worked in the first place.** Solomon 2005 does not measure how often antidepressants stop working. It measures how often depression comes back in people who are taking one. Those are different quantities and the study design cannot separate them. `[reasoning, not evidence — but the reasoning follows directly from the design as the abstract describes it]`
>
> **Also note the drug class.** The reader is asking about an SNRI in 2026. The 25% comes from a cohort recruited when Jimmy Carter was president, treated with drugs that are now third-line. Whatever it measures, it is not measuring duloxetine.

### 1.2 The 9–57% range — the other figure everyone quotes

> **Byrne SE, Rothschild AJ.** "Loss of antidepressant efficacy during maintenance therapy: possible mechanisms and treatments." *J Clin Psychiatry.* 1998;59(6):279-88. **PMID 9671339.** DOI 10.4088/jcp.v59n0602.

`[primary abstract only]` Verbatim: *"The return of depressive symptoms during maintenance antidepressant treatment has occurred in **9% to 57% of patients** in published trials."*

**Author-order note:** PubMed gives **Byrne SE** as first author. A widely-mirrored publisher page (psychiatrist.com) renders it *"Rothschild AJ, Byrne SE."* **PubMed is authoritative; cite Byrne first.** Flagged because this chain has repeatedly been burned by trusting a secondary's rendering of a citation.

Byrne & Rothschild also supply the canonical mechanism list `[primary abstract only]`, verbatim — **seven** candidate explanations:

> *"loss of placebo effect, pharmacologic tolerance, increase in disease severity, change in disease pathogenesis, the accumulation of a detrimental metabolite, unrecognized rapid cycling, and prophylactic inefficacy."*

> **Read that list carefully. It is the answer to the reader's question "is it real, or is it a mix of other things?"** The people who defined the phenomenon in 1998 listed **pharmacologic tolerance as one of seven candidates** and did not claim to know which one operates. Their own closing sentence `[primary abstract only]`: *"double-blind controlled studies are needed to ascertain the optimal strategy for this perplexing clinical problem."* **Twenty-eight years later, section 2 of this document shows those studies were done and the answer was unflattering.**

A 2019 review reaches the same place `[primary abstract only]`:

> **Kinrys G, Gold AK, Pisano VD, Freeman MP, Papakostas GI, Mischoulon D, Nierenberg AA, Fava M.** "Tachyphylaxis in major depressive disorder: A review of the current state of research." *J Affect Disord.* 2019 Feb 15;245:488-497. **PMID 30439676.**
> Verbatim: *"Rates of tachyphylaxis varied from **9% to 57%** depending on the patient population and duration of follow-up."* And: *"studies of antidepressant tachyphylaxis are largely heterogeneous."* And: *"few established treatment strategies exist to manage antidepressant tachyphylaxis."*

> **A 48-point range is not an estimate. It is a confession.** When the spread runs from 9% to 57% depending on who you count and for how long, the honest reading is that **the field does not have a rate**, because it does not have an agreed definition of the thing it is counting. Anyone quoting "about 25%" to the reader is quoting the midpoint of a disagreement as though it were a measurement.

### 1.3 The strongest deflationary argument — and it is genuinely strong

> **Zimmerman M, Thongy T.** "How often do SSRIs and other new-generation antidepressants lose their effect during continuation treatment? Evidence suggesting the rate of true tachyphylaxis during continuation treatment is low." *J Clin Psychiatry.* 2007 Aug;68(8):1271-6. **PMID 17854252.**

`[primary abstract only]` The logic: in a placebo-controlled acute trial, some people who respond to the drug are **true drug responders** and some are **placebo responders who happened to be in the drug arm**. Applying **two formulas proposed by Quitkin and colleagues**, Zimmerman & Thongy estimated how much continuation-phase relapse is attributable to placebo-response decay rather than genuine loss of drug effect.

**The numbers** `[primary abstract only]`, verbatim: *"The relapse rate in placebo responders was **24.1%**, whereas the relapse rate in antidepressant responders was **7.4%**."*

Their conclusion `[primary abstract only]`: *"the majority of relapses in patients taking antidepressants during continuation treatment could be attributed to relapses occurring in patients who were not true drug responders."*

**Provenance limits, stated at the claim:** **four studies** analysed (a fifth excluded for not reporting acute-phase response rates). This is a **re-analysis of published aggregate data using an assumption-laden formula**, not new data. The Quitkin formulas assume a specific mixture model of drug vs placebo responders that is itself contested `[reasoning, not evidence]`. **Four studies is a thin base for a field-defining deflation.**

> **But the argument's structure is sound and it directly answers the reader's framing.** If most "poop-out" is placebo-response decay in people who were never drug responders, then **the phenomenon is real as an experience and largely illusory as a pharmacology.** And note the arithmetic: **24.1% is remarkably close to Solomon's 25%.** `[reasoning, not evidence]` That may be a coincidence. It may also be the whole story — that the famous 25% *is* the placebo-decay rate wearing a pharmacological costume. **I cannot establish that, and I am flagging it as a pattern worth noticing, not a finding.**

### 1.4 Is it drug-class specific? One study. Three events.

This is the only class-comparison evidence I could find, and the reader should see exactly how thin it is.

> **Posternak MA, Zimmerman M.** "Dual reuptake inhibitors incur lower rates of tachyphylaxis than selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors: a retrospective study." *J Clin Psychiatry.* 2005 Jun;66(6):705-7. **PMID 15960562.**

`[primary abstract only]` Verbatim: *"Rates of tachyphylaxis were significantly lower (chi(2) = 6.77, df = 1, p = .01) with the dual reuptake inhibitors venlafaxine and TCAs (**3 [3.7%] of 82**) compared to rates of tachyphylaxis with SSRIs (**46 [14.1%] of 326**)."*

Design `[primary abstract only]`: **237 patients** at the Rhode Island Hospital outpatient practice, interviewed with the **semistructured Treatment Response to Antidepressant Questionnaire** about **prior** antidepressant history: 326 prior SSRI trials, 47 venlafaxine trials, 35 TCA trials. Authors' own limitation, verbatim: *"by virtue of the retrospective and non-random design of the study, these results warrant confirmation."*

> **Why this is much weaker than the p=.01 suggests, and the authors do not say all of it:**
>
> 1. **The entire dual-reuptake finding rests on THREE events.** 3 of 82. Move one patient and the rate is 2.4% or 4.9%. `[reasoning, not evidence]`
> 2. **It is patient recall**, not records. People are being asked to remember whether a drug they took years ago stopped working. `[primary abstract only — the design is stated; the inference about recall reliability is mine]`
> 3. **Channelling and time-at-risk, which the abstract does not address and which I think is the decisive problem.** In real prescribing, SSRIs come **first** and venlafaxine/TCAs come **later**, after SSRI failure. Later drugs have been taken for **less time** and are more **recent**. Tachyphylaxis is a function of time on drug. **A group with less time at risk will show fewer events regardless of pharmacology.** This confound runs in exactly the direction of the finding, and I found no evidence the authors adjusted for it. `[reasoning, not evidence]`
>
> **Verdict: the only evidence that SNRIs poop out less than SSRIs is a retrospective recall study resting on three events with an unaddressed time-at-risk confound. It is not nothing. It is close to nothing.** If it happens to be true, it favours the reader's drug. That is not a reason to believe it.

The second class-comparison study **disagrees with Posternak on the SSRI rate by a factor of three**, which is itself informative:

> **Katz G.** "Tachyphylaxis/tolerance to antidepressants in treatment of dysthymia: results of a retrospective naturalistic chart review study." *Psychiatry Clin Neurosci.* 2011 Aug;65(5):499-504. **PMID 21851459.**
> `[primary abstract only]` **n=52** successfully-treated dysthymia patients. Tolerance in **12 patients (23%)** and **13 trials (22.4%)**. **SSRI group: 41.9%. Non-SSRI group: 0 cases. P < 0.001.**

> **Posternak says SSRI tachyphylaxis is 14.1%. Katz says 41.9%.** Both are retrospective chart/recall reviews. Both are small. They differ by 3×. **This is what a literature looks like when it has no measurement instrument.**
>
> **⚠ A trap caught in passing, flagged because this chain keeps falling into it:** a secondary source I encountered attributed the **3.7% / 14.1%** figures to **Katz 2011**. They are **Posternak & Zimmerman 2005**. Katz's numbers are 0% / 41.9%. **Two different studies, conflated by a secondary, with numbers that would have looked perfectly plausible in a bibliography.** I only caught it by fetching both. **A resolving citation is not verification.**

### 1.5 Duloxetine-specific tachyphylaxis data: I could not find any

**This is a negative finding from my own searching, not a citation** `[reasoning, not evidence]`. I ran targeted searches for duloxetine tachyphylaxis, duloxetine loss of efficacy, and SNRI poop-out. **I found no study measuring tachyphylaxis rates for duloxetine specifically**, in any indication.

**One trap avoided and worth naming.** A search result from `droracle.ai` — an **AI-generated medical answer site** — asserted that *"there is no documented evidence of tachyphylaxis with duloxetine"* and cited an open-label compassionate-use study with a median 328-day duration and 8.4% discontinuation for lack of efficacy. **I did not use it and the reader should not either.** "No documented evidence of tachyphylaxis" in an **open-label uncontrolled study that was not measuring tachyphylaxis** is not evidence of absence; it is absence of measurement. **An AI-written page asserting a convenient negative is precisely the kind of source that has poisoned this chain before.**

The honest statement: **nobody has measured whether duloxetine loses effect over time. The question has not been asked.**

### 1.6 Time course

`[primary abstract only]` The only usable anchors:

- **Solomon 2005**: median maintenance interval **20 weeks**; tachyphylaxis events occurred within those intervals.
- **Byrne & Rothschild 1998 / Kinrys 2019**: rates vary by *"duration of follow-up"* — i.e. the longer you watch, the more you count, which is what you would expect if you were mostly counting natural illness course. `[reasoning, not evidence]`
- **Fava's fluoxetine cohort**: *"26 of 77 depressed patients (33.7%) who achieved complete remission with fluoxetine 20mg daily experienced symptom recurrence between 14 and 54 weeks despite continued maintenance treatment."* **`[secondary — unverified]`. I could not reach the primary.** The 14–54 week window is the most commonly cited time course for "poop-out" and **I am reporting it as a rumour with a citation attached, because that is what it is in my hands.** Do not let this number into a headline.

> **Honest summary of time course: months, not weeks, and not years — probably somewhere in the 3–12 month band. That band is assembled from an unverified secondary, an observational median, and a tautology.** It is the weakest section in this document and it should be presented as such.

### 1.7 Proposed mechanisms — which have evidence, which are hand-waving

The reader asked specifically for this. The answer is bracing.

| Proposed mechanism | Evidence | Grade |
|---|---|---|
| **Loss of placebo effect** | **Zimmerman & Thongy 2007** — 24.1% vs 7.4%, formula-based reanalysis of 4 studies `[primary abstract only]` | **The best-evidenced mechanism on this list — and it is not a pharmacological mechanism at all** |
| **Natural illness course / increase in disease severity** | Byrne & Rothschild list it; Solomon's design cannot exclude it `[primary abstract only]` | **Unexcludable by design in every observational study of tachyphylaxis** |
| **Unrecognised bipolarity / rapid cycling** | Listed by Byrne & Rothschild 1998 `[primary abstract only]` | **Named, never quantified** |
| **Non-adherence** | Named in the literature as a candidate `[secondary — unverified]` | **Never measured in the tachyphylaxis studies** — Solomon 2005 did not verify adherence |
| **Regression to the mean** | See §5.3 | **Structural, unavoidable, rarely modelled** |
| **5-HT1A / 5-HT2A receptor desensitisation** | Animal only. And **the 5-HT1A version is separately in serious trouble** — see §4.5 | **Hand-waving for humans** |
| **Serotonin transporter upregulation** | Animal: 14-day protriptyline/sertraline reduced SERT expression in rat cortex/hippocampus `[secondary — unverified]` — and note this is **down**regulation, i.e. the opposite direction from the tachyphylaxis story `[reasoning, not evidence]` | **Not established; possibly cited backwards** |
| **Dopaminergic depletion** | I found no primary human evidence | **Hand-waving** |
| **BDNF / neuroplastic decline** | Animal, and the causal chain to loss of clinical response is unmeasured `[secondary — unverified]` | **Hand-waving** |
| **Fava's "oppositional tolerance"** | See below | **A model, not a finding** |

**On Fava's oppositional model of tolerance**, which is the most-cited mechanistic account:

> **Fava GA.** "May antidepressant drugs worsen the conditions they are supposed to treat? The clinical foundations of the oppositional model of tolerance." *Ther Adv Psychopharmacol.* 2020;10. DOI 10.1177/2045125320970325.
>
> **⚠ I could not retrieve this paper. SAGE returned HTTP 403 on every route I tried.** Everything I can say about its contents is `[secondary — unverified]`. The model, as described by secondaries: continued drug treatment recruits **compensatory processes that oppose the drug's acute effect**; when those processes outpace the drug, efficacy is lost; when the drug is withdrawn, the unopposed compensation produces withdrawal, post-withdrawal syndromes, and resistance to re-treatment.

> **The honest assessment of oppositional tolerance, and I want to be even-handed because it is fashionable to be dismissive:** it is **theoretically coherent, mechanistically specific, and makes real predictions** — including that withdrawal and tachyphylaxis should be **two faces of the same process**, which is elegant and non-obvious. It is also **a framework rather than a measurement**. No study I found measures an oppositional process in a human and correlates it with loss of response. `[reasoning, not evidence]`
>
> **What makes it more than hand-waving:** it predicts the withdrawal literature, which is independently real. **What keeps it from being established:** the same clinical observations it explains are equally well explained by placebo decay and natural course, and nobody has designed a study that separates them.

> ### The finding of §1.7, stated plainly
>
> **Of the ten proposed mechanisms for antidepressant tachyphylaxis, the one with the best human evidence is "it was never a drug response in the first place."** Every genuinely pharmacological mechanism on the list — receptor desensitisation, transporter change, dopaminergic depletion, neuroplastic decline — is **animal data or theory**. `[reasoning, not evidence — but the table above is the audit]`
>
> **The reader wanted mechanism. The honest answer to "what is the mechanism of poop-out" is that there may not be a pharmacological phenomenon there to have a mechanism.** That is genuinely unresolved, and "unresolved" is the finding.

## 2. Does increasing the dose restore or maintain a fading response?

### 2.1 The duloxetine-specific answer exists, and it is a clean null

The assignment anticipated a Lilly study on 60mg non-responders escalated to 120mg. **It exists. I found it, and the citation is a trap.**

> **Kornstein SG, Dunner DL, Meyers AL, Whitmyer VG, Mallinckrodt CH, Wohlreich MM, Detke MJ, Hollandbeck MS, Greist JH.** "A randomized, double-blind study of increasing or maintaining duloxetine dose in patients without remission of major depressive disorder after initial duloxetine therapy." *J Clin Psychiatry.* 2008 Sep;69(9):1383-92. **PMID 19193339.** ClinicalTrials.gov **NCT00191061**.

**⚠ Citation trap, flagged because it nearly caught me.** The publisher's own page (psychiatrist.com) renders the author list as **"Dunner DL, Kornstein SG, Greist JH, et al."** — **first author Dunner**. **PubMed gives first author Kornstein.** I fetched PubMed directly to resolve it. **Cite Kornstein.** This is the second author-order discrepancy in this document (see §1.2) and both were introduced by publisher pages, not by PubMed.

**Design** `[primary, verified — PubMed abstract + publisher record]`: MDD patients received **duloxetine 60 mg/day for 6 weeks**. Those **not in remission** (HAM-D-17 > 7) were **randomly reassigned, double-blind**, to either **continue 60 mg** or **escalate to 120 mg** for **8 weeks**. Remitters continued on 60mg.

**The result** `[primary, verified]`:

| Arm | Remission rate |
|---|---|
| **Non-remitters continued on 60 mg** | **30.0%** |
| **Non-remitters escalated to 120 mg** | **30.5%** |

Authors' conclusion `[primary, verified]`, verbatim:

> *"Nonremitters to 60 mg of duloxetine for 6 weeks randomly reassigned to 60 mg or 120 mg of duloxetine demonstrated continued symptom improvement in the 8-week extension. **Patients randomly reassigned to 120 mg showed no advantage over those who continued on 60 mg.**"*

Adverse events `[secondary — unverified]`: the 120mg arm showed greater incidence of **hyperhidrosis and chest pain**. (Reported on the publisher page; not confirmed against PubMed.)

> ### **This is the single most decision-relevant study in this document, and it deserves to be read exactly.**
>
> **30.0% vs 30.5%.** Doubling the dose in people who had demonstrably not responded to 60mg bought **half a percentage point**. Not a non-significant trend in the right direction. **Nothing.**
>
> **And read the first half of the authors' sentence, which is the part that matters most and which almost everyone drops:** *"demonstrated continued symptom improvement in the 8-week extension."* **Both arms kept improving. The people who stayed at 60mg got better at the same rate as the people who doubled.** The improvement was real and it was **time**, not milligrams. `[reasoning, not evidence — but it is the direct reading of the authors' own sentence]`
>
> **Caveats stated at the claim, not below it:** this is **MDD, not anxiety and not pain**; it is **60→120mg, not 20→30mg or 20→60mg**; it is **non-remission, not loss of response after remission** — which is the reader's actual question and is **not** what this trial tested; and it is **Lilly-sponsored**, which in this instance cuts *against* the sponsor's commercial interest and therefore makes the null **more** credible, not less. `[reasoning, not evidence]`

### 2.2 The meta-analysis, which includes that duloxetine trial

> **Dold M, Bartova L, Rupprecht R, Kasper S.** "Dose Escalation of Antidepressants in Unipolar Depression: A Meta-Analysis of Double-Blind, Randomized Controlled Trials." *Psychother Psychosom.* 2017;86(5):283-291. **PMID 28903107.**

`[primary abstract only]` **7 double-blind RCTs, 8 study arms, 1,208 participants** (999 in the pooled analysis). Drugs: **fluoxetine** (2 studies, 448 patients), **sertraline** (2, 272), **paroxetine** (2, 146), **duloxetine** (1, **255**), **maprotiline** (1, 87).

**Primary result** `[primary abstract only]`:

> **Hedges g = −0.04 (95% CI −0.20 to 0.12), p = 0.63.** *"Dose escalation was not more efficacious in HAM-D total score reduction than maintaining standard-dose treatment"* — neither pooled nor for any individual drug.

**Secondary** `[primary abstract only]`: no differences in **response rates**, **all-cause discontinuation**, or **discontinuation due to inefficacy**. But: *"**Significantly more patients in the dose escalation group dropped out due to adverse effects** than in the standard-dose continuation group."*

Authors' conclusion `[primary abstract only]`, verbatim:

> *"dose escalation after initial non-response to standard-dose pharmacotherapy **cannot be regarded as general evidence-based treatment option** in unipolar depression."*

> **Note the confidence interval: −0.20 to 0.12.** This is not an underpowered null with a wide interval hiding a real effect. **It excludes anything larger than a small effect in either direction.** `[reasoning, not evidence]` And note the asymmetry the meta-analysis found: **escalation delivered no extra efficacy and significantly more side-effect dropouts.** The trade is **costs without benefits**, not a fair bet.

Corroborating, and more equivocal:

> **Ruhé HG, Huyser J, Swinkels JA, Schene AH.** "Dose escalation for insufficient response to standard-dose selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors in major depressive disorder." *Br J Psychiatry.* 2006;189(4):309-316. **PMID 17041180.**
> `[primary abstract only]` Eight dose-escalation studies, three meta-analyses. *"dose escalation before 4 weeks of treatment at a standard dose appeared to be ineffective."* After 8 weeks, *"some, but limited, evidence for efficacy... particularly in partial responders."* Authors' verdict, verbatim: *"dose escalation of SSRIs is **equivocally supported by evidence**"* — they could *"neither unequivocally state that dose escalation is useful nor discard it as useless."* And: *"**dose escalation unequivocally increased side-effects.**"*

> **Ruhé is the honest steelman for escalation and I am including it because it cuts against the tidy conclusion.** The signal it preserves is narrow but real: *partial responders, after 8 weeks*, might benefit. **The reader is a partial responder** (benefit on anxiety and sleep, minimal on pain). **That is the one crack in the null, and it should not be papered over.** But note Ruhé is a **2006 narrative systematic review**, superseded on the same question by **Dold 2017's quantitative meta-analysis**, which had the RCTs Ruhé lacked and found nothing. `[reasoning, not evidence]`

### 2.3 The background dose-response question — where the assignment's framing needs correcting

The assignment asked me to report that antidepressant dose-response for efficacy is *"largely flat above minimum effective doses (Furukawa 2019; also Jakubovski 2016)."*

**That framing is not right, and I am correcting it rather than confirming it.** Furukawa and Jakubovski **disagree with each other.** Presenting them as a united front would be exactly the "confident synthesis outrunning evidence" this chain has been told to stop doing.

> **Furukawa TA, Cipriani A, Cowen PJ, Leucht S, Egger M, Salanti G.** "Optimal dose of selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors, venlafaxine, and mirtazapine in major depression: a systematic review and dose-response meta-analysis." *Lancet Psychiatry.* 2019 Jul;6(7):601-609. **PMID 31178367.** DOI 10.1016/S2215-0366(19)30217-2.

`[primary, verified — PMC full text]` **77 studies, 19,364 participants** (6,881 placebo, 12,483 active). **99 SSRI treatment groups.**

**SSRI dose-efficacy curve**, verbatim: *"gradual increase up to doses between **20 mg and 40 mg fluoxetine equivalents**, and a **flat to decreasing trend** through the higher licensed doses up to 80 mg."*

| Dose (fluoxetine equivalents) | Risk ratio for response |
|---|---|
| 20 mg | **1.24** |
| 40 mg | **1.27** |
| 80 mg | **1.09** |

**Venlafaxine** `[primary, verified]`: *"initially increasing dose-efficacy relationship up to around **75–150 mg**, followed by a more modest increase."*
**Mirtazapine** `[primary, verified]`: *"efficacy increased up to a dose of about **30 mg** and then decreased."*
**Conclusion** `[primary, verified]`: *"The lower range of the licensed dose achieves the optimal balance between efficacy, tolerability, and acceptability in the acute treatment of major depression."*

> **Jakubovski E, Varigonda AL, Freemantle N, Taylor MJ, Bloch MH.** "Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis: Dose-Response Relationship of Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors in Major Depressive Disorder." *Am J Psychiatry.* 2016;173(2):174-183. **PMID 26552940.**

`[primary, verified — PMC full text]` **40 studies, 10,039 participants.** And it found **the opposite of flat**:

> *"a significant interaction between dose and time [interaction=0.0007 (95%CI: 0.0001–0.0013; p=0.0196)]"* — and **"higher doses of SSRIs were associated with increased efficacy (NNT for treatment response ≈14–16)."* Benefits *"plateau around 250mg of imipramine equivalents (50mg of fluoxetine)."*
> Also: *"higher doses of SSRIs were associated with an increased likelihood of dropouts due to side-effects."*

> ### **These two meta-analyses contradict each other and the reader is entitled to know that.**
>
> **Furukawa: flat-to-decreasing above 20–40mg fluoxetine equivalents. Jakubovski: significantly increasing, plateau at 50mg fluoxetine equivalents.** Same drug class, same outcome, opposite headline.
>
> **The most-cited methodological explanation** `[secondary — unverified]`: Jakubovski's "low dose" category *"included subtherapeutic doses (citalopram 10 mg or paroxetine 10 mg) as well as therapeutic doses (citalopram 20 mg and sertraline 50 mg)"* — so the low-dose bin was contaminated with genuinely subtherapeutic doses, manufacturing an apparent upward slope. **I could not verify this against a primary** (the Lancet correspondence returned 403). It is the standard explanation and it is plausible; it is **not** established in my hands.
>
> **And there is a third position, making it a three-way disagreement** `[secondary — unverified]`: a mega-analysis of fixed-dose trials (Hieronymus et al., *Transl Psychiatry* 2016) reportedly found dose-dependency for three SSRIs. I did not retrieve it.
>
> **Honest verdict: the dose-response question for SSRIs is genuinely contested, three ways, and I will not pick a winner.** What survives all three positions is narrower and worth stating because it is what the reader actually needs: **every one of them agrees that side-effect burden rises monotonically with dose, and none of them finds a large efficacy gain from going higher.** The disagreement is about whether the efficacy gain is *zero* or *small*. Nobody is arguing it is *large*. `[reasoning, not evidence — but it is the intersection of the three findings]`

### 2.4 **The correction that matters most in this section: neither meta-analysis includes duloxetine**

**I checked this directly because the assignment's framing depended on it** `[primary, verified — PMC full text of Furukawa 2019]`:

> Furukawa 2019's inclusion criteria, verbatim: *"five SSRIs (citalopram, escitalopram, fluoxetine, paroxetine, and sertraline), **venlafaxine, and mirtazapine**"* — selected as *"the most frequently prescribed new-generation antidepressants in the UK according to Open Prescribing."*
>
> **Duloxetine is not in that list. It was excluded.**

And **Jakubovski 2016 is SSRIs only** `[primary, verified]` — duloxetine is an SNRI and is not included.

> ### **So: the two headline dose-response meta-analyses that the "efficacy is flat above minimum doses" claim rests on BOTH EXCLUDE THE READER'S DRUG.**
>
> Applying them to duloxetine is an **extrapolation across drug class**, and this chain has been burned repeatedly by exactly this move — cross-study, cross-tracer, cross-region comparisons presented as measurements (BINDING-CORRECTIONS §4 items 4, 7; validation supplement 1 §L2). **I am not going to commit it here on the reader's behalf.**
>
> **The nearest in-class datapoint is venlafaxine, the other SNRI, and it is the one drug in Furukawa that did NOT go flat**: *"initially increasing dose-efficacy relationship up to around 75–150 mg, followed by a **more modest increase**"* `[primary, verified]`. **"A more modest increase" is not "flat."** Venlafaxine's curve keeps rising. `[reasoning, not evidence]` If one were reasoning by class membership — which is weak reasoning, and I am flagging it as such — **the SNRI in the dataset is the one that looks least flat.**
>
> **The honest statement for the reader: the "antidepressant efficacy is flat above minimum doses" background finding is real for SSRIs, contested even there, and has never been established for duloxetine.** What *has* been established for duloxetine is narrower and more direct: **Kornstein 2008, 60→120mg in non-remitters, 30.0% vs 30.5%.** That single trial is worth more to this reader than both meta-analyses, because it is **actually about their drug.**

### 2.5 Does escalation restore a *faded* response? One study, and it is not what its title promises

Everything in §2.1–2.4 is about escalation after **initial non-response**. The reader's question is different: **if it works and then stops, does more help?** That question has, as far as I can find, **exactly one study**.

> **Franchini L, Rossini D, Bongiorno F, Spagnolo C, Smeraldi E, Zanardi R.** "Will a second prophylactic treatment with a higher dosage of the same antidepressant either prevent or delay new depressive episodes?" *Psychiatry Res.* 2000 Sep 25;96(1):81-5. **PMID 10980329.**

`[primary abstract only]` **57 highly recurrent unipolar patients who had recurred on prior SSRI prophylaxis.** Re-treated acutely at **full SSRI dosages**; **51 (89.5%) responded**; 4-month continuation with **no relapses**; then **24-month maintenance at those same full doses** — fluvoxamine 300 mg/day, sertraline 150 mg/day, or paroxetine 40 mg/day.

**Results** `[primary abstract only]`, verbatim:
> *"**28 out of the 51 outpatients (54.9%) showed a further recurrence**"* during the second maintenance period.
> *"Second recurrences... were **less severe** than first recurrences, decreasing from 25.1±3.4 to 21.6±3.3 (**P<0.0001**)."*

> ### **This study answers the reader's question more directly than anything else I found, and it answers it in two directions at once.**
>
> **The encouraging half:** **89.5% responded again** when re-treated at a higher dose. Loss of response was **not permanent** and the drug was **not "used up."** `[primary abstract only]`
>
> **The discouraging half:** **54.9% recurred again anyway within 24 months, on the higher dose.** Escalation restored the response and then **failed to hold it in more than half of patients.** `[primary abstract only]`
>
> **The genuinely interesting half, which nobody quotes:** the second recurrences were **significantly less severe** (25.1 → 21.6, P<0.0001). `[primary abstract only]` **That is not the pattern tachyphylaxis predicts.** Pharmacological tolerance predicts *escalating* failure. **Attenuating severity looks more like a drug still doing something partial against an illness that is running its own course.** `[reasoning, not evidence]`
>
> **Caveats at the claim:** **n=51. No control group. Open-label. Uncontrolled.** The 89.5% acute re-response has **no comparator**, so regression to the mean and natural remission are entirely unexcluded — and in a cohort selected for *having just recurred*, regression to the mean is **maximal**. `[reasoning, not evidence]` **SSRIs, not duloxetine.** Population is **highly recurrent unipolar depression**, not the reader's phenotype. And the title's *"higher dosage"* means **higher than their previous prophylactic dose**; the abstract describes it as maintaining the acute dose rather than dropping down, and **I could not fully resolve what the comparator dose had been.** `[primary abstract only — and the ambiguity is real; I am not resolving it]`

### 2.6 Prophylactic escalation — before any fade

The assignment predicted "almost certainly no evidence." **Confirmed, and I want to be precise about what kind of nothing this is.**

**I found no study of prophylactic dose escalation in a responding patient to prevent future loss of response** `[reasoning, not evidence — this is a negative search finding]`.

This is not "the evidence is weak." **It is that the question has never been asked in a trial.** The nearest thing is Franchini 2000 (§2.5), and that is escalation **after** recurrence, in a cohort with **no control group**, where **54.9% recurred anyway**.

> **The strongest available statement, and it is a syllogism, not a study** `[reasoning, not evidence]`:
>
> 1. Escalation does not help people who **have not responded** (Kornstein 2008, duloxetine-specific, 30.0% vs 30.5% `[primary, verified]`; Dold 2017, g=−0.04 `[primary abstract only]`).
> 2. Escalation, applied **after** loss of response, restored response in 89.5% but failed to hold it in 54.9% at 24 months, uncontrolled (Franchini 2000 `[primary abstract only]`).
> 3. Side-effect burden rises with dose in **every** dataset — Dold, Ruhé, Jakubovski, and Furukawa all agree, and it is the **only** thing all four agree on `[primary, verified across all four]`.
> 4. Therefore, escalating **prophylactically**, in a person who is currently **responding**, incurs the one effect that is reliably dose-dependent — side effects — **to purchase a benefit that has never been demonstrated in anyone, at any dose, for any indication.**
>
> **That is reasoning, not evidence, and the reader should weigh it as such.** But it is the reasoning the evidence supports, and there is no countervailing evidence to weigh against it — not because the evidence is mixed, but because it is **absent**.
>
> **The specific relevance to this reader is sharp.** They are currently **responding** on the endpoint they care about. The dose-increase conversation is about **pain**, where 20mg's evidence is genuinely absent (validation supplement 1, §1.6: 20mg and 30mg are **0 for 3** on pain primaries across ~350 patients, twice with adequate power; Cochrane's own conclusion: *"lower daily doses are not [efficacious]"*). **Those are two different questions and they should not be merged.** Escalating for **pain** has a real, if modest, evidence base (NNTB 8–11). Escalating to **protect the anxiety/episode response** has **none**, and this section is the reason why.

### 3.2 The withdrawal confound — and the reframe that keeps the data usable

Every trial in §3.1 is a **randomised discontinuation** (enrichment) design. Kishi 2023 states this as an inclusion criterion `[primary, verified]`. The critique is serious and well-documented:

> **Récalt AM, Cohen D.** "Withdrawal Confounding in Randomized Controlled Trials of Antipsychotic, Antidepressant, and Stimulant Drugs, 2000-2017." *Psychother Psychosom.* 2019;88(2):105-113. **PMID 30893683.** DOI 10.1159/000496734.
>
> `[primary abstract only]` Of **30 eligible trials**, only **14 (46.6%)** with **15 relapse risk analyses** provided enough data to evaluate. Tested whether **≥50% of the relapse risk difference** appeared at the discontinuation timepoint.
>
> - **Short discontinuation assumption: 9 of 13 interpretable analyses (69.2%)** suggested withdrawal confounding.
> - **Long discontinuation assumption: 7 of 9 interpretable analyses (77.8%)** suggested withdrawal confounding.
> - Proportion of the endpoint drug–placebo difference already present **by the discontinuation timepoint**: **69.1% (range 58.7–148.0%)** short, **79.0% (range 51.5–183.3%)** long.
> - **Only one study controlled for withdrawal effects.**
>
> Authors' conclusion `[primary abstract only]`: *"withdrawal symptoms confound the results of relapse prevention RCTs"* and *"accounting for such symptoms in RCTs is an ethical, scientific, and clinical imperative."*

> **The mechanism of the confound, stated plainly** `[reasoning, not evidence — but it follows directly from the design]`: in an enrichment design, the placebo arm is not drug-naive. **They are people who were on duloxetine and had it taken away.** Duloxetine has a short half-life (**8–17h per FDA**, per BINDING-CORRECTIONS §4 item 11) and a **raw withdrawal-symptom rate around 31%** (BINDING-CORRECTIONS §4 item 14). **Anxiety, agitation, dizziness and dysphoria are both withdrawal symptoms and relapse criteria.** The trial cannot tell them apart. So some fraction of "relapse" in the placebo arm is **withdrawal misclassified as relapse**, which **inflates the apparent benefit of staying on the drug.**
>
> **Récalt & Cohen's timing signature is the strongest part of their argument:** ~69–79% of the eventual drug–placebo gap is **already present at the discontinuation timepoint** — i.e. the gap opens **immediately**, which is what withdrawal predicts and **not** what relapse of a slowly-recurring illness predicts. `[primary abstract only]`
>
> **Even-handedness, because this critique is also fashionable and deserves the same scrutiny:** only **14 of 30** trials had usable data, and the "interpretable analyses" denominators shrink to **13** and **9**. `[primary abstract only]` Some of the reported proportions **exceed 100% (148.0%, 183.3%)**, which means the drug–placebo gap at the discontinuation point was *larger* than at endpoint — a pattern that is hard to interpret and that the summary statistic absorbs without comment. `[reasoning, not evidence]` **This is a real and important critique built on a small and partly uninterpretable sample.**

> ### **The reframe — and I think this is the most useful analytic move in this document**
>
> **The withdrawal confound attacks the CONTRAST. It does not touch the DRUG ARM.**
>
> The confounded quantity is *"placebo 41.8% vs duloxetine 13.7%"* — because the 41.8% is inflated by withdrawal. **But the 13.7% is not confounded by withdrawal, because those people did not withdraw from anything.** `[reasoning, not evidence]`
>
> **So the drug-arm relapse rate is directly readable, and it is the number that answers the reader's actual question:**
>
> | Trial | Indication | Duration | **Relapse/recurrence in people who STAYED ON duloxetine** |
> |---|---|---|---|
> | **Davidson 2008** (PMID 18559291) | **GAD** | **26 weeks** | **13.7%** `[primary abstract only]` |
> | **Perahia 2009** (PMID 19552867) | MDD recurrence | **up to 52 weeks** | **14.4%** `[primary abstract only]` |
>
> **Roughly 14% of people who kept taking duloxetine had their condition come back within 6–12 months. Roughly 86% did not.**
>
> **What that 14% is, precisely** `[reasoning, not evidence]`: it is an **upper bound on tachyphylaxis**, not a measure of it. It bundles together (a) genuine loss of drug effect, (b) natural illness course breaking through a partially effective drug, and (c) non-adherence. **Tachyphylaxis is at most 14% and probably well under it.** It cannot be higher, because that is everyone who relapsed for any reason.
>
> **This is the single most honest and most useful number in this document for the reader's question "will the effect fade?", and it survives the withdrawal critique intact** — because withdrawal is irrelevant to people who didn't withdraw. **Caveats at the claim:** these are **GAD and MDD, at 60–120mg, in non-autistic populations, over 6–12 months, industry-sponsored.** The reader is on **20mg**, is **autistic**, and has a **phenotype nobody has studied**. The 14% is the best available anchor and it is an anchor from a different harbour. `[reasoning, not evidence]`

### 3.3 How long does benefit persist?

**The honest answer: the evidence runs out at 52 weeks.**

- **26 weeks** — GAD, placebo-controlled, drug arm relapse 13.7% `[primary abstract only]`
- **52 weeks** — MDD recurrence, placebo-controlled, drug arm recurrence 14.4% `[primary abstract only]`
- **52 weeks** — fibromyalgia, **no placebo arm**, uninformative about durability `[secondary — unverified]`
- **Beyond 52 weeks** — **nothing.** No placebo-controlled duloxetine data exists past one year, in any indication, that I could find. `[reasoning, not evidence — negative search finding]`

> **So when the reader asks "will it last?", the literal evidentiary answer is: it lasts at least a year in about 86% of people with GAD or MDD taking 60–120mg, and past a year nobody has looked.** `[reasoning, not evidence — synthesis of the above]`

## 4. Mechanism — why would a serotonergic drug stop this cascade?

**The standing finding is unchanged and this pass does not soften it: nobody has studied duloxetine in autistic adults, and nobody has studied the overwhelm→crash cycle pharmacologically.** Everything below is *adjacent* evidence plus explicitly-labelled reasoning. **No mechanism is being manufactured.**

**What I did not expect to find, and what changes the shape of this section: the adjacent evidence mostly points the wrong way.**

### 4.1 Four independent lines say acute serotonin elevation makes the reader's problem WORSE

#### (a) Serotonergic challenge *causes* depersonalisation — the m-CPP study, verified

> **Simeon D, Hollander E, Stein DJ, DeCaria C, Cohen LJ, Saoud JB, Islam N, Hwang M.** "Induction of depersonalization by the serotonin agonist meta-chlorophenylpiperazine." *Psychiatry Res.* 1995 Sep 29;58(2):161-4. **PMID 8570768.**

`[primary abstract only]` **VERIFIED — the PMID resolves to the paper claimed** (the earlier pass flagged it; it is real).

**What it actually shows:** **67 subjects** — normal volunteers plus patients with OCD, social phobia, and borderline personality disorder — received **double-blind, placebo-controlled challenge with the partial serotonin agonist m-CPP**. *"Challenge with m-CPP induced depersonalization significantly more than did placebo."* **18% experienced depersonalization or derealization.** Responders *"did not differ in age, sex, or diagnosis"* from non-responders.

**The confound the authors themselves report, and it is decisive** `[primary abstract only]`: there was *"a significant correlation between the induction of depersonalization and increase in **panic**, but not nervousness, anxiety, sadness, depression, or drowsiness."*

> **How to read this, precisely.** `[reasoning, not evidence]`
>
> **It does cut against a naive "more serotonin = less dissociation" story.** A serotonergic agonist produced depersonalisation in 18% of people, blind and placebo-controlled. **That tension is real and must be presented, not buried.**
>
> **But three things blunt it, and honesty requires all three:**
> 1. **m-CPP is a 5-HT2C-preferring agonist. Duloxetine is a reuptake inhibitor.** These are not the same pharmacology. An agonist slams a receptor; a reuptake inhibitor raises ambient transmitter and then the system adapts. `[reasoning, not evidence]`
> 2. **The depersonalisation tracked panic, not serotonin per se.** m-CPP is a reliable panicogen. If it induced panic and panic induced depersonalisation, the serotonergic step is **upstream of a non-specific route**, and the finding says less about serotonin-and-dissociation than it appears to. The authors report exactly this correlation.
> 3. **It is acute and single-dose.** It says nothing about a chronic adaptation.
>
> **Net: this is a real tension, correctly flagged by the earlier pass, and it is weaker than it looks — but it is not dismissible, and the direction it points is against the intuitive story.**

#### (b) SSRIs *worsen* habituation and sensory gating in humans — two studies, same group, dose-graded

This is the finding most directly relevant to a reader whose episodes are triggered by **sensory overwhelm**, and it is the one I least expected.

> **Jensen KS, Oranje B, Wienberg M, Glenthøj BY.** "The effects of increased central serotonergic activity on prepulse inhibition and habituation of the human startle response." *Neuropsychopharmacology.* 2007 Oct;32(10):2117-24. **PMID 17299503.**
> `[primary abstract only]` **Escitalopram 10 mg**, **n=18 healthy males**, **double-blind placebo-controlled crossover**. *"escitalopram was found to significantly **delay habituation** of the ASR."* No effect on PPI. Conclusion: *"increased serotonergic activity **disrupts habituation**."*

> **Oranje B, Wienberg M, Glenthoj BY.** "A single high dose of escitalopram disrupts sensory gating and habituation, but not sensorimotor gating in healthy volunteers." *Psychiatry Res.* 2011 Apr 30;186(2-3):431-6. **PMID 20971512.**
> `[primary abstract only]` **Escitalopram 15 mg**, **n=20 healthy males**, **double-blind placebo-controlled crossover**. *"Escitalopram significantly **decreased P50 suppression and habituation**, but had no effect on PPI."* Conclusion, verbatim: *"**habituation and sensory gating are disrupted by increased serotonergic activity**, while sensorimotor gating seems relatively insensitive to such a rise."*

> ### **Read this against the reader's history and it is striking.**
>
> **Raising serotonin acutely, in healthy people, blind and placebo-controlled, at two doses, made sensory gating worse and habituation slower.** `[primary abstract only, ×2]` **Habituation is the exact function whose failure produces sensory overwhelm.** `[reasoning, not evidence]`
>
> **And the drug in both studies is escitalopram — the drug that made this reader dissociate and non-functional.** `[reasoning, not evidence]`
>
> **I want to be careful here, because this is exactly the kind of elegant convergence that this chain has repeatedly mistaken for a finding.** These are **n=18 and n=20 healthy males**, **single acute doses**, **startle-paradigm laboratory measures**, and **the leap from "delayed P50 suppression in a lab" to "a theme park causes a two-week collapse" is enormous and unbridged.** The authors' own framing is a caution about SSRI dosing in patients with compromised sensory filtering — which is a hypothesis, not a demonstration. **I am not claiming this explains the reader's escitalopram reaction. I am claiming it is the only human evidence that bears on the question and it points against the intuitive story.**

#### (c) SSRIs acutely *enhance* fear learning, via the BNST — and the receptor is 5-HT2C

> **Pelrine E, Pasik SD, Bayat L, Goldschmiddt D, Bauer EP.** "5-HT2C receptors in the BNST are necessary for the enhancement of fear learning by selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors." *Neurobiol Learn Mem.* 2016 Oct 20;136:189-195. **PMID 27773594.**
>
> `[primary, verified — PMC full text]` **Rats.** Systemic **citalopram before fear conditioning enhanced fear learning** and upregulated Arc in the **oval nucleus of the BNST**. **74.3% of activated neurons expressed 5-HT2C receptors.** Local **5-HT2C antagonist infusion into the oval BNST prevented the fear-memory-enhancing effect of citalopram.**
>
> The authors' own clinical framing `[primary, verified]`: this illuminates why SSRIs *"paradoxically increase anxiety in humans when they are given acutely."*

**Animal, not human. Fear conditioning, not sensory overwhelm. Citalopram, not duloxetine.** `[reasoning, not evidence — on the limits]` But note the receptor: **5-HT2C**, the same receptor family m-CPP preferentially agonises. **Two independent lines — a human challenge study and a rat circuit study — converge on 5-HT2C as the acute anxiogenic/dissociogenic actor.** `[reasoning, not evidence]`

#### (d) Serotonin's role in stress is *not* "more is better" — it is closer to the opposite

> **Maier SF, Watkins LR.** "Stressor controllability and learned helplessness: the roles of the dorsal raphe nucleus, serotonin, and corticotropin-releasing factor." *Neurosci Biobehav Rev.* 2005;29(4-5):829-41. **PMID 15893820.**
>
> `[primary abstract only]` Verbatim: *"(a) **uncontrollable stressors sensitize serotonergic neurons in the dorsal raphe**, and that a corticotropin-releasing factor-related ligand, acting at the Type II receptor, is essential to this sensitization process, and (b) **the consequent exaggerated release of serotonin in response to subsequent input is at least in part responsible for the behavioral changes that occur**."*

> ### **This is the finding that most disturbs the intuitive story, and the assignment specifically asked about serotonin and stress resilience.**
>
> **In the single most influential animal model of stress-induced helplessness, the thing that CAUSES the helplessness is EXCESSIVE serotonin release.** `[primary abstract only]` Uncontrollable stress sensitises the dorsal raphe; the sensitised raphe dumps more 5-HT; the extra 5-HT produces the behavioural collapse.
>
> **The reader's question was "does serotonin set a stress-resilience threshold?" The Maier/Watkins answer is that serotonin is closer to the EFFECTOR of stress-induced collapse than to the buffer against it.** `[reasoning, not evidence — but it is the direct reading of the abstract]`
>
> **Rats. Learned helplessness. Not autism, not sensory overwhelm, not duloxetine.** And the model's construct validity for human depression is itself contested. `[reasoning, not evidence]` **But the assignment asked whether there is a real "serotonin sets the stress threshold" story, and the honest answer is: there is a real story, it is well-developed, and it runs the other way.**

### 4.2 The only account that reconciles the reader's experience with all four lines — and it is reasoning

**Every line in §4.1 is about ACUTE serotonergic elevation. The reader has been on duloxetine for two weeks and reports the episodes stopped.**

> ### The chronic-adaptation account `[reasoning, not evidence — this is a hypothesis, and I am labelling it as one because it is one]`
>
> If acute 5-HT elevation is **anxiogenic, dissociogenic, and gating-disrupting** via **5-HT2C** (§4.1a, b, c), and chronic SSRI/SNRI treatment produces **5-HT2C desensitisation** `[secondary — unverified]`, then:
>
> - **The acute phase should look like the reader's escitalopram experience**: dissociation, no anxiolysis, non-functional.
> - **The chronic phase should look like the reader's current experience**: episodes suppressed.
> - **The therapeutic effect is not "more serotonin." It is the adaptation to more serotonin** — a receptor-level change that runs *opposite* to the drug's acute pharmacology.
>
> **This account has three virtues:** it explains the escitalopram failure and the duloxetine success **with one mechanism**; it explains the **two-to-six week onset delay** that every antidepressant shows; and it makes a **falsifiable prediction** (below).
>
> **It has four serious problems, and they must travel with it:**
> 1. **The 5-HT2C desensitisation timeline in humans is not established.** I could not retrieve a primary. `[secondary — unverified]`
> 2. **The reader is at 2 weeks.** If the mechanism is chronic desensitisation, **2 weeks is early** — arguably too early. The account is in tension with the reader's own timeline. `[reasoning, not evidence]` **I am flagging this against the account's own interest.**
> 3. **The whole desensitisation family of explanations is under sustained attack** (§4.5).
> 4. **Nobody has tested any of this in an autistic adult, or in this phenotype, or with duloxetine.**
>
> **Grade: the best available reasoning. Not a mechanism. Not evidence. A hypothesis that fits, offered because the reader asked for the best available reasoning and is entitled to it clearly labelled.**

> ### **The prediction, which is the part that actually bears on the appointment** `[reasoning, not evidence]`
>
> **If the benefit is a chronic adaptation to a given level of serotonergic drive, then raising the dose raises the drive above the adapted set-point — and should transiently re-create the acute state.** That is: **a dose increase should predict a temporary return of dissociation/anxiety, in a person with a documented history of exactly that reaction to serotonergic escalation.**
>
> **This is a mechanism-derived prediction, not a finding, and it could be wrong.** But it is **falsifiable**, it is **specific**, and it converges with the independent clinical evidence in §2 that escalation buys side effects without efficacy. **It is worth raising at the appointment as a question, not as a conclusion.**

### 4.3 The BNST duration story — real, and it does not scale to this reader

The assignment asked whether the BNST-sustained/amygdala-phasic distinction gives a real "duration" story for 1–2 week episodes.

> **Walker DL, Toufexis DJ, Davis M.** "Role of the bed nucleus of the stria terminalis versus the amygdala in fear, stress, and anxiety." *Eur J Pharmacol.* 2003;463(1-3):199-216. **PMID 12600711.**
>
> `[primary abstract only]` Verbatim: *"although the bed nucleus of the stria terminalis may not be necessary for **rapid-onset, short-duration** behaviors which occur in response to specific threats, the bed nucleus of the stria terminalis may mediate **slower-onset, longer-lasting** responses that frequently accompany sustained threats, and that **may persist even after threat termination**."*

**The distinction is real and well-supported** `[primary abstract only]`. **This is a review of animal work; the abstract specifies no human studies.**

> ### **But the scale is wrong by three to four orders of magnitude, and this is the finding.** `[reasoning, not evidence]`
>
> **"Longer-lasting" in the BNST literature means minutes to hours in a startle paradigm. The reader's episodes last one to two weeks.**
>
> The phrase *"may persist even after threat termination"* is seductive — it sounds exactly like "a theme park on Saturday produces a collapse that lasts until the following Sunday." **It is not describing that.** It is describing a rat whose startle response stays elevated for tens of minutes after a bright light goes off.
>
> **This is precisely the error the chain made with non-fearful panic disorder** (BINDING-CORRECTIONS §4 item 6: *"Panic peaks in minutes; their episodes last 1–2 weeks"*). **An attractive duration story was mapped onto the reader on the strength of the word "sustained," when the actual timescales differ by 1,000×.** I am flagging it now rather than after someone builds a section on it.
>
> **Verdict: the amygdala/BNST phasic-sustained distinction is a real neuroscience finding and a FALSE analogy for this reader's episode duration. Do not use it to explain 1–2 week episodes.**

### 4.4 Serotonin and habituation / sensory gating — real evidence, wrong direction

**Yes, there is real human evidence, and it is covered in §4.1(b).** Summary: **escitalopram 10mg delays startle habituation** (n=18) `[primary abstract only]`; **escitalopram 15mg decreases both P50 sensory gating and habituation** (n=20) `[primary abstract only]`; **neither affects PPI**.

> **The direction is the finding.** Anyone hoping that "duloxetine raises serotonin, serotonin improves sensory gating, better gating means less overwhelm, less overwhelm means no episodes" would find a clean four-step mechanism. **The only human evidence on the middle step says the opposite.** `[reasoning, not evidence]`

### 4.5 The "why antidepressants take weeks" story — is it still believed?

**Substantially challenged, and the reader should know the textbook account is in trouble.**

The classical account: SSRIs raise synaptic 5-HT → **5-HT1A autoreceptors** initially suppress firing → over weeks the autoreceptors **desensitise** → firing normalises with reuptake still blocked → net serotonergic transmission rises → therapeutic effect. Onset delay = desensitisation time.

**The challenges** `[secondary — unverified — ⚠ I attempted the primary (*ACS Chem Neurosci*, "Delayed Antidepressant Efficacy and the Desensitization Hypothesis") and got HTTP 403; everything in this list is from secondary summaries and must be treated as such]`:

1. Clinical molecular imaging and postmortem studies **failed to find consistent 5-HT1A alterations** in MDD.
2. **5-HT1A antagonists failed to achieve consistent antidepressant efficacy** in clinical trials — which the theory predicts they should, by short-cutting the desensitisation step.
3. Evidence that **feedback inhibition persists** via remaining receptors: firing rate normalises **despite** ongoing inhibition, not because inhibition was removed.
4. Direct contradictory animal data exists (e.g. *"Lack of 5-HT1A autoreceptor desensitization following chronic citalopram treatment, as determined by in vivo microdialysis"*).
5. Desensitisation of 5-HT1A autoreceptors alone appears **not sufficient** for the behavioural response to fluoxetine.

> **Honest status: the 5-HT1A desensitisation account is the textbook story, is still widely taught, and is not well supported. Glutamatergic synaptic plasticity is the leading alternative** `[secondary — unverified]`.
>
> **⚠ A caution that cuts against §4.2 and I am stating it plainly:** §4.2's chronic-adaptation account is a **desensitisation** account — it just uses **5-HT2C** rather than 5-HT1A. **The critiques above are specific to 5-HT1A and do not automatically transfer.** But they should induce real humility about the whole family of "receptors adapt over weeks and that is the therapeutic mechanism" explanations, **including mine.** `[reasoning, not evidence]` **The most-taught version of this story has been failing its tests for two decades. That is a reason to hold the 5-HT2C version loosely.**

### 4.6 Allostatic load — no pharmacology bears on it

`[secondary — unverified]` Allostatic load is operationalised as a **multi-system biomarker index** (cortisol, epinephrine, norepinephrine, CRP, IL-6, blood pressure, HDL, HbA1c, total cholesterol, waist/hip ratio, BMI). Interventions reported to move the index: **CBT, tai chi, whole-foods diet, physical activity, yoga**; **antipsychotics in schizophrenia**; **infliximab** in depression with elevated inflammatory markers.

> **What I did not find: any evidence that duloxetine, any SNRI, or any antidepressant reduces allostatic load.** `[reasoning, not evidence — negative search finding]`
>
> **The honest statement: allostatic load is a compelling frame for the reader's cumulative-overwhelm phenomenology and there is no pharmacology attached to it.** Note also that the index is **almost entirely peripheral and metabolic** — it is a cardiometabolic construct that has been borrowed for psychological narratives. `[reasoning, not evidence]` **Invoking allostatic load to explain a drug effect would be borrowing a word, not a mechanism.**

The assignment floated this as an interesting parallel: SNRIs/TCAs prevent triggered episodic events at doses below their psychiatric doses. **Is it useful or false?**

> **Banzi R, Cusi C, Randazzo C, Sterzi R, Tedesco D, Moja L.** "Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) and serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs) for the prevention of migraine in adults." *Cochrane Database Syst Rev.* 2015 Apr 1;2015(4):CD002919. **PMID 25829028.**
>
> `[primary, verified — PMC full text]` **11 studies, 585 participants.** Only two placebo-controlled studies assessed migraine frequency; data *"inconclusive"* and could not be pooled.

> **Authors' conclusion, verbatim: *"The use of SSRIs and SNRIs for migraine prophylaxis is not supported by evidence."***
>
> **On duloxetine specifically** `[primary, verified]`: **no duloxetine trial met inclusion criteria.** Duloxetine appears only in the ongoing-studies list: *"we found four ongoing studies that were potentially eligible: two on duloxetine and two on milnacipran."*

> ### **The analogy is FALSE, and it fails in three separate places.** `[reasoning, not evidence — but each leg is `[primary, verified]`]`
>
> 1. **The premise is wrong for the drug class in question.** The "antidepressants prevent triggered episodic events" story is true for **amitriptyline**, a TCA. **Cochrane's formal conclusion is that it is not supported for SSRIs and SNRIs.** The analogy borrows amitriptyline's evidence and attaches it to duloxetine's class.
> 2. **It is wrong for duloxetine specifically.** Not a single duloxetine migraine trial met Cochrane's inclusion criteria. There is **no duloxetine migraine prophylaxis evidence base** to draw the analogy from.
> 3. **The "sub-psychiatric dose" clause is a TCA property, not a class property.** Amitriptyline prevents migraine at 10–25mg vs antidepressant doses of 75–150mg. **Nothing establishes an analogous sub-therapeutic-dose prophylactic effect for duloxetine**, and the reader is at 20mg — so this clause is doing the work of making the reader's low dose sound mechanistically principled. **It is not.**

> **This one deserves special flagging.** The analogy is **attractive, mechanistically suggestive, and would have supported a satisfying story** — a low-dose SNRI raising the threshold for triggered episodic events, exactly matching the reader's phenomenology. **It is exactly the shape of inference that BINDING-CORRECTIONS §8 calls "elegance-driven inference": the satisfying synthesis overriding contrary evidence that was within reach.** The contrary evidence is a **Cochrane review with a one-sentence conclusion**, and it was one search away. **Discard the analogy.**

## 5. What would distinguish durable effect from placebo/regression in THIS reader?

### 5.1 The math — and the reader's current evidence is much weaker than it feels

**Model** `[reasoning, not evidence — standard Poisson process for recurrent events; the arithmetic below is mine and can be checked]`: treat episodes as a Poisson process with baseline rate **λ** per month. Under the null (drug does nothing), the probability of observing **zero** episodes over **t** months is **P(0) = e^(−λt)**.

**Baseline rate:** the reader reports "several times a month." I model **λ = 2, 3, and 4** per month.

> ⚠ **An inconsistency in the input that I am flagging rather than smoothing over.** "Several times a month" combined with "episodes last 1–2 weeks" **does not arithmetically fit** — 3 episodes/month × 1.5 weeks each = 4.5 weeks of episode per 4.3-week month, i.e. continuous illness. Either the episodes usually run **much shorter** than the theme-park example, or "several times a month" is an overestimate, or they **overlap**. **The theme-park→2-week episode may be an unusually severe instance rather than the modal one.** `[reasoning, not evidence]` **This ambiguity propagates directly into every number below, and the reader is the only person who can resolve it.** It is worth resolving *before* the appointment, because the whole n-of-1 rests on it.

**P(zero episodes | drug does nothing):**

| Elapsed | λ=2/mo | λ=3/mo | λ=4/mo | Reading |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **2 weeks** ← **reader is here** | **36.8%** | **22.3%** | **13.5%** | **Unremarkable. Would happen by chance often.** |
| 1 month | 13.5% | 5.0% | 1.8% | Suggestive at best |
| **6 weeks** | **5.0%** | **1.1%** | **0.25%** | **Crosses p<0.05 even at the lowest baseline** |
| **2 months** | **1.8%** | **0.25%** | **0.03%** | **Chance is implausible at every rate** |
| **3 months** | **0.25%** | **0.012%** | **0.0006%** | **Chance is dead** |
| 6 months | 0.0006% | ~0.0000015% | — | Overkill |

> ### **The two conclusions from the math**
>
> **1. The reader's current evidence is weak, and weaker than it feels.** At two weeks, **13–37% of people whose drug does nothing at all would see exactly what the reader is seeing.** `[reasoning, not evidence]` This is not a reason for pessimism. It is a reason not to treat two weeks as informative. **It also independently confirms BINDING-CORRECTIONS §5: "~2 weeks against a baseline of several episodes a month is thin."** The math puts a number on the thinness: **roughly a one-in-three to one-in-seven chance of a pure fluke.**
>
> **2. Six weeks to three months is when the "random fluctuation" hypothesis dies.** At **6 weeks**, chance is under 5% even at the most conservative baseline. At **3 months**, it is under 1 in 400. **The reader does not need six months. They need about eight to twelve weeks.** `[reasoning, not evidence]`
>
> ### **And the crucial qualifier, which is where most n-of-1 reasoning goes wrong:**
>
> **The math kills "random fluctuation." It does NOT touch placebo, regression to the mean, or natural course.** `[reasoning, not evidence]` Those are not chance — they are **systematic** effects that produce **exactly the same** zero-episode observation. **A three-month clean run proves the improvement is real. It says nothing about whether the drug caused it.** These are different questions and the passage of time only answers the first.

### 5.2 **The better test — because the reader's episodes are TRIGGERED**

`[reasoning, not evidence — this is a study-design argument, not a finding]`

**This is the single most useful methodological point available to this reader, and it follows from something they already know about themselves.**

**Their episodes are not spontaneous. They follow sensory overwhelm.** Therefore:

> **The denominator is not time. It is trigger exposures.**
>
> **Two months with no episodes while working from home, avoiding crowds, and having a quiet winter is worth almost nothing.** The drug was never tested. `[reasoning, not evidence]`
>
> **One theme-park day with no episode afterwards is worth more than two uneventful months.**

**The trigger-conditional math.** Let **p₀** = probability of an episode given a significant sensory overload at baseline. If the reader survives **k** comparable exposures with no episode, then under the null:

**P(no episode in k exposures) = (1 − p₀)^k**

| Baseline P(episode\|overload) | Exposures needed for p<0.05 |
|---|---|
| **p₀ = 0.9** (near-deterministic) | **2** |
| **p₀ = 0.8** | **3** |
| **p₀ = 0.7** | **3–4** |
| **p₀ = 0.5** | **5** |

> **Three to five comparable sensory overloads survived cleanly is roughly a p<0.05 result — and it is a result about the thing that actually matters.** `[reasoning, not evidence]`
>
> **This test is faster, better-powered, and more specific than waiting.** It also has a property the time-based test lacks: **it is much harder for regression to the mean to fake.** Regression to the mean explains why a bad stretch is followed by a better stretch. **It does not explain why a specific, repeated, previously-reliable trigger stopped producing its effect.** `[reasoning, not evidence]`
>
> **Its limitations, at the claim:** it requires the reader to **characterise p₀ retrospectively**, which is recall-biased; it requires exposures to be genuinely **comparable**, which is a judgement call; and **deliberately seeking out overload to test a drug is a real cost with a real risk**, and I am not recommending it. **The point is that when overloads happen naturally, those are the informative data points — and they should be recorded as such, prospectively, starting now.**

### 5.3 Falsifiable markers — what each hypothesis predicts

`[reasoning, not evidence throughout this table]`

| Observation over coming months | **Genuine drug effect predicts** | **Placebo / regression / natural course predicts** |
|---|---|---|
| **Trigger-conditional response** | Episodes stay absent **even after real sensory overloads** | Episodes absent while triggers absent; **return when a real overload happens** |
| **Time course of the benefit** | Stable or consolidating over months | **Decay.** Placebo response decays; regression is a one-time reversion, not a plateau |
| **Dose–response** | 20→30mg produces some further change (in either direction) | Dose changes produce **nothing systematic** |
| **The escitalopram contrast** | Explained by drug-specific pharmacology | **Unexplained** — placebo should have worked equally well for both drugs |
| **Other duloxetine-sensitive endpoints** | Sleep, vivid dreams, RLS worsening, "floaty" feeling **track together** with the episode benefit | Episode benefit is **dissociated** from the pharmacological signs |
| **Discontinuation / missed doses** | Episodes return, on a **pharmacokinetic** timescale (days, given 8–17h half-life) | Nothing, or a slow drift |
| **Direction of any breakthrough** | Breakthroughs are **less severe** than baseline (cf. Franchini 2000's attenuation) | Breakthroughs are **as severe** as baseline |

> ### **The three sharpest markers, ranked**
>
> **1. The trigger test (§5.2).** The only marker that separates drug from regression to the mean. **The highest-value observation available.**
>
> **2. The RLS and vivid dreams — and this is underrated.** `[reasoning, not evidence]` The reader reports **worse RLS**, **vivid dreams**, and a **"floaty" feeling**. **These are pharmacological signatures. They are not plausible placebo responses** — nobody's expectation produces restless legs. **They are proof the drug is doing something neurologically real at 20mg.** That does **not** prove the drug is causing the episode benefit — two real effects of one drug need not be the same effect. `[reasoning, not evidence]` **But it removes "the drug is inert at this dose" from the table**, and it supplies a **covariate**: if the dreams and RLS fade while the episode benefit persists, that dissociation is informative. If they fade **together**, that is a coherent drug story.
>
> **3. The escitalopram contrast.** `[reasoning, not evidence]` The reader has **already run a within-subject control**, and it came out negative. **A pure placebo/expectancy account has to explain why the first serotonergic drug produced dissociation and non-function while the second produced resolution.** Expectancy does not usually reverse sign between two drugs in the same class. **This is the reader's single strongest piece of personal evidence and it long predates duloxetine.** It is **not** conclusive — the drugs differ pharmacologically, the reader's circumstances differ, and time has passed — but it is a genuine asymmetry that the deflationary account has to absorb.

### 5.4 The honest timeline

`[reasoning, not evidence]`

| When | What is knowable |
|---|---|
| **Now (2 weeks)** | **Nothing.** 13–37% chance of a fluke. The appointment will arrive **before** the data does. |
| **~4 weeks (the appointment)** | Still weak (2–14% fluke). **Meaningful only if a real trigger has been survived.** |
| **6–8 weeks** | **"Random fluctuation" becomes implausible.** Placebo/regression still fully live. |

| **6–12 months** | Matches where the **trial evidence** sits (13.7% relapse at 26wk; 14.4% at 52wk). **Past 12 months, nobody has data on anyone.** |

> **The appointment in ~2 weeks lands at roughly 4 weeks of treatment. At 4 weeks, the reader will have between 2% and 14% odds of a pure fluke, and no trigger-conditional data unless chance has supplied a trigger.** `[reasoning, not evidence]`
>
> **This is not an argument for any particular dose decision.** It is an argument that **the strongest thing the reader can bring to that appointment is an accurate account of what they cannot yet know** — and a clear statement that the pain question (where 60mg has real evidence and 20mg has none) and the episode question (where 20mg appears to be working and escalation has no supporting evidence at all) are **two different decisions that happen to share a dial.** `[reasoning, not evidence]` **How to weigh them is theirs and their doctor's.**
