# Concept Research: IFS Mechanics and Process

## Overview

The Internal Family Systems (IFS) clinical method is more procedural than its public marketing suggests. Underneath "befriend your parts" and "no bad parts" is a fairly stable sequence — most of it codified in the 1990s/2000s and now standard across IFS Institute Level 1 trainings, PESI continuing-education modules, and clinician how-to writing (Therapy with Alessio, Way of Belonging, Lucas Forstmeyer, IFS Guide). The core sequence: identify a target part, separate Self from it ("unblending"), interview the part using a six-step protocol called the **6 Fs**, get protector consent, witness the exile that the protector protects, run a formal **unburdening** ritual to release the exile's burden, and then return to the protector to renegotiate its role. A typical full arc — from "I'm anxious about something" all the way to a finished unburdening — usually spans many sessions, not one.

Two distinctions matter for evaluating whether IFS fits a given client. First, the model assumes the client can perceive parts internally (through felt sense, imagery, or inner dialogue) — this is called **in-sight** or **insight access**. When that capacity isn't available, the therapist switches to **direct access** and speaks to the part directly through the client. Direct access is canonical IFS, not a workaround; Schwartz himself taught it. Second, the entire 6-F sequence is **gated by interoception** — at multiple steps the client is asked to notice something inside their body and report on it. Some steps gate harder than others. A clear-eyed map of which steps require what perceptual capacity is essential for clients with alexithymia, low interoception, or aphantasia.

The mechanics are also where IFS's strongest critical pressure lands. The "feel toward" question — "How do you feel toward this part?" — is treated as a diagnostic gauge of Self-energy: if the answer falls within the 8 Cs (curiosity, calm, compassion, clarity, courage, confidence, creativity, connectedness), Self is present and work can proceed. If the answer is "frustrated" or "scared" or "annoyed," another part has blended in, and that part now becomes the focus. This is operationally useful and theoretically slippery: there is no falsifiable definition of Self-energy that doesn't loop through the 8 Cs, and the absence of Self gets reinterpreted as "a part is in the way." The procedure works either way, which is one reason the model is hard to disconfirm.

## Core Components

### Component 1: The 6 Fs Protocol

- **Purpose**: A canonical six-step interview procedure for getting to know a protector part. Usually attributed to Schwartz; first widely codified in *Internal Family Systems Therapy* (Schwartz, 1995) and elaborated across IFS Institute training materials, the Schwartz/Sweezy *Internal Family Systems Therapy*, 2nd ed. (2020), and Earley's *Self-Therapy* workbooks.
- **Inputs**: A target part the client can perceive in some way (felt sense, image, voice, or behavior pattern); enough Self-energy in the client to relate from instead of through.
- **Outputs**: A working relationship between Self and the protector, an articulated protective fear, and (often) identification of the exile being protected — the bridge to the witnessing/unburdening stage.
- **Sub-components**: Find, Focus, Flesh out, Feel toward, beFriend, Fears.
- **Relationships**: The 6 Fs come *after* a target part has been identified and *before* exile work. They are themselves preceded by unblending (or are interleaved with it — "Feel toward" *is* an unblending check). Some clinicians treat the 6 Fs as the protocol and unburdening as a separate downstream procedure; others treat the full sequence (6 Fs → witnessing → unburdening) as one continuous arc.

### Component 2: Unblending

- **Purpose**: To separate Self from a part that has taken over conscious experience. "Blended" means the client *is* the part — speaking from it, feeling as it. "Unblended" means the client can notice the part without being it.
- **Inputs**: Awareness that a part is active.
- **Outputs**: A felt or noticed separation — described variously as more space, a step back, the part being "over there" rather than "where I am." Verifiable by the 8 Cs check.
- **Procedure** (Cuthbert's clinician-facing summary; Therapy with Alessio):
  1. Notice the part (body tension, charged emotion, rigid thought).
  2. Name it ("a part of me is anxious," not "I am anxious").
  3. Ask it internally: "Would you step back / give me some space so I can get to know you?"
  4. Check for Self-energy: do I now feel some of the 8 Cs?
  5. If yes — proceed. If no — there's a second part blended in; ask *that* part to step back, or make it the new target.
- **Relationships**: Unblending is both a one-time move and a continuous process — clients re-blend constantly, and the therapist re-runs the unblending check throughout a session. The "Feel toward" F is the canonical mid-session check.

### Component 3: In-sight Access vs. Direct Access

- **Purpose**: Two ways the therapist can engage parts. **In-sight** = client looks inward, perceives the part, reports back, and a Self-to-part conversation happens internally. **Direct access** = therapist speaks to the part directly, with the client either consenting to "let the part talk" (explicit direct access) or simply through the part's voice (implicit direct access).
- **When in-sight is used**: When the client can perceive parts internally and has enough Self-energy to relate to them.
- **When direct access is used**: When the client is so blended that no Self is available; when the client lacks visualization or interoceptive access; when the part refuses to step back; in crisis or with severe dissociation; with children who don't easily map onto the in-sight frame.
- **Relationships**: Per Schwartz, both are canonical IFS — not workarounds. Direct access is not "lesser" IFS; it's the right choice when in-sight isn't available or productive. For clients with alexithymia, aphantasia, or chronic blending, direct access may be the *primary* mode.

### Component 4: Witnessing

- **Purpose**: After protector permission is obtained, the Self goes to the exile and listens to its story. The Self does not fix, reassure, or rush — it simply receives what the exile shows.
- **Inputs**: Protector consent (essential — bypassing this risks retraumatization and protector backlash); access to the exile (may be sensed in the body, seen as a younger figure, or accessed through direct access); enough Self-energy to stay present without flooding.
- **Outputs**: An "I see what happened to you" experience; the exile feels witnessed; the burden becomes namable.
- **Procedure**: The therapist asks "What does this part want you to know?" or "Show me/Self what happened." The exile shows — through memory, image, body sensation, or words. The client/Self stays present and compassionate. The therapist's job is to keep the Self present and to slow things down if the client starts to flood.
- **Failure modes**: Re-experiencing without Self present (the client *becomes* the exile rather than relating to it — this is retraumatization, not witnessing); rushing past the witness step toward unburdening (the burden won't release if the exile doesn't feel seen first).

### Component 5: Unburdening

- **Purpose**: A formal release ritual in which the exile gives up the burden — the painful belief, feeling, or memory-state taken on during overwhelm.
- **Inputs**: A witnessed exile that is ready (the exile says yes, or signals readiness); identified burden (a belief, feeling, body sensation, or some combination); chosen mode of release.
- **Procedure** (compiled from Way of Belonging, Therapy with Alessio, Schwartz/Sweezy 2020):
  1. **Identify the burden**: ask the exile what it carries. "Shame." "I'm worthless." "Fear." "Heaviness."
  2. **Locate the burden in the body**: where does the exile carry it? Chest, throat, stomach, head, surrounding the figure. (This step is interoception-gated.)
  3. **Choose an element**: light, water, fire, earth, wind/air. Some clinicians add space, gravity, or "any way that feels right." The element is the medium of release.
  4. **Release**: the exile gives the burden to the element — into the wind, washed in water, burned in fire, buried in earth, dissolved in light. The client visualizes/senses this happening.
  5. **Notice what arises in the burden's place**: a quality the exile was unable to express (joy, playfulness, freedom, calm). This is sometimes called the "invitation" step.
- **Do-over (or "redo")**: A corrective experience in imagination. The Self goes back to the original scene and gives the exile what it needed at the time — protection, comfort, the words it never heard. This is sometimes done before unburdening, sometimes interleaved.
- **Retrieval**: The exile is brought from the past scene to a safe present location — the body, an imagined sanctuary, a beach, a forest. Decided by the exile.
- **Integration**: The unburdened exile (now with its restored qualities) is welcomed back into the system; the protector is renegotiated — what new role does it want now that its old job is no longer needed?
- **Relationships**: The unburdening sequence is interleaved more than strictly sequenced. Witnessing → do-over → retrieval → unburdening → integration is a common order, but Way of Belonging notes retrieval can happen at any point or not at all (the exile decides). Memory reconsolidation theory has been invoked (Parts & Self) to explain why the do-over matters: the corrective experience updates the prediction the exile had encoded, not just the affect.

### Component 6: Self-Energy as Diagnostic Gauge

- **Purpose**: The 8 Cs (curiosity, calm, compassion, clarity, courage, confidence, creativity, connectedness) are how the model operationalizes "is Self present?" If the client's response to "How do you feel toward this part?" lands in the C neighborhood, Self is present and the work proceeds. If not, another part is in the way and the work pivots to that part.
- **PESI's "Geiger counter" framing**: The "Feel toward" question is described in PESI clinical training as a "Geiger Counter for Self-energy" — a real-time diagnostic.
- **Theoretical concern**: Self-energy is defined by its effects (the 8 Cs) and its absence is interpreted as a part in the way. This makes "Self" hard to disconfirm. A clinician with this lens cannot encounter a session in which Self is genuinely unavailable — only sessions in which a part is masking it. This is operationally useful and epistemically slippery; both can be true at once.

## How It Works

### Step 1: Opening — Where Are You / What's Live

**Trigger**: Session start.
**Action**: Therapist invites the client to notice what's present. Could be a body scan, a check-in question ("what's coming up for you today?"), or following whatever the client brings. The aim is to surface a target part that wants attention — usually a protector that's been active in the client's recent life.
**Result**: A target part identified, however imprecisely.

### Step 2: Find

**Trigger**: A target part is identified.
**Therapist prompts** (canonical, from Therapy with Alessio):
- "What do you notice if you turn your attention inward?"
- "Is there any specific section of your body where you feel this more?"
- "Is there anything you perceive in or around your body that doesn't fully feel like *you*?"
**Required capacity**: Interoceptive/spatial. The client has to locate the part somewhere — in the body, around the body, ahead, behind, inside the head.
**Result**: A felt or imagined location for the part.

### Step 3: Focus

**Trigger**: The part has been located.
**Therapist prompts**:
- "Can you allow whatever is emerging to be there?"
- "Spend some time recognizing that there is a part of you that wants your attention."
- "Focus on whatever is making itself known to you."
**Required capacity**: Sustained inward attention. The client has to keep attending without flinching away or trying to fix.
**Result**: The part stabilizes enough to be worked with.

### Step 4: Flesh Out

**Trigger**: The part is being attended to.
**Therapist prompts**: "What does it look like? Sound like? How old does it seem? What is it wearing? What's its facial expression?"
**Required capacity**: Visualization / imagination, plus sustained internal attention. **This is the most aphantasia-affected step**. Some clinicians substitute non-visual flesh-out: "What's it like? What does it feel like? What words come?" — for clients without inner imagery.
**Result**: A more textured, named figure (or felt sense) that's stable enough to interview.

### Step 5: Feel Toward

**Trigger**: The part is fleshed out enough to relate to.
**Therapist prompt**: "How do you feel toward this part?" or "What feeling do you have toward it right now?"
**Required capacity**: Interoceptive emotional read. The client has to notice an internal feeling state and label it. **This is the most alexithymia-affected step**, and it's also the gateway question for everything that follows.
**Result diagnostic**:
- If the answer is in the **8 Cs neighborhood** ("curious," "compassionate," "calm interest," "I want to know you," "I feel for it"): Self is present. Proceed.
- If the answer is **not in the 8 Cs** ("frustrated," "scared of it," "annoyed," "want it to go away," "I hate it"): another part is blended. Pivot to that other part — ask it to step back, or make it the new target.
- If the answer is **"nothing" / "I don't know" / "neutral"**: ambiguous — could be Self's calm openness, could be a protective dissociation. Therapist takes time to discern.

### Step 6: beFriend

**Trigger**: Self is present (the Feel-toward gate has opened).
**Therapist prompts**: "Is the part aware of you? Is it willing to connect? Let it know you're here." Often the Self introduces itself; sometimes the part is suspicious, hostile, or hopeful.
**Required capacity**: Relational/dialogic. The capacity to imagine or sense reciprocal exchange. Less interoception-dependent than Find/Focus; more like having a conversation with an imagined figure.
**Result**: A working relationship — or, if the part won't connect, information about why not.

### Step 7: Fears

**Trigger**: A working relationship is in place.
**Therapist prompts**:
- "What does this part fear would happen if it stopped doing its job?"
- "What does it achieve by doing what it does?"
**Required capacity**: Cognitive/dialogic. The part articulates its protective logic. This step is fairly accessible to literal-thinking and low-interoception clients because it's explicit and sentence-shaped.
**Result**: The protective logic is on the table; the exile being protected is usually identifiable from the answer ("It's afraid I'll feel worthless again if I stop"; "It's afraid of the kid in me who got hit").

### Step 8: Protector Permission

**Trigger**: The exile has been identified.
**Therapist prompt**: "Would the protector be willing to let us go meet the exile? What does it need to know first? What is it afraid would happen?"
**Required capacity**: Dialogic; willingness to negotiate.
**Result**: Either consent (proceed to witnessing) or a stop signal (work more with the protector first, sometimes for many sessions).
**Critical point**: Skipping this step is the most-named cause of retraumatization in IFS. "Forced" or "scripted" unburdening — proceeding without consent — is widely flagged as a failure mode.

### Step 9: Witnessing

**Trigger**: Protector consent is obtained and the exile is accessible.
**Therapist prompts**: "What does the exile want you to know?" "Let the exile show you what happened." "Stay with it."
**Required capacity**: Capacity to stay in Self while contact-witnessing pain. Often a moment of the Self literally turning toward the exile and saying "I'm here. Show me."
**Result**: The exile feels seen. Until this happens, unburdening typically won't take.

### Step 10: Do-over (Redo) and Retrieval

**Trigger**: The exile has been witnessed and is no longer alone in the original scene.
**Procedure**: Self goes into the original scene and offers what was missing — protection, words, comfort, intervention. Then, if the exile wishes, Self brings the exile out of the scene to a present-day safe place.
**Required capacity**: Imaginative/visual; capacity for symbolic intervention.
**Result**: A felt updating — the exile is no longer trapped at the time of the wound.

### Step 11: Unburdening

**Trigger**: The exile is ready to release the burden.
**Procedure**: Burden identified → located in the body → released to a chosen element (light, water, fire, earth, wind) → what arises in its place is welcomed.
**Required capacity**: Body awareness, symbolic visualization, capacity for ritual. Several gates of interoception in this step.
**Result**: The exile is unburdened; the system reorganizes; the protector that was protecting the exile is now relieved of its job.

### Step 12: Integration and Closing

**Trigger**: The unburdening has completed (or the session is ending wherever it is).
**Procedure**: Renegotiate with the protector ("now that your old job isn't needed, what would you like to do?"). Welcome the unburdened qualities into present life. Close the session — often with grounding (orienting to room, body), thanks to the parts who showed up, and a gentle invitation to notice over the coming week.
**Result**: Session ends with the system reorganized to whatever degree was reached. Homework (between-session journaling, parts check-ins) is sometimes assigned but is much more developed in self-led IFS than in in-session protocol.

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: A clean 6-F arc with a procrastinator part

- **Scenario**: Client comes in saying "I've been procrastinating on this work project. Hate it." Target part: the procrastinator.
- **Find**: "I notice this kind of leaden weight in my legs and shoulders." (Interoceptive answer.)
- **Focus**: Stays with it for a minute.
- **Flesh out**: "It's like a kid sprawled on the couch refusing to move. Maybe seven."
- **Feel toward**: "Honestly? Frustrated. I want it to *get up*." (Non-C answer — a critical part is blended.)
- **Pivot**: "Ask the frustrated part if it would step back." It does. Recheck. "Now I'm just curious about the kid."
- **beFriend**: Self introduces itself. Kid eyes Self warily, then makes a little eye contact.
- **Fears**: "What does this kid fear if it had to get up?" Answer: "Mom yelling. Failure. Getting hit if the work isn't perfect."
- **Result**: The procrastinator is a manager protecting an exile carrying childhood criticism. Clear path to (eventual) exile work, but not in this session.

### Example 2: Direct access when in-sight isn't available

- **Scenario**: Client is highly blended with a hostile part — speaking from it. "This whole thing is stupid. I shouldn't be here."
- **In-sight attempt fails**: Asking the part to step back gets nowhere; client can't notice the part as separate.
- **Therapist switches to direct access**: "Can I talk to that part directly?" Client agrees. Therapist speaks to the part: "I hear you. You think this is stupid. What are you trying to do for [client name]?" Part: "Keep us safe." Therapist: "Safe from what?" Part: "Getting hopeful and being let down again."
- **Result**: A working alliance is built without requiring the client to access Self — and over time the part may itself unblend, restoring in-sight access.

### Example 3: An unburdening ritual

- **Scenario**: After many sessions of protector work, client has reached an exile — a five-year-old who was left alone for a weekend.
- **Witnessing**: Self goes to the child. Child shows Self the empty house, the dark, the sound of the phone ringing and no one answering. Self says "I see what happened. You were alone."
- **Do-over**: Self stays with the child through the weekend in imagination — sits with her, gets her food, holds her hand at night.
- **Retrieval**: Self asks if the child wants to leave. She nods. They go to a meadow Self has visited in imagination before. Child sits in the grass.
- **Burden identification**: "What did you take from that weekend?" Child: "Nobody is coming. I'm by myself forever." Body: a stone in the chest.
- **Element choice**: Child looks around. "The water." A stream nearby.
- **Release**: Child takes the stone out of her chest, drops it in the stream. Watches it go. The chest is light. What arises: laughter. Not a lot, just a little.
- **Integration**: Child wants to stay in the meadow with Self for a while. They make a plan to check in. The protector who'd been managing the "I'll be alone forever" belief is renegotiated next session.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: "The 6 Fs are a rigid script you go through in order in every session."
  - **Reality**: They're a sequence, not a rigid script. Steps can interleave, repeat, or be revisited. A whole session can be one F if that's where the work is. The 6 Fs apply to *protectors* specifically — not exiles. Exile work uses a different sequence (witnessing → do-over → retrieval → unburdening → integration).

- **Misconception**: "Unburdening is the goal of every session."
  - **Reality**: Most sessions don't unburden. Most of IFS is protector work — building trust, getting consent, hearing fears. Unburdening, when it happens, is a relatively small fraction of total session time and only occurs when many prerequisites are met. Forcing unburdening before the system is ready is a known failure mode.

- **Misconception**: "If you can't feel parts in your body, IFS doesn't work for you."
  - **Reality**: Direct access exists for exactly this case. Some steps (Find, Feel toward) are interoception-gated; some (Fears, beFriend, Do-over) can be done largely cognitively or relationally. Adaptations are common.

- **Misconception**: "If you feel 'frustrated' toward a part, you're failing the protocol."
  - **Reality**: A non-C answer is *information*, not failure. It tells you another part is blended. The protocol's response is to pivot — work with that part. The only "failure" would be to pretend the answer was a C-quality and proceed.

- **Misconception**: "The elements (light/water/fire/earth/wind) are required for unburdening."
  - **Reality**: They're the most common menu, but the canonical instruction is "any way that feels right." Some clients prefer space, gravity, breath, or non-elemental release. The element list is a default, not a rule.

- **Misconception**: "In-sight access is the real IFS; direct access is a workaround."
  - **Reality**: Both are taught as canonical Schwartz-approved modes. Direct access has been part of the model since the early teaching materials.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **The client cannot find the part anywhere in or around the body.** Standard adaptation: ask if the part is in front of, behind, beside, above, or below — i.e., relax "in the body" to "in the field." Or shift to thinking about a recent moment when this part was active and locate it through the *behavior*. Or shift to direct access entirely.
- **The client returns "nothing / neutral / I don't know" to "Feel toward."** Ambiguous — could be Self, could be dissociation. Therapist takes time, asks more questions ("is the nothing curious or empty?"), or assumes blending and asks a possible-blending part to relax.
- **A protector refuses permission to access the exile.** The work stops at the protector and stays there for as long as needed — sometimes weeks or months. This is not failure; this is the procedure.
- **The exile floods Self.** The client *is* the exile rather than relating to it. The therapist works to restore Self — often through grounding, breath, orienting, or by asking parts to step back. Resuming witnessing happens only after Self is back. If flooding is chronic, the model says stabilization is the prior task (overlaps with Cat 8 / 9 of this deep dive).
- **The "do-over" reads as fake to a literal client.** This is a real friction point with autistic and skeptical clients. Some adaptations: frame it as "what does the part need to know now, with you here" rather than "imagine going back"; or explicitly own that this is a re-storying move and the question is whether it lands, not whether it's literally true.
- **The client cannot visualize an element.** Standard adaptations: substitute words, sound, breath, or just "let it go." The element is a vehicle, not a goal.
- **A session ends mid-arc.** Common — and the standard practice is to thank the parts who showed up, mark where the work paused, and pick up next session. Closing rituals matter.

## Related Concepts

- **The Parts Model (Cat 1)**: This is what unblending separates *from*. Without the manager / firefighter / exile vocabulary, the 6 Fs don't make sense.
- **Origins and Ontology (Cat 3)**: The procedure works whether parts are "real" subpersonalities, neural-network metaphors, or phenomenological figures. The procedure's reliability is somewhat decoupled from what one believes about the ontology.
- **Alexithymia (Cat 6)**: This is the structural challenge to the procedure. Alexithymia disrupts Find, Focus, and especially Feel toward.
- **Autism (Cat 7)**: Brings literal-thinking, masking, monotropism, and possible aphantasia. Each interacts with specific steps.
- **Failure Modes (Cat 9)**: The full catalogue of what goes wrong — premature unburdening, no Self access, recovered-memory risks during witnessing.
- **Self-Led / Journaling (Cat 10)**: Written adaptations of the 6 Fs and unburdening; how much of the procedure can be moved to externalized processing.
- **Memory reconsolidation theory** (extra-IFS): Sometimes invoked to theorize *why* the do-over works. The corrective experience is framed as updating an encoded prediction, not just creating a new memory. Independent literature exists (Ecker, Toomey, Hulley, *Unlocking the Emotional Brain*) — engagement with IFS is suggestive, not definitive.

## Research Gaps

- **Direct access procedural detail.** Most public sources describe in-sight in detail but treat direct access as a paragraph aside. The richest description is in Schwartz/Sweezy 2020 (*Internal Family Systems Therapy*, 2nd edition) and in IFS Institute Level 2 training materials, neither of which is fully in the public record.
- **Empirical study of the 6 Fs as a discriminable procedure.** There is no peer-reviewed adherence-rating instrument that I could find for the 6 Fs comparable to, e.g., the Working Alliance Inventory or PE adherence scales. The procedure is taught with consistent language but has not been operationalized for outcome research as a discrete protocol.
- **Mechanism-of-change research on unburdening.** The "do-over → memory reconsolidation" hypothesis is suggestive but not directly tested. Independent reconsolidation research exists (Ecker et al.) but its application to IFS unburdening is theoretical.
- **Public-facing detail on the Sweezy / IFS Institute "Therapeutic IFS" Level 2 unburdening protocols** for couples and complex trauma is scarce; most procedural depth lives behind the IFS Institute paywall.
- **Aphantasia adaptations of Flesh out**: rarely treated explicitly. Worth flagging when this material is folded back into Cat 7.
