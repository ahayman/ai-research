# Decision Aid for the Appointment

> Eight research passes went at this drug. Not one of them asked what the dose increase is for.

Every pass argued about milligrams. None asked what the milligrams were being spent on. That omission matters more than it sounds, because duloxetine's two effects on you sit on two different dose curves, and which curve you are trying to move decides whether an increase makes sense.

You answered the question yourself, and the answer is the reason this page exists:

> *"the pain and fibromyalgia is important to me. It's just that the psychological effects were unexpected and something I also value."*

Both. That is what makes this a real decision rather than a formality. If only the pain mattered, the arithmetic would be straightforward. If only the psychological effects mattered, it would be straightforward in the other direction. Wanting both puts you in the one position where the evidence genuinely pulls two ways, and where the answer is not sitting in a paper somewhere waiting to be found. So this page will not pick a side. It will lay out the trade, the measurement problem underneath it, and the questions worth asking, and then it stops, because the decision is yours and your doctor's.

## The case for going up, stated as strongly as it deserves

Pain is why you are on this drug at all, and pain has a threshold that you are currently underneath. Below 40mg, duloxetine has been tested for pain three times, in two different conditions, in roughly 350 patients, and it failed on pain every time `[primary, verified]`. Twice of those three, the trial had adequate power to detect an effect. The cleanest is Goldstein 2005, which randomised 457 patients with diabetic neuropathy 1:1:1:1 to placebo, 20mg, 60mg and 120mg, giving the 20mg arm and the 60mg arm roughly 114 patients each `[primary, verified]`. 60mg separated from placebo. 20mg did not. Same trial, same disease, same number of people in each arm, which means the usual escape route (the low-dose arm was too small to show anything) is closed. Cochrane's own authors say it without hedging: doses of 60mg and 120mg daily are efficacious, *"but lower daily doses are not"* `[primary, verified]`.

The second argument for going up is that the thing you would most fear losing appears to survive the trip. Nicolini 2009 is the only trial that ever contained a fixed 20mg arm for anxiety, and its higher-dose arm did slightly better rather than worse: a HAM-A change of −15.3 points at 60–120mg against −14.7 at 20mg, with response rates of 65% against 60% and remission 44% against 42% `[primary, verified on significance; the magnitudes are secondary-source]`. Those gaps are small, but they point up rather than down. So the fear that a dose increase would trade your anxiety benefit away for pain relief does not have trial evidence behind it.

Third, there is no evidence that the specific step you are contemplating damages your sleep. One trial in the entire registry ever compared a fixed 20mg arm against 60mg and 120mg head to head, and on that trial's sleep measures 20mg and 60mg are indistinguishable: insomnia 10.2% against 8.6%, somnolence 5.1% against 4.3%, discontinuation for adverse events 15.3% against 14.7% `[primary, verified — ClinicalTrials.gov registry, NCT00408876]`. The inflection is at 120mg, where insomnia doubles to 18.8% and somnolence triples to 12.5%. You have been bracing for insomnia at 60mg, and the by-dose data does not put it there.

Fourth, two arguments that circulate against dose increases do not survive contact with their own sources, and you should not carry either into the room. The first is that going up makes coming down harder. It does not, in any way that matters to you, because duloxetine's occupancy curve is steep at the bottom and flat at the top: 60mg down to 20mg costs roughly 16 occupancy points with a capsule available at every step, whereas 20mg down to zero costs roughly 72 points with no capsule below 20mg `[reasoning, not evidence — modelled from Takano's ED50]`. The cliff is below your current dose. You are already standing on it, and going up adds a gentle ramp in front of an unchanged drop. The second is that emotional blunting is dose-dependent, which was described in this project's own validation as the strongest argument against an increase. It collapses on inspection: the claim traces back through two reviews to a single 1990 case report, and the one study that measured it directly found the opposite. Aşçibaşi 2020 raised doses in 98 patients and blunting *fell* (P<0.001) `[primary, verified]`. Goodwin 2017 found no difference by any agent, at roughly 46% across the board `[primary, verified]`.

Fifth, the POTS concern that appears in the literature is real but dose-qualified to 80–240mg per day `[primary, verified]`, which is not the dose you are discussing. It is worth a conversation at 60mg. It is not an argument against it.

## The case against going up, stated as strongly as it deserves

The pain payoff is modest even when the threshold is crossed. This is the number that matters most and it is not the one this project spent most of its time arguing about. The fibromyalgia-specific Cochrane review is Welsch 2018, and it reports NNTB 11, with a confidence interval of 9 to 14, for 50% or greater pain relief `[primary, verified — CD010292]`. Its authors describe that as **"no clinically relevant benefit."** GRADE quality: LOW. Sponsor: Lilly, on all but one trial. Eleven people take the drug at 60mg so that one of them gets meaningful pain relief, and the review team that produced the number does not think it clears the bar. Set that against the anxiety response at your current dose, where the same arithmetic gives roughly NNT 5.6 `[reasoning, not evidence — derived from a secondary-source table, so provisional]`. If those numbers hold, you are already on a dose with better evidence for the thing you report benefiting from than the target dose has for the thing you were prescribed it for.

Somnolence is the live risk, and it lands on precisely what you are protecting. Duloxetine's FDA label states that somnolence is one of four adverse events with a **significant dose-dependent relationship in fixed-dose studies** `[primary, verified — read directly from the label tables]`. The incidence is 10–11% against 3% on placebo. If you encounter the figure "1.5%" anywhere, that is a **discontinuation** rate wearing an incidence's clothes, and it is off by nearly an order of magnitude. In the only trial of duloxetine ever run in ME/CFS, somnolence was 41.4% (12 of 29) at 60–120mg against 10% on placebo `[primary, verified]`. That is your diagnosis, at the dose in question, and it is the highest somnolence figure anywhere in this corpus. And the reason it bites is that your reported gain from this drug is waking less tired. Somnolence is the one adverse effect that spends the benefit you actually got.

Here the evidence needs stating precisely, because it is more tangled than any summary of it. **The direction is label-confirmed. The magnitude at your specific step is not measured.** Two trials compared 30mg against 60mg directly, and somnolence rose sharply at that step: 2.6% to 10.2% in adolescents, and 7.8% to 14.3% in Korean adults `[primary, verified — registry]`. But those are 30-to-60 steps, not 20-to-60. The single trial that directly tested 20 against 60 found somnolence essentially flat, 5.1% against 4.3%, and its 20mg arm had only 59 patients, so it is underpowered and its confidence intervals are wide `[primary, verified]`. What you have, then, is a label that asserts dose-dependence without publishing the per-dose numbers, two trials showing a real rise one step above you, and one underpowered trial showing nothing at your step. That is a genuine tension, and it does not resolve. Anyone who tells you confidently that 60mg will make you sleepy, or confidently that it will not, is going beyond the data in one direction or the other.

Now the part that matters most, because it lands on you specifically. Of the three sleep-adjacent measures in that trial, **insomnia never rises across this range at all, and somnolence sometimes does. But the one that rises at your exact step is fatigue.** It went from 0% at 20mg to 6.0% at 60mg, and supplement 5 identifies it as the only clean monotonic rise across the 20-to-60 step in the whole table `[primary, verified]`. The adverse-event term "fatigue" is not the same object as your bone-weary exhaustion, and it would be dishonest to pretend otherwise. But it is the closest thing the data has to it, **fatigue is your primary disability, and your valued gain from this drug is waking less tired.** So the single exception in the table lands precisely on what you are protecting. The project's own brief left it out and put the emphasis on somnolence instead, which is the wrong emphasis on the wrong measure.

The sensory-gating cost may also be dose-dependent, and this is the argument that speaks to the thing you have already noticed. The only human dose comparison that exists is two studies from one laboratory, using the same paradigm, the same design and the same population, one dose apart. Jensen 2008: escitalopram 10mg, n=21, P50 sensory gating **intact** `[primary, verified]`. Oranje 2011: escitalopram 15mg, n=20, P50 gating **disrupted** `[primary, verified]`. **One small dose step flipped the filter**, and that is a real finding about a real thing. It is also the only mechanism in eight passes that predicts the acute-worse effect you most distinctively reported.

What it does not tell you is how far your own step would move it, and here a tempting bit of arithmetic has to be resisted before a better one replaces it. 20mg to 60mg is a tripling of the *milligrams*, but milligrams are not the active variable. Serotonergic engagement saturates, so you are already at roughly 72% transporter occupancy and 60mg puts you at roughly 88%, which is a sixteen-point step rather than a tripling `[reasoning, not evidence]`. Reading the ladder as "1.5× flipped it, so 3× will flip it harder" treats a saturating curve as a straight line. **The concern survives; the dose multiplier does not.**

But killing the wrong multiplier is only half the job, and this page previously stopped there. Redo it in the unit that is actually doing the work and the warning comes back sharper. Oranje's filter-flipping step, converted, is **+3.7 occupancy points**. Your own escitalopram step from 10 to 20mg, the one that cost you a week, is **+5.8 points**. The step you are contemplating is **+16.7 points**, which is about **4.5 times** the step that flipped the filter and about **2.9 times** the step that cost you a week `[reasoning, not evidence — occupancy modelled, and the comparison crosses two drugs]`.

And the counterweight belongs in the same breath, because it is on your own data and it does not cross drugs. **Your duloxetine initiation two weeks ago was +71.7 occupancy points, 4.3 times larger than the step you are contemplating, in the same drug and the same person, and it produced mild floatiness.** There is no arithmetic reason to expect 16.7 points of the same drug in the same body to produce a catastrophe. Notice the asymmetry honestly: the warning borrows from escitalopram at exactly the point where the difference between the drugs is the whole question, and the counterweight does not borrow at all, which makes it structurally better-founded. It is still n=1, and removing an alarm is not a reassurance. Two arguments, opposite directions, both partly your own data, neither settling it. Oranje's own conclusion is the fair summary and you are precisely the patient in the sentence: because people already treated with these drugs *"might already suffer from disrupted sensory gating and habituation, the current results call for caution in the determination of a proper dose."* Caution in dose determination. Not a prohibition, and not a green light.

You have already accepted worse acute sensory sensitivity at 20mg as the price of what else the drug is doing, so the question is not whether you can tolerate that cost but whether it scales, and **nobody knows, because every study in that literature is a single dose in a drug-naive volunteer** `[no evidence exists]`. This is escitalopram rather than duloxetine, a P50 waveform rather than a festival, and the leap is enormous and unbridged.

One further thing the corrected history does, and it cuts the other way from most of this page. An earlier pass predicted a dose increase would re-open the acute window *"transiently,"* and that word was flatly unsupported. It now has exactly one observation behind it: you raised escitalopram from 10 to 20mg, lost about a week, and then it faded and did not return across months at the higher dose. **So the dose question has a shape it did not have. On your own history with serotonergic dose transitions, an increase plausibly costs about a week and then passes — a transient toll rather than a permanent trade.** Hold it loosely, because every link in the inference is an assumption: different drug, different receptor profile, one person, retrospective at about a year's recall, unblinded. **Nothing here predicts that 60mg will dissociate you. Nothing supports that** `[no evidence exists]`. What changes is smaller and more useful: the expected value of asking for a **slower titration** than the standard 20-to-60 jump, which is hedging rather than forecasting. And the pharmacokinetics add a cheap checkable prediction: a shorter half-life makes a transition *faster*, not sharper, so if you do go up and do get a transient it should resolve in roughly **two to three days** rather than the week escitalopram cost you `[reasoning, not evidence]`. If it lasts much longer, the read-across was wrong.

One structural point about the options, because it is the one most likely to go unnoticed in the room. The realistic offer is not simply 20 or 60. **The most likely offer is 30mg**, because titrating in small steps is ordinary practice. And 30mg is the one option where the costs and the benefits line up worst: it disturbs the running 20mg observation exactly as much as 60mg would, because any dose change resets the baseline and confounds the slope, while the pain payoff it buys is **the one that has already been directly tested and failed with adequate power** (Arnold 2012: 30mg, n=155 against 153, P=0.202) `[primary, verified]`. So on the evidence, 30mg pays the full information price for the weakest evidenced gain. **That is not an argument against it**, because there is a perfectly good reason to pass through 30mg that has nothing to do with its pain evidence: titrating for tolerability on the way to 60mg. The distinction worth carrying into the room is between **30mg as a waypoint**, which is reasonable, and **30mg as a destination**, which the trial evidence does not support.

## The trap, which is the most decision-relevant thing on this page

You have a hypothesis: that the crashes aggravate your ME/CFS and fibromyalgia, and that preventing them should therefore improve your pain slowly, at any dose, with no threshold to cross. The standard model says something different: that pain needs the noradrenergic threshold at 40–60mg and nothing below it will do.

Right now, at 20mg, those two models **disagree**. Your model predicts slow pain improvement without a dose change. The standard model predicts none. That disagreement is a working experiment, and it is running.

At 60mg, the two models **make identical predictions**. Both say the pain should improve. Going to 60mg satisfies both, which means that if your pain improves afterwards you will never know whether it was norepinephrine crossing a threshold or the accumulated absence of crashes doing what you thought it would do. The experiment does not just pause. It closes, and it does not reopen, because you cannot un-know a dose.

**The 20mg window is the only clean test of your own hypothesis that will ever exist, and it is open now.** `[reasoning, not evidence]`

This is not an argument for staying at 20mg. It is an argument that the information has a value, that the value is finite and time-limited, and that you should price it deliberately rather than discover afterwards that you spent it without noticing. It is entirely coherent to decide the pain matters more than the knowledge and to go up anyway. What is not available is going up and keeping the experiment.

**And a thumb on the scale, disclosed rather than hidden, because it is this page's own.** The corrections accumulated across this project have **clustered directionally toward "don't increase"**, and a run of one-directional corrections is a pattern worth distrusting rather than a conclusion worth reaching. That is not evidence that going up is right. It is a reason to notice that a page can accumulate a lean without any individual sentence being wrong, and to read the two columns as columns rather than as a verdict with a formality attached. This revision pushed in both directions and has tried to say so each time it did.

There is a separating observation, and it is free. Your model has to run through the fatigue limb, because the only surviving path in the literature is distress to fatigue to pain, and the direct distress-to-pain path was null in Okifuji's data `[primary, verified]`. So: **track fatigue and pain separately, and never average them. If pain improves while fatigue does not, your model is dead.** That is a real falsifier, it costs nothing, and it works whichever dose you end up on.

The honesty this section owes you: the load-bearing limb of your hypothesis is unsupported. The claim that overload triggers crashes is established, and it is described in a primary clinical consensus document using your own word for it `[primary, verified — Canadian Consensus Criteria 2003: "Overload phenomena may trigger a 'crash'"]`. The claim that crashes then aggravate the illness has never been studied, and the adjacent evidence leans mildly against it `[no evidence exists]`. Your hypothesis survives on this page because it was killed by absence of research rather than by logic, which is a real distinction and one you drew yourself. It should not be mistaken for support.

## What you can observe before the appointment, and what you cannot

Four variables are already moving. Duloxetine (two weeks), melatonin (nightly), iron (started this week), and possibly a dose change. The iron one deserves a specific note: **oral iron acts at roughly 12 weeks, so it cannot explain anything at your appointment** `[secondary — unverified]`. It is not a confound yet. It becomes one in the spring, which is exactly when you would otherwise be reading your own data for signs that the dose decision worked. Decide what you are measuring, and how you would tell these apart, before you change anything.

**Count events. Do not rate feelings.** This is the single most useful instrument change available to you, and it exists because of a specific finding about your diagnosis. In Watson's monozygotic twins discordant for ME/CFS, the CFS twins rated themselves at 10.9 on the Epworth scale, which crosses the clinical threshold for excessive daytime sleepiness, while their objective sleep latency was 8.9 minutes and entirely normal `[primary, verified]`. The introspective channel is not buried under your fatigue. It is **hijacked**: pinned near the top, answering a question it was not asked. When you introspect "am I sleepy?", your fatigue answers. Meanwhile the objective channel, actual sleep propensity, is clean and has enormous headroom. So route around introspection. Ask "did I fall asleep?", never "do I feel sleepy?" `[reasoning, not evidence — the premises are primary]`

Your own concern was that you would not notice somnolence, and you put it precisely: *"There are different types of exhaustion. Just feeling 'sleepy' isn't a bother. It's mostly that bone-weary, pain-laden exhaustion where my brain is filled with so much fog I can barely function."* Your conclusion was right. Your reason for it was not, and the correction is what makes the problem solvable.

The discriminator that works needs no introspection at all. **Count deliberate naps that succeeded and unintended dozes separately, and never merge them.** Benefit looks like the first rising while the second stays at zero. Somnolence looks like the second rising, whatever the first does. Both can happen at once, and you should not expect a clean verdict `[reasoning, not evidence]`. The reason this axis works is that the two are opposite on the question "did I choose this?", which is a question your fatigue cannot answer for you. There is a third discriminator, dose-dependence, and it is the sharpest of the lot, but it is **unavailable to you before the appointment** because you are at one dose. It only exists after the decision it would inform.

**The nap test** is worth running, and the reason is not the one you would guess. You have been running an aborted version of a standard clinical protocol your entire life. The Multiple Sleep Latency Test allows **20 minutes** and scores a no-sleep trial as 20. You lie there for 10 to 20 minutes and give up. So your lifelong hard zero may be a lifelong latency of 18 minutes that you have never once let run to completion. Running the full 20 converts a lifelong binary into a number, and a number can move where a "no" cannot `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

But the asymmetry has to come first, because it is the verdict. **The test is sound for a positive and near-worthless for a negative.** The killer is that duloxetine improved your sleep, which reduces sleep drive, which makes napping *harder*. Bonnet and Arand measured exactly this: sleep deprivation drove nap latency from 13.4 minutes to 3.8 `[primary, verified]`, and their conclusion is that measured sleep tendency is a combination of sleep drive and arousal, with drive dominating. The drug is pushing the outcome both ways at once. If it lowered your arousal, latency should fall. If it improved your nights, sleep pressure falls and latency should rise. These cancel, and nobody knows by how much. **A null is therefore uninterpretable**, and you should know that before you start so that a null does not read to you as a refutation. If you do nap, cleanly and repeatably at a fixed time on a channel that has been at zero for decades, that is a real observation and it would be the first thing anyone has ever recorded about duloxetine and daytime sleep propensity in any population `[no evidence exists]`.

The protocol matters more than it seems: four attempts rather than one, a fixed early-afternoon time, **no exertion for 30 minutes beforehand** (Bonnet and Arand found a five-minute walk raised latency from 6.9 to 11.9 minutes, which is larger than any plausible drug effect) `[primary, verified]`, no caffeine for eight hours, log the previous night's sleep, and write your prediction down before the first attempt. Do it now, because the window closes at the dose change and does not reopen.

One finding runs against your own theory and you should have it. You wondered whether not napping might be causing your poor sleep. Gotts 2015 followed 101 ME/CFS patients with 14-day sleep diaries: 21 of them (20.8%) did not nap at all, so you are in a common fifth rather than an anomalous one, and **napping was associated with worse cognition and more daytime sleepiness** `[primary, verified]`. Cross-sectional, so the direction is unresolvable, and confounded by severity. But the sign is the opposite of your assumption. **Your causal arrow may run backwards: not napping may be protecting your night sleep rather than damaging it.** That is untested, and it is worth knowing before you treat learning to nap as a goal.

## The measurement problem you cannot solve before the appointment

Your episodes are **triggered**, not spontaneous. That single fact changes the denominator. It is not time. It is **exposures** `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

Two quiet months working from home, avoiding crowds, is worth almost nothing, because the drug was never tested. One theme-park day with no episode afterwards is worth more than two uneventful months. If a significant sensory overload reliably produced an episode 80% of the time at baseline, then **three to five comparable overloads survived cleanly is roughly a p<0.05 result**, and it is a result about the thing that actually matters `[reasoning, not evidence]`. It has one further property the calendar lacks: regression to the mean cannot fake it. Regression explains why a bad stretch is followed by a better one. It does not explain why a specific, repeated, previously reliable trigger stopped producing its effect.

You have **one** such exposure. Five-plus hours at two crowded festivals, two panic attacks with heart rate above 140 at rest, historically a guaranteed one-to-two-week crash, and no crash. Your family independently braced for it and it never came, which matters because it makes the observation harder to explain as your own expectation.

One is not three. And the time-based arithmetic is worse than it feels: at two weeks, against a baseline of several episodes a month, **13% to 37% of people for whom this drug does absolutely nothing would be seeing exactly what you are seeing** `[reasoning, not evidence — Poisson, λ = 2 to 4/month]`. Roughly a one-in-three to one-in-seven chance of a pure fluke. Chance stops being a plausible explanation at about six weeks, and it is dead by three months. But that only kills random fluctuation. **Placebo, regression to the mean and natural course all survive the passage of time, because they are systematic rather than random, and they produce exactly the same zero-episode observation.** A three-month clean run proves the improvement is real. It says nothing about whether the drug caused it.

Your appointment lands at roughly four weeks. At four weeks you will have between 2% and 14% odds of a pure fluke and, unless chance supplies a trigger, no exposure-conditional data at all. **The data arrives after the decision.** That is not a reason to delay anything. It is a reason to walk in knowing precisely what you do not yet know, and to say so, because the strongest thing you can bring to that room is an accurate account of the size of your own uncertainty.

There is one asymmetry in your favour and it is underrated. The worse restless legs, the vivid dreams and the floaty feeling are **pharmacological signatures**. Nobody's expectation produces restless legs `[reasoning, not evidence]`. They do not prove the drug is causing the episode benefit, because two real effects of one drug need not be the same effect. But they remove "the drug is inert at 20mg" from the table entirely, and they give you a covariate: if the dreams and the RLS fade while the episode benefit persists, that dissociation is informative.

## The questions worth asking

**"What is my transferrin saturation?"** You are on oral iron with ferritin around 70, which is correct, because the IRLSSG threshold for oral iron is ferritin at or below 75 `[primary, verified]`. But the same guideline says **no iron if transferrin saturation is above 45%**, and ferritin cannot tell you that. Worse, **ferritin is an acute-phase reactant**, so with fibromyalgia and ME/CFS it can read falsely high, which means your true iron status may be worse than 70 suggests rather than better. Ask for the number, ask whether it is under 75, and ask for a morning fasting full panel rather than ferritin alone. The real-world failure mode here is not the ordering. Panels get ordered 92.1% of the time. Among patients with ferritin at or below 75, **only 50% actually received iron** `[secondary — unverified; conference abstract, weak]`. The failure is in acting on the result.

**"When the time comes, will you write a compounded liquid?"** The taper problem exists at your current dose, right now, and is not created by going up. There is no capsule below 20mg. Ask this now, while it is entirely hypothetical and the conversation costs nothing. If the answer is yes, you have removed the largest obstacle from a decision you may never make. If the answer is no, or if the answer is "we'll just do every other day," you have learned something important at the cheapest possible moment.

This project has been making that argument from a systematic review. You do not need one, because you already ran the experiment. Your escitalopram taper was four even 5mg steps, two weeks apart, and *"each dosage reduction did nothing as far as I could tell"* until the last, when 5mg to zero cost you two weeks of functionality. In occupancy those four identical 5mg steps are **2.0, then 3.7, then 9.2, then 60.0 points** `[reasoning, not evidence — modelled]`. **The last step is thirty times the first. Same five milligrams, thirty times the pharmacology, and you felt exactly the one the arithmetic says you should have.** That is the hyperbolic taper model's central prediction and you lived it without knowing the model existed.

Now stand where you are standing. **Duloxetine's equivalent drop, 20mg to zero, is 71.7 occupancy points, bigger than the one that cost you two weeks** `[reasoning, not evidence]`. Escitalopram at least has a 5mg tablet that can be halved, so there was a rung below the one you stepped off. Duloxetine's smallest capsule *is* the edge. And the risk model underneath the whole hyperbolic-tapering recommendation says past experience of withdrawal predicts future risk, and that duration of use raises incidence and severity `[primary abstract only — Horowitz 2023, PMID 36513909]`. You now have a documented one. So this is the most actionable item on the page: you are arguing from two weeks of your own life, and you can say it in one sentence.

One thing that reading must not become, and it is the inference this project keeps getting wrong in exactly this shape. **"You already fell off this cliff once, so you know what duloxetine's will feel like" is not available.** Your two initiations delivered matched transporter engagement and produced opposite outcomes, so your response to a given occupancy change is drug-specific. **The arithmetic transfers. The felt experience does not.** What survives is worth more than what fails: the taper-cliff argument never needed your history, since duloxetine's 71.7 points stands on duloxetine's own data. Your experience makes the argument *sayable*, not stronger. And one honest gap sits under all of it: **the link from occupancy loss to withdrawal symptoms is assumed rather than validated** `[no evidence exists]`. The review that established the curve says occupancy change *"might be related"* to withdrawal; nobody has measured the two in the same people.

**"What is this increase for: the pain, or everything?"** The two effects have different dose curves. Naming the target changes the answer, and it is the question eight research passes failed to ask.

**A trial period with a written, pre-agreed reversal plan.** Reasonable on its face and the time course supports it: steady state arrives in about three days at a 12-hour half-life, so a fortnight at the new dose with the first three to five days discarded as transition gives a readable window. Ask for the reversal criteria to be written down *before* the change, because that substitutes for the blinding you cannot have.

**The interaction list, for any other prescriber.** The absolute contraindications are **MAOIs, linezolid and methylene blue**, and the reason they matter is not that you would take them by choice. They are given by anaesthetists and infectious-disease teams, in situations where **you may be the only person in the room who knows you are on a serotonergic drug** `[primary, verified]`. **Tramadol** is the one you are most likely to actually be offered, because it is routinely prescribed for fibromyalgia, and it is worse than additive: duloxetine is a moderate CYP2D6 inhibitor, and CYP2D6 is the enzyme that converts tramadol into the metabolite that does the analgesia, so **duloxetine makes tramadol work less while the serotonergic parent drug lingers** `[primary, verified]`. Less of the benefit, more of the risk, and a patient whose tramadol "isn't working" may escalate the dose and escalate the serotonergic load with it. **Fluvoxamine** raises duloxetine exposure **six-fold**, and **ciprofloxacin** runs the same pathway, so any antibiotic prescriber needs to know.

Two more for that conversation. **Alternate-day dosing is precisely wrong** at a 12-hour half-life, because 48 hours is four half-lives and plasma falls to roughly 6% of peak before the next dose, which is a small withdrawal every two days, indefinitely `[primary, verified — in-silico modelling, not a trial]`. It is also the single most likely piece of bad advice you will receive, because it is intuitive, free, and what a busy prescriber reaches for when there is no smaller capsule. And some things are **over-warned**, which is worth knowing so you do not spend the appointment's goodwill on them: triptans (0 of 29 FDA cases met the Hunter criteria) and 5-HTP (**zero documented human cases**, ever) `[primary, verified]`.

One last fact for the room: you do not smoke, and smokers run **38.4% lower serum duloxetine and 53.6% lower dose-adjusted concentrations** than non-smokers at the same dose `[primary, verified]`. You are already at the high-exposure end of whatever number is on the prescription. That is not an argument in either direction. It is context for reading your own response, and for anyone comparing your dose to somebody else's.

**The separating observation, free and available at any dose**: his model must run through the fatigue limb, because Okifuji 2011 (PMID 20591745) found the **direct** distress→pain path **null**; only distress→fatigue→pain survived `[primary, verified]`. **Track fatigue and pain separately. **Speed of onset is a second discriminator** `[reasoning, not evidence]`: direct pharmacological analgesia is fast; allostatic unloading is slow by construction. Fibromyalgia pain is largely stable across ten years, so a substantial drift down at a fixed 20mg would be genuinely surprising and therefore informative.

**The argument against going up: 4.5×.** The contemplated step is 4.5× the step that flipped the sensory filter in a blind crossover, and 2.9× the step that cost him a week. This is the sharpest form of the gating dose warning anyone has produced, and it is arithmetic on the corpus's own numbers. **Its weakness: both comparators are *escitalopram* and the contemplated step is *duloxetine*. It crosses drugs at exactly the point where crossing drugs is the whole question.**

**The argument that the fear is overdrawn: 4.3×.** His duloxetine initiation was 4.3× *larger* than the contemplated step, same drug, same person, two weeks ago, and produced mild floatiness. If 71.7 points of duloxetine did that, there is no arithmetic reason to expect 16.7 points of the same drug in the same person to produce a catastrophe. **Its strength: unlike the warning, it does not cross drugs. Its weakness: still n=1, and removing a reason for alarm is not evidence of safety.**

**Both belong; neither wins** `[reasoning, not evidence — every occupancy figure is modelled]`. Oranje's own conclusion is the fair summary and he is exactly the patient in the sentence `[primary, verified]`: since patients frequently treated with SSRIs *"might already suffer from disrupted sensory gating and habituation, the current results **call for caution in the determination of a proper dose**."* **Caution in dose determination. Not a prohibition.**

Supporting: MMN **increased** at both doses (Oranje 2008 PMID 17971261; Wienberg 2010 PMID 19304862); habituation **delayed** at both (Jensen 2007 PMID 17299503) `[primary, verified ×3]`. The composite picture: **detects more, filters less, stops responding more slowly.**

**Limits, at the claim rather than beneath it:**
- **Cross-study, not within-study.** Two papers, not one dose-response experiment.
- **Single lab.** All four studies are from the same group. The only independent lab (Mann 2008, PMID 17895917) ran the *opposite* manipulation and found depletion breaks PPI and spares P50, a mirror image arguing for an inverted-U rather than one dial `[primary, verified]`.
- **Escitalopram, not duloxetine.**
- **n ≈ 20 healthy males.** The leap from a P50 waveform to a festival is enormous and unbridged.
- **All four are single-dose in drug-naive volunteers. Chronicity is completely unmeasured** `[no evidence exists]`.

#### The word "transiently": what the corrected history does to it, and how little that is

Until the correction, this page said flatly that **"the acute window re-opens *transiently*" is unsupported**, because the word requires chronic data and every gating study is a single dose. That was right at the time and now needs amending, **in the direction that favours the claim, which is exactly when §1's failure mode bites hardest.**

Supplement 4 wrote the prediction before anyone knew the history: *"a dose increase should predict a temporary return of dissociation/anxiety, in a person with a documented history of exactly that reaction to serotonergic escalation."* The corrected history reports exactly that, twice: transient, at the transition, fading. **The prediction was written first and confirmed second, which is the right order and is rare in this project.** It is the only prediction in eight passes tested against anything at all.

> ### ⚠ And it is confirmed in the weakest sense available. It must not move any ranking, and it moves none here.
>
> 1. **Wrong drug.** Predicted for *duloxetine*, confirmed by *escitalopram*. Whether the two drugs engage the same mechanism is the exact question at issue, so this is confirmed only if you already assume the thing in dispute. **Near-circular, and the single biggest discount.**
> 2. **NOT DISCRIMINATING, and this is the deepest problem.** Activation folklore predicts a dose increase transiently worsens things. The gating ladder predicts it. Ordinary pharmacological intuition predicts it. **A prediction many rival accounts share does not select among them when it comes true.**
> 3. **n=1, retrospective, ~1 year's recall**, no contemporaneous record.
> 4. **Unblinded.** He knew the dose changed; so did everyone else.
> 5. **The theory that generated it was already dead on other grounds.** A dead theory's surviving prediction coming true does not revive it.
>
> **Net evidential value: low but non-zero.** "Transiently" has moved from *unsupported* to *supported by one retrospective observation on a different drug that its rivals predict equally well*. A real change of state, and a very small one.

**What it does give is a shape for the dose question that it did not have.** On his own history with serotonergic dose transitions, an increase plausibly costs **about a week and then passes**: a **transient toll rather than a permanent trade** `[reasoning, not evidence]`. The inferential distance, stated honestly, because every link is an assumption:

| Link | Status |
|---|---|
| He had transient dissociation at escitalopram dose increases | `[reader report — n=1, retrospective, unblinded]` |
| → therefore at *serotonergic* dose increases generally | Requires assuming the escitalopram effect was serotonergic rather than drug-specific — **and his two occupancy-matched initiations with opposite outcomes are direct evidence against that assumption** |
| → therefore at a *duloxetine* dose increase | Requires assuming duloxetine engages the same mechanism. **Unknown** |
| → and it will be transient | Requires the adaptation to complete. **Untested at any dose of anything** |

**The honest statement for the appointment**: he has a documented history of losing a week of function at each of two serotonergic dose increases. That is a fact about him and worth telling his prescriber. Whether it generalises to duloxetine is unknown, for a citable reason: nobody has measured the thing that would settle it. **What it is NOT: a prediction that 60mg will dissociate him. Nothing supports that** `[no evidence exists]`. **What it changes is the expected value of asking for a slower titration than the standard 20→60 jump — a conversation about hedging, not a forecast.**

**And the PK supplies a cheap prediction while it is here.** The tempting reasoning says duloxetine's shorter half-life makes its transitions *sharper*, contradicting his experience. **That is backwards.** A shorter half-life produces a *faster* transition, not a harsher one: time to a new steady state ≈ 5 half-lives, so escitalopram takes ~1 week and duloxetine ~2.5 days `[reasoning, not evidence, on primary, verified label figures]`. **There is no paradox to resolve.** PK exits as an explanation for the escitalopram/duloxetine divergence and re-enters as a prediction about duration only: **if he goes up and gets a transient, it should resolve in ~2–3 days rather than ~7. If it runs much longer, the read-across was wrong.** Falsifiable, free, and one of very few checkable predictions in the project.

### The measurement design

**The core finding that makes it work** `[primary, verified — Watson 2004, PMID 15453557; n=20 MZ twin pairs discordant for CFS]`:

| Measure | CFS twin | Healthy co-twin | p |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Epworth (ESS)** | **10.9** | 8.2 | **.03** |
| Stanford (SSS) | 3.4 | 2.1 | <.001 |
| **MSLT mean sleep latency** | **8.9 min** | 10.0 min | **.33 — NS** |

**ESS 10.9 crosses the clinical threshold for excessive daytime sleepiness, in people whose objective test says they are not sleepy at all.** The introspective channel is **hijacked, not buried**: pinned near the top, answering a question it was not asked. The objective channel is clean with enormous headroom.

Mechanistically supported by **Olson 1998** (PMID 9844851, n=225 including 12% with CFS) `[primary, verified]`: **ESS correlated with every SCL-90 psychological subscale except psychoticism; mean sleep latency correlated with none of them.** Verbatim: *"The ESS cannot be used to demonstrate or exclude sleepiness as it is measured by MSLT."*

**A caution against over-citing Watson**: the "may mistake fatigue for sleepiness" line is an **inference from a group-level dissociation**, not a measurement of any individual's discrimination. **No study has ever tested, as a psychophysical question, whether an ME/CFS patient can distinguish sleepiness from fatigue.** His exact problem is real and scientifically unaddressed `[no evidence exists]`.

**The design, derived from that:**

**1. Event counts are the primary outcome.** No introspection, and a floor near zero. Count and timestamp:
- **Unintended doze** (fell asleep without deciding to)
- **Deliberate nap attempt that succeeded** — **counted separately, never merged with the above**
- Head-nod / microsleep
- Re-read the same paragraph / rewound the same scene
- Dozed while: reading / screen / conversation / car passenger
- Drowsy driving or near-miss (binary, safety-critical)

**2. Split "tired" into three items that cannot collapse into each other**, and never average them:
- **SLEEP PRESSURE**, anchored to the MSLT concept rather than a feeling: *"If I sat in a quiet dim room right now and let myself, could I fall asleep? (0 = no chance, 4 = out in minutes)"*
- **BODY** — his own words: bone-weary, pain-laden. 0–10.
- **FOG** — cognitive. 0–10.

Togo 2008 (PMID 18474105) used separate sleepiness and fatigue scales within CFS and recovered meaningful subgroups, so separated items do carry signal in this population `[primary, verified]`.

**3. Fix the time AND the context. Not optional.** Åkerstedt 2014 (PMID 24750198), verbatim: *"The context has a strong influence on KSS ratings. Thus, **physical activity, social interaction and light exposure will reduce KSS values by 1–2 units**. In contrast, **time-on-task in a monotonous context will increase KSS values by 1–2 units**."* `[primary, verified]` **A 1–2 unit context swing on a 9-point scale swamps any plausible drug effect.** Three fixed times daily; same chair, same light, same activity state; rate before activity, not after; log dose time.

**4. Log night sleep latency.** Majer/Reeves found ME/CFS subjects were *better* calibrated on sleep latency than controls `[primary, verified]`. **This is the one self-report in the whole literature where his diagnosis is an advantage.**

**5. Run any dose change as a pre-registered A→B.** Collect ≥2 weeks at 20mg **before** any increase (the baseline window is now and it closes at the dose change); then ≥2 weeks at the new dose, discarding the first 3–5 days as transition (steady state ≈3 days at a ~12h half-life). **Do NOT attempt ABAB / withdrawal-reintroduction** — duloxetine's discontinuation syndrome makes self-directed withdrawal blocks unsafe, so the canonical n-of-1 crossover is not available. That is a real methodological loss, not a formality. **Pre-register the prediction in writing**, which substitutes for the blinding he cannot have.

**6. Confounds to log daily**: sleep timing and duration; crash/PEM day (y/n); pain; **caffeine (dose and time of last)**; alcohol; **iron**; **melatonin (dose and time)**; other meds; illness; heat; previous-day exertion.

> **Caffeine deserves special flagging**: it is the one covariate that **actively masks the outcome being measured.** If caffeine intake drifts upward in response to emerging somnolence, it suppresses the very signal being hunted. A hidden compensatory loop that makes a real effect look absent. `[reasoning, not evidence]`

**The decision rule this buys** `[reasoning, not evidence]`:

| **Event counts** | **Subjective sleepiness** | **Interpretation** |
|---|---|---|
| **Rise** | Rises | **Real somnolence.** The dose trade-off is live. |
| **Rise** | **Flat** | **Real somnolence he cannot feel — exactly the failure mode he feared. Trust the counts.** |
| **Flat** | Rises | **Likely expectancy or the Watson fatigue-misattribution effect.** |
| **Flat** | Flat | No detectable somnolence at this dose. |

**`[no evidence exists]`: no study validates a behavioural dozing-event diary against drug somnolence in any population.** This is mechanistic reasoning from the MSLT/ESS dissociation, not a validated instrument. Said plainly rather than dressed up.

**One safety note that must travel with this design**: if sleep-propensity events do rise, duloxetine is not the only candidate. Mariman 2013 (PMID 24182640): of 279 patients referred with presumed CFS, only **23.3% had unequivocal CFS** and **49.8% had a sleep disorder**, mostly OSA `[primary, verified]`. **Undiagnosed sleep apnoea is the main competing explanation for any new objective sleepiness signal, and that is a clinician conversation.**

### The nap protocol

**Why it is worth running**: the MSLT protocol allows **20 minutes** and scores a no-sleep trial as 20. He quits at 10–20. **His lifelong hard zero may be a lifelong latency of 18 minutes that he has never let run.** Running the full 20 converts a binary into a number `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

**The asymmetry, which is the verdict**: **sound for a positive, near-worthless for a negative.**

**The killer confound** `[reasoning, not evidence — the premise is primary]`: duloxetine improved his sleep → less sleep drive → napping **harder**. **Bonnet & Arand 2000 (PMID 10737337)**, 12 patients with psychophysiological insomnia:

| Condition | Nap sleep latency |
|---|---|
| Baseline | **13.4 min** |
| After sleep deprivation | **3.8 min** |
| After a 5-minute walk | **11.9 min** |
| After 15 min watching TV | **6.9 min** |

Verbatim conclusion: *"measured sleep tendency is a **combination of sleep drive and level of central nervous system arousal**, where arousal has both state and trait components."* `[primary, verified]` **Sleep drive dominates.** The drug pushes both directions at once and nobody knows by how much, so **a null is uninterpretable.**

**Four further things that weaken it** `[reasoning, not evidence]`: the **effort paradox** (trying to sleep prevents sleep is the defining mechanism of psychophysiological insomnia, the phenotype he most resembles, so a deliberate test with a hypothesis attached maximises the anxiety that raises latency); the **denominator is unknown** (rare, self-selected, uncontrolled attempts); **one nap is an event, not a rate**; and he does not have a comparison dose.

**The protocol:**

| # | Rule | Why |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Four attempts, not one.** Four consecutive days, or the MSLT's own 4–5 trials at 2-hour intervals in one day | One trial is an anecdote. Four is a latency. |
| 2 | **Run the FULL 20 minutes.** Score minutes to sleep; if no sleep, score 20 | **The single highest-value change.** Converts the binary into a number. |
| 3 | **Fixed time, early afternoon (~13:00–15:00)**, same every attempt | The circadian nap zone. Wrong hour guarantees a null. |
| 4 | **No exertion for 30+ minutes before.** Sit or lie quietly first | Bonnet & Arand: a 5-minute walk raised latency 6.9 → 11.9 min — bigger than any plausible drug effect `[primary, verified]` |
| 5 | **Fixed conditions**: same room, dark, quiet, lying down, no screen | Åkerstedt 2014: context moves ratings 1–2 units `[primary, verified]` |
| 6 | **No caffeine 8h. Log it regardless** | The one covariate that actively masks the outcome |
| 7 | **Log the previous night's sleep** | **The dominant covariate.** 8 hours = low drive = the test is stacked toward null |
| 8 | **Log dose time**; keep the nap at the same interval from it | Distinguishes a plasma-tracking effect from a tonic one |
| 9 | **Pre-register the prediction in writing before attempt 1** | Substitutes for the blinding he cannot have |
| 10 | **Record three things**: minutes to sleep (or 20), fell asleep Y/N, and separately *"did I feel I could have?"* | The third is introspective and least trustworthy (Olson 1998). Record it; do not let it outvote the first two. |

**Now, before the appointment. The 20mg window closes at the dose change and does not reopen.**

**What the nap test can and cannot distinguish** `[reasoning, not evidence — the premises are primary]`:

| Discriminator | **Reduced arousal (benefit)** | **Somnolence (adverse)** | Strength |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Volitional vs intrusive** | Sleeps **when he tries**. **Unintended dozes stay at zero.** | Sleeps **when not trying**. **Unintended dozes rise.** | **STRONGEST — needs no introspection** |
| **MSLT/MWT dissociation** | Moves MSLT, leaves MWT alone | Moves **both** | **Strong.** Sangal 1992 (PMID 1555459): MSLT vs MWT **r=0.41, <17% shared variance, two factors** `[primary, verified]` |
| **Time-locking to dose** | Flat across the day | Tracks the plasma curve | **Moderate.** Needs fixed-time sampling |
| **Dose-dependence** | Unknown direction | **Label-confirmed dose-dependent** | **⚠ UNAVAILABLE BEFORE THE APPOINTMENT.** He is at one dose. |

**The honest limit, stated because it matters more than the taxonomy** `[reasoning, not evidence]`: if he can nap, functions better, and nothing intrudes, then whether a physiologist calls it "reduced tonic arousal" or "mild somnolence" does not change what he should do. **The question that bears on the dose decision is not "which is it?" but "does it cost me anything?"** — answered by the unintended-doze count, the night-sleep log, and whether hours disappear. **Those are countable. The mechanism label is not.**

**The finding that runs against his own theory** `[primary, verified — Gotts 2015, PMID 25575044, 101 CFS patients, 14-day diaries]`: **21 of 101 (20.8%) did not nap at all across 14 days.** He is in a common fifth, not an anomalous one. And: *"Napping, particularly in the afternoon is associated with poorer cognitive functioning and more daytime sleepiness in CFS."* Cross-sectional, so direction is unresolvable, and confounded by severity. **But the sign is the opposite of his assumption, and it converges with standard sleep medicine: daytime napping reduces nocturnal sleep pressure. His causal arrow may run backwards — not napping may be protecting his night sleep.** `[reasoning, not evidence]`

**A correction carried from supplement 7**: the claim that his nap-failure "is the ME/CFS MSLT finding lived" is **dead**. **Watson's twins fell asleep in 8.9 minutes; that is not nap failure.** And where the ME/CFS MSLT literature is directional at all it runs the other way: **Neu 2008 found CFS patients fall asleep *significantly faster* than controls**; Spitzer 2010 found 80% abnormal in the sleepy direction `[primary, verified ×2]`. Maksoud's systematic review calls the MSLT findings *"inconsistent"* and *"non-replicated"*; CDC's Decker found *"no reproducible perturbations."* **His phenotype resembles the insomnia hyperarousal profile (increased MSLT latency), not the ME/CFS one, and even that fit is imperfect because he does not report insomnia.** He sits between two literatures, and Dressle & Riemann 2023 name the between-space as an open question `[primary, verified]`.

### The exposure denominator, and the arithmetic of two weeks

**His episodes are triggered, not spontaneous. So the denominator is exposures, not time.** `[reasoning, not evidence]`

**Time-based (Poisson, P(0) = e^(−λt), λ = episodes/month):**

| Elapsed | λ=2/mo | λ=3/mo | λ=4/mo | Reading |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **2 weeks ← he is here** | **36.8%** | **22.3%** | **13.5%** | **Unremarkable. Would happen by chance often.** |
| 1 month | 13.5% | 5.0% | 1.8% | Suggestive at best |
| **~4 weeks ← the appointment** | | **2–14%** | | **Still weak** |
| **6 weeks** | **5.0%** | **1.1%** | **0.25%** | **Crosses p<0.05 at every baseline** |
| 3 months | 0.25% | 0.012% | 0.0006% | **Chance is dead** |

`[reasoning, not evidence — standard Poisson for recurrent events; the arithmetic is checkable]`

**⚠ An inconsistency in the input, flagged rather than smoothed**: "several times a month" combined with "episodes last 1–2 weeks" **does not arithmetically fit** (3/month × 1.5 weeks = 4.5 weeks of episode per 4.3-week month, i.e. continuous illness). Either the episodes usually run much shorter than the theme-park example, or "several times a month" is an overestimate, or they overlap. **The theme-park→2-week episode may be an unusually severe instance rather than the modal one. This ambiguity propagates into every number above, and he is the only person who can resolve it.** Worth resolving before the appointment.

**Exposure-based, P(no episode in k exposures) = (1 − p₀)^k:**

| Baseline P(episode given overload) | Exposures needed for p<0.05 |
|---|---|
| **p₀ = 0.9** (near-deterministic) | **2** |
| **p₀ = 0.8** | **3** |
| p₀ = 0.7 | 3–4 |
| p₀ = 0.5 | 5 |

**Three to five comparable sensory overloads survived cleanly is roughly p<0.05, about the thing that actually matters.** `[reasoning, not evidence]` And it has a property the calendar lacks: **regression to the mean cannot fake it.** Regression explains why a bad stretch is followed by a better one. It does not explain why a specific, repeated, previously reliable trigger stopped producing its effect.

**He has one.** Five-plus hours at two crowded festivals, two panic attacks with HR >140 at rest, historically a guaranteed 1–2 week crash, no crash, and his family independently braced for it.

**Limits, at the claim**: it requires characterising p₀ **retrospectively**, which is recall-biased; it requires exposures to be genuinely **comparable**, which is a judgement call; and **deliberately seeking overload to test a drug is a real cost with a real risk, and is not being recommended.** The point is that when overloads happen naturally, those are the informative data points, and they should be recorded as such, prospectively, starting now.

**The crucial qualifier, and it is where most n-of-1 reasoning goes wrong**: the time math kills **"random fluctuation."** It does **not** touch **placebo, regression to the mean, or natural course**, because those are systematic rather than random and produce exactly the same zero-episode observation. **A three-month clean run proves the improvement is real. It says nothing about whether the drug caused it.** `[reasoning, not evidence]`

**The honest timeline:**

| When | What is knowable |
|---|---|
| **Now (2 weeks)** | **Nothing.** 13–37% chance of a fluke |
| **~4 weeks (the appointment)** | Still weak (2–14% fluke). **Meaningful only if a real trigger has been survived** |
| **6–8 weeks** | **"Random fluctuation" becomes implausible.** Placebo/regression still fully live |
| **3 months** | Chance is dead (<0.25%). **Placebo decay should be starting if it is placebo** |
| **3–6 months, 3+ survived triggers** | **The strongest realistic evidence available.** Drug vs placebo becomes separable |
| **6–12 months** | Matches the trial evidence (13.7% relapse at 26wk; 14.4% at 52wk). **Past 12 months, nobody has data on anyone** |

**The appointment arrives before the data does. Say that out loud in the room.**

**The three sharpest markers, ranked** `[reasoning, not evidence]`:
1. **The trigger test.** The only marker that separates drug from regression to the mean.
2. **The RLS, vivid dreams and floaty feeling.** These are **pharmacological signatures** and they are not plausible placebo responses, because nobody's expectation produces restless legs. They do **not** prove the drug caused the episode benefit, since two real effects of one drug need not be the same effect. **But they remove "the drug is inert at 20mg" from the table**, and they supply a covariate.
3. **The escitalopram contrast.** He has already run a within-subject control and it came out negative. A pure placebo account has to explain why the first serotonergic drug produced dissociation and non-function while the second produced resolution. **Expectancy does not usually reverse sign between two drugs in the same class.** Not conclusive (the drugs differ, circumstances differ, time has passed), but a genuine asymmetry the deflationary account has to absorb.

| **MAOIs** (phenelzine, tranylcypromine, isocarboxazid, selegiline) | Contraindicated. **5 days off duloxetine before starting an MAOI; 14 days off an MAOI before starting duloxetine** | Psychiatry |
| **Linezolid** | Reversible MAOI | **Infectious disease teams** |
| **Methylene blue (IV)** | Potent reversible MAO-A inhibitor. FDA safety communication 2011 | **Anaesthetists** |

> **Linezolid and methylene blue catch people because they are given by clinicians who are not thinking about the patient's antidepressant.** In those rooms, **he may be the only person who knows.** If emergency use is unavoidable: stop duloxetine immediately; resume 24h after the last dose.

**REAL AND LIKELY TO BE OFFERED**

| Drug | Why it is worse than additive |
|---|---|
| **Tramadol** | **Triple hazard.** (1) Tramadol is itself an SNRI, so the serotonergic load is additive, and documented duloxetine+tramadol serotonin syndrome cases exist. (2) **Duloxetine is a moderate CYP2D6 inhibitor, and CYP2D6 converts tramadol to M1 (O-desmethyltramadol), the metabolite that does the opioid analgesia. So duloxetine reduces tramadol's pain relief while leaving the serotonergic parent circulating longer.** Less benefit, more risk. A patient whose tramadol "isn't working" may escalate the dose and escalate the serotonergic load with it. (3) Tramadol lowers the seizure threshold independently. **This is the most likely dangerous combination he will actually be offered, because tramadol is routinely prescribed for fibromyalgia.** `[primary, verified]` |
| **Fluvoxamine** | Raises duloxetine **AUC 6-fold** `[primary, verified]` |
| **Ciprofloxacin** | Same CYP1A2 pathway. **Tell any antibiotic prescriber.** |
| **Codeine** | Same prodrug problem as tramadol: duloxetine blunts conversion to morphine |

**PRECISELY WRONG**

| Advice | Why |
|---|---|
| **Alternate-day dosing** | At a ~12h half-life (**FDA range 8–17h**, not the "10–15h" in circulation), 48 hours is four half-lives and plasma falls to **~6% of peak**. That is a small withdrawal every two days, indefinitely. In-silico modelling covering duloxetine explicitly concludes that lengthening the dose interval at or below minimum therapeutic doses *"likely increases the risk of withdrawal symptoms and cannot be recommended as a prudent strategy for tapering"* `[primary, verified — O'Neill, Sørensen, Taylor & Horowitz, PMID 40848773; modelling, not a trial]`. **The most likely bad advice he will receive**, because it is intuitive, free, and what a busy prescriber reaches for when there is no smaller capsule. The counterintuitive better direction from the same literature: **dose more often at lower doses.** |

**OVER-WARNED — say so, and do not spend the appointment on them**

| Item | Reality |
|---|---|
| **Triptans** | **0 of 29 FDA cases met Hunter criteria** (7/29 met the looser Sternbach) `[primary, verified — Evans 2007, PMID 18092054]`. Orlova 2018 (PMID 29596622 / *JAMA Neurol* 75:566): 19,017 co-prescribed patients, **30,928 person-years**, incidence **0.6–2.3 per 10,000 person-years**. The American Headache Society recommends against restricting. |
| **5-HTP** | **Zero documented human cases** of serotonin syndrome, alone or in combination, after decades of widespread OTC use `[secondary — converging sources]`. Mechanistically plausible; empirically empty. |
| **Ondansetron, lithium, SAMe, tryptophan** | Flagged by interaction checkers, not supported by cases |

### What nobody knows

**Nine evidence-absences. These are the most honest and valuable claims in the project, and they are not being softened.**

1. **Nobody has studied duloxetine in autistic adults.** One three-patient case series exists.
2. **Nobody has studied the overwhelm→crash cycle pharmacologically.**
3. **Nobody has studied whether prior dissociation predicts recurrence.**
4. **Nobody has studied this decoupling — a drug that worsens acute reactivity while preventing a delayed sequela — in any human, for any drug.** The human evidence explains the acute half; the animal evidence explains the aftermath half; nothing has both.
5. **Nobody has measured what duloxetine, or any SNRI, does to daytime sleep propensity.** No MSLT study exists at any dose in any population.
6. **Nobody has recorded a locus coeruleus neuron on chronic duloxetine in an awake animal**, let alone a human.
7. **Nobody has studied antidepressant-dose-change-induced dissociation.** 44 papers on antidepressants and depersonalisation, mostly burnout studies false-hitting on an unrelated subscale; narrowed properly, 4 papers, none on topic. No prevalence, no time course, no habituation data. The only named dose-transition syndrome does not list dissociation among its eleven constituent symptoms.
8. **Duloxetine has never been imaged outside three brain regions** (thalamus, midbrain, striatum) in twenty years; escitalopram has fourteen. Every region the dissociation literature implicates (insula, anterior cingulate, prefrontal) is unmeasured for duloxetine at every dose.
9. **The occupancy → withdrawal-symptom link is assumed, not validated.** The entire hyperbolic-tapering programme rests on it. Sørensen says occupancy change *"might* be related"; Horowitz builds a clinical recommendation on top of that word. No study measures occupancy change against withdrawal severity in the same people.

**And the quantitative fact underneath all of it**: **Abanades 2011 fitted individual EC50 values across ten people on the same drug, same dose, same protocol, and found an 8.5-fold spread (0.54–4.61 ng/mL)** `[primary, verified — PMID 20940733]`. **The population curve does not describe any individual in it.** That is why "try and see" is correct clinical practice rather than laziness, why forums show wild variance, and why nothing on this page can tell him what will happen to him.
