# What Is Pragmatics? The Hidden Operating System

## Tier 1 Introduction: The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

> Pragmatics is the layer of communication that is not about the words. It has mechanics, patterns, and names, and it can be learned as a system rather than guessed as an intuition.

You said the right thing. You know you said the right thing, because you checked the words. The words were accurate, appropriate to the topic, responsive to the question that was asked. And then something went wrong anyway. Someone's face changed. The conversation got cooler. A person you thought you were getting along with stopped replying. A manager used the word "tone" in your review and you had no idea what they were referring to, because the words of the thing you said were fine. You went back over it later. You ran the transcript in your head. The sentence was correct. The grammar was correct. The information was true. And yet.

If you are an autistic adult reading this, there is a strong chance you already knew, in some form, that a second layer existed in conversations. You noticed that people seemed to mean things they didn't say. You noticed that answers to questions were judged by something other than whether they were accurate. You noticed that the same sentence went over well in one room and badly in another, and you couldn't figure out the variable. This page exists to tell you what that second layer is, to give it a name, and to reframe what it means to have spent your life noticing something you couldn't quite see.

Consider five small moments. Your partner asks, "Do you know what time it is?" You say, "Yes." They look annoyed. You weren't being sarcastic; you answered the question they asked, whether you know the time. But that wasn't really the question they asked. A coworker says, "It's a little cold in here, don't you think?" You say, "It's fine for me." They sigh. You didn't realize they were asking you to close the window; their sentence was about temperature, but their message was about the window. You're at a party and someone says, "We should hang out sometime!" You say, "Sure, how about next Tuesday at 7?" They become uncomfortable, because "we should hang out sometime" was a friendly closing phrase, not an actual proposal to schedule anything. A friend tells you about a hard day at work. You respond by analyzing the problem and suggesting three solutions. They get upset, because they wanted you to say "that sounds really hard" first; the solutions could come later, or maybe not at all. Your manager says, "When you have a chance, it would be great if you could look at the Johnson file." You take it as information about her preferences. Two days later she asks, with an edge in her voice, whether you've looked at it yet. You thought she was making a low-priority request. She thought she was giving you a deadline.

In every one of those examples, the words were doing one thing and the speaker was doing another. "Do you know what time it is?" is, on the surface, a question about your knowledge; in practice, in that context, it was a request for the time. "It's a little cold in here" was, on the surface, a comment about temperature; in practice, it was a request to close the window. "We should hang out sometime" was, on the surface, a proposal; in practice, it was a friendly goodbye. There are two layers running in every conversation: the layer made of words, and the layer made of what you are supposed to mean by choosing those specific words in this specific situation with this specific person. Most communication in adult life runs on the second layer. The first layer is mostly a carrier signal for the second. That second layer is what linguists call **pragmatics**. It is not a mysterious social grace. It is not an intuition you were supposed to be born with. It is a system, a hidden operating system running underneath the words, and it has rules, patterns, and mechanisms that can be named, mapped, and learned.

Pragmatics is the branch of linguistics that studies how context contributes to meaning: what speakers mean, as distinct from what their words say. The easiest way to hold the distinction is this. Semantics is about the meaning of sentences in the abstract: "It's cold in here" means that the temperature in this location is low. Pragmatics is about what a speaker is doing when they say that sentence in a particular situation: "It's cold in here," said to the person nearest the window with a small glance at the window, is usually a request to close it. Semantics gives you the literal content. Pragmatics gives you the action the content is being used to perform.

The field has a history, and three figures are worth knowing at the start. In the 1950s, the Oxford philosopher J.L. Austin noticed that some sentences don't describe reality; they do things in it. "I promise" doesn't report a promise; it makes one. His book, *How to Do Things with Words* (1962), is the founding text of speech act theory, the idea that every utterance is an action, not just information. John Searle extended Austin's work into a systematic theory in the late 1960s and 1970s, giving us the distinction between direct speech acts (where the form matches the function) and indirect speech acts (where they don't). "Can you pass the salt?" is a question in form and a request in function, and the rules governing that mismatch are Searle's enduring contribution. Then H.P. Grice asked a different question: if so much of what we mean is not in what we say, how does anyone ever figure out what anyone else means? His answer was the Cooperative Principle, the assumption that speakers are generally trying to communicate sensibly, along with a set of conversational maxims (roughly: be informative, be truthful, be relevant, be clear). When a speaker seems to violate a maxim, listeners don't conclude the speaker is broken; they conclude the speaker is implying something beyond the literal words. That inferred meaning is called an implicature, and it is the engine that powers most everyday indirection. These three built the foundation. Everything else in this hub extends, critiques, or applies their work.

Most of the hub's early categories cluster around four subsystems. Speech acts (Category 1) are about what an utterance is doing: requesting, refusing, promising, warning, apologizing, questioning. The same sentence can perform different acts in different contexts, and if you only track the words, you will miss the action. Implicature (Category 2) is about what a speaker means beyond what they literally say. "It's getting late" at a dinner party is rarely a statement about time. Implicature is the single most common source of pragmatic mismatch between autistic and non-autistic speakers. Politeness (Category 3) is the system of linguistic strategies speakers use to manage each other's social standing, what Brown and Levinson called "face." Indirect requests, hedges, apologies, and softened refusals are all politeness moves, and politeness is not about being "nice"; it is a machinery with specific moves and specific costs that can be read like a grammar. Conversation structure (Category 4) is about the mechanics of how conversations are built: how turns are taken, how topics are opened and closed, how repair happens when something goes wrong. Turn-taking is not the free-for-all it looks like from the outside; it is structured, rule-governed, and learnable. Those four form the core, and the hub's remaining categories (discourse markers, hedging, register, deixis, presupposition, relevance, non-literal language, narrative, topic management, and others) all build on them.

Here is the single most important claim on this page: nobody learns pragmatics naturally. Most people pick it up through exposure, and some people need explicit instruction, and that is a difference in teaching, not a difference in capability. This reframe matters because the standard story, the one most autistic adults have been told implicitly or explicitly their whole lives, is that pragmatics is something typical children just absorb, and that failing to absorb it is a developmental fault. That story is wrong in two ways. First, typical children don't absorb pragmatics from thin air. They absorb it from thousands of hours of social feedback, most of which is calibrated to their specific neurocognitive profile. The adults around them are teaching pragmatics by modeling it, reacting to it, and repairing breakdowns in ways that make sense to a typical child's attention and salience systems. That is instruction; it just doesn't look like instruction because the curriculum is invisible. Second, when the learner's neurocognitive profile is different, the same environment doesn't deliver the same lesson. The social feedback is still happening, but it is less legible, less predictable, more dependent on implicit cues that the autistic learner's attention may not have landed on. The implicit channel is narrower, and the explicit channel (actual, spoken-aloud instruction in "here is what 'we should hang out sometime' means and here is how to answer it") is almost never offered, because the adults around the child don't know they are experts in a system with a name. Put those two together and you get the actual situation: autistic adults have often missed pragmatic content through no failure of theirs, because the only instruction offered was implicit and the implicit channel was narrower for them. The gap is real. The gap is not a deficit. The gap is a teaching gap.

The reframe goes one step further. It is not only that autistic people were under-taught a system. It is that the system itself is one of several possible pragmatic systems, and autistic communication tends to run on a different one. Damian Milton named this the double empathy problem in 2012. The core claim: communication breakdowns between autistic and non-autistic people are bidirectional. Both parties are operating with pragmatic expectations the other side doesn't share. Both parties are making inferences that turn out to be wrong. Both parties walk away feeling that something went off. The breakdown is mutual. What happens next is asymmetric, though. The mismatch gets charged to the autistic side by default. The autistic person is the one who "missed the tone," "took it wrong," "didn't read the room." The non-autistic person is rarely described as "failing to interpret an autistic communicator." Research on autistic peer-to-peer communication shows that autistic-to-autistic information transfer works about as well as non-autistic-to-non-autistic transfer; the breakdown concentrates at the cross-neurotype interface. The problem isn't one broken brain. It's two systems meeting and only one of them getting blamed. The double empathy problem is load-bearing for the whole hub. It is the reason the hub teaches pragmatics as a second language rather than as a corrective therapy. You are learning the conventions of a communication style that is one option among several. You are not being fixed. You are getting access to documentation for a system you have to interact with.

That reframe shapes every page in the hub, and it comes with commitments. The hub treats autistic communication as a legitimate mode, not a deficit to be corrected; it teaches pragmatics as a second language rather than a remedial one, and it refuses the language of disorder and impairment throughout. This is not a refutation of autism as a disability. It is a disability. But not all parts of autism must be framed as pathological, and this hub operates under the assumption that many of the communication difficulties inherent in autism result from a hidden grammar system taught using methods incompatible with how autistic minds work. Every empirical claim in the hub traces to a peer-reviewed source, a published book, or is explicitly labeled as community knowledge or lived experience. The hub is built on the academic pragmatics literature (Austin, Grice, Searle, Brown and Levinson, Sperber and Wilson, Clark, Levinson, and their successors) and on the autism research that corrects and extends it (Milton, Crompton, Sasson, and others). When a claim is speculative, the hub says so. Not all evidence is the same, and the hub distinguishes between large replicated findings, small studies, theoretical claims, clinical observations, and community consensus; you should not have to guess how confident to be in a given claim.

The hub does not teach you to perform neurotypicality. It does not tell you to make more eye contact, soften your voice, smile more, or match others' body language because that is what "successful" communication looks like. It teaches you how pragmatics works so that you can make informed decisions about when, how, and whether to engage with each piece of it. The goal is literacy, not camouflage. This matters because there is now robust evidence that sustained masking is associated with exhaustion, burnout, depression, anxiety, and suicidality in autistic adults. A learning resource that trains you to mask harder is not a safe learning resource. The hub is explicitly not that. Some pragmatic questions don't have clean answers. Some autistic communities are seriously under-researched, especially autistic adults without intellectual disability, autistic women, autistic people of color, and late-diagnosed autistic adults. Some strategies work for some people and not others. Some categories rest on strong evidence, and some rest on thinner ground. The hub will tell you which is which.

Pragmatic asymmetry can also be weaponized. The same indirectness that makes ordinary conversation hard to decode can be used, deliberately, to trap autistic people into accepting things they would never consent to if they had been told directly. The hub includes a dedicated manipulation sub-hub for this reason. When stakes are high, when safety is at risk or coercive control may be present, the hub's first move is safety planning, not strategy. Safety assessment comes before pattern recognition, because a pattern you recognize while unsafe is less useful than a plan to get safe first.

So here is what to carry forward. Pragmatics is a real system with mechanics, patterns, and names. It is not magic, it is not instinct, and it is not something that some people just have. You didn't fail to learn it; you weren't taught it, because the implicit channel most people learned through is narrower for autistic cognition, and the explicit channel was almost never offered. ## Deep Dive

### Pragmatics as a field

**Pragmatics** is the branch of linguistics that studies how context contributes to meaning. Its central question is: what do speakers mean, as distinct from what their words say?

The distinction can be stated precisely. **Semantics** deals with the meaning of sentences considered in the abstract, independent of who says them or when. "It's cold in here" has a fixed semantic content: the temperature in this location is low. **Pragmatics** deals with what a speaker is doing when they produce that sentence in a particular situation. "It's cold in here," said to the person nearest the window, with a small glance at the window, is usually a request to close the window. Semantics gives you the literal content; pragmatics gives you the action the content is being used to perform.

This is not a tidy boundary. Semantics and pragmatics shade into each other, and linguists disagree about where to draw the line. But the practical distinction is stable enough to be useful: if you have ever been confused by a sentence whose words you understood perfectly, you were probably encountering a pragmatic layer that the words alone did not specify.

### Historical foundations

Three figures built the foundations of modern pragmatics. The hub's category pages develop their work in depth; this section provides the orienting sketch.

**J.L. Austin (1911-1960)** was an Oxford philosopher who noticed that some sentences don't describe reality; they do things in it. "I promise to be there" doesn't report a promise; it makes one. "I now pronounce you married" doesn't describe a marriage; it creates one. Austin called these **performatives** and spent the 1955 William James Lectures at Harvard working out the consequences. The lectures were published posthumously as *How to Do Things with Words* (1962). Austin's central insight was that every utterance is an action (a **speech act**), not just a piece of information. This is the foundation of speech act theory, which Category 1 develops.

**John Searle (born 1932)** extended Austin's work into a systematic theory in *Speech Acts: An Essay in the Philosophy of Language* (1969) and subsequent papers. Searle gave us the distinction between **direct** speech acts (where the grammatical form matches the communicative function) and **indirect** speech acts (where they don't). "Can you pass the salt?" is a question in form and a request in function. Searle's account of how listeners resolve the mismatch, by reasoning about the speaker's intentions in context, remains the standard framework for indirect speech acts.

**H.P. Grice (1913-1988)** asked a different question: if so much of what we mean is not in what we say, how does anyone ever figure out what anyone else means? His answer, delivered in the 1967 William James Lectures (published as "Logic and Conversation" in 1975), was the **Cooperative Principle**: the assumption that speakers are generally trying to communicate sensibly. He specified the principle through four conversational **maxims** (roughly: be informative, be truthful, be relevant, be clear). When a speaker appears to violate a maxim, listeners don't conclude the speaker is broken; they conclude the speaker is implying something beyond the literal words. That inferred meaning is called a **conversational implicature**, and it is the engine that powers most everyday indirection. Grice's framework is the subject of the [Cooperative Principle](tier-1-cooperative-principle.md) Tier 1 page, and implicature is treated in depth in Category 2.

Later developments include **Brown and Levinson's politeness theory** (1987), which modeled how speakers manage each other's social standing ("face") through linguistic strategies; **Sperber and Wilson's Relevance Theory** (1986/1995), which replaced Grice's maxims with a single cognitive principle about relevance; and **Clark's common ground framework** (1996), which formalized the shared knowledge that speakers rely on. These are treated in Categories 3, 10/Tier 1, and 16 respectively.

### The four core subsystems

Most of the hub's early categories cluster around four subsystems. Category pages develop each one in full; this section provides first orientation.

**Speech acts (Category 1).** What an utterance is doing: requesting, refusing, promising, warning, apologizing, complimenting, asserting, questioning. The same sentence can perform different acts in different contexts, and the same act can be performed by many different sentences. The autistic-relevant insight: if you only track the words, you will miss the action.

**Implicature (Category 2).** What a speaker means beyond what they literally say. "It's getting late" at a dinner party is rarely a statement about time. "Some of the team agreed" usually implies that not all of them did. Implicature is Grice's invention and the single most common source of pragmatic mismatch between autistic and non-autistic speakers.

**Politeness (Category 3).** The system of linguistic strategies speakers use to manage each other's social standing. Brown and Levinson (1987) formalized this around the concept of **face**: the public self-image that every person wants to maintain. Indirect requests, hedges, apologies, compliments, and softened refusals are all politeness moves. Politeness is not about being "nice"; it is a machinery with specific moves and specific costs, and it can be read like a grammar.

**Conversation structure (Category 4).** The mechanics of how conversations are built: how turns are taken, how topics are opened and closed, how interruptions work, how repair happens when something goes wrong. Turn-taking is not the free-for-all it looks like from the outside. It is structured, rule-governed, and learnable.

The hub's other categories (discourse markers, hedging, register, deixis, presupposition, relevance, non-literal language, narrative, topic management, nonverbal pragmatics, prosody, common ground, questions, refusal and disagreement, phatic communication, digital pragmatics, specific contexts, social hierarchy, information structure, and logical fallacies) all build on these four.

### The reframe: teaching gap, not deficit

The standard story about autistic pragmatic difficulty holds that pragmatics is something typical children just absorb, and that failing to absorb it reflects a developmental fault. That story is wrong in two important ways.

First, typical children don't absorb pragmatics from thin air. They absorb it from thousands of hours of social feedback, most of which is calibrated to their specific neurocognitive profile. The adults around them are, without realizing it, teaching pragmatics by modeling it, reacting to it, and repairing breakdowns in ways that make sense to a typical child's attention and salience systems. That is instruction. It just doesn't look like instruction because the curriculum is invisible.

Second, when the learner's neurocognitive profile is different, the same environment doesn't deliver the same lesson. The social feedback is still happening, but it is less legible, less predictable, less attached to obvious cues, and more dependent on implicit cues that the autistic learner's attention may not have landed on. The implicit channel is narrower. And the explicit channel, actual spoken-aloud instruction in what pragmatic conventions mean and how to respond to them, is almost never offered, because the adults around the child don't know they are experts in a system with a name.

The result: autistic adults have often missed pragmatic content through no failure of theirs, because the only instruction offered was implicit and the implicit channel was narrower for them. The gap is real. It is not a deficit. It is a teaching gap. This hub is the explicit channel.

### The double empathy problem

The reframe extends further. It is not only that autistic people were under-taught a pragmatic system. It is that the system itself is one of several possible pragmatic systems, and autistic communication tends to run on a different one.

Milton (2012) named this the **double empathy problem**. Communication breakdowns between autistic and non-autistic people are bidirectional: both parties are operating with pragmatic expectations the other side doesn't share, both make inferences that turn out to be wrong, and both walk away feeling that something went off. The mismatch then gets charged to the autistic side by default. The autistic person is the one who "missed the tone," "took it wrong," "didn't read the room." The non-autistic person is rarely described as "failing to interpret an autistic communicator."

The evidence supports the bidirectional account. Crompton, Ropar, Evans-Williams, Flynn and Fletcher-Watson (2020) found that autistic-to-autistic information transfer in diffusion-chain experiments was about as effective as non-autistic-to-non-autistic transfer; the breakdown concentrated at the cross-neurotype interface. The problem is not one broken brain. It is two systems meeting, and only one getting blamed.

The double empathy problem is load-bearing for the whole hub. It is the reason the hub teaches pragmatics as a second language rather than as a corrective therapy. See the [Double Empathy Tier 1 page](tier-1-double-empathy.md) for the full evidence base.

Austin, J. L. (1962). *How to Do Things with Words*. Harvard University Press. [Founding text of speech act theory. Introduces the performative/constative distinction and then dismantles it.] [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/How-Things-Words-J-Austin/dp/0674411528)

Grice, H. P. (1975). "Logic and Conversation." In *Syntax and Semantics, Vol. 3*. Academic Press. [Introduces the Cooperative Principle, the conversational maxims, and conversational implicature.] [PDF](https://www.ucl.ac.uk/ls/studypacks/Grice-Logic.pdf)

Searle, J. R. (1969). *Speech Acts: An Essay in the Philosophy of Language*. Cambridge University Press. [Systematic development of Austin's framework. Source of the direct/indirect speech act distinction.] [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/Speech-Acts-Essay-Philosophy-Language/dp/052109626X)

Brown, P., & Levinson, S. (1987). *Politeness: Some Universals in Language Usage*. Cambridge University Press. [The face-based theory of politeness that underwrites Category 3.] [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/Politeness-Universals-Language-Interactional-Sociolinguistics/dp/0521313554)

**Autism-pragmatics research**

Milton, D. E. M. (2012). On the ontological status of autism: the 'double empathy problem'. *Disability & Society*, 27(6), 883–887. https://doi.org/10.1080/09687599.2012.710008

Crompton, C. J., Ropar, D., Evans-Williams, C. V. M., Flynn, E. G., & Fletcher-Watson, S. (2020). Autistic peer-to-peer information transfer is highly effective. *Autism*, 24(7), 1704–1712. https://doi.org/10.1177/1362361320919286

Cage, E., & Troxell-Whitman, Z. (2019). Understanding the reasons, contexts and costs of camouflaging for autistic adults. *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 49(5), 1899–1911. https://doi.org/10.1007/s10803-018-03878-x

Cassidy, S. A., Bradley, L., Shaw, R., & Baron-Cohen, S. (2020). Is camouflaging autistic traits associated with suicidal thoughts and behaviours? Expanding the interpersonal psychological theory of suicide in an undergraduate student sample. *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 50(10), 3638–3648. https://doi.org/10.1007/s10803-019-04323-3
