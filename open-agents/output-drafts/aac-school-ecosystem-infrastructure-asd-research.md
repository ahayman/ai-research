# AAC School Ecosystem & Infrastructure for ASD Students - Research

## Research Context
**Purpose:** Phase 2 research for a $4M government grant to develop an ASD-specific AAC app, focused on ecosystem and infrastructure needs for autistic students in school settings.
**Date:** March 2026
**Phase 1 Key Findings Referenced:** OpenAAC gave AAC ecosystem an overall GPA of 1.9 (C-minus) with Education getting an F; SLPs managing 50-80+ caseloads lack unified dashboards; zero IEP integration in any AAC app; only Proloquo Coach offers structured parent training; near-zero data portability; CoughDrop has the only mature cloud-based school dashboard.

---

## 1. AAC IN SCHOOLS FOR ASD STUDENTS

### 1.1 Scale of the Population

**Autism Prevalence (CDC ADDM Network, April 2025):**
- Autism prevalence has increased from 1 in 36 to **1 in 31 children** (3.2% of 8-year-olds)
- ASD is 3.4x more prevalent among boys (4.9%) than girls (1.4%)
- Black, Asian, and Hispanic children now have higher ASD prevalence (3.66%, 3.82%, 3.30% respectively) than White children (2.77%)
- Source: [CDC Community Report on Autism 2025](https://www.cdc.gov/autism/communication-resources/community-reports.html)

**Students Served Under IDEA:**
- 2023-2024: Approximately **7.9 million children** ages 3-21 received IDEA Part B services (15.9% of all public school students)
- Autism now accounts for **nearly 14% of school-age students with disabilities** (up from 13% in 2022-23)
- Approximately **980,000 individuals** ages 3-21 with autism covered by IDEA as of 2022-23
- Autism category accounted for **42.6% of the total increase** in students served under IDEA
- Gender: 20.4% female, 79.6% male among school-age students with autism
- Sources: [NCES COE](https://nces.ed.gov/programs/coe/indicator/cgg/students-with-disabilities), [Advocacy Institute](https://www.advocacyinstitute.org/blog/?p=1464), [Disability Scoop](https://www.disabilityscoop.com/2024/05/03/autism-accounts-for-growing-percentage-of-students-in-special-ed/30864/)

**Minimally Verbal / Nonspeaking Autistic Students:**
- **25-30% of autistic individuals** are minimally verbal or fail to develop functional speech
- **26.7% of 8-year-olds with autism** classified as having "profound autism" (including nonverbal/minimally verbal)
- Estimated AAC-eligible population in schools: ~245,000-294,000 students (25-30% of ~980,000)
- Only ~12% of preschoolers in special education actually use AAC, suggesting massive underserving
- Sources: [PMC - Role of AAC for Children with Autism](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5036660/), [Frontiers in Psychiatry 2025](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2025.1549092/full)

### 1.2 IDEA Requirements for AAC/AT in IEPs

**Federal Legal Mandate:**
- IDEA 2004 (and 2006 regulations) **requires IEP teams to consider assistive technology** for every child with a disability during IEP development
- AT device defined as "any item, piece of equipment, or product system... used to increase, maintain, or improve functional capabilities"
- AT services include: selection, designing, fitting, customizing, adapting devices; coordinating with other therapies; training for child, family, AND professionals
- **Cost cannot be used as an excuse** for denying AT devices or services
- Schools must provide AT "as soon as possible" when specified in IEP
- Source: [IDEA AT Guidance (ed.gov)](https://sites.ed.gov/idea/idea-files/at-guidance/), [Sec. 300.105](https://sites.ed.gov/idea/regs/b/b/300.105), [Wrightslaw AT](https://www.wrightslaw.com/info/atech.index.htm)

**AT Consideration vs. AT Evaluation:**
- AT consideration is a brief, dynamic process done at least annually during IEP development
- AT evaluation is a more detailed assessment triggered when consideration identifies a potential need
- An AT evaluation can be requested at any time if it doesn't happen initially
- NYC example: Schools must contact family for consent within 15 days; evaluation must be completed within 60 calendar days
- Source: [NYC DOE AT](https://www.schools.nyc.gov/learning/special-education/supports-and-services/assistive-technology), [ASNAT Process](https://www.wati.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/Ch1-ATAssessment.pdf)

**The SETT Framework:**
- Most common method for AT needs evaluation: **Student, Environment, Tasks, Tools**
- Promotes collaborative decision-making across all phases
- Premise: Identify student characteristics, learning environments, and required tasks BEFORE trying to identify tools
- Used for consideration through implementation and evaluation of effectiveness
- Source: [CEC SETT Framework](https://exceptionalchildren.org/blog/sett-framework-and-evaluating-assistive-technology-remotely)

**QIAT Quality Indicators:**
- Nationwide grassroots framework: Quality Indicators for Assistive Technology Services
- Covers 8 areas including consideration, assessment, implementation, and evaluation
- Self-assessment matrices allow districts to benchmark against ideal practices
- Input gathered from 4,000+ individuals (students, families, staff, policymakers)
- Source: [QIAT](https://qiat.org/)

### 1.3 IEP Process for AAC Evaluation and Provisioning

**Evaluation Process Steps:**
1. Referral/problem definition (by parent, teacher, or any IEP team member)
2. Assessment using SETT framework or equivalent
3. Device trialing (typically 6-week loan periods)
4. Data collection during trial
5. IEP team collaborative decision on AT plan
6. Implementation with training
7. Ongoing evaluation through data analysis
- Source: [UC Denver AT Assessment](https://www.ucdenver.edu/centers/center-for-inclusive-design-and-engineering/community-engagement/assistive-tech-in-k-12/resources/assistive-technology-assessment)

**Critical Gap - Wait Times:**
- Evaluation timelines vary dramatically by district
- Some states offer free statewide AT/AAC evaluation services (e.g., Illinois)
- High demand creates waiting lists in many districts
- Device trials add 6+ weeks to the process
- Total time from referral to device in hand can be 3-6+ months

**Funding Pathways:**
- IDEA funds (school district responsibility)
- Medicaid/EPSDT (payer of last resort; requires SLP CCC report + physician referral + prior approval)
- Private insurance (must be tried before Medicaid)
- Schools **cannot compel** families to use private insurance
- Schools **cannot delay access** pending approval from other funding sources
- Medicaid pays **$4-8 billion annually** for school-based services (4th largest federal funding stream for districts)
- Sources: [TACA Funding](https://tacanow.org/family-resources/obtaining-funding-for-an-aac-device/), [ASHA Medicaid](https://www.asha.org/practice/reimbursement/medicaid/thirdparty-payment/), [ED Medicaid Guidance](https://www.ed.gov/sites/ed/files/about/offices/list/osers/docs/medicaid-funding-for-school-based-services-03-08-2024.pdf)

### 1.4 School District AAC Procurement

**Current State:**
- Districts use RFP processes with accessibility rubrics for digital content procurement
- Four of eight districts in the GAO study had dedicated AT teams; others relied on ad hoc approaches
- Apple Volume Purchase Program (VPP) used for bulk app deployment
- MDM (Mobile Device Management) solutions like Jamf used for device management
- Source: [GAO-26-107506](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-26-107506)

**AAC App Pricing (Individual):**
- Proloquo2Go: $249
- TouchChat HD: $149.99 (with WordPower: $299.99)
- LAMP Words for Life: $299.00
- Proloquo (new version): Free download, license-based pricing (student-centered license)
- CoughDrop: Subscription-based, organization tiers available
- Source: [AssistiveWare Proloquo School Licenses](https://www.assistiveware.com/products/proloquo-school-licenses)

**School Licensing Models:**
- Proloquo: Student-centered licenses (install on multiple devices per student; duration starts at assignment, not purchase)
- CoughDrop: Organization accounts with centralized management
- Most other apps: Per-device licensing through Apple VPP
- **Key gap:** No standardized school licensing model across the AAC market

### 1.5 AT Specialist vs. SLP Roles in Schools

**AT Specialist:**
- Performs assessments, provides training for students needing AT
- Trains staff and family members in use/maintenance
- Makes device purchase recommendations
- Manages inventory and provides technical support
- Most commonly starts as special ed teacher, SLP, or OT with additional AT expertise
- Source: [ASHA AT in Schools](https://www.asha.org/practice/assistive-technology-in-schools/), [AAcessible](https://www.aaccessible.org/post/how-to-hire-an-aac-at-specialist)

**SLP:**
- Identifies, diagnoses, and provides therapy for communication disorders
- Conducts evaluations and manages IEP documentation
- Works one-on-one or in groups
- **75% of school-based SLPs now serve students in AAC area** (up from 50% in 2006)
- Median caseload: **50 students** (median manageable: 40)
- Less than 60% of work week devoted to direct intervention
- Source: [ASHA 2024 Schools Survey](https://www.asha.org/siteassets/surveys/2024-schools-survey-slp-caseload.pdf)

**Critical Staffing Gap:**
- Only **4 of 8 districts** in the GAO study had dedicated AT teams
- Staff from "nearly all" districts cited staffing shortages as a barrier to AT provision
- AT specialist is NOT a widely-available dedicated role in most districts
- SLPs carry AT responsibility on top of already-overwhelming caseloads
- Source: [GAO-26-107506](https://files.gao.gov/reports/GAO-26-107506/index.html)

### 1.6 Shared Device Scenarios

**iPad Management in Schools:**
- Apple Shared iPad allows multiple users on one device via Managed Apple Accounts + MDM
- Jamf School supports Class Based Shared iPad with auto-logout, per-student storage allocation, profile assignment
- Best practice: Plan for users to return to same device (intelligent caching)
- AAC devices present unique challenges vs. general shared iPads: vocabulary is highly personal
- Source: [Apple Shared iPad](https://support.apple.com/guide/deployment-education/shared-ipad-edua714f0cdx/web), [Jamf](https://www.jamf.com/blog/what-are-shared-ipads-in-education/)

**AAC-Specific Shared Device Problems:**
- AAC vocabulary/layout is deeply personalized; sharing disrupts this
- Personal voice settings, motor access configurations not easily switchable
- Student communication data mixed on shared devices
- No standardized quick-switch user profile system in AAC apps
- IDEA requires AT to be available across settings, but shared devices may limit this

## 2. SCHOOL-HOME COORDINATION FOR ASD

### 2.1 Current Coordination Methods

**Communication Logs:**
- Most school-home communication relies on paper-based daily logs
- Templates available on Teachers Pay Teachers and similar platforms (picture-form, words-only, fill-in-the-blank)
- Communication Matrix used to compare home vs. school communication levels
- Discrepancies between home/school matrices inform coaching decisions
- **No AAC app natively integrates with school-home communication systems**
- Sources: [A Day in Our Shoes - Communication Logs](https://adayinourshoes.com/daily-home-to-school-communication-log-templates/), [TPT - AAC Report](https://www.teacherspayteachers.com/browse?search=aac+report)

**Progress Sharing:**
- IEP progress reports (typically quarterly)
- Parent-teacher conferences
- Informal notes in backpacks
- CoughDrop is the only AAC app with built-in messaging between supervisors/families
- No standardized vocabulary usage reports shared between school and home

### 2.2 School-to-Home Transition Breakdowns

**Key Research Findings:**
- Parents emphasize need to adapt service delivery to different settings
- Communication partner knowledge and adult-family input are critical mediators of outcomes
- Teachers acknowledge AAC importance but report: insufficient education, time constraints, lack of confidence, difficulty motivating students
- Research highlights "a need for more services supporting children with complex communication deficits in different contexts"
- Sources: [PMC - Parents' Perceptions of AAC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9266194/), [PMC - Teachers' Responsive Strategies](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11475093/)

**What Breaks Down:**
1. **Vocabulary mismatch:** School vocabulary not available at home and vice versa
2. **Device availability:** Devices left at school, or school-owned devices cannot go home
3. **Training gap:** Parents receive minimal training on AAC use/modeling
4. **Communication partner differences:** Different adults use different interaction strategies
5. **Environmental differences:** AAC boards/vocabulary specific to school contexts
6. **Data siloes:** No shared view of child's communication progress across settings

### 2.3 Parent Training

**Current State:**
- ASHA recommends parent training include: reviewing AAC system, core vs. fringe vocabulary, simple programming, sentence structure
- Training conducted at home shows highest success levels
- Only **Proloquo Coach** offers structured parent training in any AAC app (as identified in Phase 1)
- Most parent training is informal, brief, and delivered at IEP meetings or device handoff

**10 Strategies for Parent Carryover (ASHA Leader):**
- Target functional core words usable at home and school
- Interview staff and family to identify high-value vocabulary
- Provide visual supports for modeling
- Schedule regular check-ins between SLP and family
- Source: [ASHA Leader - 10 Strategies](https://leader.pubs.asha.org/do/10.1044/10-strategies-to-train-parents-and-improve-carryover-for-students-using-aac/full/)

**Research Concern on Core Vocabulary:**
- A 2023 systematic review found "no empirical evidence to support AAC intervention using a core vocabulary approach"
- Researchers cautioned against overemphasis on core vocabulary, noting word frequency counts are "an insufficient basis for selecting vocabulary for aided preliterate communicators"
- Source: [Tandfonline - External Challenges for AAC](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/07434618.2025.2508485)

### 2.4 AAC Consistency Across Environments

**Research on Cross-Environment Use:**
- Coordination among service providers identified as critical for successful AAC implementation
- Multiple environments significantly impact communication for aided AAC users
- By considering communication partners across settings, AAC systems more likely to be maintained over time
- Children use AAC devices more in **high-structure, high-directiveness** contexts (more adult prompting)
- Average AAC use in school: only **5-6 communication acts per typical school day** (alarmingly low)
- Source: [Tandfonline - Classroom Context Influence](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/17549507.2023.2220992)

### 2.5 "Beyond Train and Hope" - Carryover Research

- Traditional model is "train and hope" - provide device, hope it gets used
- Evidence-based carryover requires: explicit instruction across settings, multiple communication partners, environmental arrangement, and ongoing coaching
- Collaborative approach including family, peers, and service providers is essential
- Source: [PrAACtical AAC - Beyond Train and Hope](https://praacticalaac.org/praactical/beyond-train-and-hope-5-ways-to-support-carryover-of-skills-by-aac-learners/)

## 3. ASD-SPECIFIC SCHOOL INFRASTRUCTURE NEEDS

### 3.1 Visual Schedule Integration

**Current State:**
- Visual schedules are FOUNDATIONAL tools for autistic students
- Used to break down the day into pictorial representations (images, icons, written words)
- Range from simple charts posted in classroom to digital versions on tablets
- Help with: anxiety reduction, executive functioning practice, independence, transition preparation
- Transitions between activities are particularly challenging; visual schedules provide advance warning
- Sources: [AutismClassroom.com](https://www.autismclassroom.com/strategies/visual-supports), [Cornerstone Autism Center](https://cornerstoneautismcenter.com/the-importance-of-visual-schedules-for-students-with-autism/)

**AAC Integration Gap:**
- Visual schedules and AAC systems exist as **completely separate tools**
- No AAC app includes built-in visual scheduling
- Students may have a visual schedule on the wall AND an AAC device - disconnected systems
- Whether using PECS, low-tech, or high-tech AAC, visuals are necessary but siloed
- **Opportunity:** Integrated visual schedule + AAC device could reduce cognitive load and tool switching

### 3.2 Behavior Support Integration

**Communication-Behavior Link:**
- Lack of functional communication is one of the biggest drivers of behavior
- When communication improves, challenging behavior often decreases
- Meltdowns and shutdowns frequently stem from inability to communicate frustration/unmet needs
- AAC provision has been shown to reduce interfering behaviors in psychiatric inpatient settings
- Sources: [ASHA - AAC and Problem Behavior](https://pubs.asha.org/doi/abs/10.1044/aac16.2.26), [ASHA - AAC and Psychiatric Inpatients](https://pubs.asha.org/doi/10.1044/2023_AJSLP-23-00019)

**ABC Data Collection:**
- Standard behavioral documentation uses Antecedent-Behavior-Consequence (ABC) charts
- Tracks: date, time, activity, antecedent, behavior, consequence
- Paper-based ABC data sheets are still the dominant tool
- **No integration between ABC behavioral data and AAC usage data**
- Communication attempts before/during behavioral episodes are rarely correlated with AAC data

**Burnout, Inertia, Meltdown, Shutdown (BIMS):**
- Autistic people identify BIMS as important parts of daily life
- ABC data sheets used to track distress patterns
- Communication needs may be unmet, prompting use of "interfering behaviors" as communication
- Source: [PMC - Insights on BIMS](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8595127/)

### 3.3 Sensory Environment Considerations

**Sensory Challenges with AAC Interfaces:**
- Sensory overload can surpass processing ability, causing stress, anxiety, and communication breakdown
- Poorly customized AAC devices can cause sensory overwhelm and physical discomfort, leading to abandonment
- Bright primary colors can be intensely stimulating and cause discomfort
- Sources: [Autism Speaks - Sensory Issues](https://www.autismspeaks.org/sensory-issues), [ASHA Leader - Sensory Strategies](https://leader.pubs.asha.org/doi/10.1044/leader.FTR2.24042019.56)

**Design Principles for ASD:**
- Muted or pastel tones are more tolerable and calming
- No more than 2-3 colors per page to minimize distraction
- AAC devices should offer multiple color themes/schemes
- Consider age, developmental level, AND sensory needs
- Reduce background noise and visual clutter
- **No current AAC app offers a "sensory-friendly mode" or "calming mode"**

### 3.4 Classroom-Specific Vocabulary Management

**Core vs. Fringe Vocabulary:**
- Core vocabulary: Most frequent words (verbs, pronouns, descriptors, prepositions); 80% of daily language
- Fringe vocabulary: Topic/environment/individual-specific words; 20% of daily language
- Each AAC user should have a core board that travels with them
- Each environment should have a group communication board with context-specific fringe vocabulary
- Sources: [The Autism Helper - Core Vocabulary](https://theautismhelper.com/including-core-vocabulary-when-teaching-and-using-aac/), [PrAACtical AAC - Fringe Vocabulary](https://praacticalaac.org/praactical/fringe-vocabulary-how-to-select-and-not-neglect/)

**Classroom Implementation:**
- Post large core board in classroom
- Use core board during small group instruction
- SLPs coach teachers to use AAC device and core vocabulary
- Environment-specific boards for: science lab, PE, art, music, cafeteria, playground, library
- **No AAC app offers automatic context-switching of vocabulary** (e.g., detecting classroom vs. playground)

### 3.5 Transition Support

**Grade-to-Grade, School-to-School:**
- IDEA mandates transition planning starting before age 16
- AAC device settings, vocabulary, and communication history should transfer
- **Zero data portability** between AAC apps when switching systems
- OpenAAC's OBF (Open Board Format) is the only interoperability standard, but adoption is minimal
- CoughDrop initiated interoperability initiative; offers conversion tools for some formats
- Sources: [OpenBoards Format](https://www.openboardformat.org/), [OpenAAC](https://www.openaac.org/)

**School-to-Post-Secondary:**
- Post-school outcomes are poorest for autistic young adults (lowest employment rates)
- AAC devices given by Department of Education placed on student's IEP
- When student exits school, device ownership and data transition are unclear
- No standardized process for transferring AAC device data and settings to adult services
- Source: [AAP - IEPs and Transition Planning](https://publications.aap.org/pediatrics/article/152/1/e2022060199/191813/Individualized-Education-Programs-and-Transition)

### 3.6 AAC and Academic Curriculum Integration

**Research Findings (2025 Scoping Review):**
- Curricular integration understood as aligning AAC objectives with instructional methods
- **42% of studies fail to establish a curricular link** between AAC and classroom instruction
- AAC implementation often aligns with individualized plans rather than classroom instruction
- When teachers incorporate communication skills into curricular design, autistic students' participation improves
- Source: [PMC - Curricular Integration Scoping Review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12417616/)

**Implementation Strategies:**
- Distribute AAC equipment, provide foundational training
- Develop systematic adoption plan with practice-based coaching
- Alter personnel obligations to provide protected time
- Connect with AAC professionals
- Use voice output devices with key vocabulary overlays during classroom discussions

**Special Education vs. Inclusive Classroom:**
- In inclusive settings: AAC devices feature overlays of vocabulary words paired with pictures; peers encouraged to use during discussions
- Special education: More intensive, individualized AAC programming
- Both require trained staff who model AAC use
- Inclusion shown to lead to academic gains across multiple measures

## 4. DATA COLLECTION FOR ASD IEPs

### 4.1 What Data Schools Need for AAC Goals

**Measurable IEP Goal Examples:**
- "Student will verbally or using AAC, express basic needs and wants in 4 out of 5 instances"
- "Student will use AAC to initiate conversation with peers 3 times per day across 3 consecutive weeks"
- Goals must follow SMART framework: Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Relevant, Time-bound
- Must include unambiguous method of measurement and overtly stated criteria
- Sources: [AssistiveWare - IEP Goals for AAC](https://www.assistiveware.com/blog/iep-goals-aac), [A Day in Our Shoes - AT Goals](https://adayinourshoes.com/assistive-technology/)

**Data Types Needed:**
- Frequency counts (how often AAC is used)
- Accuracy measures (correct symbol selection)
- Communication functions (requesting, commenting, refusing, asking questions)
- Communication partner data (who the student communicates with)
- Context data (where/when communication occurs)
- Vocabulary growth tracking
- Unprompted vs. prompted use
- Generalization across settings

### 4.2 Current Data Collection Tools

**Paper-Based (Still Dominant):**
- Monthly tally sheets with staff notes
- Data responsibility charts (weekly calendar of who collects data on whom)
- ABC data sheets for behavior
- Communication sampling worksheets
- **Free or low cost, minimal setup, no tech barriers**

**Digital Tools Emerging:**
- **SLP Toolkit** (free web app): Present levels, progress monitoring, daily data collection
- **Kit for Teams** (by SLP Toolkit): IEP goal tracking, scheduling, caseload management for special ed teams
- **AbleSpace**: IEP data collection app with 10+ data types, auto-generated reports/graphs, Medicaid billing integration
- **Brolly**: Special education data collection
- Source: [SLP Toolkit](https://www.slptoolkit.com/), [AbleSpace](https://www.ablespace.io/)

**Critical Gap - AAC-Specific Data Collection:**
- AAC apps collect usage logs internally but **do not export to IEP data tracking systems**
- SLPs manually transcribe AAC usage observations onto paper or separate digital tools
- No integration between AAC usage data and IEP progress monitoring
- CoughDrop's reporting tools are the closest to actionable data, but still siloed from IEP systems
- **Duplicate data entry is the norm**: SLP observes AAC use, records in AAC app notes, re-enters in IEP system

### 4.3 Measuring AAC Progress for ASD Students

**Evidence-Based Practices:**
- National Professional Development Center on ASD identified 27 evidence-based practices
- Communication goals should address range of methods: verbal, AAC, nonverbal cues
- SMART framework widely recommended but inconsistently applied
- Functional communication goals should replace challenging behavior with communication strategies
- Various team members can support data collection (SLPs, teachers, paras)
- Sources: [VUMC - Data Collection Tools](https://vkc.vumc.org/assets/files/triad/tips/Progress_Monitoring_Tools_for_Autistic_Learners_Data_Collection_Overview.pdf), [Watson Institute - Data Sheets](https://www.thewatsoninstitute.org/resource/data-sheets/)

**Implementation Fidelity Problem:**
- School-based interventions for autism implemented at **only 25-50% of recommended fidelity**
- "Research to practice" gap identified since 1997 but factors still poorly understood
- Source: [ScienceDirect - Barriers and Facilitators](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1750946720301070)

### 4.4 Insurance vs. School Funding Documentation

**School (IDEA) Documentation:**
- "Educationally necessary" standard
- IEP team determination
- Must demonstrate student needs AT to access FAPE in LRE
- Annual review minimum
- District responsibility for cost

**Medicaid Documentation:**
- SLP with CCC (Certificate of Clinical Competence) required
- Physician referral required
- "Medically necessary" standard
- Prior approval before device delivery
- EPSDT covers evaluation, selection, fitting, and training for all eligible children
- Documentation must support reimbursement claims with detailed session notes

**State Variation:**
- Coverage policies vary significantly state to state
- Some states (e.g., Wisconsin, Washington) have detailed billing manuals
- School-based Medicaid reimbursement totals **$4-8 billion annually** nationally
- Texas SHARS program alone: $607 million before recent cuts
- Sources: [ASHA - Medicaid](https://www.asha.org/practice/reimbursement/medicaid/medicaid_intro/), [MACPAC Report](https://www.macpac.gov/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/School-Based-Services-for-Students-Enrolled-in-Medicaid.pdf)

## 5. TEACHER/PARAPROFESSIONAL TRAINING

### 5.1 Who Supports AAC in Classrooms

**Staffing Hierarchy:**
- **SLP**: Primary AAC expert, conducts assessments, writes IEP goals, provides therapy
- **AT Specialist** (if available): Device selection, configuration, inventory management
- **Special Education Teacher**: Daily AAC integration, data collection coaching
- **Paraprofessional/Aide**: Most frequent communication partner, prompts AAC use throughout day
- **General Education Teacher** (inclusive settings): Uses AAC during instruction
- **Peers**: Can be trained as communication partners

**Reality Check:**
- SLPs see students for limited sessions; paras are with students all day
- Paras are the **most frequent implementers** of AAC in classrooms
- Most paraprofessional training occurs on-the-job from supervising teachers
- Little training specific to ASD given to prepare paras for their roles
- IEP meetings often occur **without paraprofessional involvement**
- Sources: [PMC - Training Paraprofessionals](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4134413/), [Autism Speaks - Paraprofessionals](https://www.autismspeaks.org/tool-kit-excerpt/autism-paraprofessionals)

### 5.2 The Staffing Crisis

**Scale of the Problem (2024-2025):**
- **45 states** report teacher shortages in special education
- **39 states + DC** have shortage of special education teachers
- NYC alone: **2,200+ special education vacancies** across 474 schools, including **1,558 paraprofessional vacancies**
- IDEA enrollment increased from 6.4M (2012-13) to 7.5M (2022-23) while staffing hasn't kept pace
- **78% of SLPs** identify more job openings than job seekers
- Higher caseloads correlate with plans to leave: SLPs with median caseload of 59.7 planned to leave positions
- Sources: [Organization for Autism Research](https://researchautism.org/oaracle-newsletter/special-education-faces-teacher-shortages/), [K-12 Dive](https://www.k12dive.com/news/teacher-shortages-hinder-special-education-progress-what-are-the-solutions-IDEA-50-years/806220/), [edWeb 2024 Survey](https://home.edweb.net/2024staffingsurvey/)

**Exit Interview Data:**
- Paras leave because they lack skills AND access to training
- Teachers leave over higher workloads, burnout, lack of resources
- Constant turnover means constantly retraining new staff on AAC
- Source: [Chalkbeat](https://www.chalkbeat.org/2024/11/20/special-education-teaching-shortages-hurt-students-and-are-hard-to-solve/)

### 5.3 Training Gaps

**For SLPs:**
- Universities rarely require AAC coursework
- Gap between theory and practical AAC application
- Many clinicians lack formal AAC training during graduate education
- 75% now serve AAC students but training hasn't scaled proportionally
- Source: [OpenAAC GPA Report](https://www.openaac.org/2025/03/17/aac-ecosystem-gpa.html)

**For Teachers:**
- Limited knowledge about AAC devices
- Often think only of high-tech devices; may not consider low-tech solutions
- Myths about AT giving "unfair advantage" persist
- Rapidly changing technology makes it difficult to stay current
- Source: [GAO-26-107506](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-26-107506)

**For Paraprofessionals:**
- Research has NOT shown whether paras can effectively deliver functional communication training
- Special education teachers CAN serve as coaches for paras (found effective and feasible)
- Para training needs are unique in under-resourced schools given burnout/turnover prevalence
- Source: [Organization for Autism Research - Paras and AAC](https://researchautism.org/oaracle-newsletter/can-paraprofessionals-provide-training-to-students-who-use-aac/)

**Special Educator Perspective (PMC Study):**
- 14 special education teachers interviewed about AAC services
- Challenges identified: inadequate training, limited preparation time, inconsistent team support
- Lack of comprehensive assessment, inconsistent AAC implementation across team members
- Lack of coordination between teachers and professionals
- Source: [PMC - AAC Services in Schools: Special Educator Perspective](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8115610/)

### 5.4 AAC Device Maintenance in Classrooms

**Who Does It:**
- Often falls to whoever is willing/available
- SLPs update vocabulary when they visit (may be weekly)
- Teachers/paras expected to charge devices, keep clean, ensure availability
- No formal maintenance protocol in most districts

**What Goes Wrong:**
- Devices left in backpacks, unused
- Devices taken away as punishment (documented in OpenAAC report; legally considered removing communication rights)
- Battery dies, no charging routine established
- Vocabulary not updated to match current classroom topics
- Broken devices go unreported or unreplaced for extended periods
- Source: [OpenAAC GPA Report](https://www.openaac.org/2025/03/17/aac-ecosystem-gpa.html), [Autistic Self Advocacy Network](https://autisticadvocacy.org/actioncenter/issues/school/voice/)

## 6. ASD-SPECIFIC SCHOOL OPPORTUNITIES

### 6.1 The AAC Abandonment Crisis

**Scale:**
- **30-50% of AAC users abandon or under-use their devices**
- Environmental factors (professional/family/society attitudes and supports) are the primary barrier
- Personal factors (user attitude, SES, culture) are secondary
- Poorly customized devices causing sensory overwhelm/discomfort leads to abandonment
- Lack of inter-stakeholder communication exacerbates problems
- Sources: [PMC - Barriers in AAC Use](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11197385/), [Tandfonline - Rethinking Device Abandonment](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/07434618.2023.2199859), [ScholarWorks - Preventing Abandonment](https://scholarworks.uni.edu/ijc/vol54/iss1/7/)

**Actual Usage in Schools:**
- Teachers report children use formal AAC systems to communicate **only 5-6 times per typical school day**
- Children communicate more in structured/directive contexts (more adult prompting)
- Children communicate less in low-structure contexts (playground, unstructured time)
- Source: [Tandfonline - Classroom Context Influence](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/17549507.2023.2220992)

### 6.2 What an Ideal AAC School Ecosystem Would Look Like

**Based on Research Synthesis and GAO/OpenAAC Findings:**

1. **Unified SLP Dashboard**: One interface to manage 50-80+ students, track AAC usage, generate IEP reports, coordinate with families
2. **IEP Integration**: AAC usage data automatically populates IEP progress reports; goals trackable within the AAC system
3. **School-Home Bridge**: Shared vocabulary views, usage reports, coaching modules accessible to families, messaging between team members
4. **Visual Schedule Integration**: AAC device includes visual scheduling for transitions, reducing tool proliferation
5. **Behavioral Data Correlation**: AAC usage data linked to behavioral episode documentation (ABC data)
6. **Sensory-Adaptive Interface**: Calming mode, color theme options, reduced stimulation settings for sensory-overwhelmed students
7. **Context-Aware Vocabulary**: Automatic vocabulary suggestions based on class schedule, location, activity
8. **Data Portability**: OBF standard or better; one-click export/import of all user data, vocabulary, settings, communication history
9. **Multi-Student Device Management**: Quick-switch profiles for shared devices, per-student data isolation
10. **Peer Communication Support**: Tools for facilitating AAC user-peer interaction, peer training modules
11. **Transition Package**: Complete data/settings export for grade transitions, school changes, and post-secondary handoff
12. **Training Ecosystem**: Built-in training for SLPs, teachers, paras, and families with role-specific modules
13. **Medicaid/Billing Documentation**: Automated session documentation meeting Medicaid billing requirements

### 6.3 LMS Integration Opportunities

**Current EdTech Landscape:**
- Google Classroom: Most widely used in K-12
- Canvas (Instructure): Growing adoption, especially secondary
- PowerSchool Schoology: Common in districts
- Nearly 20% of districts use Google Classroom plus another LMS
- Google Workspace LTI tools compatible with Canvas and Schoology
- Source: [Google Education LTI](https://edu.google.com/workspace-lti/), [ListEdTech](https://www.listedtech.com/blog/k12-dual-lms-adoption/)

**AAC-LMS Integration Gap:**
- **Zero AAC apps integrate with any LMS**
- No way for AAC usage data to appear in student learning management systems
- No way for teachers to assign communication activities through LMS
- No way for curriculum vocabulary to auto-populate from LMS content
- **Massive opportunity** for differentiated instruction integration

### 6.4 Peer Communication Facilitation

**Research Base:**
- Peer-mediated interventions are recommended best practice based on 30+ years of research
- Peers can be taught to be responsive AAC communication partners at young ages with high fidelity
- Peer training typically done first, then structured interaction with AAC users
- Source: [PMC - Peer-Mediated AAC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3878449/), [PMC - Peer Mediation at Recess](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4500175/)

**Unstructured Time (Playground/Recess):**
- School playground provides ideal opportunity for social inclusion
- But autistic children often struggle in unstructured environments and may spend recess alone
- Children without disabilities tend to interact with each other, leaving children with disabilities out
- AAC Playground Communication Boards being installed in some communities (e.g., Malden, MA)
- Recess is ideal inclusive context for practicing social/communication skills
- Sources: [Mass.gov - Playground Communication Boards](https://www.mass.gov/news/malden-disability-commission-installs-playground-communication-boards), [PMC - Recess Intervention](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4547561/)

### 6.5 Behavioral Episode Documentation

**Current Process:**
- ABC (Antecedent-Behavior-Consequence) charting done on paper
- Events sequenced with timestamps
- Communication attempts before/during episodes rarely documented systematically
- No correlation between AAC data logs and behavioral incident reports

**Opportunity:**
- AAC app could auto-log communication attempts before, during, and after behavioral episodes
- Time-stamped AAC data could be correlated with behavioral incident reports
- Pattern recognition: "Student used AAC to request break 3 times in the 10 minutes before meltdown"
- This data would be invaluable for FBA (Functional Behavior Assessment) and BIP (Behavior Intervention Plans)

### 6.6 Future AAC Technology Vision

**Research Priorities (2025):**
- Intuitiveness, feasibility, sustainability
- Solutions for those who benefit least from existing technologies
- Affordability and parallel advocacy for expanded funding
- AAC that is wearable or embedded into the environment
- AAC that is more connected to user AND environment
- AAC that leverages mainstream technology advances
- Innovation must be driven by people who use the technology
- Source: [Tandfonline - Future of AAC Technologies](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/07434618.2025.2513906)

### 7.4 Unmet Needs Summary (Grant Justification)

1. **No unified SLP dashboard** for multi-student AAC management across any app except CoughDrop (limited)
2. **Zero IEP integration** in any AAC product - data flows require manual re-entry
3. **Zero LMS integration** - AAC is completely disconnected from educational technology
4. **No school-home communication bridge** built into AAC apps (except CoughDrop messaging)
5. **No visual schedule integration** with AAC - two critical autism tools remain siloed
6. **No behavioral data correlation** between AAC usage and behavioral episodes
7. **No sensory-adaptive interface** in any AAC app (calming modes, reduced stimulation)
8. **Near-zero data portability** between AAC systems (OBF exists but barely adopted)
9. **No automated IEP/Medicaid documentation** from AAC usage data
10. **No context-aware vocabulary** that adapts to classroom setting/activity
11. **No peer communication facilitation tools** built into AAC
12. **No structured training ecosystem** for all stakeholders (SLPs, teachers, paras, families) except Proloquo Coach for parents
13. **No transition data package** for grade, school, or post-secondary transitions
