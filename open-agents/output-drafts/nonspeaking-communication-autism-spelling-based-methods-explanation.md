# Spelling-Based Methods: How They Work and What the Evidence Shows

## Overview

Spelling-based communication methods -- the Rapid Prompting Method (RPM), Spelling to Communicate (S2C), and various letterboard approaches -- occupy the most fiercely contested ground in the landscape of non-speaking autism communication. These methods claim to enable non-speaking autistic individuals to communicate by pointing to letters on a board held by a trained communication partner. Proponents describe a population of non-speaking autistic people who possess age-appropriate or above-average literacy, intelligence, and complex inner lives accessible through spelling. Critics argue that these methods are variants of facilitated communication (FC) under new names, carrying the same risks of facilitator influence and the same absence of controlled evidence for communicator authorship.

**What are spelling-based methods?**
Communication approaches in which a non-speaking individual points to letters on a board (letterboard) or device while a trained partner holds the board and provides verbal prompts, pacing cues, and motor support. The resulting letter sequences are interpreted as messages authored by the individual.

**Why are they controversial?**
After more than 30 years of RPM and nearly a decade of S2C, no controlled authorship study has ever been published for either method. A 2019 systematic review found zero experimental studies of sufficient rigor to include. ASHA has explicitly warned against both methods. The fundamental question -- is the individual or the facilitator determining which letters are selected? -- remains unanswered by controlled evidence. Proponents argue the methods are distinct from FC and that the scientific establishment has failed to study them appropriately. Critics argue that the absence of controlled evidence after decades of claims is itself the most telling data point.

**Key takeaways:**
- No controlled authorship study of RPM or S2C has ever been published
- A 2019 systematic review found zero qualifying studies ("the empty review")
- ASHA has explicitly warned against both RPM and S2C
- The Jaswal 2020 and 2024 studies are the most cited research by proponents; both have received substantive published critiques
- Proponents and critics interpret the same observable facts in fundamentally different ways
- The transition from supported to independent communication is the strongest potential evidence -- but has never been systematically documented with controlled verification

## The Methods

### Rapid Prompting Method (RPM)

RPM was developed by Soma Mukhopadhyay around 1991 in India to teach her son Tito Rajarshi Mukhopadhyay, who was diagnosed with severe autism. Mukhopadhyay holds a master's degree in chemistry and a bachelor's in education. She moved to the United States and established HALO (Helping Autism through Learning and Outreach) in Austin, Texas, where she serves as Executive Director of Education.

RPM uses a "Teach-Ask" paradigm. The instructor rapidly presents academic content through multiple sensory channels simultaneously -- reading aloud, writing, drawing, using physical manipulatives -- and then immediately asks questions that the learner answers by pointing to letters on a letterboard held in the air by the instructor.

The pace is deliberately rapid, described by proponents as keeping "the learner's attention channel open." The instructor provides intensive verbal, auditory, visual, and tactile prompts throughout the session. The letterboard is held in the air (not on a table), with the instructor controlling its position, angle, height, and distance.

**Tito Mukhopadhyay**, the original RPM student (born 1989), has published multiple books including "The Mind Tree" (2003) and "How Can I Talk If My Lips Don't Move?" (2011). He uses handwriting as his preferred communication mode. His case is cited by proponents as evidence that RPM can enable complex communication. His communication has not been subjected to formal controlled authorship testing. His Wikipedia article notes that Soma Mukhopadhyay "was involved with subsequent facilitated communication interventions."

### Spelling to Communicate (S2C)

S2C was developed by Elizabeth Vosseller, an SLP, beginning around 2013, with formalized protocols published in 2015. Vosseller founded I-ASC (International Association for Spelling as Communication) in 2019 to promote the method and train practitioners.

S2C describes a progression from stencil boards (large targets requiring full arm movement) to laminated letterboards to keyboards. The communication partner holds the board and provides verbal prompts and direction. S2C uses the term "Communication Regulation Partner" (CRP) rather than "facilitator."

S2C claims several distinctions from classic FC: no hand-over-hand contact (the board is held, not the person's hand), progressive motor planning skill development, published protocols, and a goal of independence.

Critics argue these distinctions are superficial. The facilitator still controls the communication display (holding the board determines what the individual can point to and from what angle), provides high levels of verbal prompting, and makes interpretive decisions about ambiguous pointing. Without controlled testing, the claim that these differences eliminate facilitator influence is unfalsifiable.

## How a Session Works

Understanding the observable process is essential for evaluating the debate.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-2-session-process -->

**Step 1: Content presentation.** The instructor presents academic content -- reading aloud, drawing diagrams, explaining concepts. The instructor determines what content to present and how to frame it.

**Step 2: Question posed.** The instructor asks a question about the content. The instructor knows the expected answer.

**Step 3: Letterboard held up.** The instructor holds the letterboard in the air in front of the learner. The instructor controls the board's position, angle, height, and distance.

**Step 4: Pointing begins.** The learner extends their arm and begins pointing at letters. When pointing is ambiguous (the finger lands between two letters), the instructor decides which letter was selected.

**Step 5: Letters announced.** The instructor says each letter aloud as it is selected, building a message. If the sequence appears to be a misspelling, the instructor may prompt "try again" or pull the board away and reposition it.

**Step 6: Message attributed.** The completed letter sequence is read as a message authored by the learner.

At every step, the facilitator makes decisions that could influence the output. Whether these decisions do constitute influence can only be determined through controlled testing -- which is precisely the testing that has never been conducted for RPM or S2C.

## The Evidence Situation

### The Empty Systematic Review

In 2019, Tostanoski et al. published a systematic review of RPM in the Review Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders. Following standard Cochrane-style methodology, they searched multiple databases, screened hundreds of papers, and applied inclusion criteria for experimental studies with adequate methodological rigor.

They found zero qualifying studies. The review was published as a paper documenting the absence of evidence. It concluded: "no experimental studies of sufficient rigor to demonstrate a link between any communication or education improvements in the person with a disability and the use of RPM"; "no studies have tested authorship of messages produced using RPM"; and "no experimental studies addressing the effectiveness of RPM."

In 2025, an ASHA presentation titled "The Empty Review" highlighted that systematic reviews of FC, RPM, and S2C consistently find zero qualifying studies to include. This is not a gap in one area of evidence -- it is a total absence of controlled evidence.

**Proponent response**: The absence of evidence is not evidence of absence. Researchers have not conducted appropriate studies. Proponent organizations have called for more research.

**Critic response**: After 30+ years of RPM and nearly a decade of S2C, the complete absence of controlled evidence is itself significant. Proponent organizations have resources to fund research. The fact that they have produced no controlled authorship studies -- the most basic and necessary validation -- suggests that the methods may not survive such testing. The burden of proof lies with those making extraordinary claims.

### ASHA's Position

ASHA has explicitly warned against both RPM and S2C, stating: "There is no evidence that messages produced using RPM reflect the communication of the person with a disability." ASHA's Board of Directors approved position statements discouraging use of both methods, stating they "lack scientific validity and do not foster independent communication."

## The Jaswal Studies and Their Critiques

The Jaswal studies represent the most frequently cited research by S2C proponents. Both have received substantive published critiques. Neither settles the authorship question.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-4-jaswal-claims-critiques -->

### Jaswal 2020: Eye-Tracking Study

**Study**: Jaswal, Wayne & Golino (2020), "Eye-tracking reveals agency in assisted autistic communication," published in Nature Scientific Reports. Used head-mounted eye-tracking with 9 non-speaking autistic letterboard users.

**Claim**: Participants looked at and pointed to letters quickly, with patterns the authors interpreted as consistent with planning and production processes. The authors concluded that eye-tracking "reveals agency" in assisted communication.

**Published critiques**:

Catherine & Katharine (2021, EBCAI) argued the study rests on "faulty assumptions": that looking at letters proves choosing letters (it does not -- a person following a held board would also look at target letters); that there is a known language disorder combining preserved oral skills with pragmatic skills emerging only during letterboard pointing (there is not); and that the small sample (N=9) cannot support generalizable conclusions.

Stuart Vyse (Skeptical Inquirer) raised additional methodological concerns: no control condition without a facilitator present, failure to rule out subtle cueing, and the fundamental problem that the study "assumes what it sets out to prove."

facilitatedcommunication.org's analysis argued the study "fails to establish the authenticity of S2C for non-speakers" and noted that looking at letters is a necessary but not sufficient condition for authorship.

### Jaswal 2024: Literacy Study

**Study**: Jaswal, Lampi & Stockwell (2024), "Literacy in nonspeaking autistic people," published in the journal Autism. Used an iPad tapping task where participants tapped sequentially pulsing targets; researchers looked for reaction-time signatures consistent with literacy.

**Claim**: About half (17 of 31) of non-speaking autistic participants showed patterns consistent with foundational literacy skills, despite most reportedly never receiving formal literacy instruction.

**Published rebuttal**: "Illusions of literacy in nonspeaking autistic people: a response to Jaswal et al. 2024" (EBCAI, 2024) raised multiple concerns:
- Participants were recruited from RPM/S2C programs, creating practice effects with letter sequences (they regularly use letterboards)
- No comparison condition without the experimenter reading the sentence aloud
- The task measured reaction times to letter sequences, not actual reading comprehension
- The assistant's presence and behavior during the task were not controlled
- The study assessed "knowledge of orthographic conventions" rather than literacy per se

### What the Jaswal Studies Show -- and Do Not Show

The studies demonstrate that some S2C users look at letters and respond to letter sequences with patterns that differ from random behavior. They do not demonstrate that these individuals are the authors of messages produced during facilitated spelling sessions. Looking at a letter on a board that someone is holding in front of you is expected behavior regardless of who is determining which letter comes next.

## The Evidence Standoff

The same observable facts admit two competing interpretations.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-3-evidence-standoff -->

| Observable Fact | Proponent Interpretation | Critic Interpretation |
|----------------|------------------------|-----------------------|
| Learner points to letters on a held board | Motor support enables genuine communication | Facilitator positioning guides pointing |
| Messages are complex and literate | Hidden competence revealed | Consistent with facilitator authorship |
| Eye-tracking shows learner looks at letters | Agency demonstrated | Following the board is expected regardless |
| Systematic reviews find zero qualifying studies | Researchers haven't studied it properly | The method cannot pass controlled tests |
| Some individuals reportedly transition to independent typing | Proof the method works | Transition needs controlled documentation |

This ambiguity is precisely why controlled authorship studies are needed. Observable behavior cannot distinguish between a non-speaking individual who is genuinely spelling with motor support and a facilitator who is unconsciously guiding the output. Only controlled testing -- where the individual has information the facilitator lacks -- can make this distinction.

## The Independence Question

The claimed progression from fully supported spelling to independent typing is the most important claim in this debate.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-5-independence-continuum -->

**Stage 1 (Stencil board)** and **Stage 2 (Laminated letterboard)**: The facilitator holds and controls the communication display. Authorship verification is not possible at these stages because the facilitator's control of the board position creates a channel for unconscious influence.

**Stage 3 (Board on table)**: The board is not held by the facilitator, but the facilitator remains present with verbal cues. Partial verification becomes possible.

**Stage 4 (Keyboard/iPad)** and **Stage 5 (Independent typing)**: The individual uses a fixed device without the facilitator controlling the display. Controlled authorship testing is fully feasible at these stages.

**The critical gap**: The transition from unverifiable stages (1-2) to verifiable stages (4-5) has never been systematically documented with controlled testing at each stage. Some individuals are described as now typing independently, but the transition process lacks the controlled verification that would make these cases scientifically compelling.

### Ido Kedar

Ido Kedar (born 2000) was diagnosed with severe autism and assessed as intellectually disabled. He began working with Soma Mukhopadhyay around age 7. He published "Ido in Autismland" at age 15 (2012), documenting the experience of being trapped in a body that would not obey his mind. He describes himself as independently communicating via iPad typing.

Kedar's case is the strongest type of case for spelling-based methods -- an individual who began with supported methods and reportedly transitioned to independent communication. However, his communication has not been formally verified through controlled authorship testing. The distinction between "types independently in some observers' judgment" and "independently verified through controlled testing" is crucial. His Wikipedia article notes Soma Mukhopadhyay "was involved with subsequent facilitated communication interventions."

## The Motor Support vs. Authorship Influence Argument

The core dispute reduces to a single question: does motor support equal authorship influence?

**Proponent claim**: Motor support is distinct from authorship influence. Just as a physical therapist can support someone's arm during a reaching exercise without choosing the direction of the reach, an S2C practitioner can provide motor support without influencing which letters the individual selects.

**Critic response**: The analogy fails because in physical therapy, the goal direction is set by the patient's own visible reaching behavior. In letterboard spelling, the "goal" is the next letter in a message whose content is unknown. The facilitator controls the board's position, provides verbal cues about pacing and correctness, and decides which letter was selected when pointing is ambiguous. These are all channels for influence. Without controlled testing, the claim is unfalsifiable.

**The prompt dependency problem**: Even methods claiming to eliminate physical contact still involve high levels of verbal prompting, pacing cues, and facilitator decision-making about ambiguous pointing. Critics argue that these non-physical prompts can guide letter selection just as effectively as physical hand support.

## The Spellers Documentary (2023)

The Spellers documentary, released March 31, 2023, and executive produced by Jenny McCarthy-Wahlberg and Donnie Wahlberg, brought S2C to wider public attention. It won awards at the Phoenix Film Festival and promoted S2C without mentioning facilitated communication or the controlled studies that debunked FC.

Critical reviewers noted specific scenes suggesting facilitator influence: a young man pointing to letters while looking in the opposite direction from the board; a facilitator pulling the board away after apparent nonsense letter sequences; and a participant (Madison) who cries and says "I'm sad" with her spoken voice while the facilitator attributes different, more complex thoughts to her through the letterboard.

The film's executive producer, Jenny McCarthy-Wahlberg, is widely known for her promotion of discredited claims about vaccines and autism -- context that informs how the documentary has been received by the autism and scientific communities.

## Common Misconceptions

### Misconception: "S2C and RPM are completely different from facilitated communication"

**Reality**: There are described procedural differences (board holding vs. hand-over-hand support), but the fundamental structure is the same: a facilitator controls the communication display while a non-speaking individual points to letters. Whether these procedural differences eliminate facilitator influence has not been tested in controlled studies.

### Misconception: "Eye-tracking studies have proven that S2C users are the authors of their messages"

**Reality**: The Jaswal 2020 study showed that 9 S2C users looked at letters before pointing to them. Multiple published critiques argue this does not establish authorship. Looking at a letter on a board someone is holding in front of you is expected behavior regardless of who is determining which letter is next.

### Misconception: "All professionals oppose spelling-based methods"

**Reality**: While every major professional scientific and medical organization has warned against these methods, many individual SLPs, educators, and therapists continue to use or support them. The gap between organizational positions and individual practice is significant.

### Misconception: "Critics of S2C/RPM believe non-speaking autistic people cannot communicate"

**Reality**: Critics strongly support evidence-based AAC and advocate for communication access through methods where authorship is not in question. The critique targets the specific method, not the population's communicative potential.

## What Would Resolve This Debate

The debate has a clear path to resolution, if both sides were willing:

1. **Controlled authorship testing of S2C/RPM users** -- message-passing tests or equivalent designs, conducted with familiar facilitators in comfortable settings, with appropriate accommodations for autistic communication styles. If genuine authorship exists, some correct responses should appear under blinding.

2. **Systematic documentation of the independence transition** -- controlled verification at each stage as individuals move from held letterboards to independent typing. Video documentation with independent coders, verified information transfer, and replication across multiple individuals.

3. **Facilitator behavior analysis** -- detailed behavioral coding of what facilitators actually do during sessions. How often is the board repositioned? How are ambiguous points resolved? What verbal prompts are given?

4. **Comparison studies** -- randomized trials comparing outcomes for non-speaking individuals receiving S2C/RPM versus evidence-based AAC.

None of these studies exist. Their absence is the most significant fact in this debate.

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| RPM | Rapid Prompting Method -- communication method using intensive multisensory prompts and held letterboards |
| S2C | Spelling to Communicate -- formalized method derived from RPM with published protocols |
