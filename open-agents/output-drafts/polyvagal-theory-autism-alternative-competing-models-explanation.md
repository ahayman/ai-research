# Alternative & Competing Models: Is Polyvagal Even the Right Framework?

## Overview

A theory's epistemic status isn't just about whether its own predictions fit some data. It's also about whether *other* theories fit the same data — or better. If a competing framework explains the same observations with a less contested mechanism, the original theory's privileged status erodes. The autistic HRV data, the meltdown/shutdown phenomenology, the burnout literature, the interoception findings — these are the data points the user cares about. The question this chapter asks is: are these data points uniquely explained by polyvagal theory, or are they equally well (or better) explained by frameworks that don't require PVT's contested anatomical and evolutionary claims?

The answer is that **polyvagal theory is not winning this comparison**. There are at least five mainstream contemporary frameworks that explain the autonomic, interoceptive, and emotion-regulation data the user cares about, without invoking PVT's three-tier vagal hierarchy, mammalian-only myelinated vagus, dorsal-vagal-as-shutdown, or neuroception-as-special-mechanism. Two of those frameworks (neurovisceral integration and predictive processing) make essentially identical HRV predictions to PVT and are the dominant academic frameworks in autonomic and computational neuroscience respectively. A third (allostasis) is the framework the empirical autistic-burnout literature *actually uses* — Raymaker 2020's definition of autistic burnout maps directly onto McEwen's allostatic-load construct. A fourth (Lisa Feldman Barrett's theory of constructed emotion) directly contradicts PVT's architectural premise that hardwired emotion-states map to brainstem vagal substrates, and has meta-analytic neuroimaging evidence on its side. A fifth (Damasio's somatic marker hypothesis) captures PVT's "body feels it first" intuition with vmPFC-centric architecture and no contested phylogenetic claims.

**Key takeaways:**
- Polyvagal Theory has no unique explanatory advantage on the autism HRV data, the autistic burnout phenomenology, autistic emotion regulation, or autistic sensory hypersensitivity.
- For autistic burnout specifically, **allostasis decisively wins** — it's the framework Raymaker's empirical autistic-burnout research uses.
- For HRV and emotion regulation, **NVI and predictive processing dominate** the academic neuroscience literature with higher citation impact and less contested mechanisms.
- PVT survives as a useful clinical heuristic (the "ladder" metaphor genuinely helps some people name and track their states) but not as a privileged scientific framework.
- The honest assessment: **PVT loses the comparative contest**. It's not the right framework when its competitors explain the same data more parsimoniously.

## Model 1: Neurovisceral Integration (NVI) — Thayer & Lane

Among the alternatives, neurovisceral integration is the closest direct competitor to PVT. It addresses the same core data — heart-rate variability, autonomic regulation, the brain-heart connection — but does it with established neuroanatomy and without phylogenetic claims that have been empirically contested.

### Mechanism

Thayer and Lane's NVI model proposes a hierarchical neural network that integrates the **ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC)**, the **amygdala**, and the **brainstem vagal nuclei** (nucleus ambiguus and dorsal motor nucleus) into a continuous regulatory circuit. The architecture is straightforward: the vmPFC exerts tonic inhibition on the amygdala. The amygdala, when uninhibited, drives autonomic arousal via its projections to brainstem nuclei. The vagal nuclei in turn control heart rate via the vagus nerve. Higher tonic vmPFC inhibition of the amygdala means greater top-down regulatory capacity, which manifests peripherally as higher heart-rate variability and higher respiratory sinus arrhythmia (RSA).

The model thus links a central brain network (cortical–subcortical–brainstem) to a measurable peripheral signal (HRV). HRV becomes a transdiagnostic biomarker of "functional integrity of the central autonomic network." It indexes not just cardiac vagal tone but the overall capacity of the prefrontal-amygdala-vagus circuit for adaptive regulation.

<!-- DIAGRAM: 2-nvi-mechanism -->

### Evidence Base

NVI is broadly accepted in autonomic neuroscience. Thayer and Lane's 2000 *Journal of Affective Disorders* paper has over 2,679 citations. The 2009 follow-up in *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews* ("Claude Bernard and the heart-brain connection") and the 2017 Smith et al. paper on the "hierarchical basis" of NVI have established the model as a unifying framework for HRV-emotion regulation research. The structural overlap between the central autonomic network and prefrontal-amygdala emotion-regulation circuits has been validated in neuroimaging studies. Multiple meta-analyses (Holzman & Bridgett 2017; Beauchaine 2015) support the core HRV–regulation link.

In academic autonomic neuroscience, NVI — not PVT — is the standard framework. PVT is more prominent in clinical psychology and the wellness industry; NVI dominates in basic and translational neuroscience.

### How NVI Explains the Autism HRV Findings

Here's the critical point: NVI predicts everything PVT predicts about the autistic HRV data, with a mechanism that doesn't require contested phylogenetic claims.

Cheng 2020's meta-analysis of 34 studies found autistic individuals show significantly lower baseline parasympathetic HRV (Hedges' g = -0.52) and lower RSA reactivity to social stress (g = -0.72). NVI's interpretation: autistic individuals show atypical vmPFC structure and function (well-documented in neuroimaging), atypical amygdala reactivity, and atypical resting connectivity in regulatory networks. Reduced vmPFC top-down inhibition of the amygdala produces lower baseline HRV and impaired dynamic regulation under social-stress demand.

The HRV finding is *exactly* what NVI predicts for any population with reduced top-down regulatory capacity — depression, anxiety, PTSD, autism, alcohol use disorder, schizophrenia. None of these require polyvagal-specific machinery to explain.

### Why NVI Wins the Comparison

When two frameworks predict the same observation, the parsimony tie-breaker favors the framework with less contested mechanism. NVI uses established neuroanatomy (the central autonomic network is well-mapped). PVT requires contested phylogenetic claims that the 2026 Untenable consensus (39 co-authors) rejects. NVI has higher citation impact in academic neuroscience. PVT has higher visibility in clinical/wellness contexts. If the question is "what is the established scientific framework for HRV-emotion regulation?", the answer is NVI.

## Model 2: Predictive Processing / Active Inference

Predictive processing is the framework that has reshaped cognitive and computational neuroscience over the past 20 years. It's the dominant contemporary account of how the brain handles perception, action, and emotion. For our question, it's the framework that absorbs PVT's "neuroception" construct as ordinary predictive inference, and provides a far broader explanatory frame for autism, alexithymia, autonomic regulation, and emotion.

### Mechanism

The core idea, originating in Karl Friston's free energy principle and elaborated by Anil Seth, Lisa Feldman Barrett, and many others: the brain is a *prediction machine*. Rather than passively receiving sensory data and constructing meaning from it bottom-up, the brain generates probabilistic predictions ("priors") about what it will perceive next — both externally and internally. Sensory input is compared to predictions; the discrepancy is **prediction error**. The brain minimizes prediction error by (a) updating its predictions (perceptual inference) or (b) acting on the world or body to make sensory data match predictions (active inference).

For autonomic regulation specifically, the brain *predicts* what physiological state the body will need before it needs it, and pre-emptively adjusts via descending autonomic outputs. Heart rate, blood pressure, hormonal release, immune response — all are continuously re-set by the brain in anticipation of need. This is exactly Sterling's reformulation of allostasis as "predictive regulation" (Model 3). HRV, on this view, is the peripheral signature of a regulatory system that's anticipating, weighting predictions against incoming data, and continuously adjusting both predictions and bodily action.

For emotion, the predictive-processing framework was extended in Anil Seth's 2013 *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* paper ("Interoceptive inference, emotion, and the embodied self") and his companion paper in *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*. Emotions, on this view, are not hardwired outputs of evolved circuits but *inferences about what the body's interoceptive prediction errors mean in context*. The same physiological state (e.g., elevated heart rate) might be inferred as "anxiety" in one context and "excitement" in another, depending on the brain's contextual concept-construction.

<!-- DIAGRAM: 3-predictive-processing -->

### Evidence Base

Predictive processing is the current dominant framework in computational and cognitive neuroscience. Karl Friston is among the most-cited neuroscientists alive. The framework subsumes predictive coding, Bayesian brain, active inference, and free energy minimization under one umbrella. Anil Seth's interoceptive predictive coding has become a foundational framework in interoception research. Barrett & Simmons 2015 in *Nature Reviews Neuroscience* established interoceptive predictions as a unifying explanatory frame across emotion, body awareness, and psychiatric disorder.

For autism specifically: Quattrocki & Friston 2014 explicitly framed autism as an interoceptive prediction problem with developmental oxytocin abnormalities producing atypical precision-weighting on interoceptive signals. Pellicano & Burr 2012 proposed autistic perception involves weaker prior weighting in Bayesian inference ("hypo-priors"), with sensory data weighted too heavily relative to expectations. Both accounts have generated a robust research program; the 10-year review of Bayesian theories of autism (2023) summarizes the current state.

### How Predictive Processing Explains the Autism + Alexithymia + Interoception Data

The fit is much tighter than PVT's. Where PVT offers a "states" framework (ventral vagal / sympathetic / dorsal vagal), predictive processing offers a *mechanism*: precision-weighting on interoceptive predictions.

In autism, atypical precision-weighting can manifest as:
- **Hypo-priors** (Pellicano & Burr 2012): sensory data over-weighted → veridical detail-focused perception, sensory hypersensitivity, reduced susceptibility to illusions.
- **Atypical interoceptive precision** (Quattrocki & Friston 2014): interoceptive signals processed atypically, contributing to alexithymia and emotion-categorization difficulties.
- **Failed predictive control of autonomic state**: the regulatory system can't anticipate demands and lurches reactively, producing the autonomic dysregulation captured by HRV/RSA measurements.

This connects to **alexithymia** in a way PVT cannot. The Bird & Cook 2013 hypothesis — that the interoceptive deficits attributed to autism are actually alexithymia-driven (~50% co-occurrence) — fits cleanly within predictive processing: alexithymia = atypical interoceptive precision-weighting + atypical concept-construction. When alexithymia is controlled for, autism-vs-non-autism interoception differences largely disappear (Shah et al. 2016). This is the kind of mechanistic nuance PVT cannot represent.

For **autistic burnout**, predictive processing aligns with Sterling 2012's reformulation of allostasis: cumulative predictive-regulatory failure depletes the system's reserves. The framework absorbs the burnout phenomenology as allostatic dysregulation — which is exactly what Model 3 does next.

### Why Predictive Processing Wins the Comparison

Predictive processing is broader, more general, and more empirically constrained than PVT. It:
- Absorbs PVT's "neuroception" as ordinary predictive inference (no special mechanism needed).
- Explains autonomic regulation, emotion, interoception, perception, decision, psychiatric disorder, and autism under a unified framework.
- Connects directly to allostasis (Sterling 2012 reformulation) and to constructed emotion (Barrett 2017).
- Has substantially higher academic impact than PVT in computational and cognitive neuroscience.
- Generates testable, precise predictions (e.g., specific predictions about precision-weighting that can be tested with psychophysics paradigms).

When PVT and predictive processing make the same predictions, parsimony favors predictive processing. When they differ — most notably on whether neuroception is a special mechanism or just standard predictive inference — predictive processing wins because it's both more general and more empirically constrained.

## Model 3: Allostasis and Allostatic Load — Sterling & Eyer; McEwen

If predictive processing is the dominant framework in computational neuroscience and NVI is the dominant framework in autonomic neuroscience, allostasis is the framework that most directly addresses the user's centrally-stated concern: **burnout**. The autistic burnout literature is structured around allostasis-style cumulative load. The PVT reframe of burnout as "dorsal vagal shutdown" is metaphor layered on top of an allostatic-load substrate.

### Mechanism

Peter Sterling and Joseph Eyer introduced the concept of allostasis in 1988 (chapter in *Handbook of Life Stress, Cognition and Health*) with a deceptively simple principle: "stability through change." Where classical homeostasis posits fixed setpoints the body returns to (a thermostat model), allostasis posits *predictive* adjustment of physiology in anticipation of need. Heart rate, blood pressure, cortisol, glucose, immune function — none have fixed setpoints. All are continuously re-set by the brain based on anticipated demand.

When this anticipatory regulation works, it's adaptive. When it doesn't, costs accumulate. Bruce McEwen's seminal 1998 *New England Journal of Medicine* paper introduced **allostatic load**: the cumulative biological wear-and-tear from chronic over-demand on regulatory systems. McEwen identified four patterns that produce load:
1. Frequent activation of allostatic responses (chronic exposure to stressors).
2. Failure to shut off allostatic activity after stress ends (the system gets stuck in active mode).
3. Inadequate response of some allostatic systems, leading to compensatory over-activation of others.
4. Maladaptive adaptation (the system "settles" into a dysregulated state).

Allostatic load damages the cardiovascular system, immune system, brain (particularly hippocampus and prefrontal cortex), and metabolic system over time. It's the biological mechanism linking chronic stress exposure to disease — depression, anxiety, PTSD, cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, cognitive decline, immune dysregulation.

Sterling's 2012 *Physiology & Behavior* paper reframed allostasis as "predictive regulation," connecting it directly to predictive processing (Model 2). The brain anticipates physiological needs and pre-emptively adjusts; when anticipation fails, load accumulates.

<!-- DIAGRAM: 4-allostatic-load-burnout -->

### Evidence Base

Allostatic load has been operationalized via biomarker indices — combinations of cortisol, blood pressure, inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6), HRV, metabolic markers (insulin, HbA1c, lipid profile), and others — and validated in hundreds of studies linking chronic stress exposure to disease risk. McEwen 1998 is one of the most-cited papers in stress biology. The framework has been applied across social-determinants-of-health research, chronic-illness research, aging research, and psychiatric epidemiology.

It is, in plain terms, one of the most empirically grounded frameworks in stress biology.

### How Allostasis Explains Autistic Burnout — The Key Linkage

Raymaker 2020 / AASPIRE defined autistic burnout as "chronic life stress and a mismatch of expectations and abilities without adequate supports" producing "pervasive, long-term exhaustion, loss of function, and reduced tolerance to stimulus." That definition is *operationally identical* to allostatic-load-induced exhaustion.

The empirical follow-on work supports this. Higgins & Arnold 2021/2025 found autistic burnout correlates with PTSD symptoms at r ≈ 0.70 — and PTSD is the paradigmatic case of allostatic dysregulation. The autistic burnout literature, from its empirical inception, has used allostatic-load framing rather than polyvagal framing. Raymaker's qualitative work named the central drivers as: masking, sensory environment overload, social demand mismatch, lack of accommodations, repeated unmet recovery needs. Each of these is a chronic allostatic challenge. Cumulative load over years produces the depleted, dysregulated state autistic adults report.

No polyvagal claims are needed to explain autistic burnout. Allostasis is the framework the empirical literature uses. PVT's "dorsal vagal shutdown" reframe is metaphor on top of this substrate.

### Why Allostasis Wins for Burnout Specifically

This is the strongest case for an alternative model dominating PVT on a specific phenomenon. The reason:

- The empirical autistic burnout literature uses allostatic load framing, not PVT framing.
- The biomarker measurements (HRV, cortisol, inflammatory markers, metabolic markers) come from the allostatic-load tradition.
- The intervention implications differ: PVT-derived advice emphasizes "regulating vagal tone" (SSP, breathwork). Allostatic-load-derived advice emphasizes *demand reduction* — reducing the chronic over-demand that's producing the load. The biology recovers when demand is reduced, not when the autistic person learns to "regulate" themselves better.
- The advice overlaps where it matters (rest, safety, sensory accommodation, social co-regulation) but diverges where it counts: PVT-derived advice can subtly imply you're broken and need to "fix" your nervous system. Allostatic-load-derived advice acknowledges that the regulatory system is overwhelmed by external load, and the fix is upstream.

For autistic burnout specifically, **allostasis wins decisively**. The user's centrally-stated concern has allostasis as its native framework.

This connects to existing project work: see the alexithymia deep dive's autism-alexithymia-cumulative-overwhelm chapter, which works through the autistic cumulative-overwhelm phenomenology in detail using allostatic-load framing.

## Model 4: Theory of Constructed Emotion — Lisa Feldman Barrett

Where the previous three frameworks compete with PVT on autonomic regulation and burnout, Barrett's theory of constructed emotion directly contradicts PVT's *architectural premise*. PVT posits that hardwired biological emotion-states map to specific brainstem vagal substrates (ventral vagal = "social engagement," sympathetic = "fight/flight," dorsal vagal = "freeze"). Barrett's framework rejects this entire approach to emotion.

### Mechanism

Barrett's central claim, developed across her 2017 book *How Emotions Are Made* and her 2017 paper in *Social Cognitive and Affective Neuroscience*: emotions are not hardwired biological programs. They are *constructed* in the moment by the brain from three ingredients:

1. **Interoceptive predictions and prediction errors** — the body's current state, as inferred by the predictive-processing framework (Model 2).
2. **Concept knowledge** — learned categories like "anger," "sadness," "fear," "excitement," "shame." These categories differ across cultures and individuals.
3. **Context** — what's happening, who's around, what's expected.

Different brains construct different emotions from similar physiological states. The same elevated heart rate might be inferred as "anxiety" before a job interview, "excitement" before a first date, "anger" during an argument, "love" during intimacy — depending on the concept knowledge and context.

The implication: there is no "fear circuit" in the brain. There are general-purpose prediction and categorization networks that produce *instances* of fear when interoceptive prediction errors + the concept "fear" + threat context combine in a particular way. Brain regions implicated in emotion (amygdala, insula, anterior cingulate, vmPFC) are involved in many cognitive functions — not specific to discrete emotions.

<!-- DIAGRAM: 5-constructed-vs-hardwired-emotion -->

### Evidence Base

The empirical case for constructed emotion is strong:

- **Lindquist et al. 2012** in *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*: meta-analysis of 91 neuroimaging studies and 1,829 subjects. No discrete brain region maps cleanly to any single emotion category. Emotions emerge from distributed networks active in many tasks, not from emotion-specific modules.
- **Wager et al. 2015**: pattern-classifier neuroimaging analysis. Emotion-specific patterns exist but they're distributed across many brain regions, not localized to "emotion circuits."
- **Emotional granularity research** (Barrett 2004 and subsequent): people who construct more differentiated emotion categories show better emotion regulation, fewer mental health problems, lower physical health risk.
- **Cross-cultural research** (Mesquita & Frijda 1992; Lutz 1988): emotion categories vary substantially between cultures. Some cultures have emotion words that don't exist in others, and vice versa. This is hard to reconcile with universal hardwired emotion programs.

### The Direct Anti-PVT Implication

If constructed emotion is right, PVT's "ladder" architecture is at best a useful clinical metaphor and at worst a category error. PVT requires that biological states (ventral vagal, sympathetic, dorsal vagal) map onto discrete emotional states (safe-social, fight-flight, freeze-shutdown). Constructed emotion denies this mapping exists.

In practice, this means:
- "Being in dorsal vagal" doesn't have a fixed emotional signature — it depends on concept and context.
- Two autistic people experiencing the same physiological exhaustion may construct different emotions from it depending on their concept knowledge: "I'm in shutdown" vs. "I'm sick" vs. "I'm sad" vs. "I'm done caring."
- Interventions that build emotional granularity (emotion-labeling practice, concept-construction) should improve regulation independent of any vagal-tone intervention.

### How Constructed Emotion Explains Autistic Emotional Experience

The framework fits autistic experience well, particularly through the alexithymia connection:

- Alexithymia (~50% co-occurrence with autism per Bird & Cook 2013) involves low emotional granularity — fewer differentiated concepts to construct from. The phenomenology "I just feel bad" or "I can't tell what I'm feeling" is exactly what low granularity predicts.
- Sensory overload looks like cascading interoceptive prediction error that can't be efficiently categorized into emotion concepts. The pre-conscious flood is the prediction-error storm; the conscious experience depends on what concepts the brain has available.
- Treatment implications: building emotional granularity through concept-learning interventions (emotion-labeling practice, journaling, working with diverse emotion vocabularies) should improve regulation. This is what some interoception-focused therapies (Kelly Mahler's Interoception Curriculum, EAET) are doing in practice — independent of any vagal-tone framework. See the alexithymia deep dive's self-directed approaches chapter.

### Where Barrett Wins

Constructed emotion wins on emotion *construction* — how emotional experience arises from physiological state + concept + context. PVT can't represent the fine-grained mechanism of concept-construction or the dissociation between physiological state and emotional experience. For autistic emotion regulation specifically, the constructed-emotion + alexithymia framework is more clinically useful than PVT's states framework.

## Model 5: Somatic Marker Hypothesis — Damasio

Damasio's framework is older but enduring. It captures the same "body feels it first" insight that makes PVT intuitively appealing, without requiring the contested phylogenetic claims. For the user, it's worth understanding because somatic markers and neuroception are conceptually adjacent — and somatic markers is the framework with cleaner empirical anchoring.

### Mechanism

Antonio Damasio's 1994 *Descartes' Error* introduced the somatic marker hypothesis. The mechanism: when a person faces a decision, the brain rapidly previews possible outcomes. The body responds to each option with *somatic markers* — gut feelings, heart rate changes, skin conductance shifts — that signal whether the option is advantageous or risky, based on past experience. These body-state signals are processed by the **ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC)** and bias decision-making toward advantageous options before conscious deliberation completes.

Damasio's central claim: rationality requires emotion. Without body-state signals integrated by vmPFC, decisions become disconnected from their consequences. The famous evidence: Iowa Gambling Task studies showing that patients with vmPFC damage fail to develop anticipatory skin conductance responses and continue making disadvantageous choices long after non-damaged controls have learned to avoid them. The body knows before the conscious mind does.

<!-- DIAGRAM: 6-somatic-markers -->

### Evidence Base

Strong for the broad claim (body signals guide emotional and decision-making processes; vmPFC is a key integrator). Mixed for specific operationalizations (the Iowa Gambling Task has been criticized; the precise role of somatic markers vs. other cognitive processes is debated). The framework was updated in Damasio & Carvalho's 2013 *Nature Reviews Neuroscience* paper, which reframed feelings as conscious mental representations of body states regulated by the interoceptive nervous system — a framing that converges with both predictive processing (Model 2) and constructed emotion (Model 4).

### Where Damasio Overlaps PVT

Both Damasio and Porges emphasize body-to-brain signals in emotion and decision. PVT's "neuroception" — non-conscious detection of safety/threat cues — is conceptually adjacent to Damasio's "somatic markers as pre-conscious decision-guidance." Both share the broad framing: the body is upstream of cognition; the body is reason's partner, not its slave.

### Where Damasio Differs from PVT

- No three-tier vagal hierarchy.
- No phylogenetic claims about vagal nuclei.
- No claim that "social engagement" has a dedicated brainstem substrate.
- Centered on vmPFC + interoceptive cortex (insula), not brainstem nuclei.
- Updated framing converges with predictive processing and constructed emotion.

### Where Damasio Wins

For the "body feels it first" insight that motivates much of PVT's intuitive appeal, Damasio's framework is older, better-anchored in established neuroanatomy (vmPFC + insula), and doesn't require the contested phylogenetic claims. It's the cleaner version of the PVT intuition.

## The Interoception Frameworks: Craig, Garfinkel, Bird & Cook

Beyond the five main competing models, three interoception-specific frameworks are worth understanding because they target the same data PVT targets but with finer-grained mechanisms.

### Craig — The Insular Substrate

Bud Craig's 2002 paper ("How do you feel? Interoception") and 2009 follow-up ("How do you feel — now? The anterior insula") established the **insular cortex** as the cortical hub of interoception. Body signals are first represented in the posterior insula, re-represented in the mid-insula, and integrated in the anterior insula, where activation corresponds to subjective feeling. The pathway: visceral afferents (vagal, spinal lamina I) → thalamus → posterior insula → mid-insula → anterior insula → conscious feeling.

Craig 2002 has 5,400+ citations; Craig 2009 has 6,200+. The insula's role in interoception is one of the most-replicated findings in cognitive neuroscience. Anterior insula damage greater than 15% volume produces acquired alexithymia (Hogeveen 2016) — direct causal evidence that the insula is involved in feeling-construction.

Where PVT focuses on brainstem vagal nuclei and peripheral autonomic output, Craig focuses on cortical representation. These can coexist in principle, but for understanding *conscious feeling* of body states, Craig's insular model is the established framework. PVT doesn't replace it.

### Garfinkel — The Three Dimensions

Sarah Garfinkel et al.'s 2015 paper in *Biological Psychology* ("Knowing your own heart") showed that interoception is not one thing. It has three dissociable dimensions:

1. **Interoceptive accuracy** — objective performance on tasks like heartbeat detection. How well can you actually count your heartbeats?
2. **Interoceptive sensibility** — self-reported subjective awareness. How attentive do you believe you are to body signals?
3. **Interoceptive awareness** (metacognitive) — correspondence between accuracy and sensibility. Do you actually know how well you can detect body signals?

These dimensions can dissociate within individuals. A person can have high sensibility (thinks they're attuned) but low accuracy (actually performs poorly on detection tasks). Garfinkel et al. 2016 found autistic adults showed *higher* sensibility but *lower* accuracy than non-autistic adults — an interoceptive awareness mismatch, not simple deficit. This is exactly the kind of nuance PVT's framework cannot represent.

### Bird & Cook — The Alexithymia Hypothesis

Geoff Bird and Richard Cook's 2013 *Translational Psychiatry* paper proposed that the interoceptive and emotional difficulties often attributed to autism are actually attributable to *co-occurring alexithymia*, which is present in roughly 50% of autistic adults but is conceptually distinct from autism. The subsequent empirical work (Shah et al. 2016, Cook et al. 2013) supports this: when alexithymia is controlled for, autistic-vs-non-autistic interoceptive performance differences largely disappear.

This hypothesis has substantial implications for PVT-for-autism. A central appeal of PVT-for-autism is that it offers a story about *why* autistic people have emotional/regulatory differences — the ventral vagal social engagement system is "impaired." If the relevant differences are alexithymia-driven rather than autism-per-se driven, PVT's autism-specific story is targeting the wrong construct. The alexithymia deep dive in this project covers this in detail.

### Pellicano & Burr — Autism as Hypo-Priors

A final framework worth mentioning: Pellicano & Burr's 2012 *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* paper proposing autism involves weaker prior weighting in Bayesian sensory inference ("hypo-priors"). Sensory data is weighted too heavily relative to expectations, producing veridical detail-focused perception, sensory hypersensitivity, and reduced susceptibility to perceptual illusions.

This competes directly with PVT on sensory hypersensitivity. PVT explains autistic sensory hypersensitivity via a "defensive state" — the autistic nervous system is dispositionally tilted toward sympathetic/dorsal vagal activation, biasing sensory processing toward threat detection. Pellicano & Burr explain the same phenomenon via weaker prior weighting in standard Bayesian sensory inference. Their account makes testable psychophysics predictions (autistic people should be less susceptible to specific illusions; should show different adaptation curves) that have been partially supported. PVT's defensive-state account hasn't generated comparable testable predictions.

## The Convergence/Divergence Map — Central Analytical Anchor

This is the chapter's analytical centerpiece. For each phenomenon the user cares about, which frameworks predict it? Where do they converge and where do they diverge?

### Phenomenon 1: Low baseline HRV in autistic adults (Cheng 2020, g = -0.52)

Five frameworks predict this:
- **PVT**: reduced ventral vagal social engagement function
- **NVI**: reduced vmPFC inhibition of amygdala
- **Predictive processing**: atypical precision-weighting on interoceptive predictions
- **Allostasis**: indicator of cumulative allostatic load
- **Bird & Cook**: alexithymia-driven (with caveat)

**Verdict**: PVT has no unique explanatory advantage. NVI does it with established neuroscience; predictive processing does it with the dominant contemporary framework; allostasis does it with cumulative-stress framing.

### Phenomenon 2: Reduced RSA reactivity to social stress (Cheng 2020, g = -0.72)

Four frameworks predict this:
- **PVT**: vagal brake fails to engage during social-engagement demand
- **NVI**: vmPFC fails to dynamically regulate amygdala during social challenge
- **Predictive processing**: failed predictive control of autonomic response
- **Allostasis**: insufficient anticipatory regulation under stress

**Verdict**: PVT has the *most direct ladder-shaped story* here. NVI and predictive processing offer essentially the same prediction with different vocabulary. PVT's edge is interpretive clarity, not empirical specificity. None of these frameworks is *uniquely* supported by the data.

### Phenomenon 3: Autistic burnout (Raymaker 2020 — chronic exhaustion, loss of function, reduced stimulus tolerance)

- **PVT**: "dorsal vagal shutdown" — metaphor; no direct in-vivo measurement of dorsal vagal "state"
- **NVI**: chronic regulatory failure; persistent low HRV
- **Predictive processing**: failed allostatic anticipation; depleted predictive reserves
- **Allostasis**: ***direct fit — this is the empirical framework Raymaker uses***
- **Bird & Cook**: comorbid alexithymia may amplify burnout difficulty

**Verdict**: **Allostasis wins decisively**. It's the framework Raymaker and AASPIRE actually use. PVT's "dorsal vagal shutdown" framing is layered metaphor on top of an empirically-grounded allostatic-load construct.

### Phenomenon 4: Autistic emotion regulation difficulties

Five frameworks predict this:
- **PVT**: ventral vagal social engagement impairment
- **NVI**: vmPFC-amygdala-vagus regulatory circuit atypicality
- **Predictive processing**: atypical precision-weighting on interoceptive predictions
- **Constructed emotion**: atypical emotion-concept construction (low granularity, alexithymia)
- **Bird & Cook**: co-occurring alexithymia explains the emotion-regulation effects

**Verdict**: Four mainstream frameworks explain this without PVT. PVT here is one of several competing accounts, arguably the least well-supported on grounds of mechanism specificity.

### Phenomenon 5: Sensory hypersensitivity in autism

- **PVT**: defensive state biases auditory processing
- **Pellicano & Burr (predictive processing)**: hypo-priors — sensory data weighted too heavily
- **Allostasis**: cumulative sensory overload → reduced threshold

**Verdict**: PVT and Pellicano & Burr both offer specific mechanisms. Pellicano & Burr's account is more empirically constrained and connects to broader autism cognitive science.

### Where PVT Makes Unique Predictions That Others Don't

PVT *does* make some unique predictions:
1. The dorsal motor nucleus mediates freezing/shutdown specifically (rejected by Neuhuber & Berthoud 2022 and the 2026 Untenable consensus).
2. Myelinated cardiac vagal fibers are mammalian-only (rejected by Grossman 2023 with lungfish/reptile/bird evidence).
3. Three discrete biological states ladder under threat (not directly measurable in vivo; treated as clinical heuristic by most users).
4. Neuroception is a distinct non-conscious detection mechanism separate from standard predictive processing (no clear empirical separation; predictive processing absorbs the construct).

**The pattern**: PVT's unique predictions are precisely the ones that the 2026 Untenable consensus rejected. The predictions PVT *shares* with other models are the ones supported by the autism HRV data — but those don't privilege PVT over its competitors.

## The Practical Decision: When PVT Adds Value vs. When It Just Relabels

The honest assessment is layered. PVT loses the *scientific* comparison decisively on multiple fronts. But it may still add value in specific contexts as a clinical vocabulary or self-narrative tool. The decision aid below maps when each framework is most useful.

<!-- DIAGRAM: 8-decision-aid -->

**When PVT genuinely adds value**:
- As a clinical vocabulary. For autistic people who find the "ladder" metaphor (ventral → sympathetic → dorsal) useful for naming and tracking their states, the framework provides usable scaffolding. This is the Journal of Psychiatry Reform 2023 stance: "Polyvagal Approaches: scientifically questionable but useful in practice."
- As a meta-permission. PVT gives socially-acceptable language for needing rest, safety, and accommodation — that's real.

**When PVT doesn't add value and other frameworks dominate**:
- **HRV and emotion regulation** → use NVI. Mainstream framework in autonomic neuroscience.
- **Burnout, chronic exhaustion** → use allostasis. The framework empirical autistic-burnout research uses.
- **Emotion construction, alexithymia, emotional granularity** → use constructed emotion or predictive processing. Finer mechanism than PVT's states.
- **Interoception, body awareness** → use Craig + Garfinkel + Bird & Cook. Decompose interoception into accuracy, sensibility, awareness; recognize alexithymia drives the autism findings.
- **Sensory hypersensitivity** → use Pellicano & Burr / predictive processing. More testable than PVT's defensive-state account.
- **Comparative anatomy claims** → reject. The 2026 Untenable consensus has rejected PVT's core anatomical claims.

## The Final Analytical Takeaway

Is polyvagal theory the right framework for understanding autistic HRV, burnout, emotion regulation, and overwhelm?

**No. The honest comparative assessment is that polyvagal theory loses the contest.**

Five mainstream frameworks predict the same data with mechanisms that are either better-established (NVI, predictive processing), specifically empirically anchored to the user's centrally-stated concern (allostasis for burnout), or directly contradictory to PVT's architectural premises (constructed emotion). For every observation the user cares about — the autistic HRV data, the autistic burnout phenomenology, autistic emotion regulation difficulties, autistic sensory hypersensitivity — at least one alternative framework explains it more parsimoniously than PVT does.

PVT's unique predictions (three-tier vagal hierarchy, mammalian-only myelinated vagus, dorsal vagal as freezing substrate, neuroception as special mechanism) have been empirically rejected by the 2026 Untenable consensus. The predictions PVT *shares* with other models are the ones supported by the autism data — but shared predictions don't privilege PVT.

Where PVT survives — and this matters — is as a useful clinical vocabulary and self-narrative tool. The "ladder" metaphor genuinely helps some autistic people name and track their states. The Journal of Psychiatry Reform 2023's framing captures this: "scientifically questionable but useful in practice." Heuristic value is real. But heuristic value isn't theoretical privilege.

For the user's question — "is polyvagal the right framework?" — the answer is: **not really, and especially not for the burnout question that matters most**. For autistic burnout, allostasis is the framework that empirical autistic-burnout research actually uses. For HRV and emotion regulation, NVI and predictive processing dominate the academic neuroscience literature. For emotion construction and alexithymia, constructed emotion and the Bird & Cook hypothesis offer finer mechanisms. PVT can be used as scaffolding where it's genuinely useful; it should not be imported as if its mechanistic claims were established science.

The autistic HRV data is not strong evidence *for* polyvagal theory. It's evidence for any framework that links central regulatory networks to peripheral autonomic output — which is the entire post-1990 autonomic neuroscience literature. The question shifts from "is PVT supported?" to "is PVT the best framework available?" and the answer there is: no.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Active inference** | Friston's formalization in which agents act on the world (or body) to make sensory input match predictions |
| **Allostasis** | Stability through change; predictive regulation of physiology in anticipation of need (Sterling & Eyer 1988) |
| **Allostatic load** | Cumulative biological wear-and-tear from chronic over-demand on regulatory systems (McEwen 1998) |
| **Bayesian brain** | The hypothesis that the brain represents and updates beliefs about the world probabilistically; foundational to predictive processing |
| **Constructed emotion** | Barrett's theory that emotions are constructed from interoceptive prediction + concept + context, not biological programs |
| **Emotional granularity** | The fineness of distinctions a person can make between emotion categories |
| **Free energy principle** | Friston's unified theory that all living systems strive to minimize free energy (a measure of prediction error) |
| **Hypo-priors** | Pellicano & Burr's hypothesis that autistic perception involves weaker prior weighting in Bayesian inference |
| **Interoception** | The sense of the body's internal physiological condition |
| **Interoceptive accuracy** | Objective performance on interoception tasks (e.g., heartbeat detection); one of Garfinkel's three dimensions |
| **Interoceptive awareness (metacognitive)** | Correspondence between interoceptive accuracy and sensibility; the third Garfinkel dimension |
| **Interoceptive inference** | Predictive processing applied to interoception — emotion as inference about interoceptive prediction errors |
| **Interoceptive sensibility** | Subjective self-reported attention to body signals; one of Garfinkel's three dimensions |
| **NVI** | Neurovisceral integration; Thayer & Lane's model linking vmPFC, amygdala, vagus, HRV |
| **Predictive processing** | Framework treating the brain as a hierarchical prediction machine that minimizes prediction error |
| **Predictive regulation** | Sterling's 2012 reframing of allostasis as predictive control of physiology — the brain anticipates need and pre-emptively adjusts |
| **Somatic markers** | Damasio's body-state signals that guide decision-making via vmPFC |
| **vmPFC** | Ventromedial prefrontal cortex; central integrator across multiple frameworks for top-down regulation |
