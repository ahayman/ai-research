# The Wellness Industry, the Polyvagal Institute & Commercial Interests

## Overview

By 2026, a Google search for "polyvagal theory psychotherapy" returns on the order of 120,000 results. They include peer-reviewed neuroscience papers, trade books on multi-year bestseller lists, certificate-course catalogs, "polyvagal-informed" coaching directories, Instagram and TikTok influencer accounts, supplement labels, and consumer device marketing pages. The same vocabulary — "ventral vagal," "neuroception," "social engagement system," "polyvagal-informed" — appears across all of them. The vocabulary is portable. The epistemic weight behind the vocabulary varies by orders of magnitude.

This chapter is the user's stated main concern. The worry is not that polyvagal theory is interesting (it is) or that some practices associated with it have value (some do — see Cat 6 on interventions, particularly slow breathing and the diving reflex). The worry is that a contested neuroscience theory has been adopted at scale as a marketing framework, and that the marketing's claims have run far ahead of the evidence. The user wants to be able to recognize, on sight, which polyvagal-branded content is grounded and which is sales copy.

The chapter's central analytical contribution is **the three registers**: the same theory exists in academic (contested), clinical (mixed evidence, some real utility), and wellness/influencer (often pseudoscientific) registers. Knowing which register a given claim comes from is the user's single most useful filter. The chapter develops this tool, maps the institutional landscape it cuts across, traces the money flow, surveys the consumer-product ecosystem, and closes with a reusable practitioner-evaluation checklist.

> **Confidence overview.** Of 20 substantive claims independently validated for this chapter, 17 are confirmed against primary sources (Form 990 filings, FDA records, publisher pages, university repositories). Two are uncertain at the level of usage metrics or third-party-cited scale figures (the >100,000 Pulsetto user count is a company-disclosed metric; the 120,000+ Google results figure is approximate and varies). One claim was corrected: the chapter brief described a "Polyvagal Equity Project" as a stand-alone non-profit; the actual entity is PVI's "Embodied Equity" certificate course (designed by Niki Elliott) plus the adjacent Mindful Leaders Project. The cross-reference to Cat 6's FDA verification work is consistent.

### Key takeaways

- The same polyvagal vocabulary appears in academic, clinical, and wellness/influencer registers, with the epistemic weight differing by orders of magnitude. Knowing which register a claim comes from is the user's most useful filter.
- The Polyvagal Institute (PVI) is a real 501(c)(3) non-profit (EIN 84-5084757, Denver, CO, founded 2020, ~$807K revenue in 2024), and it sells paid certificate courses; tax-exempt status does not preclude commercialized education.
- Stephen Porges holds patent royalties on SSP-related technology and serves as Chief Scientific Advisor at Unyte Health (the for-profit commercial home of SSP). Deb Dana is an advisor to Unyte and runs Rhythm of Regulation as an independent commercial training operation. Disclosure conventions are stronger in academic publications than in consumer-facing marketing.
- Of six commonly named consumer "vagus nerve" devices, **only gammaCore is FDA-cleared** — and only for specific headache indications, by prescription. Truvaga, Pulsetto, Sensate, Apollo Neuro, and Hapbee all operate under the FDA general wellness device framework, which requires no clinical efficacy demonstration.
- "Vagal tone" supplements are not supported by evidence for the specific claim they make. Mass General Hospital's published position is direct on this point.
- The autism-specific concern is real: autism "cure" claims via polyvagal interventions, "rewire your nervous system" timelines, and pathologizing framings have been explicitly contested by autistic writers and run substantially ahead of the small autism-specific evidence base (n=6 for the most-cited adult SSP pilot).
- The chapter closes with a reusable 8-question practitioner-evaluation checklist (Diagram 6) and a 10-pattern red-flag gallery (Diagram 5). These are calibrated against documented overreach patterns but generalize to any wellness or psychiatric claim.

## The Three Registers — The Analytical Anchor

The same vocabulary appears in three very different epistemic environments. The user's most useful filter is to ask, before evaluating any specific claim: **which register is this coming from?**

<!-- DIAGRAM: three-registers -->

### Register 1: Academic

You find Register 1 in peer-reviewed journals, mostly in psychophysiology, autonomic neuroscience, and trauma research. The claims here are specific and falsifiable. A typical academic statement might be: "In our healthy adult sample, paced breathing at approximately 0.1 Hz produced a four-fold increase in high-frequency HRV amplitude compared to spontaneous breathing." That sentence has a method behind it. The hedging is built into the grammar.

As of 2026, Register 1 is contested. Paul Grossman's 2023 paper in *Biological Psychology* argued that all five core premises of polyvagal theory are inconsistent with established comparative neuroanatomy. In early 2026, a multi-author consensus paper — 38 international experts in vagal physiology and vertebrate evolution, with Grossman as first author — argued that the theory has been falsified. Porges has issued a scholarly rebuttal. The full debate is the subject of [Cat 3 (The Scientific Critique)](../scientific-critique-untenable-debate/index.html). For Cat 8 purposes, the relevant point is that Register 1 is where the actual fight is happening, and that fight has not propagated down to the other registers.

### Register 2: Clinical

You find Register 2 in W. W. Norton's Professional Books / Interpersonal Neurobiology Series, in continuing-education catalogs (EMDRIA, PESI, the Embody Lab, the Polyvagal Institute itself), and in the practices of licensed clinicians who have adopted polyvagal vocabulary as a way to talk with clients about autonomic experience.

The claims in Register 2 are more applied. They sound like: "The polyvagal framework offers a useful orientation for therapists working with autonomic dysregulation in trauma survivors." Or: "SSP may help reduce auditory hypersensitivity in some clients (preliminary evidence)." The framework here is being used as orientation — a conceptual scaffolding that helps the clinician and client locate experiences in a shared vocabulary. The 2023 *Journal of Psychiatry Reform* assessment titled itself "Polyvagal Approaches: scientifically questionable but useful in practice." That title is a fair summary of where careful Register 2 practitioners sit. The framework is contested at the academic level; the practice still has clinical utility.

What's important is what Register 2 *doesn't* claim, in its careful practitioners. It doesn't claim the mechanism is settled. It doesn't claim that the framework cures autism. It doesn't claim that unlicensed practitioners can replace therapy. Where Register 2 work does make those claims, it has started to slip into Register 3.

### Register 3: Wellness / Influencer

You find Register 3 on Instagram and TikTok (#vagusnerve has accumulated more than 185 million TikTok views as of 2024 reporting), in podcast wellness content, on supplement labels, in consumer-device advertising, and in the marketing of coaching certifications offered without clinical scope. The claims here are short, certain, and lightly evidenced: "Reset your nervous system in 5 minutes." "You're stuck in dorsal vagal." "These supplements optimize your vagal tone." "This $25 gua sha tool stimulates your vagus nerve." "Rewire your nervous system in 8 weeks."

Reliable scientific institutions have published explicit warnings about this register. Northwell Health, The Well by Northwell, Mass General Hospital, and McGill's Office for Science and Society have all noted, in roughly the same language, that the relationship between the vagus nerve and relaxation is being oversimplified in popular culture, and that anatomy does not support most of the social-media mechanism claims. The Conversation's analysis traced the migration from TikTok tip to clinical-trial controversy. There is no shortage of mainstream-medical critique of Register 3. It just hasn't been loud enough to slow the growth.

### How to use the registers

When you encounter a polyvagal-branded claim, locate it on the three-register map. A Register 1 claim is hedged and narrow; trust it within its scope but not beyond it. A Register 2 claim may be useful even if the underlying theory is contested; evaluate it against the [evidence-tier framework from Cat 6](../interventions-evidence-base/index.html). A Register 3 claim is presumptively suspect until specific evidence is presented.

The vocabulary is identical across all three. Only the epistemic load differs. This is the filter.

## Mapping the Institutional Landscape

Five categories of actor sit in the polyvagal ecosystem: one major non-profit, one anchor for-profit company, a small set of individuals with overlapping roles, a publisher concentration, and a consumer-product layer.

<!-- DIAGRAM: institutional-landscape -->

### The Polyvagal Institute (PVI)

PVI is a 501(c)(3) non-profit, EIN 84-5084757, headquartered in Denver, Colorado. It was founded in 2020 by Stephen Porges, Deb Dana, Karen Onderko, and Randall Redfield. Its mission, as stated in its Form 990 filings and public materials, is to advance the application of polyvagal theory through education, training, research support, and community building.

The financials are public. For tax year 2024, PVI reported gross receipts of $806,914 and expenses of $751,767, leaving net income of $55,147 and ending the year with net assets of $505,678. The prior year (2023 filing) showed higher revenue ($902,422) and lower expenses ($535,292), yielding $367,130 net income. PVI made $0 in grants in 2024; its activity breakdown is approximately 80% training, 10% facilitating research, 10% community building. This is a training-focused non-profit, not a grant-making one.

PVI's principal offering is the Polyvagal Certificate Course, a six-month cohort-based program with 35 continuing education credits available for licensed mental health professionals. The current advertised cohort (Cohort 13) runs from April through November 2026. On successful completion, students may identify themselves as "Polyvagal-Informed" on their professional materials — a credential PVI grants and that is distinct from any therapy license. PVI also offers an Organization Certification track for schools, healthcare systems, and workplaces; topic-specific courses including Deb Dana's "Polyvagal Theory: Principles and Practice for Individuals"; the "Embodied Equity" certificate (designed by Niki Elliott in partnership with the University of San Diego Professional & Continuing Education); and a learning library of webinars and on-demand content.

Tax-exempt status does not preclude commercialized education. A 501(c)(3)'s tax status implies non-distribution of profits to private individuals, but it allows substantial compensation, paid courses, and a mission of advancing a specific theoretical framework. PVI's mission is to advance polyvagal theory's application. That is its declared purpose. The framework is what the organization exists to teach. Critics outside PVI would call this a structural commitment; PVI itself would describe it as the point.

### Unyte Health

Unyte Health is the for-profit commercial home of the Safe and Sound Protocol (SSP). The company was previously named Integrated Listening Systems (ILS); the SSP has been distributed through this corporate lineage since 2017. Unyte is privately held; its revenue and ownership structure are not publicly disclosed.

The SSP is a filtered-music intervention developed and patented by Stephen Porges. The acoustic filter emphasizes the frequency range of human speech. Clients access SSP through trained mental health professionals (the "Provider" tier) on a subscription basis. Other Unyte products include the Rest and Restore Protocol, the Focus System, and PhysioCam.

Porges's relationship to Unyte is the chapter's most important commercial fact. He is **Chief Scientific Advisor** at Unyte Health. He **licenses SSP-related patents** to Unyte and **receives ongoing royalties** on those patents. He also holds licensed patent rights to NeuralSolution for PhysioCam and related Unyte-marketed technologies. This is verified across his own academic conflict-of-interest disclosures, Unyte's own team-page content, and independent reporting. It was independently re-verified in the [Cat 6 validation work](../interventions-evidence-base/index.html) for this deep dive.

Deb Dana is an advisor to Unyte-ILS. Her clinical framework (Rhythm of Regulation®) provides much of the implementation language that SSP providers use with clients.

### The popularizer chain

A handful of authors have powered polyvagal theory's migration from neuroscience journal to bestseller list and from there into the wellness ecosystem. Their work is concentrated in a small number of trade-publisher imprints — most notably W. W. Norton's Professional Books and Interpersonal Neurobiology Series.

**Bessel van der Kolk's** *The Body Keeps the Score* (Viking, 2014) is the single most influential vector for polyvagal popularization. The book has sold many millions of copies and spent multiple years on bestseller lists. It draws heavily on polyvagal theory to explain why trauma responses feel automatic. Critics including Kristen Martin in *The Washington Post* and a 2023 editorial in *Research on Social Work Practice* have specifically named polyvagal theory among the "uncertain science" the book promotes. Van der Kolk's reach is large enough that for many readers, *The Body Keeps the Score* is where polyvagal theory enters their vocabulary.

**Deb Dana** is the most influential popularizer of polyvagal theory among practicing therapists. Her clinical works — *The Polyvagal Theory in Therapy* (Norton, 2018), *Polyvagal Exercises for Safety and Connection* (Norton, 2020) — and the consumer-facing *Anchored* (Sounds True, 2021) translate the framework into a clinical methodology she has trademarked as Rhythm of Regulation®. The ladder graphic (ventral / sympathetic / dorsal vagal) that circulates on social media is most commonly her version.

**Holly Bridges** wrote *Reframe Your Thinking Around Autism* (Jessica Kingsley Publishers, 2015; second edition LicenseToThink Pty Ltd / Zebr, 2023). The book proposes that autism symptoms are a learned nervous-system response to prolonged flight/fight rather than a stable neurodevelopmental difference, and develops the Autism Reframe Technique (A.R.T.). This framing has been substantively contested by the autistic community as fundamentally pathologizing. Bridges identifies as neurodivergent herself; the dispute is a values/framework disagreement, not a question of her motives.

**Sean M. Inderbitzen, DSW, LCSW** wrote *Autism in Polyvagal Terms: New Possibilities and Interventions* (W. W. Norton, 2024, ISBN 9781324053217), in Norton's Interpersonal Neurobiology Series. Stephen Porges contributed the foreword — not co-authorship, despite some bibliographic catalog listings that include both names. Inderbitzen is described in publisher materials as an autistic psychotherapist (Mayo Clinic Health System). The book represents an autistic-author engagement with polyvagal framing for autism, which is itself a different proposition than non-autistic authors framing autism through the theory.

**Rebecca Kase, LCSW** wrote *Polyvagal-Informed EMDR: A Neuro-Informed Approach to Healing* (W. W. Norton, 2023), the first book integrating polyvagal theory with EMDR therapy. She owns Kase & CO Training and Consulting, an EMDRIA/APA/NBCC-approved continuing-education provider.

**Bonnie Badenoch, PhD, LMFT** is the field's principal synthesist of polyvagal theory with interpersonal neurobiology, attachment, memory reconsolidation, and mirror-neuron literature. Her books *The Heart of Trauma* (Norton, 2017) and *Being a Brain-Wise Therapist* (Norton, 2008) and her online courses through Sounds True have become a major vehicle for the theory's transmission into trauma-informed parenting and school applications.

The publisher concentration matters. Norton's Interpersonal Neurobiology Series carries most clinical polyvagal titles. Viking/Penguin carries van der Kolk. Sounds True carries the more lay-oriented adaptations. Jessica Kingsley carries the first edition of Bridges. The pipeline is small.

### Niki Elliott and the equity offerings

The chapter brief asked about a "Polyvagal Equity Project." Independent verification did not locate a stand-alone non-profit under that name. What does exist: PVI's **Embodied Equity** certificate course, designed by Niki Elliott, which frames implicit bias and identity-threat through a nervous-system lens and uses mindfulness, breathwork, and somatic awareness practices. The course runs as an 8-week PVI offering and is partnered with the University of San Diego Professional & Continuing Education. Elliott also leads the **Mindful Leaders Project**, an adjacent organization. The equity-oriented application is real; the corporate structure was the part that needed clarification.

### Consumer device manufacturers

The consumer device layer is the part of the ecosystem where claims most often outrun evidence. The Cat 6 evidence-tier work covers each device's intervention evidence in detail. Below we treat them as a market category — a layer of products that borrow polyvagal vocabulary without (mostly) regulatory clearance to make medical claims.

## The Money Flow

<!-- DIAGRAM: money-flow -->

Four major streams of revenue flow through the polyvagal ecosystem:

**Patents and royalties.** Porges receives royalties on SSP-related patents licensed to Unyte Health, and on licensed patent rights to NeuralSolution for PhysioCam and other Unyte-marketed technologies. He also receives author royalties from W. W. Norton on his books. The royalty arrangement is disclosed in Porges's academic publications; it is rarely surfaced in consumer-facing SSP marketing materials. This is the disclosure asymmetry we will return to.

**Course and training revenue.** PVI's ~$807K annual revenue (2024 Form 990) is the largest publicly visible single bucket. Deb Dana's Rhythm of Regulation operates separately as a commercial training business; Rebecca Kase runs Kase & CO Training and Consulting; PESI and the Embody Lab offer "polyvagal-informed" continuing-education catalogs. Across all providers, the volume of paid training-hours sold each year is substantial; precise aggregate figures are not publicly available because most providers are privately held.

**SSP / clinical-product revenue.** Therapists subscribe to Unyte to access the SSP platform; clients pay (typically through their therapist) for SSP delivery. The Unyte-to-Porges royalty pathway closes the loop here: client subscriptions → Unyte revenue → royalty share → Porges.

**Direct-to-consumer.** This is the largest dollar volume in the ecosystem, though it spans a much larger market than just polyvagal-branded products. Truvaga retails around $499. Pulsetto is in the $250–$500 range. Sensate runs about $249–$299. Apollo Neuro is in the $249–$349 range. Hapbee is subscription-based. "Vagus nerve massager" gua sha tools cost $10–$40. "Vagal tone" supplements span the price spectrum of the supplement industry. Aggregate consumer-device volume across this category alone is in the hundreds of millions of dollars annually based on company press releases and industry tracking; the broader supplement category is much larger.

None of this is illegal or unusual. Conflict of interest in commercialized scientific frameworks is common. What matters for the user is the structural pattern: **the people generating the public claims about how well the framework works are often the same people who profit when the framework remains popular.** And consumer-facing communications about evidence rarely include the conflict-of-interest disclosures that academic communications are required to include.

<!-- DIAGRAM: coi-disclosure-map -->

The asymmetry is structural. A peer-reviewed paper requires an explicit COI statement; a podcast episode where the same researcher promotes the same product has no such requirement. Most major actors in the ecosystem comply with the academic-side norm. Where the gap appears is in the consumer-facing layer — and the user encountering polyvagal content typically encounters that layer first.

## The Consumer "Vagus Nerve" Devices

<!-- DIAGRAM: consumer-devices-comparison -->

Six devices are commonly marketed in this category. Only one is FDA-cleared as a medical device.

**gammaCore** (electroCore) is the only consumer-accessible non-invasive vagus nerve stimulation device with substantive FDA clearance. It is 510(k)-cleared for acute episodic cluster headache (2017), acute migraine pain in adults (2018), preventive treatment of cluster headache (2018, the first device or drug cleared for this prevention indication), preventive treatment of adolescent migraine (ages 12–17), and paroxysmal hemicrania / hemicrania continua. It is prescription-only. The cleared indication scope matters: clearance for cluster headache does not extend to off-label use for autism, generalized anxiety, or "nervous system regulation."

**Truvaga** is the consumer-line product from the same company (electroCore) that makes gammaCore. Despite the shared corporate lineage, Truvaga itself is not FDA-cleared as a finished consumer wellness product. It markets under the FDA general wellness device framework. The hardware mechanism is similar to gammaCore's; the regulatory entity is different. Cost is approximately $499 plus subscription tiers.

**Pulsetto** is a surface electrical stimulator applied to the anterior neck. It is not FDA-cleared. It carries FCC certification, which is a separate regulatory category (radio-frequency emissions standards) that the company itself notes is not equivalent to FDA evaluation. No peer-reviewed sham-controlled RCTs in any indication exist as of 2026. The company has reported surpassing 100,000 users worldwide (a usage metric from a company press release, not an efficacy claim). Cost is in the $250–$500 range.

**Sensate** is a chest-applied infrasonic-resonance device synchronized with a headphone soundscape. It is not FDA-cleared. The mechanism is sound plus vibration; the "vagal activation" claim adds an inference layer not directly established by published independent RCTs. Cost is approximately $249–$299.

**Apollo Neuro** is a wristband that delivers low-frequency haptic vibration. The mechanism is touch-receptor stimulation rather than direct electrical vagal stimulation. It is not FDA-cleared, though it is manufactured under ISO 13485 quality standards (a manufacturing standard, distinct from FDA medical-device clearance). Apollo has more independent-looking pilot data than its peers — a 2025 *American Journal of Medicine* student RCT and University of Pittsburgh trials — but adequately-powered autism-specific endpoints remain limited. Cost is approximately $249–$349.

**Hapbee** uses ultra-low radio-frequency energy to deliver "magnetic signals" claimed to emulate the molecular effects of substances at the cellular level. It is not FDA-cleared; the company has stated its products are not medical devices. The mechanism claim is the least aligned with established neuromodulation pathways of any device in the category. Cost is subscription-based.

The pattern is clear and important. One device (gammaCore) has substantive FDA clearance for specific indications and Rx-only access. The other five operate under the general wellness device framework — which requires no clinical efficacy demonstration. Consumer-facing marketing across the category treats these products as functionally similar ("vagus nerve stimulators"). Regulatory and evidence frameworks treat them as categorically different.

> **Evidence note.** This taxonomy is consistent with the Cat 6 evidence-tier audit and was independently re-verified during this chapter's validation. None of the consumer wellness devices on this list has an adequately powered, independent, sham-controlled RCT in autism-spectrum populations. Consumers buying these products on autism-related grounds are making a wellness purchase, not a clinically validated treatment choice.

## Supplements, Massagers, and the Wider Wellness Layer

Supplements marketed with "vagal tone" framing typically include omega-3 fatty acids, magnesium, B-complex vitamins, L-theanine, and probiotics. The Massachusetts General Hospital position is direct: there is no research that points to these substances as having a specific or measurable impact on vagus nerve function or tone. A 2025 multi-species probiotic RCT (PMC12045568) did show modest morning HRV signal changes in depressed patients, but treating HRV proxy changes as direct vagal tone optimization conflates measurement with mechanism.

The conceptual issue is worth being explicit about. HRV is downstream of many things — cardiovascular fitness, sleep, hydration, recent caffeine, ambient temperature, recent stressors. Anything that reduces sympathetic load or improves baseline fitness will move HRV indices. Calling this effect "vagus nerve stimulation" obscures the actual pathway. Supplements may have benefits; the specific "vagal tone optimization" claim is marketing.

The gua sha-as-vagus-nerve-stimulation phenomenon is a clearer case of anatomy-claim failure. Cheap brass or jade tools are sold on consumer-marketplace platforms for $10–$40 with claims that gentle neck-and-face massage stimulates the deep cervical vagus nerve. McGill's Office for Science and Society and Northwell Health have both noted explicitly that superficial neck massage cannot reach the deep cervical vagus fibers in a meaningful sense. Gentle massage may produce relaxation effects through other mechanisms (general parasympathetic activation, perceived safety, attention to the body), but the "vagus nerve stimulation" branding is a specific, testable, unsupported claim.

The Instagram and TikTok ecosystem is where these claims spread fastest. #vagusnerve has accumulated more than 185 million views on TikTok alone as of 2024 reporting. The recurring content types are recognizable: "5-minute nervous system reset" reels, ear-clip device promotions, neck-tapping routines, cold-face-splash demonstrations (this one is actually supported by physiology — see Cat 6), hum-along videos, and "ladder" graphics taken out of clinical context. Polyvagal-specific vocabulary appears in coaching posts without the academic grounding that anchors it. A growing category of "polyvagal-informed coaches" operates without therapy licenses.

PVI's own scope-of-practice language acknowledges this: licensed clinicians may use the title "Applied Polyvagal Trauma Practitioner," while non-licensed individuals are advised to say "I integrate applied polyvagal trauma interventions into my practice." The distinction is real and important. Whether all non-licensed practitioners observe it is an empirical question.

## Parenting, Education, and the Conflation Pattern

<!-- DIAGRAM: parenting-education-ecosystem -->

The parenting-and-education adoption of polyvagal theory is the venue where the analytical care matters most, because well-supported attachment and trauma-informed practice shares considerable practical ground with polyvagal language. Conflating them gives the contested theory borrowed credibility.

**What is genuinely well-supported, independent of polyvagal theory:**
- Attachment theory (Bowlby, Ainsworth, and decades of subsequent research) — caregiver responsiveness in child socio-emotional development
- Trauma-informed practice in schools — the SAMHSA framework and the CDC adverse-childhood-experiences literature
- Co-regulation as a developmental concept — adult emotional/physiological state shapes infant/child state
- Mentalization-Based Treatment for parents (Fonagy and colleagues)
- Body-based emotional literacy education — broadly supported by interoception research

**What is polyvagal-specific and contested:**
- The specific claim that the ventral vagal complex is a distinct mammalian system whose activation explains social engagement
- The mapping of parent-child interaction onto a three-state ladder (ventral / sympathetic / dorsal) as if these are discrete neural states with measurable boundaries
- Specific intervention claims (e.g., SSP for school-aged children) based on the polyvagal mechanism

In "polyvagal parenting" books and trauma-informed school curricula, the well-supported and the contested often appear in the same paragraph. A typical passage might read: *"When your child melts down at the grocery store, they are not being defiant — they are in a sympathetic state, and your calm, regulated presence can help bring them back to ventral safety. Your nervous system co-regulates theirs."* The practical advice (caregiver calm helps de-escalate distressed child) is well-supported by attachment and co-regulation research, predating polyvagal theory. The mechanism story ("sympathetic state," "ventral safety," "your nervous system co-regulates theirs") is polyvagal-specific. The reader cannot tell which part is doing the explanatory work.

The diagnostic question is: **strip out the polyvagal vocabulary — does the advice still hold?** For most well-functioning polyvagal-parenting recommendations, yes. The practices are good. The mechanism story is the part that depends on a contested theory. The reader who finds practical advice helpful and extends credibility to the underlying framework is making an inference the framework hasn't earned.

This is a structural pattern, not an indictment of the authors who do this work. Bonnie Badenoch's relational integration of attachment, interpersonal neurobiology, mirror neurons, polyvagal theory, and memory reconsolidation may produce genuinely useful clinical practice even when individual theoretical inputs are contested. The integration's *value* may not be reducible to any single theoretical input's *correctness*. But the user should not extend credibility from the value of the practice to the truth of any specific framework claim.

## How the Theory Spread — Five Stages

<!-- DIAGRAM: spread-funnel -->

The migration from neuroscience journal to TikTok reel happened in five identifiable stages. Each stage borrows credibility from the stage above it. Each stage loses hedging. Each stage gains reach.

**Stage 1 — Academic publication.** Porges's 1994 *Psychophysiology* address and the subsequent specialty-journal record. Reading audience: specialists. Reach: hundreds of peer-reviewed papers. Register: hedged, specific, falsifiable.

**Stage 2 — Trade-book translation.** Van der Kolk in 2014, Dana in 2018 and onward, Bridges in 2015, Kase in 2023, Inderbitzen in 2024. Reading audience: clinicians, parents, curious lay readers. Reach: tens of millions of trade-book sales. Register: orientation, accessible language. The academic hedging gets softened for narrative flow.

**Stage 3 — Clinical and coaching training.** PVI's certificate course (~$800K annual revenue), Rhythm of Regulation, Kase & CO, PESI continuing education, Embody Lab, and a long tail of "polyvagal therapy practitioner" certifications of varying rigor. Reading audience: practitioners. Reach: hundreds of thousands of trained individuals. Register: applied, methodology-focused.

**Stage 4 — Social media migration.** Instagram therapist accounts, TikTok wellness content, podcast wellness episodes. Reading audience: general public seeking quick answers. Reach: hundreds of millions of impressions. Register: certainty without evidence.

**Stage 5 — Consumer products.** Consumer "vagus nerve" devices, "vagal tone" supplements, gua sha tools, books promising "rewire your nervous system in 8 weeks," coach-led 1:1 sessions. Reading audience: consumers with disposable income. Reach: hundreds of millions of dollars annually in direct-to-consumer revenue. Register: marketing claims.

The compounding pattern is this: each stage cites the stage above as warrant. The trade book cites the academic paper. The training cites the trade book. The social media post simplifies the training. The consumer device's marketing materials cite the social media reach as evidence of demand. By Stage 5, the Stage 1 hedging is invisible, but the Stage 1 academic citation is still being invoked. The 2023–2026 academic controversy — which casts substantive doubt on the Stage 1 foundation — has not propagated down through the stages. **A polyvagal-branded consumer product sold in May 2026 may still implicitly rest on premises that 38 international experts argued were falsified earlier the same year.**

## Concrete Red-Flag Examples

<!-- DIAGRAM: red-flags-gallery -->

The chapter's most useful concrete artifact is a gallery of ten specific patterns the user should recognize on sight. The full descriptions are in Diagram 5; in summary:

1. **Autism "cure" or "elimination" via polyvagal interventions.** Contradicts autism research consensus; pathologizing; not supported by the small autism-specific evidence base.
2. **"Rewire your nervous system in [short time]" claims.** The nervous system does change; not on marketing timelines.
3. **"Polyvagal" or "vagal tone" branded supplements.** Mass General Hospital position: no research points to specific vagal tone effects.
4. **Practitioners "diagnosing" dorsal/ventral vagal states without measurement.** Scope creep; metaphor presented as diagnosis.
5. **"Vagus nerve" devices with no FDA clearance and no published sham-controlled RCT.** General wellness framing presented as clinical efficacy.
6. **Coaches without clinical training offering polyvagal "therapy."** Outside scope; outside PVI's own guidelines.
7. **Gua sha / "vagus nerve massagers" with surface-skin-to-deep-nerve mechanism claims.** Anatomically not supported.
8. **Claims omitting conflict-of-interest disclosure.** Disclosure asymmetry between academic and consumer-facing communications.
9. **Contested mechanism conflated with well-supported practice.** Borrowed credibility; the diagnostic is "strip out polyvagal vocabulary."
10. **"Stuck in dorsal vagal" as a catch-all explanation.** Unfalsifiable framing that substitutes metaphor for diagnosis.

None of these should be the user's only signal. Clinicians make mistakes; products evolve; popularizers update framing. But seeing two or three of these in the same source is grounds to deepen scrutiny.

## The Practitioner-Evaluation Checklist (The Reusable Tool)

<!-- DIAGRAM: practitioner-checklist -->

The chapter's takeaway tool is an 8-question checklist (Diagram 6, designed to be print-friendly) that generalizes beyond polyvagal claims to any wellness or psychiatric claim:

1. **Mechanism vs. experience.** Is the claim a specific neural mechanism or a phenomenological experience? Experience claims are less load-bearing and less falsifiable but also less likely to mislead.
2. **Citations.** Are specific peer-reviewed studies cited, or only testimonials? Do the cited studies actually support the claim?
3. **FDA status (for devices).** Cleared? For what indication? Or "general wellness device" (no clinical efficacy demonstration)?
4. **Scope of practice.** Is the practitioner a licensed clinician working within license, or a coach being clear they don't diagnose or treat?
5. **Register.** Is "polyvagal-informed" treated as an orientation, or as established biological mechanism?
6. **Conflict of interest disclosure.** Are financial interests disclosed if the practitioner sells products, courses, or supplements?
7. **Falsifiability.** Could the claim ever be wrong? What evidence would the practitioner accept as falsifying it?
8. **Engagement with uncertainty.** Does the practitioner acknowledge the 2023–2026 controversy about polyvagal theory, or operate as if the theory is settled?

A "yes" or "good answer" on most questions doesn't certify effectiveness — therapy is hard to evaluate from credentials alone. A "no" or "concerning answer" on most questions is grounds to look elsewhere. Two or three concerning answers in combination is a stronger signal than any single one.

## Common Misconceptions

### Misconception: "If a product is sold as 'polyvagal,' it must have a scientific basis."

**Correction**: "Polyvagal" is not a regulated term. Anyone can use it on a product, course, or marketing page. The word's appearance tells you about positioning, not evidence.

### Misconception: "FDA clearance means a device works."

**Correction**: FDA clearance via 510(k) indicates substantial equivalence to a predicate device for specified indications. It is not certification of efficacy for general wellness use. Most consumer "vagus nerve" devices in this chapter are sold under the FDA general wellness framework, which requires no FDA evaluation at all.

> **Correction**: The Cat 6 validation work re-verified every FDA-status claim in this chapter against manufacturer disclosures and third-party reviews. The taxonomy stands: only gammaCore is FDA-cleared as a medical device, for specific indications, by prescription.

### Misconception: "Non-profit status means the organization is impartial."

**Correction**: 501(c)(3) is a tax-status category. It implies non-distribution of profits to owners. It does not imply the organization is impartial about the framework it teaches. PVI's mission is to advance polyvagal theory's application; that is its declared purpose, and non-profit status is compatible with it.

### Misconception: "If the practitioner has a license, the polyvagal advice is reliable by extension."

**Correction**: A license certifies training in a regulated profession (therapist, OT, SLP, MD, RN). It does not certify any particular theoretical framework. A licensed therapist can adopt a contested theory; the license doesn't validate the theory.

### Misconception: "Wellness products are harmless even if they don't work."

**Correction**: Beyond financial cost, opportunity cost is real. For an autistic adult navigating burnout, time and energy are limited. Forgone evidence-based options matter. Additionally, some practices in this space are not harmless: Wim Hof method combined with water immersion has 32+ documented deaths; brain-retraining programs have been associated with iatrogenic harm in ME/CFS populations (Cat 10).

### Misconception: "Critics of the wellness application are critics of the theory itself."

**Correction**: One can hold a calibrated view of polyvagal theory (contested mechanism, some clinical utility) while being skeptical of the wellness-industry deployment of its language. These are separate critiques. The chapter's stance is the second one specifically.

## Edge Cases and Sympathetic Readings

A few cases deserve careful handling, because the simplest critique is uncharitable:

- **The legitimate-utility case.** A clinician using polyvagal vocabulary as patient-friendly language for autonomic dysregulation, with no theory-as-mechanism load-bearing, is doing useful translation work. Many patients find the language helpful even if the underlying neuroanatomy is wrong. This is a defensible use.
- **The self-directed-curious case.** A user who reads widely in the trauma literature can benefit from polyvagal-framed materials' phenomenological descriptions while remaining agnostic about the mechanism. This is also defensible.
- **The diagnostic-validation case.** For someone who has long felt that their autonomic state shifts in ways they could not name, encountering even a contested framework that names the experience can be transformative — independent of whether the framework is biologically correct. This is real psychological value.
- **The lived-experience-from-an-autistic-author case.** Inderbitzen's autism-in-polyvagal-terms work comes from an autistic author. The pathologizing framing the autistic community has criticized (autism as a deficit of social engagement) is not the only autistic engagement with polyvagal theory. Both critique and adoption from autistic writers exist; both are legitimate positions.

These edge cases don't undermine the chapter's argument. They specify it: the issue is not polyvagal language per se, it is overreach in the wellness-industry register, and the conflation of well-supported practice with contested mechanism.

## Summary

The polyvagal theory's institutional ecosystem includes one major non-profit (PVI, ~$807K annual revenue), one anchor for-profit company (Unyte Health, commercial home of SSP since 2017), a popularizer chain concentrated in a small number of trade-publisher imprints (W. W. Norton's series especially), a "polyvagal-informed" training and coaching layer, and a consumer-product layer marketing devices and supplements that mostly operate under the FDA general wellness framework rather than as cleared medical devices.

The chapter's central analytical tool is **the three registers**: the same vocabulary appears in academic (contested), clinical (mixed evidence, some real value), and wellness/influencer (often pseudoscientific) registers, with orders-of-magnitude variation in epistemic weight. The user's most useful filter is to ask which register a given claim comes from.

The structural pattern the user should expect to see is **disclosure asymmetry** — financial interests are typically disclosed in academic publications but rarely surfaced in consumer-facing communications. The structural pattern in parenting and education applications is **conflation** of well-supported attachment / co-regulation practice with contested polyvagal-specific mechanism claims; the diagnostic is "strip out polyvagal vocabulary — does the advice still hold?"

The chapter's two takeaway tools are the **10-pattern red-flag gallery** (Diagram 5) and the **8-question practitioner-evaluation checklist** (Diagram 6). They are calibrated against documented overreach patterns but generalize beyond polyvagal claims to any wellness or psychiatric claim.

Many people working in this space are sincerely trying to help. Some products and practices in the ecosystem produce real benefit. The point of the chapter is not to invalidate the framework or impugn motives. It is to give the user a working set of filters — register, money flow, scope, disclosure, falsifiability, and engagement with uncertainty — so that they can navigate the 120,000+ Google results without depending on any single authority to do the curating for them.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Polyvagal Institute (PVI)** | 501(c)(3) non-profit founded 2020 by Porges, Dana, Onderko, Redfield. EIN 84-5084757; Denver, CO. Annual revenue ~$807K (2024). Offers paid certificate courses and the "Polyvagal-Informed" credential. |
| **Unyte Health** | For-profit private company, formerly Integrated Listening Systems. Commercial home of the Safe and Sound Protocol (SSP). Porges is Chief Scientific Advisor. Dana is an advisor. |
| **Safe and Sound Protocol (SSP)** | Filtered-music intervention developed and patented by Porges, commercialized through Unyte since 2017. Subscription-based; delivered through trained professionals. |
| **Rhythm of Regulation®** | Deb Dana's trademarked clinical framework for translating polyvagal theory into therapy practice. Independent commercial training operation. |
| **"Polyvagal-Informed" credential** | Designation awarded by PVI on certificate-course completion. Permits self-identification on professional materials. Distinct from a therapy license. |
| **General wellness device framework (FDA)** | A regulatory category that exempts low-risk wellness devices from clearance requirements. Most consumer "vagus nerve" devices market under this framework. "General wellness device" is not clinical efficacy. |
| **510(k) clearance** | FDA pathway indicating substantial equivalence to a predicate device for specified indications. Distinct from FDA "approval." |
| **Vagal tone** | Informal term referring to cardiac vagal control. Operationalized via HRV indices (particularly high-frequency HRV / RSA). The term has become a wellness-marketing slogan often used without measurement. |
| **Conflict of interest (COI) disclosure** | A statement of financial or institutional interests that could plausibly influence a researcher's, practitioner's, or author's claims. Required in academic publications; often absent in consumer-facing materials. |
| **Scope of practice** | The professional activities a credential or license authorizes. A licensed therapist's scope includes diagnosis and treatment; a coach's typically does not. Crossing this boundary is "scope creep." |
| **The three registers** | The chapter's central analytical tool: academic, clinical, and wellness/influencer uses of polyvagal vocabulary. |
| **Polyvagal-informed coach** | A non-licensed practitioner who integrates polyvagal vocabulary into coaching. Legitimate when scope is respected. |
