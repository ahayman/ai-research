# When Awareness Training Backfires — Iatrogenic Harm and Contraindications

## Framing this category

A casual reading of the body-focused mindfulness literature produces two conflicting impressions. The first is that mindfulness, body-scan practice, somatic experiencing, and "tune in to your body" approaches are the default first-line response to alexithymia and to trauma. The second is that the same body of practices carries non-trivial documented harm: between roughly a third and half of meditators in measured samples report meaningful adverse effects, the rates spike in trauma-exposed populations, and clinicians have published explicit cautions for somatic-symptom disorder, dissociative-subtype PTSD, and parsing-deficit alexithymia profiles.

Both impressions are partly true. This category's job is to render them coherent without overclaiming on either side, and to give the user an evidence-grounded basis for evaluating, modifying, or declining recommended interventions when the recommendation does not match their architecture.

There is one foundational distinction this category honours throughout. **The harm literature for body-focused / interoception-based interventions is real and well-documented for general populations. The harm literature SPECIFICALLY FOR ALEXITHYMIA SUBTYPES is essentially absent.** What exists for the alexithymia case is theoretical extrapolation from the meditation-related-adverse-effects literature, the somatosensory-amplification literature, and Treleaven's trauma-sensitive mindfulness framework — combined with the parsing-deficit-versus-detection-deficit framework from the interoception literature. Norman et al.'s 2019 systematic review of mindfulness for alexithymia explicitly notes the absence of adverse-event data from the four trials it reviewed.

This is the kind of evidence gap that gets papered over in many syntheses. This category does not paper over it. Instead, every major claim in this category gets labelled by evidence tier:

- **Tier 1** — well-established. Direct empirical evidence from measured samples; replicated; uncontested at the order-of-magnitude level.
- **Tier 2** — mechanistically supported. The mechanism is well-attested in adjacent literatures and is plausibly at work in the alexithymia case, but the alexithymia-stratified data does not exist to test it directly.
- **Tier 3** — theoretical only. The synthesis of Tier 1 + Tier 2 leads to a specific subtype-stratified prediction, but no published study has tested it directly.

What the user gets out of this category is three actionable things. A way to evaluate whether a body-focused recommendation matches their profile. A way to monitor for iatrogenic deterioration if they proceed. A way to redirect to alternatives if the profile mismatch is serious. Plus the conceptual scaffolding to distinguish "I am bad at this therapy" — often wrongful self-blame — from "this therapy is wrongly matched to my subtype."

## The MRAE rate question — corrected

The first thing the validator flagged in this category was numerical attribution. The parent exploration's first draft cited "20-25 per cent of meditators reporting particularly bad or frightening experiences" and attributed this to Britton 2021. Both halves of that claim need correction.

The 20-25 per cent figure was the field's *a priori* expectation threshold — not Britton's measured rate. It is an artefact of how mindfulness research approached harm before Britton's measurement framework was developed. Both Britton's measured rates and Goldberg's measured rates *exceed* that threshold.

The second issue is that early summaries blended Britton 2021 and Goldberg 2022 figures as if they came from one study. They did not.

**Britton, Lindahl, Cooper, Canby, and Palitsky (2021)** — published in *Clinical Psychological Science* and indexed at PMC8845498 — defined the MRAES-MBP measurement framework. The acronym stands for Meditation-Related Adverse Effects Scale for Mindfulness-Based Programs. The study sample was drawn from MBCT (Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy) trials, with N approximately 96. The measurement instrument is sensitive — it captures mild and transient experiences as well as severe ones. Britton's measured rates: 83 per cent reported any meditation-related side effect; 58 per cent reported negatively-valenced effects; 37 per cent reported negative impact on functioning; 14.1 per cent reported effects lasting longer than one day; 6.4 per cent reported effects lasting longer than one month.

The high 83 per cent "any side effect" figure is partly a function of using a sensitive instrument. The 14.1 per cent and 6.4 per cent duration figures are the more clinically conservative anchor from the same dataset. The 58 per cent / 37 per cent figures speak to valence and functional impact respectively.

**Goldberg, Lam, Britton, and Davidson (2022)** — published in *Psychotherapy Research* and indexed at PMC8636531 / PubMed 34074221 — is a different study with different methodology. A population-based US sample with lifetime meditation exposure, N=434. The methodology differs fundamentally: a population-based survey of people with any prior meditation experience rather than a clinical-trial sample. Goldberg's measured rates: 32.3 per cent general-item MRAE rate (a single screening item); 50.0 per cent one-or-more-specific-item rate (across a detailed checklist); 10.4 per cent MRAE duration of one month or longer; 1.2 per cent functional impairment lasting one month or longer.

Goldberg also reported the most common adverse-effect types: anxiety, traumatic re-experiencing, and emotional sensitivity. And the prospectively-identified risk factor: childhood adversity.

<div class="diagram-figure-marker">[Diagram 2 here — Britton vs Goldberg]</div>

The honest reading: **cite Britton's figures and Goldberg's figures separately. Do not conflate.** Both studies' measured rates exceed the 20-25 per cent field-expectation threshold. The duration-and-impairment figures across the two studies — Britton's 14.1 per cent / longer than a day and 6.4 per cent / longer than a month, Goldberg's 10.4 per cent / longer than a month and 1.2 per cent / functional impairment longer than a month — provide the most clinically conservative anchors. The high "any side effect" rates capture a wider net.

A defensible composite for narrative use: between roughly a third and half of meditators in measured samples report some meaningful adverse effect; roughly 6-15 per cent report effects lasting longer than a day to a month, depending on study and threshold; roughly 1 per cent report functional impairment lasting longer than a month. Anxiety, traumatic re-experiencing, and emotional sensitivity are among the most common types. Childhood adversity is a documented risk factor.

A separate landmark paper from this lineage is **Lindahl, Fisher, Cooper, Rosen, and Britton's 2017 Varieties of Contemplative Experience study** at PMC5443484. This is a qualitative anchor. The team developed a 59-category taxonomy of meditation-related experiences across seven domains: cognitive, perceptual, affective, somatic, conative, sense of self, and social. Among 32 expert Western-Buddhist teachers in the sample, 56 of 59 categories — 95 per cent of the taxonomy — were reported, with an average of 8.5 categories per expert.

Two important framings around this finding. First, the Lindahl 2017 taxonomy does not stratify by alexithymia subgroup. It cannot directly speak to alexithymia-specific harm. It speaks to the breadth and seriousness of meditation-related experience in a population of *experts* — presumably the population least at risk and most able to navigate the practices. Second, the breadth of categories reported even by experts is itself an argument that the practices are not adverse-effect-free for any population, including the most-prepared.

All three studies — Britton 2021, Goldberg 2022, Lindahl 2017 — sit firmly in **Tier 1**. The findings are well-established, replicated, and uncontested at the order-of-magnitude level.

## Mechanism of harm

Underneath the prevalence figures is a mechanism question: when body-focused practice produces iatrogenic deterioration rather than benefit, what is the mechanism? The answer is not single. There are at least four well-attested mechanism families, each documented in adjacent literatures.

<div class="diagram-figure-marker">[Diagram 3 here — Mechanisms of Harm]</div>

The first is **somatosensory amplification (SSA)**, originally framed in Barsky's work on somatic-symptom-disorder populations. The mechanism: attention to a low-intensity body sensation increases its perceived salience, increases interpretation-as-threat, increases physiological reactivity, increases sensation, and loops. In somatic-symptom-disorder populations this is the central mechanism of chronic illness anxiety. Reduce body-attention focus and the loop attenuates. Add body-attention focus — including via body-scan mindfulness — and the loop intensifies. The empirical support for this mechanism in somatic-symptom-disorder populations is strong. The mechanism's application to parsing-deficit alexithymia is not directly tested but is structurally coherent: if the alexithymic individual already has heightened interoceptive sensibility (signals are louder than baseline) but lacks the labelling architecture to translate signals into nameable affect, then sustained body-focus practice may amplify already-loud signals into unstructured distress. This is **Tier 2** evidence — strong mechanism, untested in alexithymia subgroups directly.

The second mechanism is **panic induction**. Body sensations — palpitations, dyspnea, dizziness, tingling — can trigger panic in panic-prone individuals via the catastrophic-misinterpretation model formalised by Clark in 1986 and subsequently elaborated by Barlow and Craske. The same sensations that interoceptive-exposure protocols target deliberately in panic-disorder treatment can trigger uncued panic in panic-prone individuals during open-ended body-scan practice. The asymmetry between these two contexts — therapeutic in one, harm-inducing in another — is itself important and gets a full treatment later in this category.

The third mechanism is **depersonalisation deepening**. Depersonalisation and derealisation are relatively common in dissociative-subtype PTSD, in dissociative disorders proper, and in some anxiety presentations. There is published clinical literature documenting that mindfulness can deepen rather than relieve depersonalisation in already-dissociating individuals. Lindahl, Britton, and colleagues published a specific paper on the nuanced mindfulness-depersonalisation relationship at PMC9043097, arguing that the relationship is bidirectional. In some populations, mindfulness reduces depersonalisation by re-grounding attention in the body. In others, mindfulness deepens depersonalisation by intensifying observer-stance and detachment from embodied experience. The clinical task is identifying which subgroup the individual sits in *before* prescribing the practice.

The fourth mechanism is **trauma re-experiencing**. Body-focus can trigger trauma memory and sensation re-experiencing in trauma-exposed individuals. This is the central concern that Treleaven's *Trauma-Sensitive Mindfulness* framework was developed to address. Lanius and colleagues' 2024 work on choice points for dissociative-subtype PTSD (PMC11257171) provides further structured clinical guidance for navigating this in trauma populations specifically.

These mechanisms do not operate in isolation. A parsing-deficit alexithymic with trauma history and somatic-symptom comorbidity sits at the intersection of all four. Body-focus may simultaneously amplify already-loud signals, trigger panic if those signals are catastrophically interpreted, deepen depersonalisation if dissociation is already present, and surface trauma re-experiencing. The mechanisms are partly overlapping pathways through which the same intervention can produce iatrogenic deterioration in the same individual.

## Why parsing-deficit alexithymia is not detection-deficit alexithymia

The single most important conceptual distinction in this category. Without it, the "body-focused work for alexithymia" debate collapses into incoherence, because the same intervention has *opposite-direction* predictions for the two profiles.

Detection-deficit alexithymia presents like this: body signals are absent, low-amplitude, or below subjective threshold. The person reports "I don't feel anything in my body when I'm asked to check." Self-reported interoceptive sensibility on instruments like the MAIA or the SAQ is low. Heartbeat-detection performance is at or near chance. The deficit is in detecting the signal in the first place.

For this profile, **the standard recommendation applies**. Interoceptive-awareness training is the appropriate first-line intervention because the training builds detection capacity that is genuinely missing. Mindful Awareness in Body-Oriented Therapy (Price and Hooven 2018, PMC5985305), MBSR adapted for low-interoception, and structured heartbeat-detection training are the protocols of record. Quadt and colleagues' 2021 ADIE trial (Lancet eClinicalMedicine) — interoceptive training for anxiety in autistic adults — showed positive results in this kind of profile. This is important counter-evidence to any strong "body-focused work always harms parsing-deficit alexithymics" claim. Body-focused work has population-specific value where the underlying architecture matches.

Parsing-deficit alexithymia presents like this: body signals are present, often loud or hypervigilantly attended, but uninterpretable as nameable affect. The person reports "I can feel my heart racing and my chest tight, but I don't know what I'm feeling — angry? scared? excited? It's just intensity." Self-reported interoceptive sensibility may be elevated. Heartbeat-detection performance can be at or above chance. The deficit is downstream of detection — in interpreting and labelling what is detected.

For this profile, **the standard recommendation breaks down**. More body-focus on already-loud signals risks amplifying the unstructured distress that the parsing failure produces. The Tier 2 mechanism evidence — Barsky's SSA, Lindahl on depersonalisation, Treleaven on trauma re-experiencing — all converges on this prediction.

Aaron, Blain, Snodgress, and Park's 2020 pilot at PMC7076086 reported a quadratic relationship between alexithymia and interoceptive accuracy: both very-low and very-high IA can co-occur with high alexithymia. The very-low-IA pole approximates the detection-deficit profile. The very-high-IA pole approximates the parsing-deficit / hypervigilant profile. *Both* poles have elevated alexithymia. The same study reported that body-scan-style mindfulness produced different responses across the IA distribution — suggestive of a moderation effect but the study is pilot only. Direct empirical support for the dissociation between detection and parsing as separate failure modes exists. Direct empirical support for the subtype-by-protocol moderation prediction is suggestive but not definitive.

A specific subtype of parsing-deficit deserves separate attention: **hypervigilance with mislabelling**. The person already attends to body signals at elevated rates — common in anxiety with somatic focus, in chronic-pain populations, in panic-prone individuals, in somatic-symptom disorder. They mislabel the signals, attributing them to threat, illness, or imminent loss-of-control. Adding body-focus practice deposits *more* attention onto an already over-attended channel. The mechanistic prediction is symptom amplification. The hypervigilance-with-mislabelling profile is the highest-caution subgroup for body-focused interoceptive work in the framework that follows.

## The "alexithymic patient bored / closed off in therapy" narrative

A theme that runs through alliance literature and clinical case-discussion: the alexithymic patient is described as bored, closed off, stiff, somatic-only, low-affect, hard-to-engage. The narrative frames it as a *patient-side problem*. Ogrodniczuk, Piper, and Joyce's 2020 paper at PMC7451330 documents the alliance-quality gap in high-alexithymia populations.

But the same data permit a *therapy-mismatch* reading. The clinician's protocol assumes emotional engagement that the patient cannot currently provide. The patient is not refusing; their architecture cannot generate the inputs the protocol requires. The "bored / closed off" presentation is a *signal of mismatch*, not a sign of pathology.

This reframing is clinically important because it relocates the corrective from "the patient must change" to "the protocol is wrongly matched to the architecture." The user's stated goal — distinguishing "I'm bad at this therapy" from "this therapy is wrongly matched to my subtype" — is exactly this reframing.

The implication: **distinguishing patient pathology from wrongly-matched therapy is itself a clinical skill**. It is not the default reading in standard supervision frames. The default reading attributes treatment-resistance to patient factors. The therapy-mismatch reading attributes the same data to protocol factors. Both readings need consideration; the second is systematically under-emphasised.

For the user, this matters because it grants permission to take treatment-aversion seriously as data about protocol fit rather than as data about personal failing. If you are doing the practice as instructed, showing up consistently, not avoiding, and finding it iteratively worse rather than better — the wrongful-self-blame reading ("I must be doing it wrong") is one possibility, but it is not the only one. Profile-mismatch is another. The decision framework that follows gives a structure for evaluating which reading is more likely.

## The decision framework — who should not start with body-focused work

This is the most clinically actionable section of the category. A framework for matching profile to caution-level when evaluating any body-focused or interoceptive recommendation. The framework is a clinical-decision aid for *evaluation*, not a definitive contraindication list. The user (or clinician) reviews the profile and assesses caution-level, then makes the protocol decision in light of the assessment.

<div class="diagram-figure-marker">[Diagram 4 here — Decision Framework]</div>

The framework has six profiles arranged from highest caution to lowest:

A **hypervigilance-with-mislabelling** profile — somatic-symptom disorder, anxiety with somatic focus, chronic pain with somatic vigilance — sits at high caution. Body-attention amplifies already-loud signals. The somatosensory-amplification mechanism applies directly. External / cognitive-first alternatives deserve consideration before any body-focused work.

A **dissociative-subtype PTSD** profile — PTSD with marked depersonalisation or derealisation — also sits at high caution. Body-focus can deepen depersonalisation; the Lanius choice-points framework applies directly. Stabilisation and felt-sense anchoring before any open-ended body-focus.

A **high trauma load with low integration capacity** profile — PTSD or CPTSD with poor affect-tolerance, no felt-sense anchor, limited support — also sits at high caution. Body-focus can trigger trauma re-experiencing without containment. Treleaven's 36 modifications apply. The phase-based protocol consensus — stabilise, then build interoceptive parsing skills, then process trauma narratively, then integrate — is the harm-reduction pathway. Direct exposure or unmodified body-focus before stabilisation is the iatrogenic-harm pathway.

A **parsing-deficit alexithymia** profile — signals detected, often loud, can't interpret as nameable affect — sits at moderate caution. The Tier 2 mechanism prediction applies; subtype-stratified RCT evidence does not exist. Consider external-vocabulary scaffolding (granularity-building, affect-labelling, structured journaling) before internal-introspection-first protocols. Monitor markers of deterioration if proceeding with body-focused work.

A **detection-deficit alexithymia** profile — signals not detected; low interoceptive sensibility — sits at low caution. The standard recommendation applies. Interoceptive-awareness training builds genuinely missing detection capacity. The Quadt 2021 ADIE trial in autistic adults showed positive outcomes in this profile.

A profile with **no trauma load, no somatic-symptom complications, mid-range interoception** falls at standard caution. The Tier 1 MRAE risk applies in any population — non-trivial baseline rates of adverse effects across measured samples — but no specific contraindication.

How to use this with a clinician: if a therapist recommends body-scan, interoceptive-awareness, or open-ended mindfulness without first asking which profile you sit in, that is a signal of a one-size-fits-all protocol rather than a profile-matched one. A reasonable conversation: "I have [profile]. The mechanism research suggests this profile may not be the best match for body-focused practice. Can we talk about external-vocabulary alternatives like granularity-building or affect-labelling first?" The framework gives you the language for the conversation.

The honest framing of what this framework is and is not: the framework codifies clinical reasoning that is well-supported in adjacent literatures but extrapolated to the alexithymia case. It does not say "you must not do X." It says "for this profile, the mechanism case for caution is plausible enough that a profile-matched alternative deserves consideration first." Decision authority remains with the user. Protocol authority remains with the clinician. The framework's job is to make the profile-matching question explicit rather than letting it default to a protocol that may not fit.

## Markers of iatrogenic deterioration to watch for

If you proceed with body-focused work and need to monitor, these are the signals that the protocol is producing iatrogenic deterioration rather than benefit. None are alexithymia-specific. They apply to any of the at-risk profiles in the decision framework.

<div class="diagram-figure-marker">[Diagram 5 here — Markers of Deterioration]</div>

Symptom amplification over weeks of practice. Sleep worse, anxiety worse, pain worse, somatic symptoms worse over a sustained period. Not transient discomfort during a session — iterative deepening across weeks. The somatosensory-amplification mechanism in action.

Increased depersonalisation or derealisation. More "I'm not in my body," more "this isn't real," more "I'm watching myself from outside." More observer-distance, less embodied presence. Especially concerning if depersonalisation was already present before practice started.

New-onset somatic symptoms. Body symptoms emerging during a practice period that were not present before. New chronic pain, new gastrointestinal complaints, new cardiac sensations, new fatigue patterns. Body-attention-driven amplification can produce symptoms that were sub-threshold before sustained focus brought them into salience.

Increased shutdowns or dissociation. More freezing, more zoning-out, more time-loss. Especially concerning in trauma-history populations where dissociation is already a coping pattern.

Increased intrusive trauma re-experiencing. More flashbacks, more nightmares, more body-memory floods. Especially concerning if practice is unstructured and open-ended rather than trauma-sensitive-modified per Treleaven.

Treatment-aversion despite genuine engagement effort. You are doing the practice as instructed. You are not avoiding. You are showing up. And it is iteratively worse, not better. The wrongful-self-blame reading is one possibility. Profile-mismatch is another, and worth taking seriously.

Increased emotional sensitivity or overwhelm. Affective signal becoming louder without becoming more legible. More flooding, less capacity to navigate. Goldberg 2022 documents emotional sensitivity as among the most common MRAE types — and it can present as either useful (more granular access) or harmful (overwhelm without skill scaffolding).

Loss of ground-state stability. Day-to-day functional baseline shifting downward. More days where you cannot do tasks you used to manage. More relational rupture. More avoidance. Practice that is doing its job should make ground-state more stable, not less.

The critical reframe: **when these markers appear, the protocol is the variable, not you**. The default reading from many supervision frames is "the patient must keep going / try harder / surrender into the practice." The iatrogenic-harm reading is "the protocol is wrongly matched and continuing it deepens the harm." Both readings need consideration; the second is systematically under-emphasised.

What to do when markers appear: reduce duration first; change attention object (move to external anchors — sound, sight, contact-with-floor); pause the practice (a break of weeks to months is profile-responsive titration, not failure); switch modalities to external-vocabulary alternatives; reassess the profile (did you assume detection-deficit when actually parsing-deficit; did you assume no trauma load when there is one); and consult with someone trained in trauma-sensitive mindfulness who has the framework to titrate rather than abandon.

## Treleaven's framework, and the alexithymia extrapolation

Treleaven and Britton's *Trauma-Sensitive Mindfulness: Practices for Safe and Transformative Healing* (W.W. Norton, 2018) is the foundational practical text for this category. It provides 36 specific modifications for trauma-survivor practitioners across five principles: stay within the window of tolerance; shift attention to support stability; keep the body in mind through gradual titration; support relationship; understand social context.

<div class="diagram-figure-marker">[Diagram 7 here — Treleaven and the Alexithymia Extrapolation]</div>

Critical for this category's honesty: **Treleaven's framework is trauma-focused, not alexithymia-focused**. Alexithymia is not directly named as a contraindication or as a modifiable factor. The downstream parsing-deficit-alexithymia caution is an *extrapolation* from Treleaven's trauma-focused framework, motivated by the architectural overlap between high-alexithymia trauma profiles and the trauma profiles Treleaven addresses directly.

The extrapolation is plausible. Three points of architectural overlap support the mapping. First, high-alexithymia populations often carry trauma history — the 2026 meta-analysis on alexithymia in PTSD pooled the prevalence at 53 per cent (95% CI 42-65%). Second, the parsing-deficit profile shares the "loud signal, no label" architecture that Treleaven's framework is designed to titrate around. Third, the Tier 2 mechanism support — somatosensory amplification, depersonalisation deepening, panic induction, trauma re-experiencing — is the same mechanism family Treleaven's modifications address.

But plausibility is not direct evidence. The honest reading: Treleaven gives well-structured guidance for any profile where body-focus may overwhelm capacity. Whether the modifications transfer cleanly to specific alexithymia subgroups has not been formally tested.

In practice, this means: a clinician trained in trauma-sensitive mindfulness — Treleaven offers a certification programme — has the framework to titrate body-focused practice around the at-risk profiles in the decision framework. They may not have explicit alexithymia-specific protocols (that literature does not yet exist), but the trauma-modifications transfer plausibly. A clinician without trauma-sensitive-mindfulness training applying standard MBSR or MBCT to a high-caution profile is the iatrogenic-harm pathway. The training matters; the framework matters; the explicit protocol modifications matter; the assumption that "everyone benefits from mindfulness" is exactly what Treleaven's work is designed to correct.

## Iatrogenic harm in trauma therapy specifically

A specific application of the general framework. Body-focused trauma therapies — Somatic Experiencing developed by Levine, Sensorimotor Psychotherapy developed by Ogden, body-based variants of EMDR — are simultaneously *promoted* as alexithymia-friendly (because they bypass language) AND identified as carrying *amplified risk* in alexithymic patients (because the parsing-deficit version means body signals are detected but mislabelled, often as catastrophic).

The risk profile for alexithymic trauma patients spans three intervention types. Standard exposure protocols (Prolonged Exposure, Cognitive Processing Therapy, trauma-focused CBT) produce desensitization without integration in this population, with possible chronic stuck-ness. Pugach and colleagues at PubMed 37917810 found that high-alexithymia patients were more likely to retain a full PTSD diagnosis at post-treatment and at six-month follow-up; alexithymia was associated with delayed treatment effects on avoidance; and high-alexithymia groups showed marginally lesser magnitude on negative changes in thinking and mood. Body-focused work without interoceptive scaffolding produces amplified somatic signal interpreted catastrophically, with possible re-experiencing or dissociative episodes. Mindfulness without trauma-sensitivity modifications produces re-experiencing and dissociation. Treleaven's framework was developed precisely for this last problem.

The phase-based protocol consensus — emerging across STAIR-NT (Cloitre lineage), Phase-Based CPTSD treatment, Treleaven's trauma-sensitive mindfulness, and Lanius's sensory-pathways model — is: stabilise, then build interoceptive parsing skills, then process trauma narratively, then integrate. Direct exposure or unmodified body-focus before stabilisation and parsing-skill-building is the iatrogenic-harm pathway.

The full architecture for the alexithymia-trauma case sits in **Cat 7 — Alexithymia ↔ Trauma — Bidirectional Mechanisms**. This category establishes the harm pathway in trauma therapy. Cat 7 establishes the bidirectional mechanism architecture that explains why alexithymia and trauma so often co-occur and why standard protocols often fail this population.

## The interoceptive-exposure counter-example

Interoceptive exposure (IE) in panic disorder is therapeutic. It deliberately induces panic-related body sensations and works. This is sometimes cited as refuting the "body-focus is harmful" framing. The counter-example is real and the question deserves direct engagement.

<div class="diagram-figure-marker">[Diagram 8 here — IE Counter-Example]</div>

IE in panic disorder is structured. Specific induction protocols: chair-spinning for dizziness, straw-breathing for dyspnea, stair-climbing for palpitations, hyperventilation for tingling. Each exercise has a target sensation, a duration, and a response-prevention element. The course is time-limited — typically 8 to 12 sessions. The mechanism is prediction-error correction: the patient learns "this sensation does not predict catastrophe" through repeated exposure. The catastrophic-misinterpretation cycle is broken by direct counter-evidence. The population is panic disorder, specifically — individuals whose central problem is catastrophic misinterpretation of body sensations. The goal is reducing catastrophic interpretation, building tolerance for harmless body sensations, and restoring behavioural and contextual access. Multiple RCTs support effectiveness.

Open-ended body-scan mindfulness in a high-caution profile is none of those things. It is open-ended attention to body sensations broadly, with no specific induction target. It is sustained inward focus, often 30 to 45 minutes per session, with limited per-session adaptation to participant state. The time horizon is often framed as a lifelong practice with no clear endpoint. The mechanism is cultivation of present-moment awareness, non-judgmental observation, and equanimity — not prediction-error correction in a specific catastrophic loop. The population is heterogeneous. The goal is broad: stress reduction, emotion regulation, "tune in to your body."

The two share "directing attention to body sensations" as a surface-level description but differ on every architectural variable that determines whether the intervention produces therapeutic prediction-error or iatrogenic amplification.

The honest reading: **not all body-focused interventions are equivalent**. The same surface-description covers interventions with very different mechanisms and very different population-fit profiles. The IE counter-example does not refute the parsing-deficit caution. It demonstrates that the structure of the intervention matters — and that evaluating any specific recommendation requires asking about structure, time horizon, mechanism, and population-fit, not just whether the intervention involves body-focus.

For the user, this means the right questions when evaluating any specific recommendation are not "is body-focus good or bad" — that is the wrong frame — but: What is the structure of the intervention? What is the time horizon? What is the mechanism it works through? What population is it designed for? Does my profile match that population? An IE protocol for panic disorder may be exactly right for a panic-prone alexithymic with detection-deficit profile. Open-ended MBSR body-scan may be exactly wrong for a parsing-deficit alexithymic with somatic-symptom-disorder comorbidity. Both can coexist as accurate observations.

## Alternatives when interoception-first is contraindicated

When the decision framework flags moderate or high caution, alternatives that operate from the *outside in* rather than the inside out tend to fit better. These alternatives share a design principle: each builds external-vocabulary architecture (concepts, labels, behavioural anchors, narrative structures, intersubjective context) that gives parsing-deficit alexithymia something to *parse with*. They do not require accurate internal-introspection as a prerequisite. They build the parsing architecture first, then map it back to internal states gradually. This is the inverse of the standard "tune in to your body" sequence.

<div class="diagram-figure-marker">[Diagram 6 here — Alternatives]</div>

ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy) works at the level of cognition and behaviour, not internal-affective-identification. Acceptance and values-based action without forcing identification of internal affective states. Cognitive defusion — treating thoughts as observable mental events rather than realities. Observer-self as a stance accessible without interoceptive accuracy. Values-clarification as a behavioural anchor. Useful even when "what am I feeling" remains uncertain.

Granularity-building from Barrett's framework is external-vocabulary expansion. Reading dense emotion words. Categorising others' emotional states in fiction or video. Discriminating high-arousal-positive from high-arousal-negative. Practicing nuanced labels in writing. Builds the conceptual architecture that parsing requires; moves from external observation to internal mapping after the vocabulary is in place. Exactly the inverse of "tune in first."

Affect-labelling from Lieberman's 2007 work. Putting feelings into words, even tentatively, reduces amygdala reactivity via a right ventrolateral PFC → mPFC → amygdala pathway. Labels can be scaffolded externally with context cues, observer description, or behavioural inference rather than requiring internal introspection. The mechanism is the labelling act itself, not the accuracy of the label.

Structured journaling scaffolded with explicit emotion-vocabulary lists, situation-feeling-thought-behaviour columns, and external-event triangulation. Pennebaker-style expressive writing modified for alexithymic samples per Baikie and colleagues' 2020 work at PMC7513612. The structure carries the parsing work that internal-introspection cannot reliably do alone. The page becomes the parsing architecture.

Smartphone emotion-recognition tools — MT-ALEX, Animi, AlexiLearn, structured emotion-recognition skills training (PMC6510700). Most use external-vocabulary scaffolding, situation-cue-based prompts, and structured rather than open-ended formats. The tool carries the framework so the user does not have to generate it from inside.

Mentalization-Based Therapy from Bateman and Fonagy. Perspective-taking and reflective-function building before interoception. Operates from the *intersubjective* layer — "what might the other person be feeling, and how does that map to what I might be feeling" comes before "what am I feeling internally." Fischer-Kern and colleagues' 2018 MBT-for-BPD work at PMC5996479 shows alexithymia reductions.

EAET (Emotional Awareness and Expression Therapy) developed by Lumley. Structured emotional engagement focused on conflict and expression. Multiple RCTs in fibromyalgia, IBS, MUS, and older veterans with chronic pain. Higher baseline emotional dysregulation moderates *greater* pain reduction after EAET than after CBT — meaning the more dysregulated subgroup gains disproportionately. Structured, time-limited, conflict-focused rather than open-ended body-focus.

STAIR-NT phase-based trauma treatment from the Cloitre lineage. Phase 1 — Skills Training in Affective and Interpersonal Regulation: affect-skills, vocabulary-building, distress-tolerance, interpersonal scaffolding. Phase 2 — narrative trauma processing once Phase 1 skills are in place. Designed precisely for complex-trauma populations with severe affect-regulation deficits. The sequence operationalises the harm-reduction consensus: stabilise, build parsing skills, process narratively, integrate.

A more comprehensive treatment of self-directed external-vocabulary alternatives is the next category — **Cat 11 — Self-Directed Approaches**. This category's job is to flag when interoception-first should not be the default. Cat 11's job is to give the user the actionable redirection toolkit.

## The honest evidence audit

For each major claim in this category, what tier of evidence supports it.

**Tier 1 — well-established.** Meditation-related adverse effects are common in measured samples (Britton 2021; Goldberg 2022). Childhood adversity is a documented risk factor (Goldberg 2022). Anxiety, traumatic re-experiencing, and emotional sensitivity are among the most common MRAE types (Goldberg 2022). The 32 expert teachers in the Lindahl 2017 Varieties study reported 56 of 59 categories of meditation-related challenges (95%). Treleaven's 36 specific modifications are established practical guidance for trauma populations. Dissociative-subtype PTSD requires specific protocol modifications when integrating mindfulness (Lanius 2024). High-alexithymia patients show attenuated and delayed response to standard prolonged exposure for PTSD (Pugach et al.).

**Tier 2 — mechanistically supported.** Body-attention focus amplifies symptom intensity in somatic-symptom-disorder populations (Barsky SSA framework). Mindfulness can deepen depersonalisation in already-dissociating individuals (Lindahl PMC9043097). The parsing-deficit alexithymia profile is plausibly at higher risk for body-focused interventions on mechanistic grounds (Aaron 2020 quadratic; Garfinkel 2015 three-dimensional model; SSA framework). Hypervigilance-with-mislabelling profiles are plausibly at higher risk on the same mechanistic grounds. The somatization pathway from trauma to body-symptom expression in alexithymic populations is theoretically well-supported but causally underdetermined.

**Tier 3 — theoretical only.** The specific claim that interoceptive-awareness training is risky for parsing-deficit alexithymics specifically — not directly tested in subtype-stratified RCTs. The Norman 2019 systematic review of mindfulness for alexithymia (PMC10270453) explicitly notes the absence of adverse-event data from the four trials it reviewed. Subtype-stratified prediction of which alexithymia profile will be harmed by which protocol — extrapolated from Tier 1 + Tier 2, not directly tested. Whether alexithymics specifically have higher dropout from MBSR or interoceptive training — no identified RCT explicitly reports this.

The category labels each major claim's tier explicitly, throughout. Treating Tier 3 claims with Tier 1 confidence is overclaiming. Treating Tier 1 findings with Tier 3 skepticism is underclaiming. The framework lets the user weight conclusions by what the data actually supports.

## What this category gives the user

Three actionable things, in service of one larger one.

The **decision framework** is an evaluation tool. When a clinician proposes body-focused or interoceptive work, the framework lets you assess whether your profile matches the population for which that protocol has the strongest evidence. The framework does not say "do not do this." It says "consider whether the profile fits, and consider alternatives if it does not."

The **markers of deterioration** are a monitoring tool. If you proceed with body-focused work, the markers tell you what to watch for and what to do if they appear. The wrongful-self-blame reading ("I must be doing it wrong") is one possibility, but it is not the only one. Profile-mismatch is another, and worth taking seriously.

The **alternatives** are a redirection tool. When the decision framework flags caution, external-vocabulary approaches — ACT, granularity-building, affect-labelling, structured journaling, app-based work, MBT, EAET, STAIR-NT phase-based treatment — give parsing-deficit alexithymia something to parse with, scaffolded externally rather than introspectively.

The larger thing these tools serve is the user's stated goal: an evidence-grounded basis for distinguishing "I am bad at this therapy" — often wrongful self-blame — from "this therapy is wrongly matched to my subtype." Distinguishing these two readings is itself a clinical skill. It is not the default reading in standard supervision frames. The default reading attributes treatment-resistance to patient factors. The therapy-mismatch reading attributes the same data to protocol factors. Both readings need consideration; the second is systematically under-emphasised. This category gives the user the framework to consider both.

The honest bottom line: the harm literature for body-focused interventions in general populations is real and well-documented. The harm literature for alexithymia subtypes specifically is essentially absent — what exists is theoretical extrapolation from adjacent literatures. The clinically actionable response is profile-stratified evaluation, not a blanket recommendation either way. The framework, the markers, and the alternatives give the user the structure to evaluate, monitor, and redirect — without overclaiming on the strength of the underlying alexithymia-specific evidence and without dismissing the well-attested mechanism case.
