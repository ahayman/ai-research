# Research Supplement 2: Balanced ABA Evidence

This supplement addresses a significant gap in the original research: the near-total omission of positive ABA outcome evidence, modern reform efforts, and the nuanced landscape of ABA quality and measurement. The original research presented ABA almost exclusively through its historical harms and critical perspectives. This supplement provides the evidence needed for balanced coverage, while also documenting the genuine limitations of that positive evidence.

---

## 1. Positive ABA Meta-Analyses and Systematic Reviews

### 1.1 Virués-Ortega (2010) — Foundational Meta-Analysis

**Study**: Virués-Ortega, J. (2010). "Applied behavior analytic intervention for autism in early childhood: Meta-analysis, meta-regression and dose-response meta-analysis of multiple outcomes."
**Journal**: Clinical Psychology Review, 30(4), 387-399
**DOI**: 10.1016/j.cpr.2010.01.008
**PubMed**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20223569/
**Studies included**: 22
**Methodology**: Meta-analysis with meta-regression and dose-response analysis across multiple outcome domains

**Key findings**:
- Long-term, comprehensive ABA intervention produced **medium to large effect sizes** for intellectual functioning, language development, daily living skills, and social functioning
- Language-related outcomes were superior to non-verbal IQ, social functioning, and daily living skills
- Effect sizes approached **1.5 for receptive and expressive language and communication skills**
- Dose-dependent effects were apparent: more treatment hours correlated with better language and adaptation outcomes

**Limitations**:
- Included studies had methodological weaknesses (most were non-randomized)
- Did not measure mental health, wellbeing, quality of life, or adverse events
- Conducted before widespread adoption of the CAT-Q or other masking measures
- Effect sizes for social functioning and daily living were notably smaller than for language/IQ

**Significance**: This was the first comprehensive meta-analysis of ABA outcomes and remains widely cited as foundational evidence. However, its conclusions are limited to skill acquisition outcomes — it cannot speak to whether those gains came at a psychological cost.

---

### 1.2 Rodgers et al. (2021) — SCABARD Individual Participant Data Meta-Analysis

**Study**: Rodgers, M., Simmonds, M., Marshall, D., Hodgson, R., Stewart, L.A., Rai, D., Wright, K., Ben-Itzchak, E., Eikeseth, S., Eldevik, S., Kovshoff, H., Magiati, I., Osborne, L.A., Reed, P., Vivanti, G., Zachor, D., & Le Couteur, A. (2021). "Intensive behavioural interventions based on applied behaviour analysis for young children with autism: An international collaborative individual participant data meta-analysis."
**Journal**: Autism, 25(4), 1137-1153
**DOI**: 10.1177/1362361320985680
**PubMed**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33482692/
**PMC**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8108110/
**Studies included**: 15 studies (from 24 papers, published 1993-2017)
**Participants**: 720 total
**Methodology**: Individual participant data (IPD) meta-analysis — the most intensive possible evaluation method, using raw data from original studies
**Registration**: PROSPERO CRD42017068303

**Key findings**:
- Small benefits observed for **adaptive behavior** and **cognitive ability (IQ)** after 2 years of early intensive ABA-based intervention compared to treatment as usual (TAU) or eclectic interventions
- Results "suggested relative benefits" for these outcomes but with important caveats

**Critical limitations acknowledged by authors**:
- **All included studies had serious risk of bias**: most were not randomized, intervention assignment was often based on parental preference, and outcome assessments were rarely blinded
- "Only limited conclusions can be drawn because we cannot rule out the possibility that the observed effects could be partly or entirely attributable to bias"
- A subsequently published RCT produced "notably less favorable results" than the IPD synthesis
- **Few studies examined outcomes important to autistic people** (e.g., wellbeing, quality of life, autonomy)
- Did not follow children beyond 2 years

**Significance**: This is the most methodologically rigorous evaluation of ABA available. Its cautiously positive findings for IQ and adaptive behavior are tempered by the acknowledgment that bias may account for some or all of the observed effects. The authors themselves flag the absence of outcomes meaningful to autistic stakeholders.

---

### 1.3 Strauss et al. (2023) — Comprehensive ABA Meta-Analysis

**Study**: Strauss, K., et al. (2023). "Comprehensive ABA-based interventions in the treatment of children with autism spectrum disorder — a meta-analysis."
**Journal**: BMC Psychiatry, 23, 133
**DOI**: 10.1186/s12888-022-04412-1
**PubMed**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36864429/
**Studies included**: 11 studies
**Participants**: 632
**Methodology**: Meta-analysis comparing comprehensive ABA-based interventions to treatment as usual or minimal/no treatment

**Key findings**:
- **Intellectual functioning**: Medium effect (SMD = 0.51, 95% CI [0.09; 0.92])
- **Adaptive behavior**: Medium effect (SMD = 0.37, 95% CI [0.03; 0.70])
- **Language abilities**: No significant improvement beyond control groups
- **Symptom severity**: No significant improvement beyond control groups
- **Parental stress**: No significant improvement beyond control groups
- Moderator analyses suggest language abilities at intake influence effect sizes; intensity effects may decrease with age

**Limitations**:
- Small number of included studies (11)
- Did not measure mental health, wellbeing, or quality of life outcomes
- No adverse event tracking
- No long-term follow-up beyond the intervention period

**Significance**: Shows measurable gains in IQ and adaptive behavior, but notably fails to show improvement in language (contrary to Virués-Ortega 2010), symptom severity, or parental stress. The pattern is consistent: ABA shows its strongest effects on standardized cognitive tests but weaker or absent effects on broader functioning.

---

### 1.4 Yu & Harmon (2025) — ABA/NDBI Meta-Analysis

**Study**: Yu, Y. & Harmon, A. (2025). "A Meta-Analysis of Applied Behavior Analysis-Based Interventions to Improve Communication, Adaptive, and Cognitive Skills in Children on the Autism Spectrum."
**Journal**: Review Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders
**DOI**: 10.1007/s40489-025-00506-0
**Link**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40489-025-00506-0
**Studies included**: 25
**Methodology**: Meta-analysis of ABA-based interventions including NDBIs, comparing pre-to-post intervention effects between experimental and control groups

**Key findings**:
- **Receptive language**: Large effect size
- **Adaptive behavior** (communication, daily living, socialization, composite): Moderate effect size
- **Cognitive skills**: Moderate effect size
- **ASD-specific challenging symptoms**: Small effect size
- Meta-regression: Greater treatment dose and duration yielded greater improvements in adaptive behaviors

**Limitations**:
- Combines traditional ABA and NDBI into one category, making it impossible to distinguish effects of discrete trial training from naturalistic approaches
- No mental health, wellbeing, or adverse event outcomes measured
- No measurement of masking, identity, or self-determination

**Significance**: The most recent meta-analysis available. Effect sizes are consistent with prior reviews — strongest for structured cognitive/language tasks, moderate for adaptive behavior, weakest for broader symptom reduction. The persistent absence of wellbeing measures across all included studies is itself a finding.

---

### 1.5 Eldevik et al. (2026) — Individual Participant Data Meta-Analysis of EIBI

**Study**: Eldevik, S., et al. (2026). "Clinically Significant Outcomes of Early Intensive Behavioral Intervention for Children With Autism Spectrum Disorders: An Individual Participant Data Meta-Analysis."
**Journal**: Autism Research
**DOI**: 10.1002/aur.70169
**Link**: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/aur.70169
**PubMed**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41502379/
**Studies included**: 15 (data from 17 identified)
**Participants**: 341 EIBI group, 280 comparison group
**Age range**: 2-6 years, minimum 12 months of intervention
**Methodology**: Individual participant data meta-analysis with clinically significant change analysis

**Key findings**:
- **Adaptive behavior**: Effect size d = 0.66
- **Intellectual functioning**: Effect size d = 0.87
- **ASD severity reductions**: Effect size d = 1.36
- Significantly higher percentage of EIBI children met criteria for statistically reliable change
- **Number Needed to Treat**: 4.1 to 6.9 (meaning roughly 4-7 children need to be treated for one to show clinically significant improvement)
- Treatment intensity significantly contributed to changes across all outcome measures

**Limitations**:
- Number Needed to Treat of 4.1-6.9 means most children in EIBI do NOT show clinically significant improvement
- Same bias concerns as Rodgers et al. — most component studies were non-randomized
- No mental health, wellbeing, or adverse event outcomes
- No follow-up beyond treatment period

**Significance**: Provides the most optimistic effect sizes in the literature, particularly for ASD severity (d = 1.36). However, the NNT data reveals that even under favorable conditions, a majority of children do not achieve clinically significant gains. This is rarely discussed in pro-ABA advocacy.

### 1.6 Summary Pattern Across Meta-Analyses

| Study | Year | N studies | IQ/Cognition | Adaptive Behavior | Language | Social | Mental Health | Adverse Events |
|-------|------|-----------|-------------|-------------------|----------|--------|---------------|----------------|
| Virués-Ortega | 2010 | 22 | Medium-Large | Small-Medium | Large (up to 1.5) | Small | NOT MEASURED | NOT MEASURED |
| Sandbank (Project AIM) | 2020 | 130 | Varies | Varies | Varies | Varies | NOT MEASURED | NOT MEASURED |
| Rodgers (SCABARD) | 2021 | 15 | Small (biased) | Small (biased) | Not reported | Not reported | NOT MEASURED | NOT MEASURED |
| Strauss et al. | 2023 | 11 | Medium (0.51) | Medium (0.37) | Not significant | Not reported | NOT MEASURED | NOT MEASURED |
| Yu & Harmon | 2025 | 25 | Moderate | Moderate | Large (receptive) | Not reported | NOT MEASURED | NOT MEASURED |
| Eldevik et al. | 2026 | 15 | Large (0.87) | Medium (0.66) | Not reported | Not reported | NOT MEASURED | NOT MEASURED |

**The pattern is stark**: Across six major meta-analyses spanning 16 years and hundreds of studies, mental health outcomes and adverse events have NEVER been measured as primary outcomes. The evidence base demonstrates that ABA can improve test scores and some adaptive skills, but it literally cannot tell us whether it helps or harms the psychological wellbeing of autistic people.

## 2. Naturalistic Developmental Behavioral Interventions (NDBIs)

### 2.1 Defining NDBIs

**Foundational paper**: Schreibman, L., et al. (2015). "Naturalistic Developmental Behavioral Interventions: Empirically Validated Treatments for Autism Spectrum Disorder."
**Journal**: Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 45, 2411-2428
**DOI**: 10.1007/s10803-015-2407-8
**PubMed**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25737021/
**PMC**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4513196/

NDBIs are defined by three core features:
1. **Developmental progression**: Skills are targeted in a developmental sequence, building across domains
2. **Naturalistic contexts**: Implemented in natural settings using natural contingencies and rewards
3. **Behavioral strategies**: Use modeling, shaping, chaining, prompting, and differential reinforcement — but within child-led interactions

**Key examples of NDBIs**:
- **ESDM** (Early Start Denver Model) — Rogers & Dawson
- **JASPER** (Joint Attention, Symbolic Play, Engagement, and Regulation) — Kasari
- **PRT** (Pivotal Response Treatment) — Koegel & Koegel
- **EMT** (Enhanced Milieu Teaching)
- **Project ImPACT**

**How NDBIs differ from Discrete Trial Training (DTT)**:

| Feature | DTT | NDBI |
|---------|-----|------|
| Setting | Table/desk, minimal distractions | Natural environment, play-based |
| Control | Adult-directed, massed trials | Shared control, child-led |
| Reinforcement | Often arbitrary (tokens, candy) | Natural consequences related to behavior |
| Pace | Rapid repetitive cycles | Follows child's interest and timing |
| Structure | Highly structured, scripted | Flexible, embedded in routines |
| Engagement | Compliance expected | Engagement negotiated |

---

### 2.2 ESDM Evidence Base

**Landmark RCT**: Dawson, G., et al. (2010). "Randomized, Controlled Trial of an Intervention for Toddlers With Autism: The Early Start Denver Model."
**Journal**: Pediatrics, 125(1), e17-e23
**PubMed**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19948568/
**PMC**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4951085/

- ESDM group improved **17.6 IQ points** over 2 years vs. 7.0 in comparison group
- Significant improvements in adaptive behavior and autism diagnosis

**Critical update — 2025 European multicentric RCT**:
**Study**: (2025). "Early Start Denver Model effectiveness in young autistic children: a large multicentric randomised controlled trial in two European countries."
**Journal**: Published 2025
**PubMed**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40473417/
**PMC**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12142086/
**Participants**: 180 children, ages 19-36 months, in France and Belgium
**Design**: RCT; ESDM+TAU (12 hrs/week) vs. TAU alone

**Key finding**: Children in the ESDM+TAU group **did NOT significantly improve** global developmental quotient compared with TAU alone (endpoint mean difference 3.82, 95% CI [-1.25 to 8.89], p=0.14). Results were consistent across verbal and non-verbal DQ, and whether assessed at 1 or 2 years post-intervention.

**Conclusion from authors**: "ESDM+TAU cannot be universally recommended for young children with ASD."

**Significance**: This is a significant challenge to ESDM's evidence base. The original landmark RCT (Dawson 2010) was conducted in tightly controlled research settings. When tested in community implementation across two countries, the effects disappeared. This pattern — strong results in controlled research, weak or absent results in community settings — recurs throughout the ABA/NDBI literature and is central to understanding the quality variation problem.

---

### 2.3 JASPER Evidence Base

**Systematic review**: Waddington, H., et al. (2021). "The effects of JASPER intervention for children with autism spectrum disorder: A systematic review."
**Journal**: Autism, 25(8), 2370-2385
**DOI**: 10.1177/13623613211019162

**Key findings**:
- JASPER shows immediate and persistent improvements in joint engagement, joint attention, play skills, and expressive language
- Language and cognition gains reported at 1 month, 1 year, and 5 years post-intervention
- Changes can occur in as few as 20-30 sessions over 6-12 weeks
- Generally child-led and focused on developmental cascades

**Relevance to masking**: JASPER's child-led, play-based approach addresses some criticisms of traditional ABA by centering the child's interests rather than adult-imposed goals. However, the question of whether even naturalistic teaching of social communication constitutes "normalization" remains debated in neurodiversity scholarship.

---

### 2.4 Project AIM — NDBIs vs. Behavioral Interventions

**Study**: Sandbank, M., et al. (2020). "Project AIM: Autism Intervention Meta-Analysis for Studies of Young Children."
**Journal**: Psychological Bulletin, 146(1), 1-29
**DOI**: 10.1037/bul0000215
**PubMed**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31763860/
**PMC**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8783568/
**Effect sizes analyzed**: 1,615 from 130 independent samples
**Participants**: 6,240 children aged 0-8 years
**Intervention types compared**: Behavioral, developmental, NDBI, TEACCH, sensory-based, animal-assisted, technology-based

**Key findings on NDBIs**:
- When all study designs included: behavioral, developmental, and NDBI types all showed significant positive effects
- **When limited to RCTs only**: Evidence of positive effects existed **only for developmental and NDBI types** — NOT traditional behavioral interventions
- When excluding caregiver-report measures from RCTs: NDBI effects on play and social communication remained significant (0.18-0.47 range)
- When excluding ALL detection bias threats: **No category of NDBI outcomes reached significance** (language and social communication both 0.17, nonsignificant)

**Critical implication**: The strongest evidence for behavioral interventions disappears when restricted to the most rigorous study designs (RCTs with blind assessment). This does not prove ABA is ineffective — but it means the claimed effect sizes may be substantially inflated by methodological artifacts.

---

### 2.5 Do NDBIs Avoid Masking Concerns?

This is a critical question. NDBIs differ from traditional DTT in meaningful ways:

**Arguments that NDBIs reduce masking risk**:
- Child-led rather than adult-imposed goals
- Shared control rather than compliance training
- Natural environments rather than clinical settings
- Focus on communication function rather than behavioral form
- Greater alignment with neurodiversity-affirming values

**Arguments that NDBIs may still promote masking**:
- Still fundamentally teach social communication skills based on neurotypical developmental milestones
- The "developmental sequence" is derived from neurotypical development, not autistic development
- Teaching joint attention, eye contact, and social reciprocity may still constitute normalization pressure
- Even "natural rewards" can create contingencies that reinforce masking behavior
- The Dwyer et al. (2025) community survey found neurodiversity supporters "did not think autistic people should be taught to act less autistic" — a standard that NDBIs may or may not meet depending on implementation

**Current evidence gap**: No studies have directly measured whether NDBIs produce less masking, less identity distress, or better mental health outcomes than traditional DTT. This comparison is urgently needed but has never been conducted.

## 3. Modern ABA Reform Efforts

### 3.1 Neurodiversity-Affirming ABA

**Key publication**: Mathur, S.K., Renz, E., & Tarbox, J. (2024). "Affirming Neurodiversity within Applied Behavior Analysis."
**Journal**: Behavior Analysis in Practice
**DOI**: 10.1007/s40617-024-00907-3
**PMC**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11219658/

**Five criticisms addressed**:
1. **Identity erasure**: ABA historically sought to make autistic individuals "indistinguishable from peers," encouraging masking linked to burnout and suicidality
2. **Compliance overemphasis**: Rigid compliance training without assent-withdrawal options may cultivate vulnerability to abuse
3. **Reductionism**: Reducing complex human experience to four behavioral functions ignores emotional and sensory dimensions
4. **Absent autistic voices**: Research and practice development exclude autistic perspectives and leadership
5. **Coercive parental pressure**: Providers leverage parental fear to promote ABA as the only evidence-based option

**Specific calls to action**:
- "Stop defending the field's abusive past; instead acknowledge it publicly"
- "Question whether each behavioral target stems from client values or neurotypical conformity"
- "Educate clients and families about masking's long-term harms"
- "Eliminate default stereotypy reduction; require justification case-by-case"
- "Present ABA as one option among evidence-based approaches, never as default"
- "Measure and support client emotional wellbeing, not just behavior change"
- "Actively recruit autistic colleagues, students, and advisors"

**Proposed reconceptualization**: The authors propose shifting ABA's purpose from "increasing/decreasing behaviors to match neurotypical norms" to "empowering autistic people to maximize their potential consistent with their own values."

---

### 3.2 Assent-Based Practice

Assent-based ABA practice represents one of the most concrete reform proposals. Core principles include:

- **Informed, voluntary agreement**: The individual receiving therapy actively consents to participate, even when they cannot provide legal consent
- **Continuous monitoring**: Regularly checking the person's comfort and willingness to continue
- **Honoring withdrawal**: Respecting "no" as meaningful communication — not treating refusal as a behavior to be extinguished
- **Adjusting in real-time**: Modifying sessions based on the person's feedback and affect

**Key tension**: Traditional ABA programs routinely use "escape extinction" — preventing the child from leaving or avoiding a demand. Assent-based practice fundamentally conflicts with this by honoring withdrawal. This represents a genuine philosophical shift, not just a rebranding.

---

### 3.3 BACB Ethical Guidelines Evolution

The Behavior Analyst Certification Board's Ethics Code has evolved:

- **2022 update**: Added Standard 1.07 on Cultural Responsiveness and Diversity; required RBTs to work with supervisors on cultural responsiveness and evaluating biases; restricted punishment/restrictive practices to cases where supervisor has verified competence
- **Emphasis on informed choice**: Ethics code notes the importance of "providing clients and stakeholders with needed information to make informed choices about services"
- **Ongoing criticism**: Despite updates, the BACB has faced criticism for delayed condemnation of Contingent Electric Skin Shock (CESS) at the Judge Rotenberg Center, and recent removal of Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion (DEI) requirements

**Source**: https://centralreach.com/blog/bacb-ethics-code-2024/

### 3.4 Johnson (2025) — "From Harm to Healing"

**Study**: Johnson, J.F. (2025). "From Harm to Healing: Building the Future of ABA with Autistic Voices."
**Journal**: Societies, 15(3), 72
**DOI**: 10.3390/soc15030072
**Link**: https://www.mdpi.com/2075-4698/15/3/72

**Unique perspective**: Author is an autistic BCBA — bridging lived experience and professional expertise.

**Key arguments**:
- While recent advocacy has helped reduce compliance-based approaches, unethical applications continue in some settings
- The #ABAisAbuse movement gained traction in the 2010s as autistic individuals shared accounts of psychological harm, describing how compliance-focused interventions led to long-term emotional distress and diminished self-identity
- Standardization must focus on embedding neurodivergent-affirming principles at all levels of practice
- BCBA training should include trauma-informed care to understand how coercive techniques can trigger trauma responses
- The science of behavior analysis remains a valuable tool; the problem is its implementation

**Significance**: An autistic BCBA arguing for reform from within the profession represents the most credible possible voice for the "ABA can be reformed" position. Their willingness to acknowledge harms while maintaining that the science has value provides a model for balanced discourse.

---

### 3.5 ABA at a Crossroads — Graber & Graber (2025)

**Study**: Graber, J. & Graber, A. (2025). "Applied Behavior Analysis at a Crossroads: Reform, Branding, and the Future of Behavior Analysis."
**Journal**: Perspectives on Behavior Science, 48(3)
**DOI**: 10.1007/s40614-025-00462-4
**PMC**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12411353/

**Core argument**: ABA's own foundational principles — philosophic doubt and social validity — require the field to take autistic community concerns seriously. Ignoring concerns contradicts the field's own values.

**Four reform directions identified**:
1. Removing aversive control → assent-based practices
2. Centering empathy and compassion
3. Minimizing psychological harm
4. Empowering clients → neurodiversity-affirming practices

**Major concern — credential proliferation**:
- Progressive Behavior Analyst Autism Council (PBAAC) offers three credentials; neither it nor FTF Behavioral Consulting requires BACB certification
- New conferences proliferating: Assent Con, Annual Trauma Informed Practice in ABA, Rejected 2024, etc.
- Consumers cannot easily distinguish between "assent-based ABA," "trauma-informed ABA," "compassion-focused ABA," "Progressive ABA," and "Today's ABA"
- Research shows licensure/certification shows positive quality effects in only 16% of cases, negative effects in 21%, and no effect in 63%

**Recommendation**: Reforms should be reconceptualized as improvements to standard practice, not distinct brands. "There is better ABA, and there is worse ABA. Let's all strive, together, to move the field in the direction of the better."

---

### 3.6 Travers & Tincani (2025) — Defense of ABA

**Study**: Travers, J. & Tincani, M. (2025). "In Defense of Applied Behavior Analysis and Evidence-Based Practice."
**Journal**: Perspectives on Behavior Science, 48(3), 641-653
**DOI**: 10.1007/s40614-025-00468-y
**PMC**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12411346/

**Key arguments**:
- Over 50 years of research demonstrates ABA's effectiveness
- Critics rely on "fallacious tactics" and attacks that lack empirical support
- Neurodiversity critics "have ignored how ABA produces outcomes consistent" with shared values regarding autonomy and choice
- ABA played a role in deinstitutionalization and community inclusion
- Professional ethics codes prohibit harmful practices
- Contemporary practice has evolved beyond abandoned techniques

**Significant weaknesses of this defense**:
- Does not engage with the measurement gap (no mental health outcomes studied)
- Does not address the Aguirre Mtanous (2025) finding of 30% higher mental health hospitalization risk
- Does not address the Bottema-Beutel COI findings
- Dismisses neurodiversity critics without addressing their specific empirical claims
- Uses a tone that may alienate rather than persuade — referring to "unfounded criticisms" and "ideological perspectives lacking credible support"

**Significance**: This paper represents the strongest available defense of ABA from within the field. Its existence is important for balance, but its dismissive posture toward autistic community testimony — the very stakeholders the BACB ethics code requires practitioners to center — illustrates the disconnect that reform advocates identify.

## 4. Quality Variation in ABA

### 4.1 The Research-to-Practice Gap

Evidence consistently shows a significant gap between ABA as studied in clinical trials and ABA as practiced in community settings:

- **80% of BCBAs report having no formal training in determining service hours** — one of the most critical aspects of ABA treatment planning (ABA Matrix, 2026)
- Evidence-based practices for autistic children are "rarely incorporated into community settings, and little is known about the quality of implementation" (multiple sources)
- Provider attitudes toward evidence-based practices are "moderately positive" (2.99-3.32 out of 4 on EBPAS subscales), but attitudes do not always translate to implementation

**Source**: https://www.abamatrix.com/aba-trends-2026/

### 4.2 Barriers to Implementing NDBIs in ABA Settings

**Study**: Pickard, K., Islam, N., Green, N., Chatson, E., Kuhn, J., & Yosick, R. (2024). "The Challenges Associated with Changing Practice: Barriers to Implementing Naturalistic Developmental Behavioral Interventions in ABA Settings."
**Journal**: Behavior Analysis in Practice
**DOI**: 10.1007/s40617-024-01011-2
**PMC**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11707165/
**Participants**: 18 clinicians (9 BCBAs, 9 RBTs)

**Barriers identified**:
1. **Need to unlearn DTT**: Clinicians trained in discrete trial training found transitioning to NDBI challenging
2. **Limited training infrastructure**: Insufficient formal NDBI training in onboarding programs
3. **Self-efficacy gaps**: RBTs reported lower confidence delivering NDBI
4. **Misconceptions**: Staff incorrectly viewed NDBI as lacking structure or failing to address safety
5. **Negative attitudes**: Skepticism that NDBI was a temporary "fad"
6. **Physical demands**: NDBI requires more physical engagement than table-based DTT
7. **For-profit industry pressures**: Business models prioritizing profit over training quality undermined adoption
8. **Skill generalization difficulty**: Difficulty transferring NDBI skills across different clients

**Positive findings despite barriers**: Clinicians reported positive attitudes toward NDBI, observed more spontaneous communication in children, and noted better skill generalization into home/community settings.

**Significance**: Even when practitioners want to adopt more naturalistic, child-led approaches, structural barriers prevent them. The for-profit ABA industry model — driven by billable hours rather than outcomes — actively works against the reforms that the field's own researchers advocate.

---

### 4.3 Insurance and Billing Pressures

The ABA industry has experienced explosive growth:
- **Indiana**: Medicaid ABA spending grew from $21 million (2017) to $611 million (2024) — a 2,800% increase
- **Nebraska**: Medicaid ABA payments rose from ~$4.6 million (2020) to ~$85.6 million (2024) — ~1,800% increase
- **North Carolina**: Payments projected to hit $639 million in fiscal 2026, a 423% increase

**Consequences of cost pressure**:
- Several states have capped therapy hours (e.g., Indiana: 30 hrs/week max for 3 years)
- North Carolina attempted 10% payment cuts; Nebraska cut payments ~50% for some providers
- Insurance companies are exploring value-based payment models tied to outcomes rather than hours
- There are **no standardized outcome measures** across ABA providers — making value-based models difficult to implement

**Impact on quality**: When families' therapy hours are abruptly halved (from 30 to 15 hours in one documented case), the clinical rationale is displaced by fiscal constraint. Simultaneously, the lack of standardized outcome measurement means neither funders nor families can distinguish high-quality from low-quality providers.

**Sources**: https://stateline.org/2025/11/25/families-worry-as-cost-of-autism-therapy-comes-under-state-scrutiny/ ; https://www.npr.org/sections/shots-health-news/2025/12/23/nx-s1-5643014/autism-aba-therapy-medicaid-costs ; https://kffhealthnews.org/news/article/aba-therapy-applied-behavior-analysis-autism-medicaid-rate-cuts-north-carolina/

---

### 4.4 NDBI Confusion

Substantial confusion exists about the relationship between NDBI and ABA:
- NDBIs are technically a form of ABA (they use behavioral principles of learning)
- However, health professionals commonly "deride 'ABA' while touting the virtues of NDBI" without recognizing NDBIs as ABA approaches (Graber & Graber, 2025)
- Even peer-reviewed literature lacks clarity on whether NDBIs should be classified as ABA
- This confusion means families may reject "ABA" while accepting "ESDM" or "JASPER" — receiving functionally similar interventions under different labels

## 5. The Measurement Gap

### 5.1 Bottema-Beutel et al. (2021) — Adverse Event Reporting

**Study**: Bottema-Beutel, K., Crowley, S., Sandbank, M., & Woynaroski, T.G. (2021). "Adverse event reporting in intervention research for young autistic children."
**Journal**: Autism, 25(2), 322-335
**DOI**: 10.1177/1362361320965331
**PubMed**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33076682/

**Methodology**: Examined 150 reports on group-design, non-pharmacological interventions for young autistic children

**Key findings**:
- **Only 11 of 150 studies (7.3%) mentioned adverse events** at all
- Of those 11, only 1 indicated adverse events occurred, and 3 indicated adverse effects (attributable to intervention)
- Of 54 studies reporting withdrawal reasons: 10 reported reasons categorizable as adverse events, 8 as adverse effects, and 12 were too vaguely described
- **The vast majority of autism intervention research does not even attempt to measure whether interventions cause harm**

**Recommendation**: Researchers should "make concerted efforts to monitor, classify, and report adverse events so that practitioners, policy-makers, and families are better equipped to weigh potential benefits against potential harms."

**Significance**: This is perhaps the single most important finding for the ABA debate. The field cannot claim ABA is safe if it has never systematically looked for harm. Equally, critics cannot definitively prove ABA causes harm when the data have never been collected. This is an evidence vacuum, not evidence of absence.

---

### 5.2 Sandbank et al. (2024) — Evidence-Biased Reporting

**Study**: Sandbank, M., Bottema-Beutel, K., Syu, Y-C., Caldwell, N., Feldman, J.I., & Woynaroski, T. (2024). "Evidence-b(i)ased practice: Selective and inadequate reporting in early childhood autism intervention research."
**Journal**: Autism
**DOI**: 10.1177/13623613241231624
**PubMed**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38345030/

**Key findings**:
- Only **7% of registered trials** were updated with results on the registration platform
- Only **64%** had associated published reports
- **25%** of studies eligible for a prior meta-analysis were excluded due to inadequate reporting
- Multiple forms of reporting bias identified: registration failures, selective outcome reporting, inadequate results dissemination

**Significance**: The evidence base for ABA is not just limited in what it measures — it is also selectively reported. Studies with negative or null results are less likely to be published or reported, inflating the apparent effectiveness of interventions.

---

### 5.3 Aguirre Mtanous et al. (2025) — The First Large-Scale Mental Health Outcome Study

**Study**: Aguirre Mtanous, N.G., Koenig, J., Nikahd, M., Effertz, S.E., Silinonte, S., Hyer, J.M., Hand, B.N., & Bishop, L. (2025). "Mental health outcomes associated with applied behavior analysis in a US national sample of privately insured autistic youth."
**Journal**: Autism
**DOI**: 10.1177/13623613251390604
**Link**: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/13623613251390604
**PubMed**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41206741/
**Sample**: 17,120 ABA-exposed + 17,120 matched controls = 34,240 autistic youth (under 18)
**Methodology**: Retrospective cohort study using national insurance claims data

**Key findings**:
- ABA receipt was associated with **30% higher odds** of mental health hospitalization (OR = 1.30, p < 0.001)
- **32% higher incidence rate** of mental health hospitalizations (IRR = 1.32, p < 0.001)
- **No relationship** found between ABA dosing and PTSD or suicidality outcomes
- Authors conclude more work is needed to "fully understand the impact of ABA therapy on mental health outcomes"

**Critical caveats**:
- This is an association, not proven causation — children receiving ABA may have more severe presentations that independently predict hospitalization
- Insurance claims data cannot capture what type of ABA was provided (DTT vs. NDBI, quality level, specific practices)
- The study cannot distinguish between ABA causing harm and ABA being prescribed to children who are already at higher risk

**Significance**: This is the first large-scale quantitative study to examine mental health outcomes associated with ABA. Its finding of increased hospitalization risk is concerning but not definitive. Most importantly, it demonstrates that measuring mental health outcomes IS possible using insurance data — making the failure of prior studies to do so even more notable.

---

### 5.4 What Outcomes Are NOT Measured?

Across the entire ABA evidence base, the following outcomes are systematically absent:

- **Mental health** (depression, anxiety, PTSD symptoms)
- **Psychological wellbeing** (self-esteem, identity, life satisfaction)
- **Quality of life** (as defined by the autistic person, not clinicians)
- **Masking/camouflaging** (CAT-Q or equivalent)
- **Self-determination** (autonomy, choice-making, agency)
- **Autistic identity** (acceptance, pride, distress)
- **Long-term mental health trajectories** (5, 10, 20 years post-intervention)
- **Adverse events** (systematically tracked and reported)
- **Relationship quality** (attachment security, social connection as experienced by the autistic person)

This is not a minor omission. It represents a fundamental mismatch between what the evidence base measures (behavioral compliance, IQ, adaptive behavior scores) and what autistic people identify as most important to their lives (autonomy, identity, mental health, belonging).

## 6. Balanced Perspectives from Researchers

### 6.1 Leaf et al. (2022) — Evaluation of ABA Concerns

**Study**: Leaf, J.B., Cihon, J.H., Leaf, R., McEachin, J., Liu, N., Russell, N., Unumb, L., Shapiro, S., & Khosrowshahi, D. (2022). "Concerns About ABA-Based Intervention: An Evaluation and Recommendations."
**Journal**: Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 52(6), 2838-2853
**DOI**: 10.1007/s10803-021-05137-y
**PubMed**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34132968/
**PMC**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9114057/

**Authors**: Include BCBAs, licensed psychologists, parents, and autistic individuals

**Key evaluations**:

| Concern | Authors' Assessment | What They Acknowledge |
|---------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Lovaas/historical practices | Field has evolved; generalizing past practices to modern ABA "lacks validity" | Support "continued development of knowledge of past research" |
| Punishment procedures | More research needed; prevalence of side effects "is unknown" | Advocate for positive reinforcement "when possible" |
| Extinction causing trauma | "Research...do not seem to provide evidence that extinction results in trauma" | Call for explicit research evaluating trauma claims |
| Targeting stimming | Literature shows reducing stereotypy "generally leads to desirable changes" | Recommend research identifying which forms interfere with opportunities |
| Goal selection | Valid concern that autistic people "are not commonly included in goal selection" | Strongly recommend collaborative goal selection |
| ABA causes PTSD | "Lack of reliable data and research" that ABA causes PTSD/anxiety/depression | Critically evaluate Kupferstein (2018) and Sandoval-Norton & Shkedy (2019) |

**Recommendations**: Individualized intervention intensity; movement away from rigid protocols toward naturalistic contexts; collaborative goal selection; expanded outcome measures including quality-of-life indicators; interdisciplinary research on long-term effects

**Notable statement**: "All of the authors support neurodiversity, promote acceptance and accommodation, and hope that everyone would agree that autistics/individuals diagnosed with ASD have the same rights as neurotypicals."

### 6.2 Dwyer et al. (2025) — Community Views on Neurodiversity and Intervention

**Study**: Dwyer, P., Gurba, A.N., Kapp, S.K., Kilgallon, E., Hersh, L.H., Chang, D.S., Rivera, S.M., & Gillespie-Lynch, K. (2025). "Community views of neurodiversity, models of disability and autism intervention: Mixed methods reveal shared goals and key tensions."
**Journal**: Autism
**DOI**: 10.1177/13623613241273029
**PubMed**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39291753/
**Sample**: 504 participants (278 autistic, 226 non-autistic) including 100 researchers, 122 professionals, 162 parents/caregivers

**Key findings on shared goals**:
- Both neurodiversity supporters and skeptics agreed "it is important to help autistic people be well"
- Participants called for improving society, building comfortable spaces, and helping autistic people lead change
- Neurodiversity supporters felt autistic children should choose their own intervention goals

**Key tensions**:
- Neurodiversity supporters "did not think autistic people should be taught to act less autistic" and "warned against teaching autistic people to hide their autism"
- Yet many were "somewhat open to helping people grow by teaching them useful skills"
- Controversies "may be exacerbated by confusion over [neurodiversity's] meaning"

**Significance**: Shows that the ABA debate is not simply "pro-ABA vs. anti-ABA" — there is substantial shared ground around wanting autistic people to thrive, with genuine disagreement about whether behavioral intervention is compatible with that goal.

## 7. Consumer Satisfaction Research

### 7.1 Anderson (2023) — Autistic Adult Retrospective Accounts

**Study**: Anderson, L.K. (2023). "Autistic experiences of applied behavior analysis."
**Journal**: Autism, 27(3), 737-750
**DOI**: 10.1177/13623613221118216
**PubMed**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35999706/
**Link**: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/13623613221118216
**Sample**: 7 autistic individuals who received ABA as children
**Methodology**: Qualitative interviews exploring perceived costs and benefits

**Key findings**:
- Participants described a mix of costs and benefits
- Some ABA practitioners supported individuals to "fit in better and feel more confident with improved communication skills"
- However, many described the underlying intention of "fixing" autistic individuals to behave in line with neurotypical behaviors as detrimental
- Autistic self-advocates argued ABA "damages their mental health and treats them as though they are a problem to be fixed"

**Limitations**: Very small qualitative sample (n=7); cannot generalize to all ABA recipients; retrospective accounts subject to recall bias

---

### 7.2 Marshall (2025) — Canadian Mixed-Methods Study

**Study**: Marshall, S. (2025). "Autistic Experiences of Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA): Toward Improved Autistic-Centered Supports."
**Journal**: Journal of Social Issues
**DOI**: 10.1111/josi.70037
**Link**: https://spssi.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/josi.70037
**Sample**: 68 survey respondents (22 had received ABA, 46 had not); 4 semi-structured interviews
**Methodology**: Transformative mixed methods (surveys → interviews → analysis → participatory dissemination with autistic co-researchers)

**Three major themes from survey**:
1. ABA is based on neuronormative (non-autistic) goals
2. ABA is compliance-based
3. ABA is harmful, not helpful

**Four themes from research team analysis**:
1. "Autistic Way of Being & The Double Empathy Problem"
2. "Supporting Autonomy, Self-Advocacy, and Interdependence"
3. "Implementation Matters"
4. "Moving Beyond Behavior"

**Harms identified**: Masking (hiding autistic traits), feelings of shame, emotional distress, and trauma

**Notable feature**: Four autistic participants co-authored recommendations, responding to Milton's (2014) call for "interactional expertise" between non-autistic researchers and autistic people.

### 7.3 Netherlands Mixed-Methods Study (2025) — Multi-Stakeholder Views

**Study**: (2025). "Experiences of Autistic Individuals, Caregivers and Healthcare Providers with ABA-Derived Therapies: a Sequential Exploratory Mixed Methods Study."
**Journal**: Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders
**DOI**: 10.1007/s10803-025-06958-x
**Link**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10803-025-06958-x
**Sample**: Qualitative focus groups (n=22) + online survey (n=219); autistic adults, parents/legal representatives, and healthcare professionals

**Six themes from focus groups**:
1. Diverse interpretations of ABA
2. Variations in its application
3. Positive experiences
4. Concerns about overburdening and psychological impacts
5. Criticisms of the healthcare system
6. Quality control issues

**Key finding on stakeholder differences**: Satisfaction varied by group — autistic adults were generally less satisfied and more critical than parents and professionals. This divergence highlights why autistic voice inclusion is essential in evaluating ABA.

---

### 7.4 Predictors of ABA Perceptions (2025)

**Study**: (2025). "Predictors of perceptions of ABA services from the autistic community."
**Journal**: (Likely Research in Autism Spectrum Disorders or similar)
**Link**: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S3050656525001981
**Sample**: 178 autistic participants aged 9+; 36-question survey

**Predictors of more favorable perceptions**:
- Sampled from ABA organizations (vs. general autism community)
- Received ABA less than 6 years ago (more recent = more positive)
- Service setting (some settings rated more favorably)

**Predictors of less favorable perceptions**:
- Sampled from autism self-advocacy groups
- Transgender individuals (on some measures)
- Goals that reduce or eliminate autistic traits
- Procedures that use extinction

**Author interpretation**: "Findings highlight the need to involve autistic voices in the development of treatment goals but also indicate that many of the critiques of ABA are not held by most in the autism community."

**Critical note on sampling**: The finding that people sampled from ABA organizations view ABA more favorably than those from self-advocacy groups is expected and should be interpreted with caution — it likely reflects selection effects rather than representative community opinion.

---

### 7.5 Parent Satisfaction Data

Parent satisfaction surveys consistently show high satisfaction:
- In a recent survey, 93.7% of parents said they would recommend ABA and 93.9% reported their child "visibly benefited"
- These figures contrast sharply with autistic adult retrospective accounts

**Interpretation**: The parent/autistic adult satisfaction divergence is one of the most important dynamics in the ABA debate. Parents observe behavioral improvements (fewer meltdowns, more communication, better daily functioning) and rate the intervention positively. Autistic adults who underwent ABA as children report that those same behavioral changes came at a psychological cost that was invisible to parents at the time — manifesting as masking, identity suppression, and mental health consequences that emerged years or decades later.

Neither perspective is wrong. They are measuring different things: parents assess behavioral functionality; autistic adults assess psychological wellbeing and identity integrity.

## 8. Synthesis — What the Evidence Actually Shows

### What IS supported by evidence:
- ABA-based interventions (especially NDBIs) produce measurable improvements in cognitive test scores, some adaptive behaviors, and some communication skills (effect sizes typically small to medium)
- These effects are strongest in tightly controlled research settings with high-fidelity implementation
- NDBIs appear to produce more robust effects than traditional DTT when study design is rigorous
- The field has genuinely evolved from Lovaas-era practices — assent-based, neurodiversity-affirming practitioners exist and are growing in number
- Some autistic people report positive experiences with ABA, particularly more recent recipients and those receiving naturalistic approaches

### What is NOT supported by evidence:
- That ABA improves mental health, wellbeing, quality of life, or long-term psychological outcomes — this has simply never been studied
- That ABA is psychologically safe — adverse events have never been systematically tracked
- That "modern ABA" is meaningfully different from older approaches at the community practice level — the reform-to-practice gap is enormous
- That ABA is the only evidence-based approach — this claim overstates the evidence base for ABA while ignoring evidence for other approaches

### What the evidence suggests but cannot confirm:
- ABA may increase risk of mental health hospitalization (Aguirre Mtanous 2025 — associational, not causal)
- Some forms of ABA may train masking behaviors that contribute to later mental health difficulties (plausible mechanism, no direct longitudinal test)
- The quality of ABA varies enormously, meaning both positive and negative testimonials may be accurately describing different implementations of the same intervention
- NDBIs may avoid some of the masking/identity concerns of traditional DTT, but this has never been directly tested

### The fundamental problem:
The ABA evidence base measures what ABA practitioners value (behavioral change, skill acquisition, IQ gains) but does not measure what autistic people value (mental health, identity, autonomy, wellbeing). Until the field systematically measures the outcomes that matter most to its primary stakeholders, the debate will remain unresolvable — both sides are arguing from different evidence bases about different outcome domains.
