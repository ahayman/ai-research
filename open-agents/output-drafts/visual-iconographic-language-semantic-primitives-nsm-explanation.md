# Semantic Primitives and Natural Semantic Metalanguage (NSM)

The Atoms of Meaning -- What Leibniz Dreamed Of and Wierzbicka Found

---

## 1. Introduction: What Is the Ground?

If you want to build a visual language from the ground up, you need to know what the ground is.

What are the irreducible building blocks of human meaning -- the concepts that cannot be decomposed into simpler concepts, that every human language encodes, and from which all other meanings can be constructed? This is the question that Leibniz asked in 1666 when he proposed his "alphabet of human thought." It is the question that Anna Wierzbicka has spent over five decades investigating through her Natural Semantic Metalanguage (NSM) program. And the answer she has arrived at -- 65 semantic primes, claimed to be universal across every known human language -- is the most empirically grounded candidate for those atoms of meaning.

For a visual language project, NSM offers two critical gifts:

1. **A principled inventory of what must be visually representable**: the 65 primes plus approximately 25 universal "semantic molecules."
2. **A method for defining any complex concept entirely in terms of primes**: the "explication" technique, which eliminates circularity and produces cross-linguistically translatable definitions.

But NSM also poses a fundamental design challenge. Many primes are inherently abstract -- IF, BECAUSE, NOT, VERY, MAYBE -- and resist visual representation. Drawing a picture of SOMEONE is straightforward. Drawing a picture of IF is a design problem with no obvious solution. This tension between the linguistic universality of semantic primes and the visual representability of spatial structures is one of the central challenges the visual language must resolve.

### Confidence Overview

This explanation draws on validated concept research (overall confidence 8/10). The NSM framework, the 65 primes inventory, the development history, and the cross-linguistic validation claims were all confirmed. One correction has been applied: the semantic molecules count, originally reported as "~50 universal," has been revised based on validation findings. Approximately 25 molecules appear to be truly universal, with the broader inventory reaching ~180 productive molecules for English alone.

## 2. The NSM Program: History and Development

### Origins

The NSM program was developed by Anna Wierzbicka, beginning at Warsaw University and continuing at the Australian National University, with Cliff Goddard at Griffith University as the principal collaborator and co-developer since the 1990s.

The development followed a clear arc of empirical refinement:

- **1972**: Wierzbicka proposes 14 semantic primes in *Semantic Primitives*
- **1980s**: Expansion and testing across additional languages
- **1996**: Wierzbicka's *Semantics: Primes and Universals* establishes 55 primes
- **2002**: Wierzbicka and Goddard expand the inventory to 60 primes
- **2014**: Current inventory stabilized at 65 primes

Both Wierzbicka and Goddard have expressed confidence that 65 represents the complete set. The expansion stopped -- not because research stopped, but because no new irreducible concepts were found.

### The Identification Method

How do you determine that a concept is irreducible? Through the **reductive paraphrase** method: if a concept can be defined using simpler concepts, it is not a prime. A concept qualifies as a prime if and only if:

1. **It cannot be defined in simpler terms** -- every attempt at definition is either circular or uses concepts of equal complexity.
2. **It has exact lexical equivalents in all tested languages** -- not rough translations, but words that cover the same semantic range and participate in the same combinatorial patterns.
3. **Its combinatorial properties are consistent across languages** -- it combines with other primes in the same grammatical patterns regardless of which language you test.

This is not armchair theorizing. Primes have been tested and confirmed across more than 30 typologically diverse languages, spanning every major language family: Indo-European (English, Polish, Russian, Spanish, French, Italian, Swedish, Danish), Sino-Tibetan (Mandarin), Japonic (Japanese), Koreanic (Korean), Tai-Kadai (Lao), Austronesian (Malay, Mbula), Australian (Yankunytjatjara, Arrernte), Niger-Congo (Ewe), Afro-Asiatic (Amharic), and others. The languages include both literate traditions and oral cultures.

## 3. The Complete Inventory: 65 Semantic Primes

The 65 primes fall into 15 categories. Here is the complete inventory, with each prime in the form typically used in English (remembering that the English words are *exponents* of the primes, not the primes themselves -- the underlying concepts have independent existence in every tested language).

**Substantives** (6): I, YOU, SOMEONE/PERSON, PEOPLE, SOMETHING/THING, BODY

**Determiners** (3): THIS, THE SAME, OTHER/ELSE

**Quantifiers** (5): ONE, TWO, SOME, ALL, MUCH/MANY

**Evaluators** (2): GOOD, BAD

**Descriptors** (2): BIG, SMALL

**Mental Predicates** (7): THINK, KNOW, WANT, DON'T WANT, FEEL, SEE, HEAR

**Speech** (3): SAY, WORDS, TRUE

**Actions, Events, and Movement** (3): DO, HAPPEN, MOVE

**Existence and Possession** (3): THERE IS/EXIST, BE (SOMEONE/SOMETHING), HAVE

**Life and Death** (2): LIVE, DIE

**Time** (8): WHEN/TIME, NOW, BEFORE, AFTER, A LONG TIME, A SHORT TIME, FOR SOME TIME, MOMENT

**Space** (9): WHERE/PLACE, HERE, ABOVE, BELOW, FAR, NEAR, SIDE, INSIDE, TOUCH

**Logical Concepts** (5): NOT, MAYBE, CAN, BECAUSE, IF

**Intensifier/Augmentor** (2): VERY, MORE

**Taxonomy and Partonomy** (2): KIND OF, PART OF

**Similarity** (1): LIKE/AS/WAY

### Key Properties of Primes

Several properties distinguish these from ordinary words:

- Each prime has its own lexical embodiment in every tested language -- it is not a gap that must be filled by circumlocution.
- Many primes have **allolexes** -- multiple surface forms that express the same underlying concept. In English, "someone" and "person" are allolexes of the same prime.
- Each prime has specific grammatical (combinatorial) properties that are consistent across languages. GOOD combines with SOMETHING as "something good." THINK combines with SOMEONE as "someone thinks."
- **DON'T WANT is a separate prime**, not simply NOT + WANT. This reflects the cross-linguistic finding that many languages have a single, non-decomposable word for refusal. This is significant for visual language design: some apparently compound concepts must be treated as atomic.

## 4. Semantic Molecules: The Intermediate Layer

Between the 65 atomic primes and the unlimited complexity of everyday concepts lies an intermediate layer: **semantic molecules**. These are words that are not themselves primes but can be defined entirely using primes, and that serve as necessary building blocks for defining more complex concepts.

> **Correction**: The original research estimated ~50 universal semantic molecules. Validation reveals a more nuanced picture. Approximately 25 molecules appear to be truly universal (found across all tested languages). A broader inventory of approximately 60-80 molecules may be near-universal. For English alone, approximately 180 productive semantic molecules have been catalogued -- but most of these are language-specific rather than universal.

Categories of molecules include:

- **Body parts**: HANDS, MOUTH, LEGS, EYES, EARS, HEAD
- **Physical activities**: EAT, DRINK, SIT, LIE DOWN, SLEEP
- **The verb MAKE**: (distinguished from DO)
- **Shape descriptors**: ROUND, LONG, FLAT
- **Physical qualities**: HARD, SOFT, SHARP
- **Environmental**: WATER, FIRE, SUN, GROUND/EARTH, SKY
- **Social**: MAN, WOMAN, CHILD
- **Taxonomic**: CREATURE/ANIMAL, PLANT

### Why Molecules Matter for Visual Language Design

Defining complex concepts directly from primes often produces very long, unwieldy explications. Consider the molecule EAT:

> X does something with the mouth to something. Because of this, something goes inside X's body. X does this because X wants this to be inside X's body.

Every word in that definition is a semantic prime. But including this full decomposition every time you need to reference eating would make any communication system impractical. Molecules serve as **compression** -- pre-built meaningful chunks that simplify the path from primes to complex meaning.

For a visual language, molecules suggest a natural second layer of symbols above the prime inventory. The design question is whether molecule symbols should be compositionally transparent (visibly built from prime symbols) or conventionally opaque (learned as units, like Chinese characters for common words).

## 5. The Explication Method: Defining Anything from Atoms

The core technology of NSM is the **explication** -- a reductive paraphrase that captures the complete meaning of a concept using only semantic primes, arranged in canonical sentences. An explication is not a dictionary definition; it is a decomposition that eliminates all circularity.

### Example: "Happy"

> X felt something good at that time.
> X felt something like this:
> "something good is happening to me"
> "I want this"
> "this is good"
> X felt like this because X thought something good.

Every word in this explication -- X, FELT, SOMETHING, GOOD, TIME, LIKE, HAPPEN, I, WANT, THIS, THINK, BECAUSE -- is a semantic prime. The explication captures what "happy" means across languages by stripping away culturally specific connotations while preserving the universal core.

### Example: "Angry"

> X felt something bad at that time.
> X felt something like this:
> "someone did something bad"
> "I don't want this"
> "I want to do something because of this"
> X felt like this because X thought something like this.

Note how the structural difference between "happy" and "angry" is precisely captured: happy involves GOOD + HAPPEN + WANT (good things happening, wanted), while angry involves BAD + SOMEONE DID + DON'T WANT + WANT TO DO (bad things done by someone, unwanted, provoking desire for action).

### Example: "Afraid"

> X felt something bad at that time.
> X felt something like this:
> "something bad can happen to me"
> "I don't want this"
> "I can't do anything"
> X felt like this because X thought something like this.

The structural contrast with "angry" is instructive: both involve BAD + DON'T WANT, but angry adds WANT TO DO (agency) while afraid adds CAN'T DO ANYTHING (helplessness) and CAN HAPPEN (possibility rather than actuality). The primes capture these distinctions with precision.

### The Key Insight for Visual Language

Every NSM explication is a compositional structure built from primes. If each prime can be assigned a visual symbol, then any NSM explication can -- in principle -- be visually composed. The concept "happy" would become a visual sentence: the symbol for SOMEONE, combined with the symbol for FEEL, combined with SOMETHING + GOOD + HAPPEN + WANT + THINK + BECAUSE.

The question is whether the resulting visual compositions are readable, learnable, and efficient enough for actual communication. And that question leads directly to the visual representability problem.

## 6. The Visual Representability Challenge

Not all primes are equally amenable to visual representation. The 65 primes fall into three broad zones:

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-3-visual-representability -- Visual Representability Spectrum of Semantic Primes. An SVG three-zone spectrum classifying all 65 primes: green zone (~25 primes, directly imageable -- spatial primes like ABOVE/BELOW, substantives like I/YOU/BODY, descriptors like BIG/SMALL), amber zone (~20 primes, metaphorically groundable -- GOOD/BAD via UP-DOWN metaphor, BEFORE/AFTER via timeline, FEEL via body metaphor), and red zone (~20 primes, necessarily conventional -- IF, NOT, BECAUSE, MAYBE, THINK, KNOW, WANT, SAY, TRUE). -->

### Zone 1: Directly Imageable (~25 primes)

These primes have natural visual forms:

- **Spatial primes** are inherently visual: ABOVE, BELOW, NEAR, FAR, INSIDE, SIDE, HERE, TOUCH, WHERE/PLACE. A visual language can represent these through spatial arrangement itself -- position on the page literally encodes the meaning.
- **Substantives** with bodily referents: I (pointing to self), YOU (pointing to other), SOMEONE/PERSON (figure), PEOPLE (group), BODY (human form).
- **Descriptors**: BIG, SMALL -- relative size is visually self-evident.
- **Senses**: SEE (eye), HEAR (ear), MOVE (motion arrow).
- **Life**: LIVE, DIE -- an alive figure versus a crossed-out or fallen figure.
- **Basic quantity**: ONE, TWO -- dots or tally marks. PART OF -- a piece detached from a whole.

These primes are the easy part of visual language design. They are also the least interesting, because they are precisely the primes that any pictographic system can handle.

### Zone 2: Metaphorically Groundable (~20 primes)

These primes resist direct depiction but can be visually represented through established conceptual metaphors -- the same metaphors that cognitive linguistics has documented across languages:

- **GOOD and BAD** through the UP-DOWN metaphor: GOOD IS UP, BAD IS DOWN. An upward arrow or elevated position for GOOD; a downward arrow or lowered position for BAD. This metaphor is cross-culturally robust.
- **BEFORE and AFTER** through the timeline metaphor: TIME IS A HORIZONTAL LINE. Earlier events on the left, later events on the right. NOW as a center point. MOMENT as a point, A LONG TIME as an extended segment.
- **FEEL** through the body metaphor: a hand over the heart or chest.
- **SOME, ALL, MUCH/MANY** through quantity metaphors: partial fill, complete fill, overflowing container.
- **DO** through action metaphor: a hand in motion.
- **THIS** through pointing: a directional gesture.

These metaphorical representations are not iconic -- an upward arrow does not look like "good." But the metaphors are grounded in embodied experience (we stand upright when happy, slump when sad) and are documented across many languages and cultures. The empirical question is whether they are stable enough across all cultures to serve as a foundation for visual language.

### Zone 3: Necessarily Conventional (~20 primes)

These primes are highly abstract and resist both iconic and metaphorical visual representation:

- **Logical primes**: NOT, MAYBE, CAN, BECAUSE, IF -- the machinery of reasoning.
- **Mental predicates**: THINK, KNOW, WANT, DON'T WANT -- the machinery of inner life.
- **Speech and truth**: SAY, WORDS, TRUE -- concepts that directly reference the speech modality.
- **Abstract relations**: THE SAME, OTHER/ELSE, KIND OF, LIKE/AS/WAY, VERY, MORE.
- **Existence/events**: HAPPEN, THERE IS/EXIST, BE, HAVE.

These ~20 primes are where the hardest design challenges lie. They are precisely the primes needed for grammar, logic, and complex thought -- the primes that make the difference between a picture dictionary and a language. Some possible strategies:

- **Conventional symbols with metaphorical justification**: NOT as a blocked path (the BLOCKAGE schema), IF as a forking path (the PATH-WITH-BRANCH schema), BECAUSE as a force arrow (the FORCE schema), MAYBE as a dashed or uncertain path. These are not iconic, but they have metaphorical grounding that could aid learnability.
- **Spatial grammar**: Use position rather than shape to encode abstract relationships. A symbol placed below the baseline could indicate negation. A symbol inside a dashed boundary could indicate possibility.
- **Purely arbitrary but learnable symbols**: Like the mathematical symbols +, -, =, which are completely conventional but learnable through use.

The design question is which strategy -- or combination of strategies -- minimizes learning cost while maximizing consistency and cross-cultural accessibility.

## 7. NSM Primes and Image Schemas: Complementary Inventories

A crucial insight for visual language design: NSM primes and image schemas are not competing frameworks. They are complementary inventories that cover different aspects of the same problem.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-4-nsm-image-schema-overlap -- NSM Primes vs. Image Schemas -- Overlap Map. An SVG Venn diagram with three zones: left circle (NSM primes without image schema counterparts: IF, NOT, TRUE, THINK, KNOW, WANT, SAY, KIND OF, VERY, MORE...), overlap zone (shared concepts: INSIDE/CONTAINER, ABOVE-BELOW/UP-DOWN, MOVE/PATH, PART OF/PART-WHOLE, TOUCH/CONTACT, with metaphorical bridges: BECAUSE~FORCE, WANT~ATTRACTION, NOT~BLOCKAGE, IF~PATH-BRANCH), and right circle (image schemas without prime counterparts: BALANCE, CYCLE, LINK, CENTER-PERIPHERY, FORCE, COUNTERFORCE, SPLITTING, MERGING, FULL-EMPTY). -->

### The Overlap Zone: The Visual Sweet Spot

Several primes have direct image schema counterparts:

| NSM Prime | Image Schema | Visual Form |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| INSIDE | CONTAINER | Enclosed space |
| ABOVE / BELOW | UP-DOWN / VERTICALITY | Vertical position |
| NEAR / FAR | PROXIMITY / DISTANCE | Spatial distance |
| MOVE | PATH / SOURCE-PATH-GOAL | Arrow along trajectory |
| PART OF | PART-WHOLE | Piece detached from whole |
| TOUCH | CONTACT | Elements meeting at boundary |

These overlap concepts represent the visual sweet spot -- they are both semantically primitive (part of the irreducible foundation of meaning) and spatially groundable (they have natural visual forms). A visual language symbol for INSIDE can simultaneously represent the semantic prime and the image schema, because they are the same underlying structure.

### Primes Without Schema Counterparts

Many abstract primes (IF, NOT, BECAUSE, TRUE, MAYBE, KNOW, THINK, WANT) have no direct image schema equivalents. However, some can be metaphorically bridged:

- **BECAUSE** maps onto the FORCE schema through the conceptual metaphor CAUSATION IS FORCE DYNAMICS. A cause "pushes" an effect into being.
- **WANT** maps onto ATTRACTION / COMPULSION schemas. Desire "pulls" toward an object.
- **NOT** maps onto BLOCKAGE / REMOVAL schemas. Negation "blocks" or "removes" an assertion.
- **IF** maps onto PATH-WITH-BRANCH. Conditionality is a "fork in the road."

These metaphorical bridges are the most promising avenue for visually representing abstract primes. But they must be used carefully -- the mappings need to be consistent, cross-culturally stable, and learnable.

### Schemas Without Prime Counterparts

Several image schemas have no direct prime equivalents:

- BALANCE (equilibrium, axis)
- CYCLE (recurring pattern)
- LINK (connection between entities)
- CENTER-PERIPHERY
- SPLITTING / MERGING
- FULL-EMPTY

These schemas provide additional visual vocabulary beyond what NSM requires. They can serve as the basis for representing concepts that go beyond the prime inventory -- a richer visual grammar that draws on the full repertoire of embodied spatial experience.

### The Design Implication

A visual language likely needs both inventories working together:

- **NSM primes** define *what must be representable* -- the semantic atoms from which all meanings can be composed.
- **Image schemas** define *how to represent it visually* -- the spatial-bodily structures that provide natural visual forms.
- **Conceptual metaphors** bridge the gap between the two, mapping abstract semantic primes onto visual image schema forms.

## 8. The Proposed Architecture

Drawing together NSM, image schemas, and the historical lessons from previous visual languages, a four-layer architecture emerges:

**Layer 0 -- Image Schema Foundation**: The visual primitives. CONTAINER, PATH, FORCE, BALANCE, UP-DOWN, LINK, SCALE, CYCLE, and others. These provide the raw visual vocabulary -- shapes, spatial arrangements, and dynamic patterns that can be combined into symbols.

**Layer 1 -- Semantic Primes**: All 65 primes mapped to visual symbols. The imageable primes (~25) use image schema icons directly. The metaphorically groundable primes (~20) use image schemas through documented metaphorical mappings. The necessarily conventional primes (~20) receive learnable symbols with metaphorical justification where possible.

**Layer 2 -- Semantic Molecules**: Approximately 25 universal molecules composed from prime symbols. These serve as compression -- frequently used building blocks that do not need to be decomposed in every utterance. HANDS, MOUTH, EAT, WATER, FIRE, MAN, WOMAN, CHILD, ROUND, HARD.

**Layer 3 -- Open Composition**: Any concept, however complex, definable through visual explication using primes and molecules. HAPPY = visual-SOMEONE + visual-FEEL + visual-SOMETHING + visual-GOOD + visual-HAPPEN. JUSTICE, JEALOUSY, BREAKFAST, DEMOCRACY -- all expressible through composition.

This architecture is a hypothesis. It has not been built or tested. The gap between "any concept can be explicated in primes" (linguistically proven) and "any explication can be visually composed and read" (unproven) is where the critical open questions lie.

## 9. Criticisms and Limitations

The NSM program is not without criticism, and intellectual honesty requires acknowledging these:

### Are 65 Primes Sufficient?

Some linguists argue that 65 primes are too few to capture the full complexity of human meaning. Defining complex technical, emotional, or cultural concepts in terms of only 65 building blocks produces very long, unwieldy explications that may lose nuance. A visual language may need to tolerate this: the price of universality is verbosity.

### Is Universality Overstated?

While primes have been tested in 30+ languages, the claim of exact lexical equivalence across all human languages is extraordinarily strong. The NSM program itself acknowledges "a couple of difficult and/or contested cases." The question is whether these edge cases represent noise in an otherwise robust pattern or cracks in the foundation.

### The Semantic Molecules Problem

Critics argue that semantic molecules "do little more than conceal complexity" -- that the decomposed forms of molecules show they are not truly necessary as a separate layer. For visual language design, this criticism has practical implications: if molecules are merely convenient shortcuts rather than a structurally necessary layer, the architecture could potentially be simplified.

### English-Centric Presentation

Despite cross-linguistic testing, the primes are typically presented and discussed using English exponents. Critics argue this creates an English-centric framing that may bias the identification of "universal" concepts. A visual language, by its nature, would sidestep this criticism -- visual symbols have no linguistic bias.

### Circularity Concerns

Defining primes as "indefinable" while using them in complex combinatorial patterns raises philosophical questions about whether the combinatorial rules themselves introduce hidden complexity. This is a legitimate concern, but it applies equally to any foundational system (atoms in chemistry, axioms in mathematics).

## 10. What NSM Means for the Visual Language Project

The NSM program provides the most rigorous answer available to the question "what must a visual language be able to say?" The answer is: everything that can be composed from 65 semantic primes. If the visual language can represent all 65 primes and provides rules for composing them, it can -- in principle -- express anything any human language can express.

The three critical design challenges that emerge:

1. **The visual grounding of abstract primes**: How do you draw IF, NOT, BECAUSE, MAYBE? This is not a question about graphic design -- it is a question about whether the metaphorical mappings between abstract concepts and spatial structures are stable, learnable, and cross-culturally consistent.

2. **The readability of visual explications**: A visual sentence expressing "happy" requires combining 8-10 prime symbols. "Jealousy" might require 20+. At what point does a visual explication become too long to read as a gestalt? And can compositional transparency (seeing the internal structure of a complex symbol) compensate for length?

3. **The grammar question**: NSM explications use simple sentence structures ("X felt something like this," "X thought something like this"). But even these simple structures require a visual grammar -- a way to visually encode who is the X, what is the something, and how "like this" relates to what follows. This grammar is a design problem separate from (and arguably harder than) the prime symbol design.

These are genuine open problems. But NSM transforms them from vague aspirations ("create a visual language") into specific, tractable design challenges with clear success criteria. That is the gift of having a principled semantic foundation.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| NSM | Natural Semantic Metalanguage -- a theory of semantic analysis that defines all meanings through irreducible primes |
| Semantic prime | A concept that cannot be defined in simpler terms and has exact equivalents in all known languages; also called semantic primitive or conceptual atom |
| Semantic molecule | An intermediate concept defined from primes that serves as a building block for more complex meanings |
| Explication | A reductive paraphrase that defines a concept entirely in terms of primes and molecules, eliminating circularity |
| Allolexes | Multiple surface forms that express the same underlying prime concept (e.g., "someone" and "person" in English) |
| Reductive paraphrase | The method of defining a concept by expressing it in simpler terms, ultimately reaching primes |
| Cross-linguistic equivalent | A word in another language that covers the same semantic range and combinatorial properties as a prime |
| Combinatorial properties | The grammatical patterns in which a prime participates (e.g., GOOD combines with SOMETHING as "something good") |
| Imageability | The ease with which a concept can evoke a mental image; also called concreteness |
| Experiential grounding | The process by which abstract concepts acquire meaning through connection to bodily/spatial experience; also called embodied grounding |
| Image schema | A pre-linguistic, recurrent pattern of bodily experience that structures conceptual understanding (e.g., CONTAINER, PATH, FORCE) |
| Conceptual metaphor | A systematic mapping from a concrete source domain to an abstract target domain (e.g., HAPPY IS UP) |
| Visual representability | The degree to which a concept can be encoded as a visual symbol, ranging from directly imageable to necessarily conventional |
