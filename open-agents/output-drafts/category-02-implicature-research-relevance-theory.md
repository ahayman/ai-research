# Relevance Theory (Sperber & Wilson): Research for Implicature Knowledge Base

## Research Purpose
This document provides comprehensive research on Relevance Theory as it relates to conversational implicature, written for a knowledge base teaching English pragmatics to autistic individuals. Relevance Theory is highlighted as a potentially more autistic-friendly framework than Grice's maxims because of its computational, systematic, and cognitively-grounded character.

---

## 1. Overview of Relevance Theory

### Origins and Development

Relevance Theory was developed by the French cognitive scientist **Dan Sperber** and the British linguist **Deirdre Wilson**. Their seminal work, *Relevance: Communication and Cognition*, was first published in **1986** and substantially revised in **1995** (second edition). The theory grew out of Grice's foundational work on meaning and communication but departed from it in fundamental ways, grounding pragmatics in cognitive psychology rather than in social cooperation.

The theory has been further developed by key collaborators including **Robyn Carston** (who extended the theory's treatment of explicature, lexical pragmatics, and the semantics-pragmatics distinction) and **Francisco Yus** (who applied the framework to digital communication).

**Key publications:**
- Sperber, D. & Wilson, D. (1986/1995). *Relevance: Communication and Cognition*. Oxford: Blackwell.
- Wilson, D. & Sperber, D. (2002). "Relevance Theory." UCL Working Papers in Linguistics 14: 249-287.
- Wilson, D. & Sperber, D. (2004). "Relevance Theory." In L. Horn & G. Ward (Eds.), *The Handbook of Pragmatics*. Oxford: Blackwell, 607-632.
- Carston, R. (2002). *Thoughts and Utterances: The Pragmatics of Explicit Communication*. Oxford: Blackwell.
- Wilson, D. & Sperber, D. (2012). *Meaning and Relevance*. Cambridge University Press.

### How Relevance Theory Differs from Grice's Cooperative Principle

Grice proposed that conversation is governed by a **Cooperative Principle** supported by **four maxims** (Quality, Quantity, Relation, Manner). Speakers are expected to follow these maxims, and when they appear to violate one, hearers infer an implicature to preserve the assumption of cooperation. This framework treats communication as fundamentally social and cooperative.

Relevance Theory departs from Grice in several critical ways:

| Feature | Grice's Framework | Relevance Theory |
|---------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Foundation** | Social cooperation | Cognitive architecture |
| **Number of principles** | 1 principle + 4 maxims | 2 principles (one cognitive, one communicative) |
| **What drives interpretation** | Assumption that speakers cooperate | Assumption that speakers aim for relevance |
| **Figurative language** | Violations of maxims (e.g., metaphor "violates" Quality) | No violation at all -- just alternative routes to relevance |
| **Implicature derivation** | Reasoning about maxim violation | Following a comprehension *procedure* |
| **Context** | Shared knowledge, social norms | Cognitive environment (what's manifest to the individual) |
| **Scope** | Primarily conversational | All ostensive communication (including non-verbal) |

**The core shift:** Grice asks "What social rules is the speaker following or appearing to break?" Relevance Theory asks "What interpretation yields the best cognitive payoff for the least processing effort?"

This shift matters enormously for autistic learners. Grice's framework requires understanding social norms and cooperation as a starting point. Relevance Theory starts from how the *mind processes information* -- a more tractable foundation.

### The Two Principles of Relevance

Relevance Theory rests on two fundamental principles:

#### Cognitive Principle of Relevance
> **Human cognition tends to be geared to the maximization of relevance.**

This is a claim about how human minds work in general -- not just in communication. Our cognitive systems are oriented toward processing information that gives us the greatest cognitive benefit for the least processing cost. We automatically pay attention to stimuli that seem relevant and ignore those that don't. This is not a choice or a social norm; it is a fundamental property of cognition.

Think of it as an efficiency principle built into the brain: given limited processing resources, the mind preferentially allocates attention to inputs that promise the most useful cognitive changes.

#### Communicative Principle of Relevance
> **Every act of ostensive communication conveys a presumption of its own optimal relevance.**

When someone intentionally communicates with you (produces an "ostensive stimulus" -- a signal designed to attract your attention and convey meaning), they are implicitly guaranteeing that what they're saying is *worth your processing effort*. The act of deliberately communicating creates an expectation: "This will be relevant enough to be worth your time and effort to interpret."

This principle does the work of all four of Grice's maxims at once. A speaker doesn't need to separately follow rules about being truthful, informative, relevant, and clear -- they simply need to make their utterance optimally relevant, which encompasses all of these considerations.

### What "Relevance" Means Technically

Relevance is defined as a property of inputs to cognitive processes (utterances, thoughts, perceptions, memories). It is a **balance of two factors**:

1. **Cognitive effects** (also called "contextual effects"): The positive changes an input makes to your representation of the world. These include:
   - **Contextual implications**: New conclusions you can derive by combining the new information with what you already know
   - **Strengthening**: Increasing your confidence in an existing assumption
   - **Contradicting and eliminating**: Providing evidence against an existing assumption, leading you to revise your beliefs

2. **Processing effort**: The mental resources required to derive those cognitive effects. This includes effort for:
   - Perception and decoding of the linguistic signal
   - Accessing relevant contextual assumptions from memory
   - Drawing inferences

**The formula (informal):**

> Relevance increases with greater cognitive effects.
> Relevance decreases with greater processing effort.
> **Optimal relevance = the best achievable ratio of effects to effort.**

**Formal extent conditions (from Sperber & Wilson 1995):**
- *Extent condition 1*: An assumption is relevant in a context to the extent that its contextual effects in that context are large.
- *Extent condition 2*: An assumption is relevant in a context to the extent that the effort required to process it in that context is small.

### Presumption of Optimal Relevance

When a speaker produces an utterance, the hearer is entitled to expect **optimal relevance**, which has two clauses:

> **(a)** The ostensive stimulus is relevant enough for it to be worth the addressee's effort to process it.
> **(b)** The ostensive stimulus is the most relevant one compatible with the communicator's abilities and preferences.

Clause (a) sets a minimum threshold: the utterance must at least be worth processing. Clause (b) is more demanding: the speaker has chosen the *best* way to communicate this, given their constraints. However, clause (b) includes the qualifier "compatible with the communicator's abilities and preferences" -- which means speakers might sometimes be less than maximally clear because of their own limitations (they can't think of a better way to say it) or their own interests (they might want to be somewhat vague for strategic reasons).

## 2. The Comprehension Procedure

One of Relevance Theory's most distinctive contributions is the **Relevance-Theoretic Comprehension Procedure** -- a heuristic algorithm that describes how hearers actually process utterances. This is not merely a set of norms (like Grice's maxims) but a *procedure* -- a step-by-step process that the mind follows.

### The Procedure (as stated by Wilson & Sperber 2004)

> **a.** Follow a path of least effort in computing cognitive effects: test interpretive hypotheses (disambiguations, reference resolutions, implicatures, etc.) in order of accessibility.
>
> **b.** Stop when your expectations of relevance are satisfied.

### Breaking This Down Step by Step

When you hear an utterance, your mind does the following:

**Step 1: Decode the linguistic signal.**
Extract the "logical form" -- the basic linguistic meaning of the words and their syntactic structure. This gives you an incomplete, schematic representation. Natural language massively underdetermines what a speaker means (this is Carston's "semantic underdeterminacy thesis").

**Step 2: Enrich the logical form to construct an explicature.**
Fill in what's missing:
- Resolve ambiguities (which meaning of a word?)
- Assign references (who does "he" refer to?)
- Narrow or broaden word meanings (when someone says "drink," do they mean any liquid or specifically alcohol?)
- Supply unarticulated constituents (if someone says "It's raining," figure out *where*)

**Step 3: Simultaneously derive implicatures.**
Access contextual assumptions from memory and derive implicit conclusions.

**Step 4: Check the result against expectations of relevance.**
Does this interpretation yield enough cognitive effects? If yes, stop. If no, try the next most accessible interpretation.

### Critical Feature: Mutual Parallel Adjustment

A crucial insight of the theory is that steps 2 and 3 do **not** happen sequentially. The hearer does not first fully determine what is explicitly communicated and then derive implicatures from that. Instead, **explicatures and implicatures are derived in parallel through a process of mutual adjustment**.

As Sperber and Wilson explain: the process does not run in sequential order. An addressee does not first decode the utterance, then derive explicatures, then select implicated premises, and then derive implicated conclusions. Instead, all of these subtasks run in parallel, and each can aid in solving the others.

This means:
- A tentative hypothesis about what implicature the speaker intended can help you figure out what they explicitly meant
- A tentative hypothesis about explicit meaning can suggest what implicatures to look for
- Contextual assumptions are accessed and tested alongside both

### Practical Example of the Procedure

**Context:** You ask a colleague, "Are you coming to the meeting?"
**They reply:** "I have a dentist appointment."

Your mind's procedure:
1. **Decode**: Linguistic meaning of "I have a dentist appointment" is processed
2. **Enrich** (begin constructing explicature): The speaker has a dentist appointment [today/at the relevant time]
3. **Access contextual assumptions**: People generally can't be in two places at once; dentist appointments are typically mandatory; the meeting and the appointment presumably conflict in time
4. **Derive implicature**: The speaker will not be coming to the meeting
5. **Check relevance**: This interpretation answers my question (high cognitive effects) and was reached without excessive effort -- expectations of relevance satisfied. **Stop.**

The procedure is satisfied at the first interpretation that meets relevance expectations. You don't need to consider alternative interpretations (like "They're telling me about their dental health because they think I'm interested") because the first accessible interpretation already provides adequate cognitive effects.

### The "First Satisfactory Interpretation" Principle

This is critical: the procedure tells you to **stop at the first interpretation that satisfies your expectations of relevance**. You don't need to find the *best* interpretation -- just the first *good enough* one. This is cognitively efficient but also means misunderstandings can occur when the first accessible interpretation isn't what the speaker intended.

## 3. Key Concepts in Detail

### Ostensive-Inferential Communication

Relevance Theory characterizes human verbal communication as **ostensive-inferential**. This has two sides:

**Ostension (speaker's side):** The speaker produces a stimulus that is designed to attract the hearer's attention and make manifest their intention to communicate. This is "ostensive" behavior -- behavior that openly signals communicative intent. The speaker has two layers of intention:
- **Informative intention**: The intention to inform the audience of something (the content)
- **Communicative intention**: The intention to make the audience recognize that the speaker has an informative intention (the meta-signal "I am trying to tell you something")

**Inference (hearer's side):** The hearer recognizes the ostensive signal and uses inference (not just decoding) to figure out what the speaker intended to communicate. Understanding an utterance goes far beyond decoding words -- it requires inferring the speaker's intentions.

This two-layer model is important: communication requires not just transmitting information but making it *mutually manifest* that communication is happening. When someone says "Nice weather, isn't it?" at a bus stop, the ostensive signal isn't just about the weather -- it signals "I am initiating social interaction with you."

### Cognitive Environment and Mutual Manifestness

Rather than Grice's notion of "common knowledge" or "shared knowledge," Relevance Theory uses the concepts of **cognitive environment** and **mutual manifestness**:

- **Manifest**: A fact or assumption is *manifest* to an individual if they are capable of representing it mentally and accepting it as true or probably true at a given time.
- **Cognitive environment**: The total set of assumptions manifest to an individual at any given time. This includes not just what they currently know, but what they *could* infer given their perceptual and cognitive abilities in their current situation.
- **Mutual cognitive environment**: A set of assumptions that is manifest to two or more individuals. Within a mutual cognitive environment, every manifest assumption is *mutually manifest*.
- **Mutual manifestness**: The state in which assumptions are shared across participants' cognitive environments and this sharing is itself manifest to all parties.

This is more psychologically realistic than "common knowledge" because it doesn't require people to actually *know* the same things -- only that certain assumptions are *available* to them. Communication is, in this framework, the communicator's attempt to modify the degree of manifestness of certain assumptions in the hearer's cognitive environment.

### Cognitive Effects

Cognitive effects (also called "contextual effects") are the changes that processing an input makes to an individual's beliefs or representation of the world. There are three main types:

1. **Contextual implications (new conclusions):** The new input combines with existing assumptions to yield conclusions that neither the input alone nor the existing assumptions alone could yield.
   - Example: You know "If it rains, the picnic is cancelled" (existing assumption). You hear "It's raining" (new input). You derive "The picnic is cancelled" (contextual implication).

2. **Strengthening of existing assumptions:** The new input provides additional evidence for something you already believed.
   - Example: You suspected your colleague was job-hunting. You hear they had a long lunch meeting. This strengthens your existing suspicion.

3. **Contradiction and elimination of existing assumptions:** The new input provides evidence against something you previously believed, potentially causing you to abandon that belief.
   - Example: You believed the restaurant was open on Mondays. Someone tells you it's closed for renovation. You revise your assumption.

An input that has no cognitive effects at all is irrelevant. An input that merely duplicates what you already know (without strengthening or modifying it) is irrelevant. Relevance requires *change* in your cognitive landscape.

### Processing Effort

Processing effort is the cognitive resources required to parse, decode, and derive interpretations from an input. Factors that increase processing effort include:

- Complex or unusual syntactic structures
- Unfamiliar vocabulary
- Ambiguity requiring resolution
- Accessing distant or obscure contextual assumptions
- Multiple inferential steps needed to reach the intended interpretation
- Poor articulation, noise, or other perceptual difficulties
- Figurative or indirect language (potentially -- though this is debated)

**Key insight:** All else being equal, hearers prefer the interpretation that requires the least processing effort. But greater processing effort can be justified if the cognitive effects are correspondingly greater. A dense, complex sentence might be optimally relevant if it communicates something extremely important that couldn't be said more simply.

### Explicature vs. Implicature

Relevance Theory makes a fundamental distinction between what is **explicitly communicated** (explicatures) and what is **implicitly communicated** (implicatures), but this distinction works differently from Grice's distinction between "what is said" and "what is implicated."

#### Explicature
An **explicature** is a communicated assumption that is derived partly by decoding (the linguistic meaning of the utterance) and partly by inference (pragmatic enrichment). It is an *enriched* version of the logical form of the utterance.

Crucially, explicatures go well beyond what Grice called "what is said." The process of constructing an explicature involves:
- **Disambiguation**: Choosing among possible word meanings
- **Reference assignment**: Determining who/what pronouns and other expressions refer to
- **Saturation**: Filling in contextually required values (when someone says "It's too hot," determining *too hot for what*)
- **Free enrichment**: Adding conceptual material not mandated by the linguistic form (when someone says "I've eaten," enriching this to "I've eaten [today/recently]")
- **Ad hoc concept construction**: Adjusting encoded word meanings through narrowing (restricting a word's denotation) or broadening (extending it). When someone says "The steak is raw," they likely mean RAW* (an ad hoc concept meaning something like "very undercooked") rather than literally uncooked.

#### Implicature
An **implicature** is a communicated assumption derived wholly by inference, without being a development of the logical form. It is never directly encoded. Implicatures come in two types:

- **Implicated premises**: Contextual assumptions the hearer must supply to make the utterance optimally relevant.
- **Implicated conclusions**: Conclusions the hearer is expected to derive from the explicature combined with the implicated premises.

**Example:**
- A: "Would you like some coffee?"
- B: "Coffee would keep me awake."
- **Explicature of B's utterance**: Coffee would keep B awake [tonight/at the relevant time].
- **Implicated premise**: B does not want to be kept awake tonight.
- **Implicated conclusion**: B does not want coffee.

The explicature-implicature distinction is gradient, not sharp. There's a continuum from strongly explicitly communicated content to weakly implicitly communicated content. This is one of the areas where Relevance Theory is more nuanced than the Gricean framework.

### Ad Hoc Concepts and Lexical Pragmatics

An important extension of Relevance Theory, developed primarily by Robyn Carston, concerns **lexical pragmatics** -- how the meanings of individual words are adjusted in context. The central claim is that the concept a word contributes to interpretation is often not the concept it encodes but an **ad hoc concept** -- a context-specific, occasion-bound meaning constructed on the fly.

This process takes two main forms:

1. **Narrowing (restricting the denotation):** The word "drink" used to mean specifically "alcoholic drink." The word "cut" in "cut the grass" vs. "cut the cake" activates different narrowed meanings.

2. **Broadening (extending the denotation):** The word "flat" used to describe a surface that is approximately (not perfectly) flat. The word "raw" used for a steak that is undercooked (not literally raw).

Metaphor, in this framework, is simply an extreme case of broadening. There is a continuum from literal use through loose use through hyperbole through metaphor -- all involving the same basic cognitive process of concept adjustment guided by relevance.

## 4. How Relevance Theory Handles Specific Phenomena

### Irony: The Echoic Account

Relevance Theory offers one of the most distinctive and well-developed accounts of irony in pragmatics. Rather than treating irony as "saying the opposite of what you mean" (a common simplification) or as a violation of the Quality maxim (Grice's approach), Sperber and Wilson analyze irony as an **echoic use of language**.

#### What "echoic" means:
An utterance is echoic when it achieves its relevance by representing (echoing) another utterance or thought and expressing an attitude toward it. The speaker doesn't assert the proposition; they *attribute* it to someone else (or to a general expectation, norm, or their own past belief) and express an attitude toward it.

#### The three components of irony:
For an utterance to be interpreted as ironic, the hearer must identify:
1. **That the speaker is echoing** someone's thought or words (not asserting them as their own current belief)
2. **The source** of those thoughts or words (who originally held or expressed them -- this could be a specific person, a general social expectation, or the speaker's own past self)
3. **That the speaker is dissociating** from or rejecting those thoughts or words

#### The attitude of dissociation:
Ironical attitudes are drawn from the "dissociative range" -- the speaker distances themselves from the attributed thought as ludicrously false, under-informative, or irrelevant. The specific flavor of dissociation can vary: wry, skeptical, bitter, mocking, contemptuous, amused, or resigned.

#### Example:
You plan a picnic. It pours with rain. Your friend says: "Lovely weather for a picnic."

- **What's being echoed**: The expectation (perhaps expressed earlier) that the weather would be good for the picnic
- **The source**: Either someone's earlier prediction or the general expectation/hope
- **The dissociative attitude**: The speaker treats the echoed thought as ludicrously false -- the weather is obviously terrible
- **Why it works**: The listener recognizes the gap between the echoed expectation and reality, and recognizes the speaker's mockery of that expectation

#### Why this is better than "saying the opposite":
The "saying the opposite" account fails for many real cases of irony. Consider: someone who consistently makes bad decisions says "I'm so smart." An ironic repetition of "Yeah, you're *so* smart" isn't just saying the opposite of what they mean (it doesn't reduce to "You're stupid"). It's specifically echoing and mocking the person's self-assessment.

#### Irony vs. echoic use more broadly:
Not all echoic utterances are ironic. You can echo someone's words with endorsement ("That's exactly right"), doubt ("Really?"), or surprise. Irony specifically involves a **dissociative** attitude toward the echoed content.

#### The pretense theory alternative:
Some researchers (notably Clark & Gerrig 1984) propose that irony involves *pretense* rather than echo: the ironic speaker pretends to be a person making a sincere assertion, and invites the audience to see through the pretense. Wilson and Sperber have argued against this account, noting that the echoic account handles cases where the speaker echoes a private thought (not a speech act) -- and it makes no clear sense to talk of pretending to perform a private thought. They also argue the echoic account better explains the constancy of ironic tone across different types of irony.

### Metaphor: Loose Use of Language

Relevance Theory treats metaphor not as a special, distinct phenomenon but as one point on a **continuum of looseness** in how words are used. This continuum runs:

1. **Literal use**: The word's encoded concept and the communicated concept are identical. "The water is at 100°C."
2. **Approximation / loose use**: The communicated concept is slightly broader than the encoded concept. "The water is boiling" (said when it's actually at 99.5°C). "I live near the university" (within a few miles). "Holland is flat."
3. **Hyperbole**: The communicated concept is much broader than the encoded concept. "I've told you a million times." "There were tons of people."
4. **Metaphor**: The communicated concept departs significantly from the encoded concept through extensive broadening. "My lawyer is a shark." "She demolished his argument."

#### The key insight:
All of these involve the **same cognitive process** -- the hearer constructs an ad hoc concept (a contextually adjusted meaning) that differs from the literal meaning. There is no qualitative jump between loose talk and metaphor, only a quantitative difference in degree. Metaphor involves no violation of any norm or maxim; it is simply an alternative route to achieving optimal relevance.

#### Why speakers use metaphor:
A speaker uses metaphor when a metaphorical utterance is *more relevant* than any literal alternative -- either because it achieves greater cognitive effects (communicates a richer range of implications) or because it requires less effort than a literal paraphrase would. Often, a metaphor communicates a whole cluster of loosely associated implications that would take many literal sentences to spell out.

#### Example:
"Robert is a bulldozer."

No literal paraphrase captures the full range of implications: he's physically imposing, he doesn't listen to objections, he pushes through obstacles, he may be insensitive, he gets things done by force. The metaphor communicates all of these simultaneously, achieving a wide array of cognitive effects with minimal processing effort.

### Understatement and Overstatement (Hyperbole)

**Understatement (meiosis/litotes):** Saying less than you mean. "He's not the friendliest person" (meaning he's hostile). "It was a bit of a problem" (meaning it was a disaster).

In Relevance Theory, understatement is another form of loose use. The speaker uses a weaker expression when a stronger one would be literally more accurate. This is optimally relevant because:
- It may achieve specific cognitive effects (e.g., humor, diplomatic softening)
- It may communicate an attitude (restraint, ironic detachment)
- It invites the hearer to construct a stronger interpretation

**Overstatement (hyperbole):** Saying more than is literally true. "I'm starving." "Everyone was there." "It took forever."

Hyperbole, like metaphor, involves broadening -- the hearer constructs an ad hoc concept that is weaker than the encoded concept (STARVING* = very hungry, not literally starving to death). The speaker uses the stronger expression because it is more relevant: it communicates the desired degree of emphasis with less effort than a more measured literal statement.

In Relevance Theory, neither understatement nor overstatement involves a maxim violation. They are simply different points on the continuum of loose use, all governed by the same principle: the speaker chooses the expression that is optimally relevant.

### Indirect Speech Acts

Traditional speech act theory (Searle) treats indirect speech acts as cases where the speaker performs one speech act (e.g., a question: "Can you pass the salt?") to indirectly perform another (a request). This requires the hearer to first process the literal meaning, recognize it as inadequate, and then infer the intended speech act.

Relevance Theory handles indirect speech acts differently. There is no special mechanism for indirect speech acts. The comprehension procedure applies uniformly:

1. The hearer processes "Can you pass the salt?" following the path of least effort
2. The literal interpretation (a question about the hearer's ability) yields minimal cognitive effects in the context of a dinner table
3. The request interpretation is highly accessible (because of contextual factors and pragmatic routines) and yields better cognitive effects
4. The request interpretation satisfies expectations of relevance -- **stop**

For highly conventionalized indirect requests like "Can you VP?", Relevance Theory acknowledges that **pragmatic routines** may develop -- short-circuited inferential processes that allow rapid access to the conventionalized meaning without full compositional processing each time.

Wilson and Sperber argue that the "indirect" in "indirect speech act" is misleading: what matters is simply whether the intended interpretation is communicated as an explicature or an implicature, and whether it is easily accessible. The directness/indirectness of a speech act is a matter of degree, not a categorical distinction.

## 5. Why Relevance Theory May Be Particularly Useful for Autistic Learners

### Structural Advantages of the Framework

Several features of Relevance Theory make it potentially more accessible to autistic individuals compared to Grice's framework:

#### 1. One Principle Instead of Four Maxims (Simpler Architecture)
Grice requires tracking four maxims (Quality, Quantity, Relation, Manner) and determining which one is being violated or exploited in any given case. Relevance Theory replaces all of this with a single principle: seek the interpretation that achieves the best ratio of cognitive effects to processing effort. This reduces the cognitive load of the framework itself.

#### 2. A Procedure, Not Just Rules
Grice's maxims tell you what speakers *should* do, but don't provide a step-by-step process for *how to interpret utterances*. Relevance Theory provides an explicit **comprehension procedure**: follow the path of least effort, test hypotheses in order of accessibility, stop when expectations of relevance are satisfied. This is essentially an **algorithm** -- a systematic, repeatable process that can be followed step by step.

#### 3. Computationally Explicit
The theory is grounded in concepts that are inherently computational:
- **Input** (the utterance and contextual assumptions)
- **Processing** (decoding + inference)
- **Cost-benefit analysis** (cognitive effects vs. processing effort)
- **Halting condition** (stop when relevance is satisfied)

This makes it more amenable to systematic, analytical thinking styles.

#### 4. Cognitive Rather Than Social Foundation
Grice's framework starts from social cooperation -- the assumption that speakers are trying to be helpful conversation partners following social norms. For individuals who may process social norms differently, this foundation can be problematic. Relevance Theory grounds interpretation in **cognitive processing** -- how the mind handles information -- rather than in social expectations. The question is not "What social rule is the speaker following?" but "What interpretation yields the best cognitive payoff?"

#### 5. Handles Figurative Language Without "Violation"
In Grice's framework, metaphor and irony are explained as deliberate violations of maxims. This creates a problem: you first have to recognize that a social rule is being broken, then figure out why. Relevance Theory treats figurative language as falling on a natural continuum with literal language -- no "violation" to detect. This may better reflect how figurative language is actually processed by autistic individuals who may not track maxim violations in the same way.

### Research Applying Relevance Theory to Autism

#### Happé (1993): The First Direct Test

**Francesca Happé**'s 1993 study "Communicative competence and theory of mind in autism: A test of relevance theory" was the first direct experimental application of Relevance Theory to autism. Happé used Sperber and Wilson's framework to make specific predictions about levels of communicative competence based on Theory of Mind ability:

- Individuals with **no Theory of Mind** ability should understand only the most literal, direct communications
- Those with **first-order Theory of Mind** (understanding that others have beliefs) should be able to handle more complex explicatures
- Those with **second-order Theory of Mind** (understanding that others have beliefs about beliefs) should be able to handle irony and other forms requiring recognition of attributed thoughts

Three experiments tested these predictions in autistic and neurotypical participants. The results supported Relevance Theory's predictions and demonstrated close links between social understanding and communicative competence. The study showed that some autistic individuals with developed Theory of Mind abilities could successfully interpret figurative language.

#### Williams, Wharton & Jagoe (2021): Mutual (Mis)understanding

A groundbreaking 2021 paper by **Williams, Wharton, and Jagoe** in *Frontiers in Psychology* ("Mutual (Mis)understanding: Reframing Autistic Pragmatic 'Impairments' Using Relevance Theory") fundamentally reframed how we think about autism and communication:

**Key arguments:**
- Communication difficulties in autism are **bidirectional**, not unilateral. Non-autistic people also have difficulty understanding autistic communication. This aligns with the **double empathy problem** (Milton 2012).
- Relevance Theory explains this by noting that communication depends on **mutual manifestness** -- shared cognitive environments. When autistic and non-autistic people have markedly different embodied experiences of the world, what is most salient to each person may be mismatched.
- Rather than locating the "failure" in the autistic individual's mind, Relevance Theory frames communication breakdown as a **mutual failure to achieve optimal relevance** -- a failure of alignment between two differently-disposed cognitive systems.
- The study found that conversations between two autistic individuals showed significantly increased flow, rapport, and intersubjective attunement compared to mixed autistic/non-autistic conversations.

#### Jagoe & Wharton (2021): Theory of Autistic Mind

In a companion paper in the *Journal of Pragmatics* ("Theory of autistic mind: A renewed relevance theoretic perspective on so-called autistic pragmatic 'impairment'"), the same research group argued for:
- Replacing the deficit model with a **difference model** grounded in Relevance Theory
- Recognizing that autistic individuals may have different but valid relevance-seeking strategies
- Understanding that what appears as "pragmatic impairment" may actually be a mismatch in mutual cognitive environments

### Connections to Other Autism Theories

#### Monotropism and Relevance
**Monotropism** (Murray, Lesser, & Lawson 2005) proposes that autistic cognition is characterized by highly focused attention allocation -- a tendency toward narrowly tunneled interest rather than broadly distributed attention. In relevance-theoretic terms, this has important implications:

- Stimuli outside the monotropic attention tunnel may carry **reduced salience**, making it harder for autistic individuals to access the contextual assumptions needed for relevance-based interpretation
- What is "most accessible" (and therefore tested first in the comprehension procedure) may differ systematically between monotropic and polytropic cognitive styles
- The communicative principle of relevance assumes that utterances will compete for and win the hearer's attention -- but in a monotropic system, the threshold for capturing attention may be different

#### Weak Central Coherence and Relevance
The Weak Central Coherence theory (Frith 1989, Happé & Frith 2006) proposes that autistic individuals tend to process information in a detail-focused rather than gestalt-focused way. In Relevance Theory terms:

- The mutual adjustment process (which requires integrating linguistic decoding, contextual assumptions, and inference simultaneously) may function differently with a more detail-focused processing style
- The "path of least effort" may lead to different first-accessible interpretations when processing is more locally focused
- Processing effort calculations may differ if global context is harder to access

#### Predictive Processing and Relevance
More recent computational accounts of autism (Pellicano & Burr 2012; Van de Cruys et al. 2014) suggest that autistic individuals may have different predictive processing profiles -- less reliance on prior expectations ("priors") and more weight on incoming sensory data. In Relevance Theory terms:

- Expectations of relevance are themselves predictions about what kind of interpretation to expect
- If prior expectations are weighted differently, the comprehension procedure may proceed differently
- The "first satisfactory interpretation" may be reached at different points

### Practical Implications for Teaching

Relevance Theory suggests several approaches for teaching pragmatics to autistic individuals:

1. **Frame interpretation as problem-solving, not social rule-following**: "What interpretation gives you the most useful information for the least effort?" is a question that engages analytical thinking.

2. **Teach the comprehension procedure explicitly**: Present the procedure as an algorithm that can be consciously applied when automatic processing fails.

3. **Teach on the continuum**: Don't present literal and figurative language as categorically different. Present the continuum from literal → loose → hyperbolic → metaphorical and show how the same cognitive process (concept adjustment) operates throughout.

4. **Acknowledge different cognitive environments**: Teach that communication breakdowns often result from mismatched assumptions, not from one person's "failure." This is both more accurate and less stigmatizing.

5. **Use the cost-benefit framework**: When explaining why someone said something a particular way, frame it in terms of cognitive effects and processing effort rather than social rules.

## 6. Criticisms and Limitations of Relevance Theory

### Theoretical Criticisms

#### 1. The Measurability Problem
Critics argue that "relevance" in the technical sense is **not well-defined enough to be measured**. How do you quantify cognitive effects? How do you quantify processing effort? Without precise measures, the theory risks being unfalsifiable -- any outcome can be explained post hoc by claiming the interpretation that was reached was the "most relevant" one. This is perhaps the most fundamental criticism of the theory.

#### 2. Reductionism (Levinson's Critique)
**Stephen Levinson**, the most prominent neo-Gricean critic, argues that Relevance Theory is **too reductionist** -- a single principle cannot account for the full variety of pragmatic phenomena. Specifically:

- Levinson argues the theory cannot account for **generalized conversational implicatures** (GCIs) -- implicatures that arise by default, independent of specific context. For example, saying "some" typically implicates "not all" regardless of context. Relevance Theory, being inherently context-dependent, has no mechanism for context-free default implicatures.
- Levinson proposed his own **three heuristics** (Q-heuristic for Quantity, I-heuristic for Informativeness, M-heuristic for Manner) as a more adequate framework for capturing different types of implicature.
- Levinson charges that Relevance Theory's explanations are as **ex post facto** as Gricean explanations -- they describe what happened after the fact but don't genuinely predict what will happen.

Relevance theorists respond that GCIs are not truly context-independent but are better explained as context-dependent interpretations that happen to be frequently accessible across many contexts.

#### 3. Speaker-Related Information
Critics note that Relevance Theory has difficulty explaining how **speaker-related information** -- the speaker's specific abilities, knowledge, preferences, personality -- is incorporated into the hearer's inferential process. Clause (b) of the presumption of optimal relevance mentions "the communicator's abilities and preferences," but the theory provides limited detail on how hearers assess these.

#### 4. The "First Satisfactory Interpretation" Problem
The comprehension procedure says to stop at the first interpretation that satisfies expectations of relevance. But:
- How does the hearer know their expectations are set at the right level?
- What happens when two interpretations are equally accessible and equally satisfactory?
- Doesn't the "stop at first satisfactory" instruction sometimes lead to wrong interpretations that the hearer is confident about? (The answer is yes -- and this is actually a feature, not a bug. The theory predicts that misunderstandings will occur, and explains why.)

#### 5. Lack of Empirical Verification of Mental Procedures
A common criticism is that Relevance Theory is **highly speculative** about the mental procedures involved. It describes the "comprehension procedure" as if it's a real cognitive process, but there's limited direct empirical evidence for the specific steps the theory postulates. The theory predicts outcomes (which interpretations people arrive at) but the intermediate mental steps are hard to observe directly.

#### 6. Cultural and Social Factors
Some critics argue that Relevance Theory, by grounding pragmatics in individual cognition, **underweights social and cultural factors** in communication. Language use is shaped by power dynamics, social norms, cultural conventions, and institutional settings in ways that a purely cognitive theory may not fully capture. A relevance-based account of politeness, for example, may miss the specifically social dimensions of face-management.

### Defenses and Responses

Sperber and Wilson and their collaborators have responded to many of these criticisms:

- **On measurability**: They argue that "relevance" is a comparative notion, not an absolute measure. What matters is not absolute measurement but whether one interpretation is *more* relevant than another in a given context. The theory makes testable predictions about which interpretations will be preferred.

- **On reductionism**: They argue that the apparent variety of pragmatic phenomena can indeed be unified under a single principle, and that theoretical parsimony is a virtue. They note that Levinson's multiple heuristics create problems of their own (when do the different heuristics conflict? Which takes priority?).

- **On empirical testing**: There is a growing body of experimental pragmatics research testing Relevance Theory's predictions, including reaction time studies, eye-tracking experiments, and neuroimaging work.

- **On social factors**: Later work, particularly by Francisco Yus and others, has extended Relevance Theory to account for social media communication, politeness phenomena, and cultural variation, arguing these can be accommodated within the cognitive framework.

### What This Means for Autistic Learners

The criticisms of Relevance Theory don't undermine its usefulness for teaching pragmatics. In fact, some of the "limitations" are pedagogically advantageous:

- The theory's **simplicity** (one principle, one procedure) makes it easier to teach, even if some linguists want more complexity
- The theory's **cognitive grounding** remains more accessible than social-norm-based alternatives, even if it doesn't capture everything about social communication
- The **measurability problem** is primarily a concern for researchers trying to formally test the theory -- for learners, the qualitative framework (more effects = more relevant; more effort = less relevant) is intuitively workable
- The theory's acknowledged **incompleteness** regarding social factors can be addressed by supplementing it with explicit teaching about social conventions, rather than abandoning the cognitive framework

## 7. Key Terminology Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Relevance** | A property of inputs to cognitive processes; a function of cognitive effects and processing effort |
| **Cognitive effects** | Positive changes to an individual's representation of the world (implications, strengthening, revision) |
| **Processing effort** | Mental resources required to derive an interpretation |
| **Optimal relevance** | The best achievable ratio of cognitive effects to processing effort, compatible with the speaker's abilities and preferences |
| **Ostensive communication** | Communication that openly signals the communicator's intention to communicate |
| **Inferential communication** | Communication that requires the hearer to infer the speaker's meaning beyond what is linguistically encoded |
| **Cognitive environment** | The set of all assumptions manifest to an individual at a given time |
| **Mutual manifestness** | The state where assumptions are available in the shared cognitive environment and this sharing is itself manifest |
| **Explicature** | An explicitly communicated assumption, derived by a combination of decoding and pragmatic inference |
| **Implicature** | An implicitly communicated assumption, derived wholly by inference |
| **Ad hoc concept** | A context-specific, occasion-bound concept constructed on the fly by narrowing or broadening an encoded concept |
| **Echoic use** | An utterance that achieves relevance by representing another thought or utterance and expressing an attitude toward it |
| **Comprehension procedure** | The heuristic: follow the path of least effort, test interpretive hypotheses in order of accessibility, stop when expectations of relevance are satisfied |
| **Mutual adjustment** | The parallel process by which explicatures and implicatures are simultaneously derived and refined |
| **Semantic underdeterminacy** | The thesis that linguistic meaning radically underdetermines the proposition expressed; pragmatic inference is needed to determine even explicit meaning |
| **Informative intention** | The speaker's intention to make certain assumptions manifest to the hearer |
| **Communicative intention** | The speaker's intention to make the informative intention itself manifest to the hearer |

## 9. Summary for Downstream Agents

### Core Message
Relevance Theory (Sperber & Wilson 1986/1995) offers a **cognitive, procedural, and computationally explicit** framework for understanding how people interpret utterances. It replaces Grice's four social maxims with a single cognitive principle (maximize relevance = best ratio of cognitive effects to processing effort) and provides an explicit comprehension procedure (test interpretations in order of accessibility, stop when relevance is satisfied).

### Why It Matters for This Knowledge Base
For autistic learners, Relevance Theory offers:
- **One principle** instead of four maxims
- **An algorithm** (comprehension procedure) instead of social norms
- **Cognitive grounding** (how the mind works) instead of social grounding (how cooperative people behave)
- **A continuum** from literal to figurative language (no "violation detection" required)
- **A non-deficit framing** of communication difficulties (mutual manifestness mismatch, not individual failure)
- **Recent research** (Williams et al. 2021; Jagoe & Wharton 2021) explicitly applying the framework to autism in a neurodiversity-affirming way
