# Humor, Wordplay, and Non-Literal Language in Pragmatics: Deep Research Document

## Research Purpose
This document provides comprehensive, in-depth research on humor, wordplay, and related non-literal language phenomena (understatement, hyperbole, metaphor, idioms) from a pragmatic perspective. It is written for a knowledge base teaching English pragmatics to autistic individuals. The emphasis throughout is on providing extensive examples, explaining the underlying mechanisms that make each phenomenon work, and identifying which aspects may be more or less accessible to autistic individuals based on existing research.

## 1. HUMOR IN PRAGMATICS: THEORIES AND TYPES

### 1.1 Why Humor Matters for Pragmatics

Humor is one of the most pervasive features of human communication. Research estimates that people laugh approximately 18 times per day in conversation, and that humorous exchanges occur in the majority of social interactions. Humor is not merely entertainment -- it performs critical social functions including bonding, tension relief, status negotiation, and boundary-testing. For autistic individuals, understanding humor is important not because they lack a sense of humor (research consistently shows they do not), but because much humor relies on the same pragmatic inference mechanisms that underlie other forms of non-literal language, and because failure to recognize when someone is being humorous can lead to significant social misunderstanding.

### 1.2 The Four Major Theories of Humor

#### 1.2.1 Incongruity Theory (The Dominant Theory)

Incongruity Theory is the most widely accepted theory in both philosophy and linguistics. It proposes that humor arises from the perception of something **incongruous** -- something that violates our expectations, patterns, or mental schemas.

**Core mechanism:** We hold mental representations of how the world works (scripts, schemas, frames). When input violates these representations in a particular way, the mismatch between expectation and reality produces the humor response.

**The Incongruity-Resolution model** (Suls 1972) refined this by proposing a two-stage process:
1. **Incongruity detection:** The hearer/reader encounters something that doesn't fit their expectations
2. **Resolution:** The hearer/reader finds a cognitive rule or schema that makes the incongruity make sense (at least partially)

It is the *resolution* of the incongruity, not merely its detection, that produces amusement. An incongruity that cannot be resolved at all produces confusion, not humor. An incongruity that resolves too easily or too completely produces no surprise and therefore no humor.

**Example illustrating both stages:**
> "I told my wife she was drawing her eyebrows too high. She looked surprised."

Stage 1 (incongruity): Why is it relevant that she looked surprised? Stage 2 (resolution): "Surprised" works on two levels -- the emotional reaction AND the physical appearance of eyebrows drawn too high, which makes someone look permanently surprised.

**Linguistic application -- Raskin's Script-Based Semantic Theory of Humor (SSTH):**
Victor Raskin (1985) formalized Incongruity Theory for verbal humor specifically. His SSTH proposes that a verbal joke is funny when:
1. The text is compatible with **two different semantic scripts** (mental representations of typical situations)
2. These two scripts are **opposed** to each other (e.g., real/unreal, possible/impossible, normal/abnormal, expected/unexpected)
3. A **trigger** (the punchline) causes the hearer to switch abruptly from the primary script to the secondary, opposing script

This was later expanded by Raskin and Attardo into the **General Theory of Verbal Humor (GTVH)**, which added parameters for the target of the joke, the narrative strategy, the situation, and the language used. The GTVH extends beyond simple jokes to cover all humorous texts.

**Why this matters for autistic individuals:** The incongruity-resolution model is essentially a *pattern* operation -- detect pattern violation, find alternative pattern that resolves it. This is fundamentally similar to how puns work (detect ambiguity, find second meaning). Autistic individuals who are strong pattern-recognizers may find this structure accessible when it is made explicit, even though the social context in which humor occurs can remain challenging.

#### 1.2.2 Superiority Theory

The Superiority Theory, associated with Thomas Hobbes (1651), proposes that laughter arises from a feeling of **"sudden glory"** -- a burst of self-satisfaction when we recognize our superiority over others (or over our own former selves).

**Core mechanism:** We laugh when we see others make mistakes, display ignorance, or suffer misfortune, because their position makes us feel comparatively elevated.

**Examples:**
- Laughing at someone slipping on a banana peel (we didn't slip)
- Laughing at a character's ignorance in a sitcom (we know what they don't)
- Schadenfreude (pleasure derived from others' misfortune)

**Limitations:** Superiority Theory fails to explain many types of humor -- puns, absurdist humor, and wordplay don't involve feeling superior to anyone. It works best as an explanation for a *subset* of humor rather than all humor.

**Where it still applies:** The theory illuminates the power dynamics of humor. Jokes directed *downward* (at people with less power) often function through superiority. This is why jokes about marginalized groups can feel cruel -- they enact a superiority dynamic.

#### 1.2.3 Relief/Release Theory (Freud)

Sigmund Freud's version of the Relief Theory, articulated in *Jokes and Their Relation to the Unconscious* (1905), proposes that humor functions as a **release of psychological tension**.

**Core mechanism:** The mind accumulates nervous energy through repression (of socially unacceptable thoughts), cognitive effort (of thinking), or emotional tension (of feeling). Humor allows this energy to be discharged through laughter.

Freud distinguished three types:
1. **Jokes (der Witz):** Release energy used to repress forbidden thoughts (especially about sex and aggression). Jokes allow us to express what would otherwise be socially prohibited.
2. **The Comic:** Release energy used for cognitive processing. When an expected intellectual effort turns out to be unnecessary (e.g., we expected complexity but found simplicity), the surplus cognitive energy is released as laughter.
3. **Humor proper:** Release energy mobilized for emotional responses. When we prepare for strong emotions but then find the situation less threatening than expected, the excess emotional energy is discharged as laughter.

**Current status:** The hydraulic model of nervous energy that Relief Theory relies on is considered scientifically outdated. Today, almost no scholars in philosophy or psychology explain humor as literally releasing pent-up nervous energy. However, the *phenomenological observation* remains valid: humor does seem to defuse tension, and people do seek humor in stressful situations. The mechanism is probably better described through modern affective neuroscience (e.g., the role of the mesolimbic dopamine system) rather than through hydraulic energy models.

**Where it still applies:** Relief Theory explains the observable phenomenon of humor functioning to reduce anxiety, defuse awkward situations, and cope with stress. The theory has been revived in modern form in research on humor as a coping mechanism, particularly in high-stress professions (medical workers, first responders, military) who use dark humor to create psychological distance from traumatic experiences.

#### 1.2.4 Benign Violation Theory (McGraw & Warren 2010)

The Benign Violation Theory, proposed by Peter McGraw and Caleb Warren in their 2010 paper "Benign Violations: Making Immoral Behavior Funny," integrates elements from all three classical theories. It proposes that humor occurs when and only when **three conditions are simultaneously met:**

1. **A violation occurs:** Something threatens the way we believe the world "ought" to be. Something seems wrong, inappropriate, or threatening.
2. **The violation is benign:** The situation simultaneously seems okay, safe, or acceptable.
3. **Both perceptions occur simultaneously:** The perceiver must hold both the "wrong" and "okay" assessments at the same time.

**Three ways violations become benign:**
- **Alternative norm:** A different framework makes the violation acceptable (e.g., it's okay to tackle someone -- in a football game)
- **Weak commitment:** The perceiver doesn't strongly care about the violated norm (e.g., non-religious people laughing at mild religious jokes)
- **Psychological distance:** The violation is remote -- it happened to someone else, occurred long ago, is fictional, or is physically distant (the famous formula: "comedy = tragedy + time")

**Examples:**
- Tickling: A physical "attack" that is benign because it comes from someone trusted
- Play-fighting: Aggressive actions that are benign because both parties know it's play
- A dark joke about death: A violation (death is bad) that is benign (the joke is fictional/distant)

**What happens when the balance is wrong:**
- Pure violation, not benign → disgust, horror, offense ("too soon," "that's not funny")
- Pure benign, no violation → boring, not funny (a completely normal situation isn't humorous)

**Why this matters for autistic individuals:** This theory helps explain why *some* autistic individuals find certain humor unfunny that neurotypical people enjoy -- if the autistic individual perceives only the violation (the wrongness) without simultaneously perceiving the benign-ness (because they don't share the social norm that renders it benign), the result is discomfort, not laughter. Conversely, it explains why autistic humor is sometimes experienced as "inappropriate" by neurotypical people -- the autistic individual may perceive something as a benign violation when others perceive it as a pure violation.

### 1.3 Linguistic Theories of Humor

#### 1.3.1 Humor as Maxim Flouting (Gricean Analysis)

From a Gricean perspective, much humor can be analyzed as deliberate **flouting** of conversational maxims. Flouting differs from violating: when a speaker flouts a maxim, they do so obviously and intentionally, expecting the hearer to recognize the violation and derive an implicature from it.

**Flouting the Maxim of Quality (truthfulness):**
> "I'm so hungry I could eat a horse."

The speaker obviously doesn't mean this literally. The blatant untruth signals that the hearer should derive an implicature: "I'm very hungry."

**Flouting the Maxim of Quantity (informativeness):**
> After a terrible meal: "Well, that was... food."

The statement is technically true but drastically under-informative. The hearer recognizes that the speaker could have said much more and infers the implicature: "That food was terrible."

**Flouting the Maxim of Relation (relevance):**
> A: "How did you find the exam?" B: "Well, it was definitely on paper."

B's answer is relevant only in the most trivial sense, flouting the expected relevant answer about difficulty. The implicature: "I'd rather not talk about how it went" (or the humor arises from the mismatch between the expected and delivered answer).

**Flouting the Maxim of Manner (clarity):**
> Being deliberately obscure, ambiguous, or roundabout for comic effect.

Research shows that the maxims of quality and quantity are the most frequently flouted for humorous effect. The general pattern: *the joke relies on the hearer recognizing the gap between what the maxim would require and what the speaker actually said, and finding the gap amusing rather than confusing.*

#### 1.3.2 Humor in Relevance Theory

In Sperber and Wilson's Relevance Theory framework, humor is analyzed through the **single interpretive principle**: the first interpretation that achieves an optimal balance of cognitive effects and processing effort is the one selected. Humor creates a situation where:

1. The hearer's initial interpretive path leads to one meaning
2. A trigger (punchline, tone, context) forces a reinterpretation
3. The second meaning yields unexpected cognitive effects
4. The "surprise" of the reinterpretation, combined with the cognitive reward of finding the second meaning, produces amusement

RT treats loose talk, hyperbole, and metaphor as lying on a **continuum** with literal language -- they are alternative routes to achieving optimal relevance, not fundamentally different mechanisms. This means that humor involving these devices doesn't require a special "humor module" but operates through the same pragmatic inference machinery.

### 1.4 Types of Pragmatic Humor

#### 1.4.1 Linguistic Humor: Puns, Wordplay, Ambiguity Exploitation

Linguistic humor exploits properties of language *itself* -- its sounds, structures, and multiple meanings. This type of humor is fundamentally **pattern-based**, making it potentially more accessible to autistic individuals who are strong pattern-recognizers.

**Key mechanism:** The humor arises from the *language system* rather than from social context, shared knowledge, or intention recognition. Once you see the pattern, you see the joke.

(Detailed coverage of puns and wordplay appears in Section 2.)

#### 1.4.2 Pragmatic Humor: Violating Conversational Expectations

Pragmatic humor violates what would normally be expected in a conversational exchange -- not through language tricks but through unexpected conversational moves.

**Example:**
> Server: "Enjoy your meal!" You: "You too!" (then realizing the server isn't eating)

The humor here comes from the pragmatic mismatch -- the response was an automatic social script applied to the wrong situation.

**Example of deliberate pragmatic humor:**
> Doctor: "I have bad news and worse news." Patient: "What's the bad news?" Doctor: "You have 24 hours to live." Patient: "What could be worse than that?" Doctor: "I've been trying to reach you since yesterday."

This violates the conversational expectation about what constitutes "bad" vs. "worse" news, with the punchline retrospectively reframing the entire conversation.

#### 1.4.3 Referential Humor: Jokes Requiring Shared Knowledge

Referential humor depends on the hearer possessing specific background knowledge. The humor doesn't work without that knowledge.

**Examples:**
> "A Freudian slip is when you say one thing and mean your mother." (Requires knowledge of Freud's theories about mothers)

> "There are only 10 types of people in the world: those who understand binary and those who don't." (Requires knowledge that 10 in binary = 2 in decimal)

**Why this is important for pragmatics:** Referential humor makes shared knowledge visible. When you laugh at a referential joke, you signal membership in a knowledge community. When you don't get it, you reveal the absence of that shared background. This can feel socially exposing.

#### 1.4.4 Self-Deprecating Humor: Social Functions

Self-deprecating humor involves making jokes at one's own expense. It serves several distinct social functions:

**Disarming function:** By pre-emptively mocking your own flaws, you remove others' ability to use those flaws against you. As researchers describe it, self-deprecation works as a "socially pre-emptive way" to deflect criticism by disarming the recipient before they can attack.

**Status-leveling function:** High-status individuals use self-deprecation to appear more approachable and relatable. Research by Bitterly, Brooks, and Schweitzer (published in the *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 2016) found that individuals with high social status who use self-deprecating humor are more desired for long-term relationships than high-status individuals who use other-deprecating humor.

**Bonding function:** Self-deprecation signals trust (you're revealing vulnerability) and invites reciprocal sharing. It can function to "level with each other and become of 'one mind'" and as a resource for building affiliation and solidarity (Speer 2019, *British Journal of Social Psychology*).

**Critical caveat -- the status requirement:** Self-deprecating humor works differently depending on the speaker's perceived status. For low-status individuals, self-deprecating humor can backfire, making them appear to have low self-esteem, depression, or submissiveness. For high-status individuals, it signals confidence (you can afford to mock yourself because your competence is established). Research consistently shows that self-defeating humor is associated with loneliness and reduced social function when it becomes a habitual pattern rather than a strategic choice.

**Relevance for autistic individuals:** Some autistic individuals use self-deprecating humor about autism itself as a bonding mechanism within autistic communities. Understanding when self-deprecation helps vs. hurts requires reading context -- specifically, whether you're perceived as already having status/competence that the humor merely softens.

## 2. TYPES OF HUMOR WITH EXTENSIVE EXAMPLES

### 2.1 Puns and Wordplay (Pattern-Based -- Potentially Autistic-Friendly)

Puns exploit the fact that language is full of ambiguities -- words that sound alike, words with multiple meanings, and grammatical structures that can be parsed in more than one way. Puns are fundamentally **pattern-based**: they work by activating two meanings simultaneously and letting the clash between them produce humor.

**Why puns may be autistic-friendly:** Research shows that when autistic adults told jokes, the most common types were riddles with lexical and phonological ambiguity -- essentially puns and wordplay. The pattern-detection aspect of puns aligns with autistic cognitive strengths. However, research also shows that some autistic individuals find the *ambiguity* in puns confusing rather than humorous, particularly if they tend to process one meaning at a time rather than holding both simultaneously.

#### 2.1.1 Homophonic Puns (Exploiting Sound-Alike Words)

Homophonic puns exploit words that **sound the same** (or very similar) but have different meanings and usually different spellings.

**Examples:**

> "Time flies like an arrow; fruit flies like a banana."

Analysis: In the first clause, "flies" = moves through the air, "like" = in the manner of. In the second clause, "fruit flies" = the insects, "like" = enjoy. The identical surface structure maps onto completely different grammatical parsings.

> "The pony couldn't speak -- he was a little hoarse."

Analysis: "Hoarse" (rough voice) sounds like "horse" (the animal). The setup creates an expectation about voice problems; the punchline delivers a double meaning.

> "I used to be a banker, but I lost interest."

Analysis: "Interest" = both financial interest (money earned) and personal interest (caring about something).

> "A bicycle can't stand on its own because it's two-tired."

Analysis: "Two-tired" sounds like "too tired." The bicycle literally has two tires.

> "I'm reading a book about anti-gravity. It's impossible to put down."

Analysis: "Put down" = stop reading, and literally, unable to place downward (because anti-gravity).

**Pattern to notice:** Homophonic puns require the listener to hear (or read) one word and simultaneously activate a different word that sounds the same. The humor comes from the collision between the expected meaning and the alternative meaning.

#### 2.1.2 Polysemic Puns (Exploiting Multiple Meanings of One Word)

Polysemic puns exploit words that have **multiple meanings** (polysemy -- one word form, multiple related senses).

**Examples:**

> "Being in politics is just like playing golf: you are trapped in one bad lie after another."

Analysis: "Lie" = (1) an untruth, (2) the position in which a golf ball rests. The word is the same; the meanings are different.

> "I used to be a teacher, but I lost my class."

Analysis: "Class" = (1) a group of students, (2) elegance/sophistication.

> "The man who fell into an upholstery machine is now fully recovered."

Analysis: "Recovered" = (1) regained health, (2) re-covered (new upholstery put on).

> "A will is a dead giveaway."

Analysis: "Dead giveaway" = (1) something obvious, (2) literally giving away things after death.

**Pattern to notice:** Unlike homophonic puns where two *different words* happen to sound alike, polysemic puns exploit two *different meanings of the same word*. The setup activates one meaning; the punchline forces recognition of the other.

#### 2.1.3 Homographic Puns (Same Spelling, Different Pronunciation or Meaning)

> "The bass player caught a bass while fishing." (bass /beɪs/ vs. bass /bæs/)

> "I object to that object!" (verb /əbˈdʒɛkt/ vs. noun /ˈɒbdʒɛkt/)

#### 2.1.4 Morphological Wordplay

Morphological humor plays with word formation processes -- prefixes, suffixes, compounding, and blending.

**Portmanteau/blend examples:**
- "Hangry" (hungry + angry) -- describes the specific irritability of hunger
- "Brunch" (breakfast + lunch) -- the late-morning meal
- "Frenemy" (friend + enemy) -- someone who is both
- "Mansplain" (man + explain) -- condescending explanation by a man to a woman
- "Procrastibaking" (procrastinate + baking) -- baking to avoid doing other things

**Compound wordplay:**
> "What do you call a fish with no eyes? A fsh."

Analysis: This joke removes the letter "i" (homophone of "eye") from "fish" to create "fsh" -- a visual/orthographic joke that works on the page.

**Prefix/suffix play:**
> "I'm not superstitious, but I am a little stitious."

Analysis: This deconstructs the word "superstitious" as if "super-" were a removable prefix meaning "very," leaving "stitious" as a milder version. (This is Michael Scott from *The Office*, and it works because "superstitious" isn't actually formed from "super" + "stitious" -- the morphological analysis is wrong, which is part of the joke.)

### 2.2 Absurdist/Surreal Humor (Pattern-Breaking)

Absurdist humor deliberately violates logic and reason. It presents scenarios so illogical that the brain's attempt to process them produces laughter.

**Examples:**

> "A man walks into a bar. He says 'Ouch.'" (Reinterprets "bar" from pub to metal rod)

> "What's the difference between a duck? One of its legs is both the same." (The question structure is nonsensical; the answer is nonsensical; the entire joke violates logical expectations.)

> Monty Python: "I'd like to complain about this parrot. It's dead." "No, it's not. It's resting."

**How it works (or deliberately doesn't):** Absurdist humor operates at the *incongruity* stage of the incongruity-resolution model but deliberately *denies resolution*. The normal joke pattern is: setup → expectation → punchline that reframes the setup. Absurdist humor provides a punchline that *refuses* to resolve the incongruity, leaving the hearer in a state of pleasurable confusion.

**Why autistic individuals may have a unique relationship with absurdist humor:** Research on humor preferences in autism found that autistic students "felt greater enjoyment when reading nonsense jokes and preferred them for featuring less logical reasoning." The appeal may be that absurdist humor doesn't pretend to follow social rules that then get violated -- it openly announces that the rules don't apply. There's no hidden social meaning to miss; the surface is the point.

### 2.3 Observational Humor (Noticing Patterns Others Miss -- Potentially Autistic-Friendly)

Observational humor draws comedy from commonplace aspects of everyday life -- the small details, absurdities, and inconsistencies that most people overlook.

**Examples:**

> "Why do we park in driveways and drive on parkways?" (Noticing the inconsistent naming pattern)

> "Have you ever noticed that anyone driving slower than you is an idiot, and anyone going faster than you is a maniac?" (George Carlin -- noticing the cognitive bias)

> "Why is there a light in the fridge but not in the freezer?" (Noticing a design inconsistency)

**How it works pragmatically:** Observational humor is essentially **pattern recognition applied to everyday life**. The comedian notices an incongruity in how things work, names it, and the audience laughs in recognition -- "Yes! I never noticed that, but it's true!"

**Why this may be autistic-friendly:** Research consistently identifies "observational acuity" as a strength in autism -- a heightened ability to notice and process details. As one review noted, autistic individuals often "view social norms from an outsider's perspective, allowing them to point out absurdities that others might take for granted, leading to brilliantly insightful and humorous observations." Pattern recognition, often associated with autism, "contributes to the creation of unexpected punchlines." The comedian Hannah Gadsby, who is autistic, has described how her outsider perspective enables her to see social absurdities that insiders take for granted.

### 2.4 Dark Humor / Gallows Humor

Dark humor (also called gallows humor, black humor, or morbid humor) treats serious, frightening, or painful subject matter in a light or satirical way.

**Examples:**

> "I told my wife she was drawing her eyebrows too high. She looked surprised." (This is actually mild, not dark.)

> Actual dark humor: "Other than that, Mrs. Lincoln, how was the play?" (Minimizing a horrific event)

> "I want to die peacefully in my sleep, like my grandfather, not screaming in terror like the passengers in his car."

**How it works (Benign Violation Theory):** Dark humor creates a violation (death, suffering, taboo) that is rendered benign through psychological distance. The distance can come from: (1) the subject being fictional, (2) the event being in the past, (3) the framing being absurdly detached, or (4) the situation being someone else's.

**Coping function:** Research with U.S. military veterans (PMC, 2024) and healthcare workers shows that dark humor functions as a defense mechanism, creating psychological distance from overwhelming experiences. According to Freud's defense mechanism framework, gallows humor enables emotional distancing from the uncomfortable reality of death and suffering. Modern research confirms that "humor allows people to distance themselves from problems, thereby increasing positive emotions and easing tension."

**The line between dark humor and offensive humor:** This is a frequent source of social difficulty. The same joke can be perceived as brilliantly dark or as deeply offensive depending on: (1) who tells it (someone affected by the issue vs. an outsider), (2) who hears it (someone who shares the experience vs. someone targeted by it), (3) when and where it's told (among close friends vs. in a formal setting), and (4) whether it "punches up" (at power) or "punches down" (at vulnerability).

### 2.5 Deadpan/Dry Humor (No Signal That Humor Is Intended)

Deadpan humor (also called dry humor or dry wit) delivers humorous content **without any change in expression, tone, or emotion** -- no smile, no exaggerated delivery, no wink.

**Examples:**

> Steven Wright: "I went to a restaurant that serves 'breakfast at any time.' So I ordered French Toast during the Renaissance."

> Mitch Hedberg: "I used to do drugs. I still do, but I used to, too."

> Aubrey Plaza's entire persona: statements that could be serious or humorous, delivered with complete flatness.

**Why deadpan is particularly confusing for literal processors:** Normally, humor is accompanied by **prosodic and paralinguistic signals** that indicate "this is meant to be funny":
- Changes in vocal pitch and rhythm
- Smiling or laughing
- Exaggerated emphasis
- Eye contact or eyebrow movement signaling ironic intent
- A slight pause before the punchline

Deadpan humor deliberately **removes all of these signals**. The humor depends entirely on the *content* being recognized as absurd, with the flat delivery adding an additional layer of incongruity (the mismatch between what is said and how it is said).

**Research on autistic individuals and signal-dependent humor:** Autistic individuals often rely more heavily on explicit signals to distinguish humor from serious speech. When those signals are absent (as in deadpan delivery), the intended humor may be processed as a literal, serious statement. This is why deadpan humor can be the single most confusing type -- it is engineered to remove the very cues that autistic individuals most need.

**Paradox:** Despite this, some autistic individuals are described as naturally excelling at deadpan comedy, precisely *because* their naturally flat affect and direct delivery style creates the deadpan effect without conscious effort. As research notes, "the direct and logical nature of dry humor may resonate more with individuals who think in a similarly logical manner."

### 2.6 Teasing (Humor Directed at Someone)

Teasing occupies a spectrum from warmly affectionate to aggressively hostile. It has been described as paradoxical: it "criticizes yet compliments, attacks yet makes people closer, humiliates yet expresses affection."

**Affectionate teasing examples:**
> To a friend who burned dinner: "Ah, I see you've gone with the 'extra crispy' option."
> Among close friends: "Oh, you actually put on a nice shirt? Who are you trying to impress?"

**Aggressive teasing examples:**
> In a school setting: "Nice answer, genius." (said after a wrong answer in class)
> "You actually think that looks good?"

**The spectrum:**
- **Playful teasing:** Both parties understand it's affectionate; the target can tease back; there's an underlying message of closeness ("I know you well enough to joke about this")
- **Boundary-testing teasing:** The teaser gauges the target's reaction to determine how much they can push
- **Aggressive teasing:** Uses the form of humor to deliver genuine criticism or cruelty while maintaining plausible deniability ("I was just joking!")

**Social functions of teasing:**
1. **Expressing affection indirectly:** Youth may tease peers "to express affection and increase interpersonal closeness and intimacy" (Springer, 2025)
2. **Measuring relationship strength:** The willingness to tease and be teased signals the strength of a bond
3. **Establishing hierarchy:** In asymmetric relationships, teasing can assert dominance
4. **Group bonding:** Reciprocal teasing has been described as "a way to bond, and kind of like relate to each other" (PMC, 2024)
5. **Norm enforcement:** Groups tease members who deviate from group norms

**Why teasing is particularly difficult for autistic individuals:** Distinguishing affectionate teasing from aggressive teasing requires reading paralinguistic cues (tone, facial expression), understanding the relationship context, and recognizing the speaker's *intended* effect (bonding vs. hurting). Research consistently identifies teasing as one of the most difficult social phenomena for autistic individuals to navigate because it requires simultaneous processing of contradictory signals -- the literal content is negative, but the intended meaning may be positive.

**Key pragmatic principle:** If you can't tell whether someone is teasing affectionately or being mean, look for these signals:
- Do they tease others in the group equally? (Affectionate teasing is usually reciprocal)
- Is the target laughing genuinely? (Not nervously or reluctantly)
- Would the teaser say the same thing in public, or only to the target's face?
- Does the teasing reference things that are clearly not sensitive? Or does it target genuine vulnerabilities?

### 2.7 In-Jokes and Running Jokes (Require Shared History)

An **in-joke** is a joke that depends on shared knowledge or experience specific to a particular group. It functions as a social shibboleth -- understanding it proves you're "in."

**How in-jokes work:**
> After a group trip where someone got comically lost: "Are you sure about these directions? Remember Madrid?"

Only those who were on the trip know what "Remember Madrid?" refers to. The joke is unintelligible to outsiders. This is precisely the point -- the in-joke *creates and reinforces group boundaries*.

**A running joke** is a joke that recurs over time, with each iteration building on the previous ones. Each repetition adds a layer of shared history.

**Social function:** In-jokes and running jokes serve as "social glue" -- they create a shared narrative, a private language that bonds group members together. Research on fan communities shows that "shows that utilize callback humor often build a stronger sense of community among fans, as shared knowledge of these references can spark discussions and inside jokes."

**Challenge for autistic individuals:** In-jokes require tracking the *social history* of a group's interactions. If you missed the original event, or joined the group after the joke was established, the humor is invisible. Additionally, the social purpose of in-jokes (boundary-marking) can feel exclusionary.

### 2.8 Callback Humor (Referencing Something from Earlier)

A callback is a joke that references a previous joke, event, or comment from earlier in a conversation, performance, or narrative.

**How callbacks work:**
In his "New in Town" special, comedian John Mulaney likens babysitting to hiring "a horse to watch your dog." Many unrelated jokes later, he describes a high school party: "We were like dogs without horses." The callback rewards the audience for remembering the earlier joke, and the unexpected re-emergence of the reference creates a new moment of incongruity.

**Key principles for effective callbacks (from comedy writing):**
1. The first joke must stand alone and be funny on its own
2. The callback should appear in a different context from the original
3. Enough time must pass between them that the audience has "forgotten" -- but not so much time that they can't retrieve the memory when prompted
4. The callback adds a new dimension or twist to the original joke

**Why callbacks may be accessible to autistic individuals:** Callbacks are essentially pattern recognition across time. If you have strong memory for specific details and previous statements (as many autistic individuals do), recognizing a callback may be natural. The challenge is that callbacks in live social interaction (as opposed to scripted comedy) are harder to track because they reference informal, unscripted conversation.

## 3. THE SOCIAL FUNCTIONS OF HUMOR

### 3.1 Bonding and Rapport-Building

Shared laughter is one of the most powerful bonding mechanisms in human interaction. Laughing together creates an immediate sense of connection, even between strangers. Research consistently identifies humor as "social glue that bridges gaps between individuals and serves as a tool for initiating conversations and easing social interactions, especially in unfamiliar or uncomfortable situations."

**Mechanism:** When two people laugh at the same thing, they demonstrate shared perspective, shared values, or shared knowledge. This mutual alignment creates a feeling of trust and camaraderie. Humor breaks down social barriers and reinforces group identity through shared humorous references.

**Affiliative humor** specifically aims to create or strengthen social bonds through shared laughter and inside jokes. People who use affiliative humor tend to be perceived as warmer, more likeable, and more trustworthy.

### 3.2 Tension Relief and De-Escalation

Humor can defuse tense situations by reframing them. A well-timed joke can transform a confrontation into a shared moment of amusement.

**Example:** In a heated meeting, someone says: "I think we can all agree that at least the coffee is good" -- this acknowledges the tension without addressing it directly, giving everyone a moment to recalibrate.

**How it works pragmatically:** The joke creates a temporary shift in the frame of interaction. For a moment, participants stop being adversaries and become co-participants in a humor episode. This frame-break can make it easier to return to the serious discussion with less emotional charge.

### 3.3 Status Negotiation (Who Can Joke About What)

Humor is deeply entangled with social status and power. Several principles govern who can joke about what:

**Punching up vs. punching down:** Jokes directed at people with more power than the joker ("punching up") are generally considered more acceptable than jokes directed at people with less power ("punching down"). A junior employee joking about the CEO is different from the CEO joking about a junior employee.

**In-group privilege:** Members of a group can make jokes about that group that would be offensive coming from outsiders. An autistic person making jokes about autism operates differently than a non-autistic person making the same jokes.

**Status and humor initiation:** Higher-status individuals have more license to initiate humor. In many social settings, the person with the most social power sets the tone for what is humorous. Others are expected to laugh along.

### 3.4 In-Group/Out-Group Boundary Marking

Humor marks social boundaries. In-jokes define who is "in" and who is "out." Specific types of humor signal membership in specific communities:

- **Professional jargon humor** marks membership in an occupation
- **Meme humor** marks membership in an online community
- **Cultural humor** marks membership in an ethnic or national group

When you don't get a joke, you've encountered a group boundary.

### 3.5 Softening Criticism or Bad News

Humor can make difficult messages easier to deliver and receive.

**Examples:**
> A friend who's always late: "Wow, you're actually on time! Are you feeling okay?" (softens the implicit criticism "you're always late")

> A doctor: "Well, the good news is you'll never have to worry about forgetting your medication again..." (before delivering a difficult diagnosis -- this is risky and context-dependent)

**How it works pragmatically:** Humor wraps the criticism in a social signal that says "I'm saying this with warmth, not hostility." The humorous framing invites the hearer to laugh *with* the speaker rather than feel attacked. However, this only works when the relationship supports it and the target doesn't feel the humor is minimizing their experience.

### 3.6 Testing Boundaries (Humor as a "Trial Balloon")

Humor allows people to probe attitudes, test boundaries, and gauge reactions while maintaining plausible deniability.

**How it works:** A person makes a mildly provocative joke and watches the reaction. If the hearer laughs, the boundary has been identified as safe to cross. If the hearer is uncomfortable, the speaker can retreat behind "I was just joking."

**Examples:**
- Flirtatious humor tests whether romantic interest is reciprocated
- Edgy humor tests a group's tolerance for controversial topics
- Self-disclosing humor tests whether personal information will be accepted

**Why this is pragmatically important:** The "just joking" defense creates a social safety net that allows people to explore topics they couldn't raise directly. This makes humor a powerful social probe -- but it also means that humor can be used to advance agendas (testing whether sexist or racist comments will be tolerated) while maintaining plausible deniability.

### 3.7 Power Dynamics of Humor

**Who gets to be funny:** In many social contexts, humor initiation is a status signal. The person who makes others laugh has a form of social power. This is why being funny is consistently rated as one of the most attractive social qualities.

**Who must laugh:** There is often a social expectation that lower-status individuals will laugh at higher-status individuals' jokes. Not laughing at a boss's joke can be perceived as insubordinate; not laughing at a friend's joke can be perceived as cold.

**Weaponized humor:** "I was just joking" can be used to deflect accountability for genuinely hurtful comments. This puts the target in a double bind: if they object, they're accused of lacking a sense of humor; if they don't object, the hurtful message stands.

### 3.8 When Humor Is Inappropriate (Reading the Room)

**General principles for when NOT to joke:**
1. **During someone's genuine distress:** Unless you know the person well enough to know they use humor to cope
2. **In formal settings:** Job interviews, funerals, court proceedings (unless appropriate)
3. **About sensitive topics with people you don't know well:** You can't predict their relationship to the topic
4. **When the humor targets someone present who hasn't consented to being teased**
5. **When you're the only one who would find it funny:** Humor is fundamentally social; if no one else will appreciate it, the social function fails

**Reading the room -- what this means:**
- Observe how others in the setting communicate before using humor
- Notice the emotional tone: is it relaxed, tense, sad, formal?
- Consider: What is the relationship between me and the other people here?
- Ask: Would this joke build connection, or could it cause harm?

**Cross-cultural considerations:** What one culture finds hilarious may be confusing or inappropriate in another. Australian humor often includes heavy sarcasm and self-deprecation; many East Asian cultures prefer subtle, non-confrontational humor that avoids embarrassment; American humor tends toward the explicit and direct.

**If a joke misfires:** Pause, acknowledge briefly ("That came out wrong" or "Sorry, that landed badly"), and move on. Extended apologies make it worse. The key is demonstrating awareness that the joke didn't work.

## 4. UNDERSTATEMENT AND LITOTES

### 4.1 What Understatement Is

Understatement (also called meiosis) is the deliberate presentation of something as less significant, less extreme, or less important than it actually is. It is the opposite of hyperbole.

**Litotes** is a specific form of understatement that achieves emphasis through **negation of the opposite**. Instead of saying something positive directly, the speaker denies its negative.

### 4.2 Examples of Understatement

> "He's not exactly the brightest." = He's quite dumb.

This uses litotes (negation of "bright") to convey stupidity without saying it directly.

> "It wasn't the best day." = It was terrible.

The speaker presents a terrible day as merely "not the best" -- a massive reduction of the actual severity.

> "Not bad." = Good, sometimes very good.

This is one of the most common examples of litotes in everyday English. Its meaning depends heavily on delivery:
- Said flatly: "It's acceptable"
- Said with raised eyebrows and emphasis: "It's actually really good!"
- Said by a British person: Could mean anything from "adequate" to "the best thing I've ever experienced"

> "He's not the most organized person." = He's extremely disorganized.

> "She's not entirely wrong." = She's mostly right (or even completely right).

> "It's a bit chilly." (said in a blizzard) = It's freezing.

### 4.3 British Understatement as a Cultural Phenomenon

British English has a particularly strong cultural tradition of understatement. It is a "distinctive linguistic and cultural phenomenon characterized by the deliberate minimization of the scale, intensity, or importance of events, emotions, or statements."

**Famous historical examples:**

During the Battle of Jutland in 1916, Admiral Beatty witnessed the explosion of two of his largest battlecruisers -- catastrophic losses with hundreds of deaths -- and remarked to his subordinate: "There seems to be something wrong with our bloody ships today."

During the Korean War, American Major General Robert H. Soule asked British Brigadier Thomas Brodie "How are the Glosters doing?" The Glosters were in fact being overwhelmed by Chinese forces in one of the war's bloodiest engagements. Brodie replied: "A bit sticky, things are pretty sticky down there." The American interpreted this as the situation being manageable. The Glosters were nearly annihilated.

**The Monty Python Black Knight:** In *Monty Python and the Holy Grail*, the Black Knight has his arm cut off and says "Tis but a scratch." After losing both arms: "It's just a flesh wound." This is a parody of British understatement taken to absurd extremes. The humor comes from the incongruity between the severity of the situation and the minimizing language.

**Cultural pragmatic function:** British understatement serves several purposes:
- **Emotional restraint:** Displaying composure under pressure is culturally valued
- **Social face-saving:** Understatement avoids imposing strong emotions on others
- **In-group signaling:** Those who understand the understatement are "in"; those who take it literally are "out"
- **Humor:** The gap between the understated language and the reality creates comic incongruity

### 4.4 How Understatement Works Pragmatically

In Gricean terms, understatement **flouts the Maxim of Quantity** -- the speaker provides less information than the situation requires. The hearer recognizes this flouting and derives an implicature: "The speaker said less than they could have, so the reality must be more extreme than what they said."

**The inferential path:**
1. The speaker says something that appears less extreme than the context warrants
2. The hearer recognizes that the speaker is *choosing* to understate (this is a cooperative violation, not a mistake)
3. The hearer infers that the actual state of affairs is more extreme than described
4. But the hearer often doesn't know *how much* more extreme -- understatement is inherently imprecise

**In Relevance Theory terms:** Understatement achieves relevance not through its literal content (which would be under-informative) but through the *act* of understating. The cognitive effects come from recognizing the gap between what was said and what was meant, and from any social inferences about *why* the speaker chose to understate (composure, humor, face-saving).

### 4.5 When Understatement Is Used and Why

1. **Emotional restraint:** When the speaker doesn't want to display the full intensity of their feelings ("I'm a bit disappointed" after a devastating loss)
2. **Humor:** The gap between statement and reality creates comic effect (the Black Knight)
3. **Face-saving:** Understating criticism protects the target's face ("There's room for improvement" vs. "This is terrible")
4. **Cultural convention:** In cultures that value restraint (British, Japanese), understatement is the expected register for many situations
5. **Power display:** Understating a serious situation can signal that the speaker is in control and not threatened
6. **Ironic effect:** Extreme understatement can actually *intensify* the message through its very inadequacy

### 4.6 Relevance for Autistic Individuals

Understatement is potentially problematic for literal processors because the **literal meaning is valid** -- "Not bad" *is* a truthful description of something that is good. The pragmatic meaning (that it's actually quite good or even excellent) can only be derived through recognizing the Quantity violation. Research shows that autistic individuals may process the literal meaning without accessing the intended stronger meaning, particularly when they don't recognize the contextual cues that signal understatement.

## 5. OVERSTATEMENT AND HYPERBOLE

### 5.1 What Hyperbole Is

Hyperbole is **deliberate exaggeration** for emphasis, emotion, or humor. The speaker says something that is literally false but that conveys an emotional truth. Despite being literally untrue, hyperbolic utterances are readily understood and serve multiple communicative purposes.

Research by McCarthy and Carter (2004) found that hyperbole is "extraordinarily pervasive" in everyday conversation -- much more so than metaphor or irony. They documented examples occurring multiple times per minute in some conversations.

### 5.2 Examples of Hyperbole

> "I've told you a million times."

Literal meaning: false (nobody has said anything a million times). Intended meaning: "I've told you many times and I'm frustrated that you haven't listened."

> "I'm dying of hunger."

Literal meaning: false (the speaker is not actually dying). Intended meaning: "I'm very hungry."

> "That took forever."

Literal meaning: false (nothing takes forever). Intended meaning: "That took much longer than I expected or wanted."

> "Everyone knows that."

Literal meaning: false (not literally every person on Earth knows it). Intended meaning: "This is common knowledge; you should know it."

> "This bag weighs a ton."

Literal meaning: false. Intended meaning: "This bag is very heavy."

> "I nearly died of embarrassment."

Literal meaning: false. Intended meaning: "I was extremely embarrassed."

### 5.3 "Literally" Used Non-Literally

One of the most debated examples of hyperbole in contemporary English:

> "I literally died laughing."
> "I was literally on fire with excitement."
> "My head literally exploded."

The word "literally" has undergone **semantic bleaching** -- the process by which a word gradually loses its original specific meaning and becomes a general intensifier. This process has been documented since at least the late 1700s. "Literally" now functions alongside "really," "truly," "absolutely," and "genuinely" as a generic intensifier that can be used in both literal and figurative contexts.

**Why this feels wrong to some people:** The word "literally" originally meant "in a literal manner" -- i.e., not figuratively. Using it to intensify figurative statements ("I literally died") appears to make it mean its own opposite. Prescriptivists find this objectionable. However, linguists point out that the same semantic shift has happened to "really" (originally "in reality"), "truly" (originally "in truth"), and "very" (originally from Latin *verus* = "true"). The process is normal and well-documented.

**Pragmatic function:** When someone says "I literally died," they are not confused about whether they are alive. The word "literally" functions as an intensifier that signals "I am not exaggerating *the emotion* even though I am exaggerating *the event*." It actually strengthens the hyperbole rather than contradicting it.

### 5.4 How Hyperbole Works Pragmatically

In Gricean terms, hyperbole **flouts the Maxim of Quality** (truthfulness). The speaker says something blatantly untrue, and the hearer recognizes this as a cooperative violation, deriving an implicature about the actual state of affairs.

**The inferential path:**
1. The speaker says something the hearer knows is literally false
2. The hearer assumes the speaker is being cooperative (not lying)
3. The hearer infers that the speaker is using exaggeration to convey the *direction* and *intensity* of their meaning (even though the specific quantity is wrong)
4. The hearer derives an approximation: "I've told you a million times" → "many times, and I'm frustrated"

**In Relevance Theory terms:** Hyperbole, like metaphor, is treated as "loose use" of language on a continuum with literal speech. The speaker provides an utterance that is not literally true but that achieves relevance by conveying a range of cognitive effects efficiently. "I'm dying of hunger" communicates the intensity of hunger, the urgency of the need, and the speaker's emotional state -- all in four words. This is more relevant (better ratio of cognitive effects to processing effort) than a literal description would be.

### 5.5 When Hyperbole Is Expected vs. Confusing

**Expected (conventional) hyperbole:** Many hyperbolic expressions are so common that they are essentially idioms: "starving" (very hungry), "freezing" (very cold), "tons of" (many), "ages ago" (a long time ago). These are expected in informal speech and are unlikely to be interpreted literally by anyone.

**Creative hyperbole:** More novel exaggerations require more pragmatic inference: "His ego is so big it needs its own zip code" (requires recognizing this as creative exaggeration, not literal confusion).

**Potentially confusing situations:**
- When hyperbole is used about quantities or timings that could be literal ("I waited for two hours" -- is this exact or exaggerated?)
- When the context doesn't make the literal falsity obvious ("Everyone was there" -- literally everyone, or most people?)
- When hyperbole is nested inside other non-literal language

### 5.6 The Hyperbole-Sarcasm Overlap

Hyperbole and sarcasm frequently co-occur, and their interaction can be confusing:

> After someone spills coffee: "Oh, great job! That was brilliant!"

Is this hyperbole (exaggerating the quality of the action for comic effect) or sarcasm (saying the opposite of what is meant)? In this case, it's both -- the speaker is exaggerating praise that they don't mean, creating a double layer of non-literality.

**How to distinguish:**
- **Pure hyperbole** exaggerates *in the direction of the truth*: "I'm SO tired" (really tired, exaggerated) -- the direction of meaning is preserved
- **Pure sarcasm** reverses the direction: "What a lovely day" (said during a storm) -- the direction is inverted
- **Hyperbolic sarcasm** exaggerates in the *wrong direction*: "Oh, what an AMAZING job!" (said about a terrible job) -- the sarcasm is intensified by the hyperbole

Research suggests that "sarcasm is typically seen as more critical than hyperbole" because sarcasm involves reversal of the speaker's actual evaluation, whereas hyperbole merely intensifies it.

## 6. METAPHOR AND SIMILE (Brief Treatment)

### 6.1 How Metaphor Functions Pragmatically

In Relevance Theory terms, metaphor is a form of **loose use of language** -- the speaker uses a word or phrase that is not literally true but that communicates a range of intended properties more efficiently than a literal description would.

**Example:**
> "John is a bulldozer."

The speaker doesn't mean John is literally a piece of heavy machinery. Through loose use, the word "bulldozer" makes manifest a range of properties -- powerful, unstoppable, doesn't care what's in the path, flattens opposition -- and the hearer selects the contextually relevant subset. This process is continuous with literal language use; it's the same comprehension mechanism operating with greater degrees of looseness.

Sperber and Wilson argue that "literal use, loose use, hyperbole, and metaphor form a continuum" -- they are "not genuinely distinct categories, at least from descriptive, psycholinguistic, or pragmatic points of view." The difference is one of degree, not kind.

### 6.2 Why Metaphor Is NOT the Primary Difficulty for Autistic Individuals

A crucial finding from recent research challenges the common assumption that metaphor is the central challenge in autistic figurative language processing.

**Key research findings:**

1. **Language skills matter more than autism:** A meta-analysis by Kalandadze et al. (2018, published in *Autism*) found that "differences between the autism spectrum disorder and typically developing groups were small and nonsignificant when the groups were matched based on language ability." This is a critical finding: it suggests that metaphor difficulties in autism may be largely attributable to general language processing differences rather than a specific inability to process non-literal meaning.

2. **Metaphor comprehension correlates with vocabulary:** Lampri et al. (2024, *Autism Research*) found that "expressive vocabulary skills were a key factor in the metaphor comprehension and production performance of the children with ASD."

3. **Novel metaphors may actually be preserved:** Surprisingly, some research found "no group differences" between autistic and typically developing participants in novel metaphor comprehension, with the ASD group showing difficulty primarily with *conventional* metaphors. This suggests that the difficulty may be with stored figurative meanings (requiring cultural/experiential learning) rather than with the process of metaphorical mapping itself.

4. **Metaphor generation can be a strength:** Verbal creativity research (Kasirer & Mashal 2014, PMC) found that in metaphor generation tasks, autistic individuals sometimes outperformed typically developing peers -- they produced more novel and creative metaphoric expressions.

**What IS genuinely difficult:** The research consistently shows that the pragmatic aspects of figurative language -- recognizing *when* someone is being metaphorical (as opposed to literal), recognizing the *social reasons* for choosing metaphor over literal language, and processing the *paralinguistic cues* that signal non-literal intent -- are more challenging than the metaphorical mapping process itself.

### 6.3 Conceptual Metaphor Theory (Lakoff & Johnson)

George Lakoff and Mark Johnson's *Metaphors We Live By* (1980) proposed that metaphors are not merely decorative language but fundamental structures of human thought. We don't just *talk* about things metaphorically -- we *think* about them metaphorically.

**Core conceptual metaphors:**

**TIME IS MONEY:**
- "You're *wasting* my time" (waste = use unproductively, from the domain of resources)
- "This gadget will *save* you hours" (save = preserve a resource)
- "I've *invested* a lot of time in her" (invest = allocate resources for returns)
- "You're *running out* of time" (a consumable resource depleting)

**ARGUMENT IS WAR:**
- "Your claims are *indefensible*" (can't be defended against attack)
- "He *attacked* every weak point in my argument" (military assault)
- "I've never *won* an argument with her" (war outcomes)
- "Her criticisms were right *on target*" (aiming weapons)

**LIFE IS A JOURNEY:**
- "She's at a *crossroads* in her career" (decision point on a path)
- "He's gone *off track*" (deviated from the route)
- "We're not *getting anywhere*" (lack of progress = lack of movement)
- "She's *come a long way*" (progress = distance traveled)

**Why this matters:** These metaphors are so deeply embedded in thought that native speakers rarely notice them as metaphorical. They structure how we reason about abstract concepts, not just how we talk about them. For autistic individuals, explicitly understanding the *systematic* nature of conceptual metaphors (the mapping from source domain to target domain) may make them more accessible, because the mapping itself is a learnable *pattern*.

### 6.4 Dead Metaphors vs. Novel Metaphors vs. Creative Metaphors

**Dead metaphors** are expressions whose metaphorical origins have been forgotten. They are processed as literal language:
- "The *leg* of the table" (originally metaphorical: a table doesn't have biological legs)
- "The *foot* of the mountain" (mountains don't have feet)
- "The *head* of the department" (no biological head involved)
- "The *eye* of the needle" (no biological eye)
- "The *hands* of the clock" (no biological hands)
- "The *mouth* of the river" (no biological mouth)

These are typically NOT difficult for autistic individuals because they have been conventionalized into the standard meaning of the word. You don't need to perform any metaphorical mapping -- "table leg" is simply what that part is called.

**Conventional metaphors** are recognized as metaphorical but are common and familiar:
- "He has a warm personality" (WARMTH IS AFFECTION)
- "I see what you mean" (UNDERSTANDING IS SEEING)
- "She's feeling down" (SAD IS DOWN)

These are intermediate in difficulty: they require knowing the conventional figurative meaning, which is a matter of language experience.

**Novel/creative metaphors** require active metaphorical reasoning:
- "The committee is a graveyard of good ideas" (requires constructing the mapping: ideas go in but never come out alive)
- "His lecture was a sleeping pill" (requires recognizing the shared property: inducing sleep/boredom)

### 6.5 Simile as Explicitly Marked Comparison

Similes use **explicit comparison markers** ("like," "as") to signal that a comparison is being made:
- "She is **like** a bulldozer" (simile) vs. "She **is** a bulldozer" (metaphor)
- "He eats **like** a horse" (simile) vs. "He's a horse at the dinner table" (metaphor)
- "Her voice was **as** smooth **as** silk" (simile)

**Why similes are easier to process:** Research consistently shows that similes are easier than metaphors for autistic individuals because they are "equivalent to literal statements in terms of processing" -- the explicit "like" or "as" signals that a comparison is being made, removing the need to infer the speaker's non-literal intent. As Lampri et al. (2024) explain, "metaphors introduce an implicit comparison between the target concept and the source concept, while similes are considered to be a more explicit form of non-literal language, whose processing is likely to place fewer cognitive demands on autistic individuals, compared to metaphors."

## 7. IDIOMS

### 7.1 What Idioms Are

An idiom is a fixed expression whose meaning **cannot be fully derived from the meanings of its component words**. The meaning is conventional -- you have to learn what it means as a whole, not just what its parts mean.

**Key property:** Idioms are compositionally non-transparent to varying degrees. "Kick the bucket" doesn't have anything to do with kicking or buckets -- the meaning "to die" is arbitrary relative to the component words.

### 7.2 The Transparency Spectrum

Not all idioms are equally opaque. Researchers classify idioms along a spectrum of **decomposability** (Gibbs & Nayak 1989):

#### Normally Decomposable (Transparent)
The component parts can be mapped to parts of the figurative meaning in a relatively straightforward way.

- **"Spill the beans"** = reveal a secret. "Spill" maps to "reveal/release" and "beans" maps to "the secret information." The metaphor is traceable: spilling something means letting it out accidentally, and the "beans" are the hidden things.
- **"Break the ice"** = initiate social contact in a tense situation. "Break" maps to "overcome/shatter" and "ice" maps to "social coldness/tension."
- **"Miss the boat"** = miss out on an opportunity. The metaphorical mapping is clear: an opportunity is a boat, and missing it means it leaves without you.
- **"Blow off steam"** = release pent-up emotion. The mapping to a pressure cooker releasing steam is transparent.

#### Abnormally Decomposable (Semi-Transparent)
The components relate to the figurative meaning, but the relationship is less straightforward or more metaphorically complex.

- **"Let the cat out of the bag"** = reveal a secret. You can see a *kind* of connection (releasing something hidden from a container), but it's more indirect than "spill the beans." The specific image of a cat in a bag is historically arbitrary.
- **"Carry a torch for someone"** = have unrequited love. The connection between a torch (flame) and passion exists but is mediated by the conceptual metaphor LOVE IS FIRE.
- **"Bury the hatchet"** = make peace. Requires knowing the cultural reference to Native American peace ceremonies.

#### Nondecomposable (Opaque)
The meaning cannot be distributed across the component parts. The entire expression must be learned as a unit.

- **"Kick the bucket"** = to die. There is no way to assign "kick" to any part of "dying" and "the bucket" to another part. The origin is debated and essentially irrelevant to contemporary users.
- **"By and large"** = generally, overall. The components "by" and "large" bear no recognizable relation to the meaning.
- **"Under the weather"** = feeling ill. No transparent connection between weather and illness.
- **"Barking up the wrong tree"** = pursuing a mistaken approach. While you can construct a narrative (a dog barking at a tree the squirrel isn't in), the mapping is quite specific and culturally mediated.

### 7.3 Common English Idioms with Literal vs. Idiomatic Meanings

| Idiom | Literal image | Idiomatic meaning |
|-------|--------------|-------------------|
| Break the ice | Smash frozen water | Start a conversation in an awkward situation |
| Kick the bucket | Strike a pail with your foot | Die |
| Spill the beans | Knock over a container of beans | Reveal a secret |
| Let the cat out of the bag | Release a feline from a sack | Reveal a secret accidentally |
| Hit the nail on the head | Strike a nail precisely | Say something exactly right |
| Bite the bullet | Clamp teeth on a projectile | Endure something painful bravely |
| Burn bridges | Set fire to structures over water | Destroy relationships/options |
| Beat around the bush | Strike vegetation near a shrub | Avoid getting to the point |
| Cost an arm and a leg | Price equals body parts | Very expensive |
| Pull someone's leg | Tug on a person's limb | Joke with someone / tease |
| The ball is in your court | A sphere is in your playing area | It's your turn to act/decide |
| A piece of cake | A portion of baked goods | Something very easy |

### 7.4 How Idioms Are Processed: The Dual-Route Model

Research on idiom processing has moved beyond the simple question of "literal first or figurative first?" to recognize that multiple routes operate simultaneously.

**The Hybrid/Dual-Route Model** proposes:

1. **Retrieval route:** When we encounter a familiar idiom, the *whole expression* is stored in memory as a unit (like a very long word). We can retrieve its meaning directly, without analyzing the component parts. This is fast and efficient for known idioms.

2. **Compositional route:** Simultaneously, the language system automatically decomposes the expression into its parts and computes the literal meaning. This route operates by default for all language and cannot be completely suppressed.

**Key research finding:** Both routes are activated in parallel. The Lexical Representation Hypothesis assumes that "literal word activation runs in parallel with retrieval of the idiom as a separate holistic representation, and the idiom retrieval route wins out in speed over the computation of literal word meanings." In other words, for familiar idioms, you typically access the figurative meaning *faster* than you compute the literal meaning.

**But decomposability matters:** For decomposable idioms (where the parts contribute to the meaning), the literal analysis actually *supports* figurative comprehension. For opaque idioms (where the parts don't contribute), the literal analysis can *interfere* with figurative comprehension.

Research by Hubbard et al. (2023, *Psychophysiology*) confirmed that "predictability and decomposability separately contribute to compositional processing of idiomatic language" -- both how expected an idiom is and how transparent its components are independently affect processing.

### 7.5 Context Effects on Idiom Processing

Research has examined how context biases idiom interpretation. Cacciari et al. (2020, *Journal of Psycholinguistic Research*) used idioms in sentences with either literally or figuratively biasing contexts:

> **Figurative context:** "The new schoolboy just wanted to **break the ice** with his peers..." (figurative = initiate social contact)
> **Literal context:** "The chilly Eskimo just wanted to **break the ice** on the lake..." (literal = physically shatter ice)

Their findings showed that while context influences processing, "factors such as idiom literality can supersede standard processing and integration mechanisms under certain conditions." This means that some idioms are so strongly associated with their figurative meaning that even a literal-biasing context can't fully override the figurative interpretation.

### 7.6 Why Some Idioms Are Easier Than Others for Autistic Individuals

Research on figurative language processing in autism (Lampri 2024, *Autism Research*; Kalandadze et al. 2018, *Autism*) identifies several factors:

1. **Transparency:** Transparent/decomposable idioms are easier because the component meanings provide a "bridge" to the figurative meaning. Opaque idioms must be learned as whole units.

2. **Familiarity and frequency:** More commonly encountered idioms are easier regardless of transparency, because the figurative meaning has been encountered and stored more times.

3. **Contextual support:** Idioms in supportive contexts (where the surrounding text makes the figurative meaning likely) are easier than idioms in neutral or misleading contexts.

4. **Interference from literal meaning:** Interestingly, highly transparent idioms can sometimes be *harder* for autistic individuals because the transparent literal meaning is so accessible that it interferes with accessing the intended figurative meaning. This creates a paradox: the very transparency that should help can sometimes hinder, because decomposable idioms "pull in the direction of literal compositional parsing rather than the expected idiomatic one."

5. **Learning pattern:** The meta-analysis by Kalandadze et al. found that idiom interventions for autistic individuals tend to produce "item-specific learning with minimal near-term transfer" -- meaning that autistic individuals can learn individual idioms effectively but may not generalize to new, unencountered idioms as readily. This contrasts with metaphor and sarcasm interventions, which showed "generalization beyond trained material."

**Practical implication:** For autistic individuals, idioms are best learned as a *vocabulary exercise* -- each idiom is essentially a new word to learn, with its own meaning that must be memorized. Understanding the etymological or metaphorical origin can help *some* idioms stick (knowing that "burn bridges" comes from military strategy helps remember the meaning), but for opaque idioms, there is no shortcut -- they simply must be learned.

## 8. CROSS-CUTTING THEMES: AUTISM AND NON-LITERAL LANGUAGE

### 8.1 What Research Actually Shows About Autism and Humor

The common assumption that autistic individuals "lack a sense of humor" is **not supported by research**. What research actually shows:

1. **Autistic individuals do want to laugh and make others laugh** -- they produce and process humor differently, not deficiently.
2. **Pattern-based humor** (puns, wordplay, logical jokes, observational humor) is often a relative strength.
3. **Social humor** (teasing, sarcasm, humor that requires reading intentions) is more challenging.
4. **Autistic individuals prefer logical, structured, or pattern-based humor** that doesn't require extensive social inference.
5. **Autistic children tend to laugh genuinely when they find something funny**, rather than using laughter as a purely social signal -- which means their laughter is a more reliable indicator of actual amusement.
6. **Many autistic individuals excel at comedic observations** because their "outsider perspective" allows them to notice social absurdities that insiders take for granted.

### 8.2 The Hierarchy of Non-Literal Language Difficulty

Based on the meta-analytic reviews, non-literal language types can be roughly ranked by difficulty for autistic individuals:

**Typically easier:**
- Dead metaphors (processed as literal language)
- Similes (explicitly marked as comparisons)
- Transparent idioms (components help access meaning)
- Puns based on phonological patterns (pattern-based)

**Intermediate:**
- Conventional metaphors (require stored figurative knowledge)
- Hyperbole (requires recognizing exaggeration)
- Understatement (requires recognizing deliberate under-informativeness)

**Typically harder:**
- Irony and sarcasm (require recognizing reversed meaning + speaker attitude)
- Opaque idioms (must be memorized as units)
- Teasing (requires reading relationship context and intentions)
- Deadpan humor (requires recognizing humor without prosodic signals)

**Critical caveat:** This hierarchy describes *group tendencies*, not universal truths. Individual autistic people vary enormously. Language ability, cognitive style, social experience, and the specific context all affect performance. The meta-analyses consistently show that matching for language ability reduces or eliminates many group differences.

### 8.3 Prosodic and Paralinguistic Signals for Humor

Research on humor signals shows that humor is typically (but not always) accompanied by:
- Changes in vocal pitch and rhythm
- Slight acceleration toward a punchline
- Smiling (which changes voice quality in detectable ways)
- Eye contact or eyebrow movements signaling "this is not serious"
- A distinctive "humor register" -- slightly different from the speaker's normal speech patterns

However, research also reveals a counterintuitive finding: "conversational humor is not marked by higher pitch or volume, increased speech rate, or significant pauses." The signals are more subtle than commonly assumed, which helps explain why they're difficult for autistic individuals to detect reliably.

For autistic individuals who rely on explicit signals, verbal humor markers can be helpful:
- "Just kidding"
- "No, but seriously..."
- Laughter following a statement
- "You know I'm joking, right?"

## Summary of Key Pragmatic Principles

1. **Humor is primarily social, not linguistic:** Even linguistic humor (puns) is typically deployed for social purposes (bonding, tension relief, status negotiation).

2. **Most humor involves pragmatic inference:** Understanding that someone is being funny requires the same machinery used for all pragmatic interpretation -- recognizing intentions, deriving implicatures, and processing non-literal meaning.

3. **The Benign Violation Theory provides the best general framework:** Humor requires something to feel wrong AND simultaneously okay. This explains both when humor works and when it fails.

4. **Non-literal language exists on a continuum:** Literal → loose use → hyperbole → metaphor → irony. The mechanism is the same; the degree of departure from literal truth increases.

5. **Pattern-based humor is a potential autistic strength:** Puns, observational humor, and absurdist humor rely on pattern recognition, which is often an autistic cognitive strength.

6. **Signal-dependent humor is a potential autistic challenge:** Deadpan, teasing, and sarcasm rely on paralinguistic signals or social context that autistic individuals may process differently.

7. **Idioms are vocabulary items, not inference problems:** Each idiom must be learned individually. This is a memorization task, not a reasoning task.

8. **Language ability, not autism per se, is the primary predictor** of figurative language comprehension when properly controlled.
