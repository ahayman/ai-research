# Alternative Approaches to Autistic Masking: Neurodiversity-Affirming Practices, Acceptance-Based Interventions, and Ethical Technology Design

## Research Focus
This research investigates alternatives to normalization-based interventions for autistic people, focusing on neurodiversity-affirming practices, acceptance-based therapies, environmental accommodations, ethical AAC design, and unmasking research.

---

## 1. ACCEPTANCE-BASED INTERVENTIONS

### 1.1 Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) for Autistic People

#### Pahnke et al. — NeuroACT Randomized Controlled Trial
- **Citation:** Pahnke, J., Jansson-Frojmark, M., Andersson, G., Bjureberg, J., Jokinen, J., Bohman, B., & Lundgren, T. (2023). "Acceptance and commitment therapy for autistic adults: A randomized controlled pilot study in a psychiatric outpatient setting." *Autism*, 27(5), 1422-1437.
- **DOI:** 10.1177/13623613221140749
- **PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36510817/
- **PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10291362/
- **Study Design:** Randomized two-group controlled pilot trial. 20 autistic adults received 14 weeks of NeuroACT group treatment; 19 received treatment as usual (TAU).
- **NeuroACT Program:** ACT-based group treatment adapted specifically for autistic adolescents and adults. Weekly group sessions of 150 minutes, 12-14 sessions. Each session includes a mindfulness/acceptance exercise, homework review, session theme introduction, new homework, and group evaluation.
- **Key Findings:**
  - Perceived stress and quality of life (primary outcomes) were statistically significantly improved in NeuroACT vs. TAU (effect sizes d = 0.70-0.90)
  - Psychological inflexibility, cognitive fusion, cognitive and behavioral avoidance, and autistic mannerism also significantly improved
  - Participants managed distressing thoughts better, perceived themselves as more flexible, and avoided stressful situations less
  - No significant difference between groups in depression, anxiety, sleep problems, social aspects of autism, everyday functioning, or executive challenges
  - The program was rated as logical and reasonable by participants
- **Conclusion:** NeuroACT is a promising treatment for autistic adults with co-existing stress and reduced quality of life.

#### Pahnke Doctoral Dissertation
- **Citation:** Pahnke, J. (2022). *Acceptance and Commitment Therapy for Autism Spectrum Disorder.* Doctoral dissertation, Karolinska Institutet, Stockholm, Sweden.
- **Full text:** https://brainproof.se/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/Johan-Pahnke-Komplett-avhandling-digital-version.pdf
- **Significance:** Comprehensive body of work establishing the evidence base for ACT adaptations for autistic populations.

#### Systematic Review of ACT for Autism/ID
- **Citation:** Byrne, G., & O'Mahony, T. (2020). "Acceptance and commitment therapy (ACT) for adults with intellectual disabilities and/or autism spectrum conditions (ASC): A systematic review." *Journal of Contextual Behavioral Science*, 18, 247-272.
- **Source:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2212144720301964
- **Key Finding:** ACT may be a useful intervention in reducing psychological difficulties and improving adaptive functioning skills for autistic adults, though the small number of individuals and poor methodological rigor of included studies limit conclusive findings.

### 1.2 Mindfulness-Based Approaches for Autistic Adults

#### Virtual Group-Based Mindfulness — RCT
- **Citation:** Lunsky, Y., Carter, M., Hutton, S., Wozniak, J., Chen, S., & Weiss, J. (2025). "Virtual group-based mindfulness for autistic adults: A randomized controlled trial." *Autism*.
- **DOI:** 10.1177/13623613251340101
- **PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40598750/
- **Study Design:** 6-week manualized autistic-informed group-based virtual mindfulness intervention with 63 autistic adults from across Canada.
- **Key Findings:**
  - Improvements in overall distress and stress in treatment condition
  - Improvements in self-compassion, mindfulness, and mental wellbeing
  - Gains maintained at follow-up
  - Autism-informed adaptations to standard mindfulness-based interventions can lead to self-reported improvements

#### Virtual Mindfulness — Feasibility Study
- **Citation:** Lunsky, Y., et al. (2022). "Virtual Group-Based Mindfulness Intervention for Autistic Adults: A Feasibility Study." *Mindfulness*, 13, 1706-1718.
- **DOI:** 10.1007/s12671-022-01909-4
- **PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9189269/
- **Key Finding:** An adapted virtual group-based mindfulness intervention designed based on input from autistic adults showed feasibility and preliminary effectiveness.

#### Systematic Review of Mindfulness-Based Interventions
- **Citation:** Systematic review (2024). "Mindfulness-Based Interventions for People with Autism Spectrum Disorder: A Systematic Literature Review."
- **PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11506216/
- **Key Finding:** Growing evidence supports adapted mindfulness approaches for autistic populations, though adaptations must account for sensory and processing differences.

### 1.3 Self-Compassion Interventions

#### ASPAA Program (Autism-Specific Self-Compassion)
- **Citation:** Cai, R. Y., & Galvin, J. (2024/2025). "A qualitative exploration of an autism-specific self-compassion program: The ASPAA." *Autism*.
- **PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38383994/
- **PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11134985/
- **Program Design:** Six weekly online modules co-designed with autistic researchers and community members, informed by Mindful Self-Compassion and Compassion Focused Therapy.
- **Key Findings:**
  - Significant improvements in self-compassion, positive affect, and psychological wellbeing
  - Significant reductions in anxiety and depressive symptoms
  - Reductions in emotion regulation difficulties
  - **Important caveat:** Some autistic adults reported emotional difficulties or distress when completing self-compassionate exercises

#### Self-Compassion Navigation for Autistic Adults
- **Citation:** Cai, R. Y., & Galvin, J. (2025). "Self-Compassion isn't Always Easy: Helping Autistic Adults Navigate Difficult Responses to Self-Compassion Practices." *Autism in Adulthood*.
- **DOI:** 10.1177/27546330251334425
- **Key Finding:** Self-compassion is significantly lower in autistic adults compared to non-autistic adults, but autistic adults with higher self-compassion display lower symptoms of anxiety and depression. Self-compassion mediates the positive relationship between autistic traits and anxiety/depressive symptoms.

#### Compassion Focused Intervention
- **Citation:** (2025). "Examining the Impact of a Brief Compassion Focused Intervention on Everyday Experiences of Compassion in Autistic Adults Through Psychophysiology and Experience Sampling."
- **PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12081517/
- **Key Finding:** Brief compassion-focused interventions can impact daily compassion experiences in autistic adults, though physiological and experiential responses may differ from neurotypical populations.

## 2. NEURODIVERSITY-AFFIRMING PRACTICES

### 2.1 The Neurodiversity Paradigm — Origins and Definition

#### Collective Origins of Neurodiversity
- **Citation:** Botha, M., Chapman, R., Giwa Onaiwu, M., Kapp, S. K., Stannard Ashley, A., & Walker, N. (2024). "The neurodiversity concept was developed collectively: An overdue correction on the origins of neurodiversity theory." *Autism*, 28(5).
- **DOI:** 10.1177/13623613241237871
- **PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38470140/
- **Key Findings:**
  - The concept of neurodiversity has primarily been attributed to Judy Singer, but evidence shows the concept has multiple origins
  - The neurodiversity movement started in the late 1980s and early 1990s with Autism Network International (ANI)
  - Singer wrote an Honours thesis on the subject in 1998, published as a book chapter in 1999
  - The 1992 founding of ANI, with members such as Jim Sinclair, laid much of the groundwork for neurodiversity theory
  - Nick Walker (2014) defined neurodiversity, the neurodiversity movement, and the neurodiversity paradigm — tying neurodiversity to the idea that "all brains are to a degree unique," the movement as a rights movement, and the paradigm as a broader discussion of diversity, cultural constructions, and social dynamics

#### Judy Singer's Original Contribution
- **Citation:** Singer, J. (1999). "Why can't you be normal for once in your life? From a 'problem with no name' to the emergence of a new category of difference." In M. Corker & S. French (Eds.), *Disability Discourse.* Open University Press.
- **Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judy_Singer
- **Significance:** Coined the term "neurodiversity" in her 1998 Honours thesis at University of Technology, Sydney, based on research with autistic activists on the InLv (Independent Living) email list.

#### Nick Walker's Paradigm Framework
- **Citation:** Walker, N. (2014). "Neurodiversity: Some Basic Terms & Definitions." Blog post / *Neuroqueer Heresies.*
- **Interview:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8992885/ — "Toward a Neuroqueer Future: An Interview with Nick Walker" (2021), *Autism in Adulthood*.
- **Key Definitions:**
  - **Neurodiversity:** The diversity of human brains and minds — the infinite variation in neurocognitive functioning within our species
  - **Neurodiversity Paradigm:** A framework in which neurodiversity is understood as a form of human diversity subject to the same social dynamics as other forms of diversity (including dynamics of power and oppression)
  - **Neurodiversity Movement:** A social justice movement seeking civil rights, equality, respect, and full societal inclusion for the neurodivergent

### 2.2 Leadbitter et al. 2021 — Neurodiversity-Affirming Interventions

- **Citation:** Leadbitter, K., Buckle, K. L., Ellis, C., & Dekker, M. (2021). "Autistic Self-Advocacy and the Neurodiversity Movement: Implications for Autism Early Intervention Research and Practice." *Frontiers in Psychology*, 12, 635690.
- **DOI:** 10.3389/fpsyg.2021.635690
- **PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8075160/
- **PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33912110/
- **Key Arguments:**
  - All autism intervention stakeholders need to understand and actively engage with the views of autistic people and with neurodiversity as a concept and movement
  - Intervention researchers and practitioners must move away from a normative agenda
  - Must pay diligence to environmental goodness-of-fit, autistic developmental trajectories, internal drivers and experiences, and autistic-prioritized intervention targets
  - Need to redefine effectiveness — outcomes must be endorsed by the autistic community
  - Must rely on partnerships with autistic individuals
  - Greater emphasis needed within intervention programs on natural developmental processes, coping strategies, autonomy, and well-being

### 2.3 Schuck/Lerner et al. — Framework for Neurodiversity-Affirming Interventions

- **Citation:** Lerner, M. D., Gurba, A. N., et al. (2023). "A Framework for Neurodiversity-Affirming Interventions for Autistic Individuals." *Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology*.
- **PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10430771/
- **PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37578755/
- **Key Framework Elements:**
  - Neurodiversity-affirming interventions (NAIs) should reframe intervention goals to focus on supporting the strengths — including social strengths — of autistic individuals
  - NAIs should create environments that foster autistic strengths, which can mean teaching peers how to include people with a range of differences
  - The NAI framework must not be limited to oral communicators with average-or-above IQ and few support needs; it must apply across ages, cognitive and verbal ability, and full complexity of autistic life
  - ABA has come under increasing scrutiny for failing to include autistic people in design of intervention elements and consideration of goals
  - Social skills training teaches autistic people how to mask their autism; potential harms of masking include exhaustion, anxiety, depression, frustration, and decreased self-esteem

### 2.4 Strengths-Based Approaches

#### Taylor et al. — Strengths Use Predicts Wellbeing
- **Citation:** Taylor, E. C., Livingston, L. A., Clutterbuck, R. A., Callan, M. J., & Shah, P. (2023). "Psychological strengths and well-being: Strengths use predicts quality of life, well-being and mental health in autism." *Autism*, 27(6), 1826-1839.
- **DOI:** 10.1177/13623613221146440
- **PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36639858/
- **PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10375006/
- **Key Findings:**
  - Strengths use strongly predicted better quality of life, subjective well-being, and lower levels of anxiety, depression, and stress in autistic people
  - Autistic individuals who frequently use their strengths reported significantly better outcomes than those who use them less
  - Suggests that encouraging strengths usage could significantly enhance well-being in autistic populations

#### Strengths-Based Programs for Autistic Adolescents
- **Citation:** (2023). "'He Sees his Autism as a Strength, Not a Deficit Now': A Repeated Cross-Sectional Study Investigating the Impact of Strengths-Based Programs on Autistic Adolescents."
- **PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9900207/
- **Key Findings:** Positive impacts on health and well-being, social relationships and interactions, self-confidence and self-esteem, sense of belonging, and activities and participation.

#### Expert Discussion on Strengths-Based Approaches
- **Citation:** (2022). "An Expert Discussion on Strengths-Based Approaches in Autism." *Autism in Adulthood*.
- **PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8992818/
- **Key Finding:** Positive autistic identity is associated with improved mental health and wellbeing. Supporting autistic individuals to reflect on personal values in action can foster development of positive self-identity.

### 2.5 Neurodiversity-Affirming Therapy Models and Guidelines

#### Therapist Neurodiversity Collective Guidelines
- **Source:** https://therapistndc.org/neurodiversity-affirming-therapy/
- **Core Principles:**
  - Therapy goals and best practices should be developed collaboratively with autistic clients
  - Eliminate goals relating to "masking"
  - Value autistic communication as legitimate
  - Appreciate autism as part of human variation
  - Focus on quality of life rather than normalization

#### Wagland et al. — Scoping Review of Neurodiversity-Affirming Care
- **Citation:** Wagland, Z., Sterman, J., Scott-Cole, L., Spassiani, N., & Njelesani, J. (2025). "Promoting Neurodiversity-Affirming Care for Autistic Children: A Scoping Review." *Autism in Adulthood*.
- **DOI:** 10.1177/27546330251357479
- **Key Finding:** Interventions for autistic children should focus on quality of life, value autistic communication, appreciate autism as part of human variation, and eliminate goals relating to masking.

#### Dallman et al. — Neurodiversity-Affirming Occupational Therapy
- **Citation:** Dallman, A. R., Williams, K. L., et al. (2022). "Neurodiversity-Affirming Practices for Occupational Therapy." *The Open Journal of Occupational Therapy*, 10(2).
- **Source:** https://scholarworks.wmich.edu/ojot/vol10/iss2/13/
- **Key Finding:** Educators and service providers can practice in a neurodiversity-affirming manner through adapting the environment and changing their approach to interventions.

#### Naturalistic Developmental Behavioral Intervention Framework
- **Citation:** Vivanti, G., et al. (2022). "Neurodiversity and Autism Intervention: Reconciling Perspectives Through a Naturalistic Developmental Behavioral Intervention Framework." *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*.
- **DOI:** 10.1007/s10803-021-05316-x
- **PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9508016/
- **Key Finding:** Attempts to reconcile neurodiversity principles with intervention research, proposing that naturalistic developmental behavioral approaches can be compatible with neurodiversity values when goals are collaboratively set and focus on wellbeing.

## 3. ENVIRONMENTAL ACCOMMODATIONS VS. PERSON-MODIFICATION

### 3.1 The Social Model of Disability Applied to Autism

#### Chapman 2021 — Neurodiversity and Social Ecology
- **Citation:** Chapman, R. (2021). "Neurodiversity and the Social Ecology of Mental Functions." *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, 16(6), 1360-1372.
- **DOI:** 10.1177/1745691620959833
- **PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33577400/
- **Key Arguments:**
  - Proponents of the neurodiversity movement challenge the pathologization of minority cognitive styles
  - Neurocognitive diversity should be reframed as a normal and healthy manifestation of biodiversity
  - Uses ecological functional models to take relational contributions to collectives into account alongside individual functionality
  - The ecological model has room to locate functioning or malfunctioning between people (double empathy problem) or in the relationship between people and environment
  - Applies these models to human mental functioning using the example of autism

#### Milton 2012 — The Double Empathy Problem
- **Citation:** Milton, D. E. M. (2012). "On the ontological status of autism: The 'double empathy problem.'" *Disability & Society*, 27(6), 883-887.
- **DOI:** 10.1080/09687599.2012.710008
- **Source:** https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/09687599.2012.710008
- **Key Argument:** Empathy is fundamentally dependent on mutual reciprocity or salience rather than individual cognitive faculties. Communication breakdowns between autistic and non-autistic people are bidirectional, not unidirectional deficits of autistic people. This challenges the framing that autistic people lack empathy and instead frames it as a mutual misunderstanding.

#### Milton 2022 — Double Empathy Problem: Ten Years On
- **Citation:** Milton, D., Gurbuz, E., & Lopez, B. (2022). "The 'double empathy problem': Ten years on." *Autism*, 26(8).
- **DOI:** 10.1177/13623613221129123
- **Key Update:** Reviewed a decade of research supporting the double empathy framework and its implications for intervention design.

#### Levitt & Disability Studies Scholars — Re-invigorating the Social Model
- **Citation:** (2017). "Exploring how the social model of disability can be re-invigorated for autism: in response to Jonathan Levitt." *Disability & Society*, 32(7).
- **DOI:** 10.1080/09687599.2017.1328157
- **Source:** https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/09687599.2017.1328157
- **Key Arguments:**
  - Removing social barriers by changing non-autistic people's attitudes toward autism
  - Ensuring positive language of autism
  - Preventing the categorization of autism
  - Fully enacting legal protections for autistic people

### 3.2 Universal Design and Sensory-Friendly Environments

#### Mostafa — ASPECTSS Autism Design Index
- **Citation:** Mostafa, M. (2014). "Architecture for Autism: Autism ASPECTSS in School Design." *Archnet-IJAR: International Journal of Architectural Research*, 8(1), 143-158.
- **Source:** https://www.archnet.org/publications/9101
- **ResearchGate:** https://www.researchgate.net/publication/283099110
- **Key Framework — Seven ASPECTSS Principles:**
  1. **Acoustics** — managing sound environments
  2. **Spatial Sequencing** — logical organization of spaces
  3. **Escape Space** — quiet retreat areas
  4. **Compartmentalization** — defining distinct functional areas
  5. **Transition Spaces** — managing sensory transitions between zones
  6. **Sensory Zoning** — organizing spaces by sensory quality
  7. **Safety** — designing for physical safety
- **Theoretical Basis:** Sensory Design Theory — altering the sensory environment using specific design interventions can benefit autistic individuals
- **ASPECTSS 2.0:** Expanded to include eight additional concepts: colour, lighting, materials, furniture, wayfinding/navigation, technology, sensory economics, programming/operation
- **Evidence:** First applied at the Advance School in Cairo; world's first evidence-based architectural design guidelines for autism

#### Universal Design for Neurodiversity
- **Source:** https://www.creativespirit-us.org/spaces-for-all-how-universal-design-can-transform-neurodiverse-inclusion/
- **Key Finding:** Autism-friendly design guidelines can be scaled across K-12 learning environments, workplaces, urban contexts, public spaces, commercial spaces, and residential neighborhoods. Preliminary research supports that these design strategies benefit not only autistic and neurodivergent people but other user groups with diverse needs.

#### Sensory-Friendly Design Elements
- **Key Elements Identified in Research:**
  - Dimmable lighting and soft flooring
  - Flexible spaces and furniture (movable walls, adjustable desks)
  - Clearly defined zones and wayfinding
  - Biophilic elements (plants, natural textures)
  - Sound management and acoustic treatment
  - Color and material considerations for sensory comfort

#### Systematic Review: Accommodations in Schools
- **Citation:** (2020). "Does the learning environment 'make the grade'? A systematic review of accommodations for children on the autism spectrum in mainstream school." *Scandinavian Journal of Occupational Therapy*.
- **DOI:** 10.1080/11038128.2020.1832145
- **Key Finding:** While there is a growing body of research on interventions to help autistic children adapt to school environments, accommodations to children's needs have been given comparatively less attention — representing a gap that needs addressing.

## 4. WHAT AAC APPS SHOULD DO: AUTHENTIC COMMUNICATION, NOT NORMALIZATION

### 4.1 Light & McNaughton — Communicative Competence Framework

- **Citation:** Light, J., & McNaughton, D. (2014). "Communicative Competence for Individuals who require Augmentative and Alternative Communication: A New Definition for a New Era of Communication?" *Augmentative and Alternative Communication*, 30(1), 1-18.
- **DOI:** 10.3109/07434618.2014.885080
- **PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30952185/
- **Full text:** https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.3109/07434618.2014.885080
- **Framework — Five Domains of Communicative Competence:**
  1. **Linguistic competence** — knowledge of the language code
  2. **Operational competence** — skills to operate the AAC system
  3. **Social competence** — knowledge of social rules of interaction
  4. **Strategic competence** — compensatory strategies to manage communication breakdowns
  5. **Psychosocial factors** — motivation, attitude, confidence, resilience
- **Key Argument:** Attainment of communicative competence is influenced not just by skills but also by psychosocial factors AND by barriers and supports in the environment. AAC must be evaluated in terms of the whole communicative ecosystem, not just the individual's ability to use a device.

### 4.2 AAC Best Practices for Autistic People

#### Person-Centered AAC Principles
- **Source:** National Autistic Society — https://www.autism.org.uk/advice-and-guidance/professional-practice/aug-alt-comm
- **Key Principles:**
  - No two autistic people are the same; no single AAC system works best for all
  - The best communication system increases autonomy, is easy for the communicator to use, and works effectively across environments and communication partners
  - During one exchange, a person might use non-aided AAC as well as various aided AAC depending on what works best or feels most comfortable
  - AAC use does NOT impede the development of speech; in fact, it may result in modest speech gains
  - AAC should be personalized to meet individual needs and reduce unique communicative challenges

#### Neurodiversity-Affirming AAC Practices
- **Source:** https://www.rootedtherapies.com/blog/aac-what-is-it-and-does-my-child-need-it
- **Key Principles:**
  - Therapy should shift focus from viewing communication differences as deficits to celebrating them as valuable and legitimate forms of communication
  - Move away from rigid behavioral goals
  - Welcome a variety of communication modalities: verbal speech, gestures, typed texts, and other forms of AAC
  - Setting mutually developed objectives is central to effective therapy
  - Value the strengths and preferences of the child

#### "Everyone Deserves AAC" — Speaking Autistic Adults
- **Citation:** (2021). "'Everyone Deserves AAC': Preliminary Study of the Experiences of Speaking Autistic Adults Who Use Augmentative and Alternative Communication." *Perspectives of the ASHA Special Interest Groups*.
- **DOI:** 10.1044/2021_PERSP-20-00220
- **Source:** https://pubs.asha.org/doi/10.1044/2021_PERSP-20-00220
- **Key Finding:** Even speaking autistic adults can benefit from AAC — it is not only for nonspeaking individuals. AAC can reduce the cognitive load of speech production and support authentic self-expression.

#### Aging Up AAC — Design for Autistic Adults
- **Citation:** (2024). "Aging Up AAC: An Introspection on Augmentative and Alternative Communication Applications for Autistic Adults."
- **Source:** https://arxiv.org/html/2404.17730
- **Key Arguments:**
  - AAC systems are predominantly designed for children; autistic adults have distinct needs
  - Person-centered design must account for the social and technical divide
  - Technology should support authentic communication rather than force normalization
  - AAC systems should bridge the gap between the individual's communicative intent and societal participation

#### AAC, Autism, and the Empathic Turn
- **Citation:** (2021). "AAC Technology, Autism, and the Empathic Turn." *Social Epistemology*.
- **DOI:** 10.1080/02691728.2021.1897189
- **Source:** https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/02691728.2021.1897189
- **Key Argument:** AAC technology design must incorporate an empathic understanding of autistic communication rather than imposing neurotypical communication norms.

### 4.3 Brady et al. — AAC Research and the Communication Bill of Rights

- **Key Contribution:** Brady, N. C., et al. contributed to revising the **Communication Bill of Rights** through the National Joint Committee for the Communication Needs of Persons With Severe Disabilities — intended to promote the communication rights and advocacy of "all individuals."
- **Related Research:** Brady, Thiemann-Bourque, Fleming, and Matthews published on predicting language outcomes for children learning AAC, examining both child and environmental factors.
- **Thiemann-Bourque et al. (2016):** Research on PECS and peer-mediated AAC intervention for minimally verbal preschoolers with autism.
- **Key Principle:** AAC use can result in communication gains and improved quality of life for minimally verbal or nonspeaking individuals. However, AAC abandonment rates are high, limiting societal participation — suggesting the design and implementation process needs fundamental improvement.

### 4.4 What AAC Technology SHOULD Do (Synthesis)

Based on the research, ethical AAC design principles include:
1. **Support authentic self-expression** — not train people to sound neurotypical
2. **Offer multimodal flexibility** — allow users to fluidly switch between communication methods
3. **Center autonomy** — the user decides how, when, and what to communicate
4. **Remove environmental barriers** — design for the ecosystem, not just the individual
5. **Include psychosocial support** — build confidence, motivation, and resilience into the experience
6. **Design with autistic people** — co-design, not design "for" without input
7. **Reduce cognitive load** — masking through technology is still masking
8. **Support across the lifespan** — not just children; autistic adults have AAC needs too
9. **Preserve communicative identity** — don't strip away personality or communicative style
10. **Measure success by participation and wellbeing** — not by approximation to neurotypical speech

## 5. KEY POSITION STATEMENTS AND GUIDELINES

### 5.1 ASAN — Autistic Self Advocacy Network

#### "For Whose Benefit?" White Paper
- **Citation:** Autistic Self Advocacy Network. (2021). *For Whose Benefit?: Evidence, Ethics, and Effectiveness of Autism Interventions.*
- **Full text:** https://autisticadvocacy.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/12/ACWP-Ethics-of-Intervention.pdf
- **Page:** https://autisticadvocacy.org/policy/briefs/intervention-ethics/
- **Key Positions:**
  - Autistic people rarely have a voice in creating and shaping therapies
  - Very little ethical guidance for autism therapies accounts for autistic needs, experiences, and perspectives
  - Ethical problems at the heart of ABA, as well as lack of evidence to support ABA
  - Autistic people should have the ultimate say in what autism services focus on
  - Autistic people deserve access to high-quality, evidence-based, ethical services working toward goals as defined by autistic people themselves
  - "The utter contempt and dehumanization with which ABA treats autistic people" — ABA's view of behavior inherently dehumanizes autistic people and minimizes their internal emotional and mental states

#### ASAN Response to ASHA Position Statements
- **Source:** https://autisticadvocacy.org/2018/08/asan-response-to-asha-position-statements/
- **Key Position:** ASAN opposes ASHA's broad positions on Facilitated Communication (FC) and Rapid Prompting Method (RPM) that presume all communications by users should not be considered their own words. ASAN urges individualized analysis to determine which communication supports are most effective for each person.

#### ASAN Letter on the Right to Communicate
- **Source:** https://autisticadvocacy.org/2018/07/asan-letter-to-asha-on-the-right-to-communicate/
- **Key Position:** Every person has the right to communicate, and communication supports should be individualized and person-centered.

### 5.2 UK NICE Guidelines

#### Autism in Adults — CG142
- **Source:** https://www.nice.org.uk/guidance/cg142
- **2021 Updates:**
  - Changed terminology: "people with autism" to "autistic people" (identity-first language)
  - Changed "challenging behaviour" to "behaviour that challenges"
  - Changed "symptoms" to "features"
  - Recognizes that different individuals prefer various terms: autistic spectrum condition, autistic spectrum difference, neurodiversity
  - Person-centered care: autistic person should be involved in decisions relating to their care
  - No recommendation for interventions aimed at changing core autistic features

#### Autism in Under 19s — CG170
- **Source:** https://www.nice.org.uk/guidance/cg170/chapter/recommendations
- **Key Recommendations:**
  - Interventions should be individualized
  - Environmental modifications should be considered
  - Communication support should be person-centered

### 5.3 INSAR — International Society for Autism Research

- **Website:** https://www.autism-insar.org/
- **Autistic Researchers Committee:** https://www.autism-insar.org/page/insararc
- **Policy Briefs:** https://www.autism-insar.org/page/PolicyBriefs

## 6. RESEARCH ON UNMASKING

### 6.1 Pearson & Rose 2021 — Conceptual Analysis of Masking

- **Citation:** Pearson, A., & Rose, K. (2021). "A Conceptual Analysis of Autistic Masking: Understanding the Narrative of Stigma and the Illusion of Choice." *Autism in Adulthood*, 3(1), 52-60.
- **DOI:** 10.1089/aut.2020.0043
- **PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8992880/
- **Source:** https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1089/aut.2020.0043
- **Key Findings:**
  - Masking is the conscious or unconscious suppression of natural autistic responses and adoption of alternatives across a range of domains
  - Masking relates to negative outcomes: late/missed diagnosis, mental health issues, burnout, and suicidality
  - Masking is an "unsurprising response to the deficit narrative and accompanying stigma that has developed around autism"
  - Framed as an "illusion of choice" — the social pressures make masking feel mandatory, not voluntary
  - **On unmasking:** Occurs by choice, after a long process of learning about themselves introspectively, spending time among other autistic people, learning from sharing relatable experiences through "outrospection," and therapizing themselves in various ways
  - Through self-acceptance, deep reflection, and working through trauma, some autistic people have learned to recognize subconscious layers of masking
  - Late diagnosis can trigger reprocessing of life history, making it difficult to disentangle which parts of identity are truly "them" vs. masking

#### Pearson & Rose — Book on Autistic Masking
- **Citation:** Pearson, A., & Rose, K. (2023). *Autistic Masking: Understanding Identity Management and the Role of Stigma.* Pavilion Publishing.
- **ISBN:** 978-1803882116

### 6.2 Price 2022 — "Unmasking Autism"

- **Citation:** Price, D. (2022). *Unmasking Autism: Discovering the New Faces of Neurodiversity.* Harmony Books.
- **ISBN:** 978-0593235232
- **Publisher:** https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/688819/unmasking-autism-by-devon-price-phd/
- **Key Arguments:**
  - Masking is a common coping mechanism where autistic people hide identifiably autistic traits to fit societal norms, adopting a superficial personality at the expense of mental health
  - Masking can be psychologically and physically harmful
  - Autism remains significantly underdiagnosed in women, people of color, trans and gender non-conforming people — many only recognizing traits later in life
  - The widespread understanding of autism is based on how it has presented historically in white, male, upper-class individuals
  - Masked autistic individuals are more likely to be marginalized in terms of race, gender, sexual orientation, and class
  - **Practical unmasking exercises:** celebrating special interests, cultivating autistic relationships, reframing autistic stereotypes, rediscovering values
  - Price is both a social psychologist (PhD, Loyola University Chicago) and an autistic self-advocate

### 6.3 Raymaker et al. 2020 — Defining Autistic Burnout

- **Citation:** Raymaker, D. M., Teo, A. R., Steckler, N. A., Lentz, B., Scharer, M., Delos Santos, A., Kapp, S. K., Hunter, M., Joyce, A., & Nicolaidis, C. (2020). "'Having All of Your Internal Resources Exhausted Beyond Measure and Being Left with No Clean-Up Crew': Defining Autistic Burnout." *Autism in Adulthood*, 2(2), 132-143.
- **DOI:** 10.1089/aut.2019.0079
- **PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32851204/
- **PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7313636/
- **Methodology:** Community-based participatory research; thematic analysis of 19 interviews and 19 public internet sources.
- **Definition:** Autistic burnout is a syndrome resulting from chronic life stress and a mismatch of expectations and abilities without adequate supports. Characterized by:
  1. **Pervasive, long-term exhaustion** (typically 3+ months)
  2. **Loss of skills** (regression in previously acquired abilities)
  3. **Reduced tolerance to stimulus** (increased sensory sensitivity)
- **Key Findings:**
  - Autistic burnout is DISTINCT from occupational burnout or clinical depression
  - Caused by life stressors that add to cumulative load, combined with barriers to support
  - Expectations outweigh abilities, resulting in burnout
  - Negative impacts on health, capacity for independent living, quality of life, including suicidal behavior
  - **Recovery factors:** acceptance and peer support, unmasking, social withdrawal, reduced expectations, doing things "in an autistic way"
  - Unmasking is explicitly identified as a recovery factor from autistic burnout

### 6.4 Cassidy et al. 2018 — Camouflaging as Suicide Risk Marker

- **Citation:** Cassidy, S., Bradley, L., Shaw, R., & Baron-Cohen, S. (2018). "Risk markers for suicidality in autistic adults." *Molecular Autism*, 9, 42.
- **DOI:** 10.1186/s13229-018-0226-4
- **PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30083306/
- **PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6069847/
- **Key Findings:**
  - 72% of autistic adults scored above the recommended psychiatric cut-off for suicide risk (vs. 33% in the general population)
  - Non-suicidal self-injury, **camouflaging**, and number of unmet support needs significantly predicted suicidality
  - **Camouflaging and unmet support needs appear to be risk markers for suicidality unique to autism**
  - This study provides empirical evidence that masking is not just uncomfortable but potentially life-threatening

### 6.5 Cage et al. 2018 — Autism Acceptance and Mental Health

- **Citation:** Cage, E., Di Monaco, J., & Newell, V. (2018). "Experiences of Autism Acceptance and Mental Health in Autistic Adults." *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 48(2), 473-484.
- **DOI:** 10.1007/s10803-017-3342-7
- **PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29071566/
- **PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5807490/
- **Study Design:** 111 autistic adults completed online survey examining autism acceptance and depression, anxiety, and stress.
- **Key Findings:**
  - Acceptance from external sources AND personal acceptance significantly predicted depression levels
  - Greater personal autism acceptance predicted lower depressive symptoms
  - Acceptance from others significantly predicted lower stress
  - Experiences of camouflaging related to higher rates of depression
  - **Self-acceptance of autistic identity plus perceived acceptance from friends and family is linked to well-being in autistic adults**

### 6.6 Benefits of Allowing Autistic Expression (Synthesis)

Based on the collected research, the evidence supports the following conclusions about unmasking and authentic autistic expression:

1. **Reduced burnout risk:** Unmasking is explicitly identified as a recovery factor from autistic burnout (Raymaker et al., 2020)
2. **Reduced suicide risk:** Camouflaging is a unique risk marker for suicidality in autistic adults (Cassidy et al., 2018)
3. **Improved mental health:** Self-acceptance of autistic identity predicts lower depression (Cage et al., 2018)
4. **Improved quality of life:** Strengths use predicts better quality of life, wellbeing, and mental health (Taylor et al., 2023)
5. **Enhanced self-compassion:** Self-compassion programs designed for autistic adults improve wellbeing and reduce anxiety and depression (Cai & Galvin, 2024)
6. **Reduced stress:** ACT-based interventions help autistic adults manage stress and improve quality of life without requiring masking (Pahnke et al., 2023)
7. **Better social connection:** When unmasking is safe, autistic people report deeper, more authentic relationships (Pearson & Rose, 2021)
8. **Preserved energy:** Unmasking conserves cognitive and emotional energy otherwise spent on performing neurotypicality (Price, 2022)

## 7. THEORETICAL FOUNDATIONS: WHAT TECHNOLOGY SHOULD DO INSTEAD OF NORMALIZATION

### 7.1 The Core Ethical Argument

The research converges on a clear ethical framework for technology design in autism:

**From normalization to accommodation:**
- The social model of disability (Chapman, 2021) locates disability in the environment, not the person
- The double empathy problem (Milton, 2012) shows communication breakdowns are bidirectional
- Masking causes measurable harm including burnout (Raymaker, 2020), mental health deterioration (Cage et al., 2018), and suicide risk (Cassidy et al., 2018)

**What technology SHOULD do:**
1. **Support authentic expression** — help autistic people communicate in ways natural to them, not train them to approximate neurotypical communication
2. **Reduce environmental barriers** — modify the communication environment (e.g., offering multiple modalities) rather than modifying the person
3. **Preserve identity** — technology should amplify the user's authentic voice, not replace it with a "normal" one
4. **Enable choice** — provide tools for when a person WANTS to adjust their communication, without making adjustment the default or goal
5. **Build on strengths** — leverage autistic communication strengths (precision, directness, pattern recognition) rather than treating them as deficits
6. **Measure success differently** — evaluate technology by wellbeing, participation, and self-determination, not by approximation to neurotypical norms
7. **Co-design with autistic people** — nothing about us without us (Leadbitter et al., 2021; ASAN, 2021)

### 7.2 The Harm of Normalization-Focused Technology

Technology that trains autistic people to appear neurotypical:
- Extends masking into digital spaces
- Increases cognitive load rather than reducing it
- Risks contributing to burnout, depression, and suicidality
- Undermines authentic identity development
- Sends the message that authentic autistic communication is wrong or insufficient
- Violates the principle of informed consent if users don't understand the masking framework
