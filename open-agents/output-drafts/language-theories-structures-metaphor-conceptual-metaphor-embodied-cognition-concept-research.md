## 1. The Central Claim: Metaphor Is Conceptual, Not Linguistic

### The Radical Thesis

Lakoff and Johnson's central claim, first articulated in *Metaphors We Live By* (1980), is that **our ordinary conceptual system, in terms of which we both think and act, is fundamentally metaphorical in nature**. This is not a claim about poetic language, literary devices, or rhetorical flourish. It is a claim about the basic architecture of human cognition.

The thesis has three interlocking components:

1. **Metaphor is conceptual, not linguistic**: Metaphorical expressions in language are surface manifestations of deeper conceptual mappings. When we say "I see what you mean," the words are not the metaphor -- the underlying conceptual mapping KNOWING IS SEEING is the metaphor. Language merely reflects it.

2. **Abstract concepts are understood through systematic mappings from bodily experience**: The principle of unidirectionality states that metaphorical mapping typically goes from the more concrete (source domain) to the more abstract (target domain). We understand abstract domains -- time, causation, morality, emotion, quantity, purpose -- through structured correspondences with domains grounded in direct sensorimotor experience.

3. **Metaphorical thought is largely unconscious and automatic**: Most conceptual metaphors operate below conscious awareness. We do not choose to think of arguments as wars or love as a journey -- these mappings are neurally entrenched patterns that structure perception, reasoning, and inference automatically.

[Source: [The Contemporary Theory of Metaphor - Lakoff](https://terpconnect.umd.edu/~israel/lakoff-ConTheorMetaphor.pdf)]
[Source: [Conceptual Metaphor - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conceptual_metaphor)]
[Source: [Conceptual Metaphor in Everyday Language - Lakoff & Johnson](https://cse.buffalo.edu/~rapaport/575/F01/lakoff.johnson80.pdf)]

### The Structure of Metaphorical Mapping

A conceptual metaphor is a systematic set of correspondences between a **source domain** (typically concrete, bodily, directly experienced) and a **target domain** (typically abstract). The mapping is not arbitrary -- it preserves the cognitive topology of the source domain.

Key structural principles:

- **Ontological correspondences**: Entities in the source domain correspond systematically to entities in the target domain (e.g., in LOVE IS A JOURNEY: travelers = lovers, vehicle = relationship, destination = shared life goals, crossroads = decision points, dead-end = relationship that cannot continue)
- **Epistemic correspondences**: Knowledge about the source domain maps onto the target domain, generating inferences (e.g., if you are at a crossroads on a journey, you must choose a direction; therefore, if your relationship is "at a crossroads," you must make a decision about its future)
- **Unidirectionality**: Mapping goes from concrete to abstract, not the reverse. We understand love in terms of journeys, but we do not understand journeys in terms of love.
- **Partiality**: Not every aspect of the source maps onto the target. The mapping is selective, highlighting certain aspects of the target while hiding others (ARGUMENT IS WAR highlights the adversarial, competitive aspects of argument while hiding collaborative, exploratory aspects).

[Source: [Conceptual Metaphor Theory - Springer](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1057/9780230286825_3)]

### The Invariance Principle

Lakoff's Invariance Principle (1990, 1993) provides a constraint on metaphorical mapping: **"Metaphorical mappings preserve the cognitive topology (i.e., the image-schema structure) of the source domain, in a way consistent with the inherent structure of the target domain."**

This means:
- Source domain interiors correspond to target domain interiors; exteriors to exteriors
- Everything from the source can be mapped onto the target that does not conflict with the image-schematic structure already present in the target
- The target domain has its own inherent structure that cannot be overridden by the mapping

The Invariance Principle raises the possibility that a great many, if not all, abstract inferences are actually metaphorical versions of spatial inferences inherent in the topological structure of image schemas.

[Source: [Invariance principle - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariance_principle_(linguistics))]
[Source: [The Contemporary Theory of Metaphor - Lakoff](https://terpconnect.umd.edu/~israel/lakoff-ConTheorMetaphor.pdf)]

## 2. Primary Metaphors: The Atoms of Metaphorical Thought

### The Theory of Conflation

Primary metaphors are the most basic, irreducible conceptual metaphors. They arise not from cultural convention or linguistic tradition but from **universal sensorimotor correlations experienced during early childhood**.

The mechanism was clarified by Christopher Johnson's **Theory of Conflation** (1997): during infancy and early childhood, subjective experiences and sensorimotor experiences are **conflated** -- experienced as a single, undifferentiated event. An infant being held by a caregiver simultaneously experiences the sensorimotor sensation of warmth and the subjective experience of affection. These two domains (warmth and affection) are not yet differentiated in the child's experience.

During a later phase of **differentiation**, the two domains separate -- the child comes to understand that warmth and affection are distinct concepts. But the cross-domain neural associations formed during conflation persist, creating the mechanisms for metaphorical mapping. The primary metaphor AFFECTION IS WARMTH is born.

Joseph Grady (1997) formalized the theory of primary metaphors, arguing that they:
- Arise from recurring correlations in embodied experience
- Are cognitively primitive (not decomposable into simpler metaphors)
- Are universal across cultures (because all humans share the same kind of body and develop in similar environments)
- Serve as the building blocks ("atoms") from which complex metaphors ("molecules") are composed

[Source: [Primary metaphor - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_metaphor)]
[Source: [Primary metaphors as inputs to conceptual integration - Grady](https://www.lang.osaka-u.ac.jp/~sugimoto/M&D2011/others/grady2005.pdf)]
[Source: [Metaphor is grounded in embodied experience - ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0378216604000219)]

### Catalog of Primary Metaphors

The following primary metaphors have been identified, each traced to a specific sensorimotor correlation:

| Primary Metaphor | Sensorimotor Grounding | Example Expressions |
|---|---|---|
| **MORE IS UP** | Stacking objects increases both quantity and height; pouring liquid into a container raises its level | "Prices are rising," "The stock market fell," "Turn the volume up" |
| **AFFECTION IS WARMTH** | Being held by a caregiver is simultaneously warm and affectionate | "A warm greeting," "She gave me the cold shoulder," "He's a cold person" |
| **KNOWING IS SEEING** | Visual perception correlates with conscious acquisition of knowledge | "I see what you mean," "That's a clear explanation," "His motives are transparent" |
| **INTIMACY IS CLOSENESS** | Physical proximity to caregivers correlates with emotional connection | "We're close friends," "They've grown apart," "A distant relative" |
| **TIME IS MOTION** | Experience of objects moving past us (or us moving past objects) correlates with the passage of time | "Time flies," "The deadline is approaching," "We're coming up on the anniversary" |
| **IMPORTANCE IS SIZE** | As children, important entities (adults, institutions) are physically larger | "Tomorrow's a big day," "It was a huge deal," "Just a small matter" |
| **DIFFICULTIES ARE BURDENS** | Carrying heavy objects is physically difficult | "She carries a heavy load," "Lighten your burden," "Weighed down by responsibility" |
| **PURPOSES ARE DESTINATIONS** | Reaching a physical destination achieves the purpose of a journey | "I'm going to be a star, but I'm not there yet," "She reached her goal," "He's on his way to success" |
| **HAPPY IS UP / SAD IS DOWN** | Drooping posture correlates with sadness; erect posture with positive emotional state | "I'm feeling up," "He's down in the dumps," "My spirits rose" |
| **STATES ARE LOCATIONS** | Being in a bounded physical location correlates with being in a state | "She's in trouble," "He fell into a depression," "We're in love" |
| **CAUSES ARE FORCES** | Physical forces cause physical effects | "What drove you to do that?" "She was pushed into it," "Compelled by circumstances" |
| **CHANGE IS MOTION** | Moving from one location to another correlates with changing state | "The town went from boom to bust," "Things are moving in the right direction" |
| **UNDERSTANDING IS GRASPING** | Manipulating objects leads to understanding them | "I can't grasp the concept," "Let me get a handle on this," "That's a slippery argument" |
| **SIMILARITY IS CLOSENESS** | Similar objects tend to cluster together spatially | "These colors are close," "Far from what I had in mind" |
| **CONTROL IS UP** | Physical dominance involves being above | "She's on top of things," "He has the upper hand," "Under his control" |

[Source: [Primary Metaphors - The Metaphor Society](https://metaphorsofmovement.co.uk/primary-metaphors/)]
[Source: [Primary Metaphor - Sketchplanations](https://sketchplanations.com/primary-metaphor)]
[Source: [Conceptual metaphor theory and teaching - ERIC](https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1117979.pdf)]

### The Significance of Universality

Primary metaphors are predicted to be universal because they arise from the structure of the human body interacting with the physical world -- a structure shared by all humans. Gravity makes MORE IS UP universal (stacking always goes up). Thermoregulation makes AFFECTION IS WARMTH universal (caregiver warmth is a mammalian constant). Visual dominance in human perception makes KNOWING IS SEEING universal.

This universality has been substantially confirmed by cross-linguistic research, though Kovecses (2005) demonstrates that while **generic-level** metaphors tend to be universal, **specific-level** elaborations are shaped by culture. The conceptual metaphor ANGER IS HEAT appears universal (grounded in the physiological experience of elevated body temperature during anger), but Tunisian Arabic shows culturally specific elaborations that go beyond the universal physiological embodiment.

[Source: [Metaphor in Culture: Universality and Variation - Kovecses](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/metaphor-in-culture/818345C1B0F60FC6AD67AE47460095EE)]

## 3. Complex Metaphors: Compositional Architecture

### From Atoms to Molecules

Complex metaphors are built by combining primary metaphors, often together with cultural knowledge and conventional imagery. They are "molecular" -- decomposable into primary metaphorical "atoms." This compositional structure explains both the systematicity and the cultural variation of complex metaphors.

### LOVE IS A JOURNEY

The canonical example. Its mapping structure:

| Source: JOURNEY | Target: LOVE |
|---|---|
| Travelers | Lovers |
| Vehicle | Relationship |
| Destinations | Shared life goals |
| Crossroads | Decision points |
| Dead-end street | Relationship that cannot continue |
| Impediments to travel | Relationship difficulties |
| Getting stuck | Relationship stagnation |
| Smooth road | Easy progress |
| Going in different directions | Having incompatible goals |

**Linguistic evidence**: "Our relationship hit a dead-end street." "We're at a crossroads." "The marriage is on the rocks." "We're getting nowhere." "We're going in different directions." "We've just taken off." "We may have to bail out." "We're off the track."

The vehicle in the mapping is at the superordinate category level (VEHICLE, not CAR), which maximizes the possibilities for mapping rich conceptual structure. Special cases include cars, boats, planes ("We may have to bail out"), rockets ("We've just taken off"), and trains ("We're off the track").

**Compositional analysis**: LOVE IS A JOURNEY is composed from multiple primary metaphors:
- PURPOSES ARE DESTINATIONS (the purpose of the relationship maps to the destination of the journey)
- INTIMACY IS CLOSENESS (the lovers' emotional closeness maps to physical proximity in the vehicle)
- DIFFICULTIES ARE BURDENS (relationship problems map to obstacles on the road)
- STATES ARE LOCATIONS (emotional states map to locations along the path)
- CHANGE IS MOTION (changes in the relationship map to movement along the path)

[Source: [The Contemporary Theory of Metaphor - Lakoff](https://terpconnect.umd.edu/~israel/lakoff-ConTheorMetaphor.pdf)]
[Source: [Mapping the brain's metaphor circuitry - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4267278/)]

### ARGUMENT IS WAR

Lakoff and Johnson's opening example in *Metaphors We Live By*. The mapping:

| Source: WAR | Target: ARGUMENT |
|---|---|
| Combatants | Arguers |
| Attacks | Criticisms |
| Defense | Justification/rebuttal |
| Strategy | Argumentative planning |
| Positions | Claims/stances |
| Ground gained/lost | Points won/conceded |
| Winning/losing | Persuading/being persuaded |
| Weapons | Rhetorical tools |

**Linguistic evidence**: "Your claims are **indefensible**." "He **attacked** every weak point in my argument." "His criticisms were right **on target**." "I **demolished** his argument." "He **shot down** all my arguments." "If you use that **strategy**, he'll **wipe you out**." "She **won** the argument."

Lakoff and Johnson make the profound observation: though there is no physical battle, "the structure of an argument -- attack, defense, counter-attack, etc. -- reflects this." They invite us to imagine a culture where argument is viewed not as war but as a **dance**, where participants are performers and the goal is to perform in a balanced and aesthetically pleasing way. In such a culture, the concept of "winning" an argument would be incoherent.

This demonstrates that conceptual metaphors do not merely describe reality -- they **constitute** it. The metaphor shapes what we do, how we experience arguments, and what counts as success in argumentation.

[Source: [Metaphors We Live By - Lakoff & Johnson](https://www.umsl.edu/~alexanderjm/MetaphorsWeLiveBy.pdf)]

### THEORIES ARE BUILDINGS

| Source: BUILDING | Target: THEORY |
|---|---|
| Foundation | Basic assumptions/axioms |
| Framework/structure | Logical organization |
| Support | Evidence |
| Construction | Theory development |
| Collapse | Theory failure/refutation |
| Reinforcement | Additional evidence |
| Architectural plans | Research methodology |

**Linguistic evidence**: "The theory has a solid **foundation**." "We need to **construct** a framework." "The argument **collapsed**." "The theory needs more **support**." "She's **building** on the work of previous researchers." "That evidence **undermines** his theory." "The whole edifice is **shaky**."

### TIME IS MONEY

| Source: MONEY/RESOURCE | Target: TIME |
|---|---|
| Spending | Using time |
| Saving | Conserving time |
| Wasting | Using time unproductively |
| Investing | Using time for future benefit |
| Budget | Schedule/plan |
| Cost | Time required |
| Profit | Time gained |

**Linguistic evidence**: "You're **wasting** my time." "This gadget will **save** you hours." "How do you **spend** your time?" "I've **invested** a lot of time in this." "That cost me an hour." "He's living on **borrowed** time."

This is a culturally specific complex metaphor -- not all cultures conceptualize time as a limited resource that can be quantified, allocated, and exchanged. It reflects the quantification of time in industrialized capitalist societies where time literally has monetary value (hourly wages, billable hours).

### LIFE IS A JOURNEY

The broadest and perhaps most deeply entrenched complex metaphor in many cultures:

**Linguistic evidence**: "He's at a **crossroads** in his life." "She has a long **road** ahead of her." "He's gone through some difficult **stages**." "She's lost her **way**." "He's **arrived**." "There's no **turning back**." "She's come a long **way**."

### MORALITY IS PURITY

Important for Lakoff's later work on moral reasoning and political framing:

Because it is better to be healthy than sick, morality is conceptualized in terms of health and attendant concepts like cleanliness and purity. When morality is understood as purity, its opposite is understood as impurity or dirtiness. This metaphor structures vast swaths of moral and religious discourse: "pure motives," "dirty money," "clean conscience," "moral stain," "washing away sins," "tainted reputation."

Lakoff's (1996) *Moral Politics* extended CMT into political analysis, arguing that conservative political reasoning is structured by a STRICT FATHER metaphor for the state, while liberal/progressive reasoning is structured by a NURTURANT PARENT metaphor. Both are complex metaphors composed from primary metaphors about morality, authority, care, and self-discipline.

[Source: [Metaphor, Morality, and Politics - Lakoff](http://www.wwcd.org/issues/Lakoff.html)]

### The Compositional Principle

The compositional properties of language and thought lead to an unbounded range of complex metaphorical thought expressed linguistically. Hundreds of conceptual metaphors have been cataloged, and they combine to serve as the foundation for novel metaphors. This compositional architecture explains:
- Why metaphorical systems are systematic rather than random
- Why new metaphorical expressions are immediately intelligible
- Why certain metaphorical mappings "work" and others do not
- How a small number of primary metaphors generate the vast landscape of abstract thought

## 4. Image Schemas: The Skeletal Structure of Thought

### Definition and Origin

Mark Johnson proposed in his 1987 book *The Body in the Mind* that embodied experience gives rise to **image schemas** -- abstract, pre-linguistic, dynamic patterns emerging from repeated instances of sensorimotor experience. Image schemas are not images in the visual sense; they are schematic structures -- skeletal patterns of spatial, force-dynamic, and kinesthetic experience.

Image schemas are conceived of as:
- **Pre-linguistic**: They exist before and independently of language
- **Dynamic**: They are not static templates but dynamic patterns of interaction
- **Highly schematic**: They capture only the most fundamental structural features
- **Gestalt-like**: They function as integrated wholes, not decomposable into simpler components
- **Embodied**: They arise directly from motor movement, object manipulation, and perceptual interaction

Image schemas serve as the **skeletal structure** for metaphorical mappings. When we use the metaphor THEORIES ARE BUILDINGS, the underlying image schema is SUPPORT (physical support maps to evidential support). When we talk about being "in love" or "out of trouble," we are deploying the CONTAINER schema.

[Source: [Image schema - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_schema)]
[Source: [From perception to meaning - ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/333034182_From_perception_to_meaning_Image_schemas_in_cognitive_linguistics)]

### Catalog of Major Image Schemas

#### CONTAINER (IN/OUT)

**Experiential basis**: From earliest infancy, we experience our bodies as containers (food goes in, waste comes out) and interact with containers (cups, rooms, buildings). We also experience bounded regions -- inside vs. outside spaces.

**Structural elements**: Interior, boundary, exterior -- these are the minimum requirements.

**Metaphorical extensions**:
- STATES ARE CONTAINERS: "She's **in** love," "He fell **into** depression," "Get **out of** trouble"
- CATEGORIES ARE CONTAINERS: "That falls **within** the scope of this theory," "He's **outside** the mainstream"
- THE VISUAL FIELD IS A CONTAINER: "The ship came **into** view," "He's **out of** sight"
- THE MIND IS A CONTAINER: "I can't get that idea **out of** my head," "Let that sink **in**"

**Logic inherited**: Containers have an interior, an exterior, and a boundary. If A is in the container and B is in A, then B is in the container (transitivity). Something cannot be simultaneously inside and outside the container (excluded middle for spatial containment).

#### SOURCE-PATH-GOAL

**Experiential basis**: From the first crawl to the first step, human beings experience purposeful movement -- starting at a source, traversing a path, arriving at a goal. This is the primary origin of the schema.

**Structural elements**: A source (starting point), a path (trajectory), a goal (endpoint), and a direction (orientation of movement along the path).

**Metaphorical extensions**:
- PURPOSES ARE DESTINATIONS: "She **reached** her goal," "We're getting **closer** to a solution"
- LIFE IS A JOURNEY: "He's at a **crossroads**," "She's come a long **way**"
- TIME IS MOTION: "Christmas is **approaching**," "The deadline has **passed**"
- ARGUMENT IS A JOURNEY: "We've **covered** a lot of ground," "Let me **go back** to my earlier point"

**Logic inherited**: If you start at A and follow a path to B, you pass through all intermediate points. You cannot arrive at the goal without traversing the path. You can be diverted, blocked, or turned back.

#### FORCE

**Experiential basis**: From birth, we experience forces acting on our bodies -- gravity pulling us down, hands pushing and pulling us, barriers blocking movement, momentum carrying us forward.

**Sub-schemas**:
- **COMPULSION**: External force causes movement ("She was **driven** to succeed")
- **BLOCKAGE**: A force prevents movement ("There's an **obstacle** in our way")
- **COUNTERFORCE**: Two opposing forces meet ("The forces of good and evil are in **conflict**")
- **DIVERSION**: A force deflects movement from its path ("He was **sidetracked**")
- **REMOVAL OF RESTRAINT**: A barrier is eliminated ("That **frees** you to act")
- **ENABLEMENT**: A force makes action possible ("That **empowers** you")
- **ATTRACTION**: A force draws entities together ("She was **drawn** to the idea")
- **RESISTANCE**: A force opposes movement but does not fully block it ("He **resisted** the temptation")

**Metaphorical extensions**:
- CAUSES ARE FORCES: "What **drove** you to that conclusion?"
- EMOTIONS ARE FORCES: "She was **overwhelmed** by grief," "He was **moved** by the speech"
- SOCIAL PRESSURE AS FORCE: "**Peer pressure** made him conform," "She was **pushed** into the role"

#### BALANCE

**Experiential basis**: Maintaining physical equilibrium is a constant background experience from the moment we learn to sit upright and walk. Gravitational forces, vestibular processing, and proprioception create an embodied understanding of balance.

**Structural elements**: A point of balance, forces on either side, equilibrium vs. disequilibrium.

**Metaphorical extensions**:
- JUSTICE IS BALANCE: "The **scales** of justice," "A **balanced** judgment"
- PSYCHOLOGICAL WELL-BEING IS BALANCE: "She's **well-balanced**," "He's become **unhinged**"
- FAIRNESS IS EQUAL DISTRIBUTION: "A **balanced** approach," "**Tilting** the playing field"

#### UP-DOWN (VERTICALITY)

**Experiential basis**: Gravity creates an absolute, inescapable asymmetry between up and down. Erect posture, stacking objects, rising liquids -- all create correlations between verticality and other dimensions of experience.

**Metaphorical extensions**:
- MORE IS UP: "Prices are **rising**," "Turn up the volume"
- HAPPY IS UP / SAD IS DOWN: "I'm feeling **up**," "He's **down** in the dumps"
- GOOD IS UP / BAD IS DOWN: "Things are looking **up**," "He's hit **rock bottom**"
- CONTROL IS UP: "She has the **upper** hand," "He's **under** her control"
- CONSCIOUS IS UP / UNCONSCIOUS IS DOWN: "**Wake up**," "He **fell** asleep"
- RATIONAL IS UP / EMOTIONAL IS DOWN: "**Rise** above your emotions," "He **sank** into despair"

Any creature with a body similar to ours, located within a gravitational field like the one we inhabit, would have some form of verticality schema.

#### CENTER-PERIPHERY

**Experiential basis**: Our bodies have a center (torso, organs) and a periphery (limbs, skin surface). Important things tend to be central; less important things peripheral.

**Metaphorical extensions**:
- IMPORTANT IS CENTRAL: "The **central** argument," "**Peripheral** concerns," "**Core** values"
- MAINSTREAM IS CENTER: "**Mainstream** opinion," "**Fringe** beliefs," "**Marginal** views"

#### LINK

**Experiential basis**: Physical connections between objects -- holding hands, chains, ropes, bridges.

**Metaphorical extensions**:
- RELATIONSHIPS ARE LINKS: "They're **tied** to each other," "The **bond** between them," "She **broke** the connection"
- LOGICAL CONNECTION IS PHYSICAL CONNECTION: "These ideas are **linked**," "**Unrelated** topics," "The **chain** of reasoning"

#### PART-WHOLE

**Experiential basis**: Starting with our own body, we experience the world as composed of parts and wholes. Our body is a whole with parts (arms, legs, head). Objects in the world have part-whole structure.

**Metaphorical extensions**:
- SOCIAL GROUPS ARE BODIES: "A **member** of the team," "The **body** politic," "The **head** of the organization"
- THEORIES ARE STRUCTURES WITH PARTS: "A **component** of the theory," "The **whole** picture," "A **piece** of the puzzle"

### Image Schemas as the Physics of Thought

Clausner and Croft (1999) demonstrated that all image schemas are embodied, and all image schematic domains appear to be basic domains. They function as the "physics" of conceptual thought -- providing the fundamental spatial, dynamic, and force-related structures that are then recruited for understanding abstract domains.

Image schemas are spatiotemporal relationships used in cognitive science as building blocks to conceptualize objects and events at a high level of abstraction. They capture the skeletal information of events and describe segmentation cuts essential for conceptualizing dynamic changes.

[Source: [Domains and image schemas - Clausner & Croft](https://terpconnect.umd.edu/~israel/Clausner&Croft-Construals.pdf)]
[Source: [Image Schema Combinations and Complex Events - Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s13218-019-00605-1)]

## 5. The Neural Theory of Metaphor

### Origins: The Neural Theory of Language Project

In 1988, Jerome Feldman and George Lakoff established the Neural Theory of Language (NTL) group at the International Computer Science Institute (ICSI) at UC Berkeley, with the goal of applying neural computation to results in cognitive linguistics and embodied cognition. The fundamental research question: how is language -- with its metaphorical structure -- neurally instantiated in the brain?

Srini Narayanan's 1997 dissertation provided the first neural computational theory of metaphor. Narayanan showed how metaphorical mappings could be implemented as learned neural connections between source and target domain circuits. The key insight: metaphorical reasoning is not a separate cognitive module but emerges from the same neural circuitry used for sensorimotor processing.

[Source: [The Neural Theory of Metaphor - Lakoff](http://www.neurohumanitiestudies.eu/archivio/SSRN-id1437794The_Neural_Theory_of_Metaphor.pdf)]
[Source: [Featured Research: Neural Theory of Language - ICSI Berkeley](https://www.icsi.berkeley.edu/icsi/gazette/2006/03/neural-theory-language)]

### Neural Cascade Theory

In the theory of neural cascades proposed by Narayanan and Lakoff:

- **Bidirectional cascades of neural activation** link complex linguistic form to complex metaphorical meanings via connections to and from many brain regions
- Metaphorical inferences arise via the **neural simulation of situations** that are understood, at least in part, via the activation of metaphor mapping circuits
- **Primitive concepts are all embodied** via brain circuitry linked to the body through the sensorimotor system
- Motion, for example, is characterized both via topographic maps of the visual field (in which activation moves across the visual map) and via executing circuitry for moving the body from an initial location, through a course of motion, to a final location

### Conflation and Neural Binding

The neural mechanism underlying primary metaphor formation:

1. **During early development**: Source and target domains are experienced simultaneously (warmth + affection, height + quantity). Neural circuits for both domains fire together repeatedly.
2. **Hebbian learning**: "Neurons that fire together wire together." Repeated co-activation creates strong neural connections between the source and target domain circuits.
3. **Differentiation**: As the child's conceptual system matures, the domains are differentiated -- the child learns that warmth and affection are distinct. But the neural connections persist.
4. **Metaphorical mapping**: The persistent cross-domain neural connections constitute the metaphorical mapping. Activation of the source domain circuit automatically cascades to the target domain circuit.

This is why primary metaphors are:
- **Automatic**: The neural connections fire without conscious intention
- **Universal**: The sensorimotor correlations that create them are shared by all humans
- **Deeply entrenched**: They are formed during the critical period of neural development
- **Resistant to override**: You cannot simply "think your way out of" conceptual metaphors

### fMRI Evidence for Sensorimotor Activation

Empirical neuroscience has provided substantial evidence supporting the neural theory of metaphor:

**Motor region activation for action metaphors**: Both literal action sentences ("He kicked the ball") and metaphoric action sentences ("He kicked the habit") activate motor cortex regions. Semantic activation appears in corresponding motor regions -- dorsal motor cortex for leg-related verbs and lateral motor cortex for arm-related verbs -- for both literal and figurative uses.

**Key finding**: Sensory-motor regions are activated almost immediately -- within **200 milliseconds** -- of an action verb being presented, for both literal and metaphorical contexts. This speed rules out post-hoc imagery and indicates automatic, mandatory activation.

**Gradient of activation**: Research reveals a gradient of sensorimotor activation from abstract to idiomatic to metaphoric to literal sentences:
- **Literal sentences** ("He kicked the ball"): Strongest sensorimotor activation
- **Metaphorical sentences** ("He kicked the habit"): Significant sensorimotor activation
- **Idiomatic sentences** ("He kicked the bucket" = died): Weaker sensorimotor activation
- **Abstract sentences** ("He considered the idea"): Weakest sensorimotor activation

This gradient suggests that as expressions become more conventionalized and their sensorimotor origins more opaque, they retain some but progressively less sensorimotor grounding.

**Dead metaphors still activate**: A recent EEG experiment (Harris et al., 2024) demonstrated that even so-called "dead" metaphors (e.g., "breakthrough") can activate sensorimotor areas within 250ms of exposure -- much faster than conscious appraisal. This suggests that even highly conventionalized metaphors retain some embodied grounding.

[Source: [The neural career of sensory-motor metaphors - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3131459/)]
[Source: [Are Metaphors Embodied? The Neural Evidence - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10171917/)]
[Source: [A piece of the action: Modulation of sensory-motor regions - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3819432/)]
[Source: [Mapping the brain's metaphor circuitry - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4267278/)]

### The Neural Mind (2025)

Lakoff and Narayanan's book *The Neural Mind: How Brains Think*, published by University of Chicago Press in 2025, represents the culmination of three decades of work. The book argues that:

- **Every thought is a physical thing** carried out by neural systems of the brain
- Thought happens when neurons form circuits and when simple circuits combine to form complex ones
- Thoughts derive their structures from the circuitry used for **vision, touch, and hearing**
- Simple thoughts come together into complex concepts, creating meaning, metaphors, and social/political frames
- The "metaphors we live by" are not abstractions but **deeply embodied neural constructs**

The book offers "one of the only encompassing reviews of embodied cognitive science," unifying neuroscience, computation, and cognitive linguistics into a single theory of thought.

[Source: [The Neural Mind - University of Chicago Press](https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/N/bo243406239.html)]
[Source: [Everything You Think Is Physical - LA Review of Books](https://lareviewofbooks.org/article/everything-you-think-is-physical/)]

## 6. Embodied Cognition: The Body Shapes the Mind

### Rejection of Mind-Body Dualism

Lakoff and Johnson's (1999) *Philosophy in the Flesh: The Embodied Mind and Its Challenge to Western Thought* laid out the philosophical implications of CMT. Their argument directly challenges the mind-body dualism that has dominated Western philosophy since Descartes.

Three key findings from cognitive science demand a revision of philosophical foundations:

1. **The cognitive unconscious**: The vast majority of thought is unconscious -- not Freudian-unconscious, but simply not available to introspection. Conceptual metaphors, image schemas, and most inferential processes operate automatically and below awareness.

2. **The embodied mind**: The mind is not a disembodied rational faculty. It is shaped and constrained by the body in which it is housed. Concepts are not abstract symbols manipulated by rules -- they are neural patterns grounded in sensorimotor experience.

3. **Metaphorical thought**: Abstract concepts are not literal representations of objective reality -- they are structured by metaphorical mappings from bodily experience. Time, causation, morality, the self, the mind, mathematics -- all are fundamentally metaphorical.

[Source: [Philosophy in the Flesh - Edge.org](https://www.edge.org/conversation/george_lakoff-philosophy-in-the-flesh)]
[Source: [Review: Philosophy in the Flesh - Words and Dirt](https://www.words-and-dirt.com/words/review-mark-johnson-and-george-lakoffs-philosophy-in-the-flesh/)]

### Embodied Realism

Lakoff and Johnson propose **embodied realism** as an alternative to both:

- **Objectivism** (the view that there is one objective reality and the mind can represent it accurately through abstract, disembodied reason): Rejected because the mind is embodied -- our concepts are shaped by our bodies, not by direct access to mind-independent truth.

- **Relativism** (the view that there is no objective reality and all perspectives are equally valid): Rejected because our embodied concepts are not arbitrary -- they are constrained by the real structure of our bodies and our interactions with the real physical world.

Embodied realism "demands that we become comfortable with splitting the difference between ideological camps seeking to discover the 'one true reality' and ones that claim no such reality exists." Our conceptual system is shaped by our embodiment, but that embodiment is itself shaped by real physical constraints (gravity, spatial extension, force dynamics). We have access to reality, but always through the lens of embodied cognition.

### Challenge to Western Philosophy

The implications are sweeping:

- **Descartes**: The cogito ("I think, therefore I am") assumes a disembodied thinking substance. CMT says there is no thought without a body.
- **Kant**: The categories of understanding are supposed to be pure, a priori, and universal. CMT says they are embodied, experiential, and metaphorically structured.
- **Formal logic**: Classical logic assumes literal, non-metaphorical propositions. CMT says most propositions are metaphorically structured, and their "logic" is inherited from the spatial logic of image schemas.
- **Moral philosophy**: Moral principles are supposed to be derived from pure reason. CMT says they are structured by metaphors (MORALITY IS PURITY, MORAL ACCOUNTING, JUSTICE IS BALANCE) grounded in bodily experience.
- **Philosophy of mind**: The mind is supposed to be a kind of computer manipulating abstract symbols. CMT says the mind is a neural system whose computations are grounded in, and constrained by, the body.

### Barsalou's Perceptual Symbol Systems

Lawrence Barsalou's (1999) Perceptual Symbol Systems theory provides independent convergent evidence for embodied cognition:

- Traditional cognitive science assumes concepts are **amodal symbols** -- abstract representations stripped of perceptual content
- Barsalou proposes that concepts are **multimodal re-enactments** of percepts: fragments of perceptual experience (visual, auditory, motor, proprioceptive) are extracted and stored as **perceptual symbols**
- These perceptual symbols are later recombined into **simulators** that function as concepts
- Conceptualizing a category (e.g., "chair") involves **simulating** the experience of perceiving and acting on exemplars of that category
- Language serves as a "shortcut" or "pointer" to concepts, triggering simulations rather than retrieving abstract definitions

This framework aligns with and supports CMT: if concepts are simulations of embodied experience, then metaphorical mapping is the process of running a simulation in one domain (bodily experience) to structure understanding in another domain (abstract thought).

[Source: [Grounded Cognition - Barsalou](http://www2.psychology.uiowa.edu/faculty/hollingworth/prosem/Barsalou.pdf)]
[Source: [On the Bodily Basis of Human Cognition - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8692281/)]

## 7. Critiques, Limitations, and Alternative Accounts

### Methodological Critiques

CMT has received sustained criticism across several dimensions:

**Methodology** (concepts vs. words): Critics argue that CMT emphasizes conceptual analysis (top-down) rather than rigorous empirical analysis of how words are actually used (bottom-up). The methodology of identifying metaphors in language and inferring conceptual structures from them is seen as potentially circular -- the theory predicts what you will find because the theory determines what counts as evidence.

**Direction of analysis**: CMT emphasizes a top-down approach (hypothesize a conceptual metaphor, then look for linguistic evidence) rather than a bottom-up approach (collect linguistic data, then infer conceptual structures). This has been criticized as confirmation bias.

**Category level**: CMT claims metaphorical mappings operate at the superordinate level (VEHICLE, not CAR), but critics question whether people actually think at this level of abstraction or at the more concrete basic level.

[Source: [Conceptual metaphor theory: Some criticisms and alternative proposals - Kovecses](https://benjamins.com/catalog/arcl.6.08kov)]

### Glucksberg's Class-Inclusion Model

Sam Glucksberg and Boaz Keysar (1990) proposed an alternative to CMT: the **class-inclusion model** of metaphor comprehension.

Key claims:
- Metaphors are not understood as implicit comparisons (similes) or as mappings between domains
- Instead, metaphors are **class-inclusion statements**: the topic of a metaphor is assigned to a diagnostic, ad hoc category, and the vehicle is a prototypical member of that category
- Example: "My surgeon is a butcher" does not map the domain of butchery onto the domain of surgery. Rather, it creates an ad hoc category (roughly: "people who cut things clumsily and crudely") and includes the surgeon in that category, with "butcher" as the prototype.
- This model emphasizes **online, context-dependent** meaning construction rather than pre-existing conceptual mappings

The debate between Glucksberg and Lakoff/Johnson represents a fundamental disagreement about whether metaphorical understanding relies on **pre-existing conceptual structures** (CMT) or **ad hoc categorization processes** (class-inclusion model).

[Source: [Metaphor Understanding and Accessing Conceptual Schema - Glucksberg & Keysar](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/232508433_Metaphor_Understanding_and_Accessing_Conceptual_Schema_Reply_to)]

### Gibbs's Defense and Extension

Raymond Gibbs (1992, 1994) has both defended CMT and pushed it further:

- Gibbs argued that metaphoric expressions reflect **preexisting conventional metaphoric mappings** in long-term memory
- Verbal metaphors are not simply instantiations of temporary, ad hoc categories but reflect preexisting conceptual mappings that are metaphorically structured
- Gibbs conducted extensive psycholinguistic experiments demonstrating that people process conventional metaphorical expressions as rapidly as literal expressions, suggesting that metaphorical mappings are indeed entrenched in the conceptual system
- However, Gibbs has also pushed for a more nuanced view of embodiment, emphasizing that embodied experience is not monolithic but varies across individuals, cultures, and contexts

### Mahon and Caramazza: The Grounding Problem

Bradford Mahon and Alfonso Caramazza (2008) posed the most serious philosophical challenge to embodied cognition:

**The abstract concept problem**: While it is plausible that embodiment helps us acquire concrete concepts (chair, kick, red), it is difficult to see how embodiment can account for abstract concepts like DEMOCRACY, ELECTRON, ENTROPY, JUSTICE, NUMBER, PATIENCE, and TRUTH. Representations grounded in sensorimotor systems do not seem well-suited to representing such abstract intentional contents.

**The parsimony argument**: If embodied cognition must admit "disembodied" cognitive processes to account for abstract concepts anyway, why have a special embodied theory just for concrete concepts? An amodal theory would be more parsimonious.

**Grounding by interaction**: Mahon and Caramazza propose a middle-ground position called "grounding by interaction." Concepts may have an amodal core representation, but this core is linked to sensorimotor information through spreading activation. The sensorimotor activation observed in fMRI studies may reflect this spreading activation rather than constitutive embodiment.

**Key distinction**: The fact that sensory and motor systems are activated during conceptual processing does not prove that concepts *are* embodied. The activation could equally reflect:
- Constitutive embodiment (the concept IS the sensorimotor pattern) -- the strong claim
- Secondary association (the concept triggers sensorimotor associations but is not constituted by them) -- the weak claim

There is growing consensus that some kind of **hybrid account** -- incorporating both modal (embodied) and amodal components -- is needed.

[Source: [On the need for Embodied and Dis-Embodied Cognition - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3153846/)]
[Source: [A critical look at the embodied cognition hypothesis - Mahon & Caramazza](https://digitalwellbeing.org/wp-content/uploads/2015/11/2008-A-critical-look-at-the-embodied-cognition-hypothesis-and-a-new-proposal-for-grounding-conceptual-content.pdf)]

### The "Is Everything Metaphorical?" Problem

A persistent critique is whether CMT overextends its claims:
- If nearly all abstract thought is metaphorical, what exactly is NOT metaphorical?
- Does the theory have falsifiability? Can any abstract expression be found that is genuinely non-metaphorical?
- Lakoff's response: basic-level concepts (chair, table, run, walk) and image schemas themselves are not metaphorical -- they are the literal, embodied foundation upon which metaphorical thought is built. Metaphor enters when we extend these embodied concepts to understand abstract domains.

### The Embodiment Debate: Universal vs. Variable

Critics have challenged the universality claims of CMT on grounds of embodiment:
- CMT emphasizes **universal, mechanical, monolithic** embodiment -- all humans have the same body, therefore the same primary metaphors
- Critics emphasize **non-universal, non-mechanical, non-monolithic** embodiment -- bodies differ (disability, gender, age, cultural body practices), and embodied experience is not a simple causal mechanism but is shaped by social and cultural context
- The relationship between metaphor and culture is more complex than CMT initially suggested, with cultural context playing a larger role than mere "elaboration" of universal patterns

[Source: [Criticisms of conceptual metaphor theory - Academia.edu](https://www.academia.edu/16520391/Criticisms_of_conceptual_metaphor_theory)]

### Recent Developments (2024-2025)

The field continues to evolve:
- **EEG evidence (Harris et al., 2024)** shows even dead metaphors activate sensorimotor areas within 250ms
- **L1/L2 processing studies (2024)** demonstrate that embodied effects are stronger in native languages than in second languages, suggesting that the depth of embodied grounding may vary with language proficiency
- **Digital embodiment (2025)** -- new research explores how embodied cognition and CMT apply to technology-mediated communication, using hyperscanning techniques to measure neural synchrony across physical and digital modalities
- **Grounded cognition synthesis (2025)** -- a growing minimalist account argues that abstract concepts "come in many flavors" and can be grounded through reference to events, situations, meta-cognition, interoception, and emotion, rather than requiring a single unified account

[Source: [A Review of Studies Supporting Metaphorical Embodiment - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10376178/)]
[Source: [Embodied metaphors in the digital age - Frontiers in Psychology 2025](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2025.1648733/full)]
[Source: [Issues in Grounded Cognition - Journal of Cognition 2025](https://journalofcognition.org/articles/10.5334/joc.444)]

## 8. Patterns: The Generative Architecture of Abstract Thought

### The Core Pattern

The deepest insight of CMT, and the one most relevant to a pattern-oriented learner, is that abstract thought has a **generative architecture** -- a small set of primitive structures (image schemas and primary metaphors) combine compositionally to produce the entire landscape of abstract conceptual thought.

The generative structure:

```
EMBODIED EXPERIENCE
    ↓ (repeated sensorimotor correlations)
IMAGE SCHEMAS (CONTAINER, PATH, FORCE, BALANCE, UP-DOWN, LINK, PART-WHOLE, CENTER-PERIPHERY)
    ↓ (abstracted from spatial/bodily experience)
PRIMARY METAPHORS (MORE IS UP, KNOWING IS SEEING, AFFECTION IS WARMTH, ...)
    ↓ (via conflation in childhood + Hebbian neural learning)
COMPLEX METAPHORS (LOVE IS A JOURNEY, ARGUMENT IS WAR, THEORIES ARE BUILDINGS, ...)
    ↓ (via compositional combination of primary metaphors + cultural knowledge)
ABSTRACT THOUGHT (morality, time, causation, mathematics, emotion, politics, ...)
    ↓ (via metaphorical inference -- source domain logic maps to target domain reasoning)
LANGUAGE (metaphorical expressions as surface manifestations of conceptual metaphors)
```

### The Pattern of Patterns

What makes this framework especially powerful for pattern-oriented thinkers:

1. **A finite set generates an infinite range**: Roughly 8-12 image schemas and 20-30 primary metaphors generate hundreds of complex metaphors, which in turn structure all of abstract thought. This is a *combinatorial* system, analogous to how a finite number of phonemes generates an infinite number of words.

2. **The mapping is systematic, not arbitrary**: When LOVE IS A JOURNEY, *every* aspect of journeys systematically maps to aspects of love -- not randomly, but preserving the image-schematic structure (Invariance Principle). If you understand the source domain, you can *predict* the target domain structure.

3. **The same image schema recurs across multiple metaphors**: The CONTAINER schema structures STATES ARE CONTAINERS, CATEGORIES ARE CONTAINERS, THE VISUAL FIELD IS A CONTAINER, and THE MIND IS A CONTAINER. Learning one schema gives you leverage across many domains.

4. **Metaphorical entailments are inherited**: If ARGUMENT IS WAR, then arguments have attacks, defenses, strategies, winners, and losers -- not because anyone decided this, but because the mapping *automatically* carries the inferential structure of the source domain into the target domain.

5. **Cross-domain consistency**: The same primary metaphor (e.g., MORE IS UP) shows up consistently across many complex metaphors and many languages, because it is grounded in universal embodied experience. This creates a predictable, learnable pattern across otherwise unrelated domains.

6. **The system is hierarchical**: Image schemas → primary metaphors → complex metaphors → domain-specific reasoning. Each level is built from the level below, and understanding the lower levels gives you systematic access to the higher levels.

### Why This Matters for Language Learning

For someone learning language structure, CMT reveals that:
- Apparently arbitrary vocabulary ("grasping" a concept, "falling" in love, "rising" prices) is not arbitrary at all -- it reflects systematic conceptual mappings
- Idiomatic expressions that seem opaque become transparent once you identify the underlying conceptual metaphor
- Cross-linguistic comparison becomes tractable: different languages elaborate the same primary metaphors in culturally specific ways, but the underlying mappings are shared
- Grammar itself may be metaphorically structured: grammatical constructions can be understood as image-schematic patterns applied to linguistic form

## 10. Key Terms Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Conceptual metaphor** | A systematic mapping between a source domain (concrete, embodied) and a target domain (abstract), structuring how we think about the abstract domain |
| **Source domain** | The concrete, experientially grounded domain from which structure is mapped (e.g., JOURNEY, WAR, BUILDING) |
| **Target domain** | The abstract domain that is understood through the mapping (e.g., LOVE, ARGUMENT, THEORY) |
| **Primary metaphor** | An irreducible metaphorical mapping arising from universal sensorimotor correlations during childhood |
| **Complex metaphor** | A metaphor composed from multiple primary metaphors plus cultural knowledge |
| **Image schema** | A pre-linguistic, dynamic, schematic pattern arising from repeated sensorimotor experience (e.g., CONTAINER, PATH, FORCE) |
| **Conflation** | The early-childhood state in which source and target domain experiences are undifferentiated |
| **Differentiation** | The developmental process by which conflated domains are separated, leaving cross-domain neural connections |
| **Invariance Principle** | The constraint that metaphorical mappings preserve the image-schema structure of the source domain |
| **Unidirectionality** | The principle that metaphorical mapping goes from concrete to abstract, not the reverse |
| **Embodied realism** | The philosophical position that human concepts are shaped by embodiment but constrained by real physical structure |
| **Neural cascade** | The bidirectional spreading of neural activation from linguistic form through metaphor mapping circuits to meaning |
| **Grounded cognition** | The broader theoretical framework holding that cognition is grounded in perception, action, and bodily states |
| **Perceptual symbols** | Barsalou's theory that concepts are multimodal re-enactments of perceptual experience |
| **Ontological correspondences** | The specific entity-to-entity mappings within a conceptual metaphor |
| **Epistemic correspondences** | The knowledge/inference patterns that transfer from source to target domain via metaphorical mapping |
