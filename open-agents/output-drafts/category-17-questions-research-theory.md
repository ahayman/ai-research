# Category 17: The Pragmatics of Questions — Research & Theory

Questions have been addressed in prior categories (Category 1: Speech Acts, Category 2: Implicature, Category 4: Conversation Structure, Category 6: Hedging, Category 9: Presupposition), but this document treats questions as their own integrated pragmatic phenomenon, going deeper than any prior coverage.

## 1. QUESTIONS AS A PRAGMATIC PHENOMENON

### 1.1 The Core Complexity: Form ≠ Function

The central insight of question pragmatics is that **the syntactic form of a question (interrogative structure) does not reliably predict its pragmatic function**. This is the form-function mismatch that makes questions one of the most pragmatically complex phenomena in language.

Consider the range of mismatches:

**Interrogative form, non-question function:**
- "Can you close the door?" — syntactically a yes/no question about ability, pragmatically a request for action
- "Who cares?" — syntactically a wh-question seeking identification of a person, pragmatically a statement meaning "nobody cares"
- "How are you?" — syntactically a wh-question about someone's wellbeing, pragmatically a social greeting expecting a formulaic response
- "Isn't it obvious?" — syntactically a yes/no question, pragmatically a statement asserting that it IS obvious

**Non-interrogative form, question function:**
- "I was wondering if you're free tonight." — syntactically a declarative statement, pragmatically a question
- "You're leaving already?" — syntactically a declarative with rising intonation, pragmatically a question expressing surprise
- "So you're saying we should cancel." — syntactically a declarative, pragmatically a confirmation-seeking question

This mismatch means that to understand what a question is *doing*, you cannot rely on grammar alone. You must analyze context, intonation, shared knowledge, and social relationships.

### 1.2 Searle's Classification: Questions as Directives

In speech act theory, John Searle (1969, 1975) classified questions as a subtype of **directives** — speech acts that attempt to get the hearer to do something. Under this classification, a question is an attempt to get the hearer to provide information (a verbal response).

**The logic of this classification:**
- Directives include requests, commands, orders, invitations — all attempts to get the hearer to perform some action
- Questions are attempts to get the hearer to perform the action of *telling the speaker something*
- The "direction of fit" is world-to-words: the speaker wants the world (specifically, the hearer's behavior) to match the words
- The sincerity condition is that the speaker wants the information

**The debate about this classification:**

Some linguists argue this classification is inappropriate:
- An appropriate response to a question is an **assertion** (a statement), not an action. This makes questions fundamentally different from requests for physical action.
- Questions function to update the **common ground** (shared knowledge between participants), whereas directives like "pass the salt" do not involve knowledge updating in the same way.
- Questions create a specific kind of obligation — to provide information — that differs from the obligation to perform a physical action.

Neuroscientific evidence, however, has supported Searle's view. Research published in Neuropsychologia (Egorova et al., 2023) found physiological similarities between questions and other directives (requests to hand over objects) — both showed fast motor cortex activation, suggesting they share processing pathways in the brain.

An alternative view from **erotetic logic** (the formal logic of questions) treats questions as a distinct speech act category — neither assertive nor directive, but **erotetic** — with its own logic, felicity conditions, and response requirements.

**Practical resolution:** Whether questions are "really" directives or their own category matters less for pragmatic competence than understanding what any given question is actually doing. The key insight is that questions exert social pressure to respond, similar to how directives exert pressure to act.

### 1.3 The Speech Act of Questioning: Felicity Conditions

For a genuine information-seeking question to be felicitous (appropriate and well-formed as a speech act), several conditions should hold:

1. **Preparatory condition:** The speaker does not already know the answer (if the speaker does know, we're in display/exam question territory)
2. **Sincerity condition:** The speaker genuinely wants to know the answer
3. **Propositional content condition:** The question specifies what information is sought
4. **Essential condition:** The utterance counts as an attempt to elicit information from the hearer

When these conditions are systematically violated, we get the various non-standard question types: rhetorical questions violate the sincerity condition (the speaker doesn't genuinely want an answer), display questions violate the preparatory condition (the speaker already knows), phatic questions violate all of them (neither party treats the exchange as genuinely information-seeking).

### 1.4 Epistemic Dimensions: Knowledge States and Questions

Recent work in conversation analysis (Heritage 2012, Heritage & Raymond 2012) has revealed that questions are fundamentally about **epistemic asymmetry** — differences in knowledge between participants.

**The epistemic gradient:** Questions operate on a gradient from K- (less knowledgeable) to K+ (more knowledgeable). The questioner positions themselves as K- and the respondent as K+. However, different question types convey different positions on this gradient:

- **Wh-questions** ("Where is the library?") convey the steepest gradient — the speaker knows essentially nothing about the answer and positions themselves as deeply K-
- **Polar/yes-no interrogatives** ("Is the library on Main Street?") convey a shallower gradient — the speaker has a candidate answer and is asking for confirmation or disconfirmation
- **Tag questions** ("The library is on Main Street, isn't it?") convey an even shallower gradient — the speaker is fairly confident and merely seeking agreement
- **Declarative questions** ("The library is on Main Street?") claim a near-equal epistemic footing — the speaker essentially knows but is checking

This means that **the choice of question form itself communicates how much the speaker thinks they already know.** A speaker who asks "Where did you go?" signals less knowledge than one who asks "You went to the store?" about the same event.

### 1.5 The Question Under Discussion (QUD) Framework

In formal pragmatics, the **Question Under Discussion** (Roberts 1996, 2012) is a theoretical construct that explains how discourse coherence works. The QUD is the implicit or explicit question that participants in a conversation are collectively trying to answer at any given point.

Key principles:
- Every assertion in a conversation is relevant to the extent that it addresses the current QUD
- QUDs can be explicitly raised ("So what happened at work today?") or implicitly accommodated
- Conversations can have nested QUDs — sub-questions that must be answered to address the larger question
- What counts as an **adequate answer** depends on the QUD: the answer must be at least a partial answer to the question being addressed

This framework explains why some responses feel satisfying and others feel off-topic — responses that address the current QUD feel relevant; those that don't feel evasive, irrelevant, or confusing.

## 2. EXHAUSTIVE TAXONOMY OF QUESTION TYPES BY PRAGMATIC FUNCTION

### 2a. INFORMATION-SEEKING QUESTIONS (Genuine Requests for Information)

These are the "default" or "prototypical" questions — the type where all felicity conditions are met. The speaker genuinely does not know the answer and wants it.

#### Yes/No Questions (Polar Questions)
- **Form:** Auxiliary-subject inversion — "Is it raining?" "Did she call?" "Have you eaten?"
- **Function:** Seeks a binary answer (yes or no), though fuller answers are common
- **Pragmatic nuances:**
  - Positive polar questions ("Is she coming?") are relatively neutral in bias
  - Negative polar questions ("Isn't she coming?") carry an **epistemic bias** — they signal the speaker previously expected the positive answer (that she IS coming) and has encountered evidence to the contrary. The negativity expresses surprise at the possible negative answer.
  - The form of the question signals the speaker's expectations: "Is she coming?" is relatively neutral, while "She's coming, right?" signals strong expectation that yes.

#### Wh-Questions (Content Questions)
- **Form:** Interrogative pronouns (who, what, where, when, why, how) with subject-auxiliary inversion
- **Function:** Seeks specific information — the wh-word identifies what type of information is missing
- **Pragmatic nuances:**
  - Wh-questions carry an **existential presupposition**: "What did she buy?" presupposes she bought something
  - "Why" questions carry additional presuppositions: "Why did you leave?" presupposes both that you left and that there was a reason
  - "How" questions can seek different types of information: method ("How do you make risotto?"), degree ("How angry was she?"), manner ("How did she react?")
  - The scope of the wh-word determines what counts as an adequate answer — "Where?" requires a location, "When?" requires a time, etc.
  - "Why" and "how" questions typically invite longer, more explanatory answers than "who," "what," "where," or "when"

#### Alternative Questions
- **Form:** Two or more options connected by "or" — "Do you want coffee or tea?" "Should we go today or tomorrow?"
- **Function:** Constrains the answer space — the responder is expected to choose among the offered alternatives
- **Pragmatic nuances:**
  - Alternative questions carry an **exhaustivity presupposition** — the listed options are assumed to be exhaustive (though this can be challenged: "Actually, I'd like water")
  - Intonation distinguishes alternative questions from yes/no questions: alternative questions have rising intonation on all but the last option, then falling on the last ("Do you want ↗coffee or ↘tea?"). A yes/no question reading would have rising intonation at the end ("Do you want coffee or tea↗?")
  - Answering "yes" to an alternative question ("Coffee or tea?" "Yes") is technically infelicitous (though it happens humorously or evasively), as famously analyzed in linguistic pragmatics literature

### 2b. RHETORICAL QUESTIONS (No Answer Expected)

Rhetorical questions use interrogative syntax to make **assertions**, not to seek information. The answer is either obvious or irrelevant — the "question" is really a statement in disguise.

**Common forms:**
- "Who knows?" → meaning "Nobody knows"
- "Who cares?" → meaning "Nobody cares"
- "What's the point?" → meaning "There is no point"
- "How should I know?" → meaning "I don't know / I have no way of knowing"
- "Isn't it obvious?" → meaning "It IS obvious"
- "What did I tell you?" → meaning "I told you so" (not genuinely asking what was said)
- "Can you believe it?" → meaning "It's unbelievable" (not asking about your capacity for belief)
- "Why bother?" → meaning "It's not worth bothering"
- "What's the use?" → meaning "There is no use"
- "How many times do I have to tell you?" → meaning "I've told you many times; stop doing this"
- "Do bears live in the woods?" / "Is the Pope Catholic?" → meaning "Obviously yes" (sarcastic emphasis)

**How they work in Gricean terms:**
Rhetorical questions flout the **Maxim of Quality** (do not say what you believe to be false) — the speaker asks a question they already know the answer to, which is a kind of insincerity. But because the flouting is obvious to both parties, it generates an implicature: the "question" is understood as a forceful statement.

**Why speakers use rhetorical questions instead of statements:**
1. **Emphasis and intensity:** "Who cares about that?" is more forceful than "Nobody cares about that"
2. **Engagement:** Questions, even rhetorical ones, involve the listener more than statements do — they create a brief moment where the hearer mentally processes the "answer"
3. **Dramatic effect:** Particularly in speeches and arguments, rhetorical questions create momentum
4. **Deniability:** The speaker can claim they were "just asking" if challenged
5. **Emotional coloring:** Rhetorical questions often carry emotional weight (frustration, exasperation, irony) that statements do not

**How to identify rhetorical questions:**
- **Prosodic cues:** Rhetorical questions are typically **longer in duration** than information-seeking questions, have a **smaller pitch excursion**, and are more often realized with **non-modal voice quality** (breathy or glottalized voice). The terminal pitch in wh-rhetorical questions is almost exclusively low, whereas wh-information-seeking questions more often have a high or rising terminus.
- **Context:** The answer is obvious to both parties, or the speaker clearly already has a position
- **No pause for response:** Speakers typically don't wait for an answer after a rhetorical question — they continue speaking
- **Discourse markers:** Words like "after all" or "I mean" near a question often signal rhetorical intent
- **Content:** Questions with extreme formulations ("How many times do I have to...?") or that invoke common knowledge ("Is the sky blue?") are almost always rhetorical

### 2c. EXAM/DISPLAY QUESTIONS (Asker Already Knows the Answer)

In display questions, the speaker already knows the answer and is testing whether the hearer knows it. This violates the normal preparatory condition for questions (that the speaker doesn't know), creating a distinctive pragmatic situation.

**Key distinction from information-seeking questions:**
- In genuine questions, the speaker is K- and the hearer is K+
- In display questions, the speaker is K+ but is testing whether the hearer is also K+
- The knowledge asymmetry is reversed: the questioner has MORE knowledge, not less

**Contexts where display questions are normal and expected:**

1. **Classroom settings:** "What is the capital of France?" — The teacher knows; the purpose is to test the student's knowledge and elicit practice.
2. **Job interviews:** "How would you handle a difficult customer?" — The interviewer isn't seeking information about customer handling; they're evaluating the candidate's thinking and communication.
3. **Medical consultations:** "Can you tell me what medications you're currently taking?" — The doctor may already have records; the question tests the patient's awareness and compliance.
4. **Quiz shows, trivia games, standardized tests**

**The IRF/IRE Pattern (Sinclair & Coulthard 1975; Mehan 1979):**

Classroom discourse has a highly structured three-turn pattern:

1. **Initiation (I):** Teacher asks a display question — "What year did World War II end?"
2. **Response (R):** Student provides an answer — "1945."
3. **Feedback/Follow-up (F)** or **Evaluation (E):** Teacher evaluates — "That's right!" or "Not quite. Can anyone else help?"

This IRF/IRE structure is **the dominant pattern in educational discourse worldwide.** It differs fundamentally from everyday conversation:
- In conversation, the questioner doesn't evaluate the answer (evaluating someone's answer to a genuine question would be bizarre and condescending)
- In classroom discourse, evaluation is expected and even desired — students want to know if they got it right
- The pattern creates a distinctive power dynamic: the teacher controls topic, turn allocation, and evaluation

**Display vs. Referential Questions in Education:**

Researchers distinguish between:
- **Display questions:** The teacher knows the answer; purpose is to test recall or demonstrate knowledge. These tend to elicit short, factual responses. ("What is photosynthesis?")
- **Referential questions:** The teacher does not know the answer; purpose is to elicit genuine communication. These tend to elicit longer, more complex responses. ("What do you think about climate change?")

Research shows that referential questions elicit responses with significantly more features characteristic of genuine communication — longer turns, more complex syntax, more personal engagement. Display questions tend to produce minimal, correct-answer-focused responses.

**Socratic Questioning:**

A specialized use of display-adjacent questions in education is **Socratic questioning** — a pedagogical method using sequences of questions to lead students to discover answers through their own reasoning. Unlike simple display questions, Socratic questions may not have a single "right" answer and are designed to:
- Probe assumptions: "What are you assuming when you say that?"
- Clarify thinking: "What exactly do you mean by that?"
- Explore evidence: "What evidence supports that claim?"
- Consider alternatives: "Could there be another explanation?"
- Examine implications: "If that's true, then what follows?"

The pragmatic distinction: Socratic questions occupy a space between display questions (the teacher has a direction in mind) and genuine questions (the teacher doesn't know exactly what the student will say). Research identifies prosodic cues that distinguish pedagogical from information-seeking questions — contrastive stress or particular pitch on key words can signal the teacher's pedagogical intent.

### 2d. INDIRECT REQUESTS DISGUISED AS QUESTIONS

Questions that function as requests for action rather than information. The interrogative form is used as a politeness strategy.

**Common patterns:**
- "Can you [action]?" — "Can you close the door?" (= Please close the door)
- "Could you [action]?" — "Could you pass me that?" (= Please pass me that)
- "Would you mind [action]?" — "Would you mind being quiet?" (= Please be quiet)
- "Would you [action]?" — "Would you move your bag?" (= Please move your bag)
- "Will you [action]?" — "Will you help me with this?" (= Please help me)

**Why questions instead of direct imperatives:**
Questions as requests are a core politeness strategy (Brown & Levinson 1987). By phrasing a request as a question:
- The speaker preserves the hearer's **negative face** (freedom from imposition) by ostensibly giving them the option to refuse
- The request appears to ask about the hearer's ability or willingness, not to command
- The **conventionalization** of these forms means they are processed directly as requests — native speakers don't actually compute the literal "ability" meaning of "can you"

**The whimperative (Sadock 1970):**
The term **whimperative** (a blend of "wh-" and "imperative") describes interrogative forms that function as directives. These include:
- "Why don't you sit down?" (= Please sit down / I suggest you sit down)
- "How about we take a break?" (= Let's take a break)
- "Why not try the blue one?" (= I suggest the blue one)

These are not genuine questions about reasons or methods — they are suggestions or mild directives wearing question-form clothing.

**Processing:**
Research on conventionalized indirect requests shows that "Can you VP?" utterances are processed as requests without increasing processing costs — pragmatic routines enable "short-circuited inferencing" that bypasses the literal meaning entirely. This means that the indirect request reading is the DEFAULT, not a secondary computation from the literal meaning.

**Cross-reference:** See Category 1 (Speech Acts) for detailed treatment of indirect speech acts, and Category 5 (Politeness) for the face-saving motivation behind indirect requests.

### 2e. PHATIC QUESTIONS (Social Ritual, Not Information-Seeking)

Phatic questions are questions in form but social rituals in function. They serve to establish, maintain, or reinforce social bonds rather than to exchange information.

**Classic examples:**
- "How are you?" (expected response: "Fine, thanks. You?" — NOT an honest medical report)
- "What's up?" (expected response: "Not much" or "Hey")
- "How's it going?" (expected response: "Good, you?")
- "How have you been?" (expected response: brief positive summary)
- "What do you know?" (archaic greeting, not an epistemological inquiry)

**Key characteristics:**
- **Formulaic:** Both question and answer follow predictable scripts
- **No informative value:** As Malinowski (1923) described in coining the term "phatic communion," these expressions serve purely social functions and carry no informative content
- **Truthful reply not required:** The response "Fine" does not need to be accurate — it's a social token, not a truth claim
- **Reciprocity expected:** The ritual typically requires the responder to return the question ("And you?")
- **Context-dependent:** "How are you?" asked by a doctor in a medical appointment IS information-seeking; the same words from a cashier are phatic. The pragmatic function must be inferred from context and intonation.

**Why they exist:**
Phatic questions signal willingness to observe conventional expectations for politeness and social engagement. They acknowledge the other person's presence and establish a cooperative social frame before any substantive exchange begins.

**The autistic experience with phatic questions:**
A well-documented challenge: autistic individuals may take "How are you?" as a genuine information-seeking question and provide an honest, detailed answer about their current state — which violates the phatic script and can create social awkwardness. The key insight is that "How are you?" has TWO possible pragmatic functions (phatic and genuine), and the context determines which one applies. Distinguishing between them requires reading the social situation (casual encounter = phatic; close friend noticing you look upset = possibly genuine).

**Cross-reference:** See Category 19 (Phatic Communication) for comprehensive treatment.

### 2f. LOADED QUESTIONS (Smuggling Presuppositions)

A loaded question contains a **presupposition that has not been established or agreed upon**, trapping the respondent into accepting the presupposition regardless of how they answer.

**Classic example:**
- "When did you stop beating your wife?"
  - Presupposes: You WERE beating your wife (and have stopped)
  - If you answer "Last Tuesday" — you've accepted you were beating your wife
  - If you answer "I didn't stop" — you've accepted you are currently beating your wife
  - The only escape is to **reject the presupposition itself**: "I have never beaten my wife"

**Other examples:**
- "Why do you always ignore my suggestions?" (presupposes: you always ignore my suggestions)
- "How long have you been cheating on the exam?" (presupposes: you have been cheating)
- "When are you going to grow up?" (presupposes: you are currently immature)
- "Why does this company treat its employees so badly?" (presupposes: the company treats employees badly)

**The complex question fallacy:**
In logic, the fallacy of the complex question (plurium interrogationum) occurs when a question presupposes something that has not been proven or accepted. The fallacy is **context-dependent** — the same question can be legitimate in one context and fallacious in another. "When did you stop taking the medication?" is perfectly legitimate if the doctor knows the patient was prescribed medication; it's loaded if no prior medication use has been established.

**Why loaded questions are pragmatically powerful:**
- **Answering concedes:** Any direct answer carries the question's presuppositions
- **Rejecting is costly:** To challenge the presupposition, you must interrupt the question-answer flow and explicitly deny the premise, which takes effort and can seem defensive
- **In institutional contexts** (courtrooms, interviews), loaded questions can be strategically deployed to frame the respondent

**Cross-reference:** See Category 9 (Presupposition) for comprehensive treatment of presuppositional mechanisms.

### 2g. TAG QUESTIONS

Tag questions append a short interrogative "tag" to a declarative statement, creating a hybrid form that partly asserts and partly questions.

**Standard English tag structure:**
The tag uses the opposite polarity from the main clause and matches its auxiliary:
- Positive statement → negative tag: "It's cold, **isn't it**?"
- Negative statement → positive tag: "You don't like fish, **do you**?"
- "She can swim, **can't she**?"
- "They haven't left, **have they**?"

**Invariant tags:**
Some tags don't follow the polarity-reversal rule:
- "Right?" — "We're meeting at 3, **right**?"
- "Yeah?" — "You're coming tonight, **yeah**?"
- "No?" — "That's the one, **no**?"
- "Eh?" (Canadian/British) — "Good weather, **eh**?"
- "Innit?" (British informal) — generalized invariant tag used regardless of subject or auxiliary

**The crucial role of intonation:**

Tag questions can have **rising** or **falling** intonation on the tag, and this dramatically changes their pragmatic function:

| Intonation | Speaker's Stance | Function | Example (with context) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Falling** ↘ | Speaker is fairly certain; expects agreement | Seeking confirmation of what they already believe; making a statement with soft invitation to agree | "Nice day, isn't it↘" (the speaker thinks it IS a nice day) |
| **Rising** ↗ | Speaker is genuinely uncertain | Asking a real question; seeking new information | "The meeting is at 3, isn't it↗?" (the speaker isn't sure) |

Research on natural discourse shows that **68% of tag questions feature falling intonation**, with only **21% rising** — meaning most tag questions in practice are more assertion than question.

**Pragmatic functions of tag questions:**

1. **Seeking confirmation:** "You're Aaron, aren't you?" — Checking a belief
2. **Softening assertions:** "That was a bit rude, wasn't it?" — Making a judgment less confrontational
3. **Seeking agreement/alignment:** "Great movie, wasn't it?" — Inviting solidarity
4. **Hedging:** "We should probably leave, shouldn't we?" — Reducing commitment to the proposition (Category 6 connection)
5. **Facilitating conversation:** "Nice weather, isn't it?" — Opening or maintaining social contact (Category 19 connection)
6. **Coercing agreement:** "You agree with me, don't you?" (with falling intonation) — Social pressure to agree

**Tag questions as epistemic markers:**
In Heritage's framework, tag questions claim a near-K+ position — the speaker already mostly knows the answer (or believes they do) and is merely seeking ratification. This is a shallower epistemic gradient than a full interrogative, positioning the speaker as knowledgeable but slightly uncertain.

### 2h. LEADING QUESTIONS

Leading questions are structured to suggest, imply, or predispose the hearer toward a particular answer. Unlike loaded questions (which smuggle presuppositions), leading questions guide the respondent toward the questioner's preferred answer through framing and suggestion.

**Common forms:**
- "Don't you think we should...?" → suggests the answer is "yes, we should"
- "Wouldn't it be better to...?" → suggests it WOULD be better
- "Isn't it true that...?" → frames the proposition as true
- "Surely you agree that...?" → applies social pressure to agree
- "You saw the defendant leave, didn't you?" → suggests this is what happened

**Leading questions vs. neutral questions:**
| Leading | Neutral |
|---|---|
| "Don't you think the proposal is too expensive?" | "What do you think about the proposal's cost?" |
| "Wasn't the car going very fast?" | "How fast was the car going?" |
| "You enjoyed the meal, didn't you?" | "How was the meal?" |

**Legal context:**
Leading questions are explicitly regulated in courtroom procedure:
- **Direct examination** (questioning your own witness): leading questions are generally **prohibited** — the attorney should let the witness tell their own story
- **Cross-examination** (questioning the opposing side's witness): leading questions are **permitted and standard** — the attorney uses them to control the testimony and challenge the witness's account

Research demonstrates that leading questions can **significantly reduce the accuracy of testimony** (Loftus 1975 and subsequent work). Questions like "How fast were the cars going when they *smashed* into each other?" elicit higher speed estimates than "How fast were the cars going when they *hit* each other?" — the verb choice leads the witness.

**Why leading questions work:**
- They provide a frame that the respondent must actively resist to give a different answer
- Disagreeing with a leading question requires more cognitive and social effort than agreeing
- In power-asymmetric contexts (courtroom, interview), the pressure to go along is amplified

### 2i. ECHO QUESTIONS

Echo questions repeat all or part of a previous utterance with questioning intonation, signaling that something about the original utterance requires attention.

**How they work:**
- Speaker A: "I'm moving to Belize."
- Speaker B: "You're moving to **Belize**?" (repeating with rising intonation and emphasis on the surprising element)

Or:
- Speaker A: "The meeting is at 4 AM."
- Speaker B: "4 **AM**?"

**Types of echo questions:**

1. **Full echo:** Repeats the entire utterance — "You're moving to Belize?"
2. **Partial echo:** Repeats only the surprising/unclear element — "Belize?"
3. **Wh-echo:** Replaces the problematic element with a wh-word — "You're moving WHERE?"

**Pragmatic functions:**

| Function | Example | What it signals |
|---|---|---|
| **Surprise/disbelief** | A: "I won the lottery." B: "You WON the lottery?" | The information is unexpected and possibly hard to believe |
| **Repair initiation** | A: "Put it on the frangle." B: "The what?" / "The frangle?" | The hearer didn't hear or understand a word |
| **Seeking confirmation** | A: "We leave at six." B: "Six?" | The hearer wants to verify they heard correctly |
| **Challenge/incredulity** | A: "I think we should skip the testing phase." B: "Skip the TESTING phase?" | The hearer finds the proposition problematic |
| **Processing time** | A: "We need to reorganize the entire department by Friday." B: "By Friday?" | The hearer needs a moment to absorb the information |

**Prosodic features:**
Echo questions that express surprise or disbelief typically have **higher pitch** and **greater pitch excursion** than those used simply for confirmation. The focus/emphasis falls on the element that is surprising or unclear, using contrastive stress.

**Relationship to repair:**
In conversation analysis, echo questions function as a form of **other-initiated repair** — the hearer signals a problem with the prior turn and invites the speaker to address it. Partial repetitions of the preceding turn can implement different social actions: registering the previous turn, doing confirmation, resisting presuppositions, showing surprise, and initiating repair.

### 2j. SELF-ADDRESSED QUESTIONS

Questions directed at oneself, either aloud or internally, that are not intended to elicit a response from others.

**Examples:**
- "Now where did I put my keys?" (thinking aloud while searching)
- "What was I saying?" (may or may not invite others to help)
- "Why did I do that?" (self-reproach, not seeking an answer)
- "What should I have for lunch?" (deliberating)
- "Let's see, what's next on the list?" (self-organizing)

**Pragmatic analysis:**
Self-addressed questions occupy a unique pragmatic space because they lack an addressee in the normal sense. Research on self-talk (Langland-Hassan 2018, Morin 2018) shows:

- Self-addressed questions serve a **cognitive function** — they put an issue on the speaker's mental agenda and create an urge to resolve it
- "Asking, telling, promising, and reminding are originally social acts, but when addressed not to others but oneself, they come to function as modes of thinking: wondering, making up one's mind, motivating oneself"
- Speaking self-addressed questions **aloud** increases their dialogical quality and helps activate theory of mind — the speaker constructs an internal addressee

**Social implications when spoken aloud:**
- In some contexts, others may treat a self-addressed question as open for response: "Where did I put my keys?" may prompt a helpful "I saw them on the counter"
- The line between self-addressed and other-addressed can be blurry — "What should we do?" could be thinking aloud or soliciting input
- Social norms about self-talk vary by context — talking to yourself is more accepted when the cognitive task is visible (looking for something, working on a problem) than when it appears unmotivated

### 2k. HYPOTHETICAL QUESTIONS

Questions that explore imagined scenarios, possibilities, or counterfactual situations.

**Common forms:**
- "What would you do if...?" — "What would you do if you won a million dollars?"
- "What if...?" — "What if we tried a different approach?"
- "Suppose...?" — "Suppose the client says no — then what?"
- "Imagine if...?" — "Imagine if everyone did that — what would happen?"
- "How would things be different if...?" — "How would things be different if we had started earlier?"

**Pragmatic functions:**

1. **Exploring possibilities:** "What if we rearranged the furniture?" — testing an idea before committing to it
2. **Making indirect suggestions:** "What if you talked to her about it?" — suggesting action without directly advising (preserving the hearer's autonomy)
3. **Philosophical/ethical exploration:** "What would you do if you found a wallet with $1,000?" — probing values and reasoning
4. **Risk assessment:** "What happens if the server goes down?" — identifying vulnerabilities
5. **Thought experiments:** "What if gravity worked in reverse?" — exploring concepts
6. **Face-saving indirectness:** "What if we postponed the meeting?" is less face-threatening than "I think we should postpone the meeting" because it merely raises the possibility rather than asserting a position

**Why hypothetical questions are pragmatically distinct:**
- They don't seek factual information (nobody knows the answer to a counterfactual)
- They invite **collaborative reasoning** rather than information transfer
- The "question" is really a framework for discussion, not a genuine interrogative
- In pedagogy, hypothetical questions promote "creative thinking, problem solving, evaluation and other higher order thinking skills"

## 3. HOW TO TELL WHAT TYPE OF QUESTION YOU'RE FACING

This is the central practical challenge for question pragmatics, and it's where autistic individuals often face the greatest difficulty. The same surface form ("Can you...?") can be an information-seeking question, an indirect request, a rhetorical question, or even a display question, depending on context. Here is a systematic guide to the cues that distinguish question types.

### 3.1 Context Cues

**Who is asking?**
- A teacher asking a student → likely display/exam question
- A friend in casual conversation → likely information-seeking or phatic
- A stranger on the street → likely information-seeking
- A boss in a meeting → could be display, rhetorical, or genuine, depending on other cues
- A doctor in a consultation → likely information-seeking (even if formulaic)

**What does the speaker likely already know?**
- If the answer is obvious to both parties → rhetorical
- If the speaker demonstrably knows (e.g., teacher with textbook) → display
- If the speaker couldn't possibly know → genuine information-seeking
- If the speaker has partial knowledge → confirmation-seeking (tag/declarative question)

**What is the social context?**
- Greeting sequence → phatic ("How are you?")
- Formal examination → display
- Casual social gathering → genuine or phatic
- Argument/conflict → possibly rhetorical or leading
- Institutional setting (court, clinic, school) → the institution's conventions apply

**What action would make sense as a response?**
- "Can you pass the salt?" at dinner → physical action (indirect request)
- "Can you speak Mandarin?" in conversation → verbal answer (genuine question)
- "Can you believe this weather?" → emotional alignment (rhetorical)

### 3.2 Prosodic Cues (Intonation, Stress, Rhythm)

**Rising vs. falling terminal pitch:**
- Rising terminal pitch → more likely genuine question (seeking information)
- Falling terminal pitch → more likely assertion (rhetorical question, statement)
- Tag questions: rising = genuine uncertainty; falling = seeking agreement

**Duration and tempo:**
- Rhetorical questions tend to be **slower and longer in duration** than genuine questions
- Genuine information-seeking questions tend to be more brisk and efficient

**Voice quality:**
- Rhetorical questions more often have **non-modal voice quality** (breathy, creaky) than genuine questions
- Emotional coloring (sarcastic, exasperated, weary) signals rhetorical or loaded intent

**Stress patterns:**
- Heavy stress on a particular word often signals echo questions or incredulity: "You're moving to **BELIZE**?"
- Contrastive stress can distinguish pedagogical from information-seeking questions

**Pause behavior:**
- If the speaker pauses and waits → more likely expecting an answer (genuine, display)
- If the speaker continues without pausing → more likely rhetorical

### 3.3 Lexical Cues (Specific Words and Phrases)

| Lexical marker | Likely question type | Example |
|---|---|---|
| "Can you..." / "Could you..." / "Would you mind..." | Indirect request | "Could you open the window?" |
| "Don't you think..." / "Isn't it true that..." / "Wouldn't it be better..." | Leading question | "Don't you think we should leave?" |
| "Who cares?" / "Who knows?" / "What's the point?" | Rhetorical | "Who cares about that?" |
| "...right?" / "...isn't it?" / "...don't you think?" | Tag/confirmation | "That's correct, right?" |
| "What if..." / "Suppose..." / "Imagine..." | Hypothetical | "What if we tried plan B?" |
| "How are you?" / "What's up?" | Phatic | "Hey, how's it going?" |
| "When did you stop..." / "Why do you always..." | Loaded | "When did you stop trying?" |
| "So you're saying..." / "You mean..." | Echo/confirmation | "So you're saying we cancel?" |

### 3.4 Sequential Cues (What Came Before)

**After an assertion:** A question following someone's statement is likely an echo question, challenge, or confirmation request
- A: "I think we should invest in crypto." B: "You think we should invest in CRYPTO?"

**After a greeting:** Questions in greeting sequences are phatic
- A: "Hey!" B: "Hey, how are you?"

**In an ongoing topic:** Questions that continue the current topic are likely genuine or leading; questions that introduce new topics may be transitional

**After an answer:** A follow-up question after an answer is likely deepening the inquiry or challenging the answer

**In institutional sequences:** Questions that follow the expected institutional pattern (e.g., teacher question after student answer = IRF evaluation setup) have predictable types

### 3.5 The "Reasonable Interpretation" Heuristic

When multiple cues conflict or are ambiguous, apply this heuristic:

1. **What interpretation makes this question make sense in context?** If someone at a dinner table says "Can you pass the salt?", the interpretation that makes sense is a request (they want the salt), not a question about your physical ability.

2. **What response would the speaker likely find appropriate?** If "Fine, thanks" would satisfy the speaker, the question was probably phatic. If a detailed answer is expected, it's probably genuine.

3. **Would answering literally produce a bizarre or unhelpful response?** If answering "Can you close the door?" with "Yes, I can" (without closing it) would be strange, the question is likely an indirect request.

## 4. THE PRAGMATICS OF ANSWERING

### 4.1 What Counts as an Adequate Answer

An adequate answer is one that addresses the Question Under Discussion (QUD) with appropriate informativeness. This connects directly to Grice's Maxim of Quantity: make your contribution as informative as is required for the current purposes of the exchange, and do not make it more informative than is required.

**Types of responses to questions:**

| Response type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct answer** | Precisely addresses what was asked | Q: "What time is it?" A: "3:15." |
| **Partial answer** | Addresses part of what was asked | Q: "When and where is the meeting?" A: "It's at 3." (location omitted) |
| **Over-informative answer** | Provides more than was asked | Q: "What time is it?" A: "It's 3:15, and by the way the meeting has been moved to Room 204." |
| **Under-informative answer** | Provides less than needed | Q: "How many children does John have?" A: "John has between three and five children." |
| **Indirect answer** | Addresses the question implicitly | Q: "Are you coming to the party?" A: "I have to work late." (implicature: no) |
| **Non-answer** | Changes the subject or deflects | Q: "Did you finish the report?" A: "How about that game last night?" |

### 4.2 Over-Answering

**When it's helpful:**
- Providing relevant information the asker didn't know they needed: Q: "Where is the library?" A: "It's on Main Street, but it closes at 5 today because of the holiday." (the closing time is bonus useful information)
- Anticipating follow-up questions: Q: "Is the train on time?" A: "Yes, it's on time and arriving on platform 3." (saves the likely next question)

**When it's problematic:**
- Providing irrelevant excess information: Q: "What time is it?" A: "It's 3:15. I got this watch in Switzerland. My grandmother gave it to me..." (violates Maxim of Relation)
- Providing information that creates awkwardness: Q: "How are you?" A: "Well, my marriage is falling apart, I haven't slept in days, and I think I'm coming down with something..." (violates the phatic convention)
- In institutional contexts (courtroom testimony), over-answering can introduce inadmissible information

**Autistic relevance:** Over-answering is a commonly reported pattern in autistic communication. The challenge is calibrating informativeness to the QUD — providing what's needed without providing everything you know about the topic. The key question is always: *what does the asker actually need to know right now?*

### 4.3 Under-Answering

When a speaker provides less information than the question requires, the gap itself generates implicature (Category 2 connection).

**Examples of meaningful under-answering:**
- Q: "Did you eat the cookies?" A: "I ate **some** of them." → Implicature: I didn't eat all of them (the scalar "some" implying "not all")
- Q: "How was the date?" A: "The food was good." → Implicature: The rest of the date was NOT good (if the whole date were good, the answer would be broader)
- Q: "Do you like my new haircut?" A: "It's... interesting." → Implicature: I don't like it but don't want to say so (hedging as face-saving)

**Strategic under-answering:**
- **Face-saving:** Providing less than requested to avoid saying something negative: Q: "What did you think of my presentation?" A: "Your slides were very clear." (avoiding commenting on content or delivery)
- **Evasion:** Deliberately providing an incomplete answer to avoid revealing information
- **Cultural norms:** In some cultures, speakers flout the maxim of quantity due to face concerns, preferring incomplete answers to direct ones that might cause discomfort

### 4.4 Non-Answers and Evasion

Non-answers are responses that fail to address the question at all. They can be inadvertent (the respondent misunderstood the question) or strategic (the respondent doesn't want to answer).

**Common evasion strategies:**

1. **Answering a different question:** Q: "Will you raise taxes?" A: "What I can tell you is that we're committed to economic growth." — The politician addresses the topic area but not the specific question.

2. **Attacking the question:** Q: "Why did the project fail?" A: "I reject the premise of your question. The project didn't fail." — Challenging the question itself.

3. **Attacking the questioner:** Q: "Can you explain these discrepancies?" A: "I think you have an agenda here." — Shifting focus to the questioner's motives.

4. **Providing a pre-planned message:** Regardless of what's asked, delivering a prepared talking point — "What I want to say to the American people is..."

5. **Meta-commenting:** Talking about the question rather than answering it — "That's a really good question" or "I'm glad you asked that" followed by non-answer.

6. **Topic shift:** Moving to a related but different subject — Q: "What about your company's environmental record?" A: "Let me tell you about our community engagement programs."

**Research on political evasion:**
Communication researcher Peter Bull has identified at least **35 techniques** that politicians use to evade questions. Studies of British political interviews found that politicians like Margaret Thatcher and Neil Kinnock provided direct answers to fewer than 40% of questions posed. The most common technique is "attacking the question" — reframing or challenging the question itself.

**Why non-answers often go unnoticed:**
Audiences frequently don't notice evasion because the answer is **on-topic** even if it doesn't address the specific question. If asked "Will you raise taxes?" and the politician talks about economic policy, the topic match creates a sense of responsiveness even though the specific question was dodged.

### 4.5 Preference Organization of Answers

In conversation analysis, answers to questions are organized by **preference structure** — certain answers are "preferred" (socially expected, structurally simpler) and others are "dispreferred" (socially problematic, structurally more complex).

**Key principle:** "Preferred" does not mean "what the speaker wants to hear" — it means "structurally and socially unmarked." A preferred answer is delivered quickly and directly; a dispreferred answer is delayed, hedged, and accompanied by accounts (explanations).

**For yes/no questions:**

| Question type | Preferred answer | Dispreferred answer |
|---|---|---|
| Invitation: "Want to come to dinner?" | Acceptance: "Sure, I'd love to!" | Refusal: "Um... well... I'd like to, but... I actually have plans... sorry..." |
| Request: "Can you help me move?" | Agreement: "Of course!" | Refusal: "Uh... this weekend? The thing is..." |
| Offer: "Would you like some coffee?" | Acceptance: "Yes, please!" | Refusal: "Oh, no, I'm fine, but thanks..." |
| Assessment: "Wasn't that a great movie?" | Agreement: "Yeah, it was fantastic!" | Disagreement: "Well... I don't know... parts of it were good, I guess..." |

**Structural markers of dispreferred answers:**
1. **Delay:** Pause before responding, or filled pauses ("um," "uh," "well")
2. **Prefaces:** "Well...", "The thing is...", "I don't know..."
3. **Hedging:** Qualified language rather than direct response
4. **Accounts:** Explanations for why the dispreferred answer is being given ("I'd love to, but I have to work")
5. **Mitigation:** Softening the bad news ("It's not bad, it's just...")
6. **Token agreement before disagreement:** "Yeah, but..." or "I see what you mean, however..."

**Why this matters for understanding questions:**
When you hear someone begin their answer with "Well..." followed by a pause, you can often predict that a dispreferred answer (refusal, disagreement, bad news) is coming. These structural cues are consistent across English and many other languages. Delays and hedges signal social trouble — the speaker is navigating a response that they know will be unwelcome.

**For wh-questions:**
Preference is less about yes/no and more about whether the answer provides what was sought or reveals a problem:
- Preferred: The answer resolves the question — Q: "Where's the meeting?" A: "Room 204."
- Dispreferred: The answer reveals inability to help — Q: "Where's the meeting?" A: "Um... I'm not sure actually... I think maybe... sorry, I really don't know."

## 5. QUESTIONS AND POWER

### 5.1 Question-Asking as a Power Mechanism

The right to ask questions is intimately connected to social power. In many institutional contexts, the person who asks questions exercises significant control over the interaction:

**Control dimensions:**
1. **Topic control:** The questioner determines what is discussed — the respondent must address the questioner's topic, not their own
2. **Turn allocation:** Questions create an obligation to respond, effectively giving the questioner control over who speaks next
3. **Response constraints:** The question type constrains what counts as an acceptable response — a yes/no question demands a binary answer, a wh-question demands a specific type of information
4. **Sequential control:** The questioner controls the trajectory of the conversation through follow-up questions
5. **Evaluation rights:** In many institutional contexts (classroom, interview), the questioner also has the right to evaluate the answer

### 5.2 Institutional Questioning and Asymmetry

**Doctor-patient interactions:**
Research identifies pronounced power asymmetry within medical discourse. Doctors maintain exclusive control over conversational flow through interrogatives to initiate exchanges, dictate topics, and limit patient contributions, creating rigid question-and-answer sequences. Doctors exercise control through exclusive rights to turn-taking and topic control, primarily achieved through frequent use of interrogative sentences, with power further manifested through directive speech acts and technical jargon. However, research finds that **greater physician dominance predicts lower patient satisfaction** — patients prefer doctors who give them space to raise their own topics and questions.

**Police interrogation:**
In police interviewing, institutional status, discursive roles assigned by context, and relative knowledge are significant factors affecting discourse dynamics, with power and control constantly under negotiation. The interrogator asks questions; the suspect answers — but the interrogator's questions are strategically designed to elicit particular responses, confessions, or inconsistencies.

**Courtroom examination:**
Attorneys use questions to control witnesses. Leading questions in cross-examination are essentially assertions disguised as questions — the attorney states what happened and the witness merely confirms. As Fairclough notes, power is exercised through lexical choice and syntactic structure, particularly through topic control and questions in "unequal encounters."

**Classroom interaction:**
The teacher's question-asking dominance defines classroom discourse. Teachers ask the vast majority of questions; students answer. This creates the IRF/IRE pattern where teachers initiate, students respond, and teachers evaluate — a cycle that keeps control firmly with the teacher.

**Job interviews:**
The interviewer asks; the candidate answers. The interviewer controls topics, evaluates responses, and determines the pace. The candidate's attempts to ask questions are restricted to designated moments ("Do you have any questions for us?").

### 5.3 Who Gets to Ask Questions

**The asymmetry of questioning rights:**
In everyday conversation, all participants have roughly equal rights to ask questions. But in institutional contexts, questioning rights are often restricted:

- **Courtroom:** Only attorneys and the judge ask questions; witnesses answer. A witness who asks questions of the attorney violates the institutional frame.
- **Classroom:** Teachers ask the majority of questions. Students can ask questions, but typically only when called upon or during designated Q&A periods.
- **Medical consultation:** Doctors ask most questions. Patients who ask too many questions may be perceived as "difficult" (though this norm is changing).
- **Press conferences:** Journalists ask questions; officials answer. The official may determine who gets to ask (calling on specific reporters).
- **Police interrogation:** Interrogators ask; suspects answer. Suspects who attempt to reverse this ("Why are you asking me that?") are typically redirected.

**Subverting the asymmetry:**
Answering a question with a question is a powerful pragmatic move precisely because it challenges the questioner's assumed right to ask:
- Q: "Where were you last night?" A: "Why do you want to know?" — Challenges the questioner's entitlement to the information
- Q: "How much did you pay for that?" A: "Why do you ask?" — Redirects the power dynamic
- Q: "Don't you think you should...?" A: "Is that really your concern?" — Challenges the questioner's standing

### 5.4 Questions and Face

Questions are inherently face-threatening in certain configurations:

**Threats to the hearer's face:**
- Questions that expose ignorance: "Don't you know that?" (threatens positive face)
- Questions that demand private information: "How much do you earn?" (threatens negative face — freedom from imposition)
- Questions that challenge competence: "Why did you do it that way?" (can threaten positive face)
- Loaded questions: "When did you stop..." (threatens face by presupposing wrongdoing)

**Threats to the questioner's face:**
- Admitting ignorance through questions: "This might be a stupid question, but..." (prefacing acknowledges the face-threat of not knowing)
- Questions that might be perceived as intrusive: "I hope you don't mind my asking, but..." (hedging acknowledges potential face-threat)
- Questions whose answers might be embarrassing: The questioner risks hearing something they don't want to hear

**Mitigating face-threats in questions:**
Speakers use numerous strategies to soften face-threatening questions:
- **Hedges:** "I was wondering if maybe..."
- **Disclaimers:** "This might be a stupid question, but..."
- **Indirectness:** "Do you happen to know...?" instead of "Tell me..."
- **Account-giving:** "I only ask because..."
- **Permission-seeking:** "Can I ask you something?" as a pre-question

## 6. QUESTIONS AS A REPAIR MECHANISM

Questions play a crucial role in **conversational repair** — the process of fixing problems of hearing, speaking, or understanding in conversation.

### 6.1 Open Class Repair Initiators

When you don't hear or understand what someone said, the most common repair strategy is a question-form repair initiator:

- **"What?"** / **"Huh?"** / **"Sorry?"** / **"Pardon?"** — These are "open class" repair initiators because they don't specify what the problem was. They signal: "I need you to say that again."
- These are delivered with questioning intonation and function as questions even though they have minimal lexical content

### 6.2 Specific Repair Initiators

More specific repair questions target the particular trouble source:
- **Wh-questions:** "You're meeting WHO?" / "You went WHERE?" — Asks the speaker to repeat/clarify a specific element
- **Partial repeats:** "At FOUR?" / "In Belize?" — Repeats the problematic element with questioning intonation
- **Candidate understandings:** "You mean the one on Main Street?" — Offers an interpretation for confirmation

### 6.3 Functions Beyond Repair

Repair-format questions can serve functions beyond fixing hearing/understanding problems:
- **Displaying surprise:** "You're moving to BELIZE?" — The hearer heard perfectly well but is expressing shock
- **Challenging:** "You said WHAT to her?" — The hearer understood but finds the action problematic
- **Prefacing disagreement:** Asking for clarification as a prelude to disagreeing creates space for the original speaker to revise before disagreement becomes explicit

## 8. SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS FOR AUTISTIC INDIVIDUALS

### 8.1 Common Challenges with Questions

Research consistently identifies several areas where autistic individuals may face challenges with the pragmatics of questions:

1. **Literal interpretation of indirect requests:** "Can you close the door?" may be processed as a genuine question about ability rather than a request. However, research (Kissine et al. 2015) found that autistic children CAN understand **conventionalized** indirect speech acts (like "Can you pass the salt?"). The difficulty appears to increase with less conventional forms and forms requiring more social inference.

2. **Recognizing rhetorical questions:** Because rhetorical questions have interrogative syntax but assertive function, literal processing leads to attempting to answer them. The cue that a question is rhetorical often comes from social context, obvious shared knowledge, and prosodic features — channels that may be harder to access for some autistic individuals.

3. **Phatic question scripts:** "How are you?" as a greeting (requiring "Fine, thanks") vs. as a genuine question (allowing a real answer) requires contextual discrimination. The pragmatic inference required is: *Is this person genuinely interested in my wellbeing, or is this a social ritual?*

4. **Over-answering:** Providing more information than the question requires, driven by thoroughness and completeness rather than social calibration of informativeness.

5. **Difficulty with vague or open-ended questions:** Questions like "Tell me about yourself" or "How was your weekend?" may be overwhelming because they don't specify what kind of information or level of detail is expected.

6. **Processing loaded or leading questions:** The buried presuppositions in loaded questions and the suggestive framing of leading questions may be harder to detect without explicit analysis.

### 8.2 Systematic Strategies

The pragmatic system of questions can be approached systematically:

1. **Learn the common patterns:** Many question-function mappings are highly conventional ("Can you..." = request, "How are you?" = greeting). Memorizing these conventions provides reliable shortcuts.

2. **Check the context before the content:** Before processing the literal meaning of a question, note who is asking, where you are, and what activity is underway. This context often tells you the question type before you analyze the words.

3. **Listen for prosodic cues:** Even if prosodic processing is effortful, rising intonation (genuine question) vs. falling intonation (rhetorical/assertion) provides a reliable binary indicator.

4. **Apply the "bizarre response" test:** If answering the question literally would produce a bizarre or unhelpful response ("Can you close the door?" "Yes, I can." [doesn't move]), the question probably isn't literal.

5. **Learn the dispreferred answer markers:** When you hear someone respond to your question with "Well..." + pause + hesitation, expect bad news, disagreement, or refusal. This is a reliable structural signal across English.

6. **When uncertain, ask for clarification:** "Are you asking me to close the door, or asking if I'm physically able to?" is socially slightly unusual but far less problematic than guessing wrong. Over time, patterns become internalized and explicit checking becomes less necessary.

## 10. SUMMARY: THE PRAGMATIC SYSTEM OF QUESTIONS

Questions constitute one of the most complex pragmatic systems in language because:

1. **Form-function mismatch is the norm, not the exception.** The interrogative form can serve at least 11 distinct pragmatic functions (information-seeking, rhetorical, display, indirect request, phatic, loaded, tag, leading, echo, self-addressed, hypothetical), and question-functions can be performed without interrogative syntax.

2. **Questions are fundamentally about knowledge management.** They navigate epistemic asymmetries between participants — who knows what, who has the right to know, and how confident each party is about their knowledge.

3. **Questions exert social pressure.** They create an obligation to respond (conditional relevance), they can control topics and turn-allocation, and they encode power relationships.

4. **Answering is itself a complex pragmatic act.** Adequate answers must calibrate informativeness, navigate face considerations, and respect preference organization.

5. **Context determines everything.** The same string of words ("How are you?", "Can you close the door?", "Who cares?") functions completely differently depending on who says it, to whom, where, when, and why.

For autistic individuals, the pragmatics of questions represents a domain where systematic learning is both possible and highly rewarding. The rules, while context-dependent, are largely conventional and learnable. The key shift is from "What does this sentence mean?" to "What is this sentence doing in this context?" — a shift that applies throughout pragmatics but is especially critical for questions.
