# TD Snap: The Complete Guide to Tobii Dynavox's Flagship AAC Platform

For someone who cannot speak, selecting the right communication software is not a matter of convenience -- it is a matter of voice. TD Snap, developed by Tobii Dynavox, has emerged as one of the most comprehensive augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) platforms on the market, offering an unprecedented range of communication strategies within a single application. With eight distinct page sets, native eye gaze tracking integration, bilingual code-switching, and smart home control, TD Snap aims to serve virtually every AAC user profile, from a toddler learning their first words to an adult recovering from a stroke.

But the platform has not been without controversy. A 2024 shift to a subscription pricing model drew sharp criticism from families and clinicians who argue that charging monthly fees for a communication lifeline crosses an ethical line. This deep dive examines everything TD Snap offers, how its technology works, who it serves best, and the trade-offs that come with choosing it.

## The Company Behind the Software: Tobii Dynavox

### From Carnegie Mellon to Global Leader

The story of Tobii Dynavox begins with two separate innovations on two continents. In 1983, Gary Kiliany, a Carnegie Mellon University graduate student volunteering at the Rehabilitation Institute of Pittsburgh, met a woman with cerebral palsy who could not speak. With guidance from Professor Mark Friedman, Kiliany built the "EyeTyper" -- a device that let her compose and vocalize sentences. That prototype became the seed of DynaVox Systems, one of the first companies dedicated to AAC technology.

Meanwhile, in Sweden, Tobii Technology was founded in 2001 with a focus on eye tracking hardware. By 2005, Tobii had released the world's first eye-controlled computer, opening a new frontier for people with severe motor disabilities to interact with technology using only their gaze.

### The 2014 Merger

The two companies converged in 2014 when DynaVox filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection. Tobii acquired DynaVox on May 22, 2014, merging their combined expertise in eye tracking hardware and AAC software into a single entity: Tobii Dynavox LLC. The result was the world's leading provider of assistive communication technology, now employing over 1,000 people across 65+ countries.

Tobii Dynavox has since expanded through strategic acquisitions, including **Acapela** (a Belgian speech synthesis provider) for high-quality digital voices and **Rehadapt** (a German manufacturer) for medically certified device mounting solutions.

## What Is TD Snap?

TD Snap is a symbol- and text-based communication application that runs on iPadOS, Windows, and Tobii Dynavox's dedicated hardware devices. Rather than offering a single vocabulary approach, TD Snap functions as a **communication platform** housing multiple distinct page sets -- each designed for different user profiles, access methods, and stages of language development.

The app supports three primary access methods:
- **Touch**: Direct screen tapping or pointing
- **Eye gaze**: Using Tobii Dynavox's integrated eye tracking hardware
- **Switch scanning**: Single or dual switch access for users with severe motor impairments

What makes TD Snap unique among AAC apps is the sheer breadth of its page set library. Where most competitors offer one or two vocabulary systems, TD Snap provides six first-party page sets and two third-party options, all within the same application. This means a speech-language pathologist (SLP) can evaluate multiple communication strategies for a client without purchasing separate apps.

## The Eight Page Sets: A Complete Comparison

### Core First -- The Versatile Standard

Core First is TD Snap's most widely used page set and its most flexible. It is a symbol-supported vocabulary designed for all stages of language development, from first communicators to advanced users composing complex sentences.

The defining feature of Core First is its **12 grid size options**, ranging from 1x1 (a single large button) up to 8x10 (80 cells). Critically, vocabulary locations remain consistent as users graduate to larger grids -- a word learned in a 3x3 layout stays in the same relative position in the 5x5 and 7x7 layouts. This consistency builds motor memory and allows growth without re-learning the interface.

Core First includes pre-built topic pages, quick phrases, a built-in keyboard for emerging spellers, and access to the PCS (Picture Communication Symbols) library with thousands of symbols.

### Motor Plan -- Building Automaticity

Motor Plan takes a fundamentally different approach to vocabulary organization. Instead of grouping words by theme or category, Motor Plan organizes vocabulary **taxonomically by feature and function**. The goal is to create consistent, repeatable motor patterns for each word, so users eventually navigate to words automatically -- similar to how touch typists locate keys without looking.

Available in 30, 40, and 66 grid sizes, Motor Plan is designed for users who benefit from motor planning approaches. The page set is optimized for both touch and eye gaze access and is built on decades of research into motor-based language organization.

### Express -- Simplicity for Beginners

For users who need a straightforward entry point, Express offers an 18-button grid with large, easy-to-select buttons. Navigation follows a theme-based structure that puts the most common words and phrases within immediate reach.

Express also includes built-in tools beyond communication: a book creation feature, photo viewing, a whiteboard, visual supports, access to the PCS library, and a keyboard for letter play and spelling practice. It is designed for families and caregivers who need a low-barrier starting point.

### Text -- For Literate Communicators

The Text page set serves users who do not rely on symbols for communication. It offers a text-only interface designed for people with strong reading and composition skills, or those transitioning away from symbol-based communication.

Features include phrases organized by topic, pre-stored messages that can be saved for future use, a keyboard with word prediction and editing tools, and access to smart assistants including Google and Alexa. Text represents the end of the literacy journey within TD Snap.

### Scanning -- Optimized for Switch Access

The Scanning page set adapts Core First's vocabulary for users who access their device through switch scanning rather than direct selection. It reorganizes content into set groups optimized for efficient scan patterns, with customizable auditory cues, zoom and highlighting feedback, timing settings, and scan pattern options.

This page set is essential for users with significant motor disabilities, such as those with cerebral palsy or advanced ALS, who may use a single head switch or sip-and-puff system as their primary input.

### Aphasia -- Communication and Recovery

Built specifically for adults who have lost language ability due to stroke or brain injury, the Aphasia page set combines communication tools with therapy-oriented features. It includes a whiteboard for drawing, rating scales for expressing preferences, word lists for practice, conversation practice templates, and photo storage.

The goal is dual-purpose: supporting daily communication needs while also providing tools that mirror those used in speech-language pathology sessions, enabling ongoing practice outside of therapy appointments.

### PODD -- Pragmatic Organization (Third-Party)

PODD (Pragmatic Organization Dynamic Display), created by Australian speech pathologist Gayle Porter, is available as an in-app purchase within TD Snap. It uses a 15-cell layout representing common communication areas, with language prediction features that help learners construct 1-4 keyword sentences efficiently.

PODD is designed primarily for pre-school and school-aged learners and emphasizes the pragmatic functions of communication -- not just vocabulary, but the intent behind messages.

### Gateway -- Core Word Focus (Third-Party)

Gateway, developed by Joan Bruno, is another third-party page set available via in-app purchase. It focuses on high-frequency core words and fringe vocabulary, with Home and School Core pages linking seamlessly to relevant theme-based vocabulary. Gateway is organized in stages to match different levels of language development.

## Eye Gaze Technology: How It Works

The feature that most distinguishes Tobii Dynavox from its competitors is native eye gaze tracking -- and it is worth understanding how this technology actually functions.

### The Science of Pupil-Center Corneal Reflection

Tobii's eye tracking is built on a technique called **pupil-center corneal reflection (PCCR)**. The process works in five steps:

1. **Near-infrared (NIR) illumination**: The eye tracker emits near-infrared light toward the user's eyes. NIR light was chosen because it is invisible to the human eye (causing no discomfort) and interferes minimally with ambient lighting conditions.

2. **Corneal reflection**: The NIR light creates visible reflection patterns on two surfaces of the eye -- the cornea (the outer layer) and the pupil.

3. **Glint detection**: Sensors in the eye tracker detect the corneal reflection, known as the "glint."

4. **Pupil center calculation**: Simultaneously, image processing algorithms identify the center of the pupil.

5. **Gaze point estimation**: By measuring the relative positions of the glint and the pupil center, the system calculates exactly where on the screen the user is looking. As the eye moves, the pupil shifts relative to the corneal reflection, allowing continuous tracking.

### Why Tobii's Implementation Stands Out

Tobii's eye trackers employ several advanced features that distinguish them from competitors:

- **Dynamic bright/dark pupil switching**: The system automatically alternates between bright pupil and dark pupil detection methods, optimizing accuracy across different eye colors, disabilities, and lighting conditions.
- **Universal compatibility**: The technology works across all ethnicities, accommodates glasses and contact lenses, and handles various eye conditions common in AAC user populations.
- **Fast recovery**: When a user looks away from the screen and returns, Tobii's tracker recovers gaze lock faster than competing systems.
- **15+ years of R&D**: The technology has been refined through more than a decade of research specifically for assistive applications.

### The Hardware Required

Eye gaze tracking with TD Snap requires Tobii Dynavox hardware:

- **TD I-Series** (TD I-13 and TD I-16): Dedicated Windows-based speech generating devices with built-in eye trackers, dual cameras, speakers, Bluetooth, and WLAN. The I-13 has a 13.3" display while the I-16 offers 15.6".
- **TD Pilot**: An iPad 12.9" enclosed in a specialized case containing a Tobii eye tracker. Priced at approximately $10,500-$12,000, the TD Pilot brings eye gaze to the iPad ecosystem.
- **PCEye**: A standalone eye tracker that attaches to existing computers.

## Beyond Communication: Smart Home and Assistant Integration

TD Snap extends beyond basic communication through its **Google Assistant integration**. Users can control smart home devices -- lights, music, thermostats, and more -- directly from within the communication app, using whatever access method they rely on (touch, eye gaze, or switch).

This feature is significant because it addresses a broader dimension of disability: not just the ability to communicate with other people, but the ability to control one's environment independently. For a person using eye gaze to communicate, being able to also dim the lights or play music without relying on a caregiver represents a meaningful increase in autonomy.

Smart assistant access is available through both symbol-supported interfaces (for users who navigate with symbols) and the Text page set (for literate users), across Tobii Dynavox devices, iPad, and Windows.

## True Bilingual Code-Switching

One of TD Snap's more nuanced features is its support for **true bilingual code-switching** -- the ability to switch between two languages within the same communication session without navigating to a separate page set.

Available language pairs include English/Spanish (US), English/French (Canadian), and Spanish variants (Latin America/Mexico). The Express Bilingual USA English-Spanish page set, for example, allows a user to compose a sentence that includes words from both languages -- a natural communication behavior called code-switching that bilingual speakers use constantly.

This matters because bilingual AAC users have historically been forced to choose one language for their device, even though their families and communities may operate in two or more. As AAC researchers and SLPs have increasingly emphasized, bilingual users should always have access to both languages, and restricting them to one constitutes an inequitable reduction in communicative capacity.

## The myTobiiDynavox Companion Platform

TD Snap connects to a web-based companion platform at myTobiiDynavox.com that provides several features for users, families, and clinicians:

- **Data tracking**: Monitor button usage, modeling counts, and most frequently used messages. Data can be viewed in a calendar format and exported as .xlsx files for detailed analysis.
- **Page set management**: Store, share, and transfer page set configurations between devices.
- **Professional access**: SLPs can access free professional accounts during AAC evaluations.
- **Trial management**: Start and manage 60-day free trials of TD Snap.
- **Subscription management**: Handle billing and account settings for the subscription model.

The data tracking feature is particularly valuable for SLPs, who can use the exported data to analyze communication patterns, measure progress, and adjust vocabulary configurations based on actual usage.

## Pricing and the Subscription Controversy

### The Old Model vs. The New

Prior to May 2024, TD Snap was available on iPad as a one-time purchase for approximately $100. In May 2024, Tobii Dynavox unpublished the original app from the Apple App Store and replaced it with a new version requiring a **$9.99/month subscription**. The original app was renamed "TD Snap AAC Legacy" and remains available for users who purchased it before the change.

### Why the Backlash Matters

The subscription transition drew intense criticism from the AAC community:

- **Lifetime cost concern**: For users who need AAC for their entire lives, the subscription model creates an indefinite financial obligation. Five years of access costs approximately $600 -- compared to the previous one-time $100 payment.
- **Service disruption reports**: Some families reported that forced app updates during the transition temporarily disabled communication on their devices, leaving non-speaking individuals without a voice.
- **Institutional barriers**: AT Act programs and organizations managing iPads through JAMF or similar MDM systems reported incompatibilities with the subscription model.
- **Ethical framing**: Critics argue that communication is a fundamental human right, and subscription-gating access to one's voice creates a morally untenable dependency.

Tobii Dynavox has defended the change by pointing to continuous updates, the enterprise pricing option for institutions, and the grandfathering of existing license holders. The company also offers generous trial periods (60 days through their portal plus a separate 1-month App Store trial).

## Strengths

- **Unmatched page set variety**: Eight page sets covering virtually every AAC user profile -- no competitor offers this range within a single app.
- **Native eye gaze integration**: Tobii Dynavox's 15+ years of eye tracking R&D provides the best eye gaze experience in AAC, with universal compatibility and fast recovery.
- **Growth pathway**: Users can start with Express or a 1x1 Core First grid and grow to Motor Plan 66 or Text without switching applications.
- **Bilingual code-switching**: True in-sentence language switching is a rare and important feature.
- **Data tracking**: The myTobiiDynavox companion portal provides SLPs with exportable usage analytics.
- **Smart home control**: Google Assistant integration extends the platform beyond communication to environmental independence.
- **Comprehensive access methods**: Touch, eye gaze, and switch scanning are supported across the platform.
- **Hardware ecosystem**: Dedicated devices (I-Series) and the TD Pilot offer purpose-built hardware with reliability that consumer devices cannot match.

## Weaknesses

- **Subscription model**: The shift to $9.99/month creates ongoing costs and has generated significant community backlash.
- **Ecosystem lock-in**: Eye gaze functionality requires Tobii Dynavox hardware, which starts at approximately $10,500 for the TD Pilot. Tobii explicitly does not support third-party hardware.
- **Platform limitations**: No Android support. iPad users need the subscription; only Windows users can still purchase perpetual licenses.
- **Add-on costs**: PODD and Gateway page sets require additional purchases beyond the base subscription.
- **Hardware pricing**: The TD Pilot ($10,500+) and I-Series devices represent substantial investments, though they may be covered by insurance or Medicaid.
- **Complexity**: The sheer number of options (8 page sets, 12+ grid sizes, multiple access methods) can be overwhelming for families navigating AAC for the first time.

## User Reviews and Reception

TD Snap maintains a **4.43 out of 5 stars** rating on the Apple App Store based on approximately 240 ratings. Professional reviews from SLPs tend to be strongly positive, with clinicians praising the breadth of page sets and the quality of eye gaze integration.

> "TD Snap is by far the best. Better than the most popular one out there that is being used by the schools." -- Speech-Language Pathologist, App Store Review

Negative reviews primarily focus on the subscription model change, with some parents describing it as "holding communication hostage" and recommending families look at alternatives. The tension between clinical excellence and pricing ethics defines much of TD Snap's current reception.

## Key Takeaways

- TD Snap offers the broadest range of AAC vocabulary strategies in a single application, making it ideal for SLPs who evaluate multiple approaches.
- Tobii Dynavox's eye gaze tracking is the industry gold standard, but it requires proprietary (and expensive) hardware.
- The 2024 subscription transition remains deeply controversial, though the enterprise pricing option mitigates institutional concerns.
- Features like bilingual code-switching and Google Assistant integration push TD Snap beyond pure communication into independence support.
- The platform's growth pathway -- from a single button to a full keyboard -- means users may never need to switch applications.

## Conclusion

TD Snap occupies a unique position in the AAC landscape. No other single application offers the combination of eight distinct page sets, world-leading eye gaze technology, bilingual code-switching, smart home control, and a clear growth pathway from first communication to literate independence. For families, SLPs, and institutions willing to invest in the Tobii Dynavox ecosystem, the clinical capabilities are exceptional.

The subscription pricing model, however, has introduced a persistent tension. For a community that views communication as a fundamental right, any barrier to access -- however well-intentioned -- draws scrutiny. Tobii Dynavox's challenge going forward is to sustain the rapid innovation that justifies recurring costs while ensuring that no one loses their voice because of a billing cycle.

For those evaluating AAC solutions, TD Snap deserves serious consideration -- particularly if eye gaze access is needed or if the breadth of page sets aligns with a user's potential growth. The 60-day free trial (plus an additional 1-month App Store trial) provides ample time to evaluate whether this platform is the right fit.
