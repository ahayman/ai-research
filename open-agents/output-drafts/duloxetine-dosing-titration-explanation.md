# Dosing: 20 → 30 → 60 → 120mg

> The dose question looks like one question. It is two, because the two things this drug is doing for you sit on different dose curves, and the curves disagree.

You have an appointment in about two weeks where someone will ask whether to raise your dose. That conversation will proceed as though there is a single dial with a single answer somewhere along it. There is not. Duloxetine's pain effect and duloxetine's psychological effect have different dose-response curves, and from where you are standing those curves point in different directions.

The pain curve has a threshold at roughly 40 to 60mg. Below it, essentially nothing. Above it, a real but modest effect that stops improving with further milligrams.

The psychological curve has no such step. It does not reward escalation at all.

You are at 20mg. You report pain "noted but minimal" and the effect "mostly psychological and with my sleep." That is precisely the signature of sitting below the pain threshold while already holding what the drug has to offer on the other axis. Both of those things matter to you, and you said so explicitly when you corrected an earlier framing that treated the pain as expendable. So this is a real two-sided trade rather than a lopsided one, and the rest of this page is an attempt to hand you both curves accurately enough that you can do the reasoning yourself.

## The pain curve is a threshold, not a slope

Start with pain, because pain is where the milligrams actually buy something.

Low-dose duloxetine has not been neglected for pain. It has been tested, three times, in two different pain conditions, in about 350 patients, and it failed on the pain primary every time `[primary, verified]`.

The trial that settles it is Goldstein 2005. It randomized 457 patients 1:1:1:1 to placebo, 20mg, 60mg and 120mg, which works out to roughly 114 patients per arm `[primary, verified]`. 60mg separated from placebo. 120mg separated from placebo. 20mg did not.

That design matters more than any other single fact on this page, because it removes the usual excuse. When a low-dose arm fails, the standard reply is that the arm was too small to detect the effect. Here the 20mg arm was the same size as the 60mg arm that worked: same trial, same patients, same measurement, same statistical power, one dose separated and one did not. The power excuse is unavailable in an equal-arm, fixed-dose, within-trial comparison.

30mg does not rescue it. Arnold 2012 tested 30mg in fibromyalgia, 155 patients against 153, which is adequate power, and the pain primary came back at P=0.202 `[primary, verified]`. This is worth dwelling on for a moment, because it kills an idea that had been circulating through this project and sounds very reasonable: that 30mg is the efficient dose, the one that captures most of the benefit for less of the cost. That idea came out of PET occupancy modelling, which is to say it was derived rather than observed, and nobody checked whether it had ever been tested in a trial. It has been. It failed.

Cochrane's own authors chose to put the dose-dependence in their formal conclusion, and it is the sentence nobody in this project quoted for seven passes:

> "There is adequate amounts of moderate quality evidence from eight studies performed by the manufacturers of duloxetine that doses of 60 mg and 120 mg daily are efficacious for treating pain in diabetic peripheral neuropathy but lower daily doses are not." `[primary, verified]`

Above 60mg the curve goes flat. 120mg is no better than 60mg for pain, and Cochrane and Lilly's own pooled analysis agree on that point `[primary, verified]`.

So the shape is: nothing, then a step, then a plateau. The step sits somewhere between 40 and 60mg.

## The payoff on the far side of the step is modest

Here is the number, and it is smaller than the one this project spent seven passes quoting.

There is a Cochrane review specific to fibromyalgia, Welsch 2018 (CD010292). It is more recent than the mixed-indication review everyone cites, it is larger, and it is better matched to you. It reports **NNTB 11, 95% CI 9 to 14**, for 50% or greater pain relief, and its authors' verdict, verbatim, is "no clinically relevant benefit" `[primary, verified]`. GRADE LOW. Lilly sponsored the trials.

NNTB 11 means that for roughly every eleven people who take it, about one gets a halving of pain that placebo would not have given them, and the other ten do not. That is what waits on the far side of the threshold. It is real, it is not nothing, and it is not large.

There is a genuine disagreement in the literature here and you should have it rather than a tidied version of it. Sultan 2008, one of the few independent analyses in this area with no pharmaceutical funding, concluded that "the dose of duloxetine made little difference to the result" `[primary, verified]`. That cuts against everything in the section above. But read what it rests on: the same sentence notes that "only 66 patients (in two treatment arms) received the 20 mg dose," and the statement pools all doses including 120mg `[primary, verified]`. Cochrane's contrary conclusion is a formal conclusion drawn from eight studies. Two independent syntheses disagree, the disagreement is real, and it is not resolved by picking the one you prefer.

I want to be careful about the framing here, because you corrected an earlier version of this project that treated your pain as something to be protected *from* rather than *for*. The point is not that the pain benefit is too small to want. The point is that it is small enough to be weighed rather than assumed, and a page that tells you "60mg is the analgesic dose" without also telling you NNTB 11 has told you half of something.

## The psychological curve does not step, and escalation does not work

Now the other axis, where the shape is entirely different.

The most decision-relevant study in this whole document is Kornstein 2008. Patients took duloxetine 60mg for six weeks. Those who had not remitted were then randomly reassigned, double-blind, either to stay at 60mg or to double to 120mg for eight more weeks. The remission rates were **30.0% for staying and 30.5% for doubling** `[primary, verified]`.

Half a percentage point. That is not a non-significant trend in the right direction. It is nothing.

And read the authors' full sentence, because the first half is the part that gets dropped: the patients "demonstrated continued symptom improvement in the 8-week extension" `[primary, verified]`. Both arms kept getting better. The people who stayed at 60mg improved at the same rate as the people who doubled. The gain was time, not milligrams.

The meta-analysis agrees. Dold 2017 pooled seven double-blind randomized dose-escalation trials, 1,208 participants, including that duloxetine trial. **Hedges g = −0.04, 95% CI −0.20 to 0.12, p = 0.63** `[primary abstract only]`. Read the confidence interval rather than the p-value, because it is the more informative object: it excludes anything larger than a small effect in either direction. This is not an underpowered null with a wide interval concealing a real effect. And the same analysis found that significantly more patients dropped out of the escalation arm because of adverse effects `[primary abstract only]`. So escalation delivered no extra efficacy and more side effects. That is costs without benefits rather than a fair bet.

The caveats belong here, at the claim, rather than below it. Kornstein studied depression, not anxiety and not pain. It tested 60 to 120mg, not 20 to 60mg. It tested non-response rather than loss of response after a response. And it was Lilly-sponsored, which in this instance cuts against the sponsor's own commercial interest and therefore makes the null more credible rather than less `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

There is one crack in this null and it happens to be yours. Ruhé 2006 found "some, but limited" evidence for escalation specifically in **partial responders** after eight weeks `[primary abstract only]`. You are a partial responder: real benefit on anxiety and sleep, minimal on pain. That is the one population where the null is softest, and it should not be papered over. It is also a 2006 narrative review that has been superseded on this exact question by Dold's quantitative meta-analysis, which had the randomized trials Ruhé lacked and found nothing.

One further honesty point, because it matters for how much weight the background literature can carry. The familiar claim that antidepressant efficacy is flat above minimum doses rests on Furukawa 2019 and Jakubovski 2016, and those two meta-analyses **contradict each other**: Furukawa found flat-to-decreasing above 20 to 40mg fluoxetine equivalents, Jakubovski found a significant increase `[both primary, verified]`. More to the point, **both of them exclude duloxetine** `[primary, verified]`. Furukawa covers five SSRIs plus venlafaxine and mirtazapine; Jakubovski is SSRIs only. Applying either to your drug is an extrapolation across drug class, and this project has been burned by exactly that move repeatedly. What all the dose-response literature agrees on is narrower and is the part worth keeping: side-effect burden rises with dose in every dataset, and nobody in the disagreement is arguing that the efficacy gain from going higher is large `[reasoning, not evidence — this is the intersection of the findings, not a finding]`.

## The question nobody asked about the dose you are on

Something specific about 20mg and anxiety.

Nicolini 2009 included a **fixed 20mg arm** in a generalized anxiety trial, 84 patients. 20mg beat placebo on HAM-A total score and on the psychic factor `[primary, verified]`. It did not beat placebo on the somatic factor, at p=0.07 `[primary, verified]`.

So 20mg works for anxiety, in the sense that it separated from placebo. That is the only sense that was tested.

Here is what did not happen. **20mg was never compared to 60mg.** When Lilly pooled the anxiety data to test non-inferiority against venlafaxine, the pooled analysis used the 60 to 120mg arm and excluded the 20mg arm entirely. The arithmetic reconciles to the patient: placebo 331, duloxetine 60 to 120mg 320, venlafaxine 333, and the 84-patient 20mg arm is simply not in it `[primary, verified]`.

"Beats placebo" and "is as good as 60mg" are different claims, and only the first one was ever asked.

The raw numbers look close, and I am going to show them to you with their leash on. 20mg produced a HAM-A change of −14.7, 60 to 120mg produced −15.3, and placebo produced −11.6; response rates were 60%, 65% and 42%; remission rates were 42%, 44% and 20% `[secondary — unverified]`. Those figures come from a review rather than from the primary paper. A version of them circulates with percentages attached, 45% versus 55%, and those percentages are **internally inconsistent with the absolute changes** they are supposed to describe, so they should not be presented as facts at all `[secondary — unverified]`. Note also that the placebo response was large: −11.6 points, 42% response, 20% remission. The drug adds roughly three points on top of that.

What survives is narrower and still worth having. The 20mg arm had 84 patients against a 158-patient comparator, and the two were never compared to each other. A close-looking gap in a small arm that was never tested is not evidence of equivalence. It is an absence of evidence that happens to point in a direction you might like `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

There is one comparison worth putting in front of you, with its caveats wired on, because it is the sharpest thing in the material. Response NNT for anxiety at 20mg works out to roughly 5.6 (60% versus placebo's 42%) `[reasoning, not evidence — derived from secondary-source figures]`, against NNTB 11 for fibromyalgia pain at 60mg `[primary, verified]`. Read naively, that says your current dose has better evidence for the thing you report benefiting from than the target dose has for the thing you were prescribed it for. Read carefully, it is a cross-trial, cross-indication, cross-endpoint comparison built partly on a secondary-source table with known internal inconsistencies, which is exactly the kind of elegant convergence this project has been fooled by several times. It is suggestive. It is not a measurement.

## Your sleep is safer than you think, and you are braced for the wrong thing

You value the sleep improvement highly and you are right to want to protect it. So here is the by-dose sleep evidence, which is thinner than it ought to be and clearer than you might expect.

There is exactly one duloxetine trial with posted results containing a **fixed 20mg arm**. One. Establishing that took scanning all 135 duloxetine trials with posted results on ClinicalTrials.gov `[primary, verified]`. It is NCT00408876: chronic low back pain, 13 weeks, fixed doses of 20, 60 and 120mg against placebo.

| Adverse event | Placebo (n=117) | 20mg (n=59) | 60mg (n=116) | 120mg (n=112) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Insomnia** | 2.56% | **10.17%** | **8.62%** | **18.75%** |
| **Somnolence** | 0% | **5.08%** | **4.31%** | **12.50%** |
| **Fatigue** | 0% | **0%** | **6.03%** | **8.93%** |
| Discontinued for AE | 8.5% | 15.3% | 14.7% | 24.1% |

`[primary, verified — retrieved from the ClinicalTrials.gov results registry via API; these are the registry's own counts]`

Read the 20 and 60 columns against each other. Insomnia goes slightly down. Somnolence goes slightly down. Discontinuation for adverse events is flat, 15.3% against 14.7%. The step from 20 to 60 does essentially nothing to sleep. The inflection is at 120mg, where insomnia roughly doubles and somnolence roughly triples `[primary, verified]`.

So the defensible claim is: **there is no evidence that the 20 to 60mg step damages your sleep.** That is worth having, because "it will wreck your sleep" is one of the arguments you are most likely to be offered and it is not supported by the only trial that tested it.

What is not defensible is the mirror image, that 60mg will *improve* your sleep. The same trial measured the Athens Insomnia Scale, and 60mg beat placebo at p=0.045 while 120mg did not, and 60mg beat 120mg at p=0.012 `[primary, verified]`. That looks like a finding. It is a secondary endpoint of a trial that failed its primary, with no multiplicity correction across six pairwise comparisons, in an arm of 59. And it does not replicate: two other trials measured the same instrument at the same dose and returned p=0.329 and p=0.590 `[primary, verified]`. Treat it as noise.

Now the reframe, which I think is the most useful single thing in this section.

**You are braced for insomnia, and insomnia is not the thing that moves.** It does not rise from 20 to 60 anywhere in the data: 10.2% to 8.6% in the fixed-dose trial, and at the 30-to-60 step in Korean adults, 12.7% to 13.6% and 9.4% to 7.9% `[primary, verified]`. It rises at 120mg.

**Somnolence is the thing that moves**, and it moves at the 30-to-60 step: 2.6% to 10.2% in adolescents, 0% to 6.8% and 7.8% to 14.3% in Korean adults `[primary, verified]`. In the only duloxetine trial ever run in ME/CFS patients, somnolence hit **41%** at 60 to 120mg, in 29 people `[primary, verified]`.

And **fatigue is the one row in the fixed-dose trial that rises cleanly across the 20-to-60 step**, from 0% to 6.03% `[primary, verified]`. So the summary "20mg is the same as 60mg on every sleep measure" is not quite right. It is true for insomnia, somnolence, sleep disorder and discontinuation. Fatigue is the exception, and the exception lands exactly where you would least want it.

Somnolence is genuinely dose-dependent, and duloxetine's own FDA label says so: it lists somnolence among the reactions with "a significant dose-dependent relationship in fixed-dose studies," at 10 to 11% against 3% `[primary, verified]`. The widely-quoted "1.5%" figure for duloxetine somnolence is not an incidence rate at all. It is a **discontinuation** rate `[primary, verified]`. If someone offers you 1.5% as reassurance, they are quoting the wrong column.

This connects to something you told us, and it is why this reframe matters for you more than it would for most people. You said: "There are different types of exhaustion. Just feeling 'sleepy' isn't a bother. It's mostly that bone-weary, pain-laden exhaustion where my brain is filled with so much fog I can barely function." The consequence is that somnolence may be close to undetectable to you by feel, because the channel you would use to detect it is the channel your ME/CFS already saturates. Your current and valued gain is waking less tired. Somnolence is therefore the adverse effect that lands directly on the thing you are trying to protect, and it is also the effect that rises with dose most reliably. That combination is worth naming out loud.

## The sensory gating ladder, and the 3× question

This is the newest thing on this page and it is the piece I would most want you to have.

You have already accepted a cost at 20mg. The acute sensory overwhelm is worse, and you have said it is worth it because you are not crashing afterwards. That is your call and it is a coherent one. But the cost may be dose-dependent, and there is human evidence bearing on it that nobody has previously connected to your situation.

One lab in Copenhagen ran a sequence of studies giving healthy volunteers escitalopram and measuring sensory gating via P50 suppression, which is the brain's automatic filter for redundant input. At **10mg the filter was intact** (Jensen 2008, n=21) `[primary, verified]`. At **15mg the filter was significantly disrupted** (Oranje 2011, n=20) `[primary, verified]`. Same lab, same paradigm, same population of healthy male volunteers, same double-blind placebo-controlled crossover design. The second study was explicitly designed to extend the first by raising the dose, and its stated hypothesis was that the higher dose would break gating. It did.

A 1.5× dose step flipped the sensory filter. You are contemplating a 3× step.

The caveats are substantial and they go here rather than in a box at the bottom, because filing them at the bottom is this project's signature failure. This is a **cross-study comparison**, two papers rather than one dose-response experiment `[reasoning, not evidence]`. There is **no independent replication**: all of these studies come from a single group `[primary, verified — established by author enumeration]`. There are three papers in total on serotonin and P50 suppression in healthy volunteers, from two labs, and the other lab ran the **opposite** manipulation: depleting serotonin broke prepulse inhibition and spared P50, which is a mirror image and argues for an inverted-U rather than a single monotonic dial `[primary, verified]`. The drug is **escitalopram, not duloxetine**. The subjects were about twenty healthy men. And the leap from a P50 waveform in a laboratory to five hours at a crowded festival is enormous and unbridged `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

One more correction, because a previous pass of this project got it wrong in a way that would have misled you. An earlier version suggested a dose increase would re-open the acute window "**transiently**." That word is unsupported. Every one of these studies is a single acute dose in a drug-naive volunteer. **Whether the cost persists at a steady dose has never been measured** `[no evidence exists]`.

So this is not a reason not to go up. It is a specific, human, dose-graded piece of evidence that the acute sensory cost you have already accepted may get worse if you triple the dose, held with every caveat above attached. You are the person who decides whether that trade is worth making, and you cannot decide it if nobody tells you it is on the table.

## Two arguments against going up that do not survive

Two things you may be told, or may already have read. Both are dead, and one of them was called the strongest argument on this side of the question by an earlier pass of this project.

**Emotional blunting is not known to be dose-dependent.** You may meet the claim that blunting rises with dose, so a higher dose risks more of it. A previous pass called this "the strongest argument against a dose increase" for you. It collapses. **No dose-response study of antidepressant-induced emotional blunting exists** `[primary, verified — established by absence]`. The claim propagates across review papers that all root back to the same **1990 case report**, laundered through a qualitative interview study that never measured dose `[primary, verified]`. Two reviews asserting the same thing from the same source is one piece of evidence, not two. And the one cohort in which doses were **systematically raised**, 98 patients, found blunting scores **went down**, at P<0.001 `[primary, verified]`. Blunting also correlates with depression severity, which raises the live possibility that it is substantially a symptom rather than a dose effect `[primary, verified]`. What survives is: blunting is common, around 46%; dose reduction is how it is managed clinically; case reports consistently support that management; and nobody has ever measured a dose-response relationship. That is a reason to monitor rather than to predict. A caution built on an untested premise is still an untested premise, and it does not become sound by pointing in the safe direction.

One finding here is worth carrying even though it is not comforting. Goodwin 2017 surveyed 669 treated patients and 150 recovered controls and found blunting at 46%, with **no difference according to antidepressant agent** `[primary, verified]`. If part of your model is that duloxetine is a different kind of drug from escitalopram and therefore safer on this axis, the survey data do not support that. Whatever made duloxetine different for you, it is not that duloxetine is a low-blunting drug.

**The taper argument is the weakest one available.** "Going up will make coming down harder" sounds obviously true. Duloxetine's occupancy curve is hyperbolic, with a fitted half-maximal dose around 7.9mg, which means coming down from 60mg to 20mg costs about **16 occupancy points with a capsule at every step** (60, then 30, then 20), while coming down from 20mg to zero costs about **72 occupancy points with no capsule below 20mg** `[reasoning, not evidence — modelled from Takano's fitted ED50, which rests on roughly 3 subjects per dose, single-dose, thalamus only]`. That is half the milligrams and more than four times the biological distance, with nothing to stand on. The cliff is below 20mg. **You are already standing on it.** Going up adds a gentle, well-provisioned ramp in front of a cliff whose height does not change.

But the taper question is worth asking now for a completely different reason, and this one is strong: **will this prescriber write a compounded liquid?** That is the thing that makes the cliff crossable, and the answer does not depend on today's dose decision at all. Asking it now costs nothing and tells you something about the next several years.

## POTS, held at the right size

One safety item, and it needs its dose qualifier or it becomes alarming for no reason.

Duloxetine blocks the norepinephrine transporter, and NET blockade can produce an orthostatic tachycardia phenotype. This is real, it sits in primary literature from the Vanderbilt Autonomic Dysfunction Center, and there is a formal guideline recommendation behind it: the 2015 Heart Rhythm Society consensus statement carries a **Class III, Level B-R** recommendation that drugs blocking the norepinephrine reuptake transporter "should not be administered" in POTS `[primary, verified]`.

But every duloxetine-specific source for that phenotype says "high-dose" and "chronic," and all of them trace back to a single study using **80 to 240 mg/day** `[primary, verified]`. That is four to twelve times your current dose. At ordinary doses duloxetine's average heart-rate effect is **+2.22 bpm**, and there is no sustained blood-pressure elevation across 42 studies and 8,504 patients `[primary, verified]`. So this is not a 20mg problem. It becomes a conversation worth having at 60mg, and the FDA label agrees on where the line sits: its own dose threshold for blood-pressure risk is "doses above 60 mg daily" `[primary, verified]`.

The mean is small and the tail is real. Stevens 2008 documents clinically significant tachycardia at 110 to 120 bpm in a 26-year-old man on duloxetine **20mg**, with positive dechallenge and positive rechallenge, and the authors' own conclusion is that this happens "even in low doses" `[primary, verified]`.

The direction of the risk for you is **genuinely unknown**, which is unusual enough to state plainly. Most orthostatic intolerance in ME/CFS is not POTS: one study found POTS in only 6 of 39 patients while 72% had orthostatic symptoms, and concluded that orthostatic tachycardia did not account for the symptoms `[primary, verified]`. And the same drug class that manufactures orthostatic tachycardia also **prevents** tilt-induced pre-syncope, with an odds ratio of 0.22 (95% CI 0.09 to 0.55) for premature tilt termination, and cut vasovagal reactions from 9 of 18 to 1 of 18 `[primary, verified]`. So if your orthostatic intolerance is the tachycardic type, the mechanism is unfavourable. If it is the neurally-mediated type, it may be neutral or even protective. Nobody has checked which you have, and a ten-minute active stand test would tell you before the appointment.

One number from your own population. In the only duloxetine trial ever run in CFS patients, **dizziness was 31.0% versus 6.7% on placebo** `[primary, verified]`. Hold it at its real size: that is 9 events against 2, with no statistical test attached, at 3 to 6 times your dose, in a table where headache ran the *opposite* way (10.3% on duloxetine against 40.0% on placebo) and where no tachycardia, palpitations or syncope term appears at all `[primary, verified]`. Worth knowing and worth raising. Not worth alarm.

## The trap

Now the part that I think is the actual content of this decision, and it has almost nothing to do with milligrams.

You have a hypothesis. You think the crashes aggravate your ME/CFS and fibromyalgia, and therefore that preventing the crashes should improve your pain slowly, with no noradrenergic threshold to cross at all.

The standard model says the opposite: pain needs the 40 to 60mg threshold and nothing below it will do.

**Right now, those two models make different predictions.** You are at 20mg. Under the standard model your pain should not improve, because you are below threshold. Under your model it should improve slowly, as the crashes stop accumulating. The two models are currently distinguishable, and the experiment is running whether you attend to it or not.

**At 60mg they make identical predictions.** Both say pain improves. One says it was norepinephrine crossing a threshold. The other says it was the accumulated absence of crashes. **If you go to 60mg and your pain improves, you will never know which it was.** Not "it will be difficult to tell." You will never know, because the two causes will have been applied at the same time, permanently, and there is no way to unmix them afterwards.

**The 20mg window is the only clean test of your own hypothesis that will ever exist, and it is open right now.**

I want to be precise about what this is and is not, because it would be easy to smuggle a recommendation in here and I am not going to.

It is not an argument for staying at 20mg. Information is not the only thing worth having, and if the pain is bad enough then buying relief at the cost of an experiment is an entirely rational trade.

Your hypothesis is also not on strong ground, and you should weigh that too. It has three separable claims. **Claim one, that overload triggers crashes, is established** as clinical description: the Canadian Consensus Criteria describe exactly that sequence, using your own word, verbatim: "Overload phenomena may trigger a 'crash' where the patient becomes temporarily immobilized" `[primary, verified]`. **Claim two, that the crashes aggravate the underlying illness, has never been studied** `[no evidence exists]`, and it is the load-bearing limb. **Claim three, that preventing crashes therefore improves pain, has never been tested** `[no evidence exists]`. Adjacent evidence leans against it in three places: allostatic load in CFS correlates *inversely* with fatigue duration `[primary abstract only]`; the allostatic limb that prospectively predicts pain is metabolic, while parasympathetic dysregulation predicted nothing, and your model runs entirely through the limb that fails `[primary, verified]`; and the direct distress-to-pain path was null in the study that tested it `[primary, verified]`. On current evidence, your hypothesis should not be the reason you stay at 20mg.

What the trap is: a thing you should know before you close the door, because the door does not re-open.

And there is a separating observation that costs nothing and works at any dose. Your model has to run through fatigue, because the direct distress-to-pain path was null and only distress → fatigue → pain survived `[primary, verified]`. So **track fatigue and pain separately. If pain improves while fatigue does not, your model is dead.** That is a free falsification test and you already own the instrument for it.

## Four variables, and deciding what to measure

One practical thing before the close.

Four variables are in motion or about to be. Duloxetine started about two weeks ago, and its early effects characteristically attenuate over weeks, so the drug itself is still moving. Melatonin is ongoing. Iron started this week and acts at roughly 12 weeks, which means it cannot explain anything you observe at the appointment but will be quietly working underneath everything you observe after it `[primary, verified]`. A dose change would make four.

This is not an argument against changing the dose. It is an argument for deciding what you are measuring **before** you change anything, because with four variables moving and one observer, most results will not be interpretable `[reasoning, not evidence]`. You already run daily somatic check-ins with weekly review. That is the instrument, and it is a good one. The open question is what goes into it and which prediction you write down first.

## What you actually have

Two curves.

The pain curve is flat until somewhere between 40 and 60mg, then steps up to a benefit that is real and modest: roughly one person in eleven gets a halving of pain that placebo would not have given them, on low-quality, industry-sponsored evidence that one independent synthesis disputes. The psychological curve does not step at all. Doubling the dose in people who had not responded bought half a percentage point, and the best meta-analysis puts escalation at g = −0.04 with significantly more side-effect dropouts. Your current dose has a positive anxiety trial behind it and has never been compared to 60mg, so "20mg is as good as 60mg" is untested rather than true, and so is its opposite.

The sleep evidence says the 20-to-60 step does not damage sleep, that the harm sits at 120mg, and that the effect worth watching is daytime sleepiness rather than insomnia, which is not what you are braced for and which lands squarely on the fatigue you are trying to protect. The sensory gating evidence says a 1.5× step flipped the filter in the only human dose comparison that exists, and you are considering 3×, held loosely because it is one lab, three papers, acute single doses and a different drug. The taper argument against going up does not work, because you are already at the cliff edge, though the taper question is worth asking anyway. And the two findings that would have made this decision easy, a dose-dependence of emotional blunting and a POTS risk at your dose, turn out to be respectively untested and traced to doses four to twelve times yours.

Underneath all of it sits the trap, which is the only part of this that is irreversible. Going to 60mg is a decision you can undo. Closing the 20mg window is not.

Nothing on this page tells you what to do, and that is deliberate rather than evasive. You asked for mechanism rather than prediction and for candidates rather than verdicts. The two curves are the mechanism. What they are worth to you is not something the literature knows, and it is not something it is going to find out.

## Deep Dive

### The pain threshold: every trial, read correctly

Assembling every duloxetine pain trial with its design read accurately, rather than as the earlier passes of this project characterized them:

| Trial | Indication | Dose | Design quality | Pain primary | Pain secondaries / responders |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Goldstein 2005** (PMID 15927394) | Diabetic neuropathy | **20mg** | **Equal-n (114/114/114/115), fixed, 1:1:1:1** | **Not significant** | — |
| **Russell 2008** | Fibromyalgia | **20mg** | 79 vs 144, ~70–90% power | Not significant | PGI-I positive (contested) |
| **Arnold 2012** (PMID 22971669) | Fibromyalgia | **30mg** | **155 vs 153, adequate** | **Not significant (P=0.202)** | PGI-I, FIQ, SF-36 mental |
| Japanese trial | Diabetic neuropathy | **40mg** | — | **Separates** | — |
| **Goldstein 2005** | Diabetic neuropathy | **60 / 120mg** | Equal-n | **Separates** | — |
| Raskin 2005, Wernicke 2006 | Diabetic neuropathy | 60 / 120mg | — | Separates | — |
| Arnold 2004, Arnold 2005 | Fibromyalgia | 60 / 120mg | — | Separates | — |
| **Russell 2008** | Fibromyalgia | **60 / 120mg** | 150 / 147 | **Separates** | Yes |
| **Murakami 2015** (PMID 26296539) | Fibromyalgia | **titrated to 60mg** | 196 vs 197 | Not significant (P=0.0988) | **≥30% and ≥50% responders; 6 of 7 pain secondaries** |
| **Chappell 2008** (PMID 20428412) | Fibromyalgia | **60→120mg, 84% escalated, mean 113.4mg** | 162 vs 168, *supportive* study | Not significant (P=0.053) | BPI least pain P=0.046; FIQ pain P=0.030 |

Three corrections to that table are worth spelling out, because earlier passes of this project got them wrong in both directions.

**Murakami 2015 is not "a 60mg pain failure."** It missed one pre-specified primary analysis at P=0.0988 while the **≥30% and ≥50% pain responder rates were significantly higher on duloxetine**, and six of seven pain secondaries favoured it: worst pain, least pain, pain right now, worst pain in diary, FIQ pain score, SF-36 bodily pain `[primary, verified]`. An earlier pass filed those under "global/function" to support a thesis that duloxetine fails pain at every dose. They are pain measures. And the responder rates are the exact endpoints Cochrane's NNT is built on.

**Chappell 2008 is not a 60mg trial.** Its own text: patients "not [having] ≥50% reduction… [were] blindly escalated to 120 mg QD" at week 13, and **98 of 117 (83.8%) were escalated, with a mean prescribed daily dose among escalated patients of 113.4mg** `[primary, verified]`. The trial's six-month co-primary therefore measures a population selected for 60mg non-response and then pushed to 120mg. It is a responder-enriched blind dose-escalation design, and the authors themselves describe it as "supportive" rather than pivotal. Calling it evidence that 60mg fails is a mischaracterization, and calling it evidence against dose-response is worse, because its own design *is* a dose escalation.

**Cochrane says three different things about dose and they must not be merged:**

| Cochrane statement | Dose range | Indication | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| "no significant difference nor a dose effect in the RR of improvement with increasing doses of duloxetine **from 40 mg to 120 mg**" | 40→120mg | Diabetic neuropathy | Flat above 40mg. Note the range **starts at 40mg**; it says nothing about 20mg. |
| "no dose effect exists from 60 mg to 120 mg" | 60→120mg | Fibromyalgia | Flat above 60mg. |
| "doses of 60 mg and 120 mg daily are efficacious… **but lower daily doses are not**" | below 60mg | Diabetic neuropathy | A threshold below 60mg. |

The first of those three is the one usually quoted to argue that dose does not matter. It begins at 40mg. It is not about your dose.

One further Cochrane finding neither earlier pass had: "Removal of the Gao 2010 data removed the heterogeneity contributed by this study and the data then **indicated a dose effect** (MD −1.16, 95% CI −1.49 to −0.83; 3 trials, 612 participants)" `[primary, verified]`.

**The honest shape** is a threshold with a fragile plateau. Below 40mg the analgesic effect is reliably absent, 0 for 3 across roughly 350 patients, twice with adequate power. At 40 to 60mg the effect appears, across two indications. Above 60mg it is flat. But at 60mg the effect is small enough that well-powered trials miss the primary while still showing benefit on responder analyses, which is what Murakami and Chappell both look like.

Welsch 2018 is fibromyalgia-specific, more recent (2018 against 2014), larger, and reaches a **less favourable** conclusion than the review this project spent seven passes arguing over. Not one of the four earlier passes used it, including the pass that retrieved the mixed-indication review in full. The consequence for you is that duloxetine's fibromyalgia effect is very likely **smaller** than the NNTB 8 you may have seen quoted.

Against that upside sits **NNTH 18** for stopping because of side effects `[primary, verified]`.

### Dose escalation: the direct evidence

**Kornstein 2008** (PMID 19193339), NCT00191061. Design: MDD patients received duloxetine 60mg/day for six weeks; those not in remission (HAM-D-17 > 7) were randomly reassigned, double-blind, to continue 60mg or escalate to 120mg for eight weeks `[primary, verified]`.

| Arm | Remission rate |
|---|---|
| Non-remitters continued on 60mg | **30.0%** |
| Non-remitters escalated to 120mg | **30.5%** |

Authors' conclusion, verbatim: "Nonremitters to 60 mg of duloxetine for 6 weeks randomly reassigned to 60 mg or 120 mg of duloxetine demonstrated continued symptom improvement in the 8-week extension. **Patients randomly reassigned to 120 mg showed no advantage over those who continued on 60 mg.**" `[primary, verified]`

A citation note, because the publisher nearly caused an error here. The publisher's own page renders the author list with **Dunner** first; PubMed gives **Kornstein**. Cite Kornstein. This is the second author-order discrepancy this project has caught, and both were introduced by publisher pages rather than by PubMed.

**Dold 2017** (PMID 28903107). Seven double-blind RCTs, eight study arms, 1,208 participants (999 pooled). Drugs: fluoxetine (2 studies, 448), sertraline (2, 272), paroxetine (2, 146), **duloxetine (1, 255)**, maprotiline (1, 87) `[primary abstract only]`.

- Primary: **Hedges g = −0.04 (95% CI −0.20 to 0.12), p = 0.63**. "Dose escalation was not more efficacious in HAM-D total score reduction than maintaining standard-dose treatment," neither pooled nor for any individual drug.
- Secondary: no differences in response rates, all-cause discontinuation, or discontinuation due to inefficacy. But "**significantly more patients in the dose escalation group dropped out due to adverse effects**."
- Authors' conclusion: dose escalation after initial non-response "**cannot be regarded as general evidence-based treatment option**" in unipolar depression.

**Ruhé 2006** (PMID 17041180), the honest steelman. Eight dose-escalation studies, three meta-analyses. "Dose escalation before 4 weeks of treatment at a standard dose appeared to be ineffective." After eight weeks, "some, but limited, evidence for efficacy… particularly in **partial responders**." Verdict: escalation is "equivocally supported by evidence"; the authors could "neither unequivocally state that dose escalation is useful nor discard it as useless." And: "**dose escalation unequivocally increased side-effects**" `[primary abstract only]`.

**Franchini 2000** (PMID 10980329) is the only study addressing escalation after a *faded* response, which is closer to a question you might one day have. 57 highly recurrent unipolar patients who had recurred on prior SSRI prophylaxis were re-treated at full doses: **51 (89.5%) responded**, no relapses over four months of continuation, then 24 months of maintenance at those doses, during which **28 of 51 (54.9%) recurred again** `[primary abstract only]`. The interesting part nobody quotes: the second recurrences were **significantly less severe** (25.1 → 21.6, P<0.0001), which is not what pharmacological tolerance predicts. Caveats: n=51, no control group, open-label, and the cohort was selected for having just recurred, so regression to the mean is maximal `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

**Prophylactic escalation**, meaning raising the dose in someone who is currently responding to protect against future loss of response, **has never been studied** `[no evidence exists]`. That is not "the evidence is weak." The question has never been asked in a trial. The strongest available statement is a syllogism rather than a study: escalation does not help non-responders; escalation after loss of response restores it in most and holds it in fewer than half, uncontrolled; side-effect burden rises with dose in every dataset; therefore escalating prophylactically incurs the one effect that is reliably dose-dependent to purchase a benefit never demonstrated in anyone `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

The sharp point for you: **escalating for pain has a real if modest evidence base (NNTB 11). Escalating to protect the anxiety and episode response has none.** Those are two different questions and they should not be merged.

### What was never asked about 20mg

**Nicolini 2009** (PMID 18485261), verified arm sizes:

| Arm | n |
|---|---|
| Placebo | 170 |
| **Duloxetine 20mg (fixed)** | **84** |
| Duloxetine 60–120mg | 158 |
| Venlafaxine XR 75–225mg | 169 |

Verbatim results `[primary, verified]`:

> "Each of the three active treatment groups had significantly greater improvements on HAMA total score from baseline to endpoint compared with placebo (p=0.01–0.001)."
>
> "For the HAMA psychic factor score, **both duloxetine treatment arms** and venlafaxine XR demonstrated significantly greater improvement compared with placebo (p=0.01–0.001)."
>
> "For the HAMA somatic factor score, the mean improvement in the duloxetine 60–120 mg and venlafaxine XR groups was significantly greater than placebo (p≤0.05 and p≤0.01 respectively), whose mean improvement **did not differ from the duloxetine 20 mg group (p=0.07)**."

That third sentence is grammatically awkward and load-bearing. "Whose" refers to placebo. It says placebo's improvement did not differ from duloxetine 20mg's, at p=0.07. So **20mg did not separate on the somatic factor**.

**The Allgulander reconciliation**, which establishes that the 20mg arm was excluded from the non-inferiority analysis `[primary, verified]`:

| | Nicolini 2009 (verified) | Allgulander pooled (verified) |
|---|---|---|
| Placebo | 170 | **331** |
| Duloxetine 60–120mg | 158 | **320** |
| Venlafaxine XR | 169 | **333** |
| **Duloxetine 20mg** | **84** | **EXCLUDED** |

Every column reconciles to the unit. Allgulander 2008's abstract mentions only "duloxetine 60–120 mg/day" throughout. **Non-inferiority was tested for 60–120mg against venlafaxine only. The 20mg arm was never tested for non-inferiority against anything.**

**A symmetry point stated against this page's own convenience.** The somatic null at 20mg comes from an 84-patient arm against a 158-patient comparator, at p=0.07, which is a near-miss in an arm roughly half the size of its comparator. That ratio is *worse* than the one this project rejected as an excuse when it appeared in the pain trials. So the honest statement is symmetric: **20mg separated from placebo on HAM-A total and psychic factor, and missed on somatic at p=0.07, in an arm that was half the size of its comparator and was never compared to it.** The somatic null is weak evidence of absence rather than a demonstrated failure.

**A guardrail that must not be dropped.** Li 2018 found duloxetine improves HAM-A **psychic** but not **somatic** anxiety across all doses, eight RCTs, 2,399 patients `[primary, verified]`. So the psychic/somatic split is a dose-independent property of the drug, not a 20mg-specific weakness. And critically: **the HAM-A "somatic factor" is a list of physical symptom items (cardiovascular, gastrointestinal, respiratory, autonomic). It is not your "anxiety without negative cognitive content."** Mapping you onto it would repeat a category error this project has already made once.

The only duloxetine trial with posted results containing a fixed 20mg arm, established by scanning all 135 duloxetine trials with posted results `[primary, verified — retrieved from the ClinicalTrials.gov results registry via API]`.

**The convergent result across three independent trials and four populations:** insomnia does not rise from 20 to 60 anywhere; it rises at 120mg. Somnolence rises, and it rises at the 30-to-60 step. Fatigue rises across 20 to 60 in the low-back-pain trial but is flat 30 to 60 in adolescents, so it is mixed.

**The Athens Insomnia Scale data, and why not to trust it** `[primary, verified]`:

| Arm | AIS change | vs placebo |
|---|---|---|
| Placebo | −1.23 (0.38) | — |
| 20mg | −1.43 (0.53) | p = 0.747 |
| **60mg** | **−2.30 (0.39)** | **p = 0.045** |
| 120mg | −0.93 (0.40) | p = 0.580 |
| | | **60 vs 120: p = 0.012** |

Seven things are wrong with that table and they are why it is not a finding. These are secondary endpoints **in a trial that failed its primary** (no dose beat placebo on 24-hour average pain). There is **no multiplicity correction** across six pairwise comparisons, so p=0.045 and p=0.012 would very likely not survive it. The 20mg arm is n=59, roughly half the others. The population is chronic low back pain. It is Lilly-sponsored. The 120mg arm lost 27 of 112 to adverse events, so its week-13 completers are enriched for people who tolerated it, which **flatters** 120mg and means the real 120mg harm is plausibly worse than shown. And decisively, **it does not replicate**: NCT00424593 found −2.01 vs −1.50 at p=0.329 (and at week 54, duloxetine was numerically *worse*), and NCT00408993 found −3.58 vs −3.31 at p=0.590 `[all primary, verified]`.

**The largest dataset**, Zhou 2023 (PMID 37422714), a dose-effect network meta-analysis of 216 trials and 64,696 patients `[primary, partially verified]`. Duloxetine vs placebo: somnolence OR 3.02 (2.31–3.95); insomnia OR 1.96 (1.60–2.42). Abstract, verbatim: "The dose-effect relationships curves between somnolence or insomnia and dose included **linear shape, inverted U-shape, and other shapes**." Body: "the risks of somnolence and insomnia were **not always increased linearly** along with the dose increase." Duloxetine's own curves: somnolence bell/inverted-U; insomnia plateau. Against it: MDD trials only, adverse-event reporting rather than measured sleep, and it is a model-based inference from aggregate across-trial data rather than a within-trial dose comparison, so it inherits every between-trial confound. It also partly disagrees with NCT00408876, which found insomnia doubling at 120mg in a **within-trial randomized** comparison, which on that specific point is the stronger design.

**The folk version is wrong.** "Low dose sedating, high dose activating" is not supported. The actual finding is stranger: duloxetine causes insomnia **and** somnolence in the same population at the same time. In the ME/CFS trial, 34% insomnia and 41% somnolence, simultaneously, in 29 people `[primary, verified]`. The FDA label lists both among the reactions with "a significant dose-dependent relationship in fixed-dose studies" `[primary, verified]`. These are not opposite ends of one dose axis. They are two different effects happening to different people on the same drug.

**On timing**, which you may be offered as a solution: the FDA label says once-daily duloxetine may be taken without regard to time of day `[primary, verified]`. There is **no trial of morning versus evening duloxetine dosing with sleep outcomes** `[no evidence exists]`. The standard advice is convention, not evidence. And a specific warning: the claim that "morning dosing reduces insomnia risk by approximately 60%" appears on commercial health-content sites with no citation and no traceable primary. **Treat it as fabricated until someone produces the study** `[no evidence exists]`.

### The sensory gating ladder

| Study | PMID | Dose | n | P50 sensory gating | Habituation | PPI | MMN |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Jensen 2008** | **18000656** | **10mg** | 21 | **NO EFFECT** | — | — | — |
| Oranje 2008 | 17971261 | 10mg | 18 | — | — | — | **INCREASED** |
| Jensen 2007 | 17299503 | 10mg | 18 | (not measured) | **DECREASED** | — | — |
| **Oranje 2011** | **20971512** | **15mg** | 20 | **DECREASED** | **DECREASED** | no effect | — |
| Wienberg 2010 | 19304862 | 15mg | — | — | — | — | **INCREASED** |

Jensen 2008, verbatim `[primary, verified]`: "**Escitalopram did not affect P50 suppression** but was found to increase P50 amplitude to the first (or conditioning) stimulus." Conclusion: "escitalopram did not affect P50 suppression in healthy male volunteers, which indicates that **sensory gating is not affected by a nonspecific increase in serotonergic activity**."

Oranje 2011, verbatim `[primary, verified]`: "In previous studies, we found that **a low dose of 10mg escitalopram (SSRI) disrupts habituation, without affecting sensory and sensorimotor gating** in healthy volunteers. In the current study **a higher dose of 15 mg** was used… **Escitalopram significantly decreased P50 suppression and habituation**, but had no effect on PPI."

The composite picture across the set: **detects more, filters less, stops responding more slowly.** MMN is elevated at both doses, habituation is delayed at both doses, and P50 gating breaks only at the higher one.

**The limits, in full.** It is a cross-study comparison, two papers rather than one dose-response experiment, and cross-study comparison presented as measurement is this project's single most repeated error. The mitigation is real but partial: same lab, same paradigm, same population, same design, consecutive studies, explicitly framed by the authors as a dose extension. That is about as clean as cross-study gets and it is still not within-study. There is **no independent replication**; a PubMed author search returns exactly this group and no others `[primary, verified — by absence]`. There are three papers in total on serotonin and P50 suppression in healthy volunteers, from two labs, and the second lab (Mann 2008, PMID 17895917) ran the opposite manipulation: "Selective depletion of dopamine had no significant effect on either PPI or P50 suppression, whereas **selective serotonin depletion significantly disrupted PPI, but not P50 suppression**" `[primary, verified]`. Lowering serotonin breaks PPI and spares P50; raising serotonin breaks P50 and spares PPI. **Those are mirror images and they do not describe a single dial.** Mann's authors invoke "theories relating to **optimal levels** of monoaminergic neurotransmission," and an inverted-U with different measures on each limb is the more honest reading `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

Finally: healthy male volunteers, n≈20, **single acute doses**, laboratory EEG paradigms, and escitalopram rather than duloxetine. The leap from a P50 waveform to a crowded festival is enormous and unbridged `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

**On "transiently."** An earlier pass predicted a dose increase would transiently re-open the acute window. The prediction's *direction* now has direct human dose-comparison support that the pass did not have. The word **"transiently" does not**: it came from a chronic-adaptation account that fails for independent reasons, and every gating study is single-dose, so nothing in the literature speaks to duration `[no evidence exists]`.

### The taper arithmetic

Duloxetine's SERT occupancy is hyperbolic in dose, with a fitted ED50 of **7.9mg** (Takano 2006, PMID 16506079). Applying a standard Emax model where occupancy = dose ÷ (dose + 7.9) `[reasoning, not evidence]`:

| Dose | Modelled SERT occupancy |
|---|---|
| 60mg | ~88% |
| 30mg | ~79% |
| **20mg** | **~72%** |
| 10mg | ~56% |
| 5mg | ~39% |
| 0mg | 0% |

- **60mg → 20mg**: 40 milligrams, about **16 occupancy points**, capsules at every step.
- **20mg → 0mg**: 20 milligrams, about **72 occupancy points**, **no capsule at any step**.

Half the milligrams, more than four times the biological distance, and nothing to stand on. The entire steep region of the curve lies underneath the smallest dose form the manufacturer makes.

**Grade this honestly.** The ED50 comes from a study using roughly three subjects per dose, single-dose administration rather than steady state, and thalamic measurement only. The modelled values track the measured ones well at 20mg and 60mg, which is reassuring but not independent confirmation, because the model was fitted to those measurements. The *shape* is established pharmacology and not in dispute. The *precise* occupancy figures below 20mg have never been measured in anyone, because nobody has ever given a person 5mg of duloxetine in a PET scanner `[no evidence exists]`.

The consequence for this page: the taper problem exists **right now**, at your current dose. It was true before this appointment was scheduled and it does not depend on the outcome. Going up adds a well-provisioned ramp in front of a cliff that is already there and does not get taller. "Going up makes coming down harder" is true at the margin and misleading as a headline, and it is the softest of the available reasons to stay at 20mg. This page does not offer it as one.

What *is* worth doing at the appointment, regardless of the dose decision: **ask whether this prescriber will write a compounded liquid.** Also worth knowing: **alternate-day dosing is precisely the wrong strategy** at a roughly 12-hour half-life, and it is the most likely piece of bad advice you will be offered. Modelling on this point is explicit: "increasing the dose interval at minimum therapeutic doses, or even half of that dose, likely **increases** the risk of withdrawal symptoms and cannot be recommended as a prudent strategy for tapering" `[primary, verified]`.

### POTS, dose-qualified

| Drug / dose | NET occupancy | Measured heart-rate effect |
|---|---|---|
| Duloxetine 20mg | 29.7% (human PET) | — |
| Duloxetine 60mg | 40.0% (human PET) | — |
| Duloxetine 60–120mg | ~40–50% | **+2.22 bpm** (Park 2020, 17 trials) |
| Duloxetine 80–240mg | ~51–76% | "POTS-like phenotype" (Vincent 2004, **unverified against the source**) |
| Atomoxetine 40mg | ~90% (derived) | **+16 bpm standing, in POTS** (Green 2013) |
| Reboxetine 8mg | near-complete | **+35 bpm at 75° head-up tilt** (Schroeder 2002) |

That progression, +2.2 → +16 → +35 bpm across roughly 30% → 90% → near-complete occupancy, is a coherent dose-response rather than a contradiction `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

The primary sources, verified:

- **Mar 2014** (PMID 24227635), verbatim: "**High-dose duloxetine can cause healthy volunteers to develop a POTS-like phenotype with increased orthostatic tachycardia after chronic administration** that is likely related to norepinephrine transporter inhibition (Vincent et al., 2004)." `[primary, verified]`
- **2015 HRS consensus statement** (PMID 25980576): **Class III, Level B-R**: "Drugs that block the norepinephrine reuptake transporter can worsen symptoms in patients with POTS and should not be administered." `[primary, verified]` Class III is the "net harm, do not administer" class. Corroborated by the 2020 Canadian Cardiovascular Society position statement.
- **The true primary is Vincent 2004** (PMID 15184278): duloxetine at **80, 120 and 240 mg/day**, chronic, randomized placebo-controlled, 15 healthy volunteers. Full text could not be retrieved; the POTS-like-phenotype attribution rests on Mar 2014's secondary characterization of it. **That is the single biggest remaining gap in this material and it is flagged rather than smoothed over** `[secondary — unverified]`.
- **Stevens 2008** (PMID 18728105): 26-year-old man, duloxetine **20 mg/day**, HR 110–120, **positive dechallenge and positive rechallenge**, Naranjo "probable." Authors' conclusion: "clinically significant tachycardia… **even in low doses**." `[primary, verified]`
- **Wernicke 2007** (PMID 17472422): 42 studies, 8,504 patients, **no sustained blood-pressure elevation** `[primary, verified]`.
- **Schroeder 2006** (PMID 16875978): tilt tolerance 29±2 → 35±1 min, p=0.001; **odds ratio 0.22 (95% CI 0.09–0.55)** for premature tilt termination `[primary, verified]`. Note this is an OR, not a 78% risk reduction, and the same study also reports that NET inhibition "elicited a pressor response and **increased upright heart rate**." The duality is *within* single studies, which is what makes the direction genuinely uncertain rather than merely unknown.
- **Garner & Baraniuk 2019** (PMID 31159884): n=39 CFS; POTS in **6 of 39** while 72% had orthostatic symptoms; "**orthostatic tachycardia did not account for OI symptoms in CFS**" `[primary, verified]`.
- **Arnold 2015** (PMID 25660434 / NCT00375973): dizziness **31.03% vs 6.67%**; lightheadedness 17.24% vs 10.00% `[primary, verified — retrieved from the registry; the journal AE table was not retrievable]`. Context that changes the reading: **no tachycardia, palpitations, syncope or orthostatic hypotension term appears anywhere in the table** at the 5% threshold; **headache ran the opposite way, 10.34% on duloxetine against 40.00% on placebo**; no statistical test is attached to any row; and 31.03% vs 6.67% is **9 events against 2** on n=29/30.

**Two claims that must not be repeated.** "SSRIs are preferred over SNRIs in POTS" is **invalid as a guideline claim**: the HRS statement never mentions either class in the POTS section, and the only source is Mar 2014's own speculation, whose own data refute it (n=39 POTS, sertraline 50mg vs placebo: standing HR and systolic BP not significantly different at 4 hours, and **symptoms were worse with sertraline than placebo**) `[primary, verified]`. And the phrasing "trigger the onset of symptoms in individuals predisposed to autonomic dysfunction" traces to a content-farm page rather than to primary literature `[no evidence exists]`.

**The symmetry rule.** If low NET occupancy is invoked to dismiss a benefit, the same low occupancy cannot be ignored when asserting a harm. The fix is not to drop either claim. It is to dose-qualify both. Note also that brain PET occupancy is arguably the wrong proxy for the cardiovascular effect at all, because the orthostatic effect of NET inhibition is substantially **peripheral**, at cardiac sympathetic nerve terminals outside the blood-brain barrier `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

### Arguments that died

| Argument | Status | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **"Emotional blunting is dose-dependent, so don't go up"** | **DEAD** | No dose-response study exists. Ma 2021's dose sentence cites **Price 2009, a qualitative interview study** that never measured dose; Ma 2021 and Masdrakis 2023 both root to the **same 1990 case report**. **Aşçibaşi 2020 (n=98): doses systematically RAISED, blunting scores FELL, P<0.001.** Goodwin 2017 (PMID 28628765): 46%, **no difference by ANY agent**. An earlier pass called this "the strongest argument against a dose increase." It collapses. |
| **"30mg is the efficient dose"** | **DEAD** | **Contradicted by a direct RCT.** Arnold 2012 (PMID 22971669): 30mg, n=155/153, adequately powered, pain P=0.202. Derived from PET modelling; nobody checked whether it had been tested. |
| **"NE rises at 60mg, therefore sleep worsens"** | **DEAD** | **Outcome data contradicts it.** In the only fixed-dose 20/60/120 trial, insomnia went *down* 20→60 and the validated insomnia scale got *better* at 60mg. Built on an n=2 occupancy point with a CI of −20% to +100%. When mechanism and outcome data disagree, trust the outcome data. |
| **"Going up makes coming down harder"** | **WEAK, not dead** | True at the margin. The cliff is below 20mg and he is already on it. 60→20 costs ~16 occupancy points with capsules; 20→0 costs ~72 with none. |
| **"Murakami 2015 shows 60mg fails pain"** | **FALSE** | Separated on ≥30% and ≥50% responder rates and on 6 of 7 pain secondaries. |
| **"Chappell 2008 shows 60/120mg failed"** | **FALSE** | Not a 60mg trial. 84% blindly escalated to 120mg at week 13; mean dose 113.4mg. Its design *is* a dose escalation. |
| **"~66 patients ever received 20mg"** | **REFUTED** | **~193** (Goldstein ≈114 + Russell 79), both verified per-arm. Sultan's "66" sits inside a ≥50%-pain-relief subsection and its meaning is unresolved; **do not attempt to explain it.** The refutation does not depend on the explanation. |
| **"20mg is sub-therapeutic on both transporters"** | **DEAD** | Single-dose-versus-steady-state confusion plus cross-region comparison. He is at ~75% striatal at steady state. |
| **"POTS risk at 20mg"** | **DOSE-QUALIFIED** | Traces to 80–240 mg/day. Real, and not a 20mg claim. |
| **"The 1.5% somnolence rate"** | **WRONG COLUMN** | That is a **discontinuation** rate. The label's dose-dependent incidence is 10–11% vs 3%. |
| **"Morning dosing cuts insomnia risk ~60%"** | **UNSOURCED** | No citation, no traceable primary. Treat as fabricated until someone produces the study. |

### The trap, and what to measure

The two models and their predictions:

| | At 20mg (now) | At 60mg |
|---|---|---|
| **Standard model** (pain needs the 40–60mg noradrenergic threshold) | Pain does **not** improve | Pain improves |
| **His model** (crashes aggravate CFS/fibro; preventing them improves pain slowly) | Pain improves **slowly** | Pain improves |
| **Distinguishable?** | **YES** | **NO** |

**Going to 60mg satisfies both models and destroys the experiment permanently.** If pain improves at 60mg, there is no way to know whether it was norepinephrine or the accumulated absence of crashes, because the two causes will have been applied simultaneously and irreversibly.

**The separating observation, free and available at any dose:** his model must run through the fatigue limb, because Okifuji 2011 (PMID 20591745) found the **direct** distress→pain path was **null**; only distress→fatigue→pain survived `[primary, verified]`. **Track fatigue and pain separately. If pain improves while fatigue does not, the model is dead.**

**A second free falsifier:** under the crash-protection account, a breakthrough crash should leave a durable multi-week pain signature. A breakthrough crash whose pain resolves in days falsifies it.

**Where his hypothesis stands, honestly:**

| Claim | Status |
|---|---|
| 1. Overload triggers crashes | **ESTABLISHED as clinical description.** Canadian Consensus Criteria 2003, verbatim: "**Overload phenomena may trigger a 'crash'** where the patient becomes temporarily immobilized" `[primary, verified]` |
| 2. Crashes aggravate the illness | **NO EVIDENCE.** Never studied. This is the load-bearing limb. `[no evidence exists]` |
| 3. Therefore preventing crashes improves pain | **SPECULATION.** Never tested. `[no evidence exists]` |

**Three places adjacent evidence leans against it, stated because honesty requires it:** allostatic load in CFS correlates *inversely* with fatigue duration (r=−.26, p=.047), so cumulative damage predicts the opposite `[primary abstract only]`; prospectively, the allostatic limb that predicts pain is **metabolic**, while parasympathetic dysregulation predicted nothing, and his model runs entirely through the limb that fails `[primary, verified]`; and Okifuji's direct distress→pain path was null `[primary, verified]`.

**Why it survives anyway:** it is killed by absence of research rather than by logic. His exact intersection (autistic, sensory-triggered crashes, ME/CFS, fibromyalgia) is the population these studies exclude or never assemble. A PubMed search for `"autistic burnout" AND ("chronic fatigue" OR "ME/CFS" OR "post-exertional")` returns **one result** `[primary, verified — search-level]`.

**The four variables:**

| Variable | Started | Acts at | Interpretable at the appointment? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Duloxetine 20mg | ~2 weeks ago | Early effects attenuate over weeks | Partially. Still moving. |
| Melatonin | Ongoing, nightly | Immediate | Constant background |
| **Oral iron** | **This week** | **~12 weeks** | **No. Cannot explain anything at the appointment.** |
| Dose change | Proposed | Days to weeks | Would make four |

The point is not that a dose change is wrong. It is that with four variables moving and one observer, most results will not be interpretable, so the decision worth making first is **what to measure**, not **what to change** `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

### What to raise at the appointment

Framed as questions, because the decision is yours and your prescriber's and this page does not make it.

1. "The 20mg arm in Goldstein's trial had the same number of patients as the 60mg arm that worked. Is there any reason to think the pain threshold isn't real for me?"
2. "The fibromyalgia-specific Cochrane review gives NNTB 11 with 'no clinically relevant benefit.' Is that the number you'd use?"
3. "If we go up, what exactly are we going up **for**? Pain, or something else? Because the evidence for those two is completely different."
4. "The only ME/CFS trial of this drug found 41% somnolence at 60 to 120mg. My main disability is fatigue. How would we tell drug sleepiness apart from my ME/CFS?"
5. "Will you write a compounded liquid if I ever need to taper below 20mg?" (Ask regardless of today's decision.)
6. "Can we do a ten-minute active stand test before deciding? The POTS question depends on which orthostatic phenotype I have, and nobody has checked."
7. "If we change the dose now, iron and duloxetine and melatonin are all still moving. Should we set what we're measuring first?"
