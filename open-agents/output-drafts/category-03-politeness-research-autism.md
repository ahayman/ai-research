# Politeness Theory and Autism: Research for an English Pragmatics Knowledge Base

## Research Overview

This document synthesizes research on the intersection of politeness theory (particularly Brown & Levinson's framework) and autism spectrum experiences. The purpose is to inform a knowledge base teaching English pragmatics to autistic adults -- grounded in neurodiversity-affirming principles while providing practical understanding of politeness as a communicative tool.

---

## 1. How Autistic Individuals Relate to the Politeness System

### 1.1 Production of Politeness Markers

Research consistently finds that autistic individuals use fewer conventional politeness markers in their speech compared to non-autistic peers, though the picture is more nuanced than early deficit-focused research suggested.

**The Voicemail Elicitation Task (VET) Study:** A study published in the Journal of the International Neuropsychological Society compared young adults with ASD (n=22) and neurotypical controls (n=10) using employment-specific role-play scenarios. Results showed that "individuals with ASD show deficits in politeness marker use which could impact their interpersonal relationships within the workplace environment." Politeness markers in this study included non-tangible hedging words such as "would," "could," "may," or "might" -- words that serve a purely social rather than informational function.
- Source: Cambridge Core, Journal of the International Neuropsychological Society

**The Tilburg University Study:** Tim Butterbrod's thesis at Tilburg University, "The Effect of Autistic Traits on the Use of Politeness Strategies," found that individuals with ASD used "significantly lower overall politeness markers compared to non-autistic individuals, with differences primarily driven by lower use of negative politeness markers." This is a critical finding: the deficit is specifically in *negative* politeness (the strategies that show deference, hedge impositions, and minimize the speaker's intrusion) rather than necessarily in *positive* politeness (warmth, friendliness, solidarity).
- Source: Butterbrod, Tilburg University (arno.uvt.nl/show.cgi?fid=133361)

**Predicting Pragmatic Discourse Features (2022):** A computational linguistics study collected transcribed natural conversations where ASD and non-ASD participants completed collaborative tasks with neurotypical peers, investigating three pragmatic features: politeness, uncertainty, and informativeness. The study used annotated datasets rated on three-point scales and achieved high accuracy (F1 > 0.82) in predicting pragmatic features from language, confirming that measurable differences exist in how autistic adults deploy these features in conversation.
- Source: PMC (pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9633181/)

### 1.2 Fewer Hedges, Softeners, and Indirect Forms

The evidence strongly supports that autistic individuals tend to use fewer hedging strategies, softeners, and indirect speech forms:

- **Hedging and uncertainty markers:** Autistic speech tends to contain fewer epistemic hedges ("I think," "maybe," "sort of," "it seems like") and more direct assertions. This relates to both the autistic value placed on precision/accuracy and differences in theory of mind processing.

- **Indirect speech acts:** However, research complicates the simple "autistic people don't do indirect" narrative. A 2015 study in PLOS ONE (Kissine et al.) found that "children with autism complied with indirect requests and demonstrated the capacity to inhibit the directive interpretation in different contexts." A follow-up analysis found that "autistic individuals correctly interpret non-literal uses of language so long as such uses are conventionalized" -- e.g., understanding "Can you pass the salt?" as a request. The primary difficulty appears to be with *novel* or *unconventionalized* indirect speech, not indirect speech as a category.
  - Source: PMC (pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4638355/)

- **Direct vs. indirect production:** While comprehension of conventionalized indirect speech may be preserved, *production* of indirect forms remains lower. Autistic speakers are more likely to say "I need you to move" than "Would you mind moving a little?" -- not because they can't produce the indirect form, but because the direct form feels more honest and efficient.

### 1.3 Autistic Directness and the Perception of Impoliteness

**The core asymmetry:** Autistic directness is consistently misinterpreted as rudeness by neurotypical listeners. This is one of the most well-documented findings in the literature.

- "Direct and honest feedback from an autistic person might be perceived as rude by an allistic person who expects more softened language." (Relational Psych, on the Double Empathy Problem)
- "Neurotypical individuals may misinterpret an autistic person's lack of eye contact or difficulty understanding sarcasm as disinterest or rudeness." (Neurodivergent Insights)
- "This straightforward manner can be misinterpreted as rude, condescending, or aggressive, but autistic people highly value directness because it ensures mutual clarity." (Neurolaunch)

**Autistic self-awareness about this gap:** Research by Crompton and colleagues (Scandinavian Journal of Disability Research) found that "autistic people acknowledged that other people could find their own directness and honesty rude or upsetting, and that this could cause issues when interacting with others (especially non-autistic people)." Autistic individuals are often *aware* that their communication style is perceived differently -- they simply have principled reasons for maintaining it, viewing directness as a form of honesty and respect.

### 1.4 Awareness of Face-Threatening Acts

**The Alvarez & Wolfe (2024) Study:** This landmark study, published in the Journal of Social and Personal Relationships, examined face-threatening acts (FTAs) experienced by individuals with ASD using naturalistic data from Reddit. Key findings:

- Autistic individuals experienced **positive face threats** (attacks on their desire to be liked/accepted -- e.g., being made fun of for having ASD), **negative face threats** (attacks on their autonomy -- e.g., being told how to behave), and **hybrid FTAs** (simultaneous threats to both face types).
- In response, autistic individuals used **preventive strategies** (masking, hybrid politeness, choosing not to perform the FTA), **corrective strategies** (repairing face after a threat), and **future-oriented facework** (planning for anticipated threats).
- Critically, the study found that autistic people's communication differences "violated neurotypical norms" and resulted in "stigma and social isolation" -- meaning that autistic people *themselves* frequently experience face threats from NT society.
- Source: Alvarez & Wolfe, 2024 (journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/02654075231210029)

**The Sirota (2004) Study:** Karen Gainer Sirota's ethnographic study of 8-to-12-year-old children with autism and Asperger's found that they "were able to produce a wide range of positive politeness strategies in family interaction with varying degrees of competence." This challenges the simplistic view that autistic individuals "lack" politeness -- rather, their politeness is context-dependent, more variable, and may look different from NT expectations.
- Source: Sirota, 2004, Discourse Studies (journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1461445604041769)

**The Tikiz-Erturk (2025) Study:** This qualitative case study of a 15-year-old ASD language learner found that despite "persistent challenges in comprehending abstract concepts, conversational implicature, and social role recognition," the participant "employed several adaptive strategies, including politeness strategies, off-record communication, and explicit verbal markers." Over time, the participant showed progress in using both bald-on-record and off-record approaches, as well as positive and negative politeness techniques, though gains were context-dependent and most evident in structured interactions.
- Source: Tikiz-Erturk, 2025, International Journal of Applied Linguistics (onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/ijal.12783)

## 2. The "Rudeness" Misperception

### 2.1 Autistic Directness Misinterpreted as Rudeness

The research literature documents a consistent pattern: autistic communication that is intended as honest, clear, and helpful is received by neurotypical listeners as rude, blunt, or socially inappropriate.

**Key findings:**

- Autistic individuals "tend to be more direct in their communication, using less subtlety or coded language. This may come off as rude or abrasive at times, but often it is simply a cultural difference, and not intended as rude." (Neurolaunch)
- "Non-autistic colleagues misinterpreting direct, honest and/or literal communication as rude or blunt" is a primary source of workplace conflict. (PMC workplace study)
- "While non-autistic people often prioritize social norms in an interaction, such as engaging in small talk and following a linear, reciprocal conversation, autistic people tend to value direct conversations and the ability to share ideas and information inherently interesting to them." (Reframing Autism)

**The communication mismatch:** The fundamental issue is a values conflict, not a skills deficit. NT communication prioritizes relationship maintenance through indirectness; autistic communication prioritizes information accuracy through directness. Neither is inherently superior -- they serve different functions.

### 2.2 The Double Empathy Problem

**Milton's Double Empathy Problem (2012):** Damian Milton, an autistic autism researcher, proposed that "many of the difficulties autistic individuals face when socializing with non-autistic individuals are due, in part, to mismatch and a lack of mutual understanding between the two groups." The problem is bidirectional: "most autistic people struggle to understand and empathize with non-autistic people, whereas most non-autistic people also struggle to understand and empathize with autistic people."

This framework reframes the "rudeness" perception as a **mutual comprehension failure** rather than a one-sided deficit in the autistic person.

**Empirical support -- Crompton et al. (2020):** The landmark "telephone game" study recruited nine groups of eight people (three all-autistic, three all-non-autistic, three mixed). A story was told to the first person and passed along the chain. Results:
- Autistic-to-autistic information transfer was as effective as non-autistic-to-non-autistic transfer.
- Mixed groups showed significantly degraded information transfer.
- Rapport was higher within diagnostic groups than between them.
- Source: Crompton et al., 2020, Autism (journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1362361320919286)

**The 2025 Nature Human Behaviour replication:** Crompton et al. published a larger-scale replication confirming that "both autistic and non-autistic people perform comparably well at information transfer, but rapport is higher with others of the same neurotype."
- Source: Nature Human Behaviour, 2025 (nature.com/articles/s41562-025-02163-z)

### 2.3 Workplace Studies on Communication and Social Friction

**Szechy, Turk & O'Donnell (2024):** Published in Autism in Adulthood, this study applied the Double Empathy Problem to workplace contexts. Key finding: "A significantly greater proportion of autistic participants (50.7%) accurately interpreted the behavior of an autistic employee compared with non-autistic participants (31.2%)." This demonstrates that the "misreading" goes both ways -- non-autistic colleagues are *more likely* to misinterpret autistic behavior than autistic colleagues are.
- Source: PMC (pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11317796/)

**Employment statistics:** The unemployment rate among autistic adults is estimated as high as 85%, with social communication differences being a major barrier. Workplace challenges include:
- "Difficulties with social communication styles can make interacting with colleagues difficult for autistic people, which may be misinterpreted by others in the workplace, leading to negative perceptions." (Autism.org.uk)
- "Autistic people tend to be primarily motivated to achieve work goals... whereas typical people often focus to a greater extent on social goals." (Attwood & Garnett)
- Autistic employees may be penalized for "soft skills deficits" that are actually communication style differences.

**SkillPath's reframing:** "Rather than autistic employees needing to adopt neurotypical politeness conventions, some suggest that colleagues should meet autistic people in the middle by trying to say what they mean, without passive aggression or expecting them to read between the lines."
- Source: SkillPath (skillpath.com/blog/autistic-communication-in-workplace-not-soft-skills-problem)

### 2.4 The Social Cost of Being Perceived as "Rude"

The consequences of the rudeness misperception are severe and well-documented:

- **Social isolation:** "On a large scale, the double empathy problem leads to social stigma, social exclusion, mistrust, victimization, and discrimination of autistics." (Wikipedia, Double Empathy Problem)
- **Employment loss:** Autistic adults face barriers not from inability to do job tasks but from violation of unspoken social norms.
- **Mental health impact:** The pressure to adopt NT politeness norms to avoid these consequences drives masking behavior, which is associated with depression, anxiety, burnout, and suicidal ideation (see Section 3.4).
- **Relationship damage:** Autistic directness in personal relationships can be misread as lack of caring, leading to relationship breakdown even when the autistic person's intent was to be helpful.

## 3. Face and Autistic Identity

### 3.1 Do Autistic Individuals Experience "Face" Differently?

Brown & Levinson's (1987) politeness theory defines "face" as the public self-image that every person wants to claim for themselves, consisting of two components:

- **Positive face:** The desire to be liked, admired, ratified, and related to positively.
- **Negative face:** The desire not to be imposed upon; the desire for autonomy and freedom from imposition.

The research suggests that autistic individuals **do** experience face, but with different weightings and expressions than the NT norm.

### 3.2 Positive Face: Being Liked vs. Authenticity

For neurotypical individuals, positive face needs are typically expressed through conformity to social expectations, agreement, compliment-giving, and in-group signaling. For many autistic individuals, positive face is experienced differently:

- **Authenticity over approval:** Crompton's research found that autistic social communication values center on "being honest, direct, clear, and authentic." Many autistic participants "felt that being inauthentic during interactions -- even for the sake of politeness -- was a form of deception, and therefore negative." This means that *performing* positive face work (e.g., giving compliments you don't mean, expressing enthusiasm you don't feel) can actually *damage* an autistic person's sense of self.

- **Connection through information-sharing:** Autistic positive face may be better served by "interacting for a specific purpose and talking about topics of interest" and "respecting other's differences by developing shared, negotiated interactional patterns in relationships" (Sutherland et al., 2025). The autistic version of "being liked" is more about being understood and accepted for who you are than about receiving social approval for performing correctly.

- **Values-based identity:** Research from NeuroClastic describes autistic identity as deeply values-driven: "Autistic people seem more preoccupied with information, accuracy, solutions, truth, and correctness, while neurotypical people seem more preoccupied with status, power, appearance, gathering allies, and defining groups." This means threats to an autistic person's positive face may center on being seen as *dishonest* or *inaccurate* rather than as *unlikeable*.

### 3.3 Negative Face: Autonomy May Be STRONGER

There is strong evidence that negative face needs -- the desire for autonomy and freedom from imposition -- may be *heightened* in autistic individuals:

- **Sensory autonomy:** Autistic individuals often have strong sensory needs and boundaries. "Lack of genuine understanding can lead to misinterpretations of behavior, unintentional harm through sensory overload, or the imposition of neurotypical expectations that are inherently incompatible with an autistic person's way of processing the world." Impositions that NT individuals would consider minor (unexpected touch, loud environments, schedule changes) can be deeply threatening to autistic negative face.

- **PDA as extreme negative face protection:** Pathological Demand Avoidance (increasingly reframed as "Persistent Drive for Autonomy") represents an intensified form of negative face protection: "a powerful, intrinsic need to maintain control over one's environment, decisions, and self-expression. Unlike a general preference for independence, PDA functions as a deep-seated compulsion to avoid or resist situations that feel unsafe, restrictive or imposed." 
  - Source: theautismservice.co.uk; taniarose.net

- **Resistance to arbitrary social demands:** Autistic people often have particular difficulty with social demands that lack clear rationale. "Autistic people tend to communicate directly and literally and may not experience hierarchy and authority in the same way non-autistic individuals do." (NeuroClastic) This means that negative politeness strategies based on deference to status (e.g., "I was wondering if perhaps you might consider...") may feel dishonest to autistic speakers, while the *impositions* embedded in vague NT requests ("It would be nice if someone could...") may be confusing or anxiety-inducing to autistic listeners.

### 3.4 Masking and Camouflaging: Performing NT Politeness

The connection between politeness performance and masking/camouflaging is a critical area of research.

**What masking involves:**
Research identifies three main aspects of camouflaging (Hull et al., 2017, PMC):
1. **Compensation:** Using scripts, copying others' behavior, preparing responses in advance.
2. **Masking:** Constantly monitoring one's own behaviors (eye contact, facial expression, gesture) to present a non-autistic persona.
3. **Assimilation:** Behavioral techniques to fit in (e.g., forcing oneself to make small talk, performing expected social rituals).

**Politeness performance as a subset of masking:**
"Autistic adults describe camouflaging as essential for fulfilling professional responsibilities like turn-taking in meetings, maintaining expected politeness, and navigating office social dynamics." Participants describe feeling like they're "performing rather than being myself" in social situations.
- Source: PMC (pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8814950/)

**The mental health cost:**
Research consistently links higher levels of masking with:
- Increased depression, anxiety, burnout, and exhaustion
- "Diminished self-perception, autonomy, esteem, hope for the future, and extinguishment of specialized skills and passions"
- Higher risk of suicidal ideation, with anxiety appearing to be a key pathway
- "Masking demands an enormous amount of cognitive and emotional energy that could otherwise be spent on growth, creativity, or genuine connection"
- Source: PMC (pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6483965/); Frontiers in Psychiatry (frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2023.1108110/)

**Gender differences:** Autistic women and girls tend to mask more than autistic men and boys, scoring significantly higher on standardized measures of camouflaging. This may explain why autistic women are often diagnosed later -- their masking is more "successful" in NT terms, but at higher psychological cost.

**Implications for teaching politeness:** Any framework for teaching politeness to autistic individuals must grapple with the fact that performing NT politeness is a form of masking, and that masking has documented harmful effects. The question becomes: *how do you teach the system without requiring performance of it?*

## 4. Teaching Politeness to Autistic Individuals

### 4.1 Traditional Approaches

Traditional approaches to teaching "social skills" to autistic individuals have included:
- Social stories and scripts
- Role-playing exercises
- Video modeling
- Explicit instruction in politeness formulas ("say please," "say thank you")
- ABA-based social skills groups

These approaches have been **heavily criticized** by the neurodiversity community for multiple reasons.

### 4.2 The Neurodiversity Critique

**Therapist Neurodiversity Collective's position:** "Nothing about social skills training is neurodivergence-affirming." Their critique centers on the argument that social skills training "promotes therapy models that shame neurodivergent social differences and 'extinguish' diversity in social intelligence through training autistic people to mask and camouflage."

**Reframing Autism's analysis:** Traditional social skills courses make it seem like "Autistic people have to use learned non-autistic skills all the time, in all contexts." The critique points out that "social skills instruction has been criticized, particularly by those in the autistic community, as serving the needs of third parties (e.g., teachers) rather than the autistic student by teaching and reinforcing a neurotypical standard of social behavior and minimizing autistic perspectives."

**Reported harms:**
- Standardized programs "may teach skills that participants do not find relevant or necessary, potentially leading to adverse psychological effects such as paranoia and self-doubt."
- Masking taught through social skills training is "a trauma response to stigma and a coping strategy linked to increased anxiety, depression, and burnout."
- The implicit message -- that autistic ways of communicating are *wrong* and NT ways are *right* -- is damaging to identity and self-worth.

### 4.3 The Empowerment Approach: Understanding as a Tool, Not a Rule

A neurodiversity-affirming alternative reframes politeness knowledge as **empowerment through understanding**, not compliance through training.

**Key principles:**

1. **Name it accurately:** "Neuro-affirming social skills will own and name its specific purpose: teaching Autistic people non-autistic social skills, while validating Autistic social skills by recognizing that 'social skills' and 'non-autistic social skills' are not synonymous or equivalent." (Reframing Autism)

2. **Teach the system, not the performance:** Understanding *how* politeness works (the underlying mechanics of face, hedging, indirectness) is different from *requiring* its performance. An autistic person who understands that "Would you mind..." is a negative politeness strategy designed to minimize imposition has gained analytical knowledge they can choose to deploy strategically -- this is fundamentally different from being drilled to always say "Would you mind..."

3. **Contextualize when it matters:** Not all contexts require NT politeness equally. A job interview has different pragmatic requirements than a conversation with a close friend. Teaching when politeness strategies provide genuine social benefit (and when directness is valued) empowers autistic individuals to make informed choices.

4. **Support self-advocacy:** Neurodiversity-affirming approaches focus on "helping students develop ways to express discomfort and feelings, and knowing that it isn't wrong to disagree with others and that they have the right to speak up for themselves." (Various ND-affirming resources)

5. **Environmental adaptation as shared responsibility:** Rather than placing all the burden on the autistic individual, affirming approaches advocate for mutual adaptation: "Reducing the need for masking by encouraging a person to communicate in their preferred way." (ASHA Primer on ND-Affirming Speech Services)

### 4.4 Practical Frameworks

**The tool-based approach:**
- Present politeness strategies as a *toolkit* that can be deployed strategically
- Explain *why* each strategy exists (what social function it serves)
- Provide explicit rules for when each tool is most useful (workplace formal vs. casual friend contexts)
- Respect the individual's choice about when to use or not use each tool

**Context-dependent deployment:**
- **High-stakes contexts** (job interviews, customer-facing roles, authority figures): Understanding and deploying NT politeness provides measurable social advantage
- **Medium-stakes contexts** (collegial workplace, acquaintances): Selective deployment based on relationship and goals
- **Low-stakes contexts** (close friends, autistic community, informal settings): Authenticity may be more valued than conventional politeness
- **Autistic-autistic contexts**: Directness is often the preferred and respected norm

## 5. Autistic-Autistic Politeness Norms

### 5.1 Research on Autistic-Autistic Interaction

A growing body of research demonstrates that autistic individuals interact effectively and comfortably with other autistic people, with distinct communication norms.

**Crompton et al. (2020):** The diffusion chain study demonstrated that "autistic people share information with other autistic people as well as non-autistic people do with other non-autistic people." The breakdown occurs specifically in mixed neurotype groups.

**Crompton et al. (2020, Frontiers in Psychology):** "Neurotype-Matching, but Not Being Autistic, Influences Self and Observer Ratings of Interpersonal Rapport." Interpersonal rapport is higher within than between diagnostic groups.
- Source: Frontiers in Psychology (frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2020.586171/)

**Watts et al. (2025):** A systematic review and thematic meta-synthesis of 52 qualitative papers on autistic adults' experiences of interacting with other autistic people found that "many experiences of other autistic people were positive and were associated with improved Quality of Life across a range of domains." Autistic people describe contact with other autistic people as allowing them "to be their authentic selves."
- Source: Watts et al., 2025, Autism (journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/13623613241255811)

**Sutherland et al. (2025):** The "Read My Lips, Not My Body" thematic analysis identified five themes in autistic social communication, including "The Autistic Tribe" -- the value of being around other autistic people. Other themes included "Clear, Honest, Authentic" (autistic emphasis on honesty), "Invisible Demands" (unrecognized social burden on autistic people), and "Autistic Strengths" (communication challenges as also being sources of strength).
- Source: Sutherland et al., 2025, Autism in Adulthood (journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/27546330251333919)

### 5.2 Directness as Respect in Autistic Culture

Within autistic community spaces, directness is not merely tolerated but actively valued as a form of respect:

- "Autistic community values include being honest, direct, clear, and authentic." (Crompton et al., SJDR)
- "Many autistic people consider honest communication to be a kindness to others." (Reframing Autism)
- "When not forced to mask, autistic people have deep authenticity in relationships, often use less small talk and social niceties, don't fake or force interaction, and tend to communicate frankly and work in parallel or in easy silence." (HeyASD)
- "Honesty and authenticity are central, core parts of many autistic people's identities, as living as anything other than one's authentic self is to be without an identity." (NeuroClastic, Identity Theory of Autism)

**The key insight:** In autistic-autistic interaction, *indirectness* may actually be perceived as less respectful than directness, because it introduces ambiguity and requires additional processing. What NT culture codes as "polite" (hedging, softening, indirect requests) may be experienced by autistic listeners as *confusing* or even *dishonest*.

### 5.3 An Autistic "Politeness System"?

While the research does not yet formally describe a comprehensive "autistic politeness system," the emerging picture suggests distinct norms:

- **Clarity as courtesy:** Saying exactly what you mean is the respectful choice.
- **Information-sharing as connection:** Deep engagement with topics of mutual interest serves the bonding function that small talk serves in NT interaction.
- **Explicit negotiation:** Rather than implied social rules, autistic communities tend toward "developing shared, negotiated interactional patterns in relationships" (Sutherland et al., 2025).
- **Reduced phatic communion:** Small talk, greeting rituals, and conversational lubrication are de-emphasized in favor of substantive exchange.
- **Tolerance for silence:** Parallel activity and comfortable silence are valued rather than seen as awkward.
- **Acceptance of boundaries:** Direct statements of limits ("I need to leave now," "I can't talk about that") are respected without requiring extensive softening.

## 6. Cross-Cultural Parallels

### 6.1 Direct-Communication Cultures as Parallels

Several NT cultures share communication features with autistic communication norms, providing useful parallels that normalize directness as a legitimate communication style rather than a deficit.

### 6.2 Israeli Dugriut (Straight Talk)

Tamar Katriel's foundational work "Talking Straight: Dugri Speech in Israeli Sabra Culture" (Cambridge University Press) documents how Israeli sabra culture values "dugriut" -- a "forthright and unapologetic directness in communicating."

**Key parallels with autistic communication:**
- Words are to be taken literally; meaning is in what is said, not implied.
- Directness is seen as a sign of trust and authenticity, not aggression.
- "Underneath this 'telling it like it is' is a tolerance towards conflict." (Progressive Israel)
- "While appeal to shared cultural meanings mitigates the blunt edge of 'dugri' speech for members of the culture, conversely outsiders often interpret the 'sabra's' directness as rudeness." -- This directly mirrors the autistic experience of having directness read as rudeness by NT observers.
- Source: Katriel, Cambridge University Press; Cultural Atlas (culturalatlas.sbs.com.au/israeli-culture/israeli-culture-communication)

### 6.3 Dutch Bespreekbaarheid (Speakability)

Dutch culture embodies "bespreekbaarheid" -- the principle that "no topic should be taboo" and that directness is a form of respect.

**Key parallels:**
- "Conversations with Dutch people sound different from many other cultures: blunt statements, straightforward answers, and an almost complete absence of polite hedging." (Polyglottist Language Academy)
- "What outsiders often misinterpret as rudeness is, in fact, one of the defining cultural traits of the Dutch: directness."
- "A country built on consensus, trade, and pragmatism does not waste much time on formalities when clarity is needed."
- "For the Dutch, honesty is not only the best policy but also the most efficient way to build trust and avoid misunderstandings."
- Dutch workplaces feature "relatively flat hierarchies" where "employees are expected to voice their opinions, question decisions, and provide honest feedback."
- Source: DutchReview (dutchreview.com/culture/communication-breakdown-understanding-the-dutch-approach-to-conversation/)

### 6.4 German Direktheit (Directness)

German communication culture is characterized as "low-context" -- "what's said is what's meant with little hidden meaning, and being vague can even be seen as dishonest."

**Key parallels:**
- "Germans tend to value directness, clarity, and honesty in communication -- this isn't about being rude but about being efficient, sincere, and respectful of time."
- Like autistic communication, German directness can be misread by high-context culture speakers as rude when it is intended as respectful.
- Source: Global People Transitions (globalpeopletransitions.com/german-direct-communication-styles/)

### 6.5 The Cross-Cultural-Pragmatics-Autism Connection

**The Intercultural Cognitive Pragmatics (ICP) Framework (2026):** A groundbreaking paper in Frontiers in Human Neuroscience proposes understanding autism as "a variation in how minds are shaped through communication, embodiment, and cultural participation" rather than "a deviation from a presumed universal model of social cognition." The framework argues that "social difficulties associated with autism are best interpreted as relational mismatches, embedded in asymmetric social structures and normative expectations, rather than as intrinsic cognitive deficits."

This approach "offers a method for reorienting research and practice toward mutual intelligibility, participatory scaffolding, and contextual adaptation rather than individual correction."
- Source: Frontiers in Human Neuroscience, 2026 (frontiersin.org/journals/human-neuroscience/articles/10.3389/fnhum.2026.1729076/)

**The analogy for teaching:** Just as we teach cross-cultural communication by explaining that "in Culture X, directness means respect, while in Culture Y, indirectness means respect" without declaring either culture wrong, we can teach autistic-NT pragmatic differences as a cross-cultural phenomenon. This:
- Validates autistic communication norms as a legitimate system
- Provides analytical distance (studying "their" rules rather than being told "your" rules are broken)
- Empowers strategic code-switching while preserving identity
- Draws on established frameworks that learners may find more intuitive than pathology-based models

## 7. The "Politeness as Weapon" Critique

### 7.1 How Politeness Norms Can Harm Autistic People

An important perspective from the autistic community is that politeness norms, far from being neutral social lubricant, can function as a tool of social control that disproportionately harms autistic people.

**The AutSide analysis (Jim Hoerricks):** "Politeness as Weapon: How Neurotypical Norms Enable Coercion" argues that:
- "Politeness creates the perfect fog for people who rely on manipulation to move through a room unnoticed."
- "Predators operate in the gap between what was spoken and what was intended, knowing full well that neurotypical norms prize inference over clarity and interpretation over evidence."
- "When an autistic person, often the first to feel the shift in the atmosphere, points directly at the harm -- the polite aggressor is automatically believed."
- "Politeness becomes a disciplinary system that ensures discomfort flows in only one direction -- away from the powerful, away from the person causing harm, and directly onto the person who feels it first."
- Source: AutSide Substack (autside.substack.com/p/politeness-as-weapon-how-neurotypical)

### 7.2 Implications for Teaching

This critique does not mean politeness should never be taught, but it adds essential nuance:

1. **Teach the system critically:** Understanding how politeness can be weaponized is itself a protective skill. Autistic individuals who understand *why* politeness norms exist can also recognize when they are being exploited.

2. **Validate autistic skepticism of politeness:** Many autistic people have an intuitive sense that something is wrong with a system that prioritizes smooth surfaces over honest content. This intuition should be respected, not trained away.

3. **Include self-protection:** Any pragmatics curriculum should include recognizing when politeness is being used manipulatively (passive aggression, social coercion, gaslighting through "politeness").

## 8. Theoretical Synthesis: A Framework for the Knowledge Base

### 8.1 Key Principles

Based on this research, a knowledge base teaching English pragmatics to autistic adults should be built on these principles:

1. **Descriptive, not prescriptive:** Explain how the politeness system works, not how you "should" communicate.

2. **Cross-cultural framing:** Present NT politeness as one communication culture among many, not as the default or correct system. Draw parallels to Dutch, Israeli, German communication cultures to normalize directness.

3. **Analytical empowerment:** Provide the theoretical vocabulary (face, FTAs, hedging, positive/negative politeness) as analytical tools. Understanding the machinery gives autistic learners the ability to decode NT communication *and* to make informed choices about their own production.

4. **Context-dependent toolkit:** Present politeness strategies as tools that provide advantages in specific contexts (job interviews, customer service, authority interactions) rather than universal rules.

5. **Validate autistic norms:** Explicitly acknowledge that autistic communication has its own politeness system -- one that values clarity, honesty, directness, and authentic connection.

6. **Address the costs:** Be honest about the mental health costs of masking and the fact that performing NT politeness is effortful for autistic people. Present cost-benefit analysis rather than unconditional "you should always..."

7. **Include the critique:** Teach how politeness can be weaponized, so that understanding the system serves self-protection as well as social navigation.

### 8.2 Suggested Conceptual Structure

For the knowledge base, the politeness content might be organized as:

1. **What is face?** (Brown & Levinson's framework, explained through autistic-relevant examples)
2. **Two kinds of face needs** (with explicit discussion of how autistic people may weight these differently)
3. **Politeness strategies as a toolkit** (the five strategies: bald on-record, positive politeness, negative politeness, off-record, and don't do the FTA)
4. **Decoding NT politeness** (how to recognize what NT speakers "really mean" when they're being indirect)
5. **When directness is valued** (contexts and cultures where direct communication is preferred or respected)
6. **Strategic deployment** (making informed choices about when and how to use politeness tools)
7. **Recognizing manipulation** (when politeness is weaponized; passive aggression; social coercion)
8. **Autistic communication strengths** (reframing directness as a communication asset)
