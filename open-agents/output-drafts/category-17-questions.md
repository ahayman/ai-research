# Category 17: The Pragmatics of Questions

## The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

---

## How to Read This Document

"How are you?" is not a question about your health. "Can you close the door?" is not a question about your physical abilities. "Who cares?" is not a question about who cares. "Nice weather, isn't it?" is not a meteorological inquiry.

Questions are among the most pragmatically deceptive constructions in English. Their grammatical form (interrogative) tells you almost nothing about their actual function. The same interrogative structure can be an information request, a social greeting, a command, a complaint, a rhetorical statement, or a trap.

This category gives you a **classification system** for identifying what a question is actually doing -- and the appropriate response for each type.

---

## 1. The Core Problem: Form Does Not Equal Function

English has three basic sentence types: declaratives, interrogatives, and imperatives. In theory, interrogatives ask questions. In practice, they do everything:

| What It Looks Like | What It's Actually Doing | Type |
|-------------------|------------------------|------|
| "How are you?" | Social greeting (not seeking health info) | Phatic |
| "Can you close the door?" | Request (not asking about ability) | Indirect request |
| "Who cares?" | Asserting "nobody cares" | Rhetorical |
| "Nice day, isn't it?" | Seeking agreement / making small talk | Tag question |
| "Don't you think we should leave?" | Suggesting you should leave | Leading |
| "When did you stop cheating?" | Smuggling a presupposition | Loaded |
| "What's the capital of France?" | Testing your knowledge | Display |
| "What time is it?" | Genuinely wanting to know | Information-seeking |

Only the LAST one is a "question" in the naive sense. The other seven are performing completely different speech acts while wearing question costumes.

## 2. The Eleven Question Types

### 2.1 Information-Seeking (Genuine Questions)

**What it is**: The speaker genuinely doesn't know the answer and wants it.

**Examples**: "What time is it?" / "Where's the bathroom?" / "How do you spell that?"

**How to recognize**: The speaker has no way to know the answer without asking.

**Expected response**: Provide the information requested.

**Autistic experience**: These are generally well-handled. The form matches the function -- no pragmatic decoding needed.

### 2.2 Rhetorical Questions

**What it is**: A question that doesn't expect an answer. It's a statement in question form.

**Examples**: "Who cares?" (= nobody cares) / "What's the point?" (= there is no point) / "Isn't it obvious?" (= it IS obvious) / "How was I supposed to know?" (= I couldn't have known)

**How to recognize**: The answer is obvious to everyone, or no answer is possible. The speaker's tone is assertive, not genuinely curious.

**Expected response**: None, or agreement. NOT a literal answer. "Who cares?" does not expect you to list people who care.

**Autistic experience**: Can trigger literal processing. Research shows this improves with age and explicit learning -- autistic adults often recognize rhetorical forms through learned patterns.

### 2.3 Phatic Questions (Social Ritual)

**What it is**: A greeting or social ritual in question form. The function is to acknowledge the other person and maintain social connection.

**Examples**: "How are you?" / "What's up?" / "How's it going?" / "How was your weekend?"

**How to recognize**: It's at the start of an interaction. The asker doesn't pause to wait for a detailed answer. The context is greeting, not interview.

**Expected response**: Formulaic. "Good, you?" / "Not bad!" / "Fine, thanks." (See Category 19 for the ambient signaling function of these responses.)

**Autistic experience**: THE iconic autistic pragmatic challenge. The question LOOKS like an information request but ISN'T. Answering honestly with a health report is the classic mismatch.

**Strategy**: Learn the formula. "Good, you?" covers 90% of cases. If you want to signal something (Category 19), use calibrated responses: "Hanging in there" (mild negative) or "Great!" (positive).

### 2.4 Indirect Requests

**What it is**: A request disguised as a question about ability, willingness, or circumstances.

**Examples**: "Can you close the door?" / "Would you mind moving?" / "Do you have the time?"

**How to recognize**: The literal answer would be trivially obvious ("Can you close the door?" -- obviously yes, you have arms). The context involves a desired action.

**Expected response**: Perform the action (not answer the literal question). "Can you pass the salt?" → pass the salt, don't say "Yes, I can."

**Autistic experience**: Research shows conventional forms ("Can you...?") are generally understood (Category 1 findings). Non-conventional hints ("It's cold in here" = close the window) are harder.

### 2.5 Display/Exam Questions

**What it is**: The asker already knows the answer. They're testing whether YOU know it.

**Examples**: Teacher: "What's the capital of France?" / Quiz: "Who wrote Hamlet?" / Doctor: "Can you tell me what medications you're taking?"

**How to recognize**: Institutional setting (classroom, interview, medical). The asker is in a position to evaluate your answer.

**Expected response**: Demonstrate your knowledge. The answer is being assessed.

### 2.6 Loaded Questions

**What it is**: A question that smuggles in a presupposition you may not accept (Category 9).

**Examples**: "When did you stop cheating?" / "Why did you fail?" / "What's your biggest weakness?"

**How to recognize**: Any direct answer accepts the hidden premise. Both "yes" and "no" concede something.

**Expected response**: Challenge the presupposition if you reject it: "That question assumes I was cheating, which I wasn't." Or navigate strategically (interviews: the "weakness" question is expected -- answer with a genuine-but-manageable weakness + how you address it).

### 2.7 Tag Questions

**What it is**: A statement with a question appended, seeking confirmation or agreement.

**Examples**: "Nice day, isn't it?" / "You're coming, aren't you?" / "That makes sense, right?"

**How to recognize**: A declarative sentence + a short question tag at the end.

**Two types (distinguished by intonation)**:

| Intonation on Tag | Function | Example |
|-------------------|----------|---------|
| **Rising** ↗ | Genuine question -- seeking actual confirmation | "The meeting is at three, isn't it?" ↗ (I'm actually not sure) |
| **Falling** ↘ | Seeking agreement -- expecting "yes" | "Nice day, isn't it?" ↘ (I'm expecting you to agree) |

Research: 68% of tag questions in natural speech have falling intonation -- meaning they're seeking agreement, not information.

### 2.8 Leading Questions

**What it is**: A question that suggests its own answer.

**Examples**: "Don't you think we should leave?" (suggests: yes) / "Wouldn't it be better to...?" (suggests: yes) / "Surely you agree that...?" (pressures agreement)

**How to recognize**: The question contains a built-in answer. It's more like a request for confirmation than a genuine inquiry.

**Expected response**: The suggested answer -- but you CAN disagree. "Don't you think we should leave?" can be answered "Actually, I'd like to stay a bit longer."

### 2.9 Echo Questions

**What it is**: Repeating part of what someone said as a question.

**Examples**: A: "I'm moving to Belize." B: "You're moving to BELIZE?" / A: "It costs $500." B: "FIVE HUNDRED dollars?"

**Functions**: Surprise, disbelief, repair (didn't hear clearly), confirmation-seeking.

**How to recognize**: The question echoes what was just said, with stress on the surprising element and rising intonation.

### 2.10 Hypothetical Questions

**What it is**: Exploring possibilities. Often an indirect way of making a suggestion.

**Examples**: "What would you do if...?" / "What if we tried...?" / "How would it work if...?"

**How to recognize**: "What if" / "What would" / conditional framing.

**Expected response**: Engage with the hypothetical. These are often collaborative -- the asker is thinking through possibilities and inviting you to join.

### 2.11 Self-Addressed Questions

**What it is**: The speaker asks a question they don't expect anyone else to answer.

**Examples**: "Now where did I put my keys?" / "What was I thinking?" / "How does this work again?"

**How to recognize**: The speaker is looking at something, not at you. They may be muttering. There's no pause for you to answer.

**Expected response**: None required. You CAN answer if you know ("Your keys are on the counter"), but it's not expected.

## 3. The Question-Type Decision Tree

When you hear a question and aren't sure what type it is:

```
Does the asker already know the answer?
├── YES → Display/exam question (testing you) or Rhetorical (making a statement)
│         Is an answer expected? 
│         YES → Display (demonstrate knowledge)
│         NO → Rhetorical (agree or stay silent)
├── NO → Continue...
│
Is the question asking for information or for action?
├── ACTION → Indirect request ("Can you close the door?")
│            Respond by doing the action
├── SOCIAL RITUAL → Phatic ("How are you?")
│                    Respond with a formula
├── INFORMATION → Continue...
│
Is the answer expected to be detailed or formulaic?
├── FORMULAIC → Phatic or confirmation-seeking
├── DETAILED → Genuine information-seeking question
│              Provide the information
│
Does the question contain a hidden assumption?
├── YES → Loaded question → Challenge the presupposition if you reject it
├── NO → Answer normally
│
Does the question suggest its own answer?
├── YES → Leading question → You can agree or (politely) disagree
├── NO → Straightforward question
```

## 4. Questions as Conversational Tools

Questions aren't just things you RECEIVE. They're one of the most powerful tools for MANAGING conversation:

| Goal | Question to Use | Example |
|------|----------------|---------|
| **Show interest** | Follow-up question | "What was that like?" / "How did that go?" |
| **Maintain a topic** | Continuation question | "What happened next?" / "How did they react?" |
| **Change a topic** | Bridge question | "That reminds me -- have you heard about...?" |
| **Check understanding** | Clarification question | "So you're saying...?" / "Do you mean...?" |
| **Invite participation** | Directed question | "What do you think, [name]?" |
| **Soften disagreement** | Indirect challenge | "Have you considered...?" / "What about...?" |
| **Buy thinking time** | Reflective question | "That's a good question -- can I think about it?" |

**The 2:1 guideline**: For every two things you share about yourself, ask at least one question about the other person. This creates the reciprocity that NT social norms expect.

## 5. The Autistic Experience with Questions

### 5.1 What's Generally Well-Handled

- **Information-seeking questions**: The form matches the function
- **Conventional indirect requests**: "Can you...?" is understood as a request (Category 1 research)
- **Display questions**: Demonstrating knowledge is straightforward
- **Hypothetical questions**: Exploring ideas is often engaging

### 5.2 What's Harder

- **Phatic questions**: "How are you?" as greeting, not question
- **Rhetorical questions**: Literal processing may trigger an actual answer
- **Leading questions**: The built-in suggestion may not be detected
- **Open-ended questions**: "Tell me about yourself" -- how much? What's relevant? Where to start?
- **Questions requiring context**: When the answer depends on reading the social situation

### 5.3 The "How Are You?" Problem

This is the single most discussed autistic pragmatic mismatch. Strategies:

| Approach | How It Works | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| **Script** | Memorize the formula | "Good, thanks. You?" |
| **Calibrated signal** | Use the response to broadcast state (Category 19) | "Great!" / "Hanging in there" / "Could be better" |
| **Honest-but-brief** | Give a real answer in formulaic length | "Tired but good. You?" |
| **Redirect** | Answer minimally, then ask back | "Not bad! How about you?" |

### 5.4 Questions and Anxiety

Research shows questions can trigger anxiety in autistic individuals because of:
- Processing time pressure (the 200ms response norm)
- Performance demands (giving the "right" answer)
- Ambiguity about question type (what do they actually want?)
- Open-ended questions requiring relevance judgment (what's the "right" amount to say?)

**Strategy**: It is ALWAYS acceptable to say: "That's a good question -- let me think for a second." This buys processing time and signals engagement, not incompetence.

## 6. Context-Specific Question Navigation

### 6.1 Job Interviews

| Question | What It's Actually Asking | Strategy |
|----------|--------------------------|---------|
| "Tell me about yourself" | Give a professional narrative (NOT your life story) | Present → Past → Future format, 60-90 seconds |
| "What's your biggest weakness?" | Show self-awareness + growth | Genuine weakness + how you manage it |
| "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?" | Are you ambitious? Will you stay? | Show alignment with the organization |
| "Tell me about a time when..." | Demonstrate the competency through a story | STAR method: Situation → Task → Action → Result |

### 6.2 Medical Appointments

| Question | Expected Answer |
|----------|----------------|
| "Where does it hurt?" | Specific location + quality (sharp, dull, throbbing) |
| "On a scale of 1-10?" | A single number; 1 = barely noticeable, 10 = worst imaginable |
| "When did it start?" | Specific time/date or "about X weeks ago" |
| "Any other symptoms?" | List, brief; don't self-diagnose |

**Your questions to ask**: "What are the treatment options?" / "What should I watch for?" / "Can you explain that in simpler terms?"

### 6.3 Workplace

| Question | What It Might Mean | Strategy |
|----------|-------------------|---------|
| "How's the project going?" | Status update (information) OR phatic check-in | If from your boss → status update. If from a peer → may be either. When in doubt, give a brief status + "going well" |
| "Does anyone have questions?" | Genuine invitation, but social pressure to say no | If you DO have a question, ask it. It's literally what was invited. |
| "Can I ask you something?" | Pre-sequence for a potentially sensitive question | "Sure" -- and prepare for something that may be uncomfortable |

## 7. Handling Difficult Questions

| Situation | Strategy | Phrase |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| **Don't want to answer** | Deflect to a related topic | "That's a long story -- what made you think of that?" |
| **Don't understand** | Ask for clarification | "Sorry, can you rephrase that?" / "What do you mean by X?" |
| **Hidden presupposition** | Challenge the premise | "I'd push back on the assumption that..." |
| **Honesty conflicts with social expectation** | Honest-but-diplomatic middle | "I can see why people like it -- it wasn't really my thing" |
| **"Why" feels like an accusation** | Reframe as explanation | "Here's what happened..." (instead of defending "why") |
| **Need time** | Buy processing time | "That's a good question. Let me think..." |
| **Don't know** | Say so + qualify | "I'm not sure, but I think..." / "I'd need to check on that" |

## 8. Key Takeaways

1. **Questions are rarely just questions.** Of the eleven types, only one (information-seeking) does what the grammatical form suggests. The other ten are performing different speech acts in disguise.

2. **The decision tree is your friend.** Does the asker know the answer? → Is it seeking information or action? → Is the response expected to be detailed or formulaic? → Is there a hidden assumption? This sequence correctly classifies most questions.

3. **"How are you?" is a greeting, not a question.** "Good, thanks. You?" handles 90% of cases. Use calibrated responses for the signaling function (Category 19).

4. **Tag questions with falling intonation expect agreement.** "Nice day, isn't it?" ↘ = agree. "The meeting is at three, isn't it?" ↗ = genuinely asking.

5. **Questions are powerful conversational tools.** Asking questions shows interest, maintains topics, and creates reciprocity. The 2:1 guideline: for every two shares, ask one question.

6. **Buying time is always OK.** "That's a good question -- let me think" signals engagement, not incompetence. The 200ms response norm does NOT apply to complex questions.

7. **Loaded questions have an escape.** Challenge the presupposition: "That question assumes X, which I don't accept." You're not required to answer within the question's frame.

8. **Open-ended questions have structure.** "Tell me about yourself" → Present-Past-Future, 60-90 seconds. "Tell me about a time when..." → STAR method. The structure makes them navigable.
