# Research Notes: ABA, Autistic Masking, and Mental Health

## Executive Summary

Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) is the most widely prescribed and insurance-funded intervention for autistic children in the United States, yet it is also one of the most controversial. Founded in the 1960s by Ole Ivar Lovaas at UCLA, ABA originally employed aversive techniques including electric shocks, slaps, and food deprivation to suppress autistic behaviors and make children "indistinguishable from their peers." Lovaas simultaneously ran the Feminine Boy Project--a conversion therapy program targeting gender-nonconforming boys--using the same operant conditioning principles. One subject of that program, Kirk Andrew Murphy, died by suicide at age 38.

A growing body of research from autistic adults documents significant harms: Kupferstein (2018) found 46% of ABA-exposed respondents met PTSD diagnostic thresholds; McGill & Robinson (2020) documented "hidden harms" in qualitative interviews; Anderson (2023) found autistic adults described ABA as unethical despite acknowledging some skill benefits; and Aguirre Mtanous et al. (2025) found ABA receipt was associated with 30% higher odds of mental health hospitalization in a US national sample of 34,240 youth. Meanwhile, the EUCAP 2022 survey found 92.2% of autistic people who underwent ABA opposed its use. Research integrity is also questioned: Bottema-Beutel & Crowley (2021) found 84% of ABA studies had undisclosed conflicts of interest from authors who were also ABA service providers.

Critics argue that ABA systematically trains masking by reinforcing neurotypical-appearing behaviors and extinguishing autistic ones (stimming, echolalia, atypical eye gaze), creating compliance at the expense of self-determination, authentic self-expression, and long-term mental health. While proponents argue "modern ABA" has evolved, the autistic community and a growing number of researchers question whether the fundamental goal of behavioral normalization has meaningfully changed.

## 1. History of ABA

### 1.1 Ivar Lovaas and the Origins of ABA (UCLA Young Autism Project)

**Ole Ivar Lovaas (1927-2010)** was a Norwegian-American clinical psychologist and professor at UCLA who established the **Young Autism Project** in 1962. When Lovaas arrived at UCLA in 1961, he was directed to the Neuropsychiatric Institute, where he first encountered autistic children. He began applying operant conditioning--then called "behavior modification"--to modify their behavior.

**Study**: Lovaas, O.I. (1987). "Behavioral treatment and normal educational and intellectual functioning in young autistic children." *Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology*, 55(1), 3-9.
- **DOI**: 10.1037/0022-006X.55.1.3
- **PubMed**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/3571656/
- **Sample size**: 19 children in intensive group (40 hrs/week), 19 in control group 1 (10 hrs/week), additional control group 2
- **Design**: Quasi-experimental (not randomized)
- **Key finding**: 47% (9/19) of the intensive group achieved "normal intellectual and educational functioning" and became "indistinguishable from their peers" -- the phrase that became the stated goal of ABA
- **Criticisms**: Non-randomized design; small sample; "indistinguishable from peers" as a goal critiqued as erasure of autistic identity; no quality-of-life or mental health outcomes measured

**Follow-up study**: McEachin, J.J., Smith, T., & Lovaas, O.I. (1993). "Long-term outcome for children with autism who received early intensive behavioral treatment." *American Journal of Mental Retardation*, 97(4), 359-72.
- **PubMed**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8427693/
- **Finding**: Maintained gains at follow-up; 8 of 9 "best outcome" children were still "indistinguishable"

[Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ole_Ivar_Lovaas]
[Source: https://blogs.uoregon.edu/autismhistoryproject/people/lovaas-ivar-1927-2010/]

### 1.2 The Original Use of Aversives (Including Punishment)

Lovaas's original methods included severe aversive techniques:

- **Electric shocks**: Administered through electrified floor grids and small remote-control devices taped to children's wrists. Lovaas eventually settled on a hand-held instrument called the "**hot-shot**" that delivered a painful one-second shock to a child's leg.
- **Slapping**: In a 1974 *Psychology Today* interview, Lovaas described: "When he fidgets, cries, moves about, he may get a smart slap" and "Spank them, and spank them good."
- **Shouting**: Loud, sharp "No!" used as punishment
- **Food deprivation**: Withholding food as a control mechanism
- **Physical restraint**: Forced compliance with demands

These techniques were popularized in a **1965 Life magazine** photo spread showing Lovaas's methods. Lovaas later acknowledged that aversives were "only temporarily effective" and that punishments became less effective over time. In a 1994 CBS interview, he stated: "These people are so used to pain that they can adapt to almost any kind of aversive you give them."

Smith & Lovaas (1997) later noted that aversive procedures were "no longer employed by the UCLA group because alternative procedures have been developed."

[Source: https://blogs.uoregon.edu/autismhistoryproject/people/lovaas-ivar-1927-2010/]
[Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ole_Ivar_Lovaas]

### 1.3 Connection to "Feminine Boy" Conversion Therapy Project

In **1974**, Lovaas was principal investigator of the **Clinic Program for the Evaluation and Treatment of Childhood Gender Problems** at UCLA's psychology department, with his former doctoral student **George Rekers** as project director. The program--known as the "**Feminine Boy Project**"--aimed to use behavioral conditioning to make boys who displayed "feminine" behaviors act more "masculine."

**Key facts about the connection**:
- The program received **$218,945 from the National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH)**
- Rekers's involvement was **directly inspired by Lovaas's Young Autism Project**, where operant conditioning was already being applied to "eliminate" autistic behaviors
- At UCLA in the 1960s-70s, the two programs ran **side-by-side**: one targeting autistic behaviors, one targeting gender-nonconforming behaviors
- Both used the same behavioral modification principles: reward "desired" (normative) behavior, punish "undesired" (non-normative) behavior
- For the gender program, rewards were blue chips (redeemable for treats) for "masculine" behaviors; red chips for "feminine" behaviors were subtracted, and each red chip converted to a physical "swat"

**The case of Kirk Andrew Murphy**:
- Known as "Kraig" in published studies (in the *Journal of Applied Behavioral Analysis*)
- Entered the program at age **5** after his father observed him posing in a long T-shirt saying "Isn't my dress pretty?"
- Subjected to behavioral modification to suppress "feminine" behavior
- Died by suicide at age **38** by hanging
- George Rekers denied the experiment contributed to Murphy's death, calling the connection a "hypothesis"

**Scholarly analysis**: Gibson, M.F. & Douglas, P. (2018). "Disturbing Behaviours: Ole Ivar Lovaas and the Queer History of Autism Science." *Catalyst: Feminism, Theory, Technoscience*, 4(2).
- Analyzes how ABA and the Feminine Boy Project "involved similar violences to enforce 'normalisation', including spanking, slapping, shocking, and ignoring"
- Available at: https://catalystjournal.org/index.php/catalyst/article/view/29579/23427

[Source: https://www.lgbtqnation.com/2021/03/man-behind-ex-gay-conversion-therapy-started-trying-make-autistic-children-normal/]
[Source: https://xtramagazine.com/health/autism-conversion-therapy-221581]
[Source: https://www.cnn.com/2011/US/06/07/sissy.boy.experiment/index.html]

### 1.4 Evolution of ABA Over Decades

| Era | Approximate Period | Characteristics |
|-----|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Classical ABA** | 1960s-1980s | Discrete Trial Training (DTT), aversives, 40 hrs/week, table-based drills |
| **Transitional** | 1990s-2000s | Reduced aversives, positive reinforcement emphasis, insurance funding expansion |
| **"Modern" ABA** | 2010s-present | Claims of naturalistic methods, play-based, child-led; NDBI integration |
| **Reform movement** | 2020s | Assent-based practice, neurodiversity-affirming ABA attempts |

Key developments:
- **1998**: Behavior Analyst Certification Board (BACB) established
- **2014**: All 50 US states required insurance coverage for ABA
- **2020s**: Growing tension between "neurodiversity-affirming ABA" proponents and abolitionists who argue ABA is inherently harmful

## 2. How ABA Systematically Trains Masking Behaviors

### 2.1 Reinforcement of Neurotypical Behaviors

ABA operates on operant conditioning principles: behaviors followed by reinforcement increase; those followed by punishment or extinction decrease. The fundamental concern is that the **target behaviors selected for increase** are those that make autistic children appear neurotypical:

- **Eye contact**: Reinforced despite autistic people reporting it as physically painful or overwhelming
- **Scripted social responses**: "How are you?" / "I'm fine, thanks" -- taught as rote exchanges
- **"Appropriate" body language**: Sitting still, keeping hands in lap, facing speaker
- **Neurotypical play patterns**: Redirecting from preferred solitary activities to group play
- **Emotional expression matching**: Teaching children to display "appropriate" facial expressions

**Key criticism**: Rose (2017) described how children in ABA "get rewarded for behaving like a Neurotypical" with the implicit message "being autistic is BAD."

**Research**: The Social Validity of Behavioral Interventions study (2024) found that autistic adults find goals and practices highlighting quality of life, safety, and autistic interactions acceptable, while those focusing on **normalization based on neurotypical standards are not acceptable**.
- Published in: *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*
- Available at: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10803-024-06297-3

[Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11219658/]
[Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11933200/]

### 2.2 Extinction of Autistic Behaviors (Stimming, Echolalia)

**Extinction** in ABA terminology means systematically withholding reinforcement for a behavior until it ceases. Autistic behaviors targeted for extinction include:

- **Stimming** (self-stimulatory behavior): hand-flapping, rocking, spinning, fidgeting
- **Echolalia**: repeating words or phrases (both immediate and delayed)
- **Special interests**: Intense focus on specific topics reframed as "restricted interests"
- **Atypical sensory responses**: Covering ears, avoiding textures, seeking proprioceptive input
- **Non-standard communication**: Non-verbal vocalizations, scripting from media

**Key study on stimming**: Kapp, S.K., Steward, R., Crane, L., Elliott, D., Elphick, C., Pellicano, E., & Russell, G. (2019). "'People should be allowed to do what they like': Autistic adults' views and experiences of stimming." *Autism*, 23(7), 1782-1792.
- **DOI**: 10.1177/1362361319829628
- **PubMed**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6728747/
- **Sample**: 32 autistic adults (interviews and focus groups)
- **Key findings**:
  - Stimming described as an **important adaptive, self-regulatory mechanism**
  - Helps soothe and communicate intense emotions
  - Hand-flapping reflecting positive emotions involves arms held out; during distress, hands stay near torso
  - Participants **objected to treatment aimed at eliminating stimming**
  - Reported feeling anger, nervousness, frustration, shame, and confusion when told to stop
  - Suppressing stimming reported to **increase anxiety and decrease self-regulation capacity**

**Critical gap**: Little to no research has demonstrated that stereotypy (repetitive behavior) actually interferes with learning in ABA programs. The assumption that it must be reduced is not empirically supported.

[Source: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/1362361319829628]
[Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11219658/]

### 2.3 "Quiet Hands" and Similar Compliance Demands

**"Quiet hands"** is a common directive in ABA therapy demanding that autistic children stop stimming--particularly hand-flapping. The therapist says "quiet hands" and, if the child does not comply, physically grabs and stills the child's hands.

**Julia Bascom's "Quiet Hands" essay** (2011), published on her blog *Just Stimming...*, became a landmark piece in autistic advocacy:
- Described the trauma of having her hands forcibly stilled
- Wrote that the stated goal of ABA's founder was to render autistic children "indistinguishable from [their] peers"--"not to support autistic kids, help us learn and grow and become happy and self-determined adults, but to remove all visible signs of autism"
- The essay spawned **The Loud Hands Project**, an anthology of autistic writers' essays

Similar compliance demands include:
- **"Whole body listening"**: Sit still, eyes forward, hands in lap, mouth quiet
- **"Good sitting"**: Reinforcing stationary posture
- **"Look at me"**: Forced eye contact
- **"First-then" schedules**: Withholding preferred activities until compliance is achieved

[Source: https://neuroqueer.blogspot.com/2014/03/quiet-hands-by-julia-bascom.html]
[Source: https://stimpunks.org/glossary/quiet-hands/]
[Source: https://www.autistichoya.com/2012/01/having-loud-hands.html]

### 2.4 Social Skills Training as Masking Instruction

ABA social skills curricula typically teach autistic children to perform neurotypical social scripts:
- How to initiate conversation (using learned phrases)
- How to maintain "appropriate" eye contact duration
- How to read facial expressions and respond "correctly"
- How to take turns in conversation
- How to display "appropriate" emotional responses

**Critical research finding**: Interventions focused on social interaction for autistic individuals typically focus on teaching them how to interact with neurotypical people, **ignoring the responsibility neurotypical people have** in creating positive social interactions (the "double empathy problem," Milton, 2012).

**Study**: Milton, D.E.M. (2012). "On the ontological status of autism: the 'double empathy problem'." *Disability & Society*, 27(6), 883-887.
- Argues that communication breakdown between autistic and non-autistic people is a **two-way problem**, not a deficit in the autistic person alone

**Connection to masking research**: Masking--performing neurotypical social behaviors at the cost of authentic self-expression--has been identified as a life stressor that may lead to **autistic burnout**, characterized by pervasive, long-term exhaustion, loss of function, and reduced tolerance to stimulus.

**Study**: Raymaker, D.M., et al. (2020). "'Having All of Your Internal Resources Exhausted Beyond Measure and Being Left with No Clean-Up Crew': Defining Autistic Burnout." *Autism in Adulthood*, 2(2), 132-143.
- Found masking produces **autistic burnout syndrome**
- Characterized by pervasive exhaustion, loss of previously acquired skills, reduced tolerance to sensory input

[Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11219658/]
[Source: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0277953620300459]

## 3. Compliance Training and Long-Term Effects

### 3.1 Learned Helplessness

**Study**: Sandoval-Norton, A.H. & Shkedy, G. (2019). "How much compliance is too much compliance: Is long-term ABA therapy abuse?" *Cogent Psychology*, 6(1), 1641258.
- **DOI**: 10.1080/23311908.2019.1641258
- **Full text**: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/23311908.2019.1641258
- **Key arguments**:
  - ABA promotes **learned helplessness** -- children learn their own preferences and resistance are irrelevant
  - **Prompt dependency** -- children become unable to initiate action without external direction
  - **Reduced intrinsic motivation** -- reliance on external reinforcement undermines internal drive
  - **Reward-expectation** -- children learn to perform only when reinforcement is anticipated
  - **Difficulty generalizing** -- skills learned in artificial ABA context don't transfer to real life
  - Points out that there are limited, if any, scientifically validated studies on ABA with **nonverbal autistic children**, meaning the most vulnerable population lacks evidence

**Follow-up**: Sandoval-Norton, A.H. & Shkedy, G. (2021). "Long-term ABA Therapy Is Abusive: A Response to Gorycki, Ruppel, and Zane."
- Available at: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/350770569
- Maintained their position that ABA is fundamentally abusive
- Response to Gorycki, R.P., Ruppel, P., & Zane, T. (2020). "Is long-term ABA therapy abusive: A response to Sandoval-Norton and Shkedy." *Cogent Psychology*, 7(1), 1823615.

[Source: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/23311908.2019.1641258]

### 3.2 Difficulty Recognizing and Asserting Boundaries

**Study**: Lynch, C.L. (2019). "Invisible Abuse: ABA and the Things Only Autistic People Can See." *NeuroClastic*.
- Available at: https://neuroclastic.com/invisible-abuse-aba-and-the-things-only-autistic-people-can-see/
- **Key arguments**:
  - ABA procedures that **do not allow the child to withdraw participation** teach that personal boundaries are irrelevant
  - Children learn that **compliance causes distressing situations to stop**
  - "Escape extinction"--a standard ABA procedure--means the child **cannot escape** the demand by refusing, crying, or attempting to leave
  - This **extinguishes self-advocacy** and the ability to say "no"
  - ABA commits "invisible abuses" that are often only recognizable by autistic people themselves
  - Neurotypical professionals and caregivers frequently cannot see the harm because the behaviors being suppressed (stimming, avoiding eye contact) appear "meaningless" to them

### 3.3 Vulnerability to Abuse

**Critical concern**: Given that disabled/autistic individuals face **increased risk of abuse and assault**, compliance training that extinguishes self-advocacy may create lifelong vulnerability.

Key arguments from multiple researchers:
- Lynch (2019): Children are "likely left vulnerable to abuse due to participation in a therapy that extinguishes self-advocacy"
- Sparrow (2016): Escape extinction procedures leave children unable to protest unwanted touch or situations
- When therapy lasts **40 hours/week for years**, the message that compliance is required becomes deeply internalized
- Children learn that **authority figures have the right to control their bodies** (forced eye contact, physical prompting, hand-over-hand guidance, physically stilling stimming hands)
- This conditioning may make it harder to recognize inappropriate touching or boundary violations later in life

**Reframing Autism Position Statement** (2024): "Emerging research indicates that prompt-dependency, reward-expectation, reduced performance quality, difficulty generalising, unquestioning compliance, and learned helplessness characterise the adult autistic population exposed to early intensive behavioral interventions as children."
- Available at: https://reframingautism.org.au/position-statement-on-therapies-and-interventions/

### 3.4 Kupferstein 2018 Study on ABA and PTSD

**Study**: Kupferstein, H. (2018). "Evidence of increased PTSD symptoms in autistics exposed to applied behavior analysis." *Advances in Autism*, 4(1), 19-29.
- **DOI**: 10.1108/AIA-08-2017-0016
- **Published**: January 2, 2018
- **Available at**: https://www.emerald.com/insight/content/doi/10.1108/aia-08-2017-0016/full/html
- **Also available**: https://hennyk.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/02/aia_evidence-of-increased-ptsd-symptoms-in-autistics-exposed-to-applied-behavior-analysis.pdf

**Methodology**:
- Online survey of **460 respondents** (autistic adults and caregivers of autistic children)
- Measured posttraumatic stress symptoms (PTSS) using self-designed questionnaire
- Compared ABA-exposed vs. non-ABA-exposed groups

**Key findings**:
- **46%** of ABA-exposed respondents met the diagnostic threshold for PTSD
- **47%** of the affected subgroup showed extreme severity levels
- Non-exposed controls had a **72% chance** of being asymptomatic
- Predicted that nearly half of ABA-exposed autistic children will meet PTSD criteria within **4 weeks** of commencing intervention
- Parent satisfaction may increase even without decrease in PTSS severity

**Methodological critiques** (from Leaf, Ross, et al.):
- Self-designed (non-validated) questionnaire
- Self-selected sample (potential response bias)
- No control for confounding variables
- Correlation not causation
- Questions about how "ABA exposure" was defined and verified

[Source: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/322239353]
[Source: https://www.emerald.com/insight/content/doi/10.1108/aia-02-2018-0007/full/html]

## 4. Research on ABA Outcomes and PTSD

### 4.1 Studies Showing PTSD Symptoms in ABA Recipients

#### Kupferstein (2018) -- detailed above (Section 3.4)

#### Aguirre Mtanous et al. (2025)
**Study**: Aguirre Mtanous, N.G., Koenig, J., Nikahd, M., Effertz, S.E., Silinonte, S., Hyer, J.M., Hand, B.N., & Bishop, L. (2025). "Mental health outcomes associated with applied behavior analysis in a US national sample of privately insured autistic youth." *Autism*.
- **DOI**: 10.1177/13623613251390604
- **Published**: November 9, 2025
- **Available at**: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/13623613251390604
- **Sample**: **34,240** autistic youth (17,120 ABA recipients; 17,120 matched controls)
- **Data source**: National database of privately insured youth under 18
- **Key findings**:
  - ABA receipt associated with **30% higher odds** of mental health hospitalization (OR = 1.30, p < 0.001)
  - **32% higher incidence rate** of mental health hospitalizations (IRR = 1.32, p < 0.001)
  - No relationship found between ABA dosing and PTSD or suicidality specifically
  - Authors note need for more comprehensive analysis
- **Significance**: Largest quantitative study to date examining ABA and mental health outcomes; uses insurance claims data rather than self-report

### 4.2 Kupferstein's Research and Critiques

**The critique**: Leaf, R.B., Ross, R.K., et al. (2018). "Evaluating Kupferstein's claims of the relationship of behavioral intervention to PTSS for individuals with autism." *Advances in Autism*, 4(3).
- **DOI**: 10.1108/AIA-02-2018-0007
- **Available at**: https://www.emerald.com/insight/content/doi/10.1108/aia-02-2018-0007/full/html
- **Key criticisms**:
  - Methodological weaknesses in survey design
  - No validated PTSD assessment instrument used
  - Self-selected sample introduces response bias
  - Inability to verify ABA exposure type or duration
  - Conflation of historical and modern ABA practices
- **Recommendation**: "Service providers, behavior analysts, funding agencies, and parents should carefully and objectively evaluate this study prior to making recommendations"

**Counter-perspective**: Critics of the critique note that Leaf et al. are themselves ABA practitioners with undisclosed conflicts of interest (as later documented by Bottema-Beutel & Crowley, 2021).

### 4.3 McGill & Robinson (2020)

**Study**: McGill, O. & Robinson, A. (2020). "'Recalling hidden harms': autistic experiences of childhood applied behavioural analysis (ABA)." *Advances in Autism*, 7(4), 269-282.
- **DOI**: 10.1108/AIA-04-2020-0025
- **Published**: October 23, 2020
- **Available at**: https://www.emerald.com/insight/content/doi/10.1108/AIA-04-2020-0025/full/html
- **Also available**: https://strathprints.strath.ac.uk/73753/
- **Sample**: 10 autistic adults
- **Method**: Qualitative (online/face-to-face interviews or anonymized online questionnaire)
- **Recruitment**: Social media and autism/ABA organizations
- **Key findings**:
  - **First study** to take an in-depth qualitative approach to autistic adults' experiences of ABA
  - Core theme: **"Recalling Hidden Harms of Childhood Experiences of ABA"**
  - Predominantly **detrimental impact** reported
  - Harms carried through participants' lifelong self-understanding
  - Participants described lasting effects on identity, self-worth, and relationships

### 4.4 What Outcome Measures ABA Studies Actually Use

**Critical finding**: ABA research overwhelmingly measures **behavioral compliance** rather than **wellbeing**.

**Study**: Steinbrenner, J.R., et al. (2022). Applied Behavior Analysis scoping review - outcomes classified into 8 categories:
- Cognitive, language, social/communication, problem behavior, adaptive behavior, emotional, autism symptoms, and quality of life (QoL)
- **NO included studies measured subject quality of life** -- this is the most significant gap

**Study**: Dawson, M. & Fletcher-Watson, S. (2022). "When autism researchers disregard harms: A commentary." *Autism*, 26(2), 564-566.
- **DOI**: 10.1177/13623613211031403
- **PubMed**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34291651/
- **PMC**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8814944/
- **Key findings**:
  - Investigation of **150 early autism intervention group designs** found:
    - **139 (93%)** did not mention adverse outcomes
    - **11 (7%)** had only cursory statements
    - **NONE** indicated adverse events were monitored
  - Because interventions aim to reduce "signs of autism," **loss of autistic interests, adaptive behaviors, and strengths is interpreted as beneficial** rather than harmful
  - "The accurate assessment of harms in early autism intervention research has been both disregarded and actively undermined by the selection and measurement of intervention targets"

**Typical ABA outcome measures** (what IS measured):
- Reduction in "problem behaviors" (often including stimming, meltdowns, non-compliance)
- Increase in "on-task" behavior
- Compliance with therapist instructions
- IQ scores
- Academic placement (e.g., mainstreamed vs. special education)
- Language metrics (vocabulary size, sentence length)
- Parent/caregiver satisfaction

**What is NOT typically measured**:
- Quality of life
- Subjective wellbeing
- Anxiety and depression levels
- Self-determination
- Self-esteem
- Authentic social relationships (vs. performed social scripts)
- Long-term mental health outcomes
- Autistic burnout
- PTSD symptoms
- Suicidality

## 5. Autistic Community Perspective on ABA

### 5.1 Self-Advocate Criticism

The autistic community's criticism of ABA has grown from individual blog posts to organized advocacy to peer-reviewed research:

**Key autistic critics and their contributions**:

| Person | Role | Key Contribution |
|--------|------|-----------------|
| **Julia Bascom** | ASAN Executive Director | "Quiet Hands" essay (2011); *Loud Hands: Autistic People, Speaking* anthology |
| **Ari Ne'eman** | ASAN Co-Founder | Critiqued ABA's "predatory approach to parents"; argued ABA focuses on surface behavior without acknowledging emotional content |
| **Lydia X.Z. Brown** | Autistic activist/writer | "Having Loud Hands" (2012); Autistic Hoya blog documenting ABA harms |
| **C.L. Lynch** | Autistic writer | "Invisible Abuse: ABA and the Things Only Autistic People Can See" (2019) |
| **Michelle Dawson** | Autistic researcher | Documented undisclosed COIs in ABA research; co-authored "When autism researchers disregard harms" |

### 5.2 The #ActuallyAutistic Movement's Position

The **#ActuallyAutistic** hashtag emerged on social media as a way for autistic people (as opposed to parents, professionals, or organizations speaking about autism) to share their own perspectives. Related hashtags include **#ABAisAbuse**.

**Key positions**:
- ABA is a form of **conversion therapy** that attempts to make autistic people appear non-autistic
- The goal of "indistinguishable from peers" is **erasure of autistic identity**
- Compliance taught through ABA creates **vulnerability to abuse**
- The $17+ billion ABA industry has **financial incentives** to maintain the status quo
- Even "reformed" ABA maintains the fundamental goal of **behavioral normalization**

**Survey data**:
- 2018 survey: Only **5%** of autistic people support ABA therapy

## 6. Modern ABA vs. Historical ABA

### 6.1 Claims of "New ABA" Being Different

Proponents argue that modern ABA has fundamentally changed:
- Aversives have been eliminated
- Focus is on positive reinforcement only
- Sessions are play-based and child-led
- Natural Environment Teaching (NET) replaces table-based drills
- Goals are individualized and functional
- Assent-based practices respect the child's "no"

### 6.2 Whether Modern ABA Still Promotes Masking

**Critical analysis**: Despite claims of reform, critics identify persistent concerns:

**Study**: From Harm to Healing: Building the Future of ABA with Autistic Voices (2025). *Societies*, 15(3), 72.
- Published by an autistic BCBA (Board Certified Behavior Analyst)
- Available at: https://www.mdpi.com/2075-4698/15/3/72
- Critically analyzes historical practices, current implementation, and evolving critiques
- Explores the #ABAisAbuse movement
- Argues for socially valid, inclusive practices guided by autistic voices

**Key critique**: Even without physical aversives, modern ABA may still:
- Use **planned ignoring** (withholding attention until "appropriate" behavior occurs)
- Employ **escape extinction** (not allowing the child to leave a demand situation)
- Target stimming for reduction as a "default approach" despite insufficient evidence of harm
- Set goals based on **neurotypical behavioral standards** rather than individual wellbeing
- Use **social reinforcement contingently** (withholding warmth/attention to shape behavior)
- Maintain **40-hour-per-week intensity** that dominates a child's life
- Focus on observable behavior while ignoring internal states

### 6.3 Naturalistic vs. Discrete Trial Training

**Naturalistic Developmental Behavioral Interventions (NDBIs)** represent the primary "reformed" approach:

**Key characteristics of NDBIs**:
- Combine developmental science and ABA principles
- Follow the child's lead and interests
- Teach in natural environments (home, playground)
- Focus on engagement and social motivation
- Use operant learning within natural interactions

**Key NDBIs**:
- **ESDM** (Early Start Denver Model)
- **JASPER** (Joint Attention, Symbolic Play, Engagement, and Regulation)
- **PRT** (Pivotal Response Training)

**Study**: Schreibman, L., et al. (2015). "Naturalistic Developmental Behavioral Interventions: Empirically Validated Treatments for Autism Spectrum Disorder." *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 45, 2411-2428.
- **PMC**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4513196/
- Describes NDBIs as combining best practices from developmental science and ABA

**Comparison RCT (2023)**: Multi-site study comparing 25 hrs/week DTT to 2.5-20 hrs/week JASPER
- 45% of participants gained typical spoken language (both groups)
- Children with lower receptive language who received DTT showed larger long-term gains
- But DTT group received 10x the hours

**Barriers to implementation**: A 2024 study found significant challenges in implementing NDBIs in ABA settings:
- Available at: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11707165/
- Many ABA providers trained primarily in DTT struggle to shift to naturalistic approaches
- Insurance billing structures may incentivize higher hours of traditional ABA

### 6.4 Ross, Leaf et al. Research

**Study**: Leaf, J.B., Cihon, J.H., Leaf, R., McEachin, J., Liu, N., Russell, N., Unumb, L., Shapiro, S., & Khosrowshahi, D. (2022). "Concerns About ABA-Based Intervention: An Evaluation and Recommendations." *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 52, 2838-2853.
- **DOI**: 10.1007/s10803-021-05137-y
- **PubMed**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34132968/
- **PMC**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9114057/
- **Key positions**:
  - Evaluated criticisms of ABA and provided recommendations
  - Acknowledged that "the progression of ABA should include a move away from rigidly adhering to protocols"
  - Recommended movement toward "in-the-moment analysis in more naturalistic contexts"
  - Argued that blanket claims of ABA causing masking are difficult to support with standardized measures
  - Recommended future research should evaluate conditions including/excluding extinction while measuring trauma and participant preference
  - Noted that "currently, the research evaluating procedures involving extinction do not seem to provide evidence that extinction results in trauma" but acknowledged most studies **didn't explicitly evaluate trauma**

**Response from Melton et al. (2025)**: "Does Applied Behavior Analysis Violate Principles of Ethics and Bioethics? A Response to Wilkenfeld and McCarthy (2020)."
- Available at: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/10883576251349214
- Defended ABA against bioethics-based criticisms

### 6.5 Do Reforms Address the Core Concern of Normalization?

**Study**: Wilkenfeld, D.A. & McCarthy, A.M. (2020). "Ethical Concerns with Applied Behavior Analysis for Autism Spectrum 'Disorder'." *Kennedy Institute of Ethics Journal*, 30(1), 31-69.
- **DOI**: 10.1353/ken.2020.0000
- **PubMed**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32336692/
- **Available at**: https://muse.jhu.edu/article/753840
- **Key arguments**:
  - ABA manifests "systematic violations of the fundamental tenets of bioethics"
  - Violates principles of **justice, nonmaleficence, and autonomy**
  - Infringes on autonomy of both children and (when pushed aggressively) parents
  - From a bioethical perspective, autism advocates are "fully justified in their concerns"

**The fundamental question** raised by critics: Even if aversives are removed, even if sessions are play-based, even if children's preferences are sometimes honored -- does ABA still define autism as a collection of **behaviors to be modified** rather than a neurological difference to be supported?

**Five major criticisms identified** (from neurodiversity-affirming ABA literature review, 2024):
1. ABA seeks to **erase autistic identity / encourage masking**
2. Overreliance on compliance causes **long-term mental health harm**
3. Reduces people to "**four functions**," ignoring whole-person needs
4. **Autistic voices absent** from research and practice
5. Professionals **pressure parents** toward ABA exclusively

**Neurodiversity-affirming recommendations** from within ABA (2024):
- Adopt trauma-informed care principles
- Implement "kind extinction" (validate emotions during procedures)
- Question behavior targets: "Is this different from neurotypical norms?" (not inherently problematic)
- Assess client emotional well-being continuously
- Avoid withholding comfort/safety items as contingent reinforcers
- Create neurodiversity advisory boards
- Hire and retain autistic employees
- Include autistic co-investigators in research

## 7. Research Integrity Concerns

### Bottema-Beutel & Crowley (2021)

**Study**: Bottema-Beutel, K. & Crowley, S. (2021). "Pervasive Undisclosed Conflicts of Interest in Applied Behavior Analysis Autism Literature." *Frontiers in Psychology*, 12, 676303.
- **DOI**: 10.3389/fpsyg.2021.676303
- **PubMed**: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34025538/
- **PMC**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8131529/
- **Full text**: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2021.676303/full

**Methodology**:
- Examined articles published over a **1-year period**
- Reviewed studies from **8 journals** devoted to behavioral research
- Coded for COIs related to employment as ABA clinical provider or training consultant

**Key findings**:
- **84%** of studies had at least one author with a clinical/training consultancy COI
- These were disclosed as COIs in only **2%** of studies
- **87%** of studies claiming no COIs were authored by researchers found to have COIs
- Pervasive undisclosed COIs likely lead to researcher bias
- Could partially account for persistent poor study quality in ABA research

**Significance**: This study provides a structural explanation for why ABA maintains strong "evidence-based" status despite the concerns raised by autistic people: the researchers producing the evidence are also the people providing and profiting from ABA services, and they are not disclosing this.

## Controversies & Debates

### Central Tension
The ABA debate represents a fundamental clash between two paradigms:
- **Medical/behavioral model**: Autism is a disorder requiring treatment; autistic behaviors are deficits to be remediated; "evidence-based practice" (measured by behavioral change) validates ABA
- **Neurodiversity/social model**: Autism is a neurological difference; autistic behaviors serve adaptive functions; "evidence" that doesn't measure wellbeing is not meaningful evidence; autistic people's own testimony of harm should be centered

### Key Contested Claims

| Claim | Pro-ABA Position | Anti-ABA/Neurodiversity Position |
|-------|-----------------|----------------------------------|
| ABA is "evidence-based" | Endorsed by Surgeon General, APA; meta-analyses show gains | 84% of studies have undisclosed COIs; 0% measure QoL; 93% don't mention harms |
| Modern ABA is different | No aversives; positive reinforcement; play-based | Same operant conditioning framework; still targets autistic behaviors for extinction |
| ABA teaches useful skills | Communication, safety, daily living skills | These can be taught without 40 hrs/week of behavioral conditioning |
| Stimming should be reduced | "Interferes with learning" | No evidence it interferes; serves self-regulatory function (Kapp 2019) |
| ABA causes PTSD | Kupferstein 2018 has methodological flaws | 46% met threshold; Aguirre Mtanous 2025 (n=34,240) found 30% higher hospitalization |
| ABA is "the only evidence-based treatment" | Most researched autism intervention | Because ABA dominates funding; alternatives aren't funded for research at same scale |

## Research Gaps

1. **Longitudinal studies**: No large-scale, long-term studies tracking mental health outcomes of ABA recipients into adulthood exist (Aguirre Mtanous 2025 is the first large-scale quantitative study)
2. **Non-speaking autistic perspectives**: Most experience studies rely on speaking autistic adults; non-speaking individuals' perspectives are largely absent from the research
3. **Quality of life measures**: Virtually no ABA outcome studies include validated quality-of-life instruments
4. **Comparison studies**: No randomized controlled trials comparing ABA outcomes to non-behavioral interventions (occupational therapy, speech therapy alone, unstructured support) on long-term wellbeing
5. **Dose-response for harm**: Unknown at what intensity/duration ABA transitions from potentially beneficial skill-building to harmful conditioning
6. **Masking measurement**: No standardized tool specifically measures the degree to which ABA increases masking behaviors vs. builds genuine skills
7. **Intersectionality**: Minimal research on how ABA impacts autistic people who are also BIPOC, LGBTQ+, or multiply disabled
8. **Economic analysis**: The ABA industry generates $17+ billion annually; no independent analysis of how financial incentives shape research and practice
9. **Intergenerational effects**: No research on how parents' ABA training affects family dynamics and autistic children's sense of acceptance at home

## Subtopics for Further Research

- ABA industry economics and lobbying influence on insurance mandates
- Comparison of occupational therapy vs. ABA for sensory processing support
- The Judge Rotenberg Center and continued use of electric shock devices in 2026
- International variation in ABA adoption and regulation
- Autistic-led alternatives to ABA (e.g., SPELL framework, DIR/Floortime outcomes)
- The role of parent training in ABA and its effects on parent-child attachment
- Legal challenges to ABA mandates by autistic self-advocates
