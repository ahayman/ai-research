# Research Validation Report: Internal Family Systems (IFS) Therapy — Autism, Alexithymia, and Complex Trauma Intersection

## Validation Summary

**Overall Reliability**: Moderate-to-High. The exploration is mostly factually accurate, with one significant framing problem (SAMHSA "registry inclusion") and a few notable taxonomy gaps. The 10-category structure is largely sound but is missing at least two clinically important categories (couples/IFIO and psychedelic-assisted IFS) and arguably a third (substance use).

- **Confirmed**: 13 claims (72%)
- **Uncertain**: 4 claims (22%)
- **Invalid**: 1 claim (6%)

**Key Findings**:
1. The empirical claims around the Schwartz origin story, Brewer/Bird interoception research, ~50% alexithymia-in-autism prevalence, the 2013 Shadick rheumatoid arthritis RCT, the 2025 scoping review (27 studies / 2 RCTs), the Hodgdon 2021 PTSD pilot (92% remission), Schwartz's *No Bad Parts* (2021), van der Kolk's endorsement, IFS Institute training prices, "8 Cs," Falconer's "Others Within Us"/"unattached burdens," Janina Fisher's TIST, and Sarah Bergenfield's existence and bona fides are all independently confirmed.
2. The "SAMHSA National Registry" claim is technically true historically but materially misleading as currently framed. SAMHSA's NREPP was effectively discontinued/shuttered in January 2018 (contract terminated December 2017, website phased out 2018). IFS was added in November 2015, but the registry no longer exists. Saying IFS "was added to SAMHSA's National Registry" without acknowledging the registry was shut down 7+ years ago overstates current institutional endorsement. Downstream content needs to handle this carefully.
3. The "45,764 therapists on PsychologyToday.com" claim is confirmed by the Society for the Advancement of Psychotherapy article that originated it — but this is a single-source data point from one website's self-reporting and should be cited as such, not as an independent metric of clinical penetration.
4. The taxonomy is missing at least two important categories: **IFIO/couples** (Toni Herbine-Blank's well-established sub-modality) and **IFS + psychedelic-assisted therapy** (a major and growing application; MAPS-MDMA research found ~80% of participants spontaneously used parts language). Substance use applications are also under-represented — there is now published pilot work on online group IFS for PTSD-SUD (2025) and a 2023 Schwartz/Sykes/Sweezy book on IFS for addictions.
5. The user's stated priority — alexithymia as a separate category — is well-justified by the Brewer/Bird finding (alexithymia, not autism, drives interoceptive impairment) plus the Kinnaird et al. 2019 prevalence meta-analysis. This is not puffed up.
6. The exploration appropriately surfaces critiques (Society for the Advancement of Psychotherapy, *The Nation*, Mad in America). It does not mention the **Castlewood Treatment Center** controversy — which involves a different "Schwartz" (Mark, no relation to Richard) but became entangled in IFS reputation discussions and has been used as ammunition in the *New York* magazine *The Cut* coverage. Worth flagging in Category 5.

**Systemic Issues**:
- One significant temporal-currency problem (SAMHSA registry no longer exists)
- Several claims rely heavily on Foundation-for-Self-Leadership-funded or IFS-Institute-affiliated research; the conflict-of-interest concern is named in Category 5 but should be made more explicit in Category 4 (Evidence Base) where it most belongs.
- "Sarah Bergenfield as autistic IFS practitioner" is confirmed and stronger than the exploration suggests — she has co-authored a published book (*Wired to Feel: Autism as a Condition of Sensory Surplus*, 2025, with Martha Sweezy and Beth Ann Malow MD) and was the first openly autistic person to present at the IFS conference (2022). The taxonomy could lean on her more confidently.
- Some sources are clinician blogs (Substack, individual practitioner sites) — the exploration is generally honest about this in its "Research Gaps" section. Good.

## Claim-by-Claim Validation

### Claim 1: "Schwartz developed IFS in the 1980s working with bulimic patients at the Institute for Juvenile Research, Chicago"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Original context**: Topic Overview, Category 3
- **Evidence for**: Multiple independent sources (Wikipedia, IFS Institute, Mad in America, GoodTherapy biography) confirm Schwartz earned his Ph.D. in marriage and family therapy at Purdue and worked at the Institute for Juvenile Research at the University of Illinois at Chicago. He undertook a bulimia outcome study with about 30 bulimic patients and their families and found family therapy alone "didn't do much," which led to his observation of internal "parts." The mid-1980s timing is well-attested.
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia: Richard C. Schwartz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_C._Schwartz), [IFS Institute: Founder bio](https://ifs-institute.com/about-us/richard-c-schwartz-phd), [Mad in America: Interview with Schwartz](https://www.madinamerica.com/2022/07/interview-richard-schwartz-ifs/)
- **Notes**: The narrative is consistent across hostile and friendly sources, which strengthens it.

### Claim 2: "IFS was added to SAMHSA's National Registry for Evidence-Based Programs and Practices"
- **Verdict**: UNCERTAIN (5/10) — historically true but materially misleading as currently framed
- **Original context**: Topic Overview, Category 4 sub-aspects
- **Evidence for**: A comprehensive application was submitted in April 2014; SAMHSA's independent scientific review affirmed the findings; IFS was listed on NREPP as of November 23, 2015. IFS was rated "effective" for general functioning and well-being and "promising" for several other outcomes. This is well-documented across IFS-affiliated and independent sources.
- **Evidence against**: NREPP was effectively discontinued in early 2018. SAMHSA's contract for NREPP was terminated for the convenience of the government on December 28, 2017; the website was phased out in 2018; SAMHSA launched a different "Evidence-Based Practices Resource Center" in April 2018 that "does not replace NREPP and does not contain all of the resources" previously available. The Trump administration's SAMHSA assistant secretary criticized NREPP's standards as "poor" and described its presentation as "skewed." IFS-affiliated sources (Foundation for Self Leadership) host an archived/preserved version (`samhsa-nrepp.foundationifs.org`) but this is a private mirror, not a current federal endorsement.
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia: NREPP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Registry_of_Evidence-Based_Programs_and_Practices), [The Hill: Trump administration ends registry](https://thehill.com/policy/healthcare/368404-trump-administration-ends-national-database-for-substance-abuse-mental/), [Washington Post: NREPP frozen](https://www.washingtonpost.com/national/health-science/trump-administration-freezes-database-of-addiction-and-mental-health-programs/2018/01/10/ed421654-f577-11e7-beb6-c8d48830c54d_story.html), [Foundation IFS NREPP mirror](https://samhsa-nrepp.foundationifs.org/)
- **Notes**: The exploration is technically accurate ("was added") but the way the model is currently marketed often implies live federal endorsement. The Category 4 sub-aspect "SAMHSA NREPP listing: what inclusion does and does not certify" is the right move; it just needs to make explicit that the registry no longer exists, that the listing was for general functioning/well-being only, and that "evidence-based" framing rests on a now-defunct registry's 2015 evaluation.

### Claim 3: "Shadick et al. 2013 rheumatoid arthritis RCT (n=79) found sustained reductions in pain, depression, and improvements in self-compassion at 1-year follow-up"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED with caveats
- **Original context**: Topic Overview, Key Facts, Category 4
- **Evidence for**: Verified primary source: Shadick NA et al., "A Randomized Controlled Trial of an Internal Family Systems-based Psychotherapeutic Intervention on Outcomes in Rheumatoid Arthritis: A Proof-of-Concept Study," *Journal of Rheumatology*, vol. 40, no. 11, pp. 1831–1841, 2013 (DOI: 10.3899/jrheum.121465). Patients with RA were randomized to IFS group for 9 months (n=39) or education control (n=40). Group differences favoring IFS were found for pain severity and physical function at posttreatment, and for self-assessed joint function, depression, and self-compassion at 1-year follow-up.
- **Sources**: [PubMed 23950186](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23950186/), [J Rheumatol full text](https://www.jrheum.org/content/40/11/1831)
- **Notes**: The exploration's framing as "the strongest single IFS trial" is fair — it remains the most-cited and most-rigorous IFS trial. But it is explicitly labeled "proof-of-concept" by the authors themselves, which the exploration does not fully convey. Also: this is the *only* RCT not in a depression-comparison context, and Foundation for Self Leadership / IFS-affiliated authorship is common in the IFS evidence base — a conflict-of-interest pattern worth naming.

### Claim 4: "2025 scoping review of 27 IFS studies found only two RCTs (~7%)"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Original context**: Topic Overview, Key Facts, Category 4
- **Evidence for**: Buys ME, "Exploring the evidence for Internal Family Systems therapy: a scoping review of current research, gaps, and future directions," *Clinical Psychologist*, 2025 (DOI: 10.1080/13284207.2025.2533127). Twenty-seven studies met inclusion criteria: 17 case studies, 5 quasi-experimental, 3 qualitative, 2 RCTs. The two RCTs explored IFS for depression (Haddock 2016) and rheumatoid arthritis (Shadick 2013). The review concluded the evidence was "promising" for PTSD, depression, and chronic pain but limited in scope.
- **Sources**: [Tandfonline: Buys 2025 scoping review](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/13284207.2025.2533127)
- **Notes**: Confirmed precisely as the exploration described.

### Claim 5: "~50% alexithymia prevalence in autistic adults vs. ~5% in neurotypical samples"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Original context**: Topic Overview, Key Facts, Category 6, Category 7
- **Evidence for**: Kinnaird E, Stewart C, Tchanturia K, "Investigating alexithymia in autism: A systematic review and meta-analysis," *European Psychiatry*, 2019. Weighted mean prevalence in ASD groups: 49.93% (range 33.3–63%); neurotypical groups: 4.89%. This is widely cited.
- **Sources**: [PMC6331035](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6331035/), [PubMed 30399531](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30399531/)
- **Notes**: Exact numbers match the exploration. Note: ~40% of autistic adults do NOT have elevated alexithymia, which is worth saying out loud in Category 7 to avoid the implication that alexithymia is universal in autism.

### Claim 6: "Brewer et al. — alexithymia, not autism, is associated with impaired interoception"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Original context**: Topic Overview, Category 6, Category 7
- **Evidence for**: Shah P, Hall R, Catmur C, Bird G, "Alexithymia, not autism, is associated with impaired interoception," *Cortex*, 2016 (DOI: 10.1016/j.cortex.2016.05.021). Used heartbeat detection task across two experiments. Found that alexithymia, not autism per se, was associated with atypical interoception, and that impaired interoception "should not be considered a feature of ASD" but is due to co-occurring alexithymia.
- **Sources**: [PubMed 27253723](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27253723/), [PMC4962768](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4962768/), [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0010945216300594)
- **Notes**: Minor nit: the exploration cites this as "Brewer et al." but the lead author is **Punit Shah** (with Bird as senior author). It would be more accurate to call this "Shah, Hall, Catmur, Bird (2016)" or "Bird group's 2016 *Cortex* paper." This is a citation hygiene issue, not a substance issue. Also: the heartbeat-detection paradigm itself has been critiqued (Zamariola et al. 2018 and follow-ups) — the underlying finding is widely cited but the operationalization of interoception is contested. Worth a hedge in Category 6.

### Claim 17: "Hodgdon et al. PTSD pilot study, 92% no longer met PTSD criteria at 1-month follow-up, depression effect d=−1.51"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED with significant caveats
- **Original context**: Key Facts, Category 4
- **Evidence for**: Hodgdon HB et al., "Internal Family Systems (IFS) Therapy for Posttraumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) among Survivors of Multiple Childhood Trauma: A Pilot Effectiveness Study," *Journal of Aggression, Maltreatment & Trauma*, 2021. Sample n=17 adults with PTSD and multiple childhood traumas; 16 sessions of 90 minutes; 92% no longer met PTSD criteria at 1-month follow-up; PTSD effect size d=−4.46 (very large), with significant reductions also in depression, somatization, dissociation, and affect dysregulation.
- **Sources**: [Tandfonline full text](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10926771.2021.2013375), [IFS Institute summary](https://ifs-institute.com/resources/research/internal-family-systems-ifs-therapy-posttraumatic-stress-disorder-ptsd-among)
- **Notes**: Confirmed. Caveats the exploration partially names but should emphasize more: (a) **uncontrolled pilot** with n=17 — no control group, no randomization; (b) effect sizes from uncontrolled pilots routinely shrink dramatically in subsequent RCTs; (c) the study was funded by the Foundation for Self Leadership, an IFS-affiliated funder. The d=−4.46 PTSD effect is improbably large by any standard psychotherapy benchmark and should be reported with that caution.

### Claim 18: "Multiplicity-of-mind antecedents: Assagioli's psychosynthesis, Jung's complexes, Berne's ego states"
- **Verdict**: UNCERTAIN (8/10) — broadly correct lineage but academic engagement is contested
- **Original context**: Category 3

- **Evidence against**: Some critics argue the lineage is asserted but not deeply theorized — i.e., IFS uses the *language* of multiplicity but does not seriously engage with Assagioli's or Jung's actual frameworks beyond name-dropping. There are also complaints that ego-state therapy (Watkins) has a more direct historical claim than IFS often acknowledges.
- **Sources**: [Mad in America: Schwartz interview](https://www.madinamerica.com/2022/07/interview-richard-schwartz-ifs/), [Parts & Self: Co-Constructions](https://partsandself.org/co-constructions-the-origins-of-parts/)
- **Notes**: The lineage claim is true at the level of "Schwartz cites these as influences." Whether IFS substantively builds on these traditions is contested. Worth a brief hedge in Category 3.

## Data Validation

### Statistic 1: "weighted mean alexithymia prevalence 49.93%"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Primary source check**: Kinnaird, Stewart, Tchanturia 2019 *European Psychiatry* meta-analysis; figure is exactly as reported.
- **Recency**: Current; 2019 publication, no superseding meta-analysis found.
- **Context**: Range across studies 33.3–63%; substantial heterogeneity; ~40% of autistic adults are not alexithymic.

### Statistic 2: "Neurotypical alexithymia prevalence ~5%"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED (4.89% in the same meta-analysis)
- **Primary source check**: Same Kinnaird 2019 meta-analysis.
- **Recency**: Current.
- **Context**: General population estimates from other sources range ~7–10% (community samples often higher); the 4.89% figure is specifically the comparison sample in this meta-analysis.

### Statistic 3: "27 IFS studies, 2 RCTs, 17 case studies, 5 quasi-experimental, 3 qualitative"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Primary source check**: Buys 2025 *Clinical Psychologist* scoping review; counts confirmed.
- **Recency**: Current (2025).
- **Context**: This is a scoping review, not a systematic review; inclusion criteria are broader; "promising" conclusion is the authors' framing.

### Statistic 4: "45,764 therapists on PsychologyToday with IFS"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED (single-source, single-snapshot)
- **Primary source check**: Brownstone & Hunsicker 2024 *Psychotherapy Bulletin*. The figure is theirs and represents an April 2024 PsychologyToday filter count.
- **Recency**: April 2024; almost certainly higher now.
- **Context**: Self-reported / self-categorized by therapists on a directory; not equivalent to "trained" or "certified."

### Statistic 5: "IFS Level 1 $3,990–$5,300"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Primary source check**: IFS Institute's own training pages list per-training tuition; specific Online example at $3,990. *The Nation* names the upper end.
- **Recency**: Current (2024–2025).
- **Context**: No payment plans; non-refundable $500 deposit; limited partial scholarships; no in-house pricing transparency in a single page.

### Statistic 6: "92% no longer met PTSD criteria at 1-month follow-up; effect size d=−4.46"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED but should be hedged heavily
- **Primary source check**: Hodgdon 2021 pilot. Numbers verified.
- **Recency**: Current (2021).
- **Context**: **Uncontrolled pilot**, n=17, IFS-Foundation-funded. d=−4.46 is implausibly large for a stand-alone PTSD intervention by any benchmark and would almost certainly shrink in a controlled RCT. Use with extreme care.

## Taxonomy Completeness Assessment

### Are the 10 categories mutually distinct?
**Yes, mostly.** Categories 6 (alexithymia) and 7 (autism) overlap but are appropriately separated; the exploration explicitly addresses this and makes the right call. Categories 5 (critiques) and 9 (failure modes) are appropriately distinct (epistemic vs. clinical levels). Categories 3 (ontology) and 5 (critiques) overlap somewhat but the steel-man-then-attack structure justifies separation. **No collapses recommended.**

### What is missing?
**Three significant gaps:**

1. **IFIO / IFS for couples** — Toni Herbine-Blank's "Intimacy from the Inside Out" model is a well-established and IFS-Institute-recognized application of IFS for couples therapy. There is a 2021 Norton textbook (Herbine-Blank & Sweezy, *Internal Family Systems Couple Therapy Skills Manual*). It has its own training track and certification. Excluding this from a 10-category IFS deep dive is a meaningful omission. **Recommendation: add as a category, OR fold into Category 8 (complex trauma) only if relational trauma framing dominates — but this is not really the same intersection.** The user's stated focus is autism/alexithymia/complex-trauma; IFIO is genuinely tangential to that focus, so a full category may not be warranted, but at minimum Category 5 or Category 4 should mention IFIO as a major sub-modality where relevant.

2. **IFS + psychedelic-assisted therapy** — This is a major and rapidly growing area. MAPS-MDMA Phase 3 PTSD research found ~80% of high-dose MDMA participants spontaneously used parts language. There is a substantial overlap with ketamine-assisted therapy (Nick Brüss's work). Schwartz himself has spoken extensively about psychedelics, spirit guides, and unattached burdens in this context. Falconer's *The Others Within Us* is in part a psychedelic-adjacent text. **Recommendation: This deserves explicit treatment.** The question is whether it gets a full category or is folded into Category 5 (critiques/commercial) since the psychedelic-IFS overlap is also a commercial growth vector. The honest answer for the user's profile (skeptical autistic systems thinker with complex trauma) is that a separate category is warranted both because the clinical claims are interesting and because the spiritual / parapsychological framing is highly contested.

3. **Spiritual / parapsychological / non-dual elements as a distinct category** — The exploration tucks "spirit guides," "unattached burdens," and Schwartz's spirituality course into Category 5 (critiques). This is defensible but understates how central these elements have become to Schwartz's current public framing. There is a Sounds True course "The Spirituality of IFS." Schwartz has done podcast interviews explicitly affirming "spiritual phenomena that cannot be explained by conventional science." **Recommendation: either elevate to its own category, OR be very explicit in Category 3 (ontology) and Category 5 (critiques) that this is not a fringe deviation but a turn the founder is leading.** The user's note about distinguishing IFS-Institute canon from peer-reviewed evidence makes this especially important — the spirituality material is now part of IFS-Institute canon.

### What is over-represented or could be merged?
Nothing should be merged. The 10 categories are appropriately scoped. If gaps are addressed by adding categories, the deep dive grows to 11–13 categories — still tractable.

### Is the user's stated priority (alexithymia as separate category) justified?
**Yes, strongly.** The Brewer/Bird (Shah et al. 2016) finding that alexithymia, not autism, drives interoceptive impairment is the load-bearing empirical anchor. IFS mechanics depend on interoceptive access. Alexithymia exists in non-autistic populations too. Treating alexithymia as a feature of the model's fit problem rather than as a feature of autism is precisely correct and reflects what the literature actually says. **Not puffed up.** The exploration is right to keep this separate.

### Are there contested-but-important categories that were skipped?
**Race / culture / structural critique.** This intersects with Category 5 critiques but is its own thread. IFS as an individualizing framework risks personalizing problems that are inherently structural (racism, class, gender-based oppression). There is published critique of family-therapy approaches generally on this ground (Roy-Chowdhury 2022 *Journal of Family Therapy*) and IFS-specific writing in Black Therapists Rock, Engaging Difference, and similar outlets. For a deep dive aimed at a sophisticated systems-thinker, this is a worthwhile category — but for the *autism/alexithymia/complex-trauma* user profile specifically, it is somewhat tangential. **Recommendation: do not add as a full category, but add a brief note in Category 5 or Category 9 acknowledging the structural-critique dimension exists.**

### Substance use / addiction:
The exploration's Category 4 (Evidence Base) notes "eating disorders (despite IFS's origin)" as a gap, but does not surface the 2023 Schwartz/Sykes/Sweezy book *Internal Family Systems Therapy for Addictions* or the 2025 PARTS-SUD pilot (Frontiers in Psychiatry). For complex-trauma readers, addiction comorbidity is highly relevant. **Recommendation: add a sub-aspect in Category 4 (or Category 8) on substance use applications, including the conceptual framing (firefighter parts as substance-involving) and the limited but emerging evidence.**

- **Priority**: HIGH
- **Driven by**: Claim 2 rated UNCERTAIN (5/10); the registry has been defunct since early 2018 yet the exploration treats the listing as a current institutional credential.

- **Priority**: HIGH

- **Priority**: HIGH

- **Why**: A skeptical reader needs to know both the headline number and the asterisks on it. Without sufficient hedging, this becomes the kind of "miracle cure" claim Mad in America critiques. With proper context, it is a promising pilot signal that deserves a confirmatory RCT.
