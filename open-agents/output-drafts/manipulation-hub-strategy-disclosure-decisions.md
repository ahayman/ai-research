## 1. Why This Page Exists

Most guidance on disclosure treats it as a simple, universally good thing: "tell someone." The research does not support that framing. The most rigorous work on disclosure outcomes comes from Sarah Ullman's research on sexual assault survivors (Ullman, 2010, *Talking About Sexual Assault: Society's Response to Survivors*; reviewed extensively in systematic reviews, e.g., PMC8248429). The core finding is sobering:

**Negative disclosure reactions harm survivors more than positive reactions help them.** This asymmetry is large, consistent across studies, and has direct implications for how disclosure decisions should be made. It is not that disclosure is bad; it is that disclosure to the wrong person can be worse than no disclosure at all, and disclosure to the right person is dramatically better.

This transforms the question. "Should I tell someone?" is not the right question. The right question is: **who, when, in what order, at what level of detail, and with what preparation?**

For autistic readers specifically, disclosure decisions involve additional considerations beyond the general research base:

1. **The credibility discount.** Lim, Young & Brewer (2022, PMC8813809) found autistic adults are perceived as more deceptive and less credible than neurotypical adults when telling the truth. Disclosure to a non-autism-informed audience starts at a disadvantage that has nothing to do with the content of what you are disclosing. This affects audience selection.

2. **Processing differences.** Under the cognitive load of disclosing difficult content, autistic pragmatic processing often becomes more effortful, not less. You may struggle more with nuance, tone, and framing precisely when those things matter most. Pre-planning is not optional; it is compensation for a real processing constraint.

3. **The info-dump risk.** Many autistic adults have a specific pattern of providing exhaustive detail when asked about something they have been carrying alone. This can be protective in the right context (provides full information to an advocate who needs it) and harmful in the wrong one (overwhelms a casual listener, provides ammunition to a hostile audience, or produces records that can be disclosed in legal contexts).

4. **The diagnostic disclosure question.** For autistic readers, disclosure of a difficult situation often intersects with disclosure of autism itself. These are separate strategic decisions and should be treated as such. Telling someone you are being harmed by your partner is not the same decision as telling them you are autistic, and conflating them can produce worse outcomes than separating them.

This page provides a structured way to make disclosure decisions that accounts for the research base and the autism-specific considerations.

## 2. The Disclosure Asymmetry

Ullman's research and subsequent systematic reviews establish a finding that must be front-loaded before any decision framework:

**Positive reactions to disclosure** (belief, emotional support, validation, respect for the survivor's autonomy) produce moderate positive effects on recovery. They help, but not dramatically.

**Negative reactions** (disbelief, blame, minimization, pressure to take specific actions the survivor is not ready for, unwanted advice, withdrawal, gossip, weaponization of the disclosed information) produce large negative effects. They cause measurable harm beyond what the original situation did. Some of the most persistent trauma symptoms in disclosure research are traceable not to the original abuse but to what happened when the survivor tried to tell someone.

**The asymmetry has three major implications:**

1. **Selection matters more than sheer number.** Telling three good listeners is dramatically better than telling six people where two are harmful. Quality beats quantity because one bad disclosure can produce more harm than three good disclosures produce benefit.

2. **The default is cautious, not open.** If you are unsure whether a specific person will respond well, that is already a reason to delay disclosure to that person until you have more information. You can always disclose later; you cannot undisclose.

3. **Sequencing matters.** The first person you tell has a disproportionate effect on your subsequent willingness to tell anyone. A positive first disclosure supports further disclosure; a negative first disclosure can shut down the process for years. Choose the first audience with particular care.

*Evidence quality: [VALIDATED]. Ullman's original findings have been replicated across multiple populations and disclosure contexts. The asymmetry is one of the better-established findings in the disclosure literature.*

## 3. The Audience Map

Different audiences serve different functions and carry different risks. This section provides an audience-by-audience analysis rather than a single universal recommendation.

### Reality-check network members (the safest first audience)

**Function:** External memory, pattern recognition, validation, emotional support.

**Risk profile:** Low by design. If you have built a reality-check network according to the [Reality-Check Networks page](manipulation-hub-strategy-reality-check-networks.md), these are already vetted for disclosure safety.

**What to disclose:** Generally more than less. Network members need context to serve their function. Holding back reduces their usefulness.

**When to disclose:** Early and often. Network members should be hearing about small things as well as big ones.

**Autism-specific note:** This is the one audience where the info-dump tendency is a feature rather than a bug. Giving them thorough information helps them help you.

### Advocates (the highest-value professional audience)

**Function:** Safety planning, risk assessment, navigation of complex systems, connection to other resources.

**Risk profile:** Low. Advocates at recognized DV/SA/crisis organizations are bound by confidentiality with narrow, disclosed exceptions. They do not typically share your information with your abuser, your employer, or anyone else without your consent. They also do not typically push you toward specific actions — a good advocate's job is to help you understand your options, not to decide for you.

**What to disclose:** What they need to help you. They know what questions to ask. Let them ask rather than volunteering everything at once.

**When to disclose:** When you need help thinking through a specific decision, when you need to safety-plan, when you need access to specialized resources, or when you want an external reality-check from someone trained in exactly this work. You do not have to be "in crisis" to contact an advocate.

**Practical tips:**
- Call from a device the person causing harm does not monitor
- Ask about confidentiality and mandatory reporting before sharing specifics: "Can you walk me through your confidentiality policy before I tell you what's happening?"
- You can remain anonymous with most hotlines
- If the first advocate is not a good fit, call back or try a different resource

**Autism-specific note:** Most advocates are not autism-informed. Lead with what you need, not with your diagnosis. You may need to give them more specific information about your communication preferences than you would with an autism-informed professional. See Section 6 for the diagnostic disclosure decision.

### Therapists (more complex than commonly assumed)

**Function:** Ongoing support, trauma processing, mental health treatment, professional perspective.

**Risk profile:** Variable. Depends enormously on whether you have chosen the therapist or been assigned one, whether they are trauma-informed, whether they are autism-informed, and whether they work for you directly or for an employer, insurance company, or other third party.

**What to consider:**

- **Employer Assistance Programs (EAPs)** are not confidential in the way many employees assume. Some EAPs report aggregate data to employers; individual confidentiality protections depend on the specific program. For workplace harassment situations, EAP therapy can create records that affect your employment.
- **Insurance-covered therapy** creates diagnosis codes that become part of your medical record and, in some jurisdictions, can affect future insurance eligibility, employment, custody, and immigration.
- **Couples therapy is contraindicated for coercive control situations.** This is covered on the [Can This Be Repaired? page](manipulation-hub-can-this-be-repaired.md). Do not disclose coercive control to a couples therapist with your abuser present; the disclosure can produce retaliation, and the couples therapy framing can collude with the abuser's minimization.
- **Lilienfeld (2007)** documented specific potentially harmful therapies. Some therapeutic approaches, including some approaches to "recovered memory" and some forms of directive intervention, can produce iatrogenic harm. Not all therapy is safe.
- **Autism-informed trauma therapists are rare.** The authority vacuum is real. Many well-intentioned therapists will misinterpret autistic presentations, pathologize autistic communication, or apply frameworks that do not work for autistic clients. Finding a therapist who understands both trauma and autism is difficult and often involves multiple tries.

**When to disclose to a therapist:** Usually, but with specific care about which therapist and in what context. A therapist you have already vetted and worked with for non-crisis issues is a much safer first-disclosure target than a new therapist you are seeking specifically for this situation.

**What to disclose initially:** Less than you think. Give the therapist a chance to demonstrate how they respond to small disclosures before committing to full disclosure. "I'm dealing with a difficult family situation" is an opening that lets you see how they handle it before committing to specifics.

### Medical providers

**Function:** Treatment of physical and mental health effects of the situation; connection to specialized resources; medical record creation.

**Risk profile:** Context-dependent. Medical providers are mandatory reporters in many jurisdictions for specific categories (child abuse, elder abuse, injuries from firearms in some places, imminent harm). They generally cannot disclose your information to your abuser without your consent, but medical records can be subpoenaed in legal proceedings and may be accessible to insurance companies, employers (through disability processes), and others under specific circumstances.

**What to disclose:** What is medically relevant, plus anything you want documented in your medical record as part of a pattern (symptoms of anxiety, depression, PTSD; specific events that affected your health). Medical records are a form of documentation that carries weight in other contexts.

**When to disclose:** When the situation has medical consequences or when you want a medical provider's assessment of something (physical symptoms, psychological symptoms, medication needs).

**Important framing for autistic readers:** Medical providers are particularly susceptible to the credibility discount. "I tend to look calmer than I feel" or "I may not express pain/distress the way you expect" is worth saying explicitly. Bring written notes about symptoms and what you want to discuss — do not rely on recall under the stress of a medical appointment. (See the Medical Setting context page, Tier 4, when built.)

### Family and friends (highly variable)

**Function:** Everyday support, practical help, continuity of relationship.

**Risk profile:** The most variable of any audience category. Ranges from deeply protective (a family member who will drop everything to help and who has no conflicts of interest) to actively harmful (a family member who shares information with your abuser, sides with the harmful party, minimizes, or weaponizes the disclosure). Most family members fall somewhere in between.

**What to consider:**

- **Conflict of interest.** If the family member has their own relationship with the person causing harm, they have a conflict that can distort their response. This is not always disqualifying, but it is a factor.
- **Track record.** How have they responded to previous disclosures, including small ones? Past response is the best predictor of future response.
- **Information security.** Do they share things? Is their discretion reliable? A family member who gossips will almost certainly share your disclosure eventually, whether maliciously or not.
- **Their own stability.** A family member in their own crisis, or with their own significant untreated trauma, may not be able to hold your disclosure without harming themselves or you.
- **Your history with them.** A relationship that has been thin for years is not suddenly available for high-stakes disclosure. A relationship with longstanding trust is a different situation.

**When to disclose:** Only when the answer to "will this help me?" is honestly yes based on the considerations above. Family and friends are not a default audience; they are a specific audience who may or may not be safe.

**What to disclose:** Often less than you would to an advocate or therapist. Family and friends do not need all the details to provide emotional support or practical help. Selective disclosure is legitimate.

### Police and law enforcement (high-stakes, carries specific autism risks)

**Function:** Legal intervention, criminal prosecution, emergency response, protective orders.

**Risk profile:** Mixed and must be considered carefully.

**Autism-specific risks in police encounters:**

- **Autistic communication is frequently misread by police.** Gaze aversion is read as deception. Flat affect is read as lack of remorse or as hostility. Delayed verbal responses under stress are read as non-compliance. Literal answers to questions are read as evasion. Stimming is read as threat or drug intoxication.
- **Disclosing autism during a police encounter** can help or hurt depending on the specific officer. Some departments have autism training; many do not. In a tense encounter, disclosure may or may not slow the situation down, and may or may not be heard.
- **Autistic individuals are documented as having elevated rates of criminal justice system involvement** relative to the non-autistic population, frequently as both offenders (for misunderstood behavior) and as victims whose cases are handled poorly (Slavny-Cross et al., 2022).

**When disclosure to police is indicated:**
- Immediate physical danger
- Specific legal actions you want to initiate (restraining order, criminal charges)
- Situations where an advocate has specifically recommended it and is supporting you through the process

**When disclosure to police is often contraindicated:**
- Any situation where the lethality assessment has flagged risk and an advocate has not signed off on police involvement
- Situations where the police are unlikely to believe you or to act appropriately
- Situations where involving police will destabilize your situation more than the current harm is destabilizing it

**Better approach:** if police involvement might be needed, work with a DV advocate first. They know your local police culture, which precincts and officers are more autism-informed and DV-informed, what happens after a report is made, and how to position the report for the best outcome. Police involvement is almost always better through an advocate than directly.

### HR and employers (structurally compromised)

**Function:** Documented workplace complaint process, access to workplace protections, potential resolution of workplace-specific issues.

**Risk profile:** HR works for the employer, not for you. This is structural. HR may be helpful in specific situations, but their interests and yours are not aligned by default. Disclosures to HR should be made with the understanding that HR may, at their discretion, share the information with the person you are complaining about, with other managers, with legal counsel for the employer, and with future employers through references.

**What to disclose:** Specific, documented, professionally framed facts. Not feelings, not interpretations, not speculation about motives. HR is a venue for factual complaints, not therapeutic disclosure.

**When to disclose:** When you have a specific protection you want to invoke (discrimination complaint, harassment complaint, accommodation request under the ADA), when you need the formal record a HR complaint creates, or when doing so is a required step for future legal action (EEOC complaints have specific exhaustion requirements).

**Before disclosing to HR:**
- Document first. HR complaints that are not supported by written records often go nowhere.
- Talk to an employment lawyer or advocate. The [Crisis Resources Directory](manipulation-hub-crisis-resources.md) lists options. For a 30-minute consultation, you can usually get guidance on whether and how to proceed.
- Understand the specific protection you are trying to invoke. "It wasn't fair" is not a legal claim; "This is discrimination under Title VII / retaliation for a protected activity / failure to accommodate under the ADA" is.

**Autism-specific note:** Accommodation disclosure (disclosing autism to request workplace accommodations) is a separate decision from complaint disclosure. See the Workplace context page (Tier 4, when built) for the accommodation decision specifically.

### Lawyers and legal advocates

**Function:** Legal advice, representation, document review, litigation.

**Risk profile:** Low if the relationship is formally established. Attorney-client privilege protects disclosures within that relationship from most forms of compelled disclosure. This makes lawyers one of the safest audiences for highly sensitive information.

**What to disclose:** Everything that is legally relevant. Lawyers can only advise you based on what they know. Holding back important information usually produces worse legal outcomes.

**When to disclose:** Before taking legal action, before making legally consequential statements to other parties, and whenever you are unsure whether an action you are considering has legal implications.

**Important nuances:**
- Attorney-client privilege requires a formal attorney-client relationship. Casual conversations with a lawyer friend at a party are not privileged. Ask for a formal consultation, even if brief.
- Many jurisdictions have legal aid organizations and bar association lawyer referral services that provide free or low-cost consultations. The [Crisis Resources Directory](manipulation-hub-crisis-resources.md) lists options.
- Privilege covers your communications with the lawyer but not pre-existing documents you possess. Documentation you created before retaining the lawyer may still be subpoenable.

### Online and social media audiences (high risk, usually contraindicated)

**Function:** Broad support, visibility, catharsis, potential public pressure on institutions.

**Risk profile:** Very high. Public disclosure on social media is permanent, searchable, shareable, and uncontrollable. It can reach audiences you did not intend, including the person causing harm, their family and friends, your employer, your future employers, law enforcement, and people who will weaponize the information for their own purposes.

**When public disclosure is usually contraindicated:**
- When you are still in the situation
- When legal proceedings are ongoing or possible
- When lethality indicators are present
- When you have not yet consulted with a lawyer or advocate about the implications
- When you are in an emotional state that may produce different framings than you would choose with more reflection
- When your employer or other stakeholders monitor your social media

**When public disclosure is sometimes indicated:**
- When institutional pressure is the goal and other channels have failed
- When you are significantly out of the situation and have consulted with legal counsel
- When the disclosure is part of coordinated advocacy
- When you have carefully chosen the platform, framing, and audience

**Important note on "me too"-style disclosure:** The broad movement toward public disclosure of abuse has done real good for many survivors. It has also produced specific harms to specific survivors who were not in a position to handle the consequences of public disclosure. The decision to disclose publicly is a personal one with significant implications; it should not be treated as automatically virtuous or automatically reckless.

## 4. The Timing Question

When to disclose is often as important as who to disclose to.

### Too early

- **While you are still processing your own understanding of the situation.** Disclosure before you have clarity about what is happening can produce a cascade of competing narratives from listeners that destabilizes rather than clarifies your own understanding.
- **Before you have resources to handle the consequences.** If disclosure might result in escalation from the person causing harm, loss of relationship, or legal action, you need resources in place before disclosure, not after.
- **In acute crisis.** Disclosures made in crisis states can have different framings than the survivor would choose with reflection. Urgent crises call for crisis resources (hotlines, advocates); non-urgent disclosures can usually wait for calmer states.
- **Before documentation exists.** Disclosure without a written record you can refer back to means your account depends entirely on your verbal recall, which is where the credibility discount hits hardest. Some documentation usually should precede disclosure.

### Too late

- **When you are in the situation but denying it to yourself.** Some disclosures to trusted people are useful specifically because they surface patterns you have been denying. Waiting for certainty before disclosing can mean waiting forever.
- **When the harm is compounding.** Extended isolation makes disclosure harder, not easier. The longer you go without telling anyone, the bigger the perceived weight of disclosure becomes. Incremental disclosures to safe audiences prevent this buildup.
- **After legal deadlines pass.** Some legal actions have statutes of limitations. Employment discrimination complaints have strict deadlines (typically 180–300 days for EEOC). If you are preserving legal options, legal consultation should happen early, even if you are not yet ready to act.

### The right timing

- **To trusted reality-check network members:** early and often. These are the people you should be telling in near-real-time, for exactly the functions those relationships serve.
- **To advocates:** when you need their specific help, which is usually earlier than survivors think they need it.
- **To therapists:** gradually, with attention to how each disclosure is received.
- **To family and friends:** selectively, based on the audience map.
- **To employers, law enforcement, and legal systems:** after consultation with advocates or lawyers, with documentation in place, and with clear strategic purpose.
- **Publicly:** rarely, carefully, with counsel, and usually after exit.

## 5. The "How Much to Disclose" Question

Disclosure is not binary. You do not have to tell any audience the full account to have disclosed. The level of detail should match the audience's function and your strategic purpose.

### The minimum useful disclosure

The smallest disclosure that lets the audience serve their function. For a reality-check network member, this might be "something happened with [person] that I need to talk through." For an advocate, "I need help thinking about my options in a difficult situation." For a therapist, "I'm dealing with a family dynamic that is affecting my mental health."

Starting with a minimum useful disclosure has several advantages:
- It gives the audience a chance to respond in a low-stakes way before you commit to more detail
- It lets you see how they handle the topic before deciding whether to say more
- It protects you from over-disclosure to audiences that turn out to be unsafe
- It respects your own processing speed — you do not have to explain everything at once

### Graduated disclosure

A common approach: start with the minimum useful disclosure, assess how it lands, then disclose more if the assessment is positive. This turns disclosure into an incremental process rather than a single commitment.

Signs the assessment is positive:
- The listener asks questions that show genuine engagement
- They do not immediately pivot to their own agenda
- They offer support without pressure
- They hold the information without visible discomfort that would prevent further disclosure
- They follow up later — a sign they have integrated what you told them

Signs the assessment is negative:
- Dismissal ("That doesn't sound so bad," "Everyone has problems")
- Minimization ("Are you sure you're not overreacting?")
- Immediate advice without listening
- Visible discomfort that shuts down further conversation
- Evidence they have shared your disclosure with others without permission

If the assessment is negative, stop the disclosure at the minimum level you have already reached. Do not try to "win them over" by providing more detail. More detail to a hostile audience produces more ammunition, not more understanding.

### Full disclosure

Sometimes, with the right audience, full disclosure is the right choice. Advocates and lawyers especially need complete information to help effectively. Therapists generally need substantial information over time. A carefully vetted reality-check relationship may eventually receive something like full disclosure.

But "full" is still your choice, not an obligation. You get to decide what is relevant to the audience's function. You do not owe anyone your complete story.

### The info-dump risk

For autistic readers, a specific pattern to watch: when finally given permission to talk about a situation you have been carrying alone, there is a tendency to pour out everything at once — full chronology, full context, full emotional content, full detail. This can be the right choice in a private conversation with a trusted listener who has plenty of time. It is rarely the right choice with:
- Casual acquaintances
- Professionals in time-limited appointments
- People who have asked a narrow question
- Audiences whose function is specific (advocates need certain information; full life history is not the point)
- Legal proceedings where everything you say creates record

**Strategy:** When you feel the info-dump impulse rising with an audience that is not the right fit for it, pause and ask yourself: "What is this audience's function, and what minimum disclosure serves that function?" Provide that. You can always schedule a different conversation with a different audience for the full thing.

## 6. The Separate Decision: Disclosing Autism Itself

For autistic readers, the question "should I disclose this situation?" is often entangled with "should I disclose that I am autistic?" These are separate decisions with different considerations.

### When to disclose autism along with the situation

- **To an autism-informed professional.** The diagnosis is relevant and will be integrated correctly.
- **To a reality-check network member who does not already know.** It affects their ability to contextualize what you are telling them.
- **When the autism is directly relevant to the situation.** If the manipulation pattern specifically weaponizes autism ("that's your autism talking"), the diagnostic context is part of the story.
- **When you need autism-specific accommodations from the audience** to have the conversation at all.

### When to disclose the situation without disclosing autism

- **With non-autism-informed professionals who are handling a specific, bounded issue.** An employment lawyer handling a harassment complaint does not need your autism diagnosis to file the complaint unless the harassment was autism-related.
- **With audiences who will apply the credibility discount if they learn you are autistic.** This is uncomfortable to name directly, but it is a real consideration. In some contexts, withholding the diagnosis preserves credibility you would otherwise lose.
- **In legal proceedings where the diagnosis could be used against you.** Custody cases, competency evaluations, and some employment situations can produce adverse outcomes if the diagnosis becomes part of the record. Consult a lawyer before disclosing autism in formal legal contexts.

### When to disclose autism without disclosing the situation

- **To preemptively contextualize your communication style.** "I am autistic and may need extra time to respond" is a disclosure that helps the interaction without requiring you to explain what is happening in the rest of your life.
- **When requesting accommodations for a specific purpose** that does not require explanation of anything beyond the immediate need.

### When to disclose neither

- **Casual social encounters.** You do not owe anyone either disclosure.
- **Audiences you have not vetted.** Default to caution with unknown audiences.
- **When you are depleted.** Both disclosures cost energy. If you do not have the energy for the follow-up conversation either disclosure produces, delay.

### The framing matters enormously

If you do disclose autism in connection with the situation, framing affects how it lands:

- **Avoid apologetic framing.** "I'm sorry, I'm autistic, so..." invites dismissal. Replace with neutral framing: "I am autistic. I communicate more directly than is typical, and I need..." (specific need).
- **Lead with need, not with diagnosis.** "I need a quiet room for this meeting" is often more useful than "I am autistic, so I might have trouble with sensory input, which means the lighting in here is hard for me, which could affect my concentration..."
- **Name what you want from the disclosure.** "I am autistic, which means my body language may read differently than you expect. Please don't interpret flat affect as lack of concern" is specific and actionable.
- **Expect the credibility discount and compensate.** You will probably need to say your main points more than once, with documentation backing them up, because the diagnostic disclosure will affect how the rest of the information lands.

## 7. Autism-Specific Considerations

### The pre-planning advantage (and necessity)

Autistic pragmatic processing often benefits from written preparation for difficult conversations. For disclosure conversations specifically, pre-planning is a real advantage:

- **Write notes about what you want to say.** Not a script — a list of points and a rough order. You do not have to follow the notes exactly, but having them reduces the real-time processing load.
- **Write out specific sentences you know will be hard to produce under stress.** "I need to leave this conversation if I start feeling overloaded; I am not walking out on you, I am managing my processing." Having this sentence pre-composed means you can deploy it without trying to compose it while depleted.
- **Think through likely responses in advance.** What if they dismiss it? What if they overreact? What if they try to fix it? Having a response ready for each possibility reduces the pressure of real-time improvisation.
- **Know your non-negotiables in advance.** What are you willing to share? What are you not willing to share? What do you need from them? What will you not accept? Clarity on these points before you start the conversation protects you from being talked into more than you intended.

### The meltdown risk

Disclosure of difficult content can trigger overload, especially if the audience responds badly or if the content requires sustained attention to painful material. Plan for this:

- **Have an exit phrase ready.** "I need to pause this conversation" or "I'm going to stop here and come back to it" or just "I need to leave now." Pre-composed phrases you can produce without thinking.
- **Choose a location where exit is physically possible.** Disclosing in your own home, in a car you can leave, or in a public space you can walk away from is safer than disclosing in the other person's space or in an enclosed space where exit is socially awkward.
- **Plan for post-disclosure decompression.** Expect to be depleted after. Schedule low-demand activities. Have sensory comforts available. Do not plan to do anything else important right after a difficult disclosure.
- **Let someone in your reality-check network know what you are about to do.** "I'm going to tell [person] about [situation] this afternoon. Can I text you afterward?" This is pre-disclosure support that reduces the isolation of the disclosure event itself.

### The credibility discount affects audience selection

This is worth repeating because it runs through every disclosure decision. Lim, Young & Brewer (2022) documented that autistic adults are perceived as more deceptive and less credible than neurotypical adults when telling the truth. This has specific implications:

- **Autistic-informed audiences weight your disclosure more accurately.** They are better first targets.
- **Documentation amplifies your voice.** In audience contexts where the credibility discount applies, disclosure backed by written records is dramatically more effective than verbal disclosure alone.
- **Non-autistic friends and family are variable.** The credibility discount applies more strongly to people who do not know you well; close relationships sometimes have calibrated past the general effect.
- **Strangers (including new professionals) apply the discount most heavily.** First disclosures to strangers are therefore the riskiest and should be backed by the most preparation.

### Alexithymia and disclosure

If identifying and naming your own emotional states is difficult, describing the impact of a situation to another person can be hard. Strategies:

- **Describe events and behaviors rather than feelings.** "This happens, and then I can't concentrate for several hours" is easier than "I feel anxious."
- **Use external markers.** Sleep disruption, loss of appetite, inability to focus on work, withdrawal from activities you previously enjoyed. These are concrete and observable.
- **Borrow language from what you have read.** It is not dishonest to use a phrase from a book, article, or this hub to describe something you could not find words for on your own. "I think this is what they mean by [specific concept]" is a valid framing.

### The scripts-under-pressure problem

Autistic adults often rely on specific scripts for difficult situations. Under the stress of disclosure, the scripts may not come to mind, or they may come out in a form that doesn't fit the specific conversation. This is normal. If you find yourself struggling to articulate what you wanted to say:

- **Pause the conversation without apology.** "I need a minute" is not rude. It is reasonable.
- **Refer to your notes.** Pulling out a written list in a serious conversation is fine. It is not a sign that you are being calculating; it is a sign that you are taking the conversation seriously.
- **Offer to continue in writing.** "This is a hard conversation and I process better in writing. Could I send you an email with what I'm trying to say?" is a legitimate accommodation request.

## 8. Strategies by Situation

### If you have no one you trust to disclose to

- **Start with a crisis line.** Anonymous, free, trained advocates. Even a single phone call can provide the reality-check function and can connect you to longer-term resources. See the [Crisis Resources Directory](manipulation-hub-crisis-resources.md).
- **Write down what you would say.** Even without an audience, the act of writing a disclosure narrative is organizing and clarifying. You can use it later when an audience becomes available.
- **Consider online autistic communities.** Not a replacement for verified trust, but a space where the credibility discount is lower and where similar experiences may be validated.
- **Prioritize building a reality-check network.** See the [Reality-Check Networks page](manipulation-hub-strategy-reality-check-networks.md). The first audience for full disclosure is best built before it is needed, but can be built as you go.

### If your initial disclosure goes badly

- **Stop disclosing more to that audience.** Do not try to convince them. More information to a hostile audience produces more harm, not more understanding.
- **Damage-check: what did you disclose, what did they do with it?** If there is risk of the information being shared further, consider whether you need to take protective action.
- **Do not let it shut down all future disclosures.** A bad first disclosure can produce the feeling that disclosure itself is a mistake. It is not; this specific disclosure was a mistake. Different audiences produce different results.
- **Process the disclosure event itself.** A bad disclosure is its own injury that deserves attention. The harm from the negative response is real and worth acknowledging to yourself and, eventually, to a better audience.

### If you need to disclose to a hostile or indifferent audience

- **Minimize verbal content, maximize written documentation.** In adversarial contexts, a tightly-written account is more effective than verbal explanation.
- **Bring a witness or support person.** Even a silent witness changes the dynamic and creates a second memory source.
- **Pre-plan the interaction.** Know what you want to say, what you want from them, and what you will not discuss.
- **Document the disclosure interaction itself.** What you said, what they said in response, time, date, witnesses. This becomes part of the pattern documentation.
- **Consider whether the disclosure is actually needed.** Sometimes a disclosure to a hostile audience is necessary (legal requirements, mandatory steps in a complaint process). Sometimes it is not, and the impulse to disclose is coming from a wish to be understood that the audience cannot satisfy.

### If you are disclosing to the person causing harm

This is a separate category that deserves specific attention. Disclosing to the person causing harm that you have realized what is happening is different from all other disclosures.

- **The research strongly suggests this is contraindicated in coercive control contexts.** Telling a manipulator that you have identified their manipulation often triggers retaliation. It does not produce change.
- **It is sometimes appropriate in genuine miscommunication or ignorance contexts.** If the [Manipulation vs. Miscommunication framework](manipulation-hub-miscommunication-vs-manipulation.md) points toward miscommunication or ignorance with a willing-to-learn person, direct conversation is the appropriate response.
- **If in doubt, do not disclose to the person causing harm.** The default should be disclosing to external audiences (advocates, reality-check network) first, then deciding whether direct conversation is viable based on the external input.
- **Never disclose to the person causing harm if lethality indicators are present.** Contact an advocate first. Disclosure + separation is the highest-risk combination.

### If you are considering public disclosure

- **Consult a lawyer first.** Public disclosure has legal implications that vary by jurisdiction and situation. Defamation law, privacy law, and employment law can all apply.
- **Consult an advocate first.** Public disclosure has safety implications that are hard to predict from the inside.
- **Wait until you are out of the situation if at all possible.** Public disclosure while still in the situation can trigger escalation you cannot handle.
- **Consider the permanent nature of public disclosure.** What you say on social media now will exist indefinitely. Does the version you would write today match the version you will still endorse five years from now?
- **Remember that public disclosure is not obligatory.** Not every survivor should disclose publicly. Not disclosing publicly is not a failure to speak truth or stand up for yourself. It is a legitimate strategic choice.

## 9. What This Page Cannot Do

- **Tell you who to disclose to.** The audience map provides considerations; the decision is yours based on your specific situation and specific audiences.
- **Guarantee any disclosure will be received well.** Even with good audience selection and preparation, responses vary. The page improves the odds; it cannot produce certainty.
- **Replace professional guidance for legally consequential disclosures.** If disclosure has legal implications, consult a lawyer. If it has safety implications, consult an advocate. This page is a framework; it is not specific enough for individual legal or safety counsel.
- **Prepare you for everything.** Some responses you cannot predict. Some conversations go in directions you did not expect. The preparation described here reduces but does not eliminate the uncertainty of disclosure.
- **Make disclosure safe.** Disclosure carries inherent risk that preparation and good audience selection reduce but do not eliminate. This is true for everyone, more pronounced for autistic readers, and most pronounced in coercive control contexts.

## 10. Key Takeaways

1. **"Tell someone" is bad advice. "Tell the right someone, at the right time, in the right way" is better advice.** The disclosure asymmetry (negative reactions harm more than positive reactions help) means audience selection matters more than sheer number of disclosures.

2. **The first audience matters most.** A positive first disclosure supports further disclosure; a negative first disclosure can shut down the process for years. Choose carefully.

3. **Advocates are high-value first audiences.** Professional, confidential with disclosed exceptions, trained for exactly this purpose, and available even when you do not have an immediate crisis. Underutilized.

4. **HR is not your advocate.** Structurally, HR works for the employer. Disclosures to HR should be made with lawyers or employment advocates advising, not assuming HR will protect you.

5. **Police disclosure carries specific autism risks.** Autistic communication is frequently misread. If police involvement may be needed, work through an advocate, not directly.

6. **Autism disclosure is a separate decision from situation disclosure.** These decisions should be made separately and can produce different answers.

7. **Graduated disclosure is safer than full disclosure.** Start with the minimum useful disclosure, assess how it lands, then disclose more if the audience is safe.

8. **The info-dump risk is real for autistic readers.** Notice the impulse; ask what the audience's function is; provide the disclosure that serves the function.

9. **Pre-plan disclosure conversations.** Written notes, pre-composed phrases, exit strategies, post-disclosure decompression plans. The pre-planning compensates for processing load under stress.

10. **You do not owe anyone your story.** Every disclosure is your decision. Declining to disclose is never failure; it is resource management and safety planning.
