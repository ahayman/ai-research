# Autistic Masking/Camouflaging: Definition, Components, and Measurement

## Research Focus
This document covers the scientific literature on the definition, components, and measurement of autistic masking (also termed camouflaging), including key researchers and the distinction between compensation, masking, and assimilation.

---

## 1. Definition of Autistic Masking/Camouflaging

### 1.1 Core Definitions from the Literature

Autistic masking (or camouflaging) refers to the conscious or unconscious strategies that autistic individuals employ to mask, assimilate, or compensate for their autistic characteristics in social settings, in order to blend in or avoid stigmatization. The terms "masking" and "camouflaging" are often used interchangeably in the literature, though some researchers draw distinctions between them (see Section 4).

**Hull et al. (2017)** provided one of the earliest comprehensive qualitative definitions, describing camouflaging as comprising two core behavioral processes:
- **Compensation**: Finding alternative ways around the social and communication difficulties associated with autism
- **Masking**: Hiding aspects of one's autistic presentation, or presenting a non-autistic persona to others

> **Study**: Hull, L., Petrides, K.V., Allison, C., Smith, P., Baron-Cohen, S., Lai, M.-C., & Mandy, W. (2017). "Putting on My Best Normal": Social Camouflaging in Adults with Autism Spectrum Conditions. *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 47(8), 2519-2534.
> **DOI**: [10.1007/s10803-017-3166-5](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10803-017-3166-5)
> **PubMed**: [28527095](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28527095/)
> **Sample**: 92 adults with autism spectrum conditions (55 female, 30 male, 7 other gender identities)
> **Method**: Qualitative online survey with thematic analysis

**Cook, Hull, Crane, & Mandy (2021)** in their systematic review offered a synthesized definition: "Camouflaging is defined as the employment of specific behavioural and cognitive strategies by autistic people to adapt to or cope within the predominately non-autistic social world. Camouflaging may enable an individual to, consciously or unconsciously, present a non-autistic social style, hide autistic characteristics, and/or minimise the visibility of social difficulties."

> **Study**: Cook, J., Hull, L., Crane, L., & Mandy, W. (2021). Camouflaging in autism: A systematic review. *Clinical Psychology Review*, 89, 102080.
> **DOI**: [10.1016/j.cpr.2021.102080](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cpr.2021.102080)
> **PubMed**: [34563942](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34563942/)
> **Scope**: Systematic review of 29 studies (screened 2,160 unique citations, reviewed 104 full-text articles)

**Lai et al. (2017)** operationalized camouflaging quantitatively for the first time, defining it as the discrepancy between an individual's "external" behavioral presentation (observable social functioning) and their "internal" status (underlying autistic traits and social cognition).

> **Study**: Lai, M.-C., Lombardo, M.V., Ruigrok, A.N.V., Chakrabarti, B., Auyeung, B., Szatmari, P., Happe, F., & Baron-Cohen, S. (2017). Quantifying and exploring camouflaging in men and women with autism. *Autism*, 21(6), 690-702.
> **DOI**: [10.1177/1362361316671012](https://doi.org/10.1177/1362361316671012)
> **PubMed**: [27899710](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27899710/)
> **Sample**: 60 age- and IQ-matched adults with autism (30 women, 30 men), without intellectual disability

**Pearson & Rose (2021)** defined masking as "the conscious or unconscious suppression of natural autistic responses and adoption of alternatives across a range of domains," and critically emphasized that masking is an unsurprising response to the deficit narrative and accompanying stigma around autism, framing it through social identity theory.

> **Study**: Pearson, A. & Rose, K. (2021). A Conceptual Analysis of Autistic Masking: Understanding the Narrative of Stigma and the Illusion of Choice. *Autism in Adulthood*, 3(1), 52-60.
> **DOI**: [10.1089/aut.2020.0043](https://doi.org/10.1089/aut.2020.0043)
> **PubMed**: [36601266](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36601266/)
> **Method**: Conceptual analysis paper

### 1.2 Three-Stage Model of Camouflaging (Hull et al., 2017)

Hull et al.'s 2017 qualitative study developed a three-stage model of the camouflaging process:

1. **Motivations for camouflaging**: Fitting in, increasing social connections with others, avoiding bullying/rejection
2. **Camouflaging itself**: A combination of masking (hiding autistic traits) and compensation (developing alternative strategies) techniques
3. **Consequences of camouflaging**: Exhaustion, challenging stereotypes, threats to self-perception, impacts on identity formation

This three-stage model became the theoretical foundation for the later development of the CAT-Q measurement instrument.

## 2. Components of Masking

### 2.1 Suppression of Autistic Behaviors (Stimming, Special Interests, Sensory Responses)

A major component of masking involves the active suppression of naturally occurring autistic behaviors to avoid social censure.

**Kapp et al. (2019)** found that autistic adults purposefully suppressed stimming (self-stimulatory behavior), almost exclusively for extrinsic reasons such as avoiding judgment from others. Despite stimming being frequently a positive self-regulatory experience, participants reported hiding it in public.

> **Study**: Kapp, S.K., Steward, R., Crane, L., Elliott, D., Elphick, C., Pellicano, E., & Russell, G. (2019). 'People should be allowed to do what they like': Autistic adults' views and experiences of stimming. *Autism*, 23(7), 1782-1792.
> **DOI**: [10.1177/1362361319829628](https://doi.org/10.1177/1362361319829628)
> **PubMed**: [30818970](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30818970/)
> **Sample**: 32 autistic adults (interviews and focus groups)
> **Key findings**:
> - Two themes identified: stimming as (1) a self-regulatory mechanism and (2) lacking in social acceptance
> - Participants attempted to suppress stims in public and reported stimming when alone
> - Stimming played a key role in friendships with other autistic individuals
> - Strong connection to the autistic community was the most important factor in whether stimming served social/communicative functions

**Wiskerke, Stern, & Igelstrom (2018, bioRxiv preprint)** found high rates of camouflaging of repetitive movements in autistic females and transgender adults, suggesting this suppression contributes to underdiagnosis.

> **Study**: Wiskerke, J., Stern, H., & Igelstrom, K. (2018). Camouflaging of repetitive movements in autistic female and transgender adults. *bioRxiv*, 412619.
> **DOI**: [10.1101/412619](https://doi.org/10.1101/412619)
> **Sample**: 342 individuals (56% formally diagnosed, 44% self-identified; 17% non-cisgender identity)
> **Key findings**:
> - High rates of repetitive movements in both diagnosed and self-identifying participants
> - Striking prevalence of camouflaging of repetitive movements
> - Overt repetitive movements less common in females than males
> - Camouflaging of repetitive movements may contribute to underdiagnosis in females and transgender people

Specific suppression behaviors documented in the literature include:
- Suppressing hand flapping, rocking, and other repetitive movements
- Hiding or minimizing intense engagement with special interests
- Enduring sensory discomfort without visible reaction (e.g., tolerating overwhelming noise, light, or textures)
- Suppressing echolalia
- Controlling facial expressions to hide distress or discomfort

### 2.2 Adoption/Performance of Neurotypical Social Behaviors

This component involves learning and performing social behaviors that do not come naturally to autistic individuals but are considered "normal" in neurotypical social contexts.

**Cook, Crane, Hull, Bourne, & Mandy (2022)** identified 38 distinct camouflaging behaviors through video-stimulated recall, clustered into four main categories:

1. **Masking**: Hiding autistic characteristics
2. **Innocuous engagement** (with subcategories):
   - Passive encouragement
   - Centring social partner
   - Deferential engagement
   - Reducing social risk
3. **Modelling neurotypical communication**: Copying observed "normal" social behaviors
4. **Active self-presentation** (with subcategories):
   - Reciprocal social behaviours
   - Risky social behaviours
   - Comfortable and familiar social behaviours

> **Study**: Cook, J., Crane, L., Hull, L., Bourne, L., & Mandy, W. (2022). Self-reported camouflaging behaviours used by autistic adults during everyday social interactions. *Autism*, 26(7), 1552-1563.
> **DOI**: [10.1177/13623613211026754](https://doi.org/10.1177/13623613211026754)
> **PubMed**: [34180249](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34180249/)
> **Sample**: 17 autistic adults filmed in a naturalistic social situation (conversation with a stranger), then shown video to identify camouflaging behaviors

Specific adopted behaviors include:
- Forcing or rehearsing eye contact
- Mirroring others' body language and posture
- Adopting particular conversational tones or speech patterns
- Performing expected emotional responses (laughing when others laugh, expressing concern when expected)
- Small talk and social pleasantries

### 2.3 Replication of Facial Expressions and Body Language

Autistic individuals often report consciously learning and replicating facial expressions and body language that neurotypical people produce automatically.

Research shows notable differences in how autistic individuals engage in facial mimicry. Autistic individuals can imitate facial expressions when instructed, but their imitation tends to be both slower and less precise than neurotypical individuals. Studies reveal reduced spontaneous mimicry in autistic relative to neurotypical children and adults.

Autistic individuals often rely more heavily on body posture due to diminished precision in recognizing facial expressions, which can serve as a compensatory strategy to navigate social interactions.

Key documented strategies include:
- Studying and practicing facial expressions (in mirrors, from TV, from observing others)
- Learning to match facial expression to emotional context
- Copying body language of admired or socially successful peers
- Using learned rules about eye contact (e.g., look at bridge of nose, count seconds)
- Deliberately modulating voice tone and volume

### 2.4 Social Scripting and Rehearsal

Social scripting is one of the most well-documented compensation strategies in the masking literature.

Autistic people with conversational difficulties may use complex strategies such as:
- **Scripting conversation outlines**: Preparing what to say in advance of social encounters
- **Developing conscious "rules" for conversations**: Creating explicit rules about turn-taking, topic changes, appropriate responses
- **Monitoring rule compliance**: Carefully monitoring whether conversational rules are being followed in real time
- **Learning from media**: Watching television shows and other media to observe and mimic characters' social behavior
- **Rehearsing responses**: Practicing typical social exchanges mentally or aloud before encountering them
- **Maintaining a "library" of scripts**: Having pre-prepared responses for common social situations (greetings, small talk, expressing empathy)

These strategies fall primarily under what Livingston et al. (2019) would classify as "shallow compensation" (applying simple social rules that may not work across all contexts) and "deep compensation" (more sophisticated strategies flexibly applied across contexts).

## 3. Measurement Tools

### 3.1 The CAT-Q (Camouflaging Autistic Traits Questionnaire)

The CAT-Q is the most widely used self-report measure of camouflaging in autism research.

#### Development and Validation

> **Study**: Hull, L., Mandy, W., Lai, M.-C., Baron-Cohen, S., Allison, C., Smith, P., & Petrides, K.V. (2019). Development and Validation of the Camouflaging Autistic Traits Questionnaire (CAT-Q). *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 49(3), 819-833.
> **DOI**: [10.1007/s10803-018-3792-6](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10803-018-3792-6)
> **PubMed**: [30361940](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30361940/)
> **PMC**: [PMC6394586](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6394586/)
> **Sample**: 354 autistic adults and 478 non-autistic adults (administered online)

**Development process**: The CAT-Q was developed from autistic adults' experiences of camouflaging (drawing on the Hull et al., 2017 qualitative study).

**Structure**: Exploratory factor analysis suggested three factors, comprising 25 items in total. The three subscales are:

1. **Compensation** (9 items): Behaviors aiming to compensate for autistic social and communication differences. Example items relate to using learned social rules, preparing topics of conversation, monitoring social performance.

2. **Masking** (8 items): Efforts to present a non-autistic persona in social situations. Example items relate to adjusting facial expressions, controlling body language, and hiding natural reactions.

3. **Assimilation** (8 items): Efforts to "blend in" and hide discomfort in social situations. Example items relate to fitting in with groups, avoiding standing out, and navigating social environments.

**Psychometric properties**:
- Good model fit demonstrated through confirmatory factor analysis
- Internal consistency: alpha = 0.94
- Preliminary test-retest reliability: r = 0.77
- Measurement invariance across gender and diagnostic group (equivalent factor structures)
- Convergent validity demonstrated through comparison with measures of autistic traits, wellbeing, anxiety, and depression

#### Gender Differences on the CAT-Q

> **Study**: Hull, L., Lai, M.-C., Baron-Cohen, S., Allison, C., Smith, P., Petrides, K.V., & Mandy, W. (2020). Gender differences in self-reported camouflaging in autistic and non-autistic adults. *Autism*, 24(2), 352-363.
> **DOI**: [10.1177/1362361319864804](https://doi.org/10.1177/1362361319864804)
> **PubMed**: [31319684](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31319684/)
> **Sample**: 306 autistic adults and 472 non-autistic adults

**Key findings**:
- Autistic females demonstrated higher total camouflaging scores than autistic males
- No camouflaging gender difference found for non-autistic people
- Autistic females scored higher than males on Masking and Assimilation subscales
- No gender difference on the Compensation subscale
- This suggests the sex/gender difference in camouflaging is specific to autistic populations

#### CAT-Q Short Form (CATQ-SF)

> **Study**: Validation of the camouflaging autistic traits questionnaire short form (CATQ-SF). (2024). *Comprehensive Psychiatry*.
> **PubMed**: [39191097](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39191097/)
> **Sample**: Study 1: N = 832 autistic and non-autistic adults; Study 2: N = 80 (independent replication)

**Key details**:
- 9-item short form version developed for clinical and research settings
- Retains the three-factor structure (Compensation, Masking, Assimilation)
- Internal consistency: alpha = 0.84 (Study 1), alpha = 0.89 (Study 2)
- Measurement invariance maintained
- Psychometric properties of similar or higher validity compared to the full-form CAT-Q

### 3.2 The Discrepancy Method (Lai et al., 2017)

The first method developed to quantify camouflaging, proposed by Lai et al. (2017).

**Approach**: Camouflaging is operationalized as the quantitative discrepancy between:
- **External presentation**: Behavioral presentation in social-interpersonal contexts, measured by the Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule (ADOS)
- **Internal status**: Dispositional autistic traits (measured by the Autism Spectrum Quotient, AQ) and social cognitive capability (measured by the Reading the Mind in the Eyes Test, RMET)

**Formula**: Higher discrepancy (i.e., appearing more socially capable on the ADOS than one's internal traits and cognition would predict) = greater camouflaging.

> **Key findings from Lai et al., 2017**:
> - Women with autism had significantly higher camouflaging scores than men (Cohen's d = 0.98)
> - Substantial variability in both groups
> - Greater camouflaging associated with more depressive symptoms in men
> - Greater camouflaging associated with better signal-detection sensitivity in women

### 3.3 The Compensation Checklist (Livingston et al., 2020)

> **Study**: Livingston, L.A., Shah, P., Milner, V., & Happe, F. (2020). Quantifying compensatory strategies in adults with and without diagnosed autism. *Molecular Autism*, 11, 15.
> **DOI**: [10.1186/s13229-019-0308-y](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13229-019-0308-y)
> **PubMed**: [32051011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32051011/)
> **Sample**: 117 adults (58 with autism, 59 without autism)

**Description**: A 31-item checklist quantifying self-reported social compensatory strategies, divided into four strategy types based on the Livingston taxonomy (masking, shallow compensation, deep compensation, accommodation).

**Key findings**:
- Higher compensation scores (greater repertoire of strategies) were associated with having an autism diagnosis, more autistic traits, and higher education level
- The link between diagnostic status and compensation scores was explained by autistic traits and education level
- Designed as a clinical prompt tool for autism assessments

### 3.4 Other Measurement Instruments

**Comprehensive Autistic Trait Inventory (CATI)**: 42-item measure with six subscales, including a Social Camouflage subscale.

**Girls Questionnaire for Autism Spectrum Condition (GQ-ASC)**: 21-item measure with five subscales, including a Camouflaging subscale. Specifically designed to assess autism presentation in females.

**Playground Observation of Peer Engagement (POPE)**: An observational method for children that measures camouflaging through behavioral observation of playground interactions, classifying behavior every 1 minute across 10-15 minute observation periods (game, joint engagement, solitary).

**Interactive Drawing Test (IDT)-derived methods**: Wood-Downie et al. used an observational/reflective method to investigate "behavioral camouflaging" alongside RMET-C scores to operationalize compensatory camouflaging.

### 3.5 Two Broad Methodological Approaches

The Cook et al. (2021) systematic review identified that measurement methods fall broadly into two distinct approaches:

1. **Internal-external discrepancy methods**: Measuring the gap between observable behavior and underlying traits/cognition (e.g., Lai et al., 2017)
2. **Self-report methods**: Asking individuals to report on their own camouflaging behaviors (e.g., CAT-Q)

These two approaches appear to measure related but distinct constructs: discrepancy methods capture observable behavioral presentations, while self-report methods capture self-perceived camouflaging efforts. Research suggests these two approaches do not always correlate highly, indicating they may be tapping into different aspects of camouflaging.

> **Study on comparison**: Hannon, J. et al. (2023). A comparison of methods for measuring camouflaging in autism. *Autism Research*.
> [PMC10099783](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10099783/)

## 4. Distinction Between Compensation, Masking, and Assimilation

### 4.1 Livingston & Happe's Compensation Framework (2017)

Livingston and Happe proposed the foundational theoretical framework for understanding compensation in autism, defining it as "the processes contributing to improved behavioural presentation of a neurodevelopmental disorder, despite persisting core deficit(s) at cognitive and/or neurobiological levels."

> **Study**: Livingston, L.A. & Happe, F. (2017). Conceptualising compensation in neurodevelopmental disorders: Reflections from autism spectrum disorder. *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*, 80, 729-742.
> **DOI**: [10.1016/j.neubiorev.2017.06.005](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neubiorev.2017.06.005)
> **PubMed**: [28642070](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28642070/)
> **Type**: Theoretical/conceptual paper proposing a transdiagnostic framework

Key distinction: Compensation is an adaptive process whereby new behaviors are generated to avoid negative outcomes, and is different from masking, where presumed undesirable behaviors are hidden or stopped.

### 4.2 Livingston, Shah, & Happe's Four-Type Taxonomy (2019)

Building on the 2017 framework, Livingston et al. identified four distinct types of compensatory strategies through qualitative research:

> **Study**: Livingston, L.A., Shah, P., & Happe, F. (2019). Compensatory strategies below the behavioural surface in autism: A qualitative study. *The Lancet Psychiatry*, 6(9), 766-777.
> **DOI**: [10.1016/S2215-0366(19)30224-X](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2215-0366(19)30224-X)
> **PubMed**: [31350208](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31350208/)
> **Sample**: 136 adults (58 with clinical diagnosis, 19 self-identified without formal diagnosis, 59 not diagnosed/self-identified but reporting social difficulties)

**The Four Strategy Types:**

1. **Masking**: Simple strategies that involve decreasing or increasing (i.e., regulating) pre-existing social behaviors to blend in. Masking allows a person to go unnoticed in group situations but does **not** support active participation in social interactions. It tends to be passive and can become automatic. Examples: suppressing stimming, forcing eye contact, modulating voice.

2. **Shallow Compensation**: Strategies involving the application of simple social rules that may not work across all contexts. Enables the production of neurotypical-appearing behaviors without deep social understanding (e.g., with incomplete theory of mind). Examples: "always ask how someone is doing," "nod every few seconds to show listening," scripted responses.

3. **Deep Compensation**: More sophisticated strategies that flexibly work across a variety of contexts by taking into account the unique details of a situation. Involves genuine social cognition applied through learned algorithms. Examples: analyzing social dynamics in real-time, adapting scripts based on context, reading the room and adjusting approach.

4. **Accommodation**: Strategies involving actively seeking out accommodating environments without necessarily altering one's social behavior. Rather than changing the self, the individual changes their environment. Examples: choosing professions with limited social demands, building friendships with other neurodivergent people, seeking environments that value directness.

### 4.3 The Hull/CAT-Q Three-Factor Model

The CAT-Q (Hull et al., 2019) uses a slightly different tripartite distinction:

1. **Compensation**: Using learned strategies and social rules to navigate social interactions (corresponding roughly to Livingston's shallow and deep compensation)
2. **Masking**: Presenting a non-autistic persona by suppressing autistic traits and performing neurotypical behaviors (corresponding to Livingston's masking)
3. **Assimilation**: Blending in with groups and hiding discomfort (a somewhat distinct concept capturing the desire and effort to not stand out, which overlaps with but is distinct from both masking and compensation)

### 4.4 Key Conceptual Distinctions

| Concept | Focus | Active/Passive | Generates New Behavior? |
|---------|-------|----------------|------------------------|
| **Masking** | Hiding/suppressing autistic behaviors | Primarily passive (suppression) | No — reduces existing behavior |
| **Compensation** | Developing alternative strategies | Active | Yes — creates new behavioral repertoire |
| **Assimilation** | Fitting in, not standing out | Both | May involve both suppression and adoption |
| **Accommodation** | Modifying environment | Active | No — modifies context rather than behavior |

## 6. Key Findings Summary

### 6.1 Three Preliminary Findings (from Cook et al., 2021 systematic review)

The 2021 systematic review of 29 studies supported three consistent findings:
1. **Autistic trait correlation**: Adults with more self-reported autistic traits report greater engagement in camouflaging
2. **Sex/gender differences**: Sex and gender differences exist in camouflaging, with autistic females generally reporting higher levels (though there is substantial overlap)
3. **Mental health association**: Higher self-reported camouflaging is associated with worse mental health outcomes (depression, anxiety, exhaustion, burnout, suicidality)

### 6.2 Context-Dependent Nature of Masking

A 2025 study found that autistic people masked significantly more around non-autistic (allistic) people and significantly less when alone or with autistic peers, demonstrating that masking is socially contingent rather than a fixed trait.

### 6.3 Gender and Diagnosis

Camouflaging has been identified as a key factor in the delayed diagnosis of autistic women and gender-diverse individuals. Autistic women tend to camouflage more than autistic men (Lai et al., 2017: Cohen's d = 0.98), though this is specifically on the Masking and Assimilation dimensions rather than Compensation (Hull et al., 2020).

### 6.4 Measurement Challenges

The two major measurement approaches (discrepancy vs. self-report) do not always correlate highly (Hannon et al., 2023), suggesting they capture different aspects of camouflaging. This presents an ongoing methodological challenge for the field.
