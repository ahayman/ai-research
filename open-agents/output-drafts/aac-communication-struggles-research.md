# AAC Communication Struggles: Hierarchical Classification

## Purpose

This document provides a comprehensive, hierarchical taxonomy of every type of communication struggle experienced by people who use or need Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC). Each struggle category is mapped to the specific medical conditions it encompasses, with clinical references and severity indicators.

The taxonomy is organized into 7 top-level domains, 30 sub-domains, and 100+ specific struggle types. It draws from ASHA classification frameworks, WHO ICF codes, the Mayo Clinic dysarthria classification, DSM-5 diagnostic criteria, and detailed analysis of 15 AAC-relevant conditions.

---

## Taxonomy Overview

| # | Domain | Sub-Domains | Key Conditions |
|---|--------|-------------|----------------|
| 1 | Receptive Struggles | Auditory, Linguistic, Social/Pragmatic, Reading, Temporal | Aphasia, Dementia, ASD, TBI, Down Syndrome |
| 2 | Expressive Struggles | Motor Speech, Language Formulation, Written, Nonverbal, Emotional | All 15 conditions |
| 3 | Interactive/Pragmatic Struggles | Conversational, Social Cognition, Register, Discourse | ASD, TBI, Dementia, SCD |
| 4 | Physical/Motor Access Barriers | Gross Motor, Fine Motor, Oral-Motor, Respiratory, Visual-Motor | CP, ALS, MD/SMA, Rett, Locked-In |
| 5 | Cognitive-Communication Barriers | Attention, Memory, Executive Function, Processing Speed | TBI, Dementia, MS, Parkinson's |
| 6 | Psychosocial Barriers | Confidence, Identity, Stigma, Inclusion, Partner Dependency | All conditions |
| 7 | Rate/Efficiency Barriers | Speed, Timing, Fatigue, Context Switching | All AAC users |

## 1. RECEPTIVE COMMUNICATION STRUGGLES

Receptive communication encompasses the ability to understand and process incoming communication -- spoken, written, signed, or nonverbal. While many AAC users have intact receptive abilities, several conditions profoundly impair comprehension.

**WHO ICF Codes:** d310 (receiving spoken messages), d315 (receiving nonverbal messages), d325 (receiving written messages); b167 (mental functions of language -- reception)

### 1.1 Auditory Comprehension Disorders

The ability to understand spoken language operates at multiple distinct processing levels, each of which can be independently impaired.

#### 1.1.1 Acoustic/Phonemic Processing
- **Description:** Inability to discriminate between similar speech sounds (phonemes) or process the acoustic envelope of speech. The listener cannot reliably distinguish individual speech sounds.
- **Clinical basis:** Auditory Processing Disorder (APD/CAPD); auditory discrimination deficits; temporal processing deficits at the millisecond level (temporal ordering, resolution, integration, masking).
- **Conditions:** Wernicke's aphasia (stroke), TBI (central auditory processing dysfunction from temporal bone fractures/auditory nerve damage), Down Syndrome (80-90% have hearing loss; chronic otitis media extremely common).
- **Severity range:** Mild difficulty in noise to complete inability to discriminate speech sounds.
- **AAC implication:** Auditory-only output from AAC devices may be insufficient; visual supports and written output needed.

#### 1.1.2 Word-Level Comprehension
- **Description:** Difficulty understanding individual spoken words despite adequate hearing. May include loss of word meaning (semantic comprehension failure).
- **Conditions:** Wernicke's aphasia (stroke), semantic variant PPA (dementia), Down Syndrome (auditory short-term memory weakness).
- **Key distinction:** In aphasia, word comprehension varies by word frequency, imageability, and semantic category. In semantic dementia, word meaning itself degrades progressively, starting with uncommon words.

#### 1.1.3 Sentence-Level Comprehension
- **Description:** Difficulty understanding syntactically complex sentences even when individual words are understood. Particularly affected: passive constructions, relative clauses, embedded clauses, multi-step instructions.
- **Conditions:** Broca's aphasia (agrammatic comprehension), Wernicke's aphasia, Dementia/Alzheimer's (progressive loss), TBI (cognitive processing demands), Down Syndrome (syntax comprehension gap).
- **Key distinction:** In agrammatic aphasia, comprehension breaks down specifically for "reversible" sentences where word order determines meaning ("The dog was chased by the cat").

#### 1.1.4 Discourse-Level Comprehension
- **Description:** Inability to follow extended spoken passages, conversations, lectures, or multi-turn discussions. Difficulty extracting main ideas, making cross-sentence inferences, and maintaining comprehension over time.
- **Conditions:** TBI (attention/memory interaction), Dementia (progressive), MS (processing speed/cognitive fatigue), Aphasia (compounded by word/sentence-level deficits).
- **AAC implication:** Communication partners must simplify, slow down, and chunk information.

### 1.2 Linguistic Comprehension Disorders

Beyond auditory processing, language comprehension involves semantic, syntactic, and morphological processing systems.

#### 1.2.1 Semantic Comprehension Deficits
- **Description:** Loss or impairment of word meaning, category relationships, and conceptual knowledge. Difficulty understanding vocabulary, word relationships (synonyms, antonyms, categories), and figurative language.
- **Conditions:** Semantic variant PPA (dementia -- progressive loss of word meaning starting with uncommon words), Wernicke's aphasia (semantic confusion), Intellectual disability (limited vocabulary knowledge), Down Syndrome (relative strength but delayed).
- **Severity range:** Occasional word confusion to complete loss of meaning for everyday words.
- **Unique pattern:** In semantic dementia, degradation follows a pattern -- low-frequency/less familiar words lost first, with everyday words preserved longest.

#### 1.2.2 Syntactic Comprehension Deficits
- **Description:** Difficulty understanding sentence structure, word order, and grammatical relationships.
- **Conditions:** Broca's aphasia (agrammatic comprehension), Dementia (progressive simplification), Developmental Language Disorder (morpho-syntactic impairment), Down Syndrome (syntax as particular weakness).
- **AAC implication:** AAC output and communication partner language should use simple, active-voice sentence structures.

#### 1.2.3 Morphological Comprehension Deficits
- **Description:** Difficulty interpreting grammatical morphemes -- tense markers (-ed), plurals (-s), possessives (-'s), comparatives (-er/-est), auxiliaries, prepositions.
- **Conditions:** Developmental Language Disorder (morphological deficits are the most consistent clinical marker across languages), Broca's aphasia (difficulty with function words and agreement markers), Down Syndrome.
- **Key finding:** Morphological difficulty is the single most reliable clinical marker of DLD.

### 1.3 Social/Pragmatic Reception Disorders

The ability to understand the social, contextual, and nonliteral dimensions of communication. This is distinct from linguistic comprehension -- a person may understand every word spoken yet completely miss the communicative intent.

#### 1.3.1 Prosodic Interpretation (Tone of Voice)
- **Description:** Inability to interpret emotional content, sarcasm, questioning, or emphasis from vocal prosody (pitch, loudness, timbre, rate, pauses). Called "receptive aprosodia."
- **Conditions:** ASD (atypical prosodic processing), TBI (right hemisphere damage), Right hemisphere stroke, Parkinson's (may affect perception as well as production).
- **Key distinction:** The person hears the words correctly but misses the "how" -- cannot tell if someone is angry, joking, asking, or being sarcastic from tone alone.

#### 1.3.2 Nonverbal Interpretation (Body Language, Facial Expressions, Gestures)
- **Description:** Difficulty reading body language, facial expressions, eye gaze direction, posture, and communicative gestures to extract meaning.
- **Conditions:** ASD (core diagnostic feature -- may process facial expressions through different cognitive pathways), TBI (social cognition deficits), Dementia (42% of severe cases show reduced comprehension of gestures and nonverbal cues), Right hemisphere damage.
- **AAC implication:** People with nonverbal interpretation deficits may not notice communication partner's confusion, boredom, or attempts to change topic.

#### 1.3.3 Contextual/Pragmatic Interpretation
- **Description:** Difficulty understanding conversational implicature (what is implied but not stated), reading social dynamics, understanding power relationships, and interpreting communicative intent beyond literal meaning.
- **Conditions:** ASD (literal interpretation; difficulty with "reading the room"), TBI (social cognition deficits), Social (Pragmatic) Communication Disorder (DSM-5 315.39).
- **Includes:** Difficulty understanding indirect speech acts ("Can you pass the salt?" as request, not question), conversational maxims, and shared knowledge assumptions.

#### 1.3.4 Figurative Language Comprehension
- **Description:** Difficulty understanding metaphor, idiom, sarcasm, irony, humor, simile, and other non-literal language forms.
- **Conditions:** ASD (significant deficit -- meta-analytic evidence; literal interpretations dominate), TBI, Right hemisphere damage, Intellectual disability.
- **Key finding:** Meta-analytic review confirms significant figurative language comprehension deficits in ASD (PMC5843023). Idiom processing shows literal interpretations dominating -- "It's raining cats and dogs" parsed literally.
- **AAC implication:** AAC vocabulary and training must explicitly address figurative language; communication partners should be aware of literal interpretation tendencies.

### 1.4 Reading and Written Comprehension Disorders

#### 1.4.1 Acquired Reading Disorders (Alexia)
- **Description:** Loss of previously acquired reading ability. Multiple subtypes:
  - **Surface alexia:** Difficulty reading irregular words (reading by rules -- "yacht" read as "yatcht")
  - **Deep alexia:** Semantic errors in reading ("ship" read as "boat")
  - **Phonological alexia:** Difficulty reading nonwords; relies on whole-word recognition
  - **Pure alexia (alexia without agraphia):** Can write but cannot read, even own writing
- **Conditions:** Stroke/aphasia (reading difficulties are a common feature), TBI, Dementia (progressive reading loss).
- **AAC implication:** Text-based AAC systems may be partially or fully inaccessible. Symbol-based systems may be needed even for adults who were previously literate.

#### 1.4.2 Developmental Literacy Limitations
- **Description:** Limited reading/writing skills that were never fully developed due to developmental conditions, limited educational access due to motor disabilities, or co-occurring learning disabilities.
- **Conditions:** Cerebral palsy (reduced access to literacy instruction due to motor difficulties), Intellectual disability, Down Syndrome, Angelman Syndrome, Rett Syndrome, ASD (variable).
- **AAC implication:** Limits access to text-based AAC; symbol-based or hybrid systems required.

#### 1.4.3 Symbol/Picture Comprehension
- **Description:** Difficulty understanding graphic symbols used in AAC systems. Symbols exist on a transparency continuum from transparent (photo of cup = cup) through translucent to opaque/abstract.
- **Conditions:** Intellectual disability (difficulty with abstract symbols), Angelman Syndrome (abstract reasoning challenges), Dementia (progressive loss of symbol-meaning associations).
- **Key finding:** Cognitive Load Theory suggests adding visual supports to text can actually increase cognitive demands by requiring alternating visual attention. Navigating multi-page symbol arrays requires sustained working memory.
- **AAC implication:** Symbol selection must match cognitive level; concrete photographs for some users, abstract symbols for others.

### 1.5 Temporal Processing and Speed

#### 1.5.1 Millisecond-Level Temporal Processing
- **Description:** Deficient processing of rapid acoustic changes in speech, affecting ability to resolve the temporal fine structure of spoken language.
- **Conditions:** Wernicke's aphasia (disturbed temporal resolution critical for auditory comprehension), APD/CAPD.

#### 1.5.2 Processing Speed / Delayed Processing
- **Description:** Generalized slowing of cognitive processing affecting ability to keep up with conversational pace, follow rapid speech, process multi-step instructions, and formulate timely responses.
- **Conditions:** TBI (1.54x slower than healthy controls), MS (most common cognitive deficit; predicts significant impairment 10 years later), Parkinson's, Dementia, Down Syndrome.
- **AAC implication:** Communication partners must allow extra processing time; conversations in real-time may be overwhelming.

#### 1.5.3 Information Overload
- **Description:** Inability to process multiple simultaneous information streams or cope with rapid information delivery.
- **Conditions:** TBI (all attention subtypes affected), MS (cognitive fatigue compounds), Dementia (environmental distractions worsen performance).

## 2. EXPRESSIVE COMMUNICATION STRUGGLES

Expressive communication encompasses all forms of producing outgoing communication -- speech, writing, gesture, AAC-generated output. This is the most common domain of struggle across AAC conditions.

**WHO ICF Codes:** d330 (speaking), d335 (producing nonverbal messages), d345 (writing messages); b310-b340 (voice and speech functions)

### 2.1 Speech Motor Execution Disorders

Speech production requires coordinated interaction within and between five subsystems: respiration, phonation, resonance, articulation, and prosody.

#### 2.1.1 Dysarthria (Motor Execution Failure)

Motor speech disorder caused by weakness, spasticity, or incoordination of speech muscles. Seven clinically recognized types (Mayo Clinic/Darley-Aronson-Brown classification):

**2.1.1a Flaccid Dysarthria**
- **Lesion:** Lower motor neurons (cranial nerves, neuromuscular junction, muscles)
- **Features:** Breathy voice, short phrases, hypernasality, imprecise articulation, nasal emission, audible inspiration
- **Conditions:** ALS (LMN component), Myasthenia gravis, Guillain-Barre, brainstem stroke, SMA (bulbar involvement)

**2.1.1b Spastic Dysarthria**
- **Lesion:** Bilateral upper motor neurons
- **Features:** Strained-strangled voice, monotonicity, slow rate, imprecise consonants, hypernasality, short phrases
- **Conditions:** Cerebral palsy (spastic type), bilateral stroke, ALS (UMN component), primary lateral sclerosis

**2.1.1c Ataxic Dysarthria**
- **Lesion:** Cerebellum or cerebellar circuits
- **Features:** Irregular articulatory errors, equal and excessive stress ("scanning speech"), inappropriate pitch/loudness variation, imprecise consonants, prolonged phonemes
- **Conditions:** MS (characteristic scanning speech), cerebellar stroke, Friedreich's ataxia

**2.1.1d Hypokinetic Dysarthria**
- **Lesion:** Basal ganglia (substantia nigra)
- **Features:** Reduced loudness (monoloudness), rapid speech rate (festination), rushes of speech, sound repetitions, monotone pitch, imprecise articulation
- **Conditions:** Parkinson's disease (affects up to 89-90%), progressive supranuclear palsy
- **Unique feature:** The "scaling problem" -- patients internally perceive they are speaking louder/clearer than they actually are

**2.1.1e Hyperkinetic Dysarthria**
- **Lesion:** Basal ganglia; involuntary movements
- **Features:** Harsh voice, variable loudness/rate, voice stoppages, involuntary voice breaks, strained voice, distorted vowels, irregular articulatory breakdowns
- **Conditions:** Huntington's disease, dystonia, Tourette's, tardive dyskinesia, some cerebral palsy (dyskinetic type)

**2.1.1f Unilateral Upper Motor Neuron (UUMN) Dysarthria**
- **Lesion:** Unilateral UMN damage
- **Features:** Generally milder; imprecise articulation, harsh voice, slow rate, primarily one-sided
- **Conditions:** Unilateral stroke

**2.1.1g Mixed Dysarthria**
- **Lesion:** Multiple motor systems
- **Features:** Combination of two or more types
- **Conditions:** ALS (flaccid + spastic -- the hallmark), MS (variable combinations), TBI, Wilson's disease

#### 2.1.2 Apraxia of Speech (Motor Planning Failure)

Distinct from dysarthria. A motor planning and programming disorder where the brain cannot formulate the sequence of movements for speech despite adequate muscle strength. Apraxia = planning problem; dysarthria = execution problem.

**2.1.2a Acquired Apraxia of Speech**
- **Cardinal features (Duffy classification):**
  1. Effortful, groping, trial-and-error articulatory movements with self-correction attempts
  2. Dysprosody (abnormal rhythm, stress, intonation)
  3. Inconsistent articulatory errors on repeated productions
  4. Difficulty initiating utterances
- **Additional features:** Errors increase with word length/complexity; islands of fluent speech; awareness of errors; volitional speech worse than automatic
- **Conditions:** Stroke (left hemisphere), TBI, primary progressive apraxia of speech (PPAOS -- ICD-10-CM code G31.87, effective October 1, 2025 / FY2026)

**2.1.2b Childhood Apraxia of Speech (CAS)**
- **Core symptoms (75%+ clinician agreement):** Inconsistency of errors, consonant sequencing problems, low intelligibility, groping, slow diadochokinetic rates, difficulty with multisyllabic words, prosodic disturbances
- **Conditions:** CAS (primary diagnosis, 1-2 per 1,000 children), ASD (motor speech difficulties/verbal dyspraxia in some minimally verbal individuals), Down Syndrome (CAS co-occurs in up to 15% formally diagnosed; oral motor difficulty in 98%), Rett Syndrome (severe motor apraxia), Angelman Syndrome (oral-motor dyspraxia as primary cause of speech impairment)
- **Key distinction from dysarthria:** Muscles are not weak. The motor plan is disordered.

#### 2.1.3 Structural Speech Impairment

Speech impairment from physical removal of or damage to speech structures.

- **Total laryngectomy:** Complete voice loss (98% communication impact); retains articulation but no sound source
- **Glossectomy (tongue removal):** Affects articulation of nearly all consonants/vowels; tongue is the single most important articulator (95% impact for total, 70% partial)
- **Mandibulectomy:** Affects lip closure, tongue positioning, dental relationships
- **Maxillectomy:** Oronasal fistula causing severe hypernasality
- **Radiation effects:** Mucositis (speaking painful), xerostomia (90%+ dry mouth affecting fluent speech), fibrosis (tissue stiffening), trismus (restricted jaw opening, 25-31%)
- **Conditions:** Head and neck cancers / laryngectomy
- **Key feature:** Unlike progressive or developmental conditions, this is often sudden and permanent.

#### 2.1.4 Respiratory-Phonatory Interaction
- **Description:** Inadequate breath support for speech production. All speech is produced on exhalation; weakened respiratory muscles reduce voice volume, utterance length, and speech endurance.
- **Conditions:** ALS (weakened diaphragm, respiratory muscles), CP (shallow/irregular breathing, insufficient subglottal pressure), MD/SMA (progressive respiratory weakness), Parkinson's (rigid chest wall), MS (respiratory muscle weakness).
- **Specific impacts:** Shortened utterance length, decreased volume, breathy/strained voice, running out of breath mid-sentence, difficulty sustaining phonation, need for ventilator use interfering with speech.

#### 2.1.5 Prosodic Impairment
- **Description:** Abnormal rhythm, stress, intonation, and pacing of speech. Prosody carries emotional, grammatical, and pragmatic meaning.
- **Conditions:** Parkinson's (monopitch, monoloudness -- speech sounds flat/emotionless), MS (scanning speech -- equal stress on all syllables), CAS (inappropriate stress patterns, flat intonation), ASD (atypical pitch patterns, monotone, sing-song, or overly formal), CP (limited pitch control).
- **Social impact:** Listeners perceive flat prosody as disinterest, confusion, or emotional flatness even when the speaker is engaged and enthusiastic.

#### 2.1.6 Fluency Disorders
- **Stuttering:** Repetitions, prolongations, blocks with struggle behavior and tension
- **Cluttering:** Abnormally rapid or irregular rate, blending words together, effortless but unintelligible
- **Conditions:** Primary fluency disorders; also Down Syndrome (stuttering-like disfluencies), Parkinson's (festinating speech -- rapid bursts), TBI

### 2.2 Language Formulation Disorders

#### 2.2.1 Word Retrieval / Anomia
- **Description:** Inability to access and produce known words. Universal across all aphasia types. Distinct subtypes:
  - **Semantic anomia:** Loss of word meaning (angular gyrus damage)
  - **Word selection anomia:** Can recognize/use object but cannot name it (posterior inferior temporal)
  - **Disconnection anomia:** Severed connections between sensory and language cortices
  - **Word production anomia / Tip-of-tongue:** Knows the word but cannot retrieve phonological form (mildest)
- **Compensatory patterns:** Circumlocutions ("the thing you write with"), semantic paraphasias ("pen" for "pencil"), phonemic paraphasias ("fen" for "pen")
- **Conditions:** Stroke/Aphasia (universal feature -- affects nouns and verbs most heavily), Dementia (anomia is the hallmark; 50-80% experience word-finding difficulty depending on severity), TBI (cognitive-communication), MS (related to slowed processing and impaired working memory), Parkinson's (word-finding difficulties).

#### 2.2.2 Sentence Construction Disorders
- **Agrammatism:** Telegraphic speech with omission of function words, tense markers, and grammatical morphemes. Short, non-hierarchical phrases. ("Me go store" for "I went to the store")
  - **Conditions:** Broca's aphasia, nonfluent/agrammatic variant PPA
- **Paragrammatism:** Grammatical distortion without reduction -- substitution rather than omission of grammatical elements. "Confused sentence monsters."
  - **Conditions:** Wernicke's aphasia, fluent aphasia
- **Simplified syntax:** Progressively shorter and simpler sentences, reduced mean length of utterance
  - **Conditions:** Dementia (progressive), Down Syndrome (persistent), ASD (variable)

#### 2.2.3 Vocabulary/Semantic Access
- **Description:** Reduced active vocabulary even when passive vocabulary may be intact. Category-specific naming deficits possible.
- **Conditions:** Aphasia (verb retrieval often more impaired than nouns in non-fluent types), Dementia (progressive vocabulary loss), ASD (may have extensive vocabulary but inability to use it spontaneously), Down Syndrome (~300-400 words at age 6 vs. several thousand typical).

#### 2.2.4 Discourse Organization
- **Description:** Difficulty organizing extended speech into coherent, structured, informative narrative. Includes tangential speech, excessive detail in some areas while omitting key information, poor story grammar.
- **Conditions:** TBI (rambling, disorganized, tangential speech -- the hallmark cognitive-communication pattern), ASD (difficulty with narrative construction), Dementia (loss of topic maintenance, repetition).

### 2.3 Written/Typed Expression Disorders

#### 2.3.1 Agraphia (Acquired Writing Impairment)
- **Types:** Cognitive agraphia (language-based), motor agraphia (fine motor), visuospatial agraphia
- **Conditions:** Stroke/aphasia (writing almost always affected, often more severely than speech), TBI
- **Related:** Dystypia (impaired typing on keyboard), dystextia (mobile phone)

#### 2.3.2 Micrographia
- **Description:** Handwriting becomes progressively smaller and illegible, eliminating writing as communication fallback.
- **Conditions:** Parkinson's disease (63% affected)

#### 2.3.3 Motor Writing Barriers
- **Description:** Physical inability to write or type due to weakness, paralysis, tremor, or incoordination.
- **Conditions:** ALS (progressive hand/arm weakness), CP (fine motor limitations), MD/SMA (progressive), Locked-in syndrome (complete paralysis), Rett syndrome (loss of hand function).

### 2.4 Nonverbal Expression Disorders

#### 2.4.1 Gesture Production Impairment
- **Limb apraxia (ideomotor apraxia):** Impaired ability to perform skilled gestures on command or by imitation. "Body-part-as-object" errors common. Often co-occurs with aphasia, compounding communication barriers.
  - **Conditions:** Stroke (left hemisphere), ASD (ideomotor dyspraxia widely reported), Rett Syndrome (loss of purposeful hand use -- replaced by stereotypic movements)
- **Reduced gesturing:** Gestures become smaller, less frequent, or absent.
  - **Conditions:** Parkinson's (hand/arm gestures smaller and less frequent), ALS (progressive limb weakness), CP (motor limitations), Angelman Syndrome (ataxia, fine motor impairment).

#### 2.4.2 Facial Expression Impairment
- **Facial masking (hypomimia):** Reduction or loss of spontaneous facial expression; face appears frozen or blank.
  - **Conditions:** Parkinson's (up to 70%), ALS (facial muscle weakness), Locked-in syndrome (no voluntary facial movement)
- **Atypical facial expression:** Expressions that don't match neurotypical expectations or don't align with emotional state.
  - **Conditions:** ASD (reduced facial expressiveness or atypical expressions), CP (involuntary facial movements in dyskinetic types)
- **Social impact:** Partners misinterpret facial masking as disinterest, depression, cognitive impairment, unfriendliness, or anger.

#### 2.4.3 Reduced Body Language
- **Description:** Diminished use of posture, head movements, and body positioning to convey communicative intent.
- **Conditions:** Parkinson's (stooped posture conveys disinterest), CP (postural instability), ALS/MD/SMA (progressive motor loss), Locked-in syndrome (no body movement).

### 2.5 Emotional and Identity Expression Barriers

#### 2.5.1 Emotional Conveyance Through AAC
- **Description:** Difficulty conveying emotion, enthusiasm, frustration, or mood through AAC output. Synthetic speech historically lacked prosodic variation, making all output sound flat and emotionally neutral.
- **Affected:** All AAC users, particularly those dependent on speech-generating devices.
- **Emerging solutions:** "Expressive Tones" features (anger, excitement, sadness, sarcasm, whispering); AI-powered TTS with personal voice options.
- **AAC implication:** High priority for user satisfaction and identity.

#### 2.5.2 Humor, Sarcasm, and Timing
- **Description:** Humor requires precise timing, which fundamentally conflicts with slow AAC output (8-15 WPM). A joke delivered 30 seconds late loses its effect. Sarcasm requires prosodic cues that most AAC systems cannot produce.
- **Affected:** All AAC users.
- **Emerging solutions:** AI-powered interfaces analyzing conversation context to generate quick quips (ACM: "Why So Serious?" 2025). Users often preferred full-auto AI for humor because timely participation mattered more than perfect wording.

#### 2.5.3 Personal Identity and Voice
- **Description:** Voice is a core component of personal identity. Using a synthetic voice that doesn't match one's age, gender, cultural background, or personality is alienating. "Agency is usually demonstrated through talk; differences in talking may fundamentally change how others perceive the person."
- **Affected:** All AAC users, particularly those who had a natural voice before (ALS, laryngectomy, stroke).
- **AAC implication:** Voice banking, personal voice synthesis, and voice customization are critical for user acceptance and continued use.

#### 2.5.4 Pseudobulbar Affect
- **Description:** Involuntary laughing or crying unrelated to actual emotional state. Creates profound misunderstanding in social contexts.
- **Conditions:** ALS (UMN-dominant), MS, TBI, stroke.

## 3. INTERACTIVE/PRAGMATIC COMMUNICATION STRUGGLES

Pragmatic communication involves the social use of language -- how communication is used in context rather than its linguistic form. A person may have perfect language and speech but still fail to communicate effectively due to pragmatic deficits.

**WHO ICF Codes:** d350 (conversation), d355 (discussion); DSM-5: Social (Pragmatic) Communication Disorder (315.39 / F80.89)

### 3.1 Conversational Management

#### 3.1.1 Turn-Taking
- **Description:** Difficulty alternating speaker/listener roles, knowing when to speak, timing contributions, and managing the rhythm of conversation.
- **Conditions:** ASD (core feature -- difficulty knowing when to speak and listen), TBI (impulsive interjections or failure to initiate), Dementia (progressive breakdown).
- **AAC-specific challenge:** AAC users communicate at 8-15 WPM vs. 150+ WPM for speech, making natural turn-taking extremely difficult. Partners dominate conversations while AAC users take passive roles.

#### 3.1.2 Topic Initiation, Maintenance, and Shifting
- **Description:** Difficulty starting conversations appropriately, staying on topic, contributing relevant information, and transitioning between topics smoothly.
- **Conditions:** ASD (abrupt shifts to preferred topics, tangential information, may speak at length on deep interests), TBI (tangential speech, inability to maintain topic), Dementia (frequent tangential shifts and repetition, loss of topic maintenance).
- **AAC-specific:** Limited vocabulary constrains topic range; navigation time causes topic lag.

#### 3.1.3 Conversational Repair
- **Description:** Difficulty identifying and resolving communication breakdowns -- detecting when misunderstood, requesting clarification, and rephrasing.
- **Conditions:** ASD (struggles detecting breakdowns), TBI (reduced self-monitoring), CAS (exhausting and often unsuccessful repair attempts), CP (frequent breakdowns with unfamiliar listeners).
- **AAC-specific:** AAC users may use repetition, augmentation, or substitution to repair. Until proficient, partners often take ownership of repair, reinforcing passivity.

### 3.2 Social Cognition

#### 3.2.1 Theory of Mind / Perspective-Taking
- **Description:** Difficulty understanding others' beliefs, desires, intentions, and knowledge states. Impairs ability to adjust communication based on what the listener knows or needs.
- **Conditions:** ASD (core feature -- though the "double empathy problem" reframes this as reciprocal, not solely an autistic deficit), TBI (social cognition deficits), Dementia (decreasing ability to consider listener's perspective).
- **Communication impact:** Difficulty with indirect requests, presupposition management, estimating listener knowledge.

#### 3.2.2 Emotion Recognition
- **Description:** Difficulty recognizing and interpreting emotions in others through facial expressions, vocal tone, body language.
- **Conditions:** TBI, ASD (may process through different cognitive pathways), Dementia, right hemisphere stroke.

#### 3.2.3 Social Judgment and Behavior
- **Description:** Inappropriate social behavior, failure to follow social norms, disinhibited comments.
- **Conditions:** TBI (impulsive/inappropriate remarks from impaired self-monitoring -- social cognition deficits are the "most challenging aspect of long-term TBI recovery"), frontotemporal dementia (loss of conversational filters), ASD (difficulty with social rules).

### 3.3 Register and Context Adaptation

#### 3.3.1 Formality/Style Shifting
- **Description:** Difficulty adapting language formality to context -- speaking differently in formal vs. casual settings, to adults vs. children, to authority figures vs. peers.
- **Conditions:** ASD (may use overly formal language in casual settings), TBI (failure to adapt style), SCD (DSM-5 diagnostic criterion).
- **AAC-specific:** Pre-programmed messages may not match required formality. Limited vocabulary makes register shifting difficult. Inability to use slang, in-group language, or code-switching limits social belonging.

#### 3.3.2 Audience Awareness
- **Description:** Difficulty adjusting communication based on the knowledge, age, relationship, and needs of the listener.
- **Conditions:** ASD, TBI, Dementia.

### 3.4 Discourse and Narrative Skills

#### 3.4.1 Narrative Construction
- **Description:** Difficulty organizing events into coherent stories with appropriate structure (setting, characters, problem, resolution), temporal sequencing, and cohesion.
- **Conditions:** ASD (excessive detail in some areas, omitting key information), TBI (disorganized discourse lacking informational content), DLD (macro-level deficits).
- **AAC-specific:** AAC narrative interventions target storytelling precursors: commenting, labeling, describing actions using multi-symbol utterances.

#### 3.4.2 Presupposition Management
- **Description:** Difficulty estimating what shared knowledge exists between speaker and listener, leading to either over- or under-specification of information.
- **Conditions:** ASD, TBI (right hemisphere involvement).

## 4. PHYSICAL/MOTOR ACCESS BARRIERS

These barriers affect the ability to physically access communication tools and methods, independent of language or cognitive ability. Many individuals with these barriers have fully intact cognition and language -- their struggle is purely with the physical interface to communication.

**WHO ICF Codes:** b7 (neuromusculoskeletal and movement-related functions), e1 (products and technology), e3 (support and relationships)

### 4.1 Gross Motor Barriers
- **Description:** Impaired trunk control, head control, reaching, pointing, and body positioning that affects ability to access communication devices and use gestures.
- **Conditions:** CP (severe -- lowest GMFCS levels), MD/SMA (progressive weakness), ALS (progressive), Locked-in syndrome (near-total paralysis), Rett syndrome (whole-body motor apraxia).
- **Solutions:** Adaptive seating and positioning systems, wheelchair-mounted devices, alternative access sites (chin, head, foot, mouth/tongue, sip-and-puff).

### 4.2 Fine Motor Barriers
- **Description:** Inability to perform precise touch, button pressing, keyboard use, or other fine motor tasks required for many AAC devices.
- **Conditions:** CP (spasticity, involuntary movements), Parkinson's (bradykinesia -- slow to lift fingers; tremor -- extra key presses), MS (numbness, tremor), MD/SMA (progressive weakness), ALS (progressive), Angelman syndrome (ataxia, tremor).
- **Solutions:** Keyguards, enlarged targets, touch accommodations, alternative input methods.

### 4.3 Oral-Motor Barriers
- **Description:** Impaired control of tongue, lips, jaw, velum, and associated structures for speech production. Distinct from language formulation -- the person knows what to say but the mouth cannot execute.
- **Conditions:** CP (hypertonia may cause tongue to push to palate; impaired tongue-jaw and lip-jaw coordination), Down syndrome (hypotonia, small oral cavity, relatively large tongue, weak oral muscles), CAS (motor planning disorder -- muscles capable but "instructions" imprecise), Rett syndrome (motor apraxia), Angelman syndrome (oral-motor dyspraxia), ALS (progressive weakness of 100+ muscles), Parkinson's (rigidity), Head/neck cancer (surgical changes, radiation fibrosis).

### 4.4 Respiratory Barriers
- **Description:** Inadequate breath support for speech. All speech is produced on exhalation; respiratory weakness directly limits voice volume, utterance length, and speech endurance.
- **Conditions:** ALS (weakened diaphragm -- primary), MD/SMA (progressive respiratory decline), CP (shallow/irregular breathing, poor trunk control), Parkinson's (rigid chest wall), MS (respiratory muscle weakness).
- **Ventilator interaction:** Speaking valves on tracheostomy may not be tolerated; NIV masks interfere with speech; ventilator cycles don't synchronize with speech breathing; equipment noise masks quiet speech.

### 4.5 Visual-Motor Barriers (Eye Gaze)
- **Description:** Challenges with using eye gaze as a communication access method -- the last-resort access method for many users with severe physical disabilities.
- **Challenges include:** Calibration difficulties (must be done by actual user), reduced accuracy in CP (children show longer time and less accuracy), involuntary movements in dyskinetic CP making eye gaze unviable, fatigue and decreased accuracy over time, positioning requirements, strabismus preventing focus/fixation.
- **Conditions:** CP, ALS, Rett syndrome (eye gaze is often strongest preserved motor skill), Locked-in syndrome, MD/SMA, TBI.

### 4.6 Physical Fatigue as Access Barrier
- **Description:** Communication effort exceeds available energy. Speech requires significant physical effort for many conditions; extended conversations become exhausting; quality deteriorates over time.
- **Conditions:** CP (speech is physically exhausting), MS (fatigue-communication cycle -- 80% experience fatigue; speech degrades throughout day), ALS (communication fatigue increases as muscles weaken), MD/SMA (start conversations clearly but become increasingly unintelligible; need rest periods).
- **AAC implication:** Multimodal communication essential -- switch between access methods based on energy level (e.g., direct selection when alert, scanning when fatigued).

## 5. COGNITIVE-COMMUNICATION BARRIERS

Cognitive-communication disorders arise when underlying cognitive deficits disrupt communication, rather than primary language or motor speech impairment. ASHA defines these as difficulties "with any aspect of communication that is affected by disruption of cognition."

**WHO ICF Codes:** b1 (mental functions -- attention, memory, executive function); ASHA "Big 9" Area 7

### 5.1 Attention Deficits
Based on the Sohlberg and Mateer clinical model:
- **Focused attention:** Basic response to stimuli (rarely impaired in isolation)
- **Sustained attention:** Maintaining focus during long conversations or AAC device use
- **Selective attention:** Filtering relevant speech from background noise; conversing in noisy environments
- **Alternating attention:** Shifting between tasks -- affects conversation topic-switching and multitasking during communication
- **Divided attention:** Responding to multiple inputs simultaneously -- critical for AAC use (formulating message + navigating device + monitoring conversation)
- **Conditions:** TBI (60%+ of moderate-to-severe TBI up to 10 years post-injury -- most common cognitive deficit), MS, Dementia, Parkinson's, Rett syndrome (seizures disrupt attention).

### 5.2 Memory Deficits
- **Working memory:** Critical for AAC use -- must maintain target concepts while navigating multiple pages, remembering navigation paths, locating symbols, and inhibiting distractors. Failure to account for working memory demands creates unintentional AAC barriers.
  - **Conditions:** TBI, Aphasia (impaired working memory interacts with language comprehension), Dementia, MS, Down Syndrome (auditory short-term memory weakness)
- **Long-term memory:** Affects vocabulary knowledge, word-form associations, learned conversational scripts, and AAC device navigation procedures.
  - **Conditions:** Dementia (drives repetitive questioning, losing thread), TBI
- **Prospective memory:** Remembering to communicate at the right time ("I need to tell my teacher about...")
  - **Conditions:** TBI (impaired ability to initiate communication at appropriate times)

### 5.3 Executive Function Deficits
- **Planning:** Difficulty organizing what to say before saying it; messages are disorganized or incomplete
- **Organizing:** Trouble structuring coherent messages, especially extended discourse
- **Self-monitoring:** Inability to detect and correct communication errors; reduced self-awareness of breakdowns
- **Inhibition:** Failure to suppress irrelevant or inappropriate responses
- **Initiation:** Difficulty starting conversations or generating topics
- **Cognitive flexibility:** Inability to adapt communication strategies when one approach fails
- **Conditions:** TBI (48% of adults with moderate-severe TBI; 55% chronic severe TBI), Dementia (progressive), MS, ASD (executive function differences), Parkinson's.
- **Hidden AAC burden:** Simplifying communication through AAC may actually require MORE executive function -- translating thoughts from restricted vocabulary into meaningful utterances at conversational speed demands significant cognitive effort.

### 5.4 Processing Speed
- **Description:** Generalized cognitive slowing that affects every aspect of communication -- comprehension, formulation, response generation. Need more time to understand, formulate, and respond.
- **Conditions:** TBI, MS (most commonly affected function), Parkinson's, Dementia, Down Syndrome.
- **Communication impact:** Real-time conversation feels overwhelming; conversation moves on before response is ready; difficulty in group settings with rapid turn-taking.

### 5.5 Learning and Generalization Barriers
- **Description:** Difficulty learning new AAC systems, vocabulary, navigation patterns, and generalizing skills across contexts and communication partners.
- **Conditions:** Intellectual disability, Angelman syndrome (slower rate of learning requiring consistent repetition), Down Syndrome (needs ~20x more repetitions), Dementia (declining ability to learn new systems), TBI (impaired new learning).
- **AAC implication:** Simple, consistent interfaces; motor consistency (words never change position); graduated complexity.

## 6. PSYCHOSOCIAL COMMUNICATION BARRIERS

These barriers relate to the emotional, social, and psychological dimensions of communication. They affect all AAC users regardless of underlying condition and are often underestimated in their impact.

### 6.1 Communication Confidence and Anxiety
- **Description:** Reduced willingness to attempt communication due to fear of failure, embarrassment, or frustration. Situation-dependent, not purely personality-based.
- **Cycle:** Failed communication attempts --> reduced confidence --> fewer attempts --> less practice --> reduced competence --> further reduced confidence.
- **Conditions:** All AAC users, but particularly CAS (frustration cycle from repeated motor planning failures), CP (learned helplessness from repeated breakdowns), Aphasia (sudden loss from full competence), Parkinson's (progressive decline in confidence as speech worsens).
- **Note:** 33% of caregivers cite lack of training -- contributing to partner anxiety about interacting with AAC users.

### 6.2 Identity and Self-Expression
- **Description:** Voice is a key part of identity. Using a synthetic voice that doesn't match one's age, gender, cultural background, or personality is alienating. The concept of "voice" extends beyond speech to rights, autonomy, and inclusion.
- **Particularly acute:** ALS (progressive loss creating anticipatory grief), Head/neck cancer (sudden permanent loss), Parkinson's (progressive), any adult who previously had full speech.
- **AAC implication:** Voice banking, personal voice synthesis, voice customization, and vocabulary that allows personality expression are critical.

### 6.3 Stigma and Device Acceptance
- **Description:** Carrying an AAC device signals "incapability" to some. Fear of being teased. Higher device abandonment when stigma is present.
- **Evidence:** Parent-carers acknowledge real stigma impact; students may avoid using devices in class; Italian national study found stigma contributed to device abandonment.
- **Counter-evidence:** Peers often show curiosity and acceptance.
- **Contributing factor:** Ableism across healthcare, education, and community settings; limited community knowledge of AAC.

### 6.4 Social Participation and Inclusion
- **Description:** Lack of access to AAC has "devastating effects" -- jeopardizing outcomes in education, employment, healthcare, and community activities. Puts people at risk for adverse medical events, social isolation, maltreatment.
- **Barriers:** Access barriers (motor, device, skills), opportunity barriers (attitudes, training, policies), communication barriers (devices, trained staff, vocabulary).
- **No clear data** exist on the number of AAC users engaged in employment, post-secondary education, or community volunteering.

### 6.5 Communication Partner Dependency
- **Description:** Reliance on familiar partners who interpret speech, present choices, ask yes/no questions, or dominate conversations. Individuals with communication disorders are at particular risk for learned helplessness.
- **Evidence:** Interactions between children using communication boards and adults are dominated by the adult. Partners facilitate communication while AAC users take passive roles.
- **Conditions:** Rett syndrome (highly partner-dependent -- success depends on partners reading subtle cues), Locked-in syndrome (communication only possible with attentive trained partners or technology), CP (reliant on familiar partners), Angelman syndrome (partner must know individual well).

### 6.6 Psychosocial Impact of Communication Loss
- **Depression:** Aphasia estimates range 31-70% depending on methodology and timing (pooled meta-analytic estimate ~32%; individual studies report 40-60%+), vs. 8% general population. Often under-diagnosed because screening tools rely on verbal communication. Head/neck cancer: 20% mild, 13% moderate, 20% severe depression.
- **Social isolation:** Aphasia -- severe social network reduction in first year (90%+ report feeling isolated; many cannot identify a single friend). Parkinson's -- progressive withdrawal. MS -- social withdrawal even when impairment is "objectively mild."
- **Loss of roles:** Parent, professional, community member, friend -- all disrupted by communication loss.
- **Relationship strain:** Verbal intimacy disrupted; partner becomes caregiver/interpreter.
- **Employment:** Most with moderate-severe aphasia cannot return to previous jobs; career-ending for voice-dependent professions.
- **The vicious cycle:** Communication difficulty --> social withdrawal --> fewer opportunities --> slower recovery --> more frustration.

## 7. RATE AND EFFICIENCY BARRIERS

These barriers are specific to AAC use itself -- inherent limitations of augmented communication that affect all users regardless of underlying condition.

### 7.1 Communication Speed Gap
- **The gap:** AAC users produce 8-15 WPM; natural speakers produce 120-180 WPM in conversation. This is a 10-20x rate gap.
- **Best current technology:** RoBERTa models fine-tuned on AAC corpora achieved 25.75 WPM; LLM-based word prediction reached 30.4 WPM -- still 5-10x slower than speech.
- **Impact:** Fundamental constraint on all AAC-mediated communication. Conversations are slower, interactions less spontaneous, information exchange less efficient.

### 7.2 Conversational Timing
- **Description:** AAC users regularly miss opportunities to participate in fast-paced conversation. Humor, interjections, and timely responses are particularly affected.
- **Perception impact:** Competence of AAC user is influenced by how well timing aligns with partner's expectations.
- **Group settings:** Multiple conversation threads, rapid topic shifts, simultaneous speakers compound the problem.
- **Emerging solutions:** COMPA (real-time conversation transcription for pause-and-respond), AI-powered humor interfaces.

### 7.3 Communication Fatigue
- **Description:** Both physical and cognitive effort of using AAC leads to fatigue, shorter messages, reduced participation, and eventual withdrawal.
- **Cognitive burden:** Users must contemplate how to form a thought, translate it from restricted vocabulary, navigate the device interface, and do it at conversational speed. Often underestimated.
- **Design implication:** Reduce extraneous cognitive load (unnecessary interface complexity) while supporting germane load (meaningful processing).

### 7.4 Context Switching and Topic Lag
- **Description:** While AAC users are composing a response, other participants change the topic. Result: AAC users produce responses that are out-of-context because the conversation has moved on.
- **Group conversations:** Multiple threads, rapid shifts, and simultaneous speakers compound this.
- **Finding:** Aided communicators may actually initiate conversation more frequently in multi-party settings vs. dyads, possibly because they have more preparation time when others are talking.

## Additional Communication Struggles Not Mapped to the 15 Core Conditions

The following struggle types are clinically relevant to AAC but fall outside the 15 conditions analyzed in the core taxonomy. They are included here for completeness.

### Selective Mutism
- **Description:** Anxiety-based communication disorder where individuals cannot speak in certain social situations despite having normal speech capability in other contexts. DSM-5 diagnostic criteria; falls within ASHA's scope of practice.
- **Communication struggle type:** Does not fit neatly into motor/language/cognitive framework -- fundamentally anxiety-driven. Represents a situational expressive failure with intact receptive, linguistic, and motor systems.
- **AAC relevance:** Some individuals use AAC as a bridge intervention in anxiety-provoking settings (school, medical appointments). Text-based or typed AAC may be less stigmatizing than symbol-based systems for this population.
- **Prevalence:** 0.7-2% of children in community/school samples.

### Situational Speech Loss in ASD ("Going Nonverbal")
- **Description:** Distinct from permanent minimal verbal status. Some autistic individuals who typically speak fluently lose the ability to produce speech under stress, sensory overload, emotional overwhelm, or in unfamiliar environments. This is a transient, situation-dependent phenomenon sometimes called "autistic burnout" speech loss.
- **Communication struggle type:** Intermittent expressive failure with preserved receptive abilities. May involve motor planning shutdown, anxiety-mediated speech block, or sensory overload cascading to speech system.
- **AAC relevance:** Requires AAC systems that can be quickly activated as backup when speech becomes unavailable, then set aside when speech returns. Suggests need for "emergency mode" AAC always accessible on smartphone.

### Hearing Loss / Deafness with Comorbid Disabilities
- **Description:** Approximately 40-50% of deaf/hard-of-hearing individuals have additional disabilities that may require AAC beyond sign language. When hearing loss co-occurs with intellectual disability, motor impairment, or ASD, traditional deaf communication methods (sign language, lip reading) may be insufficient.
- **AAC relevance:** Requires multimodal systems that don't depend on auditory feedback. Visual and tactile AAC approaches essential.

### Multilingual/Bilingual Communication Barriers
- **Description:** Many AAC users live in multilingual households or communities but are forced into English-only (or single-language) AAC systems. This limits communication with family members, cultural participation, and identity expression.
- **AAC relevance:** Growing clinical concern. Only a few AAC apps support true bilingual code-switching (Proloquo2Go, TD Snap). Most apps offer limited language options, and virtually none support lesser-resourced languages.

### Spasmodic Dysphonia
- **Description:** Neurological voice disorder causing involuntary spasms of the laryngeal muscles, resulting in voice breaks, strained/strangled quality, or breathy voice. In severe cases, can make speech nearly impossible.
- **AAC relevance:** Rare, but severe cases may require AAC. Primarily treated with botulinum toxin injections rather than AAC.
