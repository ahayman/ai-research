# Historical Development — From Middle Chinese to Putonghua

## Overview

Contemporary Mandarin — specifically the variety codified as Putonghua (普通话, "common speech") in the People's Republic of China — is not an ancient language that has existed unchanged for millennia. It is a *deliberately constructed standard* based on a specific dialect (Beijing speech), codified by a specific political act (the 1956 State Council directive), and promoted through one of the most ambitious language planning campaigns in human history. Understanding Putonghua requires tracing the chain: Middle Chinese (~600-1200 CE) → early Mandarin/Guanhua (~1200-1900) → National Language Movement (1913-1949) → PRC Putonghua standardization (1955-present), and simultaneously tracking what happened across the Taiwan Strait, where the same underlying standard (Guoyu, 国语) evolved independently after 1949.

This history stands in triple contrast:
- **Against Traditional Chinese**: Putonghua is one endpoint of a long evolution from Middle Chinese. Cantonese, Min, and other "traditional" varieties preserve features (entering tone, more tonal distinctions, older vocabulary) that Mandarin lost. Traditional Chinese is not an "older version" of Mandarin — the relationship is lateral, like cousins, not linear, like parent-child.
- **Against English**: English standardization was gradual, decentralized, driven by printing (Caxton, 1476), dictionaries (Johnson, 1755; Webster, 1828), and cultural prestige rather than government decree. There is no "English Putonghua" — no single government body defined Standard English in a single directive. The contrast illuminates how different standardization can be in centralized vs. decentralized political systems.

## 1. Middle Chinese: The Reconstructed Ancestor (~600-1200 CE)

### What Middle Chinese Was

Middle Chinese (中古汉语, zhōnggǔ Hànyǔ) is the ancestor of all modern Chinese dialect groups — Mandarin, Wu, Min, Cantonese, Hakka, Gan, and Xiang. It is a *reconstructed* language: no one alive speaks it, and no audio recordings exist. Our knowledge comes primarily from:

1. **The Qieyun (切韵, 601 CE)**: A rhyme dictionary compiled by Lu Fayan (陆法言) and eight other scholars. It records the pronunciation of ~11,500 characters organized by tone and rhyme category. It does not use an alphabet or phonetic transcription — instead, it uses the *fanqie* (反切) method: each character's pronunciation is described by two other characters, one giving the initial consonant and the other the final/tone.

2. **Buddhist transliterations**: When Chinese scholars transliterated Sanskrit Buddhist texts, their choices reveal what sounds Chinese had at the time.

3. **Modern dialect comparisons**: By comparing the pronunciations of the same character across Cantonese, Mandarin, Min, Hakka, etc., linguists can reconstruct what the common ancestor must have sounded like (comparative method).

4. **Korean, Japanese, and Vietnamese borrowings**: These languages borrowed Chinese vocabulary at specific historical periods. The "Sino-Korean," "Sino-Japanese" (on'yomi), and "Sino-Vietnamese" readings preserve snapshots of Chinese pronunciation at the time of borrowing.

### The Middle Chinese Sound System

Middle Chinese had a substantially richer phonological inventory than modern Mandarin:

| Feature | Middle Chinese | Modern Mandarin | Cantonese (for comparison) |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|---------------------------|
| **Tone categories** | 4 categories (平 ping, 上 shang, 去 qu, 入 ru), each splitting into yin/yang = 8 tonal distinctions | 4 tones (no entering tone) | 6-9 tones (preserves entering tone) |
| **Entering tone (入声 rusheng)** | Present — syllables ending in -p, -t, -k | **Completely lost** — the most dramatic change | Preserved: 六 = luk6, 十 = sap6 |
| **Voiced obstruents** | Full series: b, d, g, dz, z, etc. | **Completely lost** — merged into aspirated/unaspirated series | Partially lost |
| **Final consonants** | -p, -t, -k, -m, -n, -ng | Only -n and -ng survive | -p, -t, -k, -m, -n, -ng all preserved |
| **Number of initials** | ~36 (traditional analysis) | 21 | 19 |
| **Number of finals** | ~200+ rhyme categories | ~35 distinct finals | ~53 finals |

**The pattern**: Modern Mandarin represents a *massive simplification* of the Middle Chinese sound system. It lost final stop consonants entirely (-p, -t, -k), lost the entering tone category, lost voiced obstruents, and reduced the number of tonal and rhyme distinctions. This simplification increased homophony (more characters sharing the same pronunciation) — which is why modern Mandarin compensated by shifting toward disyllabic compounds.

### The Entering Tone: The Biggest Loss

The entering tone (入声, rusheng) was defined by syllables ending in the unreleased stop consonants -p, -t, -k. These syllables were short, clipped, and abrupt — the word ended with a consonant that closed the mouth or throat without release.

Examples of what happened to entering-tone characters:

| Character | Meaning | Middle Chinese | Mandarin | Cantonese |
|-----------|---------|---------------|----------|-----------|
| 六 | six | *liuk | liù (tone 4) | luk6 |
| 十 | ten | *dʑip | shí (tone 2) | sap6 |
| 百 | hundred | *pæk | bǎi (tone 3) | baak3 |
| 國 | country | *kwək | guó (tone 2) | gwok3 |
| 學 | study | *ɦæwk | xué (tone 2) | hok6 |
| 白 | white | *bæk | bái (tone 2) | baak6 |

In Mandarin, the entering tone was redistributed across the remaining four tones with no clear pattern (some went to tone 1, some to tone 2, some to tone 3, some to tone 4). This is why entering-tone characters are a notorious difficulty in classical Chinese poetry for Mandarin speakers — you cannot hear which characters were entering tone from their modern Mandarin pronunciation, but Cantonese speakers can immediately identify them.

**Three-way comparison**: English experienced an analogous phonological upheaval in the Great Vowel Shift (~1400-1700), which scrambled the relationship between spelling and pronunciation. Both Mandarin and English underwent dramatic sound changes that their writing systems did not reflect — but Chinese characters encode meaning, not sound, so the impact on the writing system was nil, whereas English spelling became famously "irregular" (though it actually preserves older pronunciation fossils).

## 2. From Middle Chinese to Mandarin: The Sound Change Cascade

### Why Mandarin Is Called Mandarin

The term "Mandarin" derives from Portuguese *mandarim*, itself from Malay *menteri* (minister), from Sanskrit *mantrin* (counselor). It referred to the Chinese officials (mandarins) and, by extension, the language they spoke — the official spoken language of the imperial court.

The Chinese term 官话 (Guānhuà, "official speech/language of officials") refers to the prestige spoken standard used for government administration from roughly the Ming dynasty (1368-1644) onward. This was not a precisely defined standard — it was a loose sociolect of educated officials, based initially on the Nanjing dialect and later shifting toward Beijing speech.

### The Nanjing-to-Beijing Shift

This is a critical and often misunderstood aspect of Mandarin standardization:

**Nanjing-based era (~1300s-1700s)**: During the Ming dynasty (capital: initially Nanjing, then Beijing from 1421), the prestige spoken standard was based on Nanjing pronunciation. Nanjing was the cultural and economic center of the Jiangnan region. Early European descriptions of "Mandarin" by Matteo Ricci (1583) and others reflect Nanjing-based speech, which still preserved some features lost in modern Beijing Mandarin (e.g., entering tone distinctions in some form, different tonal patterns).

**Beijing ascendancy (~1700s-1900s)**: As Beijing solidified its position as the political capital through the Qing dynasty (1644-1912), Beijing pronunciation gradually displaced Nanjing pronunciation as the prestige standard. The Manchu Qing court adopted Beijing-area speech. By the late Qing, "Guanhua" effectively meant Beijing-based speech, though with considerable variation.

**Key point for the three-way comparison**: The shift from Nanjing to Beijing as the phonological base is analogous to the shift in English prestige pronunciation from various regional standards to London-based Received Pronunciation (RP). In both cases, political power determined linguistic prestige. But in English, this was never codified in a single government decree — it emerged organically. In Chinese, it was eventually made explicit.

### The Phonological Change Cascade: Middle Chinese → Mandarin

The major sound changes that distinguish Mandarin from Middle Chinese (and from conservative varieties like Cantonese) occurred over roughly 600-800 years:

**1. Loss of entering tone (入声 → redistribution, ~1000-1400 CE)**
- The final stop consonants (-p, -t, -k) weakened and disappeared
- Entering-tone syllables were redistributed among the remaining tones
- This change did NOT affect southern dialects (Cantonese, Min, Hakka retain -p, -t, -k)

**2. Loss of voiced obstruents (~1000-1300 CE)**
- Middle Chinese b, d, g, dz, dʑ, z, etc. → merged with voiceless counterparts
- Aspirated in certain tonal environments, unaspirated in others (the "voicing-to-aspiration" shift)
- This is why Mandarin has zh/ch, z/c, b/p, d/t, g/k pairs (aspiration contrast) rather than voiced/voiceless contrast

**3. Loss of final -m (~1300-1600 CE)**
- Middle Chinese -m → -n in Mandarin
- 三 sān (three): Middle Chinese *sam → Mandarin sān, but Cantonese saam1
- 南 nán (south): Middle Chinese *nam → Mandarin nán, but Cantonese naam4

**4. Tone mergers and splits (~1000-1500 CE)**
- The 8 Middle Chinese tonal distinctions (4 categories × yin/yang) collapsed to 4 tones
- Yin-ping + some yang-ping → Tone 1 (high level)
- Yang-ping + some ru → Tone 2 (rising)
- Shang → Tone 3 (dipping)
- Qu + some ru → Tone 4 (falling)
- The entering tone syllables scattered across all four tones

**5. Palatalization (~1400-1600 CE)**
- Middle Chinese k, kh, x before front vowels → j, q, x
- This created the modern Mandarin j/q/x series, which does not exist in Cantonese
- 金 jīn (gold): Middle Chinese *kim → Mandarin jīn, Cantonese gam1

**Result**: Modern Mandarin has a significantly smaller phonological inventory than Middle Chinese, resulting in massive homophony. Approximately 1,300 distinct syllable slots exist in Mandarin (including tones). This compares with ~3,500+ in Middle Chinese and ~1,700+ in Cantonese. This is why Mandarin relies so heavily on disyllabic compounds to disambiguate — a structural consequence of historical sound change.

## 3. The National Language Movement (1913-1949)

### The 1913 Conference on Unification of Pronunciation (读音统一会)

In 1913, the newly established Republic of China convened a conference to establish a national pronunciation standard. This was the first formal attempt to create a single spoken standard for all of China.

The debate was fierce: northern delegates argued for a Beijing-based standard; southern delegates wanted features from southern dialects included. The compromise was a hybrid standard called "Old National Pronunciation" (老国音, lǎo guóyīn) that combined Beijing tones with some Nanjing features, including a preserved entering tone distinction. This was an artificial construct — no one actually spoke it.

The phonetic notation system Zhuyin Fuhao (注音符号, now commonly called Bopomofo: ㄅㄆㄇㄈ) was adopted at this conference (formally promulgated 1918). Based on elements of Chinese characters, it remains the primary phonetic system in Taiwan today.

### The 1932 Switch to Beijing-Based Standard (新国音)

The artificial 1913 standard proved unworkable — teachers did not know how to pronounce the entering tone in the prescribed way, and recordings and training materials could not be produced. In 1932, the Ministry of Education issued the "Vocabulary of National Pronunciation for Everyday Use" (国音常用字汇), which quietly dropped the entering tone and other non-Beijing features. This "New National Pronunciation" (新国音, xīn guóyīn) was essentially Beijing pronunciation, establishing the principle that has governed Chinese standardization ever since: **Beijing phonology as the base**.

**Three-way comparison**: This switch parallels the English adoption of London/RP pronunciation as the prestige standard, but in English this happened organically through the BBC, public schools, and social prestige — never by government decree. The Chinese case is notable for its explicitness: a specific government document changed the standard.

### The Terms: Guoyu vs. Putonghua

The Republic of China called its standard 国语 (Guóyǔ, "national language"). When the People's Republic was established in 1949, the new government deliberated and chose a different name: 普通话 (Pǔtōnghuà, "common speech"). The stated reason was ideological — "national language" implied a single nation (potentially excluding minorities), while "common speech" suggested a lingua franca shared among a multilingual population.

After 1949, the two standards evolved independently:
- **PRC**: Putonghua, with simplified characters and Pinyin romanization
- **Taiwan (ROC)**: Guoyu, with traditional characters and Zhuyin (Bopomofo)

## 4. The 1956 Putonghua Definition and Its Three Pillars

### The Definition

On February 6, 1956, the State Council of the PRC issued a directive officially defining Putonghua:

> "以北京语音为标准音，以北方话为基础方言，以典范的现代白话文著作为语法规范"
> 
> "Taking Beijing pronunciation as the standard pronunciation, northern dialects as the base dialect, and exemplary modern vernacular literary works as the grammatical norm."

This is the **three-pillar definition** and it is remarkably precise in its structure:

**Pillar 1: Beijing Pronunciation (北京语音为标准音)**
- The phonological system — tones, initials, finals — is based on Beijing dialect
- BUT: "Beijing pronunciation" ≠ "Beijing dialect" — distinctly Beijing colloquialisms, slang, and certain Beijing-specific sound patterns (e.g., heavy erhua 儿化, 胡同 hútòng → hútòr) are NOT part of Putonghua
- Putonghua is an *idealized* version of Beijing pronunciation, not actual Beijing street speech

**Pillar 2: Northern Dialects as Base (北方话为基础方言)**
- Grammar and core vocabulary draw from the broader northern dialect group, not just Beijing
- This means that northern grammatical structures (word order, particle usage, aspect markers) form the foundation
- Southern features that differ from northern norms (e.g., Cantonese grammar, Wu grammar) are excluded

**Pillar 3: Vernacular Literary Norms (典范的现代白话文著作为语法规范)**
- Grammatical correctness is judged by reference to exemplary modern vernacular writing
- This includes the works of authors like Lu Xun, Mao Dun, Lao She, etc.
- This pillar resolves ambiguity: when spoken Beijing varies from the written standard, the written standard wins
- It also provides a stable reference point, preventing the standard from drifting with every change in Beijing colloquial speech

### Why Three Pillars Instead of One?

The three-pillar structure solves a specific problem: no single source can define a standard language alone.

- If the standard were simply "Beijing dialect," it would include Beijing slang, heavy erhua, and local expressions that would be unintelligible or alien to speakers from other regions
- If the standard were simply "northern Chinese," it would be vague — which northern dialect? What about grammatical variation within the north?
- If the standard were simply "good literary Chinese," it would be a written-only standard with no spoken model

The three-pillar approach gives Beijing phonology (clear, specific), northern grammar (broad but bounded), and literary norms (stable reference) — each pillar compensating for the others' weaknesses.

**Three-way comparison**: English has no equivalent tripartite definition. The closest analog would be something like: "RP pronunciation, common grammatical structures across educated Southern British English, and norms exemplified by quality journalism and BBC broadcasting." But this has never been formally stated. Standard American English is even less formally defined. The Chinese approach to standardization is far more explicit and centralized.

## 5. Putonghua Promotion: From 54% to 91%

### The Campaign

Putonghua promotion (推广普通话, tuīguǎng Pǔtōnghuà) has been one of the PRC's longest-running social campaigns:

| Year | Event | Impact |
|------|-------|--------|
| 1956 | State Council directive defining Putonghua | Legal/administrative foundation |
| 1958 | Hanyu Pinyin adopted | Phonetic tool for teaching pronunciation |
| 1982 | Constitution Article 19: "The state promotes the nationwide use of Putonghua" | Constitutional mandate |
| 1986 | National Language Work Conference | Renewed push after Cultural Revolution disruption |
| 1994 | Putonghua Proficiency Test (普通话水平测试) established | Formal assessment system |
| 2000 | National Common Language Law (通用语言文字法) | Legal requirement for broadcasting, education, public services |
| 2001 | Putonghua proficiency required for teachers, broadcasters, civil servants | Professional gatekeeping |
| Ongoing | Annual "Putonghua Promotion Week" (every September) | Public awareness campaigns |

### Comprehension Rates

The PRC government has tracked Putonghua comprehension rates through periodic surveys:

- **1950s-1960s**: Approximately 54% of the population could "understand" Putonghua (understanding, not necessarily speaking)
- **2000**: Approximately 53% reported ability to communicate in Putonghua
- **2004**: 53% (per government survey)
- **2010**: Approximately 70%
- **2017**: 73% (State Language Commission survey)
- **2020**: 80.72% (State Language Commission report)
- **2025 target**: 85%

**Note on methodology**: These are self-reported figures from government surveys, and the criteria for "can communicate in Putonghua" have varied. The 54% → 80%+ trajectory is broadly accepted, but the precise figures should be treated with caution. Independent linguistic assessments suggest that functional Putonghua proficiency (ability to conduct everyday affairs entirely in Putonghua) may be lower than self-reported communication ability, especially in rural areas of southern and western China.

The often-cited "91%" figure appears in some sources and may refer to a more recent survey or a different metric (e.g., among younger demographics, or "can understand" rather than "can speak fluently"). The actual figure for the total population is more conservatively around 77-81% as of 2020.

### Mechanisms of Spread

- **Education**: Putonghua is the medium of instruction in all public schools nationwide. Dialect use in classrooms has been progressively restricted.
- **Media**: All national broadcasting (CCTV, national radio) uses Putonghua. Local stations may use local languages/dialects in specific slots, but Putonghua dominates.
- **Urbanization**: Mass internal migration (rural → urban) created need for a common language. Workers from Sichuan, Guangdong, and Fujian meeting in Shenzhen factories need Putonghua.
- **Internet and social media**: The digital ecosystem operates overwhelmingly in written Mandarin (using simplified characters). Voice messages, livestreaming, and short videos further spread spoken Putonghua.
- **Economic incentive**: Putonghua proficiency correlates with economic opportunity. Speaking Putonghua fluently provides access to the national job market.

## 6. Parallel Development: Taiwan's Guoyu

### Shared Origin, Independent Evolution

Taiwan's Guoyu (国语, "national language") and the PRC's Putonghua share a common origin: both derive from the 1932 New National Pronunciation standard based on Beijing phonology. After 1949, they evolved independently.

### Key Differences

| Feature | PRC Putonghua | Taiwan Guoyu |
|---------|--------------|--------------|
| **Character set** | Simplified (简体字) | Traditional (繁體字) |
| **Phonetic notation** | Hanyu Pinyin (1958) | Zhuyin/Bopomofo (1918) |
| **Pronunciation tendencies** | More erhua (儿化), stronger retroflexes (zh, ch, sh, r) | Less erhua, weaker retroflexes (often zh→z, sh→s in casual speech) |
| **Vocabulary differences** | ~2,000+ documented differences | |
| | 软件 (ruǎnjiàn, "soft-piece") = software | 軟體 (ruǎntǐ, "soft-body") = software |
| | 信息 (xìnxī) = information | 資訊 (zīxùn) = information |
| | 出租车 (chūzūchē) = taxi | 計程車 (jìchéngchē) = taxi |
| **Loanword strategy** | Tend toward semantic calques (meaning-based translation) | Tend toward phonetic transliteration |
| | 激光 jīguāng ("stimulated light") = laser | 雷射 léishè (sound borrowing from "laser") |
| **Tone values** | Beijing-standard tone values | Slightly different tone values; tone 2 and tone 3 often less differentiated |

### The Convergence Trend

Since the opening of cross-strait communication in the 1990s and especially with the rise of the internet, there has been significant reconvergence:
- PRC netizens adopt Taiwan slang and vice versa
- Shared consumption of entertainment media (dramas, music, variety shows)
- Written Mandarin online is increasingly mutually intelligible despite character-set differences
- Some vocabulary differences persist, but the trend is toward convergence rather than further divergence

## 7. Comparison with English Standardization

### English: Gradual, Decentralized, Market-Driven

| Dimension | Mandarin (Putonghua) | English |
|-----------|---------------------|---------|
| **Defining moment** | 1956 State Council directive | None — gradual prestige emergence |
| **Pronunciation standard** | Beijing-based, explicitly defined | RP (British) or General American — never officially defined |
| **Authority** | State Council, State Language Commission, Ministry of Education | No central authority (OED, Merriam-Webster are descriptive, not prescriptive) |
| **Legal status** | Constitutionally mandated (Art. 19, 1982); legally required for public services | No legal mandate for any specific variety (some countries have English as official language, but no standard defined) |
| **Spelling reform** | Simplified characters (1956/1964) mandated by law | No successful spelling reform in modern era (Noah Webster made minor changes for American English: colour → color) |
| **Phonetic tool** | Pinyin (1958) — officially adopted romanization | No official phonetic system (IPA is academic; no government-adopted pronunciation guide) |
| **Standardization trajectory** | Explicit government decree → enforcement → gradual spread | Printing press (1476) → dictionaries (1755, 1828) → BBC/media → informal prestige |
| **Timeline** | Rapid: ~70 years from directive to ~80% adoption | Very slow: ~500 years from Caxton to approximate stabilization |

### Key Differences in the Standardization Process

**Centralized vs. organic**: The most fundamental contrast. Putonghua was defined by a government committee, codified in an official document, and promoted through law, education policy, and media regulation. English "Standard English" emerged organically through printing conventions, prestige dialects, and market forces (people who spoke like London merchants, then like BBC broadcasters, had social advantages).

**Single standard vs. multiple standards**: Putonghua is THE standard — there is one, officially defined. English has at least two major standards (British and American), with additional standards in Australia, India, Singapore, etc., and no overarching authority declaring any of them primary.

**Speed**: The PRC achieved ~80% Putonghua comprehension in roughly 70 years through a deliberate campaign. English standardization has been ongoing for ~550 years and is still in flux (debates about singular "they," oxford comma, etc.).

## 8. What Middle Chinese Reveals About the Three-Way Comparison

### Feature Retention Table

| Middle Chinese Feature | Modern Mandarin | Cantonese | English (analogous) |
|----------------------|-----------------|-----------|---------------------|
| Entering tone (-p, -t, -k finals) | Lost entirely | Preserved | English once lost final consonant clusters too (OE climban → climb) |
| 8 tonal distinctions | Reduced to 4 | 6-9 preserved | N/A (English is non-tonal) |
| Voiced obstruents (b, d, g) | Lost → aspiration contrast | Partially preserved | English retains voiced/voiceless contrast |
| Final -m | Lost → merged with -n | Preserved | English retains -m vs -n distinction |
| Monosyllabic words dominant | Shifted heavily to disyllabic | Still more monosyllabic | English: mix of mono- and polysyllabic |
| Classical literary register | Abandoned (1919) | Still partially used in formal Cantonese | English: Latin/French register layer preserved |

### The Conservation Gradient

A useful mental model: Chinese dialect groups can be arranged on a "conservation gradient" from most conservative (retaining most Middle Chinese features) to most innovative:

**Most conservative → Most innovative**:
Min (preserves pre-Middle Chinese features) → Cantonese (preserves entering tone, -m, more tones) → Hakka → Wu → Gan → Xiang → Mandarin (most innovative, most features lost)

This is counterintuitive: the "standard" variety (Mandarin/Putonghua) is actually the MOST changed from the historical ancestor. It became the standard not because of conservatism but because of political power — the northern capital regions where Mandarin evolved happened to be the seats of Ming and Qing dynasties.

## Key Insights for Pattern Learners

1. **Putonghua is constructed, not inherited**: It is a deliberately engineered standard, not a natural dialect that "won." The three-pillar definition explicitly separates phonology (Beijing), grammar (northern dialects), and usage norms (literary works) — each from a different source.

2. **Mandarin is the MOST changed variety**: Counter to the common assumption that the "standard" language is the "original" — Mandarin has changed more from Middle Chinese than Cantonese has. The standard was chosen for political, not linguistic, reasons.

3. **The entering-tone loss is the single biggest structural change**: It reduced the syllable inventory dramatically, increased homophony, and drove the disyllabic shift that characterizes modern Mandarin.

4. **Centralized vs. organic standardization**: The Putonghua/English contrast is one of the clearest examples of how political systems shape language planning. Both ended up with functioning standards, but through radically different processes.

5. **Cross-strait divergence is shallow**: Despite 75+ years of separation, Putonghua and Guoyu remain mutually intelligible, with differences concentrated in vocabulary, loanword strategies, and pronunciation details rather than grammar or core structure.
