# Relevance Theory: The Cognitive Engine of Communication

## Tier 1 Introduction — The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

> Relevance Theory proposes that communication is not a code but an act of showing that you are trying to be understood — and that when communication breaks down between autistic and non-autistic speakers, the problem is a mismatch of cognitive environments, not a deficit in either mind.

---

## How to Read This Page

This is a **Tier 1 framing document**. Its job is to give you a working mental model of Relevance Theory (RT) so that the rest of the hub — especially Category 10, which goes into the full framework — makes sense when you reach it.

RT is one of two frameworks that do most of the theoretical work in this hub. The other is Grice's Cooperative Principle, introduced in a separate Tier 1 page. You should read both. They don't compete so much as operate at different levels: Grice describes the social logic of conversation, while RT describes the cognitive machinery that makes any such logic possible.

If you only take away three things from this page, take these:

1. **Relevance is a ratio** — cognitive effects divided by processing effort. Your mind automatically seeks the best deal.
2. **Communication is ostensive** — when someone talks to you, they are *showing you that they are trying to be understood*, and you use that showing as inferential evidence.
3. **Mismatches in cognitive environment produce breakdowns** — and the RT framework predicts these bidirectionally, which is why it has been used to give the double empathy problem formal grounding.

## From Grice to Sperber and Wilson

### What Grice gave us

Paul Grice's Cooperative Principle (covered in its own Tier 1 page) proposed that conversation works because listeners assume speakers are cooperating. When what a speaker says on the surface doesn't fit the context, the listener works backward from the assumption of cooperation to figure out what the speaker must have meant. Grice called this **implicature**, and he described four **maxims** — Quantity, Quality, Relation, Manner — that speakers are assumed to be following.

Grice's framework was powerful. It explained why "Can you pass the salt?" is understood as a request rather than as a weird question about your motor abilities. It explained how sarcasm works: you say the opposite of what is true, but because you're assumed to be cooperating, I work out that you must be mocking or disagreeing. It explained how hinting works.

But Grice's framework left a question open. It described *what* listeners do when they interpret utterances. It did not explain *why* listeners do this. Why should anyone assume, going in, that speakers are being cooperative? What drives the inference in the first place?

### Sperber and Wilson's move

Dan Sperber and Deirdre Wilson, in *Relevance: Communication and Cognition* (1986, revised 1995), answered that question in a way that reframed the entire field. Their answer was cognitive rather than social: **human minds are built to look for relevance**. It is not a social contract that makes listeners cooperate; it is how cognition works at the level of the mind, full stop. When your visual system is presented with a scene, it automatically attends to the parts that matter to you. When your auditory system is presented with a hum of background noise, it automatically ignores everything except what is potentially important. Minds are relevance-seeking machines. Language processing is just a special case.

On the RT account, when someone speaks to you, you are not first checking whether they are cooperating and then inferring meaning from that. You are instead running the same relevance-seeking process you run on every other input, on this input. You are computing interpretations in order of accessibility and stopping as soon as you find one that is relevant enough to be worth the effort.

### The claim is stronger than Grice's

Grice said: "interpret utterances under the assumption of cooperation." Sperber and Wilson said: "interpret utterances under the assumption of relevance — because that's how your mind processes any input, not just linguistic input."

The difference matters. Grice's maxims are norms that speakers can obey or violate. Violating a maxim is what generates implicatures — you notice the violation and work out what the speaker must have meant. RT's principle of relevance is not a norm. It is not something a speaker can violate. It is an **exceptionless generalization** about what happens when any mind is presented with an ostensive stimulus. The question is never "did they violate relevance?" It is always "what interpretation satisfies the expectation of relevance here?"

### The four maxims collapse into one principle

In Grice's framework, you have four maxims doing separate jobs. In RT, those four maxims all reduce to the single principle of relevance:

- **Quality** (be truthful): false information produces misleading cognitive effects, which reduces relevance.
- **Quantity** (say enough, not too much): too little yields too few effects; too much imposes too much effort. Both reduce the ratio.
- **Relation** (be relevant): this *is* the entire theory.
- **Manner** (be clear): greater clarity means less processing effort, which raises relevance.

Everything Grice's maxims did, the relevance ratio does more economically. This is not just theoretical tidiness. It is the claim that beneath the social surface of conversation, there is a single cognitive mechanism doing the work — and that mechanism is the same one you use to process every other input your mind encounters.

## The Relevance Equation

RT is one of the few pragmatic frameworks that gives you a formula. The formula is this:

> **Relevance = Cognitive Effects Achieved / Processing Effort Required**

An utterance is **more relevant** when it produces many cognitive effects for little processing effort. It is **less relevant** when it produces few effects for a lot of effort. Your mind automatically prefers the best ratio it can find.

### What counts as a cognitive effect

A **cognitive effect** is a change in what you believe. RT identifies three main kinds:

1. **New information added** — you learn something you didn't know.
2. **Existing beliefs strengthened** — something you already suspected is now better supported.
3. **Existing beliefs contradicted or revised** — you update what you thought you knew.

Derived conclusions also count. If someone tells you it's raining, and you already know the car windows are open, you derive a new conclusion — the car is getting wet — without the speaker having mentioned the car. That derived conclusion is a cognitive effect of the utterance, and it is part of what makes the utterance relevant to you.

### What counts as processing effort

**Processing effort** is everything your mind has to do to extract those effects:

- Decoding the words (parsing, recognizing vocabulary)
- Disambiguating (figuring out which meaning of a word is intended)
- Assigning reference (who is "he"? which "that"?)
- Retrieving relevant context from memory
- Running inferences

The more work, the higher the effort. The higher the effort, the lower the relevance — unless there are enough cognitive effects to compensate.

### The two principles

Sperber and Wilson state RT's claims as two principles:

- **Cognitive Principle of Relevance**: human cognition tends to be geared toward maximizing relevance. (This is a claim about minds in general, not just about language.)
- **Communicative Principle of Relevance**: every ostensive act of communication conveys a presumption of its own optimal relevance — when a speaker addresses you, they are implicitly promising that the utterance is worth processing.

The second principle is stronger than it sounds. It says that merely by speaking to you, a speaker has committed themselves to the claim that their utterance is worth the effort you will spend on it. If it is not worth the effort, they have failed to be optimally relevant — and you are entitled to treat this as a communication breakdown rather than as your own failure to understand.

### A worked example

You walk into a friend's kitchen. They are staring at the oven and say:

> "I left it on 400 for too long."

Your mind starts computing interpretations in order of accessibility:

- *Literal decoding*: they left something at 400 degrees for longer than some threshold.
- *Reference assignment*: "it" must be whatever is in the oven — probably the dinner they invited you to eat.
- *Enrichment*: "too long" must mean "long enough that something bad happened."
- *Derived inference*: the dinner is burned.
- *Further derivation*: dinner may be ruined; plans may need to change; they are probably frustrated.

You stop here. You have reached an interpretation that yields many cognitive effects (you now know the dinner is burned, they are probably upset, plans may change) for relatively little effort (you didn't have to ask anything). That is optimal relevance in action.

Notice what you didn't do: you didn't enumerate every possible interpretation and then pick the best. You stopped at the first interpretation that met your expectation of relevance. This is the **path of least effort**, and it is how RT says all utterance interpretation actually works.

And notice what the speaker implicitly did: by saying those words in that context, they committed themselves to the claim that you would find a relevant interpretation. They didn't have to spell out "the dinner is burned, plans are changing, I'm upset." RT's communicative principle says they were entitled to trust you to derive all that from the utterance plus the context.

## Ostensive-Inferential Communication

### Why communication is not a code

Most people think of communication as an encoding-decoding process: I have a thought, I encode it into words, I transmit the words, you decode the words, now you have the thought. This is the picture that drives most everyday talk about "getting the message across."

RT rejects this picture as incomplete. Language does encode some content — a sentence has a linguistic meaning that you can look up in a dictionary and a grammar — but that encoded content is never enough to determine what the speaker meant. Every utterance requires inferential work by the listener to turn the decoded string into an interpretation.

RT therefore characterizes communication as **ostensive-inferential**. The word "ostensive" comes from Latin *ostendere*, "to show." When you communicate, you are not just transmitting a signal. You are **showing** that you are trying to be understood. The listener uses that showing as evidence for inference.

### The two intentions

Sperber and Wilson (building on Grice's earlier analysis) say that every communicative act involves two layered intentions:

- **Informative intention**: the speaker's intention to make a set of assumptions manifest to the listener.
- **Communicative intention**: the speaker's intention to make it mutually manifest to both parties that the speaker has the above informative intention.

That second intention is the crucial one. You are not just trying to plant a thought in your listener's head. You are trying to make it *evident to both of you that you are trying to plant a thought in their head*. The listener recognizing your communicative intention is part of what makes communication work.

This is why hints feel different from statements. When you hint, you are relying on the listener's recognition that *you know that they know you are trying to communicate something*. The mutual recognition is load-bearing.

### Why this matters

On the RT picture, communication is relational from the start. Every communicative act requires both parties to model each other's minds as coordinating. The listener isn't decoding a signal; the listener is using a *displayed intention* as inferential evidence. The speaker isn't transmitting a thought; the speaker is producing a stimulus whose best interpretation, given the context, is what they wanted to communicate.

This has a large practical implication. What the listener ends up believing after the utterance depends on two things:

1. **What the listener already believed** — their existing cognitive environment.
2. **How the utterance interacts with that prior content** — the inferences the utterance licenses, given the listener's prior beliefs.

Two listeners hearing the same utterance in the same room can end up with different interpretations, because they started with different beliefs. This is not a bug in the theory. It is the mechanism. RT predicts it.

## Cognitive Environment and Mutual Manifestness

### Your cognitive environment

Your **cognitive environment** is the set of facts that are *manifest* to you. Something is manifest to you if you either already believe it or could come to believe it if it were brought to your attention.

This is wider than "everything you know." It includes potential beliefs you could form given the right prompt. You do not currently believe that the chair in front of you has exactly seventeen small scratches on its leg — but if someone pointed it out, you could confirm or deny the claim. That potential belief is part of your cognitive environment even though you haven't formed it yet.

Your cognitive environment at any moment is the product of your life history: your sensory profile, your attention patterns, your background knowledge, your recent experiences, your current surroundings. It is what is *available* to your mind for inferential use right now.

### Mutual manifestness

When two people communicate, the part of their cognitive environments that overlaps — the set of facts manifest to both of them *and* mutually known to be manifest to both of them — is called **mutual manifestness**. (Sperber and Wilson were careful to avoid the stronger notion of "common knowledge," which requires an infinite regress of "I know that you know that I know..." Mutual manifestness is weaker and more tractable.)

Communication works best when speaker and listener have substantial mutual manifestness. The more of your cognitive environments overlap, the more the speaker can leave implicit, because the listener can fill it in. The less overlap, the more the speaker has to make explicit, because there is less shared ground to trade on.

This is why you can say almost anything to your oldest friend and be understood, while you have to spell out every detail to a stranger. Your cognitive environments with your oldest friend overlap enormously. With a stranger, they barely overlap at all.

### Connection to the double empathy problem

Now comes the step that matters most for this hub. If autistic and non-autistic speakers have substantially different cognitive environments — because of different sensory profiles, different attention patterns, different life histories, different things that get automatically foregrounded versus backgrounded — then their mutual manifestness with each other will be **structurally limited**.

Each speaker is computing relevance against their *own* cognitive environment. An utterance that is maximally relevant for the speaker's cognitive environment is not necessarily maximally relevant for the listener's cognitive environment. The speaker, running on the communicative principle, trusts that the listener will find a relevant interpretation. But if the listener's cognitive environment doesn't contain the context needed to reach the intended interpretation, the listener lands somewhere else. And the speaker, looking at the listener's response, cannot understand why the listener got it "wrong."

This is the double empathy problem stated in RT terms. We will say more about this below. For now, notice the key shift: communication breakdowns between autistic and non-autistic speakers are not failures on one side. They are **predictable RT failures of mutual manifestness across different cognitive styles**. The theory predicts them. The theory assigns them symmetrically.

## Explicature and Implicature in RT

RT refines the classical distinction between "what is said" and "what is implied." Rather than a single line between them, RT proposes two different kinds of communicated content:

### Explicature

An **explicature** is what the speaker explicitly communicated. This is *not* the same as the literal, dictionary meaning of the sentence. It is the proposition the utterance encodes *plus* the contextual fleshing-out required to turn that proposition into a complete thought.

Turning an encoded sentence into a complete proposition requires several kinds of pragmatic work:

- **Reference assignment** — who is "she," which "that," when was "yesterday"?
- **Disambiguation** — is "bank" the financial one or the river one?
- **Saturation** — "I'm ready" → ready for what?
- **Free enrichment** — "I've had breakfast" → breakfast *this morning*, not at any point in my life.
- **Narrowing/broadening** — "drink" in "I need a drink" → specifically an alcoholic drink.

All of this is part of the explicature. It is the *explicitly communicated* content, even though it is not fully encoded in the words. The words point at it; the listener's inferential work completes it.

### Implicature

An **implicature** is what the speaker communicated *beyond* the explicature, through further inference. If someone says "I've had breakfast" and you take them to be declining your offer of cereal, that declining is an implicature. The explicature is "the speaker has eaten breakfast this morning." The implicature is "therefore the speaker does not want cereal now."

### Why this reorganization matters

The line RT draws between explicature and implicature is not the same as Grice's line between "what is said" and "what is implicated." Much of what Grice called "said" — reference assignment, enrichment, disambiguation — RT treats as part of the hearer's inferential work, and therefore as part of the explicature. On the RT picture, there is no sharp boundary between literal and pragmatic content. *All* content requires pragmatic work, even the apparently most literal.

For autistic readers, this is often a clarifying reframe. The distinction you were taught — "this is the literal meaning, and that is the figurative meaning" — is not really the distinction RT describes. On RT, everything requires interpretation. The question is only how much, and of what kind.

### Ad hoc concepts

One of RT's most useful contributions is the idea of **ad hoc concepts**. Word meanings, on the RT account, are frequently adjusted on the fly to fit the context.

Consider: "John is a saint."

The literal meaning of *saint* is a canonized religious figure. But almost nobody interprets this sentence literally. Instead, listeners construct an ad hoc concept SAINT*, which means something like "exceptionally kind and self-sacrificing in the specific way John is." This concept did not exist before the utterance. The listener built it in response to the utterance, using the context (what they know about John, what they know the speaker is emphasizing, what the conversation is about) to shape it.

Ad hoc concept construction happens constantly, not just in obvious metaphors. "Round" in "the earth is round" does not mean the same thing as "round" in "she has round cheeks," and neither means the same as "round" in "we had a round of drinks." Each use involves a contextual adjustment — a narrowing, a loosening, a shift — that produces a context-specific concept.

This is relevant to autism in a subtle but important way. The ad hoc concept construction process relies on the listener's cognitive environment — on what is manifest to them about the referent, the speaker, the situation. If your cognitive environment is systematically different from the speaker's, you may build a different ad hoc concept in response to the same word in the same context. Both concepts may be reasonable. They will not be the same.

## Relevance Theory and Autism

This is the most load-bearing section of this page. RT has been used in two very different ways to think about autistic communication. The older use located the difficulty in autistic minds. The newer use, which this hub follows, uses RT to give the double empathy problem a formal cognitive grounding.

### Happé (1993): the first application

Francesca Happé's 1993 paper, "Communicative competence and theory of mind in autism: A test of relevance theory" (*Cognition* 48:101-119), was the first to apply RT directly to autism. Happé mapped RT's comprehension procedure onto three levels of Theory of Mind (ToM) ability and argued that autistic individuals with different levels of ToM ability should show different kinds of pragmatic difficulty: similes accessible without ToM, metaphors requiring first-order ToM, irony requiring second-order ToM.

Happé's model was enormously influential and framed autism, for decades, as a specific pragmatic deficit rooted in impaired intention-attribution. Her experiments appeared to support the model. The three-level predictions held up in the original study and in subsequent replications.

But Happé's model had two problems that later work uncovered. First, when autistic and non-autistic participants are matched for *language ability* rather than just mental age, many of the differences in figurative language comprehension shrink substantially (Kalandadze et al. 2018 meta-analysis). Second, and more fundamentally, Happé's framing located the communication problem entirely inside the autistic mind. The non-autistic interlocutor was treated as the default — the baseline — and the autistic interlocutor was treated as the deviation. The possibility that communication between autistic and non-autistic speakers is *mutually* harder than communication within either group was not on the table.

### Williams, Wharton, and Jagoe (2021): the reframing

The key paper for this hub is **Williams, Wharton, and Jagoe (2021), "Mutual (Mis)understanding: Reframing Autistic Pragmatic 'Impairments' Using Relevance Theory," published in *Frontiers in Psychology* 12:616664 (PMC8117104)**.

Their argument is that RT, taken seriously, does not actually support Happé's deficit framing. It supports the *opposite* conclusion. Here is why:

RT says that communication works when the speaker and listener have sufficient mutual manifestness — enough overlap in their cognitive environments for the speaker's intended interpretation to be accessible to the listener. It also says that each interlocutor is computing relevance against their *own* cognitive environment.

Mismatches in cognitive environment therefore produce communication breakdowns. RT *predicts* this. The breakdowns are not one-sided. Neither interlocutor has privileged access to the other's cognitive environment. If two people with substantially different cognitive environments try to communicate, both will struggle. Both will produce utterances that are maximally relevant to themselves but not maximally relevant to the other. Both will land on interpretations that are most accessible given their own cognitive environment, which will not always match the speaker's intended interpretation.

This is exactly the structure of the **double empathy problem** as named by Damian Milton (2012, "On the ontological status of autism: the 'double empathy problem'," *Disability & Society* 27(6):883-887). Milton's claim was that when autistic and non-autistic people interact, the misunderstanding is *mutual*, not one-sided — neither group has privileged empathic access to the other. Williams, Wharton, and Jagoe's contribution is that RT gives this claim a formal cognitive mechanism. Milton named the phenomenon. Williams, Wharton, and Jagoe provided the theoretical machinery.

The consequence is that autistic pragmatic "impairments" should be reframed as mismatches in cognitive environment, not as deficits in autistic minds. When an autistic speaker and a non-autistic speaker talk past each other, neither is failing to be relevant to themselves. Both are failing to be relevant to each other — and the failure is symmetrical.

### Attenuated priors and RT

A related strand of research helps explain *why* cognitive environments may systematically differ. **Pellicano and Burr (2012), "When the world becomes 'too real': a Bayesian explanation of autistic perception," *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* 16(10):504-510**, proposed that autistic perception may operate with weaker top-down priors than non-autistic perception. In plain terms: autistic minds may rely more on bottom-up sensory evidence and less on contextual prediction.

In RT terms, this matters because contextual prediction is part of what determines which interpretations are most accessible. If contextual prediction is weaker, the ordering of candidate interpretations is different. The "most accessible first" heuristic operates against a different base. Interpretations that are maximally relevant for a non-autistic listener — who is relying heavily on prior-driven contextual prediction — may be less accessible for an autistic listener, whose ranking is built differently.

Notice what this does *not* say. It does not say that autistic listeners are "worse at relevance." Relevance computation is still happening. It is still optimizing the cognitive-effects-over-effort ratio. What differs is the cognitive environment and the accessibility ordering that the process operates on. Different input, different output — both reasonable given their input.

### What this means practically

For autistic readers, the RT framing has several concrete practical consequences.

**When communication breaks down, you can name it without blame.** The framing is: "the cognitive environments were not mutually manifest enough for the speaker's intended interpretation to be the most accessible one to me." This is a structural description of what happened. It does not locate the failure in either party.

**Asking the speaker to make explicit what would normally be inferred is legitimate.** On the RT picture, you are asking them to *produce the explicature directly* rather than leaving it to your inferential work. You are shifting effort from the listener's inference system to the speaker's explicit statement. This is a trade-off RT recognizes as valid — it just changes which side of the equation the work happens on.

**Producing literal, precise utterances is a legitimate RT strategy.** When you speak in precise, explicit language, you are minimizing the inference load on the listener. You are producing utterances with maximal explicature and minimal implicature. On the RT account, this is a *reasonable communicative strategy*, not a failure to be sophisticated. It is a different trade-off point on the effects/effort curve.

**Autistic-to-autistic communication works when cognitive environments overlap.** Crompton et al. (2020), in "Autistic peer-to-peer information transfer is highly effective" (*Autism* 24(7):1704-1712), showed that autistic-to-autistic communication is as effective as non-autistic-to-non-autistic communication, while cross-neurotype communication is less effective than either. In RT terms: when cognitive environments are more similar, mutual manifestness is higher, and relevance computation aligns. This is exactly what RT predicts. It is the strongest empirical support for the reframing Williams, Wharton, and Jagoe proposed.

## What This Page Is Not

This page is a framing document, not a complete introduction to RT. Several things are deliberately left out:

- **It is not the full framework.** RT has extensive machinery for lexical pragmatics, higher-level explicatures, mutual parallel adjustment, and more. Those are covered in **Category 10: Relevance Theory**.
- **It is not a claim that RT has replaced Grice.** Both frameworks are in active use. Grice describes the social logic of conversation at a useful level of abstraction. RT describes the cognitive mechanism underneath. They are complementary more than competitive.
- **It is not a set of rules you need to memorize.** RT is a description of how minds work. You already run the relevance procedure every waking moment. This page is giving you a vocabulary for talking about it, not a new skill you have to install.
- **It is not a claim that autistic relevance computation is broken.** The whole point of the Williams, Wharton, and Jagoe reframing is that autistic minds are computing relevance correctly — against a cognitive environment that differs from the non-autistic default.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Relevance is a ratio of cognitive effects to processing effort.** Your mind automatically prefers the best deal. This is how cognition works, not just how language works.

2. **Communication is ostensive-inferential.** Speaking to someone is *showing that you are trying to be understood*. The listener uses that showing as evidence for inference — not as a code to decode.

3. **Each interlocutor computes relevance against their own cognitive environment.** Two people with different cognitive environments will land on different interpretations of the same utterance. This is a predictable RT outcome, not a failure.

4. **The double empathy problem has a formal RT grounding.** Williams, Wharton, and Jagoe (2021) showed that RT, taken seriously, predicts symmetrical breakdowns when autistic and non-autistic speakers interact. The problem is in the mismatch, not in either mind.

5. **Explicit, literal speech is a legitimate RT strategy.** It shifts work from the listener's inferential system to the speaker's explicit statement. Maximal explicature with minimal implicature is a reasonable point on the effects/effort curve — not a failure of sophistication.
