# Relevance Theory: The Cognitive Engine of Communication

## Tier 1 Introduction: The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

> Relevance Theory proposes that communication is not a code but an act of showing that you are trying to be understood, and that when communication breaks down between autistic and non-autistic speakers, the problem is a mismatch of cognitive environments, not a deficit in either mind.

Every time someone speaks to you, your mind does something remarkable. It does not decode the words like a cipher and deliver a fixed meaning. It takes the words as evidence, combines them with everything you already know and believe, and computes the interpretation that gives you the best return on your mental effort. You do this automatically. You have been doing it since before you could name what you were doing. Relevance Theory is the framework that describes this process, and once you see what it actually says, several things about your experience as an autistic person start to make a different kind of sense.

The framework comes from Dan Sperber and Deirdre Wilson, who published *Relevance: Communication and Cognition* in 1986 (revised 1995). They were responding to a question that Paul Grice's earlier work on the Cooperative Principle had left open. Grice had shown that listeners routinely go beyond the literal meaning of what speakers say, using the assumption that the speaker is cooperating to work out what they must have really meant. That explained a great deal about how conversation works. But it did not explain *why* listeners bother doing this in the first place. What drives the inferential effort? Sperber and Wilson's answer was cognitive rather than social: human minds are built to seek relevance. Not just in language, but in all processing. Your visual system attends to what matters and ignores what does not. Your auditory system filters noise for signal. Language comprehension is the same mechanism applied to a particular kind of input. When someone speaks to you, you are not checking whether they are cooperating and then inferring meaning from that assessment. You are running the same relevance-seeking process you run on everything, on this input, computing interpretations in order of accessibility and stopping as soon as you find one that is worth the effort.

This is the core of the theory, and it can be stated precisely. Relevance is a ratio: cognitive effects achieved divided by processing effort required. A cognitive effect is any change in what you believe. You learn something new, or something you suspected gets confirmed, or something you thought you knew gets revised. Processing effort is everything your mind does to extract those effects: parsing the words, figuring out who "she" refers to, retrieving relevant context from memory, running inferences. An utterance is more relevant when it delivers many effects for little effort. It is less relevant when it demands a lot of effort for meager effects. Your mind automatically prefers the best ratio it can find, and it pursues interpretations in order of accessibility, stopping at the first one that meets your expectation of relevance. Sperber and Wilson call this the "path of least effort," and they state it as two formal principles. The Cognitive Principle of Relevance says human cognition in general tends to maximize relevance. The Communicative Principle of Relevance says something stronger: every act of ostensive communication (every time someone deliberately addresses you) conveys a presumption of its own optimal relevance. By speaking to you, the speaker has implicitly committed to the claim that the utterance is worth the effort you will spend processing it.

That word "ostensive" matters. It comes from the Latin for "to show." Sperber and Wilson characterize communication not as encoding and decoding but as *ostensive-inferential*: when you communicate, you are showing that you are trying to be understood, and the listener uses that showing as evidence for inference. Every communicative act involves two layered intentions. The informative intention is your intention to make a set of assumptions available to the listener. The communicative intention is your intention to make it evident to both of you that you have the informative intention. The listener recognizing your communicative intention is part of what makes communication work. This is why hints feel different from direct statements: when you hint, you are relying on the listener's recognition that you know that they know you are trying to communicate something. The mutual recognition is load-bearing.

On this picture, communication is relational from the start. What the listener ends up believing after an utterance depends on two things: what the listener already believed (their existing cognitive environment) and how the utterance interacts with that prior content (the inferences the utterance licenses, given the listener's prior beliefs). Two listeners hearing the same sentence in the same room can end up with different interpretations, because they started with different beliefs. This is not a flaw in the theory. It is the mechanism. And this is where the framework connects directly to your experience.

Your **cognitive environment** is the set of facts that are manifest to you, meaning everything you either currently believe or could come to believe if it were brought to your attention. It is the product of your sensory profile, your attention patterns, your background knowledge, your recent experiences, your current surroundings. When two people communicate, the part of their cognitive environments that overlaps (the set of facts manifest to both of them, and mutually recognized as manifest to both) is what Sperber and Wilson call **mutual manifestness**. Communication works best when mutual manifestness is high. The more your cognitive environments overlap, the more a speaker can leave implicit, because the listener can fill it in. The less they overlap, the more has to be made explicit. This is why you can speak in fragments with an old friend and be understood perfectly, while a stranger requires every detail spelled out.

Now apply this to autistic and non-autistic communication. If autistic and non-autistic speakers have substantially different cognitive environments, because of different sensory profiles, different attention patterns, different life histories, different things that get automatically foregrounded or backgrounded, then their mutual manifestness with each other will be structurally limited. Each speaker is computing relevance against their own cognitive environment. An utterance that is maximally relevant for the speaker's cognitive environment is not necessarily relevant for the listener's. The speaker, running on the communicative principle, trusts that the listener will find the intended interpretation. But if the listener's cognitive environment does not contain the context needed to reach that interpretation, the listener lands somewhere else. And the speaker, looking at the listener's response, cannot understand why the listener got it "wrong." This happens in both directions. Neither interlocutor has privileged access to the other's cognitive environment. Both produce utterances optimized for their own cognitive environment. Both land on interpretations most accessible given their own cognitive environment. Both walk away confused or hurt.

This is the double empathy problem given a formal cognitive mechanism. Damian Milton named the phenomenon in 2012, arguing that communication breakdowns between autistic and non-autistic people are mutual, not one-sided. Williams, Wharton, and Jagoe, in a 2021 paper in *Frontiers in Psychology*, showed that Relevance Theory, taken on its own terms, predicts exactly this outcome. Their argument was that the earlier application of RT to autism, by Francesca Happé in 1993, had gotten the framing backwards. Happé had mapped RT's comprehension procedure onto levels of Theory of Mind ability and concluded that autistic people had a specific pragmatic deficit rooted in impaired intention-attribution. Her model was influential for decades. But it had two problems that later work exposed. First, when autistic and non-autistic participants are matched for language ability rather than just mental age, many of the differences in figurative language comprehension shrink substantially (as the Kalandadze and colleagues 2018 meta-analysis showed). Second, and more fundamentally, Happé's framing located the communication problem entirely inside the autistic mind. The non-autistic interlocutor was treated as the default, the baseline, and the autistic interlocutor was treated as the deviation. The possibility that the difficulty was mutual never entered the picture.

Williams, Wharton, and Jagoe argued that RT itself does not support that one-sided framing. RT says communication works when cognitive environments overlap sufficiently. When they do not overlap, both sides struggle. The theory assigns the breakdown symmetrically. The consequence is that autistic pragmatic "impairments" should be reframed as mismatches in cognitive environment, not as deficits in autistic minds. A related strand of research helps explain why cognitive environments differ systematically. Pellicano and Burr proposed in 2012 that autistic perception may operate with weaker top-down priors than non-autistic perception, meaning autistic minds may rely more on bottom-up sensory evidence and less on contextual prediction. In RT terms, this matters because contextual prediction is part of what determines which interpretations are most accessible. If contextual prediction is weighted differently, the ordering of candidate interpretations changes. The "most accessible first" heuristic operates against a different base. Interpretations that are most accessible for a non-autistic listener, who is relying heavily on prior-driven contextual prediction, may be less accessible for an autistic listener, whose ranking is built differently. This does not mean autistic listeners are "worse at relevance." Relevance computation is still happening, still optimizing the effects-over-effort ratio. What differs is the cognitive environment the process operates on. Different input, different output, both reasonable given their starting point.

RT also refines how we understand the gap between what is said and what is meant. Rather than a simple divide between literal meaning and implied meaning, RT proposes two kinds of communicated content. An **explicature** is what the speaker explicitly communicated, but this is not the same as the dictionary definition of the sentence. It includes all the pragmatic work needed to turn the encoded words into a complete thought: figuring out who "she" refers to, whether "bank" means the financial institution or the riverbank, that "I've had breakfast" means this morning rather than at any point in a lifetime, that "I need a drink" means an alcoholic drink specifically. All of that is part of the explicature. An **implicature** is what the speaker communicated beyond the explicature, through further inference: if someone says "I've had breakfast" and you take them to be declining your offer of cereal, the declining is the implicature. The important point is that on the RT account, there is no sharp boundary between literal and pragmatic content. All content requires pragmatic work, even the apparently most literal. Word meanings are frequently adjusted on the fly to fit the context, a process RT calls **ad hoc concept construction**. When someone says "John is a saint," listeners do not access the literal meaning of a canonized religious figure. They construct a context-specific concept, something like "exceptionally kind and self-sacrificing in the specific way John is." This happens constantly, not just in obvious metaphors. And it relies on the listener's cognitive environment. If your cognitive environment is systematically different from the speaker's, you may build a different ad hoc concept in response to the same word in the same context. Both concepts may be reasonable. They will not be the same.

So what does all of this mean practically? It means several things you can use. When communication breaks down, you can name what happened without blame: the cognitive environments were not mutually manifest enough for the speaker's intended interpretation to be the most accessible one to you. That is a structural description, not a personal failing. Asking someone to make explicit what they would normally leave implicit is a legitimate move within RT. You are asking them to produce the explicature directly rather than leaving it to your inferential work, shifting effort from the listener's inference system to the speaker's explicit statement. RT recognizes this as a valid trade-off; it just changes which side of the equation the work happens on. Similarly, when you speak in precise, explicit language, you are minimizing the inference load on the listener by producing utterances with maximal explicature and minimal implicature. On the RT account, this is a reasonable communicative strategy, not a failure of sophistication. It is a different trade-off point on the effects/effort curve. And the finding that autistic-to-autistic communication works as effectively as non-autistic-to-non-autistic communication, while cross-neurotype communication degrades (Crompton and colleagues' diffusion-chain experiments), is exactly what RT predicts. When cognitive environments are more similar, mutual manifestness is higher, relevance computation aligns, and information transfers cleanly.

The evidence base here is strong but still developing. The Williams, Wharton, and Jagoe reframing has theoretical backing from RT's own formal apparatus, and empirical support from the Crompton peer-communication findings and from the broader double empathy literature. Happé's original deficit framing is weakened by the Kalandadze meta-analysis showing that language-ability matching reduces many of the differences her model predicted. The Pellicano and Burr attenuated-priors hypothesis provides a plausible cognitive mechanism for systematic differences in cognitive environments, though it remains an active area of research rather than a settled conclusion. What is settled is the direction of the reframe: RT, taken seriously, predicts mutual breakdowns across different cognitive styles, not one-sided deficits.

This matters because of the story you carry while you learn. RT is one of two frameworks (the other is Grice's Cooperative Principle) that does most of the theoretical work in this hub. They operate at different levels: Grice describes the social logic of conversation, while RT describes the cognitive machinery that makes any such logic possible. They are complementary more than competitive. Understanding RT gives you a vocabulary for something you already do every waking moment: compute relevance. You do not need to install a new skill. You need a way to talk about the skill you already have, so that when it produces a different result from the person across from you, you can see the mismatch for what it is. Not your broken faculty. Not their malice. A gap in mutual manifestness between two minds that process the world differently, both running the same cognitive procedure on different inputs, both arriving at reasonable conclusions that happen not to match.

## Deep Dive

### From Grice to Sperber and Wilson: the theoretical shift

Paul Grice's Cooperative Principle proposed that conversation works because listeners assume speakers are cooperating. When what a speaker says on the surface does not fit the context, the listener works backward from the assumption of cooperation to figure out what the speaker must have meant. Grice called this **implicature**, and he described four **maxims** (Quantity, Quality, Relation, Manner) that speakers are assumed to follow.

Grice's framework was powerful. It explained why "Can you pass the salt?" is understood as a request rather than as a question about motor ability. It explained how sarcasm works: you say the opposite of what is true, but because you are assumed to be cooperating, the listener works out that you must be mocking or disagreeing. But Grice's framework left a question open. It described *what* listeners do when they interpret utterances. It did not explain *why* listeners do this. Why should anyone assume, going in, that speakers are cooperative? What drives the inference in the first place?

Sperber and Wilson's answer, in *Relevance: Communication and Cognition* (1986, revised 1995), was cognitive rather than social. Human minds are built to look for relevance. It is not a social contract that makes listeners cooperate; it is how cognition works at the level of the mind. When your visual system is presented with a scene, it automatically attends to the parts that matter. When your auditory system encounters background noise, it automatically filters for what is potentially important. Minds are relevance-seeking machines. Language processing is a special case of the same mechanism.

The difference between Grice and Sperber and Wilson matters precisely. Grice's maxims are norms that speakers can obey or violate. Violating a maxim is what generates implicatures: you notice the violation and work out what the speaker must have meant. RT's principle of relevance is not a norm. It is not something a speaker can violate. It is an exceptionless generalization about what happens when any mind is presented with an ostensive stimulus. The question is never "did they violate relevance?" It is always "what interpretation satisfies the expectation of relevance here?"

The four maxims collapse into one principle. Quality (be truthful): false information produces misleading cognitive effects, which reduces relevance. Quantity (say enough, not too much): too little yields too few effects; too much imposes too much effort; both reduce the ratio. Relation (be relevant): this *is* the entire theory. Manner (be clear): greater clarity means less processing effort, which raises relevance. Everything Grice's maxims did, the relevance ratio does more economically.

### The relevance equation in detail

RT gives you a formula:

> **Relevance = Cognitive Effects Achieved / Processing Effort Required**

**Cognitive effects** are changes in what you believe. RT identifies three main kinds:

1. **New information added**: you learn something you did not know.
2. **Existing beliefs strengthened**: something you already suspected is now better supported.
3. **Existing beliefs contradicted or revised**: you update what you thought you knew.

Derived conclusions also count. If someone tells you it is raining, and you already know the car windows are open, you derive a new conclusion (the car is getting wet) without the speaker mentioning the car. That derived conclusion is a cognitive effect.

**Processing effort** is everything your mind does to extract those effects: decoding the words, disambiguating, assigning reference (who is "he"? which "that"?), retrieving relevant context from memory, running inferences. The more work required, the higher the effort. The higher the effort, the lower the relevance, unless there are enough cognitive effects to compensate.

**The two principles of relevance:**

- **Cognitive Principle of Relevance**: human cognition tends to be geared toward maximizing relevance. This is a claim about minds in general, not just about language.
- **Communicative Principle of Relevance**: every ostensive act of communication conveys a presumption of its own optimal relevance. When a speaker addresses you, they are implicitly promising that the utterance is worth processing. If it is not worth the effort, they have failed to be optimally relevant, and you are entitled to treat this as a communication breakdown rather than as your own failure to understand.

**Worked example.** You walk into a friend's kitchen. They are staring at the oven and say: "I left it on 400 for too long." Your mind computes interpretations in order of accessibility. Literal decoding: they left something at 400 degrees for longer than some threshold. Reference assignment: "it" must be whatever is in the oven, probably the dinner they invited you to eat. Enrichment: "too long" must mean "long enough that something bad happened." Derived inference: the dinner is burned. Further derivation: dinner may be ruined; plans may need to change; they are probably frustrated. You stop. You have reached an interpretation that yields many cognitive effects for relatively little effort. That is optimal relevance in action. You did not enumerate every possible interpretation and pick the best. You stopped at the first that met your expectation of relevance: the path of least effort. And the speaker implicitly did something too: by saying those words in that context, they committed themselves to the claim that you would find a relevant interpretation without needing everything spelled out.

### Ostensive-inferential communication

Most people think of communication as encoding and decoding: the speaker has a thought, encodes it into words, transmits the words, and the listener decodes them back into the thought. RT rejects this picture as incomplete. Language does encode some content, but that encoded content is never sufficient to determine what the speaker meant. Every utterance requires inferential work by the listener.

RT therefore characterizes communication as **ostensive-inferential**. Every communicative act involves two layered intentions:

- **Informative intention**: the speaker's intention to make a set of assumptions manifest to the listener.
- **Communicative intention**: the speaker's intention to make it mutually manifest to both parties that the speaker has the informative intention.

The second intention is crucial. You are not just trying to plant a thought in your listener's head. You are trying to make it evident to both of you that you are trying to do so. The listener recognizing your communicative intention is part of what makes communication work.

On the RT picture, communication is relational from the start. Every communicative act requires both parties to model each other's minds as coordinating. The listener is not decoding a signal; the listener is using a displayed intention as inferential evidence. The speaker is not transmitting a thought; the speaker is producing a stimulus whose best interpretation, given the context, is what they wanted to communicate.

### Cognitive environment and mutual manifestness

Your **cognitive environment** is the set of facts that are manifest to you: everything you either already believe or could come to believe if it were brought to your attention. This is wider than "everything you know." It includes potential beliefs you could form given the right prompt. You do not currently believe that the chair in front of you has exactly seventeen small scratches on its leg, but if someone pointed it out, you could confirm or deny the claim. That potential belief is part of your cognitive environment even though you have not formed it yet.

**Mutual manifestness** is the overlap between two people's cognitive environments: the set of facts manifest to both of them, and mutually known to be manifest to both. Sperber and Wilson were careful to avoid the stronger notion of "common knowledge," which requires an infinite regress of "I know that you know that I know..." Mutual manifestness is weaker and more tractable.

Communication works best when mutual manifestness is high. The more overlap, the more the speaker can leave implicit. The less overlap, the more must be made explicit.

### Explicature and implicature in RT

RT refines the classical distinction between "what is said" and "what is implied." Rather than a single line between them, RT proposes two kinds of communicated content:

**Explicature** is what the speaker explicitly communicated. This is not the same as the literal, dictionary meaning of the sentence. It is the proposition the utterance encodes *plus* the contextual fleshing-out required to turn that proposition into a complete thought:

- **Reference assignment**: who is "she," which "that," when was "yesterday"?
- **Disambiguation**: is "bank" the financial one or the river one?
- **Saturation**: "I'm ready" becomes "ready for what?"
- **Free enrichment**: "I've had breakfast" means breakfast *this morning*, not at any point in a lifetime.
- **Narrowing/broadening**: "drink" in "I need a drink" narrows to specifically an alcoholic drink.

All of this is part of the explicature. The words point at it; the listener's inferential work completes it.

**Implicature** is what the speaker communicated beyond the explicature, through further inference. If someone says "I've had breakfast" and you take them to be declining your offer of cereal, the declining is the implicature.

The line RT draws between explicature and implicature is not the same as Grice's line between "what is said" and "what is implicated." Much of what Grice called "said" (reference assignment, enrichment, disambiguation) RT treats as part of the hearer's inferential work, and therefore as part of the explicature. On the RT picture, there is no sharp boundary between literal and pragmatic content. All content requires pragmatic work.

### Ad hoc concepts

One of RT's most useful contributions is the idea of **ad hoc concepts**. Word meanings, on the RT account, are frequently adjusted on the fly to fit context.

Consider: "John is a saint." The literal meaning of *saint* is a canonized religious figure. But almost nobody interprets this literally. Instead, listeners construct an ad hoc concept SAINT*, meaning something like "exceptionally kind and self-sacrificing in the specific way John is." This concept did not exist before the utterance. The listener built it in response to the utterance, using context to shape it.

Ad hoc concept construction happens constantly, not just in obvious metaphors. "Round" in "the earth is round" does not mean the same thing as "round" in "she has round cheeks," and neither means the same as "round" in "we had a round of drinks." Each use involves a contextual adjustment (narrowing, loosening, shifting) that produces a context-specific concept.

This is relevant to autism because the ad hoc concept construction process relies on the listener's cognitive environment, on what is manifest to them about the referent, the speaker, and the situation. If your cognitive environment is systematically different from the speaker's, you may build a different ad hoc concept in response to the same word in the same context. Both concepts may be reasonable. They will not be the same.

### Happé (1993): the first application of RT to autism

Francesca Happé's 1993 paper, "Communicative competence and theory of mind in autism: A test of relevance theory" (*Cognition*, 48, 101-119), was the first to apply RT directly to autism. Happé mapped RT's comprehension procedure onto three levels of Theory of Mind (ToM) ability and argued that autistic individuals with different levels of ToM ability should show different kinds of pragmatic difficulty: similes accessible without ToM, metaphors requiring first-order ToM, irony requiring second-order ToM.

Happé's model was enormously influential and framed autism, for decades, as a specific pragmatic deficit rooted in impaired intention-attribution. Her experiments appeared to support the model. The three-level predictions held up in the original study and in subsequent replications.

But two problems emerged. First, when autistic and non-autistic participants are matched for *language ability* rather than just mental age, many of the differences in figurative language comprehension shrink substantially (Kalandadze, Norbury, Naerland, & Naess, 2018 meta-analysis). Second, Happé's framing located the communication problem entirely inside the autistic mind. The non-autistic interlocutor was treated as the default, and the autistic interlocutor was the deviation. The possibility that communication between autistic and non-autistic speakers is mutually harder than communication within either group was not on the table.

### Williams, Wharton, and Jagoe (2021): the reframing

Williams, Wharton, and Jagoe (2021), "Mutual (Mis)understanding: Reframing Autistic Pragmatic 'Impairments' Using Relevance Theory," *Frontiers in Psychology*, 12, article 616664 (PMC8117104), is the key paper for this hub.

Their argument is that RT, taken seriously, does not support Happé's deficit framing. It supports the opposite conclusion. RT says that communication works when the speaker and listener have sufficient mutual manifestness, enough overlap in their cognitive environments for the speaker's intended interpretation to be accessible to the listener. It also says that each interlocutor computes relevance against their own cognitive environment. Mismatches in cognitive environment therefore produce communication breakdowns. RT predicts this. The breakdowns are not one-sided. Neither interlocutor has privileged access to the other's cognitive environment. If two people with substantially different cognitive environments try to communicate, both will struggle. Both will produce utterances that are maximally relevant to themselves but not maximally relevant to the other. Both will land on interpretations that are most accessible given their own cognitive environment, which will not always match the speaker's intended interpretation.

This is the structure of the **double empathy problem** as named by Milton (2012). Milton's claim was that when autistic and non-autistic people interact, the misunderstanding is mutual, not one-sided. Williams, Wharton, and Jagoe's contribution is that RT gives this claim a formal cognitive mechanism. Milton named the phenomenon; Williams, Wharton, and Jagoe provided the theoretical machinery.

The consequence: autistic pragmatic "impairments" should be reframed as mismatches in cognitive environment, not as deficits in autistic minds. When an autistic speaker and a non-autistic speaker talk past each other, neither is failing to be relevant to themselves. Both are failing to be relevant to each other, and the failure is symmetrical.

### Attenuated priors and RT

Pellicano and Burr (2012), "When the world becomes 'too real': a Bayesian explanation of autistic perception," *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 16(10), 504-510, proposed that autistic perception may operate with weaker top-down priors than non-autistic perception. In plain terms: autistic minds may rely more on bottom-up sensory evidence and less on contextual prediction.

In RT terms, this matters because contextual prediction is part of what determines which interpretations are most accessible. If contextual prediction is weighted differently, the ordering of candidate interpretations changes. The "most accessible first" heuristic operates against a different base. Interpretations that are maximally relevant for a non-autistic listener (who is relying heavily on prior-driven contextual prediction) may be less accessible for an autistic listener, whose ranking is built differently.

This does not mean autistic listeners are "worse at relevance." Relevance computation is still happening. It is still optimizing the cognitive-effects-over-effort ratio. What differs is the cognitive environment and the accessibility ordering that the process operates on. Different input, different output, both reasonable given their input.

### Caveats and limits

**RT is not the full framework on this page.** RT has extensive machinery for lexical pragmatics, higher-level explicatures, mutual parallel adjustment, and more. Those are covered in Category 10: Relevance Theory.

**RT has not replaced Grice.** Both frameworks are in active use. Grice describes the social logic of conversation at a useful level of abstraction. RT describes the cognitive mechanism underneath. They are complementary more than competitive.

**RT is not a set of rules to memorize.** It is a description of how minds work. You already run the relevance procedure every waking moment. This page gives you a vocabulary for talking about it, not a new skill to install.

**Autistic relevance computation is not broken.** The whole point of the Williams, Wharton, and Jagoe reframing is that autistic minds compute relevance correctly, against a cognitive environment that differs from the non-autistic default.

**The evidence base is strong but still developing.** The Williams, Wharton, and Jagoe reframing has theoretical backing from RT's formal apparatus and empirical support from the Crompton peer-communication findings and the broader double empathy literature. Happé's deficit framing is weakened by the Kalandadze meta-analysis. The Pellicano and Burr attenuated-priors hypothesis provides a plausible cognitive mechanism for systematic differences in cognitive environments, though it remains an active area of research rather than a settled conclusion.

**The reframe does not mean communication problems are fictitious.** They are real, and they hurt. What changes is the causal explanation: mismatches in cognitive environment, not deficits in autistic minds.
