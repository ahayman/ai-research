# Validation Report: French Language Complete Structural Analysis — Exploration Research

## Executive Summary

The exploration research presents an 11-category taxonomy for a comparative structural analysis of French and English. The taxonomy is comprehensive, well-organized, and accurately describes the major structural dimensions of French. Factual claims are overwhelmingly confirmed by independent verification. The few issues identified are precision/framing concerns rather than outright errors. The taxonomy covers all essential structural levels (phonology, morphology, syntax, semantics, pragmatics, writing system, historical development) plus two valuable synthesis categories (comparative typology, pattern learner strategies). One notable gap — the absence of a dedicated category on French dialectal/sociolinguistic variation — is identified as a research opportunity.

## Claim-by-Claim Verification

### Claim 1: French has nasal vowels, front rounded vowels (/y/, /ø/, /œ/)
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED (10/10)
- **Research says**: French has 13-15 oral vowels and 3-4 nasal vowels, including front rounded vowels /y/, /ø/, /œ/ absent from English
- **Independent finding**: Standard French contrasts up to 13 oral vowels and up to 4 nasal vowels (/ɛ̃/, /œ̃/, /ɔ̃/, /ɑ̃/). The front rounded vowels /y/ (as in "tu"), /ø/ (as in "feu"), /œ/ (as in "sœur") are confirmed as absent from English. Parisian French has merged /ɛ̃/ and /œ̃/, leaving 3 nasal vowels in that variety. The range of 13-15 oral vowels depends on dialect; the "up to 17 vowels including nasals" figure in Category 1 is accurate for conservative dialects.
- **Sources**: [French phonology - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_phonology), [FluentU French Vowels](https://www.fluentu.com/blog/french/french-vowels/)

### Claim 2: French is syllable-timed vs. English stress-timed
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED with nuance (8/10)
- **Research says**: French is syllable-timed where each syllable receives approximately equal duration; English is stress-timed with stressed syllables lengthened and unstressed compressed
- **Independent finding**: French is classified as syllable-timed and English as stress-timed in traditional linguistics. However, empirical phonetics research has been unable to fully support the strict isochrony hypothesis. The contemporary view treats rhythm as a continuum — no language is totally syllable-timed or stress-timed. French and English do sit at opposite ends of this continuum, and the perceptual difference is real, but the characterization should acknowledge this is a relative distinction rather than an absolute categorical one. The research does reference this correctly by citing "approximately equal duration" but the framing could note the continuum view.
- **Sources**: [Isochrony - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isochrony), [PMC - Rhythm, Timing and the Timing of Rhythm](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2790788/)

### Claim 3: French has 50+ verb forms vs. English's 5
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED (9/10)
- **Research says**: French verbs produce "over 50 distinct forms per verb" compared to English's "maximum of 5 forms per verb (go, goes, going, went, gone)"
- **Independent finding**: A French verb conjugates across 4 personal moods (indicative: 8 tenses, subjunctive: 4, conditional: 2, imperative: 2) and 3 impersonal moods (infinitive, participle, gerund). Indicative alone yields 48 forms (8 tenses x 6 persons). Adding subjunctive (24), conditional (12), imperative (6), plus non-finite forms easily exceeds 90 forms if all tenses are counted, or approximately 50-60 for the commonly cited simple + compound forms most relevant to learners. The "over 50" claim is actually conservative. English's 5 forms (base, -s, -ing, past, past participle) is accurate.
- **Precision note**: The exact count depends on whether you include literary tenses (passe simple, subjunctive imperfect/pluperfect) and all compound tenses. The claim is valid regardless of counting method.
- **Sources**: [French conjugation - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_conjugation), [Lawless French - Verb Timeline](https://www.lawlessfrench.com/grammar/verb-timeline/)

### Claim 4: Gender ~80% predictable from noun endings
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED (10/10)
- **Research says**: Noun endings predict gender with roughly 80% accuracy
- **Independent finding**: This traces directly to Lyster (2006), "Predictability in French gender attribution: A corpus analysis" (Journal of French Language Studies, Vol. 16, Issue 1). Analyzing 9,961 nouns from Le Robert Junior Illustré, Lyster found that 81% of feminine nouns and 80% of masculine nouns have endings that systematically predict their gender (defining "reliable predictor" as any ending predicting gender for at least 90% of nouns with that ending). The ~80% figure is well-sourced and precisely correct.
- **Sources**: [Lyster 2006 - Cambridge Core](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/journal-of-french-language-studies/article/abs/predictability-in-french-gender-attribution-a-corpus-analysis/1614CE6C046E4C05A9DBBCD3321327F4), [FrenchTogether - Gender with 80% Accuracy](https://frenchtogether.com/french-nouns-gender/)

### Claim 5: Meaning-changing gender pairs (le/la tour)
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED (10/10)
- **Research says**: Le tour "the tour/trick" vs. la tour "the tower"; le mode "the method/mood" vs. la mode "the fashion"; le critique "the critic" vs. la critique "the criticism/review"
- **Independent finding**: All three examples confirmed. Additional confirmed pairs include: le livre "book" / la livre "pound"; le poste "job/position" / la poste "post office"; le vase "vase" / la vase "mud"; le voile "veil" / la voile "sail"; le manche "handle" / la manche "sleeve". These identical-form, different-gender pairs often derive from different Latin etyma that converged in form through phonological change.
- **Sources**: [Kwiziq - Noun meanings based on gender](https://french.kwiziq.com/revision/grammar/some-common-nouns-change-meaning-whether-theyre-masculine-or-feminine), [LearnFrenchWithAlexa - Words changing meaning with gender](https://learnfrenchwithalexa.com/blog/french-words-which-change-meaning-depending-on-their-gender)

### Claim 6: ~1,700 cognates with English
- **Rating**: UNCERTAIN (6/10)
- **Research says**: "approximately 1,700 cognate pairs" and "roughly 30% of all English vocabulary derives from French"
- **Independent finding**: Multiple sources cite "approximately 1,700 French cognates" with English, but this figure appears to refer specifically to words that are near-identical in form (true cognates / vrais amis), not the total number of etymologically related words. The broader historical picture is that 10,000+ French words entered English after 1066, and approximately 29-30% of modern English vocabulary is of French origin. The 1,700 figure and the 30% figure describe different things: 1,700 refers to cognates recognizable by form similarity, while the 30% figure covers all French-origin words regardless of how much they've diverged. Both figures are individually defensible but their juxtaposition could imply they're related metrics when they measure different things.
- **Recommendation**: Clarify that the ~1,700 figure refers to near-identical true cognates (transparently similar in form and meaning), while the 30% figure covers all French-origin English vocabulary including words that have diverged substantially in form.
- **Sources**: [FluentU - French Cognates](https://www.fluentu.com/blog/french/french-cognates/), [Influence of French on English - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Influence_of_French_on_English)

### Claim 7: Académie française founded 1635
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED (10/10)
- **Research says**: Académie française's prescriptivist regulation since 1635
- **Independent finding**: On 22 February 1635, at Cardinal Richelieu's urging, King Louis XIII granted letters patent formally establishing the Académie française. It was registered at the Parlement de Paris on 10 July 1637. The academy originated from informal literary meetings at Valentin Conrart's house. Its mission was "to labor with all the care and diligence possible, to give exact rules to our language." Founded with 40 members known as "les immortels." Date confirmed exactly.
- **Sources**: [Académie française - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_Fran%C3%A7aise)

### Claim 8: The Jespersen Cycle in French negation
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED (10/10)
- **Research says**: Historical ne → ne...pas → pas alone; the Jespersen Cycle as the framework for understanding French negation evolution
- **Independent finding**: French is a textbook case of Jespersen's Cycle (named by Östen Dahl in 1979). Stage I: Latin "non" before verb. Stage II: Old French "ne" before verb (sufficient alone, as in "Je ne sais"). Stage III: Reinforcement with "pas" (literally "step") producing bipartite ne...pas. Current spoken French is moving toward Stage IV: "pas" alone, with "ne" dropped. Standard French is at Stage II-III. The research correctly identifies all stages and labels it as the Jespersen Cycle.
- **Sources**: [Jespersen's cycle - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jespersen's_cycle), [University of Cambridge - It's 'not' history](https://www.cam.ac.uk/research/news/its-not-history)

### Claim 9: Tu/vous system — English lost "thou"
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED with precision adjustment (9/10)
- **Research says**: English lost "thou" by the 18th century
- **Independent finding**: "Thou" disappeared from Standard English in the first half of the 17th century (not the 18th century). Some regional dialects preserved it longer. The T-V distinction in English began under Norman French influence — the earliest OED entry for "ye" as formal singular is from 1225. By the 16th century, "ye"/"you" had largely merged, and "thou" was fading from standard use. The research's date of "by the 18th century" is slightly late — the 17th century is more accurate for Standard English.
- **Correction needed**: Change "by the 18th century" to "by the mid-17th century" for Standard English, noting that dialectal use persisted longer.
- **Sources**: [Thou - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thou), [T-V distinction - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T%E2%80%93V_distinction)

### Claim 10: French writing system — regular reading, irregular writing direction
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED (10/10)
- **Research says**: French orthography is "nearly perfectly predictable in the reading direction (spelling-to-sound) while remaining highly opaque in the writing direction (sound-to-spelling)"
- **Independent finding**: Confirmed by multiple sources. French orthography is highly predictable in the oral word reading direction but much less predictable in the spelling direction. This asymmetry is a defining characteristic of French orthography. French is "rather consistent (or transparent) in reading but opaque in spelling." The example of /sɛ̃/ being spelled as saint/sein/sain/seing/ceins/ceint illustrates the writing-direction opacity. The "nearly perfectly predictable" characterization for reading is slightly strong — "highly predictable" is more precise, as there are exceptions — but the asymmetry itself is definitively confirmed.
- **Sources**: [French orthography - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_orthography), [PMC - Relationships of French and English Morphophonemic Orthographies](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5094806/)

### Claim 11: Ne dropped ~91% in European French, ~99% in Quebec French
- **Rating**: UNCERTAIN (5/10)
- **Research says**: "ne" dropped approximately 91% in European French and 99% in Quebec French
- **Independent finding**: Research shows the ne particle is in sharp decline, but the specific percentages are harder to pin down to exact figures because they vary enormously by speaker, social class, subject type, and register. Ashby's research shows retention declining from 37% to 18% over time (i.e., dropping 63-82%). Lower-middle-class speakers retain ne in only 9% of cases (91% drop — this may be the source of the "91%" figure). Upper-class speakers retain it 26% of the time. Child-directed speech shows average retention of only 8.49% (i.e., 91.5% drop). The "91%" figure appears plausible as an average or as a specific social class finding but is not a universal rate across all European French speakers — it depends heavily on register, social class, and context. The "99%" Quebec figure is plausible given that Quebec French is known for even more advanced ne-deletion, but I could not independently confirm this precise figure.
- **Recommendation**: Present these as approximate rates that vary by register and social class, citing Ashby's research, rather than as fixed percentages. Something like "ne is dropped in the vast majority of informal speech (estimates range from 75-95% in European French depending on social context, and even higher in Quebec French)."
- **Sources**: [Lawless French - Informal Negation](https://www.lawlessfrench.com/grammar/informal-negation/), [Cairn.info - Variable Use of French Negation Particle ne](https://shs.cairn.info/journal-langage-et-societe-2011-4-page-109?lang=en)

### Claim 12: -er verbs comprise ~90% of all French verbs
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED (10/10)
- **Research says**: Group 1 (-er) contains ~90% of all verbs and is fully regular
- **Independent finding**: Multiple sources confirm -er verbs make up about 90% of all French verbs (approximately 1,000+ individual verbs). They are the "first group" (premier groupe) and are fully regular in conjugation. This is one of the most consistently cited statistics in French grammar pedagogy.
- **Sources**: [Lawless French - Regular -er Verbs](https://www.lawlessfrench.com/grammar/regular-er-verbs/), [Lingvist - -er Verbs in French](https://lingvist.com/course/learn-french-online/resources/er-verbs-french/)

### Claim 13: English has 8 inflectional morphemes
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED (10/10)
- **Research says**: English has "eight surviving inflectional morphemes"
- **Independent finding**: Standard linguistic analysis confirms exactly 8 inflectional morphemes in English: plural -s, possessive -'s, comparative -er, superlative -est, third-person singular present -s, past tense -ed, past participle -en/-ed, progressive -ing. English's inflectional morphology is described as "quite poor" compared to other languages.
- **Sources**: [IvyPanda - 8 Inflectional Morphemes](https://ivypanda.com/essays/english-inflectional-morphemes/), [ResearchGate - The Eight English Inflectional Morphemes](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/The-Eight-English-Inflectional-Morphemes_tbl1_260797681)

### Claim 14: ~17 verbs take être in compound tenses
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED (10/10)
- **Research says**: "~17 verbs of motion and state change take être"
- **Independent finding**: 17 intransitive verbs of motion and state change take être as their auxiliary in compound tenses. These are commonly memorized via the mnemonic DR & MRS VANDERTRAMPP (devenir, revenir, monter, rester, sortir, venir, aller, naître, descendre, entrer, rentrer, tomber, retourner, arriver, mourir, partir, passer). Some verbs switch auxiliary when used transitively (e.g., "monter les valises" = avoir vs. "monter au sommet" = être). All pronominal/reflexive verbs also take être.
- **Sources**: [Lawless French - Auxiliary Verbs](https://www.lawlessfrench.com/grammar/auxiliary-verbs/), [Lingoculture - French Être Verbs](https://lingoculture.com/blog/grammar/french-etre-verbs/)

### Claim 15: Serments de Strasbourg (842) as earliest French text
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED (10/10)
- **Research says**: The Serments de Strasbourg (842) is the earliest French text
- **Independent finding**: The Oaths of Strasbourg, sworn on 14 February 842, are confirmed as "the oldest extant document in France that was written deliberately and consistently in a form of Romance language" and "the earliest written evidence of a Romance language to survive." Louis the German swore his oath in an early form of Old French. The language is described as "nearly undifferentiated conservative Pre-French." The surviving manuscript was copied around the year 1000.
- **Sources**: [Oaths of Strasbourg - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oaths_of_Strasbourg)

### Claim 16: ~200 Gaulish words survive in French
- **Rating**: UNCERTAIN (6/10)
- **Research says**: "~200 Gaulish words survive: char, chemin, bouleau"
- **Independent finding**: Estimates vary significantly among scholars: Henriette Walter puts the number at about 70; other estimates range from 150 to 400. The range 200-400 is commonly cited for "French or other Gallo-Romance dialects, including Provençal" (i.e., not exclusively Modern Standard French). For Modern French specifically, 70-200 is a more defensible range. The "~200" figure falls within the range but should be presented as an estimate with acknowledged scholarly disagreement rather than as a precise count.
- **Recommendation**: Present as "estimates range from 70 to 400 depending on the scholar and how broadly 'French' is defined" rather than a single number.
- **Sources**: [List of French words of Gaulish origin - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_French_words_of_Gaulish_origin)

### Claim 17: ~700 Frankish/Germanic words in French
- **Rating**: UNCERTAIN (5/10)
- **Research says**: "~700 words: guerre, blanc, riche, jardin"
- **Independent finding**: Estimates vary dramatically from "just 500 words (≈1%)" to "10% of modern French words" to "more than 400 words of Frankish origin in common use." The figure "approximately 10% of modern French words derive from Frankish" is sometimes cited but other sources give much lower figures. The "~700" figure is not independently confirmed by any specific source I found. It falls within the plausible range but lacks a clear academic citation.
- **Recommendation**: Present as "estimates range from 400+ commonly used words to potentially several thousand if all Germanic-origin vocabulary is counted" and cite the scholarly disagreement.
- **Sources**: [List of French words of Germanic origin - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_French_words_of_Germanic_origin), [Frankish language - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frankish_language)

### Claim 18: Ordonnance de Villers-Cotterêts (1539)
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED (10/10)
- **Research says**: 1539 ordonnance mandating French in legal documents
- **Independent finding**: The Ordinance of Villers-Cotterêts was signed on 10 August 1539 by Francis I. Articles 110 and 111 mandated the use of French (replacing Latin) in all legal acts, notarized contracts, and official legislation. It is the oldest normative text still in force in France, with Articles 110 and 111 never repealed.
- **Sources**: [Ordinance of Villers-Cotterêts - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinance_of_Villers-Cotter%C3%AAts)

### Claim 19: Toubon Law (1994)
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED (10/10)
- **Research says**: The Toubon Law (1994) mandates French in advertising and public life
- **Independent finding**: Law 94-665 of 4 August 1994, named after Jacques Toubon (then Minister of Culture), mandates French in official government publications, advertisements, workplaces, commercial contracts, and other contexts. Enacted in response to increasing English usage in French public life. Allows exceptions for foreign trademarks, technical terms without French equivalents, and established anglicisms.
- **Sources**: [Toubon Law - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toubon_Law)

### Claim 20: Verlan examples (femme → meuf, arabe → beur)
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED (10/10)
- **Research says**: Verlan as productive syllable-inversion morphology with examples femme → meuf, arabe → beur, louche → chelou
- **Independent finding**: All examples confirmed. Verlan is a productive argot featuring syllable inversion, common in youth language. "Meuf" from "femme" is confirmed; "beur" from "arabe" is confirmed (widespread in the second half of the 20th century, referring to people of North African descent in France). Verlan itself is verlan for "l'envers" (the reverse). The process is productive and rule-governed, not random.
- **Sources**: [Verlan - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Verlan)

### Claim 21: BAGS adjective placement rule
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED (10/10)
- **Research says**: BAGS (Beauty, Age, Goodness, Size) adjectives precede the noun; classifying adjectives follow
- **Independent finding**: BAGS is a well-established pedagogical framework confirmed by multiple sources. Beauty (beau, joli), Age (jeune, vieux, nouveau), Goodness (bon, mauvais), Size (grand, petit, gros, long) precede the noun. Most other adjectives (nationality, color, shape, religion, technical) follow the noun. Meaning-changing adjectives (ancien, propre, pauvre, cher, certain, seul, dernier, prochain) shift meaning with position. This framework accurately describes the pattern.
- **Sources**: [Preply - French BAGS Adjectives](https://preply.com/en/blog/french-bags-adjectives-complete-guide/), [CliffsNotes - Adjective Placement](https://www.cliffsnotes.com/study-guides/french/french-i/french-i-adjectives/adjective-placement-within-sentences)

### Claim 22: 10,000+ French words entered English after Norman Conquest
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED (10/10)
- **Research says**: The Norman Conquest produced the massive French vocabulary layer in English
- **Independent finding**: "More than 10,000 French words entered the English language after the invasion and these new inclusions account for more than a third of the English vocabulary." About three-fourths of the 10,000+ borrowed words are still used today. An average English speaker knows around 10,000-15,000 French-origin words even before studying French.
- **Sources**: [Influence of French on English - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Influence_of_French_on_English), [Merriam-Webster - Norman Conquest New English Words](https://www.merriam-webster.com/wordplay/norman-conquest-new-english-words)

### Claim 23: Connaître vs. savoir distinction
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED (10/10)
- **Research says**: French makes distinctions English collapses — connaître vs. savoir for "to know"
- **Independent finding**: Confirmed. Savoir = knowing facts, knowing how to do something (followed by clause or infinitive). Connaître = being familiar/acquainted with people, places, things (followed by noun). This distinction exists in many European languages (German kennen/wissen, Spanish conocer/saber) but not in English, which collapses both into "know."
- **Sources**: [Lawless French - Savoir vs Connaître](https://www.lawlessfrench.com/grammar/savoir-vs-connaitre/)

### Claim 24: French partitive article has no English equivalent
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED (10/10)
- **Research says**: French requires "Je bois du café" where English says "I drink coffee" with no article
- **Independent finding**: The French partitive articles (du, de la, de l', des) are required for unspecified quantities of uncountable nouns. English has no equivalent article — it either uses "some/any" or no article at all. The partitive-negation interaction (du café → pas de café) is also confirmed as a syntax-morphology interaction absent from English.
- **Sources**: [Lawless French - Partitive Article](https://www.lawlessfrench.com/grammar/partitive-article/)

### Claim 25: French relative clauses use function-based pronoun system
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED (10/10)
- **Research says**: qui (subject), que (direct object), dont (de + noun), où (place/time), lequel (after prepositions)
- **Independent finding**: All five pronouns confirmed with the stated grammatical functions. The system is function-based — speakers must identify the grammatical role of the relativized element to select the correct pronoun. This contrasts with English where "that" can serve multiple functions and pronoun omission is common.
- **Sources**: [Lawless French - Relative Pronouns](https://www.lawlessfrench.com/grammar/relative-pronouns/)

### Claim 26: 1990 spelling reforms (rectifications orthographiques)
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED (10/10)
- **Research says**: The 1990 reforms included circumflex removal, compound word simplification, and were controversial
- **Independent finding**: Published 6 December 1990, approved by Académie française. Circumflex removed on i and u when not needed for disambiguation. Compound word simplification. Affected approximately 2,000 terms. Adoption voluntary. Provoked major controversy when schools began implementing them in 2016, with the #JeSuisCirconflexe hashtag trending. Both old and new spellings remain acceptable.
- **Sources**: [Reforms of French orthography - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reforms_of_French_orthography)

### Claim 27: French avoir expressions vs. English "to be" for bodily states
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED (10/10)
- **Research says**: French uses "avoir" (to have) for states that English encodes with "to be" — avoir faim/soif/peur/raison/tort
- **Independent finding**: Confirmed. "J'ai faim" (I have hunger = I am hungry), "j'ai soif" (I have thirst = I am thirsty), "j'ai peur" (I have fear = I am afraid), "j'ai froid/chaud" (I have cold/hot = I am cold/hot), "j'ai raison/tort" (I have right/wrong = I am right/wrong). This represents a fundamental conceptual difference in how the two languages categorize bodily and emotional states.
- **Sources**: [Kwiziq - Avoir faim, avoir soif](https://french.kwiziq.com/revision/grammar/avoir-faim-soif-to-be-hungry-thirsty)

### Claim 28: English has ~11 monophthongs and 8 diphthongs
- **Rating**: INVALID — needs correction (3/10)
- **Research says**: English has "~11 monophthongs and 8 diphthongs"
- **Independent finding**: English has 12 monophthongs (not 11) and 8 diphthongs according to most analyses of Received Pronunciation / General American. The count varies by dialect (RP has more distinctions than GenAm in some analyses), but 12 monophthongs is the standard figure. The diphthong count of 8 is correct. This is a minor numerical error but should be corrected.
- **Correction needed**: Change "~11 monophthongs" to "~12 monophthongs" (or use "11-12" to acknowledge dialectal variation).
- **Sources**: [GRIN - The English and French vowels](https://www.grin.com/document/43124)

### Claim 29: French vigesimal number system (70, 80, 90)
- **Rating**: N/A — not explicitly claimed but MISSING
- **Research says**: The research does not include the French number system
- **Independent finding**: The French vigesimal (base-20) counting system for numbers 70-99 (soixante-dix, quatre-vingts, quatre-vingt-dix) is one of the most structurally distinctive features of French for English speakers, and one that differs even from other French varieties (Belgian septante/nonante, Swiss huitante). This is a notable structural feature with historical Celtic/Gaulish substrate connections. Its absence from the taxonomy is a gap (see Missing Categories below).
- **Sources**: [Vigesimal - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vigesimal)

### Claim 30: French register system has six levels
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED (9/10)
- **Research says**: Six register levels: soutenu (literary), formel, courant (standard), familier (informal), populaire (colloquial), vulgaire
- **Independent finding**: This six-level system is commonly cited in French linguistics pedagogy. The exact number and labels vary somewhat across sources (some use five levels, some use different labels), but the six-level framework with these specific names is a standard pedagogical model and accurately represents the gradient. The key claim — that French has a more finely differentiated register system than English — is well-supported.
- **Sources**: [Lawless French - Register](https://www.lawlessfrench.com/linguistics/register/)

### Claim 31: English lost "thou" — dated "by the 18th century"
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED with date correction needed (7/10)
- **Research says**: English lost "thou" by the 18th century
- **Independent finding**: "Thou" disappeared from Standard English in the first half of the 17th century, not the 18th century. Regional dialects preserved it longer (some Yorkshire dialects into the 20th century), but for standard English the transition was effectively complete by 1650-1700. The research's dating is about a century late.
- **Correction needed**: The Category 7 text says "losing 'thou' by the 18th century" — this should be "by the mid-17th century" for Standard English.
- **Sources**: [Thou - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thou)

### Claim 32: French prosodic phonology of discourse is under-researched
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED (8/10)
- **Research says**: Listed as a research gap — prosodic structuring of extended French discourse has less accessible documentation
- **Independent finding**: Cross-linguistic prosodic comparison between French and English is described as "very challenging" in the literature. Existing notation systems "are not yet complete." Research on how French speakers group words into Accentual Phrases and realize phrase-final rises is active but acknowledged as less developed than English discourse prosody research. This is a legitimate research gap.

## Taxonomy Completeness Assessment

### Missing Categories

**1. French Dialectal and Sociolinguistic Variation (RECOMMENDED ADDITION)**

The research treats French as a monolithic system (primarily Metropolitan/Parisian French) but does not address:
- Quebec French: distinct phonology (affrication of /t/ and /d/ before high vowels, broader diphthongization, higher ne-deletion rates, distinct vocabulary)
- Belgian/Swiss French: different number systems (septante, huitante, nonante), distinct vocabulary
- African French: simplified morphology in some varieties, influence of substrate languages
- Creoles: Haitian Creole, Mauritian Creole as extreme products of French structural simplification
- The sociolinguistic variation within Metropolitan French: banlieue French, regional accents, the spoken/written register gap as a sociolinguistic phenomenon

This matters for a comparative analysis because it parallels English's own dialectal variation (British/American/Australian/Indian English) and because the structural simplifications in French varieties (e.g., reduced gender marking in some African varieties, reduced verb morphology in Creoles) illuminate which parts of French structure are most susceptible to change.

**2. The French Number System (minor gap)**

The vigesimal counting system (soixante-dix, quatre-vingts, quatre-vingt-dix) is not mentioned anywhere in the taxonomy. This is one of the most immediately striking structural differences for English speakers and has historical substrate connections (Celtic/Gaulish influence). It could fit within Category 6 (Semantics) or Category 8 (Historical Development), or could be mentioned in Category 11 (Pattern Learners) as a notable structural feature.

**3. French Phonotactics and Syllable Structure (minor — partially covered)**

Category 1 mentions the preference for open syllables (CV) and resyllabification, but a more explicit treatment of French phonotactic constraints compared to English (which allows CCCVCCCC as in "strengths") could be expanded. This is partially covered but could be more systematic.

## Research Opportunities

### High Priority

1. **French Dialectal Variation as Structural Laboratory**: A category on how French varies across its global varieties would not only fill a gap but would serve the comparative mission — showing which French structural features are stable across varieties and which are variable reveals what is core to "French" vs. what is historically contingent. Quebec French's advanced Jespersen Cycle (near-complete ne-deletion) and African French's simplified morphology provide natural experiments in structural change.

2. **The Vigesimal Number System**: Should be integrated into the existing taxonomy (most naturally into Category 8 Historical Development or Category 11 Pattern Learners) — it is too structurally distinctive and too immediately noticeable for learners to omit.

3. **Neurolinguistic Processing of French Gender Agreement**: The research correctly identifies this as a gap. How L2 learners' brains process gender agreement in real time vs. native speakers is an active and productive research area with implications for learning strategy.

### Medium Priority

4. **French Construction Grammar**: How French grammatical constructions carry meaning beyond their component parts — the Goldberg-style constructional analysis that has been productive for English is less developed for French in accessible sources.

5. **Quantified WALS Comparison**: The research correctly notes that no single source systematically compares all WALS features for French and English. Compiling this from individual WALS pages would be valuable for Category 10.

6. **French Information Structure**: How French uses left dislocation, cleft constructions (c'est...qui/que), and prosodic focus to manage information flow — this is partially covered in Category 3 but could be expanded as a distinct sub-topic, especially since it represents one of the areas where French's slightly freer word order (enabled by its richer morphology) contrasts most visibly with English's more rigid information structure.

### Lower Priority

7. **French-English Code-Switching Patterns**: In bilingual communities (especially Montreal, parts of West Africa), the structural patterns of French-English code-switching reveal which structural boundaries between the two languages are most permeable — a fascinating lens on the comparative analysis.

8. **French Computational and Corpus Linguistics**: How NLP systems handle French structure differently from English (gender agreement resolution, compound tense parsing, liaison prediction) — relevant for technically-minded learners.
