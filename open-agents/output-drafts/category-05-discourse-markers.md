# Category 5: Discourse Markers -- Social Punctuation

## The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

---

## How to Read This Document

"Well," "so," "like," "you know," "I mean," "actually," "basically," "right," "OK," "anyway," "oh," "just."

If you read that list and thought "those are filler words that don't mean anything," this category is going to change how you hear conversation. These words are not filler. They are **the operating system of conversational flow** -- tiny signals that tell the listener how to interpret what's coming, where the conversation is going, and what the speaker's attitude is. Strip them out and you lose the pragmatic layer that holds conversation together.

For autistic learners who may have been filtering these words out as "noise," this category reveals them as some of the most information-dense elements in English.

---

## 1. What Discourse Markers Are

### 1.1 Definition

**Discourse markers** are small words and phrases that serve **pragmatic** rather than **semantic** functions. They organize discourse, manage turn-taking, signal attitudes, hedge, and maintain social relationships. They operate at the level of **conversation structure**, not sentence meaning.

The critical test: **removing a discourse marker doesn't change what the sentence literally says, but it DOES change what it pragmatically communicates.**

| With Marker | Without Marker | What Changed |
|-------------|---------------|-------------|
| "**Well**, I think we should go." | "I think we should go." | Lost the signal that this is a reluctant or considered response |
| "**So**, what happened next?" | "What happened next?" | Lost the signal that we're returning to the main narrative |
| "**Actually**, it's on Tuesday." | "It's on Tuesday." | Lost the signal that this corrects a prior assumption |
| "I **just** wanted to check..." | "I wanted to check..." | Lost the minimizing/hedge that softens the imposition |
| "That was, **like**, the best thing." | "That was the best thing." | Lost the focus/emphasis marker |

### 1.2 Not Filler -- Processing Instructions

The Relevance Theory linguist Diane Blakemore proposed the most useful way to understand discourse markers: they are **procedural**, not **conceptual**. They don't encode concepts (like "cat" or "red"). They encode **instructions for how to process the utterance**.

- "But" doesn't *mean* "contrast" -- it **instructs the listener to look for contrast** between what came before and what comes after.
- "So" doesn't *mean* "therefore" -- it **instructs the listener to treat what follows as a conclusion** drawn from what preceded.
- "Well" doesn't *mean* "hesitation" -- it **instructs the listener to expect a non-straightforward response**.

This reframing is powerful for autistic learners: discourse markers are not vague emotional coloring. They are **specific processing instructions** that tell you how to interpret the next piece of speech.

### 1.3 Why This Matters for Autistic Learners

Research shows a specific pattern in autistic discourse marker use:

**The "um" vs. "uh" distinction** (Gorman et al., 2016; Lake et al., 2011): Autistic speakers use "uh" at normal rates but use "um" significantly less. This maps onto a crucial functional difference:
- "Uh" is **speaker-oriented** -- it signals the speaker is having a minor processing delay (self-directed)
- "Um" is **listener-oriented** -- it signals to the listener that a major delay is coming ("I'm still here, please wait") (other-directed)

The selective reduction of "um" -- the listener-oriented marker -- is consistent with the broader pattern: discourse markers that serve **social, listener-management** functions are reduced, while those that serve **cognitive, speaker-management** functions are preserved.

**The practical cost of filtering markers out**: If you've been treating "well," "so," "actually," and "you know" as meaningless filler, you've been **systematically discarding the pragmatic metadata of every conversation**. It's like reading a book with all the punctuation removed -- the words are the same, but the structure that tells you how to read them is gone.

## 2. The Major Markers: A Reference Guide

### 2.1 "WELL" -- The Dispreferred Response Signal

**The single most important discourse marker for autistic learners to understand.**

"Well" at the start of a response is one of the most information-dense signals in English. It almost always means: **the answer coming is NOT simple, straightforward, or what you were hoping for.**

| Function | Example | What It Signals |
|----------|---------|----------------|
| **Dispreferred response** | "Can you come Saturday?" → "Well... I have some things going on." | A "no" is coming, softened |
| **Buying time** | "How does this work?" → "Well, there are a few ways to explain it." | I'm thinking about how to answer |
| **Softening disagreement** | "Well, I see your point, but..." | I'm about to disagree, gently |
| **Self-correction** | "I found a house. Well, an apartment actually." | I just realized I misspoke |
| **Insufficiency** | "Is it safe?" → "Well, it depends what you mean by safe." | My answer will only be partial |
| **Topic introduction** | "Well, here's what happened..." | I'm setting up a narrative |

**The detection rule**: When you hear "well" at the start of someone's response, prepare for complexity -- a refusal, a qualification, a disagreement, or a correction. If the answer were simple and positive, they wouldn't need "well."

**What happens without it**: "Can you come Saturday?" → "I have some things going on" sounds like a flat rejection. With "well," the same content is framed as a reluctant, softened decline. The listener loses the advance warning.

### 2.2 "SO" -- The Navigation Marker

"So" tells the listener where they are in the conversation's structure.

| Function | Example | What It Signals |
|----------|---------|----------------|
| **New topic** | "So, I wanted to ask you about the project." | Transitioning from pleasantries to business |
| **Return to topic** | "So anyway, where were we?" | Getting back to the main thread after a digression |
| **Conclusion** | "So, what you're saying is..." | Drawing an inference from what was just said |
| **Pre-closing** | "So, I should probably get going." | Signaling the conversation is wrapping up |
| **Impatience** | "So?" / "So what happened?" | I'm waiting for the point |
| **Institutional opener** | "So, let's get started." | Officially beginning a meeting/class/appointment |

**The rule**: "So" at the start of an utterance means a **structural transition** is happening. Pay attention to what follows -- it's usually either the main point, a conclusion, or a conversation-phase shift.

### 2.3 "LIKE" -- The Most Misunderstood Marker

"Like" is heavily stigmatized as "Valley Girl" speech and dismissed as meaningless. It's neither. Research traces its discourse marker use to at least the 1700s, and it serves four distinct functions:

| Function | Example | What It Signals |
|----------|---------|----------------|
| **Approximation** | "It was like three o'clock." | The value is approximate, not exact |
| **Quotative** | "She was like, 'No way!'" | Introducing reported speech/thought (not necessarily exact words) |
| **Focus/emphasis** | "It was, like, the BEST meal." | Highlighting what follows as noteworthy |
| **Softening** | "I'm, like, not sure about this." | Hedging the assertion |

**Research note** (Jones et al., 2022): Autistic children use "like" at rates comparable to non-autistic peers -- one of the few discourse markers that appears to be fully preserved. This may be because "like" is so frequent in peer speech that it's acquired through sheer exposure.

### 2.4 "YOU KNOW" -- The Connection Check

| Function | Example | What It Signals |
|----------|---------|----------------|
| **Shared knowledge appeal** | "It's hard, you know?" | "I'm assuming you understand this from experience" |
| **Rapport-seeking** | "You know how it is..." | Seeking confirmation of shared perspective |
| **Hedging** | "It was, you know, not great." | Softening a negative assessment |
| **Word search** | "It was the, you know, the thing on the wall..." | Placeholder while searching for a word |

**Critical**: "You know" in conversation is NOT literally asking "Do you know?" It's a listener engagement check -- the speaker wants a backchannel ("yeah," "mm-hmm") to confirm the listener is following. Not responding to "you know?" creates an uncomfortable gap.

### 2.5 "I MEAN" -- The Self-Repair Marker

| Function | Example | What It Signals |
|----------|---------|----------------|
| **Self-correction** | "It's on Tuesday -- I mean Wednesday." | Fixing a factual error |
| **Reformulation** | "It was bad. I mean, not *terrible*, but..." | Adjusting the strength of a prior statement |
| **Intensification** | "It was good. I mean, it was *really* good." | Making a prior statement stronger |
| **Backing down** | "That's unacceptable. I mean, it's frustrating." | Weakening a prior statement that was too strong |

**The direction rule**: "I mean" + **stronger** statement = intensification. "I mean" + **weaker** statement = walking it back. The direction of the adjustment tells you what the speaker really thinks.

### 2.6 "ACTUALLY" -- The Counter-Expectation Flag

| Function | Example | What It Signals |
|----------|---------|----------------|
| **Polite correction** | "Actually, the meeting is on Tuesday, not Wednesday." | Correcting without confrontation |
| **Counter-expectation** | "I actually enjoyed it." | This goes against what you'd expect |
| **Self-correction** | "I think it's -- actually, no, it's on Friday." | Correcting myself mid-stream |
| **Emphasis** | "I actually finished the whole thing." | Emphasizing a surprising accomplishment |

**Position matters**: "Actually" at the **start** of a sentence is the strongest correction. In the **middle**, it's milder. At the **end**, it's retrospective ("That was pretty good, actually.").

**Cultural note**: "Well, actually..." (the compound marker) has become associated with condescending correction -- "well-actually-ing" someone. The combination of "well" (dispreferred signal) + "actually" (counter-expectation) creates a powerful "you're wrong and I'm about to explain why" signal.

### 2.7 "BASICALLY" -- The Simplification Signal

| Function | Example | What It Signals |
|----------|---------|----------------|
| **Simplification** | "Basically, we need to start over." | I'm boiling this down to the essence |
| **Authority assertion** | "Basically, what happened was..." | I'm taking control of the narrative |
| **Hedging** | "It's basically done." | Almost but not quite |
| **Condescension risk** | "Basically, the idea is that..." | Can sound like you're dumbing it down for the listener |

### 2.8 "RIGHT" / "OK" -- The Structural Markers

**"Right":**
- As acknowledgment: "Right." (= I understand that point)
- As confirmation-seeking: "...right?" at end of sentence (= Confirm you agree)
- As challenge: "Oh, *right*." with sarcastic tone (= I don't believe you)
- As transition: "Right, let's move on." (= Topic closed, new topic opening)

**"OK":**
- As acceptance: "OK." (= I agree/understand)
- As transition: "OK, so..." (= Moving to next item)
- As reluctant compliance: "OK..." (drawn out, with falling tone) (= I'll do it but I'm not happy)
- As pre-closing: "OK, well..." (= I'm wrapping up)

### 2.9 "ANYWAY" -- The Return/Dismissal Marker

| Function | Example | What It Signals |
|----------|---------|----------------|
| **Return to main point** | "Anyway, as I was saying..." | The digression is over; back to the thread |
| **Topic dismissal** | "Anyway, it doesn't matter." | Closing off the current topic |
| **Pre-closing** | "Anyway, I should get going." | Signaling conversation end |
| **Impatience** | "Anyway..." (interrupting) | I've heard enough; moving on |

### 2.10 "OH" -- The Change-of-State Marker

Heritage (1984) showed that "oh" marks a change in the speaker's knowledge or awareness state:

| Function | Example | What It Signals |
|----------|---------|----------------|
| **Receiving news** | "Oh, I didn't know that!" | I just learned something new |
| **Realization** | "Oh wait, I left my keys!" | I just realized something |
| **Surprise** | "Oh!" (with raised pitch) | That was unexpected |
| **Self-correction** | "Oh, I mean the other one." | I just caught my own error |
| **Noticing** | "Oh, there you are." | I just became aware of something in the environment |

**Note**: Discourse-marker "oh" (unstressed, brief, transition-positioned) is different from exclamation "Oh!" (stressed, elongated, standalone). The discourse marker signals a cognitive state change; the exclamation expresses an emotion.

### 2.11 "JUST" -- The Minimizer

"Just" is the most pervasive hedging device in modern English, especially in email:

| Function | Example | What It Signals |
|----------|---------|----------------|
| **Minimizer** | "I just wanted to check..." | This is small; I'm barely imposing |
| **Restrictive** | "It's just a scratch." | Only this, nothing more |
| **Emphatic** | "Just do it!" | No hedging -- strong directive |
| **Temporal** | "I just got here." | This happened very recently |

**The email controversy**: In workplace email, "just" is massively overused as a hedge ("Just wanted to follow up," "Just checking in," "Just a quick question"). Some argue it undermines the sender's authority by unnecessary self-minimization. Others argue it's essential negative politeness. The reality: it's a calibration tool, and its appropriateness depends on the power dynamic (see Category 22).

## 3. Compound Markers: Combined Signals

Discourse markers frequently combine, and the combination carries more nuanced meaning than either marker alone:

| Compound | Meaning | Example |
|----------|---------|---------|
| **"OK so"** | Transition + conclusion | "OK so here's what we'll do..." |
| **"Well actually"** | Hesitation + correction | "Well actually, that's not quite right." |
| **"But like"** | Contrast + hedge | "But like, is that really fair?" |
| **"So anyway"** | Conclusion + return to topic | "So anyway, back to the main point..." |
| **"Yeah no"** | Apparent agreement → actual disagreement | "Yeah no, I don't think that'll work." |
| **"No yeah"** | Apparent disagreement → actual agreement | "No yeah, you're totally right." |
| **"I mean like"** | Repair + hedge | "I mean like, it's not a big deal, but..." |

**The "yeah no" / "no yeah" rule**: The **last word is the real answer**. "Yeah no" = no. "No yeah" = yes. "Yeah no yeah" = yes (emphatic). These compounds process through disagreement to land on their real position.

## 4. Discourse Markers as Conversation Architecture

Markers map directly onto the structural phases of conversation (Category 4):

| Conversation Phase | Markers | Function |
|-------------------|---------|----------|
| **Opening** | "So..." / "Well..." / "OK..." | Beginning a new interaction or topic |
| **Turn-holding** | "Um" / "Uh" / "You know..." | Signaling you're not done |
| **Topic shift** | "Anyway" / "Oh, by the way" / "Speaking of which" | Moving to a new subject |
| **Pre-closing** | "Well..." / "So..." / "Anyway..." / "Right..." | Signaling conversation end |
| **Closing** | "OK then" / "Alright" / "Well then" | Final transition out |

**This means discourse markers give you a real-time map of where the conversation IS.** If someone says "well..." with falling intonation after a natural pause, they're signaling a pre-close. If they say "so..." at the start, they're opening a new segment. If they say "anyway..." they're closing a topic. You can navigate by the markers.

## 5. Discourse Markers as Attitude Signals

Some markers carry emotional/attitudinal information based on how they're delivered:

| Marker | Delivery | Attitude |
|--------|----------|----------|
| "Oh" | Rising pitch, quick | Surprise |
| "Oh" | Falling pitch, slow | Disappointment or realization |
| "Right" | Quick, flat | Acknowledgment |
| "Right" | Slow, drawn out, rising | Sarcasm ("Oh, *riiight*") |
| "Sure" | Quick, rising | Genuine agreement |
| "Sure" | Flat, brief | Reluctant compliance |
| "Fine" | Quick, neutral | Genuine acceptance |
| "Fine" | Clipped, tense | Passive-aggressive compliance |
| "Whatever" | Any delivery | Dismissal (stronger = more hostile) |

**The prosody rule**: For markers that can signal multiple attitudes, **prosody (tone, speed, pitch) is the decoder**. The same word with different delivery means different things. (See Category 15: Prosodic Pragmatics.)

## 6. Discourse Markers in Digital Communication

Digital discourse has developed its own markers:

| Digital Marker | Function | NOT Literal |
|---------------|----------|-------------|
| **"lol"** | Softener, hedge, signals informality | Not actual laughter |
| **"haha"** | Closer to actual amusement | Milder than "lol" in some contexts |
| **"omg"** | Emphasis/surprise marker | Rarely literal shock |
| **"tbh"** (to be honest) | Sincerity marker before a strong opinion | Like "actually" in speech |
| **"ngl"** (not gonna lie) | Confessional marker | Signals the speaker knows this may be unpopular |
| **"I mean"** | Same as speech -- reformulation/emphasis | |
| **Emoji** | Function like prosody -- convey tone that text lacks | The period-to-emoji pipeline: "OK." (cold) vs "OK!" (warm) vs "OK 👍" (casual warm) |

**"Lol" as discourse marker**: This is one of the most important digital pragmatic shifts. "Lol" rarely means "laughing out loud." It functions as a softener/hedge, equivalent to a smile or light tone in speech. "I can't make it lol" = "I can't make it [said with a light, non-serious tone]." Removing "lol" makes the same text more serious and potentially more hostile.

## 7. The Autistic Experience with Discourse Markers

### 7.1 Production Patterns

Research shows autistic speakers produce fewer discourse markers overall, but the pattern is **marker-specific**:

**Reduced**: "Um" (listener-oriented delay signal), "well" (turn management), "you know" (listener engagement), topic-shift markers
**Preserved**: "Uh" (speaker-oriented delay), "like" (hedging/quotative -- possibly acquired through frequency), structural connectives ("and," "but," "because")

The pattern maps onto the **listener-oriented vs. speaker-oriented** distinction: markers that require modeling the listener's expectations are reduced; markers that serve the speaker's own cognitive needs are preserved.

### 7.2 The Impression Cost

Research (Zane & Grossman, 2021) found that communication **style** -- not content -- drives negative impressions of autistic speakers. Discourse markers are a major component of style. Speech without markers sounds "abrupt," "disconnected," or "flat" to NT ears, even when the content is perfectly clear and accurate.

This doesn't mean autistic speakers should force themselves to use markers they don't naturally produce. It means **understanding what the absence communicates** gives you informed choice about when it matters.

### 7.3 Which Markers Give the Most Practical Benefit

A three-tier priority system for learning:

**Tier 1 (Highest practical value):**
- **"Well"** -- Understanding this as a dispreferred signal is the single highest-value pragmatic skill in this category
- **"So"** -- Navigation marker; lets you track conversation structure
- **"Um"** -- Using it signals to NT listeners that you're still engaged (floor-holding)
- **"Actually"** -- Polite correction tool
- **"But"** -- Contrast signal (conventional implicature)

**Tier 2 (High value):**
- **"I mean"** -- Self-repair without awkwardness
- **"Anyway"** -- Topic management / return to point
- **"Oh"** -- Signals you've registered new information
- **"Right" / "OK"** -- Acknowledgment and transition

**Tier 3 (Contextually valuable):**
- **"Like"** -- Hedging and approximation
- **"You know"** -- Rapport-building (if it feels natural)
- **"Just"** -- Email/request softener
- **"Basically"** -- Summary/authority signal

| "So..." | After discussion | A conclusion or summary is coming |
| "Anyway..." | After a digression | Returning to the main point |
| "Anyway..." | With falling intonation | Pre-closing; the conversation is wrapping up |
| "Actually..." | Before a statement | A correction or counter-expectation follows |
| "I mean..." | After their own statement | They're about to adjust what they just said (stronger or weaker) |
| "You know?" | Mid-sentence or end | They want a backchannel (nod, "yeah") -- not a literal answer |

| "Right..." | Slow, drawn out | "I'm skeptical" or sarcasm |

## 9. Key Takeaways

1. **Discourse markers are not filler.** They are processing instructions that tell you how to interpret what follows. Filtering them out means losing the pragmatic metadata of the conversation.

2. **"Well" is the single most important marker to understand.** "Well" at the start of a response = the answer is not straightforward. This one signal, correctly interpreted, resolves enormous amounts of pragmatic ambiguity.

3. **Markers map to conversation structure.** "So" opens, "anyway" transitions, "well" pre-closes. Tracking markers gives you a real-time map of where the conversation is.

4. **Prosody disambiguates.** "Sure" can mean genuine agreement or reluctant compliance. "Right" can mean acknowledgment or sarcasm. Tone, speed, and pitch are the decoders.

5. **Compound markers carry nuanced meaning.** "Yeah no" = no. "No yeah" = yes. "Well actually" = correction incoming. Learn the common compounds as vocabulary.

6. **The um/uh distinction reveals the pattern.** Autistic speakers preserve speaker-oriented markers (uh) and reduce listener-oriented ones (um). This isn't a deficit -- it's a different priority system. But knowing that "um" signals "please wait" to NT listeners is practically useful.

7. **Digital markers follow the same logic.** "Lol" is a softener. Emoji replace prosody. "Tbh" is "actually" for text. The principles are the same; the surface forms differ.
