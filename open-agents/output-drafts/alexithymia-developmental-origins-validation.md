## Claim-by-claim verification

### Claim 1: Heritability 30-33% (Danish twin study)
**Status**: CONFIRMED. Jorgensen, Zachariae, Skytthe, Kyvik (2007) Psychosomatics 48(2). N=8,785 twin pairs. Heritability for TAS-20 total and facets in 30-33% range. Non-shared environment ~50-56%. Shared environment ~12-20%. PMID 17917473.

### Claim 2: Heritability 42% (Italian twin study, Picardi 2011)
**Status**: CONFIRMED. Picardi et al. (2011) J Psychosomatic Research 71(4). N=729 twins. Heritability 42%, drops to 33% with depression covariate. Genetic correlation with depression r=0.65. PMID 21911104.

### Claim 3: Emotional neglect strongest predictor of adult alexithymia
**Status**: CONFIRMED at moderate evidence. Aaron et al. (2023) meta-analysis. Direction is right; the effect-size differences across maltreatment types are not so large that ordinal ranking should be presented as definitive. Hedged in text.

### Claim 4: Alexithymia mediates ACE → psychopathology
**Status**: CONFIRMED at meta-analytic level. 2024 Frontiers in Psychiatry mediation meta-analysis. Significant indirect effects across multiple psychopathology outcomes.

### Claim 5: Insecure (especially dismissive-avoidant) attachment associated with elevated alexithymia
**Status**: CONFIRMED. Lyvers et al. (2019) PubMed 30848836 and broader attachment-alexithymia literature. The dismissive-avoidant pattern most consistently associated.

### Claim 6: Parental alexithymia associated with child alexithymia (modeling effect)
**Status**: CONFIRMED at the population/family-correlation level. Distinguishing genetic transmission from environmental modeling requires twin or adoption design — the twin studies separate them statistically. Presented carefully in text.

### Claim 7: Levant Normative Male Alexithymia (NMAS) and ART
**Status**: CONFIRMED as published constructs. ART evidence base is small (pilot studies, not large RCTs). Hedged appropriately.

### Claim 8: Gender meta-analytic d ≈ 0.22
**Status**: CONFIRMED at the 2024 meta-analytic level (men slightly higher, small absolute effect, heavy cultural moderation). Specific effect-size point-estimates can vary by meta-analysis; the range is small (d=0.20 to d=0.25 range across meta-analyses).

### Claim 9: East Asian samples elevated TAS-20 means
**Status**: CONFIRMED descriptively. The construct-equivalence question complicates interpretation. Presented even-handedly.

### Claim 10: Schouten 2020 — Belgian/Japanese suppression
**Status**: CONFIRMED with corrected attribution. Authors are **Schouten, Boiger, Kirchner-Häusler, Uchida, Mesquita** — NOT English et al. Frontiers in Psychology 2020. The Belgian-suppression-correlates-with-worse-outcomes / Japanese-suppression-neutral-or-positive finding is accurately characterised.

### Claim 11: Soto et al. 2011 — Hong Kong Chinese vs European Americans
**Status**: CONFIRMED. PMC4341898. European Americans: suppression negatively associated with adjustment. Hong Kong Chinese: not.

### Claim 12: Ryder et al. 2018 — Cultural Shaping of Alexithymia
**Status**: CONFIRMED as a real publication. The construct-non-equivalence argument is faithfully represented.

### Claim 13: 11-year stability mean TAS-20 = 44.2 (Tolmunen 2000/2011)
**Status**: CONFIRMED. Tolmunen et al. (2011) PubMed 21081227 and (2017) PubMed 28314554. Cohort N=3,083. Strong relative-rank stability.

### Claim 14: Messina 2014 primary/secondary/organic heuristic
**Status**: CONFIRMED. Messina, Beadle, Paradiso (2014). Heuristic — not a validated discrete classification scheme. Presented as such.

### Claim 15: Anterior insula damage >15% produces dose-dependent acquired alexithymia (Hogeveen 2016)
**Status**: CONFIRMED. PMC4752907. About 15% of brain-injury patients meet alexithymia criteria. Referenced briefly in this category as the lesion/organic etiology pointer; full coverage is in Cat 4.

### Claim 16: Cameron 2024 — psychological treatments medium effect on TAS-20
**Status**: CONFIRMED. PMC11673933. 21 RCT arms, 1,251 participants. Medium effects across CBT-derived, third-wave, and psychodynamic interventions.

## Adaptive-function framing for gender section

The user has elevated the adaptive-function thread. For the gender/culture section in particular, the framing has been:
- Present Levant's normative male alexithymia as the documented cost in independent-cultural contexts (this is the standard and correct framing).
- Layer on Schouten 2020's finding that expressive suppression has different functional outcomes by cultural context — meaning the same emotional-restraint pattern may serve different social functions in interdependent contexts and not carry the same costs there.
- Distinguish carefully: this is about *expressive suppression* in interdependent cultures, not about the deeper emotion-identification deficit that defines alexithymia. A person with fluent emotional self-knowledge who suppresses outward expression is not the same as a person who lacks the self-knowledge in the first place.
- Do not flatten into "male alexithymia is good in some cultures." That would be a misreading of the literature. The cost-benefit calculus is culture-dependent; both the cost side and the benefit side need to be presented.

## Items deliberately not claimed

- **No specific genes for alexithymia.** Some candidate-gene studies exist (5-HTTLPR, oxytocin receptor variants), but the evidence base is small and not consistently replicated. Not claimed.
- **No precise per-individual constitutional-vs-developmental ratio.** Population-level twin studies estimate variance components; they cannot tell any individual their personal split. The text reflects this honestly.
- **No claim that ART or attachment-based therapy reverses alexithymia.** Effect sizes are small to moderate; "reversal" framing would overstate. Used "partially modify" and "reduce" framings.
- **Mexican outpatient 90% figure.** Cited only as outlier with appropriate caveat.
