# Category 13: Topic Management

## The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

---

## How to Read This Document

Topics don't just happen in conversation -- they are **introduced, maintained, shifted, and closed** through specific practices that speakers manage collaboratively, turn by turn. If you've ever been told you "changed the subject too suddenly," "talked too long about one thing," or "missed that the conversation had moved on," this category explains exactly what's happening underneath those experiences and gives you concrete tools for navigating it.

The key insight from recent research: autistic speakers are not worse at **staying on topic** -- they are different in **how they move between topics**. The frequency of on-topic responses is comparable; the manner of shifting differs. Understanding the transition system gives you the tool you're missing.

---

## 1. What "Topic" Is

### 1.1 Why Topic Is Surprisingly Hard to Define

Unlike a sentence (which has a grammatical subject), a conversation doesn't have a fixed, pre-determined topic. "Topic" is the **aboutness** of a stretch of talk, but it's:

- **Collaboratively constructed**: One person proposes a topic; it only becomes THE topic if the other person engages with it
- **Locally managed**: Topic is handled turn by turn, not planned in advance. Each contribution shapes what comes next
- **Incrementally developed**: Topics evolve through successive contributions, gradually shifting focus
- **Fuzzy-boundaried**: Conversations drift smoothly between topics, and often there's no sharp line between "Topic A" and "Topic B"

The linguist Schegloff (1990) made a crucial distinction: speakers don't "talk about a topic" (as if the topic exists independently) -- they **"speak topically"** (making each turn relevant to what came before). Topic coherence emerges locally, not from a master plan.

### 1.2 Why This Matters

Understanding that topic is collaborative means that **unilateral topic control** -- talking at length about your own interest without checking whether the other person has "bought in" -- is a failure of the collaborative mechanism. The topic isn't yours to determine alone; it's a joint product.

## 2. The Four Operations of Topic Management

### 2.1 Topic Introduction: How Topics Enter Conversation

Topics don't just appear -- they are **proffered** (offered) and then **accepted or declined** by the other person.

**How to introduce a topic:**

| Method | Example | Social Function |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| **News announcement** | "You'll never guess what happened." | Creates anticipation, secures go-ahead |
| **Discovery frame** | "I saw something interesting..." | Positions topic as worth sharing |
| **Question frame** | "Can I get your opinion on something?" | Flatters listener, invites engagement |
| **Topic initial elicitor** | "What's new?" / "Anything going on?" | Opens space for the OTHER person to introduce a topic |
| **Associative link** | "That reminds me..." | Connects new topic to current one |
| **Direct statement** | "I wanted to talk to you about X." | Clear, purposeful, works well in professional contexts |
| **Pre-sequence** | "Can I ask you something personal?" | Warning before sensitive topics |

**The proffer-acceptance cycle**: When you introduce a topic, the other person signals whether they accept it:
- **Acceptance**: Follow-up question, elaboration, "Oh really? Tell me more" → proceed
- **Minimal uptake**: "Mm-hmm" without follow-up → they may not be interested; check
- **Redirect**: They respond briefly then shift to their own topic → they didn't accept yours
- **Block**: "I'd rather not talk about that" → topic is declined; respect it

### 2.2 Topic Maintenance: How Topics Are Sustained

A topic is maintained when speakers make **relevant contributions** -- contributions that connect to what's been said before.

**What counts as "on topic":**
- Elaborating on what was said ("And then what happened?")
- Adding related information ("You know, something similar happened to me...")
- Asking follow-up questions ("How did they react?")
- Evaluating what was said ("That's incredible!")

**Topic exhaustion signals** (the topic is running out of steam):
- Turns get shorter
- Responses become less specific ("Yeah..." "Mm-hmm...")
- Pauses get longer
- Summary or assessment appears ("Well, that's that." / "Anyway...")
- Repetition of points already made

**The practical rule**: If you notice exhaustion signals, it's time to shift topics or let someone else introduce one. Continuing a topic past exhaustion creates the "why are they still talking about this?" impression.

### 2.3 Topic Shifting: How Topics Change

Topic shifts range from invisible to jarring, and the difference is **transition markers**:

**Stepwise/gradual shift** (smoothest -- often invisible):
Topic A → related idea → related idea → Topic B. Each turn is locally relevant to the prior one, but the conversation has drifted. This is the most natural and socially smooth type.

**Marked shift** (uses explicit transition markers):

| Marker | Function | Example |
|--------|----------|---------|
| "Speaking of which..." | Links new topic to current via association | "Speaking of restaurants, have you tried the new place on Main?" |
| "That reminds me..." | Memory trigger | "That reminds me, I need to call the dentist." |
| "Oh, by the way..." | Tangentially related | "Oh by the way, did you get my email?" |
| "Before I forget..." | Urgency excuse | "Before I forget, can you bring the files tomorrow?" |
| "On a related note..." | Explicit connection | "On a related note, the budget meeting is moved." |
| "Totally different subject, but..." | Announces the break | "Totally different subject, but are you free Saturday?" |
| "Random question..." | Acknowledges the break informally | "Random question -- do you know a good plumber?" |
| "Anyway..." | Dismisses current topic, returns to prior or shifts | "Anyway, so what are you doing this weekend?" |
| "So..." | Returns to main business | "So, about the project deadline..." |

**Unmarked/abrupt shift** (no transition marker):
Jumping from one topic to another without any bridging phrase. This is what research identifies as the autistic pattern -- the shift itself may be perfectly logical to the speaker, but without a transition marker, the listener experiences it as jarring.

**The critical practical insight**: The difference between a "smooth" topic shift and an "abrupt" one is often just **one phrase**. Adding "Oh, that reminds me --" before changing topics transforms an abrupt shift into a marked one, which is fully socially acceptable.

### 2.4 Topic Closure: How Topics End

Topics close through specific signals:

| Signal | Example | Function |
|--------|---------|----------|
| **Summary/formulation** | "So basically..." / "The bottom line is..." | Wraps up the point |
| **Assessment** | "Well, that's interesting." / "Yeah, that's rough." | Evaluates the topic as complete |
| **Silence** | A longer-than-usual pause after a response | Signals no one has more to add |
| **Declining specificity** | Responses become vaguer | "Yeah..." / "Right..." |
| **Pre-shift marker** | "Anyway..." / "So..." | Signals readiness to move on |

**The connection to conversation closing**: You close topics first, then close the conversation. The conversation-closing sequence (Category 4) starts when the last active topic is closed.

## 3. Reading Topic Signals

This is the most practically important skill: detecting what the OTHER person wants regarding the current topic.

### 3.1 They Want to Continue

| Signal | What It Looks Like |
|--------|--------------------|
| Follow-up questions | "What happened then?" / "How did that go?" |
| Building on your point | "Yeah, and another thing about that..." |
| Requesting elaboration | "Tell me more about..." / "What do you mean by...?" |
| Body language | Leaning in, eye contact, animated expression |
| Engaged backchannels | "Really?" / "No way!" / "That's amazing!" |

**What to do**: Continue. They're engaged.

### 3.2 They Want to Change Topics

| Signal | What It Looks Like |
|--------|--------------------|
| Shorter responses | "Yeah." / "Mm-hmm." / "Right." (without follow-up) |
| No follow-up questions | They respond but don't ask anything |
| Body language | Looking around, checking phone/watch, shifting weight |
| Introducing a tangent | They respond briefly then mention something unrelated |
| Trailing off | "Yeah..." (fading voice, no continuation) |
| Generic assessment | "Well, that's interesting." (= I'm done with this topic) |

**What to do**: Shift topics. Use a transition marker: "Anyway, what about you -- how's your week been?"

### 3.3 You Should Stop Talking About This Topic

| Strong Signal | What It Means |
|--------------|---------------|
| Multiple non-specific backchannels in a row | They stopped engaging turns ago |
| They change the subject right after you speak | They're overriding your topic |
| Others in a group have gone silent | The group has lost interest |
| Someone directly redirects | "Anyway, so about the meeting..." |
| Visible discomfort (shifting, crossed arms) | The topic itself may be uncomfortable |

**What to do**: Stop. Transition with "Anyway..." or "So what's going on with you?" Don't take it personally -- topic exhaustion happens with all topics for all speakers.

## 4. The Special Interest / Perseveration Question

### 4.1 The Reframe

Clinical literature describes autistic "topic perseveration" as returning repeatedly to topics of intense interest. The neurodiversity framing is different: this is **passionate engagement** with a deeply interesting subject.

Research (Anthony et al., 2013) shows that autistic individuals' interests are more **intense** but not necessarily more **circumscribed** than NT interests. The difference is degree, not kind. NT sports fans who talk about their team constantly are doing the same thing -- it's just more socially sanctioned.

### 4.2 When Extended Topic Talk Is Valued

| Context | Why It Works |
|---------|-------------|
| With someone who shares the interest | Mutual engagement; both are energized |
| In professional/expert settings | Deep knowledge is the point |
| In educational contexts | Thoroughness is expected |
| In autistic-autistic conversation | Infodumping is often reciprocated and valued |
| When explicitly invited | "Tell me everything about X!" |

### 4.3 When It Creates Friction

| Context | Why It's Friction |
|---------|-----------------|
| Mixed-interest social group | Others can't contribute; they disengage |
| One-on-one with someone uninterested | They're not getting a turn; the collaboration breaks down |
| When exhaustion signals are ignored | Continuing past the other person's engagement is a unilateral topic choice |
| When it prevents others' topics | If your topic fills all the space, their topics can't enter |

### 4.4 Practical Strategies

**The 30-second check**: After about 30 seconds of talking on a topic, pause and check for engagement. If you see follow-up questions and active backchannels → continue. If you see short responses and no questions → it's time to shift or invite their contribution.

**The one-for-one rule**: For every topic you introduce, let the other person introduce one too. If you've shared about your interest, actively invite theirs: "What about you? What have you been into lately?"

**The bridge strategy**: Instead of dropping your interest or dominating with it, find bridges to topics the other person cares about. "I've been reading about X [your interest], and it made me think about Y [something they mentioned earlier]."

## 5. Safe Topics and Small Talk Navigation

### 5.1 The Safe Topics Inventory

When you need to make conversation but aren't sure what to talk about:

| Always Safe | Usually Safe | Context-Dependent | Generally Avoid |
|------------|-------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Weather | Weekend plans | Work projects (at work) | Politics |
| Local happenings | Food/restaurants | Family (if close) | Religion |
| Recent entertainment | Travel/vacation | Health (if relevant) | Money/salary |
| Shared environment | Sports (if shared) | Current events (choose carefully) | Appearance/weight |
| "How was your weekend?" | Pets | Dating/relationships | Others' personal problems |

### 5.2 Bridge Topics: From Small Talk to Substance

Small talk doesn't have to stay small. Use **bridge topics** to transition from safe/surface topics to something you actually want to talk about:

```
Safe topic: "How was your weekend?"
→ Their response: "Good, we went hiking."
→ Bridge: "Oh nice, where did you go?" 
→ Bridge: "I've been wanting to explore more trails around here."
→ Substantive topic: Now you're discussing outdoor activities, local geography, shared interests
```

The key is that each step is locally relevant to the previous one. You're not jumping from "how was your weekend" to "let me tell you about my special interest" -- you're building a series of natural bridges.

## 6. Topic Management in Different Contexts

### 6.1 Meetings

| Feature | How It Differs |
|---------|---------------|
| **Topic control** | Agenda-driven; facilitator manages topics |
| **Introduction** | Tie to agenda items: "I'd like to raise something related to [item 3]..." |
| **Shifting** | Wait for transitions between agenda items, or use: "Can I flag something before we move on?" |
| **Off-topic contributions** | "I have something unrelated -- can we add it to the parking lot?" |

### 6.2 Digital Communication

| Platform | Topic Management |
|----------|-----------------|
| **Slack/Teams** | Threading is explicit topic management. One topic per thread. |
| **Text messages** | Multiple topics can run in parallel. Don't worry about transition markers. |
| **Email** | One topic per email (generally). Use subject lines as topic headers. |
| **Social media** | Topics are community-managed, not individually managed. |

### 6.3 Group Conversations (3+ people)

| Challenge | Strategy |
|-----------|---------|
| Can't find an opening | Wait for a natural pause, then: "Can I add something?" |
| Group's topic doesn't interest you | Ask one follow-up question (shows engagement), then bridge to a related topic |
| One person is dominating | Use their next pause to redirect: "That's interesting -- [other person], what do you think about...?" |
| Sub-conversations splitting off | This is normal in groups of 4+; join the sub-conversation that interests you more |

## 7. Common Mistakes and Fixes

| Mistake | What Happens | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| **Changing subject without transition** | Listener feels jarred | Add "Oh, that reminds me --" or "Totally different thing, but --" |
| **Returning to your topic without acknowledging theirs** | Feels dismissive | React to their topic FIRST ("That's great!"), THEN transition |
| **Continuing past exhaustion signals** | Listener disengages | Check for engagement every 30 seconds |
| **Not contributing to others' topics** | Feels uninterested | Ask at least one follow-up question before shifting |
| **Introducing heavy topics without warning** | Creates shock/discomfort | Use a pre-sequence: "Can I talk to you about something serious?" |
| **Bringing every topic back to your interest** | Feels self-centered | Apply the one-for-one rule |
| **Not noticing a topic proffer from someone else** | They feel ignored | Watch for "Have you heard about X?" or "I wanted to mention..." -- these are invitations to engage |

## 8. Key Takeaways

1. **Topic is collaborative.** You propose; they accept (or don't). Checking for engagement is not optional -- it's how the system works.

2. **The difference between "smooth" and "abrupt" is one phrase.** Adding "That reminds me --" or "Speaking of which --" before a topic shift transforms it from jarring to natural.

3. **Topic exhaustion has visible signals.** Shorter responses, no follow-up questions, longer pauses, vague assessments. When you see these → shift topics.

4. **Autistic topic maintenance is comparable to NT.** Recent research shows similar on-topic response rates. The difference is in the MANNER of shifting, not the FREQUENCY of staying on topic.

5. **Passion is valid; calibration is the skill.** Deep engagement with a topic is a strength. The 30-second check and the one-for-one rule help calibrate without suppressing your enthusiasm.

6. **Safe topics are bridges, not destinations.** Use them to get conversations started, then build bridges to topics that interest you.

7. **Transition markers are the cheapest, highest-value pragmatic tool.** "Oh, by the way" / "That reminds me" / "Anyway" -- learning a handful of these phrases transforms topic management immediately.
