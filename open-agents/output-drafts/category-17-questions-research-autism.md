# Category 17: Questions and Autism

## Research Document for English Pragmatics Knowledge Base

**Focus**: How autistic individuals process, produce, and navigate the pragmatic system of questions -- the research evidence, practical consequences, and strategies for building competence

**Scope Note**: Questions are among the most pragmatically complex speech acts in any language. A question's surface form (interrogative syntax) tells you almost nothing about its actual function. "How are you?" is not a request for medical information. "Can you pass the salt?" is not a question about ability. "Nice weather, isn't it?" is not a meteorological inquiry. This document covers the full landscape of how autism interacts with the question system: which question types cause difficulty, which are handled well, why the mismatches occur, and what strategies help.

---

## 1. HOW AUTISTIC INDIVIDUALS PROCESS DIFFERENT QUESTION TYPES

### 1.1 The Core Issue: Questions Are Rarely Just Questions

The fundamental challenge is that English questions serve radically different pragmatic functions depending on context, and the surface form of the question is an unreliable guide to its function. Research consistently identifies pragmatic inference -- determining what a speaker *means* by asking a question, as opposed to what the words *literally* request -- as a core area of difficulty in autism (Frontiers in Psychology, 2024; PMC 11424445).

Children with ASD "differed from typically developing children in questions demanding context utilization, and the demand of mind-reading in utterance interpretation increased the difference between groups" (Loukusa et al., 2007). This finding captures the gradient: the more a question's meaning depends on pragmatic inference rather than literal content, the more difficulty it creates.

However, this is emphatically NOT a blanket impairment. Recent research reveals a far more nuanced picture: "Some (but not all) pragmatic inferences are difficult for autistic children" (Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 2025). Different types of pragmatic inference recruit different cognitive mechanisms, and autistic individuals may perform at typical levels on some while struggling with others.

### 1.2 Information-Seeking Questions: Generally Well-Handled

Questions that genuinely request information -- "What time does the store close?", "Where is the bathroom?", "How do you spell that?" -- are the most transparent question type. The surface form matches the pragmatic function: the speaker wants information, and the grammatical structure signals what information they want.

Research on wh-question comprehension shows that autistic individuals comprehend wh-questions through the same developmental pathway as typically developing peers, though with some delay. "Both typically developing children and those with autism comprehend wh-questions before producing similar forms, indicating that development occurs in a similar manner" (PMC 3329597; Goodwin et al., 2012). The delay is linked to general language development rather than an autism-specific grammatical deficit.

However, even information-seeking questions can become pragmatically complex when:
- The question is **open-ended**: "Tell me about yourself" or "How was your day?" -- which require the listener to infer what information is relevant (see Section 4.1 on interview questions)
- The question requires **context utilization**: Answering based on situational context rather than stored knowledge (Loukusa et al., 2007)
- The question is a **display question** in an educational setting: The teacher already knows the answer and is testing the student's knowledge -- a pragmatic framing that can confuse students who interpret questions as genuine information requests

Research on answering contextually demanding questions identified three error types in children with Asperger syndrome/HFA: "Type 1, the child answered the original question incorrectly; Type 2, the child gave a correct answer but when asked a follow-up question explained the answer incorrectly; and Type 3, the child first gave a correct answer but continued answering, which ultimately led to an irrelevant answer" (Loukusa et al., 2007; PubMed 17126362). That third type -- providing correct information but continuing past the pragmatically appropriate stopping point -- reflects difficulty with knowing *how much* to answer, not inability to answer.

### 1.3 Rhetorical Questions: The Literal Interpretation Problem

Rhetorical questions are questions that do not expect an answer. "Who cares?", "What's the point?", "Isn't that obvious?" -- these function as assertions, not as requests for information. The speaker of "Who cares?" is asserting that nobody cares; they are not asking you to enumerate the people who care.

For autistic individuals, rhetorical questions can trigger a literal processing response. "People who have autism typically have trouble understanding non-literal phrases. Non-literal phrases can include metaphors, understatements, rhetorical questions, hyperboles, and verbal irony" (Total Care ABA; SkyCare ABA).

Judy Endow, an autistic author and advocate, notes that "autistic people may not automatically know how to respond to rhetorical social questions such as 'How are you?' or automatically reciprocate in social pleasantries such as 'good morning.' This is not because they are rude, obnoxious, don't care, or any of the other assumed reasons people attribute to this behavior. Instead, it is because all social information is not automatically picked up and used by a person with an autistic brain."

However, the picture is more nuanced than the stereotype suggests. A 2024 review of figurative language processing in autism (Lampri, 2024; Autism Research) and a meta-analysis of figurative language comprehension (PMC 5843023; Kalandadze et al., 2018) found that "differences between the autism spectrum disorder and typically developing groups were small and nonsignificant when the groups were matched based on language ability." Moreover, "ASD deficits in metaphor processing are present for children and adolescents, but they may be absent in adulthood."

This suggests a developmental trajectory: literal processing of rhetorical questions may be more pronounced in childhood but may diminish in adulthood as autistic individuals learn (often through explicit instruction or social experience) to recognize the rhetorical form. The learning, however, tends to be **explicit and rule-based** rather than the automatic, intuitive recognition that characterizes neurotypical processing.

### 1.4 Phatic Questions: "How Are You?" and Its Cousins

Phatic questions deserve their own section (see Section 2 below) because they represent the single most discussed pragmatic mismatch in autistic experience. But briefly: phatic questions ("How are you?", "What's up?", "How's it going?") LOOK like information requests but FUNCTION as social rituals -- their purpose is to acknowledge another person's existence and signal willingness to maintain social connection, not to obtain information about the person's actual state.

"Phatic communication is the-referential use of language to share feelings or establish a mood of sociability rather than to communicate information or ideas. Few people who ask, 'How are you?' on greeting others really want an answer to their question" (ASDNext.org; EBSCO Research).

### 1.5 Indirect Request Questions: Better Than Expected

Research on indirect requests (conventionalized forms like "Can you pass the salt?") reveals that autistic individuals handle these better than many expect.

**Conventionalized indirect requests** ("Can you...?", "Could you...?", "Would you mind...?") are processed similarly by autistic and non-autistic adults. "Conventionalized indirect requests, such as 'Can you turn on the heater?' require little inference and are simple to understand, and research has shown autistic individuals respond to these types of requests similarly to their neurotypical peers" (PMC 8831260; Kissine, 2012).

This is because "embedded imperatives beginning with 'can' or 'could' have become so conventionalized that listeners seem to automatically interpret them as requests rather than yes/no questions when presented in a context that allows for a directive interpretation" (PMC 11897084; Andretta et al., 2024). These forms are essentially frozen pragmatic constructions -- their indirect meaning has become their primary meaning through frequency of use.

**Non-conventionalized indirect requests** ("It's cold in here" meaning "close the window") are more challenging because they require the listener to infer the speaker's intention from context without any conventionalized cue. Research by Andretta et al. (2024) found that "autistic and non-autistic adults use similar cognitive mechanisms when utilizing discourse context to interpret whether a speaker intends to be literal or not, and as autistic individuals age and their Theory of Mind skills become more developed, they are able to rely more on mentalizing to interpret hints."

A PLOS ONE study found that "children with autism understand indirect speech acts" in a semi-structured task, suggesting that "HFA can understand indirect and highly indirect requests, as well as TD children" (PMC 4638355; Kissine et al., 2015). The key qualification: performance is better in structured contexts with clear contextual cues than in naturalistic conversation where multiple signals must be integrated simultaneously.

### 1.6 Tag Questions: An Under-Researched Area

Tag questions ("Nice day, isn't it?", "You're coming, aren't you?", "That was hard, right?") are a specific question type that has received relatively little direct research attention in autism studies. Tag questions are pragmatically complex because they can serve multiple functions:

- **Confirmation-seeking**: "The meeting is at 3, isn't it?" (genuinely checking)
- **Agreement-seeking**: "That was a great movie, wasn't it?" (expecting agreement)
- **Phatic/social**: "Nice weather, isn't it?" (just making conversation)
- **Challenging**: "You weren't really going to do that, were you?" (expressing disapproval)

The function depends entirely on intonation, context, and the social relationship -- all areas where autistic processing may differ. Rising intonation on a tag question signals genuine uncertainty; falling intonation signals an expectation of agreement. For autistic individuals who may process prosodic cues differently (see Category 15: Prosodic Pragmatics), disambiguating these functions may require additional effort.

Research on conversation analysis in autism suggests that tag questions, when used by conversation partners, can serve a scaffolding function -- providing structure and reducing the pragmatic burden on the autistic interlocutor by offering a binary (yes/no) response option rather than requiring an open-ended contribution (Renner, 2002, in Children with Asperger's Syndrome and their Conversational Partners).

### 1.7 Loaded Questions and Presuppositions

Loaded questions contain embedded presuppositions -- assumptions built into the question that the answerer implicitly accepts by answering. "When did you stop cheating?" presupposes that you were cheating. "Why are you always late?" presupposes that you are always late.

Research on presupposition comprehension in autism shows mixed results. Studies with Cantonese-speaking children found that "children with ASD were found to perform significantly worse than typically developing children matched on chronological age in comprehending seven types of presupposition triggers, but they performed similarly to TD children matched on language ability" (Cheung et al., 2019; PubMed 31588807).

This language-mediated pattern suggests that the challenge with loaded questions is not a failure to detect presuppositions per se, but rather a general language development factor. Interestingly, the detail-oriented, bottom-up processing style associated with autism might, in some cases, make autistic individuals MORE likely to notice the embedded assumption in a loaded question -- precisely because they process the literal content of each element rather than accepting the pragmatic frame wholesale. This connects to findings from Category 9 (Presupposition) about how autistic individuals may resist presupposition accommodation.

However, it is important to note that while some autistic individuals may detect the presupposition, they may struggle with the pragmatic response -- knowing how to challenge a loaded question socially requires understanding conversational power dynamics and face-management strategies that go beyond mere detection.

### 1.8 Scalar Implicatures and Questions

Research on scalar implicatures -- a specific type of pragmatic inference -- provides an important counterpoint to the narrative of universal pragmatic difficulty in autism. When someone says "Some of the students passed," listeners typically infer "not all" (the scalar implicature). 

Studies have consistently found that "adults with ASD show no real difficulty in interpreting scalar implicatures" and that "participants in the Asperger group produced even more pragmatic inferences than the controls" (PMC 6064869; Pijnacker et al., 2009). A 2025 study confirmed that "some (but not all) pragmatic inferences are difficult for autistic children" -- specifically, scalar implicatures were relatively preserved while ad-hoc implicatures (which require context-specific reasoning) were more impaired (Springer, 2025).

This finding matters for questions because many questions embed scalar terms: "Did some of you finish?", "Is that always true?" Understanding the pragmatic force of these scalar terms does not appear to be differentially impaired in autism, suggesting that the challenge is specifically with inferences that require Theory of Mind or contextual/social reasoning rather than linguistic-semantic reasoning.

### 1.9 Display Questions and Exam Questions

Display questions are questions to which the asker already knows the answer -- typically asked by teachers in educational settings ("What is the capital of France?") or examiners on tests. The pragmatic function is assessment, not information-gathering.

This creates a specific challenge for autistic students. Research indicates that "schools have a role to play in training and preparing students for exams, particularly autistic students who may struggle to understand the wording of questions or take them literally" (Autism.org.uk). The difficulty is multi-layered:

1. **Understanding what the question is really asking**: Exam questions often use specialized meta-language ("Discuss...", "Evaluate...", "Compare and contrast...") that requires knowledge of academic conventions
2. **Calibrating response length**: Knowing how much detail is expected -- autistic students may provide too much or too little
3. **Processing open-ended formats**: "Sometimes answering questions is difficult because students have difficulty accessing the vocabulary they need to answer the question. They may be able to visualize the answer but not convey it in words" (Autism Classroom Resources)
4. **Time pressure**: "It can be difficult for autistic pupils and students to understand how long they have to spend on each question in order to finish an exam in time" (Autism.org.uk)

## 2. THE "HOW ARE YOU?" PROBLEM

### 2.1 Why This Is THE Iconic Autistic Pragmatic Challenge

"How are you?" may be the single most discussed example in the entire autism-and-pragmatics literature. It appears in virtually every account of autistic pragmatic experience, and for good reason: it perfectly encapsulates the mismatch between literal linguistic form and pragmatic function that creates difficulty across the autism spectrum.

The question "How are you?" appears to be a straightforward information request -- it has interrogative syntax, a clear wh-word ("how"), and a clear reference ("you"). If you process it literally, you should provide information about your current state. But in English-speaking cultures, "How are you?" is a **phatic expression** -- "speech used for social or emotive purposes rather than for communicating information or ideas, intended to show a willingness to maintain basic social connection rather than non-communication or outright hostility" (ActuallyAutistic Wiki).

The expected response is formulaic: "Fine, thanks. How are you?" or "Good, and you?" or "Not bad." The content of the response is irrelevant -- what matters is the social ritual of mutual acknowledgment.

### 2.2 How Autistic Individuals Commonly Respond

Because autistic processing tends toward the literal, many autistic individuals respond to "How are you?" as a genuine information request. Common patterns include:

**Honest, detailed responses**: "Well, I didn't sleep well last night because my neighbor's dog was barking, and I have a headache, and I'm worried about a deadline at work..." This is an accurate answer to the literal question -- but it violates the phatic expectation of a brief, formulaic response.

**Confusion about the purpose**: "When faced with rote questions like 'How are you?' from neurotypical people, autistic individuals may wonder why they ask if they don't really want to know, and what purpose such a cursory, shallow exchange serves" (Today's Autistic Moment).

**Freeze response**: The question triggers processing overload because the autistic person recognizes (from past experience) that their natural response is "wrong" but cannot quickly generate the "right" one. "Autistic individuals can sometimes have trouble reading the room when asked questions and are too honest in their responses" (ASDNext.org).

**Learned scripts**: Many autistic adults develop memorized responses through masking: "The easiest and expected answer to any phatic expression of general concern is something along the lines of 'I'm good, thanks. How about you?'" (ASDNext.org). These scripts work but may feel inauthentic and contribute to masking fatigue.

### 2.3 Research on the Autistic Experience of Phatic Questions

The ActuallyAutistic Wiki, a community-authored resource, identifies several ways autistic people experience phatic expressions:

- **Processing burden**: "Small talk often requires fast processing: tracking the other person's tone and body language, figuring out what they mean, and deciding what to say next -- all in real time" (Thrive Autism Coaching)
- **Unclear purpose**: "It can feel unclear why the conversation is happening, especially when the topic seems unimportant" (Autism Spectrum News)
- **Inauthenticity tension**: Many autistic people value honesty as a core trait. Giving a formulaic "Fine" when you are not fine can feel like lying. "Neurotypical people ask insincere questions such as 'How are you?' without seeming to care about the answer" (Employment Autism)
- **Social penalty for honest answers**: "Many autistic people have lost friendships by answering loaded questions with blunt and honest answers which were not the 'right' answer even if truthful" (Employment Autism)

Judy Endow's work describes the learning process: autistic people can learn the social greeting system, but "all social information is not automatically picked up and used by a person with an autistic brain." The learning must be explicit, and the learned behavior requires conscious effort to deploy -- it does not become automatic in the way it does for most neurotypical speakers.

### 2.4 The Deeper Issue: Multiple "How Are You?" Variants

The pragmatic challenge extends beyond "How are you?" to the entire family of greeting-questions:

- "How's it going?" (expects: "Good, you?")
- "What's up?" (expects: "Not much" or a brief update)
- "How've you been?" (expects: a slightly more substantive but still brief update)
- "What's new?" (expects: one or two highlights or "Not much")
- "How are things?" (expects: "Good" or brief general update)
- "Alright?" (British English; expects: "Alright" -- not even a question in function)

Each has slightly different pragmatic expectations, and the differences are entirely conventional -- there is no logical rule that predicts which greeting-question expects which depth of response. This is precisely the kind of arbitrary, convention-based pragmatic knowledge that autistic individuals report as most challenging.

### 2.5 Strategies for Navigating "How Are You?"

**The script approach**: Develop a small repertoire of memorized responses:
- Default: "I'm good, thanks. How are you?"
- When genuinely struggling but wanting to be somewhat honest: "Today is a bit of an off day, but I'm doing my best. How about you?" (ASDNext.org recommends this as a balance between honesty and social convention)
- When genuinely well: "I'm great, actually! How are you?"

**The categorization approach**: Learn to categorize the greeting-question by context:
- From a passing acquaintance in a hallway: pure phatic, give shortest response
- From a friend at the start of a hangout: slightly more substantive, but still brief
- From a close friend or therapist: genuinely wants to know, honest answer appropriate

**The reframe**: Rather than thinking of "How are you?" as a question requiring an answer, think of it as a greeting protocol -- the linguistic equivalent of a handshake or a wave. You don't analyze a wave; you wave back.

## 3. QUESTION-ASKING IN AUTISM

### 3.1 Research on How Autistic Individuals Ask Questions

Question-asking is an essential component of social conversation. Research consistently finds that "verbal initiations, such as questions, are essential components of social conversation often lacking in children with autism" (PMC 3631576; Koegel et al., 2014). However, the reasons are more nuanced than simple inability.

**It may be motivation, not ability**: A critical finding from Pivotal Response Treatment research is that "children may have more ability to ask questions than was previously expected; however, they are in need of motivation in order to ask them" (PMC 3949143; Koegel et al., 2014). This suggests that the difficulty is not in formulating questions but in recognizing when and why questions serve a social function -- the pragmatic dimension rather than the linguistic one.

**The "why" question**: Jaime Heidel, writing at The Articulate Autistic, identifies "why" as "probably the most misunderstood word between autistics and neurotypicals. It's the one that can cause the most friction and the most problems." For autistic people, "why" is a genuine request for the reasoning behind something -- understanding the logic of a request helps the autistic brain process and comply with it. For many neurotypical listeners, especially those in authority positions, "why?" is perceived as a challenge to authority or a refusal to comply.

### 3.2 The Question Bank Study (Koegel et al., 2021)

A significant intervention study published in the Journal of Speech, Language, and Hearing Research tested a "question bank" approach with adolescents and adults with autism. The intervention used "prepractice with question banks designed to increase the number and diversity of questions asked by adolescents and adults with ASD during social conversations with their peers."

**Results**: "Following intervention, all participants improved their use of questions in natural settings with their neurotypical peers. Generalization to novel questions occurred, and gains were maintained at follow-up." Additionally, "similarly aged neurotypical peers who were naive to the experimental hypothesis rated two of the three participants with higher social desirability following intervention."

This study demonstrates that (a) question-asking can be taught explicitly, (b) the skills generalize to new contexts and new questions, and (c) improved question-asking leads to better social outcomes as rated by neurotypical peers -- confirming that question-asking is a crucial social tool.

### 3.3 When NOT Asking Questions Causes Social Friction

Not asking questions can be interpreted by neurotypical conversation partners as:
- Lack of interest in the other person
- Self-centeredness or narcissism
- Rudeness or disengagement
- Social awkwardness

"Not asking reciprocal questions in conversation is generally considered rude and can be taken as a lack of interest. Friends show interest in their friends' responses by asking questions back" (Neurolaunch). A Quora post from a neurotypical person illustrates this: "My new autistic friend does not ask any questions when we text (and only sometimes when we talk in person), is it normal? She talks a lot but it feels like an interview."

The reality is typically quite different from the perception. Research identifies several reasons autistic individuals may not ask questions:
- **Processing demands**: The person may be focused on processing incoming information and unable to simultaneously generate questions
- **Not recognizing the social norm**: The expectation to ask reciprocal questions is an implicit social rule, not a logical requirement of information exchange
- **Uncertainty about what to ask**: Not knowing what question would be appropriate, relevant, or welcome
- **Past negative experiences**: Having been told their questions were "weird" or "off-topic" can lead to question-avoidance

### 3.4 When Asking TOO MANY Questions Causes Friction

The opposite problem also occurs. Autistic individuals may ask questions that are:

**Too numerous**: "Autistic people will ask questions more often and, in more depth, than their neurotypical counterparts because multi-part questions serve a very specific purpose for the autistic brain" (The Articulate Autistic). The autistic person needs comprehensive understanding before they can act, while the neurotypical person expects a level of inference and "just figuring it out."

**Too detailed**: Questions that drill into specifics that the neurotypical interlocutor considers self-evident or unimportant. "What may seem obvious to a neurotypical individual might not be immediately clear to someone with autism due to differences in information processing" (Neurolaunch).

**Violating social hierarchy**: "Autistic people do not have an inherent understanding of social hierarchy. To autistic people, a CEO is no different than a janitor" (The Articulate Autistic). This can lead to asking questions of the "wrong" person (e.g., emailing a company's board of directors when a supervisor wouldn't answer) or asking questions in contexts where neurotypical norms dictate silent compliance.

**Perceived as interrogation**: When autistic individuals do engage in reciprocal questioning, they may ask questions in rapid succession without the expected conversational buffer (commentary, reactions, self-disclosure) between questions, creating an interrogation-like pattern.

### 3.5 Repetitive Questions: A Distinct Phenomenon

Autistic individuals may also ask the same question repeatedly, which serves different functions from conversational question-asking:

- **Anxiety reduction and reassurance**: "Repetitive questioning can serve as a coping mechanism, providing reassurance and a sense of control in uncertain situations" (Attwood & Garnett Events)
- **Emotional regulation**: "Repetitive language can function as a form of emotional regulation, providing a predictable and controllable experience" (Neurolaunch)
- **Communication initiation**: "Repetition is sometimes used as a tool to initiate or sustain interaction" (Neurolaunch)
- **Processing needs**: The person may need to hear the answer multiple times to fully process it

This is distinct from conversational question-asking and should not be conflated with the social use of questions in dialogue.

## 4. QUESTIONS IN SPECIFIC AUTISTIC CONTEXTS

### 4.1 Job Interviews

Job interviews may represent the single highest-stakes question-and-answer scenario in adult life, and research consistently identifies them as profoundly challenging for autistic individuals.

**The Maras et al. (2021) Study**: Published in *Autism*, this landmark study examined 50 adults (25 autistic, 25 non-autistic) in mock job interviews. Key findings:

- Employment experts rated autistic interviewees less favorably on standard questions
- When questions were adapted for clarity and structure, "ratings of both autistic and non-autistic participants' answers improved, but particularly for autistic interviewees"
- The most effective adaptation was "the explicit and structured request for specific details, examples, and certain types of information, reducing the need for the interviewee to infer this implicitly"
- Structured questions provided "a twofold benefit: (1) providing support for executive functions and cognitive processes such as relational processing in memory retrieval while also (2) supporting social cognition by diminishing ambiguity about what is required"

**Specific problematic question types**:

- "Tell me about yourself" -- maximally vague and open-ended, "hard to respond to if you have problems inferring what the interviewer wants" (University of Bath). Autistic candidates "can find it difficult to know the level of detail required" and the question "contains little structure, particularly regarding the need to include positive information about goals, aspirations, self-descriptions and self-evaluations."
- "Tell me about a time when..." (behavioral questions) -- require episodic memory retrieval under time pressure. "Autistic candidates can find it difficult to recall specific memories of past experiences" (Prosper Health).
- "Where do you see yourself in five years?" -- requires social performance of ambition and future orientation
- "What's your greatest weakness?" -- a paradoxical question requiring strategic self-disclosure that is simultaneously honest and flattering

**Recommended strategies**:
- Request questions in advance (a reasonable accommodation)
- Use the STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result) for behavioral questions -- it provides the structure that the question format itself lacks
- Prepare scripted answers for the most common open-ended questions
- Request a single interviewer rather than a panel, and chairs at 90-degree angles to reduce eye contact pressure

A provocative 2024 paper argued that the entire job interview format is fundamentally biased against autistic candidates: "Autism and the Case Against Job Interviews" (PMC 11090822) questioned whether the format measures job-relevant skills or merely social performance ability.

### 4.2 Medical Appointments

Medical appointments present a specific question challenge: the autistic patient must both answer and ask questions in a context that is often anxiety-inducing and sensorily overwhelming.

**Barriers identified in research** (PMC 4841263):
- "Difficulty with abstract language" -- medical questions often use vague terms ("On a scale of 1-10, how bad is the pain?", "Is it a sharp or dull pain?")
- "Feeling pressured to respond quickly" -- medical appointments are time-constrained
- "Physical touch during examinations" -- sensory overwhelm that compromises cognitive processing during question-and-answer exchanges
- Difficulty describing subjective experiences: "Many autistic patients struggle to describe pain accurately, such as identifying whether pain is shooting, stabbing, or burning"

**Strategies**:
- Prepare a symptoms worksheet before the appointment
- Write questions down in advance
- Bring a trusted advocate
- Request that the provider use direct, literal language
- Ask for processing time: "I need a moment to think about that"

### 4.3 Educational Settings

In classroom contexts, question-asking serves both learning and social functions. Research identifies several specific challenges:

**Participating through questions**: "Students with autism find spontaneous dialogue challenging given their weaker propensity for communication and anxiety about unpredictable elements" (Coghill et al., 2019). Additionally, "unspecific questions such as 'Do you understand?' are not helpful given their detailed-focused cognitive style."

**Understanding teacher questions**: Teachers' questions in class are often display questions (the teacher knows the answer) or check-questions ("Does that make sense?") that function as invitations to ask for clarification rather than literal yes/no questions. Autistic students may answer "yes" literally to "Does that make sense?" even when they are confused, because the question was taken at face value.

**Intervention evidence**: Research using Pivotal Response Treatment showed that "participants initiated more targeted questions following intervention, and all children exhibited increases in initiation of untargeted questions during social interaction in novel settings" (PMC 3949143). Scripted training of questions "generalized to untrained scripts and settings, and students significantly increased their abilities to independently ask questions after receiving training" (ERIC EJ1134216).

### 4.4 Social Settings: Questions as Conversation Tools

In casual social interaction, questions serve as the primary mechanism for:
- Showing interest in another person ("How was your vacation?")
- Maintaining conversational topics ("What happened next?")
- Signaling engagement ("Really? What did she say?")
- Building rapport ("Where are you from originally?")
- Creating reciprocity ("Enough about me -- what about you?")

Research on social conversation interventions demonstrates that autistic individuals can learn to use questions strategically. "Asking questions and relating it to yourself shows you're interested in them" (Ascent Autism). The question bank intervention (Koegel et al., 2021) showed that teaching specific question formulas improved both the quantity and quality of social interactions.

A key insight for autistic learners: "Instead of asking generic questions that can feel like a social script, this method values their expertise and enthusiasm" (Tonen Blog). Connecting questions to genuine interests -- asking about topics you actually want to know about -- produces more authentic engagement than formulaic reciprocal questions.

### 4.5 The Workplace: Asking Clarifying Questions

The workplace presents a specific question dilemma: the need to understand instructions fully (requiring questions) versus the social expectation to "figure it out" independently (discouraging questions).

**Why autistic employees ask more questions**: "Many autistic people struggle to process verbal instructions and will likely ask a lot of clarifying questions to aid their understanding. Most autistic people are bottom-up thinkers, meaning their learning begins with the details, and they gradually form those details into a whole picture" (Specialisterne Canada).

**The core misunderstanding**: "When an autistic person asks a question, it is an attempt to get an answer and/or fully understand and visualize a request being made of them, and the question is completely literal and an attempt to understand" (The Articulate Autistic). But "questions from autistic people are often misinterpreted when they are wrongly interpreted as having underlying meaning and messaging."

**The hierarchy problem**: "An autistic employee may email the Board of Directors with a question they couldn't get the answer to from their boss -- this is not insubordination or an attempt to undermine anyone, but they may have no idea that it's considered inappropriate to 'go over their boss's head'" (The Articulate Autistic).

**The article "Why Does My Autistic Employee Ask So Many Questions?"** (The Articulate Autistic) provides an employer-facing explanation of this phenomenon, noting that autistic employees' questions serve a fundamentally different purpose than neurotypical norms assume -- they are not signs of incompetence, insubordination, or passive-aggression, but genuine attempts to obtain the information needed to do the job well.

## 5. THE DOUBLE EMPATHY ANGLE

### 5.1 What Is the Double Empathy Problem?

The double empathy problem, first articulated by Damian Milton (2012), proposes that "many of the difficulties autistic individuals face when socializing with non-autistic individuals are due, in part, to mismatch and a lack of mutual understanding between the two groups, meaning that most autistic people struggle to understand and empathize with non-autistic people, whereas most non-autistic people also struggle to understand and empathize with autistic people" (Wikipedia; Autism.org.uk).

This framework "challenges deficit-based models of autism by recognizing that communication breakdowns happen between neurotypes, not within one group alone" (Relational Psych). Research has shown that "autistic individuals communicate more successfully and experience higher levels of rapport when interacting with other autistic individuals, compared to autistic-neurotypical interactions" (Frontiers for Young Minds).

### 5.2 Questions That Assume Common Ground Autistic Listeners Don't Share

Neurotypical speakers routinely ask questions that embed assumptions about shared knowledge, shared social norms, and shared interpretive frameworks:

- **"Don't you think...?"** -- Assumes the listener shares the speaker's perspective and presupposes agreement
- **"Isn't it obvious that...?"** -- Assumes something that may genuinely not be obvious to the autistic listener, creating a trap: admitting it's not obvious may seem incompetent
- **"You know what I mean?"** -- Used as a phatic check rather than a genuine question, but may trigger genuine confusion in an autistic listener who does NOT know what you mean
- **"Can't you just...?"** -- Assumes a task or social action is simple when it may not be for the autistic listener; the word "just" signals the speaker's assumption of simplicity

These questions create a double bind: answering honestly ("No, I don't think it's obvious") violates the expected social agreement, while answering formulaically ("Yeah, totally") may lead to misunderstandings downstream when it becomes clear the autistic person didn't actually share the assumed common ground.

### 5.3 When Autistic Questions Are Perceived as "Weird" or "Off-Topic"

The double empathy problem applies in reverse when autistic individuals ask questions. Research shows that "allistic peers are less likely to interact with autistic people because of immediate and unconscious negative judgments that are based purely on social communication style, and not substance" (PMC 8992906; Sasson et al., 2017).

Autistic questions may be perceived as weird or off-topic for several reasons, all of which reflect neurotype differences rather than deficits:

- **Different associative patterns**: Autistic individuals may connect to a different aspect of the conversation than the neurotypical listener expects, producing questions that seem tangential but follow a logical (if non-obvious) chain of reasoning
- **Detail focus**: Questions about specifics that the neurotypical speaker considers irrelevant ("But what color was the car?") reflect the detail-oriented processing style
- **Missing implied "obvious" information**: Asking about something the speaker assumed was self-evident ("Wait, where was this?") when the speaker thought the location was clear from context
- **Timing differences**: Asking a question about something said earlier in the conversation, after the topic has moved on, because processing time delayed the question formation

### 5.4 Credibility and Question-Answering

A troubling finding: "Autistic adults may be erroneously perceived as deceptive and lacking credibility" when answering questions (PMC 8813809; Brosnan et al., 2022). This is particularly relevant in legal, medical, and workplace contexts where how a person answers questions affects whether they are believed. The same communication differences -- atypical eye contact, flat affect, unusual prosody, over-specific or under-specific responses -- that characterize autistic communication style are also features that neurotypical listeners associate with deception.

## 6. QUESTIONS AND ANXIETY: WHY QUESTIONS THEMSELVES CAN BE THREATENING

### 6.1 The Anxiety Response to Being Asked Questions

Jaime Heidel's article "Questions Make Us Nervous" (The Articulate Autistic) captures an experience widely reported by autistic adults: the act of being asked a question itself triggers anxiety, independent of the question's content.

Reasons include:

- **Processing time pressure**: "Getting off of one train of thought to another takes enormous effort, and, while switching gears, they may freeze, stammer, stutter, or just not answer fast enough, triggering suspicion and frustration in the person talking to them"
- **Performance anxiety**: "Autistic people have to manually modify their speech patterns, facial expressions, and tone of voice so as not to come across as rude or defensive while also coming up with a correct and socially acceptable answer in the seconds of time given to them"
- **Traumatic conditioning**: "In the past, people might have become angry, impatient, or even abusive due to autistic people's lack of a quick and coherent response," which can make "ANY question an instant trigger"
- **Memory retrieval under pressure**: "Some autistic people genuinely cannot remember what they did an hour ago, and don't want to lie"
- **Ambiguity overload**: Multiple possible interpretations of the question create paralysis rather than fluent response

### 6.2 Open-Ended Questions as Anxiety Triggers

Open-ended questions are particularly anxiety-inducing for autistic individuals. Research confirms that "many autistic people have a low tolerance for ambiguity; uncertainty produces anxiety and makes it hard to start or sustain a response. Preference for concrete, specific tasks and literal interpretation makes vague prompts unhelpful" (PMC 10468547).

The aversion to open-ended questions "usually stems from the combined effects of executive-function demands, intolerance of uncertainty, literal processing, social-expectation load, and past negative feedback" (Quora community discussion with professional input).

Reframing open-ended questions into structured ones dramatically reduces anxiety and improves response quality -- a finding confirmed across educational, employment, and healthcare contexts.

## 7. MASKING AND SCRIPTED RESPONSES TO QUESTIONS

### 7.1 How Autistic People Learn to Mask Their Question-Processing Differences

Many autistic adults develop extensive masking strategies for navigating the question system. "Autism masking, also referred to as camouflaging or social camouflaging, is the process by which autistic individuals hide or disguise their natural traits to appear more neurotypical" (Autism.org.uk).

Question-specific masking includes:
- **Memorized scripts for phatic questions**: "I'm good, thanks, how are you?" delivered automatically
- **Pre-planned responses for common social questions**: "What do you do?" "Where are you from?" "Any plans this weekend?"
- **Rehearsed conversation starters**: Having prepared questions to ask others ("Scripting involves planning in advance what you want to say to someone" -- NAS Community)
- **Forced eye contact during question exchanges**: Suppressing the natural avoidance
- **Manufactured facial expressions**: Performing the expected look of interest/surprise/amusement when asked or answering questions

### 7.2 The Cost of Masking

"The persistent effort to monitor and adjust one's behavior in social settings may lead to exhaustion, anxiety, depression, and difficulties in forming an authentic self-concept" (Nature, 2025). The masking model identifies three components: "assimilation (adoption of neurotypical behaviors to fit in), masking (suppression of autistic traits to prevent negative social consequences), and compensation (use of learned strategies, such as social scripts, to navigate interactions)" (Hull et al., 2017).

For questions specifically, masking means that every conversational question exchange requires dual processing: the actual content processing (understanding and generating a response) AND the performance processing (monitoring and adjusting how the response is delivered). This doubles the cognitive load.

## 8. PRACTICAL STRATEGIES: A DECISION TREE FOR QUESTION TYPES

### 8.1 Identifying Question Type

The following decision framework can help autistic individuals quickly categorize incoming questions:

**Step 1: Is this a greeting or social ritual?**
- Indicators: Occurs at the start of an interaction, comes from someone passing by, is one of the standard phrases ("How are you?", "What's up?", "How's it going?")
- If YES: Use a formulaic response. The content does not matter; the social acknowledgment does.

**Step 2: Is this a genuine information request?**
- Indicators: The person pauses and waits for an answer, the question is about a specific topic, the person has a reason to need the information
- If YES: Answer with the relevant information. Check: am I providing the right amount of detail? (See calibration below)

**Step 3: Is this a rhetorical question?**
- Indicators: The speaker continues talking without pausing for an answer, the question expresses frustration or emotion ("Why does this always happen?"), the answer is so obvious the speaker clearly already knows it
- If YES: Do not answer literally. Acknowledge the emotion instead ("Yeah, that's frustrating").

**Step 4: Is this an indirect request?**
- Indicators: "Can you...?", "Could you...?", "Would you mind...?" followed by an action
- If YES: Treat it as a polite request for action, not a question about your ability. Do the thing.

**Step 5: Is this a conversational question (showing interest)?**
- Indicators: Follows something you said, asks for more detail or your opinion, the person seems engaged
- If YES: Provide a moderate-length response AND ask a reciprocal question back.

**Step 6: Is this a loaded or presupposition-bearing question?**
- Indicators: The question contains an embedded assumption you disagree with, answering it in any way seems to accept a false premise
- If YES: You can challenge the presupposition: "I'm not sure I agree that [presupposition]. Can you explain what you mean?"

### 8.2 When to Answer Literally vs. Formulaically vs. Not at All

| Question Type | Response Strategy | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Phatic greeting | Formulaic, brief | "How are you?" -> "Good, thanks, you?" |
| Information request | Literal, accurate | "What time is the meeting?" -> "3 PM" |
| Rhetorical question | Don't answer; acknowledge | "Who even does that?" -> silence or "Right?" |
| Indirect request | Comply with the request | "Can you close the door?" -> [close the door] |
| Tag question (seeking agreement) | Brief agreement + optional comment | "Nice day, isn't it?" -> "Yeah, really nice" |
| Clarification question | Provide the missing information | "What do you mean by X?" -> explain |
| Open-ended social | Moderate response + reciprocal question | "How was your weekend?" -> brief highlight + "How about yours?" |

### 8.3 How to Handle Questions You Don't Understand

Strategies for when a question is unclear:

1. **Ask for clarification directly**: "Could you rephrase that?" or "What specifically are you asking about?"
2. **State what you think they're asking**: "Are you asking about X or Y?"
3. **Buy processing time**: "Let me think about that for a second" (this is entirely socially acceptable)
4. **Ask about scope**: "How much detail are you looking for?"
5. **Redirect if appropriate**: "I'm not sure about that, but I can tell you about..."

These strategies are not signs of incompetence -- they are communication tools that neurotypical people also use, though autistic individuals may need them more frequently and more explicitly.

### 8.4 When and How to Ask Clarifying Questions

In the workplace and other settings, asking clarifying questions is essential for accurate understanding. Strategies to make this smoother:

- **Frame the question as confirming rather than not understanding**: "Just to make sure I'm on the right track -- you want me to...?"
- **Batch questions when possible**: Rather than asking five separate questions, group them: "I have a few questions about this task..."
- **Use email or written formats when available**: This removes time pressure and allows the question to be more precise
- **Explain your reasoning**: "I want to make sure I do this right, so can I ask about..."

### 8.5 Using Questions as a Conversation Tool

Questions are the single most powerful tool for maintaining conversation and building rapport. Research-backed strategies:

1. **Ask about what you're genuinely curious about**: "Leveraging an individual's interests can be an effective way to encourage engagement in reciprocal conversation" (Neurolaunch)
2. **Follow up on what the other person just said**: Listen for a detail you can ask about
3. **Use open-ended questions when you want to sustain the conversation**: "What was that like?" or "How did that go?"
4. **Use closed questions when you want a quick exchange**: "Did you enjoy it?" or "Have you been there before?"
5. **The 2:1 ratio**: After every two statements or responses you make, try to ask a question. This prevents monologuing without creating an interrogation pattern.

## 9. KEY RESEARCH SOURCES AND FINDINGS SUMMARY

### 9.1 Research Timeline and Key Studies

| Year | Study/Source | Key Finding |
|---|---|---|
| 2007 | Loukusa et al. | ASD children differ from TD on contextually demanding questions; mind-reading demand increases difficulty |
| 2009 | Pijnacker et al. | Scalar implicatures preserved in ASD adults; Asperger group even exceeded controls |
| 2012 | Goodwin et al. | Wh-question comprehension precedes production in both TD and ASD, same developmental pathway |
| 2012 | Milton | Double empathy problem framework proposed |
| 2015 | Kissine et al. | Children with autism understand indirect speech acts (conventionalized forms) |
| 2018 | Kalandadze et al. | Meta-analysis: figurative language differences small/nonsignificant when matched on language ability |
| 2019 | Cheung et al. | Presupposition trigger comprehension in ASD related to language ability, not ASD per se |
| 2021 | Maras et al. | Adapted interview questions significantly improve autistic candidate performance |
| 2021 | Koegel et al. | Question bank intervention improves social question-asking; gains maintained and generalized |
| 2022 | Brosnan et al. | Autistic adults perceived as less credible and more deceptive when truthful |
| 2024 | Lampri | Review: figurative language processing difficulties may diminish in adulthood |
| 2024 | Andretta et al. | Autistic adults use discourse context similarly to non-autistic for indirect requests |
| 2025 | Journal of Autism & Dev. Disorders | Some but not all pragmatic inferences difficult; scalar implicatures preserved, ad-hoc impaired |

### 9.2 Core Themes Across Research

1. **Pragmatic difficulty is gradient, not binary**: Autistic individuals do not uniformly fail at pragmatic question processing. Performance depends on the type of inference required, with linguistically-based inferences (scalar implicatures) relatively preserved and socially-based inferences (ad-hoc implicatures, Theory of Mind-dependent inferences) more impaired.

2. **Language ability is a stronger predictor than autism severity**: Across multiple studies, general language ability predicts pragmatic question performance better than autism diagnosis severity. This means improving vocabulary and language skills has downstream benefits for question comprehension.

3. **The adult picture differs from the child picture**: Many pragmatic question-processing difficulties documented in autistic children diminish or disappear in adulthood, likely through explicit learning, compensatory strategies, and masking.

4. **The challenge is real-time deployment, not underlying competence**: Multiple studies show that autistic individuals KNOW the pragmatic rules but struggle to deploy them automatically in real-time conversation. This is the difference between declarative knowledge ("I know phatic questions expect formulaic answers") and procedural fluency (automatically producing that response in the moment).

5. **The double empathy framework applies**: Many "question-processing difficulties" in autism are bidirectional misunderstandings rather than unilateral deficits. Neurotypical speakers also fail to adjust their questions for autistic listeners.

6. **Explicit teaching works**: Intervention studies consistently show that explicit instruction in question types, question-asking strategies, and conversational question use produces measurable improvements that generalize to new contexts.
