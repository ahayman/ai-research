# Concept Research: Existing Symbol Communication Systems — AAC, PECS, Widgit, and Makaton

## Overview

Before proposing a new visual communication system, it is essential to understand the ones that already exist and serve millions of people daily. Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC) encompasses the methods and technologies that supplement or replace speech for people who cannot rely on verbal language—including individuals with cerebral palsy, autism, intellectual disabilities, aphasia, stroke, and progressive neurological conditions. The symbol systems at the heart of AAC represent decades of real-world deployment, iterative design, empirical testing, and accumulated practical wisdom about what actually works when humans communicate through visual symbols.

The major symbol systems—PECS (Picture Exchange Communication System), PCS (Picture Communication Symbols), Widgit, Makaton, SymbolStix, and Blissymbolics—each embody different design philosophies and trade-offs. PECS is not primarily a symbol system but a behavioral teaching methodology that uses any symbols to teach the pragmatics of communication (initiating, requesting, commenting). PCS, the most widely used symbol set worldwide, contains over 45,000 symbols optimized for pictorial clarity and rapid recognition. Widgit, dominant in UK education, uses a rule-based schematic structure where symbols are designed according to consistent visual principles. Makaton combines signs, symbols, and speech in a multimodal approach organized into staged vocabulary levels, starting with the most immediately functional concepts. Blissymbolics, the most linguistically sophisticated system, uses approximately 120 base elements to compose over 6,500 symbols through semantic composition rules—the closest existing analogue to what a visual iconographic language would attempt at greater scale.

The field's accumulated knowledge includes a critical insight: core vocabulary research has established that approximately 200–400 high-frequency, multipurpose words account for 80% of everyday communication. These core words are primarily verbs, adjectives, pronouns, and prepositions—not nouns. This finding inverts the naive assumption that a symbol system should start with concrete object names (apple, car, dog) and instead foregrounds the functional words that make sentences possible (want, go, more, not, help, like). Any visual language project that ignores this evidence will repeat mistakes that AAC has already learned to avoid.

## Core Components

### Component 1: PECS — Picture Exchange Communication System

- **Purpose**: A behavioral teaching methodology for developing functional communication skills, particularly for individuals with autism who do not initiate communication spontaneously.
- **Creator**: Andy Bondy and Lori Frost, developed in 1985 at the Delaware Autistic Program.
- **Key Feature**: PECS is not a symbol set—it is a teaching protocol that can use any pictures or symbols. Its innovation is in the pragmatics of communication, not the design of symbols.
- **Six Phases**:
  1. **Physical Exchange**: Learner gives a picture to a communication partner to request a desired item. No verbal prompts used.
  2. **Distance and Persistence**: Learner travels to the communication book and to the partner to make requests.
  3. **Picture Discrimination**: Learner selects from multiple pictures to request specific items.
  4. **Sentence Structure**: Learner constructs sentences using a "I want" starter strip followed by picture selections.
  5. **Responding**: Learner answers "What do you want?" using the sentence strip.
  6. **Commenting**: Learner makes spontaneous comments, not just requests ("I see..." "I hear...").
- **Strengths**: Teaches communicative intent and initiative, not just symbol recognition. Empirically validated as evidence-based for autism (strong research base). Focuses on functional communication from the first session.
- **Limitations**: Primarily supports requesting behavior; less effective for narrative, explanation, or abstract communication. Dependent on physical picture cards (though digital adaptations exist). Does not teach compositional grammar.
- **Relevance to Visual Language**: PECS demonstrates that symbol-based communication can be taught effectively to individuals traditionally considered "nonverbal." Its six-phase progression from simple exchange to sentence construction to commenting provides a model for staged learning in any visual communication system. The critical lesson: start with functional communication (requests, basic social exchange), not with vocabulary breadth.

### Component 2: PCS — Picture Communication Symbols (Tobii Dynavox)

- **Purpose**: The dominant symbol set in AAC worldwide, providing a comprehensive vocabulary of pictorial symbols for communication boards, devices, and software.
- **Creator**: Originally developed by Roxana Mayer-Johnson in 1981; now owned and maintained by Tobii Dynavox.
- **Scale**: Over 45,000 symbols covering a wide vocabulary. Used in major AAC software including Proloquo2Go, TouchChat, LAMP Words for Life, and Tobii Dynavox's own platforms.
- **Design Philosophy**: Pictorial clarity. PCS symbols are designed to be visually intuitive—a picture of a cup for "cup," a picture of a person running for "run." Symbols are available in color or monochrome.
- **Market Dominance**: PCS symbols appear in 6 of the top 13+ iOS symbol-based AAC apps. Described as the most widely used AAC symbol set globally, having been used by "millions of students and adults" over 30+ years.
- **Strengths**: Wide vocabulary coverage. High pictorial clarity for concrete concepts. Extensive software integration. Long track record of real-world use.
- **Limitations**:
  - Primarily English-centric in vocabulary organization (though translations exist).
  - Limited compositionality—symbols are whole-word pictures, not composed from sub-elements.
  - Abstract concepts (if, because, however, although) are inherently harder to represent pictorially.
  - Dependence on communication partner familiarity—a PCS user cannot communicate with someone who has never seen the symbols.
  - Cultural specificity: symbols depicting everyday objects (mailbox shape, phone handset shape) may not be universal.
- **Relevance to Visual Language**: PCS represents the "maximum pictoriality" design philosophy—make each symbol as picture-like as possible. This works well for concrete nouns and simple verbs but breaks down for abstract and grammatical concepts. A visual language based on image schemas and metaphor would take a fundamentally different approach—representing conceptual structure rather than surface appearance.

### Component 3: Widgit Symbols

- **Purpose**: A systematic, rule-based symbol set designed to support literacy and make information accessible, particularly strong in UK educational settings.
- **History**: Originally developed as Rebus Symbols; rebranded as Widgit. Developed specifically to support reading and writing, not just communication.
- **Scale**: Over 12,000 symbols covering 40,000+ English vocabulary items.
- **Design Philosophy**: Rule-based schematic structure. Unlike PCS's pictorial approach, Widgit uses consistent visual rules: similar concepts share visual elements, related words show visible relationships. This schematic structure makes learning easier once the rules are understood—users can predict what unfamiliar symbols mean by recognizing familiar sub-elements.
- **Key Innovation**: The rule-based approach means Widgit symbols are partially compositional. Users who learn the visual conventions can generalize to new symbols they have not explicitly been taught.
- **Usage**: Widely used in UK schools, learning disability services, and accessible information production. Used with Widgit software (InPrint, SymWriter, Widgit Online).
- **Strengths**: Systematic visual structure aids learning. Strong literacy connection. Wide vocabulary. Consistent design conventions.
- **Limitations**: More visually complex than PCS or Makaton symbols—requires more processing effort. Still primarily English-centric in vocabulary organization. Limited compositionality compared to Blissymbolics.
- **Relevance to Visual Language**: Widgit's rule-based approach is the closest among major AAC symbol sets to what a visual iconographic language would do. The principle that visual rules make unfamiliar symbols partially predictable is exactly the compositionality principle that a metaphor-based visual language aims to implement at greater scale.

### Component 4: Makaton

- **Purpose**: A multimodal communication program using speech, signs (based on BSL), and symbols together, designed for people with communication and learning difficulties.
- **Creators**: Margaret Walker, Kathy Johnston, and Tony Cornforth, developed in the 1970s at the Royal Association in Aid of Deaf People.
- **Multimodal Approach**: Makaton's distinctive feature is that it uses three modes simultaneously:
  1. **Speech**: Users (and partners) speak in full sentences.
  2. **Signs**: Key words are signed using signs derived from BSL (British Sign Language).
  3. **Symbols**: Pictures/symbols reinforce the spoken and signed message.
  - This multi-channel reinforcement means information is encoded through auditory, visual-gestural, and visual-graphic channels simultaneously.
- **Vocabulary Structure**:
  - **Core Vocabulary**: Approximately 450 concepts organized into 8 stages of increasing complexity.
    - Stage 1: Immediate functional needs—eat, drink, more, stop, yes, no.
    - Later stages: Time, emotions, social concepts, abstract ideas.
  - **Resource Vocabulary**: Over 11,000 additional concepts for specialized needs.
- **Users**: Over 1 million users worldwide, including individuals with cognitive impairments, autism, Down syndrome, specific language impairment, multisensory impairment, and acquired neurological disorders (stroke, dementia).
- **Training**: Offered in levels 1–4, with the expectation that communication partners also learn Makaton to create an effective communication environment.
- **Strengths**: Multimodal reinforcement leverages dual/triple coding. Staged learning path is well-structured. Widely adopted and well-supported. The multi-channel approach means that even partial success in one modality (e.g., a sign without the symbol, or a symbol without the sign) can carry meaning.
- **Limitations**: Core vocabulary of 450 concepts is limited for complex communication. Primarily developed for UK English contexts. Signs are based on BSL and may not transfer to other sign language communities. Not designed for compositional sentence-building at natural language complexity.
- **Relevance to Visual Language**: Makaton's staged learning progression (start with the most functional concepts, expand gradually) is a proven model for how to introduce a symbol-based communication system. The core vocabulary of 450 concepts aligns with AAC research showing that 200–400 words cover 80% of daily communication—providing a target for a visual language's minimum viable vocabulary. The multimodal approach demonstrates the power of dual/triple coding—a visual language used alongside speech and gesture could be more effective than a visual-only system.

### Component 5: Blissymbolics

- **Purpose**: The most linguistically sophisticated AAC symbol system, using compositional semantic rules to build an open-ended vocabulary from approximately 120 base elements.
- **Creator**: Charles K. Bliss (originally Karl Blitz), developed in 1949 as "Semantography"—a universal visual language. Adopted for AAC in 1971 by Shirley McNaughton at the Ontario Crippled Children's Centre.
- **Scale**: Over 6,500 graphic symbols composed from approximately 120 basic elements. Used in 33+ countries.
- **Design Philosophy**: Semantic composition from geometric primitives. Base elements include geometric shapes (circle, square, line, point, angle), positional modifiers (above, below, forward, backward), and indicator symbols (action, thing, evaluation). Complex symbols are built by combining these elements according to compositional rules.
- **Example Compositions**:
  - "House" = enclosure (roof shape + walls)
  - "Protection" = house + person inside
  - "Feeling" = heart symbol
  - "Happy" = heart + up (feeling up)
  - "Sad" = heart + down (feeling down)
- **Indicator System**: Bliss uses indicators to mark grammatical categories:
  - Action indicator (inverted V above) marks verbs
  - Thing indicator (small square) marks nouns
  - Description indicator (small V) marks adjectives
  - Evaluation indicators mark positive/negative valence
- **Strengths**: True compositional semantics—users can create novel symbols by combining base elements. Language-independent (not tied to any spoken language's vocabulary). The most linguistically powerful AAC symbol system. Provides scaffolding for understanding language structure—users learn composition, syntax, and grammar through symbol use.
- **Limitations**:
  - Interpretive ambiguity: Bliss did not provide systematic definitions for all symbol combinations, leading to interpretation disputes.
  - Higher learning burden than pictorial systems (PCS, SymbolStix)—base elements are abstract.
  - Dependence on human interpreters for unfamiliar readers.
  - Not encoded in Unicode (as of 2009), limiting digital integration.
  - Limited adoption compared to PCS—more linguistically powerful but harder to learn.
- **Relevance to Visual Language**: Blissymbolics is the most direct historical precedent for the visual iconographic language project. It proves that compositional visual semantics can work. Its limitations reveal the challenges: the learning burden of abstract base elements, the difficulty of maintaining consistent interpretation without formal standards, and the market dominance of simpler pictorial systems. A metaphor-based visual language would aim for Blissymbolics' compositional power with better grounding (image schemas and primary metaphors rather than arbitrary geometric shapes).

### Component 6: SymbolStix and Other Symbol Sets

- **Purpose**: Additional symbol sets serving the AAC market with different design philosophies.
- **SymbolStix**: Evidence-based symbol set with over 15,000 symbols. Described as "the most complete and current symbol set available." Used in Proloquo2Go, PRC-Saltillo devices, Tobii Dynavox devices, and other platforms. Features a stick-figure style that emphasizes action and context.
- **Mulberry Symbols**: An open-source symbol set with approximately 3,400 symbols, designed for adult AAC users (avoiding the childish appearance of some AAC symbols).
- **ARASAAC**: Aragonese Centre for Augmentative and Alternative Communication symbols. Open-source, widely used in Spanish-speaking countries and increasingly globally. Over 12,000 symbols.
- **Comparison Points**: Symbol sets vary on dimensions of: iconicity (how pictorial), schematicity (how rule-based), compositionality (can you build new from old), vocabulary size, cultural specificity, age-appropriateness, and cost/openness.

### Component 7: Core Vocabulary Research

- **Purpose**: Empirical research identifying which words are most essential for communication—the foundation for any symbol system's vocabulary priorities.
- **Key Findings**:
  - Approximately 50 words account for 40–50% of daily communication.
  - 100 words account for ~60% of daily communication.
  - 200–400 words account for ~80% of daily communication.
  - 203 high-frequency words showed 80.62% commonality across speakers.
  - Core words are primarily **verbs, adjectives, pronouns, and prepositions**—NOT nouns.
  - Core vocabulary is remarkably consistent across ages, environments, activities, and speakers.
  - Fringe vocabulary (nouns, proper names, topic-specific terms) is highly variable across individuals and contexts.
- **Implications**:
  - A visual language should prioritize grammatical and relational words (want, go, more, not, help, like, is, can, with) before concrete nouns.
  - The first 200 symbols should cover core vocabulary—the functional words that make sentences possible.
  - Noun vocabulary should be expandable and personalized (fringe vocabulary) while core vocabulary remains standardized.
  - This finding is counterintuitive: most people assume a visual language should start with easy-to-picture concrete objects, but the research shows that the most communicatively essential words are abstract, relational, and functional.

## How It Works

### Step 1: Assessment and Vocabulary Selection

An AAC team (speech-language pathologist, user, family, educators) assesses communication needs and selects an appropriate symbol system and vocabulary:
- Core vocabulary (standardized high-frequency words) forms the base.
- Fringe vocabulary (personalized nouns, names, topic-specific terms) is added based on individual needs.
- Vocabulary organization: grid-based (fixed positions for quick motor access) or visual-scene-based (contextual photographs with hotspots).

### Step 2: Symbol Presentation

Symbols are presented on:
- **Low-tech**: Communication boards, picture books, PECS binders.
- **Mid-tech**: Simple voice-output devices, pre-recorded message buttons.
- **High-tech**: Tablets/iPads with AAC software, dedicated speech-generating devices (SGDs), eye-tracking systems for users with severe motor impairments.
- Tobii Dynavox devices integrate eye-tracking input with symbol-based AAC software.

### Step 3: Communication Act

The user selects symbols to construct messages:
- Simple selection: Pointing to a single symbol ("drink") to request.
- Sentence construction: Selecting multiple symbols in sequence ("I + want + more + juice") on a sentence strip.
- Aided language stimulation: Communication partners model symbol use by pointing to symbols while speaking, reinforcing the connection between symbols and meaning.

### Step 4: Skill Development

Communication skills develop through:
- PECS-style structured teaching (pragmatic communication phases).
- Modeling by communication partners (aided language stimulation).
- Expanding vocabulary as competence grows (Makaton's staged approach).
- Developing compositional skills where the system supports it (Blissymbolics).

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: Core Vocabulary in Action — Why "Want" Matters More Than "Apple"

- **Scenario**: A child learning to communicate using symbols needs to be able to express desires, refusals, and comments.
- **Illustration**: With only the word "apple," a child can label an apple. With the word "want," a child can request anything ("want apple," "want drink," "want go," "want more"). Core vocabulary research shows "want" is among the top 50 most frequent words; "apple" rarely appears in the top 1,000. A visual language that prioritizes concrete nouns over functional words would cripple its users' communicative power.
- **Key Insight**: Symbol systems (and visual languages) should be designed around communicative function, not pictorial ease. The hardest-to-picture words are the most communicatively essential.

### Example 2: Blissymbolics Composition — "Happy" and "Sad" as Heart + Direction

- **Scenario**: A Blissymbolics user needs to express emotions.
- **Illustration**: The base element for "feeling" is the heart symbol. To create "happy," the heart is combined with an upward indicator. To create "sad," the heart is combined with a downward indicator. This uses the primary metaphor HAPPY IS UP / SAD IS DOWN—the same metaphorical grounding that the visual iconographic language project proposes as its foundation.
- **Key Insight**: Blissymbolics already uses some metaphorical grounding (up = positive, down = negative). The visual iconographic language would systematize this, building the entire composition system on documented primary metaphors and image schemas rather than ad hoc geometric combinations.

### Example 3: The Widgit Rule System — Predicting Unfamiliar Symbols

- **Scenario**: A Widgit user encounters a symbol they have never seen before.
- **Illustration**: Widgit uses consistent visual rules. For example, symbols related to communication share a specific visual element (mouth/speech marks). A user who knows this convention can look at an unfamiliar symbol containing speech marks and correctly infer it relates to communication, then use the other visual elements to narrow down the specific meaning.
- **Key Insight**: Rule-based design enables generalization—users can partially interpret novel symbols by recognizing familiar sub-elements. This is the compositionality principle in action, and it is what distinguishes Widgit from the purely pictorial approach of PCS.

### Example 4: Technology Integration — Eye-Tracking AAC

- **Scenario**: An individual with severe motor impairments (e.g., locked-in syndrome or advanced ALS) uses eye-tracking technology to select symbols.
- **Illustration**: A Tobii Dynavox eye-tracking device monitors the user's gaze. The display shows a grid of symbols. The user fixates on a symbol for a dwell time (typically 0.5–1 second) to select it. Selected symbols are arranged into sentences and spoken aloud by the synthesizer. The system can handle thousands of symbols organized in navigable grids.
- **Key Insight**: Any new visual language must be compatible with existing AAC input methods—touch, switch scanning, eye tracking, head tracking. Digital-first design is not optional; it is a requirements constraint.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: AAC symbol systems are "not real language" — they are simplified communication aids.
  - **Reality**: While most AAC symbol systems are less expressive than natural language, Blissymbolics demonstrates genuine compositional semantics. AAC research increasingly recognizes that the limitation is often in the symbol system's design, not in the users' cognitive capacity.

- **Misconception**: Symbol-based communication is only for nonverbal individuals.
  - **Reality**: Many verbal individuals benefit from visual symbol supports—including autistic individuals with verbal processing difficulties, individuals with aphasia, people with intellectual disabilities, and anyone in situations where speech is unavailable or ineffective.

- **Misconception**: Pictorial symbols (like PCS) are universally understood without training.
  - **Reality**: Even highly pictorial symbols require learning. Research shows that symbol recognition rates vary significantly across cultures, ages, and cognitive profiles. "Universal" recognizability is a design goal, not a given.

- **Misconception**: Using symbol-based communication will prevent or delay speech development.
  - **Reality**: Research consistently shows that AAC use does not inhibit speech development and may actually facilitate it. Makaton's multimodal approach explicitly aims to support speech development.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **Literacy bridge**: Some AAC users transition from symbols to text-based communication as literacy develops. This trajectory—symbols as a bridge to text—is common in educational settings and suggests that a visual language might serve a similar transitional function.
- **Bilingual AAC**: Users from multilingual households may need symbols that are not tied to any specific language's vocabulary organization. This is where language-independent systems like Blissymbolics have an advantage over English-centric systems like PCS.
- **Adult AAC users**: Many AAC symbol systems were designed with children in mind. Adult users—including those who acquire communication disabilities through stroke, TBI, or ALS—have different vocabulary needs, aesthetic preferences, and functional requirements. The Mulberry symbol set was specifically designed to address this gap.
- **High-literacy AAC users**: Some individuals who cannot speak have full literacy. For them, text-based AAC (typing + speech synthesis) is more efficient than symbol-based systems. This highlights that visual symbols are most valuable when they serve users who cannot or prefer not to access written language.

## Related Concepts

- **Chinese Radical Composition** (Category 7): Blissymbolics' base-element composition system is analogous to Chinese radical composition, but with ~120 base elements rather than 214 radicals. Both demonstrate that a finite element inventory can generate an open-ended vocabulary.
- **Sign Language Lessons** (Category 8): Makaton's use of BSL-derived signs alongside symbols demonstrates the power of multimodal communication. Sign language's spatial grammar offers possibilities that current AAC symbol systems do not exploit.
- **Cognitive Science of Visual Processing** (Category 9): Symbol design principles in AAC are grounded in visual cognition research—iconicity, distinctiveness, and cognitive load considerations directly apply.
- **Visual Thinking and Autism** (Category 11): AAC research specifically for autistic individuals demonstrates both the potential and the limitations of current symbol systems for visual-spatial thinkers.
- **Semantic Primitives / NSM** (Category 3): Core vocabulary research aligns with NSM's semantic primes—both identify a small set of high-frequency, universal concepts that are essential for communication.
- **Requirements Engineering** (Category 12): AAC deployment experience provides critical data for the feasibility assessment—what works, what fails, and what the real-world constraints are.

## Research Gaps

- **Comparative efficacy studies**: Head-to-head comparisons of different symbol systems (PCS vs. Widgit vs. Blissymbolics vs. SymbolStix) for the same user populations are rare. Most evidence is for individual systems rather than comparative.
- **Compositionality in AAC outcomes**: Does using a compositional symbol system (Blissymbolics) lead to better language development outcomes than using a pictorial system (PCS)? Limited data.
- **Cross-cultural symbol recognition**: How do AAC symbols designed in Western contexts perform in non-Western cultures? Most AAC research is conducted in English-speaking countries.
- **Adult AAC vocabulary**: Core vocabulary research is primarily based on children's communication. Adult core vocabulary may differ, particularly for workplace and social contexts.
- **Digital input optimization**: How should symbol-based communication systems be redesigned for modern touchscreen and eye-tracking interfaces? Much of the underlying research predates the iPad era.
