# Category 18: Pragmatics of Refusal, Disagreement, and Conflict

## The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

---

## How to Read This Document

In English, saying "yes" is simple. Saying "no" is a performance.

This isn't an exaggeration. Conversation analysts have demonstrated that agreement is structurally **preferred** (delivered quickly, simply, without elaboration) while disagreement is **dispreferred** (requiring delay, hedging, accounts, apologies, and alternatives). The same content -- "I can't come" -- lands completely differently depending on whether it's delivered bare or wrapped in the expected social packaging.

This category covers the unified system of **saying things people don't want to hear**: refusing, disagreeing, complaining, apologizing, and navigating conflict. These are among the most socially consequential speech acts in English, and they're where the gap between autistic directness and NT social expectations creates the most friction.

---

## 1. The Fundamental Asymmetry: Yes Is Easy, No Is Hard

| Response Type | How It's Delivered | Example |
|--------------|-------------------|---------|
| **Preferred** (agreement, acceptance) | Quick, simple, direct, no elaboration needed | "Sure!" / "Sounds great!" / "Absolutely!" |
| **Dispreferred** (refusal, disagreement) | Delayed, hedged, padded with appreciation + account + apology + alternative | "Oh... that sounds great, but I have plans. I'm sorry. Maybe next week?" |

This asymmetry is **structural**, not personal. It's built into how English conversation works. The padding on dispreferred responses is not optional social decoration -- it's the **expected packaging** that signals "I would say yes if I could; I respect you enough to explain why I can't."

**The autistic friction point**: Many autistic individuals produce "bare" dispreferred responses -- "No" or "I can't" without the expected padding. This is interpreted as hostile, rude, or dismissive -- not because the refusal itself is wrong, but because the **missing packaging** signals "I don't care enough to soften this for you."

---

## 2. The Standard Refusal Sequence

The expected structure of a refusal in English:

```
1. DELAY         "Oh..." / "Hmm..." / [pause]
2. APPRECIATION   "That sounds really fun..." / "Thanks for asking..."
3. REGRET        "...but I'm sorry..."
4. ACCOUNT       "...I already have plans that evening."
5. ALTERNATIVE   "Maybe next week?" / "Rain check?"
```

**What each part does:**

| Component | Social Function | What Happens If Missing |
|-----------|----------------|------------------------|
| Delay | Signals this isn't an easy "no" -- you're deliberating | Instant "no" sounds pre-decided, dismissive |
| Appreciation | Validates the invitation/request itself | Feels like the invitation wasn't valued |
| Regret | Signals you WISH you could say yes | Feels like you don't care |
| Account | Provides a REASON (face-saving for both parties) | Feels arbitrary -- "no because I don't want to" |
| Alternative | Shows continued investment in the relationship | Feels like a permanent rejection, not a scheduling conflict |

**The hierarchy of accounts (excuses):**
1. **External obligation**: "I have a work commitment" (strongest -- forces outside your control)
2. **Prior commitment**: "I already have plans" (strong -- you'd come if you could)
3. **Health/energy**: "I'm not feeling great" (moderate -- legitimate but vaguer)
4. **Preference**: "I'm not really into that" (weakest -- threatens positive face)

## 3. The Disagreement Spectrum

From most direct to most indirect:

| Level | What You Say | When to Use |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| **0 (Bald)** | "You're wrong." | Almost never in English (emergencies, very close equals) |
| **1 (Direct)** | "I don't think that's right." | Close friends, low-stakes topics |
| **2 (Hedged)** | "I think there might be another way to look at it." | Peers, colleagues |
| **3 (Partial agreement)** | "I see your point, but I think..." | Standard professional disagreement |
| **4 (Question form)** | "Have you considered...?" / "What about...?" | When you want to challenge without confronting |
| **5 (Maximum softening)** | "That's an interesting perspective. I wonder if we might also consider..." | Boss, authority, sensitive situations |

### 3.1 The "Yes, But..." Pattern

The most common disagreement structure in English. "But" is a conventional implicature trigger (Category 2) -- it cancels whatever came before:

- "I see what you mean, **but** I think..." = I disagree
- "That's a fair point, **but** have you considered..." = I disagree
- "You're not wrong, **but**..." = I disagree

**The rule**: Everything before "but" is face-saving packaging. Everything after "but" is the real message.

### 3.2 Disagreement Markers

These phrases signal disagreement is coming (Category 5: discourse markers):

| Marker | What It Signals | Strength |
|--------|----------------|----------|
| "Well..." | Dispreferred response incoming | Mild |
| "I see what you mean, but..." | Token agreement → disagreement | Moderate |
| "To be fair..." | Introducing a counterpoint | Moderate |
| "With all due respect..." | Strong disagreement incoming (despite the words) | Strong |
| "I don't know about that..." | Doubt/disagreement | Moderate |
| "That's one way to look at it..." | "I have a different way" (Category 2: Quantity implicature) | Moderate |
| "Actually..." | Correction incoming | Moderate-strong |
| "I take your point, however..." | Formal disagreement | Moderate |

## 4. Apology: The Multi-Function Speech Act

"Sorry" performs at least four different speech acts:

| Type | Example | Function | Sincerity Required? |
|------|---------|----------|-------------------|
| **Genuine apology** | "I'm sorry I forgot your birthday." | Expressing real regret + accepting responsibility | Yes |
| **Sympathetic "sorry"** | "I'm sorry for your loss." | Expressing compassion (NOT admitting fault) | Feeling matters, not fault |
| **Phatic "sorry"** | "Sorry!" (bumping into someone) | Social lubricant (no real fault) | No -- it's a ritual |
| **Strategic "sorry"** | "I'm sorry if you were offended." | Managing a situation without accepting blame | No -- it's performative |

### 4.1 The Full Apology Template

When a genuine apology is needed:

```
1. ACKNOWLEDGMENT   "I [specific thing I did]."
2. RESPONSIBILITY   "That was my mistake." / "I should have..."
3. IMPACT           "I understand that [how it affected them]."
4. EXPLANATION      "[Brief, non-excuse reason]." (NOT "but")
5. REPAIR           "Here's what I'm doing to fix it: [action]."
6. NON-RECURRENCE   "I'll [specific change] going forward."
```

**Example**: "I'm sorry I forgot to send you the report. That was my oversight. I know it put you in a difficult position with the client. I got behind this week, but that's not an excuse. I've sent it now and I'm setting up a reminder system so it won't happen again."

### 4.2 The Non-Apology

These LOOK like apologies but aren't:

| Non-Apology | Why It's Not an Apology |
|-------------|------------------------|
| "I'm sorry you feel that way." | Blames the recipient's feelings, not the speaker's actions |
| "I'm sorry if anyone was offended." | Conditional + vague → no actual acknowledgment |
| "Mistakes were made." | Passive voice → no one accepts responsibility |
| "I'm sorry, but..." | The "but" cancels the apology |
| "I'm sorry you took it that way." | Blames the recipient's interpretation |

### 4.3 When You Don't Think You Were Wrong

Sometimes the social situation requires acknowledgment even when you don't believe you did anything wrong:

| Strategy | Example | What It Does |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| **Acknowledge impact** | "I can see how that came across. That wasn't my intention." | Validates their experience without admitting fault |
| **Express regret for outcome** | "I'm sorry this situation happened." | Regrets the outcome, not the action |
| **Offer repair** | "What can I do to make this better?" | Forward-looking, not backward-blaming |
| **Acknowledge perspective gap** | "I didn't realize it would land that way. I'll be more careful." | Acknowledges the miscommunication |

## 5. Conflict: Escalation and De-Escalation

### 5.1 Escalation Signals

When conflict is getting worse, these markers appear:

| Signal | What It Looks Like | What It Means |
|--------|-------------------|---------------|
| **Hedges disappearing** | "I think you might be..." → "You ARE..." | Face-saving is being abandoned |
| **Volume increasing** | Getting louder | Emotional intensity rising |
| **Absolutist language** | "You ALWAYS..." / "You NEVER..." | Generalizing from specific to character |
| **Register shifting up** | Suddenly formal | Creating distance (anger or authority) |
| **Personal attacks** | "This isn't about the report, it's about your attitude." | Shifting from issue to person |
| **Sarcasm entering** | "Oh, THAT'S helpful." | Contempt emerging |
| **Name shifting** | First name → full name → title | Formality = distance = anger |
| **Repetition** | Repeating the same point louder | Feeling unheard |

### 5.2 De-Escalation Phrases

| Category | Example Phrases |
|----------|----------------|
| **Acknowledging** | "I hear you." / "I can see this matters to you." / "You're making a fair point." |
| **Pausing** | "Can we take a breath?" / "I want to respond to that thoughtfully -- give me a second." |
| **Finding common ground** | "We both want [shared goal]." / "I think we agree on the problem, just not the solution." |
| **Taking responsibility** | "I think I may have contributed to this." / "Let me reconsider what you said." |
| **Proposing a break** | "Can we come back to this in an hour?" / "I need to step away for a few minutes." |
| **Redirecting** | "What would a good outcome look like for you?" / "How can we move forward?" |

### 5.3 The "Agree to Disagree" Convention

When to use it:
- The disagreement is about values/preferences, not facts
- Further discussion won't change either position
- The relationship matters more than winning the point

**Scripts:**
- "I think we see this differently, and that's OK."
- "We might not agree on this, but I respect your perspective."
- "I don't think either of us is going to convince the other. Can we leave it here?"

**When NOT to use it:**
- When the issue requires a decision (someone has to choose)
- When safety is involved
- When it's being used to shut down legitimate concerns

## 6. The Autistic Experience

### 6.1 The "Bare No"

Many autistic individuals produce refusals without the expected padding. "No" or "I can't" without delay, appreciation, account, apology, or alternative. This is NOT rudeness -- it's a factually complete answer to the question. But the missing packaging is interpreted as hostile.

**Why it happens:**
- **Processing load**: Generating the full sequence in real time is cognitively expensive
- **Authenticity**: Fabricating or exaggerating reasons feels dishonest
- **Literal processing**: "Can you come?" → "No" is a complete, accurate answer
- **Not perceiving the need**: The padding is invisible if you don't know it's expected

### 6.2 The Correction Reflex

Autistic individuals often prioritize **accuracy over face-saving** when disagreeing. If someone says something factually wrong, the autistic response may be direct correction -- without hedging, softening, or face-work.

| Autistic Intent | NT Reception |
|----------------|-------------|
| "I'm helping by correcting this error" | "They're being rude/arrogant" |
| "Accuracy matters" | "They don't care about my feelings" |
| "This is a collaborative correction" | "This is a public humiliation" |

### 6.3 Apology Challenges

| Challenge | What Happens |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Not understanding what was wrong** | Can't apologize for something you don't see as an offense |
| **Sincerity conflict** | Apologizing without genuine regret feels dishonest |
| **Performative apology** | "Sorry" as social lubricant rather than genuine regret is confusing |
| **Over-apologizing** | Some autistic individuals apologize constantly (trauma, masking, chronic correction) |
| **Alexithymia** | Difficulty identifying your own emotions makes expressing regret harder |

### 6.4 The Justice Orientation

Many autistic individuals have a strong sense of fairness and rules. When rules are violated, the impulse is to name the violation -- which can read as confrontation:

- "That's not fair" (accurate assessment, reads as complaint)
- "That's not what we agreed" (factual statement, reads as accusation)
- "You said you would" (reminder of commitment, reads as attack)

The content is often correct; the social packaging is what creates friction.

### 6.5 When Directness Is a Strength

| Context | Why Direct Refusal/Disagreement Is Valued |
|---------|------------------------------------------|
| Consent and boundaries | Clear "no" is essential, not rude |
| Safety situations | Hedging could be dangerous |
| Technical/professional review | Accuracy trumps face-saving |
| Honest feedback (when invited) | People asked for honesty and got it |
| Resisting manipulation | Directness defeats coercive politeness tactics |

## 7. Practical Scripts

### 7.1 Refusal Templates

**Declining an invitation (standard):**
> "That sounds really nice, [thanks for asking / I appreciate the invite]. Unfortunately, I [reason]. I'm sorry! [Alternative: Maybe next time? / Let me know about the next one!]"

**Declining at work (to your boss):**
> "I understand the priority. Currently I'm working on [X] and [Y] which are due [when]. I want to make sure I do quality work -- could we discuss which to prioritize, or adjust the timeline?"

**Declining a date/romantic interest:**
> "I really appreciate you asking. I don't think I'm the right fit, but I think you're [genuine compliment]. I hope you find someone great."

### 7.2 Disagreement Templates

**Standard professional disagreement:**
> "I see where you're coming from. I have a slightly different perspective -- I think [your view] because [reason]. What do you think?"

**Disagreeing with a boss:**
> "I appreciate the direction. I wanted to raise a potential concern about [specific issue] -- [brief reasoning]. Would it be worth considering [alternative]?"

**Disagreeing with a friend:**
> "Hmm, I'm not sure I see it the same way. I think [your view]. But I could be wrong -- what makes you think [their view]?"

### 7.3 The "When You Don't Think You Were Wrong" Script

> "I can see that [what happened] didn't land the way I intended. I wasn't trying to [what they perceived]. I'm sorry about the impact. Going forward, I'll [specific adjustment]."

## 8. Key Takeaways

1. **"No" requires a performance in English.** The bare refusal is structurally a preferred response delivered in a dispreferred slot -- it sounds hostile regardless of intent. The padding (delay + appreciation + account + apology + alternative) IS the expected packaging.

2. **Disagreement has levels.** Level 0 ("You're wrong") through Level 5 ("I wonder if we might also consider..."). Choosing the right level requires the Weight Formula (Category 3): more distance/power/stakes = more hedging.

3. **"But" cancels everything before it.** "I see your point, but..." = disagreement. "I'm sorry, but..." = not really sorry. Everything before "but" is packaging; everything after is the message.

4. **"Sorry" does four different jobs.** Genuine regret, sympathy, phatic lubrication, and strategic face-management. Not all require sincerity; all serve social functions.

5. **Escalation has visible markers.** Hedges disappearing, volume rising, absolutist language, personal attacks, register shifts. Recognizing these in OTHERS lets you de-escalate before conflict becomes damaging.

6. **The autistic "bare no" is accurate but unpackaged.** The refusal is complete; the social packaging is missing. Adding even one component (an account or an alternative) dramatically changes how the refusal is received.

7. **The correction reflex prioritizes accuracy over face.** This is valued in technical and professional contexts. In social contexts, adding a hedge ("I think it might actually be...") before a correction changes reception from hostile to helpful.

8. **You can acknowledge impact without admitting fault.** "I can see how that came across differently than I intended" is neither an apology nor a denial -- it's a bridge.
