# French for Pattern Learners: Decision Trees, Systematic Rules, and the Meta-Pattern

## Introduction: The Pattern Learner's Advantage

Every preceding chapter in this deep dive has documented a specific structural domain of French: phonology, morphology, syntax, verbs, gender, semantics, pragmatics, history, writing, and typological comparison. This capstone chapter does something different. It synthesizes all of those domains into a unified, pattern-based learning framework designed specifically for the kind of learner who thrives on explicit rules, systematic patterns, decision trees, and structural insight.

If French grammar initially appears to be a chaotic collection of arbitrary rules, endings, agreements, and exceptions, this chapter reveals the underlying architecture: a small number of systematic principles that, once internalized, predict the vast majority of French grammatical behavior. For a pattern-oriented learner -- someone whose cognitive style favors explicit pattern extraction over implicit acquisition -- this approach transforms French from "memorize thousands of rules" into "learn a handful of algorithms and derive the rest."

The central claim is strong: eight decision trees and one meta-pattern can cover the vast majority of French grammatical challenges that an English speaker will encounter. Each tree is designed to be complete (covering all standard cases), systematic (following consistent logic), and predictive (allowing you to handle new examples without prior memorization). Together, they constitute a pattern learner's toolkit for French.

There is a cognitive science basis for this approach. Research on autistic cognitive strengths consistently identifies pattern recognition, rule extraction, and systematic learning as areas of particular strength. Pattern-oriented learners tend to excel at extracting explicit rules from data, applying those rules consistently, and identifying when exceptions follow their own patterns. This cognitive profile is precisely what the decision-tree approach leverages: it converts implicit, statistically-acquired grammatical knowledge into explicit, rule-based algorithms. Where a typical language learner might "feel" that a noun is feminine or "just know" that the imparfait sounds right in a given context, the pattern learner applies a systematic test and arrives at the same answer through a different -- and often more reliable -- cognitive route.

## The Meta-Pattern: One Principle That Predicts Everything

The single most powerful insight for a pattern learner approaching French is the meta-pattern: **French is more morphologically synthetic than English**. This means French packs more grammatical information into individual words through inflection (verb conjugation, gender agreement, mood marking), while English distributes the same information across multiple words (auxiliaries, prepositions, word order).

From this single principle, you can derive specific predictions:

| Because French is more synthetic... | English compensates by... |
|---------------------------------------|---------------------------|
| 50+ verb forms per verb | 5 forms + auxiliary stacking |
| 2 grammatical genders | 0 grammatical genders |
| Rich agreement (5+ types per sentence) | Minimal agreement (3rd person -s) |
| Morphological conditional (parlerait) | "would" as a separate word |
| Productive, obligatory subjunctive | Vestigial subjunctive |
| 3 article types (~11 forms) | 2 types + bare nouns |
| Pre-verbal clitic pronouns (rigid order) | Post-verbal full pronouns (flexible) |
| Bipartite negation (ne...pas) | Do-support + not |
| 4-form adjective agreement | Invariable adjectives |
| Past participle gender/number agreement | Invariable past participle |

**The practical algorithm**: When you encounter something in French that confuses you, ask: "Is this a consequence of French being more synthetic?" If yes (approximately 80% of the time), expect more morphological complexity per word but fewer total words, and look for the English compensatory strategy. If no (approximately 20%), you have identified a genuinely independent parameter (prosodic type, progressive aspect, vigesimal numbers) that requires separate learning.

## Decision Tree 1: Gender Prediction from Noun Endings

**English comparison**: English has no grammatical gender. This means English speakers have no prior framework for gender prediction. But the ending-based system provides exactly the kind of rule-based approach that replaces rote memorization with structural analysis.

**The research basis**: A 2006 corpus study by Roy Lyster (McGill University) examined 9,961 nouns from Le Robert Junior Illustre and demonstrated that approximately 80% of masculine nouns and 81% of feminine nouns have endings that systematically predict their gender. Gender is not random -- it is approximately 80% rule-governed based on morphological form.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-2-gender-prediction.html -->

### The Algorithm

**Step 1**: Check against high-confidence feminine endings:
- -tion / -sion / -aison (99%): nation, passion, education
- -te / -ite (95%): liberte, societe, universite
- -ure (90%): nature, culture, voiture
- -ence / -ance (95%): science, patience, chance
- -ie / -rie (95%): vie, philosophie, boulangerie
- -ette / -elle (98%): assiette, fourchette, chapelle
- -ee (95%): idee, journee, annee -- **exception**: musee (masculine)

**Step 2**: Check against high-confidence masculine endings:
- -ment (95%): moment, gouvernement, changement
- -age (90%): voyage, fromage, village -- **exceptions**: image, page, plage (feminine)
- -eau (95%): bureau, chateau, tableau
- -isme (100%): capitalisme, realisme, tourisme
- -eur (85%): honneur, bonheur, moteur -- **exceptions**: fleur, couleur, peur, douleur, humeur (feminine)
- -al / -ail / -eil (95%): journal, travail, soleil
- -oir (95%): miroir, espoir, pouvoir

**Step 3**: Apply weak predictors as tiebreakers:
- Words ending in silent -e: ~65% feminine (weak predictor but directional)
- Words ending in a pronounced consonant: ~80% masculine

**Step 4**: Not covered by any ending rule? Learn individually (~20% of nouns).

**The exception patterns are themselves patterned**: feminine -eur words cluster around abstract/emotional qualities (fleur, couleur, peur, douleur, humeur). Masculine -e words often end in specific sub-patterns (-age, -isme, -ege). Learning the exception patterns as secondary rules reduces the truly unpredictable items to a small set.

## Decision Tree 2: Verb Conjugation -- The Three-Group System

**English comparison**: English has 5 verb forms maximum and compensates with auxiliary stacking. French has 50+ forms per verb but organizes them into three learnable groups with a critical efficiency insight: Group 1 (-er verbs) accounts for approximately 90% of all French verbs and is completely regular.

### The 90/10 Principle

- **Group 1 (-er)**: ~90% of all French verbs. Completely regular. Learn this one pattern and you can conjugate thousands of verbs.
- **Group 2 (-ir with -issant)**: ~300 verbs. Regular with an -iss- extension in plural forms. Diagnostic: present participle in -issant (finissant), not -ant (partant).
- **Group 3 (everything else)**: ~350 verbs with identifiable sub-patterns, plus the "big four" truly irregular verbs (etre, avoir, aller, faire).

### The Cross-Tense Pattern Shortcuts

These are the pattern insights that make conjugation feel like a system rather than a memorization task:

**The imparfait is 100% regular for all verbs, even "irregular" ones.** Formation: nous-form present stem + -ais, -ais, -ait, -ions, -iez, -aient. This works for every single French verb without exception. Even etre: nous sommes -> et- -> j'etais.

**The conditional = future stem + imparfait endings.** Once you know the future tense and the imparfait, the conditional is free:
- Future: parler + ai = parlerai
- Conditional: parler + ais = parlerais
- Same stem, different ending set. One pattern, two tenses.

**The subjunctive stem = 3rd person plural present stem.** ils parlent -> parl- -> que je parle. The subjunctive endings (-e, -es, -e, -ions, -iez, -ent) are applied to this stem.

**The meta-rule**: In any French tense, the stem determines WHAT you are saying, and the ending simultaneously encodes WHO (person), HOW MANY (number), WHEN (tense), and HOW CERTAIN (mood). One ending, four pieces of information. English requires separate words for each.

### The Spoken vs. Written Split

A critical insight for the pattern learner: French verb conjugation looks more complex on paper than it sounds. In the present tense of Group 1 verbs, the je/tu/il/ils forms are all pronounced identically: "je parle," "tu parles," "il parle," "ils parlent" all sound like /parl/. Only "nous parlons" (/parlO~/) and "vous parlez" (/parle/) are audibly distinct. This means that spoken French effectively has only 3 distinct present-tense forms (singular-sounding, nous-form, vous-form), not the 6 that written French displays. The written complexity is partly orthographic convention.

This spoken/written gap is a pattern in itself: French writing preserves historical morphological distinctions that speech has eroded. Knowing this pattern helps the learner understand why French spelling seems so much more complex than pronunciation -- they are tracking different stages of the language's evolution.

### The Group 3 Sub-Patterns

Even the "irregular" Group 3 verbs reveal patterns when examined systematically:

- **-oir verbs** (vouloir, pouvoir, devoir, savoir, voir): share stem-change patterns with predictable alternations
- **-dre verbs** (prendre, vendre, attendre): share the conjugation endings -ds, -ds, -d in the singular
- **-aindre/-eindre/-oindre** (craindre, peindre, joindre): share stem-change patterns with nasal consonant alternation
- **The "big four"** (etre, avoir, aller, faire): genuinely irregular and among the 4 most frequent verbs in the language; memorize these outright

The pattern within the "irregulars" means that even the 350 Group 3 verbs are not 350 independent memorization tasks. They cluster into approximately 15-20 sub-patterns, each applying to a small family of related verbs.

## Decision Tree 3: BAGS Adjective Placement

**English comparison**: English adjectives always precede the noun ("a white house," "an interesting book"). French adjectives default to the opposite position: after the noun. English speakers must reverse their default, then learn the BAGS exceptions that reverse back. Double inversion.

### The Algorithm

1. **Is the adjective in the BAGS list?** -> Place BEFORE the noun (prenominal)
2. **Does the adjective change meaning by position?** -> Check desired meaning (see table below)
3. **Default (when unsure)**: place AFTER the noun -> correct ~85% of the time

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-4-bags-placement.html -->

### The BAGS Categories

- **B**eauty: beau/belle (beautiful), joli/jolie (pretty)
- **A**ge: jeune (young), vieux/vieille (old), nouveau/nouvelle (new)
- **G**oodness: bon/bonne (good), mauvais/mauvaise (bad), meilleur/meilleure (better)
- **S**ize: grand/grande (big), petit/petite (small), gros/grosse (fat), long/longue (long), court/courte (short)

Also prenominal: autre (other), meme (same), tout/toute (all), tel/telle (such)

### The Deeper Pattern

BAGS adjectives share three properties: they are short (1-2 syllables), high-frequency (among the most common adjectives in French), and subjective/evaluative (expressing the speaker's judgment rather than classifying the noun objectively). Post-nominal adjectives tend to be classifying, technical, or specific: une voiture francaise, une porte metallique, un probleme economique.

### Position-Dependent Meaning Changes

| Adjective | Before noun (figurative) | After noun (literal) |
|-----------|--------------------------|---------------------|
| ancien | un ancien collegue (former) | un chateau ancien (ancient) |
| grand | un grand homme (great) | un homme grand (tall) |
| pauvre | un pauvre homme (pitiable) | un homme pauvre (poor/broke) |
| propre | mon propre cahier (own) | un cahier propre (clean) |
| cher | un cher ami (dear) | un restaurant cher (expensive) |
| seul | un seul enfant (only) | un enfant seul (lonely) |
| certain | un certain charme (a certain) | une chose certaine (sure) |

The pattern: prenominal = figurative/subjective meaning; postnominal = literal/objective meaning.

**Special forms**: Before vowel-initial masculine nouns, three BAGS adjectives take alternate forms to avoid vowel hiatus: beau -> bel (un bel homme), nouveau -> nouvel (un nouvel ami), vieux -> vieil (un vieil arbre).

## Decision Tree 4: Liaison Rules

**English comparison**: English has no liaison system. Final consonants are always pronounced or always silent regardless of what follows. French liaison -- pronouncing a normally silent final consonant before a vowel-initial word -- follows a systematic three-category system.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-5-liaison-rules.html -->

### The Three Categories

**Obligatory** (omission = error): Within tight syntactic bonds.
- Determiner + noun: les_amis, un_ami, des_enfants
- Pronoun + verb: ils_ont, nous_avons, on_a
- Prenominal adjective + noun: un petit_ami, les grands_arbres
- After tres/plus/moins/bien/trop: tres_interessant
- Fixed expressions: les Etats-Unis, comment_allez-vous

**Forbidden** (production = error): Across syntactic boundaries and before specific words.
- After "et" (and): NEVER. "Pain et // eau," not *"pain et_eau"
- Before aspirate-h words: le // heros, les // haricots
- After singular nouns: un etudiant // intelligent
- Before onze and oui: le // onze mars

**Optional** (register-dependent): A formality dial, not a binary rule.
- After plural nouns: les etudiants (?)intelligents
- After verbs (except etre/avoir): ils parlent (?)anglais
- After prepositions: dans (?)un an
- More liaison = more formal; less liaison = more casual

### Consonant Pronunciation in Liaison

| Written | Pronounced | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| -s | /z/ | les_amis |
| -x | /z/ | deux_amis |
| -t | /t/ | petit_ami |
| -d | /t/ (voicing change!) | grand_homme |
| -n | /n/ | un_ami |
| -f | /v/ (voicing change, rare) | neuf_ans, neuf_heures |

## Decision Tree 5: Passe Compose vs. Imparfait

**English comparison**: English can express this distinction ("I was eating" vs. "I ate") but does not require it grammatically. French requires the choice on every past-tense verb -- there is no ambiguous middle ground.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-3-passe-compose-imparfait.html -->

### The Algorithm

1. Did the action happen a specific, countable number of times? -> **Passe compose** ("J'ai mange trois fois")
2. Was the action habitual or repeated without a specific count? -> **Imparfait** ("Je mangeais souvent la-bas")
3. Did the action have a clear beginning, end, or duration? -> **Passe compose** ("J'ai travaille de 9h a 17h")
4. Does this advance the story / narrate a plot event? -> **Passe compose** ("Il est entre, il a vu")
5. Is this background description or scene-setting? -> **Imparfait** ("Il faisait beau, les oiseaux chantaient")
6. Is this a state or condition? Did the state CHANGE at a specific moment? -> If changed: **Passe compose** ("J'ai eu peur" = I became afraid). If unchanged: **Imparfait** ("J'avais faim" = I was hungry)

### The Movie Metaphor

Think of past narration as a movie:
- **Imparfait** = the background: scenery, weather, ambient sound, what people looked like and felt
- **Passe compose** = the action: plot events, changes, things that happened

You need both: "Il faisait nuit [imparfait, background]. Soudain, un bruit a retenti [passe compose, event]."

### Trigger Words

| Imparfait triggers | Passe compose triggers |
|--------------------|----------------------|
| souvent (often) | hier (yesterday) |
| toujours (always) | soudain (suddenly) |
| d'habitude (usually) | tout a coup (all of a sudden) |
| chaque jour (every day) | un jour (one day) |
| quand j'etais petit (when I was little) | une fois (once) |
| en general (in general) | a ce moment-la (at that moment) |

### The Etre Verb List (DR MRS VANDERTRAMP)

Certain intransitive verbs of motion or state change use etre (not avoir) as their auxiliary in the passe compose. The mnemonic: Descendre, Rentrer, Monter, Retourner, Sortir, Venir, Arriver, Naitre, Devenir, Entrer, Revenir, Tomber, Rester, Aller, Mourir, Partir. With etre verbs, the past participle agrees with the subject in gender and number: "Elle est allee" (she went) vs. "Il est alle" (he went).

## Decision Tree 6: Subjunctive Triggers (WEIRDO)

**English comparison**: English subjunctive is vestigial ("If I were you," "I suggest that he go"). French subjunctive is alive, productive, and grammatically obligatory in all WEIRDO contexts. This is one of the starkest French-English contrasts and a direct consequence of French's greater morphological synthesis.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-6-weirdo-subjunctive.html -->

### The WEIRDO Categories

The subjunctive is required in subordinate clauses (after "que") when the main clause expresses something other than factual assertion:

- **W -- Wishes/Wants**: vouloir que, souhaiter que, desirer que, preferer que
- **E -- Emotions**: etre content que, etre triste que, avoir peur que, regretter que
- **I -- Impersonal expressions**: il faut que, il est possible que, il est important que
- **R -- Recommendations/Requests**: demander que, exiger que, suggerer que, ordonner que
- **D -- Doubt/Denial**: douter que, ne pas croire que, ne pas penser que, nier que
- **O -- Opinions**: il est bon que, il est dommage que, c'est etrange que

### The Two-Subject Rule

Subjunctive requires TWO DIFFERENT subjects (main clause subject differs from subordinate clause subject). If the subjects are the same, use infinitive instead:

- Different subjects: "Je veux **que tu viennes**" (subjunctive)
- Same subject: "Je veux **venir**" (infinitive, NOT *"Je veux que je vienne")

### Key Exceptions

- **esperer que** (to hope that): Takes INDICATIVE in the affirmative, despite being a "wish" verb. "J'espere qu'il viendra" (not *vienne). In the negative, it takes subjunctive.
- **croire/penser que**: In the AFFIRMATIVE, these take indicative ("Je crois qu'il sait"). Only in the NEGATIVE or INTERROGATIVE do they trigger subjunctive ("Je ne crois pas qu'il sache").

### Conjunctions Requiring Subjunctive

Independent of WEIRDO, certain conjunctions always trigger subjunctive: avant que (before), pour que (in order to), bien que (although), sans que (without), a moins que (unless), jusqu'a ce que (until), de peur que (for fear that), pourvu que (provided that). Contrast: apres que takes INDICATIVE (what happened after is factual; what will happen before is anticipated/uncertain).

### The Deep Pattern Behind WEIRDO

The six WEIRDO categories are not arbitrary -- they share a common semantic thread. The subjunctive is required when the main clause expresses something other than factual assertion. Wishes are not facts. Emotions are subjective reactions, not statements of truth. Impersonal expressions frame situations as necessary or possible rather than actual. Recommendations attempt to change reality rather than describe it. Doubt explicitly questions factual status. Opinions are evaluative judgments, not neutral descriptions.

The indicative, by contrast, asserts that something IS the case: "Je sais qu'il vient" (I know he is coming -- factual assertion, indicative). The subjunctive marks that something is desired, feared, demanded, or questioned but not asserted as fact.

This deep pattern -- **indicative = factual assertion; subjunctive = non-factual stance** -- is the single principle behind all six WEIRDO categories. If you internalize this principle, you can often predict subjunctive usage even for verbs not explicitly listed in the WEIRDO categories, by asking: "Does this main clause assert a fact, or does it express a non-factual stance?"

## Decision Tree 7: Determiner Selection

**English comparison**: English has a simpler decision: "the" (specific), "a/an" (indefinite singular), or nothing (generic, plural indefinite, uncountable). French fills every position with an article -- there are no bare nouns in French.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-7-article-decision-tree.html -->

### The Algorithm

1. Is the noun specific and identified? -> **Definite**: le/la/l'/les ("Le chat dort")
2. Is the noun generic (the category as a whole)? -> **Definite** (French uses definite for generic!) ("J'aime le chocolat" = I like chocolate) -- **English uses no article here**
3. Is the noun an indefinite countable thing? -> **Indefinite**: un/une/des ("J'ai un chat," "J'ai des amis")
4. Is the noun an uncountable substance in indefinite quantity? -> **Partitive**: du/de la/de l' ("Je bois du cafe") -- **English uses no article here**
5. Is the sentence negative? -> Indefinite and partitive become **de/d'** ("Je n'ai pas de chat," "Je ne bois pas de cafe"). **Exceptions**: definite articles do NOT change in negation ("Je n'aime pas le cafe"); after etre, articles don't change ("Ce n'est pas un probleme")
6. Is there an adjective before a plural noun? -> des -> de/d' ("J'ai de bons amis") -- this rule is increasingly violated in spoken French
7. After expressions of quantity? -> **de** alone ("beaucoup de livres," "un peu de sucre," "trop de problemes"). **Exception**: la plupart des (most of the) retains the article

### The Two Most Common English-Speaker Errors

1. **Using no article for generic nouns**: *"J'aime cafe" -- WRONG. French requires: "J'aime **le** cafe."
2. **Using no article for partitive nouns**: *"Je bois cafe" -- WRONG. French requires: "Je bois **du** cafe."

Both errors come from the same source: English uses bare nouns where French requires specific grammatical forms. The French partitive article fills a slot that English simply leaves empty.

## Decision Tree 8: Clitic Pronoun Ordering

**English comparison**: English has no clitic ordering template. "I give it to him" and "I give him it" are both grammatical. All objects follow the verb. French requires all object pronouns BEFORE the verb in a strict fixed order.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-8-clitic-ordering.html -->

### The Template (Declarative and Negative Sentences)

```
Subject + (ne) + [Col 1] + [Col 2] + [Col 3] + [Col 4] + [Col 5] + VERB + (pas)
```

| Column | Pronouns | Function |
|--------|----------|----------|
| Col 1 | me, te, se, nous, vous | Reflexive / 1st-2nd person |
| Col 2 | le, la, les | 3rd person direct object |
| Col 3 | lui, leur | 3rd person indirect object |
| Col 4 | y | Locative (replaces a + noun/place) |
| Col 5 | en | Partitive (replaces de + noun/quantity) |

**Maximum 2 pronouns per verb.** Select from left to right: if you have a Col 1 pronoun, it comes first; if you have Col 2 and Col 3, Col 2 comes first.

### Examples

- "Je **le** vois" (I see him/it) -- Col 2 only
- "Je **le lui** donne" (I give it to him) -- Col 2 + Col 3
- "Il **me le** donne" (He gives it to me) -- Col 1 + Col 2
- "Je **m'en** souviens" (I remember it) -- Col 1 + Col 5
- "Il **y en** a" (There are some) -- Col 4 + Col 5

### The Imperative Exception

In **affirmative** imperatives, pronouns FOLLOW the verb with hyphens, and the order changes:

Verb + le/la/les + moi/toi/lui/nous/vous/leur + y + en

- "**Donne-le-moi**" (Give it to me) -- NOT *"Donne-moi-le"
- "**Donnez-les-leur**" (Give them to them)

In **negative** imperatives, the standard pre-verbal order returns:
- "**Ne me le donne pas**" (Don't give it to me)

### Why the Template Matters Cognitively

The clitic ordering template represents a fundamentally different production strategy from English. In English, you can plan a sentence linearly: "I [verb] [object] [to-recipient]." In French, you must know all your objects BEFORE producing the verb, because the pronouns must be placed in their correct pre-verbal slots. This means French requires more forward planning of the verb phrase -- you must mentally assemble the full pronoun inventory before articulating any of it.

For a pattern learner, the good news is that this forward planning is entirely algorithmic. Identify the pronouns, assign them to columns, place them in column order, then produce the verb. The system is rigid (which makes it learnable) rather than flexible (which would make it unpredictable). Rigidity is the pattern learner's friend.

## Putting It All Together: The Pattern Learner's Algorithm

When you encounter any French grammatical challenge, follow this master algorithm:

**Step 1: Identify the category.** Gender? Use Tree 1. Verb form? Use Tree 2. Adjective position? Use Tree 3. Connected speech? Use Tree 4. Past tense choice? Use Tree 5. Mood choice? Use Tree 6. Article selection? Use Tree 7. Pronoun placement? Use Tree 8.

**Step 2: Apply the decision tree.** Follow it step by step. The trees are designed to be exhaustive.

**Step 3: Check for exceptions.** Each tree has a known set of exceptions. These exceptions are themselves systematic -- learn the exception patterns as secondary rules, not individual items.

**Step 4: Cross-reference with the meta-pattern.** If a rule seems arbitrary, check whether it follows from the meta-pattern (French = more synthetic). If it does, the rule becomes predictable rather than arbitrary. If it does not (vigesimal numbers, aspirate-h, progressive aspect), it is a historical contingency that requires separate learning.

## Common Misconceptions

**"French grammar is too complex to learn systematically; you just have to absorb it through immersion."**

Every major aspect of French grammar follows identifiable patterns. Gender is ~80% predictable from endings. Verb conjugation is ~90% regular. Adjective placement follows the BAGS rule. Liaison has three systematic categories. The subjunctive has the WEIRDO trigger list. The passe compose/imparfait choice follows an aspectual decision tree. Pattern learners can achieve high accuracy through systematic rule application without relying on implicit absorption.

**"French verb tables need to be memorized individually for each verb."**

90% of French verbs are Group 1 (-er) and follow a single, completely regular paradigm. The conditional = future stem + imparfait endings. The imparfait is regular for ALL verbs including the most irregular ones. Once you know the pattern, you can conjugate thousands of verbs. Only the "big four" (etre, avoir, aller, faire) and ~350 Group 3 verbs require individual attention -- and even these have sub-patterns.

**"You need to feel whether to use passe compose or imparfait."**

The distinction is systematic and can be determined through a decision tree. Completed, bounded, countable, or narrative-advancing -> passe compose. Ongoing, habitual, descriptive, or background -> imparfait. Trigger words provide additional clues. The decision is algorithmic, not intuitive.

**"French exceptions make rules useless."**

French "exceptions" are themselves often patterned. Feminine -eur words cluster around abstract/emotional qualities. Masculine -e words end in specific sub-patterns. BAGS adjectives share properties (short, frequent, evaluative). Learning the exception patterns as secondary rules reduces the truly unpredictable items to a small, manageable set. The 80% accuracy of the gender prediction system, for example, is already far better than the 50% you would get from random guessing.

## The Interconnections: How the Decision Trees Work Together

The eight decision trees are not isolated -- they interact in predictable ways. Understanding these interconnections reveals the deeper systematicity of French grammar.

### Gender Cascades Through Articles, Adjectives, and Participles

When you determine a noun's gender (Tree 1), that determination immediately feeds into:
- **Article selection** (Tree 7): le vs. la, un vs. une, du vs. de la
- **Adjective agreement** (Tree 3): grand vs. grande, bon vs. bonne
- **Past participle agreement** (Tree 5, with etre verbs): alle vs. allee

A single gender decision cascades through multiple other decision trees. This is the agreement web in action -- and it explains why gender prediction matters so much. If you get the gender right at the start, all the downstream agreement falls into place. If you get it wrong, everything downstream is also wrong.

### Verb Conjugation Feeds Into Tense/Aspect Choice

Tree 2 (conjugation groups) tells you HOW to conjugate a verb. But you also need to know WHICH tense to conjugate it in. Tree 5 (passe compose vs. imparfait) makes the past-tense choice. Tree 6 (WEIRDO) determines whether to use subjunctive mood instead of indicative. These trees work in sequence:

1. First: Is this a subjunctive context? (Tree 6) If yes, conjugate in the subjunctive.
2. If indicative past: Passe compose or imparfait? (Tree 5) Choose the aspect.
3. Then: What conjugation group? (Tree 2) Apply the correct paradigm.

### Liaison Interacts With Adjective Placement

Tree 4 (liaison) and Tree 3 (BAGS) interact when a BAGS adjective precedes a vowel-initial noun. The adjective-noun combination becomes an obligatory liaison context: "un petit_ami" (/peti.ta.mi/), "les grands_arbres" (/gra~.zarbr/). The special forms bel/nouvel/vieil also emerge from this interaction -- they exist specifically to provide a consonant for liaison before vowel-initial masculine nouns.

### The Clitic Template Interacts With Negation

Tree 8 (clitic ordering) and the negation system interact because ne...pas wraps around the entire clitic-verb complex:

"Je **ne** le lui **ai pas** donne" -- ne comes before ALL clitics; pas comes after the conjugated verb. The clitic template sits inside the negation wrapper.

### Articles Interact With Negation and Quantity

Tree 7 (article selection) has modification rules that activate in specific syntactic contexts: after negation (indefinite/partitive -> de), after quantity expressions (-> de alone), before adjective + plural noun (des -> de). These are not separate trees but context-dependent modifications within a single system.

## Why Pattern Learning Works for French

There is a reason why the decision-tree approach is particularly effective for French specifically, and not just a generic learning strategy. French, as a moderately synthetic fusional language, has exactly the kind of grammar that rewards systematic analysis:

1. **High regularity within paradigms**: 90% of verbs are Group 1 regular. 80% of genders are predictable from endings. The imparfait is 100% regular. These are not small margins -- they represent the overwhelming majority of cases.

2. **Compositional patterns**: Complex forms are built from simpler ones in predictable ways (conditional = future stem + imparfait endings; subjunctive = 3rd plural present stem + subjunctive endings). Once you know the components, you can generate the combinations.

3. **Systematic exceptions**: Even the "irregular" parts of French grammar contain sub-patterns. Group 3 verbs cluster into families. Gender exceptions cluster around semantic fields. BAGS adjectives share observable properties.

4. **Finite rule sets**: The WEIRDO categories, the liaison three-category system, the clitic ordering template, the article selection tree -- each of these is a finite, enumerable set of rules. There is no open-ended ambiguity; you can learn the complete system.

For a pattern-oriented learner whose cognitive style favors explicit pattern extraction over implicit statistical learning, this grammar is remarkably well-suited to algorithmic mastery. The decision trees in this chapter are not simplifications -- they are accurate representations of how French grammar actually works, expressed in the format most accessible to a systematic mind.

## The Learning Sequence: Which Trees to Master First

For maximum efficiency, the decision trees should be learned in an order that maximizes downstream benefit:

1. **Verb conjugation (Tree 2)** first -- verbs are the structural core of every sentence
2. **Gender prediction (Tree 1)** early -- gender cascades through everything else
3. **Article selection (Tree 7)** next -- articles are required before every noun
4. **BAGS placement (Tree 3)** alongside articles -- adjective position interacts with noun phrases
5. **Passe compose vs. imparfait (Tree 5)** when past tense is introduced
6. **Liaison (Tree 4)** for pronunciation refinement
7. **WEIRDO subjunctive (Tree 6)** at intermediate level
8. **Clitic ordering (Tree 8)** at intermediate-advanced level

This sequence follows the natural progression of French instruction but also reflects the dependency structure: verb conjugation is needed for everything, gender feeds into articles and agreement, and the more complex trees (subjunctive, clitics) build on the foundations laid by simpler ones.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Meta-pattern** | The overarching principle (French = more synthetic) from which specific differences are derived |
| **Decision tree** | A systematic algorithm for making grammatical choices through sequential binary decisions |
| **BAGS** | Mnemonic for adjective categories that precede the noun: Beauty, Age, Goodness, Size |
| **WEIRDO** | Mnemonic for subjunctive triggers: Wishes, Emotions, Impersonal, Recommendations, Doubt, Opinions |
| **Passe compose** | French compound past tense (avoir/etre + past participle) for completed actions |
| **Imparfait** | French imperfect tense for ongoing, habitual, or background past situations |
| **Liaison** | Pronunciation of a normally silent final consonant before a vowel-initial word |
| **Clitic** | A pronoun that attaches to the verb and follows a fixed ordering template |
| **Partitive article** | du/de la/des, encoding indefinite quantity for uncountable nouns -- no English equivalent |
| **DR MRS VANDERTRAMP** | Mnemonic for the ~17 verbs conjugated with etre (not avoir) in the passe compose |
| **Morphological synthesis** | The degree to which a language encodes grammatical information within word forms |
| **Fusional** | A morphological type where one affix encodes multiple grammatical categories simultaneously |
| **Two-subject rule** | Subjunctive requires different subjects in main and subordinate clauses; same subject uses infinitive |
| **Lyster (2006)** | Corpus study of 9,961 French nouns demonstrating ~80% gender predictability from endings |
