# Category 13: Topic Management in Conversation - Research for Autism Pragmatics Knowledge Base

## 1. Research on Autistic Topic Management

### 1.1 The Ábalos et al. (2025) Study: Topic Shifts and Maintenance

**Study:** Ábalos et al. (2025). "Conversational Topic Shifts and Topic Maintenance in Autistic and Neurotypical Children." *Autism Research*. Published by Wiley Online Library.

**Participants:** 43 autistic and 46 age-matched neurotypical Spanish-speaking children (M = 8.55 years, SD = 1.91) during a semi-spontaneous conversation task.

**Key Findings:**
- Autistic and neurotypical children produced a **comparable number of topic-supporting (on-topic) responses** -- topic maintenance was not significantly different between groups.
- However, autistic children made **significantly more topic shifts** overall.
- Critically, autistic children's topic shifts were rated as **more drastic** -- they interrupted conversational flow more sharply than neurotypical children's topic shifts.
- The study developed a theoretically grounded coding scheme that captures relevant distinctions in how different topic shifts are perceived, identifying at least **18 different types of topic shifts** in their analysis framework.

**Nuance:** The core finding is a dissociation: **frequency of on-topic responses was similar**, but the **manner of shifting** differed. This means autistic children were not worse at staying on topic -- they were different in *how* they moved between topics. Their shifts lacked the gradual transitions, discourse markers, and contextual bridging that neurotypical speakers typically use to ease topic changes.

**Clinical Significance:** The coding scheme serves as a resource for research and clinical practice in assessing and supporting conversational skills. The researchers defined "appropriate" topic shifts as those that include an introducer (e.g., "by the way") alerting the listener of a topic change, occurring after a 3-second pause, or after the previous topic has been discussed at length. "Inappropriate" shifts were classified as abrupt, occurring without pause time, or when the prior topic had not been sufficiently discussed.

**Source:** [Ábalos et al. (2025) - Autism Research](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/aur.70204)

### 1.2 The Abbot-Smith et al. (2025) Study: Topic Maintenance and Cognitive Abilities

**Study:** Abbot-Smith, K., Matthews, D., Bannard, C., Nice, J., Malkin, L., Williams, D., & Hobson, W. (2025). "Conversational topic maintenance and related cognitive abilities in autistic versus neurotypical children." *Autism: The International Journal of Research and Practice*, 29(3). Published in PMC as PMC11894863.

**Design:** Three separate studies (Studies 2 and 3 pre-registered) examining verbally fluent school-aged children (ages 5-9) in southern England.

**Participants:**
- **Study 1:** 30 autistic (7 female) and 30 neurotypical (7 female) children, ages 5-7, matched on age, vocabulary, and non-verbal reasoning.
- **Study 2:** 48 neurotypical children (24 female), ages 5;11-6;11.
- **Study 3:** 40 autistic children (10 female), ages 5;4-9;10.

**Key Findings:**

*Study 1 (Group Comparisons):*
- Autistic children were more likely to give **off-topic responses** and **generic minimal responses** (e.g., "mm," "oh").
- Autistic children were **less likely to give non-verbal responses** (e.g., nodding, facial affect).
- Despite these differences, the autistic group provided **topic-supporting responses 62% of the time**, indicating relative strength in conversation maintenance.
- Between-group differences reflected **relative rather than absolute difficulties**.

*Studies 2 and 3 (Within-Group Predictors):*
- **Large individual differences** existed within both neurotypical and autistic groups -- approximately 19% of neurotypical 6-year-olds never produced off-topic responses, while 30% showed such responses in 10%+ of interactions.
- **Theory of Mind** emerged as a significant independent predictor of topic-supporting responses across both diagnostic groups, even when controlling for age, vocabulary, non-verbal reasoning, and working memory.
- **Working memory** showed correlational relationships with topic maintenance in both groups (positively related to contingent elaborations, negatively related to off-topic verbosity), but didn't reach significance as a unique predictor in autistic children's regression models.
- **Age** negatively predicted non-contingent (off-topic) responding in autistic children -- older children showed fewer off-topic responses.

**Central Conclusion:** "The most important finding of the study is that the conversation skills that are characteristically difficult to deploy for autistic children are also difficult for very many neurotypical children, apparently for similar reasons. Conversational skills lie on a continuum for the general population and differences by diagnostic group are a matter of degree." The authors advocate for a **whole-classroom approach** to supporting conversation skills in all children.

**Source:** [Abbot-Smith et al. (2025) - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11894863/) | [SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/13623613241286610)

### 1.3 The Abbot-Smith et al. (2023) Study: Teaching Topic Maintenance

**Study:** Abbot-Smith, K., Dockrell, J., Sturrock, A., Matthews, D., & Wilson, C. (2023). "Topic maintenance in social conversation: What children need to learn and evidence that this can be taught." *First Language*.

**Key Arguments:**
- Individual differences in children's social communication mediate the relationship between poor vocabulary/grammar and behavioral difficulties.
- Social communication skills predict difficulties with peers over and above vocabulary and grammar scores.
- Children with weaker conversation skills are less likely to make and maintain friendships.
- Teaching conversation skills is a statutory requirement in the English primary curriculum, but most upper primary mainstream teachers are not trained to teach them and don't teach them.
- The review calls for whole-classroom, tier-1 intervention approaches, though the evidence base for good practice is currently thin.

**Source:** [Abbot-Smith et al. (2023) - SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/01427237231172652)

### 1.4 Earlier Research on Topic Management in Autism

**Historical Findings:**
Previous studies (pre-2025) consistently reported that autistic children are less likely than neurotypical peers to maintain and elaborate on the interlocutor's prior topic, and that they shift topics "inappropriately" more often. However, findings on topic maintenance have been noted as **inconclusive**, and studies specifically investigating topic shifts have been limited.

**Topic Recurrence in Autism:**
Research using conversation analysis has explored the recurrence of specific topics in the talk of children with autism, finding that topic perseveration (returning repeatedly to a particular subject) can function as a communicative strategy rather than a purely repetitive behavior (Stribling et al., 2009, *Clinical Linguistics & Phonetics*).

**Discourse Marker Use:**
Research shows that discourse markers (language units supporting conversational flow) represent a key pragmatic skill used differently in autism. Studies evaluating discourse marker use in semi-structured conversations find marked increases in difficulties in less structured versus more structured contexts, suggesting autistic speakers may benefit from increased conversational structure.

**Conversational Adaptation:**
Research on conversational adaptation found that reduced conversational adaptation in autism manifests differently across individuals. For some, behavioral inflexibility may present as indiscriminate levels of talkativeness about a particular interest or preferred topic across social settings, even when contextually inappropriate (PMC9167260). Critically, autistic participants may adequately perceive their conversation partner's level of engagement when the partner is interested, but struggle to flexibly shift their own behavior when the partner becomes bored.

**Pragmatic Language Markers:**
Pragmatic language differences are important diagnostic markers for ASD. These include differences in topic management, discourse coherence, use of fillers/discourse markers, and conversational repair strategies.

**Sources:**
- [Conversation Analysis and ASD - Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10803-015-2665-5)
- [Pragmatic Language Markers - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9075340/)

### 1.5 Summary of Documented Patterns

**More frequent topic shifts?** Mixed findings. Ábalos et al. (2025) found more topic shifts, but Abbot-Smith et al. (2025) found comparable rates of topic-supporting responses (62% for autistic group). The discrepancy may reflect that autistic speakers can stay on topic but shift more often and more dramatically when they do shift.

**More "abrupt" or "unannounced" shifts?** Yes -- consistently supported. Topic shifts by autistic speakers are perceived as more drastic, lacking the gradual transitions, discourse markers ("by the way," "that reminds me"), pauses, and contextual bridges that neurotypical speakers use.

**Difficulty maintaining topics initiated by others?** Partially supported, but nuanced. Autistic children show relative difficulty (not inability) with some aspects of topic maintenance, particularly non-verbal responsiveness. However, 62% topic-supporting response rate shows considerable competence.

**Perseveration on preferred topics?** Well-documented, but see Section 2 below for nuanced discussion.

## 2. Topic Perseveration and Special Interests

### 2.1 The Phenomenon

**Perseveration** refers to the continuation of an activity, thought, or topic without a natural ending point -- getting mentally or behaviorally "stuck" on a particular subject. In conversation, this manifests as:
- Repeatedly returning to topics of intense interest
- Talking extensively about a subject without monitoring listener engagement
- Having difficulty transitioning away from a preferred topic to the conversational partner's topic
- Incorporating preferred topics into conversations even when contextually unrelated

**Special interests** affect approximately 75-95% of autistic people and involve highly focused engagement that takes up significant free time, involves deep learning, and often serves regulatory functions.

**Info-dumping** (or monologuing) is a related conversational phenomenon where an autistic person shares extensive, detailed information about a topic of interest in a monologue-like format rather than a back-and-forth dialogue. The speaker shares what they know without looking for quick replies from the listener. Within autistic community, this is often described as a "love language" -- a way of connecting by sharing knowledge and passion.

### 2.2 Clinical Framing vs. Community Framing

**Clinical/Medical Model:**
- DSM-5-TR describes special interests as "highly restricted, fixated interests that are abnormal in intensity or focus."
- Clinical literature uses terms like "perseveration," "fixation," and "restricted interests."
- These terms emphasize pathology and deviation from a neurotypical norm.
- Documentation on neurodivergent clients often refers to special interests as "perseverations" or "hyper-fixations," drawing from an impairment-focused approach that frames neurodiverse traits as "disordered" or "atypical."

**Neurodiversity-Affirming Perspective:**
- Autistic community describes these as "passionate engagement," "deep interests," or "special interests" (positively valenced).
- An ASHA (American Speech-Language-Hearing Association) article titled **"Recasting Perseveration as Passion"** (2024), written by an autistic SLP, advocates for reframing: rather than negating these interests, clinicians should promote their usefulness in treatment.
- For many autistic people, special interests provide self-regulation, emotional stability, and a sense of purpose.
- Engaging with special interests has positive outcomes for mental health, self-esteem, and stress management in autistic adults.

**The Dualistic Model of Passion (Vallerand):**
Recent research applies Vallerand's dualistic model to autism, distinguishing between:
- **Harmonious passion**: balanced and flexible engagement toward a topic/activity
- **Obsessive passion**: rigid engagement that negatively impacts functioning

This framework moves beyond the binary of "normal interest" vs. "perseveration" and recognizes that both autistic and non-autistic people can engage with their interests harmoniously or obsessively.

### 2.3 Are Autistic Special Interests Truly Different from NT Passionate Engagement?

**The Anthony et al. (2013) Study:**
"Interests in high-functioning autism are more intense, interfering, and idiosyncratic, but not more circumscribed, than those in neurotypical development" (*Development and Psychopathology*).

Key findings comparing 109 HF-ASD individuals to 76 NT individuals:
- **Number of interests**: No significant difference between groups.
- **Type of interests**: Similar topics (television, animals, construction, art), but more idiosyncratic choices in the autistic group.
- **Intensity**: Significantly more intense in the autistic group.
- **Interference**: More interfering with daily life in the autistic group.
- **Circumscription**: NOT more circumscribed (i.e., not narrower in range).

**Key Distinctions Between Autistic Special Interests and NT Hobbies:**

| Dimension | Autistic Special Interest | NT Hobby/Passion |
|---|---|---|
| **Intensity** | Often all-consuming; hours of daily engagement | Variable; typically moderated |
| **Emotional regulation** | Stopping engagement may cause distress/dysregulation | Stopping is disappointing but manageable |
| **Social function** | Interest itself is primary; socializing may serve the interest | Socializing may be primary; hobby serves social connection |
| **Flexibility** | May be difficult to shift attention away | Generally easier to shift |
| **Duration** | Can last years to decades | Often cyclical or shorter-term |
| **Topics** | Similar range, but may be more idiosyncratic | Similar range, often more conventional |

**The "Sports Fan" Comparison:**
The question of whether a sports fan who talks about nothing but football is fundamentally different from an autistic person who talks about nothing but trains is important. The research suggests the *topics* overlap substantially, but the *manner of engagement* differs: autistic interests tend to be more intense, more central to identity, more distressing to be separated from, and more likely to serve regulatory (rather than primarily social) functions.

However, this is a matter of degree, not kind. Many NT people have intensely consuming interests too, and cultural acceptance varies (a sports obsession is normalized; a train obsession is pathologized).

**Source:** [Anthony et al. (2013) - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4543385/)

### 2.4 When Extended Topic Talk Is Valued vs. When It Creates Friction

**Valued Contexts:**
- **Expert conversations**: Deep knowledge on a narrow topic is expected and appreciated (academic conferences, technical discussions, professional consultations).
- **Shared interest groups**: When both parties share the interest, extended talk is welcome and energizing (hobby clubs, online communities, fan conventions).
- **Teaching/mentoring**: One-sided knowledge sharing is the norm.
- **Online affinity spaces**: Research by Aiston, Koteyko, & van Driel (2025) in *Applied Linguistics* argues that focused interests can foster "shared focus, mutual accommodation, and multimodal expression" in online discourse environments.

**Friction Contexts:**
- **Mixed-interest social settings**: Casual conversation with people who don't share the interest.
- **Workplace small talk**: Brief, reciprocal exchanges are expected.
- **Multi-person gatherings**: Topic needs to be accessible to the whole group.
- **Initial encounters**: Getting-to-know-you conversations where reciprocal question-asking is expected.

**Source:** [Aiston et al. (2025) - Applied Linguistics](https://academic.oup.com/applij/advance-article/doi/10.1093/applin/amaf006/8015533)

### 2.5 Connection to Monotropism

**Theory:** Monotropism, developed by Dinah Murray, Wenn Lawson, and Mike Lesser (first published 2005), posits that autistic cognition is characterized by a tendency to focus attention on a small or singular number of interests at any time, with intense engagement within that attentional channel.

**How It Explains Topic Perseveration:**
- When the monotropic mind is engaged with a topic, that interest consumes most available attention.
- Competing stimuli (including the conversational partner's topics) are filtered out.
- In social discourse, people take turns determining the "current common interest" moment by moment. Monotropic individuals may find this rapid, fluid shifting of shared attention particularly difficult.
- This is a **cognitive style**, not a deficit -- within the attention tunnel, the person may experience flow states, deep thinking, and intense satisfaction.

**Implications for Topic Management:**
- Difficulty shifting to a new topic may reflect monotropic attention rather than social insensitivity.
- The person may genuinely not register that the conversational partner has introduced a new topic.
- Returning to a preferred topic may reflect the strong attentional pull of an already-activated interest rather than intentional disregard for the partner's contribution.

## 3. The "Abrupt Shift" Finding

### 3.1 What Research Shows

Across multiple studies, autistic speakers make topic shifts described as "abrupt," "unannounced," "sudden," or "drastic." The Ábalos et al. (2025) study found that while autistic children's topic shifts were more frequent, the key finding was that their shifts were **perceived as more drastically interrupting the flow of conversation** than neurotypical children's shifts.

This perception of "abruptness" relates to:
- **Missing transition markers**: Phrases like "by the way," "that reminds me," "speaking of which," "oh, before I forget" that signal a topic change is coming.
- **Missing contextual bridges**: Explaining the connection between the old topic and the new one ("We were talking about cooking, and that makes me think of restaurants -- have you tried that new place?").
- **Missing pause**: Not leaving a brief silence before introducing a new topic, allowing the prior topic to reach a natural resting point.
- **Missing acknowledgment**: Not briefly acknowledging the current topic before shifting ("That's interesting -- hey, did you see...?").

### 3.2 The Frequency vs. Manner Distinction

**Critical nuance from Abbot-Smith et al. (2025):**
- Autistic children produced topic-supporting responses 62% of the time -- comparable to many neurotypical peers.
- The **frequency** of on-topic responding may be similar, but the **manner** of shifting differs (fewer transition markers, less contextual bridging).
- This means the issue may not be that autistic speakers shift topics *more*, but that they shift topics *differently* -- without the expected "social lubrication."

### 3.3 Why This Matters

The distinction between frequency and manner has practical implications:
- If the problem were raw frequency, the intervention would be "shift topics less often."
- If the problem is manner, the intervention is "use transition markers and contextual bridges when you do shift" -- a much more specific and learnable skill.

The "abruptness" may stem from:
1. **Associative thinking**: Most autistic individuals think associatively rather than linearly. A seemingly unrelated topic shift may follow a perfectly logical (to the speaker) chain of associations that simply wasn't verbalized.
2. **Monotropic attention**: The new topic has captured attentional resources, and the connection to the prior topic wasn't maintained long enough to build a bridge.
3. **Pragmatic skill gap**: The speaker simply hasn't learned or automated the use of transition markers.
4. **Processing demands**: Building a contextual bridge while simultaneously formulating a thought about a new topic is cognitively expensive.

### 3.4 Autistic-Autistic Reception of "Abrupt" Shifts

Research on autistic-autistic conversation suggests these "abrupt" shifts may be received very differently by autistic interlocutors:

**Heasman & Gillespie (2019):** Identified two features of "neurodivergent intersubjectivity":
1. A **generous assumption of common ground** that, when understood, led to rapid rapport (and when not understood, resulted in potentially disruptive utterances).
2. A **low demand for coordination** that ameliorated many challenges associated with disruptive conversational turns.

This suggests that in autistic-autistic conversation, the "abrupt" quality of topic shifts may be less disruptive because:
- Both speakers have a higher tolerance for sudden shifts.
- There's less expectation of contextual bridging.
- Both speakers may process associative connections rapidly.
- The "generous assumption of common ground" means speakers assume their partner can follow the shift.

**Cooperative Overlap:**
Research indicates approximately 80% of autistic individuals use **cooperative overlap** -- talking along with the speaker to show enthusiastic engagement rather than to take the floor. This is often misinterpreted as "interrupting" by NT interlocutors but functions as rapport-building among those who share the style. Topic shifts within cooperative overlap are seen as energizing rather than disruptive.

## 4. Cognitive Factors in Topic Management

### 4.1 Theory of Mind and Topic Management

**The Connection:**
Maintaining a conversational topic requires tracking what the listener is interested in, what they know, and what information would be relevant to them. This is fundamentally a Theory of Mind (ToM) task.

**Research Findings:**
- Abbot-Smith et al. (2025) found that **Theory of Mind was a significant independent predictor of topic-supporting responses** across both autistic and neurotypical children, even when controlling for age, vocabulary, non-verbal reasoning, and working memory.
- This held across all three studies and both diagnostic groups.
- Happé's work connecting relevance theory (Sperber & Wilson) to autism suggests that pragmatic competence -- including topic management -- is contingent on the ability to attribute mental states (beliefs, intentions) to others.
- Autistic individuals who have stronger ToM abilities tend to show better conversational topic management.

**Practical Implication:** Topic management difficulties in autism may partly reflect challenges in real-time computation of the listener's knowledge state and interests, rather than unwillingness to engage with the listener's topics.

### 4.2 Working Memory and Topic Management

**The Connection:**
Holding the current topic in mind while simultaneously:
- Processing the partner's contribution
- Planning one's own response
- Monitoring whether one's response is on-topic
- Tracking the conversational flow

This places heavy demands on working memory.

**Research Findings:**
- Abbot-Smith et al. (2025) found working memory was **positively related to contingent elaborations** and **negatively related to off-topic verbosity** in neurotypical children.
- In autistic children, working memory showed correlational relationships but didn't reach significance as a unique predictor in regression models, though patterns were similar.
- A meta-analysis of working memory in ASD (Springer, *Neuropsychology Review*) confirms working memory differences are present but heterogeneous across individuals.

### 4.3 Executive Function: Cognitive Flexibility and Inhibition

**Cognitive Flexibility:**
- Shifting from one topic to another requires cognitive flexibility -- the ability to disengage from one mental set and engage with another.
- A meta-analysis confirmed that autistic people have greater cognitive flexibility difficulties present across the lifespan.
- However, experimental measures often do not report cognitive flexibility deficits in autism, suggesting the difficulty is more apparent in complex, real-world situations (like unstructured conversation) than in laboratory tasks.
- Planning and cognitive flexibility emerged as the most consistently affected executive function domains; working memory, attention, and inhibition showed greater heterogeneity.

**Inhibition:**
- Staying on a non-preferred topic requires inhibiting the pull toward a preferred topic.
- Inhibiting a response about one's special interest when the conversation is about something else is an executive function demand.
- Variation in restricted and repetitive behaviors (including topic perseveration) relates to inhibitory control and shifting abilities.

**How These Predict Topic Management Performance:**
- Both Theory of Mind and executive function abilities explained **unique variance** in autistic symptoms in the communication domain.
- In adolescence, Theory of Mind ability (but not executive function) was directly related to autistic symptom expression.
- The integrated functioning of executive skills is important for maintaining and updating conversational flow while bringing in relevant information from working memory and inhibiting irrelevant responses.

## 5. The Double Empathy Perspective

### 5.1 Are Autistic Topic Shifts Truly "Irrelevant"?

**The Double Empathy Problem (Milton, 2012):**
Damian Milton proposed that difficulties in autistic-NT interaction are bidirectional -- resulting from a mutual lack of understanding between neurotypes, not a unilateral autistic deficit. Published in *Disability & Society*, 27(6), 883-887.

**Applied to Topic Management:**
- What appears as an "irrelevant" topic shift to a neurotypical listener may follow a perfectly logical associative chain for the autistic speaker.
- Autistic thinking is frequently **associative** rather than linear: "one thought connects to another through sometimes loose or seemingly irrelevant connections" that share "a common basic element, even if they are not sequentially related."
- The speaker's topic shift may be deeply relevant from their perspective -- the connection simply wasn't verbalized.
- The problem may not be relevance but **mutual manifestness** (relevance theory term): the speaker and listener don't share enough context to see the same connections.

**Relevance Theory Reframing:**
Sasson & Morrison (2019 area; Cummins et al., 2021, *Frontiers in Psychology*) reframed autistic pragmatic "impairments" using relevance theory:
- Communication is described as a **two-way, intersubjective phenomenon**.
- Relevance theory positions communication as contingent on shared, mutually recognized "relevance."
- Interactions between autistic and non-autistic interlocutors are "mutually-mediated endeavours, with success influenced by ease of achieving mutual manifestness."
- When two people share a similar cognitive style (both autistic, or both NT), achieving mutual manifestness is easier.

**Source:** [Mutual (Mis)understanding - Frontiers in Psychology](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2021.616664/full) | [Milton (2012) - Tandfonline](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/09687599.2012.710008)

### 5.2 Research on Autistic-Autistic Conversation

**Crompton et al. (2020) - Diffusion Chain Study:**
- Used diffusion chains of 8 participants (n = 72 total): 3 all-autistic chains, 3 all-NT chains, 3 mixed chains.
- **Finding**: Information transfer within all-autistic chains did NOT differ from all-NT chains. Quality of communication was equivalent.
- Mixed chains showed **significantly steeper decline** in detail retention.
- **Rapport ratings** were significantly lower for mixed chains than for same-neurotype chains.
- Autistic-autistic interactions showed rapport equivalent to NT-NT interactions.

**Heasman & Gillespie (2019) - Neurodivergent Intersubjectivity:**
- Studied 30 autistic adults during collaborative video gaming.
- Found that autistic dyads could "build rapport and knowledge rapidly and effectively" through generous assumptions of common ground.
- The low demand for coordination between autistic speakers meant that conversational turns perceived as "disruptive" by NT standards were ameliorated within autistic-autistic interaction.

**Broader Pattern:**
Research consistently shows that conversations between autistic interactants have a greater sense of "flow, rapport, and intersubjective attunement" compared to their interactions with non-autistic partners. Autistic interlocutors appeared to experience improvements in their individual communicative competence in autistic-autistic conversations compared to cross-neurotype conversations.

**Sources:**
- [Crompton et al. (2020) - SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/1362361320919286) | [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7545656/)

### 5.3 NT Topic Norms Are Culturally Specific, Not Universal

**Cross-Cultural Variation in Conversational Norms:**
Research demonstrates that behavioral norms vary considerably across cultures, making social communication interpretations particularly susceptible to cultural influence:

- **Eye contact**: In China, a child's limited eye contact with an adult is a sign of respect -- the opposite of Western expectations.
- **Communicative gestures**: Pointing conventions vary across cultures (hand, head, face).
- **Communication styles**: High-context cultures (much meaning conveyed through nonverbal cues and context) vs. low-context cultures (direct, explicit communication). The "rules" of topic management embedded in Western conversational norms are not universal.
- **Responses to spontaneity**: Test items measuring response to uncertainty don't predict autism equally well across cultures (e.g., Japan).

**Implication:** The specific topic management norms that autistic speakers are said to violate -- use of transition markers, contextual bridging, gradual topic shifts -- are features of particular cultural communication styles, not universal human requirements. Other cultures and subcultures may have very different expectations for how topics are managed.

**Source:** [Cultural Factors in Autism - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7614360/)

### 5.4 When "Abrupt" Shifts Enable Creativity and Flow

Within autistic-autistic conversation and certain creative/intellectual contexts, rapid topic shifts without extensive bridging can:
- **Accelerate idea generation**: Bypassing social lubrication gets to new ideas faster.
- **Enable associative creativity**: Unexpected juxtapositions of topics can spark novel connections.
- **Maintain flow**: In monotropic processing, the shift may represent authentic tracking of the most interesting thread, and forcing gradual transitions may disrupt flow.
- **Reduce cognitive load**: Not having to construct bridges frees cognitive resources for content.

This is consistent with research on cooperative overlap, where enthusiastic "interrupting" among people who share the same communication style has "the positive effect of greasing rather than gumming up the conversational wheels" (Tannen).

## 6. Practical Strategies

### 6.1 How to Introduce a New Topic Smoothly

**Using Transition Markers:**
Transition markers are phrases that signal to the listener that a topic change is coming, giving them time to mentally prepare:

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Direct flagging** | "By the way..." / "That reminds me..." / "Speaking of which..." / "On a different note..." / "Changing the subject..." |
| **Connection-building** | "That makes me think of..." / "Related to what you said about X..." / "You mentioned Y, and that connects to..." |
| **Permission-seeking** | "Can I ask you about something else?" / "Mind if I change the subject?" / "I want to ask you something different..." |
| **Acknowledgment + shift** | "That's really interesting -- hey, have you heard about...?" / "I hear what you're saying. I've also been thinking about..." |
| **Temporal bridges** | "Oh, before I forget..." / "I've been meaning to ask you..." |

**Technique: The Bridge Sentence:**
Before shifting to a new topic, briefly:
1. **Acknowledge** the current topic ("That's a good point about the meeting schedule.")
2. **Flag the shift** ("That reminds me of something else...")
3. **Introduce** the new topic ("...did you see the email about the project deadline?")

### 6.2 How to Maintain a Topic You Didn't Choose

This is cognitively demanding for monotropic thinkers whose attention is pulled toward their own interests. Strategies include:

- **Active listening responses**: Nodding, "mm-hmm," "interesting," "tell me more" -- these maintain the topic without requiring deep engagement.
- **Asking questions about their topic**: "How did that happen?" / "What was that like?" / "What do you think about...?" Questions keep the other person talking and reduce your need to generate on-topic content.
- **Finding a connection to your interests**: Even if the topic is unfamiliar, look for an aspect that connects to something you care about. If they're talking about gardening and you like systems, you might ask about their watering schedule.
- **Setting a mental timer**: Tell yourself you'll stay on their topic for 2-3 exchanges before checking if there's an opening to shift.
- **Using the "question-statement balance"**: After they share something, respond with a brief comment + a follow-up question to keep the ball in their court.

### 6.3 How to Shift Away from a Topic Without Seeming Rude

- **Wait for a natural pause**: Don't shift mid-sentence or mid-thought.
- **Acknowledge before shifting**: "That's a great story. Hey, I've been wanting to ask you about..."
- **Use a bridge phrase**: "I'm thinking..." / "Let me switch topics for a sec..." / "I just lost my train of thought -- oh, but speaking of..."
- **Reference time or context**: "Oh, I just realized we haven't talked about..." / "Before we run out of time, I wanted to mention..."
- **For online/text**: "Topic change:" or "Random but:" can serve as explicit topic-shift markers that are socially acceptable in digital contexts.

### 6.4 Managing Your Own Tendency Toward Topic Perseveration

- **Mental timer**: Aim to speak for roughly 30 seconds to 1 minute before pausing to give others a chance to respond. Visualize conversation as a tennis match where the ball should go back and forth.
- **Check-in questions**: After talking about your interest for a while, pause and ask: "Am I going on too long about this?" or "Is this interesting to you?" or "Do you want to hear more or should we talk about something else?"
- **The "two-turn rule"**: After you've had two consecutive turns focused on your topic, deliberately ask a question about the other person's interests or experience.
- **Prepare exit strategies**: Have planned transition phrases ready: "Anyway, enough about [topic]. What have you been up to?"
- **Context-match your depth**: In casual social conversation, share the headline about your interest, not the full encyclopedia entry. Save the deep dive for people who share the interest.
- **Self-monitoring practice**: Record yourself in conversation (with permission) and review to notice patterns. Count how many turns are about your topic vs. theirs.

### 6.5 How to Read Signals That the Listener Wants a Topic Change

This is one of the most challenging skills because it requires real-time processing of subtle nonverbal cues. Key signals include:

**Body language signals:**
- Looking away or checking phone
- Shifting body weight or feet pointing toward exit
- Folded arms (may indicate boredom)
- Decreased nodding or facial responsiveness

**Verbal signals:**
- Increasingly short responses ("uh-huh," "yeah," "cool")
- Flat or monotone acknowledgments
- Attempting to introduce new topics themselves
- Changing the subject explicitly
- "Anyway..." or "So..." (transition signals)
- Decreasing question-asking about your topic

**Contextual signals:**
- Extended silence after your turn
- Others in a group conversation stopping their engagement
- The conversation has been on the same topic for an unusually long time (5+ minutes in casual settings)

**When in doubt, ask directly:**
"I realize I've been talking about [topic] for a while. Want me to keep going or should we talk about something else?" This is a perfectly acceptable social move and shows self-awareness.

### 6.6 Polite Redirection Phrases

When you want to redirect the conversation or when someone else is talking at length about a topic you're not interested in:

- "I'd love to hear more about that, but can we also talk about...?"
- "That's really interesting! I'm curious about something else too..."
- "I want to make sure we get to talk about [other thing] too."
- "I hear you really like talking about [topic]. I'm more interested in [other topic] though -- what do you think about that?"
- "I'm not super knowledgeable about [their topic], but I'd love to hear about your thoughts on [related topic I know more about]."

### 6.7 Navigating "Safe Topics" in Small Talk

**Why Small Talk Is Difficult:**
Phatic communication (small talk) is "the non-referential use of language to share feelings or establish a mood of sociability rather than to communicate information or ideas." For autistic people who are direct, honest, and facts-focused, this can feel purposeless or overwhelming due to the many micro-verbal and nonverbal cues involved.

**Safe Topic Categories:**
| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Environment** | Weather, the venue, the food at an event, something visible in the shared space |
| **Entertainment** | Movies, TV shows, books, music, podcasts, games |
| **Plans** | Weekend plans, upcoming holidays, travel plans |
| **Work/School** | General "How's work going?" / "Busy week?" |
| **Pets/Animals** | "Do you have any pets?" is almost always well-received |
| **Food** | Restaurants, cooking, dietary preferences |
| **Local events** | Community happenings, local news |
| **Shared experience** | Whatever brought you both to the current situation |

**Strategies for Small Talk:**
- **Prepare in advance**: Pick 2-3 topics you can talk about with anyone.
- **Use environmental observations**: Comment on something in the shared space as a conversation starter.
- **Bond over shared inconvenience**: Complaining mildly about weather/traffic/long lines is a reliable small talk opener.
- **Ask questions**: "What do you do for fun?" / "Seen anything good lately?" / "How do you know [the host]?"
- **Follow up**: After they answer, ask a follow-up question rather than immediately sharing your own answer.
- **Prepared exit phrases**: "I've got a deadline coming up, but it was great chatting with you." / "I should go say hi to some others, but let's continue this conversation later."

## 7. Topic Management in Different Contexts

### 7.1 Workplace: Meetings vs. Casual Chat

**Structured Meetings:**
- Meetings with clear agendas are easier for autistic employees to navigate because:
  - Topics are predetermined and listed
  - Turn-taking has more explicit structure
  - There are clearer boundaries for when topics change
  - The purpose of each topic is usually stated
- **Recommendations**: Provide agendas in advance, outline topics to be discussed, state desired outcomes, give time warnings before topics change.
- Written instructions or emails before the meeting help autistic employees prepare.

**Unstructured Meetings/Casual Chat:**
- Unstructured meetings are a significant source of stress for autistic individuals because:
  - There's no clear agenda, so purpose and goals are ambiguous
  - Topic shifts happen rapidly and unpredictably
  - Social cues determine when to contribute (which autistic speakers may miss)
  - There's pressure to contribute "relevant" comments in real-time
- Autistic individuals may "avoid speaking up when they have something to say or fail to speak as they miss pauses in conversation, or alternatively, some may speak out of turn or offer irrelevant comments because they are unsure when it is appropriate to contribute."

**Workplace Small Talk:**
- The expectation of brief, reciprocal exchanges about non-work topics (weekend plans, how was your morning) follows implicit rules that aren't taught.
- Many autistic employees report that workplace small talk is more cognitively draining than the actual work.
- Strategies: Keep a mental list of 2-3 go-to small talk topics for the break room; use reciprocal questions ("How about you?") to share the conversational load.

### 7.2 Educational Settings

**How Classroom Topic Norms Differ from Social Norms:**
- In classrooms, topics are **teacher-directed** with clear expectations for when student contributions are welcome (raised hands, discussion time).
- Topic maintenance in educational settings is about **staying on the assigned topic** rather than negotiating shared topics.
- Autistic students may struggle with:
  - Waiting to be called on (especially when they have a relevant contribution)
  - Staying on the assigned topic when it doesn't match their interests
  - Understanding the difference between a relevant question and a tangential one
  - Transitioning between topics as the lesson moves on

**Strategies for Educational Settings:**
- Visual supports: Written rules, picture schedules, topic cue cards.
- Structured routines for discussion: Clear signals for when discussion begins and ends.
- Embracing special interests: Teachers who incorporate student interests into lessons report better engagement and relationship-building.
- Whole-classroom conversation skills teaching: Abbot-Smith et al. (2023) advocate for universal conversation skills instruction that benefits all students, not just autistic ones.

**Source:** [Autism in the Classroom - Autism Speaks](https://www.autismspeaks.org/tool-kit-excerpt/autism-classroom-strategies)

### 7.3 Digital Communication

**Advantages of Digital/Text-Based Communication:**
Research consistently shows that digital communication provides significant advantages for autistic topic management:

- **Asynchronous pacing**: No need for real-time processing of topic shifts; you can take time to read, process, and respond.
- **Reduced nonverbal demands**: No need to simultaneously process facial expressions, tone, and body language while tracking topic.
- **Explicit topic structure**: Threading (Slack, Discord, forums) makes topic organization visible and explicit.
- **"Topic change" markers**: In text, it's socially acceptable to write "Topic change:" or "Random but:" or "Unrelated:" before shifting topics.
- **Parallel topics**: Multiple topics can be maintained simultaneously in different threads/channels.
- **Control over timing**: You can pause, process, and respond in your own time.
- **Edit before sending**: Unlike spoken conversation, you can review and revise before the other person sees your response.

**Research Findings:**
- Autistic people perceive benefits of computer-mediated communication in terms of "increased comprehension and control over communication, access to similar others, and the opportunity to express their true selves."
- Text-based communication reduced stress from managing eye contact, the pace of conversation, and social chit-chat.
- Written/asynchronous communication aligns more closely with the cognitive and sensory profiles of many autistic people.

**Topic Management in Specific Digital Contexts:**
| Platform | Topic Management Features |
|---|---|
| **Slack/Discord** | Threads separate topics explicitly; you can contribute to a topic even after it's "passed" in the main channel |
| **Text/SMS** | Can use explicit markers ("New subject:"); no time pressure for topic transitions |
| **Email** | Subject lines define topics; you can address multiple topics with numbered lists; no real-time pressure |
| **Social media** | Comments on specific posts are inherently on-topic; threads can branch |
| **Forums/Reddit** | Topics defined by threads; you can search and join conversations on topics that interest you |

**Challenges:**
- Back-and-forth texting can still be challenging for knowing when to respond or maintaining conversational flow.
- Special-interest-driven content: baseline observations showed autistic children's text content was often based on "preoccupations with specific topics."
- Group chats may move rapidly and be difficult to follow.

### 7.4 Social Gatherings: Multi-Person Conversations

**The Scaling Problem:**
The more people in an unstructured conversation, the harder it becomes for autistic individuals. In one-on-one interaction, you monitor yourself and one other person. In group conversation, you must simultaneously:
- Track multiple speakers' topics and contributions
- Monitor when it's appropriate to contribute
- Process rapid topic shifts initiated by multiple people
- Detect when the group's collective attention has moved to a new topic
- Find gaps to contribute without "interrupting"

**Common Experiences:**
- Waiting patiently for a turn but finding the topic has shifted by the time a gap opens.
- Being perceived as "the quiet one" despite having plenty to say.
- Contributing a comment that was on-topic when formulated but off-topic by the time it's delivered (because the group shifted topics).
- Finding it easier to participate in smaller sub-groups or one-on-one side conversations at the same gathering.

**Strategies for Group Conversations:**
- **Pair off**: Seek 1:1 or small group (2-3 people) conversations within larger gatherings.
- **Use the environment**: Offer to help with food/drinks to have a natural role that includes brief, structured interactions.
- **Accept observer role**: It's okay to listen more than talk in group settings and contribute selectively.
- **Prepare "re-entry" phrases**: "Going back to what [Name] said about..." allows you to contribute to a topic that's already shifted.
- **Choose your moments**: Contributing during a natural pause or when asked directly is easier than trying to jump into rapid cross-talk.
- **Seek structured social activities**: Board games, trivia nights, and activities with built-in structure provide natural topic management frameworks.

**Source:** [Context, Group Size, and Social Demands - Autistic Scholar](https://www.autisticscholar.com/context-groups-social-demands/)

## 8. Key Research Themes and Implications for the Knowledge Base

### 8.1 The Continuum Finding

Both Ábalos et al. (2025) and Abbot-Smith et al. (2025) emphasize that conversational skills exist on a continuum, with individual differences within groups often exceeding between-group differences. This means:
- Topic management is not a binary "can/can't" skill.
- Many neurotypical people also struggle with aspects of topic management.
- Differences between autistic and NT speakers are a matter of degree, not kind.
- Universal, whole-population approaches to conversation skill support may be more appropriate than autism-specific interventions.

### 8.2 The Bidirectional Nature of Topic Management Difficulties

The double empathy framework, Crompton's diffusion chain study, and Heasman & Gillespie's intersubjectivity research all converge on the finding that topic management "difficulties" are **interactional** rather than **individual**:
- Autistic speakers communicate effectively with each other.
- NT speakers communicate effectively with each other.
- Difficulties arise specifically in cross-neurotype interaction.
- This means the "problem" is mutual, not unilateral.

### 8.3 Manner Over Frequency

The key practical finding is that autistic speakers may not shift topics more often than NT speakers, but they shift them differently (without transition markers, contextual bridges, or pauses). This suggests that learning specific transition phrases and bridging techniques may be the most targeted and effective intervention.

### 8.4 Practical Strategy Focus

For a knowledge base teaching English pragmatics to autistic adults:
- **Teach transition markers explicitly** -- these are learnable, scriptable phrases.
- **Explain the "why"** behind topic management norms -- not "you should do this because it's polite" but "neurotypical listeners process topic shifts better when they get a transition signal."
- **Validate autistic conversational style** -- in autistic-autistic contexts, the NT rules don't apply.
- **Provide context-specific guidance** -- what works in a meeting is different from a party is different from a text thread.
- **Acknowledge the cognitive load** -- topic management in NT style is genuinely more demanding for monotropic thinkers, and that effort deserves recognition.
