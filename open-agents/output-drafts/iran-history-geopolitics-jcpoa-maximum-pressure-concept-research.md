# JCPOA: The Deal, Withdrawal & Maximum Pressure (2015-2022)

## 1. The Road to the Deal: Obama's Strategic Vision

### Why Diplomacy Over Confrontation

President Barack Obama entered office determined to end what he viewed as an era of counterproductive militarism in the Middle East. Having opposed the Iraq War, Obama sought to replace the reflex toward unilateral military action with multilateral diplomacy grounded in international institutions. The Iranian nuclear program, which had steadily advanced through the George W. Bush years despite escalating sanctions, presented the definitive test case.

Obama's strategic calculation was straightforward: Iran's nuclear infrastructure had grown too large to simply bomb away, and even a military strike would only delay the program by a few years while virtually guaranteeing Iran would pursue a weapon with full national commitment. Diplomacy, if it could work, offered a longer-lasting solution by embedding Iran within an international verification framework.

### Secret Negotiations and the Opening

The diplomatic breakthrough began with secret back-channel talks between the United States and Iran, facilitated by Oman, beginning in 2012. These discussions laid the groundwork for the election of Hassan Rouhani as Iranian president in June 2013 on a platform of economic recovery through engagement with the West. In September 2013, Obama made a historic phone call to Rouhani -- the highest-level direct contact between American and Iranian leaders since the 1979 revolution.

Formal multilateral negotiations followed, involving the P5+1 (the five permanent UN Security Council members -- the United States, United Kingdom, France, Russia, and China -- plus Germany) along with the European Union. An interim agreement, the Joint Plan of Action (JPOA), was signed in November 2013, freezing certain aspects of Iran's nuclear program while negotiations continued.

### Twenty Months of Negotiation

Negotiations stretched over 20 grueling months. Key sticking points included the number of centrifuges Iran could retain, the fate of the underground Fordow enrichment facility, the duration of restrictions, and the sequencing of sanctions relief. Secretary of State John Kerry led the U.S. negotiating team, with Energy Secretary Ernest Moniz providing technical nuclear expertise opposite his Iranian counterpart, Ali Akbar Salehi.

The Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action was finalized in Vienna on July 14, 2015.

## 2. What the Deal Actually Required: Technical Details

### Enrichment Limits

- **Enrichment level cap**: Iran agreed to limit uranium enrichment to 3.67% purity (sufficient for civilian power generation but far below the ~90% needed for weapons-grade material) for 15 years.
- **Stockpile reduction**: Iran was required to reduce its stockpile of low-enriched uranium (LEU) by 97%, from approximately 10,000 kg to just 300 kg, for 15 years.
- **Centrifuge restrictions**: Iran had to reduce its operating centrifuges to 5,060 first-generation IR-1 centrifuges at the Natanz Fuel Enrichment Plant for 10 years. An additional 1,044 IR-1 centrifuges were permitted at Fordow, but only for non-enrichment purposes (isotope production for medical and industrial uses).
- **Advanced centrifuge R&D**: Iran was barred from operating advanced centrifuges (IR-2m, IR-4, IR-5, IR-6, IR-8) for enrichment during the first 10 years, though limited research and development was permitted on a constrained timeline.

### Breakout Time Extension

The central achievement of the deal was extending Iran's nuclear "breakout time" -- the time needed to produce enough fissile material for one nuclear weapon -- from an estimated 2-3 months to at least 12 months. This one-year window was designed to give the international community enough time to detect any dash toward a weapon and respond.

### The Fordow and Arak Facilities

- **Fordow**: The deeply buried underground facility at Fordow, built inside a mountain near Qom and considered virtually impervious to conventional military strikes, was converted from an enrichment site to a nuclear physics and technology research center. No enrichment was permitted there.
- **Arak Heavy Water Reactor**: The Arak IR-40 heavy water reactor, which could have produced weapons-grade plutonium, was to be redesigned so that it would not produce significant quantities of weapons-grade plutonium. The original core was to be removed, filled with concrete, and rendered inoperable.

### Inspection and Verification Regime

The JCPOA established what the IAEA described as the most robust verification system ever negotiated for a nuclear program:

- **Comprehensive Safeguards Agreement**: Iran maintained its obligations under this pre-existing agreement.
- **Additional Protocol**: Iran agreed to provisionally apply the IAEA Additional Protocol, which provides inspectors with expanded access rights. This includes the ability to visit declared and undeclared sites, and access to the full nuclear supply chain -- from uranium mines and mills to centrifuge workshops.
- **Continuous monitoring**: The IAEA installed cameras, seals, and other monitoring equipment at key facilities. At Natanz, continuous enrichment monitoring systems tracked uranium hexafluoride flows in real time.
- **Managed Access**: For sites the IAEA suspected but Iran contested, a dispute resolution mechanism allowed the IAEA to request access. If Iran objected, the Joint Commission (representatives of all JCPOA parties) could decide by majority vote, giving Iran no veto over inspections.
- **Procurement Channel**: All nuclear-related purchases by Iran had to go through an approval process, providing transparency into Iran's procurement activities.

### Sunset Clauses: What Expires When

The JCPOA's restrictions did not all expire at once. Instead, various provisions had different timelines:

- **Year 5 (October 2020)**: UN conventional weapons embargo lifted.
- **Year 8 (October 2023)**: UN restrictions on ballistic missile-related transfers expired.
- **Year 10 (2025-2026)**: Centrifuge quantity and type restrictions expire. Iran permitted to begin using advanced centrifuges and expand enrichment capacity.
- **Year 15 (2030-2031)**: Enrichment level cap (3.67%) and LEU stockpile limit (300 kg) expire. Iran free to enrich to higher levels and accumulate larger stockpiles.
- **Year 20 (2035-2036)**: IAEA Additional Protocol becomes permanent (if ratified by Iran's parliament).
- **Year 25 (2040-2041)**: Continuous surveillance of centrifuge production facilities ends.
- **Permanent**: Iran's commitment under the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT) never to seek, develop, or acquire nuclear weapons. The IAEA's standard safeguards agreement also continues indefinitely.

Critics argued that the sunset clauses meant Iran could simply wait out restrictions and then legally build up a near-threshold capability. Supporters countered that the permanent NPT commitments, combined with the Additional Protocol and decades of monitoring data, would make any future weapons program far more detectable.

### Sanctions Relief Provisions

In exchange for nuclear concessions, Iran received relief from nuclear-related sanctions:

- **UN sanctions**: Seven UN Security Council resolutions imposing sanctions were terminated.
- **EU sanctions**: The EU lifted nuclear-related sanctions on banking, insurance, oil, gas, petrochemicals, shipping, and trade.
- **US sanctions**: The United States waived or suspended nuclear-related secondary sanctions (affecting foreign entities doing business with Iran). However, primary U.S. sanctions (which prohibit American persons from dealing with Iran) and non-nuclear sanctions targeting Iran's support for terrorism, missile program, and human rights abuses remained in place.
- **Frozen assets**: Iran gained access to approximately $100-125 billion in foreign exchange reserves held in banks abroad, though a significant portion was already committed to existing debts and obligations. The actual liquid, accessible amount was debated, with some estimates putting it between $30-55 billion.

Implementation Day was January 16, 2016, after the IAEA verified Iran had completed its initial nuclear steps.

## 3. Implementation and Compliance (2016-2018)

### Iran's Compliance

From January 2016 through Trump's withdrawal in May 2018, the IAEA issued multiple quarterly reports confirming Iran was complying with all nuclear provisions of the JCPOA. This was consistent and unambiguous: Iran kept enrichment at or below 3.67%, maintained its stockpile below 300 kg, operated only the permitted number of centrifuges, and provided inspectors with required access.

Even Trump's own Secretary of State, Mike Pompeo, acknowledged that the IAEA had confirmed Iran's compliance. The IAEA's independence on this point was critical -- it provided a factual basis that supporters of the deal repeatedly invoked.

### Economic Impact of Sanctions Relief

The Iranian economy experienced a significant recovery following Implementation Day:
- GDP growth surged to 13.8% in the Iranian year 2016/17, driven largely by a rebound in oil exports.
- Oil production recovered from roughly 2.8 million barrels per day (bpd) under sanctions to over 3.8 million bpd.
- European companies began re-entering the Iranian market, including major deals with Total (oil/gas), Airbus (aircraft), and Peugeot (automobiles).
- Iran's foreign trade grew substantially, particularly with European and Asian partners.

However, the economic benefits were unevenly distributed within Iranian society, and many Iranians complained that the promised improvements in living standards had not materialized quickly or broadly enough. This became a political vulnerability for the Rouhani government.

## 4. Opposition to the Deal

### Israeli Opposition

Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu was the deal's most vocal international opponent. His campaign against the JCPOA took several dramatic forms:

- **2012 UN "Bomb Diagram" Speech**: At the UN General Assembly, Netanyahu held up a cartoon-like diagram of a bomb with a lit fuse, drawing a literal "red line" to illustrate how close Iran was to nuclear weapons capability. While this predated the JCPOA, it established Netanyahu's visual rhetoric on the issue.
- **March 2015 Address to Congress**: In an unprecedented move, Netanyahu accepted an invitation from Republican House Speaker John Boehner to address a joint session of Congress -- without consulting or informing the White House. The speech, delivered on March 3, 2015, directly attacked the emerging deal: "This is a bad deal. It's a very bad deal. We're better off without it." The address was seen as a breach of diplomatic protocol and deepened the partisan divide on the deal. Many Democratic members boycotted the speech.
- **April 2018 "Iran Lied" Presentation**: Netanyahu gave a televised presentation claiming Israel's Mossad had obtained Iran's secret nuclear archive, proving Iran had lied about never having had a nuclear weapons program. While the archive confirmed the existence of a pre-2003 weapons research program (something already known to the IAEA), it did not demonstrate current violations of the JCPOA.

Israel's core objection was that the deal legitimized Iran's enrichment program, that the sunset clauses would eventually allow Iran to become a nuclear threshold state, and that it did not address Iran's ballistic missile program or regional activities.

### Saudi Opposition

Saudi Arabia's opposition was rooted in several concerns:
- **Exclusion from negotiations**: Saudi leaders felt they should have been consulted or included in talks that would directly affect regional security dynamics.
- **Fear of an empowered Iran**: Riyadh worried that sanctions relief would provide Iran with resources to accelerate its regional proxy activities, particularly support for Hezbollah, the Houthis, and Shia militias in Iraq.
- **Nuclear proliferation concerns**: Former Saudi Intelligence Minister Prince Turki al-Faisal warned that any enrichment capabilities Iran obtained would be sought by other countries in the region -- implying Saudi Arabia would pursue its own nuclear program.
- **Ballistic missiles**: Saudi Arabia and Israel both criticized the deal for not addressing Iran's ballistic missile program.

The Saudi Foreign Ministry stated that "Iran used economic gains from the lifting of sanctions to continue its activities to destabilize the region, particularly by developing ballistic missiles and supporting terrorist groups."

### Republican Opposition in Congress

Nearly every Republican in Congress opposed the deal. Key arguments included:

1. **"Worst deal ever"**: Trump campaigned on this slogan, but the characterization was widely shared among Republicans.
2. **Sunset clauses**: Critics argued the time-limited restrictions merely delayed rather than prevented an Iranian bomb.
3. **Zero enrichment goal abandoned**: The original diplomatic objective had been to completely end Iran's enrichment program. The JCPOA's acceptance of limited enrichment was seen as capitulation.
4. **Inadequate inspections**: Critics claimed the 24-day dispute resolution timeline for contested sites gave Iran time to hide evidence (inspectors and nuclear experts disputed this, noting that nuclear activities leave traces detectable for years).
5. **Financial windfall for terrorism**: A letter signed by 190 former generals and admirals argued that $150 billion in sanctions relief would fuel Iranian proxy groups. (The $150 billion figure was disputed; much of the frozen assets were already committed to debts.)
6. **Regional behavior unaddressed**: The deal focused solely on the nuclear issue, leaving out Iran's missile program, support for proxies, and human rights record.

In September 2015, the House voted 269-162 against approving the JCPOA. However, Senate Democrats blocked a Republican-led effort to reject the agreement through a procedural vote of 58-42 (short of the 60 needed), and Obama had pledged to veto any disapproval resolution regardless. The deal went forward as an executive agreement, not a treaty -- a decision that made it easier to implement but also easier for a future president to undo.

## 5. Trump's Withdrawal (May 8, 2018)

### The Decision and Its Influences

On May 8, 2018, President Donald Trump announced that the United States would withdraw from the JCPOA and reimpose all nuclear-related sanctions on Iran. This came despite:
- The IAEA's consistent confirmation of Iranian compliance
- Opposition from European allies
- Warnings from many U.S. national security professionals

Key figures who influenced the decision:

- **John Bolton**: Appointed as National Security Advisor in April 2018 (replacing the more cautious H.R. McMaster), Bolton was a lifelong advocate for regime change in Iran and had publicly called for bombing Iran's nuclear facilities. He pressed Trump to withdraw within a month of taking office.
- **Mike Pompeo**: As CIA Director and then Secretary of State, Pompeo was a consistent JCPOA critic who supported withdrawal and designed the subsequent "maximum pressure" campaign.
- **Benjamin Netanyahu**: His April 2018 "Iran Lied" presentation about Iran's nuclear archive was timed to influence Trump's decision. Trump cited Israel's intelligence in his withdrawal announcement.
- **Mohammed bin Salman (MBS)**: The Saudi Crown Prince cultivated a close personal relationship with Trump's son-in-law Jared Kushner and was a vocal opponent of the deal.
- **Sheldon Adelson and other donors**: Major Republican donors with strong views on Iran policy also played a role, with Adelson reportedly urging Trump to be tough on Iran.

### Stated Rationale

Trump's stated rationale focused on:
1. The deal's sunset clauses, which he claimed would allow Iran to eventually build nuclear weapons
2. The deal's failure to address Iran's ballistic missile program
3. The deal's failure to address Iran's "malign regional activities"
4. Israel's intelligence showing Iran had previously pursued nuclear weapons (though this predated the deal and was already known)
5. Iran's "destabilizing activities" across the Middle East

### The Fundamental Irony

Iran was in full compliance with the JCPOA at the time of withdrawal. The Trump administration was, in effect, withdrawing from a deal that was working exactly as designed on its core objective -- preventing Iran from acquiring nuclear weapons. The withdrawal was driven not by a failure of the deal's terms, but by objections to what the deal did not cover and by a belief that "maximum pressure" could achieve a more comprehensive agreement.

### European Response

America's European allies -- France, Germany, and the United Kingdom (the "E3") -- opposed the withdrawal and attempted to salvage the deal:

- **Joint statement**: The E3 leaders declared that the UN Security Council resolution endorsing the JCPOA remained the "binding international legal framework" and that they would continue to abide by it.
- **INSTEX**: In January 2019, the E3 created the Instrument in Support of Trade Exchanges (INSTEX), a special-purpose vehicle designed to facilitate legitimate trade with Iran outside the U.S. financial system, thus circumventing American secondary sanctions. Six additional European countries later joined.
- **Reality vs. aspiration**: INSTEX was largely a symbolic failure. It reportedly completed only a single transaction. European companies, terrified of losing access to the vastly larger U.S. market, overwhelmingly chose to comply with American sanctions rather than continue business with Iran. EU-Iran trade collapsed by 71.5% year-on-year in 2019.

The European experience demonstrated a fundamental structural reality: U.S. dominance of the global financial system meant that American secondary sanctions could effectively override European sovereignty on trade policy, even when European governments opposed those sanctions.

## 6. Maximum Pressure Campaign (2018-2021)

### Sanctions Reimposition

The Trump administration reimposed all pre-JCPOA nuclear sanctions and added new ones, creating a comprehensive economic pressure campaign:

- **Oil sanctions**: Waivers for eight countries initially permitted continued Iranian oil purchases during a wind-down period, but these waivers were revoked in May 2019. Iran's oil exports plummeted from roughly 2.5 million bpd to under 500,000 bpd.
- **Financial sanctions**: Iran's central bank and major financial institutions were sanctioned, effectively cutting Iran off from the global financial system.
- **Secondary sanctions**: Any foreign entity doing significant business with Iran risked being cut off from the U.S. financial system -- a threat that was devastating given the dollar's role as the world's reserve currency.
- **Sectoral sanctions**: Targeting Iran's petrochemical, metals, mining, and automotive industries.

### Economic Impact on Iran

The effects were devastating:
- GDP contracted approximately 6% in 2018 and nearly 7% in 2019 (IMF figures).
- The Iranian rial lost roughly 60% of its value.
- Inflation soared to over 40%.
- Unemployment rose significantly, with youth unemployment particularly severe.
- Oil revenues dropped by tens of billions of dollars annually.
- China became virtually the only significant market for Iranian oil, purchased at steep discounts.

### Iran's Response: Gradual JCPOA Violations

After initially waiting a year for Europe to provide meaningful sanctions relief (which never materialized through INSTEX or other mechanisms), Iran began a calibrated strategy of incrementally exceeding JCPOA limits:

- **May 2019**: Iran announced it would stop limiting its LEU stockpile to 300 kg.
- **July 2019**: Iran exceeded the 3.67% enrichment limit, enriching to 4.5%.
- **September 2019**: Iran began operating advanced centrifuges (IR-4, IR-6) for enrichment.
- **November 2019**: Iran resumed enrichment at the Fordow facility.
- **January 2020**: Iran announced it would no longer observe any operational limits under the JCPOA, though it continued to allow IAEA inspections.

Each step was announced publicly and designed to be reversible -- a signal that Iran was willing to return to compliance if the economic benefits of the deal were restored. This "strategic patience" strategy was championed by the Rouhani government.

### The Escalation Cycle of 2019

The summer and fall of 2019 saw a dangerous escalation of military tensions:

- **May-June 2019**: Attacks on oil tankers in the Gulf of Oman. The U.S. blamed Iran; Iran denied involvement.
- **June 20, 2019**: Iran shot down a U.S. RQ-4A Global Hawk drone over the Strait of Hormuz. Trump initially approved retaliatory strikes on Iranian targets but pulled back at the last minute, reportedly 10 minutes before the strikes were to be launched, citing concern over potential casualties.
- **July 2019**: Iran seized the British-flagged oil tanker Stena Impero in the Strait of Hormuz, in apparent retaliation for Gibraltar's seizure of an Iranian tanker.
- **September 14, 2019**: Drone and cruise missile attacks on Saudi Aramco's Abqaiq and Khurais oil processing facilities -- the world's largest oil processing complex -- temporarily knocked out roughly half of Saudi Arabia's oil production (5.7 million bpd). The Houthis claimed responsibility, but the United States, Saudi Arabia, France, Germany, and the United Kingdom assessed that Iran bore responsibility. The sophistication and direction of the attack suggested it was launched from Iranian territory or Iranian-controlled territory in Iraq, not from Yemen.

Despite the severity of the Aramco attack, neither the United States nor Saudi Arabia launched a military response, leading some analysts to conclude that maximum pressure had produced maximum tension without maximum leverage.

## 7. The Soleimani Assassination (January 3, 2020)

### Who Was Qasem Soleimani?

Major General Qasem Soleimani was the commander of the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps' Quds Force from 1998 until his death in 2020. The Quds Force is the IRGC's external operations wing, responsible for intelligence operations, unconventional warfare, and the management of Iran's network of proxy forces across the Middle East.

Soleimani was often described as the second most powerful person in Iran, behind only Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei. He personally managed relationships with Hezbollah in Lebanon, Shia militias in Iraq (where he played a prominent role in the anti-ISIS campaign), the Assad regime in Syria, and the Houthi movement in Yemen. He was both a military strategist and a diplomat, shuttling between capitals and battlefields, and was revered by many Iranians as a national hero while being designated a terrorist by the United States and other Western nations.

His strategic vision centered on "forward defense" -- the doctrine that Iran should fight its enemies on foreign soil through proxy forces and allied movements rather than waiting for conflict to reach Iranian territory.

### The Strike

On January 3, 2020, a U.S. MQ-9 Reaper drone struck two vehicles near Baghdad International Airport, killing Soleimani along with Abu Mahdi al-Muhandis, the deputy commander of Iraq's Popular Mobilization Forces (PMF), and several others. Soleimani had been traveling to meet Iraqi Prime Minister Adil Abdul-Mahdi.

### Why Trump Ordered the Strike

The stated justification was that Soleimani was planning "imminent" attacks against American personnel in the region. In the preceding weeks, Iranian-backed militias had conducted a rocket attack on an Iraqi military base that killed an American contractor, and protesters had stormed the U.S. Embassy in Baghdad. However, the "imminent threat" justification was contested. A UN special rapporteur later concluded the killing was "unlawful," and many legal scholars questioned whether it met the international law standard for preemptive self-defense.

Beyond the immediate justification, the strike reflected a broader calculation: Trump wanted to demonstrate that maximum pressure had a military dimension, and that the U.S. was willing to escalate in ways that would shock Iran's leadership.

### Iran's Response

On January 8, 2020, Iran launched over a dozen ballistic missiles at Al Asad Air Base and a facility in Erbil, Iraq, which housed American military personnel. Iran gave advance warning through Iraqi intermediaries, and U.S. forces took shelter. No Americans were killed, but over 100 U.S. service members later received traumatic brain injury (TBI) diagnoses from the blasts -- injuries the Trump administration initially downplayed.

The Iranian missile strike was carefully calibrated: it was significant enough to satisfy domestic demands for retaliation without crossing the threshold that would trigger an American military response. Both sides quietly de-escalated afterward.

### Ukrainian Flight 752

In the hours after Iran's missile strikes on U.S. bases, with Iranian air defenses on maximum alert expecting an American counter-attack, a catastrophic error occurred. On January 8, 2020, Ukraine International Airlines Flight 752, a Boeing 737-800 carrying 176 people, was shot down by two Iranian Tor-M1 surface-to-air missiles minutes after takeoff from Tehran's Imam Khomeini International Airport.

Iran initially denied responsibility but admitted three days later, on January 11, that the shoot-down was the result of "human error." The missile operator's radar had not been properly recalibrated after the air defense unit was repositioned, and the operator, expecting a U.S. retaliatory strike, mistook the civilian aircraft for an incoming missile and fired without authorization from his chain of command.

All 176 people on board were killed, including 55 Canadian citizens and 30 Canadian permanent residents, as well as citizens of Iran, Ukraine, the United Kingdom, Afghanistan, Germany, and Sweden. The tragedy provoked massive grief and anger within Iran and severe damage to the government's credibility. Anti-government protests erupted, with demonstrators denouncing the regime's initial cover-up.

An Iranian military commander was later sentenced to 13 years in prison for the incident, though the families of victims and international investigators argued that accountability was incomplete and that higher-ranking officials bore responsibility for creating the conditions that led to the disaster.

## 8. The Biden Administration and the Failed Revival (2021-2022)

### The Promise of Return

President Joe Biden entered office in January 2021 pledging to return to the JCPOA if Iran returned to compliance. However, his administration made several strategic choices that ultimately doomed the effort.

### Why It Failed

**Sequencing disputes**: Biden demanded that Iran return to full compliance before the U.S. would lift sanctions, while Iran insisted the U.S. should move first since it was the party that had violated the agreement. Neither side was willing to make the first move, creating a chicken-and-egg stalemate.

**The Rouhani window closed**: The Biden administration spent its first months debating internally and positioning for negotiations rather than moving quickly. By June 2021, the relatively moderate Rouhani government was replaced by the hardline Raisi administration, which was far less interested in engagement with the West.

**Iran's nuclear advances**: By the time serious talks were underway, Iran had made irreversible advances in nuclear knowledge and capability. Most critically, Iran had begun enriching uranium to 60% purity in April 2021, after an explosion at the Natanz facility that Iran blamed on Israeli sabotage. By late 2022, Iran had accumulated significant stockpiles of 60%-enriched uranium, and in February 2023, particles enriched to 83.7% were detected by the IAEA (Iran claimed this was an unintended fluctuation).

These advances fundamentally changed the calculus: returning to JCPOA limits became technically more difficult and less meaningful, as Iran had already gained the knowledge and experience of operating advanced centrifuges and enriching to near-weapons grade.

**IAEA safeguards dispute**: Iran insisted, as a condition for any deal, that the IAEA close its investigation into traces of enriched uranium found at three undeclared sites in Iran in 2019. The IAEA and the U.S. considered this a safeguards issue that could not be negotiated away as part of a political deal.

**Congressional opposition**: 49 Republican senators wrote to Biden warning that any Iran deal not submitted to Congress for approval "will not survive." Many Democrats were also increasingly skeptical.

**Regional dynamics**: The Abraham Accords (2020) had created a new Israeli-Gulf Arab alignment partly based on shared opposition to Iran. A return to the JCPOA would have complicated these relationships.

**Iran's domestic politics**: The 2022 Mahsa Amini protests and the regime's brutal crackdown made it politically impossible for Biden to offer Iran significant concessions, as doing so would appear to reward a government that was violently suppressing its own citizens.

By November 2022, Biden privately told a supporter at a rally that the Iran nuclear deal was "dead."

### Iran's Enrichment Progress

The collapse of the JCPOA revival talks left Iran's nuclear program essentially unconstrained:
- By November 2024, Iran held 182.3 kg of 60%-enriched uranium -- far more than the ~42 kg needed (if further enriched to 90%) for a single nuclear weapon.
- Iran was operating thousands of advanced centrifuges (IR-2m, IR-4, IR-6) that were prohibited under the JCPOA.
- Breakout time had collapsed from the JCPOA-era 12+ months to "one week or less" according to IAEA assessments.
- In February 2021, Iran ceased provisionally applying the Additional Protocol, severely curtailing IAEA inspection access. Inspectors lost access to surveillance camera footage and could no longer conduct complementary access to undeclared sites.

## 9. How the Collapse Radicalized Iranian Politics

### The Vindication of Hardliners

The failure of the JCPOA delivered a devastating blow to Iranian reformists and moderates who had staked their political credibility on engagement with the West. Their core argument -- that negotiation and compromise could produce economic benefits and integration with the international community -- was proven wrong in the eyes of many Iranians.

The hardliners' counter-narrative was vindicated: the Americans could not be trusted to honor their commitments, and any concessions made to the West would be pocketed without reciprocation. This narrative had been promoted by the IRGC, Supreme Leader Khamenei's conservative allies, and the principlist faction for years, and Trump's withdrawal provided irrefutable evidence for their position.

### The Raisi Election (June 2021)

The June 2021 presidential election reflected this political shift. Ebrahim Raisi, the ultraconservative judiciary chief, won with 62% of the vote -- but crucially, voter turnout plummeted to a historic low of 48.8%, and over 4 million ballots were spoiled or left blank, suggesting widespread public disillusionment with the entire political system rather than enthusiastic support for hardliners.

Raisi's election was facilitated by the Guardian Council's mass disqualification of reformist and moderate candidates, effectively engineering a non-competitive election. But the deeper cause was the political logic that the JCPOA collapse created: why vote for reformists who promised engagement with a West that had broken its promises?

With Raisi's inauguration on August 5, 2021, hardliners had consolidated control of every center of authority in the Islamic Republic -- the presidency, parliament, judiciary, Guardian Council, IRGC, and of course the Supreme Leader's office. The elected leadership was handed back to hardliners, and reformists who had negotiated the nuclear deal and advocated greater engagement with the West were sidelined.

### Implications

The radicalization of Iranian politics following the JCPOA collapse had consequences that extended far beyond the nuclear file:
- Iran's regional proxy activities intensified, as the IRGC consolidated its dominance over foreign policy.
- The Mahsa Amini protests of 2022-2023 met with brutal suppression, reflecting hardliner control and a complete absence of reformist restraint.
- Iran's nuclear program advanced rapidly with no domestic political constituency for restraint.
- The door to future diplomatic engagement was narrowed, as the political forces that had championed diplomacy were marginalized.

The JCPOA saga thus became a case study in how the withdrawal from an imperfect-but-functional agreement can produce outcomes worse than the deal's critics claimed to fear: an Iran that was more nuclear-capable, more domestically repressive, more regionally aggressive, and more politically radicalized than it had been under the deal.

## 10. The Aftermath: From Maximum Pressure to War (2022-2026)

### Continued Nuclear Escalation

After the JCPOA revival talks collapsed, Iran's nuclear program continued advancing without meaningful constraints. The IAEA's ability to monitor Iran's activities was severely degraded after Iran stopped applying the Additional Protocol in February 2021, creating growing uncertainty about the full scope and nature of Iran's nuclear work.

### The Path to Conflict

The October 7, 2023 Hamas attack on Israel and the subsequent cascade of regional conflicts -- the Gaza war, the Israel-Hezbollah conflict, Houthi Red Sea attacks, and strikes between Israel and Iran -- created an entirely new strategic context. The nuclear file became entangled with the broader regional confrontation.

In January 2026, U.S. and European officials reportedly presented Iran with three core demands, including a permanent end to all uranium enrichment -- a maximalist position that went far beyond the JCPOA's terms. In the same month, massive protests in Iran (the largest since the Islamic Revolution) met with violent suppression, with thousands of protesters killed.

On February 28, 2026, Israel and the United States launched joint air attacks on Iran, targeting military and government sites in Tehran, Isfahan, Qom, Karaj, and Kermanshah. The strikes killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei and were characterized by their scope as aimed at both degrading Iran's military capabilities and effecting regime change.

The maximum pressure campaign, which began as an economic strategy to force a "better deal," had ultimately contributed to a chain of escalation that ended in direct military confrontation -- an outcome that the original JCPOA had been designed specifically to prevent.
