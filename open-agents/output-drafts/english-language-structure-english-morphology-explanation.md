# English Morphology: The Language That Lost Its Endings and Rebuilt From Parts

## Overview

English morphology tells a story of catastrophic loss and creative compensation. Old English was a richly inflected language -- nouns declined through four cases across three genders, adjectives agreed with their nouns in case, gender, and number, and verbs conjugated across dozens of distinct forms. The entire system comprised dozens of inflectional endings. Then two successive waves of contact -- Viking settlers in the north from the 9th century, Norman French administrators from 1066 onward -- eroded those endings until Modern English retains exactly eight inflectional morphemes. Eight. That is the smallest inflectional inventory of any major European language.

But a language does not simply lose half its grammar and carry on unchanged. English compensated. It locked down word order to mark who-did-what-to-whom (the domain of syntax). It expanded its auxiliary system to handle tense and aspect. And it built one of the most productive derivational and word-formation systems of any language, operating across a three-layer lexicon inherited from three historical sources: Germanic, French, and Latin/Greek. The result is a language with minimal inflection but enormous lexical productivity -- a language that can turn any noun into a verb by simply using it as one, that generates new words through compounding faster than dictionaries can track them, and that signals formality not through grammatical markers but through vocabulary layer.

For a pattern-oriented reader, English morphology is a rich domain. The "irregular" verbs are actually surviving members of systematic Germanic ablaut classes. The "unpredictable" stress shifts in derived words follow regular rules tied to etymological strata. The enormous vocabulary -- over 171,000 current entries in the OED's second edition, with the online edition tracking far more -- is generated by a relatively small set of productive processes operating on roots from three etymological layers. What looks like chaos is a structured system. This document maps it.

**What this document covers:**
The complete morphological architecture of Modern English: the eight surviving inflections and their allomorphs, irregular patterns and their Germanic origins, the two-class derivational system (Latinate vs. Germanic affixes), the three-layer lexicon with its register system, compounding, conversion, and the minor word-formation processes (back-formation, blending, clipping, acronyms).

**Why patterns matter here:**
English morphology is governed by principles that predict thousands of word-formation decisions. The stratal system explains why "unhappy" is natural but "inhappy" is impossible. The ablaut classes explain why "sing/sang/sung" and "ring/rang/rung" follow the same vowel pattern. The three-layer lexicon explains why "ask," "question," and "interrogate" all mean the same thing but signal completely different registers. Once the system is visible, the apparent randomness of English word-formation dissolves into structured regularity.

**Key takeaways:**
- Modern English has exactly 8 inflectional morphemes -- down from dozens in Old English
- "Irregular" verbs and plurals are not random; they are surviving Germanic patterns that follow their own systematic rules
- Derivational affixes divide into two classes: Latinate (stress-shifting, inner) and Germanic (stress-neutral, outer), with a strong ordering constraint
- The three-layer lexicon (Germanic/French/Latin) is a structural register system, not just a collection of synonyms
- Conversion (zero-derivation) is English's unique superpower, enabled by the very loss of inflection that made the language "simpler"

## Prerequisites

Before reading this explanation, it helps to understand:

- **A morpheme is the smallest unit of meaning.** The word "unhappiness" contains three morphemes: "un-" (negation) + "happy" (root) + "-ness" (forms a noun). Morphemes can be free (able to stand alone: "happy") or bound (requiring attachment: "un-", "-ness").
- **Inflection vs. derivation.** Inflection modifies a word for grammatical purposes without changing its part of speech: "walk" becomes "walks" (still a verb). Derivation creates new words, often changing the part of speech: "happy" (adjective) becomes "happiness" (noun).
- **English has layers of historical influence.** Anglo-Saxon provides the Germanic base. The Norman Conquest (1066) introduced French. The Renaissance brought Latin and Greek borrowings. These layers shape the entire morphological system.

## The Eight Surviving Inflections

Modern English has exactly eight inflectional morphemes. Two attach to nouns, four to verbs, and two to adjectives. This is the entire inflectional system -- everything else that Old English once did with endings, Modern English now accomplishes through word order, prepositions, and auxiliary verbs.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-1-eight-english-inflections -->

### Noun Inflections (2)

**Plural -s**: dog/dogs, cat/cats, box/boxes. This single suffix handles number-marking for the vast majority of English nouns.

**Possessive -'s**: dog's, cat's, box's. This marks possession or association.

### Verb Inflections (4)

**Third-person singular present -s**: walks, runs, watches. The only surviving agreement marker in the English verb system -- where Old English had distinct endings for every person and number.

**Past tense -ed**: walked, played, wanted. Marks past time.

**Past participle -ed/-en**: walked, played / broken, eaten. Used for perfect aspect ("has walked") and passive voice ("was broken").

**Progressive -ing**: walking, playing, running. Marks ongoing action.

### Adjective Inflections (2)

**Comparative -er**: taller, bigger, happier.

**Superlative -est**: tallest, biggest, happiest.

### The Pattern Within the Pattern: Allomorphic Variation

Each inflection has phonologically conditioned allomorphs -- variant pronunciations determined entirely by the final sound of the stem. These are completely predictable:

**Plural/possessive/3sg -s:**
- /-s/ after voiceless consonants: cat**s**, book**s** (voiceless ending gets voiceless allomorph)
- /-z/ after voiced consonants and vowels: dog**s**, tree**s** (voiced ending gets voiced allomorph)
- /-ɪz/ after sibilants: box**es**, bus**es** (sibilant ending gets syllabic allomorph)

**Past tense -ed:**
- /-t/ after voiceless consonants: walk**ed**, kiss**ed**
- /-d/ after voiced consonants and vowels: play**ed**, call**ed**
- /-ɪd/ after /t/ or /d/: want**ed**, need**ed**

The principle is the same in both cases: the allomorph matches the voicing of the stem, and an epenthetic vowel is inserted to avoid an unpronounceable cluster. One rule generates all variants. This is a pattern within a pattern -- the inflectional system has its own internal regularity.

### The Ordering Constraint

Inflection always sits at the outermost layer of word structure, outside all derivational morphology:

> nation → nation-**al** (derivation) → national-**ize** (derivation) → nationalize-**s** (inflection)

You never find inflection inside derivation. This ordering is exceptionless.

## Irregular Inflection: The Germanic Survivors

English "irregular" forms are not random exceptions. They are surviving members of systematic Old English patterns that were once fully regular within their own classes. What happened is that one pattern -- the dental suffix (-ed for past tense, -s for plurals) -- became overwhelmingly dominant, and forms that once followed other patterns became reclassified as "irregular" simply because they did not conform to the new default.

### Irregular Plurals

**Umlaut (i-mutation) plurals**: foot/feet, tooth/teeth, goose/geese, mouse/mice, louse/lice, man/men, woman/women. These share a single historical mechanism: an Old English plural suffix *-iz* caused the root vowel to front (move toward the front of the mouth). The suffix itself then disappeared, but the fronted vowel remained as the only plural marker. The pattern: back vowel in the singular, front vowel in the plural.

**-en plurals (n-stem)**: ox/oxen, child/children (double-marked with both -r- and -en), brethren. These are survivors of the Old English n-stem declension, which was once a major noun class.

**Zero plurals**: sheep/sheep, deer/deer, fish/fish. The singular and plural forms are identical. These reflect a minor Old English declension class where the nominative/accusative plural was unmarked.

**Foreign plurals**: criterion/criteria (Greek), phenomenon/phenomena (Greek), stimulus/stimuli (Latin), kibbutz/kibbutzim (Hebrew). These retain the plural rules of their source languages.

### Irregular Past Tenses: The Ablaut Classes

The Germanic strong verbs form their past tenses through ablaut -- systematic vowel alternation in the root. Old English had about 177 strong verbs. A landmark 2007 study published in Nature (Lieberman et al.) tracked these through history and found that 98 survive as irregular in Modern English. The rest succumbed to analogy and became regular: "help" was once help/halp/holpen; now it is help/helped/helped.

The surviving strong verbs cluster into recognizable classes:

**Class I (i/ai → o → i):** drive/drove/driven, write/wrote/written, ride/rode/ridden, rise/rose/risen

**Class III (i → a → u):** sing/sang/sung, drink/drank/drunk, swim/swam/swum, ring/rang/rung, begin/began/begun

**Class IV (ea → o → o):** bear/bore/born, tear/tore/torn, swear/swore/sworn

**Class V (various):** give/gave/given, eat/ate/eaten, see/saw/seen

**Suppletive:** go/went/gone (where "went" is historically the past tense of "wend"), be/was/been

**Weak irregulars** (vowel shortening + dental suffix): keep/kept, feel/felt, mean/meant, sleep/slept, buy/bought, think/thought, teach/taught, catch/caught

The key insight: approximately 70% of "irregular" verbs fall into predictable groups once the ablaut classes are recognized. What is taught as random memorization is actually regular within its historical category. High-frequency verbs resist regularization -- you say "sang" hundreds of times a year, so the strong form persists. Low-frequency verbs drift toward the regular pattern because speakers encounter them too rarely to maintain the memorized form.

## Derivational Morphology: The Two-Class System

English derivational affixes -- the prefixes and suffixes that create new words -- divide into two classes based on their etymological origin and phonological behavior. This division is one of the most important hidden patterns in English morphology.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-2-derivational-morphology-tree -->

### Class I (Latinate) Affixes: Stress-Shifting

These are affixes borrowed with Latin and Greek words. They trigger stress reassignment and may alter the pronunciation of the base:

**Suffixes:** -tion (NAtion → naTIONal), -ity (SAne → saNIty), -ic (Atom → aTOMic), -ous (FAme → FAmous), -al (NAtion → naTIONal), -ify (SIMple → SIMplify), -ive (ACT → ACTive), -ence/-ance (reFER → REFerence)

**Prefixes:** in-/im-/il-/ir- (possible → impossible), con-/com- (combine), pre- (precede), pro- (produce), re- (in the Latinate sense: reduce, revolve)

**Key behavior:** Class I affixes often attach to bound roots -- morphemes that cannot stand alone. The root "-ceive" in receive, conceive, perceive, and deceive never occurs as a free word. Neither does "-duce" in reduce, produce, induce, and deduce. This is characteristic of the Latinate layer: the roots arrived already bound to their affixes.

### Class II (Germanic) Affixes: Stress-Neutral

These are native English affixes inherited from Germanic. They never shift stress and never alter the base pronunciation:

**Suffixes:** -ness (HAPpy → HAPpiness), -ful (CARE → CAREful), -less (CARE → CAREless), -ly (QUICK → QUICKly), -ish (GREEN → GREENish), -er (TEACH → TEACHer), -dom (FREE → FREEdom), -ship (FRIEND → FRIENDship), -hood (NEIGHbor → NEIGHborhood)

**Prefixes:** un- (HAPpy → UNhappy), re- (in the Germanic sense: RE-do, RE-write), mis- (MIS-lead), out- (OUT-run), over- (OVER-look), under- (UNDER-estimate)

**Key behavior:** Class II affixes always attach to free-standing words. You can always identify the base independently.

### The Ordering Constraint

Class I affixes must be closer to the root than Class II affixes. The structure is always: root → Class I → Class II → inflection. You can stack Class I on Class I (nation-al-ize-ation), and then attach Class II outside (nationalization-s). But you cannot place Class II inside Class I: forms like *ness-ity or *ful-tion are systematically ill-formed.

### The Stratal Matching Tendency

There is a strong tendency for Germanic prefixes to attach to Germanic roots and Latinate prefixes to attach to Latinate roots:

- **un-happy** (Germanic prefix + Germanic root) -- natural
- **in-hospitable** (Latinate prefix + Latinate root) -- natural
- **in-happy** (Latinate prefix + Germanic root) -- impossible
- **un-hospitable** (Germanic prefix + Latinate root) -- attested historically as an archaic form, but strongly dispreferred in modern usage; "inhospitable" is the standard form

This tendency is not an absolute law. Cross-stratal combinations do exist and are accepted: "undeniable" pairs Germanic un- with Latinate deny and Latinate -able. But the stratal system operates as a strong default that governs thousands of word-formation decisions. When a combination "sounds wrong" to a native speaker, stratal mismatch is very often the reason.

## The Three-Layer Lexicon

English vocabulary is organized into three etymological layers that systematically signal register -- the level of formality appropriate to a given context. This is not trivia. It is a structural system that every English speaker operates unconsciously.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-3-three-layer-lexicon -->

### Layer 1: Germanic (Everyday)

The oldest layer. Short words, typically one or two syllables, with initial stress. Concrete, physical, emotional. This is the language of home, childhood, and daily life.

### Layer 2: French (Formal)

Entered English after the Norman Conquest of 1066, when French-speaking administrators governed an English-speaking population. Medium-length words for social, legal, administrative, and culinary domains. "Beef," "pork," "veal," and "poultry" are French -- the animals were named in English by the farmers who tended them (cow, pig, calf, chicken), while the meat was named in French by the aristocrats who ate it.

### Layer 3: Latin/Greek (Technical/Academic)

Borrowed primarily during the Renaissance and continuing through scientific vocabulary. Long words, often three or more syllables, with stress-shifting suffixes. The language of scholarship, medicine, law, and science.

### The Triplet System

The three layers produce systematic triplets -- three words for the same concept, each at a different register level:

| Germanic (everyday) | French (formal) | Latin/Greek (technical) |
|---|---|---|
| ask | question | interrogate |
| rise | mount | ascend |
| kingly | royal | regal |
| begin | commence | initiate |
| help | aid | assist |
| end | finish | conclude |
| buy | purchase | acquire |
| hide | conceal | secrete |
| sight | vision | optics |
| gut/belly | stomach | gastric |
| hearty | cordial | cardiac |
| freedom | liberty | autonomy |
| fatherly | paternal | patriarchal |
| fire | flame | conflagration |
| fear | terror | trepidation |
| inner | interior | internal |
| folk | people | populace |

### Register as a Structural System

The Latinate-Germanic ratio in a text is a reliable measure of its formality. Child-directed speech is overwhelmingly Germanic. Legal and academic prose is heavily Latinate. A skilled writer modulates formality by shifting between layers:

- "We need to **start**" (Germanic) -- casual
- "We need to **commence**" (French) -- formal
- "We need to **initiate** proceedings" (Latin) -- institutional

These carry identical propositional content but radically different register signals. English speakers select from different etymological layers to signal social context -- not just synonym choice, but a structural system embedded in the history of the vocabulary.

## Compounding: Building Blocks From Building Blocks

Compounding -- creating new words by combining two or more existing free morphemes -- is the most productive word-formation process in English. New compounds are coined constantly: "fact-check," "binge-watch," "doomscroll."

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-5-compounding-patterns -->

### Compound Types

**Noun + Noun:** blackbird, football, bookstore, earthquake, toothpaste

**Adjective + Noun:** blackboard, greenhouse, highway, software

**Verb + Noun:** pickpocket, scarecrow, killjoy, breakfast (break + fast)

**Verb + Particle:** breakdown, takeoff, checkout, login, output

### The Compound Stress Rule

Compounds receive primary stress on the first element. Adjective-noun phrases stress the second element. This single rule creates minimal pairs:

- **BLACK**bird (compound: a species) vs. black **BIRD** (phrase: any bird that is black)
- **GREEN**house (compound: plant structure) vs. green **HOUSE** (phrase: a house painted green)
- **HOT** dog (compound: food) vs. hot **DOG** (phrase: an overheated canine)

Stress alone distinguishes compound from phrase. This is a purely phonological distinction that carries full semantic weight.

### Endocentric vs. Exocentric

**Endocentric compounds** are a subtype of their head (the right-hand element in English). A blackbird IS a bird. A bookstore IS a store.

**Exocentric compounds** are not a subtype of either element. A pickpocket is not a pocket. A scarecrow is not a crow. A redhead is not a head. The meaning lies outside both constituent parts.

### Recursive Compounding

English compounds can stack without limit: student government association budget committee meeting agenda item. Each level of nesting is binary -- [[student] [government]] [association] -- and the compound stress rule applies at each level. This recursive capacity makes English compounding essentially unbounded.

## Conversion: English's Unique Flexibility

Conversion (also called zero-derivation) is the process of changing a word's part of speech without adding any affix at all. English excels at this to a degree unmatched by most other languages, and the reason traces directly back to the loss of inflections.

### Why Conversion Works in English

In a heavily inflected language, nouns and verbs have different endings that mark their category. A Latin noun cannot be used as a verb without morphological modification because the noun-endings and verb-endings are different. In English, the bare form of a word often looks identical across categories. "Walk" functions as both noun ("a walk") and verb ("to walk") with no change. This categorical ambiguity is enabled by the absence of category-marking morphology.

### Conversion Types

**Noun to Verb:** table (to table a motion), email (to email someone), Google (to Google something), butter (to butter bread), hammer (to hammer a nail), elbow (to elbow through a crowd)

**Verb to Noun:** a run, a catch, a drive, a try, a find, a build

**Adjective to Verb:** clean (to clean), dry (to dry), empty (to empty), slow (to slow down)

**Adjective to Noun:** the poor, the rich, the dead, the good (always with the definite article for the class reading)

### Conversion as a Productivity Engine

Conversion makes English exceptionally agile at generating new vocabulary. The word "Google" was coined as a proper noun (company name) in 1998. Within years it became a verb ("to Google something"), then a gerund ("Googling"), then a modifier ("the Google era"). No affixes needed. Recent conversions include: to text, to friend/unfriend, to bookmark, to Uber, to podcast, to binge-watch.

The historical irony: the loss of inflectional morphology that made English "simpler" in one dimension made it extraordinarily flexible in another. Conversion is enabled by the same process that eliminated case and gender.

## Other Word-Formation Processes

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-4-word-formation-processes -->

Beyond derivation, compounding, and conversion, English employs several additional processes for creating new words. Each has its own structural logic.

### Back-Formation

Creating a new word by removing a perceived affix from an existing word -- essentially reversing derivation:

- editor → edit (removing -or as if it were an agent suffix)
- burglar → burgle (removing -ar as if it were an agent suffix)
- television → televise
- enthusiasm → enthuse
- baby-sitter → baby-sit

Back-formation treats an existing word as if it were derived, then strips the supposed affix to create the "root" -- even though that root never existed before.

### Blending

Fusing parts of two words into one: breakfast + lunch → brunch, smoke + fog → smog, motor + hotel → motel, information + entertainment → infotainment. Blends typically preserve the beginning of one word and the end of another.

### Clipping

Shortening an existing word: telephone → phone, omnibus → bus, examination → exam, advertisement → ad, influenza → flu, laboratory → lab, facsimile → fax.

### Acronyms and Initialisms

Forming words from initial letters: NATO, NASA, SCUBA, LASER. Some become so naturalized that they are no longer written in capitals and function as ordinary words: laser, scuba. Some become productive bases for further derivation: to RSVP, an FAQ.

### Eponymy

Words from proper names: sandwich (from the Earl of Sandwich), boycott (from Captain Boycott), algorithm (from al-Khwarizmi). Once the proper name becomes a common word, it enters the regular morphological system and can be inflected and derived normally: sandwiches, boycotted, algorithmic.

## The Big Pattern: Loss, Compensation, and Productivity

The overarching pattern of English morphology is a three-part historical trajectory:

**1. Loss.** Contact with Old Norse during the Viking period and with Norman French after 1066 eroded the inflectional system. Case endings became indistinct, gender agreement collapsed, verb conjugations simplified. Modern English retains only eight inflectional morphemes from a system that once comprised dozens.

**2. Compensation.** The grammatical work previously done by inflections shifted to other parts of the system. Word order became rigid (syntax replaced case). Prepositions multiplied (to replace case functions). The auxiliary verb system expanded (to replace verb conjugation). The determiner system grew more important (articles partly replaced the definiteness functions of inflection).

**3. Productivity.** With minimal inflectional overhead, English developed extraordinary word-formation productivity. Conversion became trivially easy. Compounding became essentially unbounded. The three-layer lexicon, built from successive waves of borrowing, provided an enormous root inventory for derivational morphology to operate on. English compensated for morphological simplification in one area by developing morphological creativity in another.

This three-part pattern -- simplify inflection, strengthen syntax, boost derivation -- defines the typological trajectory of English and explains its unique structural profile among European languages.

## Common Misconceptions

### Misconception: "English has no morphology because it is analytic"

**Reality:** English has reduced inflectional morphology but highly productive derivational morphology. It has over 60 common derivational affixes, enormously productive compounding, and unique flexibility through conversion. The total morphological system is rich -- just differently organized than in synthetic languages like Latin or Russian.

**Why people think this:** Introductory linguistics courses often contrast English with highly inflected languages, which can give the impression that English morphology is impoverished. It is impoverished in inflection; it is prolific in derivation and word-formation.

### Misconception: "Irregular verbs and plurals are random exceptions"

**Reality:** Nearly all irregular forms are surviving Germanic patterns that follow their own systematic rules. Sing/sang/sung, ring/rang/rung, and drink/drank/drunk are all Class III ablaut verbs following the i-a-u pattern. Foot/feet and tooth/teeth both reflect the same umlaut process. The "irregularity" is only relative to the later regular pattern.

**Why people think this:** The irregular forms are taught as memorization lists without historical context. Once the ablaut classes and declension types are identified, the patterns become visible.

### Misconception: "Germanic and Latinate words are interchangeable synonyms"

**Reality:** While they may share core denotative meaning, they carry different register signals, collocational preferences, and sometimes subtle semantic distinctions. "Begin" (Germanic) and "commence" (French) overlap in meaning but differ in formality, typical context, and the words they naturally combine with.

**Why people think this:** Thesaurus entries group them together without flagging the register difference. The difference is structural (which etymological layer) rather than purely semantic.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

### Cross-Stratal Attachments

The stratal matching tendency is not absolute. Forms like "undeniable" (Germanic un- + Latinate deny + Latinate -able) and "un-American" (Germanic un- + proper name) are fully accepted. The system is a strong default, not an exceptionless rule.

### Doubly-Marked Forms

"Children" carries two historical plural markers: the suffix -r- (from the Old English -ru plural) plus -en (from the n-stem pattern). "Brethren" similarly combines umlaut with -en. These are fossils of a time when multiple declension patterns coexisted.

### Competing Past Tenses

"Hanged" vs. "hung" -- the same verb with two past tenses carrying different meanings (executed vs. suspended). "Dove" vs. "dived" -- dialectal variation in regular vs. strong past. "Sneaked" vs. "snuck" -- a new irregular form that emerged by analogy, moving in the opposite direction from the usual regularization trend.

### Cranberry Morphemes

Bound roots that appear only with certain affixes and carry no independent meaning: "cran-" in "cranberry," "twi-" in "twilight," "-ceive" in "receive/conceive/perceive." These were historically meaningful but are synchronically opaque -- recognizable as morphemes only because they recur across multiple words.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Inflectional morpheme** | An affix that marks grammatical relationships (number, tense, comparison) without changing a word's part of speech |
| **Derivational morpheme** | An affix that creates new words, often changing the part of speech |
| **Free morpheme** | A morpheme that can stand alone as a word: "happy," "dog," "walk" |
| **Bound morpheme** | A morpheme that cannot stand alone: "un-," "-ness," "-ceive" |
| **Allomorph** | A phonologically conditioned variant of a morpheme: the plural -s is pronounced /-s/, /-z/, or /-ɪz/ depending on environment |
| **Class I affix** | A Latinate affix that shifts stress and can attach to bound roots; sits closer to the root |
| **Class II affix** | A Germanic affix that does not shift stress and attaches to free words; sits farther from the root |
| **Ablaut** | Systematic vowel alternation in strong verb forms: sing/sang/sung |
| **Umlaut (i-mutation)** | Historical fronting of a root vowel caused by a following high front vowel or glide: foot/feet |
| **Conversion** | Changing a word's category without any morphological change: "to table" from "a table" |
| **Zero-derivation** | Another term for conversion |
| **Back-formation** | Creating a word by removing a perceived affix: "edit" from "editor" |
| **Compound** | A word formed by combining two or more free morphemes: "blackbird," "football" |
| **Endocentric compound** | A compound that is a subtype of its head element: a blackbird IS a bird |
| **Exocentric compound** | A compound that is not a subtype of either element: a pickpocket is not a pocket |
| **Stratal phonology** | The theory that morphological rules apply in ordered layers (strata), with Latinate rules applying before Germanic ones |
| **Productive morphology** | Affixes or processes actively used to create new words in the current language |
| **Cranberry morpheme** | A bound root with no independent meaning that recurs across multiple words: "-ceive" in receive/conceive/perceive |
| **Suppletive form** | An irregular form drawn from a completely different root: "went" as the past tense of "go" (historically from "wend") |

## Summary of Patterns

The patterns of English morphology form a single coherent system:

1. **Eight inflections survive** from a system that once had dozens -- minimal inflection with maximal allomorphic regularity
2. **"Irregular" forms fall into systematic classes** -- ablaut groups, umlaut plurals, n-stem plurals, each regular within its historical category
3. **Derivational affixes divide into two ordered strata** -- Latinate (stress-shifting, inner) and Germanic (stress-neutral, outer)
4. **The three-layer lexicon maps etymology to register** -- Germanic for everyday, French for formal, Latin/Greek for technical
5. **Compounding is recursive and stress-marked** -- first-element stress distinguishes compounds from phrases
6. **Conversion exploits the lack of inflection** -- words shift category by simply appearing in new syntactic positions
7. **High frequency preserves irregularity** -- the most-used verbs resist regularization; rare ones succumb to analogy

Each pattern connects to the others. The loss of inflection enabled conversion. The stratal system links derivation to phonology. The three-layer lexicon connects vocabulary to historical contact. The compound stress rule links morphology to prosody. English morphology is not a list of facts but an integrated network of interacting systems, all shaped by the same historical trajectory: loss, compensation, productivity.
