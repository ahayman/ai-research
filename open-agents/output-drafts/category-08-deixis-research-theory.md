## 1. What Deixis IS

### 1.1 Definition

**Deixis** is the use of words and expressions whose meaning depends entirely on the context of the utterance -- specifically, who is speaking, where they are, and when they are speaking. A deictic expression is essentially a linguistic "pointing device": it points to some element of the context rather than having a fixed, stable meaning on its own.

The word "I" does not mean "Aaron" or "Sarah" -- it means *whoever is currently speaking*. The word "here" does not mean "the kitchen" or "London" -- it means *wherever the speaker currently is*. The word "now" does not mean "3pm on Tuesday" -- it means *the moment of the utterance*. These words are empty containers that get filled with meaning by the context in which they are used.

This is what makes deixis fundamentally **pragmatic** rather than purely semantic. The same word -- spelled and pronounced identically -- means different things depending on who says it, where, and when. The linguistic form stays constant; the referent shifts with every new context.

### 1.2 Etymology

The term comes from Ancient Greek: **deixis** (Greek: *deixis*, meaning "display, demonstration, or reference"), derived from *deiknynai* ("to show, to point out"). The related adjective **deictic** comes from Greek *deiktikos*, meaning "able to show," from *deiktos* ("shown"), a verbal adjective from *deiknynai*. The deeper Proto-Indo-European root is **\*deik-** ("to show"), which is also the ancestor of English words like "digit," "dictate," "indicate," and "index."

The philosopher Chrysippus (c. 279-206 BCE) added the specialized meaning "point of reference," which is the sense in which the term is used in contemporary linguistics. The related philosophical term **indexicality** comes from the corresponding Latin root.

### 1.3 Foundational Scholarly Works

#### Karl Buhler (1934): The Origo and the Deictic Field

The Austrian psychologist and linguist **Karl Buhler** was one of the earliest scholars to present a systematic theory of deixis in his masterwork *Sprachtheorie: Die Darstellungsfunktion der Sprache* (Theory of Language: The Representational Function of Language, 1934).

Buhler introduced the concept of the **origo** -- the zero-point or origin from which all deictic expressions are anchored. The origo is the **"I-here-now"** of the speaker: the first person at the current place and time of utterance. This is the coordinate center from which deictic expressions radiate outward. Every deictic expression is interpreted relative to this zero-point.

Buhler proposed the concept of the **Zeigfeld** (deictic field), which operates in three modes:

1. **Demonstratio ad oculos** ("pointing to what is before the eyes"): Operates in the here-and-now of the speaker's sensible environment. This is the prototypical use of deixis -- physically pointing at something present in the immediate context.

2. **Anaphora**: Operates within the context of the discourse itself, treated as a structured environment. Here, deictic-like expressions point not to the physical world but to other parts of the text or conversation.

3. **Deixis at phantasma** ("pointing in imagination"): Operates in the context of imagination and long-term memory. The speaker mentally transports to another time and place and uses deictic expressions anchored to that imagined setting. As Buhler described it, the "body-feeling representation" (*Korpertastbild*, or body schema) "becomes loosened from its involvement with the HERE/NOW/I deictic coordinates of waking life in our immediate environment, and becomes available to translation into an environment we construct both conceptually and orientationally."

Buhler also distinguished two fundamental types of linguistic expressions: **deictics** (or "pointing words") and **symbols** (or "naming words"), arguing that they are functionally and historically independent of each other. The deictic gesture -- pointing to something and checking whether the other's gaze follows -- he described as the very beginning of communication and language.

#### Charles Fillmore (1971/1975/1997): The Santa Cruz Lectures on Deixis

In the summer of 1971, Professor **Charles J. Fillmore** delivered a set of six lectures at the University of California, Santa Cruz, on the topic of Deixis. These lectures became foundational classics of linguistic writing, described as "clear, fascinating, full of familiar facts put together in surprising ways."

The lectures were distributed for many years as samizdat by the Indiana University Linguistics Club before being formally published in 1975, and later republished in 1997 as *Lectures on Deixis*. They were a major stimulus to the then-nascent field of linguistic pragmatics -- the study of the interaction between linguistic form and the context of utterance.

Fillmore's key contributions include:

- Identifying the **three main types** of deictic words: **personal, spatial, and temporal**
- Analyzing the deictic verbs **"come" and "go"** in terms of their deictically anchored conditions (motion toward vs. away from the speaker)
- Distinguishing between **gestural** and **symbolic** uses of deictic expressions
- Establishing the framework that later scholars built upon to add discourse deixis and social deixis as additional categories

#### Stephen Levinson (1983): Pragmatics

**Stephen C. Levinson's** textbook *Pragmatics* (Cambridge University Press, 1983) became the standard reference treatment of deixis in linguistics. Levinson's chapter on deixis provides a lucid and integrative analysis that organizes the phenomenon into clear categories.

Key elements of Levinson's treatment:

- Deixis belongs within the domain of **pragmatics** because it directly concerns the relationship between the structure of language and the context in which it is used
- Deictic expressions hold both a **fixed semantic meaning** (a general rule for interpretation) and a **denotational meaning that varies** depending on time and/or place
- Nearly all deictics are "heavily dependent on pragmatic resolution" -- for example, "Come here" may mean "come to this sofa" or "come to this city" depending on context
- He organized the deictic field around Buhler's origo concept: the speaker at the time and place of speaking

Levinson also provided the framework for **social deixis**, distinguishing between:
- **Relational social deixis**: Encoding the relationship between speaker and addressee/referent (e.g., honorifics, formal vs. informal forms)
- **Absolute social deixis**: Expressions that are not dependent on comparing relative social positions (e.g., certain authorized titles or greetings)

### 1.4 Why Deixis Is Fundamentally Pragmatic

Deixis is the strongest example of why pragmatics exists as a discipline separate from semantics. Consider the sentence:

> "I'll meet you there tomorrow."

Every content word in this sentence except "meet" is deictic:
- **"I"** -- means whoever is speaking (changes with every speaker)
- **"you"** -- means whoever is being addressed (changes with every addressee)
- **"there"** -- means some location understood from context (changes with every conversation)
- **"tomorrow"** -- means the day after this utterance (changes with every day)

The sentence has a fixed syntactic structure and a fixed semantic *template*, but its actual meaning -- who will meet whom, where, and when -- is entirely determined by the context of utterance. You cannot determine the truth or falsity of this sentence without knowing who said it, to whom, where, and when.

As Levinson put it, if semantics includes all conventional aspects of meaning, then deictic phenomena might be considered semantic -- but because their interpretation requires access to the speech situation (who, where, when), they properly belong to pragmatics.

## 2. The Five Major Types of Deixis

### 2.1 Person Deixis

#### Definition

Person deixis refers to the grammatical encoding of the roles of participants in the speech event. It concerns the use of expressions that refer to the speaker (first person), the addressee (second person), and others who are neither speaker nor addressee (third person).

#### The Core System

| Person | Singular | Plural | Function |
|--------|----------|--------|----------|
| First person | I, me, my, mine, myself | we, us, our, ours, ourselves | Refers to the speaker |
| Second person | you, your, yours, yourself | you, your, yours, yourselves | Refers to the addressee |
| Third person | he/she/it/they, him/her/them | they, them, their, theirs | Refers to others |

#### The Shifting Nature

The most distinctive feature of person deixis is that the referent shifts every time a different person takes the speaking turn. The word "I" is not a proper name -- it does not permanently designate any individual. When Aaron says "I," it means Aaron. When Sarah says "I," it means Sarah. The form stays constant; the referent rotates with every change of speaker.

This is why young children and autistic individuals sometimes struggle with personal pronouns. Research has documented that children with autism sometimes use "you" to refer to themselves and "I" to refer to the person they are addressing -- a pattern known as **pronoun reversal**. This is not a semantic error (misunderstanding what words mean) but a pragmatic-deictic error (difficulty tracking who occupies the "speaker" role at any given moment). As one study described it, "the substantial processing demand of updating the anchoring site of an utterance, and shifting the relationship between an utterance-generating speaker and a referred-to listener -- a process called 'deictic shifting' -- challenges children and triggers pronoun reversals in early development."

Neural imaging research has shown that deictic shifting "slows the response time of autism participants more than control participants," suggesting that the cognitive work of tracking shifting deictic centers imposes a particular processing burden.

#### Inclusive vs. Exclusive "We"

The first-person plural "we" harbors a major pragmatic ambiguity. Many languages (Tamil, Fijian, Tagalog, Mandarin Chinese, and many others) grammatically distinguish between:

- **Inclusive "we"** (+Speaker, +Addressee): "We" = you and me (and possibly others). Example: "We should get going" (said to someone you're leaving with)
- **Exclusive "we"** (+Speaker, -Addressee): "We" = me and my group, but NOT you. Example: "We won the championship" (said to someone who was not on the team)

English does NOT grammatically distinguish these -- the single word "we" covers both meanings. Listeners must use context to determine which meaning is intended. This is a significant source of potential ambiguity.

#### Strategic and Non-Literal Uses of "We"

English speakers deploy "we" in several pragmatically specialized ways that depart from its literal meaning:

**The Royal "We" (Pluralis Majestatis):**
Historically used by monarchs, popes, and other authority figures to speak of themselves in the plural. The origin has been traced to 1169, when English king Henry II used it to mean "God and I." Example: Queen Victoria's alleged "We are not amused." The form carries connotations of authority and grandeur.

**The Editorial "We":**
Used by opinion columnists, academic writers, and media commentators who express opinions as representatives of their organizations or as the voice of assumed collective wisdom. Example: "We believe this policy is misguided" (meaning: the newspaper's editorial board believes this). Scientific and mathematical writers also use "we" to refer to a universal truth: "We can see from the equation that..."

**The Patronizing/Nurse's "We":**
Used when "we" actually means "you." Example: "How are we feeling today?" (said by a nurse to a patient -- the nurse is not asking about her own feelings). This usage telegraphs "I am with you; we are in this together," but it can be perceived as condescending because it infantilizes the addressee by refusing to address them directly. The choice between "How are you feeling?" and "How are we feeling?" is not semantically different but is pragmatically loaded.

**The Normative/Corrective "We":**
"We don't do that here" -- ostensibly a statement about group behavior, but pragmatically functions as a directive aimed at "you." It invokes group norms and social standards while indirectly addressing the listener's behavior, presenting a corrective as an established collective norm rather than a personal command. This is softer than "You shouldn't do that" but carries the same illocutionary force.

### 2.2 Spatial Deixis (Place Deixis)

#### Definition

Spatial deixis concerns the encoding of spatial locations relative to the location of the participants in the speech event. It is manifested principally through:
- **Location adverbs**: here, there, nearby, far away
- **Demonstratives**: this/that, these/those
- **Motion verbs**: come/go, bring/take

#### The Proximal-Distal System

English operates with a basic **two-way** spatial deictic system:

| Feature | Proximal (Near Speaker) | Distal (Far from Speaker) |
|---------|------------------------|--------------------------|
| Demonstrative determiners | this, these | that, those |
| Location adverbs | here | there |
| Motion verbs (toward/away) | come, bring | go, take |

**Proximal** forms indicate closeness to the speaker. **Distal** forms indicate distance from the speaker. Older English had a three-way system: this (near me) / that (near you) / **yon/yonder** (far from both of us), but the medial and distal terms collapsed into "that" in Modern English.

Cross-linguistically, spatial deictic systems vary considerably:
- **Spanish** has a three-way system: *este* (near speaker), *ese* (near addressee), *aquel* (far from both)
- **Malagasy** has seven degrees of distance combined with two degrees of visibility
- **Many Inuit languages** have even more complex systems

#### Demonstratives as Pointing Devices

The demonstratives **this/that** and **these/those** are among the most common spatial deictic expressions. Their choice depends on the speaker's perception of relative distance:

- "**This** book is interesting" = the book near me
- "**That** book is interesting" = the book over there

But distance is not purely physical. Research has shown that "speakers' choice between proximal and distal demonstratives is contingent on language users' conceptualization of the speech situation rather than on physical properties of space." Factors beyond physical distance include:
- The speaker's sphere of personal involvement (psychological closeness)
- Emotional proximity or engagement
- Whether the referent is in the speaker's sphere of attention or control

#### The Come/Go Asymmetry

The motion verb pair **come/go** is one of the most studied deictic phenomena, extensively analyzed by Fillmore in his Santa Cruz Lectures.

The basic rule:
- **"Come"** = motion toward the speaker (or toward the speaker's location at the time being referred to)
- **"Go"** = motion away from the speaker

But the system is more complex than this simple rule suggests. Fillmore analyzed the appropriateness conditions of "come" and "go" in terms of person, place, and time:

- **"I'll come to your party"** -- motion toward the addressee's location (the speaker adopts the addressee's perspective as the deictic center)
- **"I'll go to the store"** -- motion to a place where neither speaker nor addressee is located
- **"Are you coming to the party?"** -- the party is conceptualized as the destination where the speaker will be

A more refined analysis proposes that **"go"** requires that no member of a contextually provided reference point (RP) be at the goal of the described motion, while **"come"** requires that some member thereof be at the goal. This explains why you can say "I'm coming!" (toward you) but not ordinarily "I'm going!" when moving toward someone you're talking to.

The same deictic logic applies to:
- **bring** (carry toward the deictic center) vs. **take** (carry away from it)
- **this way** (toward here) vs. **that way** (toward there)

#### Extension to Abstract and Discourse Space

Spatial deictic terms extend metaphorically from physical space to abstract and conceptual domains. This extension follows a general cognitive pattern: the human brain extends fundamental spatial cognition by abstraction to structure other areas of experience.

- **Emotional proximity**: "a **close** friend" / "a **distant** acquaintance" (spatial metaphors for social-emotional relationships)
- **Intellectual engagement**: "**This** is a fascinating idea" (proximal = engaged) vs. "**That's** not what I meant" (distal = distanced)
- **Abstract containers**: "**deep** trouble," "**empty** words," "**in** a good mood" (spatial metaphors for abstract states)
- **Discourse organization**: "**Here**, Chomsky argues that..." (the text itself is treated as a spatial domain through which the reader travels)

Research has shown that "positive or negative emotions toward somebody can be related to spatial closeness or distance" and that "the structuring of space serves as a basis for the structuring of other areas of cognition and spoken language expression, such as temporality, abstract notions, and relationships."

### 2.3 Temporal Deixis (Time Deixis)

#### Definition

Temporal deixis concerns the encoding of time points and spans relative to the time at which an utterance is made. The "now" of the speaker is the temporal origo -- the zero-point from which all temporal deictic expressions are calculated.

#### Temporal Deictic Expressions

| Type | Examples |
|------|----------|
| Pure deictic adverbs | now, then, soon, recently, lately, immediately |
| Day-relative terms | today, tomorrow, yesterday |
| Calendar-relative terms | this morning, this week, this year, last month, next Tuesday |
| Durational/distance terms | ago, hence, from now, in [time period] |
| Sequential terms | before, after, previously, subsequently |

#### The Shifting Nature

The key deictic property: every temporal expression is anchored to the moment of utterance, and that moment changes with every new utterance.

- If I say "tomorrow" on Monday, I mean Tuesday
- If I say "tomorrow" on Friday, I mean Saturday
- "Yesterday" means a different day every day
- "Now" means a different moment every moment
- "This morning" on Monday afternoon refers to Monday morning; the same phrase on Tuesday morning refers to Tuesday morning

This is what distinguishes deictic temporal expressions from **absolute** temporal expressions like "on January 15, 2026" or "at 3:00 PM" -- absolute expressions have fixed referents regardless of when they are uttered.

#### Calendar vs. Non-Calendar Temporal Expressions

Temporal deictic expressions can be categorized by the type of time-reckoning system they invoke:

- **Fundamental calendar units**: day, week, month, year (e.g., "this year," "last week," "next month")
- **Day segments**: morning, afternoon, evening, night (e.g., "this morning," "last night")
- **Named days of the week**: Monday, Tuesday, etc. (e.g., "this Tuesday," "last Friday")
- **Non-calendar expressions**: now, then, soon, recently, ago, before, after (these do not invoke calendar units but express relative temporal distance)

The interaction of "this" with time units creates interpretive complexity:
- **"This morning"** (said in the afternoon) = the morning of today (already past)
- **"This morning"** (said in the morning) = right now, the current morning
- **"This week"** = the current week (but does it include what has already passed, or only what remains?)
- **"This Tuesday"** = could mean the upcoming Tuesday OR the Tuesday just past, depending on the day of utterance and pragmatic context

#### Tense as Temporal Deixis

**Grammatical tense** is itself a form of temporal deixis. Tense locates an event in time relative to the moment of speaking:

- **Past tense**: "She **walked** to the store" -- the event occurred before the moment of utterance
- **Present tense**: "She **walks** to the store" -- the event coincides with (or is habitual around) the moment of utterance
- **Future tense**: "She **will walk** to the store" -- the event will occur after the moment of utterance

Hans Reichenbach (1947) formalized this with three temporal reference points:
- **S** = the point of speech (the moment of utterance)
- **E** = the time of the event being described
- **R** = a reference point used to locate the event

In this framework:
- Simple past tense is **absolute** (deictic): it directly locates E before S. "He went." (E before S)
- The pluperfect is **relative**: it locates E before some other reference time R, which is itself located before S. "When I got home, he **had gone**." (E before R before S)

Bernard Comrie (1985) refined this framework by allowing for multiple reference points and using ordering relations (simultaneous, before, after) rather than point configurations. This allows for more nuanced description of complex tense systems.

### 2.4 Social Deixis

#### Definition

Social deixis concerns the encoding of social information within linguistic expressions -- specifically, information about the social identities of participants and the social relationships between them. Social deictic expressions "point to" a social relationship in the same way that spatial deictic expressions "point to" a location.

#### Core Mechanisms

Social deixis manifests through:

1. **Pronoun systems (T/V distinctions)**: Many languages have formal vs. informal second-person pronouns:
   - French: *tu* (informal) / *vous* (formal)
   - German: *du* (informal) / *Sie* (formal)
   - Spanish: *tu* (informal) / *usted* (formal)

2. **Address terms**: Titles, honorifics, names, and nicknames that encode the social relationship:
   - "Professor Williams" (formal, respectful, professional distance)
   - "Dr. Smith" (professional respect)
   - "Mr. Johnson" (formal, default respectful distance)
   - "John" (first name -- familiar, equal status, or deliberately informal)
   - "Johnny" (nickname -- intimate, affectionate, or diminutive)
   - "mate" / "buddy" / "dude" (casual solidarity markers)

3. **Honorific systems**: Grammatical or morphosyntactic forms that encode relative social status, involving alternate forms such as affixes, clitics, grammatical case changes, changes in person or number, or entirely different lexical items.

4. **Register markers**: Vocabulary and grammatical choices that index the formality level of the interaction.

#### Why Address Forms Are Deictic

The choice of how to address someone is deictic because it **points to** the social relationship between the speaker and the addressee at the time of utterance, and that relationship can change. The same person might be addressed as:

- "Dr. Martinez" (by a patient in a clinical setting)
- "Professor Martinez" (by a student in a classroom)
- "Elena" (by a colleague at a department dinner)
- "Mom" (by her child)
- "Honey" (by her spouse)

The referent is the same person; the address form shifts to point at a different social relationship, in a different context.

#### The Loss of T/V in English: A Historical Case

English once had a T/V distinction:
- **Thou/thee/thy** (T-form): singular, informal, intimate
- **You/ye/your** (V-form): plural, and later formal/respectful

The distinction became socially loaded in the Middle English period (influenced by French *tu/vous*). By the 13th century, "ye/you" was used to address a superior or stranger, while "thou/thee" was reserved for familiars, intimates, and social inferiors.

The asymmetry became pragmatically charged:
- Addressing someone with "thou" could signal **intimacy** (between close friends or lovers) or **contempt** (addressing a social inferior or deliberately insulting an equal)
- At the 1603 trial of Sir Walter Raleigh, the prosecutor declared: **"I thou thee, thou traitor!"** -- using "thou" as a deliberate insult, marking Raleigh as beneath the speaker's social level

By the first half of the 17th century, "thou" disappeared from Standard English (surviving in some regional dialects, Quaker usage, and liturgical language). Modern English retained only the formal form "you" for all contexts -- losing the ability to grammatically encode the T/V social distinction.

However, English compensated by developing other social deictic strategies:
- Rich systems of address forms (Dr./Mr./Mrs./Ms./first name/nickname/terms of endearment)
- Register variation (formal vs. casual vocabulary and grammar)
- Modern dialectal innovations: "y'all," "you guys," "youse," "yinz" -- attempts to restore a plural/singular distinction lost when "you" took over all functions

#### Levinson's Framework for Social Deixis

Levinson (1983) distinguished between:

- **Relational social deixis**: Encoding the relationship between participants through four types of honorifics:
  - *Referent honorifics*: Encode the relationship between speaker and person being spoken about
  - *Addressee honorifics*: Encode the relationship between speaker and person being spoken to
  - *Bystander honorifics*: Encode awareness of who else is present (overhearers)
  - *Setting honorifics*: Encode the formality level of the situation itself

- **Absolute social deixis**: Expressions whose use is not dependent on comparing relative social positions but instead reflects authorized roles:
  - *Authorized speaker*: Individuals with authority to use specific language or titles (e.g., a judge saying "I sentence you")
  - *Authorized recipient*: Those who hold recognized status as intended audience (e.g., "Your Majesty" can only be addressed to a reigning monarch)

### 2.5 Discourse Deixis (Text Deixis)

#### Definition

Discourse deixis refers to the use of expressions within an utterance to refer to parts of the ongoing discourse itself -- including the utterance itself. Where person deixis points to participants, spatial deixis points to locations, and temporal deixis points to times, discourse deixis points to **portions of the discourse**.

#### Core Expressions

| Expression Type | Examples | Function |
|----------------|----------|----------|
| Demonstratives as discourse pointers | "this," "that" | Point to propositions or segments of discourse |
| Cataphoric markers | "the following," "as follows," "consider this" | Point forward to upcoming discourse |
| Anaphoric markers | "the above," "as I said," "as mentioned earlier," "the preceding" | Point backward to prior discourse |
| Locative metaphors | "here," "at this point," "in this section" | Treat the discourse as a spatial domain |
| Meta-discourse markers | "so," "anyway," "in other words," "to summarize" | Organize and navigate the flow of discourse |

#### How "This" and "That" Work in Discourse

In discourse deixis, "this" and "that" do not point to physical objects but to **propositions, ideas, or segments of the conversation**:

- "**That's** a good point" -- "that" refers to the proposition just expressed by the other speaker
- "**This** is what I want to say..." -- "this" refers to the upcoming proposition
- "I disagree with **that**" -- "that" refers to a prior claim or argument
- "Consider **this**: not all evidence supports the claim" -- "this" points forward to the proposition that follows

Research has found patterns in written discourse:
- **"This"** tends to refer to **upcoming** portions of the discourse or to maintain current topic focus
- **"That"** tends to refer to **prior** portions of the discourse or to distance from a topic
- Both "this" and "that" access adjacent discourse more easily than distant discourse, but "this" accesses distant discourse more frequently and easily than "that"

#### Discourse Deixis vs. Anaphora

An important distinction (though often blurred):
- **Anaphora**: An expression refers back to a previously introduced **entity**. "Susan dropped the plate. **It** shattered loudly." ("It" = the plate, an entity)
- **Discourse deixis**: An expression refers to a **proposition, fact, or segment** of discourse. "Susan dropped the plate. **That** was unfortunate." ("That" = the event of Susan dropping the plate, not the plate itself)

An expression can sometimes function both deictically and anaphorically simultaneously: "I was born in London, and I have lived **here/there** all my life." The choice between "here" and "there" is deictic (indicating whether the speaker is currently in London), while the reference to London is anaphoric (pointing back to the previously mentioned entity).

#### The Role of Discourse Deixis in Coherence

Discourse deixis serves critical organizational functions:
- **Tracking topics**: "**This** issue requires further discussion" (pointing to a topic currently under discussion)
- **Signaling transitions**: "Having discussed X, let's turn to **this** question..."
- **Creating cohesion**: "**As mentioned above**, the data suggests..."
- **Metacommentary**: "**This** is getting complicated" (commenting on the discourse itself)

In internet and digital communication, discourse deixis has developed new forms, including iconic language forms resembling arrows that point to portions of text, and practices like quoting previous messages to establish discourse reference.

## 3. The Deictic Center (Origo)

### 3.1 The Default Center

The **deictic center** -- Buhler's **origo** -- is the zero-point from which all deictic expressions are anchored and interpreted. The default deictic center consists of:

- **The speaker** (the "I" reference point)
- **At the time of the utterance** (the "now" reference point)
- **At the location of the utterance** (the "here" reference point)

The deictic center is thus "a set of theoretical points that a deictic expression is 'anchored' to" for meaning evaluation. The theory is fundamentally **egocentric**: the indexical anchorage of deictic expressions is a function of this zero-point of subjectivity. The "I"-center serves as the perceptual vantage point that surveys relations among salient contextual entities and events.

The **deictic field** "radiates out from the deictic center" and provides "cognitive frames that participants in a discourse can use to conceptualize their contextual surroundings." It contains the range of bounded participants and objects, spatial locations and landmarks, and temporal frames that point back to the deictic center.

### 3.2 How the Deictic Center Can Shift

The deictic center is not permanently fixed at the speaker. It can be **transferred** or **projected** to other reference points:

#### Shifts in Narrative

When telling a story, speakers often shift the deictic center from their actual here-and-now into the spatiotemporal coordinates of the story world:

> "I was walking down the street and suddenly **this** guy comes up to me..."

Here, the speaker shifts from past tense ("was walking") into the **narrative present** ("comes up"), and uses the proximal demonstrative "this" (rather than "that") -- all indicating that the deictic center has shifted from the current conversation into the world of the story. The speaker is "re-living" the experience, and the listener is invited to occupy the story world alongside them.

**Deictic Shift Theory (DST)** describes this as "immersion processes by which readers [or listeners] imaginatively project hypothetical deictic centers anchored to communicative and experiential loci within a narrative." William Labov's analysis of narrative structure identifies an "orientation" section that explicitly "shifts the deictic center away from the speaker's here-and-now into the spatiotemporal coordinates of the story."

#### Shifts in Empathy and Perspective-Taking

Speakers can shift the deictic center to the addressee's perspective:

> "How are things over **there**?" (said on the phone -- "there" = the listener's location, not the speaker's)

Compare this to saying "How are things **here**?" which would mean the speaker's own location. By using "there," the speaker projects the deictic center toward the listener's location.

Conversely, "come" can shift the deictic center to the addressee:
> "I'll **come** to your office" (the goal is the addressee's location -- the speaker adopts the addressee's position as the deictic anchor)

#### Shifts in Writing

Written text creates its own deictic center:
> "The reader will find **here** a summary of the argument..."
> "In the **following** chapter, we will examine..."
> "**This** paragraph introduces the key concepts..."

The deictic center shifts to the text itself -- "here" means "at this point in the text," not "at the author's physical location."

#### Deictic Projection in Telephone and Mediated Communication

When two people are in different locations:
- Person A in London, Person B in New York
- A can say: "We are **coming** next week" (deictic center = London, the shared destination)
- B can say: "We are **leaving** next week" (deictic center = New York, the point of departure)
- But B could also say: "We are **coming** next week" (shifting the deictic center to London, the destination where A is)

The ability to adopt someone else's spatial perspective as the deictic center for motion verbs is a key example of **deictic projection**.

### 3.3 Story-Internal Shifts

In narratives, the deictic center can shift between characters:

- **Narrator-to-character shifts**: "She looked out the window. **Here** was the garden she had loved as a child." (The deictic center shifts from the narrator's perspective to the character's -- "here" is anchored to the character's position, not the narrator's or reader's.)

- **Character-to-character shifts**: In omniscient narration, the focal point can move from one character to another. "John watched the door nervously. Mary, from across the room, could see **him** fidgeting." The reader must "adjust the deictic center accordingly and interpret the lens of the current focal window through the experiential subjectivity of the character-locus."

- **Embedded narrative shifts**: When characters tell stories within stories, letters, or diary entries are introduced, additional deictic layers are created, each with their own origo.

## 4. Gestural vs. Symbolic Deixis

### 4.1 The Distinction

Fillmore introduced the distinction between **gestural** and **symbolic** uses of deictic expressions, based on the kind of contextual information needed to interpret them:

**Gestural deixis** requires **audio-visual-tactile information** -- typically a physical gesture like pointing, direction of gaze, or tone of voice -- to be understood:

- "Put it **there**" [points to a specific location] -- without seeing the pointing gesture, you cannot know where "there" is
- "I broke **this** finger" [holds up a finger] -- without seeing which finger, you cannot determine the referent
- "**She** did it" [nods toward a person] -- without seeing the nod, the referent is ambiguous
- "It's about **this** big" [gestures with hands] -- the size is conveyed by the gesture, not the word

**Symbolic deixis** requires only **general knowledge of the spatiotemporal parameters of the speech situation** -- no physical gesture is needed:

- "**This** city stinks" -- you only need to know which city the speaker is currently in
- "I'm leaving **now**" -- you only need to know the approximate time of utterance
- "**I** love this country" -- you only need to know who is speaking
- "It's cold **here**" -- you only need to know the speaker's general location

### 4.2 The Significance of the Distinction

Levinson illustrated the distinction clearly: "**This** finger hurts" (gestural -- requires seeing which finger) vs. "**This** city stinks" (symbolic -- requires only knowing the speaker's location).

The distinction matters because:

1. **Gestural uses cannot be recovered from a transcript alone** -- they require the physical context of the utterance, including visual and spatial information
2. **Symbolic uses can be recovered from linguistic and situational context** -- they require only knowledge of who, where, and when
3. **Written communication can only employ symbolic deixis** -- there is no possibility of physical gesture (though written text develops compensating strategies like footnotes, diagrams, and explicit descriptions)
4. **Phone conversations lose gestural deixis** -- speakers must convert gestural references to symbolic ones or verbal descriptions ("not that one -- the one on the left, the red one")

### 4.3 Non-Deictic Uses of Deictic Forms

Some uses of demonstratives and other typically deictic words are not actually deictic at all:

- "I went to **this** city one time..." -- "this" functions as an indefinite article (similar to "a"), not as a spatial deictic pointing to a specific city
- "**There's** no way that's true" -- existential "there," not locative deixis
- "**Now**, what I was trying to say is..." -- discourse marker "now," not temporal deixis

## 5. Why Deixis Matters for Communication

### 5.1 The Constant Cognitive Work of Deictic Tracking

Every conversation requires participants to continuously track and update deictic reference points:

- **Who is "I" right now?** (shifts with every speaker change)
- **Who is "you" right now?** (shifts with every addressee change)
- **What is "here"?** (depends on where the speaker is)
- **What is "now"?** (depends on when the utterance occurs)
- **Who is "she" or "he"?** (depends on prior discourse and contextual salience)

This tracking happens so automatically for most neurotypical adults that they are unaware of its complexity. But research into language processing reveals that "discourse participants must be able to navigate both actual and virtual origos in interaction, to fully integrate text and context in their language comprehension."

The cognitive work of deictic tracking involves maintaining a mental model of:
- The current speaker and their spatial-temporal position
- The current addressee
- The referents of third-person expressions
- The "now" of the conversation
- The "here" of the conversation
- Any shifts in the deictic center (as in narratives)

### 5.2 Miscommunication from Deictic Failure

Deictic expressions are among the most common sources of referential ambiguity and miscommunication:

**Pronoun ambiguity:**
> "John told Mike that **he** needed to leave."
> Who needs to leave -- John or Mike? The pronoun "he" is ambiguous because both are viable referents.

**Spatial reference failure:**
> "I'll meet you **there**."
> Where is "there"? If the shared context hasn't established a location, this sentence is uninterpretable.

**Temporal reference failure:**
> "She said she'd do it **tomorrow**."
> But when did she say it? "Tomorrow" is anchored to when the statement was made, not when it is being reported. If she said it on Monday, "tomorrow" = Tuesday. But the reporter is telling you on Wednesday -- is the task for Tuesday (already past) or Thursday (the reporter's "tomorrow")?

**Reported speech deictic confusion:**
When reporting someone else's words, all deictic expressions must be recalculated:
- Direct: She said, "**I** will be **here** **tomorrow**."
- Indirect: She said that **she** would be **there** the **next day**.

Every deictic term shifts: "I" becomes "she," "here" becomes "there," "tomorrow" becomes "the next day." This is because the reported speech has a different origo (the original speaker at the original time and place) from the reporting speech (the current speaker at the current time and place).

### 5.3 Written Communication and the Absence of Shared Physical Context

Written communication faces special deictic challenges because writer and reader do not share a physical context:

- **No shared "here"**: The writer's "here" (e.g., their desk in Berlin) is not the reader's "here" (e.g., their couch in Tokyo)
- **No shared "now"**: The writer's "now" (the moment of writing) may be hours, days, or centuries before the reader's "now" (the moment of reading)
- **No gestural deixis possible**: There is no way to point, nod, or gesture in written text

Written language has developed **compensating strategies**:
- Explicit spatial and temporal references ("In this chapter...", "As of the time of writing, March 2026...")
- Discourse deixis that treats the text as a navigable space ("see above," "the following section," "here we argue...")
- Formal date and location markers (letterheads, timestamps, "Dear Sir/Madam")
- Footnotes, cross-references, and hyperlinks as forms of discourse deixis

### 5.4 Digital Communication: New Deictic Challenges

Technology-mediated communication creates novel problems for deixis because the sender and recipient occupy different spatial and temporal contexts connected by a digital channel:

**The problem of "here" in text messaging:**
When you send a text message, your "here" is your current physical location. But the recipient reads the message in their own "here." As research on text-message deixis has shown, "senders compose text-messages in a particular physical location which constitutes their deictic centre (sender's physical location) and recipients retrieve text-messages in another geographical location with reference to which any deictic reference will be interpreted (recipient's physical location)."

**Multiple reference frames:**
The mediated channel creates an additional set of potential deictic centers. In text messaging, reference can be made to:
1. The sender's physical location
2. The recipient's physical location
3. A third space (a place different from either participant's location -- "Let's meet **there** [at the restaurant]")
4. The digital space itself (the mobile phone screen or virtual communication space -- "I'm sending it **here** [in this chat thread]")

**Temporal complications:**
While letters take days or weeks to arrive (creating a large gap between the writer's "now" and the reader's "now"), text messages are delivered almost instantaneously -- but may be read much later. "I'm leaving **now**" sent at 3pm but read at 5pm creates a temporal deictic mismatch.

**Location ambiguity:**
"Where are you?" followed by "I'm **here**" in a text message is problematic -- "here" relative to the sender is not accessible to the recipient without further description. Video calls partially restore shared spatial reference, but the "here" of a video call is still a mediated, two-dimensional frame, not a shared physical space.

**Voicemail and asynchronous media:**
Voicemails create special deictic confusion: the speaker's "now" and "here" are from the time of recording, but the listener's "now" and "here" are from the time of playback. "Call me back **today**" on a voicemail left Monday evening but heard Tuesday morning is ambiguous.

## 6. Key Theoretical Concepts and Extensions

### 6.1 Deixis and Anaphora: The Boundary

The boundary between deixis and anaphora is "unclearly defined" (as the scholarly literature acknowledges), but the general distinction is:

- **Deixis**: Points outward to the extralinguistic context (who is speaking, where, when)
- **Anaphora**: Points backward (or forward) to another expression within the same text or discourse

Example of anaphora: "Susan dropped the plate. **It** shattered loudly." -- "It" refers to "the plate," which was mentioned in the prior sentence. This is not deictic (it does not depend on the context of utterance) but anaphoric (it depends on the co-text).

However, expressions can be **simultaneously deictic and anaphoric**: "I was born in London, and I have lived **here** all my life." -- "Here" is anaphoric (referring back to London) AND deictic (indicating the speaker is currently in London; choosing "there" instead would indicate the speaker is currently elsewhere).

### 6.2 Indexicality

The terms **deixis** and **indexicality** are "frequently used almost interchangeably," but they have somewhat different disciplinary homes:
- **Deixis** is associated with linguistics and was historically used specifically for spatiotemporal reference
- **Indexicality** is associated with philosophy (especially the work of Charles Sanders Peirce) and pragmatics, and is used more broadly

Both refer to the same underlying phenomenon: linguistic expressions whose interpretation requires knowledge of the context of utterance.

### 6.3 The Deictic Field and Narrative Theory

Building on Buhler's concept, narrative theorists have explored how deictic fields function in storytelling. The German literary theorist **Kate Hamburger** studied how deictic word usage distinguishes factual statements from fictional narration, arguing that "acts of fictional narration transfer their referentiality from the actuality of the historical world to the entertained reality of the fictive world." In fiction, the deictic center can be located at any point in the fictional world -- it is unmoored from the author's actual here-and-now.

**Deictic Shift Theory (DST)**, developed by multiple scholars including work archived at SUNY Buffalo, proposes that reading narrative involves a systematic shift of the deictic center from the reader's actual situation into the story world. The reader constructs "a mental model of spatial, temporal, and character information" that serves as the deictic center for interpreting deictic expressions within the narrative. "First and second person pronouns facilitate readerly identification with the textually inscribed position...and evoke a sense of readerly conceptual immersion in the fictional world."

## 7. Summary: Deixis as a System

Deixis is the linguistic system by which speakers anchor their utterances to the context in which they occur. It is organized around the **origo** (the "I-here-now" of the speaker) and radiates outward along five dimensions:

| Dimension | Points to | Core expressions | Key property |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|--------------|
| **Person** | Participants in the speech event | I/you/he/she/they | Referent shifts with every change of speaker |
| **Spatial** | Locations relative to the speaker | here/there, this/that, come/go | Location is always relative, never absolute |
| **Temporal** | Times relative to the moment of utterance | now/then, today/tomorrow/yesterday | Time reference anchored to the "now" of utterance |
| **Social** | Social relationships between participants | titles, honorifics, address forms, T/V pronouns | Relationship indexed at the moment of interaction |
| **Discourse** | Parts of the ongoing discourse itself | this/that (referring to propositions), "as mentioned," "the following" | The discourse itself becomes a navigable domain |

The deictic center can **shift** -- into narratives, into the perspective of another person, into written text, or into imagination -- creating layers of deictic complexity that speakers and listeners must continuously track.

Deixis is **universally present** in all known natural languages, though the specific systems vary considerably (two-way vs. three-way spatial distinctions, presence or absence of inclusive/exclusive "we," T/V pronoun systems, etc.).

For autistic individuals, deictic processing may pose particular challenges, especially:
- **Person deixis**: Tracking the shifting referent of "I" and "you" across conversational turns
- **Perspective-taking in deictic projection**: Adopting someone else's viewpoint as the deictic center
- **Implicit social deixis**: Knowing which address form or register to use without explicit rules
- **Deictic shifts in narrative**: Following when the deictic center moves into a story world or shifts between characters

Understanding deixis as a **system** -- a set of coordinated pointing devices anchored to a movable center -- can provide the explicit framework that makes these otherwise implicit processes visible and learnable.
