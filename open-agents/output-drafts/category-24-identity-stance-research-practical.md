# Category 24 Research: Practical Detection and Response

## Companion Research File to Category 24: Identity and Stance in Interaction

This file covers the practical side of stance work: how to detect stance moves in real conversations, how to recognize when a stance assignment is being installed, how stance operates in institutional settings where one party has formal positioning authority, how digital contexts change the picture, and what the empirical and clinical literature says about successful and unsuccessful refusal strategies.

This file is descriptive and reference-oriented. It is not a how-to manual. The goal is to give an autistic reader the vocabulary and the literature base to name what is happening to them, not to promise that naming it will fix it.

---

## 1. Detection: What to Look For

### 1.1 The Three-Layer Audit

A useful practical heuristic is to audit any suspicious conversational move at three layers. This is not itself a theoretical framework; it is a reading strategy drawn from the stance triangle (Du Bois 2007) and from positioning theory (Davies & Harré 1990).

**Layer 1: What is the object?** What is being evaluated? Identify the object of the stance act. Sometimes the apparent object is a decoy -- the speaker seems to be talking about a plan or an event, but the real object is you. A sentence like "that decision was so unlike you" presents itself as an evaluation of a decision, but the evaluative work is aimed at "you" -- your stable character -- not at the decision.

**Layer 2: What position is being taken?** What is the speaker positioning themselves as? Concerned? Neutral? Authoritative? Loving? Puzzled? Disappointed? The position they are performing is a move, not a fact about them.

**Layer 3: What position are you being offered?** What position is the move placing you into? Student? Patient? Unreliable narrator? Emotional one? Difficult one? Not-serious one? The position you are being offered is the thing that will shape the rest of the conversation, and it is the thing you can (sometimes) refuse.

The audit is retrospective. You do not have to do it in real time. Writing out what someone said later and asking the three questions is itself an act of stance reclamation.

### 1.2 Surface Signals That Stance Assignment Is Happening

The empirical literature on conversation analysis, discursive psychology, and coercive control (Stark 2007; Potter 1996; Goodwin 2007) has identified a set of linguistic surface signals that tend to accompany stance assignment. Not all assignments show all signals. The signals are heuristic, not diagnostic.

- **Stable predicates applied to one-off events.** "You are like this" rather than "you did this." The grammatical shift from verb to copula-plus-adjective is often where the drift from event to identity happens.
- **Generic "you" or "one."** Switching from "I" to "you" or "one" lets the speaker deliver a judgment as if it were a general truth. "One would normally..." is doing more work than it looks.
- **Third-party reference.** Bringing in absent parties ("other people notice too") recruits a phantom audience to back up the position.
- **Concern framings.** Expressions of worry or care that install the target as worried-about without consent. (See §4.2 of the main Category 24 document.)
- **Rhetorical questions.** A question the speaker does not expect you to answer -- especially one that implies a self-evident answer -- is positioning you as someone who should already know.
- **Hedged evaluations.** "I'm not saying you are, but..." performs the evaluation while denying it. The denial insulates the move from refusal.
- **Chronological compression.** Condensing events from different times as if they belonged to a single pattern. This is how a handful of distinct moments become "the way you always are."

Each of these signals is individually innocent. Any of them can appear in good-faith conversation. The reason to notice them is that they accumulate in bad-faith conversations, and the accumulation itself is detectable once you know what to look for.

### 1.3 Your Body as a Detector

Community knowledge, not clinical finding: many autistic people report a body-based sense that something is wrong in a conversation before they can name what is happening. This is worth taking seriously as a detection signal even when it does not correspond to a specific linguistic feature. The felt sense of "something just happened to me" is often the earliest sign that a stance assignment has been installed.

We label this as community knowledge rather than empirical finding because the research base is thin. There is a growing literature on interoception and autism (DuBois et al. 2016; Garfinkel et al. 2016) but it does not yet support a specific claim about stance detection. What we can say is that the felt sense is widely reported, is not reducible to "paranoia," and is worth using as a prompt to retrospectively audit the conversation.

## 2. Institutional Settings: Formal Positioning Authority

### 2.1 What "Positional Authority" Means

In some settings, one party has formal authority to position the other into consequential categories that the positioned party has limited ability to refuse. This is not the same as ordinary conversational positioning. It is positioning that is backed by institutional procedures, records, and decisions.

Examples of formal positioning authority:

- **Clinicians** position patients into diagnostic and treatment categories.
- **Supervisors and managers** position employees into performance and culture-fit categories.
- **Judges, attorneys, and officers** position defendants, plaintiffs, and witnesses into legal categories.
- **Social services staff** position service users into eligibility and risk categories.
- **Teachers and school administrators** position students into ability and behavior categories.
- **Parents and family elders**, in family-of-origin settings, position adult children into family-script categories.

Each of these settings has its own vocabulary, its own record-keeping practices, and its own procedures for appeal. But they share the structural feature that the positioned party does not have symmetric positioning power in return.

### 2.2 Why This Matters for Autistic Adults

The empirical literature documents disproportionate positioning consequences for autistic people across these settings:

- **Medical**: Doherty et al. (2022) and Shaw et al. (2024) document "diagnostic overshadowing," in which physical symptoms reported by autistic patients are attributed to autism and not investigated. This is a positioning move: the patient is placed into the category "autistic patient, probably psychosomatic," and the placement shapes what happens next.

- **Employment**: Romualdez et al. (2021), Bury et al. (2020) document the prevalence of performance reviews that translate autistic communication defaults into "not a culture fit" or "poor interpersonal skills" language. The review is a positioning move with record-keeping and financial consequences.

- **Legal**: Maras et al. (2019), Crane et al. (2018) document the credibility problems autistic witnesses face in legal settings. Autistic patterns of recall and delivery are often read as "inconsistent" or "evasive," which is a stance assignment that the positioned party can only partially counter.

- **Education**: Horgan et al. (2023) and earlier work document the positioning of autistic students into behavioral and ability categories that shape their educational trajectory, often in ways that underestimate their capacity.

- **Social services**: Camm-Crosbie et al. (2019) document the experiences of autistic adults navigating service systems where their self-reports are routinely discounted.

- **Family-of-origin**: Less formal but often the most durable, family systems have their own "positioning records" in the form of family narratives. The autistic member is positioned into a family role (the difficult one, the sensitive one, the problem) and the positioning is repeated across many conversations with a shared audience of relatives.

### 2.3 What Refusal Looks Like in These Settings

In institutional settings, stance refusal usually has to be done in two layers simultaneously: within the conversation (the usual kinds of three-move refusal) and outside it (documentation, external witnesses, formal appeals).

Within-conversation refusal in institutional settings tends to work best when it:

- **Stays on events, not character.** "On [date], I raised this specific concern" rather than "you think I'm a troublemaker."
- **Uses the institution's own vocabulary.** If the institution's procedures recognize certain categories, using those categories is often more effective than inventing new ones.
- **Accepts the formal record as partial.** Insisting on being fully seen inside the institution's procedures can backfire. Partial refusal plus external backup is often more sustainable than full in-room refusal.
- **Names the event without escalating the frame.** The goal is to prevent the event from drifting into identity, not to win the moment.

Out-of-conversation backup is covered in detail in the manipulation sub-hub's strategy pages (documentation, external evidence, reality-check networks, professional support). The Category 24 framework provides the vocabulary for why those strategies are necessary: because within-conversation refusal alone is not enough to counter formal positioning authority.

## 3. Digital Stance: Online Contexts

### 3.1 The Platform Layer

Online contexts change stance work in several ways. This section draws on work by Marwick and boyd (2011) on context collapse, Tagg and colleagues (2017) on online stance work, and recent sociolinguistic work on platform-specific style.

**Context collapse**: Offline, you can choose different stances for different audiences. Online, the audience is often undefined -- your post might be read by a close friend, a colleague, a stranger, or a future employer. The stance you perform has to work across all of them simultaneously. This is exhausting for any speaker and particularly so for autistic speakers whose default stance work is already effortful.

**Platform conventions**: Each platform has its own stance norms. A default stance that reads as neutral on one platform reads as cold, hostile, or suspicious on another. Twitter/X defaults differ from LinkedIn defaults differ from Discord server defaults. The autistic reader who has learned one platform's stance vocabulary does not automatically have another platform's.

**Persistence**: Online stance moves are durable in a way spoken ones are not. A stance move you took in 2017 is still available to be quoted, screenshotted, and redeployed in 2026. This changes the risk calculus for any stance move that might later be read as identity evidence.

**Decontextualization**: Platforms encourage the removal of stance moves from their original context. A sentence that was a stance move in a specific conversation becomes, when quote-tweeted or screenshotted, a free-floating identity claim about the speaker.

### 3.2 Digital Stance Flexibility

The stance/identity distinction is especially visible in digital contexts because it is easy to perform different stances on different platforms. The same person may have a LinkedIn identity, a Twitter identity, a gaming handle, an anonymous forum account, and a private Discord persona -- each built out of different stance patterns. The conventional framing in the popular press treats this as "inauthenticity," but pragmatically it is simply the fact that stance is context-dependent and different platforms afford different stances.

For autistic users, digital stance flexibility can be a resource (you can perform unmasked stances in community spaces without paying the cost at work) or a trap (the masked stance you perform at work can be quoted out of context into your community space, exposing you to both audiences at once).

### 3.3 Digital-Specific Stance Attacks

Digital contexts also enable specific stance attacks that are harder to pull off face-to-face:

- **Screenshot weaponization**: Taking a stance move out of context and presenting it as identity evidence.
- **Pile-on positioning**: Using the platform's audience to accumulate many small stance assignments from different actors, each individually trivial but collectively consolidating a position.
- **Profile-based assignment**: Using visible profile features (display name, bio, follower count, post history) to pre-assign a stance before any interaction happens.
- **Cross-platform context collapse**: Importing a stance move from one platform to another where the receiving audience does not have the context to read it correctly.

Each of these is a variant of the general pattern described in §5 of the main document: unilateral repositioning made possible by structural features of the communicative environment.

## 4. Repair and Stance Reclamation

### 4.1 Within-Conversation Repair

When a stance assignment has just been installed, the window for in-the-moment repair is short. Conversation analysts (Schegloff 2007) have documented that corrections and challenges are easier at or near the moment of the trouble, and harder the further out you go. The practical implication: if you notice a stance assignment happening, the earliest clear opportunity to refuse it is usually the most effective.

The three-move refusal (name the event, refuse the position, re-position yourself) described in §6 of the main document is drawn loosely from the conversation-analytic literature on repair, combined with the discursive psychology literature on counter-positioning.

One caveat: within-conversation repair is optimized for good-faith interlocutors. With a bad-faith interlocutor, repair moves can be absorbed into the positioning system. A refusal becomes "proof" of the position being refused ("see, now she's being difficult about it"). This is one of the distinguishing features of manipulation patterns, and it is why the manipulation sub-hub recommends pairing in-conversation refusal with out-of-conversation documentation and external witnesses.

### 4.2 Delayed Repair

When in-the-moment refusal is not safe or not feasible, delayed repair is sometimes an option. Delayed repair looks like:

- **Next-encounter correction.** "I've been thinking about what you said last week, and I want to go back to it." This reopens the stance event and gives you a chance to refuse it on better terms.
- **Written correction.** A written follow-up (email, text) has the advantage of being a record, and it bypasses the real-time processing demands of in-person interaction.
- **Third-party correction.** Telling a trusted third party what happened, and having them correct the position in their own subsequent interactions with the assigner. This is costly but sometimes works.

Each of these has costs. Delayed repair re-surfaces the event, which can be distressing. Written repair is permanent in ways speech is not. Third-party repair involves telling someone else the story, which means re-living it.

### 4.3 Stance Reclamation in the Absence of the Other Party

The most reliable stance work is often done outside the conversation that caused the damage. This is not less real than within-conversation repair -- it is differently real. It includes:

- **Self-narration.** Telling yourself what happened in accurate stance-theoretic language. "I was positioned as difficult. I did not become difficult. The positioning was a move they made."
- **Community narration.** Telling other people in spaces where your self-positioning will be taken seriously. The qualitative literature on autistic community spaces (Crompton et al. 2020; Botha et al. 2022) documents the role of peer interaction in sustaining claimed identities against external stance attacks.
- **Documentary narration.** Keeping records -- of events, of communications, of decisions -- that external witnesses can later use to corroborate your version of events.

These three forms of out-of-conversation narration together constitute most of what resilient stance work looks like in practice. They are the infrastructure that allows in-conversation refusals, when they happen, to be more than isolated moments.

## 5. The Limits of Refusal

### 5.1 Structural Limits

This section is the counterweight to the preceding ones. Stance refusal is a real pragmatic move. It is also a move that has limits, and those limits are structural rather than personal.

**Power asymmetry.** When the positioner has formal authority over consequential records (medical charts, performance reviews, legal filings, school IEPs), within-conversation refusal cannot by itself re-write the record. The person positioned may be able to enter a counter-statement, but the original positioning remains on the record.

**Audience pickup.** A refusal that the audience does not accept can be re-absorbed into the position being refused. In settings where the audience is pre-committed to the positioner's frame -- for instance, a family meeting in which the positioner is the accepted authority -- refusal can look from the inside like the person is making things worse.

**Cognitive load.** Executing a three-move refusal requires real-time processing of the stance move plus composition of a response plus delivery of that response plus monitoring for the other party's reaction. This is expensive for any speaker and more so when the speaker is tired, distressed, or under the particular kind of load that comes from being stance-attacked.

**Cumulative fatigue.** Refusing stance assignments in a long-running relationship or institutional setting is not a one-off event. It has to happen repeatedly, often daily. The cumulative cost can exceed the cost of partial acceptance, which is part of why long-running coercive control settings produce the stance erosion described in the main document.

### 5.2 Choosing Not to Refuse

An autism-affirming framing insists that choosing not to refuse is a legitimate choice. Not all stance assignments must be fought. Sometimes the cost of the fight exceeds the benefit, and the speaker's resources are better spent elsewhere. This is a pragmatic judgment, not a moral failure.

Choosing not to refuse inside the conversation does not mean accepting the assignment as accurate. It means holding the assignment as inaccurate while not contesting it out loud. This internal stance -- "I know this is not who I am; I am not going to argue with them about it right now" -- is itself a form of stance reclamation, even when it remains private.

### 5.3 When to Leave

Some stance assignment patterns are not survivable in repair. The manipulation sub-hub's pages on coercive control, on family-of-origin manipulation, and on certain institutional contexts all include guidance on recognizing when the pattern is not going to change and exit is the realistic option. Category 24 does not duplicate that guidance; it provides the pragmatic theory that makes the recognition possible. The key pragmatic marker is cumulative stance erosion (§5.3 of the main document): when the speaker's capacity to take stances at all has been worn down, individual refusal moves have run out of leverage, and the question is no longer repair but preservation of the speaker's remaining stance capacity.

## 6. Practical Integration: Worked Examples

This section works through three realistic examples at a descriptive level. These are not exercises; they are demonstrations of how the detection and refusal vocabulary applies.

### 6.1 Example: The Post-Meeting Email

Setting: Workplace. A junior autistic employee disagreed with a senior colleague in a team meeting. The next morning, the senior colleague sends an email: "Thanks for sharing your thoughts yesterday. I think we just need to be careful about how direct we come across -- some of the other folks on the team are sensitive, and you came on pretty strong."

**Three-layer audit:**
- **Object**: The employee's communication in the meeting.
- **Position of sender**: Caring mentor giving gentle feedback.
- **Position offered to recipient**: Aggressive speaker whose directness has upset others, who now needs to moderate herself.

**Surface signals**: Stable predicate ("how direct we come across" -- a style trait, not an event); third-party reference ("some of the other folks"); concern framing ("we need to be careful"); hedged evaluation ("you came on pretty strong" after presenting it as not-criticism).

**Stance assignment installed**: "You are someone who needs coaching on how to be less aggressive."

**Possible refusal** (written, within-conversation): "Thanks for the note. I want to make sure I'm tracking the specifics. In the meeting yesterday, I raised a concern about the project timeline and then accepted the team's decision to proceed. Can you say more about what specifically in that exchange read as too direct, so I can understand the concern?"

**What the refusal does**: Names the event (raised a concern, accepted the decision), refuses the position (asks for specifics rather than accepting the general characterization), and re-positions the speaker (as someone who is engaging with feedback, not resisting it). Whether this refusal succeeds depends on whether the sender is in good faith. If they are, the refusal opens a productive conversation. If they are not, the refusal at least creates a written record that the event was specific rather than general, which protects against future accumulation.

### 6.2 Example: The Family Text Thread

Setting: Family group chat. After a holiday dinner at which the autistic person declined a hug from a relative, another family member posts: "I'm just worried about you. You've seemed so closed off lately and I don't understand why you have to make everything so hard."

**Three-layer audit:**
- **Object**: The autistic person's behavior at dinner (specifically, declining the hug).
- **Position of sender**: Worried family member.
- **Position offered to recipient**: Closed-off person who makes things hard; the one who needs to explain herself.

**Surface signals**: Concern framing; stable predicate ("closed off"); chronological compression ("lately" -- covering how long?); generic "make everything so hard."

**Stance assignment installed**: "You are someone who has become difficult, and we deserve an explanation."

**Possible refusal**: This is a harder case. Within-conversation refusal in a family group chat rarely works, because the audience is committed and the format is public. A more effective move is usually out-of-conversation: private reply to a trusted family member, or no public reply at all, with documentation of the pattern.

**What the refusal does not do**: It does not need to change the sender's mind. It needs to prevent the assignment from consolidating into family consensus, which is a slower and more distributed kind of work.

### 6.3 Example: The Clinical Note

Setting: Medical appointment. The autistic patient reports a physical symptom. The physician writes in the chart: "Patient presents with vague somatic complaints in the context of known autism spectrum disorder and possible anxiety."

**Three-layer audit:**
- **Object**: The symptom the patient reported.
- **Position of sender**: Professional observer.
- **Position offered to recipient**: Patient whose somatic complaints are probably anxiety-driven and probably not worth further investigation.

**Surface signals**: Stable predicates ("vague"; "somatic"); institutional compression of the reported symptom into a category that shapes future care.

**Stance assignment installed**: "This patient's symptom reports are unreliable in the direction of overreporting."

**Possible refusal**: Within-conversation refusal is very limited here, because the chart is not co-authored. Effective refusal in medical settings usually involves: asking for specific symptoms to be documented verbatim; requesting specific tests; bringing a second party; and, when possible, obtaining copies of notes after the fact and submitting written corrections. The pragmatic theory from Category 24 names the move that is happening (testimonial stance assignment); the practical response relies on institutional procedures outside the conversation itself.

## 7. Notes for the Wider Hub

The detection heuristics and repair vocabulary in this research file are intended to be usable by other categories and by the manipulation sub-hub. Specifically:

- **Hedging (Category 6)** uses epistemic stance markers; this file's Layer 2 ("what position is being taken") overlaps directly with hedging analysis.
- **Common ground (Category 16)** provides the mutual-knowledge background against which stance assignments are evaluated; a stance assignment often works by pretending something is already common ground.
- **Social hierarchy (Category 22)** specifies the power asymmetries under which formal positioning authority operates; this file extends that analysis into institutional settings.
- **Manipulation sub-hub pattern pages** rely on the three-layer audit and the surface signals in §1 as their operational vocabulary for "how to tell."
- **Manipulation sub-hub strategy pages** rely on the limits-of-refusal discussion in §5 as the theoretical backing for why documentation, external evidence, and reality-check networks are necessary.
