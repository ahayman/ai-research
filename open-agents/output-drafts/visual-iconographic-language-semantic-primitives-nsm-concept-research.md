# Concept Research: Semantic Primitives and Natural Semantic Metalanguage (NSM)

## Overview

If you want to build a visual language from the ground up, you need to know what the ground is. What are the irreducible building blocks of human meaning — the concepts that cannot be decomposed into simpler concepts, that every human language encodes, and from which all other meanings can be constructed? This is the question that Anna Wierzbicka has spent over five decades investigating through her Natural Semantic Metalanguage (NSM) program, and the answer she has arrived at — 65 semantic primes, claimed to be universal across every known human language — is the most empirically grounded candidate for the "atoms of meaning" from which a visual language could be composed.

The NSM program began in 1972 with Wierzbicka's identification of 14 proposed semantic primes. Through systematic cross-linguistic testing across more than 30 typologically diverse languages (from English to Mandarin to Yankunytjatjara to Ewe to Malay), the inventory has been refined and expanded to its current 65 primes. These include concepts like I, YOU, SOMEONE, SOMETHING, GOOD, BAD, BIG, SMALL, DO, HAPPEN, MOVE, THINK, KNOW, WANT, FEEL, SAY, SEE, HEAR, WHERE, WHEN, BEFORE, AFTER, BECAUSE, IF, NOT, VERY, MORE, LIKE, KIND OF, and PART OF. The claim is extraordinary: these 65 concepts appear to have exact lexical equivalents — not translations, but independently arising expressions of the same conceptual content — in every known human language. If true, they represent the universal foundation on which all human meaning is built.

For a visual language project, NSM offers two critical gifts: a principled inventory of what must be visually representable (the 65 primes plus approximately 50 "semantic molecules"), and a method (explication) for defining any complex concept entirely in terms of primes. But NSM also poses a fundamental design challenge: many primes are inherently abstract (IF, BECAUSE, NOT, VERY, MAYBE) and resist visual representation. Drawing a picture of SOMEONE is straightforward; drawing a picture of IF is a design problem that has no obvious solution. This tension — between the linguistic universality of semantic primes and the visual representability of image schemas — is one of the central challenges that any metaphor-based visual language must resolve.

## Core Components

### Component 1: The NSM Program — History and Development

- **Purpose**: Identify the irreducible, universal building blocks of human meaning through systematic cross-linguistic analysis
- **Founding**: Developed by Anna Wierzbicka at Warsaw University (later Australian National University) beginning in the early 1970s. Cliff Goddard at Griffith University has been the principal collaborator and co-developer since the 1990s.
- **Development Timeline**:
  - 1972: Wierzbicka proposes 14 semantic primes in *Semantic Primitives*
  - 1980s: Expansion and testing across additional languages
  - 1996: Wierzbicka's *Semantics: Primes and Universals* establishes 55 primes
  - 2002: Wierzbicka and Goddard expand inventory to 60 primes
  - 2014–present: Current inventory stabilized at 65 primes
- **Methodological Principle**: Primes are identified through the "reductive paraphrase" method — if a concept can be defined using simpler concepts, it is not a prime. A concept is a prime if and only if it (a) cannot be defined in simpler terms, (b) has exact lexical equivalents in all tested languages, and (c) participates in the same combinatorial patterns across languages.
- **Cross-Linguistic Validation**: Primes have been tested and confirmed in 30+ languages, including Polish, Russian, English, Spanish, French, Italian, Swedish, Danish, Malay, Mandarin, Japanese, Korean, Lao, Mbula, Yankunytjatjara, Arrernte, Ewe, Amharic, and others. The languages span all major language families and include both literate traditions and oral cultures.

### Component 2: The 65 Semantic Primes — Complete Inventory

- **Purpose**: Enumerate the full inventory of proposed irreducible meaning atoms

**Substantives**: I, YOU, SOMEONE/PERSON, PEOPLE, SOMETHING/THING, BODY

**Determiners**: THIS, THE SAME, OTHER/ELSE

**Quantifiers**: ONE, TWO, SOME, ALL, MUCH/MANY

**Evaluators**: GOOD, BAD

**Descriptors**: BIG, SMALL

**Mental Predicates**: THINK, KNOW, WANT, DON'T WANT, FEEL, SEE, HEAR

**Speech**: SAY, WORDS, TRUE

**Actions, Events, and Movement**: DO, HAPPEN, MOVE

**Existence and Possession**: THERE IS/EXIST, BE (SOMEONE/SOMETHING), HAVE

**Life and Death**: LIVE, DIE

**Time**: WHEN/TIME, NOW, BEFORE, AFTER, A LONG TIME, A SHORT TIME, FOR SOME TIME, MOMENT

**Space**: WHERE/PLACE, HERE, ABOVE, BELOW, FAR, NEAR, SIDE, INSIDE, TOUCH

**Logical Concepts**: NOT, MAYBE, CAN, BECAUSE, IF

**Intensifier/Augmentor**: VERY, MORE

**Taxonomy and Partonomy**: KIND OF, PART OF

**Similarity**: LIKE/AS/WAY

- **Key Properties**:
  - Each prime has its own lexical (phonological) embodiment in every language
  - Many primes have "allolexes" — multiple surface forms that express the same underlying concept (e.g., "someone" and "person" are allolexes in English)
  - Each prime has specific grammatical (combinatorial) properties that are consistent across languages
  - Primes combine directly with each other in defined patterns (determiners with substantives, quantifiers with substantives, evaluators with substantives, etc.)

### Component 3: Semantic Molecules — The Intermediate Layer

- **Purpose**: Provide intermediate building blocks between primes and complex concepts
- **Definition**: Semantic molecules are words that are not themselves primes but can be defined entirely using primes, and are necessary building blocks for defining more complex concepts. They create a hierarchical structure between atomic primes and complex meanings.
- **Approximate Count**: ~50 universal semantic molecules identified
- **Categories of Molecules**:
  - **Body parts**: HANDS, MOUTH, LEGS, EYES, EARS, HEAD
  - **Physical activities**: EAT, DRINK, SIT, LIE DOWN, SLEEP
  - **The verb MAKE**: (distinguished from DO)
  - **Shape descriptors**: ROUND, LONG, FLAT
  - **Physical qualities**: HARD, SOFT, SHARP
  - **Ethnogeometrical terms**: spatial configurations
  - **Taxonomic terms**: e.g., CREATURE/ANIMAL, PLANT
  - **Environmental**: WATER, FIRE, SUN, GROUND/EARTH, SKY
  - **Social**: MAN, WOMAN, CHILD
- **Why Molecules Matter**: Defining complex concepts directly from primes often produces very long, unwieldy explications. Molecules serve as compression — pre-built meaningful chunks that simplify the path from primes to complex meaning. For a visual language, molecules suggest a natural second layer of symbols above the prime inventory.

### Component 4: The Explication Method

- **Purpose**: Define any complex concept entirely in terms of primes (and molecules), eliminating circularity
- **How It Works**: An NSM explication is a reductive paraphrase that captures the complete meaning of a concept using only semantic primes, arranged in canonical sentences. The explication must be cross-linguistically translatable — if the same explication works in English, Mandarin, and Yankunytjatjara, the original concept likely has the same structure in those languages.
- **Example Explication — "happy"**:
  - X felt something good at that time
  - X felt something like this:
    - "something good is happening to me"
    - "I want this"
    - "this is good"
  - X felt like this because X thought something good
- **Example Explication — "angry"**:
  - X felt something bad at that time
  - X felt something like this:
    - "someone did something bad"
    - "I don't want this"
    - "I want to do something because of this"
  - X felt like this because X thought something like this
- **Key Insight for Visual Language**: Every NSM explication is a compositional structure built from primes. If each prime can be assigned a visual symbol, then any NSM explication can (in principle) be visually composed. The question is whether the resulting visual compositions are readable and learnable.

### Component 5: Visual Representability of Primes

- **Purpose**: Assess which primes are amenable to visual representation and which resist it
- **Highly Imageable Primes** (concrete or spatial — easy to visualize):
  - Substantives: I (pointing to self), YOU (pointing to other), BODY (figure)
  - Space: ABOVE, BELOW, NEAR, FAR, INSIDE, SIDE, HERE, TOUCH
  - Descriptors: BIG, SMALL
  - Actions: MOVE, DO
  - Life: LIVE, DIE
- **Moderately Imageable Primes** (require metaphorical or conventional representation):
  - Quantifiers: ONE, TWO, SOME, ALL, MUCH/MANY (dots, groupings)
  - Time: BEFORE, AFTER, NOW (arrow metaphor: timeline)
  - Mental predicates: SEE (eye), HEAR (ear), FEEL (heart/hand)
  - Evaluators: GOOD, BAD (possibly through embodied metaphors — upward/downward)
- **Abstract Primes** (highly resistant to visual representation):
  - Logical: NOT, MAYBE, CAN, BECAUSE, IF
  - Speech: SAY, WORDS, TRUE
  - Mental: THINK, KNOW, WANT
  - Intensifier: VERY, MORE
  - Taxonomy: KIND OF, PART OF
  - Similarity: LIKE/AS/WAY
- **The Core Challenge**: Approximately 20–25 of the 65 primes are highly abstract and resist iconic visual representation. These are precisely the primes needed for grammar, logic, and complex thought. A visual language must find a way to represent NOT, IF, BECAUSE, and MAYBE — the primes that make the difference between a picture dictionary and a language.
- **Possible Strategies**:
  - **Conventional symbols**: Assign arbitrary but learnable symbols (like X for NOT, ? for MAYBE)
  - **Metaphorical grounding**: Use image schemas to ground abstract primes (NEGATION as REMOVAL/BLOCKAGE, CAUSATION as FORCE, CONDITIONALITY as PATH-WITH-BRANCH)
  - **Spatial grammar**: Use position rather than shape to encode abstract relationships (e.g., a symbol placed below the baseline = negated)

### Component 6: Relationship Between NSM Primes and Image Schemas

- **Purpose**: Map the overlap and gaps between Wierzbicka's semantic primes and Johnson's image schemas
- **Overlaps**: Several primes have direct image schema counterparts:
  - INSIDE → CONTAINER schema (interior/boundary/exterior)
  - ABOVE/BELOW → UP-DOWN / VERTICALITY schema
  - NEAR/FAR → PROXIMITY / DISTANCE schema
  - MOVE → PATH / SOURCE-PATH-GOAL schema
  - PART OF → PART-WHOLE schema
  - TOUCH → CONTACT schema
- **Primes Without Image Schema Counterparts**: Many abstract primes (IF, BECAUSE, NOT, TRUE, MAYBE, KNOW, THINK, WANT) do not have direct image schema equivalents, though some can be metaphorically grounded:
  - BECAUSE → FORCE schema (causation as force dynamics)
  - WANT → ATTRACTION / COMPULSION schemas
  - NOT → REMOVAL / BLOCKAGE schemas (negation as blocked path)
  - IF → PATH-WITH-BRANCH schema (conditionality as forking path)
- **Image Schemas Without Prime Counterparts**: Several image schemas are not represented among the 65 primes:
  - BALANCE (equilibrium, axis)
  - CYCLE (recurring pattern)
  - LINK (connection between entities)
  - CENTER-PERIPHERY
  - SCALE (degree, gradient)
  - SPLITTING / MERGING
  - FULL-EMPTY
- **Key Insight**: NSM primes and image schemas are complementary inventories, not competing frameworks. Primes provide the semantic atoms; image schemas provide the spatial-relational structures that can visually ground those atoms. A visual language likely needs both: the meaning inventory from NSM and the visual vocabulary from image schemas. The gap between them — abstract primes that resist visual representation — is where the hardest design work lies.

### Component 7: Criticisms of NSM

- **Purpose**: Acknowledge the limitations and contested aspects of the NSM program
- **Criticism 1 — Are 65 primes sufficient?**: Some linguists argue that 65 primes are too few to capture the full complexity of human meaning. Defining complex technical, emotional, or cultural concepts in terms of only 65 building blocks produces very long, unwieldy explications that may lose nuance.
- **Criticism 2 — Is universality overstated?**: While primes have been tested in 30+ languages, the claim of exact lexical equivalence across all human languages is extremely strong. Some critics argue that apparent equivalences mask subtle cross-linguistic differences in how primes are used combinatorially.
- **Criticism 3 — The semantic molecules problem**: Although semantic molecules were introduced as intermediate building blocks, critics argue they "do little more than conceal complexity" — that the decomposed forms of molecules show they are not truly necessary as a separate layer, and their inclusion weakens the parsimony of the system.
- **Criticism 4 — Circularity concerns**: Defining primes as "indefinable" while using them in complex combinatorial patterns raises questions about whether the combinatorial rules themselves introduce hidden complexity.
- **Criticism 5 — English-centric presentation**: Despite cross-linguistic testing, the primes are typically presented and discussed using English exponents. Critics argue this creates an English-centric framing that may bias the identification of "universal" concepts.
- **Comparison with Alternative Proposals**:
  - Jackendoff's conceptual semantics: focuses on spatial structure and event decomposition, more computationally oriented
  - Pinker's "mentalese": postulates a language of thought with more structure than NSM primes
  - Pustejovsky's generative lexicon: focuses on how word meanings combine rather than on atomic decomposition

## How It Works

### Step 1: Identifying a Semantic Prime
- **Trigger**: Encountering a concept that appears to be simple and irreducible
- **Action**: Attempt to define it using simpler terms. If every attempt at definition is either circular or uses concepts equally complex, the concept is a prime candidate.
- **Result**: Addition to the prime inventory, subject to cross-linguistic testing

### Step 2: Cross-Linguistic Validation
- **Trigger**: A proposed prime must be verified across diverse languages
- **Action**: Linguists working in different languages test whether an exact lexical equivalent exists — not just a rough translation, but a word that covers the same semantic range and participates in the same combinatorial patterns.
- **Result**: Confirmation or rejection of the proposed prime. If a concept lacks an exact equivalent in any tested language, it is not a prime.

### Step 3: Explication of Complex Concepts
- **Trigger**: A complex concept (e.g., "jealousy," "democracy," "breakfast") needs to be decomposed
- **Action**: Write a reductive paraphrase using only primes and validated molecules, structured as a series of canonical sentences
- **Result**: An explication that captures the concept's full meaning without circularity, translatable across all tested languages

### Step 4: Visual Representation (proposed)
- **Trigger**: A prime or molecule needs to be assigned a visual symbol for the visual language
- **Action**: Determine whether the prime is (a) directly imageable (draw it), (b) metaphorically groundable (find the image schema it maps onto), or (c) necessarily conventional (assign an arbitrary but learnable symbol)
- **Result**: A visual symbol for each prime, with transparent derivation for imageable primes and documented metaphorical grounding for abstract ones

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: Explication of "Afraid"
- **Scenario**: Decomposing the emotion concept "afraid" using only semantic primes
- **Illustration**: X felt something bad at that time. X felt something like this: "something bad can happen to me. I don't want this. I can't do anything." X felt like this because X thought something like this.
- **Key Insight**: Every word in this explication is a semantic prime or a canonical phrase using primes. The explication captures what is common to "afraid" across all languages — it strips away culturally specific connotations while preserving the universal core.

### Example 2: The "Visual Representability" Challenge
- **Scenario**: Attempting to assign visual symbols to the logical primes
- **Illustration**: BECAUSE could be visually represented as an arrow from cause to effect (using the FORCE image schema). IF could be represented as a branching path (PATH-WITH-BRANCH schema). NOT could be represented as a blocked or crossed-out path (BLOCKAGE/REMOVAL schema). MAYBE could be represented as a dashed/uncertain path (partial PATH schema).
- **Key Insight**: These visual representations are not iconic — they are metaphorical. A branching path does not look like "if." But the metaphor is grounded in embodied experience: uncertainty feels like a fork in the road. The question is whether these metaphorical visual representations are learnable and stable across cultures.

### Example 3: The Molecule Layer in Practice
- **Scenario**: Defining "breakfast" requires the molecule EAT (not a prime, but defined from primes as: "X does something with the mouth to something. Because of this, something goes inside X's body. X does this because X wants this to be inside X's body.")
- **Illustration**: "breakfast" = eating that happens at the beginning of the day's waking time
- **Key Insight**: Without molecules, the explication of "breakfast" would need to include the full decomposition of "eat" every time, making the explication enormously long. Molecules serve as chunking — the same cognitive compression mechanism that makes natural language efficient. A visual language will similarly need intermediate-complexity symbols above the prime layer.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: NSM claims that 65 words are enough to express everything.
  - **Reality**: NSM claims that 65 primes are the irreducible foundation from which all other meanings can be composed. Complex expression requires combining primes in sophisticated ways. The 65 primes are to language what atoms are to chemistry — not the things you build with directly, but the elements from which everything is built.

- **Misconception**: NSM primes are "just English words" presented as universal.
  - **Reality**: The primes are presented in English for convenience, but each has been independently identified in 30+ typologically diverse languages. The English words are exponents, not definitions. The prime SOMEONE is not the English word "someone" — it is the cross-linguistic concept for which "someone" is the English surface form.

- **Misconception**: If you can draw all 65 primes, you have a visual language.
  - **Reality**: Having 65 visual symbols is necessary but far from sufficient. A visual language also requires composition rules (grammar), spatial arrangement conventions (syntax), and mechanisms for recursion and embedding. The primes are the vocabulary; the grammar is a separate design challenge.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **DON'T WANT as a separate prime**: Interestingly, DON'T WANT is listed as a prime alongside WANT — it is not simply NOT + WANT. This reflects the cross-linguistic finding that many languages have a single lexical item for "don't want" / "refuse" that is not compositionally derived from negation + desire. This has implications for visual language design: some "compound" concepts may need to be treated as atomic.

- **Polysemy of prime exponents**: The English word "can" expresses two different primes: CAN (ability) and MAYBE (possibility). In other languages, these may have completely different lexical forms. A visual language would need separate symbols for each underlying concept, regardless of how they surface in any particular spoken language.

- **Cultural specificity of molecules**: While primes are claimed as universal, some molecules (like MAN, WOMAN) carry culturally variable semantic weight. Whether these molecules are truly universal or reflect Western categorical assumptions is debated.

## Related Concepts

- **Image Schemas as Visual Building Blocks** (Category 4): The visual-spatial structures that could ground abstract primes in embodied experience
- **Compositionality** (Category 5): The grammatical rules needed to combine prime-symbols into complex meanings
- **Historical Visual Language Systems** (Category 1): Blissymbolics' ~120 base elements represent a different inventory choice than NSM's 65 primes
- **Semasiographic vs. Glottographic** (Category 2): NSM's primes are semantically defined, not phonetically — they are inherently suited for semasiographic encoding
- **The Phoneme Representation Problem** (Category 6): SAY and WORDS are primes that directly reference the speech modality — how does a visual language handle primes about speaking?

## Research Gaps

- Has anyone attempted to systematically assign visual representations to all 65 semantic primes? If so, what were the results for the abstract primes (IF, BECAUSE, NOT, MAYBE)?
- How do NSM explications handle domain-specific technical language (scientific, legal, medical)? Can the explication method scale to specialized vocabulary?
- What is the formal relationship between NSM's explication method and image schema composition? Can an NSM explication be systematically translated into an image schema composition?
- How long are typical NSM explications for common concepts? Is there empirical data on the cognitive load of processing explication-based (vs. lexicalized) meaning?
- How would a visual language handle the SAY and WORDS primes — concepts that directly reference the speech modality — in a system designed to be independent of speech?
