# Foundational Safety Research: Autism-Specific Application of Safety, Manipulation, and Recovery Frameworks

**Purpose:** This document provides the empirical and community-knowledge foundation for the highest-stakes category of the English pragmatics learning hub — material on abuse, coercive control, manipulation, and trauma recovery. Its central question is not "what is known about manipulation in general?" but rather "how does what we know need to be adapted for autistic people, and where does mainstream research and advice actively fail them?"

**Prepared for:** Autism-specific pragmatics safety content authoring
**Date:** April 2026
**Research approach:** Peer-reviewed literature review, with explicit evidence-quality labels and gap identification

---

## Evidence Quality Labeling Convention

Throughout this document, claims are tagged with one of the following labels:

- **[VALIDATED]** — Replicated findings across multiple peer-reviewed studies, with converging methodologies and reasonable sample sizes. Still limited to the populations sampled.
- **[PRELIMINARY]** — Published peer-reviewed findings but with important limitations: single-study, small sample, non-random sampling, unreplicated, or contested methodology.
- **[EXTRAPOLATED]** — Claim is drawn from research on adjacent populations (e.g., general disability, intellectual disability, general DV literature) and applied to autistic adults without direct empirical validation.
- **[COMMUNITY KNOWLEDGE]** — Experiential knowledge developed in autistic communities; may converge with or precede formal research, but is not itself empirical in the clinical-research sense.
- **[CONTESTED]** — Published but actively disputed on methodological grounds by peer-reviewed critique.

The distinction matters. Every downstream page must be honest about which category it is drawing on, because the user has explicitly stated that untested strategies presented as validated are unacceptable.

## Part 1. The Empirical Base on Autism and Victimization

### 1.1 Headline prevalence findings

**Cazalis, Reyes, Leduc & Gourion (2022)** — *Frontiers in Behavioral Neuroscience.*
- Survey of 225 autistic women in France, recruited through the French autistic community.
- 68.9% disclosed sexual violence in response to an open question; **88.4% disclosed when asked with specific, behaviorally anchored questions**.
- Most respondents had been assaulted multiple times; abuse frequently began in childhood; most were unable to report or receive care.
- Compared to the commonly cited 1-in-3 rate for non-autistic women, this represents approximately a threefold elevation in targeting.
- **Evidence quality: [PRELIMINARY]** — Landmark finding with important limitations: (a) online self-selected sample from a community network, which may skew toward those with prior trauma disclosure; (b) single-country; (c) cross-sectional; (d) no non-autistic comparison group recruited in parallel. The open-vs-specific question gap is itself a critical methodological finding: mainstream survey instruments likely undercount abuse in autistic populations because they rely on the respondent spontaneously labeling their experience as "abuse" or "violence."

**Griffiths, Allison, Kenny, Holt, Smith & Baron-Cohen (2019)** — "The Vulnerability Experiences Quotient (VEQ)." *Autism Research.*
- Developed through stakeholder consultation with autistic adults; 60 items across 10 domains; Cronbach's α = 0.89 (autistic) / 0.88 (control).
- Sample: 426 autistic adults with clinical diagnosis; 268 non-autistic controls.
- Autistic adults reported higher rates on the majority of VEQ items compared to controls.
- Highlighted understudied domains: **domestic abuse, contact with social services as parents, financial exploitation and hardship.**
- Group differences in anxiety, depression, and life satisfaction were **partially mediated** by total VEQ score — i.e., the mental health gap between autistic and non-autistic adults is explained in part by the higher rate of negative life events, not just by autism "itself."
- **Evidence quality: [VALIDATED]** for the existence and magnitude of the vulnerability gap; **[PRELIMINARY]** for the specific mediation model.

**Douglas & Sedgewick (2024)** — "Experiences of interpersonal victimization and abuse among autistic people." *Autism* 28(7), 1732–1745.
- Qualitative interview study, 24 autistic adults (6 male, 15 female, 3 non-binary), ages 25–61, semi-structured online interviews.
- **Nearly all participants had repeated experiences of IPV and sexual assault, regardless of gender.**
- Six themes: experiences of abuse; autism used against you; poor family models; impact of/on friendships; handling trauma; recommendations for future practice.
- **The "weaponized features" finding.** Participants described how abusers specifically and deliberately exploited features of autism: sensory sensitivities turned into punishment, special interests used as leverage, literal-mindedness used to rewrite agreements, difficulty with conflict exploited to shut down protest, routine-dependence used to trap, and masking fatigue used to isolate. This was not "autistic people misread situations" — this was *targeted operational knowledge of autism used as a weapon by perpetrators.*
- **Evidence quality: [PRELIMINARY]** — Small qualitative sample, self-selected, but findings converge with other qualitative work. The "weaponized features" framing is a conceptual contribution more than a prevalence estimate — it changes how the pattern should be understood rather than establishing how often it occurs.

**Pearson, Rose & Rees (2022/2023)** — "'I felt like I deserved it because I was autistic': Understanding the impact of interpersonal victimisation in the lives of autistic people." *Autism.*
- Larger qualitative study (over 100 autistic people interviewed across the research program about victimization by family, friends, and romantic/sexual partners).
- Central conceptual contribution: **abuse of autistic people should be framed as "situational vulnerability," not "personal vulnerability."** Being targeted by someone willing to weaponize your neurotype is not a deficit of the autistic person.
- Participants reported internalizing the blame ("I felt like I deserved it because I was autistic"), which maps onto the shame-based mechanisms described in general IPV research but with an autism-specific content layer.
- **Evidence quality: [PRELIMINARY]** — High-quality qualitative research; findings converge across the Pearson/Sedgewick/Douglas research group.

**Pearson, Rose, Mitchell, Joseph, Douglas, Sedgewick & Botha (2024)** — "'It's not a physical prison but you can't get out.' How Autistic Adults Make Sense of the Experience of Intimate Violence and Abuse." *Autism in Adulthood.*
- Extends Pearson 2022 and Douglas 2024 with additional analysis of how autistic survivors *conceptualize* their experiences.
- Key finding: individual, interpersonal, and societal factors **normalize violence against autistic people**, making it harder to recognize one's own experience as abuse and harder to leave.
- **Evidence quality: [PRELIMINARY]** (qualitative, self-selected).

**Cooke, Ridgway, Pecora, Westrupp, Hedley, Hooley & Stokes (2025)** — "Outcomes of Experiencing Interpersonal Violence in Autism: A Mixed Methods Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis." *Trauma, Violence & Abuse.*
- 41 studies (26 quantitative, 9 qualitative, 6 mixed methods); 33,806 participants (11,486 autistic / 22,320 non-autistic).
- Random-effects meta-analysis over 9 studies with 3,647 autistic participants aged 1–80.
- Bullying or combined violence exposure (physical, sexual, psychological) produced worse intra-personal outcomes (white matter disruption, more mental-health conditions, internalizing symptoms, PTSS, suicidality) and worse interpersonal outcomes (behavioral challenges, conduct issues, impulsivity) from childhood onward.
- **Evidence quality: [VALIDATED]** for the general finding that autistic people experience worse outcomes than non-autistic people after comparable violence exposure. The life-course finding is the strongest signal in the literature.

**Prevalence of Victimisation in Autistic Individuals: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis (2023, PMC10486169).**
- Pooled prevalence of victimization: **44%**.
- Subtype rates: bullying 47%, child abuse 16%, sexual victimization 40%, cyberbullying 13%, multiple forms 84%.
- **Evidence quality: [VALIDATED]** for pooled effect; individual study quality varies.

### 1.2 Bullying as the foundational pattern

Bullying in childhood is the earliest measurable form of the pattern that continues into adult manipulation and abuse. It is foundational because the adult dynamic does not come out of nowhere — it is the continuation of a life-course pattern.

- The pooled prevalence of bullying victimization in autistic children is approximately **44–58%** depending on sample, compared to roughly 2% in matched non-autistic samples in one epidemiological study (European Child & Adolescent Psychiatry, 2023).
- Longitudinal work suggests early bullying victimization predicts later internalizing symptoms (Rodriguez et al., 2021).
- Bullying victimization is associated with heightened anxiety and depression among autistic and ADHD youth (National Survey of Children's Health 2016–2020 analysis, 2024).
- **Evidence quality: [VALIDATED]** for elevated prevalence and downstream mental health impact.

**Implication:** Adult content about manipulation and abuse cannot treat autistic survivors as if they are encountering this dynamic for the first time in adulthood. For most, it is the continuation of a pattern first established before they had language for it.

### 1.3 Gender, and the men gap

- Douglas & Sedgewick (2024) found *no gender protection* — autistic men in their sample experienced sexual violence and IPV at rates similar to autistic women and non-binary participants. This is a significant deviation from the general population pattern.
- The **Gender Differences in Prevalence of Autistic Experiences of Interpersonal Violence** systematic review (2025, Springer) confirms gender asymmetries exist but are attenuated in autistic samples.
- **Research gap:** Autistic men's abuse experiences remain significantly understudied. Social scripts around masculinity combined with autism-related communication differences likely create a specific underreporting effect that has not been quantified.
- **Evidence quality: [PRELIMINARY]** for the attenuation-of-gender-protection finding; **[GAP]** for the mechanism.

## Part 2. The Detection Problem

This is the area where community knowledge and empirical literature most need to be carefully distinguished.

### 2.1 Deception detection — what the research actually shows

**Williams, Nicholson, Grainger, Lind & Carruthers (2018)** — "Can you spot a liar? Deception, mindreading, and the case of autism spectrum disorder." *Autism Research.*
- Lie detection was significantly impaired in adults with an ASD diagnosis relative to matched comparison participants.
- Deception-detection ability was also negatively correlated with the number of autistic traits in the general population — i.e., the effect is dimensional, not categorical.
- **Critical nuance:** In neurotypical adults, deception-detection performance was negatively associated with autistic traits *but not with mindreading ability.* This is important — it means the "autistic people can't detect lies because of theory of mind deficits" story is probably wrong or at best incomplete. The mechanism is something else, possibly experiential exposure to social cues of deception.
- **Evidence quality: [PRELIMINARY]** — One well-designed study; the null mindreading-mediation finding is specifically important because it undercuts a common causal story.

**Deceptive behaviour in autism: A scoping review (2022, PMC8814957)** — summarizes that autistic individuals both detect deception less readily and engage in it less frequently and less adeptly. Both sides of the deception axis are reduced.

### 2.2 The "good faith assumption" — empirical vs. folk

The claim that autistic people "assume good faith" is widely circulated in community spaces. The empirical picture is more specific:

- Autistic individuals show **reduced use of facial impressions in trust decisions** — not because they form different impressions, but because they don't weight those impressions the same way in subsequent decisions (Should I trust you? Autistic traits predict reduced appearance-based trust decisions, 2018). This could be interpreted as *more rational* — facial trustworthiness impressions have very limited validity, and weighting them less is arguably correct.
- Atypical dynamic trust learning in individuals with high autistic traits in multi-round trust games (2024): autistic individuals and those with high autistic traits update trust more slowly in response to reputation cues.
- Trust and Deception in Children with Autism Spectrum Disorders (2016): limited social engagement may result in failure to learn social cues indicating deceit.

**What the research does *not* clearly show:** That autistic people have a monolithic "good faith assumption." What it shows is:
1. Slower updating of trust based on social/reputation cues.
2. Less weighting of nonverbal/appearance-based trustworthiness signals.
3. Poorer detection of specific lies, even transparent ones.

These combine into something *functionally similar* to a good-faith assumption, but the mechanism is closer to "the signals most people use for trust calibration are either unavailable, under-weighted, or distrusted as unreliable" than to "autistic people are naively trusting."

- **Evidence quality: [PRELIMINARY]** with multiple converging lines of evidence. The community framing of "good faith assumption" is **[COMMUNITY KNOWLEDGE]** that captures the functional reality but not the precise mechanism.

### 2.3 Pattern recognition as an alternative detection system

**Baron-Cohen's hypersystemizing hypothesis** (2008, and subsequent work including *The Pattern Seekers*, 2020):
- Autistic individuals show an enhanced drive to identify "if-and-then" regularities in input-operation-output systems.
- Pattern recognition works best in systems with lawful, repeatable change.
- Pattern recognition breaks down when systems involve less lawful change — which is precisely the domain of subtle social manipulation.
- **Evidence quality: [PRELIMINARY/CONTESTED]** — hypersystemizing is a well-developed theoretical framework with some empirical support; the "extreme male brain" extension is actively contested. For the pragmatics-safety purpose, the relevant finding is narrower: autistic cognition may detect *repeated patterns across time* better than *single-instance social cues in real time.*

**Rozenkrantz, D'Mello & Gabrieli (2021)** — "Enhanced rationality in autism spectrum disorder." *Trends in Cognitive Sciences.*
- Review paper arguing autistic individuals show judgments that are more objective and decision-making less biased than neurotypical individuals.
- Specific finding on **framing effects:** neurotypical individuals prefer "keep $20 of $50" to "lose $30 of $50" despite identical outcomes; autistic individuals show no such preference.
- Proposed mechanism: reduced interference of emotions and rewards on reasoning.
- **Evidence quality: [PRELIMINARY]** — This is a review, not a single study; the enhanced-rationality claim has been discussed in *Mad in America* and other critical venues; the core framing-effect finding has been replicated.
- **Implication for safety content:** Autistic resistance to framing effects is a *real, research-supported strength* that can be leveraged for self-protection, provided the person is not overloaded to the point where emotional regulation fails.

**Yafai, Verrier & Reidy (2014)** — Child-friendly Asch conformity study.
- Autistic children showed reduced **public conformity** compared to neurotypical peers — they were less likely to agree with an obviously-wrong majority.
- **Important adult caveat:** Research suggests adult autistic conformity patterns may differ from childhood patterns, potentially because adults have learned conformity as a masking/survival strategy.
- **Evidence quality: [PRELIMINARY]** for children; **[GAP]** for adults.

### 2.4 The detection-system reframe

Synthesizing the above: autistic individuals are not "bad at detecting manipulation" in a global sense. They are:

- Poorer at single-instance, real-time, nonverbal-cue-dependent lie detection.
- Slower to update trust based on social-reputation feedback.
- **Better** at detecting logical inconsistencies when they have the pattern across time.
- **Better** at resisting framing manipulations that rely on gain/loss presentation.
- **Better** at resisting Asch-style conformity pressure (in children; less clear in adults).

This supports a **pattern-recognition-over-time approach** to manipulation detection for autistic people, in contrast to the neurotypical-default "trust your gut on first meeting" approach. This reframe is consistent with both empirical findings and community knowledge, though neither source has tested explicit interventions built around it.

- **Evidence quality of the reframe itself: [EXTRAPOLATED]** — The individual findings are empirical; the synthesis into a coherent detection-alternative framework is theoretical.

## Part 3. Vulnerability Factors — What Is Actually Supported

### 3.1 Alexithymia

- Prevalence in autism: ~**49.9%** in a meta-analysis (Investigating alexithymia in autism: Kinnaird et al., 2019, PMC6331035); ranges 50–85% in other estimates. Compared to ~5–10% in the general population.
- **Important nuance:** Alexithymia is *common but not universal* in autism. Nearly 40% of autistic people do not show elevated alexithymia. The "autism = alexithymia" equation is empirically wrong.
- The "alexithymia hypothesis" argues that emotion-recognition difficulties often attributed to autism may actually be better explained by co-occurring alexithymia (Bird et al., and replication in *The Alexithymia Hypothesis Revisited*, 2025).
- **Mechanism of vulnerability:** If you cannot reliably identify your own feelings, you cannot use "something feels wrong" as a warning signal. Internal distress cues that would normally alert a person to exploitation are muted or mislabeled.
- **Evidence quality: [VALIDATED]** for prevalence and for the link to emotion-recognition difficulties; **[EXTRAPOLATED]** for the specific link between alexithymia and increased manipulation vulnerability — this is a clinically plausible and widely accepted extrapolation but the direct empirical study tying alexithymia scores to manipulation victimization is not well developed.

### 3.2 Masking and camouflaging

- Hull et al. (multiple studies, 2018–2021) — masking is associated with generalized anxiety, social anxiety, depression, and suicidality.
- Cage & Troxell-Whitman and related work — autistic people camouflage for employment, education, relationships, and to avoid bullying.
- A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis in Clinical Psychology Review confirmed the mental health cost of camouflaging.
- **Mechanism of vulnerability in the abuse context:**
  1. Masking depletes cognitive and emotional resources, leaving less capacity for threat detection and assertive response.
  2. Masking actively suppresses the natural warning signs an autistic person would send — tension, withdrawal, flat affect — that could prompt a bystander to intervene.
  3. Masking creates a "public self" that the abuser can weaponize against the survivor's private self ("everyone else says you're fine, why are you making a fuss?").
  4. Chronic masking produces identity confusion and reduced access to one's own preferences, which are exactly the self-knowledge assets needed to recognize coercive control.
- **Evidence quality: [VALIDATED]** for the mental health cost; **[EXTRAPOLATED]** for the specific link to manipulation vulnerability, though the conceptual connection is strong.

### 3.3 Memory differences

- Autistic individuals typically show intact or superior semantic memory but **diminished episodic recollection** — particularly for the specific spatial-temporal context and subjective re-experiencing of personal events (Boucher & Mayes, 2012; Exploring the neurocognitive basis of episodic recollection in autism, PMC6424931).
- Adults with autism show heightened PTSD risk associated with cumulative trauma and memory deficits (Haruvi-Lamdan et al., 2020, PMID 33454451).
- **Mechanism of vulnerability in manipulation contexts:**
  1. Gaslighting ("that's not what happened") is harder to resist when episodic memory of the event's context is less vivid.
  2. Pattern-level memory ("this keeps happening") may still be available even when specific-incident memory is fuzzy — this is an asset if the person learns to trust it.
  3. Journaling and external-record strategies are not just "good practice" — for autistic survivors, they may be a direct compensation for a memory architecture that does not naturally retain the evidence needed to counter gaslighting.
- **Evidence quality: [VALIDATED]** for the episodic/semantic asymmetry; **[EXTRAPOLATED]** for the gaslighting-specific implication, but this is a strong extrapolation.

### 3.4 Compliance training and ABA — the contested territory

**Kupferstein (2018)** — "Evidence of increased PTSD symptoms in autistics exposed to applied behavior analysis." *Advances in Autism.*
- Online survey, 460 respondents (autistic adults and caregivers of autistic children).
- Reported 46% of ABA-exposed respondents met the diagnostic threshold for PTSD.
- **Critique (Leaf et al., 2018, Dillenburger, and others):** non-validated survey, leading questions, caregiver reports of private events, conflation of exposure with causation, no control for other adverse experiences.
- **Journal response:** *Advances in Autism* has issued an Expression of Concern regarding the paper.
- **Evidence quality: [CONTESTED]** — This finding cannot be cited as established. However, it cannot be entirely dismissed either: the critique is methodological, not substantive, and the lived experience the paper captures is substantial.

**What can be said more safely:**
- Community reports of trauma responses following intensive behavioral intervention are widespread and consistent (Devon Price's work, ASAN position statements, multiple first-person accounts in peer-reviewed qualitative research).
- The mechanism being described — that compliance training that overrides the child's "no" creates adults who have difficulty recognizing and asserting their own boundaries — is clinically plausible and consistent with the general trauma literature on coercive control in childhood.
- **Evidence quality: [COMMUNITY KNOWLEDGE]** strongly, **[EXTRAPOLATED]** clinically, **[PRELIMINARY AND CONTESTED]** empirically. This area requires the most careful handling in downstream content.

**The safest position for the pragmatics-safety content:** Acknowledge that many autistic adults report difficulty asserting "no" and recognizing personal preferences, link this to a general history of environments that did not respect autistic refusal (which includes but is not limited to formal ABA), and avoid staking the analysis on a contested individual study.

### 3.5 Social isolation and lack of reality-check networks

- VEQ findings show autistic adults experience markedly higher rates of social isolation.
- Pearson et al. (2023) — autistic survivors had few trusted others to reality-check their experiences against.
- **Mechanism:** In non-autistic DV literature, one of the strongest protective factors is having multiple people in one's life who can independently notice something is wrong. Autistic people are more likely to have fewer, denser social connections, and those connections may themselves be other isolated autistic people who do not provide the needed reality-check diversity.
- **Evidence quality: [VALIDATED]** for the isolation prevalence; **[EXTRAPOLATED]** for the specific DV-protective-factor implication.

### 3.6 The credibility discount

**Lim, Young & Brewer (2022)** and surrounding literature.
- Autistic mock-suspects were rated as **more deceptive and less credible** than non-autistic controls by observers.
- Hypothesized autism-typical behaviors (gaze aversion, repetitive movement, literal interpretation, flat affect) *did not individually predict* deception judgments — and interestingly, did not significantly differ in prevalence between diagnostic groups.
- **When a diagnostic label was disclosed, the credibility discount diminished** — mock observers attributed behaviors to autism rather than deception (Kelley's discounting principle).
- Crane, Maras and colleagues have documented similar patterns in witness testimony contexts.
- **Evidence quality: [VALIDATED]** — The credibility discount is one of the more robustly replicated findings in the forensic-autism literature.
- **Implication for safety content:** When autistic survivors attempt to report abuse, they face a structural disadvantage — the very features of how they present may be interpreted against them. This is a real barrier that needs explicit naming, not a "confidence" problem the survivor can fix by trying harder.

## Part 4. Autism-Specific Strengths — What the Research Actually Shows

The field has moved past uncritical strengths-based framing, but several strengths do have empirical support and can legitimately be leveraged in safety content.

### 4.1 Pattern recognition across time

- See §2.3. Pattern recognition is best characterized as a *differential* strength — autistic cognition may be better at detecting patterns across multiple events than neurotypical cognition, especially when the detection is freed from real-time social-cue processing.
- **Evidence quality: [PRELIMINARY]** — The general hypersystemizing framework has support; the specific claim that this operates as a manipulation-detection asset is an extrapolation but a defensible one.

### 4.2 Resistance to framing effects

- Rozenkrantz et al. 2021 and multiple individual studies: autistic individuals show reduced framing bias in economic decisions.
- **Evidence quality: [VALIDATED]** for the narrow framing-effect finding in controlled laboratory contexts; **[EXTRAPOLATED]** for generalization to real-world manipulation resistance.
- **Caveat:** Many framing-effect experiments are conducted in non-emotional, non-time-pressured, non-social conditions. Real-world manipulation weaponizes time pressure, emotional dysregulation, and social threat — all conditions that may erode the framing-resistance advantage. The strength is real but should not be oversold.

### 4.3 Resistance to social conformity pressure

- Yafai et al. (2014) — autistic children less susceptible to Asch-style public conformity.
- **Caveat:** The adult picture is less clear and may be contaminated by learned masking.
- **Evidence quality: [PRELIMINARY]** for children; **[GAP]** for adults.

### 4.4 Justice sensitivity and truth orientation

- Multiple studies and community reports describe heightened justice sensitivity in autistic individuals — a strong aversion to unfairness and deception, combined with firm personal moral codes resistant to peer pressure.
- Philosophical framing: Victoria McGeer's concept of "cognitive empathy of principle."
- **Evidence quality: [PRELIMINARY]** — This construct is well-described and has converging qualitative and some quantitative support, but the measurement tools are less standardized than many other autism constructs. It also overlaps conceptually with cognitive rigidity and the two should not be conflated.
- **Implication:** Strong moral code and aversion to deception can be an asset for maintaining one's own evaluation of a situation against gaslighting, *provided* the person has access to their own assessment and has not been taught through childhood environments to override it.

### 4.5 Honest evaluation over romanticization

- **Community knowledge** rather than a specific empirical finding: autistic adults often describe a tendency toward literal, unflattering self-evaluation of situations. This may reduce susceptibility to some of the denial mechanisms that keep people in abusive relationships.
- **Caveat:** In other cases, the same tendency manifests as self-blame ("it must be my fault because I'm autistic"), which is the opposite effect. Pearson et al. (2023) explicitly document this.
- **Evidence quality: [COMMUNITY KNOWLEDGE]** and mixed.

## Part 5. Where Mainstream Frameworks Fail Autistic People

This section documents specific empirical or community-documented failures of standard DV/manipulation/trauma advice when applied to autistic populations. Every item in this section is a place where downstream content must either adapt or flag the failure.

### 5.1 "Trust your gut"

- **Why it fails:** Alexithymia (§3.1) means many autistic people do not have reliable access to gut feelings as discrete signals. Interoception differences compound this. The "gut" is not broken — it is often present as diffuse physical distress without clear labeling.
- **Adapted approach:** Teach pattern recognition across time, external logging, and the "my body feels a certain way when I'm near this person" observation over multiple encounters, as a *substitute* for real-time gut feelings. This is [COMMUNITY KNOWLEDGE] converging with the [VALIDATED] alexithymia literature.

### 5.2 "Read body language"

- **Why it fails:** Not because autistic people "can't read body language" (this is an overgeneralization the field has moved past) but because real-time multimodal cue integration is more effortful and produces different weights. Williams et al. 2018 is relevant here.
- **Adapted approach:** Rely on *what people do and say over time*, not on their expression and tone in a single moment. Double empathy (Milton) is also relevant: autistic and non-autistic people often misread each other bidirectionally, and a cue-reading failure is not unilaterally an autistic deficit.

### 5.3 "Be assertive"

- **Why it fails:** The credibility-discount research (§3.6) shows that autistic presentation is structurally read as less credible. Hull et al. on masking shows that being "authentically autistic" in public carries real social cost. "Be assertive" advice assumes an audience that will respond to assertion the way they respond to a non-autistic person's assertion, which is empirically not the case.
- **Adapted approach:** Distinguish between assertion *as internal knowledge* (which is a legitimate goal) and assertion *as public performance* (which may not protect and may provoke). Recommend strategies that do not require the audience to correctly interpret the assertion.

### 5.4 "Set clear boundaries"

- **Why it fails:** Autistic communication style (literal, direct, sometimes flat) may not be registered as boundary-setting by non-autistic others and can even be misread as aggression or rudeness. The reverse also occurs: autistic people may set boundaries so subtly (for fear of social cost) that the boundary is not registered.
- **Adapted approach:** Written rather than verbal boundaries; scripted language developed in advance; external witnesses; the recognition that boundary enforcement is not the same as boundary statement.

### 5.5 "Talk to a therapist"

- **Why it fails:** Most therapists, including most DV counselors, have little autism-specific training. The credibility discount applies in clinical settings as well. Standard talking therapies may conflict with autistic communication preferences. Mainstream trauma therapy often assumes verbal processing fluency.
- **Evidence base:** EMDR adaptation research (Fisher, van Diest, Leoni & Spain, 2023) identifies significant adaptations needed — clarity of expectations, sensory considerations, information format individualized to the client, attentional differences, need for extra structure and routine. The same applies to CBT (Kerns and colleagues).
- **Adapted approach:** Direct users toward explicitly autism-informed practitioners, flag what to look for in non-autism-specialist therapists, and be honest that in many locations no such specialist is available — the practical advice has to include "what do you do when the specialist you need doesn't exist in your area."

### 5.6 "Build a support network"

- **Why it fails:** Social difficulties, sensory costs of socializing, masking fatigue, and geographic/logistical factors make neurotypical-pattern support networks expensive to build. Autistic social networks are often denser and smaller, and may be shared with a perpetrator.
- **Adapted approach:** Online autistic community access, written/asynchronous support, explicit acknowledgment that support-network-building is harder and slower for autistic people and is not a moral failure when it is incomplete.

### 5.7 Standard safety planning forms

- **Feasibility and acceptability of autism adapted safety plans: an external pilot randomised controlled trial** (eClinicalMedicine, 2024) — found that standard safety plans are often not tailored to autistic needs; autism-adapted safety plans were feasible and acceptable.
- **Adapting Safety Plans for Autistic Adults with Involvement from the Autism Community** (*Autism in Adulthood*, 2024) — identified specific adaptations: trusted supporter involvement, planning when not in distress, flexibility, use of pictures/symbols, concrete rather than abstract coping strategies.
- **Evidence quality: [PRELIMINARY]** — early pilot RCT, small-scale, but represents the most direct empirical work on adapting a specific safety tool.

### 5.8 Standard trauma therapy approaches

- Rumball et al. (2020) — autistic adults may experience as traumatic a wider range of events than DSM-5 recognizes, including routine changes, sensory overload, rule-breaking by others, and events that don't fit standard PTSD criteria.
- Kerns et al. work — autism-specific trauma presentations often go unrecognized.
- **Evidence quality: [VALIDATED]** for the broader-trauma-trigger finding.

## Part 6. Autism-Specific Trauma Research

### 6.1 PTSD prevalence

**Rumball, Happé & Grey (2020)** — "Experience of Trauma and PTSD Symptoms in Autistic Adults." *Autism Research.*
- 59 autistic adults reporting exposure to traumatic events, online questionnaire.
- **40% current probable PTSD; 61% lifetime probable PTSD** in trauma-exposed autistic adults. (Other reports of the same dataset cite 45% current.)
- Compared to an estimated 8.9% conditional probability in the English general population following trauma exposure.
- **Evidence quality: [PRELIMINARY]** — Small sample, online, no clinician confirmation, self-selected. Rumball and colleagues themselves note these limits.
- **Subsequent work:** Haruvi-Lamdan et al. (2020) and Prosser, Rumball, King & Steel (2026, *Autism*) extend the work with appraisal-based models. A 2024 systematic review (*Review Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*) consolidates the area.

### 6.2 Non-DSM-5 trauma

- Autistic adults report a wider range of events as traumatic, many of which would not qualify under the DSM-5 "Criterion A" stressor definition. These include: sensory events, routine disruption, bullying that does not meet threat-to-life threshold, rule violations by trusted others, and experiences of being disbelieved.
- **Implication:** Many autistic survivors will have PTSD-like presentations from events that mainstream clinicians do not code as traumatic, and will therefore be told they cannot have PTSD or cannot receive trauma-specific treatment.
- **Evidence quality: [VALIDATED]** — Rumball's broader-stressor finding has replicated.

### 6.3 Autistic burnout / trauma overlap

**Raymaker et al. (2020)** — "'Having All of Your Internal Resources Exhausted Beyond Measure and Being Left with No Clean-Up Crew': Defining Autistic Burnout." *Autism in Adulthood.*
- Community-based participatory research; 19 interviews and 19 public online sources.
- Primary characteristics: chronic exhaustion, loss of skills, reduced sensory tolerance.
- Attributed to chronic life stress and mismatch of expectations and abilities without adequate supports.
- **Note the overlap with complex trauma:** Burnout and C-PTSD share many features, and the research does not yet clearly separate them in autistic adults. A person experiencing abuse may present with what looks like "autistic burnout" when the underlying pattern is traumatic stress response.
- **Evidence quality: [PRELIMINARY]** — Raymaker's work is the foundational empirical definition but the burnout/trauma distinction is not yet well established.

### 6.4 Trauma may mask autism, autism may mask trauma

- Late-identified autistic women especially frequently present to mental health services with complex trauma or personality disorder diagnoses that obscure the underlying autism (Skylark Counselling summary; multiple clinical case reports).
- Conversely, trauma responses may be attributed to "autism" and not treated as trauma.
- **Evidence quality: [COMMUNITY KNOWLEDGE]** and **[PRELIMINARY]** case-report literature; the systematic prevalence of this pattern has not been well quantified.

### 6.5 Therapeutic adaptation

- **EMDR:** Fisher, van Diest, Leoni & Spain (2023) — Delphi survey with EMDR therapists; identified specific adaptations. Systematic review in *Autism (SAGE)* (2023) found encouraging PTSD reductions across studies. Evidence: **[PRELIMINARY]**.
- **CBT:** Kerns et al. (2016 and related) — trauma-focused CBT is plausibly adaptable with modifications for autistic sensory, communication, and information-processing differences. Evidence: **[PRELIMINARY]**.
- **No clear evidence-based trauma therapy specifically validated for autistic adults.** This is a critical gap — the best-validated trauma therapies have not been rigorously adapted and tested in autistic populations. What exists is therapist-reported adaptation and small-n qualitative work.

## Part 7. Autistic Community Knowledge

The framework for integrating community knowledge must be explicit: this is experiential knowledge, developed by autistic people about autistic experience, and it frequently runs ahead of the clinical literature. Its status is not inferior to empirical research — it is different, with different strengths and limits, and must be labeled as such so consumers can weigh it appropriately.

### 7.1 Major organizations and their positions

- **Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN)** — US-based 501(c)(3). Early work focused on abuse in schools (aversives, restraint, seclusion). Maintains an Autism & Safety Toolkit. Position statements critical of ABA. Policy briefs accessible free.
- **Autistic Women & Nonbinary Network (AWN)** — Focuses on autistic women, nonbinary, trans, and gender-marginalized autistic people. Mission includes disability justice, gender and racial equity, trans liberation. Maintains a Trans Autistic Healthcare Guide. The "Divergent" initiative explicitly addresses IPV and trauma.
- **NeuroClastic** — Autistic-run publication; publishes experiential and analytical pieces on trauma, abuse, masking, burnout. Not a traditional peer-review venue but has become a significant conduit for community knowledge.
- **Thinking Person's Guide to Autism** — Long-running autistic- and parent-run resource with editorial standards and a track record of accurate community reporting.
- **Participatory Autism Research Collective (PARC)** — Chaired by Damian Milton; bridges academic research and autistic community input. Methodologically relevant because it models how community knowledge is formalized into research.

### 7.2 How to handle community knowledge responsibly

For each piece of community-sourced guidance, the content should:

1. **Label it as experiential rather than empirical.**
2. **Note convergence with research where it exists.** (Example: The community understanding that masking depletes cognitive resources converges with Hull et al. findings.)
3. **Note divergence from clinical recommendations where it exists, and why.** (Example: The community position that ABA harms autistic children is at odds with mainstream behavioral-intervention literature; the divergence is substantive and should be named, not smoothed over.)
4. **Note where community knowledge has driven research priorities.** (Example: Autistic burnout was a community concept before Raymaker's formal definition; the community named the phenomenon first.)
5. **Note where community knowledge is currently ahead of the research literature.** (Example: The "weaponized features" framing was present in autistic survivor communities before it appeared in Douglas & Sedgewick 2024.)

### 7.3 Specific autistic voices and how to use them

- **Damian Milton** — Academic and autistic; double empathy problem (2012, 2022 ten-year review). Research-based. Cite as peer-reviewed.
- **Devon Price** — Social psychologist and autistic; *Unmasking Autism.* Mix of research synthesis and personal advocacy; should be cited as an accessible synthesis rather than primary research, and specific empirical claims should be traced to the underlying studies before use.
- **Dora Raymaker** — Academic and autistic; autistic burnout research. Peer-reviewed primary source.
- **Sarah Hendrickx** — Clinician and autistic; extensive experience assessing autistic women. Her books combine research synthesis and clinical observation; useful as experiential/clinical source, not primary research.
- **Nick Walker** — Academic, autistic, neuroqueer theorist; *Neuroqueer Heresies.* Foundational for the neurodiversity paradigm but framework-level rather than clinical-research.
- **Monique Botha** — Co-author on Pearson et al. 2024; researcher on autistic minority stress. Peer-reviewed primary source.
- **Kieran Rose** — Co-author on multiple Pearson papers; the Autistic Advocate; community-voice and research coauthor.
- **Amy Pearson** — Academic researcher focused on autistic victimization; primary research source.

**Each citation should distinguish whether the person is being cited as empirical researcher, clinical observer, experiential writer, or theorist.** Collapsing these roles produces the kind of false certainty the user has explicitly warned against.

### 7.4 The authority vacuum

There are very few clinicians who are both autism-informed *and* DV/trauma-specialists. The result:

- Most strategies recommended in autistic community spaces are extrapolated from the general DV/trauma literature by autistic people themselves, sometimes with input from sympathetic clinicians.
- Most strategies recommended by non-autistic DV specialists are not adapted for autistic clients.
- The intersection is thin enough that *any* content in this area is working with an incomplete evidence base.

The honest framing is: **we are synthesizing the best available evidence from adjacent fields, autistic community knowledge, and the small but growing autism-specific research base, and we will be explicit about which is which.**

## Part 8. Gaps in the Autism-Specific Research Base

### 8.1 Populations underrepresented or absent

- **Black autistic women and girls:** Across a 77-year scoping review period, only three studies foregrounded Black autistic women and girls; none addressed intersectionality (PMC9745006). The scholarly neglect is severe.
- **Black autistic adults more broadly:** Absent from most research.
- **Autistic men and abuse:** Under-studied despite evidence of comparable prevalence.
- **LGBTQ+ autistic adults:** Some qualitative work (PMC10280209) but quantitative victimization data is limited.
- **Non-speaking autistic adults and AAC users:** Largely absent. The general disability literature shows that people with communication disabilities are 7× more likely to be sexually abused than non-disabled peers; this figure is not autism-specific and likely substantial undercount.
- **Autistic people with co-occurring intellectual disability:** Consistently excluded from autism-specific victimization research.
- **Autistic people in the criminal justice system as survivors:** Documented as 7× more likely to interact with CJS; likely to be victims more than offenders in many cases (Slavny-Cross et al., 2022); PTSD is under-detected and under-treated in this population.

### 8.2 Methodological gaps

- **No large probability-sample prevalence studies.** Almost all autism-victimization research uses convenience samples from community networks.
- **Very little longitudinal work.** Most studies are cross-sectional; we cannot distinguish directional effects cleanly.
- **Almost no intervention research.** We know autistic people are at elevated risk; we know very little about what specifically works to reduce that risk or to recover afterward. The Autism Adapted Safety Plans pilot RCT is a rare exception.
- **No well-validated adapted trauma therapies.** EMDR and CBT adaptations exist in clinician-survey form but have not been tested in adequately-powered RCTs with autistic adults.
- **No autism-specific manipulation detection intervention research.** The entire category of "teaching manipulation detection skills specifically to autistic adults" is essentially uninvestigated.

### 8.3 Conceptual gaps

- The separation between autistic burnout and complex trauma is not well established.
- The mechanism of reduced lie detection is contested — theory-of-mind accounts are probably wrong, but the alternative mechanism is not well specified.
- The boundary between "alexithymia" and "interoceptive differences" in manipulation-vulnerability contexts is unclear.
- The interaction between masking and manipulation vulnerability has not been directly studied, only hypothesized.

## Part 9. Honest Assessment — What We Know vs. What We're Guessing

### What we know (VALIDATED, replicable, generalizable within the sampled populations):

1. Autistic adults experience elevated rates of bullying, IPV, sexual violence, and financial exploitation relative to non-autistic adults. The magnitude is large.
2. The mental health gap between autistic and non-autistic adults is partly mediated by differential exposure to adverse life events.
3. Autistic children are bullied at dramatically higher rates than non-autistic children.
4. Autistic adults suffer worse mental health outcomes from comparable violence exposure.
5. Alexithymia is significantly more common in autism (~50%) but is not universal.
6. Masking is associated with anxiety, depression, and suicidality.
7. Episodic memory shows relative differences while semantic memory is relatively intact.
8. Autistic individuals show reduced framing effects in economic decision tasks.
9. Autistic individuals face a credibility discount from observers that is structural, not behavior-specific.
10. Autistic individuals show elevated PTSD rates following trauma exposure and report a broader range of events as traumatic.

### What is preliminary but probably real (PRELIMINARY, converging evidence):

1. Autistic adults have worse single-instance lie detection but this is probably not mediated by theory-of-mind deficits.
2. The "weaponized features" pattern — abusers specifically exploiting autistic traits as a technique — is real and reported across qualitative studies.
3. Autism-adapted safety plans are feasible and acceptable, though effectiveness remains to be established.
4. Resistance to public conformity pressure is present in autistic children; less certain in adults.
5. Justice sensitivity and truth orientation exist as real constructs but overlap with rigidity and are not sharply measured.
6. Cazalis et al.'s 88.4% figure is directionally correct about elevated sexual violence exposure in autistic women even if the exact magnitude depends on sample.

### What is extrapolation (EXTRAPOLATED, plausibly reasoned from adjacent evidence):

1. The link between alexithymia and specific manipulation vulnerability.
2. The link between episodic memory differences and vulnerability to gaslighting.
3. The link between masking and reduced threat detection.
4. The mechanism connecting childhood compliance training to adult boundary difficulties.
5. The claim that pattern recognition over time is an effective substitute for real-time cue detection.
6. The generalization from laboratory framing-effect resistance to real-world manipulation resistance.

### What is community knowledge running ahead of research (COMMUNITY KNOWLEDGE):

1. The functional reality of the "good faith assumption" even if the mechanism is imprecisely described.
2. The widespread experience of trauma responses following compliance-based interventions.
3. The mutual-invisibility problem between autism and trauma in clinical settings.
4. Practical strategies for manipulation detection that autistic adults have developed and shared, which have not been formally tested.
5. The framework of "situational vulnerability" rather than "personal vulnerability" — this was community-developed before it entered the research literature through Pearson et al.

### What is contested (CONTESTED, do not cite as established):

1. The Kupferstein 2018 ABA-PTSD finding in its specific statistical form. The underlying phenomenon being described may be real, but this paper is not the place to hang the claim.
2. The extreme-male-brain extension of the hypersystemizing hypothesis.
3. Generalizations from the Cazalis 88.4% figure to all autistic women in all contexts.

### What we are genuinely guessing (GAPS, use caution):

1. Effective interventions specifically designed for autistic survivors.
2. Manipulation-detection training adapted for autistic cognition.
3. What works for non-speaking autistic adults, autistic people with intellectual disability, autistic people in the CJS.
4. The experience and effective strategies for autistic men, autistic people of color, LGBTQ+ autistic people at the intersection of multiple marginalizations.
5. Whether adapted EMDR/CBT actually work — they are plausible but not adequately tested.

## Part 10. Implications for Downstream Pragmatics Safety Content

Based on this foundation, downstream content should:

1. **Label every claim by evidence quality.** Never present community knowledge as empirical, never present extrapolation as validated.
2. **Treat the credibility discount as a structural fact, not a confidence problem.** Safety strategies must account for the fact that autistic presentation is read against the survivor.
3. **Teach pattern-recognition-over-time as the primary detection system**, not as a backup for "trust your gut." The "gut" instruction fails under alexithymia and should not be the foundation.
4. **Use written/external records as the primary compensation strategy for gaslighting** — not because it is "good practice" but because episodic memory differences make this a mechanical necessity, not a self-improvement nicety.
5. **Distinguish assertion-as-internal-knowledge from assertion-as-public-performance.** Only the first is reliably achievable; the second depends on the audience's interpretation, which is not under the survivor's control.
6. **Name the authority vacuum.** Be explicit that few autism-informed DV/trauma clinicians exist and that practical advice has to account for this.
7. **Use the "weaponized features" framing and the "situational vulnerability" framing.** These come from the primary research and from autistic community theorizing, and they correctly place responsibility on perpetrators and on the environment, not on the autistic person's neurology.
8. **Link to autistic-led organizations (ASAN, AWN, NeuroClastic) while noting the limits of their evidence base.** They are trusted sources of community knowledge; they are not substitutes for empirical research, and neither substitutes for the other.
9. **Never present standard DV safety planning or standard trauma therapy without the autism adaptations.** The adaptation literature exists, is modest, and must be drawn on.
10. **Flag the populations for whom we have essentially no research** — non-speaking autistic people, autistic people with intellectual disability, autistic people of color, LGBTQ+ autistic people at intersections. Where we do not have evidence, the content must say so.
