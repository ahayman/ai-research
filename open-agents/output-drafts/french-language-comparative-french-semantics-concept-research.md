# Concept Research: French Semantics — How French Encodes Meaning in Contrast with English

## Overview

The semantic relationship between French and English is unique among the world's language pairs. Approximately **~1,700 French-English words are near-identical cognates** — words that are virtually the same in both spelling and meaning (information, table, art, culture, nation, important, possible, communication, distance, silence). Beyond these near-identical twins, the broader influence of French on English means that approximately **~30% of all English vocabulary is of French or Norman French origin** — a massive borrowing that occurred primarily after the Norman Conquest of 1066 and continued through centuries of cultural contact. This creates a paradoxical situation: the two languages share an enormous amount of vocabulary (making them mutually accessible at the word level), but they organize and deploy that vocabulary according to fundamentally different semantic principles (making them deceptively different at the meaning level).

The shared vocabulary is deceptive because of **faux amis** (false friends) — words that look identical in both languages but mean different things. "Attendre" looks like "attend" but means "to wait." "Assister" looks like "assist" but means "to attend/be present at." "Actuellement" looks like "actually" but means "currently." "Bras" looks like "bras" but means "arm." These false friends are the treacherous flip side of the cognate advantage — the same historical relationship that created ~1,700 genuine cognates also created hundreds of meaning divergences where the same Latin root evolved in different semantic directions in the two languages.

Beyond vocabulary sharing, French encodes meaning differently from English in several structural domains. French makes a **savoir/connaître distinction** (knowing facts vs. knowing by acquaintance) where English uses a single verb "know." French uses **avoir (to have) for states** that English expresses with "to be" — "j'ai faim" (I have hunger) vs. "I am hungry," "j'ai 30 ans" (I have 30 years) vs. "I am 30 years old." French maintains a **tu/vous register distinction** that English lost when "thou" disappeared by the mid-17th century. And most strikingly, French uses a **vigesimal (base-20) number system** for numbers 70-99 — "soixante-dix" (sixty-ten = 70), "quatre-vingts" (four-twenties = 80), "quatre-vingt-dix" (four-twenty-ten = 90) — a Celtic (Gaulish) substrate feature that survived the Romanization of Gaul and has no parallel in English or indeed in most other Romance languages.

The register asymmetry between French and English is particularly striking. English's three-layer lexicon (Germanic everyday / French formal / Latin technical) means that many "formal" English words ARE French: "commence" (French commencer), "assist" (French assister), "obtain" (French obtenir). When an English speaker reaches for a formal register, they are often unconsciously reaching for French-origin vocabulary. French, by contrast, does not have this layered system in the same way — French formal register is achieved through syntax, verb mood (subjunctive), and pronoun choice (vous), not primarily through vocabulary layering. The irony is that English borrowed French words to CREATE a formality system, while French itself uses different tools for the same purpose.

For a pattern-oriented learner, French semantics offers particularly satisfying analysis because the cognate system is SYSTEMATIC (predictable sound correspondences: English -tion = French -tion, English -ty = French -té, English -ous = French -eux/-euse), the faux amis follow identifiable patterns of semantic drift, and the structural meaning differences (avoir-states, savoir/connaître, tu/vous) reflect deep conceptual organizing principles that illuminate both languages.

## Core Components

### Component 1: The Cognate Treasure — ~1,700 Near-Identical Words + ~30% French-Origin English Vocabulary

- **Purpose**: Provide a massive shared vocabulary base between the two languages, creating mutual accessibility
- **Structure**:

  **Near-identical cognates (~1,700):**
  These are words with virtually identical spelling and meaning in both languages:
  - Abstract nouns: information, communication, situation, nation, tradition, attention, opinion, education, population, administration
  - Adjectives: important, possible, probable, certain, different, apparent, evident, normal, special
  - Nouns: art, table, culture, nature, distance, silence, violence, patience, intelligence
  - Many require only minor pronunciation adjustment — the spelling is often identical

  **Broader French-origin English vocabulary (~30% of English dictionary entries):**
  - Government and law: parliament, justice, judge, court, prison, crime, attorney, plaintiff
  - Military: army, battle, siege, lieutenant, sergeant, infantry, cavalry, artillery
  - Cuisine: beef (bœuf), pork (porc), veal (veau), poultry (pouaille), sauce, menu, chef
  - Fashion: dress, gown, fashion, style, elegant, chic, boutique
  - Arts: music, dance, art, literature, sculpture, theatre, poem
  - Social life: marriage, romance, courtship, adventure, pleasure, leisure

  **The Norman Conquest effect (1066):**
  - English was spoken by the common people; Norman French by the ruling class
  - This created the famous meat/animal split: English names for live animals (cow, pig, sheep — Germanic) vs. English names for their meat on the table (beef, pork, mutton — French) — the peasants raised them, the nobility ate them
  - The flood of French borrowing peaked in the 13th-14th centuries

  **Predictable sound correspondences (systematic cognate patterns):**
  - English -tion = French -tion: nation/nation, station/station
  - English -ty = French -té: liberty/liberté, reality/réalité, university/université
  - English -ous = French -eux/-euse: generous/généreux, curious/curieux
  - English -ment = French -ment: government/gouvernement, treatment/traitement
  - English -ence/-ance = French -ence/-ance: patience/patience, importance/importance
  - English -al = French -al/-el: national/national, professional/professionnel

  **Gaulish substrate vocabulary in French:**
  - Between 70 and 400 words of Gaulish (Celtic) origin survive in modern French, depending on how broadly one counts dialectal and derived words
  - Core Gaulish survivors (~150-180 commonly cited): "chemin" (path), "char" (cart), "bouleau" (birch), "ruche" (beehive), "tonneau" (barrel), "chêne" (oak), "bruyère" (heather), "lande" (heath/moor)
  - These words are concentrated in domains of rural life: agriculture, topography, flora, fauna
  - The vigesimal number system (see Component 6) is the most prominent structural Gaulish survival
  - English, by contrast, has virtually zero Celtic substrate vocabulary — the Anglo-Saxons displaced rather than assimilated the Celtic Britons

- **ENGLISH COMPARISON**: The cognate relationship is asymmetric. French-origin words entered English and became part of a THREE-LAYER register system (Germanic base + French formal + Latin technical). In French, these same words are just... French. The word "important" is everyday French; in English, it sits in the Latinate/French formal layer above the Germanic "big/major." English speakers learning French have an enormous head start on vocabulary — but must recalibrate register expectations, since words that feel "fancy" in English are often neutral in French

### Component 2: Faux Amis — False Friends

- **Purpose**: Identify and catalog the systematic meaning divergences between French and English cognates
- **Structure**:

  **High-frequency false friends (most dangerous for learners):**
  - **attendre** (F) = to wait ≠ attend (E) — "J'attends le bus" = I'm waiting for the bus
  - **assister** (F) = to attend/be present ≠ assist (E) — "J'ai assisté à la conférence" = I attended the conference
  - **actuellement** (F) = currently ≠ actually (E) — "Il travaille actuellement" = He currently works
  - **blesser** (F) = to injure ≠ bless (E) — "Il s'est blessé" = He injured himself
  - **librairie** (F) = bookshop ≠ library (E) — "la librairie du coin" = the corner bookshop
  - **regarder** (F) = to look at / watch ≠ regard (E) — "Je regarde la télé" = I'm watching TV
  - **sensible** (F) = sensitive ≠ sensible (E) — "Elle est très sensible" = She is very sensitive
  - **préservatif** (F) = condom ≠ preservative (E) — famously embarrassing
  - **journée** (F) = daytime/full day ≠ journey (E)
  - **monnaie** (F) = change/currency ≠ money (E)

  **Semi-false friends (overlapping but not identical meanings):**
  - **demander** = to ask (not "to demand" — which is "exiger")
  - **rester** = to stay (not "to rest" — which is "se reposer")
  - **réaliser** = traditionally "to achieve/carry out" (the English meaning "to realize/understand" is gaining ground)
  - **sympathique** = nice/likeable (not "sympathetic" — which is "compatissant")
  - **formidable** = wonderful/tremendous (not "fearsome" as in English)

  **Pattern of semantic drift:**
  - Many false friends show a pattern where French retained a meaning closer to the original Latin root while English shifted to a more specialized or metaphorical meaning
  - "Attendre" ← Latin "attendere" (to stretch toward, give attention to) — French kept the "waiting/attending" sense; English shifted to "being present at"
  - "Assister" ← Latin "assistere" (to stand by) — French kept the "standing by/being present" sense; English shifted to "helping"

- **ENGLISH COMPARISON**: False friends are a CONSEQUENCE of the massive vocabulary sharing. If two languages had no cognates, there would be no false friends. The ~1,700 true cognates and ~30% French-origin English vocabulary mean that the cognate strategy USUALLY works — but when it fails (the ~200-300 false friends), it fails dramatically. The pattern-recognition approach works well here: learning the systematic sound correspondences and then flagging the exceptions

### Component 3: The Savoir/Connaître Distinction

- **Purpose**: Encode a conceptual distinction between propositional knowledge and experiential familiarity that English collapses into a single verb
- **Structure**:

  **Savoir — knowing facts, information, skills ("knowing that" / "knowing how"):**
  - "Je sais que Paris est la capitale" (I know that Paris is the capital) — propositional
  - "Je sais nager" (I know how to swim) — ability/skill
  - "Je sais la réponse" (I know the answer) — factual knowledge
  - "Tu sais où il habite?" (Do you know where he lives?) — embedded question
  - ALWAYS followed by: que (that-clause), interrogative word (where/when/how), infinitive, or specific fact

  **Connaître — knowing by acquaintance, familiarity ("being familiar with"):**
  - "Je connais Marie" (I know Marie) — personal acquaintance
  - "Je connais Paris" (I know Paris) — experiential familiarity
  - "Je connais cette chanson" (I know this song) — familiarity through experience
  - "Tu connais ce restaurant?" (Do you know this restaurant?) — have you been there?
  - ALWAYS followed by a noun (never a clause or infinitive)

  **The conceptual difference:**
  - Savoir = knowledge through learning, information, or reasoning (epistemic)
  - Connaître = knowledge through direct experience, personal contact, acquaintance (experiential)
  - "Je sais qu'il est gentil" (I know [factually] that he is nice) vs. "Je le connais" (I know him [personally])

  **Passé composé changes meaning:**
  - "Je connaissais Marie" (I knew Marie — ongoing familiarity) vs. "J'ai connu Marie" (I met Marie — the moment of becoming acquainted)
  - "Je savais la réponse" (I knew the answer — state) vs. "J'ai su la réponse" (I found out the answer — moment of discovery)

- **ENGLISH COMPARISON**: English has a SINGLE verb "know" that covers both domains. "I know Paris" (acquaintance), "I know the answer" (fact), "I know how to swim" (skill) — all "know." The savoir/connaître split exists in many European languages (German wissen/kennen, Spanish saber/conocer, Italian sapere/conoscere) — English is unusual in LACKING this distinction. For pattern-oriented learners, the rule is simple: connaître + NOUN (always); savoir + CLAUSE/INFINITIVE/FACT (always). There is virtually no overlap

### Component 4: Avoir-State Expressions

- **Purpose**: Encode physical and psychological states using "avoir" (to have) + noun where English uses "to be" + adjective
- **Structure**:

  **The core avoir-state expressions:**
  - **avoir faim** (to have hunger) = to be hungry — "J'ai faim"
  - **avoir soif** (to have thirst) = to be thirsty — "J'ai soif"
  - **avoir chaud** (to have hot) = to be hot — "J'ai chaud"
  - **avoir froid** (to have cold) = to be cold — "J'ai froid"
  - **avoir peur** (to have fear) = to be afraid — "J'ai peur"
  - **avoir raison** (to have reason) = to be right — "Tu as raison"
  - **avoir tort** (to have wrong) = to be wrong — "Il a tort"
  - **avoir sommeil** (to have sleep) = to be sleepy — "J'ai sommeil"
  - **avoir honte** (to have shame) = to be ashamed — "Elle a honte"
  - **avoir besoin de** (to have need of) = to need — "J'ai besoin d'aide"
  - **avoir envie de** (to have desire of) = to want/feel like — "J'ai envie de dormir"

  **Age expression — avoir + years:**
  - "J'ai trente ans" (I have thirty years) = I am thirty years old
  - English: "I am thirty" (be + number)
  - French: "J'ai trente ans" (have + number + years)
  - NEVER: *"Je suis trente ans" (this is grammatically impossible in French)

  **The conceptual difference:**
  - English conceptualizes these states as PROPERTIES of the person (you ARE hungry — an attribute)
  - French conceptualizes them as THINGS the person possesses (you HAVE hunger — a possession)
  - This reflects different cognitive framing: English = adjectival states; French = nominal possessions
  - The pattern extends: "avoir l'air" (to have the air = to seem), "avoir lieu" (to have place = to take place), "avoir l'habitude de" (to have the habit of = to be used to)

  **Danger zone — "être chaud":**
  - "J'ai chaud" = I am hot (temperature) — CORRECT
  - "Je suis chaud" = I am sexually aroused (slang) — VERY DIFFERENT MEANING
  - This error is one of the most famous and embarrassing for English speakers

- **ENGLISH COMPARISON**: English uses "be" + adjective for virtually all states: I am hungry, thirsty, hot, cold, afraid, right, wrong, sleepy, ashamed. French uses "avoir" + noun for the same states. This is not just a vocabulary difference — it reveals a different conceptual metaphor: English = STATES ARE PROPERTIES; French = STATES ARE POSSESSIONS. The pattern is inherited from Latin (which also used "habere" for these states), and exists across Romance languages (Spanish "tener hambre," Italian "avere fame")

### Component 5: The Tu/Vous Register System

- **Purpose**: Encode social distance, formality, and relationship type through a pronoun-based register system
- **Structure**:

  **Tu — familiar singular:**
  - Used with: family, close friends, children, peers (in most modern contexts), God (in prayer)
  - Verb form: 2nd person singular: "tu parles" (you speak), "tu viens" (you come)
  - Signals: intimacy, equality, solidarity, informality

  **Vous — formal singular AND all plurals:**
  - Formal singular: "Vous parlez, Monsieur?" (Do you speak, Sir?)
  - Plural (any register): "Vous venez tous?" (Are you all coming?)
  - Used with: strangers, elders, authority figures, in professional contexts, in formal situations
  - Signals: respect, distance, formality, deference

  **The "tutoiement" / "vouvoiement" social negotiation:**
  - Switching from vous to tu ("tutoyer") marks a shift in relationship — often explicitly negotiated: "On peut se tutoyer?" (Can we use tu with each other?)
  - Using tu inappropriately can be perceived as rude, overly familiar, or disrespectful
  - Using vous with someone who expects tu can feel cold or distancing
  - The "correct" choice varies by: age, social status, workplace culture, region, and relationship history

  **Historical parallel — English "thou" and "you":**
  - English ONCE had exactly this system: "thou" (familiar singular) vs. "you" (formal singular / plural)
  - "Thou" began declining in the 16th century and was largely obsolete in standard English by the mid-17th century
  - The loss of "thou" is one of the most significant simplifications in English social linguistics
  - French PRESERVED the distinction; English LOST it — creating a permanent structural gap

- **ENGLISH COMPARISON**: Modern English has ONLY "you" — one form for all registers, all relationships, singular and plural. English compensates through: lexical choices ("Would you be so kind..." vs. "Hey, can you..."), title usage ("Mr. President" vs. first names), and discourse-level politeness strategies. But English cannot encode the tu/vous distinction grammatically — it must do it pragmatically. This is a case where French GRAMMATICALIZES a social distinction that English leaves to pragmatics. English lost "thou" by the mid-17th century; French shows no sign of losing tu/vous

### Component 6: The Vigesimal Number System

- **Purpose**: Encode numbers 70-99 using a base-20 (vigesimal) system rather than the base-10 system used for 1-69
- **Structure**:

  **Standard French numbers 70-99:**
  - **70** = soixante-dix (sixty-ten)
  - **71** = soixante-et-onze (sixty-and-eleven)
  - **72** = soixante-douze (sixty-twelve)
  - ...
  - **79** = soixante-dix-neuf (sixty-ten-nine)
  - **80** = quatre-vingts (four-twenties)
  - **81** = quatre-vingt-un (four-twenty-one)
  - ...
  - **90** = quatre-vingt-dix (four-twenty-ten)
  - **91** = quatre-vingt-onze (four-twenty-eleven)
  - ...
  - **99** = quatre-vingt-dix-neuf (four-twenty-ten-nine)

  **Mathematical decoding:**
  - 70 = 60 + 10
  - 80 = 4 × 20
  - 90 = 4 × 20 + 10
  - 99 = 4 × 20 + 10 + 9

  **Historical origin — Gaulish (Celtic) substrate:**
  - The Gauls (Celtic inhabitants of pre-Roman France) used a base-20 counting system
  - When Latin replaced Gaulish as the spoken language of Gaul (1st century BCE onward), the base-10 Latin system was adopted for most numbers
  - But the base-20 system survived in the 70-99 range — a remarkable example of substrate influence persisting for over 2,000 years
  - Other Celtic languages also use base-20: Welsh, Breton, Irish
  - The "15" system (quinze-vingts = 15 × 20 = 300, as in the Paris hospital "Quinze-Vingts") preserves a more extensive vigesimal usage from medieval French

  **Regional alternatives (base-10 throughout):**
  - **Belgium and Switzerland**: septante (70), huitante/octante (80, Swiss only), nonante (90)
  - **Quebec**: uses standard French 70-99 (vigesimal)
  - **Democratic Republic of Congo**: septante, octante, nonante
  - These regional forms show that French CAN be purely base-10 — the vigesimal system is specifically a Metropolitan French feature

  **Cognitive load:**
  - "quatre-vingt-dix-sept" (4 × 20 + 10 + 7 = 97) requires two arithmetic operations to decode
  - English "ninety-seven" (90 + 7) requires one
  - Belgian "nonante-sept" also requires one
  - Research suggests the vigesimal system creates measurable delays in number processing for French speakers compared to speakers of purely base-10 languages

- **ENGLISH COMPARISON**: English uses a purely base-10 system for all numbers: seventy (7 × 10), eighty (8 × 10), ninety (9 × 10). The French vigesimal system has absolutely no parallel in English. It represents one of the most visible traces of the pre-Roman Celtic (Gaulish) substrate in French — a linguistic fossil from a language that died over 1,500 years ago but left this structural imprint. English's own Celtic substrate (from the Brittonic Celtic inhabitants of England) left virtually zero structural traces — one of the great unsolved puzzles of historical linguistics

### Component 7: Register Asymmetry — The Mirror Effect

- **Purpose**: Explain why words that feel "formal" in English feel "neutral" in French, and vice versa
- **Structure**:

  **English's three-layer register system (built from French borrowing):**
  - Germanic (everyday): ask, help, buy, fire, begin, end, rise, fall
  - French (formal): question, assist, purchase, flame, commence, conclude, ascend, decline
  - Latin (technical): interrogate, facilitate, procure, conflagration, initiate, terminate, escalate, diminish

  **French has NO equivalent layering:**
  - French has one primary vocabulary layer (evolved from Latin through Vulgar Latin → Old French → Modern French)
  - "Commencer" is not "formal French" — it is EVERYDAY French for "to begin"
  - "Assister" is not "fancy" — it is the standard verb for "to attend/be present"
  - French register is controlled through: syntax (subjunctive), pronouns (tu/vous), and discourse style, not primarily through vocabulary choice

  **The mirror effect:**
  - When an English speaker says "commence" instead of "begin," they are signaling formality through a French-origin word
  - When they translate back to French, "commencer" is the ONLY available word — there is no Germanic alternative
  - English: begin (neutral) → commence (formal) → initiate (technical)
  - French: commencer (all registers) — no layering for this concept

  **Register markers in French (non-lexical):**
  - Tu vs. vous (pronoun choice)
  - Ne retention vs. ne-drop (negation)
  - Passé simple vs. passé composé (tense choice)
  - Subjunctive usage (mood choice)
  - "On" (informal "we") vs. "nous" (formal "we")
  - Sentence length and complexity

  **Consequence for learners:**
  - English speakers often sound "too formal" in French because they choose Latinate/French-origin English words and assume they carry the same elevated register
  - "Obtain" in English is formal; "obtenir" in French is completely neutral
  - "Assist" in English means to help (formal); "assister" in French means to attend (neutral)

- **ENGLISH COMPARISON**: English is UNIQUE among European languages in having a vocabulary that systematically encodes register through etymological layers. This system exists precisely BECAUSE English borrowed so heavily from French. French itself does not need this system — it was the SOURCE of the borrowing, not the recipient. The register asymmetry means that the cognate advantage is partially misleading: the WORDS match, but their REGISTER POSITIONS do not

## How It Works

### Step 1: Lexical Access and Cognate Recognition
When encountering a French word, speakers (especially English-speaking learners) automatically check for cognate matches. Near-identical cognates are recognized immediately; false friends may trigger incorrect initial interpretations.
- Trigger: Word encountered in French
- Action: Cross-linguistic comparison activated; cognate status assessed
- Result: Correct mapping (for true cognates) or interference (for false friends)

### Step 2: Semantic Category Selection
For certain meaning domains, French requires a finer-grained distinction than English: savoir vs. connaître, être vs. avoir for states, tu vs. vous for address.
- Trigger: Need to express "knowing," "being in a state," or "addressing someone"
- Action: Select the appropriate French verb/pronoun based on semantic subcategory
- Result: Correct: "je sais" (I know-fact) vs. "je connais" (I know-person)

### Step 3: Register Calibration
The shared vocabulary requires register recalibration. Words that carry formal connotations in English may be register-neutral in French, and vice versa.
- Trigger: Choosing vocabulary for appropriate register
- Action: Assess word's register position in French (often different from English)
- Result: Appropriate register: "commencer" (neutral in French, formal-register in English)

### Step 4: Numerical Computation (for 70-99)
When expressing numbers in the 70-99 range, speakers must perform arithmetic decomposition using the vigesimal system.
- Trigger: Need to express a number between 70 and 99
- Action: Decompose number: 70-79 = 60 + (10-19); 80-89 = 4 × 20 + (0-9); 90-99 = 4 × 20 + (10-19)
- Result: "quatre-vingt-dix-sept" for 97 (vs. English's direct "ninety-seven")

## Key Facts and Statistics

- ~1,700 French-English near-identical cognates (same spelling and meaning)
- ~30% of English dictionary entries are of French or Norman French origin
- By token frequency, Germanic words dominate English: the 100 most frequent English words are almost all Germanic
- ~200-300 faux amis (false friends) create systematic interference between the two languages
- French savoir/connaître distinction exists in German (wissen/kennen), Spanish (saber/conocer), but NOT in English
- English lost "thou" (familiar singular pronoun) by the mid-17th century; French retains tu/vous
- French vigesimal system (70-99) derives from Gaulish Celtic base-20 counting — a substrate feature over 2,000 years old
- Gaulish substrate words in French: estimates range from 70 to 400, depending on how broadly dialectal and derived words are counted; ~150-180 commonly cited
- Belgian/Swiss French uses base-10: septante (70), huitante/octante (80), nonante (90)
- The avoir-state construction (j'ai faim = I am hungry) exists across all Romance languages — not specific to French
- English's three-layer register system (Germanic/French/Latin) is unique among European languages and exists precisely because of French borrowing
- The Norman Conquest (1066) is the single most important event in English-French linguistic contact

## Research Notes

- The "~1,700 near-identical cognates" figure refers to words that are virtually the same in spelling and meaning; the broader count of all cognates (including partial matches) is much larger
- The "~30% French-origin" figure for English vocabulary refers to dictionary entries (types), not word frequency (tokens); by frequency, Germanic words dominate English text
- The vigesimal number system creates measurable cognitive processing costs: studies show French speakers take longer to process two-digit numbers in the 70-99 range compared to Belgian French speakers using septante/nonante — this is one of the clearest demonstrations of how linguistic structure affects cognitive processing
- The Gaulish substrate word count is contested because attribution is often uncertain — some words attributed to Gaulish may have other origins, and dialectal words inflate the count. The range of 70-400 reflects genuine scholarly disagreement, not vagueness
- The tu/vous system is undergoing gradual shift: many French workplaces that used to require vous now accept tu, especially in tech and startup culture. But the system shows no sign of collapsing the way English "thou" did — the social stakes remain high
