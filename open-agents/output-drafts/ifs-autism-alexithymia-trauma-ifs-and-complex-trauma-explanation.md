# IFS and Complex Trauma: Dissociation, Structural Dissociation, and Comparisons to EMDR, SE, and Ego State

## Why this is IFS's strongest territory — and where the asterisks live

Complex trauma is where IFS makes its strongest claims and where its sharpest limits are most visible at the same time. The model originated partly in trauma work — Schwartz's bulimia outcome study at the Institute for Juvenile Research surfaced clients describing autonomous "wounded children" buried under protective patterns, and that observation became the prototype for what IFS calls *exiles*. The exile construct maps reasonably well onto what trauma clinicians have observed for over a century: traumatized people often present as if multiple discrete selves are running with conflicting agendas, and the wounded one carries memory and affect that the rest of the system has organized to avoid.

Most published IFS pilot evidence concentrates on PTSD and complex trauma. Bessel van der Kolk's *The Body Keeps the Score* (2014) features IFS in a dedicated chapter. Frank Anderson's *Transcending Trauma: Healing Complex PTSD with Internal Family Systems* (PESI, 2021) is the most clinically extended IFS-canonical protocol for complex trauma. Janina Fisher's *Healing the Fragmented Selves of Trauma Survivors* (Routledge, 2017) integrates IFS with structural dissociation theory and sensorimotor psychotherapy under the framework she calls TIST. The trauma-modality industry has, in significant part, organized around IFS as a primary or partner modality.

But IFS does not exist alone. The trauma field already had structural dissociation theory (Van der Hart, Nijenhuis, Steele 2006), Ego State Therapy (Watkins & Watkins, 1980s), Sensorimotor Psychotherapy (Pat Ogden, 2000s), Somatic Experiencing (Peter Levine, 1990s), and EMDR (Francine Shapiro, 1989) before IFS gained popular traction. Each has its own account of what gets fragmented in trauma, what restores integration, and what role the body plays. The honest question for a complex-trauma reader is rarely "IFS yes/no." It is *where does IFS fit in the sequencing, and what stabilization or processing modality precedes or accompanies it?*

This page maps that comparison space, names where pure IFS reaches limits, and gives a clinical orientation that can travel into conversations with your therapist about modality choice. It does not pick a winner. It does not oversell IFS. It does not dismiss IFS. It tries to give you the actual shape of the trauma-treatment landscape so that you can make an informed reading of what you and your clinician are choosing.

## What the diagnoses actually mean — PTSD, C-PTSD, and dissociative disorders

To talk seriously about modality fit, the diagnostic architecture has to be in place first. The trauma-disorder space has three substantively different territories that get blurred in popular language.

**DSM-5 PTSD** is the symptom-criterion-based diagnosis you'll see in most American clinical settings. Four symptom clusters: intrusion (flashbacks, nightmares, intrusive memories), avoidance (of trauma-related cues), negative alterations in cognitions and mood, and hyperarousal (hypervigilance, sleep disturbance, exaggerated startle). DSM-5 includes a *dissociative subtype* of PTSD where depersonalization and derealization are prominent, but it does not formally recognize Complex PTSD as a separate diagnosis. C-PTSD symptoms are partially absorbed into the broader DSM-5 PTSD category and partly into other diagnoses.

**ICD-11 Complex PTSD** does formally recognize C-PTSD. It is the diagnosis the World Health Organization uses internationally. The structure: core PTSD symptoms (re-experiencing, avoidance, sense of current threat) PLUS three additional symptom clusters known together as **Disturbances in Self-Organization (DSO)**: affective dysregulation (chronic emotional storms, emotional numbing), negative self-concept (persistent shame, worthlessness, defeat), and disturbances in relationships (difficulty sustaining intimacy, feeling distant from others). C-PTSD generally results from prolonged or repeated trauma from which escape was difficult or impossible — childhood abuse, captivity, organized violence, ongoing relational trauma. It is the cleanest formal recognition of what trauma clinicians have called complex PTSD for decades.

**Dissociative disorders** are a separate territory. Dissociative Identity Disorder (DID, formerly multiple personality) involves two or more distinct personality states with their own names, ages, histories, and amnestic barriers between them — meaning the host is partly or fully unaware of what other alters do. Other Specified Dissociative Disorder (OSDD-1, formerly DDNOS-1) has many DID features but lacks one or more diagnostic criteria, often the full distinctness of alters or the amnesia. Depersonalization-Derealization Disorder is chronic experience of self or world as unreal. These are not the same as PTSD or C-PTSD; they are their own diagnostic category.

The reason this taxonomy matters for modality choice: IFS's "exile" construct works pretty well as a vocabulary for PTSD and most C-PTSD presentations. It works less well for OSDD-1. It does not work cleanly for DID. The deeper a client sits in the dissociative spectrum, the more pure IFS needs adaptation — and at the deepest end (DID), pure IFS is contraindicated as a primary modality even by clinicians who otherwise endorse it.

## Where exiles map and where they don't — the structural dissociation framework

Van der Hart, Nijenhuis, and Steele's *The Haunted Self* (W.W. Norton, 2006) is the canonical reference for structural dissociation theory. The framework predates IFS's popular rise and remains the standard model in academic trauma research.

The core concept: trauma can produce a structural division within the personality between an **Apparently Normal Part (ANP)** that handles daily life — work, relationships, "going on with normal life" — by avoiding trauma-related material, and one or more **Emotional Parts (EPs)** that hold the somatic, affective, and perceptual content of trauma memories and remain fixated on defensive action systems (fight, flight, freeze, submit, attach-cry).

Three levels of structural dissociation map onto three levels of clinical fragmentation:

**Primary structural dissociation** is one ANP and one EP — typically associated with PTSD from a single discrete event. The system has split into a daily-life self and a trauma self, and the daily-life self avoids the trauma self.

**Secondary structural dissociation** is one ANP and multiple EPs — typically associated with C-PTSD and developmental trauma. Multiple EPs because different defensive systems were activated at different points across an ongoing or repeated trauma history.

**Tertiary structural dissociation** is multiple ANPs and multiple EPs — typically associated with DID. The personality has split such that even the daily-functioning parts have separated, with amnestic barriers between them.

<!-- DIAGRAM: structural-dissociation-with-ifs-overlay -->

How does this map onto IFS vocabulary? At primary and secondary levels, the mapping works approximately. An IFS exile resembles an EP — both hold the trauma material. An IFS manager-blended-with-Self state resembles an ANP — both are organized to function in daily life by keeping the trauma material at bay. An IFS firefighter resembles an activated EP under acute distress. None of these mappings is identical, but they are useful approximations.

At the tertiary level, the mapping breaks. **DID alters are not the same as IFS exiles**. Alters have their own names, ages, sometimes their own handwriting and somatic complaints, and the system experiences amnestic barriers and switching between alters. Exiles are wounded parts within one personality system, accessible (in principle) to a unified Self. Treating alters as exiles, or exiles as alters, flattens both — and missing DID by treating it as IFS-style exile work can result in serious clinical harm.

This is one place where IFS clinicians trained primarily in IFS-Institute material sometimes go wrong, because the IFS Institute trains its practitioners to see "parts" as the universal pattern of mind, and pure-IFS theory does not cleanly differentiate normal multiplicity (parts), structural dissociation (EPs), and dissociative-disorder fragmentation (alters). Kathy Steele, co-author of *The Haunted Self* and former president of ISSTD, has a 2024 program titled *Adapting Internal Family Systems for Clients with Dissociative Disorders* that examines specifically which IFS concepts need adaptation for this population — including unblending, stepping aside, and the assumption of a unified Self that can lead.

## The phase-oriented treatment model

Judith Herman's 1992 book *Trauma and Recovery* introduced what is now called the three-phase model of trauma treatment: **Safety and Stabilization → Remembrance and Mourning → Reconnection**. Van der Hart, Nijenhuis, and Steele's structural dissociation framework adopts and refines this: Phase 1 (symptom reduction, stabilization, building integrative capacity), Phase 2 (treatment of traumatic memories), Phase 3 (integration and rehabilitation).

This three-phase architecture is the *lingua franca* of complex-trauma treatment. EMDR's eight-phase protocol can be read as a more elaborated version of the same basic flow. Sensorimotor Psychotherapy uses it. Somatic Experiencing uses it implicitly. TIST (Janina Fisher) builds explicit phase-orientation into its core framework. Where IFS sits inside this architecture is the central clinical disagreement on this page.

<!-- DIAGRAM: phase-oriented-treatment -->

The IFS Institute's position, as Schwartz and Anderson generally articulate it, is that pure IFS handles Phase 1 internally. Building Self-energy, befriending protectors, and unblending *is* stabilization in IFS terms. There is no separate stabilization phase before parts work begins. Anderson's *Transcending Trauma* extends Phase 1 work substantially — he describes befriending protectors slowly, often over months, before approaching exile work — but stays inside the IFS frame.

The TIST/structural-dissociation-trained position, articulated by Fisher, Steele, and Ogden, is that for severe complex trauma and dissociation, stabilization is a longer prerequisite phase that often includes nervous-system regulation work (somatic experiencing or sensorimotor body-tracking), distress tolerance skills, and titrated body-awareness *before* deeper IFS-style parts work is safe. Pure IFS, in this view, underestimates how much groundwork is needed before unblending and witnessing can proceed without flooding or retraumatization. Fisher's TIST explicitly integrates IFS with structural dissociation theory and sensorimotor psychotherapy on the assumption that no single one of these is sufficient.

This disagreement is currently unresolved empirically. Both positions are defensible. The choice between them is currently practitioner-dependent and depends heavily on the specific client. For a client with C-PTSD and intact-enough Self-access, Anderson's extended-Phase-1 IFS may be plenty. For a client with severe dissociation, OSDD features, or chronic nervous-system dysregulation, Fisher's TIST or a combined approach is generally more defensible.

## The major trauma modalities and how they relate to IFS

This is the comparison space. Each modality has its own theory of what gets disordered in trauma, its own mechanism of repair, and its own relationship to IFS.

<!-- DIAGRAM: modality-comparison-matrix -->

**EMDR (Francine Shapiro, 1989)** uses bilateral stimulation — eye movements, taps, or alternating tones — while the client holds a target traumatic memory in mind. The theoretical framework, the Adaptive Information Processing (AIP) model, holds that the brain has an innate processing system that integrates experience but that trauma overwhelms this system, leaving memories stored in raw, unprocessed, somatically-encoded form. EMDR is hypothesized to reactivate adaptive processing.

EMDR has a strong evidence base. Hundreds of RCTs. Recommended by WHO, APA, and VA/DoD as a first-line PTSD treatment. A 2024 individual participant data meta-analysis in *Psychological Medicine* found EMDR is *equivalent* to other trauma-focused therapies (trauma-focused CBT, prolonged exposure, cognitive processing therapy) in reducing PTSD symptom severity — not superior. So EMDR sits in a small group of front-line PTSD treatments that all do roughly equally well; it does not dominate.

For complex trauma, standard EMDR protocols often need modification. Pure EMDR can override protective parts and trigger flooding in clients with chronic dissociation, severe attachment trauma, or extensive abuse histories. Reprocessing can outrun the system's stabilization. EMDR for complex trauma typically requires a longer Phase 1 (preparation) and often parts-work integration. The most common synthesis at the moment is **IFS + EMDR sequenced**: IFS for unblending preparation and parts buy-in, EMDR for memory reprocessing, IFS for integration. This combination shows up across clinical training programs, blog literature, and emerging integrative protocols like the 2025 paper on IFS-informed EMDR for personality structural dissociation.

**Somatic Experiencing (Peter Levine, 1990s)** is body-first and slow. The premise: trauma is the trapped survival energy of incomplete defensive responses, and healing comes from completing those responses through micro-movements, sensation tracking, and gradual discharge. The two core SE techniques are **pendulation** (rhythmic alternation between contraction and expansion, between activation and resource) and **titration** (working with very tiny amounts of activation at a time to prevent flooding). Levine's slogan is that "trauma is too much, too fast, too soon" — SE deliberately slows everything down.

SE is often valuable in pre-stabilization for clients with severe nervous-system dysregulation. It is bottom-up rather than top-down, and it doesn't require detailed verbal trauma narratives — both useful features for clients who can't tolerate cognitive trauma processing yet. Some IFS clinicians explicitly sequence SE first for nervous-system regulation, then introduce IFS once the system can hold a wider window of tolerance. SE's formal RCT evidence base is limited; its grounding in polyvagal theory has been substantively criticized in current peer-reviewed literature (a 2023 *Biological Psychology* paper by 39 experts in vagal physiology and vertebrate social behavior concluded the theory's basic phylogenetic and functional-anatomical tenets do not withstand scrutiny). Treat SE as a clinically influential modality with modest formal evidence, in the same register as IFS itself.

**Sensorimotor Psychotherapy (Pat Ogden, 2000s)** is the closest cousin to IFS in body-oriented therapy. Like IFS, SP works explicitly with parts and recognizes that the psyche organizes into substates. Unlike IFS, SP enters through the body — observable movement, posture, gesture, micro-shifts in breathing — rather than through inner imagery and dialogue. SP integrates attachment theory, neuroscience, and IFS principles. The 2015 textbook *Sensorimotor Psychotherapy: Interventions for Trauma and Attachment* (Norton) is co-authored by Ogden and Janina Fisher.

For an autistic and alexithymic client, SP may be more accessible than pure IFS in one specific way: SP can enter through *observable movement* rather than through *felt sense*. If you cannot reliably feel where in the body a sensation lives, but you and your therapist can together notice that you brace your shoulders when a particular topic comes up, that brace is a usable entry point into parts work without requiring interoceptive accuracy. This doesn't fully solve the alexithymia problem — SP still leans on body awareness — but the bias toward observable rather than felt access can be a partial workaround.

**Ego State Therapy (Watkins & Watkins, 1980s)** is the historical predecessor whose existence is sometimes used to argue that IFS is in significant ways a rebranding. John G. Watkins (an analysand of Edoardo Weiss, who was analysed by Paul Federn) and Helen Watkins developed Ego State Therapy in the 1970s and 80s, building on Federn's psychoanalytic concept of ego states. ES uses hypnosis (often) to facilitate communication between states. ES does not have a discrete "Self" construct in the way IFS does — it typically frames the work as integration *between* states rather than relationship *between* a separate Self and the states.

Schwartz cites Watkins as an influence; how deep the influence runs is contested in the academic literature. Critics argue IFS is in significant respects ES with the hypnosis dropped, the "Self" concept added, and the clinical-hypnosis frame replaced with a family-systems frame and brand-friendly vocabulary. Defenders point to genuine theoretical innovations in IFS (Self as discrete construct, eight-C qualities, the "no bad parts" stance, the protector taxonomy). For complex trauma and especially for dissociative disorders, ES has a longer clinical history and is well-integrated with structural dissociation work. For lay-accessibility and self-help applicability, IFS has the easier-to-share vocabulary.

**TIST — Trauma-Informed Stabilization Treatment (Janina Fisher, 2017)** is the integrative model that combines IFS-informed parts work, structural dissociation theory, sensorimotor psychotherapy, clinical hypnosis principles, and cognitive restructuring under explicit phase-orientation. Fisher trained with Pat Ogden before developing TIST. Her *Healing the Fragmented Selves of Trauma Survivors* (Routledge, 2017) is the core text; *Transforming the Living Legacy of Trauma* (2021) is the workbook companion.

What TIST adds to IFS: explicit recognition that for severe trauma, dissociation, or self-destructive behavior, stabilization is its own phase that cannot be skipped or shortened. Conceptual frame that some dissociative parts cannot be reached through pure IFS unblending — they require longer ANP-EP separation work first. Body-tracking integration through sensorimotor heritage. Structural-dissociation-aware parts work. Honest engagement with where IFS as currently taught is insufficient for the most complex cases.

For the user's profile — complex trauma plus autism plus alexithymia — TIST is one of the strongest current synthesis points. It honors what IFS does well, names where IFS doesn't go far enough, and integrates body and structural-dissociation frameworks that fill the gaps. TIST's formal RCT evidence base is modest, like IFS's; its clinical reputation outpaces its empirical validation. But the integrative architecture is exactly the kind of thing complex-trauma practitioners reach for when no single modality is enough.

**Frank Anderson's IFS-trauma synthesis** is the IFS-canonical alternative path. Anderson is a senior lead trainer for the IFS Institute alongside Schwartz, and his *Transcending Trauma* (PESI, 2021) extends IFS for complex PTSD without leaving the IFS frame. Where Fisher's TIST integrates IFS with outside frameworks, Anderson's protocols deepen IFS itself: brain-based, neuroscience-informed, with extensive treatment of attachment trauma, shame, neglect, and dissociation, using IFS vocabulary throughout. For clients whose therapists are IFS-trained and want to stay in an IFS-centered protocol, Anderson is the most clinically extended option. For clients whose therapists draw on multiple modalities, Anderson supplies the IFS half of the integration.

## When pure IFS reaches its limits

The honest list of where IFS as a stand-alone modality reaches its limits is shorter than the marketing suggests but longer than IFS Institute training currently emphasizes.

<!-- DIAGRAM: when-pure-ifs-stalls -->

**Severe dissociation**: When the client cannot maintain a witness state long enough to do parts work — meaning they get flooded, dissociate, or shut down whenever they try to access a part — pure IFS stalls. The 6-Fs require a stable Self position; chronic dissociation can prevent that position from being stable enough. This isn't an IFS failure per se; it's a recognition that some clients need different prerequisites first.

**Chronic nervous-system dysregulation**: Parts work without prior stabilization can retraumatize. If the client lives in a chronically activated or chronically shutdown state, asking them to "feel toward" a part and witness an exile can flood the system before any healing can happen. SE for nervous-system regulation, sensorimotor body-tracking, or DBT-derived distress tolerance skills are standard prerequisites in this case.

**Active substance use, active self-harm**: These are stabilization-first situations. Pure IFS without prior safety planning, distress tolerance work, and harm-reduction approaches can push the firefighter parts into more extreme behaviors. TIST is explicitly designed for this population because Fisher named "self-destructive behaviors" as the core target.

**DID without phase-oriented prerequisites**: Pure IFS as a primary modality is contraindicated for DID even by clinicians who otherwise endorse IFS. The ISSTD treatment guidelines for DID require specialist-trained clinicians with significant DID-specific competence. IFS may be incorporated *after* substantial Phase 1 work and significant adaptation — this is what Steele's 2024 program addresses — but pure-IFS-Institute-trained practice is not enough on its own. Even Schwartz acknowledges DID needs specialist work; the IFS Institute does not currently train DID-specific competence.

**The "Self cannot be reached" problem**: When the model says everyone has Self but the client cannot access it. This deserves its own treatment, which is happening in [Category 9: Failure Modes and Contraindications](../failure-modes-contraindications/). Brief preview: pure-IFS framing has an unfalsifiability problem here — when Self isn't present, IFS theory says "a part is in the way," which is a move that cannot be disconfirmed and risks reframing genuine model-fit failure as client failure. The structural-dissociation-trained literature (Steele, Fisher) takes this problem more seriously than the IFS Institute generally does.

## What integration looks like in practice

The most common contemporary trauma protocols for complex cases involve combining or sequencing modalities rather than running pure IFS. A few of the more common patterns:

**Stabilization-first then IFS (TIST approach)**: Phase 1 stabilization work — somatic regulation through SE or sensorimotor body-tracking, distress tolerance through DBT-style skills, safety planning, building therapeutic alliance. Then careful parts-work introduction once the window of tolerance is wide enough to bear it. Then traditional IFS-style witnessing and unburdening as the system can hold it. This is Fisher's framework and is widely used in complex-trauma treatment.

**IFS + EMDR sequenced**: Some practitioners use IFS for "unblending" preparation — getting protector parts on board with the work, separating Self from blended states — and then bring in EMDR to process specific traumatic memories. After EMDR processing, the client returns to IFS for integration of what was processed. This combination addresses EMDR's tendency to override protectors in complex trauma while preserving its memory-processing power. The 2025 Springer paper on IFS-informed EMDR for personality structural dissociation is an example of this approach getting formal articulation.

**IFS + SE**: For clients whose primary block is nervous-system dysregulation rather than dissociation per se, SE is sometimes a regulation prerequisite to deeper IFS work. Clinicians sequence SE to widen the window of tolerance, then introduce IFS once the system can hold parts work without flooding.

**IFS for Phase 1 stabilization with attachment-focused parts work, then specialized modalities for processing**: For clients with severe attachment trauma but intact enough Self-access, this inversion can work — IFS for the relational stabilization, then EMDR or Sensorimotor for specific memory processing, then IFS again for integration. Anderson's *Transcending Trauma* protocols often run this way.

The honest meta-point: there is no consensus about "the right" combination for complex trauma. Different practitioners use different sequences, drawing on different theoretical loyalties and different training backgrounds. What unites most thoughtful approaches is the recognition that complex trauma is rarely served by a single modality and that phase-orientation matters.

## The user's profile — complex trauma + autism + alexithymia

For an autistic adult with suspected alexithymia and complex trauma history, the modality landscape narrows in specific ways.

The standard IFS challenge is well-documented elsewhere in this deep dive (see [Category 6](../ifs-and-alexithymia/) and [Category 7](../ifs-and-autism/)): the 6-Fs depend on interoceptive access ("where in the body do you notice this part?") and felt-sense work ("how do you feel toward this part?"). Both can be unreliable for alexithymic clients. Visualization may be limited or absent (aphantasia is somewhat more common in autistic populations). Literal interpretation can clash with metaphorical "parts" framing. Masking history can complicate which inner phenomena are "parts" versus survival strategies versus core neurotype.

For complex trauma layered on this, the question becomes: which modalities and combinations are most compatible with the user's processing profile?

**Frank Anderson's position**, as articulated across his courses and *Transcending Trauma*: brain-based IFS for complex PTSD is more accessible to clients with limited interoceptive access than pure 6-Fs, because the neuroscientific scaffolding gives cognitive entry points where felt-sense doesn't. Anderson's protocols emphasize neuroscience-informed extended befriending and brain-state regulation, both of which lean less heavily on interoception. This is one of the more accessible IFS-canonical paths for an alexithymic client.

**Janina Fisher's position**, articulated through TIST: explicitly supports adapting IFS for clients with limited interoceptive access by integrating sensorimotor body-tracking and structural-dissociation-aware parts work. The sensorimotor heritage means observable movement can be an entry point where felt sense fails. The structural dissociation framework means parts can be identified by their *function and pattern of activation* rather than by what they feel like internally — useful when interoception is unreliable.

**Bessel van der Kolk's position**: general endorsement of multiple modality integration; less specific guidance on autism or alexithymia. Van der Kolk's own scientific standards have been criticized in academic literature, including by trauma researchers in *Research on Social Work Practice*; his endorsement of any specific combination should be one input among many rather than the deciding factor.

**Pat Ogden's position**: sensorimotor psychotherapy's body-tracking-led approach can enter through observable movement when felt sense is unreliable. SP's integration with IFS principles makes it a natural partner for clients who need both parts framing and an alternative-to-interoception entry point.

A reasonable clinical map for this specific intersection — complex trauma + autism + alexithymia — looks roughly like:

1. **Stabilization phase**: Extended. Likely longer than for non-autistic, non-alexithymic complex-trauma clients. Body-based regulation that does not rely heavily on interoceptive accuracy: rhythmic activities, environmental cues, behavioral signals as proxies for emotional state, externalized practices (cards, journaling, written check-ins). The autistic profile may benefit from systems-thinking approaches to regulation — building explicit personal models of what activates and de-activates the system.

2. **Parts introduction**: Gradual. Cognitive and externalized access to parts (writing about parts, drawing them, naming them) rather than felt-sense access. Some autistic IFS practitioners (Sarah Bergenfield and others) explicitly work this way. Anderson's neuroscience-based protocols can supplement.

3. **Processing phase**: Modality choice depends on what the client and therapist together discover works. Some autistic clients find EMDR with autism-modified protocols (slower pace, more sessions, attention to alexithymia and dissociation) processes specific memories effectively. Others find IFS-style witnessing-and-unburdening works once the system has stabilized enough. Some find combinations.

4. **Integration phase**: Continued IFS-informed parts work, but also explicit life-rebuilding, relational repair, and identity integration. The autistic profile may benefit from more explicit narrative scaffolding and structured reflection during integration than neurotypical complex-trauma protocols typically offer.

Critically: this is an orientation, not a prescription. The right modality combination is something you, your therapist, and your nervous system work out together. The point of this page is to make the comparison space visible so that you can have informed conversations about modality choice — not to tell you what to do.

## What this means for clinical decision-making

A few takeaway sentences worth bringing into therapy conversations.

"My understanding is that for complex trauma, no single modality is usually enough, and the choice between IFS-Institute-canonical extension (Anderson) and integrative approaches (Fisher's TIST) is a real clinical disagreement, not a settled question."

"My alexithymia means the standard IFS interoception-dependent steps — 'where in the body' and 'how do you feel toward' — are unreliable for me. What does that mean for how we sequence things?"

"The 'Self always exists and can lead' assumption is a foundation of IFS. For my profile, do we have a working theory of what to do if Self-access is partial or intermittent rather than reliable?"

"If pure IFS stalls, what's the next move you'd recommend? I want to know the decision tree before we hit a wall."

"For the trauma processing phase specifically, are we considering IFS unburdening alone, or sequencing in EMDR or sensorimotor work? What's your reasoning?"

The point of those sentences is not to teach the therapist their job. The therapist already knows their job. The point is to make explicit that you are reading the model with eyes open, that you want to understand the comparison space rather than be sold a single modality, and that your specific profile (autism, alexithymia, complex trauma) calls for adaptations the standard IFS protocol does not provide on its own.

## Summary

Complex trauma is the territory where IFS sits closest to its strongest evidence and to its sharpest limits. The exile construct maps reasonably well onto trauma-state-dependent memory at the primary and secondary structural-dissociation levels. It does not map onto DID alters. The phase-oriented treatment model — Herman 1992, Van der Hart 2006 — is the *lingua franca* of complex-trauma treatment, and where IFS sits inside it is the central clinical disagreement: IFS Institute claims internal handling of Phase 1, while TIST/structural-dissociation-trained clinicians argue for stabilization as a longer prerequisite. EMDR, SE, Sensorimotor Psychotherapy, Ego State Therapy, and TIST are the major comparison modalities, each with its own strengths, mechanisms, and phase-fit. Pure IFS reaches limits in severe dissociation, chronic dysregulation, active substance use or self-harm, and DID. Combined approaches — TIST-style stabilization-then-IFS, IFS+EMDR sequenced, IFS+SE — are common in contemporary practice. For the user's profile (complex trauma + autism + alexithymia), Anderson's brain-based IFS extension, Fisher's TIST integration, and Sensorimotor's body-tracking-led parts work are all reasonable starting points; the right combination is something to work out with the therapist using this comparison space as a conversation tool.

### Key Points

1. IFS is in the trauma sweet spot for C-PTSD without DID-level fragmentation, but its evidence base is much thinner than its marketing — and EMDR, also first-line for PTSD, sits at equivalent (not inferior) efficacy across multiple meta-analyses.
2. Structural dissociation theory (Van der Hart, Nijenhuis, Steele 2006) is the field's standard framework; IFS exiles map approximately onto EPs at primary/secondary levels, but the mapping breaks at tertiary (DID alters ≠ exiles).
3. The IFS-Institute vs. TIST/structural-dissociation disagreement at Phase 1 is real, currently unresolved, and practitioner-dependent. Different clinicians make different defensible choices.
4. Combined approaches (TIST, IFS+EMDR, IFS+SE) are standard contemporary practice for complex trauma. Pure single-modality treatment is the exception.
5. For complex trauma + autism + alexithymia, Anderson's neuroscience-based IFS, Fisher's TIST, and Sensorimotor's observable-movement entry are the most accessible candidates. The right combination depends on the specific client and is a clinical conversation worth having explicitly.

| TIST | Trauma-Informed Stabilization Treatment. Janina Fisher 2017. Integrates IFS + structural dissociation + sensorimotor + clinical hypnosis principles. |
| AIP | Adaptive Information Processing. Shapiro's theoretical model for EMDR. |
| Pendulation | SE technique: rhythmic alternation between activation and resource. |
| Titration | SE principle: working with very small amounts of activation at a time. |
| Bilateral stimulation | EMDR mechanism: alternating left-right eye movements, taps, or tones. |
| Window of tolerance | Range of arousal in which a person can think, feel, and engage without dissociating or flooding. From Dan Siegel; widely adopted across modalities. |
| Witness state | Capacity to observe parts/sensations from a calm, curious, compassionate stance without being flooded. Called Self-energy in IFS-specific vocabulary. |
