## 1. THE RT COMPREHENSION PROCEDURE AS A DECISION TOOL

### The Core Theory (What RT Actually Claims)

Relevance Theory (RT), developed by Dan Sperber and Deirdre Wilson (1986/1995), makes a radical claim: human cognition is geared toward maximizing relevance. Every time someone speaks to you, your brain automatically assumes that what they said is worth the effort of processing — that it will produce useful cognitive effects (new conclusions, strengthened beliefs, or corrections to wrong beliefs) for a reasonable amount of mental effort.

The **Communicative Principle of Relevance** states: every ostensive stimulus (every deliberate communicative act) conveys a presumption of its own optimal relevance. In plain language: when someone deliberately says something to you, they are implicitly promising that it is worth your attention — relevant enough to justify the effort of processing it, and the most relevant thing they could have said given their abilities and preferences.

The **RT comprehension procedure** (Wilson & Sperber, 2004) is described as a "fast and frugal heuristic" — a dedicated inferential mechanism that automatically computes a hypothesis about the speaker's meaning. The procedure is:

> *"Follow a path of least effort in computing cognitive effects: test interpretive hypotheses in order of accessibility, and stop when your expectations of relevance are satisfied."*

### Turning the Procedure into an Explicit Algorithm

For neurotypical communicators, this procedure runs automatically and unconsciously. For autistic individuals who process pragmatic meaning more deliberately and explicitly (research confirms that many autistic adults rely on conscious, effortful processing of social situations rather than automatic inference), the procedure can be made into a step-by-step decision tool.

**The RT Comprehension Algorithm (Explicit Version):**

**Step 1: DECODE — What did they literally say?**
- Parse the actual words spoken
- Identify the linguistic meaning (the "logical form" in RT terms)
- Note what RT calls the "explicature": what is explicitly communicated, including filling in pronouns (who does "he" refer to?), resolving ambiguity (which meaning of "bank"?), and narrowing/broadening word meanings as needed
- This step is usually the easiest for autistic individuals — literal decoding is a strength

**Step 2: SCAN CONTEXT — What assumptions are available right now?**
- RT replaces the problematic concept of "mutual knowledge" with **"mutual manifestness"** — a fact is "manifest" to you if you are capable of accepting it as true or probably true at this moment
- Your **cognitive environment** is the total set of facts and assumptions available to you right now
- Key context sources to scan:
  - Physical environment (Where are we? What just happened? What can both of us see/hear?)
  - Conversational context (What were we just talking about? What was just said?)
  - Background knowledge about the speaker (What do I know about this person? Their relationship to me? Their likely goals?)
  - Social/cultural conventions (What are the norms in this setting?)
  - Encyclopedic knowledge (What do I know about this topic in general?)

**Step 3: COMPUTE — What interpretation requires the least effort and gives the most useful information?**
- The key insight: relevance = cognitive effects ÷ processing effort
- More relevant = bigger payoff for less work
- Ask: "If I take the literal meaning, does it give me useful new information in this context?"
- If yes → the literal meaning may be the right interpretation
- If no → the literal meaning fails to be relevant enough, so there must be something more going on
- What conclusion would make this utterance worth saying RIGHT HERE, RIGHT NOW, to ME?
- RT calls this the **"first interpretation consistent with the principle of relevance"**

**Step 4: CHECK — Does this interpretation seem like what a communicating person would intend?**
- The speaker chose these words deliberately
- Would a reasonable person intend this interpretation?
- Does it fit with what you know about the speaker's likely goals, attitudes, and communicative abilities?
- RT's key constraint: the interpretation must be one the speaker could reasonably have expected to be obvious enough for the hearer to recover

**Step 5: ACCEPT or ITERATE**
- If the interpretation passes Step 4 → accept it and respond accordingly
- If it doesn't → go back to Step 3 and try the next most accessible interpretation
- If multiple interpretations seem equally plausible → you may need to ask for clarification (this is a legitimate and often wise strategy)

### Critical Concepts Unpacked

**Cognitive Effects (Why You Should Care About an Utterance)**

Processing new information in context yields three types of useful cognitive effect:
1. **Contextual implications**: New conclusions derivable only from combining the utterance with what you already know (neither the utterance alone nor your background knowledge alone could produce them)
2. **Strengthening**: Evidence that confirms something you already suspected
3. **Contradiction/revision**: Evidence that overturns something you previously believed

If an interpretation produces none of these effects — if it tells you nothing new, confirms nothing, and contradicts nothing — then it is irrelevant on that interpretation, and you should look for a different interpretation.

**Processing Effort (The Cost Side of the Equation)**

Processing effort includes:
- Effort of perception (hearing/reading the words)
- Effort of memory retrieval (accessing relevant background knowledge)
- Effort of inference (drawing conclusions)
- More complex sentences, more indirect speech, less familiar vocabulary, less obvious contextual connections = more effort
- Speakers are expected to minimize unnecessary effort — if they make you work harder, there should be a proportional payoff in cognitive effects

**Explicature vs. Implicature (What They Said vs. What They Meant)**

RT distinguishes:
- **Explicature**: The explicitly communicated content, developed from the literal meaning by adding contextual information (filling in referents, disambiguating, adjusting word meaning). Example: "He handed her his key and she opened the door" → Explicature includes that "she" used "his key" to open "the door" (of the house/room contextually relevant)
- **Implicature**: Everything communicated that is NOT part of the explicature — the unstated conclusions the speaker intends you to draw. Example: "I have a meeting at three" (in response to "Can you help?") → The implicature is "No, I can't help"

## 2. WORKED EXAMPLES — The Procedure Applied to Confusing Utterances

### Example A: "Nice shirt."

**The confusion:** Is this a genuine compliment or sarcasm?

**Step 1 — Decode:** The literal meaning is a positive evaluation of the hearer's shirt.

**Step 2 — Scan context:**
- What shirt am I wearing? (A stained old T-shirt? A new shirt I'm proud of?)
- Who is speaking? (A friend who tends to be sarcastic? A colleague making small talk? A stranger?)
- What is their tone of voice? (Warm and admiring? Flat or exaggerated?)
- What is their facial expression? (Genuine smile? Smirk? Eye-roll?)
- What just happened? (Did I just spill coffee on myself? Did I just arrive wearing something new?)

**Step 3 — Compute relevance of the literal interpretation:**
- If I'm wearing a genuinely nice new shirt and the speaker's tone is warm → the literal meaning ("your shirt looks good") provides a small but pleasant cognitive effect (strengthening: confirms my belief that the shirt was a good choice). This is relevant enough. Accept.
- If I'm wearing a stained old T-shirt → the literal meaning ("your shirt looks good") produces NO useful cognitive effect because it's obviously false. It fails the relevance test. Something else must be going on.

**Step 4 — If literal fails, try next interpretation:**
- RT's **echoic account of irony**: the speaker is "echoing" an expected norm or someone else's thought ("one should look presentable") and expressing a **dissociative attitude** toward it — in this case, mockingly noting the gap between the norm and reality
- The sarcastic interpretation ("your shirt is a mess") achieves relevance: it provides the cognitive effect of the speaker drawing attention to the state of your clothing, possibly with humor
- This is consistent with what a communicating person might intend in this context

**RT resolution:** Context determines the answer. The procedure works because the literal interpretation either passes or fails the relevance test based on available contextual evidence. When it fails, you move to the next most accessible interpretation (sarcasm/irony), which succeeds.

**Key cues to train yourself to notice:**
- Prosody: sarcasm often features exaggerated or flat intonation
- Context fit: does the literal meaning match observable reality?
- Speaker history: does this person tend toward sarcasm?

### Example B: "I have a meeting at three."
*(In response to: "Can you help me move some boxes this afternoon?")*

**Step 1 — Decode:** The literal meaning is a statement about the speaker's schedule — they have a meeting at 3 PM.

**Step 2 — Scan context:**
- I just asked for help this afternoon
- The speaker has responded with schedule information
- We are in a work/social context where requests for help are normal

**Step 3 — Compute relevance:**
- If we take ONLY the literal meaning, the speaker has merely informed me of a calendar item. But I didn't ask about their schedule — I asked for help. A bare schedule statement would be irrelevant to my question.
- To make this utterance relevant to my question, I need to draw a further conclusion
- **Implicated premise** (assumption I need to supply): If someone has a meeting, they can't help with physical tasks at the same time
- **Implicated conclusion** (the implicature): Therefore, they can't help me this afternoon — this is an indirect refusal
- This interpretation achieves relevance: it answers my question (cognitive effect: I now know they can't help) with moderate processing effort

**Step 4 — Check intention:**
- Would a reasonable person intend this as a refusal? Yes — indirect refusals are extremely common because they are more polite than a flat "No"
- The speaker provides a *reason* for declining, which is more face-preserving than a bare refusal
- Accept this interpretation

**RT resolution:** The literal content alone is not relevant enough to justify the utterance in this context. The comprehension procedure automatically looks for further implications that would make it relevant. The indirect refusal + justification interpretation satisfies relevance expectations.

**The pattern to learn:** When someone responds to a request with a statement about their situation or schedule, they are almost always giving you a reason for declining. The information they provide IS their answer — it's just wrapped in an explanation rather than a direct "no."

### Example C: "Well, that's one way to do it."

**Step 1 — Decode:** Literally: there exists at least one way to do something, and what was just done/suggested qualifies as one such way.

**Step 2 — Scan context:**
- Someone has just done something or proposed a method
- The speaker is commenting on that method
- The word "well" at the start is a discourse marker that often signals a hedge or reluctance

**Step 3 — Compute relevance:**
- The literal meaning is trivially true — OF COURSE it's "one way to do it." Anything that was actually done is, by definition, one way to do it. This tells the hearer nothing new.
- A tautology (something obviously true) produces zero cognitive effects on its own. It fails the relevance test.
- To rescue relevance, we need an enriched interpretation. The phrase "one way" (as opposed to "the way" or "a good way" or "the right way") implicates that there are OTHER ways — and the speaker's choice to merely acknowledge it as *one* way, rather than endorsing it, signals that those other ways might be better.
- **Implicature**: "What you did works, technically, but it's not how I would have done it / not the best approach"
- This achieves relevance: it provides the cognitive effect of learning that the speaker has reservations about the method

**Step 4 — Check:**
- This is a classic hedged criticism pattern. People use it to soften negative evaluations. The "well" + understated phrasing signals reluctance to criticize directly.
- A communicating person would plausibly intend this as mild criticism. Accept.

**RT resolution:** The literal content is too obvious to be informative, so the procedure searches for a richer interpretation. The most accessible enrichment is that the speaker is deliberately withholding praise, which implicates disapproval.

**The pattern:** When someone describes your work with notably weak or minimal praise ("that's one way," "it's certainly... something," "well, it works"), the gap between what they said and what they COULD have said (enthusiastic endorsement) is itself meaningful. The restraint IS the message.

### Example D: "I'm fine."

**Step 1 — Decode:** Literal meaning: The speaker's current state is adequate/satisfactory.

**Step 2 — Scan context:**
- Why am I asking? (Did they look upset? Did something bad happen? Is this routine greeting?)
- **Prosodic cues**: How did they say it?
  - Bright, quick, relaxed → likely genuine
  - Flat, quiet, tense, or with a sigh → likely not genuine
  - Snapped/sharp → definitely not genuine, and signals irritation at being asked
- Body language: making eye contact? Turned away? Tense posture?
- What just happened? (Argument? Bad news? Stressful event?)

**Step 3 — Compute relevance under different prosodic/contextual conditions:**

**Scenario 1: Routine context, neutral prosody**
- You pass a colleague in the hallway: "How are you?" "I'm fine."
- Literal interpretation is relevant enough — it's a phatic (social bonding) exchange, and "fine" serves the social function of reciprocating the greeting
- Accept at face value

**Scenario 2: After obvious distress, with flat/tense prosody**
- Context makes the literal meaning implausible — they clearly are NOT fine
- The literal interpretation fails the relevance test because it contradicts observable evidence
- Next interpretation: the speaker is performing a social convention (claiming to be fine) while the prosodic/contextual cues communicate the opposite
- **Possible implicatures**: "I don't want to talk about it" / "I'm not fine but I'm trying to manage" / "I'm upset but I don't want to burden you"
- Which implicature is correct depends on further context (relationship closeness, setting, what happened)

**Scenario 3: Said sharply/snappily**
- The prosody signals irritation
- Implicature: "Stop asking. I don't want to discuss my state." or "Your asking is making things worse"

**RT resolution:** RT explicitly acknowledges that prosody provides input to the comprehension procedure. Wilson & Wharton (2006) argue that prosodic cues do not encode meaning the way words do — instead, they provide evidence that makes certain interpretations more or less accessible. Flat or tense prosody makes the "not actually fine" interpretation more accessible; bright prosody makes the literal interpretation more accessible. The procedure processes whichever interpretation becomes most accessible first.

**The pattern:** "I'm fine" is one of the most context-dependent utterances in English. The words matter less than HOW they're said and WHEN they're said. If prosodic and contextual cues conflict with the literal meaning, the cues win.

### Example E: "We should totally do lunch sometime."

**Step 1 — Decode:** Literal meaning: The speaker proposes that they and the hearer should eat a meal together at an unspecified future time.

**Step 2 — Scan context:**
- Where are we? (End of a party? Running into an acquaintance? Professional networking event?)
- How well do we know each other? (Close friends? Acquaintances? Just met?)
- Is the speaker making any move to actually schedule this? (Pulling out their phone? Suggesting a specific date?)
- Key word analysis: "sometime" is maximally vague — no specific time; "totally" is an intensifier that, paradoxically, can signal casualness rather than commitment; "should" is weaker than "let's" or "I want to"

**Step 3 — Compute relevance:**

**Interpretation 1: Genuine invitation**
- If genuine, the cognitive effect would be: the speaker wants to spend social time with you, and you now have a potential social plan
- But: no specific time, no follow-up action, no concrete commitment → the effort to extract a genuine plan from this utterance is very high (you'd have to do all the work of following up)
- Genuine social planning usually looks different ("Are you free Thursday? Let's grab lunch at that new place")

**Interpretation 2: Phatic expression — social bonding, not literal proposal**
- Phatic communication (Malinowski, 1923) serves to establish or maintain social relationships rather than transfer substantive information
- The cognitive effect: the speaker is signaling "I like you / I value our connection / I want to be perceived as friendly toward you"
- This interpretation requires LESS effort (no need to extract logistical information) and produces a clear cognitive effect (social positioning information)
- The vagueness of "sometime" is actually a cue: genuine invitations include specifics

**Step 4 — Check:**
- In American English, "We should do lunch sometime" is a recognized phatic formula — a social nicety signaling goodwill without literal commitment
- A communicating person in a casual social encounter would plausibly intend this as relationship maintenance rather than an actual invitation

**RT resolution:** The phatic interpretation is more accessible (less effort) and produces adequate cognitive effects (you now know the social relationship is positive). The literal invitation interpretation requires more effort and produces uncertain effects (you don't know if they mean it). RT's least-effort principle favors the phatic reading.

**The pattern:** When an apparent proposal is maximally vague about specifics (no time, no place, no follow-up), it is usually phatic rather than literal. Genuine proposals include enough detail to act on. The more vague a social proposal, the more likely it's just social lubrication.

**Caveat:** Some people DO mean this literally but express it vaguely because they're unsure of your interest. If you WANT to test whether it's genuine: respond with a specific counter-proposal ("I'm free Thursday!"). A genuine inviter will engage; a phatic speaker will hedge ("Oh, yeah, let me check my schedule...") and never follow up.

### Example F: "Don't you think it's a bit warm in here?"

**Step 1 — Decode:** Literally: a yes/no question about whether the hearer shares the speaker's perception of the room temperature.

**Step 2 — Scan context:**
- Is the room actually warm? (If not, this might be about something else entirely)
- Is there a window that could be opened? An air conditioner that could be adjusted? A thermostat nearby?
- Is the speaker closer to the heater than you? Do they look uncomfortable?
- The negative question form ("Don't you...") is significant — it presupposes the speaker already believes the answer is yes

**Step 3 — Compute relevance:**
- If taken literally (just asking for temperature assessment), the utterance provides minimal cognitive effects — the speaker already signals their own belief that it's warm through the negative question form, so getting your agreement is low-value information
- More relevant interpretation: the speaker wants the temperature changed and is asking you to do something about it
- **Implicated premise**: If it's warm and that's unpleasant, the situation should be remedied
- **Implicated conclusion**: "Please open the window / adjust the thermostat / turn on the AC"
- This indirect request interpretation achieves HIGH relevance: it gives you actionable information (what the speaker wants you to do)

**Step 4 — Check:**
- Indirect requests are standard politeness conventions in English
- It's more polite to frame a request as a question about shared perception than as a direct command ("Open the window!")
- A reasonable person would intend this as a request. Accept.

**RT resolution:** The literal question is too low-value to justify the utterance. The indirect request interpretation satisfies relevance expectations because it provides actionable cognitive effects.

**The pattern:** Questions about the physical environment ("Isn't it cold in here?" "Is that door supposed to be open?" "Did someone leave the lights on?") are frequently indirect requests for action. The speaker is describing a problem and hoping you'll recognize that the description IS the request.

### Example G: "Interesting choice."

**Step 1 — Decode:** Literally: the hearer has made a choice that the speaker finds interesting (i.e., intellectually stimulating, noteworthy, or unusual).

**Step 2 — Scan context:**
- What choice was just made? (Restaurant order? Outfit? Work decision? Color for the living room?)
- Prosody: said with genuine curiosity, or with a flat/raised-eyebrow tone?
- The word "interesting" is doing heavy lifting. In its strongest genuine sense, it means "intellectually engaging." But it's commonly used as a hedge when the speaker wants to avoid saying something negative.

**Step 3 — Compute relevance:**
- **Literal interpretation**: The speaker is genuinely fascinated by your choice. Cognitive effect: you learn that your choice is noteworthy in an intellectually engaging way. Possible in academic or creative contexts where unusual choices are valued.
- **Hedged disapproval interpretation**: "Interesting" is the polite word you use when you can't find a genuinely positive word. Like "one way to do it" (Example C), the restraint of the praise IS the message. If the speaker approved, they would say "Great choice!" or "Nice!" — the fact that they reached for the neutral/intellectual "interesting" rather than a warm positive signals that they're being diplomatic.
- **Cognitive effect of disapproval reading**: You learn that the speaker doesn't approve of your choice but doesn't want to say so directly

**Step 4 — Check:**
- The disapproval interpretation is consistent with widespread pragmatic conventions in English where "interesting" functions as a diplomatic non-endorsement
- Context determines which reading wins: if said by a professor about your thesis topic with genuine engagement = literal; if said by a friend about your outfit with a flat tone = disapproval

**RT resolution:** The word "interesting" is genuinely ambiguous between positive (intellectually engaging) and diplomatic (avoiding negativity). The procedure relies on prosody and context to determine which interpretation is more accessible.

**The pattern:** When evaluative words are notably *neutral* rather than *positive* ("interesting," "different," "unique," "bold"), this neutrality often signals suppressed negativity. The speaker could have chosen enthusiastic language and didn't — the absence of enthusiasm is itself a cue.

### Example H: A boss saying "I was hoping this would be done by now."

**Step 1 — Decode:** Literally: the boss had an expectation (the work would be done by the current time) that has not been met. The use of past tense "was hoping" frames this as a past mental state.

**Step 2 — Scan context:**
- The speaker is your boss (power differential)
- A work task is incomplete
- The boss is commenting on the gap between their expectation and reality
- Workplace setting where accountability is implicit

**Step 3 — Compute relevance:**
- **Literal interpretation**: The boss is simply sharing their past mental state — they once had a hope, and that hope was not fulfilled. As a bare statement of the boss's psychology, this is low-value information. You already know they wanted the work done; that's why they assigned it.
- **Richer interpretation**: The boss is expressing disappointment AND issuing an implicit directive: "Finish this immediately / Why isn't this done? / Your performance is below expectations."
- The phrasing "I was hoping" rather than "I need" or "Finish this now" is strategic: it frames the criticism as a personal disappointment rather than a direct command, which is a face-management strategy in workplace hierarchies (the boss appears reasonable rather than authoritarian)
- **Cognitive effects**: You learn (a) the boss is dissatisfied, (b) you are behind schedule by the boss's standards, (c) you need to prioritize this task immediately
- These effects are highly relevant — they affect your immediate work priorities

**Step 4 — Check:**
- This is a standard indirect reprimand pattern used by people in authority who want to maintain rapport while asserting expectations
- The "hoping" frame is a politeness strategy — it softens the criticism by phrasing it as personal feeling rather than objective failure
- A boss would plausibly intend all three cognitive effects above. Accept.

**RT resolution:** The literal content (boss's past hopes) is too uninformative to justify the utterance. The procedure enriches it to the most relevant interpretation: an expression of dissatisfaction that carries an implicit demand for immediate action.

**The workplace pattern:** When authority figures describe their unfulfilled expectations ("I was hoping," "I would have thought," "I was expecting"), they are (a) expressing disappointment, (b) attributing responsibility to you, and (c) implicitly demanding correction. The indirectness is strategic — it lets them criticize while maintaining the appearance of reasonableness.

### Example I: Someone saying "No, go ahead, I'll wait" with a sigh.

**Step 1 — Decode:** Literally: the speaker is giving you permission to proceed, and they will wait for their turn.

**Step 2 — Scan context:**
- What's the situation? (You're about to cut in line? You've been monopolizing a shared resource? You're taking a long time with something?)
- **Prosodic cues are critical here**:
  - Said brightly and genuinely → actual permission, cooperative spirit
  - Said with a sigh, flat tone, or strained patience → NOT genuine permission
- The sigh is a prosodic cue that communicates something the words don't

**Step 3 — Compute relevance:**
- The words alone say "permission granted"
- But the sigh adds processing effort while signaling that the speaker's actual attitude contradicts their words
- **Wilson & Sperber's treatment of prosody**: Prosodic cues don't encode meaning like words do — they provide evidence that adjusts the accessibility of different interpretations. The sigh makes the "genuine willing permission" interpretation LESS accessible and the "reluctant, resentful concession" interpretation MORE accessible.
- **The richer interpretation**: "I'm annoyed that you're going ahead of me / taking this long, but I won't confront you about it directly. I'm performing politeness while letting my displeasure leak through prosodically."
- Cognitive effects: You learn the speaker is irritated, feels imposed upon, and is being passively rather than genuinely cooperative

**Step 4 — Check:**
- People frequently say socially expected things ("go ahead," "no problem," "it's fine") while their prosody reveals the opposite
- This is consistent with the dual-track nature of communication: words handle the social surface, prosody reveals the actual attitude
- Accept the "annoyed concession" reading

**RT resolution:** The words and the prosody provide conflicting evidence. RT's procedure resolves this by treating prosody as making certain interpretations more accessible. When words and prosody conflict, the prosody typically reveals the speaker's actual attitude, while the words maintain social convention.

**The pattern:** When someone grants permission or agreement using the right words but the wrong tone (sighs, flat delivery, strained voice), the tone is more reliable than the words. The person is performing a social obligation (being polite/cooperative) while their actual emotional state leaks through non-verbal channels. Learn to trust prosody over content when they conflict.

### Example J: "I didn't say you were stupid." — With Different Stress Patterns

This is a famous example in pragmatics (often attributed to various sources) that shows how stress placement creates radically different implicatures from identical words.

**Step 1 — Decode:** The words are identical each time. Literal meaning: a denial of having said the hearer is stupid.

**Step 2 — Scan context:** The critical context variable here is **stress placement** — which word receives prosodic emphasis.

**Step 3 — Compute relevance for each stress pattern:**

**"*I* didn't say you were stupid"** (stress on "I")
- Implicature: Someone ELSE said it. I'm denying my authorship of the claim, not the truth of the claim itself.
- Cognitive effect: You learn who is responsible for the statement (not me, but someone else did say it)

**"I *didn't* say you were stupid"** (stress on "didn't")
- Implicature: Flat denial — I genuinely did not say that. You misheard or misremember.
- Cognitive effect: Correction of your false belief about what I said

**"I didn't *say* you were stupid"** (stress on "say")
- Implicature: I didn't say it explicitly, but I may have *implied* it, *thought* it, or communicated it non-verbally
- Cognitive effect: A technically true denial that leaves the underlying accusation standing — I may well think you're stupid, I just didn't SAY it

**"I didn't say *you* were stupid"** (stress on "you")
- Implicature: I said SOMEONE was stupid, but that someone wasn't you — it was someone else
- Cognitive effect: Clarification of the referent of the original statement

**"I didn't say you *were* stupid"** (stress on "were")
- Implicature: You're not stupid NOW (maybe you were before, or maybe the claim was about a specific moment, not a general trait)
- Cognitive effect: Temporal qualification of the original claim

**"I didn't say you were *stupid*"** (stress on "stupid")
- Implicature: I said you were something negative, but the word wasn't "stupid" — maybe "careless," "naive," or "uninformed"
- Cognitive effect: Correction of the specific descriptor while leaving the negative evaluation intact

**Step 4 — Check:** Each stress pattern selects a different part of the sentence to focus on, creating a contrast between the stressed element and its alternatives. This is a standard feature of English information structure. Each interpretation is what a communicating person would intend when choosing that particular stress pattern.

**RT explanation:** Stress placement increases the processing effort on the stressed word, which by the relevance-guided procedure, must be compensated by additional cognitive effects. The additional effort signals "pay attention to THIS word specifically — it's the key to what I'm actually communicating." The stressed word creates a **focus-alternative** structure: it highlights the stressed element and implicates that the correct value differs from what the hearer assumed.

**The pattern:** In English, stress on any word creates an implicit contrast with alternatives to that word. When someone stresses a particular word in a denial, they are denying THAT SPECIFIC ELEMENT while potentially leaving everything else undenied. Learn to ask: "Which word did they stress? What contrast does that create?"

### Bonus Example K: "If you want."

*(In response to: "Should we go to the Italian place for dinner?")*

**Step 1 — Decode:** Literally: the decision is conditional on the hearer's desire.

**Step 2 — Scan context:**
- I asked for input on a dinner choice
- The speaker has deflected the decision entirely back to me
- Prosody matters enormously here:
  - Said with genuine openness/warmth → truly indifferent, happy with whatever
  - Said with flat tone or slight shrug → passive non-commitment, possible reluctance
  - Said with a sigh or barely-hidden reluctance → they DON'T want to but won't say so

**Step 3 — Compute relevance:**
- I asked a question that solicited a preference. "If you want" provides zero information about the speaker's preference.
- Literal interpretation: the speaker has no preference. This IS informative (I now know they're neutral). Check: is this consistent with their prosody?
- If prosody suggests reluctance: "if you want" is a passive way of declining without taking responsibility for the rejection. The cognitive effect shifts: I learn they're NOT enthusiastic but they're making the non-preference MY responsibility.
- The key: a person who was enthusiastic about Italian food would say "Yes!" or "Sounds great!" — the conspicuous absence of enthusiasm is itself a cue.

**RT resolution:** The degree of informativeness determines the interpretation. Genuine indifference CAN be the answer, but lack of enthusiasm when enthusiasm would be easy to express signals suppressed disagreement.

### Bonus Example L: "That's a lot of food." 

*(Said when you sit down with a full plate at a buffet)*

**Step 1 — Decode:** Literally: an observation about the quantity of food on your plate.

**Step 2 — Scan context:**
- Who said it? (Close friend? Acquaintance? Parent? Stranger?)
- What is their tone? (Impressed? Judgmental? Neutral? Amused?)
- What's the social norm? (Is this a context where eating a lot is valued — like a competitive eating context — or one where portion control is expected?)

**Step 3 — Compute relevance:**
- The literal observation ("your plate has a lot of food on it") is obviously true and visible to both of you. Stating something both parties already know is, by itself, not very relevant.
- To achieve relevance, there must be an additional cognitive effect:
  - **Impressed**: "Wow, you have a healthy appetite!" (positive evaluation)
  - **Judgmental**: "That's more than you should be eating" (negative evaluation / social policing)
  - **Concerned**: "Are you sure you can eat all that?" (practical concern about waste)
  - **Humorous**: "You're really going for it!" (lighthearted teasing)

**Step 4 — Check:** Prosody, facial expression, and relationship with the speaker determine which effect is intended. The RT procedure makes each of these differentially accessible depending on the context.

**The pattern:** When someone states something obvious that both parties can already see, they're not providing information about the observable world — they're providing their ATTITUDE toward what they see. The attitude is the point, not the observation.

## 3. WHEN THE PROCEDURE FAILS

### Failure Mode 1: "First Satisfactory Interpretation" Leads to Misunderstanding

The RT comprehension procedure tells you to stop at the first interpretation that satisfies your relevance expectations. But what if the first satisfactory interpretation is wrong?

**How this happens:**
- You arrive at an interpretation that seems to make sense — it's coherent, it produces cognitive effects, and it seems like something the speaker might intend
- But you're missing a piece of context that would have made a different interpretation more accessible
- The speaker intended something different from what you landed on

**Example:** Your colleague says "The meeting's been moved." You interpret this as "moved to a different time" (because you've been worrying about a schedule conflict). But they meant "moved to a different room." Both interpretations satisfy relevance, but you had the wrong contextual assumption about what was most likely to change.

**Why this is particularly relevant for autistic individuals:**
Research (Frontiers in Psychology, 2021) shows that in interactions between autistic and non-autistic people, what is most salient to each interlocutor may be mismatched. This means the "first accessible interpretation" may be different for each person, creating systematic misunderstanding even when both parties are applying the procedure correctly — they're just applying it from different cognitive starting points.

### Failure Mode 2: Two Equally Accessible Interpretations (One Wrong)

**How this happens:**
- Two interpretations present themselves at roughly the same time
- Both produce adequate cognitive effects
- Both seem like plausible speaker intentions
- But only one is correct

**Example:** Your friend says "I saw your post." You can't tell if this is a compliment (they appreciated it), a neutral observation (just mentioning they saw it), or a criticism (they disapproved of something in it). All three interpretations are roughly equally accessible without further cues.

**What RT says about this:**
The theory acknowledges that sometimes the procedure yields indeterminate results. When multiple interpretations are equally accessible and equally relevant, the speaker has arguably failed to communicate optimally — they haven't made their meaning clear enough for the procedure to pick out a unique winner.

### Failure Mode 3: Deliberate Ambiguity

**How this happens:**
- The speaker intentionally creates multiple accessible interpretations
- They want "plausible deniability" — the ability to retreat to the innocent interpretation if challenged
- Common in flirting, workplace politics, passive-aggressive communication, and humor

**Example:** A colleague says "You're always so... thorough." The pause before "thorough" and the choice of a word that can be either positive (diligent, careful) or negative (slow, overly detailed) is deliberate. They want to communicate "you're too slow" while maintaining the ability to say "I was complimenting you!"

**Why this breaks the procedure:** The procedure assumes the speaker is trying to communicate ONE meaning efficiently. Deliberate ambiguity violates this assumption — the speaker is exploiting the procedure rather than cooperating with it.

### Failure Mode 4: Missing Cultural/Social Knowledge

**How this happens:**
- The "correct" interpretation depends on cultural conventions you don't have
- The speaker assumes these conventions are part of your shared cognitive environment, but they're not

**Example:** In some cultures, declining food multiple times before accepting is a social ritual (the host is expected to insist). In others, a single "no" is taken at face value. If you lack this cultural knowledge, you'll reach the wrong interpretation no matter how carefully you apply the procedure.

**Particular relevance for autistic individuals:**
Some social conventions are acquired through implicit social learning (osmosis from thousands of social interactions). Autistic individuals who have not implicitly absorbed these conventions are working from a different set of contextual assumptions, which can produce systematic misinterpretations that look like "pragmatic deficits" but are actually "contextual knowledge gaps."

### Failure Mode 5: Processing Overload

**How this happens:**
- Multiple conversations happening simultaneously
- Noisy or distracting environment
- Emotional overwhelm reducing cognitive resources
- Fatigue or sensory overload reducing processing capacity
- Complex or rapid speech that outpaces your inference speed

**When this hits:** The RT procedure requires cognitive resources — effort for decoding, context retrieval, inference, and checking. When resources are depleted, the procedure may not complete properly: you might stop too early (landing on a wrong interpretation) or fail to process the utterance at all.

### Recovery Strategies: Repairing After a Wrong Interpretation

Research on conversational repair (Dingemanse et al., 2015) identifies a universal system across human languages for handling communication breakdowns. Here are practical repair strategies:

**1. The Clarification Request**
- "Wait, do you mean [interpretation A] or [interpretation B]?"
- "Sorry, I'm not sure I follow — can you say more about what you mean?"
- "What do you mean by [specific ambiguous word or phrase]?"
- This is the most direct and often most effective repair strategy

**2. The Understanding Check**
- "So you're saying [your interpretation]?"
- "Just to make sure I've got this right — you mean [paraphrase]?"
- This lets the speaker confirm or correct without requiring them to explain from scratch

**3. The Contextual Probe**
- "What made you think of that?" (seeks the speaker's contextual starting point)
- "Is this related to [topic you think might be relevant]?"
- Useful when you have NO idea what interpretation to reach

**4. The Honest Admission**
- "I'm sorry, I'm not sure what you mean by that."
- "I can't tell if you're being serious or joking."
- "I'm bad at reading between the lines — can you spell it out for me?"
- For autistic individuals especially, honest admission is often more effective than guessing. Many non-autistic people appreciate directness and will readily clarify.

**5. The Delayed Processing Strategy**
- "Let me think about that for a moment."
- Taking time to consciously run through the RT procedure rather than feeling pressured to respond instantly
- Autistic adults who take longer processing time often arrive at correct interpretations — the issue is speed, not ability

**6. The Post-Hoc Check**
- After the conversation, if you're unsure about something, following up: "Hey, when you said [X] earlier, what did you mean?"
- This is especially viable in close relationships where you can revisit things

## 4. RT VS. GRICE: A PRACTICAL COMPARISON

### The Frameworks Side by Side

**Grice (1975): The Cooperative Principle and Maxims**
- People in conversation generally cooperate with each other
- Four maxims: Quality (be truthful), Quantity (be informative but not excessively so), Relation (be relevant), Manner (be clear)
- When a maxim is "flouted" (obviously and deliberately violated), the hearer infers an implicature — extra meaning the speaker must intend for the utterance to still be cooperative
- The "working-out schema": "He said X, which violates maxim Y, so he must mean Z to maintain cooperation"

**RT (Sperber & Wilson, 1986/1995): The Relevance Principle**
- There is one principle, not four: speakers aim for optimal relevance (maximize cognitive effects, minimize processing effort)
- No maxims to follow or violate — just the single heuristic of testing interpretations by accessibility and stopping when relevance is satisfied
- No requirement for "cooperation" — the theory works even in hostile or deceptive communication
- Replaces Grice's "mutual knowledge" with "mutual manifestness" (a weaker, more realistic requirement)

### Head-to-Head: Five Utterances Analyzed Both Ways

#### Utterance 1: "I have a meeting at three" (in response to "Can you help me?")

**Gricean analysis:**
- The response appears to violate the **Maxim of Relation** — it doesn't directly address the request
- But assuming cooperation, the speaker must intend it to be relevant
- Working out the implicature: the meeting prevents them from helping → this is an indirect refusal with a reason
- Result: "No, because I have a meeting"

**RT analysis:**
- The literal content (schedule information) is not relevant enough on its own to justify the utterance in response to a request
- Following the path of least effort, the most accessible interpretation that achieves sufficient relevance is: the meeting makes them unavailable → indirect refusal
- Result: "No, because I have a meeting"

**Verdict:** Both frameworks reach the same answer equally efficiently. **Tie.**

#### Utterance 2: "Nice shirt" (sarcastic)

**Gricean analysis:**
- The speaker violates the **Maxim of Quality** (be truthful) — the shirt is obviously not nice
- Since the violation is blatant and deliberate, the speaker must intend to communicate the opposite
- Working out: the speaker means the shirt is not nice → sarcasm
- Grice's account: irony is saying the opposite of what you mean

**RT analysis:**
- The literal interpretation fails the relevance test (saying something obviously false about a stained shirt provides no useful cognitive effects)
- Wilson & Sperber's **echoic account**: the speaker is not simply "saying the opposite of what they mean" — they are *echoing* an expectation or norm ("one should have a nice shirt") and expressing a dissociative attitude toward the gap between norm and reality
- This echoic analysis is richer: it explains WHY the speaker chose sarcasm (to comment on a norm) rather than just THAT they're being sarcastic

**Verdict:** RT provides a **more detailed** explanation. Grice says "they mean the opposite"; RT says "they're echoing a norm and expressing an attitude." For practical purposes, both get you to "they're being sarcastic," but RT tells you more about WHAT the sarcasm is doing. **Slight edge to RT for understanding, but Grice is faster for just detecting sarcasm.**

#### Utterance 3: "Well, that's one way to do it" (hedged criticism)

**Gricean analysis:**
- The speaker may be violating the **Maxim of Quantity** — they're being deliberately less informative than they could be (not saying what they actually think)
- The conspicuous vagueness generates the implicature: "I have a different opinion but I'm not sharing it directly"
- Result: Hedged criticism

**RT analysis:**
- The literal content is trivially true (tautological) → zero cognitive effects → fails relevance test
- The most accessible non-literal interpretation: the speaker is deliberately withholding praise, which by contrast implicates reservations about the method
- The effort of phrasing it indirectly is offset by the cognitive effects of learning about the speaker's disapproval AND their desire to soften it

**Verdict:** RT handles this more elegantly because it doesn't require identifying WHICH maxim was violated — a step that can be confusing when multiple maxims might be in play. RT simply asks "why would someone say something trivially true?" and the answer leads directly to the interpretation. **Edge to RT.**

#### Utterance 4: "Don't you think it's a bit warm in here?" (indirect request)

**Gricean analysis:**
- Violates **Maxim of Relation** — a question about temperature is not directly relevant to the conversational situation unless it leads to action
- Implicature: the speaker wants the temperature addressed → please open the window
- Some Gricean analyses would also invoke **Manner** — the speaker is being unnecessarily indirect

**RT analysis:**
- A literal question about shared temperature perception is low-value — the speaker knows their own experience and can observe the hearer's without asking
- Higher relevance achieved by interpreting as an indirect request: actionable information (change the temperature) provides strong cognitive effects
- The indirectness is explained by politeness considerations: the speaker achieves their goal while minimizing the imposition (they're framing a command as a shared perception question)

**Verdict:** Both work well. RT's advantage: it doesn't require debating which maxim was violated (Relation? Manner? Both?). RT just asks "what interpretation makes this worth saying?" and arrives at the answer directly. **Slight edge to RT for simplicity.**

#### Utterance 5: "I'm fine" (said while clearly upset)

**Gricean analysis:**
- Clear violation of the **Maxim of Quality** — the speaker is saying something false
- But also arguably the **Maxim of Quantity** — they're withholding relevant information about their emotional state
- Working out: they're either (a) denying their state, (b) signaling they don't want to discuss it, or (c) socially performing normalcy
- The analysis gets complicated because multiple maxims seem involved

**RT analysis:**
- Prosodic and contextual cues make the literal interpretation less accessible (contradicted by observable evidence)
- The next most accessible interpretation is shaped by what's manifest in the context: their distress, the social convention of claiming to be fine, and the likely communicative intention (deflecting concern, maintaining composure, or signaling that discussion is unwelcome)
- The specific implicature (which of several "not fine" meanings) is determined by further contextual factors

**Verdict:** RT handles this better because it integrates prosody naturally (prosody affects accessibility of interpretations) whereas Grice's framework focuses on verbal content and maxim violation. The multiple-maxim confusion in Grice's analysis is a practical problem for someone trying to use the framework as a tool. **Clear edge to RT for prosody-dependent utterances.**

### Summary: When to Use Which Framework

| Situation | Better Framework | Why |
|-----------|-----------------|-----|
| Someone says something obviously false | Grice | "They violated Quality (truth)" is simple and direct |
| Someone seems to be giving too much or too little information | Grice | Quantity maxim violations are intuitive |
| Prosody contradicts words | RT | RT integrates prosody naturally; Grice focuses on verbal content |
| Something seems irrelevant on the surface | Either | Both handle "hidden relevance" well |
| Multiple things seem wrong about the utterance | RT | Avoids the "which maxim?" confusion |
| Quick gut-check: "Are they being cooperative?" | Grice | The cooperative principle is a fast diagnostic |
| Deep analysis: "What exactly are they communicating and why?" | RT | RT provides more granular analysis |
| Non-cooperative communication (lies, manipulation, hostility) | RT | RT doesn't require cooperation; Grice does |

### The Case for Knowing Both

The argument: Grice gives you a quick checklist (Is this true? Informative? Relevant? Clear? If not, why not?). RT gives you a powerful inference engine (What interpretation makes this worth saying?). They are complementary tools. Use Grice's maxims as a quick diagnostic ("Something feels off — which maxim was violated?") and RT's procedure for deep analysis ("What exactly does this mean and how do I figure that out?").

## 5. THE RELEVANCE HEURISTIC FOR DAILY USE

### The Problem: The Full Procedure is Too Slow for Real-Time Conversation

The five-step algorithm described in Section 1 is valuable for post-hoc analysis (figuring out what someone meant after the conversation) and for studying specific difficult patterns. But in real-time conversation, you often have less than a second to process an utterance and respond.

### The Solution: Three One-Line Heuristics

These capture the essence of the RT comprehension procedure in forms fast enough for real-time use:

#### Heuristic 1: "What interpretation of this makes it worth saying?"

This is the core of RT distilled to a single question. It captures:
- The assumption that the speaker is trying to be relevant
- The requirement that the interpretation produce useful cognitive effects
- The principle that you should look for the MOST relevant interpretation, not just any interpretation

**When to use:** As your DEFAULT heuristic for any utterance that puzzles you.

**Example application:**
- Someone says: "I see you're working late again."
- Apply heuristic: What makes this worth saying?
  - Just observing that you're at your desk late is trivially obvious (you both know you're there)
  - To be worth saying, it must convey something beyond the observation
  - Possible relevant meanings: concern for you, criticism of your time management, implicit compliment about your dedication, passive-aggressive comment about workload distribution
  - Context (prosody, speaker, relationship, history) selects the most plausible one

#### Heuristic 2: "Why would a rational person say THIS to ME right NOW?"

This heuristic emphasizes three important contextual variables:
- **THIS** (these specific words, not other possible words) — why choose this formulation?
- **ME** (as opposed to someone else) — what do they expect me to know, do, or feel in response?
- **NOW** (at this moment in the conversation/situation) — what makes this the right time to say it?

**When to use:** When someone says something whose PURPOSE is unclear — you understand the words but not why they're telling you.

**Example application:**
- A friend mentions: "Sarah got promoted, by the way."
- Apply heuristic: Why tell ME this NOW?
  - Do they think I'll be happy for Sarah? (sharing good news)
  - Do they think I'll be envious? (gauging my reaction)
  - Was I in competition with Sarah? (warning me)
  - Is this relevant to something we were just discussing? (contextual link)
  - The answer depends on your relationship with Sarah and the conversational flow

#### Heuristic 3: "What is the most USEFUL thing this could mean?"

This heuristic focuses on the cognitive effects side of the relevance equation — what interpretation gives you the most actionable, valuable, or important information?

**When to use:** When the literal meaning seems too trivial to be the point.

**Example application:**
- Your partner says: "The kitchen is a mess."
- Apply heuristic: What's the most useful interpretation?
  - Bare observation (trivially true, not useful)
  - Request for you to clean it (actionable — you now know what to do)
  - Expression of frustration (useful — you now know their emotional state)
  - Indirect criticism of your habits (useful — you now know they've noticed a pattern)
  - The most useful interpretation is probably a combination: frustration + implicit request to clean up

### The Meta-Heuristic: When In Doubt, Ask

All three heuristics sometimes fail to produce a clear answer. When that happens, there is always a fallback: **ask the speaker what they mean.** This is not a failure of the procedure — it is a legitimate and often optimal communicative strategy. Asking for clarification:
- Costs a small amount of social effort
- But guarantees the correct interpretation
- Is far better than acting on the wrong interpretation

For autistic individuals who are building these pragmatic skills: it is always better to ask and know than to guess and be wrong. Most people respond well to honest, non-defensive requests for clarification.

### The Speed-Up Over Time

These heuristics become faster with practice. When you encounter the same type of confusing utterance repeatedly and apply the heuristic, the correct interpretation becomes increasingly accessible. Eventually, many common patterns will become semi-automatic — not because you've stopped using the procedure, but because your store of contextual assumptions (cognitive environment) has grown to include "when people say X in situation Y, they usually mean Z." This is how pragmatic competence develops — through conscious processing that gradually becomes faster and more automatic.

## 6. RT AND DIGITAL COMMUNICATION

### The Problem: Text Strips Away Half the Evidence

In face-to-face communication, the RT comprehension procedure has access to:
- Words (linguistic content)
- Prosody (intonation, stress, rhythm, volume)
- Facial expression
- Body language
- Physical context (shared environment)
- Temporal context (immediate shared experience)

In text-based communication, you lose prosody, facial expression, body language, and often physical/temporal context. The procedure must work with dramatically less input. This makes interpretation harder AND more prone to error.

### How Emoji, Punctuation, and Formatting Serve as Relevance Cues

Digital communication has evolved compensatory systems — new cues that partially replace the lost prosodic and gestural information:

#### Emoji as Prosodic Substitutes

Research (Sasamoto, 2022; Nature Humanities and Social Sciences Communications, 2025) frames emoji through RT as providing ostensive cues that communicate the speaker's emotional state through perceptual resemblance. In RT terms, emoji make certain interpretations more accessible by providing evidence about the speaker's attitude:

- "I got the job!" → Statement + positive attitude
- "I got the job" → Statement without clear attitude marker — wait, is this sarcastic? Genuine? Flat? The absence of an emoji in a context where one would be expected can itself become a cue.

**Seven pragmatic functions of emoji identified in research:**
1. Attitude/emotion signal ("I'm feeling this way")
2. Attitude/emotion intensity enhancer (amplifying the mood of the text)
3. Illocutionary force modifier (changing whether something reads as a joke, a serious statement, etc.)
4. Humor marker
5. Irony marker (crucial: a winking face or upside-down smile can signal "don't take this literally")
6. Turn-taking signal
7. Backchannel device ("I'm listening/acknowledging")

#### Punctuation as Prosody

Research (Holtgraves, 2017; Gunraj et al., 2016) demonstrates that punctuation in digital text has undergone "pragmaticalization" — it now conveys pragmatic/social meaning beyond its grammatical function:

**The "OK" Spectrum:**

| Text | Likely Interpretation | RT Explanation |
|------|----------------------|----------------|
| OK | Neutral acknowledgment | Minimal effort → minimal meaning. Default, unmarked. |
| OK! | Enthusiastic agreement | The exclamation mark adds processing effort, compensated by the cognitive effect of signaling positive attitude/energy |
| Ok | Casual acknowledgment | Lowercase signals informality, relaxed attitude |
| ok | Very casual, slightly dismissive or minimal engagement | Even less effort than "Ok" — the minimalism itself communicates casualness or low investment |
| OK. | Potentially cold, curt, or irritated | The period adds processing effort (research shows periods in texts are read as more abrupt and less sincere). The extra punctuation demands extra cognitive effects → the reader interprets attitude: displeasure, finality, or seriousness |
| Ok. | Noticeably cold/displeased | Combines the formality of the period with the word — reads as deliberate curtness |
| K | Dismissive or annoyed | Minimal effort invested in the response signals minimal investment in the conversation. The abbreviation says "you're not worth typing two characters for" |
| Okay | Somewhat formal, potentially serious | Full spelling where abbreviation is the norm requires extra effort → signals extra meaningfulness |
| Okayyy | Drawn-out, skeptical, or humorous | Letter repetition mimics prosodic lengthening — the written equivalent of saying "okaaaaay" with a raised eyebrow |

**RT explanation for all of these:** Each variation requires a slightly different amount of processing effort and provides slightly different cognitive effects. The RT procedure processes whichever interpretation becomes most accessible based on the specific textual cues. The key insight is that in digital communication, **formatting IS prosody** — it provides the same kind of attitudinal evidence that tone of voice provides in speech.

#### Other Digital Prosodic Cues

- **ALL CAPS**: Reads as shouting or intense emphasis. RT: Extra visual effort → extra cognitive effects (strong emotion, urgency)
- **Ellipsis (...)**: Trailing off, hesitation, something left unsaid. RT: The incompleteness signals that more is meant than is said, directing the reader to infer what follows
- **No punctuation at all**: In many digital contexts, this is the neutral default. RT: Minimal formatting effort = minimal attitudinal information
- **Exclamation marks!!**: Enthusiasm, excitement, emphasis. More marks = more intensity. RT: Extra effort signals extra effects
- **Question marks???**: Confusion, disbelief, or demands for explanation. Multiple = intensified
- **Reaction emojis without text**: The emoji IS the message. RT: The speaker judged that the emoji alone is optimally relevant — any added words would increase effort without proportional effects
- **Read receipts with no response**: The absence of a response is itself communicative (a null ostensive act that makes your confusion/anxiety more manifest)

### When Asynchronous Communication Makes the Procedure Harder

**The Missing Shared Temporal Context Problem:**

In face-to-face conversation, both parties share a temporal context — they know what just happened, what was just said, and what the current situation is. In asynchronous digital communication (email, text messages with delayed responses), this shared context erodes:

1. **Delayed responses change meaning:**
   - An immediate "Sure!" to a dinner invitation → enthusiastic acceptance
   - A "Sure!" sent three hours later → the delay itself becomes a cue (were they reluctant? busy? thinking it over?)
   - The RT procedure must process the delay as additional evidence, which can lead to misinterpretation (maybe they were just in a meeting)

2. **Conversational context shifts:**
   - You send a message at 10 AM about Topic A
   - The recipient reads it at 3 PM, after their context has shifted to Topic B
   - Their response may be colored by a different cognitive environment than the one you sent the message from

3. **Loss of back-channel feedback:**
   - In face-to-face conversation, listeners constantly provide feedback (nods, "uh-huh," facial reactions) that helps speakers adjust in real time
   - In async text, you compose your entire message without knowing the recipient's real-time reactions
   - This means more misunderstandings and fewer opportunities for mid-stream repair

4. **Group chat complexity:**
   - Multiple people responding at different speeds to different messages
   - Context for a given response may be unclear (which message are they responding to?)
   - The RT procedure becomes harder because the relevant "context" is ambiguous

### Digital Communication Strategies for Autistic Individuals

Based on RT principles, several strategies can help:

1. **Be explicit about your attitude:** Since digital text strips away prosodic cues, add them back deliberately. Emoji, punctuation, and explicit attitude markers ("I'm saying this warmly:") reduce the interpretive burden on your reader.

2. **Quote what you're responding to:** In async conversations, clarify context by quoting the specific message you're addressing. This ensures shared context.

3. **Don't over-interpret delays:** Async communication inherently involves variable response times. The delay is often about the recipient's schedule, not their attitude toward you.

4. **Ask about tone when uncertain:** "I can't tell the tone of your message — are you upset or just being brief?" is a perfectly reasonable digital clarification.

5. **Use the RT heuristic on digital text:** "What interpretation of this text makes it worth sending in this context?" is just as applicable to texts as to speech — but you may need to give yourself more time and more benefit of the doubt, because the evidence is thinner.

## 7. CONNECTING BACK: RT AS A LEARNABLE SKILL

### The Neuroscience-Pragmatics Bridge

Research on autistic pragmatic processing (PMC8117104; Frontiers in Psychology, 2021) reveals a crucial finding: autistic adults often DO arrive at correct pragmatic interpretations — they just do so more slowly and with more explicit effort. The RT procedure as described here essentially formalizes what many autistic adults are already doing: conscious, deliberate, step-by-step inference about speaker meaning.

The advantage of having an explicit procedure is:
1. **Reduces cognitive load** — you don't have to reinvent the inferential wheel each time; you have a template
2. **Provides a checklist** — when you're confused, you know exactly which steps to check (Did I miss a contextual cue? Did I stop at the wrong interpretation? Am I missing cultural background knowledge?)
3. **Legitimizes conscious processing** — the RT procedure is what ALL humans do; autistic individuals simply do it more consciously
4. **Enables pattern recognition** — once you've applied the procedure to common utterance types enough times, you build a library of "when people say X in context Y, they usually mean Z"

### The Long Game: From Algorithm to Pattern Library

The RT comprehension procedure is a STARTING tool, not an end state. The goal over time is:

**Phase 1: Conscious Algorithm**
- Apply the five-step procedure explicitly
- This is slow but accurate
- Focus on common confusing patterns (indirect refusals, sarcasm, phatic speech, indirect requests)

**Phase 2: Pattern Recognition**
- After applying the algorithm many times to similar utterances, you start recognizing patterns
- "Oh, this is the 'schedule information as indirect refusal' pattern" — you've seen it before
- Speed increases because you're matching to stored patterns rather than computing from scratch

**Phase 3: Semi-Automatic Processing**
- Common patterns become fast lookups rather than slow computations
- The algorithm runs in the background, only coming to conscious attention for novel or ambiguous utterances
- This is how neurotypical pragmatic processing works — it's just that it developed through implicit learning rather than explicit practice

**Phase 4: Focused Expertise**
- You know your common failure modes (the specific types of utterances you tend to misinterpret)
- You have specific strategies for those failure modes
- Your repair strategies (clarification requests, understanding checks) are well-practiced

### Summary: The RT Toolkit

| Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|------|-----------|----------------|
| Full 5-Step Algorithm | Decode → Context → Compute → Check → Accept/Iterate | Post-hoc analysis, studying new patterns, important conversations |
| Heuristic 1 | "What interpretation makes this worth saying?" | Default real-time heuristic for puzzling utterances |
| Heuristic 2 | "Why would a rational person say THIS to ME right NOW?" | When the purpose of an utterance is unclear |
| Heuristic 3 | "What is the most useful thing this could mean?" | When the literal meaning seems too trivial |
| Grice Quick-Check | "Is this true? Informative? Relevant? Clear?" | Fast diagnostic when something feels "off" |
| Repair Strategy | Ask for clarification | When the heuristics don't produce a clear answer |
| Pattern Library | Stored patterns from previous analysis | Speed up processing of familiar utterance types |
