# Concept Research: Alexithymia — Definition, History & Diagnostic Status

## Purpose

This is the conceptual foundation for the Alexithymia Deep Dive. It establishes:
1. Where the term came from (Sifneos & Nemiah, Boston psychosomatic medicine, late 1960s through 1972).
2. What the original four-feature definition contained, and what was dropped.
3. How the construct evolved through five competing/complementary frames (Sifneos/Nemiah/Taylor/Bagby; Preece's attention-appraisal CBT model; Barrett's constructed-emotion; Bird's interoception-deficit; Hobson 2019 multiroute).
4. Why alexithymia is in neither DSM-5 nor ICD-11, and what that does and doesn't mean.
5. The dimensional vs. categorical question — and why the field has settled on dimensional.
6. Prevalence figures and demographic patterns, with cross-cultural caveats.
7. What alexithymia is *not* — boundary conditions distinguishing it from apathy, suppression, avoidant attachment, depression, and lack of emotion.
8. Why definitional clarity matters for everything downstream in the deep dive: which therapy is recommended depends on which definition is silently in play.

The parent exploration and supplement already cover much of this. This document confirms primary sources, fills targeted gaps (Heidelberg 1976 conference, Beth Israel Hospital Psychosomatic Questionnaire 1973, Preece attention-appraisal model details), and lays out diagram opportunities.

## Components and Their Relationships

### Component 1: Origin (Boston, mid-1960s through 1972)

**Who.** Peter Emanuel Sifneos (1920–2008), Greek-born US psychiatrist at Beth Israel Hospital and Harvard Medical School. John Case Nemiah (1918–2009), US psychiatrist, also at Beth Israel and a key figure in psychosomatic medicine. Working together through the 1960s on cognitive style in psychosomatic patients.

**What they observed.** In samples of patients with persistent psychosomatic conditions (peptic ulcers, chronic pain, asthma), Sifneos and Nemiah noted a consistent cognitive style: patients had extreme difficulty identifying or describing their subjective feelings; their thinking was concrete, externally oriented, and lacking in fantasy or imaginative content. Their associative material in psychotherapy was thin; they could recount what happened to them but not what they felt about it. The clinical impression was that classical insight-oriented psychotherapy did not work with these patients because the verbal-emotional channel was effectively closed.

**The coinage.** In 1972 Sifneos coined "alexithymia" from Greek roots: alpha-privative ἀ- ("not") + λέξις léxis ("word") + θῡμός thȳmós ("disposition, feeling, anger") — literally "no words for feeling." The term first appeared in print in Sifneos' 1972 monograph *Short-Term Psychotherapy and Emotional Crisis* (Harvard University Press), and then more formally in Sifneos (1973), "The Prevalence of 'Alexithymic' Characteristics in Psychosomatic Patients," *Psychotherapy and Psychosomatics* 22, 255–262 ([PubMed 4770536](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/4770536/)). That 1973 paper introduced the Beth Israel Hospital Psychosomatic Questionnaire (BIQ) — the first instrument for the construct, an observer-rated 17-item measure.

**Two pivotal conferences.**
- **1972 — London.** Eleventh European Conference on Psychosomatic Research. First major international airing of the alexithymia concept.
- **1976 — Heidelberg.** Eleventh European Conference on Psychosomatic Research, September 14–17, 1976. The conference where alexithymia became established as a research construct internationally. Nemiah, Freyberger, and Sifneos jointly published the proceedings chapter "Alexithymia: A View of the Psychosomatic Process" (in O.W. Hill, ed., *Modern Trends in Psychosomatic Medicine, Vol. 3*, Butterworths 1976) — the first systematic theoretical statement of the construct beyond the original clinical observations.

**Why it matters.** The construct emerges *from psychosomatic medicine*, not from emotion research, not from neuroscience, not from autism research. This historical fact shapes the original definition (which is loaded toward concrete-thinking, body-symptom-without-affect-label patients) and explains why the parent literature often reads as if alexithymia is most natural in psychosomatic contexts — that's where it was discovered.

### Component 2: The Original Four-Feature Definition (Sifneos/Nemiah)

The original construct as articulated through the 1970s comprised four features, plus a tightly related fifth that has had a more contested history:

1. **Difficulty identifying feelings (DIF).** Confusion between bodily arousal and emotion ("am I anxious or hungry? worried or tired?"). The person detects something — internal disturbance, unease, restlessness — but cannot map it onto a discrete emotion label.

2. **Difficulty describing feelings (DDF).** Even when an emotion is identified, the person cannot put it into language. Words are unavailable, vague, or generic ("bad," "off," "weird"). Conversation about emotion stalls.

3. **Externally-oriented thinking (EOT).** A cognitive style that prefers concrete external detail (events, schedules, practical matters, technical content) over inner reflection. *"I prefer talking to people about their daily activities rather than their feelings"* is a typical TAS-20 EOT item.

4. **Restricted imaginative/fantasy life.** Originally treated as the *fourth* defining feature, alongside DIF and DDF and EOT. Patients showed thin daydreams, sparse symbolic content, low spontaneous fantasy. This was the most psychoanalytically-flavoured criterion (the original Boston group was psychoanalytically trained) and was central to the link between alexithymia and the structure of psychosomatic illness in their original model.

**The "fifth feature" question.** Some historiographies count the constellation as four features (the version above); others split DIF and DDF into separate dimensions and add restricted fantasy as a fifth. Either way, the substantive content is the same. What matters is that **restricted fantasy/imaginative capacity was in the original model** but was dropped from the most-used measure (the 20-item TAS-20, 1994) — the TAS-20 measures only DIF, DDF, and EOT. The Bermond-Vorst Alexithymia Questionnaire (BVAQ, 2001) was developed in part to recover the affective/fantasizing dimensions the TAS-20 lost.

This dropped-feature is *the* live conceptual issue in the field today. Taylor & Bagby (2024)'s "Defense of the Original Conceptualization" ([PMC11555664](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11555664/)) argues that dropping it has impoverished the construct; Preece's attention-appraisal model (2017) explicitly omits it on theoretical grounds.

### Component 3: How the Construct Evolved — Five Theoretical Frames

The deep dive treats this as a forking lineage, not a linear progression. Each frame is published, peer-reviewed, and has active proponents.

#### Frame A — The Original Lineage: Sifneos → Nemiah → Taylor → Bagby

The continuous tradition that traces from Sifneos and Nemiah's clinical observations through Graeme Taylor and R. Michael Bagby's empirical operationalization (the TAS-20, 1994) to their 2024 "Defense" paper. This lineage:
- Treats alexithymia as a multifaceted *deficit in emotion processing* with both cognitive and affective components.
- Views the construct as developmentally rooted in early caregiver–child emotion socialization (with attachment, trauma, and constitutional contributions).
- Defends the inclusion of restricted imaginative/affective dimensions as essential.
- Considers the construct empirically supported by hundreds of clinical and experimental studies across nearly four decades.

Taylor & Bagby (2024) explicitly criticize the attention-appraisal CBT-style reformulation (Frame B below) as a theoretical innovation that has not earned its place clinically.

#### Frame B — The Attention-Appraisal Reformulation (Preece, Becerra et al. 2017)

David Preece, Rodrigo Becerra, and colleagues at the University of Western Australia proposed a cognitive-behavioral reformulation of alexithymia in [Preece et al. 2017, *Personality and Individual Differences*](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0191886917304956). Key tenets:

- Alexithymia is a deficit at the **attention** and **appraisal** stages of the standard cognitive-behavioral emotion-regulation model (the Gross 1998/2014 process model).
- **EOT** corresponds to the *attention* stage (the person fails to attend to their own emotional states).
- **DIF and DDF** correspond to the *appraisal* stage (the person fails to make sense of/label what they have attended to).
- Levels of alexithymia depend on (a) the developmental level of the person's emotion schemas (how well-articulated their internal model of "what counts as anger" or "what counts as sadness" is) and (b) the extent to which they are using emotion-avoidance as a regulation strategy.
- Crucially, Preece's model **does not include** the restricted-imagination/fantasy component. This is the principled split with the Sifneos/Taylor/Bagby lineage.

The model was operationalized in the **Perth Alexithymia Questionnaire (PAQ, 2018)**, which measures DIF and DDF for both positive and negative emotions separately — addressing a known TAS-20 limitation (the TAS-20 covers only negative emotions).

#### Frame C — Constructed Emotion (Barrett 2017)

Lisa Feldman Barrett's theory of constructed emotion ([Barrett 2017, PMC5390700](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5390700/)) reframes emotions as conceptual categories the brain constructs in real time by combining (a) interoceptive sensations, (b) the situational context, and (c) prior learning of emotion concepts. Within this frame, alexithymia is naturally interpreted as **impoverished emotion-concept granularity** — a person whose conceptual repertoire for slicing internal states is thin will categorize them coarsely ("bad," "off") regardless of how rich the underlying interoceptive signal is.

This frame predicts that *granularity-building* — explicit vocabulary work, affect-labelling, expressive writing — should help, because it scaffolds the missing concept structure. It is also the most direct theoretical home for the cross-cultural variability in alexithymia: emotion concepts vary by language and culture, so the construct's measurement is partly a measure of fit between the person's emotion ontology and the test's English-language emotion ontology.

#### Frame D — Interoception-Deficit (Bird, Brewer, Murphy)

The Bird/Brewer/Murphy lineage (Brewer, Cook, Bird 2016 *Royal Society Open Science*; Murphy et al. 2018 *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General*; Brewer, Murphy & Bird 2021 *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*, [PMC8522807](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8522807/)) argues that alexithymia involves a **multidomain failure of interoception** — a generalised problem with detecting and/or interpreting bodily signals across modalities (cardiac, respiratory, gastric, muscular, thermal, taste) — not merely a problem with emotion-specific processing. Their position in 2025 is:

- The deficit may sit at any of three representational levels — *detection*, *recognition*, or *labelling* — and different individuals may be affected at different levels.
- The cardiac heartbeat-counting task (HCT) is methodologically problematic; effects emerge cleanly only when multiple confounds are controlled.
- An *independent* group (Nicholson, Williams, Grainger, Christensen, Calvo-Merino & Gaigg 2018, [PMC6089261](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6089261/)) using HCT and Bayesian analysis published a finding *against* the interoception–alexithymia link. This is a third-party challenge, not an internal walk-back.
- The "alexithymia hypothesis of autism" (Bird & Cook 2013, [PMC3731793](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3731793/)) — that emotional symptoms attributed to autism are largely driven by co-occurring alexithymia — extends from this interoception-deficit account.

#### Frame E — The Multiroute Model (Hobson, Brewer, Catmur, Bird 2019)

The most recent attempt at synthesis comes from within the Bird group itself. [Hobson, Brewer, Catmur & Bird 2019, *Emotion Review* 11(3): 247–261](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1754073919838528) propose a **multiroute model**:

> Alexithymia is the common downstream phenotype of multiple possible upstream impairments. Interoception failure is *one* route. Language/labelling impairment is *another*. Emotion-concept conceptualization may be a *third*. The clinical surface — DIF, DDF, EOT — looks similar across routes, but the underlying causal pathway differs across individuals.

This model is important because it *dissolves* the apparent debate between Frames A through D. It says: each frame may be correct about *some* alexithymics. The same surface trait can arise from different upstream architectures, which is why no single therapy works for everyone, and why subtype-matching matters.

### Component 4: Diagnostic Status — Why Alexithymia Is Not in DSM-5 or ICD-11

**The fact.** Alexithymia is not classified as a mental disorder in either DSM-5 (2013, with DSM-5-TR 2022) or ICD-11 (2019, in force 2022). It has no diagnostic code, no insurance billing path, no formal clinical "diagnosis" status.

**The reason.** A mental disorder, per DSM-5's general definition, is "a syndrome characterized by clinically significant disturbance in an individual's cognition, emotion regulation, or behavior that reflects a dysfunction in the psychological, biological, or developmental processes underlying mental functioning [...] usually associated with significant distress or disability in social, occupational, or other important activities." Alexithymia, as the field has come to understand it, is:

1. **Dimensional, not categorical.** Continuously distributed in the population. There is no natural "kind" cutoff — only pragmatic thresholds (TAS-20 ≥61 is the standard, but is convention, not nature).
2. **A personality trait, not a syndrome.** It does not cluster with discrete symptoms in the way DSM disorders do.
3. **A trans-diagnostic / comorbidity-bridge construct.** It runs *across* diagnostic categories — appearing at elevated rates in autism, PTSD, eating disorders, somatic symptom disorders, substance use, schizophrenia, ADHD, depression — and is therefore better understood as a vulnerability/maintenance factor than as a primary disorder.
4. **Not always associated with distress or disability.** Some people score high on alexithymia and report no significant impairment; the relationship between alexithymia score and quality of life is moderate, not deterministic.

A systematic critique by Salminen et al. and others has argued that alexithymia *should* be included as a personality-level dimension in future revisions, particularly given the dimensional turn in personality-disorder classification (ICD-11 PD trait domains, DSM-5 Section III alternative model). It has not yet been adopted.

**Implications of non-diagnostic status:**
- No insurance code, so therapists can rarely bill specifically for alexithymia treatment; they bill for an associated diagnosis (anxiety, depression, autism, etc.).
- No clinical "you have alexithymia" pronouncement of the kind that comes with major depression or autism. This is partly a *protection* (one less label) and partly a *gap* (without a name, the user's experience can be invalidated as personality, indifference, or moral failing).
- Real research construct nonetheless: thousands of papers, decades of empirical work, and a relatively coherent body of knowledge — but accessed only through self-test or specialist practitioners.

### Component 5: Dimensional vs. Categorical — Why the Field Settled on Dimensional

**Early position.** Sifneos and Nemiah's original framing was implicitly categorical — patients either presented with the alexithymic constellation or did not. Early measurement attempts (the BIQ) treated it as a clinical type.

**The dimensional turn.** As measurement migrated to self-report instruments (especially the TAS-20 from 1994), data accumulated showing that:
- Alexithymia is normally distributed in general population samples.
- The standard cutoff (TAS-20 ≥61) is a pragmatic convention, not a natural break.
- The trait is moderately stable over time (heritability ~30–33%; 11-year test-retest stability supports trait status), but with state fluctuations linked to depression severity and acute distress.
- Bird & Cook (2013)'s "alexithymia hypothesis of autism" explicitly *requires* a dimensional, trans-diagnostic construct — the hypothesis is that alexithymia is a dimensional trait that varies independently of autism and accounts for emotional symptoms otherwise attributed to autism itself.

**The settled position.** Alexithymia is best modeled as a **continuous trait with multiple correlated dimensions** (DIF, DDF, EOT in TAS-20; cognitive vs. affective in BVAQ; positive vs. negative in PAQ). The "alexithymic" cutoff is useful clinically and for reporting prevalence rates, but it does not carve nature at a joint. Recent latent-profile and factor-mixture-modeling work (Schaeuffele et al. 2021; Hogrefe FMM 2025) supports this dimensional reading: profile structures reflect severity along a continuum more than discrete kinds.

This has direct implications for the user's self-classification project: rather than asking "am I alexithymic?", more useful questions are "where on each axis am I (DIF, DDF, EOT, fantasy/affective)?" and "which routes/frames best describe what I notice in myself?".

### Component 6: Prevalence and Demographic Patterns

**General population.** Approximately **10%** of adults score above standard alexithymia cutoffs, with reported ranges from 5% to 13% depending on country, instrument, and threshold ([Salminen et al. 1999, PubMed 10088984](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10088984/); [Mattila 2006](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17084140/)).

**Salminen 1999 Finnish data (the canonical population study).** N=1,933 working-age adults from the Finnish population register. **Overall prevalence: 13%. Men 17%, women 10%.** Higher rates were associated with male gender, advanced age, low educational level, and low socioeconomic status.

**Cross-cultural variation.** East Asian samples consistently show higher mean TAS-20 scores than Western samples. Whether this represents (a) genuinely higher alexithymia prevalence, (b) construct-non-equivalence (the TAS-20 was developed in English and validated against Western emotion ontology), or (c) culturally normative emotional restraint that the TAS-20 misclassifies, is *unresolved*. Ryder, Sunohara, Dere & Chentsova-Dutton (2018) and the Schouten, Boiger, Kirchner-Häusler, Uchida & Mesquita (2020) Belgian-Japanese suppression study both flag this as a serious construct-validity concern.

**Demographic patterns (caveats apply).**
- **Age.** Alexithymia rises modestly with age in non-clinical populations, though direction-of-causation is unclear.
- **Gender.** Men show somewhat higher TAS-20 means than women in most studies, but the absolute meta-analytic gender gap is small and is moderated by culture. Levant's "normative male alexithymia" frames part of this as a socialization effect of restrictive emotional norms in masculine gender development, not a sex-based difference.
- **Education and SES.** Lower educational attainment and lower SES correlate modestly with higher TAS-20 — though again, possible measurement artefact (TAS-20 items lean toward verbal-emotional fluency).

**Clinical populations (much higher rates, deferred to Category 6 for full treatment):** ~50% in autism, ~53% in PTSD, ~30–50% in eating disorders, ~40% in functional neurological disorder, ~42–67% in substance use disorders, elevated in chronic pain, depression, and schizophrenia spectrum.

### Component 7: What Alexithymia Is *Not* — Boundary Conditions

Definitional clarity requires distinguishing alexithymia from neighboring constructs that look similar from the outside but differ in substance.

**Not apathy.** Apathy is *reduced motivation, interest, or emotional engagement* — a deficit in approach behavior and goal-directed activity. Alexithymia is a deficit in *emotion identification and labelling*. An alexithymic person can have strong motivations and full engagement; they just can't tell you (or themselves) what they're feeling about it. Apathy and alexithymia can co-occur (especially in some neurodegenerative profiles), but they dissociate empirically.

**Not emotional suppression.** Suppression is the *deliberate inhibition* of emotional expression — knowing what you feel and choosing not to show it. Alexithymia is the *absence of access to* feeling labels in the first place. The cross-cultural emotion-regulation literature (Soto et al. 2011; Schouten et al. 2020) makes this distinction critical: in interdependent cultures, suppression of expression can coexist with rich internal emotional fluency; that is not alexithymia.

**Not avoidant attachment.** Avoidant/dismissive attachment is a *relational* style — discomfort with closeness, downregulation of attachment-related affect. While avoidant attachment is empirically correlated with alexithymia (especially DIF and DDF in some studies), they are distinct constructs. A securely attached person can be alexithymic; a dismissively attached person can have full emotion access and just not want to share it.

**Not depression.** Depression involves persistent low mood, anhedonia, vegetative symptoms, and cognitive distortions. Alexithymia is a trait-level processing pattern. State alexithymia can rise with depression severity (the score moves up when the person is acutely depressed and partly settles when the depression remits), but the trait-level component is largely independent. Many high-TAS-20 individuals are not depressed.

**Not "lack of emotion."** This is the most important boundary. **Type II alexithymics** (Bermond's classification) report normal or elevated emotional arousal — they have feelings; the feelings are present and physiologically active — but cannot read or articulate them. The phrase "alexithymics don't have emotions" is *wrong* for at least Type II and probably for most clinically significant alexithymia. The more accurate framing: emotions happen; the person's relationship to them is impoverished, fractured, or illegible to self.

### Component 8: Why Definition Matters for the Rest of the Deep Dive

Each of the five frames (A through E above) silently selects different therapy targets:

| Frame | Implicit therapy target | Recommended approach |
|---|---|---|
| A — Sifneos/Taylor/Bagby (original) | Multifaceted deficit; affect + imagination + concrete thinking | Long-term psychotherapy, attachment-informed work, recovering imaginative capacity |
| B — Preece attention-appraisal | Attention deployment + appraisal accuracy | CBT-style emotion-schema training, granularity work, exposure to emotional content |
| C — Barrett constructed emotion | Impoverished emotion concepts | Vocabulary-building, affect-labelling, expressive writing |
| D — Bird interoception-deficit | Interoceptive detection/labelling | Body-scan, MABT, MBSR (with caveats — see Cat 8) |
| E — Hobson multiroute | Depends on which route is dominant in *this* person | Subtype-matched approach; route diagnosis is the first task |

A therapist trained primarily in Frame D will recommend body-focused awareness work. A therapist working from Frame B will run emotion-schema CBT. A therapist working from Frame C will scaffold vocabulary. The user, sitting across from any of them, has no way to evaluate the recommendation without knowing which frame is silently in play — and which frame fits *them*. Category 2 (heterogeneity) builds on this; Category 7 (therapy approaches) operationalizes it; Category 8 (iatrogenic harm) flags the cases where the wrong frame causes harm.

The argument of the deep dive is that the multiroute model (Frame E) is the closest the field has come to an honest synthesis: alexithymia is one downstream phenotype with multiple possible upstream causes. The right frame for a given person is an empirical question about *which* upstream impairment is dominant — and that question shapes everything that follows.

## Diagram Opportunities (5–7 candidates)

### Diagram 1: Historical Timeline (preferred)
**Type.** Horizontal timeline.
**What it shows.** Key milestones in the development of the construct, from origin to present:
- 1960s — Sifneos/Nemiah clinical observations at Beth Israel
- 1972 — Sifneos coins "alexithymia" (Greek etymology shown)
- 1973 — BIQ (Beth Israel Hospital Psychosomatic Questionnaire), first instrument
- 1976 — Heidelberg Conference; Nemiah/Freyberger/Sifneos chapter establishes construct internationally
- 1985 — TAS-26 (original Toronto Alexithymia Scale, 26 items)
- 1994 — TAS-20 (Bagby/Parker/Taylor)
- 2001 — BVAQ (Bermond/Vorst) — restores affective dimension
- 2013 — Bird & Cook "alexithymia hypothesis of autism"
- 2017 — Preece attention-appraisal model + PAQ
- 2017 — Barrett theory of constructed emotion
- 2018 — Murphy/Bird multidomain interoception deficit
- 2018 — Nicholson et al. independent challenge
- 2019 — Hobson multiroute model
- 2024 — Taylor & Bagby "Defense" paper
**Why it matters.** Lets the user see the construct as a historical trajectory with branching theoretical commitments, not as a settled definition.

### Diagram 2: The Four (or Five) Features Visualised
**Type.** Schematic block diagram showing the four core features (DIF, DDF, EOT, restricted fantasy) and their TAS-20 mapping.
**What it shows.**
- Four feature blocks (DIF, DDF, EOT, restricted-fantasy) as parallel components.
- Arrows from each feature block to whether it is captured by the TAS-20 (DIF=YES, DDF=YES, EOT=YES, fantasy=NO).
- Side annotation: "BVAQ recovers fantasy/emotionalising"; "PAQ adds positive-emotion versions of DIF/DDF".
- Highlight that EOT is psychometrically weakest of the three TAS-20 facets.
**Why it matters.** Visualizes the gap between the original construct and the dominant measurement instrument.

### Diagram 3: Diagnostic-Status Comparison
**Type.** Two-column comparison or contrast chart.
**What it shows.**
- Left column — properties of typical DSM-5 categorical disorder (e.g., MDD): formal diagnostic code, inclusion/exclusion criteria, syndrome (multiple symptoms), threshold (5+ of 9 items), insurance billing, "diagnosis" recognition.
- Right column — alexithymia: dimensional/trait, no diagnostic code, no clear threshold (cutoffs are pragmatic), trans-diagnostic, no insurance billing, research construct only.
- Bottom: "Why alexithymia is not in DSM-5/ICD-11" with three reasons (dimensional, trait, trans-diagnostic).
**Why it matters.** Clarifies what it means that alexithymia is "real" but "not a diagnosis."

### Diagram 4: Dimensional Distribution with TAS-20 Cutoff
**Type.** Bell-curve / population-distribution chart with severity gradient.
**What it shows.**
- Population distribution of TAS-20 scores (approximately normal).
- Three regions marked:
  - Score <52 = "non-alexithymic" (~70–75% of population)
  - Score 52–60 = "borderline" or moderate (~15–20%)
  - Score ≥61 = "alexithymic" by standard cutoff (~10%)
- Annotation: "Cutoffs are pragmatic, not natural"; "Recent LPA/FMM work supports a severity continuum, not discrete classes."
- Optional overlay: cross-cultural shift (East Asian samples show distribution shifted right).
**Why it matters.** Makes the dimensional vs. categorical question concrete and visual.

### Diagram 5: What Alexithymia Is *Not* — Boundary Diagram
**Type.** Central node ("Alexithymia") surrounded by "Not X" satellites with brief disambiguation.
**What it shows.**
- Center: Alexithymia (DIF + DDF + EOT, optional fantasy)
- Surrounding boundary terms:
  - **Not apathy** — apathy = reduced motivation/engagement
  - **Not suppression** — suppression = inhibiting expression of known feelings
  - **Not avoidant attachment** — avoidant = relational style with attachment affect
  - **Not depression** — depression = mood/vegetative syndrome
  - **Not lack of emotion** — Type II alexithymics have feelings, just can't read them
- Each satellite has a short distinguishing principle.
**Why it matters.** Inoculates the user against common misunderstandings — including the "alexithymics don't feel" misconception — that have direct identity and therapy implications.

### Diagram 6: Theoretical Lineage Map (the five frames)
**Type.** Branch/family-tree diagram.
**What it shows.**
- Root: Sifneos/Nemiah (clinical-psychosomatic origin, 1960s–1970s).
- Five branches:
  - Frame A: Taylor/Bagby (continuous tradition, defended 2024)
  - Frame B: Preece attention-appraisal (CBT reformulation, 2017)
  - Frame C: Barrett constructed emotion (2017)
  - Frame D: Bird/Brewer/Murphy interoception deficit (2016+)
  - Frame E: Hobson et al. multiroute (2019, synthesis)
- Each branch includes:
  - Implicit therapy recommendation
  - Implicit measurement instrument fit (TAS-20, BVAQ, PAQ, etc.)
  - Strength of empirical base
- Hobson multiroute shown as *encompassing* the others (each is a route within the multiroute model).
**Why it matters.** This is *the* diagram that lets the user evaluate any therapy claim in the deep dive — by showing which frame the claim sits in, and what the alternatives are.

### Diagram 7 (optional, lower priority): Etymology Card
**Type.** Compact graphic showing the Greek etymology.
**What it shows.**
- ἀ- (a-) = not / lack of
- λέξις (léxis) = word
- θῡμός (thȳmós) = disposition / feeling
- Combined: "no words for feeling"
**Why it matters.** A small, beautiful introductory hook that anchors the historical section.
