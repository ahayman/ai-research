# Category 15: Prosodic Pragmatics — Contrastive Stress as a Meaning-Selection System

## Research Document: Theoretical Foundations

---

## 1. WHAT CONTRASTIVE STRESS IS

### 1.1 Definition

Contrastive stress is the placement of prosodic prominence — a combination of higher pitch (fundamental frequency / F0), increased duration, and greater intensity (loudness) — on a specific word within a sentence to highlight it against a set of alternatives. It is a suprasegmental feature, meaning it operates above the level of individual speech sounds, modifying the pragmatic meaning of whole utterances by directing the listener's attention to one particular element.

The term "prosodic prominence" is technically more precise than "stress" here, because what listeners perceive as "stress" in running speech is actually a cluster of acoustic cues working together:

- **Pitch movement**: The fundamental frequency (F0) rises or falls sharply on the stressed word. In the ToBI (Tones and Breaks Indices) transcription system, this is typically annotated as a pitch accent — either H* (a simple high target) or L+H* (a low-to-high rise, often associated with contrastive or emphatic focus). The distinction between H* and L+H* is one of the most debated aspects of the ToBI framework, with inter-annotator agreement studies often finding the two categories difficult to distinguish reliably.
- **Duration**: The stressed syllable is lengthened. Bigger articulatory movements require more time, which results in stressed syllables being measurably longer than unstressed syllables.
- **Intensity**: Increased respiratory effort pushes more air through the glottis, causing a measurable increase in amplitude (loudness).
- **Vowel quality**: In English, unstressed vowels tend to reduce toward schwa (/ə/), while stressed vowels maintain their full quality. When a word receives contrastive stress, its vowels are produced with particularly clear, full articulation.

These cues interact in complex ways. Research on Canadian English focus marking found that focus induced lengthening of focused constituents but not necessarily enhancement of intensity or F0, while focus-adjacent constituents were weakened in terms of duration, intensity, and F0. The relative importance of any one cue depends on the values of the others, and listeners use the same acoustic information differently depending on context (e.g., intensity is a strong cue in declaratives but less reliable in questions, where duration differences become more important).

### 1.2 The Core Mechanism: Focus Activates Alternatives

The fundamental insight about contrastive stress is this: **when you stress a word, you implicitly contrast it with other words that could have appeared in that position**. Stress does not simply make a word louder — it activates a set of alternatives in the listener's mind.

This is the difference between mere emphasis and contrastive focus. If someone says "I bought a RED car," the stress on "red" doesn't just mean "pay attention to the color." It means "the color is RED as opposed to some other color you might have expected — blue, black, white." The stressed word is being selected from a set of contextually plausible alternatives.

Experimental evidence confirms this activation mechanism. Research shows that contrastive accenting causes listeners to activate alternatives to focused words and suppress non-contrastive semantic associates. The process works through a selection mechanism: initially all semantic associates are activated as part of general lexical activation, and then the contrastive accent causes non-contrastive associates to be suppressed as not relevant to the alternative set.

### 1.3 Rooth's (1992) Alternative Semantics

The most influential formal theory of how focus works is Mats Rooth's Alternative Semantics, developed in his 1985 dissertation and refined in his 1992 paper "A Theory of Focus Interpretation" (published in Natural Language Semantics). This paper has accumulated over 2,600 citations and remains foundational to the field.

**The core idea**: Every expression in a sentence has two semantic values:

1. **Ordinary semantic value**: The regular meaning of the expression.
2. **Focus semantic value**: A set of alternatives — the set of propositions (or other semantic objects) that you get by replacing the focused element with other contextually relevant items of the same type.

For example, consider: "JOHN ate the cake" (with focus on "John"):

- **Ordinary value**: the proposition that John ate the cake
- **Focus value**: the set {that Mary ate the cake, that Bill ate the cake, that Sue ate the cake, ...} — all propositions of the form "X ate the cake" where X is a contextually relevant individual

The focus semantic value is a set of propositions that differ from the actual proposition only in the focused element. This is what it means for focus to "activate alternatives."

Rooth (1992) attempts to unify the effect of focus across a variety of linguistic contexts — question-answer pairs, focus-sensitive particles like "only" and "even," contrastive contexts, and others. He uses the question-answer congruence property (the observation that the prosody of an answer constrains which questions it can felicitously answer) as a guide for what the theory needs to capture.

### 1.4 The Relationship Between Stress, Focus, and Information Structure

These three concepts form a chain:

- **Stress** (phonology): the acoustic prominence placed on a word — the physical signal
- **Focus** (semantics/pragmatics): the part of the sentence that introduces alternatives — the meaning dimension
- **Information structure** (discourse): the organization of information into what's new vs. what's given, what's being asserted vs. what's presupposed — the discourse function

Stress is the *signal* that marks focus. Focus is the *semantic category* that structures alternatives. Information structure is the *discourse-level organization* that focus contributes to. The three levels interact but are conceptually distinct.

## 2. THE "I NEVER SAID SHE STOLE MY MONEY" DEMONSTRATION

### 2.1 Seven Words, Seven Meanings

This sentence is perhaps the most famous demonstration of contrastive stress in English. It contains seven words, and stressing each one produces a different meaning — because each stress pattern activates a different set of alternatives and thereby answers a different implicit question.

**Stressed word → Meaning → Implicit question answered → Alternatives activated**

1. **"*I* never said she stole my money"**
   - Meaning: Someone else said it — not me. I'm denying that I was the one who made this claim.
   - Question answered: "Who said she stole your money?"
   - Alternatives: {I, you, he, they, someone} — the set of possible claimants

2. **"I *NEVER* said she stole my money"**
   - Meaning: I absolutely, categorically did not say it. This is an emphatic denial — I'm denying the claim has ever been made by me at any time.
   - Question answered: "Did you ever say she stole your money?"
   - Alternatives: {never, once, sometimes, always} — the set of possible frequencies/occasions

3. **"I never *SAID* she stole my money"**
   - Meaning: I didn't *say* it — but I may have implied it, thought it, written it, or communicated it through some other channel. The verbal act of saying is being contrasted with other communicative acts.
   - Question answered: "How did you communicate that she stole your money?"
   - Alternatives: {said, implied, suggested, wrote, thought, hinted} — the set of possible communicative acts

4. **"I never said *SHE* stole my money"**
   - Meaning: I said someone stole my money, but not that *she* specifically was the one who did it. The identity of the thief is being corrected.
   - Question answered: "Who did you say stole your money?"
   - Alternatives: {she, he, they, someone else} — the set of possible perpetrators

5. **"I never said she *STOLE* my money"**
   - Meaning: She did something with my money, but I didn't say she *stole* it. Maybe she borrowed it, misplaced it, lost it. The nature of the action is being corrected.
   - Question answered: "What did you say she did with your money?"
   - Alternatives: {stole, borrowed, lost, misplaced, moved} — the set of possible actions

6. **"I never said she stole *MY* money"**
   - Meaning: I said she stole money, but not *my* money specifically — maybe she stole someone else's money. The ownership is being corrected.
   - Question answered: "Whose money did you say she stole?"
   - Alternatives: {my, your, his, their, the company's} — the set of possible owners

7. **"I never said she stole my *MONEY*"**
   - Meaning: She stole something of mine, but not money specifically. Maybe she stole my ideas, my time, my belongings. The object of the theft is being corrected.
   - Question answered: "What did you say she stole?"
   - Alternatives: {money, jewelry, car, identity, ideas} — the set of possible stolen items

### 2.2 Why This Works: The Mechanism

Each stress pattern does three things simultaneously:

1. **Marks one word as the focus**: The stressed word carries the new, informative, or corrected content.
2. **Marks everything else as background**: The unstressed words are treated as given or presupposed information — they're the frame within which the focus operates.
3. **Activates alternatives to the focused word**: The listener's mind generates a set of other items that could fill the focused position.

This is precisely what Rooth's Alternative Semantics predicts. Each stress pattern generates a different focus semantic value — a different set of alternative propositions — which in turn creates a different pragmatic meaning.

### 2.3 Practical Significance

This demonstration illustrates that English speakers constantly perform a sophisticated computation every time they hear a sentence: they identify which word is stressed, compute what alternatives that stress activates, and derive the intended meaning from the contrast between the focused element and its alternatives. This computation happens automatically and unconsciously for most speakers — but it is a genuine information-processing task, and it can be made explicit and systematic.

## 3. FOCUS AND INFORMATION STRUCTURE

### 3.1 New vs. Given Information

A fundamental principle of information structure is the **given-new distinction**:

- **Given (old) information**: Content that is already established in the discourse — either because it was mentioned before, is visible in the physical context, or is inferrable from the situation. Given information tends to be **unstressed** (deaccented).
- **New information**: Content that the speaker introduces as not yet established in the discourse. New information tends to be **stressed** (accented).

As Halliday (1967) characterized it, given information is "that which is presented by the speaker as recoverable" to the listener, while new information is "that which is not recoverable." The information focus, marked by the main change of pitch on the tonic syllable, indicates the most salient new information.

Experimental evidence confirms that listeners are sensitive to this mapping: listeners verify words more quickly when given information is deaccented and new information is accented. Violations — accenting given information or deaccenting new information — cause processing delays and pragmatic deviance.

### 3.2 Focus and Background

Focus and background form a partition of the sentence:

- **Focus**: The part of the sentence that carries the new, informative, contrastive, or otherwise highlighted content. This is what the sentence "is about" informationally.
- **Background (presupposition)**: The part that's presupposed, already established, or taken for granted. This provides the frame within which the focus is interpreted.

Following Jackendoff (1972), the background can be thought of as a proposition with a variable in the position of the focused element. For "JOHN ate the cake" (focus on John), the background is "X ate the cake" — an open proposition with a variable. The focus fills that variable: X = John.

### 3.3 Focus Determines What Question a Sentence Answers

One of the most important insights from information structure research is that **the focus structure of a sentence determines which question it can felicitously answer**. This is the principle of **question-answer congruence** (Jackendoff 1972, Rooth 1992, Roberts 1996):

- **"JOHN ate the cake"** answers: "Who ate the cake?"
  - Focus: John. Background: someone ate the cake.
  
- **"John ATE the cake"** answers: "What did John do to the cake?"
  - Focus: ate. Background: John did something to the cake.
  
- **"John ate the CAKE"** answers: "What did John eat?"
  - Focus: the cake. Background: John ate something.
  
- **"JOHN ate the CAKE"** (double focus) answers: "Who ate what?"
  - Focus: John and cake. Background: someone ate something.

This is why stress matters so much for pragmatic meaning: it constrains the discourse context in which the sentence makes sense. If someone asks "What did John eat?" and you answer "JOHN ate the cake" (with stress on John), there's a mismatch — you're providing focus on the wrong element. This mismatch creates a sense of pragmatic infelicity, a feeling that the answer doesn't quite address the question.

**Craige Roberts' (1996) Question Under Discussion (QUD) framework** formalizes this insight. Roberts proposed that discourse is organized around implicit or explicit questions that interlocutors are trying to answer. The QUD is the immediate topic of discussion, and it proffers a set of relevant alternatives. Focus placement signals to the listener which QUD the speaker is addressing. As Roberts puts it, "assertions, like questions, are conventionally associated with a set of alternatives, although these alternatives are presupposed by the prosody rather than proffered."

### 3.4 Broad Focus vs. Narrow Focus

Focus can be **broad** (spanning a large constituent) or **narrow** (targeting a single word):

**Broad focus**: No specific word is singled out for special prominence. The nuclear accent falls on the last accentable constituent by default. This is the "neutral" or "all-new" reading.

- "John ate the CAKE" with default stress can be broad focus (everything is new) — answering "What happened?"
- Or it can be narrow focus on "cake" — answering "What did John eat?"

These two readings are prosodically identical in English. This is a key property: **broad focus and late narrow focus have identical accent patterns** — the pitch accent is aligned with the last accentable constituent within an intonation contour.

**Narrow focus**: A specific word receives prominence, and material after the focus is deaccented. This is unambiguous — when a word that isn't the default stress location gets stressed, the listener knows it's narrow focus.

- "JOHN ate the cake" can only be narrow focus on John — it cannot be broad focus because the stress is in a non-default position.

Under narrow focus, the pitch range of the focused word expands, the pitch range of post-focus syllables is suppressed, and pre-focus syllables are largely unaffected. Words following narrow focus are deaccented in English.

**Selkirk's (1984, 1995) Focus Projection Theory** explains how this works syntactically. The Basic Focus Rule states that an accented word is F-marked (focus-marked). F-marking can then "project" upward through the syntactic tree: F-marking of a syntactic head licenses F-marking of the entire phrase, and F-marking of an internal argument licenses F-marking of the head. This means that stress on the object in "John ate the CAKE" can mark as focused either just the accented word, the VP, or the entire sentence — depending on context.

## 4. FOCUS-SENSITIVE PARTICLES

### 4.1 What Makes a Particle "Focus-Sensitive"

Focus-sensitive particles are words whose interpretation **literally depends on which other word in the sentence is stressed**. They don't just interact with focus — their meaning is incomplete without knowing what the focus is. The phenomenon is known in formal semantics as "association with focus."

These particles quantify over the set of alternatives activated by focus. Three main classes exist:

1. **Exclusive particles** ("only," "just," "merely"): Assert that the focused alternative is the *only* one for which the proposition holds.
2. **Additive particles** ("also," "too," "as well"): Assert that the proposition holds for the focused alternative *in addition to* some other alternative.
3. **Scalar particles** ("even"): Assert that the focused alternative is the *least expected/likely* one for which the proposition holds, presupposing it holds for more expected alternatives too.

### 4.2 "Only" — The Prototypical Focus-Sensitive Particle

"Only" is the most studied focus-sensitive particle because its interaction with focus produces clear truth-conditional differences — the sentences are literally true or false in different situations depending on which word is stressed.

- **"I only ATE the cake"** (focus on "ate")
  - Meaning: Eating is the only thing I did to the cake. I didn't bake it, buy it, decorate it, or serve it.
  - Alternatives excluded: {baked, bought, decorated, served, ...}
  - Truth conditions: For all alternatives X ≠ ate: I did not X the cake.

- **"I only ate the CAKE"** (focus on "cake")
  - Meaning: The cake is the only thing I ate. I didn't eat the cookies, the pie, or the appetizers.
  - Alternatives excluded: {cookies, pie, appetizers, ...}
  - Truth conditions: For all alternatives X ≠ cake: I did not eat the X.

- **"Only I ate the cake"** / **"I only... [with stress on I]"** (focus on "I")
  - Meaning: I am the only person who ate the cake. Nobody else did.
  - Alternatives excluded: {Mary, John, Bill, ...}
  - Truth conditions: For all alternatives X ≠ I: X did not eat the cake.

These are not just different emphases — they are different *propositions* with different truth conditions. The first could be true in a world where you ate both cake and cookies; the second cannot be. This is why Beaver and Clark describe "only" as conventionally associating *exclusively* with focus — the focused element determines the domain of quantification and thereby affects truth conditions.

### 4.3 "Even" — Scalar Focus Sensitivity

"Even" presupposes that the focused element is the **least likely** member of the alternative set for which the proposition holds. It induces a scale of likelihood on the alternatives and assigns the focused element the lowest value.

- **"Even JOHN passed the test"** (focus on "John")
  - Presupposes: John was the least likely person to pass.
  - Implicates: If even John passed, then certainly Mary, Bill, and everyone more likely than John also passed.
  - Alternative set: {John, Mary, Bill, Sue, ...} ordered by likelihood of passing
  - John is at the bottom of this likelihood scale.

- **"John even PASSED the test"** (focus on "passed")
  - Presupposes: Passing was the least likely outcome for John.
  - Implicates: John also did other things with respect to the test (showed up, tried, finished) — and passing was the most surprising of these.
  - Alternative set: {showed up, tried, finished, passed} ordered by likelihood
  - Passing is at the bottom.

- **"John passed even the MATH test"** (focus on "math")
  - Presupposes: The math test was the hardest / least likely to be passed.
  - Implicates: John also passed other, easier tests.
  - Alternative set: {English test, history test, math test} ordered by difficulty

The scalar presupposition is what makes "even" distinctive. Where "only" excludes alternatives, "even" *includes* them and orders them by expectation. Recent research has debated whether the relevant scale is always based on likelihood, or whether it can be based on other notions like "helpfulness" in context.

### 4.4 "Just" — Exclusive/Minimizing Focus Sensitivity

"Just" operates similarly to "only" but often carries an additional minimizing or dismissive connotation:

- **"I just LOOKED at it"** (focus on "looked")
  - Meaning: Looking is all I did. I didn't touch it, move it, or break it. The action was minimal.
  - Defense/minimization: The speaker is framing their action as the least consequential alternative.

- **"I just looked at IT"** (focus on "it")
  - Meaning: I looked only at that thing, not at anything else.
  - Narrowing: The speaker is restricting the object of their action.

- **"I JUST looked at it"** (focus on "just" itself, with temporal reading)
  - Meaning: I looked at it very recently / a moment ago.
  - Temporal: "just" takes on its temporal meaning rather than its exclusive meaning.

### 4.5 "Also" / "Too" — Additive Focus Sensitivity

Additive particles presuppose that the proposition already holds for at least one alternative to the focused element:

- **"JOHN also came to the party"** (focus on "John")
  - Meaning: John came, and so did at least one other person. John is being added to the set of people who came.
  - Presupposes: Someone other than John (e.g., Mary) came.

- **"John also CAME to the party"** (focus on "came")
  - Meaning: Coming to the party is one of the things John did, in addition to something else (e.g., he also brought a gift, also helped clean up).
  - Presupposes: John did at least one other thing relating to the party.

- **"John also came to the PARTY"** (focus on "party")
  - Meaning: The party is one of the events John came to, in addition to other events.
  - Presupposes: John came to at least one other event.

### 4.6 Theoretical Significance

Focus-sensitive particles provide some of the strongest evidence that focus is a genuine semantic phenomenon, not just a matter of emphasis or salience. The truth conditions of sentences with "only" depend on focus placement. The presuppositions of sentences with "even" depend on focus placement. These are facts about meaning, not just about pragmatic highlighting.

Beaver and Clark's work "Sense and Sensitivity" provides a comprehensive typology, arguing that not all focus-sensitive operators work the same way. Some (like "only") conventionally associate with focus and generate truth-conditional effects. Others (like "always") may appear focus-sensitive but actually work through different mechanisms.

## 5. STRESS AND PRESUPPOSITION

### 5.1 How Focus Carves Presupposition from Assertion

When you place contrastive stress on a word, everything in the sentence *except* the focused word becomes part of the presupposition — the background information taken for granted. The focused word is the asserted, informative content.

This means that moving the focus in a sentence changes what is presupposed:

- **"Did YOU do this?"** (focus on "you")
  - Presupposes: Someone did this. (The existence of the action and its nature are taken for granted.)
  - Asserts/Questions: Whether it was *you* specifically, as opposed to other possible agents.
  - The speaker already knows "this" was done; they're trying to identify who.

- **"Did you DO this?"** (focus on "do")
  - Presupposes: The relevant situation exists and involves you and "this" in some relation.
  - Asserts/Questions: Whether the relation is *doing* as opposed to some other action (planning it, knowing about it, seeing it).
  - The speaker is probing the nature of your involvement.

- **"Did you do THIS?"** (focus on "this")
  - Presupposes: You did something.
  - Asserts/Questions: Whether the thing you did is *this particular thing* as opposed to something else.
  - The speaker knows you acted; they're trying to identify what.

### 5.2 Presupposition in Declaratives

The same mechanism operates in declarative sentences:

- **"MARY broke the vase"** (focus on Mary)
  - Presupposes: Someone broke the vase.
  - Asserts: It was Mary (not John, not the cat).
  - This presupposition is sometimes called an **existential presupposition** — the existence of a vase-breaker is presupposed.

- **"Mary BROKE the vase"** (focus on broke)
  - Presupposes: Mary did something to the vase.
  - Asserts: What she did was break it (not move it, not clean it).

- **"Mary broke the VASE"** (focus on vase)
  - Presupposes: Mary broke something.
  - Asserts: The thing she broke was the vase (not the plate, not the window).

### 5.3 Exhaustive Presuppositions and Cleft Sentences

Focus interacts with presupposition in particularly interesting ways in cleft sentences:

- **"It was JOHN who ate the cake"**
  - Existential presupposition: Someone ate the cake.
  - Exhaustive assertion: It was John and nobody else.

The exhaustive component of clefts is debated — some researchers treat it as a semantic entailment, others as a presupposition, and still others as a pragmatic implicature. Research shows that the exhaustivity of cleft sentences is harder to cancel than the exhaustivity of plain focus, suggesting it may be more deeply encoded in the grammar.

### 5.4 Focus and Presupposition Triggers

Focus placement can interact with how presupposition triggers are interpreted. The Cummins & Rohde (2015) "Evoking Context with Contrastive Stress" study demonstrated that focus placement influences three different pragmatic phenomena: scalar implicature, presupposition, and coreference. Their argument is that focus operates on these phenomena indirectly — by providing the listener with information about the likely Question Under Discussion (QUD), which then constrains interpretation across multiple pragmatic dimensions simultaneously.

## 6. STRESS AND SCALAR IMPLICATURE

### 6.1 How Stress Affects "Some"

Scalar implicature is the pragmatic inference that a weaker term (like "some") was chosen instead of a stronger term (like "all"), implicating that the stronger term doesn't apply. The classic example:

> "Some students passed the exam" → implicates → "Not all students passed"

This implicature arises because if all students had passed, the speaker should have said "all." By choosing "some," the speaker implicates "not all."

Prosodic focus interacts with this process in a measurable way:

- **"SOME students passed"** (stress on "some")
  - The stress on the scalar term "some" strongly activates the alternative "all."
  - This strongly implicates "not all" — the scalar implicature is robust.
  - The speaker is drawing attention to the scalar choice itself, emphasizing that they're saying "some" rather than "all."

- **"Some students PASSED"** (stress on "passed")
  - The focus is on the passing, not on the quantity.
  - The scalar implicature ("not all") is weaker or absent.
  - The sentence focuses on what happened (passing) rather than how many.

### 6.2 Experimental Evidence

Research has confirmed that placing focus on the weak scalar term increases the rate at which listeners derive scalar implicatures. Placing prosodic prominence on a scalar term calls particular attention to the possibility that other items on the scale (such as "all") could have been used instead. The rate of scalar implicatures increases when the scalar term is prosodically prominent.

The mechanism involves the L+H* prosodic contour (a contrastive pitch accent). A memory delay experiment revealed that contrastive intonation (L+H*) makes an exhaustive interpretation (i.e., "not all") equally available as the overt word "only," though in immediate real-time processing, the implicature in bare focus conditions is somewhat delayed relative to inferences triggered by explicit operators like "only" and "also."

### 6.3 Broader Pattern

The general principle: **stress on a scalar term strengthens the scalar implicature because it increases the salience of the stronger alternatives on the scale**. This is exactly what Alternative Semantics predicts — focus activates alternatives, and when the alternatives include stronger scalar items, the implicature that those stronger items don't apply becomes more salient.

This connects to the broader insight that contrastive focus makes alternatives salient, and salient alternatives provide the raw material for pragmatic inference. Focus doesn't just highlight — it provides the inferential machinery for computing what was *not* said.

## 7. THE DEFAULT STRESS PATTERN (NUCLEAR STRESS RULE)

### 7.1 The Rule

In neutral, unmarked English sentences, the **main sentence stress (nuclear accent) falls on the last content word** — specifically, on the stressed syllable of the last accentable constituent in the sentence. This is the **Nuclear Stress Rule** (NSR), formulated by Chomsky & Halle (1968) in "The Sound Pattern of English."

The NSR assigns primary stress to the rightmost primary-stressed syllable in a phrasal constituent and reduces all other stresses by one degree. Chomsky and Halle formulated it as a cyclic rule that applies recursively through the syntactic structure.

Examples of default (neutral) stress:

- "John ate the **CAKE**" — stress on the last content word
- "Mary lives in a house on the **HILL**" — stress on the last content word
- "The students are studying for their **EXAMS**" — stress on the last content word

### 7.2 Compounds vs. Phrases

An important exception: **compounds** tend to be stressed on the *first* element, while phrases are stressed on the *last*:

- "**BLACK**board" (compound — a type of board) vs. "black **BOARD**" (phrase — a board that's black)
- "**GREEN**house" (compound — a plant-growing structure) vs. "green **HOUSE**" (phrase — a house that's green)

This compound vs. phrase stress distinction is itself meaningful, showing that even at the word/phrase level, stress placement carries semantic information.

### 7.3 Why the Default Matters: Marked vs. Unmarked Focus

The default stress pattern is crucial because **any deviation from it carries pragmatic meaning**. When a speaker places stress on a word that wouldn't normally be stressed, the listener knows the speaker is doing something intentional — they're marking narrow focus, making a contrast, or correcting a misunderstanding.

- "John ate the CAKE" → could be neutral (broad focus) or narrow focus on "cake" — this is the **default** position, so it's ambiguous.
- "JOHN ate the cake" → can ONLY be narrow focus on "John" — this deviates from default, so it's **marked** and unambiguous.

This asymmetry is important: **default stress is informationally ambiguous; non-default stress is informationally unambiguous**. When you hear stress in a non-default position, you know exactly what the speaker is focusing. When you hear stress in the default position, you need context to determine whether it's broad or narrow focus.

### 7.4 How to Detect Non-Default Stress

For a listener, the practical skill is recognizing when stress has been placed in a non-default position. The cues are:

1. **A word earlier in the sentence has unusual prominence**: If the main pitch accent is on an early word rather than a late one, that's marked.
2. **Late words are deaccented**: After the focused word, subsequent words lose their normal pitch accents and become prosodically flat.
3. **The overall pitch contour is front-loaded**: The pitch movement (rise or fall) happens earlier in the sentence than expected.

When these cues are present, the listener should identify the stressed word as the focus and interpret the sentence accordingly.

## 8. DEACCENTING AND ITS PRAGMATIC EFFECTS

### 8.1 What Deaccenting Is

Deaccenting is the removal or reduction of prosodic prominence from a word that would normally receive it. When a word that would typically carry a pitch accent is produced without one — with reduced pitch movement, shorter duration, lower intensity, and sometimes reduced vowel quality — it is said to be "deaccented."

The core principle: **deaccenting signals that the word conveys given, old, or presupposed information**. The word is recoverable from context — either because it was mentioned before, is visible in the situation, or is inferrable.

### 8.2 Deaccenting in Discourse

Consider this exchange:

> A: "I bought a new car."  
> B: "What color is the car?"  
> A: "The car is RED."

In A's second utterance, "car" would typically be deaccented (reduced prominence) because it's given information — already established in the discourse. "Red" gets the focus accent because it's the new information answering B's question. If A produced the sentence with equal stress on "car" and "red," it would sound odd — as if A were re-introducing "car" as a new concept.

A more compressed example:

> "I bought a RED car. Then I sold the car."

In the second sentence, "car" is deaccented because it was just mentioned. The pitch accent falls on "sold" because the selling is the new event.

### 8.3 Anticipated vs. Actual Deaccenting

Research on "anticipatory deaccenting" shows that speakers sometimes deaccentuate words even before those words have been explicitly mentioned, if the referent is contextually predictable. Listeners are also sensitive to this — they process deaccented given information faster than accented given information, and accented new information faster than deaccented new information.

### 8.4 When Deaccenting is Unexpected: Pragmatic Consequences

Shifting prominence away from the end of a sentence when it is not motivated, or failing to do so when it is, leads to strong pragmatic deviance. Specifically:

- **Unexpected deaccenting**: If a word that should be new/focused is deaccented, the listener infers that the speaker considers it given or unimportant — which may create confusion if the listener doesn't share that assumption.

- **Unexpected accenting**: If a word that should be given/old is accented, the listener infers contrast or correction — the speaker is re-focusing on something for a reason. "I bought a RED car. Then I sold the CAR" (with stress on "car" in the second sentence) would suggest something like "I sold the *car itself*, not something else you might expect."

### 8.5 The Givenness Constraint (Schwarzschild 1999)

Roger Schwarzschild's (1999) influential paper "Givenness, AvoidF and Other Constraints on the Placement of Accent" provides a formal account of deaccenting. He proposes two key constraints:

1. **Givenness Constraint**: An expression may be deaccented if its denotation is "given" — recoverable from the discourse context.
2. **AvoidF**: Speakers should economize on F-marking (focus marking). Don't mark something as focused if you don't need to.

The interaction of these constraints explains why given information is deaccented: AvoidF says "don't focus unless you must," and Givenness says "you needn't focus what's already given." The result is a system that minimizes focus marking, reserving prosodic prominence for genuinely new or contrastive information.

Key to the analysis is the definition of givenness, which synthesizes insights from the literature on the semantics of focus with older views on information structure. The characteristic prominence patterns of "novelty focus" and "contrastive focus" both arise from this combination.

However, research also shows that the relationship between givenness and deaccenting is not a simple one-to-one mapping. Factors that influence whether given information is actually deaccented include: (1) whether the given item is part of a complex noun phrase; (2) its position within the prosodic phrase; and (3) whether preceding items in the phrase are themselves accentable based on their information status.

## 9. ADDITIONAL FOCUS TYPES AND INTERACTIONS

### 9.1 Types of Focus

The literature distinguishes several types of focus, each with somewhat different pragmatic and prosodic properties:

- **Information focus (presentational focus)**: The pragmatically most neutral case. The asserted focus alternative is interpreted as the most appropriate, truth-conditionally adequate alternative given the context. It introduces new information and gives rise to a contextually open set of alternatives. It does not involve a direct contrast with a given alternative.

- **Contrastive focus**: Implies the existence of a second focus alternative that must be salient in context. The speaker is explicitly contrasting the focused element with some other element that was expected, mentioned, or assumed.

- **Corrective focus**: A subtype of contrastive focus that operates over a previous statement, correcting a focal alternative explicitly present in the immediate discourse. All corrective foci are contrastive, but not all contrastive foci are corrective.

- **Verum focus (polarity focus)**: Focus on the truth/polarity of the proposition itself. In English, this is often realized through emphatic "do"-support: "He DID eat the cake" — where "did" is stressed to confirm the truth of the proposition against a doubted or negated expectation. The verum accent is used to emphasize the truth of the proposition in question. Recent debate exists about whether verum is truly a type of focus or a distinct phenomenon — Gutzmann and colleagues argue that "verum focus is verum, not focus," with almost identical discourse properties to focus but realized through different grammatical mechanisms.

### 9.2 Contrastive Topic

Büring (1997, 2003) identified a phenomenon called **contrastive topic** — where a topic (what the sentence is about) is contrasted with other possible topics:

> "What about the students? Did they pass?"
> "Well, JOHN passed..." (with a fall-rise intonation on "John")

The fall-rise intonation (which Büring calls a "B-accent," following Jackendoff 1972) on "John" marks him as a contrastive topic — he's one of the students, and the speaker is addressing his case while implying that other students' cases remain to be discussed. This intonation conveys "I'm answering your question partially — here's the part about John, but there's more to say about others."

### 9.3 Focus and Exhaustivity

Focus often receives an exhaustive interpretation. When asked "Who came to the party?" and someone answers "JOHN came," the natural interpretation is that only John came — the answer exhaustively identifies the individuals for whom the proposition holds.

This exhaustive interpretation is debated: some treat it as a semantic property of focus, others as a pragmatic implicature. Research shows that the exhaustive interpretation of cleft sentences ("It was John who came") is harder to cancel than the exhaustive interpretation of plain focus, suggesting different mechanisms may be at work.

## 10. SECOND OCCURRENCE FOCUS

### 10.1 The Puzzle

Second occurrence (SO) focus occurs when a word that is the semantic focus of a focus-sensitive operator (like "only") has already been mentioned as focus in a previous utterance:

> A: "Everyone only ate BEANS at the party."
> B: "If everyone only ate BEANS, then nobody ate the steak."

In B's sentence, "beans" is the semantic focus of "only" (it determines the meaning of "only"), but it was already focused in A's utterance — it's given information. This creates a tension: semantically, "beans" needs to be focused (for "only" to work correctly), but informationally, it's given (it was just mentioned).

### 10.2 The Resolution

Research by Beaver and Clark (and published in the journal Language) found that SO foci are prosodically marked — but differently from first-occurrence focus:

- **No mean pitch rise** on SO foci (unlike typical focus, which gets a pitch accent)
- **Longer duration** than non-focused words
- **Greater energy (intensity)** than non-focused words
- **Listeners can detect** the difference between SO foci and non-foci

This means second occurrence focus uses a reduced but real prosodic marking — duration and intensity without pitch. This finding is important because it shows that the prosody-semantics interface is more subtle than a simple "focused = accented / unfocused = deaccented" dichotomy. Focus can be marked through different combinations of acoustic cues depending on the discourse context.

## 11. RELEVANCE TO AUTISM AND PRAGMATIC PROCESSING

### 11.1 Prosodic Pragmatics in Autism: Research Overview

Research on prosody in autism spectrum conditions reveals a complex picture:

- **Impairments in prosody** are among the most notable communication characteristics of individuals with ASD, significantly impacting communicative interactions.

- There is a **production-perception asymmetry on most PEPS-C subtests**: autistic individuals' receptive (comprehension) prosodic skills generally outstrip their expressive (production) performance — with the important exception of contrastive stress, where receptive difficulties have also been documented, alongside consistent expressive deficits. The headline "receptive exceeds expressive" is a summary of the overall profile, not a universal claim.

- **Contrastive stress** is specifically flagged as an area of difficulty. Contrastive stress understanding is an area in which impaired comprehension has been reported, and both ASD and ASD-parent groups showed reduced accuracy in contrastive stress expression. Contrastive stress is critical for imparting communicative intent, and misunderstanding it may derail social interactions.

- There is a **rule-based vs. intuitive processing divide**: Research suggests that formal (rule-based) functions of linguistic prosody are more learnable for individuals on the autism spectrum, while pragmatic and emotional functions are assumed to be more intuitive and context-dependent, and thus more difficult. This is directly relevant because contrastive stress sits at the intersection — it has rule-like properties (the mechanism of focus and alternatives is systematic and predictable) but also requires contextual inference (you need to know what the relevant alternatives are).

- **Compensatory strategies can be learned**: Much of the variability in research results is due to the wide range of ages investigated, since difficulties encountered by autistic children do not always persist into adulthood. This suggests that explicit teaching of contrastive stress patterns can be effective.

- **Neural processing differences**: When perceiving prosodic cues, both affective and grammatical, activation of neural regions is more generalized in ASD than in typical development, with areas recruited reflecting heightened reliance on cognitive control, reading of intentions, attentional management, and visualization.

### 11.2 Why Explicit Rules Help

The research on contrastive stress is actually encouraging from a teaching perspective, because the system *is* rule-governed. The mapping from stress placement to meaning follows systematic patterns:

1. **Identify which word is stressed** (the acoustic signal)
2. **Generate the set of alternatives** to that word (the semantic computation)
3. **Infer what question the sentence answers** (the pragmatic computation)
4. **Derive the intended meaning** (the interpretive result)

Each step can be made explicit. The "I never said she stole my money" exercise, for instance, can be practiced systematically: stress each word, identify the alternatives, state the meaning. This converts what is normally an unconscious, intuitive process into a conscious, rule-following process — which aligns with the finding that rule-based aspects of prosody are more accessible to autistic learners.

### 11.3 The Visual/Pattern Dimension

The system of contrastive stress has strong structural regularities that lend themselves to visual representation:

- **Stress position → Alternative set → Question answered → Meaning**: This is a consistent, predictable chain.
- **Focus-sensitive particles**: The interaction between "only"/"even"/"just" and stress position follows deterministic rules — once you know which word is stressed, the meaning is predictable.
- **Default vs. marked stress**: The Nuclear Stress Rule provides a baseline; deviations from the baseline always carry meaning.
- **The given-new mapping**: Stressed = new, deaccented = given — with predictable consequences for what's presupposed.

These regularities mean that contrastive stress, despite being a prosodic (auditory) phenomenon, has the systematic structure of a pattern system — which may be particularly accessible to learners with strong pattern recognition abilities.

## 13. SUMMARY: CONTRASTIVE STRESS AS A MEANING-SELECTION SYSTEM

Contrastive stress in English functions as a **meaning-selection system** — a mechanism by which speakers select among multiple possible meanings of the same string of words by placing prosodic prominence on different elements. The system works through a chain of computations:

1. **The speaker places stress on a word** → signaling that this word is the focus
2. **Focus activates a set of alternatives** → the listener's mind generates other words that could fill that position
3. **The focus partitions the sentence** → into new information (the focus) and given information (the background/presupposition)
4. **This partition determines the Question Under Discussion** → what question the sentence is answering
5. **The QUD constrains pragmatic inference** → scalar implicatures, presuppositions, and other pragmatic meanings are computed relative to the QUD

This is not a matter of "emphasis" in the vague sense — it is a systematic, rule-governed system for encoding pragmatic meaning through prosody. The system interacts with focus-sensitive particles ("only," "even," "just," "also") to produce truth-conditional differences, with presupposition to determine what's taken for granted, and with scalar implicature to determine what's implicated but not said.

For autistic individuals, the key insight is that this system — often experienced as an opaque, intuitive aspect of neurotypical communication — is in fact highly structured and rule-governed. The rules can be learned explicitly:

- **Hear the stress → Identify the focus → Generate alternatives → Derive meaning**
- **Default stress = neutral; Non-default stress = marked = something specific is being communicated**
- **Focus-sensitive particles + stress position = deterministic meaning**

The system is complex but systematic, and it rewards pattern recognition.
