# Searle's Speech Act Taxonomy: Comprehensive Research

## Overview and Context

John R. Searle (1932-2022) built upon J.L. Austin's foundational work in speech act theory to create the most influential and systematic taxonomy of illocutionary acts in the philosophy of language. Where Austin proposed five loosely-defined categories of performative utterances in *How to Do Things with Words* (1962), Searle replaced them with a rigorously principled classification based on explicit, formally stated criteria.

Searle's taxonomy was presented primarily in two works:
- **"What is a Speech Act?"** (1969, in *Speech Acts: An Essay in the Philosophy of Language*) -- where he developed the rule-based framework for analyzing individual speech acts, using promising as his central example
- **"A Taxonomy of Illocutionary Acts"** (1975/1976, republished in *Expression and Meaning*, 1979) -- where he presented the full five-category classification system

**Why this matters for pragmatic communication:** Speech act theory provides the conceptual framework for understanding that language does not merely describe reality -- it *acts on* reality. Every utterance a person produces (or encounters) is performing one or more social actions. For individuals learning to navigate pragmatic communication, understanding these action-categories provides a systematic map of *what people are doing* when they talk.

---

## Part 1: Foundations -- The Three Levels of a Speech Act

Before examining Searle's taxonomy, it is essential to understand the three levels of action present in any utterance, originally distinguished by Austin and refined by Searle:

### 1.1 Locutionary Act
The act of producing a meaningful linguistic expression -- saying words that have a recognizable meaning. Example: Producing the sounds and grammar of "It's cold in here."

### 1.2 Illocutionary Act
The social action performed *in* saying something -- what the speaker intends to accomplish by the utterance. The same locution "It's cold in here" could perform different illocutionary acts:
- **Assertive**: Stating a fact about the temperature
- **Directive** (indirect): Requesting someone to close a window or turn up the heat
- **Expressive**: Complaining about discomfort

The illocutionary act is the primary focus of Searle's taxonomy.

### 1.3 Perlocutionary Act
The actual effect the utterance has on the hearer -- what happens *as a result of* saying something. "It's cold in here" might:
- Cause someone to close the window (perlocutionary effect: compliance)
- Make someone feel guilty (perlocutionary effect: guilt)
- Simply inform (perlocutionary effect: updated belief)

**Key distinction:** Illocutionary acts are defined by the speaker's intention and the conventional force of the utterance. Perlocutionary effects depend on how the hearer actually responds and cannot be directly controlled. You can say "I promise" (illocutionary), but you cannot say "I hereby convince you" -- convincing is a perlocutionary effect.

## Part 2: Searle's Twelve Dimensions of Variation

In *Expression and Meaning* (1979), Searle identified twelve dimensions along which illocutionary acts can differ from one another. While he acknowledged all twelve as "linguistically significant," he built his taxonomy primarily on three:

### The Three Primary Dimensions (Used to Define the Five Categories)

1. **Illocutionary Point (Purpose):** What the speech act is fundamentally trying to accomplish. A request and a command have the same illocutionary point (getting the hearer to do something) but differ in other ways.

2. **Direction of Fit:** The relationship between the words and the world (detailed in Part 3 below).

3. **Expressed Psychological State (Sincerity Condition):** The mental state that the speaker expresses by performing the act -- belief, desire, intention, regret, pleasure, etc.

### Additional Dimensions (Used to Distinguish Acts Within Categories)

4. **Strength or degree of illocutionary point:** How forcefully the point is presented. "I suggest you leave" vs. "I insist you leave" -- both are directives, but with different strength. An order has stronger illocutionary force than a request, though both aim to get the hearer to do something.

5. **Status or position of speaker and hearer:** A command requires that the speaker have authority over the hearer; a request does not.

6. **Relation to the interests of speaker and hearer:** A promise is for the benefit of the hearer; a threat is against the hearer's interest. Both are commissives (speaker commits to future action) but differ in whose interests are served.

7. **Relations to the rest of the discourse:** "I reply" relates to a previous utterance; "I conclude" relates to what precedes in an argument.

8. **Propositional content as determined by illocutionary force:** Predictions must be about the future; reports are typically about the past; promises must concern future acts of the speaker.

9. **Differences between acts that must always be speech acts and those that can be performed non-linguistically:** You can request something with a gesture, but you cannot hypothesize with one.

10. **Differences between acts that require extra-linguistic institutions and those that do not:** Declaring war, excommunicating, and sentencing require specific institutional frameworks. Stating or promising do not.

11. **Differences between acts where the corresponding illocutionary verb has a performative use and those where it does not:** "I promise" explicitly performs the act of promising (performative use), but "I threaten" does not actually constitute threatening in normal use.

12. **Style of performance:** The difference between announcing and confiding may be primarily one of manner or style rather than illocutionary point.

## Part 3: Direction of Fit -- The Core Organizational Principle

The concept of "direction of fit" is arguably Searle's most important contribution to the classification of speech acts. It was originally suggested by G.E.M. Anscombe and further developed by Searle. It describes the relationship between the words of an utterance and the state of the world.

### 3.1 Word-to-World (Downward Arrow: Words Must Match the World)

**Definition:** The speaker's words are supposed to represent an independently existing state of affairs. If the words don't match reality, the *words* are at fault (they are false or inaccurate), not the world.

**Applies to:** Assertives/Representatives

**Analogy:** Like a map that is supposed to accurately represent the terrain. If the map doesn't match the terrain, the map is wrong.

**Example:** "The cat is on the mat." If the cat is not actually on the mat, the statement fails -- we don't try to move the cat; we recognize the statement as false.

**Test:** If words and world don't match, who/what is at fault? If the *words* should change, it's word-to-world fit.

### 3.2 World-to-Word (Upward Arrow: The World Must Come to Match the Words)

**Definition:** The speaker's words describe a desired, requested, or intended state of affairs that does not yet exist. If the words don't match reality, it is the *world* that should change to match the words.

**Applies to:** Directives and Commissives

**Analogy:** Like a shopping list. If what you bring home doesn't match the list, the shopping is at fault, not the list.

**Example (Directive):** "Close the door." The door is currently open; the world should change to match the instruction. If the door remains open, the *world* has failed to match the words.

**Example (Commissive):** "I will finish the report by Friday." The report is not yet finished; the world (specifically, the speaker's future actions) should be brought into conformity with the words.

**Test:** If words and world don't match, who/what is at fault? If the *world* should change, it's world-to-word fit.

### 3.3 Null Direction (No Arrow: No Fit Required)

**Definition:** The proposition expressed is presupposed to be true. The speech act neither attempts to describe the world nor to change it. Instead, it expresses a psychological attitude about a state of affairs that both speaker and hearer already accept as real.

**Applies to:** Expressives

**Example:** "I'm sorry I broke your vase." The speaker isn't trying to establish *that* the vase was broken (both parties know it), nor trying to change anything about the vase. The speaker is expressing an attitude (regret) about an already-established fact.

### 3.4 Double Direction (Both Arrows: Words Both Represent and Create Reality)

**Definition:** The utterance simultaneously represents a state of affairs *and* brings that state of affairs into existence. The words change the world to match themselves, and in doing so, they also accurately describe the new reality they create.

**Applies to:** Declarations

**Example:** "I now pronounce you married." The words create the very state of affairs they describe. After the utterance, the couple is married -- the world has changed to match the words, and the words accurately describe the new reality.

**Why this is special:** Declarations are the only speech acts with double direction of fit. They are, in Searle's terms, the most remarkable of all speech acts because they require an institutional framework that grants the words the power to change reality.

## Part 4: Searle's Five Categories of Illocutionary Acts

### 4.1 ASSERTIVES (also called Representatives)

#### Defining Characteristics

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| **Illocutionary Point** | To commit the speaker (in varying degrees) to the truth of a proposition -- to represent a state of affairs as being the case |
| **Direction of Fit** | Word-to-world (the words must match the world) |
| **Sincerity Condition** | Belief -- the speaker believes the proposition expressed is true |
| **Propositional Content** | Any proposition P (virtually no restriction) |
| **Can be true or false** | Yes -- assertives are the only category whose members can be assessed for truth value |

#### Symbolic Notation
Searle represents assertives with: |- B(p)
Where |- indicates the assertive point, the downward arrow indicates word-to-world fit, B stands for Belief, and p is the proposition.

#### Core Mechanism
When a speaker performs an assertive, they put themselves "on record" as believing the expressed proposition is true. The degree of commitment varies across subtypes: stating is a stronger commitment than guessing, and swearing is stronger than suggesting.

#### Detailed Examples

1. **Stating/Asserting:** "The meeting starts at 3 PM." -- Direct commitment to a fact. The speaker represents this as true and can be held accountable for its truth.

2. **Describing:** "The room is large, with white walls and a window facing east." -- Representing observable features of a scene. Word-to-world: the description must match the room.

3. **Predicting:** "It will rain tomorrow." -- Committing to a future state of affairs. Though about the future, it is still word-to-world: the prediction will be judged against what actually happens.

4. **Claiming:** "This land belongs to my family." -- A stronger assertion, often in a context where the claim might be disputed. Implies the speaker has grounds for the assertion.

5. **Reporting:** "The committee voted 7-3 in favor of the motion." -- Relaying information about events. The speaker is committed to accuracy of the report.

6. **Explaining:** "The bridge collapsed because of metal fatigue in the support beams." -- Offering a causal account. The speaker commits to the explanatory relationship being accurate.

7. **Hypothesizing:** "The virus may have originated in a wet market." -- A weaker assertive, with reduced degree of commitment. The speaker presents a possibility rather than a certainty.

8. **Insisting:** "I'm telling you, he was at the restaurant at 8 o'clock." -- A very strong assertive, often in the face of doubt or denial. Maximum degree of speaker commitment.

9. **Concluding:** "Based on all the evidence, the defendant acted in self-defense." -- Drawing an inference from prior assertions. Commits the speaker to the logical relationship between evidence and conclusion.

10. **Informing:** "The library closes at 9 PM on weekdays." -- Sharing information the hearer presumably does not have. Weaker in forcefulness than insisting, but still a commitment to truth.

#### Social Function
Assertives are the backbone of information exchange in human communication. They allow us to share knowledge, coordinate our understanding of the world, build shared representations of reality, and hold each other accountable for the accuracy of our claims. Science, journalism, legal testimony, and everyday conversation all depend heavily on assertives.

The social "cost" of a failed assertive is a loss of credibility. If someone regularly makes false assertions (even sincerely), others will stop trusting their word. This is why insincere assertives (lies) are considered a serious social transgression in most cultures -- they exploit the trust that the assertive speech act framework creates.

#### Strength Gradient (Weakest to Strongest Commitment)
guessing < suggesting < hypothesizing < opining < stating < claiming < insisting < swearing/testifying

### 4.2 DIRECTIVES

#### Defining Characteristics

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| **Illocutionary Point** | To get the hearer to do something -- attempts by the speaker to get the hearer to perform some action |
| **Direction of Fit** | World-to-word (the world must come to match the words) |
| **Sincerity Condition** | Want/Desire -- the speaker wants the hearer to do the specified action |
| **Propositional Content** | That the hearer H will perform some future action A |
| **Success depends on** | Hearer's subsequent action (compliance) |

#### Symbolic Notation
Searle represents directives with: ! W(H does A)
Where ! indicates the directive point, the upward arrow indicates world-to-word fit, W stands for Want, H is the hearer, and A is the action.

#### Core Mechanism
When a speaker performs a directive, they express a desire that the hearer do something and attempt to get them to do it. Directives range from very humble attempts (requests, suggestions) to very aggressive attempts (commands, orders, demands). The key is that the speaker is trying to get the world to change through the hearer's action.

#### Detailed Examples

1. **Requesting:** "Could you please pass me the salt?" -- A humble directive. The speaker wants the hearer to pass the salt but does not presume authority to compel compliance. Often performed indirectly as questions about ability or willingness.

2. **Commanding/Ordering:** "Stand at attention!" -- A forceful directive, requiring that the speaker has authority over the hearer (e.g., military officer to soldier, parent to child). Non-compliance may have consequences.

3. **Questioning/Asking:** "What time does the train leave?" -- Searle classifies questions as a subclass of directives because they are attempts to get the hearer to perform a speech act (specifically, to provide information -- to perform an assertive). The "future action" is the act of answering.

4. **Inviting:** "Would you like to come to dinner on Saturday?" -- A polite directive that frames the action as beneficial to the hearer. It gives the hearer social permission to accept or decline without loss of face.

5. **Advising:** "You should see a doctor about that cough." -- A directive where the speaker presents the action as being in the hearer's interest. Weaker than commanding (no authority required) and hedged as a suggestion for the hearer's benefit.

6. **Suggesting:** "Maybe we could try the Italian restaurant." -- The weakest form of directive. The speaker puts forward a possible action but with minimal pressure. The hearer is free to accept or reject with no social cost.

7. **Warning:** "Don't touch that -- it's hot." -- A directive motivated by the speaker's concern for the hearer's well-being. The propositional content describes a harmful state of affairs, and the directive aims to prevent the hearer from encountering it.

8. **Daring:** "I dare you to jump off the diving board." -- A directive that exploits social pressure. The hearer is challenged to perform an action, with the implicit social cost of being seen as cowardly if they refuse.

9. **Forbidding:** "You may not leave the premises." -- A negative directive requiring authority. The speaker attempts to prevent the hearer from doing something.

10. **Begging/Pleading:** "Please, please don't fire me." -- An extremely humble directive, often with emotional appeal. The speaker explicitly signals their lack of power and their strong desire.

#### Social Function
Directives are the primary means by which humans coordinate action. They are the speech act category most sensitive to social relationships, particularly power and solidarity. The same illocutionary point (getting someone to close a window) can be realized as:
- A command (from a boss): "Close the window."
- A request (from a peer): "Could you close the window?"
- A hint (from a subordinate): "It's a bit cold in here, isn't it?"

The social rules governing which form of directive is appropriate in which context are among the most complex and culturally variable aspects of pragmatic competence. Using too forceful a directive with a social equal or superior is perceived as rude or domineering; using too weak a directive with a subordinate may be perceived as lacking confidence or clarity.

#### Important Note: Questions as Directives
Searle's classification of questions as directives is initially counterintuitive but logically consistent. A question like "What time is it?" is an attempt to get the hearer to do something (tell the speaker what time it is). The propositional content is that the hearer will perform the future action of providing information. The sincerity condition is that the speaker wants this information. Questions have world-to-word fit in the sense that the speaker wants the world (specifically, the conversational state) to change to include the desired information.

#### Strength Gradient (Weakest to Strongest Pressure)
hinting < suggesting < requesting < asking < urging < insisting < demanding < commanding < ordering

### 4.3 COMMISSIVES

#### Defining Characteristics

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| **Illocutionary Point** | To commit the speaker to a future course of action |
| **Direction of Fit** | World-to-word (the world, via the speaker's future actions, must come to match the words) |
| **Sincerity Condition** | Intention -- the speaker intends to do the specified action |
| **Propositional Content** | That the speaker S will perform some future action A |
| **Success depends on** | The speaker's subsequent fulfillment of the commitment |

#### Symbolic Notation
Searle represents commissives with: C I(S does A)
Where C indicates the commissive point, the upward arrow indicates world-to-word fit, I stands for Intention, S is the speaker, and A is the action.

#### Core Mechanism
Commissives and directives share the same direction of fit (world-to-word), which Searle acknowledged as "inelegant." The critical difference is *who* is supposed to make the world match the words: in directives, the *hearer* acts; in commissives, the *speaker* acts. When a speaker performs a commissive, they place themselves under an obligation (or present themselves as committed) to bring about a future state of affairs through their own action.

#### Detailed Examples

1. **Promising:** "I promise I'll pick you up at the airport." -- The paradigmatic commissive. The speaker undertakes an obligation to the hearer to perform a future action that is (typically) in the hearer's interest. Promising creates a social bond: the hearer can legitimately hold the speaker to the promise.

2. **Threatening:** "If you don't stop, I'll call the police." -- A commissive where the future action is *against* the hearer's interest. Unlike promises, threats do not create an obligation the hearer would want enforced. The sincerity condition is still intention (the speaker intends to carry out the threat), but the social dynamic is adversarial rather than cooperative.

3. **Offering:** "I can drive you to the station if you'd like." -- A conditional commissive. The speaker commits to a future action but makes it contingent on the hearer's acceptance. The hearer has the power to activate or decline the commitment.

4. **Vowing:** "I vow to protect and defend the Constitution." -- A very strong commissive, often performed in formal or ritualistic contexts (weddings, inaugurations, oaths of office). Higher degree of commitment than a simple promise, often invoking moral or institutional weight.

5. **Guaranteeing:** "I guarantee the product will last at least five years." -- A commissive with an implied compensatory commitment: if the guaranteed state of affairs fails to materialize, the speaker is committed to making amends (replacement, refund, etc.).

6. **Pledging:** "I pledge allegiance to the flag." -- A public, formal commissive expressing loyalty or commitment to a cause, organization, or principle. Carries social weight beyond the immediate interaction.

7. **Refusing:** "No, I won't attend the meeting." -- A negative commissive: the speaker commits to *not* doing something. The propositional content is the speaker's future non-action. Refusals are often responses to directives or offers, and they commit the speaker to continued non-compliance.

8. **Volunteering:** "I'll take care of the dishes." -- A commissive that is self-initiated rather than elicited by a directive or offer. The speaker spontaneously commits to a future action, often one that benefits others.

9. **Betting/Wagering:** "I bet you ten dollars it rains tomorrow." -- A conditional commissive that commits the speaker to a future action (paying) contingent on the truth of a proposition. Interestingly, it combines assertive (prediction) and commissive (commitment to pay) elements.

10. **Swearing/Undertaking Oath:** "I swear to tell the truth, the whole truth, and nothing but the truth." -- A solemn commissive, typically institutionally embedded, where the speaker commits to a course of conduct (truthful testimony) with formal consequences for violation.

#### Social Function
Commissives are fundamental to building trust, coordination, and cooperation in human societies. They allow individuals to:
- **Create reliable expectations:** Others can plan their actions based on the speaker's commitment
- **Build trust over time:** Fulfilling commissives builds reputation; breaking them damages it
- **Negotiate and contract:** Offers, counteroffers, and acceptances are commissive sequences that underlie commerce, diplomacy, and personal relationships
- **Signal intentions:** Even unrequested commitments (volunteering) signal social goodwill

The social cost of breaking a commissive (especially a promise) is severe: loss of trustworthiness. Human societies invest enormous effort in enforcing commissives, from informal social pressure ("You said you would!") to elaborate legal systems (contract law).

#### Commissives vs. Directives: The Shared Direction of Fit Problem
Searle acknowledged that both directives and commissives have world-to-word direction of fit, which is "inelegant" for a taxonomy that uses direction of fit as a primary criterion. The distinguishing factor is the source of the action:
- **Directives:** The *hearer* is supposed to change the world
- **Commissives:** The *speaker* is supposed to change the world

This has led some critics to suggest that commissives are not truly a separate category but a subtype of directives (self-directed directives, in a sense).

### 4.4 EXPRESSIVES

#### Defining Characteristics

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| **Illocutionary Point** | To express the speaker's psychological state about a state of affairs specified in the propositional content |
| **Direction of Fit** | Null (no direction -- the proposition is presupposed true, not asserted or requested) |
| **Sincerity Condition** | Varies with the type of expressive (sorrow for apologies, pleasure for congratulations, gratitude for thanks, etc.) |
| **Propositional Content** | A property or act ascribed to the speaker or hearer, presupposed to be true |
| **Truth value** | The propositional content is presupposed, not asserted, so expressives cannot be true or false |

#### Symbolic Notation
Searle represents expressives with: E 0 (P)(S/H + property)
Where E indicates the expressive point, 0 indicates null direction of fit, and the propositional content ascribes a property to the speaker or hearer.

#### Core Mechanism
Expressives are unique because their propositional content is presupposed to be true rather than asserted. When someone says "Thank you for helping me move," both parties already know that the hearer helped the speaker move -- the point of the utterance is not to establish that fact but to express gratitude about it. Because the truth of the proposition is taken for granted, there is no need for words to match the world (word-to-world) or for the world to match the words (world-to-word). The direction of fit is null.

The illocutionary point of expressives is "to express the sincerity condition of the speech act" -- in other words, the point is to make one's psychological state known regarding an established fact.

#### Detailed Examples

1. **Thanking:** "Thank you so much for the birthday present." -- Expresses gratitude. Presupposes: the hearer gave the speaker a birthday present. Sincerity condition: the speaker feels grateful.

2. **Apologizing:** "I'm so sorry I missed your recital." -- Expresses regret or remorse. Presupposes: the speaker missed the hearer's recital, and this was a negative event. Sincerity condition: the speaker feels sorry/regretful.

3. **Congratulating:** "Congratulations on your promotion!" -- Expresses pleasure at the hearer's good fortune. Presupposes: the hearer received a promotion. Sincerity condition: the speaker feels glad/pleased.

4. **Condoling/Offering Condolences:** "I'm so sorry for your loss." -- Expresses sympathy for the hearer's misfortune. Presupposes: the hearer has suffered a loss. Sincerity condition: the speaker feels sympathy/sorrow.

5. **Complaining:** "This coffee is terrible." -- Expresses displeasure about a state of affairs. Presupposes: a state of affairs exists (the coffee). Sincerity condition: the speaker feels displeasure. (Note: complaints can also function as assertives when the emphasis is on establishing the truth of the proposition rather than expressing feelings.)

6. **Welcoming:** "Welcome to our home!" -- Expresses pleasure at the hearer's arrival. Presupposes: the hearer has arrived. Sincerity condition: the speaker is pleased by the hearer's presence.

7. **Praising:** "You did an excellent job on this project." -- Expresses admiration or approval. Presupposes: the hearer performed some action (the project). Sincerity condition: the speaker feels admiration/approval.

8. **Deploring/Lamenting:** "It's such a shame that the old theater was torn down." -- Expresses sadness or regret about a state of affairs. Presupposes: the theater was torn down. Sincerity condition: the speaker feels sadness.

9. **Greeting:** "Hello! Good to see you!" -- Expresses pleasure at encountering someone. Presupposes: the encounter has occurred. Sincerity condition: the speaker is pleased (though greetings are often highly ritualized and may have minimal sincerity requirements in practice).

10. **Forgiving:** "I forgive you for what you said." -- Expresses the cessation of negative feelings. Presupposes: the hearer did something that caused the speaker offense. Sincerity condition: the speaker no longer holds the offense against the hearer.

#### Social Function
Expressives are the "social lubricant" of human interaction. They serve crucial functions in:
- **Acknowledging social debts:** Thanking recognizes when someone has done something beneficial; apologizing recognizes when one has done something harmful
- **Maintaining social bonds:** Congratulating, welcoming, and praising strengthen relationships by demonstrating that the speaker cares about the hearer's experiences
- **Regulating emotional climate:** Condolences, laments, and complaints help manage shared emotional states
- **Performing ritual social obligations:** Many expressives are culturally expected at particular moments (thanking after a gift, congratulating after an achievement, condoling after a death). Failure to perform the expected expressive is itself a social transgression.

**The sincerity problem:** Expressives raise interesting questions about sincerity. Many expressives are performed as social obligations regardless of the speaker's actual feelings. Someone may say "Congratulations!" while feeling envy, or "Welcome!" while feeling annoyance. Searle's sincerity condition says the speaker *should* have the relevant psychological state, but in practice, many expressives are performed ritualistically. This gap between sincere and ritual expressives is an important aspect of pragmatic competence.

### 4.5 DECLARATIONS

#### Defining Characteristics

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| **Illocutionary Point** | To bring about a change in the world through the utterance itself -- the successful performance of the act creates the correspondence between words and world |
| **Direction of Fit** | Double direction (both word-to-world and world-to-word simultaneously) |
| **Sincerity Condition** | None required (the speaker's psychological state is irrelevant to the success of the declaration) |
| **Propositional Content** | That some state of affairs obtains as a result of the utterance |
| **Requires** | An extra-linguistic institution that grants the speaker the authority to perform the act |

#### Symbolic Notation
Searle represents declarations with: D 0 (p)
Where D indicates the declarative point and the double arrows indicate both directions of fit. The 0 indicates no sincerity condition is required.

#### Core Mechanism
Declarations are the most remarkable category in Searle's taxonomy. They are the only speech acts that, when successfully performed, immediately change the world. No other category has this property. A promise commits you to a future change; a directive tries to get someone else to make a change; but a declaration *is* the change.

Declarations work because of institutional frameworks -- systems of constitutive rules that give certain people in certain roles the power to change reality through their words. A judge can sentence someone to prison because the legal system grants judges that power. A minister can marry people because the state (and/or religious institution) grants ministers that power. Without the institution, the words are just sounds.

**The "Counts As" Relation:** Central to Searle's account of declarations is the constitutive rule formula: **"X counts as Y in context C."** For example:
- Uttering "I now pronounce you married" (X) counts as the creation of a legal marriage (Y) in the context of an authorized wedding ceremony (C)
- Uttering "You're fired" (X) counts as the termination of employment (Y) in the context of an employer speaking to an employee (C)
- Uttering "I sentence you to ten years" (X) counts as the imposition of a prison sentence (Y) in the context of a judge speaking in a courtroom after a guilty verdict (C)

#### Detailed Examples

1. **Pronouncing Marriage:** "I now pronounce you husband and wife." -- Performed by an authorized officiant in a ceremony with witnesses. Creates the legal and social state of marriage.

2. **Sentencing:** "I sentence you to five years in prison." -- Performed by a judge after a guilty verdict. Creates the legal obligation of imprisonment.

3. **Firing/Terminating Employment:** "You're fired." / "Your employment is terminated effective immediately." -- Performed by someone with hiring/firing authority. Changes the employee's employment status.

4. **Declaring War:** "The United States hereby declares war on [country]." -- Performed by an authorized governmental body (in the US, Congress). Changes the legal relationship between nations.

5. **Christening/Naming:** "I christen this ship the *Queen Mary*." -- Performed in an authorized naming ceremony. The ship now bears that name.

6. **Resigning:** "I hereby resign from my position as CEO." -- Performed by the holder of a position. Changes the organizational structure.

7. **Appointing:** "I appoint you as ambassador to France." -- Performed by the head of state or other authorized officer. Creates a new official role and empowers the appointee.

8. **Excommunicating:** "You are hereby excommunicated from the Church." -- Performed by an authorized religious authority. Changes the person's status within the religious community.

9. **Declaring a Mistrial:** "I declare a mistrial." -- Performed by a judge. Nullifies the legal proceedings.

10. **Adjourning:** "This court is adjourned." / "This meeting is adjourned." -- Performed by the chair or presiding official. Ends the formal proceedings.

#### Social Function
Declarations are the speech act mechanism through which institutions exercise power. They are the linguistic engine of institutional reality -- the entire fabric of social institutions (legal systems, governments, corporations, religions, educational institutions) operates through declarations:
- **Creating status:** Awarding degrees, granting citizenship, ordaining clergy
- **Transferring authority:** Appointing, delegating, authorizing
- **Ending relationships:** Divorcing, excommunicating, expelling
- **Establishing rules:** Legislating, decreeing, proclaiming

Declarations are unique in that they cannot fail due to insincerity. A judge who secretly believes the defendant is innocent but says "I find the defendant guilty" has still performed a successful declaration -- the defendant is legally guilty regardless of the judge's private beliefs. (This is why there is no sincerity condition for declarations.)

#### The Special Subcategory: Assertive Declarations

Searle identified a hybrid category he called **assertive declarations** (or "representative declarations"). These combine features of both assertives and declarations. The speaker makes a factual judgment that also creates institutional reality.

**Examples:**
- **"I find the defendant guilty."** -- The jury foreman both reports a factual finding (assertive: the evidence supports guilt) and creates a legal status (declaration: the defendant is now legally guilty). Unlike pure declarations, this has a sincerity condition: the speaker should genuinely believe in the finding.
- **"You're out!"** (umpire's call) -- The umpire both judges that the runner failed to reach base safely (assertive) and creates the official game state (declaration).
- **"I diagnose this as pneumonia."** (medical context) -- The doctor both asserts a medical finding and establishes an institutional fact (the patient's medical record now carries this diagnosis).

Assertive declarations require both institutional authority (like declarations) AND genuine belief in the proposition (like assertives). They are the bridge between factual assessment and institutional power.

## Part 5: The Constitutive Rules Framework

### 5.1 Regulative vs. Constitutive Rules

Searle distinguishes two fundamentally different types of rules:

**Regulative Rules:** Rules that regulate pre-existing activities. The activity exists independently of the rules. Example: "Drive on the right side of the road" regulates driving, but driving existed before traffic laws. Regulative rules typically take the form: "Do X" or "If Y, do X."

**Constitutive Rules:** Rules that create the very possibility of an activity. Without the rules, the activity cannot exist. Example: The rules of chess do not regulate a pre-existing activity; they *create* chess. There is no such thing as "moving a bishop diagonally" outside the constitutive rules of chess. Constitutive rules take the form: **"X counts as Y in context C."**

### 5.2 Speech Acts as Constitutive Rules

Searle's central insight is that speech acts are governed by constitutive rules. The semantic structure of a language is a conventional realization of underlying constitutive rules. Performing a speech act is engaging in a rule-governed form of behavior.

For example, the constitutive rules for promising include:
1. **Propositional Content Rule:** The utterance predicates a future act A of the speaker S
2. **Preparatory Rules:** (a) The hearer H would prefer S's doing A to not doing A, and S believes H would prefer this. (b) It is not obvious to both S and H that S will do A in the normal course of events
3. **Sincerity Rule:** S intends to do A
4. **Essential Rule:** The utterance counts as the undertaking of an obligation to do A

These rules don't regulate promising as if promising existed independently -- they *constitute* what it means to promise. An utterance that violates these rules is not a defective promise; it may not be a promise at all.

### 5.3 Felicity Conditions (Conditions for Successful Performance)

Searle systematized Austin's notion of "felicity conditions" into four categories, which map onto the constitutive rules:

1. **Propositional Content Condition:** Specifies what the content of the utterance must be about. For promises: a future act of the speaker. For requests: a future act of the hearer. For assertions: any proposition.

2. **Preparatory Conditions:** Background circumstances that must obtain for the speech act to be appropriate. For requests: the hearer is able to do the action, and it is not obvious that they would do it anyway without being asked. For promises: the hearer would prefer the promised action to non-performance. For warnings: the speaker believes the event would not be in the hearer's interest.

3. **Sincerity Condition:** The psychological state the speaker must have. For assertions: belief. For promises: intention. For requests: desire. For expressives: varies (gratitude for thanks, sorrow for apologies, etc.). For declarations: none required.

4. **Essential Condition:** What the act fundamentally "counts as." An assertion counts as a commitment to the truth of the proposition. A promise counts as an undertaking of obligation. A request counts as an attempt to get the hearer to do something. A declaration counts as bringing about the state of affairs expressed.

### 5.4 Searle's Rules for Promising (The Paradigm Case)

In *Speech Acts* (1969), Searle provided an exhaustive rule-based analysis of promising, which served as his model for analyzing all speech acts. The rules include:

**Propositional Content Rule:** S predicates a future act A of S in expressing the proposition P.

**Preparatory Rules:**
- H would prefer S's doing A to S's not doing A, and S believes this
- It is not obvious to both S and H that S will do A in the normal course of events of S's own accord

**Sincerity Rule:** S intends to do A.

**Essential Rule:** The utterance of P counts as the undertaking of an obligation to do A.

Additionally, Searle specified normal input and output conditions (the speaker and hearer speak the same language, are not joking or acting, etc.) and certain general conditions (the speaker intends the utterance to be recognized as a promise, intends to create in the hearer the belief that conditions hold, and intends the hearer to recognize the intention).

## Part 6: How Searle Extends Austin's Original Work

### 6.1 Austin's Original Taxonomy (1962)

Austin proposed five categories of illocutionary acts in *How to Do Things with Words*:

1. **Verdictives:** Acts of giving a verdict or judgment (acquitting, diagnosing, appraising, assessing, evaluating)
2. **Exercitives:** Acts of exercising powers, rights, or influence (ordering, commanding, urging, advising, warning, appointing)
3. **Commissives:** Acts committing the speaker to a course of action (promising, betting, vowing, undertaking, planning)
4. **Behabitives:** Acts relating to attitudes and social behavior (apologizing, congratulating, thanking, cursing, challenging, toasting)
5. **Expositives:** Acts clarifying how utterances fit into discourse (stating, affirming, denying, describing, asking, answering, objecting, postulating)

### 6.2 Searle's Six Criticisms of Austin

In "A Taxonomy of Illocutionary Acts" (1975), Searle leveled six specific criticisms at Austin's taxonomy:

1. **Conflation of illocutionary verbs with illocutionary acts:** Austin built his categories by classifying English verbs, not speech acts themselves. But not every speech act has a corresponding verb in every language, and two different verbs may name the same speech act. A taxonomy of acts must be independent of the vocabulary of any particular language.

2. **Overlapping categories:** Some acts appear in multiple categories. "Describe" appears as both a verdictive and an expositive. A rigorous taxonomy should have mutually exclusive categories.

3. **Heterogeneous categories:** Austin's categories are internally inconsistent. Behabitives, in particular, are "a heterogeneous group with little unifying principle" -- they include apologizing (expressing regret), cursing (expressing anger), and challenging (attempting to get someone to do something), which seem to serve very different purposes.

4. **No consistent principle of classification:** Austin does not clearly state what criteria distinguish one category from another. Why is ordering an exercitive rather than a commissive? What principle unifies the expositives?

5. **Acts that don't fit any category:** Some illocutionary acts seem to fall outside all five categories, suggesting the taxonomy is incomplete.

6. **Some listed items are not illocutionary acts at all:** Austin's lists include some items (like "intending") that are mental states rather than speech acts.

### 6.3 Searle's Key Innovations

**6.3.1 Principled Criteria Instead of Verb Lists**

Austin classified illocutionary verbs; Searle classified illocutionary acts themselves, using explicitly stated criteria (illocutionary point, direction of fit, sincerity condition). This means Searle's taxonomy can accommodate speech acts that have no corresponding verb in English (or any language) and can recognize when different verbs describe the same type of act.

**6.3.2 Direction of Fit as an Organizing Principle**

Austin did not systematically use direction of fit. Searle made it the central organizational principle of his taxonomy, providing a clear, formal basis for distinguishing assertives (word-to-world), directives and commissives (world-to-word), expressives (null), and declarations (double).

**6.3.3 Systematic Felicity Conditions**

Austin introduced the concept of "infelicities" -- ways speech acts can go wrong. Searle transformed this into a positive framework: each speech act type is defined by the conditions that must be met for it to be successfully performed (propositional content, preparatory, sincerity, essential). This provides a constructive, rule-based understanding of what makes each speech act what it is.

**6.3.4 The Speech Act / Proposition Distinction**

Searle drew a sharp distinction between the propositional content of an utterance (what it is about) and the illocutionary force (what the speaker is doing with that content). The same proposition "You will leave the room" can occur in:
- An assertive: "You will leave the room" (prediction)
- A directive: "Leave the room!" (command)
- A commissive: "I'll make sure you leave the room" (threat)
- An expressive: "I'm so glad you're leaving the room" (relief)
- A declaration: "You are hereby expelled from the room" (expulsion)

**6.3.5 The Category of Declarations**

Austin's exercitives included some acts that change institutional reality, but he mixed them with acts that merely exercise influence. Searle created a clean, separate category for acts that create reality through utterance -- a profound philosophical insight that Searle later developed into his theory of institutional facts and social ontology (*The Construction of Social Reality*, 1995).

**6.3.6 Indirect Speech Acts**

Searle's theory of indirect speech acts (1975) was a major extension beyond Austin. Searle showed that a sentence with one illocutionary form can systematically perform a different illocutionary act. "Can you pass the salt?" is grammatically a question (about the hearer's ability) but functions as a request. Searle argued that hearers compute the intended act through a chain of inferences:

1. The speaker asked about my ability to pass the salt (literal directive: question)
2. It is obvious that I can pass the salt, so the question is not a genuine request for information
3. In the context of a meal, having the salt would be useful
4. Therefore, the speaker is probably requesting that I pass the salt (primary directive: request)

This inferential process relies on Grice's Cooperative Principle, felicity conditions, and mutual background knowledge. Importantly, many indirect speech act forms have become partly **conventionalized** -- English speakers automatically recognize "Can you...?", "Would you mind...?", "I was wondering if..." as polite request forms without going through the full inferential chain each time.

### 6.4 Comparison Table: Austin vs. Searle

| Austin's Category | Searle's Replacement | Key Improvement |
|---|---|---|
| Verdictives | Split into Assertives (judgments) and Declarations (verdicts with institutional force) | Separates the cognitive act of judging from the institutional act of declaring a verdict |
| Exercitives | Split into Directives (attempts to influence action) and Declarations (exercises of institutional authority) | Distinguishes influencing behavior from changing institutional reality |
| Commissives | Commissives (retained, refined) | Added formal criteria: direction of fit, sincerity condition = intention |
| Behabitives | Expressives (emotional/attitudinal acts) | Unified under "null direction of fit" and variable sincerity conditions |
| Expositives | Assertives (most became representatives) | Subsumed under word-to-world fit and belief sincerity condition |

## Part 7: Criticisms of Searle's Taxonomy and Alternative Approaches

### 7.1 Internal Criticisms

**7.1.1 The Commissive-Directive Problem**

Searle himself acknowledged that having two categories (directives and commissives) with the same direction of fit (world-to-word) was "inelegant." Critics argue this undermines the claim that direction of fit is a primary organizing principle. If direction of fit truly distinguished categories, commissives and directives should be the same category.

**7.1.2 Category Overlap**

Despite Searle's criticism of Austin for overlapping categories, his own taxonomy has overlap problems:
- **Threats** can be both commissives (committing to a future action) and directives (trying to get the hearer to do something through intimidation)
- **Warnings** can be both directives (trying to get the hearer to avoid something) and assertives (informing the hearer about a danger)
- **Complaints** can be both expressives (expressing displeasure) and assertives (stating that something is wrong) and directives (implicitly requesting a change)
- **"I'll be there"** could be a commissive (promise) or an assertive (prediction)

**7.1.3 The Status of Questions**

Classifying questions as a subclass of directives is controversial. Questions have very different structural properties from commands and requests. They have distinct grammatical coding in virtually all languages, different response patterns, and play a unique role in discourse. Many linguists argue questions deserve their own category.

**7.1.4 Cultural Bias**

The taxonomy was developed based primarily on English-language examples and Western cultural norms. Speech act categories and their boundaries may differ across cultures. For example, some cultures may not sharply distinguish between expressives and assertives in the way Searle's taxonomy assumes.

**7.1.5 Multi-functionality**

Searle's taxonomy assumes each speech act belongs to one category. In practice, many utterances simultaneously perform multiple speech act functions. "It's cold in here" might simultaneously assert a fact (assertive), request that someone close the window (directive), and express discomfort (expressive). A taxonomy that forces single categorization may miss this multi-functionality.

**7.1.6 No Principles for New Classifications**

The taxonomy does not provide a clear method for classifying newly-identified speech acts. If a new type of speech act is discovered or created (e.g., through new technologies like "unfriending" someone on social media), there is no procedure within the taxonomy for determining its category.

### 7.2 Bach and Harnish's Alternative Taxonomy (1979)

Kent Bach and Robert M. Harnish proposed an alternative classification in *Linguistic Communication and Speech Acts* (1979). Their approach differs from Searle's in several important ways:

**Core Principle:** For Bach and Harnish, an illocutionary act is fundamentally an attempt to communicate -- specifically, the expression of an attitude. They classify acts according to the type of attitude expressed and the intended effect on the hearer's corresponding attitude.

**Four Categories:**

1. **Constatives:** Express the speaker's belief and the intention that the hearer form a like belief. This is a much more finely differentiated category than Searle's assertives, with subcategories including:
   - *Assertives:* affirming, alleging, asserting, claiming, declaring, indicating
   - *Predictives:* forecasting, predicting, prophesying
   - *Retrodictives:* reporting (past events)
   - *Descriptives:* characterizing, classifying, describing
   - *Ascriptives:* attributing, categorizing
   - *Informatives:* advising, announcing, informing, insisting, telling
   - *Confirmatives:* certifying, confirming, corroborating, verifying
   - *Concessives:* acknowledging, admitting, conceding, granting
   - *Retractives:* correcting, denying, disavowing, recanting, renouncing, repudiating, retracting, withdrawing
   - *Assentives:* accepting, agreeing, assenting, concurring
   - *Dissentives:* demurring, disagreeing, dissenting, objecting, protesting
   - *Disputatives:* challenging, disputing, questioning, rebuffing
   - *Responsives:* answering, replying, responding, retorting
   - *Suggestives:* conjecturing, guessing, hypothesizing, speculating, suggesting
   - *Suppositives:* assuming, postulating, stipulating, supposing

2. **Directives:** Express the speaker's attitude toward a prospective action by the hearer and the intention that the utterance serve as a reason for the hearer's action. Subcategories:
   - *Requestives:* asking, begging, imploring, insisting, inviting, petitioning, pleading, praying, requesting, soliciting, summoning, urging
   - *Questions:* asking, inquiring, interrogating, querying, quizzing
   - *Requirements:* bidding, charging, commanding, demanding, dictating, directing, enjoining, instructing, ordering, prescribing, requiring
   - *Prohibitives:* enjoining, forbidding, prohibiting, proscribing, restricting
   - *Permissives:* agreeing to, allowing, authorizing, blessing, consenting, dismissing, excusing, exempting, forgiving, granting, licensing, permitting, releasing
   - *Advisories:* admonishing, advising, cautioning, counseling, proposing, recommending, suggesting, urging, warning

3. **Commissives:** Express the speaker's intention and belief that the utterance obligates them to do something. Subcategories: promising, offering, volunteering, contracting.

4. **Acknowledgments:** Express feelings regarding the hearer or the speaker's intention to satisfy social expectations. Examples: apologizing, condoling, congratulating, greeting, thanking, accepting (an acknowledgment).

**Key Differences from Searle:**

| Feature | Searle | Bach & Harnish |
|---|---|---|
| Number of categories | 5 | 4 |
| Basis of classification | Illocutionary point, direction of fit, sincerity condition | Type of expressed attitude |
| Expressives | Separate category | Subsumed under "Acknowledgments" (narrower scope -- ritualized social acts only) |
| Declarations | Separate category | Not included as a communicative category (argued to be non-communicative institutional acts) |
| Constatives | One category (assertives) | Extremely detailed with 15+ subcategories |
| Role of hearer | Hearer recognition is relevant but not definitive | Central: the act is only fully performed when the hearer recognizes the speaker's intention |

**Why Bach and Harnish excluded Declarations:** They argued that declarations are not communicative acts in the relevant sense. A judge who sentences someone is not primarily trying to communicate a mental state -- they are performing an institutional action. Bach and Harnish restricted their taxonomy to acts whose primary function is communication, which excluded institutional declarations.

### 7.3 Other Alternative Approaches

**7.3.1 Daniel Harris's Taxonomy of Communicative Acts**

Daniel W. Harris has proposed a revised taxonomy that takes into account the multi-functionality of utterances and tries to accommodate the way speech acts interact with broader conversational goals. Harris distinguishes between the basic communicative act and the various social actions it may simultaneously perform.

**7.3.2 Sbisa's Revisions**

Marina Sbisa has argued for revisions to the classical taxonomy that better account for the interactive, dialogical nature of speech acts -- the fact that speech acts are always performed within conversations and are shaped by the responses they receive.

**7.3.3 Kissine's Work**

Mikhail Kissine has examined the relationship between Searle's categories and the linguistic structures that express them, arguing that the structural coding of speech acts in natural languages does not always support Searle's category boundaries. For example, the grammatical structures of questions are quite distinct from those of commands, even though Searle groups them both as directives.

### 7.4 Summary of Key Criticisms

| Criticism | Source | Response |
|---|---|---|
| Commissives and directives share direction of fit | Internal to Searle's system | Searle acknowledges this as "inelegant" but distinguishes them by who performs the future action |
| Category overlap (threats, warnings, complaints) | Multiple critics | Searle's system forces single categorization on multi-functional utterances |
| Questions shouldn't be directives | Linguists, Kissine | Structurally and functionally, questions are very different from commands |
| Cultural bias | Cross-cultural pragmatics researchers | Taxonomy based on English/Western norms may not universalize |
| Declarations are not communicative | Bach & Harnish | They are institutional acts, not expressions of attitudes |
| Insufficiently fine-grained | Bach & Harnish | Assertives category too broad (15+ subtypes collapsed into one) |
| No procedure for classifying new acts | Various | Taxonomy is descriptive, not generative |
| Multi-functionality not accommodated | Various | Real utterances often perform multiple acts simultaneously |

## Part 8: Implications for Pragmatic Communication Teaching

### 8.1 Why Speech Act Categories Matter for Pragmatic Competence

Understanding Searle's categories provides a systematic framework for:

1. **Recognizing what people are doing with their words:** When someone says "Nice weather we're having," are they making an assertion (about the weather), performing an expressive (sharing pleasure), or initiating a greeting ritual? The category system provides a vocabulary for asking and answering this question.

2. **Understanding indirect speech acts:** Knowing the categories makes it possible to recognize when an utterance that looks like one category (a question: "Can you close the door?") is actually performing as another category (a directive: a request to close the door). This is one of the most challenging aspects of pragmatic competence for many autistic individuals, as it requires reading beyond the literal meaning.

3. **Predicting social expectations:** Each speech act category carries social obligations:
   - Assertives create expectations of truth and evidence
   - Directives create expectations of compliance or explanation for non-compliance
   - Commissives create expectations of follow-through
   - Expressives create expectations of reciprocal emotional acknowledgment
   - Declarations create expectations of compliance with the new institutional reality

4. **Understanding why miscommunication happens:** Many pragmatic difficulties arise from speech act misidentification. If person A performs an indirect directive ("It's cold in here") and person B responds with an assertive ("Yes, it is"), person A may feel their request was ignored even though person B responded literally and truthfully. Understanding speech act categories provides the vocabulary to diagnose such mismatches.

### 8.2 Pattern-Based Recognition Strategies

For pattern-oriented learners, the following structural regularities can serve as aids:

**Assertives tend to feature:**
- Declarative sentence structure (subject-verb-object)
- Verbs like "is," "was," "will be," "seems," "appears"
- Evidence markers: "apparently," "reportedly," "clearly"
- Can be challenged with "That's not true" or "How do you know?"

**Directives tend to feature:**
- Imperative sentence structure (verb-first: "Close the door")
- Modal verbs: "Can you...?", "Would you...?", "Could you...?"
- Polite softeners: "please," "if you don't mind," "I was wondering if..."
- Can be responded to with compliance, refusal, or negotiation

**Commissives tend to feature:**
- First person subject + future action: "I will," "I'll," "I'm going to"
- Performative verbs: "I promise," "I swear," "I guarantee"
- Conditional markers for offers: "If you want," "I could..."
- Can be responded to with acceptance, gratitude, or release from obligation

**Expressives tend to feature:**
- Formulaic expressions: "Thank you," "I'm sorry," "Congratulations"
- Emotional vocabulary: "glad," "sorry," "delighted," "sad"
- Exclamatory patterns: "How wonderful!", "What a shame!"
- Expected response is often a reciprocal expressive

**Declarations tend to feature:**
- Institutional/authority context
- Performative formulae: "I hereby...," "I now pronounce...," "You are..."
- Present tense: "You are fired" (not "You will be fired")
- Often occur in ritualized settings (courtrooms, ceremonies, meetings)
