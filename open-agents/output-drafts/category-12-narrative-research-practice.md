## 1. STORYTELLING IN EVERYDAY CONVERSATION

### 1.1 The Nature of Conversational Storytelling

Everyday storytelling is fundamentally different from written fiction. It is oral, interactive, co-constructed, and embedded in the flow of conversation. William Labov and Joshua Waletzky's foundational research (1967) established that oral narratives of personal experience follow a consistent structural pattern, derived from actual recorded speech rather than abstract theorization. Their framework identifies six components that structure oral narratives:

1. **Abstract** — A brief summary that announces the story ("Something crazy happened at work today")
2. **Orientation** — Background information: who, what, where, when ("So I was in the break room, it was like 2 PM, and my manager walks in...")
3. **Complicating Action** — The sequence of events that forms the story's backbone ("...and she starts opening every cabinet, slamming them shut...")
4. **Evaluation** — The narrator's attitude toward the events; why the story matters ("I had *never* seen her that angry. It was actually kind of terrifying.")
5. **Resolution** — What finally happened ("Turns out someone had eaten her lunch")
6. **Coda** — A signal that the story is over and conversation can resume ("So yeah, that's why I don't use the break room fridge anymore")

The evaluation component is crucial. It answers the implicit question: **"So what?"** Without evaluation, a story is just a sequence of events, and the listener has no idea why they should care. Labov describes evaluation as providing "justification for the narrative's claim on a greater portion of conversational time than most turns of talk." It is the single most important element that separates a good story from a pointless one.

**Key insight for autistic individuals:** Every conversational story must answer two questions:
- **"And then what happened?"** (the events — the complicating action and resolution)
- **"So what?"** (the point — the evaluation)

If your story answers the first but not the second, listeners will think: "Why are you telling me this?" If it answers the second but not the first, it's an opinion, not a story.

### 1.2 The Story Preface: Announcing You're About to Tell a Story

In ordinary conversation, speakers take short turns — a sentence or two, then someone else speaks. Stories require an exception: the teller needs to hold the floor for an extended period without being interrupted. This creates a problem that is solved through the **story preface**.

Harvey Sacks and Gail Jefferson's conversation analysis research shows that before telling a story, speakers typically produce a **preface sequence** — a short announcement that:
1. Signals "I have a story to tell"
2. Gives the listener a reason to want to hear it
3. Secures permission (a "go-ahead") to take an extended turn

**Common story prefaces include:**

| Preface Type | Examples | What It Signals |
|---|---|---|
| Dramatic announcement | "Oh my god, you'll never guess what happened" | Something exciting/surprising occurred |
| Topic link | "That reminds me of this time when..." | Story is relevant to current topic |
| Relevance claim | "You know what's funny about that?" | Story connects to what was just said |
| News delivery | "Did you hear about...?" | New information the listener may want |
| Warning/framing | "OK so this is kind of a long story, but..." | Asking for patience with length |
| Assessment preface | "The weirdest thing happened to me" | Pre-evaluating the story's significance |

**The go-ahead response:** After a story preface, the listener typically responds with something like "What?" "No, what happened?" "Tell me!" or "Oh really?" This response is the **go-ahead** — it signals that the listener is willing to suspend normal turn-taking and let the teller hold the floor.

**What happens without a preface:** If you launch into a story without a preface, listeners may:
- Not realize you need an extended turn and try to take their turn after your first sentence
- Not understand why you're telling them this
- Feel disoriented about what kind of response is expected

**Practical pattern:** Preface → Go-ahead → Story → Reaction

### 1.3 Getting the Floor for an Extended Turn

Research on turn-taking in storytelling (including work by Sacks, Schegloff, and Jefferson) shows that the organization of turn-taking during stories differs significantly from regular conversation:

- **Normal conversation:** Turns are short. After one person speaks, the next person can (and usually does) speak.
- **During a story:** The storyteller gets an extended turn. Normal turn-taking rules are temporarily suspended, and the teller maintains control of "every third slot in talk" (the N-notN-N pattern) — meaning the teller speaks, the listener gives a brief response, the teller continues, and so on.

**How speakers secure extended turns:**
- Using a story preface (as above) to explicitly request the floor
- Beginning with a dramatic or evaluative statement that creates anticipation
- Speaking with prosodic cues (intonation, pace) that signal "I'm not done yet"
- Using continuation markers like "and then," "so," "and the thing is" to hold the floor between clauses

**A critical social norm:** Taking a long turn without justification (without a preface or without the story being interesting enough to warrant the time) is a social violation. It signals that you are claiming conversational space without offering value in return.

### 1.4 Listener Participation During Stories

Storytelling is not a monologue; it is a **joint activity** in which narrator and listener actively collaborate. Neal Norrick's research on conversational narrative emphasizes that genuine conversational storytelling is always interactive, negotiated, and co-constructed — not simply designed by a single teller for a passive audience.

#### Backchannels

Victor Yngve coined the term "backchannel" in 1970 to describe how listeners provide continuous feedback during someone else's turn without trying to take the floor. Backchannels are short responses that signal attention, understanding, or emotional reaction.

**Types of backchannels:**

| Type | Examples | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Non-lexical | "Mm-hmm," "Uh-huh," "Hmm" | "I'm listening, continue" |
| Acknowledgment | "Yeah," "Right," "OK" | "I understand" |
| Assessment | "Wow," "No way," "Oh no" | "I have a reaction to this" |
| Surprise/interest | "Really?" "Seriously?" | "This is interesting, tell me more" |
| Empathic | "Oh god," "That's awful" | "I feel something about this" |
| Completion | Finishing the speaker's sentence | "I'm so engaged I can predict what's next" |
| Clarification request | "Wait, who?" "The tall one?" | "I need more info to follow" |

**Critical insight:** Backchannels are **not optional.** They are expected. A listener who provides no backchannels — who sits in complete silence during a story — creates significant social discomfort. The teller may:
- Think the listener isn't paying attention
- Feel anxious about whether the story is landing
- Stop the story to ask "Are you listening?"

Research shows that the absence of backchannels correlates with negative perceptions of the listener, including lower ratings of friendliness, activeness, and extraversion. Even a simple "mm-hmm" at regular intervals fulfills a vital social function.

#### Active Listener Contributions

Beyond backchannels, listeners contribute to stories through:
- **Collaborative co-narration:** When the listener was present for the events, they may add details, correct the teller, or take over parts of the telling
- **Prompting questions:** "And then what?" "What did you do?" — these drive the story forward
- **Emotional reactions:** Laughter, gasps, expressions of shock — these validate the teller's evaluation
- **Assessments at the end:** "That's hilarious!" "That's so messed up" — these confirm the story has landed

### 1.5 How Listeners Signal Engagement vs. Boredom

**Signals of engagement:**
- Consistent eye contact
- Leaning forward or toward the speaker
- Frequent and varied backchannels ("mm-hmm," "wow," "and then?")
- Follow-up questions ("Wait, what did she say?")
- Emotional reactions matched to the story content (laughing at funny parts, looking concerned at worrying parts)
- Mirroring the teller's emotional expressions
- Asking for details ("What did it look like?" "How long were you waiting?")

**Signals of boredom or disengagement:**
- Minimal or absent backchannels (silence, or only perfunctory "mm" responses)
- Reduced or absent eye contact
- Looking at phone, watch, or around the room
- Shortened responses: "Mm." "Yeah." "Cool." (one-word, flat tone)
- Body turning away, leaning back, or closed postures (crossed arms)
- Fidgeting, yawning, or other physical restlessness
- Attempting to change the subject before the story is finished
- Fake smiles that don't reach the eyes
- No follow-up questions when the story ends
- Producing a minimal assessment ("Huh. That's... something") rather than a genuine reaction

**What to do if you notice disengagement:**
1. **Speed up:** Skip unnecessary details and get to the point faster
2. **Re-engage:** "OK, here's the important part..." or "But here's where it gets interesting..."
3. **Check in:** "Am I losing you?" (use sparingly; this can feel awkward)
4. **Wrap up:** If someone is clearly not interested, cut the story short gracefully: "Anyway, long story short, [resolution]"

### 1.6 The Punchline / Climax Delivery

The climax is the moment the story has been building toward — the point of maximum tension, humor, or surprise. Effective delivery depends on:

**Timing and buildup:**
- The setup must create enough anticipation without taking too long
- Strategic pauses before the key moment increase impact ("And she turns around and... it was her *ex-husband*")
- Varying sentence length: short, punchy sentences at the climax contrast with longer setup sentences
- Prosodic emphasis: changing pitch, volume, or pace to signal "this is the important part"

**The "trigger" principle:** A punchline or climax works by creating a sudden shift — something unexpected that recontextualizes what came before. The setup leads the listener to expect one thing; the climax delivers something different.

**Evaluation at the climax:** This is where evaluation is most concentrated. The teller typically signals the importance through:
- Intensifiers: "I *literally* could not believe it"
- Repetition: "She said no. She just flat out said no."
- Comparators (Labov's term): Contrasting what happened with what might have happened ("If I had been two minutes later, I would've missed it entirely")
- Quoting dialogue: Reported speech is more vivid than summary ("She looked at me and said, 'Get out of my house'" vs. "She told me to leave")

### 1.7 How a Story "Lands" (or Doesn't)

**A story "lands" when:**
- The listener's response matches the emotional tone the teller intended (laughing at a funny story, expressing outrage at an unfair situation, showing sympathy at a sad one)
- The listener produces a strong assessment: "That's *amazing*," "Oh my god, that's horrible"
- The listener connects the story to shared experience: "That's just like what happened to Sarah!"
- The listener asks follow-up questions that show genuine interest
- There's a moment of shared emotional response — mutual laughter, shared head-shaking, etc.

**A story "doesn't land" when:**
- The listener responds with a flat or generic assessment: "Huh." "That's... interesting." "Oh, OK."
- There's an awkward silence after the punchline
- The listener immediately changes the subject
- The listener says something that shows they missed the point: "Oh, so how is she doing now?" (when the point was about the funny misunderstanding, not about the person's wellbeing)
- The listener produces the dreaded: "You had to be there."

**The "had to be there" problem:** This phrase signals that a story has failed to transport the listener into the experience. It typically happens when:
- The story relies on shared context the listener doesn't have
- The humor or significance depended on knowing the people involved
- The teller couldn't recreate the atmosphere, tone, or timing that made the original moment memorable
- The story lacks sufficient evaluation — the teller felt something strongly but couldn't convey *why*

This connects to the concept of **tellability** — what makes a story worth telling in a particular context. A tellable story has features that are "significant or surprising and worthy of being reported." A story that was highly tellable to someone who was present may lose its tellability when retold to someone who wasn't.

### 1.8 The Two Questions Every Story Must Answer

To summarize the core requirements of conversational narrative:

**Question 1: "And then what happened?"**
This is the referential function of narrative — the events, in sequence. This is the skeleton of the story. Without events, there is no story.

**Question 2: "So what?"**
This is the evaluative function — the reason the story matters, the point, the emotional or social significance. Without evaluation, events are just a list of things that happened.

**Most narrative failures come from failing to answer one of these questions adequately.**

## 2. COMMON NARRATIVE FAILURES AND WHY THEY CAUSE SOCIAL FRICTION

### 2.1 Missing the Point: Telling a Story Without Evaluation

**What it looks like:** "So I went to the store, and they were out of milk, so I went to the other store, and they had milk, so I bought it."

**Why it fails:** The listener thinks: "Why are you telling me this?" There's no complication, no emotional significance, no unexpected element. The teller has answered "what happened" but not "so what."

**The Gricean connection:** This violates Grice's Maxim of Relevance. Listeners assume that people tell stories for a reason — that the story is somehow relevant or interesting. When it isn't, the listener must do extra cognitive work trying to figure out why it was told, and may conclude the speaker doesn't understand social norms about what merits a story.

**Fix:** Before telling a story, ask yourself: "What is the point? What do I want the listener to feel or understand?" If you can't answer this, it may not be worth telling as a story. It might be better as a simple statement: "I had a hard time finding milk today."

**Research note:** Studies of narrative in autism consistently find that autistic individuals may produce narratives that focus heavily on events (the referential function) while underutilizing evaluation (the emotional/social significance function). This manifests as narratives that are "locally coherent" (each sentence makes sense) but "globally incoherent" (the listener can't determine the overall point). Research by Diehl et al. (2006) and others documents that autistic narrators may focus on specific local details (e.g., particular objects in the setting) rather than the gist or emotional significance of the story.

### 2.2 Too Much Orientation: Excessive Scene-Setting

**What it looks like:** "OK so it was Tuesday. No wait, it might have been Wednesday. Actually, I think it was Tuesday because I had my dentist appointment that week and that's always on Monday, so it must have been Tuesday. Anyway, I was at this restaurant — do you know the one on Fifth Street? No, not that one. The other one. The Italian place. Well, it used to be Italian but now it's more of a fusion thing. So I was there with my friend — you don't know her, but she's this woman I met at yoga..."

**Why it fails:** This violates Grice's Maxim of Quantity — providing far more information than is required. The listener is waiting for the story to start, and every additional detail creates the expectation that it must be relevant (because why else would you mention it?). When the details turn out to be irrelevant, the listener feels their time has been wasted.

**Social friction:** The listener may:
- Start showing disengagement signals (checking phone, reduced eye contact)
- Try to hurry the story along ("OK, so what happened?")
- Internally become frustrated or bored
- Develop the expectation that you generally tell boring stories (which affects future willingness to listen)

**Fix:** 
- **The two-sentence orientation rule:** Try to set the scene in two sentences maximum. "I was at a restaurant with a friend. We'd just ordered."
- **Only include details that matter to the punchline.** If it doesn't matter which restaurant or which day, don't specify them.
- **Prune while speaking.** If you catch yourself going into excessive detail, stop and say: "Anyway, the point is..."

### 2.3 Too Little Orientation: Jumping into Action Without Context

**What it looks like:** "So she just *slammed* it down on the table and walked out!"

**Why it fails:** The listener has no idea who "she" is, what was slammed, where this happened, or what led up to it. The complicating action means nothing without orientation. The listener must interrupt to ask clarifying questions, which disrupts the narrative flow.

**Social friction:**
- The listener feels confused and mildly excluded
- Repeated clarification requests can make the teller feel like a bad storyteller
- The emotional impact of the climax is diluted because the listener is still processing basic facts

**Fix:** 
- **Provide minimal but sufficient context before the action.** "My sister and I were having dinner, and I mentioned her ex, and she just *slammed* her fork down and walked out."
- **The "listener's shoes" test:** Before telling a story, briefly consider: what does the listener need to know to understand the first event? Give them that, then proceed.

### 2.4 Chronological Confusion: Events Out of Order Without Clear Markers

**What it looks like:** "So we ended up at the hospital. Oh but before that, we were at the park. And actually the reason we went to the park was because earlier that morning... wait, let me back up."

**Why it fails:** Listeners expect stories to be told in chronological order unless clearly marked otherwise. Out-of-order events without temporal markers ("before that," "earlier that day," "what I didn't know at the time was") create confusion. The listener has to constantly revise their mental model of what happened.

**Social friction:**
- The listener feels lost and may stop trying to follow
- The narrative feels disorganized, which reflects poorly on the teller
- The emotional arc is disrupted because events are presented at the wrong moments for dramatic effect

**Fix:**
- **Default to chronological order.** If in doubt, tell events in the order they happened.
- **If you must go out of order, signal clearly:** "OK, to understand this, you need to know something that happened that morning..." or "I didn't find this out until later, but..."
- **Use temporal markers consistently:** "first," "then," "after that," "the next thing I knew"

### 2.5 Losing the Thread: Digressing Without Returning

**What it looks like:** "So I was at the airport — which, by the way, they totally renovated the terminal, have you been there recently? It's so different now. They have this whole new food court with... actually that reminds me, did I tell you about the restaurant that opened near my house? ..." [original airport story is never finished]

**Why it fails:** The listener was invested in the airport story and is now tracking a completely different topic. The common phrase "but I digress" exists specifically because this is such a frequent problem. Unintentional digressions that go unresolved violate the listener's expectation that narrative threads will be completed. Research on conversational discourse shows that too many digressions leave listeners "wondering what the main point of the piece was in the first place."

**Social friction:**
- Listener frustration (they want to know how the airport story ends)
- Loss of coherence — the conversation feels scattered and purposeless
- If habitual, listeners may lose patience with the teller generally

**Fix:**
- **Self-monitor:** Notice when you've gone off topic. A useful phrase: "But anyway, back to what I was saying..."
- **Save tangents for later:** "Oh, that reminds me of something — I'll tell you in a sec, but first let me finish this."
- **Use verbal flags:** "Quick tangent:" signals that you know you're digressing and will come back.

### 2.6 The Anticlimactic Ending

**What it looks like:** "So I spent three hours waiting, and I was SO nervous, and they finally called my name... and it was fine. Everything was normal." Or: "And then... I just went home."

**Why it fails:** An anticlimactic ending occurs when the narrative builds up tension and expectation only to conclude with a trivial or mundane resolution. Research on audience reception shows that when this happens, readers/listeners "feel cheated, confused, and unsatisfied" because there was an implicit contract that the buildup would lead to a proportional payoff.

**The unspoken contract:** The amount of conversational time and emotional investment a teller asks for should be proportional to the payoff. Labov recognized that the evaluation component "provides justification for the narrative's claim on a greater portion of conversational time." When the payoff doesn't justify the time investment, the listener feels that contract was violated.

**Social friction:**
- The listener may respond with awkward silence or a flat "Oh... OK"
- The listener may feel they wasted their attention
- Future stories from this teller may be met with less enthusiasm ("Last time she told me this big buildup story and it went nowhere")

**Fix:**
- **Calibrate the buildup to the payoff.** If the ending is mundane, don't build it up dramatically.
- **Some stories work precisely because they're anticlimactic** — but only if the anticlimax IS the point, and you frame it that way: "So after all that panic and drama... it turned out to be nothing. I felt so ridiculous."
- **Reframe stories with weak endings as being about your reaction or feeling rather than the event:** "The scariest three hours of my life, and it was completely fine" makes the story about the emotional experience, not the event.

### 2.7 Missing the Audience: Inappropriate Story for the Listener or Context

**What it looks like:** Telling a detailed story about a wild party to a work colleague you barely know. Telling a gross medical story over dinner. Telling a story about an expensive purchase to a friend who's struggling financially. Telling an inside joke to someone who doesn't know the people involved.

**Why it fails:** Effective storytelling requires **audience design** — tailoring the story to the specific listener and context. Without proper consideration of context, "a joke meant to be light-hearted and playful may come across as insensitive or offensive if told in an inappropriate setting or to the wrong audience." This connects to the concept of cultural and contextual sensitivity in narrative.

**Social friction:**
- The listener may feel uncomfortable, offended, or excluded
- The listener may judge the teller as lacking social awareness
- It can damage the relationship or professional reputation

**Fix:**
- **Consider the listener:** What is their relationship to you? What do they know? What might make them uncomfortable?
- **Consider the context:** Is this a formal or informal setting? What topics are appropriate here?
- **Read the room:** If you start a story and notice discomfort, you can pivot: "Actually, never mind — not the right time for that story."

### 2.8 The "Had to Be There" Problem

**What it looks like:** You tell a story that was hilarious when it happened, but when you tell it, no one laughs. Often ends with the teller saying, "I guess you had to be there" or "It was funnier in person."

**Why it fails:** The story's tellability was bound to the specific context of the original experience. The humor or significance depended on elements that can't be reproduced in a retelling: the timing, the setting, the people's personalities, the build-up of a shared experience, the facial expressions, the atmosphere.

**The tellability problem:** Researchers define tellability as the quality that makes a story "worth telling" — features that are "significant or surprising and worthy of being reported in specific contexts." A story can be highly tellable in the moment it happens (you really did have to be there) but lose its tellability in retelling because the essential context doesn't transfer.

**Fix:**
- **Before telling, ask:** "Will this be funny/interesting to someone who wasn't there?" If not, either skip it or tell it differently.
- **Provide the context that was shared:** Instead of "Mark made the funniest face," try "Mark has this thing where he tries really hard to keep a straight face when something embarrasses him — like, his mouth will be totally flat but his eyes are panicking..."
- **Frame it honestly:** "OK, this is probably one of those 'you had to be there' things, but..." — this lowers expectations and reduces the risk of an anticlimactic response.
- **Recreate the atmosphere:** Use vocal performance, gesture, reported speech, and sensory details to transport the listener as close to the original experience as possible.

## 3. EXPLAINING AND DESCRIBING

### 3.1 How to Give a Clear Explanation

Explanation is a distinct discourse genre from storytelling. While stories are organized chronologically and driven by events, explanations are organized logically and driven by concepts.

**Core principles of clear explanation:**

**Chunking:** Research on pedagogical discourse shows that breaking information into smaller, manageable units ("chunks") reduces cognitive load. Rather than presenting all information at once, effective explainers present it in discrete segments, each building on the previous one. This principle (also called "The Segmenting Principle") indicates that learning is optimized when material is presented in segments rather than as a continuous block.

**Ordering:** Present information in a logical sequence:
- Known → Unknown (start with what the listener already knows)
- Simple → Complex (build from basic to advanced)
- General → Specific (overview first, then details) — or sometimes Specific → General depending on the listener
- Cause → Effect (explain why something exists before how it works)

**Checking understanding:** Effective explainers periodically verify that the listener is following. This can be done through:
- Direct questions: "Does that make sense so far?"
- Comprehension probes: "So what do you think would happen if...?"
- Watching for confused expressions or lack of backchannels
- Inviting questions: "Stop me if anything's unclear"

**Signposting:** Using discourse markers to show the structure: "There are three main reasons. First... Second... Third..." This gives the listener a cognitive scaffold — they know what to expect and can track progress.

**Analogy and comparison:** Connecting new information to things the listener already understands: "It's kind of like how..." This leverages existing knowledge to create understanding.

### 3.2 How to Describe Something the Listener Hasn't Seen

Description is a spatial and sensory discourse genre. Research on spatial cognition and language shows that when describing something, speakers must select from available frameworks and organize information strategically.

**Principles of effective description:**

**Salient features first:** Start with the most noticeable or defining characteristics. Research on salience shows it is "the quality or state of an object with the help of which it stands out noticeably against the background of other units." When describing a room, for example, start with the dominant feature (a huge window, an unusual color) rather than cataloguing every item from left to right.

**Spatial organization:** Choose a consistent reference framework:
- **Viewer-based:** "As you walk in, to your left is..." (organized from a specific viewpoint)
- **Object-based:** "At the center of the room is... and around it are..." (organized around a focal point)
- **Functional:** "The cooking area is on one side... the eating area is on the other..." (organized by use)

**Concrete over abstract:** Use specific, vivid details rather than vague generalizations. "A bright red door with a brass knocker" is more useful than "a nice door."

**Comparative frames:** Relating the unfamiliar to the familiar: "It's about the size of a school bus" or "Imagine a regular coffee shop, but everything is pink."

### 3.3 The Instruction Genre: Step-by-Step vs. Overview-First

Research on procedural discourse identifies two main approaches to giving instructions:

**Step-by-step (sequential):** Present each action in order. This is the default structure for procedural texts.
- Best when: the listener needs to follow along in real time; the sequence is strict; each step depends on the previous one
- Structure: "First, do X. Then do Y. After that, Z."
- Risk: The listener may feel lost without knowing where the process is going

**Overview-first (top-down):** Give a summary of the whole process, then fill in the details.
- Best when: the listener needs to understand the big picture before the details make sense; the process is complex; the listener needs to make decisions along the way
- Structure: "Basically, what you're going to do is [summary]. Let me walk you through the details."
- Advantage: The listener has a mental map and can situate each step within it

**Practical hybrid:** The most effective approach often combines both: "OK, here's what we're going to do: basically, we need to [overview]. Let me walk you through it step by step. First..."

### 3.4 How to Give Directions (Spatial Narrative)

Giving directions is a specialized form of spatial discourse that combines instruction with description. Research on wayfinding instructions shows that three cognitive operations are involved:
1. Activating an internal representation of the environment (your mental map)
2. Planning a route through that mental representation
3. Formulating the procedure the listener should execute

**Key components of effective directions:**

**Landmarks over distances:** Research shows that landmarks are crucial in human wayfinding. "Turn right at the big church" is more useful than "go 400 meters east." Local landmarks along the route and global landmarks that support general orientation are both important.

**Action + Reference Point structure:** Good directions pair what to do with what to look for: "When you see the gas station, turn left."

**Orientation information:** Not just turn-by-turn directions, but information that helps the listener orient themselves: "You'll be heading toward the river" or "The mountains should be on your right."

**Confirmation points:** "After about 5 minutes, you should see a park on your left. If you see that, you're going the right way."

**Common ground:** Both the direction giver and follower must establish shared understanding. This means checking: "Do you know where [landmark] is?" before using it as a reference point.

## 4. ARGUMENTATION AND PERSUASION

### 4.1 How to Structure an Argument

The Toulmin Model (developed by philosopher Stephen Toulmin) provides a practical framework for everyday argumentation. It breaks arguments into six components:

1. **Claim:** The main assertion you're making ("We should switch to a different vendor")
2. **Grounds (Evidence):** The facts, data, or observations that support the claim ("The current vendor has missed three deadlines this quarter")
3. **Warrant:** The reasoning that connects the evidence to the claim — often unstated ("Reliable delivery is essential for our business") — this is the logical bridge
4. **Backing:** Additional support for the warrant ("Our clients have contract deadlines that depend on timely delivery")
5. **Qualifier:** Words that temper the claim, making it more reasonable ("Usually," "In most cases," "I think," "probably")
6. **Rebuttal:** Acknowledgment of potential counterarguments ("I know they're cheaper, but...")

**In everyday conversation, the structure is usually simpler:**
- **Claim + Evidence:** "We should eat here — it has great reviews."
- **Claim + Evidence + Qualifier:** "I think we should probably go with this option — it seems like the best balance of cost and quality, but I'm open to other ideas."

**Key insight:** In everyday arguing, inferred warrants (unstated logical connections) are common. People don't usually spell out every step of their reasoning. But when arguments break down, it's often because the warrant isn't shared — the two people have different unstated assumptions about what matters.

### 4.2 How to Present a Case to Someone Who Disagrees

Research on argumentation and face-work shows that everyday arguing has a regular structure: Speaker A makes a claim, Speaker B disputes it, then Speaker A either supports their claim or directly addresses the disagreement. Each move has implications for "face" — the identity claims of self, other, and relationship.

**Effective strategies when someone disagrees:**

1. **Establish common ground first:** "I think we actually agree on the main thing — that we want [shared goal]. Where we differ is how to get there."

2. **Validate their position:** "I understand why you think that — it makes sense given [their reasoning]. Here's what I'm seeing differently..." Research shows that "what works best is feeling like someone else really gets you; that they understand your perspective to such a degree that they would adopt it too if they were in your situation."

3. **Use qualifiers:** Soften absolute claims. "I might be wrong, but..." or "In my experience..." is more persuasive than "You're wrong because..."

4. **Acknowledge complexity:** "I don't think there's one right answer here, but my concern is..."

5. **Ask questions rather than making assertions:** Research suggests that asking questions can be an effective alternative to direct argumentation for persuasion. Questions invite the other person to think through the issue rather than defend their position.

6. **Avoid attacking the person:** Keep the focus on the idea, the evidence, or the reasoning — not on the other person's intelligence, motives, or character.

### 4.3 Logical Argument vs. Persuasive Narrative

These are two fundamentally different approaches to convincing someone:

**Logical argument (Logos):**
- Relies on evidence, reasoning, and logical structure
- Aims to convince through rational analysis
- Works best when the audience values facts and is open to changing their mind based on evidence
- Structure: Claim → Evidence → Reasoning → Conclusion
- Strength: Stands on its own merits regardless of who presents it

**Persuasive narrative (Pathos + Ethos):**
- Relies on stories, emotional engagement, and personal credibility
- Aims to persuade through connection, empathy, and identification
- Works best when the audience is emotionally invested or resistant to pure logic
- Structure: Story → Emotional response → Desired conclusion
- Strength: Makes abstract issues feel concrete and personal

**Research finding:** Studies show that "people are often influenced more by anecdotes than by aggregate statistical evidence." When people are transported into a story, it "blunts their attention to facts" — which means narrative persuasion can be more powerful than logical argument, but also more manipulative.

**The practical reality:** Most effective persuasion combines both. Aristotle's three modes of persuasion — logos (logic), pathos (emotion), and ethos (credibility) — work together. A compelling argument has good evidence AND connects emotionally AND comes from a credible source.

### 4.4 How Stories Are Used as Evidence in Argumentation

In everyday conversation, people frequently use stories as evidence for claims:

- **"My cousin tried that and it didn't work"** — personal anecdote as evidence against a course of action
- **"I read about this woman who..."** — third-party narrative as supporting evidence
- **"Every time I've done that, the same thing happens"** — repeated experience as pattern evidence

**How stories function as evidence:**
- They make abstract claims concrete and vivid
- They provide specific examples that feel more "real" than statistics
- They create emotional engagement with the conclusion
- They allow the listener to draw their own conclusions (rather than being told what to think)

**Limitations of narrative evidence:**
- A single story does not prove a general rule (anecdotal evidence fallacy)
- Stories can be cherry-picked to support any position
- Emotional impact can overwhelm rational assessment
- When weak facts are presented in story form, they become more convincing than they should be (and when strong facts are presented in story form, they may actually become LESS convincing, according to research by Kellogg School)

## 5. CONVERSATIONAL NARRATIVE vs. WRITTEN NARRATIVE

### 5.1 Oral Storytelling vs. Written Storytelling

| Feature | Oral (Conversational) | Written |
|---|---|---|
| **Planning** | Spontaneous, real-time | Planned, revised, edited |
| **Audience feedback** | Immediate and visible | Delayed and indirect |
| **Sentence structure** | Simpler, shorter, more repetition | More complex, varied |
| **Vocabulary** | More common words, less jargon | Can use specialized vocabulary |
| **Repair** | Speaker can self-correct in real-time | Writer revises before publishing |
| **Prosody** | Pitch, volume, pace, and pause convey meaning | Must be conveyed through word choice and punctuation |
| **Gesture and expression** | Available and actively used | Absent; must be described |
| **Co-construction** | Listener actively contributes | Reader is passive during reception |
| **Coherence markers** | More frequent ("so," "and then," "you know") | Fewer, more formal ("consequently," "furthermore") |
| **Repetition** | Common and functional (helps working memory) | Usually avoided |
| **Context** | Shared physical setting, shared knowledge | Must be constructed entirely in text |
| **Length** | Flexible, negotiated in real-time | Predetermined by format |

**Key difference:** The biggest difference between oral and written storytelling is that oral storytelling is spontaneous, in the moment, and performative. When you tell a story verbally, you see your audience's reactions and can adjust accordingly — adding detail where there's interest, speeding up where there's boredom. Written communication doesn't allow for this real-time adjustment.

### 5.2 Texting and Messaging Narrative

Text messaging creates a hybrid form that is written but shares many characteristics of oral discourse. Research shows that texting prioritizes efficiency and communicates "quickly with little regard for grammar, formality, punctuation or formal structure."

**Characteristics of texting narrative:**

- **Fragmented delivery:** Stories are told across multiple short messages rather than in one block
- **Real-time co-construction:** The listener can respond between fragments, influencing the telling
- **Emoji as evaluative devices:** Emoji function as pragmatic markers — they "upgrade or downgrade different speech acts" and serve as emotional commentary. A face emoji can function like a verbal backchannel or evaluation (😱 replaces "That's terrifying!")
- **Abbreviation and compression:** "omg wait" serves as a story preface; "lol" serves as evaluation
- **Temporal ambiguity:** Messages may arrive out of order or after a delay, creating different coherence challenges than face-to-face
- **Screenshot and quote evidence:** Texts can include forwarded evidence (screenshots of other conversations) as a form of embedded narration

**Emoji functions in narrative:**
- Activity emoji serve as separate discourse units that represent events
- Face emoji are expressive elements that comment on the proposition
- Emoji can signal story completion, frame playful interactions, or serve as closing sections

### 5.3 Email Narrative

Email occupies a middle ground between speech and formal writing. Research shows that email "may allow for a wider range of formality and informality, and blurred boundaries between each, than in traditional non-electronic writing."

**Characteristics of email narrative:**
- More structured than texting, closer to written prose
- Must "stand alone without the benefit of interpersonal interaction"
- Register (formality level) varies widely depending on relationship and context: academic emails tend to be more informal than business-letter emails
- Narrative types in email include descriptive, persuasive, expository, and personal narratives
- Context must be explicit because the reader may encounter the email hours or days later
- Subject line functions somewhat like a story preface — framing expectations

**Workplace email narrative:** Research characterizes workplace email as "language use somewhere between written and oral registers and formal and informal discourse." Navigating this requires "adaptive and varied knowledge depending on particular goals, hierarchy, and context."

### 5.4 Social Media Narrative

Social media has created a distinctive narrative form: the **micro-narrative** — short, impactful stories crafted for quick consumption and wide sharing.

**Platform-specific narrative characteristics:**

**Twitter/X:**
- Extreme compression (originally 140, now 280 characters)
- Thread format allows extended narrative across multiple posts
- Hashtags connect individual stories to broader conversations
- Public audience design — stories are crafted for wide reception, not a specific listener
- Evaluation often comes from audience (likes, retweets, replies)

**Instagram:**
- Visual narrative: images carry primary narrative load; text provides evaluation and context
- Stories format: ephemeral, "in-the-moment" narratives that create "genuine connection through their raw, unpolished feel"
- Compressed narrative arc — even 30-second stories have a beginning, middle, and end

**General social media narrative features:**
- **Audience-designed for wide reception:** Unlike conversational stories designed for a specific listener, social media stories must work for a diverse, often unknown audience
- **Compressed evaluation:** Hashtags and emoji add "emotional nuance and visual interest within limited character counts"
- **Performative:** Social media stories are performances of identity as much as reports of experience
- **Asynchronous reception:** Unlike conversation, the audience encounters the story at different times without the teller present
- **Missing backchannels:** The teller cannot see audience reactions in real time (likes and comments arrive later)

## 6. COHERENCE MARKERS — The Specific Words and Phrases That Create Narrative Structure

### 6.1 Overview

Discourse markers are words, phrases, or expressions that signal the structure, coherence, and flow of discourse. Research by Deborah Schiffrin and others shows that discourse markers create links between units of talk and serve as "processing instructions intended to help the hearer in integrating the unit hosting the marker into a coherent mental representation of the unfolding discourse." They reduce the cognitive effort required from the listener by signaling how each new piece of information relates to what came before.

### 6.2 Temporal Markers (Sequence and Time)

These markers organize events in time and signal the order in which things happened.

| Marker | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| "First" / "At first" | Signals the beginning of a sequence | "First, we went to the museum." |
| "Then" / "And then" | Signals the next event in sequence | "Then she called me back." |
| "Next" | Signals the following step | "Next, you'll want to add the eggs." |
| "After that" | Signals temporal succession | "After that, things got weird." |
| "Before that" | Signals going back in time | "But before that, I should mention..." |
| "Meanwhile" | Signals simultaneous events | "Meanwhile, my sister was having her own crisis." |
| "Eventually" / "Finally" | Signals end of a long sequence | "Finally, they let us in." |
| "At that point" | Signals a specific moment in the timeline | "At that point, I just gave up." |
| "Suddenly" | Signals unexpected change | "Suddenly, the lights went out." |
| "By then" | Signals accumulated time | "By then, I was already exhausted." |

**In conversation, "and then" is the most common temporal marker.** Labov noted that clauses in oral narrative are typically "sequentially ordered" — each clause represents an event that happened after the previous one. "And then" makes this default ordering explicit.

### 6.3 Causal Markers (Reason and Result)

These markers signal cause-and-effect relationships — why something happened or what resulted from it.

| Marker | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| "Because" / "'Cause" | Signals the reason for something | "I left because I was uncomfortable." |
| "So" | Signals a result or consequence | "So I decided to just go home." |
| "That's why" | Explains the reason retroactively | "That's why I don't trust him anymore." |
| "Therefore" | Formal logical consequence | "Therefore, I think we should reconsider." |
| "As a result" | Signals an outcome | "As a result, the whole plan fell apart." |
| "Since" | Signals a reason (presupposed as known) | "Since you're here, can you help me?" |
| "Which means" | Signals an implication | "Which means we have to start over." |
| "That led to" | Signals causation in narrative | "That led to a huge argument." |

**Key insight:** Research shows that causal relations are "often expressed implicitly" in discourse — speakers frequently leave the causal connection unstated, expecting the listener to infer it. "I forgot my umbrella. I got soaked" doesn't need "therefore" because the causal link is obvious. But when the causal connection is NOT obvious, explicit markers become essential.

### 6.4 Contrastive Markers (Contrast and Concession)

These markers signal that what follows contrasts with, contradicts, or qualifies what came before.

| Marker | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| "But" | Signals contrast or unexpected turn | "I thought it'd be easy, but it wasn't." |
| "However" | More formal contrast | "The plan seemed good. However, there were problems." |
| "Although" / "Even though" | Signals concession | "Although I was tired, I kept going." |
| "On the other hand" | Signals an alternative perspective | "On the other hand, it was a good learning experience." |
| "Actually" | Signals correction of expectation | "I actually kind of liked it." |
| "Instead" | Signals substitution | "Instead of complaining, she just left." |
| "Still" / "Yet" | Signals persistence despite contrast | "It was hard, but I still enjoyed it." |
| "Except" | Signals exception | "Everything was great, except the ending." |

**Research finding:** Relations involving concession (acknowledging something while presenting a contrasting point) tend to be explicitly marked with discourse connectors, unlike causal relations which can be implicit. This suggests that listeners have more difficulty inferring contrastive relationships without explicit markers.

### 6.5 Additive Markers (Adding Information)

These markers signal that new information supplements or extends what was just said.

| Marker | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| "And" | Basic addition | "And she brought her dog." |
| "Also" | Additional point | "She also forgot her phone." |
| "Plus" | Informal addition (often evaluative) | "Plus, he was really rude about it." |
| "On top of that" | Adding to an already significant list | "On top of that, they charged us extra." |
| "Not only... but also" | Emphatic addition | "Not only was it late, but it was also wrong." |
| "Moreover" / "Furthermore" | Formal addition | "Moreover, the data supports this conclusion." |
| "What's more" | Conversational emphatic addition | "What's more, he didn't even apologize." |

**Note on "and":** In conversational narrative, "and" is the most frequent connector. It often serves not just as addition but as a general-purpose narrative connector — "and then," "and so," "and the thing was."

### 6.6 Evaluative Markers (Attitude and Significance)

These are uniquely important in narrative because they carry the evaluation — they tell the listener WHY the story matters and HOW to feel about it.

| Marker | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| "The thing is" / "Here's the thing" | Introduces the key point | "The thing is, she never told anyone." |
| "The best part was" | Highlights a positive peak | "The best part was when he realized his mistake." |
| "The worst part was" | Highlights a negative peak | "The worst part was we couldn't even leave." |
| "What really got me was" | Signals personal emotional peak | "What really got me was how calm she was about it." |
| "I couldn't believe" | Signals surprise/disbelief | "I couldn't believe he said that." |
| "Honestly" / "Seriously" | Intensifies sincerity/disbelief | "Honestly, I've never seen anything like it." |
| "The crazy thing is" | Frames something as surprising | "The crazy thing is it actually worked." |
| "The point is" | Explicitly states the evaluation | "The point is, you can't trust everyone." |
| "I mean" | Signals elaboration or clarification of attitude | "It was bad. I mean, REALLY bad." |

**Labov's evaluative devices in more detail:**
- **Intensifiers:** Expressive phonology (emphasis, drawn-out vowels), repetition ("He was gone. Completely gone."), and ritual utterances
- **Comparators:** Devices that contrast what happened with what could have/should have happened — negatives ("I didn't even know"), futures, questions, superlatives ("It was the worst day of my life")
- **Correlatives:** Multiple parallel constructions that build intensity
- **Explicatives:** Devices that explain the narrator's feelings or reasoning

**Critical function:** Research shows that discourse markers can be "used together and/or repetitively in narratives to present important information." By placing markers before and after significant narrative elements, speakers "plan and organize their story and can verify understanding in the listener."

### 6.7 Topic Management Markers (Navigation and Control)

These markers help manage the flow of a conversation, especially during and between stories.

| Marker | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| "So" (discourse marker) | Signals return to main topic or new phase | "So, what were you saying about...?" |
| "Anyway" / "Anyhow" | Signals return from digression or topic shift | "Anyway, the point is..." |
| "Speaking of which" | Signals topic shift via association | "Speaking of which, did you hear about...?" |
| "Going back to" | Signals return to previous topic | "Going back to what you said earlier..." |
| "By the way" | Signals a tangent or aside | "By the way, I meant to ask you..." |
| "So yeah" | Signals story conclusion/wrap-up | "So yeah, that was my weekend." |
| "Oh, that reminds me" | Signals associative topic shift | "Oh, that reminds me — I need to tell you something." |
| "Long story short" | Signals skipping to the conclusion | "Long story short, we missed the flight." |
| "But I digress" | Acknowledges going off-topic | "But I digress — where was I?" |

**Structural function:** Research by Schiffrin and others shows that these markers serve as "topic-framers, giving turns and marking topic shifts." They help both speaker and listener track where they are in the conversational structure.

### 6.8 How the Presence/Absence of Markers Affects Perceived Coherence

**With markers:**
"I got to the airport early **because** I was worried about traffic. **But** there was a long line at security. **So** I almost missed my flight. **The thing is**, I had checked in online, **but** they still made me wait. **Anyway**, I made it, **but** barely."

**Without markers:**
"I got to the airport early. I was worried about traffic. There was a long line at security. I almost missed my flight. I had checked in online. They still made me wait. I made it. Barely."

**The difference:** Both passages convey the same events. But the unmarked version requires significantly more cognitive effort from the listener to process. Research confirms that discourse markers "decrease the cognitive effort required from the hearer to interpret the utterance" by signaling how each piece of information relates to the previous one. Without them, listeners must independently infer whether each new clause is a cause, a contrast, an addition, or a result.

**In conversation, insufficient marking creates the impression that:**
- The speaker is disorganized or uncertain
- The narrative lacks a clear point or direction
- The speaker may not understand the relationship between the events they're describing

**However:** Excessive or inappropriate marking can also cause problems. Overusing formal markers ("Furthermore," "Moreover," "Consequently") in casual conversation sounds stilted and unnatural. Conversational narrative relies primarily on simple markers: "so," "and then," "but," "because," "anyway," and "the thing is."

**Research note on autism and discourse markers:** While the research doesn't consistently show that autistic individuals use fewer discourse markers overall, studies indicate that the use of evaluative markers specifically — the ones that carry emotional and social significance — may be reduced. This connects to the broader finding that narrative evaluation (the "so what") is the area of greatest difficulty.

## Summary: Key Principles for Narrative Competence

1. **Every story needs a point (evaluation).** Before telling a story, know why you're telling it and make that clear to the listener.

2. **Use story prefaces.** Signal that you're about to tell a story to secure the floor and set expectations.

3. **Calibrate detail to significance.** Don't overload orientation for a simple story; don't under-explain a complex one.

4. **Provide backchannels when listening.** Even minimal responses ("mm-hmm," "right," "wow") signal engagement and are socially expected.

5. **Watch for listener signals.** If the listener seems bored or lost, adjust — speed up, add evaluation, or wrap up.

6. **Use coherence markers.** "But," "so," "because," "the thing is" — these simple words do enormous work in making speech easy to follow.

7. **Match the story to the audience and context.** Consider what the listener knows, cares about, and would find appropriate.

8. **Stories and arguments serve different functions.** Know when to use a story (to connect emotionally, to make something vivid) vs. an argument (to convince through evidence and reasoning).

9. **Different channels require different narrative strategies.** Texting, email, social media, and face-to-face conversation each have their own narrative conventions.

10. **When in doubt, keep it short and clear.** A brief story told well is always better than a long story told badly.
