# Category 24 Research: Theory of Identity and Stance

## Companion Research File to Category 24: Identity and Stance in Interaction

This file provides the detailed theoretical grounding for the main Category 24 document. It covers the foundational linguistic, sociolinguistic, and philosophical work that underwrites the stance/identity distinction, and connects that work to the pragmatic machinery used throughout the Pragmatics Learning Hub and its Manipulation Sub-Hub.

---

## 1. The Intellectual Context

Before the 1980s, pragmatic theory treated the speaker and the hearer as relatively fixed points. A speaker had intentions; a hearer interpreted them. The task of pragmatics was to explain how interpretation worked given those fixed points. The Grice programme, classical speech act theory, and early politeness theory all assumed this picture.

Three developments pushed the field past that assumption:

1. **The discourse turn** in sociolinguistics. Researchers (including Deborah Tannen, John Gumperz, Elinor Ochs) began treating interaction, not the sentence, as the primary unit of analysis. Once interaction was in focus, the speaker and the hearer stopped being fixed points -- they became things that got built, turn by turn, in the interaction itself.

2. **The linguistic anthropology of identity**. Ethnographic work on language socialization (Ochs, Schieffelin, Heath, and others) showed that identities are not the input to interaction but the output. Children do not enter interaction as pre-formed selves who then learn words for what they already are. They become selves through participation in interaction.

3. **The discursive psychology tradition**. Bronwyn Davies, Rom Harré, Jonathan Potter, Margaret Wetherell and others argued that psychological categories -- belief, attitude, personality, self -- are better understood as discursive accomplishments than as internal states waiting to be expressed.

Out of these currents, two complementary vocabularies emerged. One is the vocabulary of **stance**. The other is the vocabulary of **positioning** and **identity work**. They are not rivals. They describe the same set of phenomena from adjacent angles. This research file covers both, because the Category 24 document uses both.

## 2. Du Bois and the Stance Triangle

### 2.1 Du Bois's Core Move

John W. Du Bois's chapter "The stance triangle" in Alexandra Jaffe's *Stance: Sociolinguistic Perspectives* (Du Bois 2007) is the single most widely cited theoretical statement on stance in interaction. Du Bois's move is to define stance as a **public, observable, three-part act**.

The three parts:

1. **Evaluate** an object.
2. **Position** the subject (self) with respect to the object.
3. **Align** with another subject who has also taken a stance on the same object.

Du Bois renders this geometrically as a triangle. The two subjects occupy two of the vertices; the object occupies the third. An arrow runs from each subject to the object (evaluation); an arrow runs between the two subjects (alignment).

This is deliberately formal. Du Bois is not describing a feeling. He is describing a move in a game -- a move that has structure, that can be tracked across turns, that commits the speaker to consequences.

### 2.2 Evaluation

Evaluation is the linguistic act of characterizing an object as good or bad, true or false, interesting or boring, right or wrong. In English, evaluation is marked by an open-ended inventory of adjectives, adverbs, modal verbs, prosodic emphases, and discourse markers. "That's ridiculous." "I think it's fine." "Obviously correct." "Honestly pretty good."

Du Bois's point is that evaluation is not an optional add-on to description. Much of what looks like description is already evaluation. Calling an argument "straightforward" is an evaluation. Calling a plan "ambitious" is an evaluation. The evaluative layer is dense and often implicit.

### 2.3 Positioning

Positioning is the act of locating yourself -- the speaker -- relative to the object you have just evaluated. If you call a plan "ambitious," you have simultaneously positioned yourself as someone who sees ambition as marked (otherwise why name it?). If you call it "ambitious but doable," you have positioned yourself as supportive. If you call it "ambitious but unrealistic," you have positioned yourself as skeptical.

Positioning is where the speaker's stake in the object becomes visible. This is why positioning is the part of the triangle that most interacts with identity: repeated positionings of yourself in particular ways are the raw material out of which identity is built.

### 2.4 Alignment

Alignment is the relational move. When you take a stance on an object, you are almost always taking it in the context of someone else's prior stance on the same object. You can align with that prior stance, disalign from it, or -- this is the subtle case -- partially align, accepting some parts of the prior position while adjusting others.

Du Bois emphasizes that alignment is a distinct move from positioning. You can position yourself the same way another speaker did without aligning with them (for instance, if you are signaling independence) and you can align with another speaker without taking the identical position (for instance, when you say "I see what you mean" to accept their framing without endorsing their conclusion).

### 2.5 Why the Triangle Matters

The stance triangle matters because it makes stance **auditable**. Once you see that every stance move has three parts, you can trace each part. You can ask:

- What object is being evaluated?
- What position is the speaker taking with respect to it?
- What alignment move is being performed toward the previous speaker?

This tracing is what makes it possible to notice manipulation patterns like concern trolling, where the evaluation ("are you sure you're okay?") is framed innocently, but the positioning (self as carer, other as worried-about-one) and the alignment (forcing the target into a position they did not claim) are the manipulation.

## 3. Positioning Theory: Davies and Harré

### 3.1 The Original Paper

Bronwyn Davies and Rom Harré's 1990 paper "Positioning: The Discursive Production of Selves" (Davies & Harré 1990) introduced the vocabulary of **positioning** into social psychology. Their target was the then-dominant concept of "role," which they regarded as too static. Roles were fixed categories assigned by institutions. Positioning, by contrast, was a dynamic, ongoing, conversational process.

Their core claims:

1. **Selves are discursively produced.** A person's self is not the expression of an underlying essence but the residue of positions they have occupied in conversations.
2. **Positioning is mutual.** Every time you position yourself, you simultaneously position the other person. If you cast yourself as the explainer, you have cast them as the learner.
3. **Positions carry rights and obligations.** Once you are positioned as (say) the expert, you have the right to make authoritative claims and the obligation to answer questions.
4. **Positions are defeasible.** You can refuse a position the other speaker has offered you. But refusal is itself a move in the positioning game, and it is not always available.

### 3.2 The Expanded Framework

Harré and Luk Van Langenhove's book *Positioning Theory* (Harré & Van Langenhove 1999) extended the framework. Three distinctions from the book are used in the main Category 24 document:

- **First-order vs. second-order positioning.** First-order positioning is the default, unreflective positioning that happens when speakers simply take up positions in the flow of talk. Second-order positioning is what happens when someone refuses or challenges a first-order position. The move "I do not accept that framing" is a second-order positioning move.

- **Interactive vs. reflexive positioning.** Interactive positioning is when one speaker positions another. Reflexive positioning is when a speaker positions themselves. Most conversational positioning is both simultaneously.

- **Tacit vs. intentional positioning.** Most positioning is tacit -- it happens without anyone making a conscious decision. Intentional positioning is what happens when a speaker deliberately places themselves or another into a particular position. Manipulation patterns tend to use intentional positioning that is disguised as tacit.

### 3.3 Application to the Manipulation Sub-Hub

The manipulation sub-hub of this project uses positioning theory as a technical vocabulary for describing what manipulation does. Coercive control, DARVO, gaslighting, concern trolling, diagnostic weaponization -- each of these patterns can be described as a repeated, intentional-but-disguised positioning move in which one party uses institutional, emotional, or informational advantages to place another party into positions they did not claim and cannot readily refuse.

## 4. Identity as Emergent: Ochs, Bucholtz and Hall

### 4.1 Ochs on Identity as Language Socialization

Elinor Ochs's paper "Constructing Social Identity: A Language Socialization Perspective" (Ochs 1993) is an early and precise statement of the emergence view. Ochs argues that identities are not pre-given categories that speakers express. They are achievements, built through repeated participation in language practices that are associated with particular social positions.

The mechanism is **indirect indexicality**. A linguistic feature (say, a softer speech style) directly indexes a stance (deference, non-imposition). That stance, repeated and conventionalized across many speakers in many contexts, comes indirectly to index an identity (femininity, junior status, politeness). The linguistic feature does not mean the identity -- it means the stance, which over time points to the identity.

This two-step indirection matters because it explains why stance moves can build identity without anyone intending them to. You do not have to mean "I am deferential" to be read as deferential. You only have to perform the stance moves that conventionally index deference.

### 4.2 Bucholtz and Hall's Five Principles

Mary Bucholtz and Kira Hall's 2005 paper "Identity and interaction: A sociocultural linguistic approach" (Bucholtz & Hall 2005) synthesized a decade of sociocultural linguistic work into five principles, which have become the standard framework for thinking about identity-in-interaction:

1. **Emergence**: Identity is the product, not the source, of linguistic and other semiotic practices. It emerges in interaction.

2. **Positionality**: Identities may include macro-demographic categories (gender, ethnicity, class), local cultural positions, and temporary interactional stances. All three levels are identity work.

3. **Indexicality**: Identity is built through indexical processes -- linguistic features point to stances; stances point to identities. The processes include overt mention of identity categories, implicatures about what is said, stance and style, and use of particular linguistic structures.

4. **Relationality**: Identities are constructed through relations with other identities, including through similarity, difference, authenticity/artifice, authority/delegitimation.

5. **Partialness**: Any account of identity is partial. Identity is produced jointly by multiple agents, is in flux, and cannot be exhaustively captured by any single description.

The paper is widely cited because it gives analysts a checklist for thinking about any identity-relevant moment in interaction: emergence (what is being built?), positionality (at what level?), indexicality (through what semiotic resources?), relationality (in contrast to what?), partialness (what is this account missing?).

### 4.3 Implications for Category 24

The emergence principle is what makes the stance/identity distinction matter. If identity were prior to interaction, there would be nothing for stance work to do except express it. Because identity is emergent -- built through accumulated stance -- stance work is identity work, and interventions at the stance level can affect the identity level over time.

The relationality principle is what makes stance assignments possible at all. If my identity is built in contrast to yours, then positioning you in a particular way also positions me. A concern troll who casts you as worried-about is simultaneously casting themselves as concerned. You cannot refuse the position without also refusing their self-positioning, which makes refusal emotionally harder.

## 5. Stance as Style: Kiesling and Eckert

### 5.1 Kiesling: Style Is Stance Made Durable

Scott Kiesling's chapter "Style as stance" in Jaffe's volume (Kiesling 2009) proposes that linguistic **style** -- the relatively stable way a speaker talks -- is best understood as the sedimentation of repeated stance moves. A speaker who repeatedly performs a stance of, say, confident expertise will over time be recognizable as having an "expert style." The style is not a thing separate from stance; it is what stance looks like when you zoom out.

This argument matters for Category 24 because it provides the bridge from the micro-level (single stance moves) to the macro-level (identity). Kiesling's claim is that there is no extra step between them. Identity-as-style just is the accumulated pattern of stance moves, with the additional fact that listeners learn to recognize the pattern as characteristic of a particular kind of person.

### 5.2 Eckert: The Indexical Field

Penelope Eckert's paper "Variation and the indexical field" (Eckert 2008) introduces the concept of the **indexical field**: the set of potential meanings that a linguistic feature carries. Eckert's key move is to argue that a given feature -- a particular vowel, a particular word choice, a particular intonation -- does not have one indexical meaning. It has a field of potential meanings, and which meaning is activated depends on the stance act the speaker is performing.

For example, using a word like "awesome" in a formal setting does not just mean "informal." It can index youthfulness, or friendliness, or unseriousness, or deliberate solidarity, or generational identity. Which of these meanings lands depends on the speaker, the context, the audience, and the stance the speaker is performing.

Eckert's framework matters for Category 24 because it explains why the same behavior can be stance-read differently by different interlocutors. An autistic speaker's flat prosody is not a single unambiguous signal. It occupies a field of potential meanings (cold, bored, tired, neutral, honest, autistic, confident, dismissive), and which meaning gets activated depends on the interlocutor's expectations. This is where the double empathy problem (Milton 2012) meets sociolinguistic theory: stance readings are field-dependent, and fields are co-constructed.

### 5.3 Jaffe's Editorial Synthesis

Alexandra Jaffe's introduction to *Stance: Sociolinguistic Perspectives* (Jaffe 2009) functions as a state-of-the-field statement. Three of Jaffe's observations are load-bearing for this category:

- **Stance is always social.** Even apparently private stances (an epistemic hedge, an affective reaction) are performed for an audience, real or imagined, and shaped by that audience.
- **Stance is always hierarchically interpretable.** A stance move can be read at multiple levels: as a move in this turn, as a move in this conversation, as a move typical of this speaker, as a move typical of people like this speaker.
- **Stance and power are inseparable.** Who gets to position whom, whose positions stick, and whose are discounted -- these are distributed according to existing power relations.

## 6. Testimonial Injustice: Fricker and Medina

### 6.1 Fricker on Credibility Deficits

Miranda Fricker's book *Epistemic Injustice: Power and the Ethics of Knowing* (Fricker 2007) introduces the concept of **testimonial injustice**: the wrong done to a speaker when their testimony is given less credibility than it deserves because of prejudice against their social identity. Fricker's core examples involve race and gender -- a woman's account of workplace harassment discounted because she is a woman; a Black witness's testimony disbelieved because of racial prejudice.

Fricker treats testimonial injustice as a philosophical problem in social epistemology. But the mechanism is pragmatic: credibility discount is accomplished through stance moves. The speaker is assigned the stance of unreliable knower -- through tone, through what gets taken up and what gets dropped, through the framing of their account in subsequent talk, through the way they are described to third parties. None of these moves need to be explicit. The cumulative effect is that the speaker's words stop functioning as testimony.

For Category 24, Fricker's work provides the technical vocabulary for a specific stance assignment: being positioned as someone whose words do not count. This assignment is especially consequential in institutional settings (legal, medical, social services, workplace) because those settings have formal procedures for registering credibility, and the stance assignments happen within those procedures.

### 6.2 Medina's Extension

José Medina's *The Epistemology of Resistance* (Medina 2012) extends Fricker's framework in two ways that matter for Category 24. First, Medina argues that testimonial injustice is cumulative: each individual credibility discount contributes to an accumulated epistemic position that becomes harder to exit over time. Second, Medina emphasizes the role of **resistance** -- the counter-moves that speakers make to push back against being positioned as unreliable. Medina's point is that resistance is not optional and not guaranteed to succeed, but it is the place where epistemic agency can be reasserted.

Medina's vocabulary of epistemic resistance maps directly onto Category 24's section 6 on stance reclamation. The three-move refusal described there -- name the event, refuse the position, re-position yourself -- is one specific form of epistemic resistance in Medina's sense.

## 7. Butler and the Performative Constitution of Identity

A brief but important citation: Judith Butler's *Gender Trouble* (Butler 1990) and subsequent work argued that identity categories like gender are not the expression of prior essences but are **performatively constituted** through repeated acts. The argument is philosophical, and Category 24 does not depend on its strong form. But Butler's point -- that the repetition of acts is what produces the appearance of a stable category -- is consonant with the emergence view in sociolinguistics (Ochs; Bucholtz & Hall) and with Kiesling's style-as-stance argument.

Category 24 cites Butler only for the general point. It does not take on Butler's theoretical framework wholesale, because Butler's argument operates at a scale (social categories, cultural norms, citational chains across history) that is larger than the conversational-turn scale at which pragmatics works. The compatibility is structural, not substantive.

## 8. Stance Markers: Biber and Finegan

### 8.1 The Corpus-Linguistic Inventory

Douglas Biber and Edward Finegan's corpus work on stance markers (Biber & Finegan 1989; Biber 2006) gave linguists their first systematic catalog of how stance is marked linguistically in English. Their categories -- **epistemic, affective, evaluative, attitudinal** -- are now standard in the stance literature.

The taxonomy is useful for Category 24 because it lets the main document offer a compact inventory of linguistic resources without overclaiming. The inventory is descriptive: these are the grammatical and lexical forms that English speakers use to perform stance. How those forms land in any particular interaction depends on context, audience, and the stance triangle.

### 8.2 Prosody and Non-Linguistic Stance

The corpus inventory captures words. Stance also operates at the level of prosody (pitch, loudness, speech rate, voice quality) and non-verbal channels (gaze, facial expression, body posture). The conversation-analytic literature (Goodwin; Couper-Kuhlen) has documented many of these, and their findings integrate with the stance framework. For an autistic audience, the prosodic and non-verbal channels are especially important because they are often where stance misreadings happen -- the words are fine, but the prosodic wrapper is read as marking a stance that was not intended.

## 9. Integration and Scope Limits

### 9.1 What the Theory Can Do

The theoretical apparatus assembled here -- Du Bois's triangle, Davies and Harré's positioning, Ochs and Bucholtz-and-Hall on emergence, Kiesling and Eckert on style and indexical fields, Fricker and Medina on testimonial injustice -- provides a consistent vocabulary for describing what happens when one person is placed into a conversational position they did not claim. It lets the analyst name the moves, track them across turns, and see how they accumulate into identity-relevant outcomes.

### 9.2 What It Cannot Do

The theory does not predict whether a particular stance move will succeed. Whether a stance assignment sticks depends on audience, on institutional context, on prior history, and on power asymmetries that the stance theory can name but cannot quantify. The theory is descriptive and explanatory, not predictive in the strong sense.

The theory also does not specify an ethics. It can say "this is a stance assignment"; it cannot, by itself, say "this stance assignment is wrong." The ethical analysis is carried out by adjacent frameworks -- Fricker's epistemic injustice, the coercive control literature, autism-affirming clinical and community work. Category 24 treats the ethical frameworks as partners to the descriptive theory, not replacements for it.

### 9.3 Disciplinary Cautions

A few methodological notes:

- **Do not confuse stance with personality.** Stance is an interactional move; personality is an attributional frame applied to a history of moves. Treating stance as personality is itself a stance move (usually a flattening one).
- **Do not treat the stance triangle as exhaustive.** Du Bois's triangle is a clarifying simplification. Real stance moves can be ambiguous, multi-layered, and contested. The triangle is a first pass, not the final word.
- **Do not conflate positioning with power.** Positioning can happen without differential power. Coercive positioning is a specific subset in which power amplifies the positioning move. Category 24 is specifically interested in this subset, but does not claim that all positioning is coercive.

## 10. Key Claims for the Manipulation Sub-Hub

The following claims are the load-bearing ones from this theoretical file that the manipulation sub-hub depends on:

1. **Stance is a public, observable act** (Du Bois 2007). It is not a feeling. Therefore a stance assignment is a move someone has done to you, not an interpretation you are imposing.

2. **Positioning is mutual and includes rights and obligations** (Davies & Harré 1990). When someone positions you, they have also repositioned themselves and have changed the obligation structure of the conversation.

3. **Identity is emergent, built out of accumulated stance** (Ochs 1993; Bucholtz & Hall 2005). This is why repeated stance assignments can consolidate into identity -- there is no separate prior identity that stance is merely expressing.

4. **The same linguistic feature can carry multiple indexical meanings** (Eckert 2008). Autistic stance defaults occupy fields of meaning that are co-constructed with the interlocutor, which is why misreadings are systematic rather than random.

5. **Credibility discount is a specific, namable stance assignment** (Fricker 2007; Medina 2012). It is not merely "not being believed"; it is being placed into the position of unreliable knower, with predictable consequences for what happens next in the conversation.

6. **Resistance is possible but limited** (Medina 2012). Refusal moves are real pragmatic acts, but their success depends on structural conditions the individual does not control.

These claims are what the Category 24 main document rests on, and what the manipulation sub-hub pages rely on when they link to this category.
