## Claim-by-Claim Verification

### 1. Mianzi/Lian Distinction — Two Dimensions of Face
**Claim**: Chinese has two words for "face" mapping two fundamentally different dimensions: mianzi (social prestige/reputation) and lian (moral integrity/character).
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Multiple academic sources confirm this distinction. The Greater China Journal (2017) describes lian as "sense of shame in relation to social standards of morality and behaviour" and mianzi as "status, prestige, social position." A ScienceDirect corpus-based study (2017) confirms mianzi is "other-oriented as a social self" with reputation as its distinguishing component, while lian is "self-oriented as a personal self" with morality as its distinguishing component.
**Sources**:
- [The Concept of Face in Chinese Culture — Greater China Journal](https://china-journal.org/2017/02/25/the-concept-of-face-in-chinese-culture-and-the-difference-between-mianzi-and-lian/)
- [Interplay of mianzi and lian — ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0388000117300347)

### 2. Mianzi Can Exist Without Lian
**Claim**: A person can have high mianzi but low lian (e.g., a corrupt official).
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: The Greater China Journal explicitly states: "a corrupt official who disregards social and moral standards has no lian; however, if he has status and prestige, he has mianzi, despite having achieved his success by immoral means."
**Sources**:
- [Greater China Journal — Face Concept](https://china-journal.org/2017/02/25/the-concept-of-face-in-chinese-culture-and-the-difference-between-mianzi-and-lian/)

### 3. "Lose Face" Borrowed from Chinese into English
**Claim**: The English expression "lose face" was borrowed from Chinese, documented first in English in the 19th century from Chinese contact.
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Wiktionary and multiple etymology sources confirm "lose face" is a calque from Chinese 丟臉/丢脸 (diūliǎn) or 丟面子/丢面子 (diū miànzi). It first appeared in pidgin English in the 1830s. Sir Robert Hart published essays referencing China having "lost face" in 1876.
**Sources**:
- [Wiktionary — lose face](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lose_face)
- [Phrases.org — Lose Face](https://www.phrases.org.uk/meanings/lose-face.html)
- [ScienceDirect — How the Chinese lost face](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0378216613001379)

### 4. Guanxi — Reciprocal Obligation Network
**Claim**: Guanxi (关系) denotes a complex system of social connections, reciprocal obligations, and interpersonal trust networks. Failure to reciprocate is a serious social violation.
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Wikipedia and multiple academic sources confirm guanxi involves "implicit expectations of loyalty, obligation, and mutual commitment." Harvard PON and China-Mike sources confirm: "Reciprocal favors are the key factor to maintaining one's guanxi web, and failure to reciprocate is considered an unforgivable offense." The three sub-dimensions (ganqing, renqing, xinren) are documented.
**Sources**:
- [Guanxi — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guanxi)
- [Harvard PON — Importance of Relationship Building](https://www.pon.harvard.edu/daily/international-negotiation-daily/negotiation-in-china-the-importance-of-guanxi/)
- [China-Mike — Guanxi](https://www.china-mike.com/chinese-culture/guanxi/)

### 5. 你/您 (Ni/Nin) T-V Distinction
**Claim**: Mandarin has a two-tier second-person pronoun system: 你 (nǐ, informal) and 您 (nín, formal/respectful). 您 usage is declining among younger speakers and in Southern dialects.
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Wikipedia on Chinese pronouns confirms the distinction. A John Benjamins study (2023) confirms that nin "tends to be used mostly in the North and Northeast of China in spoken language," confirming the regional constraint. Research also confirms evolving usage among younger generations in informal communication.
**Sources**:
- [Chinese pronouns — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_pronouns)
- [Beyond the deferential view of nin — John Benjamins](https://benjamins.com/online/prag/articles/prag.23008.kad)

### 6. Chinese Kinship Terminology — Most Elaborate System
**Claim**: Chinese has the most elaborately differentiated kinship terminology of any major world language, distinguishing paternal elder brother (伯伯), paternal younger brother (叔叔), maternal brother (舅舅), etc.
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Wikipedia on Chinese kinship confirms it is "among the most complicated of all the world's kinship systems" with "a specific designation for almost every member's kin based on their generation, lineage, relative age, and gender." The specific terms 伯伯 (bóbo, paternal elder uncle), 叔叔 (shūshu, paternal younger uncle), and 舅舅 (jiùjiu, maternal uncle) are all confirmed.
**Sources**:
- [Chinese kinship — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_kinship)
- [That's Mandarin — Chinese Family Tree](https://www.thatsmandarin.com/chinese-language/family-tree/)

### 7. Fictive Kinship Extension to Non-Relatives
**Claim**: Kinship terms are systematically extended to non-relatives: 阿姨 (āyí) for older women, 叔叔 (shūshu) for older men, 大姐/大哥 for slightly older peers, 爷爷/奶奶 for elderly strangers.
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Multiple sources confirm this practice. Contextual Chinese Dictionary confirms 叔叔 is used "to address a male stranger who is roughly a generation older than you" and "is primarily used by children and young adults for men who appear to be between 35 and 60 years old." Quora cultural discussions confirm the practice is "rooted in Confucian family ethics."
**Sources**:
- [Contextual Chinese Dictionary — shushu](https://contextualchinese.com/%E5%8F%94%E5%8F%94)
- [Quora — Why Chinese address older people as aunt/uncle](https://www.quora.com/Why-do-Chinese-people-address-older-people-as-aunt-uncle)

### 8. Sentence-Final Particles as Pragmatic Markers
**Claim**: Chinese has a rich inventory of sentence-final particles (吗, 呢, 吧, 了, 啊, 嘛, etc.) encoding speaker attitude, interpersonal stance, and illocutionary force, with no structural equivalent in English.
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Wikipedia confirms sentence-final particles are "minimal lexemes that occur at the end of a sentence and do not carry referential meaning, but may relate to linguistic modality, register or other pragmatic effects." Multiple academic studies confirm the functions listed: 吗 for questions, 吧 for suggestions/softening, 嘛 for obviousness, 了 for change of state, etc. The hierarchical pragmatic organization is confirmed.
**Sources**:
- [Sentence-final particle — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence-final_particle)
- [Fang 2022 — Sentence-Final Particles in Mandarin, Studia Linguistica](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/stul.12198)

### 9. Gift-Giving Refusal Ritual — Three Refusals
**Claim**: Chinese gift-giving follows a ritualized sequence of offer-refuse-insist repeated 2-3 times before acceptance.
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: SinoActive confirms: "the ritual refusals required by traditional good manners are three." The Cultural Atlas confirms that in Chinese culture, "speakers are generally not supposed to accept an invitation or an offer right away. They should normally refuse several times before accepting. Such refusals are termed as 'ritual refusals' and are almost obligatory." The research does note that modern practice has become more flexible (1-2 refusals may suffice), which the concept research does not mention.
**Minor nuance**: The research presents the three-refusal pattern as the standard, which it is traditionally. Modern practice is more variable (1-3 refusals), but the traditional pattern description is accurate.
**Sources**:
- [SinoActive — Three refusals](https://sinoactive.com/why-you-need-to-refuse-three-times-a-gift/)
- [Cultural Atlas — Chinese Etiquette](https://culturalatlas.sbs.com.au/chinese-culture/chinese-culture-etiquette)

### 10. Brown and Levinson (1987) Politeness Theory
**Claim**: Brown and Levinson proposed a universal politeness model based on positive face (desire for approval) and negative face (desire for autonomy), centered on Face-Threatening Acts.
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Wikipedia and multiple academic sources confirm the theory's core propositions: "1) everyone has face; 2) any speech acts can threaten face; and 3) speakers attempt to minimize the threat by employing various linguistic strategies." The three social factors (power, distance, imposition) are confirmed.
**Sources**:
- [Politeness theory — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politeness_theory)

### 11. East Asian Criticism of Brown and Levinson

**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: The statistical finding is confirmed in a ScienceDirect study (2017): "task imposition does not have a statistically significant impact on the speaker's determination of politeness weightiness in East Asia while it does in the US and Latin America." Multiple critical reviews confirm the theory's "Western bias."
**Sources**:

## Corrections and Clarifications

### Minor Corrections

1. **Gift refusal count**: The research presents the three-refusal pattern as standard. Modern practice has become more flexible — contemporary Chinese may refuse only 1-2 times rather than the traditional 3. The traditional pattern is accurately described but could note this modernization trend.

2. **Hu Shih dates**: The research states Hu Shih's dates as (1891-1962). This is correct. His article "A Preliminary Discussion of Literary Reform" is described as published in 1917, which is confirmed (January 1917 in New Youth magazine).

### Items Warranting Additional Context

1. **Sentence-final particle inventory**: The research lists 10 particles. Academic sources confirm this is a reasonable core set, though some researchers identify additional particles or sub-types. The core set presented is accurate and representative.

2. **Guanxi vs. Networking comparison**: The comparison table accurately captures the key differences, though some scholars argue that Western professional networking is converging with guanxi-like practices in the age of social media.
