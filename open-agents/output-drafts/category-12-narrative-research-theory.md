# Category 12: Narrative Structure & Discourse Coherence — Research Theory

## Research Summary

This document provides comprehensive theoretical foundations for teaching narrative structure and discourse coherence as pragmatic phenomena, specifically oriented toward an autistic audience learning English pragmatics. The research covers Labov's foundational narrative model, the critical distinction between cohesion and coherence, information structure in narrative, discourse types beyond narrative, and audience design principles.

## 1. WHAT NARRATIVE IS AS A PRAGMATIC PHENOMENON

### 1.1 Definition

Narrative is the organized telling of events, linking them temporally, causally, and thematically. At its most technical, Labov defines an oral narrative of personal experience as "a report of a sequence of events that have entered into the biography of the speaker by a sequence of clauses that correspond to the order of the original events" (Labov & Waletzky, 1967). At minimum, a narrative requires just two temporally ordered clauses — if their order is changed, the meaning changes. That temporal sequencing is the bedrock requirement.

But narrative is far more than a sequence of events. It is a fundamental human cognitive capacity. Jerome Bruner (1986, 1990) argued that narrative constitutes one of two basic "modes of thought":

- **Paradigmatic (logico-scientific) mode**: Deals in abstract categories, formal logic, settled certainties. Seeks universal truth conditions. This is how we think about math, physics, classification systems.
- **Narrative mode**: Deals in human intentions, actions, consequences, experiences. Seeks verisimilitude rather than truth. This is how we think about people, motivations, social situations.

Bruner proposed that there may even be a biological basis for narrative apprehension of the world — a "human readiness for narrative," a "predisposition to organize experience into a narrative form, into plot structures and the rest." Narratives "subjunctivize" reality, "trafficking in human possibilities rather than settled certainties" — stories present the world not as it necessarily or always is, as settled facts, but as "psychologically in process, and as such contingent or subjunctive."

This matters for pragmatics because narrative is not simply a grammatical exercise. Stringing clauses together in temporal order is syntax. Making those clauses into a *story* — one that serves a communicative purpose, engages a listener, conveys a point — is pragmatics.

### 1.2 Why Narrative Is Pragmatic (Not Just Grammatical)

Narrative is a pragmatic phenomenon because it requires:

**Audience Design**: Narrators must adjust their story to the listener. What does the listener already know? What context needs to be provided? What can be assumed? Allan Bell's (1984) audience design framework shows that speakers systematically adjust their language style based on who they are talking to, including addressees, auditors (known but not directly addressed), overhearers, and eavesdroppers. In narrative, this means deciding what background to include, how much detail to provide, what vocabulary level to use.

**Perspective Management**: Well-structured narratives construct a hierarchical framework for the presentation of related events while incorporating different levels of perspective from both the narrator and the participants in the story. The narrator must manage multiple viewpoints — their own current perspective, their perspective at the time of events, and the perspectives of characters in the story. Research on viewpoint alignment shows that the distance between the viewpoints of narrator, character, and reader/listener is dynamic and regulated by linguistic choices (grammatical person, referential expressions, verbs of perception, verb tense) as well as contextual factors.

**Information Packaging**: The narrator must decide what information to present first, what to reveal later, how to order new information relative to what the listener already knows, and how to structure information flow to create the desired effect (suspense, surprise, clarity, humor). This draws on the pragmatic principles of given-before-new information ordering and end-focus/end-weight in English.

**Relevance Calibration**: Every element in a narrative must justify its presence. The degree of relevance of communicated information depends on two factors: processing effort (how hard it is to understand) and cognitive effects (how much it changes the listener's mental model). Skilled narrators calibrate what to include and exclude based on whether each element contributes to the overall narrative point.

**Tellability Judgment**: Before even beginning a story, a narrator must judge whether the story is worth telling in this context, to this audience, at this time. Ochs and Capps (2001) describe tellability as "the quality of a narrative as a tellable account with a sequence of reportable events that make a point in a rhetorically effective manner, but also the contextual relevancy of the story for the participants involved." Stories that lack tellability are met with the devastating response: "So what?"

### 1.3 Labov & Waletzky (1967): The Foundational Model

William Labov and Joshua Waletzky's 1967 paper "Narrative Analysis: Oral Versions of Personal Experience" established the most influential framework for analyzing oral narrative structure. Their approach differs from earlier narrative analysis by focusing on oral (spoken) narratives rather than literary texts, analyzing naturally occurring stories told by real people about their real experiences.

The framework identifies two fundamental functions of narrative:
- **Referential function**: Orienting and grounding a story in its contextual world by referencing events in sequential order as they originally occurred
- **Evaluative function**: Conveying the meaning, significance, and emotional weight of the events

The L&W framework proved useful far beyond its original scope — it has been applied to oral memoirs, folk tales, novels, therapeutic interviews, and "the banal narratives of everyday life."

### 1.4 Narrative as Socially Embedded

Harvey Sacks, the founder of conversation analysis, showed that narratives in conversation are not just told — they are collaboratively constructed. Before telling a story in conversation, a narrator must:

1. **Secure the floor**: Stories require extended turns. In normal conversation, turns are short. The narrator must signal that they need more time — typically through a "story preface."
2. **Get permission**: The story preface projects what the story will be about and what it will take for it to reach completion. Recipients must "align as story recipients" for the telling to proceed.
3. **Project the type**: The preface tells the listener what kind of story to expect — a complaint story, an amusing story, a shocking story — so the listener knows what kind of response will be appropriate.
4. **Deliver and receive response**: The storyteller delivers the telling, and the listener waits for the projected climax as a component part of the big-package action of storytelling.

This means that narratives in conversation are not monologues — they are jointly managed interactional achievements.

Ochs and Capps (2001) developed a five-dimensional framework for understanding conversational narrative variation:
- **Tellership**: Ranges from a single active teller with passive audience to collaborative co-construction by multiple tellers
- **Tellability**: Ranges from high (dramatic, clearly worth telling) to low (mundane, questionable relevance)
- **Embeddedness**: Ranges from highly embedded in surrounding conversation to detached/standalone
- **Linearity**: Ranges from closed (clear temporal/causal sequence) to open (uncertain, fluid ordering)
- **Moral stance**: Ranges from certain/constant to uncertain/fluid

## 2. LABOV'S NARRATIVE STRUCTURE — THE CLASSIC SIX-PART MODEL

Labov's model identifies six structural components in a fully formed oral narrative. Not all must be present in every narrative — only the four central ones (orientation, complicating action, evaluation, resolution) are considered essential. A minimal narrative needs only two temporally ordered clauses. But a "fully formed" narrative characteristically contains all six.

### 2a. ABSTRACT

**Definition**: An initial clause or set of clauses that encapsulates the entire narrative — a summary of what the story is about before it begins.

**Function**: The abstract serves multiple pragmatic purposes:
- Attracts the listener's interest
- Gets the listener to want to hear the rest of the story
- Projects the type and topic of the narrative
- Functions as a story preface in conversational context (securing extended turn space)
- Sets up expectations about what kind of story is coming

**Examples**:
- "Did I ever tell you about the time I almost drowned?"
- "You won't believe what happened at work today."
- "This is about the most frightening experience of my life."
- "Something really funny happened on the way here."

**What Happens When It's Missing**: Without an abstract, the listener has no frame for interpreting what follows. They don't know if the story is meant to be funny, scary, instructive, or sympathetic. They don't know what kind of response is expected. The story can feel like it comes out of nowhere. In conversation, without a proper preface/abstract, the narrator may fail to secure the conversational floor — other speakers may continue talking or interrupt because they don't realize a story is beginning. The absence of an abstract doesn't make the narrative unintelligible, but it removes an important pragmatic scaffolding that helps the listener orient their attention and expectations.

### 2b. ORIENTATION

**Definition**: Clauses that set the scene by providing information about who was involved, what was happening, when it happened, and where it happened. Orientation clauses introduce the characters, the setting, and the initial situation.

**Function**: 
- Establishes the contextual frame necessary for understanding the events that follow
- Introduces participants and their relationships
- Anchors the story in time and place
- Describes the initial behavioral situation before the complicating action begins
- Provides the "normal state" against which complications will be measured

**Examples**:
- "It was a Saturday morning in July. My brother and I were at the lake. The weather was perfect — not a cloud in the sky." (time, participants, place, conditions)
- "I was working at the restaurant downtown. My manager, Sarah — she'd only been there about two weeks." (activity, place, character introduction, background)

**What Happens When It's Missing**: Without orientation, listeners become lost. They don't know who the people in the story are, where events are taking place, or when they occurred. The listener has to do excessive inferential work to construct a mental model of the scene. Stories without orientation feel disembodied — things happen, but the listener can't visualize the scene or track the participants. The result is confusion, repeated interruptions for clarification ("Wait, who is this? Where were you?"), and difficulty engaging emotionally with the narrative because the listener can't build a mental picture. Research on narrative coherence shows that context (time and place orientation) is one of three critical dimensions — without it, narratives are rated as significantly less coherent.

### 2c. COMPLICATING ACTION

**Definition**: The sequence of events that forms the core of the narrative — what actually happened, told in the order it occurred. These are the narrative clauses proper, the clauses separated by temporal juncture (meaning if you change their order, you change the story). The complicating action is a chain of causal or instrumental events that culminates in the most reportable event.

**Function**:
- Constitutes the backbone of the story
- Reports events in temporal sequence ("And then what happened?")
- Builds toward the climax or most reportable event
- Creates the tension or problem that makes the story worth telling
- Each clause advances the timeline of the story

**Examples**:
- "So I stepped off the dock. And the water was way deeper than I expected. I went straight under. I tried to swim but my shoes were pulling me down. I grabbed at the edge of the dock but it was too slippery."
- "She walked right up to the manager. She put the report on his desk. She said, 'I quit.' And she walked out."

**What Happens When It's Missing**: Without complicating action, there is no story. A narrative with only orientation and evaluation is just a description with a judgment attached — "I was at the lake and it was scary" tells us nothing about what happened. The complicating action is the only truly essential component; every other element serves to frame, contextualize, or evaluate it. Without events, there is nothing to narrate. A "narrative" without complicating action fails the minimum definition — it cannot have two temporally ordered clauses.

### 2d. EVALUATION

**Definition**: The means by which the narrator conveys the point of the story — why it is worth telling, what the emotional significance is, what the listener should take away. Evaluation addresses the implicit question "So what?" It conveys the narrator's attitude toward the narrative events and establishes their relative importance.

**Function**:
- Answers the question "So what?" — justifies the narrative's claim on the listener's attention
- Conveys the emotional weight and significance of events
- Highlights what makes the story tellable (reportable)
- Suspends the forward movement of action to emphasize key moments
- Establishes the "point" of the story
- Differentiates a mere chronicle from a meaningful narrative

**Types of Evaluation** (Labov's taxonomy):

*External evaluation*: The narrator steps outside the story to address the listener directly.
- "I'll tell you, I was terrified."
- "You can't imagine how embarrassing that was."
- "This was the worst moment of my life."

*Embedded evaluation*: Evaluation is placed in the mouth of a character within the story.
- "And my mom said, 'That's the bravest thing I've ever seen you do.'"
- "He turned to me and said, 'We're in serious trouble.'"

*Internal evaluation*: Evaluation is woven into the narrative action itself through specific linguistic devices:
- **Intensifiers**: Devices that strengthen the force of an event — expressive phonology (stretched vowels, emphatic stress), repetition ("I ran and ran and ran"), quantifiers ("all," "every"), ritual utterances
- **Comparators**: Devices that contrast what happened with what could/should/might have happened — negatives ("I didn't even scream"), futures ("I thought I was going to die"), modals ("I could have been killed"), questions ("What was I supposed to do?"), commands, comparisons with hypothetical alternatives
- **Correlatives**: Two events occurring simultaneously
- **Explicatives**: Explanatory clauses that clarify causation or reasoning

**Evaluative Concentration**: While evaluative material can be spread throughout a narrative, it is frequently concentrated in an evaluation section placed just before the most highly evaluated action — the "point" or climax of the narrative. This concentration suspends the forward movement of the action, creating a moment of heightened significance.

**What Happens When It's Missing**: This is perhaps the most devastating structural absence. Without evaluation, the narrative becomes a bare chronicle of events with no apparent significance. The listener is left asking "So what?" — and Labov noted that "pointless stories are met (in English) with the withering rejoinder, 'So what?'" A story without evaluation fails to justify the narrator's claim on conversational time and the listener's attention. It answers "what happened?" but fails to answer "why does it matter?" The listener may feel confused about why they were told the story, or may attempt to supply their own evaluation ("Was that supposed to be funny?"), which is socially awkward for both parties.

### 2e. RESOLUTION

**Definition**: The conclusion of the narrative — what finally happened, how the complicating action was resolved, what the outcome was. The resolution provides closure to the narrative arc, resolving the tensions built up in the complicating action.

**Function**:
- Provides narrative closure
- Reports the final events that resolve the tension/problem
- Answers the question "What finally happened?"
- Completes the arc from initial situation through complication to outcome
- Often overlaps with the most reportable event

**Examples**:
- "The lifeguard pulled me out and I was fine."
- "So they gave me a full refund and apologized."
- "And it turned out the spider wasn't even real — my brother had put it there as a joke."

**What Happens When It's Missing**: Without resolution, the story feels unfinished. The listener is left hanging — the tension of the complicating action is never relieved. This can be done deliberately for rhetorical effect (to create suspense or convey ongoing uncertainty), but when unintentional, it leaves the listener unsatisfied and confused. They may ask "So what happened?" or "And then?" — indicating the narrative has not reached completion. Some stories deliberately lack resolution to convey that a situation is ongoing or unresolved, but this itself is an evaluative choice.

### 2f. CODA

**Definition**: A clause or set of clauses that bridges the narrative back to the present moment of telling. The coda signals that the narrative is complete and returns the conversational frame to the here-and-now.

**Function**:
- Signals that the story is over — closes the narrative frame
- Returns the listener from the story world to the present conversation
- Makes the question "What happened then?" no longer appropriate
- Often points out the lasting relevance or consequence of the story
- Bridges the past narrative to the present situation
- Returns the conversational floor to shared turn-taking

**Examples**:
- "And that's how I ended up here."
- "I still have the scar, look." (pointing to present evidence)
- "So now whenever I see a spider, I check if it's real first."
- "And that's why I never go swimming without a buddy."
- "That's it." (simple coda)

**What Happens When It's Missing**: Without a coda, the story can feel like it just stops rather than ends. The listener may not realize the story is over and continue waiting for more. Alternatively, there can be an awkward transition back to regular conversation because the boundary between "story world" and "present conversation" hasn't been clearly marked. The coda functions like a return journey — it brings the listener back from the displaced time and place of the narrative to the shared present. Without it, the listener may be stranded in the story world.

## 3. COHERENCE VS. COHESION — THE CRITICAL DISTINCTION

This is one of the most important distinctions in discourse analysis, and it is particularly relevant for understanding what makes text or speech "make sense."

### 3.1 COHESION: Surface-Level Linguistic Links

**Definition**: Cohesion refers to the grammatical and lexical linking within a text that holds it together and gives it connectedness at the surface level. It is the set of linguistic resources that every language has for linking one part of a text to another.

**Foundational Framework**: Halliday & Hasan (1976) identified five major types of cohesive devices in English, divided into grammatical cohesion and lexical cohesion:

#### Grammatical Cohesion

**A. Reference**: The use of linguistic items to point to other items in the text (or outside it).

*Anaphoric reference* (pointing backward): The most common type. A pronoun or other referring expression points back to something already mentioned.
- "**John** arrived late because **he** missed the bus." (he → John)
- "I saw **two girls**. **They** were laughing." (they → two girls)
- "I bought **a new car**. **It** was bright red." (it → a new car)

*Cataphoric reference* (pointing forward): A referring expression points ahead to something not yet identified, creating anticipation.
- "**He** was nervous — **John** had never spoken in public before." (He → John)
- "**This** is what I want to tell you: **we're moving**." (This → we're moving)

*Exophoric reference* (pointing outside the text): Reference to the shared situational context.
- "Look at **that**!" (pointing to something visible)
- "**It**'s cold in here." (referring to the ambient temperature)

Reference chains — the tracking of a person or thing across multiple sentences through a series of referring expressions — are fundamental to narrative cohesion. In a story, a character might be introduced by name ("Sarah"), then referred to by pronoun ("she"), by description ("my sister"), by role ("the driver"), and so on. The listener must track these reference chains to understand who is doing what.

**B. Conjunction**: Cohesive ties that signal logical relationships between clauses or sentences. Halliday & Hasan (1976) identified four types:

*Additive* (adding information): and, furthermore, for instance, similarly, likewise, in addition, besides, moreover, also
- "She speaks French. **And** she speaks German too."

*Adversative* (contrasting or correcting): but, yet, however, instead, on the other hand, nevertheless, at any rate, as a matter of fact, in fact, actually
- "The weather was terrible. **However**, we decided to go anyway."

*Causal* (indicating cause/effect/reason): so, consequently, therefore, hence, because, as a result, for this reason, it follows, under the circumstances
- "The bridge was closed. **So** we had to take the long way around."

*Temporal* (indicating time relationships): then, next, after that, first, previously, simultaneously, finally, at last, meanwhile, subsequently
- "**First** we packed the car. **Then** we drove to the airport."

**C. Ellipsis**: The omission of elements that are recoverable from the preceding text — what Halliday and Hasan called "substitution by zero." Ellipsis avoids repetition and gives prominence to new information.

*Nominal ellipsis* (omitting a noun):
- "I'll have a chocolate ice cream." / "I'll have [Ø] vanilla." (omitting "ice cream")
- "These biscuits are stale. Get some fresh [Ø]." (omitting "biscuits")

*Verbal ellipsis* (omitting a verb or verb group):
- "Have you been working?" / "Yes, I have [Ø]." (omitting "been working")
- "She can play the piano and he can [Ø] too." (omitting "play the piano")

*Clausal ellipsis* (omitting an entire clause):
- "Who was at the door?" / "The mailman [Ø]." (omitting "was at the door")

**D. Substitution**: Replacing a word or phrase with a substitute form to avoid repetition.

*Nominal substitution*: "one," "ones," "same"
- "I need a new phone. This **one** is broken." (one = phone)
- "She ordered coffee and I ordered the **same**." (same = coffee)

*Verbal substitution*: "do," "do so," "do it"
- "She plays tennis every day. I wish I could **do so** too." (do so = play tennis every day)

*Clausal substitution*: "so," "not"
- "Is it going to rain?" / "I think **so**." (so = it's going to rain)
- "Will they be there?" / "I hope **not**." (not = they won't be there)

#### Lexical Cohesion

Lexical cohesion involves the relationships between vocabulary items that create text connectedness.

**Repetition**: The most direct form — simply repeating a word.
- "The **cat** sat on the mat. The **cat** looked up at me."

**Synonymy**: Using a synonym or near-synonym.
- "He bought a **car**. The **vehicle** was bright red."

**Hyponymy/Superordinate**: Using a more general or more specific term.
- "We saw a **poodle**. The **dog** was friendly." (poodle → dog; dog is the superordinate/hypernym)
- "I bought some **fruit**. The **apples** were especially good." (fruit → apples; apples is the hyponym)

**Antonymy**: Using an opposite to create contrast.
- "The **old** house was replaced by a **new** apartment building."

**Collocation**: Words that tend to co-occur — their association creates cohesive texture.
- "The **doctor** examined the **patient** in the **hospital** after the **surgery**." (medical collocation field)
- "**Rain** was falling. She opened her **umbrella** and splashed through the **puddles**." (weather collocation field)

**Lexical chains**: Sequences of related words that run through a text, creating a thread of semantic connection. A narrative about a kitchen accident might include: *stove, pan, oil, hot, burn, bandage, hospital* — these words form a lexical chain that contributes to textual unity.

### 3.2 COHERENCE: Deep-Level Logical and Thematic Unity

**Definition**: Coherence refers to the deep-level meaning relationships that make a text or discourse "make sense" as a unified whole. While cohesion operates at the surface level (words and grammar linking sentences together), coherence operates at the level of meaning, logic, and overall conceptual organization.

Coherence is fundamentally a cognitive property — it exists in the mind of the reader/listener, not just in the text itself. A text is coherent when its parts connect logically and thematically, creating a unified representation.

**Key Dimensions of Coherence** (especially in narrative):

**Causal Connections**: Events are connected by cause and effect. One thing leads to another in a way that makes sense.
- "He forgot to set his alarm. [CAUSE] He overslept. [CAUSE] He was late for work. [CAUSE] His boss was angry."
- Each event causally motivates the next. The listener can construct a causal chain.

**Temporal Ordering**: Events are presented in a time sequence that the listener can follow.
- Chronological order is the default (events told in the order they happened)
- Flashbacks, flash-forwards, and reordering require explicit signals
- Temporal coherence means the listener can reconstruct the timeline

**Thematic Unity**: Everything relates to a central point or topic. Every element contributes to the overall narrative.
- A coherent story about "the time I almost drowned" should have all elements relating to that central event
- Digressions, tangents, and unrelated details weaken thematic unity
- The theme must be introduced, elaborated upon, and eventually resolved

**Relevance**: Each element contributes to the overall narrative purpose.
- Every sentence should earn its place — it should add necessary information, context, evaluation, or atmosphere
- Irrelevant details confuse the listener about what's important
- Connected to Grice's maxim of relation: be relevant

**Rhetorical Structure Theory (Mann & Thompson, 1988)**: RST provides a formal account of how coherence works at the discourse level. It identifies a set of coherence relations — such as elaboration, contrast, cause, evidence, background, purpose — that connect units of text. Each relation links a nucleus (the more important element) to a satellite (a supporting element). The theory explains coherence by postulating a hierarchical, connected structure in which every part of the text relates to some other part through a definable relation.

### 3.3 THE CRITICAL DISTINCTION: How They Can Come Apart

**Cohesion without Coherence** (grammatically linked nonsense):

Consider this passage: "I bought some hummus to eat with celery. Green vegetables can boost your metabolism. The Australian Greens is a political party. I couldn't decide what to wear to the new year's party."

Each sentence connects to the next through lexical links (celery → green vegetables → Greens → party). The text has cohesion — there are surface-level connections. But it has no coherence — it makes no sense as a unified text. There is no central topic, no logical progression, no point. This is what Enkvist (1978) called "pseudo-coherence" — abundant cohesive signals that fail to yield a unified whole.

Another example: "My favorite animal is the domestic cat. Cats were domesticated almost 10,000 years ago in ancient Mesopotamia. Mesopotamia is a name that literally means 'the land between two rivers,' taken from Greek. The Greek language is one of the oldest written languages." Each sentence flows cohesively from the previous one, but the paragraph wanders through unrelated topics.

**Coherence without (much) Cohesion** (logical but choppy):

Consider a child's speech: "Mommy, I no eat doughnut. I will not anymore. Mommy, I no like it. No doughnuts. Here. Not all." The sentences lack clear connectors and grammatical linking. Yet we understand perfectly what is being communicated — the child doesn't want the doughnut. The message is coherent (unified meaning, clear intent) despite minimal cohesion.

Or consider a note: "Party at 8. Bring wine. Back door is open." Three sentences with no explicit connection. Yet they are perfectly coherent — they convey a unified set of instructions about attending a party.

**The Practical Takeaway**: Cohesion is the set of tools (pronouns, conjunctions, repetition, etc.) that help connect sentences together. Coherence is the deep logical/thematic connection that makes a text make sense. Good text has both. But they are independent — you can have one without the other. The distinction matters because:
- A speaker can use all the right connective words and still not make sense (cohesion without coherence)
- A speaker can make perfect sense with choppy, unconnected sentences (coherence without cohesion)
- Skilled communicators use cohesive devices in service of coherence — the surface links reflect and reinforce the deep connections

## 4. INFORMATION STRUCTURE IN NARRATIVE

### 4.1 Given vs. New Information

**The Core Principle**: In English, there is a strong tendency to present information in given-before-new order. Given information (what the listener already knows or can assume) typically comes first in the sentence, and new information (what the listener doesn't yet know — the actual payload of the sentence) comes later.

**Ellen Prince's (1981) Taxonomy**: Prince refined the given/new distinction into a more nuanced taxonomy:
- **Discourse-old**: Information that has been explicitly mentioned in the prior discourse
- **Discourse-new**: Information being introduced for the first time in the discourse
- **Hearer-old**: Information the speaker believes the listener already knows (from general knowledge or shared experience)
- **Hearer-new**: Information the speaker believes is entirely new to the listener
- **Inferrable**: Information that the listener can deduce from what has already been said (e.g., mentioning "a house" makes "the door" inferrable)

These categories interact: something can be hearer-old but discourse-new (the listener knows it from life but it hasn't been mentioned yet in this conversation).

**Why This Matters for Narrative**: Narrators constantly make judgments about what their listener knows. "Given" information anchors the listener — it connects new content to what's already established. "New" information advances the story. The flow from given to new creates the forward momentum of narrative.

### 4.2 Theme and Rheme

**Definitions**:
- **Theme**: The starting point of the clause — what the clause is about. In English, the theme is typically the first major element of the clause. Due to English SVO (Subject-Verb-Object) structure, the theme is often the subject.
- **Rheme**: The rest of the clause — what is being said about the theme. The rheme typically contains the new information, the "comment" on the topic.

Example: "**The cat** [THEME] / sat on the mat [RHEME]."
- The theme (the cat) is what we're talking about
- The rheme (sat on the mat) is what we're saying about it

**Types of Theme**:
- **Topical theme**: The experiential element (the participant, process, or circumstance) that comes first
- **Textual theme**: Conjunctions and discourse markers that connect to prior text (But, And, However, Then)
- **Interpersonal theme**: Elements that express the speaker's stance or engage the listener (Unfortunately, Of course, Frankly)

### 4.3 Thematic Progression in Narrative

How information flows from one sentence to the next creates the texture of narrative. Three major patterns:

**Constant theme pattern** (common in narrative): The same theme is maintained across multiple sentences.
- "**Sarah** opened the door. **She** looked outside. **She** saw nothing. **She** closed the door."
- This pattern is common in narrative when tracking a single character's actions.

**Zigzag/linear pattern**: The rheme of one sentence becomes the theme of the next.
- "**Sarah** opened **the door**. **The door** led to **a garden**. **The garden** was full of **roses**."
- This pattern creates a chain-like progression and is good for introducing new elements.

**Split theme pattern**: A theme is divided into sub-themes that are each developed.
- "**Two paths** led from the gate. **The left path** went to the river. **The right path** went to the forest."

### 4.4 End-Focus and End-Weight

**End-Focus Principle**: In English, the end of a clause is the natural position for the most important new information. The sentence ending is universally perceived as the stress position. Arranging information so that the key new content falls at the end is the principle of end-focus.
- "I saw **a remarkable thing** yesterday." (focus on the remarkable thing)
- vs. "**Yesterday** I saw something." (weaker — the important content is diffused)

**End-Weight Principle**: Longer, heavier grammatical elements tend to be placed toward the end of the sentence. Short before long is the structural preference.
- "She gave [the man] [the detailed report on quarterly earnings]." (short indirect object before long direct object)
- We understand sequences where short/simple elements come before long/complex ones better than the reverse.

### 4.5 Focus Constructions

English provides several ways to manipulate information structure for narrative effect:

**Cleft sentences** ("It was X that..."): Focus attention on a specific element.
- "**It was Sarah** who opened the door." (focus: Sarah, not someone else)
- "**It was the door** that Sarah opened." (focus: the door, not the window)

**Pseudo-clefts** ("What X did was..."): Focus attention on the action or outcome.
- "**What she did** was open the door." (focus: the action of opening)

**Topicalization/Fronting**: Moving an element to the front of the sentence for emphasis.
- "**That door**, I will never open again." (topic: that specific door)
- "**Carefully**, she turned the handle." (emphasis: the manner of action)

### 4.6 How Skilled Narrators Manage Information Flow

Skilled storytellers use information structure strategically:

**Creating Suspense**: Withholding key new information, using given information to build anticipation. Delaying the most important new information creates tension.
- "There was something in the room. Something I hadn't noticed before. It was sitting right there on the table. A letter. From my father." (New information progressively revealed)

**Creating Surprise**: Setting up expectations through given/new patterns, then violating them.
- "I opened the door expecting to see my package. Standing there was my ex-husband." (Expected new information subverted)

**Creating Clarity**: Ensuring every new piece of information has a clear anchor in given information. Methodically building from known to unknown.

**Pacing**: Controlling the ratio of given to new information. More given information slows the pace (building tension, providing detail). More new information speeds it up (rapid events, action sequences).

## 5. DISCOURSE TYPES BEYOND NARRATIVE

While narrative is a primary discourse type, it exists alongside other fundamental ways of organizing extended discourse. Each type has its own coherence requirements, structural expectations, and pragmatic conventions.

### 5.1 DESCRIPTION (What Something Is Like)

**Definition**: Descriptive discourse creates a verbal picture by presenting the attributes, qualities, and characteristics of a person, place, object, or scene.

**Coherence Requirements**:
- **Spatial organization**: Elements are arranged by location (left to right, top to bottom, near to far, outside to inside)
- **Dominant impression**: Everything contributes to a unified sensory or emotional impression
- **Sensory detail**: Appeals to sight, sound, touch, taste, smell
- **Static rather than dynamic**: Describes states rather than events
- **No temporal progression**: Time doesn't advance; the description is a snapshot

**Key Difference from Narrative**: Description is *static* — it presents a state of affairs. Narrative is *dynamic* — it presents events that change the state of affairs. However, description frequently appears *within* narratives (as part of orientation, for example).

**Structure**: Typically moves from general to specific, or organizes spatially (e.g., describing a room from one wall to another). Uses adjectives, sensory verbs, comparisons, and detailed noun phrases.

**Example**: "The kitchen was small and cluttered. A yellow light hung over the sink, casting shadows on the peeling wallpaper. The counter was covered with dishes. A cat was sleeping on a pile of newspapers near the window."

### 5.2 EXPLANATION (How/Why Something Works)

**Definition**: Explanatory discourse (also called expository discourse) aims to explain, inform, or clarify a subject. It focuses on facts, theories, and concepts presented in a clear, logical manner.

**Coherence Requirements**:
- **Logical organization**: Information follows a clear reasoning structure
- **Causal chains**: "This happens because of that" — cause-effect relationships
- **Process steps**: For how-things-work explanations, sequential ordering of stages
- **Hierarchical structure**: General concepts broken into specific sub-topics
- **Factual accuracy**: Claims supported by evidence
- **Signaling**: Clear markers of structure (First, Second, Therefore, As a result)

**Text Structure Patterns**:
- Cause and effect ("Global temperatures rise because greenhouse gases trap heat...")
- Comparison/contrast ("Unlike photosynthesis, respiration...")
- Problem-solution ("The problem of water scarcity can be addressed by...")
- Process/sequence ("First, the raw materials enter the factory. Then, they are sorted...")
- Definition/classification ("There are three types of rock: igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic...")

**Key Difference from Narrative**: Explanation is *atemporal* — it describes how things work in general, not what happened once. Its coherence comes from logical relations (cause, comparison, classification) rather than temporal/causal event sequences.

### 5.3 ARGUMENTATION (Why a Claim Is True)

**Definition**: Argumentative discourse presents a claim and supports it with evidence and reasoning, with the goal of convincing the audience.

**Coherence Requirements**:
- **Clear claim/thesis**: A stated position
- **Evidence**: Facts, data, examples, expert testimony supporting the claim
- **Warrant**: The logical bridge connecting evidence to claim (Toulmin model)
- **Acknowledgment of counterarguments**: Addressing opposing views strengthens coherence
- **Logical progression**: From premises to conclusion
- **Consistent stance**: Maintaining a position throughout (though nuance is allowed)

**The Toulmin Model of Argument Structure** (Toulmin, 1958):
- **Claim**: The assertion being argued ("Class sizes should be reduced")
- **Grounds/Data**: The evidence ("Research shows smaller classes improve test scores")
- **Warrant**: The connecting principle ("Improved test scores indicate better education")
- **Backing**: Support for the warrant ("Multiple longitudinal studies confirm this correlation")
- **Qualifier**: Limits on the claim ("In most cases," "probably")
- **Rebuttal**: Exceptions or counterarguments ("Unless budget constraints make it impossible")

**Key Difference from Narrative**: Argumentation is *persuasive* — it aims to change the listener's mind. Its coherence comes from the logical relationship between claims and evidence, not from temporal ordering or descriptive detail.

### 5.4 INSTRUCTION (How to Do Something)

**Definition**: Instructional (procedural) discourse tells the reader/listener how to do something, providing step-by-step directions.

**Coherence Requirements**:
- **Strict sequential ordering**: Steps must be in the correct order — doing step 3 before step 2 can cause failure
- **Completeness**: No steps can be missing
- **Clarity**: Each step must be unambiguous
- **Appropriate granularity**: Steps must be broken down to the right level of detail for the audience
- **Temporal/procedural connectors**: First, then, next, after, finally, once, before
- **Imperative mood**: Commands ("Mix the flour and sugar," "Click the submit button")

**Structure**: Typically includes:
- Goal statement (what you will accomplish)
- Materials/requirements (what you need)
- Steps (in order)
- Optional: warnings, tips, troubleshooting

**Key Difference from Narrative**: Instructions are *prospective* — they describe actions to be done in the future. Narrative is *retrospective* — it describes actions already done in the past. Instructions use imperative mood; narrative uses past tense. Instructions require the audience to *do* something; narrative requires the audience to *understand/feel* something.

### 5.5 CONVERSATION (Interactive Exchange)

**Definition**: Conversation is spoken discourse that is interactive, spontaneous, and context-dependent. Unlike monologic discourse types, conversation is dialogic — meaning is co-constructed and negotiated between participants in real time.

**Coherence Requirements**:
- **Turn-taking management**: Participants coordinate who speaks when, through a system of turn-constructional units and transition-relevance places (Sacks, Schegloff, & Jefferson, 1974)
- **Adjacency pairs**: Paired utterances where the first creates an expectation for the second (question→answer, greeting→greeting, offer→acceptance/refusal, request→compliance/refusal)
- **Topic management**: Topics are introduced, maintained, shifted, and closed through collaborative work
- **Repair mechanisms**: When misunderstandings occur, participants use repair sequences to fix them
- **Sequential relevance**: Each utterance must be relevant to what came before (or must explicitly signal a topic change)
- **Feedback/backchanneling**: "Uh-huh," "right," "yeah," "mmm" — signals that the listener is following

**Key Difference from Narrative and Other Types**: Conversation has no single author — coherence is jointly constructed. Other discourse types have a single producer responsible for coherence; in conversation, coherence is an emergent property of the interaction between participants. Conversation can *contain* all other discourse types — a conversation may include a narrative ("Let me tell you what happened"), a description ("You should see the place"), an explanation ("Here's how it works"), an argument ("I disagree because..."), and instructions ("Here's what you should do").

### 5.6 Cross-Type Coherence Principles

While each discourse type has distinctive coherence requirements, some principles apply across all types:
- **Topical unity**: Stay on topic or explicitly signal topic changes
- **Logical connections**: Each part should relate to other parts through identifiable relations
- **Appropriate signaling**: Use discourse markers, connectors, and structural cues to guide the audience
- **Audience calibration**: Adjust detail level, vocabulary, and organization to the audience's needs
- **Completeness**: Include all necessary information for the audience to follow
- **Relevance**: Every element should serve a purpose

## 6. AUDIENCE DESIGN IN NARRATIVE

### 6.1 How Narrators Adjust to Their Listener

Allan Bell's (1984) audience design framework establishes that speakers systematically adjust their language based on their audience. In narrative, this manifests as:

**Vocabulary adjustment**: Using simpler or more complex words based on the listener's perceived language competence or familiarity with the topic.
- To a child: "The dog was really, really big"
- To an adult: "It was an enormous Great Dane"
- To a dog expert: "It was a Harlequin Great Dane, easily 180 pounds"

**Detail calibration**: Including more or less background information based on what the listener already knows.
- To someone who knows the location: "I was at Murphy's" (no explanation needed)
- To a stranger: "I was at Murphy's — it's this bar on the corner of Fifth and Main"

**Perspective selection**: Choosing which character's viewpoint to foreground based on the listener's likely sympathies or interests.
- To a parent: emphasize how the child felt
- To a fellow driver: emphasize the traffic conditions

**Register matching**: Adjusting formality, slang usage, and speech style to match the relationship with the listener.

### 6.2 What to Include vs. Leave Out

Every narrative involves selection — the narrator must decide what to tell and what to omit. This decision is fundamentally pragmatic, based on:

**Common ground**: What the narrator and listener already share. Shared knowledge doesn't need to be stated (but may need to be activated). Unshared knowledge must be explicitly provided.

**Narrative relevance**: Does this detail contribute to the point of the story? Details that don't serve the narrative's evaluative point should generally be omitted. The maxim of quantity applies — give enough information, but not more than needed.

**Listener interest/comprehension**: Will this detail help the listener understand or engage, or will it bore/confuse them? Too much detail slows the narrative and can lose the listener. Too little detail leaves gaps.

**Recipient design**: Clark & Murphy's (1982) concept of recipient design (later elaborated by Bell) describes how speakers design their utterances so that interlocutors can easily compute what they meant on the basis of the utterance itself and their common ground. In narrative, this means the narrator constructs the story specifically for this listener.

### 6.3 How to Signal the Point (Evaluation as Communication)

The evaluation section of a narrative is the primary mechanism for signaling the point — why this story matters, what the listener should take from it. But evaluation must be calibrated to the audience:

**Explicit evaluation**: Directly stating the point. More common with unfamiliar audiences or when the point might be missed.
- "The scary part was..."
- "I couldn't believe it because..."
- "The thing you have to understand is..."

**Implicit evaluation**: Using linguistic devices (intensifiers, comparators, repetition, reported speech) to convey the point without stating it. More common with familiar audiences or when directness would diminish the effect.
- Repetition for emphasis: "I ran and ran and ran"
- Comparators: "I thought I was going to die"
- Reported speech: "And she turned to me and said..."

**Prosodic evaluation**: Using tone of voice, pace, volume, and emphasis to convey the emotional weight of events. (Difficult to represent in text but crucial in oral narrative.)

### 6.4 The Tellability Requirement

A story must be worth telling. This is not just a quality of the story itself — it is a judgment made in context.

**What makes a story tellable**:
- **Departure from the expected**: Stories are typically about events that deviate from the normal, expected, or prototypical. Something unexpected, unusual, dangerous, funny, or remarkable happened.
- **Emotional significance**: The events had emotional weight — fear, joy, anger, embarrassment, surprise.
- **Relevance to the current conversation**: The story connects to what is currently being discussed or to the shared interests of the participants.
- **Social function**: The story serves a social purpose — bonding, entertaining, warning, teaching, identity construction.

**Labov's concept of reportability**: A reportable event is one that itself justifies the delivery of the narrative and the claim on social attention needed to deliver it. The most reportable event is the semantic and structural pivot on which the narrative is organized.

**The tellability paradox** (noted by various researchers): The more reportable an event, the less believable it may be. Extraordinary stories invite skepticism. Narrators must balance reportability (making the story worth telling) with credibility (making the story believable).

**Failed tellability**: When a story fails the tellability test, the social consequences are significant. The response "So what?" or "Why are you telling me this?" signals that the narrator has misjudged the story's relevance or significance. This is a pragmatic failure, not a grammatical one — the story may be perfectly well-formed linguistically but fail communicatively.

### 6.5 Recipient Design and Common Ground

**Connection to Deixis (Category 8)**: The narrator must manage deictic reference — using "here" and "there," "now" and "then," "this" and "that" — in ways that the listener can follow. In narrative, the deictic center shifts between the present moment of telling and the past moment of the events being narrated. The narrator must signal these shifts clearly so the listener knows when "here" means "here where we are now" vs. "here where I was in the story."

**Connection to Common Ground (Category 15)**: Common ground — the shared knowledge, beliefs, and assumptions between speaker and listener — is the foundation of audience design. Every narrative decision (what to explain, what to assume, how much context to provide) is based on the narrator's model of what the listener already knows. This model is continuously updated during the telling through:
- Listener feedback (nods, "uh-huh," questions, puzzled expressions)
- Grounding signals (confirmations that information has been understood)
- Repair sequences (when misunderstandings are detected and corrected)

**The social nature of narrative**: Everyday narrative consistently serves the need to create selves and communities (Ochs & Capps, 2001). Stories are not just about conveying events — they are about building and maintaining social connections, constructing identity, and negotiating shared understanding. The audience is not a passive receiver but an active participant in the narrative process.

## 7. ADDITIONAL RESEARCH FINDINGS RELEVANT TO THE KNOWLEDGE BASE

### 7.1 Narrative and Autism

Research shows specific patterns in how autistic individuals experience narrative:

- Autistic individuals may produce narratives that are less globally coherent — containing more errors of global coherence than those generated by neurotypical participants, even when cognitive skills are adequate.
- Meta-analyses show differences in both macrostructure (narrative coherence and cohesive adequacy) and microstructure (number of words, different words, utterances) as well as internal state language (talk about perceptions, thoughts, beliefs, and feelings).
- Narrative assessment can reveal pragmatic difficulties that are missed on sentence-level tasks — making narrative a particularly diagnostic domain.
- Narrative coherence is a pragmatic skill — it requires audience design, information management, evaluation, and relevance calibration, all of which are pragmatic competencies.

**Implication for the knowledge base**: Teaching narrative structure explicitly — the components, their functions, and the pragmatic reasoning behind each — may provide autistic learners with a systematic framework that makes the implicit rules of storytelling visible and learnable. The Labovian model is particularly useful because it is structural and rule-based, which aligns well with pattern-learning strengths.

### 7.2 Story Grammar (Stein & Glenn, 1979)

An alternative to Labov's model that is widely used in educational and clinical contexts. Stein & Glenn's story grammar divides a story into:
- **Setting**: Introduction of characters and context
- **Initiating event**: Something that triggers the story (a problem, opportunity, or change)
- **Internal response**: The character's emotional/cognitive reaction
- **Plan**: What the character decides to do
- **Attempt**: The character's action to achieve their goal
- **Consequence**: The outcome of the attempt
- **Reaction**: The character's response to the outcome

This model emphasizes the goal-directed nature of narrative — characters encounter problems, form plans, take actions, and experience consequences. It is complementary to Labov's model and is particularly useful for understanding narrative from the perspective of character motivation and goal structure.

### 7.3 Discourse Markers as Structural Signals

Discourse markers — words and phrases like "so," "well," "anyway," "you know," "I mean," "like," "right," "actually" — serve as structural signals in narrative and other discourse types. They:
- Signal transitions between narrative sections (abstract → orientation → complicating action)
- Mark evaluative comments ("You know what? I couldn't believe it")
- Manage turn-taking ("So anyway...")
- Signal the beginning or end of stories ("So this one time..." / "So yeah, that's what happened")
- Organize information flow ("First of all..." / "The thing is...")

These markers are crucial for narrative coherence because they help the listener track where they are in the narrative structure.
