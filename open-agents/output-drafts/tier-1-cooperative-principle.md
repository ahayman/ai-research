# The Cooperative Principle: The Rules of the Game

## Tier 1 Introduction: The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

> Grice's contribution was to name the invisible assumption that makes conversation work: listeners treat speakers as if they are trying to communicate usefully, and they use that assumption to figure out what speakers actually mean.

People constantly say things that do not literally match what they mean. Someone asks how a movie was, and the answer is, "Well, the popcorn was good." Someone asks whether you can come to an event, and the answer is, "I have an early flight the next day." Someone describes a colleague as "not exactly the sharpest tool in the shed." None of these utterances literally contain the information being communicated. And yet, most of the time, listeners figure out what the speaker meant. They do this fast, and usually without noticing they did anything at all.

In 1967, the philosopher H. P. Grice set out to explain how this works. He delivered a series of lectures at Harvard that became the foundation of the field called pragmatics: the study of how context and inference produce meaning beyond the literal words. His answer was surprisingly simple. Listeners bridge the gap between what is said and what is meant by assuming that the speaker is trying to communicate helpfully. Not trying to be nice or polite, but trying to make the conversation work, to contribute something that fits what the conversation is for. When a listener encounters an utterance, they ask, implicitly and quickly, "What would the speaker have to mean for this to be a useful contribution right now?" That single question does an enormous amount of inferential work. Grice stated the assumption formally as the **Cooperative Principle**: "Make your conversational contribution such as is required, at the stage at which it occurs, by the accepted purpose or direction of the talk exchange in which you are engaged."

Three things about the Cooperative Principle are critical to understand from the start. First, it is not a moral rule about being nice. Two people in a furious argument can both be "cooperating" in Grice's technical sense. A hostile cross-examination is cooperative. A biting sarcastic remark is cooperative. "Cooperative" here means roughly "rationally participating," not "being agreeable." Second, it is a description of what listeners assume, not a rule speakers must follow. Grice was not issuing instructions. He was describing the default expectation listeners bring to conversation, an expectation so deep that listeners use it to interpret what they hear. When speakers appear to violate the Cooperative Principle, listeners do not typically conclude "that person is being uncooperative." They conclude "there must be something they mean by this that makes it cooperative after all," and then they go looking for that hidden meaning. Third, and this is the real payoff: the Cooperative Principle is the engine of indirect meaning. Almost everything you have ever noticed as "people saying one thing and meaning another" can be analyzed as a move within this system. Sarcasm, hints, politeness, understatement, allusion, tact, diplomatic evasion, innuendo: all of them exploit the Cooperative Principle. They work because the listener is assuming cooperation and is willing to do inferential work to preserve that assumption.

To make the framework more concrete, Grice proposed that cooperation can be measured along four dimensions. He called them **maxims**. Quantity asks whether the utterance gives the right amount of information, not too much and not too little. Quality asks whether it is true, or at least believed to be true. Relation asks whether it is relevant to what the conversation is actually about. Manner asks whether it is clear: orderly, unambiguous, appropriately brief. These are not commandments. They are a rough taxonomy of what "cooperative contribution" breaks down into, four questions a listener silently asks of any utterance. When the answer to all four is "yes," the utterance can be taken at face value. When the answer to any one is "visibly no," something interesting happens: the listener assumes the speaker is still cooperating and goes hunting for a meaning that reconciles the apparent violation with the assumption of cooperation.

The meaning a listener arrives at through that hunt is what Grice called an **implicature**: a meaning that is communicated but not said, computed by assuming the speaker is cooperating and asking what the speaker would have to mean for the utterance to make sense. Consider Grice's own example. Someone asks how Smith is doing at his new job. The answer is, "Oh, quite well I think. He likes his colleagues and he hasn't been to prison yet." The literal content is true; Smith really does like his colleagues, and he really has not been to prison. But the last phrase flouts Quantity wildly: it raises an issue, Smith's potential criminality, that nobody asked about. Because the listener assumes cooperation, the listener does not conclude the speaker has lost their mind. Instead, the listener infers that the speaker is flagging something: there is some history making prison a live possibility for Smith, and the speaker wants the listener to know this without stating it outright. That inferred meaning is the implicature. The listener learned something the speaker never said, learned it quickly, and learned it without the speaker being in any way dishonest, because every word the speaker uttered was literally true. The Cooperative Principle plus the maxims plus the assumption of rational communication did all the work.

Each maxim generates its own characteristic kind of implicature when flouted. Flouting Quantity by saying too little ("How was the conference?" "It happened.") implicates that the speaker does not want to elaborate, usually because the experience was bad. Flouting Quantity by saying too much generates the Smith-and-prison effect. Flouting Quality is where sarcasm and irony live: you walk in drenched from a downpour and someone says "Lovely weather we're having!" and the literal falsity is so obvious that the listener infers the speaker means the opposite. Irony, sarcasm, metaphor, hyperbole, and understatement are all Quality flouts where the literal meaning is impossible or absurd and the listener reasons backward from the assumption of cooperation. A crucial point about Quality flouts: they only work when both participants recognize that the literal content cannot be meant seriously. A failed sarcasm is one where the listener takes the literal meaning because the speaker did not make the flout visible enough, and that is a failure of the marking, not of the listener. Flouting Relation (giving an answer that seems off-topic) forces the listener to search for the connection: "Is Sarah coming to the party?" "It's supposed to rain tomorrow" implicates that Sarah's attendance depends on the weather. Flouting Manner (using an elaborately roundabout phrasing where a simple one would do) implicates that the simple phrasing would be inaccurate or misleading. Grice's example: describing a singer's performance as "Miss X produced a series of sounds that corresponded closely with the score of 'Home Sweet Home'" rather than "Miss X sang" implicates, through the bizarre circumlocution, that the performance did not deserve to be called singing.

Grice distinguished several types of implicature. Conversational implicatures depend on context and can be cancelled; conventional implicatures are carried by specific words (like "but," "even," "yet") and are harder to cancel. Generalized implicatures arise in most contexts where a phrase is used ("some" usually implicates "not all"); particularized implicatures depend on a specific conversational setup. Scalar implicatures are a heavily studied subtype where a weaker term on a scale ("some," "or," "warm") implicates the negation of the stronger one ("not all," "not both," "not hot"). The full treatment of these distinctions belongs to [Category 02: Implicature](category-02-implicature.md). The key insight for this introduction is that implicature is the default, not the exception. Most of what English speakers communicate with each other is not literally said. The literal content of an utterance is usually the starting point for inference, not the endpoint.

Which brings us to why this matters for you.

A common experience reported by autistic adults, across memoir, research interviews, and clinical literature, is roughly this: "I have spent my life taking people at their word, and then discovering later that they meant something else entirely." If that describes your experience, the relief of having a name for it can be significant. The gap is real, it is systematic, and it has been studied for sixty years. But the more important reframe is this: you were not failing. You were executing a legitimate reading strategy, a literal one, that is itself cooperative in Grice's sense. You were observing Quality at the level of literal content. The person you were talking to was flouting a maxim to signal an implicature, and you took the literal utterance as the message. That is a real interpretive choice, not a processing error.

The research supports this reframing rather than undermining it. Several careful studies have found that autistic adults often perform comparably to non-autistic adults on offline implicature tasks; given time to reflect, autistic participants derive the intended implicatures at similar rates. Where the difference shows up is in real-time processing: implicature computation appears to be slower or more effortful under time pressure, and the cognitive load is higher. The current consensus is that the difference is one of processing load, not of capacity or absence. You can do it. It costs more. That finding matters because it undercuts the older "autistic people cannot do pragmatics" framing, which was always a caricature. What is true is that the inferential work required to derive implicatures in real time is more expensive for many autistic listeners, and in a conversational flow where the speaker has already moved on, expensive inference can fail to complete in time. That is a load problem, not a comprehension problem.

The double empathy framing, which underlies this entire hub, extends directly into Grice's framework. When an autistic speaker and a non-autistic speaker are trying to communicate, they may both be cooperating in Grice's sense. But they may be instantiating the Cooperative Principle differently. The non-autistic speaker may flout Quantity or Relation or Manner to signal an implicature; the autistic listener may hold more tightly to literal content and take the utterance at face value. Neither person is failing. The speaker signaled; the listener read differently. Meanwhile, the autistic speaker may be maximally cooperative at the level of Quality: literal truthfulness, precise accuracy, careful epistemic hedging, specific detail. The non-autistic listener may read that literal-level cooperation as under-cooperation at the level of social calibration ("too much information," "too blunt," "missing the point"). Neither speaker is failing the Cooperative Principle. They are prioritizing different maxims. Even the autistic speaker's habit of giving very specific information about a topic they know well is often a legitimate cooperative move, not a failure; it is only "too much information" when the specific context actually called for less. In a context that called for depth and precision, the same utterance is exactly right. Context-dependence is built into the Quantity maxim from the start.

Three practical implications follow. Asking what was meant ("Do you mean X, or do you mean Y?") is not a failure to grasp the implicature. It is an explicit, legitimate request for the implicature to be made explicit, a request for the speaker to re-state their contribution at the level of literal Quality. This is cooperative, and in many situations it is the fastest and kindest thing you can do. Stating your own literal meaning and checking that it landed ("I want to make sure I'm understanding, are you telling me X?") is not a social lapse; it is an explicit Quality-level confirmation that can prevent hours of downstream misreading. And literal reading itself is a legitimate strategy, not a diagnostic. The old framing that autistic people take things literally because they have a deficit in theory of mind has not held up well under closer research. Literal reading is a choice you can make about where to put interpretive weight, and in many contexts it is the safest reading to start with. The art is knowing when to keep going past it, and that skill can be learned explicitly.

None of this means Grice's framework is the only possible theory of how conversational inference works. Relevance Theory, developed by Dan Sperber and Deirdre Wilson, argues that relevance alone is sufficient and that Grice's four maxims collapse into a single principle of relevance maximization. Relevance Theory has been particularly influential in autism research and has produced many of the most useful recent findings about real-time processing load. If Grice's framework does not click for you, Sperber and Wilson's approach is worth trying (it has its own [Tier 1 page](tier-1-relevance-theory.md) in this hub). And none of this is a claim that pragmatic inference is the only thing that matters in communication. Tone, affect, body language, timing, context, shared history, power, and emotional safety all matter too. The Cooperative Principle is one lens. It is a powerful one. It is not the whole picture.

What Grice's framework gives you, ultimately, is a tool, not a verdict. Once you see how the machinery works, you can use it to decode what a speaker was doing. You can use it to notice when a speaker is relying on implicatures you have not received. And noticing that gap explicitly is itself a form of mastery over the system. You are not remediating a broken faculty. You are acquiring a second communicative dialect that you can deploy strategically, with the Cooperative Principle as your map to how the other dialect generates meaning.

## Deep Dive

### Grice's framework in detail

H. P. Grice first presented his account of conversational inference in a series of lectures at Harvard University in 1967. The core material was published in 1975 as "Logic and Conversation" in *Syntax and Semantics, Vol. 3: Speech Acts*, edited by Cole and Morgan. He later collected his pragmatic work in *Studies in the Way of Words* (1989).

The **Cooperative Principle** is stated as follows:

> *Make your conversational contribution such as is required, at the stage at which it occurs, by the accepted purpose or direction of the talk exchange in which you are engaged.*

The key phrases are "such as is required," "at the stage at which it occurs," and "the accepted purpose or direction." Each phrase is doing real work. What is required *now*, given *this* purpose, is what cooperative communication looks like. A story has a different purpose than a court deposition has a different purpose than giving directions. Cooperating in Grice's sense means contributing appropriately to whatever this particular exchange is trying to do.

The four maxims break "cooperative contribution" into dimensions:

**Quantity** (two sub-maxims):
- Make your contribution as informative as is required for the current purposes of the exchange.
- Do not make your contribution more informative than is required.

**Quality** (supermaxim: try to make your contribution one that is true):
- Do not say what you believe to be false.
- Do not say that for which you lack adequate evidence.

Grice singled out Quality as the most important maxim. Without a baseline assumption that speakers are trying to tell the truth, communication essentially collapses; there is no anchor for interpretation.

**Relation**: Be relevant.

The brevity of this maxim is deceptive. What counts as "relevant" is one of the hardest problems in pragmatics, and an entire rival framework, Relevance Theory (Sperber & Wilson, 1986/1995), argues that relevance is the only maxim you actually need. On their view, all of the other maxims can be derived from a single more fundamental principle about how listeners hunt for the interpretation that maximizes cognitive payoff for processing cost.

**Manner** (a cluster of sub-maxims):
- Avoid obscurity of expression.
- Avoid ambiguity.
- Be brief (avoid unnecessary prolixity).
- Be orderly.

Manner is about *how* something is said, not *what* is said. The same information can be presented in a way that observes Manner or in a way that flouts it.

### How implicature works

An **implicature** is a meaning that is communicated but not said. The term itself was Grice's coinage. The mechanism has four steps:

1. The speaker produces an utterance.
2. The listener notices that the utterance appears to flout one of the maxims.
3. The listener assumes the speaker is still cooperating at the level of the Cooperative Principle as a whole.
4. The listener searches for an interpretation that reconciles the apparent flout with the assumption of cooperation. That interpretation is the implicature.

**Quantity flout (too little):** "How was the conference?" "It happened." The information is technically true but strikingly insufficient. The listener infers the speaker is choosing to say very little, most likely because the experience was bad. The absence of more information is itself informative.

**Quantity flout (too much):** Grice's Smith example. "How is Smith doing at his new job?" "Oh, quite well I think. He likes his colleagues and he hasn't been to prison yet." The prison remark is wildly more specific than the question called for, implicating that there is some history making prison a live possibility.

**Quality flout:** You walk in drenched from a downpour. Someone says, "Lovely weather we're having!" The utterance is clearly false, and both parties know it. The listener infers the speaker means the opposite of what they literally said. Irony, sarcasm, metaphor, hyperbole, and understatement are all Quality flouts.

**Relation flout:** "Is Sarah coming to the party?" "It's supposed to rain tomorrow." On its face, the answer is not relevant. The listener goes looking for the interpretation that would make it relevant: perhaps Sarah hates driving in rain, or the speaker wants to change the subject.

**Manner flout:** "Miss X produced a series of sounds that corresponded closely with the score of 'Home Sweet Home'" rather than "Miss X sang 'Home Sweet Home.'" The elaborate phrasing observes Quantity and Quality but the unnecessary obscurity flouts Manner, implicating that the performance did not deserve to be called "singing."

Grice distinguished several types of implicature:

- **Conversational vs. conventional.** Conversational implicatures depend on context and can be cancelled. Conventional implicatures are carried by specific words (like "but," "even," "yet") and are harder to cancel.
- **Generalized vs. particularized.** Generalized implicatures arise in most contexts where a phrase is used ("some" usually implicates "not all"). Particularized implicatures depend on a specific conversational setup.
- **Scalar implicatures.** A specific and heavily studied subtype where a weaker term on a scale ("some," "or," "warm") implicates the negation of a stronger one ("not all," "not both," "not hot").

Quantity is the maxim most frequently discussed in autism research. The relevant thing to notice is that "too much" and "too little" are context-dependent. Grice built context-dependence right into the maxim: "as informative as required" is not an absolute; it is measured against the purpose of this particular exchange.

### The evidence base for autistic implicature processing

The research on autistic adults and Gricean implicature has evolved substantially since the early theory-of-mind studies.

**Pijnacker, Hagoort, Buitelaar, Teunisse and Geurts (2009).** "Pragmatic inferences in high-functioning adults with autism and Asperger syndrome." *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 39(4), 607-618.

Pijnacker et al. tested scalar and other pragmatic inferences in autistic adults and found that performance on offline (untimed) tasks was comparable to non-autistic controls. This was one of the first studies to challenge the assumption that autistic people simply cannot derive implicatures.

**Wilson and Bishop (2021).** "'Second guessing yourself all the time about what they really mean': Cognitive differences between autistic and non-autistic adults in understanding implied meaning." *Autism Research*, 14, 93-101.

Wilson and Bishop found that autistic adults showed greater difficulty with implied meanings in naturalistic contexts, but the difficulty was related to processing demands rather than a categorical inability to derive implicatures.

**Wilson and Bishop (2022).** "Judging meaning: A key to understanding pragmatic difficulties in autism." *PeerJ*, 10, e13110.

The follow-up study confirmed that pragmatic difficulties in autism are better understood as differences in how judgments about meaning are made under processing pressure, rather than as a global impairment in pragmatic competence.

**Vicente and Falkum (2023).** Work on pragmatic flexibility and literal/non-literal interpretation in autistic populations, emphasizing context-sensitive performance. [CITATION UNVERIFIED]

Vicente and Falkum's work highlights that autistic individuals show pragmatic flexibility that is more context-sensitive than earlier deficit models predicted.

**Jary et al. (2025).** Research on real-time implicature processing in autistic adults, indicating processing load differences rather than absence of capacity. [CITATION UNVERIFIED]

Jary and colleagues found that real-time implicature processing in autistic adults involves higher cognitive load, supporting the processing-cost account rather than the deficit account.

**Andrés-Roqueta et al. (2025).** Referenced in the autism and pragmatics literature as showing comparable offline implicature performance in autistic adults.

The convergent picture across these studies is that the difference between autistic and non-autistic implicature processing is one of processing load, not of capacity or absence. Autistic adults can derive implicatures; the real-time computation costs more.

### The Cooperative Principle and the double empathy problem

**Milton (2012).** "On the ontological status of autism: the 'double empathy problem.'" *Disability & Society*, 27(6), 883-887.

Milton's double empathy framework maps directly onto Grice's system. When an autistic speaker and a non-autistic speaker interact, both may be cooperating in Grice's sense while instantiating the Cooperative Principle differently. The framework lets us state the mismatch precisely:

- The non-autistic speaker may flout Quantity (or Relation, or Manner) to signal an implicature. The autistic listener may hold more tightly to literal content and take the utterance at face value. This is not failure on either side. The speaker signaled; the listener read differently.
- The autistic speaker may be maximally cooperative at the level of Quality: literal truthfulness, precise accuracy, careful epistemic hedging, specific detail. The non-autistic listener may read that literal-level cooperation as under-cooperation at the level of social calibration ("too much information," "too blunt," "missing the point"). Neither speaker is failing the CP. They are prioritizing different maxims.
- The autistic speaker's habit of flouting Quantity deliberately, giving very specific information about a topic they know well, is often a legitimate cooperative move. It is only "too much information" when the specific context actually called for less. In a context that called for depth and precision, the same utterance is exactly right.

The double empathy problem, in Gricean terms, is a mismatch in which maxims each party treats as primary, not a deficit in either party's ability to cooperate.

### Neo-Gricean and post-Gricean developments

Grice's framework has been refined and challenged by subsequent theorists. The neo-Gricean refinements by Horn and Levinson preserved the maxim-based architecture while simplifying and extending it. Horn reduced the four maxims to two opposing principles (a Q-principle favoring informativeness and an R-principle favoring brevity). Levinson proposed a three-principle system.

The most significant departure is **Relevance Theory** (Sperber & Wilson, 1986/1995), which argues that all four of Grice's maxims can be reduced to a single principle: listeners seek the interpretation that achieves the greatest cognitive effect for the least processing effort. Relevance Theory has been particularly influential in autism research because it provides a precise account of why processing load differs across individuals: different cognitive environments make different interpretations optimally relevant, and the computation required to bridge the gap between environments is what creates the additional load for cross-neurotype communication. Williams, Wharton and Jagoe (2021) used Relevance Theory to formalize the double empathy problem in cognitive terms. See the [Relevance Theory page](tier-1-relevance-theory.md) for the full account.

### Caveats and limits

**This page is not a complete treatment of implicature.** Scalar implicatures, embedded implicatures, cancellability, reinforceability, the neo-Gricean refinements by Horn and Levinson, and the post-Gricean work on explicature are all covered in [Category 02: Implicature](category-02-implicature.md). This page is the framing; Category 02 is the machinery.

**Grice's framework is not the only possible theory of conversational inference.** Relevance Theory provides a competing and in some respects more powerful account. The two frameworks are not mutually exclusive for practical purposes; both provide useful lenses for understanding how indirect meaning works.

**The maxims are not prescriptions.** You are not being a bad conversationalist if you "violate" a maxim. You are being a bad conversationalist only if you are making yourself genuinely impossible to interpret in a context where the other person is trying.

**Pragmatic inference is not the only thing that matters in communication.** Tone, affect, body language, timing, context, shared history, power, and emotional safety all matter. The Cooperative Principle is one lens. It is a powerful one. It is not the whole picture.

**The evidence base on autistic implicature processing is strong but evolving.** Some findings (comparable offline performance) have been replicated across multiple studies. Others (real-time processing load specifics) would benefit from larger samples and cross-cultural replication. Two of the citations on this page (Jary et al. 2025; Vicente & Falkum 2023) carry [CITATION UNVERIFIED] flags, meaning the specific publication details have not been independently confirmed.

**The framework does not erase individual responsibility.** You can still behave badly in a conversation. So can a non-autistic partner. What the Gricean reframe denies is that literal reading is inherently a deficit, and what the double empathy extension denies is that cross-neurotype miscommunication is the autistic party's fault.
