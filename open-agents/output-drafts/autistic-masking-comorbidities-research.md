# Autistic Masking and Comorbidities: Research Findings

## Research Focus
This document covers the scientific literature on comorbid conditions that interact with autistic masking/camouflaging, including alexithymia, PTSD/complex trauma, eating disorders, substance use, OCD, ADHD, chronic fatigue, and sensory processing difficulties.

---

## 1. Alexithymia and Autism

### 1.1 What Is Alexithymia?

Alexithymia (literally "no words for emotion") is a subclinical trait characterized by:
- Difficulty identifying one's own emotions
- Difficulty describing emotions to others
- Externally oriented thinking style (difficulty distinguishing emotions from bodily sensations)
- Limited emotional imagination

It is not a disorder per se but a dimensional personality trait that exists on a spectrum, measurable via the Toronto Alexithymia Scale (TAS-20).

### 1.2 Prevalence in Autistic vs. General Populations

**Key Finding:** Alexithymia is dramatically overrepresented in autistic populations compared to the general population.

- **General population prevalence:** ~5-10%
- **Autistic population prevalence:** ~50% (range: 33-65% depending on study and measurement method)

**Kinnaird, Stewart, & Tchanturia (2019).** "Investigating alexithymia in autism: A systematic review and meta-analysis." *European Psychiatry*, 55, 80-89.
- DOI: 10.1016/j.eurpsy.2018.09.004
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30399531/
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6331035/
- **Methodology:** Systematic review and meta-analysis
- **Key Findings:**
  - Alexithymia is common, rather than universal, in ASD
  - Approximately 50% of high-functioning individuals with ASD have alexithymia
  - Prevalence in the typical population estimated between 5-10%
  - Co-occurring autism and alexithymia represents a specific subgroup with distinct clinical needs
  - Apparent differences in interoceptive accuracy in autism may be related to higher levels of alexithymia, as alexithymia is associated with lower interoceptive accuracy

### 1.3 The Alexithymia Hypothesis (Bird & Cook)

The "alexithymia hypothesis" is a landmark theoretical contribution arguing that emotional processing deficits commonly attributed to autism are actually caused by co-occurring alexithymia, not autism itself.

**Bird, G. & Cook, R. (2013).** "Mixed emotions: The contribution of alexithymia to the emotional symptoms of autism." *Translational Psychiatry*, 3(7), e285.
- DOI: 10.1038/tp.2013.61
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23880881/
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3731793/
- **Key Arguments:**
  - Emotional impairments observed in autism are due to alexithymia rather than being a feature of autism per se
  - 40-65% of adults with autism meet criteria for alexithymia
  - Alexithymia is characterized by reduced ability to identify and describe one's own emotions, resulting in reduced empathy and impaired ability to recognize others' emotions
  - This challenges the idea that empathy deficits are core to autism itself

**Cook, R., Brewer, R., Shah, P., & Bird, G. (2013).** "Alexithymia, not autism, predicts poor recognition of emotional facial expressions." *Psychological Science*, 24(5), 723-732.
- DOI: 10.1177/0956797612463582
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23528789/
- **Methodology:** Two experiments using psychophysical procedures to tease apart contributions of alexithymia vs. autism severity to facial emotion recognition
- **Key Findings:**
  - Experiment 1: Alexithymia correlates strongly with the precision of expression attributions; autism severity was unrelated to expression-recognition ability
  - Experiment 2: Alexithymia is not associated with impaired ability to detect expression variation; rather, it is associated with difficulties interpreting intact sensory descriptions
  - Conclusion: Existing diagnostic criteria attributing emotion recognition deficits to autism may need revision

### 1.4 Brewer et al. - Extending the Alexithymia Hypothesis

**Brewer, R., et al.** Research extending the alexithymia hypothesis to facial expression production.
- Found that alexithymic traits, but not autistic traits, predicted variance in facial expression production
- Supports the prediction that alexithymic traits contribute to reduced facial expression production characteristic of ASD
- Co-occurring alexithymia accounts for difficulties in: neural responses to empathy paradigms, skin conductance responses to emotional pictures, identification of emotions in faces, understanding vocal affect, moral decision-making, and eye gaze fixation on faces

**Source:** "Alexithymia, but not autism spectrum disorder, may be related to the production of emotional facial expressions." *Molecular Autism* (2016). https://molecularautism.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s13229-016-0108-6

### 1.5 Alexithymia and the Masking Paradox

The interaction between alexithymia and masking creates a particularly cruel paradox:

1. **Masking demands emotional performance:** Autistic people are socially pressured to produce "appropriate" facial expressions, vocal tones, and emotional responses
2. **Alexithymia prevents emotional identification:** ~50% of autistic people cannot reliably identify their own emotions
3. **The result:** Many autistic people are performing emotions they cannot identify, calibrating to social norms they can observe but not internally reference

This creates:
- **Cognitive overload:** Must consciously compute what should be felt and expressed, without interoceptive guidance
- **Emotional disconnection:** Prolonged masking may worsen alexithymic traits by habituating the person to ignoring internal signals
- **Burnout acceleration:** The cognitive cost of emotional performance without internal reference points is extremely high
- **Identity confusion:** Difficulty distinguishing genuine emotions from performed ones

**Relevant source:** Strobl, L. "Sensory Processing, Alexithymia, Masking, and Emotional Awareness in Autism and ADHD." https://www.lilianstrobl.com/sensory-processing-alexithymia-masking-and-emotional-awareness-in-autism-and-adhd/

**Exploring the Relationship between Alexithymia and Social Camouflaging in High-Functioning ASD.** *International Journal of Indian Psychology*. https://ijip.co.in/index.php/ijip/article/view/9458
- Found a notable link between alexithymia and camouflaging behaviours, suggesting important considerations for mental health interventions within the ASD community

### 1.6 The Alexithymia-Interoception Connection

Alexithymia is closely linked to interoceptive difficulties (the ability to sense internal bodily states like hunger, heart rate, temperature):

- Lowered interoception is related to heightened alexithymia in both eating disorders and autism
- Apparent differences in interoceptive accuracy in autism may actually be attributable to co-occurring alexithymia rather than autism per se
- This has downstream effects on emotion regulation, hunger/satiety awareness, and body awareness

**Source:** "The relationship between alexithymia, interoception, and neural functional connectivity during facial expression processing in autism spectrum disorder." *Neuropsychologia* (2023). https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0028393223000039

## 2. PTSD and Complex Trauma

### 2.1 PTSD Rates in Autistic People

**Key Finding:** Autistic adults are significantly more likely to develop PTSD, and a broader range of events can be traumatic for them.

**Rumball, F. (2020).** "Experience of Trauma and PTSD Symptoms in Autistic Adults: Risk of PTSD Development Following DSM-5 and Non-DSM-5 Traumatic Life Events." *Autism Research*, 13(12).
- DOI: 10.1002/aur.2306
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32319731/
- **Sample size:** 59 autistic adults who reported exposure to traumatic events
- **Methodology:** Online questionnaires
- **Key Findings:**
  - Lifetime probable PTSD rate: **61%** for DSM-5 trauma
  - Lifetime probable PTSD rate: **63%** for non-DSM-5 trauma
  - Current probable PTSD rate: **45%** in trauma-exposed ASD adults
  - These were the highest rates of PTSD reported in the autism literature at the time
  - Autistic participants found a wider range of life events traumatic (e.g., death of a pet, parental divorce, bullying) even when these events did not meet DSM-5 Criterion A for traumatic events
  - Most common adversities: bullying, death of a loved one, sensory stressors, being talked down to, feeling excluded, distress from changes in daily life, meltdowns, anxiety following social interactions

**Haruvi-Lamdan, N., et al. (2020).** "Heightened risk of posttraumatic stress disorder in adults with autism spectrum disorder: The role of cumulative trauma and memory deficits."
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33454451/
- Examined the role of cumulative trauma exposure and cognitive factors in PTSD risk among autistic adults

**Hoover, D.W. & Kaufman, J. (2018).** "Autism, Adverse Events, and Trauma." *Autism Spectrum Disorders* (NCBI Bookshelf).
- https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK573608/
- Adults with ASD are more than four times more likely to be diagnosed with PTSD than adults without ASD

**Recent meta-analysis (2025):** "Prevalence of Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) in autistic children or young people (CYP) and adults: A systematic review and meta-analysis."
- Published in *Clinical Psychology Review*
- https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0272735825000881

### 2.2 Masking and Trauma Responses

Masking shares structural parallels with trauma responses, particularly the fawn response:

**Pete Walker's Fawn Response:**
Walker coined the term "fawn response" to describe a trauma-driven pattern of people-pleasing behaviors designed to diffuse danger when the brain senses social or relational threat.
- Source: https://www.pete-walker.com/codependencyFawnResponse.htm

**Parallels between fawning and masking:**
- Both involve suppressing authentic responses to meet perceived expectations
- Both are driven by fear of negative social consequences
- Both become automatic over time, making it difficult to distinguish from genuine behavior
- Both cause identity confusion and loss of self

**Key Distinctions:**
- Masking = hiding autistic traits to blend in with neurotypical society and avoid discrimination
- Fawning = an appeasement response rooted in fear, involving agreeing to everything, never asserting needs, actively soothing others
- An autistic person might mask by faking a smile; they fawn by never challenging anyone or expressing needs

**Clinical misidentification risk:** Autistic fawning is frequently misread by clinicians as a safe state, while autistic self-regulatory behaviors (stimming, withdrawal) are misread as trauma responses. This dual misidentification means many autistic people don't receive correct diagnoses for either condition.
- Source: https://neuroclastic.com/the-6fs-of-trauma-responses/

### 2.3 Kerns et al. - Anxiety and Autism

**Kerns, C.M.** is a leading researcher on the overlap between anxiety, trauma, and autism:

**Kerns, C.M., et al. (2012).** "The Presentation and Classification of Anxiety in Autism Spectrum Disorder." *Clinical Psychology: Science and Practice*.
- https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/cpsp.12009
- Key contribution: Distinguished between "traditional" DSM-defined anxiety and "atypical" anxiety more closely related to autism
- Of 63% of autistic individuals with impairing anxiety, 48% showed traditional symptoms while 15% showed only "atypical" symptoms
- This means standard anxiety measures miss ~15% of autistic anxiety

**Broader anxiety prevalence in autism:**
- Approximately 40% of school-aged children with ASD also have an anxiety disorder
- Some estimates reach as high as 84% experiencing significant anxiety levels
- Comorbid anxiety amplifies social skills deficits, resistance to change, and repetitive behaviors
- Point-in-time prevalence of individual anxiety disorders ranges from 2% to 84% depending on measurement approach

**Sources:**
- https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5772195/ (Comorbid ASD and anxiety: brief review)
- https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5764108/ (Update on anxiety in youth with ASD)
- https://asap.psych.ubc.ca/2018/10/01/qa-dr-connor-kerns/ (Kerns Q&A on anxiety research program)

### 2.4 Autism-Specific Trauma Vulnerability

ASD may serve as a vulnerability marker for PTSD through multiple mechanisms:
1. **Increased exposure risk:** Higher rates of bullying, social exclusion, abuse
2. **Broader trauma perception:** Events not meeting DSM-5 Criterion A can still be traumatic (sensory overwhelm, social rejection, forced masking)
3. **Cognitive processing differences:** May affect how traumatic memories are encoded and processed
4. **Communication barriers:** Difficulty reporting trauma or articulating distress
5. **Masking as ongoing trauma:** The daily demand to suppress one's authentic self constitutes chronic stress that may meet criteria for complex trauma

**Source:** Kerns et al. research program at the Anxiety Stress and Autism Program (ASAP), University of British Columbia. https://asap.psych.ubc.ca/

## 3. Eating Disorders

### 3.1 Prevalence of Eating Disorders in Autistic People

**Key Finding:** Eating disorders, particularly anorexia nervosa, are dramatically overrepresented in autistic populations, especially among AFAB individuals.

**Westwood, H. & Tchanturia, K. (2017).** "Autism Spectrum Disorder in Anorexia Nervosa: An Updated Literature Review." *Current Psychiatry Reports*.
- DOI: 10.1007/s11920-017-0791-9
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28540593/
- **Key Findings:**
  - 20-30% of patients with anorexia nervosa meet diagnostic criteria for ASD
  - This represents massive overrepresentation given ~1-2% population ASD prevalence

**Westwood, H., et al. (2016).** "Using the Autism-Spectrum Quotient to Measure Autistic Traits in Anorexia Nervosa: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis."
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26542816/
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4746216/
- **Methodology:** Systematic review and meta-analysis of 7 studies
- **Key Findings:** Those with anorexia nervosa have significantly elevated autistic traits compared to controls

**Recent meta-analysis:** "Investigating the Presence of Autistic Traits and Prevalence of Autism Spectrum Disorder Symptoms in Anorexia Nervosa: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis." *International Journal of Eating Disorders*.
- https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/eat.24307
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11784827/
- **Finding:** 29% of individuals with anorexia nervosa meet diagnostic criteria for ASD as determined by gold-standard clinical observations
- Average rate of ASD in eating disorder populations: 22.9%

### 3.2 Brede et al. - The Autism-Eating Disorder Connection

**Brede, J., et al. (2020).** "'For me, the anorexia is just a symptom, and the cause is the autism': Investigating restrictive eating disorders in autistic women." *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 50, 4280-4296.
- DOI: 10.1007/s10803-020-04479-3
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32274604/
- **Sample size:** 44 semi-structured interviews
- **Methodology:** Thematic Analysis of qualitative interviews with autistic women, parents, and healthcare professionals
- **Key Findings:**
  - Autistic women related eating difficulties to: sensory sensitivities, thinking styles, sense of identity, and emotional/social processing
  - Participants viewed anorexia as a downstream symptom of unaddressed autism-related needs
  - Attention to unrealistic appearance ideals may be part of camouflaging (masking) with neurotypical peers, rather than deep-rooted internalization of beauty standards
  - Food restriction serves as a coping mechanism for distressing hyper-sensory experiences
  - Hyposensitivity to internal sensations may result in difficulties interpreting hunger cues

**Babb, C., Brede, J., Jones, C.R.G., et al. (2021).** "'It's not that they don't want to access the support... it's the impact of the autism': The experience of eating disorder services from the perspective of autistic women, parents and healthcare professionals." *Autism*, 25(5).
- DOI: 10.1177/1362361321991257
- https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1362361321991257
- Found that standard eating disorder treatment is poorly suited to autistic patients

### 3.3 Camouflaging as a Direct Predictor of Eating Disorders

**Bradley, S., Moore, F., Duffy, F., Clark, L., Suratwala, T., Knightsmith, P., & Gillespie-Smith, K. (2024).** "Camouflaging, not sensory processing or autistic identity, predicts eating disorder symptoms in autistic adults." *Autism*, 28(11).
- DOI: 10.1177/13623613241245749
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38634458/
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11497744/
- **Sample size:** 180 autistic participants recruited from NHS settings and community groups
- **Methodology:** Quantitative; online questionnaires (May 2022 - October 2022)
- **Key Findings:**
  - **Camouflaging significantly predicted eating disorder symptoms**
  - Sensory processing was related but did not significantly predict eating disorder symptom severity
  - No significant relationship between autistic identity and eating disorder symptom severity
  - This is a critical finding: it positions masking/camouflaging as the mechanism linking autism to eating disorders, rather than sensory or cognitive factors alone

### 3.4 The PEACE Pathway (Clinical Application)

The PEACE (Pathway for Eating Disorders and Autism developed from Clinical Experience) pathway at the South London and Maudsley NHS Trust represents clinical translation of this research:
- Better autism identification in eating disorder patients
- Staff training on autism-adapted treatment
- Treatment adaptations for autistic patients
- Improved service experiences

**Source:** Tchanturia, K., et al. "Eating Disorder and Autism Collaborative project outline." *BJPsych Bulletin*. https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/bjpsych-bulletin/article/eating-disorder-and-autism-collaborative-project-outline-promoting-eating-disorder-research-embedded-in-a-neurodiversityaffirming-culture/B13353F0D1B839E71A55B0A400495ABF

### 3.5 Mechanisms Connecting Masking to Eating Disorders

Multiple pathways link masking/camouflaging to disordered eating:

1. **Control compensation:** When autistic people cannot control their social environment (which masking attempts to manage), food restriction provides a domain of absolute control
2. **Interoceptive confusion:** Alexithymia and interoceptive difficulties (common in autism) make it hard to distinguish hunger from anxiety or other internal states
3. **Sensory food aversions:** Sensory sensitivities restrict food options; this can be misdiagnosed as or evolve into anorexia
4. **Social masking through body conformity:** Pursuing thinness as a form of social camouflage to meet appearance norms
5. **Emotional regulation:** Food restriction may serve as a concrete, predictable way to regulate overwhelming emotions that can't be named (alexithymia)

## 4. Substance Use and Self-Medication

### 4.1 Key Research

**Weir, E., Allison, C., & Baron-Cohen, S. (2021).** "Understanding the substance use of autistic adolescents and adults: a mixed-methods approach." *The Lancet Psychiatry*, 8(8), 673-685.
- DOI: 10.1016/S2215-0366(21)00160-7
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34217427/
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8310943/
- University of Cambridge research
- **Sample size:** 2,386 participants (1,183 autistic, 1,203 non-autistic; ages 16-90) for quantitative analysis; 919 for qualitative analysis
- **Methodology:** Mixed-methods; online questionnaires with both closed and open-ended questions; binomial logistic regression; content analysis; Fisher's exact tests
- **Key Findings:**
  - Autistic individuals were **less likely** to drink alcohol regularly (16.0% vs 22.2%) or binge-drink (3.8% vs 8.2%)
  - However, autistic adults were **nearly 9 times more likely** to use recreational drugs to self-medicate
  - Autistic people were **over 3 times more likely** to use substances to manage mental health symptoms (anxiety, depression, suicidal thoughts)
  - Cannabis was mainly used to calm internal symptoms such as rumination
  - Alcohol was often used to participate in social situations (i.e., to enable masking)
  - Five autistic participants specifically noted that receiving their autism diagnosis was crucial to discontinuing substance use/misuse

### 4.2 Self-Medication Patterns

The research reveals a specific pattern of substance use in autistic adults that differs from neurotypical patterns:

| Substance | Primary Purpose in Autistic Users |
|-----------|----------------------------------|
| Alcohol | Social lubrication; enabling masking in social settings |
| Cannabis | Calming rumination; reducing sensory overload; managing anxiety |
| Stimulants | Mental focus; managing ADHD-type symptoms |
| Various drugs | Reducing sensory overload; providing routine; managing unwanted symptoms |

### 4.3 Late/Missed Diagnosis Connection

Self-medication is particularly common in late-diagnosed or undiagnosed autistic adults who spent years misunderstanding their own needs. Substances become tools for coping with:
- Intense social anxiety
- Sensory overload
- Emotional exhaustion from masking
- Depression and suicidal ideation

Many adults entering addiction treatment services have undiagnosed autism and/or ADHD.

**Lundstrom, S., et al. (2011).** "Increased Risk for Substance Use-Related Problems in Autism Spectrum Disorders: A Population-Based Cohort Study."
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27734228/
- ASD without co-occurring ADHD or intellectual disability was related to a **doubled risk** of substance use-related problems

### 4.4 Note on Blanchard Research

Specific research by "Blanchard" on autism and substance use was not located in the academic databases searched. The most comprehensive and frequently cited study in this area is the Weir, Allison, & Baron-Cohen (2021) study from Cambridge published in *The Lancet Psychiatry*, described above. It is possible that the Blanchard reference pertains to a conference presentation, a chapter, or a less widely indexed publication. Further manual database searching may be required.

## 5. Other Comorbidities

### 5.1 OCD Co-Occurrence

**Prevalence:**
- OCD prevalence in autistic populations: 2.6-37.2% (depending on study and diagnostic criteria)
- Pooled prevalence of OCD among ASD youth: **11.6%**
- Pooled prevalence of ASD among OCD youth: **9.5%**
- General population OCD prevalence: ~1.6%
- Individuals diagnosed with autism have **twice the likelihood** of later OCD diagnosis
- Those with OCD have **nearly fourfold risk** of later autism diagnosis

**Source:** "Prevalence and Correlates of the Concurrence of Autism Spectrum Disorder and Obsessive Compulsive Disorder in Children and Adolescents: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis." *PMC* (2024). https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11048346/

**Diagnostic Challenges:**
- Restrictive, repetitive behavior, cognitive rigidity, anxiety, and avoidance occur in both conditions
- OCD compulsions are ego-dystonic (disturbing); ASD repetitive behaviors are ego-syntonic (pleasurable)
- When specific diagnostic guidelines are used to distinguish OCD from autism, significantly lower comorbidity rates are found
- Many earlier studies failed to adequately distinguish autism-related perseverative thoughts from OCD-related obsessive thoughts

**Masking Connection:**

**Long, H., Cooper, K., & Russell, A. (2024).** "'Autism is the Arena and OCD is the Lion': Autistic adults' experiences of co-occurring obsessive-compulsive disorder and repetitive restricted behaviours and interests." *Autism*.
- DOI: 10.1177/13623613241251512
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11497754/
- Participants mask repetitive restricted behaviors by turning visible behaviors into covert ones to prevent social stigma
- OCD intensifies the need for repetition and turns potentially neutral repetitive experiences into negative ones
- Focused interests may become incorporated into obsessional content

**Shared Mechanisms:** High comorbidity and sequential risk suggest partially shared etiological mechanisms, with dopaminergic, glutamatergic, and serotonergic systems implicated in both conditions.

### 5.2 ADHD Overlap and Masking

**Key Finding:** Both ADHD and autism involve camouflaging, but with different patterns.

**van der Putten, W., et al. (2024).** "Is camouflaging unique for autism? A comparison of camouflaging between adults with autism and ADHD." *Autism Research*.
- DOI: 10.1002/aur.3099
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38323512/
- **Key Findings:**
  - Adults with ADHD scored lower on total camouflaging and on compensation and assimilation subscales compared to autistic adults
  - Autism traits (not ADHD traits) were the significant predictor of camouflaging, independent of diagnosis
  - Both groups reported **similar amounts of masking** (trait suppression)
  - Adults with ADHD reported less compensation strategies compared to autistic adults
  - Adults with ADHD report more camouflaging than neurotypical individuals, but less than autistic adults

**Camouflaging components differ:**
- **Assimilation** (adopting neurotypical behaviors): Higher in autism than ADHD
- **Masking** (suppressing traits): Similar in both autism and ADHD
- **Compensation** (using learned social scripts): Higher in autism than ADHD

**AuDHD (co-occurring autism + ADHD):**
- Individuals with both conditions face compounded masking demands
- ADHD impulsivity may paradoxically "break through" autism masking, creating inconsistent social presentations
- The combination may lead to higher rates of burnout due to competing demands (autism masking requires rigid control; ADHD makes sustained control exhausting)

**Source:** https://embrace-autism.com/audhd-and-camouflaging/

### 5.3 Chronic Fatigue / ME/CFS

**Key Finding:** Central sensitivity syndromes including ME/CFS are dramatically overrepresented in autistic populations.

**Casanova, E.L., et al. (2022).** "Autism and chronic ill health: an observational study of symptoms and diagnoses of central sensitivity syndromes in autistic adults." *Molecular Autism*.
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8842858/
- **Key Findings:**
  - 21% of autistic adults reported a diagnosis of ME/CFS or related central sensitivity syndromes
  - **60%** scored at clinical cutoff levels for these syndromes
  - Children with autistic traits are **78% more likely** to have chronic disabling fatigue by age 18, even when controlling for depressive symptoms

**Autistic Burnout vs. ME/CFS:**
- Autistic burnout is a physical and cognitive shutdown caused by sustained masking, sensory overload, and social interpretation fatigue
- Key symptoms overlap: executive function decline, social withdrawal, emotional dysregulation, sensory sensitivity increases
- **Key distinction:** Burnout is triggered by social/sensory/emotional overload and tends to improve with rest, reduced masking, and environmental changes; ME/CFS symptoms persist despite rest
- Post-viral fatigue may follow periods of high stress, burnout, or constant sensory overload

**Masking as fatigue driver:**
- Years of masking, managing sensory overwhelm, and translating oneself to neurotypical standards causes cumulative fatigue
- The cognitive load of real-time social computation (what expression to make, what tone to use, what script to deploy) is physically exhausting
- Sensory processing alone can cause significant fatigue: constant filtering of overwhelming lights, sounds, textures, and smells depletes energy reserves

**Sources:**
- https://www.dralicenicholls.com/cfs-me-or-autistic-burnout/ (CFS/ME vs. Autistic Burnout comparison)
- https://neurodivergentinsights.com/autism-and-health-issues/ (Autism and health conditions)

### 5.4 Sensory Processing Difficulties Exacerbated by Masking

Masking intensifies sensory processing difficulties through multiple mechanisms:

1. **Suppression of regulatory behaviors:** Masking requires suppressing stimming and other sensory regulation strategies, removing the autistic person's primary coping tools while simultaneously increasing sensory load
2. **Forced exposure:** Masking often requires staying in sensory-overwhelming environments (noisy offices, fluorescent-lit spaces, crowded rooms) that the person would otherwise leave
3. **Cognitive resource competition:** The executive function resources needed for masking compete with resources needed for sensory filtering, reducing the person's capacity to manage sensory input
4. **Cumulative overload:** Without the ability to decompress (because masking prevents visible distress), sensory overload accumulates until it triggers meltdowns or shutdowns

### 5.5 Overall Psychiatric Comorbidity Burden

**Lever, A.G. & Geurts, H.M. (2016).** "Psychiatric Co-occurring Symptoms and Disorders in Young, Middle-Aged, and Older Adults with Autism Spectrum Disorder." *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 46(6).
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26861713/
- **Sample size:** N(max) = 344, ages 19-79, IQ > 80
- **Key Findings:**
  - **79%** met criteria for a psychiatric disorder at least once in their lives
  - Depression and anxiety were the most common comorbidities
  - Older adults less often met criteria for any psychiatric diagnosis, particularly social phobia
  - More than 70% of autistic samples in several studies report co-occurring psychiatric diagnoses

### 5.6 Camouflaging and Mental Health - Meta-Level Evidence

**Hull, L., et al. (2021).** "Is social camouflaging associated with anxiety and depression in autistic adults?" *Molecular Autism*, 12(1), 13.
- DOI: 10.1186/s13229-021-00421-1
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7885456/
- **Key Findings:**
  - Camouflaging was associated with greater symptoms of generalised anxiety, depression, and social anxiety
  - Camouflaging more strongly predicted generalised and social anxiety than depression
  - Effects held beyond the contribution of autistic traits and age

**Systematic review and meta-analysis (2024):** "A systematic review and meta-analysis of mental health outcomes associated with camouflaging in autistic people."
- https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1750946724001673
- People who report more camouflaging strategies also report more mental health difficulties: depression, anxiety, stress, suicidal thoughts, and lower general well-being

**Camouflaging and suicidality:** "Camouflaging and suicide behavior in adults with autism spectrum condition: A mixed methods systematic review."
- https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S3050656525000124
- Camouflaging in adults with ASD is consistently associated with higher risk of suicidal behavior
- The psychological burden of camouflaging may exacerbate mental health issues, contributing to elevated suicide risk

**Cage, E., Di Monaco, J., & Newell, V. (2018).** "Experiences of Autism Acceptance and Mental Health in Autistic Adults." *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 48, 473-484.
- DOI: 10.1007/s10803-017-3342-7
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29071566/
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5807490/
- **Sample size:** 111 autistic adults
- **Key Findings:**
  - 27 participants spontaneously reported experiences of masking/camouflaging
  - Those who reported camouflaging also reported higher depression symptoms
  - Those who camouflaged reported fewer experiences of acceptance

## 6. Cross-Cutting Themes and Synthesis

### 6.1 The Comorbidity Cascade Model

The research across these domains suggests a cascading model where masking sits at the center:

```
Masking/Camouflaging
       |
       v
Chronic Stress & Cognitive Depletion
       |
       +---> Alexithymia worsening (emotional disconnection)
       +---> Anxiety disorders (hypervigilance, social anxiety)
       +---> Depression (identity loss, exhaustion)
       +---> PTSD/Complex trauma (from chronic threat perception)
       +---> Eating disorders (control-seeking, interoceptive confusion)
       +---> Substance use (self-medication for masking demands)
       +---> OCD (increased rigidity under stress)
       +---> Autistic burnout / Chronic fatigue
       |
       v
Reduced capacity to mask → social consequences → increased pressure to mask → cycle continues
```

### 6.2 Gender/Sex Differences

Multiple comorbidities show gender-mediated patterns:
- Eating disorders are disproportionately found in AFAB autistic individuals
- AFAB autistic people tend to mask more effectively, leading to later diagnosis and longer exposure to masking demands
- The relationship between camouflaging and mental health outcomes may differ by gender, though research shows no consistent pattern

### 6.3 The Diagnostic Overshadowing Problem

Comorbidities frequently mask autism and vice versa:
- Eating disorders may be the presenting complaint while autism goes undiagnosed
- PTSD may be diagnosed while the autistic context that generated the trauma is missed
- OCD may be identified while autism-related repetitive behaviors are overlooked
- Substance use disorders may lead to treatment that ignores underlying autistic needs
- Autism diagnosis itself may prevent clinicians from investigating comorbid conditions ("diagnostic overshadowing")

**Source:** "May Female Autism Spectrum Be Masked by Eating Disorders, Borderline Personality Disorder, or Complex PTSD Symptoms? A Case Series." *Brain Sciences* (2024). https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3425/14/1/37
