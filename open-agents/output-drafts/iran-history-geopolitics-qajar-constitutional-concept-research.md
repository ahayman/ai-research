# Qajar Dynasty, Great Game & Constitutional Revolution (1796-1925)

## 1. Qajar Establishment: Agha Mohammad Khan and the Reunification of Iran

### From Tribal Chieftain to Shah

The Qajar dynasty emerged from one of the original **Qizilbash Turkmen tribes** that had helped found the Safavid Empire nearly three centuries earlier. After the death of **Karim Khan Zand** in 1779, Iran descended into chaos as competing warlords and tribal leaders fought for supremacy. From this turmoil emerged **Agha Mohammad Khan Qajar**, a figure of exceptional ruthlessness and determination.

Agha Mohammad Khan had been **castrated as a child** by his family's enemies (the Afsharid dynasty), a trauma that reportedly fueled his legendary cruelty. Nevertheless, he proved a brilliant military and political strategist. Over fifteen years (1779-1794), he systematically defeated all rivals:

- He conquered the Zand heartland in southern Iran
- He defeated **Lotf Ali Khan**, the last Zand pretender, in 1794
- He reasserted Iranian control over **Georgia** and parts of the Caucasus
- He unified Iran's fractured territories under a single authority for the first time since Nader Shah

### Tehran as Capital

In a fateful decision, Agha Mohammad Khan chose **Tehran** -- then a modest town near the ruins of ancient Ray -- as his capital. The choice was strategic: Tehran was centrally located, close to Qajar tribal lands in Mazandaran, and positioned to defend against threats from both Russia (to the north) and internal rivals.

In **1796**, Agha Mohammad Khan was formally crowned Shah. However, his reign was short-lived: he was **assassinated by his own servants** in 1797, reportedly after ordering a punishment of excessive cruelty.

### The Price of Reunification

Agha Mohammad Khan's reunification of Iran came at a terrible human cost. He was known for extreme violence even by the standards of 18th-century Iranian politics. When the city of **Kerman** resisted his siege, he reportedly ordered **20,000 men blinded** and had thousands of women and children enslaved. He razed cities and massacred populations that defied him.

Despite this brutality, his achievement was significant: he created a unified state that would endure (in various forms) for 130 years and established a dynasty that, for all its weaknesses, would navigate Iran through the most dangerous period of European imperial expansion.

He was succeeded by his nephew **Fath-Ali Shah** (r. 1797-1834), whose long reign would be defined by catastrophic wars with Russia.

## 2. The Russo-Persian Wars: Losing the Caucasus

### First Russo-Persian War (1804-1813) and the Treaty of Gulistan

The defining trauma of early Qajar rule was the catastrophic collision with the expanding **Russian Empire** in the Caucasus. For centuries, Iran had considered Georgia, Armenia, Azerbaijan, and Dagestan as part of its sphere of influence or direct territory. Russia's southward expansion changed everything.

The immediate trigger was Russia's aggressive moves in the Caucasus. In **1801**, Russia formally annexed the Georgian kingdom of Kartli-Kakheti, which had been an Iranian vassal. In **January 1804**, the newly appointed Russian governor of the Caucasus, General **Pavel Tsitsianov**, attacked and captured the city of **Ganja**, killing its governor Javad Khan Qajar and massacring a significant number of inhabitants. Iran, under Crown Prince **Abbas Mirza**, went to war.

The resulting **Russo-Persian War of 1804-1813** was a disaster for Iran. Despite some initial Persian successes, the Russian military advantage in technology, organization, and logistics proved decisive. The war ended with the **Treaty of Gulistan** (October 24, 1813), which imposed devastating terms:

**Territorial losses**: Iran ceded **Dagestan, all of Georgia, and large parts of what is now Armenia and Azerbaijan** -- including the khanates of Qarabagh, Ganja, Shirvan, Shaki, Baku, Derbent, and parts of Talysh.

**Strategic concessions**: Only Russia was permitted to maintain **warships on the Caspian Sea**, leaving Iranian shores permanently vulnerable. Russia also extracted a promise to support Abbas Mirza as heir to the Iranian throne -- inserting Russian influence directly into Iranian succession politics.

### Second Russo-Persian War (1826-1828) and the Treaty of Turkmenchay

The humiliation of Gulistan festered. In **1826**, encouraged by initial Ottoman defeats of Russia and by domestic hawks, Iran launched a war to recapture its lost Caucasian territories. It was a catastrophic miscalculation.

Russian reinforcements under General **Ivan Paskevich** turned the war decisively in Russia's favor. Russian forces captured **Tabriz** itself -- the historic Safavid capital and the most important city in Iranian Azerbaijan. Iran had no choice but to sue for peace.

The **Treaty of Turkmenchay** (February 22, 1828) was even more devastating than Gulistan:

**Territorial losses**: Iran ceded the **Erivan Khanate** (much of modern Armenia), the **Nakhchivan Khanate**, and the remainder of the **Talysh Khanate**. The boundary between Russia and Iran was permanently set at the **Aras River** -- where it essentially remains today.

**Financial indemnity**: Iran was required to pay Russia **20 million silver rubles** (10 *korur* in gold) -- a staggering sum for a pre-industrial state.

**Caspian Sea**: Iran lost all remaining naval rights on the Caspian.

**Capitulations**: The treaty granted Russia **extraterritorial legal rights** over Russian subjects in Iran, and allowed for the **free emigration of Armenians** from Iranian territories to Russian-controlled lands. In the period 1828-1831, approximately **45,000 Armenians from Iran and 100,000 from the Ottoman Empire** migrated to Russian Armenia, fundamentally altering the demographic composition of the South Caucasus.

**Lasting significance**: The treaties of Gulistan and Turkmenchay are regarded as among the most humiliating episodes in Iranian history. They permanently stripped Iran of territories it had held for centuries. Three separate nations -- **Georgia, Armenia, and Azerbaijan** -- would eventually emerge from these lost territories upon the dissolution of the Soviet Union in 1991. The treaties also established a pattern of **unequal relations** between Iran and European powers that would define the entire Qajar era.

## 3. The Great Game: Iran as Buffer State

### The Strategic Context

The **Great Game** -- the term for the 19th-century rivalry between the **British Empire** and the **Russian Empire** for influence in Central Asia -- placed Iran at the epicenter of global geopolitical competition. For Britain, the paramount concern was protecting its colonial "jewel," **India**, from potential Russian overland invasion. For Russia, the goal was continued southward expansion toward warm-water ports and the wealth of the Indian subcontinent.

Iran occupied a critical position in this calculus: it was simultaneously a **buffer state** that separated the two empires, a **prize** whose resources and strategic location both coveted, and a **pawn** whose internal affairs both manipulated.

### British Strategy

Britain's approach to Iran was driven by the imperative of **containing Russia**:

- Britain sought to maintain Iran as an **independent buffer state** -- strong enough to resist Russian encroachment, but not so strong as to threaten British interests in the Persian Gulf or Afghanistan.
- British diplomats cultivated influence at the Qajar court, offering loans, military advisors, and diplomatic support -- always with strings attached.
- Britain insisted on Iran's **withdrawal from Herat** (in modern Afghanistan), which Iran historically claimed. When Naser al-Din Shah attempted to recapture Herat in 1856, Britain declared war.

### The Anglo-Persian War (1856-1857)

The **Anglo-Persian War** was triggered when Iran captured Herat in October 1856. Britain responded with a military campaign under **Major General Sir James Outram**, attacking Iranian positions on the southern coast near Bushehr and in southern Mesopotamia.

The war ended with the **Treaty of Paris (March 4, 1857)**, mediated by Napoleon III. Iran was forced to:
- **Withdraw from Herat** and relinquish all claims to Afghanistan
- Accept British **arbitration** in any future disputes with Afghan states
- Effectively surrender its historic claims to territories it considered part of greater Iran

This was a defining humiliation: Iran's territorial ambitions in the east were permanently blocked by British power.

### Russian Strategy

Russia's approach was more direct and territorial:
- Having already absorbed Iran's Caucasian provinces, Russia continued to expand into Central Asia, conquering the Khanates of **Kokand, Bukhara, and Khiva** by the 1870s-1880s.
- Russian diplomats competed with their British counterparts for influence at the Qajar court.
- Russia extended economic penetration through loans, trade concessions, and infrastructure projects (telegraph lines, roads).

### Iran as Client State

The cumulative effect was devastating for Iranian sovereignty. The Persian monarchy became **dependent on British and Russian loans** for revenue. Both powers had competing investments in Iranian infrastructure -- not for Iran's benefit, but as instruments of influence. Iranian domestic politics became a three-way game between the Shah, British interests, and Russian interests, with the Shah playing one against the other but steadily losing autonomy.

## 4. Economic Exploitation: Concessions and Foreign Debt

### The Reuter Concession (1872)

The most egregious example of Qajar economic mismanagement was the **Reuter Concession** of 1872. Desperate for revenue, **Naser al-Din Shah** granted **Baron Julius de Reuter** (the German-born British founder of the Reuters news agency) an extraordinary package of exclusive rights for **70 years**, including:

- Construction of all **railways** across the country
- Exploitation of all **mines** (except copper)
- Establishment of all future **factories and industries**
- Development of all **irrigation systems**
- Rights to build **tramways and public works**

In exchange, Reuter offered the Shah **£200,000** upfront and 25% of net revenues. Lord Curzon, the future British Viceroy of India, described it as "the most complete and extraordinary surrender of the entire industrial resources of a kingdom into foreign hands that has probably ever been dreamed of."

The concession sparked **domestic outrage** and fierce **Russian opposition** (as it gave Britain an overwhelming economic advantage in Iran). Under immense pressure, Naser al-Din Shah was forced to cancel the concession within approximately a year. However, the cancellation led to a **second Reuter concession**, which established the **Imperial Bank of Persia** -- giving Reuter control over Iran's banking system and the right to issue currency.

### The D'Arcy Oil Concession (1901)

Perhaps even more consequential was the oil concession granted to **William Knox D'Arcy** on May 28, 1901, by **Mozaffar al-Din Shah**. The 18-point agreement granted D'Arcy:

- **Exclusive rights** to prospect for oil across **three-quarters of Iran** for **60 years** (the five northern provinces were excluded due to their proximity to Russia)
- In exchange, the Shah received **£20,000** cash, an equivalent amount in company shares, and a promise of **16% of future profits**

The terms were grossly unfair even by contemporary standards. The concession was granted by a **non-constitutional government** (the Constitution did not yet exist), and subsequent disputes about how to calculate "net profits" ensured that Iran received far less than even the meager 16% promised.

### The Pattern of Exploitation

These concessions represented a systematic pattern:
- Qajar shahs, chronically short of funds due to extravagant spending and weak tax collection, sold off Iran's economic future for short-term cash
- Foreign powers gained control over Iranian resources at exploitative terms
- The Iranian people bore the costs while the shah and his courtiers pocketed the payments
- Each concession generated public outrage that gradually built into a revolutionary movement

## 5. The Tobacco Protest (1891): Iran's First Popular Movement

### The Concession

In **March 1890**, Naser al-Din Shah granted a **50-year monopoly** over the growth, sale, and export of all Iranian tobacco to **Major Gerald F. Talbot**, a British subject. In exchange, the Iranian treasury would receive:
- An annual payment of **£15,000**
- **One quarter of net profits**

The concession was breathtaking in scope: tobacco was consumed by virtually all classes of Iranian society, and the monopoly threatened the livelihoods of thousands of Iranian farmers, merchants, and bazaar traders.

### The Protest Movement

In the spring of 1891, as agents of the **Imperial Tobacco Corporation** arrived in Iran and began asserting control over the tobacco business, massive protests erupted. The movement was remarkable for its breadth, uniting three normally separate social forces:

1. **Bazaar merchants** (*bazaaris*): The commercial class whose livelihoods were directly threatened
2. **Shia clergy** (*ulama*): Religious leaders who saw the concession as both an affront to Islam and a threat to their own influence
3. **Reformist intellectuals**: A small but growing class influenced by constitutionalist ideas

### The Historic Fatwa

The protest reached its climax in **December 1891** when the most important religious authority in Iran, the *marja-e taqlid* **Grand Ayatollah Mirza Hassan Shirazi** (based in Samarra, Iraq), issued an extraordinary fatwa:

> *"In the name of God, the Merciful, the Beneficent. Today the use of both varieties of tobacco, in whatever fashion, is reckoned war against the Imam of the Age -- may God hasten his advent."*

The effect was electrifying. Residents of Tehran **immediately stopped smoking**. The boycott spread rapidly to neighboring provinces. Iranian merchants shut down the **main bazaars** throughout the country in solidarity. Even women in the **Qajar royal harem** quit smoking, and the Shah's own servants **refused to prepare his water pipe**.

### Impact and Legacy

By **January 1892**, seeing that the British government was wavering in its support for the tobacco company, the Shah **cancelled the concession**. He was forced to pay £500,000 in compensation to the company -- further indebting Iran to foreign lenders.

The Tobacco Protest was historically significant for multiple reasons:
- It was **Iran's first successful mass popular movement**, demonstrating that collective action could force the Shah to back down
- It revealed the **enormous political power of the senior Shia clergy** when they chose to mobilize public opinion
- It established the **alliance between bazaaris and clergy** that would prove decisive in every subsequent Iranian revolution, including 1905-1911 and 1979
- It directly contributed to the intellectual and political ferment that produced the **Constitutional Revolution** a decade later

## 6. The Constitutional Revolution (1905-1911)

### Causes

The Constitutional Revolution was the culmination of decades of accumulated grievances:

- **Foreign economic domination**: Concessions like Reuter and D'Arcy had sold off Iran's resources to foreigners
- **Tax policy**: A Belgian director of customs was enforcing tariff collections "with bureaucratic rigidity" to pay for Russian loans that had financed the Shah's extravagant European tours
- **Trade destruction**: Tax breaks on imports of foreign manufactured textiles were devastating Iran's domestic craft industries, ruining bazaar merchants
- **Royal debt**: The Shah had accumulated enormous foreign debts whose interest payments drained the treasury
- **Intellectual ferment**: A growing class of Iranian intellectuals exposed to European constitutionalist ideas demanded reform
- **The Tobacco Protest precedent**: The 1891 movement had demonstrated that collective action could succeed

### The Revolution Begins (1905-1906)

The revolution was triggered by a series of events in late 1905 and early 1906. Merchants protesting the Belgian customs director's policies took **bast** (sanctuary) in mosques and the grounds of the British legation. The movement rapidly expanded from economic grievances to demands for fundamental political reform.

In **August 1906**, the ailing **Muzaffar al-Din Shah** signed a royal decree calling for the election of a **Constituent Assembly** -- remembered as "Constitution Day" in Iranian history. The first **Majlis** (National Assembly) opened in **October 1906**, composed of:
- More than **60 bazaaris** (merchants)
- **25 clerics**
- **50 landlords and notables**

These deputies divided into two factions: the **Moderates** (*Mo'tadel*) and the **Liberals** (*Azadikhah*).

The Majlis drafted Iran's first **Constitution** (*Qanun-e Asasi*), which Muzaffar al-Din Shah signed into law in **December 1906**, just days before his death. This was a landmark achievement: **the first constitution in the Islamic world**, predating even the Ottoman Young Turk revolution of 1908.

### The Counter-Revolution (1908)

Muzaffar al-Din Shah's successor, **Mohammad Ali Shah**, was hostile to constitutional government. In **June 1908**, he launched a coup against the Majlis:

- He confined himself to the **Bagh-e Shah** fortress in western Tehran
- He enlisted the Russian-officered **Cossack Brigade**, commanded by Russian Colonel **Vladimir Liakhov**
- On **June 23, 1908**, Liakhov's forces **bombarded the Majlis building**, killing several constitutionalist leaders

This "Minor Tyranny" (*Estebdad-e Saghir*) provoked fierce resistance, particularly in **Tabriz**, where the constitutionalist fighters **Sattar Khan** and **Bagher Khan** held out against royalist forces. Sattar Khan was appointed commander-in-chief by the revolutionary High Council. Despite overwhelming odds -- 40,000 of the Shah's soldiers were sent against Tabriz -- the rebels drove out royalist forces.

### Restoration and Collapse (1909-1911)

In **July 1909**, constitutionalist forces from Tabriz and the Bakhtiari tribal confederation marched on Tehran and forced **Mohammad Ali Shah's abdication**. His young son, **Ahmad Shah**, was placed on the throne, and the constitution was restored.

However, the **Second Majlis** faced constant interference. The revolution effectively ended in **December 1911** when the Shah's ministers, backed by **12,000 Russian troops**, expelled the deputies from parliament. The direct cause was Iran's attempt to hire an American financial advisor, **Morgan Shuster**, to reform the tax system -- a move both Russia and Britain opposed because an effective Iranian government would threaten their spheres of influence.

The Constitutional Revolution, though ultimately crushed, was profoundly significant:
- It established the **principle of constitutional governance** in Iran
- It created **parliamentary traditions** that persisted (however weakly) through subsequent regimes
- It demonstrated the political potential of the **bazaari-clerical-intellectual alliance**
- It revealed the willingness of foreign powers to **destroy Iranian democracy** when it threatened their interests -- a lesson seared into Iranian political consciousness

## 7. The Anglo-Russian Convention (1907): Dividing Iran Without Consent

### The Agreement

On **August 31, 1907**, in **St. Petersburg**, Britain and Russia signed the **Anglo-Russian Convention**, resolving their rivalries in Central Asia. The agreement divided Iran into three zones:

1. **Russian sphere** (northern Iran): All of northern Iran, which bordered Russia's Transcaucasian and Central Asian possessions, was designated as Russia's exclusive sphere of influence.
2. **British sphere** (southeastern Iran): A zone extending from Kerman in the south-central region to Bandar Abbas on the coast, bordering British India, was designated as Britain's exclusive sphere.
3. **Neutral zone** (central and southwestern Iran): The area between the two spheres, where both powers could compete for influence.

### Iran's Exclusion

Critically, **the government of Iran had not been consulted about the agreement**. It was informed only after the fact. Although Iran was not in a position to prevent implementation, the Iranian government **refused to recognize the accord's legitimacy**, viewing it as a fundamental violation of Iranian sovereignty and independence.

Iranian nationalists felt **particularly betrayed by Britain**, a country many constitutionalists had idealized as a model of democratic governance during the Constitutional Revolution. The discovery that Britain would carve up Iran in a backroom deal with Russia without even consulting Iran shattered these illusions.

The Convention was a pivotal moment in shaping **Iranian attitudes toward the West**: it demonstrated that Western powers' professed commitment to democracy and national sovereignty applied only to themselves, not to nations they considered subordinate.

## 8. The Discovery of Oil (1908): The Resource Curse Begins

### The D'Arcy Concession and the Search

William Knox D'Arcy, having secured the 1901 concession, hired geologist **George Bernard Reynolds** to prospect in the Iranian desert. Conditions were extreme: smallpox raged, bandits roamed, water was scarce, and temperatures regularly exceeded 50°C.

After seven years of fruitless exploration, D'Arcy's fortune was exhausted. He sold most of his rights to the **Burmah Oil Company**. By early 1908, having sunk more than **£500,000** into the venture, D'Arcy and Burmah decided to abandon Iran entirely. In early May 1908, they sent Reynolds a telegram ordering him to **"cease work, dismiss the staff, dismantle anything worth the cost of transporting to the coast for re-shipment, and come home."**

Reynolds **delayed following these orders**. On **May 26, 1908**, his drill struck oil at **Masjed Soleyman** in southwestern Iran. It was the first major oil discovery in the Middle East.

### The Anglo-Persian Oil Company

The strike led to the establishment of the **Anglo-Persian Oil Company (APOC)** in 1909. This company would be renamed the **Anglo-Iranian Oil Company (AIOC)** in 1935 and eventually became **British Petroleum (BP)** in 1954.

In **1914**, on the eve of World War I, **First Lord of the Admiralty Winston Churchill** persuaded the British government to purchase a **51% controlling stake** in APOC, making the British government the majority owner of Iran's oil industry. Churchill's rationale was strategic: the Royal Navy was converting from coal to oil power, and securing a reliable oil supply was a matter of national security.

This arrangement ensured that Iran's most valuable natural resource was controlled by the **British government itself** -- not merely a private company, but a foreign state. The profits flowed overwhelmingly to Britain, while Iran received a fraction of the revenues. Disagreements over the method of calculating royalties were chronic and never satisfactorily resolved from the Iranian perspective.

The oil discovery was a turning point that made Iran **permanently strategically important** to global powers, but the exploitative terms of the concession became a source of deep and lasting resentment that would eventually culminate in Mossadegh's nationalization of the oil industry in 1951.

## 9. World War I: Occupation, Famine, and Catastrophe

### Neutrality Violated

When World War I erupted in 1914, Iran declared **neutrality**. It was ignored by all belligerent powers. Iran's strategic position -- bordering the Ottoman Empire, Russia, and British India, and sitting atop newly discovered oil reserves -- made it too valuable to leave alone.

Iran was **occupied by the forces of three empires simultaneously**:
- **Russian forces** occupied the north, including Azerbaijan and Khorasan
- **British forces** occupied the south and southwest, protecting the oil fields and the approaches to India
- **Ottoman forces** invaded from the west

Iranian territory became a theater of war between these powers, with battles fought across Iranian soil, destroying infrastructure, disrupting agriculture, and displacing populations.

### The Persian Famine of 1917-1919

The most devastating consequence of occupation was the **Persian Famine of 1917-1919**, one of the most catastrophic humanitarian disasters in Iranian history.

**Causes**: The famine resulted from the convergence of multiple factors:
- **Successive droughts** in 1917-1918 devastated agricultural production
- **Requisition and confiscation of foodstuffs** by occupying armies to feed their soldiers stripped the countryside bare
- **Russian troops blockaded roads** in the northeast province of Khorasan, prohibiting any transfers of grain except those destined for the Russian army
- The collapse of transport infrastructure made food distribution impossible
- The **1918 influenza pandemic** compounded the death toll

**Death toll**: According to mainstream scholarship, approximately **2 million Iranians** died during the famine period from hunger and from diseases including cholera, plague, typhus, and influenza. Some scholars, notably **Mohammad Gholi Majd**, have argued for much higher figures of 8-10 million excess deaths, though these estimates remain contested among academic historians.

**British responsibility**: The famine occurred substantially within British-occupied or British-influenced zones. Britain's priority was feeding its own troops and maintaining its strategic position, not protecting the Iranian civilian population. This fact became deeply embedded in Iranian historical memory.

The famine remains one of the **least-known major famines in world history** -- what scholars have called a "forgotten famine." Its near-total absence from Western historical narratives stands in stark contrast to its central importance in Iranian collective memory as evidence of the human cost of foreign occupation.

## 10. Post-WWI Chaos: Soviet Gilan and Tribal Revolts

### The Power Vacuum

The end of World War I left Iran in a state of near-total collapse. The central government under the young **Ahmad Shah** was virtually powerless. Russian troops had withdrawn following the Bolshevik Revolution, but new threats emerged. British forces remained in occupation of much of the country. Tribal groups and regional warlords operated with effective autonomy.

### The Jangal Movement and the Persian Socialist Soviet Republic

In the northern province of **Gilan**, along the Caspian coast, a remarkable revolutionary movement had been developing since 1915. The **Jangal Movement** ("Jungle Movement"), led by **Mirza Kuchik Khan**, was originally a nationalist, anti-imperialist, and anti-foreign resistance movement based in the dense forests of Gilan.

In **May 1920**, the situation took a dramatic turn when the **Soviet Navy**, commanded by **Fyodor Raskolnikov** and accompanied by **Grigoriy Ordzhonikidze**, entered the Caspian port of Anzali, ostensibly pursuing White Russian counter-revolutionary forces and materials.

In **June 1920**, with Soviet support, Mirza Kuchik Khan proclaimed the **Persian Socialist Soviet Republic** -- a radical leftist government in Gilan. However, the alliance between Mirza Kuchik Khan and the Soviets was fraught with tension:
- Mirza Kuchik Khan wanted national independence and social reform, not Bolshevik revolution
- Soviet agents pushed for more radical communist policies
- A **coup within the republic** in July 1920 removed Mirza Kuchik Khan from leadership
- Internal conflicts between Iranian nationalists and Soviet-backed communists paralyzed the movement

### Collapse

By **1921**, the Soviet Union, having reached an agreement with Britain, decided to withdraw support for the Gilan Republic. Government forces led by Colonel **Reza Khan** (the future Reza Shah) overran the dispersed revolutionary forces. Mirza Kuchik Khan fled into the mountains and **died of exposure** in December 1921. His severed head was reportedly sent to Tehran as proof of his death.

The Gilan episode demonstrated both the **appeal and the limits of Soviet influence** in Iran, and provided a foretaste of the Cold War dynamics that would dominate Iranian politics in subsequent decades.

## 11. Reza Khan's Coup and the End of the Qajar Dynasty (1921-1925)

### The Coup of February 1921

On **February 22, 1921**, **Reza Khan**, the commander of the Persian Cossack Brigade based in Qazvin province, marched on Tehran and **seized the capital** in a largely bloodless coup. He acted in collaboration with **Sayyid Zia al-Din Tabatabaei**, a political journalist with British connections.

The coup was **partially assisted by the British government**, whose primary motivation was halting Bolshevik penetration of Iran. Britain feared that Soviet influence -- demonstrated by the Gilan Republic -- posed a direct threat to British India. A strong military government in Tehran, London calculated, would provide a more reliable buffer than the feeble Qajar court.

### Rise to Supreme Power

Reza Khan moved methodically to consolidate power:

1. **February 1921**: Appointed himself **commander-in-chief** and **minister of war** in the new government. Zia al-Din Tabatabaei was made prime minister.
2. **May 1921**: Reza Khan forced the removal of Zia al-Din, who was exiled from Iran after just three months in power.
3. **1921-1923**: Reza Khan used his military authority to suppress tribal revolts, defeat regional warlords, and crush the Gilan Soviet Republic, establishing himself as the indispensable strongman.
4. **October 1923**: He became **prime minister**, pressuring parliament to send Ahmad Shah into permanent exile in Europe.
5. **1925**: The Majlis formally **deposed the Qajar dynasty** and a constituent assembly elected Reza Khan as Shah, establishing the **Pahlavi dynasty**.

Reza Khan initially considered establishing a **republic** (inspired by Mustafa Kemal Ataturk's transformation of Turkey), but the Shia clerical establishment opposed a republican form of government, fearing it would marginalize their influence. Reza Khan pragmatically accepted the monarchical form and crowned himself **Reza Shah Pahlavi** in 1926.

## 12. The Role of the Religious Establishment

### Clerics as Political Actors

A defining feature of the Qajar era was the emergence of the **Shia clerical establishment as an independent political force**. The Safavids had created the institutional framework; the Qajars witnessed its maturation into a power center that could rival the state.

### Tobacco Protest (1891)

The Tobacco Protest demonstrated the clergy's capacity for **mass political mobilization**. Grand Ayatollah Mirza Shirazi's fatwa showed that a single religious ruling could paralyze the economy and force the Shah to reverse policy. The *marja-e taqlid* (supreme source of emulation) could command more effective obedience than the Shah himself.

### Constitutional Revolution (1905-1911)

The clerical role in the Constitutional Revolution was **more ambiguous and divided**:

- **Pro-constitution clerics** like Ayatollahs **Tabatabaei** and **Behbahani** argued that constitutional government was compatible with, and even required by, Islamic principles. They provided crucial religious legitimacy to the movement.
- **Anti-constitution clerics** like **Sheikh Fazlollah Nuri** argued that a constitution based on human legislation was incompatible with divine law (*sharia*). Nuri was eventually executed by the constitutionalists in 1909.

This division foreshadowed the ongoing debate within Iranian Shiism about the proper relationship between religious authority and political governance -- a debate that would culminate in Khomeini's theory of *velayat-e faqih* (guardianship of the jurist) in 1979.

### The Financial Power of the Clergy

The Shia clergy's political independence rested on their **financial autonomy**. Unlike Sunni clerics, who were typically state employees, Shia *mujtahids* controlled independent revenue streams:
- **Khums** (one-fifth tax on certain categories of wealth)
- **Zakat** (alms tax)
- **Religious endowments** (*awqaf*)
- **Voluntary donations** from followers

This financial independence meant the clergy could act as a **counter-power** to the state, a dynamic that had no equivalent in the Sunni world and that made Iranian politics fundamentally different from politics in Ottoman/Turkish, Arab, or Central Asian Muslim societies.

## 13. How Foreign Interference Created Deep Iranian Distrust

### The Pattern of Betrayal

The Qajar era established a **pattern of foreign interference** that created deep, lasting distrust of Western powers in Iranian political culture:

1. **Territorial theft**: Russia stripped Iran of its Caucasian territories through superior military force and imposed humiliating treaties (Gulistan, Turkmenchay).

2. **Economic exploitation**: Britain and its agents secured control of Iran's most valuable resources (tobacco, oil, banking, railways) at exploitative terms, enriching foreign shareholders while impoverishing Iranians.

3. **Sovereignty violations**: The Anglo-Russian Convention of 1907 divided Iran into spheres of influence **without even consulting Iran** -- treating a sovereign state as a colonial territory in all but name.

4. **Democratic sabotage**: When Iranians created their own democratic institutions (the Constitutional Revolution), foreign powers **destroyed them** -- Russia bombarded the Majlis, and both Russia and Britain actively undermined parliamentary governance.

5. **Humanitarian catastrophe**: Despite Iran's neutrality in WWI, foreign occupation contributed to a **famine that killed approximately 2 million people**, a catastrophe largely ignored by the Western powers responsible.

6. **Coup support**: Britain supported Reza Khan's 1921 coup, installing a military strongman to serve British strategic interests rather than Iranian democratic aspirations.

### The Psychological Legacy

These experiences created a **political culture of suspicion** toward foreign powers, particularly Britain and Russia/the Soviet Union, that persists to this day. In Iranian political discourse, the assumption that foreign powers are secretly manipulating events behind the scenes is not paranoia -- it is the **logical conclusion drawn from 130 years of documented history** in which foreign powers repeatedly did exactly that.

This history explains why:
- The oil nationalization movement under **Mossadegh** (1951-1953) generated such passionate support
- The CIA/MI6 coup against Mossadegh in **1953** was so deeply traumatic
- The 1979 Revolution framed itself explicitly as **anti-imperialist**
- Iranian distrust of Western diplomatic assurances remains so deep
- The concept of **"neither East nor West"** resonated so powerfully as a revolutionary slogan
