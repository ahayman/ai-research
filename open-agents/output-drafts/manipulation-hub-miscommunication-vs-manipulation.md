# Manipulation vs. Miscommunication: A Decision Framework

## Pragmatic Manipulation and Self-Protection Hub — Cross-Cutting Framework Page

**Read before this page:** [Safety Assessment](manipulation-hub-safety-assessment.md) — if any lethality indicators are present in your situation, the strategies on this page are not where to start. Call the National Domestic Violence Hotline (1-800-799-7233 in the US) or the equivalent in your region.

## 1. Why This Page Exists

The most consequential question an autistic person can ask in a difficult interaction is:

**Is this a one-off misunderstanding, or is it part of a pattern I should take seriously?**

This question is dangerous in both directions.

**The false positive** — treating miscommunication as manipulation — damages legitimate relationships, isolates you from people who would actually help you, trains you to distrust ordinary social friction, and can make you feel like the world is more hostile than it actually is. Applied over time, it produces the kind of paranoid vigilance that is itself a trauma response and that makes you harder to help.

**The false negative** — treating manipulation as "just miscommunication" — keeps you in harm's way, trains you to override your own pattern recognition, and feeds the compliance training that says your natural responses are always the wrong ones. Applied over time, it produces a person who has learned not to notice when something is wrong.

For autistic readers, there is a specific trap at the center of this question. The **double empathy problem** (Milton, 2012, *Disability & Society* 27(6)) is real: genuine miscommunication between autistic and non-autistic people is common, bidirectional, and often nobody's fault. The "it's just miscommunication" explanation has the ring of truth even when the pattern is actually coercive control — because sometimes it *is* just miscommunication, and distinguishing the two is genuinely difficult. Pearson et al. (2022, *Autism*; 2024, *Autism in Adulthood*) documented this directly: autistic survivors frequently internalize the "maybe I'm just being too literal / too sensitive / misunderstanding" framing, often for years, before recognizing the actual pattern.

This page is the tool for making the distinction. It is not a diagnostic instrument — no page can tell you definitively what is happening in your situation. It is a structured way to ask better questions, using the evidence you actually have.

## 2. The Four Possibilities (Not Two)

The common framing — "is it miscommunication OR manipulation?" — presents a false binary. The actual landscape has at least four possibilities, each requiring a different response:

| Possibility | What It Is | What It Responds To |
|------------|------------|---------------------|
| **Miscommunication** | A genuine double empathy mismatch. Neither party is acting in bad faith; the pragmatic gap produced an unintended result. The person is available to learn from the exchange. | Clarification, explanation, patience. Usually resolves with better communication channels (written instead of verbal, explicit instead of implicit). |
| **Ignorance** | The person does not understand autism, or does not know that the thing they did has a specific impact on autistic people. They are not hostile, just uninformed. | Education, if you have the energy. Links to resources. Explaining the specific impact rather than the general category. Not every ignorant person is willing to learn — that determines whether this is actually ignorance or is sliding into carelessness. |
| **Carelessness** | The person knows better, or has been told, but isn't putting in the effort to adjust. The harm is real, but the motivation is apathy or convenience, not exploitation. | Clear feedback with consequences attached. Setting limits on what you will continue to participate in. This is the category most likely to transform into manipulation if the carelessness is rewarded. |
| **Manipulation** | The person is exploiting the pragmatic gap — either deliberately (knowingly choosing tactics that work against you) or habitually (the behavior is automatic and self-protective even if unconscious). The pattern consistently benefits them; raising concerns makes things worse rather than better. | Self-protection. Pattern documentation. Reality-check networks. Strategies from the [Pattern pages] and [Strategy pages]. In severe cases, disengagement and [Safety Planning]. |

Lumping these four together — either as "it's my fault, I should try harder" or as "they're abusing me" — produces wrong answers. The decision framework below is designed to separate them.

### A critical note about intentionality

The pragmatic analysis does not depend on intent. Whether a behavior is deliberate or habitual, knowing or unconscious, the **effect** on you is the same and the **appropriate response** is largely the same. You do not need to prove someone is "deliberately manipulating you" to take self-protective action. The distinction between "manipulation" and "carelessness" matters for how you frame the relationship to yourself, but it does not change whether you should protect yourself.

This removes the mind-reading requirement. You cannot know another person's intentions with certainty. You can observe their behavior, its consistency, and its effect on you. That is enough.

## 3. How Each Looks in Practice

### Miscommunication (genuine double empathy)

> You give a colleague direct feedback on a report: "The methodology section has three errors and the conclusion doesn't follow from the data." They look hurt and withdraw. Later they tell you "that felt really harsh." You explain that you were trying to help them catch the errors before review. They say "oh — I thought you were saying my work was bad in general." You clarify. They adjust. The next time, they know to ask "are you saying the whole thing is bad or just these specific sections?" and you know to add "this is useful work, here are the specific things I think need attention." The interaction repairs.

The marker: **repair is possible when both parties learn from the exchange**. Milton's double empathy problem correctly predicts this — bidirectional adjustment resolves the mismatch.

### Ignorance

> Your manager schedules your performance review in an open-plan area with fluorescent lights. You ask if it can be moved to a quiet space. They say "why?" You explain that the sensory environment affects your ability to think clearly. They say "oh, I didn't know that was a thing — sure, let's find a conference room." Next time, they schedule the review in the conference room automatically.

The marker: **the person did not know, they learn when told, and they act on what they learned**. This is not manipulation. It's an uninformed person becoming informed. For autistic readers, this is actually the best outcome you can hope for from most neurotypical people who do not already know about autism — a willingness to learn.

### Carelessness

> The same manager. You have asked multiple times over six months for the performance review to be in a quiet space. Sometimes they remember. Sometimes they don't. When they don't, they apologize and say "sorry, I forgot, I'll try to remember next time." The apology sounds sincere. But the pattern is consistent: when it's convenient for them to remember, they remember; when it's inconvenient, they don't. Your ability to think clearly in performance reviews is affected.

The marker: **the person knows, but the knowledge is not sufficiently motivating to change their behavior when it costs them something**. This is not manipulation, exactly — they aren't exploiting your needs for their benefit. But the harm is real, and the pattern will continue unless something forces a change.

### Manipulation

> The same manager again. You ask for a quiet space. They say "you're being too sensitive" or "nobody else needs this" or "I can't accommodate every little thing." When you escalate to HR, your manager tells HR you are "difficult to work with" and cites your previous requests as evidence. Your next performance review contains language about being "not a team player." The accommodation has become the basis for a negative review. Other colleagues tell you the manager has been describing you as "high-maintenance" behind your back.

The markers:
- The accommodation request is reframed as a character flaw
- The pattern consistently benefits the manager (they avoid accommodation work AND get to use your request against you)
- Raising the concern made things worse, not better
- Other people have been recruited into the narrative
- The autistic characteristic itself (sensory need) is being weaponized as evidence against you

This is no longer ignorance or carelessness. The behavior is now serving a function for the manager, and the function is your delegitimization.

## 4. The Decision Framework

Five tests for distinguishing the four possibilities. No single test is definitive. The pattern across tests is what matters.

### Test 1: The Repair Test

**Does explaining make things better or worse?**

When you clarify what you meant, or explain the impact of something, does the other person:
- Engage with the substance and adjust their behavior → miscommunication or ignorance
- Say they'll adjust, then not adjust, but acknowledge the problem → carelessness
- Dismiss the clarification, attack your delivery, or change the subject → manipulation
- Use the clarification as new ammunition ("So you admit you said X") → manipulation

The repair test is the most diagnostic single signal, because the four possibilities differ fundamentally in how they respond to communicative repair.

### Test 2: The Consistency Test

**Does the pattern consistently benefit the other person at your expense?**

- The outcomes are mixed — sometimes it benefits them, sometimes you, sometimes neither → miscommunication or legitimate conflict
- The outcomes consistently benefit them, but they don't seem aware of the pattern → carelessness (at best) or unconscious manipulation
- The outcomes consistently benefit them, and when you point this out, the pattern continues → manipulation

Coercive control is a **pattern, not an incident** (Stark, 2007, *Coercive Control*). A single lopsided outcome is not evidence of anything. A repeated pattern of lopsided outcomes is evidence.

### Test 3: The Trajectory Test

**Over time, is the relationship getting better, worse, or the same?**

- Getting better: miscommunications are being resolved through communication → miscommunication
- Getting worse in specific areas, improving in others: partial repair, normal relational friction → miscommunication or situational conflict
- Static: nothing changes, but nothing gets worse either → carelessness
- Getting progressively worse: the list of "not allowed" topics grows, your voice gets smaller, you feel more tired, more confused, more isolated → manipulation

Genuine miscommunications tend to resolve or at least stabilize. Manipulation tends to escalate, because the manipulator's tactics become more aggressive as you learn to resist the earlier ones.

### Test 4: The Three-Conversation Test

**Run the same concern through three separate conversations. Do you get the same answer, or different answers depending on what serves them?**

If you raise a concern on Monday and the person says "that didn't happen," then on Tuesday they say "it happened but it wasn't a big deal," then on Wednesday they say "it happened and it was a big deal but it was your fault" — you are watching a rotating defense, not a genuine perspective.

If you raise the same concern and get roughly the same answer each time — even an answer you disagree with — you are dealing with a person who has a stable position, even if that position is wrong.

Miscommunications have stable explanations (the person can tell you what they meant, and the explanation is consistent). Manipulation has rotating explanations (the person tells you whatever defuses the current challenge).

### Test 5: The Witnessed Behavior Test

**Does their behavior toward you change when other people are present?**

- The same whether witnessed or not → miscommunication, ignorance, or carelessness (they're being consistently themselves)
- Noticeably better when witnessed, worse when alone → manipulation
- Noticeably worse when witnessed (openly cruel in front of others) → often a specific kind of manipulation involving social dominance displays; high risk if combined with other indicators

A person whose "private face" is dramatically different from their "public face" when interacting with you is performing. The public face is what they know others will find acceptable; the private face is what they are actually willing to do.

### Using the Tests

No single test produces a verdict. Use them together:

| Test Results | Likely Category |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Repair works, consistency mixed, trajectory improving, explanations stable, behavior consistent whether witnessed or not | Miscommunication |
| Repair works if educated, consistency sometimes lopsided, trajectory improving, explanations stable, behavior consistent | Ignorance |
| Repair fails (behavior doesn't change), consistency lopsided, trajectory static, explanations stable, behavior consistent | Carelessness |
| Repair fails and makes things worse, consistency consistently lopsided, trajectory worsening, explanations rotate, behavior better when witnessed | Manipulation |

The more tests that point in the same direction, the more confident you can be. When tests point in different directions, default to caution and use time: observe more before acting.

## 5. Autism-Specific Considerations

The decision framework above is adapted from mainstream coercive control research (Stark 2007, Pearson et al. 2022/2024, Douglas & Sedgewick 2024). Several things work differently for autistic readers, and each creates a specific risk in this decision.

### The internalization trap

Pearson et al. (2022, *Autism*) conducted extensive qualitative research with autistic survivors of interpersonal victimization. A core finding: autistic survivors frequently **internalize the blame** — "I felt like I deserved it because I was autistic." The quote became the paper's title. The mechanism is not just self-doubt; it is the convergence of several autistic experiences:

- Lifetime of being told your natural responses are wrong (compliance training)
- The double empathy problem genuinely being a thing (so "maybe I misunderstood" is plausible every time)
- The credibility discount (Lim, Young & Brewer, 2022, PMC8813809) meaning others also tell you you misunderstood
- Alexithymia making it hard to argue about your own internal states
- Episodic memory differences making it hard to anchor events in time

The result: the "maybe I'm overreacting" thought is often trained into you, not evidence about the situation you're in.

**Practical implication for the decision framework:** If your first instinct when reading through the tests is "I can't tell, it's probably my fault," treat that instinct as **data about your training**, not evidence about the situation. Then apply the tests anyway, using external evidence (what actually happened, in what sequence, with what witnesses, with what documentation) rather than your internal feeling about whether you're overreacting.

*Evidence quality: [PRELIMINARY] for the Pearson qualitative finding; [COMMUNITY KNOWLEDGE] for the broader pattern of compliance-trained self-doubt, with converging clinical observation but no formal empirical validation.*

### Double empathy is real AND it can be weaponized

These are both true at the same time, and holding both is essential.

The double empathy problem (Milton, 2012) is correct: communication failures between autistic and non-autistic people are bidirectional, often nobody's fault, and usually resolvable with better channels and explicit communication. It is not true that every negative interaction is manipulation. Many negative interactions with neurotypical people are exactly what Milton described — two different communicative styles producing an unintended mismatch.

And: the double empathy frame can be deployed as cover. Someone who is controlling you can reach for "it was just a misunderstanding" every time you raise a concern. Saying "you're being too literal" can be an accurate observation about pragmatic mismatch, or it can be a thought-terminating cliché designed to avoid accountability. The phrase is the same; the function is different.

**How to tell:** Apply the tests in Section 4. If repair works when you clarify, if explanations are stable, if the trajectory is improving, the double empathy frame is probably accurate. If repair fails, explanations rotate, and things are getting worse, the double empathy frame is being deployed as cover regardless of whether the person consciously knows they are doing it.

### Your pattern recognition over time is a relative strength

Williams et al. (2018, *Autism Research*) specifically undermined the common theory that autistic difficulties with lie detection are mediated by theory-of-mind deficits. They are not. The mechanism is something else — likely slower trust updating from social-reputation cues, and under-weighting of appearance-based trustworthiness signals. This matters because it means the right intervention is not "try harder to read faces in real time" — that works against your grain. The right intervention is "track the pattern across time" — which works with your grain.

Rozenkrantz et al. (2021, *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*) found that autistic individuals show reduced susceptibility to framing effects and several other cognitive biases. This is a real strength that applies directly to the decision framework: you are relatively resistant to the persuasive pressure that single-instance social exchanges exert on neurotypical cognition. Use this. A single conversation feels less determinative for you than it does for most people, and that is an asset, not a deficit, when trying to distinguish manipulation from miscommunication.

**Practical implication:** Do not try to decide during a single difficult conversation. Observe. Record (mentally or in writing). Compare across time. Your slower trust-updating is appropriate caution, not social deficit.

*Evidence quality: [PRELIMINARY] for Williams 2018 (well-designed single study with specifically important null mindreading-mediation finding); [PRELIMINARY] for Rozenkrantz 2021 (review paper with some replicated findings and active critical discussion in Mad in America and other venues).*

### The credibility discount affects your calibration inputs

Lim, Young & Brewer (2022) found autistic adults are perceived as more deceptive and less credible than neurotypical adults *when telling the truth*. This has a consequence that is easy to miss: if you calibrate your sense of whether you're "overreacting" by watching how others react to your descriptions of what happened, you are calibrating to a biased instrument. Other people's skepticism of your account is partly a fact about their calibration, not about your accuracy.

**Practical implication:** When deciding whether to take a pattern seriously, do not weight others' reactions to your account as evidence that your account is wrong. Their reaction is evidence about their calibration. Your account is evidence about what happened. These are different inputs and they should not cancel each other out.

*Evidence quality: [VALIDATED] for the credibility discount as a structural fact.*

### The compliance training problem, revisited

The Safety Assessment page mentioned this briefly; it needs fuller treatment here because the decision framework is where the compliance training is most likely to distort your judgment.

Many autistic adults grew up in environments that trained them to suppress natural responses, prioritize others' comfort, and treat "no" as the beginning of a negotiation rather than a complete sentence. Whether this came from ABA, "social skills" training, relentless pressure to mask, or just the accumulated weight of being corrected thousands of times for natural behavior, the effect is the same: **by adulthood, your automatic response to "am I being harmed?" is often "no, I am being difficult."**

This is not accurate information about your current situation. It is a trained response. It will fire reliably even when the situation is objectively harmful. It is the specific psychological condition that makes coercive control easier to establish and harder to escape.

**Practical implication:** When you run the decision framework and your gut says "I'm probably overreacting," treat that as a hypothesis to test, not a conclusion to accept. Apply the external tests in Section 4 anyway. If the external tests point toward manipulation and your internal sense points toward "I'm overreacting," the external tests are more reliable than your internal sense, because your internal sense has been trained by people who benefited from you not trusting it.

*Evidence quality: The compliance training → adult vulnerability pipeline is [COMMUNITY KNOWLEDGE] with strong experiential support and some clinical case literature. The Kupferstein 2018 ABA-PTSD study is under Expression of Concern and cannot be cited as established. The underlying phenomenon may be real but the empirical status is preliminary.*

## 6. Strategies by Conditional

The decision framework points toward different strategies depending on which category you're in:

### If the evidence points to miscommunication

- **Clarify directly.** "I want to make sure we're on the same page. When you said X, did you mean Y or Z?"
- **Shift communication channels.** If verbal exchanges are producing mismatches, try written. If written is producing mismatches, try verbal with explicit check-ins. For autistic readers, written communication is often more reliable because it allows deliberate composition and creates a review-able record.
- **Make implicit expectations explicit.** Double empathy mismatches often come from unstated assumptions on both sides. Surfacing the assumptions is usually sufficient.
- **Give the relationship time to adjust.** Miscommunications resolve with repeated repair. You should see improvement over time, even if individual interactions remain imperfect.
- **Don't over-apply the manipulation frameworks.** If you're reading pattern pages in this hub and constantly recognizing your partner/friend/family member, pause. The patterns are designed to be recognizable — that's the point — but recognition is not diagnosis. Apply the decision framework rigorously before concluding.

### If the evidence points to ignorance

- **Assess willingness to learn.** Does the person ask questions when you explain? Do they retain what you tell them? Do they adjust?
- **Provide concrete resources if you have the energy.** Not vague "read about autism" but specific resources on the specific thing they need to understand (e.g., "Here's an article on sensory processing; the relevant section is pages 3-5").
- **Set limits on how much educational labor you will provide.** You are not obligated to be anyone's autism tutor, especially not at the cost of your own wellbeing.
- **Watch for the transformation to carelessness.** If someone remains "ignorant" after multiple patient explanations, they are no longer ignorant. They are careless at best and manipulative at worst.

### If the evidence points to carelessness

- **Attach consequences to the behavior.** Carelessness continues as long as it is cost-free. Real costs change behavior; polite requests do not.
- **Document the pattern.** Keep a written record so you can demonstrate the pattern over time if needed (HR escalation, medical provider change, etc.).
- **Consider whether the relationship is worth maintaining at its current level.** Careless relationships are sustainable but costly. They do not get better on their own. Decide what level of ongoing cost is acceptable.
- **Be alert to carelessness becoming manipulation.** When a careless person discovers that their carelessness is benefiting them at your expense, some of them will continue the behavior precisely because it benefits them. This crosses the line into manipulation even if the original behavior was not.

### If the evidence points to manipulation

- **Stop trying to repair through communication.** The [Repair Test] has shown you that explanation makes things worse. Continuing to explain is wasting energy and providing new ammunition.
- **Start documenting.** Written records become mechanical necessity in manipulation contexts, not just good practice. [See the Documentation strategy page.]
- **Build or activate your reality-check network.** Trusted people who can validate your perception are essential compensation for the credibility discount and the gaslighting pressure. [See the Reality-Check Networks strategy page.]
- **Return to the [Safety Assessment page].** If any lethality indicators are present, this is not a hub decision. Call NDVH or the equivalent in your region.
- **Consider the [Can This Be Repaired? page]** for the engage-vs-exit decision.

### If you cannot tell

- **Wait and observe.** Time is your friend when distinguishing manipulation from miscommunication. The pattern clarifies itself over weeks and months.
- **Document as if you might need the evidence later.** Even if this turns out to be miscommunication, the documentation harms nothing. If it turns out to be manipulation, you will have evidence that your future self is going to need.
- **Consult your reality-check network.** A trusted third party can often see patterns you cannot see from inside them.
- **Default to caution.** The asymmetry of errors matters: treating miscommunication as manipulation is usually recoverable (you can repair the relationship if you eventually realize you were wrong); treating manipulation as miscommunication is often not recoverable (damage accumulates).

## 7. What This Page Cannot Do

- **Tell you definitively which category your situation falls into.** The tests in Section 4 are orientation tools, not diagnostic instruments. They work across multiple observations over time. They will not produce certainty from a single difficult conversation.
- **Account for mixed situations.** Real people are not always purely one thing. Someone can be genuinely ignorant about autism AND weaponize what they learn once they're told. Someone can be careless in some areas and manipulative in others. The framework handles the dominant pattern; the edge cases require judgment.
- **Work well when you are in crisis.** This page assumes you have the cognitive resources to apply tests and compare observations. When you are depleted, in crisis, or post-meltdown, the framework is too demanding. In those states, defer decisions, reach out to a reality-check person, and come back to the framework when you have more resources.
- **Replace professional judgment.** If you are trying to decide whether to leave a relationship, disclose to HR, make a police report, or take other consequential action, the framework is not sufficient. Advocates, therapists, and attorneys can help you weight factors the framework cannot address.
- **Eliminate the possibility of error.** False positives and false negatives are both possible. The framework reduces the rate; it does not eliminate it. You will sometimes be wrong in both directions. That is a feature of operating under uncertainty, not a failure of the tool.

## 8. Key Takeaways

1. **Four possibilities, not two.** Miscommunication, ignorance, carelessness, and manipulation each require different responses. Lumping them together produces wrong answers.

2. **The Repair Test is the most diagnostic single signal.** Miscommunication and ignorance respond to clarification. Carelessness does not respond. Manipulation responds by getting worse.

3. **Pattern over time is more reliable than any single incident.** Coercive control is a pattern, not an incident (Stark, 2007). Your relative strength is pattern recognition across time — use it rather than trying to decide in real time.

4. **Intentionality doesn't matter for response.** Deliberate and habitual manipulation have the same effect on you and require the same self-protection. You don't need to prove intent.

5. **Your internalization trap is trained, not evidence.** "Maybe I'm overreacting" is often the shape of compliance training, not information about the current situation. Run the external tests anyway.

6. **Double empathy is real AND can be weaponized.** Both are true. Apply the Repair Test to tell which is happening in any specific case.

7. **Your credibility discount affects your calibration inputs.** Others' skepticism of your account is evidence about their calibration, not about your accuracy.

8. **When in doubt, document and wait.** Time clarifies patterns. Documentation protects future-you. The asymmetry of errors favors caution.

9. **This page does not replace the [Safety Assessment page].** If lethality indicators are present, the decision framework on this page is not where to start. Call the hotline.
