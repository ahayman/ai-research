# Concept Research: Symbol-Based Communication — PECS, Makaton, and Visual Support Systems

## Overview

Symbol-based communication systems use pictures, symbols, signs, or visual supports as the primary medium of expression for individuals who cannot rely on speech. These systems are often the first form of augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) introduced to non-speaking autistic children, and they remain in widespread use across educational settings worldwide. The theoretical foundations vary — the Picture Exchange Communication System (PECS) is grounded in applied behavior analysis and B.F. Skinner's analysis of verbal behavior, Makaton combines manual signs with graphic symbols alongside speech, and Blissymbols represents a compositional semantic system where meaning is constructed from approximately 100-120 base elements. Each system makes different assumptions about how non-speaking individuals learn to communicate and what role visual representation plays in language development.

The evidence base for symbol-based systems is uneven. PECS has the strongest research support among dedicated symbol systems, with multiple meta-analyses and randomized controlled trials demonstrating improvements in communication initiation and frequency, though evidence for effects on speech development and spontaneous commenting is less robust. Makaton has been adopted on a massive scale — over 100,000 users in the UK alone — but without the kind of rigorous controlled evaluation typically expected of an intervention with such broad reach. Blissymbols, with over 6,500 authorized symbols built from compositional semantic principles, represents an intellectually sophisticated system that has been used primarily with individuals with cerebral palsy and has seen less adoption in the autism community. Across all systems, research on symbol transparency (how guessable a symbol's meaning is) and iconicity (the degree to which a symbol resembles its referent) has direct implications for learnability and system design.

Understanding symbol-based communication requires examining not just the systems themselves but the broader ecosystem of visual supports — visual schedules, social stories, visual timers, choice boards — that structure the communicative environment for autistic individuals. These supports capitalize on the visual-spatial processing strengths documented in autism research and provide predictability and structure that can reduce anxiety and increase communicative opportunity. The relationship between symbol-based communication and language development is a key research question: does learning to communicate through symbols build toward more complex language, or does it represent a parallel communicative system? The evidence suggests that for many individuals, symbol systems serve as a bridge to more complex communication, though some will use symbols as their primary mode throughout life.

## Core Components

### Component 1: PECS (Picture Exchange Communication System)
- **Purpose**: Teach functional communication through the physical exchange of picture symbols, beginning with requesting and systematically expanding to more complex communicative functions
- **Developers**: Andy Bondy, PhD, and Lori Frost, MS, CCC-SLP, developed PECS in 1985 at the Delaware Autism Program
- **Theoretical foundation**: Applied behavior analysis (ABA) principles and B.F. Skinner's analysis of verbal behavior, specifically the concept of "mands" (requests)
- **Six phases of instruction**:
  - Phase I: How to Communicate — learner exchanges a single picture for a desired item (no verbal prompts)
  - Phase II: Distance and Persistence — learner travels to communication partner and communication book to make exchanges
  - Phase III: Picture Discrimination — learner selects from multiple pictures to request specific items
  - Phase IV: Sentence Structure — learner constructs sentences on a sentence strip (e.g., "I want" + item picture)
  - Phase V: Responding to Questions — learner uses pictures to answer "What do you want?"
  - Phase VI: Commenting — learner uses pictures to respond to questions like "What do you see?" and to make spontaneous comments
- **Evidence base**: A meta-analysis (Flippin et al., 2010, ASHA) found PECS effective for increasing communication frequency but noted that evidence for speech gains was weaker; a 2025 systematic review of 37 RCTs in China showed significant improvements across cognitive function, social skills, and mental health; a systematic review analyzing 51 studies (2012-2024) found consistent positive outcomes
- **Limitations**: Small number of controlled studies relative to widespread use; limited evidence for maintenance and generalization across settings; most studies focus on requesting, with less evidence for higher communicative functions

### Component 2: Makaton
- **Purpose**: Support communication through the combined use of manual signs (hand gestures), graphic symbols (images), and spoken language used simultaneously
- **Developers**: Margaret Walker and colleagues at the Royal Association for Deaf People, developed in the UK in the 1970s
- **Approach**: Key word signing — signs are used alongside speech to emphasize important words, not as a replacement for speech; designed for individuals who can hear but have communication difficulties
- **Adoption**: Over 100,000 children and adults use Makaton in the UK; increasingly used in mainstream and special education settings; programs in 40+ countries
- **Evidence base**: Generally shows that Makaton promotes speech development; however, the program was adopted on a massive scale without rigorous controlled evaluation; some concern that individuals may become overly reliant on signs, though this risk appears small in practice
- **Relationship to autism**: Allows autistic children and adults to communicate independently using signs and symbols, with many naturally dropping signs as speech develops; simpler than full sign language (BSL/ASL), focusing on key words using obvious gestures
- **Limitations**: Less systematic evidence base compared to PECS; primarily evidence from UK clinical practice rather than controlled trials

### Component 3: Blissymbols (Blissymbolics)
- **Purpose**: Provide a compositional semantic graphical language where complex meanings can be constructed from simple base elements
- **Developer**: Charles K. Bliss (1897-1985), originally developed 1942-1949 while a refugee at the Shanghai Ghetto and in Sydney; inspired by Chinese characters
- **Structure**: Over 6,500 authorized symbols in the current Blissymbolics Communication International (BCI) vocabulary (2025 BCI-AV includes 6,183 approved symbols), built from approximately 100-120 base elements; symbols can be combined to create new meanings compositionally
- **AAC adoption**: Pioneered for AAC use by Shirley McNaughton in 1971 at the Ontario Crippled Children's Centre for children with cerebral palsy; primarily used in physical disability communities rather than autism
- **Evidence**: Practitioners report that users who learn Blissymbolics find it easier to learn traditional orthography afterward; limited formal evidence base specifically for autism populations
- **Compositional nature**: Unlike picture systems where each concept requires a separate image, Blissymbols allow users to construct novel meanings by combining elements — a system that leverages pattern recognition and systematic thinking
- **Limitations**: Steeper learning curve than photographic or pictographic systems; less intuitive for unfamiliar communication partners; limited adoption in autism specifically

### Component 4: PCS (Picture Communication Symbols) and Widgit
- **Purpose**: Provide standardized symbol sets used within AAC devices and communication boards
- **PCS (Mayer-Johnson)**: The most widely used symbol set in the US; simple line drawings with high iconicity for concrete concepts; used in Boardmaker software, TouchChat, and many AAC systems
- **Widgit Literacy Symbols (WLS)**: The dominant symbol set in UK education; designed with literacy in mind, incorporating grammatical markers and a systematic visual structure
- **Iconicity research**: Symbol transparency (guessability without instruction) varies significantly across symbol sets; transparent symbols are easier to learn; PCS generally more transparent than Blissymbols for concrete concepts but less so for abstract ones; ARASAAC (free, open-source) has shown the best iconicity across grammatical categories in comparative studies
- **Key distinction**: PCS and Widgit are symbol sets (visual libraries), not communication systems; they are used within systems like PECS, communication boards, or AAC apps

### Component 5: Visual Supports and Environmental Communication
- **Purpose**: Structure the communicative environment to increase predictability, reduce anxiety, and create opportunities for communication
- **Types**:
  - **Visual schedules**: Sequences of pictures/symbols showing the order of activities; provide predictability and reduce transition anxiety
  - **Social stories**: Short narratives (Carol Gray, 1991) using text and pictures to explain social situations and expected behaviors
  - **Choice boards**: Arrays of symbols allowing individuals to select between options
  - **Visual timers**: Provide concrete representation of time passing; support transitions and waiting
  - **First-then boards**: Show a required activity followed by a preferred activity; used to support task completion
- **Relationship to autism**: Visual supports capitalize on the visual-spatial processing strengths documented in autism research; provide concrete, persistent information (unlike speech, which is transient)
- **Evidence**: Strong evidence for visual schedules reducing challenging behavior and increasing independence; social stories have moderate evidence; visual supports are recommended by NICE guidelines and autism clinical practice guidelines

## How It Works

### Step 1: Communication Function Assessment
- **Trigger**: Individual identified as needing communication support
- **Action**: Assess current communicative behaviors (pointing, reaching, leading, vocalizing, challenging behavior as communication); identify communicative intent that cannot be expressed through available means
- **Result**: Understanding of which communicative functions are present (requesting, rejecting, social) and which need support

### Step 2: Symbol System Selection
- **Trigger**: Assessment indicates need for symbol-based support
- **Action**: Match system to individual profile — PECS for individuals who benefit from structured behavioral teaching with clear exchange mechanics; Makaton for individuals who can imitate signs and benefit from multimodal input; high-tech symbol-based AAC for individuals with the motor skills for touchscreen access
- **Result**: Selected system matched to motor, cognitive, and communicative profile
- **Key considerations**: Symbol transparency for the individual (some individuals respond better to photographs, others to line drawings, others to abstract symbols); motor requirements of the system; availability of trained professionals

### Step 3: Initial Teaching (System-Specific)
- **PECS Phase I**: No-error teaching of the physical exchange — learner reaches for desired item, is physically prompted to pick up picture and place in partner's hand; partner immediately provides the item and verbal label
- **Makaton**: Signs taught in context alongside speech; emphasis on motivating activities where communication is functional; signs modeled consistently by all communication partners
- **Visual supports**: Introduced in structured routines; taught through use rather than explicit instruction; predictability builds understanding

### Step 4: Expansion of Communicative Functions
- **Trigger**: Learner demonstrates reliable use of initial communication
- **Action**: Systematically expand beyond requesting — teach rejecting, commenting, asking questions, social greetings, emotional expression; expand vocabulary; increase communication partners
- **Result**: Broader communicative competence; movement from instrumental communication (getting needs met) toward social and expressive communication
- **Key challenge**: Many programs stall at the requesting stage; advancing to commenting and social functions requires intentional programming

### Step 5: Transition and Integration
- **Trigger**: Changing needs, growing competence, or changing environments
- **Action**: May transition from symbol-based to text-based systems; integrate symbol supports with high-tech AAC; fade visual supports as independence grows; or maintain symbol system as primary communication mode
- **Result**: Communication system that evolves with the individual; symbols may remain the primary mode, become supplementary, or serve as a bridge to text-based communication

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: PECS in a Classroom
- **Scenario**: A 5-year-old non-speaking autistic child in a special education classroom learns PECS Phase I. During snack time, they see a preferred food (crackers). A picture of crackers is on the table. The physical prompter (behind the child) gently guides the child to pick up the picture and hand it to the communication partner, who immediately says "Crackers! You want crackers!" and provides the crackers. After dozens of repetitions, the child independently picks up and exchanges the picture without prompting.
- **Illustration**: Demonstrates the errorless learning approach and the physical exchange mechanism that distinguishes PECS from other picture-pointing systems
- **Key Insight**: PECS requires the learner to initiate communication by traveling to the partner and making an exchange — it is not a pointing system, and this initiation is a critical communicative skill

### Example 2: Makaton in Everyday Life (UK Context)
- **Scenario**: A parent signs key words while speaking to their minimally verbal autistic child: "Do you WANT [sign: want] JUICE [sign: juice] or WATER [sign: water]?" The child signs "juice" back. Over time, the child begins saying "juice" while signing, and eventually says "juice" without the sign.
- **Illustration**: Shows the multimodal nature of Makaton — signs, symbols, and speech used together — and the bridge-to-speech trajectory
- **Key Insight**: Makaton's key word signing approach (signing important words while speaking full sentences) provides visual emphasis on critical vocabulary without requiring the child to process full signed sentences

### Example 3: Blissymbols Compositionality
- **Scenario**: A Blissymbols user needs to express a concept they have never been explicitly taught. Using the compositional rules, they combine the symbol for "feeling" with the symbol for "up" to express happiness/excitement, or combine "house" + "knowledge" to refer to school — constructing novel meanings from familiar elements.
- **Illustration**: Demonstrates the generative power of a compositional symbol system compared to fixed picture libraries where each concept requires a pre-made image
- **Key Insight**: Blissymbols' compositionality may be particularly well-suited to the systematic, pattern-based cognitive style associated with autism, though this hypothesis has not been formally tested

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: PECS is just "pointing at pictures"
  - **Reality**: PECS is a structured behavioral teaching program with six specific phases, not merely a collection of picture cards. The physical exchange mechanism (picking up and handing over a picture) teaches communicative initiation in a way that pointing does not.

- **Misconception**: Using signs or symbols will discourage speech development
  - **Reality**: Research across PECS and Makaton consistently shows that symbol/sign use does not inhibit speech and may facilitate it by reducing communicative frustration and providing a visual scaffold for language.

- **Misconception**: Blissymbols has "over 3,000 symbols"
  - **Reality**: The current Blissymbolics Communication International vocabulary includes over 6,500 authorized symbols (2025 BCI-AV: 6,183 approved symbols). The 3,000 figure may reflect the historical scope of Bliss's original work rather than the current system.

- **Misconception**: Symbol-based communication is only for young children or people with intellectual disability
  - **Reality**: Symbol-based systems are used across the lifespan and across cognitive profiles. Many adults use symbol-based AAC as their primary communication mode, and visual supports are beneficial for autistic individuals regardless of cognitive ability.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **PECS and motor difficulties**: The physical exchange mechanism in PECS requires sufficient motor coordination to pick up a picture and hand it to a partner; individuals with severe motor impairments may need adapted versions or alternative systems
- **Symbol systems across cultures**: Symbol transparency varies across cultures; a symbol that is iconic in one cultural context may be opaque in another; most commercially available symbol sets reflect Western cultural contexts
- **Digital-native symbols**: Younger AAC users may be more familiar with emojis and digital icons than traditional symbol sets, raising questions about whether standard AAC symbol libraries need updating
- **Regression during transitions**: Moving from one symbol system to another (e.g., PECS to high-tech AAC app) can cause temporary communication regression as the individual adapts to the new system

## Related Concepts

- **AAC Technology**: Symbol-based communication is a subset of the broader AAC landscape; many high-tech AAC devices use PCS, Widgit, or other symbol sets as their visual vocabulary
- **Visual-Spatial Cognition**: Enhanced visual processing in autism provides the cognitive foundation for why symbol-based communication may be particularly effective for autistic individuals
- **Motor-Language Dissociation**: Motor planning difficulties may affect the ability to use certain symbol systems (e.g., PECS exchange requires hand coordination; Makaton signing requires imitation of hand shapes)
- **Gestalt Language Processing**: Some theoretical overlap between how gestalt language processors acquire language in chunks and how symbol-based systems teach communication in structured phases
- **Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA)**: PECS is explicitly grounded in ABA principles; the relationship between ABA and autism is itself deeply contested in the autistic community

## Research Gaps

- **PECS beyond Phase III**: Most research focuses on the early requesting phases of PECS; there is markedly less evidence on outcomes for Phases IV-VI (sentence construction, responsive requesting, commenting)
- **Makaton controlled trials**: Despite over 100,000 users in the UK, Makaton lacks the rigorous controlled evaluation that its widespread use warrants
- **Blissymbols and autism**: Very limited research on Blissymbols specifically for autistic populations; the compositional nature of the system may align well with autistic cognitive strengths but this has not been empirically tested
- **Symbol preference and autism**: Little research on whether autistic individuals systematically prefer certain symbol types (photographs vs. line drawings vs. abstract symbols) and whether preferences correlate with cognitive profiles
- **Long-term outcomes**: Few studies track symbol-based communication outcomes beyond initial training; the lifespan trajectory is poorly documented
- **Generalization**: Evidence for generalization of symbol-based communication across settings, partners, and contexts remains limited, particularly for PECS
