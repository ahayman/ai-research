## 0. The framing problem this category has to solve

A casual reading of this body of literature gives two contradictory impressions. The first impression is that mindfulness, body-scan practice, somatic experiencing, and "tune into your body" approaches are the default first-line response to alexithymia and to trauma. The second impression is that the same body of practices carries non-trivial documented harm: between roughly a third and half of meditators in measured samples report meaningful adverse effects, the rates spike in trauma-exposed populations, and clinicians have published explicit cautions for somatic-symptom-disorder, dissociative-subtype PTSD, and parsing-deficit alexithymia profiles.

Both readings are partly true. The category's job is to render them coherent without overclaiming on either side. The frame that does the work:

**Three evidence tiers**, applied throughout:

- **Tier 1 — well-established**: meditation-related adverse effects (MRAE) are common in measured general-population and clinical samples. Specific figures from Britton 2021 and Goldberg 2022. Specific risk factors: childhood adversity, trauma history, comorbidity. Specific common adverse-effect types: anxiety, traumatic re-experiencing, emotional sensitivity, depersonalisation.
- **Tier 2 — mechanistically supported**: body-attention focus can amplify symptom intensity (somatosensory amplification literature, Barsky); body sensations can trigger panic in panic-prone individuals; body-focus can deepen depersonalisation in already-dissociating individuals; body-focus can trigger trauma re-experiencing. These mechanisms are well-attested in adjacent literatures and are *plausibly* at work in parsing-deficit alexithymia, but the alexithymia-specific subgroup data does not exist.
- **Tier 3 — theoretical extrapolation**: the specific claim that *interoceptive-awareness training is risky for parsing-deficit alexithymics* is extrapolated from Tier 1 + Tier 2 plus the parsing-vs-detection deficit framework (Garfinkel three-dimensional model, Aaron 2020 quadratic relationship). The claim has not been directly tested in subtype-stratified RCTs. The Norman 2019 systematic review of mindfulness for alexithymia explicitly notes the absence of adverse-effect data from the four trials it reviewed.

The category honors this distinction at every layer — the explanation, the diagrams, the decision aid, the framing of the alternatives.

## 1. The MRAE rate question — corrected

### 1.1 What the parent exploration originally said

The parent exploration's first draft cited "20-25 per cent" of meditators reporting "particularly bad or frightening" experiences and attributed this to Britton 2021. The validator (Supplement 1) flagged this:

- The 20-25 per cent figure was the field's *a priori* expectation threshold, not Britton's measured rate.
- The Britton 2021 and Goldberg 2022 figures are different (different studies, different methodologies, different samples). The supplement attributed "32% / 50% / 37%" to Britton when those numbers are actually from Goldberg.

### 1.2 What Britton 2021 actually reports

**Britton WB, Lindahl JR, Cooper DJ, Canby NK, Palitsky R (2021).** "Defining and measuring meditation-related adverse effects in mindfulness-based programs." *Clinical Psychological Science*, 9(6), 1185-1204. PMC8845498.

This is a measurement-framework paper. Britton developed the MRAES-MBP (Meditation-Related Adverse Effects Scale — Mindfulness-Based Programs) and applied it in a sample drawn from MBCT (Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy) trials. Sample N≈96.

Britton's measured rates:
- **83 per cent** reported any meditation-related side effect (MRSE)
- **58 per cent** reported negatively valenced effects
- **37 per cent** reported negative impact on functioning
- **14.1 per cent** reported effects lasting longer than one day
- **6.4 per cent** reported effects lasting longer than one month

The high 83 per cent figure for "any side effect" is partly a function of using a sensitive instrument that captures mild and transient experiences as well as severe ones. The 14.1 per cent and 6.4 per cent figures for duration are the more clinically conservative end of the same dataset. The 58 per cent / 37 per cent figures speak to valence and functional impact.

### 1.3 What Goldberg 2022 actually reports

**Goldberg SB, Lam SU, Britton WB, Davidson RJ (2022).** "Prevalence of meditation-related adverse effects in a population-based sample in the United States." *Psychotherapy Research*, 32(3), 291-305. PMC8636531 / PubMed 34074221.

This is a population-based study in a US sample with lifetime meditation exposure. N=434.

Goldberg's measured rates:
- **32.3 per cent** general-item MRAE rate (a single screening item)
- **50.0 per cent** one-or-more-specific-item rate (across detailed checklist items)
- **10.4 per cent** MRAE duration ≥1 month
- **1.2 per cent** functional impairment ≥1 month

Most common adverse-effect types: **anxiety, traumatic re-experiencing, and emotional sensitivity**. Childhood adversity was the prospectively-identified risk factor.

### 1.4 The honest framing

**Britton 2021 and Goldberg 2022 are different studies with different methodologies and different figures.** The deep dive must cite them separately. Both studies' measured rates exceed the 20-25 per cent field-expectation threshold. The duration-and-impairment figures (Britton's 14.1%/>1 day and 6.4%/>1 month; Goldberg's 10.4%/>1 month and 1.2%/functional impairment >1 month) provide the most clinically conservative anchor. The high "any side effect" rates (Britton 83%; Goldberg 50% one-or-more-specific-item) capture a wider net that includes mild and transient effects.

A defensible composite for narrative use: **the order-of-magnitude is "between roughly a third and half of meditators in measured samples report some meaningful adverse effect, with smaller percentages — roughly 6-15 per cent depending on study and threshold — experiencing effects lasting longer than a day to a month, and roughly 1 per cent experiencing functional impairment lasting longer than a month".** Anxiety, re-experiencing, and emotional sensitivity are among the most common. Childhood adversity is a documented risk factor.

### 1.5 The Lindahl 2017 Varieties of Contemplative Experience study

**Lindahl JR, Fisher NE, Cooper DJ, Rosen RK, Britton WB (2017).** "The varieties of contemplative experience: A mixed-methods study of meditation-related challenges in Western Buddhists." *PLoS One*, 12(5), e0176239. PMC5443484.

This is the qualitative anchor. A 59-category taxonomy of meditation-related experiences was developed across seven domains: cognitive, perceptual, affective, somatic, conative, sense of self, and social. Among 32 expert Western-Buddhist teachers in the sample, 56 of 59 categories (95%) were reported, with an average of 8.5 categories per expert.

**The taxonomy does not stratify by alexithymia subgroup.** It cannot directly speak to "alexithymia-specific harm." It speaks to the breadth and seriousness of meditation-related experience in a population of *experts*, who are presumably the population least at risk and most able to navigate the practices. The breadth of categories reported by experts is itself an argument that the practices are not adverse-effect-free for any population, including the most-prepared.

## 2. Mechanism of harm

### 2.1 Somatosensory amplification

**Barsky's somatosensory amplification (SSA) framework**: body-attention focus amplifies the perceived intensity of body signals. Originally developed for somatic-symptom-disorder populations, where chronic illness anxiety and body-focus iteratively reinforce each other. The mechanism: attention to a low-intensity sensation increases its perceived salience, increases interpretation-as-threat, increases physiological reactivity, increases sensation, loops.

**Empirical support**: strong in somatic-symptom-disorder, hypochondriasis, and chronic-pain populations. Reduces under standard CBT-for-illness-anxiety protocols. The mechanism is not contested.

**Implication for body-focused mindfulness in parsing-deficit alexithymia**: if the alexithymic individual already has heightened interoceptive sensibility (body signals are *louder* than baseline) but lacks the labelling architecture to translate signals into nameable affect, then sustained body-focus practice may amplify already-loud signals into unstructured distress. This is the parsing-deficit caution. It is a Tier 2 mechanistic claim, not a Tier 1 alexithymia-stratified RCT finding.

### 2.2 Panic induction

Body sensations — palpitations, dyspnea, dizziness, tingling — can trigger panic in panic-prone individuals via the catastrophic-misinterpretation model (Clark 1986; Barlow). The same sensations that interoceptive exposure deliberately targets in panic-disorder treatment can trigger uncued panic in panic-prone individuals during open-ended body-scan practice.

**Counter-example**: structured **interoceptive exposure** is therapeutic in panic disorder. The protocol deliberately induces the panic-related sensations (chair-spinning for dizziness, hyperventilation for tingling, etc.) to break the catastrophic-misinterpretation cycle through prediction-error correction. *This is not a refutation of the body-focus-can-harm claim*; it is evidence that **the structure of the intervention matters**. Interoceptive exposure is a structured, time-limited, prediction-correcting protocol with a specific population (panic disorder). Open-ended body-scan mindfulness is not. The two are not equivalent. Section 12 returns to this asymmetry.

### 2.3 Depersonalisation deepening

Depersonalisation and derealisation are relatively common in dissociative-subtype PTSD, in dissociative disorders proper, and in some anxiety-disorder presentations. There is published clinical literature documenting that mindfulness can deepen rather than relieve depersonalisation in already-dissociating individuals.

**Lindahl, Britton et al. published a specific paper on the nuanced mindfulness-depersonalisation relationship** (PMC9043097), arguing that the relationship is bidirectional: in some populations, mindfulness reduces depersonalisation by re-grounding attention in the body; in others, mindfulness deepens depersonalisation by intensifying observer-stance and detachment from embodied experience. The clinical task is identifying which subgroup an individual sits in *before* prescribing the practice.

### 2.4 Trauma re-experiencing

Body-focus can trigger trauma memory and sensation re-experiencing in trauma-exposed individuals. This is the central concern that Treleaven's *Trauma-Sensitive Mindfulness* framework was developed to address.

**Treleaven D, Britton WB (2018).** *Trauma-Sensitive Mindfulness: Practices for Safe and Transformative Healing.* W.W. Norton.

Treleaven's framework provides 36 specific modifications for trauma-survivor practitioners across five principles: attention, arousal, relationship, dissociation, social context. Examples include: choice in attention object (not always the breath), eyes-open practice options, shorter durations, anchoring to neutral or safe-feeling body regions, explicit titration around dissociation markers, and careful pacing of insight-style practices.

**Critical for this category: Treleaven's framework is trauma-focused, not alexithymia-focused.** Alexithymia is not directly named as a contraindication or as a modifiable factor. The alexithymia-specific adaptation is an *extrapolation* from Treleaven's trauma-focused framework via the parsing-deficit reasoning. The deep dive must honor this distinction.

The extrapolation is plausible — many alexithymics carry trauma history (PTSD-alexithymia ~53 per cent per the 2026 meta-analysis); parsing-deficit alexithymia shares the "loud signal, no label" architecture that Treleaven's framework is designed to titrate; the Tier 2 mechanism support is solid. But the formal application of Treleaven's framework to alexithymia subgroup-stratified samples has not been published.

## 3. The dissociative-subtype PTSD-mindfulness concern

**Lanius RA, et al. (2024).** "Choice points for mindfulness-informed treatment in dissociative-subtype PTSD." PMC11257171.

This is real published clinical literature. Lanius treats the dissociative subtype of PTSD as requiring specific protocol modifications when integrating mindfulness practice. Key choice points:

- Whether the patient can sustain dual-attention awareness without flipping into dissociation
- Whether body-focused content triggers depersonalisation
- Whether the intervention is structured-and-titrated versus open-ended
- Whether the patient has a felt-sense anchor (a body region or sensory channel that feels safe and grounding)
- Whether interoceptive scaffolding has been built before deeper trauma-focused mindfulness work

**Not alexithymia-specific.** The framework is dissociative-subtype-PTSD-specific. But it applies to many alexithymics with trauma history, and the underlying logic — that the same practice can therapeutically reground or iatrogenically detach depending on baseline architecture — is structurally identical to the parsing-deficit caution.

## 4. Why parsing-deficit ≠ detection-deficit alexithymia

The single most important conceptual distinction in this category. Without it, the "body-focused work for alexithymia" debate collapses into incoherence, because the same intervention has *opposite-direction* predictions for the two profiles.

### 4.1 Detection-deficit profile

**Phenomenology**: body signals are absent, low-amplitude, or below subjective threshold. The person reports "I don't feel anything in my body when I'm asked to check." Self-reported interoceptive sensibility (MAIA/SAQ) is low. Heartbeat-detection performance is at or near chance.

**Standard recommendation**: interoceptive-awareness training is the appropriate first-line intervention. The training builds detection capacity that is genuinely missing. MABT (Price & Hooven 2018), MBSR adapted for low-interoception, and ADIE-style heartbeat-detection training (Quadt et al. 2021, Lancet eClinicalMedicine) are the protocols of record. Quadt's ADIE trial in autistic adults shows positive results for an interoceptive-training protocol — important counter-evidence to the strong "body-focused work always harms parsing-deficit alexithymics" claim.

### 4.2 Parsing-deficit profile

**Phenomenology**: body signals are present, often loud or hypervigilantly-attended, but uninterpretable as nameable affect. The person reports "I can feel my heart racing and my chest tight, but I don't know what I'm feeling — angry? scared? excited? It's just intensity." Self-reported interoceptive sensibility may be elevated. Heartbeat-detection performance can be at or above chance — the deficit is downstream of detection.

**Standard recommendation breaks down**: more body-focus on already-loud signals risks amplifying the unstructured distress that the parsing failure produces. The Tier 2 mechanism support (Barsky SSA, Lindahl on depersonalisation, Treleaven on trauma re-experiencing) all converge on this prediction.

### 4.3 The Aaron 2020 quadratic finding

**Aaron RV, Blain SD, Snodgress MA, Park S (2020).** Pilot mindfulness intervention. PMC7076086.

The Aaron group reported a quadratic relationship between alexithymia and interoceptive accuracy: both very-low and very-high IA can co-occur with high alexithymia. The very-low-IA pole approximates the detection-deficit profile; the very-high-IA pole approximates the parsing-deficit/hypervigilant profile. *Both* poles have elevated alexithymia. This is direct empirical support for the dissociation between detection and parsing as separate failure modes.

The Aaron pilot also reported that body-scan-style mindfulness produced different responses across the IA distribution — suggestive of a moderation effect but pilot-only.

### 4.4 Hypervigilance with mislabelling

A specific subtype of parsing-deficit. The person already attends to body signals at elevated rates (hypervigilance — common in anxiety with somatic focus, in chronic-pain populations, in panic-prone individuals, in somatic-symptom disorder). They mislabel the signals (attribute them to threat, illness, or imminent loss-of-control). Adding body-focus practice deposits *more* attention onto an already over-attended channel. The mechanistic prediction is symptom amplification.

The hypervigilance-with-mislabelling profile is the highest-caution subgroup for body-focused interoceptive work. It overlaps substantially with the somatic-symptom-disorder population that Barsky's SSA framework was developed to characterise.

## 5. The "alexithymic patient bored / closed off in therapy" narrative

A theme that runs through alliance literature and clinical case-discussion: the alexithymic patient is described as bored, closed off, stiff, somatic-only, low-affect, hard-to-engage. The narrative frames it as a *patient-side problem*.

**Ogrodniczuk JS, Piper WE, Joyce AS (2020).** "Alexithymia and therapeutic alliance" PMC7451330. Documents the alliance-quality gap in high-alexithymia populations.

But the same data permit a *therapy-mismatch* reading. The clinician's protocol assumes emotional engagement that the patient cannot currently provide. The patient is not refusing; their architecture cannot generate the inputs the protocol requires. The "bored / closed off" presentation is a *signal of mismatch*, not a sign of pathology.

This reframing is clinically important because it relocates the corrective from "the patient must change" to "the protocol is wrongly matched to the architecture." The user's stated goal — distinguishing "I'm bad at this therapy" (often wrongful self-blame) from "this therapy is wrongly matched to my subtype" — is exactly this reframing.

The implication: distinguishing patient pathology from wrongly-matched therapy is itself a clinical skill. It is not the default reading in standard supervision frames.

## 6. The decision framework — who should NOT start with body-focused / interoceptive work

This is the most clinically actionable section of the category. The framework is a clinical-decision aid for *evaluation*, not a definitive contraindication list. The clinician (or self-evaluating user) reviews the profile and assesses caution-level.

| Profile | Caution level | Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| **Hypervigilance with mislabelling** (somatic-symptom disorder, anxiety with somatic focus, chronic pain with somatic vigilance) | **High** | Body-attention amplifies already-loud signals; SSA mechanism; risk of symptom intensification |
| **Dissociative-subtype PTSD** | **High** | Body-focus can deepen depersonalisation; Lanius choice-points framework applies |
| **High trauma load with low integration capacity** | **High** | Body-focus can trigger re-experiencing without containment; Treleaven framework applies |
| **Parsing-deficit alexithymia profile** (signals detected, can't interpret) | **Moderate** | Tier 2 mechanism prediction; not directly tested in subtype-stratified RCTs; alternatives may fit better |
| **Detection-deficit alexithymia profile** (signals not detected) | **Low — may be appropriate** | Standard recommendation; ADIE evidence in autism supports interoceptive-training value here |
| **No trauma load, no somatic-symptom complications** | **Standard mindfulness caution applies** | Tier 1 MRAE risk applies generally; no specific contraindication |

The framework is presented as: **identify the profile first, then make the protocol decision**. The default "everyone should try mindfulness" recommendation is supplanted by a profile-stratified one.

## 7. Markers of iatrogenic deterioration to watch for

These are not specific to alexithymia but apply across body-focused interventions for the at-risk populations identified in Section 6.

- **Symptom amplification over weeks of practice**: sleep worse, anxiety worse, pain worse, somatic symptoms worse. Sustained increase, not transient discomfort.
- **Increased depersonalisation / derealisation / unreality**: more "I'm not in my body," more "this isn't real," more "I'm watching myself from outside."
- **New-onset somatic symptoms**: previously absent body symptoms emerging during a practice period.
- **Increased shutdowns or dissociation**: more freezing, more zoning-out, more time-loss.
- **Increased intrusive trauma re-experiencing**: more flashbacks, more nightmares, more body-memory floods.
- **Treatment-aversion despite good engagement effort**: the patient is doing the practice as instructed and finding it iteratively worse, not better. Self-blame ("I must be doing it wrong") is the wrong reading.

The clinically critical move: when these markers appear, the protocol is the variable, not the patient. Reduce duration, change attention object, stop the practice for a period, switch modalities, or reassess the profile.

## 8. Alternatives when interoception-first is contraindicated

When the profile flags high or moderate caution, alternatives that operate from the *outside in* rather than the inside out tend to fit better:

- **ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy)**: acceptance and values-based action without forcing identification of internal affective states. Cognitive defusion, observer-self, and behavioural commitment are accessible through external means.
- **Granularity-building (Barrett framework)**: external-vocabulary expansion. Reading dense emotion words; categorising others' emotional states in fiction or video; discriminating high-arousal-positive from high-arousal-negative; practicing nuanced emotion labels in writing. Builds the conceptual architecture that parsing-deficit alexithymia needs *before* internal-introspection becomes productive.
- **Affect-labelling (Lieberman 2007)**: putting feelings into words, even tentatively, reduces amygdala reactivity via a right ventrolateral PFC → mPFC → amygdala pathway. Labelling can be scaffolded externally with context cues, observer description, or behavioural inference rather than requiring internal introspection.
- **Structured journaling**: scaffolded with explicit emotion-vocabulary lists, situation-feeling-thought-behaviour columns, and external-event triangulation. Pennebaker-style expressive writing, Baikie 2020 alexithymia adaptation.
- **App-based work**: MT-ALEX (Mindtastic Alexithymia) RCT data; Animi; AlexiLearn; smartphone-based emotion-recognition skills training (PMC6510700). Most use external-vocabulary scaffolding.
- **Mentalization-Based Therapy (MBT, Bateman & Fonagy)**: perspective-taking and reflective-function building before interoception. Operates from the *intersubjective* layer rather than the interoceptive layer. Fischer-Kern's MBT-for-BPD work shows alexithymia reductions.
- **EAET (Emotional Awareness and Expression Therapy, Lumley)**: structured emotional engagement focused on conflict and expression rather than open-ended body-focus. Multiple RCTs in chronic pain, fibromyalgia, MUS, older veterans.

## 9. Iatrogenic harm in trauma therapy specifically

A specific application of the general framework. Body-focused trauma therapies — Somatic Experiencing (Levine), Sensorimotor Psychotherapy (Ogden), body-based EMDR variants — are simultaneously *promoted* as alexithymia-friendly (because they bypass language) AND identified as carrying *amplified risk* in alexithymic patients (because the parsing-deficit version means body signals are detected but mislabelled, often as catastrophic).

The risk profile for alexithymic trauma patients:
- **Standard exposure (PE / CPT / TF-CBT)**: desensitization without integration; possible chronic stuck-ness; alexithymia attenuates and delays response (Pugach et al., PubMed 37917810).
- **Body-focused work without interoceptive scaffolding**: amplified somatic signal interpreted catastrophically, possible re-experiencing or dissociative episodes.
- **Mindfulness without trauma-sensitivity modifications**: re-experiencing and dissociation; Treleaven's framework was developed precisely for this.

The **phase-based protocol consensus** — emerging across STAIR-NT (Cloitre lineage), Phase-Based CPTSD treatment, Treleaven trauma-sensitive mindfulness, Lanius's sensory-pathways model — is: *stabilize → build interoceptive-parsing skills → process trauma narratively → integrate*. Direct exposure or unmodified body-focus before stabilization and parsing-skill-building is the iatrogenic-harm pathway.

Forward-pointer to **Cat 7 (Alexithymia ↔ Trauma — Bidirectional Mechanisms)** for the trauma-specific architecture.

## 10. The interoceptive-exposure counter-example

Interoceptive exposure (IE) in panic disorder is a well-validated therapeutic protocol. It deliberately induces panic-related body sensations — dizziness via chair-spinning, dyspnea via straw-breathing, palpitations via stair-climbing, tingling via hyperventilation — to break catastrophic-misinterpretation cycles through prediction-error correction.

**Sources**: standard CBT-for-panic protocols (Barlow / Craske); [PMC1559685](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1559685/), [PMC3727659](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3727659/), [Psychology Tools framework](https://www.psychologytools.com/resource/interoceptive-exposure).

Why this is a counter-example to the "body-focus is harmful" framing — and why it doesn't refute the parsing-deficit caution:

- IE is **structured**: specific induction protocols, fixed durations, prediction-tracking, response-prevention. Open-ended body-scan mindfulness is not.
- IE is **time-limited**: typical course is weeks, not lifelong practice.
- IE is **prediction-correcting**: the patient learns "this sensation does not predict catastrophe" through repeated exposure. Open-ended body-focus is not designed around prediction-error correction.
- IE is for a **specific population**: panic disorder, where the catastrophic-misinterpretation model is the diagnostic core. The population is different from somatic-symptom disorder, dissociative-subtype PTSD, or alexithymia-with-trauma.

**The honest reading**: not all body-focused interventions are equivalent. A structured exposure protocol in panic disorder is not the same intervention as open-ended body-scan mindfulness in a parsing-deficit alexithymic with trauma history. The category should hold both at once.

## 11. The honest evidence audit

For each major claim in this category, the evidence tier:

### Tier 1 — well-established
- Meditation-related adverse effects are common in measured samples (Britton 2021; Goldberg 2022).
- Childhood adversity is a documented risk factor for MRAE (Goldberg 2022).
- Anxiety, traumatic re-experiencing, and emotional sensitivity are among the most common MRAE types (Goldberg 2022).
- The 32 expert teachers in the Lindahl 2017 Varieties study reported 56 of 59 categories of meditation-related challenges (95%; PMC5443484).
- Treleaven's Trauma-Sensitive Mindfulness 36 specific modifications are established practical guidance for trauma populations.
- Dissociative-subtype PTSD requires specific protocol modifications when integrating mindfulness (Lanius 2024).
- High-alexithymia patients show attenuated and delayed response to standard prolonged exposure for PTSD (Pugach et al.).

### Tier 2 — mechanistically supported, theoretical extrapolation with adjacent evidence
- Body-attention focus amplifies symptom intensity in somatic-symptom-disorder populations (Barsky SSA framework).
- Mindfulness can deepen depersonalisation in already-dissociating individuals (Lindahl, PMC9043097).
- The parsing-deficit alexithymia profile (Aaron 2020 quadratic; Garfinkel three-dimensional model) is *plausibly* at higher risk for body-focused interventions on mechanistic grounds.
- Hypervigilance-with-mislabelling profiles are *plausibly* at higher risk for body-focused interventions on mechanistic grounds.
- The somatization pathway from trauma to body-symptom expression in alexithymic populations is theoretically well-supported but causally underdetermined.

### Tier 3 — theoretical only / direct evidence absent
- The specific claim that interoceptive-awareness training is risky for parsing-deficit alexithymics specifically — not directly tested in subtype-stratified RCTs.
- The Norman 2019 systematic review of mindfulness for alexithymia (PMC10270453) explicitly notes the absence of adverse-event data from the four trials it reviewed.
- Subtype-stratified prediction of which alexithymia profile will be harmed by which protocol — extrapolated from Tier 1 + Tier 2, not directly tested.
- Whether alexithymics specifically have higher dropout from MBSR or interoceptive training — no identified RCT explicitly reports this.

The category labels each major claim's tier explicitly throughout.

## 12. The clinical bottom line

Three takeaways for the user:

1. **The harm literature for body-focused / interoception-based interventions is real and well-documented for general populations.** Order-of-magnitude is ~32-50 per cent reporting some meaningful adverse effect in measured samples; ~6-15 per cent reporting effects lasting longer than a day to a month; ~1 per cent reporting functional impairment lasting longer than a month. Anxiety, traumatic re-experiencing, and emotional sensitivity are among the most common.

2. **The harm literature SPECIFICALLY FOR ALEXITHYMIA SUBTYPES is essentially absent.** What exists is theoretical extrapolation from the meditation-related-adverse-effects literature combined with the parsing-deficit framework and the Tier 2 mechanism literatures. Norman 2019's systematic review of mindfulness for alexithymia explicitly notes this absence.

3. **The clinically actionable response is profile-stratified evaluation, not a blanket recommendation.** The decision framework in Section 6 is the user's actionable tool. The markers of deterioration in Section 7 are the user's monitoring tool. The alternatives in Section 8 are the user's redirection tool.

The user's stated need — "an evidence-grounded basis for declining/modifying recommended interventions that may worsen their alexithymia, AND for distinguishing 'I'm bad at this therapy' (wrongful self-blame) from 'this therapy is wrongly matched to my subtype'" — is met by this combination. The framework gives them a way to evaluate. The markers give them a way to monitor. The alternatives give them a way to redirect. The honest evidence audit gives them a way to weight claims.
