# Research Notes: Epistemic Hedging and the Certainty/Evidentiality Spectrum in English

## Executive Summary

English possesses a rich, primarily lexical system for encoding how certain a speaker is about what they're saying and where they got their information. Unlike some languages that grammatically require speakers to mark their evidence source, English gives speakers *optional* but socially significant tools for calibrating their commitment to truth claims. This system operates along two key dimensions: **epistemic modality** (how certain you are) and **evidentiality** (how you know).

For autistic learners, this system presents a particular challenge and a particular opportunity. The challenge: many hedging expressions that *look* like uncertainty signals are actually social lubricants -- the speaker is quite certain but hedges to protect face, show humility, or follow social convention. The opportunity: once the system is understood as a *system* -- with learnable rules, predictable patterns, and identifiable functions -- it becomes navigable rather than mystifying. The deep autistic commitment to accuracy and honesty can be reframed not as incompatible with hedging, but as a strength that, when combined with hedging literacy, produces communication that is both truthful *and* socially effective.

Research consistently shows that autistic individuals have a measurable "preference for certainty and explicit communication" (PeerJ, 2022), linked to the cognitive trait of Intolerance of Uncertainty (IU). This isn't a deficit -- it's a legitimate cognitive style that values precision. But navigating a world where neurotypical speakers routinely encode social meaning through apparent uncertainty markers requires understanding the system from the inside out.

## 1. EPISTEMIC MODALITY: The Certainty Spectrum

### 1.1 Definition and Core Framework

Epistemic modality is a linguistic category concerned with the speaker's degree of certainty about a proposition -- their estimation of its truth value. As Lyons (1977) established, "epistemic modality is concerned with matters of knowledge, belief, or opinion rather than fact." [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epistemic_modality]

Rather than a binary certain/uncertain division, linguistics recognizes a **gradient continuum** running from absolute certainty to complete uncertainty, with multiple distinguishable points along the way. [Source: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0306457310000208]

Epistemic modality is realized through multiple linguistic mechanisms:
- **Modal auxiliary verbs**: may, might, must, could, should, will, would
- **Adverbs**: possibly, probably, certainly, perhaps, maybe, definitely, undoubtedly
- **Lexical verbs**: think, believe, suppose, guess, reckon, wonder, know, assume
- **Adjectives**: possible, probable, likely, certain, unlikely
- **Nouns**: possibility, probability, likelihood, certainty, chance
- **Phrases/clauses**: "it seems that," "it appears that," "as far as I know," "in my opinion"

[Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7138759/]

### 1.2 The Certainty Spectrum: Detailed Mapping

#### Weather Examples (The Classic Demonstration)

| Certainty Level | Expression | What It Signals |
|----------------|------------|-----------------|
| **Absolute certainty** | "It IS raining." | Direct knowledge; assertive commitment; no hedging |
| **Near-certainty (inference)** | "It must be raining." | Strong inference from evidence (e.g., people coming in with wet umbrellas) |
| **Expectation** | "It should be raining." | Based on prior knowledge (e.g., the forecast said rain) |
| **High probability** | "It's probably raining." | Good chance, but not guaranteed |
| **Moderate-high probability** | "It's likely raining." | More than 50% but not strongly committed |
| **Open possibility** | "It may be raining." | Genuine possibility being presented |
| **Weaker possibility** | "It might be raining." | Slightly less committed than "may" |
| **Even weaker** | "It could be raining." | One of several possibilities |
| **Speculative** | "Perhaps it's raining." | Throwing out an idea without strong backing |
| **Explicit uncertainty** | "I don't know if it's raining." | Openly marking the limits of one's knowledge |

#### Workplace Decision Examples

| Certainty Level | Expression | Hidden Social Function |
|----------------|------------|----------------------|
| **Absolute certainty** | "This deadline is Friday." | Stating a fact; no room for negotiation |
| **Strong inference** | "This must be the right approach." | Confident but acknowledging it's inference, not direct knowledge |
| **Expectation** | "This should work." | Based on experience/knowledge but leaving room for surprises |
| **High probability** | "We'll probably need more budget." | Strong belief but diplomatically phrased |
| **Moderate** | "We may need to reconsider." | OFTEN pragmatic hedging -- speaker may be quite sure but softening a disagreement |
| **Weaker possibility** | "We might want to push the deadline." | Classic workplace hedge -- "might want to" often means "I think we should" |
| **Speculative** | "Perhaps we could try a different approach." | Floating an idea while protecting both speaker's and listener's face |
| **Explicit uncertainty** | "I'm not sure what the best path is." | Can be genuinely epistemic OR can be a strategy to invite input |

#### Medical Situation Examples

| Certainty Level | Expression | Clinical Function |
|----------------|------------|------------------|
| **Diagnosis confirmed** | "You have strep throat." | Direct statement based on test results |
| **Strong clinical inference** | "This must be a bacterial infection." | Evidence strongly supports this conclusion |
| **Clinical expectation** | "This should respond to antibiotics." | Based on standard medical knowledge |
| **Probable diagnosis** | "It's probably just a virus." | High confidence, but hedged appropriately given diagnostic uncertainty |
| **Possible diagnosis** | "It may be stress-related." | One of several possibilities being considered |
| **Working hypothesis** | "It could be an allergy." | Offering a possibility without commitment |
| **Speculative** | "Perhaps the symptoms are connected." | Thinking aloud, inviting collaborative reasoning |
| **Open uncertainty** | "I'm not sure what's causing this yet." | Honest acknowledgment of current diagnostic limits |

Research on medical hedging identifies 10 distinct types of uncertainty language used by clinicians: numeric probabilistic statements ("there's an 80% chance"), qualitative probabilistic statements ("there's a good chance"), non-probabilistic uncertainty ("hard to say"), plausibility shields ("we expect"), emotion-based statements ("we're concerned"), attribution shields ("according to Dr. X"), adaptors ("sort of"), metaphors ("the chips are stacking up"), time references ("too soon to tell"), and contingency statements ("if we are lucky"). [Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3540426/]

Importantly, physicians' *explicit* expressions of uncertainty (like "I don't know") reduce patient-perceived competence and trust, while *implicit* expressions (hedging language) do not trigger the same negative response. This illustrates why the system exists: hedging allows uncertainty to be communicated without the social costs of baldly stating "I don't know." [Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8369117/]

#### Everyday Life Examples

| Certainty Level | Expression | Context |
|----------------|------------|---------|
| **Certain** | "The store closes at 9." | Direct knowledge |
| **Strong inference** | "She must be upset -- she left without saying goodbye." | Inferring from behavioral evidence |
| **Expectation** | "The bus should be here in five minutes." | Based on schedule knowledge |
| **Probable** | "He's probably stuck in traffic." | Likely explanation for lateness |
| **Possible** | "They may have forgotten." | Entertaining the possibility |
| **Weaker** | "It might be cheaper online." | Suggesting without checking |
| **Speculative** | "Maybe we should ask someone." | Floating an idea |
| **Uncertain** | "I have no idea where I put my keys." | Complete uncertainty |

### 1.3 Adverbs of Certainty: The Probability Scale

English adverbs of certainty form a well-defined scale from strongest to weakest:

**Highest certainty (near-100%)**:
- certainly, definitely, undoubtedly, absolutely, clearly, obviously, without doubt, unquestionably

**High certainty (~80-95%)**:
- probably, presumably, almost certainly, in all likelihood, no doubt

**Moderate certainty (~50-80%)**:
- likely, apparently, seemingly, evidently

**Low certainty (~20-50%)**:
- possibly, perhaps, maybe, conceivably

**Very low certainty (<20%)**:
- improbably, unlikely, doubtfully

"Possibly" and "perhaps" indicate something is *within the realm of feasibility* without indicating likelihood, while "probably" signals the speaker believes the outcome is *more likely than not*. [Source: https://learnenglish.britishcouncil.org/grammar/c1-grammar/modals-probability]

### 1.4 Epistemic Stance Markers in Conversation

Research on spoken English reveals specific patterns:

- **"I think"** is the most frequent epistemic stance marker in English conversation (Karkkäinen, 2003). It serves multiple functions: genuinely marking uncertainty, framing a personal perspective, and softening assertions. [Source: https://benjamins.com/catalog/pbns.115]

- **"I guess"** suggests *less* certainty than "I think" and often signals "softening" or "deciding in real time." [Source: https://files01.core.ac.uk/download/pdf/14926159.pdf]

- **"I believe"** occupies a middle ground -- it can signal genuine belief-based (rather than knowledge-based) certainty, or it can frame what follows as personal conviction rather than objective fact.

- **"I suppose"** signals reluctant or qualified acceptance -- "I suppose you're right" carries a flavor of "I can't argue against it, but I'm not enthusiastic."

- **"I reckon"** (more common in British/Australian English) is roughly equivalent to "I think" but with a slightly more informal, calculating tone.

- **"I wonder"** marks genuine uncertainty or introduces speculation -- it explicitly frames what follows as an open question in the speaker's mind.

Speakers tend to show consistent preferences for specific epistemic forms, developing personal "epistemic styles" that others come to associate with them. [Source: https://academic.oup.com/applij/article/38/5/613/2952204]

## 2. EVIDENTIALITY IN ENGLISH: Marking the Source of Knowledge

### 2.1 Overview: English as a Lexical-Evidential Language

Evidentiality is a linguistic category whose primary meaning is **source of information** -- it covers how information was acquired, independently of whether the speaker considers it true or false. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evidentiality]

A critical distinction: **evidentiality is not the same as epistemic modality.** Evidentiality marks *where you got the information*; epistemic modality marks *how sure you are about it*. These often correlate (direct evidence usually produces higher certainty) but they are separate dimensions.

English does NOT have grammatical evidentiality -- speakers are not *required* to mark their information source the way speakers of Turkish, Quechua, or Tibetan are. However, English has a rich **lexical** system of optional evidential marking that speakers deploy strategically. [Source: https://socialsci.libretexts.org/Bookshelves/Linguistics/Analyzing_Meaning_-_An_Introduction_to_Semantics_and_Pragmatics_(Kroeger)/17:_Evidentiality/17.03:_Evidentiality_and_epistemic_modality]

### 2.2 The Evidence Source Hierarchy

#### Tier 1: Direct Sensory Evidence (Strongest)

| Marker | Evidence Type | Example |
|--------|--------------|---------|
| "I saw..." | Visual perception | "I saw him leave at noon." |
| "I heard..." | Auditory perception | "I heard the crash from my office." |
| "I felt..." | Tactile perception | "I felt the building shake." |
| "I noticed..." | Attentive perception | "I noticed she was limping." |
| "I watched..." | Sustained visual | "I watched the whole thing unfold." |

Direct sensory evidence is the strongest form of evidential marking in English. When a speaker says "I saw it happen," they are claiming first-person perceptual access -- the strongest possible evidential basis.

#### Tier 2: Inference from Evidence (Strong)

| Marker | Evidence Type | Example |
|--------|--------------|---------|
| "It must be..." | Strong inference | "It must be cold outside -- everyone's wearing coats." |
| "It looks like..." | Visual inference | "It looks like they've already left." |
| "It sounds like..." | Auditory inference | "It sounds like you had a rough day." |
| "It seems..." | General inference | "It seems the project is behind schedule." |
| "It appears..." | Formal inference | "It appears that the data supports this conclusion." |
| "Evidently..." | Inference from visible evidence | "Evidently, they changed the policy." |
| "Apparently..." | Inference or hearsay | "Apparently the store is closing down." |

These perception verbs (seem, appear, look, sound, feel) have evolved in English from literal sensory meaning to evidential function. They now encode not just sensory experience but inference based on available evidence. When someone says "It looks like rain," they are not literally reporting what rain looks like -- they are marking an inference drawn from visual evidence (dark clouds, humidity, etc.). [Source: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/223510522_Auditory_evidentiality_in_English_and_German_The_case_of_perception_verbs]

**Note on "apparently"**: This adverb is ambiguous between inferential ("based on what I can see, apparently the meeting was cancelled") and hearsay ("apparently they're getting divorced" = someone told me). Context determines which reading applies. [Source: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/332326530_English_Evidential_-ly_Adverbs_in_Main_Clauses_A_Functional_Approach]

#### Tier 3: Hearsay/Reported Information (Moderate)

| Marker | Evidence Type | Example |
|--------|--------------|---------|
| "I heard that..." | Reported information | "I heard that they're restructuring the department." |
| "They say..." | General hearsay | "They say it's going to be a cold winter." |
| "According to..." | Attributed source | "According to the report, sales are up 15%." |
| "Reportedly..." | Journalistic hearsay | "The company reportedly plans to expand." |
| "Allegedly..." | Distancing hearsay | "He allegedly committed fraud." |
| "Supposedly..." | Skeptical hearsay | "Supposedly, it's the best restaurant in town." |
| "Someone told me..." | Specific hearsay | "Someone told me the meeting was moved." |
| "[Person] said that..." | Quotative | "Sarah said that she'd be late." |

Hearsay markers carry varying degrees of speaker commitment:
- "According to [source]" attributes responsibility elsewhere -- the speaker is reporting, not endorsing.
- "Reportedly" and "allegedly" actively *distance* the speaker from the claim. "Allegedly" in particular signals "this is what's been claimed, but I'm not vouching for it." These derive from past participles of speech-act verbs and most clearly signal that a claim has been reported by others. [Source: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/332326530_English_Evidential_-ly_Adverbs_in_Main_Clauses_A_Functional_Approach]
- "Supposedly" adds a layer of skepticism -- the speaker may doubt the reported information.
- "They say" signals common knowledge or folk wisdom without a specific source.

#### Tier 4: Assumption/Reasoning (Weaker)

| Marker | Evidence Type | Example |
|--------|--------------|---------|
| "I assume..." | Reasonable assumption | "I assume you've seen the email." |
| "I suppose..." | Tentative assumption | "I suppose it could wait until Monday." |
| "I expect..." | Experience-based assumption | "I expect the traffic will be bad." |
| "I figure..." | Informal reasoning | "I figure we should leave early." |
| "I imagine..." | Hypothetical reasoning | "I imagine she's not happy about it." |
| "Presumably..." | Logical assumption | "Presumably they've already been informed." |

#### Tier 5: Speculation (Weakest)

| Marker | Evidence Type | Example |
|--------|--------------|---------|
| "Maybe..." | Open speculation | "Maybe they got lost." |
| "Perhaps..." | Slightly more formal speculation | "Perhaps we should reconsider." |
| "I wonder if..." | Active speculation | "I wonder if the train is delayed." |
| "Conceivably..." | Remote possibility | "Conceivably, they could finish by Friday." |
| "For all I know..." | Epistemic humility | "For all I know, they've already solved it." |

### 2.3 The Crucial Difference: English's Optional Evidentiality

In languages with grammatical evidentiality (like Turkish, where the past tense has different forms for witnessed vs. non-witnessed events), speakers MUST mark their evidence source every time they make a statement. English speakers CHOOSE when to mark evidence source. This choice itself carries meaning:

- **Marking evidence source** often signals either: (a) the speaker wants credit for having direct evidence, (b) the speaker wants to disclaim responsibility for hearsay, or (c) the speaker is being epistemically careful/honest.
- **Not marking evidence source** often signals the speaker treats the information as established fact, common knowledge, or simply not worth qualifying.

This optionality is one reason the system is harder for learners -- there are no rigid rules about when you *must* use these markers, only social conventions about when you *should*.

## 3. THE PRAGMATIC vs. EPISTEMIC HEDGE DISTINCTION

### 3.1 The Core Problem: Two Different Systems Using the Same Words

This is perhaps the single most important insight for autistic learners:

**The same hedging expression can serve two completely different functions:**

1. **Epistemic hedge**: The speaker genuinely doesn't know and is honestly marking their uncertainty.
   - "I think the meeting is at 3." → The speaker is genuinely unsure of the time.

2. **Pragmatic/social hedge**: The speaker is quite certain but softens their statement for social reasons.
   - "I think you might be wrong about this." → The speaker is fairly sure the other person is wrong but wraps it in hedging to protect the other person's face.

As research confirms: "The use of epistemic markers is not [always] related to matters of knowledge or lack of it. [Epistemic modality] is the vehicle towards the achievement of the speaker's communicative needs. It is the linguistic realisation of negative as well as positive politeness strategies." [Source: https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1131486.pdf]

### 3.2 How to Tell the Difference: A Framework

**Contextual Clues for Identifying Epistemic vs. Pragmatic Hedging:**

| Factor | Epistemic (Genuine Uncertainty) | Pragmatic (Social Softener) |
|--------|-------------------------------|---------------------------|
| **Topic** | Factual matters (times, dates, quantities) | Opinions, evaluations, corrections |
| **Stakes** | Low social stakes | High social stakes (disagreement, criticism, requests) |
| **Speaker's access** | Speaker may lack direct knowledge | Speaker likely has direct knowledge or strong opinion |
| **Accompanying signals** | May check phone, look uncertain, ask follow-up | May maintain eye contact, continue firmly after the hedge |
| **Pattern** | Isolated hedging on a factual question | Systematic hedging when delivering potentially unwelcome content |
| **Replacement test** | Removing the hedge would change the informational content | Removing the hedge would change the social register but not the content |

**Examples Analyzed:**

"I think the train leaves at 4:15." → **Probably epistemic.** The speaker is genuinely uncertain about a factual matter. They'd check if they could.

"I think we might want to reconsider this approach." → **Probably pragmatic.** The speaker likely has a clear opinion that the approach is wrong. The hedging ("I think" + "might" + "want to") is a triple-layer social buffer. Decoded: "This approach is wrong and we should change it."

"I believe this is an important issue." → **Ambiguous.** Could be epistemic ("I'm not certain, but I believe...") or pragmatic-assertive ("In my considered view..."). Context and tone determine.

"I'm not sure that's entirely accurate." → **Almost certainly pragmatic.** This is a polished way of saying "That's wrong." The phrase "not entirely accurate" is itself a hedge on "inaccurate," and the "I'm not sure" adds another layer. The speaker is probably quite sure.

"Maybe we could try doing it differently." → **Probably pragmatic.** This is a suggestion dressed as speculation. The speaker likely has a specific idea of what "differently" means.

### 3.3 The Dual-Channel Model

The most useful framework for autistic learners: hedging operates on **two channels simultaneously**:

**Channel 1 -- Content/Information**: "Here is what I think/know about the world."
**Channel 2 -- Social/Relational**: "Here is how I position myself relative to you in saying this."

When someone says "I think you might be mistaken," the content channel transmits "You are mistaken" and the social channel transmits "I respect your right to hold your view, I'm not attacking you, and I'm leaving you room to save face."

Neurotypical listeners process both channels simultaneously and usually weight them appropriately. The hedging doesn't confuse them about the content because they extract the social meaning separately. For autistic listeners who process language more literally, the hedging on Channel 2 can obscure the message on Channel 1 -- making it genuinely unclear whether the speaker is uncertain or politely certain.

### 3.4 Common Pragmatic Hedges Decoded

| What They Say | What They Often Mean | Why They Hedge |
|---------------|---------------------|----------------|
| "I was just wondering if..." | "I want to ask you..." | Reduces the imposition of asking |
| "I might be wrong, but..." | "I'm fairly confident that..." | Pre-emptive face protection |
| "Correct me if I'm wrong..." | "I believe I'm right about this..." | Invites agreement while appearing humble |
| "Don't you think...?" | "I think..." | Frames own opinion as shared/obvious |
| "It's just my opinion, but..." | "This is what I believe" | Disclaims authority while asserting a view |
| "I don't know, maybe..." | "I have an idea..." | Softens the introduction of a suggestion |
| "I could be wrong about this..." | "I'm quite sure about this..." | Protects speaker's face if they ARE wrong |
| "Not to be rude, but..." | Awareness that what follows may be perceived as rude | Pre-emptive apology for directness |
| "With all due respect..." | Signals respectful disagreement (sometimes signals significant disagreement) | Face-saving prelude to contradiction |

## 4. THE SOCIAL RULES OF EPISTEMIC MARKING

### 4.1 When to Hedge Even When Certain

English social norms create situations where speakers are **expected** to hedge even when they're quite sure:

**a) When correcting someone:**
- Unhedged: "You're wrong. The capital of Australia is Canberra."
- Hedged: "Actually, I think the capital might be Canberra, not Sydney."
- Why: Blunt correction threatens the other person's "positive face" (their desire to be seen as competent). Hedging softens the threat.

**b) When you have expertise the listener lacks:**
- Unhedged: "Your code has a memory leak."
- Hedged: "It looks like there might be a memory leak here."
- Why: Expertise asymmetry can feel like a power imbalance. Hedging signals that you're collaborating, not lecturing.

**c) When offering opinions in group settings:**
- Unhedged: "This is the best solution."
- Hedged: "I think this might be a good approach."
- Why: Presenting opinions as certainties in collaborative settings can be perceived as domineering.

**d) When delivering bad news:**
- Unhedged: "Your proposal was rejected."
- Hedged: "Unfortunately, it seems like the committee didn't go with your proposal this time."
- Why: The hedge + the softeners ("unfortunately," "this time") buffer the emotional impact.

**e) When you're new or lower-status:**
- Unhedged: "We should change the whole system."
- Hedged: "I was wondering if it might be worth considering some changes."
- Why: Lower-status speakers face greater social penalties for appearing presumptuous.

### 4.2 When NOT to Hedge Even When Uncertain

**a) When safety is at stake:**
- Hedged (inappropriate): "There might be a fire in the building."
- Direct: "There's a fire. Everyone needs to evacuate now."
- Why: Uncertainty about exact nature of the threat shouldn't delay safety-critical communication.

**b) When giving clear instructions:**
- Hedged (inappropriate): "You might want to maybe take your medication."
- Direct: "Take your medication."
- Why: The audience needs clarity, not politeness.

**c) When signaling confidence is required:**
- Hedged (inappropriate): "I suppose I could probably do this job."
- Direct: "I'm confident I can do this job."
- Why: Job interviews, pitches, and leadership moments require projected certainty.

**d) When hedging would be misleading:**
- Hedged (inappropriate): "It's possible this product contains allergens." (when it definitely does)
- Direct: "This product contains allergens."
- Why: Hedging factual safety information is ethically wrong.

### 4.3 The Certainty-Humility Calibration

English social norms operate on an implicit rule: **appear slightly less certain than you actually are.** This is the "certainty-humility calibration."

Research confirms that audiences interpret confidence as competence. However, there's a social ceiling -- excessive confidence reads as arrogance, while insufficient confidence reads as incompetence. The optimal zone is slightly below actual certainty level. [Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10233055/]

A skilled candidate who hedges appropriately may be perceived as less competent than an overconfident candidate who doesn't hedge, precisely because "the skilled candidate's hedging and qualification reads as uncertainty rather than accuracy." This creates a systematic disadvantage for honest, calibrated communicators. [Source: https://morethandigital.info/en/paradox-of-confidence-overconfidence-and-under-confidence-explained/]

The calibration differs by context:
- **Academic/scientific discourse**: Heavy hedging expected and respected. "This suggests that..." is more appropriate than "This proves that..."
- **Business/corporate**: Moderate hedging. "I believe this will work" is better than either "this will definitely work" or "this might possibly work."
- **Emergency/military**: Minimal hedging. Clarity and decisiveness valued over humility.
- **Casual social**: Light hedging. Excessive hedging in casual conversation can read as anxious or evasive.

### 4.4 Expertise and Hedging Permissions

Experts are "allowed" to hedge less on their topic -- a doctor saying "this is pneumonia" carries authority that wouldn't be appropriate from a non-expert. Conversely, experts who hedge *more* than expected can signal either exceptional intellectual honesty or concerning uncertainty.

In academic writing, hedging is central -- "it expresses possibility rather than certainty and collegiality rather than presumption" (Hyland, 1998). Scientists who don't hedge sufficiently are seen as overstepping their evidence. But the same scientists speaking to the public often need to hedge *less* to avoid being misinterpreted as uncertain about well-established findings. [Source: https://benjamins.com/catalog/pbns.54]

### 4.5 Gender and Hedging

Research on gender and hedging is more nuanced than popular accounts suggest:

**The traditional claim**: Women hedge more than men, reflecting tentativeness and lower confidence. (Lakoff, 1975)

**The more accurate picture**: Holmes (1995) reinterpreted the data, arguing that women's use of hedges "expresses interpersonal warmth and not, as many researchers have maintained, linguistic tentativeness, while it is typically men who employ hedges to convey imprecision and incertitude." [Source: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1023/A:1025064205478]

**The current consensus**: Context matters more than gender. No main effects were found for speaker gender in controlled studies; rather, "contextual influences eclipsed the effects of gender." Recent corpus data suggest that men hedge just as much as women but use different linguistic forms -- for instance, men use "I guess" more often than women. The claim that "women hedge because they're less confident" is an oversimplification. [Source: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1023/A:1025064205478]

**Social pressure**: Women face greater pressure to hedge in professional settings, where directness from women may be perceived as aggressive. Men face greater pressure *not* to hedge, where hedging from men may be perceived as weak. These are social expectations, not inherent tendencies. [Source: https://fiveable.me/key-terms/introduction-gender-studies/hedging]

**Relevance for autistic individuals**: Autistic people of all genders may face a double bind -- their natural directness violates hedging norms, but learning to hedge may feel dishonest. Understanding that hedging serves different functions (not just uncertainty marking) can help resolve this tension.

## 5. HEDGING AND TRUTH: The Autistic Concern

### 5.1 The Central Question

Many autistic individuals grapple with a fundamental question: "If I'm 95% sure of something but I say 'I think it might be,' am I lying?"

This concern is deeply connected to the autistic communication profile. Research consistently finds that autistic people "say what they mean and speak their truth because that's how they interact with the world." Their communication style is characterized by "directness, clarity, and reduced reliance on social nuances -- a cognitive style intertwined with the perceived inclination towards truthfulness." [Source: https://neurolaunch.com/autism-direct-communication/]

This directness is a *strength*: "Precision, honesty, and clarity are their strengths. Many autistic individuals' directness, honesty, and logical clarity can offer fresh, valuable perspectives." [Source: https://neurolaunch.com/autism-direct-communication/]

But it creates friction: "Autistic people are so often called rude or disrespectful because our honesty makes people think we are intentionally being unpleasant." [Source: https://autisticnick.com/why-are-autistic-people-so-honest]

### 5.2 Reframing: Hedging as Multi-Channel Communication, Not Dishonesty

The resolution to the "is hedging dishonest?" question requires understanding that communication transmits on multiple channels:

**Hedging is not lying about your certainty level. It is communicating on two channels simultaneously:**

- **Content channel**: "Here's what I think is true."
- **Social channel**: "I respect your autonomy and right to evaluate this independently."

When a speaker says "I think the report might need some revisions," they're not lying about their certainty. They're saying:
- Content: "The report needs revisions" (this is what they believe)
- Social: "I'm framing this as my perspective, not as an absolute decree, because I value your agency"

This is analogous to how "please" doesn't mean you're uncertain about whether you want something -- "Please pass the salt" doesn't mean "I'm only 60% sure I want salt." "Please" operates on the social channel while the content channel is perfectly clear.

### 5.3 The Epistemic Honesty Reframe

For autistic learners, a useful reframe: **True epistemic honesty includes being honest about the SOCIAL CONTEXT of your communication, not just the propositional content.**

If you know something is true AND you know that stating it bluntly will damage a relationship, then honestly communicating the *full situation* requires acknowledging both the truth AND the social reality. Hedging is a tool for doing this.

Consider: "I think there might be an error in line 47" vs. "There's an error in line 47."

Both convey the same factual information. The first additionally conveys: "I'm approaching this collaboratively." If the speaker genuinely intends a collaborative approach, the hedge is honest -- it accurately represents their communicative intent, not just the propositional content.

### 5.4 The Cost of Over-Directness

Research on autistic communication and social outcomes shows genuine costs:

"Being radically blunt all the time tends to alienate people, which serves to be offending to many and often burns bridges in terms of relationships." [Source: https://myautismmind.com/being-real-without-offending/]

"The directness that makes autistic people such trustworthy communicators can cause friction in settings where people expect polite evasion." [Source: https://wellwisp.com/are-autistic-people-blunt/]

This isn't about "fixing" autistic communication. It's about giving autistic individuals the *choice* to deploy hedging strategically when they determine the social benefits outweigh the cost of appearing less certain than they are. "Selectively withholding or softening blunt truths isn't inherently dishonest -- it's a compassionate social skill." [Source: https://wellwisp.com/are-autistic-people-blunt/]

### 5.5 The Masking Question

An important caveat: there's a difference between **learning pragmatic hedging as a tool** and **masking as a survival strategy**.

Research on autistic camouflaging shows that sustained social masking -- "constantly monitoring one's own behaviors to show a non-autistic persona to others" -- leads to "exhaustion, anxiety, depression, and difficulties in forming an authentic self-concept." [Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8814950/]

The goal isn't to mask. The goal is to **understand the system** so that:
1. You can accurately decode others' hedging (parsing Channel 1 from Channel 2)
2. You can choose to deploy hedging when *you* decide it serves your goals
3. You can make informed decisions about when directness is more important than social lubrication
4. The system becomes *legible* rather than mysterious

Understanding hedging as a learnable system gives autistic individuals more agency, not less.

## 6. MODAL VERBS AS AN EPISTEMIC SYSTEM

### 6.1 The Dual Nature of Modal Verbs

Every English modal verb has (at least) two meanings -- a **deontic** meaning (about obligations, permissions, abilities) and an **epistemic** meaning (about certainty and possibility). The epistemic meanings developed historically from the deontic ones. [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modal_verb]

This dual nature is a major source of confusion. "You must leave" (deontic = obligation) vs. "It must be cold outside" (epistemic = strong inference) use the same word with fundamentally different meanings.

### 6.2 Modal Verb Epistemic Values

| Modal | Epistemic Meaning | Certainty Level | Example (Epistemic) | Compare: Deontic Meaning |
|-------|-------------------|----------------|---------------------|-------------------------|
| **must** | Strong inference/near-certainty | ~95% | "He must be tired." (I infer this from evidence) | "You must finish by Friday." (obligation) |
| **will** | Confident prediction | ~90% | "That will be John at the door." | "You will report to me." (command) |
| **should** | Reasonable expectation | ~80% | "The package should arrive today." | "You should apologize." (advice/moral obligation) |
| **would** | Tentative prediction/hypothetical | ~70% | "That would explain it." | "Would you help me?" (polite request) |
| **can** | Theoretical possibility | Variable | "This can happen in winter." | "You can leave now." (permission) |
| **could** | Weaker possibility | ~40% | "It could be true." | "Could you help?" (polite request) |
| **may** | Open possibility | ~40% | "It may rain later." | "You may leave." (permission) |
| **might** | Weaker possibility | ~30% | "She might be at home." | (rare deontic use) |

**Note on the percentage estimates**: These are approximate and contextual. The same modal can signal different certainty levels in different contexts. "Must" in "He must be the new teacher" (I see him in a classroom with textbooks) might be 95%, but "must" in "There must be a solution" (hopeful rather than evidential) might be more aspirational than certain.

### 6.3 The Past-Tense Distance Effect

One of the most elegant patterns in English hedging: **past-tense forms of modals create psychological distance that softens the illocutionary force.** This doesn't mark past time -- it marks social/hypothetical distance.

| Present Form (More Direct) | Past Form (More Hedged) | Distance Created |
|---------------------------|------------------------|-----------------|
| "Can you help?" | "Could you help?" | Acknowledges the listener may not be able/willing |
| "Will you do this?" | "Would you do this?" | Makes compliance feel optional |
| "Shall we go?" | "Should we go?" | Adds tentativeness to the suggestion |
| "I want to ask you..." | "I wanted to ask you..." | Creates temporal distance from the imposition |
| "I think..." | "I was thinking..." | Frames the thought as tentative/in-progress |

The mechanism: "Using 'could' in a request doesn't ask about past ability -- it creates psychological distance that softens the request." The past-tense form "pushes the dependency structure slightly backward, inviting cooperation rather than commanding it." [Source: https://www.allinthedifference.com/difference-between-can-you-and-could-you/]

**The politeness escalation in requests:**

1. "Help me." (imperative -- minimal hedging)
2. "Can you help me?" (ability framing -- slight hedge)
3. "Could you help me?" (past-tense distance -- moderate hedge)
4. "Would you be able to help me?" (double distance: would + ability framing)
5. "I was wondering if you might be able to help me?" (maximum distance: past progressive + wonder + might + ability)
6. "I don't suppose there's any chance you could help me?" (negative framing + suppose + chance + could -- extreme hedge, almost self-deprecating)

Each step adds another layer of hedging, increasing the listener's perceived freedom to refuse. In neurotypical communication, the "right" level depends on the imposition size, the relationship closeness, and the power differential. [Source: https://continuingstudies.uvic.ca/elc/studyzone/330/grammar/330-modals-polite]

### 6.4 Stacking Modal Hedges

English allows multiple hedges to be stacked, creating intensified softening:

- "I think it might possibly be a good idea." (three hedges: I think + might + possibly)
- "Perhaps we could maybe consider..." (three hedges: perhaps + could + maybe)
- "I was wondering if it might be worth looking into..." (multiple hedges: past progressive + wondering + might + be worth)

In general, more hedges = more politeness/tentativeness. But there's a point of diminishing returns where excessive hedging reads as anxious, evasive, or passive-aggressive.

## 7. RESEARCH ON AUTISTIC EPISTEMIC MARKING

### 7.1 The Preference for Certainty

A key 2022 study (Bury et al., published in PeerJ) directly investigated the relationship between autism and epistemic preferences in conversation. The study compared 102 autistic adults and 109 non-autistic adults on tests of implied meaning and grammaticality.

**Key findings:**
- Accuracy and confidence were "both reduced to a similar extent on the test of implied meaning in the autistic group compared to the control group, although these were only subtle differences."
- This pattern was *specific to inference-making* -- the groups did not differ on the grammar test.
- "Individuals who reported discomfort with uncertainty were less likely to make confident, normative inferences in conversation and more likely to report more day-to-day communication difficulties."
- A "preference for certainty and explicit communication" was identified as a key factor, linked to the cognitive trait of Intolerance of Uncertainty (IU).

[Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8919847/]

The researchers specifically concluded that this "preference for certainty and explicit communication commonly occurs in autistic people, and this trait may be a critical factor in the communication difficulties experienced by autistic people, as communicative situations often involve ambiguity, uncertainty and implied meanings." [Source: https://peerj.com/articles/10398/]

### 7.2 Intolerance of Uncertainty and Dichotomous Thinking

A 2023 study in *Scientific Reports* found a significant relationship between autistic traits, intolerance of uncertainty, and dichotomous (black-and-white) thinking:

"Autistic traits can result in dichotomous (black-and-white) thinking through intolerance of uncertainty." The researchers found "a significant indirect effect of autistic traits on dichotomous thinking mediated by intolerance of uncertainty." [Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10462756/]

The proposed mechanism: "To circumvent the discomfort of uncertainty and not end up feeling 'stuck,' autistic individuals may tend to attribute a binary outcome to uncertain states of truth and thereby end up at a 'black or white' outcome that feels certain, thereby reducing their anxiety. Black-and-white thinking may therefore be a safety behavior, conscious or not, used by autistic individuals to reduce uncertainty and associated anxiety in the short term." [Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10462756/]

**Relevance to epistemic hedging**: This research suggests that the entire gradient certainty spectrum may be cognitively uncomfortable for some autistic individuals. Rather than sitting with "maybe it's raining, ~40% chance," the cognitive pull is toward "it either IS raining or it ISN'T" -- collapsing the probability distribution into a binary. Understanding hedging as a *system* may help by providing structured frameworks for the gradient that make it more concrete and navigable.

A 2025 study further confirmed that "intolerance of uncertainty mediates the relationship between autistic traits and a propensity for deliberation," suggesting that the discomfort with uncertainty drives more extensive (and sometimes exhausting) deliberative reasoning. [Source: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10803-025-06987-6]

### 7.3 The Double Empathy Problem and Hedging

The "double empathy problem" (Milton, 2012) provides critical framing: communication breakdowns between autistic and non-autistic people are bidirectional. It's not that autistic people "can't" understand hedging -- it's that the hedging system was developed by and for neurotypical communication norms.

"Autistic people were having trouble understanding neurotypical people in some contexts, due to differences in experiences, interaction/communication style, and social cognition between the two groups." [Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_empathy_problem]

Studies show that "autistic adults generally perform better in empathy, rapport, and communication effectiveness when paired with other autistic adults" -- suggesting that the problem is one of cross-neurotype translation, not deficit. [Source: https://www.thetransmitter.org/spectrum/double-empathy-explained/]

When two autistic people communicate, the directness that neurotypical norms would call "blunt" functions perfectly well -- both parties receive clear information without the need for social hedging. The "problem" with hedging only arises in cross-neurotype communication.

### 7.4 Pragmatic Language Differences

Research on autistic pragmatic language confirms specific patterns relevant to hedging:

- Autistic individuals were "more likely than non-autistic people to give less confident and non-normative responses on the Implicature Comprehension Test, suggesting that neurotypical and autistic people form inferences in conversation in different ways." [Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8919847/]

- The autistic communication profile reflects "a legitimate cognitive preference for explicitness and certainty rather than a pragmatic impairment per se." [Source: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/jtsb.12377]

- Recent AI-based analysis of language patterns found that pragmatic language variability is "central to autism and genetically related phenotypes," suggesting these patterns have a deep neurological basis. [Source: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/13623613241304488]

### 7.5 Potential Patterns in Autistic Epistemic Marking

While direct research on autistic *production* of epistemic markers is limited, the existing evidence suggests several patterns:

**Potential for over-certainty**: The preference for binary outcomes may lead some autistic individuals to express certainty where hedging would be socially expected. Saying "that's wrong" when neurotypical norms call for "I'm not sure that's quite right" is perceived as blunt or rude, even when the speaker is factually correct.

**Potential for over-uncertainty**: Paradoxically, the intolerance of uncertainty combined with awareness of one's own knowledge limits may lead some autistic individuals to hedge *more* than neurotypical speakers on topics where they actually have strong evidence -- refusing to commit to "probably" when they'd really need "definitely" for comfort.

**Literal interpretation of others' hedges**: When a colleague says "I think we might want to reconsider," the autistic listener may interpret this as genuine uncertainty rather than recognizing it as a pragmatic softener for "we need to change course."

**Binary hedging**: Rather than using the full gradient (definitely → probably → maybe → possibly), some autistic individuals may tend toward binary expressions: either "I know" or "I don't know," with less use of the intermediate hedging vocabulary.

## 8. LINGUISTIC TAXONOMY: Types of Hedges (Lakoff, Prince et al., Hyland)

### 8.1 Lakoff's Original Hedges (1972)

George Lakoff introduced the term "hedge" to describe words "whose job it is to make things fuzzier." His original examples include: sort of, kind of, loosely speaking, more or less, roughly, pretty (much), relatively, somewhat, rather, mostly, technically, strictly speaking, essentially, in essence, basically, principally, particularly, largely. [Source: https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Hedges:-A-study-in-meaning-criteria-and-the-logic-Lakoff/a717c0779d452a0176b1253e10eb4784a0d22bfe]

Lakoff drew on fuzzy set theory: some categories don't have clear borders, and hedges are the tools that handle "graded membership." A penguin is "sort of a bird" (atypical member); a robin is a bird (prototypical member). Hedges let speakers navigate this fuzziness.

### 8.2 Prince et al.'s Approximators and Shields (1982)

Prince, Frader, and Bosk distinguished two fundamental types:

**Approximators**: Affect the propositional content itself -- they change *what* is being said.
- *Adaptors*: "sort of," "kind of," "somewhat," "a little bit" -- indicate deviation from prototype
- *Rounders*: "about," "approximately," "roughly," "around" -- indicate numerical imprecision

**Shields**: Affect the speaker's commitment -- they change *how committed the speaker is* to what's being said.
- *Plausibility shields*: "I think," "I believe," "probably," "as far as I can tell" -- indicate speaker uncertainty
- *Attribution shields*: "according to X," "reportedly," "they say" -- shift responsibility to another source

[Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9513629/]

### 8.3 Hyland's Taxonomy (1998)

Ken Hyland developed the most widely-used taxonomy for academic hedging:

**By Form:**
1. Modal verbs (may, might, could, would)
2. Epistemic lexical verbs (suggest, indicate, appear, seem, assume, believe)
3. Epistemic adjectives (possible, probable, likely, uncertain)
4. Epistemic adverbs (possibly, probably, perhaps, apparently, seemingly)
5. Epistemic nouns (possibility, assumption, probability, estimate)

**By Function:**
1. **Content-oriented hedges**: Concern the accuracy and reliability of propositions
   - "The results suggest that..." (hedging the claim itself)
2. **Reader-oriented hedges**: Concern the relationship with the audience
   - "One might argue that..." (inviting the reader into the reasoning)

Hyland emphasizes that hedging in academic writing is NOT a sign of weakness -- it "allows writers to express tentativeness, providing a safeguard against overstated claims and promoting a balanced, credible tone." [Source: https://benjamins.com/catalog/pbns.54]

### 8.4 Boosters: The Opposite of Hedges

The complement of hedges: **boosters** are expressions that increase commitment to a claim:

- certainly, definitely, clearly, obviously, of course, undoubtedly, without question
- "It is clear that..." / "The evidence demonstrates..." / "We can confidently state..."
- "In fact..." / "Indeed..." / "Actually..."

Boosters and hedges form a system -- the choice between them is always relative to context. A sentence without explicit hedging or boosting carries a "default" commitment level.

## 9. Honesty Signals and Meta-Commentary

### 9.1 The "To Be Honest" Paradox

English has a set of expressions that explicitly signal honesty or truthfulness:
- "To be honest..."
- "Honestly..."
- "Frankly..."
- "Truthfully..."
- "In all candor..."

These create a paradox: "It's a long-standing joke that anytime someone begins a statement with 'Honestly,' or its equivalent, they're about to lie." While this isn't literally true, these markers do carry a specific pragmatic function -- they signal that what follows may violate social expectations. "To be honest, I didn't like it" is more common than "To be honest, I enjoyed it" because the honesty marker anticipates that the honest opinion might be unwelcome. [Source: https://www.npr.org/2025/11/07/nx-s1-5597966/honestly-internet-culture]

The usage of "honestly" has increased dramatically in recent decades, suggesting it's evolving from a literal honesty marker to a general discourse marker for emphasis or contrast.

### 9.2 Epistemic Vigilance

The cognitive science concept of "epistemic vigilance" (Sperber et al., 2010) describes humans' built-in system for evaluating the reliability of communicated information. We monitor:
- **Source reliability**: Is this person generally trustworthy? Knowledgeable?
- **Content plausibility**: Does this claim fit with what I already know?
- **Manner cues**: Does the speaker's hedging/boosting match the expected confidence level?

Hedging interacts with epistemic vigilance: appropriate hedging signals intellectual honesty and calibration, which *increases* perceived trustworthiness. Over-hedging signals incompetence or evasiveness. Under-hedging signals overconfidence or deception. [Source: https://www.dan.sperber.fr/wp-content/uploads/2010_clement-et-al_epistemic-vigilance.pdf]

## Research Gaps

1. **Direct studies of autistic epistemic marker *production***: Most research focuses on autistic comprehension of hedges/implicatures, not on how autistic individuals *produce* epistemic markers. There is a need for studies examining whether autistic adults hedge differently (in frequency, type, or context) from neurotypical adults.

2. **Cross-cultural hedging and autism**: Nearly all research is in English-speaking contexts. How do autistic individuals in cultures with different hedging norms (e.g., Japanese high-context communication vs. Dutch direct communication) experience the epistemic system?

3. **Hedging strategies explicitly taught to autistic adults**: While social skills programs sometimes address "softening language," there appears to be limited research on teaching the *underlying system* of epistemic modality and evidentiality in a way that respects autistic cognitive styles.

4. **Longitudinal outcomes**: Does explicit knowledge of the hedging system (as a learnable pattern) reduce the masking burden for autistic individuals compared to learning hedging through pure behavioral imitation?

5. **The relationship between alexithymia, interoceptive awareness, and epistemic marking**: Some autistic individuals have difficulty identifying their own internal states -- this could affect their ability to calibrate certainty, since "how sure am I?" is itself an internal state that must be accessed.

6. **Digital communication and hedging**: How does text-based communication (where tone of voice and facial expression are absent) change the hedging landscape for autistic communicators? Emojis, "lol," and "haha" may serve as digital hedges with their own rules.
