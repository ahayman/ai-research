# Prosodic Pragmatics: How Stress Placement Changes Meaning in English

## Comprehensive Worked Examples for Pattern-Based Learners

**Purpose:** A practical reference mapping stress patterns to meaning changes across sentence types and contexts. Designed for autistic individuals who benefit from explicit, rule-based explanations of pragmatic phenomena that neurotypical speakers process intuitively.

**Key Insight for Autistic Readers:** Research confirms that the formal, rule-based functions of prosody are more learnable for autistic individuals than the intuitive, context-dependent functions (Grice, 2023). This document treats stress-meaning relationships as *systematic rules with predictable outputs*---because that is exactly what they are. Every stress shift follows the same underlying logic: **the stressed word is the answer; the alternatives it excludes are the meaning.**

---

## The Core Rule (Memorize This)

> **Whatever word you stress is the word you are *contrasting* with alternatives.**

When you stress a word, you are implicitly saying: "This, *not something else*." The meaning change comes from *what alternatives the listener fills in*.

| Stress Falls On | What It Does | The Listener Thinks |
|---|---|---|
| A noun | Contrasts that noun with other nouns | "Not X, but Y" |
| A verb | Contrasts that verb with other verbs | "Not did-A, but did-B" |
| A pronoun | Contrasts that person with other people | "Not him, but her" |
| An adverb/adjective | Contrasts that quality with other qualities | "Not quickly, but slowly" |
| A negation word | Emphasizes the negation itself | "This absolutely did NOT happen" |
| A hedge word | Can neutralize the hedge (creates sarcasm) | "I don't actually think/believe this" |

**The Question-Answer Test:** For any stressed word, ask: "What question is this sentence answering?" The stressed word is always the answer to that implicit question.

## 1. THE SEVEN-MEANINGS SENTENCE

### Full Analysis: "I never said she stole my money"

This sentence has seven words. Stressing each one produces a distinct meaning. This is the single most famous example in English prosody.

---

#### Variant 1: "**I** never said she stole my money"

| Element | Value |
|---|---|
| **Stressed word** | I |
| **Alternatives activated** | Someone else (he, she, they, my boss, your friend...) |
| **Resulting meaning** | Someone else said it, not me. I am being falsely credited with this claim. |
| **Implicit question answered** | "Who said she stole your money?" |
| **Real-world context** | You're in a meeting. Your coworker tells the boss, "Aaron said the client stole our budget." You turn to the boss and say: "**I** never said she stole my money." You're correcting who made the claim. You're not denying the claim itself---just that you were the one who made it. |

---

#### Variant 2: "I **never** said she stole my money"

| Element | Value |
|---|---|
| **Stressed word** | never |
| **Alternatives activated** | sometimes, once, at some point (any non-zero frequency) |
| **Resulting meaning** | This claim was never made. Not once. Not ever. Total denial of the event of saying it. |
| **Implicit question answered** | "Did you ever say she stole your money?" |
| **Real-world context** | Someone accuses you of having said something at a party last week. You're insisting it never happened at all: "I **never** said she stole my money. Not once. You're making that up." This is the strongest denial variant---it attacks the truthfulness of the accusation. |

---

#### Variant 3: "I never **said** she stole my money"

| Element | Value |
|---|---|
| **Stressed word** | said |
| **Alternatives activated** | implied, suggested, hinted, wrote, thought, texted |
| **Resulting meaning** | I didn't *say* it out loud---but I may have communicated it another way. Maybe I implied it. Maybe I wrote it. Maybe I just thought it. The denial is specifically about the speech act of *saying*. |
| **Implicit question answered** | "How did you communicate that she stole your money?" |
| **Real-world context** | Your partner confronts you: "You told everyone she's a thief." You reply: "I never **said** she stole my money." The careful listener notices you're only denying the *saying*---not the *believing* or *implying*. This variant is often used as a technically-true evasion. You did communicate it, just not by literally saying it. |

**Pattern note:** This is the most deceptive variant. In legal and political contexts, people exploit the gap between "said" and "communicated" to tell technical truths while misleading. "I never **said** that" can mean "I strongly implied it but maintained deniability."

---

#### Variant 4: "I never said **she** stole my money"

| Element | Value |
|---|---|
| **Stressed word** | she |
| **Alternatives activated** | he, they, someone else, the other person |
| **Resulting meaning** | I said *someone* stole my money, but I didn't say it was *her*. You're accusing me of blaming the wrong person. |
| **Implicit question answered** | "Who did you say stole your money?" |
| **Real-world context** | After a break-in, you told police that money was stolen. Your neighbor asks, "Why did you tell the police Sarah stole your money?" You say: "I never said **she** stole my money"---meaning you reported a theft, but you never named Sarah as the thief. |

---

#### Variant 5: "I never said she **stole** my money"

| Element | Value |
|---|---|
| **Stressed word** | stole |
| **Alternatives activated** | borrowed, lost, misplaced, spent, moved, took |
| **Resulting meaning** | She did something with my money, but "stole" is too strong a word. Maybe she borrowed it. Maybe she lost it. I'm denying the *characterization* of the action, not the action itself. |
| **Implicit question answered** | "What did you say she did with your money?" |
| **Real-world context** | Your roommate is upset because your other roommate heard you "accused her of stealing." You say: "I never said she **stole** my money. I said she borrowed it and forgot to pay me back. There's a difference." You're correcting the verb, not the basic facts. |

---

#### Variant 6: "I never said she stole **my** money"

| Element | Value |
|---|---|
| **Stressed word** | my |
| **Alternatives activated** | your, his, her, their, the company's |
| **Resulting meaning** | She stole money, yes. But it wasn't *my* money. It was someone else's. |
| **Implicit question answered** | "Whose money did you say she stole?" |
| **Real-world context** | In a workplace investigation, a colleague says, "You reported that she stole your department's budget." You correct: "I never said she stole **my** money. I said she stole the client's money. Get the facts straight." You're correcting whose money was involved. |

---

#### Variant 7: "I never said she stole my **money**"

| Element | Value |
|---|---|
| **Stressed word** | money |
| **Alternatives activated** | jewelry, car, idea, lunch, phone, identity |
| **Resulting meaning** | She stole something of mine, but it wasn't money. It was something else. |
| **Implicit question answered** | "What did you say she stole?" |
| **Real-world context** | Your friend says, "I heard you told people she took your cash." You reply: "I never said she stole my **money**. I said she stole my *idea*. I pitched that concept and she presented it as her own." |

---

### The Seven-Meanings Pattern Summary

| # | Stressed Word | Category Contrasted | Core Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **I** | Who made the claim | Someone else said it |
| 2 | **never** | Whether it happened | The claim was never made |
| 3 | **said** | How it was communicated | I didn't say it (but maybe implied it) |
| 4 | **she** | Who was accused | I accused someone else |
| 5 | **stole** | What action occurred | She did something else with it |
| 6 | **my** | Whose property | It was someone else's |
| 7 | **money** | What was taken | She took something else |

**The Generalization:** Each word belongs to a *semantic category* (person, action, object, possession, etc.). Stressing it activates alternatives *within that category* while leaving everything else as assumed background.

## 2. STRESS IN QUESTIONS: How Stress Changes What a Question Asks

### The Rule for Questions

In a question, **the stressed word narrows what information you're seeking**. Default stress (on the wh-word or the last content word) asks a broad, open question. Moving stress to a different word presupposes certain facts and asks about one specific element.

---

### Question Set 1: "Did you do this?"

| Variant | Stress | What it actually asks | What it presupposes | Tone/Attitude |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| "Did **YOU** do this?" | YOU | Asks about the *person* | Someone did this. Was it you specifically? | Accusatory, singling out |
| "Did you **DO** this?" | DO | Asks about the *action* | You were involved somehow. But did you actually *do* it (vs. watch, plan, supervise)? | Clarifying the nature of involvement |
| "Did you do **THIS**?" | THIS | Asks about the *object/act* | You did something. Was it specifically this thing? | Checking specifics, possibly incredulous |

**Worked context:**

A teacher discovers graffiti on a desk.
- "Did **YOU** do this?" --- asked to one specific student, singling them out from the class.
- "Did you **DO** this?" --- the student was seen near the desk. "Were you the one who actually drew it, or were you just standing there?"
- "Did you do **THIS**?" --- pointing at the desk. The student already admitted to drawing on something. "Was it *this* desk specifically?"

---

### Question Set 2: "Where are you going?"

| Variant | Stress | What it actually asks | What it presupposes | Tone/Attitude |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| "**WHERE** are you going?" | WHERE | Broad, default question about destination | You are going somewhere. | Neutral curiosity |
| "Where are **YOU** going?" | YOU | Asks why *this person* is leaving | Others might be staying. Why are you the one going? | Surprised or challenging |
| "Where are you **GOING**?" | GOING | Questions the act of departure itself | You appear to be leaving. Emphasizes the going itself. | Can convey "Should you really be leaving?" |

**Worked context:**

It's 3pm at the office.
- "**WHERE** are you going?" --- casual. Someone grabbed their coat, you're curious about the destination.
- "Where are **YOU** going?" --- implies others are staying. "Everyone else is working late but you're heading out?"
- "Where are you **GOING**?" --- stressing "going" suggests you question whether they should be leaving at all. "We're in the middle of a crisis and you're *going*?"

---

### Question Set 3: "What did she say?"

| Variant | Stress | What it actually asks | What it presupposes | Tone/Attitude |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| "**WHAT** did she say?" | WHAT | Default: asks about the content | She said something. What was it? | Neutral curiosity |
| "What did **SHE** say?" | SHE | Asks specifically about *her* response | Others have already weighed in. What was *her* take? | Isolating one person's input from others |
| "What did she **SAY**?" | SAY | Questions the act of saying itself | Something happened in the conversation. Did she actually *say* something (vs. stay silent, imply, gesture)? | Surprised she spoke, or doubting she spoke clearly |

**Worked context:**

After a group meeting where a decision was debated:
- "**WHAT** did she say?" --- "I missed the meeting. Fill me in on her comments."
- "What did **SHE** say?" --- "I've heard what Tom and Lisa think. What was *her* position specifically?" Or: "She's usually quiet---what was *her* take?"
- "What did she **SAY**?" --- "She was mumbling and I couldn't make it out. Did she actually articulate words?" Or: "She seemed to disagree but only made faces---did she actually *say* anything?"

---

### Question Set 4: "Why did you leave?"

| Variant | Stress | What it actually asks | What it presupposes | Tone/Attitude |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| "**WHY** did you leave?" | WHY | Default: asks for the reason | You left. What was the reason? | Neutral to concerned |
| "Why did **YOU** leave?" | YOU | Asks why *you specifically* left | Others stayed. What made you the one who left? | Singling out, possibly accusatory |
| "Why did you **LEAVE**?" | LEAVE | Questions the specific action of leaving | You could have done something else (stayed, spoken up, complained). Why was *leaving* your choice? | Challenging the decision itself |

**Worked context:**

After someone walked out of a dinner party:
- "**WHY** did you leave?" --- "What happened? Was something wrong? Give me the reason."
- "Why did **YOU** leave?" --- "Everyone else stayed and dealt with the awkward situation. Why did *you* bail while the rest of us stuck it out?"
- "Why did you **LEAVE**?" --- "You could have said something to the host, or moved to another room, or just ignored it. But you chose to *leave entirely*. Why that extreme response?"

---

### Question Set 5: "How did that happen?"

| Variant | Stress | What it actually asks | What it presupposes | Tone/Attitude |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| "**HOW** did that happen?" | HOW | Default: asks about the mechanism/process | Something happened. By what process? | Neutral curiosity |
| "How did **THAT** happen?" | THAT | Expresses disbelief at what happened | Something specific occurred. You find it surprising or hard to believe. | Incredulity, shock |
| "How did that **HAPPEN**?" | HAPPEN | Questions that it happened at all | The event itself seems impossible or unexpected. | Deep disbelief, "this shouldn't have been possible" |

**Worked context:**

A coworker's code deleted the production database:
- "**HOW** did that happen?" --- "Walk me through the technical steps. What went wrong in the process?"
- "How did **THAT** happen?" --- "Of all the things that could go wrong... *the production database*? Really?" Focus on the severity of what happened.
- "How did that **HAPPEN**?" --- "We have safeguards, backups, permissions, review processes. It should have been impossible for this to *happen*." Questions the fundamental possibility.

---

### Question Set 6 (Bonus): "When did you decide?"

| Variant | Stress | What it actually asks |
|---|---|---|
| "**WHEN** did you decide?" | WHEN | Default: asks about timing |
| "When did **YOU** decide?" | YOU | "Was this your decision to make? Who gave you the authority?" |
| "When did you **DECIDE**?" | DECIDE | "Have you actually decided, or are you still deliberating?" |

---

### The Question Stress Pattern (Generalization)

| Where Stress Falls | What Changes |
|---|---|
| On the **wh-word** | Default, broad question. No special presuppositions. |
| On a **noun/pronoun** | Narrows focus to that specific entity. "Why this one and not others?" |
| On the **verb** | Questions the nature or reality of the action itself. |
| On a **modifier/determiner** | Questions the specific quality or instance. |

## 3. STRESS IN RESPONSES: How Stress Determines What You're Correcting

### The Rule for Responses

When you answer a question and stress a particular word, **the stressed word is the correction**. Everything unstressed is accepted as given. The listener's mental model gets updated *only at the stressed point*.

---

### Worked Example: "Did John eat the cake?"

| Response | Stressed Word | What's Being Corrected | What's Accepted As True |
|---|---|---|---|
| "No, **MARY** ate the cake" | MARY | The person (not John) | Someone ate the cake |
| "No, John **BAKED** the cake" | BAKED | The action (not eating) | John did something with the cake |
| "No, John ate the **PIE**" | PIE | The object (not the cake) | John ate something |
| "No, John ate **A** cake" | A | The determiner (not THE cake---it was a different, unspecified one) | John ate cake |

---

### Worked Example: "Did Sarah drive to the store?"

| Response | Stressed Word | What's Being Corrected | What's Accepted |
|---|---|---|---|
| "No, **TOM** drove to the store" | TOM | Wrong person | Someone drove to the store |
| "No, Sarah **WALKED** to the store" | WALKED | Wrong method | Sarah went to the store |
| "No, Sarah drove to the **BANK**" | BANK | Wrong destination | Sarah drove somewhere |
| "No, Sarah drove **FROM** the store" | FROM | Wrong direction | Sarah and the store were involved in a driving event |

---

### Worked Example: "Is the meeting on Thursday at 3?"

| Response | Stressed Word | What's Being Corrected |
|---|---|---|
| "No, it's on **TUESDAY** at 3" | TUESDAY | Wrong day (time is correct) |
| "No, it's on Thursday at **4**" | 4 | Wrong time (day is correct) |
| "No, **THE CALL** is on Thursday at 3" | THE CALL | Wrong event (day and time are correct for a different event) |
| "No, it's on Thursday at 3 **AM**" | AM | Technically correct but you got the am/pm wrong |

---

### Worked Example: "You told me to send it to the client"

| Response | Stressed Word | What's Being Corrected |
|---|---|---|
| "No, **SHE** told you to send it" | SHE | Wrong person gave the instruction |
| "No, I told you to **REVIEW** it" | REVIEW | Wrong action |
| "No, I told you to send it to the **TEAM**" | TEAM | Wrong recipient |
| "No, I **ASKED** you to send it" | ASKED | Wrong speech act (asked, not told---softer authority) |

---

### The Correction Stress Pattern (Generalization)

> **The stressed word in a correction is the ONLY thing that changes. Everything else stays the same in the listener's mental model.**

This follows what linguists call the **Question Under Discussion (QUD)** framework (Roberts, 1996; Rooth, 1992): every utterance answers an implicit question, and the focused (stressed) element is the answer to that question. Everything else is presupposed.

## 4. STRESS AND HEDGES: When Emphasis Flips Meaning

### The Hedge Stress Rule

> **In English, hedge words (think, might, probably, could, just, perhaps) soften a claim. Stressing the hedge word REVERSES the softening---it often creates sarcasm, dismissiveness, or the opposite of the hedge's literal meaning.**

This is one of the most difficult pragmatic patterns for autistic individuals because it requires detecting that stress on a word *inverts* its function rather than *intensifying* it.

---

### Worked Example: "I think you might be wrong"

| Variant | Effect | Full Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| "I **THINK** you might be wrong" | Stress on the hedge *strengthens the speaker's certainty*. "Think" is being used sarcastically or emphatically. | "I don't just think it---I'm quite sure you're wrong. I'm being polite by saying 'think' but my stress tells you I actually know." |
| "I think you **MIGHT** be wrong" | Stress on the modal *preserves genuine uncertainty*. The speaker genuinely considers it a possibility. | "There's a real possibility here. I'm not sure, but it's worth considering that you could be wrong." |
| "I think you might be **WRONG**" | Stress on the adjective *focuses the content*. The hedge words are spoken quickly and unstressed. | "The point is the wrongness. I'm being gentle about it, but the emphasis is on the conclusion: wrong." |

**The Sarcasm Flip:** Compare:
- Unstressed hedge: "I *think* you might be wrong" = genuine uncertainty, polite softening
- Stressed hedge: "I **THINK** you might be wrong" = "I am quite confident you are wrong and I'm using 'think' almost mockingly"

---

### Worked Example: "It's probably fine"

| Variant | Effect | Full Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| "It's **PROBABLY** fine" | Stress on the hedge highlights the uncertainty. Can be sarcastic: "It's *probably* fine... but I clearly don't believe it is." | Often conveys *doubt or worry* rather than reassurance. The speaker is drawing attention to the "probably" to signal they're NOT confident. |
| "It's probably **FINE**" | Stress on the adjective. The hedge is genuine background softening. | Reassuring. "Don't worry. The situation is fine. I'm saying probably just to be accurate, but really, it's fine." |

**The critical difference:**
- "It's **PROBABLY** fine" (said while watching someone's questionable cooking) = "I have real doubts about this"
- "It's probably **FINE**" (said to someone worrying about a test) = "Relax, you'll be okay"

---

### Worked Example: "She might have a point"

| Variant | Effect | Full Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| "She **MIGHT** have a point" | Stress on the modal creates a concessive, reluctant tone. | "I don't want to admit it, but... okay, possibly she has a point. But only possibly. I'm grudgingly acknowledging this." |
| "She might have a **POINT**" | Stress on "point" affirms the content. | "Actually, what she said was substantive. She has a real point. The 'might' is just me being fair-minded." |

**Contextual demonstration:**

During an argument where you're defending your position and a third person weighs in against you:
- "She **MIGHT** have a point" = You're barely conceding. Through gritted teeth.
- "She might have a **POINT**" = You've actually been persuaded somewhat. The emphasis shows genuine intellectual engagement with her argument.

---

### Worked Example: "You could be right"

| Variant | Effect | Full Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| "You **COULD** be right" | Stress on the modal = very weak, possibly dismissive agreement. | "In theory, it's possible. I'm acknowledging the logical possibility while implying I think you're probably not right." |
| "You could be **RIGHT**" | Stress on the adjective = genuine consideration. | "I'm actually thinking about this, and... yeah, you might actually be right. I hadn't considered that." |
| "**YOU** could be right" | Stress on the pronoun = surprise at the source. | "Of all people, *you* might actually have the right answer here. I didn't expect it from you." (Can be backhanded or genuinely surprised.) |

---

### Worked Example: "I'm just wondering"

| Variant | Effect | Full Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| "I'm **JUST** wondering" | Stress on "just" *amplifies the minimization so far it becomes suspicious*. | "I'm claiming this is no big deal, but my stress tells you it IS a big deal. I'm not 'just' wondering---I have a real concern and I'm softening it." Often passive-aggressive. |
| "I'm just **WONDERING**" | Stress on "wondering" = genuine inquiry emphasis. | "I'm genuinely curious. This is a real question. I'm not making an accusation---I literally want to know." |

**The passive-aggressive pattern:**

"I'm **JUST** wondering why you didn't invite me" means: "I am NOT 'just wondering.' I am hurt and angry that you didn't invite me, and I'm using 'just wondering' as a thin veneer of casualness."

This is the same pattern as:
- "I'm **JUST** saying..." = I am definitely not 'just' saying. I am making a pointed criticism.
- "I was **JUST** asking..." = My question was not casual. You should have picked up on the subtext.
- "It's **JUST** a suggestion..." = This is a directive disguised as a suggestion.

---

### Worked Example: "Perhaps we should reconsider"

| Variant | Effect | Full Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| "**PERHAPS** we should reconsider" | Stress on the hedge = the speaker likely means "we definitely should reconsider" and is being superficially diplomatic. | In a meeting, this is often a power move. The "perhaps" is window dressing. The stress on it signals: "I'm being polite but this is not actually optional." |
| "Perhaps we should **RECONSIDER**" | Stress on the verb = genuine emphasis on the action needed. | "The current plan has problems. Let's rethink." Direct and substantive. |
| "Perhaps **WE** should reconsider" | Stress on the pronoun = questions who needs to reconsider. | "This isn't just my problem to rethink. *We* all need to reconsider. All of us. Including you." |

---

### The Hedge Stress Pattern (Generalization)

| Pattern | Effect |
|---|---|
| **Hedge word unstressed** (natural position) | Hedge functions normally: softens, qualifies, creates politeness |
| **Hedge word stressed** | Hedge is *highlighted*, which often **inverts its function**: creates sarcasm, passive aggression, false modesty, or signals the opposite of the hedge's literal meaning |
| **Content word stressed** (with hedge unstressed) | The substantive claim is foregrounded; the hedge is genuine background politeness |

**Why this is hard for autistic readers:** The literal meaning of "I **THINK** you're wrong" and "I think you're **WRONG**" is identical. The difference is *entirely* carried by stress. And the stressed version of the hedge ("I **THINK**") actually signals *greater certainty*---the exact opposite of what "think" literally means. This is a pragmatic inversion that must be learned as a pattern, because it cannot be derived from the words alone.

## 5. STRESS AND NEGATION: How Stress Changes What Gets Denied

### The Negation Stress Rule

> **When a sentence contains "not/didn't/never," the stressed word determines the *scope* of the negation---what part of the claim is being denied. Unstressed words are accepted as background facts.**

---

### Full Worked Example: "I didn't say you were stupid"

#### Variant 1: "I **DIDN'T** say you were stupid"

| Element | Value |
|---|---|
| **Scope of negation** | The entire event of saying it |
| **What's denied** | That I ever said this, in any form, to anyone |
| **What's implied** | Full, emphatic denial. "This never happened." |
| **Implicit question** | "Did you say I was stupid?" |
| **Context** | Someone confronts you with "You called me stupid!" and you flatly deny it. |

#### Variant 2: "I didn't **SAY** you were stupid"

| Element | Value |
|---|---|
| **Scope of negation** | The specific speech act of "saying" |
| **What's denied** | That the words were spoken aloud |
| **What's implied** | I may have *implied* it, *written* it, *thought* it, or *suggested* it---but I didn't *say* it. This is technical denial with loopholes. |
| **Implicit question** | "How did you communicate that I'm stupid?" |
| **Context** | "I didn't **SAY** you were stupid. I said your approach was 'not well thought through.' You inferred the rest." |

#### Variant 3: "I didn't say **YOU** were stupid"

| Element | Value |
|---|---|
| **Scope of negation** | The specific person |
| **What's denied** | That the accusation was directed at this particular person |
| **What's implied** | I said *someone* was stupid---just not you. |
| **Implicit question** | "Who did you say was stupid?" |
| **Context** | "I didn't say **YOU** were stupid. I said the *plan* was stupid. Or maybe I said *Tom's idea* was stupid. Don't take it personally." |

#### Variant 4: "I didn't say you were **STUPID**"

| Element | Value |
|---|---|
| **Scope of negation** | The specific characterization |
| **What's denied** | That the word "stupid" was used |
| **What's implied** | I said something negative about you, but "stupid" is too strong. Maybe I said you were "uninformed" or "mistaken" or "not thinking clearly." |
| **Implicit question** | "What exactly did you call me?" |
| **Context** | "I didn't say you were **STUPID**. I said you were *being careless*. Those are very different things." |

---

### Additional Negation Example: "She doesn't always do that"

| Variant | Meaning |
|---|---|
| "She **DOESN'T** always do that" | Emphatic negation: "That's not her typical behavior! You're mischaracterizing her." |
| "She doesn't **ALWAYS** do that" | Frequency correction: "She does it sometimes, but not *always*. You're overgeneralizing." |
| "She doesn't always do **THAT**" | Object correction: "She does things frequently, but not specifically *that* thing." |
| "**SHE** doesn't always do that" | Person correction: "Other people do it, but she specifically does not." |

---

### Additional Negation Example: "I'm not angry because you lied"

This is a famous scope ambiguity example:

| Variant | Meaning |
|---|---|
| "I'm not **ANGRY** because you lied" | I'm not angry---I'm *disappointed* (or hurt, or sad). The lying happened; my emotion is different than you think. |
| "I'm not angry because you **LIED**" | I'm angry, but not because of the lying. The lying isn't what upset me. Something else about the situation is the problem. |
| "I'm not angry because **YOU** lied" | I'm angry about lying, but not *your* lying specifically. Someone else's lie is what upset me. |
| "I'm **NOT** angry because you lied" | I am emphatically not angry. You're misreading my emotional state entirely. |

---

### The Negation Stress Pattern (Generalization)

| Where Stress Falls | What Gets Negated |
|---|---|
| On **not/didn't/never** itself | The entire proposition. Full denial. |
| On the **verb** | The specific action (but not the involvement or relationship) |
| On a **noun/pronoun** | The specific entity (but not the action or event type) |
| On an **adjective/adverb** | The specific characterization (but not the general claim) |

**Key insight:** Stressing the negation word ("I **DIDN'T** do it") is the broadest denial. Stressing any *other* word narrows the denial to just that element---which means *everything else is implicitly admitted*.

This is why "I didn't say you were **STUPID**" is actually a partial admission: you're admitting you said something negative, you're just denying the specific word.

## 6. STRESS AND FOCUS-SENSITIVE PARTICLES: Only, Even, Just, Also

### Why These Words Are Special

The words *only, even, just,* and *also* are called **focus-sensitive particles** because their meaning *depends on which word in the sentence receives stress*. The particle "attaches" to whatever is stressed, even if the particle and the stressed word are far apart in the sentence.

---

### ONLY

"Only" *restricts*: it says "this and nothing more."

**Sentence: "She only told him"**

| Variant | Stress | Meaning | What's Restricted |
|---|---|---|---|
| "She only **TOLD** him" | TOLD | She told him---but didn't show him, email him, or demonstrate. Just told. | The action is restricted: telling and nothing more |
| "She only told **HIM**" | HIM | She told one person: him. Nobody else was told. | The recipient is restricted: him and nobody else |
| "**SHE** only told him" | SHE | Of all the people who could have told him, only she did. | The agent is restricted: only she (not others too) |

**More examples with "only":**

"I only **KISSED** her" = I didn't do anything beyond kissing. (Restricts the action.)
"I only kissed **HER**" = She's the only person I kissed. (Restricts the object.)

"He only **READS** the news" = He reads it but doesn't watch or listen. (Restricts the method.)
"He only reads the **NEWS**" = He doesn't read books, fiction, or anything else. Just news. (Restricts what's read.)

"They only **VISIT** on holidays" = They visit but don't call, write, or help. (Restricts the activity.)
"They only visit on **HOLIDAYS**" = They never come at other times. (Restricts the timing.)

---

### EVEN

"Even" signals *surprise*: the stressed element is unexpected for the category.

**Sentence: "Even John passed the test"**

| Variant | Stress | Meaning | What's Surprising |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Even **JOHN** passed the test" | JOHN | John was the *least likely* person to pass, and he passed. Everyone else passing was expected. | The person: John passing is the surprising part |
| "Even John **PASSED** the test" | PASSED | John interacting with the test was expected (he took it), but the surprising thing is that he *passed* rather than failed. | The outcome: passing is the surprising part |

**More examples with "even":**

"Even **CHILDREN** understood it" = It was so clear that the group you'd least expect to understand (children) got it. (Surprising who understood.)
"Even children **UNDERSTOOD** it" = Children encountering it was expected, but the surprising part was their comprehension. (Surprising what happened.)

"She didn't even **TRY**" = The minimum expected action (trying) didn't happen. (The absence of effort is surprising.)
"She didn't **EVEN** try" = Stressing "even" intensifies the sense that this is below the minimum threshold. (Emphasizes how far below expectations.)

"Even **I** can do that" = I'm presenting myself as the lowest bar. If *I* can do it, anyone can. (Self-deprecating emphasis.)

---

### JUST

"Just" is multifunctional: it can mean *only/merely*, *recently*, or *exactly*. Stress determines which meaning dominates and what it attaches to.

**Sentence: "I just looked at it"**

| Variant | Stress | Meaning | What's Minimized |
|---|---|---|---|
| "I just **LOOKED** at it" | LOOKED | I merely looked---didn't touch, take, or alter it. | The action: only looking, nothing more |
| "I just looked at **IT**" | IT | I only looked at that one specific thing. Nothing else. | The object: only that particular item |
| "I **JUST** looked at it" | JUST | Emphasizes the recentness---I looked at it moments ago. OR intensifies the minimizing: "I *merely* looked, that's all!" | Depending on context: time (recently) or scope (merely) |

**More examples with "just":**

"I'm just **ASKING**" = My action is limited to asking. I'm not accusing, I'm not judging---merely asking. (Minimizes the action.)
"I'm **JUST** asking" = (With stress on "just") Can become defensive or passive-aggressive: "Why are you reacting so strongly? It was *only* a question." (The stress on the minimizer can signal that the asker knows the question is provocative.)

"It's just a **GAME**" = The thing in question is merely a game (not life-or-death). (Minimizes the object.)
"It's **JUST** a game" = More emphatic minimization, often used to dismiss someone's emotional reaction. (Stress on the minimizer dismisses the concern more forcefully.)

---

### ALSO / TOO

"Also" and "too" are *additive*: they say "in addition to something else."

**Sentence: "John also came to the party"**

| Variant | Stress | Meaning | What's Being Added |
|---|---|---|---|
| "**JOHN** also came to the party" | JOHN | John is being *added to the list* of people who came. Others came; John did too. | A person is added to an existing set |
| "John also **CAME** to the party" | CAME | John did something else regarding the party (maybe organized it), and *in addition*, he also attended. | An action is added to John's other actions |
| "John also came to the **PARTY**" | PARTY | John came to some other event, and additionally, came to the party. | A destination/event is added to John's attendance list |

**More examples with "also/too":**

"**SARAH** also speaks French" = Adding Sarah to the list of French speakers. Others were already known to speak French. (Adding a person.)
"Sarah also speaks **FRENCH**" = Sarah speaks other languages, and French is being added to her repertoire. (Adding a language.)

"I **ALSO** need to mention..." = The speaker is adding to a list of things already mentioned. Stress on "also" itself emphasizes the additive nature. (Meta-emphasis: "I have MORE to say.")

---

### Focus-Sensitive Particle Pattern (Generalization)

| Particle | Function | Stress On Particle Itself | Stress On Another Word |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Only** | Restricts/excludes | Intensifies restriction | Determines what is restricted |
| **Even** | Signals surprise | Intensifies surprise | Determines what is surprising |
| **Just** | Minimizes/limits | Intensifies minimization (can become defensive) | Determines what is minimized |
| **Also/Too** | Adds to a set | Emphasizes the addition | Determines what is being added |

## 7. STRESS IN EVERYDAY CONTEXTS

### Workplace Scenarios

---

**Scenario: Team disagreement about a decision**

| Sentence | Stress | Meaning | Subtext |
|---|---|---|---|
| "I thought **WE** agreed on this" | WE | Emphasizes the collective nature of the agreement. | "This was a group decision and you're going rogue." |
| "I thought we **AGREED** on this" | AGREED | Emphasizes that agreement existed. | "We reached consensus and you're now breaking it." |
| "I **THOUGHT** we agreed on this" | THOUGHT | The hedge "thought" is stressed---signals realization that maybe there wasn't agreement after all. | "Apparently I was wrong about there being consensus. Did I misunderstand?" Or sarcastically: "I *thought* we agreed but clearly you had other plans." |
| "I thought we agreed on **THIS**" | THIS | Emphasizes the specific item agreed upon. | "We agreed on this specific plan---what you're proposing is a different plan." |

---

**Scenario: Email attribution**

| Sentence | Stress | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| "**I** sent the report yesterday" | I | "It was me who sent it, not someone else. Giving credit/accepting responsibility." |
| "I **SENT** the report yesterday" | SENT | "I did send it---contrary to the implication that I didn't. Check your inbox." |
| "I sent the **REPORT** yesterday" | REPORT | "I sent the report specifically. You may be thinking of a different document." |
| "I sent the report **YESTERDAY**" | YESTERDAY | "The timing was yesterday, not today or last week. Get the timeline right." |

---

### Relationship Scenarios

---

**Scenario: Emotional misunderstanding**

"I never said I didn't love you"

| Variant | Stress | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| "I never said I didn't **LOVE** you" | LOVE | "I may have expressed frustration, but I never said I didn't *love* you. Maybe I said I didn't *like* what you did. Love was never in question." |
| "I **NEVER** said I didn't love you" | NEVER | "Not once did those words come out of my mouth. You're inventing things I never said." |
| "I never **SAID** I didn't love you" | SAID | "I didn't say it... but maybe my actions implied it. Or maybe you inferred it. But the words were never spoken." (This is a dangerous variant---it's technically true but acknowledges the communication occurred non-verbally.) |
| "I never said **I** didn't love you" | I (second one) | "I didn't say *I* didn't love you---maybe I said *someone else* doesn't. Or maybe I was talking about how *you* feel." |

---

**Scenario: Plans and expectations**

| Sentence | Stress | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| "I thought you **WANTED** to go" | WANTED | "You expressed desire to go. Have you changed your mind?" |
| "I thought **YOU** wanted to go" | YOU | "I assumed it was your preference. If it wasn't your idea, whose was it?" |
| "I **THOUGHT** you wanted to go" | THOUGHT | "Apparently I was wrong about what you wanted." (Can be hurt, confused, or accusatory.) |

---

### Service / Consumer Scenarios

---

**Scenario: Restaurant order**

| Sentence | Stress | Meaning | What's Wrong |
|---|---|---|---|
| "I ordered the **LARGE**" | LARGE | I got the wrong size. | Size is wrong (I got medium or small) |
| "I **ORDERED** the large" | ORDERED | I ordered it but it never arrived. | The order wasn't fulfilled |
| "**I** ordered the large" | I | Someone else at the table got my order and I got theirs. | The orders were swapped |
| "I ordered the large **COFFEE**" | COFFEE | I got a large something, but it's the wrong item. | The item is wrong (I got tea or juice) |

---

**Scenario: Scheduling correction**

| Sentence | Stress | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| "Actually, it's on **TUESDAY**" | TUESDAY | The day is wrong. It's Tuesday, not whatever day you said. |
| "Actually, **IT'S** on Tuesday" | IT'S | This specific event is on Tuesday. You might be confusing it with a different event. |
| "**ACTUALLY**, it's on Tuesday" | ACTUALLY | Stress on "actually" = I'm correcting you and I want to signal the correction clearly. Can come across as pedantic. |
| "Actually, it's on Tuesday at **NOON**" | NOON | Adding a correction about the time as well. |

---

### Social / Correction Scenarios

---

**Scenario: Being introduced incorrectly**

| Sentence | Stress | What You're Correcting |
|---|---|---|
| "Actually, I'm **SARAH**" | SARAH | They got your name wrong |
| "Actually, I'm her **SISTER**" | SISTER | They got the relationship wrong |
| "Actually, **I'M** the manager" | I'M | They assumed someone else was the manager |
| "Actually, I'm **FROM** Portland" | FROM | They said you live in Portland, but you're originally from there and now live elsewhere (or vice versa) |

---

**Scenario: Declining an invitation**

| Sentence | Stress | Subtext |
|---|---|---|
| "I **CAN'T** come Saturday" | CAN'T | Strong: impossible, not a choice. "I would if I could but I cannot." |
| "I can't come **SATURDAY**" | SATURDAY | Saturday specifically is the problem. "Could we do a different day?" |
| "**I** can't come Saturday" | I | "I can't, but maybe someone else from our household/group can." |
| "I can't **COME**" | COME | "I can't physically attend, but maybe I could join remotely or contribute another way." |

## 8. QUICK REFERENCE: THE STRESS-MEANING MAP

### Table 1: Stress Position and the Implicit Question

For any declarative sentence, the stressed word reveals what question the speaker is implicitly answering.

| Stress Falls On... | The Sentence Answers the Question... | Example |
|---|---|---|
| A **subject/agent** (who) | "Who did it?" | "**JOHN** broke the window" |
| A **verb/action** (what happened) | "What did they do?" | "John **BROKE** the window" |
| A **object** (what) | "What was affected?" | "John broke the **WINDOW**" |
| A **recipient** (to whom) | "Who received the action?" | "Give it to **HER**" |
| A **location** (where) | "Where did it happen?" | "It happened in **PARIS**" |
| A **time** (when) | "When did it happen?" | "It happened on **MONDAY**" |
| A **manner** (how) | "How was it done?" | "She sang **BEAUTIFULLY**" |
| A **reason** (why) | "Why did it happen?" | "I left because I was **TIRED**" |
| A **negation** | "Did it happen at all?" | "I **DIDN'T** break the window" |
| A **hedge/modal** | Often inverts the hedge (see Section 4) | "I **THINK** you're wrong" |

---

### Table 2: Stress and Activated Alternatives

| When You Stress... | You Activate Alternatives In The Category Of... | Example Alternatives |
|---|---|---|
| A person's name | Other people | John, Mary, Tom, the boss, nobody... |
| A verb | Other actions | broke/fixed/moved/ignored/stole... |
| A noun (object) | Other objects | window/door/car/phone/vase... |
| A time word | Other times | Monday/Tuesday/morning/yesterday... |
| A location | Other places | Paris/London/here/upstairs... |
| A quantity | Other quantities | one/three/many/none... |
| A modifier | Other qualities | big/small/red/broken/old... |
| A possessive | Other owners | my/your/his/their/nobody's... |

---

### Table 3: Default Stress Position and Deviation Effects

In English, **default (unmarked) stress falls on the last content word** in the sentence. This is called the **nuclear stress** position.

| Stress Pattern | Type | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Stress on the **last content word** (default) | **Broad focus / all-new information** | The whole sentence is new information. No special contrast. Neutral delivery. |
| Stress **earlier** than the last content word | **Narrow focus / contrastive** | The stressed word is being *contrasted with alternatives*. Everything after it is presupposed (already known). |
| Stress on a **function word** (the, a, is, was, do) | **Strong contrast or correction** | Function words are normally unstressed, so stressing them creates strong emphasis. "I said **THE** report, not **A** report." |
| **Multiple stressed words** | **Multiple foci or list** | Each stressed word answers a separate implicit question. "**JOHN** brought **WINE** and **MARY** brought **CAKE**." |
| **No clear stress** (flat intonation) | **Disengagement or reading aloud** | Can signal boredom, depression, sarcasm (deadpan delivery), or literal reading. |

---

### Table 4: Common Stress Patterns and Their Social Functions

| Pattern | Social Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Stress on **a pronoun** | Singling someone out, accusing, or crediting | "**YOU** did this" / "**I** fixed it" |
| Stress on **a negation** | Emphatic denial | "I did **NOT** say that" |
| Stress on **a hedge word** | Sarcasm, false modesty, or passive aggression | "I **JUST** wanted to help" |
| Stress on **a correction word** | Correcting specific misinformation | "It's on **TUESDAY**, not Thursday" |
| Stress on **"actually"** | Signaling you're about to correct someone | "**ACTUALLY**, that's not how it works" |
| Stress on **a repeated word** | Insistence or frustration | "I **SAID** I would do it" |
| Stress on **an unexpected word** | Creating dramatic effect or humor | "He ate the **ENTIRE** cake" |

---

### Table 5: Quick Diagnostic Tool

**When you hear emphasis on a word and aren't sure what it means, use this checklist:**

| Step | Question to Ask Yourself |
|---|---|
| 1 | **What word is stressed?** Identify the emphasized word. |
| 2 | **What category does it belong to?** (person, action, object, time, place, quality) |
| 3 | **What alternatives exist in that category?** (What other people/actions/objects could fill that slot?) |
| 4 | **Is the speaker choosing this over those alternatives?** If yes, they're making a *contrastive* statement. |
| 5 | **Is the stressed word a hedge or minimizer?** If yes, check whether the stress *inverts* its meaning (see Section 4). |
| 6 | **What question would this be the answer to?** The stressed word is the answer. The implicit question tells you the speaker's focus. |
| 7 | **What's left unsaid?** Everything *not* stressed is assumed to be agreed-upon background. The speaker's real point is the stressed word. |

## Appendix: Why Stress-Meaning Rules Are Difficult for Autistic Individuals (And Why This Reference Exists)

Research on autism and prosody (Grice, 2023; Grossman et al., 2010; Diehl et al., 2015) consistently finds that:

1. **Receptive prosody deficits in contrastive stress** are well-documented in autism. Autistic individuals show significantly poorer ability to use stress cues to identify contrastive focus compared to controls.

2. **The formal/rule-based aspects of prosody are more learnable** than the intuitive, context-dependent ones. This reference exploits that strength by presenting stress-meaning relationships as *explicit, systematic rules*.

3. **The pragmatic functions of prosody (sarcasm, hedging, passive aggression) are especially challenging** because they require integrating prosodic cues with social context and speaker intention---exactly the kind of multi-channel processing that autistic cognition may handle differently.

4. **Literal interpretation tendency** means that "I **THINK** you're wrong" may be processed as simply "I think you're wrong" without the stress-driven meaning inversion. This reference makes those inversions explicit and catalogues them.

**The goal of this reference** is to convert implicit, intuition-based prosodic knowledge into explicit, rule-based pattern recognition---playing to autistic cognitive strengths while addressing a genuine pragmatic gap.
