# Concept Research: The Scientific Critique of Polyvagal Theory — the "Untenable" Debate

## Overview

Polyvagal Theory (PVT), first introduced by Stephen Porges in his 1994 Society for Psychophysiological Research presidential address and elaborated in his 2011 book, has become a near-ubiquitous framework in trauma-informed psychotherapy, somatic practice, and the wellness industry. It has also become one of the most scientifically contested theories of autonomic function in contemporary physiology. The scientific critique is not new — Grossman and Taylor's 2007 paper on respiratory sinus arrhythmia (RSA) already questioned the theory's measurement assumptions — but it consolidated dramatically in 2022–2026 through a sequence of major publications culminating in a 39-author international expert consensus paper that called the theory "untenable."

This category walks through the empirical case against PVT's core anatomical and evolutionary claims, anchored in three landmark publications: Neuhuber & Berthoud's 2022 functional anatomy review, Grossman's 2023 single-author critique of the five basic premises, and the 2026 "Why the Polyvagal Theory is Untenable" consensus paper (Grossman et al., signed by 39 invited experts in autonomic neuroscience, comparative physiology, and vertebrate evolution). It then gives fair representation to Porges's 2026 response ("When a Critique Becomes Untenable"), which argues that critics attack a reconstructed version of the theory rather than its actual peer-reviewed claims. Finally, it surveys the post-publication discourse — the clinician responses, the trade-press coverage, and the falsifiability meta-debate — to show how the field is actually responding rather than implying premature consensus.

**Key framing point**: The critique does *not* claim autistic HRV or RSA findings are wrong. Cheng et al. 2020's meta-analysis showing lower baseline parasympathetic HRV (g = −0.5168) and reduced RSA reactivity to social stress (g = −0.7246) in autism remains valid. What the critique challenges is PVT's specific *interpretation* of those autonomic data — the claim that they reflect a uniquely mammalian, evolutionarily-derived, dual-vagal architecture with a "social engagement system." The data are real; the explanation Porges places over them is what's under fire.

## Core Components

### Component 1: The Five Premises Under Attack

PVT rests on five interlocking claims, identified explicitly in Grossman 2023 and the 2026 Untenable consensus. Each is challenged on independent empirical grounds.

**Premise 1**: Respiratory Sinus Arrhythmia (RSA) is a direct measure of central vagal outflow to the heart.
- **Purpose in PVT**: Anchors clinical and research use of HRV as a window onto "vagal tone" and "ventral vagal" activity.
- **What critics say**: RSA is a peripheral measure shaped by respiratory rate, tidal volume, blood pressure, baroreflex activity, and sympathetic tone. Using it as an index of central vagal drive is, in Grossman's words, "a category mistake." This is the longest-running critique — Grossman & Taylor (2007) made it nearly two decades before the 2026 consensus.

**Premise 2**: There is a meaningful functional distinction between the dorsal motor nucleus of the vagus (DMN/DMV/DMX) and the nucleus ambiguus (NA/Amb) in mammalian cardiac control — specifically, that the DMN provides unmyelinated, slow "reptilian-vintage" cardiac vagal control while the NA provides myelinated, fast "mammalian" cardiac vagal control.
- **Purpose in PVT**: Generates the entire "two vagal branches" architecture and the dorsal-vs-ventral state model.
- **What critics say**: In mammals, cardiac vagal control is overwhelmingly via the nucleus ambiguus. Best estimates: ~80% of cardiac vagal preganglionic neurons in cats, ~60% in monkeys; DMN contribution is real but small. Calling it a "split" misrepresents the proportions. Furthermore, Neuhuber & Berthoud (2022) argue the framing of NA "branchiomotor source nuclei" communicating "directly with the visceromotor portion of the nucleus ambiguus" is misleading neuroanatomy.

**Premise 3**: Myelinated cardioinhibitory vagal fibers (the "smart vagus") originate from a ventral source and are uniquely mammalian.
- **Purpose in PVT**: This is the evolutionary load-bearing claim. The "Social Engagement System" depends on the idea that mammals evolved a new, faster vagal pathway tied to face/voice and social behavior.
- **What critics say**: Multiple comparative studies show myelinated cardiac vagal fibers across vertebrate lineages. Monteiro et al. (2018) demonstrated myelinated cardiorespiratory vagal fibers in the South American lungfish (*Lepidosiren paradoxa*) from a ventrolateral brainstem source resembling a primordial nucleus ambiguus. Sharks have myelinated vagal cardiac fibers (~45% of cardiac preganglionics). Fish, amphibians, reptiles, and birds all have characterized myelinated vagal cardiac fibers. The "uniquely mammalian myelinated vagus" claim is contradicted by the comparative-physiology literature.

**Premise 4**: Vagal evolution correlates with the emergence of mammalian social behavior — i.e., reptiles lack sophisticated sociality and lack the myelinated vagus; mammals have both because the two co-evolved.
- **Purpose in PVT**: This is the theory's *theory of why*. It explains why a "social engagement system" exists and why "danger" and "life threat" map onto evolutionarily older vagal states.
- **What critics say**: Non-mammalian vertebrates demonstrate complex social behaviors: long-term pair-bonding, monogamy, extended/communal parental care, social learning, cooperative hunting, territoriality, dominance hierarchies. Doody, Burghardt & Dinets (2023) specifically challenge the "asocial reptile" framing. The phylogenetic mapping PVT proposes — myelinated vagus = mammalian sociality — has the comparative data backwards.

**Premise 5**: Earlier physiological research literature supports the polyvagal architecture (the historical interpretation of vagal physiology).
- **Purpose in PVT**: Provides scholarly legitimacy and continuity with established work.
- **What critics say**: The 2026 Untenable paper systematically reinterprets cited historical sources and argues they don't support the polyvagal reading. They argue PVT selectively cites older work in ways that don't match modern (or even mid-20th-century) understanding of vagal anatomy.

### Component 2: The Three Anchor Critique Papers

**Anchor 1: Grossman & Taylor 2007** — *Toward understanding respiratory sinus arrhythmia: relations to cardiac vagal tone, evolution and biobehavioral functions*. Biological Psychology, 74(2), 263–285.
- The earliest formal critique. Established that RSA is not a reliable direct measure of cardiac vagal tone.
- Largely about Premise 1.
- Cited by every subsequent critique.

**Anchor 2: Neuhuber & Berthoud 2022** — *Functional anatomy of the vagus system: How does the polyvagal theory comply?* Biological Psychology, 174, 108425. PubMed: 36100134. DOI: 10.1016/j.biopsycho.2022.108425.
- Functional neuroanatomy review of vagal nuclei.
- Concludes evidence "does not support a role of the 'dorsal vagal complex' in freezing as proposed by the PVT."
- Argues mammalian freezing responses are primarily mediated by the nucleus ambiguus, not the DMN.
- Critiques the "branchiomotor → visceromotor NA" communication framing as misleading.
- Largely about Premise 2 and Premise 4.

**Anchor 3: Grossman 2023** — *Fundamental challenges and likely refutations of the five basic premises of the polyvagal theory*. Biological Psychology, 180, 108589. PubMed: 37230290. DOI: 10.1016/j.biopsycho.2023.108589.
- Single-author comprehensive critique.
- Frames PVT's five basic premises and argues each "has been shown to be either untenable or highly implausible based on the available scientific literature."
- Pre-reviewed by leading experts in evolutionary biology, vagal neuroanatomy, and cardiovascular autonomic physiology.
- Sets the stage for the 2026 consensus paper.

**Anchor 4: Grossman et al. 2026 (the "Untenable" consensus)** — *Why the Polyvagal Theory Is Untenable: An international expert evaluation of the polyvagal theory and commentary upon Porges, S.W. (2025). Polyvagal theory: current status, clinical applications, and future directions*. Clinical Neuropsychiatry, 23(1), 100–112. PubMed: 41768017. PMC: PMC12937499. DOI: 10.36131/cnfioritieditore20260110.
- 39 total co-authors: Paul Grossman as first author plus 38 invited experts (one invitee declined; 38 of 39 invited accepted).
- Co-authors span autonomic neuroscience, cardiovascular physiology, comparative physiology, evolutionary biology, neuroanatomy, and animal behavior.
- Conclusion: "the PVT is untenable, because it is not defensible based on existing neurophysiological and evolutionary evidence."
- Directly addresses Porges's 2025 article (*Polyvagal theory: current status, clinical applications, and future directions*. Clinical Neuropsychiatry, 22(3), 169–184) by title.

### Component 3: Porges's Response

**Porges 2026** — *When a Critique Becomes Untenable: A Scholarly Response to Grossman et al.'s Evaluation of Polyvagal Theory*. Clinical Neuropsychiatry, 23(1), 113–128. PubMed: 41768026.
- Published in the same journal issue as the consensus critique.
- Argues the critique "repeatedly evaluates a reconstructed proxy of the theory shaped by persistent category errors," specifically calling out:
  - "Conflation of neuroanatomy with neurophysiology"
  - "Reduction of theory to measurement"
- Defends PVT as "a systems-level, pathway-specific framework of autonomic state regulation" with "explicit conditions of falsifiability."
- Frames disagreements as "differences in measurement preference, level of analysis, or theoretical framing rather than evidence against the theory's organizing principles."
- Concludes: "the charge of scientific untenability does not apply to Polyvagal Theory as formulated"; the critique "fails to engage the theory on its own terms."
- Does not engage critics sequentially or claim-by-claim; instead reasserts the theory's conceptual scope.

### Component 4: The Lungfish Problem (Monteiro et al. 2018)

A single comparative-physiology paper carries disproportionate weight in the critique because it is the cleanest empirical refutation of Premise 3.

**Monteiro, D.A., Taylor, E.W., Sartori, M.R., Cruz, A.L., Rantin, F.T., & Leite, C.A.C. (2018)**. *Cardiorespiratory interactions previously identified as mammalian are present in the primitive lungfish*. Science Advances, 4(2), eaaq0800. PMC: PMC5833999. DOI: 10.1126/sciadv.aaq0800.

**What they found**:
- South American lungfish (*Lepidosiren paradoxa*) — an air-breathing fish more closely related to tetrapods than to ray-finned fish.
- Electron microscopy: predominance of myelinated fibers in the cardiac vagus.
- Vagal conduction velocities of ~10 m/s, comparable to mammalian B-fibers (i.e., genuinely myelinated, fast-conducting).
- Cardiorespiratory coupling (an RSA-like phenomenon) present in this primitive air-breather.
- Vagal preganglionic neurons distributed across four locations, "chiefly within the dorsal vagal motor nucleus" plus scattered cells in **ventrolateral regions that may represent a primordial nucleus ambiguus**.

**Why this matters for PVT**:
- Directly contradicts the claim that myelinated cardiac vagal control with ventral-source origin is uniquely mammalian.
- The phylogenetic story PVT tells (asocial reptilian DMV vagus → social mammalian NA vagus) collapses if a primitive air-breathing fish already has the supposedly mammalian features.
- Other comparative work (Taylor, Wang & Leite 2022; Taylor & Joyce 2018) finds myelinated cardiac vagal fibers across vertebrate lineages from sharks onward.

### Component 5: The Nucleus Ambiguus Dispute

**PVT framing**: Mammalian cardiac vagal control is *split* between DMN (unmyelinated, slow, "primitive") and NA (myelinated, fast, "smart"). The split itself carries the theory's social/defensive distinction.

**Critic framing**: In mammals, the great majority of cardiac vagal preganglionic neurons are in the nucleus ambiguus. Best species-level estimates: **~80% in cats, ~60% in monkeys**. Humans likely sit in that range. The DMN contribution to cardiac output is real but minor.

**Why this matters**:
- If 80% of cardiac vagal control in mammals is NA-based, calling it a "split between nuclei" overstates the dorsal contribution and obscures the actual anatomy.
- The functional duality PVT proposes — "ventral vagal" state vs. "dorsal vagal" state — doesn't map cleanly onto an anatomy where one nucleus dominates and the other plays a minor cardiac role.
- Porges's response argues critics ignore qualitative differences between NA and DMN projections (embryological origin, myelination, transcriptomic profile — citing Coverdell et al. 2019, Jalil et al. 2023). His defense: even if numerically lopsided, the qualitative differentiation is real and matters.

### Component 6: The Dorsal Vagal Freezing Claim

**PVT framing**: When the "social engagement system" fails and sympathetic mobilization fails, mammals fall back to an evolutionarily ancient "dorsal vagal" shutdown — immobilization, dissociation, syncope, "freeze." This is the load-bearing claim for the popular polyvagal account of autistic shutdown, dissociation in trauma, and chronic-illness collapse states.

**What Neuhuber & Berthoud 2022 actually found**:
- Evidence "does not support a role of the 'dorsal vagal complex' in freezing as proposed by the PVT."
- Mammalian freezing responses appear primarily mediated by the nucleus ambiguus, not the DMN.
- "Profound bradycardia is neither common nor frequent among humans during emotional freezing or psychological dissociation."
- The DMN "should not be linked to passive defensive behavior."

**What this does and doesn't claim**:
- Doesn't deny that freeze/shutdown states exist clinically.
- Doesn't deny that some bradycardia can accompany severe stress.
- Does deny that the DMN is the *mechanism* PVT claims.
- Does deny that the popular "polyvagal ladder" account of dorsal vagal shutdown has direct in-vivo neuroanatomical support.

### Component 7: The Falsifiability Meta-Debate

This is where the disagreement gets philosophically tangled, because Porges and his critics disagree about what *kind* of theory PVT is.

**Critics' position**:
- Several core PVT claims make specific empirical predictions (e.g., "only mammals have myelinated vagal cardiac fibers"; "freezing is mediated by DMN") — these have been empirically tested and falsified.
- When PVT is restated to evade falsification (e.g., "the theory was not proposed to be either proven or falsified, but rather to be informed by research and modified"), it loses scientific status.
- The clymene Medium piece (cognitive neuroscientist, 2024) calls Porges's "not proposed to be falsified" framing "shocking ignorance of the scientific method."

**Porges's position**:
- "Contrary to claims that Polyvagal Theory lacks falsifiability, the theory has generated a range of specific, testable predictions."
- PVT is "a systems-level, pathway-specific framework," not a simple anatomical claim — so attacking individual anatomical predictions as if they're the theory misunderstands what the theory is.
- Disagreements reflect "differences in measurement preference, level of analysis, or theoretical framing."

**The honest disagreement**:
- Both sides agree the theory has empirical content.
- They disagree about which empirical content is load-bearing and which is illustrative.
- They disagree about whether the systems-level claims survive when the anatomical claims fail.

### Component 8: The Post-Publication Discourse

The 2026 publication of the Untenable consensus + Porges's response triggered a fast wave of clinician and media commentary. The shape of the response is itself informative.

**Trade press (broadly critical of PVT)**:
- InSight+ (Medical Journal of Australia), Becca Whitehead, March 9, 2026: *"Polyvagal Theory debunked in comprehensive new study"* — covers Grossman's framing, quotes him on existing literature offering "more refined, more complex explanations." Doesn't reproduce Porges's response in equal weight.
- Journal of Psychiatry Reform, Giroux, Ahlers & Miawotoe (October 2023): *"Polyvagal Approaches: scientifically questionable but useful in practice"* — pre-empts the 2026 consensus with the same framing. Acknowledges critique, defends clinical utility.

**Clinician responses (defending clinical utility, conceding scientific points)**:
- Psychology Today, Annie Chen, LMFT, February 2026: *"A Clinician's Perspective on the Polyvagal Controversy"* — "not ready to abandon PVT," argues clinical "knowing" differs from experimental science.
- Psychology Today, MaryCatherine McDonald PhD, April 2026: *"Polyvagal Theory Has Not Been 'Debunked'"* — uses the aspirin-without-mechanism analogy; warns against overcorrection.
- Trauma Therapist Institute, Rebecca Kase, LCSW: *"Significant, Not Sacred: A Clinical Educator's Response to Grossman"* — "you can critique the wiring without dismissing the revolution"; bridges theory and practice.
- Lauren Auer (Substack): "The debate is real. The questions are legitimate. And the framework is still worth using."

**More skeptical clinician pieces**:
- Leah Benson Therapy: *"What's Wrong with Polyvagal Theory"* — argues PVT is largely pseudoscientific marketing.
- 3 Brains Intelligence: *"Why It's Time to Move Beyond the Polyvagal Theory"* — moves to predictive processing / NVI alternatives.

**Pseudoscience-position pieces**:
- Medium / clymene (cognitive neuroscientist, 2024): *"Polyvagal Theory is Pseudoscience. Here's Why"* — explicit falsifiability argument; calls PVT a "belief system" by Popperian criteria.

**Polyvagal Institute defense**:
- Defends the theory; cites Strain et al. 2024 (optogenetic evidence claimed to support dorsal vagal complex distinctions), Coverdell et al. 2019, Jalil et al. 2023 (transcriptomic and embryological evidence claimed to differentiate NA and DMN).
- Direct Porges quote: "Scientific critique must rest on accurate representation of a theory's foundational claims, not caricature or rhetorical deflection."

**Common shape of the clinician centrist response**: (1) concede that critics raise valid scientific questions about anatomical and evolutionary claims; (2) defend the clinical framework as separable from the contested neuroanatomy; (3) emphasize that body-based practices (slow breathing, co-regulation, safety cues) work via mechanisms that predate PVT and don't require it.

## How It Works: The Anatomy of the Dispute

### Step 1: Identify what PVT claims that's empirically testable

The theory has both *systems-level* claims (states, transitions, safety, social engagement) and *specific empirical* claims (anatomy, phylogeny, RSA as vagal tone proxy). Critics target the empirical claims. Defenders argue the systems-level claims are what matter.

### Step 2: Apply comparative anatomy and physiology

For each empirical claim, the critique brings to bear the actual literature:
- **RSA-as-vagal-tone**: Grossman & Taylor 2007 + huge subsequent literature on RSA confounds.
- **DMN/NA split**: Cardiac vagal preganglionic neuron distribution studies (cats ~80% NA; monkeys ~60% NA; lower DMN cardiac contribution across mammals).
- **Mammalian uniqueness**: Monteiro 2018 (lungfish), Taylor & Joyce 2018, Taylor/Wang/Leite 2022 (vertebrate comparative phylogeny).
- **Dorsal vagal freezing**: Neuhuber & Berthoud 2022 (direct neuroanatomy).
- **Asocial reptile**: Doody, Burghardt & Dinets 2023 (animal behavior literature on reptile sociality).

### Step 3: Read Porges's response

Porges argues the critics:
- Confuse neuroanatomy (structural claims) with neurophysiology (functional claims).
- Reduce the theory to its measurement claims and miss the systems-level architecture.
- Attack a reconstructed proxy of the theory rather than the theory as articulated.
- Use measurement methods (peak-to-trough RSA) the theory itself doesn't endorse.

### Step 4: Read the post-publication clinician response

Most defenders concede the anatomical critique. They split between two stances:
- **Therapeutic-utility defense**: the framework works clinically even if the mechanism story is wrong.
- **Reformulation defense**: PVT survives if reframed as a systems-level account of autonomic state regulation, decoupled from the contested phylogeny.

### Step 5: Identify what survives and what doesn't

This is the synthesis. The honest assessment is not "PVT is debunked" but more like:
- **Empirically falsified components**: uniquely mammalian myelinated vagus claim; dorsal-vagal-as-freezing-mediator claim; "asocial reptile" evolutionary framing; "RSA = vagal tone" as a clean equation.
- **Substantially weakened components**: the meaningful functional duality of DMN vs NA in mammalian cardiac control; the specific phylogenetic story about vagal-sociality coevolution.
- **Empirically real underlying phenomena that don't need PVT to explain**: autonomic state changes during stress; HRV correlations with emotion regulation; social safety modulating physiology; the actual existence of immobilization/freeze responses; autistic differences in HRV/RSA.
- **What the critique does NOT touch**: the clinical utility of safety-focused, body-aware therapy; the value of co-regulation; the value of breath, voice, and somatic work — all of which have evidence bases independent of PVT.

## Terminology Glossary

| Term | Definition | Also Known As |
|------|------------|---------------|
| **Polyvagal Theory (PVT)** | Porges's 1994 framework positing two functionally distinct vagal branches (ventral myelinated "smart" vs. dorsal unmyelinated "primitive") mapped to mammalian sociality and defensive states. | The Porges model |
| **Respiratory Sinus Arrhythmia (RSA)** | The natural variation in heart rate across the breathing cycle; central to PVT's measurement claims. | RSA, vagal tone proxy |
| **Nucleus ambiguus (NA, Amb)** | Brainstem motor nucleus; in mammals, the primary source of cardiac vagal preganglionic neurons with myelinated B-fiber projections. | NA, Amb, "ventral vagal source" in PVT |
| **Dorsal Motor Nucleus of the Vagus (DMN, DMV, DMX)** | Brainstem motor nucleus innervating viscera (gut, etc.); PVT claims it provides "primitive" unmyelinated cardiac vagal control mediating freeze states. | DMV, DMX, "dorsal vagal complex" in PVT |
| **Cardiac vagal preganglionic neuron** | Brainstem neuron projecting to the heart via the vagus; in mammals overwhelmingly NA-located. | CVPN |
| **Myelinated B-fiber** | Vagal fiber type with conduction velocity ~3–15 m/s; PVT's claimed "fast smart" vagus. | (No common alias) |
| **Falsifiability** | Karl Popper's criterion that scientific theories must make predictions that could in principle be shown false. | Popperian falsifiability |
| **Untenable** | Grossman et al.'s framing meaning "not defensible based on existing evidence." | (Term of art in the 2026 paper) |
| **Systems-level theory** | Porges's defense: PVT operates at the level of organized autonomic states and pathways, not at the level of individual anatomical claims. | (No common alias) |
| **Phylogenetic claim** | A claim about the evolutionary history of a trait across species. | Comparative phylogeny |
| **Cardiorespiratory coupling** | The general phenomenon of coordination between breathing and heart rate, of which RSA is one expression. Present in lungfish and many non-mammals. | (No common alias) |

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: The Lungfish as Critical Test

If PVT's Premise 3 is correct (uniquely mammalian myelinated vagus with ventral source), then a 2018 study of South American lungfish should have found unmyelinated cardiac vagus from a DMN-only source. Instead, Monteiro et al. found:
- Predominantly myelinated cardiac vagal fibers
- Conduction velocity ~10 m/s (mammalian B-fiber range)
- Vagal preganglionic neurons in *both* DMN *and* ventrolateral regions interpreted as a primordial NA
- Cardiorespiratory coupling resembling RSA

This is what philosophers of science call a "crucial test" — a single empirical result that, if it stands, falsifies a specific theoretical prediction. Defenders of PVT have not, to date, published a comparable comparative-anatomy paper showing the lungfish result is artifactual or misinterpreted.

### Example 2: The Dorsal Vagal Freezing Example

Imagine an autistic adult experiencing what they describe as a "shutdown" after a high-demand work week — cognitive slowing, social withdrawal, motor sluggishness, low affect. Popular polyvagal-informed material describes this as "dorsal vagal collapse" with the implication of DMN-driven bradycardia.

What direct measurement of such a person would actually show: variable HRV pattern, possibly low baseline parasympathetic tone, but typically **not** profound bradycardia. The experience is real. The labeling as "dorsal vagal state" is a clinical heuristic, not a direct measurement. Neuhuber & Berthoud's point is that the proposed *mechanism* — DMN activation — lacks direct in-vivo support; mammalian freeze responses appear NA-mediated. This doesn't deny the lived experience; it questions the mechanistic story attached to it.

### Example 3: The RSA Category Mistake

A clinician uses HRV biofeedback and notes that during slow-breathing practice, the client's RSA increases. The popular polyvagal interpretation: "ventral vagal" activity has increased; "social engagement system" is online; safety is registered.

The Grossman critique: RSA increased because the client is breathing slowly (mechanical/respiratory effect on stroke volume timing), with a baroreflex contribution, possibly some genuine vagal contribution, *all bundled together*. Calling this "ventral vagal activity" treats RSA as if it were cardiac vagal nerve traffic. It isn't. It's a peripheral cardiovascular phenomenon shaped by multiple variables. Slow breathing genuinely produces health benefits — but those benefits don't validate the polyvagal interpretive overlay.

## Common Misconceptions

### Misconception 1: "The 2026 paper proved PVT is wrong."
**Reality**: The paper presents the consensus position of 39 experts that PVT's anatomical and evolutionary premises are not defensible given existing evidence. It does not claim that every clinical observation associated with polyvagal-informed therapy is wrong; it claims the specific *theoretical architecture* PVT proposes is not supported by current neurophysiology and comparative evolution.

### Misconception 2: "Critics are saying HRV doesn't matter / autistic vagal-tone findings are wrong."
**Reality**: The autonomic data are real and reproducible. Autistic individuals do show lower baseline parasympathetic HRV and reduced RSA reactivity to social stress (Cheng 2020 meta-analysis). What's contested is the *polyvagal interpretation* of those data — the claim that they reflect specifically a "ventral vagal social engagement system" deficit rather than a more general autonomic regulation difference predicted equally well by Neurovisceral Integration, predictive-processing, or allostatic-load frameworks.

### Misconception 3: "Porges has been silent / the field is just rejecting PVT."
**Reality**: Porges has published an explicit scholarly response (Porges 2026, *Clinical Neuropsychiatry* 23(1):113–128). He argues critics misrepresent the theory and that PVT survives as a systems-level framework. He has not retracted any claims. The discourse is active, not settled by acclamation.

### Misconception 4: "Therapeutic practices based on PVT are pseudoscience."
**Reality**: Many practices that have been wrapped in polyvagal language — slow breathing, social safety cues, co-regulation, paced exposure, somatic awareness, body-based interventions — have evidence bases that predate PVT and don't depend on it. The critique targets the *theoretical claims about mechanism*, not the clinical practices themselves. (However, claims like "this device stimulates the ventral vagal complex" that depend on PVT's specific anatomy do inherit the theory's empirical problems.)

### Misconception 5: "PVT is just one neuroscientist's pet theory."
**Reality**: PVT was peer-reviewed and published in major journals. It has been influential for 30 years. It has empirical content. The critique is specifically that the empirical content has been examined and substantial portions have not held up — not that PVT is fringe or marginal.

### Misconception 6: "If anatomy is wrong, the whole clinical edifice falls."
**Reality**: This is the central question of the post-publication discourse. The clinician centrist position (McDonald, Chen, Kase, Auer) is that anatomical refutation doesn't invalidate clinical utility because the practices work for reasons that may be different from PVT's stated mechanism. Whether this is intellectually honest "translation" or post-hoc rescue is the active dispute.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

**Edge case 1: Strain et al. 2024 optogenetics**. The Polyvagal Institute cites recent optogenetic work as supporting dorsal vagal complex distinctions. Critics argue these findings concern specific subpopulations and don't restore the broader PVT phylogenetic story.

**Edge case 2: Functional NA/DMN distinctions exist, even if cardiac control is NA-dominant**. The two nuclei do differ embryologically, transcriptomically (Coverdell 2019, Jalil 2023), and in projection targets. Critics don't deny the distinction exists; they deny it carries the functional duality and phylogenetic story PVT claims.

**Edge case 3: Cardiorespiratory coupling is real**. Both sides accept that breath-heart-rate coordination is genuine and physiologically interesting. The disagreement is whether RSA specifically measures central vagal drive.

**Edge case 4: Some PVT clinical concepts have independent support**. Co-regulation has substantial evidence from attachment research. Social safety modulating physiology is empirically robust. The question is whether PVT's specific *mechanism story* for these phenomena holds — not whether the phenomena exist.

## Related Concepts

- **Cat 1 (Foundations)**: Establishes what PVT actually claims, so the critique can be evaluated.
- **Cat 2 (Anatomy)**: Establishes the factual ground that the critique stands on — what mammalian vagal anatomy actually looks like.
- **Cat 4 (Autism vagal tone evidence)**: The downstream empirical question — whether autistic HRV findings constitute a deficit of "ventral vagal" function or are better described in a different framework.
- **Cat 9 (Alternative models)**: Neurovisceral Integration, predictive processing/active inference, allostasis, theory of constructed emotion — frameworks that explain the same data without PVT's contested claims.
- **Cat 5 (Burnout)**: The critique flows downstream: if dorsal-vagal-as-freezing is empirically weak, then "autistic shutdown = dorsal vagal collapse" inherits that weakness as mechanism story.
- **Cat 8 (Wellness industry)**: A scientifically contested theory plus a billion-dollar commercial wellness ecosystem creates particular incentives to defend the framework regardless of where the science lands.

## Research Gaps

1. **Strain et al. 2024 optogenetics**: The Polyvagal Institute cites this as new evidence. The specific findings, scope, and whether they actually rescue Premises 2/3 deserve direct examination in any future expansion.
2. **The "systems-level" reformulation**: Porges's defense relies on PVT being properly understood at a systems level. There's room for a careful conceptual analysis of whether the systems-level reformulation preserves enough content to count as "the theory" or whether it's a different theory with the same name.
3. **Comparable comparative-physiology rebuttal**: To date no comparative-physiology paper has appeared rebutting Monteiro 2018's lungfish findings. If one appeared, it would substantially shift the empirical picture.
4. **Clinical RCT data on PVT-specific interventions**: Even if anatomy is contested, an empirical question remains — do PVT-derived interventions (SSP, etc.) outperform comparable interventions whose theoretical basis isn't PVT? That sits in Cat 6 (interventions evidence base) more than here.
