# Spoken AAC: How AI is Giving a Voice to Those Who Cannot Speak

Imagine knowing exactly what you want to say -- every thought fully formed, every word on the tip of your tongue -- but being physically unable to speak a single syllable. For millions of people living with ALS, aphasia from stroke, non-speaking autism, and other conditions that rob them of speech, this is daily reality. The AAC (augmentative and alternative communication) industry has long offered solutions, but many of them feel like relics of a different era: clunky symbol boards, robotic voices, and rigid preset vocabularies that force users to communicate in someone else's words rather than their own.

Spoken AAC is a different kind of communication tool. Built on a large language model that learns how each individual user communicates, Spoken lets people type or tap to build sentences that are read aloud in natural-sounding voices -- including, remarkably, clones of their own voice. It is text-based rather than symbol-based, cross-platform rather than iOS-only, and powered by AI that gets smarter with every conversation. In a market dominated by apps designed primarily for children, Spoken has carved out a distinctive niche as the AAC solution built for literate adults who want to communicate freely, quickly, and in their own way.

## A Personal Mission: The Origin Story

Spoken's origin is deeply personal. In 2015, founder Michael Bond watched his grandmother suffer a stroke that left her with severe aphasia. Her mind was intact -- she understood everything happening around her -- but she had completely lost the ability to speak. When Bond looked for modern tools to help her communicate, he was stunned by what he found: speech therapy tools that, in his assessment, had not significantly evolved in 50 years.

Bond decided to build something better. That same year, he entered the Start-Up Chile accelerator program with his early concept for Spoken. At the program's Demo Day in November 2015, Spoken won first place against 18 competing startups, validating Bond's vision that AI-powered communication technology could fill a critical gap in the AAC landscape.

Development continued over the following years, with the app officially launching in 2019. In February 2022, Spoken joined AlphaLab Health, a startup accelerator run by Allegheny Health Network and Innovation Works in Pittsburgh. This partnership gave Bond's team access to clinical resources, healthcare industry mentorship, and the funding needed to accelerate development of the app's AI capabilities.

## The AI Engine: How Spoken's Predictions Work

At the heart of Spoken is a large language model (LLM) -- the same class of AI technology that powers tools like smartphone autocomplete, but significantly more sophisticated and purpose-built for AAC communication. Understanding how this technology works reveals why Spoken represents a genuine leap forward in assistive communication.

### Predictive Text That Actually Learns

When a user opens Spoken, they see a row of word prediction buttons above their text composition area. These are not static suggestions pulled from a generic dictionary. Instead, Spoken's LLM analyzes the context of what the user has already typed and predicts the most likely next words based on natural language patterns.

What makes this genuinely powerful is personalization. The system learns from each user's individual communication history. If a user frequently talks about their dog named "Biscuit" or regularly orders the same coffee drink, Spoken's predictions begin to prioritize those words and phrases. The more someone uses the app, the fewer taps it takes to say what they want to say -- a critical efficiency gain for people who may have limited motor function in addition to speech loss.

As one researcher put it in the title of a major AAC study: "The less I type, the better." Every keystroke saved is not just a convenience but a reduction in fatigue, frustration, and communication delay.

### Teaching Spoken About Yourself

Beyond passive learning, Spoken offers active personalization. Users can complete a short survey that teaches the app about their life -- names of family members, favorite places, common topics of conversation. This information is fed into the prediction model, allowing Spoken to suggest contextually relevant words from the very first conversation.

Users can also add custom vocabulary through the "Add Words" menu, and even paste text from articles or emails so Spoken can extract and learn unfamiliar terms. This flexibility means the app's vocabulary is truly unlimited -- users are never constrained to a preset word list.

### Incognito Mode: Privacy in Prediction

One thoughtful feature is Incognito Mode, introduced in late 2025. This allows users to discuss sensitive topics -- medical symptoms, personal frustrations, private matters -- without those words being incorporated into the prediction model. It is a recognition that AAC users deserve the same communication privacy that speaking people take for granted.

## Natural Voices: Beyond the Robot

One of the most common complaints about traditional AAC devices is their robotic, synthetic-sounding voices. For someone who has lost the ability to speak, being forced to communicate through a voice that sounds nothing like a human being adds another layer of indignity to an already difficult situation.

Spoken has invested heavily in voice quality. The app offers a wide selection of lifelike, customizable voices covering a variety of accents and identities. Recent additions include voices named Hawthorn, Jessamine, Lilac, Lotus (introducing the app's first Indian accent), and Tawari (its first New Zealand accent). All voices feature adjustable speed and pitch, allowing users to find a vocal identity that feels authentic to them.

### Voice Cloning: Speaking in Your Own Voice

Perhaps the most emotionally significant feature is voice cloning through ElevenLabs integration, introduced in version 1.9.3. Users who have recordings of their natural voice -- even just 10 seconds of audio -- can create a synthetic clone that allows the app to speak in something closely resembling their own voice. For someone with ALS who is gradually losing the ability to speak, the ability to "bank" their voice and continue using it through Spoken is profoundly meaningful.

For users who do not have voice recordings or prefer a free alternative, Apple Personal Voice offers a similar capability built into iOS, iPadOS, and macOS. While the quality is lower than ElevenLabs, it works offline and requires no additional subscription.

## Four Ways to Communicate

Spoken is not a one-size-fits-all input system. The app offers four distinct input methods, recognizing that different users have different abilities and preferences:

**Tap-to-Type** is the primary method. Users tap predictive word buttons to build sentences quickly, with the LLM continuously refining suggestions based on context. This is the fastest method for most users and requires the least motor precision.

**Keyboard Typing** provides standard text input through the device's on-screen or external keyboard. This is ideal for users who are comfortable typing and want maximum control over their word choices.

**Handwriting Recognition** offers a canvas where users can write words by hand. The app recognizes handwritten text and converts it to typed text that can be spoken aloud. This can be more intuitive for some users than typing.

**Drawing Recognition** is perhaps the most innovative input method. The drawing canvas, trained on data from Google's Quick, Draw! game, can recognize simple sketches and translate them into words. If a user draws a rough cup shape, Spoken can identify it as "cup." This is particularly useful for people who have difficulty recalling the names of objects but can visualize them -- a common symptom in certain types of aphasia.

## Cross-Platform Availability

In a market where many leading AAC apps -- including Proloquo2Go and TouchChat -- are available exclusively on iOS, Spoken stands out by supporting iOS, Android, and Mac. This cross-platform availability is more than a convenience; it is a matter of accessibility and equity. Not every family can afford an iPad, and not every institution provides iOS devices. By supporting Android, Spoken makes AI-powered AAC available to a significantly broader population.

The Mac version extends Spoken's utility to desktop computing environments, allowing users to communicate in professional settings, during video calls, or while working at a computer.

## Accessibility Built In

Spoken includes a range of accessibility features designed for users with varying physical and cognitive needs:

- **Large Print** increases text size throughout the app for users with visual impairments.
- **Dark Mode** provides a darker color scheme that reduces eye strain, particularly useful for users who spend extended time looking at their device.
- **Word Dividers** add visual separation between prediction buttons, making it easier for users with motor impairments to tap the correct word.
- **Scroll Arrows** provide large, tap-friendly controls for navigating through prediction options.
- **Show Large (Full Screen Display)** displays the composed sentence at full-screen size with large type, enabling communication in noisy environments or across a room.
- **Alert & Speak** plays an attention-getting sound before the message is spoken, helping the user get their communication partner's attention.
- **Eye Tracking** compatibility with iOS eye tracking means users with severe motor impairments can potentially operate the app with eye movements alone.

## Who Is Spoken For?

Spoken is explicitly designed for literate teenagers and adults. Its primary target conditions include:

**ALS (Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis)**: As ALS progressively weakens the muscles used for speech, patients need AAC tools that are fast, efficient, and can adapt as their abilities change. Spoken's predictive text reduces the physical effort of communication, and voice cloning allows users to preserve their vocal identity.

**Aphasia (from Stroke or Brain Injury)**: People with aphasia often retain the ability to read and write even when speech is impaired. Spoken's text-based approach aligns well with these preserved abilities, and the drawing recognition feature can help when word retrieval is difficult.

**Non-Speaking Autism**: Literate autistic adults and teenagers who do not use spoken language can use Spoken as a primary communication tool without being limited to picture symbols designed for children.

**Other Conditions**: Apraxia of speech, selective mutism, cerebral palsy, and Parkinson's disease are also target conditions, though the degree of benefit depends on the individual's literacy level and motor capabilities.

### Who Is Spoken NOT For?

It is equally important to understand Spoken's limitations in terms of target users. The app is **not suitable for pre-literate users** -- young children or individuals who have not yet developed reading and writing skills. These users need symbol-based AAC systems like Proloquo2Go, TouchChat, or LAMP Words for Life, which use picture grids and motor planning rather than text.

Spoken is also **not ideal for users with significant cognitive impairments** that affect literacy comprehension. And users with **severe motor impairments** who cannot type, tap, write, or use eye tracking may find the app's input methods insufficient.

## Pricing: Free to Speak, Premium to Thrive

Spoken offers a tiered pricing model that balances accessibility with sustainability:

### Free Tier
The free version provides core AAC functionality: the ability to type words and have them spoken aloud. It includes basic word predictions and the device's default text-to-speech voice. This ensures that basic communication is never gated behind a paywall.

### Premium Trial
New users automatically receive a premium trial (approximately 600 spoken words) with no payment information required. This allows users to experience the full feature set before deciding whether to subscribe.

### Premium Subscription

| Platform | Monthly | Annual | Lifetime |
|----------|---------|--------|----------|
| **iOS** | $12.99 | $99.99 | $249.99 |
| **Android** | $6.99 | $59.99 | $99.99 |

The pricing disparity between iOS and Android is notable. Android users pay roughly half the iOS price at every tier -- the lifetime Android price ($99.99) is lower than the annual iOS price ($99.99). This may reflect platform-specific cost structures (Apple's 30% App Store commission) or a deliberate strategy to make the app more accessible on the more affordable platform.

Premium unlocks personalized predictions, multiple high-quality voices, custom vocabulary, saved phrases, incognito mode, voice cloning integration, and all accessibility features.

## How Spoken Compares to Other AAC Apps

Understanding Spoken's position requires comparing it to the broader AAC landscape:

| Feature | Spoken | Proloquo2Go | TouchChat | Grid |
|---------|--------|-------------|-----------|------|
| **Approach** | Text-based | Symbol-based | Symbol + Text | Symbol + Text |
| **AI/Predictions** | LLM-powered | Basic frequency | Limited | Some prediction |
| **Platform** | iOS, Android, Mac | iOS only | iOS only | Windows, iOS |
| **Target Users** | Literate adults | All ages | All ages | All ages |
| **Vocabulary** | Unlimited text | Pre-built grids | Pre-built + custom | Pre-built + custom |
| **Setup Time** | Minutes | Hours to days | Hours to days | Hours to days |
| **Price** | Free-$249.99 | ~$250 one-time | ~$300 one-time | ~$500 |
| **Voice Quality** | Natural + cloning | Natural | Natural | Natural |
| **Symbol Support** | Limited icons | Extensive | Extensive | Extensive |

Spoken's key differentiator is that it requires virtually no setup. Where symbol-based apps often need an SLP (speech-language pathologist) to customize communication boards over multiple sessions, a user can download Spoken and begin communicating immediately. The trade-off is that Spoken only works for people who can read and type.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

### Strengths

- **AI That Actually Learns**: Unlike static prediction systems, Spoken's LLM genuinely adapts to individual communication patterns
- **Cross-Platform**: The only major AAC app available on iOS, Android, and Mac
- **Voice Cloning**: ElevenLabs integration and Apple Personal Voice let users communicate in something close to their own voice
- **No Setup Required**: Download and start communicating immediately
- **Free Basic Tier**: Core communication is never gated behind payment
- **Modern Design**: Clean, adult-appropriate interface (not childish symbol boards)
- **Multiple Input Methods**: Typing, tapping, handwriting, and drawing recognition
- **Privacy Features**: Incognito mode for sensitive conversations
- **Active Development**: Regular updates with new voices, features, and improvements

### Weaknesses

- **Literacy Required**: Completely inaccessible to pre-literate users or those with significant cognitive impairments
- **No Symbol Communication**: Cannot serve users who need picture-based AAC
- **Internet Needed for Some Features**: Voice cloning requires connectivity and a separate ElevenLabs subscription
- **Subscription Cost**: While a free tier exists, the most valuable features require ongoing payment
- **iOS Premium Pricing**: iOS users pay substantially more than Android users for identical features
- **Limited Evidence Base**: As a relatively new product, lacks the extensive clinical research behind established AAC systems
- **AI Identity Gap**: Research suggests LLM predictions may not fully capture users' personal communication style and identity
- **Motor Demand**: Typing and tapping may be challenging for users with significant motor impairments

## User Reception

Spoken has received generally positive reviews from users. On the Educational App Store, it holds a 4.5-star rating. On Google Play, it has approximately 3.83 out of 5 stars from around 290 ratings.

User testimonials highlight the app's utility for sudden speech loss. One App Store reviewer wrote about their wife who had a brain tumor removed and lost speech temporarily: "She had full comprehension and knew the words to say she just couldn't speak them." Spoken provided a bridge during recovery, allowing her to communicate her thoughts while her brain healed.

The app has also been recognized by the National Aphasia Association as a resource for people living with aphasia, and has been featured in SLP (speech-language pathology) resource guides at multiple universities.

## The Road Ahead

Spoken continues to evolve rapidly. Recent updates in late 2025 introduced ElevenLabs voice cloning, Apple Personal Voice support, autocorrect, incognito mode, and expanded voice options with greater global accent diversity. The trend is clear: Spoken is investing in making communication faster, more natural, and more personal.

The broader trajectory of AI in AAC is promising. Research published at CHI 2023 explored how AI language models can enhance communication for AAC users, finding that context-aware predictions and personalization significantly reduce the effort required to communicate. As large language models continue to improve, apps like Spoken stand to benefit directly from advances in natural language processing.

The challenge ahead is balancing AI efficiency with personal identity. Users want to communicate quickly, but they also want to sound like themselves -- not like an algorithm's best guess at what a "typical" person would say. Spoken's approach of learning from individual speech patterns rather than relying solely on generic language models positions it well for this balance, but the gap between AI-generated suggestions and authentic personal voice remains an active area of research and development.

## Key Takeaways

- Spoken AAC is a text-based, AI-powered communication app designed for literate adults and teenagers who have lost the ability to speak
- Its large language model learns from each user's individual communication patterns, making predictions faster and more accurate over time
- Voice cloning through ElevenLabs allows users to communicate in something resembling their own voice
- Cross-platform support (iOS, Android, Mac) sets it apart from iOS-only competitors
- A free tier ensures basic communication is always accessible, with premium features available via subscription
- The app is not suitable for pre-literate users or those requiring symbol-based AAC
- Spoken fills a critical niche for adults with ALS, aphasia, stroke, and non-speaking autism who want to communicate freely in their own words

## Conclusion

Spoken AAC represents a meaningful evolution in assistive communication technology. By applying modern AI -- specifically large language models and neural text-to-speech -- to a problem that has historically been addressed with static, one-size-fits-all tools, Spoken has created something genuinely different: an AAC app that learns, adapts, and gets better the more it is used.

It is not the right tool for everyone. Pre-literate users, young children, and people who need symbol-based communication will be better served by established alternatives. But for literate adults who have lost the ability to speak and want to communicate naturally, quickly, and in their own voice, Spoken offers something that was simply not available a few years ago.

Michael Bond set out to build the tool his grandmother needed. In doing so, he may have helped define what the next generation of AAC technology looks like: personal, adaptive, and powered by AI that treats every user as an individual.
