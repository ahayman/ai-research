# Existing Symbol Communication Systems: What Works, What Doesn't, and What They Teach Us

---

## 1. Why Study the Predecessors?

Before designing a new visual communication system, there is a necessary discipline: study the ones that already exist. Not for academic completeness, but because these systems represent decades of real-world deployment -- decades of discovering what actually happens when humans try to communicate through visual symbols. The failures are as instructive as the successes.

Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC) encompasses the methods and technologies that supplement or replace speech for people who cannot rely on verbal language. The populations served include individuals with cerebral palsy, autism, intellectual disabilities, aphasia after stroke, and progressive neurological conditions such as ALS. The symbol systems at the heart of AAC -- PECS, PCS, Widgit, Makaton, Blissymbolics, SymbolStix -- each embody different design philosophies and, critically, different trade-offs between ease of learning and power of expression.

The accumulated evidence from this field yields one finding that overturns intuition and reshapes everything: the most important words for communication are not the ones you can easily draw a picture of.

## 2. The Six Major Systems

### PECS: Teaching Communication, Not Just Symbols

The Picture Exchange Communication System, developed by Andy Bondy and Lori Frost in 1985 at the Delaware Autism Program, is not primarily a symbol system at all. It is a behavioral teaching methodology that can use any pictures or symbols. Its innovation is in the pragmatics of communication -- teaching people who do not spontaneously initiate communication to do exactly that.

This distinction is critical and often missed. Most AAC systems focus on the symbols -- their design, their vocabulary, their visual properties. PECS focuses on the communicative act itself. Before any symbol design matters, the user must learn that communicating is a thing you do, that it produces results, and that you can initiate it.

PECS operates through six phases, each building on the last. Phase 1 teaches the most fundamental communicative act: physically handing a picture to another person to request something. No verbal prompts are used -- the system avoids creating prompt dependence. The learner discovers through physical experience that giving a symbol to a partner produces a desired result. Phase 2 expands this -- the learner must now travel to find their communication book and travel to find a partner, building persistence and motivation. This is not about symbol complexity; it is about communicative initiative. Phase 3 introduces discrimination -- choosing the correct picture from several options. Phase 4 introduces syntax: constructing sentences using an "I want" starter strip followed by picture selections. This is the first moment where anything resembling grammar appears -- and it is Phase 4 of 6, not Phase 1. Phase 5 teaches responsive communication -- answering "What do you want?" using the sentence strip. Phase 6 reaches spontaneous commenting: "I see a bird," "I hear music." Only in Phase 6 does the user move beyond requesting into describing and sharing.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-3-pecs-six-phases.html -->

The progression reveals a design principle that applies to any visual communication system: communicative function should drive vocabulary introduction, not the other way around. PECS does not start with "here are 200 symbols to learn." It starts with "here is how to ask for something you want." The vocabulary expands as communicative competence grows.

The evidence base for PECS is strong -- it is classified as an evidence-based practice for autism by multiple systematic reviews. Phase 1 can be achieved in hours, not weeks. A visual language that requires months of study before enabling a single communicative act has already failed the PECS test.

### PCS: The Market Leader

Picture Communication Symbols, originally developed by Roxie Johnson in the early 1980s and now owned by Tobii Dynavox, is the dominant symbol set in AAC worldwide. With over 79,000 symbols, PCS provides a pictorial symbol for virtually any common word -- a picture of a cup for "cup," a picture of running for "run." PCS symbols appear in 6 of the top 13+ iOS symbol-based AAC apps and have been used by millions of people over more than 30 years.

The design philosophy is maximum pictorial clarity. Make each symbol as immediately recognizable as possible. This works excellently for concrete nouns and simple actions. But it encounters a hard wall when it reaches abstract concepts: how do you draw "if"? How do you draw "because"? How do you draw "although"? PCS handles these through arbitrary symbolic conventions -- text labels, abstract shapes -- that must be memorized rather than visually interpreted. The result is a system with enormous vocabulary breadth but no compositional depth. Every new concept requires a new, dedicated symbol designed by the publisher. Users cannot create novel symbols by combining existing ones.

### Widgit: Rules You Can See

Widgit, originally developed as Rebus Symbols and now dominant in UK educational settings, takes a different approach. Where PCS maximizes pictoriality, Widgit maximizes visual consistency. Symbols are designed according to rules: related concepts share visual elements, and consistent conventions signal semantic relationships. A user who learns that a specific visual element indicates "communication" can partially interpret any unfamiliar symbol containing that element.

This is the compositionality principle in embryonic form. Widgit does not achieve full compositionality -- users cannot freely combine base elements to create arbitrary new meanings. But it demonstrates that rule-based visual design enables generalization. A user can encounter a symbol they have never seen and make an educated guess about its meaning by recognizing familiar sub-elements. This is what distinguishes Widgit from PCS: the ability to infer rather than merely recall.

### Makaton: Three Channels at Once

Makaton, developed by Margaret Walker, Kathy Johnston, and Tony Cornforth in the 1970s, is distinctive for its multimodal approach. It uses three channels simultaneously: speech (full sentences spoken aloud), signs (key words signed using signs derived from British Sign Language, in English grammatical order rather than BSL grammar), and symbols (pictures reinforcing the spoken and signed message). This triple-coded delivery means that information reaches the learner through auditory, visual-gestural, and visual-graphic channels at the same time.

The theoretical basis for this approach connects directly to Paivio's dual coding theory: information encoded through multiple channels creates stronger, more retrievable memory traces than single-channel encoding. Makaton essentially implements triple coding. If a concept is received through speech, through a visible gesture, and through a visual symbol simultaneously, it has three independent retrieval paths in memory. Even partial success in one modality -- a sign without remembering the symbol, or a symbol without the sign -- can carry the meaning forward.

The vocabulary is structured in stages. The core vocabulary of approximately 450 concepts is organized across 8 stages of increasing complexity. Stage 1 covers the most immediately functional needs: eat, drink, more, stop, yes, no. These are not random selections -- they are the words that produce the most immediate functional benefit. Later stages introduce time, emotions, social concepts, and abstract ideas. A resource vocabulary of over 11,000 additional concepts provides specialized terms for individual needs.

Makaton has achieved significant scale: it reports over 1 million users worldwide across more than 40 countries, including individuals with cognitive impairments, autism, Down syndrome, specific language impairment, and acquired neurological disorders such as stroke and dementia. (The million-user figure comes from organizational claims and has not been independently audited, but the system's widespread institutional adoption in UK educational and care settings is well documented.)

Makaton's staged learning progression -- from most functional to most abstract -- is a proven model. And its alignment with core vocabulary research is not accidental: the 450 core concepts map closely to the 200-400 words that AAC research identifies as covering 80% of daily communication. The lesson: vocabulary prioritization should be driven by communicative frequency data, not by intuition about what is "basic."

The multimodal principle also has a direct implication for the visual iconographic language: it should not be designed as a standalone, visual-only system. A visual language used alongside speech and gesture -- with communication partners speaking, signing key concepts, and referencing visual symbols -- will be more effective than any single-channel system. The goal is adding a modality, not replacing existing ones.

### Blissymbolics: The Closest Precedent

Blissymbolics stands apart from every other AAC symbol system in one critical respect: it has genuine compositional semantics. Created by Charles K. Bliss (born Karl Kasiel Blitz) in 1949 as "Semantography" -- a universal visual language -- it was adopted for AAC in 1971 by Shirley McNaughton at the Ontario Crippled Children's Centre and is now used in 33 or more countries.

The system uses approximately 120 base elements -- geometric shapes, directional markers, and grammatical indicators -- to compose over 5,000 authorized symbols. The composition is semantic, not arbitrary. "Feeling" is represented by a heart symbol. "Happy" is heart + upward indicator (HAPPY IS UP). "Sad" is heart + downward indicator (SAD IS DOWN). "Protection" is enclosure + person (a person inside a bounded region). The same primary metaphors that the visual iconographic language proposes as its foundation are already present in Blissymbolics, though used ad hoc rather than systematically.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-4-blissymbolics-composition.html -->

Blissymbolics also has a grammatical indicator system. An inverted V placed above a symbol marks it as an action (verb). A small square marks it as a thing (noun). A small V marks it as a description (adjective). Evaluation indicators mark positive or negative valence. This enables the same base elements to function in different grammatical roles -- a degree of grammatical flexibility that no other AAC symbol system achieves.

But Blissymbolics also reveals the challenges. Its base elements are abstract geometric shapes that must be memorized -- they are not grounded in embodied experience the way image schemas are. The learning burden is higher than for pictorial systems like PCS. Interpretation disputes arise because Bliss did not provide systematic definitions for all possible combinations. And the market reality is stark: PCS, with its simpler pictorial approach, dominates. Blissymbolics is more linguistically powerful but harder to learn, and in the real world of AAC deployment, learnability often wins.

### SymbolStix and Others

SymbolStix, an evidence-based symbol set featuring over 15,000 stick-figure style symbols emphasizing action and context, serves the broader AAC market alongside PCS. Its stick-figure aesthetic focuses attention on what the person in the symbol is doing rather than on incidental visual detail -- a design choice that prioritizes action recognition over object recognition.

Open-source alternatives have emerged to address gaps in the commercial market. ARASAAC (from the Aragonese Centre for Augmentative and Alternative Communication) provides over 12,000 symbols widely used in Spanish-speaking countries and increasingly globally. Its open-source model means that any developer can integrate ARASAAC symbols into new software without licensing fees -- a significant factor for adoption in resource-limited settings.

Mulberry Symbols, with approximately 3,400 symbols, was designed specifically for adult AAC users. This addresses a real and persistent problem: many AAC symbol sets were designed with children in mind, and their aesthetic -- rounded, colorful, simplified -- can feel infantilizing to adult users. An adult who acquired a communication disability through stroke or ALS should not have to communicate through symbols that look like they belong in a preschool classroom. The Mulberry project explicitly targets age-appropriate visual design.

The diversity of symbol sets in the AAC ecosystem illustrates a broader point: one size does not fit all. Different user populations have different needs, different aesthetic preferences, different cognitive profiles, and different functional requirements. A visual iconographic language should be designed with this diversity in mind -- providing a compositional core that can be visually styled for different user groups without changing the underlying semantic structure.

## 3. The Core Vocabulary Discovery

The single most important finding from AAC research for any visual language project is this: approximately 200-400 high-frequency words account for 80% of daily communication. And these words are not what most people would guess.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-2-core-vocabulary-funnel.html -->

The research is consistent across speakers, ages, environments, and activities. Approximately 50 words cover 40-50% of everything people say. 100 words cover about 60%. 200-400 words cover about 80%. A study found 203 high-frequency words showed 80.62% commonality across speakers.

The counterintuitive finding: core vocabulary is dominated by verbs, adjectives, pronouns, and prepositions -- not nouns. Words like "want," "go," "more," "not," "help," "like," "is," "can," "with" appear at the top of every frequency list. Nouns -- the words that are easiest to draw pictures of (apple, dog, car, house) -- are overwhelmingly fringe vocabulary: highly variable across individuals, contexts, and topics.

The implications are direct and non-negotiable. A visual language that starts with easy-to-picture concrete objects will produce users who can label things but cannot communicate. "Apple" labels one object. "Want" enables a thousand requests: want apple, want drink, want go, want more, want help, want stop. The first 200 symbols must cover the functional words that make sentences possible, even though these are the hardest words to represent visually.

This finding inverts the naive design impulse. It is natural to start with what is easy to depict. The evidence demands starting with what is most communicatively powerful. Any visual language project that ignores this evidence will repeat mistakes that AAC has already learned to avoid.

## 4. The Fundamental Trade-Off

Across all six systems, a single tension defines the landscape: iconicity trades off against compositionality.

Iconicity means visual transparency -- how easily a symbol can be recognized without training. PCS maximizes this: a picture of a cup is recognizable as "cup" even to someone who has never seen the symbol before. Emoji push iconicity to its extreme.

Compositionality means productive power -- the ability to build new meanings from existing parts according to systematic rules. Blissymbolics maximizes this: a user who knows 120 base elements can, in principle, compose symbols for concepts they have never encountered.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-5-iconicity-compositionality-tradeoff.html -->

Current systems cluster along a frontier: more iconic tends to mean less compositional. PCS sits at one end (high iconicity, zero compositionality). Blissymbolics sits at the other (low iconicity, full compositionality). Widgit occupies the middle ground (moderate iconicity, limited compositionality).

The visual iconographic language aims for a position that no current system occupies: high compositionality with moderate iconicity. The mechanism for achieving this is grounding: building base symbols not from arbitrary geometric shapes (Blissymbolics' approach, which sacrifices iconicity for compositionality) but from image schemas -- the pre-linguistic spatial-relational structures (CONTAINER, PATH, FORCE, BALANCE) that arise from universal bodily experience. If image schemas are genuinely experientially universal, then symbols grounded in them should be more learnable than arbitrary shapes while retaining the ability to compose systematically.

Whether this is achievable remains an empirical question. No image-schema-based visual symbol system has been tested. But the existing landscape demonstrates that the trade-off space exists and that no current system occupies the target position. The gap is real.

## 5. AAC Technology and the Digital Requirement

Modern AAC operates through a layered technology stack. At the base, symbol sets (PCS, Widgit, SymbolStix) provide the visual vocabulary. Software platforms (Proloquo2Go, TD Snap, Grid 3) present these symbols for selection. Input methods range from direct touch on tablets, through switch scanning for users with limited motor control, to eye-tracking systems (Tobii Dynavox) for users with severe motor impairments who select symbols by gaze fixation.

The output side converts symbol selections into synthesized speech, displayed text, or printed symbols. The complete cycle -- from symbol selection through software processing to speech output -- enables communication between a symbol user and a non-symbol-literate partner.

Any new visual language must integrate with this existing ecosystem. Digital-first design is not optional: it is a hard constraint. The symbols must be renderable on any screen (SVG is the natural format). Input methods must accommodate touch, switch scanning, eye tracking, and keyboard. Speech synthesis bridging -- converting visual compositions to spoken language for non-users -- is a practical necessity, not a luxury.

The technology dimension also introduces a requirement that symbol design alone cannot address: speed. Communication through symbol selection is inherently slower than speech. Experienced AAC users achieve perhaps 10-20 words per minute compared to 125-150 for typical speech. Predictive systems, frequency-based symbol ordering, and motor-optimized grid layouts all attempt to reduce this gap. A compositional visual language introduces additional possibilities -- a user who can compose novel meanings from base elements does not need to navigate through screens of pre-made symbols to find the right one -- but also additional challenges, since composition takes time.

## 5a. What People Get Wrong

Several persistent misconceptions about AAC symbol systems distort how people evaluate visual communication:

**"Symbol-based communication is not real language."** Most AAC symbol systems are less expressive than natural language, but this reflects the design of the systems, not the cognitive capacity of the users. Blissymbolics demonstrates genuine compositional semantics. And AAC research consistently shows that users' communicative competence exceeds what their symbol system allows them to express -- the bottleneck is in the tool, not the person.

**"Pictorial symbols are universally understood."** Even highly pictorial symbols require learning. Research shows that symbol recognition rates vary significantly across cultures, ages, and cognitive profiles. A picture of a mailbox means "mail" in countries with a specific mailbox shape and nothing in countries where mail delivery looks different. "Universal" recognizability is a design aspiration, not an achieved reality.

**"Using symbol communication prevents speech development."** Research consistently shows the opposite: AAC use does not inhibit speech development and may facilitate it. Makaton's multimodal approach explicitly aims to support speech development. The evidence on this point is clear enough that professional organizations (ASHA, RCSLT) have issued position statements against withholding AAC out of fear it will prevent speech.

**"A nonspeaking person has nothing to say."** The Raven's Matrices finding (explored in depth in Category 11) demonstrates that nonspeaking autistic individuals may possess rich cognitive lives invisible through the verbal channel. Many nonspeaking individuals, when given appropriate communication tools, demonstrate sophisticated thought, humor, and social understanding.

## 6. What These Systems Teach

### Lesson 1: Start Functional, Not Comprehensive

PECS achieves its first communicative act in hours. Makaton starts with eat, drink, more, stop. The most successful AAC systems get users communicating immediately with a tiny vocabulary, then expand. A visual language that requires learning 500 symbols before the first useful expression is already dead.

### Lesson 2: Core Vocabulary First, Always

The 200-400 most communicatively powerful words are verbs, prepositions, pronouns, and adjectives. Not nouns. Design for communicative power, not pictorial ease.

### Lesson 3: Compositionality Is the Prize

Blissymbolics is the only AAC symbol system that enables users to express concepts they have never been explicitly taught symbols for. This productive power -- the ability to compose novel meanings from known parts -- is what separates a symbol set from a language. It is the core engineering challenge.

### Lesson 4: Multimodal Beats Unimodal

Makaton's triple coding (speech + sign + symbol) demonstrates that multiple channels reinforce each other. A visual language used alongside speech and gesture, not instead of them, will be more effective than a visual-only system.

### Lesson 5: Grounding Matters

Blissymbolics already uses metaphorical grounding in some compositions (HAPPY IS UP, SAD IS DOWN). But it uses it inconsistently, alongside arbitrary geometric conventions. The visual iconographic language proposes to systematize this -- to build the entire composition system on documented primary metaphors and image schemas. If successful, this would make Blissymbolics' compositional power available with lower learning cost.

### Lesson 6: The Market Will Choose Easy Over Powerful

PCS dominates not because it is the best-designed system but because it is the easiest to learn. Blissymbolics is more powerful but harder. Any visual language that cannot compete on learnability will remain a niche tool regardless of its theoretical elegance. This is the most uncomfortable lesson from AAC history, because it means that design quality alone does not determine adoption. Blissymbolics is, by any linguistic measure, a better symbol system than PCS. It is also less widely used. The market chose accessibility over power. A visual iconographic language must find a way to offer both -- or accept that it will remain in the Blissymbolics tier of adoption: respected, influential, but not dominant.

### Lesson 7: Bilingual Bridging Is Non-Negotiable

Every AAC system that succeeds in practice includes a mechanism for communication with non-users. PCS users can point to symbols while a communication partner reads the labels. Blissymbolics can be glossed in English text. Makaton signs are used alongside speech. A visual language that can only be read by other visual language users is a private code, not a communication system. Speech synthesis bridging -- the ability to convert a composed visual expression into spoken or written language that anyone can understand -- is a practical requirement for real-world deployment.

## 7. Implications for the Visual Language Project

The evidence from AAC systems provides a concrete set of requirements:

1. **Minimum viable vocabulary**: 200-400 concepts covering core vocabulary. This is non-negotiable.
2. **Priority order**: Verbs and function words first; nouns are expandable fringe vocabulary.
3. **Staged learning**: Follow the PECS/Makaton model -- immediate functional communication from day one.
4. **Compositional architecture**: Blissymbolics proves compositionality is possible; image schemas may make it more learnable.
5. **Digital-first**: SVG symbols, input method editors, speech synthesis bridging, AAC platform compatibility.
6. **Multimodal design**: Visual language alongside speech and gesture, not replacing them.
7. **Learnability is a survival requirement**: If it is harder to learn than PCS without demonstrably greater expressive power, it will not be adopted.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **AAC** | Augmentative and Alternative Communication -- methods supplementing or replacing speech |
| **SGD** | Speech-Generating Device -- hardware producing synthesized speech from symbol selection |
| **PECS** | Picture Exchange Communication System -- a behavioral teaching protocol for communication (Bondy & Frost, 1985) |
| **PCS** | Picture Communication Symbols -- Tobii Dynavox's pictorial symbol set (79,000+ symbols; creator: Roxie Johnson) |
| **Core Vocabulary** | The 200-400 high-frequency words accounting for ~80% of daily communication |
| **Fringe Vocabulary** | Context-specific, personally relevant words (mostly nouns) supplementing core vocabulary |
