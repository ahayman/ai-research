# Category 16: Common Ground and Grounding

## The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

---

## How to Read This Document

Every pragmatic skill in this hub depends on one underlying ability: estimating what the other person **knows, believes, and assumes** -- and calibrating your communication accordingly. This estimation is what linguists call **common ground**, and the collaborative process of building and verifying it is called **grounding**.

Common ground is the infrastructure beneath everything else. Presuppositions (Category 9) are assumptions about what's in common ground. Audience design (Category 12) is calibrating to common ground. Register (Category 7) is selected based on estimated common ground. Even hedging (Category 6) is partly about signaling uncertainty about common ground ("I think" = "I'm not sure if we share this assumption").

Understanding common ground as a system -- with sources, mechanisms, and failure modes -- turns the vague instruction "consider what the other person knows" into a structured, actionable process.

---

## 1. What Common Ground Is

### 1.1 Definition

**Common ground** is the sum of the mutual knowledge, mutual beliefs, and mutual assumptions shared by participants in a conversation at any given moment.

The key word is **mutual**. Common ground is not just "I know X and you know X." It's "I know X, you know X, **and we both know that we both know X**." This mutuality is what makes it usable for communication -- you can only rely on shared knowledge if you both know it's shared.

### 1.2 The Three Sources

Clark and Marshall (1981) identified three ways common ground is built:

| Source | What It Provides | Examples |
|--------|-----------------|---------|
| **Community membership** | Knowledge shared by belonging to the same groups | English speakers know what "hello" means; Americans know about Thanksgiving; programmers know what "variable" means |
| **Physical co-presence** | What both participants can currently perceive | You can both see the rain; you can both see the whiteboard; you can both hear the alarm |
| **Linguistic co-presence** | What has been said earlier in this conversation | "As you just mentioned..." / "Going back to what you said about..." |

**Community membership** is the broadest and most stable source. You belong to multiple communities simultaneously (national, linguistic, professional, generational, subcultural), and each contributes assumed shared knowledge.

**Physical co-presence** is the most immediate and reliable. If you can both see it, it's in common ground.

**Linguistic co-presence** grows during the conversation. Every accepted utterance adds to the shared record.

### 1.3 How Common Ground Grows

Common ground is not static. It expands throughout a conversation:

```
Start of conversation: 
  Community CG + Physical CG only

After first exchange:
  + Whatever was just said (and accepted)

After five minutes:
  + Shared references, established topics, mutual 
    understanding of the current discussion

After an hour:
  + Rich shared context: everything discussed, 
    agreed upon, clarified, and jointly established
```

This growth is why conversations between strangers start with more explicit language ("A colleague of mine who works in the marketing department...") and get progressively more abbreviated as the conversation develops ("Yeah, Sarah mentioned that too").

## 2. Grounding: The Process of Establishing Understanding

### 2.1 What Grounding Is

**Grounding** is the collaborative, turn-by-turn process by which speakers and listeners ensure mutual understanding. A message is not "communicated" just because someone said it -- it's communicated only when both parties believe the listener understood what the speaker meant, **to a standard sufficient for current purposes**.

That last phrase matters. Passing the salt requires minimal grounding (a nod and a reach). Confirming a surgical procedure requires maximum grounding (verbal repetition, written records, physical markings).

### 2.2 The Two Phases

Every conversational contribution has two phases:

**Phase 1: Presentation** -- The speaker produces an utterance, designed for THIS specific listener based on estimated common ground.

**Phase 2: Acceptance** -- The listener provides evidence of understanding (or not). The speaker monitors for this evidence. If positive → move on. If negative or absent → repair.

### 2.3 Evidence of Understanding (Strongest to Weakest)

| Level | What the Listener Does | What It Signals | Example |
|-------|----------------------|----------------|---------|
| **Action** | Does what was asked | Full understanding confirmed | You ask them to close the door; they close the door |
| **Demonstration** | Shows understanding by paraphrasing or applying | "So you're saying we should..." | Strong evidence they got the meaning |
| **Relevant next turn** | Continues the conversation on-topic | "And then what happened?" | Evidence they understood enough to contribute |
| **Acknowledgment** | Backchannels: "mm-hmm," "right," "OK" | "I'm following, continue" | Evidence of attention, not necessarily deep understanding |
| **Continued attention** | Keeps looking at speaker, doesn't leave | "I'm still here" | Weakest -- absence of a problem signal, not presence of understanding |

### 2.4 Evidence of Non-Understanding

| Signal | What It Means | What to Do |
|--------|--------------|------------|
| "What?" / "Huh?" / "Sorry?" | Didn't hear or didn't understand | Repeat or rephrase |
| Silence (where a response was expected) | Possible confusion or processing | Check: "Does that make sense?" |
| Inappropriate response | They understood something different | Clarify: "Sorry, I meant..." |
| Confused facial expression | Processing difficulty | Simplify or rephrase |
| "Wait, what do you mean?" | They heard but didn't understand | Explain differently |

### 2.5 The Principle of Least Collaborative Effort

Both parties try to minimize the total work spent achieving mutual understanding. This is why:
- People use pronouns instead of full names (less effort when the referent is clear)
- Expressions get shorter over time ("the coffee shop on the corner of Main and 5th" → "the coffee shop" → "there")
- Shared jargon replaces explanation ("Can you do a PR?" instead of "Can you create a pull request for this code change?")

When grounding is going smoothly, language gets more efficient. When it breaks down, it gets more explicit.

## 3. Audience Design: Calibrating to Common Ground

### 3.1 How Speakers Adjust

Every utterance is **designed for its specific listener**. The same information takes completely different forms depending on the estimated common ground:

| Listener | How You Say It | Why |
|----------|---------------|-----|
| Expert colleague | "Can you review the API endpoint authentication?" | Shared domain knowledge → technical shorthand |
| Non-technical friend | "Can you look at the security thing for the website?" | Less shared domain knowledge → simplified |
| Complete stranger | "I need someone to check whether our website login system is secure." | Minimal shared knowledge → full explanation |

### 3.2 The Refashioning Phenomenon

Clark and Wilkes-Gibbs (1986) showed that when people refer to the same thing repeatedly, their expressions get progressively shorter:

| Reference # | Expression | Why |
|------------|-----------|-----|
| 1st | "The person who looks like they're ice skating" | New to common ground → full description |
| 2nd | "The ice skater" | Now in CG → shortened |
| 3rd | "Her" | Well-established → pronoun |

This compression is evidence that common ground is working. Each successful reference adds to shared knowledge, allowing future references to be more efficient.

### 3.3 The Curse of Knowledge

Once you know something, it's extremely hard to model what it's like NOT to know it. This is the **curse of knowledge** -- and it's one of the most consequential cognitive biases in communication.

Research shows:
- Awareness of the curse doesn't eliminate it
- Financial incentives don't eliminate it
- Even explicit instruction to "imagine you don't know" only partially helps

**Practical strategies:**
- **Before explaining**: Ask what they already know. "How familiar are you with X?"
- **During explaining**: Watch for confusion signals. Offer calibration: "Am I going into too much detail?"
- **After explaining**: Check: "Does that make sense?" (But note: people often say "yes" to avoid seeming slow -- follow up with: "What questions do you have?" which is more effective)

## 4. Common Ground Across Relationship Types

| Relationship | Amount of CG | What to Assume | Common Pitfall |
|-------------|-------------|---------------|---------------|
| **Strangers** | Minimal -- community/cultural CG only | Very little; explain more | Assuming they know things they don't |
| **Acquaintances** | Some shared context | Shared environment/workplace but not personal history | The middle ground is tricky -- what SHOULD they know? |
| **Close friends** | Extensive personal CG | Shared history, in-jokes, shorthand | Assuming they know EVERYTHING (even friends have separate lives) |
| **Professional colleagues** | Domain knowledge shared; personal may not be | Technical jargon is OK; personal references are not | New team members don't have the team's CG yet |
| **Family** | Deep personal CG | Shared history, values, shorthand | Outdated CG -- they may have changed since you last spoke |

**The closeness-communication bias** (Savitsky et al., 2011): Surprisingly, people communicate LESS carefully with close friends and partners -- they overestimate how well they're understood. The intimacy creates a false sense of transparency.

## 5. Common Ground in Digital Communication

Digital communication disrupts the normal CG sources:

| Channel | What's Lost | Compensation Strategy |
|---------|------------|----------------------|
| **Email** | Physical co-presence, real-time feedback | Provide more context; use explicit subject lines |
| **Text/chat** | Prosody, facial expression, timing | Emoji as tone markers; be more explicit about intent |
| **Video call** | Physical co-presence (partial), natural gaze | Verbal acknowledgments replace body language |
| **Forwarded message** | Original context; who was in the original conversation | "For context, this was discussed in..." |
| **Group chat** | Not everyone has read every message | "For those who missed it..." / start with a summary |

**The temporal displacement problem** (connects to Category 8: Deixis): "Today" in an email written Monday means Tuesday if read Tuesday. "This" in a forwarded chain may refer to something the new reader has no context for.

## 6. The Autistic Experience with Common Ground

### 6.1 What Research Shows

**Both groups generate CG, but NT speakers are more efficient** (Karin et al., 2025). The capacity is there; the real-time calibration is the challenge.

**The knowing-vs-tracking gap**: Research consistently suggests autistic individuals may possess the knowledge needed for common ground management but struggle with **spontaneous, real-time deployment**. In scaffolded tasks, performance improves. In unscripted conversation, the demands of simultaneous monitoring (tracking what the listener knows, watching for grounding evidence, adjusting language) are cognitively expensive.

**Overspecification and underspecification**: Autistic speakers may do BOTH:

| Pattern | When It Happens | Why | Social Consequence |
|---------|----------------|-----|-------------------|
| **Overspecification** | Explaining things the listener already knows | Uncertainty about what's shared → defaulting to MORE info | Perceived as condescending, "lecturing" |
| **Underspecification** | Assuming the listener shares expert knowledge | Curse of knowledge + deep expertise | Listener is confused, can't follow |

The pattern often depends on the topic: for areas of deep interest/expertise, underspecification is more common (assuming everyone knows what you know). For general social topics, overspecification may be more common (providing too much background out of uncertainty).

### 6.2 The Double Empathy Perspective

Williams, Wharton & Jagoe (2021) showed that common ground breakdowns in autistic-NT conversation are **bidirectional**. The NT speaker ALSO misjudges what's in common ground:
- NT speakers may assume autistic listeners share social/cultural knowledge they don't
- Autistic speakers may assume NT listeners share domain/technical knowledge they don't
- Both are making CG estimation errors -- the errors are just in different domains

Crompton et al. (2020): Information transfer is equally effective in autistic-autistic and NT-NT pairs. Common ground works FINE within neurotype. The breakdown is specifically at the neurotype boundary.

### 6.3 Reduced Backchanneling Affects Grounding

Because grounding depends on listener feedback (backchannels, acknowledgments), and because autistic listeners produce fewer backchannels (Category 14), the NT speaker may NOT receive adequate grounding evidence. They don't know if they've been understood, so they may repeat, elaborate, or simplify unnecessarily -- which the autistic listener experiences as condescending.

## 7. Practical Toolkit

### 7.1 Estimating Common Ground with a New Partner

| Question to Ask Yourself | What It Tells You |
|--------------------------|-------------------|
| What communities do we share? (profession, culture, age group) | What communal CG exists |
| What can we both currently see/hear/experience? | What perceptual CG exists |
| What have we already discussed? | What linguistic CG exists |
| What is this person likely to know given their background? | Estimated CG |
| What am I unsure about? | Where to CHECK rather than assume |

### 7.2 CG-Checking Phrases

**Before explaining:**
- "How familiar are you with X?"
- "Have you heard of X?"
- "I'm not sure how much background you have on this..."
- "Do you know about X, or should I give some context?"

**During explaining:**
- "Does that make sense so far?"
- "Am I going into too much detail?" (Gives permission to say yes)
- "Should I back up and explain Y first?"
- "What questions do you have?" (More effective than "Do you have questions?" which invites "no")

**When you're the listener and lost:**
- "Can you give me some context on that?"
- "Sorry, I'm not familiar with X -- can you explain?"
- "I want to make sure I'm following -- are you saying [your understanding]?"
- "Can you back up a bit? I got lost at [specific point]."

### 7.3 Managing the Curse of Knowledge

| Strategy | When to Use It |
|----------|---------------|
| **Ask first** | "What do you already know about X?" -- before any explanation |
| **Start broad** | Give the big picture before details |
| **Watch for signals** | Confused face, silence, "uh-huh" without engagement = you've lost them |
| **Offer calibration** | "Am I explaining too much? Or do you want more detail?" |
| **Use analogies** | Connect unfamiliar concepts to familiar ones |
| **Check explicitly** | "Can you tell me back what you understood?" (strongest check) |

### 7.4 The Over/Under Decision

| If You're Unsure Whether They Know Something | Do This |
|----------------------------------------------|---------|
| Low-stakes conversation | Briefly mention it and watch for recognition signals |
| High-stakes conversation | Ask: "Are you familiar with X?" |
| You're explaining YOUR expertise | Assume they know less than you think; watch for boredom signals |
| They're explaining THEIR expertise | Assume they know more than you; ask questions rather than re-explaining |

## 8. Key Takeaways

1. **Common ground is the infrastructure beneath all pragmatics.** Presupposition, implicature, register, audience design, and politeness all depend on estimating what's shared.

2. **Three sources: community, physical co-presence, linguistic co-presence.** Knowing where your CG comes from helps you calibrate what to assume.

3. **Grounding is collaborative.** A message isn't communicated until BOTH parties believe it was understood. Speakers present; listeners provide evidence of understanding; both adjust.

4. **Backchannels are grounding evidence.** "Mm-hmm," "right," "OK" tell the speaker: "I'm following." Producing them helps conversation flow; their absence creates uncertainty.

5. **The curse of knowledge is real and persistent.** Once you know something, you overestimate how much others know about it. Ask first, explain second.

6. **Over- and underspecification are both possible.** Too much explanation = condescending. Too little = confusing. The fix for both is checking: "How familiar are you with this?"

7. **Common ground works within neurotype.** The breakdown is at the autistic-NT boundary, not within autistic-autistic or NT-NT pairs. This is the double empathy problem manifesting in the CG system.

8. **Expressions compress as CG grows.** "The coffee shop on Main and 5th" → "the coffee shop" → "there." This is evidence the system is working.

9. **When in doubt, check.** "Are you familiar with X?" / "Does that make sense?" / "Am I explaining too much?" -- these are not signs of insecurity; they are good communication practice that most people don't do enough.
