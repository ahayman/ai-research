# Concept Research: Polyvagal-Adjacent Interventions and Their Evidence Base

## Overview

A constellation of interventions has been marketed under or adjacent to polyvagal theory: filtered-music therapy (Safe and Sound Protocol), transcutaneous auricular vagus nerve stimulation (taVNS) wearables, slow paced breathing, humming and chanting, cold-water exposure, implanted vagus nerve stimulators, a growing consumer-wearables market (gammaCore, Truvaga, Pulsetto, Sensate, Apollo Neuro), supplements claiming "vagal tone" benefits, and a yoga/breathwork ecosystem ranging from rigorously studied trauma-sensitive yoga to high-risk Wim Hof and holotropic protocols. The evidence quality across this landscape varies by **two to three orders of magnitude**.

This chapter builds a reusable **critical-appraisal framework** with five evidence tiers (A–E), evaluates every commonly promoted intervention against it, and produces a decision-grade map for an autistic adult dealing with burnout who wants to try what works and skip what is hype. Conflicts of interest — particularly Stephen Porges's patent royalties from Unyte Health for SSP — are disclosed explicitly because the user wants the institutional context.

The central finding: the techniques with the strongest evidence (slow breathing, humming, the diving reflex via cold-face exposure, trauma-sensitive yoga) **predate polyvagal theory entirely** and have independent physiological mechanisms that do not require polyvagal theory to be correct. The interventions most heavily branded with polyvagal language (SSP, "polyvagal" consumer wearables, vagal-tone supplements) generally have the **weakest** evidence base, often manufacturer-funded, often without sham controls, and frequently sold by parties with direct financial interest in the framework's continued popularity.

## The Evidence-Tier Framework (the analytical anchor)

This framework is reusable across any wellness or psychiatric claim. Apply each criterion before accepting a marketing claim at face value.

### Tier A — Strong Evidence

Criteria:
- Multiple independent randomized controlled trials (RCTs)
- Adequate sample sizes (typically n ≥ 50 per arm)
- Sham or active control comparison
- Pre-registered, pre-specified primary endpoint
- Replicated by groups independent of original developer
- Meta-analyses or systematic reviews show effect
- Mechanism is plausible and consistent with established physiology
- No serious conflict of interest concerns in the evidence base

### Tier B — Moderate Evidence

Criteria:
- At least one well-controlled RCT with adequate power, or multiple smaller controlled trials
- Effects replicated in at least two independent labs
- May lack consistent meta-analytic synthesis
- Mechanism plausible but not fully established
- Some conflict-of-interest exposure but not exclusive

### Tier C — Promising / Preliminary

Criteria:
- Open-label pilots, feasibility studies, small uncontrolled trials
- No sham comparison or wait-list only
- Mechanism plausible
- Insufficient to support strong efficacy claims; supports doing larger trials

### Tier D — Commercial Only / Insufficient

Criteria:
- Manufacturer-funded studies, often unpublished or only in non–peer-reviewed venues
- Marketing claims outrun the data
- No sham controls; effects indistinguishable from placebo/expectancy/regression to mean
- Mechanism claim invokes a contested or speculative pathway
- Evidence base controlled by entity with financial stake

### Tier E — Potentially Harmful (in specific contexts)

Criteria:
- Documented adverse events in vulnerable populations
- Risk profile not adequately characterized
- Strong cure-claims for conditions where iatrogenic harm has been reported
- Used in place of standard care for serious conditions
- Hyperventilation, breath-hold + water, or "limbic retraining" cure-claims for ME/CFS-spectrum illness fit this tier

## Core Components — Each Intervention Evaluated

### Component 1: Safe and Sound Protocol (SSP)

- **What it is**: A commercial 1-to-5-hour filtered-music intervention developed by Stephen Porges and sold through Unyte Health. The acoustic filter is designed to emphasize the frequency range of human speech, with the claimed mechanism that the filtered music recruits the middle-ear muscles (innervated by the facial and trigeminal nerves) and activates the "social engagement system" via the ventral vagal complex.
- **What the studies actually show**:
  - **Kawai/Domínguez-Lara et al. 2023** ([PMC10049251](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10049251/)) — Adult ASD pilot. **n = 6 adults**, ages 21–44, mean 27.1. Five daily 60-min sessions, 1-month follow-up. Exploratory; no sham, no control group. Only the **Social Awareness subscale of the SRS-2 Family-Report** showed statistically significant improvement. Authors explicitly called for "multicenter randomized controlled trials."
  - **Porges et al. 2014** Listening Project Protocol (predecessor of SSP) — n ~ 36 children. Reported decreased auditory hypersensitivity and improved language processing. Open-label.
  - **Heilman et al.** — additional autism pilots with varied sample sizes.
  - **AOTA 2024 exploratory study** in young children — adjunct to occupational therapy; uncontrolled.
  - **Sound-based interventions systematic review** ([PMC12163202](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12163202/)) — found two SSP papers, evidence rated mixed.
- **Conflict-of-interest disclosure**: Porges holds patents on the SSP technology and receives royalties from Unyte/Integrated Listening Systems. He serves as **Chief Scientific Advisor at Unyte Health**, the company that sells SSP. He co-founded the Polyvagal Institute (501(c)(3)) and is a Distinguished University Scientist at Indiana University. These COIs are disclosed in his publications but rarely highlighted in marketing materials.
- **What's missing**: No sham-controlled, pre-registered RCT of SSP in autism with adequate power has been published as of 2026. The mechanism claim (acoustic filtering → middle-ear muscle activation → ventral vagal complex) inherits all the uncertainties of polyvagal theory itself (see Cat 3, Scientific Critique).
- **Verdict**: **Tier B–C** depending on population and outcome — preliminary evidence in autism, often dropping to Tier C for any specific subdomain. Not Tier A, despite marketing.

### Component 2: Transcutaneous Auricular Vagus Nerve Stimulation (taVNS)

- **What it is**: A wearable that delivers mild electrical stimulation to the auricular branch of the vagus nerve via an ear clip. Mechanism is anatomically grounded — the auricular branch (Arnold's nerve) does carry vagal afferents and stimulation modulates central vagal-related pathways.
- **What the studies actually show**:
  - **PMC10568329 (2023, at-home feasibility in autism)** — 12 patients with ASD (ages 7–16), daily 1-hour sessions for 2 weeks under remote supervision. 88.5% completion across 132 sessions. Anxiety scores (CASI-R, PRAS-ASD, CGI) and sleepiness (CASQ) improved at 2 weeks. **Feasibility study, not powered for efficacy; no sham control.**
  - **PMC10875019 / Frontiers 2024** — RCT *protocol* (not yet results) for ~80 children with high-functioning ASD; 40 sham, 40 active. Pre-registered, double-blind. Will provide a real test.
  - **Broader taVNS literature** outside autism has stronger evidence (depression, inflammatory conditions); some has been replicated.
- **What's missing**: Published, completed, sham-controlled RCT in autism with adequate power. The Frontiers protocol design is methodologically promising.
- **Verdict**: **Tier B–C** — emerging; promising mechanism; not yet decisive in autism. Worth tracking. Adverse events in the feasibility study were mild (headache, transient electrode-site pain, itchiness, stinging).

### Component 3: Slow Breathing (Resonance Frequency ~6 breaths/min)

- **What it is**: Voluntary slow breathing at approximately 6 breaths per minute (varies individually 4.5–7.0 bpm). Mechanism is well-established: at this frequency, heart rate and breathing synchronize, producing large respiratory sinus arrhythmia (RSA) and increasing vagally-mediated HRV.
- **What the evidence shows**:
  - **Lehrer protocol** (Lehrer et al. 2000, 2003) — original resonance-frequency biofeedback method. Replicated extensively.
  - **Russo et al. 2017** review — slow breathing improves cardiovascular function, increases HRV and BP oscillations.
  - **Laborde et al. 2022** — psychophysiological effects of slow-paced breathing at six cycles per minute with or without HRV biofeedback.
  - **Multiple meta-analyses** (2017–2024) — consistent effects: increased SDNN, RMSSD, decreased state anxiety. Effect sizes moderate to large depending on outcome.
  - **The phenomenon predates polyvagal theory** by decades. Vagally-mediated HRV is well-established cardiovascular physiology.
- **In autism**: HRV biofeedback shows some pilot evidence in young people with ASD ([PMC9463620](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9463620/)) and a 2024 mini-review ([PMC11208699](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11208699/)); a 2023 medRxiv pilot RCT in adolescents reported physiological changes. Effects on self-reported anxiety are more mixed.
- **Cost**: Free.
- **Verdict**: **Tier A** for general HRV and stress-reduction effects. **Tier B** for autism-specific anxiety endpoints (preliminary positive). Best evidence-to-cost ratio in the entire chapter.

### Component 4: Humming, Chanting, Bhramari Pranayama, "Voo" Exercise

- **What it is**: Vocal cord vibration during sustained exhalation. The vagus nerve innervates the larynx via the recurrent laryngeal branch; vibration of the vocal folds provides afferent stimulation. Bhramari pranayama (humming-bee breath) is the traditional yogic form.
- **What the evidence shows**:
  - **Lundberg and colleagues**: humming during exhalation increases nasal nitric oxide output ~15-fold compared with quiet exhalation. This is a real, replicated finding.
  - **Holter-based study of Bhramari** ([PMC10182780](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10182780/)) — examined HRV during humming, physical activity, emotional stress, and sleep. Effects on HRV were measurable.
  - **PMC10775838** — Effect of various respiration lengths on HRV during simple Bhramari humming.
- **Cost**: Free.
- **Verdict**: **Tier A–B** — real physiological effect (nasal NO is well-established); HRV effects are real but the autism-specific literature is thin. Effective for state shift toward parasympathetic dominance. Safe.

### Component 5: Cold Face Splash / Cold Plunge / Cold Exposure

- **What it is**: Exposure of the face (and sometimes body) to cold water or ice triggers the **mammalian diving reflex** — a robust, evolutionarily conserved autonomic response.
- **Mechanism (well-characterized)**: Cold water on the face stimulates the **trigeminal nerve** (anterior nasal mucosa and paranasal region). Signals project to the brainstem, which sends efferent signals via the **vagus nerve**, slowing heart rate 10–25%. Combined with apnea and peripheral vasoconstriction, this is the canonical diving response. Documented in StatPearls and standard physiology references.
- **Different evidence tiers for different uses**:
  - **Cold face splash to short-circuit a high-arousal episode**: **Tier A** (the diving reflex is well-established physiology). The reflex is fast and reliable.
  - **Cold plunge / cold-water immersion as a sustained therapy**: **Tier B–C** at best. A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis (PMC11778651, PLOS One) of 11 RCTs (n = 3,177 total) found stress reduction at 12 hours post-CWI, but **no significant effects at immediate, 1-hour, 24-hour, or 48-hour post-exposure**. Limited data on mood and depression. Most studies are on physiological outcomes; well-controlled mental-health RCTs are scarce.
  - **Wim Hof method** (cold + cyclic hyperventilation + commitment mindset): **Tier C–E** depending on use. PLOS One systematic review (PMC10936795) showed mixed results. Live Science cited cardiology experts warning that some practices pose drowning risk and dangerous heart-rhythm irregularities. Combining breath-hold + water is explicitly contraindicated by Hof himself but widely practiced.
- **Cost**: Free for face splash (sink + cold water); thousands for cold plunge tubs.
- **Verdict**: **Diving reflex via face splash — Tier A**, free, fast, safe. **Cold plunge as sustained therapy — Tier B–C**. **Combined breath-hold + cold immersion — Tier E** (avoid for autistic-burnout audience seeking calm; cardiovascular risk).

### Component 6: Implanted Vagus Nerve Stimulator (VNS)

- **What it is**: A surgically implanted device that wraps around the left cervical vagus nerve and delivers programmed electrical pulses.
- **FDA-approved indications**: Refractory epilepsy (1997), treatment-resistant depression (2005), post-stroke motor rehabilitation, cluster headache, obesity. **Not approved for autism.**
- **Evidence in autism**: Very limited. Some case reports and preclinical/translational discussion ([PMC5496407](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5496407/)) discuss VNS as potential adjuvant to behavioral therapy for autism; PMC9600938 explores VNS for fear/anxiety in ASD. **No RCTs in autism.**
- **Verdict**: Not relevant for the general autism / burnout audience. Mentioned for completeness because consumer wearables imitate the language of implanted VNS.

### Component 7: Consumer "Vagus Nerve" Devices

| Device | FDA Status | Mechanism Claim | Evidence Quality | Tier |
|--------|-----------|------------------|-------------------|------|
| **gammaCore (electroCore)** | FDA-cleared (Rx): migraine, cluster headache, paroxysmal hemicrania, hemicrania continua. Handheld neck-applied transcutaneous cervical VNS. | Stimulates cervical vagus via skin | Multiple controlled trials in headache populations; published cost-effectiveness analyses | **B** (for cleared indications only; not for autism/anxiety) |
| **Truvaga / Truvaga Plus (electroCore consumer line)** | **Not** FDA-cleared as a finished consumer wellness product. Marketed as a "general wellness" device. Uses underlying electroCore technology. | Cervical VNS for stress/sleep | Limited independent peer-reviewed evidence | **C–D** |
| **Pulsetto** | **Not** FDA-cleared. General wellness device. | Cervical VNS for relaxation/sleep | No peer-reviewed RCTs as of 2026; ongoing trials in TMJ, MS, TBI; HARMONY autonomic-regulation trial in progress. Manufacturer-funded studies plus master's theses. | **D** |
| **Sensate** | **Not** FDA-cleared. | Chest-applied infrasonic bone-conduction vibration; theorized to engage vagal pathways | All published evidence is company-generated; pilot studies lack control groups; registered trial NCT05519995 results unpublished | **D** |
| **Apollo Neuro** | **Not** FDA-cleared as a medical device. | Wrist-worn low-frequency vibration; "touch therapy" | Has more independent-looking pilot data than competitors: University of Pittsburgh double-blind crossover trial showed HRV improvements; 2025 AmJMed preliminary RCT in medical/pharmacy students showed wellbeing improvements at 12 weeks. Still preliminary; not specific to autism. | **C** |

**Pattern**: gammaCore is the only consumer-accessible non-invasive VNS device with substantive FDA clearance. The rest are sold as general wellness devices, which is a regulatory category that does **not** require FDA clearance. None has a sham-controlled RCT in autism. None has demonstrated equivalence to gammaCore's published evidence.

### Component 8: "Vagus Nerve" Supplements (probiotics, omega-3, magnesium, gua sha tools)

- **The "vagus nerve" framing is largely marketing**. Real effects exist for some of these on HRV or inflammation, but the causal pathway is rarely "increases vagal tone" in any directly measurable sense.
- **Omega-3 fatty acids**: Some RCT evidence of HRV improvements and well-established cardiovascular and anti-inflammatory effects. The HRV finding is real; "vagal" framing is incidental.
- **Probiotics**: A 2025 RCT (PubMed 40298641) found that a multi-species probiotic improved morning HRV indices (used as a proxy for vagal function) in patients with depression vs. healthy controls. Effect is modest and the gut–vagus axis is a real area of research.
- **Magnesium, gua sha tools, "vagus nerve oils"**: No credible evidence that these specifically modulate vagal tone in clinically meaningful ways. Magnesium has its own (limited) anxiety/sleep evidence base but it is not a "vagal" intervention.
- **Verdict**: **Tier D** for the "vagal tone" framing. Some products may have small independent effects through other mechanisms.

### Component 9: Yoga and Breathwork Ecosystem

#### Trauma-Sensitive Yoga (TSY)

- **What it is**: A modified yoga practice developed at the Trauma Center (Brookline, MA) by David Emerson, Jenn Turner, and Bessel van der Kolk for individuals with chronic PTSD, particularly interpersonal-trauma survivors. Trademark "Trauma Center Trauma-Sensitive Yoga" (TCTSY).
- **Evidence**: 
  - **van der Kolk et al. 2014 RCT** — compared TSY to a women's-health education control in chronic PTSD predominantly from interpersonal trauma. TSY participants showed significantly greater PTSD symptom reduction; many no longer met diagnostic criteria. Significant decreases in depression and self-injurious behaviors.
  - **Long-term follow-up** showed continuing yoga practice predicted maintenance of gains.
  - **Veterans-population feasibility trials** (PMC9839891, PMC9704511) — TCTSY vs. cognitive processing therapy for women veterans with PTSD from military sexual trauma; both feasible and acceptable.
- **Conflict-of-interest note**: David Emerson founded the Trauma Center; the work has been commercialized into certifications.
- **Verdict**: **Tier B** — one well-designed RCT plus replication signals; mechanism plausibly involves interoceptive learning and titrated arousal, not specifically polyvagal-theory mechanisms.

#### "Polyvagal Yoga" / PVT-Aligned Yoga Certifications

- **Highly variable** depending on instructor and training. No standardized evidence base. Many programs borrow PVT language to brand standard somatic practices. The underlying yoga practices may be well-evidenced even if the polyvagal framing is loose.
- **Verdict**: **Tier C** (the practices may be effective; the PVT framing is largely descriptive overlay).

#### Wim Hof Method

- **Three pillars**: cyclic hyperventilation, cold exposure, "commitment mindset."
- **Evidence**:
  - PMC10936795 (PLOS One systematic review): mixed results; some positive immune and stress-perception findings; effects vary by population.
  - 7-week RCT in chronic spinal cord injury — no improvement on mental health primary outcome; cold exposure group showed improved inspiratory lung function, lower fasting glucose.
  - Pain perception reduction documented.
- **Safety concerns**: Combining breath-hold (cyclic hyperventilation produces hypocapnia, can drop oxygen) with cold immersion has caused drownings. Live Science quoted cardiology experts citing potentially dangerous arrhythmias. Wim Hof's own materials warn never to do breathing in or near water.
- **Verdict**: **Tier C** for well-supervised practice in healthy populations; **Tier E** when combined with unsupervised water immersion or for cardiovascular-risk individuals.

#### Holotropic Breathwork

- **What it is**: Intensive hyperventilation-based breathwork developed by Stanislav Grof, often 2–3 hour sessions, designed to induce altered states.
- **Evidence**: Mixed and limited. Hyperventilation causes hypocapnia leading to tetany, paresthesias, dizziness; in vulnerable individuals can precipitate panic, dissociation, rare hallucinations or seizure. 1993 Scottish Charities Office report led to Findhorn Foundation suspending its breathwork program over psychosis/seizure risk concerns.
- **Contraindications** commonly listed: pregnancy, cardiovascular disease, uncontrolled hypertension, glaucoma, detached retina, epilepsy, serious psychiatric conditions.
- **Verdict**: **Tier C** in screened populations with proper facilitation; **Tier E** for unscreened use or for individuals with the listed contraindications. Not a first-line tool for autistic burnout.

#### Alternate-Nostril Breathing, "Voo" Exercises, Slow Pranayama

- Small to moderate literature; generally positive on HRV and state anxiety; safe.
- **Verdict**: **Tier B**.

## How It Works — The Critical-Appraisal Checklist (Reusable Tool)

When evaluating any "polyvagal" or "vagus nerve" intervention claim, walk through these eight questions. This is the reusable scaffold the user requested.

1. **Is there a sham control?** A sham is a fake version that matches sensation, time, attention, but not the active mechanism. Most consumer-device studies fail this test.
2. **Is it an RCT?** Pilot, feasibility, open-label, pre-post: all weaker. RCT with concealed allocation and intention-to-treat is the gold standard.
3. **Was the primary endpoint pre-specified and pre-registered?** Outcomes "discovered" in the data are exploratory; only pre-specified primary endpoints count for efficacy claims.
4. **Is the sample size adequate?** A pilot of n = 6 cannot demonstrate efficacy; it can only demonstrate feasibility. SSP autism research is dominated by n < 30 studies.
5. **Are conflicts of interest disclosed?** Porges holds patents and royalties on SSP. The Polyvagal Institute is a 501(c)(3) that sells courses; Unyte Health is a for-profit selling SSP. Apollo Neuro, Pulsetto, Sensate fund most of their own studies.
6. **Is the mechanism plausible?** "Vibration of a wrist activates the vagus nerve" is far less plausible than "vibration of vocal folds activates the laryngeal vagal afferents." Some mechanism claims map to known anatomy; some are speculative.
7. **What's the cost vs. benefit?** Slow breathing: free. SSP: hundreds via licensed provider plus device. Cold plunge tub: $5,000+. Always compute cost-per-effect-size.
8. **What harms have been reported?** Many wellness interventions are advertised as risk-free. Hyperventilation + water has killed people. "Limbic retraining" cure-claims for ME/CFS have provoked active harm-reduction advocacy (see Cat 10).

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: Applying the Framework to a Marketing Claim — "Polyvagal massager increases vagal tone"

**Walk through the checklist**:
1. Sham control? — Almost never.
2. RCT? — Almost never; usually open-label or testimonials.
3. Pre-registered endpoint? — No.
4. Sample size? — Often n < 30 or testimonials.
5. COI? — Manufacturer-funded.
6. Mechanism? — Mechanical pressure on the neck has no clear path to "vagal tone increase"; would risk carotid artery compression in some users.
7. Cost-benefit? — Cost: $30–200. Benefit: not demonstrated above placebo.
8. Harms? — Not characterized.

**Verdict**: **Tier D**. The product is not unsafe per se, but the specific "polyvagal" mechanism claim is unsupported.

### Example 2: Building a 10-Minute Daily Routine from Tier A/B Components

For an autistic adult with burnout who wants to try low-risk, free, high-evidence interventions:

- 5 minutes slow breathing at 5–6 bpm (Tier A) — wake or transition
- 2 minutes humming on exhale (Tier A–B) — embed in slow breathing
- Cold face splash (Tier A diving reflex) — when acute over-arousal hits, splash with ice-cold water for ~30 seconds
- Optional: 10 minutes trauma-sensitive yoga–style somatic check-in (Tier B) — if working with a trauma-informed teacher

Cost: $0. Risk: minimal. Evidence: strongest tier available.

### Example 3: Evaluating Whether to Try SSP

- The framework is honest: SSP has small open-label pilots, no sham-controlled RCT, the developer has direct financial interest, the mechanism inherits all the polyvagal-theory uncertainties from Cat 3.
- This does **not** mean SSP doesn't work or shouldn't be tried; it means it is **Tier B–C**, the autistic adult should know the COI structure, and expect that any improvement might also be attributable to therapist attention, regression to the mean, and placebo response (which can be substantial in autism-spectrum populations).
- Reasonable approach: pre-specify a clear endpoint (e.g., "less sensory overwhelm in supermarkets over 4 weeks"), use a self-rated scale before/after, treat it as an n = 1 trial with realistic expectations.

## Common Misconceptions

- **"FDA-cleared means safe and effective."** FDA clearance is not the same as FDA approval. Clearance often means "substantially equivalent to a predicate device." Wellness devices typically don't need clearance at all. The relevant question is whether RCT evidence exists for the specific use you care about.
- **"If a device says 'vagus nerve' on the box, it stimulates the vagus nerve."** Wrist vibration (Apollo Neuro), chest infrasonic resonance (Sensate), and most "polyvagal" branded devices do not have a direct, demonstrable anatomical path to the vagus nerve. Some may help anyway, through general somatic relaxation; the specific "vagus nerve" claim is what's weak.
- **"Slow breathing is a 'polyvagal' technique."** Slow paced breathing produces vagally-mediated HRV effects that were well-characterized decades before polyvagal theory. The technique works regardless of whether polyvagal theory is correct.
- **"Cold plunges are the diving reflex."** The diving reflex is specifically triggered by cold *facial* exposure. Cold plunges that don't include facial immersion (chest-deep or hot tubs alternating) don't produce the same trigeminal-vagal cascade. Whole-body cold exposure has its own evidence base — see Cat 10 — but it's separate.
- **"More vagal tone is always better."** Excessive vagal tone produces bradycardia, hypotension, syncope. The goal is not to maximize vagal tone but to restore healthy autonomic flexibility.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **POTS, hEDS, autonomic dysfunction**: Many autistic adults also have postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome (POTS) or hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos (hEDS). For these individuals, aggressive parasympathetic activation can worsen orthostatic intolerance. Slow breathing supine is fine; cold-face immersion can be problematic for severe POTS.
- **Cardiovascular disease, arrhythmia, anticoagulation**: Cold plunge can be dangerous; Wim Hof contraindicated.
- **Trauma history with body-based cues**: Trauma-sensitive yoga and somatic practices are intentionally designed for this; generic yoga or breathwork can trigger flashbacks or panic in vulnerable individuals.
- **ME/CFS-spectrum fatigue / long COVID**: Hyperventilation-based protocols can be harmful in post-exertional malaise populations; "brain retraining" programs make cure-claims that are actively disputed in the patient community. See Cat 10.

## Research Gaps

- No sham-controlled, adequately powered, pre-registered RCT of SSP in autism as of 2026.
- The Frontiers taVNS RCT protocol (PMC10875019) results have not yet published; this will be the most informative single piece of evidence.
- The longest-term follow-up data for SSP and consumer wearables is sparse; nearly all data is short-term.
- HRV biofeedback in autistic adults: pilot evidence is positive, but adult-specific RCTs are rare.
- Mechanism-specific tests of cold plunge vs. cold face splash: most studies don't isolate the trigeminal-vagal trigger.
- No head-to-head trials comparing consumer VNS wearables (Truvaga, Sensate, Pulsetto, Apollo Neuro) against each other or against gammaCore on common endpoints.
- Iatrogenic harm characterization for breathwork is dated (Findhorn 1993) and the modern literature is fragmented.
- Autism-specific cost-effectiveness analyses for any of these interventions do not exist.
