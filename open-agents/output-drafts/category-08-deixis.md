# Category 8: Deixis -- Context-Dependent Reference

## The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

---

## How to Read This Document

Consider the sentence: "I'll meet you there tomorrow."

Every word in that sentence except "meet" is a **deictic expression** -- a word whose meaning changes depending on who says it, where, and when. "I" means whoever is speaking. "You" means whoever is listening. "There" means some location both parties understand. "Tomorrow" means the day after the sentence is spoken.

If you received this sentence in a text message from an unknown number, you'd have no idea what it means. You'd be missing the **deictic context** -- the who, where, and when that fills these words with meaning.

Deixis is the pragmatic system that handles this. It's less about social norms (unlike politeness or hedging) and more about **how language connects to reality through shifting reference points**. For autistic learners who value precision, understanding deixis explains why certain common words are inherently imprecise -- and provides strategies for navigating that imprecision.

---

## 1. What Deixis Is

### 1.1 Definition

**Deixis** (from Greek *deiknynai*, "to show/point") is the use of words whose meaning depends entirely on context -- specifically, on who is speaking, where they are, and when they are speaking. Deictic expressions are linguistic **pointing devices**: they point to elements of the situation rather than having fixed meanings.

The word "I" doesn't mean "Aaron" or "Sarah" -- it means *whoever is currently speaking*. The word "here" doesn't mean "the kitchen" or "London" -- it means *wherever the speaker currently is*. The word "now" doesn't mean "3 PM on Tuesday" -- it means *the moment of the utterance*.

These words are **empty containers** that get filled with meaning by context.

### 1.2 The Deictic Center (Origo)

The linguist Karl Bühler (1934) introduced the concept of the **origo** -- the zero-point from which all deictic expressions are anchored. The origo is the **"I-here-now"** of the speaker:

| Dimension | Default Anchor |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Person** | The speaker ("I") |
| **Place** | Where the speaker is ("here") |
| **Time** | When the speaker is speaking ("now") |

Every deictic expression is interpreted relative to this anchor point. And critically, the anchor point **shifts every time a new person speaks**. When you become the speaker, "I" now means you, "here" means where you are, "now" means your current moment.

This constant shifting is what makes deixis cognitively demanding -- and it's one reason deictic processing is particularly effortful for autistic individuals (research shows slower response times for deictic shifting tasks, PMC3155703).

### 1.3 Why Deixis Is Pragmatic, Not Semantic

Deixis is the clearest demonstration of why pragmatics exists as a field. The sentence "I'll meet you there tomorrow" has a fixed **linguistic structure** but no fixed **meaning** -- its meaning changes completely depending on who says it, to whom, where, and when. The words stay the same; the reference shifts. Understanding requires context, not just grammar.

## 2. The Five Types of Deixis

### 2.1 Person Deixis: Who Is Speaking?

Person deixis uses pronouns to point to participants in conversation:

| Category | Words | Points To |
|----------|-------|-----------|
| **First person** | I, me, my, mine, we, us, our | The speaker (and possibly their group) |
| **Second person** | You, your, yours | The addressee |
| **Third person** | He, she, they, it, him, her | Someone else (not speaker or addressee) |

**The shifting nature**: "I" means a different person every time someone new speaks. In a conversation between three people, "I" refers to three different individuals across different turns. This is obvious once pointed out, but the real-time processing of this shift -- remapping "I" to a new referent with every speaker change -- is cognitively non-trivial.

**The "we" problem**: English has a single first-person plural pronoun that carries at least six distinct pragmatic functions:

| Type | Example | Who It Includes |
|------|---------|----------------|
| **Inclusive** | "Shall we go?" | You and I together |
| **Exclusive** | "We went to Paris last year." | Me and others, not you |
| **Corporate/institutional** | "We are committed to quality." | The organization |
| **Confrontational** | "We need to talk." | Really means: I have a problem with YOU |
| **Normative** | "We don't do that here." | The group -- and you're violating the norm |
| **Patronizing** | "How are we feeling today?" (nurse) | Pretends shared experience where none exists |

**The generic "you"**: "You" can mean the specific listener OR "anyone/people in general":
- "You should eat more vegetables." -- Generic (anyone) OR specific (you, the listener)?
- "You never know what's going to happen." -- Generic (no one knows)
- The ambiguity is usually resolved by context, but not always

**Strategy**: When "we" or "you" is ambiguous, asking "Who specifically do you mean?" is a precise question, not a strange one.

### 2.2 Spatial Deixis: Where Is the Speaker?

Spatial deixis organizes language around the speaker's location:

| Proximal (near speaker) | Distal (far from speaker) |
|------------------------|--------------------------|
| this | that |
| these | those |
| here | there |
| come (toward speaker) | go (away from speaker) |

**"This" vs. "that" in conversation:**
- "This is my friend Sarah." (Introducing someone present -- proximal)
- "That guy we met yesterday." (Referring to someone absent -- distal)
- "This is what I'm talking about." (Current topic -- proximal in discourse)
- "That's a good point." (Just-stated idea -- distal, moving away in time)

**"Come" vs. "go" -- the speaker-centered asymmetry:**
- "Come to my office." (Movement TOWARD the speaker)
- "Go to the conference room." (Movement AWAY from the speaker)
- "I'll come to you." (The speaker shifts the deictic center to the listener's location -- an empathic deictic shift)

**The digital challenge**: In text messages and emails, "here" and "there" lose their physical anchor:
- "I'm here" in a text -- where is "here"? The sender's location, which you may not know
- "Click here" on a webpage -- "here" is a virtual location
- "I'll be there in five minutes" -- "there" may refer to a location discussed in a previous message

### 2.3 Temporal Deixis: When Is the Speaker Speaking?

Temporal deixis anchors language to the moment of speaking:

| Expression | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| now | The time of speaking |
| then | Some other time (past or future) |
| today | The day of speaking |
| tomorrow | The day after speaking |
| yesterday | The day before speaking |
| ago | Before the time of speaking |
| next [time unit] | The [time unit] after the current one |
| last [time unit] | The [time unit] before the current one |

**The asynchronous communication problem**: Temporal deixis was designed for face-to-face conversation where speaker and listener share the same "now." Asynchronous communication breaks this:
- An email written Monday saying "I'll send it tomorrow" -- if read Wednesday, when is "tomorrow"? Monday's tomorrow (Tuesday) or Wednesday's tomorrow (Thursday)?
- A voicemail saying "Call me back today" -- if heard the next day, what does "today" mean?
- A text saying "See you tonight" -- written at 8 AM, read at 1 AM after midnight

**"Next Friday" vs. "this Friday"**: One of the most ambiguity-prone temporal deictic expressions in English. If today is Wednesday:
- "This Friday" = the Friday coming up in two days (to most speakers)
- "Next Friday" = this coming Friday OR the Friday after that (varies by speaker, region, and context)
- There is genuine dialectal variation in how these resolve. When precision matters, use the date.

**Non-literal temporal expressions**: "In a minute," "in a second," "just a moment" -- almost never literal. "I'll be there in a minute" means "soon, but I don't know exactly when."

### 2.4 Social Deixis: What Is the Relationship?

Social deixis encodes the social relationship between speaker and addressee:

| Form | What It Points To |
|------|------------------|
| "Dr. Smith" | Formal relationship, professional respect |
| "Professor Jones" | Academic hierarchy |
| "Sarah" | Familiar/friendly relationship |
| "Sare" / "Dave" | Close, informal relationship |
| "Sir" / "Ma'am" | Deference, status recognition |
| "Dude" / "Mate" | In-group solidarity |
| "Honey" / "Babe" | Intimate relationship |

Social deixis connects directly to register (Category 7) and hierarchy (Category 22). The choice of address form **points to** the social relationship the speaker is claiming or performing. Calling someone "Dr. Smith" in one sentence and "David" in the next signals a relationship shift.

**English lost its T/V distinction**: Many European languages have formal/informal second-person pronouns (French *tu/vous*, German *du/Sie*, Spanish *tú/usted*). English used to have this: "thou" (informal) vs. "you" (formal). By the 17th century, "you" won entirely, and English now relies on other social deictic devices (address forms, register, hedging) to encode what a T/V pronoun would.

### 2.5 Discourse Deixis: Where Are We in the Conversation?

Discourse deixis refers to parts of the ongoing discourse itself:

| Expression | Points To |
|-----------|-----------|
| "This is important." | What I'm about to say |
| "That's a good point." | What you just said |
| "As I mentioned earlier..." | Something said before in this conversation |
| "The following example..." | What comes next in the text |
| "The above analysis..." | What came before in the text |

Discourse deixis organizes information flow. "This" typically points **forward** (upcoming information) while "that" typically points **backward** (already-stated information).

**Pronoun reference tracking**: One of the most common sources of ambiguity in English:
- "John told Mike that **he** should leave." -- Who should leave? "He" could be John or Mike.
- "The manager told the employee that **she** needed more training." -- Who needs training?
- "Sarah gave Lisa **her** book back." -- Whose book?

In face-to-face conversation, these ambiguities are often resolved by eye gaze, gesture, or shared context. In writing, they must be resolved by the reader -- and sometimes they simply can't be.

## 3. The Deictic Center Can Shift

The default deictic center is the speaker's "I-here-now." But the center can be **projected** to other perspectives:

### 3.1 Narrative Shifting

When telling a story, the deictic center often shifts to the story world:
- "I was walking down the street and suddenly **this** guy comes up to me..."
  - "This" is proximal -- as if the speaker is re-experiencing the moment
  - The present tense "comes" (instead of "came") shifts the temporal center into the narrative

### 3.2 Empathic Shifting

The speaker adopts the listener's perspective:
- "I'll **come** to you." (Shifting the spatial center to the listener's location -- normally you "go" away from yourself)
- "How are things over **there**?" (Adopting the listener's perspective on their location)

### 3.3 Digital Splitting

In digital communication, the deictic center can fracture:
- In a video call, "here" might mean the speaker's physical room OR the virtual meeting space
- In a group chat, "we" might mean the people in the chat, the people in the room, or some other group
- In a forwarded email, the temporal anchors ("today," "tomorrow") belong to the original sender's time frame, not the reader's

## 4. Deixis and the Autistic Experience

### 4.1 Pronoun Reversal: The Classic Finding

Kanner (1943) first documented that some autistic children reverse "I" and "you" -- saying "You want a cookie" when they mean "I want a cookie." This became one of the defining early observations about autism and language.

**Modern research has revised this:**
- Pronoun reversals occur in less than 10% of autistic children's speech (vs. ~1% in typical development)
- Both autistic and non-autistic children produce reversals at low absolute rates -- the difference is degree, not kind
- The phenomenon is less common than early reports suggested
- It typically resolves, though some autistic individuals retain a preference for using proper names over pronouns into adulthood

**Two explanations:**
1. **Echolalia account**: The child echoes what was said TO them ("Do you want a cookie?" → "You want a cookie")
2. **Perspective-shifting account**: "I" and "you" require understanding that reference shifts with every speaker change -- a deictic perspective shift that takes longer to develop

Evidence from sign language supports the perspective-shifting account: deaf autistic children make similar reversal errors in sign (reversing palm orientation for "I" and "you") -- which cannot be echolalia since the sign forms are different.

### 4.2 Deictic Shifting as Processing Cost

Research (PMC3155703) using fMRI found that deictic shifting activates different neural patterns in autistic vs. non-autistic individuals, with autistic participants showing diminished right anterior insula-precuneus connectivity during deictic processing. This suggests the perspective shift required by deixis is **genuinely more effortful** for autistic cognition -- not impossible, but costlier.

This maps onto the broader pattern from Relational Frame Theory: deictic relations (I-you, here-there, now-then) involve three frames at increasing complexity:
- **Simple**: "I am here, you are there" (basic perspective distinction)
- **Reversed**: "If I were you, where would I be?" (perspective reversal)
- **Double-reversed**: "If I were you and you were me, where would you be?" (double reversal)

Each level of complexity increases processing demands, and autistic individuals show greater difficulty at the reversed and double-reversed levels.

### 4.3 The Autistic Strength: Explicit Reference

Here's the reframe: autistic individuals' preference for **explicit, non-deictic reference** (using names instead of pronouns, specific times instead of "later," specific places instead of "there") is actually **more communicatively precise**.

"John should meet Sarah at the conference room at 3 PM on Tuesday" is unambiguous. "I'll meet you there tomorrow" requires four deictic resolutions that can each fail.

The NT convention of relying heavily on deictic expressions assumes shared context that isn't always actually shared. The autistic preference for explicitness avoids this assumption -- and in written communication, asynchronous messaging, and complex multi-party situations, it's objectively superior.

### 4.4 The Double Empathy Angle

NT speakers may **over-rely** on deictic expressions, assuming shared context that doesn't exist:
- "Can you bring that thing to the meeting?" (What thing? Which meeting?)
- "She told me about it." (Who told? About what?)
- "Let's do it sometime." (Do what? When?)

When autistic listeners ask for clarification ("Which thing?" "Who?"), this is not a pragmatic deficit -- it's a **reasonable response to genuinely ambiguous language**. The NT speaker failed to provide adequate reference, not the autistic listener failed to understand.

## 5. Practical Guide to Deictic Navigation

### 5.1 When You're the Speaker: Precision Strategies

| Instead of... | Consider... | Why |
|--------------|-------------|-----|
| "I'll send it to you tomorrow." | "I'll send the report to you by Tuesday." | Removes temporal and referential ambiguity |
| "Can you bring that?" | "Can you bring the Johnson file?" | Removes referential ambiguity |
| "She said she'd be here." | "Sarah said she'd be at the office by 3." | Removes person, spatial, and temporal ambiguity |
| "We should do this sometime." | "Would you like to get coffee next week?" | Makes commitment concrete |
| "Let me know about it." | "Let me know about the budget proposal." | Identifies the referent |

Your natural precision is a strength. Don't let anyone convince you that being specific is "weird" -- it's clear.

### 5.2 When You're the Listener: Clarification Strategies

When a deictic expression is ambiguous, ask:

| Ambiguous Element | Clarifying Question |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Who** ("she," "they," "we") | "Sorry, who do you mean?" / "When you say 'we,' who's included?" |
| **Where** ("there," "here") | "Where exactly?" / "Which location?" |
| **When** ("tomorrow," "later," "soon") | "What day specifically?" / "What time are you thinking?" |
| **What** ("it," "that," "this") | "Which one?" / "Sorry, what are you referring to?" |

These are not strange questions. They are precision requests that prevent real miscommunication.

### 5.3 Reading Pragmatic Uses of Deixis

Some deictic expressions carry pragmatic meaning beyond their reference:

| Expression | Literal Reference | Pragmatic Signal |
|-----------|------------------|------------------|
| "We need to talk." | You and I need to talk | Something is wrong; brace for confrontation |
| "We don't do that here." | This group doesn't do that | You're violating a norm; correction incoming |
| "How are **we** feeling?" | How are you feeling | (From a nurse) Patronizing solidarity |
| Using full name: "**William**" | The person named William | Formality shift → possible anger or seriousness |
| "**This** is what I'm talking about!" | The current thing | Emphasis / validation |
| "Oh, **that**." | Something previously mentioned | Dismissal or recognition, depending on tone |

### 5.4 Digital Communication Tips

| Challenge | Strategy |
|-----------|---------|
| "Here" in a text (where?) | Add location: "I'm here at the restaurant" |
| "Today"/"tomorrow" in email | Add the date: "tomorrow (Tuesday the 8th)" |
| "This" in a forwarded message | Specify: "This refers to the Q3 budget discussion" |
| "We" in a group chat | Specify: "The design team should..." instead of "We should..." |
| Time zones in async communication | State the time zone: "3 PM EST" |

## 6. Key Takeaways

1. **Deictic expressions are empty containers.** "I," "here," "now," "this," "tomorrow" have no fixed meaning -- they're filled by context. This is by design, not a flaw.

2. **The deictic center shifts with every speaker change.** Every time someone new speaks, "I" remaps, "here" remaps, and the entire reference frame recalibrates. This is cognitively demanding, and research shows it's more effortful for autistic cognition.

3. **"We" is the most pragmatically loaded pronoun in English.** It can be inclusive, exclusive, corporate, patronizing, confrontational, or normative. When it's ambiguous, ask who's included.

4. **Async communication breaks temporal deixis.** "Tomorrow" in an email means a different day depending on when you read it. Adding dates prevents ambiguity.

5. **Your preference for explicit reference is a strength.** Using names instead of pronouns, specific times instead of "later," and specific locations instead of "there" is more precise. It's not overcommunicating -- it's clear communicating.

6. **Asking for clarification is not a deficit.** When someone says "Can you bring that thing?" and you ask "Which thing?", you're responding appropriately to genuinely ambiguous language.

7. **Pronoun reversal is a deictic challenge, not a random error.** It reflects the genuine cognitive complexity of perspective-shifting pronouns -- and it resolves with development.
