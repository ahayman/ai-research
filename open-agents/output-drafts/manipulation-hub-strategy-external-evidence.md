## 1. Why This Page Exists

The [Documentation page](manipulation-hub-strategy-documentation.md) addresses the records you create for yourself. This page addresses a related but distinct question: **what other evidence exists, or could exist, that is not in your own writing, and how do you preserve, collect, or develop it?**

External evidence is evidence that exists in systems, documents, or testimony outside your own head and your own records. It has specific properties that make it more persuasive than personal documentation in many contexts:

- **It carries weight independent of your testimony.** Audiences that would discount your verbal account may accept the same information when it comes from a neutral source.
- **It is harder to dismiss as "just your version."** An email sent by the other person is not "your version" of what they said; it is what they said.
- **It builds pattern evidence that individual records cannot.** Multiple sources converging on the same pattern are much stronger than any single source.
- **It is admissible in legal, workplace, and institutional proceedings** in ways that informal journaling is not.
- **It cannot be dismissed as obsessive note-keeping.** The credibility discount (Lim & Magiati, 2022) sometimes makes your own documentation read as pathological rather than protective. External evidence does not have this problem.

For autistic readers specifically, external evidence is particularly valuable because the credibility discount is real and structural. Your verbal testimony and even your personal records are systematically underweighted by audiences calibrated for neurotypical presentation. Evidence from outside your own perspective bypasses this discount.

The research base for this page:

- **Employment law practitioner literature** on evidentiary standards for hostile environment claims (synthesized from Title VII case law, EEOC guidance, and employment lawyer practical guides). [ESTABLISHED LEGAL PRACTICE], grounded in evidentiary law rather than RCT evidence.
- **DV advocacy practice** on evidence gathering for protective orders and criminal proceedings (Campbell Danger Assessment materials, NDVH guidance, specific state-level protective order procedures). [ESTABLISHED PRACTICE].
- **Digital forensics and evidence preservation literatures** for electronic communications and digital records.
- **The documentation asymmetry principle** implied by Lim & Magiati (2022) and related credibility-discount research: evidence that does not depend on your verbal presentation is more persuasive than evidence that does.

*Evidence quality note: This page is about established legal and practitioner practice more than RCT-tested interventions. The practices described are widely used and have passed the tests of legal admissibility and practitioner experience, but they have not been tested as RCT interventions in the way therapy or safety planning have been. Use with that caveat.*

## 2. Types of External Evidence

External evidence comes in several forms, each with different characteristics, uses, and risks. Knowing which type applies in your situation determines what strategies to use.

### Communications from the person causing harm

**Examples:** Emails, texts, voicemails, social media messages, handwritten notes, recorded conversations (where legal), memos, performance reviews, written statements.

**Why they matter:** These are the single most valuable category of external evidence because they are literally the words of the person causing harm, in their own voice, with timestamps and metadata. They are not "your version" of anything; they are the original source.

**What to preserve:**
- Any message that demonstrates the pattern you are documenting
- Any message that contradicts a later claim (especially relevant for gaslighting)
- Any message that acknowledges something the person later denies
- Any message that makes a specific threat, demand, or statement of intent
- Any message that shows the timing of events (when something happened, who contacted whom first, who responded and when)
- Patterns across time (not just individual messages) — the cumulative picture

**How to preserve:**
- **Screenshots with visible timestamps.** The timestamp is as important as the content. Screenshots that do not show the date and time are weaker than those that do.
- **Forwarding to a secure external account.** An email account the person causing harm does not know about. Create one if needed.
- **Platform data exports.** Most major platforms (email, Facebook, Instagram, Slack, Discord) allow you to download your account data. This preserves metadata that screenshots do not capture.
- **Physical printouts.** For high-value evidence, a physical printout stored securely is a backup against digital destruction.
- **Multiple copies in multiple locations.** The principle from the Documentation page — single-location storage is fragile — applies doubly to external evidence.

**Critical safety note:** Your preservation of evidence may be discoverable. In some contexts (custody, criminal defense), the other side can request access to your records through legal discovery. Consult a lawyer before a legal proceeding about what you have and what will be disclosed.

**Critical legal note on recording:** Laws about recording conversations vary dramatically by jurisdiction. Some places are "one-party consent" (you can record any conversation you are in without the other party's knowledge); others are "two-party consent" or "all-party consent" (recording without consent is illegal and the recording is inadmissible). Check your jurisdiction before recording anything. An illegally-obtained recording can damage your position rather than help it.

### Third-party observations

**Examples:** Witnesses to specific events, statements from friends or family who were present, testimony from neutral observers (neighbors, coworkers, teachers, medical providers), records of third parties who interacted with the person causing harm.

**Why they matter:** Third-party observations break the "he said / she said" dynamic. When someone else was present and saw or heard what you are describing, their account is an independent data point.

**What to preserve:**
- **Names and contact information of witnesses** at the time of the incident, not reconstructed later
- **A brief written account** of what the witness observed, while memories are fresh, ideally from the witness themselves
- **Any evidence of the witness's presence** (photos from the event, sign-in sheets, security footage)

**How to gather:**
- **Ask witnesses if they would be willing to write a brief account.** Frame it as "I want to make sure my memory is accurate; would you mind writing down what you saw?" — not as "I need you to testify against [person]."
- **Respect their autonomy.** Not everyone is willing to be involved. Pressuring them produces worse evidence than accepting their limits.
- **Identify witnesses quickly.** Witness memory degrades fast. A written account from a witness at the time of the event is dramatically stronger than one reconstructed weeks or months later.

**Limits of witness evidence:**
- Witnesses can be hostile (they may have their own relationship with the person causing harm that biases their account)
- Witnesses can be unreliable (memory is imperfect even when intentions are good)
- Witnesses can disappear (people move, lose contact, refuse to participate when the time comes)
- Your relationship with the witness may be affected by asking for their involvement

### Records from systems and institutions

**Examples:** Medical records documenting injuries or symptoms; police reports (even if no charges were filed); workplace emails and memos; school records; tax records showing financial history; property records; court records; corporate communications.

**Why they matter:** Institutional records carry automatic credibility because they were created by an institution in the normal course of its operations, not by you for the purpose of defending yourself. Courts, HR departments, and insurance companies treat institutional records as more reliable than personal documentation.

**What to preserve:**
- Medical records showing injuries, symptoms, mental health care relevant to the situation
- Police reports, even if no arrest or charges resulted — the existence of a report is itself evidence
- Workplace records: performance reviews, HR complaints, emails about accommodation requests, time and attendance records
- Financial records showing economic abuse patterns
- Any institutional record that confirms dates, times, or events you are claiming

**How to obtain:**
- **Medical records:** Request copies from your healthcare providers. Under HIPAA (US) and equivalent laws, you have the right to your own records.
- **Police reports:** Request copies from the department that took the report. There may be a small fee. Some reports are public record; others require specific procedures.
- **Workplace records:** You may have rights to request copies of your personnel file under state or country labor laws. This varies by jurisdiction.
- **Court records:** Many court records are public. You can access them through the court's public records system or through legal research tools.
- **Financial records:** Your own bank statements, tax returns, credit reports. Free credit reports are available annually in the US and many other countries.

**Time-sensitivity:** Many institutions have retention policies that limit how long records are kept. Request early. Some records (like surveillance footage) may be retained for only 30 days or less.

### Patterns across sources

**Examples:** Email metadata showing communication patterns; phone records showing call frequency and timing; financial records showing control patterns; calendar entries showing where you were and when.

**Why they matter:** Individual events can be explained away; patterns across time and sources are much harder to dismiss. The shift from "one incident" to "pattern across six months of records" is one of the most persuasive moves in any evidentiary context.

**What to preserve:**
- Metadata that shows timing and frequency of communications
- Financial patterns that show control (withholding money, monitoring spending, creating debt without your knowledge)
- Calendar and scheduling patterns (who controlled your schedule, when you were "allowed" to do things)
- Pattern evidence across sources (one record shows one thing; three records together show a pattern)

**How to assemble:**
- **Create a timeline.** Organize events chronologically with sources. This produces a picture that no individual source alone creates.
- **Look for convergence.** Multiple records that independently confirm the same event or pattern are much stronger than single records.
- **Identify gaps and address them.** If your timeline has obvious gaps, ask what evidence could fill them.

### Expert testimony and evaluations

**Examples:** Mental health evaluations documenting the effects of abuse; risk assessments by trained advocates; medical evaluations documenting injury or chronic effects; specialized assessments (Danger Assessment by a trained advocate).

**Why they matter:** Expert testimony provides a trained perspective that carries specific weight in legal and institutional contexts. A mental health evaluation stating that you show symptoms consistent with PTSD from sustained psychological abuse is different from your own statement that you are traumatized.

**How to obtain:**
- **Mental health evaluations:** Through a therapist who specializes in trauma or through a specific forensic evaluator if a legal proceeding is involved.
- **Advocate assessments:** Through DV or SA advocacy organizations. Campbell Danger Assessments are done by trained advocates.
- **Medical evaluations:** Through healthcare providers. Specific specialties (forensic nursing, sexual assault nurse examiners, occupational medicine for workplace harm) may be more relevant than general practice.

**Limits:**
- Expert testimony is expensive when obtained for legal purposes
- Quality varies by the specific expert
- The expert's opinion is only as good as the information you provide them
- Expert testimony can be challenged and counter-challenged in adversarial proceedings

## 3. The Timing Question

When to gather external evidence is nearly as important as what to gather.

### Early is better than late

**Evidence degrades over time.** Memory fades. Records are deleted or destroyed. Witnesses become harder to reach. Platforms change policies. Metadata is lost. The longer you wait, the less is available.

**People leave.** Witnesses move away. Coworkers leave companies. Healthcare providers retire or change practices. Legal relationships end. Each of these reduces the pool of available evidence.

**Patterns become visible only in retrospect** — but the records that reveal the patterns need to be preserved as they are created, not reconstructed afterward.

### But premature evidence-gathering has risks

**Discovery by the person causing harm can escalate the situation.** If you are in a high-risk situation, a new pattern of "gathering evidence" behavior can itself be a trigger. This is particularly important in coercive control contexts.

**Evidence gathered too early may not be usable.** If you are not yet committed to a specific action (legal, workplace, or otherwise), the evidence may sit unused and potentially become discoverable in contexts you did not anticipate.

**You may not yet know what evidence matters.** Early in a situation, you do not know which direction things will go. Evidence gathered for one purpose may or may not be useful for the purpose that eventually becomes relevant.

### The balance

The practical answer: **start preserving obvious evidence early and expand gathering as the situation clarifies**. Specific steps:

1. **Immediately preserve communications that come to you.** Emails, texts, messages. This is low-cost and high-value.
2. **Request institutional records as soon as you realize a situation is developing.** Medical records, police reports, workplace records. These are time-sensitive and can disappear.
3. **Document witnesses' presence at the time of events.** Even if you are not yet asking them for statements.
4. **Defer proactive evidence gathering** (recording conversations, confrontational exchanges that create evidence, formal statements) until you have a clear purpose and have consulted with an appropriate professional.
5. **Revisit the evidence question as the situation evolves.** What was the right amount of gathering six months ago may not be the right amount now.

## 4. Evidence for Different Purposes

The appropriate evidence strategy depends on what you are preparing for. Different purposes have different requirements.

### Evidence for personal reality anchoring

**Purpose:** Maintaining your own sense of what happened against gaslighting pressure.

**Requirements:**
- Accessible to you (so you can refer to it when you need anchoring)
- Meaningful to you (does not need to meet legal standards)
- Emotionally tolerable (severe material that retraumatizes you when reviewed has limited use)

**Appropriate sources:** Your own documentation, emails and texts from the person causing harm, photos, small items with significance, reality-check network members who remember.

**Note:** This is the lowest-stakes purpose for evidence gathering and has the most relaxed standards. Any form of preservation that serves the function is legitimate.

### Evidence for therapy processing

**Purpose:** Supporting therapeutic work on what happened.

**Requirements:**
- Shareable with your therapist
- Specific enough to support processing
- Organized in a way that does not consume therapy time

**Appropriate sources:** Selected documentation (not all of it), key communications, timeline of major events, specific examples of patterns the therapist and you are working on.

**Note:** Do not bring everything to therapy. Select specific material for specific therapeutic purposes.

### Evidence for workplace complaints

**Purpose:** Supporting a formal workplace complaint (harassment, discrimination, accommodation denial, retaliation).

**Requirements:**
- Contemporaneous (dated, ideally with metadata)
- Specific (exact dates, exact quotes, specific incidents)
- Professional (framed in terms of workplace conduct, not emotional reactions)
- Comprehensive (pattern evidence, not just single incidents)
- Legally-relevant (focused on conduct that is actually actionable under your applicable laws)

**Appropriate sources:** Workplace emails, HR communications, performance reviews, meeting notes, calendar records, witness accounts, documentation of specific incidents.

**Critical note:** Workplace complaints typically have deadlines. EEOC complaints in the US must generally be filed within 180 to 300 days of the incident, depending on state. Other jurisdictions have different deadlines. If you are preserving evidence for potential workplace action, consult an employment lawyer about the deadlines that apply.

### Evidence for legal proceedings (DV, custody, criminal, civil)

**Purpose:** Supporting a specific legal action.

**Requirements:**
- Admissible under the evidence rules of your jurisdiction
- Obtained legally (not in violation of recording laws, privacy laws, etc.)
- Relevant to the specific claims being made
- Preservable (chain of custody matters for some types of evidence)
- Supported by professional evaluation where applicable

**Appropriate sources:** Depends entirely on the specific legal action. General categories: communications, institutional records, medical records, financial records, witness testimony, expert evaluations.

**Critical note:** **Do not gather evidence for legal proceedings without consulting a lawyer first.** The rules about what counts, what does not, what is admissible, and what can be used against you are complex and jurisdiction-specific. A lawyer can guide you on what to gather, how, and what to avoid.

### Evidence for medical care

**Purpose:** Documenting medical effects of the situation for treatment, disability claims, or medical legal purposes.

**Requirements:**
- Medical relevance (symptoms, treatment, impact on functioning)
- Professional documentation (from healthcare providers, not just self-report)
- Temporal relationship to the causing events

**Appropriate sources:** Medical records you request from your providers, symptom logs you create, prescriptions and treatment records, records of specialist consultations.

**Practical note:** For autistic readers specifically, medical records often need to explicitly address the fact that your presentation may not match the severity of your condition. Phrases like "patient typically minimizes distress" or "patient presents with flat affect that does not reflect the severity of her reported symptoms" in your medical record are protective. Ask your provider to include these observations when appropriate.

### Evidence for safety planning

**Purpose:** Supporting Danger Assessment and tailored safety planning with an advocate.

**Requirements:**
- Specific details about the person causing harm and their behavior
- History of violence, threats, stalking, strangulation
- Access to weapons
- Pattern of escalation
- Specific threats or behavior indicating lethality risk

**Appropriate sources:** Your memory (an advocate will help you structure it), relevant medical records if applicable, police reports if any, documented communications showing threats.

**Note:** Safety planning evidence is gathered in collaboration with an advocate. You do not need to compile everything on your own before contacting one — the advocate will guide you through what is needed.

## 5. Specific Evidence-Building Strategies

### The meta-email

A specific technique for workplace and similar contexts: **after a significant verbal exchange, send an email to the other party (or to yourself, or both) summarizing what was said.** This is the "follow-up email" technique from the [Documentation page](manipulation-hub-strategy-documentation.md), used here as a proactive evidence-building tool.

**Template:**
> Subject: Follow-up from our conversation [date]
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I wanted to follow up on our conversation this afternoon so we're both on the same page.
>
> You said [specific thing they said].
> We agreed that [specific agreement].
> You expressed concern about [specific concern].
> I explained [specific point you made].
>
> If I've captured anything inaccurately, please let me know.
>
> Thanks,
> [Your name]

This does three things at once:
1. Creates a timestamped written record of the conversation
2. Gives the other person a chance to correct genuine misunderstandings (distinguishing miscommunication from manipulation — see the [Manipulation vs. Miscommunication page](manipulation-hub-miscommunication-vs-manipulation.md))
3. Puts them in a position where silence becomes acquiescence

If they respond confirming, you have confirmation. If they respond correcting, you have clarification. If they respond with hostility, you have a new data point about the dynamic. If they do not respond, their silence is itself informative.

**When this technique works well:** Workplace contexts, formal professional relationships, family situations where written communication is plausible, any situation where you would expect follow-up emails to be normal.

**When this technique does not work well:** Situations where the person causing harm does not use email or written communication, situations where a follow-up email would itself be a trigger, coercive control contexts where even communication volume is monitored.

### Strategic third-party copying

A related technique: **when communicating important information in writing, copy a third party who can serve as witness.** This works when the third party has a legitimate reason to be on the communication (a supervisor, a union rep, another relevant stakeholder) and when the copying is not itself a red flag.

**Example:** "I'm writing to confirm that I am requesting [accommodation] as discussed in our meeting on [date]. [Name of HR manager] — I am copying you so we have a shared record of this request."

This is a form of "creating evidence in the normal course of business" — the communication has a legitimate purpose, the copying has a legitimate purpose, and the record it creates is unimpeachable.

**When to use:** Professional contexts where the evidence is important and the copying is plausible. Not for personal relationships.

### Pattern documentation over time

A strategy that goes beyond individual incidents: **maintain a running record of specific patterns, with supporting evidence for each instance.**

**Example pattern categories:**
- Times the person dismissed a specific type of concern
- Accommodations that were requested and denied
- Commitments that were made and not kept
- Contradictions between what was said and what was later claimed

For each pattern, maintain a list of specific instances with date, context, and (where available) documentary evidence.

**Why this works:** A single instance of any pattern is deniable. Five instances of the same pattern across different contexts are much harder to deny. Ten instances become a demonstrable pattern. The shift from "one incident" to "documented pattern over six months" is qualitatively different in any evidentiary context.

### The "sent from an outside account" technique

In situations where your primary communication channels may be monitored: **create a secondary account that the person causing harm does not know about, and use it to communicate with external support** (advocates, lawyers, therapists, reality-check network members).

**Purposes:**
- Preserves communication with outside support that cannot be monitored
- Creates a record of when you reached out for help
- Provides a place to store evidence away from monitored systems

**Practical setup:**
- Use a free email provider
- Access only from devices the person causing harm does not have access to (personal phone not backed up to shared accounts, public computers, work devices if work is not the problem)
- Do not enable sync across devices where the person has access
- Do not save the password anywhere they can find it

### Coordinated witness briefing

For situations where specific witnesses may be relevant: **brief the witness on what you are observing and ask them to pay attention and document what they see.**

**Example:** "I've been having a difficult situation with [person], and I'm trying to document what's happening so I can think about my options. Would you be willing to write down what you notice in the [specific context where the witness is present]?"

This converts a passive observer into an active witness. It is more effective than hoping they will remember later.

**Important notes:**
- Only do this with witnesses you trust completely — the person you ask could tell the person causing harm
- Make sure you do not cross into coaching (telling the witness what to see or say)
- Respect the witness's autonomy if they decline

### Preserving evidence of institutional failures

Sometimes the relevant evidence is not of the original harm but of the institutional response to it. If you report harassment to HR and HR does nothing, the evidence of HR's inaction becomes important.

**What to preserve:**
- Your original report (who you reported to, when, what you said)
- The institution's response (or lack thereof)
- The timeline of follow-up
- Any subsequent actions taken by the institution
- Any retaliation you experienced after reporting

This evidence supports later claims of retaliation, failure to investigate, or institutional complicity — which are often separate legal claims from the underlying harassment.

## 6. Digital Forensics Basics

Modern evidence gathering often involves digital materials. Basic competence in digital preservation prevents losing evidence to technical issues.

### Screenshots: how to do them right

- **Show the timestamp.** If the app does not show the time on the screen, use the system time (operating system clock) as a secondary reference. Screenshots without time are weaker than those with.
- **Include context.** A screenshot of a single message out of context is easier to dismiss than a screenshot showing the conversation thread.
- **Capture the sender information.** Make sure the sender's name or username is visible.
- **Store the screenshots in multiple locations.** Your phone, a cloud account, an email to yourself.
- **Name screenshots descriptively.** "Screenshot_2026-04-15_threatening_text_from_X.png" is more findable than "IMG_2487.png".

### Email preservation

- **Do not delete.** Archive rather than delete. You can hide messages from your inbox without losing them.
- **Forward to a secure account.** Sending important emails to a secondary account creates a copy in a location the other party does not know about.
- **Export email archives.** Most email systems allow you to export your data (in MBOX, PST, or similar formats). Periodic exports protect against account compromise.
- **Preserve metadata.** Email headers contain sending IP addresses, routing information, and other data that is sometimes relevant. Exporting preserves this; screenshots do not.

### Text messages

- **Most phones do not back up texts by default.** Check whether yours does, and enable backup if not already enabled.
- **Screenshots are the standard format.** iOS and Android both have good screenshot support.
- **For Android:** Third-party apps can export SMS to readable formats.
- **For iOS:** iCloud backs up texts if enabled. iTunes backup on a computer is another option.
- **Carrier records:** Your phone carrier keeps records of texts, but these are limited (typically metadata only, not content) and require legal process to obtain.

### Social media

- **Platform data downloads.** Facebook, Instagram, Twitter/X, LinkedIn, and most other major platforms allow you to download your full account data. This preserves posts, messages, photos, and metadata in a format that survives account deletion.
- **Archive the other person's public content if relevant.** The Wayback Machine (archive.org) can archive public web pages, including public social media posts. If the other person's public content is relevant, archiving it prevents them from deleting it later.
- **Be aware of platform policies.** Some platforms prohibit certain forms of archiving in their terms of service. Check before doing bulk archiving.

### Video and audio

- **Consent laws apply.** As noted in Section 2, recording laws vary. Check your jurisdiction before recording.
- **Metadata matters.** Date, time, location, device ID. Most phones capture this automatically; do not strip it.
- **File format durability.** MP4 is more durable than specialized formats. Save copies in standard formats.
- **Cloud backup.** Google Photos, iCloud, or similar services can preserve video and audio even if the device is lost or destroyed.

### Chain of custody considerations

For evidence that may be used in legal proceedings, the "chain of custody" — the record of who had possession of the evidence and when — can matter. Key principles:

- **Preserve the original.** Always keep the original file; make copies for sharing.
- **Do not edit the original.** Any editing (even cropping) can raise questions about authenticity.
- **Document when and how you obtained it.** A brief note about when you took the screenshot, from what device, can matter if the authenticity is challenged.
- **Consult a lawyer about chain of custody** if the evidence is likely to be used in court. Practices vary by jurisdiction and evidence type.

## 7. Autism-Specific Considerations

### Pattern recognition is your strength

As discussed in several other strategy pages, autistic pattern recognition is a relative strength. For external evidence building, this translates directly: you are often better at identifying which individual events are part of a larger pattern and at assembling evidence for that pattern systematically.

**Practical implications:**
- **Trust your sense that a pattern exists** even if individual incidents seem minor. Your pattern recognition is probably accurate.
- **Use systematic thinking for evidence organization.** Timelines, categorized lists, linked records. The same systematic thinking that sometimes gets pathologized in social contexts is an asset here.
- **Document connections between events.** If you notice that a specific kind of incident always follows a specific kind of trigger, that pattern is evidence of something — even if you cannot immediately articulate what.

### The credibility discount means external evidence matters more for you

The Lim & Magiati (2022) finding that autistic adults are perceived as more deceptive and less credible than neurotypical adults when telling the truth has specific implications for evidence gathering:

- **Your verbal testimony will be systematically underweighted.** This is structural, not something you can fix by being more confident or convincing.
- **External evidence bypasses this underweighting.** An email from the other person is an email from the other person, regardless of who is reporting it.
- **You may need more external evidence than a neurotypical person in the same situation.** This is not fair, but it is real. Plan accordingly.

**Practical implication:** if you have a choice between your own detailed documentation and external evidence of the same event, prioritize the external evidence even though the personal documentation is easier. The external evidence does more work for you in any context where you need to convince someone.

### The over-documentation risk, revisited

As noted on the [Documentation page](manipulation-hub-strategy-documentation.md), autistic readers sometimes produce evidence in volumes that non-autistic audiences read as "obsessive" rather than "thorough." This risk also applies to external evidence building:

- **Compiling massive evidence archives can be read as pathological by skeptical audiences.** If an HR investigator sees that you have a 200-page organized document set, they may treat your complaint as "fixation" rather than "complaint."
- **The solution is not to compile less, but to present less selectively.** Maintain your comprehensive record for yourself; select carefully what to share with any specific audience.
- **An advocate or lawyer can help you select.** "What should I show the HR investigator?" is a question trained people can answer better than you can from the inside.

### Processing the evidence has costs

Gathering and organizing evidence of ongoing harm is itself retraumatizing. Every time you review a threatening text to preserve it, you are re-encountering the threat. This has real costs.

**Practical strategies:**
- **Batch the work.** Rather than handling each piece of evidence as it comes in, batch evidence review into scheduled sessions you prepare for.
- **Have a recovery plan.** After working with difficult evidence, give yourself time to decompress. This is not indulgence; it is capacity protection.
- **Use low-engagement preservation methods when possible.** Forwarding an email to a backup account is less engaging than re-reading it and writing a summary. Use lower-cost methods when the situation allows.
- **Ask for help with the most difficult material.** A trusted reality-check network member or therapist can sometimes help you organize evidence you cannot handle alone.

### The autism-specific evidence types

Some categories of evidence are particularly relevant for autistic readers:

- **Accommodation denial records:** Written records showing that you requested specific accommodations and the response you received. These are directly relevant to ADA and similar disability rights claims.
- **Records of diagnostic weaponization:** Instances where the person causing harm used your autism against you ("that's your autism talking"). These become evidence of a specific pattern.
- **Sensory gaslighting records:** Instances of your sensory experience being dismissed or contradicted. Connect to the broader pattern of reality-denial.
- **Medical records documenting trauma responses that would not be "traumatic" under DSM-5 criteria:** Rumball et al. (2020) research on autistic trauma supports the legitimacy of PTSD responses to events that the DSM does not code as traumatic. A medical provider documenting this can strengthen your position.

### The authority vacuum applies here too

There are very few people — advocates, lawyers, therapists, investigators — who understand both autism and manipulation. When you present evidence to someone outside this intersection, you may need to explain more than a neurotypical person would. The evidence itself may not be self-explanatory to a person who does not understand the autistic context.

**Practical strategies:**
- **Prepare explanatory material.** A brief document explaining how autism affects your communication can help investigators, lawyers, and others interpret your evidence correctly.
- **Use specific examples of misinterpretation.** "When I said 'I'm fine,' I meant [specific thing]. My communication style is literal; I was reporting a fact, not dismissing the problem."
- **Point to resources.** Reference materials that support your explanations (research papers, advocacy organization publications, etc.) can help.

## 8. Risks of External Evidence Gathering

### Discovery and escalation

The same risks that apply to documentation apply to external evidence: if the person causing harm discovers you are gathering evidence, it can trigger retaliation or escalation. In coercive control contexts specifically, the discovery risk is serious.

**Mitigations:**
- Gather evidence in ways that do not create new visible behavior
- Store evidence in locations they cannot access
- Be particularly careful with digital evidence on shared devices
- Coordinate with an advocate about safe evidence-gathering in high-risk situations

### Legal exposure

Not all evidence gathering is legal. Specific risks:

- **Recording laws** vary by jurisdiction. Illegal recordings can be inadmissible and can create legal liability for you.
- **Accessing someone else's accounts** is generally illegal, even if they are your partner or family member.
- **Taking documents that are not yours** (from a workplace, for example) can create legal problems.
- **Violating privacy laws** by sharing certain types of information can create liability.

**Mitigation:** consult a lawyer before gathering evidence that involves any of these risk areas. A brief consultation can prevent substantial problems.

### Weaponization of your evidence

Your evidence can sometimes be used against you. Examples:

- **Custody cases:** Evidence you gathered to support your own claims can be characterized as "obsessive surveillance" by the other party's lawyer.
- **Workplace investigations:** Evidence you gathered can be used by the employer to build a case that you are "difficult" or "fixated."
- **Criminal defense:** Evidence you possess can be subpoenaed by the other party's defense.

**Mitigations:**
- Keep evidence professional and factual
- Consult a lawyer before using evidence in legal proceedings
- Do not volunteer more evidence than is asked for

### Psychological cost

Sustained evidence gathering is emotionally expensive. It keeps you focused on the harm rather than on recovery. It can feed obsessive review patterns. It can become an identity ("the person who has the evidence") that outlasts its usefulness.

**Mitigations:**
- Set limits on evidence-gathering sessions
- Recognize when evidence is sufficient for your purpose and stop gathering
- Balance evidence gathering with activities that build recovery and capacity
- Work with a therapist to process the emotional load of evidence gathering, not just its practical use

### The "documentation as identity" trap

A specific risk: your evidence collection can become a substitute for action. You gather and gather and never use it. This is sometimes appropriate (you are building capacity for future action), but it can also become avoidance dressed up as diligence.

**Mitigation:** periodically ask yourself what the evidence is for. If the answer is not clear, that is a signal to either clarify the purpose or stop gathering.

## 9. Strategies by Situation

### If you are preserving evidence for possible future legal action

- **Consult a lawyer early.** A brief consultation can prevent costly mistakes. Many lawyers offer free initial consultations for this purpose.
- **Preserve broadly, share selectively.** Keep comprehensive records; share only what is strategically useful.
- **Understand the deadlines.** Many legal actions have statutes of limitations. Know what applies to your situation.
- **Do not tell the other party you are gathering evidence.** This can trigger destruction of evidence by them.
- **Store securely and multiply.** Multiple copies, multiple locations, at least one they cannot access.

### If you are preserving evidence for workplace action

- **Use non-work systems for storage.** Work devices and accounts can be seized or surveilled.
- **Understand your employer's investigation process.** HR investigations have specific formats; evidence that works in one format may not work in another.
- **Consult an employment lawyer.** Workplace law is specialized; general legal advice may not apply.
- **Document HR's response as well as the original harm.** Retaliation and failure-to-investigate are separate claims with their own evidence.

### If you are preserving evidence for DV/IPV situations

- **Work with a DV advocate.** They have specific guidance on evidence gathering in high-risk situations.
- **Lethality indicators change the calculation.** If any lethality indicators are present, evidence gathering is secondary to safety planning.
- **Physical evidence should not be handled in ways that risk discovery.** Storage matters.
- **Medical records of injuries are particularly valuable.** Make sure providers document injuries thoroughly and accurately.

### If you are preserving evidence for your own reality anchoring

- **Standards are more flexible.** You do not need legal admissibility for personal use.
- **Emotional tolerability matters more.** Material that retraumatizes you when reviewed has limited value.
- **Select what helps you.** You do not need to preserve everything — just what supports your own sense of what happened.
- **Combine with reality-check network confirmation.** A trusted person who remembers something is as valuable as a written record in many cases.

### If you are in a low-resource situation

- **Prioritize low-cost preservation.** Forwarding to a secondary email account is low-cost and high-value.
- **Use free tools.** Gmail, Dropbox, Google Drive, and similar free tools are sufficient for most evidence preservation needs.
- **Request institutional records that are free or low-cost.** Many medical records, police reports, and public records can be obtained for free or very low fees.
- **Prioritize the most important evidence.** If you cannot preserve everything, preserve the most consequential items.
- **Use your reality-check network.** Trusted people can hold copies of important evidence for you.

### If you are in a high-risk situation

- **Consult an advocate.** They have specific guidance on evidence gathering in dangerous circumstances.
- **Do not gather evidence in ways that create new risk.** If a new behavior would be noticed by the person causing harm, consider whether the evidence is worth the risk.
- **Use highly secure storage.** Personal accounts they do not know about, stored on devices they cannot access.
- **Keep evidence out of sight.** Physical evidence in shared spaces is particularly risky.
- **Prioritize safety planning over evidence building.** If you have to choose, safety comes first.

## 10. What This Page Cannot Do

- **Replace a lawyer.** For any legal proceeding, a lawyer is essential. This page provides orientation; legal advice is specialized.
- **Guarantee admissibility.** Even carefully gathered evidence can be ruled inadmissible by a specific judge in a specific proceeding. Rules vary by jurisdiction.
- **Replace an advocate.** For DV/IPV situations, an advocate's specific expertise in evidence gathering for your jurisdiction and situation is essential.
- **Tell you what to do with the evidence.** Gathering evidence is one thing; deciding whether and how to use it is another. The use decision depends on factors outside this page.
- **Work for every type of situation.** Some situations are so resource-constrained, so high-risk, or so specific that generic guidance does not apply. Professional consultation becomes essential.

## 11. Key Takeaways

1. **External evidence carries more weight than personal documentation in most evidentiary contexts.** The credibility discount makes this especially important for autistic readers.

2. **Communications from the person causing harm are the highest-value evidence.** Their own words are not "your version" of anything; they are the original source.

3. **Institutional records carry automatic credibility.** Medical records, police reports, workplace records, and similar institutional documentation are treated as reliable in ways personal documentation is not.

4. **Pattern evidence beats single-incident evidence.** The shift from one event to documented pattern across multiple sources is qualitatively different.

5. **Timing matters.** Early preservation is much easier than late reconstruction. Records degrade, witnesses move, memory fades.

6. **Recording laws vary by jurisdiction.** Check before recording anything. An illegally-obtained recording can damage your position.

7. **Storage safety is part of evidence strategy.** Evidence stored where the person causing harm can find it is not protective evidence.

8. **The follow-up email technique is a force multiplier.** Summarizing verbal conversations in writing creates both a record and a diagnostic tool.

9. **Evidence gathering has costs.** Retraumatization, discovery risk, legal exposure, psychological weight. The benefits usually outweigh the costs in protective contexts, but the costs are real.

10. **Consult professionals for high-stakes evidence gathering.** Lawyers for legal matters, advocates for DV matters, employment lawyers for workplace matters. The consultation is almost always worth the cost.
