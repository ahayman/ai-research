# Deep Dive Exploration: Internal Family Systems (IFS) Therapy — Autism, Alexithymia, and Complex Trauma Intersection

## Tone for Downstream Agents

Rigorous and skeptical-respectful. The user is an adult autistic systems thinker with suspected alexithymia and a complex-trauma history. They want the model's actual mechanics and a clear-eyed view of evidence and contested claims, not therapeutic marketing copy. Where evidence is thin, say so. Where IFS Institute marketing claims more than the research supports, name that gap. Where critics overreach, name that too. No false reassurance; trust the reader. Distinguish carefully throughout between (a) what IFS Institute teaches as canon, (b) what peer-reviewed research has established, (c) what skeptical clinicians argue, and (d) what autistic and alexithymic clients/clinicians report. These are four different evidence registers and they should not blur.

A central tension to honor across every category: **IFS depends on noticing and "feeling into" internal states. The user's interoceptive access is low. Externalized signals — written reflection, structured representation, behavior, data — work better. The research must engage this honestly, not paper over it.**

## Topic Overview

Internal Family Systems (IFS) is a psychotherapeutic model developed by Richard C. Schwartz beginning in the early 1980s, originally emerging from his clinical work with bulimic patients at the Institute for Juvenile Research in Chicago. The model proposes that the human mind is naturally multiple — composed of "parts" (subpersonalities) organized into protective roles (managers and firefighters) and wounded ones (exiles) — surrounding a core "Self" said to embody eight qualities (the 8 Cs: curiosity, calm, compassion, clarity, courage, confidence, creativity, connectedness). Healing, in the IFS frame, comes from the Self building relationships with parts, witnessing their burdens, and helping them "unburden" — release the painful beliefs and feelings absorbed during overwhelm.

IFS has become extraordinarily popular over the past decade, particularly in trauma-informed clinical communities and in self-help circles around Schwartz's bestselling 2021 book *No Bad Parts*. In November 2015 it was added to SAMHSA's National Registry for Evidence-Based Programs and Practices (NREPP) — but importantly, NREPP was effectively shuttered when its federal contract was terminated in December 2017 and the registry was phased out in 2018. Marketing copy that says "IFS is on the SAMHSA registry" is referring to a registry that no longer exists; the current "samhsa-nrepp.foundationifs.org" mirror is a private archive maintained by the IFS Foundation, not a federal endorsement. The 2015 listing was also for "improvement of general functioning and well-being," not for PTSD or any specific clinical condition. IFS has been endorsed by Bessel van der Kolk and other prominent trauma authors. As of April 2024, 45,764 therapists on PsychologyToday.com listed IFS as a modality, and the IFS Institute has built a tiered training and certification pipeline (Level 1, 2, 3 plus Certification) at $4,000–$5,000+ per level.

The empirical picture is more constrained than the popular reception suggests. A 2025 scoping review of 27 IFS studies found only two RCTs (~7% of the literature). The strongest single study remains the 2013 rheumatoid-arthritis RCT showing sustained benefits in pain, depression, and self-compassion. Pilot work supports IFS for PTSD and depression. Critics — including a 2024 *Society for the Advancement of Psychotherapy* analysis and a *Nation* feature — argue the model's claims (universality of Self, ontology of "parts," "no bad parts") run far ahead of what's been validated, and that the IFS Institute's commercial structure (centralized certification under Schwartz, expensive paywalls, expanding "unattached burdens" doctrines) mixes scientific ambition with proprietary branding in ways that warrant scrutiny.

For the autism/alexithymia/complex-trauma intersection, IFS sits at a structural fault line. Its mechanics rely on noticing inner sensations, "feeling toward" parts, and using imagery and metaphor to track inner figures. Roughly 50% of autistic adults meet criteria for alexithymia (vs. ~5% in neurotypical samples), and recent research suggests it is alexithymia, not autism per se, that disrupts interoceptive accuracy. This means a substantial portion of autistic adults bring to IFS exactly the perceptual profile the model leans on least gracefully. A small but growing body of writing — by autistic IFS practitioners like Sarah Bergenfield and others on Substack — proposes adaptations: externalizing parts onto cards or figurines, journaling-led parts work, accepting cognitive ("hearing in mind") access rather than felt-sense access, and reframing autism as "hardware" not a "part." Whether these adaptations are extensions of IFS or a meaningful departure from it is itself a contested question.

## Key Facts & Statistics

- **~50% of autistic adults meet criteria for alexithymia** (weighted mean prevalence 49.93% across studies), compared to **~5% in neurotypical groups** — alexithymia, not autism itself, predicts impaired interoceptive accuracy [Source: [PMC: Investigating alexithymia in autism — meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6331035/)] [Source: [Brewer et al., Alexithymia, not autism, is associated with impaired interoception](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4962768/)]
- **Only ~7% of the IFS evidence base is RCT-quality.** A 2025 scoping review of 27 IFS studies found 2 RCTs (7%), 17 case studies (63%), 5 quasi-experimental, 3 qualitative — despite ~40 years of model development [Source: [Tandfonline: Scoping review of IFS evidence](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/13284207.2025.2533127)]
- **45,764 therapists** advertised IFS on PsychologyToday.com as of April 2024 — adoption far outstrips empirical validation [Source: [Society for the Advancement of Psychotherapy: Problematic Popularity](https://societyforpsychotherapy.org/internal-family-systems-exploring-its-problematic-popularity/)]
- **The 2013 rheumatoid-arthritis RCT** (Shadick et al., *J Rheumatol*) — the strongest single IFS trial — found sustained reductions in pain, depression, and improvements in self-compassion at 1-year follow-up vs. education control (n=79) [Source: [PubMed: Shadick et al. 2013 RA RCT](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23950186/)]
- **In Hodgdon et al.'s PTSD pilot, 92% of completers no longer met PTSD criteria at 1-month follow-up.** Important caveats: this was an uncontrolled effectiveness pilot with **n=17**, conducted under IFS Foundation funding, with effect sizes (e.g., d=−4.46 for PTSD severity) that are implausibly large for any psychotherapy and almost certainly reflect both the very small sample and selection effects. Treat this as preliminary signal at best, not as confirmatory evidence [Source: [Hodgdon et al. PTSD pilot effectiveness study](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10926771.2021.2013375)]
- **IFS Level 1 training costs $3,990–$5,300** for ~100 hours, with additional certification ($200), recertification every two years ($150), and Level 2/3 fees stacked on top — concentrating the training pipeline through a single Institute controlled by the founder [Source: [The Nation: The Rapid and Dubious Rise](https://www.thenation.com/article/society/internal-family-systems-scam/)]

## Category Taxonomy

### Category 1: The Parts Model — Managers, Firefighters, Exiles, and Self

- **Slug**: `parts-model-and-self`
- **Summary**: The foundational taxonomy of IFS: the mind as composed of "parts" organized into three functional roles — Managers (proactive protectors), Firefighters (reactive protectors), and Exiles (wounded parts carrying burdens) — surrounding a core "Self" believed to be undamaged by trauma. Covers what each part type looks like, how they relate, and why the model frames them as having "non-extreme" intentions even when their behavior is destructive.
- **Why It Matters**: This is the conceptual scaffolding everything else hangs on. Without understanding the three protector/exile structure and what IFS means by "Self," none of the mechanics, evidence, or adaptations make sense. It is also where the user's first decision point sits: do these categories map onto their own experience, and does the language work for a literal-thinking system?
- **Key Sub-Aspects**:
  - Managers: planning, controlling, organizing, perfectionism, self-criticism
  - Firefighters: numbing, distraction, addiction, dissociation, rage outbursts
  - Exiles: shame, terror, grief, vulnerability, frozen-in-time trauma states
  - Self: the eight C qualities (curiosity, calm, compassion, clarity, courage, confidence, creativity, connectedness) and what IFS claims about its undamageability
  - The "no bad parts" thesis — what it means and what it does not mean
- **Content Depth Available**: high
- **Dependencies**: None — this is the entry point
- **Sources**: [IFS Institute: Model Outline](https://ifs-institute.com/resources/articles/internal-family-systems-model-outline), [Wikipedia: IFS Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_Family_Systems_Model), [Williamsburg Therapy Group: Managers, Exiles, Firefighters](https://williamsburgtherapygroup.com/blog/managers-exiles-and-firefighters-how-internal-family-systems-therapy-works)

### Category 2: Mechanics and Process — Unblending, Witnessing, Unburdening, and the 6 Fs

- **Slug**: `mechanics-process-6-fs`
- **Summary**: The actual procedural how-to of IFS sessions: the 6-F sequence (Find, Focus, Flesh out, Feel toward, beFriend, Fears) for getting to know a protector; the unblending move that separates Self from a flooded part; the witnessing process where Self listens to an exile's story; and the formal unburdening ritual involving "do-overs," retrieval, and elemental release. Includes the distinction between in-sight access (parts perceived internally) and direct access (therapist speaking to a part directly when in-sight is not possible).
- **Why It Matters**: This is where the model meets the user's load-bearing question. Each step assumes interoceptive and visualization capacity. "Find" asks where in the body. "Focus" asks for sustained inward attention. "Feel toward" asks for an internally-sensed emotional read. A reader with low interoceptive access needs to know exactly which steps are negotiable and which are structural — and where adaptations exist.
- **Key Sub-Aspects**:
  - The 6 Fs in detail with what each step actually requires of the client
  - Unblending: the prerequisite for all further work
  - Witnessing the exile's story
  - Unburdening rituals (light, water, fire, earth, wind)
  - In-sight vs. direct access
  - "Do-over" and "retrieval" — corrective experiences in imagination
- **Content Depth Available**: high
- **Dependencies**: Requires understanding of Category 1 (parts model)
- **Sources**: [Therapy with Alessio: 6 Fs](https://www.therapywithalessio.com/articles/the-6-fs-in-ifs-the-6-steps-to-get-to-know-our-protectors), [Way of Belonging: Steps for Healing an Exile](https://www.wayofbelonging.com/post/steps-for-healing-an-exile-in-ifs), [PESI: 6-Step IFS Process](https://www.pesi.com/blogs/6-step-ifs-process-to-jumpstart-healing/)

### Category 3: Origins, Theoretical Claims, and the Ontology of Parts

- **Slug**: `origins-ontology-parts`
- **Summary**: How IFS came into being — Schwartz's 1980s bulimia outcome study at the Institute for Juvenile Research where clients described autonomous "parts" that family-therapy reorganization could not reach — and what the model actually claims about the mind. Covers the multiplicity-of-mind thesis (multiplicity as default, not pathology), the historical lineage through Assagioli's psychosynthesis and Jung's complexes, and the unresolved question of whether "parts" are metaphor, neural networks/dissociated affective states, or something the IFS Institute increasingly treats as ontologically real (as in Falconer's "unattached burdens" doctrine).
- **Why It Matters**: For a systems-thinker who needs to know what is being claimed before deciding whether to use it, this is essential ground. The model's clinical leverage and its credibility problem both come from the same place: it speaks of "parts" with enough specificity to be operationally useful, while remaining deliberately vague about whether they are real entities, useful fictions, or phenomenological metaphors. Schwartz himself has shifted on this over time.
- **Key Sub-Aspects**:
  - The 1982 bulimia outcome study and the clinical breakthrough
  - Schwartz's family-therapy roots (Bowen, Minuchin, structural family therapy)
  - Antecedents: Assagioli's psychosynthesis, Jungian complexes, Berne's ego states
  - Multiplicity-of-mind as default thesis
  - Metaphor vs. neural-network framing vs. literal subpersonalities
  - Falsifiability problem: empirical evidence for distinct subpersonalities is lacking
- **Content Depth Available**: high
- **Dependencies**: Requires Category 1 for vocabulary
- **Sources**: [Mad in America: Interview with Richard Schwartz](https://www.madinamerica.com/2022/07/interview-richard-schwartz-ifs/), [Society for the Advancement of Psychotherapy: Problematic Popularity](https://societyforpsychotherapy.org/internal-family-systems-exploring-its-problematic-popularity/), [Parts & Self: Co-Constructions Origins](https://partsandself.org/co-constructions-the-origins-of-parts/), [Medium: Ontology of Parts](https://medium.com/@babunow99/the-ontology-of-parts-4d13bfbbc4c4)

### Category 4: Evidence Base — What Has Actually Been Validated

- **Slug**: `evidence-base-validated`
- **Summary**: An honest accounting of what RCT and pilot evidence supports. The 2013 Shadick et al. rheumatoid-arthritis RCT (n=79, sustained 1-year benefits in pain, depression, self-compassion); the Haddock 2016 depression RCT in female university students showing equivalence to CBT/IPT (not superiority); the Hodgdon et al. PTSD pilot effectiveness study (uncontrolled, n=17, IFS-Foundation-funded, effect sizes implausibly large — preliminary signal at best); the 2025 PARTS-SUD substance-use pilot. Examines what SAMHSA NREPP inclusion (November 2015) actually meant — and the fact that NREPP was effectively shuttered in 2018, so "SAMHSA-listed" is now a marketing claim about a defunct registry rather than current federal endorsement. Catalogues what remains unvalidated: the existence of distinct subpersonalities as discriminable entities, the universality of Self, and most condition-specific claims beyond the three studies above.
- **Why It Matters**: The user needs to know what claims are backed by what level of evidence so they can calibrate trust. "IFS is evidence-based" is technically true but does heavy lifting — it generally means "has at least one RCT for one specific condition" rather than "has a robust replicated literature." Distinguishing pilot from confirmatory work, within-group from between-group effects, and IFS-Foundation-funded from independent studies, is essential for non-credulous reading.
- **Key Sub-Aspects**:
  - 2013 rheumatoid-arthritis RCT (Shadick et al.): design, findings, what it does and does not establish
  - Haddock 2016 depression RCT: equivalence to CBT/IPT, not superiority
  - Hodgdon et al. PTSD pilot (2021): n=17, uncontrolled, IFS-Foundation-funded — preliminary signal, not confirmatory evidence
  - 2025 scoping review findings: 2 RCTs out of 27 studies (~7% RCT rate)
  - 2025 PARTS-SUD substance-use pilot
  - SAMHSA NREPP listing: what inclusion did and didn't certify, and why "currently SAMHSA-endorsed" is misleading because NREPP was discontinued in 2018
  - Conditions where evidence is missing or thin: anxiety, eating disorders (despite IFS's origin in bulimia work), dissociative disorders, autism, alexithymia, all neurodivergent populations
  - Funding-source bias: how much of the IFS evidence base is produced or funded by the IFS Foundation/Institute
- **Content Depth Available**: medium-high
- **Dependencies**: Helpful to understand Category 1–2 first
- **Sources**: [PubMed: Shadick et al. 2013 RA RCT](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23950186/), [Tandfonline: 2025 Scoping Review](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/13284207.2025.2533127), [Tandfonline: Hodgdon PTSD Pilot](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10926771.2021.2013375), [PubMed: Haddock Depression RCT](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27500908/)

### Category 5: Critiques, Controversies, and Commercial Dynamics

Examines the IFS Institute's commercial structure (Schwartz as founder/face/IP-holder/certification gatekeeper), the tiered $4K+ training paywall, and the model's epistemic vulnerabilities: difficulty falsifying the existence of distinct parts, Self-energy as unfalsifiable when absent ("a part is in the way"), and "no bad parts" as a near-tautological claim. **Important disambiguation**: the Castlewood Treatment Center recovered-memory lawsuits (2010s) involved Mark Schwartz, no relation to Richard Schwartz; readers regularly confuse the two and the deep dive should make this distinction cleanly. - **Why It Matters**: A systems thinker reading IFS marketing material will hit a wall of frictionless universalism — every part has a positive intention, all clients have an undamaged Self, the model fits everyone. That's a red flag pattern. This category gives the reader the critical apparatus to read primary IFS sources without being captured by them, and to distinguish what Schwartz and the Institute claim from what the field has actually established.

### Category 6: IFS and Alexithymia — The Interoception Problem

- **Slug**: `ifs-and-alexithymia`
- **Summary**: The structural mismatch between IFS mechanics and low interoceptive access — and what the literature, clinicians, and clients actually do about it. IFS asks "where do you notice this part in your body?" and "how do you feel toward it?" Alexithymia disrupts both questions. This category examines whether IFS can train interoceptive capacity over time (Gendlin's Focusing literature is informative here, since Focusing has wrestled with the same problem for longer), whether clients can access parts cognitively or visually instead of viscerally, and what specific adaptations IFS practitioners working with alexithymic clients use. Engages honestly with the question: is this a poor fit, a slow fit, or a fit that requires structural modification of the model?
- **Why It Matters**: This is the user's central question, and it deserves its own page — not folded into autism, not folded into trauma. Roughly half of autistic adults are alexithymic, but alexithymia exists in non-autistic populations too, and the interoception-IFS tension is a feature of the model itself, not of any single neurotype. Treating it as its own object lets the reader see the mechanism clearly: which IFS steps require interoception, which can be substituted, and what is lost or gained in substitution.
- **Key Sub-Aspects**:
  - What alexithymia is and what it disrupts (interoceptive accuracy, affect granularity, emotional labeling)
  - Which IFS steps depend on interoception (Find, Focus, Flesh out, Feel toward)
  - Cognitive access ("hearing parts in mind"), visual access (seeing without feeling), and externalized access (objects, drawings, journaling)
  - Gendlin's Focusing tradition and the parallel "felt sense" problem
  - Whether interoceptive capacity can be built — and if so, gently, without overshoot
  - When alexithymia is a contraindication vs. an adaptation challenge
  - Risk: "fake" or performed parts work that satisfies the protocol without doing the work
- **Content Depth Available**: medium (literature is thinner than autism or trauma; much from clinician blogs and Focusing tradition)
- **Dependencies**: Requires Categories 1, 2
- **Sources**: [IFS Telehealth Collective: Supporting Your Neurotype](https://ifstherapyonline.com/ifs-telehealth-collective-blog/supporting-your-neurotype-with-internal-family-systems), [PMC: Alexithymia general deficit of interoception](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5098957/), [Brewer et al.: Alexithymia not autism impairs interoception](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4962768/), [Therapy Wisdom: Gendlin Focusing](https://therapywisdom.com/gendlins-6-steps-of-focusing-with-jan-winhall/), [Self Care Spot: Autism and IFS](https://www.theselfcarespot.com/post/autism-and-ifs-therapy)

### Category 9: Failure Modes, Contraindications, and the Self-Cannot-Be-Reached Problem

- **Slug**: `failure-modes-contraindications`
- **Summary**: An explicit catalog of what goes wrong in IFS work. Premature unburdening (rushing to release before protectors consent or trust is built) and its retraumatization risk. Spiritual bypass — using "parts" language and "compassion for all parts" to avoid present-tense responsibility, particularly in conflict with abusive others or one's own behavior. Therapist over-reliance on Self-energy framing such that the absence of Self gets reframed as "a part in the way" — an unfalsifiable move. The self-cannot-be-reached problem in chronic dissociation or severe dysregulation, where the model says everyone has Self but in practice some clients cannot access it without significant nervous-system stabilization first. Populations and presentations where IFS may be a poor fit: active psychosis, severe untreated DID without phase-oriented prerequisites, settings without external safety, contexts where externalizing inner conflict masks ongoing external harm.
- **Why It Matters**: Knowing the failure modes is how a sophisticated reader uses any model. Without this category, IFS marketing tells the user only its success stories. The user's complex-trauma history makes some of these failure modes load-bearing concerns — particularly retraumatization and the spiritual-bypass risk in "compassion for all parts" applied to abusers.
- **Key Sub-Aspects**:
  - Premature unburdening: protector consent, witnessing-first, the do-over step
  - Retraumatization risk and its prevention
  - Spiritual bypass via IFS language ("merciful manager" pattern; forgiveness-as-bypass)
  - Self-energy as unfalsifiable category (when absent, "a part is in the way")
  - "Parts" language as deflection from accountability
  - Severe dissociation and the limit of pure IFS
  - When external safety or stabilization is the prior task
  - "Forced" or "scripted" unburdening — Self is not present, a part is doing the unburdening

### Category 10: Self-Led IFS, Journaling, and Adaptations for Externalized Processing

- **Slug**: `self-led-journaling-externalized`
- **Summary**: The practical territory of doing IFS-adjacent work outside or alongside therapy, with explicit attention to written/journaling adaptations because writing is how the user processes best. ### Category 11: Psychedelic-Assisted IFS, Spirituality, and the "Unattached Burdens" Turn

- **Slug**: `psychedelic-spirituality-unattached-burdens`
- **Summary**: Where the IFS model is currently heading — and the boundary between IFS as a clinical modality and IFS as a spiritually-framed movement. Covers four overlapping developments. (1) **Psychedelic-assisted IFS**: the growing intersection between IFS and ketamine-, MDMA-, and psilocybin-assisted therapy, including the MAPS MDMA-PTSD trials where roughly 80% of high-dose participants spontaneously generated parts language without prompting; the "MAPS-friendly" therapist communities that lean IFS; and the conceptual fit between psychedelic ego-softening and "unblending." (2) **The "unattached burdens" doctrine**: Robert Falconer's 2023 book *The Others Within Us* (with a foreword by Schwartz) frames some burdens as not originating in the client's own history but as transpersonal/discarnate intrusions. Schwartz endorses this framing publicly. Mainstream clinical psychology has not engaged with it. (3) **Schwartz's spiritual reframings**: the Sounds True course *The Spirituality of IFS*, public statements affirming phenomena "that cannot be explained by conventional science," and IFS-as-contemplative-practice positioning. (4) **The legacy burdens / generational unburdening expansion**: ancestral and lineage work, often blending IFS with epigenetic-trauma framing. The category honestly examines whether these are extensions of a clinical model or a meaningful change in what IFS *is* — without either dismissing them outright or endorsing them as established.
- **Why It Matters**: For a systems thinker who needs to know the actual shape of the model they're evaluating, this is decisive. The IFS that gets practiced under "IFS-Informed" therapy and that appears in *No Bad Parts* is a clinical psychotherapy model. The IFS that the founder is currently teaching at Sounds True and writing about with Falconer is something with strong overlap to spiritual direction and parapsychology. A reader needs to know that these are both real and to be able to tell which version of IFS a given practitioner, book, or training is operating in. It also matters because psychedelic-assisted therapy is becoming more accessible (Oregon, Colorado, FDA pathways), and IFS is rapidly becoming a default conceptual framework for that work — so the reader will encounter this overlap whether or not they seek it out.
- **Key Sub-Aspects**:
  - MAPS MDMA-PTSD trials and the spontaneous parts-language phenomenon (~80% of high-dose participants)
  - Ketamine-assisted IFS — emerging clinical practice and the IFS Institute's positioning
  - Conceptual fit: ego-softening/dissolution and unblending
  - Falconer's *The Others Within Us* (2023) and the "unattached burdens" doctrine
  - Schwartz's foreword to Falconer and his *Spirituality of IFS* course (Sounds True)
  - Legacy burdens, generational/ancestral unburdening
  - Where the model crosses from clinical psychology into spiritual direction or parapsychology
  - How to identify which "version" of IFS a practitioner, author, or training is operating in
  - Why mainstream clinical psychology has largely declined to engage with the spirit-framed expansions
  - Implications for safety: dissociation risk, suggestibility under altered states, recovered-memory and confabulation concerns
- **Content Depth Available**: medium-high (good primary sources from Schwartz, Falconer, MAPS; thinner critical engagement; substantial coverage of psychedelic-assisted therapy generally)
- **Dependencies**: Requires Categories 1, 3 (ontology of parts), 5 (critiques baseline)
- **Sources**: [Robert Falconer: Unattached Burdens](https://robertfalconer.us/blog/robertfalconer/ifs-model/unattached-burdens/), [Falconer: *The Others Within Us*](https://www.amazon.com/Others-Within-Us-Internal-Possession/dp/B0C12JXVBJ), [Sounds True: The Spirituality of IFS](https://www.soundstrue.com/products/the-spirituality-of-internal-family-systems), [MAPS MAPP1 MDMA-PTSD trial](https://maps.org/mdma/ptsd/), [IFS Institute: Psychedelic-Assisted Therapy positioning](https://ifs-institute.com/), [Mad in America: A Mad Perspective on IFS Training](https://www.madinamerica.com/2025/07/a-mad-perspective-on-ifs-training/)

1. **Category 1: The Parts Model** — All other content depends on this vocabulary. Start here.
2. **Category 2: Mechanics and Process** — The 6 Fs and unburdening. With this, the rest of the deep dive can be read as commentary on what these mechanics actually require.
3. **Category 3: Origins, Theoretical Claims, and Ontology of Parts** — Now that the reader knows what is being claimed, examine where it came from and whether the claims hold up.
4. **Category 4: Evidence Base** — What has actually been validated. Sets a calibration anchor before reading either marketing or critiques.
5. **Category 5: Critiques, Controversies, and Commercial Dynamics** — With Categories 1–4 in place, the critical literature lands as informed critique rather than knee-jerk skepticism.
6. **Category 11: Psychedelic-Assisted IFS, Spirituality, and Unattached Burdens** — Read after the critiques baseline so the spiritual/psychedelic turn lands as "where the model is currently heading," not as a side-issue. 7. **Category 6: IFS and Alexithymia** — Begin the intersection block with the user's central question. Place this first because it is a feature of the model itself, not of any specific neurotype.
8. **Category 7: IFS and Autism** — Build on Category 6 (alexithymia overlap) and add the autism-specific layer.
9. **Category 8: IFS and Complex Trauma** — Trauma is where the strongest evidence sits but also where the deepest contraindications appear. Read this third in the intersection block because failure modes (Cat 9) require it.

## Potential Overlaps & Boundaries

- **Category 6 (Alexithymia) vs Category 7 (Autism)**: ~50% comorbidity and shared interoception challenges create overlap, but they are distinct intersection problems. Alexithymia is a feature of the model's structural dependence on internal sensing; autism brings a separate bundle (literal thinking, masking, monotropism, sensory profile). Keep them separate. Cross-reference, do not merge.
- **Category 5 (Critiques) vs Category 9 (Failure Modes)**: Critiques target the model's claims and commercial structure (epistemic level); failure modes target what goes wrong in practice (clinical level). They are related but answer different questions. Separating them lets the reader see that "critique-level concerns" and "practice-level concerns" are different objects.
- **Category 3 (Ontology) vs Category 5 (Critiques)**: The ontology question (what are parts, really?) is a critique target but is best given its own page because it is also a positive theoretical claim with intellectual content of its own. Treat ontology as a steel-man, then let Category 5 examine where the steel-man fails.
- **Category 8 (Complex Trauma) vs Category 9 (Failure Modes)**: Some complex-trauma presentations are themselves failure modes for pure IFS (severe dissociation, no Self access). Cross-reference, but the trauma category should be primary trauma content; the failure-modes category should be the place where the limits are catalogued in their own right.
- **Category 2 (Mechanics) vs Category 10 (Self-Led)**: Many self-led practices are simplifications of full mechanics. The mechanics page should describe the full clinical procedure; the self-led page should describe what survives translation to written/solo practice and what is lost.
- **Category 5 (Critiques) vs Category 11 (Psychedelic/Spirituality)**: Critiques summarize the doctrinal-expansion problem; Cat 11 owns the substantive content of where the model is going (psychedelic-assisted contexts, "unattached burdens," Schwartz's spiritual reframings). Cat 5 should reference Cat 11 for the full treatment rather than duplicate it.
- **Category 11 (Psychedelic/Spirituality) vs Category 8 (Complex Trauma)**: Significant overlap because most psychedelic-IFS work is in trauma contexts. Cat 8 should mention psychedelic-IFS as one trauma-modality option; Cat 11 should be the primary location for examining what changes about IFS when conducted under altered states.

## Research Gaps

Several gaps surfaced in the search and should be flagged for the per-category research agents to attempt to fill or honestly report:

- **No peer-reviewed studies of IFS specifically in autistic populations**, alexithymic populations, or in clients with documented low interoceptive accuracy. The autistic-clinician writing on adaptations is almost entirely on Substack and clinician blogs, not in peer-reviewed journals. Per-category research should make this gap explicit rather than glossing it.
- **Limited evidence on long-term outcomes of self-led IFS** in any population, and effectively none in complex-trauma populations. Schwartz's books make claims that haven't been tested at the self-led level.
- **The "unattached burdens" / spirit-possession turn** (Falconer, *The Others Within Us*, 2023) — now treated as a substantive object in its own right (Category 11) rather than a sub-aspect of critiques. There is still no mainstream clinical-psychology engagement with these claims; the deep-dive should describe what the doctrine says without endorsing it, and report the engagement gap explicitly.
- **Schwartz's centralized control of training and IP** is documented in critical journalism (*The Nation*, Mad in America) but has not been studied as a sociological or science-policy phenomenon in academic literature.
- **Alexithymia + IFS specifically** is discussed by IFS-clinician practitioners in blogs and one IFS Telehealth Collective post but lacks peer-reviewed empirical examination. Adjacent literatures (Focusing/Gendlin + alexithymia; expressive writing + alexithymia) are the closest empirical anchors and should be drawn on.
- **No comparative effectiveness studies** between IFS and alternative parts-work modalities (Ego State, Sensorimotor, TIST). Comparisons are made on theoretical grounds, not empirical ones.
- **Aphantasia + IFS** is mentioned as an adaptation challenge but I found no substantive treatment of it. Per-category research on Category 7 (autism) should attempt to dig further.
- **Daniel Lobel and other named IFS critics** were difficult to locate primary sources for in this round; per-category research on Category 5 should pursue the named-critic literature more thoroughly.
