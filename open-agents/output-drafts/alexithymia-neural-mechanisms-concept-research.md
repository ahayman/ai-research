# Concept Research — Neural Mechanisms of Alexithymia

## Why this category exists

Categories 1–3 establish *what alexithymia is* (a contested trait construct), *how it fractures* (seven heterogeneity axes), and *how it is measured* (six instruments, none sufficient alone). Category 4 asks the next question: where in the brain does this construct sit, and what mechanisms generate the phenotype?

The mechanism question matters in three concrete ways:

1. **Therapy matching**. If a person's alexithymia is principally a failure of interoceptive *detection*, body-focused awareness training is plausibly indicated. If it is principally a failure of *interpretation/labelling* of signals already detected, the same intervention may amplify symptom intensity rather than help.
2. **Subtype validity**. The behavioural distinctions in Category 2 (Type I vs Type II, primary vs secondary, parsing vs detection) gain or lose credibility depending on whether they map onto separable brain mechanisms.
3. **Construct legitimacy**. Several competing theoretical frames make different mechanistic claims (Sifneos psychoanalytic, Bird interoceptive, Barrett constructed-emotion, Quattrocki-Friston predictive-processing, Preece attention-appraisal). The neural evidence is the arena where they contest each other.

The honest answer the user needs to take into Categories 5–11 is: **alexithymia does not have a single neural address**. The most replicated single finding is anterior-insula reduction. Beyond that, the picture is multi-system and probably multiroute (Hobson, Brewer, Catmur & Bird 2019). Different alexithymics may have different upstream causes converging on the same downstream phenotype.

---

## 1. Anterior insula — the most replicated single finding

### Structural evidence

The anterior insular cortex (AIC) shows reduced grey matter volume in alexithymic populations across multiple studies. The meta-analytic synthesis by van der Velde, Servaas, Goerlich, Bruggeman, Horton, Costafreda and Aleman (2013, *Neuroscience and Biobehavioral Reviews*) compiled functional imaging studies and identified the anterior insula and the dorsal anterior cingulate as the two most consistently implicated regions. A subsequent voxel-based morphometry meta-analysis by Xu et al. (2018) reached convergent conclusions for grey-matter reductions specifically in the left anterior insula.

### Functional evidence

During emotional-stimulus processing (face viewing, emotional words, autobiographical recall), high-alexithymia participants show reduced activation in anterior insula compared to non-alexithymic controls. The signal is more reliable for negative-valence stimuli; positive-valence findings are sparser, partly because most paradigms use negative material.

### Lesion evidence — the strongest causal claim

The single most cited piece of evidence for anterior-insula causality in alexithymia is **Hogeveen, Bird, Chau, Krueger and Grafman (2016)**, *Neuropsychologia* (PMC4752907). Sample: 200 Vietnam War veterans with focal penetrating brain injuries, plus 51 non-injured controls. Method: voxel-based lesion-symptom mapping of TAS-20 scores against quantified lesion location and volume.

Key findings:

- **>15% of anterior-insula volume lost reliably produces post-injury alexithymia** in previously non-alexithymic adults.
- The dose-response holds **after controlling for ACC damage and total lesion volume** — i.e., the effect is specific to AI rather than generic cortical injury.
- About **15% of brain-injury patients meet alexithymia criteria** post-injury, a substantially elevated rate over the ~10% general-population prevalence.

The Hogeveen study is the cleanest causal evidence the field has. Lesion-symptom mapping bypasses the correlation-vs-causation problem of fMRI, and the controlled covariate analysis isolates the AI as the locus rather than as a generic damaged-brain signature.

**Caveat for the user**: this evidence speaks to *acquired/organic* alexithymia (Messina et al. 2014's third category). It demonstrates that AI is *sufficient* for an alexithymia phenotype, but it does not establish that *primary* developmental alexithymia originates from the same locus, even if it shows convergent AI involvement on imaging.

### What the AI is doing functionally

The anterior insula is the cortical hub for **interoceptive integration** (Craig 2009; Critchley & Garfinkel 2017). It receives ascending visceral and bodily inputs, integrates them with descending cognitive context, and is implicated in subjective awareness of bodily state. The dominant mechanistic story is that AI dysfunction (reduced volume, reduced activation, dose-dependent lesion effects) impairs the integration step that converts raw bodily afferents into the conscious *felt sense* on which emotion-labelling depends. This is the architectural substrate for the interoceptive-deficit account in §4.

## 2. Anterior cingulate cortex — salience and emotional appraisal

### Structural and functional findings

The **dorsal/supragenual ACC** shows altered activation in alexithymia across multiple studies. The pattern is somewhat counter-intuitive: high-alexithymia participants often show **hyperactivation** of supragenual ACC during emotional stimuli, alongside reduced AI activation. The interpretation in van der Velde 2013 and subsequent work: the ACC is doing extra work to compensate for impoverished interoceptive integration upstream — effortful salience-detection without the visceral signal that would normally accompany it.

Subgenual/perigenual ACC findings are more mixed. Some studies report reduced activation; others find no difference. The reliability is lower than for AI.

### GABA findings

Magnetic-resonance-spectroscopy studies of ACC GABA concentration in alexithymia are sparse. A small literature reports reduced GABA in high-alexithymia participants, consistent with reduced inhibitory tone and possibly with the hyperactivation finding. This is not yet a large evidence base; treat as suggestive, not established.

### What the ACC is doing functionally

ACC is part of the salience network with AI. It is implicated in detecting motivationally relevant stimuli and signalling the need for attentional or behavioural adjustment. The ACC-AI dyad is therefore the critical pair for the salience model of emotion: a signal needs both visceral integration (AI) and salience tagging (ACC) to become a felt emotion accessible for naming. Alexithymia at this level is plausibly a salience-network dysfunction, not just an AI dysfunction.

## 3. Amygdala, orbitofrontal cortex, medial prefrontal cortex

### Amygdala

Reduced amygdala activation in alexithymia during emotional-face viewing has been reported in several studies (e.g., Reker et al. 2010; Kano et al. 2003) but **replication is mixed**. Some studies find reduced amygdala activation specifically for negative-valence faces; others find no difference; a minority find increased activation. Possible reasons for inconsistency: high heterogeneity within "alexithymic" samples, different paradigms, different baseline contrasts, and small samples.

Amygdala-ACC coupling has been reported as reduced in alexithymia by Pohl, Kraemer et al. (2018) using resting-state fMRI. This is consistent with a reduced top-down regulatory loop, but again, the replication base is limited.

### Orbitofrontal cortex (OFC)

OFC findings show reduced activation during emotional-decision tasks in some studies (e.g., Bagby et al. 2010 on monetary-reward processing) and reduced grey matter in voxel-based morphometry. The OFC is implicated in valuation and reward learning, and the alexithymia phenotype's selective impairment under decision-ambiguity (Zhang et al. 2017; see Category 11 trade-off material) is consistent with OFC-mediated somatic-marker dysfunction.

### Medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC)

The mPFC is part of the default mode network and is implicated in self-referential processing and emotion-concept generation. Alexithymia is associated with altered mPFC activation in self-evaluation and emotion-attribution tasks, consistent with Barrett's constructed-emotion frame (§7) where mPFC is part of the conceptual-categorisation system.

### Net assessment

Amygdala / OFC / mPFC findings are **suggestive but less robust** than AI findings. The reasons are partly methodological (smaller samples, more heterogeneity) and partly substantive (these regions are not in the salience network, so the alexithymia signal is more diluted). For downstream content, the AI and ACC findings should be presented as well-replicated; amygdala/OFC/mPFC should be presented as plausible-but-mixed.

## 4. The Bird/Murphy/Brewer multidomain interoception lineage — corrected attribution

### Why this matters and the universal correction

The parent exploration's framing — "Bird walked back the interoception-deficit hypothesis" — is **incorrect**. Two different things were conflated:

- The **autism-specific claim** that interoceptive deficits underlie autism. *This* the Bird group did walk back: variance loads on alexithymia rather than on autism (Shah, Hall, Catmur & Bird 2016).
- The **alexithymia claim** that alexithymia involves a multidomain interoception failure. The Bird group has *strengthened* this position, not retreated.

The walk-back is asymmetric. A separate independent group (Nicholson, Williams, Grainger, Christensen, Calvo-Merino & Gaigg 2018, PMC6089261) published a third-party challenge using cardiac heartbeat counting that supports the null hypothesis. That paper is from City University London / UCL — not from the Bird lab — and represents an external challenge, not an internal walk-back.

### The actual publication trajectory

**(a) Brewer, Cook & Bird (2016).** "Alexithymia: a general deficit of interoception." *Royal Society Open Science* 3(10):150664. PMC5098957. Foundational paper. N=208 (105 typical adults + 103 reporting psychiatric diagnoses). Self-reported alexithymia was associated with poor non-affective interoception and increased perceived similarity between affective and non-affective body states. Interpretation: alexithymia involves a *general* interoception failure, not just an emotional one. **Methodological caveat**: this study used self-reported interoception measures, not behavioural heartbeat detection.

**(b) Shah, Hall, Catmur & Bird (2016).** "Alexithymia, not autism, is associated with impaired interoception." *Cortex* 81:215–220. PubMed 27253723. Two heartbeat-detection experiments across autistic and non-autistic samples. Interoceptive-accuracy variance loaded on alexithymia, not on autism diagnosis. **This is the autism-specific walk-back.**

**(c) Murphy, Brewer, Catmur & Bird (2018).** "Alexithymia is associated with a multidomain, multidimensional failure of interoception: Evidence from novel tests." *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General* 147(3):398–408. PMC5824617. Three experiments using novel non-cardiac interoception tasks (respiratory output, muscular effort, taste sensitivity). High-alexithymia individuals showed reduced interoceptive accuracy and reduced *use* of interoceptive cues across all three domains, even after controlling for autism, depression and anxiety. **This is a strengthening of the multidomain interoception account.**

**(d) Murphy, Brewer, Hobson, Catmur & Bird (2018).** "Is alexithymia characterised by impaired interoception? Further evidence, the importance of control variables, and the problems with the Heartbeat Counting Task." *Biological Psychology* 136:189–197. PubMed 29803614. N=287. Initial bivariate analyses showed no relationship between alexithymia and HCT performance. *After controlling* for confounds (age, gender, time-estimation ability, BMI, knowledge of average HR, HRV, depression, anxiety, systolic BP, resting HR), the predicted negative relationship emerged. The paper has two findings: (i) the association is real once confounds are controlled, (ii) the HCT itself is methodologically problematic. The methodological critique should not be misread as a substantive walk-back.

**(e) Nicholson, Williams, Grainger, Christensen, Calvo-Merino & Gaigg (2018).** "Interoceptive impairments do not lie at the heart of autism or alexithymia." *Journal of Abnormal Psychology* 127(6). PMC6089261. **Independent group from City University London / UCL — NOT from the Bird lab.** Multiple experiments using cardiac HCT, with Bayesian analyses supporting the null hypothesis. Neither autism nor alexithymia was associated with HCT performance. The Bird group's response is that the cardiac HCT is a poor measurement tool; the Nicholson group treats this as substantive evidence against the interoception-deficit account. **This is the active third-party challenge.**

**(f) Hobson, Brewer, Catmur & Bird (2019).** "The Role of Language in Alexithymia: Moving Towards a Multiroute Model." *Emotion Review* 11(3):247–261. SAGE DOI 10.1177/1754073919838528. The most current Bird-group synthesis. Alexithymia is reframed as the common downstream phenotype of *multiple* possible upstream impairments. Interoception is *one* route. Language and labelling is another. Emotion-concept conceptualisation may be a third. Different alexithymics may sit at different upstream loci. This is the closest the lineage comes to qualifying its earlier position — interoception is still one route, but no longer the only or necessarily the dominant one for any given person.

**(g) Brewer, Murphy & Bird (2021).** "Atypical interoception as a common risk factor for psychopathology: A review." *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews* 130:470–508. PMC8522807. PubMed 34358578. Authoritative current synthesis. Direct quote: *"many individuals with alexithymia experience a generalised interoceptive impairment, rather than one specific to emotional processing."* Impairment hypothesised at multiple representational levels — conscious detection, recognition/labelling, or both — and individuals may differ in which level is affected. Honestly notes that meta-analytic data on objectively measured interoceptive accuracy is mixed.

### Bottom line for the user

The Bird group's published 2025 position is:

1. **Alexithymia involves a multidomain, multidimensional interoception failure** affirmed across non-cardiac modalities (respiration, muscular effort, taste). Not walked back.
2. **The cardiac heartbeat-counting task is methodologically problematic**; effects emerge only after careful control of confounds. Methodological clarification, not substantive retreat.
3. **Autism per se is not associated with interoception deficits**; variance loads on co-occurring alexithymia. *This* is the autism-specific walk-back.
4. **The deficit may sit at multiple levels** — detection, recognition, labelling — and different alexithymics may sit at different levels. Clarified, not retracted, between 2016 and 2021.
5. **Language/conceptualisation is a parallel route** (Hobson 2019 multiroute model). Qualifies but does not abandon the interoception-route account.

The Nicholson 2018 paper sits as an active third-party challenge, not as a Bird-internal retraction.

## 5. The Garfinkel three-dimensional interoception model

### The model

**Garfinkel, Seth, Barrett, Suzuki & Critchley (2015).** "Knowing your own heart: Distinguishing interoceptive accuracy from interoceptive awareness." *Biological Psychology* 104:65–74. Sample N=80. The architectural foundation for distinguishing *detection* from *labelling* in interoception research.

Three dissociable dimensions:

- **Interoceptive accuracy (IAcc)**: objective performance on a behavioural task — e.g., counting heartbeats accurately during a fixed interval. The *detection* dimension.
- **Interoceptive sensibility (ISen)**: self-reported tendency to attend to body signals; the dimension captured by questionnaires like MAIA. The *attentional disposition* dimension.
- **Interoceptive awareness (IAw)**: metacognitive correspondence between accuracy and confidence — i.e., whether the person knows whether they're accurate. The *metacognitive* dimension.

The three dimensions **dissociate empirically**. A person can have high sensibility (lots of body attention) and low accuracy (poor metacognition about what the signals mean) — the somatic-amplification pattern characteristic of somatic-symptom disorder, hypervigilant-anxiety populations, and parts of the alexithymia spectrum.

### Why this matters for alexithymia

The Garfinkel model is the architectural basis for the **parsing-deficit reading** of alexithymia. The classic clinical observation — that alexithymics often *do* report bodily sensations but cannot interpret them as emotions — fits cleanly into the IAcc / ISen / IAw decomposition: a person can have intact or even elevated detection (accuracy) and attention (sensibility) while still failing the interpretive step that converts a detected signal into a labelled emotion. The breakdown is at the *labelling* layer, not at the *afferent* layer.

This is the model that distinguishes:

- **Detection-deficit alexithymia**: low IAcc, low ISen, reduced upstream signal — a candidate population for whom interoceptive-awareness training might plausibly help by amplifying weak afferents.
- **Parsing-deficit alexithymia**: normal-to-high IAcc and/or ISen with low IAw or low conceptual labelling — a population for whom interoceptive-awareness training risks amplifying already-loud signals without resolving the labelling failure.
- **Hypervigilant-with-mislabelling**: high ISen with low IAcc and low IAw — the somatic-symptom-disorder pattern, where attention is plentiful and inference is wrong. Open-ended interoceptive training is most likely to backfire in this profile.

The Garfinkel model is therefore the architectural substrate for the differential-therapy thesis the user wants to take into Categories 7–8.

### Garfinkel 2016 — adding trait prediction error

A subsequent paper by the Garfinkel group introduced *trait prediction error* — the chronic discrepancy between predicted and actual interoceptive states — as a fourth-order construct, particularly relevant to anxiety and panic. This is consistent with the Quattrocki-Friston predictive-processing frame in §7.

## 6. The quadratic relationship — Aaron, Blain, Snodgress, Park 2020

### The finding

**Aaron, Blain, Snodgress & Park (2020).** "Quadratic relationship between alexithymia and interoceptive accuracy, and results from a pilot mindfulness intervention." *Frontiers in Psychiatry* 11:132. PMC7076086.

**Universal correction**: this paper is by **Aaron, Blain, Snodgress & Park**, not by Trevisan, Mehling & McPartland. The misattribution in the parent exploration is corrected.

Key empirical claim: alexithymia and interoceptive accuracy show a **U-shaped (quadratic) relationship**. Both *low* IA and *high* IA can co-occur with high alexithymia. The deficit, by this reading, lies in *interpretation/labelling* of body signals rather than in detection per se.

### Why this matters mechanistically

If the relationship were strictly negative (alexithymia → low IA), the interpretation would be straightforward: alexithymia is detection failure, and amplifying detection should help. The quadratic finding rules this out. Two distinct alexithymic profiles are present in the same population:

- A **low-IA profile** consistent with detection failure.
- A **high-IA profile** consistent with hypervigilant-but-mislabelling — body signals are detected acutely but the interpretive step fails or generates mislabelled inferences.

The pilot mindfulness body-scan intervention in the same study showed effect-modification by baseline IA — early signal that the *same* intervention works differently in the two profiles.

### Bottom line

The Aaron 2020 finding is the clearest single-paper architectural support for treating alexithymia as a *parsing/interpretive* failure rather than a uniform *detection* failure. It dovetails with Garfinkel 2015 (different layers can dissociate) and with the Hobson 2019 multiroute model (different upstream causes converge on the alexithymia phenotype).

## 7. Quattrocki & Friston 2014 — predictive processing and oxytocin

### The frame

**Quattrocki & Friston (2014).** "Autism, oxytocin and interoception." *Neuroscience and Biobehavioral Reviews* 47:410–430. The interoceptive-predictive-coding account.

The active-inference / predictive-processing frame casts interoception as a top-down predictive system. The brain generates predictions about expected bodily states; afferent signals adjust those predictions only if they are *precision-weighted* — i.e., only if the brain assigns enough confidence to the incoming signal to allow it to update the prediction.

Quattrocki and Friston propose that alexithymia (in their target population, autism with co-occurring alexithymia) involves a **failure in precision-weighting of interoceptive prediction errors**. Either the precision assigned to interoceptive afferents is chronically low (signals are detected but ignored as noise) or chronically miscalibrated (signals are over-weighted when they should be down-weighted). The phenotype: detected signals fail to update emotion concepts, or detected signals over-update them in a destabilising way.

### Oxytocin's modulatory role

Oxytocin is hypothesised to modulate precision-weighting in the salience network, including AI and ACC. Quattrocki & Friston propose that alexithymia involves reduced or atypical oxytocinergic tone, which contributes to the precision-weighting failure. This is the mechanistic motivation for the small intranasal-oxytocin trials reporting larger effects in high-alexithymia participants (Luminet et al. 2011; Quirin & Carter 2014 review, PMC4172094).

### Implication for the user

If the user's alexithymia involves a precision-weighting failure (rather than a sensory-detection failure), interventions that simply amplify body-attention will not address the locus. What is theoretically needed is *re-calibration of precision* — work that helps the system assign appropriate confidence to interoceptive signals so they can update predictions. This is the mechanistic motivation for IFS-style scaffolded approaches (where parts work provides external structure for assigning meaning to signals) and for psychoeducation-heavy approaches that build emotion concepts before deploying body-attention.

## 8. Barrett's theory of constructed emotion

### The frame

**Barrett (2017).** "The theory of constructed emotion." *Social Cognitive and Affective Neuroscience* 12(1):1–23. PMC5390700.

Barrett's frame is the most ambitious theoretical reformulation of emotion. Emotions are not biologically given categories with dedicated neural circuits; they are *constructed* by the brain in real time using a population-coded conceptual system. The brain generates emotion concepts as predictive simulations integrating interoceptive signals, exteroceptive context, and learned conceptual schemas. The same physiological pattern can be experienced as anxiety in one context and excitement in another — what differs is the conceptual frame, not the body.

The implication for alexithymia:

- Alexithymia is a state of **impoverished emotion-concept system** — the conceptual library used to construct emotions is sparse, undifferentiated, or poorly populated.
- **Emotional granularity** — the fineness of one's emotion concepts — is inversely related to alexithymia. High-granularity individuals can distinguish many emotional states; low-granularity individuals lump them into broad good/bad categories.
- The neural correlate is a default-mode-network and conceptual-system involvement, not just an interoceptive-network deficit. mPFC, posterior cingulate, and related regions are part of the conceptual machinery.

### Why this is a different mechanistic story from Bird/Murphy

Bird's account locates the deficit in *interoception* (the afferent signal). Barrett's account locates the deficit in *conceptualisation* (the categorisation of signals into emotion concepts). The Hobson 2019 multiroute model treats these as parallel routes to the same downstream phenotype. They are not necessarily competing — they may both be true for different alexithymics or for the same alexithymic at different layers.

### Practical implication

If the deficit is conceptual rather than interoceptive, the intervention class is *conceptual training* — building richer emotion vocabulary, reading fiction, learning labels, expanding granularity. Lieberman (2007) and the affect-labelling literature live in this frame. This is the architectural basis for the user's existing IFS deep-dive content on granularity-building and the externalised parts work.

## 9. Preece's attention-appraisal model

### The frame

**Preece, Becerra, Robinson, Dandy & Allan (2017).** Attention-appraisal model of alexithymia. *Personality and Individual Differences*. Operationalised by the Perth Alexithymia Questionnaire (PAQ).

Preece reframes alexithymia in a CBT-style cognitive frame. Emotion regulation requires (a) attention to the emotion when it occurs, then (b) appraisal of the emotion (identifying, labelling, evaluating), then (c) some response to it. Alexithymia is operationalised as a *failure at the attention or appraisal stage*. Difficulty Identifying Feelings is reframed as a failure of attention (the person doesn't deploy attention to the emotion when it arises) or appraisal (the person attends but cannot categorise).

The PAQ measures DIF and DDF separately for positive and negative emotions, plus a general externally-oriented thinking factor. This valence-separation operationalises Preece's frame: a person can be alexithymic for negative emotions but not positive, or vice versa.

### Why this matters

Preece's frame is **complementary** rather than competing with Bird, Barrett, and Quattrocki-Friston. It is a **cognitive-behavioural-level description** of what's happening at the conscious-process level. Bird's frame is afferent-network-level. Barrett's is conceptual-system-level. Quattrocki-Friston's is computational-precision-level. Preece's is attention-and-appraisal-level. They sit at different abstraction layers; a comprehensive account probably needs all of them.

For therapy matching, Preece's frame gives a CBT-shaped path: train attention to emotional cues, train appraisal granularity, train labelling. This is theoretically friendly to CBT-derivative approaches and to externalised cognitive scaffolding (journaling, vocabulary work). It does not require interoceptive-detection training as a starting point.

## 10. The original Sifneos psychoanalytic frame — still defended

### The frame

**Taylor & Bagby (2024).** "Alexithymia: A Defense of the Original Conceptualization of the Construct and a Critique of the Attention-Appraisal Model." *Clinical Neuropsychiatry*. PMC11555664.

Sifneos (1972) and Nemiah & Sifneos (1976) defined alexithymia in a psychosomatic-medicine context as a four-feature construct: difficulty identifying feelings, difficulty describing feelings, externally-oriented thinking, and *restricted imaginative/fantasy life*. The construct was originally tied to a psychodynamic model of failed symbolic-mentalisation. Emotions are unprocessed at the bodily level because they cannot be elaborated symbolically.

Taylor & Bagby in 2024 explicitly defend this original conceptualisation against Preece's attention-appraisal reformulation. They argue that:

- The fantasy/imagination criterion is essential and was wrongly dropped from TAS-20.
- Alexithymia is best understood as a developmental-psychosomatic phenomenon, not a CBT-process phenomenon.
- Preece's reformulation flattens the construct into emotion-regulation-failure, losing the psychoanalytic core.

For the user, the take-home is that the original frame is alive in 2025 and continues to defend a different mechanistic picture — one in which alexithymia is a failure of *symbolic elaboration of affect*, not an interoceptive or conceptual or attentional deficit. This is closest to the Hobson 2019 *language* route.

## 11. Synthesis — the Hobson 2019 multiroute model

### The integrative frame

**Hobson, Brewer, Catmur & Bird (2019).** "The Role of Language in Alexithymia: Moving Towards a Multiroute Model." *Emotion Review* 11(3):247–261.

The most current Bird-group synthesis, and the most useful integrative frame for the deep-dive user. The thesis: alexithymia is the **common downstream phenotype** of multiple possible upstream impairments. The same TAS-20 score can be generated by:

- An **interoception route** — failure in detection, integration, or representation of bodily signals (anterior insula, ACC, salience network).
- A **language/labelling route** — failure in the verbal-symbolic system that maps physiological-conceptual states onto words (left lateral PFC, the affect-labelling pathway documented by Lieberman 2007).
- An **emotion-concept route** — impoverished conceptual system in the Barrett sense (mPFC, default-mode network).
- A **precision/predictive-coding route** — atypical precision-weighting in the Friston sense (salience network plus modulatory systems).
- An **attention/appraisal route** — at the conscious-process level in the Preece sense (executive control networks).

These are not necessarily competing accounts. Different alexithymics may sit at different routes. The same alexithymic may have impairments at multiple routes. The therapeutic implication is that the *appropriate* intervention is route-specific — interoceptive training for the detection-deficit profile, granularity/affect-labelling work for the conceptual/language profile, attention-deployment work for the attentional profile, and so on.

The user's natural question — *"where does my alexithymia live in the brain?"* — has, on this account, no single answer. It depends on which upstream route is implicated. The user's profile (autism + alexithymia + the heterogeneity-aware self-classification work in Categories 2–3) is consistent with a multiroute presentation: some interoceptive component, some conceptual/language component, some attentional component, with the proportion varying.

### Practical take-home

The mechanistic answer for the user is multiroute. The therapy-matching answer (Categories 7–8) follows directly: there is no one-size-fits-all alexithymia therapy because there is no one-size-fits-all alexithymia mechanism.

## Appendix B — Where downstream content needs to be careful

- Do NOT cite PMC6089261 as a Bird-internal paper. It is **Nicholson et al. 2018**, an independent group.
- Do NOT cite PMC7076086 as Trevisan et al. The actual authors are **Aaron, Blain, Snodgress & Park (2020)**.
- The Hogeveen 2016 lesion finding is the strongest single causal claim. It speaks to acquired alexithymia and is sufficient evidence that AI is *causally* involved. It is not yet directly replicated.
- The Murphy 2018b "after controlling for confounds" finding is real but should be presented as a methodological clarification, not a substantive claim about effect size in unselected populations.
- The amygdala / OFC / mPFC findings are real but mixed. Do not over-claim replicability.
- The Hobson 2019 multiroute model is the appropriate synthesis frame for the user. Use it as the closing structural answer to "where does my alexithymia live."
- The Sifneos psychoanalytic frame (Taylor & Bagby 2024) is still defended and should be acknowledged, not dismissed. It maps roughly onto the Hobson "language/symbolic-elaboration" route.
- Quattrocki-Friston is **theoretical**. Do not present it as established mechanism; present it as the most articulated computational-level account.
