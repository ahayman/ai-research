# Indirect Speech Acts: Comprehensive Research

## 1. What Indirect Speech Acts Are

### The Fundamental Concept

An indirect speech act occurs when the **form** (grammatical structure) of an utterance does not match its **function** (communicative intent). The speaker means something beyond or different from the surface meaning of their words.

Most languages distinguish at least three basic sentence types with default communicative functions:

| Sentence Form | Default Function | Technical Term |
|---|---|---|
| Declarative ("The door is open.") | Making a statement | Representative/Assertive |
| Interrogative ("Is the door open?") | Asking a question | Question |
| Imperative ("Open the door.") | Issuing a command/request | Directive |

An indirect speech act occurs when a speaker uses one form to perform a different function. The **literal act** (what the sentence form would normally do) differs from the **primary act** (what the speaker actually intends to accomplish).

### Foundational Framework: Austin and Searle

The concept originates in J.L. Austin's 1962 work *How to Do Things with Words*, which distinguished three levels of action in any utterance:

- **Locutionary act**: The act of saying something -- producing a meaningful expression with sense and reference
- **Illocutionary act**: The act performed *in* saying something -- the communicative force (asserting, requesting, promising, warning, etc.)
- **Perlocutionary act**: The act performed *by* saying something -- the further effects on the audience (persuading, frightening, amusing, getting someone to do something)

John Searle extended this framework significantly, classifying illocutionary acts into five categories:
1. **Representatives/Assertives**: Committing the speaker to the truth of something (stating, claiming, reporting)
2. **Directives**: Attempting to get the hearer to do something (requesting, commanding, suggesting)
3. **Commissives**: Committing the speaker to a future action (promising, threatening, offering)
4. **Expressives**: Expressing psychological states (thanking, apologizing, congratulating)
5. **Declarations**: Bringing about a change in reality (pronouncing married, firing someone, declaring war)

Indirect speech acts are acts where the speaker performs one illocutionary act (the **primary** act) by means of performing another (the **literal** or **secondary** act).

### Examples Across All Categories

**Directives disguised as other act types:**
- "Can you pass the salt?" -- Question form, request function
- "I notice the report isn't finished yet." -- Statement form, request/command function
- "It would be nice if someone took out the trash." -- Statement form, request function
- "The garbage is really piling up." -- Statement form, request function
- "You're standing on my foot." -- Statement form, demand function (demand to move)

**Commissives disguised as other act types:**
- "I wouldn't do that if I were you." -- Statement form, threat/warning function
- "Would you like me to help with that?" -- Question form, offer function
- "That's a nice car you have there. It would be a shame if something happened to it." -- Statement form, threat function

**Representatives disguised as other act types:**
- "Do you really think that's a good idea?" -- Question form, assertion function (asserting it's a bad idea)
- "Isn't it obvious?" -- Question form, assertion function

**Expressives disguised as other act types:**
- "That's an... interesting choice." -- Statement form, criticism function
- "Well, you certainly tried your best." -- Statement form, expressing disappointment
- "How kind of you to finally join us." -- Exclamation form, criticism function (of lateness)
- "I see you went with the bold approach." -- Statement form, expressing disapproval

### Searle's Felicity Conditions and How They Enable Indirectness

Searle identified four conditions that must be met for a speech act to be successful (felicitous):

1. **Propositional content condition**: Constraints on what the proposition describes
2. **Preparatory conditions**: Background circumstances that must hold (e.g., for a request: the hearer has the ability to perform the act; the speaker believes the hearer can do it; it is not obvious the hearer would do it anyway)
3. **Sincerity condition**: The speaker must have the appropriate psychological state (e.g., for a request: the speaker genuinely wants the act done)
4. **Essential condition**: What the act "counts as" (e.g., a request counts as an attempt to get the hearer to do something)

Searle's crucial insight was that **indirect speech acts work by referring to the felicity conditions of the intended act**. Speakers make indirect requests by:

- **Questioning the preparatory condition (ability)**: "Can you pass the salt?" (= I'm questioning whether you have the ability, which is a preparatory condition for requesting)
- **Questioning the preparatory condition (willingness)**: "Would you be willing to move your car?"
- **Stating the sincerity condition**: "I want you to leave." / "I'd like you to close the window."
- **Questioning the propositional content**: "Would you close the door?"
- **Stating reasons for the act**: "You're standing on my foot." (= stating why a request is justified)

This is why "Can you pass the salt?" works as a request: it questions a preparatory condition (ability) of the speech act of requesting. The hearer recognizes this reference to the felicity conditions and infers the intended primary act.

### How Hearers Derive the Indirect Meaning (Searle's Account)

Searle proposed that hearers use the following inferential chain (drawing on Grice's Cooperative Principle):

1. The hearer recognizes the literal meaning of the utterance
2. The hearer notices that the literal meaning seems irrelevant or uninformative in context (e.g., asking about someone's ability to pass salt at dinner is trivially obvious)
3. The hearer assumes the speaker is being cooperative (following Gricean maxims)
4. The hearer recognizes that the utterance refers to a felicity condition of a related speech act
5. The hearer infers the intended primary speech act

For example, at the dinner table:
- Speaker: "Can you pass the salt?"
- Hearer's inference: The speaker surely knows I can pass the salt (literal question is trivially answerable). The speaker must mean something else. The ability to pass the salt is a preparatory condition for requesting me to pass the salt. Therefore, the speaker is requesting me to pass the salt.

## 2. Conventional Indirect Speech Acts

### Definition

Conventional indirect speech acts are patterns of indirect language use that have become so common and formulaic in a speech community that they are essentially **idiomatic** -- recognized immediately as performing a particular function without requiring step-by-step inferential processing. They occupy a middle ground between fully literal speech and completely novel, context-dependent hints.

The Cross-Cultural Speech Act Realization Project (CCSARP), conducted by Blum-Kulka and Olshtain (1984), provided a foundational classification system for request strategies, identifying three levels of directness:
1. **Direct strategies**: Grammatical mood marks the force (imperatives, explicit performatives)
2. **Conventionally indirect strategies**: Require contextual preconditions conventionalized in the language
3. **Non-conventionally indirect strategies**: Require the hearer to infer the request from context (hints)

### Comprehensive Catalog of Conventional Indirect Patterns

#### A. Requests Disguised as Ability Questions

These question whether the hearer has the **ability** (preparatory condition) to perform the requested act:

| Pattern | Example | Function |
|---|---|---|
| "Can you...?" | "Can you close the door?" | Request |
| "Could you...?" | "Could you send me that file?" | Request (more polite/tentative) |
| "Are you able to...?" | "Are you able to meet tomorrow?" | Request |
| "Is there any way you could...?" | "Is there any way you could cover my shift?" | Request (heavily hedged) |

**Note**: "Could you" is generally perceived as more polite than "can you" because the past tense/subjunctive form adds distance and tentativeness.

#### B. Requests Disguised as Willingness/Preference Questions

These question the hearer's **willingness** (another preparatory condition):

| Pattern | Example | Function |
|---|---|---|
| "Would you...?" | "Would you pass the butter?" | Request |
| "Will you...?" | "Will you hold this for me?" | Request (less formal) |
| "Would you mind...?" | "Would you mind turning down the music?" | Request |
| "Would you be willing to...?" | "Would you be willing to review my draft?" | Request |
| "Do you want to...?" | "Do you want to hand me that?" | Request (casual) |
| "Would you be so kind as to...?" | "Would you be so kind as to open the window?" | Request (very formal) |
| "Would it be possible for you to...?" | "Would it be possible for you to reschedule?" | Request |

#### C. Requests Disguised as Self-Reports (Sincerity Condition)

The speaker states their own desire or mental state (the sincerity condition for requesting):

| Pattern | Example | Function |
|---|---|---|
| "I was wondering if..." | "I was wondering if you could help me." | Request |
| "I was hoping you might..." | "I was hoping you might have a moment." | Request |
| "I'd like you to..." | "I'd like you to review this proposal." | Request (direct-ish but softened) |
| "I'd appreciate it if..." | "I'd appreciate it if you could respond by Friday." | Request |
| "I need you to..." | "I need you to finish this today." | Request (authoritative) |
| "It would mean a lot if..." | "It would mean a lot if you could come." | Request/invitation |

**Note**: The progressive aspect ("I was wondering") adds temporal distance and tentativeness. The past tense in "I was hoping" creates even more indirectness than "I'm hoping."

#### D. Requests Disguised as Yes/No Questions About Circumstances

| Pattern | Example | Intended Function |
|---|---|---|
| "Do you have the time?" | Full utterance | Request to be told the time |
| "Do you have a pen?" | Full utterance | Request to borrow a pen |
| "Is this seat taken?" | Full utterance | Request to sit there |
| "Are you using this chair?" | Full utterance | Request to take the chair |
| "Do you know where the station is?" | Full utterance | Request for directions |
| "Is anyone sitting here?" | Full utterance | Request to sit down |

#### E. Suggestions Disguised as Questions

| Pattern | Example | Function |
|---|---|---|
| "Why don't you...?" | "Why don't you take a break?" | Suggestion |
| "Why not...?" | "Why not try the other approach?" | Suggestion |
| "Have you thought about...?" | "Have you thought about applying to that job?" | Suggestion |
| "Have you considered...?" | "Have you considered talking to HR?" | Suggestion |
| "What if you...?" | "What if you tried restarting it?" | Suggestion |
| "How about...?" | "How about we meet at noon?" | Suggestion/Proposal |
| "What about...?" | "What about the Italian place?" | Suggestion |
| "Wouldn't it be better to...?" | "Wouldn't it be better to wait?" | Suggestion |
| "Don't you think you should...?" | "Don't you think you should call them?" | Suggestion (bordering on criticism) |

#### F. Invitations and Offers Disguised as Questions

| Pattern | Example | Function |
|---|---|---|
| "Would you like to...?" | "Would you like to join us for dinner?" | Invitation |
| "Can I get you anything?" | Full utterance | Offer |
| "Do you want to...?" | "Do you want to grab coffee?" | Invitation |
| "Shall we...?" | "Shall we get started?" | Proposal/Invitation |
| "Care for a...?" | "Care for a drink?" | Offer |
| "How would you feel about...?" | "How would you feel about moving the meeting?" | Proposal |

#### G. Permission Requests Disguised as Questions

| Pattern | Example | Function |
|---|---|---|
| "Do you mind if I...?" | "Do you mind if I open the window?" | Permission request |
| "Would it be okay if...?" | "Would it be okay if I left early?" | Permission request |
| "Is it all right if...?" | "Is it all right if I sit here?" | Permission request |
| "May I...?" | "May I ask a question?" | Permission request |
| "I don't suppose I could...?" | "I don't suppose I could borrow your car?" | Permission request (very tentative) |

#### H. Hedged Performatives

The speaker names the speech act but softens it:

| Pattern | Example | Function |
|---|---|---|
| "I'd like to ask you to..." | "I'd like to ask you to reconsider." | Request |
| "I must ask you to..." | "I must ask you to leave." | Demand (softened) |
| "I have to insist that..." | "I have to insist that we follow protocol." | Demand |
| "I wanted to suggest..." | "I wanted to suggest a different approach." | Suggestion |
| "I'd like to recommend..." | "I'd like to recommend we postpone." | Recommendation |

#### I. Pre-Request Sequences (Getting Precommitment)

These are utterances that function as groundwork for an upcoming request:

| Pattern | Example | Function |
|---|---|---|
| "Can I ask you a favor?" | Full utterance | Pre-request |
| "Are you busy right now?" | Full utterance | Pre-request / availability check |
| "Got a minute?" | Full utterance | Pre-request |
| "Can I ask you something?" | Full utterance | Pre-request |
| "I hate to bother you, but..." | Full utterance | Pre-request with apology |

#### J. Conventional Softening Devices (Hedges)

These lexical and phrasal devices reduce the force of speech acts:

- **Modal verbs**: could, would, might, may (vs. can, will)
- **Past tense distancing**: "I wanted to ask..." (vs. "I want to ask...")
- **Progressive aspect**: "I was wondering..." (vs. "I wonder...")
- **Conditional framing**: "If you could...", "If it's not too much trouble..."
- **Minimizers**: "just" ("Could you just move over a bit?"), "a little" ("Could you help me a little?")
- **Downtoners**: "maybe", "perhaps", "possibly", "sort of", "kind of"
- **Subjectivizers**: "I think", "I believe", "in my opinion", "it seems to me"
- **Consultative devices**: "do you think", "would you say"
- **Apologetic openers**: "Sorry to bother you", "I hate to ask", "Excuse me"
- **Appreciative closers**: "if you don't mind", "if that's okay", "when you get a chance"

## 3. Non-Conventional Indirect Speech Acts (Hints and Implicature)

### Definition

Non-conventional indirect speech acts (also called **hints**, **insinuations**, or **off-record strategies**) are utterances where the intended meaning is not conventionally associated with the linguistic form. Unlike conventional indirect speech acts (which are quasi-formulaic), non-conventional ones require the hearer to rely heavily on **context**, **shared knowledge**, and **inferential reasoning** to derive the speaker's intent.

These are the most indirect end of the spectrum. They offer maximum plausible deniability because the speaker can always claim they meant only the literal content.

### Examples with Analysis

**Environmental observations as requests:**
- "It's cold in here." --> Request to close a window or turn up the heat
- "It's getting dark." --> Request to turn on a light (or suggestion to leave)
- "The trash is overflowing." --> Request for someone to take out the trash
- "I notice the dishes are still in the sink." --> Request/criticism about someone not doing dishes
- "The lawn is looking pretty long." --> Request for someone to mow the lawn

**Statements of personal state as requests:**
- "I'm so thirsty." --> Request for a drink
- "I'm starving." --> Suggestion to get food / request for food
- "I'm exhausted." --> Request to leave or go to bed
- "My back is killing me." --> Request for help, sympathy, or to stop a physical activity

**Observations as criticism:**
- "That's an interesting choice." --> Disguised criticism (implying it's a bad/unusual choice)
- "Well, that's one way to do it." --> Criticism (implying there's a better way)
- "I see you went with the bold approach." --> Criticism
- "That's certainly... different." --> Criticism
- "Hmm, I wouldn't have done it that way." --> Criticism
- "How long did that take you?" --> Possible criticism (implying it should have been faster, or grudging admiration)
- "You must have been in a hurry." --> Criticism (implying carelessness)

**Statements as refusals:**
- "I have an early meeting tomorrow." --> Refusal of a late-night invitation
- "I'm pretty behind on my work." --> Refusal of a social invitation
- "That sounds expensive." --> Refusal or expression of reluctance

**Questions as challenges or criticism:**
- "Are you sure about that?" --> Expressing doubt/disagreement
- "Have you really thought this through?" --> Criticism of a decision
- "Is that really the best we can do?" --> Criticism of quality

**Statements as threats:**
- "I know people who know people." --> Veiled threat
- "That's a nice business you have there." --> Veiled threat (especially in certain contexts)
- "I'd hate for anyone to find out about that." --> Blackmail/threat

### The Gricean Mechanism

Non-conventional indirect speech acts work through **conversational implicature**, based on H.P. Grice's (1975) Cooperative Principle and its four maxims:

1. **Maxim of Quality**: Be truthful; don't say what you believe to be false
2. **Maxim of Quantity**: Be appropriately informative; say neither too much nor too little
3. **Maxim of Relation (Relevance)**: Be relevant to the conversation
4. **Maxim of Manner**: Be clear, brief, orderly; avoid ambiguity

Non-conventional indirect speech acts typically work by **apparently violating** one of these maxims, which forces the hearer to search for an interpretation that makes the utterance cooperative after all.

**Example: "It's cold in here."**
1. The speaker states an obvious environmental fact (violating Quantity -- this is uninformative, since both parties can feel the temperature)
2. The hearer assumes the speaker is being cooperative (Cooperative Principle)
3. The hearer searches for why this utterance is relevant (Relevance)
4. The hearer infers: the speaker must want something done about the cold -- close the window, turn up the heat, etc.
5. This inference becomes the pragmatic meaning: a request

**Example: "That's an interesting choice."**
1. The word "interesting" is literally positive or neutral
2. But in context, if the choice is clearly questionable, the utterance appears to violate Quality (the speaker doesn't genuinely find it interesting in a positive way)
3. The hearer infers: the speaker is being diplomatic; they actually disapprove
4. Pragmatic meaning: criticism

**Classic Gricean example: The recommendation letter**
A professor writes a recommendation for a philosophy student: "Dear Sir, Mr. X's command of English is excellent, and his attendance at tutorials has been regular. Yours, etc."
- This violates Quantity (far too little information for a recommendation)
- The hearer infers: the professor has nothing positive to say about the student's philosophical abilities
- Pragmatic meaning: this is a negative recommendation disguised as a positive one

### Properties of Non-Conventional Indirect Speech Acts

1. **Context-dependent**: The same utterance ("It's cold in here") could be a simple observation, a request, or a complaint depending on context
2. **Cancellable**: The speaker can deny the implied meaning ("I was just making an observation")
3. **Non-detachable**: The implicature arises from the content, not the specific words (rephrasing preserves it)
4. **Calculable**: The hearer can reconstruct the reasoning chain
5. **Indeterminate**: There may be multiple possible interpretations

## 4. Why Indirectness Exists

### 4.1 Politeness Theory: Brown and Levinson's Framework

The most influential account of why people use indirect speech comes from Penelope Brown and Stephen Levinson's *Politeness: Some Universals in Language Usage* (1987). Their theory rests on the concept of **face** -- a person's public self-image that they want to maintain.

**Two types of face:**
- **Positive face**: The desire to be liked, appreciated, approved of, and included
- **Negative face**: The desire for autonomy, freedom from imposition, and the right to act unimpeded

**Face-threatening acts (FTAs)**: Many speech acts inherently threaten face:
- Requests threaten the hearer's **negative face** (imposing on their autonomy)
- Criticism threatens the hearer's **positive face** (attacking their self-image)
- Refusals threaten the original speaker's **positive face** (rejecting them)
- Orders threaten the hearer's **negative face** (restricting their freedom)

**Five super-strategies for performing FTAs** (from least to most indirect):
1. **Bald on-record**: Perform the FTA directly, without mitigation ("Give me that.")
2. **Positive politeness**: Perform the FTA while attending to positive face ("Hey buddy, you're so good at this -- could you help me?")
3. **Negative politeness**: Perform the FTA while attending to negative face ("I'm terribly sorry to bother you, but would you mind possibly helping me when you get a chance?")
4. **Off-record (indirect)**: Perform the FTA indirectly through hints ("Wow, this is too heavy for one person...")
5. **Don't perform the FTA**: Say nothing

The indirect strategy (off-record) maximizes face protection because the speaker never explicitly performs the face-threatening act. The hearer can choose to "hear" the hint and comply, or can ignore it without either party losing face.

**The weightiness formula**: Brown and Levinson proposed that speakers calculate the seriousness of an FTA based on:
- **D** (social Distance between speaker and hearer)
- **P** (relative Power of hearer over speaker)
- **R** (absolute Ranking of the imposition)

The greater the combined weight (D + P + R), the more indirect the speaker will be.

### 4.2 Leech's Politeness Principle

Geoffrey Leech (1983) proposed a complementary framework with six maxims:

1. **Tact Maxim**: Minimize cost to other; maximize benefit to other
2. **Generosity Maxim**: Minimize benefit to self; maximize cost to self
3. **Approbation Maxim**: Minimize dispraise of other; maximize praise of other
4. **Modesty Maxim**: Minimize praise of self; maximize dispraise of self
5. **Agreement Maxim**: Minimize disagreement; maximize agreement
6. **Sympathy Maxim**: Minimize antipathy; maximize sympathy

Indirect speech acts serve the **Tact Maxim** especially: by phrasing a request as a question ("Could you possibly...?"), the speaker minimizes the apparent cost to the hearer by framing compliance as optional rather than obligatory.

### 4.3 Pinker's Strategic Speaker Theory

Steven Pinker, Martin Nowak, and James Lee (2008, "The Logic of Indirect Speech," *PNAS*) proposed a three-part theory based on game theory and evolutionary psychology:

**Component 1: Plausible Deniability**

Indirect speech allows speakers to maintain plausible deniability when they are uncertain whether the hearer is cooperative or antagonistic. Their game-theoretic model shows:
- A cooperative listener can detect and accept the indirect request
- An uncooperative or hostile listener cannot prove the request was made
- This is optimal when the speaker is uncertain about the listener's type

Classic example: A driver pulled over for speeding says, "Gee, officer, is there some way we could take care of this here?" rather than "I'll give you $50 to forget this ticket." If the officer is corrupt, they understand the bribe. If the officer is honest, the driver hasn't committed a provable act of bribery.

**Component 2: Relationship Type Negotiation**

Pinker draws on Alan Fiske's relational models theory, identifying three relationship types:

- **Dominance/Authority ranking**: "Don't mess with me." Based on hierarchical deference.
- **Communality/Communal sharing**: "What's mine is thine; what's thine is mine." Based on shared identity and closeness.
- **Reciprocity/Equality matching**: "You scratch my back; I'll scratch yours." Based on balanced exchange.

Each relationship type has its own logic of exchange. Indirect speech arises when a speaker wants to perform an act that presupposes one relationship type but the actual or desired relationship is different. For example:

- Offering to pay for a friend's dinner presupposes reciprocity, but the friendship operates on communality. Saying "Let me get this" (communal framing) rather than "You can get the next one" (reciprocity framing) navigates this.
- A sexual come-on presupposes communality (intimacy), but the relationship may currently be reciprocity (acquaintances). Indirectness ("Would you like to come up for coffee?") avoids the emotional cost of explicitly proposing a relationship-type shift that might be rejected.

The emotional costs of a mismatch in the assumed relationship type create a need for plausible deniability and thereby select for indirectness, even when there are no tangible costs.

**Component 3: Language as a Digital Medium**

Pinker argues that overt language creates **common knowledge** (I know that you know that I know...) in a way that indirect language does not. A direct statement puts something "on the record" -- it becomes mutually known and creates social obligations. An indirect statement can be understood by both parties without creating this mutual knowledge, allowing both to pretend (plausibly) that the request/offer/threat was never made.

This explains why indirect speech persists even when both parties understand the intended meaning with high confidence. The issue is not whether the meaning is understood, but whether it becomes common knowledge that formally changes the social situation.

### 4.4 Power Dynamics

Indirectness patterns are heavily influenced by power differentials:

- **Low-power speakers to high-power hearers**: Maximum indirectness (employees to bosses, students to teachers, citizens to officials). "I was wondering if perhaps it might be possible to..."
- **High-power speakers to low-power hearers**: Can be more direct but still use indirectness for rapport ("Why don't you go ahead and take the rest of the day off?" -- framing an order as a suggestion)
- **Equal-power speakers**: Moderate indirectness, negotiated based on social distance and relationship type

### 4.5 Additional Motivations for Indirectness

- **Avoiding responsibility**: "Someone should really clean this up" (avoiding naming who)
- **Testing the waters**: Indirect expressions of interest allow retreat without loss of face
- **Social lubricant**: Indirectness creates a collaborative interpretive act between speaker and hearer, reinforcing social bonds
- **Humor and play**: Much humor involves indirect speech acts (irony, understatement, overstatement)
- **Cultural norms**: Some cultures strongly prefer indirect communication styles; directness may be perceived as rude regardless of content

## 5. The Processing Question: How Do People Understand Indirect Speech Acts?

### The Central Debate

When someone hears "Can you pass the salt?", do they:
(a) First compute the literal meaning ("question about ability"), recognize it as contextually odd, then derive the indirect meaning ("request to pass salt")? -- **Literal-first / Standard Pragmatic Model**
(b) Go directly to the intended meaning ("request") without processing the literal meaning at all? -- **Direct Access Hypothesis**
(c) Process both meanings simultaneously, with the more salient/conventional meaning winning? -- **Graded Salience Hypothesis**

### Model 1: Standard Pragmatic Model (Literal-First)

**Proponents**: Grice (1975), Searle (1975), Clark & Lucy (1975)

**The claim**: Comprehension follows a sequential process:
1. Compute the literal meaning
2. Test it against context
3. If the literal meaning is contextually inappropriate, derive the non-literal meaning via pragmatic inference

**Key evidence -- Clark & Lucy (1975)**:
In their landmark study, 23 subjects were timed drawing deductions from pairs of conversationally conveyed requests (e.g., "Can you open the door?" vs. "Must you open the door?"). They found that sentences conveying positive requests behaved as if positive even when literally negative (e.g., "Why not open the door?"), and that response times suggested literal meanings were being processed. The data were consistent with a model where literal meaning is computed before indirect meaning.

**Strengths**: Explains how novel indirect speech acts are understood; accounts for the occasional experience of "catching" the literal meaning

**Weaknesses**: Predicts that indirect speech acts should always take longer to process than direct ones, which is not consistently supported by reaction time data

### Model 2: Direct Access Hypothesis

**Proponents**: Gibbs (1983, 1994), later supported by various eye-tracking studies

**The claim**: For conventional indirect speech acts, people go directly to the intended meaning without first computing the literal meaning. Context and convention allow immediate access to the pragmatic meaning.

**Key evidence -- Gibbs (1983)**:
In sentence-priming experiments, Gibbs found that participants did NOT always compute the literal meanings of conventional indirect requests like "Can you pass the salt?" Participants were biased toward conventional nonliteral interpretations even when sentences were read in literal contexts. Processing times for indirect requests were not systematically longer than for the same sentences used as direct speech acts.

**Key evidence -- Holtgraves (1994)**:
In four experiments examining how speaker status affects comprehension:
- **Conventional** indirect requests were recognized quickly regardless of speaker status, without requiring processing and rejection of the literal meaning
- **Non-conventional** indirect requests WERE affected by speaker status: when interactants were equal in status, comprehension was slower and involved activation of the literal meaning; when the speaker was higher-status, processing was faster (because high-status speakers are expected to make requests)

**Implication**: There appear to be two different processing routes -- one for conventional indirect speech acts (direct access) and one for non-conventional ones (more inferential processing).

### Model 3: Graded Salience Hypothesis

**Proponent**: Rachel Giora (1997, 2003)

**The claim**: All **salient** meanings of an utterance are processed by default, regardless of context. Salience is determined by: conventionality, frequency, familiarity, and prototypicality.

For highly conventional indirect speech acts like "Can you pass the salt?", the request meaning IS the salient meaning (due to frequency and convention), so it is accessed first or simultaneously with the literal meaning. Context cannot suppress salient meanings but can enhance non-salient ones.

**Key predictions**:
- Conventional indirect speech acts: The indirect meaning is salient, so it's accessed rapidly (consistent with direct access findings)
- Novel/unfamiliar indirect speech acts: The literal meaning is salient, so it's accessed first (consistent with literal-first findings)
- This resolves the apparent contradiction between the two earlier models

### Model 4: Relevance Theory

**Proponents**: Dan Sperber and Deirdre Wilson (1986/1995)

**The claim**: Hearers follow a path of least effort in computing cognitive effects, testing interpretive hypotheses in order of accessibility and stopping when expectations of relevance are satisfied.

**Key principles**:
- **Relevance** = cognitive effects / processing effort
- The hearer assumes the speaker's utterance is optimally relevant
- The hearer tests the most accessible interpretation first
- If this interpretation satisfies the expectation of relevance, processing stops

For "Can you pass the salt?" at dinner:
- The request interpretation is highly accessible (due to convention) and immediately relevant (someone needs salt)
- The literal interpretation (question about ability) provides minimal cognitive effects at high effort
- The hearer adopts the request interpretation because it meets the relevance threshold with least effort

**On indirect answers**: Relevance Theory predicts that indirect answers require more processing effort than direct ones, but they also yield additional cognitive effects (implicatures) that compensate for the extra effort. For example:
- Q: "Is John a good cook?" A: "He's English." -- The indirect answer requires effort to process but yields the rich implicature that John is probably not a good cook (playing on stereotypes).

### Model 5: Neural Evidence

Recent neuroimaging studies have found that:
- **Core language network regions** process the linguistic content of indirect speech acts
- **Theory of Mind (ToM) network regions** (medial prefrontal cortex, temporoparietal junction) are additionally recruited for indirect speech acts, especially those that are more socially complex
- More indirect speech acts engage ToM regions more strongly than less indirect ones
- Individual differences in indirect speech act processing correlate with activity outside the core language network

This neural evidence supports the idea that indirect speech act comprehension involves both linguistic processing AND social cognition/mentalizing, with the balance depending on how conventional the indirect form is.

### Current Consensus

The field has largely moved toward a **hybrid view**:

1. **Highly conventional** indirect speech acts ("Can you...?", "Would you mind...?") are processed via **direct access** -- their indirect meaning has become the default/salient meaning through frequency and convention
2. **Non-conventional** indirect speech acts (hints, novel implicatures) require **inferential processing** that may involve computing the literal meaning first
3. **Context** (including speaker status, relationship type, and situational factors) modulates processing for non-conventional forms
4. **Both literal and non-literal meanings** may be activated to varying degrees, with the stronger/more salient meaning winning the competition

## 6. Autism and Indirect Speech Acts

### Overview of the Research Landscape

The relationship between autism and indirect speech acts is more nuanced than the common characterization of autistic people as "literal-minded" would suggest. Research reveals a **selective** pattern of pragmatic abilities and difficulties, not a global pragmatic impairment.

### Key Finding: Conventional Indirect Requests Are Largely Preserved

**Kissine, Cano-Chervel, et al. (2015)** -- "Children with Autism Understand Indirect Speech Acts: Evidence from a Semi-Structured Act-Out Task" (*PLOS ONE*)

- **Participants**: 15 children with autism (ages 7-12) compared with 20 typically developing children (ages 2;7-3;6)
- **Method**: Semi-structured task using Mr. Potato Head, where children received indirect requests in different phases
- **Key finding**: Children with autism complied with indirect requests in the first phase and demonstrated the capacity to inhibit the directive interpretation in later phases (when the "request" was meant literally)
- **Significance**: This challenges the view that autistic children have a blanket difficulty with non-literal language

**Beaurrain & Kissine (2016)** -- "Evidence from autism: Conventionalization in indirect speech acts"

- Autistic individuals correctly interpret non-literal uses of language **so long as such uses are conventionalized** and the conventions have entered their lexicons
- They readily process "Can you..." utterances as indirect requests
- This suggests that conventional indirect speech acts may be stored as **lexicalized** form-function mappings, not requiring on-the-fly pragmatic inference

**Kissine's theoretical framework**: Proposed that "egocentrically anchored pragmatic phenomena" do not cause difficulties for autistic people. Specifically, knowing that "Can you pass me the salt?" counts as a request (a convention one can learn) does not require reading the speaker's mind. The primary pragmatic deficit in autism is in reading speakers' minds, so autistic individuals should mainly fail at inferences requiring attribution of speakers' beliefs and intentions.

### Key Finding: Selective Pragmatic Impairment -- Indirect Requests vs. Irony

**Deliens, Papastamou, Ruytenbeek, Geelhand, & Kissine (2018)** -- "Selective Pragmatic Impairment in Autism Spectrum Disorder: Indirect Requests Versus Irony" (*Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*)

- **Participants**: 24 adults with ASD matched with neurotypical adults
- **Method**: Two act-out pragmatic tasks -- one examining indirect request comprehension, one examining irony comprehension
- **Core finding**: **Intact comprehension of indirect requests** combined with **marked difficulties in understanding irony**
- **Interpretation**: Preserved pragmatics in ASD may be limited to **egocentric processing of context** (which does not rely on assumptions about the speaker's mental states), while impaired pragmatics involves **mentalizing** (inferring the speaker's attitude, as required for irony)

This is a landmark finding because it shows that the pragmatic profile in autism is **not uniformly impaired** but rather selectively affected based on whether the task requires Theory of Mind.

### High-Functioning Autism and Indirect Requests for Information

**Study on indirect requests for information in high-functioning autism** (*Cognitive Processing*, 2021)

- Few studies had addressed indirect request processing specifically in high-functioning autism, and results were conflicting
- This study found that high-functioning autistic individuals can understand indirect and highly indirect requests **as well as** typically developing individuals
- However, the mechanisms may differ -- autistic individuals may rely more on learned patterns and contextual rules rather than on mentalizing

### The Literalism Question: A Predictive Processing Account

**Vicente, Michel, & Petrolini (2023)** -- "Literalism in Autistic People: A Predictive Processing Proposal" (*Review of Philosophy and Psychology*)

This paper offers a novel account of why autistic people tend toward literal interpretation:

- **Traditional explanations** (executive dysfunction, Theory of Mind deficits) are criticized as circular -- they assume a literalist bias to explain the literalist bias
- **Predictive processing proposal**: Literalism results from an **atypical balance between predictions and error signals** in language processing
  - Autistic individuals may have **weaker top-down predictions** (prior expectations about what a speaker means) relative to **stronger bottom-up error signals** (the actual linguistic input)
  - This creates greater **uncertainty about predictions**, which is resolved by defaulting to the **safest interpretation** -- the literal one
  - The literal meaning is "safest" because it requires the fewest assumptions beyond what is explicitly stated
- This account explains why:
  - **Conventional** indirect speech acts may be handled well (because their conventionality provides strong prediction data even with a weaker predictive system)
  - **Novel or non-conventional** indirect speech acts are more challenging (because they require strong top-down predictions about speaker intent)

### Figurative Language Meta-Analysis

**Kalandadze, Norbury, Naerland, & Naess (2018)** -- "Figurative Language Comprehension in Individuals with Autism Spectrum Disorder: A Meta-Analytic Review" (*Autism*)

- **Overall effect size**: Individuals with ASD showed poorer figurative language comprehension than typically developing peers (Hedges' g = -0.57, moderate effect)
- **Critical moderator**: When groups were **matched on language ability**, differences were **small and nonsignificant**
- **Type of figurative language matters**: Metaphors were more difficult than irony/sarcasm for ASD groups (counterintuitively, given that irony requires more mentalizing)
- **Implication**: Much of the apparent figurative language deficit in autism may be attributable to underlying language ability differences rather than a specific pragmatic impairment

### Figurative Language Processing Review (2024)

**Lampri et al. (2024)** -- "Figurative language processing in autism spectrum disorders: A review" (*Autism Research*)

- Confirmed that difficulty processing figurative language is a defining feature of ASD
- However, autistic children develop pragmatic skills, including understanding figurative speech, **at a slower developmental rate** -- not necessarily to a lower ceiling
- **Linguistic vs. Social pragmatics distinction**: Autistic individuals may perform relatively well on **linguistic pragmatic tasks** (resolving ambiguities, understanding metaphors based on linguistic analysis and reasoning) while struggling with **social pragmatic tasks** (requiring perspective-taking and contextual integration involving others' mental states)

### Theory of Mind and Indirect Speech Act Types

Different types of non-literal language require different levels of Theory of Mind:

| Type | ToM Required | Typical ASD Performance |
|---|---|---|
| Conventional indirect requests ("Can you...?") | Minimal (conventionalized) | Generally preserved |
| Non-conventional indirect requests (hints) | Moderate (requires contextual inference) | Variable; may be preserved with strong context |
| Sarcasm | Moderate-high (requires detecting attitude mismatch) | Often impaired |
| Irony | High (requires attributing false belief/attitude to speaker) | Typically impaired |
| Second-order irony ("As if he'd ever be on time") | Very high (requires nested mental state attribution) | Most impaired |

First-order Theory of Mind appears sufficient for autistic children to comprehend metaphors, while processing irony requires second-order ToM skills.

### Masking and Compensation in Autistic Adults

Research on autistic adults in social and workplace contexts reveals compensation strategies for indirect speech:

- **Memorized scripts and patterns**: Many autistic adults develop extensive libraries of conventional phrases and their meanings (e.g., learning that "we should do lunch sometime" is a polite closing, not a genuine invitation)
- **Rule-based processing**: Rather than intuitively reading pragmatic intent, autistic adults may apply explicit rules (e.g., "if someone says 'that's interesting' in a flat tone, it probably means they disagree")
- **Pattern matching**: Drawing on past experiences to match current utterances with previously observed meanings
- **Exhaustion cost**: These compensation strategies require conscious effort and contribute to **autistic burnout**, particularly in workplace settings where indirect communication is constant
- **Preference for directness**: Many autistic people prefer to avoid implications, hints, and assumptions, finding them challenging to interpret; neurotypical people often rely on indirectness to avoid vulnerability and prevent hurt feelings, creating a fundamental communication style mismatch

### Summary: The Autism-Indirect Speech Acts Picture

The research paints a picture of **selective, not global, pragmatic impairment**:

1. **Conventional indirect speech acts** (formulaic patterns like "Can you...?") are largely preserved -- these can be learned as conventions without requiring real-time mentalizing
2. **Non-conventional indirect speech acts** (hints, insinuations) show variable performance -- context-rich scenarios may support comprehension, but the absence of conventionalized forms makes these harder
3. **Irony and sarcasm** are the most consistently impaired -- these require attributing mental states (beliefs, attitudes, intentions) to the speaker
4. **Language ability** is a critical moderator -- when matched for language ability, many apparent pragmatic deficits shrink or disappear
5. **Compensatory strategies** (scripting, rule-based processing, pattern matching) can support indirect speech act comprehension but at a cognitive cost
6. The **predictive processing account** offers a promising mechanistic explanation: weaker top-down predictions lead to preference for literal (safest) interpretations, with conventionalized patterns providing enough predictive data to overcome this bias

## 7. Indirect Speech Acts in Everyday Contexts

### 7.1 Making Requests at Work

The workplace is a prime environment for indirect speech acts due to constant face-management across hierarchies.

**Employee to manager (upward requests):**
- "I was wondering if there's any flexibility with the deadline?" (= I need more time)
- "Do you think it might make sense to bring in another person on this?" (= I'm overwhelmed)
- "I wanted to run something by you when you have a moment." (= I need to discuss a problem)
- "Would it be possible to get some feedback on my performance?" (= I want a raise/promotion discussion)

**Manager to employee (downward directives softened):**
- "Let's make sure this gets done by Friday." (= Do this by Friday -- "let's" creates false collaboration)
- "I'd like to see this handled differently going forward." (= You're doing it wrong; change your approach)
- "Have you had a chance to look at that report yet?" (= Why haven't you finished the report?)
- "You might want to reconsider that approach." (= Don't do it that way)

**Peer to peer:**
- "I could use another pair of eyes on this." (= Please review my work)
- "The conference room is free if you want to move this discussion." (= This conversation shouldn't be happening in the open office)

**Email-specific patterns:**
- "Per my last email..." (= You didn't read/respond to what I already told you)
- "As I mentioned..." (= I already said this and you should have been paying attention)
- "Just circling back on this..." (= Why haven't you responded?)
- "I want to make sure we're aligned on..." (= I think you're doing the wrong thing)
- "Going forward, let's make sure to..." (= You made a mistake; don't do it again)
- "Friendly reminder that..." (= This isn't friendly; do the thing you haven't done)

### 7.2 Giving and Receiving Criticism

Criticism is one of the most face-threatening acts, so it is almost always delivered indirectly in most social contexts.

**Indirect criticism patterns:**
- **The sandwich/hedge**: "I really like the overall direction. I wonder if we might strengthen the conclusion a bit. Great work overall." (= The conclusion is weak)
- **The question frame**: "Have you considered alternative approaches?" (= Your approach is wrong)
- **The comparison**: "Last time, the results were a bit more polished." (= This is sloppy)
- **The self-inclusion**: "Maybe we could all be a bit more careful about..." (= You specifically need to be more careful)
- **The observation**: "I've noticed that the numbers don't quite add up in section 3." (= You made errors)
- **The conditional**: "If it were me, I might approach it differently." (= You did it wrong)
- **The understated negative**: "This isn't your strongest work." (= This is poor quality)
- **The attribution to others**: "Some people might find this confusing." (= I find it confusing)
- **The praise with "but"**: "You make a good point, but I think there might be another angle." (= I disagree with your point)

**Passive-aggressive criticism patterns:**
- "No offense, but..." (almost always precedes something offensive)
- "With all due respect..." (signals that disrespect follows)
- "I'm just being honest..." (framing hurtful criticism as a virtue)
- "Nice of you to finally join us." (criticism of lateness disguised as a compliment)
- "I guess you did the best you could." (implying the best wasn't good enough)
- "I'm sorry you feel that way." (non-apology; invalidating the other's feelings)
- "Just so you know..." / "For future reference..." (establishing you made an error without directly saying so)
- "That was certainly... brave of you." (implying the choice was foolish)

### 7.3 Declining Invitations

Declining invitations is a face-threatening act (threatening the inviter's positive face), so it is heavily mitigated with indirect strategies.

**The standard indirect refusal sequence** (Beebe, Takahashi, & Uliss-Weltz, 1990):
1. Expression of regret: "I'm so sorry..." / "I wish I could..."
2. Indirect refusal via excuse: "I have an early meeting tomorrow." / "I already have plans."
3. Offer of alternative: "Maybe next time?" / "Could we do next week instead?"

**Common patterns:**
- "I'd love to, but I have a thing." (= No)
- "That sounds great, but I'll have to check my schedule." (= Probably no; giving myself time to find an excuse)
- "I'm not sure I can make it, but I'll try." (= I won't be there)
- "Maybe next time!" (= Generic non-commitment; may or may not mean it)
- "I'm pretty exhausted lately." (= No, and I don't want to explain why)
- "Things are really crazy at work right now." (= No)
- "I'll see what I can do." (= Unlikely, but leaving the door open)
- "Let me get back to you on that." (= I need time to figure out how to say no)

**Why "no" is rarely said directly:**
- It threatens the inviter's positive face (rejection)
- It threatens the relationship (perceived as unfriendly)
- The indirect refusal preserves the fiction that the decliner would attend if circumstances allowed
- The excuse (even when clearly fabricated) provides a face-saving explanation that both parties can accept

### 7.4 Expressing Disagreement

Disagreement threatens both the speaker's and hearer's positive face, making indirect expression very common.

**Graduated indirectness in disagreement:**

*Most indirect (off-record):*
- "Hmm, that's one way to look at it." (= I disagree but won't say so directly)
- "I see what you mean..." (often followed by silence or topic change, indicating disagreement)
- "That's an interesting perspective." (= I find it wrong but won't say so)

*Moderately indirect (hedged disagreement):*
- "I see your point, but I wonder if..." (= I disagree, and here's why)
- "I think there might be another way to think about this." (= I think you're wrong)
- "I'm not sure I'd go quite that far." (= I strongly disagree with your extreme position)
- "That's partly true, but..." (= It's mostly wrong)
- "I think we might be looking at this from different angles." (= We disagree)
- "I hear what you're saying, but in my experience..." (= My experience contradicts yours)
- "With respect, I think the data suggests something different." (= You're wrong and here's proof)

*Least indirect (direct but mitigated):*
- "I have to respectfully disagree." (= I disagree)
- "I think that's not quite right." (= That's wrong)
- "I don't think I agree with that interpretation." (= I disagree)

**The "yes, but" pattern**: Perhaps the most common structure for indirect disagreement:
- "Yes, and..." (agreement + addition) vs. "Yes, but..." (surface agreement + actual disagreement)
- The "yes" acknowledges the other person's contribution (positive face), while "but" introduces the disagreement
- Variants: "True, but...", "Sure, though...", "Right, although..."
- The hearer quickly learns that "yes, but" means "no, and here's why"

### 7.5 Flirting and Expressing Romantic Interest

Romantic contexts are perhaps the richest domain for indirect speech acts, because the stakes of directness are extremely high: direct expressions of romantic interest risk rejection (threatening the speaker's positive face), make the other uncomfortable (threatening the hearer's negative face), and create common knowledge that may make future interaction awkward.

**Pinker's analysis**: A sexual come-on is the quintessential indirect speech act because:
- The speaker proposes a shift from a reciprocity/acquaintance relationship to a communality/intimacy relationship
- Direct proposals make this relationship-type shift explicit, creating common knowledge of the proposal even if rejected
- Indirect proposals allow plausible deniability ("I was just being friendly") that preserves both parties' face

**Common indirect patterns:**
- "Would you like to come up for coffee?" (= Would you like to come up for sex? -- so conventionalized it's almost direct)
- "We should hang out sometime." (= I want to see you in a non-group context / I'm interested)
- "I'm having a great time talking to you." (= I'm attracted to you)
- "You have such interesting taste in music." (= I'm drawn to you)
- Extended eye contact, mirroring, proximal positioning (= non-verbal indirect signals)
- "I don't usually do this, but..." (= I want to signal that this feels special)
- "You're really easy to talk to." (= I'm enjoying the connection / I'm attracted to you)
- "I know this great little place..." (= I'd like to take you on a date)

**The structural ambiguity of flirting:**
Lucy McDonald (in philosophical work on flirting) notes that flirters typically **do not make it clear** to their interlocutors what they intend to do, and **this ambiguity is constitutive of flirting itself** -- it's not merely a byproduct but part of what makes flirting *flirting* rather than a direct proposition.

Flirting involves "pull moves" -- pretending to block a presupposition of intimacy (feigning affront or disinterest) while actually accommodating it. This creates a **playful tension** between apparent and actual communicative intent that is itself pleasurable.

**Research on linguistic markers** (Ranganath, Jurafsky, & McFarland, 2013):
Speed-dating studies found that flirtatious speech is marked by:
- Joint focus on the woman as a target of conversation
- Higher rates of questions and elaboration
- Laughter and appreciative responses
- Self-disclosure and expression of shared interests

### 7.6 Teacher-Student Interactions

Classroom discourse is a rich domain for indirect speech acts, shaped by institutional power dynamics and pedagogical goals.

**Teacher directive patterns:**

*Control directives (managing behavior):*
- "I'm waiting." (= Be quiet)
- "Does anyone want to share what's so funny?" (= Stop laughing and pay attention)
- "I can see some people aren't ready yet." (= Certain students need to settle down)
- "It's getting a bit loud in here." (= Be quiet)
- "I'm sure everyone would like to hear what you're saying." (= Stop having side conversations)
- "Let's focus, shall we?" (= Pay attention)

*Instructional directives (guiding learning):*
- "What do we think about this?" (= I want you to think about this -- "we" softens the directive)
- "Can someone tell me what happens next?" (= Tell me what happens next)
- "Why might the author have chosen that word?" (= Analyze the author's word choice -- disguised as a question)
- "Does anyone see a pattern here?" (= Look for a pattern)

*Feedback/correction through indirectness:*
- "Are you sure about that?" (= That's wrong; try again)
- "Interesting answer. Does anyone see it differently?" (= That's not quite right)
- "You're on the right track." (= Not quite there yet)
- "Can you think about that a bit more?" (= Your answer is incomplete/wrong)
- "What makes you say that?" (= Justify your answer, or reconsider it)
- "Almost!" (= Wrong, but I don't want to discourage you)

**Student patterns:**
- "I don't understand the question." (= I don't know the answer)
- "Is this going to be on the test?" (= I don't see the point of learning this)
- "Could you go over that one more time?" (= I'm lost)
- "My computer crashed and I lost my file." (= I didn't do the assignment)

**Power and indirectness in classrooms:**
Research in Austrian CLIL classrooms found unexpected levels of indirectness in teacher directive speech acts. Teachers' indirect directives serve multiple functions: maintaining authority while preserving rapport, guiding students toward answers without giving them directly (Socratic method), and modeling polite interaction norms.

The use of interrogative and declarative forms (being indirect) increases politeness in classroom discourse, and a teacher's directive may be perceived as instructional while a similar utterance from a student may be perceived as disruptive -- showing how power relations determine how the same indirect speech act is interpreted.

### 7.7 Cross-Cultural Variation

The forms and norms of indirect speech vary significantly across cultures, though indirectness itself appears to be universal:

**CCSARP findings** (Blum-Kulka & Olshtain, 1984):
Studying requests across 8 languages (Australian/American/British English, Canadian French, Danish, German, Hebrew, Russian), the project found:
- The overall distribution along the scale of indirectness follows **similar patterns** in all languages
- However, the specific proportions between direct and indirect strategies are **culture-specific**
- What counts as conventionally indirect varies across languages

**Specific cross-cultural differences:**
- **English vs. Korean**: English speakers frequently use indirect speech acts (ISAs) for polite requests, while Korean speakers rarely use ISAs; instead, Korean relies on **honorifics** for politeness. Conventional indirect strategies like hints are not significantly correlated with politeness in Korean, while performatives and want-statements are perceived as direct but polite.
- **English vs. Japanese**: Japanese uses more indirect-level requests than German, with higher-context cultural norms emphasizing group harmony and face preservation
- **English vs. Arabic**: Egyptian Arabic tends toward more directness than American English in some contexts, but Arabic also has elaborate indirect politeness systems involving religious expressions and honorifics
- **Chinese, Korean, and American refusals**: All groups employed indirect refusal strategies more frequently than direct ones, but American subjects used direct refusals significantly more than Chinese and Korean subjects

**The key insight**: Indirectness and politeness don't correlate the same way across cultures. In some cultures, directness is perceived as warm and honest, while in others it is perceived as rude and face-threatening. The universal is the existence of indirectness; the particular is which forms, when, and how much.
