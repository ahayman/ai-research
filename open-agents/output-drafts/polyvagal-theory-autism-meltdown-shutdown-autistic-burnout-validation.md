## Claim-by-Claim Validation

### Claim 1: "Raymaker et al. 2020, *Autism in Adulthood* 2(2):132–143, defined autistic burnout via thematic analysis of 19 interviews + 19 public internet sources, identifying chronic exhaustion, loss of skills, and reduced tolerance to stimulus as the primary triad."
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: Multiple independent sources confirm authors (Raymaker, Teo, Steckler, Lentz, Scharer, Delos Santos, Kapp, Hunter, Joyce, Nicolaidis), journal (*Autism in Adulthood*), volume 2 issue 2, pages 132–143 (June 2020 issue), methodology (community-based participatory research, thematic analysis), sample (19 interviews + 19 public sources), and the three-feature triad.
- **Sources**: [Liebert Pub abstract](https://www.liebertpub.com/doi/abs/10.1089/aut.2019.0079), [PDX Scholar mirror](https://pdxscholar.library.pdx.edu/socwork_fac/378/), [APA research summary](https://www.apa.org/education-career/k12/infusing-diversity/mental-physical-health/research-summary-autistic-burnout)
- **Notes**: Raymaker's framing uses cumulative load + barriers to support + loss of access to coping mechanisms. This is consistent with allostatic load. The paper does NOT invoke polyvagal theory.

### Claim 2: "Raymaker 2020 frames burnout as an allostatic-load model, not a polyvagal model."
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: The Raymaker 2020 paper explicitly does not use polyvagal vocabulary; its operational framing is cumulative load + barriers to relief. Independent secondary work (Mantzalas et al. CMAB integrating Job Demands-Resources, Conservation of Resources, Social-Relational disability; mitochondrial-allostatic-load framework PMC9732262) is consistent. McEwen's allostatic load is the dominant framework cited by empirical autistic-burnout researchers.
- **Sources**: [Raymaker 2020 full text](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7313636/), [PMC9732262 mitochondrial AL framework](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9732262/)
- **Notes**: The popular reframing of burnout as "chronic dorsal vagal state" is post-hoc and not the framework used in the empirical literature. This is correctly flagged in the chapter.

### Claim 3: "Cheng, Huang & Huang (2020), meta-analysis of 34 HRV studies in autism, found Hedges' g = −0.5168 baseline parasympathetic HRV, g = −0.5860 baseline RSA, g = −0.7246 RSA reactivity under social stress."
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: Multiple independent sources confirm authors, journal (*Neuroscience and Biobehavioral Reviews* 118:463–471, November 2020, DOI 10.1016/j.neubiorev.2020.08.007, PMID 32818581), and the specific effect-size values. The g = −0.7246 figure for RSA reactivity under social stress is the largest reported effect and is highlighted in the original abstract.
- **Sources**: [PubMed 32818581](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32818581/), [ScienceDirect S014976342030525X](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S014976342030525X), [Ovid Cheng 2020 PDF](https://www.ovid.com/journals/nebior/pdf/10.1016/j.neubiorev.2020.08.007~heart-rate-variability-in-individuals-with-autism-spectrum)
- **Notes**: The chapter is careful to note that this is HRV/RSA reactivity, NOT a direct measurement of meltdown. The reframing of meltdown as sympathetic over-arousal is *consistent with* the Cheng data but not a direct test.

### Claim 4: "Cheng 2020 evidence supports the reframing of autistic meltdown as sympathetic over-arousal."
- **Verdict**: UNCERTAIN (7/10)
- **Original context**: "Meltdown-as-sympathetic-over-arousal is empirically defensible. The reframing has some mechanistic anchoring."
- **Evidence for**: Reduced RSA reactivity to stress = reduced parasympathetic withdrawal = relatively greater sympathetic dominance under stress. This is consistent with sympathetic over-arousal as a substrate for meltdown.
- **Evidence against**: Cheng 2020 measured HRV in laboratory paradigms, not meltdown specifically. The inferential chain from "reduced RSA reactivity in lab social stressor" to "meltdown is sympathetic over-arousal" includes an assumption that the lab autonomic profile generalises to the much-more-extreme overwhelm state of a meltdown. This is plausible but not directly tested.
- **Why uncertain**: The chapter correctly hedges this. The evidence base is one step removed from a direct test.
- **What evidence actually supports**: Autistic individuals show reduced parasympathetic withdrawal under social stress in lab paradigms. This is *consistent* with the sympathetic-over-arousal reframing of meltdown; it does not directly validate it.
- **Notes**: Chapter language uses appropriate hedges ("consistent with," "some empirical support," "empirically defensible").

### Claim 5: "Neuhuber & Berthoud (2022, *Biological Psychology* 174:108425, PMID 36100134) argue that emotional freezing in mammals is mediated by the nucleus ambiguus (ventral), not the dorsal motor nucleus."
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: Multiple independent sources confirm the paper's title, journal, authors, and core argument. The conclusion that the basic phylogenetic and functional-anatomical tenets of PVT "do not withstand closer scrutiny" is widely cited in the post-2022 critical literature. Comparative neuroanatomy supports NA-mediated rather than DMV-mediated freezing in mammals.
- **Sources**: [PubMed 36100134](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36100134/), [Polyvagal Institute critical discussion page](https://www.polyvagalinstitute.org/vagal-paradox), [Wikipedia polyvagal critique summary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyvagal_theory)
- **Notes**: Multiple recent papers (e.g., the 2024 "five basic premises" refutation in *Biological Psychology*) build on Neuhuber & Berthoud's critique. The chapter's use is calibrated — the critique applies to the *mechanism* of shutdown, not necessarily to the descriptive utility of the heuristic.

### Claim 6: "There is no direct in-vivo measurement of a 'dorsal vagal state' in humans."
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: The dorsal motor nucleus innervates sub-diaphragmatic viscera with unmyelinated fibres and is not amenable to specific non-invasive assay. HRV indices (RMSSD, HF-HRV, RSA) reflect cardiac vagal tone mediated via the nucleus ambiguus, not the DMV. "Dorsal vagal state" in PVT-informed practice is inferred from behavioural pattern (immobilisation, withdrawal) and self-report, not measured directly. This is acknowledged even in PVT-sympathetic clinical literature.
- **Sources**: General neuroscience literature on vagal anatomy; HRV literature on what RSA actually indexes; multiple recent neurodivergent-informed sources noting the inability to isolate a discrete "dorsal vagal shutdown" state.
- **Notes**: This is a correct and important claim. The chapter's emphasis on this point is well-calibrated.

### Claim 7: "Phung, Penner, Pirlot & Welch (2021), *Frontiers in Psychology*, qualitative study with 8 autistic youth distinguishing burnout, inertia, meltdown, shutdown."
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: Multiple independent sources confirm authors, journal (*Frontiers in Psychology*, vol 12, Nov 2021, DOI 10.3389/fpsyg.2021.741421, PMID 34803822), sample size (n=8 autistic children/youth, ages 8–18), methodology (one-to-one interviews, reflexive inductive thematic analysis), and the four-state framework (burnout/inertia/meltdown/shutdown).
- **Sources**: [PMC8595127](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8595127/), [Frontiers full text](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2021.741421/full)
- **Notes**: The chapter's specific quotes from autistic youth ("Just feeling drained..."; "I'll completely freeze up, I can't really talk, I'll stutter") are from this study.

### Claim 8: "Higgins/Arnold 2025 (ScienceDirect S3050579825000105) found r = 0.70 correlation between autistic burnout (ABSI) and PTSS (PCL-5) in trauma-exposed autistic adults."
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: Independent search confirmed the study, the sample (n = 91 trauma-exposed autistic adults without intellectual disability), the methodology (Pearson correlations between ABSI and PCL-5, PHQ-9, GAD-7), and the specific values: burnout × PTSS r = 0.70 (p < 0.001), burnout × depression r = 0.61 (p < 0.001), burnout × generalised anxiety r = 0.67 (p < 0.001).
- **Sources**: [ScienceDirect S3050579825000105](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S3050579825000105), [ResearchGate 397831629](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/397831629)
- **Notes**: The exploratory factor analysis in the paper suggested shared underlying factors between burnout and PTSS, particularly self-image and memory. Chapter correctly notes substantial overlap but not identity. Sample size (n=91) is modest; cross-sectional design limits causal inference. These caveats should be reflected in the explanation.

### Claim 9: "Rumball, Happé & Grey (2020, *Autism Research*) found 63% lifetime probable PTSD from non-DSM-5 stressors and 61% from DSM-5 stressors in autistic adults."
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: Multiple independent sources confirm the 63% non-DSM-5 / 61% DSM-5 lifetime probable PTSD figures, plus 45% current probable PTSD in the DSM-5-exposed subgroup. The methodology (self-report of trauma exposure plus standardised PTSD measure) and the finding that "social misperceptions, bullying, and aversive sensory experiences" were associated with PTSS as often as Criterion-A events are reproduced across sources.
- **Sources**: [Wiley aur.2306](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/aur.2306), [ResearchGate 340850651](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/340850651)
- **Notes**: This is the foundational paper for the "trauma-without-Criterion-A" pattern in autistic adults. Cross-link to existing alexithymia-deep-dive cumulative-overwhelm work is appropriate.

### Claim 10: "Bougoure, Zhuang, Brett et al. (2026), *Autism* 30(1):20–36, psychometric validation of AASPIRE Autistic Burnout Measure with n=379 autistic adults, 27 items, ω = 0.98, AUC = 0.92."
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: Multiple independent sources confirm authors, journal (*Autism* 30(1):20–36, online July 2025 / print January 2026, PMID 40698409), sample (n=379 autistic adults online, 12-month longitudinal follow-up), measure structure (27 items, predominantly unidimensional), internal consistency (ω = 0.98), test-retest (r = 0.59 over 12 months), and discrimination (AUC = 0.92, 95% CI 0.86–0.97).
- **Sources**: [PubMed 40698409](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40698409/), [SAGE Pub 13623613251355255](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/13623613251355255), [Macquarie University research portal](https://researchers.mq.edu.au/en/publications/measuring-autistic-burnout-a-psychometric-validation-of-the-aaspi/)
- **Notes**: Chapter correctly cites this as the first research-grade quantitative validation. The measure shows medium-to-large correlations with autistic traits, camouflaging, occupational burnout, depression, and anxiety — consistent with the construct-validity argument.

### Claim 11: "Ali, Mandy & Happé (2025) systematic review covering 48 studies (30 qualitative, 7 quantitative, 11 mixed methods) and ~4,000 autistic people, *Clinical Psychology Review*."
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: Multiple independent sources confirm the systematic review's existence, authorship (Dorota Ali, Will Mandy, Francesca Happé, plus others), study count (48), participant count (~4,000), journal (*Clinical Psychology Review*, Volume 122, Article 102669), and PMID 41207162.
- **Sources**: [PubMed 41207162](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41207162/), [King's College London publications portal](https://kclpure.kcl.ac.uk/portal/en/publications/burnout-as-experienced-by-autistic-people-a-systematic-review/), [UWA research repository](https://research-repository.uwa.edu.au/en/publications/2fa43a06-3a78-467f-9ecf-a4c3a4eca386)
- **Notes**: Chapter's "~48+ studies as of 2025" framing is accurate; 48 is the systematic review's inclusion count, with growth ongoing.

### Claim 12: "Chua (2023), 'Using Polyvagal Theory to Understand Autistic Meltdown,' *Asian Journal of Interdisciplinary Research*, ResearchGate 375872547."
- **Verdict**: UNCERTAIN (8/10)
- **Original context**: "Chua's 'Using Polyvagal Theory to Understand Autistic Meltdown' (2023, *Asian Journal of Interdisciplinary Research*, RG 375872547) frames meltdown explicitly in these terms."
- **Evidence for**: Paper exists at the ResearchGate ID and the journal location; framing of meltdown via polyvagal theory's three-stage (social engagement / mobilisation / immobilisation) model is confirmed.
- **Evidence against**: Journal is regional and less indexed; full evaluation of methodological rigor requires deeper read. The paper appears to be a theoretical/conceptual piece rather than original empirical work — which is consistent with the chapter's framing (it's a framework piece, not a direct empirical test).
- **Why uncertain**: Journal-level credibility uncertain; chapter's treatment of the paper as "a framework piece, evaluated critically" rather than as primary evidence is appropriate, but the chapter should be explicit that this is a conceptual/heuristic application paper rather than original empirical evidence.
- **What evidence actually supports**: The paper demonstrates that the polyvagal framing of meltdown is articulated in the published literature. It does not provide independent empirical validation of that framing. The chapter handles this correctly.

### Claim 13: "Trauma Geek (Janae Elisabeth) — Medium / website — argues for adopting some PVT concepts while pushing back on autism-as-deficit framing, 'faulty neuroception' language, and ventral supremacy."
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: The Medium article and the trauma geek website confirm the position. - **Sources**: [Trauma Geek Medium article](https://autietraumageek.medium.com/a-neurodiversity-paradigm-lens-on-polyvagal-theory-pvt-7f9495cffbd8), [Trauma Geek main site](https://www.traumageek.com/)
- **Notes**: This is clinical/lived-experience commentary, not empirical research. Chapter treats it appropriately as a neurodiversity-affirming reading worth including for the user's framing sensitivity.

### Claim 14: "Polyvagal language can substitute for accurate diagnosis when chronic exhaustion has underlying causes like ME/CFS, depression, severe sleep deprivation, or POTS."
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: Multiple sources confirm the differential-diagnosis caution. ME/CFS specifically requires post-exertional malaise as a defining feature (DePaul/CDC criteria; IOM/SEID criteria); this differentiates it from autistic burnout. POTS is diagnosable by tilt-table testing or orthostatic tachycardia and has dedicated treatment pathways. Sleep deprivation alone can produce a burnout-pattern phenomenology. The "polyvagal substitute" pattern is widely acknowledged in chronic-illness advocacy and in autism-informed clinical writing.
- **Sources**: [Neurodiverse Network — burnout vs depression vs CFS](https://www.neurodiversenetwork.com.au/post/understanding-the-difference-between-autistic-burnout-depression-stress-and-chronic-fatigue), [Vitality 360 — neurodiversity and fatigue](https://www.vitality360.co.uk/blog/neurodiversity-and-fatigue), [Ani Møller — burnout vs ME/CFS vs AuDHD fatigue](https://www.animoller.com/resources/burnout-vs-mecfs-audhd-fatigue), [Dr Alice Nicholls — CFS/ME or autistic burnout?](https://www.dralicenicholls.com/cfs-or-autistic-burnout/)
- **Notes**: The chapter's three-criterion differential (PEM crashes → ME/CFS workup; persistent anhedonia → depression evaluation; orthostatic symptoms → POTS workup) is clinically sound and matches advocacy/clinical guidance.

## Research Opportunities

### Opportunity 1: Direct empirical test of meltdown-as-sympathetic-over-arousal mapping
- **Priority**: MEDIUM
- **Driven by**: Claim 4 rated UNCERTAIN (7/10)
- **Why**: Cheng 2020 data is consistent with the mapping but does not directly measure meltdown. An ecological-momentary-assessment study with wearable HRV during real-world meltdown events would substantially raise the evidence level.
- **Suggested queries**: "autism meltdown wearable HRV", "autistic meltdown ambulatory physiological", "in-vivo autism overwhelm autonomic"
- **Expected impact**: Could move Claim 4 from UNCERTAIN to CONFIRMED if positive, or to INVALID if the reframing turns out not to fit naturalistic data.

### Opportunity 2: Comparing polyvagal vs allostatic-load frameworks for burnout
- **Priority**: MEDIUM
- **Driven by**: The chapter's structural claim that allostatic load fits the empirical literature better than polyvagal reframing
- **Why**: A head-to-head theoretical comparison (and ideally an empirical study testing distinct predictions of each framework) would clarify what each frame can and cannot explain.
- **Suggested queries**: "allostatic load vs polyvagal", "burnout framework comparison neuroscience", "competing models autistic burnout"
- **Expected impact**: Would strengthen the chapter's central structural argument with explicit framework comparison rather than narrative juxtaposition.

### Opportunity 3: Differential diagnosis screening tools for chronic autistic exhaustion
- **Priority**: HIGH
- **Driven by**: Claim 14 — the substitute-for-diagnosis caution
- **Why**: Clinical guidance for distinguishing autistic burnout from ME/CFS, depression, dysautonomia, and unmet accommodation needs is uneven. A practical screening approach for primary care or self-assessment would have direct utility.
- **Suggested queries**: "autistic burnout differential diagnosis screening", "ME/CFS PEM autistic adults", "chronic exhaustion neurodivergent workup"
- **Expected impact**: Practical clinical-decision-aid value for the user and for autistic readers more broadly.

### Opportunity 4: Updated counts of burnout literature (post-2025)
- **Priority**: LOW
- **Driven by**: The literature is growing rapidly; 48 studies is a 2025 snapshot
- **Why**: For a chapter that emphasises this as an actively-developing empirical area, periodic re-counts maintain accuracy.
- **Suggested queries**: "autistic burnout 2026 review", "AASPIRE burnout measure replication"
- **Expected impact**: Maintains current standing of the empirical-base claim.
