# Non-Literal Language Processing in Autism: Comprehensive Research Review

## Category 11: Non-Literal Language (Figurative Language, Humor, Irony, Sarcasm)

**Purpose**: Knowledge base for teaching English pragmatics to autistic individuals
**Research Date**: April 2026
**Scope**: Meta-analyses, experimental studies, intervention research, community perspectives

---

## 1. THE META-ANALYTIC PICTURE

### 1.1 Kalandadze et al. (2018): The Landmark Meta-Analysis

The most comprehensive meta-analysis of figurative language comprehension in autism was conducted by Kalandadze, Norbury, Naerland, and Naess (2018), published in *Autism*. This study synthesized 41 studies with 45 independent effect sizes.

**Key findings:**
- **Overall effect size**: Hedges' g = -0.57, indicating that individuals with ASD showed moderately poorer comprehension of figurative language than typically developing (TD) peers
- **Critical moderator - language matching**: When groups were matched on language ability, differences were **small and nonsignificant**. This is arguably the most important finding in the entire literature -- it suggests that figurative language difficulties in autism are substantially driven by underlying language ability, not by an autism-specific deficit in figurative processing per se
- **Type of figurative language matters**: Metaphors showed larger group differences (Hedges' g = 0.72) than irony and sarcasm (Hedges' g = 0.48) in this analysis, though this finding has been debated and may reflect task properties rather than true differences
- **Chronological age was NOT a significant moderator**: This suggests the gap persists across development when not accounting for language ability
- **Cross-linguistic differences were NOT significant**: The pattern holds across languages studied

**Source**: [Kalandadze et al. (2018) - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5843023/)

### 1.2 The "Autistic People Can't Understand Figurative Language" Narrative is Substantially Wrong

The Kalandadze findings fundamentally challenge the widespread assumption that autistic people categorically cannot understand figurative language. The reality is:

1. **When language ability is controlled for**, differences shrink dramatically or disappear entirely
2. **Many autistic individuals with strong language skills** comprehend figurative language at typical levels
3. **The heterogeneity is enormous** -- some autistic individuals struggle significantly while others excel
4. **Development occurs** -- young adult participants with autism performed at the level of control children but better than children with autism, indicating ongoing improvement
5. **Compensation and learning strategies** can bring performance to typical levels, especially for adults

### 1.3 The Andrés-Roqueta 2025 Finding: A Surprising Dissociation

A 2025 study by Andrés-Roqueta and colleagues, "Some (but not all) Pragmatic Inferences are Difficult for Autistic Children" (published in *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*), found a surprising pattern:

- **Autistic children performed WORSE on scalar implicatures** (e.g., interpreting "some" to mean "not all") but **SIMILARLY on metaphors** compared to neurotypical peers
- This was the **opposite** of what the linguistic-vs.-social pragmatics framework predicted
- The framework (Andrés-Roqueta & Katsos, 2017) had predicted that autistic participants would succeed at linguistic-pragmatic tasks (like scalar implicatures) but struggle with social-pragmatic tasks (like metaphors)
- Instead, the results went in the opposite direction

**What this means**: The simple binary distinction between "linguistic pragmatics" (presumed easy for autistic people) and "social pragmatics" (presumed hard) may be insufficient. Autistic pragmatic processing is more complex and heterogeneous than previously theorized.

**Source**: [Andrés-Roqueta et al. (2025)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10803-025-06838-4)

### 1.4 Additional Meta-Analyses and Reviews

**Idiom/Proverb Meta-Analysis**: A systematic review and meta-analysis of idiom and proverb processing in autism (11 studies, 235 autistic and 224 TD individuals) found a **medium effect size** favoring TD over ASD groups, with a trend toward smaller differences among participants with higher verbal intelligence.

**Source**: [Idiom and Proverb Processing Meta-Analysis](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s41809-021-00079-4)

**Metaphor Meta-Analysis (Morsanyi et al., 2020)**: A systematic review and meta-analysis found an overall advantage for TD groups compared to ASD individuals with a medium-to-large effect size, though the effect was substantially moderated by task properties and matching variables.

**Lampri et al. (2024) Review**: Published in *Autism Research*, this comprehensive review found that Theory of Mind (ToM) and verbal skills correlated most strongly with figurative language comprehension in ASD, more than executive functions. The review emphasized that differences in research methodology and task properties may have led to significant discrepancies between study results.

**Source**: [Lampri 2024 Review](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/aur.3069)

**Opening New Worlds of Meaning (2025 Scoping Review)**: The most recent comprehensive review, following PRISMA-ScR reporting and covering 2010-2025, mapped pedagogical and clinical interventions targeting figurative-language skills in school-age learners with ASD. Key finding: under structured conditions, many autistic children demonstrate substantial improvements and often perform at levels approaching TD peers on targeted tasks. However, in spontaneous, unstructured communicative situations, considerable difficulties may persist.

**Source**: [Opening New Worlds of Meaning (2025)](https://www.mdpi.com/2076-328X/15/11/1556)

### 1.5 Summary: The Hierarchy of Difficulty

Based on the meta-analytic evidence, a rough hierarchy of difficulty for autistic individuals emerges:

| Figurative Type | Difficulty Level | Key Factor |
|---|---|---|
| Conventional metaphors | Moderate | Language ability dependent |
| Novel metaphors | Lower than expected | May tap analogical reasoning strengths |
| Idioms (transparent) | Moderate | Context and familiarity help |
| Idioms (opaque) | Higher | Require rote learning |
| Hyperbole/Understatement | Higher | Less frequent, less exposure |
| Sarcasm/Irony | Highest | Requires second-order ToM |

## 2. IRONY AND SARCASM IN AUTISM

### 2.1 The Theoretical Framework: Why Irony is the Hardest Type

**Happe's 1993 Landmark Study**: Francesca Happe (1993) conducted the first experimental research making explicit connections between Theory of Mind (ToM) and figurative language comprehension in ASD. Her key finding:

- **First-order ToM** (understanding that someone has a belief) is sufficient for **metaphor** comprehension
- **Second-order ToM** (understanding that someone has a belief about someone else's belief) is necessary for **irony** comprehension
- Only individuals with ASD who passed second-order ToM tasks performed well on irony tasks

This maps onto relevance theory (Sperber & Wilson, 1986), which predicts that communication requiring higher levels of metarepresentation will be more challenging. Understanding irony requires recognizing that:
1. The speaker said X
2. The speaker doesn't believe X
3. The speaker intends the listener to recognize they don't believe X
4. The speaker has an attitude (usually negative/mocking) toward the proposition they're echoing

**Source**: [Happe 1993](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8243028/)

### 2.2 Echoic Mention Theory and Irony

Under relevance theory, irony involves "echoic mention" -- the speaker echoes a previous utterance, expectation, or norm to express an attitude toward it. Research shows:

- Irony is more easily comprehended when the speaker **explicitly echoes** a previous statement vs. when the allusion is implicit
- Children aged 6-10 were more likely to detect irony when the statement explicitly echoed a prior utterance
- This has direct implications for autistic individuals: irony is easier to detect when the "echo" is obvious

**Source**: [Echoic Mention Theory Research](https://escholarship.org/content/qt624931c2/qt624931c2.pdf)

### 2.3 The Role of Prosody in Irony Detection

Research on the neural basis of irony comprehension in autistic children (Wang et al., 2006, published in *Brain*) found:

- Children with ASD were **less accurate** than TD children at interpreting communicative intent behind ironic remarks
- ASD children showed difficulty utilizing **both prosodic and contextual cues**
- **Neuroimaging** revealed that children with ASD showed **significantly greater activity** in the right inferior frontal gyrus and bilateral temporal regions compared to TD children, suggesting more effortful processing
- The increased neural activity fell within the same network recruited by TD children, suggesting the same processes but at greater cost

**Critical point for learners**: Autistic individuals may process irony using the same mechanisms as neurotypical people but with significantly more cognitive effort. This is not a qualitative difference but a quantitative one in processing demands.

**Source**: [Wang et al. (2006) - Neural basis of irony comprehension](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3713234/)

### 2.4 The Atypical Pattern of Irony Comprehension

Research specifically on the atypical pattern of irony comprehension in autistic children found:

- Autistic participants detected ironic intended meaning **to a lesser extent** than TD controls
- They relied on the **same cues** (context incongruence more than acoustical or visual cues)
- However, the ASD group performed significantly poorer and did not reliably leverage either cue type
- English-speaking children with ASD are specifically **less capable of using prosodic cues** (such as intonation) for irony comprehension

**Source**: [Atypical Pattern of Irony Comprehension](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/applied-psycholinguistics/article/atypical-pattern-of-irony-comprehension-in-autistic-children/B831099748711E51CAF93EC4935D0C0F)

### 2.5 What Helps: Teaching Sarcasm Detection

**Persicke et al. (2013)** evaluated a training package using rules and "in vivo" multiple exemplar training to teach three children with autism (ages 6-7) to detect and respond appropriately to sarcastic statements.

**Key findings:**
- The training package was **effective**
- **Generalization was obtained** across novel exemplars, settings, and people
- Participants had prior training in prerequisites: prosody, body language, facial expressions, physical context, conversational audience, desires, emotions, and sensory perspective-taking
- The approach used **Relational Frame Theory (RFT)**, treating the ability to distinguish sarcastic from sincere comments as a learned relational skill

**Prerequisites for sarcasm detection training:**
1. Understanding that tone of voice carries meaning
2. Reading facial expressions and body language
3. Understanding context (what just happened)
4. Recognizing that speakers can say the opposite of what they mean
5. Understanding that sarcasm expresses an attitude (often frustration, amusement, or criticism)

**Source**: [Persicke et al. (2013)](https://dornsife.usc.edu/aba/wp-content/uploads/sites/130/2023/10/Persicke2013Sarcasm.pdf)

## 3. HUMOR AND AUTISM

### 3.1 Challenging the "Humorlessness" Stereotype

The stereotype that autistic people "don't get" or "don't have" humor is substantially inaccurate. Recent research systematically challenges this view:

**2025 Benign Violation Theory Study**: A study with 38 autistic and 462 non-autistic participants found:
- **No overall differences** in humor appreciation between groups -- both rated jokes as funnier than other texts
- Autistic participants showed a **more pronounced "joke facilitation effect"** with faster reading times for jokes
- **Restricted interests and repetitive behavior** (often characterized as deficits) had **significant positive effects** on humor appreciation, violation appraisal, and benign appraisal
- The gap in sharing humor between autistic and non-autistic individuals may be driven by social communication differences, not appreciation differences

**Source**: [Benign Violation Theory - Autism 2025](https://www.emerald.com/aia/article-abstract/doi/10.1108/AIA-02-2025-0009/1271963/Benign-violation-theory-of-humor-appreciation-in)

**Humor in ASD (2024/2025)**: Research explicitly separating humor appreciation from text comprehension found:
- When comprehension demands are controlled for, **autistic individuals appreciate humor similarly to neurotypical individuals**
- The distinction between comprehension (cognitive) and appreciation (emotional/affective) is critical
- Appreciation is not necessarily indicative of comprehension and may be somewhat independent from it

**Source**: [Humor in ASD: Differentiation](https://www.degruyterbrill.com/document/doi/10.1515/humor-2024-0068/html)

### 3.2 Types of Humor: What Maps to Autistic Strengths vs. Challenges

**Humor types that may map to autistic STRENGTHS:**

1. **Wordplay and puns**: Pattern-based, relying on recognizing multiple meanings. Puns challenge the brain to hold multiple meanings simultaneously -- a skill linked to pattern recognition. Knock-knock jokes with their predictable format and reliance on wordplay can be particularly accessible
2. **Observational humor**: Pointing out inconsistencies, arbitrary social norms, or illogical patterns in neurotypical behavior. Many autistic comedians excel here
3. **Absurdist/nonsense humor**: Students with autism felt **greater enjoyment** reading nonsense jokes than TD students, despite lower comprehension scores for incongruity-resolution jokes
4. **Info-dumping humor**: Sharing extensive knowledge about special interests in humorous contexts
5. **Dry/deadpan humor**: Literal delivery that creates humor through understatement or unexpected precision

**Humor types that may be MORE CHALLENGING:**

1. **Sarcasm and irony-based humor**: Requires second-order ToM, detecting tone, understanding social context
2. **Teasing and banter**: Requires distinguishing friendly ribbing from genuine criticism -- a distinction between "laughing with" vs. "laughing at" that can be very difficult
3. **Social/referential humor**: Jokes that depend on shared social knowledge, in-group references, or current cultural context
4. **Humor involving ambiguous social situations**: Where the humor depends on understanding what's socially expected vs. what happened
5. **Non-verbal humor in cartoons**: Studies show impaired understanding of humor in non-verbal cartoons and pure auditory stimuli

### 3.3 Research on Humor Comprehension, Appreciation, and Style

**Wu et al. (2014)** studied humor in high school students with autism:
- Students with autism comprehended nonsense jokes and incongruity-resolution jokes **less well** than controls
- But they felt **greater enjoyment** when reading nonsense jokes
- Tendencies toward affiliative, self-enhancing, and self-defeating humor were **lower** than controls
- Only aggressive humor tendency was **equal** between groups
- Preference for nonsense jokes featuring less logical reasoning and using homophones for double-meaning

**Source**: [Wu et al. - Humor in High School Students with Autism](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1750946714001561)

**Context enhances humor comprehension**: Research shows that children with autism can use verbal context to recognize verbal humor, and their appreciation of riddles improved significantly when context was provided.

**Source**: [Role of Context in Verbal Humor Processing](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0022096521000849)

### 3.4 Laughter and Humor Perception

A 2024 study published in *Scientific Reports* found remarkable differences in how autistic adults perceive laughter:

- Autistic adults rated **posed laughter as more authentic** and emotionally arousing than non-autistic adults, perceiving it as similar to genuine laughter
- Both groups could differentiate between genuine and posed laughter, but the distinction was less marked for autistic individuals
- Autistic adults reported **laughing less**, deriving **less enjoyment** from laughter, and experiencing **difficulty understanding the social meaning** of other people's laughter
- However, autistic adults reported **using laughter socially as often** as non-autistic adults
- Autistic children tend to laugh genuinely when they find something funny rather than using laughter as a social cue ("social laughter" is less common)

**Source**: [Autistic adults perceive laughter differently (2024)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-56903-8)

### 3.5 Autistic Humor Production and Community Humor

**Autistic comedians demonstrate the production side:**
- **Hannah Gadsby** (diagnosed 2016): Won Emmy and Peabody awards for *Nanette*. Their comedy blends observational humor with personal storytelling, using autistic perspectives to illuminate societal issues. Gadsby is the first openly autistic comedian to achieve global fame with three Netflix specials
- **Fern Brady**: Scottish comedian who has written and performed about autism extensively
- The emergence of multiple openly autistic comedians directly counters the "no sense of humor" stereotype

**Autistic community humor online:**
- Platforms like Twitter, Tumblr, TikTok, and Reddit have become hubs for autistic humor
- The "Autism Creature" (Yippee) became a viral meme embraced by the autistic community
- Common themes: info-dumping jokes, social norm observations, sensory experience humor, masking/burnout jokes, special interest intensity humor
- In-group humor creates community belonging and validates shared experiences
- Autistic memes often celebrate love of patterns and routines, turning "deficits" into humor

**Source**: [Autism Humor - Neurolaunch](https://neurolaunch.com/autism-humor/)

## 4. METAPHOR AND AUTISM

### 4.1 The Surprising Finding: Metaphor is NOT the Primary Difficulty

Despite early assumptions, metaphor comprehension is not necessarily the core figurative language difficulty in autism:

**Kasirer and Mashal (2014)** at Bar-Ilan University found:
- The ASD group understood **fewer conventional metaphors** than TD peers
- However, **no group differences** were observed on novel metaphor comprehension tasks
- Adults with ASD demonstrated **greater verbal creativity** than TD individuals, generating **more creative and novel metaphors** rather than relying on familiar ones
- Example: While TD participants generated conventional metaphors like "feeling lonely is like an abandoned dog," ASD participants generated creative ones like "feeling successful is like taking an elevator to heaven"

**Source**: [Kasirer & Mashal (2014)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4128218/)

### 4.2 Novel vs. Familiar Metaphors: A Critical Distinction

- **Conventional/familiar metaphors** function as fixed concepts stored in mental lexicon, relying heavily on vocabulary knowledge. These are essentially "dead metaphors" processed like idioms
- **Novel metaphors** are innovative nonliteral expressions with low familiarity requiring sophisticated pragmatic and cognitive operations
- Autistic individuals may show a **paradoxical pattern**: worse with conventional metaphors (which depend on cultural exposure and rote vocabulary) but comparable or even superior with novel metaphors (which tap analogical reasoning and creative thinking)

### 4.3 The Analogical Reasoning Connection

**Morsanyi et al. (2022)** directly linked metaphor comprehension with analogical reasoning:
- **Accuracy** for both verbal analogy and metaphor tasks was **very similar** across ASD and matched TD groups
- **Reaction times** were longer for the ASD group on both tasks
- Stronger correlations between verbal analogical reasoning and working memory capacity in the ASD group suggested **more effortful processing**
- A mediation analysis revealed that the inter-task correlation could be accounted for by **vocabulary knowledge** -- the primary common mechanism linking metaphor and analogy involves language skills

**Key insight**: Metaphor comprehension in autism is closely tied to analogical reasoning ability, which is often a relative strength in autism. The difficulty is not in mapping relationships but in the speed and effort of processing.

**Source**: [Morsanyi et al. (2022)](https://bpspsychub.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/bjop.12542)

### 4.4 Eye-Tracking Evidence: How Autistic Adults Process Metaphors Online

A 2024 eye-tracking study provided unprecedented insight into real-time metaphor processing:

- Adults with ASD comprehended metaphorical utterances **with the same accuracy as controls**
- However, they were approximately **800 milliseconds slower**
- Eye movement analysis revealed **significantly longer fixation times** on both target and distractor images
- Longer fixation on the **distractor image** (representing the literal interpretation) suggests **difficulty overcoming the literal interpretation**
- This provides direct evidence for a "literality bias" in online processing, even when offline accuracy is preserved

**What this means**: Autistic adults CAN understand metaphors just as well as neurotypical adults, but the process involves more cognitive effort and involves actively overriding an initial literal interpretation. This is not a deficit in understanding -- it's a different processing pathway that arrives at the same destination.

**Source**: [Online Metaphor Comprehension Eye-Tracking Study (2024)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10803-024-06562-5)

### 4.5 The Predictive Processing Account of Literalism

Vicente, Michel, and Petrolini (2023) proposed a novel explanation for literalism in autism grounded in predictive processing theory:

- Literalism results from an **atypical balance between predictions and error signals** in language processing
- This may make individuals **more uncertain about their own predictions**
- Such uncertainty is often resolved by resorting to the **safest interpretation -- the literal one**
- Low confidence in predictions escalates comprehension difficulties
- The model accounts for why autistic individuals can learn figurative meanings explicitly but may not spontaneously infer them

**Source**: [Literalism in Autistic People: Predictive Processing (2023)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s13164-023-00704-x)

### 4.6 Metaphor Production: An Autistic Strength?

**Kasirer and Mashal (2020)** further studied verbal and figural creativity in children with ASD:
- Children with ASD generated **a greater quantity of creative metaphors**
- They showed greater use of **cross-category insertions** on figural creativity tasks
- While producing fewer conventional metaphors, they produced **more creative and novel** ones
- This suggests that the autistic tendency toward unusual associations may be a **genuine creative advantage** in metaphor generation

**Source**: [Verbal and Figural Creativity in ASD Children (2020)](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2020.559238/full)

## 5. IDIOM PROCESSING IN AUTISM

### 5.1 The Meta-Analytic Picture for Idioms

The systematic review and meta-analysis by Kalandadze et al. specifically examining idiom and proverb processing found:
- 11 studies, 235 autistic and 224 TD individuals
- **Medium effect size** favoring TD over ASD groups
- A trend for **smaller group differences** among participants with higher verbal intelligence
- Age was **unrelated** to the magnitude of group differences

### 5.2 The Transparency Gradient

Not all idioms are equally difficult. The research consistently shows a transparency gradient:

**Transparent idioms** (where the figurative meaning can be derived from the literal components):
- "Spill the beans" -- you can see the connection between spilling/revealing and beans/information
- "Break the ice" -- the metaphorical connection to social warmth is visible
- These are **easier** for autistic individuals because the mapping between literal and figurative meaning provides a logical bridge

**Opaque idioms** (where the figurative meaning cannot be derived from literal components):
- "Kick the bucket" -- no logical connection between kicking buckets and dying
- "Let the cat out of the bag" -- the origin is obscure
- These essentially require **rote memorization** of arbitrary meaning associations
- These are harder for autistic individuals and must be learned as vocabulary items

### 5.3 Context Effects on Idiom Processing

Research consistently shows that context significantly aids idiom comprehension in autism:
- When contextual cues are available, autistic individuals' performance improves significantly
- In decontextualized conditions, idiom comprehension is much more impaired
- The "highly supported" vs. "highly spontaneous" pragmatic reasoning distinction is particularly relevant: the more explicit scaffolding and contextual cues available, the easier it becomes for autistic individuals to infer pragmatic meaning

### 5.4 Do Autistic Individuals Process Idioms via the Literal Route First?

Evidence suggests that autistic individuals may indeed process idioms through the literal route:
- The eye-tracking evidence from metaphor processing (showing longer fixation on literal distractors) likely extends to idioms
- The predictive processing account (Vicente et al., 2023) suggests lower confidence in predictions leads to literal-first processing
- However, highly familiar idioms may be stored as units, bypassing literal processing even in autistic individuals
- The dual-route model (literal analysis vs. direct retrieval) suggests that autistic individuals may rely more heavily on the literal analysis route for less-familiar expressions

### 5.5 The Relationship Between Idiom and Joke Comprehension

A 2023 study on adolescents with ASD found a relationship between semantic joke comprehension and idiom comprehension, suggesting shared underlying mechanisms -- specifically, the ability to access and integrate non-literal meanings. Adolescents with ASD processed semantic jokes at the same speed as TD peers but with lower accuracy.

**Source**: [Semantic Joke and Idiom Comprehension](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10296426/)

## 6. INTERVENTION AND TEACHING APPROACHES

### 6.1 What the Evidence Says Works

The 2025 scoping review "Opening New Worlds of Meaning" identified seven intervention studies targeting figurative language in ASD children/adolescents. Key findings across all approaches:

**Moderately effective interventions:**
- Readers theatre
- Story mapping
- Video self-modeling
- Thinking maps
- Explicit instruction

**Highly effective interventions:**
- RFT-based intervention with visual aids
- "X is like Y" with thinking maps and comparative strategy
- RFT multiple exemplar training with and without visual aids
- Contextual instruction with worksheet visual aids

**Universal finding**: In every study, participants improved on the specific figurative forms practiced. Generalization varied by context.

**Source**: [Opening New Worlds of Meaning (2025)](https://www.mdpi.com/2076-328X/15/11/1556)

### 6.2 Explicit Instruction: The Foundation

The most consistently effective approach leverages autistic strengths in rule-learning:

1. **Define the category explicitly**: "Sarcasm is when someone says the opposite of what they mean, usually to express frustration or amusement"
2. **Provide rules and decision trees**: "If someone says something positive about a clearly negative situation, they might be being sarcastic"
3. **Multiple exemplar training**: Expose learners to many different examples of the same figurative type
4. **Graduated complexity**: Start with obvious, well-cued examples and progressively reduce the cues
5. **Explicit contrast**: Show the difference between literal and figurative uses side by side

### 6.3 Visual Supports for Figurative Language

Visual supports have been consistently identified as effective scaffolds:
- **Thinking maps** that visually represent the mapping between literal and figurative meaning
- **Comic strip formats** that show the progression from context to figurative statement to intended meaning
- **Flowcharts** for deciding whether a statement is literal or figurative
- **Visual aids paired with RFT-based training** produced the highest effect sizes in intervention research

### 6.4 Context-Based vs. Dictionary-Based Teaching

Research supports **context-based approaches** over simple dictionary-based definitions:
- Context-based instruction helps learners understand **when and why** figurative language is used, not just what it means
- Dictionary approaches teach meaning but not pragmatic function
- The most effective programs combine both: explicit definition + rich contextual examples + practice in naturalistic settings

### 6.5 Technology-Assisted Approaches

Research on technology-based interventions is limited for figurative language specifically but shows promise:
- **Augmented reality (AR)** provides enhanced visual stimuli for understanding abstract language concepts
- **Video modeling** allows repeated exposure to naturalistic examples of figurative language
- **Interactive apps** can provide immediate feedback on figurative language interpretation
- **Text-based formats** (where figurative language is written rather than spoken) may be easier for autistic individuals, as studies show humor in writing is easier to detect than in speech

However, the evidence base remains limited: only five of 13 technology intervention studies showed clearly positive results for general language skills, with six showing mixed results.

### 6.6 Relational Frame Theory (RFT) Approaches

RFT-based interventions treat figurative language comprehension as a form of relational learning:
- The ability to understand that one thing can "stand for" or "be like" another is trained as an operant skill
- Multiple exemplar training builds flexible relational responding
- Both behavioral-analytic (RFT) and cognitive-linguistic approaches yield learning
- RFT approaches effectively taught both metaphors and sarcasm by treating the relations as operant skills

**Source**: [RFT Applications in Autism](https://apollobehavior.com/what-is-relational-frame-theory/)

## 7. THE PATTERN-BASED VS. SOCIAL-INFERENCE DISTINCTION

### 7.1 The Core Framework

A critical distinction for how autistic individuals process non-literal language emerges from the research:

**Pattern-Based Non-Literal Language** (may map to autistic strengths):
- **Puns and wordplay**: Depend on recognizing multiple meanings of words -- a pattern-recognition task
- **Novel metaphors**: Tap analogical reasoning, finding structural similarities across domains
- **Formulaic expressions**: Once learned, these are pattern-matched ("when someone says X, they mean Y")
- **Riddles**: Follow predictable structures with logical (if lateral) solutions
- **Absurdist humor**: Relies on incongruity detection, not social inference
- **Transparent idioms**: The mapping between literal and figurative is visible

**Social-Inference-Based Non-Literal Language** (typically harder for autistic individuals):
- **Sarcasm and verbal irony**: Requires reading speaker's attitude, second-order ToM
- **Teasing and banter**: Must distinguish friendly from hostile intent
- **Indirect requests/hints**: "It's cold in here" meaning "close the window"
- **White lies and politeness formulas**: Require understanding social motivation for untruth
- **Social humor**: Depends on shared cultural knowledge and social norms
- **Opaque idioms**: Arbitrary meaning requires cultural transmission

### 7.2 Indirect Speech Acts: A Middle Ground

Research on indirect speech acts in autism reveals a nuanced picture:
- Children with autism **CAN understand indirect requests** in structured, context-rich situations
- A 2015 study found that children with autism complied with indirect requests and could inhibit directive interpretations
- High-functioning autistic individuals can understand indirect and highly indirect requests (hints) as well as TD children
- However, **Theory of Mind skills enhanced TD children's understanding but not autistic children's**, suggesting different processing routes
- In **unstructured conditions**, performance was significantly depressed

**Key insight**: Autistic individuals may process indirect speech acts through pattern-matching and contextual analysis rather than through ToM-based inference, which works in structured situations but is less reliable in ambiguous ones.

**Source**: [Children with Autism Understand Indirect Speech Acts (2015)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4638355/)

### 7.3 Implications for Teaching Design

This distinction should fundamentally shape how a learning hub teaches different subtypes:

1. **For pattern-based types** (puns, metaphors, transparent idioms):
   - Teach the underlying patterns and rules
   - Leverage analogical reasoning strengths
   - Provide categorization systems
   - Build on existing pattern-recognition abilities
   
2. **For social-inference types** (sarcasm, irony, teasing):
   - Provide explicit rules and decision trees
   - Teach contextual cue recognition explicitly
   - Use graduated exposure from obvious to subtle
   - Acknowledge that processing will be slower and more effortful
   - Focus on "good enough" detection rather than instant recognition

### 7.4 The Autistic Community's Relationship with Non-Literal Language

The autistic community itself offers important perspectives:
- Many autistic adults report **developing sophisticated understanding** of figurative language through systematic learning
- The process is often described as learning a "second language" -- the rules are learned explicitly rather than absorbed implicitly
- Some autistic individuals become highly skilled at specific types of figurative language, particularly those amenable to pattern analysis
- The community distinguishes between "not understanding" figurative language and "processing it differently" -- a crucial reframing
- Autistic adults frequently report that their **literal-first processing** can coexist with strong figurative language comprehension, especially in written format where processing time is available

## 8. THE DOUBLE EMPATHY ANGLE

### 8.1 The Double Empathy Problem: Framework

Damian Milton (2012) proposed the "double empathy problem" -- the idea that communication difficulties between autistic and neurotypical people are **mutual**, not one-sided. This framework fundamentally reframes social communication difficulties in autism.

### 8.2 Crompton et al. (2020): The Empirical Evidence

Catherine Crompton and colleagues at the University of Edinburgh provided groundbreaking empirical support:

- Autistic people shared information with other autistic people **as effectively** as non-autistic people did with other non-autistic people
- In **mixed groups** (autistic + non-autistic), much less information was shared
- People in mixed groups experienced **lower rapport**
- This demonstrates that autistic people have the skills to communicate effectively -- the breakdown is specifically at the **cross-neurotype interface**

**Source**: [Crompton et al. (2020)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1362361320919286)

### 8.3 Do NT Listeners Fail to Understand Autistic Humor?

The double empathy framework predicts that neurotypical people should also have difficulty understanding autistic humor, and the evidence supports this:

- **Non-autistic observers** both detect and demonstrate the double empathy problem when evaluating interactions
- Autistic humor styles (highly observational, pattern-focused, absurdist, literalist-as-humor) may be perceived as "weird" or "not funny" by NT audiences unfamiliar with autistic perspectives
- The **"different sense of humor" vs. "no sense of humor"** distinction is critical: autistic people do not lack humor -- they have humor that is structured differently and draws from different sources

**Key evidence:**
- A 2024 study showed that non-autistic observers both **detected** the double empathy problem (noticing communication difficulties in mixed pairs) and **demonstrated** it (rating autistic communication less favorably)
- Autistic individuals report that NT people frequently **miss their jokes** or **interpret their humor as earnest statements**
- The "solitary laughter" phenomenon -- where autistic children laugh at things that don't typically make others laugh -- may represent genuinely finding humor in different patterns rather than "inappropriate" laughter

**Source**: [Non-autistic observers and the double empathy problem (2024)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11308351/)

### 8.4 Reframing for the Learning Hub

For a pragmatics teaching resource, the double empathy perspective suggests:

1. **Don't frame figurative language difficulty as a deficiency** -- frame it as a difference in processing that can be bridged through explicit learning
2. **Acknowledge that NT communication is also arbitrary and confusing** -- sarcasm, for instance, is an objectively strange way to communicate (saying the opposite of what you mean)
3. **Validate autistic humor** -- pattern-based, observational, and absurdist humor are legitimate and often sophisticated forms
4. **Teach bidirectionally** -- help autistic individuals understand NT figurative language AND help them recognize that NT people may not understand autistic communication styles
5. **Emphasize that learning NT pragmatics is a skill** -- like learning a second language, not fixing a deficit

## 9. COMPENSATION, MASKING, AND ADULT OUTCOMES

### 9.1 How Autistic Adults Compensate for Figurative Language Challenges

Research on camouflaging and compensation strategies reveals that many autistic adults develop sophisticated approaches:

**Compensation strategies include:**
- Building mental "libraries" of figurative expressions and their meanings
- Using contextual analysis rules (e.g., "if the literal meaning doesn't make sense in context, check for figurative meaning")
- Memorizing common sarcastic and ironic phrases
- Using working memory strategies to analyze non-literal possibilities
- Developing explicit "if-then" rules for common figurative situations
- Over-time learning through experience and explicit study

**However, compensation has costs:**
- Greater cognitive effort and processing time
- Mental fatigue, especially in fast-moving social situations
- Anxiety about missing figurative meanings
- Research links camouflaging with adverse mental health outcomes: stress, anxiety, depression

**Source**: [Camouflaging in Autism - Systematic Review](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0272735821001239)

### 9.2 The Developmental Trajectory

Young adult participants with autism performed at the level of control children (who were younger) but better than children with autism, suggesting:
- Figurative language ability **develops** throughout the lifespan in autism
- The gap may narrow with age, experience, and explicit learning
- Adults with strong language skills may reach typical levels of comprehension in structured settings
- However, real-time processing may remain slower and more effortful even when accuracy is high

## 10. KEY TAKEAWAYS FOR THE LEARNING HUB

### 10.1 What the Research Says Teaching Should Do

1. **Lead with the strength-based narrative**: Autistic people CAN and DO understand figurative language. The deficit model is wrong. What exists is different processing that can be systematically supported.

2. **Teach the taxonomy explicitly**: Name and categorize every type of figurative language. Autistic learners benefit from knowing the complete landscape and having a systematic framework.

3. **Leverage pattern recognition**: For puns, metaphors, transparent idioms, and wordplay, teach the underlying patterns. These are amenable to rule-based learning.

4. **Provide explicit rules for social-inference types**: For sarcasm, irony, and teasing, provide decision trees, contextual cue checklists, and explicit rules. Acknowledge that these are genuinely harder because they require real-time social inference.

5. **Use visual supports extensively**: Thinking maps, flowcharts, comparison tables, and visual representations of the literal-to-figurative mapping.

6. **Provide multiple examples (multiple exemplar training)**: Single examples don't generalize well. Provide many varied examples of each figurative type.

7. **Teach the "why"**: Don't just teach what figurative language means -- teach why people use it. Understanding the communicative function (humor, emphasis, politeness, social bonding) helps predict when it will occur.

8. **Validate autistic communication**: Acknowledge that literal communication is valid and that the need to decode figurative language reflects NT communication norms, not universal truth.

9. **Build from written to spoken**: Written figurative language is easier to process (more time, no prosody to decode). Start there and build toward spoken/real-time processing.

10. **Address the hierarchy**: Start with types that are easier (transparent idioms, conventional metaphors) and build toward harder types (sarcasm, irony, social humor).

### 10.2 What the Research Says NOT to Do

1. **Don't assume blanket inability** -- the heterogeneity is enormous
2. **Don't rely on implicit learning** -- what NT people absorb through exposure, autistic learners may need explicitly taught
3. **Don't test without teaching** -- provide the knowledge before testing comprehension
4. **Don't ignore context** -- always teach figurative language in context, not as isolated dictionary entries
5. **Don't conflate appreciation with comprehension** -- an autistic person may understand the meaning of a joke without finding it funny, or may find something funny without fully understanding the intended meaning
6. **Don't treat all figurative language as one monolithic category** -- the different types have very different cognitive demands and processing profiles
