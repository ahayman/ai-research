# Category 24: Identity and Stance in Interaction

## The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

---

## How to Read This Document

Most of the categories in this hub describe how meaning is **packaged** -- speech acts, implicature, politeness, hedging, register. This category describes something different: how **people** are packaged inside conversations. Every time you speak, you are not only doing something with words -- you are taking a **position** in the interaction, and you are placing the other person in a position too. Linguists call these positioning moves **stance**, and the relatively stable pattern that emerges from repeated stance moves over time is what they call **identity**.

The stance/identity distinction is load-bearing for the rest of the hub. It is what lets you tell the difference between:

- "You misread that joke" (a description of an event)
- "You are unable to read jokes" (an identity claim built from that event)

Or between:

- "I disagreed with you this morning" (a stance move in one conversation)
- "You are someone who always pushes back on me" (an identity assignment)

Or between:

- A colleague asking a careful question (stance: curious peer)
- A colleague asking a question that positions you as the confused one who needs help (stance assignment: struggling novice)

The manipulation sub-hub of this project depends heavily on this category. Many of the manipulation patterns documented there -- concern trolling, post-meltdown shaming, DARVO, diagnostic weaponization, compliance traps, performative allyship -- operate by smuggling **stance assignments** into conversations without announcing them. Once you can see stance as a layer, those moves become visible.

This document is a reference, not a tutorial. Read the sections you need. Cross-links to other categories and to the manipulation sub-hub pages are collected at the end.

## 1. What Stance Is

### 1.1 The Three-Part Move

**Stance** is a public act. It is not a mood, not a feeling, not a private attitude. It is something you **do** in an interaction, and others can see you doing it.

The most widely used account of stance is John W. Du Bois's **stance triangle** (Du Bois 2007). Du Bois proposed that every stance act has three simultaneous components:

1. **Evaluation**: the speaker evaluates some object (an idea, an event, a person, a proposition). "That decision was a mistake."
2. **Positioning**: the speaker positions themselves relative to that object. "I would not have done it."
3. **Alignment**: the speaker aligns (or disaligns) with another person who has already taken a stance on the same object. "I agree with you" / "I see it differently."

These three acts happen in one move. If you say "Yeah, that was a terrible decision," you have evaluated the object (terrible), positioned yourself (disapproving), and aligned with the previous speaker (yeah). A single short utterance performs all three parts of the triangle.

This matters because it means stance is **always relational**. You cannot take a stance in isolation. Every stance act places you in a network with an object and with other speakers, whether you intended that or not.

### 1.2 Stance Is Public

The key move Du Bois makes is this: stance is a **public, observable, consequential act**, not an internal state. When you take a stance, you commit yourself to a position that others can hold you to later. This is what makes stance a pragmatic concept rather than a psychological one.

For an autistic reader, this distinction matters. You may feel like you have not committed to anything, because you did not consciously endorse a position. But from a pragmatic point of view, if you produced the utterance, the stance was taken. The interpretive work other people do is not about your inner state -- it is about the public record of what you said.

## 2. Stance vs. Identity

### 2.1 The Central Distinction

| | **Stance** | **Identity** |
|---|---|---|
| **Timescale** | One move, one moment | Stable pattern over time |
| **Scope** | This conversation | Across conversations |
| **How it's made** | A single utterance | Repeated stance-taking |
| **How it's undone** | The next utterance | Slowly, if at all |
| **Who holds it** | Speaker performs it | Community validates it |

**Stance** is what is happening right now. "I am positioning myself as skeptical of this proposal."

**Identity** is what crystallizes when the same kinds of stance moves get repeated across enough conversations that people (including you) start treating them as a property of the person. "I am a skeptic."

Identity is not prior to stance. Identity is **built out of** stance over time. This is the core insight of Elinor Ochs's account of language socialization (Ochs 1993): children do not learn to express pre-existing identities -- they acquire identities **through** accumulated stance-taking in interaction. The same is true in adulthood. Who you are, pragmatically, is the residue of what you have publicly done in conversations.

Mary Bucholtz and Kira Hall's influential framework (Bucholtz & Hall 2005) makes this explicit in their **emergence principle**: identity is not the source of linguistic practice but its product.

### 2.2 Why the Distinction Matters

If identity were prior to stance -- if there were a "real you" that your utterances merely expressed -- then being mispositioned would be harmless. You would still be who you are, regardless of how others described you.

But because identity is **built out of** stance moves that are then validated (or refused) by the community, being repeatedly stance-assigned in a particular way can gradually become identity. This is the mechanism behind many of the manipulation patterns documented elsewhere in this project. If someone positions you as "the unreasonable one" often enough, and the people around you do not push back, that stance assignment starts to harden into a social identity you did not choose.

### 2.3 The Autistic Stakes

For autistic people, the stance/identity distinction is especially high-stakes because:

- **Default stance misreadings are common.** A flat affect delivery of a neutral fact often gets stance-read as "cold" or "dismissive," because non-autistic interlocutors expect a particular affective packaging (Milton 2012; Sheppard et al. 2016).
- **Identity consolidation from misread stances is asymmetric.** Other people's stance readings of you tend to stick, even when your own stance readings of them do not.
- **Masking is sustained stance performance.** The literature on autistic masking (Hull et al. 2017, 2019; Cook et al. 2021) is essentially a literature on what it costs to perform a non-native stance for long periods of time.

## 3. How Stance Is Performed

### 3.1 Linguistic Resources

Stance is performed through concrete, observable linguistic choices. The main categories linguists use (Biber & Finegan 1989; Jaffe 2009):

**Epistemic stance markers** -- how certain you are.
- "I know" / "I think" / "I guess" / "it seems" / "apparently" / "definitely" / "probably"

**Affective stance markers** -- how you feel about it.
- "Unfortunately" / "luckily" / "sadly" / "wonderfully" / "bizarrely"

**Evaluative stance markers** -- your judgment of it.
- "Correct" / "wrong" / "reasonable" / "absurd" / "fine" / "acceptable"

**Alignment markers** -- how you connect to the previous speaker.
- "Yeah" / "right" / "exactly" / "well" / "but" / "actually" / "I mean"

Any utterance longer than one word usually carries several stance markers at once. A sentence like "Well, I guess that's probably fine" is almost nothing but stance markers -- it says very little about the world, and a great deal about where the speaker is positioning themselves.

### 3.2 Non-Linguistic Resources

Stance is also performed through prosody, facial expression, gaze, posture, and timing. Kiesling's work on style (Kiesling 2009) argues that **style is stance made durable** -- the stable stylistic features a speaker uses are recognizable exactly because they are repeated stance moves.

Penelope Eckert's indexical field theory (Eckert 2008) adds a further layer: individual linguistic features (a particular vowel, a particular intonation contour, a particular word choice) carry not one but a **field of potential meanings**, and which meaning gets activated depends on the stance act the speaker is performing.

This matters for autistic readers because it means that your prosodic and affective defaults -- whatever they are -- are being read as stance moves, whether you intended them that way or not.

### 3.3 Default Stance vs. Marked Stance

A **default stance** is what you get when a speaker is not making a visible effort. For non-autistic English speakers, the default includes things like: moderate vocal warmth, periodic agreement markers, softened epistemics on sensitive topics, attention to the other speaker's face.

A **marked stance** is what you get when a speaker departs from the default in a way that signals something specific -- more distant, more intimate, more formal, more skeptical.

Autistic people often have different defaults. Flatter prosody, fewer softeners, more direct epistemic markers. These are not deficits -- they are different defaults. But because they differ from what non-autistic interlocutors expect, they often get **read as marked** when they were not intended that way. A neutral autistic "I disagree" gets stance-read as cold contradiction, because the listener expected a softened default.

## 4. Positioning: Stance Assignment

### 4.1 Positioning Theory

Bronwyn Davies and Rom Harré (Davies & Harré 1990; Harré & Van Langenhove 1999) developed **positioning theory** to describe how people are placed into roles through conversation. The central insight: positioning is mutual and ongoing. In every interaction, the speakers are simultaneously **positioning themselves** and **positioning each other**. These positions are not neutral -- they come with rights and obligations attached.

If I position you as the expert in a conversation, I have also given you the obligation to answer questions and the right to disagree with my framings. If I position you as the learner, I have given myself the right to explain and taken from you the right to redirect.

### 4.2 Stance Assignment

A **stance assignment** happens when one speaker positions another into a stance the other did not claim. Crucially, this is done **pragmatically, not explicitly**. The speaker does not say "I am now treating you as the unreasonable one." They simply talk **as if** the other person were already in that position, and wait to see whether the other person accepts it.

A concrete example from the manipulation sub-hub: concern trolling. The concern-troll adopts the stance of the worried-carer and assigns the target the stance of the worried-about-one. Neither move is announced. Both are performed by speaking in certain ways -- asking "are you sure you're okay?" in a particular tone, with a particular face, repeatedly -- until the target either accepts the worried-about-one position or does the hard work of refusing it.

Stance assignments are hard to refuse for three reasons:

1. **They are implicit.** Refusing requires first naming what is happening, which the assigner can then deny.
2. **They are pragmatically installed.** The longer the conversation runs on the assigned footing, the harder it is to re-set.
3. **They have an audience.** Other people present will often accept the assignment at face value, making refusal look like protesting-too-much.

### 4.3 Positional Asymmetry

Not all positioners are equal. When one party has more institutional, social, or discursive power, their positioning moves are stickier. A manager's stance toward an employee, a clinician's stance toward a patient, a parent's stance toward an adult child -- these are not symmetric with the reverse direction.

This is the pragmatic substrate under many of the institutional patterns in the manipulation sub-hub: legal venues, medical settings, educational settings, social services, and workplaces all contain actors with formal authority to position individuals, and those positions then carry consequences that the person positioned has limited means to reverse.

## 5. Stance Erosion and Stance Attacks

### 5.1 Event-to-Identity Drift

The most common stance attack is also the simplest: **take an event and redescribe it as an identity**.

- "You had a meltdown" → "You are unstable"
- "You disagreed with me that time" → "You are disagreeable"
- "You asked for accommodations" → "You are high-maintenance"
- "You spoke directly in that meeting" → "You are aggressive"

In each pair, the first sentence describes a discrete event that happened in one place at one time. The second sentence claims a stable property of the person. The drift is accomplished by the same grammatical structure that lets English speakers turn verbs into adjectives and then into nouns -- "had a meltdown" → "meltdown-prone" → "unstable."

This drift is the pragmatic engine behind the post-meltdown shaming pattern and the emotional invalidation double bind. Both patterns work by taking a single affective event and treating it as diagnostic of who the person is.

### 5.2 Credibility Discount as Stance Assignment

Miranda Fricker's work on **testimonial injustice** (Fricker 2007) provides the vocabulary for one of the most consequential stance assignments: being positioned as an **unreliable knower**.

When a speaker is assigned the stance of unreliable narrator, their testimony is discounted not because of its content but because of who is delivering it. Fricker's concept is philosophical, but its mechanism is pragmatic: credibility discount is accomplished through the same kinds of stance moves as any other positioning act -- through tone, through framing, through what gets taken up and what gets quietly dropped.

For autistic people, credibility discount is a particularly pervasive stance assignment. Lim, Young and Brewer's work on autistic credibility (Lim, Young & Brewer 2022) and Pearson and colleagues' research on autistic victimization (Pearson, Rees & Forster 2022; Pearson et al. 2023) both document the pattern: autistic speakers are routinely assigned the stance of unreliable narrator regardless of the accuracy or detail of what they report.

### 5.3 Cumulative Stance Erosion

In long-running relationships -- especially in coercive control settings -- stance attacks accumulate. Each individual move is small enough to deny. But over months and years, the accumulated effect is that the autistic partner no longer has a stance position in the relationship at all. They speak only in the positions the other person allows.

This is what the intimate partner violence literature calls coercive control (Stark 2007), and its pragmatic dimension is stance erosion. The target is not silenced with a single attack; their capacity to take stances at all is gradually worn down. This matches the pattern documented in the manipulation sub-hub's page on intimate partner relationships.

### 5.4 Unilateral Repositioning

A stance attack can also happen in a single move if the positioner has enough power: the **unilateral reposition**. A clinician writes "non-compliant" in the chart. A supervisor writes "not a culture fit" in the review. A family member tells the rest of the family you have "become so difficult since the diagnosis." Each is a one-move reposition that the target has no practical means to appeal. The pragmatic act and the institutional consequence are bundled.

## 6. Recovery and Stance Reclamation

### 6.1 What Refusal Can and Cannot Do

You can refuse a stance assignment. This is a real pragmatic move, and it often works. But refusal is not a magic spell. It is a move that depends on:

- **Having standing to refuse.** If the positioner has institutional authority over you, your refusal may be recorded as further evidence for their position.
- **Audience pickup.** If other people present accept the refusal, it holds. If they do not, the refusal can be re-absorbed as further evidence of the assigned position.
- **Repetition.** A single refusal rarely sticks. Stance positions consolidate through repetition, and so does refusal.

Sections of the manipulation sub-hub (particularly the pattern pages on darvo, gaslighting, compliance trap, and concern trolling) document specific refusal strategies. This category provides the mechanism those strategies rely on.

### 6.2 The Three-Move Refusal

A commonly effective refusal pattern has three parts:

1. **Name the event, not the identity.** "In that meeting, I disagreed with the timeline" (event). This blocks the drift to "you are disagreeable."
2. **Refuse the position explicitly.** "I do not accept the framing that disagreeing makes me a difficult employee."
3. **Re-position yourself.** "I am someone who raises concerns when I see risks. That is the role I am in."

The three moves together turn an implicit stance assignment into an explicit one, which is then easier to argue with. The cost is that naming makes the conflict overt, and in some settings this is worse than accepting the assignment.

### 6.3 Structural Limits

Stance reclamation has real limits. The limits are structural, not personal. When one party has formal authority to write records, make decisions, or exclude you from the conversation, the pragmatic category of "stance refusal" cannot fully compensate. This is why the manipulation sub-hub emphasizes documentation, external evidence, reality-check networks, and professional support -- all of which are ways of bringing external witnesses into the positioning work, because within-conversation refusal alone is not enough.

### 6.4 Self-Positioning

The most reliable reclamation work is done outside the threatening conversation: in your own accounts of yourself, in autistic community spaces, in relationships with people who are willing to take your own self-positioning seriously. Botha and colleagues' qualitative work on claimed autistic identity ("Autism is me," Botha et al. 2022) describes this self-positioning work in detail. The identity is not discovered -- it is claimed and maintained through repeated stance acts, including the stance act of naming it.

## 7. Stance Across Categories

### 7.1 Internal Cross-Links

- **Category 1 (Speech Acts)**: every speech act is also a stance move. Asserting is an epistemic stance; asking is a stance of not-knowing; promising is a stance of commitment.
- **Category 3 (Politeness)**: face-work is stance-work. Positive politeness is a stance of solidarity; negative politeness is a stance of non-imposition.
- **Category 6 (Hedging)**: hedges are epistemic stance markers. "I think" is a stance move before it is anything else.
- **Category 7 (Register)**: register selection is a stance move at the scale of the whole conversation.
- **Category 16 (Common Ground)**: stance assignments are often installed by pretending something is in common ground when it is not.
- **Category 22 (Social Hierarchy)**: hierarchy sets the positional asymmetries that make stance assignments stickier or looser.
