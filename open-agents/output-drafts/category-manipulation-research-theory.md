# Pragmatic Manipulation and Self-Protection: Theoretical Research Document

## Category Purpose

This document provides the theoretical and empirical research foundation for the Pragmatic Manipulation and Self-Protection category of the English Pragmatics Learning Hub. This is the highest-stakes category in the hub. Its purpose is not to make autistic people scared, but to make manipulation patterns **visible** so they can be recognized and resisted.

The central thesis: autistic individuals don't just struggle to learn the pragmatic system -- their struggles are actively **weaponized** against them. The pragmatic asymmetry between autistic and neurotypical communication isn't just a gap to bridge; it's a vulnerability that abusers, institutions, and social systems exploit.

## Part 1: Theoretical Frameworks for Pragmatic Manipulation

### 1.1 Gaslighting: Origins and Philosophical Analysis

#### Historical Origin

The term "gaslighting" derives from Patrick Hamilton's 1938 stage play *Gas Light* and its more widely known 1944 film adaptation directed by George Cukor, starring Ingrid Bergman. In the story, a husband (Gregory) manipulates his wife (Paula) into believing she is mentally unstable -- dimming the gas lights in their home and denying any change, moving objects and accusing her of forgetfulness -- until she begins to doubt her own perception of reality. The term was first attached to the psychological phenomenon by anthropologist Anthony Wallace in 1961 (*Culture & Personality*). Merriam-Webster named "gaslighting" its 2022 Word of the Year, defining it as "psychological manipulation" designed to make someone question their "perception of reality," leading to "dependence on the perpetrator."

**Sources:**
- Hamilton, P. (1938). *Gas Light* (stage play).
- Cukor, G. (Director). (1944). *Gaslight* [Film].
- Wallace, A. F. C. (1961). *Culture and Personality*.
- Merriam-Webster. (2022). Word of the Year: Gaslighting.

#### Abramson (2014): The Non-Epistemic Account

Kate Abramson's "Turning up the Lights on Gaslighting" (*Philosophical Perspectives*, 2014) provides one of the earliest rigorous philosophical analyses. Abramson argues that a necessary condition for gaslighting is that the gaslighter has the goal of **destroying the target's standing to issue challenges**. She rejects the idea that the gaslighter claims special epistemic status or that mere lying defines gaslighting. Instead, she argues gaslighters use assertions and manipulative threats, and the victim has "motive to assent" only because of personal or professional harm threatened.

Critically, Abramson's account does *not* frame gaslighting as primarily epistemic -- she argues it works through emotional and social manipulation rather than epistemic claims. This has been contested by subsequent scholars.

**Source:** Abramson, K. (2014). Turning up the lights on gaslighting. *Philosophical Perspectives*, 28, 1-30.

#### Stark (2019): Gaslighting, Misogyny, and Psychological Oppression

Cynthia A. Stark's 2019 analysis in *The Monist* develops a specifically feminist account of gaslighting. Stark defines **manipulative gaslighting** as an act of sidestepping evidence supporting the victim's testimony and labeling the victim as psychologically or cognitively impaired. She identifies two core tactics:

1. **Sidestepping**: Dodging evidence that supports the victim's testimony
2. **Displacing**: Attributing cognitive or characterological defects to the victim

Stark argues for three further claims:
- That manipulative gaslighting is a **method of enacting misogyny**
- That it is often a **collective phenomenon**
- That, as collective, it qualifies as a **mode of psychological oppression**

**Relevance to autism**: Stark's framework maps directly onto autistic experience. When someone says "You're just being autistic about this," they are performing both sidestepping (ignoring the content of the autistic person's testimony) and displacing (attributing a cognitive defect -- autism itself -- as the reason the testimony should be dismissed). The displacement tactic is structurally identical whether the attributed "defect" is femininity or autism.

**Source:** Stark, C. A. (2019). Gaslighting, misogyny, and psychological oppression. *The Monist*, 102(2), 221-235.

#### Sweet (2019): The Sociology of Gaslighting

Paige L. Sweet's groundbreaking 2019 article in *American Sociological Review* reframes gaslighting as a fundamentally **sociological** rather than purely psychological phenomenon. Sweet argues that gaslighting is consequential when perpetrators **mobilize stereotypes and structural and institutional inequalities** against victims to manipulate their realities.

Key mechanisms identified by Sweet:
- Abusers mobilize **gendered stereotypes** against victims
- They exploit **structural vulnerabilities** related to race, nationality, and sexuality
- They leverage **institutional inequalities** to erode victims' realities
- "Micro tactics of abuse are situated in macro conditions of inequality"

Sweet demonstrates that gaslighting works precisely *because* it draws on existing power structures -- the abuser doesn't need to be especially clever if society already provides templates for discrediting their target.

**Relevance to autism**: Sweet's structural account is essential because it explains why autistic people are so vulnerable to gaslighting. Society already provides the stereotypes ("autistic people can't read social situations," "autistic people are unreliable narrators of their own experience"), the structural vulnerabilities (dependence on support systems, employment precarity), and the institutional inequalities (medical systems that discount autistic testimony, legal systems that misread autistic behavior). An abuser targeting an autistic person doesn't need to build a gaslighting apparatus from scratch -- **society has already built it for them**.

**Source:** Sweet, P. L. (2019). The sociology of gaslighting. *American Sociological Review*, 84(5), 851-875.

#### How Gaslighting Functions Pragmatically

From a pragmatic linguistics perspective, gaslighting operates through several specific mechanisms:

1. **Common ground corruption**: Gaslighting systematically manipulates the shared knowledge, beliefs, and assumptions that conversation participants rely on (Stalnaker's "common ground"). The gaslighter denies previously established facts, introduces false presuppositions, and forces the target to question their own contributions to the common ground.

2. **Presupposition manipulation**: Gaslighters embed false information as presuppositions -- treating contested claims as already-established background truths. Research on "nested presuppositions" identifies these as inherently manipulative pragmatic structures that smuggle information into discourse without asserting it (making it harder to challenge).

3. **Implicature exploitation**: Manipulators exploit conversational implicatures, using indirect speech acts to convey threats or invalidation while maintaining plausible deniability. Research identifies manipulation as "parasitic" on the Gricean maxims -- it works by exploiting the cooperative assumptions that underlie normal conversation.

4. **Reality-rewriting**: Through persistent denial, misdirection, contradiction, and lying, the gaslighter gradually replaces the target's reality model with the gaslighter's preferred version.

**Sources:**
- Masia, V. (2020). *The Manipulative Disguise of Truth: Tricks and threats of implicit communication*. John Benjamins.
- Research on nested presuppositions as manipulative pragmatic structures (*Studies in Communication Sciences*, 2019).

### 1.2 Epistemic Injustice (Miranda Fricker, 2007)

Miranda Fricker's landmark 2007 book *Epistemic Injustice: Power and the Ethics of Knowing* identifies two fundamental forms of injustice done to people **in their capacity as knowers**:

#### Testimonial Injustice

Testimonial injustice occurs when **prejudice causes a hearer to give a deflated level of credibility to a speaker's testimony**. The credibility deflation is driven by identity prejudice -- stereotyped assumptions about social groups. Fricker describes this as operating through "a socially situated capacity to control others' actions" that manifests in "patterns of incredulity, misinterpretation, silencing."

**Direct relevance to autistic experience**: Autistic people's testimony is routinely discounted because of **how they deliver it** -- flat affect, different prosody, directness, lack of expected hedging or emotional display. The content of what they say may be accurate, insightful, and important, but the pragmatic packaging triggers identity prejudice that deflates their credibility. This is testimonial injustice operating through pragmatic channels.

Research has identified **five specific types of epistemic injustice** affecting autistic people (Catala, Faucher & Poirier, 2021):
1. **Systematic testimonial injustice**: Routine discounting of autistic testimony
2. **Preemptive testimonial injustice (quieting)**: Not even soliciting autistic people's views
3. **Testimonial smothering**: Autistic people self-censoring because they anticipate disbelief
4. **Contributory hermeneutical injustice**: Using frameworks that distort autistic experience
5. **Expressive hermeneutical injustice**: Penalizing autistic modes of expression

#### Hermeneutical Injustice

Hermeneutical injustice occurs when **a gap in collective interpretive resources unfairly disadvantages someone in understanding their own experiences**. This is structural injustice -- it exists in the collective vocabulary, concepts, and frameworks available to a society.

**Direct relevance to autistic experience**: There is "a key asymmetry between neurotypical people and autistic people when it comes to having the linguistic means to describe and express their experiences and sensibilities" (Dinishak, 2021). Neurotypical language has evolved to signify neurotypical thoughts and feelings. Autistic experiences often **lack words** in neurotypical frameworks:

- "I can't process right now" has no precise NT equivalent
- Sensory overwhelm is often described only through NT concepts of "sensitivity" (implying fragility)
- Autistic burnout was only recently named despite being a longstanding experience
- Masking/camouflaging lacked terminology until the autistic community created it

Dinishak (2021) argues that scientific and popular knowledge about autism has been "produced mostly without the perspectives of autistic people, as they have often been deemed less capable authors of their own experience." This results in a lack of representativeness and the circulation of ideas about autism that are "misrepresentative and harmful." Importantly, "autistic experiences for which words and concepts were lacking did actually exist in the absence of words and concepts to capture them" -- the experiences were real; the language was missing.

Chapman and Carel (2022) extend this analysis, arguing that autistic thriving has been "rendered both invisible and unthinkable by interlocking forms of testimonial and hermeneutical injustice." They identify an epistemic catch-22: **one can be recognized as autistic or as thriving, but not both** -- because mainstream conceptions of the good life have excluded autistic manifestations of happiness and flourishing.

**Sources:**
- Fricker, M. (2007). *Epistemic Injustice: Power and the Ethics of Knowing*. Oxford University Press.
- Catala, A., Faucher, L. & Poirier, P. (2021). Autism, epistemic injustice, and epistemic disablement: a relational account of epistemic agency. *Synthese*, 199, 9013-9039.
- Dinishak, J. (2021). Autistic autobiography and hermeneutical injustice. *Metaphilosophy*, 52, 556-569.
- Chapman, R. & Carel, H. (2022). Neurodiversity, epistemic injustice, and the good human life. *Journal of Social Philosophy*, 53(4), 614-631.
- Uisma, A.-M., Virkki, T. & Ylilahti, M. (2026). Misunderstood, minimised, misrepresented: Autistic young adults' experiences of epistemic injustices in healthcare interactions around autism. *Sociology of Health & Illness*.
- Nesic, J. (2023). Affording autistic persons epistemic justice. *PhilArchive*.

### 1.3 Coercive Control Theory (Evan Stark, 2007)

Evan Stark's 2007 book *Coercive Control: How Men Entrap Women in Personal Life* reframed domestic abuse as a **pattern of domination** rather than a series of isolated violent incidents. Stark defines coercive control as "a pattern of domination that includes strategies to isolate, exploit, and regulate the everyday behavior of victims."

Key components:
- **Coercion**: "The use of force or threats to compel or dispel a particular response"
- **Control**: "Structural forms of deprivation, exploitation, and command that compel obedience indirectly"
- When combined, these create a **"condition of unfreedom"** experienced as entrapment

Stark details how abusers achieve control through indirect tactics: isolation, deprivation, exploitation, and **microregulation** -- controlling dress, speech, sexual activity, and work. The abuser doesn't need to be violent in every interaction; the *pattern* of control is what creates the trap.

**How pragmatic norms become tools of coercive control**: When someone says "If you could just learn to say it nicely, people would listen to you," they are performing a coercive control move. The message is: **your content doesn't matter until your packaging meets my standards**. For an autistic person whose "natural" pragmatic style is already outside NT norms, this creates an impossible compliance requirement that functions as ongoing control -- they can never perform "correctly" enough, so they are perpetually at fault for their own mistreatment.

**Relevance to autistic experience**: Research has confirmed that autistic individuals are at heightened risk of coercive control in intimate relationships. Abusive partners exploit autistic traits: social isolation, communication differences, routine dependence, and caregiver dependency become vectors for control. Autistic people can be "isolated by their own neurodivergence, and abusive partners can easily isolate them further from social supports and protective friends." Communication barriers make it harder for autistic people to "report abuse or explain their experiences to others."

**Sources:**
- Stark, E. (2007). *Coercive Control: How Men Entrap Women in Personal Life*. Oxford University Press.
- Neurodiverse Connection. (2023). Autism, grooming and coercive control: an introduction and overview.
- Somerset Domestic Abuse. Neurodiversity and domestic abuse: understanding the hidden risks.
- BWJP. Neurodiversity and the legal system's response to domestic violence.

### 1.4 DARVO (Jennifer Freyd, 1997)

Jennifer Freyd's 1997 concept of DARVO (**Deny, Attack, Reverse Victim and Offender**) describes a common reaction pattern displayed by perpetrators when held accountable. The acronym breaks down as:

1. **Deny**: The perpetrator denies the harm or abuse ever took place
2. **Attack**: When confronted with evidence, the perpetrator attacks the person they harmed
3. **Reverse Victim and Offender**: The perpetrator claims they are the actual victim

Research by Harsey, Zurbriggen & Freyd (2017) found that DARVO is empirically effective: participants exposed to DARVO responses **perceived victims as less believable, more responsible for the violence, and more abusive**, while perceiving perpetrators as **less abusive and less responsible**. DARVO works not because it's logically sound, but because it exploits cognitive biases and social scripts.

#### How DARVO Exploits Specific Pragmatic Systems

Each step of DARVO maps onto distinct pragmatic manipulation mechanisms:

**Deny = Common Ground Corruption (Gaslighting)**
The denial stage corrupts shared reality. "That never happened." "You're remembering it wrong." "I never said that." This attacks the common ground -- the shared knowledge base that conversation depends on. For autistic people who may already doubt their social perceptions ("Maybe I did misread it..."), this is devastatingly effective.

**Attack = Ad Hominem, Often Targeting Autistic Traits**
The attack stage typically involves ad hominem arguments -- attacking the person rather than addressing their claims. For autistic people, these attacks specifically target autistic traits:
- "You always overreact to everything" (targeting sensory sensitivity)
- "You're reading too much into it" (targeting pattern recognition / literal interpretation)
- "You don't understand social situations" (targeting social cognition differences)
- "That's just your autism talking" (diagnostic weaponization)

**Reverse = Tu Quoque, Appeal to Emotion, Victim Narrative**
The reversal exploits the tu quoque fallacy (deflecting by accusing the accuser) and appeal to emotion. The perpetrator constructs a victim narrative: "You're the one who's always difficult." "I'm the one suffering here." "Everyone else agrees with me." This is especially effective against autistic people because they may lack the pragmatic fluency to quickly counter a false narrative, and because social witnesses often side with the person performing distress more "convincingly."

**Sources:**
- Freyd, J. J. (1997). Violations of power, adaptive blindness, and betrayal trauma theory. *Feminism & Psychology*, 7, 22-32.
- Harsey, S. J., Zurbriggen, E. L. & Freyd, J. J. (2017). Deny, attack, and reverse victim and offender (DARVO): What is the influence on perceived perpetrator and victim credibility? *Journal of Aggression, Maltreatment & Trauma*, 29(8), 897-916.
- DARVO entry, jjfreyd.com (Jennifer Freyd's research page).

### 1.5 Tone Policing as a Systemic Tactic

Tone policing is an ad hominem argument that **focuses on the emotion with which something is expressed rather than its factual or logical content** in order to dismiss a person's argument. It is a silencing tactic that derails substantive discussion by shifting attention from what was said to how it was said.

#### Distinction from Legitimate Feedback

Tone policing must be distinguished from legitimate, mutual feedback about communication style in a relationship of equals. The difference lies in:
- **Power dynamics**: Who benefits from the redirect? If addressing "tone" consistently serves to avoid addressing content that threatens the more powerful party, it's policing, not feedback.
- **Reciprocity**: Is the tone expectation applied equally? If one party's directness is "aggressive" but the other party's passive-aggression or sarcasm is "just how they communicate," there is a double standard.
- **Context**: Is the focus on tone used to avoid accountability? "You're being aggressive" as a response to "You promised to do X and didn't" is deflection, not communication coaching.

#### The Specific Vulnerability for Autistic People

Autistic people face a uniquely devastating version of tone policing because **their "natural" tone is already outside NT norms**. Several factors compound this:

1. **Flat affect**: Autistic people may not display expected emotional expressions, leading to accusations of not caring, being cold, or being "robotic." Research confirms that "autistic individuals may experience intense emotion yet display it through subtler facial shifts, limited eye contact, and verbal precision" -- the emotions are present, but the display doesn't match NT expectations.

2. **Prosodic differences**: Research from the University of Rochester and elsewhere documents that autistic individuals may have atypical intonation patterns that neurotypical listeners interpret as disinterest, rudeness, or oddness -- even when the speaker is deeply engaged.

3. **Directness**: Autistic communication tends toward literal, direct expression. In NT frameworks, this lack of hedging and softening is often read as aggressive, rude, or confrontational.

4. **The permanent target problem**: Because autistic prosody, affect, and directness are **stable traits** rather than choices, autistic people are perpetual targets for tone policing. They cannot simply "fix their tone" without engaging in exhausting masking -- and even then, the mask may slip. This means any interaction can be derailed by tone policing at any point, creating a permanent silencing mechanism.

As one advocacy source puts it: "Society has taken it upon itself to make arbitrary rules for how autistic people should communicate, and absolved itself of any responsibility to provide accommodation for what is a very real communication disability."

- NeuroLaunch. Autism and tone policing: Impact and strategies for inclusive communication.
- Autistic Science Person. (2021). Neurotypicals: Listen to our words, not our tone.
- Ollibean. Autism, accommodation, and double standards.

### 1.6 The Double Empathy Problem (Milton, 2012)

Damian Milton's 2012 theory of the "double empathy problem" provides essential framing for this entire category. The theory proposes that communication difficulties between autistic and non-autistic people arise from **bidirectional differences in communication style and social-cognitive characteristics**, not from an inherent deficit in autistic people.

Key insight: "Most autistic people struggle to understand and empathize with non-autistic people, whereas most non-autistic people also struggle to understand and empathize with autistic people." The breakdown is mutual, but it is **only pathologized in one direction**. Autistic people are diagnosed with a "communication disorder" while neurotypical people who fail to understand autistic communication are simply considered... normal.

This asymmetry in how the communication gap is framed has profound implications for manipulation:
- When an NT person misunderstands an autistic person, **it's the autistic person's fault** for having "impaired communication skills"
- When an autistic person misunderstands an NT person, **it's also the autistic person's fault** for having "processing difficulties"
- The autistic person is always the one who must adapt, accommodate, and mask

This framing creates a permanent vulnerability: **the autistic person is always presumed to be the one who got it wrong**, which means their testimony about interpersonal events (including abuse) is structurally discounted.

The double empathy problem has been supported by extensive research, including studies by Catherine Crompton, Morton Ann Gernsbacher, Elizabeth Pellicano, and Sue Fletcher-Watson.

**Sources:**
- Milton, D. (2012). On the ontological status of autism: the 'double empathy problem'. *Disability & Society*, 27(6), 883-887.
- Milton, D., Gurbuz, E. & Lopez, B. (2022). The 'double empathy problem': Ten years on. *Autism*, 26(8), 1901-1903.

## Part 2: How Pragmatic Asymmetry Is Weaponized Against Autistic People

### 2.1 "You Said It Wrong, So It Doesn't Count"

This is perhaps the most pervasive pattern. An autistic person raises a valid concern, makes a legitimate complaint, or provides accurate information -- but because the **pragmatic packaging** doesn't meet neurotypical expectations, the content is dismissed.

#### How it works pragmatically:

The autistic person's message violates expected politeness conventions (Brown & Levinson), register norms, or prosodic patterns. Instead of processing the propositional content, the neurotypical interlocutor processes the pragmatic "violations" and uses them to **invalidate the entire speech act**. The legitimate complaint becomes "rude." The accurate observation becomes "inappropriate." The reasonable request becomes "demanding."

#### Specific mechanisms:
- **Missing hedges**: "This is wrong" instead of "I wonder if maybe this might not be quite right?" The direct version is pragmatically identical in propositional content but violates NT softening norms, triggering face-threat responses.
- **Wrong register**: Using formal/precise language in casual settings or casual/direct language in formal settings
- **Unexpected prosody**: Flat intonation when NT listeners expect emotional modulation, or unexpected emphasis patterns
- **Absent performatives**: Not performing expected emotional displays (concern-face, sympathy-voice) that NT listeners use as sincerity markers

#### The double bind:
If the autistic person tries to add hedges, soften tone, and perform expected emotional displays (masking), the effort is exhausting and unsustainable, and when the mask slips, the "real" (unmasked) version is used to discredit previous communications. If they don't mask, their communications are perpetually discredited. **There is no available strategy that consistently works.**

### 2.2 Diagnostic Weaponization

Diagnostic weaponization occurs when **the autism diagnosis itself is used to invalidate an autistic person's perceptions, opinions, and experiences**. Common forms:

- "You're just being autistic about this"
- "That's your autism talking"
- "You're overthinking it" (pathologizing pattern recognition)
- "You're being too literal" (pathologizing accurate interpretation)
- "You don't understand social situations, so your read on this is wrong"
- "You always read too much into things"

#### How it functions as epistemic injustice:

Diagnostic weaponization is a specific form of testimonial injustice where the autism diagnosis serves as a **permanent credibility-deflating label**. Unlike other credibility-deflating attributes (being angry, being drunk, being confused), which are temporary states, an autism diagnosis is permanent. This means the autistic person's testimony can be deflated **at any time, about any topic**, simply by invoking their diagnosis.

The particularly insidious aspect: many of the traits used to dismiss autistic perceptions are actually cognitive strengths in different contexts. Pattern recognition, literal accuracy, sustained attention to detail, and resistance to social pressure are valuable -- except when they're used to identify problems that others don't want identified.

#### Relationship to gaslighting:

Diagnostic weaponization primes autistic people for gaslighting by establishing a baseline of self-doubt. If you've been told your entire life that your perceptions are unreliable ("you're being too literal," "you don't understand the social dynamics"), you're already halfway to accepting a gaslighter's version of reality.

Research on autistic youth and adults confirms that discrimination and stigma through invalidation are "significantly associated with more emotion dysregulation, higher suicidality, lower self-esteem, and lower quality of life" (PMC research, 2025).

### 2.3 Sensory Gaslighting

Sensory gaslighting involves telling autistic people their sensory experiences aren't real:
- "The lights aren't that bright"
- "It's not that loud"
- "You're being dramatic"
- "You're being overly sensitive"
- "Just tough it out"
- "You need to be more resilient"

Research confirms that up to 90% of autistic people are highly sensitive to at least one sensory modality (sound, sight, taste, smell, or touch). These are **neurologically real differences in sensory processing**, not psychological weaknesses or character flaws.

#### Why sensory gaslighting is foundational:

Sensory gaslighting is often the **first form of reality-denial** autistic people encounter, frequently beginning in childhood. When a child is told repeatedly that their sensory experience isn't real ("it's not that loud"), several things happen:

1. The child learns that their direct perception of reality is unreliable
2. The child learns to distrust their own sensory apparatus
3. The child learns that other people's perceptions override their own
4. The child develops a pattern of deferring to others' version of reality

This establishes vulnerability to broader gaslighting: "If I was wrong about the lights being too bright, maybe I'm wrong about this person being unkind to me. Maybe I'm wrong about what I heard them say. Maybe I'm wrong about what I remember."

### 2.4 Emotional Invalidation via Pragmatic Norms

Neurotypical communication relies heavily on prosodic and paralinguistic cues to assess emotional states. When autistic people express emotions through different channels or at different intensities, NT interlocutors frequently misread or invalidate those emotions:

- **"You don't seem upset, so it can't be that bad"**: Penalizing flat affect. Research confirms autistic people "may experience intense emotion yet display it through subtler facial shifts, limited eye contact, and verbal precision" (TherapyDen; MyAutismMind). The emotions are present; the display is different.

- **"If you were really sorry, you'd sound like it"**: Penalizing different prosodic expression. This demands performative emotional display as proof of sincerity, rather than accepting verbal content.

- **"You obviously don't care"**: Misreading autistic emotional expression as absence of emotion. Research shows neurotypical observers "misread flat affect as indifference or rudeness, creating painful social distance," and that this leads to "the common stereotype that autistics lack empathy."

- **"You're overreacting"**: The inverse -- when autistic people DO display strong emotions (meltdown, shutdown, intense distress), it's framed as disproportionate, dramatic, or attention-seeking.

#### The pragmatic trap:

Autistic people face a **pragmatic double bind** in emotional expression:
- Express emotions in autistic ways (direct verbal statement, flat affect) --> "You don't really feel that way"
- Express emotions in NT-expected ways (facial expression, prosodic cues, tears) --> Often impossible to perform on demand, or if performed through masking, unsustainable
- Express emotions intensely (meltdown, strong vocal response) --> "You're overreacting / being dramatic / being manipulative"

There is no available emotional expression mode that is consistently accepted as authentic.

**Sources:**
- Research on flat affect and autism (PMC articles on prosody, facial expression).
- University of Rochester research on speech prosody in autism.
- Misinterpretation of facial expressions of emotion in verbal adults with ASD (PMC, 2014).

### 2.5 The Compliance Trap

The compliance trap is captured in the statement: **"If you could just learn to [mask/perform/comply], people would treat you better."** This framing:

1. Makes pragmatic conformity the autistic person's **responsibility**
2. Frames any mistreatment as the autistic person's **fault** for failing to perform correctly
3. Positions the solution as **more masking** rather than changing how others respond
4. Creates an unfalsifiable standard: if compliance doesn't work, "you're not complying hard enough"

As one advocacy source summarizes the compliance culture trap: **"Perform until you break, and then be blamed for breaking."**

#### Historical context -- ABA and compliance training:

The compliance trap has institutional roots in Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) and social skills training approaches that prioritize neurotypical conformity. Critics argue that "dictating how a neurodivergent person is expected to communicate in specific social situations takes away their self-determination" and that "techniques that reward compliance might inadvertently teach children to override their natural boundaries, potentially increasing vulnerability."

The Therapist Neurodiversity Collective has described social skills "training" as dehumanizing, arguing that it frames autistic communication as inherently deficient rather than different.

#### How compliance training creates vulnerability:

Research has documented that early compliance-focused interventions can create patterns that increase vulnerability to abuse:
- Learning to suppress "no" responses and prioritize others' comfort
- Learning to mask natural responses to make others comfortable
- Learning that acceptance is conditional on performance
- Learning that natural behavior is inherently wrong and must be corrected

This training creates exactly the psychological conditions that abusers exploit: a person who has learned to prioritize others' reality over their own, suppress their own boundaries, and believe their natural responses are wrong.

### 2.6 Institutional Gaslighting

Institutional gaslighting occurs when organizations and systems -- rather than individual abusers -- systematically invalidate autistic communication and experience.

#### Medical Settings

**Diagnostic overshadowing** is the primary mechanism: clinicians attribute symptoms to autism rather than investigating them as distinct medical concerns. This includes:
- Misinterpreting signs of pain as "autism-related behavior"
- Dismissing mental health symptoms as "part of autism"
- Overlooking physical health conditions because communication differences make symptom reporting atypical
- Assuming autistic patients are "exaggerating, untruthful, or drug-seeking" because they don't show conventional non-verbal signs of pain

Research finds that almost **40% of general practitioners report no formal training in autism** and report limited confidence in managing autistic patients (Shaw et al., 2024). Many autistic people "lack trust in doctors and their ability to access treatment because of experiences of being misinterpreted, criticized, traumatized, infantilized, doubted, and seen as unreliable witnesses to their own health experiences."

Uisma, Virkki & Ylilahti (2026) empirically document how "often-intertwined forms of epistemic injustices are related to a lack of representativeness in how autism is understood as well as to how the exclusion of certain kinds of knowledge and knowers, along with the devaluations of expressive styles, impact autistic people's epistemic agency, identities and access to care."

Shaw et al. (2024) identify a **"triple empathy problem"** in healthcare: breakdowns occur between autistic patients and clinicians, between clinicians and the healthcare system, and between the system and autistic communities.

#### Workplace

Autistic employees face systemic pragmatic invalidation through:
- **Performance reviews based on "communication style"** rather than work quality: "facing unfair performance reviews based on social norms rather than job performance"
- **Constructive dismissal**: Supervisors who "unjustly criticize an employee's work performance to create documentation for termination, knowing they cannot terminate an employee because of a disability"
- **Accommodation requests framed as "difficult behavior"**: Requesting sensory accommodations or communication modifications positioned as being "high maintenance" or "not a team player"
- **Masking demands**: Nearly **75%** of autistic employees report masking all or some of the time, including masking "to connect with colleagues, appear more relatable, form friendships, advance their careers, and avoid the burden of educating colleagues about the neurodivergent experience"

The Harvard Business Review has directly stated: **"Autism doesn't hold people back at work. Discrimination does."**

#### Education

- **Participation grades**: Penalize autistic students who don't contribute in NT-expected ways (raising hands, jumping into discussion, making eye contact during contributions)
- **Group work penalties**: Autistic students "struggle with interpreting and responding to social cues" in collaborative settings, and grades reflect social performance rather than subject mastery
- **"Professional communication" requirements**: Standards that are implicitly neurotypical, requiring masking as a prerequisite for academic success
- **Failure to create supportive pathways**: "Hundreds of incredibly talented neurodivergent individuals studying in universities and colleges may never make it into the workforce due to lack of opportunities, differences in communication skills, or other factors"

#### Criminal Justice System

Research documents systematic misinterpretation of autistic behavior at every stage:

- **Police interactions**: "Officers can misinterpret autistic speech as uncooperative or disrespectful; autistic people can misunderstand or have difficulty complying with expectations of officers, or fail to appreciate an officer's authority to enforce compliance." Only **37% of police officers** surveyed in England and Wales had received autism training.

- **Credibility assessment**: Autistic people are "perceived as more deceptive and less credible than non-autistic people when telling the truth" because "lack of eye contact, unusual speech patterns, and fidgeting" are interpreted as signs of deception -- behaviors that are typical autistic traits.

- **Interrogation vulnerability**: Research shows autistic individuals may not be more suggestible to memory distortions, but they "can be more compliant and prone to guessing when pushed," and "the risk of compliance or suggestibility among individuals with ASD is exasperated by leading and repeated questions, negative feedback, and misinformation."

- **Trial proceedings**: In a study of 93 cases, **59% of prosecution barristers and 46% of judges** said or did something during trial that made defense lawyers concerned they did not adequately understand autism. In only about half of jury trial cases were jurors informed that the defendant was autistic.

**Sources:**

#### Social Services

Masking during assessments conceals actual support needs: "Autistic individuals who mask may not receive appropriate support services because their support needs remain hidden." This creates a paradox: the better someone is at appearing "normal" during an assessment, the fewer supports they receive -- and the more likely they are to experience burnout, crisis, and vulnerability.

**Source:** PMC. "Masking is life": Experiences of masking in autistic and nonautistic adults (2022).

## Part 3: The Research Base on Autistic Vulnerability to Manipulation

### 3.1 Prevalence of Victimization

The research on autistic vulnerability to violence, exploitation, and manipulation is extensive and deeply concerning:

#### Intimate Partner Violence and Sexual Violence

- **Cazalis et al. (2022)**: In a French community sample of **225 autistic women**, 88.4% reported experiencing at least one form of sexual assault (via standardized questionnaire) / 68.9% (via open question). Two-thirds were first assaulted before age 18. 75% had been assaulted multiple times. Published in *Frontiers in Behavioral Neuroscience*.

- **Systematic review data (Trundle et al., 2023)**: 40% of autistic people reported sexual victimization; 16% reported childhood abuse; **84% reported victimization in more than one category**.

- **Douglas & Sedgewick (2024)**: Domestic violence prevalence among autistic adults: 23.7%. Physical violence: 56-58.5%. Sexual violence: 31-56.8%. Stalking and harassment: 58.5%. Published in *Autism*, 28(7).

- **Gender and intersectional data (Reuben et al., 2021)**: Rates of interpersonal trauma were highest among transgender men (90%), cisgender women (78%), non-binary people (76%), and transgender women (73%).

- **Comparative data**: Autistic women experience sexual violence at **2-3 times** the rate of the general population (where ~30% of women experience sexual violence). The 61% rate among autistic women without intellectual disability compares to 35% among non-autistic women in the same study.

#### Bullying

- Meta-analytic prevalence: **44% victimization** (pooled estimate), with individual studies reporting rates as high as **94%**
- Cross-cultural meta-analysis: 67% victimization, 29% perpetration, 14% both
- Risk of victimization is "significantly higher than that in typically developing students and students with other disabilities"
- Those most at risk: individuals with Asperger syndrome / milder social understanding differences, early adolescents, those in mainstream school settings

#### Fraud and Financial Exploitation

- In a Cambridge study of **426 autistic adults**, almost **half** reported being "tricked or pressured into giving someone money or possessions" -- compared to 20% of non-autistic adults.
- Lie detection ability is "significantly diminished" in those with full ASD diagnosis, related to how many ASD traits a person has
- "Autistic individuals are often very honest people who can tend to take people at face value" -- a strength in many contexts that becomes a vulnerability with unscrupulous individuals

### 3.2 Why Autistic People Are Vulnerable: A Mechanistic Account

The vulnerability is not a deficiency in autistic people -- it arises from the **interaction between autistic cognitive/communicative style and a social world built for neurotypical interaction**. Key mechanisms:

#### 1. Processing Delays and Alexithymia

When coupled with autism's communication differences, sensory processing challenges, and need for structure, alexithymia (difficulty identifying one's own feelings) "leaves us exceptionally vulnerable to the mind-bending manipulation of gaslighting." Autistic people "often experience processing delays, so especially in combination with alexithymia, it could be that by the time you realize you have been manipulated, the culprit is long gone."

#### 2. Literalness and Good Faith Assumption

Autistic communication is generally characterized by directness, literalness, and good-faith assumption. These are strengths in honest communication, but vulnerabilities when dealing with deceptive interlocutors. "The autistic way of communicating doesn't contain subtext and is predicated solely on the words said" -- which means autistic people may not detect subtext-based manipulation.

#### 3. Difficulty Detecting Deception

Research confirms that "the main reason adults with autism are so much more susceptible to such schemes is their inability to recognize that the perpetrators are intentionally deceiving them and do not have their best interests in mind." However, this is nuanced: autistic individuals may actually be **less influenced by misleading social cues** in some contexts, and their pattern recognition may detect inconsistencies that NT observers miss -- but in real-time social interaction, the processing demands may overwhelm these advantages.

#### 4. Social Isolation and Limited Reference Points

Autistic people often have smaller social networks and fewer close relationships. This means:
- Fewer people to reality-check concerning interactions with
- Greater dependence on the relationships they do have
- Less exposure to diverse interaction patterns that would make manipulation patterns recognizable
- Greater vulnerability to isolation tactics used in coercive control

#### 5. History of Being Told Their Perceptions Are Wrong

Years of sensory gaslighting, diagnostic weaponization, and pragmatic invalidation create a baseline self-doubt that primes vulnerability. As one source notes: "Some autistic individuals are uniquely susceptible to gaslighting as they live in a society for which they are not wired and rely on others' translations regularly."

#### 6. Compliance Training

As documented in Section 2.5, many autistic people have been trained through social skills programs, ABA, or broader social pressure to:
- Suppress their natural responses
- Prioritize others' comfort over their own boundaries
- Accept that their instincts about social situations are unreliable
- Comply with demands from authority figures even when uncomfortable

These trained behaviors are precisely what abusers exploit.

### 3.3 Trauma and PTSD in Autistic Populations

The cumulative impact of these experiences manifests in elevated trauma and PTSD rates:

- Autistic adults are **more than four times more likely** to be diagnosed with PTSD than adults without ASD
- Adults with ASD are "exposed to a significantly greater number of adverse events in comparison with individuals without ASD"
- Autistic people experience **more severe PTSD symptoms** compared to non-autistic peers
- Current PTSD diagnostic criteria "may not fully capture how trauma is experienced and expressed by autistic individuals, leading to underdiagnosis" -- meaning the true prevalence may be even higher
- Autistic youth and adults "frequently experience social adversity in the form of stigma, discrimination, invalidation, and social rejection"

## Part 4: Pragmatic Self-Defense Framework

### 4.1 Pattern Recognition as Protection

The core protective strategy is **making the manipulation patterns visible**. Autistic people often have exceptional pattern recognition -- the goal is to give them the patterns to look for:

1. **Identify the shift from content to packaging**: When a conversation moves from *what you said* to *how you said it*, note the shift. Is the content being addressed, or only the delivery?

2. **Notice credibility challenges that reference diagnosis**: Any statement that uses your autism to invalidate your perception ("that's just your autism talking") is performing diagnostic weaponization. The diagnosis describes *how* you process, not *whether* your processing is valid.

3. **Track reality-denial patterns**: Gaslighting is a pattern, not a single incident. Isolated "you remember that differently" moments happen in all relationships. Systematic patterns of "that never happened / you're remembering it wrong / you're being too sensitive" are gaslighting.

4. **Recognize DARVO sequences**: If raising a concern leads to denial, then attack on you, then claims that you're the one being harmful -- that's a DARVO sequence. The sequence itself, not any individual component, is the tell.

5. **Identify compliance demands**: "If you could just..." followed by a demand for masking or pragmatic performance, followed by the implication that your mistreatment is your fault for not complying -- this is the compliance trap.

### 4.2 Practical Strategies for Self-Protection

Research and advocacy organizations recommend several practical approaches:

- **Documentation**: Keep records of interactions, especially those that feel "off." Autistic pattern recognition may detect manipulation before conscious awareness catches up.
- **Trusted external reference points**: Maintain connections with people who can reality-check concerning interactions. Support groups, therapists familiar with autism, and trusted friends serve this function.
- **Boundary scripts**: Pre-prepared responses for common manipulation scenarios. "I need to think about that before responding." "That's not what we're discussing right now." "My experience is valid regardless of how it's delivered."
- **Understanding rights**: Knowledge of legal protections (ADA, Equality Act, etc.) for disability discrimination in employment, healthcare, education, and public services.
- **Digital safety**: Guidance on privacy settings, recognizing manipulation tactics in online communication, and protecting personal information.

## Part 5: Synthesis -- The Weaponization Framework

### 5.1 How the Pieces Fit Together

The theoretical frameworks and empirical evidence converge on a clear picture:

1. **The pragmatic system is not neutral**. It was developed by and for neurotypical communicators. Autistic people are perpetual outsiders to pragmatic norms they did not create and cannot fully perform.

2. **This outsider status creates vulnerability**. Epistemic injustice (Fricker) means autistic testimony is systematically discounted. Hermeneutical injustice means autistic experiences lack adequate language. The double empathy problem (Milton) means communication failures are blamed solely on the autistic party.

3. **Abusers exploit this vulnerability**. Gaslighting (Abramson, Stark, Sweet) leverages existing social structures that already discredit autistic perceptions. Coercive control (Stark) uses pragmatic conformity demands as tools of domination. DARVO (Freyd) is especially effective against people whose testimony is already structurally discounted.

4. **Institutions amplify individual abuse**. Medical diagnostic overshadowing, workplace communication-style discrimination, educational participation penalties, and criminal justice misinterpretation create systemic versions of the same patterns that individual abusers use.

5. **The cumulative impact is devastating**. Elevated rates of IPV, sexual violence, bullying, fraud, and exploitation; PTSD rates four times higher than the general population; chronic masking exhaustion; and internalized self-doubt that makes future exploitation more likely.

### 5.2 What Makes This Category Different

This is not a "social skills" lesson. Teaching autistic people to perform NT pragmatics better does not protect them from manipulation -- it may actually increase vulnerability by:
- Training compliance and self-suppression
- Teaching that one's natural communication is defective
- Creating dependence on external validation of one's social performance
- Exhausting cognitive resources through masking, leaving fewer resources for detecting manipulation

Instead, this category teaches **pragmatic literacy as self-defense**: understanding how the system works not to perform it better, but to recognize when it's being used against you.

### 5.3 The Goal

The goal is not to make autistic people paranoid or fearful. The goal is to make the patterns **visible**. Autistic people are often excellent at pattern recognition once they know what patterns to look for. The pragmatic manipulation patterns documented in this research have identifiable structures, predictable sequences, and recognizable signatures.

**Once you can see the pattern, you can name it. Once you can name it, you can resist it.**
