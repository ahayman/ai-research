# Concept Research: IFS and Autism — Literal Thinking, Sensory Profile, Masking, and Adaptations

## Overview

Autism brings a specific bundle of cognitive, attentional, sensory, and social features that interact with IFS mechanics in ways that are structurally distinct from alexithymia (which Category 6 covers). Roughly half of autistic adults are also alexithymic, and the two issues compound — but the autism-specific issues exist on their own and matter even when interoceptive access is intact.

The autism × IFS intersection sits on five fault lines: (1) the metaphor problem — "parts" as a model can land cleanly as a literal map for some autistic readers, or feel imprecise/wrong for others; (2) monotropism — the autistic attentional style that channels attention into one stream at a time; (3) masking — a lifetime of camouflaging autistic traits raises the question of whether masked behaviors are "manager parts" or autism + suppression; (4) sensory profile — session structure and what gets misread as "a part is activated"; and (5) aphantasia — the inability to form mental imagery, which is more common among autistic people and which IFS visualization-based steps assume away.

A small, growing literature by autistic IFS practitioners — Sarah Bergenfield (whose 2025 book *Wired to Feel*, co-authored with Martha Sweezy and Beth Ann Malow MD, gives this intersection book-length IFS-Institute-adjacent treatment), Sanni Tsuruoka (IFS with Sanni), Emma at the IFS & Neurodiversity Substack, the Self Leadership Journey Substack, and the Autside Substack — has converged on a working position: **autism is the hardware/operating system, parts are software running on it, and you don't try to "unburden" hardware**. This is also where the universalist Self claim of canonical IFS gets pushed back on hardest: does Self look the same in autistic minds, or does the model need adjustment?

## Core Components

### Component 1: The Metaphor Problem

- **Purpose**: Establish whether "parts" works as a useful model for a given autistic reader
- **Inputs**: Client's literal vs. metaphorical thinking style, their prior comfort with metaphor, their tolerance for reified abstraction
- **Outputs**: Either a working frame ("parts" as map) or a friction point that requires reframing or substitution
- **Relationships**: Determines viability of every downstream IFS mechanic

The "parts" framing is itself a metaphor — Schwartz has been deliberately ambivalent about whether parts are real subpersonalities, neural networks, dissociated affective states, or phenomenologically useful fictions. This vagueness is therapeutically flexible; it can also be a problem for systems thinkers who need to know what the model actually claims before using it.

Two failure modes in literal-thinking autistic readers:

- **Reification anxiety**: "Are these real entities or am I making this up?" If treated as actually real, the model crosses into parapsychological territory; if treated as merely metaphor, the agentic language ("the part wants to...") feels false.
- **Imprecision rejection**: For some autistic readers, the metaphor is too loose — the model speaks of "parts" as if their boundaries were knowable when in fact they are constructed in the moment.

When the metaphor works for autistic readers:
- It provides a structured, named map for intractable inner conflict
- It leverages pattern-recognition strengths (autistic clients often catch part-patterns quickly once oriented)
- It externalizes — "a part of me is feeling X" creates the same kind of distance an autistic person might naturally use through systematic representation

Concretizing adaptations include:
- **Index cards**: one part per card, written down, externalized
- **Spreadsheets / tabular representations**: protective patterns in rows; columns for "what triggers this," "what it does," "what it protects against"
- **Neural-network reframing**: "parts" recast as patterns of brain activity rather than as little people inside
- **Color-coded cards** representing emotional states (Self Leadership Journey)
- **Stress balls labeled with emotions, physical transition cues** (clapping, tapping)
- **Personalized metaphors**: substitute the "parts" frame with metaphors matching the client's special interests — train enthusiasts use "changing tracks," computer users use "switching programs"
- **Brain-science explanations**: explicitly tying state shifts to prefrontal cortex, parasympathetic nervous system, neural pathway strengthening

### Component 2: Monotropism

- **Purpose**: Recognize the autistic attentional style and design IFS work around it rather than against it
- **Inputs**: Sustained attention to a narrowed channel; difficulty switching between channels
- **Outputs**: Either deep IFS work (if monotropism is leveraged) or session disruption (if the work demands rapid switching)
- **Relationships**: Affects which 6 Fs steps work well and which feel costly

**Monotropism** was first articulated by autistic researcher Dinah Murray in 1992 and elaborated in the influential 2005 paper "Attention, monotropism and the diagnostic criteria for autism" (Murray, Lesser, Lawson — *Autism* journal, vol 9(2), pp 139–156). The theory proposes that autistic minds tend to channel attentional resources into a small number of interests at a time (mono = single, tropism = channel), filtering out competing stimuli. It has been increasingly accepted as a unifying framework for autism that does not pathologize.

**As a potential asset for IFS:**
- Sustained focus on one part can deepen the work substantially
- Less wandering between parts, less "what should we work on today?" friction
- Special-interest depth and pattern recognition are assets in protector mapping
- The "attention tunnel" — focused immersion within a narrow channel — overlaps with what Csikszentmihalyi calls flow and what Heasman et al. (2024, *Journal for the Theory of Social Behaviour*) describe as "autistic flow"
- Some autistic practitioners argue Self is most accessible during these immersive states rather than through "stepping back from parts"

**As a potential challenge:**
- Switching between parts within a session can be costly — transitions cost cognitive energy and the cost is higher for monotropic minds
- The 6 Fs sequence (Find → Focus → Flesh out → Feel toward → beFriend → Fears) implies movement through stages; monotropic clients may want to stay in one stage longer
- Therapist-prompted shifts ("let's check in with another part now") can feel disorienting
- A dysregulated monotropic system may struggle to disengage from a part that has captured attention

Monotropism-informed adaptations:
- Pace sessions around the client's attentional flow rather than forcing structured check-ins
- Allow extended dwell-time on a single part across multiple sessions
- Recognize that attention-shifting itself may need to be a parts conversation ("I notice it costs us to leave this part — what's that about?")

### Component 3: Masking and "Parts" Identification

- **Purpose**: Distinguish autistic survival strategies (masking) from manager parts in IFS terms
- **Inputs**: A lifetime of camouflaging behaviors developed under social pressure
- **Outputs**: A working frame for which behaviors get treated as parts and which get treated as autism-adaptation that doesn't need "unburdening"
- **Relationships**: This is where the hardware/software distinction is load-bearing

**Masking** (also called camouflaging) is the suite of strategies autistic people develop to appear neurotypical: suppressing stims, forcing eye contact, scripting conversations, rehearsing responses to unwritten social rules. The Hull et al. and Mandy et al. research programs have documented its prevalence and its costs (Hull, Mandy and colleagues — Camouflaging Autistic Traits Questionnaire, CAT-Q). Higher self-reported masking correlates with worse mental health outcomes — depression, anxiety, autistic burnout.

The IFS question: are masked behaviors "manager parts" or are they autism + suppression?

The neurodivergence-affirming answer that has crystallized across multiple autistic IFS practitioners: **autism is hardware/neurotype, masking is software a part developed to keep you safe in a world that punished autistic expression**. So:

- "Suppress my stims around colleagues" — that's a part, a manager doing protective work; it can be befriended and its fears understood, and over time the system can decide to relax that protector when it's safe
- "Process information in nonlinear, pattern-first ways" — that's autism, hardware; you don't try to make a part of you that does this "unburden" or change

The risk in pure IFS: if a therapist treats every autistic feature as a part to work with, autism itself gets pathologized. Sanni Tsuruoka writes plainly: "Autism is part of the hardware: the foundational wiring of your system, how your brain processes information, how you sense the world, how you relate to time, energy, and connection. It is damaging to neurodivergent folk to call neurology a part."

The reverse risk: if a therapist defaults to "that's just autism, don't touch it," genuinely-burdened protective patterns (perfectionism, people-pleasing, compulsive masking) get left untreated.

The working frame across autistic IFS practitioners:
- Hardware = neurology = autism. Not a part. Not unburdened.
- Software = parts = patterns that formed in response to experiences (including the experience of being autistic in a non-autistic world). Workable.
- Boundary case: a "masking part" is a protector, but the autistic processing style underneath the mask is hardware.

### Component 4: Sensory Profile Considerations

- **Purpose**: Recognize sensory needs as prerequisites for therapeutic work, not as content of therapy
- **Inputs**: Environmental factors (lighting, sound, movement, eye-contact expectations); body-based dysregulation
- **Outputs**: Either a sensorily-regulated session that can do parts work or a dysregulated one that cannot
- **Relationships**: Overlaps with but is distinct from interoception (Cat 6); also distinct from "what part is activated"

Sensory considerations are not IFS's primary territory — they belong more to occupational therapy and to sensory-integration approaches — but they affect IFS sessions directly:

- **Lighting**: Cool-white/blue overhead fluorescents are particularly hard for many autistic adults; warmer 2200–3000K and dimmable lighting helps
- **Sound**: HVAC hum, traffic, fluorescent buzz
- **Movement / posture**: Sitting still in a chair facing the therapist may not be the regulating posture; pacing, fidgeting, holding objects, side-by-side seating can help
- **Eye contact**: For many autistic adults, sustained eye contact during emotionally-loaded conversation costs cognitive resources that could otherwise go to the parts work
- **Telehealth vs. in-person**: Often telehealth is sensorily simpler — no commute, controlled environment, ability to pace

A specific failure mode worth flagging: **sensory dysregulation framed as "a part is activated."** Sometimes a client is genuinely dysregulated by the room, not by an inner protector being triggered. Treating sensory overload as a parts event misses the actual cause and adds confusion. The corrective: ask first whether the room is OK, before asking whether something inside is activating.

When sensory framing IS appropriate: when the client has a part that gets activated by a particular sensory pattern — for example, a part that responds to a specific tone of voice that resembles a parent's — the parts language is fine. The distinction is whether the sensory event is the cause (regulate the room) or the trigger (work with the part triggered by the sensory event).

### Component 5: Aphantasia and IFS

- **Purpose**: Adapt IFS for clients who cannot form mental images at all (aphantasia) or who have weak imagery
- **Inputs**: Absence of mental imagery; reliance on cognitive/somatic/external channels
- **Outputs**: Adapted IFS that does not require the "Flesh out" step in its visualization-dependent form
- **Relationships**: Specifically affects 6 Fs step 3 (Flesh out)

**Aphantasia** is the inability to form voluntary mental imagery — congenital aphantasia was the term coined by Adam Zeman's group at Exeter in 2015. Estimated population prevalence is roughly 0.7% complete aphantasia (VVIQ score 16) plus 2.6% moderate aphantasia (VVIQ 16–23), so around 3% in the general population (Zeman et al. 2015).

**Possible association with autism**: Dance, Jaquiery, Eagleman, Porteous, Zeman, and Simner (2021, *Consciousness and Cognition*) found that aphantasic individuals reported more autistic traits than controls — particularly weaknesses in imagination and social skills as measured by the Autism Spectrum Quotient. This is correlation, not strong causation, but it suggests aphantasia is over-represented in autistic populations. The 2025 Wei et al. *Autism Research* paper "Expanding Perspectives on Visual Mental Imagery in Autism" reviews mental imagery variation in autism more broadly and finds heterogeneity (some autistic individuals have strong imagery, some are aphantasic, some hyperphantasic).

**The IFS problem**: The 6 Fs' "Flesh out" step asks "what does this part look like?" — what does it wear, how old does it seem, what is its expression? This presupposes mental imagery. For aphantasic clients, the question is unanswerable in its literal form.

**Adaptations** that have surfaced in clinician blogs and the IFS Telehealth Collective:
- **Felt-sense substitution**: "Where in the body do you notice this part? What is its texture, weight, temperature?" — this works for aphantasic clients with intact interoception, but fails for the aphantasic + alexithymic intersection
- **Cognitive description**: "If you could describe this part in words without picturing it, what would you say about it?" — works for verbal-cognitive access
- **Externalized representation**: Drawing the part on paper (drawing without "seeing internally" is possible for aphantasic clients), choosing an object that represents it, writing about it
- **Auditory access**: Some aphantasic clients do have inner speech / inner voice — "what does this part sound like, what does it say?"
- **Somatic mapping with externalization**: pointing on a diagram of the body where the part lives
- **Symbolic placeholder**: assigning the part a name and a category (manager / firefighter / exile) and a function description, without any sensory texture

**The hardest case**: aphantasia + alexithymia + autism. No mental imagery. No interoceptive read. Possibly literal-thinking. The model's standard channels — visual, somatic, emotional — are all attenuated. What's left: cognitive description, behavioral observation, written processing, externalized representation. This is exactly the user's profile. The IFS literature is thin here. Bergenfield's *Wired to Feel* and the Telehealth Collective's "Supporting Your Neurotype" post are the closest published treatments.

### Component 6: The "Autistic Self" Question

- **Purpose**: Examine whether the model's universalist claim about Self holds in autistic minds
- **Inputs**: The 8 Cs of canonical Self (curiosity, calm, compassion, clarity, courage, confidence, creativity, connectedness)
- **Outputs**: Either confirmation that Self is universal but expressed differently, or evidence that the model's framing is itself neuro-normative
- **Relationships**: This is where Cat 7 connects to the broader critique literature on universalist Self claims (see Cat 5)

The canonical IFS position: Self is universal, undamaged by trauma, present in everyone, characterized by the 8 Cs. Healing happens when Self builds relationships with parts.

**Sarah Bergenfield's "Autistic Self"** concept, introduced at the 2022 IFS Conference (where she became the first openly autistic person to present at the conference): in an autistic system, **everyone is autistic** — including Self. Self is not a neurotypical visitor inside an autistic mind; Self is autistic too. The 8 Cs are still available, but they are expressed through autistic neurology.

**Emma at IFS & Neurodiversity** (an IFS therapist, clinical psychologist, and AuDHDer) takes a closely-related position in "Can Self Be Autistic?": Self is universal, and shines through all neurotypes — but its expression is shaped by the system's neurology. She uses a prism metaphor: Self is white light; individual neurobiology is the prism; the spectrum that emerges is genuinely diverse without changing the source. So the 8 Cs are present in autistic people but appear differently:

- **Calm** in monotropic minds may not look like polytropic-calm — it can be focused-immersion calm rather than spacious-stillness calm
- **Connection** may happen through objects, movement, rhythm, and shared interests rather than through eye contact and small talk
- **Curiosity** may be deep-channel curiosity rather than wide-net curiosity
- **Compassion** may be quiet, intense, action-oriented rather than verbally-expressive

**The "special-interest immersion as Self-access" hypothesis**: Csikszentmihalyi's flow concept overlaps significantly with descriptions of autistic special-interest engagement. The 2024 Heasman et al. paper "Towards autistic flow theory" formalizes this. Some autistic IFS practitioners suggest that for monotropic clients, accessing Self through immersion in a special interest may work where the "step back from parts" route does not. This is a meaningful departure from canonical IFS technique.

**The pushback against universalist Self**: where the autistic-IFS literature is most provocative is in not just affirming "Self in autistic people looks different" but in asking whether the 8 Cs themselves are neuro-normatively framed. "Calm" and "connectedness" in particular are claimed by some autistic practitioners to have been written from a neurotypical baseline. The honest answer is: this is contested. The 8 Cs are flexible enough to admit autistic expression; they were also originally articulated by a non-autistic therapist working primarily with non-autistic clients.

### Component 7: Are These Adaptations IFS or Something Else?

- **Purpose**: Honest examination of whether neurodiversity-affirming IFS still is IFS
- **Inputs**: The full set of adaptations above
- **Outputs**: A working position on what the adapted practice actually is
- **Relationships**: Connects to the broader question of model fidelity vs. clinical pragmatics

Where neurodiversity-affirming IFS departs from the canonical model:
- The hardware/software distinction is not in canonical IFS literature; it is a neurodiversity-affirming addition
- "Self can be autistic" / "everyone in the system is autistic" is a meaningful modification of universal Self
- Externalized practices (cards, drawings, spreadsheets) sit alongside in-sight access
- Special-interest immersion as Self-access is not a canonical Self-access route
- "Don't unburden autism" is an explicit constraint not in the canonical model
- Honoring monotropic pacing modifies the 6 Fs sequence

Where it remains recognizably IFS:
- The parts taxonomy (managers, firefighters, exiles) is preserved
- The protector-first / earn-permission-to-meet-exile sequencing is preserved
- The unburdening process is preserved (for parts, not for autism itself)
- The "no bad parts" therapeutic stance is preserved
- The relational stance of Self toward parts is preserved

Honest framing: this is **IFS with neurodiversity-affirming adaptations**, sufficiently within the IFS frame to count as IFS, but with substantive constraints added that are not in the canonical literature. The adaptations carry the legitimacy of IFS-Institute-adjacent work (Bergenfield co-authored with Sweezy; the IFS Conference platformed her in 2022) without yet being absorbed into Institute training as standard. Whether the canonical model evolves to incorporate them is an open question.

## How It Works

### Step 1: Establish hardware/software framing before any parts work

The client and therapist explicitly distinguish:
- Autism / neurotype = hardware. Off-limits for parts work.
- Patterns that formed in response to experience (including being autistic in a hostile world) = software. Workable.

Why: Without this, every autistic feature gets vulnerable to being treated as a "part," which pathologizes the neurology.

### Step 2: Choose the access channel(s) that work

Rather than insisting on the canonical visual/somatic 6 Fs:
- Visual access (default IFS) — works for autistic clients with intact imagery
- Somatic access — works for those with intact interoception
- Cognitive access — "hearing parts in mind," knowing what they think without feeling them
- Auditory access — inner voice/inner speech
- External access — cards, spreadsheets, drawings, written dialogue
- Hybrid — most autistic clients combine channels

### Step 3: Map parts using the chosen channel

Index-card method: write one part per card. Include:
- A working name (descriptive, not metaphorical)
- The role / function (manager, firefighter, exile, or "unclear yet")
- Triggers (what activates it)
- Behavior (what it does)
- What it protects against (its fear)
- Its relationship to autism (does it manage masking? does it cope with sensory overwhelm? does it carry burdens from autism-related rejection?)

Spreadsheet method: same fields, in a table. Useful for systems thinkers.

### Step 4: Work with one part at a time, monotropically

Pick a part; stay with it across as many sessions as needed. Resist the urge to move quickly to the next part. Allow attention-tunnel depth.

### Step 5: Distinguish parts that need unburdening from autism that does not

Test: "If I imagine this pattern fully resolved/unburdened, do I imagine being less autistic, or do I imagine being autistic and more comfortable in it?"

If the answer is "less autistic" — the work is being aimed at the wrong target.

### Step 6: Access Self through whatever route works

Canonical: step back from parts, notice 8 Cs.

Autistic alternatives:
- Special-interest immersion (autistic flow)
- Sensory regulation followed by cognitive distance
- Written reflection from a Self-perspective
- Externalized representation that places parts on paper, leaving the client/therapist as observer

Test for Self-presence: are the 8 Cs available, expressed through the autistic system?

### Step 7: Witness, befriend, unburden — preserving canonical mechanics

For parts that do need unburdening (not the autism itself), the canonical witnessing/unburdening process works, with the access channel substituted as needed.

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: Aphantasic + Alexithymic + Autistic User Working with a People-Pleasing Manager

**Scenario**: A client with all three features cannot visualize the part, cannot reliably feel its bodily location, and finds metaphorical language imprecise.

**Adaptation**: Index card written in client's hand: "Nice-One Manager — activates around colleagues, especially in meetings. Behavior: agrees out loud while disagreeing internally. Protects against: being seen as difficult, losing standing, conflict. Probable origin: childhood pattern when being literal/direct led to social punishment. Relationship to autism: manages masking; the literal-direct style underneath is the autism — that part doesn't get unburdened, the protective rule does."

**Key Insight**: External representation does the work that internal access would do for non-aphantasic, non-alexithymic clients. The model's mechanics survive translation; the channel changes.

### Example 2: Monotropic Client Working a Single Exile Across 12 Sessions

**Scenario**: Therapist initially proposes moving between parts; client gets disoriented.

**Adaptation**: Therapist and client agree to dwell with one exile across multiple sessions, allowing attention-tunnel depth. The "what's there today?" question is replaced with "where are we with this part?"

**Key Insight**: Honoring monotropism made deep work possible where switching had blocked it. Fewer parts, deeper relationship.

### Example 3: Sensory Dysregulation Misattributed to a Part

**Scenario**: Client becomes distressed mid-session. Therapist asks, "what part is activated?"

**Better practice**: Therapist asks first, "is the room OK right now? lighting, sound, temperature?" Client realizes the AC unit's hum has been escalating their dysregulation; they pause to regulate, then return to the parts work.

**Key Insight**: Asking the room question first preserves the actual cause/trigger distinction. Some sensory events are causes (regulate the room); some are triggers (work with the part).

### Example 4: Masking-Protector Befriended Without Touching Hardware

**Scenario**: Client identifies a chronic masking pattern at work. The IFS instinct: befriend, hear its fears, eventually unburden.

**Adaptation with hardware distinction**: The protector's protective rule ("hide stims, force eye contact, script responses") is befriended and over time relaxed in safer contexts. The underlying autistic processing style — pattern-first, literal-direct, monotropic — is recognized as hardware and not "fixed."

**Key Insight**: The masking-protector can rest. The autism it was protecting can be expressed.

### Example 5: Special-Interest Immersion as Self-Access

**Scenario**: Client cannot find Self through "step back from parts" instruction; the 8 Cs are unavailable in that mode.

**Adaptation**: Client reports that Self-energy is most accessible during deep immersion in their special interest. Therapist frames this not as "you're avoiding parts work" but as a legitimate Self-access route. Parts work then proceeds from that base state.

**Key Insight**: Csikszentmihalyi-style flow / Heasman et al. autistic flow is a Self-access channel that canonical IFS does not name explicitly.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: "Autism is a part to work with."
  - **Reality**: Autism is neurotype/hardware. Patterns that formed in response to being autistic in a non-autistic world are parts (software). Treating autism itself as a part to unburden pathologizes the neurology.

- **Misconception**: "Autistic clients can't do IFS because of metaphor difficulties."
  - **Reality**: Many autistic clients do IFS extremely well — pattern recognition is an asset for protector mapping. Some need adaptations (concretization, externalization); some take to the metaphor cleanly. Heterogeneity is the rule.

- **Misconception**: "Masking parts and autism are the same thing."
  - **Reality**: Masking is a software pattern (protector); the autistic processing style underneath is hardware. Befriending the masking-protector and leaving the hardware alone is the honest move.

- **Misconception**: "Aphantasia means IFS won't work."
  - **Reality**: Aphantasia blocks the visualization-dependent form of Flesh-out. Substitutions exist (cognitive description, externalization, somatic mapping). The model is more flexible than its standard presentation suggests.

- **Misconception**: "Self looks the same in everyone."
  - **Reality**: This is the canonical IFS claim; it is contested by the autistic-IFS literature. The intermediate position (Emma at IFS & Neurodiversity, Bergenfield) is that Self is universal but expressed through neurotype — the 8 Cs are present but their texture is different.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **Edge case**: Client who is autistic but does not have alexithymia or aphantasia — most of the above adaptations are optional, not required. Don't assume the bundle.
- **Edge case**: Client who is autistic + ADHD (AuDHD) — monotropic and dysregulated attention coexist; pacing strategies look different again. (Not deeply covered in this category; flagged for further reading.)
- **Edge case**: Client whose special interest has become rigid/compulsive — the special-interest-as-Self-access route may not work cleanly; the immersion may itself be a firefighter pattern.
- **Edge case**: Client whose masking has been so total that they cannot tell what is hardware and what is software anymore — slow, careful work is required; masking-deconstruction can itself be its own process.
- **Edge case**: Therapist trained in canonical IFS without neurodiversity-affirming adaptations — may inadvertently treat autism as a part. The user should know this risk and be ready to ask whether their therapist has done any work on adaptations for autistic clients.

## Related Concepts

- **Cat 6 (IFS and Alexithymia)**: ~50% of autistic adults are alexithymic. Cat 7 references but does not duplicate Cat 6's interoception material.
- **Cat 2 (Mechanics)**: Cat 7 modifies the 6 Fs for autistic clients; Cat 2 establishes the canonical mechanics.
- **Cat 1 (Parts Model)**: Cat 7 preserves the parts taxonomy; the modifications are around access, pacing, and Self.
- **Cat 5 (Critiques)**: The "universal Self" claim that Cat 5 critiques is exactly where the autistic-IFS literature pushes back hardest.
- **Cat 9 (Failure Modes)**: A canonical-IFS therapist treating autism as a part is itself a failure mode worth flagging in Cat 9.
- **Monotropism literature** (Murray, Lawson, Lesser): foundational for understanding the attentional-style implications.
- **Flow / autistic flow** (Csikszentmihalyi; Heasman et al.): relevant to the special-interest-as-Self-access route.

## Research Gaps

- **No peer-reviewed empirical studies** of IFS specifically in autistic populations. The Bergenfield/Sweezy/Malow 2025 book is the closest to formal IFS-Institute-adjacent treatment; everything else is clinician blogs, Substacks, and conference presentations. Peer-reviewed validation of the proposed adaptations does not yet exist.
- **No formal study** of aphantasia + IFS adaptations. The aphantasia literature exists, the IFS adaptation discussion exists in scattered blogs and the IFS Telehealth Collective post, but they have not been brought together formally.
- **No comparative data** on autistic-affirming-IFS vs. canonical-IFS outcomes for autistic clients.
- **The "Autistic Self" thesis** (Bergenfield) is presented at conferences and discussed across Substacks; a published, peer-reviewed examination is not yet available.
- **Monotropism × IFS pacing implications** are theoretically clear but have not been operationalized in any structured protocol.
- **The user's specific intersection** (autistic + alexithymic + aphantasic + complex trauma) is the hardest case and is mentioned only in passing in the available literature. Bergenfield's *Wired to Feel* gets closest but is sensory-focused rather than aphantasia-focused.
