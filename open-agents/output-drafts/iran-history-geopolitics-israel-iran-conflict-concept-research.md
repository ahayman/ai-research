# Israel & Iran: From Allies to Enemies

## 1. The Peripheral Doctrine: A Strategic Marriage (1948-1979)

### Ben-Gurion's Strategic Vision

When Israel was established in 1948, it found itself surrounded by hostile Arab states committed to its destruction. Prime Minister David Ben-Gurion developed the "Peripheral Doctrine" (also called the "Alliance of the Periphery") — a strategy to forge alliances with non-Arab states on the edges of the Arab world as a counterbalance. The logic was elegant: the enemy of my enemy is my friend. Iran, Turkey, and Ethiopia — all non-Arab, all with their own reasons to feel threatened by Arab nationalism — became Israel's strategic partners.

Iran was the crown jewel of this strategy. In 1950, Iran became the second Muslim-majority country to recognize Israel as a sovereign state, after Turkey. The two countries established what became known as the "Trident" pact in 1958, along with Turkey — a secret trilateral alliance focused on intelligence sharing, military cooperation, and countering Arab nationalism, particularly Nasser's Egypt.

### The Depth of Cooperation

The pre-revolution Israel-Iran relationship was extensive and multifaceted:

**Intelligence:** SAVAK (Iran's secret police, established with CIA help in 1957) and Mossad maintained deep cooperation. Israel helped train SAVAK agents, and the two services shared intelligence on Arab states and the Soviet Union. This cooperation was so close that Israeli intelligence officers maintained a permanent presence in Tehran.

**Oil:** After the 1967 Six-Day War intensified the Arab oil embargo against Israel, Iran became a critical energy lifeline. Iranian oil flowed to Israel through the joint Eilat-Ashkelon pipeline, and Iran supplied Israel with a significant portion of its oil needs throughout the 1970s.

**Trade and Commerce:** Israeli construction firms, agricultural experts, and engineers were active throughout Iran. El Al operated direct flights between Tel Aviv and Tehran. Trade was brisk and growing.

**Military:** Israel sold weapons and provided military training to Iran. The two countries cooperated on Project Flower, a joint missile development program, and on various defense technology initiatives.

**Diplomatic:** Israel maintained two successive ambassadors in Tehran and operated a full embassy — something unthinkable in any Arab capital at the time.

### The Iranian Perspective

For the Shah, the alliance with Israel served multiple purposes. Israel provided a counterbalance to hostile Arab neighbors (particularly Nasser's Egypt and Ba'athist Iraq). Israeli technical expertise aided Iran's modernization. And the relationship gave the Shah leverage in Washington, where Israel's friends were powerful. The Shah also used Israel as a channel to Kurdish rebels in Iraq, whom both countries supported as a way to weaken their common adversary.

## 2. The Revolution: Ideology Replaces Strategy (1979)

### Khomeini's Anti-Israel Doctrine

The 1979 Islamic Revolution transformed Iran-Israel relations from close partnership to existential enmity virtually overnight. Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini had been attacking the Shah's relationship with Israel since the early 1960s, casting it as proof of the monarchy's corruption and subservience to foreign powers.

Upon taking power, Khomeini severed diplomatic relations, withdrew recognition of Israel, and handed the former Israeli embassy to the Palestine Liberation Organization. He designated the last Friday of Ramadan as "Quds Day" (Jerusalem Day), calling for the liberation of Jerusalem from "Zionist occupation." He referred to Israel as a "cancerous tumor" and made opposition to Zionism a pillar of the Islamic Republic's identity.

### Genuine Conviction or Strategic Calculation?

Khomeini's anti-Israel stance combined both genuine ideological conviction and strategic calculation:

**Genuine Elements:** Khomeini had expressed hostility to Zionism since the early 1960s, well before the revolution made it politically advantageous. His writings contained deeply antisemitic themes, arguing that Jews were "opposed to the very foundations of Islam and wish to establish Jewish domination throughout the world." This went beyond anti-Zionism into genuine ideological hostility.

**Strategic Elements:** Khomeini recognized that the Palestinian cause was the most emotionally resonant issue in the broader Muslim world. By championing Palestine more aggressively than any Arab leader, Iran could transcend the Sunni-Shia divide and project influence across the entire Islamic world. The anti-Israel stance became a gateway for spreading revolutionary ideology to the otherwise unreceptive, predominantly Sunni Arab street.

The Islamic Republic's subsequent behavior has reflected this duality. Anti-Israel rhetoric remains a central pillar of regime legitimacy, but Tehran has also demonstrated pragmatism when necessary.

### The Paradox: Iran-Israel Arms Deals During the Iran-Iraq War

Perhaps the most revealing episode of pragmatism occurred during the Iran-Iraq War (1980-1988). Despite Khomeini's anti-Israel rhetoric, Israel clandestinely sold weapons, spare parts, and ammunition to the Islamic Republic beginning within months of the revolution and continuing throughout the war.

Estimates of the scale vary enormously. The Jaffe Institute for Strategic Studies at Tel Aviv University estimated Israel sold around $500 million worth of arms annually, while US government reports indicated total Israeli arms sales to Iran may have approached $2 billion per year in the early 1980s. Items included spare parts for Iran's American-made F-4 Phantom jets, artillery ammunition, and anti-tank missiles.

Israel's strategic rationale was straightforward: keeping the war going ensured that two potential enemies — Iran and Iraq — remained preoccupied with each other. As Israeli Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin later acknowledged, Israel viewed Saddam Hussein's Iraq as the more immediate threat. Israel also hoped to maintain some influence in Tehran and protect Iran's remaining Jewish community.

Iran accepted the weapons because it desperately needed them — its American-made arsenal required American (or Israeli) spare parts. This episode revealed that both sides' positions, however ideologically framed, were fundamentally shaped by strategic calculation.

The Iran-Contra affair (1985-1986) later revealed that Israel had facilitated US arms shipments to Iran as part of a scheme to secure the release of American hostages held by Hezbollah in Lebanon, with proceeds diverted to Nicaraguan Contra rebels.

## 3. The 1990s Shift: Iran Becomes Israel's Primary Threat

### The Post-Cold War Recalibration

The end of the Cold War and the 1991 Gulf War fundamentally reshaped the Middle Eastern strategic landscape. Iraq, battered by the Gulf War and constrained by sanctions, ceased to be the primary threat in Israeli strategic thinking. The Soviet Union's collapse removed a major patron of Arab states. The Madrid Conference and Oslo Accords suggested that the Arab-Israeli conflict might be heading toward resolution.

In this context, Israeli strategic planners — and particularly Likud politician Benjamin Netanyahu — began reframing Iran as Israel's primary existential threat. This shift was partly driven by genuine assessment of Iran's growing capabilities and partly by domestic political calculations.

### Netanyahu's Three-Decade Campaign

Netanyahu's public campaign against Iran's nuclear program began in January 1992, when, as a Knesset member, he warned Iran was "three to five years" from producing a nuclear weapon. This became a defining theme of his political career:

- **1992:** Warned Iran was 3-5 years from a nuclear weapon
- **1995:** Claimed Iran would have nuclear weapons "by 1999"
- **1996:** Addressed US Congress, framing Iranian nuclear ambitions as a catastrophe
- **2002:** Told Congress Iran was "racing" to build nuclear weapons
- **2012:** Famous "red line" speech at the UN General Assembly with a cartoon bomb diagram
- **2015:** Addressed Congress to oppose the JCPOA, calling it a "historic mistake"

Each time previous timelines expired without materialization, Netanyahu simply reset the clock. Critics accused him of "crying wolf" and exploiting the Iran threat for domestic political purposes. Supporters argued he was providing essential early warning about a genuinely dangerous program. Regardless of one's assessment, the pattern is clear: the Iran nuclear threat became Netanyahu's signature issue and a defining element of Israeli political discourse.

### Israeli Domestic Politics and Iran

The Iran threat has profoundly shaped Israeli elections and military doctrine. Candidates are routinely evaluated on their "toughness" toward Iran. The Israeli defense establishment has organized much of its force structure, intelligence capabilities, and long-range strike planning around the Iran contingency. The threat narrative has also been used to justify large defense budgets and to argue against territorial concessions to Palestinians (on the grounds that a Palestinian state could become an Iranian proxy).

## 4. The Shadow War: Covert Operations (2000s-2020s)

### Stuxnet: The First Cyberweapon

The most sophisticated covert operation against Iran's nuclear program was Stuxnet, a malicious computer worm first uncovered in June 2010 but believed to have been in development since at least 2005. Jointly developed by the United States and Israel under "Operation Olympic Games" (as confirmed by Edward Snowden in 2013), Stuxnet specifically targeted the Siemens supervisory control and data acquisition (SCADA) systems controlling centrifuges at the Natanz enrichment facility.

The worm caused centrifuges to spin at improper speeds, destroying approximately 1,000 of Iran's 5,000 centrifuges while displaying normal readings to operators. It was the first known cyberweapon designed to cause physical destruction of infrastructure and represented a new frontier in covert warfare.

### Assassinations of Nuclear Scientists

Between 2010 and 2020, at least five Iranian nuclear scientists were assassinated in operations widely attributed to Mossad:

- **Masoud Alimohammadi** (January 2010): Killed by a motorcycle bomb
- **Majid Shahriari** (November 2010): Killed by a magnetic bomb attached to his car
- **Darioush Rezaeinejad** (July 2011): Shot dead
- **Mostafa Ahmadi Roshan** (January 2012): Killed by a magnetic bomb
- **Mohsen Fakhrizadeh** (November 2020): Killed in a sophisticated roadside ambush, reportedly using a remote-controlled machine gun. Fakhrizadeh was considered the "father" of Iran's covert nuclear weapons program.

Iran blamed Israel for all these assassinations. Israel neither confirmed nor denied involvement, maintaining its policy of ambiguity.

### Intelligence Operations

In 2018, Mossad operatives conducted a spectacular operation inside Tehran, extracting Iran's entire nuclear archive — approximately 100,000 files — from a warehouse. Netanyahu publicly displayed the documents as evidence that Iran had concealed its nuclear weapons work (the AMAD Project). The operation demonstrated an extraordinary level of Israeli intelligence penetration inside Iran.

### Cyber and Sabotage Operations

Beyond Stuxnet, Israel is believed to have conducted numerous cyber and sabotage operations against Iranian facilities, including explosions at the Natanz facility in April 2021, fires at various industrial and military sites, and disruptions to Iran's port infrastructure.

## 5. Syria: The Proxy Battlefield (2013-Present)

### Israel's "Campaign Between the Wars"

As Iran deepened its military presence in Syria to support the Assad regime during the civil war, Israel launched what it called the "Campaign Between the Wars" — a sustained air campaign targeting Iranian military assets, weapons shipments, and personnel in Syria. Beginning around 2013 and intensifying dramatically after 2017, Israel conducted hundreds of airstrikes on Syrian territory.

These strikes targeted Iranian weapons convoys (particularly advanced missiles being transferred to Hezbollah), Iranian military bases and infrastructure in Syria, Quds Force and IRGC commanders, and Iranian drone and air defense systems. While Israel rarely acknowledged specific strikes, the campaign was an open secret, with Israeli officials including Netanyahu confirming the general policy.

Iran accepted these losses as the cost of maintaining its Syrian presence, which it viewed as strategically essential for its land corridor to Hezbollah in Lebanon and for projecting power on Israel's northern border.

## 6. Hezbollah: Iran's Primary Deterrent Against Israel

### Creation and Purpose

Hezbollah was established in 1982 in the chaos of Israel's invasion of Lebanon, with direct Iranian Revolutionary Guard sponsorship. Over four decades, it grew from a small militia into the most powerful non-state armed actor in history, serving as Iran's primary deterrent against Israeli military action.

### The Deterrent Logic

The strategic logic was clear: if Israel or the United States attacked Iran's nuclear facilities or attempted regime change, Hezbollah would unleash its massive rocket and missile arsenal against Israeli population centers, overwhelming Iron Dome defenses through sheer volume. At its peak, Hezbollah possessed 150,000-200,000 rockets and missiles — the largest non-state arsenal in history — ranging from short-range Katyushas to precision-guided missiles capable of striking anywhere in Israel.

Hezbollah also operated anti-ship cruise missiles (it struck the Israeli corvette INS Hanit with a C-802 missile during the 2006 war), Iranian-supplied drones, and an extensive tunnel network along the Israeli-Lebanese border designed for cross-border infiltration.

### The 2006 War

The 2006 Lebanon War was the most significant test of this deterrent. Hezbollah launched approximately 4,000 rockets into Israel over 34 days, causing significant civilian disruption, killing 44 Israeli civilians, and forcing over a million Israelis into shelters. The Israeli military, despite massive air and ground operations, failed to decisively defeat Hezbollah. The war demonstrated both the potency and limitations of Iran's proxy deterrent.

### Degradation of the Proxy Network (2023-2026)

The October 7, 2023 Hamas attack and subsequent Israeli military campaigns significantly degraded Iran's proxy network. Israel's operations in Gaza devastated Hamas's military capabilities. Operations in Lebanon, including the assassination of key Hezbollah leaders, weakened the organization significantly. By early 2026, Hezbollah was described as "a shadow of the force it once was," significantly reducing Iran's forward deterrent capability.

## 7. Direct Confrontation: 2024-2026

### The April 2024 Exchange

On April 1, 2024, Israel bombed an Iranian consulate complex in Damascus, killing several senior IRGC commanders including General Mohammad Reza Zahedi. Iran responded on April 13-14 with its first-ever direct military strike on Israeli territory — launching over 300 drones, cruise missiles, and ballistic missiles. The vast majority were intercepted by Israel, the United States, Jordan, and other regional partners. Israel's retaliatory strike on April 19 was limited, reportedly targeting an air defense battery near Isfahan.

The exchange established important precedents: Iran demonstrated willingness to strike Israel directly (breaking a long-standing taboo), but both sides appeared to calibrate their responses to avoid full-scale war. The October 2024 exchange followed a similar pattern.

### The 12-Day War (June 2025)

The direct war that many had long feared finally erupted on June 13, 2025, when Israel launched a major military operation against Iran. Over 12 days, Israel conducted nearly 360 attacks across 27 Iranian provinces, targeting over 150 distinct locations including nuclear facilities, military installations, and leadership targets.

The war demonstrated Israel's overwhelming air superiority and ability to penetrate Iran's air defenses. On June 22, the United States joined by bombing three Iranian nuclear sites with heavy bunker-buster munitions. A ceasefire was announced on June 24, brokered by Trump.

Iran reported at least 610 citizens killed; Israel reported 28 casualties. The war effectively set back Iran's nuclear program significantly and exposed the limitations of Iran's conventional military capabilities against a technologically superior adversary.

### The 2026 War

In late February 2026, following the Iranian regime's brutal suppression of mass protests (which killed thousands of civilians), the United States and Israel launched a far larger joint operation. Beginning on February 28, around 2,000 airstrikes targeted military, government, and leadership sites across Iran. Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei was assassinated when Israeli jets dropped 30 bombs on his compound, killing him along with family members.

Iran retaliated with over 500 ballistic and naval missiles and almost 2,000 drones targeting Israel and US military bases across the region. As of early March 2026, the conflict was ongoing, with Trump demanding Iran's "unconditional surrender" and stating the war would last "4-5 weeks."

## 8. The Nuclear Dimension: Asymmetric Ambiguity

### Israel's Undeclared Arsenal

Israel maintains a policy of "deliberate ambiguity" regarding its nuclear weapons, neither confirming nor denying their existence. The standard formulation — "Israel will not be the first country to introduce nuclear weapons to the Middle East" — relies on a creative interpretation of "introduce" (meaning Israel will not publicly test or acknowledge its arsenal).

International estimates suggest Israel possesses approximately 90 nuclear warheads, deliverable by aircraft, Jericho ballistic missiles, and submarine-launched cruise missiles from its Dolphin-class submarines. Israel has never signed the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT) and bars the IAEA from inspecting its nuclear facilities at Dimona.

### Iran's Program

Iran, unlike Israel, is a signatory to the NPT and maintains its nuclear program is peaceful. However, the IAEA confirmed Iran pursued weapons-related work under the AMAD Project until 2003. By mid-2025, before Israeli and US strikes, Iran had enriched approximately 972 pounds of uranium to 60% purity — far beyond civilian needs and just a short technical step from weapons-grade 90%.

The IAEA estimated Iran could produce enough weapons-grade uranium for 5-6 nuclear weapons in less than two weeks. Iran operated roughly 18,000 centrifuges with an installed enrichment capacity near 64,000 SWU/year.

### The Hypocrisy Question

Iran has consistently pointed to what it calls the hypocrisy of Israel — a non-NPT state with an undeclared nuclear arsenal facing no international consequences — demanding that Iran, an NPT signatory, be prevented from enriching uranium for purportedly civilian purposes. This argument resonates widely in the developing world and at the Non-Aligned Movement, even among countries that have no sympathy for Iran's government.

From Israel's perspective, the distinction is clear: Israel has possessed nuclear weapons for decades without threatening to destroy any country, while Iran's leaders have explicitly called for Israel's elimination, making Iranian nuclear capability an existential threat in a way that Israel's arsenal is not.

## 9. How Each Side Views the Other

### Iran's View of Israel

The Islamic Republic's official position frames Israel as:

- An illegitimate colonial entity occupying Muslim land
- An extension of Western (particularly American) imperialism in the Middle East
- An oppressor of the Palestinian people
- A nuclear-armed threat that faces no accountability
- The "Little Satan" to America's "Great Satan"

However, the Iranian position has nuances. Iran distinguishes (at least rhetorically) between Judaism as a religion and Zionism as a political ideology. Iran maintains a Jewish community (the largest in the Middle East outside Israel) and reserves seats in parliament for Jewish representatives. The regime's position is officially anti-Zionist rather than antisemitic, though the line between the two is frequently blurred in practice, and Khomeini's own writings contained undeniably antisemitic themes.

### Israel's View of Iran

Israeli strategic culture views Iran as:

- An ideologically driven regime committed to Israel's destruction
- The primary sponsor of terrorism against Israeli civilians (through Hezbollah, Hamas, and Islamic Jihad)
- A nuclear proliferation threat that could fundamentally alter the regional balance of power
- A rational but dangerous adversary whose ideology makes deterrence unreliable
- The organizing principle of a regional "Axis of Resistance" designed to encircle and threaten Israel

Israel's Begin Doctrine — the principle that Israel will not allow any hostile state in the region to acquire nuclear weapons, as demonstrated by the 1981 strike on Iraq's Osirak reactor and the 2007 strike on Syria's Al-Kibar reactor — has been the intellectual framework for its approach to Iran's nuclear program.

## 10. Current Military Balance

### Israeli Advantages

Israel possesses overwhelming qualitative military superiority:

- **Air Force:** F-35I Adir stealth fighters, F-15I Ra'am and F-16I Sufa strike aircraft with precision munitions, extensive aerial refueling capability
- **Missile Defense:** Multi-layered system including Iron Dome (short range), David's Sling (medium range), and Arrow-2/Arrow-3 (ballistic missile defense)
- **Nuclear Deterrent:** Estimated 90 warheads deliverable by multiple platforms
- **Intelligence:** Among the world's most capable intelligence services, with demonstrated ability to operate inside Iran
- **Cyber:** World-class offensive cyber capabilities (demonstrated by Stuxnet)
- **US Support:** Guaranteed American military resupply, intelligence sharing, and diplomatic cover

### Iranian Capabilities

Iran's military strengths are different in character:

- **Missile Arsenal:** The largest ballistic missile arsenal in the Middle East, including the Shahab-3, Emad, and Khorramshahr series, plus hypersonic Fattah missiles
- **Drone Fleet:** Extensive drone capabilities including the Shahed-136 series, proven in combat through Ukraine
- **Proxy Network:** Although degraded, still maintains relationships with Hezbollah, Iraqi militias, Houthis, and Palestinian groups
- **Strategic Depth:** Geographic size (1.6 million km2) and population (88 million) provide inherent resilience
- **Asymmetric Capabilities:** Naval mines, anti-ship missiles, and ability to threaten Strait of Hormuz oil flows

The June 2025 and February-March 2026 conflicts demonstrated that in conventional warfare, Israel (with US support) can overwhelm Iran's air defenses and strike targets with relative impunity. Iran's retaliatory capacity through ballistic missiles and drones, while significant, has proven insufficient to impose unacceptable costs on Israel given its layered missile defense system.
