# Conversational Repair: Comprehensive Research

## Research Summary

Conversational repair is one of the most fundamental mechanisms in human interaction -- the set of practices by which participants deal with problems in speaking, hearing, and understanding. This research covers the foundational framework from conversation analysis, the four types of repair, the preference hierarchy, positional organization, contextual variation, digital repair practices, and specific research on repair in autism.

---

## 1. What Repair Is

### 1.1 Foundational Definition

Conversational repair is defined as **the set of practices by which participants in conversation deal with problems in speaking, hearing, and understanding** (Schegloff, Jefferson & Sacks, 1977). It is the cover term for a range of practices, found in all languages that have been studied, by which trouble of all sorts is managed in interaction.

Repair typically happens in two phases:
1. **Repair initiation**: some trouble is indicated
2. **Repair proper**: the trouble is attended to or "fixed"

Key terminology:
- **Trouble source**: the element of talk that causes a problem -- this could be a mispronunciation, a wrong word, an unclear reference, a misheard phrase, or an ambiguous statement
- **Repair**: the fix itself -- the practice that resolves the trouble
- **Repairable**: the item in the talk that the repair addresses

### 1.2 The Foundational Paper: Schegloff, Jefferson & Sacks (1977)

The paper "The Preference for Self-Correction in the Organization of Repair in Conversation" (Language, 53(2), 361-382) is the foundational text for repair in conversation analysis. It established several key principles:

- An **"organization of repair" operates in conversation**, addressed to recurrent problems in speaking, hearing, and understanding
- Self-correction and other-correction are related **organizationally**, with self-correction preferred to other-correction
- The mechanism that produces a strong empirical skewing in which **self-repair predominates over other-repair** is built into the sequential structure of conversation itself
- Recipients of the trouble source tend to **"withhold" their other-initiations**, expanding the transition-relevance place and providing the producer of the trouble source more space for self-initiation

This paper shifted the field from thinking about "error correction" to understanding "repair" as a much broader organizational system.

### 1.3 Repair Is NOT Just Correction

This distinction is critical. Repair covers:
- **Mishearing**: "Did you say Tuesday?" "No, Thursday."
- **Misunderstanding**: "No, I meant the other project."
- **Word search**: "What's that place called... the one on Main Street... you know..."
- **Self-editing**: "Turn left -- no, right. Turn right."
- **Clarification**: "When you say 'soon,' do you mean today?"
- **Reformulation**: Speaker rephrases for clarity without anyone signaling a problem
- **Inappropriate responses**: Managing when someone says something socially off-track

Participants may encounter frequent occurrences of disfluencies (e.g., "umms," "ahhs"), hesitation markers, misarticulations, use of a wrong word, inaccessibility to a word being searched for, failure to hear or to be heard, and inappropriate understandings by receivers, or other talk troubles. All of these fall under the umbrella of "repair."

As Schegloff emphasized, **repair may deal with more diffuse problems such as impropriety or transgressions of social norms**, including issues of politeness (such as omission of "please") -- not just factual errors.

## 2. The Four Types of Repair

Repair is classified along two dimensions: **who initiates** (identifies the problem) and **who performs** (fixes the problem). This creates a 2x2 matrix:

| | Self-repair (speaker fixes) | Other-repair (listener fixes) |
|---|---|---|
| **Self-initiated** (speaker identifies problem) | Self-Initiated Self-Repair (SISR) | Self-Initiated Other-Repair (SIOR) |
| **Other-initiated** (listener identifies problem) | Other-Initiated Self-Repair (OISR) | Other-Initiated Other-Repair (OIOR) |

### 2.1 Self-Initiated Self-Repair (SISR)

**Definition**: The speaker both identifies and fixes their own problem.

**Frequency**: This is the most common type of repair, accounting for the large majority of all repairs in natural conversation. Schegloff et al. (1977) demonstrated that self-repair predominates over other-repair, and self-initiation predominates over other-initiation.

**Timing**: Usually happens within the same turn (same-turn self-repair), making it the fastest form of repair. It can also occur:
- In the **transition space** just after the turn ends
- In the **third turn** after the problematic utterance

**Initiation markers** (how speakers signal they are about to repair):
- **Cut-offs**: abrupt stopping mid-word ("Turn le-- right")
- **Sound stretches**: elongating a sound while searching ("I went to the sto:::re... the market")
- **Restarts**: beginning the utterance again ("The meeting -- the conference starts at nine")
- **"I mean"**: explicit repair marker ("Call Sarah -- I mean, call Karen")
- **"Sorry" / "No wait"**: self-correction flags ("It's on Tuesday -- sorry, Wednesday")
- **"Well actually"**: self-correction with epistemic upgrade ("It cost twenty -- well actually, twenty-five dollars")
- **"Or rather"**: substitution marker ("She's a doctor -- or rather, a surgeon")
- **Non-lexical perturbations**: "uh," "um," "er" (hesitation markers that signal search or upcoming repair)
- **Pauses**: brief silences that indicate processing trouble

**Repair operations** (what the speaker actually does):
- **Replacement**: substituting one word/phrase for another ("Turn left -- right")
- **Insertion**: adding a word that was missed ("Go to the store -- the grocery store")
- **Deletion**: removing something said ("He was -- anyway, the point is...")
- **Reordering**: rearranging elements ("To John give -- give to John the book")
- **Reformulation**: restating the whole thing differently
- **Abandonment**: giving up on the current formulation and starting fresh

**Examples**:
- "Turn left -- no, right. Turn right." (replacement)
- "The meeting is at two -- three. Three o'clock." (replacement)
- "I'm heading off to Sue's -- I mean Mary's house tonight." (replacement with explicit marker)
- "She lives on... uh... Oak Street. No, Elm Street." (search + replacement)
- "We need to -- what I'm trying to say is..." (abandonment + reformulation)

**Why this is the "preferred" type**: Self-initiated self-repair is the least face-threatening form of repair. The speaker maintains control over the correction. No one else has to point out the problem. It preserves the speaker's competence and autonomy. In Goffman's terms, it protects both positive face (desire to be seen as competent) and negative face (desire for autonomy). The "preference" here is structural/organizational, not psychological -- conversation is organized to give speakers maximum opportunity for self-repair before others step in.

### 2.2 Other-Initiated Self-Repair (OISR)

**Definition**: The listener signals there is a problem, and the original speaker fixes it. This is the second most common type.

**How it works**: The listener uses a **repair initiator** -- a signal that something in the prior turn was unclear, unheard, or not understood. The original speaker then provides the repair.

**The Repair Initiator Hierarchy** (from least specific to most specific):

This is a critical concept. Repair initiators vary in how much information they give the speaker about what went wrong. They are arranged from "weakest" (least specific) to "strongest" (most specific):

1. **Open class / unspecified repair initiators**: Signal a problem but don't identify what it is
   - "Huh?" / "What?" / "Sorry?" / "Pardon?" / "Hm?"
   - These are the "weakest" -- the speaker must figure out what went wrong
   - The speaker typically repeats or reformulates the entire prior turn
   - Example: A: "Put it on the whenst." B: "Huh?" A: "Put it on the windowsill."

2. **Class-specific interrogatives**: Question words that narrow down the trouble type
   - "Who?" / "Where?" / "When?" / "Which one?"
   - These point to the type of information that was unclear
   - Example: A: "She called yesterday." B: "Who?" A: "Maria called yesterday."

3. **Partial repeat + question word**: Repeating part of the trouble source with a question word
   - "She went where?" / "He said what?"
   - More specific -- shows what was heard and what was missed
   - Example: A: "I left it at the schnurgle." B: "At the what?" A: "At the garage."

4. **Partial or full repeat** (with rising intonation): Echo questions
   - "At three?" / "The blue one?" / "Tuesday?"
   - Very specific -- pinpoints the exact element in question
   - Example: A: "Meet me at three." B: "At three?" A: "Yeah, three o'clock, at the café."

5. **Candidate understanding / "you mean" format**: Offering a possible interpretation for confirmation
   - "You mean the blue one?" / "Do you mean Tuesday?" / "So you're saying...?"
   - The most specific type -- does much of the repair work for the speaker
   - Example: A: "Give me that thing." B: "You mean the stapler?" A: "Yeah, the stapler."

**The "Huh?" universal**: Research by Dingemanse, Torreira & Enfield (2013, PLOS ONE) examined 196 instances of "huh?" across 10 languages (Siwu, Italian, Mandarin Chinese, Spanish, Cha'palaa, Icelandic, Lao, Dutch, Murriny Patha, and Australian English) and found it in roughly the same form and function across all of them. They argued it is a "universal word" -- not because it is innate, but because it is shaped by the selective pressures of the conversational environment of other-initiated repair. The technical term for this type of sequence is **"open other-initiated repair"**: repair is initiated not by the speaker of the first turn but by the other participant ("other-initiated"), and the repair initiator signals that there is a problem but leaves open what the problem is ("open").

**Timing of other-initiation**: Research shows that next speakers **withhold** other-initiations of repair to provide an opportunity for current speakers to resolve the trouble via self-repair. Quantitative analysis reveals that the most frequent cases of other-initiation occur after gaps of approximately **700 milliseconds** -- listeners wait before flagging a problem. This withholding is part of the structural preference for self-repair.

**Universal patterns**: Dingemanse et al. (2015, PLOS ONE) conducted a systematic comparison of conversation in 12 languages from 8 language families and found a universal system for the real-time resolution of frequent breakdowns in communication. The system is **frequently used (on average about once per 1.4 minutes in any language)** and has detailed common properties across unrelated languages. Three functionally distinct types of repair initiator are shared and used in the same kinds of contexts. This reveals the fundamentally cooperative nature of human communication.

### 2.3 Self-Initiated Other-Repair (SIOR)

**Definition**: The speaker identifies a problem in their own talk and invites the listener to fix it. The speaker signals trouble; someone else provides the solution.

**Key manifestation -- Word search**: The most common form is the **word search**, where the speaker cannot find the right word and either explicitly or implicitly asks for help:
- "What's that thing called... the... you know..."
- "What's the word I'm looking for?"
- "She works at that place... the... uh..."
- "It's on the tip of my tongue..."

**How it works**: Word search organizations vary:
- Searches can be initiated and carried out **without recruiting co-participants' assistance** (the speaker finds the word themselves -- this would be SISR)
- Co-participation can be **invited to varying degrees** (gestures, gaze, trailing off)
- Searches can be **collaboratively completed** without the speaker's visible attempts to solicit assistance (the listener jumps in)

**Collaborative completion**: When a listener provides the searched-for word, this is called **collaborative completion** or **collaborative turn completion**. Research shows that for some dyads, correction and completion function as interactionally acceptable collaborative repair strategies, maintaining progressivity and a focus on topic development rather than on repair itself.

**Other forms of SIOR**:
- **Checking understanding**: "Does that make sense?" / "Am I making sense?" / "Do you follow?"
- **Asking for the right word**: "What's the word for when something goes up and down?" / "How do you say X in French?"
- **Requesting confirmation**: "Is that right?" / "Did I get that right?"

**Why it is less common**: Self-initiated other-repair requires **vulnerability** -- the speaker must admit they don't know something or can't produce the right formulation. This involves a degree of face threat to the speaker's own positive face (competence). However, because the speaker initiates it themselves, it is less face-threatening than being corrected by someone else.

**Multimodal aspects**: Research on word searches shows they are multimodal events. Speakers use gaze (looking up or away during search, then looking at the listener when inviting help), hand gestures (reaching, pointing, iconic gestures representing the target word's referent), and facial expressions (furrowed brow, "thinking face") to both conduct and invite help with word searches.

### 2.4 Other-Initiated Other-Repair (OIOR)

**Definition**: The listener both identifies and fixes the problem -- outright correction.

**The face-threatening nature**: This is the most face-threatening type of repair because it implicitly says: "You got it wrong, and I know the right answer." It threatens the speaker's **positive face** (their desire to be seen as competent, knowledgeable, and respected).

**Forms**:
- **Direct correction**: "You mean Tuesday, not Thursday." / "It's actually spelled with an 'e'." / "No, that's not right -- it was 2019, not 2020."
- **Softened correction**: "Don't you mean...?" / "I think you mean..." / "Did you mean...?" / "Actually, I believe it's..."
- **Embedded correction** (Jefferson, 1987): A correction slipped into the ongoing talk without making it the focus. The corrector incorporates the correct version into their own turn, as if in passing, without stopping the conversation to expose the error.
  - Example: A: "We went to Portsmith last week." B: "Oh nice, I love Portsmouth. What did you do there?" (B uses the correct pronunciation without flagging it)
- **Exposed correction** (Jefferson, 1987): The correction interrupts the ongoing conversational action to become the business at hand. This interruption to progressivity is noticeable and liable to require an account.
  - Example: A: "We went to Portsmith last week." B: "You mean Portsmouth." A: "Right, Portsmouth."

**Gail Jefferson's (1987) distinction**: In "On exposed and embedded correction in conversation" (Talk and Social Organisation, pp. 86-100), Jefferson identified these two forms:
- **Embedded correction**: does not rise to the conversational surface, does not emphasize a lapse in competence, and does not permit accounting. It corrects **covertly, in a by-the-way manner** that does not interrupt the progressivity of the talk.
- **Exposed correction**: interrupts the ongoing action to become the business at hand. This is more face-threatening because it highlights the error.

**When it occurs more freely**:
- **Asymmetric relationships**: teacher-student, parent-child, expert-novice, doctor-patient
- **Institutional settings**: classrooms, workplaces with clear hierarchies
- **When factual accuracy is paramount**: medical contexts, legal contexts, safety-critical communication
- **Between intimates**: close friends and family sometimes correct each other more freely (though even here, softening is common)

**How it is softened in equal relationships**:
- Hedging: "I think..." / "I believe..." / "If I'm not mistaken..."
- Interrogative form: "Don't you mean...?" / "Isn't it actually...?"
- Humor: making the correction playful
- Attribution to uncertainty: "I could be wrong, but..."
- Providing the correction within an agreement: "Right, and actually it was on Wednesday..."

**Rarity in casual conversation**: OIOR is rare in conversation between equals precisely because it threatens face. The entire repair organization is built to minimize the need for it. Speakers get maximum opportunity for self-repair. Listeners use repair initiators (OISR) rather than just correcting. Even when listeners do correct, they tend to soften or embed the correction.

## 3. The Repair Preference Hierarchy

### 3.1 The Hierarchy

Schegloff, Jefferson & Sacks (1977) identified two structural preferences:
1. **Self-repair is preferred over other-repair** (less face-threatening)
2. **Self-initiation is preferred over other-initiation**

This produces a preference ordering:

**SISR > OISR > SIOR > OIOR**

(Self-Initiated Self-Repair > Other-Initiated Self-Repair > Self-Initiated Other-Repair > Other-Initiated Other-Repair)

From most preferred to least preferred:
1. **SISR**: Speaker catches and fixes it themselves (best outcome -- minimal disruption, no face threat)
2. **OISR**: Listener flags the problem, speaker fixes it (listener respects speaker's competence by letting them do the fixing)
3. **SIOR**: Speaker admits trouble, listener helps (speaker retains some control by initiating)
4. **OIOR**: Listener both identifies and fixes (most face-threatening, most interruptive)

### 3.2 How the Preference is Built Into Conversation Structure

The preference for self-repair is not just a social norm -- it is **built into the sequential organization of turns**. Schegloff et al. demonstrated this through several structural features:

**Opportunity spaces for self-repair come first**:
- **Within the turn** (same-turn repair): The speaker has the first opportunity, right in their own turn, to notice and fix a problem
- **In the transition space**: Even after the turn ends, there is a brief window where the speaker can add a repair before the next speaker takes the floor
- **Third turn**: After the listener's response, the original speaker can repair if the response reveals a misunderstanding

**Other-initiation is systematically delayed**:
- Listeners **withhold** their repair initiations, giving the speaker time to self-repair
- Other-initiations typically occur after gaps of ~700 milliseconds
- This delay expands the transition-relevance place, giving the speaker more room

**Other-repair is positioned last**:
- By the time someone else corrects, all other options have been exhausted
- This is why OIOR is so rare -- it only happens when the trouble source has passed through multiple opportunity spaces for self-repair without being caught

### 3.3 The Politeness Connection

The repair preference hierarchy directly reflects **politeness considerations** (Brown & Levinson, 1987; Goffman, 1967):

- **Positive face** (desire to be respected, seen as competent): Self-repair preserves the speaker's positive face; other-repair threatens it
- **Negative face** (desire for autonomy, not being imposed upon): Other-initiation imposes on the speaker; self-initiation preserves autonomy
- **Face-work**: The entire repair system is a form of face-work -- structurally organized to protect participants' social identities

The connection works because:
- Self-repair lets the speaker maintain the impression of competence
- Other-initiated self-repair (OISR) is a compromise -- the listener flags the problem but lets the speaker do the face-saving work of fixing it
- Other-initiated other-repair (OIOR) is the most dispreferred because it does both the flagging and the fixing, leaving the original speaker no role in the repair

Structurally "preferred" responses in interaction (like accepting an invitation) are done faster than "dispreferred" responses (like rejecting invitations), and repair follows the same pattern -- self-repair is produced quickly, other-repair is delayed, hedged, and softened.

## 4. Positional Organization of Repair

### 4.1 Five Positions for Repair

There are five positions for repair relative to the trouble source:

1. **Same-turn repair**: Repair within the turn containing the trouble source
   - Example: "Turn le-- right, turn right."
   - The most immediate and least disruptive position

2. **Transition space repair**: Repair in the gap between the trouble-source turn and the next turn
   - Example: A: "Meet me at two." [brief pause] "I mean three."
   - Still self-initiated, still relatively non-disruptive

3. **Second position repair (next-turn repair initiation)**: Repair initiated by the other speaker in the next turn
   - Example: A: "I left it at the schnurgle." B: "Huh?" A: "At the garage."
   - This is the standard position for other-initiated repair

4. **Third position repair**: Repair that occurs after the sequence A-B-A, when the trouble becomes apparent from the second pair part
   - Example: A: "Can you pass the salt?" B: [passes pepper] A: "No, sorry, I meant the salt."
   - Schegloff (1992) called this "the last structurally provided defense of intersubjectivity in conversation"

5. **Fourth position repair**: Occurs in a four-part sequence when trouble becomes visible even later
   - Example: A asks, B responds, A acknowledges, but the acknowledgment reveals trouble, and B then initiates repair from this fourth position

### 4.2 The Trajectory of Self-Repair

Self-initiated self-repair can be initiated and carried out:
- **Within the turn** containing the trouble source (same-turn self-repair) -- most common
- **In the transition-relevance place** after the trouble-source turn (transition space repair)
- **After next turn** (third turn repair and third position repair) -- when the speaker realizes from the listener's response that they were misunderstood

The first two positions (within the turn and within the transition space) are the structural opportunities for the speaker to initiate repair before anyone else does.

## 5. Third-Position Repair

### 5.1 Definition and Mechanism

Third-position repair occurs when the trouble **only becomes apparent after the second pair part** -- that is, when the original speaker sees from the listener's response that their first turn was misunderstood.

The sequence:
1. **Turn 1 (A)**: A says something containing a trouble source (but neither party recognizes it yet)
2. **Turn 2 (B)**: B responds based on a misunderstanding of Turn 1
3. **Turn 3 (A)**: A realizes from B's response that B misunderstood, and repairs

### 5.2 Structure of Third-Position Repair

Third-position repairs typically involve two components:
1. **Rejection of the recipient's understanding**: "No, I don't mean X" or "I'm not saying that"
2. **The repair proper**: Repetition, reformulation, specification, or explanation of the trouble source, often prefaced by "I mean"

Common formats:
- "No, I meant..." / "Sorry, I should have been clearer..."
- "I don't mean X, I mean Y"
- "That's not what I meant -- what I meant was..."
- "No no, what I'm saying is..."

### 5.3 Examples

**Example 1 -- Reference confusion**:
- A: "Did you call the doctor?" (meaning the dentist, whom A calls "doctor")
- B: "No, I feel fine. Why would I call the doctor?"
- A: "No, I meant the dentist. Did you call the dentist about your appointment?"

**Example 2 -- Emergency dispatch (from research)**:
- Dispatcher: "What's your address?"
- Caller: "It's on Elm Street."
- Dispatcher: "No, I mean the house number." (third-position repair addressed to the caller's problematic understanding of "address" as meaning just the street name)

### 5.4 Why Third-Position Repair Matters

Schegloff (1992) described third-position repair as **"the last structurally provided defense of intersubjectivity in conversation"** because it is at this point in a sequence that problems revealed to the speaker by the recipient's response can -- if unchecked -- lead to a breakdown in mutual understanding that escapes beyond the local structure of a sequence.

If third-position repair fails or is not attempted, the misunderstanding can cascade, leading to increasingly divergent understandings between participants.

Third-position repair carries special relevance in **institutional interactions** (medical consultations, service encounters, legal proceedings), where misunderstandings may have serious consequences and the activities of such settings may necessitate a third action to make sequences progress to the next phase.

## 6. Repair in Different Contexts

### 6.1 Classroom Repair

Classroom interaction has a distinctive repair organization that differs significantly from everyday conversation:

**IRF Structure**: Classroom talk follows the Initiation-Response-Feedback (IRF) pattern (Sinclair & Coulthard, 1975), where a teacher initiates an exchange, a student responds, and the teacher provides feedback. Repair is embedded in this structure.

**Key differences from casual conversation**:
- **Other-initiation is far more prevalent** than in natural conversation -- teachers regularly flag student errors
- **Other-repair (correction) is normalized**: Teachers routinely correct students without the same face-threatening implications as in peer conversation
- **The preference for self-repair is partially suspended**: While teachers may still prompt self-repair ("Try again," "Are you sure?"), direct correction is more acceptable

**Teacher repair strategies**:
- **Repeating**: echoing the student's answer (possibly with emphasis on the error)
- **Acknowledging a correct answer**: confirming and moving on
- **Expanding or modifying**: building on a partial answer
- **Indicating an incorrect answer**: explicit correction
- **Prompting self-repair**: "Think about that again" / "Almost -- try once more"
- **Praising**: rewarding correct self-repair

**When the social identities of teacher and student are mapped against self- and other-forms of initiation and correction**, it is possible to discern structural preferences of classroom discourse along the repair axis. Teachers in multilingual/L2 interactions face additional challenges regarding asymmetric language choices and skills.

### 6.2 Workplace Repair

Power asymmetry fundamentally shapes repair in professional settings:

**Correcting upward** (subordinate correcting a boss):
- Requires significant face-saving work
- The degree of finesse required depends on the individual and company culture
- Strategies include: framing as a question ("Did you mean Wednesday?"), citing an external source ("The report says..."), offering the correction privately, embedding it in agreement ("Right, and the numbers show...")
- High power distance cultures make upward correction even more delicate

**Correcting laterally** (peer to peer):
- Similar to casual conversation but with professional stakes
- More embedded correction, less exposed correction
- Often framed as collaborative problem-solving rather than correction

**Correcting downward** (boss correcting subordinate):
- More direct correction is normalized, similar to classroom settings
- Still benefits from softening: "Good thought, but actually..." / "Almost -- the figure is..."
- Research on power distance shows that individuals with high perception of power distance may avoid upward correction entirely, while those with low power distance perception are more willing to correct regardless of hierarchy

**Effective workplace repair**: Repair strategies contribute to a collaborative environment by fostering a communication culture where queries and clarifications are encouraged. Effective feedback involves more than just pointing out what someone did wrong -- it requires tact, empathy, and a focus on solutions rather than problems.

### 6.3 Service Encounters

Service encounters (ordering food, buying goods, making appointments) have their own repair patterns:

**Common trouble sources**:
- Mishearing in noisy environments (coffee shops, restaurants)
- Unfamiliar product names or menu items
- Ambiguous quantities or specifications
- Accent or language differences in multilingual service contexts

**Repair patterns**:
- Staff routinely use **confirmation checks**: "A large latte?" / "Two of those?"
- Customers use repair to modify orders: "Actually, make that a small"
- Embedded repair is common: repeating the order with corrections integrated
- In multilingual service settings (e.g., tourism), participants combine repetition of the trouble-source turn with **comprehension-enhancing techniques** such as lexical replacement, rephrasing, and explication of potentially problematic words

Research on fast-food and café service encounters shows that interaction in these settings is heavily **scripted**, with repair mechanisms built into the script ("Would you like fries with that?" is partly a repair mechanism for incomplete orders).

### 6.4 Digital Communication Repair

Digital communication has developed its own distinctive repair practices:

**Asterisk repair (*-repair)**:
- A message consisting solely of the corrected text with an asterisk: *morning (to correct "mroning")
- Research identifies this as a **repair morpheme that has no counterpart in spoken English** but follows discernible rules for use and deployment within the online community
- While *-repair follows many principles of repair used in spoken English, it has **developed natively in an online environment** using an extra-alphabetical character unique to the online community (Collister, 2011, Journal of Pragmatics)

**Message editing**:
- Platforms like WhatsApp, iMessage, Slack, and Discord now allow editing of sent messages
- This creates a new repair modality: the trouble source can be **silently replaced** (like embedded correction in speech)
- Some platforms show "edited" labels, making the repair visible but not exposing the original error
- This changes the repair dynamics: the speaker can self-repair without the listener ever encountering the trouble source

**"Oops" repair**:
- Explicit acknowledgment of error in a follow-up message: "Oops, I meant Thursday not Tuesday"
- Functions like self-initiated self-repair in spoken conversation but in a subsequent message

**Autocorrect-triggered repair**:
- A unique digital phenomenon where the technology introduces the trouble source
- "Ducking autocorrect" -- speakers must repair errors they didn't make
- Often accompanied by meta-commentary about the technology: "stupid autocorrect"

**Typing indicators and read receipts as repair-adjacent features**:
- Typing indicators mimic social cues of face-to-face conversation by signaling someone is about to speak
- Read receipts can create new trouble sources (seen but not responded to)
- These features add layers of complexity to digital repair that don't exist in spoken interaction

**Key differences from spoken repair**:
- **Asynchronous timing**: Repair can happen minutes, hours, or days later
- **Permanence**: The trouble source may remain visible (in chat history) even after repair
- **Editability**: Some platforms allow retroactive repair (editing/deleting messages)
- **Multimodality**: Repair can use emoji, GIFs, voice messages, images alongside text
- **Absence of prosody**: The repair initiator hierarchy must be conveyed without intonation (rising question marks, ALL CAPS, emoji do some of this work)

## 7. Repair and Autism: Research Findings

### 7.1 Overview of the Research

Research on conversational repair in autism spans several decades and includes studies of children, adolescents, and adults across the autism spectrum. The key finding across studies is that **autistic individuals do recognize communicative breakdown and attempt repair**, but the strategies used, the frequency of certain repair types, and the success of repair attempts differ from neurotypical patterns.

### 7.2 Repair Initiation and Performance in Autistic Individuals

**Volden (2004) -- Conversational repair in speakers with autism spectrum disorder** (International Journal of Language & Communication Disorders, 39(2), 171-189):
- People with ASD, across a variety of ages and language levels, **recognized communicative breakdown and responded to requests for clarification** with a wide variety of repair strategies
- The ASD group **could respond to communication failures** and employed various techniques to respond to conversational failures and **add more information as failures persisted**
- However, they were **significantly more likely than language-age-matched controls to respond to a repair request with an inappropriate response**
- As the need to repair became more pressing (second or third attempts), autistic individuals were **more likely to provide a bizarre or inappropriate response or to change the topic completely**
- The ASD group produced **more inappropriate language** during repair sequences

**Key pattern**: The ability to recognize that repair is needed appears to be intact. The difficulty lies in **executing effective repair strategies**, especially under repeated pressure.

### 7.3 Prosodic and Non-Verbal Aspects of Repair in Autism

**Dindar, Korkiakangas, Laitila & Kärnä (2016) -- Interactional challenges in conversations with autistic preadolescents** (Journal of Pragmatics):
- Examined the role of prosodic and non-verbal features in other-initiated repair sequences involving autistic preadolescents
- Found that in **84% of cases, there is no eye contact** between the speaker producing a trouble-source turn and the one who initiates repair
- ASD persons' tendency to **avoid direct eye contact** and occurrences of **deviant prosodic features** in their speech are factors that affect the fluidity of interaction and are related to the creation of understanding problems
- The **two most common causes of understanding problems** were: (1) **overly literal interpretation of speech** and (2) **topical discontinuities** (unexpected topic shifts)
- Children use both verbal and nonverbal resources to initiate the management of apparent trouble, though they are occasionally **unable to effectively communicate the particular source of difficulty** to co-participants

### 7.4 Non-Verbal Repair Strategies

Research on non-verbal repair strategies in autistic children (Keen, 2005, Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders) found:
- Young children with autism **attempted to repair breakdowns** in communication that occurred while interacting with their mothers
- Repair attempts included: gesture, vocalization, showing, pointing, and leading
- However, the **range of non-verbal repair strategies was more limited** compared to typically developing peers
- School-aged autistic children **attempt to clarify their requests** when initially unsuccessful, but their repair attempts tend to be **ambiguous or unintelligible**

**Interesting counter-finding for adults**: Research on autistic adults found that autistic adults **used gesture more than controls to facilitate conversational turn-taking**, demonstrating a novel nonverbal strategy for regulating conversational dynamics. This suggests that autistic individuals may develop **compensatory nonverbal strategies** over time.

### 7.5 Specific Challenges for Autistic Individuals in Repair

Based on the research, autistic individuals face several specific challenges in the repair system:

**Trouble source production**:
- **Prosodic differences**: Atypical intonation, stress, and rhythm can create trouble sources that neurotypical listeners find difficult to parse
- **Topical discontinuities**: Shifting topics without signaling can create confusion that requires repair
- **Literal interpretation**: Responding to the literal rather than intended meaning of an utterance can create the appearance of a trouble source (when the actual problem is a mismatch in pragmatic interpretation)

**Repair initiation**:
- May have difficulty using the appropriate level of specificity in repair initiators (e.g., using "What?" when "You mean the blue one?" would be more effective)
- May not deploy the non-verbal cues (gaze, facial expression) that typically accompany repair initiation
- Research suggests repair initiation may be **more dependent on theory of mind** than general pragmatic capacity -- understanding what the other person doesn't understand requires modeling their mental state

**Repair execution**:
- Under repeated repair requests, may escalate to **inappropriate responses** or **topic changes** rather than providing more specific information
- May rely on **repetition** (saying the same thing again) rather than **reformulation** (saying it a different way) when a repair attempt fails
- The ability to vary repair strategies -- to try a different approach when the first one doesn't work -- appears to be a specific area of difficulty

### 7.6 Echolalia as a Repair-Related Phenomenon

Research has reframed echolalia (repetition of others' speech) as potentially functional in the context of repair:

- **Functional echolalia** may serve as turn-taking, information-providing, labeling, calling, protesting, requesting, completing, affirming, directing, and maintaining social interaction (Sterponi & Shankey, 2014, Frontiers in Psychology)
- Sociolinguistic research has led to **reframings of autistic language practices** such as echolalia, other forms of repetition, topic perseveration, and the negotiation of repair
- Echolalia may function as a **conversation maintenance strategy** or as a **cognitive strategy** during processing difficulty
- What looks like "failed repair" (repeating the same thing) may in fact be a functional attempt to maintain the interaction or signal engagement

### 7.7 The Double Empathy Problem and Repair

The **double empathy problem** (Milton, 2012) fundamentally reframes repair difficulties in autistic-neurotypical interaction:

- Communication breakdowns in autistic-neurotypical interaction are **bidirectional** -- both parties contribute to misunderstanding
- Research by Crompton et al. (2020) showed that **autistic individuals communicate more successfully and experience higher levels of rapport when interacting with other autistic individuals**, compared to autistic-neurotypical interactions
- Neurotypical individuals often **misinterpret autistic communication behaviors**, perceiving them negatively due to unfamiliarity or lack of shared social frameworks (Heasman & Gillespie, 2018)
- This means that the higher frequency of repair in autistic-neurotypical conversation may not indicate a deficit in the autistic participant but rather a **mismatch in communication styles** that requires both parties to adapt

**Implications for understanding repair in autism**:
- Repair difficulties may be partly a function of **cross-neurotype communication**, not inherent deficit
- Both parties bear responsibility for repair in cross-neurotype interaction
- Autistic communication is valid rather than deficient -- the repair system may work differently but not necessarily worse in autistic-autistic interaction
- Therapy that starts from the double empathy framework asks **both parties** to develop mutual understanding, rather than asking only the autistic person to adapt

### 7.8 Practical Implications for Autistic Individuals Learning Repair

Based on the research, several practical considerations emerge:

**Strengths**:
- Autistic individuals **can recognize communicative breakdown** -- the monitoring system works
- They **do attempt repair** across all four types
- They may develop **compensatory strategies** (increased gesture use) over time
- In autistic-autistic interaction, repair may proceed more smoothly

**Areas of difficulty**:
- **Varying repair strategies** when the first attempt doesn't work
- **Using non-verbal cues** (gaze, facial expression) to signal or manage repair
- **Identifying the specific trouble source** for the other person
- **Managing repeated repair requests** without escalating to inappropriate responses
- **Reading non-verbal repair initiators** from others (subtle facial expressions of confusion)

**Strategy considerations**:
- Explicit teaching of the repair initiator hierarchy (from "Huh?" to "Do you mean...?") can be helpful
- Learning to **reformulate rather than repeat** on second and third repair attempts
- Recognizing that repair is normal (once every 1.4 minutes in any language!) and not a sign of failure
- Using explicit verbal repair initiators rather than relying on non-verbal signals
- Developing scripts for common repair situations: "I didn't catch that," "Could you say that differently?", "Let me try saying that another way"

## Research Notes

- The frequency claim that self-repair accounts for "about two-thirds of all repairs" is widely cited in conversation analysis textbooks and teaching materials, though the exact proportion varies across studies and contexts. The key empirical finding from Schegloff et al. (1977) is the strong skewing toward self-repair, demonstrated both through frequency counts and through the structural organization of turn-taking.
- The 700ms delay finding for other-initiated repair comes from Kendrick (2015), published in Frontiers in Psychology.
- The 1.4-minute frequency of other-initiated repair comes from Dingemanse et al. (2015), based on their 12-language sample.
- Research on autism and repair is still developing. Most studies have focused on children and adolescents; adult autistic repair strategies remain under-researched.
- The double empathy framework is increasingly influential but remains debated in clinical settings where deficit-based models still predominate.
