# Category 4: Conversational Structure -- The Invisible Architecture

## The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

---

## How to Read This Document

Categories 1-3 covered what sentences **do** (speech acts), what they **imply** (implicature), and **why** they're indirect (politeness). This category moves from the sentence level to the **conversation level**: how entire conversations are structured from first word to last.

Conversations are not random. They follow a structural architecture as rule-governed as grammar -- but rarely taught explicitly. The rules govern who speaks when, what responses are expected, how problems get fixed, and how conversations begin and end. For autistic individuals who may find conversations unpredictable or exhausting, understanding this architecture is like getting the blueprints to a building you've been navigating in the dark.

## 1. The Turn-Taking System: Who Speaks When

### 1.1 The Discovery

In 1974, Harvey Sacks, Emanuel Schegloff, and Gail Jefferson published what became the most cited paper in the history of the journal *Language*: "A Simplest Systematics for the Organization of Turn-Taking for Conversation." They showed that ordinary conversation -- which seems chaotic and unplanned -- is governed by a precise, rule-based system that speakers navigate in real time.

Their origin story is revealing: Sacks began by analyzing phone calls to a suicide prevention center, noticing that even in these most desperate conversations, speakers followed systematic patterns. The insight generalized: **all** conversation has structure.

### 1.2 Turn Constructional Units (TCUs): What Counts as a Turn

A turn is built from **turn constructional units** -- the smallest pieces of speech that could be a complete turn. Crucially, a turn doesn't have to be a full sentence:

| Type | Example | Complete Turn? |
|------|---------|---------------|
| **Single word** | "Yes." / "Tuesday." / "Really?" | Yes -- if it answers a question or completes an exchange |
| **Phrase** | "In the morning." / "The red one." | Yes -- in the right context |
| **Clause** | "When I got there..." | Possibly -- but signals more is coming |
| **Sentence** | "I went to the store and bought milk." | Yes |
| **Multiple sentences** | A story, explanation, or extended turn | Yes -- if the speaker has the floor |

### 1.3 Transition Relevance Places (TRPs): When Speaker Change Can Happen

A **transition relevance place** is the point where it becomes possible -- not required -- for someone else to start speaking. TRPs are predicted by four types of cues:

| Cue Type | What to Look For |
|----------|-----------------|
| **Syntactic** | The sentence is grammatically complete |
| **Prosodic** | Falling intonation, slowing tempo, final syllable lengthening |
| **Pragmatic** | The speech act is complete (a question has been asked, a request made) |
| **Visual** | Speaker shifts gaze to listener, stops gesturing |

When all four cues align, there's a strong TRP -- a clear signal that the turn is ending. When only some are present, the TRP is weaker and transition is less certain.

### 1.4 The Three Rules of Turn Allocation

At every TRP, three rules apply in order:

**Rule 1**: Current speaker **selects** next speaker (by naming them, directing gaze at them, or asking them a question).
→ If Rule 1 applies, the selected person **must** speak next.

**Rule 2**: If no one is selected, **any participant may self-select**. First starter gets the turn.
→ This creates the competitive pressure that leads to quick transitions.

**Rule 3**: If no one self-selects, current speaker **may continue**.
→ This explains why silence after a possible TRP often leads the same speaker to keep going.

These three rules cycle at every TRP, creating a system that is **locally managed** (organized moment by moment) and **party-administered** (the speakers themselves manage it, with no external moderator).

### 1.5 The 200-Millisecond Puzzle

Research across 10 languages found that the median gap between turns is approximately **200 milliseconds**. This is astonishingly fast -- it takes about 600ms just to plan and initiate a simple word. How can speakers respond in 200ms?

The answer: **speakers begin planning their response while the current speaker is still talking**. They predict when the turn will end and pre-plan their contribution. Turn-taking is not reactive -- it's **anticipatory**.

This has profound implications for autistic learners: if auditory processing is even slightly delayed, or if the cognitive resources needed for prediction are occupied by other demands (sensory processing, social anxiety, interpreting meaning), hitting the 200ms window becomes much harder.

### 1.6 Overlap vs. Interruption

Not all simultaneous talk is the same:

**Overlap**: Both speakers talk at once, usually briefly, often at or near a TRP. This is **normal** and usually not rude. Schegloff identified four types of non-problematic overlap:
- Terminal overlap (starting just as the other speaker finishes)
- Recognitional onset (responding once you've recognized the point)
- Progressional overlap (jumping in when the speaker is struggling to continue)
- Choral overlap (both speakers saying the same thing: "Jinx!")

**Interruption**: One speaker cuts off another **before** a TRP -- before their turn is complete. This IS socially marked and carries meaning (power assertion, urgency, or rudeness).

**Cooperative overlap** is particularly relevant for autistic individuals: ~80% of autistic people (in community surveys) use a style where they interject enthusiastically during someone else's turn -- not to steal the floor but to **show engagement and connection**. This is a legitimate conversational style (linguist Deborah Tannen calls it "high-involvement style"), but NT speakers who prefer strict turn-taking may perceive it as interruption.

### 1.7 Backchannels: The Listener's Job

While one person speaks, the other is expected to provide **backchannels** -- signals that they're listening, following, and engaged:

| Backchannel | Function |
|-------------|----------|
| "Mm-hmm" | "I'm listening, continue" |
| "Yeah" | "I follow / I agree" |
| "Right" | "I understand that point" |
| "Uh-huh" | "Go on" |
| "Really?" | "That's surprising / tell me more" |
| "I see" | "I understand" |
| Nodding | Visual attention signal |

**Backchannels are NOT turns.** They don't take the floor. They support the current speaker.

**Their absence is meaningful.** If someone stops backchanneling -- stops nodding, stops saying "mm-hmm" -- it signals disengagement, confusion, disagreement, or distraction. NT speakers monitor for backchannels constantly and feel uncomfortable when they're missing.

**Why this matters for autistic learners:** Research shows autistic individuals produce fewer and less prosodically varied backchannels (Wehrle et al., 2023). This can cause NT interlocutors to feel the autistic person isn't listening, even when they are. Understanding that backchannels are expected -- and that their absence sends a signal you may not intend -- is practically useful.

### 1.8 Silence and Pauses

Silence in conversation is never truly empty -- it always communicates:

| Duration | What It Typically Signals |
|----------|-------------------------|
| < 200ms | Normal transition gap -- no meaning |
| 200-600ms | Slight hesitation -- may signal thinking |
| 600ms - 1 second | Noticeable pause -- often precedes a dispreferred response |
| > 1 second | Significant -- something is wrong (confusion, reluctance, anger, processing) |

**The dispreferred signal:** A pause of ~561ms (median) before a response is one of the most reliable signals that a dispreferred response is coming (refusal, disagreement, bad news). If someone asks you a question and you hear silence before their response, prepare for a "no."

**Cultural variation:** Finnish and Japanese speakers tolerate longer pauses; Mediterranean speakers tolerate less. English falls in the middle.

### 1.9 Floor Signals: How to Get, Keep, and Give Up the Floor

**Yielding the floor** (signaling your turn is ending):
- Falling intonation
- Syntactic completion
- Shifting gaze to the listener
- Stopping gesturing
- Asking a question

**Holding the floor** (signaling you're not done):
- Filled pauses: "uh," "um" (these explicitly hold the turn)
- Averting gaze (avoiding eye contact reduces the TRP signal)
- Rush-throughs (speeding up at a potential TRP to push past it)
- Latching (starting the next clause immediately without any gap)
- "And..." / "So..." / "But..." at the end of a clause (conjunction chaining)
- Listing ("First... second... third...")

**Seeking the floor** (signaling you want to speak):
- Audible in-breath (signals impending speech)
- Body lean forward
- Making eye contact with the current speaker
- "Well..." / "So..." as a pre-turn marker
- Increased backchanneling (signaling readiness)
- Starting to speak at a TRP (Rule 2: first starter gets the turn)

## 2. Adjacency Pairs: The Building Blocks of Sequence

### 2.1 What They Are

An **adjacency pair** is the most basic unit of conversational sequence: two utterances by two speakers, placed one after the other, where the first creates an expectation for the second.

Five defining properties (Schegloff & Sacks, 1973):
1. Two turns
2. By different speakers
3. Placed adjacently
4. In a fixed order (first pair part, then second pair part)
5. Type-matched (a question makes an answer relevant, not a greeting)

### 2.2 Conditional Relevance: The Engine

When a first pair part is produced, the second pair part becomes **conditionally relevant** -- it is now expected, and its absence is noticeable. This is the key concept:

- **Before** a question is asked: silence is just silence
- **After** a question is asked: silence is the *noticeable absence of an answer*

If someone asks "What time is it?" and gets silence, the silence is not neutral. It will be pursued ("Hello?" "Did you hear me?"), interpreted ("Are you ignoring me?"), or sanctioned ("I asked you a question").

### 2.3 Major Adjacency Pair Types

| First Pair Part | Preferred SPP | Dispreferred SPP |
|----------------|---------------|------------------|
| Question | Answer | Non-answer / "I don't know" |
| Greeting | Greeting | No response |
| Request | Grant | Refuse |
| Offer | Accept | Decline |
| Invitation | Accept | Decline |
| Assessment ("Nice day!") | Agreement | Disagreement |
| Compliment | Accept/Deflect | Reject |
| Complaint | Apology/Remedy | Denial/Justification |
| Accusation | Denial | Admission |
| Farewell | Farewell | No response |
| Summons ("Hey!") | Answer ("What?") | No response |

### 2.4 What Happens When the Expected Response Doesn't Come

When a second pair part is missing, conversation grinds against the expectation:

1. **Repetition**: "What time is it?" [silence] "I said, what time is it?"
2. **Pursuit**: "Did you hear me?" / "Hello?" / "Well?"
3. **Interpretation of silence**: Silence after a question is typically interpreted as a dispreferred response in the making (reluctance, refusal)
4. **Social sanction**: "I asked you a question." / "Are you ignoring me?"

## 3. Preference Organization: The Structural Asymmetry

### 3.1 What "Preference" Means (NOT What You Want)

In conversation analysis, "preference" is a **structural** concept, not a psychological one. It describes the asymmetry between different types of second pair parts -- some are structurally simpler and others are structurally more complex.

**Preferred responses** are:
- Delivered quickly (< 200ms gap)
- Structurally simple and direct
- Short
- Unmitigated (no hedging, no accounts)

**Dispreferred responses** are:
- Delayed (~561ms median gap)
- Preceded by markers: "well...", "uh...", in-breath
- Padded with appreciation, hedging, accounts
- Longer and more complex

### 3.2 The Anatomy of a Dispreferred Response

A fully formed dispreferred response has up to six components:

```
1. Delay / silence          "..." (0.5+ seconds)
2. Preface marker          "Well..." / "Uh..." / "The thing is..."
3. Appreciation / agreement "That sounds great, but..."
4. Account / explanation    "I actually have plans that night."
5. The actual declination   "So I probably can't make it."
6. Alternative / mitigation "But maybe next weekend?"
```

**Compare:**
- Preferred: "Want to come Saturday?" → "Sure!" (one word, immediate)
- Dispreferred: "Want to come Saturday?" → [pause] "Oh... that sounds really fun, and I wish I could, but my parents are visiting this weekend and we already have dinner plans. I'm really sorry. Rain check?" (40 words, delayed, padded)

### 3.3 Why This Matters Enormously for Autistic Learners

**Producing dispreferred responses**: If you need to say no, disagree, or deliver bad news, the NT expectation is the full six-component structure. A bare "No" in response to an invitation is structurally a dispreferred response delivered as if it were preferred -- and it reads as hostile, rude, or contemptuous. It's not the refusal that's the problem; it's the **missing padding**.

**Recognizing dispreferred responses from others**: If someone pauses, says "well...", and starts talking about their schedule, a "no" is coming -- even if they haven't said it yet. Recognizing this pattern lets you anticipate the refusal before it's explicit.

**The detection shortcut**: Pause + "well..." = dispreferred response incoming. This is one of the most reliable patterns in English conversation.

## 4. Repair: When Communication Breaks Down

### 4.1 What Repair Is

**Repair** is the set of practices by which speakers deal with problems in speaking, hearing, and understanding. It covers far more than "correction" -- it includes mishearing, misunderstanding, word searching, self-editing, and clarification.

Research shows repair occurs approximately **once every 1.4 minutes** in conversation across all studied languages. It is not a sign of failure -- it is a fundamental, expected, and constant feature of talk.

### 4.2 The Four Types

Repair is classified by **who identifies the problem** (initiates) and **who fixes it** (performs):

| | **Self-repair** (speaker fixes it) | **Other-repair** (listener fixes it) |
|---|---|---|
| **Self-initiated** (speaker identifies problem) | "Turn left -- no, right." The most common type (~2/3 of all repairs). Least face-threatening. | "What's that word... the thing on the wall..." → Speaker invites listener to supply the word. |
| **Other-initiated** (listener identifies problem) | "Huh?" / "What?" → Speaker then clarifies. Second most common. | "You mean Tuesday, not Thursday." Most face-threatening -- implies "you got it wrong." |

### 4.3 The Preference Hierarchy

Repair has its own preference system:

**Self-repair is preferred over other-repair.**
**Self-initiation is preferred over other-initiation.**

Full hierarchy: Self-Initiated Self-Repair > Other-Initiated Self-Repair > Self-Initiated Other-Repair > Other-Initiated Other-Repair

This hierarchy exists because **correcting someone else is a face-threatening act**. The system is structurally designed to give speakers maximum opportunity to fix their own problems before others step in.

Evidence: When a listener notices a problem, they typically **wait ~700ms** before initiating repair -- giving the speaker a chance to self-correct first. This built-in delay is the preference system in action.

### 4.4 Repair Initiators: How Listeners Signal Problems

When listeners initiate repair, they use signals that range from **open** (non-specific) to **restricted** (specific):

| Initiator Type | Examples | What It Tells the Speaker |
|---------------|---------|--------------------------|
| **Open class** (least specific) | "Huh?" / "What?" / "Sorry?" | "Something was wrong but I don't know what" |
| **Question word** | "Where?" / "When?" / "Who?" | "I understood everything except this one element" |
| **Partial repeat** | "At three?" (with rising intonation) | "Did you say THIS? I want to confirm." |
| **Candidate understanding** | "You mean the blue one?" | "I think you meant THIS -- am I right?" |
| **Full repeat** (most specific) | "You said turn LEFT?" | "I heard X -- is that correct?" |

**Why this matters for autistic learners:** Open-class initiators ("Huh?") are the hardest to respond to because they don't tell you what went wrong. You have to guess. More specific initiators ("You mean Tuesday?") are easier because they tell you exactly where the problem is. If you're initiating repair yourself, using specific forms is more efficient and clearer.

### 4.5 Self-Repair Markers

When speakers catch and fix their own problems, they use recognizable markers:

| Marker | Example |
|--------|---------|
| Cut-off + restart | "Turn le- turn right." |
| "I mean" | "It's on Tuesday -- I mean Wednesday." |
| "Sorry, I meant" | "Sorry, I meant the other one." |
| "No wait" | "No wait, that's not right." |
| "Actually" | "Actually, it's at three, not two." |
| "Or rather" | "She went to Boston -- or rather, Cambridge." |

### 4.6 Repair and Autism

Research specifically on autistic repair sequences shows:
- Autistic individuals may have difficulty with **open-class repair initiators** (the non-specific "Huh?") because they require guessing what went wrong
- In a Finnish study (Dindar et al., 2016), 84% of trouble-source turns in autistic group therapy lacked eye contact between participants, making visual repair cues unavailable
- Echolalia can function as a **repair strategy** -- repeating an utterance while processing its meaning
- The double empathy perspective applies: repair breakdowns in mixed-neurotype conversations may reflect mutual difficulty, not one-sided autistic deficit

## 5. How Conversations Begin

### 5.1 The Opening Sequence

You can't just start talking about what you want to talk about. Openings establish that both parties are **available for and willing to engage in** conversation.

**The universal opening structure:**

```
1. Summons → Answer          "Hey!" → "What's up?"
2. Greeting → Greeting       "How are you?" → "Good, you?"
3. [Topic introduction]      "So, I wanted to ask you about..."
```

### 5.2 Greetings by Relationship Type

| Relationship | Typical Opening | Expected Response |
|-------------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Intimate partner** | "Hey" / "Hey babe" | "Hey" / "Hi love" |
| **Close friend** | "Hey! How's it going?" | "Good! What's up?" |
| **Acquaintance** | "Hi [name], how are you?" | "Good, thanks. You?" |
| **Stranger (approaching)** | "Excuse me, sorry..." | "Yes?" / [attentive look] |
| **Professional** | "Good morning, [title/name]" | "Good morning" |
| **Phone call** | "Hello?" → "Hi, it's [name]" | "Oh hi [name]!" |
| **Email** | "Hi [name]," / "Dear [name]," | (No immediate response expected) |
| **Text** | "Hey" / "Hey are you free?" | "Hey! Yeah what's up" |

### 5.3 The "How Are You?" Problem

"How are you?" is a **phatic greeting ritual** (see Category 19), not a genuine question. The expected response is formulaic:

| Context | Appropriate Responses | Inappropriate Response |
|---------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Casual | "Good!" / "Not bad" / "Fine, you?" | A detailed health report |
| Professional | "Well, thanks. And you?" | A list of complaints |
| Passing (hallway) | "Good!" (while still walking) | Stopping to answer fully |

**However**: As discussed in Category 19, the specific formulaic response carries **ambient state signaling** -- "Great!" vs. "Hanging in there" broadcasts your general state and calibrates subsequent conversation.

### 5.4 How to Open a Conversation with a Stranger

The full sequence:
```
1. Attention-getter:    "Excuse me..."
2. Apology for intrusion: "Sorry to bother you..."
3. Reason/context:      "I'm looking for the train station."
4. Actual request:      "Could you point me in the right direction?"
```

Each step serves a politeness function: the attention-getter is a summons, the apology protects negative face, the reason provides an account, and the request is hedged.

## 6. How Conversations End

### 6.1 The Closing Problem

You cannot simply stop talking. If you do, the other person will interpret the sudden silence as a problem -- confusion, anger, or rudeness. Closing a conversation requires a **negotiated process** where both parties agree to stop.

Schegloff and Sacks (1973) identified this as the "closing problem": how do two people transition from "in conversation" to "not in conversation" without either party feeling cut off?

### 6.2 The Closing Sequence

```
Phase 1: Pre-closing signal
  "Well..." / "So..." / "Anyway..." / "I should probably get going..."

Phase 2: Mutual acceptance
  "Yeah, me too." / "OK, well it was great talking to you."

Phase 3: Arrangement/future reference (optional)
  "We should get lunch sometime." / "Let's catch up soon."

Phase 4: Terminal exchange
  "Bye!" → "Bye!" / "See you!" → "Take care!"
```

### 6.3 Pre-Closing Signals

These are the markers that signal "I'm ready to end this conversation":

| Signal | What It Sounds Like |
|--------|-------------------|
| **"Well..."** (with falling intonation) | The most common pre-closing marker |
| **"So..."** (summarizing) | "So, I think that covers it." |
| **"Anyway..."** | Signals return from a tangent to closing |
| **"I should probably..."** | "I should probably let you go." |
| **"I'll let you go."** | A polite fiction: "I want to stop but I'm framing it as YOUR need." |
| **Body language** | Shifting weight, looking at watch/phone, gathering belongings |
| **Summary statement** | "Well, it was great seeing you!" |

### 6.4 The "We Should Get Together" Problem

"We should get lunch sometime" at the end of a conversation is one of the most confusing utterances in English for autistic individuals. It **looks** like a commissive (a commitment to future action) but usually functions as a **phatic closing ritual** -- a warm way to end the encounter with no actual commitment.

**How to tell the difference:**
- If it's accompanied by concrete details ("How about next Thursday?"), it's genuine
- If it's generic and unspecified ("sometime"), it's usually ritual
- Whether follow-up happens is the definitive test

### 6.5 Strategies for Ending Conversations

**When YOU want to end:**
1. Wait for a natural pause or topic completion
2. Produce a pre-closing: "Well, I should get going..."
3. If the other person accepts: proceed to terminal exchange
4. If they reopen (start a new topic): you may need to be more explicit: "I really do need to head out."

**When the OTHER person won't stop:**
1. Reduce backchanneling (stop saying "mm-hmm")
2. Give shorter responses
3. Use pre-closing markers: "Well..." / "So anyway..."
4. Invoke an external reason: "I have a meeting in five minutes."
5. If needed, be direct: "I'm sorry, I need to go."

## 7. Topic Management

### 7.1 How Topics Are Introduced

Topics don't just appear -- they are **proffered** (offered) using specific devices:

| Device | Example | What It Does |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| **News announcement** | "Guess what?" / "You won't believe this." | Signals upcoming news; creates a slot for a go-ahead ("What?") |
| **Topic initial elicitor** | "Anything new?" / "What's been going on?" | Invites the other person to introduce a topic |
| **Pre-topical sequence** | "Did you hear about...?" | Tests whether the topic is newsworthy before committing to it |
| **Setting talk** | "So I was at the store today..." | Creates a narrative frame for the topic |
| **Disjunctive marker** | "Oh, by the way..." / "Totally different subject, but..." | Explicitly marks a topic change |

### 7.2 How Topics Shift

Topic shifts range from smooth to abrupt:

| Type | Marker | Example | Social Acceptability |
|------|--------|---------|---------------------|
| **Gradual drift** | None needed | Natural evolution from work → commute → traffic → road construction | Fully acceptable |
| **Marked shift** | "Speaking of which..." / "That reminds me..." | Links new topic to old via association | Acceptable |
| **Disjunctive shift** | "Totally different subject, but..." / "Random question..." | Explicitly announces the break | Acceptable if flagged |
| **Unannounced shift** | [No marker] | Jumping from one topic to another without transition | Can seem jarring to NT speakers |

**Autistic relevance:** Research (Ábalos et al., 2025) found autistic speakers made topic shifts that were more "drastic" -- but in autistic-autistic conversation, these same shifts enabled "creativity and humor and greater conversational flow." The issue isn't the shift itself but whether the listener is expecting it.

### 7.3 Safe Topics (The Neutral Inventory)

When you need to make conversation but aren't sure what to talk about:

| Always Safe | Usually Safe | Context-Dependent | Generally Avoid |
|------------|-------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Weather | Weekend plans | Work/projects (at work) | Politics |
| Recent events (neutral) | Food/restaurants | Shared hobbies | Religion |
| Travel | Entertainment | Mutual acquaintances | Money/salary |
| Local happenings | Sports (if shared) | Family (if close) | Health problems |
| Shared environment | Technology | Current events | Appearance/weight |

## 8. The Autistic Experience with Conversation Structure

### 8.1 Turn-Taking

The most rigorous study (Wehrle et al., 2023) found that autistic adults share the same preference for short-gap transitions as NT speakers but need a **warm-up period** -- longer gaps at the start of conversation that normalize as the interaction progresses. This suggests a **calibration** difference, not a fundamental inability.

Sensory processing is a significant factor: auditory processing delays of even tens of milliseconds can make the 200ms transition window much harder to hit. This is compounded when visual, proprioceptive, and environmental sensory demands compete for processing resources.

### 8.2 Cooperative Overlap as an Autistic Style

The autistic tendency to interject mid-turn is often not interruption but **cooperative overlap** -- enthusiasm-driven participation that signals engagement. In autistic-autistic conversation, this style flows naturally. In mixed-neurotype conversation, it's misread as rudeness.

### 8.3 The Backchannel Gap

Autistic individuals produce fewer and less varied backchannels. This creates a feedback loop: the NT speaker feels unheard, becomes uncomfortable, and may disengage -- when the autistic listener was fully engaged but not signaling it in the expected way.

### 8.4 Opening and Closing Difficulties

**Openings**: The "cold start" problem -- beginning a conversation requires navigating the phatic ritual (greetings, "How are you?") before reaching substantive content. This ritual can feel pointless, creating reluctance to initiate.

**Closings**: Recognizing closing signals ("Well...") and producing the expected closing sequence can be challenging. The result may be conversations that end "abruptly" from the NT perspective -- the autistic speaker simply stops when the content is complete, without the expected wind-down.

### 8.5 Repair Strategies

The most challenging repair type for autistic individuals is **open-class other-initiated repair** ("Huh?" / "What?") -- because it doesn't specify what went wrong. More specific repair initiators ("You mean Tuesday?") are easier to respond to.

**Practical strategy**: When you don't understand something, use specific repair initiators rather than open ones. Instead of "What?" try "Sorry, did you say three o'clock or four?"

### 8.6 The Double Empathy Perspective

Crompton et al.'s research (2020, replicated 2025 in Nature Human Behaviour) showed that conversational structure works **equally well** in autistic-autistic pairs as in NT-NT pairs. The structural breakdowns occur specifically in **mixed-neurotype** conversations. Autistic-autistic conversations have their own structural norms: tolerance for longer pauses, acceptance of topic shifts, reduced phatic requirements, cooperative overlap as default.

## 9. Practical Toolkit

### 9.1 Conversation Opening Script

```
[Getting attention]  "Hey, [name]" / "Excuse me"
[Greeting ritual]    "How are you?" → [Listen for response; reciprocate: "Good, you?"]
[Bridge to topic]    "So, I wanted to ask you about..." / "Have you heard about...?"
```

### 9.2 Conversation Closing Script

```
[Wait for natural pause]
[Pre-closing]        "Well, I should probably get going." / "Anyway, it was great talking."
[If accepted]        → "Let's catch up soon." (ritual, unless you specify a time)
[Terminal exchange]   "Bye!" / "Take care!" / "See you later!"
```

### 9.3 Dispreferred Response Template

When you need to say no:
```
[Brief pause]        (Don't respond instantly -- the pause is expected)
[Appreciation]       "That sounds really fun..." / "I appreciate the offer..."
[Account]            "...but I have [reason]."
[Apology]            "I'm sorry."
[Alternative]        "Maybe another time?" / "How about next week?"
```

### 9.4 Repair Strategies

| Problem | Strategy |
|---------|---------|
| You didn't hear | "Sorry, could you repeat that?" (specific > "Huh?") |
| You didn't understand | "Sorry, what do you mean by [specific part]?" |
| You said the wrong thing | "Wait, I mean [correction]." / "Sorry, not X -- Y." |
| You need processing time | "Let me think about that for a second." (explicit floor-hold) |
| The other person seems confused | "Does that make sense?" / "Let me put it another way." |

### 9.5 Group Conversation Survival Guide

| Challenge | Strategy |
|-----------|---------|
| Can't find an opening to speak | Wait for a TRP, take an audible in-breath, lean forward slightly |
| Keep getting talked over | Raise your hand slightly, or say "[Name], I wanted to add something" |
| Lost track of the topic | "Sorry, can you back up? I want to make sure I'm following." |
| Want to change the subject | "That reminds me..." or "Totally different topic, but..." |
| Don't know when it's your turn | Watch for gaze directed at you (speaker selecting you) |

## 10. Key Takeaways

1. **Conversation has architecture.** Turn-taking, adjacency pairs, preference organization, repair, openings, closings -- these are all structural systems with consistent rules.

2. **The 200ms window is real but not obligatory.** Speakers anticipate turn endings and pre-plan. If you need more time, you can hold the floor with "um," "uh," or "Let me think..."

3. **Silence is never neutral.** After a first pair part, silence communicates. A pause before responding signals a dispreferred response. Extended silence signals a problem.

4. **Dispreferred responses need padding.** "No" is a complete sentence but not a complete dispreferred response. The expected structure is: pause + appreciation + account + apology + alternative.

5. **Repair is normal.** It happens every 1.4 minutes on average. Use specific repair initiators ("Did you say Tuesday?") rather than open ones ("Huh?").

6. **Openings and closings are rituals.** They follow predictable scripts. Learning the scripts makes the most anxiety-inducing parts of conversation (starting and stopping) manageable.

7. **Your conversational style is valid.** Cooperative overlap, direct topic shifts, and reduced phatic ritual are features of autistic communication that work well in autistic-autistic interaction. Understanding NT expectations gives you the choice of when to adapt.
