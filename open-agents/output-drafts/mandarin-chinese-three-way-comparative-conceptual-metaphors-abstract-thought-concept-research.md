# Conceptual Metaphors — How Mandarin Maps Abstract Thought

## Overview

Conceptual Metaphor Theory (CMT), developed by George Lakoff and Mark Johnson in *Metaphors We Live By* (1980), proposes that abstract thought is fundamentally structured by metaphorical mappings from concrete, embodied experience. We understand TIME through SPACE, ARGUMENT through WAR, IDEAS through FOOD — not as literary ornament but as the deep cognitive architecture of meaning itself.

Contemporary Mandarin Chinese is one of the most revealing test cases for CMT because it operates with distinctive features that differ from *both* Traditional Chinese (Cantonese, literary Chinese, Taiwanese Guoyu) *and* English. This three-way comparison reveals:

1. **Vertical time metaphors** (上 shàng 'up' = earlier, 下 xià 'down' = later) alongside horizontal ones — an axis available in all Chinese varieties but absent in English, generating Lera Boroditsky's famous and contested hypothesis about linguistic relativity
2. **Heart-mind unification** (心 xīn) — emotion and cognition housed in a single organ-concept where English separates "heart" from "brain/mind." This is shared across all Chinese varieties and has parallels in Arabic (qalb) but not in English or most European languages
3. **Body-organ emotion mappings** grounded in Traditional Chinese Medicine — liver = anger (肝火), gallbladder = courage (胆), spleen = temper (脾气) — a systematic distributed network where English concentrates almost everything in the heart
4. **Compound ideographs as frozen metaphors** — characters like 明 (sun+moon = bright) and 好 (woman+child = good) that visually encode metaphorical reasoning in a way no alphabetic script can
5. **Shared universal metaphors** (ANGER IS HEAT, MORE IS UP, KNOWING IS SEEING) alongside culture-specific ones (面子 miànzi 'face', 关系 guānxi 'relationship web')
6. **Productive metaphorical extension of 吃 chī (eat)** — a single verb that extends metaphorically to suffering, loss, surprise, jealousy, and more, illustrating how Mandarin creates new meanings from concrete experience

The three-way frame is essential because many of these features are shared across all Chinese varieties (heart-mind, body-organ metaphors, compound ideographs) while others show Mandarin-specific developments (PRC-era metaphorical coinages, simplified-character effects on visual metaphor). English provides the contrasting baseline as a non-tonal, alphabetic, subject-prominent language with a different metaphorical repertoire.

## 1. Vertical Time: The Dual-Axis System and the Boroditsky Controversy

### The Linguistic Facts (Confirmed Across All Chinese Varieties)

All Chinese varieties use vertical spatial metaphors for time. This is not Mandarin-specific — it reflects a shared Chinese conceptualization:

**Vertical axis (上/下, up/down):**
- 上个星期 (shàng gè xīngqī) — "upper week" = last week
- 下个月 (xià gè yuè) — "lower month" = next month
- 上午 (shàngwǔ) — "upper noon" = morning (forenoon)
- 下午 (xiàwǔ) — "lower noon" = afternoon
- 上半年 (shàng bànnián) — "upper half-year" = first half of the year
- 下学期 (xià xuéqī) — "lower semester" = next semester
- 上集 (shàng jí) — "upper volume" = first volume/episode
- 下集 (xià jí) — "lower volume" = next volume/episode

The mapping: 上 (shàng, up) = earlier in time; 下 (xià, down) = later in time. This may originate from the traditional vertical writing direction (top-to-bottom), where earlier text literally appears *above* later text.

**Horizontal axis (前/后, front/back) — PRIMARY in all Chinese varieties:**
- 前天 (qiántiān) — "front-day" = day before yesterday
- 后天 (hòutiān) — "back-day" = day after tomorrow
- 以前 (yǐqián) — "before-front" = in the past
- 以后 (yǐhòu) — "after-back" = in the future
- 前年 (qiánnián) — "front-year" = year before last

**Critical finding**: Chinese speakers use horizontal time metaphors MORE FREQUENTLY than vertical ones. Chen (2007) found that in corpus data, approximately 64% of spatial-temporal metaphors were horizontal (front/back) while only about 36% were vertical (up/down). The horizontal axis is the *primary* system in both Chinese and English; the vertical axis is an *additional* system available in Chinese but not in English.

### Cantonese Comparison

Cantonese uses the same dual-axis system:
- 上個禮拜 (soeng5 go3 lai5 baai3) — "upper week" = last week
- 下個月 (haa6 go3 jyut6) — "lower month" = next month
- 之前 (zi1 cin4) — "before" (horizontal axis)

The vertical time system is pan-Chinese, not Mandarin-specific.

### The Boroditsky Hypothesis and Replication Controversy

**The original claim**: Lera Boroditsky (2001) published a landmark study arguing that Mandarin speakers conceptualize time along a vertical axis, while English speakers use a horizontal axis, and that Chinese-English bilinguals showed a vertical cognitive bias even when tested in English. This was presented as evidence for the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis (linguistic relativity).

**The replication crisis:**
- **Chen (2007)**: "Do Chinese and English speakers think about time differently? Failure of replicating Boroditsky (2001)" — four separate experimental conditions, no vertical bias found
- **January & Kako (2007)**: Failed to replicate
- **Tse & Altarriba (2008)**: No evidence of vertical time bias

**Supporting studies:**
- **Boroditsky, Fuhrman & McCormick (2011)**: Provided supporting evidence with different methodology
- **Fuhrman et al. (2011)**: Found vertical time effects in Chinese speakers
- **Miles et al. (2011)**: Found evidence for vertical time in Chinese speakers

**Current consensus**: The vertical time metaphor is **real as a linguistic pattern** (nobody disputes that Chinese uses 上/下 for time) but **contested as a cognitive influence** (whether it shapes non-linguistic temporal reasoning remains unresolved). The relationship between language-specific metaphors and cognition appears to be task-dependent, context-sensitive, and modulated by bilingualism, instruction language, and cultural priming.

### Three-Way Comparison: Time Metaphor Axes

| Axis | Mandarin | Cantonese / Traditional Chinese | English |
|------|----------|---------------------------------|---------|
| **Horizontal (front/back)** | PRIMARY: 前 front=past, 后 back=future | PRIMARY: same system | PRIMARY: "look forward," "look back" |
| **Vertical (up/down)** | SECONDARY: 上 up=earlier, 下 down=later | SECONDARY: same system | NOT AVAILABLE for time |
| **Horizontal (left/right)** | Not common linguistically (but culturally in timelines) | Not common | Not common linguistically (but left-to-right reading creates timeline bias) |

**The pattern insight**: Chinese has *more spatial axes available* for temporal metaphor than English. It is not that Chinese "thinks about time vertically" — it is that Chinese has an additional mapping that English lacks.

## 2. Heart-Mind Unification: 心 (Xīn)

### The Concept (Pan-Chinese)

In Chinese philosophy and linguistic practice, 心 (xīn) refers simultaneously to the heart (physical organ) and the mind (cognitive/emotional faculty). The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy notes: "The xin is the nearest equivalent to the English term for mind in classical Chinese philosophy" and is "frequently translated as 'heart-mind' because ethical guidance in Chinese thought arises from both the cognitive function of the mind and the affective states attributed to the heart."

This is not a Mandarin innovation — it is inherited from Classical Chinese and shared across all Chinese varieties:
- Mandarin: 心 (xīn)
- Cantonese: 心 (sam1)
- Taiwanese Hokkien: 心 (sim)
- Classical Chinese: 心 with the same unified semantics

### Grammaticalized in the Writing System

The heart radical (心 as standalone, 忄 as left-side variant, ⺗ as bottom variant) appears across the entire cognitive-emotional spectrum:

**Cognitive characters ("mind" in English):**

| Character | Pinyin | Meaning | Radical form |
|-----------|--------|---------|-------------|
| 想 | xiǎng | To think/ponder | 心 (bottom) |
| 思 | sī | To contemplate | 心 (bottom) |
| 忆 | yì | To remember | 忄 (left) |
| 忘 | wàng | To forget | 心 (bottom) |
| 意 | yì | Intention/meaning | 心 (bottom) |
| 念 | niàn | To think of | 心 (bottom) |
| 悟 | wù | To comprehend | 忄 (left) |
| 慧 | huì | Wisdom | ⺗ (bottom) |

**Emotional characters ("heart" in English):**

| Character | Pinyin | Meaning | Radical form |
|-----------|--------|---------|-------------|
| 情 | qíng | Feeling/emotion | 忄 (left) |
| 怒 | nù | Anger | 心 (bottom) |
| 恐 | kǒng | Fear | 心 (bottom) |
| 悲 | bēi | Sorrow | 心 (bottom) |
| 愁 | chóu | Worry | 心 (bottom) |
| 恨 | hèn | Hatred | 忄 (left) |
| 愉 | yú | Pleased | 忄 (left) |
| 惧 | jù | Dread | 忄 (left) |

**Dispositional characters (personality):**

| Character | Pinyin | Meaning | Radical form |
|-----------|--------|---------|-------------|
| 性 | xìng | Nature/character | 忄 (left) |
| 忍 | rěn | To endure | 心 (bottom) |
| 惯 | guàn | Habitual | 忄 (left) |
| 恒 | héng | Perseverance | 忄 (left) |

### Three-Way Comparison: Seat of Cognition and Emotion

| Feature | Mandarin / All Chinese | Traditional Chinese / Literary | English |
|---------|----------------------|-------------------------------|---------|
| **Basic unit** | 心 = unified heart-mind | Same unified concept | Two separate concepts: "mind" vs. "heart" |
| **Thinking and feeling** | Same domain (both 心) | Same | Separate domains ("I think" vs. "I feel") |
| **Reason vs. emotion** | Complementary aspects of 心 | Same | Often opposed: "head vs. heart," "reason vs. passion" |
| **Seat of cognition** | Heart/chest (心) | Heart/chest | Head/brain |
| **Writing system encoding** | Visible: heart radical in both cognition and emotion words | Same | Not visible: "think" and "feel" share no morphological marker |

### Cross-Linguistic Parallels

The heart-mind unification is not unique to Chinese:
- **Arabic**: قلب (qalb, heart) serves as seat of both understanding and feeling in Quranic/Classical Arabic
- **Japanese**: 心 (kokoro) preserves the unified semantics borrowed from Chinese
- **Korean**: 마음 (maeum) functions similarly as heart-mind

The Chinese-Arabic parallel is particularly striking: both traditions unify cognition and emotion in the heart, while European languages (English, French, German) consistently split them. This suggests the heart/mind duality is a European cultural construction, not a universal cognitive divide.

## 3. Body-Organ Emotion Mappings: The TCM Framework

### The Full Organ Map

Chinese maps emotions and psychological states onto internal organs far more systematically than English. This system derives from Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), where the five major organs (五脏 wǔzàng) each associate with specific emotions and TCM elements:

| Organ | Chinese | TCM Emotion | TCM Element | Example Expressions |
|-------|---------|-------------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Heart** (心 xīn) | 心 | Joy (喜 xǐ) + all cognition | Fire | 心烦 (xīnfán, heart-vexed = agitated), 心疼 (xīnténg, heart-ache = feel sorry for someone) |
| **Liver** (肝 gān) | 肝 | Anger (怒 nù) | Wood | 肝火旺 (gānhuǒ wàng, liver-fire-flourishing = irascible), 大动肝火 (dàdòng gānhuǒ, greatly-move liver-fire = extremely angry) |
| **Spleen** (脾 pí) | 脾 | Worry/pensiveness (思 sī) | Earth | 脾气 (píqi, spleen-qi = temper/temperament), 脾气暴躁 (píqi bàozào = hot-tempered) |
| **Lungs** (肺 fèi) | 肺 | Sadness/grief (悲 bēi) | Metal | 肺腑之言 (fèifǔ zhī yán, lungs-bowels-of-words = heartfelt words from the depths) |
| **Kidneys** (肾 shèn) | 肾 | Fear (恐 kǒng) | Water | Less common in everyday speech; primarily in TCM context |
| **Gallbladder** (胆 dǎn) | 胆 | Courage | — | 胆大 (dǎndà, gallbladder-big = courageous), 胆小 (dǎnxiǎo, gallbladder-small = cowardly), 胆量 (dǎnliàng, gallbladder-capacity = nerve/guts) |
| **Intestines** (肠 cháng) | 肠 | Deep feeling/sentiment | — | 断肠 (duàncháng, severed-intestines = heartbroken), 牵肠挂肚 (qiānchángguàdù, pull-intestines-hang-belly = deeply worried) |

### Three-Way Comparison: Organ-Emotion Granularity

| Emotional Domain | Chinese Organ | English Expression | English Organ? |
|-----------------|---------------|-------------------|----------------|
| General emotion | 心 (heart) | "heartbroken," "heartfelt" | Heart |
| Anger | 肝 (liver) | "burning with rage" (no organ) | None specific (archaic: "livid" from liver) |
| Courage | 胆 (gallbladder) | "You've got guts" | Guts (vague) |
| Temperament | 脾 (spleen) | "bad-tempered" (no organ) | None (archaic: "vent your spleen") |
| Deep sorrow | 肠 (intestines) | "gut-wrenching" | Gut (occasional) |
| Grief | 肺 (lungs) | "sigh" (no organ reference) | None |
| Fear | 肾 (kidneys) | "scared to death" (no organ) | None |
| Intuition | — | "gut feeling" | Gut |

**Key difference**: English has a residual organ-emotion system (heart, gut) with traces of others (spleen, liver). Chinese has a *productive, systematic* organ-emotion network where new expressions are generated following the pattern. The Chinese system maps 7+ organs to distinct emotional domains; English concentrates almost everything in the heart.

### Cantonese Comparison

Cantonese shares the same TCM-grounded organ-emotion system:
- 肝火盛 (gon1 fo2 sing6) — "liver-fire flourishing" = irascible
- 膽大 (daam2 daai6) — "gallbladder-big" = courageous
- 脾氣 (pei4 hei3) — "spleen-qi" = temper

This is a pan-Chinese phenomenon, not Mandarin-specific. Traditional Chinese and Mandarin share the same metaphorical organ map because both inherit from the TCM tradition that predates the Mandarin/Cantonese split.

## 4. Compound Ideographs as Frozen Metaphors

### Characters That Visually Encode Metaphor (会意字 huìyìzì)

Chinese has a category of characters — compound ideographs — where the visual composition of the character *is itself a metaphor*:

| Character | Components | Visual Logic | Meaning | Metaphorical Mapping |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|---------|---------------------|
| 明 (míng) | 日 (sun) + 月 (moon) | Two brightest objects combined | Bright, clear | BRIGHTNESS = COMBINED LIGHT SOURCES |
| 好 (hǎo) | 女 (woman) + 子 (child) | Mother with child | Good | GOODNESS = MOTHER-CHILD BOND |
| 安 (ān) | 宀 (roof) + 女 (woman) | Woman under a roof | Peace/safety | PEACE = SHELTERED WOMAN |
| 家 (jiā) | 宀 (roof) + 豕 (pig) | Pig under a roof | Home/family | HOME = DOMESTIC ANIMALS SHELTERED |
| 信 (xìn) | 人 (person) + 言 (speech) | Person standing by words | Trust/believe | TRUST = PERSON + SPEECH |
| 休 (xiū) | 人 (person) + 木 (tree) | Person leaning on tree | Rest | REST = PERSON + SUPPORT |
| 森 (sēn) | 木 (tree) x 3 | Three trees together | Dense forest | DENSITY = MULTIPLICATION |
| 林 (lín) | 木 (tree) x 2 | Two trees | Grove/forest | COLLECTIVE = DOUBLING |
| 从 (cóng) | 人 (person) x 2 | One person following another | Follow/from | FOLLOWING = ONE BEHIND ANOTHER |
| 众 (zhòng) | 人 (person) x 3 | Three people | Crowd/masses | MULTITUDE = MANY PEOPLE |

### Important Caveat: Folk Etymology vs. Actual Etymology

Modern etymological research has shown that many traditional compound-ideograph analyses are folk etymologies. A significant number of characters traditionally explained as compound ideographs actually originated as semantic-phonetic compounds whose phonetic element became obscured by pronunciation changes over millennia.

For example:
- 信 (trust): traditionally analyzed as "person" + "speech" = trustworthiness, but possibly originally a phonetic compound
- 家 (home): the "pig under roof" analysis may reflect folk reasoning rather than original composition intent

However, even when etymologically questionable, the visual-metaphorical reading:
1. **Functions as a powerful mnemonic** for learners
2. **Resonates as cultural logic** for native speakers
3. **Has been taught as cultural truth** for centuries in Chinese education
4. The visual metaphor may not be historically primary but is *cognitively real and culturally productive*

### Three-Way Comparison: Visual Metaphor in Writing

| Feature | Mandarin (Simplified) | Traditional Chinese | English |
|---------|----------------------|--------------------|---------| 
| Visual metaphor in characters | Present but sometimes obscured by simplification (e.g., 众 from 眾) | Fully preserved original forms | Impossible — alphabetic letters encode sounds, not meanings |
| Metaphorical reading of characters | Active (taught in schools, used mnemonically) | Active (same tradition) | Not applicable |
| Character simplification effects | Some metaphorical transparency reduced (e.g., 愛 → 爱 — "love" lost 心 heart component in simplification) | Original visual metaphors intact | N/A |

**Simplification and metaphor loss**: A notable three-way comparison point is that simplified characters sometimes *reduce* metaphorical transparency. The traditional 愛 (love) contains 心 (heart) at its center — love literally has heart in it. The simplified 爱 removed the heart component. This is a case where Mandarin (as used in the PRC) has *less* visual-metaphorical transparency than Traditional Chinese.

### Nothing Like This in Alphabetic Scripts

This visual-metaphorical encoding layer is unique to logographic/morphographic writing systems. In English, the word "bright" gives no visual clue to its meaning — the letters b-r-i-g-h-t encode sounds, not concepts. In Chinese, 明 *visually depicts* the metaphorical logic. The writing system is not just speech transcription — it is a parallel meaning-encoding system with its own metaphorical layer.

## 5. Universal vs. Culture-Specific Metaphors

### Universal Metaphors (Found Across All Three Systems)

Research in cognitive linguistics has identified metaphors that appear in virtually all languages studied, supporting the embodied cognition hypothesis that they arise from universal bodily experience:

| Metaphor | Mandarin | Cantonese/Traditional | English | Grounding |
|----------|----------|-----------------------|---------|-----------|
| ANGER IS HEAT/FIRE | 发火 (fāhuǒ, emit-fire = get angry) | 發火 (faat3 fo2) | "burning with rage" | Body temperature rises when angry |
| HAPPINESS IS UP | 高兴 (gāoxìng, high-excitement = happy) | 高興 (gou1 hing3) | "I'm on top of the world" | Upright posture correlates with positive affect |
| SADNESS IS DOWN | 低落 (dīluò, low-fall = depressed) | 低落 (dai1 lok6) | "I'm feeling down" | Slumped posture correlates with negative affect |
| MORE IS UP | 涨价 (zhǎngjià, rise-price) | 漲價 (zoeng2 gaa3) | "prices are rising" | Physical piling up = more |
| KNOWING IS SEEING | 看出来 (kànchūlái, look-out-come = realize) | 睇到 (tai2 dou2) | "I see what you mean" | Vision = primary knowledge source |
| LIFE IS A JOURNEY | 人生道路 (rénshēng dàolù, life road) | Similar | "life's journey" | Forward motion through time |
| IDEAS ARE FOOD | 消化 (xiāohuà, digest = absorb an idea) | Same | "food for thought," "digest information" | Mental processing = physical digestion |

### Widespread but Variable Metaphors

| Metaphor | Mandarin | English | Variation |
|----------|----------|---------|-----------|
| TIME IS SPACE | Both horizontal (前/后) AND vertical (上/下) | Horizontal only (forward/back) | Axis availability differs |
| IMPORTANCE IS SIZE | 大事 (dàshì, big-matter = important event) | "big deal" | Very similar |
| DIFFICULTIES ARE BURDENS | 负担 (fùdān, bear-burden = burden) | "heavy burden" | Very similar |

### Culture-Specific Metaphors (Chinese But Not English)

| Metaphor | Chinese Expression | Meaning | English Parallel? |
|----------|-------------------|---------|------------------|
| GALLBLADDER IS COURAGE | 胆大 (dǎndà, big-gallbladder) | Brave | Vestigial: "lily-livered" inverts it |
| SPLEEN IS TEMPERAMENT | 脾气 (píqi, spleen-qi) | Temper | Archaic: "vent your spleen" |
| FACE IS SOCIAL STANDING | 面子 (miànzi, face) | Social prestige | "save face" borrowed FROM Chinese |
| RELATIONSHIP IS WEB | 关系 (guānxi, relation-connection) | Social network | No equivalent concept |
| EATING IS EXPERIENCING | 吃苦 (chīkǔ, eat-bitter = endure hardship) | Broad metaphorical extension | "swallow your pride" (very limited) |
| HEART-MIND UNITY | 心 spans cognition + emotion | Single organ for thinking and feeling | Heart and mind are separate |

### Culture-Specific Metaphors (English But Not Chinese)

| Metaphor | English Expression | Chinese Equivalent? |
|----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| ARGUMENT IS WAR | "attack a position," "defend a claim" | 辩论 (debate) — less militaristic |
| TIME IS MONEY | "spend time," "invest time," "waste time" | 浪费时间 (waste time) — partial overlap, but less systematic |
| THEORIES ARE BUILDINGS | "construct an argument," "foundation," "framework" | Partial overlap |
| LOVE IS A JOURNEY | "we've come a long way," "at a crossroads" | Limited parallel |

## 6. The 吃 (Chī, Eat) Productivity Tree

### A Case Study in Metaphorical Extension

The verb 吃 (chī, eat) is one of the most metaphorically productive words in Mandarin Chinese. From its concrete meaning of physically consuming food, it extends metaphorically into domains of experience, suffering, advantage, and emotional impact:

| Expression | Literal | Metaphorical Meaning | Metaphorical Logic |
|-----------|---------|---------------------|--------------------|
| 吃苦 (chīkǔ) | Eat bitter | Endure hardship | SUFFERING IS CONSUMING BITTER THINGS |
| 吃亏 (chīkuī) | Eat loss | Suffer a loss/be disadvantaged | LOSS IS SOMETHING CONSUMED |
| 吃惊 (chījīng) | Eat surprise | Be startled/shocked | SURPRISE IS CONSUMED |
| 吃醋 (chīcù) | Eat vinegar | Be jealous (romantic) | JEALOUSY IS CONSUMING SOUR THINGS |
| 吃力 (chīlì) | Eat strength | Be strenuous/effortful | EFFORT IS CONSUMING ENERGY |
| 吃香 (chīxiāng) | Eat fragrance | Be popular/in demand | POPULARITY IS CONSUMING PLEASANT THINGS |
| 吃透 (chītòu) | Eat through | Thoroughly understand | UNDERSTANDING IS COMPLETELY CONSUMING |
| 吃官司 (chī guānsī) | Eat lawsuit | Be sued/face legal trouble | LEGAL TROUBLE IS CONSUMED |
| 吃药 (chīyào) | Eat medicine | Take medicine | (Literal extension) |
| 吃素 (chīsù) | Eat plain/vegetarian | Be vegetarian / be easy to bully | BEING PUSHOVER = EATING PLAIN FOOD |
| 吃不消 (chībùxiāo) | Eat-not-digest | Cannot bear/withstand | INABILITY TO COPE = INDIGESTION |

### Three-Way Comparison

**Mandarin 吃**: Highly productive — extends to suffering, surprise, jealousy, popularity, understanding, legal trouble. The verb 吃 is a universal receptor for negative experiences and effortful processes.

**Cantonese 食 (sik6)**: Similar metaphorical extensions exist:
- 食死貓 (sik6 sei2 maau1, eat dead cat = take the blame)
- 食自己 (sik6 zi6 gei2, eat self = take a loss)

But Cantonese has its own additional metaphorical extensions not shared with Mandarin.

**English "eat"**: Very limited metaphorical extension:
- "Eat your words" (retract a statement)
- "Eat humble pie" (accept humiliation)
- "What's eating you?" (what's bothering you?)
- "Eat the cost" (absorb a financial loss)

English has far fewer productive "eat" metaphors. The IDEAS ARE FOOD metaphor exists ("food for thought," "digest information," "half-baked idea"), but it uses various food-related verbs rather than concentrating extensions on a single verb.

### Why 吃 Is So Productive in Chinese

Several factors contribute:
1. **Monosyllabic verb system**: Chinese favors short verbs that combine with other morphemes — 吃 is a maximally simple one-syllable verb ideal for compounding
2. **Embodied universality**: Eating is perhaps the most fundamental human physical experience, making it a rich source domain
3. **Cultural centrality of food**: Food occupies a more central cultural role in Chinese society ("Have you eaten?" as a greeting: 吃了吗？) making food metaphors culturally resonant
4. **Compound word formation**: Chinese word formation through compounding naturally extends existing morphemes into new domains

## 7. Three Levels of Metaphorical Encoding (Unique to Chinese)

Chinese uniquely operates with metaphor at three distinct levels simultaneously:

### Level 1: Cognitive (Shared with All Languages)
Conceptual metaphors structure thought: TIME IS SPACE, ANGER IS HEAT, HAPPINESS IS UP. This level is universal and arises from embodied experience.

### Level 2: Lexical (Shared with Many Languages, Unusually Transparent in Chinese)
Compound words encode metaphorical mappings. Chinese is *unusually transparent* here because its compound word formation makes the metaphorical structure visible:
- 电脑 (diànnǎo, electric + brain = computer) — the metaphor is decomposable
- English "computer" — from Latin "computare" (to calculate) — the etymology is opaque to most speakers

### Level 3: Graphemic (Unique to Logographic Scripts)
Characters visually encode metaphor: 明 (sun + moon = bright). No alphabetic script has this layer. A Chinese reader engaging with a character can potentially process:
1. The conceptual metaphor (THINKING IS HEART-MIND ACTIVITY)
2. The lexical meaning (the word's definition)
3. The visual-graphemic structure (the 心 radical visible in cognition/emotion characters)

### Three-Way Comparison

| Encoding Level | Mandarin | Traditional Chinese | English |
|---------------|----------|--------------------|---------| 
| Cognitive | Full set of conceptual metaphors | Full set (same tradition) | Full set (different tradition) |
| Lexical | Highly transparent compounds | Same (shared vocabulary) | Less transparent — Latin/Greek borrowings obscure |
| Graphemic | Present but reduced by simplification | Fully present | Absent — alphabetic script cannot encode this |

**The simplification effect**: This is where the three-way comparison reveals something important. Traditional Chinese preserves the full graphemic metaphorical layer (all three levels at full strength). Mandarin simplified characters sometimes reduce the graphemic layer (two-and-a-half levels). English has only two levels. So the three systems form a gradient: Traditional Chinese > Mandarin > English in terms of metaphorical encoding depth.

## Key Relationships

## Diagram Specifications

### Diagram 1: Dual-Axis Time Model — Chinese vs. English
- **Type**: Spatial comparison diagram (two panels)
- **Purpose**: Shows how all Chinese varieties use both horizontal AND vertical axes for time while English uses only horizontal
- **Elements**:
  - English panel: horizontal timeline only (past ← present → future), with "look back" and "look forward"
  - Chinese panel: BOTH horizontal (前/后) AND vertical (上/下) axes with examples
  - Frequency annotation: "64% horizontal, 36% vertical in Chinese corpus data"
  - Boroditsky controversy callout: "Linguistic pattern: confirmed. Cognitive influence: contested."
- **Notes**: Key insight is MORE axes, not DIFFERENT axes

### Diagram 2: Heart-Mind Unification — 心 vs. English Head/Heart Split
- **Type**: Conceptual comparison diagram
- **Purpose**: Shows how Chinese 心 unifies what English separates into "mind" and "heart"
- **Elements**:
  - Chinese side: single unified circle (心) containing both cognitive words (想, 思, 忆, 悟) and emotion words (情, 怒, 恐, 悲)
  - English side: two separate circles — "Mind/Head" and "Heart" with tension arrow between
  - Arabic qalb shown as parallel to Chinese model
  - Heart radical (心/忄) highlighted in character examples
- **Notes**: Include cross-linguistic parallel with Arabic

### Diagram 3: Body-Organ Emotion Map — Chinese vs. English
- **Type**: Body silhouette diagram with organ annotations
- **Purpose**: Maps emotions to internal organs in Chinese, showing granularity difference vs. English
- **Elements**:
  - Body outline with 7 organs labeled
  - Chinese side: Heart=joy/cognition, Liver=anger, Spleen=worry, Lungs=grief, Kidneys=fear, Gallbladder=courage, Intestines=deep feeling
  - English side: Heart=love/emotion, Gut=intuition (much sparser)
  - TCM Five Elements color coding
  - Example expressions at each organ
- **Notes**: The visual contrast dramatically illustrates Chinese distributing across 7+ organs what English concentrates in 1-2

### Diagram 4: Compound Ideograph Gallery — Frozen Metaphors
- **Type**: Grid/gallery with visual decomposition
- **Purpose**: Shows compound ideographs broken into components with metaphorical logic
- **Elements**:
  - Each entry: Character → Component 1 + Component 2 → Logic → Meaning
  - 明 (日+月→bright), 好 (女+子→good), 安 (宀+女→peace), 家 (宀+豕→home), 信 (人+言→trust), 休 (人+木→rest)
  - Folk etymology caveat for selected items
  - Simplification comparison where relevant (愛→爱 losing 心)
- **Notes**: Visually striking — show characters decomposed with the reasoning visible

### Diagram 5: Metaphor Universality Spectrum
- **Type**: Horizontal spectrum/continuum
- **Purpose**: Maps metaphors along the universal-to-culture-specific spectrum for all three systems
- **Elements**:
  - Left: Universal (all languages)
  - Center: Widespread (many languages)
  - Right: Culture-specific (one/few)
  - Plotted: ANGER IS HEAT (universal), HAPPINESS IS UP (universal), TIME IS SPACE (universal, axis varies), HEART-MIND UNITY (Chinese + Arabic), GALLBLADDER IS COURAGE (Chinese), FACE IS STANDING (Chinese origin, borrowed into English)
  - Three-system markers for each metaphor
- **Notes**: Connect to embodied cognition (universal = bodily grounding, specific = cultural filtering)

### Diagram 6: 吃 (Chī) Productivity Tree
- **Type**: Radial/tree diagram
- **Purpose**: Shows how the single verb 吃 (eat) extends metaphorically into multiple domains
- **Elements**:
  - Center: 吃 (eat) — concrete meaning
  - Branches: 吃苦 (hardship), 吃亏 (loss), 吃惊 (surprise), 吃醋 (jealousy), 吃力 (effort), 吃香 (popularity), 吃透 (understand)
  - Color coding by semantic domain (suffering, emotion, cognition, social)
  - English "eat" comparison with far fewer extensions
  - Cantonese 食 shown as parallel but partially different
- **Notes**: Illustrates how Mandarin builds metaphorical vocabulary from concrete verbs through compounding
