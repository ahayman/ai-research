# French Semantics: How It Works

## Overview

The semantic relationship between French and English is unlike any other major language pair. The two languages share an enormous vocabulary -- approximately **~1,700 near-identical cognates** (words that are virtually the same in both spelling and meaning) and a broader overlap of approximately **~30% of all English dictionary entries** being of French or Norman French origin. This shared foundation creates an illusion of transparency: a speaker of either language can often guess the meaning of words in the other by sight alone.

But the illusion is deceptive. Beneath the surface-level vocabulary bridge, French and English organize meaning according to fundamentally different principles. French makes distinctions that English collapses -- two verbs for "know" (savoir/connaître), "have" instead of "be" for bodily states (j'ai faim vs. I am hungry), a grammaticalized formality distinction (tu/vous) where English has only "you." And French preserves a 2,000-year-old Celtic fossil in its number system that requires arithmetic to decode (quatre-vingt-dix-sept = 4 x 20 + 10 + 7 = 97).

Perhaps most paradoxically, the very words English borrowed from French to create its formal register are not formal at all in French. "Commence" feels elevated in English; "commencer" is everyday French. English used French vocabulary to build a three-layer register system (Germanic/French/Latin) that French itself does not have.

**What is French semantics in contrast with English?**
The study of how the two languages encode meaning differently despite their massive shared vocabulary -- covering cognates, false friends, conceptual splits (savoir/connaître), different metaphorical frames (avoir-states vs. be-states), register asymmetry, and structural differences in the number system and address system (tu/vous).

**Why does it matter for learners?**
Because the vocabulary bridge creates overconfidence. An English speaker who sees "attendre" will guess "attend" -- and be wrong (it means "to wait"). The shared vocabulary USUALLY works, but when it fails, it fails in systematically predictable ways. Understanding the patterns of divergence is as important as knowing the cognates.

**Key takeaways:**
- ~1,700 near-identical cognates and ~30% French-origin English vocabulary create a massive bridge -- but it is deceptive
- An estimated 85 to several hundred false friends (faux amis) create systematic interference; the number depends on how broadly the term is defined
- French makes meaning distinctions that English collapses (savoir/connaître, avoir/être for states, tu/vous)
- Words that feel "formal" in English are often register-neutral in French -- the register systems work differently
- The vigesimal number system (70-99) is a living 2,000-year-old Celtic substrate feature

## Core Components

### The Cognate Bridge: ~1,700 Near-Identical Words

The vocabulary overlap between French and English is massive and systematic. Approximately 1,700 words are near-identical in both spelling and meaning: information, table, art, culture, nation, important, possible, communication, distance, silence, intelligence, tradition, different, normal, special, nature. These require only pronunciation adjustment -- the spelling is often identical.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-1-cognate-bridge -->

Beyond these near-identical twins, approximately 30% of all English dictionary entries derive from French or Norman French. This borrowing occurred primarily after the **Norman Conquest of 1066**, when William the Conqueror installed French-speaking rulers over English-speaking populations. The result was a systematic stratification: English words for live animals (cow, pig, sheep -- Germanic, from the peasants who raised them) vs. English words for the meat on the table (beef, pork, mutton -- French, from the nobility who ate them). This social split created the foundations of English's three-layer register system.

The cognate relationship follows **systematic sound correspondences** -- predictable patterns that allow learners to convert between French and English vocabulary:
- English **-tion** = French **-tion**: nation, station, education
- English **-ty** = French **-té**: liberty/liberté, university/université, reality/réalité
- English **-ous** = French **-eux/-euse**: generous/généreux, curious/curieux
- English **-ment** = French **-ment**: government/gouvernement
- English **-ence/-ance** = French **-ence/-ance**: patience, importance
- English **-al** = French **-al/-el**: national, professionnel

These correspondences are productive -- once learned, they can be applied to hundreds of word pairs. The pattern is not merely a historical curiosity; it is a functional tool for vocabulary acquisition.

A less well-known layer of French vocabulary derives from Gaulish, the Celtic language spoken before Roman conquest. An estimated 150-180 commonly cited words of Gaulish origin survive in modern French, concentrated in domains of rural life: "chemin" (path), "char" (cart), "bouleau" (birch), "chêne" (oak), "ruche" (beehive), "tonneau" (barrel). The vigesimal number system (see below) is the most prominent structural Gaulish survival. English, by contrast, has virtually zero Celtic substrate vocabulary -- the Anglo-Saxons displaced rather than assimilated the Celtic Britons, creating one of the unsolved puzzles of historical linguistics.

**ENGLISH COMPARISON:** The cognate relationship is asymmetric in register. French-origin words entered English and became part of a three-layer register system: Germanic base (ask, help, buy), French formal layer (question, assist, purchase), Latin technical layer (interrogate, facilitate, procure). In French, these same words are simply... French. The word "important" is everyday French; in English, it sits in the Latinate/French formal layer above the Germanic "big." English speakers learning French have an enormous head start on vocabulary, but must recalibrate register expectations.

### Faux Amis: The Treacherous Gap

The dark side of the cognate bridge is the **faux amis** (false friends) -- words that look identical in both languages but mean different things. The same historical relationship that created ~1,700 genuine cognates also produced meaning divergences where the same Latin root evolved in different semantic directions.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-2-faux-amis -->

**Complete false friends** (no overlap in meaning):
- **attendre** (F) = to wait ≠ attend (E) = to be present at. "J'attends le bus" = I'm waiting for the bus
- **assister** (F) = to attend, be present ≠ assist (E) = to help. "J'ai assisté à la conférence" = I attended the conference
- **actuellement** (F) = currently ≠ actually (E) = in fact. "Il travaille actuellement" = He currently works
- **blesser** (F) = to injure ≠ bless (E) = to consecrate
- **librairie** (F) = bookshop ≠ library (E) = lending library. The French word for library is "bibliothèque"
- **sensible** (F) = sensitive ≠ sensible (E) = practical, reasonable
- **préservatif** (F) = condom ≠ preservative (E) = food additive. Famously embarrassing.

**Semi-false friends** (partial overlap):
- **demander** = to ask (not "to demand," which is "exiger")
- **rester** = to stay (not "to rest," which is "se reposer")
- **formidable** = wonderful, tremendous (not "fearsome")
- **sympathique** = nice, likeable (not "sympathetic," which is "compatissant")

**The drift pattern:** Many false friends follow a systematic pattern. French typically retains the meaning closer to the original Latin root, while English shifted to a more specialized or metaphorical meaning. "Attendre" comes from Latin "attendere" (to stretch toward, give attention to) -- French kept the "waiting/attending to" sense; English shifted to "being present at." "Assister" comes from Latin "assistere" (to stand by) -- French kept "standing by/being present"; English shifted to "helping." This drift direction is itself a pattern that aids prediction.

**How many false friends exist?** Estimates range from 85 (curated lists of high-risk pairs) to several hundred (broader lists including semi-false friends and partial overlaps). The exact count depends on how broadly "false friend" is defined -- whether it includes only pairs with no meaning overlap or also extends to pairs with partial overlap (like "demander," which occasionally can mean "demand" in formal contexts but primarily means "ask").

**ENGLISH COMPARISON:** False friends exist BECAUSE of the massive vocabulary sharing. If two languages had no cognates, there would be no false friends. The ~1,700 true cognates and the ~30% French-origin English vocabulary mean that the cognate strategy usually works -- the hit rate is high. But when it misses, the error can be significant or embarrassing. The efficient approach treats false friends as flagged exceptions within an otherwise productive cognate system.

### The Savoir/Connaître Distinction: Splitting "Know"

French divides the English verb "know" into two conceptually distinct verbs, each with its own grammatical behavior and semantic domain.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-3-savoir-connaitre -->

**Savoir** encodes epistemic knowledge -- knowing facts, information, and skills. It is followed by que-clauses, infinitives, interrogative words, or specific facts:
- "Je sais que Paris est la capitale" (I know that Paris is the capital -- propositional)
- "Je sais nager" (I know how to swim -- skill/ability)
- "Je sais la réponse" (I know the answer -- factual)
- "Tu sais où il habite?" (Do you know where he lives? -- embedded question)

**Connaître** encodes experiential knowledge -- knowing through acquaintance, familiarity, and personal experience. It is ALWAYS followed by a noun:
- "Je connais Marie" (I know Marie -- personal acquaintance)
- "Je connais Paris" (I know Paris -- experiential familiarity, from having been there)
- "Je connais cette chanson" (I know this song -- familiarity through experience)

**The rule is nearly exceptionless:** Connaître takes a NOUN. Savoir takes everything else (clauses, infinitives, questions, facts). There is virtually no overlap.

The aspect distinction interacts with both verbs to produce meaning shifts. In the imparfait (ongoing state): "connaissais" = knew, "savais" = knew. In the passé composé (completed event): "ai connu" = met (the moment acquaintance began), "ai su" = found out (the moment knowledge was acquired). The perfective aspect converts ongoing states into bounded events.

**ENGLISH COMPARISON:** English has a single verb "know" that covers both domains: "I know Paris" (acquaintance), "I know the answer" (fact), "I know how to swim" (skill). English is unusual among European languages in collapsing this distinction. German (wissen/kennen), Spanish (saber/conocer), Italian (sapere/conoscere), and Portuguese (saber/conhecer) all maintain the split. Learning the French distinction also prepares a learner for these other languages.

### Avoir-State Expressions: Possessing vs. Being

French and English use different **conceptual metaphors** to frame the same human experiences. French treats physical and emotional states as POSSESSIONS (things you HAVE). English treats them as PROPERTIES (things you ARE).

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-4-avoir-states -->

The systematic mapping:

| French (avoir + noun) | Literal translation | English (be + adjective) |
|----------------------|---------------------|--------------------------|
| J'ai faim | I have hunger | I am hungry |
| J'ai soif | I have thirst | I am thirsty |
| J'ai chaud | I have hot | I am hot |
| J'ai froid | I have cold | I am cold |
| J'ai peur | I have fear | I am afraid |
| Tu as raison | You have reason | You are right |
| Il a tort | He has wrong | He is wrong |
| J'ai sommeil | I have sleep | I am sleepy |
| Elle a honte | She has shame | She is ashamed |
| J'ai 30 ans | I have 30 years | I am 30 years old |

The pattern extends to many other expressions: "avoir besoin de" (to have need of = to need), "avoir envie de" (to have desire of = to want/feel like), "avoir l'air" (to have the air = to seem), "avoir lieu" (to have place = to take place).

**The danger zone:** "J'ai chaud" (I have hot = I am hot, temperature) is correct. "Je suis chaud(e)" (I am hot) is slang for sexual arousal. This is one of the most notorious errors English speakers make in French, because the literal translation of "I am hot" produces exactly the wrong meaning.

This is not just a vocabulary difference -- it reflects a different underlying conceptual frame, inherited from Latin. All Romance languages share this pattern: Spanish "tener hambre," Italian "avere fame," Portuguese "ter fome." English is the outlier, using "be" where an entire family of languages uses "have."

**ENGLISH COMPARISON:** English uses "be" + adjective for virtually all states. French uses "avoir" + noun. The conceptual metaphor difference -- STATES ARE POSSESSIONS (French) vs. STATES ARE PROPERTIES (English) -- is systematic and extends across the entire domain of physical and emotional states. Once the pattern is recognized, it applies predictably.

### The Vigesimal Number System: A Celtic Fossil

For numbers 1-69, French uses a straightforward base-10 system. For numbers 70-99, it switches to a **vigesimal (base-20) system** that requires arithmetic to decode.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-5-vigesimal-system -->

- **70** = soixante-dix (sixty-ten = 60 + 10)
- **80** = quatre-vingts (four-twenties = 4 x 20)
- **90** = quatre-vingt-dix (four-twenty-ten = 4 x 20 + 10)
- **97** = quatre-vingt-dix-sept (4 x 20 + 10 + 7 = 97) -- requiring two arithmetic operations

This system is a living fossil from **Gaulish**, the Celtic language spoken in pre-Roman France. The Gauls used base-20 counting. When Latin replaced Gaulish as the spoken language (from the 1st century BCE onward), the base-10 Latin system was adopted for most numbers, but the base-20 system survived in the 70-99 range -- a remarkable example of substrate influence persisting for over 2,000 years.

Other Celtic languages also use base-20: Welsh, Breton, Irish. The medieval Parisian hospital "Quinze-Vingts" (15 x 20 = 300, a hospital for 300 blind people) preserves a more extensive vigesimal usage from an earlier period of French.

**Regional alternatives exist.** Belgian French uses septante (70) and nonante (90). Swiss French adds huitante (80). These purely base-10 alternatives demonstrate that French CAN express these numbers without the vigesimal system -- the vigesimal forms are specifically a Metropolitan French feature.

Research suggests the vigesimal system creates measurable processing costs. Decoding "quatre-vingt-dix-sept" requires two arithmetic operations (multiplication, then addition). Decoding English "ninety-seven" or Belgian "nonante-sept" requires one (simple addition). This is one of the clearest demonstrations of how linguistic structure affects cognitive processing.

**ENGLISH COMPARISON:** English uses a purely base-10 system throughout: seventy (7 x 10), eighty (8 x 10), ninety (9 x 10). The French vigesimal system has no parallel in English. It represents one of the most visible traces of the pre-Roman Celtic substrate in French -- and one of the most concrete examples of a dead language's structure surviving inside a living one.

### The Tu/Vous Register System

French grammaticalizes a social distinction that English leaves to pragmatics. The pronoun "tu" signals intimacy, familiarity, and solidarity. The pronoun "vous" (when used for a single person) signals respect, distance, and formality. The same pronoun "vous" also serves as the plural "you" for any register.

The negotiation between tu and vous -- "tutoiement" and "vouvoiement" -- is a live social process. Switching from vous to tu often requires explicit negotiation: "On peut se tutoyer?" (Can we use tu with each other?). Using tu with someone who expects vous can be perceived as rude or presumptuous. Using vous with someone who expects tu can feel cold or distancing.

English ONCE had exactly this system. "Thou" was the familiar singular; "you" was the formal singular and all-purpose plural. "Thou" began declining in the 16th century and was largely obsolete in standard English by the mid-17th century. Modern English has only "you" for all contexts, compensating through lexical politeness ("Would you be so kind..."), title usage ("Mr. President" vs. first names), and discourse-level strategies.

**ENGLISH COMPARISON:** English cannot grammatically encode the tu/vous distinction. It must do so pragmatically -- through word choice, tone, and social conventions rather than through pronoun selection. This is a case where French GRAMMATICALIZES a social distinction that English leaves entirely to context. English lost "thou" over 350 years ago; French shows no sign of losing tu/vous.

### Register Asymmetry: The Mirror Effect

The shared vocabulary between French and English creates a persistent register mismatch. English built a three-layer register system FROM French borrowing. French itself does not have this layering.

English's three layers:
- **Germanic** (everyday): ask, help, buy, fire, begin, end
- **French** (formal): question, assist, purchase, flame, commence, conclude
- **Latin** (technical): interrogate, facilitate, procure, conflagration, initiate, terminate

In French, the "French-layer" words are not formal. "Commencer" is the everyday, neutral word for "to begin." "Assister" is the standard verb for "to attend." There is no Germanic alternative in French -- there is no lower-register synonym. French controls register through grammar (tu/vous, ne-retention vs. ne-drop, subjunctive usage, passé simple) rather than through vocabulary layering.

The consequence for learners: English speakers often sound "too formal" in French because they choose Latinate/French-origin English words and assume they carry the same elevated register in French. They do not.

## Common Misconceptions

### Misconception: "French and English share so much vocabulary that they're basically the same"

**Reality:** The shared vocabulary creates accessibility at the word level but masks deep organizational differences. The cognate bridge helps with recognition, but the savoir/connaître split, the avoir-state system, the tu/vous distinction, and the register asymmetry mean that knowing the words is not the same as knowing how they function.

### Misconception: "Faux amis are rare exceptions"

**Reality:** Depending on definition, there are an estimated 85 to several hundred false friends between French and English. They are a systematic consequence of the vocabulary sharing, not random noise. The cognate strategy has a high hit rate, but the miss rate is non-trivial, and the errors can be significant.

### Misconception: "French numbers are needlessly complicated"

**Reality:** The vigesimal system is not a design flaw -- it is a 2,000-year-old structural survival from Gaulish Celtic, the language spoken before Roman conquest. It persists because language change is path-dependent: features that become established can persist indefinitely, even when more efficient alternatives exist nearby (as Belgian septante/nonante demonstrates).

## Summary

### Key Points

1. ~1,700 near-identical cognates and ~30% French-origin English vocabulary create a massive shared foundation, but the bridge is deceptive because register positions and some meanings diverge
2. An estimated 85 to several hundred faux amis create systematic interference; many follow a predictable drift pattern where French preserves the Latin meaning and English shifts
3. The savoir/connaître distinction splits "know" along an epistemic/experiential axis -- a division English lacks but most other European languages maintain
4. Avoir-state expressions reflect a different conceptual metaphor: French frames states as possessions (j'ai faim), English frames them as properties (I am hungry)
5. The vigesimal number system (70-99) is a living Celtic fossil over 2,000 years old
6. The tu/vous distinction grammaticalizes a formality level that English lost when "thou" disappeared
7. Words that feel "formal" in English are often register-neutral in French -- the register systems operate on different principles

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Base-10 (decimal) | A number system where each position represents a power of 10; used in English and for French numbers 1-69 |
| Base-20 (vigesimal) | A number system where groupings are based on 20; used in French for numbers 70-99, inherited from Gaulish Celtic |
| Cognate | A word in one language that shares etymology and (usually) meaning with a word in another language |
| Conceptual metaphor | A systematic mapping between a source domain and a target domain that structures how a concept is understood (e.g., STATES ARE POSSESSIONS) |
| Faux ami (false friend) | A word that looks the same in two languages but has different meanings |
| Gaulish | The Celtic language spoken in pre-Roman France (Gaul), extinct by approximately the 5th century CE |
| Norman Conquest | The 1066 invasion of England by William of Normandy, which introduced French as the language of the English ruling class |
| Register | The level of formality in language use, determined by social context |
| Semi-false friend | A word that partially overlaps in meaning between two languages but has significant differences in primary usage |
| Sound correspondence | A systematic relationship between sounds in cognate words across two related languages |
| Substrate | Linguistic features from an earlier, displaced language that persist in the language that replaced it |
| Tutoiement | The practice of addressing someone with "tu" (familiar) in French |
| Vouvoiement | The practice of addressing someone with "vous" (formal) in French |
