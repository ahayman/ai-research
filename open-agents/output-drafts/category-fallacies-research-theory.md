# Logical Fallacies as Pragmatic Tactics: Comprehensive Research

## Research Purpose
This document provides comprehensive theoretical and practical coverage of logical fallacies reframed as **pragmatic tactics** -- persuasive moves that exploit the social systems of language (presupposition, face dynamics, hierarchy, common ground, topic management, relevance) to achieve rhetorical goals. It is intended for a knowledge base teaching English pragmatics to autistic individuals, with emphasis on making the hidden social mechanics of fallacious reasoning explicit, systematic, and detectable.

**The central thesis:** Informal fallacies are not primarily errors in logic -- they are **pragmatic maneuvers** that work because they exploit the very mechanisms that make cooperative communication possible. The same systems that enable us to communicate efficiently (presupposition accommodation, face management, the Cooperative Principle, common ground tracking, relevance-driven interpretation) also create vulnerabilities that skilled arguers can exploit. Understanding fallacies as pragmatic phenomena explains both why they are persuasive and why they are socially difficult to counter.

**Cross-references to other categories:**
- **Category 2 (Implicature):** Fallacies exploit Gricean maxims and the inferential system of implicature
- **Category 3 (Politeness/Face):** Many fallacies work by threatening or exploiting face dynamics; correcting fallacies is itself a face-threatening act
- **Category 4 (Conversation Structure):** The Gish gallop and other volume-based fallacies exploit turn-taking constraints
- **Category 9 (Presupposition):** Loaded questions and begging the question exploit presupposition triggers and accommodation
- **Category 10 (Relevance Theory):** Red herrings and whataboutism exploit relevance expectations
- **Category 13 (Topic Management):** Red herrings, whataboutism, and deflection exploit topic-shift norms
- **Category 16 (Common Ground):** Many fallacies work by smuggling unestablished premises into the common ground
- **Category 17 (Questions):** Loaded questions and complex questions exploit the pragmatics of questioning
- **Category 18 (Refusal/Disagreement):** Countering fallacies is a form of disagreement, subject to all the dispreference structures documented there
- **Category 22 (Social Hierarchy):** Appeals to authority, arguments from position, and tone policing all exploit hierarchy dynamics

## PART ONE: FALLACIES AS PRAGMATIC PHENOMENA -- THE THEORETICAL REFRAME

### 1. Why Fallacies Are a Pragmatic Problem

#### 1.1 The Traditional View: Fallacies as Logic Errors

The study of fallacies begins with Aristotle's *Sophistical Refutations* (Sophistici Elenchi, c. 350 BCE), the first systematic catalog of faulty arguments. Aristotle identified thirteen fallacies, divided into two groups: those dependent on language (in dictione) -- such as ambiguity, amphiboly, and accent -- and those independent of language (extra dictionem) -- such as begging the question, affirming the consequent, and ignoratio elenchi (missing the point).

For over two thousand years, fallacies were treated primarily as **logical defects** -- errors in the structure of reasoning that, once identified, could simply be dismissed. The standard textbook approach (found in Copi, 1953; Copi & Cohen, 1990; Hurley, 2003; and dozens of others) presents fallacies as a taxonomy of mistakes: learn the names, recognize the patterns, reject the argument.

This approach has a critical blind spot: **it cannot explain why fallacies work.**

If fallacies were merely logical errors, they would be as easy to detect as a mathematical mistake. But fallacies are persuasive -- not just to the unsophisticated, but routinely to intelligent, educated people in high-stakes settings. Courtrooms, political debates, academic discussions, and boardroom negotiations are all environments where fallacies succeed regularly, often against people who could name the fallacy being committed.

The reason is that fallacies do not operate primarily at the level of logic. They operate at the level of **pragmatics** -- the social systems through which language achieves its effects.

#### 1.2 The Pragmatic Turn: Fallacies as Dialogue Violations

Beginning in the 1970s and accelerating through the 1990s, a revolution occurred in fallacy theory. Three interconnected research programs reframed fallacies not as isolated logical errors but as violations of the norms governing **rational dialogue**:

**Charles Hamblin (1970)** is widely credited with revitalizing the study of fallacies through his landmark book *Fallacies* (Methuen, London). Hamblin surveyed the entire history of fallacy theory from Aristotle through the twentieth century and concluded that the "standard treatment" of fallacies was intellectually bankrupt: "it is as debased, worn-out and dog-eared as any in logic." His book exposed the ad hoc, unsystematic nature of traditional fallacy lists and argued that a proper theory of fallacies required a theory of **argumentation** -- specifically, a formal model of how arguments function in dialogue contexts.

Hamblin's key insight was that fallacies cannot be properly understood as features of individual arguments in isolation. They must be understood as **moves in a dialogue** -- and whether a move is fallacious depends on the type of dialogue, the commitments of the participants, and the rules governing the exchange.

**The pragma-dialectical school (van Eemeren & Grootendorst)** developed the most influential modern theory of fallacy, the pragma-dialectical approach, beginning in the 1980s. Frans H. van Eemeren and Rob Grootendorst, both of the University of Amsterdam, published their foundational work *Speech Acts in Argumentive Discussions* (Foris, 1984) and later *Argumentation, Communication, and Fallacies* (Lawrence Erlbaum, 1992) and *A Systematic Theory of Argumentation: The Pragma-Dialectical Approach* (Cambridge University Press, 2004).

**Douglas Walton** developed an extensive body of work on fallacies as context-dependent reasoning failures in different dialogue types. His key works include *Informal Logic: A Handbook for Critical Argumentation* (Cambridge University Press, 1989), *A Pragmatic Theory of Fallacy* (University of Alabama Press, 1995), and dozens of monographs on individual fallacies (on ad hominem, appeal to emotion, slippery slope, straw man, etc.). Walton proposed that arguments which are fallacious in one dialogue type may be perfectly legitimate in another -- making fallacies inherently context-dependent and thus inherently pragmatic.

**The Informal Logic movement** (Ralph H. Johnson and J. Anthony Blair, particularly their textbook *Logical Self-Defense*, McGraw-Hill Ryerson, 1st edition 1977, 3rd edition 2006; also Trudy Govier, *A Practical Study of Argument*, Wadsworth, 1st edition 1985, 7th edition 2010) argued that the formal deductive model of logic was inadequate for evaluating the arguments people actually encounter in everyday life, and that informal fallacies required their own theoretical framework grounded in pragmatic norms of argumentation.

#### 1.3 The Gricean Connection: Fallacies as Maxim Exploitation

H. P. Grice's Cooperative Principle and its four maxims (Quality, Quantity, Relation, Manner) describe the rational expectations that make conversation possible (see Category 2). Grice himself distinguished between maxim **violation** (quietly failing to observe a maxim) and maxim **flouting** (ostentatiously failing to observe a maxim to generate an implicature). But there is a third category that Grice mentioned but did not develop: maxim **exploitation** -- using the listener's expectation that maxims are being observed in order to mislead them.

This is precisely what many informal fallacies do. They **appear** to cooperate while actually manipulating the inferential expectations that cooperation creates.

| Gricean Maxim | What Listeners Expect | How Fallacies Exploit This |
|---|---|---|
| **Quality** (Be truthful; have adequate evidence) | The speaker believes what they say and has evidence | Misleading statistics, selective evidence, and appeals to false authority exploit the listener's trust that the speaker is being truthful. Cherry-picking data *looks like* observing Quality while actually violating it. |
| **Quantity** (Be as informative as required, not more) | The options/information presented are exhaustive and sufficient | The **false dilemma** presents only two options; listeners infer (via Quantity) that these are all the options. The **Gish gallop** overwhelms with so much information that the listener cannot process it all, exploiting the assumption that each claim merits attention. |
| **Relation** (Be relevant) | Every contribution is relevant to the current topic | **Red herrings** and **whataboutism** introduce irrelevant material. But the listener's default assumption of relevance (what Sperber & Wilson call the "presumption of optimal relevance") means they try to find a relevant interpretation -- and in doing so, they follow the deflection. |
| **Manner** (Be clear, orderly, avoid ambiguity) | Words are used consistently and clearly | **Equivocation** shifts word meaning mid-argument. **Loaded language** uses emotionally charged terms that smuggle in evaluative content under the guise of description. The listener assumes consistent, perspicuous language use and fails to notice the shift. |

This Gricean analysis reveals something crucial: **fallacies are parasitic on cooperation.** They work because listeners are cooperatively processing the speaker's contributions -- making charitable interpretations, filling in gaps, assuming relevance, trusting truthfulness. Fallacies exploit this cooperative disposition.

Jacobs and Jackson (1982, "Conversational argument: A discourse analytic approach," in Cox & Willard, eds., *Advances in Argumentation Theory and Research*) made this point explicitly: fallacies exploit the "procedures and conventions that people use to manage disagreements" -- that is, they exploit the pragmatic infrastructure of argumentation. Their conversational approach to argument showed that many fallacies could be understood as illegitimate uses of otherwise legitimate conversational moves.

#### 1.4 Relevance Theory and Fallacies

Sperber and Wilson's Relevance Theory (1986/1995, *Relevance: Communication and Cognition*, Blackwell) provides an even more specific mechanism. According to RT, human cognition is geared toward maximizing relevance -- achieving the greatest cognitive effect for the least processing effort (see Category 10).

Several fallacy types exploit this cognitive architecture:

- **Red herrings** succeed because the human relevance-processing system automatically tries to find the relevance of any new input. When someone introduces an irrelevant topic, the listener's cognitive system works to connect it to the current discussion -- and in doing so, follows the deflection.

- **Appeals to emotion** succeed because emotionally arousing information achieves high cognitive effects with low processing effort. Emotional content is highly "relevant" in the RT sense -- it engages the listener quickly and produces strong reactions. The problem is that these effects are not truth-tracking: being emotionally moved by an argument is not evidence that the argument is sound.

- **Simplistic framing** (including false dilemmas) succeeds because simpler representations require less processing effort. A false dilemma offers a cognitively cheap way to evaluate a situation: "either you're with us or against us" requires far less processing than "there are seventeen different positions one might hold on this issue, each with different tradeoffs." Listeners gravitate toward the simpler frame because their cognitive systems are optimized for efficiency.

- **The Gish gallop** exploits the fact that each new claim demands processing effort to evaluate. When claims arrive faster than they can be processed, the listener faces a cognitive overload: they cannot maintain adequate attention to each claim, so many pass unchallenged -- and unchallenged claims tend to be accepted (through what might be called "relevance-driven credence").

#### 1.5 Presupposition and Fallacy

Many fallacies exploit the presupposition system described in Category 9. The key mechanism is **presupposition accommodation** -- the process by which listeners automatically accept presuppositions they have no reason to reject (Lewis, 1979, "Scorekeeping in a language game," *Journal of Philosophical Logic* 8: 339-359; Stalnaker, 1974, "Pragmatic presuppositions," in Munitz & Unger, eds., *Semantics and Philosophy*).

When a speaker says "The King of France is bald," the listener accommodates the presupposition that there is a King of France. This is usually a cooperative, efficient process -- it allows conversation to proceed without constantly checking every background assumption.

But fallacies exploit accommodation:

- **Loaded questions** ("Have you stopped beating your wife?") force the answerer to accommodate the presupposition that they have been beating their wife. Any direct answer ("yes" or "no") accepts the presupposition. The listener/audience accommodates it automatically.

- **Begging the question** hides the conclusion in the premises as a presupposition. "We should ban this dangerous chemical" presupposes that the chemical is dangerous -- the very point that should be argued for. Listeners accommodate the presupposition without noticing it.

- **Poisoning the well** pre-loads presuppositions into the common ground before the target speaks: "Before my opponent gives his usual misleading presentation..." presupposes that the opponent's presentations are characteristically misleading. By the time the opponent speaks, the audience has already accommodated the presupposition.

- **No True Scotsman** exploits definitional presupposition: "No true Scotsman would do such a thing" presupposes a particular definition of "true Scotsman" that conveniently excludes any counterexample.

The exploitation of presupposition is particularly insidious because presuppositions are, by definition, **backgrounded** -- they are not the focus of the utterance, so they receive less critical scrutiny than asserted content. The listener's attention is on the foreground assertion while the real work of persuasion is being done in the background, through the presuppositions.

#### 1.6 Face Dynamics and Fallacy

Brown and Levinson's (1987) politeness theory (see Category 3) explains why many fallacies are so difficult to counter: **correcting a fallacy is a face-threatening act**, and the social cost of this face threat often exceeds the perceived benefit of logical accuracy.

Face dynamics interact with fallacies in three ways:

**1. Fallacies as face attacks:**
- **Ad hominem** is a direct attack on the interlocutor's positive face (their desire to be respected and approved of). It threatens the speaker's competence, character, or credibility.
- **Tone policing** attacks the speaker's delivery rather than their content, threatening their positive face (the right to express themselves in their chosen manner) and their negative face (the right to not be coerced into performing a particular emotional register).
- **Poisoning the well** is a preemptive face attack: it damages the target's credibility before they have even spoken.

**2. Fallacies that exploit face-protection reflexes:**
- **Appeal to emotion** exploits the listener's reluctance to appear cold or unfeeling. Challenging an emotional appeal threatens the speaker's positive face (their expressed feelings) and risks making the challenger appear callous -- damaging *their own* positive face.
- **Appeal to authority** exploits the social cost of challenging high-status speakers. Questioning an authority's claim threatens the authority's positive face and risks positioning the questioner as presumptuous -- a threat to their own positive face in the hierarchy.
- **Bandwagon / appeal to popularity** exploits the face cost of standing against the group. Disagreeing with "what everyone knows" threatens the speaker's positive face (marking them as an outsider) and the group's collective positive face (suggesting the group is wrong).

**3. The face cost of correction:**
This is the central pragmatic insight. Naming a fallacy in conversation is a face-threatening act along multiple dimensions:
- It threatens the opponent's **positive face** (implying they are illogical, dishonest, or manipulative)
- It threatens the opponent's **negative face** (it is a meta-conversational imposition -- demanding they change their argumentative approach)
- It threatens the corrector's own **positive face** (they risk appearing pedantic, hostile, or evasive -- as though they are using technical jargon to dodge the substance)
- It can threaten the **audience's face** (by implying that the audience was fooled by the fallacy)

This face dynamic creates what we will call the **Correction Trap** (see Section 7 below).

#### 1.7 Hierarchy and Fallacy

Social hierarchy (see Category 22) creates asymmetric fallacy dynamics:

- **Higher-status speakers can deploy fallacies with less social cost.** A boss who commits ad hominem against a subordinate pays less face cost than a subordinate who commits ad hominem against a boss. Similarly, a professor who appeals to their own authority pays less than a student who appeals to theirs.

- **Lower-status speakers face greater social cost in identifying fallacies.** A subordinate who says "That's an ad hominem, you haven't addressed my argument" faces social penalties (appearing insubordinate, confrontational, or arrogant) that a peer would not.

- **Appeals to authority** are fundamentally hierarchy-dependent. The fallacy works *because* listeners have internalized the norm that higher-status speakers are more likely to be right. In many cases, deferring to authority is a reasonable heuristic (you *should* generally defer to your doctor about medical questions). The fallacy occurs when authority in one domain is illegitimately transferred to another, or when the authority claim is used to block inquiry rather than supplement it. But the line between reasonable deference and fallacious appeal is pragmatically determined, not logically.

- **Tone policing** is almost always deployed downward in a hierarchy -- by those with more power against those with less. The person being tone-policed is typically someone whose message challenges the existing power arrangement; the tone police response redirects attention from the content of the challenge to the manner of its delivery.

## PART TWO: THE PRAGMA-DIALECTICAL FRAMEWORK

### 2. Van Eemeren and Grootendorst's Critical Discussion Model

The most comprehensive theoretical framework for analyzing fallacies as pragmatic phenomena is the pragma-dialectical approach developed by Frans H. van Eemeren and Rob Grootendorst at the University of Amsterdam.

#### 2.1 Key Publications

- van Eemeren, F. H., & Grootendorst, R. (1984). *Speech Acts in Argumentive Discussions*. Dordrecht: Foris.
- van Eemeren, F. H., & Grootendorst, R. (1992). *Argumentation, Communication, and Fallacies: A Pragma-Dialectical Perspective*. Hillsdale, NJ: Lawrence Erlbaum Associates.
- van Eemeren, F. H., & Grootendorst, R. (2004). *A Systematic Theory of Argumentation: The Pragma-Dialectical Approach*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press.
- van Eemeren, F. H. (2010). *Strategic Maneuvering in Argumentative Discourse*. Amsterdam: John Benjamins.
- van Eemeren, F. H., Garssen, B., Krabbe, E. C. W., Snoeck Henkemans, A. F., Verheij, B., & Wagemans, J. H. M. (2014). *Handbook of Argumentation Theory*. Dordrecht: Springer.

#### 2.2 The Four Stages of a Critical Discussion

Van Eemeren and Grootendorst model argumentation as a **critical discussion** -- an idealized procedure for resolving differences of opinion through rational exchange. This model has four stages, each with its own rules:

| Stage | Purpose | What Happens |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Confrontation** | Establish that a difference of opinion exists | One party advances a standpoint; the other expresses doubt or an opposing standpoint. Both parties recognize there is a disagreement to be resolved. |
| **2. Opening** | Establish the rules of engagement | Parties agree on procedural rules, shared premises, burden of proof allocation, and what would count as a resolution. (In real conversations, this is usually implicit.) |
| **3. Argumentation** | Exchange arguments | The party with the burden of proof advances arguments; the other party critically examines them, raises objections, requests clarification. |
| **4. Concluding** | Determine the result | The parties assess whether the standpoint has been successfully defended or must be retracted. The discussion is resolved. |

#### 2.3 The Ten Rules for Critical Discussion

Van Eemeren and Grootendorst proposed ten rules that participants must follow for a critical discussion to function properly. Fallacies are defined as **violations of these rules**. This is the core of the pragma-dialectical definition of fallacy: a fallacy is any argumentative move that obstructs the rational resolution of a difference of opinion by violating one or more rules of critical discussion.

| Rule # | Rule Name | The Rule | Example Violation (= Fallacy) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Freedom Rule** | Parties must not prevent each other from advancing standpoints or casting doubt on standpoints. | **Ad hominem** (attacking the person to discredit their right to argue), **tone policing** (questioning someone's right to speak based on manner rather than content), **appeals to force** (argumentum ad baculum) |
| 2 | **Burden of Proof Rule** | A party who advances a standpoint is obliged to defend it if asked to do so. | **Shifting the burden of proof** ("Prove I'm wrong!"), **appeal to ignorance** (arguing something is true because it hasn't been proven false) |
| 3 | **Standpoint Rule** | An attack on a standpoint must relate to the standpoint that has actually been advanced. | **Straw man** (distorting the opponent's position before attacking it) |
| 4 | **Relevance Rule** | A standpoint may be defended only by advancing argumentation related to that standpoint. | **Red herring** (introducing irrelevant material), **irrelevant appeals to emotion**, **ignoratio elenchi** (missing the point) |
| 5 | **Unexpressed Premise Rule** | A party may not disown an unexpressed premise or falsely attribute an unexpressed premise to the other party. | **Attributing extreme versions** of an opponent's position, **denying implicit commitments** |
| 6 | **Starting Point Rule** | A party may not falsely present a premise as an accepted starting point, nor deny a premise that represents an accepted starting point. | **Begging the question** (smuggling the conclusion into the premises), **loaded questions**, **poisoning the well**, **false common ground claims** ("As we all know...") |
| 7 | **Argument Scheme Rule** | A party may not regard a standpoint as conclusively defended if the defense does not take place by means of an appropriate argument scheme that is correctly applied. | **Hasty generalization** (wrong scheme application), **false analogy** (inappropriate scheme), **slippery slope** (flawed causal chain), **false cause** |
| 8 | **Validity Rule** | A party may only use arguments that are logically valid or capable of being validated by making explicit one or more unexpressed premises. | **Affirming the consequent**, **denying the antecedent**, **formal fallacies** generally |
| 9 | **Closure Rule** | A failed defense of a standpoint must result in the proponent retracting the standpoint, and a successful defense must result in the opponent retracting doubt. | **Moving the goalposts** (refusing to accept a successfully defended standpoint by changing the criteria), **ad hoc rescue** |
| 10 | **Usage Rule** | A party must not use formulations that are insufficiently clear or confusingly ambiguous, and must interpret the other party's formulations as carefully and accurately as possible. | **Equivocation** (shifting word meanings), **amphiboly** (syntactic ambiguity), **deliberately vague language** |

#### 2.4 The Pragma-Dialectical Definition of Fallacy

In the pragma-dialectical framework, a fallacy is not defined by its logical form. It is defined functionally:

> **A fallacy is a speech act (or pattern of speech acts) that violates one or more rules of a critical discussion, thereby obstructing the resolution of a difference of opinion.**

This definition has several important consequences:

1. **Fallacies are context-dependent.** The same argument form may be fallacious in one dialogue context and legitimate in another. An appeal to authority is fallacious when the authority is irrelevant (a celebrity endorsing a medical treatment) but not when the authority is relevant and qualified (a doctor recommending a treatment based on clinical evidence).

2. **Fallacies are identified by their effect on the discussion,** not by their logical form. A formally valid argument can still be a fallacy if it violates a discussion rule (e.g., begging the question can be formally valid -- "P, therefore P" -- but it violates the Starting Point Rule).

3. **Fallacies are speech acts.** They are things people *do* in conversation, not just abstract argument structures. This is why they have a pragmatic dimension: they involve real speakers, real audiences, real social dynamics, and real conversational contexts.

#### 2.5 Strategic Maneuvering

In his later work (van Eemeren, 2010), van Eemeren introduced the concept of **strategic maneuvering** -- the idea that arguers simultaneously pursue two goals: maintaining dialectical reasonableness (following the rules) and achieving rhetorical effectiveness (winning the argument). Fallacies occur when the pursuit of rhetorical effectiveness overrides dialectical reasonableness -- when the arguer's strategic maneuvering "derails."

This concept is crucial for the pragmatic understanding of fallacies because it explains why fallacies are so common: arguers are not simply trying to be reasonable; they are also trying to be persuasive. The pressure to win creates a constant temptation to bend or break the rules of rational discussion, and the social dynamics of conversation often reward rule-breaking (the arguer who deploys a compelling ad hominem may "win" the argument in the eyes of the audience, even though they have violated Rule 1).

### 3. Walton's Dialogue Types and Context-Dependent Fallacy

Douglas N. Walton's extensive body of work (over fifty books and hundreds of articles) provides the most detailed analysis of how fallacies depend on dialogue context.

#### 3.1 Key Publications

- Walton, D. N. (1989). *Informal Logic: A Handbook for Critical Argumentation*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press.
- Walton, D. N. (1995). *A Pragmatic Theory of Fallacy*. Tuscaloosa: University of Alabama Press.
- Walton, D. N. (1998). *Ad Hominem Arguments*. Tuscaloosa: University of Alabama Press.
- Walton, D. N. (2000). *Scare Tactics: Arguments That Appeal to Fear and Threats*. Dordrecht: Kluwer.
- Walton, D. N. (2004). *Relevance in Argumentation*. Mahwah, NJ: Lawrence Erlbaum.
- Walton, D. N. (2006). *Fundamentals of Critical Argumentation*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press.
- Walton, D. N. (2008). *Informal Logic: A Pragmatic Approach*. 2nd edition. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press.

#### 3.2 Six Dialogue Types

Walton (1995, 1998) identified six basic dialogue types, each with different normative goals and different rules for what counts as a legitimate argumentative move:

| Dialogue Type | Goal | Initial Situation | Example Context |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Persuasion** | Resolve a difference of opinion | Two parties hold conflicting views | Academic debate, policy discussion |
| **Negotiation** | Reach a mutually acceptable deal | Parties have competing interests | Contract negotiation, plea bargaining |
| **Inquiry** | Establish what is true | Shared ignorance about facts | Scientific investigation, fact-finding |
| **Deliberation** | Decide what to do | Need to choose a course of action | Committee meeting, family decision |
| **Information-seeking** | One party needs information the other has | Knowledge asymmetry | Doctor-patient, teacher-student Q&A |
| **Eristic** | Vent emotions, attack the opponent | Personal conflict, hostility | Quarrel, shouting match |

#### 3.3 Dialogue Shifts and Fallacy

Walton's central insight is that many fallacies can be understood as **illicit dialogue shifts** -- moves that are appropriate in one dialogue type but inappropriate when smuggled into another:

- **Ad hominem** is legitimate in an eristic quarrel (where the goal is to vent and attack) but fallacious in a persuasion dialogue (where the goal is to resolve a disagreement through reason). It is also legitimate in certain institutional contexts: a lawyer impeaching a witness's credibility by showing a pattern of dishonesty is performing a *legitimate* ad hominem in the context of a legal inquiry.

- **Appeal to emotion** is legitimate in deliberation (when the emotional consequences of a decision are relevant to what we should do) but fallacious in an inquiry (where the goal is to establish facts, not decide what to feel about them). "This policy will cause immense suffering" is a legitimate argument in a policy deliberation; "This chemical formula makes me sad" is not a legitimate argument in a chemistry inquiry.

- **Appeal to authority** is legitimate in information-seeking (you go to a doctor *because* they have medical authority) but fallacious in an inquiry that the authority is trying to close prematurely ("I'm the professor, so my hypothesis is correct").

- **Threats and appeals to force** are sometimes legitimate in negotiation (where leveraging consequences is part of the process) but never legitimate in persuasion or inquiry.

This context-dependence is itself a pragmatic phenomenon: what counts as a fallacy depends on the **pragmatic norms** governing the particular type of conversation.

#### 3.4 Argumentation Schemes

Walton (with Chris Reed and Fabrizio Macagno, *Argumentation Schemes*, Cambridge University Press, 2008) cataloged over 60 common patterns of defeasible reasoning ("argumentation schemes"), each with associated "critical questions." An argument becomes fallacious when the critical questions are not satisfactorily answered.

For example, the argumentation scheme for **Appeal to Expert Opinion** is:

> Premise 1: E is an expert in domain D.
> Premise 2: E asserts that claim C is in domain D.
> Premise 3: E asserts that C is true.
> Conclusion: C is true (presumptively).

The critical questions are:
1. How credible is E as an expert?
2. Is E an expert in the relevant field D?
3. What did E assert that implies C?
4. Is E personally reliable as a source?
5. Is C consistent with what other experts assert?
6. Is E's assertion based on evidence?

When these critical questions go unasked or are deflected, the appeal to expert opinion can become fallacious. The pragmatic element is that **asking critical questions has social costs** -- questioning an expert's credibility or domain relevance is a face-threatening act, especially when the expert is present and holds higher status.

### 4. The Informal Logic Tradition

#### 4.1 Johnson and Blair

Ralph H. Johnson and J. Anthony Blair, both at the University of Windsor, founded the field of informal logic with their textbook *Logical Self-Defense* (1977) and the journal *Informal Logic* (1978-present). Their key contribution was arguing that the deductive model of logic is inadequate for evaluating everyday arguments, which are typically:

- **Non-deductive** (they don't aim for logical necessity; they aim for reasonable support)
- **Expressed in natural language** (not formal symbolism)
- **Context-dependent** (their evaluation requires understanding the conversational situation)
- **Defeasible** (they can be overridden by new information)

Johnson and Blair defined fallacy as a pattern of argument that appears to be a good argument but is not -- where "appears" does the crucial pragmatic work. A fallacy is deceptive precisely because it mimics the surface features of a good argument. The deception is not logical; it is **perceptual and social**.

#### 4.2 Trudy Govier

Trudy Govier's *A Practical Study of Argument* (1985, and many subsequent editions) contributed the concept of the **ARG conditions** for a good argument:

- **A**cceptability: The premises must be acceptable (roughly: rationally believable).
- **R**elevance: The premises must be relevant to the conclusion.
- **G**round (sufficiency): The premises, taken together, must provide sufficient grounds for the conclusion.

Fallacies typically violate one or more of these conditions while disguising the violation:

| ARG Condition | How Fallacies Violate It | Pragmatic Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Acceptability | Premises that *seem* acceptable but are not (begging the question, false dilemma) | Presupposition accommodation, common ground manipulation |
| Relevance | Premises that *seem* relevant but are not (red herring, ad hominem, appeal to emotion) | Relevance processing bias (RT), topic management norms |
| Ground/Sufficiency | Evidence that *seems* sufficient but is not (hasty generalization, anecdotal evidence, slippery slope) | Narrative bias, causal implicature |

2. **The pragmatic mechanism** -- which system of language use it exploits
3. **Why it works socially** -- the social dynamics that make it persuasive
4. **Why correcting it is costly** -- the face/social penalties for calling it out

### 5. Ad Hominem

#### 5.1 Logical Structure
Instead of addressing argument A, the speaker attacks the person P who made A. The implicit reasoning is: "P is bad/untrustworthy/biased, therefore A is false."

Walton (1998, *Ad Hominem Arguments*) distinguishes several subtypes:
- **Abusive ad hominem**: Direct character attack ("He's an idiot, ignore his argument")
- **Circumstantial ad hominem**: Alleges bias based on circumstances ("Of course she supports that policy -- she profits from it")
- **Tu quoque**: "You do it too" (a special case, treated separately below)
- **Poisoning the well**: Preemptive character attack (treated separately below)
- **Guilt by association**: "You associate with X, who believes Y, so your views are tainted"

#### 5.2 Pragmatic Mechanism
Ad hominem exploits **face dynamics** (Category 3) and the **person-argument conflation** that is built into everyday language.

In pragmatic terms, every utterance carries information at two levels: (1) the propositional content (what is said) and (2) the social/identity positioning (who is saying it and what that implies about them). Attacking the speaker shifts attention from level 1 to level 2.

The pragmatic force of ad hominem also exploits the **credibility heuristic**: in everyday communication, the credibility of the speaker is a legitimate factor in evaluating claims. You *should* weigh a doctor's medical advice more heavily than a random stranger's. Ad hominem exploits this legitimate heuristic by making it the *only* factor, or by attacking credibility irrelevantly.

#### 5.3 Why It Works Socially
Ad hominem works because:
- Humans are evolved to be sensitive to **reputational information**. Information about a person's character activates social evaluation systems that are older and faster than analytical reasoning.
- In group settings, ad hominem can activate **in-group/out-group dynamics**: if the target is already disliked by the audience, the attack confirms existing social positioning.
- The audience faces a **framing dilemma**: once a person's character has been attacked, every subsequent argument they make is heard through the lens of that attack. The character information contaminates the evaluation of the argument (a well-documented cognitive bias: the halo/horns effect).

#### 5.4 Why Correcting It Is Costly
If you respond to ad hominem by saying "That's an ad hominem -- attack my argument, not me," you face multiple pragmatic penalties:
- You appear to be **deflecting** from the character accusation (which the audience may interpret as confirmation)
- You appear **pedantic** (using Latin terminology in casual discourse violates register norms)
- You have performed a **meta-communicative act** -- shifting from the topic to the conversational process -- which can be perceived as evasive
- You have committed a **face-threatening act** against the attacker (accusing them of arguing unfairly)

The pragmatic double bind: addressing the personal attack validates the frame (the conversation is now about you, not your argument); ignoring it lets the attack stand unchallenged.

### 6. Straw Man

#### 6.1 Logical Structure
The speaker misrepresents the opponent's position P as a distorted version P', then attacks P'. The audience may not notice the substitution.

Talisse and Aikin (2006, "Two forms of the straw man," *Argumentation* 20: 345-352) distinguish:
- **The typical straw man**: Overt distortion of the opponent's position
- **The hollow man**: Attacking a position that nobody actually holds ("Some people say we should have no rules at all...")
- **The weak man**: Selecting the weakest version of the opponent's position instead of the strongest

#### 6.2 Pragmatic Mechanism
Straw man exploits **common ground** (Category 16). The speaker describes the opponent's position to the audience, and because the speaker appears to be cooperating (observing the Quality maxim by accurately reporting what was said), listeners accommodate the description as accurate. The distortion enters the common ground as though it were an established fact.

Straw man also exploits the **information asymmetry** between the original speaker and the audience. The audience may not have access to the original formulation, so they rely on the straw-manner's characterization.

Macagno and Walton (2017, "Interpreting straw man argumentation," in van Eemeren, ed., *Prototypical Argumentative Patterns*) analyze straw man as a violation of Walton's charity principle: the norm that arguers should interpret each other's positions as charitably (strongly) as possible. The straw-manner does the opposite -- interpreting the position as uncharitably as possible.

#### 6.3 Why It Works Socially
- The audience rarely has a transcript. They are comparing their *memory* of what was said with the speaker's *characterization* of what was said, and memory is fallible.
- The distorted version is usually simpler and more extreme than the original, making it easier to process (exploiting RT's effort-minimization principle).
- The distortion often makes the target's position *more entertaining to attack*, which engages the audience.

#### 6.4 Why Correcting It Is Costly
Correcting a straw man requires restating your original position, which:
- Takes time and turn-space (you lose the floor for "old" material)
- Appears **repetitive** ("You already said that")
- Shifts the conversation to a **meta-dispute** about what you actually said, rather than the substantive issue
- Can be dismissed as **quibbling** ("You're splitting hairs -- the point stands")
- The corrector may not be able to articulate the distinction between their actual position and the distorted version clearly enough to make the audience see the difference

### 7. Appeal to Authority (Argumentum ad Verecundiam)

#### 7.1 Logical Structure
"Expert/authority E says X is true, therefore X is true."

As noted in Section 3.4, Walton shows this is not inherently fallacious -- it is a legitimate argumentation scheme with critical questions. It becomes fallacious when:
- The authority is not an expert in the relevant domain
- The experts in the field disagree
- The authority is cited to *close* inquiry rather than *inform* it
- The authority's status is used as a substitute for evidence

#### 7.2 Pragmatic Mechanism
Appeal to authority exploits **social hierarchy** (Category 22) and the **prestige pathway** to status. In every social group, deference to expertise is a norm -- and a generally useful one. The fallacious version parasitizes this useful norm.

It also exploits the **common ground heuristic**: "If an authority asserts X, then X can be treated as given information" -- effectively smuggling claims into the common ground via prestige rather than evidence.

#### 7.3 Why It Works Socially
- **Heuristic value**: Deferring to experts is a genuinely useful shortcut in a world where individuals cannot verify every claim personally. The fallacy exploits a heuristic that is *usually* reliable.
- **Social reinforcement**: People who challenge authorities face social sanction (appearing arrogant, ignorant, or insubordinate).
- **The credibility cascade**: Once an authority is accepted, their credibility extends to adjacent claims. If Dr. Smith is a respected oncologist, listeners extend credibility to Dr. Smith's opinions on nutrition, public policy, or even politics.

#### 7.4 Why Correcting It Is Costly
Challenging an appeal to authority is a **double face-threatening act**:
- It threatens the authority's positive face (their claim to expertise and competence)
- It threatens the authority's negative face (their right to be deferred to)
- It threatens the corrector's own positive face: they appear arrogant ("Who are you to question Dr. Smith?") or ignorant ("Dr. Smith has a PhD, do you?")

In hierarchical settings (workplaces, classrooms, medical consultations), challenging authority carries real social and material consequences -- not just face costs but potential retaliation.

### 8. Red Herring (Ignoratio Elenchi)

#### 8.1 Logical Structure
The speaker introduces topic B in response to a question or argument about topic A. The shift may be gradual (a series of small topic changes) or abrupt (a sudden, dramatic change of subject).

The term "red herring" likely derives from the practice of using smoked fish to train foxhounds by dragging it across a trail to test whether the dogs could maintain the scent despite the distraction (though this etymology is debated; Quinion, 2002, notes it may be apocryphal).

#### 8.2 Pragmatic Mechanism
Red herring exploits **relevance processing** (Category 10) and **topic management norms** (Category 13).

Under Relevance Theory, the listener's cognitive system automatically searches for the relevance of any new input. When the speaker introduces topic B, the listener's system works to find a connection between B and the current discussion topic A -- and if any connection can be found, however tenuous, the listener follows it.

Under conversation analysis, **topic shifts** are a normal part of conversation and are not typically challenged. The norms of polite conversation allow topics to evolve, drift, and change, and calling out a topic shift as illegitimate requires explicit meta-commentary ("Wait, you're changing the subject"), which is itself marked and face-threatening.

Red herrings also exploit the **Gricean expectation of relevance**: listeners assume that contributions are relevant to the current topic, so they try to interpret the new material as relevant -- and in doing so, they follow the deflection.

#### 8.3 Why It Works Socially
- Topics shift naturally in conversation; it is often difficult to distinguish a deliberate deflection from a natural association
- Audiences have limited attention and short memories -- once the conversation has moved to topic B, returning to topic A requires effort and social justification
- The new topic is often more emotionally engaging than the original (red herrings are frequently paired with appeals to emotion)

#### 8.4 Why Correcting It Is Costly
- Calling out a topic shift requires **meta-talk** ("You're changing the subject"), which shifts the conversation from substance to process -- and this shift itself looks like a deflection
- The speaker can claim the new topic IS relevant ("I was making a broader point") and now you are in a dispute about relevance rather than substance
- In group settings, others may have found the new topic genuinely interesting, and pulling the conversation back appears controlling

### 9. False Dilemma (False Dichotomy, Either/Or Fallacy)

#### 9.1 Logical Structure
"Either A or B. Not A. Therefore B." -- where the disjunction is presented as exhaustive but is not. There exist options C, D, E, etc. that are not mentioned.

#### 9.2 Pragmatic Mechanism
False dilemma exploits the **Quantity maxim**: listeners expect that the information provided is as complete as the situation requires. If a speaker presents two options, the listener's default inference (via the Quantity maxim) is that these are all the relevant options -- because if there were more, a cooperative speaker would have mentioned them.

This is a scalar implicature at the level of option sets: "A or B" implicates "A or B and no other relevant alternatives" just as "some" implicates "not all."

False dilemma also exploits **cognitive laziness** in the Relevance Theory sense: evaluating two options requires far less processing effort than generating and evaluating a range of alternatives. The listener takes the path of least cognitive effort and works within the frame provided.

#### 9.3 Why It Works Socially
- Binary framing is **narratively compelling** (hero vs. villain, us vs. them, right vs. wrong)
- In political and public discourse, binary framing aligns with **tribal affiliation** (party A vs. party B)
- Suggesting a third option can be perceived as **fence-sitting** or **indecisiveness**, which carries face costs in many social contexts
- The binary frame creates **urgency** (if there are only two options, the choice is immediate; if there are twenty, deliberation is needed)

#### 9.4 Why Correcting It Is Costly
- Proposing additional options requires more cognitive effort from the audience, and they may resist this effort
- In polarized social contexts, suggesting a middle position is attacked from both sides
- The corrector can be dismissed as "unable to make a decision" or "trying to have it both ways"

### 10. Slippery Slope

#### 10.1 Logical Structure
"If A, then B. If B, then C. If C, then D. ... If Y, then Z. Z is terrible. Therefore, not A." The argument claims an unbroken causal chain from a modest initial step to a catastrophic outcome.

Walton (1992, *Slippery Slope Arguments*, Clarendon Press) provides the most thorough analysis, distinguishing several subtypes:
- **Causal slippery slope**: Claims a factual causal chain
- **Precedent slippery slope**: Claims that allowing A will create a precedent requiring B
- **Conceptual slippery slope**: Claims that there is no principled distinction between A and Z (sorites-style)

#### 10.2 Pragmatic Mechanism
Slippery slope exploits **causal implicature** -- the human tendency to accept causal chains without examining each link individually. Listeners process the chain as a gestalt: "A leads to Z" -- skipping over the intermediate steps where the chain might break.

It also exploits **narrative coherence**: the slippery slope tells a *story* about what will happen, and stories are processed differently from arguments. Narrative processing engages different cognitive resources and is subject to different evaluation criteria (plausibility rather than logical necessity).

#### 10.3 Why It Works Socially
- Fear of catastrophic outcomes activates risk-aversion biases (loss aversion) -- the emotional weight of Z overwhelms the probabilistic assessment of the chain
- The audience faces an asymmetric wager: if they dismiss the slippery slope and Z actually happens, they are blamed; if they accept the slippery slope and Z does not happen, no one notices
- Slippery slopes are **unfalsifiable in advance**: you cannot prove that a chain of events will not occur; you can only argue that it is unlikely

#### 10.4 Why Correcting It Is Costly
- Challenging a slippery slope requires challenging each link in the causal chain, which is time-consuming and appears to minimize the risk ("Don't you care about Z?")
- If any part of the chain is even slightly plausible, the corrector appears to be denying reality
- The corrector can be accused of **naivety** ("That's what they said about [historical example]")

### 11. Loaded Question / Complex Question

#### 11.1 Logical Structure
A question that contains a presupposition that the answerer has not accepted. The classic example: "Have you stopped beating your wife?" presupposes that you have been beating your wife. Both "yes" and "no" accept the presupposition.

#### 11.2 Pragmatic Mechanism
This is a **pure presupposition exploit** (Category 9). The loaded question triggers presupposition accommodation: the audience processes the presupposed content (that the person beats their wife) as background information -- given, not new -- and focuses on the foreground question (whether they have stopped).

The pragmatic force is that questions create a **conditional relevance** for answers (Category 4, Category 17). Once a question has been asked, the addressee is under social obligation to provide a relevant answer. Any direct answer to the loaded question accepts the presupposition. Refusing to answer appears evasive. Challenging the presupposition requires a meta-communicative move ("Your question assumes something that isn't true") that disrupts the conversational flow.

#### 11.3 Why It Works Socially
- The question format gives the questioner **interactional control** (Category 17): questions demand answers, and the questioner sets the frame
- Audiences process presuppositions at a lower level of scrutiny than assertions
- The target faces a **pragmatic double bind**: answer the question (accept the presupposition) or refuse (appear evasive)

#### 11.4 Why Correcting It Is Costly
- Challenging the presupposition requires explicitly stating what is being presupposed, which may force the target to articulate the very accusation they want to deny ("Your question assumes I beat my wife, which I do not")
- The meta-communicative correction ("That's a loaded question") can appear rehearsed or lawyerly
- In public settings (press conferences, debates, hearings), the audience may remember the presupposition even after the correction -- the "don't think of an elephant" problem (Lakoff, 2004)

---

### 12. Appeal to Emotion (Argumentum ad Passiones)

#### 12.1 Logical Structure
Instead of providing evidence for claim C, the speaker evokes an emotional response (fear, pity, anger, disgust, pride) that predisposes the audience to accept C.

Subtypes include:
- **Appeal to fear** (argumentum ad metum): "If we don't do X, terrible Y will happen"
- **Appeal to pity** (argumentum ad misericordiam): "Think of the suffering children"
- **Appeal to anger/outrage**: "Aren't you furious about this injustice?"
- **Appeal to pride/flattery**: "Smart people like you understand that..."
- **Appeal to disgust**: "This practice is repulsive and must be stopped"

#### 12.2 Pragmatic Mechanism
Appeal to emotion exploits the **interaction between emotion and relevance processing**. Emotional content achieves high cognitive effects with low processing effort (it is highly "relevant" in the RT sense). The emotional response creates a feeling of *certainty* that mimics the feeling of having been persuaded by evidence.

It also exploits **face-protection dynamics**: emotions are bound up with face. Challenging someone's emotional appeal means challenging their emotional experience, which threatens their positive face. "You shouldn't feel afraid about this" attacks the legitimacy of their emotional response.

Walton (1992, *The Place of Emotion in Argument*, Penn State University Press; 2000, *Scare Tactics*) argues that emotion is not inherently fallacious in argumentation. Fear of real dangers is a legitimate consideration; pity for real suffering is a legitimate motivator. The fallacy occurs when emotion is used as a **substitute** for evidence rather than a **complement** to it.

#### 12.3 Why It Works Socially
- Emotional engagement creates **solidarity** between the speaker and the audience -- sharing an emotion is a powerful bonding mechanism
- Emotional arguments are **memorable** and **shareable** (they spread more readily than dry logical arguments)
- Resisting emotional appeals positions you as **outside the group** that shares the emotion

#### 12.4 Why Correcting It Is Costly
- Challenging emotional appeals makes you appear **cold, callous, or lacking empathy**
- The corrector faces a face dilemma: they must separate "I agree this situation is concerning" from "but your argument for X doesn't follow from that concern" -- and this distinction is extremely difficult to communicate without appearing dismissive of the emotion
- In group settings, emotional appeals create **social pressure**: others are visibly moved, and your analytical response isolates you

---

### 13. Tu Quoque / Whataboutism

#### 13.1 Logical Structure
"You did X too" or "What about when Y happened?" The speaker deflects criticism by pointing to the critic's own behavior or to a different situation.

#### 13.2 Pragmatic Mechanism
Tu quoque exploits **fairness norms** and **consistency expectations** -- deep social principles that are not logical principles. The underlying pragmatic claim is: "You cannot criticize me for X unless you are free of X yourself." This is the "hypocrisy charge."

It also exploits **topic management** (Category 13): the whataboutism introduces a new topic (the critic's behavior) that demands a response, deflecting from the original criticism. The critic now faces a **priority dilemma**: should they defend themselves against the counter-accusation or continue pressing the original point?

Tu quoque exploits what Jacobs and Jackson (1982) call the **reciprocity norm** in argumentation: the expectation that standards are applied symmetrically. If you hold me to standard S, you must also meet standard S. This is a legitimate norm in many contexts -- but tu quoque exploits it by using it to deflect from substance.

#### 13.3 Why It Works Socially
- **Hypocrisy is universally condemned** across cultures. The charge of hypocrisy is one of the most damaging social accusations. Once someone is labeled a hypocrite, their moral authority is undermined regardless of the merits of their argument.
- **Fairness feels more important than truth** in many social contexts. "That's not fair" is a more emotionally compelling response than "That's not true."
- The audience may genuinely be interested in the counterexample, making the deflection feel like a legitimate contribution.

#### 13.4 Why Correcting It Is Costly
- Dismissing the tu quoque ("My behavior isn't the topic here") appears to be **avoiding accountability**
- The corrector must somehow acknowledge the counterexample while maintaining that it is irrelevant -- a nuanced position that is difficult to articulate clearly
- In many social contexts, the consistency norm is so strong that being labeled a hypocrite is worse than being wrong on the substance

---

### 14. Bandwagon / Appeal to Popularity (Argumentum ad Populum)

#### 14.1 Logical Structure
"Most people believe X, therefore X is true" or "Everyone is doing Y, therefore Y is correct."

#### 14.2 Pragmatic Mechanism
Bandwagon exploits **common ground construction** (Category 16). The phrase "everyone knows that..." or "most people agree..." attempts to install a claim into the common ground by asserting that it is already there. If the listener accepts that "everyone knows X," then X becomes part of the shared background -- and questioning X means questioning what "everyone knows," which is socially costly.

It also exploits the **informational cascade** effect: people use others' beliefs as evidence. If many people believe X, then (absent other information) X is probabilistically more likely to be true. This is a legitimate heuristic in many situations (you should probably eat at the crowded restaurant rather than the empty one). The fallacy exploits this legitimate heuristic.

#### 14.3 Why It Works Socially
- **Conformity pressure** is a powerful social force (Asch, 1951; demonstrated that individuals will deny their own perceptions when a group disagrees)
- **In-group membership** is signaled by shared beliefs. Rejecting a popular belief risks being perceived as an outsider.
- **Cognitive offloading**: if "everyone" already believes X, the listener is spared the effort of evaluating X independently

#### 14.4 Why Correcting It Is Costly
- Rejecting the popular position marks you as a **contrarian** or **outsider**
- The correction requires you to argue against perceived social consensus, which can feel presumptuous
- The response "Well, everyone can be wrong" is true but sounds arrogant

---

### 15. Moving the Goalposts

#### 15.1 Logical Structure
A requests evidence for claim C. B provides evidence E. A then demands different or additional evidence E' that was not part of the original request, while implying that E was inadequate.

#### 15.2 Pragmatic Mechanism
Moving the goalposts exploits the **Cooperative Principle** and specifically the implicit **contract** that is established during the Opening stage of a critical discussion (van Eemeren & Grootendorst's Stage 2). When A asks "What evidence would convince you?", an implicit agreement is formed about the criteria for resolution. Moving the goalposts violates this agreement retroactively.

It also violates what van Eemeren and Grootendorst call the **Closure Rule** (Rule 9): "A failed defense of a standpoint must result in the proponent retracting the standpoint, and a successful defense must result in the opponent retracting doubt." Moving the goalposts prevents the closure rule from operating: the proponent can never successfully defend their position because the criteria keep changing.

#### 15.3 Why It Works Socially
- The goalpost-mover appears to be **maintaining high standards** ("I'm not easily convinced, which shows I'm rigorous")
- Each individual demand for more evidence appears reasonable in isolation -- the pattern is only visible across multiple exchanges
- Providing additional evidence is psychologically easier than confronting the pattern ("Maybe if I just give them ONE more piece of evidence...")

#### 15.4 Why Correcting It Is Costly
- Calling out goalpost-moving requires pointing to a **pattern across time**, which is more complex than pointing to a single error
- The goalpost-mover can always claim "I just want to be thorough" or "The new evidence is relevant"
- The corrector risks appearing to want a **premature conclusion** ("Why are you trying to shut down inquiry?")

### 16. Tone Policing

#### 16.1 Logical Structure
Instead of addressing the content of argument A, the speaker criticizes the emotional tone, delivery, or manner in which A was expressed. "I'd take your argument more seriously if you weren't so angry" or "You'd be more persuasive if you were calmer."

#### 16.2 Pragmatic Mechanism
Tone policing exploits **register norms** (Category 7) and **politeness norms** (Category 3). The tone police invokes the norm that rational discussion requires calm, detached delivery -- and that departing from this norm disqualifies the speaker's contribution.

This is a violation of van Eemeren and Grootendorst's **Freedom Rule** (Rule 1): it attempts to prevent a party from advancing their standpoint by attacking the manner of its expression rather than its content.

Tone policing also exploits the **Manner maxim** (be perspicuous, orderly, brief): the tone police claims that the speaker's emotional delivery violates Manner, and that this violation delegitimizes the content.

#### 16.3 Why It Works Socially
- There IS a real register norm in academic and professional discourse that favors calm, measured delivery. Tone policing parasitizes this legitimate norm.
- Tone policing **reframes the discussion**: instead of "Is the argument correct?", the question becomes "Was the argument delivered properly?" -- a much easier question for the audience to evaluate.
- Tone policing is especially effective against marginalized groups who have legitimate reasons for emotional expression. The anger of the oppressed is reframed as evidence of irrationality.
- It shifts the **burden of proof**: the target must now demonstrate that their emotional tone is justified before their argument will be considered.

#### 16.4 Why Correcting It Is Costly
- If you respond calmly, you validate the frame (that emotional delivery was the problem)
- If you respond angrily, you confirm the accusation
- Naming tone policing requires meta-commentary about the conversation itself, which can be perceived as escalation

#### 16.5 Special Relevance for Autistic Individuals
Tone policing is particularly important for autistic people because:
- Autistic individuals may have **atypical prosody** (Category 15) that is misread as anger, condescension, or indifference
- Autistic individuals may **not modulate emotional expression** in the ways neurotypical listeners expect
- Autistic individuals who have learned to identify fallacies may be especially vulnerable to the accusation that their *manner* of identification is the problem, not the fallacy itself
- The entire concept of tone policing maps onto the broader autistic experience of being told that *how* you say something matters more than *what* you say

### 17. Gish Gallop

#### 17.1 Logical Structure
The speaker makes a large number of arguments (often of varying quality) in rapid succession, too many for the opponent to address in the available time.

Named after Duane Gish (1921-2013), a biochemist and creationist debater who was famous for this tactic in debates about evolution. The term was coined by Eugenie Scott of the National Center for Science Education.

#### 17.2 Pragmatic Mechanism
The Gish gallop exploits **turn-taking** (Category 4) and **topic management** (Category 13). In conversation, each participant has a limited amount of turn-space. Addressing each claim requires time, and the Gish galloper produces claims faster than they can be rebutted.

It also exploits the **exhaustiveness implicature**: unanswered claims tend to be treated as conceded. If the respondent only addresses 3 of 15 claims, the audience may interpret the unaddressed 12 as conceded -- not because the respondent agrees with them, but because the conversational norm of addressing raised points was not met.

Additionally, the Gish gallop exploits **cognitive load**: evaluating the truth of each claim requires processing effort, and when claims arrive faster than the listener's processing capacity, evaluation quality degrades.

#### 17.3 Why It Works Socially
- Prolific argumentation creates an **impression of command** -- the Gish galloper appears knowledgeable and prepared
- The respondent appears **unable to keep up**, which the audience reads as intellectual inferiority
- Each unanswered claim plants a seed of doubt

#### 17.4 Why Correcting It Is Costly
- Naming the tactic ("You're overwhelming me with too many claims to address") appears to be an admission of defeat
- Selecting which claims to address involves visible **triage**, and the audience may assume the unaddressed claims are the ones you cannot refute
- Asking for fewer claims at a time appears to be **controlling the conversation** -- a face-threatening act against the galloper's negative face

---

### 18. Begging the Question (Petitio Principii)

#### 18.1 Logical Structure
The conclusion is assumed in one of the premises. "God exists because the Bible says so, and the Bible is the word of God." The argument is circular: it presupposes the very thing it purports to prove.

#### 18.2 Pragmatic Mechanism
Begging the question exploits **presupposition accommodation** (Category 9). The conclusion is embedded as a presupposition in one of the premises, so it enters the discourse as background information rather than asserted content. Because presuppositions receive less critical scrutiny than assertions, the circular reasoning goes unnoticed.

It violates van Eemeren and Grootendorst's **Starting Point Rule** (Rule 6): the conclusion is treated as an already-accepted starting point when it is actually the point at issue.

The pragmatic subtlety is that many cases of begging the question are not as transparently circular as the textbook examples. The conclusion and the premise may use different words to express the same proposition ("Compulsory seat-belt legislation is justified because it's the kind of thing the law should require"). The listener must notice that the premise and conclusion are semantically equivalent despite lexical difference -- a demanding inferential task.

#### 18.3 Why It Works Socially
- The premise and conclusion *feel* like different claims because they are expressed differently
- Complex arguments with multiple steps can hide circularity -- the conclusion may be restated as a premise several steps removed from its original assertion
- The arguer appears to be providing reasons when they are actually restating their position

#### 18.4 Why Correcting It Is Costly
- Demonstrating circularity requires showing that two differently-worded statements mean the same thing, which can appear pedantic
- The arguer can always rephrase ("That's not what I meant") and the corrector must demonstrate circularity again

---

### 19. Equivocation

#### 19.1 Logical Structure
A word or phrase is used with two different meanings in the course of an argument. "The end of a thing is its perfection. Death is the end of life. Therefore, death is the perfection of life." ("End" shifts from "purpose/goal" to "termination.")

#### 19.2 Pragmatic Mechanism
Equivocation exploits the **Manner maxim**: listeners assume that a speaker uses words consistently within a single argument. This expectation of consistent usage is deeply embedded in language processing -- we do not typically re-evaluate word meanings on each occurrence.

It also exploits the **lexical pragmatics** described in Relevance Theory: listeners construct ad hoc concepts for words based on context (see Category 10), and if the equivocator controls the context carefully, they can lead the listener to construct different concepts for the same word at different points without noticing.

#### 19.3 Why It Works Socially
- Many words are genuinely polysemous (have multiple related meanings), so the shift between meanings can be subtle
- The equivocator can always claim they meant the *other* meaning if caught
- In rapid conversation, listeners do not have time to track meaning shifts across multiple turns

#### 19.4 Why Correcting It Is Costly
- Demonstrating equivocation requires explicit semantic analysis ("In your first premise, you used 'freedom' to mean X, but in your conclusion, you used it to mean Y") -- which is highly marked, technical, and face-threatening
- The equivocator can claim consistent meaning ("That IS what I meant both times")

---

### 20. No True Scotsman

#### 20.1 Logical Structure
A: "No Scotsman puts sugar on his porridge."
B: "But my uncle Hamish is a Scotsman and he puts sugar on his porridge."
A: "Well, no TRUE Scotsman puts sugar on his porridge."

The speaker protects a universal claim from counterexamples by redefining the category to exclude them.

(The name was coined by Antony Flew, 1975, *Thinking About Thinking*, Fontana/Collins.)

#### 20.2 Pragmatic Mechanism
No True Scotsman exploits **definitional common ground** (Category 16) -- the shared understanding of what words mean. By retroactively modifying the definition of a category, the speaker removes the counterexample from the common ground without appearing to change their position.

It also exploits the **accommodating** nature of conversation: when a speaker introduces a new definition or refinement, listeners typically accommodate it rather than challenging it, because definitional disputes appear tangential to the substantive issue.

#### 20.3 Why It Works Socially
- The refined definition often appeals to an idealized or aspirational version of the category ("a REAL friend," "a TRUE professional")
- Challenging the redefinition requires a meta-discussion about definitions, which feels like a distraction from the substantive point
- The speaker appears to be making a *normative* rather than *descriptive* claim, which is harder to falsify

#### 20.4 Why Correcting It Is Costly
- Calling out the redefinition ("You just changed what you mean by 'Scotsman'") sounds pedantic
- The speaker can claim they always meant the refined version ("Obviously I meant a proper Scotsman")
- Definitional disputes are notoriously unresolvable and audiences lose patience with them

### 21. Poisoning the Well

#### 21.1 Logical Structure
Before the target speaks, the attacker pre-loads negative information about them. "Before my opponent speaks, you should know that he has a financial interest in this outcome." Any subsequent argument from the target is received through the lens of this preemptive discrediting.

#### 21.2 Pragmatic Mechanism
Poisoning the well exploits **common ground** (Category 16) and **presupposition** (Category 9). The negative information about the target is introduced into the common ground before the target has the opportunity to speak. Once installed in the common ground, this information functions as a **contextual assumption** that colors the interpretation of everything the target subsequently says.

It also exploits the **primacy effect**: information encountered first has a disproportionate influence on subsequent judgments. The well-poisoner ensures that the audience's first contact with the target is through the lens of negative framing.

This is a violation of van Eemeren and Grootendorst's **Freedom Rule** (Rule 1): it attempts to prevent the target from effectively advancing their standpoint by preemptively undermining their credibility.

#### 21.3 Why It Works Socially
- Once the audience has processed the negative framing, they cannot easily "unprocess" it (a phenomenon well-documented in cognitive psychology)
- The target must now address BOTH their substantive argument AND the credibility attack, dividing their limited turn-space
- The well-poisoner appears to be providing helpful background information ("I'm just giving you the full picture")

#### 21.4 Why Correcting It Is Costly
- The target must address the credibility attack first, which means they spend their turn on defense rather than substance
- Ignoring the attack lets it stand unchallenged
- Complaining about the well-poisoning ("That was unfair") appears weak and deflective

---

### 22. False Equivalence

#### 22.1 Logical Structure
Two positions or phenomena are presented as equivalent when they differ in significant ways. "Both sides have valid points" when one side has overwhelming evidence and the other does not. "He's just as bad" when one actor's behavior is categorically different from the other's.

#### 22.2 Pragmatic Mechanism
False equivalence exploits the **balance heuristic**: the cultural norm (particularly strong in journalism and public discourse) that presenting "both sides" demonstrates fairness and objectivity. This norm leads audiences to expect that when two positions are presented, they have roughly equal epistemic weight.

It also exploits the **scalar implicature** of comparison: if someone says "A and B both have merits," the implicature is that the merits are roughly comparable in magnitude.

Additionally, false equivalence exploits the **common ground assumption** that the speaker has selected comparisons that are genuinely comparable. If a speaker puts A and B side by side, the listener accommodates the framing that A and B are the same *type* of thing and can be evaluated on the same scale.

#### 22.3 Why It Works Socially
- Appearing balanced and fair is a strong social desideratum -- those who present "both sides" are perceived as more credible than those who take a clear position
- Audiences who are not deeply informed about the topic use the speaker's framing to calibrate their expectations
- False equivalence provides cognitive comfort: "The truth is probably somewhere in the middle" is a low-effort heuristic that avoids the work of actual evaluation

#### 22.4 Why Correcting It Is Costly
- Correcting false equivalence requires arguing that the comparison itself is illegitimate ("These two things are not comparable"), which can appear partisan or biased
- The corrector must demonstrate the asymmetry, which requires detailed evidence
- The response "You're just saying your side is right" reframes the correction as mere partisanship

## PART FOUR: THE CORRECTION TRAP

### 7. Why Correcting Fallacies Is Socially Penalized

This section consolidates the correction-cost analysis from each individual fallacy into a unified framework. The Correction Trap is the central pragmatic insight of this category: **the social systems that make fallacies effective are the same systems that penalize correction.**

#### 7.1 The Five Faces of the Correction Trap

| Trap | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **The Pedantry Trap** | Naming a fallacy by its technical name makes you appear pedantic, as though you are using specialized jargon to intimidate rather than engage. | "That's an ad hominem" → "Oh, so you're going to lecture me on Latin now?" |
| **The Meta-Communication Trap** | Correcting a fallacy requires shifting from substance to process (from the topic to how the conversation is being conducted). This shift can be perceived as evasive -- as though you are dodging the substance by complaining about procedure. | "You're changing the subject" → "If my point about [deflection topic] isn't relevant, why can't you answer it?" |
| **The Face-Threat Trap** | Naming a fallacy is inherently a face-threatening act: it accuses the other person of arguing unfairly, which threatens their positive face (competence) and negative face (autonomy). | "That's a straw man" → "Are you calling me a liar?" |
| **The Evasion Trap** | Refusing to engage with a fallacious framing can be perceived as evasion -- as though you cannot answer the (loaded, misdirected, or emotionally manipulative) claim. | "I'm not going to address a loaded question" → "So you can't deny it?" |
| **The Double-Bind Trap** | The fallacy creates a frame where any response validates the fallacy, and refusing to respond also validates it. There is no response within the frame that does not lose ground. | "Have you stopped cheating?" → Yes (you were cheating), No (you're still cheating), Refuse (you're hiding something) |

#### 7.2 The Asymmetry of Effort

Correcting a fallacy almost always requires more conversational resources (time, turn-space, cognitive effort, social capital) than deploying one:

| Resource | Deploying the Fallacy | Correcting the Fallacy |
|---|---|---|
| **Time** | Often one sentence ("That's just your opinion") | Requires unpacking the fallacy, explaining why it is flawed, and restating the original point |
| **Cognitive load** | Low (heuristic-based, pattern-matching) | High (requires identifying the specific flaw, articulating it, and anticipating counter-responses) |
| **Social capital** | Low cost (fallacies often align with social dynamics -- defending the group, deferring to authority, expressing emotion) | High cost (correction goes against social dynamics -- challenging the group, questioning authority, resisting emotion) |
| **Audience engagement** | High (fallacies are often entertaining, emotional, or narrative) | Low (corrections are analytical, technical, and meta-communicative) |

This asymmetry means that in real-time conversation, the person deploying a fallacy has a structural advantage. The correction will almost always take longer, require more effort, and cost more socially than the fallacy itself.

#### 7.3 The Performative Contradiction

There is a deeper issue: the act of naming a fallacy can itself be deployed as a fallacy. "That's a straw man!" can be used as a **conversational trump card** -- a way to dismiss an argument without engaging with it. If I simply label your argument as a fallacy, I have performed the same move as the fallacies I am supposedly correcting: I have shifted from substance to meta-commentary, used a technical label to gain social advantage, and avoided engaging with the content of your argument.

This creates a **regress problem**: if naming a fallacy can itself be a rhetorical tactic, then the corrector's correction can be corrected, and so on. The escape from this regress requires not just identifying the fallacy but **demonstrating why it is a fallacy** -- showing the audience exactly how the argument fails. But this demonstration is precisely what the Correction Trap makes socially costly.

#### 7.4 The Autistic Double Bind

For autistic individuals, the Correction Trap is compounded by several factors:

1. **Autistic individuals often have strong pattern-recognition abilities** and may detect fallacies more readily than neurotypical interlocutors. But detection without socially safe correction strategies leads to frustration: you can see the problem but cannot point it out without social penalty.

2. **Autistic communication tends toward directness and precision** (see Category 7 on register). The indirect, hedged, face-saving correction strategies that neurotypical speakers use to mitigate the Correction Trap ("Well, I think there might be another way to look at it...") may not come naturally.

3. **Autistic prosody** may be misread as hostile or condescending (Category 15), making any correction appear more aggressive than intended.

4. **The "tone policing" meta-fallacy** is frequently deployed against autistic speakers who identify fallacies. The autistic person who says "That's a logical fallacy" may be told they are "being argumentative," "missing the point," or "not reading the room" -- all of which are pragmatic sanctions for violating social norms rather than assessments of logical correctness.

5. **Autistic individuals may struggle with the implicit negotiation** that neurotypical speakers use to correct fallacies indirectly (through questions, reframings, and gentle topic redirections rather than direct naming).

## PART FIVE: FALLACIES IN ARGUMENTATION THEORY -- DEEPER FRAMEWORKS

### 8. The Relationship Between Argumentation Norms and Politeness Norms

This is one of the most important and underexplored tensions in pragmatics: **the norms that govern good argumentation and the norms that govern polite conversation are frequently in conflict.**

#### 8.1 The Conflict

| Argumentation Norm | Politeness Norm | The Conflict |
|---|---|---|
| Challenge unsupported claims | Don't question others' competence (positive face) | Asking "What's your evidence?" threatens face |
| Point out logical errors | Don't embarrass others publicly | Saying "That doesn't follow" is a face attack |
| Insist on relevance | Allow natural topic drift | Saying "That's irrelevant" disrupts social flow |
| Demand clarity | Don't impose on others' autonomy (negative face) | Saying "What exactly do you mean?" sounds interrogative |
| Hold people to their commitments | Allow face-saving retreats | Saying "But you said X earlier" prevents face-saving |
| Evaluate all claims on their merits | Defer to status/authority | Challenging a superior's argument violates hierarchy |
| Pursue truth regardless of social cost | Maintain social harmony | Truth-seeking can be socially destructive |

This conflict is not accidental -- it is structural. Argumentation norms prioritize **truth** and **logical rigor**. Politeness norms prioritize **social cohesion** and **face maintenance**. These are different goals, and they regularly pull in opposite directions.

#### 8.2 How Different Cultures Resolve the Tension

The resolution of this tension varies across cultures and contexts:

- **Academic seminars** resolve it in favor of argumentation norms (within limits) -- you CAN say "That doesn't follow" and it is treated as a contribution rather than an attack. But even in academic settings, there are face-management strategies: "That's an interesting point, but I wonder if..." is preferred to "You're wrong."

- **Courtrooms** have explicit institutional rules that override politeness norms: witnesses can be aggressively cross-examined, and the judge's role includes making face-threatening evaluations.

- **Casual conversation** resolves it overwhelmingly in favor of politeness norms. Identifying a friend's logical fallacy at a dinner party is a social violation regardless of its logical accuracy.

- **Online discourse** often resolves it in favor of argumentation norms (or eristic norms), partly because the reduced social cues of text-based communication lower the perceived face costs. This is why "That's a logical fallacy" is far more common online than in face-to-face conversation.

#### 8.3 Walton's Dialogue Types as Norm-Conflict Resolution

Walton's dialogue typology (Section 3.2) can be read as a map of how different conversational contexts resolve the argumentation-politeness conflict:

| Dialogue Type | Which Norms Dominate | Fallacy Tolerance |
|---|---|---|
| **Inquiry** | Argumentation norms | Very low -- the goal is truth; fallacies are blocked |
| **Persuasion** | Mixed, with argumentation norms primary | Low to moderate -- but strategic maneuvering is expected |
| **Deliberation** | Mixed, context-dependent | Moderate -- pragmatic and emotional considerations are legitimate |
| **Information-seeking** | Mixed, depends on hierarchy | Moderate -- the information-seeker has limited right to challenge |
| **Negotiation** | Rhetorical norms | High -- many "fallacies" (appeals to emotion, threats, strategic ambiguity) are legitimate moves |
| **Eristic (quarrel)** | No norms dominate | Maximum -- anything goes; there is no expectation of rational resolution |

This framework is crucial for autistic individuals because it provides a **diagnostic tool**: before identifying a fallacy, first identify the **type of dialogue** you are in. A move that is fallacious in persuasion dialogue may be legitimate in negotiation or deliberation. And a move that is fallacious in any context may still be socially penalized to identify, depending on the politeness norms in force.

### 9. The Informal Logic Contribution: Argumentation as a Social Practice

Johnson (2000, *Manifest Rationality: A Pragmatic Theory of Argument*, Lawrence Erlbaum Associates) introduced the concept of the **dialectical tier** of argument. An argument has two tiers:

1. **The illative tier**: The logical relationship between premises and conclusion (the traditional focus of logic)
2. **The dialectical tier**: The arguer's engagement with alternative positions, objections, and criticisms (the social dimension)

Johnson argued that a good argument must satisfy both tiers: it must be logically sound (illative tier) AND it must demonstrate awareness of and engagement with opposing views (dialectical tier). An argument that is logically valid but ignores all objections is dialectically inadequate.

This distinction maps directly onto the pragmatic analysis of fallacies:

- **Formal fallacies** are failures at the illative tier -- the logical structure is defective
- **Informal fallacies** are typically failures at the dialectical tier -- the arguer is failing to engage fairly with the opponent's position, is smuggling in premises, is deflecting from objections, or is otherwise violating the social norms of argumentative exchange

The informal fallacies, which are almost entirely pragmatic in nature, outnumber the formal fallacies by a wide margin. Govier's (2010) taxonomy lists approximately 15 formal fallacies and over 60 informal ones. This ratio itself is evidence that fallacy is primarily a pragmatic phenomenon.

### 11. The Correction Strategy Toolkit

While a full treatment of correction strategies belongs in the practical/applied document, the research literature offers several evidence-based approaches:

#### 11.1 Questioning Rather Than Labeling

Instead of naming the fallacy ("That's a straw man"), ask a question that exposes it:
- "Can you repeat back what you think my position is?" (forces the straw-manner to either accurately restate or visibly distort)
- "What evidence supports that causal chain?" (challenges slippery slope without naming it)
- "I'd like to stay on the original topic -- can you respond to [specific point]?" (addresses red herring through topic management)

This approach uses the **pragmatic power of questions** (Category 17) to shift the burden back to the fallacy-deployer without the face costs of direct accusation.

#### 11.2 Reframing Rather Than Correcting

Instead of pointing out that a fallacy has been committed, reframe the conversation:
- "I think the key question here is..." (redirects from the fallacious framing to the substantive issue)
- "That's a related point, but the specific question we need to answer is..." (acknowledges the red herring without attacking)
- "Let me state my actual position..." (corrects a straw man by restating, without accusing)

#### 11.3 Acknowledging the Legitimate Kernel

Many fallacies contain a legitimate element that has been overextended. Acknowledging this element before correcting the overextension reduces face cost:
- "You're right that authority matters in this field, and Dr. Smith is very credible. The question is whether her expertise in X transfers to the claim about Y." (acknowledges appeal to authority's legitimate element)
- "I understand your concern about where this could lead. Let me address the specific step in the chain I think is unlikely..." (acknowledges slippery slope's legitimate concern)

#### 11.4 Meta-Commentary as a Last Resort

Direct meta-commentary ("You're committing a fallacy") is the highest-cost correction strategy and should be used only when:
- Other strategies have failed
- The fallacy is persistent and systematic
- The stakes are high enough to justify the social cost
- The relationship can absorb the face threat

Even then, the meta-commentary should be **specific** ("You asked me to provide evidence, and I did. Now you're asking for different evidence. Can we agree on what would satisfy this question?") rather than **labeled** ("You're moving the goalposts").

## PART SEVEN: KEY THEORETICAL DISTINCTIONS

### 13. Formal vs. Informal Fallacies -- The Pragmatic Divide

| Dimension | Formal Fallacies | Informal Fallacies |
|---|---|---|
| **Definition** | Errors in the logical STRUCTURE of the argument | Errors in the CONTENT, CONTEXT, or PRAGMATIC deployment of the argument |
| **Detection method** | Can be detected by examining logical form alone, regardless of content | Require understanding context, content, and social dynamics |
| **Examples** | Affirming the consequent, denying the antecedent, undistributed middle, illicit major/minor | All 18 fallacies detailed in Part Three |
| **Context-dependence** | Context-independent: always invalid regardless of context | Highly context-dependent: may be legitimate in some dialogue types |
| **Frequency** | Relatively rare in natural discourse (people rarely construct formal syllogisms) | Extremely common in all forms of discourse |
| **Pragmatic dimension** | Minimal -- the error is structural | Central -- the "error" is in how the argument exploits social and communicative norms |
| **Social cost of correction** | Low (the error is demonstrable and impersonal) | High (correction involves face threats, meta-commentary, and social risk) |

The key insight: **informal fallacies are the ones that matter for pragmatics**, and they are the ones most difficult to counter, precisely because they exploit the social infrastructure of communication rather than violating rules of formal logic.

### 14. The Spectrum of Intentionality

Not all fallacies are deliberate. The pragmatic analysis recognizes a spectrum:

| Level | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Fully intentional** | The speaker knowingly deploys a fallacy as a rhetorical tactic | A politician who has been coached to use whataboutism to deflect from a scandal |
| **Semi-intentional** | The speaker feels that something about the argument is advantageous but does not consciously analyze why | A person who instinctively attacks the speaker rather than the argument when feeling threatened |
| **Unintentional but motivated** | The speaker's reasoning is genuinely flawed, but the flaw serves their interests (motivated reasoning) | A person who genuinely believes the false dilemma because the excluded options are inconvenient |
| **Genuinely unintentional** | The speaker is making a sincere reasoning error | A person who commits a slippery slope because they genuinely believe each link in the chain is inevitable |

For the pragmatic analysis, **intentionality is secondary**. Whether a fallacy is deliberate or accidental, it has the same pragmatic effect: it exploits the social systems of communication in the same way, and it creates the same Correction Trap for the interlocutor. The pragmatic analysis focuses on **mechanism** (how the fallacy works) rather than **intention** (whether the speaker meant it).

This is important for autistic learners because it removes the need for **mind-reading**: you do not need to determine whether someone "meant" to commit a fallacy in order to recognize the pragmatic dynamics at play. The pattern is the same regardless of intent.

### 15. Fallacies vs. Heuristics -- The Blurry Boundary

Many fallacies exploit **reasoning heuristics** that are generally useful:

| Heuristic | When It Is Useful | When It Becomes a Fallacy |
|---|---|---|
| **Defer to experts** | Medical advice, technical decisions, specialized knowledge | When authority in one domain is extended to another; when authority is used to block inquiry |
| **Follow the crowd** | Restaurant selection, danger avoidance, cultural norms | When popularity is used as evidence of truth |
| **Consider the source** | Evaluating testimony, assessing reliability | When the source's character is used to dismiss their argument entirely |
| **Trust emotion** | Danger detection, moral intuition, empathy | When emotion is used as a substitute for evidence |
| **Assume causal chains** | Planning, risk assessment, prediction | When the chain is extended beyond evidence (slippery slope) |
| **Seek balance** | Conflict resolution, fairness, perspective-taking | When asymmetric situations are forced into false equivalence |
| **Expect consistency** | Social contracts, fairness, reciprocity | When inconsistency is used to deflect from substance (tu quoque) |

The boundary between useful heuristic and fallacious reasoning is not always sharp. This is one of the reasons fallacies are so effective: they piggyback on reasoning strategies that are *usually* reliable. The challenge is recognizing when a generally useful heuristic is being misapplied -- and this recognition is a pragmatic judgment, not a logical one.

---

### 16. The Social Functions of Fallacies

A final theoretical point: fallacies are not always deployed to "win" an argument. They serve several social functions:

| Social Function | How Fallacies Serve It | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Group solidarity** | Bandwagon, appeal to emotion, and ad hominem against out-group members reinforce in-group cohesion | "We all know that [out-group] can't be trusted" |
| **Face maintenance** | Tu quoque and whataboutism protect the speaker's face by deflecting criticism | "Before you criticize me, look at yourself" |
| **Hierarchy maintenance** | Appeal to authority and tone policing maintain existing power structures | "The director has made the decision. Do you think you know better?" |
| **Conflict avoidance** | False equivalence and appeal to moderation allow the speaker to avoid taking a position | "Both sides have valid points" (when they do not) |
| **Social signaling** | Using certain fallacies signals group membership and loyalty | Deploying whataboutism to defend one's political tribe signals loyalty to that tribe |
| **Topic control** | Red herrings and Gish gallops allow the speaker to control what is discussed | Flooding a meeting with tangential points to prevent discussion of an uncomfortable topic |

Understanding these social functions is essential for the pragmatic analysis because it explains why fallacies persist even when they are widely recognized: they serve social purposes beyond mere persuasion. A person who deploys a bandwagon argument may not be trying to convince you of a proposition; they may be trying to include you in a group, or to signal their own membership. Recognizing the social function helps in choosing the appropriate response.
