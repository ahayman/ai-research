# Relevance Theory Applied to Autism: Deep Research Document

## Research Purpose
This document provides comprehensive, in-depth research on how Relevance Theory (Sperber & Wilson 1986/1995) has been applied to understanding autistic communication. It is written for a knowledge base teaching English pragmatics to autistic individuals. A prior document (category-02-implicature-research-relevance-theory.md) briefly introduced Happé (1993) and Williams/Wharton/Jagoe (2021). This document goes substantially deeper into every aspect of that research and its implications.

## 1. HAPPÉ (1993): The First Direct Application of RT to Autism

### 1.1 Background and Motivation

Francesca Happé's 1993 paper "Communicative competence and theory of mind in autism: A test of relevance theory" (published in *Cognition*, 48, 101-119) was the first study to directly apply Sperber and Wilson's Relevance Theory framework to understanding autistic communication. The paper was groundbreaking because it moved beyond simply cataloguing autistic communication "deficits" and instead used a rigorous theoretical framework to make *specific, testable predictions* about what autistic individuals at different cognitive levels should and should not be able to understand.

The study was motivated by two converging observations:
1. Sperber and Wilson's (1986) Relevance Theory makes the role of **intention recognition** explicit in communication. Understanding what someone means requires recognizing what they *intended* to communicate -- and this requires the ability to attribute mental states.
2. Baron-Cohen, Leslie, and Frith (1985) had proposed that autistic individuals have a specific impairment in **Theory of Mind** (ToM) -- the ability to attribute beliefs, desires, and intentions to others. If ToM is genuinely impaired in autism, and if RT is correct that communication depends on intention recognition, then RT should make *precise predictions* about which communicative phenomena would be difficult for autistic individuals at different levels of ToM ability.

### 1.2 Happé's Three-Level Model

Happé's central theoretical contribution was mapping RT's comprehension procedure onto three levels of Theory of Mind ability, generating specific predictions for each:

#### Level 1: No Theory of Mind
An individual with no ToM ability cannot attribute any mental states to others. In RT terms, this means they cannot recognize a speaker's **informative intention** (what the speaker intends to inform them of) or their **communicative intention** (the speaker's intention to make their informative intention recognized). Such an individual can still process language *semantically* -- they can decode the literal linguistic meaning of sentences. But they cannot engage in the inferential process that RT describes as necessary for deriving **implicatures** or recognizing non-literal uses of language.

**Prediction:** Individuals with no ToM should understand **similes** (because similes are comparatively explicit -- "She is *like* a bulldozer" makes the comparison overt) but fail at **metaphors** (because "She *is* a bulldozer" requires recognizing the speaker's intention to be understood non-literally) and fail at **irony** (because ironic utterances require recognizing that the speaker is echoing a thought with a dissociative attitude).

#### Level 2: First-Order Theory of Mind
An individual with first-order ToM can attribute mental states to others: "She thinks X," "He wants Y." In RT terms, this means they can recognize a speaker's informative intention. They can understand that a speaker intends to communicate something beyond the literal meaning of their words.

**Prediction:** Individuals with first-order ToM should be able to understand **metaphors**, because understanding metaphor in RT requires recognizing the speaker's intention to communicate a meaning that is a "loose" version of the literal meaning. The hearer needs to recognize "the speaker doesn't literally mean she is a piece of heavy machinery; the speaker intends to communicate that she shares certain properties with a bulldozer (powerful, unstoppable, doesn't care what's in her path)." This requires first-order intention attribution.

#### Level 3: Second-Order Theory of Mind
An individual with second-order ToM can attribute mental states about mental states: "She thinks that he thinks X." In RT terms, this enables recognition not just of what a speaker intends to communicate, but of the speaker's attitude toward what they are communicating.

**Prediction:** Second-order ToM should be needed for **irony**, because irony in RT is analyzed as an "echoic" use of language. When someone says ironically "What lovely weather!" during a storm, they are *echoing* a thought or expectation (that the weather would be lovely) and expressing a *dissociative attitude* toward it (that this thought is absurd). Understanding irony thus requires: (a) recognizing the speaker's informative intention (what they mean), (b) recognizing that the speaker is echoing an attributed thought, and (c) recognizing the speaker's attitude of dissociation toward that thought. This involves meta-representing someone else's meta-representation -- a second-order operation.

### 1.3 The Three Experiments

Happé reported three experiments testing these predictions. Participants were autistic individuals grouped by their ToM ability (determined by standard false-belief tasks) and compared with typically developing controls matched for mental age.

**Experiment 1:** Tested comprehension of **similes** vs. **metaphors**. Participants heard stories ending in a figurative utterance and had to explain what the speaker meant. As predicted:
- Autistic individuals who failed ToM tasks could understand similes but not metaphors.
- Autistic individuals who passed first-order ToM tasks could understand both similes and metaphors.

**Experiment 2:** Extended to **irony**. As predicted:
- Even autistic individuals who passed first-order ToM tasks struggled with irony.
- Only autistic individuals who also passed second-order ToM tasks showed competence with irony.

**Experiment 3:** Used more naturalistic story materials (the "Strange Stories" test, which Happé later published as a separate instrument). This tested whether the findings extended to more ecologically valid materials. Results were consistent: communicative competence was closely tied to ToM level, with even autistic individuals who passed all standard ToM tests showing more difficulty than controls on the most complex naturalistic materials.

### 1.4 How This Maps to RT's Comprehension Procedure

Happé's framework maps onto the RT comprehension procedure as follows:

The RT comprehension procedure states: "Follow a path of least effort in computing cognitive effects: test interpretive hypotheses in order of accessibility, and stop when your expectations of relevance are satisfied" (Wilson & Sperber 2002: 261).

For an individual with **no ToM**, the procedure is truncated. They can decode linguistic meaning and may search for relevance in the decoded content, but they cannot access the speaker's intended meaning through inferential processes that require attributing intentions. They process language for relevance but only at the level of what Happé called **"local" processing** -- deriving relevance from the decoded linguistic content itself, without the higher-order inferential steps that require mindreading.

For an individual with **first-order ToM**, the procedure operates more fully. They can recognize that a speaker *intended* to communicate something, and they can test hypotheses about what the speaker meant. They can recognize that the literal meaning of "She's a bulldozer" doesn't achieve relevance in the context (because the person is obviously not a piece of machinery), and they can search for an alternative interpretation that does achieve relevance (she shares certain properties with a bulldozer). This is the mechanism by which metaphors become accessible.

For an individual with **second-order ToM**, the full procedure operates, including the ability to recognize echoic use. They can identify that a speaker is echoing a thought attributable to someone (possibly the hearer, or a cultural expectation, or a previous utterance) and expressing a dissociative attitude toward it.

### 1.5 Subsequent Support and Challenge

Happé's model has been enormously influential. It was the first to provide a specific cognitive mechanism for why autistic individuals have differential difficulties with different types of figurative language, rather than simply observing a general "pragmatic deficit."

**Support:**
- Multiple subsequent studies confirmed the general pattern: metaphor comprehension correlates with first-order ToM, irony comprehension with second-order ToM (see meta-analysis by Kalandadze et al. 2018, published in *Research in Autism Spectrum Disorders*).
- Papp (2006, "A Relevance-Theoretic Account of the Development and Deficits of Theory of Mind in Normally Developing Children and Individuals with Autism," *Theory & Psychology*) extended Happé's framework by arguing that the two-tiered ToM system (first-order/second-order) was insufficient. Papp proposed that multiple layers of meta-representation are needed to account for the comprehension of different communicative phenomena -- metaphors, understatements, overstatements, direct and indirect speech acts, lies, deceits, humor, jokes, irony, and sarcasm. Each requires a progressively more sophisticated level of mindreading.

**Challenge:**
- **The role of language ability:** Subsequent meta-analyses (e.g., Kalandadze et al. 2018) found that when autistic individuals are matched with controls for *language ability* rather than just mental age, many of the differences in figurative language comprehension diminish substantially. This suggests that core language skills, not just ToM, are a major factor.
- **The deficit framing:** The most fundamental challenge came from the neurodiversity movement and from researchers like Williams, Wharton, and Jagoe (discussed in detail below), who argued that Happé's model located the "problem" entirely in the autistic individual's mind. It treated autistic communication as "testing ground" for pragmatic hypotheses based on assumed deficits, rather than considering communication as a bidirectional process.
- **Wearing (2010):** Philosopher Catherine Wearing published "Autism, Metaphor and Relevance Theory" in *Mind & Language* (25(2), 196-216). She addressed an apparent challenge: RT treats metaphor as being on a continuum with literal language (both involve the same comprehension procedure, with metaphor being "loose use"), yet autistic individuals show a dissociation between literal and metaphorical comprehension. Wearing argued that RT can actually accommodate this -- the processing demands of deriving the intended ad hoc concept in metaphor are greater than for literal language, even though the same procedure is used, because metaphor requires more extensive contextual inference and narrowing/loosening of concepts.

## 2. WILLIAMS, WHARTON & JAGOE (2021): The Mutual Misunderstanding Approach

### 2.1 The Paper and Its Significance

"Mutual (Mis)understanding: Reframing Autistic Pragmatic 'Impairments' Using Relevance Theory" by Gemma L. Williams, Tim Wharton, and Caroline Jagoe was published in *Frontiers in Psychology* in April 2021 (12: 616664). This paper represented a paradigm shift in how RT is applied to autism. Rather than using RT to model what autistic individuals *cannot* do (as Happé had), Williams et al. used RT to model what happens when autistic and non-autistic people *try to communicate with each other* -- and showed that the difficulty is genuinely **mutual**.

### 2.2 The Double Empathy Problem Meets Relevance Theory

The paper builds on Damian Milton's **double empathy problem** (Milton 2012, "On the ontological status of autism: The 'double empathy problem'," *Disability & Society*, 27(6), 883-887). Milton defined the double empathy problem as "a disjuncture in reciprocity between two differently disposed social actors which becomes more marked the wider the disjuncture in dispositional perceptions of the lifeworld."

Milton's insight was that communication difficulties between autistic and non-autistic people are not caused by autistic people having a "deficit" -- they are caused by a *mismatch* between two differently configured minds. Non-autistic people have just as much difficulty understanding autistic minds as autistic people have understanding non-autistic minds. The problem is bidirectional.

Williams et al. realized that RT provides the *formal mechanism* for explaining how this mismatch operates at the level of individual utterances. The key concept is **mutual manifestness**.

### 2.3 The Mechanism: Mutual Manifestness and Cognitive Environments

In RT, a person's **cognitive environment** consists of all the facts and assumptions that are *manifest* to them -- that is, available for representation, whether through perception, memory, or inference. These include:
- Perceptual information from the immediate environment
- Background knowledge and beliefs
- Assumptions about the other person
- Cultural norms and expectations

A **mutual cognitive environment** exists when the same assumptions are manifest to both interlocutors, AND both recognize that these assumptions are mutually manifest. This mutual recognition is crucial because successful communication depends not just on sharing information, but on knowing that you share it.

Williams et al.'s core argument is: **Because autistic and non-autistic people have markedly different embodied experiences, sensory profiles, attention patterns, and ways of organizing knowledge, their cognitive environments will overlap only partially.** What is manifest to an autistic person may not be manifest to a non-autistic person, and vice versa. More critically, neither party may recognize the extent of this mismatch.

When both parties *assume* mutual manifestness that does not actually exist, they each interpret the other's utterances using their own cognitive environment, deriving interpretations the speaker never intended. The result is **mutual misunderstanding** -- not because either party is deficient, but because the cognitive environments are insufficiently aligned.

### 2.4 The Study Design

Williams et al. studied eight core autistic adult participants who each engaged in three 10-minute naturalistic conversations about loneliness:

1. **Familiar cross-dispositional:** With a chosen non-autistic conversation partner (partner X -- typically a spouse, close friend, or family member)
2. **Unfamiliar matched-dispositional:** With an unfamiliar autistic stranger (partner A)
3. **Unfamiliar cross-dispositional:** With an unfamiliar non-autistic stranger (partner B)

This three-condition design allowed comparison of:
- Familiar vs. unfamiliar (does knowing someone help?)
- Same neurotype vs. different neurotype (does sharing a neurotype help?)

All conversations were transcribed using Conversation Analysis conventions and analyzed qualitatively through iterative coding. Four analytic "motifs" emerged: **flow**, **tuning-in**, **running along edges of meaning**, and **mutual manifestness**.

### 2.5 Key Findings

#### Finding 1: Matched-dispositional conversations showed dramatically better communication
All five autistic-autistic (matched-dispositional) conversations were characterized by significantly increased flow, rapport, and intersubjective attunement compared to cross-dispositional conversations. This held even though the matched-dispositional partners were *strangers*, while the cross-dispositional partners were often long-term intimates.

Specific improvements included:
- More fluid turn-taking with fewer uncomfortable gaps
- Spontaneous humor and shared laughter
- Sustained mutual engagement
- For three participants (A1, A6, A8): the best individual communicative performance observed across all their conversations

#### Finding 2: Individual communicative competence improved with matched partners
This was perhaps the most striking finding. Participant A6, for example, showed frequent stuttering, false starts, and labored speech when conversing with non-autistic partners. But when conversing with autistic stranger A5, "stumbles, pauses and re-starts...are almost entirely absent and replaced with concise, cogent turns."

The researchers interpreted this through RT: when increased mutual manifestness makes understanding less effortful, more cognitive resources become available for language production. The monotropic attention system, instead of being divided between speaking and monitoring the non-autistic partner's reactions, could be more fully devoted to formulating and expressing ideas.

#### Finding 3: Familiar cross-dispositional conversations were often the most difficult
Counter-intuitively, conversations between autistic individuals and their *familiar* non-autistic partners (spouses, close friends) were often more strained than conversations with non-autistic *strangers*. Williams et al. explain this: long-term familiarity creates stronger (and potentially more erroneous) assumptions of mutual manifestness. Each partner assumes they know what the other means, when in fact their cognitive environments remain partially opaque to each other.

#### Finding 4: Specific examples of RT-analyzable miscommunication

**Example 1: The Loneliness Definition (A4 and X4)**
Autistic participant A4 repeatedly states he doesn't "know what the word loneliness means." His familiar non-autistic partner X4 interprets this as A4 simply not experiencing loneliness. But A4 is actually trying to communicate something more nuanced -- that his experience of being alone is qualitatively different from what X4 understands as "loneliness." The RT analysis: A4 and X4 each assume the concept LONELINESS is mutually manifest in the same way, when in fact their phenomenological experiences of aloneness are radically different. Neither recognizes the extent of the mismatch.

**Example 2: The Dog (A1 across conversations)**
A1 mentions his dog across all three conversations. With unfamiliar autistic partner A2, the dog mention generates "laughter, emphatic agreement...shared parallel anecdotes" -- the dog is immediately recognized as carrying deep significance. With non-autistic stranger B1, the same dog mention produces "distinct lack of engagement." The RT analysis: for A1 (and, it turns out, A2), companion animals are deeply manifest as emotionally significant. B1 does not share this manifestness, and the topic fails to achieve relevance for B1.

**Example 3: Alienation and Consolation (A7 and X7)**
A7 tries to express profound alienation: "I feel like the weird one...I've lived here most of my life but I'm lacking people." X7 misinterprets this as a practical problem of circumstance ("it's chance") and offers consolation that actually dismisses A7's emotional content. Later, A7 discusses uncertainty about people's reliability; X7 pivots to technology reliability. The RT analysis: they are "talking at cross-purposes," with "information that A7 is working hard to convey" never becoming mutually manifest.

### 2.6 The Monotropism Connection

Williams et al. explicitly connect their findings to **monotropism** (Murray, Lesser, & Lawson 2005), the theory that autistic cognition is characterized by intensely focused attention allocation. In RT terms, monotropism affects what enters the cognitive environment: stimuli within the monotropic "attention tunnel" are processed with heightened intensity, while stimuli outside it may not register at all.

The researchers propose that when "two monotropic individuals synchronize their 'torch-beams' of intensified attention, something like a hyper-confluence of cognitive environments may occur, with increased affective reward." This explains the dramatically enhanced communication quality in autistic-autistic pairs: their attention systems are operating similarly, so their cognitive environments naturally align more closely, and mutual manifestness is achieved with less effort.

This has a flip side: when interacting with non-autistic individuals whose attention is more diffusely distributed, the monotropic individual's cognitive environment may diverge sharply from the polytropic individual's. Topics that are intensely manifest to one may barely register for the other.

### 2.7 Practical and Clinical Implications

The paper identifies three pathways to bridging the dispositional gap:

1. **Familiarity and accommodation:** Long-term partners can learn to provide additional processing time, minimize interruptions, and accommodate each other's communication styles -- though familiarity can also create false assumptions of mutual manifestness.

2. **Warm curiosity and questioning:** Some cross-dispositional pairs achieved rapport through frequent genuine questions about the other's experiences, rather than assuming they already understood.

3. **Emotional vulnerability:** Sharing personal information early creates "affective common ground" that can substitute for the cognitive alignment that comes naturally in matched-dispositional pairs.

The paper also raises concerns about assessment: if autistic individuals demonstrate optimal communicative competence specifically in autistic-autistic interactions, then traditional assessment by non-autistic clinicians may systematically underestimate actual abilities.

## 3. JAGOE & WHARTON (2021): Theory of Autistic Mind

### 3.1 The Paper

"Theory of autistic mind: A renewed relevance theoretic perspective on so-called autistic pragmatic 'impairment'" by Gemma Williams was published in the *Journal of Pragmatics* (180, 121-130) in July 2021 as a companion piece to the Williams, Wharton & Jagoe *Frontiers* paper.

### 3.2 The Central Argument: Clinicians Need a Theory of Autistic Mind

The paper's title contains a deliberate inversion. For decades, autism research focused on whether autistic people have a "Theory of Mind" -- the ability to attribute mental states to others. The question was always directed at the autistic person: "Can you understand what *we* are thinking?"

Williams (2021) inverts this: clinicians, researchers, and non-autistic interlocutors need a **"theory of autistic mind"** -- the ability to understand how autistic people think, feel, and experience the world. The problem is not (only) that autistic people struggle to read non-autistic minds; it is that non-autistic people equally struggle to read autistic minds. Research by Brewer et al. (2016), Edey et al. (2016), and Sheppard et al. (2016) has demonstrated that non-autistic people have significant difficulty inferring autistic people's mental and affective states.

### 3.3 Critique of Traditional RT Applications to Autism

Williams (2021) challenges the traditional way RT has been applied to autism, including Happé's foundational 1993 work:

**The deficit-first framing:** Previous applications of RT to autism (Happé 1993; Papp 2006) all started from the premise that autistic people have impaired ToM abilities, and used RT to explain the *consequences* of those impairments. This frames autism as inherently communicatively deficient and treats autistic language use as "a natural testing ground for pragmatic hypotheses" -- using autistic people to test theories, rather than developing theories that serve autistic people.

**The unidirectional assumption:** Traditional applications located all communicative responsibility in the autistic individual. If communication failed, it was because the autistic person could not recognize the speaker's intention. Williams (2021) argues this ignores the non-autistic interlocutor's equal responsibility for achieving mutual understanding.

**The homogeneity assumption:** Traditional applications treated "autistic communication" as a monolithic category defined by deficits. Williams (2021) argues for recognizing the enormous diversity of autistic communicative styles and strengths.

### 3.4 The Renewed RT Perspective

Rather than abandoning RT, Williams (2021) argues that RT's core concepts are actually *more* useful than Happé realized -- but need to be applied differently:

- **Mutual manifestness** should be understood as dispositionally variable. What is manifest depends on an individual's embodied experience, sensory profile, attention patterns, and cognitive style. Autistic and non-autistic people will have different things manifest, not because either is deficient, but because they have different perceptual and cognitive configurations.

- **Optimal relevance** should be understood as perspective-dependent. What counts as "relevant enough" and what constitutes "the most relevant stimulus compatible with the communicator's abilities and preferences" varies between neurotypes. An autistic speaker who provides highly detailed, technically precise information is not violating relevance -- they are being optimally relevant *according to their cognitive environment*.

- **The comprehension procedure** operates in both directions. When communication fails between autistic and non-autistic people, RT should analyze what went wrong in *both* directions -- what the autistic hearer failed to access in the non-autistic speaker's cognitive environment, AND what the non-autistic hearer failed to access in the autistic speaker's cognitive environment.

### 3.5 Clinical Implications

The paper has significant implications for speech and language therapy and clinical practice:

1. **Assessment must account for the interlocutor:** Measuring autistic communicative competence through interactions with non-autistic assessors may systematically underrepresent actual abilities. Clinicians should consider assessing communication in matched-dispositional contexts as well.

2. **Intervention should target the dyad, not the individual:** Rather than teaching autistic individuals to "read" non-autistic minds better, intervention should focus on building mutual understanding -- teaching both parties to recognize and accommodate each other's cognitive environments.

3. **Autistic communication is not deficient, it is different:** Clinicians need to learn to recognize valid autistic communicative strategies rather than treating them as impairments. This requires developing their own "theory of autistic mind."

## 4. RT and Ostensive Communication in Autism

### 4.1 What Ostensive Communication Requires

In RT, **ostensive-inferential communication** has two layers:
1. The **informative intention**: The speaker's intention to inform the hearer of something.
2. The **communicative intention**: The speaker's intention to make the hearer *recognize* the informative intention.

An **ostensive stimulus** is any behavior designed to attract the hearer's attention and focus it on the speaker's intended meaning. Examples include catching someone's eye, pointing, touching their arm, clearing one's throat before speaking, or simply beginning to speak in someone's direction. The ostensive stimulus says, in effect: "Pay attention -- I am about to communicate something relevant to you."

### 4.2 The Ostension Challenge in Autism

Ostensive communication requires:
1. **Producing** ostensive stimuli (signaling that one intends to communicate)
2. **Recognizing** others' ostensive stimuli (noticing that someone is trying to communicate)
3. **Interpreting** the ostensive stimulus as evidence that what follows will be relevant

Research on joint attention in autism is directly relevant here. Joint attention -- the shared focus of two individuals on an object or event -- is one of the earliest forms of ostensive communication. Studies consistently find differences in how autistic children initiate and respond to joint attention bids:
- Autistic children may use fewer eye-gaze based joint attention initiations (pointing while looking at the other person)
- They may be slower to follow gaze-based joint attention bids from others
- However, they DO follow points, and they DO engage in joint attention through modalities other than eye gaze

Research by Caruana et al. (2024, published in *Autism*) found that "both autistic and non-autistic participants intuitively and voluntarily used gaze information to facilitate coordination," but with interesting differences: autistic participants were initially slower to respond to communicative (ostensive) gaze shifts, but equally responsive to instrumental gaze cues. This suggests that the ostension recognition system may function differently in autism -- not absent, but calibrated differently.

### 4.3 Sperber's Three Comprehension Strategies and Autism

Sperber (1994, "Understanding verbal understanding") proposed that the RT comprehension procedure comes in three versions corresponding to different developmental levels:

1. **Naive optimism:** The hearer assumes the speaker is both competent (able to communicate clearly) and benevolent (intending to communicate truthfully). They accept the first interpretation that satisfies relevance expectations. This is the default strategy.

2. **Cautious optimism:** The hearer assumes the speaker is benevolent but not necessarily competent. They accept the first interpretation that the speaker *might have intended* to be optimally relevant, allowing for the possibility of speaker error.

3. **Sophisticated understanding:** The hearer recognizes that the speaker may be neither competent nor benevolent -- they may be lying, manipulating, or being deliberately misleading. The hearer searches for what the speaker *intended* them to think, which may differ from what is actually true.

These three strategies are highly relevant to autism:

**Autistic individuals may default more strongly to naive optimism.** There is evidence that autistic people tend to take utterances at face value more readily, to assume speakers mean what they say, and to be more trusting of communicated information. In RT terms, this is not a "deficit" -- it is a consistent application of the naive optimistic strategy. The problem arises only when speakers are *not* being straightforwardly honest and competent.

**Detecting lies and sarcasm requires sophisticated understanding.** Research consistently shows that autistic individuals have more difficulty detecting lies and sarcasm -- precisely the communicative acts that require moving beyond naive optimism to sophisticated understanding. This is not necessarily because they cannot attribute mental states, but because the threshold for triggering suspicion of non-benevolence may be higher.

**This maps onto the autistic experience of being easily deceived or manipulated.** Many autistic adults report being taken advantage of by people who exploit their tendency to take statements at face value. In RT terms, this reflects persistent use of the naive optimistic strategy in situations where cautious optimism or sophisticated understanding would be more appropriate.

### 4.4 Whether Autistic Individuals Process Ostension Differently

Recent research suggests that rather than being "blind" to ostension, autistic individuals may process communicative signals through different channels:

- **Less reliance on eye gaze, more on explicit verbal signals:** Non-autistic individuals treat eyes as inherently socially salient. Research suggests autistic individuals may treat eye gaze more instrumentally -- paying attention to it when it serves a clear purpose, rather than automatically monitoring it for ostensive cues. This means they may rely more on **explicit verbal markers** of communicative intent ("I need to tell you something," "Listen to this") rather than subtle nonverbal ostensive cues (eye contact, raised eyebrows, shifts in body orientation).

- **Less reliance on prosodic cues, more on linguistic content:** Ostensive communication often relies on prosodic signals (changes in pitch, rhythm, emphasis) to indicate "this is important" or "I'm being ironic." Autistic individuals may process prosodic information differently, potentially relying more on the linguistic content itself for interpretation.

- **This has implications for what counts as a "clear" ostensive signal.** If autistic individuals rely on different channels for ostension recognition, then non-autistic speakers who rely primarily on nonverbal and prosodic ostensive cues are effectively failing to communicate *their intention to communicate*. The ostensive failure is on the speaker's side as much as the hearer's.

## 5. RT and Cognitive Theories of Autism

### 5.1 Weak Central Coherence

**The theory:** Frith (1989) proposed, and Happé and Frith (2006) updated, the Weak Central Coherence (WCC) theory, suggesting that autistic individuals have a cognitive style characterized by preference for local (detail-focused) processing over global (gestalt, big-picture) processing. Later formulations reframed this as "detail-focused cognitive style" rather than a deficit in global processing -- autistic individuals CAN process globally when instructed to, but they default to local processing when no instructions are given (a review of over 50 studies by Happé & Frith 2006 found robust local bias with mixed findings on global processing deficits).

**Connection to RT:** In RT terms, WCC affects how an individual constructs context for interpreting utterances. The comprehension procedure requires accessing contextual assumptions -- information from memory, perception, and inference -- and combining them with the decoded linguistic meaning to derive cognitive effects. If an individual's default processing style leads them to focus on local, specific details rather than broad contextual patterns, this will systematically affect:

- **Which contextual assumptions are most accessible:** Detail-focused processors may access specific, precise information more readily than broad social or cultural assumptions. For example, when someone says "It's getting late," a detail-focused processor might access the specific time of day rather than the broader contextual implication "I want to leave."

- **Which interpretive hypotheses are tested first:** The RT procedure says to test hypotheses "in order of accessibility." If detail-focused processing makes literal, specific interpretations more accessible than broad, contextual ones, the procedure will test literal interpretations first. Often, these will satisfy relevance expectations -- "It's getting late" IS informative about the time -- and the procedure will stop before reaching the intended non-literal interpretation.

- **This is not a failure of the procedure; it is the procedure working as designed, with different input.** The same algorithm, applied to a different cognitive environment with different accessibility rankings, produces different output. This is exactly what Williams et al. (2021) argue.

### 5.2 Monotropism

**The theory:** Murray, Lesser, and Lawson (2005) proposed that autistic cognition is characterized by **monotropism** -- a tendency to allocate attention intensely to a small number of interests at a time, rather than distributing it broadly. This creates an "attention tunnel" where stimuli within the focus receive intensely heightened processing, while stimuli outside the tunnel may not be processed at all. This differs from the polytropic (broadly distributed) attention typical of non-autistic cognition.

**Connection to RT:** Monotropism has profound implications for RT's central concepts:

- **Cognitive environment construction:** What is "manifest" to an individual depends on what enters their attention. A monotropic individual's cognitive environment will be *intensely detailed* in some domains and *sparse* in others. If a conversation topic falls within their attention tunnel, they may have an extraordinarily rich cognitive environment -- far more detailed than a polytropic interlocutor's. If the topic falls outside, they may have very little to work with.

- **What counts as "relevant":** Relevance is defined as the ratio of cognitive effects to processing effort. For a monotropic individual, stimuli related to their current interest zone will generate massive cognitive effects (because they connect to a dense network of highly activated assumptions) while requiring minimal effort. Stimuli outside this zone may generate few effects and require high effort to process. This means that relevance is not just individually variable -- it is *dramatically* different between monotropic and polytropic minds, even when both are processing the same utterance.

- **The "hyper-confluence" phenomenon:** Williams et al. (2021) observed that when two monotropic individuals synchronize their attention tunnels, the resulting communication is exceptionally fluid and rich. This makes sense in RT terms: when both interlocutors have the same topic in their attention tunnel, their cognitive environments are densely populated with the same assumptions, mutual manifestness is high, and communication requires minimal inferential effort.

- **Processing effort in conversation:** Monotropism also explains why conversations themselves are effortful for autistic individuals. A conversation requires simultaneously processing: (a) the other person's speech, (b) one's own speech production, (c) nonverbal cues, (d) the physical environment, and (e) the social context. For a monotropic system, which processes best when focused on one thing, this multi-channel demand is inherently more effortful than for a polytropic system.

### 5.3 Predictive Processing

**The theory:** Pellicano and Burr (2012) proposed that autistic perception is characterized by **reduced priors** -- weaker top-down predictions about incoming sensory information, resulting in greater reliance on bottom-up sensory data. Van de Cruys et al. (2014) offered a related account where the issue is not weaker priors but an atypical updating process -- autistic individuals may weight sensory evidence more heavily and update priors more cautiously. The result is a perceptual system that is more "data-driven" and less "expectation-driven."

Vicente, Michel, and Petrolini (2023, "Literalism in Autistic People: a Predictive Processing Proposal," *Review of Philosophy and Psychology*) directly connected predictive processing to autistic literalism. They argued that if autistic individuals have lower confidence in their own predictions, they will default to the "safest" interpretation -- the literal one. Non-literal interpretations require the hearer to predict that the speaker means something other than what they literally said. If the hearer's predictive system is less confident, it will be less willing to deviate from the literal meaning.

**Connection to RT:** Predictive processing connects to RT in several ways:

- **Relevance assessment is predictive.** The RT comprehension procedure involves *predicting* what interpretation will achieve relevance. You test the first hypothesis that is accessible, check whether it meets your expectations of relevance, and stop if it does. Reduced confidence in predictions could lead to:
  - **More literal interpretations** (the literal meaning is the "safest" prediction)
  - **More effortful processing** (each interpretive step involves less confident predictions, requiring more evidence)
  - **Different relevance thresholds** (less confident predictors may accept weaker cognitive effects because they are less certain that stronger effects are available)

- **Context construction is predictive.** What assumptions you bring to bear on an utterance depends partly on your predictions about what is relevant. If predictive processing is different, the entire context that feeds the comprehension procedure is constructed differently.

- **Falkum and Vicente (2023, "Accounting for the preference for literal meanings in autism spectrum conditions," *Mind & Language*)** proposed that literalism may be linked to rule-following behavior broadly, noting that neurotypical children also go through a literalist stage that parallels rule-following developmental patterns.

### 5.4 Enhanced Perceptual Functioning

**The theory:** Mottron et al. (2006, "Enhanced Perceptual Functioning in Autism: An Update, and Eight Principles of Autistic Perception," *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*) proposed that autistic perception is characterized by enhanced low-level perceptual processing, greater independence of perceptual processes from higher-order cognition, and more veridical (faithful-to-reality) memory for perceptual details.

**Connection to RT:** Enhanced perceptual functioning means that the sensory input to the comprehension process is richer and more detailed for autistic individuals. This has several implications:

- **More perceptual information competes for attention during communication.** While processing an utterance, an autistic individual may be simultaneously processing rich sensory detail from the environment that a non-autistic individual would filter out. This increases the total processing load and may affect how much processing resource is available for pragmatic inference.

- **Literal language may carry more information.** If perceptual experience is richer and more detailed, then literal descriptions of the world may be more relevance-generating for autistic individuals. When someone says "The sunset is beautiful," a person with enhanced perceptual functioning may derive rich cognitive effects from the literal statement (connecting to detailed perceptual memories) that a non-autistic person would need figurative language to achieve.

- **Non-literal language may offer less "bang for the buck."** If literal language is already generating rich cognitive effects, the additional effects achieved by non-literal interpretation may not justify the additional processing effort. The comprehension procedure, optimizing the effort-effect ratio, may stop at the literal interpretation more often.

### 5.5 Does RT Provide a Unifying Framework?

The various cognitive theories of autism (WCC, monotropism, predictive processing, enhanced perceptual functioning) are often presented as competing explanations. But RT may offer a framework that *unifies* them by showing how they all affect the same underlying process: the construction and use of the cognitive environment for relevance-based interpretation.

All four theories describe different aspects of how the autistic cognitive system constructs, maintains, and accesses the information that feeds into the comprehension procedure:
- **WCC** affects what level of granularity contextual assumptions are represented at (local vs. global)
- **Monotropism** affects which domains of knowledge are highly activated vs. dormant at any given time
- **Predictive processing** affects how confidently the system generates and tests interpretive hypotheses
- **Enhanced perceptual functioning** affects the richness of the sensory input that competes with linguistic processing

RT does not need to adjudicate between these theories. It simply notes that all of them systematically affect the cognitive environment and thereby systematically affect the output of the comprehension procedure. The result is a communication system that works differently -- not deficiently -- and that produces optimal output *given its input*, even when that output differs from what a non-autistic system would produce.

## 6. Why RT May Be Especially Useful for Autistic Learners

### 6.1 The Single-Principle Advantage

Grice's framework asks learners to internalize four separate maxims (Quality, Quantity, Relation, Manner) plus the overarching Cooperative Principle, and then to detect when one is "flouted" or "violated." This is a complex social-interpretive task. RT reduces everything to a **single principle**: utterances are expected to be optimally relevant. One principle is easier to learn, remember, and apply than four-plus-one.

### 6.2 The Algorithmic/Procedural Nature

The RT comprehension procedure is explicitly algorithmic:
1. Follow the path of least effort.
2. Test interpretive hypotheses in order of accessibility.
3. Stop when your expectations of relevance are satisfied.

This is a **procedure** -- a step-by-step process that can be consciously followed. It is not a set of social norms that must be intuitively sensed. For individuals with a **systemizing** cognitive style (Baron-Cohen's empathizing-systemizing theory), an explicit procedure is far more tractable than an implicit social understanding. Many autistic individuals already develop explicit if-then rules for social situations; RT provides a *principled* version of this strategy.

### 6.3 The Cognitive (Not Social) Foundation

Grice's framework is fundamentally social: it assumes speakers cooperate to achieve mutual purposes, and it requires tracking cooperation and its violations. RT is fundamentally **cognitive**: it describes how the human information-processing system works. The Cognitive Principle of Relevance is a claim about how minds process information, not about how people cooperate socially. This means that an autistic learner does not need to first understand social cooperation to use RT -- they need to understand how their own mind processes information, which may be a more accessible starting point.

### 6.4 No Need to Detect "Violations"

In Grice's framework, non-literal language is explained through **maxim violation**: the speaker appears to break a rule, and the hearer infers why. This requires:
1. Knowing the rule
2. Detecting that it has been broken
3. Generating an explanation for why it was broken
4. Deriving the implicature from that explanation

This is cognitively demanding and requires social knowledge at every step. RT treats non-literal language as arising through the **same procedure** as literal language -- no violation, no special mechanism. The hearer simply follows the comprehension procedure, and the first interpretation that achieves relevance may be literal or non-literal. No "violation detection" is needed.

### 6.5 The "First Satisfactory Interpretation" Rule as a Practical Heuristic

The RT comprehension procedure's core instruction -- "stop when your expectations of relevance are satisfied" -- is a practical, action-oriented heuristic. It tells you:
- **What to do** (test interpretive hypotheses in order of accessibility)
- **When to stop** (when you find one that is relevant enough)
- **What "relevant enough" means** (it produces sufficient cognitive effects to be worth the processing effort)

This is a heuristic that can be consciously applied. It does not require reading the speaker's mind; it requires monitoring your own cognitive experience: "Does this interpretation make sense to me? Does it tell me something useful? Is it worth the effort I put into understanding it?"

### 6.6 Compatibility with Systemizing Cognitive Style

Baron-Cohen's empathizing-systemizing theory proposes that autistic cognition is characterized by a strong drive to analyze and construct systems with lawful, predictable input-operation-output patterns. RT's comprehension procedure is precisely such a system:
- **Input:** An utterance + contextual assumptions
- **Operation:** Test interpretive hypotheses in order of accessibility
- **Output:** The first interpretation that satisfies relevance expectations

This if-then-else structure maps naturally onto a systemizing cognitive style. An autistic learner who has internalized this procedure has a *system* for processing communication, rather than needing to rely on intuitive social understanding.

### 6.7 RT's Treatment of Literal vs. Non-Literal as a Continuum

In many traditional frameworks, there is a binary division: language is either "literal" or "figurative," and different mechanisms apply to each. RT eliminates this binary. All utterances are processed through the same procedure. The difference between literal and non-literal interpretation is a matter of how far the hearer needs to "adjust" the encoded concept to derive the speaker's intended meaning:

- **Literal use:** The encoded concept closely matches the intended concept. "Open the door" means OPEN THE DOOR.
- **Narrowing:** The encoded concept is adjusted to be more specific. "I need a drink" means I NEED AN ALCOHOLIC DRINK (the concept DRINK is narrowed).
- **Loosening/approximation:** The concept is adjusted to be broader. "The table is flat" means THE TABLE IS APPROXIMATELY FLAT.
- **Metaphorical extension:** The concept is adjusted substantially. "She's a bulldozer" means SHE SHARES CERTAIN PROPERTIES WITH A BULLDOZER (forceful, relentless, etc.).

These are not different "types" of language requiring different mechanisms. They are different *degrees* of the same pragmatic adjustment process. This continuum-based view may be more useful for autistic learners than a binary literal/figurative distinction, because it means they do not need to first categorize an utterance as "figurative" before they can interpret it -- they simply apply the same procedure and see where it leads.

## 7. Practical Applications: Using RT Consciously

### 7.1 The Conscious Comprehension Procedure

For an autistic person learning to navigate non-autistic pragmatics, the RT comprehension procedure can be adapted as a conscious decision-making tool:

**Step 1: Identify the utterance and context.**
What did the person say? What is the situation? What were we just talking about? What do I know about this person?

**Step 2: Consider the literal interpretation first.**
Does the literal meaning make sense in this context? Does it tell me something relevant -- something that changes my understanding of the situation or has practical implications for me?

**Step 3: If the literal interpretation achieves relevance, accept it and move on.**
Most of the time, people mean roughly what they say. The literal interpretation is often adequate. RT tells you this is perfectly fine -- you are not required to search for deeper meanings unless the literal one fails.

**Step 4: If the literal interpretation does NOT achieve relevance, search for an alternative.**
Signs that the literal interpretation may not be what the speaker intended:
- The literal meaning is obviously false ("What lovely weather!" during a storm)
- The literal meaning seems unrelated to the conversation topic
- The literal meaning seems insufficiently informative for the effort the speaker invested
- The speaker's tone, facial expression, or body language seems at odds with the literal words

**Step 5: Test alternative interpretations in order of accessibility.**
What related meaning could the speaker have intended? Consider:
- Is the speaker making a comparison (metaphor)?
- Is the speaker exaggerating or understating (hyperbole/litotes)?
- Is the speaker expressing the opposite of what they mean (irony)?
- Is the speaker making a request indirectly?
- Is the speaker making a social-bonding utterance (small talk)?

**Step 6: Accept the first alternative that achieves relevance.**
When you find an interpretation that makes sense in context, produces useful cognitive effects, and seems compatible with the speaker's apparent attitude, accept it and respond accordingly.

### 7.2 Worked Examples

#### Example 1: "Nice of you to join us"
**Context:** You arrive 15 minutes late to a meeting. A colleague says, "Nice of you to join us."

**Step 2 (Literal test):** Is it literally "nice"? Well, arriving isn't particularly nice or generous -- you were supposed to be there. The literal interpretation is peculiar because "nice" implies an optional kindness, but attendance was expected.

**Step 3:** The literal interpretation doesn't achieve relevance -- it doesn't tell you anything useful.

**Step 4:** The literal interpretation seems at odds with the context (you're late, which is typically a social violation) and possibly the speaker's tone (which may be dry or pointed).

**Step 5:** The speaker could be:
- Commenting ironically on your lateness (echoing the idea that your arrival is a pleasant surprise, when actually your lateness is a problem)
- Making a mild rebuke wrapped in sarcastic politeness

**Step 6:** The ironic/sarcastic interpretation achieves relevance: it communicates that the speaker noticed your lateness and is annoyed about it. Accept this interpretation and respond accordingly (apologize for being late).

#### Example 2: "It's getting cold in here"
**Context:** You're in a room with a friend. They say, "It's getting cold in here."

**Step 2 (Literal test):** Is the room cold? If yes, this is informative. But wait -- is the *literal fact* all they're communicating? What cognitive effects does "it's cold" produce for you?

**Step 3:** The literal interpretation IS relevant (it's a true statement about the environment). But it may not be *optimally* relevant. Why is the speaker investing the effort to tell you something you can also perceive? (This is the key RT insight -- speakers are expected to be *optimally* relevant, not just somewhat relevant.)

**Step 5:** The speaker might be:
- Making small talk (acknowledging a shared experience)
- Making an indirect request (please close the window / turn up the heat / pass a blanket)
- Explaining why they're about to do something (put on a jacket, close a window)

**Step 6:** The indirect request interpretation achieves greater relevance (it has practical implications for your behavior) and is compatible with the context. Consider responding with an offer: "Want me to close the window?"

**Note:** If you're wrong and they were just making small talk, no harm is done -- offering to close the window is a helpful response either way. This is one of the advantages of the RT approach: it often leads to reasonable responses even when the specific interpretation is slightly off.

#### Example 3: "How's your steak?"
**Context:** You're at a restaurant with someone. Your steak is overcooked and dry. They ask, "How's your steak?"

**Step 2 (Literal test):** This is a question requesting information about the quality of your steak. The literal interpretation achieves relevance -- they're asking and you can answer.

**Step 3:** The literal interpretation IS fully relevant here. This is a straightforward question. **Answer it directly.**

**The key insight:** Not everything requires searching for hidden meanings. RT's procedure correctly predicts that many utterances are meant literally and should be taken at face value. The procedure saves you effort by stopping at the literal interpretation when it works.

### 7.3 When the Procedure Works Well

The RT comprehension procedure works best when:
- **The speaker is being relatively direct.** The closer the intended meaning is to the literal meaning, the more reliably the procedure identifies it.
- **You have sufficient context.** The more you know about the situation, the conversation history, and the speaker's likely goals, the more accurately you can assess which interpretation achieves relevance.
- **The speaker's cognitive environment partially overlaps with yours.** When you share some background knowledge and assumptions with the speaker, the contextual assumptions needed for interpretation are more likely to be available to you.
- **The communication channel is rich.** Face-to-face conversation provides tone, facial expression, and gesture as additional evidence for interpretation. Written communication removes these cues, making the procedure harder to apply.

### 7.4 When the Procedure Struggles

The RT comprehension procedure may lead to misunderstanding when:

- **The literal interpretation achieves *some* relevance but not the intended one.** If "It's getting cold" is literally true and somewhat informative, the procedure may stop there without reaching the intended indirect request. This is the most common RT-analyzable source of misunderstanding for autistic individuals: the literal interpretation satisfies relevance expectations, preventing the search for an intended non-literal meaning.

- **The speaker's cognitive environment is very different from yours.** If you lack the cultural knowledge, relationship history, or social assumptions that the speaker takes for granted, the intended interpretation may simply not be accessible to you. No amount of procedural application can compensate for genuinely missing contextual information.

- **The speaker relies on implicit ostensive cues.** If the speaker signals their communicative intent primarily through subtle changes in eye gaze, body posture, or prosody, and you do not process these cues in the same way, you may miss that they are trying to communicate something beyond the literal content.

- **Multiple interpretations satisfy relevance.** Sometimes two or more interpretations each seem adequate. The procedure says to accept the first one that is relevant enough, but if the *wrong* one happens to be more accessible, you'll accept the wrong one. This is especially likely with ambiguous statements or dry humor.

### 7.5 Handling Misunderstandings: The RT Repair Strategy

When you realize that communication has broken down (the other person reacts unexpectedly to what you said, or their response doesn't make sense), RT provides a framework for repair:

1. **Assume the misunderstanding is mutual.** Neither of you is "wrong" -- your cognitive environments diverged, and mutual manifestness was not achieved.

2. **Make the implicit explicit.** State what you understood them to mean, and ask if that is correct: "When you said X, did you mean Y?" This gives the other person the chance to confirm or correct your interpretation.

3. **Provide more context.** If they misunderstood you, provide additional contextual assumptions that make your intended meaning more accessible: "What I meant was..."

4. **Recognize that repair itself is a normal part of communication.** RT predicts that misunderstandings will occur whenever cognitive environments diverge. Repair is not a sign of failure; it is a sign that both parties are working to achieve mutual manifestness.

## 8. Research Connections and Cross-References

### 8.1 Supporting Evidence from Crompton et al. (2020)

Crompton et al. (2020, "Autistic peer-to-peer information transfer is highly effective," *Autism*, 24(7), 1704-1712) provided independent empirical support for the Williams et al. findings. Using a "telephone" task (information passed along a chain of people), they found that information degraded significantly when passed between autistic and non-autistic individuals, but was preserved effectively when passed between people of the same neurotype. A parallel study (Crompton et al. 2020, "Neurotype-Matching, but Not Being Autistic, Influences Self and Observer Ratings of Interpersonal Rapport," *Frontiers in Psychology*, 11: 586171) found that rapport ratings were significantly higher in neurotype-matched pairs. These findings are consistent with RT's prediction that matched cognitive environments facilitate mutual manifestness.

### 8.2 Cross-Neurotype Communication as Intercultural (Żuromski & Pacholik-Żuromska 2026)

Two recent contributions support and extend the double-empathy / RT framework — one empirical, one theoretical:

- **Empirical support.** Marocchini & Baldin (2024, "Cross-neurotype communication from an autistic point of view," *International Journal of Language & Communication Disorders*) conducted focus groups with 23 autistic adults and found that participants described autistic-autistic communication as markedly easier, confirming the mutual manifestness account.

- **Framework extension.** Żuromski & Pacholik-Żuromska (2026, "Intercultural Cognitive Pragmatics as a tool for understanding autism," *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience*) proposed applying the framework of **Intercultural Cognitive Pragmatics** to cross-neurotype communication. This treats autistic-non-autistic communication as analogous to cross-cultural communication -- a mismatch in pragmatic systems, not a deficit in one system. This is fully consistent with the RT account: just as speakers from different cultures may have different cognitive environments leading to different relevance expectations, speakers from different neurotypes may have similarly different cognitive environments.

### 8.3 Compensatory Strategies and Masking

Research on autistic **camouflaging** and **compensatory strategies** (Hull et al. 2017; Livingston et al. 2019, "Compensatory strategies below the behavioural surface in autism," *The Lancet Psychiatry*, 6(9), 766-777) is directly relevant to the RT framework. Many autistic adults develop explicit compensatory strategies for pragmatic processing -- consciously learned rules for interpreting and producing social communication. In RT terms, these strategies represent *conscious, effortful application of the comprehension procedure* -- doing explicitly and deliberately what non-autistic individuals do automatically and unconsciously.

The cost of this compensatory processing is significant. Research consistently links masking and compensation to burnout, anxiety, depression, and suicidal ideation. In RT terms, the cost comes from the additional processing effort: the autistic individual is doing the same inferential work as a non-autistic individual, but with less automated support, requiring more conscious cognitive resources. This is analogous to doing long division by hand while others use a calculator -- the same operation, but enormously more effortful.

RT thus provides a framework for understanding *why* masking is so exhausting: it involves sustained conscious deployment of inferential resources that are, for non-autistic individuals, automatic and effortless.

## 10. Summary of Key Themes for the Knowledge Base

### For the autistic learner, the essential takeaways are:

1. **Relevance Theory offers a single, learnable algorithm for interpreting communication.** Follow the path of least effort, test interpretations in order of accessibility, and stop when relevance is satisfied. This is a system, not an intuition.

2. **Communication difficulty between autistic and non-autistic people is MUTUAL, not one-sided.** RT explains why: both parties bring different cognitive environments, and misunderstandings arise from failures of mutual manifestness on both sides.

3. **Literal interpretation is not "wrong" in RT -- it is often the correct output of the procedure.** The comprehension procedure correctly produces literal interpretations when literal meaning achieves relevance. The issue arises only when the speaker intended something beyond the literal, and the literal interpretation happened to satisfy the hearer's relevance expectations first.

4. **Processing non-literal language through RT does not require a "figurative language detector."** There is no switch to flip from "literal mode" to "figurative mode." The same procedure handles both. Non-literal interpretation emerges naturally when the literal interpretation fails to achieve relevance.

5. **When communication breaks down, the RT framework provides a repair strategy:** make the implicit explicit, provide additional context, and recognize that repair is normal rather than a sign of failure.

6. **Masking and compensatory strategies are the conscious application of the same inferential process that non-autistic people perform automatically.** RT explains both why these strategies work (they approximate the comprehension procedure) and why they are exhausting (they require sustained conscious effort for what is typically an automated process).

7. **Your cognitive environment is different, not deficient.** What is manifest to you -- what you notice, what you find relevant, what contextual assumptions you access -- is determined by your perceptual, attentional, and cognitive profile. RT treats this as input to the procedure, not as error in the procedure.
