# Concept Research: Physiological Differences Between Excitement and Anxiety

## Overview

Excitement and anxiety are both high-arousal emotional states that activate the sympathetic nervous system (SNS), producing many overlapping physical sensations—increased heart rate, heightened alertness, and elevated adrenaline. This overlap creates a fundamental challenge for emotion identification, particularly for autistic individuals with alexithymia who may have difficulty interpreting interoceptive signals (internal body cues).

Despite their physiological similarities, excitement and anxiety differ in measurable ways: neurochemical profiles (dopamine-dominant vs. cortisol-dominant), autonomic nervous system patterning (vagal tone preservation vs. withdrawal), cognitive appraisal direction (approach vs. avoidance), and specific somatic markers (where and how sensations manifest in the body). Understanding these differences at a physiological level provides concrete, body-based anchors for distinguishing between the two states.

Research from Nummenmaa et al. (2014) demonstrated that different emotions produce statistically separable body sensation maps across cultures, suggesting emotions have distinctive physical signatures. The challenge for individuals with alexithymia is that interoceptive processing differences make detecting and interpreting these signatures more difficult (Murphy et al., 2018; Shah et al., 2016).

## Core Components

### Component 1: Sympathetic Nervous System (SNS) Activation
- **Purpose**: Mobilizes body for action in response to perceived arousal (both positive and negative)
- **Inputs**: Sensory information processed through amygdala and prefrontal cortex; cognitive appraisals
- **Outputs**: Release of epinephrine and norepinephrine; increased heart rate, blood pressure, respiration
- **Relationships**: Activated in both excitement and anxiety; the qualitative pattern of activation differs based on concurrent vagal activity and neurochemical context

### Component 2: Parasympathetic Nervous System (PNS) / Vagal Regulation
- **Purpose**: Regulates arousal through the vagus nerve; maintains homeostasis and social engagement
- **Inputs**: Safety cues, social engagement signals, breathing patterns
- **Outputs**: Heart rate variability modulation, digestive function, facial muscle tone
- **Relationships**: Key differentiator—excitement tends to maintain vagal tone (ventral vagal engagement) while anxiety involves vagal withdrawal; described in Porges' Polyvagal Theory

### Component 3: HPA Axis (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal)
- **Purpose**: Produces cortisol in response to perceived threat or sustained stress
- **Inputs**: Threat appraisals from amygdala; anticipatory anxiety signals from limbic structures
- **Outputs**: Cortisol release; sustained stress response; metabolic changes
- **Relationships**: Anxiety activates the HPA axis strongly, producing elevated cortisol. Positive excitement produces minimal or no cortisol elevation. This is a key biochemical differentiator.

### Component 4: Dopaminergic Reward System
- **Purpose**: Generates motivation, anticipatory pleasure, and approach behavior
- **Inputs**: Anticipated reward; novelty; positive predictions from ventral tegmental area (VTA)
- **Outputs**: Dopamine release in nucleus accumbens and prefrontal cortex; feelings of motivation and energy
- **Relationships**: Dominant in excitement; produces approach motivation. Relatively suppressed during anxiety, which is dominated by avoidance motivation.

### Component 5: Interoceptive Processing System
- **Purpose**: Detects, interprets, and integrates internal body signals into conscious awareness
- **Inputs**: Signals from viscera, cardiovascular system, musculoskeletal system, gut
- **Outputs**: Conscious awareness of body state; emotional labeling; behavioral regulation
- **Relationships**: The insular cortex is the primary brain region for interoceptive processing. Differences in interoception—particularly common in autism and alexithymia—affect the ability to distinguish between excitement and anxiety.

### Component 6: Cognitive Appraisal System
- **Purpose**: Evaluates the meaning and significance of arousal signals
- **Inputs**: Context, memory, expectations, learned associations
- **Outputs**: Emotional labeling; behavioral direction (approach vs. avoidance)
- **Relationships**: Brooks (2014) demonstrated that the same physiological arousal can be experienced as excitement or anxiety depending on cognitive appraisal. This is the basis for "anxiety reappraisal" research.

## How It Works

### Step 1: Trigger Detection
Both excitement and anxiety begin when the brain detects a significant stimulus. The amygdala performs rapid threat/reward assessment.

- **Trigger**: Environmental stimulus (upcoming event, social situation, new opportunity)
- **Action**: Amygdala activates sympathetic nervous system; initial arousal response begins
- **Result**: Heart rate increases, breathing quickens, alertness heightens—this initial response is nearly identical for both states

### Step 2: Neurochemical Divergence
Within seconds, the neurochemical profiles begin to diverge based on the brain's appraisal.

- **Trigger**: Prefrontal cortex and limbic system evaluate context (safe/rewarding vs. threatening)
- **Action (Excitement)**: VTA releases dopamine into reward circuits; norepinephrine released but without significant cortisol; ventral vagal tone maintained
- **Action (Anxiety)**: Amygdala signals HPA axis activation; cortisol production begins; vagal tone withdraws; norepinephrine shifts toward vigilance rather than approach
- **Result**: Two distinct biochemical cascades emerge from the same initial arousal

### Step 3: Autonomic Pattern Differentiation
The autonomic nervous system response diverges into distinct patterns.

- **Trigger**: Neurochemical context modulates ANS response
- **Action (Excitement)**: Sympathetic activation WITH maintained parasympathetic tone (co-activation); higher heart rate variability; respiratory sinus arrhythmia preserved; skin conductance increases moderately
- **Action (Anxiety)**: Sympathetic dominance WITH parasympathetic withdrawal; lower heart rate variability; respiratory sinus arrhythmia reduced; skin conductance increases sharply
- **Result**: Measurably different autonomic signatures, though overlapping in surface-level sensations

### Step 4: Somatic Manifestation
Physical sensations emerge in characteristic body regions.

- **Trigger**: Autonomic and neurochemical responses produce peripheral body changes
- **Action (Excitement)**: Warmth/energy in chest and limbs; "butterflies" may include sense of opening/expansion; muscles prepare for approach; breathing may quicken but remains relatively deep
- **Action (Anxiety)**: Tension concentrates in chest, throat, shoulders, stomach; "butterflies" feel more like churning/constriction; muscles tighten defensively; breathing becomes shallow and rapid; digestive disruption
- **Result**: Different topographical patterns of sensation, as documented by Nummenmaa et al. (2014)

### Step 5: Interoceptive Processing
The brain attempts to interpret and label the body sensations.

- **Trigger**: Interoceptive signals arrive at insular cortex
- **Action (Typical)**: Insula integrates body signals with context to produce an emotional label
- **Action (Alexithymia)**: Reduced interoceptive accuracy makes differentiation difficult; may perceive only "high arousal" without being able to distinguish valence; confusion between body states is common
- **Result**: For neurotypical individuals, relatively automatic labeling occurs. For individuals with alexithymia, the ambiguous arousal may default to anxiety interpretation.

### Step 6: Behavioral Direction
The identified emotion drives behavioral response.

- **Trigger**: Emotional label (accurate or inaccurate) combines with context
- **Action (Excitement)**: Approach behavior; engagement; exploration; social connection
- **Action (Anxiety)**: Avoidance behavior; withdrawal; vigilance; protective responses
- **Result**: Misidentification of excitement as anxiety can lead to unnecessary avoidance of positive experiences

## Key Relationships

| From | To | Relationship Type | Description |
|------|-----|------------------|-------------|
| SNS Activation | Both States | Shared trigger | Both excitement and anxiety activate the sympathetic nervous system |
| Vagal Tone | Emotional Valence | Differentiator | Maintained vagal tone = more likely excitement; withdrawn vagal tone = more likely anxiety |
| HPA Axis | Cortisol | Causal | Anxiety activates HPA axis producing cortisol; excitement does not significantly activate it |
| Dopamine System | Approach Motivation | Causal | Excitement involves strong dopaminergic activity; anxiety suppresses it |
| Interoception | Emotion Identification | Enables | Accurate interoception enables distinguishing between excitement and anxiety |
| Alexithymia | Interoception | Impairs | Alexithymia represents a general failure of interoception affecting emotion identification |
| Cognitive Appraisal | Emotion Label | Determines | Same arousal can be labeled excitement or anxiety depending on appraisal (Brooks, 2014) |
| Autism | Interoception | Modifies | Autistic individuals show altered interoceptive processing patterns |
| Insular Cortex | Body State Awareness | Processes | Primary brain region integrating interoceptive signals |
| Polyvagal System | Social Engagement | Regulates | Ventral vagal state supports positive social arousal; dorsal vagal/sympathetic states support defensive responses |

## Terminology Glossary

| Term | Definition | Also Known As |
|------|------------|---------------|
| Interoception | The sense of the internal state of the body; the ability to perceive signals from inside the body such as heartbeat, hunger, temperature, and pain | Body awareness, internal sensing |
| Alexithymia | A subclinical condition characterized by difficulty identifying, describing, and distinguishing one's own emotions; associated with general interoceptive deficits | Emotional blindness (informal) |
| Sympathetic Nervous System (SNS) | Branch of the autonomic nervous system that mobilizes the body for action; produces the "fight-or-flight" response | Fight-or-flight system |
| Parasympathetic Nervous System (PNS) | Branch of the autonomic nervous system that promotes rest, digestion, and recovery; calms the body after arousal | Rest-and-digest system |
| Vagal Tone | A measure of the activity of the vagus nerve, reflecting parasympathetic influence on the heart; higher vagal tone is associated with better emotional regulation and flexibility | Vagal activity |
| Heart Rate Variability (HRV) | The variation in time intervals between heartbeats; higher HRV indicates greater autonomic flexibility and is associated with positive emotional states | Beat-to-beat variability |
| Respiratory Sinus Arrhythmia (RSA) | The natural variation in heart rate that occurs with breathing (heart rate increases during inhalation, decreases during exhalation); a marker of parasympathetic function | Vagal-mediated HRV |
| HPA Axis | The hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis; a neuroendocrine system that produces cortisol in response to stress and threat | Stress axis |
| Cortisol | A glucocorticoid hormone released by the adrenal glands during stress; elevated in anxiety but not in positive excitement | Stress hormone |
| Dopamine | A neurotransmitter associated with reward, motivation, and anticipatory pleasure; elevated during excitement | Reward chemical (informal) |
| Polyvagal Theory | Stephen Porges' theory proposing that the autonomic nervous system has three hierarchical states (ventral vagal, sympathetic, dorsal vagal) that underlie different behavioral and emotional responses | — |
| Ventral Vagal State | The most evolved autonomic state, associated with safety, social engagement, and calm positive arousal; involves the myelinated vagus nerve | Social engagement system |
| Somatic Markers | Body sensations that become associated with specific emotional states and guide decision-making; concept from Antonio Damasio's research | Gut feelings (informal) |
| Cognitive Appraisal | The mental process of evaluating the significance and meaning of a situation, which determines which emotion is experienced from a given state of arousal | Emotional interpretation |
| Arousal Congruence | The principle that emotional states sharing similar arousal levels (both high or both low) are easier to reappraise between than states with different arousal levels | — |
| Valence | The positive or negative quality of an emotional experience; excitement has positive valence, anxiety has negative valence | Emotional tone |
| Insular Cortex | A brain region deep within the lateral sulcus that is the primary hub for interoceptive processing, integrating body signals with emotional awareness | Insula |

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: Pre-Presentation Arousal
- **Scenario**: An autistic person with alexithymia is about to give a presentation on their special interest topic. They notice their heart racing and stomach churning.
- **Illustration**: The arousal could be excitement (they love this topic and want to share it) or anxiety (fear of social judgment). With alexithymia, they may default to interpreting it as anxiety and want to avoid the presentation. Body-based checking: Are their muscles tight and defensive (anxiety) or energized and ready (excitement)? Is their breathing shallow and constricted (anxiety) or quick but deep (excitement)? Is their mind generating "what if it goes wrong" thoughts (anxiety) or "I can't wait to share this" thoughts (excitement)?
- **Key Insight**: The same arousal has very different implications depending on whether it reflects excitement or anxiety. Systematic body checking provides more reliable data than trying to "identify the feeling" directly.

### Example 2: Social Event Anticipation
- **Scenario**: Receiving an invitation to a party. The person notices their stomach fluttering and a sense of heightened energy.
- **Illustration**: Checking: Is the stomach sensation more like fluttering openness (excitement about seeing people) or churning tightness (dread of social demands)? Are hands warm (excitement) or cold and clammy (anxiety)? Is there a pulling-toward feeling (excitement/approach motivation) or a pulling-away feeling (anxiety/avoidance motivation)?
- **Key Insight**: Approach vs. avoidance motivation is one of the most reliable behavioral indicators even when body signals are ambiguous.

### Example 3: New Opportunity at Work
- **Scenario**: Offered a new project with challenging deadlines. Notices increased heart rate, difficulty concentrating, and restlessness.
- **Illustration**: This is a classic ambiguous arousal scenario. The physiological response to "exciting challenge" and "overwhelming threat" can be nearly identical in the initial moments. Key differentiators: Does the restlessness feel like wanting to get started (excitement) or wanting to escape (anxiety)? Is the breathing pattern energized or restricted? Over time (minutes to hours), does cortisol-driven fatigue set in (anxiety) or does dopamine-driven energy sustain (excitement)?
- **Key Insight**: Time course matters—excitement tends to be energizing over time while anxiety is depleting. Checking back in with your body 15-30 minutes later can provide clearer data.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: Excitement and anxiety feel completely different and should be easy to tell apart
  - **Reality**: Research shows they share the same foundational sympathetic arousal. Both involve increased heart rate, adrenaline, and heightened alertness. The differences are in secondary markers (cortisol, vagal tone, muscle tension patterns) that require interoceptive skill to detect. Alison Wood Brooks (2014) demonstrated that they are so similar physiologically that people can reappraise anxiety as excitement more easily than they can calm down.

- **Misconception**: If you can't tell what you're feeling, you're not paying enough attention
  - **Reality**: Alexithymia involves a measurable deficit in interoceptive processing, not a lack of effort. Research by Murphy et al. (2018) showed that alexithymia represents a general failure of interoception affecting both emotional and non-emotional body signals. This is neurologically based, not a character failing.

- **Misconception**: Autistic people don't feel emotions as strongly
  - **Reality**: Research consistently shows autistic individuals feel emotions with equal or greater intensity. The difficulty lies in identifying, differentiating, and describing the emotions—not in experiencing them. Shah et al. (2016) found that interoceptive impairment, not autism per se, predicts emotional identification difficulty.

- **Misconception**: The "butterflies in your stomach" feeling is always anxiety
  - **Reality**: Both excitement and anxiety produce gastrointestinal sensations through the gut-brain axis. The qualitative difference is in the nature of the sensation: excitement tends to produce fluttery/opening sensations while anxiety produces churning/constricting sensations, though individual variation exists.

- **Misconception**: You should try to calm down when feeling anxious
  - **Reality**: Brooks (2014) found that reappraising anxiety as excitement (staying at the same arousal level but shifting valence) is more effective than trying to calm down, because it is "arousal congruent"—it requires only a cognitive shift in valence rather than both a cognitive and physiological shift.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **Mixed States**: It is possible to feel both excited and anxious simultaneously (e.g., starting a new job). The autonomic nervous system can produce complex mixed-state activation patterns. In these cases, the practical approach is to acknowledge both states rather than forcing a single label.
- **Alexithymia Severity Spectrum**: Alexithymia exists on a spectrum. Individuals with high alexithymia may need more external/contextual cues to differentiate emotions, while those with moderate alexithymia may benefit more from body-scanning techniques.
- **Sensory Overload Interaction**: In autistic individuals, sensory overload can produce arousal that mimics anxiety regardless of the emotional context, making differentiation even more challenging. The body signals from sensory overwhelm overlap substantially with anxiety signals.
- **Medication Effects**: SSRIs, beta-blockers, and other medications can alter physiological arousal patterns, potentially making body-based differentiation less reliable.
- **Trauma History**: Previous traumatic experiences can cause the nervous system to default to threat interpretation of ambiguous arousal, biasing toward anxiety labeling.
- **Time of Day and Physiological State**: Baseline autonomic state varies with circadian rhythm, caffeine intake, sleep quality, and other factors. Body signal interpretation is more reliable when baseline is understood.

## Related Concepts

- **Emotional Granularity**: The ability to make fine-grained distinctions between similar emotional states. Lower emotional granularity is associated with alexithymia and predicts worse emotion regulation outcomes.
- **Mindfulness-Based Interoceptive Training**: Structured programs that build body awareness through systematic attention to internal signals. Kelly Mahler's Interoception Curriculum is specifically designed for neurodivergent individuals.
- **The Interoception Curriculum (Mahler)**: A structured five-step approach: Notice → Name → Link → Understand → Manage. This framework provides a systematic method for building interoceptive skills.
- **Russell's Circumplex Model of Affect**: A model that maps emotions along two dimensions: arousal (low to high) and valence (negative to positive). Excitement = high arousal + positive valence; anxiety = high arousal + negative valence.
- **Damasio's Somatic Marker Hypothesis**: The theory that body sensations (somatic markers) guide decision-making by providing emotional context. Impaired interoception can disrupt this system.
- **Differential Emotions Theory**: The theory that each basic emotion has a unique neural and physiological signature, supported by Nummenmaa's body mapping research.

## Research Gaps

- Limited research specifically comparing bodily maps of excitement vs. anxiety in autistic populations
- Few longitudinal studies on whether interoceptive training specifically improves excitement/anxiety differentiation in alexithymia
- The Nummenmaa body map study examined broad emotion categories (happiness, fear, anger, etc.) but did not specifically isolate "excitement" as a distinct category from happiness
- Most polyvagal theory applications to autism focus on anxiety reduction rather than positive arousal enhancement
- Limited quantitative data on the time course of neurochemical divergence (how quickly cortisol vs. dopamine cascades differentiate the two states)
- Need for more research on how specific autistic sensory profiles interact with interoceptive processing of positive vs. negative arousal
