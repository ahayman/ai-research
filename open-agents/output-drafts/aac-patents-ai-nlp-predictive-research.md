## Executive Summary

The AAC patent landscape at the intersection of AI, NLP, and predictive technology is moderately dense but with significant white space for innovation. The market is dominated by a few key players:

- **PRC-Saltillo / Semantic Compaction Systems** - Owns foundational Minspeak/semantic compaction patents (many now expired), historically aggressive in patent enforcement
- **Tobii (AB)** - World's largest eye-tracking patent portfolio (~850 filings, 400+ granted)
- **Google/Alphabet** - Strong patents in predictive keyboards, neural network decoding, and LLM-based communication acceleration
- **Microsoft** - Acquired SwiftKey (TouchType) and Nuance Communications; holds predictive keyboard patents
- **Meta Platforms** - Brain-computer interface patents for text prediction
- **Snap Inc.** - Pictorial symbol prediction patent
- **Apple** - Personal Voice synthesis patents with AAC applications
- **Spoken Inc.** - Direct AAC predictive speech patent
- **Qualcomm** - LLM sentence prediction patent (pending)

**Key Findings:**
- Core Minspeak/semantic compaction patents are EXPIRED, opening opportunities in icon-based prediction
- The intersection of LLMs + AAC is largely unpatented territory with only research publications
- Context-aware AAC prediction is primarily academic research with limited patent protection
- Eye-gaze + LLM acceleration (Google's SpeakFaster) is published research, not yet patented as a system
- Symbol/icon prediction for AAC specifically remains an underpatented area

## Patent Litigation History in AAC

### Prentke Romich / Semantic Compaction Systems v. Speak For Yourself (2012)

**Background:** In February 2012, PRC and Semantic Compaction Systems sued Speak For Yourself LLC over its $299.99 iPad AAC app (vs. PRC devices at ~$8,000). The lawsuit alleged infringement of patents related to dynamic keyboards and methods for dynamically redefining keys in AAC systems, based on a patent issued in 1995 (US5748177A and related).

**Impact:**
- Despite no injunction being granted, the patent holder successfully got the app removed from Apple App Store and Google Play Store
- The case gained national media attention because it directly affected children who depended on the app to communicate
- TIME, PCWorld, The Register, and Techdirt covered the story extensively

**Resolution:** Settled with confidential terms. All claims and takedown notices were dropped. Speak For Yourself later resumed availability.

**Key Lesson for New Market Entrants:** Even expired foundational patents may have continuation patents or related claims. PRC/SCS has historically been aggressive in enforcing AAC patents. Any company entering the AAC space should conduct thorough freedom-to-operate analysis.

### WordLogic v. TouchType/SwiftKey (2015)

**Background:** WordLogic Corporation sued TouchType Ltd. (makers of SwiftKey) over US8552984 (predictive keyboard redirection method). WordLogic claimed all prediction technology uses their patented method.

**Key Lesson:** Broad predictive keyboard patents remain a litigation risk for any keyboard-based AAC system.

   - Qualcomm US20250173561A1 (LLM sentence prediction)
   - Apple Personal Voice patents (expanding AAC voice banking capabilities)
   - US20240264666A1 (eye sign language communication)

3. **Consider Defensive Patent Filing in White Space Areas:**
   - LLM-powered AAC icon/symbol prediction
   - Context-aware multimodal AAC input fusion
   - Personalized AAC language model adaptation
   - AAC conversation flow prediction
   - Abbreviated text expansion specifically for AAC users

### Design-Around Opportunities

- **Minspeak/Semantic Compaction:** Basic patents expired. Can implement icon-sequence-to-language encoding without infringing, but avoid using UNITY, MINSPEAK, or related trademarks.
- **Dynamic Keyboards (US5748177A):** Expired 2015. Dynamic symbol grid interfaces are now freely implementable.
- **Predictive Text:** Use own neural network architectures rather than exact methods described in WordLogic or Microsoft patents.
- **Eye Tracking:** Consider licensing from Tobii or implementing independently developed algorithms (very difficult given portfolio breadth).
