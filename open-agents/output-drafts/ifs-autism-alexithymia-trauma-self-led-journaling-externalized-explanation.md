# Self-Led IFS, Journaling, and Adaptations for Externalized Processing

## What this page is for

This is the closing category of the deep dive. By now you have the parts model, the mechanics, the empirical floor, the critiques, the current spiritual turn, the alexithymia structural problem, the autism-specific intersection, the complex-trauma fit case, and the failure-mode catalogue. This page is where the question becomes practical: **what can a writer do, between sessions, in their own notebook, that is genuinely IFS-aligned and not over-extending into territory that needs a clinician?**

The user this page is written for is doing IFS-adjacent work alongside therapy, processes best in writing, has unreliable interoception, and has a complex-trauma history. That profile changes the answer. For someone in this configuration, journaling-led parts work is not a fallback — it is potentially the *primary* mode, the channel that does the structural work the body-scan channel cannot. But complex trauma is also exactly the territory where solo unburdening carries the highest documented risk. The honest framing is therefore not "self-led vs. therapist-led" but "what survives translation into solo written practice without therapist support, given this profile, and where does the work belong in the room with a clinician?"

This page assumes you have read [Cat 6](../ifs-and-alexithymia/index.html) (the structural argument for externalized access), [Cat 8](../ifs-and-complex-trauma/index.html) (where pure IFS reaches its limits in trauma), and [Cat 9](../failure-modes-contraindications/index.html) (premature unburdening, the merciful manager, performed parts work). It builds on those, rather than restating them.

**Confidence overview**: Of seventeen substantive claims in this page, thirteen are confirmed and three are uncertain (specific 2025–2026 IFS Institute Level 1 pricing varies by region; the proportion of "IFS-Informed" practitioners with substantive coursework is not measurable; the precise structure of cohort-based programs varies). One claim was invalid as originally framed in the task brief — *The Internal Family Systems Workbook* is by Schwartz solo, published by Sounds True in 2024, not "Schwartz & Sweezy 2023, PESI" — and has been corrected throughout. The structural-translation framing of "the re-read step externalizes the unblending move" is this page's own analytical synthesis, not a direct quote from any source.

**Key takeaways**:

- The self-led IFS landscape has four layers: books, apps and online courses, web/blog prompt content, and adjacent regulation tools. *No Bad Parts* (Schwartz, Sounds True 2021) and Earley's *Self-Therapy* (2009 onward) are the load-bearing book-layer entries.
- Journaling-led parts work has a small set of repeatable, well-documented techniques: letters in both directions, two- and three-pass dialogues, parts mapping, burden inventories, polarisation tracking, and trigger journals.
- Writing-led parts work likely produces a *different* therapeutic event than full felt-sense IFS — possibly slower, possibly less bodily, possibly equally lasting. Honest claim, not a shortfall.
- The IFS-Informed / IFS-Trained / IFS-Certified distinction is real, official, and routinely blurred in the marketplace. Knowing what to ask matters more than the label.
- For your profile (complex trauma, low interoception, with therapist), solo journaling sits in the alongside-therapy zone — not in the green zone of pure self-led practice. Manager-work and trigger-tracking are appropriate solo; exile and unburdening work belong in session.

## The self-led IFS landscape

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-1 -->

There are four layers worth knowing about, and the depth difference between them is significant.

**The book layer.** *No Bad Parts: Healing Trauma and Restoring Wholeness with the Internal Family Systems Model* (Schwartz, Sounds True 2021) is the flagship and the entry point most readers find first. It mixes accessible explication of the model with experiential exercises: Getting to Know a Protector, Mapping Your Parts, Unblending and Embodying, Daily IFS Meditation, Working with a Challenging Protector, Advanced Parts Mapping, Working with Triggers, and a body meditation. Each exercise is structured but reasonably forgiving. The audiobook foreword is by Alanis Morissette.

*The Internal Family Systems Workbook: A Guide to Discover Your Self and Heal Your Parts* (Schwartz, Sounds True Inner Workbooks line, 2024) is the structured-exercise companion. It contains 50+ practices, exercises, and meditations, with QR codes pointing to guided audio practices. (Note: an earlier version of this brief misattributed this workbook to Schwartz and Sweezy through PESI in 2023. The actual book is Schwartz solo, Sounds True 2024 — the correction matters if you are buying the book.)

*You Are the One You've Been Waiting For: Applying Internal Family Systems to Intimate Relationships* (Schwartz) was originally self-published through Trailheads in 2008 and substantially revised by Sounds True in 2023. It is couples-relationship-focused but contains substantial self-led parts-work content, particularly around the "courageous love" and "internal mate" frameworks.

*Self-Therapy: A Step-By-Step Guide to Creating Wholeness Using IFS* (Jay Earley, 2009 first edition, 3rd edition with Karen Donnely 2022) pre-dates *No Bad Parts* by twelve years and is the longest-running self-led IFS manual in print. Earley treats IFS as a complete self-help method; the book includes session transcripts, help sheets, and structured exercises. Schwartz wrote the foreword in some editions. Bonnie Weiss's *Self-Therapy Workbook* (2013) is its exercise companion, "designed for people using IFS to do personal work on themselves" with explicit journal-style parts work. Weiss has also published topic-focused workbooks on the Inner Critic, Inner Champion, and perfectionism.

*Transcending Trauma: Healing Complex PTSD with Internal Family Systems* (Frank Anderson, PESI 2021) is primarily clinician-facing but with substantial self-applicable material; Anderson's downloadable companion meditations and worksheets extend the book into solo practice for readers who already understand the basics.

For autistic readers specifically, *Wired to Feel: Autism as a Condition of Sensory Surplus* (Bergenfield, Sweezy, Malow, New Harbinger 2026) is the first IFS-grounded book written specifically for autistic readers, by an autistic IFS Level 3 practitioner co-authoring with Martha Sweezy and a sleep-medicine MD. (Some retailer listings front *Embodying Autism*; same book, same authors, same ISBN 9781648486357.) Cat 7 treats this in depth.

**The apps and online layer.** *The IFS Journal* (theifsjournal.com) is a guided-journaling product and free web app, created by therapist "Becky" with collaborator James — independent of the IFS Institute, editorially consistent with IFS canon. It offers parts-mapping worksheets, structured journaling prompts, "5 Guided Prompts to Meet a Protector Part," and a "How to Identify Your IFS Parts" guide. The free web app walks beginners through a parts-meeting sequence.

Insight Timer hosts Schwartz's own guided meditations — including "Internal Family Systems Meditation" and "Embodiment: IFS" — and Tim Fortescue's "An IFS Parts Mapping Guided Meditation" and "An Advanced Parts Mapping IFS Guided Meditation Exercise." All available on the free tier. Sounds True hosts paid online courses including *Introduction to Internal Family Systems*, *Befriending Your Inner Parts*, *Greater Than the Sum of Our Parts*, and *The Spirituality of IFS* — the last is the spiritual/transpersonal turn covered in [Cat 11](../psychedelic-spirituality-unattached-burdens/index.html), not a clinical self-led course in the same sense as the others.

The IFS Institute's own Online Circle is a cohort-based introductory program, opening Spring and Fall, with a Continuity Membership for completers and Level 1 graduates that includes monthly Q&A sessions with lead trainers and Schwartz himself.

**The web and blog layer.** Propagate Hope Counseling publishes the most rigorous freely-available IFS journaling content online: "Ultimate Guide to IFS Journaling" and the clinician-facing "Clinical Techniques of IFS Journaling" guide. Casually Luxe's "How to Talk With Parts" example exercise gives a concrete letter template ("Dear [part name], I'm noticing you today because…"). Self Leadership Journey's Substack post on parts mapping and ifs.space's "IFS Journaling: How to Talk to Your Parts on Paper" are also useful. Aggregator sites — MyLifeNote's "70 IFS Journal Prompts," LeStallion's "50 Prompts," Sagebrush Counseling's "Parts Work Journal Prompts" — vary substantially in quality. Treat them as prompt menus to sample, not as authoritative practice guides.

**Adjacent tools.** *Stuck Not Broken* (Justin Sunseri, LMFT) is a polyvagal-focused podcast, *not* IFS, but commonly recommended adjacent in trauma communities for the nervous-system regulation that supports parts work. Sunseri's Polyvagal Trauma Relief System is paid; the podcast (332+ episodes) is free. Worth flagging: Polyvagal Theory itself was challenged in 2023 by a 39-author critique calling some of its neuroanatomical claims "untenable" — the show is useful for self-regulation framing, but the underlying neuroscience is genuinely contested. iRest yoga nidra is similarly adjacent: nervous-system regulation from a separate tradition.

## Journaling-led parts work — the actual techniques

The published journaling-led IFS literature converges on a small set of repeatable structures. Each is documented in at least two independent sources. The techniques in this section are not invented; they are the working consensus across Schwartz's books, Earley and Weiss, Bonnie Weiss's workbook, the IFS Journal site, Propagate Hope, Casually Luxe, ifs.space, and the Substack writing community.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-2 -->

**Letter to a part.** Write directly to the part by name, in second person. The Casually Luxe and IFS Journal template: *"Dear [part name], I'm noticing you today because…"* — continue with what the part has been doing, what you appreciate, what you would like to ask. Schwartz uses this in *No Bad Parts* under "Working with a Challenging Protector."

**Letter from a part to Self.** Reverse direction: write *as* the part, in first person, speaking to the Self. Allow the defensive, hostile, or scared tone; do not edit. This is the writing equivalent of the IFS protocol's direct access — the move where a therapist asks a part to speak directly when in-sight access is unavailable.

**Two-pass dialogue.** Sequence: write as the part uninterrupted until it has said everything; re-read what was written; write back as Self responding to what was said. The IFS Journal site phrases the protocol's explicit norm as "the rule that everyone gets to finish their sentence." The two-pass structure externalises the unblending move: pause and re-read forces the reader out of the part's stance into the observer position.

**Three-pass dialogue (the unblending pass added).** Insert a pass between the part's voice and the Self response: a pass where the writer writes about *how they feel toward what the part said*. This is the "Feel toward" 6-F move written down. If the answer is anything other than the 8 Cs (curious, calm, compassionate, clear, courageous, confident, creative, connected), there is another part in the way; that part gets its own brief letter before continuing.

**Parts mapping.** Schwartz's *No Bad Parts* exercise. Notice a part calling for attention; locate it in body or mind; on paper, draw or label the part. Hold the page at arm's length and ask: how does this part relate to other parts? Draw lines or circles to show alliances, polarisations, protections. Practice being an observer of the system. *No Bad Parts* includes both a basic and "Advanced Parts Mapping" version. Tim Fortescue's Insight Timer meditations guide the same process auditorily.

**Burden inventory.** List for each part: what beliefs is it carrying ("I am a burden," "I am unsafe," "I have to be perfect"), what feelings ("terror," "shame," "helplessness"), what was the formative scene if known. The burden inventory is a Schwartz move from clinical IFS adapted to writing; Bonnie Weiss's *Self-Therapy Workbook* contains the most structured written burden-inventory exercises.

**Polarisation tracking.** For each named part, identify its polarisation partner — the part that takes the opposite stance. Perfectionist manager polarised with rebellious firefighter, for instance. Write what each says about the other. Earley's *Self-Therapy* includes a chapter on polarisations specifically; Weiss's workbook adds the structured version.

**Trigger journal.** Longitudinal log: date, situation, which part was activated, what the part felt or said, how the situation resolved. Adapted from the *No Bad Parts* "Working with Triggers" exercise; Propagate Hope's clinical-techniques guide turns this into a multi-week structured tracking practice. **For a systems-thinker who notices structure best in aggregated data, this is the channel.** Real-time experience cannot hold the longitudinal pattern. The journal can.

**Inner Critic protocol** (Bonnie Weiss). The most-cited IFS application of journaling — Weiss's *Self-Therapy for Your Inner Critic* and *The Illustrated Workbook for Self-Therapy for Your Inner Critic* provide a specific written protocol for inner-critic parts: identify, name, ask its concern, identify what exile it protects, dialogue with both.

## Why writing-led parts work may be the primary mode for low-interoception readers

This is a structural argument, not a marketing claim. Cat 6 makes the deeper case for substitute access channels in alexithymia; what concerns us here is what the writing modality specifically does, and where it loses leverage.

**The page is an external surface.** It holds material outside the body, where it can be read again. For a reader whose access to internal sensation is unreliable, the page substitutes for the felt-sense channel as a place to put parts material. The information has somewhere to go that is not contingent on perceiving it from the inside.

**Writing forces a slower pace.** The 6-F protocol live runs at the speed of a session. Writing slows it. Each step takes as long as it takes. There is no clinician waiting; there is no felt-sense-snapping-shut. For readers who think faster than they can feel, this is a clean structural fit — the modality matches the cognition.

**Re-reading creates an externalised step-back.** The unblending move in clinical IFS is internal: notice you are blended with a part, separate. In writing, the equivalent is finishing a paragraph and re-reading it. **The page does the unblending move *for* you.** This is a real structural translation, not a workaround. (The framing of the re-read step as the externalised unblending move is this page's own analytical synthesis. Schwartz does not explicitly describe it this way; multiple journaling-led IFS sources teach the technique without naming what the technique structurally is. The synthesis is defensible but is the page's contribution rather than a sourced claim.)

**Multi-pass methods externalise the Self-energy diagnostic.** The three-pass dialogue (write-as-part, write-as-feel-toward, write-as-Self) externalises the "Am I in Self?" check into a written act. If the middle pass produces anything other than 8-Cs language, that is the diagnostic answering itself.

**Patterns become visible over weeks.** Real-time experience cannot hold longitudinal pattern. The journal can. Which parts recur, when, in response to which triggers — that information lives in the aggregate, not in any single moment. For systems thinkers, the longitudinal layer is often where the most useful insights live.

**Granularity-building works better in writing.** Lisa Feldman Barrett's emotional-granularity argument: putting feelings into words trains the system to distinguish nearby states. Writing is the granularity-training modality. Even the act of having to choose between "frustrated" and "resentful" does developmental work over time.

**Where writing loses leverage.** Honest accounting of what does not survive the translation:

- **The unburdening event.** IFS frames unburdening as a somatic-experiential release with a felt shift. Writing can approximate it (a do-over scene written in detail) but the felt shift may not arrive. A reasonable position: writing-led parts work may produce a *different* therapeutic event than full IFS — possibly slower, possibly less bodily, possibly equally lasting. This is a trade-off, not a shortfall.
- **Corrective experience in imagination.** Schwartz's "do-over" and "retrieval" rituals depend on visualisation-with-feel. Aphantasic readers and low-interoception readers can simulate these in writing, but the body-shift may not occur.
- **In-session emotional release.** Crying, shaking, somatic discharge — these happen in clinical sessions and are unlikely in solo writing.
- **Live witnessing.** The therapist as witness is a real ingredient; their presence is part of why exile material can be held without overwhelm. The page does not witness in the same way a clinician does.

## Other externalisation techniques

Writing is not the only externalisation channel. The full menu, named here so you know what exists:

**Index cards.** One part per card; physically arrange the cards on a table to show relationships. Card moves represent shifts in the system. Used by autistic IFS practitioners (Bergenfield references variants) and in some IFS Institute training material.

**Stones, figurines, sand tray.** Object representation: a bowl of stones or small figures; choose one for each part; arrange on a board or tray. Originates partially in Jungian sand tray and family-systems Constellation work; adopted by IFS-Informed practitioners and detailed in the IFS Telehealth Collective's "Creative Externalization" companion piece.

**Drawings and visual mapping.** The *No Bad Parts* parts-mapping exercise is fundamentally a drawing exercise; can be elaborated into formal portraits of each part, shapes, colours. **Important note: drawing works for aphantasic readers because the visual happens on the page, not in the mind.** Cat 7 treats the aphantasia case directly.

**Spreadsheet / database structures.** Less canonical but well-suited to systems-thinker users: each part as a row; columns for role, age, primary feeling, what it protects against, polarisation partner, trigger pattern, current burden, target unburdening goal. Allows longitudinal data tracking and pattern recognition across months. There is no published authoritative source for this — it is an extrapolation from the trigger-journal idea — but for a user who reasons in structured data this can be the cleanest representational layer.

**Voice memos.** For users who process in spoken language better than written; record yourself speaking as a part, then listen back and respond as Self. The auditory parallel to the two-pass written method.

**Multi-chair work** (Gestalt-derived). Sit in one chair as a part, move to another chair as Self, alternate. The IFS Institute teaches this in trainings as a body-anchored alternative to in-sight work; the self-led version is documented in *Self-Therapy* and the workbooks. Not strictly externalisation (the parts are still inside) but the chair-change forces a physical pattern interrupt that the body registers.

## The IFS-Informed / IFS-Trained / IFS-Certified distinction

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-3 -->

The IFS Institute uses three labels for practitioners. The distinctions are official but readily blurred in the marketplace, and for someone selecting a practitioner the difference matters.

**IFS-Informed.** The Institute's own definition (per their "Learning Streams" page): *"Those who take Learning Hub courses, attend annual conferences, or participate in workshops from IFS Trainers may represent this level of education by saying they are IFS Informed."* The label covers anyone who uses an IFS lens without completing Level 1. The range is enormous: it includes practitioners who have done extensive Sounds True coursework, attended several IFS conferences, and completed the IFS Online Circle on one end, and on the other end a practitioner who has read *No Bad Parts* once and is using the language. **The label does not certify clinical competence in IFS or any formal supervision.**

**IFS-Trained.** Awarded by the IFS Institute upon completion of Level 1 training: approximately 90–100 hours of cohort-based training, currently in the ~$4,000+ range (exact figures vary by region and year — Spanish trainings, UK trainings, and US trainings price differently, and the figures shift each cycle). A three-level pathway exists: Level 1 (Foundations), Level 2 (Deepening — topic-specific advanced trainings), Level 3 (Mastery). **Training does not include a clinical-competence examination; completion is mainly attendance-based and consultation-based with assistant trainers.**

**IFS-Certified.** A separate, additional process layered on top of training. Requires Level 1 (and Level 2 or 3 in some cases), supervised consultation hours, case presentations, formal evaluation by senior IFS Institute trainers, and is renewed every two years. Currently about $200 application fee plus $150 every two years for recertification. As of 2024–2025, certified practitioners are a fraction of trained practitioners.

**Where the labels blur in practice.** Psychology Today and most therapist directories accept self-reported IFS designation. A profile saying "IFS" or "IFS-Informed" tells the reader very little about actual training depth. The IFS Institute Provider Directory at ifs-institute.com/practitioners is the strongest filter — it lists only practitioners who have completed Institute training. For everything outside that directory, the label range is genuinely enormous.

**What to ask.** Five questions surface the actual depth of any practitioner labeling themselves "IFS":

1. What level — Informed, Trained, or Certified?
2. When and through whom did you train?
3. Are you in current consultation?
4. How do you adapt for low-interoception or autistic clients?
5. What's your stance on solo unburdening in complex trauma?

Question 4 and question 5 are the diagnostic ones for your profile. Question 5 specifically tells you whether the practitioner has integrated trauma-and-dissociation-informed thinking into their practice — Twombly's framework, Anderson's complex-PTSD intensives, Fisher's TIST — or whether they are running pure IFS protocol without the safety scaffolding. The answer to question 5 in particular distinguishes a practitioner you can do exile work with from one with whom you should keep solo journaling firmly in the manager-and-mapping zone.

## When solo IFS is appropriate vs. risky for your profile

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-4 -->

This is the load-bearing decision point. The honest framing is drawn from Schwartz's own warnings in *No Bad Parts* (which repeatedly cautions against rushing protectors), Earley's *Self-Therapy* (which assumes peer or solo practice on relatively well-resourced clients), Twombly's *Trauma and Dissociation Informed IFS* (2022), Anderson's *Transcending Trauma*, and Fisher's TIST framework.

**Appropriate territory for solo practice.** Mild to moderate concerns; everyday-life manager parts (perfectionism, procrastination, inner critic). Established Self-access most of the time. Stable life context — housing, employment, relationships not in crisis. With-therapist supplementation, where solo work feeds session material rather than replacing the session. The specific moves that are broadly low-risk:

- Parts identification, naming, and mapping
- Trigger-tracking and longitudinal observation
- Letters to and from manager parts
- Daily-life unblending micro-practice — the "step back" move in everyday situations

**Risky or contraindicated for solo practice.** Active complex PTSD or severe dissociation; the trauma-and-dissociation-informed IFS literature is consistent that these populations need a clinician who can phase the work and titrate exposure. Severe ongoing dysregulation. Active eating-disorder or substance-use crisis (pre-stabilisation). "Going to your most painful exile" without support — Schwartz himself names this as a frequent failure mode. **Premature unburdening** — releasing burdens before protectors consent, before witnessing is complete, or in the absence of Self-energy. This is the most-cited solo failure mode in the clinical literature ([Cat 9](../failure-modes-contraindications/index.html) treats it in depth).

**Working with parts when not in Self.** A key indicator: another part is doing the work and calling itself Self. The two-pass written method partly mitigates this (the re-read produces a step-back) but does not eliminate the risk. **Rumination loops** — same parts, same content, weeks running; the writing is not producing change, it is rehearsing the burden. This is one of the markers that solo work has gone sideways and needs to come into the session.

**Your specific case is "alongside therapy."** This is the most common modern configuration and is explicitly supported by Schwartz's own published work. What it looks like in practice: the weekly therapy session is the primary container; between-session journaling is data-gathering and observation, not deep exile work; bringing the journal (or its summary) into session when material has built up.

In the matrix above, your profile sits in the yellow zone. You are not in the green zone of pure solo practice — complex trauma and unreliable interoception both push you out of that — but you are also not in the red zone where stabilisation comes first and even manager-work is too activating. You are in the middle: solo manager-work and mapping are appropriate; pattern review is appropriate; bringing material to therapy is appropriate; **solo unburdening of exile material is not.**

**Markers that solo work is going sideways and to bring it to the session immediately**: rumination loops (same content, no movement, weeks running); increased dissociation after journaling sessions; parts-language taking over normal speech (everything becomes "a part of me"); a sense of being "spoken through" by parts; new or worsened sleep disturbance, intrusion, or somatic symptoms in days following journaling; sense of having "unburdened" something but feeling worse; the same exile keeps coming back as if no unburdening took. These are the patterns Cat 9 catalogues; treat any one of them as a signal to bring the material to the therapist rather than continue solo.

## A weekly structure that could work — concretely

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-5 -->

This is one worked example, drawn from the structure Propagate Hope's clinician-facing guide and the IFS Journal site recommend. It is illustrative, not prescriptive. Adjust to your own session day, your own bandwidth, and what is actually happening that week.

**Monday — brief check-in (5 min).** "What part is loud today?" One paragraph. Don't dialogue with it yet; just notice and name. This is data, not work.

**Tuesday — letter to a manager (15–25 min).** Pick one part you have already been noticing. Run the two-pass dialogue: write as the part, re-read, check stance, respond as Self. If another part interrupts during the stance check, run the three-pass version with a brief detour for the interrupting part. Keep this in manager territory — perfectionist parts, inner critic parts, procrastination parts, pleaser parts. **Do not deliberately go to exile material solo.**

**Wednesday — trigger log (5 min in the evening).** Three-line entries across the whole week, not just Wednesday: date, situation, which part activated, how it resolved. The dotted self-loop in Figure 5 makes this explicit — Wednesday is just the day this entry gets formally logged, but the logging itself runs continuously.

**Thursday — pattern review (10 min).** Re-read recent entries, including the trigger log accumulating across the week. Note: which parts recur? What triggers them? Is there movement, or is this the same content rehearsing itself (a rumination loop)?

**Friday — therapy prep (5–10 min).** Pull one or two entries to bring to the session. Note one specific question for the therapist. Do not bring the full journal; bring the distilled patterns. The goal is to feed the session, not to outsource the session to your notebook.

**Saturday (or your usual session day) — therapy session.** The pivot point. Solo material informs the conversation; the clinician holds the room for whatever needs holding. Exile material that surfaced during the week — but that you noticed and stepped back from rather than working solo — comes here.

**Sunday — post-session integration (15 min).** What arose in the session? What part wants follow-up? Note for next week. This is often the most useful entry of the week — the session metabolises material that the solo work could not, and the integration entry holds what you came out with.

Total time: roughly 60–90 minutes a week. Most weeks most people can fit it. **If a week is overloaded, drop everything except the Wednesday trigger log and the Sunday integration entry.** Those are the highest-yield entries for a low-bandwidth week — the trigger log captures the data, the integration entry catches the session.

## What to try this week

Concrete suggestions, scaled to a low-stakes first attempt:

**If you have not yet tried any of this**: pick three protector parts you already know about (the procrastinator, the inner critic, the pleaser, whichever set is yours). Give each one a single page in a notebook. Spend 5 minutes per part introducing yourself in writing — "I'm noticing you. Tell me what you're worried about." Don't push. Don't go to exile material. The goal is to make first contact and produce written evidence that the contact happened.

**If you have done some solo IFS reading already**: try the two-pass dialogue with one manager part, structured exactly as Figure 2. Write as the part uninterrupted for one paragraph. Re-read. Check your stance. If it is one of the 8 Cs, write back as Self. If it is something else (annoyed, scared, frozen, impatient), name the second part briefly and write a note saying "another part is in the way" — that is the work for today; stop there.

**If you process best in structured data**: start a trigger log spreadsheet. Five columns: date, situation, part activated, what it said or felt, how it resolved. Run it for two weeks. At the end of two weeks, sort the data by which parts activate most, which situations trigger them, which resolutions worked. Bring the analysis to the next therapy session.

**Always — bring it to the room.** The most reliable single piece of guidance for this profile: whatever you write between sessions, bring some version of it to the therapist. The journal is feeding the session, not replacing it. The patterns the journal surfaces become the session's agenda. The exile material that appears in the writing — and you will notice when an exile is calling, even if you don't engage it solo — gets named in the session.

The empirical floor for writing-led parts work is genuinely thin. There is no peer-reviewed study of journaling-led IFS in any population. The closest empirical anchors are Pennebaker's expressive-writing tradition (which produces measurable health outcomes but is not IFS-structured) and the IFS-internal practice writing (which is consensus among practitioners but uncontrolled). What this means: the case for journaling-led parts work in low-interoception readers is structural and clinical-experiential, not RCT-validated. That distinction matters and should be preserved when explaining the practice to someone else.

## Common Misconceptions

### Misconception: "Self-led IFS means doing IFS alone instead of with a therapist."

**Reality**: For complex-trauma profiles, the strongly-recommended frame is "alongside therapy." Self-led practices that pair well with weekly therapy are the appropriate target; pure-solo unburdening is the high-risk move that the trauma-and-dissociation-informed clinical literature consistently warns against.

### Misconception: "If I journal correctly I'll get the same result as a session."

**Reality**: Writing-led parts work likely produces a *different* therapeutic event — possibly slower, possibly less bodily, possibly more durable as data — but not interchangeable with a session. The honest claim is "useful and complementary," not "equivalent."

### Misconception: "An IFS-Informed therapist is basically the same as an IFS-Trained therapist."

**Reality**: IFS-Informed ranges from "read the book once" to "extensive coursework + Online Circle + conferences." The label has no quality floor. Ask the practitioner directly about training depth, supervision status, and adaptation experience.

### Misconception: "If I can't feel into my body, I can't do IFS."

**Reality**: Cognitive, written, and externalised access channels are documented across the autistic-IFS-clinician literature; the model has more flexibility than its standard 6-F prompt implies. Cat 6 establishes this in detail; Cat 10 is the practical application.

### Misconception: "If a part feels too young or overwhelming I should push through."

**Reality**: Schwartz himself repeatedly warns to back off. *No Bad Parts* and Earley's *Self-Therapy* both treat "too much, too fast" as the signal to stop and bring the material to a clinician. This guidance is in the source material itself; it is not a Cat 10 invention.

The actual workbook is Schwartz solo, Sounds True (Inner Workbooks line) 2024, ISBN 9781649633118, 50+ practices with QR audio. The correction matters if you are buying the book.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Self-led IFS | IFS practice without a therapist, mediated by book, app, workbook, or journal |
| Parts mapping | Drawing or listing parts and their relationships on paper |
| Letter to a part | Written direct address to a part by name |
| Two-pass dialogue | Write as part → re-read → respond as Self |
| Three-pass dialogue | Adds a "feel toward" written check between part-voice and Self-response |
| Burden inventory | Written list of beliefs and feelings each part carries |
| Trigger journal | Longitudinal log of which parts activate when and how they resolve |
| Polarisation | Two parts in opposing stance (e.g., perfectionist / rebellious) |
| Direct access | IFS protocol move — talking directly to a part. In writing: writing as the part |
| Premature unburdening | Releasing burdens before protector consent or witnessing is complete |
| 8 Cs | The qualities of Self: curiosity, calm, compassion, clarity, courage, confidence, creativity, connectedness |
| IFS-Informed | Any IFS exposure short of formal Level 1 training |
| IFS-Trained | Level 1 IFS Institute training completed |
| IFS-Certified | Level 1 + supervised consultation + formal evaluation + biennial renewal |
| Externalisation | Placing parts on an outside surface — paper, cards, objects, voice recording |
| Aphantasia | Absence of voluntary mental imagery (separable from interoception) |
