### Claim 6: "~80% of vagal fibers are afferent (sensory), not efferent — and ~90% of afferents plus ~70% of efferents are unmyelinated C-fibers."
- **Verdict**: UNCERTAIN (7/10) — directionally correct, but the precise figures are widely repeated approximations from older anatomical studies and vary by cervical level
- **Original context**: Key Facts
- **Evidence for**: The 80% afferent figure is the textbook standard repeated across NCBI StatPearls, ScienceDirect Topics overview, Physiopedia, and Wikipedia. C-fiber dominance (60–80%) in cervical vagus afferents is also well-attested.
- **Evidence against**: More precise modern morphometric work (e.g., cadaveric studies, Settell et al. 2020 in pigs) finds the ratio is closer to 70:30 to 85:15 depending on species and cervical level; the "exactly 80/20" figure is a convenient teaching number, not a measured constant. The "90% of afferents + 70% of efferents unmyelinated" framing comes from Foley & DuBois (1937) and is still repeated but may not reflect modern measurements.
- **Why uncertain**: True in the broad-strokes sense the exploration uses it (afferent-dominant nerve, mostly C-fibers — which *is* the substantively important point against simplistic "vagal brake" framings). But the specific percentages are textbook tradition, not precision measurements.
- **What evidence actually supports**: The vagus nerve is afferent-dominant (60–85% depending on species/level) and predominantly unmyelinated; the popular "vagal brake" concept (myelinated efferents to the heart) describes a minority of vagal fibers.
- **Sources**: [ScienceDirect Vagus Nerve](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/neuroscience/vagus-nerve), [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vagus_nerve)
- **Notes**: Downstream writing should hedge with "approximately" or cite the original source explicitly.

### Claim 7: "PVT claims myelinated vagal fibers to the heart originate primarily in the nucleus ambiguus and are unique to mammals. Critics counter that lungfish, fish, reptiles, amphibians, and birds also possess myelinated vagal cardiac fibers from a ventral source, and that mammalian heart-rate control is overwhelmingly via the nucleus ambiguus — not split between nuclei as PVT predicts."
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Original context**: Key Facts
- **Evidence for**: Multiple sources independently confirm (a) PVT explicitly claims mammalian uniqueness of myelinated vagal cardiac fibers — Porges has stated "only mammals have a myelinated vagus"; (b) Monteiro et al. (2018) in *Science Advances* demonstrated lungfish have myelinated cardiorespiratory vagus from a ventrolateral brainstem source; (c) sharks, bony fish, birds also have characterized myelinated cardiac vagal fibers (Taylor & Joyce 2018; multiple comparative studies); (d) the mammalian DMV→cardiac projection is real but smaller (~20% of cardiac vagal neurons) than the NA projection (~80%), so the critique-of-PVT framing as "split between nuclei" is more accurate than the PVT-original framing.
- **Sources**: [Lungfish paper / PMC5833999](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5833999/), [Grossman 2023 / ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0301051123001060)
- **Notes**: One subtle issue — the exploration writes this critique-of-PVT in a way that elides what PVT *actually* says vs. what critics *say it says*. Per-category writing should give Porges's defense fair representation (he argues the critics misread him).

### Claim 8: "The Safe and Sound Protocol has been studied in autism but in small, uncontrolled pilot studies (e.g., n = 24, no sham control); reviewers have explicitly called for 'multicenter randomized controlled trials' before claiming efficacy."
- **Verdict**: INVALID (as stated) — substantive content correct but with a specific factual error
- **Original context**: Key Facts (5th bullet)
- **What's wrong**: The cited study ([PMC10049251](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10049251/)) had **n = 6**, not n = 24. The authors explicitly call it "exploratory" and acknowledge "small exploratory study without a sham control group."
- **What's actually correct**: PMC10049251 (Domínguez-Lara et al. 2023, *Int J Env Res Public Health*) studied **n = 6 adults with ASD** (ages 21–44; mean 27.1) over 5 daily sessions with 1-month follow-up. The authors called for "multicenter randomized controlled trials" specifically. There are other SSP pilots in autism (Porges et al. 2014 Listening Project Protocol n = 36 children, Heilman et al. 2023 n varied) — perhaps the exploration conflated sample sizes from multiple studies.
- **Why this misconception exists**: SSP autism literature is small and scattered across pilots; the n = 24 figure may be from a different SSP study (possibly a feasibility study or pediatric trial) not the cited PMC10049251.
- **Sources**: [PMC10049251 verified](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10049251/)
- **Notes**: **Must be corrected before downstream writing.** The broader claim that "SSP autism research is small, mostly uncontrolled pilots with no sham" is true; only the specific n is wrong.

### Claim 9: "Slow breathing, humming, chanting, and brief cold exposure do have independent peer-reviewed evidence for vagal/parasympathetic activation and stress reduction."
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Original context**: Key Facts (last bullet)
- **Evidence for**: Slow breathing at ~6 breaths/min consistently increases HRV/RSA across RCTs (Lehrer 2007; Russo et al. 2017; multiple breathwork meta-analyses). Humming/Bhramari pranayama elevates nasal nitric oxide and short-term HRV. Cold-face exposure triggers the well-characterized "diving reflex" with documented vagal/bradycardia response.
- **Sources**: [Oxygen Advantage breathing review](https://oxygenadvantage.com/blogs/science/how-to-stimulate-the-vagus-nerve-with-breathing-exercises)
- **Notes**: Confirmed. The framing "predate (and don't require) polyvagal theory" is also accurate — slow breathing/vagal tone literature substantially predates Porges 1994.

## Category Taxonomy Assessment

### Category 1: Foundations, History & Core Claims — CONFIRMED
- **Verdict**: Solid. Real, distinct, well-bounded. Has high material density (1994 paper, 2011 book, 2025 update, all five premises, the ladder model). Properly placed as #1.
- **Refinement suggestion**: Add explicit treatment of the "Jacksonian dissolution" claim — this is one of the *specific* premises critics target and it's worth introducing here so the critique chapter can reference it.

### Category 2: The Vagus Nerve Itself — Anatomy & Physiology — CONFIRMED
- **Verdict**: Strong category. Necessary scaffolding. The "factual ground" framing is exactly right.
- **Refinement suggestion**: Include a sub-aspect on **what RSA actually measures and its known confounds** (respiration depth, body position, age, fitness, medication) — Beauchaine 2015 [PMC4389219] is cited but the *limits* of RSA as a marker should be explicit, because they undercut a lot of popular "vagal tone" measurement claims.

### Category 3: The Scientific Critique — CONFIRMED
- **Verdict**: Strong. Captures the key papers (Grossman 2023, Neuhuber & Berthoud 2022, "Untenable" 2026, Porges 2026 response). Falsifiability sub-aspect is the right call.
- **Refinement suggestion**: Add the 2025–2026 *response-to-the-response* discourse: Psychology Today blog posts ("Polyvagal Theory Has Not Been Debunked"), clinician responses ("Significant, Not Sacred"), the Insight+/MJA coverage. Treating the *post-publication discourse* as data lets readers see how the field is actually metabolizing the dispute, which is information the user implicitly wants. Also add Neuhuber & Berthoud 2022 with a proper citation (it's currently only listed as "via the Wikipedia/critique sources").

### Category 4: Autism, Vagal Tone & Autonomic Differences — CONFIRMED
- **Verdict**: Good. Has the Cheng 2020 meta-analysis, the Kushki counter-paper, and biofeedback. Properly distinguishes correlational vs. causal.
- **Refinement suggestion**:
  - Add the **heterogeneity finding** (autistic subgroups with opposite hyper- vs. hypo-arousal profiles) which partially reconciles Cheng and Kushki.
  - Explicitly cross-reference **autism + alexithymia + interoception** literature — this dovetails directly with Category 9 (alternative models) and with the project's existing alexithymia work.
  - The exploration's note that "RSA reactivity to social stress" is the largest effect size in autism is worth elevating, since *that's* the part that most resembles a clinically-meaningful autonomic difference.

### Category 5: Meltdown, Shutdown & Autistic Burnout — CONFIRMED with refinement
- **Verdict**: This is the user's centrally-stated concern, so it needs to be excellent. The category is real and distinct, but its framing should be tightened.
- **Refinement suggestions**:
  - The category currently bundles three states (meltdown, shutdown, burnout) that have *different* relationships to polyvagal framing. Meltdown-as-sympathetic-overactivation has at least some empirical support (HRV reactivity to social stress is documented). Shutdown-as-dorsal-vagal is the most metaphorical (no in-vivo measurement). Autistic burnout has a growing empirical literature (Raymaker 2020; AASPIRE burnout measure; ~48 studies as of 2025) that does **not** use polyvagal framing — Raymaker explicitly grounds burnout in "cumulative load + chronic barriers to relief," which is closer to allostatic load.
  - **Recommendation**: keep the category but explicitly structure it as "three states, three different epistemic statuses." Distinguish (a) what direct measurement supports, (b) what's clinical heuristic, (c) what's narrative metaphor.
  - Cross-reference the existing `alexithymia-autism-cumulative-overwhelm` work as a comparison framework — autistic burnout has a non-polyvagal empirical foundation that the user will want to know about.

### Category 6: Interventions & Their Evidence Base — CONFIRMED
- **Verdict**: Strong. Includes the right intervention types and the right evidence-quality tiers.
- **Refinement suggestion**:
  - Add **consumer tVNS devices specifically** (Truvaga, Sensate, Pulsetto, Apollo Neuro). None are FDA-cleared as wellness devices; only gammaCore is FDA-cleared (for migraine/cluster headache). This is exactly the wellness-industry / pseudoscience-disentanglement the user wants.
  - Add a **"how to evaluate a polyvagal intervention claim"** sub-aspect (sham control? RCT? primary endpoint? sample size?) — this becomes a reusable critical-appraisal tool.

### Category 7: Co-Regulation, Social Engagement & Polyvagal-Informed Therapy — CONFIRMED
- **Verdict**: Real and well-bounded.
- **Refinement suggestion**:
  - The category currently emphasizes whether the *therapeutic practices* depend on PVT being correct — good, but the per-category research should be very explicit that **co-regulation, attunement, paced breathing, and titrated arousal all have evidence bases independent of PVT** (attachment theory, Rogers-Schein-Greenberg client-centered work, Linehan's DBT, the broader somatic tradition going back to Wilhelm Reich).
  - Sensorimotor Psychotherapy and Somatic Experiencing have their own evidence bases that can be assessed independently.

### Category 8: The Wellness Industry, Polyvagal Institute & Commercial Interests — CONFIRMED
- **Verdict**: Necessary and correctly framed.
- **Refinement suggestion**:
  - Add **specific examples of overreach claims** (autism "cure" content, "5-minute nervous system reset" reels, "vagal tone" supplements with no mechanism, gua sha "vagus stimulation"). Concrete examples are more useful than generalities for the user's stated red-flag-detection goal.
  - The Polyvagal Institute is a 501(c)(3) but offers paid courses; Unyte Health is for-profit and sells SSP. Mapping the **money flow** clearly is genuinely valuable — and the Porges patent royalty disclosure is the central conflict-of-interest fact.

### Category 9: Alternative & Competing Models — NEEDS REFINEMENT
- **Verdict**: Real category but currently under-built relative to its importance.
- **Refinement suggestions**:
  - **Add predictive processing / active inference frameworks** (Friston, Seth, Barrett) — these are arguably the *dominant* contemporary framework for interoception and emotion regulation and are conspicuously missing.
  - **Add allostasis (Sterling & Eyer, McEwen)** as a separate model. Allostatic load is directly relevant to autistic burnout — Raymaker's burnout model is essentially allostatic-load-style, and McEwen's work is a competitor to polyvagal framing for explaining chronic-stress sequelae.
  - **Add Lisa Feldman Barrett's theory of constructed emotion** — explicitly anti-Porges-style "hardwired emotion programs" framework with substantial empirical backing.
  - **Add Damasio's somatic marker hypothesis** — older but still influential, and useful for understanding the "body-feels-it-first" claim that's adjacent to PVT.
  - Without these additions, Category 9 risks looking like a single-model alternative (NVI) when the actual landscape has at least 4–5 plausible competitors.

### Category 10: Practical Decision Aid — CONFIRMED
- **Verdict**: Excellent synthesis category. Action-oriented framing matches user's stated need.
- **Refinement suggestion**: Make sure the per-category research includes a **concrete decision tree** the user can apply ("is this intervention claiming a mechanism beyond what evidence supports? are there cheaper/safer alternatives? am I distracting from a real problem like sensory overload, demand overload, or comorbidity?"). The current sub-aspect list points this way; just ensure the writing actually produces a usable tool.
