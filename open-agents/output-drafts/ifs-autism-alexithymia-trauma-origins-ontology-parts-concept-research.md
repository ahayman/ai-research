# Concept Research: IFS Origins, Theoretical Claims, and the Ontology of Parts

## Overview

Internal Family Systems was developed by Richard C. Schwartz, a Purdue-trained family therapist, beginning in the early-to-mid 1980s while he was on staff at the Institute for Juvenile Research at the University of Illinois at Chicago. The model emerged from a clinical disappointment: Schwartz had set out to demonstrate that structural and strategic family therapy — the dominant systemic models he had been trained in — could resolve bulimia. It largely could not. Clients reported that even when family dynamics shifted, internal "parts" continued to drive their bingeing and purging. Schwartz, by his own account, started by treating these parts as metaphors. After encountering Sandra Watanabe's "Inner Cast of Characters" work and continuing his own clinical observations, he came to treat them as real.

The model fuses two ingredients: family-systems thinking (Bowen and Minuchin lineage) ported to the intra-psychic level, and a long-running tradition of "multiplicity-of-mind" claims with antecedents in Janet (dissociation), Jung (complexes and archetypes), Assagioli (psychosynthesis subpersonalities), Berne (Transactional Analysis ego states), Watkins (Ego State Therapy), Hilgard (neo-dissociation theory and the "hidden observer"), and — at the cognitive-science end — Minsky's *Society of Mind*. Schwartz claims the lineage; critics argue he name-drops more than he engages.

The load-bearing question for this category is not "is IFS effective?" (that lives in Cat 4) but "what is IFS actually claiming when it talks about parts?" There are at least three answers in circulation: parts are useful clinical metaphors; parts are phenomenological labels for neural networks or state-dependent affective circuits; parts are discrete subpersonalities with their own perspectives, memories, and intentions. Schwartz has personally moved across these positions, the IFS Institute often speaks as if (3), clinicians often work as if (1) or (2), and Robert Falconer's "unattached burdens" doctrine — endorsed by Schwartz — pushes ontology even further than (3) toward something transpersonal. None of these positions has been adjudicated empirically; the central critique aimed at IFS is that the model is not structured in a way that *could* be adjudicated. Negative findings are reframed as "Self being blended with a part," which is unfalsifiable in the strict Popperian sense.

This page steel-mans what Schwartz and the model are actually claiming, then identifies precisely where the claims outrun the evidence. It does not relitigate the clinical effectiveness question (Cat 4) nor the commercial structure (Cat 5) nor the spiritual extensions (Cat 11), though it forward-references each.

## Core Components

### Component 1: The Origin Story

- **Purpose**: Establish what actually happened in Schwartz's clinical work in the early 1980s and what was published vs. observed in-house.
- **Inputs**: Schwartz's family-therapy training at Purdue (PhD in Marriage and Family Therapy); appointment at the Institute for Juvenile Research (IJR), University of Illinois at Chicago; a cohort of approximately 30 bulimic adolescents and their families, recruited with colleague Mary Jo Barrett in the early 1980s.
- **Outputs**: The clinical observation that family-therapy reorganization alone failed to extinguish symptoms; client reports of "parts" with autonomous voices; Schwartz's earliest publications on bulimia and family therapy (e.g., "Bulimia and Family Therapy: A Case Study," *International Journal of Eating Disorders*, 1983); the seminal *Internal Family Systems Therapy* text (Schwartz 1995, Guilford).
- **Relationships**: This origin sits upstream of every other IFS claim. The "1982 bulimia outcome study" frequently cited in marketing copy refers to the *clinical observation phase* of Schwartz and Barrett's work in the early 1980s — not, so far as the published record shows, a controlled outcome trial published in 1982. The IFS Institute's own "Evolution of IFS" article describes the period in narrative terms; it does not cite a 1982 outcome paper. Schwartz's first peer-reviewed bulimia paper appears to be the 1983 *International Journal of Eating Disorders* case study.

### Component 2: The Family-Systems Inheritance

- **Purpose**: Locate where IFS's *systems* half came from — the part of the model that gave it the word "Family" in the name.
- **Inputs**: Murray Bowen's family-systems theory (differentiation of self, multigenerational transmission); Salvador Minuchin's structural family therapy (boundaries, alliances, hierarchy, executive subsystem); and to a lesser extent Jay Haley's strategic family therapy and Virginia Satir's experiential family therapy ("Parts Party" technique).
- **Outputs**: The "internal family" metaphor itself; the structural distinction between protectors and exiles (mirroring parental and child subsystems); the idea that a "leader" (the Self) is needed to coordinate the system without being subsumed by it.
- **Relationships**: This is the half of IFS where Schwartz had genuine, deep training. Critics (and Schwartz himself in interviews) agree that the move he made was importing systems thinking from family-of-origin work into the intra-psychic. It is also the source of one of the model's strongest claims: that *relationships between parts* matter as much as the parts themselves.

### Component 3: The Multiplicity-of-Mind Lineage

- **Purpose**: Locate where IFS's *parts* half came from — the long pre-history of "the mind is many" claims that IFS now sits inside.
- **Inputs**:
  - **Pierre Janet (late 19th c.)**: dissociation defined as a lack of integration among multiple "systems of ideas and functions that constitute personality." The first systematic clinical theory of psychic multiplicity.
  - **Carl Jung (early-to-mid 20th c.)**: complexes ("psychic fragments… consisting of a core connected with one or more archetypes around which cluster ideas and images") that Jung explicitly described as acting "independently and autonomously"; archetypes as universal substrate.
  - **Roberto Assagioli (mid 20th c.)**: psychosynthesis, in which the personality is "a multiplicity of subpersonalities often in conflict with each other," organized around the "I" — a "point or center of pure self-awareness… the direct reflection or projection of the Self." This is the closest historical antecedent to IFS's Self-and-parts architecture.
  - **Eric Berne (1950s–60s)**: Transactional Analysis ego states (Parent, Adult, Child) — three ego states a person can shift among. Less granular than IFS but established the clinical move of speaking *with* internal states.
  - **John & Helen Watkins (1970s–90s)**: Ego State Therapy, built explicitly on Federn's ego psychology and Janet's dissociation theory; works hypnotically to access and dialogue with discriminable ego states. IFS shares more procedural DNA with Watkins than is typically acknowledged.
  - **Ernest Hilgard (1970s)**: neo-dissociation theory and the "hidden observer" — the empirical demonstration that a hypnotized subject can have a separate "observer" capable of reporting pain that the dissociated stream is not consciously experiencing. Provides experimental scaffolding for dissociated streams of consciousness.
  - **Marvin Minsky (1980s)**: *Society of Mind* (1986), which frames human cognition as a vast society of simple "agents" that combine into "agencies"; consciousness and self emerge from coordination among these agents rather than from any central executive.
- **Outputs**: A multi-tradition justification for the multiplicity-of-mind thesis IFS depends on. None of these authors built IFS, but each makes Schwartz's claim ("the mind is naturally multiple") less radical than it sounds in a Western unitary-self context.
- **Relationships**: Schwartz himself names Freud's id/ego/superego, object relations, Berne's TA, Assagioli's psychosynthesis, and CBT schemata as antecedents in his "Evolution of IFS" article. He typically does *not* foreground Janet or Watkins, despite their being the closest procedural ancestors. Critics charge that the lineage is *asserted* but not *theorized* — IFS uses the language of these traditions without seriously engaging their conceptual structures. This is a fair critique. The depth of theoretical engagement varies; the depth of citation is broadly accurate.

### Component 4: The Multiplicity-of-Mind Thesis

- **Purpose**: State precisely what IFS is committing to when it says the mind is naturally multiple.
- **Inputs**: The clinical observation (across multiple traditions) that people speak of internal "voices," "sides," "selves," and "parts"; cognitive science findings on dual-process theory (Type 1 and Type 2 reasoning); modular-mind theories (Fodor and successors); contemporary research on multiplicity that explicitly distinguishes non-pathological from pathological forms.
- **Outputs**: The IFS claim has two parts:
  1. **Multiplicity is normal** — having parts is not pathology; everyone has them.
  2. **Pathology is structural** — what makes parts dysfunctional is the *roles* they have been forced into and the *burdens* they carry, not their existence.
- **Relationships**: This is genuinely well-supported as a *non-pathological* framing — multiplicity research, dissociation research, and dual-process theory all support the descriptive claim that minds are not unitary. Where it overreaches: the leap from "minds are not unitary" to "minds contain discriminable subpersonalities with their own histories, intentions, and developmental arcs" is much larger than the supporting literature warrants. Society of Mind, for instance, posits *agents* that are *mindless* individually — not subpersonalities. Dual-process theory posits *processing modes*, not characters.

### Component 5: The Three Ontology Positions

- **Purpose**: Lay out, cleanly, what people actually mean when they talk about "parts."
- **Inputs**: Schwartz's writing (in which he has publicly held all three positions across different contexts), the IFS Institute's marketing materials, peer-reviewed psychotherapy literature, philosophy-of-psychology commentary, and clinician practice patterns.
- **Outputs**:
  - **Position 1 — Metaphor / Heuristic**: Parts are useful clinical fictions. They are a way to organize phenomenological experience that helps clients separate "I am angry" from "a part of me is angry." Proponents who function in this register tend to use part-language without ontological commitment. Schwartz's earliest period (early 1980s) sat here.
  - **Position 2 — Neural Networks / Dissociated Affective States**: Parts are phenomenological labels for state-dependent affective and cognitive networks — broadly compatible with structural-dissociation theory (Van der Hart, Nijenhuis, Steele) and with neuroscience of state-dependent memory. The Wikipedia article on IFS explicitly notes that "other proponents describe these terms as phenomenological metaphors for neural networks or dissociated affective states." This is the position most compatible with mainstream cognitive neuroscience.
  - **Position 3 — Discrete Subpersonalities**: Parts are real, discriminable mental entities with their own perspectives, memories, intentions, and developmental histories. This is what IFS Institute marketing typically describes. Schwartz has stated: "All of us are born with many sub-minds — or parts. These parts are not imaginary or symbolic." He has also said, more cautiously: "I am simply reporting what I've learned over years of exploring this inner territory without judgment regarding the ontological reality of that data." Both quotes are real; they live in tension.
  - **Position 4 (extension) — Transpersonal / Discarnate**: Falconer's *The Others Within Us* (2023, foreword by Schwartz) introduces "unattached burdens" — energies that are neither part of the client nor generationally inherited but are described as parasitic entities, tricksters, egregores, or thought forms. This is covered in Cat 11; flagged here because it represents the strongest possible ontology claim: parts as literal entities, some of them external in origin.
- **Relationships**: A given practitioner, book, or training session can sit anywhere on this spectrum. The model itself does not require a single answer; in practice, clinicians often work as if (1) or (2) while the Institute markets as if (3), and Schwartz/Falconer publicly endorse (4). For a systems-thinker reader, the question is whether this multi-stability is a strength (the model is robust to whatever ontology is true) or a weakness (the model is unfalsifiable because it cannot be pinned down).

### Component 6: The Falsifiability Problem

- **Purpose**: Identify where IFS's claims become unfalsifiable in the Popperian sense — and what consequences that has.
- **Inputs**: Karl Popper's falsifiability criterion; the *Society for the Advancement of Psychotherapy* 2024 "Problematic Popularity" essay; the Wikipedia article on the IFS model; the philosophy-of-psychotherapy literature on what counts as testable.
- **Outputs**: Two distinct problems.
  1. **The discriminant validity problem**: There is no published instrument that operationalizes "a part" in a way that lets researchers confirm or disconfirm whether two reported parts are distinct entities or one phenomenon being described in two ways. The Society for the Advancement of Psychotherapy critique states that the model "presents unique challenges for falsifiability… empirically substantiating — or refuting — the existence of the hypothesized, distinct, subpersonalities presents difficulties." There is no neuroimaging finding that has identified discrete subpersonalities as the model describes them. Proxy findings exist (state-dependent activation; default-mode-network shifts) but none cleanly support discrete *subpersonalities*.
  2. **The Self-blending escape hatch**: When a client cannot access "Self," IFS reframes this as "a part is in the way" or "Self is blended with a part." This makes negative results unfalsifiable — there is no possible failure of the model that cannot be reattributed to interference from another part. A theory that cannot fail is a theory that cannot be tested.
- **Relationships**: This is the central epistemic critique. It does not entail that IFS is *wrong* — many useful clinical heuristics are unfalsifiable (think "rapport" or "therapeutic alliance"). It does entail that "evidence-based" claims about IFS are claims about *outcomes* (whether clients improve), not claims about *mechanism* (whether the model's account of internal architecture is correct). For a systems thinker who insists on being able to specify what would falsify a claim, this distinction matters.

### Component 7: Schwartz's Position Drift

- **Purpose**: Describe how Schwartz himself has moved across ontology positions over forty years, and why this matters.
- **Inputs**: The Mad in America 2022 interview, the IFS Institute's "Evolution of IFS" article, Schwartz's *No Bad Parts* (2021), Schwartz's foreword to Falconer's *The Others Within Us* (2023), and the Sounds True course *The Spirituality of IFS*.
- **Outputs**: A rough trajectory.
  - **Early 1980s (Position 1)**: "I started to work with parts but assumed that they were just metaphors for different thoughts and impulses." (Mad in America interview, paraphrased)
  - **Late 1980s onward (Position 3)**: After Sandra Watanabe convinced him parts were "very real," Schwartz "was totally convinced of the reality of parts — and has been ever since."
  - **2021 (*No Bad Parts*) (Position 3+)**: "These parts are not imaginary or symbolic." But also: "I am simply reporting what I've learned… without judgment regarding the ontological reality of that data." Reads as Position 3 with a Position 1 escape hatch.
  - **2023 onward (Position 4)**: Foreword to Falconer endorsing unattached burdens; *Spirituality of IFS* course affirming spiritual phenomena "that cannot be explained by conventional science."
- **Relationships**: For a reader trying to evaluate IFS, the founder's ontology drift matters because (a) it means there is no canonical answer to what "parts" are; (b) it means a practitioner trained in any given decade may operate from a different ontology than one trained later; (c) it means "what Schwartz says today" is not always continuous with "what Schwartz said in 1995." Cat 5 examines what this drift looks like as a commercial and doctrinal phenomenon; here it is an epistemic phenomenon.

## How It Works (the historical-conceptual chain)

### Step 1: Schwartz trains as a structural/strategic family therapist (late 1970s)
- **Trigger**: PhD program at Purdue in Marriage and Family Therapy; immersion in Bowen, Minuchin, Haley.
- **Action**: Schwartz internalizes the systems-therapy worldview — symptoms are products of family system dynamics; reorganize the family, resolve the symptom.
- **Result**: Schwartz arrives at the Institute for Juvenile Research expecting to demonstrate this with a difficult population.

### Step 2: The bulimia outcome work (early 1980s)
- **Trigger**: Schwartz and Mary Jo Barrett recruit ~30 bulimic adolescents and their families at IJR.
- **Action**: They apply structural and strategic family interventions, expecting symptomatic remission. They observe persistent bingeing and purging in clients whose family dynamics had visibly reorganized.
- **Result**: Schwartz asks clients *why* and hears repeatedly about internal "parts" with their own voices, agendas, and behavior. The "1982 bulimia study" of marketing copy refers to this clinical observation phase, not to a published controlled outcome trial. The first peer-reviewed publication appears to be Schwartz's 1983 *International Journal of Eating Disorders* case study.

### Step 3: Schwartz starts speaking *to* parts (mid 1980s)
- **Trigger**: Curiosity about the autonomous-feeling internal voices clients describe.
- **Action**: Schwartz experiments with addressing parts directly — asking them what they want, fear, protect against. Treating parts conversationally, as one would treat family members in structural family therapy.
- **Result**: Clinical traction in cases where pure family work had stalled. Schwartz still treats parts as metaphors at this stage.

### Step 4: The shift to ontological commitment (late 1980s)
- **Trigger**: Encountering Sandra Watanabe's "Inner Cast of Characters" workshop and her insistence that parts are "very real." Continuing clinical observation.
- **Action**: Schwartz shifts from Position 1 (metaphor) to Position 3 (discrete subpersonalities).
- **Result**: The 1995 *Internal Family Systems Therapy* (Guilford) presents parts and Self as the canonical IFS architecture. By this point Schwartz speaks of parts as having their own perspectives and intentions.

### Step 5: Institutionalization and ontological drift (2000s–present)
- **Trigger**: Founding of the Center for Self Leadership (later IFS Institute) in 2000; *No Bad Parts* publication (2021); Falconer collaboration; Sounds True spirituality course.
- **Action**: The IFS Institute crystallizes Position 3 in marketing materials. Schwartz personally moves toward Position 4 (transpersonal/discarnate) via the unattached-burdens doctrine.
- **Result**: A model whose canonical claims sit at Position 3, whose clinical practice often functions at Position 1 or 2, and whose founder is publicly leaning toward Position 4. The ontology question becomes structurally underdetermined within the IFS Institute itself — which is the precondition for the falsifiability critique to land.

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: The Bulimia Origin
- **Scenario**: A 17-year-old client at IJR in 1982. Schwartz reorganizes the family per Minuchin's structural model — the parents become more aligned, the client gets clearer boundaries with siblings. Family dynamics measurably shift. The client continues to binge and purge.
- **Illustration**: When Schwartz asks why, the client says something like "there's a part of me that has to do this — I can't make it stop." Schwartz initially treats this as metaphor; the symptom's persistence forces him to take it more literally.
- **Key Insight**: This is the empirical-clinical fact at the bottom of IFS. It is not a controlled study; it is a clinical observation that the field interpreted as a paradigm-suggesting anomaly.

### Example 2: The Sandra Watanabe Pivot
- **Scenario**: Schwartz attends a workshop on the "Inner Cast of Characters" in the late 1980s. He asks the presenter whether she really thinks these inner figures are real. She responds, by Schwartz's account, "No, they're very real."
- **Illustration**: Schwartz is described as moving over the following years from Position 1 (metaphor) to Position 3 (discrete subpersonalities).
- **Key Insight**: The shift is described as experiential and clinical — clients began to track in ways that, for Schwartz, made the metaphor framing untenable. Critics note that the same clinical phenomenology is consistent with Position 1 or Position 2 and does not entail Position 3. The "felt reality" of an entity is not evidence of its discrete existence as an entity.

### Example 3: The Self-Blending Escape
- **Scenario**: A client cannot access "Self." They report feeling flat, numb, dissociated, hostile, or terrified — none of the 8 Cs are present.
- **Illustration**: An IFS therapist, working from canonical training, will say "a part is in the way" or "let's see if we can ask the part to step back so Self can come forward." If Self does not appear, the therapist will identify yet another part interfering.
- **Key Insight**: This move is clinically useful — it gives the therapist a structured way to keep working when initial accessing fails. It is also unfalsifiable. There is no observation that would force the conclusion "this client does not have Self," because absence is always reattributed to interference. The clinical utility and the falsifiability problem are the same move seen from two angles.

### Example 4: Minsky as Conceptual Cover
- **Scenario**: An IFS-friendly writer cites Minsky's *Society of Mind* as scientific support for IFS's multiplicity-of-mind claim.
- **Illustration**: Minsky's actual claim is that the mind is built up from many *mindless* simple agents that, in combination, produce intelligence. Minsky's *agents* are not subpersonalities. They have no perspective, no intentions, no memories of their own.
- **Key Insight**: The lineage citation is rhetorically effective but structurally misleading. Minsky's view is closer to Position 2 (subroutines / networks) than Position 3 (subpersonalities). Using Society of Mind as cover for Position 3 mischaracterizes it.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: IFS comes from Jungian psychology.
  - **Reality**: Schwartz cites Jung as one of several influences but his actual training was structural and strategic family therapy. The closest direct antecedent for the Self-and-parts architecture is Assagioli's psychosynthesis. IFS is not Jungian therapy with new vocabulary; it is family-systems thinking ported inward, dressed in vocabulary borrowed from several depth-psychology traditions.

- **Misconception**: There was a published 1982 bulimia outcome study that founded IFS.
  - **Reality**: The "1982 bulimia outcome study" appears to refer to the in-house clinical observation phase of Schwartz and Barrett's work at IJR, not to a controlled outcome trial published in 1982. Schwartz's first peer-reviewed bulimia paper appears to be the 1983 *International Journal of Eating Disorders* case study; his foundational textbook is the 1995 *Internal Family Systems Therapy* (Guilford). Saying "the 1982 outcome study showed IFS works" overstates what was published.

- **Misconception**: Multiplicity of mind is a fringe claim.
  - **Reality**: Non-pathological multiplicity is supported by dissociation research, dual-process theory, modular-mind theory, and clinical-phenomenology studies. The descriptive claim that minds are not unitary is mainstream. The contested move is from "minds are not unitary" to "minds contain discrete subpersonalities with their own perspectives and histories" — that leap is not supported by the same literature.

- **Misconception**: IFS's "Self" is a metaphysical claim about a soul.
  - **Reality**: Some practitioners frame Self in mainstream terms (a state of integrated prefrontal-cortex function with affective regulation; Daniel Siegel-style "neurological integration"). Others, including increasingly Schwartz himself, frame Self in spiritual/transpersonal terms. The model permits both. Cat 11 covers the spiritual extension; Cat 4 covers the neuroscience adjacency.

- **Misconception**: If parts cannot be neuroimaged, the model is useless.
  - **Reality**: Lots of useful clinical heuristics are not directly imageable (the working alliance; transference; rumination as a process). What the falsifiability critique actually establishes is narrower: claims about *mechanism* (parts as discrete subpersonalities) cannot currently be tested, but claims about *outcomes* (people improve in IFS) can be and have been (modestly) tested.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **Aphantasia (no inner imagery)**: A client with no visual mental imagery cannot "see" a part. Procedurally this matters more for Cat 7 (autism) than for ontology, but it produces an interesting test case: such a client experiences parts only as voices, sensations, or thoughts. If parts are real entities, they should still be discriminable; if parts are visual metaphors only, they should be inaccessible. In practice, aphantasic clients can do parts-work via writing and dialogue without the visual layer, which is consistent with Position 2 more cleanly than with Position 3 framed visually.
- **Severe alexithymia**: A client with no felt sense of internal states cannot "feel toward" a part. This is the user's own profile and is the load-bearing concern of Cat 6. For the ontology question here, alexithymia is an edge case where the clinical phenomenology that produced Schwartz's Position 3 conviction does not occur — and yet the model claims to work. If it does work in this population (which is undertested), it works for reasons compatible with Position 1 or 2, not Position 3.
- **Dissociative Identity Disorder**: Clients with DID present discriminable alters with their own names, ages, and memories. This is the closest empirical phenomenon to literal subpersonalities, and structural-dissociation theory provides the framework for understanding it. IFS Institute material sometimes implies that DID is just IFS at the extreme; structural-dissociation researchers push back, arguing that the clinical phenomena and the conceptual frameworks do not map cleanly. Cat 8 covers this disagreement.
- **Religious/cultural multiplicity frameworks**: Many traditions (e.g., Hindu thought, Jungian shadow work, indigenous spirit-traditions) have multi-figure models of the inner life. Schwartz's Position 4 (with Falconer) connects IFS to these traditions explicitly. For a Western-secular reader, this can read as a category drift; for a reader from a tradition with established multiplicity frameworks, it can read as overdue acknowledgment. Cat 11 is the place where this is examined fully.

## Related Concepts

- **Structural dissociation theory (Van der Hart, Nijenhuis, Steele)**: Posits ANP (Apparently Normal Part) and EP (Emotional Part) as discriminable post-trauma, organized at primary/secondary/tertiary levels of complexity. Procedurally adjacent to IFS exiles; ontologically more constrained — claims are about post-trauma fragmentation, not normative multiplicity. Cat 8.
- **Active imagination (Jung)**: The deliberate engagement with autonomous inner figures via dialogue and image. Procedurally close to IFS direct access. Treated by Jung as a method for engaging with archetypal content.
- **Empty chair / Parts Party (Gestalt, Satir)**: Procedural ancestors for IFS's externalization moves. Clients are asked to embody parts and dialogue between them.
- **Default mode network and self-referential processing (cognitive neuroscience)**: A live area of research into the neural correlates of self-experience. Some IFS-friendly writers frame Self as a DMN-related state; this is suggestive but not yet empirical confirmation of Self as the model describes it.
- **Schemata (CBT) and modes (Schema Therapy, Young)**: The CBT lineage's version of multiplicity. Schwartz cites schemata as antecedents. Schema Therapy's "modes" are the closest CBT analog to IFS parts and have a more developed empirical base.
- **Polyvagal theory (Porges)**: Sometimes invoked alongside IFS to frame dorsal-vagal shutdown as a "firefighter" response. The pairing is rhetorically convenient; polyvagal theory itself has been substantially critiqued (Grossman et al.) and the marriage to IFS does not strengthen IFS's empirical foundations.

## Research Gaps

- **No published 1982 outcome study located.** The "1982 bulimia outcome study" appears to be a marketing-copy abbreviation for the early-1980s clinical observation phase. The earliest peer-reviewed Schwartz publication on bulimia I could locate is the 1983 *International Journal of Eating Disorders* case study. If a 1982 publication exists, it may be in an obscure journal not surfaced by general search; downstream agents should treat the "1982 outcome study" framing as a clinical-observation reference unless a specific 1982 publication is verified.
- **Direct primary-source verification of Schwartz's "Schwartz personally drifted across ontology positions" claim** rests on synthesizing his Mad in America 2022 interview, *No Bad Parts* 2021, and his foreword to Falconer 2023. The pattern is clear in aggregate; no single source explicitly traces the drift. This is a synthesized observation; downstream content should frame it as such.
- **Empirical literature on discrete subpersonalities specifically (Position 3) is essentially absent.** Discriminant-validity instruments do not exist in the published literature. Negative findings are rare because the question is rarely posed in falsifiable form.
- **Minsky's actual position is often mischaracterized in IFS literature.** Push Singh's MIT examination of the *Society of Mind* makes clear that Minsky's agents are mindless individually and that the multiplicity is computational, not characterological. Downstream content should not let the Minsky citation do more work than it can.
- **The relationship between IFS and structural dissociation theory** is contested but underdiscussed in IFS-Institute material. Cat 8 will return to this; here it is flagged as a Position-2 ally that IFS canonical material does not lean on as much as it could.
