# Concept Research: Measurement of Alexithymia

## Why This Category Matters

Almost everything downstream — therapy choice, self-classification, evaluation of harm risk, comorbidity inference — runs through one operational decision: **which instrument was used to label the person "alexithymic"?** The same individual can be classified differently by TAS-20, BVAQ, PAQ, TSIA, and LEAS, because each instrument measures a partially different construct. Treatment-matching claims that ignore this are operating on a borrowed certainty.

For the user's stated goal — picking instruments to triangulate self-classification — this category is a navigation guide, not a diagnostic checklist. The aim is to make the user able to read a study or therapy claim and say: "this used X, which captures the cognitive facets but not the affective ones," or "this used a self-report on introspection, which has a known internal limit," and then act accordingly.

## The Dominant Self-Report: TAS-20

### Origins and Structure

The Toronto Alexithymia Scale, 20-item version, was developed by Bagby, Parker, and Taylor and published as a pair of papers in 1994 in the *Journal of Psychosomatic Research* (Bagby, Parker, & Taylor 1994; Bagby, Taylor, & Parker 1994). It was designed to operationalise the construct that Sifneos and Nemiah described clinically in the 1970s. The 20 items use a 5-point Likert scale; total scores range 20–100. Higher = more alexithymic.

Three factors:

- **DIF (Difficulty Identifying Feelings)** — "I am often confused about what emotion I am feeling." 7 items.
- **DDF (Difficulty Describing Feelings)** — "It is difficult for me to find the right words for my feelings." 5 items.
- **EOT (Externally-Oriented Thinking)** — "I prefer to talk to people about their daily activities rather than their feelings." 8 items.

### Replication of the Factor Structure

The three-factor structure is the single most replicated psychometric finding in alexithymia. The most rigorous synthesis is Schroeders, Kubera, and Gnambs (2022), *Assessment*, [PMC9597132](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9597132/). This is a meta-analytic confirmatory factor analysis: 28 studies, N > 17,000, across 19 languages and clinical and community samples. The three-factor solution fit reasonably well across populations; bifactor models with a general alexithymia factor plus three specific factors fit slightly better, suggesting a hierarchical structure (general alexithymia + DIF/DDF/EOT subdomains).

### Strengths

- **Most validated and most cited.** Decades of cross-cultural data, multiple language versions, normative data in clinical populations.
- **Brief.** 20 items, ~3 minutes to complete.
- **Free for non-commercial research use** — the scale is widely circulated; commercial use requires permission from MHS Inc., which holds publishing rights.
- **Embedded in the literature.** If the goal is comparing oneself to published study populations, TAS-20 is what those populations were measured with.

### Weaknesses

- **EOT factor instability.** The DIF and DDF factors replicate consistently and have good internal consistency (typically α ~.78–.85). The EOT factor is more fragile: lower internal consistency (α often .55–.70), some items reverse-scored, and the factor sometimes splits or fails to emerge in cross-cultural samples (especially in some East Asian samples where an additional cultural-loading effect is suspected).
- **Negative emotions only.** The TAS-20 items refer almost exclusively to negative or neutral affective states. Positive emotion is essentially absent. A person who can identify and describe joy but not sadness is treated identically by the scale to a person impaired across all valences.
- **Loss of the Sifneos fantasy criterion.** The original 1970s clinical description included impoverished fantasy/imagination as a fourth feature; the TAS-20 dropped it because the relevant items did not cluster in factor analyses. The construct narrowed accordingly.
- **The self-report-on-introspection paradox** (see below).
- **Cutoff debates.** Most published research uses ≥61 = "alexithymic," 52–60 = "borderline/possible alexithymic," ≤51 = "non-alexithymic" — Bagby's original cutoffs. Some authors argue for category-free dimensional use; others use ≥51 as the "any-alexithymia" threshold. Cutoff choice changes prevalence estimates substantially.

### The Self-Report-on-Introspection Paradox

This is the deepest critique of the TAS-20 (and of any self-report on emotion-introspection). Lundh (2001), [PubMed 11593459](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11593459/), formulated the problem clearly: *self-report on introspective ability requires the very introspective ability whose absence is being measured.* If a respondent cannot tell whether they are anxious, hungry, or angry, they cannot reliably self-rate "I am often confused about what emotion I am feeling." They may underrate (because they don't notice the confusion) or overrate (because they confuse other states with confusion). Lane et al. and other authors have replicated this concern empirically — TAS-20 scores correlate only weakly with performance-based emotion-awareness tasks like LEAS, suggesting the two methods tap related but partially independent constructs.

The TAS-20 manual acknowledges this and treats it pragmatically — a self-report measure with known limits is better than nothing, and the scale's construct validity is supported by external criterion correlations (alexithymia predicts outcomes in expected directions). But for users trying to self-classify, the paradox means: TAS-20 alone is not a complete answer.

### Cross-Cultural Findings

East Asian samples consistently produce higher mean TAS-20 scores than Western samples (Ryder et al. 2018 and replications). The interpretation is contested: it could reflect higher true prevalence, cultural differences in emotional expression norms (collectivist restraint, Confucian emotional moderation), or measurement-equivalence problems (e.g., the EOT items being read differently in different cultures). The deep dive Categories 5 and 11 treat this in more depth.

## BVAQ — Bermond-Vorst Alexithymia Questionnaire

### Origins and Structure

Vorst and Bermond (2001), *Personality and Individual Differences* 30(3):413–434, [doi 10.1016/S0191-8869(00)00033-7](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0191886900000337). The instrument extends the alexithymia construct to include the affective/imaginative dimensions that Sifneos originally specified and that the TAS-20 dropped.

Five factors, 40 items (a 20-item BVAQ-B short version exists):

- **Verbalising** (cognitive) — putting feelings into words.
- **Identifying** (cognitive) — knowing what one feels.
- **Analysing** (cognitive) — understanding the causes of feelings.
- **Fantasising** (affective) — engagement in imaginative life.
- **Emotionalising** (affective) — strength of emotional arousal.

The first three factors are "cognitive alexithymia" (overlapping with the TAS-20 territory). The last two are "affective alexithymia" (broadly absent from the TAS-20).

### Strengths

- **Captures the affective dimension TAS-20 misses.** A respondent with intact arousal and fantasy but impaired identification will be flagged as cognitively alexithymic on BVAQ but possibly normal on TAS-20 — and the BVAQ result better matches Bermond's Type II.
- **Distinguishes Type I from Type II.** Type I = low on both cognitive and affective dimensions. Type II = low cognitive but normal/high affective (feelings present but illegible). The BVAQ's two-axis design is what allows this distinction.
- **Useful for psychosis-risk research.** Cognitive alexithymia (BVAQ identifying/verbalising/analysing) has been linked specifically to psychosis vulnerability (van der Velde et al. 2015, [PMC4451258](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4451258/)); affective alexithymia has not. Distinguishing them matters.

### Weaknesses

- **Less cross-cultural data than TAS-20.** The norm base is primarily Dutch-language and German-language; English and other-language adaptations exist but with smaller validation samples.
- **The Type I / Type II categorical split is weakly supported.** Empirical work by the Preece group and others suggests the underlying cognitive and affective dimensions are continuous; the categorical Type I / Type II distinction does not robustly emerge in latent-class or factor mixture analyses (Schaeuffele et al. 2021, [PubMed 33667948](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33667948/)). The BVAQ's *dimensional* output (cognitive score × affective score) is more defensible than the *categorical* Type I/II classification.
- **Same self-report-on-introspection paradox** as the TAS-20.
- **Frontiers 2018 review** ([PMC5925324](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5925324/)) — the BVAQ has good factor structure replication in some samples, less consistent in others; the affective factors are sometimes less reliable than the cognitive ones.

### Use Case

When the user wants to know whether their alexithymia is *cognitive only* (Type II profile) or *cognitive and affective combined* (Type I profile). For self-classification triangulation, BVAQ pairs naturally with TAS-20 — TAS-20 anchors the user against the published literature; BVAQ adds the affective dimension TAS-20 omits.

## PAQ — Perth Alexithymia Questionnaire

### Origins and Structure

The newest of the major instruments. Preece, Becerra, Robinson, Dandy, and Allan (2018), [research-repository.uwa.edu.au/.../the-psychometric-assessment-of-alexithymia-development-and-valida](https://research-repository.uwa.edu.au/en/publications/the-psychometric-assessment-of-alexithymia-development-and-valida). Developed at the University of Western Australia. The PAQ is theoretically grounded in the *attention-appraisal model of alexithymia* (Preece et al. 2017).

24-item full version. Five facets crossed across two valences:

- **Negative-Difficulty Identifying Feelings (N-DIF)**
- **Positive-Difficulty Identifying Feelings (P-DIF)**
- **Negative-Difficulty Describing Feelings (N-DDF)**
- **Positive-Difficulty Describing Feelings (P-DDF)**
- **General-Externally-Oriented Thinking (G-EOT)**

The valence split is the structural innovation. The PAQ is the only major self-report that explicitly measures alexithymia for *positive* emotions separately from negative ones.

A brief 6-item PAQ-S exists for screening.

### Strengths

- **Theoretically grounded.** The attention-appraisal model frames alexithymia as a failure at the attention or appraisal stage of emotion regulation. PAQ items map onto this model directly, making PAQ scores theoretically interpretable rather than just empirically descriptive.
- **Both valences.** A respondent who can identify negative emotions but not positive ones is captured. This matters because some clinical presentations (post-trauma, depressive, autism) involve different patterns across valence.
- **Good psychometric properties.** Five-factor structure replicates; internal consistency is generally α > .80 across factors.
- **Available in multiple languages** with growing validation literature.

### Weaknesses

- **Less cross-cultural data than TAS-20.** As the newest instrument, the PAQ has not yet accumulated decades of normative data across populations.
- **Cutoffs less standardised.** Recommended cutoffs exist but the literature has not converged in the way TAS-20's ≥61 has.
- **Same self-report-on-introspection paradox.**
- **Construct overlap with TAS-20.** The negative-valence DIF and DDF facets overlap heavily with TAS-20; the unique value of the PAQ comes from the positive-valence facets and the EOT measure.

### Use Case

When valence-specific patterns matter — e.g., the user suspects different impairment for positive vs. negative emotions, or wants the attention-appraisal-model framing as scaffolding for therapy decisions. PAQ + TAS-20 is a defensible pairing for users wanting both established benchmarks and broader emotional coverage.

## TSIA — Toronto Structured Interview for Alexithymia

### Origins and Structure

Bagby, Taylor, Parker, and Dickens (2006), [PubMed 16361872](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16361872/). An *observer-rated semi-structured interview*, not a self-report. The interviewer asks open-ended questions and rates the respondent's answers on the same four-factor structure as the original Sifneos description: Difficulty Identifying Feelings, Difficulty Describing Feelings, Externally-Oriented Thinking, and Imaginal Processes (the original fourth Sifneos feature, restored here).

24 items rated by the interviewer on a 0–2 scale. Total scores reflect the rater's judgment of the respondent's emotional capacities, informed by their actual answers and emotional engagement during the interview.

### Strengths

- **Less affected by self-report bias.** A respondent who underrates their own confusion, or who lacks the introspective access needed to self-report accurately, is rated based on observable performance during the interview rather than on self-perception.
- **Clinically rich.** The interview surfaces qualitative material that scales miss — the way a person describes (or fails to describe) an emotional event tells the rater things that a Likert response cannot.
- **Restores the imaginal-processes facet** that the TAS-20 lost. The TSIA is the closest contemporary instrument to the original Sifneos four-feature description.
- **Good convergent validity** with the TAS-20 (typically r = .50–.70) and incremental validity over TAS-20 in predicting external criteria.

### Weaknesses

- **Burden.** Requires a trained interviewer (the manual specifies clinician training; reliability requires practice). Takes 30–45 minutes plus rating time.
- **Limited self-administration.** Cannot be done from home as a self-test.
- **Inter-rater reliability requires calibration.** Without proper training, two raters can produce divergent scores.
- **Less cross-cultural validation** than TAS-20.
- **Not freely available** — administration manual and training materials are typically purchased.

### Use Case

Research and high-stakes clinical assessment. For most users self-classifying, TSIA is impractical. For users working with a therapist who can administer it (or the equivalent unstructured clinical interview informed by TSIA's domains), it is the gold standard for cutting through self-report limits.

## LEAS — Levels of Emotional Awareness Scale

### Origins and Structure

Lane, Quinlan, Schwartz, Walker, and Zeitlin (1990), *Journal of Personality Assessment* 55(1–2):124–134. A *performance-based* measure (not self-report). The respondent reads 20 short interpersonal vignettes ("You and your best friend are in the same line of work. There is a prize given annually to the best performer. The two of you work hard to win the prize…") and writes free-response answers describing what *they* would feel and what the *other person* in the vignette would feel. Trained coders rate each response on a 0–5 developmental scale modelled on Piaget and Werner:

- **Level 0:** No emotion words (cognitive content only).
- **Level 1:** Bodily sensation words ("I would feel hot").
- **Level 2:** Action words ("I would want to leave").
- **Level 3:** Single, undifferentiated emotion words ("I would feel bad").
- **Level 4:** Complex/blended emotions ("I would feel sad and proud at the same time").
- **Level 5:** The respondent shows understanding of complex blends in *both* themselves *and* the other person.

### Strengths

- **Performance-based — bypasses the self-report paradox.** The respondent doesn't rate their own emotional awareness; they demonstrate it. The coder rates the actual emotional language they produce.
- **Captures developmental complexity.** A respondent with a binary "good/bad" emotion vocabulary is rated differently from one with a richly differentiated vocabulary.
- **Maps onto theoretical models** that frame alexithymia as impoverished emotion-concept granularity (Barrett's theory of constructed emotion; Lane's developmental account).

### Weaknesses

- **Correlates only weakly with TAS-20** (typically r = .15–.30). The two instruments tap related but distinct constructs: TAS-20 measures self-perceived difficulty; LEAS measures the developmental complexity of emotion-language available to the respondent. A person can be high on one and low on the other. The 2018 meta-analytic synthesis ([Frontiers in Psychology](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2018.00453/full)) confirms this dissociation.
- **Coder burden.** Requires trained raters; coding is time-intensive (typically 30+ minutes per protocol).
- **Free-response format** is unfamiliar to many respondents and harder to score reliably than Likert items.
- **Limited norms for some clinical populations.**

### Use Case

When the question is whether the respondent's *emotion-concept system* is impoverished (low LEAS) vs. their *attention to feelings* is the issue (low TAS-20 with intact LEAS). The combination is diagnostically useful: high TAS-20 + low LEAS suggests a Barrett-style emotion-concept deficit; high TAS-20 + average LEAS suggests an attention-appraisal or interoceptive issue.

For users self-classifying, LEAS is hard to self-administer (needs a trained coder), but the LEAS-vignette material is publicly described in the literature, and a self-led version using the same vignettes plus a reflective rubric can be useful as a diagnostic exercise even without formal scoring.

## OAQ-G2 — Observer Alexithymia Questionnaire

### Origins and Structure

Haviland, Warren, and Riggs (2000), *Psychosomatics* 41(5):385–392. A 33-item observer-rated scale (recently revised to a 12-item short form, OAQ-G2). The respondent's intimate other (spouse, parent, close friend, clinician) rates the respondent on five facets: distant, uninsightful, somatising, humourless, and rigid.

### Strengths

- **Bypasses the self-report paradox entirely.** The rater is someone other than the alexithymic person.
- **Useful when self-report is suspect** — e.g., dissociative or actively-defended populations, or where the respondent has limited self-awareness.
- **Captures externally-observable features** like rigidity and humourlessness that self-report scales don't directly tap.

### Weaknesses

- **Less commonly used.** The published literature is much smaller than TAS-20, with fewer cross-cultural validations and norms.
- **Rater bias.** A spouse rating a partner's alexithymia is rating through their own perception and relationship dynamics; ratings can be confounded by relationship satisfaction.
- **Doesn't fully overlap with self-report constructs.** The OAQ measures the externally-observable phenotype, which is related to but not identical with the internal self-perceived construct measured by TAS-20.
- **Requires a willing rater** with substantial knowledge of the respondent.

### Use Case

When self-report is likely to fail (severe alexithymia, dissociation, defensive responding) and the respondent has an intimate other able and willing to rate them. As a triangulation tool alongside TAS-20, OAQ-G2 can flag discrepancies that diagnostic interviews can then explore.

## Beyond the Big Six — Other Instruments and Measures

Several smaller instruments address specific niches:

- **Alexithymia Provoked Response Questionnaire (APRQ)** — Krystal-style situational vignettes. Rarely used in contemporary research.
- **Modified Beth Israel Hospital Psychosomatic Questionnaire (BIQ)** — historical, largely superseded by TSIA.
- **Sifneos's original Schalling-Sifneos Personality Scale** — historical.
- **Online Emotional Awareness scale (eLEAS)** — a digital adaptation of LEAS designed for self-administration with computerised scoring; promising but less validated than the original.
- **MIDI / TAQ-26 / NMAS (Levant)** — domain-specific instruments. The Normative Male Alexithymia Scale (Levant et al.) measures the gender-socialisation-driven phenotype specifically.

These appear in the literature occasionally but are not the standard recommendations for users seeking self-classification.

## Interoception Measurement

Because the parsing-deficit framework (Category 2) hinges on the distinction between *failing to detect* interoceptive signals and *failing to interpret* signals one already detects, interoception measurement is the natural complement to alexithymia measurement. Pairing the two gives a richer picture of where the deficit sits.

### The Heartbeat-Counting Task (HCT) and Heartbeat-Detection (HCD)

Schandry's (1981) heartbeat-counting paradigm: the participant silently counts their heartbeats over short time windows (e.g., 25, 35, 45 seconds) without taking a pulse. Their estimate is compared to the actual count from ECG. Interoceptive accuracy = 1 - mean error.

The HCT has been the dominant behavioural interoception measure for four decades and is **methodologically problematic.**

- Zamariola et al. and others have shown that HCT performance is heavily confounded by knowledge of average heart rate, BMI, gender, time-estimation ability, age, anxiety, and depression. Without controlling for these, "interoceptive accuracy" partly reflects general knowledge and cognitive style.
- **Murphy, Brewer, Hobson, Catmur, Bird (2018b)**, [PubMed 29803614](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29803614/). N=287. Initial bivariate analyses showed no relationship between alexithymia and HCT — but after controlling for the confound battery, the predicted negative relationship emerged. Two findings: (i) the alexithymia-interoception association is real but small, (ii) HCT alone, without confound control, is uninformative.
- The Heartbeat-Detection Task (HCD; participants judge whether external tones are synchronous with their own heartbeats) is methodologically cleaner but harder to administer and has a smaller literature.
- The 2018 J Abnormal Psychology paper by **Nicholson, Williams, Grainger, Christensen, Calvo-Merino, Gaigg** ([PMC6089261](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6089261/)) — using HCT and Bayesian analysis — found *no association* between either alexithymia or autism and HCT performance, and the Bayesian analyses *supported* the null. This is an independent group's third-party challenge to the Bird/Murphy/Brewer position, *not* an internal walk-back.

The reading the field has converged on: HCT alone is too noisy to reliably detect alexithymia-interoception associations. Multidomain interoception measurement (cardiac, respiratory, gastric, muscular) is more informative.

### MAIA — Multidimensional Assessment of Interoceptive Awareness

Mehling et al. (2012, 2018 v2) — a self-report inventory of interoceptive awareness across eight scales: Noticing, Not-Distracting, Not-Worrying, Attention Regulation, Emotional Awareness, Self-Regulation, Body Listening, Trusting. 37 items.

- Captures *interoceptive sensibility* in Garfinkel's three-dimensional model — the self-reported tendency to attend to body signals.
- Free for non-commercial use; widely available.
- Suffers a *milder* version of the self-report-on-introspection paradox (it asks about body attention, not introspective ability per se), but high-alexithymia respondents can still misread their own attentional patterns.

### Garfinkel's Three-Dimensional Interoception Model

Garfinkel, Seth, Barrett, Suzuki, and Critchley (2015), *Biological Psychology* 104:65–74. The core architectural distinction:

- **Interoceptive Accuracy (IAcc):** objective performance on a behavioural task (HCD/HCT). "How accurate are you at detecting body signals?"
- **Interoceptive Sensibility (ISen):** self-reported tendency to attend to body signals (MAIA). "How much do you attend to body signals?"
- **Interoceptive Awareness (IAw):** the metacognitive correspondence between accuracy and sensibility — whether your sensibility (subjective attention) matches your accuracy (objective performance). "How well-calibrated is your knowledge of your body-signal-reading ability?"

These three are dissociable. A person can have high ISen (lots of body attention) and low IAcc (poor performance) — meaning they pay close attention to their body but their inferences about what the signals mean are unreliable. This is the classic somatic-symptom-disorder profile (high body attention, catastrophic misinterpretation) and overlaps with what the parsing-deficit framework calls "hypervigilance with mislabelling."

For alexithymia self-classification, Garfinkel-3D is the architectural backbone for the question "where in the perception → interpretation → labelling pipeline am I impaired?"

### Quadratic Relationship Between Alexithymia and Interoception

**Aaron, Blain, Snodgress, and Park (2020)**, *Frontiers in Psychiatry* 11:132, [PMC7076086](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7076086/). N=76. Found a **quadratic** rather than linear relationship between alexithymia and interoceptive accuracy: *both* low-IA and high-IA participants tended to show elevated alexithymia, with mid-range IA being the protective profile. This finding (small sample, single study) is consistent with the parsing-deficit framework: low-IA = no-detection-deficit alexithymia; high-IA = hypervigilance-with-mislabelling alexithymia; mid-IA + good metacognition = non-alexithymic. The deficit is not in detection per se but in the appropriateness of body attention and the labelling that follows.

### 2026 PLoS One Systematic Review

[PMC11542822](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11542822/) — a 2026 systematic review and meta-analysis of subjective interoception × alexithymia. Provides triangulation guidance for measurement; should be consulted as the most recent integrative source for measurement decisions.

## The Self-Report Critique and Why It Matters

### Lundh's Argument

Lars-Gunnar Lundh (2001), [PubMed 11593459](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11593459/). The simplest formulation: *the TAS-20 asks the alexithymic person to describe their emotional experience using introspection, but the construct being measured is precisely the impairment of that introspection.* The contradiction is structural. Self-reports of introspective ability cannot be more accurate than the underlying introspective ability. People with severe DIF cannot know how confused they are — they can only know how confused they *notice* themselves to be.

This applies equally to TAS-20, BVAQ, PAQ, and MAIA. It is part of why these instruments correlate only modestly with each other and only modestly with performance-based measures (LEAS, HCT, HCD).

### How the Field Handles It

Three pragmatic responses:

1. **Triangulate.** Use multiple measurement modalities (self-report + interview + performance-based + observer-rated). Discrepancies between them are diagnostically informative.
2. **Use observer-rated and performance-based measures (TSIA, LEAS, OAQ-G2).** These bypass the paradox at the cost of practical burden.
3. **Accept the limit.** Treat self-report scores as one piece of evidence among others, hedge interpretations of borderline scores, and do not treat the self-report number as a ground truth.

For self-classifying users, the implication is: a single TAS-20 score is a useful starting point, not a verdict. Pairing instruments is the practical path.

## Triangulation: Pairing Instruments for Self-Classification

The user's stated goal is identifying which alexithymia subtype applies to them. The single best instrument doesn't exist. Triangulation is the answer.

### Recommended Pairings

**Minimal pairing for self-classification:**

- **TAS-20** (anchors against published literature; gives the standard categorical label)
- **MAIA** (interoceptive sensibility — captures whether body attention is high, low, or neutral)

This minimal pairing covers two axes: cognitive alexithymia (TAS-20) × interoceptive sensibility (MAIA). It distinguishes parsing-deficit profiles (high TAS-20 + low MAIA = no-detection-deficit; high TAS-20 + high MAIA + somatic complaints = hypervigilance-with-mislabelling) from cleaner cognitive deficits (high TAS-20 + average MAIA).

**Stronger pairing for cognitive vs. affective distinction:**

- **TAS-20** + **BVAQ** + **MAIA**

Adds the affective alexithymia dimension (BVAQ fantasising and emotionalising). Distinguishes Type I (low on both cognitive and affective) from Type II (low cognitive, intact affective).

**Stronger pairing for valence sensitivity:**

- **TAS-20** + **PAQ** + **MAIA**

Captures whether the user's pattern differs across positive and negative emotions.

**Comprehensive triangulation:**

- **TAS-20** + (**BVAQ** *or* **PAQ**) + **MAIA** + **LEAS-self-administered**

Adds the emotion-concept developmental level. The LEAS is harder to self-administer formally but the vignettes can be used reflectively even without coder scoring.

**With clinician access:**

- The above + **TSIA** + **clinician-administered LEAS**

Bypasses the self-report paradox via observer rating and performance-based measurement.

### What Each Pairing Tells the User

| Pairing | What It Distinguishes |
|---|---|
| TAS-20 only | Generic alexithymia label, no subtype. |
| TAS-20 + MAIA | Parsing-deficit vs. detection-deficit vs. hypervigilance profile. |
| TAS-20 + BVAQ | Cognitive vs. cognitive+affective alexithymia (Type I vs Type II). |
| TAS-20 + PAQ | Whether positive and negative emotion impairments differ. |
| TAS-20 + LEAS | Self-perceived difficulty vs. emotion-concept developmental level. |
| TAS-20 + TSIA | Self-rating vs. observer-rating discrepancy. |
| Full triangulation | Multidimensional profile suitable for matching to therapy modality. |

### Discrepancies Are Diagnostic

Disagreement between instruments is not noise. It is the *most informative* output of triangulation:

- High TAS-20 + low LEAS → strong evidence of impoverished emotion concepts (Barrett-style deficit). Fits therapies like granularity-building, expressive writing, emotion-vocabulary scaffolding.
- High TAS-20 + average LEAS → emotion concepts are present; the issue is attention or appraisal. Fits attention-appraisal-targeted therapy (Preece) and ACT-style approaches.
- High TAS-20 + high MAIA + chronic body symptoms → hypervigilance-with-mislabelling profile. Fits cognitive reappraisal of body signals; *contraindicates* open-ended interoceptive awareness training.
- High TAS-20 + low MAIA → no-detection-deficit profile. Fits scaffolded interoceptive training (with caution; trauma history changes this), MABT-style touch-mediated approaches.
- High TAS-20 self-rating + low TSIA observer rating → defensive responding or inflated self-perceived difficulty. Diagnostically interesting; warrants clinical attention.
- Low TAS-20 self-rating + high TSIA observer rating → poor self-insight, the self-report-on-introspection paradox in its strongest form. Self-report is unreliable here.

## Practical Access — Where to Find These Instruments

### Free or Open-Access

- **TAS-20** — widely circulated for non-commercial research and individual self-test; online versions exist (Embrace Autism, several research sites). The official scale is owned by Multi-Health Systems Inc. (MHS); commercial use requires licensing.
- **MAIA / MAIA-2** — freely available from [osher.ucsf.edu/research/maia/](https://osher.ucsf.edu/research/maia/). Self-administered.
- **PAQ / PAQ-S** — freely available from the Preece group at the University of Western Australia for non-commercial research.
- **BVAQ** — non-commercial research access generally permitted; copyright held by Vorst and Bermond.

### Purchase or Trained-Practitioner Access

- **TSIA** — administration manual purchased from MHS; requires trained interviewer.
- **LEAS** — research instrument; coding manual available; coder training required for reliable use.
- **OAQ-G2** — variable access; some versions in published literature, others through licensing.

### What This Means for Self-Classification

A user can independently complete **TAS-20 + MAIA + PAQ** at no cost in under 30 minutes total. This covers the cognitive-alexithymia dimension, the affective dimension (via PAQ valence split), and interoceptive sensibility. Adding the **BVAQ** if available extends affective coverage further. **LEAS-style reflection** (using published vignettes, even without formal coding) can supplement.

For users with clinician access, adding TSIA and clinician-administered LEAS bypasses the self-report paradox.

## Caveats Worth Carrying Forward

1. **No instrument bypasses the construct ambiguity.** All these tools are measuring a contested construct. Different theoretical frames (Sifneos psychoanalytic, Bermond cognitive-affective, Preece attention-appraisal, Bird interoceptive, Barrett constructed-emotion) emphasise different aspects and prefer different instruments. Pairing instruments helps, but does not resolve, this deeper indeterminacy.
2. **Self-report scores fluctuate with state.** Depression, acute distress, sleep deprivation, and recent emotional events shift TAS-20 scores. A single administration is a snapshot, not a fingerprint. For trait stability, multiple administrations across weeks/months are needed.
3. **Cutoffs are conventions, not natural kinds.** A score of 60 is not categorically different from a score of 62. Treating cutoffs as binary diagnostic thresholds overstates their precision.
4. **Cultural validity is not guaranteed.** The TAS-20 has known cross-cultural concerns; the BVAQ, PAQ, and others have less cross-cultural data. Users from non-Western cultural backgrounds should weight scores accordingly.
5. **Changes over time are interpretable but with caution.** If a user takes TAS-20 before and after an intervention and the score drops, it could mean the intervention worked, or it could mean the user has learned to *answer* the items differently (e.g., having learned the construct is now answering more reflectively). Multi-method pre-post is more defensible than single-instrument pre-post.
