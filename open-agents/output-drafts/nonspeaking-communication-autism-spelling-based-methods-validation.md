# Research Validation: Spelling-Based Methods — S2C, RPM, and Letterboard Communication

## Validation Summary

The Spelling-Based Methods research document accurately presents one of the most contested topics in autism communication. All major factual claims were independently verified. The document correctly identifies the absence of controlled evidence for RPM/S2C while fairly representing proponent perspectives. The handling of the Jaswal studies and their critiques is balanced and accurate.

**Overall Confidence Rating: 9/10** — High confidence in factual accuracy. The document navigates a minefield of competing claims with appropriate precision.

## Claim-by-Claim Validation

### Claim 1: RPM developed by Soma Mukhopadhyay around 1991 for her son Tito
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Multiple sources confirm Soma Mukhopadhyay developed RPM around 1991 to teach her son Tito Rajarshi Mukhopadhyay, who was diagnosed with severe autism. She holds a master's degree in chemistry and a bachelor's in education.
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soma_Mukhopadhyay), [NeuroClastic interview](https://neuroclastic.com/an-interview-with-soma-mukhopadhyay-pioneer-of-rapid-prompting-method-rpm/)

### Claim 2: Soma Mukhopadhyay is Executive Director of Education at HALO in Austin, Texas
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: HALO (Helping Autism through Learning and Outreach) is based in Austin, TX, with Soma serving as Executive Director of Education.
- **Sources**: [HALO-Soma](http://www.halo-soma.org/about_soma_tito.php), [NAA Conference](https://nac.nationalautismassociation.org/soma-mukhopadhyay/)

### Claim 3: S2C developed by Elizabeth Vosseller, formalized protocols published 2015, I-ASC founded 2019
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Elizabeth Vosseller, SLP, developed S2C protocols in 2015. I-ASC (International Association for Spelling as Communication) was established in June 2019. Vosseller is founder and Executive Director.
- **Sources**: [I-ASC](https://i-asc.org/), [I-ASC Vosseller bio](https://i-asc.org/our-team/elizabeth-vosseller/), [Puzzle Peace Counseling](https://puzzlepeacecounseling.com/s2c/)

### Claim 4: Tostanoski et al. (2019) systematic review found zero experimental studies of RPM meeting inclusion criteria — the "empty review"
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: The 2019 systematic review in the Review Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders found: "no experimental studies of sufficient rigor to demonstrate a link between any communication or education improvements in the person with a disability and the use of RPM"; "no studies have tested authorship of messages produced using RPM"; and "no experimental studies addressing the effectiveness of RPM." The review resulted in an empty review.
- **Note**: The lead author attribution may need checking — some sources list Schlosser as lead with Tostanoski as co-author (the citation is "Schlosser, Hemsley, Shane, Todd, Lang, Lilienfeld, Trembath, Mostert, Fong & Odom, 2019" in some references, and "Tostanoski et al." in others). The substantive finding is confirmed regardless.
- **Sources**: [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40489-019-00175-w), [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/332956082), [FPG UNC](https://fpg.unc.edu/publications/rapid-prompting-method-and-autism-spectrum-disorder-systematic-review-exposes-lack)

### Claim 5: Jaswal et al. (2020) eye-tracking study — 9 non-speaking autistic letterboard users, published in Nature Scientific Reports
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: "Eye-tracking reveals agency in assisted autistic communication" by Jaswal, Wayne & Golino was published in Scientific Reports (May 2020) with 9 participants. The study used head-mounted eye-tracking with non-speaking autistic letterboard users.
- **Sources**: [Nature Scientific Reports](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-020-64553-9), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32398782/)

### Claim 6: Published critiques of Jaswal 2020 — Catherine & Katharine (2021) in EBCAI, Skeptical Inquirer (Vyse)
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: "A recent eye-tracking study fails to reveal agency in assisted autistic communication" was published in EBCAI (Vol 15, No 1, 2021). Stuart Vyse published a detailed critique in Skeptical Inquirer. Both critiques argue the study does not establish authorship and rests on faulty assumptions. Specific criticisms include: small sample size (N=9), no control condition without facilitator, failure to rule out subtle cueing, and the argument that looking at letters is necessary but not sufficient for authorship.
- **Sources**: [EBCAI 2021](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/17489539.2021.1918890), [Skeptical Inquirer](https://skepticalinquirer.org/exclusive/of-eye-movements-and-autism-the-latest-chapter-in-a-continuing-controversy/), [Catherine & Katharine blog](https://catherineandkatharine.wordpress.com/2020/12/11/what-scientific-reports-wont-publish-my-critique-of-jaswals-fc-s2c-eye-tracking-study/)

### Claim 7: Jaswal et al. (2024) literacy study — iPad tapping task, 17 of 31 showed literacy patterns, published in journal Autism
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: "Literacy in nonspeaking autistic people" by Jaswal, Lampi & Stockwell was published in the journal Autism (2024). The study used an iPad tapping task with 31 non-speaking autistic participants, finding about half (17 of 31) showed patterns consistent with foundational literacy skills.
- **Sources**: [SAGE/Autism](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/13623613241230709), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38380632/), [ScienceDaily](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2024/03/240306203115.htm)

### Claim 8: "Illusions of literacy" rebuttal published in EBCAI 2024
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: "Illusions of literacy in nonspeaking autistic people: a response to Jaswal, Lampi & Stockwell, 2024" was published in Evidence-Based Communication Assessment and Intervention (EBCAI, Vol 17, No 3-4, 2024). The rebuttal raises concerns about: participants recruited from RPM/S2C programs (practice effects), no comparison condition without experimenter reading aloud, the task measuring reaction times rather than reading comprehension, and uncontrolled assistant behavior.
- **Sources**: [EBCAI/Taylor & Francis](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/17489539.2024.2381445), [facilitatedcommunication.org](https://www.facilitatedcommunication.org/blog/illusions-of-literacy-in-nonspeaking-autistic-people-a-response-to-jaswal-et-al-2024)

### Claim 9: ASHA explicitly warns against both RPM and S2C
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: ASHA's Board of Directors approved position statements discouraging use of RPM/S2C, stating they "lack scientific validity and do not foster independent communication." ASHA warns that "RPM and S2C raise significant questions about author validity."
- **Sources**: [ASHA Warning](https://www.asha.org/slp/asha-warns-against-rapid-prompting-method-or-spelling-to-communicate/), [ASHA Press Room](https://www.asha.org/about/press-room/fc-and-rpm/)

### Claim 10: Spellers documentary (2023) executive produced by Jenny McCarthy-Wahlberg
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: The Spellers documentary was released in 2023, executive produced by Jenny McCarthy-Wahlberg and Donnie Wahlberg. The research document correctly notes the film promotes S2C without mentioning FC or controlled studies.
- **Note**: The research accurately identifies McCarthy-Wahlberg's role. Her association with the anti-vaccine movement is noted in the research and is relevant context.
- **Sources**: [NCSA](https://www.ncsautism.org/blog//researchers-not-flimmakers), [IMDB](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt27558471/)

### Claim 11: Ido Kedar — began RPM around age 7, published "Ido in Autismland" at age 15
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED with caveat
- **Evidence**: Ido Kedar, born in 2000, was diagnosed with severe autism. He published "Ido in Autismland" (2012). He began working with Soma Mukhopadhyay. The research document correctly notes his communication "has not been subjected to formal controlled authorship testing" and appropriately distinguishes between "types independently in some observers' judgment" and "independently verified through controlled testing."
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ido_Kedar)

## Issues Found

### Minor Issues
1. **Author attribution for 2019 systematic review**: The research document attributes the empty systematic review to "Tostanoski et al. (2019)." Some sources list Schlosser as lead author. The actual citation may be Schlosser, R.W., Hemsley, B., et al. — the paper has many authors and "Tostanoski" appears as a co-author. This should be verified against the actual publication to ensure correct attribution.
2. **S2C protocol publication date**: The document says "formalized protocols published in 2015." I-ASC states Vosseller "developed S2C in 2015" but the formal protocol publication date may differ from when she began using the approach (around 2013). The distinction is minor but worth noting.

### No Major Issues Found
