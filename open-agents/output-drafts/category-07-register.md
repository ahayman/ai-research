# Category 7: Register and Code-Switching

## The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

---

## How to Read This Document

Every other category in this hub describes a specific pragmatic mechanism (speech acts, implicature, hedging, etc.). Register is different. Register is the **master variable** that determines the settings for ALL other mechanisms. Your hedging level, politeness strategy, discourse marker frequency, directness, vocabulary, and even grammar all shift together based on register -- the situational "mode" you're operating in.

If the previous categories are individual instruments, register is the conductor that tells each one how loud to play.

---

## 1. What Register Is

### 1.1 Definition

**Register** is the variety of language a speaker uses based on social context -- who you're talking to, where, and why. It encompasses vocabulary, grammar, pronunciation, hedging, politeness level, and directness, all shifting together as a **cluster** when the situation changes.

The key distinction: **Dialect is where you're FROM. Register is where you ARE.**

A dialect is tied to geography, class, ethnicity -- it's relatively stable. Register is tied to situation -- it changes many times a day. The same person speaks one way in a courtroom and another way at a barbecue. Both are "correct" for their context. The ability to shift between them is what linguists call **communicative competence**.

### 1.2 The Key Insight: Register Shifting Is Systematic

What appears to be free, unconstrained variation in how people talk actually follows systematic rules. Given the social situation -- who is present, what's being discussed, what the relationship is, what medium is being used -- the register can be **predicted**. It is not arbitrary.

For neurotypical speakers, register shifting becomes intuitive in childhood. They develop an unconscious sense of which language "fits" a situation. For autistic speakers, this intuition may not develop the same way -- but the **system** is still there, and being systematic is something autistic minds do well.

### 1.3 What Determines Register: Three Independent Variables

The linguist M.A.K. Halliday identified three dimensions that independently determine register:

| Dimension | What It Covers | Example |
|-----------|---------------|---------|
| **Field** | What you're talking about (subject matter, domain) | Medical terminology at a hospital vs. football jargon at a game |
| **Tenor** | Who you're talking to (relationship, power, distance) | Speaking to your boss vs. your best friend |
| **Mode** | How you're communicating (channel, medium) | Face-to-face vs. email vs. text message |

These three are **independently variable**. Each can change while the others stay the same:

- **Same field, different tenor**: Explaining a medical condition to a doctor colleague (technical, peer) vs. explaining it to a frightened patient (simplified, reassuring)
- **Same tenor, different field**: Chatting casually with a friend about cars vs. chatting casually about politics
- **Same field and tenor, different mode**: Describing symptoms to a doctor in person vs. in a patient portal message

## 2. The Five Register Levels

Martin Joos (1961) identified five distinct levels of formality that English speakers shift among. Think of them as five "modes" that the language can operate in:

### 2.1 Frozen (Static)

**Definition**: Fixed, ritualistic language that never changes.

**Examples**: Wedding vows, the Pledge of Allegiance, the Miranda warning, religious scripture, legal oaths, "Dearly beloved, we are gathered here..."

**Properties**: Memorized, often archaic ("thee," "hereinafter"), no adaptation to audience. The same words are spoken regardless of who is present.

**When you encounter it**: Ceremonies, legal proceedings, liturgy, national rituals.

### 2.2 Formal

**Definition**: Professional, one-directional, carefully planned language for audiences not personally known to the speaker.

**Examples**: Academic lectures, business presentations, keynote speeches, news broadcasts, official letters, dissertations.

**Properties**: Complete sentences, no contractions, no slang, specialized vocabulary, planned rather than spontaneous, minimal audience interaction.

**When you encounter it**: Public speaking, academic writing, official communications, formal reports.

### 2.3 Consultative

**Definition**: Professional but interactive. The standard register for communication with people you don't know intimately.

**Examples**: Doctor-patient conversations, teacher-student interactions, business meetings, job interviews, customer service, professional emails.

**Properties**: More relaxed than formal, two-way interaction with feedback, some hedging and politeness markers, technical vocabulary as needed, clear and organized but not stiff.

**This is the "default" register for English.** When you don't know what register to use, consultative is the safest choice.

### 2.4 Casual (Informal)

**Definition**: Relaxed language among people who share social bonds.

**Examples**: Friend conversations, family dinner talk, texts to friends, casual workplace chat, social media posts.

**Properties**: Slang, contractions, ellipsis (dropping words: "Going to the store" → "Going to the store?" → "Store?"), inside jokes, incomplete sentences, nicknames, profanity, rapid topic shifts.

**When you encounter it**: With friends, family, in-group members, familiar colleagues.

### 2.5 Intimate

**Definition**: Private language between people who share deep personal history.

**Examples**: Couples, very close friends, parent to infant, siblings.

**Properties**: Highly abbreviated, private vocabulary and code words, non-verbal cues replace words, shared references that outsiders wouldn't understand, extreme ellipsis ("You know what?" "Yeah." -- an entire exchange compressed into minimal words).

### 2.6 Summary Table

| Level | Audience | Key Features | Example |
|-------|----------|-------------|---------|
| **Frozen** | Public/ritual | Fixed, unchanging, archaic | "I do solemnly swear..." |
| **Formal** | Unknown, public | Complete sentences, no slang, planned | "The quarterly results indicate..." |
| **Consultative** | Professional | Interactive but careful, some hedging | "Could you walk me through this?" |
| **Casual** | Friends/family | Contractions, slang, incomplete sentences | "Hey, wanna grab lunch?" |
| **Intimate** | Very close | Abbreviated, private codes, non-verbal | "The thing?" "Yeah." "K." |

## 3. What Changes Across Registers

When you shift register, **multiple features shift simultaneously**:

| Feature | Formal | Casual |
|---------|--------|--------|
| **Vocabulary** | "automobile," "residence," "commence" | "car," "place," "start" |
| **Grammar** | "I would like to request..." | "Can I get..." |
| **Contractions** | "I would not," "cannot" | "I wouldn't," "can't" |
| **Pronunciation** | "going to," careful enunciation | "gonna," reduced forms |
| **Hedging level** | High (Categories 3, 6) | Low |
| **Directness** | Low ("I was wondering if perhaps...") | High ("Hey, can you...?") |
| **Sentence length** | Long, complex | Short, fragmented |
| **Address forms** | "Dr. Smith," "Professor Jones" | "Sarah," "Mike," "dude" |
| **Discourse markers** | Fewer, formal ("furthermore") | More, casual ("like," "you know") |
| **Politeness strategy** | Negative politeness dominant | Positive politeness dominant |

### 3.1 The Same Message in Five Registers

**Asking someone to be quiet:**

| Register | What You Say |
|----------|-------------|
| Frozen | "Silence in the court." |
| Formal | "I would ask that everyone please refrain from speaking at this time." |
| Consultative | "Could you keep it down a bit? We're trying to concentrate." |
| Casual | "Hey, shh!" / "Dude, shut up." |
| Intimate | [Finger to lips] / "Babe. Shh." |

**Requesting information:**

| Register | What You Say |
|----------|-------------|
| Frozen | "State your name for the record." |
| Formal | "Could you please provide your full name and date of birth?" |
| Consultative | "What's your name and date of birth?" |
| Casual | "What's your name?" |
| Intimate | "Wait, when's your birthday again?" |

**Expressing disagreement:**

| Register | What You Say |
|----------|-------------|
| Frozen | "The defendant enters a plea of not guilty." |
| Formal | "With respect, I believe there may be an alternative interpretation of the data." |
| Consultative | "I see your point, but I think there might be another way to look at it." |
| Casual | "Nah, I don't think so." |
| Intimate | "Nope." / [Raised eyebrow] |

**Apologizing:**

| Register | What You Say |
|----------|-------------|
| Frozen | "The company formally apologizes for any inconvenience caused." |
| Formal | "I sincerely apologize for the oversight. It will not happen again." |
| Consultative | "I'm sorry about that. Let me fix it." |
| Casual | "My bad!" / "Sorry, dude." |
| Intimate | "Ugh, sorry." / [Grimace] |

## 4. Register as Social Signal

### 4.1 Register Mismatch: When the "Wrong" Register Is Used

Using the wrong register is one of the most immediately noticeable social errors in English. The response is visceral -- people can **feel** a register mismatch even if they can't articulate what's wrong.

**Too formal in a casual context:**
- Signals: distance, coldness, hostility, social awkwardness, or that you don't consider yourself part of the group
- Example: Using "I would like to request your assistance" at a friend's barbecue
- How it's received: "Why are you being so weird?" / "Are you mad at me?"

**Too casual in a formal context:**
- Signals: disrespect, incompetence, deliberate provocation, or that you don't understand the social norms
- Example: Using "Hey, what's up" in an email to a CEO you've never met
- How it's received: "This person doesn't understand professional norms" / "Are they being deliberately rude?"

### 4.2 Register Shifts as Deliberate Signals

People deliberately shift registers to communicate meaning:

| Shift | What It Signals | Example |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| **Suddenly more formal** | Anger, distance, authority, or "this is now serious" | A parent using a child's full name: "*William James Henderson!*" |
| **Suddenly more casual** | Building rapport, defusing tension, inclusion | A boss saying "Look, between you and me..." |
| **Matching your register** | Alignment, acceptance, rapport | Shifting to match a new acquaintance's level |
| **Deliberately mismatching** | Power move, exclusion, or irony | Using formal language sarcastically ("Oh, I do *beg* your pardon") |
| **Dropping contractions** | Becoming more formal = more serious | "I am not going to ask again" (vs. "I'm not gonna...") |
| **Using your full name** | Formality + possible anger | "Robert, we need to talk" (vs. "Rob, hey...") |
| **"Per my last email"** | Passive-aggressive formality | Formal register wielded as a weapon |

### 4.3 Accommodation Theory

Linguist Howard Giles showed that speakers naturally adjust their speech toward (**convergence**) or away from (**divergence**) their conversation partner:

- **Convergence**: You match the other person's register to build rapport and signal solidarity. You start using their vocabulary, matching their formality level, even adjusting your speech rate.
- **Divergence**: You deliberately make your speech MORE different to mark social distance or assert distinct identity.

NT speakers do this automatically. Autistic speakers may need to do it consciously -- which is cognitive work, but learnable.

## 5. Register in Key Contexts

### 5.1 Workplace Email Register

Email register is determined by **power distance** and **relationship closeness**:

| Recipient | Register | Key Features |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| CEO / Senior leader | Formal | "Dear Ms. Chen," no contractions, hedged, brief, sign-off: "Best regards" |
| Direct manager | Consultative | "Hi Sarah," some contractions OK, professional but relaxed |
| Peer / Colleague | Casual-consultative | "Hey," contractions fine, may include humor, relaxed sign-off |
| Direct report | Consultative-casual | "Hi team," clear and friendly, may be more direct |

**The register escalation ladder** (in follow-up emails):
1. "Just wanted to follow up on this!" (casual, friendly)
2. "Following up on the below." (consultative, neutral)
3. "I wanted to circle back on this request." (formal, firmer)
4. "Per my previous email..." (formal + pointed)
5. "As previously discussed and outlined in my email of [date]..." (formal + documented)

Each step up in formality signals increasing urgency or frustration.

### 5.2 Digital Communication Register

| Platform | Expected Register | Why |
|----------|-----------------|-----|
| **LinkedIn** | Formal-consultative | Professional identity, public-facing |
| **Work email** | Consultative (varies by recipient) | Professional default |
| **Slack/Teams** | Casual-consultative | Fast, informal, team-oriented |
| **Text to friend** | Casual-intimate | Private, established relationship |
| **Text to boss** | Consultative | Professional despite personal channel |
| **Twitter/X** | Casual (varies) | Public but informal, character-limited |
| **Reddit** | Casual | Anonymous, community norms vary by subreddit |
| **Instagram** | Casual | Visual-first, captions are secondary |

### 5.3 Address Forms and Register

| Form | Register Level | Example |
|------|---------------|---------|
| Title + Last name | Formal | "Dr. Patel," "Professor Jones," "Mr. Chen" |
| First name | Consultative-casual | "Sarah," "David" |
| Nickname | Casual | "Sare," "Dave" |
| Terms of endearment | Intimate | "Honey," "babe," "love" |
| In-group terms | Casual (in-group) | "Dude," "mate," "bro," "y'all" |

**The asymmetric address problem**: When someone addresses you by first name but you should use their title ("Sarah" but "Dr. Patel"), the asymmetry signals a **power differential**. If you're unsure, use the more formal form until invited to use something else.

## 6. Register and the Autistic Experience

### 6.1 The Pedantic Speech Pattern

Research consistently documents that autistic individuals may use **overly formal language** in casual contexts -- what clinicians call "pedantic speech." This typically includes:
- Lengthy, detailed utterances with more information than the context requires
- Language that sounds more like written text than spoken conversation
- Formal vocabulary choices where casual synonyms are expected
- Precise, technical terminology even with non-expert audiences
- Complete sentences where fragments would be normal

**Why this happens** (multiple contributing factors):
- **Language acquired from books/media**: Autistic individuals who are heavy readers may internalize the formal register of written language as their default
- **Preference for precision**: "Automobile" is more precise than "car" in some senses
- **Comfort with rule-governed systems**: Formal register HAS more explicit rules; casual register relies on implicit social knowledge
- **Reduced context-sensitivity**: Vermeulen's "context blindness" theory suggests the context-to-register mapping may not happen automatically

### 6.2 The "Flat Register" Pattern

Some autistic individuals use a more **consistent** register across contexts rather than shifting. This isn't truly "flat" -- it's a **different default** that doesn't shift as much as NT speakers expect.

**Strengths of register consistency:**
- Authenticity -- you sound like "yourself" in every context
- Reliability -- people know what to expect from you
- Honesty -- no performative code-switching

**Costs of register consistency:**
- Too formal in casual contexts → perceived as cold, distant, robotic
- Too casual in formal contexts → perceived as disrespectful or incompetent
- Not shifting when context shifts → perceived as "not reading the room"

### 6.3 Code-Switching, Masking, and Identity

For autistic individuals, register-switching often maps onto **masking/camouflaging** (Hull et al., 2019). The experience of performing a different register can feel like performing a different identity -- and it carries cognitive cost.

Research on camouflaging identifies three components:
- **Compensation**: Using learned strategies to navigate social situations
- **Masking**: Suppressing natural autistic behaviors
- **Assimilation**: Trying to fit in with non-autistic peers

Register shifting can involve all three: compensating with learned politeness formulas, suppressing direct communication style, and assimilating to expected workplace language norms.

**The identity question**: "Which register is the 'real me'?" is one autistic people frequently struggle with. The neurodiversity-affirming answer: your natural register IS the real you. Other registers are **tools** you can use, not identities you must perform.

### 6.4 Vermeulen's Context Blindness

Peter Vermeulen's (2012) concept of "context blindness" provides a unifying framework: register selection depends on reading contextual cues (Where am I? Who am I with? What's the social expectation?). If this context-reading doesn't happen automatically, register selection doesn't either -- not because the registers themselves are unavailable, but because the **context-to-register mapping** isn't triggered.

This is good news: it means the **knowledge** can be present even when the **spontaneous deployment** is challenged. Explicit learning of context-to-register mappings can bridge the gap.

### 6.5 Autistic-Autistic Register Norms

Research from the double empathy literature (Crompton et al., 2020; Sutherland et al., 2024) suggests autistic communities have different register expectations:
- Reduced phatic communication requirements
- Higher tolerance for formal or precise language in casual contexts
- Acceptance of "infodumping" (extended turns on topics of interest)
- Directness valued rather than penalized
- Register consistency perceived as honesty rather than awkwardness

## 7. Practical Register Selection Guide

### 7.1 The Five-Question Algorithm

When you're unsure what register to use:

1. **Where am I?** (Setting determines baseline)
   - Professional/institutional → start at Consultative
   - Social/casual → start at Casual
   - Ceremonial → start at Formal/Frozen

2. **Who am I talking to?** (Relationship adjusts the baseline)
   - Authority figure → shift up (more formal)
   - Peer → stay at baseline
   - Close friend/family → shift down (more casual)

3. **What am I talking about?** (Topic can override)
   - Serious/sensitive topic → shift up regardless of relationship
   - Light/social topic → can shift down regardless of setting

4. **What channel am I using?** (Mode matters)
   - Written tends more formal than spoken
   - Email more formal than text/Slack
   - Video more formal than phone

5. **What register are THEY using?** (Match when unsure)
   - If they're casual → you can be casual
   - If they're formal → be formal
   - If they shift → consider shifting with them

### 7.2 The "When in Doubt" Rules

1. **Consultative is the universal safe default.** If you don't know which register to use, consultative (professional but friendly) works almost everywhere.

2. **Start more formal, dial back.** It's always easier to relax from formal to casual than to recover from being too casual. Let the other person set the casualness level.

3. **Match the other person.** If they use contractions, you can. If they use your first name, use theirs. If they're formal, stay formal.

4. **Register mismatches UP are forgiven; mismatches DOWN are penalized.** Being "too formal" at a barbecue is awkward. Being "too casual" in a meeting is damaging.

5. **When register suddenly changes, something just happened.** A sudden shift to formality signals anger, distance, or seriousness. A sudden shift to casualness signals rapport-building or de-escalation.

### 7.3 Context Quick Reference

| Context | Expected Register | Address Form |
|---------|-----------------|-------------|
| Job interview | Formal-consultative | Mr./Ms./Dr. + Last name |
| First day at work | Consultative | Follow their lead |
| Email to boss | Consultative | First name (usually, after invited) |
| Email to client | Consultative-formal | Mr./Ms. + Last name until told otherwise |
| Team Slack channel | Casual-consultative | First name |
| Coffee with colleague | Casual | First name / nickname |
| Meeting a friend's parents | Consultative | Mr./Ms. + Last name unless told otherwise |
| Texting a friend | Casual-intimate | First name / nickname |
| Doctor's appointment | Consultative | Dr. + Last name |
| Formal dinner/event | Formal-consultative | Title + Last name |

## 8. Key Takeaways

1. **Register is the master variable.** It determines the settings for hedging, politeness, directness, vocabulary, and grammar. Knowing which register to use is knowing which settings to apply for everything else.

2. **Five levels: Frozen → Formal → Consultative → Casual → Intimate.** Most everyday life happens in the consultative-to-casual range. Consultative is the safe default.

3. **Register is systematic and predictable.** Three variables (field, tenor, mode) determine register selection. This makes it learnable through explicit rules.

4. **Register shifts are social signals.** Sudden formality = something's wrong. Sudden casualness = rapport-building. Matching someone's register = alignment. These signals carry meaning.

5. **The pedantic speech pattern is a register mismatch, not a deficit.** Using formal register in casual contexts is using the "wrong mode" for the situation -- but the linguistic competence is fully intact.

6. **Start formal, dial back.** Over-formality is awkward but forgivable. Under-formality is damaging. When unsure, lean formal.

7. **Your natural register is valid.** Register consistency is valued in autistic-autistic communication. Understanding NT register expectations gives you the choice of when and how to adapt -- it's a tool, not an obligation.
