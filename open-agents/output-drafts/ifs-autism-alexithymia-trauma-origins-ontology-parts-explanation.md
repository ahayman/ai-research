# IFS Origins, Theoretical Claims, and the Ontology of Parts

## Opener

Pages one and two told you what IFS is and how a session works. This page asks the harder question: what is IFS actually claiming? Where did it come from, what is it borrowing from, and when it speaks of "parts," is it pointing at something real, something useful, or something that cannot be checked?

For a systems thinker, this matters. A model whose marketing copy says "your inner family is real" and whose clinical practice often functions as if "parts" is a useful organizing fiction is operating at two layers at once. Knowing which layer you are at — and which layer a given practitioner, book, or training is at — changes how you read the model. The aim of this page is to steel-man what IFS is claiming, locate it in its actual intellectual lineage (which is more complicated than the marketing suggests), and then identify with precision the places where the claims outrun what the evidence can support.

The headline conclusion is simple: IFS's *outcome* claims (people improve) are testable and have been partly tested. IFS's *mechanism* claims about parts as discrete entities, and about a universal undamaged Self, are not currently testable as the model formulates them. The falsifiability problem is real, narrow, and important.

## Schwartz's Origin Story — and what was actually a study versus a clinical observation

Richard C. Schwartz earned a PhD in Marriage and Family Therapy at Purdue and joined the staff of the Institute for Juvenile Research at the University of Illinois at Chicago in the late 1970s. He was a structural and Bowenian family therapist by training — Minuchin, Bowen, Haley, Satir — and he believed, with something close to evangelical conviction, that systemic family work could resolve conditions that individual psychodynamic models had struggled with.

In the early 1980s, with a colleague named Mary Jo Barrett, Schwartz set out to demonstrate this with a difficult population: roughly thirty bulimic adolescents and their families. The plan was to apply structural and strategic family interventions, watch the family dynamics reorganize, and document the symptom remission. The plan did not work. By Schwartz's own account in his 2022 *Mad in America* interview, the family dynamics did shift visibly — but the bingeing and purging continued. When he asked clients why, they kept describing internal "parts" with their own voices, agendas, and behavior. There was, they said, "a part of me" that had to do this; nothing they or their families could do would make it stop.

This is the empirical-clinical observation at the bottom of IFS. It is genuinely interesting, and it is also genuinely *not* a controlled outcome study. IFS marketing copy sometimes refers to a "1982 bulimia outcome study" that demonstrated the limits of family therapy and led Schwartz to develop IFS. The IFS Institute's own APA-style bibliography lists no such 1982 outcome study. Schwartz's earliest 1982 publication is "On becoming a family therapist" in *Family Therapy Networker*, not on bulimia. His first peer-reviewed bulimia paper is "Bulimia and family therapy: a case study," *International Journal of Eating Disorders*, 1983, vol. 2, pp. 75–82 — a *case study*, by his own framing. The seminal IFS textbook is *Internal Family Systems Therapy* (Schwartz, 1995, Guilford Press), more than a decade later.

> **Correction**: There is no published 1982 bulimia outcome study by Schwartz that founded IFS. The work that produced the "parts" insight was an in-house clinical observation phase with approximately thirty patients and their families, conducted with Mary Jo Barrett at the Institute for Juvenile Research in the early 1980s. The earliest peer-reviewed bulimia paper is from 1983, and it is a case study. IFS's foundational textbook came in 1995. When marketing material refers to "the 1982 outcome study," it is collapsing a multi-year clinical observation into a single citable event.

This matters less for whether IFS works (a question Cat 4 takes up in detail) than for how its claims are framed. The model arose from clinical anomaly — from family-systems work that didn't quite work as advertised, and from clients whose self-reports refused to fit the family-systems frame. That is a respectable origin for a clinical model. It is not the same kind of origin as a published controlled trial, and it should not be cited as if it were.

By the mid-to-late 1980s, Schwartz was speaking *to* parts — addressing them as one might address family members in a structural family therapy session, asking what they wanted, feared, and protected against. He still treated parts as metaphors at this stage. The shift came after he attended a workshop by a clinician named Sandra Watanabe on what she called the "Inner Cast of Characters." When Schwartz asked her, in his telling, whether she really thought these inner figures were real, she answered that they were "very real." Over the following years he stopped treating parts as metaphors and began treating them as genuine sub-minds. The 1995 textbook and everything since reflect that shift.

## Theoretical Lineage — what IFS borrows, and from whom

<!-- DIAGRAM: lineage-tree -->

IFS is sometimes described as Jungian therapy with new vocabulary. That is not quite right. The pedigree has two halves, and the half that matches Schwartz's actual training is the one usually under-emphasized in IFS materials.

**The family-systems half** is where Schwartz had genuine, deep training. Murray Bowen's family-systems theory contributed the idea of differentiation of self and multigenerational transmission of patterns. Salvador Minuchin's structural family therapy contributed boundaries, alliances, hierarchy, and the idea that a healthy family has an executive subsystem that does not get pulled into peer-level conflict with its own children. Jay Haley contributed strategic interventions; Virginia Satir contributed the experiential "Parts Party" technique. Schwartz's central move was to take this family-systems toolkit and apply it inward — to treat the inner life as a system of related agents needing coordination, with a "leader" (the Self) playing the executive role.

**The multiplicity-of-mind half** is the half where the lineage gets messier and where the depth of Schwartz's actual engagement is contested. The multiplicity-of-mind tradition runs back at least to Pierre Janet in the 1890s, who defined dissociation as "a lack of integration among two or more systems of ideas and functions that constitute personality" and provided the first systematic clinical theory of psychic multiplicity. Carl Jung's early-twentieth-century work on complexes — psychic fragments organized around archetypes that "act independently and autonomously" — extended this. Roberto Assagioli, contemporaneously, developed psychosynthesis, which is the closest direct architectural antecedent to IFS: a therapy organized around "subpersonalities" coordinated by an "I" that is itself "the direct reflection or projection of the Self." Eric Berne's mid-century Transactional Analysis reduced the granularity to three ego states (Parent, Adult, Child) and established a clinical tradition of speaking *with* internal states rather than just about them. John and Helen Watkins built Ego State Therapy in the 1970s, using hypnotic procedures to dialogue with discriminable ego states — a procedure remarkably close to IFS's "direct access" technique, though Watkins is rarely cited in IFS canonical material. Ernest Hilgard's neo-dissociation theory and the "hidden observer" experiments offered, in the 1970s, one of the few experimental supports for dissociated streams of consciousness, though the hidden-observer phenomenon has been substantially contested in the methodology literature (Spanos and Hewitt argued it could be produced by demand characteristics; the debate has continued for forty years without clean resolution).

> **Evidence note**: The hidden-observer literature has a validity rating of about 7/10 as evidence for dissociated streams. The phenomenon is real and replicable; whether it shows what Hilgard claimed it showed (genuine dissociation) versus what Spanos argued (response to social-psychological cues) is unsettled.

Marvin Minsky's *Society of Mind* (1986) is invoked in IFS-friendly writing as scientific cover for the multiplicity claim. It is worth being precise about what Minsky actually said: the mind, in his framework, is built up from many simple agents that are *individually mindless*. Intelligence emerges from their interaction. Minsky's agents are not subpersonalities; they have no perspectives, intentions, or memories of their own. Citing *Society of Mind* in support of the IFS Position 3 claim that parts are "real entities with their own perspectives and intentions" is a structural mischaracterization. Minsky is much closer to Position 2 (parts as labels for processing subroutines) than to Position 3.

Schwartz's own typical citation list is Freud's id/ego/superego, object relations, Berne's TA, Assagioli's psychosynthesis, and CBT schemata. Janet, Watkins, and Minsky tend to be referenced more loosely. Critics — including the depth-psychology writer Bradford White and others — argue that Schwartz uses the *language* of these traditions without engaging their actual frameworks at depth. This critique is fair. The lineage is broadly accurate as citation; whether IFS substantively builds on these traditions is contested.

## The Multiplicity-of-Mind Thesis

<!-- DIAGRAM: multiplicity-contrast -->

What is IFS actually claiming when it says the mind is naturally multiple? Two things, and they are not the same.

The first is the *descriptive* claim: minds are not unitary; people experience internal "voices," "sides," "selves," "parts." This claim is well-supported. It is supported by dissociation research going back to Janet. It is supported by dual-process theory, which has accumulated substantial experimental evidence that human reasoning operates through two distinguishable systems — a fast, automatic, associative System 1 and a slower, deliberate, rule-based System 2 — and likely more sub-systems beneath that. It is supported by modular-mind research, although strict-modularity claims face their own critiques. It is supported by emerging non-pathological-multiplicity literature, which distinguishes everyday inner-conflict experiences from clinical dissociation. It is supported by the cross-cultural phenomenology of inner conflict, which is approximately universal.

The second claim is the *entity* claim: not just that minds are plural, but that they contain *discriminable subpersonalities* with their own perspectives, intentions, and developmental histories. This is the larger leap, and it is what IFS Position 3 (and the IFS Institute's marketing language) commits to. None of the supporting traditions above directly establish this. Dual-process theory posits processing modes, not characters. Modular-mind theory posits domain-specific systems, not biographies. Dissociation theory describes states, not agents with goals. The leap from "minds are plural" to "minds contain subpersonalities" is the conceptual move IFS makes, and it is where a serious systems thinker has to be most careful.

The model's claim is appealing precisely because it sits in the middle: plural enough to match phenomenological experience, but specific enough to be operationally useful in therapy. The trouble is that "operationally useful" and "empirically established" are different things, and IFS materials sometimes write as if they were the same.

## The Three (Plus One) Ontology Positions

<!-- DIAGRAM: ontology-positions -->

When practitioners, books, and clients use the word "parts," they can mean any of four things. The model itself does not require a single answer; that is part of why it has spread.

**Position 1 — Metaphor / Heuristic.** Parts are useful clinical fictions. They are a way to organize phenomenological experience that helps clients separate "I am angry" from "a part of me is angry." Practitioners working in this register use parts language without ontological commitment; what matters is whether the language helps the client work with their experience. Schwartz's earliest period (early 1980s) was here.

**Position 2 — Neural networks / dissociated affective states.** Parts are phenomenological labels for state-dependent affective and cognitive networks. This framing is broadly compatible with structural-dissociation theory (Van der Hart, Nijenhuis, Steele) and with neuroscience of state-dependent memory. The Wikipedia article on IFS notes that "other proponents describe these terms as phenomenological metaphors for neural networks or dissociated affective states." This is the framing most compatible with mainstream cognitive neuroscience, and many trauma-informed clinicians (notably Janina Fisher) operate here even when they use IFS vocabulary.

**Position 3 — Discrete subpersonalities.** Parts are real, discriminable mental entities with their own perspectives, memories, intentions, and developmental histories. This is what IFS Institute marketing typically describes. In *No Bad Parts* (2021), Schwartz writes: "All of us are born with many sub-minds — or parts. These parts are not imaginary or symbolic." The same book also contains hedge language: "I am simply reporting what I've learned over years of exploring this inner territory without judgment regarding the ontological reality of that data." The hedge does work, but the dominant framing in canonical IFS material since the late 1980s has been Position 3.

**Position 4 — Transpersonal / discarnate.** Robert Falconer's *The Others Within Us* (2023, with a foreword by Schwartz) introduces "unattached burdens" — energies that are described as not part of the client's own history and not generationally inherited but as parasitic entities, tricksters, egregores, or thought forms. Schwartz's *Spirituality of IFS* course at Sounds True affirms "spiritual phenomena that cannot be explained by conventional science." This is the strongest possible ontology claim and is the proper subject of a later page (Cat 11). It is mentioned here because it is the position toward which the founder is currently leaning, and because a careful reader needs to know that the ontology question does not stop at Position 3.

A given practitioner can sit anywhere along this spectrum. So can the model itself, depending on which book, training, or interview you read. This is partly a strength — the model is robust to whatever ontology turns out to be true — and partly a weakness, because it makes the question "what is IFS claiming?" structurally hard to answer.

## Falsifiability and the Empirical-Evidence Question

<!-- DIAGRAM: falsifiability-map -->

This is the central epistemic critique, and it deserves to be stated cleanly.

The 2024 *Society for the Advancement of Psychotherapy* essay "Internal Family Systems: Exploring Its Problematic Popularity" puts it directly: "The model presents unique challenges for falsifiability, the hallmark for scientific theory. Data that would suggest that the model is not true are not easy to envision. Empirically substantiating — or refuting — the existence of the hypothesized, distinct, subpersonalities presents difficulties that undermine the model's scientific rigor. Currently, empirical evidence supporting the distinctiveness and functionality of the internal subpersonalities is lacking. More importantly, measures for testing their discriminant validity do not exist."

That is the critique in the published literature, in the words of clinical psychologists publishing in a clinical-society bulletin. It rests on two specific gaps.

First, there is no instrument that operationalizes "a part" in a way that lets researchers determine whether two reported parts are distinct entities or one phenomenon being described in two ways. There is no neuroimaging finding that has identified discrete subpersonalities as the model describes them. Proxy findings exist — state-dependent activation patterns, default-mode-network shifts during self-referential processing — but none cleanly support the existence of *subpersonalities* in Position 3's sense.

Second, when a client cannot access "Self," IFS reframes this as "a part is in the way" or "Self is blended with a part." In a clinical setting this is useful; it gives the therapist a structured way to keep working when initial accessing fails. As an epistemic move it is unfalsifiable. There is no possible failure mode of the universal-Self claim, because absence of Self is always reattributed to interference from another part. A theory that cannot fail is a theory that cannot be tested.

The falsifiability map is more useful than a blanket judgment. IFS has *outcome* claims that are falsifiable: claims about whether people with a given condition improve under IFS treatment. These have been partly tested. The 2013 Shadick rheumatoid-arthritis RCT is the strongest single trial; the 2025 scoping review by Buys identified twenty-seven studies but only two RCTs. The evidence base is "promising" but not robust (Cat 4 takes this up in detail). IFS has *mechanism-adjacent* claims that are partly falsifiable: claims about whether Self-states produce distinguishable physiological signatures, or whether clients can identify recurring inner figures with stable characteristics. These could be tested with the right instruments, and partial support exists. And IFS has *mechanism* claims about parts as discrete entities and about Self universality that are unfalsifiable as currently formulated.

This is a serious critique. It is also a narrow one. It does not entail that IFS is wrong. Many clinical heuristics — the working alliance, transference, rumination as a process, even the concept of "rapport" — are not directly falsifiable in the strict Popperian sense, and we still find them clinically useful. What the falsifiability problem does entail is that "evidence-based" claims about IFS are claims about *outcomes*, not about *mechanism*. Saying "IFS is evidence-based" because pilots show clinical improvement is a reasonable claim about effectiveness. Saying "IFS is evidence-based" because parts are demonstrably real is overreach. These are different claims and they should be kept separate.

## The Steel-Manned IFS Position, and Where Critics Push Back

The strongest version of IFS goes like this. Minds are demonstrably plural at the descriptive level — the supporting literature is substantial. Treating that plurality as if it had structure (protectors, exiles, Self) gives clinicians a vocabulary for working with the inner life that produces measurable improvements in distress, self-compassion, and trauma symptoms in the conditions where it has been tested. Whether the structure is *literally* real (Position 3), a useful translation of underlying neural-and-affective states (Position 2), or a productive metaphor (Position 1) does not change the clinical leverage. The model's robustness to ontological commitment is a feature, because it lets practitioners and clients work at whatever level fits them. Forty years of clinical practice and a small but growing peer-reviewed literature support continuing to use the model.

This is a respectable position. It is also where the critique lands most cleanly.

If the ontology is genuinely flexible (Positions 1, 2, and 3 all "work"), then IFS Institute marketing language that commits to Position 3 — "These parts are not imaginary or symbolic" — is doing something more than describing flexible clinical practice. It is making a metaphysical claim that the rest of the model does not need and that the evidence does not support. This is not a small thing for a model whose canonical training is gatekept by a single Institute, whose certification is centralized under the founder, and whose foundational training costs four to five thousand dollars per level (Cat 5 takes the commercial dynamics in detail). The Position 3 framing also creates the conditions under which the move to Position 4 — Falconer's unattached burdens, Schwartz's spirituality course — becomes coherent. If parts are real entities, why couldn't some of them be external? The slippery slope here is not a fallacy; it is a description of the actual trajectory of the model in the past five years.

There is also the procedural unfalsifiability of the Self-blending escape. A clinical heuristic can be unfalsifiable and still useful. A *theoretical claim* about the universality of an undamaged Self that is unfalsifiable is something else — it is an article of faith dressed up as a scientific claim. For a systems thinker, the mismatch between the model's flexible clinical performance and its rigid metaphysical packaging is what produces the queasy feeling that something is being smuggled in.

## What This Means for the Rest of the Deep Dive

The ontology question forwards into two later pages.

**Cat 5 (Critiques, Controversies, and Commercial Dynamics)** examines the *consequences* of the unfalsifiability problem in the IFS Institute's commercial structure: centralized certification, expensive paywalled training, the "no bad parts" near-tautology, and the Schwartz-as-founder/face/IP-holder pattern. The ontology question is one of several critique points; Cat 5 sets it alongside the others.

**Cat 11 (Psychedelic-Assisted IFS, Spirituality, and the "Unattached Burdens" Turn)** picks up Position 4 in detail. It examines Falconer's book, Schwartz's *Spirituality of IFS* course, the MAPS MDMA-PTSD trials' finding that roughly eighty percent of high-dose participants spontaneously generated parts language without prompting, and the question of whether what is currently being taught at the IFS Institute is best described as a clinical psychotherapy or as a clinical-spiritual hybrid.

For now, the operational guidance is this. When you read IFS material, ask which position it is operating at. When a clinician describes their practice, ask which position they hold and how settled they are in it. When a training advertises Schwartz's authorship, ask which Schwartz era it is drawing from. The model has changed shape across forty years; "what IFS claims" depends substantially on which IFS you mean.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Subpersonality | A semi-autonomous psychological pattern with its own perspective, often treated as a discrete inner figure (Assagioli's term, adopted broadly) |
| Multiplicity of mind | The thesis that the mind is naturally plural rather than unitary |
| Self (IFS) | The undamaged core agency in the IFS model, characterized by curiosity, calm, compassion, clarity, courage, confidence, creativity, connectedness |
| Complex (Jung) | A psychic fragment clustered around an archetype, acting autonomously |
| Ego state (Watkins / Berne) | A discriminable state of the personality with its own mood, behavior, and memory access |
| Hidden observer | Hilgard's term for a dissociated stream of consciousness that can report on experiences the main awareness does not register |
| Agent (Minsky) | A simple processing unit in *Society of Mind* — mindless individually, intelligent collectively |
| Falsifiability | Karl Popper's criterion that a scientific claim must specify what observations would disconfirm it |
| Discriminant validity | Whether a measurement instrument can distinguish the construct it claims to measure from related-but-distinct constructs |
| Unattached burden | Falconer's term (endorsed by Schwartz) for an inner content described as not part of the client's own history but a foreign or transpersonal intrusion |
