# Historical Development — From Middle Chinese to Putonghua

How a Thousand Years of Sound Change and a Single Government Directive Created the World's Most-Spoken Standardized Language

---

## 1. The Core Idea

Contemporary Mandarin -- specifically the variety codified as Putonghua (普通话, "common speech") in the People's Republic of China -- is not an ancient language transmitted unchanged across millennia. It is a **deliberately constructed standard** based on a specific dialect (Beijing speech), codified by a specific political act (the 1956 State Council directive), and promoted through one of the most ambitious language planning campaigns in human history. It took approximately 70 years to go from government decree to ~80% national comprehension -- a remarkably fast achievement for a country of 1.4 billion spanning dozens of mutually unintelligible dialect groups.

The three-way comparison reveals something counterintuitive:

- **Against Traditional Chinese (Cantonese)**: Mandarin is the *most changed* variety from the shared ancestor (Middle Chinese). Cantonese preserves more tonal distinctions, more final consonants, and more of the original sound inventory. The "standard" was chosen for political reasons, not because it was the most conservative or "original."
- **Against English**: English standardization was gradual, decentralized, and market-driven -- no single government body ever defined "Standard English" in a directive. The contrast illuminates how profoundly political systems shape language planning.

Understanding how Putonghua got to where it is today requires tracing three threads: (1) the phonological changes that turned Middle Chinese into Mandarin, (2) the political decisions that chose and defined the standard, and (3) the campaign that spread it across the world's most populous country.

## 2. Middle Chinese: The Reconstructed Ancestor

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-1-triple-timeline -- Three parallel tracks: Mandarin sound changes, Cantonese conservation, English evolution. Shows Mandarin as most innovative. -->

Middle Chinese (中古汉语, zhongggu Hanyyu) is the ancestor of all modern Chinese dialect groups -- Mandarin, Wu, Min, Cantonese, Hakka, Gan, and Xiang. It is a *reconstructed* language: no one alive speaks it, and no audio recordings exist. Our knowledge comes primarily from the **Qieyun** (切韵, 601 CE), a rhyme dictionary compiled by Lu Fayan and eight other scholars that records the pronunciation of ~11,500 characters organized by tone and rhyme category.

The Qieyun used the *fanqie* (反切) method: each character's pronunciation was described by two other characters, one giving the initial consonant and the other the final plus tone. This is like describing the pronunciation of "cat" by saying "the *c* of 'cup' plus the *at* of 'bat.'" Additional evidence comes from Buddhist transliterations of Sanskrit, from modern dialect comparisons, and from Sino-Korean, Sino-Japanese, and Sino-Vietnamese borrowings that preserve snapshots of Chinese pronunciation at the time of borrowing.

### What Middle Chinese Sounded Like

Middle Chinese had a substantially richer sound system than modern Mandarin:

| Feature | Middle Chinese | Modern Mandarin | Cantonese |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Tone categories** | 4 categories x yin/yang = 8 distinctions | 4 tones | 6-9 tones |
| **Entering tone (-p, -t, -k)** | Present | **Completely lost** | Preserved |
| **Voiced obstruents** | Full series (b, d, g, dz...) | **Lost** | Partially preserved |
| **Final consonants** | -p, -t, -k, -m, -n, -ng | Only -n, -ng | All six preserved |
| **Number of initials** | ~36 | 21 | 19 |
| **Number of finals** | ~200+ categories | ~35 | ~53 |

The pattern is stark: Modern Mandarin represents a **massive simplification** of the Middle Chinese sound system. It lost final stop consonants entirely, lost the entering tone category, lost voiced obstruents, and reduced tonal and rhyme distinctions dramatically. The result is approximately 1,300 distinct syllable slots in Mandarin (including tones), compared with ~3,500+ in Middle Chinese and ~1,700+ in Cantonese. This is why modern Mandarin relies so heavily on disyllabic compounds to disambiguate -- a structural consequence of historical sound change.

## 3. The Sound Change Cascade

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-3-tone-reduction-cascade -- MC 8 tones mapped to Mandarin 4 and Cantonese 6-9. Entering-tone chaotic redistribution. Character examples. -->

The major sound changes that transformed Middle Chinese into Mandarin occurred over roughly 600-800 years (approximately 1000-1600 CE). Each change is independently documented and well-established in the scholarly literature.

### The Five Big Changes

**1. Loss of entering tone (~1000-1400 CE).** The entering tone (入声, rusheng) was defined by syllables ending in unreleased stop consonants: -p, -t, -k. These syllables were short and clipped. In Mandarin, the final stops disappeared entirely, and the entering-tone syllables were redistributed among the four remaining tones -- but with no regular pattern. 六 (six) became liu4, 十 (ten) became shi2, 百 (hundred) became bai3, 國 (country) became guo2. The redistribution is chaotic -- there is no rule telling you which tone a former entering-tone character landed in. Cantonese speakers can immediately identify entering-tone characters (六 = luk6, 十 = sap6, 國 = gwok3) because the final stops survive.

**2. Loss of voiced obstruents (~1000-1300 CE).** Middle Chinese had true voiced consonants b, d, g (like English "b" in "bat"). These merged with voiceless counterparts, splitting by aspiration depending on tone: some became aspirated (p, t, k), others unaspirated (b, d, g in Pinyin, which are actually voiceless unaspirated). This is why Mandarin has zh/ch, z/c, b/p, d/t, g/k pairs (aspiration contrast) rather than the voiced/voiceless contrast found in English.

**3. Loss of final -m (~1300-1600 CE).** Middle Chinese -m merged with -n in Mandarin. 三 (three): Middle Chinese *sam became Mandarin san, but Cantonese saam1 preserves the -m. 南 (south): Middle Chinese *nam became Mandarin nan, but Cantonese naam4. This is why some Cantonese speakers find certain Mandarin rhyme distinctions confusing -- characters that rhyme in Mandarin (ending in -n) may not rhyme in Cantonese (some end in -m, others in -n).

**4. Tone mergers and splits (~1000-1500 CE).** The 8 Middle Chinese tonal distinctions collapsed to 4 tones through a series of mergers. The entering-tone syllables scattered across all four remaining tones. Cantonese preserves 6-9 tonal distinctions (counting entering tones separately), making it far closer to the Middle Chinese system.

**5. Palatalization (~1400-1600 CE).** Middle Chinese k, kh, x before front vowels became j, q, x -- a sound change that created the modern Mandarin j/q/x series. 金 (gold): Middle Chinese *kim became Mandarin jin, but Cantonese gam1 preserves the original k-initial. This series does not exist in Cantonese.

### The Great Vowel Shift Parallel

English experienced an analogous phonological upheaval: the Great Vowel Shift (~1400-1700), which systematically raised all long vowels by one position in the mouth. The word "bite" changed from something like "beet" to its modern pronunciation; "house" changed from something like "hoose." Both Mandarin and English underwent dramatic sound changes that their writing systems did not fully reflect -- but the consequences diverged. English spelling, which froze during this shift, became notoriously "irregular." Chinese characters, which encode meaning rather than sound, were unaffected in their fundamental logic.

## 4. From Guanhua to Putonghua: The Political Decisions

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-2-standardization-milestones -- 10 milestones from 1913 to 2020, Republic and PRC eras, comprehension rates. -->

### The Nanjing-to-Beijing Shift

The term Guanhua (官话, "official speech") referred to the prestige spoken standard used by government officials from roughly the Ming dynasty (1368-1644) onward. During the early Ming, this was based on **Nanjing pronunciation** -- Nanjing was the cultural and economic center of the Jiangnan region, and early European descriptions of "Mandarin" by Matteo Ricci (1583) reflect Nanjing-based speech. As Beijing consolidated its position as political capital through the Qing dynasty (1644-1912), **Beijing pronunciation gradually displaced Nanjing pronunciation** as the prestige standard.

This is analogous to the shift in English from various regional prestige standards to London-based Received Pronunciation -- but in English, this was never codified in a government decree. In Chinese, it eventually was.

### The 1913 Conference: A Failed Hybrid

The Republic of China convened a Conference on Unification of Pronunciation in 1913 -- the first formal attempt to create a single spoken standard. Northern delegates wanted a Beijing base; southern delegates wanted southern features included. The compromise was an artificial hybrid called "Old National Pronunciation" (老国音) that included both Beijing tones and a preserved entering tone (from Nanjing). This standard was unworkable: no one naturally spoke it, teachers could not teach it, and recordings could not be standardized.

### The 1932 Pivot

In 1932, the Ministry of Education quietly abandoned the hybrid standard and issued a new vocabulary guide based purely on Beijing pronunciation. The entering tone was dropped. This "New National Pronunciation" (新国音) established the principle that has governed Chinese standardization ever since: **Beijing phonology as the base**.

The phonetic notation system Zhuyin Fuhao (注音符号, now called Bopomofo: BoPoMoFo) had been adopted at the 1913 conference and remains Taiwan's primary phonetic system today.

### 1949: The Name Change

When the PRC was established, the new government chose a different name for the standard: Putonghua (普通话, "common speech") instead of the Republic's Guoyu (国语, "national language"). The stated reason was ideological -- "national language" implied a single nation, while "common speech" suggested a shared lingua franca in a multilingual state. After 1949, the two standards evolved independently: PRC Putonghua with simplified characters and Pinyin, Taiwan Guoyu with traditional characters and Bopomofo.

## 5. The 1956 Definition: Three Pillars

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-4-three-pillars -- Architecture diagram showing three pillars supporting Putonghua, with includes/excludes for each. -->

On February 6, 1956, the State Council issued the directive that officially defined Putonghua:

> "以北京语音为标准音，以北方话为基础方言，以典范的现代白话文著作为语法规范"
>
> "Taking Beijing pronunciation as the standard pronunciation, northern dialects as the base dialect, and exemplary modern vernacular literary works as the grammatical norm."

This three-pillar definition is a masterpiece of language engineering:

**Pillar 1 -- Beijing Phonology (北京语音为标准音)**: The sound system (tones, initials, finals) comes from Beijing dialect. Crucially, this means Beijing *phonology*, not Beijing *dialect*. Heavy Beijing erhua, local slang, and casual contractions are excluded. Putonghua is an idealized version of Beijing pronunciation.

**Pillar 2 -- Northern Dialect Base (北方话为基础方言)**: Grammar and core vocabulary come from the broader northern dialect group (~800 million speakers across northern China), not just Beijing. This prevents the standard from being purely a "Beijing language."

**Pillar 3 -- Vernacular Literary Norms (典范的现代白话文著作为语法规范)**: Grammatical correctness is judged against exemplary modern vernacular writing -- the works of Lu Xun, Mao Dun, Lao She, and others. This provides a stable reference point. When spoken Beijing drifts (as all spoken language does), the written standard wins.

### Why Three Pillars?

Each pillar compensates for the others' weaknesses:
- Beijing dialect alone would include slang and excessive erhua
- Northern dialects alone would be too vague (which northern dialect?)
- Literary norms alone would be a written-only standard with no spoken model

The three-pillar approach gives a clear phonological model (Beijing), a broad grammatical base (northern), and a stable normative reference (literature).

**English has no equivalent.** No government body has ever defined Standard English in a single document. The closest analogs -- BBC pronunciation guidelines, Oxford style guides, AP style -- are advisory, not legally binding.

## 6. Spreading the Standard: 54% to 80%

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-5-comprehension-growth -- Line chart with policy annotations and English comparison. -->

Putonghua promotion has been one of the PRC's longest-running social campaigns:

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1956 | Putonghua defined |
| 1958 | Hanyu Pinyin adopted as teaching tool |
| 1982 | Constitution Article 19: "The state promotes nationwide use of Putonghua" |
| 1994 | Putonghua Proficiency Test (PSC) established |
| 2000 | National Common Language Law: legal requirement for broadcasting, education |
| 2001 | Proficiency required for teachers, broadcasters, civil servants |

### The Numbers

Self-reported comprehension rates tell the story:
- **~54%** (1960s) -- roughly half the population could understand Putonghua
- **53%** (2000) -- essentially flat for decades (disrupted by the Cultural Revolution)
- **~70%** (2010) -- rapid acceleration begins
- **80.7%** (2020) -- official State Language Commission figure
- **85%** (2025 target)

The stagnation from the 1960s to 2000 reflects both Cultural Revolution disruption and the enormous challenge of reaching rural populations in China's south and west. The acceleration after 2000 was driven by four converging forces: (1) compulsory Putonghua-medium education, (2) mass urbanization and internal migration, (3) national media dominance (CCTV, internet), and (4) economic incentive -- Putonghua fluency opens the national job market.

Important caveat: these are self-reported survey figures. The criteria for "can communicate in Putonghua" have varied, and actual functional fluency may be lower than reported, especially in rural areas.

## 7. Cross-Strait Divergence

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-6-cross-strait-divergence -- 1932 shared standard, 1949 split, independent evolution, 1990s reconvergence. -->

The 1949 split created a natural experiment in independent language evolution. PRC Putonghua and Taiwan Guoyu share the same 1932 Beijing-based standard but diverged across multiple dimensions:

| Dimension | PRC Putonghua | Taiwan Guoyu | Divergence Level |
|-----------|--------------|--------------|-----------------|
| **Characters** | Simplified | Traditional | HIGH |
| **Phonetic system** | Pinyin (1958) | Bopomofo (1918) | HIGH |
| **Vocabulary** | Semantic calques (激光 laser = "stimulated light") | Phonetic transliteration (雷射 laser = sound borrowing) | MODERATE (~2,000+ differences) |
| **Pronunciation** | More erhua, stronger retroflexes | Less erhua, weaker retroflexes | LOW-MODERATE |
| **Grammar** | Essentially the same | Essentially the same | LOW |

The deepest divergence is in deliberate policy choices (character set, phonetic notation). Vocabulary differences are real but shallow -- both sides can understand the other's words from context. Grammar is nearly identical. The two varieties remain fully mutually intelligible in conversation.

Since the 1990s, internet contact and shared entertainment media have driven significant reconvergence, particularly in vocabulary and slang. PRC netizens adopt Taiwan expressions and vice versa.

## 8. The Conservation Gradient

One of the most important insights for understanding Contemporary Mandarin's place in the Chinese language family:

**Most conservative ←→ Most innovative**

Min → Cantonese → Hakka → Wu → Xiang → Mandarin

Mandarin is the MOST innovative variety -- the one that has changed the most from the historical ancestor. It became the standard not because of linguistic conservatism but because of **political power**: the northern capital regions where Mandarin evolved happened to be the seats of the Ming and Qing dynasties, and later the Republican and PRC governments.

This is counterintuitive: the "standard" language is actually the most changed one. It is analogous to how Modern English, which underwent the Great Vowel Shift, the Great Plurality, and massive French/Latin vocabulary infusion, is far more changed from Proto-Germanic than Icelandic -- but English became the global standard through political and economic power, not linguistic conservatism.

## 9. Why This Matters for Learning

1. **Putonghua is engineered, not inherited.** When you learn Putonghua, you are learning a deliberately designed standard, not a "natural" dialect. The three-pillar definition means the standard has clear, articulable rules.

2. **Homophony has structural causes.** The reason Mandarin has so many homophones (and thus relies on disyllabic compounds, context, and characters for disambiguation) is a direct consequence of the massive sound-system simplification from Middle Chinese. This is not a "flaw" -- it is a historical outcome.

3. **Characters are more phonetically useful than they seem** -- for older stages of the language. The phonetic component in semantic-phonetic compounds often reflects Middle Chinese pronunciation. If a phonetic component seems to "not work" in Mandarin, it may have worked perfectly 1,000 years ago (and may still work in Cantonese).

4. **Cross-strait differences are manageable.** If you learn Putonghua, you can understand Guoyu with minimal adjustment. Vocabulary differences are learned through exposure, not systematic study.

## Common Misconceptions

**"Mandarin is the oldest/most original form of Chinese."** False. Mandarin is the MOST changed variety from Middle Chinese. Southern varieties like Cantonese and Min are more conservative.

**"Putonghua IS Beijing dialect."** False. Putonghua is based on Beijing phonology but explicitly excludes Beijing slang, heavy erhua, and local colloquialisms. The three-pillar definition separates phonology from dialect.

**"The entering tone disappeared because it was 'hard'."** Sound changes are not driven by difficulty. All languages change; the specific changes in Mandarin followed regular phonological processes (final stop lenition, coda simplification) that occur in many languages worldwide.

**"Taiwan speaks a completely different language from the mainland."** False. Putonghua and Guoyu are mutually intelligible varieties of the same standard. Differences are real but shallow, concentrated in vocabulary and pronunciation details rather than grammar.

## Glossary

- **Middle Chinese (中古汉语)**: The reconstructed ancestor language (~600-1200 CE) of all modern Chinese dialect groups
- **Qieyun (切韵)**: The 601 CE rhyme dictionary that is our primary source for Middle Chinese pronunciation
- **Fanqie (反切)**: The method of describing pronunciation using two reference characters
- **Entering tone (入声, rusheng)**: A tone category defined by final stop consonants (-p, -t, -k), lost in Mandarin but preserved in Cantonese
- **Guanhua (官话)**: "Official speech" -- the prestige spoken standard of imperial officials
- **Putonghua (普通话)**: "Common speech" -- the PRC's officially defined standard Mandarin
- **Guoyu (国语)**: "National language" -- Taiwan's name for its standard Mandarin
- **Three pillars**: Beijing phonology + northern dialect base + vernacular literary norms = the 1956 definition of Putonghua
- **Zhuyin/Bopomofo (注音符号)**: Taiwan's phonetic notation system, adopted 1918
- **Hanyu Pinyin**: The PRC's romanization system, adopted 1958
- **PSC (普通话水平测试)**: The Putonghua Proficiency Test, established 1994
