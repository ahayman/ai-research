### Claim 2: VDS founded in 1997 by statistician Walter Krämer, originally as "Verein zur Wahrung der deutschen Sprache," renamed 2000
- **Source text**: "Founded 1997 by statistician Walter Krämer (originally as Verein zur Wahrung der deutschen Sprache, renamed 2000)"
- **Verification**: Walter Krämer is a professor of economic and social statistics at TU Dortmund. The VDS was founded in 1997 as "Verein zur Wahrung der deutschen Sprache" (VWDS) and renamed to Verein Deutsche Sprache in April 2000. All details confirmed.
- **Minor note**: Calling Krämer a "statistician" is acceptable shorthand; he is more precisely a professor of economic and social statistics (an economist-statistician). Not an error, just slightly imprecise.
- **Rating**: **CONFIRMED**

- **Verification**: Confirmed. In June 2013, Deutsche Bahn issued a glossary of 2,200 anglicisms to be replaced by German equivalents. The claim about "recruiting managers from Lufthansa" is not independently verified in search results but is plausible given Mehdorn's background (he previously served on the Lufthansa board).
- **Note**: The research says "with the aim of a stock market launch" — this is confirmed; Mehdorn pursued an IPO for Deutsche Bahn.
- **Rating**: **CONFIRMED** (confidence 9/10 — minor ambiguity about Mehdorn's direct role in the anglicization vs. the glossary timing)

### Claim 7: AfD proposed in 2018 to amend the Basic Law to include German as official language
- **Source text**: "The AfD proposed in 2018 to amend the Basic Law to include German as the official state language. Other parties opposed it; a two-thirds majority was unachievable."
- **Verification**: Confirmed. On 2 March 2018, the Bundestag debated the topic of adding the German language to the Basic Law, prompted by an AfD motion. The proposal failed because a two-thirds majority was required and other parliamentary groups opposed it. ### Claim 10: Advertising statistics — English slogans on slogans.de grew from ~2% in the 1980s to 18% in the 1990s, 30% in the early 2000s, and over 55% today
- **Source text**: "On the database Slogans.de, English-language slogans in German advertising grew from ~2% in the 1980s to 18% in the 1990s, 30% in the early 2000s, and over 55% today."
- **Verification**: The TESOL Working Paper (Partsch 2017) and multiple secondary sources referencing Slogans.de data confirm: 1,108 slogans in the 1980s with only 22 in English (which is ~2%), 18% in the 1990s, 30% in the early 2000s, and "over half (55%)" today. All figures match.
- **Additional claim**: "Only 6 of 20 leading German brands use German-language slogans" — Confirmed by the Endmark study referenced in The Local (2016).
- **Rating**: **CONFIRMED**

### Claim 13: All borrowed English verbs enter German as weak (regular) verbs — described as a "universal principle"
- **Source text**: "All borrowed English verbs are conjugated as weak (regular) verbs — no borrowed verb ever enters the strong (irregular) class. This is a universal principle."
- **Verification**: The claim that borrowed verbs default to the weak class is **strongly supported** for modern German and modern borrowing. Historically, there are rare exceptions (e.g., Proto-Germanic borrowed Latin *scrībō* as strong verb *skrībaną*). For contemporary English-to-German borrowing, the claim holds without exception: *googeln, downloaden, surfen, chillen* are all weak. The characterization as a "universal principle" is standard in contact linguistics (the productive class absorbs loans).
- **Rating**: **CONFIRMED** (with the caveat that "universal" is slightly overstated for historical cases, but accurate for modern borrowing)

- **Source text**: "Approximately 2,000–3,000 Western vocabulary items had to be absorbed rapidly"

The reverse direction (West → East terms absorbed) is harder to quantify. The claim is directionally correct (a large number of unfamiliar terms) but the specific number may be inflated or based on a single uncited source.

### Claim 17: East German loanwords — Datsche from Russian dacha, Broiler, Plaste
- **Source text**: "Datsche (country house, ← Russian dacha), Broiler (grilled chicken), Plaste (plastic, ← Russian plast)"
- **Verification**: Confirmed. All three are well-attested East German vocabulary items:
  - **Datsche** — from Russian дача (dacha), a countryside plot with a summer house
  - **Broiler** — grilled/roasted chicken (actually from English via French, not directly from Russian as the text implies with "French via Russian"; in East Germany it replaced "Brathähnchen")
  - **Plaste** — East German term for plastic (replaced by "Plastik" after reunification)
- **Minor note**: The research says "Broiler ← French via Russian for grilled chicken." The actual etymology is more complex — "Broiler" is an English word that entered East German usage, possibly through trade/food terminology. It is not straightforwardly "French via Russian." This is a minor etymological imprecision.
- **Rating**: **CONFIRMED** (with minor etymological caveat on Broiler)
