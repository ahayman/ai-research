# The Double Empathy Problem

## Tier 1 Introduction: The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

> When communication between an autistic and a non-autistic person breaks down, both people are failing to understand each other. The burden of that failure has historically been assigned to one side. The evidence does not support that assignment.

If you are autistic and you have spent your life being told — implicitly by silences, explicitly by reports, cruelly by playgrounds, bureaucratically by assessments — that you are the one who cannot read people, cannot hold conversations, cannot manage social situations, cannot "do" empathy, then this page exists to tell you what the last decade of research has actually found.

The communication breakdowns you have experienced with non-autistic people are not one-sided autistic failures. They are mutual failures: bidirectional mismatches in shared social understanding, communication norms, sensory-attentional orientation, and expectations about what a conversation is for. When an autistic person and a non-autistic person talk and something goes wrong, both parties have failed to successfully model the other's mind. Both have misread cues. Both have made assumptions that did not survive contact with the other person. Historically, only one side of that failure has been labeled, diagnosed, and assigned homework. The other side has been treated as the invisible default, as "how communication works" rather than as one particular communicative style among several.

In 2012, Damian Milton, an autistic sociologist, published a four-page paper in *Disability & Society* that named this phenomenon. He called it the **double empathy problem**, and it reframed a field. Milton's argument was brutally simple. The dominant account of autistic communication, built around the "theory-of-mind deficit" hypothesis, predicted one-way misunderstanding: autistic people should fail to read neurotypical minds, while neurotypical people, with their intact modules, should read autistic minds just fine. Milton asked: if that is the prediction, why do non-autistic people also consistently fail to understand autistic people? Why do they routinely describe autistic communication as "odd," "flat," "inappropriate," "strange," "cold," every one of those adjectives being a report of a failed interpretation, not the autistic person's but the observer's? The answer, Milton argued, is that understanding between minds is relational. It depends on sufficient overlap in how two people perceive, attend, remember, and expect. When the overlap is small, the understanding is small, and it is small for both of them.

Since 2012, that argument has become an empirical research program. Three lines of evidence, from different labs using different methods, converge on the same conclusion.

The first is about first impressions. When non-autistic observers watch brief clips of autistic adults, they form negative judgments within milliseconds, rating the autistic targets as less likable, less intelligent, and less desirable as interaction partners. But when those same observers read transcripts of what the autistic targets said, with no audiovisual cues, the negative bias evaporates. The bias is not tracking anything the autistic person is saying. It is tracking how they look and sound while they say it: style cues the observers have not learned to interpret, mistaken for reliable information about the person. And when observers are told the target is autistic, the bias attenuates substantially, because the disclosure activates knowledge that partly corrects for the mismatch. The negativity is not reading an essential deficit. It is reading a cognitive-environmental gap.

The second is about what happens when autistic people communicate with each other. In Crompton and colleagues' diffusion-chain experiments, participants passed a story along a chain of eight people, some chains all-autistic, some all-non-autistic, some mixed. The deficit prediction is clear: if autistic people have a pragmatic impairment, all-autistic chains should lose the most information. That is not what happened. All-autistic chains lost information at roughly the same rate as all-non-autistic chains. The mixed chains were where information degraded. Autistic-to-autistic transfer was comparably effective to non-autistic-to-non-autistic. The breakdown was a property of the cross-neurotype interface, not of either group alone. The deficit account cannot explain this result. The double empathy account can.

The third is about mindreading running both ways. When researchers asked non-autistic observers to infer mental states from body movements produced by autistic actors, the observers were substantially worse than when they read movements from non-autistic actors. The "mind-blindness" that the deficit account assigned to autistic people was present in non-autistic observers looking at autistic targets. And when autistic targets' expressions were evaluated, they were rated as roughly equally expressive, not under-reacting but reacting differently, in ways non-autistic observers had not learned to decode. The reading problem is not inside autistic bodies. It is at the interface between differently-wired minds.

Three different research programs, three different methodologies, one consistent finding: the communication gap between autistic and non-autistic people is bidirectional, and it is driven by the mismatch between cognitive environments, not by a deficit in either one.

So what does this mean for you?

It means reinterpretation. The standard story about your past conversations was: *I said something wrong. I missed a cue. I failed to read them. I am socially broken.* The double empathy interpretation is: *Something was mismatched between my communication style and theirs. I may have missed what they implied. They may have missed what I actually meant. Both failures happened. Both were reasonable given what each of us had to work with. The one that got labeled was mine, and that labeling was not empirically justified.* This is not a dodge, and it is not "nothing is ever your fault." Specific communication repairs are still worth making: clarifying, asking follow-ups, explaining your meaning when you notice drift. But the global explanation for why so many conversations went wrong is not that you are the broken one. It is that you were interacting across a cognitive-environmental gap that neither party knew how to bridge, and the entire apparatus around you assigned the blame to your side because your side was the minority.

It means strategy. Autistic peer spaces are a legitimate strategic resource, not a retreat. The data shows that communication between autistic people is often more effective, more disclosing, and more affiliation-building than communication across the neurotype gap. If conversations with other autistic people have always felt easier and more "real" to you, that impression is backed by evidence, and acting on it is strategically rational. Disclosure often reduces the thin-slice negativity. In contexts where it is safe, telling people you are autistic frequently improves how they respond to you. And asking non-autistic interlocutors to make implicit things explicit, to say directly what they mean instead of relying on unstated implicature, is not rude, not demanding, not "making it about you." It is a legitimate request. The interpretive work of reaching mutual understanding is supposed to be shared. If it has historically been offloaded entirely onto you, that was not the arrangement the evidence supports.

It means safety. When someone consistently blames you for "not understanding" them while making no reciprocal effort to understand you, when they always position your interpretations as wrong and theirs as obvious, they are performing an asymmetric interpretive demand that the evidence does not support. That asymmetry is one of the structures underlying gaslighting, tone policing, diagnostic weaponization, and pragmatic-packaging invalidation, patterns the manipulation sub-hub treats in detail. The double empathy framing is the load-bearing move that lets you recognize the asymmetry as an attack rather than as your own failure. Not every asymmetric demand is intentional manipulation. Some of it is ordinary people operating inside a framework that taught them autistic minds are broken, and the miscommunication-versus-manipulation page is the place to learn the difference. But the recognition is the same: the demand was not justified, and you are allowed to name that.

It also does not mean you have nothing to learn. You have plenty to learn: about how non-autistic pragmatics works, about what implicatures and hedges and indirect speech acts are doing, about how to recognize when a conversation is drifting into a mismatch zone. This entire hub exists to teach that. What the reframe changes is not whether you learn, but the story you carry while you learn. You are acquiring a second communicative dialect that you can deploy strategically, not remediating a broken faculty.

The evidence base is strong but not finished. Some of the findings on this page have been replicated multiple times across labs. Others are more preliminary and would benefit from larger samples, more diverse populations, and replication across cultural contexts. The double empathy framework does not mean communication problems are fictitious. They are real and they hurt. It does not mean every autistic-autistic interaction is automatically easy. Individual variation within the autistic population is enormous. And it does not mean autism reduces to a communication difference. Autism is a complex neurodevelopmental condition with dimensions far beyond pragmatics. What the framework does is correct a systematic misattribution in the communicative dimension specifically: the misattribution that placed the full burden of interpretive failure on the autistic side of every conversation.

The correction is empirical, not sentimental. The decade of research since Milton's 2012 paper has made it harder, not easier, to maintain the deficit story. You do not have to maintain it either. You are not broken. You are one half of a mismatch that was misdescribed as your broken half. Every page in this hub is built on that reframe.

If you want to explore how the double empathy problem plays out in specific pragmatic domains, the hub's category research-autism files each build on this foundation. The [relevance theory page](tier-1-relevance-theory.md) formalizes the cognitive mechanism. The [cooperative principle page](tier-1-cooperative-principle.md) explains the conversational rules that get misapplied across the neurotype gap. The [manipulation sub-hub](manipulation-hub-safety-assessment.md) uses this reframe as the basis for recognizing pragmatic attack.

## Deep Dive

### Milton (2012) in detail

Damian Milton's 2012 paper, published in *Disability & Society* (volume 27, issue 6, pages 883–887), is only four pages long. It reframed a field.

The paper's target was the dominant psychological and clinical story about autism that had congealed in the 1980s and 1990s: the **theory-of-mind deficit account**. That account, associated with Baron-Cohen, Leslie and Frith's 1985 Sally-Anne experiments and their descendants, held that autistic people have a specific impairment in the ability to attribute mental states (beliefs, desires, intentions) to other people. On that account, when an autistic person misreads a non-autistic person's intention, the explanation is cognitive damage inside the autistic person's head. A broken module. A missing capacity.

Milton's move was to ask: **if the theory-of-mind deficit account is correct, why do non-autistic people also consistently fail to understand autistic people?**

The theory predicts one-way misunderstanding. Autistic people should fail to read neurotypical minds; neurotypical people, with their intact modules, should read autistic minds just fine. But that is not what the data shows. Non-autistic people misread autistic people's intentions, expressions, emotional states, and meanings all the time. They routinely describe autistic communication as "odd," "flat," "inappropriate," "strange," "cold," "rude," "rigid." Every one of those adjectives is a report of a failed interpretation. Not the autistic person's. The non-autistic person's.

Milton's claim is that both failures are happening simultaneously. The mismatch is bidirectional. The theory-of-mind framework catalogued only one half of it because the framework was built by and for non-autistic researchers studying autistic people as objects. The other half, non-autistic people failing to read autistic minds, was invisible to the framework because it was the framework's blind spot.

He gave the phenomenon a name: the **double empathy problem**. "Empathy" here does not mean "feeling another person's emotions." It means something closer to *mentalizing* or *mindreading*: the act of successfully modeling another mind well enough to predict what they mean, what they want, and what they will do next. The problem Milton describes is that when two minds with substantially different processing styles, sensory profiles, interests, and communication conventions attempt to model each other, both models underperform. Each side experiences the other as hard to read. Each side fills the gap with unflattering inference. Each side walks away confused or hurt. The same phenomenon is happening twice, symmetrically, and only one side of it has been pathologized.

Milton's definition in the paper itself is precise: "a disjuncture in reciprocity between two differently disposed social actors which becomes more marked the wider the disjuncture in dispositional perceptions of the lifeworld." Translated: when two people inhabit meaningfully different phenomenal worlds (different sensory experiences, different attentional priorities, different communicative conventions), their ability to reciprocally understand each other degrades, and the degradation is proportional to the size of the gap between their worlds.

The paper also made a harder point. Milton argued that the very framework that located the deficit in autistic minds was itself a product of the disjuncture: non-autistic researchers interpreting autistic behavior through their own cognitive environment and seeing what was strange to them, not what was wrong with the target. The theory-of-mind account, on this reading, was itself an artifact of the double empathy problem. It was non-autistic mindreading of autistic minds failing, and then being reified as "autistic mindreading failure."

### The evidence base

The studies below are the ones this hub leans on most heavily. They are not the whole literature, but they are the load-bearing ones.

#### Thin-slice judgment studies

**Sasson, Faso, Nugent, Lovell, Kennedy and Grossman (2017).** "Neurotypical peers are less willing to interact with those with autism based on thin slice judgments." *Scientific Reports*, 7, article 40700.

This is probably the most cited piece of empirical evidence for double empathy outside Milton's own work. Sasson and colleagues filmed autistic and non-autistic adults in brief social scenarios. They then showed these clips, some as short as a few seconds, to non-autistic observers and asked them to rate the targets on traits like intelligence, likability, awkwardness, attractiveness, and whether they would want to interact with them.

The findings were striking and have been replicated in follow-ups:

- Non-autistic observers rated autistic targets substantially less favorably on almost every dimension.
- The effect was present within **milliseconds of exposure**, before the observers had processed any content of what the autistic targets were saying.
- The effect persisted when the exposure was longer.
- Critically, **the effect disappeared when the same content was presented as transcript with no audiovisual cues**. When observers could only read what the autistic targets had said, without seeing their faces, hearing their voices, or watching their movements, the negative bias evaporated.

That last finding is the double empathy smoking gun. The negative first impression was not caused by anything autistic targets were saying. It was caused by how they looked and sounded while they said it, the style of expression rather than the substance of thought.

**Sasson and Morrison (2019).** "First impressions of adults with autism improve with diagnostic disclosure and increased autism knowledge of peers." *Autism*, 23(1), 50–59.

The follow-up showed that when observers were told the target was autistic, their impressions became significantly more favorable. Observer-side autism knowledge also predicted more favorable impressions independently. This shows the thin-slice negativity is not tracking some essential "autistic incompetence"; if it were, disclosure would not help. It also makes disclosure a practical tool: in contexts where it is safe, it often improves how people respond to you.

**DeBrabander, Morrison, Jones, Faso, Chmielewski and Sasson (2019).** "Do first impressions of autistic adults differ between autistic and nonautistic observers?" *Autism in Adulthood*, 1(4), 250–257.

This study closes the loop. If thin-slice negativity is about observer-side mismatch rather than target-side deficit, it should look different when the observers are autistic. DeBrabander et al. showed the same clips to autistic and non-autistic observers. Autistic observers were **substantially more interested in future interaction with autistic targets** than non-autistic observers were. The asymmetric interaction preference that non-autistic observers showed was largely absent in autistic observers, or ran in the opposite direction. The thin-slice effect is not an impartial read of "who is socially competent." It is observer-side affinity for targets who look and sound like the observer.

#### Autistic peer-to-peer communication

**Crompton, Ropar, Evans-Williams, Flynn and Fletcher-Watson (2020).** "Autistic peer-to-peer information transfer is highly effective." *Autism*, 24(7), 1704–1712.

Crompton and colleagues used a "diffusion chain" methodology, a telephone game where a story is passed along a chain of eight people. They compared all-autistic chains, all-non-autistic chains, and mixed chains.

**All-autistic chains lost information at roughly the same rate as all-non-autistic chains.** The big information loss happened in the mixed chains. Autistic-to-autistic information transfer was comparably effective to non-autistic-to-non-autistic. The breakdown was a property of the cross-neurotype interface, not of either group alone. Participants in the mixed chains also rated their interactions as having lower rapport.

**Morrison, DeBrabander, Jones, Faso, Ackerman and Sasson (2020).** "Outcomes of real-world social interaction for autistic adults paired with autistic compared to typically developing partners." *Autism*, 24(5), 1067–1080.

Morrison and colleagues paired adults in three kinds of dyads for unstructured five-minute conversations. Autistic participants in autistic-autistic dyads disclosed more about themselves and trended toward preferring future interaction with autistic partners. The authors concluded that their results "support reframing social interaction difficulties in autism as a relational rather than an individual impairment."

**Heasman and Gillespie (2019).** "Neurodivergent intersubjectivity." *Autism*, 23(4), 910–921.

Heasman and Gillespie filmed 30 autistic adults in collaborative video gaming and identified two distinctive features of autistic-autistic communication: a generous assumption of common ground, and a low demand for coordination (allowing turns that non-autistic norms would treat as "disruptive" without treating them as problems). These are not deficits. They are different conventions, conventions that work for people who share them and create friction at the boundary with people who don't.

#### Bidirectional mindreading

**Edey, Cook, Brewer, Johnson, Bird and Press (2016).** "Interaction takes two: Typical adults exhibit mind-blindness towards those with autism spectrum disorder." *Journal of Abnormal Psychology*, 125(7), 879–885.

The title is the punchline. Edey et al. used a task where participants inferred mental states from animated point-light displays generated from real body movements. Non-autistic observers were substantially worse at reading mental states from movements produced by autistic actors than from non-autistic actors. The "mind-blindness" runs both directions.

**Sheppard, Pillai, Wong, Ropar and Mitchell (2016).** "How easy is it to read the minds of people with autism spectrum disorder?" *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 46(4), 1247–1254.

Non-autistic observers were significantly less accurate when inferring the mental states of autistic targets from video clips of their reactions. Critically, raters judged the autistic and non-autistic targets as roughly equally expressive; the autistic participants were not under-reacting. They were reacting differently, in ways non-autistic observers had not learned to decode.

**Mitchell, Sheppard and Cassidy (2021).** "Autism and the double empathy problem: Implications for development and mental health." *British Journal of Developmental Psychology*, 39(1), 1–18.

The synthesis paper. Mitchell, Sheppard and Cassidy argued that if autistic people are systematically misread by the non-autistic majority from childhood onward, and if that misreading shapes how they are treated, then the long-term mental-health burden of autism may be substantially driven by the relational double empathy failure rather than by anything intrinsic to autistic cognition.

#### Relevance Theory formalization

**Williams, Wharton and Jagoe (2021).** "Mutual (Mis)understanding: Reframing autistic pragmatic 'impairments' using Relevance Theory." *Frontiers in Psychology*, 12, article 616664.

Williams, Wharton and Jagoe gave the double empathy problem a formal cognitive grounding in Sperber and Wilson's Relevance Theory. Their core argument: a person's **cognitive environment** consists of everything that is manifest (available for representation) to them. Communication works when cognitive environments overlap enough that speakers and hearers can estimate what is mutually manifest and construct utterances that will be optimally relevant. When autistic and non-autistic people interact, their cognitive environments overlap only partially. Each interprets the other's utterances using their own cognitive environment, deriving interpretations the speaker never intended. The result is mutual misunderstanding, not because either party has a broken comprehension module, but because the shared ground on which the comprehension procedure runs is smaller than either party assumed.

This is the formal mechanism for Milton's sociological observation. The paper is cited throughout this hub. See the [Relevance Theory category](tier-1-relevance-theory.md) and the Common Ground category for deeper treatment.

#### Ten years on

**Milton, Gurbuz and López (2022).** "The 'double empathy problem': Ten years on." *Autism*, 26(8), 1901–1903.

Milton and colleagues revisited the argument a decade later. They noted which parts of the original claim have accumulated empirical support (bidirectional mindreading difficulties, thin-slice effects, autistic-peer effectiveness, mixed-chain information loss) and which remain underinvestigated (scaling across contexts, subpopulations, relationship types). They also pushed back against watered-down popularizations. Their summary definition: the double empathy problem refers to "a breakdown in mutual understanding (that can happen between any two people) and hence a problem for both parties to contend with, yet more likely to occur when people of very differing dispositions attempt to interact."

### Caveats and limits

**Double empathy does not mean communication problems are fictitious.** They are real. They happen. Conversations go wrong. People get hurt. The reframe is about what causes the problems, not about whether they exist.

**Double empathy does not mean autistic people don't benefit from pragmatic learning.** Learning how non-autistic pragmatics works is valuable, not because you are deficient but because most of the people you will communicate with are non-autistic, and knowing their system gives you informed choices. The reframe is that you are learning a second system on top of a first one that is also legitimate.

**Double empathy does not mean autistic-autistic communication is always easy.** Individual variation within the autistic population is enormous. Crompton et al. (2020) and Heasman and Gillespie (2019) show that autistic-autistic communication is *on average* more effective than mixed-neurotype communication, not that every autistic pairing is frictionless.

**Double empathy does not mean autism is "just" a communication difference.** Autism is a complex neurodevelopmental condition with dimensions far beyond communication: sensory differences, executive-function patterns, co-occurring conditions, and substantial support needs for some people. The framework addresses the pragmatic dimension specifically.

**The evidence base is strong but not finished.** Some findings have been replicated multiple times across labs. Others would benefit from larger samples and cross-cultural replication. Milton, Gurbuz and López (2022) are explicit about which aspects have the strongest support and which remain open.

**The framework does not erase individual responsibility.** You can still behave badly in a conversation. So can a non-autistic partner. What the framework denies is that the baseline of autistic communication is a deficit against which autistic people should be held globally responsible for interpretive failures that are in fact shared.

### What this page is not

This page is a Tier 1 introduction. It is not, and does not try to be, several other things you might reasonably want.

- **It is not a complete literature review.** The studies cited here are the backbone, but the full literature includes dozens more papers. For deeper coverage in a specific pragmatic domain, each category's research-autism file cites domain-relevant double-empathy work.

- **It is not a political argument.** The double empathy problem has political and policy implications. This page is the empirical and conceptual reframe on which those arguments can rest, but it stops short of making them here.

- **It is not a claim that autism is nothing but a communication difference.** The caveats above make this explicit.

- **It is not a replacement for the category research files.** The Tier 1 pages are entry points. The depth lives in the categories.
