# Category 21: Pragmatic Competence in Specific Contexts -- The Workplace

## The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

---

## How to Read This Document

Every other category in this hub teaches a pragmatic *mechanism* -- speech acts, hedging, turn-taking, register. This category is different. This is where those mechanisms come together inside a **single environment** that has its own rules, its own hierarchy, its own stakes, and its own version of every pragmatic principle you've already learned.

The workplace is not just "another context." It is the context where pragmatic competence has the most measurable material consequences. Misread a joke at a party and the worst outcome is awkwardness. Misread a power dynamic at work and the consequence can be a failed project, a lost promotion, or a terminated career. For autistic individuals, the stakes are compounded: 85% of autistic adults are unemployed or significantly underemployed (Roux et al., 2015; recent estimates range from 40% to 85% depending on methodology), and research consistently identifies social-communicative challenges -- not technical skill deficits -- as the primary barrier.

This document treats the workplace as a **complete pragmatic system**. It synthesizes and deepens everything touched on in earlier categories -- email register (Categories 7 and 20), hierarchy and politeness (Category 3), hedging in professional contexts (Category 6), meeting turn-taking (Category 4), workplace small talk (Category 19), saying no to work requests (Category 18), and interview questions (Category 17) -- into one integrated reference for navigating the most consequential pragmatic environment most adults encounter.

## 1. The Workplace as a Pragmatic Environment

### 1.1 What Makes Workplace Communication Distinct

The workplace differs from every other communication context in four fundamental ways:

| Property | What It Means | Why It Matters |
|----------|--------------|----------------|
| **Involuntary proximity** | You don't choose your coworkers, and you can't easily leave | You must maintain working relationships with people you may not naturally connect with |
| **Dual hierarchy** | Both formal (org chart) and informal (social capital) hierarchies operate simultaneously | Every interaction is calibrated to at least two power relationships |
| **Persistent consequences** | What you say is remembered and accumulates into a reputation | A single pragmatic failure doesn't vanish -- it becomes part of how people perceive you |
| **Instrumental goals** | Communication must accomplish tasks AND maintain relationships simultaneously | You can't optimize for one channel only. Pure task-focus reads as cold; pure relationship-focus reads as unproductive |

**The core distinction**: In social life, you communicate to maintain relationships. At work, you communicate to **accomplish goals THROUGH relationships**. The relationship is not the end -- it's the means. But neglecting it destroys the means, which destroys the end.

### 1.2 The Dual Hierarchy

Every workplace operates two parallel hierarchies:

**Formal hierarchy** (the org chart):
- Explicit, documented, visible
- Determines who reports to whom, who approves what, who has decision-making authority
- Shapes which register level is expected (Category 7) and how much hedging is required (Category 6)

**Informal hierarchy** (social capital):
- Implicit, undocumented, invisible to newcomers
- Determined by expertise, tenure, relationships, personality, institutional knowledge
- The person with the most formal authority is NOT always the person with the most influence

| Formal Power | Informal Power | Example |
|-------------|---------------|---------|
| CEO | High | The CEO has both formal authority and informal influence |
| New manager | Low (initially) | A new manager has formal authority over their team but hasn't earned informal credibility yet |
| Senior individual contributor | Low (formally) but HIGH (informally) | The engineer who's been there 15 years, knows every system, and everyone consults informally |
| Executive assistant | Low (formally) but HIGH (informally) | The person who controls access to the executive -- a gatekeeper with enormous practical influence |
| Intern | Low on both | Lowest on both hierarchies -- the most hedging required |

**The autistic challenge**: The formal hierarchy is visible and learnable. The informal hierarchy is communicated through subtle social signals -- who defers to whom in meetings, who gets interrupted and who doesn't, whose emails get answered first, who eats lunch with whom. This is exactly the kind of implicit social information that autistic individuals often miss.

**The practical solution**: Ask a trusted colleague explicitly. "I'm new here -- who are the people I should make sure to keep in the loop on this project?" or "Who should I talk to if I need to get buy-in for this idea?" People are usually willing to explain the informal map when directly asked.

### 1.3 The Professional Persona

The **professional persona** is a register-level concept (Category 7): at work, you operate in a different communicative mode than you do at home. This is NOT about being fake. It's a systematic shift in how you communicate:

| Dimension | Personal Register | Professional Register |
|-----------|------------------|----------------------|
| **Emotional expression** | Full range, spontaneous | Modulated, measured, controlled |
| **Topic range** | Anything | Work-relevant, plus carefully bounded personal topics |
| **Directness** | Variable (depends on relationship) | Calibrated to hierarchy and stakes |
| **Disclosure** | Deep, reciprocal | Strategic, selective, appropriate |
| **Language** | Casual, colloquial | Consultative to formal (Joos levels 2-3) |
| **Conflict** | Can be explicit | Must be managed, indirect, constructive |
| **Humor** | Unconstrained | Safe topics only; never punch down |
| **Opinions** | Freely shared | Hedged, evidence-based, diplomatically framed |

**The autistic reframe**: Many autistic individuals experience professional persona maintenance as exhausting masking. There IS overlap. But there's an important distinction: EVERYONE shifts register at work -- neurotypical people do it too. The professional persona is not masking autism specifically; it's the universal workplace register. The autistic-specific challenge is that the shift may require more conscious effort, the rules may be harder to identify, and the cost of errors is asymmetrically higher.

### 1.4 The Stakes: Why Workplace Pragmatics Matter More

Pragmatic failures at work have consequences that pragmatic failures in social life typically don't:

| Social Context Failure | Workplace Context Failure |
|----------------------|--------------------------|
| "That person is a bit blunt" | "That employee is not a culture fit" |
| Awkward moment, forgotten | Noted in performance review |
| Friend might distance themselves | Manager might not give you important projects |
| Temporary social discomfort | Missed promotion, pip, termination |
| You can choose different friends | You can't choose different coworkers (without quitting) |

This asymmetry is important to understand. It's not that workplace communication is inherently harder than social communication -- it uses the same mechanisms. It's that the **consequences of miscalibration** are more severe and more durable.

## 2. Workplace-Specific Pragmatic Contexts

### 2.1 Meetings

Meetings are the most complex pragmatic environment in the workplace. They combine multi-party conversation (Category 4), hierarchy-aware turn-taking, public performance, and real-time decision-making.

#### Turn-Taking in Meetings

Meeting turn-taking differs from casual conversation in critical ways:

| Conversation Turn-Taking | Meeting Turn-Taking |
|-------------------------|-------------------|
| Self-selection is normal | Often managed by a chair/facilitator |
| Overlap is tolerated | Overlap reads as interrupting |
| Any topic can be introduced | Topics are constrained by agenda |
| All participants have roughly equal rights | Turn rights are hierarchy-dependent |
| Exits are flexible | Exits require permission or ritual |

**How to get a turn in a meeting:**

| Strategy | When to Use | Language |
|----------|------------|---------|
| **Wait for a pause** | Small meetings, casual | [pause] "I had a thought about that--" |
| **Use a discourse marker** | Standard, any meeting | "To add to that..." / "Building on what [Name] said..." |
| **Address the chair** | Formal meetings | "If I could jump in here--" / "[Chair's name], I wanted to raise a point." |
| **Use the hand-raise** | Video calls, large meetings | Physical hand raise or digital hand-raise button |
| **Use the chat** | Video calls | Type your point in the chat; say "I put a thought in the chat" |
| **Pre-arrange with the chair** | When you have something important but struggle to get turns | Email before the meeting: "I'd like 5 minutes to present X" |

**The autistic experience in meetings**: Research on turn-taking (Category 4) shows that autistic individuals often process verbal input more slowly, need more time to formulate responses, and may miss the 200ms transition-relevance windows that neurotypical speakers exploit. By the time you've formulated your thought, the conversation has moved on. The pre-arranged turn and the chat-as-parallel-channel strategies are especially useful.

#### How to Disagree in a Meeting

Disagreement in meetings is a performance -- it's public, observed, and remembered. The disagreement spectrum from Category 18 applies with **heightened stakes**:

| Audience | Minimum Hedging Level |
|----------|--------------------|
| Peers only (brainstorm) | Level 2: "I think there might be another angle--" |
| Peers + your manager | Level 3: "I see the logic there. One thing I'd want to consider is--" |
| Peers + senior leadership | Level 4: "That's a strong approach. Could we also explore--?" |
| Client-facing meeting | Level 5: "That's an excellent point. I wonder if we might additionally consider--" |

**Critical rule**: NEVER disagree with your boss in front of their boss unless you're flagging something dangerous, illegal, or factually incorrect in a way that will be immediately verifiable. Private disagreement (after the meeting) is almost always safer than public disagreement.

#### How to Present Ideas in Meetings

The pragmatic structure of presenting an idea at work:

```
1. FRAME           "I've been thinking about [topic] and wanted to share an approach."
2. CONTEXT         "The current situation is [brief context]."
3. PROPOSAL        "What I'd suggest is [your idea]."
4. EVIDENCE        "The reason I think this would work is [evidence/reasoning]."
5. ACKNOWLEDGE     "I know there are tradeoffs -- [address obvious concerns]."
6. INVITE          "What do you all think?" / "I'd love to get your input."
```

**What each part does:**

| Component | Function |
|-----------|----------|
| Frame | Signals an idea is coming; gets attention; sets expectations |
| Context | Establishes shared ground (Category 16); makes the idea comprehensible |
| Proposal | The actual idea -- clear, concise, specific |
| Evidence | Credibility; shows you've thought it through |
| Acknowledge | Preemptively addresses objections (reduces face threat to potential objectors) |
| Invite | Signals you're not imposing; opens the floor; shows you value input |

**The autistic advantage here**: Systematic thinking, thorough analysis, and precise communication are STRENGTHS in idea presentation. The challenge is usually in the framing (too little social packaging) or the invite (forgetting to open the floor). The content quality is often excellent.

### 2.2 Email and Slack/Teams

Covered in depth in Category 20 (Digital Pragmatics). Key workplace-specific additions:

**Email hierarchy rules:**
- **Email to your boss**: Consultative register. Brief, structured, action-oriented. Lead with the key point or request. Don't bury the ask in paragraph 3.
- **Email to a peer**: Consultative register but warmer. Slightly less formal. Can include brief personal opener ("Hope your week is going well!").
- **Email to a direct report**: Clear, specific, supportive. Provide context for requests (the "why"). Don't be cryptic -- clarity is kindness when you have power.
- **Email to someone senior (skip-level)**: Formal register. Shorter. Respectful of their time. CC your boss unless there's a specific reason not to.
- **Email to an external client/partner**: Formal to consultative. Mirror their register level. When in doubt, err formal.

**The "reply all" decision tree:**
1. Does everyone on the thread need to see your response? -> Reply all
2. Are you just saying "thanks" or "sounds good"? -> Reply to sender only
3. Are you raising a concern about someone on the thread? -> Reply to sender only (NEVER criticize someone in a reply-all)
4. Unsure? -> Reply to sender only. You can always forward later; you can never un-reply-all.

**Slack/Teams: See Category 20, Section 5.** The key workplace-specific principle: **written messages at work are permanent records**. Slack messages can be searched, screenshotted, forwarded, and subpoenaed. Never write anything in Slack you wouldn't want read aloud in a meeting.

### 2.3 Performance Reviews: Giving and Receiving Feedback

Performance reviews are one of the most pragmatically loaded workplace interactions. They combine **face-threatening acts** (Category 3), **hedging** (Category 6), and **institutional power** (you can't walk away from this conversation).

#### Receiving Feedback

The pragmatic demands when receiving feedback:

| What Happens | Expected Response | What NOT to Do |
|-------------|------------------|----------------|
| Positive feedback | "Thank you. I've really been working on that." | Deflect it entirely ("Oh, it was nothing") -- this denies the evaluator's assessment |
| Constructive criticism | "I appreciate you flagging that. Can you give me a specific example so I know exactly what to work on?" | Become defensive. Explain why you did it. Argue. Cry (if possible to control) |
| Unfair criticism | "I hear you. I'd like to share my perspective on that -- could I give some context?" | Attack the evaluator. Say "that's not fair" (even if true -- reframe it) |
| Vague criticism | "I want to make sure I understand -- could you give me a concrete example?" | Accept vague criticism silently (you can't fix what you can't identify) |
| Praise followed by "but" | Focus on what comes after "but" -- that's the real message (Category 18, Section 3.1) | Bask in the praise and miss the criticism |

**The pragmatic structure of receiving feedback well:**

```
1. LISTEN         Don't interrupt. Take notes if helpful.
2. ACKNOWLEDGE    "I hear that." / "Thank you for sharing that."
3. CLARIFY        "Can you give me an example?" / "What would success look like?"
4. RESPOND        Share context IF relevant (brief, not defensive)
5. COMMIT         "I'll work on that. Can we check in on this in [timeframe]?"
```

**The autistic experience**: Receiving critical feedback can be intensely dysregulating. The expectation to remain composed while processing negative evaluation, formulate measured responses, and NOT become visibly upset is one of the most difficult workplace pragmatic demands. Strategies:
- It is entirely acceptable to say: "I want to take this seriously. Can I take a day to process and come back with questions?"
- Take notes during the review -- this gives you something to focus on and creates a record
- If you feel overwhelmed, requesting a brief break ("Could I get some water?") is always acceptable

#### Giving Feedback (to Peers or Reports)

The **Situation-Behavior-Impact (SBI)** model is the most effective framework:

```
SITUATION:  "In yesterday's client call..."
BEHAVIOR:   "...you interrupted the client twice while they were explaining their concern..."
IMPACT:     "...which made them visibly frustrated and they stopped sharing details we needed."
```

**Why SBI works**: It's specific, observable, and non-judgmental. It describes what happened and its effect without characterizing the person. Compare:

| SBI Feedback | Characterization Feedback |
|-------------|--------------------------|
| "In yesterday's meeting, when you said X, the client seemed frustrated" | "You're rude to clients" |
| "The report had three calculation errors, which delayed the review" | "You're careless" |
| "When you respond to emails within an hour, clients comment on how responsive we are" | "You're a great communicator" (too vague to be actionable) |

**Critical rule**: NEVER give negative feedback publicly. Always private, always 1:1, always with specific examples.

### 2.4 Job Interviews

Job interviews are a unique pragmatic context because they require performing competence for a stranger with power over your future, under conditions of uncertainty about the rules.

**The hidden pragmatic demands of job interviews:**

| What They Ask | What They Actually Want | The Pragmatic Demand |
|--------------|----------------------|---------------------|
| "Tell me about yourself" | A 60-90 second professional narrative connecting your experience to this role | Narrative competence (Category 12); self-presentation; relevance |
| "What's your greatest weakness?" | Evidence of self-awareness and growth, NOT an actual weakness | Self-deprecation as a performance; hedging; framing |
| "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?" | Evidence of ambition that aligns with the company | Future-oriented self-presentation; showing investment |
| "Tell me about a time you handled conflict" | A STAR-format story showing emotional intelligence | Narrative (Category 12); showing social competence |
| "Do you have any questions?" | Evidence that you've researched the company and are genuinely interested | This is NOT optional. Having no questions signals disinterest |

**Research findings (Maras et al., 2021; Finn et al., 2023)**: Autistic candidates are systematically disadvantaged by standard interview formats -- not due to lack of qualifications, but because:
- Open-ended questions are too ambiguous ("Tell me about yourself" -- what aspect? how much detail? what's relevant?)
- Behavioral questions require narrative construction under time pressure
- The format rewards impression management over actual competence
- Autistic interviewees rated lower on "hirability" even when their answers had equivalent content

**Practical strategies:**
- **Prepare STAR stories in advance**: Situation, Task, Action, Result. Have 5-7 prepared stories covering different competencies (leadership, conflict, teamwork, initiative, problem-solving)
- **For "tell me about yourself"**: Prepare a 60-90 second professional summary. Structure: [current role] + [relevant experience] + [why this position interests you]
- **For "greatest weakness"**: Choose a genuine minor weakness you've worked on. Structure: [weakness] + [what you've done about it] + [evidence of improvement]. "I used to struggle with delegating because I wanted to make sure everything was done to my standard. I've been working on this by setting clear expectations upfront and building check-in points, which has helped me lead a larger team effectively."
- **Ask for clarification**: "Could you tell me more about what you're looking for in that question?" is completely acceptable and shows conscientiousness

### 2.5 Water Cooler / Break Room: Workplace Small Talk

See Category 19 for the full phatic communication system. Workplace-specific additions:

**Why workplace small talk matters more than social small talk**: In social life, you can avoid small talk by choosing different social contexts. At work, small talk is the **ambient relationship maintenance** that determines whether people trust you, include you, and advocate for you.

| What Small Talk Does at Work | What Happens Without It |
|-----------------------------|------------------------|
| Builds trust ("I know this person") | You remain an unknown quantity |
| Creates informal channels for information | You're out of the loop on important context |
| Signals team membership | You're perceived as separate, detached, "not a team player" |
| Creates social capital for when you need favors | Requests come with no relational foundation |
| Makes collaboration smoother | Every interaction starts cold |

**The minimum viable workplace small talk protocol:**

```
MORNING:     "Hey, good morning!" + [one follow-up based on context]
             "How was your weekend?" (Monday) / "How's your week going?" (mid-week)
KITCHEN:     "Hey! How's it going?" + [one topic if you're both lingering]
             Weather, weekend plans, something visible (new coffee mug, etc.)
PASSING:     Nod + smile + "Hey" (minimum viable acknowledgment)
EXIT:        "Have a good evening!" / "See you tomorrow!"
```

**Total daily investment**: 3-5 minutes spread across the day. The return on this investment is disproportionately high.

**Safe workplace small talk topics** (see Category 19 for the full list):
- Weather (always safe, always available)
- Weekend plans / how was your weekend
- Food ("Where did you get lunch?" / "That smells good")
- Sports (if you know the person follows sports)
- Local events, restaurants, shows
- Vacation plans
- Pets (universally safe and warmly received)
- Shared work context ("Busy day, huh?")

**Topics to AVOID at work:**
- Politics (unless you work in politics)
- Religion
- Salary/compensation (complex -- it's legally protected to discuss, but culturally sensitive)
- Gossip about colleagues (never)
- Complaints about management (to anyone you don't deeply trust)
- Personal health details (brief mentions are fine; extended discussion is oversharing)
- Romantic/sexual topics

### 2.6 Onboarding: Reading the Unwritten Rules

Starting a new job is arguably the single most pragmatically demanding situation in professional life. You must simultaneously learn:
- The formal rules (documented, told to you)
- The informal rules (undocumented, discovered through observation and error)
- The social landscape (who matters, who's allies, who's in conflict)
- The communication norms (how this specific workplace talks)

**Research (Korte & Lin, 2013)**: It takes 12-18 months to fully learn the unwritten rules of a new workplace. According to a 2024 Workhuman study, 60% of all employees report that navigating company culture is challenging due to unwritten rules.

**What "unwritten rules" look like:**

| Category | Example Questions You Need Answered |
|----------|-----------------------------------|
| **Time norms** | What time do people actually arrive/leave? Is arriving at exactly 9:00 fine, or does everyone arrive at 8:30? Is staying late expected or unusual? |
| **Communication norms** | Does this team prefer Slack or email? Are meetings usually on time or 5 minutes late? Is it OK to message your boss after hours? |
| **Meeting norms** | Do people eat in meetings? Use laptops? Is there banter before meetings start, or do they jump straight to agenda? |
| **Dress code** | What's the ACTUAL dress code? (The employee handbook says "business casual" but everyone wears jeans) |
| **Social norms** | Do teams eat lunch together? Are there Friday afternoon drinks? Is there an expectation to attend social events? |
| **Work norms** | How quickly are emails expected to be returned? Is working from home actually OK or just officially OK? Is asking for help seen as weakness or collaboration? |

**Strategies for autistic onboarding:**

1. **Observe before participating.** Spend the first 2 weeks watching: What do people do in the first 10 minutes of the day? How do meetings start? What does the team Slack channel look like?

2. **Find an informant.** Identify one friendly, experienced colleague and ask: "I'm still getting the lay of the land -- is there anything about how things work here that's not in the handbook?" People are usually flattered to be asked and generous with information.

3. **Ask process questions without apology.** "What's the typical process for X?" is always acceptable for new employees. Don't frame it as "I don't know" (which sounds incompetent); frame it as "I want to do this the way your team does it" (which sounds respectful).

4. **Write things down.** Keep a private document of the rules you discover. This serves as a reference and reduces the cognitive load of holding implicit knowledge in working memory.

5. **Give yourself permission to get things wrong.** Everyone makes cultural errors when they're new. The recovery matters more than the error. "Oh, I didn't realize -- thanks for letting me know!" is the all-purpose response to discovering you've violated an unwritten rule.

## 3. Workplace-Specific Speech Acts

### 3.1 Making Requests: The Direction Matters

The single most important pragmatic variable in workplace requests is **direction**: upward (to someone with more power), lateral (to a peer), or downward (to someone with less power).

#### Requesting Upward (Boss / Senior Colleague)

Maximum hedging. You are threatening the negative face of someone with power over you:

| Hedging Level | Example | When to Use |
|--------------|---------|-------------|
| Maximum | "I was wondering if there might be a possibility of..." | Unusual/big request; new relationship |
| High | "Would it be possible to..." / "I wanted to ask about..." | Standard requests to your boss |
| Moderate | "Could we discuss..." / "I'd like to suggest..." | Established relationship; routine matter |
| Too low | "I need you to..." / "You should..." | NEVER to someone above you (unless safety issue) |

**Template for requesting something from your boss:**
> "Hi [Name], I wanted to bring something up. [Context: brief explanation of the situation]. I was wondering if [specific request]. [Reason it would help: ties to work goals, not just personal preference]. I'm happy to discuss what would work best."

#### Requesting Laterally (Peers)

Moderate hedging. You're asking someone who has no obligation to help you:

| Strategy | Example |
|----------|---------|
| **Acknowledge their workload** | "I know you're busy, but..." |
| **Frame as collaboration** | "Would you be up for collaborating on..." |
| **Offer reciprocity** | "I could handle X if you could take care of Y" |
| **Give them an out** | "No worries if the timing doesn't work" |
| **Be specific about scope** | "It would take about 15 minutes" (reduces perceived burden) |

**Template:**
> "Hey [Name], quick favor -- would you have time to [specific, scoped request]? I need it for [brief context]. Totally understand if you're swamped -- no rush on this one."

#### Requesting Downward (Direct Reports)

Low hedging, but **clarity and context are critical**. When you have power over someone, clear communication is a form of respect:

| Good | Bad |
|------|-----|
| "I need the Johnson report by Thursday at 3pm. The client meeting is Friday morning and I need time to review." | "Get me the Johnson report sometime this week." |
| "Could you take the lead on organizing the team lunch? Here's the budget and dietary restrictions." | "Handle the team lunch." |
| "I'd like you to prioritize Project X over Project Y this week. Here's why: [context]." | "Focus on what matters." |

**The power-clarity principle**: The MORE power you have, the MORE clarity you owe. Vague instructions from someone with power create anxiety, wasted effort, and blame that flows downward. When you have authority, being explicit is not micromanaging -- it's kindness.

### 3.2 Giving Feedback (Positive and Negative)

#### Positive Feedback

Positive feedback at work is NOT just "being nice." It serves specific pragmatic functions:

| Function | Example |
|----------|---------|
| **Reinforcement** | "The way you structured that presentation made it really easy to follow" (tells them what to keep doing) |
| **Relationship building** | "I really appreciate you jumping in on that" (builds social capital) |
| **Public recognition** | "I want to call out [Name]'s work on this" (in a meeting -- signals value to the team) |
| **Correction by contrast** | Praising the behavior you WANT can be more effective than criticizing the behavior you don't |

**Rules for workplace praise:**
- Be **specific** (not "great job" but "the way you handled that client's objection was really effective")
- Praise **publicly** when possible (meetings, Slack channels, emails with their manager CC'd)
- Praise **behavior**, not character ("That presentation was well-structured" > "You're smart")
- Praise **promptly** (within 24-48 hours of the behavior)

#### Negative Feedback

See Section 2.3 above for the SBI framework. Additional rules:

- **Always private.** No exceptions.
- **Always specific.** "Your communication needs work" is useless. "In Tuesday's meeting, when you interrupted the client, it disrupted the conversation" is actionable.
- **Always about behavior, not character.** "The report had errors" (fixable behavior) vs. "You're careless" (character judgment).
- **Always paired with a path forward.** "Here's what I'd suggest for next time..."
- **Never "sandwich"** (praise-criticism-praise). Research shows people see through it, and it dilutes both the praise and the criticism. Be direct, be kind, be specific.

### 3.3 Saying "No" at Work

This is Category 18 applied to the workplace context. The stakes are higher because:
- You can't easily replace the relationship (you'll see this person tomorrow)
- The request may come from someone with power over you
- "No" can be interpreted as "not a team player"

**The Workplace "No" Spectrum:**

| What You Mean | How to Say It (to Your Boss) | How to Say It (to a Peer) |
|--------------|-----------------------------|-----------------------------|
| "I can't do this" | "I want to make sure I deliver quality work. Right now I'm committed to X and Y. Could we discuss which to prioritize, or adjust the timeline?" | "I'm maxed out this week -- I don't think I could give this the attention it deserves. Could we look at next week?" |
| "I disagree with this approach" | "I appreciate the direction. I have a concern about [specific issue] -- [brief reasoning]. Would it be worth considering [alternative]?" | "I see where you're going with this. I have a different instinct -- what if we tried [alternative]?" |
| "This isn't my job" | "I want to make sure this gets handled well. It's a bit outside my area -- would [Name/Team] be better positioned for this?" | "I'm not sure I'm the right person for this one. Have you tried [Name]? They'd be great at it." |
| "I don't want to" | DO NOT say this directly. Reframe as workload, timing, or fit. | "I don't think I'd be the best person for this -- [reason]." |

**The critical rule**: At work, "no" almost always needs to be packaged with a **reason** (workload, timing, competing priorities) and an **alternative** (different timeline, different person, different scope). A bare "no" -- even with hedging -- signals unwillingness rather than inability.

### 3.4 Self-Advocacy: Raises, Promotions, and Accommodations

Self-advocacy is one of the most difficult speech acts for many autistic individuals, because it requires:
- Positive self-assessment (which feels like bragging)
- Future-oriented claims (which feel uncertain)
- Implicit comparison with others (which feels uncomfortable)
- Navigating power asymmetry (requesting from someone who can deny you)

#### Asking for a Raise

**The structure:**

```
1. FRAME      "I'd like to discuss my compensation."
2. EVIDENCE   "Over the past [period], I've [specific accomplishments with metrics]."
3. MARKET     "Based on my research, the market rate for my role and experience is [range]."
4. REQUEST    "I'd like to discuss an adjustment to [specific number or range]."
5. OPENNESS   "I'm open to discussing what would work -- I want this to be a conversation."
```

**Key principles:**
- **Document everything before the meeting.** Bring a list of your accomplishments, metrics, and market research.
- **Use "I" statements, not comparisons.** "I've taken on additional responsibility" -- never "I do more than [colleague]."
- **Frame as mutual value.** "I want to continue growing here and feel that my compensation reflects my contribution."
- **Accept the outcome gracefully, even if disappointing.** "I understand. Could we set specific goals for the next review period so I know what I'm working toward?"

#### Asking for Accommodations

Under the ADA (Americans with Disabilities Act), employers with 15+ employees must provide reasonable accommodations that don't pose undue hardship. Communication-relevant accommodations include:

| Accommodation | What It Addresses |
|--------------|------------------|
| Written instructions instead of verbal | Processing speed; working memory |
| Email/Slack instead of phone calls | Real-time verbal processing difficulty |
| Agenda provided before meetings | Need for preparation time; reduced anxiety |
| Permission to ask clarifying questions in writing after meetings | Processing lag; difficulty formulating questions in real time |
| Quiet workspace / noise-canceling headphones | Sensory processing; focus |
| Reduced mandatory social events | Social energy management |
| Clear, explicit task descriptions | Difficulty with implicit expectations |
| Regular 1:1 check-ins instead of only annual review | Need for ongoing clear feedback |
| Permission to work from home (partial or full) | Reduced sensory/social load |
| Flexible scheduling | Energy management; peak performance windows |

**The disclosure decision (see Section 4.4)** determines whether you can formally request accommodations. But many accommodations can be requested WITHOUT disclosure:

- "I do my best work when I have things in writing -- could you send me a summary after our meeting?" (No disclosure needed)
- "I find I'm most productive with advance notice on meeting topics -- would it be possible to share agendas beforehand?" (No disclosure needed)
- "I'd like to try taking notes via email after meetings instead of speaking up in real-time -- I process better that way." (No disclosure needed)

These requests frame the accommodation as a **work style preference** rather than a **disability need**. Many neurotypical people make similar requests. The accommodation is the same; the framing is different.

## 4. Workplace Pragmatics and Autism

### 4.1 The Employment Gap

The numbers are stark:

- **Unemployment**: Estimates range from 40% (National Library of Medicine, 2021) to 85% (commonly cited figure, Roux et al., 2015) of autistic adults are unemployed
- **Underemployment**: Among employed autistic adults, 45% work part-time compared to 19% of the general population; many work in positions below their qualification level
- **Longitudinal data (Bury et al., 2024)**: An 8-year study showed that employment status among autistic adults remains persistently low and unstable over time
- **Primary barriers**: Social communication challenges, sensory difficulties, and employer misunderstanding -- NOT lack of technical skills or qualifications

**What the research consistently shows**: The employment gap is primarily a **pragmatic and social communication gap**, not a skills gap. Autistic adults are capable of excellent work but face systematic barriers in the communicative aspects of getting, keeping, and advancing in employment.

### 4.2 Common Friction Points

| Friction Point | What Happens | Cross-Reference |
|---------------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Small talk** | Skipping or struggling with workplace phatic exchange → perceived as cold, unfriendly, "not a team player" | Category 19 |
| **Hierarchy navigation** | Failing to calibrate register/hedging to the power relationship → perceived as disrespectful or presumptuous | Categories 3, 6, 7 |
| **Implicit expectations** | Missing unwritten rules about dress, timing, communication norms → perceived as careless or defiant | Section 2.6 above |
| **Meeting participation** | Difficulty getting turns, processing speed issues, literal processing of indirect speech → perceived as disengaged or unhelpful | Categories 4, 2 |
| **Email tone** | Too direct, insufficient hedging, missing social lubrication → perceived as rude or aggressive | Categories 6, 7, 20 |
| **Feedback reception** | Visible emotional reaction to criticism, or correction of the evaluator → perceived as defensive or argumentative | Section 2.3 above |
| **Self-promotion** | Not mentioning achievements, deflecting praise, not "selling" oneself → invisible contributions, passed over | Section 3.4 above |
| **Networking** | Avoiding or struggling with networking events → missed opportunities, weaker professional relationships | Category 19, Section 2.5 |
| **Ambiguous instructions** | Asking for clarification on vague instructions → perceived as incompetent (unfairly) | See script in Section 5 |

### 4.3 The Double Empathy Problem at Work

Szechy et al. (2024) applied the double empathy problem (Milton, 2012) specifically to workplace contexts:

- Non-autistic coworkers accurately understood autistic employees' communication only 31.2% of the time
- Autistic coworkers understood the same autistic employees 50.7% of the time
- When non-autistic coworkers received autism education, their understanding improved significantly
- Written communication from autistic employees was frequently misconstrued as impolite when no impoliteness was intended

**The implication**: Workplace communication failures between autistic and non-autistic colleagues are **bidirectional**. The autistic employee is not the only one who "doesn't understand" -- the non-autistic colleague is equally failing to understand the autistic communication style. The difference is that the autistic employee bears the professional consequences.

### 4.4 The Disclosure Decision

Whether, when, and how to disclose autism at work is one of the most consequential decisions autistic employees face. Research (Johnson et al., 2020; PMC9620671; PMC10197981) identifies clear patterns:

**Reasons TO disclose:**
- Access to formal accommodations (ADA protections require disclosure)
- Explanation for behaviors others might misinterpret (avoiding negative assumptions)
- Reduced masking burden (not having to hide your processing differences)
- Greater authenticity and reduced stress
- Research finding: Disclosed employees were 3x more likely to be employed than non-disclosed (though causation is unclear -- it may be that more supported individuals are more likely to both disclose and be employed)

**Reasons NOT to disclose:**
- Fear of stigma and discrimination (30% of disclosed autistic employees reported negative consequences)
- Being treated differently or patronized
- Concerns about career advancement ("They'll never promote me if they know")
- Workplace culture may not be safe (no demonstrated inclusion practices)
- The disclosure can't be undone

**Factors that predict positive outcomes:**
1. **Organizational culture**: Companies with demonstrated neurodiversity initiatives → better outcomes
2. **Manager understanding**: Managers who already understand autism → better outcomes
3. **Specificity of disclosure**: "I process verbal information better in writing" → better than "I'm autistic" (gives people actionable information, not a label to interpret)
4. **Timing**: After establishing competence → better than during hiring (unfair but empirically supported)

**The pragmatic framework for disclosure:**

```
IF    you need formal accommodations that can't be framed as preferences
      → Disclose to HR and your direct manager

IF    your workplace has an active neurodiversity program
      → Disclosure is lower-risk; consider disclosing

IF    you're experiencing repeated misunderstandings
      → Targeted disclosure ("I process X differently") may help

IF    you're managing fine without disclosure
      → Disclosure is optional, not obligatory

IF    the workplace culture is hostile or uninformed about disability
      → Delay or avoid disclosure; document any discrimination
```

**How to disclose (when you choose to):**

> "I wanted to share something that I think will help us work together more effectively. I'm autistic, which means I process some things differently. Specifically, [1-2 concrete examples: "I do better with written instructions" / "I may need a moment to process before responding in meetings" / "I'm very direct, which sometimes reads differently than I intend"]. I'm sharing this because I want to do my best work here, and I've found that being open about this helps. If you have questions, I'm happy to talk about it."

**What this template does:**
- Frames disclosure as **collaborative** ("help us work together"), not as requesting pity
- Provides **specific, actionable** information, not just a label
- Normalizes it ("I've found this helps") rather than dramatizing it
- Opens the door for questions WITHOUT demanding an immediate response

### 4.5 When Autistic Communication Is a Workplace STRENGTH

The deficit framing of autism at work is incomplete. Research (Cope & Remington, 2022; PMC8992926) documents specific strengths:

| Autistic Trait | Workplace Strength |
|---------------|-------------------|
| **Direct communication** | Clarity that eliminates ambiguity; coworkers "know where they stand"; builds trust in relationships |
| **Attention to detail** | Catches errors others miss; thorough documentation; quality assurance |
| **Pattern recognition** | Identifies system-level problems; sees connections across data; anomaly detection |
| **Deep focus** | Sustained concentration on complex tasks; less distracted by social interruptions |
| **Honesty / low political behavior** | Integrity; transparency; resistance to groupthink; ethical decision-making |
| **Systematic thinking** | Strong process design; clear documentation; reproducible workflows |
| **Justice sensitivity** | Flags ethical concerns; advocates for fair treatment; compliance strength |
| **Special interest depth** | Domain expertise that surpasses generalist colleagues |

**The reframe**: Autistic communication is not universally "worse" at work. It is differently calibrated. In contexts that value precision, clarity, and honesty -- technical work, quality assurance, research, analysis, compliance, security -- autistic communication traits are actively advantageous. The challenge comes primarily in contexts that value social performance: networking, sales, client entertainment, and political navigation.

**The industry data**: Companies with neurodiversity hiring programs (SAP, Microsoft, JPMorgan, EY, Dell, IBM) consistently report that autistic employees match or exceed performance metrics in roles aligned with their strengths. JPMorgan's Autism at Work program reported that autistic employees in certain tech roles were 48% faster and up to 92% more productive than neurotypical peers.

### 4.6 Research on Autistic Employees' Experiences

Key findings from the research literature:

**Communication as primary barrier** (Szechy et al., 2024; Eisner et al., 2024): Communication challenges were the single most-cited barrier across autistic employees, coworkers, and managers. Phone calls, ambiguous verbal instructions, and open-plan office social dynamics were the most frequently reported difficulties.

**Manager relationship is critical**: Autistic employees who reported a supportive, understanding manager were significantly more likely to sustain employment and report job satisfaction. The manager's willingness to provide clear feedback, explicit instructions, and reasonable accommodations was the strongest predictor of workplace success.

**Masking costs** (Hull et al., 2017; Cage & Troxell-Whitman, 2019): Autistic employees who engage in extensive camouflaging/masking at work report higher rates of burnout, anxiety, depression, and job turnover. The pragmatic performance of "passing" as neurotypical has a measurable psychological cost.

**Peer education helps**: When non-autistic coworkers received education about autism -- specifically about communication style differences -- the quality of workplace relationships improved, misinterpretations decreased, and the autistic employee's performance evaluations improved (Szechy & O'Donnell, 2025).

## 5. Practical Workplace Scripts

### 5.1 Asking for Clarification Without Seeming Incompetent

The fear: "If I ask what they mean, they'll think I'm stupid."
The reality: Asking for clarification demonstrates thoroughness, not incompetence.

**The framework: Reframe as precision, not confusion.**

| Don't Say | Do Say |
|-----------|--------|
| "I don't understand" | "I want to make sure I get this right -- could you clarify [specific point]?" |
| "What do you mean?" | "When you say [X], do you mean [interpretation A] or [interpretation B]?" |
| "I'm confused" | "Let me confirm my understanding: you'd like me to [restate]. Is that right?" |
| "Can you explain that again?" | "Could you walk me through the [specific part] one more time? I want to make sure I don't miss anything." |

**The pro move**: Offering two specific interpretations ("Do you mean A or B?") demonstrates that you HAVE been thinking about it, you just want to confirm which direction is correct. This reads as precision, not confusion.

### 5.2 Disagreeing with Your Boss

**The hierarchy rule**: Never disagree directly. Always frame disagreement as a question, concern, or alternative perspective.

**Level 1 (low stakes, good relationship):**
> "I see the direction. One thing I'd want to flag -- [concern]. What do you think about [alternative]?"

**Level 2 (moderate stakes):**
> "I appreciate the approach. I have a concern about [specific issue] based on [evidence]. Would it be worth exploring [alternative] as well?"

**Level 3 (high stakes, or newer relationship):**
> "Thank you for outlining this. I want to make sure I'm raising something that might be important. I've noticed [observation/data] which suggests [concern]. I could be missing context -- but I wanted to flag it in case it's useful for the decision."

**The key elements in all three:**
- Appreciation/acknowledgment first (validates their idea before you challenge it)
- Framing as "flagging" or "raising a concern" (not "you're wrong")
- Providing evidence (not just opinion)
- Leaving room for you to be wrong ("I could be missing context")
- Framing your alternative as additional, not replacement ("as well" / "in case it's useful")

### 5.3 Responding to "How's the Project Going?"

This question has TWO possible functions (Category 17), and misidentifying the function causes problems:

**Function 1: Phatic check-in (the common case)**
- Asked in passing, hallway, kitchen, beginning of meeting
- They want a **brief status signal**, not a detailed report
- Expected response: 1-2 sentences maximum

| Status | Response |
|--------|----------|
| On track | "Going well! On track for the deadline." |
| Minor issues | "Coming along -- a couple things to sort out but nothing major." |
| Significant issues | "I actually have some concerns I'd love your input on. Can we set up 15 minutes this week?" |

**Function 2: Genuine status request**
- Asked in a 1:1 meeting, project review, or formal check-in
- They want a **substantive update**
- Expected response: Structured update covering progress, blockers, next steps

> "Overall we're tracking well against the timeline. We've completed [X] and [Y]. The main blocker right now is [Z] -- I'm working on it by [approach]. Next steps are [A] and [B], and I should have an update by [date]."

**How to tell which one you're getting**: Location and context. Passing in the hallway = phatic. Scheduled meeting = substantive. Ambiguous? Default to brief, then offer more: "Going well overall. Want me to walk you through the details?"

### 5.4 Navigating Networking Events

Networking events are structured small talk (Category 19) with a professional purpose. The pragmatic demands:

**The networking conversation structure:**

```
1. APPROACH     Walk up to someone standing alone or a small open group
2. INTRODUCE    "Hi, I'm [Name]. I work in [team/role] at [company]."
3. RECIPROCATE  "What about you? What do you do?" / "How are you connected to [event]?"
4. FIND COMMON  Listen for shared interests, projects, challenges
5. EXCHANGE     "Would you like to connect on LinkedIn?" / "Here's my card."
6. EXIT         "It was great meeting you! I'm going to grab a drink / say hi to someone."
```

**Time per conversation**: 5-10 minutes is standard. Networking conversations are meant to be SHORT. Lingering too long with one person is actually a breach of networking etiquette -- the expectation is that you circulate.

**The autistic advantage**: If you can find someone with a shared professional interest, the deep-dive conversation that autistic people often prefer IS valuable networking. The trick is finding the overlap and keeping it to 10-15 minutes maximum.

**Energy management**: You do NOT need to network for the entire event. Arriving, having 3-4 genuine conversations, and leaving after 45-60 minutes is a completely successful networking event.

### 5.5 Giving a Presentation

**The pragmatic structure:**

```
1. OPENING      Hook + purpose statement (30 seconds)
                "I'm going to walk you through [topic] and by the end, 
                you'll have [what they'll gain]."

2. ROADMAP      Tell them what's coming (15 seconds)
                "I'll cover three things: [1], [2], and [3]."

3. BODY         One section per point. Each section: 
                Claim → Evidence → Implication

4. SUMMARY      Restate the key points (30 seconds)
                "So the three takeaways are..."

5. CALL TO      What do you want them to do? (15 seconds)
   ACTION       "The next step I'd recommend is..."

6. QUESTIONS    "I'd love to hear your thoughts. Questions?"
```

**The autistic strengths in presentations**: Thorough preparation, systematic structure, and deep knowledge. The challenges: reading the room (are they bored? confused? engaged?), managing time, and handling unexpected questions.

**Practical tips:**
- **Prepare for questions in advance.** Think of the 5 most likely questions and prepare answers.
- **Use slides as a scaffold.** They keep you on track AND give the audience something to look at (reducing eye contact pressure).
- **Time yourself in practice.** A 10-minute slot means 7-8 minutes of content and 2-3 minutes for questions. Always under-run rather than over-run.
- **"I don't know" is acceptable.** "That's a great question. I don't have that data offhand -- let me follow up with you after." This is professional and honest.

### 5.6 Handling Workplace Conflict

**The escalation recognition system** (from Category 18, Section 6, applied to workplace):

| Signal | What's Happening | Your Response |
|--------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Hedges disappearing** | "I think maybe" → "This is wrong" | The conversation is escalating. Add YOUR hedges to compensate. |
| **Register shifting down** | Professional → personal | Someone is becoming emotionally activated. Slow down. |
| **Absolutes appearing** | "You always..." / "You never..." | Argumentative framing. Don't take the bait. |
| **Public vs. private** | Criticism delivered publicly | Request to move to private: "Can we discuss this one-on-one?" |
| **Email tone shift** | Short, clipped, CC-adding behavior | The email record is being built. Be very careful with your responses. |

**The workplace conflict de-escalation template:**

> "[Name], I can see this is frustrating. I want to resolve this. Can we [step back / take 10 minutes / continue this tomorrow / discuss one-on-one]? I think we'll find a better solution with [less pressure / more information / a fresh perspective]."

**What this template does:**
- Acknowledges the emotion without escalating it
- States your intent (resolution, not victory)
- Proposes a structural change (more time, fewer witnesses, different medium)
- Frames the change as beneficial for the OUTCOME, not as retreat

**The golden rule of workplace conflict**: **Never respond to an angry email immediately.** Write your response, save it as a draft, wait at least 2 hours (ideally overnight), then re-read and edit before sending. The first draft is always too reactive.

## 6. Key Takeaways

1. **The workplace is a complete pragmatic system with its own rules.** It combines every mechanism from earlier categories (speech acts, hedging, register, turn-taking, politeness, small talk, refusal) but adds hierarchy, persistent consequences, and instrumental stakes. Learning workplace pragmatics is learning to operate ALL the mechanisms simultaneously, calibrated to a specific environment.

2. **Two hierarchies operate simultaneously.** The formal hierarchy (org chart) determines register requirements. The informal hierarchy (social capital) determines real influence. Learning the informal hierarchy requires observation and explicit asking -- it's not documented anywhere.

3. **The professional persona is not masking -- it's register.** Everyone shifts to a professional register at work. The autistic-specific challenge is that the shift requires more conscious effort and the rules are harder to read. But it IS the same system that neurotypical people use -- it's a universal shift, not a demand placed only on autistic individuals.

4. **Direction determines hedging level.** Upward requests (to bosses) require maximum hedging. Lateral requests (to peers) require moderate hedging with reciprocity. Downward communication (to reports) requires less hedging but MORE clarity. When you have power, clarity is kindness.

5. **The employment gap is a pragmatic gap, not a skills gap.** Research consistently shows that autistic adults have the technical skills for employment but face systematic barriers in the communicative aspects of work. This knowledge base exists to help close that gap by making the implicit explicit.

6. **Workplace small talk is a high-ROI investment.** 3-5 minutes of daily small talk builds trust, creates information channels, and generates social capital. The return is disproportionate to the cost.

7. **Disclosure is a strategic decision with no universal right answer.** The decision depends on organizational culture, manager relationship, specific needs, and personal risk tolerance. Targeted disclosure ("I process better in writing") can be more effective than categorical disclosure ("I'm autistic").

8. **Autistic communication IS a workplace strength in the right contexts.** Clarity, precision, honesty, attention to detail, systematic thinking, and deep focus are competitive advantages. The challenge is in contexts that prioritize social performance over content quality.

9. **"I want to make sure I get this right" is the most useful workplace phrase.** It reframes requests for clarification as conscientiousness rather than confusion. It works upward, laterally, and downward.

10. **Recovery matters more than perfection.** Every professional makes pragmatic errors. The recovery -- "I didn't realize; thanks for letting me know!" -- determines the lasting impression more than the original mistake.
