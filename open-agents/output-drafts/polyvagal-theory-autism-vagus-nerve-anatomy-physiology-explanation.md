# Vagus Nerve Anatomy & Physiology

## Why this chapter exists

The polyvagal debate is unusually polarized. Proponents and critics often talk past each other because they're making different *kinds* of claims — anatomical, evolutionary, clinical, interpretive — without separating them. The honest way to evaluate the controversy is to start from the floor everyone agrees on, which is the actual neuroanatomy of the vagus nerve.

This chapter is that floor. Everything here is uncontested across mainstream neuroanatomy. The Foundations chapter (Category 1) covers what Porges *claims* about the vagus. The Critique chapter (Category 3) covers where the Untenable consensus says those claims fail. This page deliberately stays out of both of those interpretive frames. The aim is that after you've read this, you can read Categories 1 and 3 and tell which sentences are anatomy and which are interpretation.

If you're autistic and pattern-oriented, this layout should suit you: structured, factual, with the interpretive load held back until later chapters. The teaching moments where popular polyvagal writing tends to overstate or oversimplify are flagged as we encounter them — not to settle the debate here, but to give you the anatomical handles you'll need when the debate comes up.

## Overview

The vagus nerve is the **tenth cranial nerve** (CN X). It is the longest and most branched cranial nerve. The name comes from Latin *vagus*, "wandering" — accurate, because it wanders from the medulla all the way down to roughly the splenic flexure of the colon, branching into nearly every major thoracic and upper abdominal organ along the way.

A few facts you should leave this chapter holding:

1. **Two motor nuclei.** The vagus has two motor nuclei in the medulla — the **dorsal motor nucleus of the vagus (DMN)** and the **nucleus ambiguus (NA)** — plus a primary sensory relay (the **nucleus tractus solitarius**, NTS). DMN and NA do different things. They have different fiber types and different projection targets.

2. **The vagus is mostly sensory.** About **80% of vagal fibers are afferent** — carrying interoceptive information up from the body. Only about 20% are efferent. This is the *single most important fact* for evaluating popular polyvagal writing, which tends to emphasize the small fraction of myelinated efferent fibers that produce the "vagal brake" on the heart.

3. **The vagus is mostly unmyelinated.** Roughly **80% of vagal fibers are unmyelinated C-fibers** — slow-conducting, small-diameter. The faster myelinated fibers are a minority, and most of those are sensory rather than motor.

4. **Respiratory sinus arrhythmia (RSA) is real.** It's a rhythmic acceleration of heart rate on inhale, deceleration on exhale, mediated by the nucleus ambiguus and gated by the respiratory rhythm generator. But it's also confounded by a dozen things — breathing rate, tidal volume, body position, age, fitness, medications — so a single "vagal tone" reading is harder to interpret than wearable apps tend to admit.

These four facts are the spine of this chapter. The rest is connective tissue.

**Confidence overview.** Based on independent validation: 11 of 14 anatomical claims confirmed; 3 rated uncertain (all involving precision around fiber percentages — directionally correct, specifically rounded approximations); 0 invalid. The exact 80%/90%/70% breakdown traces to Foley & DuBois 1937 in cats and is widely treated as a teaching shorthand rather than a precision constant. Modern morphometry (Settell 2020 in pigs) confirms the general picture with some species and cervical-level variation.

## Prerequisites

You don't need much. Helpful to have a rough sense of:

- **Brainstem** as the medulla-pons-midbrain region between spinal cord and forebrain.
- **Parasympathetic** as one branch of the autonomic nervous system, generally promoting "rest and digest" states.
- **Cranial nerves** as 12 paired nerves emerging from the brain rather than the spinal cord.
- **Afferent** vs. **efferent**: afferent = toward the CNS (sensory); efferent = away from CNS (motor).
- **Myelinated** vs. **unmyelinated**: myelin is a fatty insulation around axons that speeds signal conduction. Myelinated fibers conduct faster.

That's enough.

## Part 1: The Brainstem Nuclei

<!-- DIAGRAM: brainstem-anatomy -->

The medulla oblongata contains four nuclei associated with the vagus nerve. Three of them are operational; the fourth is a sensory relay specific to a small skin patch on the ear.

### The dorsal motor nucleus of the vagus (DMN)

Located in the **dorsal medial medulla**, just beneath the floor of the fourth ventricle. It's a long column of preganglionic parasympathetic neurons running roughly parallel to the midline.

**What it does**: provides the bulk of parasympathetic visceromotor output to the gut, GI tract, pancreas, liver, and proximal colon. Slow, tonic modulation of digestive function.

**Fiber type**: predominantly **unmyelinated** C-fibers. Slow-conducting (about 0.5–2.5 m/s).

**Projection targets**: primarily *subdiaphragmatic* — below the diaphragm. Stomach (motility, acid secretion). Pancreas. Liver. Small intestine throughout its length. Proximal half of large intestine, up to the splenic flexure of the colon. Some contribution to lung.

Functional shorthand: think of the DMN as the **gut nucleus**. Slow. Unmyelinated. Digestive parasympathetic output.

**One refinement to know.** A recent paper (Coverdell et al. 2024 in *iScience*) showed that DMN neurons can also project to the heart and produce bradycardia and anxiolytic effects in mice. So the clean teaching dichotomy of "DMN = subdiaphragmatic only, NA = supradiaphragmatic only" is a strong tendency rather than an absolute rule. For pedagogy, the dichotomy holds. For precision, there's some overlap.

### The nucleus ambiguus (NA)

Located in the **ventrolateral medulla**, deeper and more ventral than the DMN. A column of cells embedded in the reticular formation.

**What it does**: two distinct populations.

- **Branchial motor neurons**: innervate the striated (voluntary-like) muscles of the soft palate, pharynx, larynx, and upper esophagus. These are the muscles you use for swallowing and speaking.
- **Cardio-inhibitory parasympathetic neurons**: located in the NA's "external formation." These are the predominant source of cardiac parasympathetic output in mammals — the famous "vagal brake" on heart rate. They synapse on cardiac ganglia near the sinoatrial node and inhibit pacemaker firing.

**Fiber type**: predominantly **myelinated B-fibers** (the cardio-inhibitory output, ~3–15 m/s) plus myelinated A-fibers (branchial motor to laryngeal/pharyngeal muscles).

**Projection targets**: *supradiaphragmatic* — above the diaphragm. Heart (rate control). Larynx (via the recurrent laryngeal nerve). Pharynx. Soft palate. Upper esophagus.

Functional shorthand: think of the NA as the **face-voice-heart nucleus**. Faster. Myelinated. The seat of the moment-to-moment cardiac brake and the motor control of voice and swallow.

### The nucleus tractus solitarius (NTS)

Located in the dorsomedial medulla, adjacent to the DMN. Anatomically a long column running through the medulla.

**What it does**: the *sensory* relay. It receives the central termination of visceral afferents traveling in the tractus solitarius from CN VII (facial — taste anterior tongue), CN IX (glossopharyngeal — taste posterior tongue, carotid sinus/body), and CN X (vagus — everything else).

**Why it matters**: this is where the ~80% of vagal fibers that are afferent first enter the central nervous system. Stretch in the lung, distension in the gut, change in blood pressure at the carotid sinus, vibration of the vocal folds — all of this arrives at the NTS.

From there, the NTS projects to the parabrachial nucleus (further brainstem integration), hypothalamus (endocrine and homeostatic adjustment), amygdala (affective evaluation), and via thalamus to the **insular cortex** — the cortical substrate for conscious interoception.

If you've done any reading on interoception, this is the pathway. Bird, Murphy, Brewer, and Garfinkel's work on autism-interoception-alexithymia rests on this anatomical chain: visceral afferents → NTS → parabrachial → thalamus → insula.

### The spinal trigeminal nucleus relay

A small detail, included because it matters for one specific intervention. The auricular branch of the vagus (Arnold's nerve) supplies a small patch of skin on the external ear. Its central termination is in the spinal trigeminal nucleus, which relays to NTS. This anatomical route is the basis for **transcutaneous auricular vagus nerve stimulation (taVNS)** — surface electrodes on the ear, intended to activate vagal afferents non-invasively. Covered in detail in the Interventions chapter (Category 6).

## Part 2: The Two Motor Nuclei and Their Targets

<!-- DIAGRAM: two-nuclei-projection-targets -->

The most useful single anatomical fact to internalize is the projection split between DMN and NA. They're both efferent nuclei, but they project to mostly different places, via mostly different fiber types, doing mostly different jobs.

**Above the diaphragm (NA territory, mostly myelinated)**:
- Heart — the cardio-inhibitory "vagal brake." Moment-to-moment HR control. NA fires; ACh releases at SA node; pacemaker slows.
- Larynx — via the recurrent laryngeal nerve (a famously long nerve that loops down around the aortic arch before returning up). Voice production, cough, airway protection.
- Pharynx — swallowing.
- Soft palate — closure during swallow.
- Upper esophagus.

**Below the diaphragm (DMN territory, mostly unmyelinated)**:
- Stomach — gastric motility, acid secretion.
- Pancreas — exocrine secretion.
- Liver.
- Small intestine — throughout.
- Proximal colon — up to the splenic flexure.

**Where the vagus stops**: at the splenic flexure of the colon. Distal colon, sigmoid, and rectum get their parasympathetic supply from sacral spinal nerves (S2–S4), not vagus. This boundary is anatomically clean. If you're reading "the vagus controls your whole gut" in popular writing, that's a small but real overstatement.

**Lungs**: mixed. NA contributes some, DMN contributes some. The lung is innervated by both nuclei via somewhat overlapping pathways.

The clinical implication is that "vagal" interventions targeting different parts of the body are recruiting different nuclei. Slow breathing or paced cardiac biofeedback engages the NA. Things that engage the gut (post-meal physiology, gut peptide signaling) involve DMN. There is no single "the vagus" knob.

## Part 3: Fiber Composition (and Why It Matters)

<!-- DIAGRAM: fiber-composition -->

Now we come to the most important factual claim in this chapter, because it's the one that most directly affects how to read popular polyvagal writing.

### What the vagus actually contains

Using classical morphometric data (Foley & DuBois 1937 in cats, replicated and refined since), the vagus contains roughly:

- **~80% afferent (sensory) fibers** carrying signals from viscera up to the brain
- **~20% efferent (motor) fibers** carrying signals from the brain out to viscera

And of those:

- **~90% of afferents are unmyelinated C-fibers** — slow-conducting, small-diameter
- **~70% of efferents are unmyelinated C-fibers** (these are mostly from the DMN)
- **~30% of efferents are myelinated B-fibers** (these are mostly from the NA — including the cardio-inhibitory "vagal brake")

> **Evidence note**: These percentages are textbook approximations from older morphometry, not measured constants. They're accurate within roughly ±5–10% but vary by species and cervical level. Modern morphometry (Settell et al. 2020 in pigs) reports about 47 ± 7% myelinated fibers in pig cervical vagus, suggesting the numbers shift somewhat across species. The robust point — vagus is afferent-dominant and predominantly unmyelinated — survives despite the imprecision in the specific percentages.

So when you multiply through: roughly **6% of all vagal fibers** are the myelinated B-fibers that constitute the "vagal brake." The other 94% are doing other things.

### Why this matters

A popular polyvagal explanation tends to go something like: *the myelinated vagus supports social engagement and the ventral vagal state; activating it calms you down through the cardiac brake.* Anatomically, the cardiac brake is real. The cells exist; the fibers run; the SA node responds. That part is uncontested.

What the popular framing tends to gloss over is that the cardiac brake describes a small minority of vagal fibers. Most of what the vagus does is sense the body and modulate the gut. When someone "stimulates the vagus" — through breath, cold exposure, or electrical stimulation — they are mostly recruiting *sensory* fibers that send signals *up* to the brainstem, not motor fibers that send signals down to the heart. Any downstream calming effect comes from upstream regulatory circuits responding to interoceptive input, not from direct efferent action of the stimulated fibers in most cases.

This is not a critique of polyvagal theory per se; that's Category 3's job. It's a teaching note. The next time you read a popular description that treats "the vagus" as synonymous with "the cardiac brake," you have the anatomical context to know that's a partial picture.

### What the fiber types mean functionally

| Fiber type | Conduction speed | What they do in the vagus |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------------------|
| A-fibers (myelinated, large) | 15–120 m/s | Sensory visceral afferents (some), plus motor to larynx/pharynx from NA |
| B-fibers (myelinated, small) | 3–15 m/s | Preganglionic parasympathetic to heart — the "vagal brake" |
| C-fibers (unmyelinated) | ~0.5–2.5 m/s | Vast majority of vagal afferents (interoceptive sensation); most DMN efferents (slow GI parasympathetic) |

A useful intuition pump: the difference between B-fibers and C-fibers is roughly the difference between a fast hardwired internet connection and a slow dial-up. Both connect, but they support different operations. Moment-to-moment cardiac modulation needs B-fiber speeds; tonic gut secretion can be served by C-fiber speeds.

## Part 4: Afferent Dominance — What the 80% Do

The most under-emphasized fact about vagal anatomy is that the nerve is fundamentally a sensory system. The motor function is real but is a minority of the nerve's bandwidth.

The afferents carry several streams of interoceptive information:

- **Cardiovascular**: arterial baroreceptors (carotid sinus, aortic arch) signal blood pressure; chemoreceptors (carotid body, aortic body) signal blood gas levels and pH.
- **Pulmonary**: stretch receptors in airways and lung parenchyma signal lung volume and airway irritation.
- **Gastric and intestinal**: mechanoreceptors signal distension; chemoreceptors signal nutrients, osmolarity, gut peptide levels.
- **Hepatic portal**: glucose, lipid, and other circulating signals.
- **Laryngeal**: position of vocal folds, irritation triggering cough.
- **Auricular**: a small patch of external ear (basis for taVNS).

These signals enter the NTS and then cascade through the integration nuclei. Most of what they do is *not* available to conscious experience. They modulate breathing, heart rate, gastric motility, hormone release, and a host of other autonomic functions automatically. A subset reaches the insular cortex and contributes to conscious interoceptive perception — knowing that you're hungry, that your heart is racing, that your stomach is tight.

This is why "the vagus" matters for emotion regulation, autonomic balance, and interoceptive awareness, even though most of its fibers are not the famous cardiac brake. The afferent stream is the body-to-brain pipe. What the brain does with that pipe shapes everything downstream.

## Part 5: Where the Vagus Goes — The Anatomical Map

<!-- DIAGRAM: vagus-anatomical-map -->

The vagus exits the skull at the jugular foramen and descends bilaterally — there is a left vagus and a right vagus, with different but overlapping innervation patterns. Both descend through the carotid sheath in the neck, into the thorax, and through the esophageal hiatus of the diaphragm into the abdomen.

Major branches along the way:

**Cervical**:
- Auricular branch (the small patch of ear; basis for taVNS)
- Pharyngeal branches (swallow)
- Superior laryngeal nerve (upper larynx, sensory)
- Recurrent laryngeal nerve (intrinsic laryngeal muscles; loops around aortic arch on left, subclavian artery on right)
- Cardiac branches (cervical cardiac, descending toward heart)
- Carotid sinus afferents (via the carotid sinus nerve interface with CN IX)

**Thoracic**:
- Pulmonary branches (lungs)
- Cardiac branches (heart, joining cervical cardiac branches at the cardiac plexus)
- Esophageal plexus (esophagus)

**Abdominal**:
- Gastric branches (stomach)
- Hepatic branch (liver)
- Celiac branches (pancreas, spleen, kidney)
- Mesenteric branches (small intestine, proximal colon up to splenic flexure)

The right and left vagus have somewhat different distributions. Cardiac vagal innervation is mostly bilateral but the right vagus tends to dominate sinoatrial node control while the left is more involved with atrioventricular conduction.

The functional implication: vagal effects on different organs depend on which branches are activated. A face-cold exposure (diving reflex) activates trigeminal afferents that trigger brainstem-mediated cardiac vagal output — but the cardiac efferents are NA-mediated and myelinated, so the response is fast (seconds). A meal activates DMN gastric efferents — slow tonic modulation operating over many minutes.

## Part 6: The Face-Heart Cranial Nerve Circuit

<!-- DIAGRAM: face-heart-cranial-circuit -->

Several cranial nerves cluster around the medulla and adjacent pons, share overlapping nuclei, and collectively innervate face, middle ear, voice, swallowing, and heart. This anatomical clustering is *uncontested*.

The nerves involved:

| CN | Name | What it innervates (relevant subset) |
|----|------|--------------------------------------|
| V | Trigeminal | Face sensation; muscles of mastication; **tensor tympani** in middle ear |
| VII | Facial | Facial expression muscles; **stapedius** in middle ear; taste anterior tongue; lacrimal/salivary parasympathetic |
| IX | Glossopharyngeal | Stylopharyngeus (swallow); taste posterior tongue; carotid sinus afferents |
| X | Vagus | Larynx, pharynx, heart, lungs, gut (as discussed) |
| XI | Accessory (spinal) | Sternocleidomastoid, trapezius (head orientation) |

A few things about this cluster that aren't controversial:

- **Tensor tympani** (CN V) and **stapedius** (CN VII) are tiny muscles in the middle ear. They tighten the ossicles during loud sound exposure, dampening transmission. This is the **acoustic reflex**.
- CN V and VII share brainstem real estate. CN IX and X both contribute to the nucleus ambiguus.
- The recurrent laryngeal nerve (a vagal branch) provides motor supply to most intrinsic laryngeal muscles. This is the substrate for voice prosody — pitch, volume, intonation.
- CN XI (spinal accessory) turns the head. It's anatomically separate from the others (its nucleus is in the upper cervical spinal cord, not the medulla) but it gets functionally grouped here because head orientation toward sound and gaze is part of the same social-perceptual loop.

### What's interpretation, not anatomy

The polyvagal theory packages this cluster as a unified "social engagement system" coordinated by the myelinated cardiac vagus from the nucleus ambiguus. The construct of a unified social engagement system is *Polyvagal Theory's interpretation* of the anatomical clustering. Mainstream neuroanatomy notes the clustering as observation but does not commit to the unified-system construct. Whether the cluster behaves as a coordinated functional system in vivo, with the myelinated cardiac vagus as its central regulator, is the *empirical question* that Category 3 examines.

For purposes of this chapter: the cranial nerves are real, the clustering is real, the muscles and their innervations are real. The teaching note is that you'll see this anatomy invoked both by polyvagal proponents (as a "social engagement system") and by critics (as "an anatomical cluster on which a specific functional interpretation has been layered"). Both can point to the same diagram and tell different stories.

## Part 7: Respiratory Sinus Arrhythmia (RSA)

<!-- DIAGRAM: rsa-mechanism -->

Now we turn from anatomy to functional measurement. RSA is the single most-cited noninvasive index of cardiac vagal control. It deserves careful treatment because it's also where popular polyvagal writing most often goes wrong — by treating RSA as a precision biomarker of "vagal tone" rather than as a state-dependent measurement subject to many confounds.

### What RSA is

Heart rate is not constant. In healthy resting adults, it varies rhythmically with the respiratory cycle: speeds up slightly on inhale, slows on exhale. This rhythmic modulation is RSA. It's most pronounced at slow breathing rates (around 6 breaths/minute) and diminishes at faster breathing.

### Why it happens

The dominant model attributes RSA to **rhythmic vagal gating of cardiac output**. The brainstem respiratory rhythm generator (centered on the pre-Bötzinger complex in the medulla) generates the breathing cycle. During inspiration, it sends an inhibitory signal to the cardiac vagal preganglionic neurons in the nucleus ambiguus. Those neurons go quiet; the vagal brake is released; heart rate accelerates toward the SA node's intrinsic pacemaker rate (around 100–110 bpm in adults). During expiration, the inhibition stops; the vagal brake reapplies; heart rate decelerates.

A secondary contribution comes from baroreflex modulation. Breathing-related changes in venous return and blood pressure trigger reflex adjustments that compound with the central gating. The relative weighting of central feedforward versus peripheral feedback contributions is debated in detail — see Eckberg's work for the feedforward emphasis — but the basic picture (vagally-mediated, respiratory-coupled) is uncontested.

### How RSA is measured

Several methods:

- **Spectral analysis** of the inter-beat interval time series. Compute the high-frequency band (0.15–0.40 Hz). HF power is RSA power.
- **Peak-to-trough method**: the difference between maximum and minimum heart rate within each respiratory cycle.
- **Porges-Bohrer method**: band-pass filter centered on respiratory frequency.

For most purposes — including most published research and most wearable devices — HF power or RMSSD (which closely correlates with HF) is the practical index.

## Part 8: RSA Confounds — Why "Vagal Tone" Readings Mislead

<!-- DIAGRAM: rsa-confounds -->

RSA is real. It is also surprisingly easy to push around with factors that have nothing to do with underlying parasympathetic function. The major confounds:

- **Breathing rate.** Slower breathing produces larger RSA. The "resonance frequency" — where RSA peaks — is around 6 breaths/minute for most adults. Voluntary paced breathing at this rate dramatically inflates RSA amplitude. This is the physiological basis of HRV biofeedback, but it also means that a "vagal tone" reading taken during paced breathing isn't comparable to one taken during normal breathing.

- **Tidal volume.** Deeper breathing produces larger RSA. Doubling tidal volume in the supine position can roughly double RSA amplitude. Asking someone to "breathe deeply" before a measurement mechanically inflates the number.

- **Body position.** Supine RSA > sitting RSA > standing RSA. Head-down tilt produces extreme RSA.

- **Age.** RSA peaks in young adulthood and declines roughly monotonically with age. A 65-year-old's "low" RSA may be perfectly normal for age.

- **Fitness.** Endurance-trained adults have higher resting RSA. Some of what looks like "high vagal tone" is fitness.

- **Medications.** Beta-blockers, atropine, anticholinergics, SSRIs, SNRIs, and several other commonly prescribed drugs all affect RSA. Any reading needs interpretation in light of medication status.

- **Voluntary respiratory control.** If you know your breathing is being measured, you may breathe more deliberately. This alone changes the reading.

- **Recent meal, caffeine, alcohol.** All shift autonomic baseline.

- **Sleep stage and time of day.** Diurnal and ultradian variation.

> **Evidence note**: Beauchaine (2015, [PMC4389219](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4389219/)) reviews RSA as a transdiagnostic biomarker of emotion dysregulation. He emphasizes that **RSA reactivity** (change during a task) is generally more clinically informative than RSA baseline level — partly because reactivity within a single individual controls for most of the confounds above.

### What this means for using HRV data

For an individual paying attention to their own data — say, an autistic adult tracking HRV via a wearable for self-knowledge — a few practical principles:

- **Compare yourself to yourself**, not to population norms or other people.
- Take readings at the **same time of day**, in the **same position**, in roughly the **same recent-meal/medication state**.
- **Pay attention to trends across weeks** rather than to single daily readings.
- **Trends matter more than absolute values**.
- Don't read your wearable's "vagal tone" label as a diagnostic measurement of vagal function — read it as "this metric of this person at this moment under uncontrolled conditions."
- If using paced breathing as part of the protocol, **use the same pace every time** so that the breathing-rate effect is held constant.

For someone reading the polyvagal literature: when a study cites "RSA differences" between groups, ask: was breathing rate controlled? was body position controlled? were medications matched? was the comparison baseline vs. baseline, or reactivity vs. reactivity? These methodological details determine whether the finding is interpretable.

## Part 9: Other HRV Metrics

<!-- DIAGRAM: hrv-metrics-comparison -->

RSA is one window onto heart rate variability. There are others. Each captures a slightly different aspect of autonomic modulation. The Task Force of the European Society of Cardiology (1996) standardized the major metrics.

### Time-domain metrics

- **RMSSD** — Root Mean Square of Successive Differences. Computed directly from successive RR-interval differences. **Primarily vagal/parasympathetic**. Closely correlates with HF (the frequency-domain analog). For short recordings (≤5 minutes), this is generally the best single vagal index.
- **SDNN** — Standard Deviation of NN intervals across the recording. Captures overall variability; mixed sympathetic + parasympathetic contributions. Used for 24-hour ambulatory recordings.
- **SDANN** — Standard Deviation of the Average NN intervals over 5-minute segments. Captures long-period (hour-scale) rhythms. Only meaningful in 24-hour data.
- **pNN50** — percentage of successive RR intervals differing by >50 ms. Vagal index similar to RMSSD. Older, mostly replaced by RMSSD.

### Frequency-domain metrics

- **HF** (high frequency, 0.15–0.40 Hz) — corresponds to typical adult breathing rates; captures RSA. Vagal.
- **LF** (low frequency, 0.04–0.15 Hz) — mixed band. Includes baroreflex modulation, some sympathetic and parasympathetic contribution. **Not a clean sympathetic index.**
- **VLF** (very low frequency, 0.0033–0.04 Hz) — slow rhythms: thermoregulation, hormonal, RAAS. Only meaningful in 24-hour data.
- **LF/HF ratio** — historically interpreted as "sympathovagal balance." **This interpretation is largely abandoned in serious HRV research.** Heathers (2012) titled their critique "Sympathovagal balance from heart rate variability: an obituary." Billman (2013) showed the assumptions underlying the ratio (LF as pure sympathetic, HF as pure parasympathetic, linear interaction) don't hold. Consumer wearables and some older clinical writeups still display it; treat with skepticism.

### Practical relationships

- **RMSSD ≈ HF**: both index cardiac vagal control. Correlate 0.85–0.95 in most recordings.
- For short recordings, prefer **RMSSD** in time domain or **HF** in frequency domain.
- For 24-hour ambulatory data, **SDNN** captures global variability.
- **Avoid reading meaning from LF/HF.**

## Common Misconceptions

### "Stimulating the vagus directly relaxes you because the vagus is the parasympathetic nerve"

**Reality**: The vagus is mostly *afferent* (sensory). Stimulating it sends signals up to the brainstem, which then orchestrates downstream responses through multiple pathways. Any calming effect is the result of upstream regulatory circuits responding to interoceptive input, not direct efferent action of the stimulated fibers (in most cases). The mechanism is more "uploading interoceptive signal to regulatory networks" than "pressing a relaxation button."

### "The vagus nerve is the 'vagal brake'"

**Reality**: The cardiac brake from nucleus ambiguus is one specific subset of vagal output — by approximate fiber count, around 6% of all vagal fibers. The other 94% are doing other things (sensory traffic, gut/digestive modulation, voice/swallow motor control). Treating "vagus" and "vagal brake" as synonymous is a common simplification but not anatomically accurate.

### "LF/HF ratio measures sympathetic-to-parasympathetic balance"

**Reality**: This interpretation has been substantially undermined in the modern HRV literature (Billman 2013, Heathers 2012). LF power includes baroreflex contributions and is not a clean sympathetic index. The "balance" claim assumes a simplistic linear interaction that doesn't hold in practice. Avoid reading meaning from LF/HF.

### "RSA is a stable trait — your 'vagal tone' is a fixed number"

**Reality**: RSA is highly state-dependent. Breathing rate, tidal volume, body position, fitness, medications, time of day, recent meals — all shift it. Trends over time under controlled conditions are interpretable; single readings under uncontrolled conditions are not.

### "The dorsal motor nucleus controls 'shutdown' or 'freeze' states"

**Reality**: This is a polyvagal-specific functional label that the broader neuroanatomy literature does not endorse. Mainstream physiology characterizes the DMN by what it innervates (subdiaphragmatic viscera — gut, GI tract) and by its fiber type (unmyelinated). Whether DMN neurons mediate freeze responses in mammals is the subject of active scientific debate covered in Category 3 (Neuhuber & Berthoud 2022 argue against this functional attribution). For this anatomy chapter, the DMN is best described by its targets and fibers, not by a behavioral state label.

### "Mammals uniquely have a myelinated cardiac vagus from nucleus ambiguus"

**Reality**: This claim is foundational to polyvagal theory's evolutionary premise — but is contested by comparative neuroanatomy. Sharks, lungfish, bony fish, amphibians, reptiles, and birds also show myelinated cardiac vagal projections from ventrolateral brainstem sources. The shark's ambiguus homologue innervates the heart entirely with myelinated axons. In mammals, the nucleus ambiguus is *dominant* for cardiac parasympathetic supply — that's accurate. But this is not a uniquely mammalian innovation. Category 3 covers this dispute in detail; for this chapter, just note that the evolutionary uniqueness claim is one of the contested premises rather than uncontested anatomy.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **Bilateral vagotomy**: historically used for peptic ulcer disease. Profound effects on gastric motility and acid secretion; modest effect on resting heart rate. Illustrates the regional specificity of vagal function.
- **Bezold-Jarisch reflex**: vagal afferents from cardiac chemoreceptors trigger profound bradycardia and hypotension. Sometimes invoked in fainting episodes. A pure vagal reflex example.
- **Diving reflex**: facial cold exposure → trigeminal afferents → brainstem → vagal cardiac efferents → bradycardia. Present across many mammals and some other vertebrates.
- **Developmental trajectory**: fetal and neonatal vagal cardiac dominance is less than adult. Baseline RSA is lower in infancy and rises through childhood. Has implications for interpreting pediatric autism HRV research (covered in Category 4).
- **Implanted cervical VNS** (FDA-approved for refractory epilepsy and treatment-resistant depression): primarily activates afferent A and B fibers because they have lower stimulation thresholds. The reflex modulation upstream is the presumed therapeutic mechanism.

- **Category 1 (Foundations)** uses the anatomy here to describe what polyvagal theory claims — the "ventral vagal complex," the "dorsal vagal complex," the "social engagement system."
- **Category 3 (Critique)** uses the anatomy here to argue that several of those polyvagal claims are inconsistent with comparative neuroanatomy. The cross-species myelinated cardiac vagus issue (sharks, lungfish, etc.) is central to that critique.
- **Category 4 (Autism Vagal Tone Evidence)** uses RSA and the broader HRV metric family to discuss baseline and reactivity differences in autistic populations. The confounds section above is directly relevant.
- **Category 6 (Interventions)** uses the anatomy to evaluate interventions like slow breathing, HRV biofeedback, taVNS, and the Safe and Sound Protocol. The auricular branch / spinal trigeminal nucleus pathway is the substrate for taVNS.
- **Category 9 (Alternative Models)** discusses the Neurovisceral Integration Model (Thayer & Lane), which uses the vmPFC → amygdala → vagus circuit without polyvagal evolutionary claims, and the predictive processing / active inference frameworks, which build on interoceptive afferent integration in the insula.

When you read those chapters, the anatomy here should help you keep track of which claims are factual ground and which are interpretation.

## Key Points Summary

1. The vagus has **two motor nuclei** (DMN, NA) and a **sensory relay** (NTS) in the medulla. DMN handles gut/GI parasympathetic with mostly unmyelinated fibers. NA handles heart/larynx/pharynx with mostly myelinated fibers.

2. About **80% of vagal fibers are afferent** (sensory). The vagus is primarily a sensing organ that secondarily controls some parasympathetic output.

3. About **80% of vagal fibers are unmyelinated C-fibers**. The famous "vagal brake" describes a small minority (~6%) of all vagal fibers.

4. The DMN projects mostly **subdiaphragmatically** (gut, intestine, pancreas, liver, proximal colon). The NA projects mostly **supradiaphragmatically** (heart, larynx, pharynx). This is a useful first approximation with some real exceptions.

5. The vagus stops at the **splenic flexure of the colon**. Distal colon and rectum get sacral parasympathetic supply, not vagus.

6. The **face-heart cranial nerve cluster** (CN V, VII, IX, X, XI) is real, uncontested anatomy. Whether it functions as a unified "social engagement system" is the polyvagal-specific interpretive layer.

7. **RSA** is the rhythmic respiratory-coupled fluctuation in heart rate, mediated by NA cardiac neurons gated by the brainstem respiratory rhythm generator.

8. RSA is **state-dependent** with many confounds (breathing rate, tidal volume, body position, age, fitness, medications). Single readings under uncontrolled conditions are not reliable diagnostics.

9. For practical HRV interpretation, **RMSSD** (time domain) or **HF** (frequency domain) is the appropriate vagal index. **LF/HF ratio** is largely abandoned as a sympathovagal balance measure.

10. Quoted percentages (80%, 90%, 70%) are **textbook approximations** with ±5–10% variability across species and cervical levels. The qualitative facts (afferent-dominant, predominantly unmyelinated) are robust; the precise numbers should be treated with appropriate hedging.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Vagus nerve (CN X)** | Tenth cranial nerve; longest cranial nerve; mixed motor/sensory; primary parasympathetic supply to most thoracic and upper abdominal viscera |
| **DMN / DMV / DVN** | Dorsal motor nucleus of the vagus — parasympathetic preganglionic neurons; mostly unmyelinated; targets subdiaphragmatic viscera |
| **Nucleus ambiguus (NA)** | Motor nucleus in ventrolateral medulla; gives rise to branchial motor fibers (larynx, pharynx) and cardio-inhibitory parasympathetic fibers (heart); mostly myelinated |
| **NTS** | Nucleus tractus solitarius — primary central relay for visceral sensory afferents from CN VII, IX, X |
| **Afferent** | Carrying signal toward the central nervous system; sensory |
| **Efferent** | Carrying signal away from the central nervous system; motor |
| **Myelinated** | Axon wrapped in lipid sheath; faster conduction |
| **Unmyelinated** | Axon without lipid sheath; slower conduction; small diameter |
| **A-fibers** | Largest myelinated axons (15–120 m/s) |
| **B-fibers** | Smaller myelinated axons (3–15 m/s); preganglionic parasympathetic |
| **C-fibers** | Unmyelinated axons (~0.5–2.5 m/s); majority of vagal fibers |
| **Interoception** | Sensing the internal state of the body |
| **RSA** | Respiratory sinus arrhythmia — rhythmic heart rate fluctuation with breathing cycle |
| **HRV** | Heart rate variability |
| **RMSSD** | Root mean square of successive RR-interval differences; short-term vagal time-domain index |
| **SDNN** | Standard deviation of NN intervals; overall variability |
| **HF** | High frequency (0.15–0.40 Hz) HRV power band; vagal/RSA |
| **LF** | Low frequency (0.04–0.15 Hz) HRV power band; mixed |
| **Vagal tone** | Loose term for tonic parasympathetic activity; usually operationalized as RMSSD or HF |
| **Vagal brake** | Tonic parasympathetic restraint on the SA node slowing heart rate below intrinsic pacemaker rate |
| **Subdiaphragmatic** | Below the diaphragm — abdominal viscera |
| **Supradiaphragmatic** | Above the diaphragm — thoracic and cervical structures |
