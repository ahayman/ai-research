# Category 15: Prosodic Stress, Focus, and Meaning-Selection in Autism

## Research Document for English Pragmatics Knowledge Base

**Focus**: How contrastive stress and prosodic focus interact with autism -- comprehension, production, and the teaching opportunity

**Scope Note**: Category 14's autism research covers broad prosodic differences (intonation range, rhythm, emotional prosody). This document focuses SPECIFICALLY on contrastive stress, focus marking, meaning-selection through stress placement, and the interaction of stress with sarcasm, hedging, and focus-sensitive particles like "only"/"even"/"just."

---

## 1. AUTISTIC PROCESSING OF CONTRASTIVE STRESS: THE CORE RESEARCH

### 1.1 What Contrastive Stress Is and Why It Matters

Contrastive stress (also called "narrow focus" or "contrastive pitch accent") is when a speaker places extra prominence on one word in a sentence to signal contrast, correction, or emphasis. The classic demonstration: "I never said she stole my money" has seven different meanings depending on which word is stressed -- each stress placement activates a different set of alternatives and thereby selects a different meaning (ELT-Cation, 2016; various teaching resources).

In linguistic terms, focus introduces a "focus semantic value" -- it signals the presence of alternatives relevant to interpretation (ScienceDirect, 2023). Stressing "WALKED" in "I WALKED to your house" isn't just emphasis; it *corrects* an implication (you drove), *excludes* alternatives (biked, ran), and *restructures* the information in the discourse. Missing this cue doesn't just miss emphasis -- it misses the entire pragmatic point of the utterance.

### 1.2 The Su et al. (2025) Study: Perception and Interpretation of Contrastive Pitch Accent

A major 2025 study by Pumpki Lei Su, Duane G. Watson, Stephen Camarata, and James Bodfish, published in *Languages* (MDPI), investigated how autistic children perceive and interpret contrastive pitch accent during spoken language processing.

**Key findings:**
- Autistic children as a group **were able to discriminate** prosodic forms (they could hear that stress was placed differently)
- Autistic children as a group **were able to interpret** the pragmatic function of contrastive pitch accent during spoken language comprehension
- **However**, differences emerged not in accuracy but in **speed of processing**: autistic children showed significantly slower reaction times during discrimination tasks
- A **subgroup of autistic children with language impairment** showed significantly slower processing of contrastive pitch accent in a visual-world paradigm task
- Speed of contrastive pitch accent processing was **significantly associated with pragmatic language skills and autism symptom severity**

**Critical implication**: The issue for many autistic individuals may not be inability to use contrastive stress, but rather a **processing speed penalty** -- in real-time conversation, the meaning encoded by stress placement may arrive too late to influence the ongoing interpretation. By the time the pragmatic significance of the stress registers, the conversation has moved on.

### 1.3 The Segal et al. (2017) Study: Narrow Focus Comprehension in Adolescents

Segal, Kaplan, Patael, and Kishon-Rabin (2017) published in *Folia Phoniatrica et Logopaedica* a study comparing 17 adolescents with ASD, 17 age-matched TD peers, and 15 vocabulary-matched TD children on the Hebrew Narrow Focus Test.

**Study design**: Three subtests --
- Subtest A: Identify which word is stressed (psychoacoustic ability only)
- Subtest B: Understand the meaning of focus stress in contrastive contexts
- Subtest C: Understand focus stress as indicating a mistake/correction

**Results**:
- ASD participants showed **reduced overall performance** compared to age-matched peers
- But showed **similar performance** to TD children on subtests A and B
- ASD participants performed **better** on subtest C (correction contexts)
- **Nonverbal intelligence** significantly correlated with performance on subtests B and C in the ASD group

**The authors' conclusion**: "Comprehension of narrow focus in adolescents with ASD is related to their cognitive-linguistic abilities and not to the autistic condition itself or to its severity."

This is an important finding: it suggests that with adequate cognitive-linguistic resources, autistic adolescents CAN comprehend contrastive stress -- the challenge may be more about processing speed and cognitive load than about a fundamental inability.

### 1.4 The Jiang & Wang (2025) Study: Prosodic Focus and "Only" Reading

Jiang and Wang (2025), published in the *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, investigated how prosodic focus affects the "covert only" interpretation of scalar quantifiers in Mandarin-speaking children aged 3-8.

**The critical finding**: 

> "Autistic children's comprehension was **unaffected by prosody**, even with basic theory of mind and executive function skills, with individual traits' influences on their interpretation minimally affected by focus, highlighting **reduced sensitivity**."

In contrast, non-autistic children showed prosodic focus sensitivity, using ToM and EF skills to derive prosodically-guided interpretations.

**What this means in practice**: When a non-autistic listener hears "She ate SOME of the cookies" (with stress on "some"), they derive an "only" inference -- she ate only some, not all. The stress on "some" activates the alternative "all" and triggers the scalar implicature. Autistic children in this study processed the sentence **as if no word had been specially stressed** -- they missed the pragmatic information entirely.

The authors attribute this to "autism's intrinsic aspects, likely linked to **information integration impairments** or **decreased social motivation**."

### 1.5 Earlier Foundational Studies

**Baltaxe (1984)**: One of the earliest studies, examining contrastive stress use in autistic children, found that contrastive stress is "an early developing device to mark the topic-comment distinction" and is "important for the acquisition of pragmatic knowledge." Autistic children showed impaired use of contrastive stress compared to typical peers, regardless of nonverbal IQ.

**Paul, Augustyn, Klin, & Volkmar (2005)**: Examined prosody perception and production in ASD speakers aged 14-21. Found significant between-group differences in stress perception and production, establishing that prosodic difficulties in autism extend beyond childhood into adolescence and adulthood.

**Diehl & Paul (2012, 2015)**: Multiple studies establishing that:
- Adolescents with high-functioning ASD have difficulty using prosody to disambiguate syntax
- The ASD group was **less likely to act in concordance with the prosodic cue** compared to TD peers
- Notably, in initial trials, children with ASD used prosodic cues **as rapidly and efficiently** as TD peers -- but when a different cue was presented in subsequent trials, the younger ASD group was more likely to **respond based on the initial cue** rather than adapting to the new one

This "stickiness" or perseveration effect is particularly relevant: autistic individuals may lock onto one interpretation and have difficulty flexibly updating it when prosodic cues shift.

**Drakopoulou Kalantzi (2024)**: A CUNY dissertation studying 42 children (26 NT, 16 autistic) on comprehension and production of contrastive reference. Found that younger children across both groups tended to be underinformative, but the decrease with age was more pronounced in autistic children. Older autistic children were also more likely to be **overinformative** -- providing unnecessary contrast where context made it redundant.

## 2. THE GRAMMATICAL vs. PRAGMATIC PROSODY DISTINCTION

### 2.1 The Grice et al. (2023) Framework

Grice, Wehrle, Kruger, Spaniol, Cangemi, and Vogeley (2023) published a comprehensive narrative review of 51 studies in *Language and Linguistics Compass*, establishing a critical distinction in prosodic functions:

**Formal/Grammatical prosody** (more rule-based, more preserved in autism):
- Lexical stress (which syllable carries stress: "REcord" vs. "reCORD")
- Lexical tone (in tonal languages)
- Syntactic structure marking (phrase boundaries, question intonation)
- These are "determined by rules and remain stable regardless of context"

**Pragmatic/Interactional prosody** (more context-dependent, more affected in autism):
- Information structure marking (new vs. given information)
- Contrastive focus (correcting or narrowing meaning)
- Attitude and affect signaling (sarcasm, uncertainty, enthusiasm)
- Turn-taking regulation
- These "require greater flexibility in communication and are highly context-dependent"

**The key finding from the review**: There is "tentative evidence for **greater differences in the perception of intuitive functions**" -- that is, pragmatic prosody is more impaired than grammatical prosody in autism.

### 2.2 Why This Distinction Matters

The grammatical-pragmatic distinction maps onto a deeper cognitive difference:

**Grammatical prosody** is like a lookup table: the stress pattern of "REcord" (noun) vs. "reCORD" (verb) is fixed. You learn the rule once and apply it categorically. This plays to autistic cognitive strengths in rule-learning and pattern recognition.

**Pragmatic prosody** requires real-time theory of mind: to stress the right word, you need to model what your listener already knows (given information) versus what's new (focus), what they might be assuming incorrectly (contrast), or what social/emotional coloring you want to add (affect). This requires exactly the kind of rapid, implicit social inference that autism makes more effortful.

### 2.3 The Wehrle et al. (2023) Profile Study

Wehrle and colleagues (2023), published in *Journal of Communication Disorders*, used the PEPS-C assessment (Profiling Elements of Prosodic Systems - Communication) to profile prosodic abilities across ASD individuals, their parents, and control groups.

**Profile findings:**
- Receptive prosody **exceeded** expressive prosody in both ASD and parent groups
- Receptive prosody deficits in ASD were observed specifically in **contrastive stress**
- Both ASD and parent groups showed reduced accuracy in imitation, lexical stress, and contrastive stress **expression**
- Lower accuracy across PEPS-C subtests related to increased **pragmatic language violations**
- Parents showed similar (but subtler) patterns, suggesting prosodic differences are part of the **broad autism phenotype** and may be heritable

### 2.4 The PEPS-C Assessment and What It Reveals

The PEPS-C (Peppe & McCann, 2003) breaks prosody into testable components:

- **Chunking**: Using prosodic boundaries to group words into phrases
- **Focus**: Using stress to signal which element is important, new, or contrastive
- **Affect**: Using prosodic features to convey emotion
- **Turn-end**: Using intonation to signal questions vs. statements

Studies using PEPS-C have consistently found that autistic individuals show difficulty with **Focus** (contrastive stress) in both receptive and expressive domains, with Focus difficulties being among the most reliable prosodic markers of ASD.

## 3. PRODUCTION OF CONTRASTIVE STRESS IN AUTISM

### 3.1 Stress Placement Errors

Research consistently finds atypical stress placement in autistic speech production:

- **Inappropriate stress location**: Autistic children showed ability to produce contrastive pitch accents, but the placement of the stress was "incorrect given the discourse context" (Production of Syllable Stress in Speakers with ASD, PMC, 2009)
- **Function word accentuation**: Studies report "inappropriate prosodic marking of information structure and frequent accentuation of function words (which usually have a reduced form and thus tend not to be accented)" (Grice et al., 2023). This means autistic speakers may stress "the," "a," "to," or "of" when they should be unstressed, creating a distinctive speech rhythm
- **Sentence-initial default**: Those with ASD "accentuated the beginning of a sentence irrespective of its information value" -- rather than placing stress on the most informationally relevant word, they defaulted to stressing early positions

### 3.2 The Depape et al. (2012) Paradox

Depape, Chen, Hall, and Trainor (2012) published in *Frontiers in Psychology* a study on prosody and information structure in high-functioning adults with autism, revealing a striking paradox:

- Adults with ASD with **high language functioning** generally used a **larger pitch range** than controls but **did not mark information structure** appropriately
- Adults with ASD with **moderate language functioning** generally used a **smaller pitch range** but **marked information structure appropriately** to a large extent

**Interpretation**: The high-language-functioning group may have learned that prosody involves pitch variation, but applied it globally rather than strategically. They used MORE melody but placed it in the wrong locations relative to the discourse structure. The moderate-language-functioning group used less variation overall but deployed it more accurately where it mattered.

This is crucial for understanding "robotic" vs. "singsong" autistic speech: both patterns may reflect the same underlying difficulty with pragmatic stress placement, just manifested differently.

### 3.3 The "Monotone" Myth

Research presents a more nuanced picture than the common characterization:

- While acoustic evidence shows most autistic speakers produced a "more melodic intonation style than controls," naive listeners perceived the speech of adults with ASD as "more monotonous" (Grice et al., 2023; Nature Scientific Reports, 2021)
- ASD speech has been variously described as "robotic," "wooden," "stilted," "monotone," "bizarre," "over precise," and "singsong" -- these contradictory descriptions reflect the heterogeneity
- The common thread is not absence of pitch variation but **misalignment of pitch variation with pragmatic function**: stress falls on the wrong words, pitch peaks occur at unexpected locations, and the prosodic pattern doesn't match what the listener expects for the discourse context

### 3.4 Broad Autism Phenotype Evidence

Prosodic differences extend to first-degree relatives:
- Parents of autistic individuals show "slower speech rate" similar to (but subtler than) the ASD group pattern (Wehrle et al., 2023)
- Greater utterance-final fundamental frequency excursion and slower speech rate were found in both ASD and parent groups
- These patterns are "etiologically meaningful" -- prosodic differences are not just a clinical feature but part of the genetic architecture of autism (Patel et al., 2019, 2020)

## 4. STRESS, SARCASM, AND AUTISM

### 4.1 The Prosodic Sarcasm Problem

Sarcasm represents the most consequential intersection of stress, meaning, and autism. Sarcasm is frequently cued **only** by prosodic features -- the words are literally true or at least plausible, and only the way they're said signals non-literal intent. This makes sarcasm detection heavily dependent on exactly the prosodic processing skills that are most challenged in autism.

### 4.2 The Cambridge JIPA Study: Prosody and Sarcasm

A study published in the *Journal of the International Phonetic Association* (Cambridge Core) examined whether prosody marks sarcasm **early** in an utterance (before the "target word" that carries the sarcastic payload):

**Study design:**
- 8 American English speakers recorded utterances in sarcastic vs. sincere contexts
- Pre-target portions were presented to listeners who either did (n=51) or did not (n=44) self-identify as being on the autism spectrum
- Stimuli were examined for syllable duration and f0-related properties

**The critical question**: Can listeners detect sarcasm from prosodic cues that appear **before** the key word? If so, sarcasm detection doesn't just require hearing the stressed/marked word -- it requires tracking prosodic patterns across the entire utterance from the very beginning.

This has profound implications for autistic listeners: if sarcasm is detectable only from early prosodic shaping (subtle pitch, timing, and rhythm changes that precede the punchline), then the processing delay identified in the Su et al. (2025) study becomes even more problematic. By the time the autistic listener registers that the prosody is "doing something unusual," the sarcastic utterance may already be complete.

### 4.3 Neural Basis of Irony Comprehension

Wang, Lee, Sigman, and Dapretto (2006), published in *Brain*, used fMRI to examine irony comprehension in high-functioning children with ASD:

**Behavioral findings:**
- Children with ASD were **less accurate** than TD children in detecting communicative intent, **particularly** when the event context strongly indicated non-literal interpretation
- There were significant differences in how children with ASD use prosodic **and** contextual cues to interpret irony

**Neural findings:**
- Children with ASD showed **greater activity** than TD children in the right inferior frontal gyrus and bilateral temporal regions
- This increased activation fell within the same network recruited by TD children
- The increased activation reflects **"more effortful processing needed to interpret the intended meaning"**

**Key insight**: Autistic children aren't using different brain regions for irony; they're using the SAME regions but working HARDER. This is consistent with compensation through increased cognitive effort rather than a fundamentally different processing pathway.

### 4.4 The Hedge-Stress-Sarcasm Triple Interaction

The user's original observation is that stress placement on hedges and minimizers can flip meaning to sarcasm. This creates a particular challenge for autistic processing because three systems interact simultaneously:

1. **The hedge system**: Words like "just," "only," "a little," "sort of" normally function as minimizers/softeners. Understanding their pragmatic function already requires contextual sensitivity.

2. **The stress system**: Placing contrastive stress on ANY word activates alternatives. Stressing "just" in "I was JUST trying to help" could emphasize the minimizing function (sincerely) OR could mark it sarcastically (implying the opposite).

3. **The sarcasm system**: Sarcastic stress on a hedge simultaneously activates the hedge's meaning AND its negation. "I'm SURE that'll work" uses stress on a certainty marker to signal uncertainty/doubt.

For an autistic listener who may already be processing hedges at a reduced confidence level (Category 14 findings) and processing stress placement with a time delay (Su et al., 2025), adding the sarcasm layer creates a computational challenge that may exceed real-time processing capacity.

## 5. STRESS AND FOCUS-SENSITIVE PARTICLES: "ONLY," "EVEN," "JUST"

### 5.1 How Focus Particles Work

Focus-sensitive particles are words whose meaning **depends on** what is stressed in the sentence. Their central property is that they are "focus sensitive -- they must occur with some phrase which is phonologically prominent" (Krifka, linguistics proceedings).

Examples:
- "John only ate the CAKE" → he ate nothing else (stress on "cake" = "only" restricts the object)
- "John only ATE the cake" → he didn't bake/buy it (stress on "ate" = "only" restricts the action)
- "JOHN only ate the cake" → nobody else did (stress on "John" = "only" restricts the agent)

The **same sentence with the same words** has three different meanings depending entirely on stress placement. The word "only" doesn't change position -- it's the prosodic focus that tells the listener what "only" is limiting.

### 5.2 The Jiang & Wang (2025) "Only" Finding -- Expanded

The Jiang & Wang (2025) study on scalar quantifiers is directly relevant:

- Non-autistic children used prosodic focus to derive the "covert only" reading (when "SOME" was stressed, they inferred "only some, not all")
- Autistic children's interpretation was **unaffected by prosody** -- they didn't derive the "only" inference more readily when prosodic focus was present
- This wasn't explained by basic ToM or EF skills -- even autistic children with adequate ToM and EF didn't show prosodic sensitivity
- The authors suggest this reflects something intrinsic to autism: "information integration impairments or decreased social motivation"

### 5.3 What Missing Focus-Sensitivity Means in Practice

If you don't track which word carries stress, you lose the ability to correctly interpret:

- **"Only"**: "She only READS the emails" (doesn't reply) vs. "She only reads the EMAILS" (not the reports)
- **"Even"**: "EVEN John passed" (surprising that John of all people passed) vs. "Even John PASSED" (surprising that passing happened at all)
- **"Just"**: "I JUST wanted to help" (merely/only wanted to help -- sincere minimizing) vs. "I just WANTED to help" (had the desire but failed to actually help)

Missing these stress-meaning connections leads to misunderstanding the speaker's intended scope, which can cascade into social errors -- responding to the wrong aspect of what someone said, missing the implied criticism, or not catching the "even John" implication that John was expected to fail.

### 5.4 Scalar Implicature and Autistic Traits (2025)

A 2025 study in *Journal of Pragmatics* (ScienceDirect) on "Speech prosody and pragmatic scalar inferences: Divergent cognitive strategies in adults with high and low levels of autistic traits" found:

- Higher autistic trait levels are linked to greater difficulty with pragmatic scalar inferences
- But **compensatory cognitive strategies** can offset this
- When prosodic cues were present, individuals with **low** autistic traits showed greater delays in response times but were **less likely** to change their interpretations
- Individuals with **high** autistic traits used different cognitive pathways -- **divergent strategies** rather than deficit-based responses

This is encouraging: it suggests that autistic adults can arrive at correct pragmatic interpretations through different routes, even if the intuitive prosodic pathway is less accessible.

## 6. THE PITCH PARADOX: HYPERSENSITIVITY TO SOUND, HYPOSENSITIVITY TO MEANING

### 6.1 The ERP Evidence

A 2022 study in *Frontiers in Psychiatry* (Chien et al.) provided ERP (event-related potential) evidence of a striking dissociation:

**Simple pitched tones:**
- The autism group showed **larger** mismatch negativity (MMN) -- indicating **hyper-sensitivity** to pitch changes at the early auditory processing level
- They also produced greater trial-to-trial variability

**Prosodic utterances:**
- The autism group showed **smaller** P3 responses when emotional prosody changed (delight to frustration)
- This indicates **hypo-sensitivity** to the communicative meaning carried by pitch changes

**The critical disconnect**: "There was no significant correlation between ERPs to pitch and ERPs to prosody." The pitch-processing system and the prosody-interpretation system operate independently. Enhanced hearing of pitch differences does **not** automatically translate into enhanced understanding of what those pitch differences mean.

### 6.2 Implications for Contrastive Stress

This paradox directly explains the contrastive stress challenge:

- Autistic individuals may **hear** the pitch accent on a stressed word with exquisite precision
- But **interpreting** that pitch accent as "this word is the focus, alternatives are being activated, the discourse structure has shifted" requires an additional processing step that doesn't benefit from the enhanced pitch discrimination
- The enhanced low-level processing may even create noise: registering too many small pitch variations that neurotypical listeners automatically filter out, making it harder to identify which pitch change is the pragmatically significant one

### 6.3 The Complexity Factor

Research suggests that "increasing stimulus complexity spectrally and temporally resulted in any advantage in pitch discrimination disappearing." As stimuli become more complex (from pure tones to speech to conversational prosody), the autistic processing advantage at the perceptual level vanishes or reverses, while the interpretation challenge grows.

## 7. THE RECEPTIVE vs. EXPRESSIVE DISTINCTION

### 7.1 The General Pattern

Across multiple studies, a consistent pattern emerges:

**Receptive prosody** (understanding stress in others' speech) generally **outstrips** expressive prosody (producing appropriate stress in one's own speech) in autism:
- Wehrle et al. (2023): "Receptive prosody exceeds expressive prosody in ASD"
- The PEPS-C profile across studies shows the "ASD prosodic profile is one of discordance, where receptive prosodic skills generally outstripped performance on expressive subtests"
- Grossman et al. (2010): Children with HFA were "as capable as their TD peers in receptive tasks of lexical stress and affective prosody" while showing atypical production patterns

### 7.2 Does This Hold for Contrastive Stress Specifically?

The evidence is more nuanced for contrastive stress:
- Receptive contrastive stress IS impaired in some studies (Diehl & Paul) but not others (Segal et al., depending on subtest)
- The Su et al. (2025) finding of processing speed differences suggests receptive comprehension may be **accurate but slow**
- Expressive contrastive stress appears consistently more impaired: "both the ASD and ASD Parent groups exhibited reduced accuracy in imitation, lexical stress, and contrastive stress expression" (Wehrle et al., 2023)

### 7.3 The Improvement-with-Age Pattern

Several studies suggest that prosodic comprehension improves with age in autism, potentially through compensation:
- "Autistic adults' improved performance in prosody perception compared to children may be related to **compensation strategies**, where learnt rules may replace an intuitive understanding of social cues" (Grice et al., 2023)
- Segal et al. (2017) found that cognitive-linguistic ability, not autism severity, predicted narrow focus comprehension in adolescents
- However, Grice et al. warn that "while compensation strategies might suffice in controlled experimental settings, this might not be the case in unpredictable, novel, or fast-paced everyday settings"

### 7.4 Can Autistic Individuals Learn Stress-Based Meaning?

**Yes -- the evidence strongly suggests explicit teaching works:**

- "Children and adolescents with high functioning autism are able to apply basic knowledge of lexical and affective prosody when it is the **main focus of their attention**" (Grossman et al., 2010)
- The formal/rule-based nature of contrastive stress makes it particularly teachable: "Formal functions of linguistic prosody are assumed to be **more learnable** for individuals on the autism spectrum, as they are rule-based" (Grice et al., 2023)
- A 2025 study showed that **sung speech training** improved prosodic focus marking in autistic children, demonstrating that explicit, structured intervention can improve focus production (Journal of Speech, Language, and Hearing Research)
- Prosody interventions "specifically targeting speech prosody using established and emerging evidence-based practices... resulted in **moderate to large improvements** in speech prosody in persons with ASD" (ASHA systematic review, 2020)

## 8. PROCESSING OF PROSODIC UNCERTAINTY CUES

### 8.1 The Frontiers in Psychiatry (2024) Study

Directly relevant to the hedge-and-stress interaction, a 2024 study examined prosodic cues of uncertainty in autistic vs. non-autistic adults:

- 28 adults with ASD and 28 neurotypical adults rated synthetic speech expressing uncertainty through varied pause, intonation, and hesitation cues
- **Both groups recognized different levels of uncertainty**
- The researchers treated uncertainty ascription as part of "affective theory of mind"

**Significance**: This suggests that when prosodic uncertainty cues are **isolated and presented clearly** (as in synthetic speech), autistic adults CAN perceive them. The difficulty likely arises in natural speech where uncertainty cues compete with semantic content, social context, and other prosodic information -- consistent with the complexity/information-integration account.

## 9. PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS FOR THE KNOWLEDGE BASE

### 9.1 Strategies for Detecting Contrastive Stress in Others' Speech

Based on the research, the following strategies leverage autistic strengths while addressing specific challenges:

**1. The "Which Word Pops Out?" Check**
Since autistic individuals often have enhanced pitch discrimination, the raw perceptual information IS available. The strategy is to consciously attend to which word seems louder, higher-pitched, or longer, and then ask: "Why would they stress THAT word?"

**2. The Alternatives Test**
Contrastive stress activates alternatives. When you notice a stressed word, consciously generate what it's contrasting with:
- "I walked to YOUR house" → not someone else's house
- "I WALKED to your house" → not drove, biked, etc.
- "I walked to your HOUSE" → not your office, your car, etc.

**3. The "Default vs. Non-Default" Detection**
In English, default stress (nuclear accent) falls on the LAST content word of a phrase. If stress appears earlier, something pragmatic is happening. Learning to detect when stress is NOT in the default position is a reliable signal that the speaker is doing something with focus.

**4. The Context Mismatch Check**
If someone seems to be emphasizing a word that doesn't need emphasis given the conversation, consider whether they're:
- Correcting a misunderstanding
- Expressing disagreement/contrast
- Being sarcastic

### 9.2 Strategies for Using Stress Yourself

**1. Decide What's New/Contrastive Before Speaking**
Identify the one piece of information in your sentence that the listener doesn't already know or that you're correcting. Place extra stress on that word.

**2. The "Only" Test for Placement**
If you can insert "only" or "specifically" before the word and the meaning stays the same, that word should carry the stress.

**3. Practice with Minimal Pairs**
Use the seven-meaning sentence exercise: practice saying "I never said she stole my money" with stress on each word in turn, and articulate what each version means.

### 9.3 High-Stakes Stress Contexts

The knowledge base should especially flag contexts where stress-based meaning shifts are most consequential:

**1. Sarcasm detection**: When stress falls on a word that ALREADY expresses the expected sentiment strongly, suspect sarcasm. "What a LOVELY day" with exaggerated stress in a rainstorm.

**2. Focus particles ("only"/"even"/"just")**: The meaning of the entire sentence depends on what these words associate with through stress. Misreading the stress → misreading the scope → misunderstanding the whole proposition.

**3. Hedge-stress interactions**: When someone stresses a minimizer ("I was JUST trying to help"), the stress can either reinforce the hedge (sincerely minimizing) or undercut it (sarcastically). Context and tone together disambiguate.

**4. Correction contexts**: "I said TUESDAY, not Thursday" -- stress marks the correction target. Missing this cue means missing the correction entirely.

## 10. THE TEACHING OPPORTUNITY: WHY CONTRASTIVE STRESS IS LEARNABLE

### 10.1 Contrastive Stress Is Rule-Based and Systematic

This is the single most important point for the knowledge base: contrastive stress follows discoverable, teachable rules. It is NOT arbitrary or intuition-dependent the way some aspects of pragmatic prosody are.

**The core rule**: The stressed word is the FOCUS. Focus activates ALTERNATIVES. The sentence is interpreted relative to those alternatives.

**The corollary rules**:
- Default stress = last content word = broad focus (entire sentence is new information)
- Non-default stress = narrow focus = speaker is highlighting a specific contrast
- Focus-sensitive particles (only, even, just) associate with the focused element
- Sarcasm often involves stress on a word whose expected meaning is being inverted

### 10.2 The Seven-Meanings Sentence as a Teaching Tool

"I never said she stole my money" with systematic stress on each of the seven words:

1. **I** never said she stole my money → Someone else said it
2. I **NEVER** said she stole my money → I deny ever saying it
3. I never **SAID** she stole my money → I implied/thought it but didn't say it
4. I never said **SHE** stole my money → Someone else stole it
5. I never said she **STOLE** my money → She borrowed/found it
6. I never said she stole **MY** money → She stole someone else's money
7. I never said she stole my **MONEY** → She stole something else

This is a PATTERN. Each stress placement selects one word as the variable and holds the rest constant. An autistic learner who grasps this pattern can apply it to ANY sentence.

### 10.3 The "Focus = Variable, Rest = Constant" Framework

The most powerful teaching frame for pattern-oriented thinkers:

Think of each sentence as an equation. Most words are constants. The stressed word is the **variable** -- it's the one element the speaker is questioning, correcting, or highlighting. The alternatives to that variable are the implicit set of possible values.

"I WALKED to your house":
- Constants: I, to, your, house
- Variable: walked (could have been drove, biked, ran, took the bus)
- The speaker is specifying the value of the variable and excluding alternatives

This framework transforms prosodic pragmatics from an intuitive social skill into a parseable logical operation -- precisely the kind of reframing that research suggests helps autistic learners most.

### 10.4 Evidence That Explicit Instruction Works

- Prosody interventions directly targeting speech prosody show "moderate to large improvements" (ASHA, 2020)
- Rule-based prosodic functions are "more learnable" for autistic individuals (Grice et al., 2023)
- Children apply prosodic knowledge correctly "when it is the main focus of their attention" (Grossman et al., 2010)
- Sung speech training improved focus marking in autistic children (JSLHR, 2025)
- Adult compensation strategies using "learnt rules" can replace intuitive processing (Grice et al., 2023)

The challenge is not learnability but **automaticity**: applying these rules fast enough in real-time conversation. The knowledge base should be explicit that conscious rule-application is a valid strategy even if it requires more cognitive effort than neurotypical intuitive processing.

## 12. SYNTHESIS: THE KEY FINDINGS FOR THE KNOWLEDGE BASE

### What the research tells us:

1. **Autistic individuals CAN perceive contrastive stress** -- they hear the pitch changes. The challenge is in **interpreting** and **integrating** that information with discourse context in real time.

2. **Processing speed is a key bottleneck**: Even when accuracy is preserved, autistic listeners process contrastive stress more slowly. In fast-paced conversation, this delay means the pragmatic information arrives too late.

3. **Pragmatic prosody is harder than grammatical prosody**: The distinction between rule-based (preserved) and context-dependent (impaired) prosodic functions is one of the most robust findings in the field.

4. **The pitch paradox**: Enhanced low-level pitch sensitivity does NOT translate into enhanced prosodic comprehension. The bottleneck is at the meaning level, not the perceptual level.

5. **Stress placement in production is reliably affected**: Stressing function words, defaulting to sentence-initial stress, and misaligning stress with information structure are common patterns.

6. **Focus-sensitive particles create a double challenge**: "Only"/"even"/"just" require tracking stress AND computing scope, and autistic listeners may process the sentence as though no word was specially stressed.

7. **Sarcasm through stress is particularly difficult**: It requires detecting non-default stress, recognizing the mismatch between words and prosody, and inferring speaker intent -- all simultaneously and rapidly.

8. **BUT: Contrastive stress is SYSTEMATIC and TEACHABLE**. It follows rules. Focus activates alternatives. The stressed word is the variable. Explicit instruction works. This is perhaps the most important practical finding for an autistic learner.

9. **Compensation is real and effective**: Adults show improved prosodic comprehension compared to children, likely through learned rules replacing intuitive processing. The knowledge base itself is an intervention.
