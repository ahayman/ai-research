# Pragmatics -- How Meaning Exceeds What Is Said

How a Small Set of Inference Mechanisms Systematically Generates Meaning Beyond Literal Content

---

## 1. The Core Idea

Here is a conversation:

> A: "Are you going to the party tonight?"
> B: "I have to work."

B has not said "no." The word "no" does not appear. The word "party" does not appear. Nothing in the four words "I have to work" says anything about parties, refusals, regret, or scheduling conflicts. And yet every competent speaker of English instantly understands that B is declining the invitation, that work is the reason, and possibly that B regrets the conflict.

Where does all of that meaning come from?

This is the central question of **pragmatics**: the study of how context, speaker intention, and shared knowledge combine to produce meaning that goes far beyond the literal content of an utterance. And the answer pragmatics provides is surprisingly systematic. It is not that speakers randomly guess what each other mean. It is that human communication operates through a small, identifiable set of inference mechanisms -- and these mechanisms generate vast amounts of meaning from minimal linguistic input, reliably, automatically, and in milliseconds.

The pattern is this: **literal language is a skeleton. Inference is the body.** Every utterance you have ever understood required your mind to flesh out an incomplete linguistic signal using context, shared knowledge, and reasoning about the speaker's intentions. What you "said" is never what you "communicated." The gap between the two is not noise -- it is the main event.

This section of the deep dive maps the mechanisms that bridge that gap. They turn out to be surprisingly few, and they interlock in precise ways. Once you see the pattern, you will recognize it everywhere -- in every conversation, every email, every overheard remark.

## 2. The Gap: Sentence Meaning vs. Speaker Meaning

Before diving into the mechanisms, let us be precise about what the gap actually is.

**Sentence meaning** is the meaning that a sentence has by virtue of its words and grammar, independent of who says it, when, where, or why. It is what a dictionary and a grammar book could, in principle, extract. The sentence "It is cold in here" has the sentence meaning: the temperature in this location is low.

**Speaker meaning** is what a particular speaker intends to communicate by uttering a sentence in a particular context. The same sentence, "It is cold in here," uttered by a guest in your living room, might mean: *please close the window.* Or: *your heating is broken.* Or: *I am explaining why I am wearing my coat indoors.* Or: *I am making small talk because the silence is uncomfortable.*

The core discovery of pragmatics is that the gap between sentence meaning and speaker meaning is not occasional or accidental. It is **systematic and pervasive**. Virtually every utterance in natural conversation communicates more than its sentence meaning. The mechanisms that bridge this gap are not ad hoc -- they follow predictable patterns governed by a small number of principles.

Here is the key insight, stated as a thesis:

> **The Underdetermination Thesis**: The linguistic meaning of an utterance massively underdetermines what the speaker communicates.

This is not a failure of language. It is language's most powerful design feature. By leaving most meaning to inference, language achieves extraordinary economy (speakers do not need to spell everything out), flexibility (the same sentence works in infinite contexts), and social agility (indirectness allows speakers to navigate face, power, and relationship dynamics). The price is that understanding requires an elaborate apparatus of pragmatic reasoning -- but human minds perform this reasoning effortlessly, automatically, and in real time.

## 3. Grice's Cooperative Principle and the Four Maxims

The first systematic account of how speakers communicate beyond literal meaning came from **H. Paul Grice** (1913-1988), a British philosopher of language. His 1967 William James Lectures at Harvard, published as "Logic and Conversation" in 1975 and collected in *Studies in the Way of Words* (1989), remain the foundational framework for pragmatics.

### The Cooperative Principle

Grice proposed that conversation is governed by a tacit agreement:

> **"Make your conversational contribution such as is required, at the stage at which it occurs, by the accepted purpose or direction of the talk exchange in which you are engaged."**

This is not a moral rule. It is a descriptive observation about what speakers and hearers assume. Conversation works because participants tacitly agree to cooperate toward mutual understanding. Even lying, evasion, and rudeness are parasitic on this assumption -- they only work because the hearer expects cooperation.

### The Four Maxims

Under the Cooperative Principle, Grice identified four specific maxims -- rules of thumb that speakers are generally expected to follow:

**1. Maxim of Quantity** (Be informative)
- Give as much information as is required for the current purpose.
- Do not give more information than is required.

**2. Maxim of Quality** (Be truthful)
- Do not say what you believe to be false.
- Do not say that for which you lack adequate evidence.

**3. Maxim of Relation** (Be relevant)
- Be relevant. (Grice acknowledged this maxim was "deceptively simple" and that "relevance" itself needed further analysis -- a gap that Sperber and Wilson would later fill with Relevance Theory.)

**4. Maxim of Manner** (Be clear)
- Avoid obscurity of expression.
- Avoid ambiguity.
- Be brief (avoid unnecessary prolixity).
- Be orderly.

These maxims are not commandments that speakers always obey. They are expectations that hearers use as a baseline for interpretation. And the real power of the framework emerges precisely when speakers *do not* follow them.

### Before Grice: Natural vs. Non-Natural Meaning

Before developing the Cooperative Principle, Grice drew a foundational distinction in his 1957 paper "Meaning" that clarifies what pragmatics actually studies:

- **Natural meaning**: Causal, law-like connections. "Those spots mean measles." If the patient does not have measles, the spots do not mean measles. Natural meaning cannot be faked.
- **Non-natural meaning**: Speaker-intended meaning that depends on the recognition of intentions. "Those three rings on the bell mean the bus is full." This holds even if the bus is not actually full. Non-natural meaning is what pragmatics studies.

The distinction matters because it pins down exactly what kind of "meaning" is at stake in communication: meaning that depends on the speaker *intending* the hearer to recognize their intention. This reflexive, intention-based structure is what makes human communication fundamentally different from natural signs.

## 4. Conversational Implicature: How Flouting Maxims Generates Meaning

The real power of Grice's framework emerges not when the maxims are followed but when they are conspicuously violated -- or **"flouted."** When a speaker blatantly fails to observe a maxim, the hearer, still assuming cooperation, infers that the speaker must be communicating something beyond the literal content. This inferred meaning is a **conversational implicature**.

The pattern is uniform: *the hearer detects an apparent maxim violation, assumes the speaker is still cooperating, and reasons backward to what the speaker must have meant to make the utterance cooperative after all.*

**Flouting Quantity** (saying too little):
- Boss: "How is the new employee?"
- Colleague: "Well, he's always on time."
- The colleague conspicuously omits any comment on competence, creativity, or output. Implicature: the employee is mediocre or worse, and punctuality is the only positive thing to say.

**Flouting Quality** (saying something obviously false):
- "Oh, that's just great" (said when something terrible happens).
- The speaker says something patently untrue. Implicature: sarcasm -- the speaker means the opposite.

**Flouting Relation** (saying something apparently irrelevant):
- A: "How did the exam go?"
- B: "Well, the weather was nice today."
- B's response seems irrelevant. Implicature: the exam went badly, and B is changing the subject rather than admitting it.

**Flouting Manner** (being deliberately obscure):
- "She did the thing with the stuff at the place" (when the speaker could be more specific).
- The deliberate vagueness implicates that the speaker does not want to be explicit -- perhaps because children are present or the topic is sensitive.

### Types of Implicature

Grice distinguished two kinds of conversational implicature:

**Particularized conversational implicatures (PCIs)** arise only in specific contexts. The "new employee" example is particularized -- the implicature depends entirely on the particular conversational situation.

**Generalized conversational implicatures (GCIs)** arise regardless of context, carried by certain words or constructions. "Some students passed" generally implicates "not all students passed." This arises from the word "some" itself, not from any particular conversational situation.

### Properties of Conversational Implicature

Grice identified five diagnostic properties that distinguish implicatures from logical entailments:

1. **Cancelability**: Implicatures can be explicitly canceled without contradiction. "Some students passed -- in fact, all of them did." No contradiction.
2. **Non-detachability**: The implicature attaches to the semantic content, not the particular words. Paraphrase the utterance, and the implicature typically persists. (Exception: manner-based implicatures do depend on the form of expression.)
3. **Calculability**: A competent hearer can work out *why* the implicature arises by reasoning from the Cooperative Principle, the maxims, and the context. Implicatures are not arbitrary.
4. **Reinforceability**: Making an implicature explicit does not create redundancy. "Some, but not all, students passed" sounds natural, confirming that "not all" was inferred rather than stated.
5. **Non-conventionality**: Implicatures are not part of the semantic content of the words themselves. They are generated by the interaction of what is said with context and the maxims.

These five properties form a diagnostic test. If an inferred meaning can be canceled without contradiction, survives paraphrase, can be worked out from context, can be reinforced without redundancy, and is not tied to specific word forms -- it is a conversational implicature.

### Conventional Implicature: The Odd Sibling

Grice also identified a distinct category: **conventional implicatures** are conveyed by particular words but are not part of the truth-conditional content. "He is British, but brave" -- the word "but" conventionally implicates a contrast between being British and being brave, but the sentence is true if and only if he is both British and brave (the same truth conditions as "and"). Words like "but," "therefore," "even," "yet," and "still" all carry conventional implicatures. Unlike conversational implicatures, these are not cancelable, not calculable from the Cooperative Principle, and are attached to specific linguistic forms.

## 5. Relevance Theory: One Principle to Replace Four Maxims

**Dan Sperber** (b. 1942, French cognitive scientist) and **Deirdre Wilson** (b. 1941, British linguist) looked at Grice's four maxims and saw a problem: why four? The maxims sometimes conflict, "relevance" (Grice's Maxim of Relation) was left unexplained but arguably did most of the work, and the framework did not explain how hearers select from among many possible interpretations. Their answer, developed in *Relevance: Communication and Cognition* (1986, revised 1995), was to replace the entire Gricean apparatus with a single principle.

### The Code Model vs. the Inferential Model

Relevance Theory begins with a fundamental distinction. The traditional view of communication, inherited from information theory (Shannon and Weaver), treats it as a **code model**: the speaker encodes a thought into words, transmits the words, and the hearer decodes the words back into the thought. This model is inadequate because:

- Sentences are ambiguous; words are polysemous.
- Pronouns, demonstratives, and tense require context to resolve.
- Much of what is communicated is never encoded in words at all.
- Understanding requires far more than linguistic decoding.

Sperber and Wilson propose instead the **inferential model**: communication is fundamentally a process of inferring speaker intentions from evidence, of which the utterance is only one piece. The hearer uses the utterance together with contextual knowledge to construct hypotheses about what the speaker intended to communicate.

### Ostensive-Inferential Communication

Communication, on this view, is **ostensive-inferential**:

- **Ostensive**: The speaker makes it obvious that they intend to communicate something. Speaking is an act of ostension -- it draws the hearer's attention to the fact that the speaker wants to convey information. This involves two layers of intention:
  - **Informative intention**: the intention to inform the audience of something.
  - **Communicative intention**: the intention to make it mutually manifest that communication is being attempted.
- **Inferential**: The hearer infers what the speaker intended, using the utterance as evidence together with contextual assumptions.

### The Two Principles

The theory rests on two bold claims:

> **Cognitive Principle of Relevance**: Human cognition tends to be geared to the maximization of relevance.

Relevance is defined by two dimensions:
- Greater cognitive effects (new conclusions, strengthened beliefs, revised assumptions) = greater relevance.
- Greater processing effort = lesser relevance.

> **Communicative Principle of Relevance**: Every act of ostensive communication conveys a presumption of its own optimal relevance.

An utterance is optimally relevant when: (1) it is relevant enough to be worth the hearer's processing effort, and (2) it is the most relevant stimulus the speaker is willing and able to produce.

This single communicative principle replaces all four of Grice's maxims. Be informative (because uninformative utterances lack cognitive effects). Be truthful (because false information leads to incorrect cognitive effects). Be relevant (the core of the principle). Be clear (because obscurity increases processing effort for no gain).

### The Comprehension Procedure

How does a hearer actually use this? The comprehension procedure is:

1. Follow a path of least effort in computing cognitive effects.
2. Test interpretive hypotheses (reference assignments, disambiguations, implicatures) in order of accessibility -- the easiest to process come first.
3. Stop when the expected level of relevance is achieved.

This is not conscious deliberation. It is an automatic cognitive process. The hearer does not consider all possible interpretations and select the best one. They follow a fast, heuristic path and stop at the first interpretation that satisfies expectations of relevance.

### Explicature vs. Implicature

Relevance Theory draws a more nuanced line than Grice between explicit and implicit content:

- **Explicature**: An explicitly communicated proposition, arrived at by decoding the linguistic form *and* pragmatically enriching it through disambiguation, reference assignment, saturation, free enrichment, and ad hoc concept construction. Even "what is said" requires substantial pragmatic work. "I've had breakfast" has the explicature *the speaker has had breakfast today* -- the temporal restriction "today" is not encoded in the words.
- **Implicature**: An implicitly communicated proposition, derived wholly through inference.

The key insight is that pragmatics penetrates all the way down. There is no clean divide between a purely linguistic level and a pragmatic level. Even constructing "what was said" requires inference.

### Metaphor and Irony in Relevance Theory

Relevance Theory does not treat metaphor and irony as special mechanisms. They are natural outcomes of ordinary comprehension:

- **Metaphor** is an instance of "loose use" -- the speaker communicates an ad hoc concept that broadens the literal meaning. "She's a bulldozer" triggers the construction of a concept BULLDOZER* (related to but broader than the literal concept) that yields adequate cognitive effects for minimal effort.
- **Irony** is an instance of "echoic use" -- the speaker echoes an attributed thought while expressing a dissociative attitude toward it. (More on this in Section 9.)

## 6. Speech Act Theory: Saying as Doing

While Grice and Sperber-Wilson focus on how speakers communicate *information* beyond the literal, **Speech Act Theory** reveals a different dimension of the gap: speakers do not just say things -- they *do* things with words. And what they do is often very different from what their words literally describe.

### Austin: The Discovery

**J.L. Austin** (1911-1960), a British philosopher, delivered the William James Lectures at Harvard in 1955, published posthumously as *How to Do Things with Words* (1962). Austin noticed that many utterances are not descriptions at all -- they are actions:

- "I name this ship the *Queen Elizabeth*."
- "I bet you sixpence it will rain tomorrow."
- "I promise to return the book."
- "I now pronounce you husband and wife."

These **performative utterances** do not describe a pre-existing state of affairs; they bring one into being. Saying "I promise" *is* the act of promising. The utterance is the action.

Austin initially distinguished **constatives** (statements that describe the world and can be true or false) from **performatives** (utterances that perform actions and succeed or fail). But as he worked through the lectures, the distinction collapsed. Constatives can fail too (a statement based on false presuppositions misfires). Performatives can have truth-like evaluations. Many utterances are simultaneously descriptive and performative.

### Austin's Three-Act Model

The collapse of the constative/performative distinction led Austin to a more general insight: **every utterance performs multiple acts simultaneously**:

**1. Locutionary Act** -- the act of saying something meaningful:
- Phonetic act: producing sounds.
- Phatic act: producing words conforming to a grammar.
- Rhetic act: using those words with a definite sense and reference.

**2. Illocutionary Act** -- the act performed IN saying something:
- Asserting, questioning, commanding, promising, warning, apologizing, thanking, etc.
- "The door is open" has the same locutionary content whether it functions as an assertion, a request, or a warning.

**3. Perlocutionary Act** -- the act performed BY saying something (the effects on the audience):
- Persuading, frightening, amusing, inspiring, annoying, informing.
- A warning (illocutionary) may frighten the hearer (perlocutionary).

### Felicity Conditions

Since performatives cannot be true or false, Austin asked: when do they succeed or fail? He identified **felicity conditions** -- conditions required for a speech act to succeed:

1. A conventional procedure must exist (the institution of marriage must exist for "I pronounce you married" to work).
2. The persons and circumstances must be appropriate (a random person cannot pronounce you married).
3. The procedure must be executed correctly and completely.
4. Sincerity: participants must have the requisite intentions (a promise made with no intention of keeping it is defective).
5. Subsequent compliance: participants must conduct themselves accordingly afterward.

When conditions 1-3 are violated, the act **misfires** (fails to come off). When conditions 4-5 are violated, the act is **abused** (goes through but is defective).

### Searle's Systematization

**John Searle** (b. 1932), who studied under Austin at Oxford, systematized speech act theory in *Speech Acts* (1969) and *Expression and Meaning* (1979). His most influential contribution was a taxonomy of illocutionary acts organized by **illocutionary point** (purpose) and **direction of fit** (relationship between words and world):

| Category | Purpose | Direction of Fit | Examples |
|----------|---------|-----------------|----------|
| **Assertives** | Commit to truth of proposition | Words-to-world | Stating, claiming, reporting |
| **Directives** | Get hearer to do something | World-to-words | Requesting, commanding, advising |
| **Commissives** | Commit speaker to future action | World-to-words | Promising, threatening, offering |
| **Expressives** | Express psychological state | No direction of fit | Thanking, apologizing, congratulating |
| **Declarations** | Change reality by utterance | Both directions | Declaring war, firing, pronouncing married |

### Indirect Speech Acts

Searle's deepest insight was the analysis of **indirect speech acts** -- cases where the illocutionary force of an utterance differs from what its grammatical form suggests:

- "Can you pass the salt?" -- Grammatically a yes/no question. Functionally a request.
- "It's cold in here." -- Grammatically an assertion about temperature. Functionally a request.

The hearer's inferential process:
1. Recognize the literal meaning (a question about ability).
2. Recognize that the literal meaning cannot be the primary purpose (the speaker obviously knows you can pass salt).
3. Apply the Cooperative Principle -- the speaker must be communicating something relevant.
4. Recognize that the literal question addresses a *preparatory condition* for a request (the hearer's ability to perform the action).
5. Infer that a request is being made indirectly.

Indirect speech acts are pervasive. We rarely issue bald commands ("Pass the salt"). We prefer indirect forms that leave the hearer's autonomy intact -- connecting speech act theory directly to politeness theory.

## 7. Wittgenstein's Language Games: Meaning Is Use

While Grice, Sperber-Wilson, and Austin-Searle all analyze the mechanisms of communication from the inside, **Ludwig Wittgenstein** (1889-1951) provides the philosophical foundation: the very concept of "meaning" is not what the tradition assumed.

### Two Philosophies, One Mind

Wittgenstein is unique in philosophy for having produced two major, fundamentally opposed systems. His early work, the *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus* (written 1918, published in German in 1921, in English in 1922), presented a "picture theory" of language: propositions are logical pictures of facts, and meaning is the state of affairs a sentence depicts. Language has one essential function -- to represent reality.

In his later work, *Philosophical Investigations* (published posthumously in 1953), Wittgenstein repudiated almost everything in the *Tractatus*. The picture theory captures only one of the countless things we do with language, and a relatively uncommon one at that.

### Meaning as Use

> "For a large class of cases -- though not for all -- in which we employ the word 'meaning' it can be defined thus: the meaning of a word is its use in the language." (*Philosophical Investigations*, section 43)

The implications are radical:
- Meaning is not a mental image, a logical form, or a reference to an object.
- Meaning is constituted by the patterns of use in which a word participates.
- To know the meaning of a word is to know how to use it correctly in the relevant social practices.
- Different contexts of use yield different "meanings," and there need not be a single common essence underlying them all.

This directly supports the pragmatic perspective: if meaning is use, then understanding meaning requires understanding context, purposes, and practices -- exactly what pragmatics studies.

### Language Games

Wittgenstein introduced **language games** (*Sprachspiele*) to capture the idea that language is embedded in activities:

> "I shall also call the whole, consisting of language and the activities into which it is woven, the 'language-game'." (*PI*, section 7)

Language games are the activities in which language plays a role -- and there are innumerably many: giving orders, describing objects, reporting events, speculating, forming hypotheses, making jokes, translating, asking, thanking, cursing, greeting, praying. Each has its own rules, criteria for success, and context. Understanding a word means understanding which language game it belongs to and how it functions within that game.

### Family Resemblance

Wittgenstein used **family resemblance** (*Familienaehnlichkeit*) to challenge the classical assumption that categories are defined by necessary and sufficient conditions:

> "I can think of no better expression to characterize these similarities than 'family resemblances'; for the various resemblances between members of a family: build, features, colour of eyes, gait, temperament, etc. etc. overlap and criss-cross in the same way." (*PI*, section 67)

Consider the concept "game." Board games, card games, ball games, Olympic games, word games, children's ring-a-ring-a-roses. Is there something common to all? Competition? No -- solitaire. Amusement? Not always -- professional sports. Rules? Some have explicit rules, others vague guidelines. There is no single feature shared by everything we call a "game." Instead, there is a network of overlapping similarities.

This matters for pragmatics because it means most of our concepts do not have sharp boundaries. Word meanings are not fixed containers but flexible tools whose precise contribution shifts with context -- exactly the kind of fluidity that pragmatic inference navigates.

### Forms of Life and the Private Language Argument

Language games are embedded in broader **forms of life** (*Lebensformen*): "To imagine a language means to imagine a form of life" (*PI*, section 19). Language works because we share vast numbers of practices, reactions, and background assumptions. A being who could decode English grammar but shared none of our forms of life would understand nothing.

Wittgenstein's **private language argument** (sections 243-315) reinforces this: a language understandable only by a single individual is impossible. The famous **beetle-in-the-box** thought experiment (section 293) illustrates it: if everyone has a box with a "beetle" inside, but no one can look in anyone else's box, whatever is in the box plays no role in the use of the word "beetle." Meaning is constituted by public, shared practices -- not by private mental states.

The lesson for pragmatics: communication works not because speakers and hearers have identical private experiences, but because they participate in shared forms of life and language games.

## 8. Presupposition and Deixis: The Hidden Infrastructure

Two phenomena reveal just how much meaning is smuggled into utterances without being explicitly stated.

### Presupposition: What Is Taken for Granted

A **presupposition** is a background assumption that must hold for an utterance to make sense. The critical diagnostic: presuppositions **survive negation**.

- "Have you stopped cheating on the exam?" -- presupposes you were cheating.
- "I have *not* stopped cheating on the exam" -- still presupposes you were cheating.

The presupposition (cheating was occurring) survives whether the sentence is affirmed or denied. This distinguishes presuppositions from entailments, which do not survive negation.

### Presupposition Triggers

Certain linguistic constructions systematically introduce presuppositions. This is one of the most rule-governed aspects of pragmatics:

| Trigger | Example | Presupposition |
|---------|---------|---------------|
| Definite descriptions | "The professor is late" | There exists a professor |
| Factive verbs | "She regrets leaving" | She left |
| Change-of-state verbs | "He stopped smoking" | He was smoking |
| Iteratives | "She returned to Paris" | She has been there before |
| Cleft sentences | "It was John who broke the vase" | Someone broke the vase |
| Temporal clauses | "Before she left, she locked the door" | She left |
| Counterfactuals | "If I were rich, I'd travel" | I am not rich |
| Questions | "When did you sell your car?" | You sold your car |
| Implicative verbs | "He managed to open the door" | He tried to open it |

The pattern: every one of these constructions smuggles in an unstated assumption that the hearer must recover. The hearer does this automatically -- and in doing so, accepts the presupposed content into the conversational record without it ever being explicitly asserted or debated.

### The Projection Problem

A central puzzle in presupposition theory: how do presuppositions behave in complex sentences? When a presupposition trigger is embedded inside a larger sentence (under negation, in a conditional, in a question), does the presupposition "project" to the whole sentence or not?

- "If John was smoking, he has stopped" -- the presupposition that John was smoking does NOT project (it is "filtered" by the conditional).
- "Either John never smoked, or he has stopped" -- again, filtered.
- "John believes that the King of France is bald" -- may or may not project, depending on attribution.

Lauri Karttunen (b. 1941) developed a framework classifying operators as "plugs" (block presuppositions), "holes" (let them through), and "filters" (sometimes block, sometimes pass), providing a systematic account of presupposition behavior in complex sentences.

### Semantic vs. Pragmatic Presupposition

Two competing views:

- **Semantic view** (Frege, Strawson): Presuppositions are conditions that must be met for a sentence to have a truth value at all. "The King of France is bald" is neither true nor false if there is no King of France.
- **Pragmatic view** (Robert Stalnaker, Karttunen): Presuppositions are propositions that speakers take to be part of the **common ground** -- the shared background knowledge of the participants. If I say "My sister called" and you did not know I had a sister, you simply **accommodate** -- adding that information to the common ground on the fly.

Accommodation is particularly interesting: it shows how presuppositions can be used strategically. "When would you like to schedule the follow-up appointment?" presupposes a follow-up is happening, making it harder to refuse.

### Deixis: Where Meaning Requires a Map

**Deixis** (from Greek *deiktikos*, "pointing") refers to expressions whose meaning depends on the context of utterance. Deictic expressions are indexical arrows pointing from the utterance outward into the world. Without knowing who is speaking, when, where, and to whom, deictic expressions have no determinate reference.

Five types form a systematic inventory:

**1. Person deixis**: I, me, you, he, she, they -- reference shifts with every change of speaker.
**2. Spatial deixis**: here/there, this/that, proximal/distal -- defined relative to the speaker's location. (Languages differ: Japanese has a three-way spatial distinction -- near speaker, near addressee, far from both.)
**3. Temporal deixis**: now, then, today, yesterday, tomorrow, ago -- defined relative to the moment of speaking. Tense systems are fundamentally deictic.
**4. Discourse deixis**: "this argument," "the following," "as I mentioned above" -- reference to parts of the ongoing discourse itself.
**5. Social deixis**: French *tu* vs. *vous*, German *du* vs. *Sie*, Japanese honorifics -- linguistic encoding of power, solidarity, respect, and social distance.

Deixis illustrates a fundamental principle: even the most basic aspects of linguistic meaning require contextual supplementation. The sentence "She put it there yesterday" is grammatically perfect but communicates almost nothing without context. Who is "she"? What is "it"? Where is "there"? When is "yesterday"? The sentence is a template that context must fill. This is not a failure of language -- it is language's design. Deictic expressions allow language to be reusable across infinite situations precisely because they derive their content from context rather than encoding it.

## 9. Politeness Theory: Why We Speak Indirectly

If communication were only about transmitting information efficiently, we would always speak directly. We do not. The reason is **face** -- the public self-image that every competent adult claims for themselves.

### Face and Its Two Dimensions

**Penelope Brown** (American anthropological linguist) and **Stephen Levinson** (b. 1947, British linguist and anthropologist) built on sociologist **Erving Goffman's** (1922-1982) concept of "face" to develop their influential theory, published as *Politeness: Some Universals in Language Usage* (1987). They define two components:

- **Positive face**: The desire to be liked, approved of, and ratified by others. The want that one's personality, values, and goals be seen as desirable.
- **Negative face**: The desire to be unimpeded -- to have freedom of action and not be imposed upon.

Nearly every speech act threatens someone's face. Requests threaten the hearer's negative face (they impose). Criticisms threaten the hearer's positive face (they signal disapproval). Even compliments can threaten negative face (they may create an obligation to reciprocate).

### Five Super-Strategies

Brown and Levinson identify five strategies for handling face-threatening acts (FTAs), ranked from most to least direct:

1. **Bald on record**: "Close the window." Direct, efficient, face-threatening. Used when efficiency matters most or the threat is minimal.
2. **Positive politeness**: "That's a great shirt! Hey, could you close the window?" Attends to the hearer's desire to be liked.
3. **Negative politeness**: "I'm sorry to bother you, but would you mind closing the window?" Minimizes imposition, shows deference.
4. **Off record**: "It's cold in here." Indirect hint; speaker can plausibly deny the intended interpretation.
5. **Don't do the FTA**: Decide the face threat is too great and say nothing.

### The Weightiness Formula

Speakers calculate strategy choice based on three sociological variables:

**W(FTA) = D(S,H) + P(H,S) + R(x)**

Where D = social distance, P = relative power of hearer over speaker, and R = rank of imposition. Higher weightiness leads to more elaborate indirectness.

### Cross-Cultural Critiques

Brown and Levinson's model has been criticized for Western bias. Japanese scholars Matsumoto (1988) and Ide (1989) argued that negative face (individual autonomy) does not map well onto Japanese culture, where politeness centers on maintaining proper social relations and where honorific systems are obligatory, not strategic. Chinese scholars note that "face" in Chinese culture splits into *mianzi* (social reputation) and *lian* (moral character), a distinction the two-dimensional model misses. Spencer-Oatey (2000, 2008) proposed **rapport management** with multiple face dimensions to better capture cross-cultural variation.

Despite these critiques, the model's core insight holds: **speakers systematically trade off communicative efficiency against social harmony, and this trade-off drives pervasive indirectness in human language.**

### Politeness as a Source of Implicature

Politeness is one of the primary motivations for conversational implicature. Speakers flout Grice's maxims for face-preservation reasons:
- Flouting Quantity (giving less information to avoid bad news).
- Flouting Manner (being deliberately vague to minimize face threat).
- Flouting Quality (using ironic understatement to soften criticism).

The connection is direct: the very indirectness that pragmatics studies is largely driven by the social demands that politeness theory describes. The gap between sentence meaning and speaker meaning exists, in large part, because directness is socially dangerous.

## 10. Irony, Sarcasm, and Scalar Implicature

### Irony: Not "The Opposite" -- Something More Precise

The traditional account treats irony as saying the opposite of what you mean. "What lovely weather!" said in a downpour means "What terrible weather!" But this account is too simple. Irony does not always involve the opposite, and it always carries an attitudinal component -- mockery, bitterness, wry amusement -- that simple negation cannot capture.

### The Echoic Account (Sperber and Wilson)

Relevance Theory offers a more precise analysis. Irony is **echoic use**: the speaker echoes an attributed thought, utterance, or expectation while expressing a **dissociative attitude** toward it.

The pattern has three components:
1. The speaker makes an utterance that echoes or alludes to a thought, expectation, or norm.
2. The utterance is presented not as the speaker's own view but as something attributed to someone else (or to a cultural norm, a general expectation, etc.).
3. The speaker expresses a dissociative attitude: mockery, skepticism, bitterness, wry amusement, scorn.

Examples:
- After a friend drops a vase: "What graceful handling." -- Echoes the expectation that the friend would be careful. Attitude: mockery.
- After a terrible restaurant meal: "Well, that was worth every penny." -- Echoes the restaurant's implicit promise of quality proportional to price. Attitude: bitter sarcasm.
- Weather forecast predicts sunshine; it pours: "What lovely weather!" -- Echoes the forecast. Attitude: wry amusement.

The echoic account is superior because: (a) not all irony involves saying the opposite -- "That was a fine thing to do" (said of a blunder) does not simply mean "That was a bad thing to do," it conveys reproach, mockery, and exasperation; (b) it explains the emotional component that simple negation misses; (c) it explains why irony requires an identifiable target -- something being echoed and mocked.

### The Pretense Account (Clark and Gerrig)

An alternative view, from Herbert Clark and Richard Gerrig (1984), treats irony as **pretense**: the speaker "makes as if" to perform a speech act (praising the weather), expecting the hearer to see through the pretense and recognize the mocking attitude. The speaker does not actually assert that the weather is lovely -- they pretend to, and the audience recognizes the pretense.

### Sarcasm

Sarcasm is often treated as irony with a sharper, more personal edge. While irony can be gentle or playful, sarcasm is typically directed at a person and intended to wound. Some researchers distinguish sarcasm from irony by its intentional hostility; others treat sarcasm as "bitter irony."

### Scalar Implicature: The Quantity Engine

**Scalar implicature** is a specific, highly systematic type of generalized conversational implicature. It arises from informational scales and illustrates the Maxim of Quantity at work with mechanical precision.

The pattern: when a speaker uses a weaker term from an ordered scale, the hearer infers that the speaker is not in a position to use a stronger term.

**Horn scales** (named after **Laurence Horn**, b. 1945, who formalized the concept):
- <all, most, many, some> -- saying "some" implicates "not all"
- <and, or> -- saying "or" implicates "not both"
- <always, often, sometimes> -- saying "sometimes" implicates "not always"
- <certain, probable, possible> -- saying "possible" implicates "not certain"
- <hot, warm> -- saying "warm" implicates "not hot"
- <love, like> -- saying "I like him" may implicate "I don't love him"

The mechanism is clean and predictable: the Maxim of Quantity requires speakers to be as informative as required. If the speaker could truthfully have used a stronger term ("all" instead of "some") and did not, the hearer infers that the stronger term does not apply.

Example:
- "Some students passed the exam."
- Literal meaning: at least some students passed (compatible with all passing).
- Scalar implicature: not all students passed.
- The speaker, being cooperative and informative, would have said "all" if all had passed. Since they did not, the hearer infers that not all did.

Scalar implicatures have the standard properties: they are **cancelable** ("Some students passed -- in fact, all of them"), **reinforceable** ("Some, but not all, students passed"), and **calculable** from the Maxim of Quantity.

## 11. The Fundamental Pattern: Communication as Inference

### Pulling the Threads Together

All of the theories covered in this section -- Grice, Relevance Theory, Speech Act Theory, Wittgenstein, presupposition, deixis, politeness, irony, scalar implicature -- converge on a single fundamental insight:

**What speakers communicate systematically exceeds what their words literally encode. This excess is generated by a small, identifiable set of inference mechanisms operating against a background of shared knowledge and social practice.**

The mechanisms are:

| Mechanism | What It Does |
|-----------|-------------|
| Cooperative Principle + implicature | Hearer reasons from maxim violations to intended meaning |
| Relevance-guided processing | Hearer finds first interpretation that achieves optimal relevance |
| Speech act recognition | Hearer identifies what action is being performed |
| Presupposition recovery | Hearer extracts unstated background assumptions |
| Deictic resolution | Hearer maps context-dependent expressions to referents |
| Politeness/face calculation | Hearer accounts for social indirectness |
| Non-literal meaning processing | Hearer recognizes echoing, pretense, scalar reasoning |

### The Architecture of a Single Utterance

To see how all these mechanisms work together, return to the opening example. When B says "I have to work" in response to "Are you going to the party tonight?", the hearer's mind performs all of the following -- automatically, in milliseconds:

1. **Decode linguistic meaning**: B is obligated to perform labor.
2. **Resolve deixis**: "I" = B; implicit "tonight" = this evening.
3. **Enrich content (explicature)**: B has a work obligation that overlaps with the time of the party.
4. **Apply the Cooperative Principle**: B's response must be relevant to A's question.
5. **Derive implicature 1**: B will not go to the party (via Relation -- the response is relevant only if it answers the question).
6. **Derive implicature 2**: Work prevents attendance (via Quantity -- this is the reason).
7. **Derive implicature 3**: B would go if not for work, or B prefers not to go -- context-dependent.
8. **Recognize speech act**: Indirect refusal + explanation + possible apology.
9. **Assess politeness**: B uses an off-record/indirect strategy rather than a bald "No" -- preserving both B's positive face (not seeming antisocial) and A's positive face (softening the rejection).

Four words. Nine inference steps. A meaning so rich it would take paragraphs to state explicitly. And every competent speaker processes all of this without effort, without conscious awareness, in real time.

### Why Language Works This Way

The gap between sentence meaning and speaker meaning is not a deficiency. It is an extraordinarily efficient design serving multiple purposes simultaneously:

- **Economy**: Speakers do not need to spell everything out. Shared knowledge, context, and inference do the heavy lifting.
- **Flexibility**: The same sentence works in infinite contexts because its meaning is partially constructed on the fly.
- **Social navigation**: Indirectness allows speakers to manage face, power, and relationships -- communicating sensitive content while maintaining plausible deniability.
- **Creativity**: The inferential nature of communication enables humor, irony, metaphor, poetry, and all forms of creative language use.

The inference mechanisms are the price we pay for this efficiency. And for most people, they work so seamlessly that they are invisible -- like the operating system running beneath the applications. Pragmatics makes the operating system visible.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Pragmatics** | The study of how context contributes to meaning beyond what is linguistically encoded |
| **Underdetermination** | The principle that linguistic meaning does not fully determine what is communicated |
| **Cooperative Principle** | Grice's principle that speakers cooperate by making contributions appropriate to the conversation's purpose |
| **Maxim** | One of Grice's four rules of thumb for cooperative communication: Quantity, Quality, Relation, Manner |
| **Flouting** | Conspicuously failing to observe a maxim in order to generate an implicature |
| **Violating** | Secretly failing to observe a maxim (e.g., lying -- violating Quality without the hearer realizing) |
| **Conversational implicature** | Meaning inferred (not stated) through the interaction of what is said with the Cooperative Principle and context |
| **Particularized implicature (PCI)** | An implicature arising only in a specific conversational context |
| **Generalized implicature (GCI)** | An implicature arising regardless of particular context, carried by certain words or constructions |
| **Conventional implicature** | Meaning conveyed by specific words (e.g., "but," "even") that is not part of truth-conditional content and is not cancelable |
| **Scalar implicature** | An implicature arising from use of a weaker term on an informational scale (e.g., "some" implicates "not all") |
| **Horn scale** | An ordered set of linguistic expressions ranked by informational strength |
| **Relevance** | A property of inputs to cognition, defined by the ratio of cognitive effects to processing effort |
| **Cognitive effects** | New conclusions, strengthened beliefs, or revised assumptions produced by processing an input |
| **Explicature** | An explicitly communicated proposition, arrived at by linguistic decoding plus pragmatic enrichment |
| **Implicature** | An implicitly communicated proposition, derived wholly through inference |
| **Ostensive-inferential communication** | Communication through making intentions manifest (ostension) and having them inferred |
| **Ad hoc concept** | A temporary, context-specific concept constructed during comprehension by narrowing or broadening a lexical meaning |
| **Locutionary act** | The act of producing a meaningful utterance (what is said) |
| **Illocutionary act** | The act performed IN saying something (asserting, requesting, promising, warning, etc.) |
| **Illocutionary force** | The type of action a speaker performs in making an utterance |
| **Perlocutionary act** | The effect achieved BY saying something (persuading, frightening, amusing) |
| **Performative utterance** | An utterance that performs an action by being uttered ("I promise," "I declare") |
| **Felicity conditions** | Conditions that must be met for a speech act to succeed |
| **Indirect speech act** | A speech act whose illocutionary force differs from what its grammatical form suggests |
| **Direction of fit** | The relationship between words and world in a speech act (words-to-world, world-to-words, both, or neither) |
| **Language game** | An activity in which language plays a role, governed by its own rules and context (Wittgenstein) |
| **Meaning as use** | Wittgenstein's thesis that the meaning of a word is constituted by how it is used in practice |
| **Family resemblance** | A mode of categorization where members share overlapping similarities without any single common feature |
| **Form of life** | The broader pattern of human activity and practice in which language games are embedded |
| **Private language argument** | Wittgenstein's argument that a language understandable only by a single individual is impossible |
| **Presupposition** | A background assumption taken for granted in an utterance, surviving negation |
| **Presupposition trigger** | A linguistic construction that systematically introduces a presupposition |
| **Projection problem** | The question of how presuppositions behave when embedded in complex sentences |
| **Common ground** | The shared background knowledge and assumptions of conversational participants |
| **Accommodation** | The process by which hearers accept new presuppositions into the common ground without explicit assertion |
| **Deixis** | Context-dependent reference whose meaning shifts with who is speaking, when, where, and to whom |
| **Positive face** | The desire to be liked, approved of, and ratified by others |
| **Negative face** | The desire not to be imposed upon; to have freedom of action |
| **Face-threatening act (FTA)** | A speech act that threatens the positive or negative face of speaker or hearer |
| **Bald on record** | Performing an FTA directly, without softening or indirectness |
| **Off record** | Performing an FTA indirectly, so the speaker can plausibly deny the intended interpretation |
| **Echoic use** | Using language to echo an attributed thought while expressing a dissociative attitude -- the Relevance Theory account of irony |
| **Pretense account** | The view (Clark and Gerrig) that irony involves the speaker pretending to perform a speech act |
