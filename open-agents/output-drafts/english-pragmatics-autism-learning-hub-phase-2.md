# Phase 2: From Knowledge Base to Learning Platform Blueprint

## The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

---

## Overview

Phase 1 produced the core knowledge base: 22 deep-dive categories across 102 documents (~5.19MB) covering the full scope of English pragmatics. Phase 2 completes the knowledge base, validates it, and produces everything needed to build an interactive learning platform in a separate repository.

The goal: when Phase 2 is complete, the entire research corpus can be copied to a new repo where website development begins with a complete blueprint -- no research gaps, no design questions left unanswered.

### Stage 1.5: Pragmatic Manipulation Sub-Hub (Scope Expansion)

**Status:** IN PROGRESS
**Build plan:** [manipulation-hub-build-plan.md](manipulation-hub-build-plan.md) — single source of truth for build order, page scope, safety commitments, and progress tracking
**Rationale:** After reviewing the initial Pragmatic Manipulation category, it became clear that each sub-topic within it (recognition patterns, contexts, strategies) deserves its own explainer page with nuance, examples, and contextualized strategies. The summary-level treatment was insufficient for the stakes. The user committed to building this out with strict research discipline — every strategy on every page must trace to validated research, established safety frameworks, or clearly-labeled community knowledge.

**Safety commitment:** No strategy goes on a page without being traceable to a specific source in the foundational research documents. No page repeats claims flagged as "NOT well-supported" in Section 12 of the trauma framework document. Every page must self-verify against the downstream-use checklist in the DV frameworks document.

**Foundational research (COMPLETE):**
- DV/IPV/Coercive Control frameworks (Duluth, Walker, Stark, Campbell, workplace bullying) with strategy-level evidence map
- Trauma frameworks (Herman, C-PTSD, SAMHSA, validated treatments, Lilienfeld PHTs, Section 12 guardrail)
- Autism-specific application with explicit evidence-quality labels and gap identification

**Sub-hub structure (~35-42 pages planned):**

*Part A — Pattern pages (12-15):* Recognition pages for specific manipulation tactics
- Gaslighting, DARVO, tone policing, diagnostic weaponization, sensory gaslighting, post-meltdown shaming, compliance trap, weaponized accommodations, bandwagon isolation, memory gaslighting, information asymmetry exploitation, concern trolling, performative allyship, others as identified

*Part B — Context pages (8-10):* Strategies specific to settings
- Intimate partner relationships (highest-stakes), family of origin, workplace, medical settings, educational settings, friendships and "mate crime," online/digital, legal and criminal justice, social services, religious/community settings

*Part C — Strategy pages (10-12):* How specific tactics work
- Safety assessment first (FOUNDATION), documentation, reality-check networks, verbal/behavioral boundary-setting, strategic disengagement, disclosure decisions, de-escalation, safety planning and escape, building external evidence, professional support, recovery and post-traumatic growth

*Part D — Cross-cutting frameworks (5):*
- Safety assessment framework (COMPLETE), manipulation-vs-miscommunication decision tree, power differential analysis, "can this be repaired?" question, crisis resources directory

**Pages completed:**
| Page | Status |
|------|--------|
| [Safety Assessment](manipulation-hub-safety-assessment.md) (foundation page, establishes template for all others) | COMPLETE |

**Structural requirement:** Every sub-hub page cross-links to the relevant pragmatics categories so readers can jump to mechanism explanations without having to read the entire pragmatics curriculum first. This cross-linking pattern should become a project-wide convention in Stage 5 (Organization).

### Stage 5: Organization, Structure, and Learning Tracks

**Status:** NOT STARTED
**Purpose:** Design the information architecture for the learning platform

#### 5a: Content Organization
- Map the 24 categories + 4 intro pages into a navigable structure
- Define prerequisite relationships (which categories depend on others)
- Identify natural groupings beyond the current tier system
- Design the table of contents / site map

#### 5b: Learning Tracks
- Design multiple paths through the material for different learner profiles
- Examples:
  - **"Quick Start" track**: Most practical categories first (Phatic, Questions, Hedging, Register)
  - **"Deep Understanding" track**: Theory-first (Speech Acts → Implicature → Relevance Theory → everything else)
  - **"Workplace Survival" track**: Categories most relevant to employment
  - **"Social Navigation" track**: Friendship, dating, casual interaction focused
  - **"Digital First" track**: For learners whose primary communication is online
- Each track defines: entry point, sequence, which sections of each category to prioritize, what to skip

#### 5c: Difficulty/Complexity Mapping
- Within each category, identify content difficulty levels
- Map content to age ranges / experience levels
- Define what "mastery" looks like at each level

**Deliverable:** Information architecture document, learning track definitions, difficulty map

### Stage 6: Interactive Learning Design Research

**Status:** NOT STARTED
**Purpose:** Research how effective interactive learning platforms work, so the website can be built on proven pedagogy

#### 6a: Platform Analysis
- **Duolingo**: Gamification model, question types, spaced repetition, skill trees, heart system, streak mechanics
- **Brilliant**: Problem-first approach, guided discovery, interactive visualizations, conceptual scaffolding
- **Khan Academy**: Mastery-based progression, video + practice, knowledge map
- **Anki/spaced repetition systems**: Memory science, optimal review intervals
- **Other relevant platforms**: Memrise, Babbel, Coursera interactive elements

**Research questions:**
- What question/exercise types do these platforms use?
- How do they handle progression and mastery?
- What gamification elements actually improve learning vs. just engagement?
- How do they handle conceptual vs. procedural knowledge?
- What works specifically for social/pragmatic skills (not just factual recall)?

#### 6b: Pedagogy Research
- Spaced repetition science (Ebbinghaus, Leitner, modern research)
- Mastery-based learning (Bloom's mastery learning, competency-based progression)
- Scaffolding theory (Vygotsky's ZPD, gradual release of responsibility)
- Scenario-based learning / branching dialogues
- Role-play and simulation in pragmatic skill development
- Transfer of learning: how to ensure skills learned in an app transfer to real life

#### 6c: Autistic-Specific Learning Design
- What learning modalities work best for autistic learners? (visual, systematic, pattern-based)
- How to handle the executive function demands of self-directed learning
- Motivation and engagement for autistic learners (interest-driven, autonomy, clear structure)
- Avoiding masking-inducement: how to teach skills without implying mandatory performance
- Energy management integration: how to build in awareness of cognitive load

**Deliverable:** Interactive learning design research document covering platforms, pedagogy, and autism-specific considerations

### Stage 7: Exercise Data Schema and Format

**Status:** NOT STARTED  
**Depends on:** Stage 6 (need to know what question types exist before defining the schema)

**Purpose:** Define a structured data format (JSON/YAML) for exercises that a frontend can consume directly

#### 7a: Question Type Taxonomy
Based on Stage 6 research, define the full set of question/exercise types the platform will use. Examples might include:
- Multiple choice (identify the correct pragmatic interpretation)
- Fill in the blank (complete a hedged sentence)
- Matching (match speech act to social function)
- Ordering/sequencing (arrange conversation moves in correct order)
- Scenario judgment (read a scenario, choose the best response)
- Drag-and-drop (place discourse markers in appropriate positions)
- Scale/slider (rate the formality level of an utterance)
- Free response with rubric (for advanced learners)
- Audio/prosody exercises (identify stress patterns, tone)
- Comparison (which version is more/less polite, formal, hedged)

#### 7b: Schema Design
- Define the JSON/YAML schema for each question type
- Include metadata: category, difficulty, learning track, prerequisite skills, age range
- Include explanation/feedback text for correct and incorrect answers
- Support for hints, scaffolding, and progressive difficulty
- Localization hooks (for potential future non-English adaptations)

#### 7c: Sample Data
- Create a representative sample of exercises for 2-3 categories to validate the schema
- Test that the schema handles edge cases (multi-part questions, context-dependent answers, "there's no single right answer" scenarios)

**Deliverable:** Schema specification document + sample exercise data files

### Stage 8: Comprehensive Exercise Data

**Status:** NOT STARTED  
**Depends on:** Stage 7 (need validated schema before bulk production)

**Purpose:** Produce the full exercise dataset covering all categories, all difficulty levels, and all learning tracks

#### Scope
- Target: minimum exercises per category TBD (based on Stage 6 pedagogy research -- likely 30-50+ per category for adequate spaced repetition coverage)
- Cover all question types from Stage 7a
- Multiple difficulty levels per category
- Ensure coverage across age ranges and learning tracks
- Include "real world" scenarios drawn from the practical research in each category

#### Quality criteria
- Every exercise teaches or reinforces a specific pragmatic concept
- Incorrect answer options are plausible (common mistakes, not obviously wrong)
- Feedback text explains WHY the answer is correct/incorrect
- Scenarios are realistic and representative of actual communication situations
- Content is neurodiversity-affirming (no "right way to be," just "how the system works")

**Deliverable:** Complete exercise dataset in the Stage 7 schema format

### Stage 10: Accessibility and UX Research for Autistic Users

**Status:** NOT STARTED
**Purpose:** Research sensory-friendly, cognitively accessible design principles for the learning platform

#### 10a: Sensory Design
- Color palette research (avoiding sensory overload, high contrast options)
- Typography (readable fonts, spacing, size)
- Animation and motion (when helpful vs. when distracting/distressing)
- Sound design (optional audio, no autoplay, volume control)
- Visual complexity management (information density, whitespace)

#### 10b: Cognitive Accessibility
- Information architecture for autistic users (clear, predictable, systematic)
- Navigation patterns (explicit breadcrumbs, progress indicators, no hidden menus)
- Cognitive load management (chunking, progressive disclosure, save-and-resume)
- Executive function support (clear next steps, session planning, reminders)
- Processing time (no timed elements, or generous timing with opt-out)

#### 10c: Interaction Design
- Input methods (keyboard navigation, screen reader compatibility, touch-friendly)
- Feedback mechanisms (clear, immediate, non-punishing)
- Customization (dark mode, font size, reduced motion, content density settings)
- Error handling (forgiving, clear correction paths, no penalization for mistakes)

#### 10d: Existing Research and Standards
- WCAG 2.1/2.2 guidelines (baseline accessibility)
- Autism-specific UX research (if any exists)
- Sensory processing research applied to UI design
- Universal Design for Learning (UDL) principles
- Research on autistic preferences in digital interfaces

**Deliverable:** UX/accessibility research document with design principles, guidelines, and specific recommendations
