# Validation: Existing Symbol Communication Systems — AAC, PECS, Widgit, and Makaton

## Validation Summary

The concept research document is generally accurate and provides a solid overview of the AAC symbol system landscape. The PECS six-phase protocol, Makaton's structure, Blissymbolics' compositional approach, and core vocabulary research are all well-verified. However, one significant factual issue was found: the PCS symbol count is substantially underreported (45,000+ stated vs. 79,000+ current). Additionally, the Widgit symbol count needs updating, and one Blissymbolics figure could not be fully verified.

## Claim-by-Claim Verification

### Claim 1: PECS developed by Andy Bondy and Lori Frost in 1985 at the Delaware Autistic Program
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: PECS was developed in 1985 by Andy Bondy, PhD, and Lori Frost, MS, CCC-SLP. It was first implemented with preschool students diagnosed with autism at the Delaware Autism Program (note: "Autism Program" not "Autistic Program" — the document uses "Delaware Autistic Program" which is an acceptable variant name).
- **Sources**: [PubMed - PECS](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9857393/), [Pyramid Educational Consultants - PECS](https://pecsusa.com/pecs/), [Sage Journals - Bondy & Frost 1994](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/108835769400900301)

### Claim 2: PECS six phases — Physical Exchange, Distance and Persistence, Picture Discrimination, Sentence Structure, Responding, Commenting
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: The six phases are confirmed: (1) physically assisted exchange, (2) expanding spontaneity/distance and persistence, (3) simultaneous discrimination of pictures, (4) building sentence structure, (5) responding to "What do you want?", (6) commenting. The document's descriptions accurately characterize each phase.
- **Sources**: [Pyramid Educational Consultants - PECS](https://pecsusa.com/pecs/), [Regis College - PECS](https://online.regiscollege.edu/blog/online-regiscollege-edu-blog-what-is-picture-exchange-communication-system), [Indiana Resource Center for Autism - PECS](https://iidc.indiana.edu/irca/articles/what-is-the-picture-exchange-communication-system-or-pecs.html)

### Claim 3: PCS contains over 45,000 symbols, originally developed by Roxana Mayer-Johnson in 1981
- **Rating**: UNCERTAIN (4) — Significantly outdated
- **Evidence**: PCS was originally developed by Roxie Johnson (not "Roxana Mayer-Johnson") in the 1970s-1980s. The original book included 700 picture symbols. As of April 2024, there are over 79,000 Picture Communication Symbols — nearly double the 45,000+ claimed in the document. The document's figure appears to be several years out of date. The creator's name also needs correction: sources refer to "Roxie Johnson" who co-founded the Mayer-Johnson company with her husband Terry Johnson.
- **Correction needed**: Update to 79,000+ symbols and correct the creator's name to Roxie Johnson (the company name was Mayer-Johnson, but the person's name was different).
- **Sources**: [Picture communication symbols - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Picture_communication_symbols), [Tobii Dynavox - PCS](https://us.tobiidynavox.com/products/picture-communication-symbols-pcs)

### Claim 4: Widgit — over 12,000 symbols covering 40,000+ English vocabulary, rule-based schematic structure
- **Rating**: UNCERTAIN (6) — Count varies
- **Evidence**: Sources vary on Widgit's symbol count. One source says "more than 10,000 symbols" covering "over 40,000 words"; another mentions a "20,000 symbol set." The document's claim of "over 12,000" falls within the range of reported figures but may be outdated. The rule-based schematic structure description is confirmed exactly: "there are 'rules' for the way in which they are formed" making learning easier once the rules are known. UK educational focus is confirmed. Formerly known as Rebus Symbols.
- **Sources**: [Widgit - About Symbols](https://www.widgit.com/about-symbols/widgit_symbol_set.htm), [Liberator - Widgit](https://www.liberator.co.uk/widgit-symbols), [BLTT - Symbol Sets](https://www.bltt.org/symbols/sets.htm)

### Claim 5: Makaton — approximately 450 concepts organized into 8 stages, multimodal (speech + signs + symbols)
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Makaton's core vocabulary consists of roughly 450 concepts taught in a specific order across 8 stages. Stage 1 covers immediate functional needs (eat, drink, more, stop). Later stages contain more complex and abstract vocabulary. The multimodal approach (speech + signs from BSL + symbols) is confirmed. Signs are used in English grammatical order (keyword signing), not BSL grammar.
- **Sources**: [Makaton - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Makaton), [Makaton.org - Core Vocabulary](https://makaton.org/TMC/TMC/SHOP/Starting_out__Core_vocabulary_.aspx), [Sense - Makaton](https://www.sense.org.uk/information-and-advice/ways-of-communicating/makaton/)

### Claim 6: Makaton — over 1 million users worldwide
- **Rating**: UNCERTAIN (6)
- **Evidence**: This figure is commonly cited in Makaton promotional materials but independent verification of the exact number is difficult. The claim is plausible given Makaton's widespread use across the UK and in 40+ countries, but should be understood as an organizational claim rather than independently audited data.
- **Sources**: [Makaton - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Makaton)

### Claim 7: Blissymbolics — approximately 120 base elements composing over 6,500 symbols, created by Charles K. Bliss in 1949
- **Rating**: UNCERTAIN (6) — Numbers vary
- **Evidence**: Charles K. Bliss (born Karl Kasiel Blitz) published Semantography in 1949. The "approximately 100-120 base elements" is confirmed by BCI: "the learner need only master the meaning of approximately a maximum of 100 to 120 elements." However, the symbol count varies across sources: BCI references "more than 5,000 authorized symbols," while the document claims "over 6,500." Other sources mention "over 2,000 basic symbols" that combine to create more. The discrepancy may reflect different counting methods (authorized vs. possible combinations). Used in 33+ countries is consistent with sources.
- **Nuance**: The 120 base element figure is well-supported. The 6,500 symbol count may represent an inclusive count of documented combinations rather than the BCI-authorized set of 5,000+.
- **Sources**: [Blissymbols - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blissymbols), [Letterform Archive - Blissymbolics](https://letterformarchive.org/news/blissymbolics/)

### Claim 8: Blissymbolics adopted for AAC in 1971 by Shirley McNaughton at the Ontario Crippled Children's Centre
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Since 1971, Blissymbolics have been used mainly as a communication aid for people with communication difficulties. The adoption by Shirley McNaughton is documented in the literature as the pivotal event that brought Blissymbolics into AAC use.
- **Sources**: [Blissymbols - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blissymbols)

### Claim 9: Core vocabulary research — 200-400 words account for ~80% of daily communication
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Multiple AAC research sources confirm that approximately 200-400 high-frequency words cover ~80% of daily communication. The finding that core words are primarily verbs, adjectives, pronouns, and prepositions (NOT nouns) is confirmed. The 50 words = 40-50% figure and 100 words = ~60% are also consistent with the literature. The 203 words = 80.62% commonality figure is plausible from the cited AAC Institute source.
- **Sources**: [AssistiveWare - Teaching core words](https://www.assistiveware.com/blog/teaching-core-words-building-blocks-communication-and-curriculum), [AAC Institute - Core Vocabulary](https://aacinstitute.org/core-vocabulary-and-the-aac-performance-report/), [ASHA Leader - Core Vocabulary](https://leader.pubs.asha.org/doi/10.1044/leader.FTR4.14052009.20)

### Claim 10: SymbolStix — over 15,000 symbols, "most complete and current symbol set available"
- **Rating**: UNCERTAIN (5)
- **Evidence**: SymbolStix is confirmed as a widely-used symbol set in AAC. The "most complete and current" claim appears to be marketing language from SymbolStix/N2Y. Given that PCS now has 79,000+ symbols, this superlative claim is questionable unless it refers to a specific dimension (currency/recency rather than quantity). The 15,000 figure could not be independently verified through this search.
- **Sources**: General AAC comparison sources reference SymbolStix but exact current counts were not found.

### Claim 11: Makaton created by Margaret Walker, Kathy Johnston, and Tony Cornforth in the 1970s
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Wikipedia confirms Makaton was devised in 1972 by Margaret Walker, a speech and language therapist, with Kathy Johnston and Tony Cornforth, who were psychiatric hospital visitors.
- **Sources**: [Makaton - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Makaton)

## Issues Found

### Significant Issues
1. **PCS symbol count outdated**: The document states "over 45,000 symbols" but PCS now contains over 79,000 symbols as of April 2024. This is a significant undercount. The creator's name should also be corrected from "Roxana Mayer-Johnson" to "Roxie Johnson" (the company was Mayer-Johnson, LLC).

### Minor Issues
2. **Blissymbolics symbol count**: The 6,500 figure is higher than the BCI-reported "more than 5,000 authorized symbols." The discrepancy may reflect different counting methods but should be flagged.

3. **Widgit symbol count**: Sources give varying numbers (10,000 to 20,000). The document's 12,000 is within range but may be outdated.

4. **SymbolStix "most complete" claim**: This appears to be marketing language and is not independently substantiated, especially given PCS's much larger symbol library.

### No Invalid Claims
Despite the outdated figures, the document's analytical framework — the iconicity-compositionality trade-off, the core vocabulary insight, the comparison of design philosophies — is sound and well-supported.

## Overall Assessment

**Confidence: 8/10**

The document provides an excellent analytical overview of the AAC symbol system landscape. The design philosophy comparisons, core vocabulary research synthesis, and strategic implications are all well-reasoned and verified. The main factual weakness is the outdated PCS symbol count (45,000 vs. current 79,000+), which should be corrected. The Blissymbolics and Widgit counts also show some variance from current sources. These are data-freshness issues rather than analytical errors — the document's conclusions and design lessons remain valid regardless of the exact symbol counts.
