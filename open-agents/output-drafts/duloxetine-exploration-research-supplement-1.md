# Supplemental Research 1: Duloxetine Clinical Trial Evidence

## Purpose and Headline

The original research misread two trials in the deflationary direction. The validation pass corrected both — but **overcorrected in the pro-duloxetine direction**, and missed three trials that change the picture materially.

This supplement resolves the trial evidence from primary sources where obtainable. The short version:

**Both prior passes were wrong, and the truth is more useful than either.**

- The original research said 20mg "failed." That is wrong for anxiety and overstated for pain.
- The validator said 20mg is "under-studied, not disproven" and that the reader's benefit is "the **expected** result." That is right for anxiety and **wrong for pain** — low-dose duloxetine has now been tested for pain three times, in ~350 patients, across two indications, and failed every time, including once with **adequate power**.
- Neither pass found **Chappell 2008**, a 6-month fibromyalgia trial in which **60/120mg failed both co-primary endpoints**. That is the dose the reader is being asked to increase to.

**The synthesis that survives**: at 20mg, duloxetine is a serotonergic drug that does serotonergic things (anxiety, global improvement) and does not do noradrenergic things (pain). The PET occupancy curve and the clinical trial dose–response **independently agree on this**. The reader's reported benefit pattern — anxiety yes, no pain claim — is exactly the low-dose signature the trials show. The researcher's *pharmacological reframe was correct*; the deflationary spin was not; and the validator's rehabilitation of 20mg-for-pain was not either.

## Assignment 1: Russell 2008 (PMID 18395345)

### 1.1 Citation and funding — verified

**Russell IJ, Mease PJ, Smith TR, Kajdasz DK, Wohlreich MM, Detke MJ, Walker DJ, Chappell AS, Arnold LM.** "Efficacy and safety of duloxetine for treatment of fibromyalgia in patients with or without major depressive disorder: Results from a 6-month, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, fixed-dose trial." *Pain.* 2008 Jun;136(3):432–444. doi:10.1016/j.pain.2008.02.024. **PMID 18395345.** ✓ Resolves to the paper claimed.

**Funding/COI**: **Sponsored by Eli Lilly and Company.** Kajdasz, Wohlreich, Detke, Walker, and Chappell were affiliated with Lilly Research Laboratories; multiple authors were **employees and stockholders** of Eli Lilly. Russell, Mease, and Arnold are academic fibromyalgia researchers with extensive industry relationships. **Evidence quality: HIGH (funding is not in dispute).**

### 1.2 Design and arm sizes — RESOLVED, and the validator's numbers were slightly off

**Confirmed from two independent sources** (the Lilly five-trial pooled safety analysis, PMC2721139; and a secondary review):

| Arm | n | Share |
|---|---|---|
| Duloxetine **20 mg/day** | **79** | 15.2% |
| Duloxetine 60 mg/day | **150** | 28.8% |
| Duloxetine 120 mg/day | **147** | 28.3% |
| Placebo | **144** | 27.7% |
| **Total** | **520** | |

- **Randomization ratio ≈ 1:2:2:2** — **CONFIRMED**. The validator's structural claim is correct.
- The validator estimated "≈74 in the 20mg arm vs ≈148 in each other arm." The **actual** figures are **79 / 150 / 147 / 144**. Directionally right, numerically off. Use 79.
- Cochrane's "20 mg (223 participants)" reconciles exactly: **79 + 144 = 223** ✓
- **Evidence quality: HIGH.** Two independent sources agree.

**Duration and the titration**: 6 months total (28 weeks of placebo-controlled treatment). Verbatim from the Lilly pooled safety analysis: *"After 15 weeks, patients in the 20 mg duloxetine group were increased to 60 mg QD and continued on that dose for 13 more weeks."*

> **Interpretive consequence — important and previously unstated.** The titration was at **week 15**, not "3 months." This means **the only clean 20mg data in this trial is the first ~15 weeks.** Any statement about "the 20mg group at 6 months" is describing **patients who had been on 60mg for 13 weeks**. Both the original research ("titrated up to 60mg after three months by design") and the validator treat this as a footnote. It is not: it means the 6-month arm labelled "20mg" **is not a 20mg arm**, and no 6-month conclusion about 20mg can be drawn from this trial in either direction.

### 1.3 Did the 20mg arm separate? — **GENUINELY UNRESOLVED, and Cochrane contradicts itself**

This is the crux, and I must report it as unresolved rather than pick a side.

**Evidence that 20mg DID separate on PGI-I:**

1. **Cochrane CD007115.pub3, Analysis 3.8** — verbatim: *"Four studies reported the PGI‐I (Arnold 2005; Russell 2008; Chappell 2008; Arnold 2012), which was **significantly in favour of duloxetine at the 20 mg, 30 mg, 60 mg and 120 mg doses** (120 mg dose MD ‐0.44, 95% CI ‐0.66 to ‐0.23; 3 trials, 826 participants). **The magnitude of change at each dose was very similar, with no dose effect. However, the magnitude of change failed to reach a level considered to be clinically significant.**"*
2. **Scholz, Hammonds & Boomershine**, *J Pain Res* 2009 (PMC3004624) — verbatim: *"While PGI-I scores showed statistical improvement in the 20 mg/day duloxetine group, this group failed to meet the coprimary endpoint of pain reduction."*

**Evidence that 20mg did NOT separate on anything:**

3. **Cochrane CD007115.pub3, fibromyalgia narrative** — verbatim: *"**The 20 mg dose of duloxetine in Russell 2008 (223 participants) did not show significant differences in any of the reported measures.**"*

> **⚠ These two Cochrane statements contradict each other within the same review.** Analysis 3.8 says PGI-I was significant at 20mg. The narrative says 20mg was significant on nothing. Both are Cochrane's own words.
>
> **This resolves the mystery of how the researcher and the validator reached opposite conclusions while both citing real sources.** The original research followed Cochrane's narrative (statement 3); the validator followed Scholz + Cochrane's Analysis 3.8 (statements 1–2). **Neither fabricated anything. Cochrane is internally inconsistent, and the primary paper is paywalled.**

**My verdict**: **UNRESOLVED (5/10).** The weight of evidence *slightly* favours "20mg did separate on PGI-I" — two sources say so and one of them is Cochrane's own quantitative analysis, which is more reliable than review prose. But I could not confirm it against Russell's full text, and **I will not resolve it in either direction.**

**⚠ COI on the source the validator relied on — RETRIEVED (the validator could not get this):**

The validator flagged: *"The Scholz/Boomershine review is not independent of the pro-duloxetine position and I could not retrieve its COI statement (tandfonline 403)."*

**I retrieved it via PMC.** Verbatim disclosure: *"CSB is on the speaker's bureau for **Eli-Lilly Inc.**, Pfizer Inc., and Forest pharmaceuticals Inc. and receives support from Pfizer Inc. and the National Institutes of Health."* All three authors are at Vanderbilt.

**Chad S. Boomershine is on Eli Lilly's speaker's bureau.** The validator's caution was **warranted and is now confirmed**. This matters specifically because the *interpretation* the validator leaned on most heavily — that the 20mg null was a power artifact "and not due to lack of efficacy in pain relief" — is an **opinion authored by someone paid to speak for the manufacturer**. It is not a finding. Cite it as an interested party's interpretation or not at all.

### 1.4 Was the 20mg arm powered? — my own estimate, clearly labelled

**No power calculation for the 20mg arm was retrievable.** The trial was evidently powered for the 60/120mg arms. The following is **my calculation, not a retrieved figure** — treat it as an estimate with stated assumptions.

Assuming BPI average pain severity SD ≈ 2.1 (typical for fibromyalgia trials):

| Comparison | n vs n | SE of difference | Difference needed for p<0.05 |
|---|---|---|---|
| 120mg vs placebo | 147 vs 144 | ≈ 0.246 | ≈ 0.48 |
| **20mg vs placebo** | **79 vs 144** | **≈ 0.294** | **≈ 0.58** |

Power of the 20mg arm to detect an effect of the size actually observed at higher doses:

- If 20mg truly matched **120mg** (observed difference 0.92): power ≈ **88%** — adequate.
- If 20mg truly matched **60mg** (estimated difference ~0.75): power ≈ **72%** — moderately underpowered.

**Conclusion**: the 20mg arm was **moderately underpowered — not catastrophically so.** Scholz's framing ("50% fewer patients") is accurate about the design but **overstates the consequence**. Had 20mg produced a genuinely 120mg-sized analgesic effect, this trial would probably have detected it (~88% power). The honest statement is: *"the 20mg arm had roughly 70–90% power depending on the true effect size; a null there is weak evidence of absence, not no evidence."*

**Evidence quality: MODERATE — this is my inference from published arm sizes and a standard SD assumption, not a retrieved power calculation.** Flag as such downstream.

From the **primary abstract** (PubMed, verified):

| Timepoint | Endpoint | 120mg | Placebo | p |
|---|---|---|---|---|

| 3 months | PGI-I | **2.89** | 3.39 | **0.004** |

NO.** Cochrane, verbatim: *"120 mg was no more effective than 60 mg."* And: *"It is notable that no dose effect exists from 60 mg to 120 mg."* Lilly's own pooled analysis (PMC2781036) states it too, verbatim: *"previous analyses found **no differences in efficacy outcomes between 60 mg/d or 120 mg/d**."*

- **Evidence quality: HIGH** for the 60/120mg figures (primary abstract). **LOW** for the 20mg point estimate (unretrieved).

The validator's claim is **fully confirmed**, and I retrieved it from Lilly's own publication rather than a secondary review.

**Source**: Arnold LM, Clauw DJ, Wohlreich MM, Wang F, Ahl J, Gaynor PJ, Chappell AS. "Efficacy of Duloxetine in Patients With Fibromyalgia: Pooled Analysis of 4 Placebo-Controlled Clinical Trials." *Prim Care Companion J Clin Psychiatry.* 2009;11(5):237–244. **PMID 19956462 / PMC2781036.**

- **Who wrote it**: **Wohlreich, Wang, Ahl, Gaynor, and Chappell are all employees of "Eli Lilly and Company and/or one of its subsidiaries, Indianapolis, Indiana."** Arnold (Cincinnati) and Clauw (Michigan) are academics who received Lilly support.

## Assignment 2: Nicolini 2009 — the most important paper for this reader

### 2.1 Citation — verified, and the validator's implicit framing needs correcting

**Nicolini H, Bakish D, Duenas H, Spann M, Erickson J, Hallberg C, Ball S, Sagman D, Russell JM.** "Improvement of psychic and somatic symptoms in adult patients with generalized anxiety disorder: examination from a duloxetine, venlafaxine extended-release and placebo-controlled trial." *Psychological Medicine.* 2009 Feb;39(2):267–276. doi:10.1017/S0033291708003401. **PMID 18485261.** ✓ Resolves to the paper claimed.

**Note**: this is in ***Psychological Medicine***, not a psychopharmacology journal, and it is framed as an **examination of psychic vs somatic symptoms** — i.e. it is a *symptom-domain analysis of a registration trial*, not the trial's primary efficacy report. That framing matters for §2.4.

### 2.2 Design and arms — **the 20mg arm was genuinely fixed-dose. CONFIRMED.**

From the **primary abstract** (verbatim, verified):

| Arm | n |
|---|---|
| Placebo | **170** |
| **Duloxetine 20 mg (fixed)** | **84** |
| Duloxetine 60–120 mg (flexible) | **158** |

- **Duration: 10 weeks.** Multi-centre, double-blind, placebo-controlled.
- Adult outpatients, DSM-IV GAD, mean age 42.8, 57.1% women.
- **Was 20mg genuinely a fixed-dose arm? YES** — the validator is correct. It was included *"to satisfy European regulatory requirements."*
- **Evidence quality: HIGH.**

### 2.3 Did the 20mg arm beat placebo? — **YES on two measures, NO on a third. The validator got this wrong.**

**From the primary abstract, verbatim:**

> *"Each of the three active treatment groups had **significantly greater improvements on HAMA total score** from baseline to endpoint compared with placebo (**p=0.01–0.001**)."*

> *"For the **HAMA psychic factor score**, **both duloxetine treatment arms** and venlafaxine XR demonstrated **significantly greater improvement compared with placebo** (p=0.01–0.001)."*

> *"For the **HAMA somatic factor score**, the mean improvement in the duloxetine 60–120 mg and venlafaxine XR groups was significantly greater than placebo (p⩽0.05 and p⩽0.01 respectively), whose mean improvement **did not differ from the duloxetine 20 mg group (p=0.07)**."*

**Verdict:**

| Endpoint | Duloxetine 20mg vs placebo | Status |
|---|---|---|
| **HAM-A total** | **SIGNIFICANT** (p=0.01–0.001) | ✅ separated |
| **HAM-A psychic factor** | **SIGNIFICANT** (p=0.01–0.001) | ✅ separated |
| **HAM-A somatic factor** | **NOT significant (p=0.07)** | ❌ did not separate |

> **⚠ CONTRADICTS THE VALIDATOR.** The validator wrote: *"HAM-A fell 45% on 20mg vs 55% on 60–120mg; **all changes statistically significant vs placebo**"* and repeated *"with all changes statistically significant versus placebo."*
>
> **That is false at the factor level.** The 20mg arm **did not separate from placebo on the HAM-A somatic factor (p=0.07)**. The validator took this phrasing from a secondary review (PMC2840566), which flattens it. The primary abstract is explicit and contradicts the review.
>
> The validator's headline conclusion — that 20mg beat placebo for GAD — **survives and is correct**. But the "all changes significant" gloss does not, and this was the claim the validator used to argue the reader's benefit is "the **expected** result."

**Evidence quality: HIGH** (primary abstract, verbatim, unambiguous).

### 2.4 Was it non-inferior to 60–120mg? — **NO. Non-inferiority was never tested for 20mg. This is decisive.**

This is the single most important distinction in the assignment, and I can resolve it cleanly.

**Allgulander C, Nutt D, Detke M, Erickson J, Spann M, Walker D, Ball SG, Russell JM.** "A non-inferiority comparison of duloxetine and venlafaxine in the treatment of adult patients with generalized anxiety disorder." *J Psychopharmacol.* 2008;22(4):417–425. **PMID 18635722.**

Allgulander 2008 was *"a prespecified plan to pool data from **two nearly identical** 10-week, multicentre, randomized, placebo-controlled, double-blind studies."* Pooled: **duloxetine n=320, venlafaxine XR n=333, placebo n=331.**

**The arithmetic reconciles exactly, which confirms the trial architecture:**

| | Hartford 2007 (PMID 17414743) | Nicolini 2009 (PMID 18485261) | Allgulander pooled |
|---|---|---|---|
| Placebo | 161 | 170 | **331** ✓ |
| Duloxetine 60–120mg | 162 | 158 | **320** ✓ |
| Venlafaxine XR | 164 | 169 | **333** ✓ |
| **Duloxetine 20mg** | **— (no such arm)** | **84** | **EXCLUDED** |

**Conclusions:**

1. **Nicolini 2009 is the primary report of the only duloxetine trial that ever contained a fixed 20mg GAD arm.** Hartford 2007 has no 20mg arm. The 20mg arm exists once, at n=84.
2. **Non-inferiority WAS formally tested — but only for duloxetine 60–120mg versus venlafaxine.** The 20mg arm was **excluded from the non-inferiority analysis entirely.**
3. **Therefore: 20mg has NEVER been tested for non-inferiority to 60–120mg.** Not against venlafaxine, not against the higher duloxetine arm.

> **⚠ CONTRADICTS THE VALIDATOR's framing.** The validator wrote that 20mg showed *"a response in the same neighbourhood as 60–120mg"* and *"near-parity with 60–120mg."* The validator did add a good caveat ("45% vs 55% HAM-A reduction is not a demonstration of equivalence"). But "near-parity" still overstates it, because:
>
> - The 45%/55% comparison is a **naive between-arm eyeball**, not a statistical comparison.
> - **No test of 20mg vs 60–120mg was performed at all.** With n=84 vs n=158, the trial had no power for one.
> - **"Beat placebo" and "as good as 60mg" are different claims, and only the first was tested.** The assignment asked me to keep these apart. They must stay apart.
>
> The honest claim: **"20mg beat placebo on HAM-A total and psychic factor in one 84-patient fixed-dose arm of one Lilly trial. It was never compared to 60mg. It failed on the somatic factor."**

**Evidence quality: HIGH** (the arithmetic reconciliation is exact and the non-inferiority design is stated in the primary abstract).

| Arm | HAM-A change | "% decrease" | Response | Remission |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Duloxetine 20mg | **−14.7 ± 1.0 (SE)** | 45% | **60%** | **42%** |

**Derived figures (my calculation):**

- **Drug–placebo difference at 20mg: ≈ 3.1 HAM-A points.** At 60–120mg: ≈ **3.7 points**.

If −15.3 is a 55% decrease, baseline ≈ 27.8. GAD registration trials typically enrol at baseline HAM-A ≈ 24–26, and **both implied baselines are too high, and they disagree with each other**. Either the review garbled the percentages, or they are computed on a different denominator than stated.
>
> **The validator reproduced "−14.7 (45%) vs −15.3 (55%)" without noticing that these numbers are mutually inconsistent.** Do not put these percentages in front of the reader as facts. **Use the absolute changes and the response/remission rates; drop the percentages until someone reads the primary paper.**

2. **NNT ~5.6 for anxiety response at 20mg is *better* than NNT 8 for fibromyalgia pain at 60mg** (§4). If those numbers hold, the reader is currently on a dose with better evidence for the thing they report benefiting from than the target dose has for the thing they were prescribed it for. **This is the most decision-relevant single comparison in the supplement** — and it is built on a secondary-source table, so it must be labelled as provisional.

**Evidence quality: LOW-MODERATE.** Numbers are secondary-source, internally inconsistent, from an n=84 arm, in a Lilly trial. The *significance* finding (§2.3) is solid; the *magnitudes* are not.

## Assignment 3: The "~66 patients have ever received 20mg" claim — **REFUTED**

### 3.1 What Sultan actually says, and where

**Sultan A, Gaskell H, Derry S, Moore RA.** "Duloxetine for painful diabetic neuropathy and fibromyalgia pain: systematic review of randomised trials." *BMC Neurol.* 2008;8:29. **PMID 18673529 / PMC2529342.**

**Verbatim, from the Results section under "Efficacy" → "50% Pain Relief":**

> *"Five of the trials used 60 mg, and all six used 120 mg; **only 66 patients (in two treatment arms) received the 20 mg dose**. The dose of duloxetine made little difference to the result."*

**Funding — and this is worth noting**: *"No specific funding was obtained for this work. Pain Research is supported in part by the Oxford Pain Research Trust."* and *"No author has any direct stock holding in any pharmaceutical company."* **Sultan 2008 is one of the few genuinely independent sources in this entire literature.** (Oxford Pain Research / Moore et al. are a well-known independent group.)

### 3.2 The claim does not survive arithmetic — **REFUTED**

The validator wrote: *"**Fewer than 70 patients have ever received 20mg in a duloxetine pain trial.**"*

**This is false.** Two trials contain 20mg arms:

| Trial | Indication | 20mg arm n | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Goldstein 2005** (PMID 15927394) | Painful diabetic neuropathy | **≈114** | 457 randomized **1:1:1:1** to 20/60/120mg/placebo → 457÷4 ≈ 114 |
| **Russell 2008** (PMID 18395345) | Fibromyalgia | **79** | Directly confirmed, §1.2 |
| **TOTAL** | | **≈193** | |

**~193 patients have received 20mg in duloxetine pain trials — roughly three times Sultan's figure.**

> **The validator's own two claims are mutually inconsistent.** It states Russell's 20mg arm held **"≈74"** patients *and* that **"fewer than 70 patients have ever received 20mg."** 74 > 70. The contradiction sits within a single validation report, in the same claim block. Neither figure was checked against the other.

**What "66" actually is**: the sentence sits inside the **"50% Pain Relief"** subsection. It is a count of patients with **evaluable dichotomous ≥50%-pain-relief data at 20mg**, not a count of patients randomized to 20mg. Sultan's Additional File 1 (the per-arm table) was not retrievable in the web version, so I cannot fully reconstruct the subset — but **193 randomized vs 66 evaluable for one responder endpoint is an ordinary attrition/reporting gap**, and reading the endpoint-specific figure as a global census is a straightforward misreading.

**Evidence quality: HIGH** on the refutation (Russell's n=79 is doubly confirmed; Goldstein's 1:1:1:1 with n=457 is stated in multiple sources). **MODERATE** on the exact Goldstein arm size (inferred from ratio × total, not directly retrieved — mark as ≈114).

### 3.3 Goldstein 2005 — **the equal-power test that kills the "underpowered" excuse**

**Goldstein DJ, Lu Y, Detke MJ, Lee TC, Iyengar S.** "Duloxetine vs. placebo in patients with painful diabetic neuropathy." *Pain.* 2005;116(1-2):109–118. **PMID 15927394.**

- **457 patients randomized 1:1:1:1** to duloxetine 20mg/d, 60mg/d, 120mg/d, or placebo. **≈114 per arm.**
- 12 weeks, fixed-dose.
- **Result**: *"Duloxetine 60 and 120 mg/d demonstrated statistically significant greater improvement compared with placebo on the 24-h Average Pain Score, beginning 1 week after randomization and continuing through the 12-week trial. **The 20 mg once-a-day treatment group had a nonsignificant decrease in pain severity compared to placebo.**"*

> **This is the most important trial in this assignment and neither prior pass mentioned it.**
>
> **In Goldstein 2005, the 20mg arm and the 60mg arm had the SAME number of patients (≈114 each). 60mg separated from placebo. 20mg did not.**
>
> **The "it was underpowered" explanation is unavailable here.** It is a within-trial, equal-n, fixed-dose comparison. When 20mg and 60mg are given the same statistical opportunity, 60mg separates on pain and 20mg does not. **That is a real dose–response for analgesia, not a power artifact.**

**Evidence quality: MODERATE-HIGH.** The design and result are consistently reported across multiple independent reviews; I could not retrieve Goldstein's full text (mark arm sizes as inferred from the stated 1:1:1:1 ratio).

### 3.4 So is "20mg is under-studied, not disproven" accurate? — **HALF RIGHT. Split it by endpoint.**

**The validator's formulation is correct for anxiety and wrong for pain.** Assembling all the low-dose pain data:

| Trial | Indication | Dose | n (drug vs placebo) | Powered? | **Pain result** | **Global/other** |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Goldstein 2005** | PDN | **20mg** | ≈114 vs ≈114 | **YES — equal to the 60mg arm, which separated** | ❌ **ns** | — |

**Honest verdict, split by endpoint:**

- **For PAIN: "under-studied, not disproven" is NOT accurate.** Low-dose duloxetine (20–30mg) has been tested **three times**, in **two different pain conditions**, in **~350 patients**, and **failed on pain every time** — including **twice with adequate power** (Goldstein's equal-n 20mg arm; Arnold 2012's 30mg arm). - **For ANXIETY: "under-studied" is exactly right** — one 84-patient arm, positive on 2 of 3 measures, never replicated, never compared to a higher dose.

> **This also retro-refutes Scholz's "similar to 60mg" claim (§1.5).** Arnold 2012's adequately powered **30mg** arm produced a BPI drug–placebo difference of only **0.34** (−2.04 vs −1.70). Russell's **120mg** arm produced **0.92**. It is not plausible that **20mg** produces ~0.75–0.92 (Scholz's "similar to 60mg") when **30mg — a higher dose, adequately powered — produces 0.34.** Scholz's characterisation is most likely a **chance high estimate in a 79-patient arm**, promoted into an argument by an author on Lilly's speaker's bureau. **Evidence quality: HIGH** on the pattern (three trials, two indications, consistent). **MODERATE** on the retro-refutation of Scholz (it is an inference across trials, not a within-trial comparison).

### 7.3 The longest efficacy data — **has no placebo arm**

**"A 1-year safety and efficacy study of duloxetine in patients with fibromyalgia."** **PMID 19454869.**

- **N=350**, 95.7% female.
- **Result**: pain reduction during the open-label phase continued through the 52-week phase in **both** duloxetine groups.

> **⚠ There is NO PLACEBO ARM.** This trial randomizes **duloxetine against duloxetine**. It can establish that 60mg and 120mg behave similarly over a year; it **cannot establish that either beats placebo**, and it cannot distinguish drug effect from natural history, regression to the mean, or the ~30–40% placebo response that fibromyalgia trials reliably produce. Everyone enrolled had already responded well enough to continue through 8 open-label weeks — a **selected, enriched population**.
>
> **This is why the FDA label says what it says.** The longest *controlled* fibromyalgia efficacy data is 6 months (Russell 2008 positive, Chappell 2008 failed). Everything beyond that is uncontrolled.

**Does benefit persist?** **Unknown, and not answerable from the current evidence base.** Cochrane records ≥50% pain response sustained at 28 weeks (**RR 1.58, 95% CI 1.10–2.27**) — that is the best long-term controlled signal, and it comes from the same 6-month trials that split on their co-primaries.

### 7.4 Years-scale safety data

- **Safety**, unlike efficacy, has longer-horizon data. The Lilly five-trial pooled safety analysis (**PMC2721139**) concluded duloxetine **20–120mg/day** showed *"a similar safety and tolerability profile in the treatment of fibromyalgia to that seen in the treatment of other indications."*
- An **open-label 52-week Japanese phase-III extension** exists (**PMID 27796152**).
- **No years-scale (>1yr) controlled efficacy data exists for fibromyalgia.** Safety follow-up beyond 1 year is open-label and sponsor-run.
- **Asymmetry worth naming for the reader**: the safety database is large and long; **the efficacy database is small and short.** "Well-tolerated long-term" is much better established than "still working long-term."

**Evidence quality: MODERATE-HIGH** on the structure of the evidence base; **LOW** on any durable-benefit claim.

## §8: Synthesis — the pattern both prior passes missed

Assembling every trial in this supplement by **dose** rather than by indication produces a strikingly consistent picture:

| Dose | Pain | Global / mental / psychic |

| **60–120mg** (Goldstein 2005, PDN) | ✅ **separates** | — |
| **60–120mg** (Arnold 2015, **CFS**) | ✅ **BPI pain + interference, SF-36 bodily pain** | ✅ mental fatigue, CGI-S; ❌ general fatigue |
| **60–120mg** (Chappell 2008, FM, 6mo) | ❌ **ns (P=0.053)** | ❌ ns (P=0.073) |

**The pattern**: **low-dose duloxetine reliably moves global/mental/psychic measures and reliably does NOT move pain. The analgesic effect appears only at ≥60mg — and even there it is inconsistent (Chappell 2008) and modest (NNTB 8, GRADE low).**

> **This independently corroborates the original research's central pharmacological reframe — from clinical trial data the researcher never cited.**
>
> The PET occupancy curve says: at 20mg, **SERT ~71%** (nearly saturated), **NET ~30%** (barely engaged); NET is the analgesic arm; meaningful NET engagement needs ≥60mg and arguably more.
>
> The clinical trial dose–response says: at 20–30mg you get **serotonergic outcomes** (anxiety, global impression, mental) and **no analgesia**; analgesia requires ≥60mg.
>
> **These are two completely independent evidence streams — human PET imaging from one Japanese lab, and ~1,500 patients across eight industry trials — and they agree.** That convergence is far stronger than either alone, and it is the most defensible thing in this entire deep dive.
>
> **The researcher had the right mechanism and the wrong trial evidence. The validator fixed the trial evidence for anxiety and broke it for pain.** The synthesis that survives both passes:
>
> **At 20mg, duloxetine is functionally a serotonergic drug. It does what serotonergic drugs do — and the reader reports exactly that. It does not do what noradrenergic drugs do — and the reader does not report that either.**

**The reader's reported benefit profile** — anxiety/episodes ↓↓ (large), morning fatigue ↓ (moderate), **no pain claim**, RLS ↑ (serotonergic side effect, per validation Claim 10) — **is precisely the low-dose duloxetine signature.** It is not anomalous. It is not evidence of placebo. And it is not evidence that the drug is "working" in the sense their prescription intends.

**Honest caveats that survive:**
- The reader's ~2-week window against "several episodes a month" remains **very thin evidence** (~1–2 expected episodes). **The researcher's regression-to-the-mean and placebo cautions are NOT refuted by this supplement** — Nicolini's placebo arm dropped **11.6 HAM-A points** with a **42% response rate**. The validator's claim that the placebo hypothesis "collapses" is **wrong**; what collapses is only the claim that a 20mg anxiolytic benefit is *pharmacologically unsupported*. Those are different things, and the validator ran them together.
- **20mg is below the FDA-approved therapeutic minimum for every psychiatric indication** (GAD starts at 30mg; MDD at 40mg). This does not mean it cannot work; it means the reader is outside the labelled range and should know it.
