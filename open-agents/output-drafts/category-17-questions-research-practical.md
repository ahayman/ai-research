# Navigating Questions in English: A Practical Guide

## Research for: English Pragmatics Knowledge Base — Category 17: Questions as Pragmatic Actions

---

## 1. QUESTION-TYPE IDENTIFICATION GUIDE

### The Core Problem for Autistic Communicators

Questions are the single most deceptive speech act in English. They look like they are all doing one thing — requesting information — but in practice, questions serve at least a dozen distinct social functions. A question like "Can you close the door?" has the grammatical form of a yes/no question about ability, but its pragmatic function is a polite command. An autistic individual whose default processing runs the "Literal Interpreter" first (Embrace Autism, 2022) will naturally parse this as an inquiry about physical capability and answer "Yes" — which is the literally correct answer but the pragmatically wrong response. The expected action is to close the door.

Research confirms that autistic adults can process **conventionalized** indirect requests (like "Can you pass the salt?") nearly as well as neurotypical peers, because these forms are so formulaic they can be learned as fixed patterns (Kissine et al., 2015; Leinonen & Ryder, 2021, *Cognitive Processing*). However, **non-conventionalized** indirect requests ("It's cold in here" = close the window) require inferring the speaker's unspoken intention, which demands more effortful processing. Recent research (PMC 8831260; PMC 11897084) found that autistic adults are actually quite skilled at using discourse context to interpret hints — but it takes more conscious effort and time than it does for neurotypical speakers.

The decision tree below is designed to make this conscious inferential process faster by giving it an explicit structure.

### The Question-Type Decision Tree

**STEP 1: Does the asker already know the answer?**

Ask yourself: Is this person in a position where they would already have this information?

| Signal | Interpretation | Go to... |
|--------|---------------|-----------|
| **YES — They definitely know** | This is a **display question** or **rhetorical question** | Step 1A |
| **MAYBE — They might know, but want confirmation** | This is a **leading question** or **confirmation-seeking question** | Step 1B |
| **NO — They genuinely don't know** | Continue to Step 2 | Step 2 |

**Step 1A: They already know the answer**

Further distinguish:

- **Is the asker in an authority/testing role?** (teacher, interviewer, quiz host, manager evaluating knowledge)
  - → **Display question**: They want you to demonstrate your knowledge. Answer fully and clearly.
  - Examples: "What is the capital of France?" (in a classroom); "How would you handle a merge conflict?" (in a coding interview); "What does this error message mean?" (manager testing your understanding)
  - **Expected response**: Show what you know. Be thorough but concise.

- **Is the asker making a point rather than asking?**
  - → **Rhetorical question**: They are asserting the opposite of what they appear to ask. No answer is required.
  - Examples: "Who cares?" (= nobody cares); "Isn't that the whole point?" (= that IS the point); "What's the use?" (= there is no use); "How should I know?" (= I don't/can't know)
  - **Expected response**: Nod, agree, or stay silent. Do NOT provide a literal answer. "Who cares?" does not want a list of people who care.
  - **Detection cue**: Rhetorical questions often have an exasperated or emphatic tone, and the asker usually continues talking without pausing for a response.

- **Is the asker expressing frustration or criticism?**
  - → **Accusatory rhetorical question**: "What time do you call this?" (= you are late); "How many times do I have to tell you?" (= I am frustrated at repeating myself); "What were you thinking?" (= I think your decision was bad)
  - **Expected response**: Acknowledge the underlying emotion, not the literal question. "Sorry I'm late" — NOT "It's 9:17 AM."
  - **Danger zone**: These are particularly tricky for literal processors. The question form disguises a complaint or accusation.

**Step 1B: They might know and want confirmation**

- **Tag question with falling intonation?** ("This is right, isn't it?" / "We're meeting at 3, aren't we?")
  - → **Confirmation-demanding**: They believe they are correct and want you to agree. The falling intonation on the tag signals assertiveness (Kimps, 2018; *Tag Questions in Conversation*).
  - **Expected response**: Confirm if you agree ("Yes, that's right"). If you disagree, you need a **softened** disagreement: "Actually, I think it might be 3:30" — not just "No."

- **Tag question with rising intonation?** ("This is right, isn't it?" said with genuine uncertainty)
  - → **Confirmation-seeking**: They genuinely aren't sure and want your input.
  - **Expected response**: Give your honest assessment. "Yes, that looks right" or "Actually, I think there might be an error in line 3."

- **Leading question in conversation?** ("Don't you think the movie was great?" / "You liked it, didn't you?")
  - → The speaker has telegraphed their preferred answer. They think the movie was great and want you to agree.
  - **Expected response**: If you agree, agree enthusiastically. If you disagree, soften: "It had some great moments, but the ending didn't quite work for me" — not "No, I hated it."

- **Leading question in adversarial context?** (courtroom, debate, argument)
  - → The speaker is trying to control your answer. "Isn't it true that you were at the scene?"
  - **Expected response**: Be careful. You can reject the framing: "That's not quite how I would describe it."

**STEP 2: Is the question asking for INFORMATION, ACTION, or SOCIAL CONNECTION?**

Ask yourself: If I gave only a verbal informational answer, would the asker be satisfied?

| Signal | Interpretation | Go to... |
|--------|---------------|-----------|
| **The question is about a fact, opinion, or explanation** | → **Information-seeking question** | Step 3 |
| **The question mentions ability or willingness** ("Can you..." / "Would you...") | → Likely an **indirect request for action** | Step 2A |
| **The question is a greeting or social ritual** ("How are you?" / "What's up?") | → **Phatic question** | Step 2B |
| **The question is an offer or invitation** ("Would you like to...?" / "Can I get you...?") | → **Offer/invitation** | Step 2C |

**Step 2A: Indirect Requests for Action**

These are commands disguised as questions. English speakers use interrogative form for politeness — the question form gives the listener a theoretical "out" even though the expected response is compliance.

**Conventionalized indirect requests** (these are so common they function as fixed formulas):
- "Can you close the door?" → Close the door.
- "Could you pass the salt?" → Pass the salt.
- "Would you mind turning down the music?" → Turn down the music.
- "Do you have the time?" → Tell me what time it is.
- "Do you know where the bathroom is?" → Tell me where the bathroom is. (NOT "Yes, I do.")

**Recognition pattern**: If the question asks about your **ability** or **willingness** to do something, and the action is physically possible and immediately relevant, it is almost certainly a request, not a question.

**Non-conventionalized indirect requests** (these require inference from context):
- "It's cold in here." → Close the window or turn up the heat.
- "I'm really thirsty." → Offer a drink.
- "That box looks heavy." → Offer to help carry it.
- "I wonder what time it is." → Tell them the time.

**Recognition pattern**: A statement about a problem or need that is made within earshot of someone who could solve it is likely an indirect request. Ask: "Could I do something about the situation they're describing?"

**Step 2B: Phatic (Social Ritual) Questions**

Phatic questions are not really questions at all — they are **greetings, social lubricant, and connection rituals**. The word "phatic" was coined by Malinowski (1923) and refers to language used for social bonding rather than information transfer.

**The "How are you?" family** (greetings):
- "How are you?" / "How's it going?" / "How are things?" / "What's up?"
- **Expected response**: Brief, positive, formulaic. "Good, thanks! How about you?" / "Not bad, you?" / "Pretty good!"
- **NOT expected**: A genuine medical report, a list of your problems, or a detailed account of your emotional state (unless the person is a close friend asking with genuine concern in a private context).
- **How to tell the difference**: If asked in passing (hallway, checkout line, start of a meeting), it is phatic. If asked by a close friend who sits down, makes eye contact, and uses a concerned tone, it may be genuine. Context and tone are the differentiators.

**The "What's new?" family** (conversation openers):
- "What's new?" / "What have you been up to?" / "Anything exciting happening?"
- **Expected response**: One or two things, kept brief. "Not much — been busy with work. How about you?" This is an invitation to chat, not a request for a comprehensive life update.

**Step 2C: Offers and Invitations**

- "Would you like some coffee?" → Yes please / No thank you. Simple acceptance or declination.
- "Do you want to come to the party?" → Accept, decline, or ask for details.
- "Can I help you with that?" → Accept or decline help.
- **Note**: In some cultures and contexts, the first offer is expected to be declined, and the asker will offer again. In mainstream American English, it is generally acceptable to accept on the first offer.

---

**STEP 3: What kind of informational response is expected?**

You have determined this is a genuine information-seeking question. Now determine the expected level of detail.

| Signal | Interpretation | Expected Response |
|--------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Fixed, brief answer exists** ("What time is it?" / "Where's the bathroom?") | **Factual question** | Give the specific answer. "It's 3:15." / "Down the hall on the left." |
| **Open-ended but bounded** ("What did you think of the movie?" / "How was your weekend?") | **Opinion/experience question** | 2-4 sentences. Share a highlight and an opinion, then reciprocate. |
| **Deep, detailed answer expected** ("Can you explain how the new system works?" / "What's your analysis of the data?") | **Explanation question** | Full, structured response. Check that the asker wants the full version first: "Do you want the quick version or the detailed breakdown?" |
| **"Why" question** ("Why did you choose that approach?") | **Justification question** | Potentially loaded — see Step 4 below. Give your reasoning, but check whether they are genuinely curious or subtly critical. |

---

**STEP 4: Secondary Checks — Tone, Context, and Hidden Agendas**

Even after identifying the question type, run these checks:

**A. Check for presuppositions (hidden assumptions)**:
- "When did you stop making that mistake?" presupposes you were making a mistake.
- "Why don't you ever listen?" presupposes you never listen.
- "Have you finally finished?" presupposes you are slow.
- **Strategy**: If you reject the presupposition, say so explicitly: "I'm not sure I agree with the premise of that question." See Section 5 for full strategies.

**B. Check for face-threatening load**:
- Is answering this question honestly going to hurt someone's feelings or your own standing?
- "Do I look OK in this?" / "Was my presentation good?" → These often seek reassurance, not objective critique.
- See Section 5 for navigation strategies when honesty and social expectations conflict.

**C. Check the social power dynamic**:
- Questions from a boss, teacher, doctor, or authority figure carry different weight than questions from a peer.
- "How's the project going?" from your boss = status report expected. From a coworker = casual small talk possible.
- "Do you understand?" from a teacher = check that you can demonstrate understanding, not just say "yes."

**D. Check for multiple embedded questions**:
- "What happened with the client and when can we fix it?" contains two questions. Answer both.
- "Tell me about your experience with Python — can you give some examples?" = provide overview + specific examples.

---

### Quick-Reference: The Five-Second Classification

When a question comes at you and you need to classify it fast, run these checks in order:

1. **GREETING?** → Is this a "How are you?" variant? → Respond with formulaic positive response.
2. **REQUEST?** → Could I do something about what they're asking? → Do the thing (or politely decline).
3. **RHETORICAL?** → Are they continuing to talk without waiting for my answer? → Don't answer literally.
4. **LOADED?** → Does the question contain a hidden assumption I disagree with? → Challenge the premise.
5. **GENUINE?** → None of the above? → Provide the information they are asking for.

The "Gap" column identifies where autistic processing (which defaults to literal interpretation) may diverge from neurotypical social expectations. Understanding the gap is not about "fixing" autistic communication — it is about having the information to choose your response consciously.

| 2 | **Phatic opener** | "What's up?" | "Not much! How about you?" | Literal: "The ceiling" or a detailed list of current activities | "What's up?" = "Hello, I acknowledge you." It is not a request for spatial or situational data. |

| 11 | **Tag question (uncertainty)** | "The meeting is at 2, isn't it?" (rising intonation) | Confirm or correct: "Yes, 2 o'clock" or "Actually, it got moved to 2:30" | Same as expected (this one is straightforward) | Minimal gap — this is a genuine information request. |
| 12 | **Leading question** | "Don't you think we should go?" | Agree: "Yeah, probably" (or soften disagreement) | "No, I want to stay." (direct disagreement) | The question form telegraphs the preferred answer. Direct contradiction feels confrontational. |

| 14 | **Offer** | "Would you like some water?" | "Yes, please" or "No, thank you" | Same as expected (usually straightforward) | Minimal gap. |
| 15 | **Reassurance-seeking** | "Do I look OK?" | "You look great!" (supportive) | Honest assessment: "Your shirt is wrinkled and your hair is messy." | The question seeks comfort, not a clothing inspection. Find something genuine and positive to say. |
| 16 | **Interview: "Tell me about yourself"** | "Tell me about yourself." | 60-90 second professional narrative (Present-Past-Future) | Full autobiography starting from childhood, or "What do you want to know?" | This is not a biographical question. It means: "Give me a relevant professional summary." See Section 4. |
| 17 | **Interview: weakness** | "What's your biggest weakness?" | A real weakness with a growth narrative | Brutally honest: "I have poor time management" (with no mitigation) | The question tests self-awareness + recovery, not confession. Always add what you're doing about it. |
| 18 | **Medical: pain scale** | "On a scale of 1-10, how much does it hurt?" | Number with brief qualifier: "About a 6 — a dull ache that gets worse when I move" | Difficulty mapping subjective experience to a number; long pause; asks for definitions of the scale | Prepare your own anchor points in advance. See Section 4. |
| 19 | **Status update (boss)** | "How's the project going?" | Brief progress summary with key issues flagged | Extremely detailed technical report OR phatic "Fine" | Match the context: boss wants a 2-3 sentence update, not a comprehensive report or a one-word dismissal. |
| 20 | **Conversation maintenance** | "What happened next?" | Continue the story — the listener is showing interest | May restart the story from the beginning, or interpret as a demand for chronological precision | This question means "I'm engaged — keep going." Continue from where you were. |
| 21 | **Deflection/avoidance** | "What do you do for fun?" | Name 1-2 accessible hobbies; reciprocate | Deep dive into a special interest with no reciprocation | The social function is mutual exchange. Share briefly, then ask them back. |
| 22 | **Clarification request** | "Sorry, what did you say?" | Repeat or rephrase your previous statement | May feel accused of speaking unclearly; may become anxious | This is a neutral repair mechanism, not criticism. Simply restate. |
| 23 | **"Why?" as accusation** | "Why would you do that?" | Justify your reasoning (softened) | Detailed, literal explanation that may come across as defensive | Acknowledge their concern first, then explain: "I can see why that seems odd — my thinking was..." |
| 24 | **Social reciprocity question** | "How was your vacation?" | 2-3 highlights, then "How about you? Done anything fun lately?" | Detailed day-by-day itinerary without reciprocating | The exchange is 2-3 sentences + return the conversational ball. |
| 25 | **Pre-request** | "Are you busy right now?" | "Not especially, what's up?" (recognizing a request is coming) | "Yes/No." (answering only the literal question) | "Are you busy?" is almost always a pre-sequence for a request. They want to know if you're available for something. Signal openness: "What do you need?" |

## 3. QUESTIONS AS CONVERSATIONAL TOOLS

Questions are not just things that happen to you — they are powerful tools you can deploy strategically. For autistic individuals who find unscripted social interaction demanding, having a repertoire of questions-as-tools provides a way to **structure** conversations rather than simply react to them.

### 3.1 Questions to Show Interest

**Function**: Signal that you are engaged, that you care about the other person's experience, and that you want to hear more. These questions keep the conversational burden on the other person (useful when you are not sure what to say).

| Question | When to use | Effect |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| "What was that like?" | After someone describes an experience | Shows empathy; invites them to share feelings, not just facts |
| "How did that go?" | After someone mentions an event or project | General interest signal; lets them choose what to share |
| "How did that make you feel?" | After someone describes something emotional | Deep engagement signal; use with people you know well |
| "What did you think of it?" | After someone mentions a movie, book, restaurant, etc. | Invites their opinion; signals you value their perspective |
| "Really? Tell me more." | Any time (technically a request, not a question) | Universal engagement signal; works in almost any context |

**Example conversation flow**:
> Them: "We just got back from Iceland."
> You: "Oh wow, what was that like?"
> Them: "It was incredible — the landscapes are unreal."
> You: "What was the highlight?"
> Them: "Probably the Northern Lights. We drove out to the middle of nowhere..."
> You: "How did that feel, seeing them in person?"

Notice: You have said almost nothing about yourself, but you are perceived as an excellent conversationalist because you are showing genuine interest. Research consistently shows that people rate conversation partners who ask follow-up questions more favorably (Huang et al., 2017, *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*).

### 3.2 Questions to Maintain a Topic

**Function**: Keep the conversation going on the current subject when you sense there is more to say.

| Question | When to use | Effect |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| "What happened next?" | Mid-story, any narrative | Signals engagement; prompts continuation |
| "How did they react?" | After someone describes an interaction | Keeps the story going; shows interest in social dynamics |
| "Then what?" | Casual version of "What happened next?" | Informal engagement marker |
| "How did it end up?" / "How did it turn out?" | When the outcome is still unclear | Asks for resolution; signals you are following the story |
| "Wait, back up — what did you mean by [X]?" | When you want to understand a detail better | Shows careful listening; appropriate interruption |

**Key principle**: In conversation analysis, these are called "continuer" signals. They tell the speaker: "I have allocated you the conversational floor. Keep going. I am listening."

### 3.3 Questions to Change a Topic

**Function**: Shift the conversation to a new subject when the current one has run its course, or when you need to exit an uncomfortable topic.

| Question | When to use | Effect |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| "That reminds me — have you heard about...?" | When the current topic has a natural connection to a new one | Smooth transition; the connection justifies the shift |
| "Oh, speaking of [related thing], did you know...?" | Leverages a keyword from the current topic | Feels natural, not abrupt |
| "Hey, I've been meaning to ask you — [new topic]?" | When you need to change direction completely | Signals a deliberate but friendly shift |
| "By the way, how did [previous thing you discussed] go?" | Bringing back a previous topic | Shows you remember past conversations (strong social signal) |
| "So, completely different question — [new topic]?" | When you need a clean break | Transparent about the shift, which is actually fine |

**Timing**: Topic changes work best when there is a natural pause or lull. Interrupting someone mid-sentence to change topics is perceived as rude. Wait for a completion point.

**Autistic advantage**: If you notice a conversation has entered a loop or a dead zone (people repeating themselves, energy dropping), introducing a new topic with a question is a socially valued skill.

### 3.4 Questions to Check Understanding

**Function**: Verify that you have correctly understood what someone has said or meant. This is especially valuable for autistic communicators who may process pragmatic meaning more deliberately.

| Question | When to use | Effect |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| "So you're saying [paraphrase]?" | After complex or ambiguous input | Confirms your interpretation; shows careful listening |
| "Do you mean [interpretation A] or [interpretation B]?" | When genuinely ambiguous | Gives them specific options to confirm/deny |
| "Just to make sure I understand — [summary]?" | Professional/workplace setting | Perceived as thorough and professional, not incompetent |
| "When you say [X], do you mean [literal meaning] or [figurative meaning]?" | When you suspect non-literal language | Explicitly disambiguates without revealing confusion |
| "Can you give me an example of what you mean?" | When an abstract statement is unclear | Grounds their statement in concrete terms (an autistic-friendly strategy) |

**Critical insight**: Asking for clarification is NOT a sign of incompetence. Research on professional communication consistently shows that people who ask clarifying questions are perceived as more careful, more engaged, and more competent than those who proceed based on assumptions. The key is framing: "Just to make sure I've got this right..." positions you as thorough, not confused.

### 3.5 Questions to Invite Participation

**Function**: Bring someone into a conversation, particularly useful in group settings.

| Question | When to use | Effect |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| "What do you think, [name]?" | Someone has been quiet in a group | Inclusive; shows awareness of the group dynamic |
| "[Name], you know about this — what's your take?" | When someone has relevant expertise | Flattering; positions them as valuable |
| "Has anyone else experienced something like that?" | After sharing an experience | Opens the floor; invites shared experience |
| "What's your perspective on this, [name]?" | More formal group setting | Professional invitation to contribute |

### 3.6 Questions to Soften a Statement

**Function**: Express your own opinion in a way that feels less confrontational by wrapping it in question form.

| Question | What it really means | When to use |
|----------|---------------------|-------------|
| "Don't you think that's a bit risky?" | "I think that's risky." | When you want to express concern without sounding alarmist |
| "Wouldn't it be better to...?" | "I think we should..." | When proposing an alternative to someone else's plan |
| "Isn't there a simpler way to do this?" | "I think this approach is overcomplicated." | When critiquing someone's approach |
| "Could it be that...?" | "I believe that..." | When introducing a hypothesis you support |
| "Have you considered...?" | "You should think about..." | When suggesting something they may have missed |

**Why this works**: The question form preserves the listener's autonomy. Instead of telling them what to think, you are inviting them to arrive at the conclusion themselves. This is a cornerstone of Face Theory (Brown & Levinson, 1987) — protecting the listener's "negative face" (their desire not to be imposed upon).

**Autistic note**: This kind of indirectness may feel dishonest. You ARE expressing your opinion — you're just wrapping it. Think of it less as deception and more as social packaging, like wrapping a gift. The content doesn't change; the presentation shows consideration.

### 3.7 Questions to Express Disagreement Indirectly

**Function**: Signal that you disagree with something without the social friction of a direct "no" or "you're wrong."

| Question | What it really means | Level of directness |
|----------|---------------------|-------------------|
| "But what about [counterexample]?" | "Your argument has a flaw." | Medium — introduces evidence against their position |
| "Have you considered [alternative]?" | "I think there's a better option." | Soft — positions it as something they might explore |
| "How does that account for [problem]?" | "Your explanation doesn't cover this." | Medium-firm — challenges completeness |
| "I see what you mean, but wouldn't [objection]?" | "I partially disagree." | Softer — acknowledges their point before diverging |
| "Is that always the case?" | "I think there are exceptions / you're overgeneralizing." | Soft — questions universality |
| "What if [alternative scenario]?" | "I think there are other possibilities." | Soft — introduces alternatives without direct opposition |
| "I wonder if that's the whole picture?" | "I think you're missing something." | Medium — suggests incompleteness diplomatically |

**Escalation ladder**: If you disagree, start soft and increase directness only as needed:
1. "Hmm, interesting. I wonder if..." (gentlest)
2. "That's a good point. I'm not sure about [X] though..."
3. "I see where you're coming from, but what about...?"
4. "I actually see it differently — my thinking is..."
5. "I have to respectfully disagree because..." (most direct while remaining polite)

## 4. COMMON QUESTION PATTERNS IN SPECIFIC CONTEXTS

### 4.1 JOB INTERVIEWS

Job interviews are a minefield of pragmatic complexity. Almost every question has a surface meaning and a deeper evaluative purpose. Understanding what the interviewer is *actually* assessing — what they are "listening for" — is critical.

**General principle**: Interviewers are not making conversation. Every question is a **data-collection instrument** for evaluating specific competencies. Your job is to figure out which competency they are probing and provide evidence for it.

**Accommodation note**: Under the ADA, autistic job candidates can request reasonable accommodations such as receiving interview questions in advance, a quiet interview room, a smaller interview panel, or phone/video interview as a first round (Job Accommodation Network; Hire Autism). You do not need to disclose your diagnosis to request accommodations, and research suggests autistic candidates may be rated more favorably when they do disclose.

---

**"Tell me about yourself."**

- **Surface**: Open-ended biographical question.
- **Hidden meaning**: "Give me a relevant professional narrative that shows you're a good fit for this role."
- **NOT asking for**: Your childhood, personal life, hobbies, or full work history.
- **Structure (Present-Past-Future formula)**:
  1. **Present**: "I'm currently [role] at [company], where I focus on [1-2 key responsibilities relevant to this job]."
  2. **Past**: "Before that, I [1-2 previous relevant roles or achievements]."
  3. **Future**: "I'm excited about this opportunity because [what draws you to the role/company]."
- **Length**: 60-90 seconds. Maximum 2 minutes.
- **Example**: "I'm currently a data analyst at Acme Corp, where I build dashboards and automate reporting pipelines. Before that, I spent three years at a startup where I wore a lot of hats — everything from SQL queries to presenting findings to the board. I'm looking for a role where I can go deeper on the technical side, which is why this position caught my eye."

---

**"What's your biggest weakness?"**

- **Surface**: Identify a flaw.
- **Hidden meaning**: "Show me you have self-awareness AND a growth mindset."
- **The trap**: Answering with a real weakness AND no mitigation sounds like a confession. Answering with a fake weakness ("I'm too much of a perfectionist!") sounds rehearsed and insincere.
- **Formula**: Real weakness (specific) + Awareness (how you recognized it) + Action (what you're doing about it)
- **Example**: "I sometimes get so focused on getting the details right that I lose track of the bigger deadline. I've learned to set explicit checkpoint times for myself — like, at 2 PM, stop what you're doing and assess whether you're on track for the day's priorities."
- **Autistic note**: Resist the urge to be comprehensively honest. This is not a therapy session; it is a performance of self-awareness. Pick ONE weakness and show you are managing it.

---

**"Where do you see yourself in 5 years?"**

- **Surface**: Career planning question.
- **Hidden meaning**: "Are you going to stay long enough to be worth training? Are your ambitions compatible with our organization?"
- **What NOT to say**: "I don't know" (signals no ambition) / "In your job" (sounds threatening) / "Somewhere else" (signals you'll leave).
- **Formula**: Growth within their ecosystem + skill development
- **Example**: "I'd like to have grown into a senior role where I'm mentoring others and leading projects end-to-end. I'm particularly interested in developing expertise in [area relevant to the company]."

---

**"Why should we hire you?"**

- **Surface**: Self-advocacy question.
- **Hidden meaning**: "Convince me. Match your skills to our needs."
- **Structure**: Reference the job requirements, then map your experience to each.
- **Example**: "You're looking for someone who can handle complex data pipelines and communicate findings to non-technical stakeholders. In my current role, I do both — I built our entire ETL process and I present quarterly metrics to the executive team."

---

**Behavioral questions: "Tell me about a time when..."**

- **Hidden meaning**: "Give me concrete evidence of past behavior as a predictor of future performance."
- **Structure (STAR method)**:
  - **S**ituation: Set the scene briefly (1-2 sentences)
  - **T**ask: What was your responsibility? (1 sentence)
  - **A**ction: What did you specifically do? (2-4 sentences — this is the meat)
  - **R**esult: What happened? Quantify if possible. (1-2 sentences)
- **Common behavioral questions and what they really test**:
  - "Tell me about a time you resolved a conflict" → Tests interpersonal skills, emotional regulation
  - "Describe a time you failed" → Tests resilience, learning from mistakes, self-awareness
  - "Tell me about a time you worked on a team" → Tests collaboration, flexibility
  - "Give me an example of when you had to deal with ambiguity" → Tests comfort with uncertainty, initiative

### 4.2 MEDICAL APPOINTMENTS

Medical appointments involve specific question patterns that can be challenging for autistic patients who process subjective experience differently.

**Preparation strategy**: Before the appointment, write down your answers to predictable questions. Bring notes. This is a legitimate and widely recommended practice that healthcare professionals appreciate.

---

**"Where does it hurt?"**

- **Expected**: Point to the location. Describe the type of pain (sharp, dull, throbbing, burning, aching, stabbing, pressure).
- **Autistic challenge**: Interoception differences may make it hard to localize pain precisely. Alexithymia may make it difficult to categorize the sensation.
- **Strategy**: Prepare descriptors in advance. Use analogies: "It feels like someone pressing on it" / "It feels like a rubber band snapping." If you can't localize: "It's somewhere in this general area" [gesture] is acceptable.

---

**"On a scale of 1-10, how much does it hurt?"**

- **Expected**: A number with a brief qualifier.
- **Autistic challenge**: The scale feels arbitrary. What is a 7? Does it account for pain tolerance? Should I be objective or subjective?
- **Strategy**: Create your own anchor scale in advance:
  - 1-2: Noticeable but doesn't affect activities
  - 3-4: Annoying; distracting but manageable
  - 5-6: Significantly affects concentration or movement
  - 7-8: Hard to do anything else; want it to stop immediately
  - 9-10: Worst pain you've ever experienced; unable to function
- **When you can't decide**: "It's between a 5 and a 7 — it depends on whether I'm sitting or moving. Sitting is maybe a 4, but when I stand up it jumps to a 7."

---

**"When did it start?"**

- **Expected**: Approximate timeline. "About three weeks ago" / "Since Monday" / "It's been coming and going for six months."
- **Strategy**: Check your calendar or phone before the appointment to pin down dates. "I think it started around March 20th" is more useful to a doctor than "A while ago."

---

**Sensitive topic questions** ("Do you drink alcohol?" / "Are you sexually active?" / "Do you use recreational drugs?"):
- **Expected**: Honest answers. These are clinical questions, not moral judgments. Doctors need accurate information for safe treatment.
- **Autistic advantage**: Your tendency toward literal honesty is actually *exactly what doctors need*. Medical contexts are one place where directness is valued over social packaging.
- **Key phrase**: "Is this confidential?" (Yes, with specific legal exceptions — and you can ask what those are.)

---

**How to ask your own questions**:
- Prepare a written list before the appointment.
- If the doctor is rushing, say: "I have a few questions — could we go through them?"
- If you don't understand an explanation: "Could you explain that in simpler terms?" or "Can you draw me a diagram?"
- If you need time to process: "Can I take a moment to think about that?"
- Ask for written summaries: "Could you write down the key points for me to take home?"
- After a diagnosis or treatment plan: "What would happen if I don't do this?" / "Are there alternatives?" / "What are the side effects?"

---

### 4.3 SOCIAL SMALL TALK

Small talk questions follow predictable patterns. Learning these patterns gives you a script library to draw from.

---

**"What do you do?"** (= What is your job/occupation?)

- **Expected**: Job title + 1 sentence about what that involves. "I'm a software developer — I build web applications for a healthcare company."
- **NOT expected**: A detailed description of your daily tasks, or a philosophical meditation on the nature of work.
- **If you don't have a traditional job**: "I'm studying [X] at [university]" / "I'm between jobs right now — I've been doing some freelance [X]" / "I'm a stay-at-home parent" — then redirect: "How about you?"
- **Reciprocation is mandatory**: After answering, ask them the same question back, OR ask a follow-up about their answer.

---

**"Where are you from?"**

- **Expected**: City/state/country. Brief. "I'm from Portland, Oregon" or "I grew up in Texas but I've been here about five years."
- **Follow-up to keep it going**: "How about you?" or "How long have you been in [current location]?"
- **If the question feels loaded** (asked of someone who looks "foreign"): This is a complex social situation. You can answer at the level you're comfortable with: "I'm from Chicago" (where you live now) or "Originally from Korea, but I've been in the US since I was ten" (more detail). You are not obligated to share your full background.

---

**"Do you have kids?"**

- **Expected**: Yes/no + brief detail. "Yeah, two — they're 8 and 12" or "No, not yet" or "No, it's just me and my dog."
- **Sensitive terrain**: This can be painful for people dealing with infertility, loss, or who are child-free by choice. If YOU are asked and don't want to elaborate: "Not at this point — how about you?" smoothly redirects.

---

**Questions to ask others** (to show interest and keep conversation flowing):
- "How long have you been doing [their job]?"
- "What do you enjoy most about it?"
- "Have you been to [event/place] before?"
- "What's kept you busy lately?"
- "Have you tried [the food/drinks] here? Any recommendations?"
- "Did you do anything fun this weekend?"

**The 2-sentence-then-ask-back rule**: In small talk, share approximately 2 sentences about yourself, then redirect with a question. This creates natural back-and-forth rhythm and prevents monologuing.

---

### 4.4 WORKPLACE

**Asking for clarification without seeming incompetent**:

The framing matters more than the question itself. Research confirms that asking clarifying questions is perceived as a hallmark of effective professional communication (Harvard DCE, 2023).

**Frame as thoroughness, not confusion**:
- Instead of: "I don't understand what you want." → "Just to make sure I deliver exactly what you need — could you clarify [specific point]?"
- Instead of: "What do you mean?" → "I want to make sure I'm on the same page — when you say [X], do you mean [interpretation A] or [interpretation B]?"
- Instead of: "I'm confused." → "Could you walk me through the expected output? I want to make sure I'm aligned with your vision."

**After a meeting**: "I'm going to send you a quick summary of what I took away as my action items — could you let me know if I've missed anything?" (This is EXCELLENT professional practice and gives you a written record.)

---

**Asking for help without seeming needy**:

- Frame as collaboration, not dependence: "Could I get your input on [X]? I've got a first pass done but I'd value a second pair of eyes."
- Reference their expertise: "You're the expert on [X] — could I pick your brain for five minutes?"
- Offer reciprocity: "I could use some help with [X] — happy to return the favor when you need it."
- Time-bound the request: "Could I get 10 minutes of your time this afternoon?" (Makes it feel smaller and more manageable.)

---

**"How's the project going?" — Status update vs. phatic**:

This question is context-dependent and can be genuinely confusing:

| Context | Likely Meaning | Expected Response |
|---------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Boss asks in a 1-on-1 meeting** | Status update request | 2-3 sentence summary: progress, blockers, next steps |
| **Boss asks while passing in hallway** | Phatic check-in | Brief positive: "Going well — on track for Friday" |
| **Coworker asks at lunch** | Casual conversation | Brief: "Pretty good — finally got the API working" |
| **Stakeholder asks in a meeting** | Formal status request | Structured update with metrics if available |

**When unsure, ask**: "Do you want the quick version or the detailed update?" This is a socially intelligent move that prevents both under-answering and over-answering.

## 5. HANDLING DIFFICULT QUESTIONS

### 5.1 Questions You Don't Want to Answer

**Deflection strategies** (ranked from softest to firmest):

**Level 1: Redirect**
- "That's a long story — but tell me about [their thing]!"
- "Oh, it's complicated — what about you, though?"

**Level 2: Give a partial answer**
- "What's your salary?" → "I'm comfortable with where I am" or "It's competitive for the industry."
- "Why are you single?" → "Just haven't found the right person yet — these things take time."

**Level 3: Use humor**
- "How much money do you make?" → "Not enough!" [laugh]
- "Why don't you have kids?" → "My houseplants are enough responsibility for now."

**Level 4: Name the boundary**
- "I appreciate the question, but that's something I prefer to keep private."
- "I'd rather not get into that — hope you understand."
- "That's a bit personal for me — can we talk about [other topic] instead?"

**Level 5: Direct refusal**
- "I'm not comfortable answering that."
- "That's not something I discuss."

**Autistic note**: Many autistic people feel compelled to answer every question asked of them. You are NOT obligated to answer. Silence, a redirect, or a polite refusal are all socially acceptable responses to intrusive questions. It may feel rude to you, but it will be perceived as setting healthy boundaries.

### 5.2 Questions You Don't Understand

**Clarification strategies** (in order of social cost, from lowest to highest):

1. **Echo the question with rising intonation**: "My biggest weakness...?" — This buys time and often prompts the asker to rephrase.

2. **Ask for specificity**: "Could you be more specific about what aspect you're interested in?" / "In what sense?"

3. **Offer your interpretation**: "Do you mean [X] or [Y]?" — This shows you are engaging, not just lost.

4. **Ask for an example**: "Could you give me an example of what you mean?"

5. **Paraphrase and check**: "So you're asking about [your interpretation]? Is that right?"

6. **Transparent admission**: "I want to give you a good answer — could you rephrase that?" or "I'm not sure I'm understanding the question correctly."

**Key research finding**: In conversation analysis, clarification requests are classified as "repair sequences" — they are a natural, normal part of conversation, not a failure. All speakers need repair sometimes. Repair mechanisms are categorized by who initiates the fix and who resolves it, and "other-initiated self-repair" (where you signal confusion and they rephrase) is the most socially comfortable type (Schegloff et al., 1977).

### 5.3 Questions with Hidden Presuppositions

Some questions contain built-in assumptions that, if you answer the question as asked, you implicitly accept the assumption.

**Classic examples and how to challenge them**:

| Loaded Question | Hidden Presupposition | Challenge Strategy |
|----------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| "When did you stop making that mistake?" | You were making a mistake | "I'm not sure I'd characterize it as a mistake — can you tell me what you're referring to?" |
| "Why are you always late?" | You are always late | "I don't think that's accurate — I was late today, but I'd like to understand the concern." |
| "Have you finally finished?" | You took too long | "I finished on schedule — was there a concern about timing?" |
| "Why don't you ever listen?" | You never listen | "I hear what you're saying. I may have missed something specific — what did I miss?" |
| "Don't you care about [X]?" | You don't care about X | "I do care about [X] — what gives you the impression I don't?" |
| "What's wrong with you?" | Something is wrong with you | "Nothing is wrong with me. I'm doing [X] because [reason]." |

**General formula for challenging presuppositions**:
1. **Identify** the hidden assumption
2. **Reject** it explicitly but calmly
3. **Redirect** to the real issue
4. "I don't accept the premise of that question. The real issue is [X]."

### 5.4 Questions Where the "Right" Answer Isn't the Honest Answer

This is one of the hardest areas for autistic communicators who value honesty. Some questions operate in a social space where the expected answer prioritizes relationship maintenance over factual accuracy.

**Category A: Reassurance-seeking questions**

| Question | What they want | Navigation strategy |
|----------|---------------|-------------------|
| "Do I look OK?" | Reassurance | Find something genuinely positive to highlight: "That color really suits you" |
| "Was my presentation good?" | Encouragement | Lead with strengths, then offer constructive feedback IF asked: "Your opening was really strong — the data visualization really landed" |
| "Am I overreacting?" | Validation | Validate the emotion, even if you think the reaction is disproportionate: "Your feelings make sense given the situation" |
| "Do you like what I made?" | Appreciation | Find a genuine positive: "I can see how much work you put into this" |

**The strategy is not to lie — it is to select which truths to emphasize.** "That color suits you" is true even if their hair is messy. You do not need to provide a comprehensive audit when a targeted compliment will serve the social function.

**Category B: Social contract questions**

| Question | Expected answer | What you want to say | Navigation |
|----------|----------------|---------------------|------------|
| "Can I be honest with you?" | "Of course" | "That depends" | Say "Of course" — they are asking for permission, and saying anything else is perceived as hostile |
| "You don't mind, do you?" | "No, not at all" | "Actually, yes" | If you DO mind, say so gently: "Actually, I'd prefer [alternative]" — but know that the question format pushes toward "no" |
| "Are you OK?" (when visibly upset) | "I'm fine" OR honest response | Depends | Both are acceptable. "I'm fine" closes the topic. Honest response invites support. Choose based on what you need. |

**Category C: The honest-but-diplomatic option**

You don't have to choose between brutal honesty and social lying. There is a middle path:

- **Sandwich method**: Positive + constructive + positive. "Your argument is really well-structured. I think the evidence in section 3 could be stronger. But overall, it's very persuasive."
- **"Yes, and" method**: Accept their framing, then add nuance. "Yes, it looks great. The one thing I'd change is [specific adjustment]."
- **Timing method**: "That's really good. Can I share one small thought when you have a moment?" (Separates the praise from the critique; gives them a chance to opt into feedback.)

### 5.5 "Why?" Questions That Feel Like Accusations

"Why" questions are particularly fraught because they request a reason, and the act of requesting a reason implies that one is needed — which implies that the action was questionable. Research in communication studies confirms that "why" questions trigger the amygdala's threat-detection response, activating fight-flight-freeze patterns because they can feel like blame or judgment (Wellness Counseling, 2023).

**Reframing strategies**:

| Accusatory "Why" | What they really mean | Reframing response |
|------------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| "Why did you do that?" | I disagree with your decision | "My thinking was [reasoning]. I can see how it might look different from your perspective — what's your concern?" |
| "Why didn't you tell me?" | I feel left out or disrespected | "I should have kept you in the loop. I was focused on [X] and didn't think about the communication side. I'll make sure to loop you in next time." |
| "Why can't you just [do the thing]?" | I'm frustrated that you're not doing it | "I understand the frustration. The challenge is [specific obstacle]. Here's what I'm doing about it: [plan]." |
| "Why would you think that?" | Your reasoning seems wrong to me | "I based it on [evidence]. What am I missing?" (turns it into a collaborative problem-solving question) |
| "Why are you being so difficult?" | I perceive you as uncooperative | "I'm not trying to be difficult. I have concerns about [X]. Can we work through them?" |

**The formula**: Acknowledge their underlying emotion → Explain your reasoning briefly → Redirect toward a solution or collaboration.

**Autistic note**: If someone asks "Why?" and you feel your body tense up, take a breath before responding. The defensiveness you feel is normal — it is your brain's threat response to what feels like an accusation. The reframing strategy gives you a moment to separate the emotional response from the informational response.

## 6. THE QUESTION-ANSWER TIMING ISSUE

### 6.1 How Long Is an Acceptable Pause Before Answering?

Research from conversation analysis provides precise data on this:

**The universal norm**: Across languages and cultures, the average gap between one person stopping and the next starting is approximately **200 milliseconds** — about the length of a single syllable (Stivers et al., 2009, *PNAS*). The median inter-speaker gap in English, Dutch, and Swedish ranges from 110-130 ms, with the most common interval around 200 ms (Roberts et al., 2015).

**What this means**: The conversational system is *extremely* fast. Listeners begin planning their response before the speaker has even finished — which is why some responses can come within 200 ms of the question ending, far too fast for someone to hear the entire question and then formulate an answer from scratch. Neurotypical speakers are essentially predicting the end of the question and pre-loading their response.

**For autistic communicators**: If your processing style involves fully hearing the question, analyzing its pragmatic function, and then formulating a response, you are operating on a different timeline than the one the conversational system was designed for. This is not a deficit — it is a different processing architecture. But it is useful to know what the normative timeline IS so you can strategically manage the gap.

### 6.2 When a Delayed Answer Signals a Dispreferred Response

This is a critical finding from conversation analysis and neurolinguistic research:

**The key discovery**: Listeners automatically interpret a delay before answering as a signal that a dispreferred (negative, rejecting, disagreeing) response is coming. Research by Bögels et al. (2015, *PLOS ONE*) found that:

- A "yes" delivered at 300 ms after a question is perceived as natural and expected.
- A "no" delivered at 300 ms evokes a surprise response (N400 brain wave) — because at short delays, listeners expect positive/agreeing responses.
- A "no" delivered at 1000 ms (1 second) does NOT evoke a surprise response — because by that point, the delay itself has already signaled that a negative response is coming.
- A delay of 1.7 seconds causes the questioner to *revise their question* to accommodate an expected negative response (e.g., "Or would you prefer not to?").

**In practical terms**:
| Response timing | What listeners interpret |
|----------------|------------------------|
| **< 500 ms** | Natural, expected response; probably positive/agreeing |
| **500 ms - 1 second** | Slight hesitation; might be neutral or mildly dispreferred |
| **1 - 2 seconds** | Significant hesitation; listeners begin expecting a "no," a disagreement, or a problem |
| **2 - 3 seconds** | Strong signal of dispreference; the asker may start to revise or prompt |
| **> 3 seconds** | Uncomfortable silence in most social contexts; the asker will likely repeat, rephrase, or assume you didn't hear |

**The autistic timing problem**: If you take 2-3 seconds to process a question (which is a normal processing time for deliberate pragmatic reasoning), listeners may interpret your pause as reluctance or disagreement — even if you are about to agree or give a neutral answer. Your processing delay gets "read" as a social signal it was never intended to be.

### 6.3 How to Buy Time

When you need extra processing time, use these phrases to signal "I'm thinking, not refusing":

**Tier 1: Universal time-buyers (appropriate in any context)**
- "Hmm, let me think about that for a second..."
- "That's a good question..." (widely used, buys 2-3 seconds)
- "Let me think..." (simple, direct, no social cost)

**Tier 2: Professional/formal time-buyers**
- "That's an interesting question — let me consider it for a moment."
- "I want to give you a thoughtful answer..." (signals you are taking the question seriously)
- "There are a few ways to look at this..." (signals a complex answer is coming)
- "Let me gather my thoughts on that..." (transparent and professional)

**Tier 3: Casual/social time-buyers**
- "Oh man, where do I start..." (signals a big story is coming)
- "Hmm, that's a tough one..." (acknowledges difficulty)
- "You know, I've been thinking about that..." (even if you haven't — this gives you time)

**Tier 4: Strategic non-answers that buy major time**
- "Can you say more about what you mean by [X]?" → Turns the question back to them; buys 10-30 seconds
- "Before I answer that, can I ask [clarifying question]?" → Legitimate and buys time
- "I'd like to think about that and get back to you." → Acceptable in professional settings; buys hours/days

**Critical strategy for processing time**: If someone asks you a question and you know you need more than 3 seconds, **say "hmm" or "let me think" immediately**. The verbal acknowledgment within the first second signals that you heard the question and are processing. Without it, the silence feels empty and creates social pressure.

### 6.4 When It's OK to Say "I Don't Know"

"I don't know" is a complete, valid, and often respected answer. The key is context and delivery:

**When "I don't know" is perfectly fine**:
- Factual questions where you genuinely lack information: "What's the company's revenue?" → "I don't know offhand, but I can find out."
- Opinion questions where you haven't formed an opinion: "What do you think of the new policy?" → "Honestly, I haven't had a chance to form an opinion yet."
- Complex questions that need research: "What's the best approach for this?" → "I don't know yet — let me look into it and get back to you."

**When "I don't know" needs a qualifier**:
- In job interviews: "I don't know" alone sounds unprepared. Add: "I'm not sure about the specifics, but here's how I'd approach finding out..."
- When someone asks for help: "I don't know, but [person] might — want me to connect you?"
- When your boss asks: "I don't know yet, but I'll have an answer by [time]."

**When "I don't know" is the wrong response**:
- Phatic questions: "How are you?" → "I don't know" sounds concerning. Say "Good, thanks!"
- Questions that are actually requests: "Do you know where the bathroom is?" → "I don't know" is technically responsive but unhelpful. If you do know, say where it is. If you genuinely don't: "Sorry, I'm not sure — you could ask at the front desk."
- Rhetorical questions: "What are we going to do?" (said in exasperation) → "I don't know" takes the rhetorical question literally. A better response: reassurance, silence, or suggestion.

## APPENDIX: QUICK-REFERENCE CARDS

### Card 1: The Five-Second Classification
```
INCOMING QUESTION → 
  Is it a greeting? → Formulaic positive response
  Could I DO something? → Do the thing (it's a request)
  Are they still talking? → It's rhetorical (stay quiet)
  Does it assume something false? → Challenge the premise
  None of the above → Answer the actual question
```

### Card 2: Response Timing Cheat Sheet
```
0-1 second:  Normal response window
1-2 seconds: Say "hmm" or "let me think" to fill the gap
2-3 seconds: Listeners expect something negative is coming
3+ seconds:  Awkward — use a time-buyer phrase
```

### Card 3: The Reciprocation Rule
```
Someone asks you a question → 
  1. Answer (2-3 sentences max for small talk)
  2. Ask them the same or related question back
  3. Show interest in their answer
```

### Card 4: Difficulty Escalation
```
Don't want to answer → Redirect → Partial answer → Humor → Name boundary → Direct refusal
Don't understand → Echo question → Ask for specificity → Offer interpretation → Ask for example → Ask for rephrase
Disagree → "I wonder if..." → "That's a good point, but..." → "I see it differently..." → "I respectfully disagree because..."
```

### Card 5: Context-Dependent Responses
```
"How's it going?" from:
  - Stranger/cashier → "Good, thanks!"
  - Colleague in hallway → "Good! Busy week. You?"
  - Close friend → Can be honest: "Actually, kind of rough..."
  - Boss in meeting → Brief professional update
  
"Do you have any questions?" from:
  - Doctor → Yes, ask your prepared list
  - Teacher → Ask if genuinely confused (this is expected)
  - Job interviewer → Always say yes (have 2-3 prepared)
  - Cashier → "No, I'm good, thanks"
```
