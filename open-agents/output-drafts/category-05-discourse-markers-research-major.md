# Major English Discourse Markers: Exhaustive Research for Pragmatics Knowledge Base

## Purpose and Audience

This research document provides exhaustive detail on 12 major English discourse markers, intended for a knowledge base teaching English pragmatics to autistic individuals. Each marker is treated as a standalone system with multiple pragmatic functions, real-world examples, listener-signal analysis, absence effects, common misconceptions, and positional behavior.

---

## 1. "WELL" -- The Most Studied Discourse Marker in English

### Overview

"Well" is arguably the single most studied discourse marker in English linguistics. Schiffrin (1987) classified it as a "marker of response," while Jucker (1993) provided a relevance-theoretic account identifying four core functions. Lakoff (1973) first noted that "well" appears when a speaker "senses some sort of insufficiency in their answer." It is the quintessential signal that what follows will not be a simple, straightforward response.

### Key Academic Sources

- **Schiffrin (1987)**: *Discourse Markers* -- classified "well" as a response marker operating in the participation framework
- **Jucker (1993)**: Relevance-theoretic account identifying four pragmatic functions
- **Lakoff (1973)**: First analysis of "well" as an insufficiency marker
- **Pomerantz (1984)**: Identified "well" as a preface to disagreements in preference organization
- **Svartvik (1980)**: Documented "well" as an editing/self-correction marker

### Pragmatic Function 1: Signaling a Dispreferred Response

**What it does**: "Well" warns the listener that the upcoming answer will not be the simple, expected, or desired response. In conversation analysis terms, it marks a "dispreferred" response -- one that goes against what the question structurally projects.

**What it signals to the listener**: "The answer you're about to get is not the straightforward one you were hoping for." Pomerantz (1984) found that "well" prefaces disagreements, alternating with "yes but" and silence in this environment. A dispreferred response is characteristically delayed through silence and by prefacing the disagreement turn with tokens such as "well," "uhm," and "uh."

**Examples**:
1. Q: "Can you come to the party on Saturday?" A: "Well... I have some things going on that day." (= probably no, but I'm softening the refusal)
2. Q: "Did you like the presentation?" A: "Well, it had some interesting points." (= I didn't really like it, but I'm being polite)
3. Q: "Is the project on schedule?" A: "Well, we've made progress on several fronts." (= no, it's behind, but let me frame this carefully)
4. Q: "Do you think I should take the job?" A: "Well, there are a lot of factors to consider." (= I have reservations but don't want to say no directly)
5. Q: "Are you happy with the results?" A: "Well, they're not exactly what we expected." (= I'm disappointed)

**What happens if it's missing**: Without "well," the same content sounds abrupt and harsh. Compare: "Can you come Saturday?" / "I have some things going on" -- this sounds like a flat rejection rather than a softened one. The listener loses the advance warning that the answer will be complicated. Research on conversation design (Google, 2023) confirms that without discourse markers, responses feel "blunt, disjointed, or even rude."

### Pragmatic Function 2: Buying Processing Time (Delay Device)

**What it does**: "Well" functions as a delay device, giving the speaker time to formulate a response while signaling that a response is coming. Jucker (1993) identified this as a "delay language" function.

**What it signals to the listener**: "I'm thinking about how to answer this. Give me a moment." Unlike "um" or "uh" (which signal cognitive difficulty), "well" signals that the speaker is actively deliberating about *how* to frame their answer, not *whether* they have one.

**Examples**:
1. "Well, let me think about that for a second..." (explicit processing)
2. "Well... the situation is more complicated than it seems." (processing while signaling complexity)
3. Q: "What's the best route to take?" A: "Well, it depends on the time of day." (buying time while also qualifying)
4. Q: "How does this work?" A: "Well, there are a few different ways to explain it." (deciding which explanation to choose)
5. "Well..." [long pause] "...I suppose we could try that approach." (maximum processing time)

**What happens if it's missing**: The speaker either launches directly into a response (which may sound overconfident or dismissive of the question's complexity) or produces a silent pause (which can be interpreted as not knowing the answer at all).

### Pragmatic Function 3: Softening Disagreement (Face-Threat Mitigator)

**What it does**: "Well" mitigates face-threatening acts. When someone is about to disagree, correct, or refuse, "well" acts as a buffer that preserves the social relationship. Jucker (1993) classified this as the "face-threat mitigator" function. The discourse marker indicates "denials, refusals, and objections to a given utterance."

**What it signals to the listener**: "I'm about to say something you may not want to hear, but I'm doing my best to be respectful about it."

**Examples**:
1. "Well, I see your point, but I think there's another way to look at it." (disagreeing gently)
2. "Well, that's one interpretation, certainly." (implying it's not the correct one)
3. "Well, I wouldn't say it's *wrong* exactly..." (hedged disagreement)
4. "Well, in my experience, it usually works differently." (polite correction)
5. "Well, I appreciate the effort, but we might need to go in a different direction." (softened rejection)

**What happens if it's missing**: "I see your point, but I think there's another way to look at it" is noticeably more confrontational without the "well." The sentence goes from diplomatic disagreement to direct challenge. Research confirms the utterance would be judged as "impolite," "unfriendly," or "dogmatic" within the communicative context.

### Pragmatic Function 4: Introducing Self-Correction (Editing Marker)

**What it does**: Svartvik (1980) and Schiffrin (1987) documented "well" as an editing marker. When speakers realize they've said something incorrect or imprecise, "well" precedes the correction.

**What it signals to the listener**: "I just realized what I said wasn't quite right, and I'm about to fix it."

**Examples**:
1. "I found a house I'd like to buy. Well, it's an apartment actually." (self-correction of category)
2. "We finished the project on Tuesday. Well, Monday evening technically." (precision correction)
3. "She's from France. Well, French-speaking Switzerland, to be precise." (narrowing correction)
4. "I think there were about fifty people there. Well, maybe more like thirty." (numerical correction)
5. "He said he'd be here at noon. Well, he said 'around noon.'" (accuracy correction)

**What happens if it's missing**: Without "well," corrections can sound contradictory rather than clarifying. "I found a house. It's an apartment actually" -- without "well," the listener may be confused about whether this is new information or a correction.

### Pragmatic Function 5: Marking Insufficiency of Prior Answer

**What it does**: This is Lakoff's (1973) original insight. "Well" appears when the speaker senses that their answer is only a partial response to the question. Jucker (1993) described this as the "insufficiency marker" -- the speaker realizes their response doesn't fully address what was asked.

**What it signals to the listener**: "The answer I can give you is not complete. There's more to this than I can easily say."

**Examples**:
1. Q: "What's causing the delay?" A: "Well, there are several factors at play." (= my answer will be partial)
2. Q: "Is it safe?" A: "Well, it depends on what you mean by safe." (= the question needs qualifying)
3. Q: "Did the meeting go well?" A: "Well, some parts were productive." (= implying other parts were not)
4. Q: "Can you summarize the report?" A: "Well, it covers a lot of ground." (= a summary will be insufficient)

### Pragmatic Function 6: Topic Introduction and Framing

**What it does**: "Well" can introduce a new topic or preface direct reported speech. The discourse marker has four distinct uses in Modern English, one of which is as a "frame" that introduces a new topic.

**What it signals to the listener**: "I'm about to start something new" or "I'm setting up what comes next."

**Examples**:
1. "Well, this meeting has been very productive." (transition to closing)
2. "Well, let me tell you what happened." (introducing a narrative)
3. "Well, she said to me, 'I'm not going to stand for this.'" (introducing reported speech)
4. "Well, here's the thing..." (framing a key point)

**Important note on intonation**: "Well, this meeting has been very productive" is a respectful way to end a meeting, while emphasizing "WELL, this meeting has been very productive" with heavy stress is a tense, perhaps angry way to bring a meeting to an end.

### Typical Position in Conversation

"Well" occurs almost exclusively in **turn-initial position** -- at the very beginning of a speaker's response. It is syntactically restricted in this way; unlike "actually" or "basically," it cannot freely move to other sentence positions when functioning as a discourse marker.

### Common Misconceptions

1. **"Well is just a filler/hesitation marker"**: While "well" can buy time, it is not equivalent to "um" or "uh." It carries specific pragmatic information about the *type* of response coming. Research shows it was previously treated as a "filler or expletive" with "no function at all," but now is recognized as having multiple distinct functions.
2. **"Well always means disagreement"**: "Well" does not always signal disagreement. It marks any non-straightforward response -- including partial answers, topic shifts, self-corrections, and complicated situations.
3. **"Well means the speaker is lying"**: While "well" can indicate the speaker is being indirect, this is about pragmatic complexity, not deception.
4. **"Well is the same in all contexts"**: Position, intonation, and sequential placement all affect what "well" signals.

## 2. "SO" -- The Topic Management Marker

### Overview

"So" is the primary discourse marker for managing what conversation is *about*. It signals structural transitions: introducing new topics, returning to previous ones, drawing conclusions, and closing conversations. Bolden (2009) conducted the landmark study showing that "so" marks actions as "emerging from incipiency" -- i.e., things the speaker had been waiting to bring up.

### Key Academic Sources

- **Bolden (2009)**: "Implementing incipient actions: The discourse marker 'so' in English conversation" -- the most cited study on "so" as a topic launcher
- **Bolden (2008)**: "'So What's Up?': Using the discourse marker so to launch conversational business"
- **Schiffrin (1987)**: Treated "so" as marking result/consequence
- **Howe (1991)**: First to notice "so" in topic-initial utterances
- **Van Dijk**: Observed "so" linking speech acts where the second functions as a "conclusion"
- **Beach (1993)**: Work on "okay" transitions that interact with "so"

### Pragmatic Function 1: Introducing a New Topic or Returning to a Main Topic

**What it does**: "So" marks that what follows has been on the speaker's agenda -- it was "incipient" (pending, waiting to be raised). Bolden (2009) showed that "so"-prefacing is "overwhelmingly used to initiate courses of action." The speaker uses "so" to signal that they're launching into something that has been waiting in the background.

**What it signals to the listener**: "I've been wanting to bring this up. Here it comes." or "Let me get back to what we were talking about before."

**Examples**:
1. "So, I wanted to talk to you about the budget." (launching a pre-planned topic)
2. "So, getting back to what you were saying earlier..." (returning to a prior topic)
3. "So, how was your trip?" (opening a new conversational thread)
4. "So, the reason I called is..." (revealing the purpose of contact)
5. [After small talk] "So, what's the plan for tomorrow?" (transitioning from phatic talk to business)

**What happens if it's missing**: Without "so," topic introductions feel abrupt and disconnected. "I wanted to talk to you about the budget" launched without "so" can feel jarring -- it lacks the connective tissue that signals "this is relevant to our interaction." Research confirms that without "so," conversation sounds "robotic and impersonal" and "users have to work harder to connect the dots."

### Pragmatic Function 2: Signaling a Conclusion or Result

**What it does**: "So" in its traditional inferential/causal function links a cause to its result or an argument to its conclusion. Van Dijk observed that "so" links two speech acts where the second functions as a "conclusion."

**What it signals to the listener**: "Based on everything we've discussed, here's the takeaway."

**Examples**:
1. "The roads are icy, so we should leave early." (logical consequence)
2. "She didn't respond to any of my messages, so I assume she's not interested." (inference)
3. "We've been over budget for three months, so we need to make cuts." (conclusion from evidence)
4. "So, what you're saying is we should wait?" (summarizing the other person's argument)
5. "So, the bottom line is..." (drawing a final conclusion)

### Pragmatic Function 3: Indicating Impatience or Prompting Action ("So?")

**What it does**: Standalone "so" or "so?" with rising intonation functions to prompt the listener to act, respond, or explain. Raymond (2004) notes that participants use "so" to "prompt action by a recipient."

**What it signals to the listener**: "Get to the point" or "What's the relevance?" or "I'm waiting for you to do something."

**Examples**:
1. "So?" [with rising intonation] (= Why should I care? / What's your point?)
2. A: "I talked to your mother today." B: "So?" (= And? What happened? / Why does that matter?)
3. "So, are we going to do this or not?" (impatient prompting)
4. "So... what did the doctor say?" (prompting someone to share information)

**What happens if it's missing**: The impatience or prompting function has no equivalent single-word replacement. Without "so?", the speaker must use a full sentence like "What's your point?" or "Why are you telling me this?" -- both of which are more confrontational.

### Pragmatic Function 4: Pre-Closing Marker

**What it does**: "So" signals that the conversation is about to wrap up. It marks the transition from active discussion to closing.

**What it signals to the listener**: "We're approaching the end of this interaction."

**Examples**:
1. "So, I should probably let you go." (telephone pre-closing)
2. "So, I think that covers everything." (meeting pre-closing)
3. "So, I'll see you tomorrow then?" (confirming and closing)
4. "So... it was great catching up!" (social visit pre-closing)

### Pragmatic Function 5: Lesson/Meeting Opener ("So, let's get started")

**What it does**: In institutional settings, "so" marks the transition from informal preliminary talk to the official business. Bolden (2009) showed this is a specific case of implementing an incipient action: the "business" of the meeting has been pending since everyone gathered.

**What it signals to the listener**: "The informal phase is over. We're beginning now."

**Examples**:
1. "So, let's get started." (classroom/meeting opener)
2. "So, where were we last time?" (returning to ongoing institutional activity)
3. "So, today we're going to be talking about..." (framing a lesson)
4. "So, the agenda for today is..." (meeting launch)

### Typical Position in Conversation

"So" appears primarily in **turn-initial position**, especially when launching new topics or conclusions. It can also appear **mid-turn** when connecting clauses causally ("I was tired, so I went home"). The standalone "So?" occupies an entire turn.

### Common Misconceptions

1. **"So is just a causal conjunction"**: While "so" can mark cause-and-effect, its discourse marker functions extend far beyond this -- topic management, pre-closing, prompting, and institutional openings are all distinct from logical causation.
2. **"Starting a sentence with 'so' is bad grammar"**: This is a prescriptivist complaint with no linguistic basis. Bolden (2009) demonstrated that "so"-prefacing is a systematic, rule-governed practice in natural conversation.
3. **"So is interchangeable with therefore"**: "Therefore" is purely logical/inferential. "So" has social and interactional functions that "therefore" cannot perform.

## 3. "LIKE" -- The Most Stigmatized and Most Misunderstood Marker

### Overview

"Like" is the most socially stigmatized discourse marker in English and simultaneously one of the most pragmatically sophisticated. It performs at least four distinct functions (approximation/hedging, quotative, focus/emphasis, and softening), none of which are "meaningless." Research by Jones, Zane, and Grossman (2022) showed that even autistic children use "like" at similar rates to neurotypical peers, suggesting it represents a pragmatic strength rather than a deficit. The linguist Alexandra D'Arcy has traced its use back to the 1700s, demolishing the myth that it originated with 1980s Valley Girls.

### Key Academic Sources

- **Tagliamonte & D'Arcy (2004)**: "He's like, she's like: The quotative system in Canadian youth"
- **Underhill (1988)**: Early analysis of "like" as a discourse marker with multiple functions
- **Andersen (2001)**: Classified four functions: hedging, self-rephrasal, focus-marking, quotation
- **Jones, Zane, & Grossman (2022)**: PMC study showing autistic children use "like" with similar frequency and functions as neurotypical peers
- **Schweinberger (2015)**: Corpus-based analysis of "like" across varieties of English

### Pragmatic Function 1: Hedging/Approximation

**What it does**: "Like" signals that what follows is not exact -- it is approximate, loose, or should not be taken too literally. Speakers use "like" to "distance themselves from the truth conditions of what they are saying, excusing themselves from the responsibility for what is being said." The core meaning involves "indicating upcoming loose language" or being a "deliberate marker of vagueness."

**What it signals to the listener**: "The following information is approximate, not precise. Don't hold me to the exact number/time/description."

**Examples**:
1. "It was like 3 o'clock." (= approximately 3, maybe 2:45 or 3:15)
2. "There were like fifty people there." (= roughly fifty, could be forty or sixty)
3. "It took like an hour." (= approximately, not precisely 60 minutes)
4. "She was like really upset." (= the word 'upset' is an approximation of her emotional state)
5. "It's like a ten-minute walk." (= roughly, not exactly measured)

**What happens if it's missing**: Without "like," the statement becomes a precise claim. "It was 3 o'clock" versus "It was like 3 o'clock" -- the first commits the speaker to exactness; the second gives wiggle room. Removing "like" changes the pragmatic force from approximate to definitive.

### Pragmatic Function 2: Quotative ("be like")

**What it does**: "Be like" introduces reported speech or thought. Tagliamonte and D'Arcy (2004) documented that quotative "be like" has become the dominant quotative form among speakers under 30, surpassing "said," "goes," and "thinks." Corpus studies find "no evidence of it before the 1980s." Crucially, "be like" can introduce *thoughts* as well as *speech*, something "said" cannot do.

**What it signals to the listener**: "I'm about to show you what someone said or thought. This may be a dramatized/reconstructed version rather than verbatim."

**Examples**:
1. "She was like, 'No way am I doing that.'" (reported speech)
2. "And I was like, 'Are you serious?'" (reported speech with dramatization)
3. "He was like, 'I don't even care anymore.'" (reported speech conveying attitude)
4. "And I'm just sitting there like, 'This can't be happening.'" (reported *thought*, not spoken aloud)
5. "My mom was like, 'You need to clean your room,' and I was like, 'I already did.'" (dialogue reconstruction)

**What happens if it's missing**: Without "be like," speakers must use "said," "told me," "thought," or "goes." These alternatives lack the dramatizing and thought-reporting qualities of "be like." "She said 'No way'" is more formal and less vivid than "She was like, 'No way.'"

### Pragmatic Function 3: Focus/Emphasis Marker

**What it does**: "Like" draws attention to upcoming information that is new, important, or noteworthy. It functions as a "non-contrastive focuser," highlighting the element that follows as the most salient part of the utterance.

**What it signals to the listener**: "Pay attention to what comes next -- it's the important/surprising part."

**Examples**:
1. "It was, like, the BEST concert I've ever been to." (emphasizing superlative)
2. "He was, like, seriously angry." (emphasizing degree)
3. "They called and we're, like, in arrears." (highlighting unexpected/important information)
4. "He was really upset. Like, he couldn't catch his breath he was crying so much." (elaborating with emphasis)
5. "Like, they got the worst kitchens in England." (foregrounding the claim)

**What happens if it's missing**: Without the focus "like," the emphasis is lost. "It was the best concert" is a flat statement; "It was, like, the BEST concert" directs the listener's attention to the intensity of the speaker's experience.

### Pragmatic Function 4: Softening Assertions

**What it does**: "Like" reduces the force of an assertion, making it more tentative and less confrontational. This overlaps with hedging but operates on the entire proposition rather than a specific number or description.

**What it signals to the listener**: "I'm not being super definitive about this. Take it as a suggestion or observation rather than a firm claim."

**Examples**:
1. "I like don't really understand the point of this meeting." (softened criticism)
2. "It's like not really working, is it?" (softened negative assessment)
3. "I was like kind of hoping we could leave early." (softened request)

### Typical Position in Conversation

"Like" can appear in almost any syntactic position, which is unusual for discourse markers. It occurs:
- **Pre-NP**: "like fifty people"
- **Pre-AP**: "like really upset"
- **Pre-VP**: "like died laughing"
- **Clause-initially**: "Like, what are we supposed to do?"
- **As part of quotative construction**: "She was like..."

### Common Misconceptions

1. **"Like is meaningless filler"**: This is the most pervasive misconception. "Like" signals specific pragmatic information: approximation, focus, quotation, or softening. Research by Jones et al. (2022) explicitly states "like" serves "important pragmatic functions that are not encoded by any other discourse marker."
2. **"Like is a women's/girls' thing"**: In numerous statistically significant studies, men were found to use "like" more frequently than women. The association with young women is a social stereotype, not a linguistic fact.
3. **"Like originated with Valley Girls in the 1980s"**: D'Arcy traced "like" as a discourse element back to the 1700s (Frances Burney's *Evelina*, 1788). Only quotative "be like" may have emerged in the 1980s. The beatniks used "like" extensively in the 1950s-60s.
4. **"Intelligent people don't say 'like'"**: Usage of "like" correlates with social context and register, not intelligence. Linguists, professors, and other highly educated speakers use "like" regularly in casual speech.
5. **"Like should be eliminated from speech"**: Removing "like" would remove genuine pragmatic information. A speaker who says "It was like 3 o'clock" is communicating something different from "It was 3 o'clock."

### Relevance to Autism

The Jones, Zane, and Grossman (2022) PMC study is directly relevant. Their findings showed that "this group of older autistic children use like as often as their peers and use it to signify similar information about upcoming speech to their listener." The authors concluded that "like" represents "a pragmatic strength in autism that has heretofore gone unnoticed." This challenges the assumption that all pragmatic markers are areas of difficulty for autistic individuals.

## 4. "YOU KNOW" -- The Shared Knowledge Marker

### Overview

"You know" is a discourse marker that fundamentally operates on the **interpersonal** plane of communication. Ostman (1981) provided the first book-length treatment ("You Know: A Discourse-Functional Approach"), and subsequent research has shown it functions across multiple domains: appealing to shared knowledge, building rapport, hedging, and marking word-search difficulties. Its core meaning is debated, but most scholars agree it involves inviting the listener to access common ground.

### Key Academic Sources

- **Ostman (1981)**: *You Know: A Discourse-Functional Approach* -- first major study
- **Schiffrin (1987)**: Treated "you know" as operating in the participation framework
- **Erman (2001)**: Examined "you know" as an interpersonal marker
- **Vincent (2005)**: "The grammaticalization of you know: From shared knowledge to control over the co-speaker"

### Pragmatic Function 1: Appealing to Shared Knowledge/Common Ground

**What it does**: "You know" invites the listener to access knowledge that both participants are assumed to share. It functions as "an inferential marker which invites the addressee to complete the argument by drawing the appropriate inferences." The speaker strives towards "getting the addressee to cooperate and/or to accept the propositional content of his utterance as mutual background knowledge."

**What it signals to the listener**: "You already have the context needed to understand this. I'm treating this as something we both know."

**Examples**:
1. "It was one of those days, you know, where nothing goes right." (= you've experienced days like this too)
2. "He's from that town in Connecticut, you know, the one with the big park." (= we both know this place)
3. "She's always been like that, you know." (= you've seen this behavior too)
4. "You know how it is when you start a new job." (= you share this universal experience)
5. "The meeting went about as well as you'd expect, you know." (= we both know the background)

**What happens if it's missing**: Without "you know," statements can feel like one-directional information delivery rather than collaborative conversation. "It was one of those days where nothing goes right" is a description; "It was one of those days, you know, where nothing goes right" is an invitation to empathize.

### Pragmatic Function 2: Seeking Rapport/Confirmation

**What it does**: Even when the listener may NOT actually know what the speaker is referring to, "you know" functions at what Ostman called "the deference level of politeness" -- it's "a striving towards attaining a camaraderie relationship between speaker and hearer." The speaker creates an *assumption* of shared experience to build closeness.

**What it signals to the listener**: "We're on the same page. We understand each other. We're connected."

**Examples**:
1. "It was really frustrating, you know?" (seeking empathic confirmation)
2. "I just feel like nobody's listening, you know?" (seeking validation)
3. "Life's too short, you know?" (philosophical rapport-building)
4. "You know, I really appreciate everything you've done." (warm interpersonal framing)
5. "It's not easy, you know, being the new person." (inviting empathy)

### Pragmatic Function 3: Hedging/Softening

**What it does**: "You know" softens the force of an assertion, making it less direct and more tentative. It operates within the interpersonal domain as a hedging device.

**What it signals to the listener**: "I'm not being too definitive about this. I'm leaving room for your perspective."

**Examples**:
1. "He's, you know, not the most reliable person." (softened criticism)
2. "I think we should, you know, maybe consider other options." (hedged suggestion)
3. "The food was, you know, okay." (understated negative assessment)
4. "It's, you know, a bit much." (softened complaint)

### Pragmatic Function 4: Marking Word Search/Planning Difficulty

**What it does**: "You know" signals that the speaker is searching for the right word or formulation. This is a cognitive function where the marker holds the conversational floor while the speaker processes.

**What it signals to the listener**: "I'm looking for the right way to say this. Bear with me."

**Examples**:
1. "It's, you know, that thing... the thing you use to..." (active word search)
2. "She's very, you know... intense." (searching for the right adjective)
3. "It was one of those, you know, situations where..." (searching for how to frame it)
4. "He was being, you know... difficult." (diplomatically searching for the right word)

**What happens if it's missing**: Without "you know" during a word search, silences and pauses can be interpreted as the speaker not knowing the answer rather than searching for the right expression.

### How to Distinguish from Literal "Do You Know?"

**Literal "Do you know?"** is a genuine question expecting a yes/no answer:
- "Do you know where the library is?" (information-seeking question)
- "Do you know Sarah?" (factual question about acquaintance)

**Discourse marker "you know"** never expects a yes/no answer:
- "It was, you know, one of those things." (not asking if you literally know)
- "You know, I've been thinking..." (not asking if you're aware of the thinking)

**Key distinguishing features**: The discourse marker "you know" has reduced stress, occurs mid-sentence or clause-finally, and does not have the rising intonation of a question. The literal question "Do you know?" has question intonation and expects a response.

### Typical Position in Conversation

"You know" occurs mainly in **medial position** (mid-sentence) and **final position** (clause-finally seeking confirmation). It can also appear in **initial position** to frame what follows.

### Common Misconceptions

1. **"You know is just a verbal tic"**: While overuse can become habitual, the marker carries real interpersonal and cognitive functions.
2. **"You know means the speaker is inarticulate"**: Research shows sophisticated speakers use "you know" to manage complex interpersonal relationships and to navigate difficult topics.
3. **"You know always appeals to actual shared knowledge"**: The marker can be used even when the addressee doesn't actually know what the speaker is referring to -- it creates an *assumption* of shared ground.

## 5. "I MEAN" -- The Self-Repair Marker

### Overview

"I mean" is the primary discourse marker English speakers use to modify, correct, or clarify something they have just said. Schiffrin (1987) analyzed it as a speaker-oriented marker operating on the ideational plane. Unlike "you know" (which is listener-oriented), "I mean" is fundamentally about the speaker managing their own output. Its most frequent collocate is "but," indicating its strong association with repositioning and adjustment.

### Key Academic Sources

- **Schiffrin (1987)**: Speaker-oriented marker in the ideational structure
- **Fox Tree & Schrock (2002)**: Processing effects of "I mean"
- **Frontiers study (2024)**: Corpus-based comparison of "you know" and "I mean" in political interviews

### Pragmatic Function 1: Self-Correction/Clarification

**What it does**: "I mean" introduces a correction to something the speaker just said. The correction can be factual, terminological, or conceptual.

**What it signals to the listener**: "What I just said wasn't quite right. Here's the corrected version."

**Examples**:
1. "We need to leave at 3. I mean 4. Sorry, 4 o'clock." (factual correction)
2. "He was angry. I mean, not angry exactly, more like frustrated." (precision correction)
3. "It's in the living room. I mean the dining room." (location correction)
4. "She's his sister. I mean his cousin." (relationship correction)
5. "We went to Paris. I mean, we went to Lyon first, then Paris." (sequence correction)

**What happens if it's missing**: Without "I mean," corrections can sound like contradictions. "We need to leave at 3. 4 o'clock." is confusing -- was it 3 or 4? "I mean" explicitly signals that the second statement supersedes the first.

### Pragmatic Function 2: Reformulation/Elaboration

**What it does**: "I mean" introduces a restatement of the same idea in different words, often to make it clearer or more precise. This is not correction (the original statement wasn't wrong) but reformulation (the speaker wants to express it better).

**What it signals to the listener**: "Let me say that another way to make sure I'm being clear."

**Examples**:
1. "The project has challenges. I mean, we're not going to hit the deadline." (making the implication explicit)
2. "She's talented. I mean, she can play three instruments and she's only twelve." (providing evidence)
3. "It's complicated. I mean, there are at least five different stakeholders involved." (clarifying what 'complicated' means)
4. "He's careful with money. I mean, he checks every receipt twice." (illustrating through example)
5. "The response was positive. I mean, everyone I talked to said they loved it." (elaborating on 'positive')

### Pragmatic Function 3: Intensification (Strengthening a Prior Statement)

**What it does**: When "I mean" is followed by a STRONGER statement than what preceded it, it functions as an intensifier. The speaker is saying "actually, let me be even more emphatic about what I just said."

**What it signals to the listener**: "What I said before didn't go far enough. Let me strengthen it."

**Examples**:
1. "That was bad. I mean, it was an absolute disaster." (escalation)
2. "He's smart. I mean, he's a genuine genius." (intensification)
3. "I'm worried. I mean, I'm terrified." (emotional escalation)
4. "It was good. I mean, it was the best meal I've ever had." (superlative intensification)
5. "She's talented. I mean, she's world-class." (degree escalation)

### Pragmatic Function 4: Backing Down (Weakening a Prior Statement)

**What it does**: When "I mean" is followed by a WEAKER statement, the speaker is retreating from their original claim. This often occurs when the speaker realizes they've been too strong, too critical, or too committal.

**What it signals to the listener**: "I went too far with what I just said. Let me moderate it."

**Examples**:
1. "That was terrible. I mean, it wasn't great." (retreat from strong negative)
2. "I hate it. I mean, I don't hate it, I just don't love it." (moderating intensity)
3. "He's incompetent. I mean, he's still learning." (charitable reframing)
4. "We're never going to finish. I mean, it might take longer than planned." (moderating pessimism)
5. "That's wrong. I mean, it might not be the best approach." (softening criticism)

**What happens if it's missing**: Without "I mean," the speaker cannot smoothly moderate. "That was terrible. It wasn't great." sounds contradictory without the explicit marker that the second statement replaces the first.

### Typical Position in Conversation

"I mean" appears in **medial position**, between the original statement and the correction/reformulation. It can also appear in **initial position** at the start of a turn, in which case it typically signals that the speaker is about to clarify something said in a previous turn.

### Common Misconceptions

1. **"I mean is just filler"**: "I mean" carries specific information about the relationship between what was just said and what will follow.
2. **"I mean always indicates uncertainty"**: It can actually indicate increased certainty (intensification function).
3. **"I mean and you know are interchangeable"**: They are not. "I mean" is speaker-oriented (managing one's own speech); "you know" is listener-oriented (managing the relationship with the hearer).

## 6. "ACTUALLY" -- The Counter-Expectation Marker

### Overview

"Actually" is a discourse marker that signals a discrepancy between what was expected or assumed and what is in fact the case. Clift (2001) published the landmark study "Meaning in interaction: The case of actually" in *Language*, and Aijmer and Simon-Vandenbergen (2004) described it as a "pragmatic marker of expectation." Its core meaning involves "stating something in an unexpected way, manner or position."

### Key Academic Sources

- **Clift (2001)**: "Meaning in interaction: The case of actually" (*Language*, 77(2): 245-291)
- **Oh (2000)**: Analysis of "actually" in conversational interaction
- **Aijmer & Simon-Vandenbergen (2004)**: "A model and a methodology for the study of pragmatic markers: the semantic field of expectation"

### Pragmatic Function 1: Polite Correction

**What it does**: "Actually" introduces information that corrects a misconception, error, or assumption. Its counter-expectational meaning softens the correction by framing it as "surprising but true" rather than "you're wrong."

**What it signals to the listener**: "What you assumed/said isn't quite right, but I'm correcting you gently."

**Examples**:
1. "Actually, the meeting is on Tuesday, not Wednesday." (factual correction)
2. "Actually, I'm from Canada, not the US." (identity correction)
3. "Actually, it's pronounced 'es-PRESS-oh,' not 'ex-PRESS-oh.'" (pronunciation correction)
4. "Actually, I've already finished that report." (correcting an assumption about status)
5. "Actually, she's the department head, not the assistant." (role correction)

**What happens if it's missing**: Without "actually," corrections become blunt confrontations. "The meeting is on Tuesday, not Wednesday" is a bald correction. "Actually, the meeting is on Tuesday" softens it by framing the information as unexpected rather than framing the listener as wrong.

### Pragmatic Function 2: Counter-Expectation (Marking Surprise/Unexpectedness)

**What it does**: "Actually" marks information that contradicts the listener's (or even the speaker's) expectations. It is used to flag information as surprising or noteworthy precisely because it defies assumptions.

**What it signals to the listener**: "This might surprise you" or "Contrary to what you might think..."

**Examples**:
1. "I actually enjoyed the movie." (counter to expectation that I wouldn't)
2. "She actually agreed to help." (surprising given context)
3. "The test was actually pretty easy." (counter to expected difficulty)
4. "He's actually really nice once you get to know him." (correcting a first impression)
5. "Actually, I've never been to New York." (surprising given context)

### Pragmatic Function 3: Self-Correction/Change of Mind

**What it does**: "Actually" can mark the speaker's own shift in knowledge, decision, or orientation -- similar to "well" but with the added element of counter-expectation.

**What it signals to the listener**: "I'm changing what I just said/decided. I've realized something different."

**Examples**:
1. "I'll have the salad. Actually, I'll have the soup." (changing order)
2. "I was going to say no. Actually, let me think about it." (reconsidering)
3. "I don't think -- actually, wait, you might be right." (mid-sentence correction)
4. "We should go left. Actually, no, right." (direction correction)

### Pragmatic Function 4: Emphasis/Assertion

**What it does**: "Actually" can emphasize that something is genuinely true, especially when the speaker anticipates skepticism or when the truth is noteworthy.

**What it signals to the listener**: "This is genuinely true, even though it might be hard to believe."

**Examples**:
1. "I actually did all the reading for class." (emphasis on genuineness)
2. "She actually knows five languages." (emphasis on impressive truth)
3. "We actually finished ahead of schedule." (emphasis on achievement)

### Position Effects

**Sentence-initial "actually"**: In initial position, "actually" functions predominantly as a strong indicator of discrepancy or counter-expectancy (79.2% of cases according to research by Clift). It has a backward orientation, typically indicating strong contrast with elements from the preceding discourse. "Actually, I disagree" is a clear correction.

**Sentence-medial "actually"**: Mid-sentence, "actually" can function either as emphasis or mild counter-expectation. "I actually enjoyed it" is less confrontational than "Actually, I enjoyed it."

**Sentence-final "actually"**: Final "actually" reinforces the claim expressed in an utterance, since what is unexpected requires more attention and processing. "I enjoyed it, actually" has a retrospective, almost confessional quality.

### Typical Position in Conversation

"Actually" is flexible in position -- it can appear **initially**, **medially**, or **finally** in a clause, with different pragmatic effects at each position (see above).

### Common Misconceptions

1. **"Actually is rude"**: While "actually" can *feel* condescending (especially in initial position before a correction), its pragmatic function is to *soften* corrections by framing information as unexpected rather than framing the listener as wrong. The rudeness is in the correction itself, not in "actually."
2. **"Actually means the same as 'in fact'"**: While they overlap, "actually" has a broader range of functions including self-correction and emphasis that "in fact" does not share.
3. **"Actually always signals disagreement"**: It frequently marks self-correction, surprise, or emphasis without any disagreement.
4. **"Well, actually..." is always condescending**: The combination "well, actually" has become a cultural stereotype (the "well, actually" guy), but in normal conversation, this combination simply marks a counter-expectational response.

## 7. "BASICALLY" -- The Simplification Marker

### Overview

"Basically" signals that the speaker is providing a simplified, summarized, or essential version of something more complex. Beeching (2016) analyzed it in *Pragmatic Markers in British English*, showing that its hedging function developed from a core meaning paraphrasable as "this is the short and simple version, but there is more to it." Its procedural function is to signal to the hearer how the utterance should be understood.

### Key Academic Sources

- **Beeching (2016)**: *Pragmatic markers in British English: Meaning in social interaction*
- **Tagliamonte et al. (2022)**: "'Did you just basically steal everything?' -- A study of discourse-pragmatic variation and change"

### Pragmatic Function 1: Simplification/Summary

**What it does**: "Basically" signals that what follows is a reduced, simplified version of a more complex reality. The core meaning is "this is the short and simple version, but there is more to it."

**What it signals to the listener**: "I'm giving you the essential version. There are details I'm leaving out, but this captures the core idea."

**Examples**:
1. "Basically, the project is on hold until we get more funding." (simplifying complex situation)
2. "So basically what happened is the system crashed and we lost all the data." (narrative summary)
3. "The rules are basically the same as last year, with a few tweaks." (general summary with caveat)
4. "Basically, you just click here and then here." (simplifying a procedure)
5. "It's basically a faster version of the old model." (product summary)

**What happens if it's missing**: Without "basically," the listener doesn't know whether they're getting the full picture or a simplification. "The project is on hold" sounds like the complete explanation; "Basically, the project is on hold" signals there's more to the story.

### Pragmatic Function 2: Asserting Authority/Expertise

**What it does**: "Basically" can position the speaker as someone who understands the full complexity and is choosing to simplify for the listener's benefit. This can function as a claim of authority or expertise.

**What it signals to the listener**: "I understand this well enough to give you the simplified version." (Can also imply: "I know more about this than you.")

**Examples**:
1. "Basically, what happened was the board overrode the committee's recommendation." (authoritative narrative)
2. "Basically, the whole theory comes down to supply and demand." (expert simplification)
3. "What they're basically saying is they don't want to negotiate." (interpretive authority)
4. "Basically, you've been doing it wrong." (authoritative correction)

### Pragmatic Function 3: Hedging/Approximation

**What it does**: "Basically" signals that the description is approximate -- close to reality but not exact. It has the functions of "summarization, approximation, and metacommenting with a weak or strong hedging function."

**What it signals to the listener**: "This is roughly accurate but don't take it as a precise characterization."

**Examples**:
1. "They're basically the same thing." (= very similar but not identical)
2. "I'm basically done with the project." (= nearly but not completely done)
3. "He basically agreed to everything." (= agreed to most things)
4. "It's basically impossible to get there on time." (= extremely difficult, perhaps not literally impossible)

### Pragmatic Function 4: Potential Condescension

**What it does**: When "basically" is used in contexts where the listener is equally or more knowledgeable, it can come across as condescending -- implying that the listener needs a simplified version.

**What it signals to the listener (when condescending)**: "I don't think you can handle the full complexity, so let me dumb it down for you."

**Examples of condescending use**:
1. "Basically, the idea is that prices go up when demand increases." (to an economics professor)
2. "Basically, a computer processes information." (to a software engineer)
3. "Basically, what you need to understand is..." (framing the listener as needing education)

**What happens if it's missing**: Without "basically," the same content is informational rather than potentially condescending. "Prices go up when demand increases" is a neutral statement of fact. "Basically, prices go up when demand increases" implies the speaker is simplifying for the listener.

### Typical Position in Conversation

"Basically" appears primarily in **initial position** (clause-initial), where it frames everything that follows as a simplification. It also occurs in **pre-verbal medial position** ("I basically just went home").

### Common Misconceptions

1. **"Basically is always condescending"**: The condescending reading is context-dependent. In many uses, "basically" is a neutral signal that a summary is coming.
2. **"Basically is meaningless filler"**: It carries procedural meaning -- telling the listener how to interpret what follows.
3. **"Basically indicates the speaker doesn't understand the topic"**: Often the opposite -- it indicates the speaker understands it well enough to summarize.

## 8. "RIGHT" / "OK" -- The Acknowledgment/Transition Markers

### Overview

"Right" and "OK" are distinct markers that share some overlapping functions. "Right" primarily functions as an acknowledgment/backchannel marker and a confirmation-seeker. "OK" primarily functions as a transition marker between conversational phases. Beach (1993) published the landmark study "Transitional regularities for casual 'Okay' usages," showing that "Okay" functions pivotally at or near transition/opportunity spaces.

### Key Academic Sources

- **Beach (1993)**: "Transitional regularities for casual 'Okay' usages" (*Journal of Pragmatics*, 19: 325-352)
- **Gaines (2011)**: Analysis of "okay" in classroom discourse
- **Gardner (2001)**: Analysis of "right" as a response token

### "RIGHT" -- Functions

#### Function 1: Acknowledgment/Backchannel

**What it does**: "Right" signals that the speaker has heard and understood what was just said. As a backchannel, it functions similarly to "yeah," "uh-huh," and "mmm" but with a stronger implication of comprehension.

**What it signals to the listener**: "I understand what you're saying. Continue."

**Examples**:
1. A: "So you need to turn left at the intersection." B: "Right." (= I'm following your directions)
2. A: "The deadline was moved to Friday." B: "Right." (= I've registered this information)
3. A: "She won't be joining us because she's out of town." B: "Right." (= I understand the situation)
4. A: "The first step is to open the settings menu." B: "Right." (= I'm following along)

#### Function 2: Confirmation-Seeking (Tag "...right?")

**What it does**: When "right?" appears at the end of a statement with rising intonation, it seeks confirmation or agreement from the listener. It functions similarly to tag questions but is more informal.

**What it signals to the listener**: "Please confirm that what I've just said is correct" or "You agree with me, right?"

**Examples**:
1. "The meeting is at 3, right?" (seeking factual confirmation)
2. "You were there too, right?" (seeking memory confirmation)
3. "This is the right way to do it, right?" (seeking procedural confirmation)
4. "We're all on the same page, right?" (seeking agreement)
5. "That's what she said, right?" (seeking corroboration)

**What happens if it's missing**: Without the tag "right?", statements become flat assertions. "The meeting is at 3" is a declaration; "The meeting is at 3, right?" opens space for correction and shows the speaker is aware they might be wrong.

#### Function 3: "Right" as Agreement/Emphasis

**What it does**: A standalone "Right!" with emphasis or falling intonation signals strong agreement.

**Examples**:
1. A: "We really need to fix this process." B: "Right! Exactly." (emphatic agreement)
2. A: "That's not acceptable behavior." B: "Right." (firm agreement)

### "OK" / "OKAY" -- Functions

#### Function 1: Topic/Activity Transition

**What it does**: "OK" marks transitions between phases of an interaction. Beach (1993) showed that recipients and current speakers rely on "Okay" pivotally, at or near transition/opportunity spaces: "decidedly in response to prior talk, yet also in transitionally relevant ('state of readiness') ways via shifts/movements to next-positioned matters."

**What it signals to the listener**: "We're moving on to the next thing."

**Examples**:
1. "OK, let's move on to the next item." (agenda transition)
2. "OK, so that's settled. Now..." (closing one topic, opening another)
3. "OK, everyone ready? Let's begin." (activity transition)
4. "OK, I think we're done here." (activity closing)

#### Function 2: Acceptance/Agreement

**What it does**: "OK" signals acceptance of a proposition, request, or plan. It differs from "right" in that it implies compliance or willingness, not just understanding.

**What it signals to the listener**: "I accept/agree to what you've said."

**Examples**:
1. A: "Can you send me the report by noon?" B: "OK." (agreeing to the request)
2. A: "We'll meet at the restaurant at 7." B: "OK." (accepting the plan)
3. A: "You'll need to redo this section." B: "OK." (accepting the feedback)

#### Function 3: "OK so" as a Compound Marker

**What it does**: "OK so" combines the transitional function of "OK" with the topic-launching function of "so." Research by Tuma (2022) shows "okay so" marks transitions between task components in collaborative work. This compound marker signals both the closure of what came before AND the launch of what comes next.

**What it signals to the listener**: "The previous phase is complete. Here's what we're doing now."

**Examples**:
1. "OK so, where were we?" (resuming after interruption)
2. "OK so, the plan is..." (launching into the main topic after preliminaries)
3. "OK so let me explain how this works." (transitioning from setup to instruction)
4. "OK so, first things first." (beginning a structured sequence)

### Typical Position in Conversation

- **"Right"**: Occurs as a standalone turn (backchannel) or at the end of a clause (tag question). Rarely begins a new topic.
- **"OK"**: Occurs at **turn-initial** position, especially at transition points. Also functions as a standalone response turn.
- **"OK so"**: Always in **turn-initial** position, launching a new conversational phase.

### Common Misconceptions

1. **"OK always means agreement"**: "OK" can signal understanding or transition without implying agreement with the content. A teacher saying "OK" after a student's wrong answer is transitioning, not agreeing.
2. **"Right means the listener agrees"**: "Right" as a backchannel means "I'm following," not necessarily "I agree."
3. **"OK is too informal for professional settings"**: "OK" is the most universal transition marker in English and is used at all levels of formality in spoken discourse.

## 9. "ANYWAY" / "ANYHOW" -- The Return/Dismissal Markers

### Overview

"Anyway" (and its variant "anyhow") is the primary English discourse marker for managing digressions. Ferrara (1997) published the definitive study "Form and function of the discourse marker anyway: implications for discourse analysis" using a corpus of 167 oral narratives. She found that "anyway" signals how to "bracket discourse coherently into foreground/background or main structure/side structure." Traugott (2020) classified it among Topic Orientation Markers.

### Key Academic Sources

- **Ferrara (1997)**: "Form and function of the discourse marker anyway" (*Linguistics*, 35: 343-378)
- **Traugott (2020)**: Classification as a Topic Orientation Marker with metalinguistic functions
- **Park (2010)**: "Topic Orientation Markers" -- four uses in discourse

### Pragmatic Function 1: Returning to the Main Point After a Digression

**What it does**: This is the primary function. Ferrara (1997) found that "teller-triggered occurrences predominate three-to-one, showing the function to be primarily self-digression management." The speaker uses "anyway" to signal "I've gone off track, and now I'm returning to the main thread."

**What it signals to the listener**: "That tangent is over. Let me get back to what I was saying."

**Examples**:
1. "...and then we saw this amazing sunset -- but anyway, as I was saying, the train was late." (returning after scenic digression)
2. "He used to work at -- well, I don't remember where exactly -- anyway, the point is he has experience." (returning after failed recall)
3. "I could go on about the history of it, but anyway, here's what you need to know." (cutting short a digression)
4. "We ended up talking about all sorts of things -- anyway, the outcome was that she agreed to help." (returning to narrative thread)
5. "Sorry, I got sidetracked. Anyway, what I wanted to tell you was..." (explicit acknowledgment of digression)

**What happens if it's missing**: Without "anyway," digression returns are unmarked. The listener must figure out on their own that the speaker has shifted back to the main topic. Conversations without "anyway" can feel disorganized and hard to follow.

### Pragmatic Function 2: Topic Closure/Dismissal

**What it does**: "Anyway" can signal that the preceding talk is being dismissed as unimportant, irrelevant, or not worth pursuing further. This is different from returning to a main point -- it's more like sweeping something away.

**What it signals to the listener**: "What we were just talking about doesn't matter. Let's drop it."

**Examples**:
1. "I probably shouldn't have said anything. Anyway..." (dismissing previous statement)
2. "It's a long story. Anyway, the important thing is..." (dismissing complexity)
3. "I don't know why I brought that up. Anyway..." (dismissing one's own digression)
4. "That's not really relevant anyway." (dismissing someone else's point)
5. "Whatever, anyway, it doesn't matter." (emphatic dismissal)

**What happens if it's missing**: Without "anyway," dismissal must be done with explicit statements like "That doesn't matter" or "Never mind" -- which are more direct and potentially more face-threatening.

### Pragmatic Function 3: Pre-Closing Marker

**What it does**: "Anyway" can signal that the conversation is wrapping up. It marks the transition from active topic-focused discussion to closing routines.

**What it signals to the listener**: "We're approaching the end of this conversation."

**Examples**:
1. "Anyway, I should let you go." (phone conversation pre-closing)
2. "Anyway, it was great seeing you." (in-person pre-closing)
3. "Anyway, I need to get back to work." (workplace pre-closing)
4. "Anyway, thanks for listening." (narrative closing)

### Pragmatic Function 4: Signaling Impatience

**What it does**: "Anyway" can convey impatience with the current state of conversation, especially when combined with certain intonation patterns.

**What it signals to the listener**: "We've spent too long on this. Let's move on."

**Examples**:
1. "Yes, yes, anyway, the point is..." (cutting through extended discussion)
2. "Anyway, can we get back to the actual issue?" (redirecting with mild frustration)
3. "ANYway..." (with stressed first syllable, signaling impatience with current topic)

### "Anyway" vs. "Anyhow" vs. "Anyways"

- **Anyway**: Standard form, universally accepted
- **Anyhow**: More informal variant with identical functions; slightly more common in British English
- **Anyways**: Informal variant common in North American speech; considered nonstandard by prescriptivists but used widely

### Typical Position in Conversation

"Anyway" occurs in **turn-initial** position (starting a new segment) or at **turn boundaries** (transitioning between segments). It often co-occurs with "but" ("but anyway"), "so" ("so anyway"), or pauses.

### Common Misconceptions

1. **"Anyway is rude"**: While "anyway" can signal dismissal, its primary function is structural (managing digressions). It's a neutral tool most of the time.
2. **"Anyway means I'm not interested in what you said"**: Usually "anyway" is self-directed -- the speaker is managing their OWN tendency to digress, not dismissing the listener.
3. **"Anyways is wrong"**: "Anyways" is a widespread spoken variant. Prescriptive objections to it are social conventions, not linguistic rules.

## 10. "OH" -- The Change-of-State Marker

### Overview

"Oh" is one of the most theoretically important discourse markers in English. Heritage (1984) published the foundational analysis "A Change-of-State Token and Aspects of its Sequential Placement" in *Structures of Social Action*, defining "oh" as a marker that shows the speaker has "undergone some kind of change in his or her locally current state of knowledge, information, orientation or awareness." Schiffrin (1987) treated it as a "marker of information management." It is overwhelmingly the most commonly used interjection, with 8,000 occurrences per million words in British English conversation.

### Key Academic Sources

- **Heritage (1984)**: "A Change-of-State Token and Aspects of its Sequential Placement" -- the definitive study
- **Schiffrin (1987)**: "Oh" as marker of information management
- **Fox Tree & Schrock (2002)**: Processing effects of "oh"
- **Local (1996)**: Phonetic analysis of "oh"

### The Core Theory: Change-of-State Token

Heritage (1984) identified that "oh" marks a moment when the speaker's internal state of knowledge, attention, or awareness shifts. Articulating what would otherwise be a private cognitive process serves as a vehicle for various conversational actions, including "noticing, having one's attention drawn to something, remembering, being reminded, informed, or corrected, and arriving at discoveries or realizations of various kinds."

The particle "oh" may propose that the speaker has undergone:
- A shift in **information** when produced as a response to an informing
- A shift in **attention** when produced as a preface to an answer
- A shift in **orientation** when produced as a preface to a second assessment

### Pragmatic Function 1: Receiving New Information

**What it does**: "Oh" marks that the speaker has just received and processed new information. It makes the private cognitive act of "learning something new" publicly visible.

**What it signals to the listener**: "I just learned something. I'm updating my understanding."

**Examples**:
1. A: "Sarah got promoted." B: "Oh! That's great!" (new information + positive assessment)
2. A: "The store closes at 5 today." B: "Oh, I didn't know that." (explicit information update)
3. A: "We're actually having the party at Jake's house." B: "Oh, okay." (adjusting expectations)
4. A: "The test was postponed." B: "Oh, really?" (receiving news with mild surprise)
5. A: "She doesn't eat gluten." B: "Oh, I'll remember that for next time." (incorporating new information)

**What happens if it's missing**: Without "oh," the response to new information lacks the acknowledgment of cognitive change. Compare: A: "Sarah got promoted." B: "That's great!" (without "oh") -- this sounds like the speaker already knew, or doesn't register that this is news. The "oh" explicitly performs the work of showing "I just now learned this."

### Pragmatic Function 2: Self-Correction/Realization

**What it does**: "Oh" before a self-correction marks the moment of realization that something needs to be fixed. It makes the discovery of an error visible.

**What it signals to the listener**: "I just realized I made a mistake or forgot something."

**Examples**:
1. "We need to go left -- oh wait, no, right." (real-time realization of error)
2. "Oh, that's not what I meant to say." (recognizing a mistake)
3. "Oh, I forgot to mention -- the deadline changed." (suddenly remembering)
4. "Oh, actually, you're right about that." (conceding after reassessment)
5. "Oh, I just realized I've been using the wrong word." (mid-conversation correction)

### Pragmatic Function 3: Surprise/Unexpectedness

**What it does**: "Oh" registers that something is unexpected. The degree of surprise correlates with prosodic features (pitch height, duration, intensity).

**What it signals to the listener**: "I wasn't expecting that."

**Examples**:
1. "Oh, I didn't realize you were coming today!" (surprised presence)
2. "Oh! I had no idea they were engaged!" (genuine surprise at news)
3. "Oh, that's interesting -- I would have guessed the opposite." (intellectual surprise)
4. "Oh, wow, that's way more expensive than I expected." (quantity surprise)

### Pragmatic Function 4: Noticing/Attention Shift

**What it does**: "Oh" marks the moment when something enters the speaker's awareness. This could be a physical object, a connection between ideas, or a person's presence.

**What it signals to the listener**: "Something just caught my attention."

**Examples**:
1. "Oh, there you are! I've been looking for you." (noticing a person)
2. "Oh, look at that!" (drawing attention to something noticed)
3. "Oh, I see the connection now." (noticing an intellectual pattern)
4. "Oh, I just got the joke!" (delayed realization)

### Critical Distinction: "Oh" as Discourse Marker vs. "Oh" as Exclamation

This distinction is crucial for the knowledge base:

**"Oh" as discourse marker** (change-of-state token):
- Low emotional intensity
- Functions to mark cognitive change
- Often followed by a statement
- "Oh, I see" / "Oh, right" / "Oh, okay"

**"Oh" as exclamation** (emotional expression):
- High emotional intensity
- Expresses strong emotion (joy, fear, frustration, etc.)
- Often followed by additional emotional expressions
- "Oh my God!" / "Oh no!" / "Oh, how wonderful!"

The key distinguishing feature is **intonation and intensity**. The discourse marker "oh" conveys little emotion and reads as a cognitive state change. The exclamation "Oh!" carries an emotional charge and is punctuated with an exclamation point.

### Typical Position in Conversation

"Oh" appears in **turn-initial** position -- it is always the first element of a response. It can also appear **mid-turn** before self-corrections ("I think -- oh wait, no"). It never appears in final position as a discourse marker.

### Common Misconceptions

1. **"Oh is just an emotional exclamation"**: Heritage's (1984) most important contribution was showing that "oh" primarily marks cognitive state changes, not emotions.
2. **"Oh means the speaker is surprised"**: Surprise is only one type of change-of-state. "Oh" can mark new information received without any surprise at all ("Oh, okay").
3. **"Oh is meaningless"**: "Oh" performs critical conversational work by making private cognitive processes publicly available, allowing the conversation to proceed smoothly.

## 11. "NOW" / "THEN" / "AND THEN" -- Temporal/Sequential Markers

### Overview

These three markers share roots in temporal reference but have developed significant discourse-organizational functions beyond time-marking. Schiffrin (1987) dedicated a chapter to temporal adverbs as discourse markers, arguing that "now" points forward in discourse time while "then" can point either forward or backward. Their discourse marker functions are distinct from their literal temporal meanings.

### Key Academic Sources

- **Schiffrin (1987)**: Chapter 8, "Temporal adverbs: now and then" in *Discourse Markers*
- **Fraser (1990)**: General analysis of discourse marker function
- **Bestgen & Vonk (1995)**: "The Role of Temporal Segmentation Markers in Discourse Processing"

### "NOW" -- Functions

#### Function 1: Attention-Getter/Topic Shift Marker

**What it does**: "Now" in discourse marker function signals that something new and important is coming. Schiffrin argues that "now" indicates "a speaker's progression through a discourse which contains an ordered sequence of subordinating parts." It is "also used to indicate the upcoming shift in talk, or when the speaker wants to negotiate the right to control what will happen next in talk."

**What it signals to the listener**: "Pay attention. I'm shifting to something new and important."

**Examples**:
1. "Now, here's the interesting part." (flagging important upcoming content)
2. "Now, I want to be clear about something." (demanding attention for a key point)
3. "Now, some of you may disagree with this, but..." (preparing the audience for a controversial claim)
4. "Now then, let's look at the next slide." (British English attention-getter + transition)
5. "Now, what you need to understand is..." (asserting control over the discourse direction)

#### Function 2: Temporal Progression in Narrative

**What it does**: In narrative, "now" marks the progression to a new temporal phase or a shift to the present moment of speaking.

**Examples**:
1. "At first she was reluctant. Now she loves it." (temporal progression)
2. "We used to do it the old way. Now we use the software." (then-vs-now contrast)
3. "Now, at this point in the story, things get complicated." (narrative progression)

**What happens if it's missing**: Without "now," topic shifts can feel unmarked and sudden. "Here's the interesting part" without "Now" lacks the attention-directing quality.

### "THEN" -- Functions

#### Function 1: Sequential/Temporal Ordering

**What it does**: "Then" marks the next step in a sequence of events or actions.

**What it signals to the listener**: "The following event/action comes next in the sequence."

**Examples**:
1. "First we'll check in, then we'll find our seats." (procedural sequence)
2. "She opened the door, then she screamed." (narrative sequence)
3. "You add the flour, then the eggs." (instructional sequence)

#### Function 2: Consequence/Conditional Result

**What it does**: "Then" can mark the consequent of a conditional ("if... then...") or an inference.

**Examples**:
1. "If that's the case, then we need to change our approach." (conditional result)
2. "You don't want to go? Then stay home." (consequence of a choice)
3. "If it rains, then we'll go to the museum instead." (contingency plan)

#### Function 3: Topic Resumption ("...then?")

**What it does**: "Then" at the end of a question can signal a return to a previous concern or a seeking of updated information.

**Examples**:
1. "So what happened then?" (requesting continuation of narrative)
2. "What should we do then?" (seeking a decision after discussion)
3. "Where are we going then?" (seeking confirmation after deliberation)

### "AND THEN" -- Functions

#### Function 1: Narrative Sequencing

**What it does**: "And then" is the basic narrative connector, linking one event to the next. It is one of the first discourse markers children acquire, forming the backbone of early narrative structure. It signals temporal succession and, often, mild causality.

**What it signals to the listener**: "The next event in the story is coming."

**Examples**:
1. "We went to the store, and then we went to the park." (simple narrative sequence)
2. "She said hello, and then she sat down, and then the waiter came over." (extended sequence)
3. "I was just sitting there and then suddenly the lights went out." (sequence with surprise element)
4. "And then what happened?" (prompting narrative continuation)

#### Function 2: Climax/Surprise Building

**What it does**: "And then" can create anticipation by linking events in a way that builds toward a climax or punchline.

**Examples**:
1. "Everything was going fine, and then..." [dramatic pause] (building suspense)
2. "She walked in, and then she saw the surprise party." (climactic moment)
3. "We looked everywhere and then, right at the last minute, we found it." (resolution after buildup)

**What happens if it's missing**: Without "and then," narrative sequences lack clear temporal linkage. "We went to the store. We went to the park." is a list of events without clear ordering. "And then" explicitly marks one as following the other.

### Typical Position in Conversation

- **"Now"**: Turn-initial or clause-initial, before the important content
- **"Then"**: Usually clause-initial or immediately after a conditional clause
- **"And then"**: Clause-initial, linking sequential clauses

### Common Misconceptions

1. **"Now always refers to the present time"**: As a discourse marker, "now" often has nothing to do with present time -- "Now, I want to be clear" could be said at any point in a conversation.
2. **"And then is childish"**: While "and then" is among the first connectors children learn, it remains a fundamental narrative tool used by adults at all levels of sophistication.
3. **"Then and and then are identical"**: "And then" explicitly signals continuation and connection; "then" alone can mark contrast, conditionality, or sequence depending on context.

## 12. "JUST" -- The Minimizer

### Overview

"Just" is one of the most pragmatically complex words in English. Lee (1987) identified multiple senses: depreciatory, restrictive, specificatory, and emphatic. Research at Brown University (Emet, 2017) explored the meaning and discourse role of "just," noting that while "'I think' may be quite tentative, 'I just think' conveys decisiveness." The word has been at the center of a controversy about gendered language advice, with the "Just Not Sorry" Gmail plugin (2015) highlighting "just" as an "undermining word" -- a claim that linguist Deborah Cameron sharply criticized.

### Key Academic Sources

- **Lee (1987)**: "Categories in the description of just" -- identified multiple semantic-pragmatic functions
- **Emet (2017)**: "The meaning and discourse role of just" (Brown University)
- **Beeching (2016)**: Analysis in *Pragmatic markers in British English*
- **Cameron**: Critique of prescriptive advice about "just" in emails
- **Wiegand (2019)**: Analysis of "just" as an exclusive/emphatic marker

### Pragmatic Function 1: Minimizer/Hedge

**What it does**: "Just" minimizes the imposition, significance, or effort associated with an action or request. This is a politeness strategy: by presenting something as small and unimportant, the speaker reduces the face-threat. Hedging -- "using linguistic devices that reduce the strength of your commitment to a proposition" -- is "often done for purposes of politeness, or in other words to make whatever you're saying or doing more palatable to the other person."

**What it signals to the listener**: "This is a small thing. Don't worry about it. I'm not asking much."

**Examples**:
1. "I just wanted to ask you a quick question." (minimizing the imposition of the request)
2. "I was just wondering if you'd got my email." (hedged request for action)
3. "I'm just checking in." (minimizing the intrusion)
4. "Could I just borrow your pen for a second?" (minimizing the favor)
5. "I just need five minutes of your time." (minimizing the time commitment)

**What happens if it's missing**: Without "just," requests become more direct and potentially more imposing. "I wanted to ask you a question" is more direct than "I just wanted to ask you a quick question." The "just" adds a layer of politeness by suggesting the request is minor.

### Pragmatic Function 2: Restrictive/Exclusive ("only")

**What it does**: "Just" functions as a synonym for "only," restricting the referent to a minimal value.

**What it signals to the listener**: "Only this and nothing more."

**Examples**:
1. "I just had one question." (= only one, not many)
2. "She just arrived." (= only just now, very recently)
3. "It's just me." (= only me, nobody else)
4. "We just need the signature." (= that's the only remaining requirement)
5. "I'm just a student." (= I'm nothing more than a student -- self-deprecation)

### Pragmatic Function 3: Emphatic ("just do it")

**What it does**: In emphatic use, "just" intensifies rather than minimizes. This is the opposite end of its functional spectrum. Research shows that "I just think" can convey decisiveness -- "end of story" or "I've made up my mind."

**What it signals to the listener**: "Do this without hesitation, qualification, or delay."

**Examples**:
1. "Just do it." (emphatic command -- no hedging or hesitation)
2. "Just tell them the truth." (emphatic advice -- straightforward action)
3. "I just think it's wrong." (emphatic opinion -- not tentative)
4. "Just listen to me for one second." (emphatic request for attention)
5. "It's just so frustrating!" (emphatic expression of emotion)

### Pragmatic Function 4: Specificatory/Exactness

**What it does**: "Just" can mean "exactly" or "precisely."

**What it signals to the listener**: "This is precisely right. Not approximately, but exactly."

**Examples**:
1. "That's just what I needed." (= exactly what I needed)
2. "It's just right." (= perfect, exactly correct)
3. "That's just the point." (= exactly the point)
4. "You're just the person I wanted to see." (= precisely the right person)

### The Cameron Controversy: "Just" in Emails

**Background**: In 2015, the Chrome extension "Just Not Sorry" was launched, inspired by advice literature (Tara Mohr and others) suggesting that women should stop using "just," "sorry," and similar words in emails because they "undermine your message." The plugin highlighted these words as they were typed, warning users they were using "undermining language."

**The linguistic critique**: Deborah Cameron, the prominent feminist linguist and author of *Verbal Hygiene*, strongly criticized this approach. Her core arguments were:

1. **Words have multiple functions**: "Just" has "a range of functions" and "cannot simply be asserted to be 'demeaning' in every context." The minimizer "just" in "I just wanted to ask" is a politeness strategy, not a weakness.

2. **Hedging is a skill, not a deficit**: "When you ask someone to do something you're imposing on them: showing you're aware of that, and trying to minimize the imposition, is a basic form of politeness." Removing hedges doesn't make communication more effective -- it makes it ruder.

3. **The gendered framing is problematic**: Data from telephone corpus studies suggest that "men hedge just as much as women, they just use different linguistic forms to do it." The advice to stop saying "just" targets women's linguistic strategies while leaving equivalent male strategies unmarked.

4. **Prescriptive advice oversimplifies**: Cameron argued that the generalization about women's speech being "undermining" is an "over-generalization" -- "none are true for every woman in every context."

**Why this matters for the knowledge base**: The "just" controversy illustrates a broader principle: discourse markers serve real pragmatic functions. Advice to eliminate them misunderstands their purpose. "I just wanted to ask" is not a sign of insecurity -- it's a sophisticated politeness strategy that manages the social dynamics of making a request.

### Typical Position in Conversation

"Just" appears primarily in **pre-verbal** position ("I just wanted," "just tell them") or **pre-NP** position ("just one question"). The emphatic function often places "just" in initial position ("Just do it").

### Common Misconceptions

1. **"Just always weakens a statement"**: "Just" can strengthen (emphatic function) or specify (exactness function) as well as weaken.
2. **"Using 'just' shows lack of confidence"**: The minimizing function is a deliberate politeness strategy, not an involuntary sign of insecurity.
3. **"Women should stop saying 'just'"**: This prescriptive advice, popularized by self-help literature and apps like "Just Not Sorry," was critiqued by linguists as misunderstanding the pragmatic functions of hedging.
4. **"Just is always a discourse marker"**: "Just" has adverbial meanings (= "recently": "I just arrived"; = "barely": "I just made it") that are distinct from its discourse marker functions.

## Cross-Cutting Themes

### What Happens When Discourse Markers Are Missing?

Research consistently shows that removing discourse markers from speech produces specific negative effects:

1. **Abruptness**: Responses feel sudden and unprepared. A question followed by a bare answer without "well" or "so" lacks cushioning.
2. **Disjointed quality**: Without markers like "anyway," "so," or "now," topic transitions are unmarked and conversations feel fragmented.
3. **Impoliteness**: Without hedging markers ("well," "just," "you know"), direct statements can be perceived as rude, aggressive, or dogmatic.
4. **Robotic quality**: Google's Conversation Design guidelines explicitly note that speech without discourse markers sounds "robotic and impersonal."
5. **Increased processing burden**: The listener must do extra cognitive work to figure out how utterances relate to each other. Discourse markers are "procedural" -- they tell the listener HOW to process what follows.
6. **Social distance**: Without interpersonal markers like "you know," conversation lacks rapport-building and feels transactional.

Research has confirmed that when discourse markers are absent, utterances are judged as "unnatural," "awkward," "disjointed," "impolite," "unfriendly," or "dogmatic."

### Discourse Markers and Autism

Research findings relevant to autistic individuals:

1. **Mixed usage patterns**: Evidence shows that discourse marker use is "divergent in autism," with some markers (like "um") used less frequently. However, Jones et al. (2022) found that autistic children use "like" at comparable rates to peers.

2. **Pragmatic function understanding**: ASD is associated with "reduced sensitivity to pragmatic demands of interaction, including adapting language to signal stance, urgency, or interpersonal alignment." Understanding the explicit rules behind discourse markers can help bridge this gap.

3. **Discourse markers as learnable rules**: Because discourse markers follow systematic, rule-governed patterns (not arbitrary social conventions), they are particularly amenable to explicit teaching. Each marker has identifiable functions, typical positions, and predictable effects.

4. **The "hidden curriculum" of conversation**: Discourse markers are part of what is sometimes called the "hidden curriculum" of social communication. Making their functions explicit -- as this knowledge base does -- is the key intervention for individuals who don't acquire them implicitly.
