# Concept Research: Co-Regulation, Social Engagement & Polyvagal-Informed Therapy

## Overview

Polyvagal Theory's deepest practical footprint is not in laboratory psychophysiology — where the theory is actively contested as of 2026 (see Category 3) — but in the clinical world. A generation of trauma-informed therapists has built training programs, books, and clinical curricula around polyvagal vocabulary. Deb Dana's *Rhythm of Regulation* framework is the canonical clinical operationalisation, with three Norton books (2018, 2020, 2021) and a sequenced training series through the Polyvagal Institute. Polyvagal language has been absorbed by EMDR (Rebecca Kase 2023), Somatic Experiencing (Peter Levine, 40+ year collaboration with Porges since 1978), Sensorimotor Psychotherapy (Pat Ogden, Hakomi lineage), and Internal Family Systems (IFS therapists explicitly map Self-energy onto ventral vagal activation). Therapists working with autistic and traumatised adults will encounter polyvagal vocabulary almost everywhere they look.

The crucial analytical question for an autistic adult evaluating these therapies is **separability**: which of the actual therapeutic practices *require* polyvagal theory to be biologically correct, and which would work for reasons that don't depend on Porges's contested anatomical claims? The short answer — developed across this chapter — is that **most of the load-bearing practices predate polyvagal theory and have their own evidence bases**. Attuned therapeutic presence is grounded in Bowlby and Ainsworth's attachment theory (1960s–1970s, well before PVT). Paced breathing is established cardiovascular physiology (Lehrer, Bernardi 1990s onward). Titration and pendulation were coined by Peter Levine in the 1970s as Somatic Experiencing developed; Levine's first meeting with Porges was 1978, but SE's clinical structure was already taking shape independently. Body-awareness work in psychotherapy traces back through Hakomi, Reich, Gestalt, and the somatic tradition broadly. The "polyvagal ladder" itself — Dana's signature pedagogical tool — is psychological scaffolding for clients, not a measured nervous-system state in real time.

This has two practical implications for the user. **One**: polyvagal-informed therapy practices can be genuinely helpful and worth pursuing even if polyvagal theory turns out to be wrong about specific brainstem nuclei, because most of the active ingredients have independent evidence bases. **Two**: when a therapist or program rests its claims *specifically on the polyvagal mechanism* (e.g., "we activate your ventral vagal complex"), the user should treat that claim with the same scrutiny they would apply to any contested neuroscience claim. The practices may still help; the mechanism story may still be a metaphor. The chapter also adds a critical layer the user requested: **mental health comorbidities**. Anxiety, depression, and PTSD co-occur with autism at rates of 28–42% (anxiety), 23–37% (depression), and 11–60% (PTSD, lifetime, by population) — and an autistic adult navigating polyvagal-informed therapy is almost certainly navigating these comorbidities too. Beauchaine's 2015 RSA-as-transdiagnostic-biomarker paper provides the integrating framework: low resting RSA is a non-specific marker of emotion-regulation difficulty *across* psychopathology, which means PVT-informed practices that improve RSA are doing real cardiovascular work — but their specificity to polyvagal mechanism is exactly what the transdiagnostic finding undermines.

## Core Components

### Component 1: Deb Dana's Rhythm of Regulation Framework

- **Purpose**: Translate polyvagal theory into a teachable, client-facing clinical practice.
- **Inputs**: Porges's three-state hierarchy (ventral vagal / sympathetic / dorsal vagal); attachment theory's emphasis on relational safety; structured worksheet pedagogy from social-work practice.
- **Outputs**: A reproducible therapeutic curriculum centred on four verbs — **befriend, map, navigate, shape** — that therapists apply across session arcs and clients apply between sessions.
- **Relationships**: Foundational dependency on Porges (Category 1); Dana sits on Polyvagal Institute founding board and is paid advisor to Unyte Health (Category 8 — commercial-interest map); her clinical writings are the most-circulated polyvagal application of the past decade.
- **Biographical anchor**: Deb Dana, LCSW (licensed clinical social worker). Coordinator of the Traumatic Stress Research Consortium at the Kinsey Institute, Indiana University. Founding member of the Polyvagal Institute. Consultant to Khiron Clinics. Advisor to Unyte Health.
- **Published spine of the framework**:
  - *The Polyvagal Theory in Therapy: Engaging the Rhythm of Regulation* (Norton, **2018**) — foreword by Porges; the foundational clinical text.
  - *Polyvagal Exercises for Safety and Connection: 50 Client-Centered Practices* (Norton, **2020**) — fifty named exercises, indexed by autonomic state target.
  - *Anchored: How to Befriend Your Nervous System Using Polyvagal Theory* (Sounds True, **2021**) — the trade book for clients (not therapists).
  - *Polyvagal Card Deck*, *Polyvagal Flip Chart*, *Polyvagal Practices* — supporting clinical tools.
- **Four-verb curriculum**:
  1. **Befriend** — Build relationship with the autonomic nervous system as something one can come to know rather than something happening *to* one. Anti-shame framing: "your nervous system is trying to keep you safe."
  2. **Map** — Personal Profile Map and Triggers/Glimmers Map. The client identifies specific cues that move them up or down the ladder. Glimmer = small cue of safety; trigger = small cue of threat.
  3. **Navigate** — Real-time skills for shifting state, including paced breathing, body posture, vocal prosody, environment changes, social contact.
  4. **Shape** — Repeated practice over time to broaden the window of ventral access; building "neural pathways of safety."
- **The polyvagal ladder** — Dana's signature visual metaphor. A vertical ladder with ventral vagal (green) at top, sympathetic (yellow/amber) in middle, dorsal vagal (red) at bottom. Used as a real-time self-mapping tool in session and between sessions.
- **Session structure** — In Dana's training and consulting work, she emphasises an opening that establishes ventral-vagal-to-ventral-vagal connection (therapist regulated, attuned), a middle that may move into activation work (titrated approach to sympathetic or dorsal material), and a closing that re-establishes ventral state before the client leaves. This is *not* unique to PVT — it parallels the well-established arousal-regulation arc taught in trauma-informed CBT, EMDR's "container" exercises, and Hakomi's tracking practices.

### Component 2: Co-Regulation as Therapeutic Stance

- **Purpose**: The mechanism by which one regulated nervous system supports a dysregulated one through prosody, facial expression, sustained attention, and embodied calm.
- **Inputs**: Bowlby's attachment theory (1969–1980); Ainsworth's Strange Situation paradigm (1969–1978); Sroufe's formalisation of co-regulation as dyadic emotion regulation (1996); Schore's right-brain neurobiology of attachment (2000s); Porges's reframing in PVT terms (1995 onwards).
- **Outputs**: A therapeutic stance the therapist embodies before any specific technique is applied. Distinguished from "regulation skills training" (teaching the client to self-regulate) by being a relational *quality* the therapist brings.
- **Relationships**: The independent evidence base in attachment theory is *much older* than PVT. Bowlby's volume one of the *Attachment and Loss* trilogy was published 1969, well before Porges's 1994 presidential address. Ainsworth's Strange Situation publications are 1969–1978. The neural-mechanism story is something polyvagal theory offers *on top of* an already well-established psychological phenomenon.
- **Key insight for the user**: When a clinician says "I co-regulate with my client," this is overwhelmingly a description of *attachment-theory-grounded* therapeutic presence. Whether the underlying biological substrate is best described by PVT, NVI, or something else does not change the practice. The relational quality is what helps; the explanation of why it helps is what is contested.
- **Sroufe quote** (paraphrased from the 1996 textbook *Emotional Development*): attachment is "the dyadic regulation of emotion" — the *infant–caregiver pair* is the regulatory unit, not the infant alone.

### Component 3: Polyvagal-Informed EMDR (Rebecca Kase)

- **Purpose**: Add a polyvagal-theory orientation to the eight-phase EMDR Standard Protocol without modifying the protocol itself.
- **Inputs**: Francine Shapiro's EMDR Standard Protocol (1989, refined through the 1990s, eight phases); polyvagal vocabulary for autonomic state assessment.
- **Outputs**: Case conceptualisation through a polyvagal lens; modified history-taking (mapping the client's autonomic patterns); attention to therapeutic presence and pacing during phase 4 (desensitisation); enhanced container exercises that explicitly reference autonomic state.
- **Relationships**: Kase explicitly maintains fidelity to the EMDR evidence base — she is not modifying what is being done so much as offering a theoretical orientation for *why* and *when* certain phases are applied.
- **Published spine**: *Polyvagal-Informed EMDR: A Neuro-Informed Approach to Healing* (W. W. Norton, **23 May 2023**, ISBN 978-1-324-03031-7). EMDR International Association (EMDRIA) has hosted Kase on podcasts and offers continuing-education courses.
- **What is actually added**: 
  - Neuro-informed history-taking: mapping the client's autonomic patterns (which states they spend most time in, what triggers shifts, what glimmers exist).
  - Polyvagal vocabulary for the client to use in tracking session experience ("I'm feeling dorsal" or "I'm in ventral right now").
  - Clearer language around pacing — when to slow down because the client is sliding into sympathetic over-arousal or dorsal shutdown.
  - Explicit emphasis on therapeutic presence as a co-regulating influence.
- **What is NOT modified**: The eight phases themselves remain intact. Bilateral stimulation (eye movements, tones, taps) is unchanged. Standard installation, body scan, closure, and re-evaluation phases proceed as in standard EMDR.
- **Critical evaluation**: Kase's framework can be read two ways:
  - **Therapeutically generous reading**: It adds vocabulary that helps clinicians attune to where the client is in any given moment and gives clients a usable self-mapping tool. None of these benefits require PVT being literally correct.
  - **Skeptical reading**: If the polyvagal orientation doesn't modify the protocol and EMDR's existing evidence base already supports its phases, then the polyvagal layer is doing primarily *psychoeducational* work — labelling and language — not adding a new mechanism. The underlying EMDR evidence base is doing the therapeutic heavy lifting.

### Component 4: Somatic Experiencing (Peter Levine) and Its Relation to PVT

- **Purpose**: A trauma therapy emphasising body-based tracking of physical sensations, titration, pendulation, and discharge of stored survival energy.
- **Inputs**: Levine's biological-and-ethological observations (1970s onwards); his PhD work in medical biophysics; Bessel van der Kolk's body-based work; the broader somatic-therapy tradition (Reich, Lowen, bioenergetics).
- **Outputs**: A trauma therapy taught through Somatic Experiencing International (SE International), now offering training programs in over 40 countries.
- **Relationships with PVT**:
  - **Independent origin**: SE's core techniques (titration, pendulation, tracking sensation, discharge) were developed by Levine in the 1970s, with his first writings appearing in the 1970s–1980s.
  - **Levine–Porges collaboration**: First meeting in 1978; what SE practitioners describe as "40 years of fraternal collaboration." Porges has appeared in SE training materials; the two have co-presented.
  - **Adoption of PVT vocabulary**: Modern SE materials make heavy use of the three-state hierarchy. Some SE materials describe collapse as "dorsal vagal shutdown" — which is exactly the kind of claim the 2026 Untenable consensus critiques (PVT's specific anatomical claim that bradycardia/collapse is *dorsal* vagal turns out to be inconsistent with comparative neuroanatomy; the bradycardic vagal response is overwhelmingly *nucleus-ambiguus*-mediated in mammals).
- **Independent SE evidence**:
  - **Brom et al. 2017** (Journal of Traumatic Stress) — first SE-specific RCT. n = 63 (33 SE / 30 waitlist). 15 weekly SE sessions. Large effect sizes: d = 0.94–1.26 for PTSD symptoms, d = 0.70–1.08 for depression symptoms. 44.1% lost the PTSD diagnosis.
  - **Andersen et al. 2017** (PubMed 28680540) — RCT of brief SE for chronic low back pain with comorbid PTSD symptoms.
  - **Payne, Levine & Crane-Godreau 2015** — theoretical paper on SE mechanisms in *Frontiers in Psychology*.
  - Several pilot studies in cancer survivors, refugees, and earthquake survivors.
- **Critical perspective**: SE's evidence base is smaller than CBT or PE for PTSD, but it does have RCT support that is independent of PVT being correct. If you separate "the techniques of titration, pendulation, somatic tracking, and discharge" from "the polyvagal explanation of why they work," the techniques retain their evidence base.

### Component 5: Sensorimotor Psychotherapy (Pat Ogden) and Its Relation to PVT

- **Purpose**: A body-oriented trauma psychotherapy combining mindfulness, somatic tracking, and cognitive integration.
- **Inputs**: Hakomi method (Ron Kurtz, 1970s onwards); Gestalt therapy; mindfulness practices; attachment theory; trauma neuroscience.
- **Outputs**: Sensorimotor Psychotherapy Institute (SPI) training programs; Pat Ogden's foundational text *Trauma and the Body: A Sensorimotor Approach to Psychotherapy* (Norton, 2006, in the Interpersonal Neurobiology series alongside Schore, Siegel, van der Kolk).
- **Origin and lineage** (importantly *predates* PVT):
  - Early 1970s: Pat Ogden begins integrating somatic and psychotherapy approaches.
  - 1970s–1980s: Ogden apprentices with Ron Kurtz; trains in Hakomi.
  - 1981: Co-founds the Hakomi Institute.
  - Mid-1980s onwards: Founds Hakomi Bodywork, later Hakomi Integrative Somatics.
  - 2000s: Sensorimotor Psychotherapy Institute formally established.
  - 2002: First published Sensorimotor Psychotherapy paper.
  - 2006: *Trauma and the Body* published.
- **Relationship with PVT**: Modern SP materials integrate polyvagal vocabulary, but the method's foundational components (body-awareness tracking, mindful sensation work, phase-oriented treatment) were developed independently of PVT. Polyvagal language was added to a framework that was already substantively complete.

### Component 6: Internal Family Systems (IFS) Integration with Polyvagal Language

- **Purpose**: IFS is a parts-based therapy developed by Richard Schwartz (1980s–1990s) where the therapist helps the client identify and relate to "parts" of self (Exiles, Managers, Firefighters) from a "Self" position characterised by what Schwartz calls the eight C's: calm, curiosity, compassion, clarity, confidence, courage, creativity, connectedness.
- **Inputs**: Family systems therapy lineage (Bowen, Minuchin); Schwartz's clinical observations of multiplicity in eating-disorders treatment; integration with attachment theory in the 2000s; later cross-fertilisation with polyvagal language in the 2010s onwards.
- **PVT integration claim**: IFS therapists working in the polyvagal-informed integration explicitly map **Self-energy onto ventral vagal activation**. When a client is in Self (the eight C's), their nervous system is in ventral vagal state. When parts take over, the client moves into sympathetic (Firefighters) or dorsal (some Exile presentations). The therapist's job is to support Self-leadership, which is also support for ventral vagal anchoring.
- **Cross-reference**: The user already has a substantial IFS deep dive in this project at `output-final/ifs-autism-alexithymia-trauma-deep-dive/`. That deep dive covers IFS's independent evidence base, its application to autism and alexithymia, its commercial and licensing structure (IFS Institute, Schwartz's certification programs), and its own controversies. This Category 7 should *cross-reference* that work rather than re-cover it.
- **Critical evaluation of the IFS-PVT integration**:
  - **Generous reading**: The eight C's and ventral vagal both describe a "regulated, relationally-open state." Calling them the same state is observationally reasonable and gives the clinician a unified vocabulary across two productive frameworks.
  - **Skeptical reading**: This is *vocabulary alignment*, not mechanism. IFS's effectiveness data (such as it is) was developed without PVT; PVT's mechanism claims are independent of IFS. Mapping one onto the other is an interpretive convenience for the therapist, not an additional evidence claim.
  - **Where it matters most for an autistic adult**: IFS is one of the therapy modalities where polyvagal language has been most enthusiastically absorbed. If you encounter an IFS therapist who refers heavily to polyvagal mechanism (rather than just the descriptive ladder), apply the same scrutiny you would to any therapist resting heavily on contested neuroscience.

### Component 7: Mental Health Comorbidities — Anxiety, Depression, PTSD

- **Purpose**: Connect PVT's clinical claims to the empirical literature on RSA and psychopathology, particularly relevant for autistic adults who overwhelmingly carry one or more of these comorbidities.
- **Inputs**: Beauchaine 2015 RSA-as-transdiagnostic-biomarker (PMC4389219); Beauchaine et al. 2019 RSA reactivity meta-analysis across psychopathology dimensions (PMC6453712); the broader RSA-anxiety literature; autism-comorbidity epidemiology.
- **Outputs**: Evidence-based context for how PVT-informed therapies position themselves to treat these conditions.
- **Key epidemiological anchors** (autism + comorbidity):
  - **Anxiety disorders**: ~28–42% lifetime prevalence in autistic adults (varies by study and population; pooled estimates from autism-specific psychiatric comorbidity meta-analyses).
  - **Depression**: ~23–37% lifetime prevalence in autistic adults.
  - **PTSD**: Wide range (~11–60% by population; higher in autistic adults with significant trauma histories). Importantly, PTSD presentation in autism may be different from neurotypical presentation, and standard PTSD screening can underdiagnose.
  - **At least one psychiatric diagnosis**: ~70% of autistic individuals carry at least one comorbid psychiatric diagnosis; ~40% carry two or more.
- **RSA findings across these conditions**:
  - **Beauchaine 2015** (*Current Opinion in Psychology*, PMC4389219): Low resting RSA is reliably observed across internalising disorders (depression, anxiety), externalising disorders (conduct disorder), schizophrenia, autism spectrum disorder, and nonsuicidal self-injury. This is what makes RSA a *transdiagnostic* biomarker — its low-resting signal is non-specific to any one condition.
  - **Beauchaine et al. 2019** (*Psychophysiology*, PMC6453712): Meta-analysis of RSA *reactivity* across structural psychopathology dimensions. Findings are more mixed than resting RSA. RSA *withdrawal* (active reduction in response to stress) is most consistently associated with externalising behaviour dimensions; for internalising (depression, anxiety) and thought-problem dimensions, the pattern is less consistent.
  - **Resting RSA and anxiety disorders meta-analysis** (Chalmers et al.): n = 2086 patients with anxiety disorders / n = 2294 controls / 36 studies. Confirmed lower HRV (high-frequency component) in anxiety disorders.
  - **PTSD-specific resting RSA meta-analysis** (PubMed 30779926): Lower resting RSA in PTSD relative to controls.
- **Key implication for the user**: The transdiagnostic nature of low RSA cuts against PVT's specificity claims. If autistic adults, depressed adults, anxious adults, traumatised adults, conduct-disordered adolescents, and people with NSSI all show low resting RSA, then "low RSA" by itself does not localise to a specific polyvagal-defined dysregulation. Anything that improves RSA may help, but the improvement does not specifically validate the polyvagal mechanism.
- **Treatment-selection implication**: PVT-informed therapies that treat "the nervous system" as the unit of intervention may genuinely help across these comorbidities — but no more than (and possibly less than) gold-standard treatments for each specific condition (CBT for anxiety, behavioral activation or CBT for depression, PE/EMDR/CPT for PTSD). For an autistic adult with multiple comorbidities, the question is not "should I do PVT-informed therapy *or* gold-standard treatment for my specific condition?" — it's "what combination addresses my actual presenting picture?"

### Component 8: The Critical Evaluation — Which Practices Actually Require PVT?

This is the central analytic question the chapter exists to answer. The user wants to know which therapy practices are worth pursuing without buying the contested theory.

**Practices that DO NOT require PVT being correct**:
- **Attuned therapeutic presence**: Predates PVT by decades. Grounded in attachment theory (Bowlby, Ainsworth, Sroufe), Rogers's client-centered therapy, and the broader humanistic tradition. Evidence base in alliance/relationship research is among the most robust in psychotherapy.
- **Paced breathing in session**: Well-established cardiovascular physiology. Lehrer, Bernardi, and others have published extensively on slow-paced breathing and HRV from the 1990s onward. FDA-cleared device-guided slow breathing (e.g., RESPeRATE) treats hypertension. AHA recognises slow breathing as adjunctive treatment.
- **Titrated arousal / pendulation**: Coined by Peter Levine in the 1970s as Somatic Experiencing developed. Predates Levine's first meeting with Porges (1978) and well predates PVT (1994).
- **Co-regulation**: Attachment-theory-grounded since Bowlby (1969). Sroufe explicitly defined attachment as "dyadic regulation of emotion" in 1996. The neural mechanism story PVT offers is post-hoc explanation, not the source of the clinical practice.
- **Body awareness / tracking somatic sensations**: Somatic tradition predates PVT through Hakomi (Kurtz 1970s), bioenergetics (Lowen 1958), Gestalt body-awareness work (Perls), Reich (1930s), and the broader somatic-psychology field.
- **Mindfulness-based interventions**: MBSR (Kabat-Zinn 1979), MBCT (Segal, Williams, Teasdale 2001), DBT mindfulness module (Linehan 1993) — all predate or are independent of PVT.
- **EMDR's standard eight phases**: Shapiro's original work 1989, refined through the 1990s. Evidence base built before PVT-informed adaptations.
- **IFS as a parts-based therapy**: Schwartz's IFS predates the IFS-PVT integration. The Self-led therapeutic stance was already articulated independently.

**Practices that USE PVT framing specifically (but may or may not depend on it)**:
- **The polyvagal ladder as psychoeducation**: This is a teaching tool. It works as psychological scaffolding regardless of whether the underlying nervous-system claim is biologically accurate. A client who learns "I notice I'm in dorsal" gets a vocabulary for self-tracking; whether this vocabulary maps onto actual dorsal motor nucleus activity is a separate empirical question.
- **Specific "ventral vagal activation" claims**: Some interventions market themselves on the specific mechanism of activating the ventral vagal complex (notably SSP — Safe and Sound Protocol). Here the mechanism claim is load-bearing. If PVT's specific anatomical predictions don't hold, the mechanism story falls.
- **Glimmers and triggers**: The vocabulary is polyvagal-specific (Dana's coinage). The practice — noticing small cues of safety and small cues of threat — is generalisable.
- **Polyvagal-informed history-taking**: The vocabulary and emphasis are polyvagal; the underlying practice of mapping a client's emotional patterns is universal across trauma therapies.

### Component 9: Scaffolding vs Mechanism — The Conceptual Distinction

- **Purpose**: Articulate the difference between PVT as *psychological scaffolding for clients* and PVT as *biological mechanism claim*.
- **Scaffolding use**:
  - The ladder gives clients a vocabulary for self-tracking.
  - It externalises the experience of dysregulation ("my nervous system is in survival mode," not "I am broken").
  - It supports compassionate self-reflection rather than self-blame.
  - It opens the door to body-based interventions without requiring the client to first agree that they have a psychological problem.
  - **None of this requires PVT being biologically correct** — it requires it to be psychologically useful.
- **Mechanism use**:
  - Claims about specific brainstem nuclei (DMN vs NA).
  - Claims about evolutionary phylogeny (reptilian vs mammalian vagal control).
  - Claims that the social engagement system is biologically distinct from other autonomic regulation.
  - Claims that specific interventions (SSP, certain devices) activate the ventral vagal complex specifically.
  - **These claims are exactly what the 2026 Untenable consensus disputes** (Category 3).
- **The user's practical takeaway**: When a therapist or program uses polyvagal language as scaffolding (a way of teaching self-tracking and self-compassion), the practice may be worth engaging with even if the underlying theory is contested. When a therapist or program rests *specifically on the mechanism* (claiming to activate or change a specific neural pathway), apply the same scrutiny you would to any contested neuroscience claim.

## How It Works

### Step 1: Initial Therapeutic Encounter
The polyvagal-informed therapist typically opens by attempting to *establish ventral-vagal connection* — slow voice, attuned gaze, regulated breathing, a calm presence. This is co-regulation in attachment-theory terms; it is also the relationship-quality variable that consistently predicts therapy outcomes regardless of theoretical orientation. PVT adds a vocabulary for what is being done, not a new technique.

### Step 2: Autonomic Mapping (Dana's Framework)
The therapist works with the client to build their Personal Profile Map: which states they spend most time in, what cues move them between states, what their unique constellation of triggers and glimmers looks like. This is a structured assessment of the client's emotional regulation patterns. It parallels the case formulation process in any trauma-informed therapy; the polyvagal vocabulary is added.

### Step 3: In-Session State Tracking
During session, the therapist and client periodically check the client's state on the ladder. "Where are you on the ladder right now?" This is a structured form of arousal monitoring, comparable to using a SUDS (Subjective Units of Distress) scale in CBT or a 0–10 distress scale in EMDR. The polyvagal vocabulary makes it conversational.

### Step 4: Navigation Skills
When the client identifies they are out of ventral, the therapist supports them in returning. Tools include paced breathing, body movement, sensory anchoring, social contact, prosody/music. **The tools themselves overlap heavily with what any trauma-informed therapy uses.** What's branded as polyvagal is overwhelmingly the vocabulary and the explicit autonomic-state targeting.

### Step 5: Shaping (Between-Session Practice)
The client practices state-shifting between sessions, building repertoire. Dana's books offer named exercises; clients work with the therapist to choose which to integrate. This parallels the homework structure in CBT.

### Step 6: Comorbidity Layer (For Autistic Adults)
Because most autistic adults present with at least one comorbid condition (anxiety, depression, PTSD), the polyvagal-informed therapist may treat across these conditions using polyvagal vocabulary as the integrative frame. The Beauchaine 2015 transdiagnostic biomarker work supports the idea that emotion-regulation interventions help across conditions. It also undermines the claim that PVT specifically explains autism, since the same RSA findings appear in depression, anxiety, and PTSD non-autistic samples.

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: The "Polyvagal-Informed EMDR" Therapy Session

A clinician trained in EMDR who has read Kase's 2023 book conducts a desensitisation session. Standard protocol: target identified, SUDS rating, bilateral stimulation, etc. The polyvagal-informed additions include: at the start, the therapist explicitly checks where the client is on the ladder; when the SUDS climbs during processing, the therapist watches for signs of sympathetic over-arousal or dorsal collapse rather than relying solely on the SUDS score; if the client moves toward dorsal, the therapist pauses to support ventral re-anchoring before resuming. The *mechanism* of EMDR — bilateral stimulation, target processing — is unchanged. What's added is pacing sensitivity and a shared vocabulary.

### Example 2: Dana's Triggers/Glimmers Map for an Autistic Adult with Burnout

The client identifies their triggers (sensory overload in supermarkets, unstructured social demands, unexpected schedule changes, masking pressure) and their glimmers (specific friend's voice, deep pressure, predictable routines, sensory-friendly environments, certain music, time with animals). The map externalises these as patterns rather than personal failings. The autistic-specific resonance is strong: many of these are well-established autistic accommodations under any framework.

### Example 3: An IFS Session Using Polyvagal Vocabulary

The client identifies a "shutdown part" — an exiled part that takes over when overwhelm becomes too much. The IFS-trained therapist working with PVT vocabulary helps the client notice that they are in dorsal when this part is active; the work then involves accessing Self-energy (ventral) to be with the shutdown part. The mechanism PVT proposes (specific brainstem activation patterns) is not the operational frame for the session — the frame is the client's felt sense of states and parts.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: "Polyvagal-informed therapy is fundamentally different from other trauma therapies."
  - **Reality**: The core practices (attuned presence, paced arousal regulation, body awareness, somatic tracking) overlap heavily with other trauma-informed approaches. PVT adds a vocabulary and an explicit autonomic-state targeting; it does not introduce fundamentally new techniques.

- **Misconception**: "If polyvagal-informed therapy helps me, that validates polyvagal theory."
  - **Reality**: The therapy can help for reasons that don't require PVT being right (attachment-based co-regulation, well-established breathing physiology, the somatic tradition's body-awareness practices, the alliance/relationship variable). Therapeutic benefit does not specifically validate the contested neuroscience.

- **Misconception**: "Polyvagal-informed therapists are doing something biologically specific to my vagus nerve."
  - **Reality**: The interventions used in session (talking, breathing, body awareness, mindful attention) have systemic physiological effects, but the claim that they target specific polyvagal-defined pathways is exactly what is contested. Most therapeutic effects do not require this specificity.

- **Misconception**: "Co-regulation is a polyvagal concept."
  - **Reality**: Co-regulation as a clinical and developmental concept predates PVT by more than 25 years. Bowlby, Ainsworth, and Sroufe established the practice and the evidence base; PVT later offered a neural-mechanism story that is now contested.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **The autistic adult who finds the ladder vocabulary unhelpful**: Some autistic adults (particularly those with strong alexithymia features) report that the ladder doesn't map onto their experience — they don't experience clear discrete states, or they experience overlap of "ventral" and "dorsal" simultaneously, or the colour-coding feels arbitrary. This is not a failure of the client; it reflects the ladder's status as a clinical heuristic rather than a measured biological state. Alternative frameworks (predictive processing, allostatic load) may map better.

- **The trauma client where polyvagal language inadvertently scaffolds avoidance**: For some clients, the framing "my nervous system needs to be in ventral first" can become a justification for avoiding necessary therapeutic work. A skilled therapist holds the dialectic — supporting regulation *and* approaching the difficult material. A less skilled application can use polyvagal language to perpetually defer the work.

- **The therapist whose polyvagal training is the only trauma training they've had**: This is the highest-risk scenario for the client. Polyvagal-informed certifications can be obtained without first developing core trauma-therapy competence. An autistic adult should ask about the therapist's prior trauma training and theoretical orientation before assuming polyvagal competence equates to general trauma competence.

## Research Gaps

- **Direct head-to-head evidence**: No RCT compares polyvagal-informed therapy to a matched active control (e.g., trauma-focused CBT delivered with equivalent therapeutic alliance). Existing PVT-informed evidence is largely case-series, pre-post, or modality-internal (e.g., is polyvagal-informed EMDR equivalent to standard EMDR in head-to-head trials? — not yet established).
- **The specificity question**: There is no published study cleanly separating the *vocabulary/scaffolding* contribution of polyvagal language from the *putative mechanism* contribution. Without such a study, the question "does PVT specifically add something?" remains analytically open.
- **Adult autism-specific outcomes**: Most polyvagal-informed therapy publications come from trauma populations broadly. Autism-specific outcome data with adults is sparse — and the IFS-PVT integration in autistic adults specifically is essentially case-study level as of 2026.
- **Long-term outcome data**: Pre-post improvements are common; durable change at 12-month or longer follow-up is much less studied.
- **Iatrogenic potential**: Where polyvagal framing is used to perpetually defer trauma processing ("my nervous system isn't ready yet"), there is anecdotal concern but no systematic study of this failure mode.
