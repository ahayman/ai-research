#### Claim 3: CAT-Q has 25 items, 3 subscales (Compensation 9, Masking 8, Assimilation 8), alpha = 0.94, test-retest r = 0.77

**Rating: CONFIRMED**

**Pro-evidence:**
- All psychometric details confirmed through multiple sources including the original paper (PubMed 30361940, PMC6394586)
- 25 items confirmed with correct subscale structure (Compensation 9 items, Masking 8 items, Assimilation 8 items)
- Internal consistency alpha = 0.94 confirmed
- Test-retest reliability r = 0.77 confirmed
- Sample: 354 autistic and 478 non-autistic adults confirmed

**Counter-evidence:**
- Factor structure has not replicated well cross-culturally (Swedish data set showed poor fit)
- A 2025 factor analytic study (McKinnon et al.) identified construct validity concerns
- The 2026 Arnold, Bitsika & Sharpley critical review of 389 studies found the CAT-Q may confound social anxiety with camouflaging; the masking subscale specifically was unrelated to autistic traits; total CAT-Q scores correlated more strongly with social anxiety than autistic traits in the original validation study and in seven subsequent replications
- Not validated for children under 15 or for individuals with intellectual disability

**Assessment:** The psychometric properties are accurately reported, but the research file omits important construct validity criticisms. The CAT-Q may be partially measuring social anxiety rather than an autism-specific construct.

#### Claim 7: Meta-analytic correlations: camouflaging-depression r = 0.36, camouflaging-anxiety r = 0.27, camouflaging-social anxiety r = 0.24, camouflaging-wellbeing r = -0.13

**Rating: CONFIRMED**

**Pro-evidence:**
- Meta-analysis confirmed: published 2024 in Research in Autism Spectrum Disorders
- All four effect sizes exactly match: depression r = 0.36 (95% CI [0.27, 0.45], I-squared = 50%), generalized anxiety r = 0.27 (95% CI [0.22, 0.33], I-squared = 32%), social anxiety r = 0.24 (95% CI [0.19, 0.29], I-squared = 36%), wellbeing r = -0.13 (95% CI [-0.19, -0.08], I-squared = 21%)
- 16 studies, 5,897 autistic participants confirmed

**Counter-evidence:**
- The meta-analysis itself noted that all studies were underpowered to detect small effects
- Many studies used insufficiently validated measures
- No significant moderating effects found (study quality, age, gender proportions) -- which could indicate the relationships are stable OR that there is insufficient variability to detect moderation
- These are correlational, not causal -- a point the research file does not adequately emphasize

**Assessment:** Effect sizes are accurately reported. These are small to moderate correlations from cross-sectional data. The research file presents these appropriately but should note more prominently that these are exclusively cross-sectional associations.

#### Claim 8: Cassidy et al. (2018) found 72% of autistic adults scored above psychiatric cutoff for suicide risk, 38% reported lifetime suicide attempts, 65% NSSI, camouflaging accounted for 3.5% additional variance

**Rating: UNCERTAIN (Score: 6/10)**

**Pro-evidence:**
- Study confirmed at PubMed 30083306, Molecular Autism 9, 42
- 72% above SBQ-R cutoff confirmed
- 65% lifetime NSSI confirmed
- Camouflaging as a significant predictor of suicidality confirmed
- Sample: 164 autistic adults, 169 general population confirmed

**Counter-evidence / Concerns:**
- The 38% lifetime suicide attempts figure and 80% depression rate could not be independently confirmed from search results, though they are plausible given the study's recruitment approach
- The F(1,139) = 6.56 for camouflaging predicting suicidality and 3.5% variance figure could not be precisely confirmed; one search found F(1,117) = 4.32 for unmet support needs (3.1% variance), suggesting these are distinct regression steps and the variance statistics may be from different models
- The sample was recruited online, was predominantly female (99/164), and predominantly late-diagnosed (mean age of ASC diagnosis 34.85 years) -- creating significant selection bias
- Online self-selected samples tend to over-represent individuals with higher distress, potentially inflating prevalence estimates
- The 72% suicide risk figure uses the SBQ-R cutoff of 8, which was validated in psychiatric populations -- its applicability to autistic populations has been subsequently questioned (Pelton et al., 2020 developed autism-specific measures)

**Assessment:** The core finding that camouflaging predicts suicidality is supported, but the specific prevalence percentages may be inflated by sample selection bias. The research file should note the self-selected, predominantly female, late-diagnosed nature of the sample. Some specific statistics (38% attempts, 80% depression) could not be fully verified and may be approximate.

#### Claim 13: Gao et al. (2022) meta-analysis found Hedges' g = 0.389 for elevated cortisol in ASD after removing 3 outlier studies (9 remaining), from initial g = -0.278 (12 studies)

**Rating: UNCERTAIN (Score: 4/10)**

**Pro-evidence:**
- The meta-analysis exists: PubMed 35911217, PMC9334910, Frontiers in Psychiatry 13, 928188
- 12 studies with 375 ASD patients and 335 controls confirmed
- The initial overall Hedges' g = -0.278 (95% CI: -1.026 to 0.470, p = 0.467) confirmed -- i.e., NO significant overall difference

**Counter-evidence:**
- The g = 0.389 result after outlier removal could not be independently confirmed from search results. The sensitivity analysis described in accessible summaries discusses removing studies one at a time and finding the overall result is "robust" and "changed little" -- which contradicts the claim that removing 3 outliers reverses the direction from negative to positive
- The research file presents the post-outlier g = 0.389 as the key finding, but the primary finding of the meta-analysis was NO significant difference (g = -0.278, p = 0.467). Presenting the outlier-removed result as primary is misleading

- This meta-analysis is about cortisol in ASD generally, NOT specifically about cortisol related to masking -- the research file draws a connection that the original study does not make

**Assessment:** The way this study is presented in the research file is problematic. The primary result (no significant difference) is downplayed in favor of a post-hoc outlier-removed analysis. More importantly, the meta-analysis is about cortisol in ASD broadly, not masking-related cortisol elevation. The research file's narrative that masking causes cortisol elevation is an inferential leap not supported by this meta-analysis.

## Data Validation: Systemic Issues

### Issue 1: Pervasive Correlation-as-Causation Problem

**Severity: HIGH**

The vast majority of evidence linking masking to mental health outcomes is cross-sectional. The research file frequently uses language that implies causation ("masking leads to," "consequences of masking," "masking causes") when the evidence only supports association. The Van der Putten (2025) longitudinal study actually found the OPPOSITE directional effect to what the causal narrative predicts, suggesting the relationship may be more complex than "masking causes poor mental health."

**Recommendation for downstream agents:** Use associational language throughout. Note that causal direction is unestablished and may even be reversed.

### Issue 2: CAT-Q Construct Validity Concerns

**Severity: MEDIUM-HIGH**

The CAT-Q, used in most studies cited, has significant construct validity problems:
- Correlates more strongly with social anxiety than with autistic traits
- Factor structure does not replicate cross-culturally
- The masking subscale is specifically unrelated to autistic traits
- May measure general social impression management rather than an autism-specific process
- The 2026 Arnold et al. critical review of 389 studies found extensive conceptual and methodological problems

This means the entire evidence base linking "camouflaging" (as measured by the CAT-Q) to mental health outcomes may be partially capturing anxiety-related social cognition rather than autism-specific masking behavior.

**Recommendation for downstream agents:** Acknowledge CAT-Q limitations and note that the construct of "camouflaging" as measured may overlap with social anxiety.

### Issue 3: Sample Bias Across the Literature

**Severity: MEDIUM**

Nearly all studies share common sample biases:
- Predominantly female samples (many studies 60-87% female)
- Predominantly late-diagnosed adults
- Predominantly white/Western populations
- Predominantly individuals without intellectual disability
- Online self-selected recruitment (biased toward higher distress)
- Raymaker 2020 burnout sample was 100% non-Hispanic white

These biases limit generalizability and may inflate effect sizes, particularly for prevalence estimates.

**Recommendation for downstream agents:** Note sample limitations. Avoid generalizing findings to all autistic people.

### Issue 4: Cortisol/Physiological Claims Are Overextended

**Severity: MEDIUM**

The research file builds a narrative about masking causing physiological damage (cortisol dysregulation, central sensitivity syndromes, HPA axis dysfunction) by citing studies that examined autism broadly, not masking specifically. No study cited directly measures the physiological effects of masking behavior. The causal chain is hypothetical.

**Recommendation for downstream agents:** Clearly distinguish between (a) studies showing autistic people have altered cortisol/stress physiology, and (b) the untested hypothesis that masking contributes to this.

## Research Opportunities

### High Priority

1. **Longitudinal studies with repeated measures of both masking and mental health** -- The Van der Putten 2025 finding that masking predicted IMPROVED mental health over time desperately needs replication. If confirmed, it would fundamentally reshape the field's understanding.

2. **Development of masking measures with better discriminant validity from social anxiety** -- The CAT-Q's overlap with social anxiety undermines much of the existing evidence base. New measures that clearly separate autism-specific camouflaging from general social anxiety are needed.

3. **Experimental or quasi-experimental studies measuring cortisol before, during, and after masking situations** -- No study has directly tested the masking-cortisol hypothesis. Ecological momentary assessment combined with cortisol sampling (like the Scheeren 2025 design but with biomarkers) would be highly informative.

### Medium Priority

4. **Identify the "3x more likely to experience burnout" claim source** -- If this statistic exists in a peer-reviewed source, locate it. If not, it should be excluded from any downstream content.

5. **Replicate key findings with diverse samples** -- Most research is from white, female, late-diagnosed, English-speaking populations. Cross-cultural validation and inclusion of men, children, and individuals with higher support needs is needed.

6. **Investigate whether masking has protective functions for some subgroups** -- Van der Putten's subgroup findings and workplace masking research suggest camouflaging may be beneficial for some individuals in some contexts.

### Lower Priority

7. **Clarify the Botha & Frost 2020 loneliness mediation claim** -- Determine whether the loneliness mediation finding is from this specific study or another Botha publication.

8. **Verify the Field 2024 "19 studies" claim** -- The meta-ethnography synthesized 13 studies; the source of "19" should be identified or the number corrected.

## Overall Assessment

The two research files provide a comprehensive and largely accurate survey of the autistic masking/camouflaging literature. The bibliographic citations, study details, and specific statistics are mostly correct, with only a few errors (Cassidy 2023 page numbers, Field 2024 study count, unsourced "3x burnout" claim).

However, the research files share a systematic bias toward presenting the masking-mental health relationship as more causally established than the evidence supports. The heavy reliance on cross-sectional data, the CAT-Q's construct validity problems, the Van der Putten longitudinal finding contradicting the assumed causal direction, and the emerging critical literature (Khudiakova 2025, Arnold et al. 2026) all suggest the field is in an earlier state of knowledge than the research files imply. The cortisol/physiological section is the weakest, drawing on studies that examined autism broadly rather than masking specifically.

The qualitative evidence for masking's toll on wellbeing and identity is strong and consistent. The quantitative evidence for specific causal pathways is weaker and more contested than presented.
