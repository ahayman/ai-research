# Restless Legs: Why It Got Worse

> Your restless legs got worse on duloxetine. That is real. What follows is an honest account of what is known, what is guessed, and what nobody has ever measured.

Your restless legs got worse after you started duloxetine. That observation is the single most reliable thing on this page, because it is about you. Every other number here comes from someone else's body, and most of those bodies were studied in groups of eight or nine.

The mechanism is coherent. Restless legs syndrome is, as best anyone can tell, a brain-iron and dopamine disorder `[primary, verified]`, and serotonergic transmission inhibits dopaminergic transmission. More serotonin therefore means less effective dopamine signalling, which means worse RLS. Antidepressants have a second route available to them as well, which is histamine H1 blockade, and that is why sedating antihistamines are classic RLS aggravators. Duloxetine has negligible H1 affinity, with a Ki above 500 nM `[primary, verified]`, so for this drug the second route is closed and the serotonergic route is the only one on the table. That deduction is clean and it survives scrutiny. But it remains a deduction: the serotonin-inhibits-dopamine step is inferred from pharmacology, not measured in RLS patients taking an SNRI `[reasoning, not evidence]`. The mechanism tells you the story is coherent. It does not tell you the story is true, and it does not tell you how large the effect is in you.

So hold the mechanism loosely, because you have already made the highest-yield move available in this whole area, and you made it without being told to.

## The iron, which you got right

Your ferritin is about 70, and you started oral iron this week on your own initiative. That was correct. The International Restless Legs Syndrome Study Group guideline says oral iron should be considered when ferritin is at or below 75 μg/L `[primary, verified]`. At 70 you were already inside the treatment zone, not near it. This matters because the RLS threshold sits far above the threshold your lab uses. Most labs flag iron deficiency somewhere around 15 to 30 μg/L. The consequence is that a ferritin of 40 is normal for anaemia and low for RLS, and a person can be told "your iron is normal" while sitting well inside the range where iron is indicated.

There is one thing you still need, and it is not another ferritin. It is transferrin saturation, usually written TSAT. The same guideline says no iron treatment if TSAT is above 45% `[primary, verified]`. That is the contraindication check, and ferritin cannot substitute for it, because they measure different things. So the panel to ask for is ferritin, serum iron, TIBC and TSAT together, drawn in the morning and fasting, because serum iron and TSAT swing substantially with time of day and with food `[primary, verified]`.

Ferritin has a second problem that lands specifically on you. Ferritin is an acute-phase reactant, which means inflammation pushes it up independently of how much iron you actually have `[primary, verified]`. With fibromyalgia and ME/CFS in the picture, a ferritin of 70 may be reading high relative to your true iron status, and the real number could be lower. That cuts in your favour: it means the case for iron is, if anything, slightly stronger than the number suggests.

Something else cuts the other way, and honesty requires both. The trial that actually studied oral iron at your ferritin enrolled 18 people with ferritin between 15 and 75 μg/L and found significant improvement against placebo at 12 weeks `[primary, verified]`. At about 70 you sit at the very top of that window, which makes you the least iron-deficient kind of patient it enrolled, and therefore the least likely to benefit `[reasoning, not evidence]`. Eighteen people is also the entire primary evidence base for oral iron at your ferritin level. So: correct action, real guideline support, thin trial evidence, and you personally sit at the weak end of the enrolled range. All four of those are true at once.

Two practical points are worth raising with your doctor. The first is timing, and it is decision-relevant. Oral iron acts over roughly 12 weeks, because the endpoint of that trial was 12 weeks and moving brain iron takes months rather than weeks `[primary, verified]`. You started it this week. The consequence is that iron cannot explain anything you observe at your appointment in two weeks. It is a slow background variable and it should be left alone to run.

The second point is counterintuitive enough that it is worth saying out loud. Taking oral iron less often can absorb better than taking it more often, because a dose raises hepcidin, and hepcidin then blocks absorption of the next dose. The guideline prefers once-daily to twice-daily `[secondary — unverified]`, and the hepcidin literature supports alternate-day over daily `[secondary — unverified]`. Note that the 18-person trial used twice-daily dosing `[primary, verified]`, which is the older protocol. If you were told to take it twice a day, that is not a mistake so much as an out-of-date convention, and it is a reasonable thing to ask about.

There is one more piece of the iron story, and it is the piece nobody says. The evidence that iron helps RLS is about RLS in general and iron deficiency in general. Whether correcting iron reduces *antidepressant-induced* RLS specifically has never been studied `[no evidence exists]`. It sounds obvious that it would. That is an assumption, not a finding.

While we are correcting things: the claim that iron panels get routinely skipped in primary care is wrong, and the correct version is more useful to you. Panels are ordered about 92.1% of the time. The failure is in acting on the result: among patients whose ferritin came back at or below 75, only about 50% were given iron `[secondary — unverified, from a conference abstract in a single health system]`. So the gap is not testing, it is the moment when a ferritin of 45 comes back, gets read as "normal," and nothing happens. That tells you what to actually ask, which is not "will you check my ferritin" but "what is the number, and is it under 75?"

## The melatonin, where every previous answer was wrong

You take melatonin nightly, and it is part of what is keeping the RLS from stopping you sleeping. Three earlier passes over this material reached three confident conclusions about it, in different directions, and all three were wrong. Here is what the literature actually contains.

There are two studies. In the first, eight people with RLS took 3mg of melatonin at 7pm, and their periodic leg movements went up `[primary, verified]`. In the second, nine people with periodic limb movement disorder took 3mg of melatonin for six weeks, and their periodic leg movements went down, along with their PLM index and their PLM-arousal index, with well-being improving in seven of the nine `[primary, verified]`. Same drug. Same 3mg dose. Opposite result on the same kind of measurement, in adjacent populations.

Both studies are tiny, and both are worse than tiny suggests. The first had no placebo condition at all, so its comparison was against baseline, and expectancy was entirely uncontrolled in an eight-person study of a partly subjective outcome `[primary, verified]`. The second was open-label and uncontrolled in nine people `[primary, verified]`. Neither has been replicated. The supporting mechanism is real but distant: melatonin inhibited calcium-dependent dopamine release in rat hypothalamus, in vitro, in 1983 `[primary, verified]`, and the onset of nocturnal melatonin secretion tracks the circadian worsening of RLS symptoms in a seven-person chronobiology study `[primary, verified]`. Those are genuine findings. They are also a long way from "3mg of oral melatonin suppresses human striatal dopamine tonight," and mechanism is not evidence.

So the honest grade is weak-to-moderate and hypothesis-generating. Nobody should tell you melatonin is aggravating your restless legs, because a two-study literature that disagrees with itself cannot support that. Nobody should dismiss the question either, because one of those two studies is a real experimental challenge study and the 3mg dose it used is exactly the dose sold over the counter. The evidence points both ways and it is weak in both directions.

If you want to test it, a melatonin holiday is cheap, fast, free and fully reversible, because melatonin's half-life is about 40 minutes. But there is a catch that the obvious version of this plan misses. You are two weeks into a drug whose early side effects characteristically attenuate over weeks. So if you stop melatonin now and the RLS improves, that result is equally consistent with melatonin removal and with duloxetine simply settling in, and you will not be able to tell which. A negative result is therefore more informative than a positive one: if nothing changes, melatonin is exonerated and you can stop wondering. A positive result will be ambiguous.

One free thing points the right way, from the same eight-person study. Bright light produced a small but significant decrease in sensory leg discomfort, while having no effect on the movements `[primary, verified]`. Melatonin did the reverse, affecting the movements and not the discomfort. That is a clean double dissociation in eight people, which is to say it is interesting and thin. Morning bright light has no interaction with duloxetine and costs nothing.

## What has actually been measured about duloxetine

Here a claim died, and it died in a way worth understanding, because you are likely to meet it.

There is an authoritative-sounding sentence in the review literature stating that duloxetine increases periodic limb movements. It is extrapolated from venlafaxine. There is no duloxetine study behind it `[secondary — unverified]`. **Duloxetine has never been measured for periodic limb movements in sleep. At any dose. Ever** `[no evidence exists]`. The polysomnography studies of duloxetine do not report them. So if someone tells you duloxetine raises your PLMS, ask what they are reading, because the honest answer is that nobody knows.

What does exist points, if anything, the other way. The one systematic review of this question covered 18 prospective studies and concluded that onset or exacerbation of restless legs and rises in periodic limb movements are *uncommon* after starting an antidepressant, and that for the drugs actually studied the movements produced *"do not disrupt sleep and are thus unlikely to be clinically significant"* `[primary, verified]`. Duloxetine is not mentioned in it. Separately, in a pharmacovigilance program covering 340,099 psychiatric inpatients, duloxetine's rate of severe newly-diagnosed RLS was 0.01%, against mirtazapine's 0.07% `[primary, partially verified]`. That is a seven-fold difference in a third of a million people, and it establishes that duloxetine is not a mirtazapine-class offender. It does not establish that duloxetine is innocent, because it counted severe new inpatient diagnoses, which is a very high bar, and it is not the same thing as your existing RLS getting worse. Absence of severe new cases is not absence of aggravation.

The closest thing to on-point evidence is a single case. A 67-year-old woman had her duloxetine increased from 60mg to 80mg, and the very next night developed creeping sensations in her shoulders and arms with an urge to move, worse at rest and relieved by movement. Reducing the dose resolved it, with no recurrence at six months `[primary, verified]`. That is n=1, and it is confounded, because her escitalopram was cut from 15mg to 10mg at the same time, so the case cannot distinguish a duloxetine increase from an escitalopram reduction `[primary, verified]`. It is still the closest thing that exists, and its onset was overnight, which tells you something useful about how fast you would know.

The conclusion is narrow and I want to be precise about it. Your RLS got worse on 20mg, and that is real and observed. The claim that it will get worse again, proportionally, if the dose goes up is not evidenced for this drug in anyone. So the right posture is to monitor, not to predict. Those are different things: monitoring means you decide in advance what you will watch and how you will tell causes apart, and predicting means you have decided the answer before the experiment.

## Augmentation is the wrong word

You will encounter the word "augmentation" if you read about RLS, and it does not apply here. Augmentation is a specific complication of dopamine agonist treatment, in which symptoms start earlier in the day, get more severe, spread to other body parts, and worsen when the dose goes up `[primary, verified]`. It is a dopamine agonist phenomenon. Antidepressants do not cause augmentation. They cause direct exacerbation, which is a different thing with a different mechanism and different management.

This is not pedantry. Calling your worsened RLS "augmentation" would import a management algorithm, namely reduce or rotate the dopamine agonist, that has nothing to do with your situation. And the distinction matters for what happens next, because the field has moved: the 2025 American Academy of Sleep Medicine guideline conditionally recommends *against* the standard use of ropinirole, pramipexole, rotigotine and levodopa, explicitly because of augmentation risk `[primary, verified]`. The drugs that were first-line for RLS a decade ago are the ones being backed away from now.

Gabapentinoids carry roughly 0.9% augmentation, not zero `[secondary — unverified]`. "Much lower" is accurate; "no risk" is not.

## Options that do not conflict, and what they cost

Duloxetine offers nothing for RLS and plausibly aggravates it. Pregabalin is a strong guideline-supported option for RLS, and it is not serotonergic, so it carries neither the serotonergic RLS penalty nor the serotonergic dissociation risk that is your specific documented vulnerability `[primary, verified]`. That asymmetry is real and it is decision-relevant.

It also has costs, and they land on you specifically, so I am going to put them next to the benefit rather than in a footnote.

A randomized controlled trial of pregabalin combined with duloxetine in fibromyalgia exists, and it is positive: the combination beat placebo, beat pregabalin alone, and beat duloxetine alone `[primary, verified]`. Earlier passes over this material proposed the combination as a clever untested idea while that trial existed, which was this project's largest single factual error. The same trial also reports that moderate-to-severe drowsiness was more frequent during the combination than during placebo `[primary, verified]`. That is the exact harm an ME/CFS patient can least afford, and it comes from the very trial that makes the case for the combination.

The cognitive cost is measured, not speculated. In a 12-week double-blind placebo-controlled trial in 32 healthy volunteers, pregabalin significantly impaired three of six target cognitive measures: processing speed, executive control, and verbal fluency, with the subjects themselves noticing `[primary, verified]`. That is Class I evidence, stated as such in the paper. Verbal fluency is your documented weak spot, and processing speed and executive control are the core of ME/CFS cognitive dysfunction. A drug that measurably degrades verbal retrieval in healthy volunteers is a specific concern for someone who already struggles with verbal processing.

The reassuring version of this, that RLS doses sit below the cognitively-active range, is contradicted `[primary, verified]`. The pivotal 52-week RLS trial used 300mg/day, the usual effective RLS range is quoted as 150 to 450mg, and impairment has been demonstrated at 300mg. The ranges overlap; they do not sit apart. The defensible version is narrower: cognitive risk is dose-dependent, starting low may reduce it, and it has not been shown to eliminate it.

So pregabalin is a serious option with a real cost, and not a clever unlock. Its moment arrives if the RLS becomes intolerable, or if duloxetine's benefit fades, or if a dose increase produces dissociation. Its cost is aimed at your primary disability. That trade is yours and your doctor's to make, and it should be made with the trial and the drowsiness both on the table.

Bupropion is worth knowing as the mechanistic proof rather than as an option for you. It is noradrenergic and dopaminergic with no serotonergic action, which makes it the natural experiment that isolates serotonin as the culprit, and a 60-person randomized trial found it does not exacerbate RLS `[primary, verified]`. But bupropion is often activating, it can worsen anxiety, and anxiety relief is your main reported benefit from duloxetine. It has no fibromyalgia indication. It is the right answer for depression plus RLS and probably the wrong answer for anxiety plus fibromyalgia plus RLS.

## Three variables, and why that is the actual problem

Here is the thing that matters most, and it is not about any single one of these.

Three RLS variables are already in motion. Duloxetine started two weeks ago, and its early adverse effects characteristically attenuate over weeks, which means your RLS may improve on its own without anything else changing. Melatonin is running nightly in the background, fast on and fast off. Iron started this week and will not declare itself for about 12 weeks. A fourth variable, a dose change, is two weeks away.

Clean isolation is no longer available to you. That is not a failure on your part; it is what happens when sensible actions get taken at sensible times without a measurement plan. But it has a consequence worth being explicit about: if the dose changes now, four variables are moving at once, and no result will be interpretable. Not the restless legs, not the sleep, not the fatigue. If your RLS improves over the next month, at least four explanations fit, and you will have no way to choose between them.

What survives all of this is short. Your RLS got worse on 20mg and that is real, and it is better evidence about you than any group study on this page. The mechanism by which duloxetine could do that is coherent and unmeasured. Whether it gets worse again at a higher dose is not something the literature can tell you, because duloxetine has never been measured for this, which means the honest posture is to watch rather than to forecast. The iron was the right move and it will not have anything to say for three months. The melatonin question is genuinely open in both directions, and testing it now would give you an ambiguous answer if it improves and a useful one if it does not. And pregabalin is a real option whose real cost is pointed at the part of you that can least afford it. The most valuable thing you can do before the appointment is not to change something. It is to decide, in advance, what you are going to measure, and how you would tell these causes apart.

## Deep Dive

### The mechanism, and its limits

Two routes are described by which antidepressants worsen RLS `[primary, verified as the standard account]`:

1. **Increased serotonin.** Serotonergic transmission inhibits dopaminergic transmission. RLS is fundamentally a brain-iron and dopamine disorder. More 5-HT means less effective dopaminergic signalling, which means worse RLS.
2. **Histamine H1 blockade.** Independently worsens RLS. This is why sedating antihistamines are classic aggravators.

**The deduction for duloxetine:** duloxetine has negligible H1 affinity (Ki > 500 nM) `[primary, verified]`, so route 2 is unavailable to it and the serotonergic route is the only one on the table. The inference is legitimate and survives scrutiny. **The caveat that must travel with it:** this is the standard textbook account, not a demonstrated causal chain in humans. The serotonin-to-dopamine inhibition step is inferred from pharmacology, not measured in RLS patients on SNRIs `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

**Why the brain-iron story is the strongest part:** CSF ferritin is about 65% lower and CSF transferrin about 3× higher in RLS patients than controls; MRI and post-mortem both show reduced substantia nigra iron; the RLS risk allele on BTBD9 is associated with lower serum ferritin, and BTBD9-null flies show an RLS-like phenotype reversed by dopamine agonists `[primary, verified]`. Brain iron deficiency is the best-established pathophysiology in RLS. Crucially, peripheral iron can be normal while brain iron is low.

### Iron: thresholds, timescale, and the real failure mode

**Source:** Allen RP, Picchietti DL, Auerbach M, et al. "Evidence-based and consensus clinical practice guidelines for the iron treatment of restless legs syndrome/Willis-Ekbom disease in adults and children: an IRLSSG task force report." *Sleep Med.* 2018;41:27–44.

| Finding | Action |
|---|---|
| **Ferritin ≤ 75 μg/L** | **Oral iron should be considered** (if tolerated and safe) |
| **Ferritin ≤ 100 μg/L** | Consider **IV iron** when oral is not appropriate |
| Low peripheral iron defined as | ferritin **<75 μg/L**, *or* TSAT **<20%** with ferritin <300 μg/L |
| **TSAT > 45%** | **No iron treatment** |
| Ferritin above 75 μg/L | Oral absorption is minimal; supplementing is largely futile |

All rows `[primary, verified]`.

**Test specifics.** Morning, fasting. Serum iron and TSAT swing substantially with time of day and with food, and a random afternoon draw can mislead. Order ferritin + serum iron + TIBC + %TSAT together. Ferritin alone is insufficient because ferritin is an acute-phase reactant and can read falsely normal or high during inflammation, which is a real concern in ME/CFS and fibromyalgia `[primary, verified]`.

**Two caveats pointing opposite ways, both true:**
- Ferritin as an acute-phase reactant means his true iron status may be *below* 70, strengthening the case `[reasoning, not evidence]`.
- At ~70 he sits at the very top of the 15–75 window enrolled by the only trial at this ferritin level, making him the least iron-deficient patient it studied and the least likely to benefit `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

**The trial itself.** Wang J, et al. "Efficacy of oral iron in patients with restless legs syndrome and a low-normal ferritin: A randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled study." *Sleep Med.* 2009 Oct;10(9):973–5. PMID 19230757 `[primary, verified]`.
- n = 18 non-anemic RLS patients. Entry: IRLS ≥11, serum ferritin 15–75 μg/L.
- Ferrous sulfate 325mg (65mg elemental) + vitamin C 100mg, **twice daily**, 12 weeks.
- Significant increase in serum ferritin and improvement in RLS symptoms vs placebo at 12 weeks.
- **Eighteen people is the entire primary evidence base for oral iron at this ferritin.**

**Timescale, and why it is decision-relevant.** The trial endpoint is 12 weeks `[primary, verified]`. Some sources report improvement as early as 2–6 weeks `[secondary — unverified]`. Expect roughly three months to move brain iron. **The appointment is in two weeks. Iron cannot be the explanation for anything observed at that appointment.** It was started this week, it will not have declared itself, and it should be left alone to run.

**Dosing frequency, and the hepcidin logic.** A dose of oral iron raises hepcidin, which then blocks absorption of the next dose. The consequence is counterintuitive: less frequent dosing can absorb better than more frequent dosing.
- IRLSSG prefers **once-daily to twice-daily** `[secondary — unverified; guideline full text not retrieved]`.
- The hepcidin literature supports **alternate-day over daily**, taken with vitamin C `[secondary — unverified]`.
- **Wang 2009 used twice-daily** `[primary, verified]`, which is the older protocol. If the reader was told BID, that reflects convention rather than current absorption science, and it is a reasonable thing to raise.

**The "routinely skipped" correction.** The prior research claimed iron panels are routinely skipped in primary care. **This framing is wrong.** The best available data (a retrospective study reported as a conference abstract in *Annals of Family Medicine* 2025, single health system, weak) reports that **iron panels were ordered for 92.1% of RLS patients**, but among those with **ferritin ≤75 μg/L, only 50% received iron supplementation** `[secondary — unverified]`.

> **The gap is not testing. It is acting on the result.** The failure mode is a ferritin of 45 coming back, being read as "normal," and no iron being started. The useful question is therefore **"what is the number, and is it under 75?"** rather than "will you check my ferritin?" **A ferritin of 40 is normal for anaemia and low for RLS.**

**The gap nobody names.** Does correcting iron reduce *antidepressant-induced* RLS specifically? **No evidence found; searched directly and repeatedly** `[no evidence exists]`. The iron literature addresses RLS with iron deficiency in general. The antidepressant-induced subset has not been studied. The plausible-sounding assumption that iron repletion will buffer a drug-induced RLS is an assumption, not a finding.

### Melatonin: a two-study literature that disagrees with itself

**Study 1, pointing toward harm.** Whittom S, Dumont M, Petit D, Desautels A, Adam B, Lavigne G, Montplaisir J. "Effects of melatonin and bright light administration on motor and sensory symptoms of RLS." *Sleep Med.* 2010 Apr;11(4):351–5. **PMID 20226733** `[primary, verified]`.
- **n = 8** RLS subjects (mean age 53.3, range 38–63, 6F/2M). Within-subject, three conditions: baseline, melatonin, bright light.
- **Melatonin 3mg at 19:00.** Bright light 3,000 lux, 19:00 to midnight.
- Suggested Immobilization Test (SIT); SIT-PLM index.
- **Melatonin significantly increased SIT-PLM** vs both baseline and bright light.
- **Bright light significantly decreased sensory leg discomfort**, with no effect on leg movements. A clean double dissociation: melatonin moved the movements and not the discomfort; bright light moved the discomfort and not the movements.
- **NO PLACEBO CONDITION.** "Baseline" is not a blinded control. Expectancy is entirely uncontrolled in an n=8 study of a partly subjective outcome.
- The 3mg dose is the standard OTC consumer dose, which makes the finding *more* applicable to this reader, not less.

**Study 2, pointing toward benefit, and every prior pass missed it.** Kunz D, Bes F. "Exogenous melatonin in periodic limb movement disorder: an open clinical trial and a hypothesis." *Sleep.* 2001;24(2):183–7. **PMID 11247054** `[primary, verified]`.
- **n = 9** PLMD patients (without RLS). **Melatonin 3mg at 22:00–23:00, six weeks.**
- **Melatonin REDUCED** PLMs, PLM index, PLMs with arousals, and PLM-arousal index. Well-being improved in 7 of 9.
- Open-label, uncontrolled, adjacent population.

> **Same drug. Same 3mg dose. Opposite motor result, on the same endpoint, in adjacent populations.** Kunz does not refute Whittom, because the populations and designs differ. But it is a real tension in a two-study literature, and the claim that "nothing points the other way" is false for periodic limb movements, which is the endpoint Whittom actually measured.

**The supporting chain, in which every link is low-tier:**
- **Mechanism:** Zisapel N, Egozi Y, Laudon M. *Brain Res.* 1983. **PMID 6412963.** Melatonin inhibited calcium-dependent dopamine release. **Rat hypothalamus, in vitro** `[primary, verified]`. The inferential distance to "3mg oral melatonin suppresses human striatal dopamine in vivo" is very large `[reasoning, not evidence]`.
- **Circadian correlation:** Michaud M, Dumont M, Selmaoui B, Paquet J, Fontaine B, Montplaisir J. *Ann Neurol.* 2004;55(3):372–80. **n=7**, modified constant routine. Salivary melatonin acrophase ~2h *before* the acrophase of leg discomfort and PLMS `[primary, verified]`. Real, but n=7 and correlational.
- **Case report:** *SLEEP* 2024;47(Suppl 1):A508. n=1, RLS controlled within a week of stopping melatonin. **Conference abstract** `[secondary — unverified]`.
- Melatonin does **not** appear on the American Geriatrics Society list of drug classes identified as worsening RLS `[secondary — unverified]`.

> **Grade: WEAK-TO-MODERATE. Hypothesis-generating, not established.** One unreplicated n=8 within-subject experimental study with no placebo condition, supported by a chain that is low-tier at every link, and contradicted on the same endpoint by an n=9 open trial in an adjacent population. Enough to justify a cheap, reversible stop-and-observe trial. **Not enough to state as fact that melatonin worsens RLS, and not enough to dismiss the question either.**

**On the melatonin holiday, and why "clean" is the wrong word.** The one-variable-at-a-time principle is sound, and melatonin is fast on and off (t½ ~40 min), free, reversible, and observable within days, which makes it the cheapest experiment available. But it is **not cleanly interpretable now either**, because the reader is two weeks into a drug whose early side effects characteristically attenuate over weeks. A holiday run now is confounded with duloxetine's own early-adaptation trajectory.

> **The honest framing: a melatonin holiday is cheap, safe, reversible and worth doing, but it tests the background variable rather than the one that changed, and at two weeks its result is confounded with the drug's own settling-in. A negative result (no change) is more informative than a positive one.**

**The bonus finding.** Bright light decreased sensory symptoms in the same n=8 study, with the same caveats `[primary, verified]`. Morning bright light is free, has no interaction with duloxetine, and has independent support for circadian regulation and sleep. It is a low-cost thing pointing the right way.

### PLMS: what is measured, and what is extrapolated

**The claim that died.** Nicolas & Ruby 2020 (PMID 33224081) states that duloxetine *"has a similar effect on sleep as venlafaxine: reduced length of REM sleep, fragmented sleep, and increase in periodic limb movement"* `[primary, verified that the review says this]`.

> **This is a review assertion with no duloxetine PLMS study behind it.** No polysomnography study of duloxetine has ever reported periodic limb movements. Not Kluge, not Chalon, and the RBD case report explicitly found none. **The claim is extrapolated from venlafaxine** `[secondary — unverified; no primary duloxetine PLMS measurement traceable anywhere]`.
>
> **Duloxetine has never been measured for PLMS. At any dose. Ever** `[no evidence exists]`. A downstream reader who meets Nicolas & Ruby will find an authoritative-sounding sentence. It should not be repeated as a duloxetine finding.

**What the SSRI/venlafaxine evidence shows, for other drugs.** Ferri R, et al. "Periodic leg movements during sleep associated with antidepressants: A meta-analysis." *Neurosci Biobehav Rev.* 2023;148:105126. **PMID 36914081** `[primary, verified]`. 12 studies. Analyses involving **SSRIs or venlafaxine** showed an **overall large effect size**, clearly larger than other antidepressants; fluoxetine largest. Heterogeneity substantial; mostly Level III evidence. **Duloxetine is not identified in the abstract as an included drug.**

**Dose-dependence is not established for any SNRI.** The only dose-escalation PSG study is Salín-Pascual RJ, et al. *J Clin Psychiatry.* 1997;58(8):348–50. **PMID 9515972** `[primary, verified]`: 8 normal volunteers, venlafaxine 75mg × 2 nights then 150mg × 2 nights; 6 of 8 showed PLMS at >25/hour.

> **This looks like dose-dependence and is not.** Dose is perfectly confounded with consecutive nights of exposure, because the 150mg nights are also nights 3 and 4. It is also venlafaxine, n=8, healthy volunteers, four nights. **There is no clean dose-response study of PLMS for any SNRI, and none whatsoever for duloxetine.**

**The systematic review that points the other way.** Kolla BP, Mansukhani MP, Bostwick JM. "The influence of antidepressants on restless legs syndrome and periodic limb movements: A systematic review." *Sleep Med Rev.* 2018 Apr;38:131–140. **PMID 28822709** `[primary, verified — abstract retrieved verbatim]`. **18 prospective studies.**

> *"Current data indicate that onset or exacerbation of restless legs syndrome and rise in frequency of periodic limb movements are **uncommon** following the initiation of an antidepressant."*
>
> *"Among the various antidepressants, **mirtazapine** may be associated with higher rates."*
>
> *"**Sertraline, fluoxetine, and amitriptyline appear to increase periodic limb movements that do not disrupt sleep and are thus unlikely to be clinically significant.**"*
>
> *"**Bupropion may reduce restless legs syndrome symptoms**, at least in the short term."*

**Duloxetine is not mentioned.** There is no ranked position for duloxetine. The general SSRI/SNRI treatment-emergent RLS figure of 2–10% is the best available `[secondary — unverified]`.

**The pharmacovigilance data.** Fugger G, et al. "Secondary Restless Legs Syndrome during psychopharmacological treatment: real-world evidence from a multinational pharmacovigilance program." *Int J Neuropsychopharmacol.* **PMID 42251748** `[primary, partially verified]`. AMSP program, **340,099 psychiatric inpatients**, 99 hospitals, 2001–2016. **67 cases** of newly diagnosed **severe** RLS (0.02%). Over 80% attributable to mirtazapine and quetiapine.

| Drug | Exposed | RLS cases | Rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mirtazapine** | 54,170 | 36 | **0.07%** |
| Venlafaxine | 37,584 | 3 | 0.01% |
| **Duloxetine** | **13,853** | **1** | **0.01%** |
| Sertraline | 19,057 | 1 | 0.01% |
| Escitalopram | 24,790 | 1 | ~0.00% |

> **The caveat that changes the meaning:** this counts **severe, newly-diagnosed, inpatient** RLS, which is a very high bar, and it is not the same as "my existing RLS got worse." **Absence of severe new cases is not absence of aggravation.** What it does establish is that duloxetine is not a mirtazapine-class offender: mirtazapine's rate is 7× duloxetine's in 340,099 people.

**The one directly on-point case.** Shao Y, et al. "Suspected duloxetine-induced restless legs syndrome phenotypic variant: a case report." *BMC Psychiatry.* 2024 May 10;24(1):349. **PMID 38730422** `[primary, verified]`.
- 67-year-old woman, geriatric psychiatric ward. Known RLS history.
- **Duloxetine increased 60 → 80 mg/day.**
- **The next night before bedtime:** itching and creeping sensations deep inside bilateral shoulders and arms, urge to move, worse at rest, relieved by movement.
- **Resolved on reduction to duloxetine 40mg + pramipexole 0.125mg.** No recurrence at 6 months.
- Authors: *"psychiatrists should pay attention to RLS variants when increasing doses of duloxetine."*

> **n = 1, and confounded:** escitalopram was *simultaneously* reduced from 15mg to 10mg, so the RLS could be an escitalopram-reduction effect as easily as a duloxetine-increase effect. **The case report itself cannot distinguish them.** It remains the closest thing to a duloxetine dose-escalation RLS signal that exists, it is directionally consistent with the reader's concern, and its onset was **overnight**, which is decision-relevant for how fast he would know.

**The four breaks in the chain, named:**
1. **Duloxetine has never been measured for PLMS.** The only assertion that it raises PLM is a review extrapolating from venlafaxine.
2. **PLMS dose-dependence is not established for any SNRI.** The only escalation study confounds dose with night.
3. **The one systematic review of 18 prospective studies says increases are "uncommon"** and produce PLMs *"that do not disrupt sleep."* That directly contradicts the premise that PLMS will silently fragment his sleep.
4. **The mechanism is coherent but not evidenced.** Duloxetine's lack of H1 affinity makes the serotonergic route the only one available to it. Coherence is not evidence.

> **Verdict: monitor, do not predict.** His RLS did get worse on 20mg. That is real, observed, personal, and better evidence about him than any group data here. The *dose-dependence* is unevidenced. A dose increase is a reasonable thing to watch that signal during. It is not a documented hazard.

### Augmentation versus direct exacerbation

**Augmentation** is a specific treatment complication of **dopamine agonists**: symptoms begin **earlier in the day**, become **more severe**, **spread to other body parts**, and **worsen with dose increases**. It is a paradoxical drug-induced deterioration `[primary, verified]`.

**Antidepressants do not cause augmentation. They cause direct exacerbation.** Different phenomenon, different mechanism, different management `[primary, verified]`.

Why the distinction is load-bearing: calling antidepressant-induced RLS worsening "augmentation" imports a management algorithm (reduce or rotate the dopamine agonist) that does not apply.

**The numbers, stated carefully, because the commonly-quoted pair is mislabelled:**

| Drug class | Augmentation incidence | Source note |
|---|---|---|
| **Dopamine agonists (specifically)** | **6.0%** (95% CI 4.1–8.8) | Meta-analysis, 60 studies, 11,543 participants `[secondary — unverified]` |
| **Gabapentinoids** | **0.9%** (95% CI 0.2–3.3) | Same meta-analysis `[secondary — unverified]` |
| *Pooled across all drug types* | *5.6%* | **This is the figure usually misquoted as the dopamine-agonist rate. It is not.** |
| Long-term dopamine agonist cohorts | 20–30% | **Cross-sectional clinic prevalence, not trial incidence** `[secondary — unverified]` |

> **Three honesty notes.** First, the gabapentinoid CI (0.2–3.3) **overlaps** the short-term dopamine-agonist CI (1.4–7.3), so "much lower" is a fair summary and "categorically different" is not. Second, the source meta-analysis is titled *"Incidence of Augmentation in Primary RLS Patients May Not Be That High"*, which means it is routinely cited to maximise a gap its own authors argue is overstated. Third, the AASM 2025 guideline contains **no augmentation percentages at all**; the figures come from the separate meta-analysis.

**The field has moved.** The **2025 AASM guideline conditionally recommends against the *standard* use** of ropinirole, pramipexole, rotigotine and levodopa `[primary, verified]`, explicitly because of augmentation risk. Note the wording is "against the standard use," not "against long-term use." The drugs that were first-line a decade ago are the ones being backed away from now, and this is why iron has moved to the centre of RLS care.

**Bupropion: the exception, and the natural experiment.** Bayard M, et al. "Bupropion and Restless Legs Syndrome: A Randomized Controlled Trial." *J Am Board Fam Med.* 2011;24(4):422–8 `[primary, verified]`.
- **29 on bupropion SR 150mg/day vs 31 on placebo**, 6 weeks, double-blind.
- **At 3 weeks:** bupropion 10.8-point vs placebo 6.0-point reduction on the IRLS severity scale (p=0.016).
- **By 6 weeks the difference was no longer significant.**
- Conclusion: bupropion **does not exacerbate** RLS and is *"a reasonable choice if an antidepressant is needed in individuals with RLS."*

> **The robust claim is "does not worsen." The "improves" claim is weaker than it is usually presented**, because the effect was significant at 3 weeks and not sustained at 6, in a 60-person trial. Bupropion is a noradrenaline-dopamine reuptake inhibitor with **no serotonergic action**, which makes it the natural experiment isolating serotonin as the culprit, and that is mainly what it is worth to this reader.

### Options, with their costs

**Non-drug:**

| Option | Grade |
|---|---|
| **Iron repletion if ferritin ≤75** | **ESTABLISHED** `[primary, verified]`. Already started. |
| **Bilateral high-frequency peroneal nerve stimulation** | **Conditionally recommended, AASM 2025** `[primary, verified]`. The only non-drug intervention with a formal guideline recommendation. |
| **Morning bright light** | **SUGGESTIVE** `[primary, verified]`. Whittom's n=8 showed reduced sensory symptoms. Free, no interactions. |
| **Moderate exercise, sleep hygiene, relaxation** | Guideline-endorsed, weak evidence. **Caveat specific to this reader: "moderate exercise" carries PEM risk in ME/CFS. This generic advice does not transfer safely and should not be repeated uncritically.** |
| **Pneumatic compression, massage, warm/cool baths, acupuncture, CBT** | Limited evidence, low risk. CBT is newly recommended in 2024/25 guidance `[secondary — unverified]`. |
| **Reducing aggravators** | Alcohol, caffeine, nicotine, sedating antihistamines, dopamine-blocking antiemetics (metoclopramide, prochlorperazine) `[secondary — unverified]`. |

**Drug options compatible with duloxetine:**

| Option | Grade | Note |
|---|---|---|
| **Gabapentin enacarbil / gabapentin / pregabalin** | **AASM 2025: all three STRONG recommendations, moderate certainty** `[primary, verified]` | No serotonergic conflict. 13 RCTs + 7 observational studies; relieve symptoms in roughly two-thirds of patients. |
| **Dopamine agonists** | **Recommended against for standard use, AASM 2025** `[primary, verified]` | Augmentation. The field has moved. |
| **Reducing the duloxetine dose** | **CASE REPORTS** `[primary, verified]` | Resolved the one on-point duloxetine-RLS case. |
| **Switching to bupropion** | **RCT-BACKED for RLS** `[primary, verified]` | Probably wrong for this reader's symptom profile. |

**Pregabalin, with the costs attached.**

*The case for it:* Non-serotonergic (α2δ subunit of voltage-gated calcium channels), so it carries neither the serotonergic RLS penalty nor the serotonergic dissociation risk that is this reader's documented vulnerability `[primary, verified]`. AASM 2025 gives it a **strong recommendation** for RLS `[primary, verified]`. It is EMA-licensed for generalised anxiety disorder (not FDA-approved for GAD, which is a regulatory divergence rather than an evidence one), with 14 studies and 4,822 patients, HAM-A response OR 1.51 (95% CI 1.31–1.75) `[primary, verified]`. **Conflict of interest that must travel with that figure: the meta-analysis was funded by Viatris, the pregabalin manufacturer, and one author was employed by Viatris** `[primary, verified]`.

*The combination trial that earlier passes said did not exist:* **Gilron I, Chaparro LE, et al. "Combination of pregabalin with duloxetine for fibromyalgia: a randomized controlled trial." *PAIN.* 2016;157(7):1532–1540. PMID 26982602** `[primary, verified]`.
- Randomized, double-blind, **4-period crossover**; 41 randomized, 6 weeks per period at maximally tolerated doses.
- Daily pain: placebo 5.1, pregabalin 5.0, duloxetine 4.1, **combination 3.7**.
- ≥moderate global relief: 18% / 39% / 42% / **68%**. **Combination superior to placebo, to pregabalin, and to duloxetine.**
- **And, from the same trial: *"Moderate-severe drowsiness was more frequent during combination vs placebo."***

> Earlier passes proposed this combination as a clever untested inference while a positive RCT existed. **That was this project's largest single factual error.** The combination is not novel; it is a mainstream research question, and Cochrane has a review of combination pharmacotherapy for fibromyalgia (PMID 29457627) that includes Gilron 2016 and rates the field **VERY LOW** `[primary, verified]`.
>
> The pharmacokinetics are clean: pregabalin is renally excreted, not CYP-metabolised, so there is **no pharmacokinetic interaction with duloxetine** `[primary, verified]`. **But "no CYP overlap" is not "no interaction."** The **pharmacodynamic** additive-sedation signal is documented in the very trial that makes the case.

*The cognitive cost, measured:* **Salinsky M, Storzbach D, Munoz S. "Cognitive effects of pregabalin in healthy volunteers: a double-blind, placebo-controlled trial." *Neurology.* 2010;74(9):755–61. PMID 20194915** `[primary, verified]`.
- **32 healthy volunteers**, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, **12 weeks**; pregabalin titrated over 8 weeks to **600mg/day**. **Class I evidence**, stated in the paper.
- **"Three of 6 target cognitive measures (Digit Symbol, Stroop, Controlled Oral Word Association) revealed significant test-retest differences between the pregabalin and placebo groups, all showing negative effects with pregabalin (p < 0.05)."**
- **"These cognitive effects were paralleled by complaints on the Portland Neurotoxicity Scale (p < 0.01)"**, meaning the subjects noticed.

> **Domain-specific and unfortunate.** Digit Symbol is processing speed. Stroop is executive control and inhibition. **COWA is verbal fluency**, a direct verbal-retrieval test, and **this reader's documented weak spot.** Processing speed and executive control are the core of ME/CFS cognitive dysfunction. A drug that measurably degrades verbal fluency in *healthy* volunteers is a specific concern for someone who already struggles with verbal processing.
>
> **The counterweight is weaker than it looks.** Hindmarch, Trick & Ridout 2005 (PMID 16205916) found only minor transient impairment, but it is **n=24, 450mg/day, acute three-way crossover.** An acute crossover in 24 people is not a symmetric counterweight to a 12-week Class I parallel trial. **The asymmetry favours Salinsky.**

*The reassurance that does not survive:* **"RLS doses sit below the cognitively-active range" is CONTRADICTED** `[primary, verified]`.

| Source | Pregabalin RLS dose |
|---|---|
| **Allen et al., NEJM 2014** (pivotal, 52 weeks) | **300 mg/day** |
| Mayo Clin Proc RLS algorithm | *"usually in the range of 150 to 450 mg daily"* |
| Winkelman & Wipper, *RLS: A Review* | 75 to 600 mg daily |

> **The RLS range overlaps the cognitively-active range; it does not sit below it.** The review's upper bound (600mg) **is Salinsky's dose.** The pivotal RLS trial used **300 mg/day**, and cognitive data at 300mg specifically is mixed rather than reassuring: one perioperative study at 150mg BID found significant negative effects on executive function including inhibition and working memory versus placebo `[secondary — unverified]`.
>
> **The RLS literature names the harm directly.** Mayo algorithm on alpha-2-delta ligands in RLS: *"Class adverse effects include daytime drowsiness, dizziness, unsteadiness, and cognitive disturbances."* AASM 2025 on pregabalin: *"Adverse effects included somnolence and dizziness… Patients who are at a high risk of side effects with this class of medications may choose other treatment options."* `[primary, verified]`
>
> **The defensible version is narrower: cognitive risk is dose-dependent, starting low (75mg) may reduce it, it has not been shown to eliminate it, and the pivotal RLS dose is one at which impairment has been demonstrated.**

*Other pregabalin costs:*

| Downside | Grade | Note |
|---|---|---|
| **Sedation, dizziness** | **ESTABLISHED** | Dose-dependent; reducible by slow titration. AEs 17% vs 9% placebo. **70–90% of participants in *all* arms reported some AE.** |
| **Weight gain** | ESTABLISHED | Real and often unwelcome. |
| **Dependence / withdrawal** | **CASE REPORTS → emerging systematic evidence** | Withdrawal within **12h–7 days** of stopping. **Documented at regular therapeutic doses with no abuse history.** Misuse potential is established and higher than gabapentin's; controlled in the UK (Class C). |

> **Worth flagging: swapping duloxetine for pregabalin to escape a hard taper is not obviously a win.** Pregabalin has its own withdrawal syndrome, documented at therapeutic doses. It trades one discontinuation problem for another.

> **The honest, narrow claim:** pregabalin is a legitimate, guideline-supported option for this reader's RLS, where duloxetine offers nothing. Its fibromyalgia advantage over duloxetine is **not demonstrable** (the NNT confidence intervals overlap almost totally: pregabalin 9.7 [7.2–15] vs duloxetine 8 [4–21], and the GRADE contrast of HIGH vs LOW is substantially an artifact of which Cochrane team wrote which review). Adding it to duloxetine has **one positive RCT and one documented harm**, additive drowsiness, which lands exactly on this reader's primary disability. **That trade-off is his and his doctor's to make, and it should be presented with the trial and the drowsiness, not as a clever untested idea.**

### The three variables, and the measurement problem

| Variable | Status | Time course |
|---|---|---|
| **Duloxetine** | 2 weeks in | Early AEs characteristically attenuate over weeks, **so RLS may improve on its own** |
| **Melatonin** | Nightly, ongoing background | Fast on/off (t½ ~40 min) |
| **Iron** | **Started this week** | **~12 weeks to act** |
| **Dose increase** | Proposed, ~2 weeks away | Days to weeks |

> **Three are already moving and a fourth is proposed. If the dose changes now, four variables are in motion and no result will be interpretable: not the RLS, not the sleep, not the fatigue.**

### What nobody has studied

1. **Duloxetine and PLMS.** Never measured, at any dose, ever `[no evidence exists]`.
2. **Whether correcting iron reduces antidepressant-induced RLS specifically.** Searched directly and repeatedly; nothing `[no evidence exists]`.
3. **Melatonin and RLS on an SNRI background.** Whittom's subjects' SNRI status could not be verified `[no evidence exists]`.
4. **Whether duloxetine is better or worse than other serotonergic antidepressants for RLS.** Nobody has compared them; duloxetine has no ranked position `[no evidence exists]`.
5. **PLMS dose-dependence for any SNRI.** The only escalation study confounds dose with cumulative nights `[no evidence exists]`.

### What to raise at the appointment

Framed as questions to ask, not decisions to make. The decision belongs to the reader and his physician.

- **"What is my TSAT?"** It is the contraindication check for the iron already started, and ferritin cannot substitute for it. Above 45% means no iron.
- **"What is my ferritin number, and is it under 75?"** Not "is it normal." The RLS threshold sits far above the lab's deficiency flag.
- **"Given the fibromyalgia and ME/CFS, could my ferritin be reading high?"** Ferritin is an acute-phase reactant, so the true iron status may be lower than 70.
- **"Should the iron be alternate-day rather than daily?"** The hepcidin argument is counterintuitive and worth raising.
- **"Iron will not report back for about three months. Does that change the timing of anything else?"**
- **"Duloxetine has never been measured for periodic limb movements. Does that change how we would watch this?"** The literature cannot forecast this; it can only be observed.
- **"If we change the dose now, what will we be able to conclude?"** Three RLS variables are already in motion.
- **"Is pregabalin worth knowing about for the RLS specifically, and what would it cost me cognitively?"** There is one positive combination RCT, and it documents additive drowsiness.
