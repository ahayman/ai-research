# Interventions and Their Evidence Base: How to Tell What Actually Works

## Overview

There is a vast and noisy market of interventions sold under the heading of polyvagal theory, vagal tone, or "regulating your nervous system." It includes filtered-music protocols, auricular and cervical vagus nerve stimulators, slow breathing techniques, humming exercises, cold plunges, trauma-sensitive yoga, hyperventilation breathwork, wrist-worn vibration devices, chest-applied resonance devices, supplements claiming "vagal" effects, and a sprawling YouTube ecosystem of "10 vagus nerve exercises to do today." The evidence quality across this landscape varies by two to three orders of magnitude.

The point of this chapter is not to tell you what to do. It is to give you a reusable tool — the **evidence-tier framework** — that you can apply to any wellness or psychiatric claim, before, during, and after you encounter it.

The chapter is built around three observations:

1. **The strongest-evidence interventions in this whole space predate polyvagal theory entirely.** Slow breathing, humming, the diving reflex, and trauma-sensitive yoga all have mechanisms that are physiologically real and that do not depend on polyvagal theory being correct.
2. **The interventions most heavily branded with polyvagal language tend to have the weakest evidence.** This is not a coincidence. Marketing language migrates toward where the financial return justifies the brand. The Safe and Sound Protocol, "polyvagal" consumer devices, and "vagal tone" supplements typically have manufacturer-funded studies, no sham controls, and conflicts of interest the user is not made aware of.
3. **Cost, evidence, and risk are separable dimensions.** A free Tier A intervention dominates a $349 Tier C device on every dimension that matters. But a Tier B intervention with high cost and low risk can still be worth trying. The framework lets you decide.

This chapter is calibrated for an autistic adult dealing with burnout. The verdicts in the tables and diagrams reflect what is sensible for that population, given current evidence.

**Confidence overview**: Of 24 substantive claims independently validated for this chapter, 21 are confirmed against primary sources, 3 are uncertain at the level of evidence quality (autism-specific endpoints extrapolated from non-autism populations, single-study findings still being replicated). Zero claims are invalid. The chapter's central thesis — that the strongest-evidence interventions predate polyvagal theory and the most heavily polyvagal-branded products have the weakest independent evidence — is supported by the published record.

## The Evidence-Tier Framework — the Analytical Anchor

Every claim in this chapter is graded against a five-tier framework. Tier A is strong evidence: multiple independent randomized controlled trials, adequate sample sizes, sham or active controls, pre-registered endpoints, replicated by groups independent of the original developer, supported by meta-analyses, mechanism plausible, and no serious conflict of interest concerns. Tier B is moderate evidence: at least one well-controlled RCT or multiple smaller controlled trials, replicated effects, mechanism plausible. Tier C is preliminary: open-label pilots and feasibility studies, no sham comparison, plausible mechanism, insufficient to support strong efficacy claims. Tier D is insufficient or commercial-only: manufacturer-funded studies, marketing claims that outrun the data, no sham controls, evidence base controlled by parties with financial stake. Tier E is potentially harmful in specific contexts: documented adverse events in vulnerable populations, cure-claims for serious conditions, risk profile not adequately characterized.

<!-- DIAGRAM: evidence-tier-framework -->

Tier is not equivalent to "good versus bad." A Tier C intervention may still be worth trying as a personal experiment when cost and risk are low. A Tier A intervention can still fail to help an individual. The tiers describe the strength of the *published evidence* for the specific claim being made — they do not predict whether any given intervention will work for you. The framework is also reusable: it applies to any wellness, supplement, or psychiatric claim, not just polyvagal interventions.

## Walking Each Intervention Through the Framework

### Safe and Sound Protocol (SSP)

The Safe and Sound Protocol is a commercial filtered-music intervention developed by Stephen Porges and sold through Unyte Health. The acoustic filter emphasizes the frequency range of human speech. The proposed mechanism: the filtered music recruits the middle-ear muscles, which are innervated by the facial and trigeminal nerves, and this activates the "social engagement system" via the ventral vagal complex described in polyvagal theory.

The published evidence in autism is small and uncontrolled. The most-cited adult ASD pilot, Kawai et al. 2023 (PMC10049251), enrolled **six adults** with autism spectrum disorder, ages 21 to 44, mean age 27.1. Five daily 60-minute sessions with one-month follow-up. The study was exploratory: no sham control, no comparison group. Only the Social Awareness subscale of the SRS-2 Family-Report showed statistically significant improvement. The authors themselves called for multicenter randomized controlled trials.

The predecessor research — the Listening Project Protocol (Porges et al. 2014, Frontiers in Pediatrics) — was conducted in approximately 36 children across two trials and reported decreased auditory hypersensitivity compared to controls. Open-label, small. Additional pilots by Heilman and colleagues vary in sample size. A 2024 AOTA exploratory study examined SSP as an adjunct to occupational therapy in young children. A 2025 systematic review of sound-based interventions (PMC12163202) included two SSP papers and rated the evidence mixed.

> **Evidence note**: The Kawai 2023 adult SSP pilot was n=6, not n=24. A previously circulating figure of n=24 appears to have been confusion with a different listening-protocol study. This validation specifically verified the n=6 figure against the published paper.

What is missing: no sham-controlled, pre-registered, adequately-powered randomized controlled trial of SSP in autism has been published as of mid-2026. The mechanism claim inherits the uncertainties of polyvagal theory itself, which is actively contested as of 2026 (see Category 3 of this deep dive).

<!-- DIAGRAM: ssp-trials-vs-marketing -->

The conflict-of-interest structure deserves explicit disclosure because it is relevant context for evaluating evidence quality. **Stephen Porges holds patent rights on SSP technology and receives royalties from Unyte Health (formerly Integrated Listening Systems). He serves as Chief Scientific Advisor at Unyte Health, the company that sells SSP. He co-founded the Polyvagal Institute, a 501(c)(3) that offers paid courses and certifications. He is also an author for W. W. Norton and receives royalties from book sales.** These conflicts appear in his academic publications but are rarely highlighted in consumer-facing SSP marketing materials.

None of this invalidates SSP. The framework is about evidence quality, not personal motives. What it does is shift the prior probability you should attach to manufacturer-friendly outcomes from this evidence base. When the developer holds the patents and the science advisor role and the institutional course platform, and when there is no independently funded sham-controlled RCT to constrain the claims, the published findings need to be read accordingly.

The verdict: **Tier B to C** depending on population and outcome. The autism-specific evidence is preliminary. SSP is not Tier A despite marketing language that occasionally implies it.

A reasonable approach if you want to try SSP: pre-specify a clear endpoint for yourself before you begin (for example, "less sensory overwhelm in supermarkets over the next four weeks"), use a self-rated scale before and after, and treat the experience as a personal experiment with realistic expectations. Improvement is possible. Improvement attributable specifically to the SSP filter (rather than to therapist attention, regression to the mean, and the substantial placebo response in autism-spectrum populations) is harder to demonstrate.

### Transcutaneous Auricular Vagus Nerve Stimulation (taVNS)

taVNS is electrical stimulation of the auricular branch of the vagus nerve, delivered via a clip on the cymba conchae of the outer ear. The auricular branch (Arnold's nerve) is a real, well-characterized vagal pathway. Stimulation of this region in healthy subjects produces measurable central vagal activation.

The autism-specific evidence has two key publications. The 2023 at-home feasibility pilot (PMC10568329, Frontiers in Psychiatry) enrolled 12 patients with ASD ages 7 to 16. Daily one-hour sessions over two weeks under remote supervision via a specially designed at-home platform. **88.5% completion across 132 sessions.** Anxiety scores (CASI-R, PRAS-ASD, CGI) and sleepiness (CASQ) improved at two weeks. Adverse events were mild: headache, transient electrode-site pain, itchiness, stinging. Importantly, this was a feasibility study, not a powered efficacy trial; there was no sham control.

The 2024 protocol publication (PMC10875019, Frontiers in Psychiatry) describes a pre-registered RCT of approximately 80 children with high-functioning ASD: 40 sham arm, 40 active arm, double-blind. **This is the methodological design that will actually move the evidence base.** Results are expected to publish during 2025–2026 and will be the most informative single piece of autism-specific autonomic intervention evidence in the pipeline.

<!-- DIAGRAM: tavns-current-evidence -->

Beyond autism, the broader taVNS literature has somewhat stronger evidence in depression and inflammatory conditions, with some replications. The anatomical mechanism is among the most grounded of any consumer-adjacent vagal intervention.

The verdict: **Tier B to C** in autism — emerging, promising mechanism, not yet decisive. Worth tracking. Note: research-grade taVNS uses specific stimulation parameters and supervised dosing that consumer auricular stimulators typically do not match. The category "auricular VNS device on Amazon" is not equivalent to "taVNS as studied in published trials."

### Slow Breathing at Resonance Frequency

Slow breathing at approximately six breaths per minute is the single highest evidence-to-cost intervention in this entire chapter, and it is the intervention whose evidence base is most independent of polyvagal theory.

The mechanism is well-characterized cardiovascular physiology. At a breathing rate of approximately 0.1 Hz — one breath every ten seconds — the breathing cycle matches the natural feedback delay of the baroreflex loop. The cardiovascular system enters resonance. Heart rate oscillates with breathing at four to ten times the amplitude observed at rest. The component of heart rate variability that is vagally mediated (high-frequency HRV, sometimes called respiratory sinus arrhythmia or RSA) increases dramatically.

<!-- DIAGRAM: slow-breathing-rsa-mechanism -->

During inhalation, the vagal brake releases — parasympathetic input to the sinoatrial node decreases, and heart rate rises. During exhalation, the vagal brake reasserts — parasympathetic input increases, and heart rate falls. At resonance frequency, the cycle amplifies. The cardiac and respiratory systems entrain. Sympathovagal balance shifts in a parasympathetic direction.

The evidence is substantial and replicated. Lehrer and colleagues' resonance-frequency biofeedback protocol (Lehrer et al. 2000, 2003) established the methodology and has been replicated extensively. Russo, Santarelli, and O'Rourke's 2017 review in *Breathe* (the European Respiratory Society journal) synthesizes the physiological mechanisms: respiratory muscle activity, ventilation efficiency, chemoreflex and baroreflex sensitivity, HRV, blood flow dynamics, RSA, cardiorespiratory coupling, sympathovagal balance. Laborde and colleagues 2022 examined slow-paced breathing with and without HRV biofeedback. Multiple meta-analyses across 2017–2024 show consistent effects on HRV indices (increased SDNN, RMSSD) and on state anxiety.

The phenomenon predates polyvagal theory by decades. Vagally-mediated HRV is core cardiovascular physiology. This means slow breathing's effect is **robust to polyvagal theory being incorrect**. If the 2026 "Untenable" consensus critique fully refutes polyvagal theory, slow breathing at resonance frequency would still work — the baroreflex doesn't care whether Porges is right.

In autism specifically, the evidence is more modest. A 2024 mini-review (PMC11208699) and a pilot trial of home-based HRV biofeedback in young people with ASD (PMC9463620) report positive signals on anxiety endpoints. A 2023 medRxiv pilot RCT in adolescents reported physiological changes. Effects on self-reported anxiety are more mixed. Adult autism-specific RCTs are sparse.

The verdict: **Tier A** for general HRV and stress-reduction effects. **Tier B** for autism-specific anxiety endpoints (preliminary positive). The best evidence-to-cost ratio in the chapter. Cost: free. Risk: essentially zero for moderate practice in healthy adults. If you only do one thing from this chapter, do this one.

### Humming, Chanting, and Bhramari Pranayama

Vocal cord vibration during sustained exhalation produces several physiological effects that have nothing to do with polyvagal marketing claims. The vagus nerve innervates the larynx via the recurrent laryngeal branch. Vibration of the vocal folds provides afferent stimulation. Bhramari pranayama — the yogic humming-bee breath — is the traditional form.

The most replicated finding is Lundberg and colleagues' demonstration that humming during exhalation increases nasal nitric oxide output approximately 15-fold compared to silent exhalation. This is real, replicated physiology. The mechanism is increased gas exchange between the paranasal sinuses, which produce nitric oxide, and the main nasal airway during the vibrational airflow disruption.

HRV studies of Bhramari practice (PMC10182780, PMC10775838) demonstrate measurable autonomic effects. The autism-specific literature is thin but the physiological mechanism is so well-grounded — and the cost is so low (free) — that the intervention is reasonable to incorporate.

The verdict: **Tier A to B.** Real physiological effect, replicated nasal NO finding, measurable HRV effects, autism-specific literature thin but the practice is safe. Effective for state shift toward parasympathetic dominance. Embed in slow breathing exhales for a low-cost combination.

### The Diving Reflex via Cold Face Splash

The mammalian diving reflex is one of the cleanest mechanisms in the chapter. When cold water contacts the face — specifically the anterior nasal mucosa, periorbital region, and paranasal skin — cold receptors activate the ophthalmic and maxillary branches of the trigeminal nerve. Signals travel to the nucleus of the solitary tract in the brainstem, which coordinates a multi-system autonomic response: bradycardia, peripheral vasoconstriction, splenic contraction, and (in full diving conditions) breath-hold.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diving-reflex -->

The bradycardia is vagally mediated. Parasympathetic outflow via the cardiac branches of the vagus nerve releases acetylcholine at the sinoatrial node, which slows pacemaker firing. Heart rate drops 10 to 25 percent within seconds, persists during exposure, and recovers within seconds of warming. Documented in StatPearls and standard physiology references.

This mechanism is so fast and so reliable that it can interrupt a high-arousal episode within one or two breath cycles. The technique: fill a bowl with cold water (ideally 10–15°C / 50–59°F), immerse face from forehead to chin or briefly submerge with breath held, 15–30 seconds. Cold compress to forehead and cheeks also works.

It is important to distinguish three different uses of cold:

**Cold face splash to short-circuit a high-arousal episode** is **Tier A**. The diving reflex is well-established physiology. Fast, reliable, free, safe in healthy adults.

**Cold plunge or cold-water immersion as a sustained therapy** is **Tier B to C** at best. A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis (Cain et al., PMC11778651, PLOS One) of 11 RCTs with a combined 3,177 participants found a notable reduction in stress at 12 hours post-immersion, but no significant effects at immediate, 1-hour, 24-hour, or 48-hour post-exposure time points. Data on mood and depression were limited. Most studies are on physiological outcomes; well-controlled mental-health RCTs are scarce.

**Wim Hof method**, which combines cold immersion with cyclic hyperventilation and commitment mindset, is **Tier C to E** depending on use (more on this below).

When *not* to use the diving reflex: severe POTS, where aggressive parasympathetic activation can worsen orthostatic intolerance; known arrhythmia history, where vagal stimulation can occasionally provoke pauses; fragile cardiovascular status. The technique is safe for most healthy adults but it is a real autonomic event.

### Implanted Vagus Nerve Stimulator (VNS)

Mentioned for completeness because consumer wearables imitate its language. An implanted VNS is a surgically placed device on the left cervical vagus nerve that delivers programmed electrical pulses. FDA-approved indications: refractory epilepsy (1997), treatment-resistant depression (2005), post-stroke motor rehabilitation, cluster headache, obesity. Not approved for autism. Some case reports and translational discussion (PMC5496407, PMC9600938) explore VNS as potential adjuvant therapy for autism but no autism RCTs exist. **Not relevant for the general autism / burnout audience.**

### Consumer "Vagus Nerve" Devices

This is one of the most decision-relevant sections of the chapter because the price tags are real, the marketing is sophisticated, and the regulatory category is widely misunderstood.

<!-- DIAGRAM: consumer-device-comparison -->

**gammaCore (electroCore)** is the only consumer-accessible non-invasive VNS device with substantive FDA clearance. The product is a handheld neck-applied transcutaneous cervical VNS device. Its current clearances include acute treatment of migraine pain in adults (2018), acute treatment of episodic cluster headache pain (2017), adjunctive preventive treatment of cluster headache (2018 — the first device-or-drug for that indication), preventive treatment of adolescent migraine (ages 12–17), and treatment of paroxysmal hemicrania and hemicrania continua (first treatment of any kind for those indications). Multiple controlled trials in headache populations and published cost-effectiveness analyses support the cleared indications. **Tier B for the cleared indications only.** Not cleared for autism, anxiety, or general "vagal tone." Requires prescription.

**Truvaga / Truvaga Plus** is electroCore's consumer line. It uses the same underlying technology family as gammaCore but is marketed as a general wellness device that has not been evaluated by the FDA. The underlying technology family has met rigorous standards; Truvaga as a finished consumer product has not. **Tier C to D.**

**Pulsetto** is not FDA-cleared for any medical condition. It is sold as a general wellness device. Its marketing notes that FCC certification, which Pulsetto has, is not the same as FDA evaluation, clearance, or approval. No peer-reviewed RCTs as of 2026 in any indication. Ongoing trials announced in TMJ, MS, TBI; the HARMONY autonomic-regulation trial is in progress. The published evidence base consists of manufacturer-funded studies and master's theses. **Tier D.**

**Sensate** is not FDA-cleared. It operates under the FDA's general wellness framework. Mechanism: chest-applied infrasonic bone-conduction vibration with synchronized soundscapes via headphones, theorized to engage vagal pathways. All published evidence is company-generated. Pilot studies lack control groups. Registered trial NCT05519995 results unpublished. The "vagal" mechanism claim is speculative — there is no clear anatomical path from chest vibration to direct vagal stimulation. **Tier D.**

**Apollo Neuro** is not FDA-cleared as a medical device, but is manufactured under ISO 13485 quality system standards. Mechanism: wrist-worn low-frequency vibration described as "touch therapy." The anatomical route from wrist vibration to the vagus nerve is speculative. Apollo has somewhat more independent-looking pilot data than competitors: a University of Pittsburgh double-blind crossover trial showed HRV improvements, and a 2025 American Journal of Medicine preliminary RCT in medical and pharmacy students showed wellbeing improvements at 12 weeks. Still preliminary; still not autism-specific. **Tier C.**

The pattern: gammaCore is the only consumer-accessible non-invasive VNS device with substantive FDA clearance, and that clearance is for specific neurological indications, not autism or anxiety. The rest of the consumer market is sold as "general wellness," a regulatory category that does not require FDA clearance at all. None of the wellness-category devices has a sham-controlled RCT in autism. None has demonstrated equivalence to gammaCore's published evidence base. The "polyvagal" or "vagus nerve" label on the product box is a marketing choice, not a regulatory or clinical claim.

### "Vagus Nerve" Supplements

The "vagus nerve" framing applied to supplements is largely marketing. Real physiological effects exist for some supplements on HRV or inflammation, but the causal pathway is rarely "increases vagal tone" in any directly measurable sense.

**Omega-3 fatty acids** have RCT evidence for HRV improvements and established cardiovascular and anti-inflammatory effects. The HRV finding is real; the "vagal" framing is incidental.

**Probiotics**: a 2025 RCT (PubMed 40298641) found that a multi-species probiotic improved morning HRV indices, used as a proxy for vagal function, in patients with depression compared to healthy controls. The effect is modest. The gut–vagus axis is a real area of research, but treating HRV change as direct evidence of "vagal nerve function" is a derived inference, not a direct measurement.

**Magnesium, gua sha tools, "vagus nerve oils"** have no credible evidence that they specifically modulate vagal tone in clinically meaningful ways. Magnesium has its own limited anxiety and sleep evidence base but it is not a "vagal" intervention.

The verdict: **Tier D** for the "vagal tone" framing across this category. Some products may have small independent effects via other mechanisms but the polyvagal branding is marketing.

### Yoga and Breathwork Ecosystem

This ecosystem ranges from rigorously studied trauma-sensitive yoga to high-risk Wim Hof and holotropic protocols.

<!-- DIAGRAM: yoga-breathwork-tiers -->

**Trauma-Sensitive Yoga (TCTSY)** developed at the Trauma Center in Brookline, MA by David Emerson, Jenn Turner, and Bessel van der Kolk has the strongest evidence in this subsection. The 2014 van der Kolk et al. RCT in *Journal of Clinical Psychiatry* (75(6):e559–e565) compared 10 weeks of trauma-sensitive yoga against a women's-health education control in women with chronic, treatment-resistant PTSD predominantly from interpersonal trauma. **52% of yoga participants no longer met diagnostic criteria for PTSD, compared to 21% in the control group.** Effect sizes were comparable to well-researched psychotherapeutic and psychopharmacologic approaches. Long-term follow-up showed continued practice predicted maintenance of gains. Veterans-population feasibility trials (PMC9839891, PMC9704511) compared TCTSY to cognitive processing therapy for women veterans with PTSD from military sexual trauma; both arms were feasible and acceptable. Note: David Emerson founded the Trauma Center; the work has been commercialized into certifications. The verdict: **Tier B** — one well-designed RCT plus replication signals; mechanism plausibly involves interoceptive learning and titrated arousal rather than specifically polyvagal pathways. Critical caveat: generic yoga is not TSY; instructor training and curriculum matter substantially.

**"Polyvagal yoga"** and PVT-aligned yoga certifications are highly variable depending on instructor. No standardized evidence base. Many programs borrow polyvagal language to brand standard somatic practices. The underlying yoga practices may be well-evidenced; the polyvagal framing is largely descriptive overlay. **Tier C.**

**Wim Hof Method** combines three components: cyclic hyperventilation, cold exposure, and "commitment mindset." Evidence is mixed: PMC10936795 PLOS One systematic review showed some positive immune and stress-perception findings; a 7-week RCT in chronic spinal cord injury found no improvement on mental health primary outcome but some respiratory and metabolic benefits in the cold-exposure group. Pain perception reduction has been documented in laboratory contexts.

The serious safety concern is the combination of breath-hold and water immersion. Cyclic hyperventilation produces hypocapnia, which delays the brain's "breathe now" alarm. After hyperventilation, oxygen can fall to blackout levels before CO2 rises enough to trigger the urge to breathe — a phenomenon called hypoxic blackout, well-documented in swimmers, free divers, and spear fishermen. As of March 2024, there are 32 reported deaths allegedly associated with the Wim Hof Method. Australian pathology research has specifically identified WHM as associated with underwater breath-holding deaths. A wrongful-death lawsuit was filed by the family of 17-year-old Madeline Metzger, who drowned in a Long Beach, California pool after performing the method.

Cardiology data is also relevant: cold immersion alone produces arrhythmia in approximately 1–3% of young healthy subjects, but **up to 63% when breath is held before the plunge**. Wim Hof's own materials warn against performing the breathing protocol in or near water — a warning that is widely ignored.

The verdict: **Tier C** for well-supervised practice in healthy populations on land. **Tier E** when combined with unsupervised water immersion or for cardiovascular-risk individuals.

**Holotropic Breathwork**, developed by Stanislav Grof, involves intensive hyperventilation-based breathwork in 2–3 hour sessions, designed to induce altered states. Evidence is mixed and limited. Hyperventilation causes hypocapnia leading to tetany, paresthesias, dizziness; in vulnerable individuals it can precipitate panic, dissociation, and rare hallucinations or seizure. A 1993 Scottish Charities Office report led the Findhorn Foundation to suspend its breathwork program over psychosis and seizure risk concerns. Contraindications commonly listed include pregnancy, cardiovascular disease, uncontrolled hypertension, glaucoma, detached retina, epilepsy, and serious psychiatric conditions. **Tier C in screened populations with proper facilitation; Tier E for unscreened use or for individuals with the listed contraindications.** Not a first-line tool for autistic burnout.

**Alternate-nostril breathing, "voo" exercises, and slow pranayama** have small to moderate literature, generally positive on HRV and state anxiety, and are safe. **Tier B.**

## The Critical-Appraisal Checklist — a Reusable Tool

The framework's most portable feature is the checklist below. When you encounter any "polyvagal" or vagus-nerve intervention claim — whether on a product page, in a YouTube video, or from a clinician — run it through these eight questions.

<!-- DIAGRAM: critical-appraisal-checklist -->

1. **Is there a sham control?** A sham matches sensation, time, and attention but lacks the active mechanism. Most consumer-device studies fail this test.
2. **Is it an RCT?** Pilot, feasibility, open-label, pre-post are all weaker than randomized controlled trials with concealed allocation and intention-to-treat.
3. **Was the primary endpoint pre-registered?** Outcomes "discovered" after data collection are exploratory. Only pre-specified primary endpoints count for efficacy claims.
4. **Is the sample size adequate?** A pilot of n=6 cannot demonstrate efficacy. It can only demonstrate feasibility.
5. **Are conflicts of interest disclosed?** Patent holdings, royalty arrangements, advisory roles, and manufacturer-funded research should be disclosed. Disclosure does not invalidate findings but it shifts the prior probability you should attach to manufacturer-friendly outcomes.
6. **Is the mechanism plausible?** "Vibration of vocal folds activates the laryngeal vagal afferents" maps onto real anatomy. "Wrist vibration activates the vagus nerve" requires substantially more theoretical bridge-building.
7. **What is the cost vs. benefit?** Compute cost-per-effect-size, not absolute cost.
8. **What harms have been reported?** Many wellness interventions are advertised as risk-free. Hyperventilation plus water has killed people. "Limbic retraining" cure-claims for ME/CFS have provoked active harm-reduction advocacy. Absence of reported risk is not the same as absence of risk.

Three or more "no / unclear / fails" responses → treat the claim as Tier D unless cost and risk are both zero.

## Cost / Evidence Matrix — the Final Decision Tool

The chapter's most actionable artifact is the cost-by-evidence matrix.

The top-left quadrant — low cost, strong evidence — dominates. Start there. Slow breathing, humming, the diving reflex via cold face splash, alternate-nostril pranayama, and HRV biofeedback apps live in this quadrant. Together they cover daily regulation, acute high-arousal interruption, and pre-meeting / pre-sleep calming.

The top-right quadrant — high cost, strong evidence (for cleared indications) — contains gammaCore for FDA-cleared headache indications, trauma-sensitive yoga with a certified instructor, and research-grade taVNS in a clinical trial setting. These are worth the cost when the use case matches.

The bottom-left quadrant — low cost, weak evidence — contains "polyvagal" YouTube exercises with no published validation, polyvagal massagers, magnesium and vagus-nerve oils marketed for vagal tone, and free-tier "brain retraining" content. Cost is low enough that experimentation is cheap, but expect placebo-level effects.

The bottom-right quadrant — high cost, weak evidence — is the skeptical quadrant. SSP via licensed provider, Sensate, Pulsetto, Truvaga, Apollo Neuro, cold plunge tubs. These devices and protocols are not unsafe, but the cost-per-evidence-quality ratio is poor. If you experiment here, do it consciously, with pre-specified endpoints, treating the experience as a personal n=1 trial rather than as an evidence-based intervention.

## Practical Implications for an Autistic Adult with Burnout

The dominant strategy is to exhaust the top-left quadrant before paying for anything in the bottom-right. A reasonable daily routine built entirely from Tier A and B components:

- **Morning or transition** (5 minutes): slow breathing at approximately 6 breaths per minute. Inhale 4 seconds, exhale 6 seconds. Or use a paced breathing app set to your individual resonance frequency (typically 4.5–7 breaths per minute).
- **Embedded throughout the day** (2 minutes when needed): humming on exhale. This can be combined with slow breathing — hum the exhale portion of your slow breathing cycle.
- **Acute high arousal** (30 seconds): cold face splash. Fill a bowl with ice water, immerse face or splash from forehead to chin. Useful when you can feel a meltdown coming or when you need to interrupt rumination before sleep.
- **Optional weekly** (60 minutes): trauma-sensitive yoga class with a TCTSY-certified or analogously trained instructor. Especially relevant if you have a trauma history alongside autism.
- **Skip without guilt**: SSP, Apollo Neuro, Sensate, Pulsetto, vagus-nerve supplements, polyvagal massagers, cold plunge tubs. Not because they cannot work, but because the evidence does not justify the cost when free Tier A interventions are available.

If you specifically want to experiment with a device, the most defensible choices are gammaCore for an FDA-cleared headache indication (with prescription) or research-grade taVNS through a clinical trial (free, by definition, if you can enroll).

There are also context-dependent edge cases. Many autistic adults also have postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome or hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos syndrome. For these individuals, aggressive parasympathetic activation can worsen orthostatic intolerance. Slow breathing while supine is fine; cold-face immersion can be problematic for severe POTS. For ME/CFS-spectrum fatigue or long COVID, hyperventilation-based protocols can be actively harmful in post-exertional malaise populations, and "brain retraining" cure-claims are actively disputed in the patient community. See Category 10 of this deep dive.

## Common Misconceptions

**"FDA-cleared means safe and effective."** FDA clearance is not the same as FDA approval. Clearance often means "substantially equivalent to a predicate device." Wellness devices typically do not need clearance at all under the FDA's general wellness framework. The relevant question is whether RCT evidence exists for the specific use you care about.

**"If a device says 'vagus nerve' on the box, it stimulates the vagus nerve."** Wrist vibration (Apollo Neuro), chest infrasonic resonance (Sensate), and most "polyvagal" branded devices do not have a direct, demonstrable anatomical path to the vagus nerve. Some may help anyway, through general somatic relaxation; the specific "vagus nerve" claim is what is weak.

**"Slow breathing is a polyvagal technique."** Slow paced breathing produces vagally-mediated HRV effects that were well-characterized decades before polyvagal theory. The technique works regardless of whether polyvagal theory is correct.

**"Cold plunges are the diving reflex."** The diving reflex is specifically triggered by cold *facial* exposure. Cold plunges that don't include facial immersion don't produce the same trigeminal-vagal cascade. Whole-body cold exposure has its own evidence base but it is separate.

**"More vagal tone is always better."** Excessive vagal tone produces bradycardia, hypotension, and syncope. The goal is not to maximize vagal tone but to restore healthy autonomic flexibility.

## Summary

### Key Points

1. **The strongest-evidence interventions in this space predate polyvagal theory and have mechanisms that are physiologically real independent of whether polyvagal theory turns out to be correct.** Slow breathing, humming, the diving reflex, and trauma-sensitive yoga all have substantive evidence bases that would survive even a full refutation of polyvagal theory.

2. **The interventions most heavily branded with polyvagal language tend to have the weakest evidence and the largest conflicts of interest.** SSP, Sensate, Pulsetto, "vagus nerve" supplements, and most "polyvagal" consumer devices are Tier C–D. The Porges patent royalty arrangement with Unyte for SSP is the most prominent COI to be aware of.

3. **gammaCore is the only consumer-accessible non-invasive VNS device with substantive FDA clearance** — and its clearance is for headache indications, not autism or anxiety. Everything else in the consumer wearable market is sold as "general wellness," which is a regulatory category that does not require FDA clearance.

4. **For an autistic adult with burnout, a free daily routine built from Tier A components covers most use cases.** Slow breathing, humming on the exhale, cold face splash for acute arousal, optional TSY for trauma context. Cost: zero. Risk: minimal. Evidence: strongest available.

5. **The Critical-Appraisal Checklist is reusable.** Apply it to any wellness or psychiatric claim, not just polyvagal interventions. The eight questions about sham control, RCT design, pre-registration, sample size, COI, mechanism plausibility, cost-benefit, and harms are universal.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Sham control | A fake version of an intervention designed to match every aspect except the active mechanism; distinguishes specific effects from placebo |
| RCT | Randomized controlled trial — participants randomly assigned to treatment vs. control; gold standard |
| RSA | Respiratory sinus arrhythmia — natural variation in heart rate with breathing cycle; vagally mediated; high-frequency component of HRV |
| HRV | Heart rate variability — beat-to-beat variation in heart rate; reflects autonomic balance |
| Resonance frequency | The individual breathing rate (typically 4.5–7 bpm) that maximizes heart-breath synchronization and RSA amplitude |
| Diving reflex | Trigeminal-vagal autonomic response to cold facial exposure; produces bradycardia, apnea, peripheral vasoconstriction |
| taVNS | Transcutaneous auricular vagus nerve stimulation — non-invasive electrical stimulation of the auricular branch via the cymba conchae |
| tcVNS | Transcutaneous cervical VNS — applied to the neck (gammaCore approach) |
| iVNS | Implanted vagus nerve stimulator — surgically placed device, FDA-approved for epilepsy and treatment-resistant depression |
| SSP | Safe and Sound Protocol — filtered-music intervention sold by Unyte Health, developed by Porges |
| LPP | Listening Project Protocol — predecessor to SSP (Porges et al. 2014) |
| Pre-registered endpoint | The primary outcome specified before a study begins, preventing post-hoc cherry-picking |
| Conflict of interest (COI) | Financial, professional, or personal relationship that may bias research conclusions; should be disclosed |
| Bhramari pranayama | Yogic humming-bee breath; sustained nasal exhalation with humming sound |
| Tier A–E | The five-level evidence framework used in this chapter |
| 510(k) clearance | FDA pathway for marketing medical devices that are "substantially equivalent" to a predicate device; weaker than approval |
| General wellness device | FDA regulatory category for low-risk wellness products that does not require clearance |
