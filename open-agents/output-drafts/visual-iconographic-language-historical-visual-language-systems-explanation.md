# Historical Visual Language Systems: From Leibniz to Icono

What Four Centuries of Attempts Reveal About the Hard Problems of Visual Language Design

---

## 1. Introduction: The Recurring Dream

The idea that humanity could communicate through pictures rather than words -- that visual symbols could bypass the babel of spoken tongues and speak directly to the mind -- is at least four centuries old. From Leibniz's algebraic dream of a universal character in 1666, through the pictographic and compositional systems of the twentieth century, to a peer-reviewed icon-based language proposal in 2023, each generation has reinvented this ambition, each time with different tools and different assumptions about what "visual language" means.

None of these systems achieved what its creator envisioned. But the reasons for each system's partial success or failure are profoundly instructive. They constitute an empirical record -- a series of controlled experiments in visual communication design -- that reveals where the genuine hard problems lie. Understanding this history is not academic nostalgia. For anyone attempting to build a new visual language, these seven systems represent the available data on what works, what fails, and where the uncharted territory begins.

This explanation draws on validated concept research (overall confidence 8/10). Of the specific factual claims, the vast majority were confirmed independently. ## 2. Leibniz's *Characteristica Universalis* (1666): The Algebraic Foundation

Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz was nineteen years old when he published *De Arte Combinatoria* in 1666, outlining a vision that would occupy him for the rest of his life: a *characteristica universalis*, a universal symbolic notation in which every fundamental concept would have a unique "real" character, and complex ideas would be constructed by combining simpler symbols according to fixed logical rules. The companion system, the *calculus ratiocinator*, would allow mechanical computation over these symbols to derive truths -- making reasoning as straightforward as arithmetic.

Leibniz's insight was structural. He recognized that meaning could be decomposed into primitives and recombined through formal rules. He called this an "alphabet of human thought" -- a finite inventory of fundamental concepts from which all others could be derived. This is the same principle that drives modern symbolic logic, programming languages, and, critically, Wierzbicka's Natural Semantic Metalanguage.

**What happened**: Leibniz never completed the system. He elaborated it in unpublished manuscripts around 1677-1690 but could not solve the foundational problem: determining empirically what the primitive concepts actually were. The philosophical ontology of his era provided no method for identifying irreducible semantic atoms.

**The lesson**: Decomposition-and-recombination is correct in principle. But a visual language built on this approach requires an empirically grounded inventory of primitives. Leibniz lacked what NSM and image schema theory now provide -- cross-linguistically validated semantic atoms and spatially grounded cognitive structures.

## 3. ISOTYPE (1925): Pictographic Clarity Without Grammar

In the 1920s, the Viennese philosopher Otto Neurath developed the International System of Typographic Picture Education -- ISOTYPE -- as a tool for communicating social, economic, and technological information to general audiences. His guiding principle was direct: "Words divide, pictures unite."

ISOTYPE used simplified, abstracted pictograms of people and things as repeatable units. A key design rule: greater quantities were represented by more pictograms of the same size, never by enlarging a single pictogram. This made quantitative comparison immediate and intuitive.

The system was a collaborative effort. Neurath served as philosopher-director. Gerd Arntz, a graphic artist who joined in 1929, designed approximately 4,000 signs and symbols. Marie Reidemeister (later Marie Neurath) served as the "transformer" -- the human bridge between domain experts who wanted to include every detail and visual designers who needed simplicity. This transformer role is often overlooked, but it was as important as the symbols themselves. It embodied the insight that visual communication is not just drawing pictures -- it is translating between levels of abstraction.

**What happened**: ISOTYPE succeeded brilliantly within its intended domain. Its principles became the foundation of modern information graphics, international signage, and data visualization. Every pictogram on an airplane safety card, every icon on a smartphone, descends from ISOTYPE's design principles.

**What it could not do**: ISOTYPE was never intended as a language and cannot function as one. It lacks grammar, compositionality, and the ability to express abstract propositions. You can show that steel production in Germany was higher than in France in 1935 -- but you cannot say "If steel production had been higher in 1932, the economic recovery might have been faster." ISOTYPE communicates data, not propositions.

**The lesson**: Pictographic clarity works brilliantly for concrete, context-bounded communication. But pictograms without grammar are a vocabulary, not a language. The distinction between a collection of symbols and a system capable of open-ended expression is the distinction between a dictionary and a language.

## 4. Blissymbolics (1949): The Most Successful Failure

Charles Bliss was a concentration camp survivor who blamed the catastrophes of the twentieth century on misunderstandings caused by spoken language -- nationalism, propaganda, the manipulation of words. His response was Semantography, published in 1949: a semantic visual writing system designed to eliminate the ambiguities of speech entirely.

Blissymbolics is the most linguistically sophisticated visual language system ever created. It uses approximately 120 basic geometric symbols (Bliss-characters) that combine to form over 6,500 Bliss-words. The system operates on a gridded square matrix with a "ground" line at the base and a "sky" line at the top, creating a vertical semantic space where position affects meaning -- earth-related concepts appear at the bottom, abstract concepts at the top.

### Composition Rules

Blissymbolics achieves genuine semantic compositionality through several mechanisms:

- **Left-to-right combination**: Characters combine sequentially to form compound words. "House" + "medical" = "hospital." "Eye" + "forward-action" = "to look."
- **Vertical semantic positioning**: Where a symbol sits on the grid encodes its meaning domain.
- **Grammatical indicators**: Small supralinear symbols mark parts of speech and grammatical features. A small square marks a noun. A small cone marks a verb. An inverted cone marks an adjective. Additional markers handle tense ("ago," "now," "then/future"), number (prefix), and possession (final position).
- **Metaphorical composition**: "Feeling" + "up" = "happy." This is a visual expression of the conceptual metaphor HAPPY IS UP -- precisely the kind of compositional metaphorical encoding that connects Blissymbolics to modern cognitive linguistics.

### The Unexpected Path to Adoption

Bliss's book was largely ignored upon publication. Then in 1971, Shirley McNaughton at the Ontario Crippled Children's Centre began using Blissymbolics to teach communication to children with cerebral palsy -- an application Bliss never intended. The system worked. Children who could not speak learned to communicate through symbol composition. By the time Blissymbolics Communication International (BCI) was established, the system was used in over 33 countries, primarily as an augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) tool.

Bliss himself was furious. He fought bitter legal battles with BCI until his death in 1985, insisting they had corrupted his universal language dream. But the AAC application succeeded precisely because the conditions were right: communication partners helped disambiguate, vocabulary was staged and taught, and the alternative -- no communication at all -- created enormous motivation to learn.

**Three critical problems revealed**:

1. **Proper names**: Blissymbolics has no mechanism for encoding proper names without phonetic fallback. You cannot write "Tokyo" or "Maria" in pure Blissymbolics.
2. **Complex syntax**: Embedded clauses, counterfactuals, and nested hypotheticals are extremely difficult to express. "If I had known you were coming, I would have baked a cake" pushes the system past its grammatical limits.
3. **Communities form around need, not ideals**: Blissymbolics found adoption not because of its universality but because it met a specific accessibility need. Languages need communities, and communities form around practical necessity.

**The lesson**: Semantic compositionality from geometric primitives works. The indicator system for grammar is clever and extensible. But the system reveals the hard limits of semasiographic design: proper names, complex syntax, and the social dynamics of language adoption.

## 5. LoCoS (1964): The Grid as Grammar

Yukio Ota conceived LoCoS -- the Lovers Communication System -- in 1964 while studying at the Venice National Art Institute. The name was aspirational: a visual Esperanto through which people could communicate "as lovers do," with effortless understanding. It was explicitly designed for deaf, mute, and illiterate users.

LoCoS uses 18 basic shapes, each assigned a consonant value that enables a kind of pseudo-phonetic reading. Symbols are positioned within a 3x3 grid, and their position within the matrix encodes relationships between concepts. "Sun"/"day" is a circle outline. "Feeling" is a heart shape. "Existence at a point" is a dot.

**Two innovations deserve attention**:

The **grid-based spatial grammar** was genuinely original. Rather than relying on linear sequence (the dominant approach in writing systems), LoCoS used position in a 2D matrix to encode meaning relationships. This intuition -- that spatial arrangement can do grammatical work -- anticipates some of the most interesting possibilities in visual language design.

The **phonetic bridge** -- consonant values assigned to basic shapes -- attempted to solve a problem that most visual languages ignore entirely: how does a semasiographic system interface with the world of spoken language? LoCoS's solution was rough but conceptually important.

**What happened**: LoCoS remained primarily an academic and design curiosity. Ota later applied his pictographic principles to the internationally recognized "running man" emergency exit sign, which became his most lasting practical contribution. The m-LoCoS project explored adapting the system for mobile communication interfaces.

**The lesson**: The 3x3 grid is elegant but limiting -- it constrains expressiveness to what can be composed in a small matrix. For simple compositions it works; for complex expressions it becomes ambiguous. A viable visual language may need to support both linear and spatial arrangement, using each where it is strongest.

## 6. Elephant's Memory (mid-1990s): The Case for Non-Linearity

Timothee Ingen-Housz, a French-Dutch media artist, created Elephant's Memory in the mid-1990s as an experimental "language kitchen" -- a playful environment for exploring the nature of visual language itself, not a practical communication tool. The system comprises approximately 150+ combinable logograms with a grammar based on size and spatial relationships rather than linear sequence.

> **Evidence note**: The logogram count varies across sources, with most indicating "more than 150 signs" or "more than a hundred combinable elements." The figure has been adjusted from the originally reported ~200 to ~150+ to better match available documentation.

What made Elephant's Memory radical was its commitment to **non-linearity**. Where every other visual language system -- including Blissymbolics -- retained the linear left-to-right reading convention of alphabetic writing, Elephant's Memory rejected linearity entirely. Reading and writing had no fixed direction. The eye floated across the composition and constructed meaning relationally, guided by the visual weights and connections of the signs. Meaning emerged from spatial composition -- relative size, proximity, enclosure, connection -- rather than from sequential ordering.

The system drew on influences from Wittgenstein's philosophy of language games, Chinese calligraphy, and the Swiss pictographic design tradition, and was deployed primarily as an educational tool for children and teenagers.

**What it demonstrated**: Non-linear spatial grammar is theoretically powerful. It can encode simultaneous relationships that linear sequence cannot -- multiple agents acting at once, nested containment, radial connections. Two-dimensional composition has strictly more representational bandwidth than one-dimensional sequence.

**What it also demonstrated**: The cognitive cost is real. Readers unfamiliar with the system found it disorienting. Centuries of linear text have created strong expectations about reading order, and abandoning those expectations requires significant retraining. A viable visual language may need to support both linear and spatial reading modes, or provide clear compositional cues that guide the eye through complex compositions.

## 7. Xu Bing's *Book from the Ground* (2003-2014): Cultural Icons as Proto-Language

The Chinese artist Xu Bing spent seven years gathering, experimenting with, revising, and arranging thousands of pictograms drawn from smartphones, computers, and public signage to construct a graphic novel telling 24 hours in the life of "Mr. Black," a typical urban white-collar worker. The result, *Book from the Ground*, was published by MIT Press in 2014.

Xu Bing's approach was the inverse of every system discussed so far. Rather than designing symbols from scratch, he asked: do the pictograms that already exist in contemporary culture -- emoji, app icons, public signage -- already constitute a visual vocabulary sufficient for narrative?

The answer was a qualified yes. The book is readable. People from different language backgrounds can follow the story. Xu Bing's studio even created translation software that converts English or Chinese text into the book's visual lexicon.

**The critical limitation**: The symbols work because readers share a cultural context, not because the symbols are inherently meaningful. A mailbox icon, a shopping cart, a floppy disk -- these are meaningful only within a particular technological and urban culture. They are not universal; they are widely shared among a specific demographic in a specific historical moment.

**The lesson**: Cultural context does tremendous heavy lifting in symbol interpretation. A system that relies on culturally specific icons is not truly semasiographic -- it is parasitic on shared cultural knowledge. True universality requires grounding in something more fundamental than contemporary convention: bodily experience, spatial relationships, perceptual structures -- the domain of image schemas.

## 8. Icono (2023): The Academic Proposal

Peter Kramer of the University of Padua published "Icono: a universal language that shows what it says" in *Frontiers in Psychology* in July 2023 -- the most recent academic attempt at visual language design, and the first to be peer-reviewed in a major scientific journal.

In Icono, words are typically strings of icons that illustrate their referents. "Train" = icon of a train. "Future" = clock icon with clockwise arrow. "Mammal" = icons of a cow and a mouse -- their combination's meaning given by what they have in common. Icono explicitly aims to match the expressive power of Chinese while being inherently reader-friendly and international.

**The key innovation** is self-illustrating semantics: unlike Blissymbolics (which uses abstract geometric composition), Icono uses concrete pictographic icons arranged in strings where the composition itself hints at the meaning. The sentence structure is graphically revealed before linguistic parsing begins.

**Target applications** include people with dyslexia, aphasia, cerebral palsy, and autism with speech impairment -- plus broader use as an international auxiliary written language.

**What remains to be seen**: Icono is well-theorized but awaits community formation and practical testing. The peer-reviewed approach sets a valuable academic precedent. But the system faces the same challenge as all pictographic approaches: concrete concepts are easy (train, cow, mouse), while abstract concepts require metaphorical composition (future = clock + rotation). Whether the metaphorical compositions are culturally transparent or require learning is an empirical question that has not yet been tested.

## 9. The Design Space: Four Dimensions of Variation

These seven systems do not fail or succeed randomly. They occupy different positions in a design space defined by at least four dimensions, and understanding this space is essential for any new visual language project.

### Dimension 1: Iconicity Spectrum

How much do the symbols look like what they represent?

- **High iconicity**: ISOTYPE, Xu Bing -- pictures resemble their referents directly.
- **Medium iconicity**: LoCoS, Icono, Elephant's Memory -- mix of pictographic and ideographic elements.
- **Low iconicity**: Blissymbolics, Leibniz -- abstract geometric or algebraic symbols.

High iconicity aids initial learnability but creates a ceiling for abstract concepts. Low iconicity enables abstraction but increases the learning curve.

### Dimension 2: Compositionality

Can primitives combine to produce new meanings?

- **Fully compositional**: Blissymbolics, Leibniz -- meaning derived from composition of base elements.
- **Partially compositional**: LoCoS, Icono, Elephant's Memory -- composition exists but with limited rules.
- **Non-compositional**: ISOTYPE, Xu Bing -- individual symbols, no productive grammar.

Compositionality is the property that separates a vocabulary from a language. Without it, a system cannot express novel propositions.

### Dimension 3: Grammar Type

How does the system encode relationships between symbols?

- **Linear grammar**: Blissymbolics (left-to-right with indicators), Icono (icon strings).
- **Spatial grammar**: LoCoS (3x3 grid), Elephant's Memory (free spatial composition).
- **No grammar**: ISOTYPE (statistical presentation rules only), Xu Bing (narrative sequencing only).
- **Formal logic**: Leibniz (calculus ratiocinator -- never completed).

Grammar is what enables a system to say who did what to whom. Without grammar, "dog bites man" and "man bites dog" are indistinguishable.

### Dimension 4: Relationship to Phonetics

Does the system bridge to spoken language?

- **Phonetically bridged**: LoCoS (consonant assignments).
- **Phonetically independent**: Blissymbolics, ISOTYPE, Elephant's Memory, Leibniz.
- **Culturally dependent**: Xu Bing (symbols require cultural familiarity), Icono (icon recognition requires shared cultural knowledge).

This dimension determines whether a system can handle proper names, borrowed words, and technical terminology -- the point where any semasiographic system must interface with the spoken world.

## 10. The Pattern That Emerges

Across four centuries, a consistent pattern emerges from these seven systems:

**Pattern 1: Vocabulary is easy; grammar is hard.** Creating recognizable visual symbols for concrete objects is straightforward. Every system achieves this. The point of failure is grammar -- the rules that combine symbols into propositions, that encode who does what to whom, that express negation, conditionality, and temporal displacement. Grammar is what separates emoji (a vocabulary) from language.

**Pattern 2: Concrete is easy; abstract is hard.** Every system can represent "house," "sun," "person." The challenge begins with "if," "because," "despite," "although," and "the possibility that." Abstract concepts require either metaphorical composition (Blissymbolics' "feeling + up" = "happy") or conventional symbols that must be learned. The iconicity-expressiveness trade-off is the central tension of visual language design.

**Pattern 3: Success never comes as planned.** No system achieved its creator's original goal. Leibniz never completed his system. Neurath's ISOTYPE became information design, not language. Bliss's universal language became an AAC tool for children with cerebral palsy. LoCoS became the "running man" exit sign. Xu Bing's universal narrative is readable only within a particular technological culture. The consistent lesson: languages find their communities, and communities form around practical need, not philosophical ideals.

**Pattern 4: The phoneme problem remains unsolved.** No fully semasiographic system has solved the problem of proper names, borrowed words, and technical terminology without phonetic fallback. Blissymbolics cannot write "Tokyo." ISOTYPE cannot write "Einstein." This gap between meaning-based and sound-based representation is a fundamental architectural challenge that any new visual language must address.

## 11. What These Systems Collectively Teach

For anyone designing a new visual language, the historical record provides specific, actionable lessons:

1. **Start with an empirically grounded inventory of primitives.** Leibniz failed because he had no method for identifying irreducible concepts. NSM's 65 semantic primes and Johnson's image schemas now provide what Leibniz lacked.

2. **Grammar is not optional.** ISOTYPE and Xu Bing demonstrate that pictograms without grammar cannot constitute a language. Any system that aims for open-ended expressiveness must have compositional rules, not just symbols.

3. **Plan for a mixed semasiographic-glottographic architecture.** Blissymbolics' inability to handle proper names is not a minor limitation -- it is an architectural failure. Chinese's mixed strategy (semantic radicals + phonetic radicals) evolved over millennia for good reason.

4. **Support both linear and spatial composition.** Blissymbolics' linear grammar works for simple sequences but struggles with simultaneous relationships. Elephant's Memory's spatial grammar encodes what linearity cannot but loses reading order. A viable system likely needs both modes.

5. **Ground abstract symbols in embodied experience.** Blissymbolics' "feeling + up" = "happy" is more learnable than an arbitrary symbol for "happy" because it reflects the conceptual metaphor HAPPY IS UP -- a mapping grounded in bodily experience. Image schemas provide this grounding systematically.

6. **Build for a community with a practical need.** Philosophical universality is not a sufficient motivation for adoption. Blissymbolics succeeded in AAC because it solved a real problem for a real community. A new visual language must find its community.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Characteristica universalis | Leibniz's proposed universal symbolic notation for all knowledge |
| Calculus ratiocinator | Leibniz's proposed mechanical reasoning system operating on universal symbols |
| ISOTYPE | International System of Typographic Picture Education -- Neurath's pictogram system for statistical communication |
| Transformer (ISOTYPE) | The role of mediating between domain experts and visual designers to create effective pictographic communication |
| Blissymbolics | Charles Bliss's semantic visual writing system using ~120 base geometric elements; also known as Semantography |
| Bliss-character | A basic geometric symbol in Blissymbolics |
| Bliss-word | A compound symbol formed from multiple Bliss-characters |
| Indicator | Small supralinear symbols in Blissymbolics that mark grammatical features (part of speech, tense, number) |
| LoCoS | Lovers Communication System -- Yukio Ota's pictogram-ideogram hybrid with grid-based spatial grammar |
| Elephant's Memory | Timothee Ingen-Housz's non-linear logographic visual communication system (~150+ logograms) |
| Logogram | A written symbol that represents a word or morpheme |
| Icono | Peter Kramer's icon-based universal language where words are self-illustrating icon strings (2023) |
| AAC | Augmentative and Alternative Communication -- technologies and methods for supporting communication for individuals who cannot rely on speech |
| Compositionality | The property of a system whereby the meaning of a complex expression is determined by the meanings of its parts and the rules for combining them |
| Semasiographic | Writing that represents meaning directly, without encoding the sounds of a spoken language |
| Iconicity | The degree to which a symbol visually resembles what it represents |
