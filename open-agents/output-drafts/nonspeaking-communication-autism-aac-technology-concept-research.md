# Concept Research: AAC Technology — Speech-Generating Devices, Apps, and Access Methods

## Overview

Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC) encompasses the full range of tools, strategies, and techniques that supplement or replace spoken communication for individuals who cannot rely on speech alone. For non-speaking autistic individuals — estimated at 25-30% of the autistic population — AAC represents the most evidence-supported pathway to functional communication. The term "augmentative" refers to methods that supplement existing speech, while "alternative" refers to methods that replace speech entirely. AAC exists on a continuum from low-tech (picture cards, communication boards) through mid-tech (simple voice-output devices) to high-tech (tablet-based apps, dedicated speech-generating devices with eye-gaze tracking).

The modern AAC landscape is dominated by tablet-based applications running on consumer hardware (primarily iPads), dedicated speech-generating devices (SGDs) with specialized access methods, and eye-gaze tracking systems. Three major AAC apps serve the autism community: Proloquo2Go (AssistiveWare), TouchChat (TobiiDynavox/PRC-Saltillo), and LAMP Words for Life (PRC-Saltillo). Each implements different vocabulary organization philosophies — Proloquo2Go uses its Crescendo core-word system, TouchChat offers multiple vocabulary sets including WordPower, and LAMP uses Language Acquisition through Motor Planning principles with consistent icon placement. The evidence base for AAC is robust: systematic reviews consistently show that AAC interventions improve communication outcomes and, critically, do not inhibit speech development — a persistent myth that continues to delay AAC introduction for many children.

Access to AAC is mediated by the physical method used to select vocabulary items. While touchscreen access is most common, many non-speaking autistic individuals require alternative access methods due to motor planning difficulties. Eye-gaze tracking (pioneered commercially by Tobii Dynavox), switch scanning, head tracking, and emerging brain-computer interfaces each serve populations with different motor profiles. The choice of access method is a critical clinical decision that can determine whether an AAC system succeeds or is abandoned — and abandonment rates remain high, estimated at 30-50% across AAC users, driven by inadequate training, poor customization, and stakeholder attitudes.

## Core Components

### Component 1: Speech-Generating Devices (SGDs)
- **Purpose**: Dedicated hardware designed specifically for AAC, providing speech output from symbol or text selection
- **Types**: Dedicated devices (Tobii Dynavox I-Series, PRC-Saltillo Accent series) vs. consumer tablets (iPads) running AAC software
- **Key features**: Durability, multiple access methods (eye-gaze, touch, switch), mounting options for wheelchairs, environmental control integration
- **Relationships**: SGDs serve as the hardware platform for AAC software; dedicated devices offer more robust access methods than consumer tablets
- **Evidence**: A 2025 systematic review of 20 studies (1998-2023) found that SGD interventions for autistic preschoolers produced small to high effects on requesting and social communication outcomes
- **Funding**: In the US, SGDs can be funded through Medicaid, Medicare, private insurance, and school districts under IDEA; dedicated devices ($5,000-$15,000+) are typically covered while consumer tablets ($500-$1,500 + app cost) face variable coverage

### Component 2: High-Tech AAC Applications
- **Purpose**: Software that transforms tablets or dedicated devices into communication tools through symbol-based or text-based interfaces
- **Major platforms**:
  - **Proloquo2Go** (AssistiveWare): Uses Crescendo vocabulary system with progressive language feature; core words stay in consistent positions to support motor planning; available on iOS
  - **TouchChat** (PRC-Saltillo): Offers multiple vocabulary sets (WordPower, Spelling, Basic Communication); approximately $100 less than Proloquo2Go; available in English, Spanish, Hebrew, Arabic, Canadian French
  - **LAMP Words for Life** (PRC-Saltillo): Based on Language Acquisition through Motor Planning principles; consistent icon placement across vocabulary levels; specifically designed for autism; one-time purchase ~$299
  - **TD Snap** (Tobii Dynavox): Communication platform with eye-gaze, touch, and switch access; includes page sets designed for different access methods
- **Relationships**: Apps run on SGD hardware or consumer tablets; vocabulary organization philosophy determines learning trajectory and motor planning demands

### Component 3: Eye-Gaze Access Systems
- **Purpose**: Allow AAC users to select vocabulary items by looking at targets on screen, bypassing the need for hand/finger motor control entirely
- **Key technology**: Tobii Dynavox I-Series (I-13, I-16) are the dominant commercial eye-gaze AAC devices; use infrared cameras to track corneal reflection and pupil position
- **Access capabilities**: Works in varied lighting including outdoors; enables not just communication but environmental control (doors, lights, phone operation)
- **Target population**: Individuals with severe motor impairments who cannot reliably access touchscreens, including those with co-occurring cerebral palsy, ALS, or severe motor planning difficulties
- **Relationships**: Eye-gaze is one of several access methods; can be combined with touch and switch access on the same device

### Component 4: Core Vocabulary Systems
- **Purpose**: Organize AAC vocabulary around the principle that a small set of high-frequency words (core vocabulary) accounts for approximately 80% of all utterances
- **Structure**: Core vocabulary (~200-400 words) includes function words (I, want, go, help, more, not, like) that are used across all contexts; fringe vocabulary covers the remaining 20% of topic-specific words (names, places, specialized nouns)
- **Motor planning principle**: Core words maintain consistent positions on the communication display so that selection becomes automatic through motor memory, analogous to touch-typing on a QWERTY keyboard
- **Evidence**: A systematic quality review (Springer, 2023) found consistent support for core vocabulary approaches in AAC interventions, though evidence specifically testing motor learning in AAC remains limited
- **Relationships**: Core vocabulary systems are implemented within AAC apps (Crescendo in Proloquo2Go, WordPower in TouchChat); the core-fringe distinction drives vocabulary organization across all modern AAC systems

### Component 5: Alternative Access Methods
- **Purpose**: Provide input mechanisms for individuals who cannot use direct touch or eye-gaze
- **Types**:
  - **Switch scanning**: User activates one or two switches (buttons, proximity sensors, sip-and-puff); the device highlights options sequentially and the user activates the switch when the desired item is highlighted
  - **Head tracking**: Camera tracks head position/movement to control cursor on screen
  - **Partner-assisted scanning**: Communication partner verbally offers choices; user signals (eye blink, vocalization, movement) when desired option is named
  - **Brain-computer interfaces (BCI)**: Emerging technology using EEG or implanted electrodes to detect neural signals for communication; UC Davis demonstrated real-time speech synthesis from brain activity for ALS patient (2025); not yet clinically available for autism
- **Relationships**: Access method selection depends on motor profile; multiple methods can be used by the same individual across contexts

### Component 6: Multimodal Communication
- **Purpose**: Recognition that effective AAC involves combining multiple communication modes rather than relying on a single system
- **Modes**: Gestures, vocalizations, facial expressions, sign language, low-tech picture boards, high-tech SGDs, and natural speech may all be used by the same individual depending on context, communication partner, and energy level
- **Evidence**: Research on multimodal communication in autism shows that non-speaking individuals use a combination of communicative means, and that clinicians should support all modes rather than privileging one
- **Relationships**: Multimodal AAC integrates across all other components; the goal is total communication, not exclusive reliance on any single method

## How It Works

### Step 1: Assessment and Access Method Selection
- **Trigger**: Individual identified as needing AAC support (non-speaking or minimally speaking)
- **Action**: Speech-language pathologist (SLP) evaluates motor capabilities, cognitive profile, vision, hearing, and communication needs; trials multiple access methods; evaluates existing communication strategies
- **Result**: Recommendation for specific AAC system, vocabulary level, and access method; this is an iterative process that may require multiple trials
- **Key principle**: There are no cognitive or linguistic prerequisites for AAC — the "AAC readiness" myth has been debunked; AAC should be introduced as early as possible

### Step 2: Vocabulary Selection and Customization
- **Trigger**: AAC system selected and acquired
- **Action**: SLP and family customize vocabulary for the individual's life contexts; core vocabulary provides the base; fringe vocabulary personalized for home, school, community, interests; visual appearance and layout adjusted for motor and visual profile
- **Result**: Personalized communication system with vocabulary relevant to the user's daily life

### Step 3: Aided Language Modeling (Teaching Phase)
- **Trigger**: Customized system available
- **Action**: Communication partners model AAC use by pointing to symbols on the device while speaking naturally; this shows the AAC user how to construct messages; does not require the AAC user to imitate immediately
- **Result**: User develops receptive understanding of the symbol system; begins to associate symbols with meanings and communicative functions

### Step 4: Functional Communication Training
- **Trigger**: User shows emerging understanding of symbol meanings
- **Action**: Structured opportunities to use AAC across contexts; teaching progresses through communicative functions: requesting → rejecting → commenting → asking questions → social interaction; evidence shows multicomponent interventions combining SGDs with modeling, time delay, and peer education produce the strongest outcomes
- **Result**: Expanding communicative competence across functions, partners, and contexts

### Step 5: Motor Learning and Automaticity
- **Trigger**: Continued use of AAC system
- **Action**: Consistent symbol placement supports motor planning; repeated access to the same locations builds motor memory; vocabulary is progressively expanded as competence grows (the Crescendo approach)
- **Result**: Communication becomes faster and more automatic; user develops fluency with their system

### Step 6: Ongoing Support and System Evolution
- **Trigger**: Changing needs across lifespan
- **Action**: Regular reassessment of vocabulary, access methods, and communication needs; technology upgrades; transition planning between educational settings; adult services coordination
- **Result**: AAC system evolves with the user; prevents abandonment through responsive adjustment

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: Jordyn Zimmerman — Late AAC Introduction
- **Scenario**: Jordyn Zimmerman is a non-speaking autistic woman who was not given access to AAC until age 18. She had been in segregated educational settings and assessed as having intellectual disability. After receiving an iPad with Proloquo2Go, she demonstrated literacy, intelligence, and communicative competence that had gone unrecognized for 18 years.
- **Illustration**: Demonstrates the devastating consequences of delayed AAC introduction and the myth that individuals must demonstrate prerequisite skills before receiving AAC
- **Key Insight**: Zimmerman subsequently earned a Bachelor's degree in Education Policy from Ohio University, a Master's degree from Boston College, was named to the Forbes Accessibility 100 (2025), and serves as Board Chair of CommunicationFIRST — all accomplishments that were enabled by access to AAC technology that should have been provided years earlier

### Example 2: Eye-Gaze System for Severe Motor Impairment
- **Scenario**: A non-speaking autistic individual with co-occurring motor difficulties that prevent reliable hand use receives a Tobii Dynavox I-Series with eye-gaze tracking. Using only their eyes, they can select symbols, construct messages, control their phone, and operate environmental controls (lights, doors).
- **Illustration**: Shows how eye-gaze technology can bypass motor barriers entirely, providing communication access to individuals who cannot use touchscreens
- **Key Insight**: The same device that enables communication also enables environmental control and digital access, expanding independence well beyond messaging

### Example 3: The Speech Facilitation Effect
- **Scenario**: A 4-year-old autistic child with fewer than 5 spoken words begins using an AAC app. After 6 months of consistent AAC use with aided language modeling, the child has approximately 50 words on their device AND has begun producing 15-20 spoken words — more than tripling their pre-AAC speech output.
- **Illustration**: Directly contradicts the myth that AAC prevents speech development
- **Key Insight**: Research consistently shows that AAC can actually support speech development, likely because it reduces communicative frustration and provides a visual-motor scaffold for language

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: A child needs to demonstrate certain cognitive or linguistic prerequisites before they are "ready" for AAC
  - **Reality**: There are no evidence-based prerequisites for AAC. The concept of "AAC readiness" has been thoroughly debunked. AAC should be introduced as early as possible and should not wait for failed speech therapy attempts.

- **Misconception**: Using AAC will prevent a child from developing speech
  - **Reality**: A systematic review found that "augmentative and alternative communication interventions do not inhibit speech production; instead, AAC may also support speech production." Multiple studies show speech gains during AAC use.

- **Misconception**: Low-tech AAC is appropriate for "lower-functioning" individuals while high-tech is for "higher-functioning" individuals
  - **Reality**: Technology level should match motor profile, communication needs, and context — not presumed cognitive level. Many individuals benefit from both low-tech and high-tech options across different situations (multimodal communication).

- **Misconception**: AAC is a "last resort" after speech therapy has failed
  - **Reality**: AAC and speech therapy are complementary, not sequential. Early AAC introduction alongside speech therapy produces better outcomes than waiting for speech therapy to fail before introducing AAC.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **Situational AAC use**: Some autistic individuals who can speak in some contexts lose reliable speech access during stress, overload, or shutdown, and benefit from AAC as a supplementary mode rather than a primary one. This is sometimes called "part-time AAC use" or "AAC for speaking autistic people."
- **System transitions**: Moving between AAC systems (e.g., from PECS to a high-tech SGD) requires careful planning to avoid communication regression during the transition period
- **Multilingual AAC**: Limited research exists on AAC for multilingual autistic individuals; most systems are designed for monolingual use, creating barriers for families who speak languages not well-supported by major AAC apps
- **Adult AAC introduction**: Most AAC research focuses on children; adults who have spent decades without communication access face unique challenges in AAC introduction, including entrenched behavioral patterns and a lack of age-appropriate research

## Related Concepts

- **Symbol-Based Communication (PECS, Makaton, Blissymbols)**: Symbol systems are a subset of the broader AAC landscape, using pictures, signs, or graphical symbols as the communication medium
- **Motor-Language Dissociation**: The finding that motor planning difficulties, not language comprehension deficits, may be the primary barrier to speech in many non-speaking autistic individuals — directly supports the rationale for AAC
- **Visual-Spatial Cognition**: Enhanced perceptual processing in autism suggests that visual-based AAC systems may align with autistic cognitive strengths
- **Spelling-Based Methods (S2C, RPM)**: Occupy contested territory between evidence-based AAC and discredited facilitated communication; letterboards and keyboards are technically AAC tools, but the methods used to teach and support their use are where controversy arises
- **Universal Design for Learning (UDL)**: Educational framework emphasizing multiple means of representation, expression, and engagement — AAC is a key UDL accommodation

## Research Gaps

- **Adult AAC introduction**: Extremely limited research on AAC outcomes for non-speaking autistic adults who receive AAC for the first time after childhood, despite this being a large and underserved population
- **Motor learning in AAC**: While core vocabulary systems are designed around motor planning principles, direct evidence testing motor learning mechanisms in AAC use remains limited
- **Multilingual AAC**: Very little research on AAC for multilingual autistic individuals or families; most commercial systems prioritize English
- **Long-term outcomes**: Few longitudinal studies track AAC outcomes beyond 1-2 years; the lifespan trajectory of AAC use in autism is poorly understood
- **Intersectionality**: Research on how race, income, geography, and cultural factors affect AAC access and outcomes is virtually nonexistent despite known disparities
- **BCI for autism**: Brain-computer interface research for AAC is primarily focused on acquired conditions (ALS, stroke); applicability to developmental motor planning differences in autism is unexplored
