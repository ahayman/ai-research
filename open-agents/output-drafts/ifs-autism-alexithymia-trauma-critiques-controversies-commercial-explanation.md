# Critiques, Controversies, and Commercial Dynamics: How To Read the IFS Conversation

## Overview

A reasonable person searching "IFS therapy criticism" in 2026 will land in a noisy room. *The Nation* says the rise is dubious. *The Cut* says the therapy can break you. Mad in America says training culture deflects dissent. The IFS Institute says the most-cited bad-practice case isn't IFS at all. Reddit threads cycle through all of it without sorting which argument is which.

This page does the sorting. It treats the IFS critique conversation as three structurally distinct objects that get conflated in casual reading, and adds two pieces of background that the reader needs to understand any of it: the IFS Institute's commercial structure, and the Mark Schwartz / Richard Schwartz disambiguation. The goal is to leave you able to read primary critique sources without being captured by them and without dismissing them — which is what a sophisticated reader of any contested clinical modality needs.

**What is "critiquing IFS"?**
Three different things. (1) Epistemic critiques target the model's truth-claims: whether parts and Self can be empirically falsified, whether universality of Self has any evidence, whether "no bad parts" is a clinical stance or a metaphysical claim. (2) Commercial / structural critiques target the IFS Institute as an institution: founder-as-IP-holder, tiered training paywall, recertification cadence, Foundation–Institute funding loop. (3) Brand / over-claiming critiques target the gap between marketing language ("evidence-based") and the actual evidence base. Each operates at a different level. Each has different remedies.

**Why does it exist as a serious conversation now?**
Three things converged. IFS popularity exploded after Schwartz's *No Bad Parts* (2021) and the accompanying social-media moment — Brownstone, Hunsicker and Greene's *Psychotherapy Bulletin* essay (June 2024) documents 45,764 therapists listing IFS on PsychologyToday as of April 2024. The empirical base did not keep pace: Buys's 2025 scoping review found 27 IFS studies total and only two RCTs. And in October 2025, *The Cut* published Rachel Corbett's "The Therapy That Can Break You," centered on Castlewood Treatment Center allegations, which generated a wave of commentary and an institutional response. The conversation is louder than it was two years ago. It is also noisier.

**Key takeaways:**
- The critique conversation is three distinct objects (epistemic / commercial / brand), not one undifferentiated "IFS criticism."
- Mark Schwartz (Castlewood, EdD, named in five 2011–2013 lawsuits) is not Richard Schwartz (IFS founder, PhD, never named in any Castlewood lawsuit). They are not related.
- The IFS Institute's response to *The Cut* is factually correct on the disambiguation. That does not retire the broader process-level concern that hypnotic and imagery-based methods carry false-memory induction risks across modalities.
- The doctrinal expansions into "unattached burdens" and the spirit / psychedelic territory are critique objects in their own right and get full treatment in Category 11 of this deep dive.

## How to Read Critiques as a Sophisticated Reader

<!-- DIAGRAM: three-categories-of-critique -->

Before engaging any specific critique, sort it. Is this critic arguing that the model's truth-claims fail (epistemic)? That the institution's commercial structure is concerning (commercial)? That the marketing over-claims relative to the actual evidence (brand)? These are different arguments. They have different evidentiary bases. They require different responses. Conflating them is what makes the conversation feel impossible to navigate.

Then steel-man the critique. Each of the named sources is making a specific argument that deserves to be reconstructed at full strength before being accepted or rejected. Brownstone, Hunsicker and Greene argue that IFS popularity has outrun its evidence base and that application to vulnerable populations (psychotic features, dissociative disorders without prerequisites) is concerning. McAllen's *Nation* piece argues commercial growth is decoupled from rigorous evidence. Mad in America's July 2025 attendee piece argues training-culture features deflect dissent. Each can be steel-manned independently.

Then identify where critics overreach. The "IFS = cult" framing overreaches; the "IFS adoption has structural features cult-research literature treats as risk-features" framing is more defensible. *The Cut*'s "Castlewood is IFS" framing collapses two distinct figures and the IFS Institute's response on this point is correct. "IFS has no evidence" overreaches; two RCTs and a growing pilot literature exist, even though the evidence base is thin. Reading critically goes both directions.

Finally, distinguish founder claims from field claims. Schwartz's claims about Self as an a priori universal, his claims about "unattached burdens" as discarnate entities, and his claims in the Sounds True "Spirituality of IFS" course are founder claims. They are not the same as field consensus. A sophisticated reader tracks which claims are Schwartz, which are IFS-Institute marketing, and which are independent peer-reviewed findings.

## Falsifiability and Universality (the Epistemic Critique)

<!-- DIAGRAM: falsifiability-map -->

The epistemic critique has two prongs that the systems-thinker reader will recognize from Karl Popper's philosophy of science. A claim is scientific in the Popperian sense if it is, in principle, capable of being shown false by evidence. Some IFS claims pass this test cleanly. Others do not, as currently formulated.

**What is testable.** Claims like "IFS produces symptom reduction in PTSD" or "IFS produces sustained pain reduction in rheumatoid arthritis at 1-year follow-up" are testable. Shadick et al. (2013, *J Rheumatol*) tested the second one and found a positive signal in a proof-of-concept RCT. Hodgdon et al. (2021) tested the first in a small uncontrolled pilot and reported large effects with the asterisks the small uncontrolled pilots warrant. The Buys 2025 scoping review documents what has been tested. This is normal therapy-research territory. The model's specific procedural moves can be operationalized and tested for fidelity. The model's session-level phenomena (does the client report a "part," does an apparent shift toward 8-Cs Self-energy occur) can be measured.

**What is not falsifiable as currently formulated.** The claim that all clients possess an "undamaged Self" with eight specific qualities is a strong universal claim. As currently operationalized, it cannot be disconfirmed. If Self appears in session, the protocol confirms it. If Self does not appear, the protocol reframes the absence as "a part is in the way" or "blended part." Either outcome counts as confirmation. This is what philosophers of science call an *ad hoc rescue*: an auxiliary clause that protects the theory from any conceivable disconfirming observation.

> **Evidence note**: The "as currently formulated" hedge is load-bearing. Some IFS practitioners argue that Self-presence shows up behaviorally — the 8 Cs become observable — and that the universality claim is in-principle testable even though current operationalization is loose. The argument that the universality claim is unfalsifiable as currently formulated is defensible. The categorical "fails the Popperian test" claim goes a step further than the literature explicitly states. Validity rating 8/10.

The "no bad parts" claim has a similar two-level structure. As a *clinical stance* — meet every part with curiosity rather than condemnation, refuse to treat any part as the enemy — it has substantial therapist-stance value. This version is testable: you can measure therapist behavior for fidelity to the stance, and you can study whether the stance produces better outcomes than a more confrontational alternative. As a *metaphysical claim about the moral structure of human minds*, "no bad parts" is a strong universal assertion that the evidence does not support and probably cannot support. The universalized version is also where critics including Sean Cuthbert locate the spiritual-bypass risk — applying compassion-for-all-parts to active abusers, evading present-tense accountability, papering over real harm.

The autistic / pattern-thinker reader will recognize this as a familiar shape: a claim that operates beautifully at one level and fails at a higher one. Hold both. The clinical method may help while the universal ontology remains empirically open.

## Schwartz, the Institute, the Foundation: Commercial Structure

<!-- DIAGRAM: ifs-institute-commercial-structure -->

The commercial structure of IFS is unusually concentrated for a modality marketed as evidence-based. None of the individual elements is unique — most therapy modalities have a charismatic founder, branded trainings, and a research foundation — but the concentration of roles in a single founder, combined with a tightly scaled training pipeline, is the structural feature critics name.

**Founder concentration.** Richard Schwartz is simultaneously the model's developer, primary public face, IP-holder (the IFS Institute holds branding and certifies training), gatekeeper of what counts as IFS, and bestselling author of the lay-public canon. *No Bad Parts* (Sounds True, 2021) is the entry point most readers encounter. *You Are the One You've Been Waiting For* and *The Internal Family Systems Workbook* extend it. McAllen's *Nation* feature documents Schwartz's elevated celebrity status, including retreats with Alanis Morissette and a $7,000 Costa Rica trauma seminar. This is the founder-as-face pattern.

**The training pipeline (verified 2025–2026).** From IFS Institute's own help-center pages: General Application $75, non-refundable. Level 1 deposit $500, non-refundable. Level 1 total tuition ~$3,990–$5,300 (varies by venue and format). Level 2 modules ~$2,550–$2,950 each. Level 3 ~$2,700–$2,900. Video review fee $200 (sessions up to 60 minutes). Certification fee $200 ($50 application + $150 certification). Recertification fee $150, every 2 years. The Nation tabulates a minimum certification pathway at ~$10,000 in fees alone, before recertification. McAllen reports IFS Institute staff growth from 12 to 35 employees, trainers from 30 to 100+, annual providers trained ~500 in 2017 to ~4,000 in 2023, with a waiting list of ~20,000.

> **Evidence note**: The Nation's growth statistics are single-source — McAllen's reporting based on interviews. The growth direction is corroborated by independent data (PsychologyToday 45,764 listings as of April 2024), but the specific staff/trainer/throughput numbers are not independently audited. Treat as "The Nation reports" rather than "Institute discloses." Validity rating 8/10.

**The Foundation–Institute loop.** The Foundation for Self Leadership (501(c)(3), EIN 20-1318139) funds IFS-related research. Its public materials emphasize independence: an Independent Review Council, arm's-length operation, and a stated policy of not influencing results. Its donor base "has historically come primarily from individuals in the extended IFS community" (Foundation's own statement). Several of the most-cited IFS pilots — including Hodgdon et al.'s 2021 PTSD pilot — involve Foundation funding or IFS-Institute-affiliated authorship. This is a conflict-of-interest pattern critics name. Whether the safeguards are adequate is contested. The general field problem — most therapy-modality research is funded by people with prior commitment to the modality (CBT research is heavily CBT-aligned, EMDR research is EMDR-aligned) — is real, but the specific concern for IFS is that the literature is so thin that even modest conflict-of-interest patterns have outsized influence on what gets cited.

**Sub-modality extensions.** IFIO ("Intimacy from the Inside Out"), Toni Herbine-Blank's couples-IFS sub-modality, has its own training track and certification under the IFS Institute. The 2021 textbook *Internal Family Systems Couple Therapy Skills Manual: Healing Relationships with Intimacy from the Inside Out* (Herbine-Blank & Sweezy) was published by **PESI Publishing**, not Norton — a citation hygiene point worth getting right; some secondary sources misattribute the publisher. *Internal Family Systems Therapy for Addictions* (Sykes, Sweezy, Schwartz, PESI 2023) extends IFS into substance-use territory; the PARTS-SUD pilot (Ally et al., *Frontiers in Psychiatry* 2025; n=10) is the first published empirical work on the application. These are commercial extensions of the IFS brand. They are not, on their own, evidence of bad practice — they are evidence that IFS is scaling into adjacent clinical territories at a faster pace than the empirical base for those applications can support.

## "Evidence-Based" Branding vs. Evidence (the Brand Critique)

Jonathan Shedler's general critique of "evidence-based therapy" — directed primarily at how the label gets used in CBT-and-manualized-therapy marketing — applies cleanly to IFS marketing. Shedler's argument, simplified: "evidence-based" has become a code phrase. It means manualized treatment, often brief, often cognitive-behavioral, with a small set of RCTs supporting it. The phrase functions as marketing. To question claims for "evidence-based" therapy is to risk being labeled "anti-science." Proponents conflate "studies exist" with "scientific support." Methodological choices (active vs. waitlist controls; follow-up windows; comorbidity exclusions) inflate apparent efficacy. The "master narrative" — "in the dark ages, therapists practiced unscientific therapy; science shows the evidence-based therapies are superior" — is a rhetorical move, not a finding.

Apply this lens to IFS. IFS marketing prominently displays "evidence-based" framing, anchored on the 2015 SAMHSA NREPP listing, two RCTs, and a growing pilot literature. The 2015 SAMHSA NREPP listing is the single most-cited credential. SAMHSA terminated NREPP for the convenience of the government on December 28, 2017. The registry no longer exists. IFS marketing that implies live federal endorsement misrepresents this. The Foundation for Self Leadership maintains a private mirror at samhsa-nrepp.foundationifs.org, but a private mirror is not a current federal credential.

The Buys 2025 scoping review counted 27 IFS studies in total: 17 case studies, 5 quasi-experimental, 3 qualitative, 2 RCTs. The two RCTs are Shadick (2013, RA) and Haddock (2016, depression). The review described the evidence as "promising" for PTSD, depression, and chronic pain but limited in scope — which is the careful reading. "Promising-but-limited" is the honest characterization. "Evidence-based" implies more than the literature supports.

The Brownstone, Hunsicker and Greene essay is the most concentrated published version of this critique applied to IFS. They name a "strikingly small evidence-base" given IFS's 30-year existence, alongside the IFS Institute's expansive healing claims. They flag overapplication to populations where IFS has not been studied, including conditions with psychotic features where reality-testing difficulties could be exacerbated by encouraging "splitting of self." They propose research priorities: study why therapists pursue IFS training, how clinicians use social media to promote IFS tenets, which presenting concerns receive IFS treatment in practice, and potential harm thresholds for specific diagnoses.

## *The Nation*'s Allegations

McAllen's *Nation* feature ("The Rapid and Dubious Rise of the Internet's New Favorite Therapy," April 2025) is the most prominent commercial-structural critique in mainstream U.S. journalism. Its specific claims are these.

The training pipeline is rapidly scaling. Schwartz has become a celebrity figure with retreats at the Alanis Morissette level and Costa Rica seminars at $7,000. Pricing structure compounds: minimum ~$10,000 to certification, ongoing $150 every two years for recertification. Sub-modality extensions (couples, addictions, chronic pain, ketamine-IFS retreats at $8,500 per person, Rio Retreat Center PARTS workshops at ~$4,000 plus lodging) extend brand reach.

Specific evidence concerns: the 2016 *Journal of Marital and Family Therapy* depression study (Haddock et al., 37 participants) found "no significant difference" between IFS and other treatments. The 2013 *Journal of Rheumatology* RA study (Shadick et al., 79 participants) showed pain reduction but "no sustained improvements in anxiety, self-advocacy, or disease advocacy." McAllen quotes the American Psychological Association's Lynn Bufka indicating IFS "is in need of much more research" before efficacy recommendations.

Named clinical critics in the piece include Florida psychotherapist Leah Benson, who calls the "evidence-based" designation "a cloak for the insidious damage" and warns that IFS encourages trauma-narrative obsession; Awais Aftab, Case Western Reserve psychiatry professor, who notes IFS "has the kind of quirky and mysterious vibes...perfectly designed for...TikTok and Instagram age."

McAllen also notes Schwartz's hesitation discussing "unattached burdens" in mainstream press, citing his fear it would "jeopardize gains." Critics in the piece compare the unburdening process to exorcism. Benson warns that the framing teaches patients to view destructive thoughts as external entities requiring expulsion. (Cat 11 of this deep dive treats this material in depth.)

## *Society for the Advancement of Psychotherapy*'s "Problematic Popularity"

Brownstone, Hunsicker and Greene's *Psychotherapy Bulletin* essay (Vol. 59 No. 3, June/September 2024) is the most concentrated peer-published academic critique. The authors are Lisa M. Brownstone, PhD (Assistant Professor of the Practice in Counseling Psychology, University of Denver); Madeline J. Hunsicker, MA (counseling psychology candidate, University of Iowa); and Amanda K. Greene, PhD, MPH (research associate, University of Michigan Medical School Center for Bioethics and Social Sciences in Medicine).

Their main argument: IFS popularity has dramatically outrun its empirical support, and the gap is itself a clinical concern. The 45,764-therapists-on-PsychologyToday figure dates from this paper. They flag overapplication to populations IFS has not been studied with — psychotic-features conditions where encouraging "splitting of self" may exacerbate reality-testing difficulties; dissociative disorders without phase-oriented prerequisites where the approach potentially contradicts trauma-treatment principles emphasizing integration. They reference litigation regarding false-memory introduction "possibly facilitated by IFS's dissociative processes." They flag training and education gaps: only one in five doctoral psychology programs offer trauma-informed care training; accreditation standards don't consistently require it; early-career therapists are vulnerable to misleading workshops.

Their conclusion is not "IFS is harmful." It is: IFS application must align with empirical evidence rather than social-media popularity. The recommendation is research priorities, not prohibition. This is the careful version of the critique.

## Mark vs. Richard Schwartz: Castlewood Disambiguation

<!-- DIAGRAM: mark-vs-richard-schwartz -->

The Castlewood Treatment Center controversy is, in 2026, the highest-traffic source of confusion for any reader Googling "IFS controversy." The disambiguation is essential.

**Mark Schwartz, EdD** (note: not PhD), was clinical co-director of the Castlewood Treatment Center, a residential eating-disorder facility near St. Louis, Missouri. He had previously directed trauma units at other psychiatric hospitals, including Two Rivers Psychiatric Hospital in Kansas City, where a similar lawsuit settled out of court in December 1998 (allegations involving therapy-induced satanic-cult memories). He is not related to Richard Schwartz, the IFS founder. They have not been in professional contact for many years.

**The Castlewood litigation, in brief.** First lawsuit: November 21, 2011, Lisa Nasseff sued Castlewood and Schwartz, alleging she was hypnotized while on psychotropic medications and that the hypnotic treatment caused false memories of physical abuse, sexual abuse, multiple rapes, satanic ritual abuse, and beliefs of belonging to a satanic cult. Through 2012–2013, at least four additional former patients filed similar claims; total of five plaintiffs. The cases were dismissed in 2013 in resolutions described by attorneys as "favorable to both sides" — a procedural outcome that commonly indicates settlement with non-disclosure terms and does not constitute either vindication or condemnation. Mark Schwartz left Castlewood in 2013 and entered a disciplinary agreement with the Missouri Board of Psychology under which he allowed his Missouri license to expire.

**Richard Schwartz's connection to Castlewood.** Per the IFS Institute's November 4, 2025 response statement, Richard Schwartz served as a paid consultant and trainer at Castlewood at some point in its history but "never worked as a therapist for the clients" and "was never named in any of the lawsuits related to Castlewood." Court records and litigation reporting consistently confirm this — only Mark Schwartz, not Richard Schwartz, was the named defendant. The Institute's response on the disambiguation is factually correct.

**Why the confusion persists.** Same surname. Both worked in trauma/eating-disorder space. Richard Schwartz did have a documented professional connection to Castlewood. *The Cut*'s October 2025 Rachel Corbett feature uses the Castlewood allegations as evidence in a broader critique of IFS, which collapses the two figures.

**What the Castlewood pattern does and does not tell us about IFS.** The disambiguation is correct and necessary. It does not retire the broader concern. Hypnotic, trance-like, and imagery-based methods carry false-memory induction risks — this is a generalizable concern across modalities, well-documented in the recovered-memory literature and in critiques across the Janet/Charcot/Freud lineage and contemporary trauma therapy. The IFS Institute's own protocol — "IFS does not accept previously unknown, unrealized, or 'recovered' memories as fact until corroborated by evidence" — is the right policy, but it has to survive in-session pressure when imagery work surfaces vivid material. The Mad in America July 2025 attendee piece flags trance-like sessions in IFS training as a problem the attendee experienced. This is the process-level concern that survives the disambiguation. A serious reader holds both: *The Cut*'s framing collapses the two Schwartzes, and the broader process-level concern about hypnotic / imagery-based methods generalizes beyond IFS and includes IFS imagery work as one specific application.

## Cult-of-Personality Concerns

The careful framing matters here, and the framing does most of the work. The criticism is not "IFS is a cult." It is: IFS adoption has structural features that cult-research literature treats as risk-features, distinct from claiming the modality is itself a cult. The structural features include a charismatic founder; in-group language; training as multi-level initiation; recertification cadence; mutual endorsement networks among IFS-aligned authors and adjacent figures (Schwartz–Falconer; Schwartz–van der Kolk's *The Body Keeps the Score* features IFS prominently and Schwartz endorsed van der Kolk in turn; Schwartz–Sweezy–Sykes); and patterns where dissent gets reframed as the dissenter's parts-work problem rather than engaged on its own terms.

> **Evidence note**: The "structural features have cult-of-personality risks" framing is journalistic and first-person, not academic-organizational-sociology. There is no published peer-reviewed analysis of IFS adoption as an organizational phenomenon. Validity rating 7/10.

The Mad in America July 2025 first-person piece is the most concentrated source of the dissent-deflection concern. The attendee describes the "you just need to do more work with your parts" deflection as institutionalized — staff repeating it whenever attendees raised concerns about the training itself. There is a legitimate version of this move (in mid-treatment, doing more parts work *can* address what the client is naming) and an evasive version (foreclosing the question entirely). A practitioner who can hold both — engage the part *and* engage the meta-concern about the model on its own terms — is doing the work. A practitioner who only does the first move is performing the deflection critics name.

## Sub-Modality Extensions: Brief Note

This page is mostly about the main modality, but two adjacent commercial extensions deserve brief mention because they show how the IFS brand scales.

**IFIO** ("Intimacy from the Inside Out") is Toni Herbine-Blank's couples-IFS sub-modality. Its 2021 textbook *Internal Family Systems Couple Therapy Skills Manual* (Herbine-Blank & Sweezy) was published by **PESI Publishing**, not Norton, and the publisher attribution is worth getting right because secondary sources mis-cite it. IFIO has its own training track and certification under IFS Institute branding.

**IFS for Substance Use Disorders** is the focus of Sykes, Sweezy and Schwartz's *Internal Family Systems Therapy for Addictions* (PESI, 2023). The PARTS-SUD pilot (Ally et al., *Frontiers in Psychiatry* 16:1544435, 2025) is the first published empirical work on this application: n=10 with comorbid PTSD+SUD, 12 weekly groups + 6 individual sessions, PTSD reduction 1.7 points/week (p=0.002), 70% retention at 12 weeks, 86% mean acceptability. Cambridge Health Alliance recruitment. n=10 is a very small pilot; effect estimates are imprecise; it is uncontrolled.

Both extensions are commercial in the sense that they expand the IFS brand into adjacent clinical territories. They are not, on their own, evidence of bad practice — they are evidence of the pattern the brand-overclaiming critics name: scaling outpaces empirical base.

## Forward Reference: The Spiritual / Psychedelic Turn

The doctrinal expansions into "unattached burdens" (Robert Falconer's term, fully developed in *The Others Within Us: Internal Family Systems, Porous Mind, and Spirit Possession*, 2023, with foreword by Richard Schwartz), Schwartz's Sounds True course "The Spirituality of IFS," the MAPS-MDMA Phase 3 PTSD trials' finding that ~80% of high-dose MDMA participants spontaneously used parts language, and the rapidly growing ketamine-IFS clinical territory are full critique objects in their own right. They get full treatment in **Category 11: Psychedelic-Assisted IFS, Spirituality, and Unattached Burdens** of this deep dive. This category notes them as load-bearing critique targets and forwards the reader.

The pertinent point for the present page: this material is not a fringe deviation. It is a turn the founder is publicly leading. The Sounds True course exists. The Schwartz foreword to Falconer's book exists. The podcast appearances explicitly affirming "spiritual phenomena that cannot be explained by conventional science" exist. A sophisticated reader of the IFS critique conversation cannot honestly leave this material out of the picture. Cat 11 holds the substantive engagement; Cat 5 records that it is part of the critique landscape.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: "IFS is a cult." **Reality**: The careful critique is that IFS has *structural features* cult-research literature treats as risk-features. This is a meaningful concern. It is not the categorical claim that IFS is a cult.
- **Misconception**: "Mark Schwartz, who was sued, is the IFS founder." **Reality**: Mark Schwartz, EdD, was Castlewood's clinical co-director. Richard Schwartz, PhD, is the IFS founder. They are not related.
- **Misconception**: "IFS has no evidence." **Reality**: Two RCTs and a growing pilot literature exist. The Buys 2025 scoping review describes the evidence as "promising" but limited.
- **Misconception**: "IFS is on the SAMHSA evidence-based registry." **Reality**: IFS was added to NREPP in November 2015. SAMHSA terminated NREPP on December 28, 2017. The registry no longer exists.
- **Misconception**: "Schwartz personally profits from every certification." **Reality**: Money flows through the IFS Institute as a corporate entity. Schwartz's direct financial benefit is via book royalties, training appearances, retreats, and IP. Specific personal-profit claims should be hedged.

## Summary

The IFS critique conversation in 2026 sorts into three structurally distinct objects: epistemic (truth-claims about parts and Self), commercial (Institute / Foundation / training pipeline), and brand-overclaiming (marketing vs. evidence). Sorting before engaging is what lets a sophisticated reader navigate it.

The disambiguation question — Mark Schwartz vs. Richard Schwartz — is not optional background. It is the most common live confusion among readers approaching the conversation in 2026, and getting it wrong distorts everything downstream. The Castlewood litigation involves Mark, not Richard. The IFS Institute's response on this point is correct. The broader process-level concern about hypnotic / imagery-based methods and false-memory induction risk generalizes across modalities and survives the disambiguation.

The doctrinal-expansion territory — unattached burdens, the spirituality of IFS, psychedelic-assisted applications — is its own conversation and its own critique target, treated in full in Cat 11.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Falsifiability | Karl Popper's criterion that a scientific claim must be capable, in principle, of being shown false by evidence. |
| Ad hoc rescue | Rescuing a theory from apparent disconfirmation by adding auxiliary clauses ("a part is in the way") rather than treating the disconfirmation as evidence against the theory. |
| NREPP | National Registry of Evidence-Based Programs and Practices. SAMHSA terminated December 28, 2017. IFS added November 2015. The registry no longer exists. |
| IFIO | Intimacy from the Inside Out — Toni Herbine-Blank's couples-IFS sub-modality. |
| PARTS-SUD | Program for Alleviating and Reducing Trauma, Stress, and Substance Use — Ally et al. 2025 IFS pilot for comorbid PTSD/SUD (n=10). |
| Foundation for Self Leadership | IFS-affiliated 501(c)(3) (EIN 20-1318139) that funds IFS research with stated independence safeguards. |
| Castlewood | Eating-disorder treatment center near St. Louis, MO, where Mark Schwartz was clinical co-director. Subject of recovered-memory lawsuits 2011–2013. Later rebranded as Alsana. |
| Spiritual bypass | Using spiritual / therapeutic language to avoid present-tense responsibility, accountability, or the work of staying with hard material. |
| Cult of personality (in this context) | A structural pattern in which an organization's authority, identity, and gatekeeping concentrate around a single charismatic figure. The criticism is that IFS exhibits structural features of this pattern, not that IFS is a cult. |
