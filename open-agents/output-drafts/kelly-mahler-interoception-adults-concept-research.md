# Concept Research: Kelly Mahler's Interoception Approach (Adults)

## Overview

Kelly Mahler, OTD, OTR/L is an occupational therapist and clinician-educator based in Hershey, Pennsylvania, who has built — over roughly 20 years of work — a clinical interoception model centred on the framing of interoception as the "eighth sensory system." Her catalog combines a flagship workbook-format curriculum, a set of activity-card decks, an assessment instrument (CAIA), live and on-demand courses for clinicians, and a small but growing peer-reviewed evidence base. Her organisation operates under the kelly-mahler.com brand; she is not affiliated with a university research lab in the usual sense — her authorship is clinician-led and her published research is co-authored with practitioner colleagues (Hample, Amspacher, McLaughlin, Anson) and largely tested in special-education school settings.

The bulk of Mahler's catalog is designed for educators, occupational therapists, and parents working with **children and adolescents in K–12 and special-education contexts**. Her published research is exclusively with autistic children. However, she does have specific adult-targeted offerings — most concretely *My Interoception Workbook* (2019, co-authored with autistic self-advocates Chloe Rothschild and Jarvis Alma) and *Interoception Through the Lens of an Autistic Adult* (an on-demand course featuring Rothschild) — and her flagship *Interoception Curriculum* claims to be adaptable across the lifespan including adults. The honest characterisation is: **a small adult-targeted core, a larger pediatric body of work that she markets as adaptable to adults, and roughly zero peer-reviewed evidence specifically supporting the adult application.**

This research document maps her model precisely, inventories what is genuinely adult-targeted vs. pediatric-being-recommended-for-adults, and prepares the ground for an honest comparison with the IFS deep-dive Cat 6 page on alexithymia.

## Core Components

### Component 1: Interoception as the "Eighth Sensory System"
- **Purpose**: Clinical reframing that places interoception alongside the seven traditional sensory modalities (vision, hearing, smell, taste, touch, vestibular, proprioception) as a discrete sense to be assessed and trained.
- **Inputs**: Body signals — cardiac, respiratory, gastric, muscle tension, temperature, urinary/bowel, fatigue, hunger, thirst.
- **Outputs**: Conscious awareness of internal state, which then feeds emotional identification and self-regulation.
- **Relationships**: Mahler positions this framing as an extension of A.D. (Bud) Craig's neurobiological work on interoception — Craig's 2002 *Nature Reviews Neuroscience* paper established the anterior insula as the integrative substrate for interoception, and his 2009 paper *How do you feel — now?* established the AIC as the basis for subjective awareness. Mahler imports this neuroscience as backing for her clinical model but does not cite Garfinkel's three-component decomposition (accuracy / sensibility / awareness) as a distinct framework.
- **Origin**: The *Interoception: The Eighth Sensory System* book (2015/2017, AAPC Publishing) was Mahler's first major publication. Its full subtitle is *Practical Solutions for Improving Self-Regulation, Self-Awareness and Social Understanding of Individuals with Autism Spectrum and Related Disorders* — i.e. an autism-framed clinical book, not an adult-targeted book per se. The book includes one chapter on assessment and one on practical exercises but is written for clinicians and educators working with autistic children primarily.

### Component 2: The Interoception Awareness Pathway (Mahler's clinical model)
- **Purpose**: A staged sequence that takes a person from raw body signal to regulated action. This is the load-bearing structure of *The Interoception Curriculum* (2019, $159, 635 downloadable pages, 25 lessons).
- **Stages**:
  1. **Notice the body signal** (Lessons 1–16: 16 of 25 lessons) — discriminating tension from relaxation, fast heart from slow heart, full bladder from empty, hot from cold, hungry from satiated. The vast majority of the curriculum sits here.
  2. **Connect signal to a feeling word** (Lessons 17–20) — building the body-emotion bridge: "this fluttering chest, in me, often means anxious." The crucial framing: emotion-body mappings are *individualized*, not universal.
  3. **Connect to need / select action** (Lessons 21–25) — using the identified state to choose a regulating response.
- **Output**: "Body trust" — Mahler's term for the stabilised relationship between perceived body signal and effective self-regulating action.
- **Relationships**: This pathway maps reasonably onto Garfinkel's three-component model (Stage 1 ≈ accuracy; Stage 2 ≈ sensibility/awareness; Stage 3 ≈ regulation as an applied outcome) but is less analytically rigorous — Mahler does not separate accuracy from awareness from sensibility. The model is essentially a single linear pipeline rather than three dissociable dimensions.

### Component 3: Mahler's Catalog — Adult-Specific vs. Pediatric-Adaptable
A precise inventory.

**Genuinely adult-targeted (designed-from-the-start for adolescents/teens/adults):**
- ***My Interoception Workbook*** (2019) — co-authored with Chloe Rothschild (autistic self-advocate, age 27 at publication) and Jarvis Alma (also a self-advocate). $39 USD. Three sections, 18 chapters covering personal interoception experience, practical tips for hunger/fullness/thirst/pain/temperature/sleep/bathroom/relaxation/anxiety, and self-advocacy strategies. **Crucially, the book itself states it is *not* designed to build interoceptive awareness — it is for "outsmarting or compensating for interoception challenges."** It is recommended as a *precursor* to the (pediatric-rooted) Interoception Curriculum. Self-directed use is permitted.
- ***On-Demand Course: Interoception Through the Lens of an Autistic Adult*** — features Chloe Rothschild narrating her interoception journey. Marketed to OTs, educators, mental-health providers, "adult service providers," behaviourists, and caregivers. Adult-experience-as-text but professionally framed.
- ***On-Demand Course: Interoception and the Non-Speaking Client*** — the non-speaking population includes adults; this is one of the few products that explicitly addresses adult clinical work.
- ***Noticing Out Loud: An Adult's Guide to Interoception Modeling*** (2025) — a free 17-page PDF. The title is misleading: this is a guide for adults who *model* interoception aloud for *children*, not a guide for adults building their own interoception. So it is adult-authored and adult-distributed but pediatric-applied.

**Pediatric-rooted but marketed as adaptable to adults:**
- ***The Interoception Curriculum*** ($159, 25 lessons) — the FAQ explicitly states "can be adapted for preschoolers through adults" with modifications to language and visuals. Curriculum content (lesson plans, worksheets, visuals) is K–12-flavoured by default. One published adult-self-learner testimonial appears on the product page, but the curriculum was not designed in adult populations and has not been tested in adults.
- ***Interoception Activity Cards*** (170 cards) — described as "preschool through adulthood." Single deck; no adult version. Cards include activities like paying attention to heartbeat after stairs, noticing breath patterns, etc. — these activities have face validity for adults but are not differentiated.
- ***Interoception Yoga Cards / Exercise Cards / Big Book of Interoception Games*** — bundled with the curriculum master bundle. Pediatric-leaning aesthetic.
- ***Comprehensive Assessment of Interoceptive Awareness, 3rd ed. (CAIA-3)*** — Mahler's assessment tool. Adult version exists alongside child versions. This is one of the genuinely adult-applicable assessment instruments in her catalog.

**Pediatric-only (explicit):**
- Children's books published under the Mahler imprint
- Toileting course (focused on building toileting readiness in children, though a 2026 "Toileting 2.0" live course is being marketed neuro-affirmingly)

**Honest summary of the adult/pediatric split**: Of Mahler's roughly 12–15 distinct products and offerings, **2 are designed-for-adults from inception** (the Workbook, the Lens-of-an-Autistic-Adult course), **2 more are adult-applicable by population** (CAIA-3 adult, Non-Speaking Client course), and **the remaining 8–10 are pediatric-rooted with adaptation language attached**. The flagship Interoception Curriculum sits in the third bucket: adult-adaptable on paper, never adult-tested in research, K–12-flavoured by default. **Roughly 25–30% of her catalog is genuinely adult-targeted; the remaining 70–75% is pediatric work being recommended for adult use.**

### Component 4: The Workshop and Training Layer
- Live online courses appear on her site at single-event pricing; she also runs sponsored trainings ranging from 50-minute keynotes to 2-day workshops, customised to host-organisation populations.
- Conference appearances include OCALI (Ohio Center for Autism and Low Incidence), STAR Institute, AOTA-approved CE courses, and the kelly-mahler-2025 Australia tour. The 2025 talk *Interoception, the 8th Sense: The Science of How We Each Uniquely Feel* is a representative title.
- Audience composition: predominantly OTs, special educators, school-based clinicians. Adult-clinical audiences are a smaller share. Workshops are not adult-clinical-only; they are mixed-population.

### Component 5: The Research Base
- **Mahler's own peer-reviewed publications**:
  - Mahler, McLaughlin, Anson (2020), "Interoception Across Varying Degrees of Mental Wellness," *AJOT*, 74(S1) — descriptive cross-sectional, not an intervention study.
  - Hample, Mahler, Amspacher (2020), "An interoception-based intervention for children with autism spectrum disorder: a pilot study," *Journal of Occupational Therapy, Schools, & Early Intervention*, 13(4), 339–352 — pilot, children only.
  - Mahler et al. (2022), "Impact of an Interoception-Based Program on Emotion Regulation in Autistic Children," *Occupational Therapy International* — N=14 students aged 9–19, one-group pre-post design (NOT an RCT), special-education classroom. A scoping review classified this study as "moderate risk of bias in outcome measurement."
  - Mahler et al. (2024), "An interoception-based intervention for improving emotional regulation in children in a special education classroom: Feasibility study," *Occupational Therapy in Health Care*, 38(3), 636–650 — feasibility, children.
  - Listed as "in development" on her site: a randomised controlled trial of the Interoception Curriculum with school-aged children. **No adult RCT, no adult feasibility study, no adult pilot is reported.**
- **Mainstream interoception research the model invokes**:
  - Bud Craig (2002, *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*; 2009 *NRN*; 2011 *Annals NYAS*) — anterior insula as substrate for interoception and integrated awareness. Foundational neuroscience.
  - Sarah Garfinkel et al. (2015, *Biological Psychology*; Garfinkel & Critchley 2016) — three-component model: accuracy (heartbeat-detection task performance), sensibility (self-report orientation inward), awareness (metacognitive correspondence). Garfinkel's framework dissociates components that Mahler treats as one. Mahler does not appear to discuss the three-component model directly in her materials.
  - Hugo Critchley — co-author with Garfinkel; broader interoception/affect work.
- **Interoception–alexithymia literature**:
  - Shah, Hall, Catmur, Bird (2016, *Cortex*) — "Alexithymia, not autism, is associated with impaired interoception." Establishes that the autism–interoception link is mediated by alexithymia.
  - Brewer, Cook, Bird (2016, *Royal Society Open Science*) — alexithymia involves general (multi-modal) interoceptive deficit, not just cardiac.
  - Murphy and colleagues — separable interoception dimensions.
  - Khalsa et al. — meta-analyses on interoception interventions.
- **Aaron, Snodgress, Blain, Park (2020, *Frontiers in Psychiatry*)** — N=76 young adults; the relationship between alexithymia and interoceptive accuracy was best described as **quadratic**: both very high *and* very low interoceptive accuracy correlate with elevated alexithymia. The accompanying mindfulness body-scan pilot showed **no group difference between intervention and control** on interoception/affect measures. This is the load-bearing finding that complicates "more interoception is always better" claims, including those made implicitly by interoception-training programs like Mahler's.
- **Mindfulness-and-alexithymia evidence**:
  - Norman, Marzano, Coulson, Oskis (2019) — meta-analysis of mindfulness interventions on alexithymia; small statistically significant TAS-20 reduction, with caveats about heterogeneity and harm risk in psychiatric populations.
  - Khalsa et al. (2025) — large meta-analysis on mindfulness and self-reported interoception; small-to-medium positive effect.
  - Lindahl et al. (2017), Britton et al. (2021) — meditation-related adverse events, particularly in trauma populations.

## How It Works (Mahler's Awareness Pathway, applied to adults)

### Stage 1: Notice (Lessons 1–16 of the Interoception Curriculum)
- **Trigger**: Practitioner or self-learner selects a body system and a contrast pair (relaxed/tense; full/empty; warm/cool).
- **Action**: Generate body signals through small movements or context (drink water → notice; hold breath → notice; tense shoulders → notice). Compare states. Build a personal vocabulary for what each state feels like *for this individual*, not a normative dictionary.
- **Result**: Discrimination capacity increases. The practitioner explicitly disavows teaching "what hunger feels like" as a universal — Mahler's framing is that the perceived signal is unique to each person.

### Stage 2: Connect (Lessons 17–20)
- **Trigger**: Stage 1 signal vocabulary is established to some baseline.
- **Action**: Bridge from body signal to feeling word. "When my chest is tight and my breath is shallow, I most often call this anxiety." This is granularity-building work, parallel to (but not citing) Lisa Feldman Barrett's emotional-granularity literature.
- **Result**: A personal, individualised body→feeling map.

### Stage 3: Act (Lessons 21–25)
- **Trigger**: Body signal is identified and labelled.
- **Action**: Choose a regulating response — co-regulation, sensory tools, breath, environmental change.
- **Result**: "Body trust" — confidence that the felt signal can be acted on.

The adult-application question is: **which of these stages is genuinely supported by the adult-relevant products?**
- *My Interoception Workbook* — Stage 1 (compensation/outsmarting, not awareness-building) and tangentially Stage 3 (advocacy strategies for needs).
- *Interoception Curriculum* — Stages 1–3, but the curriculum was developed in pediatric populations and "adapted" for adults via the buyer adjusting language and visuals.
- *Activity Cards* — Stage 1 mostly.
- *Lens-of-an-Autistic-Adult course* — meta-experiential, narrating Rothschild's lived journey; not a curriculum.

## Diagram Specifications

### Diagram 1: Mahler's Interoception Awareness Pathway
- **Type**: Flowchart (linear with feedback)
- **Purpose**: Show the staged sequence from raw body signal to regulated action.
- **Elements**:
  - Body signal (input)
  - Stage 1: Notice (Lessons 1–16) — discriminate sensations
  - Stage 2: Connect (Lessons 17–20) — link to feeling word
  - Stage 3: Act (Lessons 21–25) — choose regulating response
  - Outcome: "Body trust"
- **Connections**: Body signal → Stage 1 → Stage 2 → Stage 3 → Body trust, with feedback loop from Body trust back to Stage 1 (refined discrimination over time).
- **Notes**: Use weighted node sizing — Stage 1 covers 16 of 25 lessons, so it should be visually larger.

### Diagram 2: Adult vs. Pediatric Content Map
- **Type**: 2x2 matrix or quadrant chart
- **Purpose**: Make the catalog split visually honest. Clearly mark what is genuinely adult-targeted, what is adult-applicable, and what is pediatric being recommended for adults.
- **Axes**: X = "Designed for adults vs. Designed for children." Y = "Tested in adults vs. Not tested in adults."
- **Items to plot**:
  - Adult-designed + adult-tested: empty quadrant (no Mahler product is both)
  - Adult-designed + not-tested-in-adults: My Interoception Workbook; Lens-of-an-Autistic-Adult course; CAIA-3 adult version
  - Pediatric-designed + adult-recommended (untested in adults): Interoception Curriculum; Activity Cards; Yoga/Exercise Cards
  - Pediatric-designed + pediatric-tested only: Mahler's published research base; children's books; school-based interventions
- **Notes**: This is the diagram that prevents the page from papering over the catalog imbalance.

### Diagram 3: Mahler's Pathway as Front-End for IFS's 6 Fs
- **Type**: Two-track parallel diagram with bridges
- **Purpose**: Show where Mahler's curriculum could prepare a low-interoception adult for IFS-style felt-sense work.
- **Track 1 (Mahler)**: Body signal → Notice → Connect to word → Act
- **Track 2 (IFS 6 Fs)**: Find (in body) → Focus → Flesh out → Feel toward → beFriend → Fears
- **Bridges**:
  - Mahler Stage 1 (Notice) → IFS Find ("where in the body")
  - Mahler Stage 1+2 (Notice + Connect) → IFS Focus and Feel toward
  - Mahler Stage 2 (Connect) → IFS Feel toward (requires reading own emotional stance)
  - Mahler Stage 3 (Act) → outside the IFS protocol — diverges into self-regulation goals
- **Notes**: The first three Fs of IFS map cleanly onto Mahler's Stage 1+2. Mahler's Stage 3 takes a self-regulation route that is *not* parts work; the protocols share the front-end and diverge at the outcome.

### Diagram 4: Garfinkel's Three-Component Model overlaid on Mahler's Pathway
- **Type**: Overlay/Venn or annotated stack
- **Purpose**: Show where the empirical literature goes finer-grained than Mahler's clinical model.
- **Elements**:
  - Garfinkel's accuracy (objective heartbeat-detection performance) — partially captured by Mahler Stage 1, but Mahler does not measure accuracy as such.
  - Garfinkel's sensibility (self-report orientation inward) — captured implicitly by all stages.
  - Garfinkel's awareness (metacognitive correspondence of confidence and accuracy) — *not captured* in Mahler's pathway. Mahler does not separate "I think I notice" from "I actually do notice" as a research-relevant distinction.
- **Notes**: This diagram makes explicit a gap that downstream evidence-base discussion will reference.

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: An adult uses *My Interoception Workbook* for self-directed work
- **Scenario**: An autistic adult with suspected alexithymia buys the workbook ($39), works through the 18 chapters over several weeks, possibly with a friend.
- **What's actually happening**: They are *not* building interoceptive awareness in the awareness-curriculum sense. They are reading personal accounts from Rothschild and Alma, receiving practical tips for managing hunger/thirst/temperature, and developing self-advocacy language. The workbook is positioned as a precursor to *The Interoception Curriculum* — a "before you try the awareness work" warm-up.
- **Key insight**: Self-directed adult engagement with Mahler's catalog is genuinely possible, but the explicitly self-directed product (*My Interoception Workbook*) is a compensation/advocacy guide, not an awareness-builder.

### Example 2: An adult buys *The Interoception Curriculum*
- **Scenario**: Same adult, $159, downloads 635 pages of lesson plans designed for K–12 special education, attempts to "adapt" the language and visuals as the FAQ recommends.
- **What's happening**: They are running pediatric lesson scaffolding on themselves. The activities (notice your heart after stairs; compare tense shoulders to relaxed shoulders) have face validity for adults but the worksheets, visuals, and pacing assume a different cognitive scaffolding context.
- **Key insight**: The curriculum is not unusable for adults. It is also not designed for adults, and the buyer is doing the adaptation work themselves. One published adult-self-learner testimonial exists; no adult RCT does.

### Example 3: An adult takes the *Lens-of-an-Autistic-Adult* on-demand course
- **Scenario**: The adult buys the course featuring Chloe Rothschild's narration of her interoception journey.
- **What's happening**: This is experiential exposure to an autistic adult's framing of interoception, plus some concrete strategies. It is not a structured curriculum to follow week-by-week.
- **Key insight**: This is the most adult-targeted experiential content in the catalog, and it functions more as orientation than as program.

### Example 4: An adult uses Mahler's pathway as front-end for IFS work
- **Scenario**: The adult has done some IFS reading; the felt-sense step ("where do you notice this part in your body?") is inaccessible. They run Mahler's Stage 1 noticing exercises to build discrimination, then attempt the IFS protocol.
- **What's happening**: This is exactly the "training-modality" position discussed in IFS Cat 6. It has the same caveat: Aaron 2020's quadratic finding suggests building interoceptive accuracy is not always therapeutically positive, and Mahler's pathway does not address this risk.
- **Key insight**: The conceptual fit is real — Mahler's Stage 1 maps onto IFS Find — but the empirical case for "interoception training prepares you for IFS" is unproven for either program in adult populations.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: "Mahler's work has been validated in autistic adults with alexithymia."
  - **Reality**: Mahler's published intervention studies are all with children in school settings. There is no adult RCT, no adult feasibility study, and no peer-reviewed evidence on alexithymic adult response.

- **Misconception**: "*The Interoception Curriculum* is an adult curriculum."
  - **Reality**: It is a K–12 / special-education curriculum the FAQ describes as adaptable to adults. The buyer does the adaptation; the lessons are not adult-native.

- **Misconception**: "*My Interoception Workbook* builds interoceptive awareness."
  - **Reality**: The workbook itself states it is for "outsmarting and compensating for interoception challenges" and recommends *The Interoception Curriculum* as the awareness-building next step.

- **Misconception**: "Building more interoceptive awareness is always therapeutically positive."
  - **Reality**: Aaron 2020's quadratic finding — both high and low interoceptive accuracy correlate with alexithymia — and the body-scan adverse-event literature (Lindahl 2017, Britton 2021) complicate this claim. Mahler's materials do not engage this risk explicitly.

- **Misconception**: "Mahler is a research scientist."
  - **Reality**: Mahler is a clinician-educator with clinically-oriented peer-reviewed publications (some pilot, some feasibility, mostly with practitioner co-authors and pediatric populations). The framing of her work is clinically practical, not basic-science. This is not a criticism — it is a category clarification.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **Adults with co-occurring complex trauma**: Mahler uses "undoing before doing" language and references trauma-informed pacing. This is welcome but the published intervention research has not been done in trauma populations. Risk of body-scan-style adverse events in alexithymic + complex-PTSD adults is real and not directly addressed in Mahler-specific evidence.
- **Adults with aphantasia + alexithymia**: Mahler's curriculum lessons rely on visual scaffolds (worksheets, visuals). Aphantasia is not directly addressed. Adults with both alexithymia and aphantasia may find both the body-noticing and the visual-curriculum scaffolds challenging.
- **Adults seeking parts work, not self-regulation**: Mahler's framework targets self-regulation as the outcome. An adult who has engaged IFS and wants parts-work scaffolding will find Mahler's Stage 1+2 useful as front-end and Stage 3 outside the IFS frame.

## Research Gaps

1. **Adult intervention evidence**: Mahler's program has no published adult RCT, no adult feasibility study, and no adult pilot. All published evidence is pediatric.
2. **Alexithymic-adult-specific evidence**: The relevant population for the user's profile (autistic adult, suspected alexithymia, low interoception) has zero published Mahler-specific data.
3. **Aaron 2020 risk engagement**: Mahler's materials do not explicitly address the quadratic-relationship finding that "more is not always better" for interoceptive accuracy.
4. **Comparison with Garfinkel framework**: Mahler does not formally adopt or critique the three-component model. This is a missing synthesis layer.
5. **Adverse-event reporting**: Mahler's program does not, to the extent visible from public materials, formally report adverse-event rates for body-scan-style attention practices in trauma populations. This is a standard reporting gap in clinician-led interoception work generally, not specific to her.
6. **Adult-clinical fidelity vs. K–12 fidelity**: When the curriculum is "adapted" for adults, what does fidelity mean? No published adult fidelity protocol exists.
