## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This research identified **40+ significant patents and patent families** across switch access, alternative input methods, brain-computer interfaces, and hardware interaction technologies relevant to AAC (Augmentative and Alternative Communication). The patent landscape is dominated by several key players:

**Major Patent Holders (by category):**
- **Eye Tracking:** Tobii AB (~850 filings, 400+ granted) -- dominant position
- **BCI/Neural Interface:** Neuralink (56 publications, 18 granted), Blackrock Neurotech (65 documents, 27 families), Synchron, Stanford/UC System
- **EMG/Muscle Signals:** Control Bionics, Microsoft, Meta (CTRL-labs)
- **AAC Language Systems:** PRC-Saltillo/Semantic Compaction Systems (Minspeak patents)
- **Head/Facial Tracking:** Perceptive Devices (8+ patents)
- **BCI + AR for AAC:** Cognixion (27 documents, 6 families) -- FDA Breakthrough designation
- **BCI + Text Prediction:** Meta/Facebook (US10795440)

**Risk Assessment for New AAC Entrants:**
- HIGH RISK: Eye tracking hardware (Tobii's massive portfolio creates formidable barriers)
- HIGH RISK: EMG-based switch access (Control Bionics, Microsoft, Meta patents)
- MEDIUM RISK: BCI communication (many patents but technology still maturing; licensing may be available)
- MEDIUM RISK: Semantic compaction / icon sequencing (PRC-Saltillo owns Minspeak IP)
- LOW-MEDIUM RISK: Head/facial gesture control (smaller patent portfolios)
- LOW RISK: Traditional mechanical switch interfaces (many older patents expired)
- LOW RISK: Sip-and-puff mechanical systems (older technology, some patents expired)

## 1. BRAIN-COMPUTER INTERFACE (BCI) PATENTS

### 1.1 US7120486B2 -- Brain Computer Interface (ECoG-Based)
- **Patent Number:** US7120486B2
- **Title:** Brain Computer Interface
- **Assignee:** Washington University in St. Louis (WUSTL)
- **Inventors:** Eric C. Leuthardt, Daniel W. Moran, Jeffrey G. Ojemann, Gerwin Schalk, Jonathan Rickel Wolpaw
- **Filing Date:** December 12, 2003
- **Grant Date:** October 10, 2006
- **Status:** Granted (likely expired or near expiry based on 20-year term)
- **Description:** Describes an electrocorticography (ECoG)-based brain computer interface system that uses ECoG signals from the brain and translates that activity into user intent. The intent is then communicated to a device such as a screen cursor, wheelchair, or prosthetic device.
- **Link:** https://patents.google.com/patent/US7120486B2/en
- **Related Products:** Wadsworth Center BCI research, various research-grade BCI systems
- **Relevance to AAC:** **HIGH** -- Foundational BCI patent for translating brain signals into communication/control commands. Connection to Wadsworth Center, which demonstrated early BCI-based communication.

### 1.9 Synchron -- Stentrode Endovascular BCI
- **Patent Number:** US10485968B2 (and US2019/0046119A1)
- **Title:** Stentrode (Stent-electrode recording array)
- **Assignee:** Synchron (Tom Oxley, inventor)
- **Status:** Granted
- **Description:** Endovascular brain implant deployed via jugular vein into the superior sagittal sinus near the motor cortex. 16-electrode array on a self-expanding nitinol stent. - **Clinical Status:** 8 patients implanted, able to wirelessly control operating systems. FDA Breakthrough designation (August 2020).
- **Relevance to AAC:** **HIGH** -- Less invasive BCI approach for ALS/paralysis patients. Demonstrated communication capability. Actively in clinical trials at Carnegie Mellon, University of Pittsburgh, Mount Sinai.

## 2. EMG / MUSCLE SIGNAL PATENTS

### 2.1 US11284844B2 -- EMG Assistive Communications Device with Context-Sensitive UI
- **Patent Number:** US11284844B2 (also WO2019099831A1)
- **Title:** Electromyography (EMG) Assistive Communications Device with Context-Sensitive User Interface
- **Assignee:** Control Bionics Holdings Pty Ltd (changed to Control Bionics Limited, April 2022)
- **Inventors:** Peter S. Ford, James E. Schorey, Robert W. Wong
- **Filing Date:** November 16, 2018
- **Publication Date:** May 16, 2019
- **Grant Date:** 2022
- **Status:** Active (granted)
- **Description:** System with processor and bioelectrical sensors that monitors EMG signals. Automatically adjusts resting threshold or switch threshold based on bioelectrical signal trends. Determines whether signal amplitude exceeds switch threshold to trigger user interface. Addresses problems of spasms, fasciculation, and high muscle tone with "Signal Off" feature.
- **Link:** https://patents.google.com/patent/US11284844B2/en
- **Related Products:** NeuroNode, NeuroNode Trilogy, NeuroNode Duo
- **Relevance to AAC:** **HIGH** -- Directly covers EMG-based AAC switch access. Core technology of Control Bionics' commercial AAC products. The adaptive threshold feature is a key differentiator.

## 11. STRATEGIC ANALYSIS & IMPLICATIONS

### White Space Opportunities (Lower Patent Density)

1. **AI-Powered Contextual Prediction for AAC** -- While Meta has the BCI text prediction patent, there is relatively open space for AI/LLM-based text prediction integrated with traditional AAC input methods (switch, touch, eye gaze). The combination of modern transformer models with AAC-specific vocabularies and communication patterns is underpatented.

2. **Multi-Modal Fusion for AAC** -- Few patents cover the intelligent fusion of multiple input modalities (e.g., combining partial eye gaze data with partial EMG data with partial facial gesture data to improve accuracy). Georgia Tech's Tongue Drive is one example, but the space is relatively open for software-based multi-modal AAC.

3. **Personalized/Adaptive Switch Interfaces** -- While Control Bionics has adaptive thresholding for EMG, there is space for AI-driven adaptive interfaces that learn and predict user intent across different switch types and access methods.

4. **Mobile/Smartphone-Based AAC with Alternative Access** -- Most patents cover dedicated hardware. Software-based solutions using smartphone sensors (accelerometers, cameras, microphones) for alternative AAC access are relatively underpatented.

5. **Social Media & Modern Communication for AAC** -- Patents tend to focus on basic message construction. Integration of AAC with modern communication platforms (messaging apps, social media, video calls) with alternative input methods is underpatented.

### Areas of High Patent Risk

1. **Eye Tracking Hardware** -- Tobii's 850-filing portfolio is a near-moat. Any hardware-based eye tracking for AAC will likely require licensing from Tobii or very careful design-around work.

2. **Semantic Compaction / Minspeak** -- PRC-Saltillo's acquisition of all Semantic Compaction IP means any icon-sequencing AAC system must avoid Minspeak-specific claims. Alternative language representation methods are needed.

3. **EMG-Based Gesture Recognition** -- Microsoft, Meta/CTRL-labs, and Control Bionics collectively cover much of the EMG wearable controller space. Any EMG-based AAC switch should conduct thorough freedom-to-operate analysis.

4. **Invasive BCI for Speech** -- Stanford and Neuralink's licensed patents, combined with Neuralink and Synchron's proprietary portfolios, create significant barriers for invasive BCI-based AAC.

### Licensing Opportunities

1. **Tobii IP Licensing Program** -- Tobii explicitly offers IP licensing for eye tracking technology integration. This could be a path for incorporating eye tracking into AAC products without building from scratch.

2. **Stanford Technology Licensing** -- Stanford's speech decoding patents are available for licensing through their Office of Technology Licensing. Already licensed to Blackrock Neurotech and Neuralink.

3. **Blackrock Neurotech Partnership** -- Their partnership model (demonstrated with Cognixion) suggests willingness to collaborate on AAC-focused BCI applications.

### Key Expiring Patents (Approaching or Past 20-Year Term)

- **US7120486B2** (BCI, 2003 filing) -- Expired or near expiry. Foundational BCI technology now available.
- **US6160536A** (Dwell time selection, ~2000 filing) -- Likely expired. Dwell selection methods should be freely usable.
- **US6833786B1** (Pneumatic demultiplexer, ~2000 filing) -- Likely expired. Sip-and-puff multiplexing designs available.
- **US7076738B2** (Sequenced symbols, older filing) -- Near or past expiry.
- **Earlier PRC device patents** (US7,177,797; US7,194,404; US7,318,019 -- all B1 patents from mid-2000s) -- Approaching or past expiry.

### Recommendations for New AAC Entrant

1. **Prioritize software innovation** over hardware patents -- AI/ML-based prediction, multi-modal fusion, and adaptive interfaces have more white space.
2. **Consider Tobii licensing** for eye tracking rather than developing proprietary eye tracking hardware.
3. **Develop novel language representation** methods that do not infringe Minspeak/Semantic Compaction patents.
4. **Monitor BCI space closely** -- technology is approaching clinical viability for AAC, especially non-invasive (Cognixion) and minimally invasive (Synchron) approaches.
5. **Explore partnerships** with BCI companies (Cognixion, Blackrock) rather than building BCI technology from scratch.
6. **File patents on AI-powered AAC prediction** systems, multi-modal input fusion, and adaptive access methods to build defensive portfolio.
7. **Consider EMG-based access** carefully -- strong patents from Microsoft and Meta may require design-around or licensing, but Control Bionics' adaptive threshold approach shows the direction of innovation.
