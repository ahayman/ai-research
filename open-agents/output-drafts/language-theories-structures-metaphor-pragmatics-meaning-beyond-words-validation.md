## Section 1: Grice's Cooperative Principle and Conversational Implicature

### Claim 1: H. Paul Grice (1913–1988) was a British philosopher of language
**Rating**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Multiple sources (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Wikipedia, Britannica) confirm Herbert Paul Grice was born March 13, 1913, in Birmingham, England, and died August 28, 1988, in Berkeley, California. He was a British-born philosopher of language.

### Claim 2: Grice delivered William James Lectures at Harvard in 1967, published as "Logic and Conversation" in 1975, collected in *Studies in the Way of Words* (1989)
**Rating**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Confirmed across multiple authoritative sources. "Logic and Conversation" was delivered as part of the William James Lectures at Harvard in 1967 and published in 1975 in *Syntax and Semantics* (vol. 3, ed. Cole & Morgan). *Studies in the Way of Words* was published in 1989.

### Claim 3: Grice's 1957 paper "Meaning" distinguished natural meaning (meaningN) from non-natural meaning (meaningNN)
**Rating**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Open University, Stanford faculty materials, and multiple philosophy sources confirm the 1957 paper "Meaning" introduced the natural/non-natural meaning distinction. The "spots mean measles" example for natural meaning and the "three rings on the bell" example for non-natural meaning are accurately reported. The characterization that natural meaning cannot be faked while non-natural meaning can hold even when false is correct.

### Claim 4: The Cooperative Principle stated as "Make your conversational contribution such as is required..."
**Rating**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: The quoted formulation matches the standard rendering from Grice's work as reproduced in multiple textbooks and encyclopedias.

### Claim 5: Four maxims — Quantity, Quality, Relation, Manner — with their sub-maxims
**Rating**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: All four maxims and their sub-maxims are accurately described. Quantity (be informative, not more than required), Quality (do not say what you believe false, do not say that for which you lack evidence), Relation (be relevant), and Manner (avoid obscurity, ambiguity, be brief, be orderly) all match standard sources. The research correctly notes Grice acknowledged "relevance" needed further analysis.

### Claim 6: Flouting maxims generates conversational implicatures
**Rating**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: The mechanism of flouting (conspicuous violation that triggers inference) is accurately described. The examples for flouting each maxim are pedagogically sound and consistent with standard pragmatics textbooks.

### Claim 7: Distinction between Particularized (PCI) and Generalized (GCI) conversational implicatures
**Rating**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: The distinction is accurately drawn. PCIs depend on specific context; GCIs arise regardless of particular context. The examples given are standard.

### Claim 8: Properties of conversational implicature — cancelability, non-detachability, calculability, reinforceability, non-conventionality
**Rating**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: All five properties are confirmed by Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, multiple pragmatics textbooks, and university course materials. The descriptions and examples are accurate. The note that manner-based implicatures are an exception to non-detachability is a correct and important nuance.

### Claim 9: Conventional implicatures conveyed by "but," "therefore," "even," "yet," "still" — not cancelable, not calculable, attached to specific forms
**Rating**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Grice's category of conventional implicature is accurately described. The "but" example ("He is British, but brave") correctly illustrates that the contrast is not truth-conditional. The properties (not cancelable, not calculable from the Cooperative Principle, detachable/attached to specific forms) are confirmed by the Stanford Encyclopedia and other sources.

## Missing Important Concepts

### 1. Conversation Analysis (Turn-Taking, Adjacency Pairs, Repair)
**Significance**: HIGH
Conversation Analysis (CA), developed by Harvey Sacks, Emanuel Schegloff, and Gail Jefferson, is a major branch of pragmatics focused on the structural organization of talk-in-interaction. Key concepts include:
- **Turn-taking**: The systematic rules governing who speaks when
- **Adjacency pairs**: Two-part exchange structures (question-answer, greeting-greeting, offer-acceptance/refusal)
- **Conversational repair**: How speakers address problems in hearing, production, or understanding
- **Preference organization**: Some second-pair parts are structurally "preferred" (e.g., acceptance over refusal)

This is arguably the most significant omission, as CA represents a distinct tradition within pragmatics that is not well captured by the theories covered.

### 2. Information Structure (Topic-Comment, Given-New, Focus)
**Significance**: MODERATE
The way speakers organize information in utterances based on what is assumed to be shared knowledge (given) versus new information is a core pragmatic phenomenon. Concepts include topic/comment structure, focus/background, and the given-new contract (Clark & Haviland). This intersects heavily with deixis and presupposition but is not explicitly treated.

### 3. Neo-Gricean Developments (Horn's Division, Levinson's Three Heuristics)
**Significance**: MODERATE
The research mentions Horn scales but does not cover the broader neo-Gricean program:
- **Horn's Division**: Reducing Grice's four maxims to two principles — the Q-Principle (say enough) and the R-Principle (say no more than needed)
- **Levinson's Three Heuristics**: Q-heuristic (what is not said is not the case), I-heuristic (what is simply described is stereotypically exemplified), M-heuristic (marked expression signals marked interpretation)
- Levinson's *Presumptive Meanings* (2000) is a major work not cited

### 4. Discourse and Reference (Anaphora Resolution, Discourse Representation Theory)
**Significance**: LOW-MODERATE
The treatment of reference is limited to deixis. Anaphoric reference across discourse (how "he," "she," "it" connect to earlier mentions), bridging inferences, and formal frameworks like Discourse Representation Theory (Kamp 1981) are related pragmatic topics that are not covered.

## Research Opportunities

### Opportunity 1: Experimental Pragmatics
Recent decades have seen a surge in experimental work testing pragmatic theories empirically. Key areas include:
- Processing time studies on scalar implicature (do "some" interpretations compute "not all" automatically or with effort?)
- Eye-tracking studies on reference resolution
- Developmental pragmatics (when do children acquire implicature?)
- The debate between "defaultism" (Levinson) and "contextualism" (Relevance Theory) on whether GCIs are computed by default

### Opportunity 2: Cross-Cultural Pragmatics Beyond Politeness
The research covers cross-cultural critiques of politeness theory well, but broader cross-cultural pragmatic variation deserves attention:
- Differences in directness norms across cultures
- Variation in speech act realization (how requests, apologies, compliments differ cross-linguistically)
- The interface between pragmatics and translation studies

### Opportunity 3: Computational Pragmatics — Deeper Treatment
The research mentions LLM pragmatic competence briefly (Section 8). This could be expanded with:
- Specific benchmark tasks (e.g., GLUE/SuperGLUE pragmatic tasks, implicature resolution datasets)
- The Rational Speech Acts (RSA) framework (Frank & Goodman 2012) as a computational model of Gricean pragmatics
- Pragmatic failures in AI systems as evidence for the difficulty of pragmatic inference
