## Claim-by-Claim Validation

### Section 1: Speech Act Theory and English-Specific Patterns

**Claim: Austin's *How to Do Things with Words* (1962) originated speech act theory; Searle formalized it.**
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- Austin's work was published in 1962 by Clarendon Press, based on his 1955 William James Lectures at Harvard. Searle's *Speech Acts* (1969) explicitly builds on Austin's framework.
- Sources: [How to Do Things with Words - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/How_to_Do_Things_with_Words), [Speech Acts - Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/speech-acts/)

**Claim: Austin distinguished locutionary, illocutionary, and perlocutionary acts.**
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- This three-part distinction is correctly described. Austin replaced his earlier constative/performative distinction with this more general framework.

**Claim: Searle's taxonomy has five categories: assertives (representatives), directives, commissives, expressives, declarations.**
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- Multiple sources confirm Searle's five-category taxonomy with exactly these labels. The document correctly labels "assertives" with the alternative name "representatives."
- Note: Austin's own taxonomy had different labels (verdictives, exercitives, commissives, behabitives, expositives). The document correctly attributes the five categories listed to Searle, not Austin.
- Sources: [Searle's Classification - Fiveable](https://fiveable.me/key-terms/introduction-semantics-pragmatics/searles-classification-of-speech-acts), [A Taxonomy of Illocutionary Acts - Searle](http://www.thatmarcusfamily.org/philosophy/Course_Websites/Readings/Searle%20-%20Taxonomy%20of%20Illocutionary%20Acts.pdf)

**Claim: English has a characteristically heavy reliance on indirect speech acts, especially for directives.**
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- Cross-cultural pragmatics research consistently identifies English (particularly British and Australian varieties) as preferring indirect request strategies. The CARLA request strategies archive and Brown & Levinson's work support this characterization.
- Sources: [Request Strategies - CARLA](https://archive.carla.umn.edu/speechacts/requests/strategies.html)

**Claim: "Can you pass the salt?" is never interpreted as a question about physical ability in a dining context.**
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- This is a textbook example of a conventionalized indirect speech act, widely cited in pragmatics literature from Searle (1975) onward.

**Claim: Grice's 1975 "Logic and Conversation" proposed the cooperative principle with four maxims: Quality, Quantity, Relation, Manner.**
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- The paper was published in 1975 in *Syntax and Semantics* (Cole & Morgan, eds.). The four maxims are correctly identified and accurately described.
- Sources: [Cooperative principle - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cooperative_principle), [Grice 1975 original text](https://lawandlogic.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/grice1975logic-and-conversation.pdf)

**Claim: Flouting maxims generates conversational implicatures; observing them also generates implicatures.**
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- The examples given (the faint-praise reference letter for flouting Quantity, the gas station for observing Relation) are standard textbook examples accurately presented.

**Claim: British English speakers systematically use litotes (understatement through negation) as a politeness strategy.**
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- This is well-documented in cross-cultural pragmatics. The characterization of "It's not bad" as a positive assessment and "It's not the worst" as a mediocre assessment aligns with research on British English pragmatic conventions.

### Section 3: Politeness Theory

**Claim: Brown and Levinson's 1987 politeness theory builds on Goffman's concept of "face" and identifies positive face and negative face.**
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- The theory was first published in 1978 and expanded in 1987. It builds on Goffman's face concept. Positive face = desire to be approved of; negative face = desire not to be imposed upon. Correctly described.
- Sources: [Politeness theory - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politeness_theory)

**Claim: Five super-strategies ordered from most face-threatening to least: (1) bald on record, (2) positive politeness, (3) negative politeness, (4) off-record, (5) don't do the FTA.**
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- All five super-strategies are correctly identified and correctly ordered. Multiple academic sources confirm this five-part hierarchy.
- Sources: [Five Politeness Strategies - ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Five-Politeness-Strategies-Brown-Levinson-1978-74_fig1_277887318)

**Claim: English is characterized as a negative-politeness-dominant language.**
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- Cross-cultural pragmatics research consistently characterizes British English (and other Anglophone varieties) as preferring negative politeness strategies. This contrasts with Mediterranean and Latin American cultures that tend toward positive politeness.
- Sources: [Politeness - Cross-Cultural Pragmatics - Cambridge](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/abs/crosscultural-pragmatics/politeness/AD14680E29011893DF59BE6E8AFE5D26)

**Claim: Ten negative politeness sub-strategies identified by Brown and Levinson.**
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- The list given (be conventionally indirect; question/hedge; be pessimistic; minimize the imposition; give deference; apologize; impersonalize; state as general rule; nominalize; go on record as incurring a debt) matches the standard enumeration from Brown & Levinson.

### Section 4: Discourse Markers

**Claim: Deborah Schiffrin's 1987 *Discourse Markers* popularized the term.**
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- Published by Cambridge University Press in 1987. Schiffrin defined discourse markers as "sequentially dependent elements that bracket units of talk."
- Sources: [Discourse Markers - Cambridge](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/discourse-markers/A03E5F3A2E1A32F803256EF79F37374E)

**Claim: Earlier work by Labov and Fanshel (1977) used the term.**
- **Rating: UNCERTAIN (7)**
- Labov and Fanshel's *Therapeutic Discourse* (1977) is a well-known work in conversation analysis, but whether they specifically used the term "discourse markers" in the same technical sense is not directly confirmed by my searches. Schiffrin is universally credited as the scholar who established the systematic study.

**Claim: Discourse markers can account for over 10% of tokens in spontaneous conversation.**
- **Rating: UNCERTAIN (7)**
- This is a plausible figure cited in corpus pragmatics literature, but the specific "over 10%" threshold was not directly confirmed in my searches. The high frequency of discourse markers in spoken English is well-established.

**Claim: "Well" has been traced back to Old English, making it one of the oldest continuous discourse markers.**
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- Research by Andreas Jucker (Cambridge Core) traces "well" as a discourse marker to Old English, where it functioned as an emphatic attention-getting device. In Middle English it functioned as a textual frame, and in Early Modern English (Shakespeare's plays) its uses diversified considerably.
- Sources: [The discourse marker well in the history of English - Cambridge Core](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/english-language-and-linguistics/article/abs/discourse-marker-well-in-the-history-of-english1/93DD3C7A4DDBBC710604D27E2D89BDEA)

**Claim: "Like" has dramatically increased since the 1980s, particularly among younger speakers.**
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- This is well-documented in sociolinguistic research. The quotative "be like" construction emerged in the 1980s and has spread rapidly across age groups and varieties of English.

**Claim: Hedges can be stacked to increase tentativeness, following a compositionality principle.**
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- The examples given (from "Close the window" through progressive hedge additions) accurately demonstrate the systematic, compositional nature of hedging in English.

- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- "A Simplest Systematics for the Organization of Turn-Taking for Conversation" was published in *Language* 50 (1974), pp. 696-735.
- Sources: [Sacks et al. 1974 - Columbia CS](http://www.cs.columbia.edu/~julia/cs4706/Sacks_et_al_1974.pdf)

**Claim: Typical gap between turns averages only 200 milliseconds.**
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- Research shows inter-turn gaps average approximately 200ms (range -100ms to 500ms), which is faster than typical reaction time, confirming that listeners begin planning responses before the current speaker finishes.

**Claim: Self-repair is preferred over other-repair; self-initiation over other-initiation.**
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- This preference hierarchy is one of the foundational findings of conversation analysis, directly from Schegloff, Jefferson, and Sacks (1977).
