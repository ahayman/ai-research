I ran two rounds of search. The first round covered Psychology Today (State College, Centre County, Bellefonte, and broader PA by insurance + autism filters), Neurodivergent Therapists Directory (ndtherapists.com), Inclusive Therapists, TherapyDen, Zocdoc, Headway, Grow Therapy, Makin Wellness, Prosper Health, Penn State Health, Centre Psychology Group, Move Forward Counseling, and targeted alexithymia/interoception specialists licensed in PA. The second round (post-client-update) expanded to every PA-licensed telehealth-only adult-autism option I could locate via Headway, Grow Therapy, Alma, Rula, SonderMind, Octave, LifeStance, Thriveworks, Prosper Health, a full read of the ndtherapists.com PA directory (46 listings), Zencare PA neurodivergent, Kindred Therapy LLC, Forward Wellness Counseling, Central PA EFT Community, ICEEFT directory, IFS Institute directory, Kelly Mahler (Pennsylvania-based developer of the Interoception Curriculum), Neurodivergent Practitioners Directory, NeuroSpark Health, and Autistic Therapist Directory.

**Honest assessment (unchanged in substance after the second pass):** The landscape is narrow on two fronts and the expanded search did not find a hidden goldmine of perfect matches. It confirmed and slightly reordered what was already there, resolved several uncertainties, and added two new honorable-mention candidates.

1. **Alexithymia as an explicit specialty is still essentially absent from PA therapist directories.** Across both search rounds — including a full read of ndtherapists.com PA (46 clinicians), the ICEEFT directory, and the IFS Institute directory — I still could not find a single PA-licensed therapist who lists "alexithymia" as a named treatment focus on a public profile. This is consistent with the national picture: alexithymia is a clinical feature that therapists treat under other headings (emotion-focused work, interoception, IFS parts work, somatic approaches, DBT emotion regulation, EFT), not a separately advertised specialty. Notably, **Kelly Mahler — the developer of the Interoception Curriculum, the single most-cited clinical tool in the autism-alexithymia literature — is PA-based** (Elizabethtown College adjunct, Hershey PA), but she is an occupational therapist and researcher, not a practicing psychotherapist, so she is not a direct referral option.
2. **Adult-autism-specific therapists who are also Geisinger-in-network are a small group.** After two full passes, the clinicians who are *both* clearly Geisinger-in-network *and* actively positioned around adult autism on their public profiles are: Jena Gindlesperger (State College, confirmed), Sara Duda (Coplay, confirmed — now resolved, see below), Kelsey Chizmar (waitlist through Sept 2026, confirmed), Hope Krashnak (Kingston, confirmed Geisinger and IFS — but waitlist-only), and Move Forward Counseling as a practice. The strongest clinical fits for the alexithymia piece (Jenn Sevier, Zoe Darazsdi, Sage Stevens) are either self-pay-only or have access barriers.

**What to do about it:** Pursue a statewide PA-telehealth adult-autism specialist (Sevier, Duda, or Darazsdi) as the primary path, with Jena Gindlesperger in parallel as the one verified Geisinger + State College match. Use the Geisinger behavioral-health concierge line (888-839-7972) as a cross-check before committing, not instead of the search — it is the authoritative source for current in-network status.

**Candidates found (post-filter, both passes):** ~55 therapists examined in detail total; ~30 newly evaluated in this second pass. **Updated shortlist:** 4 strong top recommendations (Sevier, Duda, Darazsdi, Gindlesperger — reordered with telehealth-first weighting), 6 honorable mentions (Sage Stevens retained, Hope Krashnak and Ronald Vogt newly added, Kelsey Chizmar / Victoria Prisco / Move Forward retained), 7 ruled out.

### Intake-call questions to confirm alexithymia fit

Since almost no PA therapist publicly advertises alexithymia, the client has to screen for it in the consult call. These are the questions that reliably distinguish a therapist who can actually do this work from one who will default to generic CBT:

1. **"About 50% of autistic adults have alexithymia. How do you approach therapy when the client can't identify what they're feeling?"** — A strong answer will name specific tools: feelings wheels, interoceptive awareness training (Kelly Mahler's work), body-based/somatic work, IFS parts work, "name it to tame it" variants, journaling with specific emotion vocabulary, or emotion-focused therapy adaptations for ASD. A weak answer will say something like "we'd work on emotional awareness" without specifics.
2. **"Have you worked with autistic clients who struggle to identify emotions in their own body — not just to name them, but to notice the physical signal at all?"** — This probes whether they understand the interoception piece, which is the upstream problem for most autistic alexithymia.
3. **"What percent of your caseload is autistic adults vs. autistic children?"** — This is the single cleanest test for whether "autism specialist" means what the client needs. The answer should be predominantly adults.
4. **"What's your approach when a client can't answer 'how do you feel?'"** — Good answers involve reframing the question (body scan, situation → behavior → probable feeling inference, external anchors). Bad answers are "we'd just sit with it" with no plan.
5. **"Are you familiar with the Pellicano & Burr / Lawson / Quattrocki & Friston interoception-and-autism literature?"** or more simply, **"Do you use interoceptive awareness training in your work?"** — Tells you whether they're reading current autism research or running on 15-year-old CBT manuals.

If any candidate fumbles questions 1-4, cross them off regardless of credentials.
