# Concept Research: Comorbidities and Population Overlap — Where Alexithymia Lives Clinically

## Scope and Framing

This category answers a specific cross-sectional question: across the major populations in which alexithymia is highly prevalent, what are the rates, the dominant TAS-20 facet patterns, the clinical-significance findings (does alexithymia track outcome?), and the treatment-matching considerations?

It is **not** the place for the bidirectional causation story. That story sits in two adjacent categories:
- **Cat 7 (Alexithymia ↔ Trauma — Bidirectional Mechanisms)** for the trauma cluster (PTSD, complex PTSD, somatization pathway, peritraumatic dissociation, treatment moderation, re-traumatization, narrative encoding failure).
- **Cat 8 (Autism × Alexithymia × Cumulative Overwhelm)** for the specific autism case (Bird & Cook 2013 alexithymia hypothesis with the cumulative-overwhelm extension).

Cat 6 establishes rates and patterns. Where prospective evidence on directional contribution exists for a given population, Cat 6 notes it briefly with a forward-pointer. Where it doesn't, Cat 6 says so.

## Universal Corrections Applied

Per the validation supplement and the parent-pipeline corrections list:

1. **PTSD prevalence**: 53% (95% CI: 42–65%) from the 2026 36-study meta-analysis (ScienceDirect S0013700626000023). The earlier "53–90%" framing is dropped. The 90% figure (Hernandez-Ramirez 2025 Mexican outpatient cohort) is cited as a single-population outlier, not as an upper bound.
2. **Autism + alexithymia**: 33–63% range from Kinnaird 2019 (PMC6331035), weighted mean 49.93%. The earlier "40–65%" range is replaced with Kinnaird's empirically-derived range.
3. **ADHD**: 20–44% (the "~40%" parent figure is on the high side of a wider range).
4. **Britton 2021 vs Goldberg 2022** are different studies. Numbers are not interchangeable. Cat 6 doesn't lean on either set, but cites them carefully when iatrogenic-harm context appears.

## Population Coverage

### 1. Autism

**Prevalence**. The Kinnaird, Stewart, and Tchanturia (2019) systematic review and meta-analysis (PMC6331035, PubMed 30399531) is the field's anchor finding. Pooled prevalence across the included autism studies: weighted mean 49.93%, range 33.3%–63%. This is dramatically elevated relative to the ~10% general-population rate.

**Pattern**. The autism-alexithymia profile in TAS-20 facets is heterogeneous. The Bird & Cook (2013) "alexithymia hypothesis" frames the elevation as primary cognitive-alexithymia (high DIF and DDF). EOT shows up unevenly across studies. The cluster-analysis literature suggests autism samples include both the high-cognitive subtype (DIF/DDF elevated, EOT closer to typical) and the comprehensive subtype (all three facets elevated).

**Bird & Cook 2013 alexithymia hypothesis**. The headline claim: many emotional-processing symptoms previously attributed to autism per se (e.g., reduced empathic accuracy, poor emotion recognition in faces, atypical autonomic response to others' distress) may be driven by co-occurring alexithymia rather than autism itself. The empirical finding (Cook, Brewer, Shah, Bird 2013, Psychological Science) is that alexithymia, not autism, predicts poor recognition of emotional facial expressions in autistic samples. The hypothesis is contested but has substantially shaped the field.

**Clinical significance**. Autistic individuals with high alexithymia show worse mental-health outcomes (depression, anxiety, suicidality), poorer emotion-recognition performance, and different therapy-response profiles than autistic individuals with typical-range alexithymia. This is increasingly recognised as a within-autism heterogeneity factor.

**Forward pointer**. **Cat 8** treats autism × alexithymia × cumulative overwhelm in depth: how the cognitive-architectural starting point of autism interacts with autonomic dysregulation, sensory overload, and masking demands to produce a specific clinical phenotype. Cat 6 establishes the rate; Cat 8 establishes the mechanism.

**Treatment-matching consideration**. Autism + alexithymia therapy needs differ from non-autistic alexithymia. Externalised approaches (granularity-building via Barrett 2021 framework, structured journaling, Lumley-style EAET adapted) tend to fit better than insistence on internal-introspection-first protocols. The user's existing IFS deep dive covers parts work adapted for low-interoception autistic clients in detail.

**Sources**:
- Kinnaird, Stewart, Tchanturia (2019), Investigating alexithymia in autism: A systematic review and meta-analysis. *European Psychiatry*. PMC6331035, PubMed 30399531.
- Bird & Cook (2013), Mixed emotions: the contribution of alexithymia to the emotional symptoms of autism. *Translational Psychiatry*. PMC3731793.
- Cook, Brewer, Shah, Bird (2013), Alexithymia, not autism, predicts poor recognition of emotional facial expressions. *Psychological Science*. DOI 10.1177/0956797612463582.

### 2. PTSD (classic, ICD-11 / DSM-5 PTSD)

**Prevalence**. The 2026 systematic review and meta-analysis (ScienceDirect S0013700626000023): 36 studies, pooled prevalence **53% (95% CI: 42–65%)**, mean TAS-20 score 56.12. This is the most defensible single figure.

The often-quoted "53–90%" framing in older summaries conflates this pooled estimate with a single-cohort outlier. The 90% rate comes from Hernandez-Ramirez (2025), Mexican PTSD outpatient cross-sectional study (joghnp 138201) — a clinical-treatment-seeking cohort with possible cultural and measurement-norm contributions. It should be cited as an outlier, not as a representative upper bound.

**Pattern**. DIF is consistently the dominant elevated facet in PTSD. DDF is substantial. EOT shows elevations but more variably. The Frewen et al. (2008) original meta-analysis (PMC, PubMed 18404647) reported a *large* effect size (d = 0.8–1.2 across 12 studies, N=1,095 PTSD individuals) for the PTSD-alexithymia association, with male combat samples showing the highest effects. Frewen's clinical/neural correlates work (PubMed 18266495, n=105 PTSD patients) found alexithymia correlated with re-experiencing, hyperarousal, numbing, dissociative symptoms, and retrospectively reported childhood emotional neglect.

**Clinical significance**. Alexithymia in PTSD is associated with worse symptom severity, more dissociative symptoms, more somatic morbidity, slower treatment response, and higher dropout in trauma-focused therapy. Honkalampi et al. (2023, PubMed 37987839) multi-wave study found baseline alexithymia predicted hyperarousal and total PTSD severity at all three follow-up time points, partially mediated by general psychological distress.

**Brief note on directional contribution**. Prospective evidence supports pre-trauma alexithymia as a vulnerability factor (McCaslin et al. 2006 NYC police prospective study, PubMed 16788996, alexithymia significantly predicted post-9/11 PTSD over and above pre-trauma symptoms in n=54 followed). The full architecture — vulnerability, peritraumatic dissociation, narrative encoding failure, chronicity maintenance, treatment moderation, re-traumatization, somatization — is treated in **Cat 7**.

**Treatment-matching consideration**. Prolonged Exposure (PE) shows attenuated and delayed response in high-alexithymia patients (Pugach et al., PubMed 37917810): more likely to retain full PTSD diagnosis post-treatment and at 6-month follow-up. CBT/CPT trauma-focused outcomes are similarly negatively impacted by alexithymia. Phase-based protocols with affect-skills training first (STAIR-NT, Cloitre lineage) appear to address the alexithymic-PTSD profile more effectively than direct exposure. Cat 7 covers the treatment architecture in depth.

**Sources**:
- 2026 systematic review (ScienceDirect S0013700626000023), pooled prevalence 53% (CI 42–65%), 36 studies.
- Frewen et al. (2008), Posttraumatic stress and alexithymia: A meta-analysis. *Journal of Traumatic Stress*. PubMed 18404647, Wiley DOI 10.1002/jts.20320.
- Hernandez-Ramirez (2025), Prevalence of alexithymia in patients with PTSD. *JOGHNP* 138201. (Cited as outlier.)
- McCaslin et al. (2006). PubMed 16788996.
- Honkalampi et al. (2023). PubMed 37987839.
- Pugach et al. (PE moderation). PubMed 37917810.

### 3. Complex PTSD (CPTSD)

**Prevalence**. CPTSD samples typically show alexithymia rates **at the upper end of or above the PTSD pooled range** (53% CI 42–65%), consistent with their more pervasive emotion-regulation deficits. A clean meta-analytic estimate specifically for CPTSD vs classic PTSD is not yet available — the ICD-11 CPTSD literature is young, the diagnostic boundary is not always clean, and most published samples mix the two.

**Pattern**. CPTSD samples typically show the comprehensive cluster pattern (all three TAS-20 facets elevated) with prominent dissociation comorbidity. The Schimmenti / Caretti developmental-trauma synthesis treats alexithymia, dissociation, and affect dysregulation as connected sequelae of disorganised attachment plus chronic developmental trauma — that is, CPTSD-typical alexithymia behaves like a *core manifestation* of the affective-dysregulation domain, even where it is not a *core ICD-11 diagnostic criterion*.

**Clinical significance**. Cloitre's foundational work on childhood-abuse survivors (ScienceDirect S0005789405800607): after controlling for PTSD symptomatology, emotion-regulation deficits and interpersonal problems contributed to functional impairment equal to PTSD itself. STAIR (Skills Training in Affective and Interpersonal Regulation) Narrative Therapy was developed precisely because traditional trauma-focused therapy was insufficient for complex-trauma survivors with severe affect-regulation deficits — an operationalised acknowledgement that alexithymia and related deficits must be addressed first in this population.

**Brief note on directional contribution**. Bidirectional. Developmental trauma → alexithymia is well-established. Adult alexithymia → maintenance and chronicity is also well-supported in the longitudinal literature. **Cat 7** treats both arms.

**Treatment-matching consideration**. Phase-based protocols (STAIR-NT, Phase-Based CPTSD treatment, Cloitre's lineage; Lanius's sensory-pathways model) are the consensus approach. Direct exposure typically contraindicated until affect-regulation skills (including alexithymia-targeted vocabulary work) are in place.

**Sources**:
- Cloitre et al. STAIR-NT lineage. ScienceDirect S0005789405800607.
- Schimmenti & Caretti developmental-trauma synthesis (Italian school).
- ICD-11 CPTSD network analyses: Frontiers in Psychiatry 2024, DOI 10.3389/fpsyt.2024.1372620.

### 4. Eating Disorders

**Prevalence**. The Nowakowski, McFarlane, and Cassin (2013) critical review (jeatdisord.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/2050-2974-1-21) put the rate at approximately **30–50%** across eating-disorder samples, with anorexia restricting subtype tending to show the highest rates. Bulimia and binge-eating disorder show elevated but somewhat lower rates than anorexia restricting.

**Pattern**. **DIF specifically predicts poor prognosis** in the eating-disorder literature — this is the standout facet finding. DDF is also elevated. EOT shows variable patterns. The DIF specificity reflects the emotion-avoidance model of eating-disorder maintenance: when the person cannot identify the affective state driving the urge to restrict/binge/purge, the food behaviour functions as a proximal regulation strategy without engaging the underlying affective process.

**Clinical significance**. Higher baseline alexithymia (especially DIF) predicts:
- Worse treatment response in inpatient and outpatient programs.
- Higher dropout from emotion-focused interventions.
- Poorer long-term remission rates.
- Persistence of eating-disorder symptoms across follow-up.

The emotion-avoidance maintenance model (Treasure, Schmidt and colleagues) treats alexithymia as not merely correlated with eating disorders but as a maintaining mechanism: the person cannot tolerate or process the affect that drives the food behaviour, so the food behaviour persists as a regulation tool.

**Brief note on directional contribution**. Cross-sectional and prospective evidence from inpatient eating-disorder samples shows that alexithymia is partially state-dependent (some reduction with weight restoration in anorexia) but a substantial trait component persists. The trait component predicts post-discharge recurrence. There is also evidence (an EMDRIA-cited eating-disorder inpatient study) that *higher baseline alexithymia* is associated with *higher effect sizes* from specialised treatment that addresses alexithymia, dissociation, and emotion regulation — opposite-direction evidence that with the right protocol, this population can show substantial gains.

**Treatment-matching consideration**. Standard CBT-E and IPT effects on eating-disorder symptoms are partly attenuated in high-alexithymia patients. Approaches that specifically target emotion identification (DBT skills, MBT, emotion-focused therapy adapted) and somatic awareness (with care given the body-image complications) appear to do better. Lumley-style EAET has shown promise in adjacent emotion-regulation populations.

**Sources**:
- Nowakowski, McFarlane, Cassin (2013). *Journal of Eating Disorders*. DOI 10.1186/2050-2974-1-21.
- EMDRIA-cited eating-disorder inpatient treatment improvement study.

### 5. Depression

**Prevalence**. High comorbidity. Specific pooled prevalence is not as cleanly meta-analytically established as for PTSD or autism, but cross-sectional studies consistently show alexithymia rates of approximately 30–50% in MDD outpatient samples and higher in inpatient depressive samples.

**Pattern**. DIF and DDF prominently elevated in MDD samples, often co-elevated; EOT more variable. Genetic correlation work (the Italian twin study, Picardi 2011) found a genetic correlation r = 0.65 between alexithymia and depression, indicating substantially overlapping genetic architecture.

**Clinical significance — the state vs trait alexithymia question**. The depression literature is the central place where the state-vs-trait distinction has been worked out. Alexithymia scores rise during depressive episodes and partially remit when depression remits (Saarijärvi, Honkalampi, and others). This is the key empirical finding behind the "state alexithymia" concept.

However, the trait-stability work (Tolmunen et al. 2011, 11-year follow-up; PubMed 21081227) shows that *relative-rank stability is high even across mood episodes*. Someone who is alexithymic at the 70th percentile during a depressive episode is likely still around the 70th percentile when the depression remits — they may move down in absolute score but their position in the population is largely preserved. The honest reading: alexithymia has both a trait core and a state component that fluctuates with depression severity.

**Brief note on directional contribution**. Bidirectional. Alexithymia predicts worse depression treatment outcomes (slower response, lower remission rates, higher recurrence). Depression severity also feeds back into elevated alexithymia scores. Genetic correlation suggests shared upstream architecture for both.

**Treatment-matching consideration**. CBT for depression shows attenuated effects in high-alexithymia patients. Behavioural activation may be relatively more accessible than cognitive-restructuring approaches that depend on accurate emotion-identification. Antidepressants reduce alexithymia scores partly via mood improvement (the state component).

**Sources**:
- Saarijärvi et al. State-vs-trait alexithymia in MDD.
- Honkalampi et al. depression-alexithymia stability work.
- Picardi et al. (2011) Italian twin study, r = 0.65 genetic correlation alexithymia-depression. PubMed 21911104.
- Tolmunen et al. (2011, 2017) trait stability. PubMed 21081227, 28314554.

### 6. Somatic Symptom Disorder, Fibromyalgia, Chronic Pain

**Prevalence**. The Aaron, Fisher, and colleagues (2019) meta-analysis (PMC6688175) on alexithymia in chronic pain provides the field's anchor: prevalence range **15–52%** across pain populations. Fibromyalgia and somatic-symptom-disorder samples sit toward the upper end. Chronic-pain populations more broadly span the range. Functional gastrointestinal disorders (IBS) show similar elevations.

**Pattern**. Heterogeneous. Some chronic-pain samples show DIF-dominant patterns; some show comprehensive elevation. Fibromyalgia samples often show elevated DIF + DDF with elevated EOT.

**Important caveat**. Aaron et al. 2019 documented that **when controlling for negative affect (depression, anxiety), the alexithymia–pain-intensity association attenuates substantially**. The independent contribution of alexithymia to pain experience, separate from co-occurring depression and anxiety, is smaller than uncontrolled associations suggest. This is a critical methodological hedge.

**Clinical significance**. Alexithymia is associated with:
- Greater pain catastrophising.
- Higher somatic-symptom amplification.
- Worse functional outcomes.
- Lower response to standard pain-management interventions.

The mediation literature (Frontiers in Psychology 2020, DOI 10.3389/fpsyg.2020.545881) frames alexithymia as a mediator from negative life events / emotional dysregulation to somatic-symptom expression — but the same caveat applies: when negative affect is controlled, the mediation attenuates.

**Brief note on directional contribution**. The somatization pathway (affect that cannot be cognitively encoded gets routed into autonomic and somatic channels) has strong theoretical and clinical support but limited prospective causal evidence. The Sifneos / Nemiah / Taylor / Bagby psychosomatic origins frame is compatible with the pathway but underdetermines its directionality.

**Forward pointer**. **Cat 7** treats the trauma-somatization mechanism (alexithymia mediating from trauma exposure to somatic-symptom expression, FND/PNES, and chronic-pain morbidity in PTSD populations).

**Treatment-matching consideration**. **Lumley's Emotional Awareness and Expression Therapy (EAET)** has multiple RCTs supporting it in fibromyalgia, IBS, MUS, and older veterans with chronic pain (PMC7309024 and follow-ups). Higher baseline depression, anxiety, and PTSD symptoms moderate *greater* pain reduction after EAET than after CBT — meaning EAET's emotional-engagement protocol does relatively better in the more emotionally dysregulated subgroup. The Lumley work is the cleanest published example of symptom-severity-by-therapy-assignment moderation in this literature.

**Sources**:
- Aaron RV et al. (2019), Alexithymia in chronic pain meta-analysis. PMC6688175.
- Frontiers in Psychology mediation model (2020). DOI 10.3389/fpsyg.2020.545881.
- Lumley et al. EAET. PMC7309024.
- Carty et al. ~2023 EAET PTSD-symptom moderation finding.

### 7. ADHD

**Prevalence**. Range **20–44%** across ADHD samples in available meta-analytic and review work. The "~40%" figure that has appeared in some summaries is on the high side of this range; the lower bound (20%) is consistent with smaller studies. The honest range is reported.

**Pattern**. Emotion-recognition and emotion-labelling deficits are the prominent ADHD-alexithymia signature. DIF and DDF tend to be elevated; EOT variable. The pattern overlaps with the emotion-dysregulation phenotype increasingly recognised as a core feature of adult ADHD.

**Clinical significance**. ADHD + alexithymia is associated with worse psychosocial functioning, elevated mood comorbidity, and higher rates of substance use. The emotion-dysregulation literature in ADHD (Barkley and colleagues) treats alexithymia as one of several markers of the same underlying executive / emotion-regulation deficit cluster.

**Brief note on directional contribution**. Cross-sectional data only; prospective evidence on directional contribution is thin. The shared-substrate hypothesis (executive-function deficits underlying both ADHD and emotion-recognition difficulties) is compatible with the data and parsimonious.

**Treatment-matching consideration**. Standard ADHD pharmacotherapy (stimulants) addresses some of the emotion-dysregulation phenotype indirectly. DBT skills modules adapted for ADHD have shown promise. Externalised emotion-tracking (apps, journaling with structured emotion vocabulary) fits the executive-function profile.

**Sources**:
- ADHD-alexithymia comorbidity literature (multiple reviews).
- Barkley emotion-dysregulation in ADHD.

### 8. Substance Use Disorders

**Prevalence**. The Honkalampi et al. (2022, PMC9790486) meta-analysis is the field's anchor. Across clinical samples, alexithymia prevalence runs **42–67%**. The overall effect size (Cohen's d) was 0.62 across all samples; **0.83 in clinical samples specifically** — a large effect size indicating substantial elevation relative to non-clinical comparison groups.

**Pattern**. DIF prominently elevated; DDF substantial; EOT variable. The emotion-regulation-via-substance hypothesis frames the high DIF as the cognitive substrate for substance-use behaviour: when the person cannot identify the affective state driving distress, the substance functions as a proximal regulation strategy without engaging the underlying affect.

**Clinical significance**. Alexithymia in SUD predicts:
- Worse treatment retention (higher dropout).
- Lower abstinence rates.
- Higher relapse rates.
- More somatic and psychiatric comorbidity.

**Brief note on directional contribution**. Bidirectional. Pre-existing alexithymia raises vulnerability to substance-use as an emotion-regulation strategy. Chronic substance use also degrades emotion-recognition capacity (acute and possibly subchronic neurotoxic effects on insula and other regions). The cross-sectional prevalence captures the steady-state mixture.

**Treatment-matching consideration**. Standard SUD treatment (12-step, motivational interviewing, contingency management) shows attenuated response in high-alexithymia patients. DBT-substance-use modules and emotion-regulation-focused approaches (including MBT and the emotion-focused therapies) show promise. Externalised emotion-tracking (journaling, apps, behavioural-cue-tracking) fits the clinical profile.

**Sources**:
- Honkalampi K et al. (2022), Association between alexithymia and substance use. *Cohen's d=0.62 overall; d=0.83 in clinical samples*. PMC9790486.

### 9. Schizophrenia Spectrum / Psychosis Vulnerability

**Prevalence**. Elevated relative to general population, with rates varying widely by sample (clinical inpatient versus outpatient, first-episode versus chronic). Pooled prevalence figures are less consistently reported than for autism, PTSD, or eating disorders.

**Pattern**. **Cognitive alexithymia (Type II in Bermond's model) rises across the psychosis-risk gradient**. The empirical finding: van der Velde et al. (2015) and the Ozdemir et al. (2025) meta-analysis (PMC12050105) show progressively higher cognitive-alexithymia scores from healthy controls → at-risk-mental-state populations → first-episode psychosis → chronic schizophrenia. Affective alexithymia (Type I in Bermond's model) shows less consistent gradient elevation. This is the cleanest published example of TAS-20 / BVAQ facet patterning differentiating across a clinical risk gradient.

**Important hedge**. The Type II → psychosis link is from a **narrow research lineage** (primarily Polish and Dutch groups; van der Velde, Ozdemir, related programs). The finding has been replicated within this lineage but the broader cross-cultural and cross-laboratory replication base is thinner than for the autism, PTSD, or eating-disorder findings. Cite with appropriate caution.

**Clinical significance**. Alexithymia in psychosis-spectrum populations is associated with worse social functioning, more negative symptoms, and possibly altered insight. The cognitive-alexithymia gradient may serve as a vulnerability marker for transition to psychosis in at-risk populations, but this is exploratory rather than established.

**Brief note on directional contribution**. The directional question for psychosis is less developed than for trauma. Possibilities include: (a) cognitive alexithymia as a vulnerability trait; (b) cognitive alexithymia as a downstream consequence of early-stage psychotic process affecting emotion-cognition; (c) shared substrate (default-mode network and salience-network alterations) producing both. Cross-sectional gradient data are compatible with all three.

**Treatment-matching consideration**. Standard psychosis treatment (antipsychotics + psychosocial intervention) is not specifically alexithymia-targeted. Cognitive remediation programs that address emotion-recognition specifically (e.g., facial-affect-recognition training) have shown some benefit in social-cognitive impairment in schizophrenia and may be relevant.

**Sources**:
- van der Velde et al. (2015), psychosis-spectrum cognitive-alexithymia gradient.
- Ozdemir et al. (2025), Alexithymia in schizophrenia and psychosis vulnerability meta-analysis. PMC12050105.

### 10. Functional Neurological Disorder (FND) and Psychogenic Non-Epileptic Seizures (PNES)

**Prevalence**. Range **35–75% across studies, with ~40% as a reasonable point estimate**. Demartini et al. (2014) is the most-cited single estimate. Recent reviews (PMC11608540) confirm the range.

**Pattern**. Heterogeneous. Some FND samples show DIF-dominant patterns; some show comprehensive elevation. The role-of-alexithymia-in-functional-motor-symptoms paper (PMC4173967) frames the mechanism as patients misattributing autonomic symptoms of anxiety (tremor, paresthesias, paralysis) to physical illness because the cognitive-emotional layer of identification is missing.

**Clinical significance**. FND is **under-studied as a treatment population for alexithymia-targeted interventions**. The biopsychosocial integrative model (PMC11608540) treats FND as not autonomous but as developing on the grounds of co-occurring vulnerabilities — trauma, autism, alexithymia, BPD, PTSD. The trauma-subtype FND population (PMC9706184) shows alexithymia partially accounting for the relationship between FND + probable PTSD and symptom severity / lower physical health.

**Brief note on directional contribution**. The somatization pathway (alexithymia → autonomic-symptom misattribution → functional-symptom elaboration) has theoretical and cross-sectional support; prospective causal evidence is limited. **Cat 7** treats the trauma-somatization architecture in depth.

**Treatment-matching consideration**. FND treatment typically combines physical-rehabilitation approaches (physiotherapy, occupational therapy) with psychological intervention (CBT-FND protocols, trauma-focused work where indicated). Specific alexithymia-targeted protocols are not yet standardised in FND treatment. The under-studied status of this population is a clinical gap.

**Sources**:
- Demartini et al. (2014), Alexithymia and somatic symptoms in FND.
- PMC11608540 (recent FND review).
- PMC4173967 (functional-motor-symptoms paper).
- PMC9706184 (trauma-subtype FND).

### 11. Anxiety Disorders

**Prevalence**. High comorbidity across anxiety disorders (panic disorder, GAD, social anxiety, OCD-spectrum). Specific pooled prevalence figures vary by disorder; the 30–50% range is consistent across most anxiety samples.

**Pattern**. DIF and DDF prominently elevated; EOT variable. The pattern overlaps substantially with depression-alexithymia, consistent with the negative-affectivity shared variance.

**Clinical significance**. Alexithymia in anxiety disorders predicts more severe symptoms, more somatic anxiety expression, and possibly lower CBT response. The somatic-amplification pathway (alexithymics show heightened subjective-physical-symptom intensity for a given autonomic activation level) is particularly relevant for panic disorder.

**Brief note on directional contribution**. Bidirectional and overlapping with the depression literature. Genetic and developmental shared substrate is the most parsimonious account.

**Treatment-matching consideration**. Standard anxiety CBT is partly attenuated in high-alexithymia patients. ACT, MBT, and emotion-focused approaches show promise. For panic disorder specifically, interoceptive exposure (the standard CBT-panic component) needs careful pacing in alexithymic patients given the parsing-deficit framework — see the iatrogenic-harm category in this deep dive (Cat 9 in the new numbering, currently under construction).

**Sources**:
- Anxiety-alexithymia cross-sectional literature (multiple reviews).

### 12. Acromegaly and Other Medical Conditions Where Alexithymia Persists Despite Remission

**Why this matters as a category**. A small but interesting literature shows that alexithymia is elevated in certain medical conditions (acromegaly, some cardiovascular and dermatological conditions) and **persists or only partially remits despite primary-condition treatment**. This is interesting evidence for the trait-component (vs. purely state) of alexithymia in these contexts.

**Pattern**. Variable across conditions; depends on the etiological mix in the specific population.

**Clinical significance**. The persistence-despite-remission finding is important for the broader question of whether alexithymia in medical populations reflects a stable trait (perhaps amplified by the medical condition but not caused by it) or a state response that should remit with primary-condition treatment. The honest reading: it's likely a mixture, with the trait component outweighing the state component in most studied populations.

**Sources**:
- Acromegaly-alexithymia studies.
- Persistent-alexithymia-despite-remission medical-condition literature.

### 13. First Responders (Police, Firefighters, Paramedics)

**Prevalence**. Elevated alexithymia rates have been documented in first responders relative to general population, alongside elevated trauma exposure rates. Specific pooled prevalence varies by sample.

**Pattern**. Variable; some studies show elevated EOT + elevated DDF (consistent with task-focused, externally-oriented professional culture); others show elevated DIF (consistent with a trauma-exposed profile).

**Clinical significance — the contested directional question**. The direction of causation in first responders is **contested**:
- Some research treats alexithymia as a **risk factor** for PTSD development in trauma-exposed occupations.
- Some treats alexithymia as a **protective trait** that allows the person to remain in trauma-exposed work without acute decompensation (the adaptive-function framing — but with documented long-term costs).
- Some treats alexithymia as a **trauma-induced consequence** of cumulative occupational exposure.

The Riethof et al. (2020, female healthcare professionals SAGE), the police officers with/without PTSD work (Tandfonline 2023, DOI 10.1080/20008066.2023.2281187), and the post-October-7th aftermath literature (ScienceDirect 2025) all illustrate this contested terrain.

**Brief note on directional contribution**. The Heinrichs et al. (2005) firefighter prospective study (PubMed 16330591) found alexithymia *increased alongside* PTSD symptoms across 2-year follow-up — suggesting that in this sample, alexithymia behaved more as a *consequence* than as an *antecedent*. The McCaslin NYC police prospective work supports the antecedent framing for police. The honest reading: the direction may differ by occupation, by exposure type, and by individual. This is an active research question.

**Forward pointer**. **Cat 7** treats the bidirectional architecture in depth. Cat 6's role is to acknowledge that first responders are a high-prevalence population and that the directional question for this population specifically is contested.

**Treatment-matching consideration**. Standard first-responder mental-health programs (Critical Incident Stress Debriefing, EMDR, trauma-focused CBT) have been increasingly modified to address alexithymia and dissociative-spectrum responses in this population.

**Sources**:
- Riethof et al. (2020), SAGE.
- Police PTSD/alexithymia work, Tandfonline 2023.
- Heinrichs et al. (2005). PubMed 16330591.
- McCaslin et al. (2006). PubMed 16788996.

## Common Patterns Across Populations

Several patterns recur across the populations covered above:

1. **DIF is the most consistently elevated facet**. Across autism, PTSD, eating disorders, substance use, depression, anxiety, and FND, Difficulty Identifying Feelings is the most reliably elevated TAS-20 facet. DDF is nearly as consistent. EOT shows more population-by-population variability.

2. **High alexithymia predicts worse treatment response across populations**. This is one of the most robust findings in the field. CBT, PE, CBT-E, standard SUD treatment, and standard depression/anxiety treatment all show attenuated response in high-alexithymia patients. This is part of why treatment-matching by alexithymia subtype matters.

3. **Bidirectional causation is the norm rather than the exception**. Almost no major comorbidity shows a clean unidirectional story. The default reading is bidirectional with shared substrate, with directional emphasis varying by population.

4. **State vs trait variability is real but relative-rank stability is high**. Alexithymia scores fluctuate with mood, distress, and primary-condition severity. But in longitudinal data, individual rank-order is preserved across years. The trait core is real; the state component is real; both operate.

5. **The negative-affectivity shared variance complicates causal inference**. When studies control for depression and anxiety, many alexithymia-outcome associations attenuate substantially. This is the methodological caveat that hangs over the entire comorbidity literature.

## The Transdiagnostic Question

**Is alexithymia a transdiagnostic mechanism?** That is, does it represent a common substrate that contributes to many disorders rather than an artifact of disorder-specific variance?

**The Brewer, Murphy, and Bird (2021) thesis** (PMC8522807, *Neuroscience and Biobehavioral Reviews*, 130:470–508) argues yes. Their headline claim: variation in interoception (the perception of internal bodily state) is a **transdiagnostic risk factor for psychopathology**. Atypical interoception, with which alexithymia substantially overlaps, is positioned as a common substrate underlying autism's emotion-recognition difficulties, PTSD's affective dysregulation, eating disorders' body-state misperception, anxiety's somatic amplification, and depression's anhedonia.

**Recent network analyses** support transdiagnostic positioning. ICD-11 CPTSD network analyses (Frontiers 2024) treat emotional processing and dissociative experiences as central nodes connecting affective dysregulation, negative self-concept, and disturbed-relationship features. Alexithymia conceptually clusters with the central affect-dysregulation node in these networks.

**The honest hedge**. The transdiagnostic-mechanism thesis is evidence-supported but not closed. Alternative readings remain plausible:
- **Negative-affectivity confound**. Alexithymia, depression, anxiety, and most affective psychopathology share a substantial general-distress variance. Some of the transdiagnostic appearance may reflect this shared general factor rather than a specific alexithymia mechanism.
- **Population-specific causal structures**. The directional contribution and clinical significance of alexithymia may differ enough across autism, PTSD, eating disorders, etc., that a single transdiagnostic-mechanism label obscures more than it illuminates.
- **Construct stability**. The TAS-20 and related instruments may capture different things in different populations (the construct-non-equivalence concern). If so, "alexithymia" in autism and "alexithymia" in PTSD may not be the same construct, even with similar measurement scores.

**The defensible position**: alexithymia behaves like a transdiagnostic mechanism in much of the available evidence, especially the interoception-overlap, network-analysis, and shared-treatment-response findings. But "transdiagnostic mechanism" is not the same as "single underlying cause" — it more honestly captures "a frequently-implicated common feature across disorders, with mechanisms that may vary across populations."

## Treatment-Matching Considerations

Bringing the population coverage together for the practical question: which therapy approaches have evidence in which comorbid population?

## Summary

Cat 6 establishes the cross-sectional terrain. Across more than a dozen clinical populations, alexithymia is meaningfully prevalent (typically 30–60%, varying by population and measurement), tends to predict worse treatment response, shows DIF dominance most consistently, and behaves bidirectionally rather than unidirectionally with most comorbid conditions. The transdiagnostic-mechanism reading (Brewer, Murphy, Bird 2021) has substantial support but is not closed.

The mechanism question — does alexithymia cause the disorder, does the disorder cause alexithymia, or do they share substrate? — is treated in depth in **Cat 7** for the trauma cluster and **Cat 8** for the autism case. This category establishes the rates and patterns; the trauma and autism categories establish the mechanism architecture.
