# Concept Research: The Heterogeneity Problem — Subtypes, Dimensions, and Typologies

## Why this category exists

The umbrella label "alexithymia" obscures at least seven distinct ways the construct fractures in the empirical literature. A person scoring high on a TAS-20 cutoff can have one of several quite different presentations: feelings absent altogether; feelings present but illegible; bodily signals over-attended but mislabeled somatically. Each presentation has different neural correlates, different developmental etiology, and — most consequentially — points to different therapeutic interventions. The same intervention can help the first profile and harm the third.

This page is the spine of the alexithymia deep dive. Its job is not to settle which subtype scheme is "true" but to give the reader a navigation toolkit: a map of the seven axes along which alexithymia varies, an evidence-weight assessment for each axis (which schemes are descriptively useful but empirically contested, which are robust), and a self-navigation decision tree that helps the reader locate themselves on each axis without forcing a categorical box.

## Axis 1: TAS-20 facets — DIF, DDF, EOT

### What the three facets capture

The TAS-20 is the dominant self-report measure in alexithymia research, with three subscales:

- **DIF (Difficulty Identifying Feelings)**: 7 items asking the respondent to rate statements like "I am often confused about what emotion I am feeling" and "When I am upset, I don't know if I am sad, frightened or angry." This facet captures the central phenomenological complaint — uncertainty about which emotion is occurring.
- **DDF (Difficulty Describing Feelings)**: 5 items, "I find it hard to describe how I feel about people," "It is difficult for me to find the right words for my feelings." DDF asks about translation: from inner state to language.
- **EOT (Externally Oriented Thinking)**: 8 items, "I prefer to analyze problems rather than just describe them," "I prefer to just let things happen rather than to understand why they turned out that way." EOT measures cognitive style — preference for surface, practical, external content over internal-emotional content.

### How the facets dissociate

DIF and DDF share substantial variance — about 60% in most factor-analytic work — and people typically score similarly on both. EOT is the unstable one. Schroeders, Kubera, and Gnambs (2022, [PMC9597132](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9597132/)) ran a meta-analytic confirmatory factor analysis across 36 samples and 17,279 individuals and found that the EOT factor's reliability and factor loading were systematically poorer than DIF or DDF, with poor cross-cultural invariance. The three-factor model holds in many samples, but the three factors are not equally well-measured and EOT does not always replicate as a coherent factor in non-Western or older-adult samples.

This dissociability matters clinically. Three rough patterns appear in cluster analyses:
- **High on all three**: the "comprehensive" profile; the most clearly clinically alexithymic.
- **High DIF + high DDF + normal EOT**: feelings are present but illegible; the person notices something is happening internally and cannot name it. Clinically this often presents as confusion rather than emptiness.
- **Normal DIF + normal DDF + high EOT**: task-focused, externally-oriented, but with intact emotion access. This profile shows up in some occupational populations and looks more like a cognitive-style choice than an emotion-processing failure.

### What TAS-20 does not capture

- **Positive emotion**: The TAS-20 items are almost entirely about negative emotion ("upset," "feel sad," "afraid"). The Perth Alexithymia Questionnaire (Preece 2018) was developed in part to address this gap by adding positive-valence items.
- **Imaginative/fantasy life**: Sifneos's original 1972 four-feature definition included "constricted imaginative life." This dropped out of the TAS-20 during instrument development. The BVAQ retains it (the "fantasizing" subscale). For people whose alexithymic profile includes a vivid fantasy life — common in autistic populations — the TAS-20 will miss part of the picture.
- **Affective dimension** (low arousability, low emotional intensity): also captured by BVAQ but not TAS-20. People who experience reduced emotional intensity but can still describe what little they feel will often score low on TAS-20 despite a real alexithymic phenotype.

## Axis 2: Bermond Type I / Type II — the cognitive-affective distinction

### The two-dimensional structure of BVAQ

The Bermond-Vorst Alexithymia Questionnaire ([Vorst & Bermond 2001](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0191886900000337)) was designed to capture both halves of what Bermond argued was the alexithymia construct. Five subscales:

- **Identifying** (cognitive)
- **Verbalizing** (cognitive)
- **Analyzing** (cognitive — capacity to reflect on the meaning of one's feelings)
- **Fantasizing** (affective — imagination, daydreaming)
- **Emotionalizing** (affective — actual experiencing of emotional reactions)

The first three load on a higher-order **cognitive** factor; the last two load on an **affective** factor. This two-dimensional structure has replicated across multiple studies in different languages. Bagby and colleagues' subsequent psychometric work confirms the two-dimensional structure as the most robust BVAQ finding.

### From dimensions to types — the Bermond typology

Bermond used the two dimensions to derive a 2x2 categorical typology:

- **Type I**: low cognitive AND low affective. Sifneos's original "affectless" psychosomatic phenotype. Feelings appear absent both as inner experience and as cognitive accessibility.
- **Type II**: high/normal affective, low cognitive. Feelings are present (the body reacts, arousal happens, fantasy life is intact) but illegible — the person cannot identify, verbalize, or analyze them.
- **Type III**: low affective, high/normal cognitive. The person can name and describe emotions but does not feel them strongly. Sometimes called "emotionally numb" or "cognitively-articulate but flat."
- **Type IV**: high/normal cognitive AND high/normal affective. Non-alexithymic.

### Empirical status of the typology

The dimensions are robust. The discrete categorical types are not. A five-factor BVAQ model fits the data better than the higher-order two-factor model that Type I/II rests on; the cleaner statistical move is to treat the cognitive and affective dimensions as continuous and report scores on each rather than assign discrete type labels.

For downstream clinical use, the most defensible reading is dimensional: "where does this person sit on the cognitive axis, and where on the affective axis?" rather than "which of four boxes is this person in?" The Type I / Type II labels remain useful as descriptive shorthand but should not be reified.

### Type II → psychosis vulnerability — strength of evidence

This deserves careful framing because the user-facing impact is significant.

**The pattern is real-as-published**:
- **van der Velde et al. 2015** ([PMC4451258](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4451258/)). The strongest single piece of evidence. A parametric-cohort design comparing healthy controls (cognitive-alexithymia score 48.8), siblings of schizophrenia patients (55.2), ultra-high-risk individuals (68.0), and patients with schizophrenia (64.6). Statistically significant gradient: cognitive alexithymia rose with risk; affective alexithymia did not. This is the closest the literature comes to a graded vulnerability marker.
- **Larøi et al. 2008** linked BVAQ alexithymia to schizotypy in non-clinical samples; DIF and DDF specifically associated with negative schizotypy.
- **Cedro et al. 2001** ([Psychological Reports 89(1):95-98](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.2466/pr0.2001.89.1.95)). Polish schizophrenia patients scored higher on TAS-20 than controls; alexithymia was associated with hallucinations specifically.
- **Ozdemir et al. 2025** ([PMC12050105](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12050105/)). Systematic review and meta-analysis. Confirms moderate associations between DIF/DDF and subclinical negative symptoms in community samples.

**The hedges that must accompany this**:
- The lineage is largely from one Dutch group (van der Velde, Aleman, Krabbendam) plus Polish (Cedro) replications. Independent non-Dutch / non-Polish BVAQ replications in psychosis-risk samples are limited.
- **No prospective ultra-high-risk cohort data**: nobody has yet shown that the Type II pattern *predicts* transition to psychosis prospectively. Van der Velde 2015 is parametric-cross-sectional, not prospective.
- The Ozdemir 2025 review explicitly notes: limited number of studies report dimensional relationships, ~60% of studies do not control for distress-related covariates, cross-sectional designs predominate.
- The categorical Type II distinction itself is weakly supported in the broader BVAQ literature; the cognitive-alexithymia *dimension* is the more defensible claim.

**Recommended framing for the page**: "Higher cognitive alexithymia (high DIF/DDF) is elevated parametrically across the psychosis-risk spectrum (van der Velde 2015), suggesting a possible vulnerability marker. The Type I / Type II categorical distinction itself is weakly supported; the underlying dimensions are real. Prospective evidence that the Type II pattern *predicts* transition to psychosis is not yet published."

## Axis 3: Primary / Secondary / Organic — etiological taxonomy

[Messina, Beadle, Paradiso 2014](https://old.jpsychopathol.it/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/08-Messina1.pdf) proposed a clinical etiology heuristic:

### Primary alexithymia
- Lifelong, dispositional, presumed constitutional.
- Twin studies put heritability at ~30% (Danish twin study, N=8,785 pairs, [Jorgensen et al. 2007, PubMed 17917473](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17917473/)) to ~42% (Italian twin study, [Picardi et al. 2011, PubMed 21911104](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21911104/)).
- The heritable signal is real but partial — most variance is environmental. Primary doesn't mean "purely genetic," it means "present from early life and shaped by constitutional factors."
- This profile often co-occurs with autism, where the constitutional cognitive-architecture difference shapes emotion processing from infancy onward.

### Secondary alexithymia
- Developmental — emerges from trauma, prolonged stress, sociocultural pressure, attachment disruption, or "state" alexithymia in chronic illness.
- Childhood maltreatment is meta-analytically documented as a mediator from adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) to adult psychopathology via alexithymia.
- The "normative male alexithymia" Levant proposed sits here: a learned restriction of emotional vocabulary driven by gender-role socialization.
- Secondary alexithymia is typically more responsive to therapy than primary — it represents a learned pattern rather than a constitutional difference.

### Organic / Acquired alexithymia
- Post-lesion: stroke, traumatic brain injury, neurodegeneration.
- Reliably localizes to anterior insula. [Hogeveen, Bird, Chau, Krueger, Grafman 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4752907/) showed in a large cohort of Vietnam-war head-injured veterans that anterior insula damage of >15% reliably produces acquired alexithymia; about 15% of brain-injury patients meet alexithymia criteria, most with anterior insula involvement.
- Treatment for acquired alexithymia is fundamentally different — neurorehabilitation, compensatory strategies, environmental scaffolding rather than insight-oriented therapy.

### Where the heuristic helps and where it doesn't

The Messina et al. paper is a clinical heuristic, not a large empirical study. It hasn't been validated as a discrete classification scheme — there's no test that reliably places an individual into one of the three buckets. But the heuristic captures something useful: the etiology of someone's alexithymia conditions what therapies are likely to help. A trauma survivor's secondary alexithymia (where emotion access existed and was suppressed by adaptive learning) calls for different work than a constitutionally autistic adult's primary alexithymia (where emotion access was differently constituted from the start) than a stroke survivor's organic alexithymia (where the relevant brain region is no longer producing the input the rest of the system needs).

The heuristic also breaks down in mixed cases. Someone who is both autistic (primary) and trauma-exposed (secondary) is the most common alexithymic case in clinical populations and doesn't fit cleanly into one bucket.

## Axis 4: Latent-profile / cluster subtypes — the severity-continuum reframe

This is one of the user-flagged YELLOW items. The parent exploration's "four-class scheme" framing needs a corrective reframe.

### The original four-class proposal

Chen et al. (2011, 2017) used cluster analysis on TAS-20 data and proposed four subtypes:
- **General-high (comprehensive)**: elevated on all three TAS-20 facets
- **High-cognitive (introvert-high)**: elevated DIF + DDF, normal EOT — feelings illegible
- **High-EOT (extrovert-high)**: elevated EOT, normal DIF/DDF — externally-oriented but with intact access
- **Non-alexithymic**: low on all facets

These labels became widely cited in clinical literature and have intuitive appeal — they map onto recognizable presentations.

### Why the four-class scheme has not consistently replicated

Recent latent-profile-analysis (LPA) and factor-mixture-modelling (FMM) work has tested the four-class structure and largely failed to find it.

- **Schaeuffele, Schulz, Knaevelsrud, Renneberg, Boettcher (2021)** ([Psychiatry Research, PubMed 33667948](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33667948/)). LPA on the BVAQ in N=612 students. Solutions corresponded to "individuals with various degrees of alexithymia 'severity'" rather than supporting the four BVAQ-derived subtypes. The authors explicitly questioned the implementation of subtype frameworks in research.
- **Hogrefe FMM 2025** (European Journal of Psychological Assessment 41(1), [Hogrefe DOI 10.1027/1015-5759/a000828](https://econtent.hogrefe.com/doi/10.1027/1015-5759/a000828)). Factor mixture modelling in a Chinese student sample. Found a TWO-class solution: High-EOT alexithymia (18.2%) and Non-alexithymia (81.8%). NOT a Chinese-vs-German comparison and NOT a three-class finding — a binary class structure in a single Chinese sample. The substantive conclusion (FMM doesn't support the Chen four-class typology) is correct.
- **2025 Chinese nursing students LPA** ([BMC Medical Education 2025](https://bmcmededuc.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12909-025-08019-4)). Three categories (not four), distinguished primarily by severity. The "high-risk group" showed greatest mental health symptoms.
- **2025 elderly LPA** ([Frontiers Psychology 2025](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2025.1701168/full)). Three-profile solution: Pervasive Alexithymia (21.15%), Adaptive (72.81%), Affective-Cognitive Dissociation (6.04%). The largest profile is the non-alexithymic group.

### The reframe

The current best evidence supports a **severity continuum** along the TAS-20 facets and BVAQ cognitive/affective dimensions, with **two or three latent classes typically emerging** when LPA is run rigorously, and those classes typically distinguished primarily by severity rather than by qualitatively different profiles.

The Chen four-class labels remain useful as **descriptive language** people sometimes find helpful for self-recognition. But they should not be treated as validated discrete entities. The user's self-classification project is better served by "where on each axis am I?" than by "which of four boxes fits me?".

A common pattern across the LPA studies: the smallest cluster usually carries an "affective-cognitive dissociation" pattern that maps onto Bermond's Type II — the cognitive-alexithymic without the affective component. This pattern is recognizable but small (often ~5-10% of samples) and is best understood as a secondary structure within a primarily severity-organized population.

## Axis 5: State vs Trait

### Trait status — the long-follow-up evidence

Alexithymia behaves as a trait in the technical sense: relative ranking is preserved over long intervals.

- **Tolmunen et al. 2011** ([PubMed 21081227](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21081227/)) and follow-up **Tolmunen et al. 2017** ([PubMed 28314554](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28314554/)). The Finnish Health 2000/2011 follow-up. N=3,083 subjects assessed in 2000 and 2011. Mean TAS-20 was approximately 44.2 in both 2000 and 2011 — no group-level drift. Relative-rank stability across 11 years was high, consistent with trait status.
- The Danish twin study (N=8,785) shows about 30% of TAS-20 variance is heritable, consistent with a partly constitutional trait.

### State component — what fluctuates

Despite the trait core, alexithymia scores fluctuate with:

- **Depression severity**: TAS-20 scores rise during depressive episodes and partially remit when depression remits. Some of the apparent "alexithymia" in depressed populations is state-driven and resolves with successful depression treatment.
- **Acute distress**: Trauma exposure, acute stress, and dissociative episodes can produce transient elevations.
- **Therapy response**: Inpatient psychotherapy and structured outpatient interventions can produce measurable change. The Cameron et al. 2024 systematic review ([PMC11673933](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11673933/)) reports medium effect sizes on TAS-20 across 21 RCT arms (n=1,251). The state-changeable component is real and clinically actionable.
- **Chronic illness**: Many medical conditions produce measurable alexithymia elevations that may partially remit with primary-condition treatment, suggesting state-like overlay.

### Implications

The state-vs-trait split has practical consequences for self-classification. A first TAS-20 score during a depressive episode or trauma flare-up is not a clean baseline. The trait-component reading requires either (a) testing during a stable mood / non-acute state, or (b) re-testing across different mood states to estimate the trait floor.

For people with autism + depression comorbidity (a common profile), the trait alexithymia from autism and the state elevation from depression compound. Disentangling them requires either depression remission first, or careful interpretation of which TAS-20 items move with mood and which don't.

## Axis 6: The interoceptive split — detection, parsing, hypervigilance

This is the user's central concern and the most consequential axis for therapy choice. It needs careful, even-handed framing with corrected attribution.

### The three positions

Three positions appear in the literature on interoceptive function in alexithymia:

**Position A: Detection-deficit**. Alexithymics fail to detect interoceptive signals at all — body states are not adequately registered, and emotions therefore have nothing to "be made of." Bird, Brewer, and colleagues are the lineage most associated with this position, though their actual claim is more nuanced (see below).

**Position B: Parsing-deficit**. Alexithymics detect signals but cannot interpret/categorize them as emotions. They feel something — heart racing, stomach tight, breath quickened — but do not parse the signal as "anxiety" vs. "anger" vs. "hunger" vs. "tired." The deficit is in labeling or conceptualizing rather than in raw detection.

**Position C: Hypervigilance-with-mislabeling**. Alexithymics over-attend to body signals but interpret them somatically/catastrophically rather than as emotions. The signal is over-detected and mis-categorized. This profile overlaps heavily with somatic symptom disorder, anxiety, chronic pain, and parts of the FND population.

### Corrected attribution — what the Bird/Murphy/Brewer lineage actually argues

The parent exploration's "Bird walked back the interoception-deficit hypothesis" framing is incorrect. Several key citations need careful attribution.

**Brewer, Cook, Bird (2016)** "Alexithymia: a general deficit of interoception" ([Royal Society Open Science, PubMed 27853532](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27853532/)). Foundational paper. N=208. Self-reported alexithymia associated with poor non-affective interoception and increased perceived similarity between affective and non-affective body states.

**Murphy, Brewer, Catmur, Bird (2018)** "Alexithymia is associated with a multidomain, multidimensional failure of interoception: Evidence from novel tests" ([J Exp Psychol Gen, PMC5824617](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5824617/)). Three experiments — respiration, muscular effort, taste sensitivity. High alexithymia → reduced interoceptive accuracy and reduced use of interoceptive cues across all three domains, after controlling for autism, depression, anxiety. This is a *strengthening*, not a walk-back.

**Murphy, Brewer, Hobson, Catmur, Bird (2018)** "Is alexithymia characterised by impaired interoception? Further evidence, the importance of control variables, and the problems with the Heartbeat Counting Task" ([Biological Psychology, PubMed 29803614](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29803614/)). N=287. After controlling for confounds (age, gender, time-estimation, BMI, knowledge of HR, HRV, depression, anxiety, BP, resting HR), the predicted negative relationship between alexithymia and HCT performance emerged. The HCT is methodologically problematic, but the relationship is real once confounds are handled.

**Nicholson, Williams, Grainger, Christensen, Calvo-Merino, Gaigg (2018)** "Interoceptive impairments do not lie at the heart of autism or alexithymia" ([J Abnormal Psychology, PMC6089261](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6089261/)). **CRITICAL ATTRIBUTION**: this is **NOT** a Murphy/Catmur/Bird paper. It is from an INDEPENDENT group (City University London / UCL). Bayesian analyses supported the null hypothesis — neither autism nor alexithymia was associated with HCT performance. This makes the field debate *more* contested than an internal-walk-back framing would suggest, because the no-association position is held by an independent third party.

**Hobson, Brewer, Catmur, Bird (2019)** "The Role of Language in Alexithymia: Moving Towards a Multiroute Model" ([Emotion Review, SAGE](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1754073919838528)). The Bird group's most recent synthesis. Alexithymia is the **common downstream phenotype of multiple upstream impairments**: interoception is one route, language/labelling is another, emotion-concept conceptualization may be a third. This is a clarification, not an abandonment, of the interoception-route thesis.

**Brewer, Murphy, Bird (2021)** "Atypical interoception as a common risk factor for psychopathology: A review" ([Neurosci Biobehav Rev, PMC8522807](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8522807/)). Authoritative current synthesis. Direct quote: "many individuals with alexithymia experience a generalised interoceptive impairment, rather than one specific to emotional processing." Impairment hypothesized at multiple representational levels — conscious detection, recognition/labelling, or both — and individuals may differ in which level is affected. Multiroute, multilevel.

### The parsing-deficit reading — corrected source

The parsing-deficit reading is defensible but its source is not "Bird walking back." It comes from:

**Garfinkel, Seth, Barrett, Suzuki, Critchley (2015)** "Knowing your own heart" ([Biological Psychology, PMID 25451381](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25451381/)). Three-dimensional model of interoception:
- **Interoceptive accuracy (IA)**: objective performance on interoception tasks (e.g., heartbeat detection).
- **Interoceptive sensibility (IS)**: self-reported tendency to attend to body signals.
- **Interoceptive awareness (IAw)**: metacognitive correspondence between accuracy and sensibility — the gap between what you think you can detect and what you can actually detect.
These three dimensions are dissociable. A person can have high IS (lots of body attention) but low IA (poor metacognition about what the signals mean) — exactly the hypervigilance-with-mislabeling profile.

**Aaron, Blain, Snodgress, Park (2020)** "Quadratic Relationship Between Alexithymia and Interoceptive Accuracy" ([Frontiers in Psychiatry 11:132, PMC7076086](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7076086/)). **CRITICAL ATTRIBUTION**: this is **NOT** a Trevisan/Mehling/McPartland paper. The actual authors are Aaron, Blain, Snodgress, Park. The U-shaped finding: alexithymia and interoceptive accuracy show a quadratic relationship — both low *and* high IA can co-occur with high alexithymia. The deficit is in interpretation/labeling that can occur with either weak or hypervigilant body-signal detection.

### The multiroute synthesis (Hobson et al. 2019) — most defensible current frame

Putting these together, the most defensible synthesis is:

> Alexithymia is the common downstream phenotype of multiple possible upstream impairments. **Interoception is one route** — the Bird/Murphy/Brewer lineage continues to argue this with substantial evidence (Brewer 2016, Murphy 2018ab, Brewer 2021). **Language/labelling is another route** — Hobson 2019 makes the case that some alexithymics have intact body-signal detection but impoverished emotion-concept vocabulary, so the label-mapping fails. **Emotion-concept conceptualization** (in Barrett's constructed-emotion frame) is a possible third route. Different alexithymics can be on different routes, and the deepest deficit in some sub-profiles may include detection while in others it is only labeling.

For the deep-dive page, this multiroute frame is the most useful clinical organization. It avoids forcing a choice between Bird and Garfinkel positions (they are now compatible), it makes therapy-matching tractable (the route conditions which intervention class is appropriate), and it is honest about the heterogeneity of mechanisms within the alexithymic phenotype.

### Independent-group challenge — Nicholson 2018

The Nicholson et al. 2018 paper deserves explicit acknowledgement. Their Bayesian analyses supporting the null hypothesis (no association between alexithymia OR autism and HCT performance) is a real third-party challenge to the Bird/Murphy/Brewer position. The Bird group's response is that the HCT is a poor instrument and effects emerge with proper confound control; the Nicholson group's reply is that effects vanish under Bayesian-null testing. The field has not resolved this debate. The user should know that this is not Bird's internal walk-back but an active multi-group debate with independent challengers.

### What this means for therapy choice

Each route implies a different default intervention:

- **Interoception-route alexithymia**: interoception-focused interventions (MABT, body-scan with appropriate scaffolding) are most theoretically motivated. ADIE-style interoceptive accuracy training has shown some effect (statistically significant but clinically modest, did not meet predefined clinical-meaningfulness threshold; see Quadt et al. 2021).
- **Language-route alexithymia**: emotional granularity training (Barrett), affect-labelling (Lieberman 2007), structured journaling, vocabulary scaffolding. These work *external-to-internal*: they build the concept first and then map back to inner state.
- **Hypervigilance-with-mislabeling**: interoception-first work is contraindicated; the body-signal channel is over-attended already. Defusion-based approaches (ACT) and reattribution work (CBT, somatic-symptom-focused therapy) are more theoretically motivated.

The empirical support for these therapy-route mappings is theoretical, not RCT-tested. No published trial has stratified outcomes by alexithymia interoception subtype. The direction is plausible; the evidence is extrapolated.

## Axis 7: Cognitive vs Affective (BVAQ) — the most replicated structural finding

This axis partially overlaps with Axis 2 (Bermond Type I/II) but is worth pulling out separately because the dimensional structure is what's robust, not the categorical typology.

### What replicates

The two-dimensional cognitive-affective structure of the BVAQ is the most replicated structural finding in the alexithymia measurement literature. Across multiple languages and cultures:

- **Cognitive dimension** (identifying, verbalizing, analyzing): the same content the TAS-20 captures, measured with overlapping but distinct items.
- **Affective dimension** (fantasizing, emotionalizing): emotion-experiencing intensity and imaginative life. NOT measured by TAS-20.

A person can score normally on the cognitive dimension and low on the affective dimension (cognitively articulate but emotionally flat — this is closer to Bermond Type III), or vice versa (Type II — feelings present but illegible). The dimensions can dissociate.

### What this means for measurement

- The TAS-20 alone gives a partial picture, focused on cognitive alexithymia. Someone with low affective alexithymia (rich emotional life, vivid fantasy) but high cognitive alexithymia (cannot identify or describe) will score high on TAS-20 and look "alexithymic" even though half the construct is intact.
- Conversely, someone with low cognitive alexithymia (can identify emotions) but high affective alexithymia (reduced emotional intensity) may score normally on TAS-20 yet have a real alexithymic phenotype on the affective half.
- For triangulation: pair TAS-20 with BVAQ to get both halves, or pair TAS-20 with Perth Alexithymia Questionnaire (PAQ; Preece 2018) which covers positive emotions and the appraisal model.

This is the topic of Category 3 (Measurement); here the point is that the cognitive-affective dimensional structure is the spine of why measurement matters — different instruments capture different halves of the construct.

## Where the axes overlap — and where they don't

The seven axes are not orthogonal. They overlap in patterned ways:

| Axes that tend to align | Why |
|----|----|
| Axis 1 high DIF/DDF + Axis 2 high cognitive | TAS-20 mainly measures cognitive alexithymia |
| Axis 3 organic + Axis 6 detection-route | Anterior insula lesions impair interoception in detection-route way |
| Axis 3 secondary (trauma) + Axis 5 state component | Trauma-derived alexithymia is more state-changeable |
| Axis 4 high-cognitive cluster + Axis 2 Type II | Both describe "feelings present but illegible" |
| Axis 6 detection-route + Axis 2 Type I | "Affectless" presentation overlaps with detection failure |

| Axes that often dissociate | Why |
|----|----|
| Axis 1 EOT vs DIF/DDF | EOT is a cognitive-style measure that doesn't always track emotion-access |
| Axis 5 trait vs Axis 5 state | Both can be present; trait alexithymia + acute state distress = compound |
| Axis 6 hypervigilance + Axis 2 Type I | Hypervigilance is high-affective, not low |
| Axis 3 primary + Axis 6 hypervigilance | Constitutional alexithymia + somatic-symptom amplification is a distinct profile |

The implication: the same person can be at different positions on different axes. A common autistic-adult profile is primary (Axis 3) + high cognitive low affective (Axis 2 Type II) + high DIF / high DDF / variable EOT (Axis 1) + detection-or-language-route (Axis 6) + relatively trait (Axis 5) + somewhat dissociated (Axis 7 cognitive high, affective intact). A common trauma-derived profile is secondary (Axis 3) + variable cognitive-affective (Axis 2) + state component prominent (Axis 5) + hypervigilance-route (Axis 6 — body signals over-attended).

The axes give a navigation toolkit, not a diagnostic algorithm. They tell you what to attend to, not what category to assign.

## The decision tree — self-navigation questions

The user has explicitly requested a decision-tree element to help them locate themselves on the axes without a categorical diagnosis. Here are the questions the tree should ask, organized by axis:

### Detection question
"When you check in with your body — heart rate, stomach, muscle tension, breath — can you reliably register what's happening? Or do you find that body signals seem absent or unclear?"
- **Reliably register**: rules out detection-route deficit; pushes toward parsing or language route.
- **Signals absent/unclear**: detection-route concern; gentler interoception scaffolding warranted.

### Parsing question
"When you DO detect body signals, can you tell whether they're emotional (anxiety, anger, sadness) vs. non-emotional (hunger, fatigue, illness)?"
- **Yes**: parsing intact; alexithymia probably language-route or affective.
- **No, they all feel similar**: parsing-route deficit; granularity/labeling work indicated; standard mindfulness contraindicated for the hypervigilance subset.

### Presence vs absence question
"Do you have feelings that are 'there but illegible' — you sense something is happening internally but can't name it? Or do you experience an absence of feeling — not 'I can't name it' but 'there's nothing to name'?"
- **There but illegible**: closer to Type II. Affective dimension intact, cognitive impaired. Granularity-building / labeling work most appropriate.
- **Absence**: closer to Type I. Affective dimension impaired. Different therapeutic landscape — psychoeducation, scaffolded affect-induction may be appropriate; pure introspection may not yield much.

### Context dependence question
"Are your feelings absent or unclear in some contexts but present and clear in others?"
- **Yes, context-dependent**: state component is significant; not pure trait alexithymia. Investigate which contexts produce access.
- **No, consistent across contexts**: trait component dominant.

### Etiology question
"Has this been the case as long as you can remember? Or did it emerge after a specific event, illness, or period of prolonged stress?"
- **Lifelong**: primary alexithymia; constitutional component likely.
- **Post-event**: secondary alexithymia; more state-changeable; trauma-informed work often appropriate.
- **Post-stroke / brain injury**: organic alexithymia; neurorehabilitation framework, not insight-oriented therapy.

### Hypervigilance question
"Are you hypervigilant about body signals AND prone to interpreting them somatically (medical concerns, physical symptoms) rather than emotionally? Have you been told you have somatic symptom disorder, fibromyalgia, IBS, or chronic pain?"
- **Yes**: hypervigilance-with-mislabeling profile. Interoception-first work specifically contraindicated. Defusion / reattribution work indicated.
- **No**: rule out this complication.

### Emotional intensity question
"Setting aside whether you can identify them, how intense are your emotions when they happen? Do you experience strong reactions, vivid fantasy life, dreams with emotional content? Or is your inner emotional life relatively flat?"
- **Intense / vivid**: affective dimension intact; cognitive alexithymia (Axis 2 cognitive-high) is the locus.
- **Flat / muted**: affective dimension impaired; closer to Type I.

The tree does not diagnose. It identifies which axes are most relevant for the individual to attend to, which then conditions therapy choice and self-directed work.

## Caveats — the limits of self-classification

- **The recursion problem**. People with alexithymia are by definition less able to introspect on emotional/internal states. Self-report on emotion-introspection has known reliability limits in this population (Lundh 2001 critique). The decision-tree answers should be triangulated with: behavioral evidence, others' reports, and observable patterns over time.
- **State contamination**. Acute distress, depression, or trauma flare-ups can distort answers. The tree is most reliable when answered during a relatively stable state.
- **Comorbidity confounds**. Autism, ADHD, complex trauma, somatic symptom disorder, depression — each shapes how the questions land. The same answer can mean different things in different comorbidity contexts.
- **Self-classification is not diagnosis**. The tree helps the user know which axes to attend to and which interventions are theoretically appropriate. It does not substitute for clinical assessment using validated instruments, especially for therapy decisions.
- **Categorical thinking creep**. The whole point of the dimensional reframe is that boxes mislead. Even after using the tree, the user should hold their location on each axis as continuous and probabilistic, not categorical. "Mostly toward Type II with some hypervigilance overlay" is more accurate than "I am Type II."
