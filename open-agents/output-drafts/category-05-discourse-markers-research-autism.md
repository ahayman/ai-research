# Discourse Markers and Autism: Research Synthesis

## Purpose
Research synthesis for a knowledge base teaching English pragmatics to autistic individuals. This document covers the intersection of discourse markers and autism spectrum conditions, drawing on empirical research, computational linguistics, conversation analysis, and the neurodiversity paradigm.

---

## 1. Autistic Production of Discourse Markers

### 1.1 The General Finding: Reduced but Nuanced

The overall pattern across multiple studies is that autistic individuals use discourse markers less frequently than non-autistic peers. However, this finding is far more nuanced than a simple deficit account would suggest. The reduction is not uniform across all markers -- some are significantly reduced, others are preserved, and the reasons for reduction appear to be specifically tied to the pragmatic (listener-oriented) functions of particular markers rather than a blanket inability to produce them.

**Key studies establishing the general pattern:**

- Research consistently shows autistic speakers produce fewer discourse markers overall in semi-structured interviews and naturalistic conversation (Jones, Zane, & Grossman, 2022; multiple studies reviewed in Sng, Carter, & Stephenson, 2018).
- However, the pattern is marker-specific rather than categorical.

### 1.2 The "Um" vs. "Uh" Distinction: A Critical Finding

One of the most robust and theoretically important findings in this area is the differential use of the fillers "um" and "uh" by autistic speakers.

**Gorman et al. (2016) -- "Uh and Um in Children With Autism Spectrum Disorders or Language Impairment":**
- Children with ASD (ages 4-8) were significantly less likely to use "um" than typically developing peers.
- Their use of "uh" was unaffected -- no significant difference from controls.
- This dissociation is critical because it maps onto the different pragmatic functions of these fillers.

**Lake et al. (2011) -- "Uh, Um, and Autism: Filler Disfluencies as Pragmatic Markers":**
- Adolescents with ASD produced "um" less frequently than both typically developing peers and peers with "optimal outcomes" (those who had lost their ASD diagnosis).
- "Um" rate was associated with autism symptom severity.
- Crucially, "um" rate was NOT associated with executive function or language abilities.
- This rules out the explanation that reduced "um" use reflects general cognitive or linguistic difficulty.

**The theoretical explanation:**
- "Uh" is primarily speaker-oriented -- it signals that the speaker is experiencing a minor delay in speech planning. It is essentially self-directed.
- "Um" is primarily listener-oriented -- it signals to the listener that a major delay is coming, effectively saying "I'm still here, I'm still talking, please wait." It manages the listener's expectations and maintains the conversational floor cooperatively.
- The selective reduction of "um" (but not "uh") in autistic speakers is consistent with broader pragmatic differences: "um" serves a social, listener-oriented function that requires modeling the listener's mental state and communicative needs.

**Clinical significance:**
- "Um" usage has been proposed as an easily quantifiable behavioral marker that, in concert with other observations, may help distinguish ASD from language impairment (where both "um" and "uh" may be affected differently).
- The "um" fillers have been shown to distinguish children with and without ASD with notable reliability (Gorman et al., 2018 brief report).

### 1.3 The Discourse Marker "Like": A Counterexample

**Jones, Zane, & Grossman (2022) -- "Like, It's Important":**
- Studied 21 autistic children (ages 10-17) and 20 non-autistic peers matched on age, sex, IQ, and language scores.
- Found NO significant difference in overall frequency of discourse marker "like" between groups.
- Found NO significant difference in the relative proportions of "like" subtypes between groups.
- This is a notable counterexample to the general finding of reduced discourse marker use.

**Why "like" may be preserved:**
"Like" serves four distinct functions:
1. **Important/new** -- signals that upcoming content is noteworthy
2. **Loose/approximate** -- signals that what follows is not exact ("like, five minutes")
3. **Reformulative** -- signals a rephrasing or clarification
4. **Quotative** -- introduces reported speech ("she was like, 'no way'")

The authors note that "like" may be preserved because:
- It is extremely frequent in peer speech, providing abundant input for acquisition
- Some of its functions (approximate, quotative) may be more formulaic/structural than interpersonally oriented
- It may have been partially acquired through repetition and pattern-matching rather than pragmatic inference
- The finding suggests that the reduced discourse marker use observed in earlier research "does not persist to all such markers"

### 1.4 Other Markers: What We Know

**Markers typically reduced in autistic speech:**
- "Um" (robust finding across multiple studies)
- "Well" as a turn-management device
- "You know" (as a listener-engagement marker)
- Topic-shift markers ("by the way," "anyway," "so")

**Markers that may be more preserved:**
- "Like" (Jones et al., 2022)
- "Uh" (Gorman et al., 2016)
- Structural connectives ("and," "but," "because") -- though their use in narrative may differ qualitatively

**Markers needing more research:**
- "I mean" (reformulative marker)
- "Actually" (expectation-contrast marker)
- "Oh" (change-of-state marker)
- "Right" / "okay" (acknowledgment markers)

### 1.5 Placement and Positioning Differences

Research on topic management reveals that even when autistic speakers do use discourse markers or transition devices, there may be differences in positioning:

**Abalos et al. (Autism Research, 2025) -- "Conversational Topic Shifts and Topic Maintenance":**
- Autistic children produce more topic shifts than neurotypical peers.
- These shifts are described as "abrupt," "unannounced," "sudden," or "proximally relevant."
- Inappropriate topic shifts are characterized by being abrupt, occurring without pause time, or when the previous topic has not been discussed at length.
- Appropriate topic shifts include an introducer (e.g., "by the way") alerting the listener to a topic change, occur after a 3-second pause, or when the previous topic has been discussed at length.
- Neurotypical children produced a significantly higher proportion of appropriate topic shifts than autistic peers.

This suggests that even when discourse is organized logically, the signaling of transitions may be absent or attenuated.

### 1.6 Computational Linguistic Analysis

**NLP approaches to autism discourse:**
Recent advances in computational linguistics have enabled more objective, quantitative analysis of autistic speech patterns:

- Automated analysis reveals differences in conjunctions and other discourse features that distinguish autistic from non-autistic speech (multiple studies using NLP methods).
- A computational study of pragmatic discourse features in adults with ASD investigated politeness, uncertainty, and informativeness in collaborative conversations, finding distinctive pragmatic profiles. Neural models trained with BERT embeddings could predict these features with F1 scores exceeding 0.82 (Lake et al., ACL 2021).
- Machine learning approaches using linguistic features like Mean Length of Utterance (MLU) and Mean Length of Turn Ratio have been applied to autism speech classification.

**Zane & Grossman (2024) -- "Normal but Different":**
- Autistic adolescents who score within normal ranges on standardized language tests still produce frequent "linguistic irregularities" in spontaneous discourse.
- This highlights that standardized testing misses the pragmatic-discourse level where autistic language use most consistently differs.
- The irregularities are described as "idiosyncratic" but not ungrammatical -- suggesting that discourse-level organization, not grammar, is the key area of difference.

## 2. Autistic Comprehension of Discourse Markers

### 2.1 Processing Pragmatic Signals

Research on how autistic listeners process discourse markers is less extensive than production research, but available evidence suggests important differences:

**Scalar implicature processing:**
- Studies on scalar implicatures (where "some" implies "not all") show mixed results. Adults with ASD sometimes perform similarly to controls on these inferences, but the underlying processing mechanisms may differ.
- A 2025 study found that autistic children showed "poorer scalar implicature but similar metaphor comprehension" -- a reverse contrast from what was expected. The authors proposed that a dimension "opposing guided to spontaneous pragmatic processes" may explain when autistic individuals struggle vs. succeed with pragmatic inferences.
- Some adolescents with ASD computed implicatures normally but may not rely on the same epistemic reasoning processes as non-autistic peers.

**Discourse context use:**
- Recent research (2025) found that autistic adults CAN use discourse context to determine a speaker's intention to request -- for example, recognizing that "I'm thirsty" in certain contexts is an indirect request rather than a literal statement.
- Performance was correlated with emotion-identification task scores, suggesting that pragmatic inference ability may vary within the autistic population.

### 2.2 The "Well" Problem: Dispreferred Response Signals

"Well" is one of the most pragmatically loaded discourse markers in English. In conversation analysis, "well" at the beginning of a response is a strong signal that a dispreferred response is coming -- that is, the speaker is about to disagree, decline, or deliver unwelcome news.

**How "well" functions:**
- If a response begins with an in-breath, hesitation particle, or "well," the chances are the response will be dispreferred (conversation analysis research).
- "Well" in responses to questions can signal departures ranging from dispreference or non-straightforwardness, through resistance regarding the relevance of a question, to steering away from what preceded.
- These signals allow listeners to recognize quickly the type of response being produced and plan their next turn.

**The problem for autistic listeners:**
While no study has specifically isolated autistic processing of "well" as a dispreferred signal, the broader pragmatic inference literature suggests this is a likely area of difficulty:
- Processing "well" as a pragmatic signal requires rapid, implicit inference about the speaker's communicative intent -- precisely the kind of "spontaneous pragmatic processing" that research suggests may be more challenging for autistic individuals.
- If an autistic listener does not register "well" as a warning signal, they may be unprepared for the disagreement or refusal that follows, experiencing it as more abrupt or unexpected than a non-autistic listener would.
- This connects to broader research on autistic difficulties with recognizing indirect speech acts and hedging cues.

### 2.3 The Filtering Problem: "Meaningless" Words That Carry Meaning

A critical insight for autistic individuals learning pragmatics:

**The pattern:**
- Many autistic people report filtering out words perceived as "meaningless" or "noise" in conversation.
- Discourse markers are prime candidates for this filtering because they carry no propositional content -- "well," "so," "you know," "I mean," "actually" do not add to the factual content of a sentence.
- From a logical/propositional perspective, these words ARE redundant. An autistic person processing language primarily for informational content may rationally deprioritize them.

**The cost of filtering:**
However, these "meaningless" words are doing critical pragmatic work:
- **Turn-management signals**: "So" at the start of a turn signals topic initiation or return. "Anyway" signals topic closure. Missing these means missing the conversation's structural scaffolding.
- **Topic-shift cues**: "By the way," "oh, that reminds me," "speaking of which" -- these prepare the listener for a change. Without them, topic changes feel sudden and disorienting.
- **Hedging and face-saving cues**: "Well," "kind of," "sort of," "I guess" signal that the speaker is softening their message. Missing these means missing the emotional temperature of the conversation.
- **Stance markers**: "Actually" signals that what follows contradicts an expectation. "Obviously" signals shared knowledge. These frame how content should be interpreted.
- **Engagement markers**: "You know," "right?" -- these check listener engagement and maintain connection. Missing these means missing bids for conversational participation.

### 2.4 Prosody and Information Structure

Discourse markers interact with prosodic cues that also pose challenges:

- Prosody marks information structure by highlighting new vs. given information (Grice, 2023 review of 51 studies).
- Autistic individuals show tentative evidence for greater differences in the perception of intuitive prosodic functions.
- Research has linked perceptual inflexibility -- reduced ability to adapt to variations in speech -- to autism.
- This means that even when discourse markers are present, if they are delivered with atypical prosody, their pragmatic force may not register.

## 3. The "Filler" Misconception and Autism

### 3.1 Why "Filler" Is a Misleading Term

The very word "filler" implies something that fills space without contributing content. This framing is actively harmful for autistic individuals trying to understand conversation, because it validates the impulse to ignore these words.

**What "fillers" actually do:**
- **"Um"**: Signals to the listener that a significant pause is coming and the speaker is maintaining the floor. This is a cooperative, listener-oriented act.
- **"Uh"**: Signals brief speech-planning difficulty. More speaker-oriented.
- **"Like"**: Signals approximation, emphasis, reformulation, or quotation. Rich pragmatic content.
- **"Well"**: Signals non-straightforward response. Critical for anticipating disagreement.
- **"So"**: Signals causal connection, summary, or topic transition. Structural scaffolding.
- **"You know"**: Signals shared knowledge, seeks acknowledgment, or maintains engagement.
- **"I mean"**: Signals self-repair or clarification. Tells the listener to update their understanding.
- **"Actually"**: Signals contrast with expectation. Reframes preceding discourse.

### 3.2 The Autistic Experience of "Meaningless" Words

From autistic self-reports and community discussions:
- Text-based communication is widely preferred by autistic individuals precisely because it strips away many of the real-time processing demands of spoken discourse, including rapid interpretation of discourse markers, prosodic cues, and turn-taking signals.
- Written internet-mediated communication provides "more control, thinking time, clarity and fewer sensory issues and streams of information that must be processed and interpreted."
- This preference itself demonstrates that the processing load of real-time discourse -- of which markers are a significant component -- is genuinely taxing.

### 3.3 The Cost of Missing Turn-Management Signals

When discourse markers that manage turn-taking are filtered out or not processed:
- The autistic listener may not recognize when someone is about to yield the floor (missing "so, yeah" as a turn-completion signal).
- They may not recognize when someone is holding the floor through a pause (missing "um" as a floor-holding signal).
- They may not recognize when a topic is being closed ("anyway," "so yeah") vs. extended.
- The result is conversational desynchronization: overlapping speech, missed turns, or awkward silences that both parties experience as uncomfortable.

## 4. Discourse Coherence in Autistic Narrative

### 4.1 Narrative Coherence Research

Extensive research has documented differences in narrative coherence for autistic individuals:

**Key findings across studies:**
- Autistic adults tend to focus more on non-essential details while narrating events.
- They may inaccurately use transition words.
- They tend to make less use of cohesive devices overall.
- ASD participants appear less capable of maintaining global coherence in their narratives relative to neurotypical peers.

**Specific differences in narrative skill:**
- Reduced mention of characters' internal states (emotions, thoughts, motivations).
- Omission of key story elements (setting, central conflict, resolution).
- Inclusion of tangential story details that detract from overarching plot and themes.
- Limited use of causal language to connect and interpret the meaning of narrated experiences.

### 4.2 Connective Use: "And," "But," "So," "Because"

**Causal and temporal connectives:**
- Narrative coherence is partly measured by the frequency of causal and temporal connectives (e.g., "because," "so," "and then," "after that").
- Research on causal connectives in autistic children's narratives (Sah & Torng, 2015; Spanish-speaking study, Cambridge Core, 2024) shows that autistic children produce fewer causal links between story events.
- The use of "and then" as a default connector (stringing events together temporally without indicating causal or logical relationships) is commonly observed.

**The "and then" pattern:**
- Many autistic narrators rely heavily on "and then" to connect events sequentially.
- This creates narratives that are chronologically ordered but lack the causal scaffolding ("because," "so," "therefore," "as a result") that helps listeners understand WHY events occurred.
- From a central coherence perspective, this pattern reflects a tendency to focus on sequential details without integrating them into a causal framework.

### 4.3 Central Coherence and Discourse Organization

**Theoretical framework:**
- Central coherence theory (Happe & Frith) proposes that autistic individuals tend toward local processing -- attending to details rather than integrating them into a global gestalt.
- At the discourse level, this maps onto: strong detail recall but weaker narrative structure; event-by-event reporting rather than thematic organization; difficulty maintaining the "thread" that connects individual utterances to the overall point.

**Executive function and narrative:**
- Theory of mind, executive functioning, and central coherence all relate to discourse-level language organization.
- Information needs to be held together and organized coherently around a topic.
- Children with ASD who have impaired executive function skills for temporal reasoning produce narratives with fewer new elements and causal links (Frontiers in Psychology, 2018).

### 4.4 Microstructure vs. Macrostructure

Research distinguishes between:
- **Microstructure**: sentence-level features (grammar, vocabulary, sentence complexity) -- often intact in autistic speakers.
- **Macrostructure**: discourse-level features (overall coherence, story grammar, thematic organization) -- more consistently different in autistic speakers.

This maps directly onto the discourse marker issue: discourse markers operate at the interface between microstructure and macrostructure, connecting individual utterances into larger discourse structures. Their reduced use contributes to the macrostructural differences observed in autistic narrative.

## 5. The Double Empathy Angle

### 5.1 How NT Listeners Perceive Autistic Speech

**The impression formation research:**

**Zane & Grossman (2018/2021) -- "Judgments of Spoken Discourse and Impression Formation":**
- Both ASD and neurotypical raters evaluated the speech of ASD speakers less favorably than NT speakers.
- Linguistic cues led to less favorable impressions of ASD speakers from BOTH NT and ASD raters.
- Only ratings of "awkwardness" were driven more by characteristics of autistic adults than characteristics of raters -- other negative impressions were influenced more by rater bias.
- Critically: negative impressions disappear when impressions are based on conversational content lacking audio-visual cues, suggesting that STYLE, not SUBSTANCE, drives negative impressions.

**Sasson et al. (2017) -- "Neurotypical Peers Are Less Willing to Interact":**
- NT peers form negative first impressions of autistic individuals within seconds ("thin slice judgments").
- These snap judgments are based on superficial features of communication style rather than content.

### 5.2 The Role of Missing Discourse Markers

The absence of discourse markers in autistic speech likely contributes to the perception of "awkwardness" or "abruptness" by NT listeners:

- Without hedging markers ("well," "kind of," "I guess"), statements sound more blunt and direct than intended.
- Without engagement markers ("you know," "right?"), speech sounds more monologic and less interactive.
- Without turn-management signals ("so," "anyway"), topic transitions seem abrupt and unannounced.
- Without floor-yielding signals, it may be unclear when the speaker has finished.
- Without softening markers before disagreement, disagreements feel more confrontational.

**The bidirectional nature of the problem:**
The double empathy problem (Milton, 2012) frames this as a mutual failure of understanding:
- The autistic speaker may not produce markers that NT listeners rely on for interpretation.
- The NT listener may not recognize that the absence of these markers is a difference in communication style rather than a signal of disinterest, rudeness, or social incompetence.
- Both parties contribute to the communication breakdown.

### 5.3 The "Autism Accent"

Research has identified what some call an "autism accent" -- a constellation of speech features including atypical prosody, rhythm, and intonation that can mark autistic speech as different:
- Differences in intonation, pronunciation, atonality, grammatical patterns, and irregular speaking rhythms.
- This "accent" interacts with discourse marker use: when discourse markers are present but delivered with atypical prosody, they may not function as intended; when they are absent, the prosodic differences become even more salient.

## 6. Autistic-Autistic Communication and Discourse Markers

### 6.1 The Crompton Telephone Game Study

**Crompton et al. (2020) -- "Autistic Peer-to-Peer Information Transfer Is Highly Effective":**
- Used a "telephone game" methodology with chains of 8 people.
- Three conditions: all-autistic chains, all-NT chains, and mixed chains.
- **Key finding**: Autistic people shared information with other autistic people as effectively as NT people shared with other NT people.
- Information sharing was significantly poorer in mixed-neurotype chains.
- Rapport was lower in mixed pairs than in matched-neurotype pairs.
- This strongly supports the double empathy interpretation: communication difficulty is not located within the autistic individual but emerges from the mismatch between communication styles.

### 6.2 Autistic Communication Norms

Within autistic communities, distinctive communication patterns have been documented:

**Infodumping:**
- Extended, detailed sharing about topics of passionate interest.
- Within autistic communities, this is celebrated as a sign of trust and authentic engagement, sometimes described as a "love language."
- In NT social contexts, it is often misinterpreted as one-sided or lacking awareness of the listener.

**Parallel play/parallel communication:**
- Being together without continuous verbal exchange.
- Comfort with silence that NT norms might interpret as disengagement.

**Direct communication style:**
- Preference for explicit, literal communication.
- Reduced use of hedging and face-saving markers because the community norm values clarity over social lubrication.

**Interest-based conversation:**
- Autistic individuals tend to value conversations centered on shared interests rather than social-bonding small talk.
- This means the function served by engagement markers ("you know," "right?") in NT conversation may be served differently -- through detailed knowledge-sharing rather than phatic communion.

### 6.3 Do Autistic Communities Develop Their Own Markers?

Formal research on distinctive autistic discourse markers is limited, but several observations emerge:

**In online communication:**
- Autistic adults show a strong preference for text-based communication, which provides more processing time and removes the real-time demand of interpreting paralinguistic cues.
- Online autistic communities have developed specific conventions: tone indicators (/s for sarcasm, /j for joke, /gen for genuine), explicit labeling of communicative intent, and detailed content warnings.
- These function as explicit discourse markers -- doing the same pragmatic work that "well," "you know," and prosodic cues do in NT spoken conversation, but through explicit labeling rather than implicit signaling.

**In spoken autistic-autistic conversation:**
- The Crompton (2020) finding of equal information transfer suggests that autistic dyads DO achieve conversational coordination, but potentially through different mechanisms than NT dyads.
- Research on autistic-autistic conversation specifically analyzing discourse marker use is a significant gap in the literature.
- Anecdotal and community reports suggest that autistic-autistic conversations may rely more on explicit verbal meta-communication ("I'm changing the topic now," "that reminds me of something different") rather than conventional discourse markers.

## 7. Practical Implications: Teaching Discourse Markers

### 7.1 Which Markers Give the Most "Bang for the Buck"?

Based on the research, the following discourse markers are prioritized by their likely impact on social communication success:

**Tier 1: Highest Impact (Learn First)**

| Marker | Function | Why It Matters |
|--------|----------|---------------|
| "Well..." | Signals non-straightforward response | Missing this means being blindsided by disagreements |
| "So" | Topic initiation, summary, causal connection | Central to conversation structure |
| "Um" | Floor-holding, signals major delay coming | Without it, pauses lead to interruption |
| "Actually" | Expectation contrast | Signals that correction/new information follows |
| "But" | Contrast/contradiction | Critical for following argument structure |

**Tier 2: High Impact**

| Marker | Function | Why It Matters |
|--------|----------|---------------|
| "You know" | Engagement check, shared knowledge marker | Signals bids for acknowledgment |
| "I mean" | Self-repair, clarification | Tells listener to update understanding |
| "Anyway" / "So anyway" | Topic return/closure | Signals conversation is returning to main topic or wrapping up |
| "Oh" | Change of state | Signals new realization or information processing |
| "Right" / "Okay" | Acknowledgment, transition | Signals receipt of information and readiness to move on |

**Tier 3: Important for Fluency**

| Marker | Function | Why It Matters |
|--------|----------|---------------|
| "Like" | Approximation, emphasis, quotation | Already often preserved in autistic speech |
| "By the way" | Topic introduction (tangential) | Explicitly signals departure from current topic |
| "Speaking of which" | Associative topic connection | Links new topic to what was just said |
| "To be honest" / "Honestly" | Stance marker | Signals upcoming candor or potentially face-threatening content |
| "Kind of" / "Sort of" | Hedging | Softens assertions, reduces social friction |

### 7.2 Why Explicit Teaching Works

Evidence-based approaches for autism pragmatics emphasize explicit instruction:

- Autistic individuals often learn pragmatic skills better through explicit rules than through implicit social learning (multiple intervention studies reviewed in PLOS One systematic review).
- Direct instruction on WHEN, HOW, and WHY to use specific communication features is more effective than naturalistic exposure alone.
- Video modeling and feedback-based interventions have shown gains in pragmatic communication skills for autistic adults.

**Applying this to discourse markers:**
- Rather than hoping discourse markers will be acquired through immersion (which may not work if the markers are being filtered out), explicit teaching of marker functions can provide the "decoder ring."
- Each marker can be taught as a rule: "When someone starts with 'well...' they are probably about to disagree or say something you don't expect."
- This approach leverages autistic strengths in rule-learning and pattern recognition.

### 7.3 Teaching Frameworks

**Production-focused teaching:**
- Provide explicit templates: "When you want to change the topic, say 'by the way' or 'that reminds me of' before introducing the new topic."
- Practice floor-holding: "If you need to think, say 'um' or 'let me think about that' rather than going silent."
- Practice topic transitions: "Before shifting, use a bridge: 'Speaking of X...' or 'Oh, that's interesting -- on a different note...'"

**Comprehension-focused teaching:**
- Provide a "decoder" for common markers:
  - "Well..." = "I'm about to disagree or say something complicated"
  - "So..." at the start = "I'm starting/returning to the main point"
  - "Actually..." = "What I'm about to say contradicts what you might think"
  - "You know?" = "Are you following me? Do you agree?"
  - "I mean..." = "Let me rephrase/clarify what I just said"
  - "Anyway..." = "Let's get back on track / I'm wrapping this up"

### 7.4 The Masking Consideration

An important caveat from the camouflaging research:

- Many autistic individuals DO learn to use discourse markers as part of "camouflaging" or "masking" -- adopting neurotypical communication patterns through conscious effort and scripting.
- Compensation involves "developing strategies to overcome social and communication difficulties, such as using learned phrases or scripts in social situations."
- While this can improve social communication success, the persistent effort of monitoring and adjusting behavior leads to exhaustion, anxiety, and depression.
- A pragmatics knowledge base should present discourse markers as TOOLS for understanding and being understood, not as masks to wear. The goal is comprehension and strategic use, not constant performance.

## 8. Narrative Coherence and the Discourse Marker Connection

### 8.1 The Assessment Framework

A comprehensive framework for assessing narrative coherence (from clinical research) includes:
1. **Context**: Is the setting established?
2. **Chronology**: Are events in temporal order?
3. **Causality**: Are causal relationships made explicit?
4. **Congruence**: Does the narrative stay on topic?
5. **Characters**: Are characters' mental states referenced?
6. **Cohesion**: Are cohesive devices used effectively?

Discourse markers contribute directly to elements 2-4 and 6:
- Temporal markers ("then," "after that," "meanwhile") support chronology.
- Causal markers ("because," "so," "therefore," "as a result") support causality.
- Topic markers ("anyway," "as I was saying," "the point is") support congruence.
- Referential markers ("this," "that," "which") support cohesion.

### 8.2 The Connection to Central Coherence

The central coherence account of autism suggests that the discourse marker gap is not random -- it reflects a genuine cognitive difference in how information is organized:
- If processing favors local detail over global structure, the connective tissue between ideas (discourse markers) may be deprioritized.
- This is not a "deficit" in the ability to use these markers, but a difference in the salience of discourse structure.
- Explicit teaching can provide the structural scaffolding that may not emerge naturally from implicit processing.

## 9. The Broad Autism Phenotype Connection

### 9.1 Pragmatic Language in Family Members

Research on the Broad Autism Phenotype (BAP) has found:
- Pragmatic language differences -- including discourse organization -- are present in non-autistic relatives of autistic individuals, particularly in families with multiple autistic members.
- Siblings of autistic children show lower pragmatic abilities in subgroups identified with BAP-related difficulties.
- This suggests that the discourse marker patterns observed in autism may represent the extreme end of a continuous distribution that runs through families.

### 9.2 Implications for the Knowledge Base

The BAP research suggests:
- Discourse marker processing differences exist on a spectrum, not as a binary.
- Some non-autistic individuals also struggle with these features, though to a lesser degree.
- Teaching discourse marker functions explicitly has potential value beyond the autistic population.

## 10. Research Gaps and Limitations

### 10.1 What We Don't Know Yet

1. **Comprehension studies are lacking**: Most research focuses on production of discourse markers. We need more studies specifically testing autistic comprehension/processing of markers like "well," "so," "actually" in real-time conversation.

2. **Adult autistic speakers**: Much research is on children. Adult autistic discourse marker use, particularly in those who have developed compensatory strategies, is under-studied.

3. **Autistic-autistic conversation**: The Crompton (2020) study examined information transfer but did not specifically analyze discourse marker use in autistic dyads. This is a critical gap.

4. **Marker-specific studies**: Beyond "um," "uh," and "like," there is very little research on autistic use of specific discourse markers ("well," "so," "actually," "I mean," "you know").

5. **Cross-cultural variation**: Almost all research is on English-speaking populations. Discourse marker systems vary across languages, and autistic pragmatic profiles may interact with language-specific marker systems differently.

6. **Online/text-based discourse markers**: While we know autistic individuals prefer text communication, there is little research on how discourse markers function (or are replaced) in autistic text-based communication.

7. **The role of alexithymia**: Alexithymia (difficulty identifying and describing emotions) co-occurs frequently with autism and may independently affect the use of stance and hedging markers.

### 10.2 Methodological Considerations

- Much early research used deficit-focused framing that may have biased analysis.
- The double empathy framework suggests that communication difficulties observed in mixed interactions may look different in matched-neurotype interactions.
- Self-report data from autistic adults provides important but under-utilized perspective on discourse marker processing.
