## 1. Why This Page Exists

Documentation preserves what you observed against later rewriting. But documentation lives in files and notebooks — it is only as useful as your ability to access it and believe it when you read it back. Under sustained gaslighting, people can reach a point where they read their own records and think *maybe I wrote that wrong, maybe I misremembered even then*. The written record is no longer enough, because the cognitive ground it stands on is being eroded.

A **reality-check network** is the social infrastructure that supplements written documentation: people who know your situation, who believe your account, and who can tell you — when you need to hear it — *yes, what you are describing really happened, and it really is a problem*. They are the external validators when your internal compass is under attack.

For autistic readers specifically, the need for this network is acute and the mechanism is specific:

1. **The credibility discount** (Lim, Young & Brewer, 2022, PMC8813809) means that general social support is not enough. If the people you talk to about your situation are part of the general population that rates autistic accounts as less credible, they may provide warmth but not reality-check. You need people calibrated for your testimony.

2. **Alexithymia** makes internal calibration unreliable. If you cannot clearly identify what you are feeling or whether your reaction is "proportionate," you cannot use your own emotional response as a check on whether the situation you are describing is as bad as you think it is. External observers who know what they are looking for can see things you cannot see from inside.

3. **The isolation strategies** used by manipulators specifically target social networks. Stark (2007) identified isolation as one of the four core elements of coercive control. Pence & Paymar (1993) include it as a spoke of the Power and Control Wheel. If manipulation is happening to you, the people you would naturally turn to are often the people being cut off — that is a feature of how manipulation works, not an accident.

4. **Research on autistic peer networks** (Crompton et al., 2020, *Autism*) shows that autistic-to-autistic information transfer is dramatically more effective than autistic-to-neurotypical transfer. This finding has direct implications for reality-check networks: a small network of autistic peers can function better as a validator than a larger network of neurotypical people who require extensive translation of your experience.

A reality-check network is not "having friends." It is a specific social resource with a specific function. This page is about building and using one.

## 2. What a Reality-Check Network Is (and Isn't)

### What it is

- **A small group of trusted people** (usually 2–5 for most purposes; can be as small as one in constrained situations).
- **People who know enough of your situation** to recognize patterns when you describe incidents.
- **People who believe you by default** — not uncritically, but starting from the assumption that your account is probably accurate.
- **People who can hold discomfort without needing to fix it** — they can hear you describe a difficult situation without immediately pushing you toward any specific action.
- **People who will tell you what they observe** even when it is not what you want to hear — and especially when what you are telling them contradicts what you are telling yourself.
- **People who are reachable in the moments you actually need them** — not just theoretically available but practically accessible.

### What it isn't

- **It isn't a support group for processing feelings.** Support and reality-checking are different functions. Support says "that sounds really hard"; reality-checking says "yes, what you just described is the third time that exact thing has happened in two weeks, and that pattern matters." Both are useful; this page is specifically about the second.
- **It isn't a committee that makes decisions for you.** The reality-check network helps you see your situation clearly. What you do with that clarity is your decision, not theirs.
- **It isn't a jury that judges your case.** You are not trying to "prove" anything to them. You are asking them to help you see what is in front of you.
- **It isn't a replacement for professional help.** Therapists, advocates, and lawyers provide specialized knowledge that even trusted friends cannot. The reality-check network is a complement to professional resources, not a substitute.
- **It isn't universal.** Not everyone in your life belongs in your reality-check network. Most people should not. A small network of the right people is much more useful than a large network of mixed ones.
- **It isn't secret from the person causing harm as long as they are not the source of harm.** If your partner is lovely and your boss is a problem, your partner is probably part of your reality-check network. If your partner is the problem, your reality-check network needs to exist outside of what your partner knows about.

## 3. What a Reality-Check Network Does for You Specifically

Several functions that documentation alone cannot provide:

### External memory for events

When gaslighting is sustained, you can reach a state where you are uncertain whether specific events happened the way you remember them, even when your written records say they did. A reality-check person who heard about the event close to when it happened — even without documentation — is a second memory store. "I told you about this when it happened; you remember, right?" is a check that cannot be rewritten by gaslighting, because it lives in someone else's head.

### Pattern recognition across your accounts

Individual events can feel too minor to mention. A reality-check person who hears about many events over time can often see the pattern faster than you can, because they are not inside the relationship and not subject to its calibration pressure. The person who says "you've mentioned three different situations like this in the past month" is giving you the pattern picture you may not be able to produce yourself.

### Calibration against distortion

The credibility discount and compliance training both push you toward thinking your situation is less bad than it is. A reality-check person — if they understand the situation and are not themselves subject to the same distortions — can provide a counterweight. "No, that really is unusual. Most relationships/workplaces/doctors/family dynamics do not work like that" is information that anchors you when you are being told that everything is normal and you are overreacting.

### Memory of you when you were not under attack

Sustained manipulation often includes reshaping the target's sense of who they are — eroding confidence, questioning competence, producing self-doubt that was not there before. A reality-check person who knew you before the current situation can remember you as you were, and can tell you when they are noticing changes that are not improvements.

### A safe place to think out loud

Some things you cannot think clearly about until you try to say them to another person. A reality-check relationship is specifically a space where you can think out loud without the cost of performance, polish, or reciprocal emotional management. This is especially valuable for autistic readers, for whom most social interactions require significant ongoing accommodation. A reality-check relationship is one where you can be less careful.

### Reachability in crisis

In acute moments — after a particularly difficult incident, during a crisis of confidence about whether you are "overreacting" — having someone you can contact who knows what is going on is qualitatively different from having to explain the whole situation from the beginning every time you need to check in. The network is specifically for the moments when the energy to explain is not available.

## 4. Who Belongs in Your Network

Not everyone who loves you should be in your reality-check network. The criteria are specific, and some of the criteria disqualify people you might naturally want to include.

### Criteria for inclusion

**They believe you by default.** They start from the assumption that your account is probably accurate. They do not require convincing, argument, or evidence before taking your concerns seriously. They have a track record of believing you about smaller things, which is usually how you know they will believe you about bigger things.

**They can hold discomfort without fixing.** They can listen to you describe a difficult situation without immediately pushing you toward any specific action. They ask what you need before assuming. The alternative — people who hear about your situation and immediately insist you must leave/confront/call the police/forgive — is not reality-checking; it is their own anxiety about your situation being displaced onto you.

**They know enough about your situation to recognize patterns.** If every time you mention an incident you have to start from "so my partner and I have this dynamic where..." you are not reality-checking, you are recruiting a new audience each time. Reality-check people need context so they can see patterns, which means they have heard about previous events.

**They are not in the situation with you.** People who also depend on the person causing harm, or who share social networks with them, or who have their own relationship with them, have conflicts of interest that can distort their reality-checking. They may be reliable narrators about many things and not about this specific thing.

**They can be reached in practice.** A theoretically perfect reality-check person who lives in a different time zone and is hard to contact is much less useful than a less-ideal person who responds to texts the same day. Practical access matters.

**They will tell you what they see, including what you do not want to hear.** If their feedback is consistently warm agreement with whatever you say, they are not reality-checking. They are social-support-reflecting. Real reality-checking includes moments where the person says "I know this is hard to hear, but I think the situation is worse than you're describing" — and sometimes "I think it may be better than you're describing."

**They respect your autonomy.** They do not pressure you toward a specific outcome. They do not make you feel that talking to them will result in them launching into their own agenda for what you should do.

**They understand autism, or are willing to learn.** This is especially important for readers whose autism affects how they communicate about their own experiences. A reality-check person who interprets your descriptions through neurotypical expectations may systematically misread them. Autistic peers, or neurotypical people who have learned enough about autism to listen carefully, are much more useful.

### People who should generally NOT be in the network

- **Anyone who shares significant context with the person causing harm** (mutual friends, shared family, coworkers of the problem colleague). Conflict of interest and information leakage are both risks.
- **People who have historically disbelieved you about other things.** Past is prologue. If they have a track record of calibrating your accounts downward, they will do it again.
- **People who have their own strong views about what you should do.** Therapists with a specific agenda, family members who want particular outcomes for their own reasons, friends who are projecting their own situations onto yours. Advice-givers who do not start by listening are advice-givers; they are not reality-checkers.
- **People who cannot be trusted with sensitive information.** Anyone who gossips, anyone who cannot resist sharing compelling stories, anyone whose discretion has been unreliable.
- **People who will punish you for the information.** If telling someone about your situation will result in them treating you differently — dropping you, looking down on you, shifting the dynamic of the relationship — they are not safe to include.
- **People who need you to take care of them emotionally.** Some relationships are structured so that the other person's wellbeing depends on your reassurance. When you need reality-checking, they will shift the focus back to their own needs. This is not malicious but it is not useful.
- **The person causing harm themselves.** Obviously, but worth stating. A manipulator who convinces you that they are your best support is executing a specific tactic.

### The autistic peer consideration

As noted in the [Power Differential page](manipulation-hub-power-differential.md), autistic peer networks function better than their size suggests. Specifically for reality-check purposes:

- **Autistic peers can hear accounts of autism-weaponization without needing to be taught the context.** They already know what it is to be told "you're being too literal" or "you're overreacting" when you were accurately perceiving a problem.
- **They have less credibility discount against your testimony.** Crompton et al. (2020) found autistic-to-autistic information transfer is dramatically more effective than autistic-to-neurotypical.
- **They understand the compliance training problem from experience,** and will recognize when you are describing it.
- **They can often provide specific, usable feedback that neurotypical people cannot** — not because neurotypical people are bad at this, but because the experiential base that informs the feedback is different.

This is not "autistic people are always better reality-checkers than neurotypical people." It is that autistic peers have a specific advantage for this specific function that should be weighed alongside other factors. A single autistic peer who understands your situation can be more useful than five neurotypical friends who need extensive translation.

## 5. How to Build One When You Don't Have One

A common situation: you recognize that you need a reality-check network and realize you do not currently have one. This is not unusual, especially if manipulation has already reduced your social resources or if you have been masking heavily and maintaining only surface relationships.

Building a network takes time — typically months to years for a functional network, though individual relationships can become useful within weeks. The work is incremental.

### Starting points

**Re-examine existing relationships.** Many people have potential reality-check candidates already present but under-utilized. A sibling you have drifted from, a college friend who always seemed grounded, a former therapist, a coworker at a previous job. These relationships can sometimes be revived with lower activation energy than building new ones.

**Autistic communities, online and local.** Online autistic communities (Reddit's r/autism, specific Facebook groups, Discord servers, Mastodon instances) can be sources of reality-check relationships. The online context is useful for autistic readers who find in-person relationship building hard. The caveat: online communities can also be performative, so some work is needed to find individuals rather than just audiences.

**Interest-based groups.** Shared special interests are among the most reliable bases for autistic-to-autistic friendship. A book club, a gaming group, a hobby community, a professional interest organization — groups where the ostensible purpose is the interest, not the relationship itself — often produce more sustainable relationships than "making friends" as an explicit goal.

**Therapists and advocates as proto-network members.** A good autism-informed therapist can function as one node in a reality-check network, especially in the early stages when other connections are thin. So can an advocate. These are professional relationships, not friendships, but they can provide reality-checking function while you build out the rest.

**Support groups for specific situations.** Sometimes the need produces the network. If you are in a specific situation (IPV survivor, workplace bullying, estranged from family), support groups for that situation can produce reality-check relationships with people who understand the context without needing explanation.

### What the early work looks like

- **Being a little more honest than usual.** Not all at once. Incrementally. Try saying a more specific thing than you would normally say, and see how the person responds. If they respond well, you can try another more specific thing next time. Network building is a graduated trust exercise.
- **Noticing who you feel safer with.** The body tends to register safety and its absence, even when you cannot verbalize why. People you relax around, people whose messages you look forward to reading, people whose presence reduces your masking load — these are candidates. People you tense up around or feel performative around are probably not candidates, however much you like them.
- **Testing small disclosures.** Share something small and see what happens. Do they listen? Do they change the subject? Do they try to fix it? Do they remember it next time? How they respond to small things predicts how they will respond to bigger things.
- **Accepting that not everyone will work out.** Some people will seem promising and not work out. Some will decline the role implicitly or explicitly. This is not failure; it is the normal pattern of social relationship formation. The goal is to end up with a few good candidates, not to convert every potential candidate.

### When building is blocked

Some situations make network building very hard:

- **Active isolation by a manipulator.** If the person causing harm actively monitors your contacts, blocks you from seeing people, or retaliates for contact with outsiders, building a network is both more necessary and more dangerous.
- **Deep depletion.** If you are in burnout or crisis, the capacity to build new relationships is limited. Focus on maintaining one connection rather than building several.
- **Very constrained social opportunities.** Rural areas, high-demand caregiving situations, disability-related mobility constraints, and other resource constraints can make it hard to meet new people.

In these situations, one node in a network is dramatically better than zero. Even a single person who can play this role is transformational. The target is not "five close friends"; it is "one person who believes you and responds to your messages." Everything else can follow from that.

## 6. How to Use the Network

A reality-check network that exists but is not used produces nothing. Using it is a specific skill.

### What to say when you reach out

Clear framing helps. Instead of launching into an account of what happened and expecting the other person to know what you need, try framing:

- **"Can I reality-check something with you?"** — signals that you want their honest read, not just commiseration.
- **"I need to describe something and get your honest take on whether I'm reading this right."** — the same, more elaborated.
- **"Can I tell you what just happened? I don't need you to fix it; I just need to say it out loud to someone who isn't them."** — distinguishes support from fix-it-mode.
- **"I think I'm starting to doubt my own memory of [event]. Can I check with you about what I told you at the time?"** — uses their role as external memory.
- **"You know that thing with [person] I mentioned before? There's been another one."** — uses their role as pattern witness.

### What to ask for specifically

Being specific about what you need makes it more likely you will get it:

- "I need you to tell me whether this sounds as bad to you as it feels to me."
- "I need you to remember what I told you last week when I was calmer, because I can't tell if I'm being dramatic now."
- "I need you to just listen and not try to solve it."
- "I need you to tell me if you notice me saying things that contradict things I said before."
- "I need you to tell me if you think I'm in over my head here."

### What not to ask for

- **Do not ask them to decide for you.** The reality-check network helps you see your situation; the decision is yours. Offloading the decision is unfair to them and produces bad decisions for you.
- **Do not ask them to be uniformly supportive.** If you only want confirmation that you are right, you are not reality-checking. You are building a fan club. Fan clubs have their uses but they are not reality-checks.
- **Do not ask them to be the sole source of support.** Over-leaning on any one person burns out the relationship. Distribute the load across the network if possible.
- **Do not ask them to confront the person on your behalf.** This is a boundary that reality-check relationships should usually preserve. Their role is to help you think; taking action against the person causing harm is not their job.

### Frequency and maintenance

Reality-check relationships require maintenance. A person you only contact when things are bad will eventually tire of the dynamic and fade. Some maintenance principles:

- **Contact them during calm periods too.** Not every conversation should be about the situation. The relationship is a real one; treat it as one.
- **Reciprocate.** Be available to them when they need something, to the degree you can. Reality-check relationships often go both ways, with different people taking the reality-check role at different times in their lives.
- **Acknowledge what they are doing.** A simple "thank you for being here for this — it really matters" is both honest and maintenance.
- **Do not dump more than they can hold.** Be aware of their capacity. A reality-check person in their own crisis is not a reality-check resource; let them have their own crisis without overloading them with yours.

## 7. Reality-Checking vs. Validation-Seeking

A critical distinction: **reality-checking is asking for truth; validation-seeking is asking for agreement.** Both have their uses; only the first is what this page is about.

The difference is in the quality of the feedback you are willing to receive:

- **Reality-checking includes the possibility of "I don't think you're reading this right."** You are asking for honest observation, which means the honest observation might contradict you. A reality-check relationship that never contradicts you is not reality-checking.
- **Validation-seeking avoids that possibility.** You are asking for affirmation; you will interpret disagreement as betrayal or misunderstanding and seek agreement elsewhere until you find it.

Both responses feel emotionally similar when you are in distress — both produce relief — but they do different things to your calibration over time. Reality-checking keeps you anchored to what is actually happening. Validation-seeking lets you drift toward a comforting version that may or may not match reality.

**For autistic readers specifically, this distinction matters because the credibility discount and the compliance training problem cut in both directions.** Sometimes your internal sense is accurate and outsiders are telling you it is not, because their neurotypical calibration is wrong. Sometimes your internal sense is distorted by compliance training and outsiders are correctly telling you your situation is worse than you think it is. You cannot tell which case you are in from your own side — the whole point of reality-checking is to get external information. If your network only ever agrees with you, you have not built reality-checking; you have built a mirror.

### How to know which you are doing

- **Do you dismiss feedback you disagree with?** If so, you are probably validation-seeking.
- **Do you seek a different person when the first one says something you don't want to hear?** If so, you are validation-seeking.
- **Do you feel betrayed when a trusted person questions your interpretation of an event?** The feeling is normal; the behavior of acting on it is validation-seeking.
- **Do you take in contradictory feedback and sit with it, even if uncomfortably, before deciding what to do?** That is reality-checking.

The goal is not to be perfect at this. The goal is to notice when you are drifting from one to the other, and to pull back toward reality-checking when you catch yourself.

## 8. Autism-Specific Considerations

### Your network may be smaller than expected and that is fine

Autistic adults tend to have smaller social networks than neurotypical adults on average. This is a statistical fact, not a deficit to fix. A reality-check network of two people is useful. A network of one person can be useful in constrained situations. The number is not the point; the function is. Do not waste energy feeling inadequate about the size of your network.

### Autistic peer networks have specific advantages

As discussed in Section 4, autistic peer networks function better than their size suggests for this specific purpose. The research on autistic-to-autistic information transfer (Crompton et al., 2020) supports this empirically. Practical implication: **weight autistic peer relationships heavily when considering your network**. A small number of autistic peers is a significant resource, not a consolation prize for not having more neurotypical friends.

### Online relationships count

A significant portion of autistic adults maintain their closest relationships primarily through online channels. The research on online vs. in-person relationships for autistic adults suggests that online relationships can carry full friendship weight, despite mainstream framings that treat them as lesser. For reality-check purposes, an online friend who is responsive to messages and understands your context is a real network member, not a placeholder.

### Written communication is often more useful than verbal

For many autistic readers, describing a complex situation in writing is easier than describing it verbally — there is more time to think, less pressure to respond, and no real-time social processing load. Reality-check networks that operate primarily through text (messaging, email, letters) are therefore often more accessible and more useful than phone or in-person conversations for autistic readers. This is a feature, not a limitation.

### Alexithymia and articulation

If you have difficulty identifying and naming your emotional states, describing what you are experiencing to a reality-check person can be hard. Practical strategies:

- **Describe events rather than feelings.** "He did X, then I did Y, then he did Z" is easier to say than "I felt really bad." The reality-check person can often work out the emotional significance from the event description.
- **Use external markers.** Physical sensations ("my hands were shaking"), behavioral changes ("I stopped being able to focus on my work for an hour"), and measurable effects ("I haven't slept well for three nights") are more accessible than internal states for many alexithymic autistic adults.
- **Pre-write when possible.** If you know you are going to have a conversation about a situation, writing notes in advance is legitimate preparation, not cheating.

### The isolation red flag

If anyone in your life — partner, family member, friend, workplace, religious community — is actively trying to reduce your contact with other people in your life, treat this as a manipulation signal. **Isolation is one of the four core elements of coercive control** (Stark, 2007). Common forms:

- "Your friends don't really understand you; they're a bad influence"
- "Why do you need to talk to her so much? We should be enough for each other"
- "I don't like how you are when you've been around them"
- Making it difficult or costly for you to see specific people (scheduling conflicts, emotional withdrawal, picking fights before visits)
- Framing your reality-check people as the problem in your life

This is important because the reality-check network is the specific thing that isolation tactics are trying to eliminate — and they work. If you notice that you have stopped contacting people you used to see, it is worth asking why, and specifically asking whether the reduction was your decision or a response to pressure.

**Protect your network.** If someone tries to isolate you from a specific person, consider that information about the isolator, not about the person you are being told to avoid.

## 9. Strategies by Situation

### If you have a robust network already

- **Use it.** The most common mistake is having a network and not using it, either out of "not wanting to burden people" or because the compliance training says you should handle things yourself.
- **Maintain it.** Contact them during calm times. Reciprocate when you can. Do not let the relationship exist only for your benefit.
- **Update their context.** When situations change, tell them. Reality-checking is only useful if the reality-checkers know what the current reality is.

### If you have one or two people but not a network

- **Start by maximizing what you have.** One reliable person is a massive resource. Use them well.
- **Build gradually.** Add one more person over time. Do not try to build a network of five overnight.
- **Accept that one is enough for the current period.** If your current situation requires reality-checking and you only have one person, that one person can carry the function. Plan to build more when capacity allows.

### If you have no one

- **This is a real and common situation, not a character flaw.** Isolation is a feature of many manipulation situations and a feature of many autistic adults' lives regardless. It is also the hardest situation the hub addresses.
- **Start with professionals if needed.** A therapist, advocate, or crisis line can function as a node in a network while you build organic connections. They are not permanent solutions but they are legitimate starting points.
- **Online autistic communities can be genuine connections.** They are not a lesser version of in-person connection for this purpose.
- **Build incrementally.** The first node is the hardest. The second is easier. The third can feel like you have arrived. Focus on the first.
- **Reality-check through writing in the meantime.** Journaling, written self-dialogue, reading accounts by other people in similar situations — these are substitutes that work at reduced effectiveness until you can build human connection.
- **Contact a crisis line for reality-checking when you need it.** The [Crisis Resources Directory](manipulation-hub-crisis-resources.md) lists lines that will provide exactly this kind of external validation. You do not need to be in "crisis" to call; asking for a reality check on a situation is a valid reason.

### If your network is being actively eroded

- **Treat the erosion itself as data.** Whoever is reducing your contacts is doing something to you whether or not they intend to.
- **Protect the contacts you have not yet lost.** If you can see which relationships are being targeted, prioritize maintaining them even if it costs you in the relationship with the person doing the eroding.
- **Reach back to people you have lost contact with.** A message to someone you have not spoken to in a year — even a message that just says "hi, I've been thinking about you" — can reopen a channel. This is not always possible but it is sometimes possible.
- **Document the erosion.** The [Documentation page](manipulation-hub-strategy-documentation.md) explains why. The specific pattern of "which people have I stopped seeing, when, after what" is evidence of something.

### If the network tells you something you do not want to hear

- **Take it seriously, especially if multiple people converge on the same observation.** If one person thinks you are misreading the situation, they might be wrong. If three people who do not know each other all point to the same thing, the likelihood that they are all wrong is low.
- **Do not shop for a different answer.** If you find yourself explaining the situation to a fourth person after the first three said something you did not like, notice what you are doing.
- **Sit with the discomfort before acting.** You do not have to accept their interpretation immediately, but you should not dismiss it immediately either. Give it time to settle.
- **Ask them to be more specific.** Sometimes "I think you're misreading this" is actually "I think you are missing a specific piece of information" and asking "what specifically" reveals something useful.

## 10. What This Page Cannot Do

- **Build a network for you.** This page explains what a network is and how to use it, but the relationships have to be built one at a time in real life by the person who will use them.
- **Replace professional support.** Therapists, advocates, and lawyers provide specialized knowledge that even the best reality-check network cannot substitute for. The network is a complement.
- **Work instantly.** Network-building takes time. If you are in crisis right now and have no network, the crisis lines in the [Crisis Resources Directory](manipulation-hub-crisis-resources.md) are the immediate resource. Network-building is longer-term work.
- **Guarantee accurate feedback.** Even the best reality-check network is fallible. People can be wrong. Groups can be wrong together. The network improves calibration; it does not produce certainty.
- **Help when the network is the source of harm.** If your existing social network is itself dismissive, disbelieving, or colluding with a harmful situation, this page does not apply to that network. You need to build a new one outside the compromised one. That is hard work but sometimes necessary.

## 11. Key Takeaways

1. **A reality-check network is not "having friends."** It is a specific social resource with a specific function: external validation against gaslighting and internal distortion.

2. **The credibility discount means general social support is not enough.** You need people calibrated for your testimony, not just warm to your feelings.

3. **Small is fine.** Two to five people is typical; one is enough in constrained situations. The function matters, not the number.

4. **Autistic peers have specific advantages for this function.** Crompton et al. (2020) found autistic-to-autistic communication is dramatically more effective. A few autistic peers can outperform a larger neurotypical network.

5. **Reality-checking is not the same as validation-seeking.** Reality-checking includes the possibility that you are being told something you do not want to hear. If your network only ever agrees with you, you have built a mirror, not a check.

6. **Framing your ask matters.** "Can you reality-check something for me?" produces different responses than "let me tell you what happened." Tell people what you need.

7. **Isolation is a manipulation tactic.** If someone is trying to reduce your contact with your network, treat that as information about them. Protect the network.

8. **Online relationships count.** Especially for autistic readers, text-based and online relationships can carry full network weight.

9. **Maintenance matters.** Contact your network during calm periods too. Do not let relationships exist only for your crises.

10. **One node is transformational compared to zero.** If you have no network, build the first person first. Everything else follows from that.
