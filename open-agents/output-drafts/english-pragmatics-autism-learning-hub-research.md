# English Pragmatics for Autistic Learners: Feasibility Research

## Project Vision

This document is the initial research for a comprehensive learning hub that will teach English pragmatics to autistic individuals of all ages. The central thesis: pragmatics is not intuition -- it is a **learnable system** with consistent rules. Neurotypical speakers acquire this system implicitly through constant social reinforcement. Autistic individuals, who often don't perceive the social cues that serve as this reinforcement mechanism, are never explicitly taught the system -- and are then blamed for not knowing it.

This is not a deficit model. This is a **teaching gap**. The system exists. It's consistent. It can be made explicit. That's what this project aims to do.

## Part 1: The Reframe -- Why This Isn't About Metaphor

### The Standard Narrative (and Why It's Wrong)

The clinical literature has long characterized autistic pragmatic difficulties as rooted in "literal-mindedness" and an inability to understand metaphor. This framing is incomplete at best and misleading at worst.

**What the research actually shows:**

- A meta-analytic review (Kalandadze et al., 2018, PMC5843023) found that while autistic individuals showed poorer figurative language comprehension overall, the picture is highly variable across figurative language types.
- A 2025 study (Andrés-Roqueta et al., Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders) found that autistic children showed **poorer scalar implicature but similar metaphor comprehension** to neurotypical peers -- the reverse of what the "literal-mindedness" narrative predicts.
- Many autistic individuals show near-typical performance on novel metaphor comprehension yet have marked deficits in irony/sarcasm comprehension -- suggesting the issue is **social inference**, not metaphor per se.
- With proper context and support, many autistic children demonstrate substantial improvements in figurative language comprehension (PMC5843023).

### The Real Issue: A Hidden Operating System

The better explanation is captured in what educators call the **"hidden curriculum"** -- the vast body of unwritten social rules that govern communication. As the Indiana Resource Center for Autism describes it:

> "There are many unwritten rules in society that govern our behavior. While most of us intuitively understand these rules, many autistic individuals have not automatically learned the conventions and nuances that make up their social environment."

This hidden curriculum includes:
- When to speak and when to stay silent
- How to modulate directness based on social context
- What words *really mean* versus what they literally say
- How to read the "weight" of a social situation
- When a question is actually a request, a criticism, or a social ritual
- How to navigate the politeness system that English speakers unconsciously enforce

This is **pragmatics** -- the study of how context, intention, and social convention shape meaning beyond the literal words. It is a rigorous field of linguistics with well-defined categories, rules, and principles. The fact that neurotypicals learn it implicitly doesn't make it intuitive -- it makes it *invisible*.

### The Double Empathy Problem

Damian Milton's **double empathy problem** (2012) provides essential framing for this project. Milton demonstrated that communication breakdowns between autistic and non-autistic people are **bidirectional** -- non-autistic people struggle just as much to understand autistic communication styles. The problem isn't that autistic people are "deficient"; it's that two different communication systems are colliding without mutual understanding.

This means a pragmatics learning hub should not aim to make autistic people "pass as neurotypical." It should aim to make the neurotypical pragmatic system **legible** -- to expose its rules, explain its logic, and give autistic learners the tools to navigate it on their own terms.

**Key source:** Milton, D. (2012). "On the ontological status of autism: the 'double empathy problem.'" *Disability & Society*, 27(6), 883-887. [DOI](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/09687599.2012.710008)

## Part 2: Comprehensive Taxonomy of English Pragmatic Categories

The following represents a synthesis of pragmatic categories from linguistic theory and clinical/educational frameworks. Each category would become a module in the learning hub.

### Category 1: Speech Acts -- Utterances That Do Things

**What it is:** The theory that every utterance performs an action -- not just conveying information but *doing* something (requesting, promising, warning, threatening, complimenting, etc.).

**Core framework:** Austin's three-layer architecture (locutionary / illocutionary / perlocutionary acts) and Searle's taxonomy of five speech act types.

**Searle's Five Types:**
1. **Assertives** -- statements, descriptions, predictions (committing to truth)
2. **Directives** -- requests, commands, questions (attempting to get someone to do something)
3. **Commissives** -- promises, threats, offers (committing to future action)
4. **Expressives** -- thanks, apologies, congratulations (expressing psychological states)
5. **Declarations** -- firing, sentencing, christening (bringing about a state of affairs)

**Why this is hard for autistic learners:** The mismatch between the *form* of an utterance and its *function*. "Can you pass the salt?" is grammatically a yes/no question but functionally a request. "Nice weather we're having" during a storm is formally an assertion but functionally an ironic comment or social bonding ritual. The mapping from form to function is governed by context, and neurotypicals learn the mapping implicitly.

**Subcategories to develop:**
- Direct vs. indirect speech acts
- Conventional indirect speech acts ("Could you...?", "Would you mind...?")
- Non-conventional indirect speech acts (hints, insinuations)
- Performative utterances
- Felicity conditions (what must be true for a speech act to succeed)
- Common speech act sequences in everyday life (ordering food, making small talk, asking for help, giving feedback)

**Key sources:**
- Austin, J.L. (1962). *How to Do Things with Words*. Oxford University Press.
- Searle, J.R. (1969). *Speech Acts*. Cambridge University Press.
- Searle, J.R. (1975). "Indirect Speech Acts." In *Syntax and Semantics*, Vol. 3.
- [Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy: Speech Acts](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/speech-acts/)
- Andrés-Roqueta & Katsos (2020). "Children with Autism Understand Indirect Speech Acts" (PMC4638355)

### Category 2: Grice's Cooperative Principle and Conversational Implicature

**What it is:** The foundational theory that conversation operates on an unspoken agreement to cooperate, governed by four maxims. When speakers deliberately violate a maxim, they generate *implicatures* -- meanings that go beyond what was literally said.

**The Four Maxims:**
1. **Quantity** -- Say enough, but not too much
2. **Quality** -- Be truthful; don't say what you believe to be false
3. **Relation** -- Be relevant
4. **Manner** -- Be clear, brief, orderly; avoid ambiguity

**How implicature works:** When someone *appears* to violate a maxim, the listener assumes they're still cooperating and infers a hidden meaning. Example: "How was the movie?" / "Well, the popcorn was good." (Violates Relation -- the listener infers the movie was bad.)

**Why this is hard for autistic learners:** The entire system is built on *inferring unstated meaning from apparent rule violations*. If you don't know the rules exist, you can't detect the violations, and you can't generate the inferences. Autistic listeners may take the utterance at face value ("Oh, the popcorn was good") and miss the implicature entirely.

**Types of implicature to cover:**
- **Scalar implicatures** -- "Some students passed" implicates "not all." Research shows autistic individuals handle these relatively well (Pijnacker et al., 2009).
- **Particularized implicatures** -- context-dependent inferences ("The popcorn was good")
- **Generalized implicatures** -- arise without special context ("A woman walked in" implicates "not one the speaker knows well")
- **Flouting** -- deliberately breaking maxims for effect (irony, sarcasm, understatement, overstatement)

**Key sources:**
- Grice, H.P. (1975). "Logic and Conversation." In *Syntax and Semantics*, Vol. 3.
- Andrés-Roqueta, C. et al. (2018). "Comprehension of Generalized Conversational Implicatures by Children With and Without ASD." *Frontiers in Psychology*, 9:272.
- Pijnacker, J. et al. (2009). "Scalar Implicature in Absence of Epistemic Reasoning? The Case of ASD."

### Category 3: Politeness Theory -- The Face System

**What it is:** Brown and Levinson's (1987) theory that all social interaction involves managing two universal human needs: **positive face** (the desire to be liked/approved of) and **negative face** (the desire to be autonomous and unimpeded). Nearly all pragmatic indirectness in English exists to protect these two faces.

**Core concepts:**
- **Face-threatening acts (FTAs)** -- any act that threatens someone's face (requests threaten negative face; criticisms threaten positive face)
- **Five super-strategies** for performing FTAs:
  1. Bald on-record ("Close the door.")
  2. Positive politeness ("Hey buddy, could you close the door?")
  3. Negative politeness ("I'm sorry, would you mind closing the door?")
  4. Off-record / indirect ("It's getting cold in here...")
  5. Don't perform the FTA (stay silent)
- **The Weight Formula**: W = D + P + R (Distance + Power + Rank of imposition)
  - More distance between speakers = more politeness needed
  - More power differential = more politeness needed (upward)
  - Greater imposition = more politeness needed

**Why this is hard for autistic learners:** English is a **negative-politeness dominant** language. Speakers are expected to constantly hedge, soften, and indirectify their speech to avoid threatening others' autonomy. Autistic speakers who communicate directly ("Close the door") aren't being rude -- they're using bald on-record strategy in a context where English social convention expects negative politeness. Without knowing the Weight Formula exists, there's no way to know *when* directness is acceptable and when it isn't.

**Subcategories to develop:**
- Positive vs. negative politeness strategies with examples
- The Weight Formula as a practical decision tool
- Hedging as politeness (see Category 6)
- Cultural variation in politeness norms
- Email/text politeness conventions
- Workplace-specific politeness rules
- How to interpret politeness being used *on you* (recognizing softened criticism, indirect requests, etc.)

**Key sources:**
- Brown, P. & Levinson, S.C. (1987). *Politeness: Some Universals in Language Usage*. Cambridge University Press.
- Leech, G. (1983). *Principles of Pragmatics*. Longman.

### Category 4: Conversational Structure -- The Invisible Architecture

**What it is:** Conversation follows highly structured rules that are never explicitly taught. These rules govern who speaks when, what kinds of responses are expected, and how problems get fixed.

**4a: Turn-Taking**

The turn-taking system (Sacks, Schegloff & Jefferson, 1974) is one of the most fundamental structures in conversation. Rules include:
- Current speaker may select next speaker
- If no selection, anyone may self-select
- Transition-relevant places (TRPs) signal when a turn can end
- Overlap and interruption have different social meanings
- Pauses carry meaning (a 0.7-second gap before answering "yes" means something different from an immediate "yes")

**Research on autism and turn-taking:** A PLOS One study (2023) found that autistic adults produced longer gaps between turns specifically in early stages of dialogue, but the pattern normalized as conversation progressed -- suggesting the difficulty is with **initiation and calibration**, not a fundamental inability.

**4b: Adjacency Pairs and Preference Organization**

Conversations are built from paired sequences where the first part creates an expectation for the second:
- Question → Answer
- Greeting → Greeting
- Offer → Acceptance/Refusal
- Complaint → Apology/Justification
- Invitation → Accept/Decline

**Preference organization** is the structural asymmetry between:
- **Preferred responses** (simple, quick, no delay): "Want to come to dinner?" → "Sure!"
- **Dispreferred responses** (delayed, hedged, elaborated, often with accounts): "Want to come to dinner?" → "Oh... that sounds great, but... I actually have this thing..."

**Why this matters for autistic learners:** Dispreferred responses are heavily padded with hedges, delays, and face-saving accounts. An autistic person giving a direct "no" to an invitation is performing a dispreferred response without the expected padding -- which is interpreted as rude. Conversely, an autistic person may not recognize that someone else's hesitation and hedging IS a "no."

**4c: Repair**

When communication breaks down, there are systematic ways to fix it:
- **Self-repair** (speaker corrects themselves)
- **Other-initiated self-repair** ("Wait, what do you mean?")
- **Other-repair** (listener corrects the speaker -- socially dispreferred)

Research shows autistic speakers have more difficulty with repair sequences, particularly involving prosodic and non-verbal cues (Wiklund & Laakso, 2016, ScienceDirect).

**Key sources:**
- Sacks, H., Schegloff, E.A. & Jefferson, G. (1974). "A Simplest Systematics for the Organization of Turn-Taking in Conversation." *Language*, 50(4), 696-735.
- Schegloff, E.A. (2007). *Sequence Organization in Interaction*. Cambridge University Press.
- Rendle-Short, J. (2019). "Using Conversation Analysis to Assess the Language and Communication of People on the Autism Spectrum." In *Using Conversation Analysis for Research and Practice*.
- [Turn-timing in conversations between autistic adults](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0284029) (PLOS One, 2023)

### Category 5: Discourse Markers -- Social Punctuation

**What it is:** Small words and phrases ("well," "so," "like," "you know," "I mean," "actually," "basically," "right," "OK," "anyway") that serve pragmatic rather than semantic functions. They organize discourse, manage turn-taking, signal attitudes, and maintain social relationships.

**Major markers and their functions:**

| Marker | Functions |
|--------|-----------|
| **Well** | Signals a dispreferred response; buys processing time; softens disagreement |
| **So** | Marks new topic or return to main topic; signals conclusion; can indicate impatience |
| **Like** | Hedges; introduces approximate quotes; softens assertions; marks focus |
| **You know** | Appeals to shared knowledge; seeks rapport; hedges |
| **I mean** | Self-repair; clarification; reformulation |
| **Actually** | Marks counter-expectation; polite correction; emphasis |
| **Basically** | Simplification marker; authority claim; hedged summary |
| **Right/OK** | Acknowledgment; topic transition; checking understanding |
| **Anyway** | Topic shift; return to main point; closing signal |

**Why this is hard for autistic learners:** These words appear semantically empty and may be dismissed as filler. But they're doing critical pragmatic work -- signaling to listeners how to interpret what follows. Missing "well" before a response means missing the signal that a dispreferred response (disagreement, refusal, bad news) is coming. Missing "so" at the start of an utterance means missing a topic transition signal.

**Key sources:**
- Schiffrin, D. (1987). *Discourse Markers*. Cambridge University Press.
- Fraser, B. (1999). "What are Discourse Markers?" *Journal of Pragmatics*, 31, 931-952.

### Category 6: Hedging -- The English Art of Strategic Imprecision

**What it is:** The systematic use of language to reduce the force of statements, creating plausible deniability, softening potential face threats, and signaling tentativeness. English is exceptionally rich in hedging devices.

**Types of hedges:**
- **Epistemic hedges** -- "I think," "it seems," "it might be," "perhaps"
- **Approximators** -- "sort of," "kind of," "roughly," "more or less"
- **Shields** -- "according to," "apparently," "from what I've heard"
- **Performative hedges** -- "I would say," "I was wondering if," "I don't suppose"
- **Tag questions** -- "isn't it?", "don't you think?"
- **Modal hedges** -- "could," "might," "may," "would"

**The Compositionality Principle:** Hedges stack systematically. Each added hedge reduces the force of an utterance:
1. "You're wrong." (Bald on-record)
2. "I think you're wrong." (One hedge)
3. "I think you might be wrong." (Two hedges)
4. "I think you might be slightly wrong about this." (Three hedges)
5. "I was just wondering if you might perhaps be slightly off on this one." (Maximum hedging)

**Why this is hard for autistic learners:** Hedging is the primary mechanism of English politeness. Without understanding hedges:
- Direct speech sounds aggressive to neurotypical ears ("You're wrong" vs. "I wonder if...")
- Hedged speech from others may be taken at face value ("I think you might be wrong" may be heard as genuine uncertainty rather than softened certainty)
- Academic and professional writing requires specific hedging conventions

### Category 7: Register and Code-Switching

**What it is:** The ability to adjust language style based on context -- who you're talking to, where, and why. Register encompasses formality level, vocabulary choice, sentence structure, and pragmatic conventions.

**Register dimensions:**
- **Frozen** -- fixed, ritualistic (Pledge of Allegiance, wedding vows)
- **Formal** -- professional, academic, one-way (lectures, business letters)
- **Consultative** -- professional but interactive (doctor-patient, teacher-student)
- **Casual** -- informal, assumes shared background (friends, family)
- **Intimate** -- private, highly abbreviated, shared codes (close relationships)

**Why this is hard for autistic learners:** Register-switching requires real-time assessment of social context and rapid adjustment of language. Research shows that while neurotypical speakers code-switch instinctively, autistic individuals approach it as a deliberate, learned behavior requiring significant cognitive effort. This is closely related to **masking** -- and one goal of a pragmatics hub should be to teach register awareness without encouraging performative exhaustion.

**Subcategories to develop:**
- Workplace register conventions
- Email/text message register expectations
- When and how register shifts signal social cues ("suddenly formal" = anger or distance)
- Digital communication registers (Slack vs. email vs. text vs. social media)
- Register mismatches and their social consequences

### Category 8: Deixis -- Context-Dependent Reference

**What it is:** Words and expressions whose meaning depends entirely on context -- who is speaking, where, and when.

**Types of deixis:**
- **Person deixis** -- I, you, we, they (who is "we"?)
- **Spatial deixis** -- here, there, this, that, come, go
- **Temporal deixis** -- now, then, yesterday, tomorrow, ago, next
- **Social deixis** -- forms of address, honorifics, titles (reflects social relationships)
- **Discourse deixis** -- "this," "that," "the following" (refers to parts of the discourse itself)

**Why this matters for autistic learners:** Deixis requires constant tracking of the speaker's perspective, social position, and shared context. Misunderstanding who "we" refers to in a conversation, or what "here" means when reading a message sent from elsewhere, creates communication breakdowns that aren't about vocabulary or grammar.

### Category 9: Presupposition -- What's Assumed Before a Word Is Spoken

**What it is:** Background assumptions that must be true for an utterance to make sense. Presuppositions are baked into the structure of language and are taken for granted by the speaker.

**Examples:**
- "Have you stopped procrastinating?" → Presupposes you *were* procrastinating
- "John's brother is tall" → Presupposes John has a brother
- "She managed to finish" → Presupposes finishing was difficult
- "When did you realize you were wrong?" → Presupposes you *were* wrong and that you *did* realize it

**Why this matters for autistic learners:** Presuppositions are a form of implicit meaning that can be manipulated. "Loaded questions" work by smuggling presuppositions. Advertising, political speech, and everyday manipulation rely on unexamined presuppositions. Making presupposition visible as a mechanism gives learners a powerful analytical tool.

### Category 10: Relevance Theory -- The Cognitive Processing Engine

**What it is:** Sperber and Wilson's (1986/1995) theory that human communication is governed by a single principle: the search for relevance. Every utterance creates an expectation that it is worth the processing effort -- listeners follow the path of least effort to find an interpretation that satisfies their expectation of relevance.

**Core concepts:**
- **Ostensive-inferential communication** -- speakers make their communicative intentions *manifest* (ostension), and listeners *infer* the intended meaning
- **Cognitive principle of relevance** -- human cognition gravitates toward maximizing relevance
- **Communicative principle of relevance** -- every act of communication conveys a presumption of its own optimal relevance
- **Processing effort vs. cognitive effects** -- the less effort required and the greater the effects, the more relevant an utterance is

**Why this matters for autistic learners:** Relevance theory offers a *computational* framework for pragmatic inference -- a system for predicting what the speaker most likely intended. Instead of relying on social intuition, learners can ask: "What interpretation would be most relevant here given what we both know?" This is potentially a very powerful tool for pattern-thinking autistic minds.

**Key sources:**
- Sperber, D. & Wilson, D. (1986/1995). *Relevance: Communication and Cognition*. Blackwell.
- Wilson, D. & Sperber, D. (2004). "Relevance Theory." In Horn & Ward (eds.), *The Handbook of Pragmatics*. Blackwell.

### Category 11: Non-Literal Language -- Irony, Sarcasm, Humor, and Understatement

**What it is:** Language where the intended meaning differs from or contradicts the literal meaning. This is a broad category encompassing multiple distinct phenomena.

**Types:**
- **Verbal irony** -- saying the opposite of what you mean ("What lovely weather" during a storm)
- **Sarcasm** -- irony with a critical or hostile edge, often targeting a person
- **Understatement / litotes** -- deliberately minimizing ("It's not exactly cheap" = very expensive)
- **Overstatement / hyperbole** -- deliberately exaggerating ("I've told you a million times")
- **Idioms** -- fixed expressions whose meaning can't be derived from parts ("kick the bucket," "break the ice")
- **Metaphor** -- understanding one thing in terms of another (see research showing this is NOT the core difficulty)
- **Humor** -- a broad category involving expectation violation, incongruity, wordplay

**Research findings:**
- A meta-analysis (PMC5843023) found autistic individuals had more difficulty with irony and sarcasm than with metaphor
- Irony comprehension requires "second-order" theory of mind (understanding what someone believes about what someone else believes)
- Context, prosody, and explicit instruction significantly improve figurative language comprehension in autistic individuals
- Many autistic individuals develop strong appreciation for wordplay, puns, and structural humor (which rely on pattern recognition rather than social inference)

**Why the distinction matters:** This category should be broken into subcategories that separate pattern-based non-literal language (wordplay, puns, some metaphors) from social-inference-based non-literal language (sarcasm, irony, understatement). Autistic learners likely need different approaches for each.

### Category 12: Narrative and Discourse Coherence

**What it is:** The ability to construct and comprehend extended stretches of connected discourse -- telling stories, giving explanations, making arguments, and following others' narratives.

**Elements of coherent narrative:**
- **Context** -- setting the scene (who, where, when)
- **Chronology** -- ordering events temporally
- **Causality** -- connecting events with cause and effect
- **Cohesion** -- linguistic links between sentences (pronouns, conjunctions, repetition)
- **Coherence** -- logical and thematic unity
- **Character perspective** -- representing mental states and motivations

**Research on autism:**
- Autistic adults produced less coherent narratives than neurotypical peers across multiple dimensions (Losh & Capps, 2003; Baixauli et al., 2020)
- Particular difficulties with causal connections and references to characters' intentions/goals
- **However**: research also shows narrative skills can improve with pragmatically oriented training (Cognitive Pragmatic Treatment, Hilviu et al., 2023)

**Subcategories to develop:**
- Story structure (orientation → complication → resolution)
- Information structure (given vs. new information; theme vs. rheme)
- Reference tracking (who is "he"?)
- Topic management (introducing, maintaining, shifting topics)
- Audience design (adjusting your narrative to what the listener already knows)

**Key sources:**
- [Narrative production in autistic adults](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0378216620300977) (ScienceDirect, 2020)
- [Can Narrative Skills Improve in ASD?](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10520104/) (PMC, 2023)
- [Narrative abilities of autistic and non-autistic adolescents](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/aur.3272) (Autism Research, 2025)

### Category 13: Topic Management

**What it is:** The pragmatic skill of introducing topics, maintaining them, shifting between them, and closing them in conversation.

**Components:**
- **Topic introduction** -- how to bring up a new subject
- **Topic maintenance** -- staying on topic, providing relevant contributions
- **Topic shifting** -- smoothly transitioning between subjects
- **Topic closure** -- signaling that a topic is concluded
- **Perseveration** -- staying on a topic past its social expiration (common autistic trait, often framed as a deficit but can be a strength in appropriate contexts)

**Research on autism:**
- Early research characterized autistic children as producing more "abrupt," "unannounced," or "sudden" topic shifts (Ábalos et al., 2025, Autism Research)
- However, more recent studies (Abbot-Smith et al., 2025) found that autistic children did NOT significantly differ from neurotypical peers in topic maintenance ability
- Individual differences in topic management correlated with theory of mind and working memory
- Topic management can be explicitly taught with evidence of improvement (Abbot-Smith et al., 2023)

**Key sources:**
- [Conversational Topic Shifts and Topic Maintenance in Autistic and Neurotypical Children](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/aur.70204) (Autism Research, 2025)
- [Topic maintenance in social conversation](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/01427237231172652) (2023)
- [Conversational topic maintenance and related cognitive abilities](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11894863/) (PMC, 2025)

### Category 14: Nonverbal Pragmatics

**What it is:** The non-linguistic channels that carry pragmatic meaning -- often conveying more than the words themselves.

**Components:**
- **Prosody** -- intonation, stress, rhythm, pitch, volume (carries emotional and pragmatic meaning)
- **Facial expressions** -- often expected to "match" the content of speech
- **Eye contact / gaze** -- signals attention, turn-yielding, interest, honesty (culturally variable)
- **Gesture** -- deictic (pointing), iconic (miming), beat (rhythmic emphasis), emblematic (thumbs up)
- **Proxemics** -- use of physical space and distance
- **Haptics** -- touch as communication (handshakes, pats, hugs)
- **Chronemics** -- use of time as communication (punctuality, response speed)

**Why this is hard for autistic learners:**
- Prosody differences are well-documented: some autistic speakers use monotone delivery, others use exaggerated pitch variation -- both can be misread
- Eye contact norms vary dramatically across cultures and situations; forced eye contact causes distress for many autistic people
- Gesture production and comprehension delays are common and impede verbal acquisition
- Many of these channels operate simultaneously, creating a high-bandwidth signal that must be integrated in real-time

**Important note for the learning hub:** This category requires a neurodiversity-affirming approach. The goal is NOT to train autistic people to perform neurotypical nonverbal behavior. The goal is to **decode** what neurotypical people are communicating nonverbally, and to understand how autistic nonverbal behavior may be interpreted by neurotypical receivers.

**Key sources:**
- [Social communication in autism, explained](https://www.thetransmitter.org/spectrum/social-communication-autism-explained/) (The Transmitter)
- [NIDCD: Autism Spectrum Disorder: Communication Problems](https://www.nidcd.nih.gov/health/autism-spectrum-disorder-communication-problems-children)
- [Interactional challenges: prosody and non-verbal communication in other-initiated repairs](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0378216616000096) (ScienceDirect, 2016)

### Category 15: Prosodic Pragmatics -- Stress as a Meaning System

**What it is:** The system by which sentence-level stress placement selects between entirely different pragmatic interpretations of the same words. This sits at the interface between phonology (which describes the *mechanics* of stress -- pitch, duration, intensity) and pragmatics (which describes what the stress *means*). Phonology tells you *how* English stress works. This category tells you *what it does to meaning*.

This is distinct from Category 14 (Nonverbal Pragmatics), which covers prosody broadly as one of several nonverbal channels. Here, the focus is specifically on how **contrastive stress** and **focus placement** function as a *meaning-selection system* -- choosing between multiple possible interpretations that are all latent in the same string of words.

**The core mechanism: Focus activates alternatives.**

When a speaker stresses a word, they mark it as the **focus** of the utterance. Focus works by activating a set of *alternatives* -- other things that could have been said in that slot. The listener infers meaning by considering what was focused against what wasn't.

**Worked example (from the hedging compositionality scale):**

Take the sentence: "I think you might be slightly wrong about this."

| Stress Pattern | Activated Alternative | Pragmatic Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| "I think you might be *slightly* wrong" | *slightly* vs. *very/completely* | Sarcasm -- stressing the minimizer implies the opposite is true |
| "I *think* you might be slightly wrong" | *think* vs. *know/am certain* | Genuine uncertainty -- the speaker is flagging their own epistemic state |
| "I think you *might* be slightly wrong" | *might* vs. *are* | Gentle correction -- softened assertion that allows the listener to save face |
| "I think you might be slightly *wrong*" | *wrong* vs. *right* | Positive uncertainty -- speaker acknowledges the possibility of being right |

The same sentence, word for word, communicates four different things based solely on which word receives contrastive stress. This is not optional prosodic decoration -- it is a **core meaning-selection mechanism** in English.

**Why this is especially difficult for autistic learners:**

The research is specific and striking on this point:

1. **Pragmatic prosody is the hardest type.** A profile study (ScienceDirect, 2023) found that autistic individuals show "less consistent differences in grammatical prosody" (e.g., question vs. statement intonation) but "more pronounced differences in pragmatic prosody" (e.g., contrastive stress to highlight a word). The difficulty is specifically with prosody-as-meaning, not prosody-as-grammar.

2. **Reduced sensitivity to prosodic focus.** A 2025 study (Tian et al., MDPI Languages) on contrastive pitch accent found that "autistic children's comprehension was unaffected by prosody" -- they processed the sentence as though no word had been stressed, missing the pragmatic information entirely.

3. **Receptive outstrips expressive, but both are affected.** Autistic individuals generally understand prosodic cues better than they produce them, but pragmatic prosody (contrastive stress, focus marking) is the area where both reception and production diverge most from neurotypical patterns.

4. **The sarcasm trap.** Sarcasm is often marked *only* by prosodic cues -- the words are literally the opposite of the intended meaning, and only stress/intonation signals the reversal. Research (Wang et al., PMC3713234) confirms that "impairments in utilizing both prosodic and contextual cues are associated with ASD" for irony comprehension. A 2023 study (Cambridge Core) specifically examined whether prosody marks sarcasm early in utterances and included autistic participants, finding differential processing patterns.

**Subcategories to develop:**

- **Contrastive stress and alternative activation** -- the core mechanism described above
- **Focus-sensitive particles** -- words like "only," "even," "just," and "also" whose meaning *literally changes* based on which other word in the sentence is stressed:
  - "I only *ate* the cake" (I didn't bake it)
  - "I only ate the *cake*" (not the pie)
  - "*Only* I ate the cake" (nobody else did)
- **New vs. given information marking** -- stressed words typically convey new information; destressed words mark given/known information. Missing this signal means missing what the speaker considers the *point* of their utterance.
- **Stress and sarcasm/irony** -- how stress placement can flip an entire utterance's meaning (stressing the "wrong" word in a hedge stack reads as sarcasm)
- **Stress in questions** -- "Did YOU do this?" vs. "Did you DO this?" vs. "Did you do THIS?" (different presuppositions, different accusations)
- **Emphatic stress** -- "I AM telling the truth" (contradicting a perceived doubt)
- **Deaccenting and its meaning** -- removing stress from a word signals it's old information or unimportant, which carries pragmatic implications

**Connection to other categories:**
- Category 6 (Hedging): Stress placement can invert the meaning of hedges (your observation)
- Category 11 (Non-Literal Language): Sarcasm is often cued solely by prosodic stress
- Category 14 (Nonverbal Pragmatics): Prosody is the bridge between verbal and nonverbal
- Category 10 (Information Structure): Focus/stress is the spoken realization of information structure
- Category 2 (Grice): Stress placement can trigger implicatures by foregrounding specific maxim violations

### Category 16: Common Ground and Grounding

**What it is:** The process by which speakers establish and maintain mutual knowledge, beliefs, and assumptions during conversation (Clark & Brennan, 1991). Successful communication requires constantly tracking what the other person knows and doesn't know.

**Core concepts:**
- **Common ground** -- shared knowledge between interlocutors
- **Grounding** -- the collaborative process of confirming mutual understanding
- **Audience design** -- adjusting your speech based on what you believe the listener knows
- **Referential communication** -- choosing how to refer to things based on shared knowledge
- **Perspective-taking** -- modeling what the other person can see/know/believe

**Why this matters for autistic learners:** Research (PMC4764348) shows that common ground establishment is a complex pragmatic skill that develops in adolescence and is particularly challenging for autistic individuals. The difficulty isn't just theory of mind -- it's the real-time computation of "what do we both know, and how does that change what I should say?"

### Category 17: Pragmatics of Questions

**What it is:** Questions in English serve many functions beyond information-seeking. Understanding what a question is *really doing* is a core pragmatic skill.

**Types of questions by pragmatic function:**
- **Information-seeking** -- genuine request for information ("What time is it?")
- **Rhetorical** -- no answer expected ("Who knows?", "Isn't that obvious?")
- **Exam/display questions** -- asker already knows the answer ("What's 2+2?")
- **Indirect requests** -- disguised as questions ("Can you close the door?")
- **Confirmation-seeking** -- checking shared understanding ("So you're saying...?")
- **Loaded questions** -- smuggling presuppositions ("Have you stopped being lazy?")
- **Phatic questions** -- social ritual ("How are you?") -- answer expected is formulaic, not honest
- **Tag questions** -- seeking agreement or softening ("Nice day, isn't it?")
- **Leading questions** -- directing toward a desired answer

**Why this is hard for autistic learners:** Answering "How are you?" honestly with a detailed health report is a classic autistic pragmatic mismatch -- the form (question) suggests information is sought, but the function (phatic communion) requires a formulaic response. Without explicit instruction that questions have multiple pragmatic types, every question looks like an information request.

### Category 18: Pragmatics of Refusal, Disagreement, and Conflict

**What it is:** The socially complex act of saying "no," disagreeing, or engaging in conflict -- all of which are heavily regulated by politeness conventions in English.

**Components:**
- **Refusal strategies** -- direct vs. indirect; the expected pattern of excuse + appreciation + alternative
- **Disagreement markers** -- "Well...", "I see what you mean, but...", "To be fair..."
- **Face-saving** -- how to disagree without threatening positive face
- **Complaint sequences** -- the expected structure of complaints and responses
- **Conflict escalation/de-escalation markers**
- **Apologizing** -- the structure and function of apologies (who, when, how much, what counts)

**Why this is hard for autistic learners:** Direct refusal or disagreement ("No" / "You're wrong") violates English politeness norms. The expected softening, hedging, and face-saving adds layers of complexity. Many autistic individuals also struggle with the performative aspects of apology -- understanding that "I'm sorry" functions as a social repair rather than (necessarily) an admission of fault.

### Category 19: Phatic Communication -- Small Talk and Social Ritual

**What it is:** Communication whose purpose is social bonding rather than information exchange. Malinowski's (1923) concept of "phatic communion" -- language used to establish and maintain social relationships.

**Examples:**
- Greetings and farewells ("Hi, how are you?" / "See you later")
- Weather talk ("Can you believe this rain?")
- Formulaic responses ("Not bad, you?" / "Can't complain")
- Small talk at social events
- Check-ins ("How was your weekend?")

**Functions of phatic communication:**

Phatic exchanges are often dismissed as meaningless filler, but they serve at least three distinct functions:

**Function 1: Social bonding and relationship maintenance.** This is the standard account. Greeting rituals, small talk, and check-ins establish and maintain social connections, signal group membership, and create the baseline of trust on which deeper communication is built.

**Function 2: Channel testing.** Like a "ping" in networking -- phatic exchanges confirm that the communication channel is open and functioning. "Hey, how's it going?" is partly confirming "are we on speaking terms? Is this a good time to talk?"

**Function 3: Ambient state signaling (context-sensing protocol).**

This is the function most often missed in both the linguistic literature and in autism-focused social skills materials, and it may be the most practically important one for autistic learners.

Formulaic responses to phatic questions are not interchangeable. They broadcast **low-resolution state information** that calibrates what topics and tones are appropriate for the rest of the interaction. They function as a conversational "temperature reading."

Consider the range of formulaic responses to "How's it going?":

| Response | Signal | Calibration Effect |
|---|---|---|
| "Great! Really good." | Positive state, open energy | Positive topics welcome; safe to share good news |
| "Good, good." | Neutral/stable, nothing notable | Standard conversation; no special caution needed |
| "Not bad." | Mildly positive or neutral-guarded | Proceed normally but don't assume enthusiasm |
| "Oh, you know. Hanging in there." | Mild difficulty, resilient tone | Some sensitivity warranted; don't lead with overtly celebratory topics |
| "Could be better." | Negative state, understated | Caution -- the person may be dealing with something. Inappropriate to lead with your own good news without reading further |
| "Honestly? It's been a week." | Negative, inviting follow-up | The person is signaling they *want* to talk about what's going on |
| "Living the dream." (flat tone) | Sarcastic negative | Shared commiseration appropriate; performative positivity would land badly |

This signaling system works because both parties understand that:
1. The question isn't asking for literal information -- it's opening a channel
2. The response isn't providing literal information -- it's broadcasting ambient context
3. The broadcaster expects the recipient to **use this signal to calibrate subsequent behavior**

**Why this is critically important for autistic learners:**

If an autistic person dismisses phatic exchanges as "meaningless" or "not communicating anything of substance," they are not just missing social bonding -- they are **missing the context-sensing data that governs what comes next**. Concrete consequences:

- **Topic selection:** Someone who says "could be better" has signaled that they're in a negative state. Leading with "Guess what, I just got promoted!" without acknowledging their signal risks coming across as oblivious or self-centered -- not because of any social "rule" about promotions, but because the temperature reading said "negative context" and the speaker ignored it.

- **Emotional tone calibration:** The phatic response tells you whether to bring energy, restraint, humor, or seriousness to the conversation. Mismatching this tone creates the social friction that gets attributed to autistic people "not reading the room."

- **Invitation detection:** Some phatic responses signal a desire to talk more ("It's been a week"), while others signal a desire to keep things brief ("Good, good"). Missing this distinction means either prying into someone's life when they've signaled closure, or failing to follow up when they've invited it.

- **Reciprocity expectation:** The phatic exchange is also a prompt for you to broadcast YOUR state. Not returning the signal ("And you?") or providing your own temperature reading deprives the other person of the same calibration data.

**The reframe for autistic learners:** Phatic exchanges are not pointless social noise. They are a **lightweight data exchange protocol**. Think of them as the HTTP headers of a conversation -- they don't contain the content, but they carry metadata that determines how the content will be processed. The question "How's it going?" is the request; the formulaic response is the status code. "Great!" is a 200 OK. "Could be better" is a 503 Service Degraded. You don't need to parse the full response body to act on the status code.

**Subcategories to develop:**
- Formulaic greetings and their expected response patterns
- The signaling spectrum: mapping responses to conversational calibration
- Weather talk and other "safe" topics as social lubrication
- Small talk as topic-bridge (how phatic exchanges transition into substantive conversation)
- Farewell rituals and closing signals
- Cultural and regional variation in phatic conventions
- How to *use* phatic exchanges strategically (broadcasting your own state, reading others')

### Category 20: Digital Pragmatics

**What it is:** The pragmatic conventions that have emerged in digital communication -- email, text, social media, Slack, video calls.

**Key phenomena:**
- **Punctuation as pragmatic signal** -- a period at the end of a text message conveys finality or anger ("OK." vs "OK" vs "ok" vs "okk")
- **Emoji as pragmatic markers** -- serving functions similar to prosody and facial expressions
- **Response time as meaning** -- slow response can signal disinterest, anger, or simply being busy
- **Read receipts and "seen"** -- creates pragmatic obligations
- **Capitalization** -- ALL CAPS = shouting; no caps = casual
- **Platform-specific conventions** -- email formality expectations vs. Slack informality
- **GIFs and memes as speech acts** -- expressing emotions, making references, signaling group membership

**Why this may be easier for autistic learners:** Digital communication strips away many nonverbal channels that are challenging. Some autistic individuals find written communication more comfortable. However, new pragmatic conventions have emerged that are equally implicit and rapidly changing.

### Category 22: Social Hierarchy and Power Pragmatics

**What it is:** The pragmatic system by which social hierarchies are established, signaled, detected, maintained, negotiated, and navigated through language and interaction. This is not a single pragmatic mechanism -- it is a **cross-cutting system** that modulates nearly every other category in this taxonomy. Politeness levels, hedging intensity, register selection, turn-taking rights, topic control, interruption norms, even phatic greeting conventions -- all shift based on where the interlocutors sit relative to each other in a social hierarchy.

This category exists because understanding the hierarchy system is a prerequisite for correctly deploying most other pragmatic skills. You can master the mechanics of hedging, politeness, and register in isolation, but if you can't detect *where you are in a hierarchy*, you won't know *which settings to use*.

#### The Physics of Social Hierarchy

Social hierarchies are not a single phenomenon. They are a family of related structures that share an underlying logic but differ significantly in how they form, how they signal, and what pragmatic behavior they demand.

**Two fundamental pathways to status (Cheng et al., 2013):**

| Pathway | Basis | How Status Is Gained | Stability | Pragmatic Style |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Dominance** | Control over resources, ability to impose costs | Intimidation, aggression, coercion, resource control | Unstable -- maintained by threat | Direct, assertive, interrupts freely, controls topics |
| **Prestige** | Demonstrated competence, knowledge, or skill | Others voluntarily defer because the person has valued expertise | Relatively stable -- maintained by continued competence | Can be direct or indirect; earns speaking rights rather than seizing them |

Most real-world hierarchies involve **both** pathways operating simultaneously, often in tension. A company may have a formal hierarchy (org chart = dominance-adjacent, based on positional authority) and an informal one (who people actually go to for answers = prestige-based). The person with the title and the person with the influence may not be the same person. Navigating this requires reading *two* hierarchies at once.

#### Types of Hierarchies and Their Pragmatic Rules

**1. Formal/Institutional Hierarchies**
- Explicitly defined (org charts, military ranks, academic titles, legal authority)
- Pragmatic rules are relatively predictable: use titles, hedge upward, accept directives
- **Easier** for autistic learners because the structure is explicit
- Example: doctor-patient, teacher-student, manager-employee, officer-civilian

**2. Informal/Emergent Hierarchies**
- Form spontaneously in any group of people (friend groups, project teams, social gatherings, online communities)
- No titles, no org chart -- status is negotiated through interaction itself
- **Much harder** for autistic learners because the hierarchy must be inferred from behavior
- Form rapidly: research shows status differentiation begins within minutes of strangers meeting

**3. Expertise/Competence Hierarchies**
- Based on who knows the most about the topic at hand
- Can shift dynamically (you may be high-status when discussing your specialty and low-status when the topic changes)
- Autistic individuals often navigate these well -- prestige-based status aligns with valuing competence
- Pragmatic rule: the expert earns topic control and speaking rights, but must not *claim* them too overtly (see the paradox below)

**4. Social/Popularity Hierarchies**
- Based on social capital, likability, network centrality
- Common in schools, social groups, and organizations
- The most opaque hierarchy for autistic individuals -- status is determined by factors (charisma, social fluency, alliance networks) that are the hardest to detect without NT social intuition

**5. Situational/Contextual Hierarchies**
- Determined by the context: the host at a dinner party, the driver in a car, the person whose house you're in, the regular at a bar introducing a newcomer
- Shift from situation to situation
- Require reading the *situation* to detect who currently holds status

#### How Hierarchies Are Signaled (The Detection Problem)

This is the core difficulty for autistic individuals. Neurotypical people detect hierarchy position through a constellation of cues that they process automatically and largely unconsciously:

**Linguistic signals:**
- **Hedging asymmetry** -- lower-status speakers hedge more; higher-status speakers can be more direct. If two people are talking and one says "I think we should probably consider maybe adjusting the timeline" while the other says "We need to push it back," the second speaker is signaling (or has) higher status.
- **Interruption patterns** -- higher-status speakers interrupt more, and their interruptions are tolerated. Lower-status speakers who interrupt are sanctioned. Research shows the most powerful speaker in a conversation has the most turns, the longest turns, initiates exchanges, controls topics, and interrupts others.
- **Topic control** -- who introduces topics? Who gets to change the subject? Who do others follow? Higher-status speakers set the agenda.
- **Questioning rights** -- who asks questions of whom? In asymmetric interactions, the higher-status person asks more questions (doctors, managers, interviewers).
- **Deference markers** -- "If you don't mind," "Sorry to bother you," "I was wondering if maybe..." -- these accumulate in the speech of lower-status speakers addressing higher-status ones.
- **Address terms** -- first name vs. title, formal vs. informal address. Asymmetric address (they call you by your first name; you call them "Dr. Smith") signals hierarchy.
- **Speaking time** -- higher-status individuals speak more and for longer turns. In medical settings, physicians occupy 83% of speaking time.

**Non-linguistic signals:**
- **Gaze patterns** -- higher-status people look more while speaking and less while listening (the "visual dominance ratio"). Lower-status people avert gaze when stared at.
- **Posture** -- expansive, open posture signals dominance; contracted posture signals deference. Higher-status people lean back; lower-status people lean forward.
- **Spatial positioning** -- seating position (head of table), physical elevation, center vs. periphery of a group
- **Speaking order** -- who speaks first in a meeting often signals or claims status. Who speaks *last* before a decision is often the actual decision-maker.
- **Deference behaviors** -- who waits for whom, who holds doors, who speaks more quietly, who smiles more (lower-status individuals smile more as appeasement signals)

**Behavioral/contextual signals:**
- **Who defers to whom** -- watch who adjusts their opinion when disagreement emerges. The person others defer to has status.
- **Who gets listened to** -- when two people offer competing suggestions and the group follows one, that person has higher status.
- **Who is consulted** -- people seek input from high-status members before making decisions.
- **Who can break rules** -- higher-status individuals can violate social norms (arrive late, dress casually, use humor) without consequence. This is called "idiosyncrasy credit."
- **Alliance patterns** -- who sits near whom, who addresses whom first, who makes eye contact with whom when a joke is made

#### Why Autistic Individuals Struggle With Hierarchies

The autistic community has coined the term **"hierarchy blindness"** to describe this phenomenon:

1. **The detection problem.** Most hierarchy signals are implicit, multi-channel, and fast. They require integrating linguistic, prosodic, gestural, spatial, and behavioral cues simultaneously -- exactly the kind of high-bandwidth, real-time social processing that is most challenging for autistic individuals.

2. **The intuition gap.** Neurotypical children begin absorbing hierarchy structures from early childhood through social observation and reinforcement. As Jaime Heidel (The Articulate Autistic) writes: "The intuitive concept of social hierarchy that neurotypical people seem to learn as children doesn't appear to happen the same way for autistic folks." This isn't a cognitive inability -- it's that the implicit learning channel is unavailable.

3. **The value mismatch.** Research (Grand Valley State University, 2023) shows that autistic people "find status-seeking illogical and prefer egalitarian relationships." Many autistic individuals have a strong sense of fairness and will question hierarchy structures that seem arbitrary or unearned. This creates friction when the hierarchy *is* arbitrary (as many are) but is still socially enforced. As NeuroClastic puts it: "Autistic people tend to ignore dominance/authority structures unless they actually make sense to them."

4. **The directness trap.** Autistic speakers who communicate directly with someone who holds (or believes they hold) higher status are violating an implicit pragmatic norm -- the expectation that lower-status speakers hedge, soften, and defer. The autistic person isn't being disrespectful; they're using one pragmatic register in a context that demands another. But the person receiving it reads it as a status challenge.

5. **The literal interpretation of authority.** An autistic person may interpret a casual suggestion from a boss ("Maybe we should think about redesigning the interface") as genuinely optional -- because it was framed as a suggestion. But the pragmatic reality may be that this was a directive wrapped in hedging, and the expected response is to start redesigning the interface. Conversely, a direct order ("Redesign the interface by Friday") may be interpreted as inflexible when a neurotypical employee would understand there's implicit room for negotiation.

#### The Hierarchy Paradox: Competence vs. Claiming

A critical pattern that confuses autistic individuals who are high in expertise:

In prestige-based hierarchies, competence *earns* you status -- but you're **not supposed to claim it overtly**. There's a paradox at the heart of English pragmatic norms: you should be confident but not arrogant, knowledgeable but not condescending, authoritative but not authoritarian. The acceptable way to display competence is through *demonstration*, not *assertion*. Saying "I'm the expert here" is a status claim that typically *lowers* your perceived status (unless you already have institutional authority backing it). But demonstrating expertise through well-timed contributions and being sought out by others *raises* it.

Autistic individuals who are genuinely expert may violate this norm by being too overtly correct, correcting others publicly, or stating their qualifications directly -- all of which are socially penalized not because they're wrong, but because the pragmatic system demands that competence be *displayed* rather than *declared*.

#### A Framework for Navigating Hierarchies

For autistic learners, the goal is not to develop neurotypical-level intuitive hierarchy detection. The goal is to build an explicit, conscious framework -- a checklist or decision process that can substitute for intuition.

**Step 1: Identify the hierarchy type.**
- Is this a formal/institutional setting? → Check for explicit structures (titles, roles, org charts)
- Is this an informal gathering? → The hierarchy will be emergent and must be observed
- Is there a specific expertise domain? → Look for who speaks authoritatively on the topic

**Step 2: Detect hierarchy position through observable signals.**

Observable linguistic cues (no social intuition required):
- Who speaks the most? (likely higher status)
- Who introduces topics? (likely higher status)
- Who hedges more? (likely lower status)
- Who interrupts, and whose interruptions are tolerated? (interruption tolerance = status)
- Who is asked for input? (being consulted = status)
- What address forms are used? (asymmetric address = hierarchy)
- Who speaks last before decisions? (often the actual authority)

Observable behavioral cues:
- Who defers when there's a disagreement?
- Whose suggestions get followed?
- Who can arrive late or break small norms without consequence?

**Step 3: Calibrate your pragmatics accordingly.**

| Your position | Pragmatic adjustments |
|---|---|
| **Lower status** (speaking to boss, authority, elder, host) | More hedging, more deference markers, fewer interruptions, ask rather than assert, use titles/formal address if in doubt |
| **Roughly equal** (peer, colleague, friend) | Standard hedging, reciprocal turn-taking, shared topic control, can disagree with face-saving |
| **Higher status** (you're the expert, manager, host) | Can be more direct, but the paradox applies: directness is *available* but *humility* is rewarded. Don't claim status; let it be granted. |
| **Unclear** | Default to **slightly higher hedging than you think necessary**. It's safer to over-defer than to under-defer. You can always dial back formality; it's much harder to recover from being perceived as challenging someone's status. |

**Step 4: Monitor for hierarchy shifts.**

Hierarchies are not static. They shift when:
- The topic changes (expertise hierarchies rotate)
- The setting changes (the boss at work may have no status at a social gathering)
- New people enter the group
- Conflict introduces dominance dynamics into a previously prestige-based group
- Someone gains or loses institutional authority

#### Connection to other categories

This category is cross-cutting. Social hierarchy modulates:
- **Category 3 (Politeness):** Brown & Levinson's Weight Formula explicitly includes Power (P) as a variable -- more power differential = more politeness required upward
- **Category 6 (Hedging):** Hedging volume is directly proportional to status gap -- the larger the gap, the more hedging when speaking upward
- **Category 7 (Register):** Register selection is heavily determined by hierarchy position -- wrong register = status violation
- **Category 4 (Turn-Taking):** Speaking rights, interruption tolerance, and topic control are all allocated by status
- **Category 13 (Topic Management):** Who gets to introduce, change, and close topics is a hierarchy signal
- **Category 19 (Phatic Communication):** Even greetings encode hierarchy -- who initiates, who responds, what degree of informality is permitted
- **Category 15 (Prosodic Pragmatics):** Higher-status speakers speak more slowly with more pauses; lower-status speakers speak faster and fill pauses

- **Materials:** *Think Social!* curriculum, *Social Thinking* vocabulary system
- **Evidence:** Widely used; some peer-reviewed evidence; criticized by some neurodiversity advocates for potentially encouraging masking
- **Website:** [socialthinking.com](https://www.socialthinking.com)

#### 2. UCLA PEERS Program (Elizabeth Laugeson)
- **Developer:** Dr. Elizabeth Laugeson, UCLA Semel Institute
- **Target:** Adolescents and young adults with ASD, ADHD, anxiety, depression
- **Approach:** 14-16 week parent-assisted social skills training; structured curriculum covering conversational skills, peer interaction strategies, handling social dilemmas, managing social reputation
- **Evidence:** Strong evidence base -- randomized controlled trials show significant improvement in social skills knowledge and application; **gains maintained 3-5 years later**
- **Key feature:** Parent/caregiver involvement as "social coaches"
- **Books:**
  - *The Science of Making Friends* (2013) -- for adolescents
  - *PEERS for Young Adults* (2017) -- for adults
  - *The PEERS Curriculum for School-Based Professionals* (2014)
- **Website:** [UCLA PEERS Clinic](https://www.uclahealth.org/medical-services/psychiatry/child-and-adolescent/ucla-peersr-clinic)

#### 3. Cognitive Pragmatic Treatment (CPT / A-CPT)
- **Developers:** Bosco, Angeleri, Gabbatore -- University of Turin, Italy
- **Target:** Adolescents and adults with ASD, traumatic brain injury
- **Approach:** Multimodal treatment targeting comprehension and production of pragmatic phenomena including indirect speech acts and irony, using linguistic, extra-linguistic (gestures), and paralinguistic (prosody) channels
- **Evidence:** Pilot studies show improvement in pragmatic skills and narrative coherence
- **Assessment tool:** Assessment Battery for Communication (ABaCo)
- **Key sources:**
  - [Improvement of Communicative-pragmatic Ability in Adolescents with ASD](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/15475441.2021.1924177) (Language Learning and Development, 2021)
  - [Can Narrative Skills Improve in ASD?](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10520104/) (PMC, 2023)

#### 4. Social Stories and Comic Strip Conversations (Carol Gray)
- **Developer:** Carol Gray (since 1991)
- **Target:** Children and adolescents with ASD
- **Approach:** Short written descriptions of social situations with specific information about what to expect and why; Comic Strip Conversations use stick figures and conversation symbols to illustrate what people say, think, and feel
- **Evidence:** Widely used with some supporting research; most effective for specific, concrete social situations
- **Key principle:** Making implicit social information explicit and concrete
- **Books:**
  - *The New Social Story Book* (revised editions)
  - *Comic Strip Conversations* (1994)

#### 5. Pragmatic Intervention Programme (PICP)
- **Developers:** Pereira, Ramalho, Lousada (Portugal)
- **Target:** Children with ASD and Developmental Language Disorder
- **Evidence:** 2025 non-randomized controlled trial showed effectiveness
- **Key source:** [PICP effectiveness study](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/13623613241287017) (Autism, 2025)

### Approaches and Methods

#### Video Modeling
- Uses recorded examples of appropriate social interactions for learners to observe and imitate
- Can be combined with Social Stories for multi-modal learning
- Some evidence for effectiveness, especially with visual learners

#### Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) -- Pragmatic Components
- Discrete trial training for specific pragmatic skills
- Natural Environment Teaching (NET) for generalization
- **Significant controversy:** many autistic self-advocates consider ABA harmful due to compliance focus
- Newer approaches aim to be more neurodiversity-affirming

#### Peer-Mediated Instruction
- Training neurotypical peers to facilitate social interaction
- Evidence shows improved social engagement
- Addresses the "double empathy problem" by working on both sides

#### Technology-Assisted Interventions
- Virtual reality social skills programs
- Apps targeting specific pragmatic skills (e.g., "Between the Lines" by Hamaguchi)
- Computer-based interventions with immediate feedback
- AAC tools for non-speaking or minimally speaking individuals
- **Potential advantage:** removes real-time social pressure, allows practice at own pace

## Part 4: Neurodiversity-Affirming Approach

### Core Principles for the Learning Hub

The learning hub must be grounded in neurodiversity-affirming principles. This means:

1. **Bilingual, not remedial.** Frame pragmatic learning as acquiring knowledge of a second communication system -- the neurotypical pragmatic system -- not as fixing a deficit. Many autistic people already have a rich communicative system that works well with other autistic people.

2. **Transparent, not prescriptive.** Explain *why* neurotypical conventions exist (face-saving, social bonding, hierarchy management) rather than just dictating rules. Understanding the underlying logic makes the system learnable rather than arbitrary.

3. **Agency-preserving.** Give learners the knowledge to *choose* when and how to use neurotypical conventions, not the obligation to always perform them. Masking is exhausting and harmful when it becomes a constant requirement.

4. **Bidirectional.** Include content that helps neurotypical people understand autistic communication. The double empathy problem is real -- both sides need education.

5. **Strength-based.** Acknowledge and leverage autistic cognitive strengths:
   - Pattern recognition (pragmatic rules ARE patterns)
   - Systematic thinking (the categories and taxonomies of pragmatics map well to systematic minds)
   - Direct communication (valued in many contexts -- honesty, efficiency, clarity)
   - Deep interest and focus (when engaged, autistic learners can master complex systems)

6. **Multi-age.** Content should serve children, adolescents, and adults -- including late-diagnosed individuals who may have developed their own strategies but want to fill gaps.

### What Current Programs Get Wrong

Based on the Therapist Neurodiversity Collective and ASHA's primer on neurodiversity-affirming services:

- Many programs focus on **compliance** rather than **comprehension** (teaching autistic people to perform behaviors without understanding why)
- Most target children only, leaving adolescents and adults without resources
- Few programs address the **bidirectional** nature of communication breakdowns
- Perspective-taking is often taught in one direction only (autistic person must understand NT perspective) rather than as a mutual skill
- The "hidden curriculum" framing is powerful but needs to be paired with explicit acknowledgment that the curriculum is *arbitrary social convention*, not moral truth

## Part 5: Assessment and Measurement Tools

### Standardized Assessments

1. **Children's Communication Checklist-2 (CCC-2)** -- Bishop
   - Parent/caregiver rating scale, ages 4-16
   - 70 items across 10 scales including pragmatic skills
   - Yields General Communication Composite and Social Interaction Deviance Composite
   - Identified 13/16 children with ASD as pragmatically impaired
   - [Pearson Assessments](https://www.pearsonassessments.com/en-us/Store/Professional-Assessments/Speech-&-Language/Children's-Communication-Checklist-2-%7C-U-S-Edition/p/100000193)

2. **Test of Pragmatic Language (TOPL)**
   - Norm-referenced, ages 6-18
   - Measures: physical setting, audience, topic, purpose, visual-gestural cues, abstraction

3. **Comprehensive Assessment of Spoken Language (CASL)**
   - Ages 3-21
   - Pragmatic subtests: pragmatic judgment, idiomatic language, nonliteral language, inferencing

4. **Assessment Battery for Communication (ABaCo)**
   - Developed alongside CPT (Italian origin)
   - Assesses comprehension and production across linguistic, extra-linguistic, and paralinguistic channels

5. **Brief Observation of Social Communication Change (BOSCC)**
   - Newer measure for assessing broader social communication skills
   - Used in intervention research

6. **Social Responsiveness Scale (SRS)**
   - Provides insights into social cognition and pragmatic language skills

### DSM-5: Social (Pragmatic) Communication Disorder (SPCD)

SPCD was introduced in DSM-5 as a distinct diagnosis characterized by:
- Deficits in using communication for social purposes
- Impaired ability to change communication to match context
- Difficulties following rules for conversation and storytelling
- Difficulties understanding nonliteral meaning

**Assessment challenges:** Available tools are not yet sensitive enough to reliably differentiate SPCD from ASD pragmatic difficulties. The boundaries remain "fuzzy" (Saban-Bezalel et al., 2023).

## Part 6: Research Gaps and Opportunities

### What's Missing

1. **Adolescent and adult interventions.** The systematic reviews consistently note a near-complete absence of evidence-based pragmatic interventions for autistic adolescents and adults. Almost all programs target young children.

2. **Self-directed learning resources.** Nearly all existing interventions require a clinician, therapist, or trained facilitator. There is virtually nothing designed for independent, self-paced learning by autistic individuals themselves.

3. **Digital/interactive platforms.** While some apps exist, there is no comprehensive, interactive platform that teaches the full scope of English pragmatics in a systematic, autism-informed way.

4. **Neurodiversity-affirming materials at scale.** Individual therapists are developing affirming approaches, but there's no widely available resource that combines rigorous linguistics with neurodiversity-affirming principles.

5. **Practice opportunities.** Most resources teach about pragmatics but don't provide structured opportunities to *practice* in safe, low-stakes environments.

6. **Autistic-led development.** Most pragmatic interventions are designed by non-autistic researchers and clinicians. Resources developed with significant autistic authorship and perspective are rare.

### The Opportunity

This project fills a gap that no existing resource occupies:
- **Comprehensive** -- covers the full taxonomy of English pragmatics, not just "social skills"
- **Grounded in linguistics** -- treats pragmatics as a rigorous, learnable system
- **Neurodiversity-affirming** -- frames learning as empowerment, not remediation
- **Self-directed** -- designed for independent learners of all ages
- **Interactive** -- eventually incorporating practice scenarios and feedback
- **Autistic-informed** -- developed from an autistic perspective
