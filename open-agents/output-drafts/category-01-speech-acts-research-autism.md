# Speech Act Theory and Autism: Comprehensive Research Synthesis

## Overview

Speech act theory, originating with J.L. Austin (1962) and systematized by John Searle (1969, 1975), provides a framework for understanding how utterances function as actions -- asserting, requesting, promising, declaring, and expressing. For autistic individuals, the relationship between linguistic form and communicative function represents one of the most consequential areas of pragmatic difficulty, though recent research reveals a far more nuanced picture than the simple "autistic people are literal" stereotype suggests.

This document synthesizes research findings across six major areas: comprehension of indirect speech acts, production patterns, the double empathy reframing, intervention approaches, the form-function mismatch as a core challenge, and adult-specific research.

---

## 1. Comprehension of Indirect Speech Acts

### 1.1 The Conventional vs. Non-Conventional Distinction

The single most important finding in this area is that autistic individuals' comprehension of indirect speech acts is **selective, not globally impaired**. The critical variable is whether the indirect speech act is **conventionalized** or **non-conventionalized**.

**Conventionalized indirect speech acts** are forms like "Can you pass the salt?" where the interrogative structure has been lexicalized as a request form. Autistic individuals generally process these correctly.

**Non-conventionalized indirect speech acts** are forms like "It's cold in here" (intended as a request to close a window), where the hearer must derive the speaker's intention purely from context and inference about mental states. These present significantly greater difficulty.

#### Key Studies:

**Paul & Cohen (1985)** -- *Journal of Speech and Hearing Research* (PubMed: 4087881)
- Eight adults with autism and 8 IQ-matched controls completed indirect request tasks
- In a structured condition (prefaced with "I'm going to tell you to color some circles"), both groups performed similarly
- In an unstructured conversational condition, autistic participants' performance was significantly depressed, especially on sentences requiring greater semantic processing
- Participants performed well on conventionalized forms: "Can you colour this circle blue?"; "Why not colour this circle blue?"; "You should colour this circle blue"; "I'll be happy if you colour this circle blue"
- Participants had problems with more complex, less conventionalized forms: "I'll be sad unless you colour this circle blue"; "Doesn't it need to be coloured blue?"

**Ozonoff & Miller (1996)** -- *Brain and Language*, 52, 411-434 (PubMed: 8653388)
- Administered pragmatic language measures to non-intellectually-disabled adults with autism and matched controls
- Battery included measures of humor, inference, and indirect request comprehension
- Autistic participants performed significantly less well than controls on all measures
- Authors noted that some participants had "overlearned" the rule that "Can you...?" questions should be interpreted non-literally, suggesting rote pattern-learning rather than flexible pragmatic inference

**Kissine, De Brabanter, & Leybaert (2012)** -- *Autism*, published by SAGE
- Tested compliance with requests in children with autism, manipulating sentence type
- Found that autistic children correctly interpret non-literal uses of language so long as such uses are **conventionalized** and the conventions have entered their lexicons
- They readily process "Can you" utterances as indirect requests
- This was a landmark finding: conventionalized indirect speech acts do NOT require Theory of Mind

**Kissine et al. (2015)** -- *PLOS ONE* (PMC4638355, PubMed: 26551648)
- Tested 15 children with autism (ages 7-12) and 20 typically developing children (ages 2;7-3;6)
- Used a semi-structured act-out task rather than metalinguistic judgment
- Children with autism complied with indirect requests in the first phase and demonstrated capacity to inhibit the directive interpretation in later phases
- Results call for "a more nuanced view of pragmatic dysfunction in autism"
- Argued that previous studies indicating lack of comprehension either relied on unnatural stimuli or used metalinguistic tasks (asking ABOUT meaning rather than testing behavioral response to it)

**Deliens, Papastamou, Ruytenbeek, Geelhand, & Kissine (2018)** -- *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 48(9), 2938-2952 (PubMed: 29633109)
- Compared 24 adults with ASD and matched neurotypical adults in two act-out pragmatic tasks
- Task 1: Generation of indirect request interpretations
- Task 2: Comprehension of irony
- **Critical finding**: Intact contextual comprehension of indirect requests contrasted with marked difficulties in understanding irony
- Concluded that "preserved pragmatics in ASD is limited to egocentric processing of context, which does not rely on assumptions about the speaker's mental states"
- This is the key theoretical distinction: indirect requests can be processed using contextual cues about one's own needs/situation (egocentric), while irony requires modeling the speaker's beliefs and attitudes (allocentric/mentalistic)

**Frost, Nagano, & Zane (2024)** -- *Cognitive Processing* (PMC11897084)
- 26 autistic and 26 neurotypical college students
- Read narratives ending with utterances open to literal or request interpretations
- Selected best paraphrase (literal vs. request) after each narrative
- **Key finding**: "Test scores, but not autism diagnosis, predict how adults infer whether an utterance is a request"
- Autistic adults used context similarly to neurotypical adults when interpreting requests
- Working memory predicted variance on the indirect request task
- Emotion recognition was the best predictor of utterance interpretation

### 1.2 Hints and Non-Conventionalized Implicature

Where the research consistently finds difficulty is with **hints** -- utterances where the intended meaning must be entirely derived from context and speaker intention modeling.

**Understanding indirect requests for information in high-functioning autism** -- Mazzaggio, Foppolo, Job, & Surian (2021), *Cognitive Processing* (PMC8831260, PubMed: 34487273)
- Examined understanding of indirect requests for information specifically
- Conventionalized indirect requests ("Can you tell me...?") were processed well
- Non-conventionalized requests requiring more inferencing showed greater difficulty
- The distinction between conventionalized and non-conventionalized forms was the critical variable

### 1.3 Scalar Implicatures and Pragmatic Inference

Research on scalar implicatures (e.g., inferring that "some" means "not all") provides additional evidence for selective pragmatic difficulty:

**Ad-hoc and scalar implicatures in children with autism spectrum disorder** (2021) (PubMed: 33621945)
- Children with ASD showed difficulties with both scalar and ad-hoc implicatures
- Theory of Mind reliably predicted performance on scalar but not ad-hoc implicatures
- IQ modulated performance on both types

**"Some (but not all) Pragmatic Inferences are Difficult for Autistic Children"** (2025) -- *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders* (PubMed: 40246746)
- Title itself captures the nuanced finding
- More recent investigations have "mostly failed to spot any difference between autistic and neurotypical individuals" on scalar implicatures specifically
- Raises "concerns about any general claim of pragmatic difficulties in autism"
- Autism is classically associated with over-literal interpretation, but this has mostly been observed with figurative language specifically, not all pragmatic inference types

### 1.4 Figurative Language: The Meta-Analytic Picture

**Kalandadze, Norbury, Naerland, & Naess (2018)** -- *Autism* (PMC5843023, PubMed: 27899711)
- Meta-analysis of 41 studies, 45 independent effect sizes
- Overall effect: individuals with ASD showed poorer figurative language comprehension (Hedges' g = -0.57, moderate effect)
- **Critical finding**: When groups were matched on language ability, differences were **small and nonsignificant**
- This suggests figurative language difficulty may be partly attributable to core language ability rather than a specific pragmatic deficit
- Metaphors were more difficult than irony/sarcasm for the ASD group
- Cross-linguistic differences and chronological age were not significant moderators

### 1.5 The Irony/Sarcasm Challenge

While indirect requests show relatively preserved processing, irony and sarcasm consistently present difficulty:

- Irony requires understanding that the speaker **believes the opposite** of what they're saying
- This demands allocentric (other-person-centered) mental state attribution
- Deliens et al. (2018) showed this dissociation cleanly: same participants, intact on indirect requests, impaired on irony
- The attenuated impression of irony in autism "might rather be related to...a reduced tendency to interpret communicative signals in terms of complex intentional mental states" (PMC6188779)

## 2. Production of Speech Acts

### 2.1 Early Communicative Functions: The Requesting-Commenting Imbalance

One of the most robust findings in early autism communication research is an imbalance in communicative functions:

**Communicative Acts of Children with Autism Spectrum Disorders in the Second Year of Life** -- (PMC2756334)
- Young children with ASD communicate predominantly for **behavior regulation** (getting needs met) rather than for **commenting** or **sharing attention** on objects/events
- Requesting (an instrumental function) develops more readily than commenting (a social-sharing function)
- Children with ASD were less likely to point, show objects, or use eye gaze to communicate
- Were more likely to directly manipulate the examiner's hand (a purely instrumental communicative strategy)

**Nonverbal Communication in Two- and Three-Year-Old Children with Autism** -- Stone et al. (1997), *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders* (PubMed: 9455728)
- "Children with autism requested more often and commented less often than controls"
- The imbalance maps directly onto speech act categories: directive speech acts (requests) are overrepresented relative to expressive/assertive speech acts (comments, observations)
- Two-and-three-year-old children with autism displayed deficits in joint attention ability, especially high-level skills

This pattern has profound implications for speech act theory: autistic children may develop **directive** speech acts (Searle's category of acts that attempt to get someone to do something) before **assertive** or **expressive** speech acts (acts that state how things are or express feelings about them). This is the reverse of typical development, where commenting/declarative functions tend to emerge alongside or even before instrumental requesting.

### 2.2 Speech Act Distribution in Verbal Autistic Individuals

Research on speech and language patterns in autism identifies several distinctive features of speech act production:

**Language and Speech Characteristics in Autism** -- (PMC9578461)
- Differences identified in: repetitiveness, idiosyncrasy, pedantry, pragmaticism, coherence, and reciprocity
- Generally slower speech rate, greater intonational range
- Differences in prosodic phrasing and stress

**Predicting pragmatic discourse features in the language of adults with autism** -- (PMC9633181)
- Examined pragmatic features including politeness, uncertainty, and informativeness in dyadic conversational data
- Adults with ASD and those without completed collaborative tasks
- Computational analysis revealed measurable differences in pragmatic feature distribution

### 2.3 Grice's Maxims and Autistic Communication

**Grice's Cooperative Principles Violation in the Communication of Children with Autism** -- Wahyunianto et al.
- 67.65% of utterances by autistic children violated 1 maxim
- 20.59% violated 2 maxims
- 2.94% violated 3 maxims
- The **maxim of relevance** was the most frequently violated
- Language and social development problems were considered responsible
- Difficulty maintaining focus was identified as a specific cause of relevance violations

**Understanding of the Gricean maxims in children with autism spectrum disorder: Implications for pragmatic language development** -- (ScienceDirect)
- Typically developing children performed better than children with ASD on the Conversational Violations Test
- However, children with autism **progressively develop** this pragmatic language ability
- This suggests a developmental delay rather than a permanent absence

### 2.4 Echolalia as Speech Act Production

A crucial reconceptualization: echolalia (present in 75-80% of verbal autistic individuals) itself serves speech act functions.

**Prizant & Duchan (1981)** -- *Journal of Speech and Hearing Disorders*
- Analyzed over 1,000 echolalic utterances from four autistic children
- Discovered **seven functional categories** of immediate echolalia
- Approximately half had interactive linguistic functions: turn-taking, declarative utterances, yes/no answers, requests, labeling, providing information, calling, affirming, protesting, issuing directives
- One quarter were used for interactive turn-taking
- A small percentage had important cognitive (self-regulatory) functions

**Functional echolalia in autism speech** -- Kissine et al. (2023), *Frontiers in Psychology* (PMC9997079)
- Updated analysis confirming echolalia as both communicative and cognitive strategy
- Verbal formulae (delayed echolalia) may function as complete speech acts
- "Names might not be stored in memory in isolation, but be more easily retrievable as part of a whole linguistic unit -- a verbal formula associated with a speech act related to the referent"
- This connects echolalia to gestalt language processing: entire speech acts are stored as units

**Gestalt Language Processing Framework:**
- Based on Ann Peters' (1977) observation that some children acquire language as whole phrases first
- Echolalia represents a gestalt approach where children assign "a single unit of meaning to longer segments of spoken language"
- What a child initially perceives as "comesitdownatthetable" may function as the speech act of directing/requesting
- Gradually, the child isolates smaller parts to produce original, flexible speech acts

## 3. The Double Empathy Reframing

### 3.1 Milton's Double Empathy Problem (2012)

**Milton, D.E.M. (2012)** -- "On the ontological status of autism: the 'double empathy problem'" -- *Disability & Society*, 27(6), 883-887
- Proposed that communication difficulties between autistic and non-autistic people are **bidirectional**, not unidirectional
- "When people with very different experiences of the world interact with one another, they will struggle to empathise with each other"
- Autistic-neurotypical communication gaps arise from **mutual** misunderstanding, not solely from autistic deficit
- This reframes the entire question: autistic directness is not a failed attempt at indirect speech but a legitimate communicative style that neurotypical interlocutors misinterpret

**Milton, Gurbuz, & Lopez (2022)** -- "The 'double empathy problem': Ten years on" -- *Autism*
- Reviewed a decade of research supporting or partially supporting the double empathy theory
- Since 2015, increasing numbers of experimental, qualitative, and real-life social interaction studies have provided support
- Many studies emerging under critical autism studies and neurodiversity paradigm banner

### 3.2 The Empirical Evidence for Bidirectional Miscommunication

**Crompton, Ropar, Evans-Williams, Flynn, & Fletcher-Watson (2020)** -- "Autistic peer-to-peer information transfer is highly effective" -- *Autism* (PubMed: 32431157)
- Used a diffusion chain methodology (n=72): participant told a story, retold to next person, and so on across chains of 8
- Three conditions: all-autistic chains, all-non-autistic chains, mixed chains
- **Key finding**: Autistic people shared information with other autistic people **as effectively as** non-autistic people did with other non-autistic people
- In mixed groups, significantly less information was transferred
- Rapport was lower in mixed-neurotype groups
- This demonstrates that autistic communication is **not inherently deficient** -- it works well between autistic individuals

**Information transfer within and between autistic and non-autistic people (2025)** -- *Nature Human Behaviour* (PMC12283400, PubMed: 40369213)
- Large-scale replication and extension
- Both autistic and non-autistic people are similarly accurate at sharing information with others of the same neurotype
- Both experience poorer information transfer in mixed-neurotype settings
- Rapport is higher with same-neurotype partners and when diagnosis is disclosed
- "These findings validate the presence of effective communicative skills in autistic people, even if they do not necessarily adhere to non-autistic social norms"

**Geelhand, Papastamou, Jaspard, & Kissine (2025)** -- "Autistic adults display different verbal behavior only in mixed-neurotype interactions" -- *Autism* (PubMed: 39572980)
- 134 adults: 23 autistic dyads, 23 non-autistic dyads, 21 mixed-neurotype dyads
- Participants unaware of partner's neurotype
- Online referential communication task (chat and microphone, no video)
- Non-autistic dyads completed the task faster
- **Most notable finding**: In mixed dyads, autistic Directors were more verbose than non-autistic Directors
- "Even in the absence of non-verbal cues neurotype mismatch triggers autistic adults to display different verbal behavior"
- This suggests the communication difference is activated by the mismatch itself, not an inherent property of autistic speech

### 3.3 Autistic Communication as a Valid Cultural Style

**"A Difference in Typical Values": Autistic Perspectives on Autistic Social Communication** -- Sutherland et al. (2024), *Scandinavian Journal of Disability Research*
- Nine autistic adults participated in two-week online focus groups
- Identified distinctive autistic communication values:
  - **Interacting for a specific purpose** and talking about topics of genuine interest
  - A reduced or easily depleted "social battery"
  - Being **honest, direct, clear, and authentic**
  - **Respecting others' differences** by developing shared, negotiated interactional patterns
- Participants described autistic individuals as "more preoccupied with information, accuracy, solutions, truth, correctness, substance"
- Neurotypical individuals described as "more preoccupied with status, power, appearance, gathering allies, defining groups"
- Being authentic was highly valued; being inauthentic for politeness was seen as a form of deception
- Some communication differences were linked to shared neurotype; others reflected experiences with bullying, ableism, and enforced neurotypical norms

**Autistic Communication Differences: A Primer** -- Reframing Autism
- Autistic communication tends toward explicit, direct expression without the softening and hedging that neurotypical norms expect
- This directness is perceived as "blunt" or "rude" by non-autistic individuals
- From an autistic cultural perspective, this represents **different values** (prioritizing clarity and honesty) rather than failed social performance

### 3.4 Social Consequences of Direct Speech Acts in Neurotypical Contexts

**Autism and Employment Challenges: The Double Empathy Problem and Perceptions of an Autistic Employee in the Workplace** -- (PMC11317796, PubMed: 39139509)
- Autistic people who read stories about autistic employees understood them accurately 50.7% of the time
- Non-autistic people understood the same stories accurately only 31.2% of the time
- A lack of autism knowledge led to negative perceptions of autistic employees' competency and social acceptability
- Being autistic and having more autism knowledge were significant predictors of accurate behavior interpretation

**Workplace Communication Consequences:**
- Autistic employees' direct communication style can be perceived as showing off knowledge, being rude, or lacking "soft skills"
- Unwritten workplace rules encoded in indirect speech acts are inaccessible because "they can't be quantified"
- Social isolation results from repeated misinterpretations of communicative intent
- The direct speech style that autistic people use can actually be highly valuable: "they often mean exactly what they say with no hidden agendas or social manipulation"

## 4. Intervention Approaches: Teaching Speech Acts

### 4.1 Evidence-Based Intervention Methods

**Systematic Review of Pragmatic Language Interventions** -- Parsons et al. (2017), *PLOS ONE* (PMC5398499, PubMed: 28426832)
- Identified 22 studies reporting on 20 pragmatic language interventions for children with ASD aged 0-18
- Found a **small but significant** effect favoring pragmatic language interventions (Hedges' g = 0.274)
- Active inclusion of the child and parent was a significant mediator of intervention effect
- Clinic-based interventions had significant moderate effects; home and school settings showed no significant effect
- Integrated programs with caregiver education/training showed moderate effects

### 4.2 Video Modeling

**Review of Video Modelling and Scripts in Teaching Conversational Skills** -- *Review Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders* (Springer)
- Video modeling explicitly shows how an individual behaves in a given situation
- Types: traditional (showing another person), video self-modeling (showing the individual themselves), point-of-view (from the individual's perspective)
- Teaching autistic children conversational speech using video modeling has demonstrated effectiveness (PMC1286179)
- Video modeling for social communication goals of autistic adults has also been developed (ASHA, 2024)

### 4.3 Social Scripts

**Scripting as Evidence-Based Practice** -- (ERIC: ED595395)
- Scripts act as prompts for interactions or provide examples of appropriate verbal exchanges in predetermined situations
- Allow routines to be taught step-by-step in adherence to social rules "which may not be obvious to individuals with ASD"
- Research suggests scripted interventions may be as effective as video modeling
- Audio scripts may be more effective than visual scripts
- Well-supported as evidence-based approach when combined with visual supports and video modeling

### 4.4 The UCLA PEERS Program

**PEERS (Program for the Education and Enrichment of Relational Skills)** -- UCLA (PubMed: 21858588)
- Manualized, parent-assisted social skills group intervention
- Originally developed by Dr. Elizabeth Laugeson and Dr. Fred Frankel (2005)
- Randomized controlled trial showed significant improvements in:
  - Overall social skills
  - Frequency of social engagement
  - Social skills knowledge
  - Reduced ASD symptoms related to social responsiveness
- Moderate to large pooled effects across measures
- Largest effect: social knowledge improvement
- **Gains maintained 3-5 years later**
- Targeted skills: finding/choosing friends, conversational skills, humor, online communication, conflict management
- Extends to young adults with ASD (PubMed: 26109247)

### 4.5 Explicit Instruction Approaches

**Social Communication Intervention Project** -- Adams et al. (2012), *International Journal of Language & Communication Disorders*
- Randomized controlled trial of speech and language therapy for school-age children with pragmatic and social communication problems
- Found some evidence of intervention effect on blind and parent/teacher-reported communication outcomes
- No significant effect on standardized language assessment outcomes
- Highlights the gap between learning pragmatic rules explicitly and applying them flexibly

### 4.6 Developmental Social Pragmatic Interventions

**Binns & Oram Cardy (2019)** -- *Review Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*
- Systematic review of developmental social pragmatic (DSP) interventions for preschoolers with ASD
- Compelling evidence for effectiveness on **foundational** social communication skills:
  - Attention, joint attention bids, reciprocal interactions, initiating communication
- These target the precursors to speech acts rather than speech acts directly

### 4.7 Limitations and Neurodiversity Critique

**Social Knowledge & Performance in Autism: A Critical Review** -- (PMC10613329)
- "Research has long assumed autistic social challenges arise from lack of social knowledge, but emerging evidence suggests these differences may be better accounted for by difficulties with social performance rather than knowledge deficits"
- Social skills interventions produce modest gains, mostly in knowledge
- Measures of skill generalization and performance in novel settings show much less improvement
- Teaching children to limit talking about interests may "inadvertently encourage masking of the child's autistic way of being"

**Neurodiversity-Affirming Interventions Framework** -- (PMC10430771)
- Interventions criticized for leading individuals to mask or "pass" as neurotypical at a "huge cost to their mental health and well-being"
- Many programs attempt to teach "normative behavior" without evidence for what normative behavior looks like
- Neurodiversity advocates favor intervention when: provided respectfully, focused on useful skills, and improving subjective quality of life
- Goals should support autistic social strengths and create supportive environments

**Camouflaging/Masking Research:**
- Hull et al. (2017) -- "Putting on My Best Normal" (PMC5509825): Qualitative study of 92 autistic adults; camouflaging composed of assimilation, masking, and compensation (including social scripts); short- and long-term consequences include exhaustion and threats to self-perception
- Systematic review (2025) linked camouflaging to exhaustion, autistic burnout, anxiety, depression, and suicide risks
- Systematic review and meta-analysis of mental health outcomes (ScienceDirect, 2024) confirmed consistent association between camouflaging and negative mental health

## 5. The Form-Function Mismatch as Core Challenge

### 5.1 Speech Act Theory: The Framework

Austin (1962) distinguished three simultaneous acts performed in any utterance:
- **Locutionary act**: The act of producing a particular linguistic expression with specific phonological form, syntactic structure, and conventional meaning
- **Illocutionary act**: The act a speaker performs *in* saying something, characterized by its illocutionary force (asserting, questioning, ordering, warning, promising)
- **Perlocutionary act**: The act a speaker performs *by* saying something -- the effects on the audience (persuading, frightening, amusing)

Searle (1975) formalized **indirect speech acts**: utterances where the literal sentence type does not match the intended illocutionary force. "Can you pass the salt?" has interrogative locutionary form but directive illocutionary force.

Searle's solution to how hearers recognize indirect speech acts relies on Grice's Cooperative Principle: the hearer calculates that the literal meaning can't be the intended meaning (because it would violate conversational norms), and infers the intended speech act through understanding the felicity conditions of the intended act.

### 5.2 What Goes Wrong: The Processing Account

For autistic individuals, the form-function mismatch presents a challenge at multiple levels:

**Level 1: Conventionalized Mismatches (Relatively Preserved)**
- "Can you pass the salt?" -- interrogative form, directive function
- These are processed through learned associations (the "Can you...?" frame = request)
- Kissine (2012) argued these have been **lexicalized**: the indirect meaning is stored in the lexicon, not computed on-the-fly
- Autistic individuals can learn and store these patterns
- Processing does NOT require Theory of Mind or mental state attribution

**Level 2: Non-Conventionalized Mismatches (Impaired)**
- "It's cold in here" -- declarative form, directive function (close the window)
- "That's an interesting tie" -- declarative form, expressive function (could be compliment or ironic criticism)
- These require real-time computation involving:
  - Recognizing that the literal meaning is insufficient
  - Generating hypotheses about speaker intention
  - Evaluating context to select the most likely intended meaning
  - Accessing the speaker's mental states (beliefs, desires, goals)
- This is where Theory of Mind becomes critical and where autistic processing shows difficulty

**Level 3: Irony and Sarcasm (Most Impaired)**
- "Great weather we're having" (during a storm) -- the speaker means the **opposite** of what they say
- Requires not just inferring intent but modeling the speaker's **belief that the listener will recognize the contrast between statement and reality**
- Demands second-order mental state attribution
- Consistently the most difficult for autistic individuals across studies

### 5.3 The Predictive Processing Account

**Vicente, Michel & Petrolini (2023)** -- "Literalism in Autistic People: a Predictive Processing Proposal" -- *Review of Philosophy and Psychology*
- Proposes that literalism results from atypical functioning of the predictive system
- In predictive processing, the brain constantly generates predictions about incoming information and updates based on prediction errors
- An atypical balance between predictions and error signals makes autistic individuals **more uncertain about their own predictions**
- This uncertainty is resolved by "resorting to the literal interpretation" as the safest default
- Predictions play a key role in everyday language comprehension
- A low level of confidence in one's own predictions escalates comprehension difficulties
- This account explains why the literalist bias is **stronger** for some types of non-literal meaning (irony, sarcasm) than others (conventionalized indirect speech acts)
- Conventionalized forms have stronger, more reliable priors (they've been encountered many times in the same form), so predictions are more confident

### 5.4 Kissine's Cognitive Flexibility Account

**Kissine (2012)** -- "Pragmatics, Cognitive Flexibility and Autism Spectrum Disorders" -- *Mind & Language*, 27(1), 1-28
- Pragmatic deficits in ASD are **not homogenous**: inter-subjective dimensions are affected, while other pragmatic capacities are preserved
- Argued that cognitive flexibility deficits underlie pragmatic difficulties
- Typically developing children take interlocutor perspective into account; poor cognitive flexibility makes this difficult for autistic individuals
- The difficulty is specifically with "grasping the inter-subjective character of communicative stimuli"
- This means the problem is not with language processing per se, but with the flexible switching between one's own perspective and the speaker's perspective that non-literal language demands

### 5.5 Literalism as Selective, Not Absolute

**Figurative language processing in autism spectrum disorders: A review** -- Lampri (2024), *Autism Research*
- "Literal interpretations of utterances with intended nonliteral meanings have been considered characteristic of autistic individuals"
- However, "a growing body of research indicates that a subset of individuals with ASD do not differ significantly from typically developing controls with similar language ability"
- Cognitive flexibility -- the process of switching between literal and conveyed meanings -- is the challenging component
- The literalism appears to be strongest in:
  - Novel or unfamiliar non-literal forms
  - Forms requiring speaker mental state attribution
  - Situations with limited contextual support
  - Tasks requiring metalinguistic judgment (talking about meaning) rather than behavioral response

## 6. Autistic Adults and Speech Acts

### 6.1 The Research Gap

Research on speech acts in autistic adults is significantly less extensive than in children. Several factors contribute to this:
- Historical focus on early intervention and developmental trajectories
- Diagnostic criteria historically emphasized childhood presentation
- Many autistic adults are undiagnosed or late-diagnosed
- Adult pragmatic language research is a relatively newer field

### 6.2 Key Adult-Specific Findings

**Linguistic and pragmatic language skills in adults with Autism Spectrum Disorder: A pilot study** -- Volden & Phillips (ScienceDirect)
- Assessed adults with ASD aged 18-67 years using various language batteries
- Adults with ASD performed significantly less well than neurotypical controls in pragmatic tests
- Specifically impaired: comprehension of inferred meaning and appreciation of humor
- These findings demonstrate that pragmatic differences persist into adulthood

**Sng, Carter, & Stephenson (2018)** -- *Review Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 3(5)
- Systematic review of comparative pragmatic differences in conversational skills
- Found that individuals with ASD have difficulties with pragmatic communication to achieve a social end (e.g., during social conversations)
- Research in conversation skills is "fragmented and lacks uniformity, with few common measures"

**Frost, Nagano, & Zane (2024)** -- *Cognitive Processing* (PMC11897084)
- The most important recent adult study
- Demonstrated that autistic college students can use discourse context to determine speaker intention as effectively as neurotypical students
- Working memory and emotion recognition (not autism per se) predicted performance
- Challenges the assumption that indirect speech act difficulty persists uniformly into adulthood

**Predicting pragmatic discourse features in the language of adults with autism** -- (PMC9633181)
- Used computational analysis of politeness, uncertainty, and informativeness in conversational data
- Found measurable pragmatic differences between adults with and without ASD in collaborative tasks
- This represents a more ecologically valid approach than forced-choice tasks

### 6.3 Pragmatic Language and Theory of Mind in Adults

**Associations between pragmatic language and Theory of Mind in individuals with a history of autism** -- *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B* (2025)
- Pragmatic language abilities draw heavily on Theory of Mind processing
- Pragmatic impairments in autism reflect difficulty in "automatically and rapidly accessing, understanding and predicting another person's mental state"
- This rapid, automatic processing is what differentiates adult performance from childhood -- adults may develop compensatory strategies that work in many situations but break down under time pressure or complexity

### 6.4 Camouflaging and Compensatory Speech Acts in Adults

A significant body of adult research examines how autistic adults learn to perform neurotypical speech acts through conscious effort:

**Hull et al. (2017)** -- "Putting on My Best Normal" (PMC5509825)
- Qualitative study of 92 autistic adults
- Camouflaging strategies include: forcing eye contact, using conversational scripts, using learned rules to respond to non-verbal behavior
- Many of these strategies are essentially **learned speech act performances** -- performing request-softening, small talk, and social lubrication that come naturally to neurotypical speakers
- Short-term consequence: exhaustion
- Long-term consequences: threats to self-perception, identity confusion, autistic burnout

**The consequences of social camouflaging in autistic adults** -- (ScienceDirect, 2025)
- Systematic review linking camouflaging to exhaustion and autistic burnout
- Many describe it as "cognitively demanding" -- consistent with the idea that neurotypical speech acts require effortful computation for autistic adults rather than automatic processing

**A framework for neurodiversity-affirming interventions** -- (PMC10430771)
- Reframes the goal: instead of teaching autistic adults to perform neurotypical speech acts, create environments where direct communication is accepted
- Distinguishes between **useful pragmatic skills** (e.g., understanding that your boss's "suggestion" is actually a directive) and **masking** (performing unnecessary social rituals to avoid stigma)

## 7. Theoretical Integration: Why Speech Acts Matter for Understanding Autism

### 7.1 The Speech Act Taxonomy Applied to Autism

Applying Searle's five categories of speech acts to autistic communication patterns:

| Speech Act Type | Definition | Autistic Pattern |
|---|---|---|
| **Assertives** | Statements of fact/belief | Often strong; may violate quantity/relevance maxims by providing too much or too-detailed information |
| **Directives** | Attempts to get someone to act | Develop early (requesting); may be overly direct by NT standards; difficulty recognizing indirect directives from others |
| **Commissives** | Commitments to future action | Tends to be literal -- a promise is taken as absolute commitment; difficulty with hedged commissives ("I'll try to make it") |
| **Expressives** | Expressions of psychological states | May be atypical in form (monotone delivery, unusual timing) but genuinely experienced; social/relational expressives (small talk, compliments) may be absent or formulaic |
| **Declarations** | Utterances that change institutional reality | Generally understood when explicit ("I now pronounce you...") but implicit declarations and their social consequences may be missed |

### 7.2 The Core Insight

The research converges on a key insight: autistic difficulty with speech acts is **not** a global inability to communicate pragmatically. It is a specific difficulty with the subset of speech acts that require:

1. **Real-time computation** of non-conventionalized meaning
2. **Mental state attribution** to the speaker
3. **Flexible switching** between literal and intended interpretations
4. **Recognition of implicit social contracts** encoded in speech act patterns

Meanwhile, autistic individuals demonstrate **strengths** in:
1. **Literal truth value** of assertions (high reliability in assertive speech acts)
2. **Directness** of directives (clear, unambiguous requests)
3. **Commitment reliability** in commissives (meaning what they say about future actions)
4. **Learning conventionalized patterns** when explicitly taught

### 7.3 Implications for a Visual Language Framework

For individuals who struggle with the implicit mapping between linguistic form and communicative function, a system that makes this mapping **explicit and visual** could be transformative. Rather than requiring the listener to compute "Is this a question or a request?" based on context and speaker mental states, a visual representation could:

- Mark the intended speech act directly (this is a REQUEST, not a QUESTION)
- Make the form-function relationship visible rather than implicit
- Represent hedging, indirectness, and social softening as visible structural features rather than invisible pragmatic expectations
- Allow the user to process speech act information through the visual/spatial channel rather than the social-inferential channel
