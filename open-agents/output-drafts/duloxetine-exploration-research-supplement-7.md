## What this document does differently

The reader diagnosed this project's error and he is right:

> *"Every study will have counter-evidence... Any reasonable hypothesis will always find a counter example in the population. That doesn't prove the hypothesis wrong; it just proves the hypothesis wrong for some of the population. The problem, to my mind, is you're trying to find a single explanation that can't be disproved instead of compiling a list of possibly valid explanations."*

Six passes have practised serial monogamy with hypotheses. Each pass crowned a mechanism, the next pass killed it, and the reader was left with a sequence of obituaries. **This document stops doing that.** It produces a ranked list of live candidates rated for fit to him, and a short list of the genuinely dead.

**The distinction that shortens the list rather than lengthening it:**

| Killed by | Status |
|---|---|
| **Heterogeneity** (a null average, a population that didn't respond) | **NOT DEAD.** A null average is compatible with a large individual effect. **Candidate.** |

# PART 1 — THE NAP FINDING

## 1.1 Is the ESS-high/MSLT-normal dissociation replicated? **No. I enumerated the entire literature.**

`[primary, verified — search-level, reproducible]` PubMed: `("multiple sleep latency"[tiab]) AND ("chronic fatigue"[tiab] OR "myalgic encephalomyelitis"[tiab])` returns **15 records**. That is the whole literature. Here is all of it.

| Study | PMID | Design | MSLT finding |
|---|---|---|---|

| **Le Bon 2000** | **11382899** | 46 unselected outpatients, 2 nights PSG + MSLT | **30% objectively sleepy (MSLT <10 min)**; 69% not; *"Objective and subjective measures of sleepiness were **not associated with CFS**"* `[primary, verified]` |
| **Watson 2004** | 15453557 | 20 MZ twin pairs discordant for CFS | MSLT **8.9 vs 10.0 min, p=.33 NS**, both non-pathologic; **ESS 10.9 vs 8.2, p=.03** `[primary, verified]` |

| **Majer 2007** | 18053240 | population-based | no significant PSG/MSLT differences `[primary, verified]` |
| **Neu 2008** | **19021860** | 16 CFS vs 13 apnea vs 12 controls | **"CFS patients showed significantly SHORTER mean SL than matched controls but within normal range"** `[primary, verified]` |
| **Decker 2009 (CDC)** | **19807920** | 35 CFS vs 40 controls | *"**no reproducible perturbations of sleep architecture, multiple sleep latency times or Epworth Sleepiness Scores are found to be associated consistently with CFS**"* `[primary, verified]` |
| **Spitzer & Broadman 2010** | **20230458** | 92 FM/CFS with MSLT, neurology practice, retrospective | **73/92 (80%) ABNORMAL by standard criteria**; *"a sleep disorder characterized by **objective hypersomnia**"*; **HLA DQB1\*0602 positive in 43%** `[primary, verified]` |

| **Maksoud 2021** | **34065013** | **systematic review, 20 studies** | *"Many of the parameters measured including slow-wave sleep, apnea-hypopnea index, spectral activity and **multiple sleep latency testing were inconsistent across the studies**... limited by recruitment decisions, confounding factors, small sample sizes and **non-replicated findings**"* `[primary, verified]` |
| **Mohamed 2023** | **36948138** | **meta-analysis, 24 studies, 801 adults** | **MSLT not analysed at all** `[primary, verified]` |

*(Remaining four of the 15: Olson 1998 (9844851), Mariman 2013 (24182640), Ambrogetti 1994 (8007866), Vetrugno 2007 (17095290 — multiple sclerosis, not CFS).)*

> ### **Verdict: the dissociation is NOT replicated. It is one study's finding in a literature its own field calls inconsistent and non-replicated, and it is contradicted in BOTH directions.**
>
> - **Toward more sleepiness**: Spitzer 80% abnormal; Buchwald 41% abnormal; Le Bon 30% objectively sleepy; **Neu 2008 found CFS patients fall asleep SIGNIFICANTLY FASTER than controls.** `[primary, verified ×4]`
> - **Toward no difference**: Watson, Reeves, Majer. `[primary, verified ×3]`
> - **The field's verdict**: Maksoud's systematic review — **"inconsistent," "non-replicated."** CDC's Decker — **"no reproducible perturbations."** `[primary, verified ×2]`
> - **The best meta-analysis did not analyse MSLT at all.** `[primary, verified]`
>
> **Two methodological catches that matter:**
> 1. **Neu 2014 cannot count as a replication.** Its cohort was selected for the absence of objective sleepiness. A normal MSLT was an inclusion criterion. Supplement 6 cites Neu 2014 in its bibliography; if a downstream agent counts it toward "MSLT is normal in CFS," that is circular. `[primary, verified]`
> 2. **Spitzer's 80% is probably a contaminated population** — HLA DQB1\*0602 in 43% is a narcolepsy marker, and the sample is a neurology referral practice. **But that cuts both ways**: it is exactly the Mariman/Buchwald point that clinic populations carry comorbid sleep disorders, and it means the "MSLT is normal in ME/CFS" claim is a claim about *well-characterised research cohorts*, not about people who show up in clinics. `[reasoning, not evidence]`

## 1.9 **The nap test as an n-of-1 instrument — verdict, and how to run it**

### Is the "lifelong hard-zero = no noise" reasoning sound?

> ### **Structurally yes. Practically, it is sound for a POSITIVE result and nearly worthless for a NEGATIVE one — and the asymmetry is the whole verdict.** `[reasoning, not evidence]`

**Where the reasoning is sound:** a channel with a lifelong floor has no false-positive noise to speak of. If P(nap) ≈ 0 across decades, one unambiguous success is genuinely surprising and therefore informative. That structure is correct.

**Five things that break it, in descending order of severity:**

1. **⚠ THE KILLER: duloxetine improved his sleep, which REDUCES sleep drive, which makes napping HARDER.** `[reasoning, not evidence — the premise is primary]`
   Bonnet & Arand measured this directly: **sleep deprivation drove nap latency from 13.4 min to 3.8 min** `[primary, verified]`. **Sleep drive dominates.** Their conclusion is explicit: *"measured sleep tendency is a **combination of sleep drive and level of central nervous system arousal**."*
   > **The drug is pushing the outcome in both directions at once.** If it lowers tonic arousal, latency should fall. If it improves his night sleep, sleep pressure falls and latency should rise. **These cancel, and nobody knows by how much.** A null result is therefore uninterpretable: "still can't nap" is fully compatible with "tonic arousal reduced, but I'm also less sleep-deprived than I've been in thirty years." **This confound is not in the brief and it is the most important thing in this section.**

2. **He has been running an aborted test.** *"I just lay there for 10-20 minutes before giving up."* **The MSLT protocol allows 20 minutes and scores a no-sleep trial as 20.** `[reasoning, not evidence]` **His lifelong "hard zero" may be a lifelong latency of 18 minutes that he has never let run.** A 20-minute latency is long, but it is a *number*, and a number can move. A "no" cannot.

3. **The effort paradox is his specific risk.** Trying to sleep prevents sleep — that is the defining mechanism of psychophysiological insomnia, the very phenotype he most resembles (§1.4). **A deliberate test, with a hypothesis attached, maximises exactly the performance anxiety that raises latency.** `[reasoning, not evidence]`

4. **The denominator is unknown.** "I don't take naps, ever" plus "I haven't tried since the duloxetine" means his attempts are rare and self-selected, under uncontrolled conditions. **A lifelong zero over an unknown and possibly small number of badly-run attempts is a weaker prior than it feels.** `[reasoning, not evidence]`

5. **One nap is an event, not a rate.** A single success does not establish nap ability. `[reasoning, not evidence]`

### **How to run it so that it is interpretable**

`[reasoning, not evidence — the design is mine; the parameters are taken from the MSLT protocol and from Bonnet & Arand's measured confounds]`

**Do it NOW, before the appointment and before any dose change. The 20mg window closes the moment the dose moves, and it does not reopen.**

| # | Rule | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | **Four attempts, not one.** Four consecutive days, or the MSLT's own design of 4–5 trials at 2-hour intervals in one day. | One trial is an anecdote. Four is a latency. |
| **2** | **Run the FULL 20 minutes. Do not give up at 10.** Score the minutes to sleep; if no sleep, score 20. | Converts his lifelong binary into a number that can move. **This is the single highest-value change.** |
| **3** | **Fixed time: early afternoon, ~13:00–15:00.** Same time every attempt. | The circadian nap zone. Testing at the wrong hour guarantees a null. |
| **4** | **No exertion for 30+ minutes before. Sit or lie quietly first.** | Bonnet & Arand: **a 5-minute walk raised latency from 6.9 to 11.9 min** `[primary, verified]` — a bigger effect than any plausible drug effect. |
| **5** | **Fixed conditions**: same room, dark, quiet, lying down, no screen. | Åkerstedt 2014 `[primary, verified]`: context moves sleepiness ratings by 1–2 units either way. |
| **6** | **No caffeine for 8h. Log it regardless.** | The one covariate that actively masks the outcome (supplement 6 §6.5). |
| **7** | **Log the previous night's sleep duration and quality.** | **The dominant covariate.** If he slept 8 hours, sleep drive is low and the test is stacked toward null. |
| **8** | **Log dose time** and keep the nap at the same interval from it. | Distinguishes a plasma-tracking effect from a tonic one (§1.10). |
| **9** | **Pre-register the prediction in writing before attempt 1.** | Substitutes for the blinding he cannot have. |
| **10** | **Record three things per attempt**: minutes to sleep (or 20), fell asleep Y/N, and — separately — *"did I feel I could have?"* | The third is introspective and therefore the least trustworthy (Olson 1998) `[primary, verified]`. Record it, but do not let it outvote the first two. |

> ### **The verdict he needs before he starts:**
>
> **A positive is informative. A negative is not.** `[reasoning, not evidence]`
>
> **If he naps — clean, repeatable, at a fixed time, on a lifelong hard-zero channel — that is a real observation and it is worth having.** It would be the first thing anyone has ever recorded about duloxetine and daytime sleep propensity, in any population (§1.8).
>
> **If he does not nap, he has learned nothing**, because the improved-sleep/reduced-drive confound predicts a null under *both* hypotheses. **He should know that before he runs it, so that a null does not read to him as a refutation.**

### 1.10 **Benefit or adverse effect? Partially distinguishable — and the axis that works is not the one you'd guess**

He has a **41% somnolence signal** hanging over a dose increase (§1.8). If candidate 1 is right, becoming able to nap = tonic arousal reduced = **good**. If somnolence is materialising, the same observation = **bad**. Can these be told apart?

| Discriminator | **Reduced tonic arousal (benefit)** | **Somnolence (adverse)** | Strength |
|---|---|---|---|

> ### **The practical rule, and it is free:**
>
> **Count two things separately and never merge them:**
> 1. **Deliberate nap attempts that succeeded** (the benefit channel)
> 2. **Unintended dozes — fell asleep without deciding to** (the harm channel)
>
> **Benefit looks like: (1) rises, (2) stays at zero.**
> **Somnolence looks like: (2) rises**, whatever (1) does.
> **Both can happen at once.** They are not mutually exclusive and the reader should not expect a clean verdict.

> ### **The honest limit, stated because it matters more than the taxonomy:** `[reasoning, not evidence]`
>
> **The label may be less decision-relevant than it looks.** If he can nap, functions better, and nothing intrudes, then whether a physiologist would call it "reduced tonic arousal" or "mild somnolence" does not change what he should do. **The question that actually bears on the dose decision is not "which is it?" but "does it cost me anything?"** — and that is answered by the unintended-doze count, the night-sleep log, and whether hours disappear. **Those are countable. The mechanism label is not.**
