# Voice Customization for Autistic AAC Users in School Settings

## Research Summary

This research investigates the intersection of voice customization technology, autistic user needs, and the practical realities of school environments. It covers sensory and perceptual factors unique to autistic AAC users, identity considerations (including gender diversity), school-specific challenges like bullying and IEP requirements, legal frameworks (COPPA 2025, FERPA, state biometric laws), and emerging opportunities for ASD-specific voice features.

---

## 1. Autistic Voice Preferences and Sensory Processing

### 1.1 Enhanced Pitch Perception in Autism

Autistic individuals demonstrate heightened auditory sensitivity, particularly in pitch discrimination. A meta-analysis of 22 studies (464 participants) found a small-to-medium positive effect size supporting enhanced pitch perception in ASD (ASHA JSLHR, 2022). Key findings:

- Autistic people outperform neurotypical peers on pitch discrimination tasks, especially with simple tones and nonspeech stimuli
- Enhanced pitch discrimination does **not** necessarily translate to superior linguistic processing
- The advantage may be strongest in younger children, those with formal autism diagnoses, and those with delayed language onset
- Autistic individuals show increased auditory perceptual capacity overall -- they are better at detecting both unexpected and expected sounds

**Implication for AAC voice design**: Autistic users may be more sensitive to subtle differences in synthetic voice pitch, timbre, and tonal quality than neurotypical users. Voice options that sound "close enough" to typical listeners may be noticeably different -- or noticeably wrong -- to autistic users.

### 1.2 Auditory Sensitivities and Sound Distress

Sound sensitivity affects an estimated 50-70% of autistic individuals (SPARK for Autism; Kennedy Krieger IAN). The most distressing sound qualities include:

- Loud, sharp, or high-pitched sounds
- Sudden or unexpected noises
- Repetitive or continuous sounds
- Crowded or noisy environments (compounding factor in schools)

These sensitivities can interfere with communication, attention, and participation in social activities. In school settings, background noise compounds the challenge of processing AAC voice output.

**Implication for AAC voice selection**: Some voices may cause sensory distress. A voice with certain pitch or tonal characteristics that a neurotypical listener finds pleasant could be aversive to an autistic user. Voice previewing and trial periods are essential.

### 1.3 Preference for Predictable, Consistent Voices

Research on autistic children's interactions with social robots provides indirect evidence about voice preferences:

- Social robots are preferred partly because of their "consistent voice and gestures" which provide "a structured interaction environment" (PMC, 2024)
- "Controlled voice and consistent social information together could contribute to a more tractable and structured conversational environment, making it more predictable for autistic individuals" (Frontiers in Psychology, 2023)
- High variability in speech, motion, and responses can lead to reduced attention levels
- The unpredictable nature of human social interaction "often triggers anxiety" in autistic children; consistent, predictable interaction reduces this

**Critical insight**: Some autistic AAC users may actually **prefer** a more consistent, even "robotic" voice over a natural-sounding but variable one. The predictability of synthetic speech -- its lack of emotional variation, consistent pacing, and stable pitch -- may be a feature, not a bug, for some users. This challenges the assumption that more natural-sounding voices are always better.

### 1.4 Prosody Perception Differences

Autistic individuals show significant differences in how they perceive and produce prosody (Springer, 2021; PMC, 2024):

- Difficulties in emotional prosody recognition may stem from atypical processing of acoustic features that encode differences between sad, neutral, and joyful intonations
- Children with ASD can produce categorically accurate prosodic patterns, but these are perceived as "odd" by adult listeners
- Network analysis reveals that atypical prosody in autism is "different in different ways" -- there is no single "autistic voice" profile
- Aberrant emotional prosody circuitry predicts social communication impairments in children with autism (Stanford, PMC 2024)

**Implication**: AAC voice output that conveys emotional prosody may be differently interpreted by autistic users than by neurotypical communication partners. The prosodic cues that make a voice sound "natural" to a teacher may not carry the same meaning for the autistic user.

### 1.5 Echolalia, Gestalt Language Processing, and Voice Identity

Echolalia -- the repetition of previously heard speech -- is now understood as functional communication rather than meaningless repetition:

- Prizant identified 14 communicative functions of delayed echolalia, including turn-taking, requesting, protesting, and providing information (ASHA)
- Gestalt Language Processing (GLP) is a natural language development pathway where individuals learn language in larger "chunks" heard in meaningful contexts
- Crucially, "as some individuals gain functional speech, they report missing the fluency and emotional expressiveness of gestalt language and do not feel pride in their new speech, failing to personally identify with it as it does not seem as natural" (Frontiers in Psychology, 2023)

**Voice identity connection**: For gestalt language processors, the voices they echo and repeat become deeply integrated into their communication identity. The cadence, pitch, and quality of frequently echoed voices may shape what feels "natural" to them. An AAC voice that matches the prosodic patterns of their gestalt language -- rather than conventional "natural" speech -- might feel more authentic.

## 2. Voice and Identity for Autistic AAC Users

### 2.1 The "Aging Up AAC" Study

The landmark "Aging Up AAC" study (arxiv, 2024) conducted in-depth interviews with 12 autistic adults about AAC pain points and desired improvements. Key voice-related findings:

- "A person's identity is strongly tied to their voice, and having a unique, personalized AAC voice can be empowering"
- Participants want "inclusive and diverse text-to-speech (TTS) voices so that they can find a voice that matches their identity, not necessarily their body's voice"
- Many participants were transgender or gender nonconforming and wanted voices to reflect this
- Participants suggested "providing access to more adult vocabulary, switching from symbol-based to text-based AAC as someone grows up, or combining the two modalities"
- The study noted "significant overlap between trans* and autistic populations, making gender-diverse speech synthesis valuable to the autistic community"
- Future research should focus on "creating voices not based on voice cloning and understanding how trans-feminine, trans-masculine, nonbinary, and genderqueer people sound and/or want to sound with TTS"

### 2.2 Gender Diversity and Voice Needs

The intersection of autism and gender diversity creates unique voice needs:

- Transgender and nonbinary people are 3-6x more likely to be autistic (NPR, citing largest study to date)
- Voice dysphoria frequency is higher in autistic trans individuals compared to non-autistic trans individuals
- 68% of nonbinary adults using assistive technology report at least one instance of voice-related distress impacting daily task completion -- "ranging from skipping voice-based appointments to abandoning AAC devices entirely" (Interspeech 2025, "Voices of cyborg awesomeness")
- CommunicationFIRST documented a nonbinary AAC user's frustration: voices are listed as "men's, women's, or children's voices" with no gender-neutral option available for AAC apps
- Project Q, a genderless voice, exists but is not yet available for AAC platforms
- Neural TTS advances now enable voices that "sit intentionally outside the gender binary: warm but unmarked, resonant but not 'deep,' fluid but not performative"

### 2.3 Nonspeaking Autistic Perspectives on Voice and Identity

Key perspectives from nonspeaking autistic self-advocates:

**Ido Kedar** (communicates via iPad and letterboard; author of "Ido in Autismland"):
- "Not talking is not the same as not thinking"
- "Poor fine motor is not the same as not thinking; impulsive actions are different than not understanding right from wrong; poor facial affect is not the same as not having feelings"
- Challenges professionals to learn about autism from nonspeaking typers who can describe their experiences "from the inside out"

**Amy Sequenzia** (nonspeaking autistic activist and writer, uses AAC/FC/RPM):
- Advocates for "the right of individuals to communicate in any form that works for them"
- Serves on the Board of Directors of the Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN)
- Challenges forced conformity to spoken communication norms

**Reframing Autism Position Statement on Communication**:
- "Autistic voice should embrace any self-identified mode of expression, including speaking and nonspeaking patterns of sound, gesture, movement, and silence"
- Challenges "ableist assumptions and practices of communicative normativity"

**CommunicationFIRST Research Priorities (May 2024 Summit)**:
- "Every person, regardless of their age, disability, or support needs, should get a chance to listen to voices and choose their own voice, with that choice being entirely their own and not coerced"
- "AAC users may prefer voices that don't match the gender you think they have, sometimes because they're trans or sometimes for other reasons"
- AAC research and development must "strengthen the expression of personal identity" and "support integrated use of multimodal communication"

### 2.4 Self-Determination and Communication Rights

The Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN) positions voice choice as a fundamental right:
- "Effective Communication: Your Right to Your Voice" (ASAN Action Center)
- Communication access through AAC and other modalities is a right, not a privilege
- Behavior should be "interpreted as meaningful rather than as symptoms to eliminate"
- Neurodiversity justice "rejects ableist frameworks in favor of approaches that honor each person's support needs while presuming their competence and right to self-determination"

The right to choose one's AAC voice is an extension of communication rights -- it encompasses not just *what* is said but *how* it sounds.

## 3. School-Specific Considerations

### 3.1 Voice and Peer Interaction in Inclusive Classrooms

Research on how AAC voice affects peer perception:

- **Voice output improves peer attitudes**: "Children's attitudes were more favorable when a peer communicated with voice output" versus no voice output (Augmentative and Alternative Communication, 2002)
- **Girls more positive than boys**: "Overall, girls' attitudes toward the peer using AAC were more positive than boys' attitudes"
- **Speech-generating devices preferred**: "More positive attitudes were associated with individuals using speech-generating devices than low-tech communication boards or books"
- **iPad reduces stigma**: Mobile technologies like iPads offer "improved social acceptability" compared to dedicated AAC devices
- **Peer-mediated interventions work**: When neurotypical peers were taught AAC interaction strategies, "students using AAC enjoyed their interactions, saw their peers as friends, and were more involved in class activities" (TIES, University of Minnesota)
- **Communication speed disparity**: AAC communication is 10x slower than speech (135 WPM oral speech vs. ~10 WPM for AAC), which impacts natural conversational flow

### 3.2 Bullying and AAC Voice Stigma

Autistic students face disproportionate bullying rates:

- Up to 94% of autistic students report experiencing bullying; 87% report weekly bullying; 40% daily -- compared to 15% of neurotypical peers (Embrace Autism)
- AAC devices and other visible accommodations "simultaneously create reasons for peer ostracization"
- Stigma is one of the five main barriers to AAC adoption, with "stigma associated with autism and limited verbal language... pervasive, stemming from lack of understanding about capabilities"
- Children who use speech-generating devices "do not consistently use their devices throughout the day" -- which may reflect social avoidance in certain contexts
- Parent-carers "initially worried that AAC devices will single their child out to peers as being different"
- Some schools restrict device access "due to concerns about damage, keeping tablets stored in cupboards" -- functionally silencing students

**Voice quality as a bullying vector**: While no specific study documents "the robot voice kid" phenomenon by name, the convergence of high bullying rates, visible device use, and robotic-sounding voices creates an obvious vector for teasing. Natural-sounding voices that match the user's age, gender, and cultural context may reduce this vulnerability.

### 3.3 Teacher and Aide Perceptions

- Many communication partners (educators, paraprofessionals) "lack the skills necessary to effectively support successful communicative interactions with those who use AAC" (PMC, 2021)
- Teachers described challenges including "inconsistent supports for AAC use, limited staff for AAC service delivery, and lack of collaboration time and training"
- Educators reported that robotic or culturally mismatched voices (e.g., "American" in an Australian context) were "not representative voice" -- contributing to AAC abandonment
- All team members "must possess the skills and training to use the system effectively, have clearly defined roles in the teaching process, and collaborate effectively"

### 3.4 IEP Considerations for Voice Selection

Under IDEA, IEP teams must consider assistive technology needs at every meeting:

- IDEA Section 300.324(a)(2)(v) requires IEP teams to "consider whether the child needs AT devices and services"
- School districts must provide AAC devices when needed for FAPE (Free Appropriate Public Education)
- "Lack of availability or cost cannot be used as an excuse for denying AT devices or services" (Wrightslaw)
- Voice selection should be documented as part of AAC device specifications in the IEP
- In August 2024, the Department of Education issued guidance reinforcing that "Schools Must Include Assistive Technology in IEPs" (ASHA Leader)

**Gap in current practice**: While IDEA mandates AT consideration and provision, there is no specific requirement or guidance about voice selection quality, voice identity matching, or user voice preference. Voice selection is typically left to the SLP's judgment, potentially overriding the user's preferences.

### 3.5 Zones of Regulation and Emotional Voice Expression

The Zones of Regulation framework, widely used in schools for autistic students, uses four color-coded zones to help students identify and regulate emotional states. This intersects with AAC voice in two ways:

1. **Expressing emotions through AAC**: AAC can be used to label and communicate emotional states ("I'm in the red zone"). But current AAC voices typically deliver all emotional content in the same flat tone -- undermining the emotional communication.

2. **Emotional prosody in voice output**: Emerging "Expressive Tones" features (e.g., CereProc) add pitch variation and intonation to AAC output, allowing the user to convey feeling states through voice quality, not just vocabulary. This could support emotional self-regulation by giving students a voice that reflects their inner state.

### 3.6 PBIS Alignment

PBIS (Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports) emphasizes teaching prosocial behavior, self-regulation, and social-emotional competence. AAC voice features that support appropriate emotional expression -- different registers for different contexts, emotional tone options -- could strengthen PBIS implementation for AAC users by:

- Allowing voice-expressed requests rather than behavioral expressions of frustration
- Supporting appropriate social register (quiet library voice vs. excited playground voice)
- Enabling self-advocacy ("I need a break" spoken with appropriate urgency)

## 4. Minimally Speaking vs. Nonspeaking Distinction

### 4.1 Part-Time AAC Users

Part-time AAC use is increasingly recognized as valid and valuable:

- "Some people use AAC part-time, switching between verbal speech and AAC as needed" (AssistiveWare)
- Autistic adults report that "AAC is easier for them than speech" in many contexts
- "Choosing to use AAC allows autistic people to fight burnout and boost self-advocacy"
- When autistic people "had autonomy to choose how they wanted to communicate when they wanted to communicate, they felt successful"
- Almost universally, autistic adults who use speech and AAC "had to find AAC without professional assistance"

**Voice cloning opportunity**: Minimally speaking individuals who have some natural voice may be ideal candidates for voice cloning. Their AAC voice could match their natural voice, creating a seamless experience when switching between modalities. However, for those whose natural speech is intermittent or varies in quality, the question of *which* version of their voice to clone becomes complex.

### 4.2 Nonspeaking Individuals -- Whose Voice Is It?

For individuals who have never developed intelligible speech, voice identity raises profound questions:

- There is no "natural voice" to clone or match
- The AAC voice they use may become their voice identity over time
- Family members or peers may donate voice characteristics (SpeakUnique's Voice Design service allows nominating up to 2 "voice donors" matching desired accent, age, and gender)
- The Voice Keeper allows a family member or peer to "create a personal voice for that child" in just 1 minute
- ModelTalker (Nemours) focuses specifically on "providing realistic-sounding voices for children, especially those that have never had a voice of their own"

**Identity question**: If a child has never spoken, who decides what they should sound like? This touches on fundamental questions of autonomy and presumed competence. The CommunicationFIRST position that "every person... should get a chance to listen to voices and choose their own voice" applies here -- even if the choice is between pre-existing options rather than cloning their own voice.

### 4.3 Multimodal Communication

Research supports multimodal approaches:

- "AAC, gestural, and verbal communication can be worked on simultaneously" (ASHA)
- AAC does not inhibit speech development -- it supports it
- Minimally verbal children (traditionally defined as having 20 or fewer words) benefit from AAC alongside other communication modalities
- The voice identity should be consistent across modalities where possible

## 5. Voice Cloning for Children -- Ethical and Legal Framework

### 5.1 COPPA 2025 Updates

The FTC's amended COPPA Rule (effective June 23, 2025; full compliance by April 22, 2026) explicitly addresses voiceprints as protected biometric data:

- "Personal information" now includes biometric identifiers defined as "an identifier that can be used for the automated or semi-automated recognition of an individual, such as fingerprints; handprints; retina patterns; iris patterns; genetic data, including a DNA sequence; **voiceprints**; gait patterns; facial templates; or faceprints"
- Requires explicit parental consent before collecting biometric data from children under 13
- Updated consent verification methods required
- The FTC "decided against making any changes to the rule to avoid potential conflicts with FERPA"

### 5.2 FERPA and Biometric Data in Schools

FERPA protections now extend to biometric data in educational settings:

- "Biometric records such as fingerprints for meal payments, facial recognition for attendance, and retina scans for building access constitute personally identifiable information under FERPA"
- Student data protected under FERPA when stored by third parties acting on behalf of school districts
- In March 2025, the Department of Education required all state agencies to certify FERPA compliance by April 30, 2025 -- unprecedented enforcement
- FERPA applies to voice data collected as part of educational services

### 5.3 State Biometric Privacy Laws

State-level laws add additional layers of protection:

- **Illinois BIPA** (Biometric Information Privacy Act): Landmark legislation covering voiceprints. BIPA explicitly lists "voiceprint" as a biometric identifier requiring prior notice and written consent before collection.
- **BIPA enforcement in schools**: In October 2025, an Illinois court approved an **$8.75 million class action settlement** against Google LLC for alleged collection of "face models or voice models of approximately 660,000 Illinois students" as part of its education platform. This directly demonstrates the legal risk of collecting voiceprints from students.
- **23+ states** now have some form of biometric privacy law (State of Surveillance, 2025)

### 5.4 Can a School Facilitate Voice Cloning for a Student?

Legal analysis suggests several requirements:

1. **Parental consent**: Required under COPPA (under 13) and recommended for all minors
2. **FERPA compliance**: Voice data constitutes a student record if collected by or on behalf of the school
3. **Data processing agreements**: School must have proper agreements with any third-party voice cloning service
4. **On-device processing preferred**: Apple Personal Voice's on-device processing avoids many cloud data issues ("encrypted and stored securely on device")
5. **Cloud service complications**: If using ElevenLabs or similar cloud services, the voice data leaves the device, triggering additional COPPA/FERPA/BIPA obligations
6. **School IT policy approval**: Most districts require vetting of all cloud-based services through their data governance process

**Best practice recommendation**: On-device voice creation (Apple Personal Voice) is the lowest-risk approach for schools. Cloud-based services require formal data processing agreements, parental consent documentation, and IT department approval.

### 5.5 Consent Complexity for Nonverbal Minors

Voice cloning consent raises unique questions for nonspeaking children:

- The child whose voice is being created may not be able to give verbal consent
- Parental consent alone may not reflect the child's preferences
- Presumption of competence requires offering the child meaningful choice in voice selection
- SLP or AT specialist should facilitate preference assessment even for very young or significantly impacted children

## 6. Practical Barriers in Schools

### 6.1 Internet Connectivity

Cloud-based voice services face connectivity challenges in schools:

- ElevenLabs and other premium voices require internet connectivity
- If not connected, apps like Speech Assistant AAC "will automatically switch to the selected iOS voice" -- potentially changing the student's voice identity mid-day
- Offline-capable apps include: SabiKo (works fully offline with natural-sounding bundled voices), Speech Assistant AAC (offline functionality), and Proloquo2Go (iOS system voices)
- Speech Assistant AAC allows downloading ElevenLabs recordings for phrases while on WiFi for later offline use
- Many school WiFi networks are unreliable, have content filtering that may block voice synthesis services, or have bandwidth limitations

**Recommendation**: Any voice solution for school use should have robust offline fallback that maintains the student's voice identity.

### 6.2 Device Sharing

Device sharing in schools conflicts with voice identity:

- "AAC devices should not be passed around or shared amongst students because the device represents that child's voice"
- Some apps (Speech Assistant AAC) support multiple user profiles with individual voice settings
- Best practice: Each student should have a dedicated device. "You can customize the appearance of each AAC user's case so that they identify that iPad as their voice"
- Guided Access can lock an iPad into a specific AAC app, preventing other uses
- When device sharing is unavoidable, profile switching must be quick and reliable to maintain each student's voice identity

### 6.3 SLP Role in Voice Selection and Customization

SLPs are the primary professionals responsible for AAC voice decisions in schools:

- SLPs "analyze the communication needs of students with disabilities and assist in the selection and implementation of a variety of communication tools and strategies"
- SLPs conduct AAC evaluations "within the student's natural environment and educational setting"
- Voice banking "should be completed when a communicator's energy and skills are sufficient to generate clear speech"
- SLPs provide training for communication partners (teachers, aides, peers, families)

**Training gaps**: Many school-based SLPs lack specific training in voice customization technology. Modern voice cloning, neural TTS options, and privacy compliance requirements are evolving faster than clinical training programs.

### 6.4 Staff Training Requirements

- All team members need training in the student's specific AAC system
- Training must cover: basic device operation, vocabulary navigation, voice settings, troubleshooting
- Communication partners need modeling skills -- they should use the AAC system themselves
- Staff turnover in schools (paraprofessionals, substitutes) creates ongoing retraining needs
- PODD systems and core vocabulary boards should be available in classrooms and playgrounds for staff use during natural interactions

### 6.5 AAC Abandonment

The backdrop for all school-based voice considerations is the high abandonment rate:

- 30-50% of AAC users abandon or under-use their devices
- Key factors related to abandonment: "Not Maintaining/Adjusting the System, Attitude, Lack of Training, Lack of Support, and Poor Fit"
- Voice quality is specifically identified as a barrier: "the voice... is robotic or American (in an Australian context)... not representative voice"
- CommunicationFIRST identified user priorities including "creating more voices so users can sound different from each other, adding emphasis and inflection options, and allowing users to communicate using different accents and genders that match their individual and cultural identities"

## 7. Emerging Opportunities for ASD-Specific Voice Features

### 7.1 Context-Adaptive Voice

AAC "code-switching" is an emerging concept:

- "Code-switching in AAC involves modifying how a person communicates to match the context, social cues, and interpersonal dynamics" (ASHA Leader, 2025)
- This includes "modulating the tone and pace of communication, mimicking the natural rhythm of peers"
- Pragmatic competence in AAC requires "adapting communication style based on environment, communication partner, and needs"
- Examples: speaking differently in the library vs. playground, modifying vocabulary for different listeners
- Programming implications: language sampling of peers in targeted environments should guide vocabulary and voice style selection

**Technical opportunity**: Voice profiles for different contexts (quiet classroom voice, playground voice, reading aloud voice, talking to friends voice) would support social pragmatics development. This is currently not available in any AAC app but is technically feasible with modern TTS.

### 7.2 Age-Progression of Voice

A critical unmet need for pediatric AAC users:

- Children's voices change significantly with growth, especially during puberty
- "Vocal growth accelerates from birth to age 3, then decelerates, then enters a rapid growth phase during adolescence" (PMC)
- Girls' habitual speaking pitch drops between ages 7-8; boys between ages 8-9
- "It would be useful for children to be able to select or change voices as they grow" -- speech therapists could "download a voice for a six-year-old child's device, and as the child grows, the therapist or family could download an older child's voice"
- The Voice Keeper and Acapela My-Own-Voice enable ongoing voice updates
- ModelTalker (Nemours, nonprofit) specifically focuses on providing pediatric voices, at a cost of $100 per voice

**Current gap**: No AAC platform offers automatic or semi-automatic voice aging. Voice changes require manual intervention -- selecting a new voice, which means a discontinuity in voice identity. An AI-driven voice aging feature that gradually matures the user's voice would be transformative.

### 7.3 Cultural and Accent Matching

Diverse students need culturally appropriate voices:

- "Individuals from culturally and linguistically diverse backgrounds who use AAC are often offered English-only AAC systems" (ASHA)
- AssistiveWare has created bilingual children's voices (Emilio, Valeria) with "accents that sound bilingual -- with a hint of Spanish to their English"
- "Using the same voice creates a more seamless experience than changing voices between languages"
- Culturally responsive approaches should include "adding culturally relevant and personal/home vocabulary" and appropriate voice selection
- Recent speech synthesis research demonstrates "high-quality and controllable speech synthesis... across multiple languages and accents" (arxiv, 2025)
- The "1000 African Voices" project advances inclusive multi-speaker, multi-accent synthesis

**School implication**: In diverse school settings, a student whose family speaks Mandarin at home and English at school should ideally have an AAC voice that reflects both cultural identities, not default to a generic American English voice.

### 7.4 Emotional Prosody in Voice Output

Emerging technology for expressive AAC voices:

- CereProc's "Expressive Tones" feature adds pitch variation and intonation to AAC output
- Speech devices with expressive features can "simulate tone of voice, such as pitch variation and rhythm, contributing to conveying feelings more vividly"
- ElevenLabs' real-time voice synthesis can now capture "intended emotional cadence, prosody, and intent" with latencies of 75-150ms
- For autistic users who struggle with emotional prosody recognition, having explicit emotional tone controls (happy, sad, excited, calm) could support both expression and understanding

### 7.5 Slurred-to-Clear Speech Technology

ElevenLabs' capability for dysarthric speech has AAC implications:

- Technology can analyze "intended emotional cadence, prosody, and intent of the slurred input and map it onto the high-fidelity digital clone in real-time"
- Latencies now 75-150ms -- "eliminating the awkward 'type-wait-play' delay of previous generations"
- For minimally speaking autistic individuals with unclear speech, this could transform partial speech into clear output while preserving their natural voice characteristics
- Voice cloning requires only 10 seconds of audio with ElevenLabs

### 7.6 Voice Design for Those Who Never Spoke

SpeakUnique's three-tier service model addresses the full spectrum:

1. **Voice Build**: For those who can still speak intelligibly
2. **Voice Repair**: For those with impaired speech -- "SpeakUnique can 'repair' your voice and create a synthetic version"
3. **Voice Design**: For those who "have never had intelligible speech" -- users can "nominate up to 2 people to be your voice 'donors' -- people who share your desired regional accent, age and gender"

This is the first commercial service specifically designed for people who have never had a voice to bank, making it uniquely relevant for nonspeaking autistic individuals.

### 7.7 Apple Personal Voice for On-Device Privacy

Apple Personal Voice offers the most privacy-compliant path for school-based voice cloning:

- Requires approximately 15 minutes of reading on-screen phrases
- Voice model is "encrypted and stored securely on device"
- Uses Face ID/Touch ID/passcode for access
- Third-party AAC apps can request to use Personal Voice
- On-device processing eliminates cloud data privacy concerns
- Compatible with AssistiveWare's Proloquo products and other AAC apps

**Limitation for nonspeaking users**: Requires the user to read and speak phrases clearly enough for the system to model. This excludes nonspeaking individuals and many minimally speaking users.

## 8. Key Research Gaps Identified

1. **No direct studies** on autistic AAC users' preferences for synthetic vs. natural-sounding voices
2. **No longitudinal research** on how voice identity develops for children who grow up using AAC
3. **No data** on whether voice quality directly affects bullying rates for AAC users in schools
4. **Limited research** on nonspeaking autistic people's voice preferences -- most voice preference research involves speaking autistic adults
5. **No studies** on the impact of voice cloning on AAC adoption/abandonment rates
6. **No research** on school IT departments' readiness to support voice customization technology
7. **No controlled studies** comparing outcomes with matched vs. generic AAC voices for autistic students
8. **Minimal research** on gender-neutral/nonbinary voice options specifically in AAC contexts
9. **No research** on context-adaptive voice switching and its impact on autistic students' pragmatic language development

## 9. Recommendations for Practice

### For IEP Teams
- Include voice selection as a documented AT consideration in every AAC-related IEP
- Ensure the student (not just parents/SLP) has meaningful input into voice selection
- Re-evaluate voice appropriateness at every annual review, especially during puberty
- Document voice preferences as part of the student's communication profile

### For SLPs
- Offer voice trials with multiple options before finalizing voice selection
- Consider sensory sensitivities when selecting voices (pitch, tone, volume)
- Assess whether the student prefers more or less prosodic variation
- Stay current on voice cloning technology and privacy requirements
- Support part-time AAC users in having consistent voice identity across modalities

### For School Administrators
- Develop clear policies on biometric data (voiceprints) aligned with COPPA, FERPA, and state laws
- Favor on-device voice solutions to minimize data privacy risk
- Ensure reliable WiFi or provide offline-capable voice options
- Budget for dedicated devices (not shared) for AAC users
- Include AAC training in required professional development

### For Technology Developers
- Prioritize gender-neutral and gender-diverse voice options
- Develop age-progression voice features
- Create context-adaptive voice profiles (classroom, playground, home)
- Ensure robust offline functionality
- Design for user voice preference assessment, not just SLP/parent selection
- Offer "voice repair" for minimally speaking users, not just voice cloning for clear speakers
