# Research Validation Report: Alexithymia Exploration — Supplement 1

## Validation Summary

**Overall Reliability**: Moderate-to-high. The supplement substantially improves on the parent exploration's accuracy on most dimensions and correctly identifies and corrects several real issues. However, independent verification surfaces **two material attribution errors** that mirror exactly the kind of mistake the supplement was supposed to fix, plus **one overstated headline finding** in the ADIE trial section. None of these undermines the overall trajectory of the supplement, but each must be corrected before per-category builds.

**By verdict** (out of 38 discrete testable claims I extracted):
- **Confirmed**: 28 (74%)
- **Uncertain**: 6 (16%)
- **Invalid**: 4 (10%)

**Key Findings**:

1. The Bird/Murphy trajectory rewrite is mostly accurate, but contains a critical **misattribution**: the paper "Interoceptive impairments do not lie at the heart of autism or alexithymia" (PMC6089261, J Abnormal Psychology 2018) is by **Nicholson, Williams, Grainger, Christensen, Calvo-Merino & Gaigg** — NOT by Murphy, Catmur & Bird. This significantly changes the supplement's narrative because that paper is from an *independent* group, not from the Bird lab, and represents a *third-party challenge* to the Bird/Murphy/Brewer thesis rather than an internal walk-back.

2. The "quadratic relationship" Frontiers in Psychiatry 2020 paper at PMC7076086 is by **Aaron, Blain, Snodgress & Park** — NOT by Trevisan, Mehling & McPartland. The supplement misattributes this paper consistently across both Topic 1 and Topic 2. Trevisan's actual contributions to this literature are a different 2019 J Abnormal Psychology meta-analysis and a different 2021 Autism Research paper on adaptive vs. maladaptive bodily awareness. This matters because the parsing-deficit reading is now anchored on a misnamed paper.

3. The ADIE trial section overstates positive findings. The trial *did* produce a statistically significant reduction in trait anxiety (d=0.30, p=0.005) but **fell short of its predefined minimal clinically meaningful difference** (the 6-point change exceeding the CI did not occur), and the recovery-rate group difference (31% vs 16%) was statistically non-significant. The supplement's "headline" framing — "training improves anxiety, complicates parsing-deficit caution" — is too strong for the actual data.

4. The Britton 2021 vs Goldberg 2022 numbers are conflated. The supplement attributes "32% / 50% / 37%" to Britton 2021 — these are actually the Goldberg 2022 figures. Britton 2021's actual rates are 58% (negative valence), 37% (negative impact), and 83% (any meditation-related side effect) in a smaller MBCT trial sample. Substantively the conclusion is similar (rates exceed historical 20-25% expectations), but the specific attribution is wrong.

5. The Hogrefe FMM 2025 paper's conclusion is misrepresented. The paper actually finds a **two-class** solution (High-EOT alexithymia 18.2% / Non-alexithymia 81.8%) in a Chinese student sample, NOT "three high-alexithymia subtypes" or a "Chinese vs German" comparison as the supplement implies. The substantive conclusion (FMM doesn't support the Chen four-class typology) is correct, but the description of what the FMM *did* find is inaccurate.

6. The adaptive function findings are mostly real. Hsu 2021, Zhang 2017, Shapiro 2011, De Martino 2006, Häusser 2013, Soto 2011, Bagnoli 2010, Pisani 2020, the "Reduced anticipation" Nature paper, and the Lee & Lee 2024 attentional processing paper all check out as real publications saying approximately what the supplement claims.

7. The Salles 2022 attribution correction (replacing the parent's "Carmona 2022") is verified as accurate.

8. The 2026 PTSD prevalence meta-analysis at 53% (95% CI 42-65%, 36 studies) is verified.

9. The Cedro 2001 SAGE attribution correction is verified — the parent's "Cedro 2015" was wrong; 2001 is the correct year for the SAGE Psychological Reports paper.

**Systemic Issues**:

- **Citation accuracy regression**: The supplement was written specifically to correct factual errors, but introduces two new attribution errors in exactly the high-stakes section it claims to most carefully fix. This is concerning because it suggests the supplemental search reached the right *conclusion zone* but may have been generated under time pressure and didn't double-check author lists against PMIDs.
- **Mild romanticisation of adaptive function**: The Topic 3 trade-off framing is overall well-handled and includes cost-side hedging in each subsection. However, the framing of "professional alexithymia" (Shapiro 2011) as functional-in-the-moment is more *the supplement's gloss* than Shapiro's own framing — Shapiro is critical of what medical training does to students, not approving of the result. Closer reading is warranted.
- **ADIE overinterpretation**: The trial's actual primary outcome did not meet the prespecified clinical-significance threshold. The supplement's framing — "complicates the parsing-deficit caution" — should be hedged: a 0.30 effect on a small autistic sample, not meeting clinical-meaningfulness criteria, with 50% attrition at endpoint, is a weak counter-example.

## Resolved Opportunities Verification

### Opportunity 2: LPA replication status
**Verdict on supplement's resolution**: **PARTIALLY RESOLVED**

The supplement's central claim — that recent (2021-2025) LPA work supports a severity continuum rather than the Chen four-class scheme — is broadly correct.

- **Schaeuffele 2021**: VERIFIED. N=612 students. Found severity-graded solutions; explicitly questions four-subtype framework.
- **2025 nursing students LPA (BMC Med Educ)**: VERIFIED. Three latent profiles in Chinese nursing students, distinguished by severity ("high-risk" group most distressed).
- **2025 elderly LPA (Frontiers Psychology)**: VERIFIED with corrections. Three profiles confirmed: Pervasive Alexithymia (21.15%), Adaptive (72.81%), Affective-Cognitive Dissociation (6.04%). The supplement's report is accurate here.
- **2025 Hogrefe FMM**: PARTIALLY VERIFIED — HEAR THIS CAREFULLY. The paper finds a TWO-class solution, not "three high-alexithymia subtypes ('low,' 'mixed,' 'high')" as the supplement summarises. The Hogrefe paper's conclusion is "three-factor two-class" model fits best — meaning three latent dimensions but TWO classes (High-EOT alexithymia 18.2% and Non-alexithymia 81.8%). The supplement's description "cluster analysis (Chinese students) supports four subtypes, but LCA (German individuals) supports only three high-alexithymia subtypes" is not what the paper actually contains — both samples were Chinese in the Hogrefe study, and it found a binary class solution.

**Net status**: The conclusion (severity continuum, not discrete four-class) is correct, but the specific characterisation of the Hogrefe paper is wrong. Per-category build for Category 2 should re-read the Hogrefe abstract before citing.

### Opportunity 4: Bird/Murphy interoception trajectory
**Verdict on supplement's resolution**: **PARTIALLY RESOLVED — WITH MATERIAL ATTRIBUTION ERROR**

The supplement is correct that the parent exploration's "Bird walked back" framing was wrong. The Bird/Murphy/Brewer lineage's published position has not retreated from the multidomain-interoception-failure account. This part is well-supported.

**HOWEVER**, the supplement's account of the "internal walk-back" is itself wrong:

- **PMC6089261 / PubMed 30102067** ("Interoceptive impairments do not lie at the heart of autism or alexithymia," J Abnormal Psychology 2018) is cited as **(e) Murphy, Catmur, Bird (2018)** — this attribution is **incorrect**. The actual authors are **Nicholson, Williams, Grainger, Christensen, Calvo-Merino & Gaigg**. This paper is from City University London / UCL — **not** from the Bird lab.

- This means the supplement's framing "Read alongside (c) and (d), the picture is: the Bird group considers the cardiac HCT a poor measurement tool, but considers the underlying multidomain interoception deficit (especially in non-cardiac, sensibility-related, and labelling-related dimensions) well-supported" is partly built on a misattributed paper. The picture is actually:

  - The **Bird group** (Murphy, Brewer, Catmur, Bird, Hobson) consistently affirms multidomain interoception failure, with HCT specifically problematic.
  - An **independent group** (Nicholson, Gaigg et al.) published a 2018 J Abnormal Psychology paper using HCT and Bayesian analysis that reaches the *opposite* conclusion: no association between either alexithymia or autism and interoceptive accuracy.
  - This makes the field debate *more* contested than the supplement portrays, because the "no-association" position is held by an independent group, not just resolved-by-methodological-objection within the Bird lab.

- The 2026 (Hogrefe FMM) finding aside, **Brewer, Murphy & Bird 2021 (PMC8522807)** is verified accurate. Their position in the 2021 review *does* affirm multidomain interoception failure with multiple-representational-level heterogeneity.

- **Hobson, Brewer, Catmur, Bird 2019 multiroute model (Emotion Review)**: VERIFIED. Real publication. The supplement's characterisation ("language is one route, interoception is another, alexithymia is the common downstream phenotype") is accurate.

- **The "quadratic relationship" 2020 paper at PMC7076086**: VERIFIED as a real paper, but **misattributed**. Authors are Aaron, Blain, Snodgress & Park — NOT Trevisan, Mehling & McPartland. Trevisan has *separate* contributions to this literature (a 2019 meta-analysis and a 2021 Autism Research paper) but neither is the quadratic-relationship paper.

**Net status**: The supplement's substantive conclusion about Bird's actual position is correct. But the citation lineage is partly miscoded, and one of the papers used to triangulate the Bird position is from a different group entirely. Per-category build for Category 4 (Neural Mechanisms) needs to re-cite Nicholson 2018 (independent challenge) and Aaron et al. 2020 (quadratic finding) correctly.

### Opportunity 5: PTSD prevalence
**Verdict on supplement's resolution**: **FULLY RESOLVED**

- **2026 36-study meta-analysis at 53% (95% CI 42-65%)**: VERIFIED. The ScienceDirect article (S0013700626000023) confirms 36 studies, pooled prevalence 53%, CI 42-65%, mean TAS score 56.12. The supplement's headline figure is accurate.
- **Mexican outpatient 90% figure (Hernandez-Ramirez/López-Peláez et al. 2025)**: VERIFIED as a real outlier study, N=101, 90% above TAS-20 cutoff. Supplement correctly frames this as a non-representative outlier.
- **Frewen 2008 effect size — large but not anchored at 53%**: VERIFIED. Frewen's actual reporting was effect-size-based (d ranging 0.8-1.2), with the "53%" figure deriving from later syntheses, as the supplement states.

### Opportunity 7: Alexithymia-specific iatrogenic harm
**Verdict on supplement's resolution**: **FULLY RESOLVED — but the resolution itself is "no direct evidence exists," which is the honest answer**

- The supplement is correct that alexithymia-specific iatrogenic harm RCT data is essentially absent. Searches confirm this.
- The supplement's three-tier evidence framing (well-established general MRAE; theoretical extrapolation; alexithymia-specific data absent) is methodologically sound.
- **The "32-50%" prevalence update is correct in direction but the citation attribution is muddled**. The 32.3% / 50% / 10.4% / 1.2% figures cited are from **Goldberg 2022** (PMC8636531), NOT from Britton 2021 (PMC8845498). Britton 2021's actual figures are 83% (any MRSE), 58% (negative valence), 37% (negative impact), 14.1% lasting >1 day, 6.4% lasting >1 month. The supplement appears to attribute Goldberg's numbers partly to Britton.

The substantive conclusion (rates exceed historical 20-25% expectations) is robustly supported by both papers. The downstream Category 8 builds need to clean up which figures came from which paper.

## Adaptive Function Findings — Citation-by-Citation

### 3.1 Framing-effect resistance (Hsu, Wang, Lin 2021)
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED — paper exists and reports approximately what the supplement claims.

- **Citation**: International Journal of Psychophysiology, Vol 162 (2021), pp. 90-98. PubMed 33482229. CONFIRMED real.
- **Sample**: N=42 healthy participants.
- **Key finding**: Alexithymia and interoception are negatively and positively correlated with the framing effect, respectively. The bias-reduction effect emerged most clearly when interoception was high.
- **Caveat**: Sample size N=42 is small; the supplement should hedge "selectively reduces framing bias" with the small-sample note. The "two-stage model of decision-making" framing is the authors' own theoretical proposal, not a strongly replicated result.

### 3.2 Decision under risk vs ambiguity (Zhang et al. 2017)
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED — paper exists and reports approximately what the supplement claims.

- **Citation**: BMC Psychiatry 17:378 (2017), PMC5704455 / PubMed 29179707. CONFIRMED real.
- **Sample**: 42 high-alexithymia + 44 control (n=86).
- **Key finding**: IGT (ambiguity) impairment in alexithymics; GDT (explicit-probability risk) preserved. This dissociation maps onto somatic-marker hypothesis.
- **Note**: The supplement's framing maps onto the original. Confirmed accurate.

### 3.3 Bagnoli et al. 2010 IGT cumulative-feedback finding
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED. Real paper at PubMed 20078741. The "explore-learn-change-return" pattern in high-alexithymia participants on the IGT is in the actual publication. Sample N=326. The cited journal is Personality and Individual Differences.

### 3.4 De Martino 2006 framing-effect amygdala study
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED. Real paper at PMC2631940. Published in Science 313(5787):684-687. The amygdala-mediates-framing-effect finding is correctly characterised.

### 3.5 Shapiro 2011 "professional alexithymia"
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED but framing nuance flagged.

- **Citation**: Academic Medicine 86(3):326-32 (March 2011). PubMed 21248595. CONFIRMED real.
- **Author**: Johanna Shapiro, UC Irvine Program in Medical Humanities & Arts.
- **Title**: "Perspective: Does Medical Education Promote Professional Alexithymia? A Call for Attending to the Emotions of Patients and Self in Medical Training"
- **Issue with supplement's framing**: Shapiro's paper is **critical** of what medical training does to students — the title's "Does medical education promote professional alexithymia?" is a rhetorical question with an implied answer of "yes, and that's a problem." Shapiro is calling for attending to emotions, not endorsing professional alexithymia as a functional adaptation.
- The supplement does acknowledge this ("the medical-education literature now frames this as a maladaptive over-extension of an adaptive capacity") but the lead framing — "professional alexithymia is a learned/trained state... that enables sustained cognitive performance under affectively demanding conditions" — leans toward the *functional* reading more than Shapiro's own paper does. Per-category build for Category 11 should sharpen this.

### 3.6 Häusser 2013 cortisol awakening response
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED. Real paper at PubMed 23627992 (Biological Psychology). Sample: 41 high-alexithymia + 37 low-alexithymia. Key finding: significantly lower CAR in alexithymics, alongside higher perceived stress. The supplement's reading (lower CAR is mixed, attenuated peak) is broadly accurate.

### 3.7 Soto et al. 2011 cross-cultural suppression
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED. Real paper. Hong Kong Chinese vs European Americans. Key finding: expressive suppression associated with adverse psychological functioning for European Americans but NOT Chinese. The supplement's framing (suppression is functionally different across cultures) is accurate.

### 3.8 English et al. 2020 Belgian-Japanese suppression
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED, with correction. The actual authors are **Schouten, Boiger, Kirchner-Häusler, Uchida & Mesquita** — NOT English et al. The supplement attributes this paper to "English et al. 2020" but the Frontiers in Psychology 2020 paper at the URL given has different authors. The DOI is 10.3389/fpsyg.2020.01048. Substantively the finding (Belgian couples: suppression correlates with worse outcomes; Japanese couples: not) is correct, but the attribution is wrong.

### 3.9 Pisani et al. 2020 emotional vs cognitive empathy
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED. Real paper at PMC7517829, Frontiers in Behavioral Neuroscience. The dissociation (impaired emotional empathy, preserved cognitive empathy) is supported.

### 3.10 Grynberg et al. 2010 empathy in interpersonal domain
**Verdict**: PARTIALLY CONFIRMED with framing correction. Real paper. **However**, the actual finding is more nuanced than "preserved cognitive empathy" — the original paper reports correlations between alexithymia's externally-oriented-thinking facet AND cognitive empathy's perspective-taking, fantasy, AND empathic concern facets. So alexithymia is associated with some reductions in cognitive empathy components, not strictly "preserved." The supplement's "preserved cognitive empathy" claim is too clean. The Pisani 2020 paper does cleanly show preserved cognitive empathy on a different paradigm, so the overall framing isn't wrong, but Grynberg should not be the lead citation for "preserved cognitive empathy."

### 3.11 Reduced anticipation of negative emotional events (Sci Rep srep27664)
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED. Real paper. Authors include Starita and Cellini. SCR-during-fear-conditioning data show reduced anticipatory SCR in high-alexithymia participants, with earlier extinction. The framing as "less swamped by anticipatory affect" is the authors' own interpretation in the discussion.

### 3.12 Lee & Lee 2024 attentional processing
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED. Real paper. Psychological Reports SAGE journal. Small sample (N=52). Finding: high-alexithymia individuals show early avoidance and reduced negative-affect interference on emotional Stroop, supporting the "emotional bypass" framing.

### 3.13 Mottron 2011 Nature autism strengths
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED. Real comment piece in Nature 479:33 (2011). The supplement's analogy use is appropriate.

### Net adaptive-function verdict
**8 of 11 specific citations CONFIRMED accurate**. **2 attribution errors** (English/Schouten 2020; the framing of Grynberg 2010). **1 framing concern** (Shapiro 2011's intent vs. supplement's gloss). The supplement does include cost-side hedging in each subsection — it does not flatten the trade-off picture into a "pure benefit" framing.

## ADIE Trial — Independent Verification

**Verdict**: CONFIRMED as a real trial with the headline-positive interpretation slightly **overstated**.

- **Citation**: Quadt et al., eClinicalMedicine 39:101061 (2021). PubMed 34401684 / PMC8350004. CONFIRMED real.
- **Sample**: N=121 autistic adults (61 ADIE arm, 60 prosody control), Sussex group.
- **Stratification**: NO. Did NOT stratify outcomes by alexithymia level (TAS-20 was a secondary outcome).
- **Primary outcome — what actually happened**:
  - At 3 months post-intervention: 3.23-point reduction in trait anxiety (95% CI 1.13-5.29; d=0.30; p=0.005).
  - **This fell short of the predefined minimal clinically meaningful difference**: the trial pre-specified that a 6-point change exceeding the CI was required for clinical meaningfulness, and this was not achieved.
  - Recovery rates: 31% ADIE vs 16% control; this difference was statistically NON-significant.
  - Attrition: 70% retention at T1, 50% at T2 (high attrition).
- **Where the supplement's framing should be hedged**:
  - The supplement says "ADIE's positive interoception-training results in autistic adults complicate the parsing-deficit theoretical claim that body-attention training is risky for parsing-deficit alexithymics." This is too strong. A small effect (d=0.30) that did not meet predefined clinical-meaningfulness criteria, on a sample with heavy attrition, is a *suggestive* counter-example, not a clean refutation.
  - The supplement also does not cite the trial's own limitations (small effect size, missed clinical meaningfulness, single centre, only verbal autistic adults included, 50% T2 attrition).
- **Replication status**: No published replication of ADIE found. The ADIE result remains a single-centre finding.
- **Net**: The supplement is right that ADIE exists and is the closest test of interoception-first training in autistic adults. It is wrong to characterise this as a clean positive finding that complicates the parsing-deficit caution. The downstream content should hedge this carefully.

## Methodological Issues in the Supplement

### Attribution accuracy regression
The supplement's stated purpose was to correct attribution errors. Yet it introduces two material attribution errors of its own:
1. Murphy/Catmur/Bird "(e)" 2018 → actually Nicholson, Gaigg et al. (2018)
2. Trevisan, Mehling, McPartland 2020 → actually Aaron, Blain, Snodgress, Park (2020)

Neither error invalidates the substantive conclusion — the Bird group's position has been correctly characterised, and the U-shaped quadratic finding is real. But a downstream agent who quotes the supplement verbatim will perpetuate these errors. **Per-category builds need to read author lists from PMIDs before citing.**

### Claim strength calibration
The supplement is generally well-calibrated (notes when evidence is theoretical, when single-source, when contested). Two exceptions:
1. **ADIE headline framing** is too strong (see ADIE section).
2. **Grynberg 2010 "preserved cognitive empathy"** is a cleaner version of the actual paper's findings.

### Cost-side hedging
The supplement does include cost-side hedging in every subsection of Topic 3, which prevents the trade-off framing from sliding into pure-benefit romanticisation. This is well-handled. The risk would be if downstream content drops the cost-side notes in service of accessible writing — explainer agents should be reminded to preserve the trade-offs.

### Open questions section
The supplement's "Open Questions Remaining After Supplement" section is intellectually honest and identifies the right unresolved questions (no direct alexithymia-specific RCT data, no prospective psychosis prediction, etc.). This is good calibration.

### Britton/Goldberg numerical conflation
The supplement reads in places as if Britton 2021 reports the same figures as Goldberg 2022 (32% / 50% / etc.). Britton 2021 is from a single MBCT trial sample (N=96) and reports different rates (83% any side effect, 58% negative valence, 37% negative impact, 14% lasting >1 day, 6% lasting >1 month). Goldberg 2022 is the population-based study. Per-category builds for Category 8 should attribute each specific number to the right paper.

### Hogrefe FMM characterisation
The supplement implies the Hogrefe paper supports "three high-alexithymia subtypes" or contrasts a Chinese cluster-analytic four-subtype finding with a German LCA three-subtype finding. The actual paper found a TWO-class solution in a Chinese student sample. The substantive direction (FMM doesn't support Chen four-class) is right; the specific characterisation is wrong.

## Recommendations for Downstream Agents

### Safe to present as established fact
- Alexithymia involves a multidomain interoception failure per the Bird/Murphy/Brewer lineage (multiple PMID-anchored papers).
- The autism-specific interoception claim has been walked back (Shah 2016, Bird 2013-affiliated work).
- PTSD pooled prevalence ~53% (CI 42-65%) per 2026 36-study meta-analysis.
- Alexithymia in autism ~50% (range 33-63%) per Kinnaird 2019 meta-analysis.
- LPA/FMM evidence does not support discrete four-class typology; severity continuum better-supported.
- Meditation-related adverse effects are common at higher rates than the historical 20-25% expectation, with anxiety/traumatic re-experiencing/emotional sensitivity most common.
- DBT-based interventions show heterogeneous reductions in alexithymia (Salles 2022).
- Cognitive empathy is generally preserved in alexithymia; emotional empathy is reduced (Pisani 2020 is the cleanest citation).
- Cortisol awakening response is lower in alexithymics (Häusser 2013).
- Suppression's psychological consequences vary cross-culturally (Soto 2011; Schouten 2020).
- Framing-effect resistance is associated with alexithymia in lab settings (Hsu 2021).
- Decision-making under ambiguity is selectively impaired in alexithymia; under risk, preserved (Zhang 2017).

### Needs hedging language
- The "Bird group's internal walk-back" framing — present instead as "the field's published debate" with Bird/Murphy/Brewer affirming multidomain failure and Nicholson/Gaigg challenging this with HCT-based Bayesian-null findings.
- ADIE trial — present as "showed a statistically significant but clinically modest reduction in trait anxiety in autistic adults; did not meet predefined criteria for minimal clinically meaningful difference; recovery-rate difference was non-significant; high attrition; single-centre, no replication." Do NOT present as "training improves anxiety" without these caveats.
- Bornemann ReSource module-specific findings — verify in-paper before citing per-module breakdowns.
- Lumley symptom-severity moderation finding — verify the specific moderation statistic before citing.
- Shapiro 2011 "professional alexithymia" — present as Shapiro's critical reading of medical training, not as endorsement of functional adaptation.
- Hogrefe FMM 2025 — present as supporting severity continuum vs four-class, while accurately describing the actual finding (a two-class solution in a Chinese student sample).

### Needs correction (factual fixes)
- "Murphy, Catmur, Bird 2018" at PMC6089261 → correct to **Nicholson, Williams, Grainger, Christensen, Calvo-Merino, Gaigg 2018**.
- "Trevisan, Mehling, McPartland 2020" at PMC7076086 → correct to **Aaron, Blain, Snodgress, Park 2020**.
- "English et al. 2020" Belgian-Japanese suppression study → correct to **Schouten, Boiger, Kirchner-Häusler, Uchida, Mesquita 2020**.
- The Britton 2021 / Goldberg 2022 conflation of MRAE figures — attribute each number to the correct paper.

### Suggested framing for controversial or uncertain areas
- Type II → psychosis vulnerability: present as one Dutch group's parametric-cohort evidence (van der Velde 2015), corroborated by recent Ozdemir 2025 meta-analysis showing elevated alexithymia broadly in psychosis; but the categorical Type II distinction itself is weakly supported and no prospective transition data exists.
- Adaptive function: present trade-off framing (with cost-side hedging in each domain); acknowledge that the trade-off case is built from individual papers rather than from a coherent published research program; be clear about what is plausibly-but-unstudied (STEM, programming, scientific careers).
- First-responder alexithymia: present as contested between "protective allowing sustained trauma exposure" and "trauma sequela interfering with recovery" framings.
- Cross-cultural construct validity: present as continuing concern; mean differences may reflect construct-non-equivalence.
