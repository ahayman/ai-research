# Category 23: Framing and Frame Analysis -- Research & Theory

## Overview

Frame analysis is the study of how people, in interaction and in discourse, settle on an interpretation of *what kind of activity is going on here* -- and of how that interpretation, once settled, structures everything else that follows. Its central claim is that communication is impossible without a meta-layer specifying the type-of-message being exchanged. A bite, a word, an assessment, a meeting, a touch, a silence -- each becomes interpretable only once it has been placed inside a frame. The frame tells participants which moves count, which interpretations are allowed, which next-actions are licensed, and which contradictions are to be overlooked. Getting the frame wrong is not a minor misreading; it is a misunderstanding at a level above word-meaning.

This document traces the development of frame analysis from its foundations in the 1950s and 1960s through its contemporary use in sociology, cognitive linguistics, communication studies, and political discourse analysis. It covers Bateson's meta-communication, Goffman's primary frameworks and keyings, Fillmore's frame semantics, Lakoff's political framing, Entman's four functions, Tannen's interactive frames, the behavioral-economics framing effect of Kahneman and Tversky, and the therapeutic reframing tradition of Watzlawick and colleagues. It is written to support the main category document *category-23-framing-frame-analysis.md* and to serve as a reference layer for the practical and autism-specific research files.

**Relationship to other categories.** Frame analysis sits above the other pragmatic systems this hub documents. Implicature (Category 2), politeness (Category 3), register (Category 7), presupposition (Category 9), narrative (Category 12), topic management (Category 13), common ground (Category 16), and social hierarchy (Category 22) each operate *inside* a frame and are partially determined by which frame is in effect. Frame analysis is also the technical tool on which the Pragmatic Manipulation Sub-Hub depends for its treatment of frame attacks, frame swaps, and frame installations.

## 1. THE BATESON ORIGIN: META-COMMUNICATION

### 1.1 "A Theory of Play and Fantasy"

The modern study of framing begins with Gregory Bateson's essay "A Theory of Play and Fantasy," delivered in 1954 and collected in *Steps to an Ecology of Mind* (Bateson, 1972). Bateson, an anthropologist and cyberneticist, was watching monkeys at the Fleishhacker Zoo and noticed that they were engaged in what looked like fighting but plainly was not fighting: the nips they gave each other were not the bites that an actual fight would have involved. The monkeys had, somehow, communicated to each other that the activity was of a different type than it would superficially appear.

Bateson's conclusion was that ordinary animal and human communication includes a layer *above* the direct content -- a layer specifying what type of message the direct content is to be taken as. He called this layer **meta-communication**. A play-bite is a bite plus the meta-message *this is not a real bite*. The meta-message is not encoded in the physical act itself; it is encoded in the surrounding cues -- the posture, the rhythm, the participants' prior relationship, the absence of escalation. Without the meta-message, the act cannot be read correctly.

### 1.2 The Paradox of Play

Bateson treated play as the paradigmatic case because play illustrates the paradox cleanly. *This is play* is a meta-communicative statement that says, in effect: *these actions in which we are about to engage do not denote what the actions for which they stand would denote*. A play-bite means "bite," but a play-bite does not mean what a bite means. Play is structurally a frame transformation in exactly the sense Goffman would later elaborate: one set of actions being treated, by mutual agreement, as tokens of another type of action.

The insight generalizes: every act of communication carries a meta-layer specifying how it should be read. That meta-layer is the frame. If participants get the frame right, communication proceeds normally. If they get it wrong, the content may be clear while the type-of-thing is wrong -- and then the whole interpretation collapses.

### 1.3 Bateson's Legacy

Bateson's meta-communication became the theoretical starting point for several research traditions:

- **Erving Goffman** adopted Bateson's insight as the core of *Frame Analysis* (1974), citing Bateson explicitly.
- **The Palo Alto school** of family therapy -- Bateson, Weakland, Jackson, Haley, Watzlawick -- built clinical techniques on meta-communication, including the reframing work discussed in Section 6 below.
- **Dell Hymes** and the **ethnography of communication** tradition treated the recognition of speech-event types (a lecture, a greeting, a prayer) as a core component of communicative competence.
- **Conversation analysis** (Sacks, Schegloff, Jefferson) took up the question of how participants signal the type of activity in progress through the structural properties of talk.

Bateson's insight is now so widely assumed that it is easy to underestimate. Before Bateson, the dominant model treated communication as content-transmission: a speaker encodes a message, a listener decodes it. After Bateson, it became impossible to treat communication as content alone; the type-of-message layer has to be part of the model. Frames are what that layer contains.

## 2. GOFFMAN'S FRAME ANALYSIS

### 2.1 The Book and Its Argument

Erving Goffman's *Frame Analysis: An Essay on the Organization of Experience* was published in 1974. It is the longest of Goffman's books and the one in which he attempts a systematic account of how experience gets organized into interpretable types. Goffman's opening question -- "Under what circumstances do we think things are real?" -- is reformulated into the more tractable question *"What is it that's going on here?"* (Goffman, 1974, p. 8). The answer, for any given stretch of experience, is a frame.

Goffman's central claim is that the answer to *what's going on here* is never read off from the raw facts. It is always constructed through an interpretive schema that participants bring to the encounter. The schema determines which facts are relevant, which are to be overlooked, which actions count as moves, and which next-actions are licensed. Goffman treats these schemata as cultural objects -- learnable, teachable, contested, and historically variable.

### 2.2 Primary Frameworks

Goffman distinguishes two basic types of primary framework:

**Natural frameworks** are used to read events as unguided physical occurrences. The rain is falling. The rock fell off the cliff. The tree blew over. These readings do not involve agency, intention, or moral evaluation; they involve physics, biology, and chance. Natural frameworks answer *what's going on* with "something is happening in the natural world."

**Social frameworks** are used to read events as guided, intentional action by agents. She is arguing. He is apologizing. They are negotiating. These readings involve agency, intention, moral evaluation, and responsibility. Social frameworks answer *what's going on* with "an agent is doing something on purpose for some reason."

A single event can be read through either type of framework, and which framework gets applied makes an enormous interpretive difference. A man falling on the ground is a physical event (natural framework) if we think he slipped on ice; it is a performance (social framework) if we think he is doing stagecraft; it is an assault (a different social framework) if we think he was pushed. The event is the same; the frame differs; the interpretation differs accordingly.

### 2.3 Keys and Keyings

Goffman's great contribution in *Frame Analysis* is his systematic treatment of how primary frameworks get transformed. The core transformation is the **keying**: a primary framework is used as a model for another activity that is understood by all participants as *being patterned on* the original but *not* being an instance of it.

Goffman lists five major types of keying:
- **Make-believe** -- dramatic performance, fiction, pretense play.
- **Contests** -- boxing matches, wrestling, chess, games that model primary activities (fighting, competing) within rule-governed constraints.
- **Ceremonials** -- weddings, funerals, graduations, inaugurations that model primary social events as formal rituals.
- **Technical redoings** -- rehearsals, demonstrations, practice runs, training simulations.
- **Regroundings** -- activities pulled out of their normal contexts and used for something else (a doctor's office used for a film shoot, a chess game being played to test a computer's algorithms).

What all keyings share is that the transformation is *open*. Everyone knows it is happening. A rehearsal is not a real performance, and both actors and director know it. The knowledge is itself part of the frame.

### 2.4 Laminations

Goffman observed that keyings can be stacked. A **lamination** is a frame transformation applied to an already-transformed frame. A play (a keying of primary action) being rehearsed (a keying of the play) is a lamination. A story told at dinner about a play being rehearsed is a further lamination (the story is a keying of the rehearsal of the keying of the primary action).

Lamination is extremely common in ordinary conversation. Reported speech, quoted thoughts, imitations, hypotheticals, counterfactuals, and role-plays are all laminations. Each layer is a frame transformation; the interpretation of the content requires participants to track which layer they are in. When you tell a friend "and then my boss said, [imitated voice] 'I need this by Friday,'" the listener must track three layers: the original event (the boss speaking), the report (you telling the story), and the imitation (the vocal performance of the boss's voice). Laminations can get far deeper than this; competent speakers routinely navigate four or five layers without losing track.

### 2.5 Fabrications

A **fabrication**, in Goffman's terminology, is a frame transformation that is *not* open. One or more participants are intentionally kept in the wrong frame while others operate in the true frame. The asymmetry is the defining feature. A surprise party is a benign fabrication: the honoree thinks the frame is *ordinary evening* while everyone else knows the frame is *surprise party*. A con is an exploitative fabrication: the mark thinks the frame is *legitimate business deal* while the con artist knows the frame is *theft disguised as deal*.

Goffman distinguishes **benign fabrications** (done for the target's benefit or amusement, often revealed at the end) from **exploitative fabrications** (done to take advantage of the target). The structural form is identical in both; the difference is in purpose and outcome. Exploitative fabrications are the structural core of most manipulation. The sub-hub's treatment of frame attacks draws directly on this Goffmanian category: the manipulation target is being operated on from inside a frame they do not know is in effect, which gives the manipulator systematic informational and interpretive advantages.

### 2.6 Frame Breaks

When a frame fails mid-interaction, Goffman calls the moment a **frame break**. Frame breaks can be externally caused (a fire alarm interrupts a lecture), internally caused (a participant refuses to sustain the frame), or accidentally caused (an overheard remark reveals that the frame everyone was assuming was wrong). The subjective experience of a frame break is acute disorientation: for a moment, participants do not know what kind of activity they are in, and ordinary competence is suspended.

Frame breaks are useful diagnostic events. If you feel a sudden loss of footing in an interaction, a frame break has probably just happened. The question then is what the new frame is -- because usually, in the next few seconds, one will be proposed.

### 2.7 Frame Disputes

Participants can also disagree about which frame is in effect. Goffman calls these **frame disputes**. Unlike a frame break, a frame dispute is a sustained disagreement about the type of activity. One party thinks the conversation is a negotiation; another thinks it is a fight. One party thinks the touch was affectionate; another thinks it was an assault. Frame disputes are structurally different from content disagreements: they are not about *what happened* but about *what kind of thing what happened was*.

Frame disputes are extremely common in the contexts the Pragmatic Manipulation Sub-Hub covers. Coercive control contexts, family-of-origin contexts, and workplace-bullying contexts very often involve a sustained frame dispute in which one party is trying to impose a frame (*this is normal family life*, *this is normal workplace friction*) and the other party is trying to impose a different frame (*this is abuse*). Frame analysis is the technical vocabulary for that dispute.

## 3. FILLMORE AND FRAME SEMANTICS

### 3.1 The Cognitive-Linguistics Frame

Charles Fillmore's *frame semantics* is a different theoretical tradition from Goffman's frame analysis, though they share terminology and intersect in important ways. Fillmore developed his theory across several papers and book chapters in the 1970s and 1980s, most canonically in "Frame Semantics" (Fillmore, 1982) and "Frames and the Semantics of Understanding" (Fillmore, 1985).

Fillmore's core claim is that **words do not have meanings in isolation**; they have meanings relative to structured scenes that they evoke. To understand a word is to have access to the scene the word depends on. The verb *buy* cannot be understood apart from a scene involving a buyer, a seller, goods, money, and an exchange. That scene is the COMMERCIAL_TRANSACTION frame. The frame is the background; the word is a profile against the background.

### 3.2 Examples of Lexical Frames

Fillmore developed the idea with a series of influential examples:

- **COMMERCIAL_TRANSACTION frame.** Evoked by *buy*, *sell*, *pay*, *charge*, *cost*, *spend*. The frame has roles: Buyer, Seller, Goods, Money. Each verb profiles the same scene from a different angle -- *buy* foregrounds the Buyer's perspective; *sell* foregrounds the Seller's; *pay* foregrounds the Money-transfer; and so on.
- **RISK frame.** Evoked by *risk*, *gamble*, *stake*, *venture*. The frame has roles: Protagonist, Harm, Valued Object, Chance, Purpose, Decision-maker. Different uses of *risk* profile different configurations of these roles.
- **REVENGE frame.** Evoked by *avenge*, *revenge*, *retaliate*, *get even*, *payback*. The frame has roles: Offender, Harm, Avenger, Punishment, and a causal-temporal ordering.
- **BREAKFAST frame.** Fillmore's famous example: the word *breakfast* depends on a cultural frame involving eating, a morning period, the first meal of the day after a sleep period, and culturally typical foods. Without the frame, the word is uninterpretable.

### 3.3 Frames Install Commitments

The interpretive consequence is that **choosing a word is choosing a frame, and installing a frame commits you to the frame's entire structure**. Using *avenge* rather than *hit back* commits the speaker to the REVENGE frame with its moral weight. Using *venture* rather than *risk* commits the speaker to the RISK frame with an entrepreneurial coloring. Using *collateral damage* rather than *civilian deaths* installs a frame in which the deaths are incidental to a military objective -- which is why the choice of phrase has been politically contested.

Fillmore's semantics gives a precise technical basis for the otherwise vague intuition that word choice matters. Word choice matters because word choice is frame installation.

### 3.4 The FrameNet Project

Fillmore's theoretical work gave rise to **FrameNet** (Ruppenhofer, Ellsworth, Petruck, Johnson, & Scheffczyk, 2016), a computational lexicographic project based at the International Computer Science Institute in Berkeley. FrameNet catalogs thousands of English frames, their roles, and the words that evoke them. It has been extended to other languages and is used in natural language processing, translation, and second-language pedagogy. For the purposes of this category, FrameNet demonstrates that Fillmore's claim is not abstract -- the frames of English can be catalogued in practice, and the entries illustrate how tightly words are bound to the structured scenes they evoke.

## 4. LAKOFF AND POLITICAL / COGNITIVE FRAMING

### 4.1 Conceptual Metaphor and Framing

George Lakoff's work on framing extends the Fillmore tradition into political discourse and cognitive science. Lakoff and Mark Johnson's *Metaphors We Live By* (1980) had argued that much of abstract thought is structured by conceptual metaphors mapping concrete source domains onto abstract target domains (ARGUMENT IS WAR, LIFE IS A JOURNEY, IDEAS ARE OBJECTS). Lakoff's subsequent work on political framing treats these conceptual metaphors as the structural basis of ideological frames.

### 4.2 *Moral Politics* and the Strict-Father / Nurturant-Parent Model

In *Moral Politics* (Lakoff, 1996) and the more popular *Don't Think of an Elephant!* (Lakoff, 2004), Lakoff argues that American political discourse is organized around two competing frames rooted in different models of the family. The *strict-father* model frames the nation as a family needing discipline, authority, consequences, and self-reliance. The *nurturant-parent* model frames the nation as a family needing care, empathy, fairness, and collective responsibility. The two frames generate opposing positions on taxes, welfare, crime, foreign policy, and almost every other political issue -- because each frame pulls in a whole structured set of commitments, and positions on specific issues follow from the frame rather than from independent reasoning.

Lakoff's empirical claims have been contested -- political positions are not always as frame-consistent as his theory predicts -- but the methodological insight is well-supported and widely used in political communication research: **positions on specific policies are often downstream of the frame through which the underlying domain is being read**.

### 4.3 "Don't Think of an Elephant"

Lakoff's most widely cited observation is the title of the 2004 book. If you tell someone "don't think of an elephant," they will think of an elephant. Negating a frame activates the frame. The consequence for political communication is that **you cannot defeat a frame by denying it**. Denying a frame still runs the frame. To defeat a frame, you have to install a different frame -- one that addresses the same domain but organizes it differently.

This has direct application for the Pragmatic Manipulation Sub-Hub. "I am not crazy" runs the *crazy* frame; "I have specific evidence for this claim" installs a different frame. "I am not a bad parent" runs the *bad parent* frame; "here is what my child actually needs" installs a different frame. The protective reframe is always a *substitution*, not a denial.

### 4.4 Strategic Framing in Advocacy and Media

Lakoff's framing work has been taken up extensively in advocacy communication, public-health messaging, and political strategy. The FrameWorks Institute (based in Washington, DC) has produced empirical research on how different frames around issues like child development, climate change, and racism affect public understanding and policy preferences. The consistent finding is that frames -- not individual facts -- drive whether information is integrated, accepted, and acted upon. Public-health campaigns that present facts inside a misaligned frame routinely fail to move behavior, while campaigns that install an appropriate frame first and then deliver the facts inside it succeed. Frames are the containers; without the right container, the content does not carry.

## 5. ENTMAN AND THE FOUR FUNCTIONS

### 5.1 "Framing: Toward Clarification of a Fractured Paradigm"

Robert Entman's 1993 article in the *Journal of Communication* is the most-cited single definition of framing in communication studies. Entman's goal was to consolidate a fragmented literature by offering a precise, operationalizable definition. He wrote: "To frame is to select some aspects of a perceived reality and make them more salient in a communicating text, in such a way as to promote a particular problem definition, causal interpretation, moral evaluation, and/or treatment recommendation" (Entman, 1993, p. 52).

### 5.2 The Four Functions

Entman's definition identifies four distinct functions a frame can perform:

1. **Problem definition.** What is the frame saying the problem is? A dispute can be framed as a misunderstanding, a disagreement, a fight, or an act of abuse. Each framing names a different problem.
2. **Causal diagnosis.** What is the frame saying caused the problem? Individual disposition, situational pressure, systemic factors, external circumstances -- the same event admits radically different causal diagnoses depending on the frame.
3. **Moral evaluation.** Who is the frame holding responsible, blameless, virtuous, or at fault? Moral assignment is baked into the frame and usually travels silently with it.
4. **Treatment recommendation.** What is the frame suggesting should be done? Treatments are implicit in frames: if the problem is X and the cause is Y, the frame usually points toward a particular remedy.

A single frame move can accomplish all four functions simultaneously. "She's hormonal" defines the problem (emotional dysregulation), diagnoses the cause (biology), evaluates morally (she is irrational), and recommends treatment (discount her, wait it out, medicate). The efficiency is precisely why frames are so powerful. One word installs an entire interpretive package.

### 5.3 Entman's Framework as a Diagnostic

For the purposes of this hub, Entman's four functions serve as a diagnostic checklist. When you suspect a frame is being installed on a situation -- by an interlocutor, an institution, a record, or your own inner voice -- you can ask:

- What is this frame saying the problem *is*?
- What is this frame saying *caused* the problem?
- Who is this frame saying is *at fault*?
- What is this frame saying should be *done*?

If any of the four answers feels wrong when you examine it explicitly, the frame is likely to be misaligned with the situation. If all four feel wrong, the frame is a frame attack and needs to be refused and replaced.

Entman's definition also clarifies why framing is hard to resist from inside: when all four functions are being performed simultaneously, arguing against one of them leaves the others in place. You can object to the moral evaluation while still accepting the problem definition and causal diagnosis -- in which case the frame continues to operate. Effective refusal targets the whole package, typically by substituting a different frame rather than contesting components individually.

## 6. TANNEN AND INTERACTIVE FRAMES

### 6.1 Tannen's Contribution

Deborah Tannen's *Framing in Discourse* (1993, edited volume) and her individual articles extended frame analysis into linguistic and discourse-analytic territory. Tannen's core contribution is showing how frames are signalled in real interaction -- through prosody, lexical choice, pronoun use, tense, paraphrase, repetition, reported speech, and the pragmatic markers that pattern around frame shifts. Frames, in Tannen's work, are not abstract cognitive structures imposed on interaction; they are produced, sustained, and contested in the moment-to-moment details of how people talk.

Tannen's article "What's in a frame?" (1993) uses a videotape of participants describing a film they had just watched to show how different speakers brought different expectations to the same material -- different frames -- and how those expectations surfaced in the specific linguistic features of their descriptions.

### 6.2 Tannen and Wallat: Interactive Frames in Pediatric Examinations

The most widely cited empirical paper in the frame-analysis literature is Deborah Tannen and Cynthia Wallat's (1987) "Interactive Frames and Knowledge Schemas in Interaction." The study examined a videotape of a pediatrician conducting an examination of a child with cerebral palsy. The pediatrician was simultaneously operating in multiple frames:

- **The examination frame** (with the child): playful, reassuring, simple vocabulary, singsong prosody.
- **The consultation frame** (with the mother): informative, clinical but accessible, answering questions.
- **The reporting frame** (dictating into a tape recorder for the medical record): formal, technical, third-person, full clinical vocabulary.

The pediatrician shifted among these frames rapidly, cued by whom she was addressing. Each shift reorganized her vocabulary, her prosody, her posture, and her stance. Tannen and Wallat showed that the shifts were systematic, trackable, and diagnostic of the frame in effect at any given moment.

Their other contribution was distinguishing **interactive frames** (the type of activity -- examination, consultation, reporting) from **knowledge schemas** (the participants' expectations about the content of the activity, e.g., what a routine pediatric examination involves). The two are analytically distinct and can come apart: the mother's knowledge schema about examinations was different from the pediatrician's, and the resulting mismatch generated friction even when the interactive frame was aligned.

### 6.3 Frames in Real-Time Interaction

Tannen's broader work on conversational style (*That's Not What I Meant!*, 1986; *You Just Don't Understand*, 1990) applies frame analysis to ordinary misunderstandings between people who do not share the same interactive conventions. Tannen argues that many everyday conflicts are not about content -- they are frame disputes, driven by participants applying different frames to the same situation. One person reads an argument as a fight requiring winners and losers; another reads it as a collaborative problem-solving exercise. One reads a question as a request for information; another reads it as an invitation to connect. The content does not settle the dispute, because the dispute is at the frame level.

This application of frame analysis is particularly relevant to the double empathy problem in autism (covered in the autism research file): frame mismatches between autistic and non-autistic interlocutors are real, bidirectional, and not usefully described as "deficits" on either side.

## 7. KAHNEMAN AND TVERSKY: THE FRAMING EFFECT

### 7.1 "The Framing of Decisions and the Psychology of Choice"

Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky's 1981 paper in *Science* introduced the **framing effect** in behavioral economics. The effect is that people's preferences over choices change depending on how the choices are described -- even when the underlying options are mathematically identical. The most famous example is the Asian Disease Problem:

- Version A: A disease is expected to kill 600 people. Program 1 will save 200 lives for certain. Program 2 will save all 600 with 1/3 probability and 0 with 2/3 probability. Most respondents choose Program 1.
- Version B: A disease is expected to kill 600 people. Program 1 will result in 400 deaths for certain. Program 2 will result in 0 deaths with 1/3 probability and 600 deaths with 2/3 probability. Most respondents choose Program 2.

The options are mathematically identical across versions. The difference is only in the framing: lives-saved (gain frame) versus lives-lost (loss frame). Respondents are risk-averse in the gain frame and risk-seeking in the loss frame, systematically reversing their preferences based on how the same options are described.

### 7.2 Implications

Kahneman and Tversky's framing effect is narrower than Goffman's or Lakoff's framing -- it concerns specifically how the wording of decision options affects risk preferences -- but it demonstrates the same underlying point: **the container in which content is placed changes how the content is processed**. You cannot get to the content without passing through the frame, and the frame is always doing interpretive work whether participants notice it or not.

For the hub's purposes, the framing effect is the experimental cousin of the Goffmanian and Lakoffian framing concepts: it gives a laboratory demonstration of the phenomenon that the more qualitative frame-analytic traditions describe in natural settings.

## 8. REFRAMING AS TECHNIQUE: WATZLAWICK AND THE PALO ALTO SCHOOL

### 8.1 Brief Therapy and the Reframing Move

Paul Watzlawick, John Weakland, and Richard Fisch's *Change: Principles of Problem Formation and Problem Resolution* (1974) developed reframing into a core technique of brief therapy at the Mental Research Institute in Palo Alto. The book's central argument is that many problems are sustained not by the underlying facts but by the *frame* through which those facts are being read -- and that changing the frame can dissolve the problem without changing the facts.

Watzlawick et al. distinguished two types of change. **First-order change** is change *within* the current frame: trying harder, trying differently, trying the opposite. First-order change often fails because the frame itself is the problem, and operations within the frame cannot escape it. **Second-order change** is change *of* the frame: substituting a different interpretive schema so that the situation becomes tractable. Second-order change is what reframing aims at.

### 8.2 Reframing in Practice

A classic Watzlawick example: a couple in conflict because one partner is "too controlling" and the other is "not taking enough responsibility." Each partner's efforts to solve the problem (the controlling one pulls back; the irresponsible one waits for instructions) reinforce the pattern, because both are operating inside the frame *the problem is the other person's personality*. A second-order reframe -- "you are collaborators in a pattern that neither of you wants" -- substitutes a different frame in which both partners are on the same side against a shared pattern. The new frame makes different next-actions available.

Reframing is morally neutral as a technique. It can be used to help people see their situations more accurately, or it can be used to install inaccurate frames that serve the reframer's interests. The Watzlawick tradition is careful to distinguish the two, but the technique itself is not self-policing. This is why the Pragmatic Manipulation Sub-Hub treats reframing as both a protective tool and a manipulative tool, with frame analysis as the diagnostic that lets you tell them apart.

### 8.3 Reframing in Cognitive Therapy

Reframing became a central technique in cognitive therapy as well (Beck, 1976). Cognitive reappraisal -- the deliberate substitution of a more accurate or adaptive interpretation for a distorted one -- is the clinical form of reframing. Decades of empirical research (Gross, 2002; Ochsner & Gross, 2005) show that cognitive reappraisal can reduce negative affect and change physiological stress responses, though the size and durability of the effects depend on the context and the individual. For this hub, the relevant point is that reframing is not mystical; it is a well-studied technique with a coherent mechanism.

## 9. MEDIA, POLITICS, AND PUBLIC DISCOURSE FRAMES

### 9.1 Framing in Media Studies

Framing research has been extensive in media studies. Gitlin's *The Whole World Is Watching* (1980) documented how media frames shape the public reception of social movements. Iyengar's *Is Anyone Responsible?* (1991) distinguished **episodic frames** (presenting an issue as a specific event or individual case) from **thematic frames** (presenting an issue as a general pattern requiring systemic interpretation) and showed experimentally that the two produce different attributions of responsibility: episodic frames attribute responsibility to individuals, thematic frames to systems. The finding has been replicated across many issues and remains a touchstone in media-effects research.

Scheufele (1999) synthesized the media-framing literature into a process model: frame-building (how frames get into media content), frame-setting (how media frames interact with audience schemas), and frame-effects (how audience responses feed back). The model is now standard in political communication research.

### 9.2 Framing in Political Interviews and Public Rhetoric

Bull and colleagues have studied framing in political interviews, showing how politicians use frame shifts to evade questions, recast hostile premises, and install preferred problem-definitions (Bull, 2003). The moves include: challenging the presupposition of the question, substituting a different question, reframing the issue into a different domain, and using anecdotes to install episodic frames that block thematic ones. These moves are not unique to politics -- they appear in workplace meetings, family arguments, and institutional encounters -- but the political-interview setting makes them especially visible.

### 9.3 Framing in Social Movement Research

Benford and Snow (1988, 2000) developed the concept of **collective action frames** in the social-movement literature. Movements succeed in part by constructing frames that diagnose a problem, identify a cause, and prescribe action in ways that mobilize participation. The framing process is contested: competing movements offer different frames for the same issue (e.g., *reproductive rights* versus *pro-life*), and which frame achieves wider uptake affects mobilization, policy, and public opinion. The social-movement framing literature provides an extended case study of how frame contests play out at scale.

## 10. FRAMES AS INSTITUTIONAL AND RECORD-BASED STRUCTURES

### 10.1 Institutional Frames

Institutions maintain frames that outlast any single interaction. The frames *student*, *patient*, *employee*, *defendant*, *claimant*, *service user*, *resident* -- each one is an institutional frame that the institution installs on a person and then maintains across encounters. Goffman's later work on **total institutions** (*Asylums*, 1961) and on stigma (*Stigma*, 1963) examined how institutions impose frames that restructure the self-understanding of the people inside them. Foucault's analyses of discipline, clinical medicine, and psychiatric classification run adjacent to Goffman's and make complementary points about how institutional frames organize who counts as normal and who counts as a problem.

### 10.2 Records as Pre-Installed Frames

Records are a particularly durable form of institutional framing. An educational record, a medical chart, a social-services assessment, an HR file, or a court document frames the person who is its subject *before* the next reader encounters that person. The next reader approaches the person through the record, reading them in terms of what the record says. The record is, in Goffmanian terms, a frame that has already been installed and is already running when the interaction begins.

For the Pragmatic Manipulation Sub-Hub, this is structurally important. Educational records of autistic children often carry frames of difficulty, disruption, and deficit that shape how subsequent educators read those children. Social-services assessments of autistic adults carry frames that shape subsequent eligibility and treatment decisions. Medical records carry frames that shape subsequent clinical interactions. The autistic person is not starting from a neutral interpretive position; they are starting from inside a frame that has been written about them by someone else, and contesting the frame requires contesting the record. Frame analysis is the technical tool for recognizing the record *as* a frame rather than as a transparent description.

## 11. CRITIQUES AND LIMITATIONS OF FRAME ANALYSIS

### 11.1 The Concept-Definition Problem

Entman's 1993 article was titled "Framing: Toward Clarification of a Fractured Paradigm" precisely because the field was (and still is) characterized by definitional inconsistency. Different authors use "frame" to mean different things: a cognitive schema, a rhetorical device, a lexical structure, a cultural narrative, a meta-communicative cue, an institutional classification. Entman tried to consolidate; others (D'Angelo, 2002; Van Gorp, 2007) have continued the work. The practical consequence for this hub is that "framing" is not a single unified theory but a family of related approaches with overlapping concerns. This document treats that family in its entirety and does not try to force a single definition on it.

### 11.2 The "Everything Is a Frame" Problem

Because frames are so pervasive -- every utterance is framed, every situation is framed, every record is framed -- there is a risk of the category becoming vacuous. If everything is a frame, the concept loses diagnostic power. The response (developed in the practical-research file) is to focus on cases where framing is doing interpretive work that *could be different* -- where an alternative frame is available, where the current frame is contested, or where the frame is installing consequences that a different frame would not install. Those are the cases where frame analysis earns its keep.

### 11.3 Empirical Grounding

Goffman's *Frame Analysis* is a work of theoretical sociology, not empirical research, and some of its claims have never been rigorously tested. The Kahneman-Tversky framing effect, the Tannen-Wallat study, and the media-framing experimental literature (Iyengar, Scheufele, Nelson) provide stronger empirical grounding than the core Goffmanian claims, but even in those traditions, the concept of a "frame" is sometimes operationalized loosely. For this hub, the honest summary is: the framing concept is extraordinarily useful, is supported across multiple traditions, and has experimental support in several specific domains; but it is not a settled theory with a single testable formulation, and readers should hold the claims with appropriate calibration.

### 11.4 Power and Framing

A separate critique, from critical theory and from media sociology, is that Goffman's frame analysis under-theorizes power. Who gets to install a frame is a power question, and the distribution of framing power is not equal. Institutions frame individuals; employers frame employees; assessors frame those assessed; majorities frame minorities; adults frame children. Frame analysis that ignores these asymmetries risks treating all framings as symmetric interpretive negotiations when in practice the outcome is often settled by who has institutional backing. The practical and autism research files foreground this asymmetry explicitly, because for autistic readers the asymmetry is often the load-bearing feature.

## 12. CROSS-DISCIPLINARY CONNECTIONS

Frame analysis connects to several adjacent theoretical traditions in ways worth noting briefly.

- **Script theory** (Schank & Abelson, 1977). Scripts are stereotyped event sequences (the RESTAURANT script, the DOCTOR_VISIT script). Scripts are closely related to Fillmore's frames and to Tannen and Wallat's knowledge schemas. The vocabulary differs but the core idea -- structured background knowledge organizing interpretation -- is the same.
- **Schema theory** in cognitive psychology. Bartlett's (1932) classic work on memory showed that remembered material is reconstructed through schemas that fit cultural expectations. Schemas operate at a level that overlaps with frames, particularly in Tannen and Wallat's sense of knowledge schemas.
- **Relevance theory** (Sperber & Wilson, 1986; Category 10 of this hub). Relevance theory treats interpretation as the search for the intended meaning given the cognitive context. The cognitive context includes what we would call frames, though relevance theory uses its own vocabulary.
- **Conversation analysis** (Sacks, Schegloff, Jefferson). CA shares with frame analysis an interest in how participants signal and recognize the type of activity in progress, though CA tends to work at a finer grain of sequential detail.
- **Critical discourse analysis** (Fairclough, 1989; Wodak, 2001). CDA foregrounds the ideological and power-laden dimensions of framing, treating frames as sites of political struggle.
- **Narrative theory**. Narratives install frames over extended stretches of experience; frame analysis and narrative analysis are natural partners and are cross-linked throughout this hub (see Category 12).

## 13. SUMMARY: WHAT THE THEORY GIVES US

Frame analysis offers, across its several traditions, a coherent set of tools for understanding how experience gets organized into interpretable types. The essentials:

1. **Meta-communication** (Bateson): every utterance carries a layer specifying what kind of message it is.
2. **Primary frameworks** (Goffman): every situation is read through either a natural or a social framework, which determines whether agency and responsibility are in play.
3. **Keyings and fabrications** (Goffman): frames can be transformed openly (keyings) or covertly (fabrications), and covert transformations are the structural basis of manipulation.
4. **Lexical frames** (Fillmore): words evoke structured scenes, so word choice is frame choice.
5. **Cognitive and political frames** (Lakoff): frames resist direct negation and can only be displaced by substitution.
6. **The four functions** (Entman): frames define problems, diagnose causes, evaluate morally, and recommend treatments, and any frame can be examined against these four.
7. **Interactive frames** (Tannen, Tannen and Wallat): frames are produced and maintained in the moment-to-moment linguistic details of talk, and frame shifts are trackable through those details.
8. **The framing effect** (Kahneman and Tversky): the same options produce different preferences when framed differently, giving experimental proof that frames are doing interpretive work.
9. **Reframing** (Watzlawick et al., Beck, Gross): substituting a different frame is a well-understood technique that can help or harm depending on whether the substituted frame is more or less accurate.
10. **Institutional and record-based frames**: frames persist in documents and institutions, pre-loading subsequent interactions.

Taken together, these give us the vocabulary we need to describe what is happening when a frame is being installed, maintained, contested, swapped, or broken. The practical and autism research files apply this vocabulary to the specific situations where autistic readers need it most.
