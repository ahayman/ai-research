# Image Schemas as Visual Building Blocks

## The Cognitive Geometry Beneath Language

Before you ever spoke a word, you understood containers. You understood paths. You understood force.

At six months old, you dropped a toy into a cup and fished it out. That act — putting something inside a bounded region, watching it disappear behind a boundary, then retrieving it — was not just play. It was the formation of a cognitive structure that would organize your thought for the rest of your life: the CONTAINER schema. Interior. Boundary. Exterior. Things can be in, out, or at the edge. This structure was in place before you had any word for "in" or "out" or "inside." The words, when they arrived later, mapped onto a pattern your mind had already built from physical experience.

This is the fundamental insight of image schema theory, first articulated by Mark Johnson in *The Body in the Mind* (1987): the recurring spatial-temporal patterns that arise from our pre-linguistic bodily interaction with the world form the cognitive infrastructure on which all later thought — including language — is constructed. These patterns are not mental images (they are too abstract for that) and they are not verbal concepts (they exist before language). They are something more elemental: the spatial geometry of experience itself, distilled into schematic form by an infant mind that is relentlessly pattern-detecting from the moment it opens its eyes.

For the project of designing a visual language that could express any human thought, image schemas offer something extraordinary. They are simultaneously pre-linguistic (acquired before any spoken language develops), embodied (arising from universal bodily experience), and inherently spatial (naturally expressible as visual forms). Unlike semantic primes, which are defined verbally and include many concepts resistant to visualization (IF, BECAUSE, TRUE), image schemas are the kind of thing you can draw. Johnson himself drew them — simple circles for containers, arrows for paths, thick lines for forces. The question is whether these drawings can become the foundation of a complete visual language.

## The Inventory: Patterns the Body Teaches the Mind

Johnson's original inventory identifies at least sixteen recurring schemas, though the list has never been closed — different researchers include different schemas, and the boundary between a "basic" schema and a composition of simpler schemas remains debated. The core inventory includes:

**Spatial schemas** form the largest family. The CONTAINER schema (interior, boundary, exterior) is grounded in the body itself — your skin is the first container you ever know, separating your insides from the external world. Every cup, room, box, and building reinforces the pattern. The PATH schema (source, trajectory, goal) emerges from every experience of movement from one place to another — crawling across a room, walking to a parent, throwing a ball. The UP-DOWN schema captures the asymmetry of the vertical axis: up correlates with more, with active, with conscious (you stand up when awake, lie down when sleeping; piles grow upward). NEAR-FAR encodes the significance of distance — near things can be grasped, far things require effort to reach. CENTER-PERIPHERY distinguishes focal attention from background awareness. CONTACT records the tactile event of surfaces meeting. SURFACE itself represents the extended plane that supports objects.

**Force schemas** capture the dynamics of physical interaction. The FORCE schema in its full elaboration includes compulsion (being pushed), blockage (being stopped), counterforce (pushing back), enablement (a path cleared), attraction (being pulled toward), and diversion (being redirected). BALANCE represents equilibrium around an axis — the fulcrum that your body maintains every time you stand upright. LINK captures the connection between entities, grounded in holding hands, chains, bridges, the experience of social bonds made tangible. SCALE records continuous variation along a dimension: size, intensity, brightness varying as a gradient rather than as discrete steps.

**Multiplicity schemas** handle quantity and process. PART-WHOLE represents the relationship of components to a larger entity — your hand is part of your body, a wheel is part of a car. SPLITTING and MERGING capture divergence and convergence — one thing becoming many, many things becoming one. CYCLE records recurring paths that return to their starting point, grounded in heartbeat, breathing, day-night-day. ITERATION captures the repetition of identical actions: the hammering, the walking, the rhythmic pattern.

**State schemas** describe conditions. FULL-EMPTY marks the capacity of a container — abundance versus depletion. OBJECT (or COLLECTION) establishes the fundamental fact that there are bounded, discrete things in the world.

Five properties distinguish image schemas from other cognitive structures. They are **pre-linguistic** — acquired before language develops. They are **embodied** — arising from physical interaction, not verbal instruction. They are **spatial** — inherently structured in space and time. They are **schematic** — abstract patterns, not specific images (the CONTAINER schema is not a picture of any particular cup; it is the abstract structure of containment that applies to cups, rooms, bodies, and metaphorically to emotional states). And they are **productive** — they combine with each other to form more complex structures, generating enormous expressive potential from a small set of primitives.

## How Infants Build Schemas: Mandler's Perceptual Meaning Analysis

The developmental psychologist Jean Mandler proposed the mechanism by which infants form image schemas from raw sensory experience. She called it Perceptual Meaning Analysis (PMA) — a data summarization device that reformats the rich, continuous, multi-modal sensory input that floods an infant's perception into a much sparser, more abstract code. PMA is not conscious extraction. It is an innate, domain-general attentional mechanism that generates concepts from attended perceptual data.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-2-developmental-sequence.html -->

The developmental sequence begins with rich perceptual input — the infant's world of moving objects, contacted surfaces, filled and emptied containers, agents acting on patients. Certain spatial information is innately salient to the infant mind: paths of object motion that change spatial relations (a ball rolling into a cup), objects going into or out of sight, contingencies between paths (one object chasing another), and the critical distinction between self-moving entities and caused-motion entities (the difference between things that move on their own and things that are pushed). This last distinction — animacy detection — is one of the earliest and most robust cognitive achievements, observable in infants as young as three months.

Through repeated exposure, PMA consolidates these spatial observations into schematic patterns. The repeated experience of "things going into bounded regions" consolidates into the CONTAINER schema. The repeated experience of "entity moving from here to there" consolidates into the PATH schema. The repeated experience of "one entity causing another to move" consolidates into the CAUSED MOTION composition (FORCE + PATH).

These schemas then serve as the scaffolding for initial broad concepts. The concept ANIMAL emerges not from the perceptual features that distinguish animals (legs, fur, eyes — which vary wildly between a dog and a fish) but from the motion pattern: animals move on their own (self-propelled motion). The concept VEHICLE emerges from the opposite pattern: vehicles move only when caused to move by an external agent. Infants as young as nine months distinguish these categories — not by appearance, but by motion pattern. The image schemas come first; the category distinction follows.

This developmental priority is the strongest argument for image schemas as the foundation of a visual language. If schemas are the first concepts every human constructs — prior to and independent of any spoken language — then they represent the deepest possible level of cross-linguistic, cross-cultural cognitive universality. A visual system built on image schemas would be grounded in cognitive structures that every human develops in infancy, regardless of which language they will later learn.

## DISL: The Proof That Visual Formalization Works

The most important development for the visual language project is the Diagrammatic Image Schema Language (DISL), published by Maria Hedblom, Fabian Neuhaus, and Till Mossakowski in *Spatial Cognition and Computation* (published online 2024, in Volume 25, 2025). DISL takes the informal diagrams that Johnson and other cognitive linguists have drawn for decades and formalizes them into a complete visual notation system with defined symbols for conceptual primitives and explicit composition rules for combining them.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-3-disl-notation.html -->

DISL defines a set of visual symbols for conceptual primitives drawn primarily from developmental psychology — specifically from Mandler and Pagan Canovas's work on spatial primitives. Objects are represented as circles (filled for active agents, outlined for passive patients). Paths are represented as arrows (solid for actual motion, dashed for potential or unrealized motion). Regions are represented as bounded rectangles (solid boundary for impermeable containers, dashed for permeable ones). Forces are represented as thick arrows, with width encoding magnitude. Contacts are represented by two entities sharing a boundary point.

The crucial innovation is DISL's composition rules, which define how primitives combine within panels following spatial logic. An object placed inside a region represents containment. An object connected to a path arrow represents traversal. An object at the receiving end of a force arrow represents a patient undergoing causal action. These compositions are not arbitrary — they follow the same spatial logic that the schemas themselves embody.

For temporally extended scenarios, DISL uses a strip format inspired by comic strip conventions: a sequence of panels read left to right, each depicting a specific image-schematic scene. The strip as a whole conveys a temporal narrative. This is the same cognitive tool that humans have used for millennia — sequential visual panels representing the flow of events through time, from Egyptian tomb paintings to modern graphic novels.

DISL also includes a logical exchange format that makes its diagrams machine-readable, enabling computational processing of image schema representations. This dual nature — human-readable visual diagrams that are simultaneously machine-parseable formal structures — is exactly what a practical visual language would need.

What DISL demonstrates, conclusively, is that image schemas *can* be given formal, unambiguous visual representations. A small set of conceptual primitives generates a wide range of schemas through composition. The composition is cognitively motivated, not arbitrary. The resulting representations are readable and interpretable. This is not a theoretical possibility; it is a working system.

What DISL does not yet do is equally important to understand. It is a descriptive, analytical notation — a tool for researchers to formally describe image schemas, not a productive communication system for expressing original thoughts. It represents spatial-physical scenarios effectively but does not address abstract domains (emotions, logic, social relationships). It lacks a mechanism for metaphorical extension (mapping spatial schemas onto non-spatial meanings). It is designed for cognitive science papers, not for everyday messaging. DISL is a powerful foundation that needs several additional layers — compositionality rules, metaphorical mappings, a phonetic subsystem — to become a language.

## The Metaphorical Bridge: From Space to Abstract Thought

The most consequential claim of image schema theory is not about space. It is about everything else. George Lakoff and Mark Johnson demonstrated in *Metaphors We Live By* (1980) that abstract thought is systematically structured by metaphorical mappings from embodied experience. These are not literary flourishes. They are the cognitive architecture of reasoning about non-spatial domains.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-4-metaphor-mapping-chain.html -->

The chain works like this. During early development, spatial experiences systematically co-occur with non-spatial experiences. Piling objects makes piles go higher — more stuff correlates with upward spatial position. This experiential correlation generates a primary metaphor: MORE IS UP. The spatial schema (VERTICALITY) becomes the cognitive infrastructure for reasoning about quantity. You say "prices are high" and "morale is low" and "production rose" and "standards fell" — not because you lack other vocabulary, but because the vertical schema IS your cognitive model for quantity.

Joseph Grady's Primary Metaphor Hypothesis (1997) identifies the specific sensorimotor correlations that generate these mappings and argues they are universal because the bodily experiences that produce them are universal. All humans have bodies that interact with gravity, containers, paths, and forces. Key primary metaphors and their schema sources include:

- **PURPOSES ARE DESTINATIONS** (PATH schema): Achieving goals requires moving toward them. "I'm on the path to success." "We've arrived at a conclusion." "She's lost her way."
- **STATES ARE CONTAINERS** (CONTAINER schema): Emotional states feel enclosed. "I'm in trouble." "She's full of joy." "He fell out of love." "Deep in thought."
- **CAUSES ARE FORCES** (FORCE schema): Hard tasks feel like moving heavy objects. "She was driven to succeed." "He was pushed into a corner." "A heavy burden."
- **KNOWING IS SEEING** (PERCEPTION schema): Understanding co-occurs with visual clarity. "I see what you mean." "A clear explanation." "An obscure theory."
- **INTIMACY IS CLOSENESS** (PROXIMITY schema): Emotional closeness co-occurs with physical proximity. "Close friends." "A distant relative." "We grew apart."
- **AFFECTION IS WARMTH** (TEMPERATURE-CONTACT schema): Being held by caregivers is both warm and affectionate. "A warm personality." "A cold response." "Heated argument."
- **TIME IS MOTION** (PATH schema): Events approach and recede. "The deadline is coming." "That's behind us." "Looking forward to the future."

If this chain holds — image schemas ground primary metaphors, and primary metaphors structure abstract thought — then a system that visually represents image schemas could, through metaphorical extension, represent any concept that human thought can express. The visual primitives for CONTAINER, PATH, and FORCE become not just drawings of spatial scenarios but the building blocks of all meaning.

## The Neural Evidence

Image schemas are not merely a philosopher's theory. Neuroscientific evidence supports their reality as cognitive structures grounded in the sensorimotor cortex.

Neuroimaging studies show that processing metaphorical uses of image schemas activates both the relevant sensorimotor regions and abstract reasoning regions. When you read "she's in trouble" (CONTAINER schema used metaphorically for an emotional state), the brain activates spatial processing areas involved in actual containment reasoning alongside the regions that process emotional states. This dual activation is consistent with the metaphorical mapping account — the abstract meaning is processed through the spatial infrastructure.

Developmental evidence is equally compelling. Infant looking-time paradigms confirm that pre-linguistic infants are sensitive to containment violations (an object appearing to pass through a solid barrier), path continuity violations (an object appearing to teleport from one location to another), and force dynamic events (one object causing another to move). These sensitivities are present before language, consistent with the claim that image schemas are acquired before words.

The implication for visual language design is profound. If image schemas are neurally grounded in sensorimotor processing, then visual symbols that activate these schemas should engage the same deep, pre-linguistic cognitive machinery that processes real spatial experience. This means image schema-based visual symbols would not require the same kind of purely conventional learning that alphabetic letters require — they would tap into cognitive resources that every human already possesses. The learning curve for the visual primitives themselves should be shallow, because the mind is already primed to recognize containers, paths, and forces.

## Schema Composition: The Combinatorial Engine

The expressive power of image schemas does not reside in any individual schema. It resides in their composition.

A single CONTAINER schema can represent containment — something is inside something. A single PATH schema can represent movement — something goes from here to there. But combine PATH with CONTAINER and you get "entering" — an entity traversing a path that terminates inside a bounded region. Combine FORCE with PATH and you get "pushing along" — a force causing movement along a trajectory. Combine FORCE with PATH with CONTAINER and you get "putting something into a box" — an agent applying force that drives an object along a path into a bounded region. The DISL strip shown earlier in this article represents exactly this three-schema composition across three temporal panels.

The number of possible compositions is combinatorially large. With sixteen to twenty base schemas, the number of binary compositions (Schema A + Schema B) already exceeds two hundred. Ternary compositions (A + B + C) number in the thousands. Add the metaphorical extension layer — where each spatial composition can represent an abstract domain through primary metaphor — and the expressive territory becomes enormous.

Consider a complex metaphorical expression like "I'm stuck in a dead-end job." This single sentence activates at least three schemas simultaneously. CONTAINER ("stuck in" — the job is a container you cannot exit). PATH ("dead-end" — the job is a path that terminates without a goal). FORCE ("stuck" — you are blocked, unable to move along the path or escape the container). In a visual composition, all three schemas would be present simultaneously: a bounded region (CONTAINER for the job), a path within it terminating at a wall (PATH with BLOCKAGE for the dead-end), and an entity positioned at the blocked point (the speaker, stuck).

The question of whether such multi-schema blends can be represented without visual overwhelm is a genuine design challenge. But the cognitive reality is that humans already process such blends fluently — we understand "stuck in a dead-end job" instantly, without consciously decomposing it into three schemas. The visual composition needs to be at least as readable as the verbal metaphor — which means the visual forms must be clean enough that their combination is parsed holistically rather than analytically. This returns to the lexicalization principle: frequent multi-schema compositions should eventually become single visual units, just as the verbal metaphor "dead-end job" is processed as a single phrase rather than as three separate spatial concepts.

## The Open Schema List Problem

One significant difference between image schemas and semantic primes deserves attention. Wierzbicka's NSM defines exactly 65 primes — a closed, enumerated set where every element is explicitly listed and every non-prime is explicitly excluded. The image schema inventory, by contrast, has never constituted a closed set. Johnson's original 1987 list included roughly sixteen schemas, but subsequent researchers have proposed additions (ATTRACTION, RESISTANCE, COMPULSION as separate from the general FORCE schema; MATCHING, COLLECTION, SUPERIMPOSITION) while others have argued that some of Johnson's schemas are compositions rather than primitives (is CYCLE really basic, or is it PATH composed with itself?).

This openness is both a strength and a weakness for visual language design. The strength: it allows flexibility. If a visual language community discovers that a particular spatial pattern needs its own symbol rather than being composed from existing primitives, the system can accommodate it without revising its foundations. The weakness: without a fixed, closed inventory, the system cannot guarantee that all users share exactly the same set of primitives. Different users might decompose the same concept differently, leading to systematic variation rather than universal uniformity.

The practical resolution is to define a core set — perhaps the twelve to fifteen schemas that appear in virtually every researcher's inventory (CONTAINER, PATH, FORCE, BALANCE, LINK, CENTER-PERIPHERY, PART-WHOLE, UP-DOWN, NEAR-FAR, SCALE, CONTACT, CYCLE, SPLITTING, MERGING) — and treat additional schemas as compositional or community-defined extensions. This mirrors how natural languages have a core vocabulary that is stable and a periphery that varies across dialects and registers.

## The Gap: Where Schemas Cannot Reach

Intellectual honesty requires confronting the limits of image schema grounding. Not everything human language needs to express has an obvious spatial-physical basis.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-5-abstract-concept-gap.html -->

Image schemas handle spatial relationships directly: in, on, through, across. They handle physical actions: moving, containing, linking, balancing. They handle causal dynamics: pushing, blocking, enabling. Through well-established metaphorical extension, they handle emotions (CONTAINER: "full of rage"), goals (PATH: "on the road to recovery"), knowledge (PERCEPTION: "I see what you mean"), social relationships (LINK/PROXIMITY: "close friends"), morality (VERTICALITY/BALANCE: "upright citizen," "moral balance"), and time (PATH: "the future is coming").

But several domains pose genuine challenges. **Logical operators** — NOT, IF, MAYBE — do not have obvious image schema groundings. Some researchers propose that negation maps onto the BLOCKAGE sub-schema of FORCE (negation as "blocking" a path to meaning) and that conditionality maps onto a BRANCHING PATH (a fork in the road where the path depends on a condition). These proposals are theoretically motivated but less empirically established than the core metaphors. **Propositional attitudes** — "I believe that she thinks that he knows..." — involve nested mental state attribution that pushes beyond what spatial composition easily handles. **Cultural and institutional concepts** — democracy, copyright, mortgage — depend on social conventions rather than embodied experience. **Precise quantification** — the difference between "some" and "37%" — demands a specificity that schematic spatial patterns do not naturally provide.

The central design question is whether compositional metaphor can reach all the way to these domains, or whether there is a ceiling beyond which image schemas cannot ground meaning and conventional (non-schematic) symbols must take over. The honest answer is that we do not know yet. The metaphorical extension evidence is strong for core abstract domains (emotion, goal-pursuit, causation, time) but weaker for logic and cultural constructs. A visual language built on image schemas will likely need a hybrid approach: schema-based symbols for the vast majority of meanings, with some conventional symbols for domains that resist spatial grounding.

## Common Misconceptions About Image Schemas

Three misconceptions persistently distort discussions of image schema theory, and each has practical consequences for visual language design.

**Misconception: Image schemas are mental images.** People hear "image schema" and think of a vivid, specific mental picture — a particular cup, a particular road, a particular push. But image schemas are schematic, not imagistic. The CONTAINER schema is not a picture of any specific container; it is the abstract structure of inside/outside/boundary that applies equally to a teacup, a prison cell, and the metaphorical "container" of an emotional state. This distinction matters for visual design because the symbols must capture the schematic structure, not the specific appearance of any instance. A circle with a dot inside it is a better representation of CONTAINER than a detailed drawing of a cup — because the cup is too specific, while the abstract shape captures the schema itself.

**Misconception: Image schemas are just spatial vocabulary.** Critics sometimes dismiss schemas as a fancy way of restating words like "in," "on," and "through." But the relationship runs in the opposite direction. Image schemas are pre-linguistic cognitive structures that exist before and independently of any language's spatial vocabulary. The fact that English uses "in" for containment is a consequence of the CONTAINER schema, not the other way around. Languages express schemas differently — Japanese uses postpositions, Finnish uses case endings, Tzeltal uses body-part terminology for spatial relations — but the underlying schemas appear to be universal. The schemas are deeper than any language's particular encoding of them.

**Misconception: DISL already provides a communication-ready visual language.** DISL is an analytical notation for researchers. It can represent spatial-physical scenarios with formal precision, but it lacks mechanisms for metaphorical extension (how does a DISL diagram say "I'm in trouble"?), for abstract logical operators (how does it say "if"?), for cultural vocabulary (how does it say "democracy"?), or for the pragmatic flexibility needed for everyday communication (how does it distinguish a question from a statement?). DISL is a foundation — a proof of concept that image schemas can be formally visualized — but it is not the building. Several additional layers of design are required: compositionality rules, metaphorical mapping conventions, a phonetic subsystem, and pragmatic devices for real-world discourse.

## From Schemas to Visual Alphabet: Design Implications

If image schemas are to serve as the visual primitives of a language, several design requirements follow.

**The symbol set must be small and distinct.** Johnson's inventory provides roughly sixteen to twenty core schemas. DISL demonstrates that these can be represented with a compact set of visual primitives (circles, arrows, rectangles, thick lines, contact points). Each primitive must be visually distinguishable from every other at typical reading sizes — a design challenge but not an insurmountable one, given that alphabets routinely distinguish twenty-six or more letters at small sizes.

**Composition must be rule-governed.** DISL's composition rules (object inside region = containment, force arrow connecting agent to patient = causation) show that schemas compose according to spatial logic. A visual language needs to formalize these rules into a grammar — the subject of the Compositionality category — so that any composition of primitives has a predictable, unambiguous meaning.

**Metaphorical extension must be systematic.** The mapping from spatial schemas to abstract meanings cannot be left implicit. A visual language needs to explicitly define how the CONTAINER symbol means "emotional state" when used with an emotion indicator, how the PATH symbol means "purpose" when used with a goal indicator, and how the FORCE symbol means "causation" when used with an event indicator. These metaphorical mappings are the mechanism that transforms a spatial notation into a general-purpose language.

**Lexicalization must be permitted.** Frequently used schema compositions should be eligible to crystallize into single symbols — just as the Chinese character 看 (to look) composed from 手 (hand) + 目 (eye) is now processed as a single unit by literate readers. The transition from compositional transparency to holistic recognition is natural and inevitable; the system should design for it rather than resist it.

**The gap must be addressed honestly.** Where image schemas cannot ground meaning, the system needs alternative strategies. For logical operators, the proposed visual encodings (negation as BLOCKAGE boundary, conditionality as BRANCHING PATH) should be tested empirically. For cultural constructs, conventional symbols assigned by community consensus may be necessary. For phonetic content (names, borrowed words), an entirely separate subsystem is needed — the subject of the Phoneme Representation Problem category.

## NSM Primes and Image Schemas: Complementary Coverage

A visual language cannot be built on image schemas alone, and it cannot be built on semantic primes alone. The two frameworks cover different territory, and their combined coverage exceeds either.

NSM provides 65 semantic primes — atomic concepts from which all other meanings can be defined. Many are abstract: IF, BECAUSE, TRUE, MAYBE, SAY, WORDS, THINK. These are precisely the concepts that image schemas struggle to represent visually. But NSM also includes spatial primes that map directly onto image schemas: INSIDE maps to CONTAINER, MOVE maps to PATH, ABOVE and BELOW map to UP-DOWN, NEAR and FAR map to PROXIMITY, TOUCH maps to CONTACT. In this overlap zone, the integration is natural: the NSM prime specifies the semantic content, and the image schema provides the visual form.

Where the frameworks diverge, each compensates for the other's weakness. Image schemas provide visual representations for spatial-physical concepts that NSM primes describe verbally. NSM primes provide semantic definitions for abstract concepts that image schemas can only reach through speculative metaphorical extension. A visual language that draws on both — using image schemas as the visual vocabulary and NSM primes as the semantic inventory — has a stronger foundation than one that relies on either alone.

The integration architecture might work like this: for concepts in the overlap zone (spatial concepts), the image schema IS the visual symbol for the NSM prime. For concepts outside the overlap (abstract primes like IF, BECAUSE, TRUE), the visual symbol draws on the proposed metaphorical groundings (IF as BRANCHING PATH, BECAUSE as FORCE arrow, TRUE as SOLID line) but is explicitly taught as a convention rather than assumed as transparent. For concepts where neither framework provides an obvious visual form (cultural constructs like democracy, copyright), the system falls back on compositional definition — breaking the concept into simpler parts, each of which has a visual representation — or on community-assigned conventional symbols.

## The Convergence: Why Image Schemas Are the Right Foundation

The argument for image schemas as the foundation of a visual language is not that they can represent everything. It is that they represent the largest possible territory with the smallest possible set of primitives, grounded in the deepest possible level of human cognitive universality.

Semantic primes (NSM) provide the semantic inventory — the list of atomic meanings a language must be able to express. Image schemas provide the visual vocabulary — the spatial forms through which those meanings can be drawn. Where the two frameworks overlap (INSIDE maps to CONTAINER, MOVE maps to PATH, ABOVE/BELOW maps to UP-DOWN), the integration is natural. Where they diverge (NSM primes like IF, BECAUSE, TRUE have no obvious schema counterparts), the gap identifies exactly where additional design work is needed.

DISL proves the formalization is possible. Mandler's developmental theory establishes the cognitive universality. Grady's primary metaphors provide the extension mechanism to abstract domains. The neural evidence confirms the cognitive reality. And the open questions — whether metaphorical extension reaches all the way to logic and cultural constructs, whether visual parsing of schema compositions is fast enough for fluent reading, whether schema-based symbols are recognized consistently across cultures — are empirical questions that a research program can answer.

The pattern-seeker's reward, in this domain, is that the patterns run deep. Image schemas are not an arbitrary organizational choice for a visual language. They are the patterns that the human mind uses to organize all experience — spatial and non-spatial, physical and abstract, personal and cultural. A visual language built on these patterns would be building on the cognitive bedrock of human meaning-making itself.
