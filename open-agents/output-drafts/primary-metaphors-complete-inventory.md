# Complete Inventory of Primary Metaphors in the Literature

## Overview

This document catalogs every primary metaphor identified in the cognitive linguistics literature, drawing primarily from:

- **Joseph Grady (1997)**: *Foundations of Meaning: Primary Metaphors and Primary Scenes* (PhD dissertation, UC Berkeley) -- the foundational work that proposed the theory and coined the term "primary metaphor"
- **Grady & Johnson (1997)**: "Converging Evidence for the Notions of Subscene and Primary Scene" (BLS 23)
- **Grady (2005)**: "Primary Metaphors as Inputs to Conceptual Integration" (*Journal of Pragmatics* 37)
- **Lakoff & Johnson (1999)**: *Philosophy in the Flesh* (Basic Books), Chapter 3: "Primary Metaphor and Subjective Experience" -- contains the canonical table of primary metaphors on pp. 50-54
- **Grady (1997b)**: "THEORIES ARE BUILDINGS Revisited" (CLS 33)
- **Lakoff & Narayanan (2025)**: *The Neural Mind* -- extending the neural theory of metaphor

### What Is a Primary Metaphor?

A primary metaphor is a cognitively primitive, irreducible metaphorical mapping between a **subjective experience/judgment** (target domain) and a **sensorimotor experience** (source domain). Primary metaphors arise from **conflation** during early childhood -- the repeated co-occurrence of sensorimotor and subjective experiences that are initially undifferentiated. They are the "atoms" of metaphorical thought from which complex ("molecular") metaphors are composed.

Key properties:
- **Embodied**: Grounded in universal bodily experience
- **Unconscious**: Acquired automatically, operate below awareness
- **Universal**: Predicted to be cross-linguistically universal (same body, same physics)
- **Irreducible**: Cannot be decomposed into simpler metaphors
- **Compositional**: Combine to form complex metaphors (e.g., LOVE IS A JOURNEY = PURPOSES ARE DESTINATIONS + INTIMACY IS CLOSENESS + DIFFICULTIES ARE BURDENS + STATES ARE LOCATIONS + CHANGE IS MOTION)

### How Many Primary Metaphors Are There?

Lakoff & Johnson (1999, pp. 50-54) present a "representative" table containing entries that are commonly cited as numbering approximately 23-24. Grady's (1997) dissertation analyzes a somewhat larger set in its appendix, with detailed "primary scene" decompositions. The literature does not claim this is an exhaustive inventory -- Lakoff & Johnson explicitly describe their list as representative, not complete, and note that "there are a large number of primary metaphors." Some sources suggest the total could be in the hundreds if one counts every fine-grained experiential correlation, though the canonical inventory of well-documented, widely-cited primary metaphors numbers roughly **25-30**.

The count depends on granularity decisions:
- Is HAPPY IS UP the same as GOOD IS UP? (Lakoff & Johnson treat them separately in some discussions)
- Should PURPOSES ARE DESTINATIONS and PURPOSES ARE DESIRED OBJECTS count as one or two? (The 1999 table lists both)
- Are VIABILITY IS ERECTNESS and PERSISTING IS REMAINING ERECT the same metaphor or two related ones?
- Is EMOTIONAL INTENSITY IS HEAT a primary metaphor or a complex one reducible to INTENSITY IS HEAT?

## The Canonical Table: Lakoff & Johnson (1999), pp. 50-54

The following table reproduces the primary metaphors from *Philosophy in the Flesh*, Chapter 3, with additional detail from Grady (1997, 2005) on the conflation/primary scene that grounds each mapping. Entries are numbered for reference.

---

### 1. AFFECTION IS WARMTH

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Affection |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Temperature (warmth) |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Being held by a caregiver: the infant simultaneously experiences physical warmth (body heat, skin contact) and the subjective feeling of being loved/comforted. The two experiences are initially undifferentiated. |
| **Example Expressions** | "A warm greeting," "She gave me the cold shoulder," "He's a cold person," "They warmed up to each other," "A warm smile" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); Lakoff & Johnson (1999, p. 50) |

---

### 2. IMPORTANT IS BIG

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Importance |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Size |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | For a child, important entities (parents, other adults, institutional buildings) are physically larger. Important objects take up more space and are harder to ignore. Size and importance are conflated in early experience. |
| **Example Expressions** | "Tomorrow's a big day," "It's a huge deal," "Just a small matter," "A big mistake," "This is bigger than both of us" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); Lakoff & Johnson (1999, p. 50); Yu (2017) |

---

### 3. HAPPY IS UP / SAD IS DOWN

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Emotional valence (happiness/sadness) |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Vertical orientation (up/down) |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Drooping posture correlates with sadness, fatigue, and illness; erect, upright posture correlates with positive emotional states, health, and vitality. The child's own body and the bodies of caregivers provide the correlation. |
| **Example Expressions** | "I'm feeling up today," "He's down in the dumps," "My spirits rose," "She fell into a depression," "I'm on top of the world" |
| **Source** | Lakoff & Johnson (1980, 1999, p. 50) |

---

### 4. INTIMACY IS CLOSENESS

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Emotional intimacy |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Physical proximity |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Physical closeness to caregivers correlates with emotional bonding. Being held, cuddled, and near loved ones is simultaneously a spatial and an emotional experience. |
| **Example Expressions** | "We're close friends," "They've grown apart," "A distant relative," "We've been drifting apart," "She's my nearest and dearest" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); Lakoff & Johnson (1999, p. 50) |

---

### 5. BAD IS STINKY

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Negative evaluation (badness) |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Olfaction (bad smell) |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Bad-smelling things (spoiled food, waste, decay) are genuinely harmful or dangerous. The disgust response to bad smells conflates with the judgment of badness. The infant learns that things that smell bad are things to avoid. |
| **Example Expressions** | "That idea stinks," "Something smells fishy," "This deal is rotten," "A stinking mess," "That movie stinks" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); Lakoff & Johnson (1999, p. 50) |

---

### 6. DIFFICULTIES ARE BURDENS

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Difficulty (of a task or situation) |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Muscular effort / weight (heaviness) |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Carrying heavy objects requires effort and makes tasks harder. The physical sensation of bearing weight is conflated with the subjective experience of difficulty. |
| **Example Expressions** | "She carries a heavy load of responsibilities," "Lighten your burden," "Weighed down by debt," "That's a heavy problem," "I feel a weight on my shoulders" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); Lakoff & Johnson (1999, p. 50) |

---

### 7. MORE IS UP

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Quantity |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Vertical orientation / elevation |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Adding more of a substance to a container increases its vertical level. Stacking objects increases both quantity and height simultaneously. Pouring water raises the surface. The experience of quantity and verticality are conflated in countless daily interactions. |
| **Example Expressions** | "Prices are rising," "The stock market fell," "Turn the volume up," "Crime rates dropped," "High numbers" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); Lakoff & Johnson (1980, 1999, p. 51) |

---

### 8. CATEGORIES ARE CONTAINERS

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Category membership |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Containment / bounded regions |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Objects that are grouped together are often physically contained in the same bounded region (a box, a room, a fenced area). Being "in" a category is conflated with being "in" a physical container. |
| **Example Expressions** | "He's in the top tier," "She falls outside that category," "That doesn't fit into any box," "Are you in or out?" "Within the field of linguistics" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); Lakoff & Johnson (1999, p. 51) |

---

### 9. SIMILARITY IS CLOSENESS

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Similarity (perceived resemblance) |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Spatial proximity |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Similar objects tend to be found near each other (same species cluster together, matching socks are stored together, like items are shelved together). Spatial proximity and similarity are repeatedly experienced together. |
| **Example Expressions** | "These colors are close," "Far from what I had in mind," "Those ideas are in the same ballpark," "That's not even in the same neighborhood" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); Lakoff & Johnson (1999, p. 51) |

---

### 10. LINEAR SCALES ARE PATHS

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Degree / scalar measurement |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Motion along a path |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Progress along a physical path correlates with increasing distance from a starting point. Scalar increase (e.g., temperature rising, wealth growing) maps onto forward movement along a line. The experience of traversing a path provides the template for understanding scalar progression. |
| **Example Expressions** | "Prices went through the roof," "The temperature climbed," "Way beyond expectations," "He went too far," "That goes a long way toward solving the problem" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); Lakoff & Johnson (1999, p. 51) |

---

### 11. ORGANIZATION IS PHYSICAL STRUCTURE

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Abstract organization / logical structure |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Physical structure (parts assembled into wholes) |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Experiencing physical objects that have parts arranged in a stable configuration provides a template for understanding abstract organization. Building with blocks, assembling things, and experiencing the structure of objects conflates with understanding how abstract systems are put together. |
| **Example Expressions** | "The theory has a solid foundation," "The argument fell apart," "Let me construct an argument," "The framework of the analysis," "A well-built case" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997, 1997b); Lakoff & Johnson (1999, p. 51) |

**Note**: This is one of the primary metaphors that composes the complex metaphor THEORIES ARE BUILDINGS (together with PERSISTING IS REMAINING ERECT / VIABILITY IS ERECTNESS).

---

### 12. HELP IS SUPPORT

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Assistance / help |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Physical support (holding up, propping) |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Being physically supported (held up by a caregiver, propped up when learning to stand) correlates with being helped. Physical support prevents falling; abstract help prevents failure. |
| **Example Expressions** | "I'll back you up," "She supported his proposal," "The evidence backs up the claim," "He's a pillar of the community," "I need something to lean on" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); Lakoff & Johnson (1999, p. 51) |

---

### 13. TIME IS MOTION

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Passage of time |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Motion (of objects or self through space) |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | The experience of events unfolding correlates with the experience of motion. Objects approach and recede; we move through landscapes. The passage of time is experienced alongside motion in all its forms. Two variants exist: the Moving Time metaphor (time moves toward/past the observer: "The deadline is approaching") and the Moving Observer metaphor (the observer moves through time: "We're coming up on the anniversary"). |
| **Example Expressions** | "Time flies," "The deadline is approaching," "We're coming up on the anniversary," "Those days are behind us," "Looking ahead to next year" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); Lakoff & Johnson (1999, pp. 51-52) |

---

### 14. STATES ARE LOCATIONS

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Being in a state (emotional, physical, or social) |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Being in a bounded location |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Being in a particular physical location correlates with being in a particular state. Being in the kitchen means being in the state of cooking; being in bed means being in the state of rest. Location and state are conflated throughout early experience. |
| **Example Expressions** | "She's in trouble," "He fell into a depression," "We're in love," "I'm in a good place right now," "She's out of danger," "He's in deep water" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); Lakoff & Johnson (1999, p. 52) |

---

### 15. CHANGE IS MOTION

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Change of state |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Physical movement from one location to another |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Moving from one location to another is the prototypical experience of change. The child moves from the crib to the playroom (change of activity), from indoors to outdoors (change of environment). Change and motion are deeply conflated. |
| **Example Expressions** | "The town went from boom to bust," "Things are moving in the right direction," "She went from happy to furious," "The conversation shifted," "We've moved on" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); Lakoff & Johnson (1999, p. 52) |

---

### 16. ACTIONS ARE SELF-PROPELLED MOTIONS

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Performing an action (purposeful activity) |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Self-propelled bodily movement |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | The earliest volitional actions are bodily movements -- reaching, crawling, walking. Agency and self-propelled motion are conflated: to do something is to move your body. Later, abstract actions inherit the motion structure. |
| **Example Expressions** | "What steps will you take?" "Let's proceed," "She made a bold move," "We need to get going on this," "He plowed through the work" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); Lakoff & Johnson (1999, p. 52) |

---

### 17. PURPOSES ARE DESTINATIONS

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Achieving a purpose |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Reaching a physical destination |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | To achieve a given purpose, one typically has to go to a specific location. The infant crawls to the toy; the child goes to the kitchen for food; the adult drives to the store. Arriving at a destination and achieving a purpose are repeatedly conflated. As Grady (1997) notes: "It is a very common occurrence in everyday life that one has to go to a specific location in order to achieve a given purpose." |
| **Example Expressions** | "She reached her goal," "He's on his way to success," "I'm not there yet," "We've arrived at a solution," "The finish line is in sight" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); Lakoff & Johnson (1999, p. 52) |

---

### 18. PURPOSES ARE DESIRED OBJECTS

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Achieving a purpose |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Acquiring a desired object |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Children want objects (food, toys, comfort items) and achieve satisfaction by obtaining them. The experience of wanting something and getting it conflates purpose-achievement with object-acquisition. |
| **Example Expressions** | "I got the job," "She grabbed the opportunity," "He seized the moment," "The prize is within reach," "She finally got what she wanted" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); Lakoff & Johnson (1999, p. 52) |

**Note**: This differs from #17 in its source domain: destinations emphasize the journey/process; desired objects emphasize the acquisition/possession.

---

### 19. CAUSES ARE PHYSICAL FORCES

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Causation |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Physical force (pushing, pulling, propelling) |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Physical forces cause physical effects: pushing a ball makes it roll, pulling a door makes it open. The child experiences causation as physical force from the earliest motor interactions. Abstract causation inherits this force-dynamic structure. |
| **Example Expressions** | "What drove you to do that?" "She was pushed into making a decision," "Compelled by circumstances," "The pressure of deadlines," "He was pulled in two directions" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); Lakoff & Johnson (1999, p. 52); Talmy (1988) on force dynamics |

---

### 20. RELATIONSHIPS (BETWEEN PEOPLE) ARE ENCLOSURES

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Being in a relationship |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Being in an enclosed space together |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | People who are in a relationship spend time in the same enclosed spaces (homes, rooms, vehicles). The experience of being together in a bounded space is conflated with the experience of being in a relationship. |
| **Example Expressions** | "We're in a relationship," "She's trapped in a bad marriage," "He broke out of the relationship," "They're building walls around themselves," "An open relationship" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); Lakoff & Johnson (1999, p. 53) |

---

### 21. CONTROL IS UP

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Having control / dominance |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Vertical elevation (being physically above) |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Physical dominance involves being above -- larger animals tower over smaller ones, a parent lifts a child, the winner of a wrestling match is on top. Height and control/dominance are repeatedly conflated. |
| **Example Expressions** | "She's on top of things," "He has the upper hand," "Under his control," "The boss is above me," "They have the high ground" |
| **Source** | Lakoff & Johnson (1980, 1999, p. 53) |

---

### 22. KNOWING IS SEEING

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Knowledge / intellectual understanding |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Visual perception |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Visual perception is the dominant mode of acquiring information about the world. Seeing an object and knowing it is there are simultaneously experienced. The conflation of seeing and knowing is one of the most thoroughly studied primary metaphors -- Christopher Johnson (1997) documented the developmental stages in children's acquisition of "see" from purely visual to cognitive meaning. |
| **Example Expressions** | "I see what you mean," "That's a clear explanation," "His motives are transparent," "A murky argument," "Let me shed some light on this" |
| **Source** | C. Johnson (1997); Grady (1997); Lakoff & Johnson (1999, p. 53); Sweetser (1990) |

---

### 23. UNDERSTANDING IS GRASPING

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Understanding / comprehension |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Manual manipulation (grasping, holding objects) |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Manipulating objects with the hands is the primary way of learning about them in infancy. Grasping, turning, and examining objects provides understanding of their properties. Physical grasping and cognitive understanding are conflated. |
| **Example Expressions** | "I can't grasp the concept," "Let me get a handle on this," "That's a slippery argument," "She has a firm grip on the material," "Hold that thought" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); Lakoff & Johnson (1999, p. 53) |

---

### 24. SEEING IS TOUCHING

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Visual perception (as active exploration) |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Tactile contact |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | The eyes "reach out" to objects in the visual field in a way that parallels reaching out to touch them. Looking at something and touching it are often simultaneous -- the infant sees the toy and reaches for it. The folk model of vision as rays emanating from the eyes reinforces this conflation. |
| **Example Expressions** | "Her eyes touched mine," "His gaze fell upon the painting," "A piercing look," "She ran her eyes over the document," "His eyes swept the room" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); Lakoff & Johnson (1999, p. 54) |

## Beyond the Canonical 24: Additional Primary Metaphors in the Literature

The following primary metaphors appear in Grady's (1997) dissertation, in his other publications, or in extensions by other researchers in the tradition. They are not always included in the "representative" table of Lakoff & Johnson (1999) but are discussed as primary metaphors elsewhere in the literature.

---

### 25. DESIRE IS HUNGER

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Desire / wanting |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Hunger (physiological appetite) |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | The infant experiences hunger and the desire for food as a single, undifferentiated state. Hunger IS wanting. Later, the wanting dimension generalizes to non-food objects while retaining the hunger structure. Grady (1997) identifies this as a clear primary metaphor; extensive empirical work by Lima (2006) and others has confirmed its cross-linguistic productivity. |
| **Example Expressions** | "I'm hungry for success," "She has an appetite for adventure," "He craves attention," "Politicians hunger for power," "A thirst for knowledge" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); Gibbs, Lima & Francozo (2004); MetaNet (Berkeley) |

---

### 26. VIABILITY IS ERECTNESS / PERSISTING IS REMAINING ERECT

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Functional viability / continued existence |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Being upright / standing erect |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Objects that are upright are functional; objects that have fallen over are typically broken, dead, or non-functional. Plants grow upright when healthy and droop when dying. People stand when active and lie down when ill or dead. Erectness and viability are pervasively conflated. |
| **Example Expressions** | "The theory still stands," "The company is standing strong," "Their argument collapsed," "A fallen empire," "The house of cards fell" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997, 1997b); Lakoff & Johnson (1999); used in decomposition of THEORIES ARE BUILDINGS |

**Note**: Grady (1997b) uses this as one of the primary metaphors composing THEORIES ARE BUILDINGS, alongside ORGANIZATION IS PHYSICAL STRUCTURE and CAPACITY TO ENDURE IS STURDINESS.

---

### 27. INTENSITY IS HEAT

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Intensity (of emotion, activity, conflict) |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Temperature (heat) |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Emotional arousal produces physiological heat (flushing, elevated skin temperature). Physical activity generates heat. Intense situations literally make us hot. The conflation of intensity and heat is grounded in thermoregulation and autonomic response. |
| **Example Expressions** | "A heated argument," "Things are really heating up," "The heat of the moment," "Cool down," "A burning desire," "White-hot rage" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); Kovecses (2000); MetaNet: EMOTIONAL INTENSITY IS TEMPERATURE |

**Note**: This primary metaphor contributes to the complex metaphor ANGER IS A HOT FLUID IN A CONTAINER (= INTENSITY IS HEAT + EMOTIONS ARE FLUIDS + THE BODY IS A CONTAINER FOR EMOTIONS).

---

### 28. GOOD IS BRIGHT / BAD IS DARK

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Positive/negative evaluation |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Brightness/darkness (visual luminosity) |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Light enables seeing (and therefore knowing/safety); darkness impedes it (and therefore danger/ignorance). Bright environments correlate with waking, activity, and social interaction; dark environments with sleep, vulnerability, and the unknown. Good outcomes and brightness are conflated from earliest experience. |
| **Example Expressions** | "A bright future," "A dark period in history," "She has a brilliant mind," "A dim view," "Look on the bright side," "Gloomy prospects" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); Forceville (2014); cross-linguistic evidence in Kövecses (2005) |

**Note**: Some researchers treat GOOD IS BRIGHT and BAD IS DARK as a single bipolar primary metaphor; others treat them as two. The evaluative dimension (good/bad) maps onto the luminosity dimension (bright/dark) as a coherent pair.

---

### 29. IMPORTANT IS HEAVY / DIFFICULTY IS HEAVINESS

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Importance / gravity / seriousness |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Physical weight (heaviness) |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Heavy objects demand more attention, effort, and respect. They cannot be easily moved or ignored. The physical property of weight conflates with the subjective property of importance/seriousness. Distinct from DIFFICULTIES ARE BURDENS (#6), which maps weight to difficulty rather than importance. |
| **Example Expressions** | "A weighty matter," "A grave situation," "This carries a lot of weight," "A heavy decision," "Light reading" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); Yu (2017) on IMPORTANCE AS SIZE AND WEIGHT; Lakoff & Johnson (1999) |

**Note**: Yu (2017) argues that IMPORTANT IS BIG and IMPORTANT IS HEAVY are two distinct primary metaphors with different sensorimotor bases (size vs. weight), though they often co-occur.

---

### 30. CONSIDERING IS WEIGHING

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Deliberation / evaluation |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Weighing objects (comparing heft) |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | The physical act of holding objects in the hands and comparing their weight provides a template for the abstract act of comparing options and evaluating their relative merit. |
| **Example Expressions** | "Let me weigh the options," "On balance, I think...," "Weighing the pros and cons," "The evidence tips the scales," "A balanced perspective" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); mentioned in multiple secondary sources as a canonical primary metaphor |

---

### 31. ANALYZING IS CUTTING / TAKING APART

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Analysis / decomposition of an idea |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Cutting / physically separating |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Understanding what something is made of often requires physically breaking it apart or cutting it open. The child who takes a toy apart is analyzing it. Physical dissection and intellectual analysis are conflated. |
| **Example Expressions** | "Let's dissect this argument," "A trenchant analysis," "A cutting remark," "We need to break this down," "A sharp distinction," "An incisive critique" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); referenced in Lakoff & Johnson (1999) |

---

### 32. CAPACITY TO ENDURE IS STURDINESS

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Ability to withstand adversity / durability |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Physical sturdiness / structural robustness |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Objects that are physically sturdy last longer and resist damage. Flimsy objects break easily. The physical property of sturdiness conflates with the abstract capacity to endure over time. |
| **Example Expressions** | "A sturdy argument," "A flimsy excuse," "The company weathered the storm," "Built to last," "A rock-solid commitment" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997b); used in decomposition of THEORIES ARE BUILDINGS |

---

### 33. INTERRELATED IS INTERWOVEN

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Being conceptually interrelated / connected |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Physical interweaving / entanglement |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Things that are physically interwoven are difficult to separate and function as a unit. Threads in fabric, roots of adjacent plants, and tangled cords all demonstrate how physical interweaving creates functional unity. |
| **Example Expressions** | "Their lives are intertwined," "A tightly woven argument," "The threads of the narrative," "Unraveling the mystery," "A tangled web of lies," "The fabric of society" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); referenced in Lakoff & Johnson (1999) as a component of complex metaphors |

## Candidate Additional Primary Metaphors

The following mappings are discussed in the primary metaphor literature as potentially primary, though their status is more debated. Some may be reducible to combinations of the above; others may represent genuinely independent experiential correlations.

---

### 34. EMOTIONAL EFFECT IS PHYSICAL CONTACT

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Being emotionally affected |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Physical contact / being touched |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Physical contact (being touched, held, hit) produces immediate sensory and emotional responses. The experience of being physically affected conflates with being emotionally affected. |
| **Example Expressions** | "I was touched by her kindness," "A moving speech," "Struck by grief," "That hit me hard," "She was deeply affected" |
| **Source** | Discussed in Lakoff & Johnson (1999); Grady (1997) |

---

### 35. EYES ARE CONTAINERS FOR EMOTIONS

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Emotional expression through the eyes |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Container schema applied to eyes |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Emotions are perceived in others' eyes: tears fill the eyes, anger flashes in the eyes, love shines in the eyes. The eyes are experienced as a location where emotions are visible and contained. |
| **Example Expressions** | "Her eyes were full of sadness," "There was fear in his eyes," "Joy shining in her eyes," "Emptiness in his gaze" |
| **Source** | Grady (1997); noted in Lakoff (1993) |

---

### 36. COMMUNICATION IS SENDING / IDEAS ARE OBJECTS

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Communicating ideas |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Sending/transferring physical objects |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Giving a physical object to someone transfers possession of that object. Communication of ideas follows the same structure: the speaker "sends" the idea and the listener "receives" it. (This is also known as the Conduit Metaphor, identified by Reddy 1979.) |
| **Example Expressions** | "Let me give you an idea," "She got the message across," "I can't put my feelings into words," "His words carried meaning," "That idea came through clearly" |
| **Source** | Reddy (1979); Lakoff & Johnson (1980); debated as to whether this is a single primary metaphor or a complex composed of multiple primaries |

**Note**: The primary status of this metaphor is debated. Grady (1997) suggests that what Reddy called the "conduit metaphor" may actually be a complex metaphor composed of several primary metaphors including UNDERSTANDING IS GRASPING and others.

---

### 37. ACCEPTING IS SWALLOWING / TAKING IN

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Acceptance (of an idea, situation, or proposition) |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Ingestion (swallowing) |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | Taking food into the body is the prototype of accepting something from outside. What is swallowed becomes part of you. What is spit out is rejected. |
| **Example Expressions** | "I can't swallow that excuse," "That's a lot to take in," "A hard pill to swallow," "She couldn't stomach the idea," "He ate up every word," "I can't digest all this information" |
| **Source** | Related to Lakoff & Johnson (1999); extends DESIRE IS HUNGER framework |

### 38. AGREEMENT IS SPATIAL PROXIMITY / BEING ON THE SAME SIDE

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Subjective Experience** (target) | Agreement / solidarity |
| **Sensorimotor Experience** (source) | Being in the same location / on the same side |
| **Conflation/Primary Scene** | People who agree with each other physically stand together, sit together, and face the same direction. People who disagree face off, stand apart, or turn away. Physical alignment and intellectual agreement are conflated. |
| **Example Expressions** | "We're on the same page," "They're on opposite sides," "She's with me on this," "We see eye to eye," "He came around to my point of view" |
| **Source** | Lakoff & Johnson (1999); related to SIMILARITY IS CLOSENESS but focused specifically on agreement/alignment |

## Debate About the Total Count

### Why the Numbers Vary

Different sources cite different totals for the number of primary metaphors:

1. **"About 23"**: This is the most commonly cited number, referring to the table in Lakoff & Johnson (1999, pp. 50-54). However, careful counting of that table reveals approximately 24 entries (depending on how PURPOSES ARE DESTINATIONS and PURPOSES ARE DESIRED OBJECTS are counted).

2. **"25-30"**: This range reflects the addition of primary metaphors from Grady's (1997) dissertation that are discussed in the text but not all included in the 1999 table -- particularly DESIRE IS HUNGER, VIABILITY IS ERECTNESS, INTENSITY IS HEAT, GOOD IS BRIGHT/BAD IS DARK, CONSIDERING IS WEIGHING, ANALYZING IS CUTTING, CAPACITY TO ENDURE IS STURDINESS, and INTERRELATED IS INTERWOVEN.

3. **"Hundreds"**: Lakoff & Johnson (1999) and Grady both emphasize that the published list is "representative," not exhaustive. They argue that there are "a large number" of primary metaphors, possibly hundreds, corresponding to every recurring correlation between subjective and sensorimotor experience. The canonical list captures the most well-documented and linguistically productive ones.

### Why It's Hard to Fix an Exact Number

- **Granularity**: Is HAPPY IS UP the same metaphor as GOOD IS UP, or a different one? Is MORE IS UP the same as CONTROL IS UP? (Lakoff & Johnson treat them as different.)
- **Decomposition**: Some proposed primary metaphors might actually be composed of simpler ones. Is COMMUNICATION IS SENDING genuinely primary, or is it a complex metaphor?
- **Cultural variation**: Kovecses (2005) argues that while generic-level primary metaphors are universal, specific-level elaborations vary by culture, making it difficult to determine exactly where "primary" stops and "culturally-specific complex" begins.
- **Open-endedness**: The theory predicts that any recurring experiential correlation can generate a primary metaphor. New ones could in principle be discovered.

### The Scholarly Consensus

The working consensus in cognitive linguistics is:
- **~24 well-documented primary metaphors** appear in the canonical Lakoff & Johnson (1999) table
- **~30-35** are discussed across Grady's various publications when you include the ones used in complex metaphor decomposition
- **The inventory is open-ended** -- it is a representative sample, not a closed set
