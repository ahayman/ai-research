# Concept Research: Historical Visual Language Systems — From Leibniz to Icono

## Overview

The dream of a visual language that transcends the boundaries of spoken tongues is at least four centuries old. From Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz's 1666 proposal for a *characteristica universalis* — an algebraic symbolic language that would make reasoning as mechanical as arithmetic — through Otto Neurath's ISOTYPE pictograms for statistical communication in the 1920s, Charles Bliss's Semantography in 1949, Yukio Ota's LoCoS in 1964, Timothée Ingen-Housz's Elephant's Memory in the 1990s, Xu Bing's *Book from the Ground* in 2003–2012, and Peter Kramer's Icono published in *Frontiers in Psychology* in 2023, each generation has attempted to solve the problem from a different angle. Each system made fundamentally different design choices about iconicity versus abstraction, compositionality, grammar, the relationship to spoken language, and the target audience.

None has achieved adoption as a general-purpose communication system. But the reasons for each system's partial success or failure are profoundly instructive. Blissymbolics, the most linguistically sophisticated, found a second life in augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) for children with cerebral palsy — a use its inventor never intended and bitterly resisted. ISOTYPE's pictograms became the foundation of modern information design, influencing everything from airport signage to infographics, despite never functioning as a language. LoCoS and Elephant's Memory explored radically different approaches to visual grammar — grid-based positioning versus free-floating spatial association — and each illuminated different affordances and limitations of the visual medium. Icono represents the most recent academic attempt, explicitly designed to match the expressive power of Chinese by creating icon-based words that illustrate their own meaning. Together, these systems constitute an empirical record of what works, what fails, and where the hard problems lie in visual language design.

## Core Components

### Component 1: Leibniz's *Characteristica Universalis* (1666–1679)

- **Purpose**: Create a universal symbolic notation in which every fundamental concept would be represented by a unique "real" character, with complex ideas constructed by combining simpler symbols according to fixed logical rules. The companion *calculus ratiocinator* would allow mechanical computation over these symbols to derive truths.
- **Design Approach**: Algebraic-symbolic. Leibniz envisioned an "alphabet of human thought" — a finite set of primitive concepts assigned symbols that could be manipulated combinatorially. Modeled on and extending conventional algebra and its notation.
- **Key Innovation**: The first clear articulation that meaning could be decomposed into primitives and recombined through formal rules — the same insight that later drove both mathematical logic and NSM.
- **What Happened**: Leibniz outlined the idea in his 1666 dissertation *De Arte Combinatoria* and elaborated it in unpublished manuscripts around 1679. He never completed the system. The project was too ambitious for the available philosophical ontology — Leibniz had no way to empirically determine what the "primitive concepts" actually were.
- **Legacy**: Leibniz's vision directly foreshadowed symbolic logic (developed by Boole, de Morgan, Peirce, Frege), Turing completeness, and modern artificial intelligence. His concept of the *lingua characteristica* (notation) vs. *calculus ratiocinator* (reasoning engine) maps directly onto the distinction between programming languages and computation.
- **Lesson for Visual Language Design**: The decomposition-and-recombination approach is correct in principle, but requires an empirically grounded inventory of primitives. Leibniz lacked what Wierzbicka's NSM and Johnson's image schemas now provide.

### Component 2: ISOTYPE — International System of Typographic Picture Education (1925–1940s)

- **Purpose**: Communicate social, technological, biological, and historical information to general audiences through standardized pictograms. Neurath's guiding principle: "Words divide, pictures unite."
- **Design Approach**: Pictographic-statistical. ISOTYPE used simplified, abstracted pictures of people or things as repeatable units. Greater quantities were represented by more pictograms of the same size, never by enlarging a single pictogram. The system was designed for one-directional communication (expert to public), not dialogue.
- **Key Innovation**: The "transformer" role — Marie Reidemeister (later Marie Neurath) served as the bridge between domain experts and visual designers, balancing expert desire for detail with user need for clarity. This human design process was as important as the symbols themselves.
- **Creative Team**: Otto Neurath (philosopher-director), Gerd Arntz (graphic artist, joined 1929), Marie Reidemeister (transformer).
- **What Happened**: ISOTYPE was successful within its intended domain — statistical communication and public education. It became the foundation for modern information graphics, international signage, and data visualization. But it was never intended as a general-purpose language and cannot express abstract propositions, conditionals, or complex grammar.
- **Legacy**: The majority of graphic communication methods developed by ISOTYPE are accepted as basic principles of visual communication design. Every pictogram on an airplane safety card, every icon on a smartphone, descends from ISOTYPE principles.
- **Lesson for Visual Language Design**: Pictographic clarity works brilliantly for concrete, context-bounded communication. But pictograms alone cannot constitute a language — they lack grammar, abstraction, and compositionality. The "transformer" concept (a human bridge between meaning and visual form) is crucial and often overlooked.

### Component 3: Charles Bliss's Semantography / Blissymbolics (1949–present)

- **Purpose**: Create a universal semantic writing system that would overcome the misunderstandings caused by spoken language, which Bliss blamed (from his experience as a concentration camp survivor) for nationalism and war.
- **Design Approach**: Semantic-compositional. Blissymbolics uses approximately 120 basic geometric symbols (Bliss-characters) that combine to form over 6,500 Bliss-words. Symbols are constructed on a gridded square matrix where orientation, size, and relative position affect meaning. A "ground" line at the base and a "sky" line at the top create a vertical semantic space.
- **Key Innovation**: True semantic compositionality. "House" + "medical" = "hospital." "Feeling" + "up" = "happy." "Eye" + "forward-action" = "to look." Bliss also created an indicator system for grammar: a small square (thing/noun), a small cone (action/verb), a small inverted cone (evaluation/adjective), plus markers for tense, number, and possession.
- **Composition Rules**:
  - Characters combine left-to-right to form words
  - Vertical position on the grid encodes semantic information (earth-related concepts at bottom, sky/abstract at top)
  - Indicators (small supralinear symbols) mark parts of speech and grammatical features
  - "Ago," "now," and "then (future)" suffix time-related characters
  - Number prefixes indicate quantity
  - The possession modifier appears as the final character
- **What Happened**: Bliss's book *Semantography* (1949) was largely ignored. In 1971, Shirley McNaughton at the Ontario Crippled Children's Centre began using the symbols to teach communication to children with cerebral palsy — an application Bliss never intended. By the time Blissymbolics Communication International (BCI) was established, the system was used in 33+ countries, primarily as an AAC tool. Bliss himself was furious at the "corruption" of his universal language dream and fought bitter legal battles with BCI until his death in 1985.
- **Legacy**: The most successful semantic visual language ever created, though its success came in a context (AAC) radically different from its creator's intent. Now maintained by BCI with a standardized vocabulary and teaching methodology.
- **Lesson for Visual Language Design**: Semantic compositionality from geometric primitives works. The indicator system for grammar is clever and extensible. But Blissymbolics reveals three critical problems: (1) proper names cannot be handled without phonetic fallback, (2) complex syntax (embedded clauses, counterfactuals) is extremely difficult to express, and (3) the system found adoption not through universality but through meeting a specific accessibility need. Languages need communities, and communities form around practical necessity, not philosophical ideals.

### Component 4: LoCoS — Lovers Communication System (1964–present)

- **Purpose**: Enable effortless communication "as lovers do" — a visual Esperanto designed as communication for deaf, mute, and illiterate individuals.
- **Design Approach**: Pictogram-ideogram hybrid with spatial grammar. LoCoS uses 18 basic shapes, each assigned a consonant value (enabling a kind of pseudo-phonetic reading). Symbols are positioned within a 3×3 grid, and combinations of basic shapes create complex meanings.
- **Key Innovation**: The grid-based spatial grammar. Position within the 3×3 matrix encodes relationships between concepts. Also innovative: the assignment of consonant values to basic shapes, creating a bridge between the visual and phonetic that most visual languages avoid.
- **Basic Vocabulary**: "Sun"/"day" = circle outline. "Feeling" = heart shape. "Existence at a point" = dot. All concepts and nouns can be created by combining basic symbols.
- **What Happened**: LoCoS remained primarily an academic and design curiosity. Ota later applied his pictographic principles to the ISO 7010 exit sign (the internationally recognized "running man" exit symbol), which became his most lasting practical contribution. The m-LoCoS UI project explored adapting the system for mobile communication interfaces.
- **Legacy**: LoCoS demonstrated that a grid-based spatial grammar is intuitive for simple compositions but becomes ambiguous for complex expressions. The consonant-assignment idea anticipated the phoneme representation challenge but didn't solve it convincingly.
- **Lesson for Visual Language Design**: The 3×3 grid is elegant but limiting — it constrains expressiveness to what can be composed in a small matrix. The phonetic bridge (consonant values) is a genuinely interesting idea that deserves more exploration. LoCoS shows that a system designed for simplicity can achieve basic communication but struggles to scale.

### Component 5: Elephant's Memory (1996–1998)

- **Purpose**: Develop an entirely visual communication system as an experimental "language kitchen" — a playful environment for exploring the nature of language itself.
- **Design Approach**: Non-linear logographic. Approximately 200 combinable logograms with a grammar based on size and spatial relationships rather than linear sequence. Reading/writing has no fixed direction — the eye floats across the composition and constructs meaning relationally, guided by the visual weights and connections of the signs.
- **Key Innovation**: Non-linearity. Where every other visual language system (including Blissymbolics) retained the linear left-to-right reading convention of alphabetic writing, Elephant's Memory rejected linearity entirely. Meaning emerges from spatial composition — relative size, proximity, enclosure, connection — rather than from sequential ordering.
- **Influences**: Wittgenstein's philosophy of language games, Chinese calligraphy, Swiss pictographic design tradition.
- **What Happened**: Elephant's Memory was created by Timothée Ingen-Housz, a French-Dutch media artist, and deployed primarily as an educational tool for children and teenagers. The system operated as a research project and artistic intervention rather than a practical communication tool.
- **Legacy**: Demonstrated that visual grammar can be genuinely non-linear, exploiting two-dimensional spatial relationships in ways that linear text cannot. But also demonstrated the cognitive cost: readers unfamiliar with the system find it disorienting without clear reading order.
- **Lesson for Visual Language Design**: Non-linear spatial grammar is theoretically powerful — it can encode simultaneous relationships that linear sequence cannot. But human reading is a learned behavior, and centuries of linear text have created strong cognitive expectations. A viable visual language may need to support both linear and spatial reading modes, or provide clear compositional cues that guide the eye.

### Component 6: Xu Bing's *Book from the Ground* (2003–2012)

- **Purpose**: Create a readable narrative composed entirely of universally understood symbols and icons — proving that a story could be told without any words from any spoken language.
- **Design Approach**: Narrative-pictographic. Xu Bing spent seven years gathering, experimenting with, revising, and arranging thousands of pictograms drawn from smartphones, computers, and public signage to construct a complete graphic novel telling 24 hours in the life of "Mr. Black," a typical urban white-collar worker.
- **Key Innovation**: Demonstrated that existing, culturally embedded pictograms (emoji, app icons, public signage) already constitute an emerging global visual vocabulary. Xu Bing's studio also created a character database software where users can enter English or Chinese text and receive a translation into the book's visual lexicon.
- **What Happened**: Published by MIT Press in 2014, the book was internationally exhibited and reviewed. It succeeded as art and as a proof of concept, but its "language" is parasitic on shared contemporary urban culture — the symbols are only "universal" for people who use smartphones and live in modern cities.
- **Legacy**: Demonstrated both the surprising reach and the fundamental limitation of culturally emergent pictographic communication. The symbols work because readers share a cultural context, not because the symbols are inherently meaningful.
- **Lesson for Visual Language Design**: Cultural context does tremendous heavy lifting in symbol interpretation. A system that relies on culturally specific icons (mailbox, shopping cart, floppy disk) is not truly universal — it is merely widely shared within a particular technological moment. True universality requires grounding in something more fundamental than cultural convention.

### Component 7: Icono (2023)

- **Purpose**: Create a universal written language where words are strings of icons that illustrate what they stand for, aiming to match the expressive power of Chinese while being inherently reader-friendly and international.
- **Design Approach**: Icon-string compositional. In Icono, words are typically strings of icons that illustrate their referents. "Train" = icon of a train. "Future" = clock icon with clockwise arrow. "Mammal" = icons of a cow and a mouse — their combination's meaning given by what they have in common. Sentence structure is graphically revealed before linguistic parsing begins.
- **Key Innovation**: Self-illustrating semantics. Unlike Blissymbolics (which uses abstract geometric composition), Icono uses concrete pictographic icons arranged in strings where the composition itself hints at the meaning. Also innovative: the explicit goal of matching Chinese-level expressiveness with a peer-reviewed theoretical foundation.
- **Author**: Peter Kramer, University of Padua. Published in *Frontiers in Psychology*, Volume 14, July 28, 2023.
- **Target Applications**: People with dyslexia, aphasia, cerebral palsy, autism with speech impairment — plus broader use as an international auxiliary written language.
- **What Happened**: Too early to assess adoption. The peer-reviewed publication gives Icono more academic legitimacy than most visual language proposals. The system is well-theorized but awaits community formation and practical testing.
- **Lesson for Visual Language Design**: Icono's icon-string approach is intriguing but faces the same challenge as all pictographic systems: concrete concepts are easy (train, cow, mouse), but abstract concepts require metaphorical composition (future = clock + rotation). The question is whether the metaphorical compositions are culturally transparent or require learning. The peer-reviewed approach to visual language design sets a valuable precedent.

## How It Works: The Design Space

### Design Dimension 1: Iconicity Spectrum

Each system occupies a different position on the iconicity-abstraction spectrum:
- **High Iconicity**: ISOTYPE, Xu Bing (pictures look like what they represent)
- **Medium Iconicity**: LoCoS, Icono, Elephant's Memory (mix of pictures and ideographic composition)
- **Low Iconicity**: Blissymbolics, Leibniz (abstract geometric/algebraic symbols)

### Design Dimension 2: Compositionality

- **Fully Compositional**: Blissymbolics (meaning derived from composition of base elements), Leibniz (algebraic combination)
- **Partially Compositional**: LoCoS (grid-based composition), Icono (icon strings with implicit composition rules), Elephant's Memory (spatial composition)
- **Non-Compositional**: ISOTYPE (individual pictograms, no grammar), Xu Bing (culturally embedded icons arranged narratively)

### Design Dimension 3: Grammar Type

- **Linear Grammar**: Blissymbolics (left-to-right with indicators), Icono (icon strings with graphical sentence structure)
- **Spatial Grammar**: LoCoS (3×3 grid), Elephant's Memory (free spatial composition based on size/proximity)
- **No Grammar**: ISOTYPE (statistical presentation rules only), Xu Bing (narrative sequencing only)
- **Formal Logic**: Leibniz (calculus ratiocinator — never completed)

### Design Dimension 4: Relationship to Phonetics

- **Phonetically Bridged**: LoCoS (consonant assignments), Chinese (phonetic radicals)
- **Phonetically Independent**: Blissymbolics, ISOTYPE, Elephant's Memory, Leibniz
- **Culturally Dependent**: Xu Bing (symbols parasitic on cultural context), Icono (icon recognition requires cultural familiarity)

  - String (Icono): Icon sequence with graphical sentence structure
- **Connections**: Labeled arrows showing reading order/direction for each
- **Notes**: Highlight how each grammar type handles the relationships "agent," "action," and "patient" differently

### Diagram 5: Success and Failure Patterns
- **Type**: Flowchart / decision tree
- **Purpose**: Map the factors that led each system to its particular outcome (adoption, non-adoption, partial success in unintended domain)
- **Elements**:
  - Design choices (compositionality, grammar, phonetic bridge, learnability)
  - Environmental factors (community need, institutional support, technological moment)
  - Outcomes (universal adoption attempt, domain-restricted success, AAC adoption, art/design influence)
- **Connections**: Decision paths showing which combination of factors led to which outcomes
- **Notes**: Highlight that no system achieved its creator's original goal — success always came in unexpected directions

### Diagram 6: Iconicity vs. Expressiveness Trade-off
- **Type**: Curve / spectrum diagram
- **Purpose**: Show the inverse relationship between iconicity (how much symbols look like what they represent) and expressiveness (ability to encode abstract concepts)
- **Elements**:
  - X-axis: Iconicity (high → low)
  - Y-axis: Expressiveness (limited → full)
  - Systems plotted along the curve
  - The "natural language" reference point for expressiveness
  - The "emoji" reference point for iconicity
- **Connections**: Curve showing the trade-off, with annotations about why each system occupies its position
- **Notes**: The challenge for a metaphor-based visual language is to break this trade-off by using image schemas (which are both visual and abstract) as the bridge

### Diagram 7: Evolution from Pictograph to Abstract Symbol
- **Type**: Sequential transformation diagram
- **Purpose**: Show how visual symbols evolve from concrete pictographs to abstract forms over time, using examples from Chinese, Blissymbolics, and sign language
- **Elements**:
  - Chinese: Oracle bone → bronze inscription → seal script → clerical → modern character (for "sun," "mountain," "water")
  - Blissymbolics: Pictographic intent → geometric abstraction
  - Sign languages: Iconic gesture → conventionalized sign
- **Connections**: Transformation arrows showing stages of abstraction
- **Notes**: Illustrate that all visual communication systems drift toward abstraction under pressure of efficiency and frequency of use

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: Blissymbolics Composition
- **Scenario**: Expressing "I feel happy" in Blissymbolics
- **Illustration**: "Person" (stick figure) + "feeling" (heart) + "up" (upward arrow) — with the verb indicator (small cone) over the compound
- **Key Insight**: The system achieves basic compositional semantics through geometric combination. "Happy" is literally composed as "feeling-up" — a visual expression of the conceptual metaphor HAPPY IS UP. This is exactly the kind of compositional metaphorical encoding that a new visual language would systematize.

### Example 2: ISOTYPE Data Communication
- **Scenario**: Showing steel production by country
- **Illustration**: Rows of identical factory icons, one per million tons, arranged by country. More factories = more production.
- **Key Insight**: ISOTYPE works brilliantly for quantitative comparison but cannot express "If steel production had been higher in 1932, the economic recovery might have been faster." The system communicates data, not propositions.

### Example 3: Icono's Self-Illustrating Words
- **Scenario**: Expressing "mammal" in Icono
- **Illustration**: Icons of a cow and a mouse side by side — "mammal" is what they have in common
- **Key Insight**: This is a visual analogy, not a definition. It works for categories that can be exemplified by prototypes, but how do you express "abstract," "justice," or "irony" through exemplar pairs? The system faces a ceiling for concepts that lack concrete prototypes.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: Visual languages are just "universal picture dictionaries" — collections of recognizable icons.
  - **Reality**: The critical distinction is between a vocabulary (collection of symbols) and a language (vocabulary + grammar + compositionality). Emoji is a vocabulary; Blissymbolics is (closer to) a language. Most "visual language" projects are actually visual vocabularies that lack the grammatical machinery for open-ended expression.

- **Misconception**: Previous visual languages failed because their symbols weren't clear enough.
  - **Reality**: Symbol clarity was rarely the primary problem. The failures are grammatical (inability to express complex syntax), practical (no community of users), and theoretical (no solution to the phoneme problem). Even perfectly clear symbols cannot express "If I hadn't seen you yesterday, I would have called" without grammatical infrastructure.

- **Misconception**: Chinese characters prove that visual writing works at scale, so other visual languages should too.
  - **Reality**: Chinese is a glottographic system — it encodes the sounds and morphemes of spoken Chinese. Over 80% of characters include a phonetic component. Chinese is not a semasiographic system that represents meaning independently of spoken language. The comparison is misleading.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **Emoji as an emergent quasi-semasiographic system**: Emoji function as a limited semasiographic system for emotional tone and concrete objects in digital communication. They demonstrate that culturally embedded pictograms can communicate across language barriers — but only within a restricted expressive range. Emoji cannot express propositions, conditions, or abstract arguments.

- **Mathematical notation as a successful semasiographic system**: Mathematical notation represents meaning directly without encoding speech sounds, works identically across all spoken languages, and achieves extraordinary precision and productivity. But it is domain-restricted to quantitative and logical relationships. The question is whether its success model can be extended to general-purpose communication.

- **Blissymbolics in AAC vs. as universal language**: Blissymbolics succeeded in AAC precisely because the context was constrained — communication partners could help disambiguate, the vocabulary was staged and taught, and the alternative (no communication at all) created high motivation to learn. These are different conditions from universal language adoption, where people already have working languages.

## Related Concepts

- **Semasiographic vs. Glottographic Systems** (Category 2): The theoretical framework that classifies each historical system as either meaning-representing or speech-representing
- **Semantic Primitives and NSM** (Category 3): Wierzbicka's primes as a candidate for what Leibniz was looking for — the "alphabet of human thought"
- **Image Schemas as Visual Building Blocks** (Category 4): The theoretical core that could ground a new visual language in embodied experience rather than cultural convention
- **Compositionality** (Category 5): The technical challenge that Blissymbolics partially solved and that any successor must address more completely
- **The Phoneme Representation Problem** (Category 6): The challenge that no fully semasiographic system has solved without phonetic fallback
