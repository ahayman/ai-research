# Information Structure: Given vs. New, Theme vs. Rheme, Focus

## Research Document: Theoretical Foundations

---

## Overview

Information structure is the linguistic system that governs how speakers organize information within and across sentences — what is presented as already known ("given") versus what is introduced as the communicative point ("new"), what is the topic of the sentence versus what is being said about it, and which element receives informational prominence ("focus"). It is not a single monolithic system but a cluster of interacting dimensions that speakers manage simultaneously, largely unconsciously, in every utterance they produce.

Information structure operates at the interface of syntax, phonology (prosody), semantics, and pragmatics. It is sometimes called "information packaging" (after Chafe 1976), reflecting the insight that speakers make choices not just about *what* to say but about *how to package* what they say — how to arrange the same propositional content into a form that is maximally processable and communicatively effective for the listener in the current discourse context.

For autistic individuals, information structure is a particularly consequential area because it operates largely through implicit expectations that are rarely made explicit in language instruction. When someone says "That's an odd way to put it" or "You're not answering my question" (even though the propositional content of the answer is correct), the issue is often not *what* was said but *how it was packaged* — which element was placed in focus, what was treated as given vs. new, whether the sentence began with expected or unexpected material. Understanding information structure as a learnable system — with identifiable principles and predictable patterns — converts these invisible expectations into visible, analyzable mechanisms.

**Note on cross-references**: This document provides standalone treatment of information structure. ## 1. THE PROBLEM INFORMATION STRUCTURE SOLVES

### 1.1 The Same Propositional Content, Differently Packaged

Consider these sentences — they all describe the same event (the same proposition), but they differ in how the information is arranged:

1. "John bought the car."
2. "The car was bought by John."
3. "It was John who bought the car."
4. "It was the car that John bought."
5. "What John bought was the car."
6. "The car, John bought."
7. "There was this car that John bought."

Each sentence asserts the same truth-conditional content: John is the agent, buying is the action, the car is the patient. A truth-conditional semantics would treat them as equivalent. But they are not communicatively equivalent. Each answers a different implicit question, fits a different discourse context, and creates different expectations about what will come next. The differences are information-structural.

**Why these differences matter**: If someone asks "Who bought the car?" and you answer "The car was bought by John" (sentence 2), you are putting "the car" in the subject/topic position — treating it as the starting point — when the question is about the *buyer*. The information is correct, but the *packaging* doesn't fit the discourse context. The answer "JOHN bought the car" (sentence 1, with stress on John) or "It was JOHN who bought the car" (sentence 3) packages the same content in a way that directly addresses the question. This mismatch between question and answer packaging is a significant source of communicative friction.

### 1.2 Chafe's "Packaging" Metaphor

Wallace Chafe (1976) introduced the now-standard metaphor of information "packaging" in his influential paper "Givenness, Contrastiveness, Definiteness, Subjects, Topics, and Point of View." Chafe observed that the same semantic content can be expressed through multiple syntactic forms, and the choice among these forms reflects the speaker's assessment of what the listener currently has in mind. As Chafe characterized it:

> "I have been using the term *packaging* to refer to the kind of phenomenon at issue here, with the idea that it has to do primarily with how the message is sent and only secondarily with the message itself, just as the packaging of toothpaste can affect sales in partial independence of the quality of the toothpaste inside." (Chafe, 1976, p. 28)

The key insight is that information structure is about the *presentation* of information, not the information itself. Two speakers describing the same event may choose different packaging depending on:
- What they believe the listener already knows
- What they want the listener to pay attention to
- What question they are implicitly answering
- Where in the discourse they are (what has been said before, what will come next)
- What they want to contrast or highlight

### 1.3 The Cooperative Motivation

Information structure serves the Cooperative Principle (Grice, 1975). Specifically, it serves the **Maxim of Manner** ("be perspicuous" — avoid obscurity, ambiguity, unnecessary prolixity; be orderly) and can also serve the **Maxim of Relation/Relevance** (make your contribution relevant to the current discourse topic). When speakers package information in a way that matches the discourse context — putting given before new, focusing on the element the listener needs, matching the question under discussion — they reduce processing effort for the listener. When they package information in a way that mismatches the context, they create processing difficulty even when the propositional content is perfectly true and relevant.

Clark and Haviland (1977) formalized this as the **Given-New Contract**: an implicit agreement between speakers and listeners that the speaker will mark given information (information the listener can already identify) as given and new information (information the listener cannot yet identify) as new, and that the listener will use given information as an anchor to integrate new information into their existing mental model. Violations of this contract — presenting as "given" something the listener does not in fact know, or presenting as "new" something already established — create processing difficulty and can cause communication breakdowns.

## 2. THE GIVEN-NEW DISTINCTION

### 2.1 What "Given" and "New" Mean

The given-new distinction is the most fundamental dimension of information structure. At its simplest:

- **Given (old) information**: Content that the speaker treats as already available to the listener — either because it was mentioned earlier in the discourse, is visible in the physical context, or can be inferred from what has been established. Given information is the *anchor* — the point of connection between the current utterance and the listener's existing mental model.

- **New information**: Content that the speaker treats as not yet available to the listener — the actual *payload* of the utterance, the reason for speaking. New information is what advances the discourse.

However, the given-new distinction is more complex than a simple binary, and different researchers have defined "given" and "new" in different ways, leading to significant theoretical debate.

### 2.2 Halliday (1967): Given as "Recoverable"

Michael Halliday, in his foundational work on information structure within Systemic Functional Linguistics (SFL), defined given and new in terms of **recoverability**:

- **Given**: "that which is presented by the speaker as recoverable, anaphorically or situationally" — information that the listener can recover from the preceding text, the situation, or shared knowledge.
- **New**: "that which is presented as non-recoverable" — information the listener cannot recover and must therefore be told.

For Halliday, the key marker of new information in English is prosodic prominence. The **information focus** falls on the tonic syllable — the syllable carrying the main pitch movement in an information unit (roughly, an intonation phrase). Material that is new is accented; material that is given is deaccented.

Halliday distinguished between **marked** and **unmarked** information focus:
- **Unmarked focus**: The tonic syllable falls on the last lexical item in the information unit. This is the default — when there is no special reason to focus elsewhere, prominence falls at the end. (This corresponds to the end-focus principle.)
- **Marked focus**: The tonic syllable falls on a non-final element, indicating that the speaker is choosing to highlight that element specifically — typically for contrastive or corrective purposes.

This means that in English, the default position for new information is **at the end of the clause**, and the default position for given information is **at the beginning**. When speakers deviate from this default, they signal something special — contrast, correction, emphasis, surprise.

### 2.3 Prince (1981): A Taxonomy of Givenness

Ellen Prince (1981), in her important paper "Toward a Taxonomy of Given-New Information," argued that researchers had been conflating several different notions under the umbrella of "given" and "new." She proposed a more nuanced taxonomy based on two independent dimensions:

**Discourse status** — has the referent been mentioned in the discourse?
- **Discourse-old**: The referent has been explicitly mentioned in the prior discourse (evoked). "John walked in. **He** sat down." — "He" is discourse-old.
- **Discourse-new**: The referent has not been mentioned before in this discourse. "**A man** walked in." — "a man" is discourse-new.

**Hearer status** — does the speaker believe the listener already knows about the referent?
- **Hearer-old**: The speaker believes the referent is already known to the listener, whether or not it has been mentioned in the current discourse. "Did you hear about **the earthquake in Turkey**?" — hearer-old if both interlocutors follow the news.
- **Hearer-new**: The speaker believes the referent is entirely new to the listener. "I met **a woman named Dr. Patel** at the conference." — hearer-new if the listener has never heard of this person.

**Inferrables**: A crucial intermediate category — entities that haven't been mentioned and aren't independently known to the listener, but can be inferred from entities that have been mentioned. "I went to a wedding last weekend. **The bride** wore blue." — "the bride" is discourse-new (not previously mentioned) but **inferrable** from "wedding" (weddings have brides). The use of the definite article "the" signals that the speaker treats "bride" as accessible even though it's discourse-new.

**Why Prince's distinctions matter**: They explain why certain expressions feel natural or odd in context. "I went to the store. The cashier was rude." works because "cashier" is inferrable from "store." But "I went to the store. The pilot was rude." creates a jarring effect — "pilot" is not inferrable from "store," so using the definite article (which presupposes accessibility) creates a processing mismatch. The listener has to work to figure out why the speaker is treating "pilot" as given information.

### 2.4 Chafe (1976, 1987, 1994): Activation States

Wallace Chafe developed an influential alternative to the binary given/new distinction, based on the metaphor of **cognitive activation**. He proposed that concepts in a person's mind exist in three activation states:

- **Active** (given): Currently in the focus of the listener's consciousness — "lit up" in working memory. Active concepts are typically pronominalized or deaccented because no processing effort is needed to access them.
- **Accessible** (semi-active): Not currently in focus but easily activatable — either because they were recently active (and have faded), because they are inferrable from something currently active, or because they are part of a schema associated with something active. Accessible concepts can be referred to with definite noun phrases and may or may not receive prosodic prominence.
- **Inactive** (new): Not currently in consciousness at all. Inactive concepts require full noun phrases and typically receive prosodic prominence because they need to be brought into the listener's awareness from scratch.

This three-way distinction captures gradience that the binary given/new distinction cannot. Consider:

> "I went to a restaurant last night. The waiter was incredibly rude."

"Waiter" is not discourse-old (not previously mentioned) and is not strictly "given." But it is not fully "new" either — it is **accessible** via the schema activated by "restaurant" (restaurants have waiters). Chafe's framework predicts that accessible entities will pattern somewhere between given and new — they can take definite articles (like given entities) but may receive some prosodic prominence (like new entities), depending on how easily accessible they are.

Chafe connected activation states to the **limitation on working memory**: given information is what is currently in the listener's limited-capacity consciousness; new information is what needs to be brought in. This cognitive grounding makes the system predictable — the speaker's task is to manage the listener's attention, bringing concepts into consciousness one at a time in a manageable order.

### 2.5 Clark and Haviland (1977): The Given-New Contract

Herbert Clark and Susan Haviland (1977) formalized the cooperative dimension of information structure as the **Given-New Contract**, which they described as a tacit agreement between speakers and listeners:

**The speaker's obligation**: Mark as "given" only that information for which the listener already has a unique antecedent in memory. Mark as "new" the information the listener should integrate.

**The listener's strategy** (a three-step process):
1. Identify the given information in the incoming sentence
2. Search memory for a matching antecedent (a "unique antecedent")
3. Integrate the new information by attaching it to the antecedent found in step 2

When the contract is honored, processing is rapid. When it is violated, the listener must perform an **accommodation** or **bridging inference** — constructing the missing antecedent through inference. Clark and Haviland showed experimentally that bridging inferences take measurable processing time.

**Example of smooth given-new processing**:
> "Mary got some beer out of the car. The beer was warm."

"The beer" in the second sentence is given (it was just mentioned). The listener finds the antecedent easily and integrates the new information ("was warm").

**Example requiring a bridging inference**:
> "Mary got some picnic supplies out of the car. The beer was warm."

"The beer" has no direct antecedent — "picnic supplies" was mentioned, not "beer." The listener must construct a bridge: picnic supplies can include beer, therefore "the beer" is part of the picnic supplies. This bridge takes extra processing time (verified experimentally by Clark and Haviland using reading time measurements).

**Example of a violation**:
> "Mary went to the park. The beer was warm."

There is no obvious bridge between "park" and "beer." The listener struggles to find an antecedent, and the sentence feels disconnected or confusing. The given-new contract has been violated — the speaker has marked "beer" as given (using "the") when the listener has no basis for treating it as accessible.

### 2.6 The Interaction with Definiteness

The given-new distinction interacts closely with the English article system:

- **Definite articles ("the")**: Signal that the speaker treats the referent as identifiable to the listener — the listener should already have or be able to construct an antecedent for it. This roughly maps to "given" or "accessible."
- **Indefinite articles ("a/an")**: Signal that the speaker treats the referent as not yet identifiable to the listener — a new entity being introduced into the discourse. This roughly maps to "new."

This is a rough mapping, not a perfect one. There are systematic exceptions:
- **Bridging definites**: "The" used with inferrables — "I got in the car. The engine wouldn't start." (The engine is discourse-new but inferrable.)
- **Indefinite given**: "A friend of mine — you know, the one who lives in Boston — called me." (The referent is eventually identified as hearer-old, but introduced with "a.")
- **Generic definites**: "The dog is a loyal animal." (Not referring to any specific known dog.)

Still, the rough mapping between definite/given and indefinite/new is a useful heuristic, and violations of it are a common source of processing difficulty.

## 3. THEME AND RHEME

### 3.1 Origins: The Prague School

The theme-rheme distinction originates with the **Prague School** of linguistics, particularly the work of Vilém Mathesius (1882–1945), who developed the concepts of **Functional Sentence Perspective (FSP)** in the 1920s–1940s. Mathesius argued that sentences have a communicative function beyond their grammatical structure, and that this function involves organizing information into what the speaker starts from (the theme) and what they say about it (the rheme).

**Theme (základ)**: The point of departure of the sentence — what the speaker is talking about. Mathesius characterized it as "that which is known or at least obvious in the given situation and from which the speaker proceeds."

**Rheme (jádro, literally "core")**: The core of the utterance — what the speaker says about the theme, the communicative payload. The rheme carries the new or important information.

The Prague School view embedded theme-rheme in a broader theory of how language serves communicative functions. Unlike the American structuralist tradition, which focused on formal grammatical description, the Prague School was interested in language *in use* — how speakers deploy grammatical resources to achieve communicative goals. This functional orientation directly influenced later work by Halliday and others.

### 3.2 Communicative Dynamism (Firbas)

Jan Firbas (1928–2000), a key figure in the second generation of the Prague School, developed the concept of **Communicative Dynamism (CD)** — the idea that each element in a sentence contributes differently to the "pushing forward" of communication.

Firbas argued that rather than a binary theme/rheme split, elements in a sentence exist on a **gradient** of communicative dynamism:

- **Theme proper**: The element with the lowest CD — the starting point, carrying the least new information.
- **Transition**: Elements with intermediate CD — typically the verb, which connects theme to rheme.
- **Rheme proper**: The element with the highest CD — the communicative peak, carrying the most new information.

For example, in "John (theme) bought (transition) a new car (rheme)":
- "John" has low CD — we already know who we're talking about.
- "bought" has intermediate CD — it advances the message but doesn't carry the main informational payload.
- "a new car" has the highest CD — this is the point of the utterance, the new information.

Firbas identified several factors that determine an element's CD:
1. **Linear modification**: Position in the sentence. In English, later elements tend to have higher CD (this aligns with end-focus).
2. **Contextual factor**: Whether the element is context-dependent (given/recoverable) or context-independent (new). Context-dependent elements have lower CD regardless of position.
3. **Semantic factor**: The inherent semantic content of the element. Dynamic semantic roles (Agent, Patient of action verbs) tend to carry more CD than static ones.

The relative weight of these factors varies by language. In Czech (Firbas's primary language of study), which has relatively free word order, the contextual factor is dominant — given information tends to come first and new information last, with word order adjusted accordingly. In English, with its more fixed SVO order, the interplay between linear position and contextual weight is more complex.

### 3.3 Halliday's Theme/Rheme in Systemic Functional Linguistics

Michael Halliday (1967, 1985, 1994) adopted the Prague School's theme-rheme distinction and reinterpreted it within his Systemic Functional Linguistics (SFL) framework, with some significant modifications.

**Halliday's definition of Theme**: The Theme is "the element which serves as the point of departure of the message; it is that which locates and orients the clause within its context" (Halliday & Matthiessen, 2004, p. 64). In English, the Theme is **positionally defined** — it is whatever comes first in the clause, up to and including the first experiential element (participant, process, or circumstance).

**Halliday's definition of Rheme**: Everything that follows the Theme. The Rheme is where the clause develops — it is what the speaker says about the theme.

**Key difference from the Prague School**: Halliday defines Theme **positionally** (it's whatever is first), whereas the Prague School defines it **functionally** (it's whatever is the point of departure, regardless of position). In English, these largely coincide because English has relatively fixed word order (SVO). But in free word order languages, the two definitions can diverge. This positional definition has been criticized (e.g., by Downing, 1991, and Fries, 1995) as too mechanistic, but Halliday defended it on the grounds that clause-initial position in English IS the grammaticalized position for theme.

### 3.4 Types of Theme (Halliday)

Halliday distinguished three types of Theme, reflecting the **metafunctions** of his SFL framework:

**Topical (Experiential) Theme**: The first element that participates in the transitivity structure of the clause — a participant, process, or circumstance. Every clause must have a topical theme.
- "**Sarah** opened the door." (participant as theme)
- "**Yesterday** the team won the match." (circumstance as theme)
- "**Running** is my favorite exercise." (process as theme — nominalized)

**Textual Theme**: Elements that connect the clause to its discourse context — conjunctions, conjunctive adjuncts, and continuatives. These precede the topical theme.
- "**But** Sarah didn't answer."
- "**However**, the results were inconclusive."
- "**Well**, I suppose so."
- "**And then** he left."

**Interpersonal Theme**: Elements that enact the speaker-listener relationship — vocatives, modal adjuncts, finite verbs in questions. These also precede the topical theme.
- "**Frankly**, I don't care." (modal adjunct)
- "**John**, can you help?" (vocative)
- "**Is** Sarah coming?" (finite verb in yes/no question)
- "**Perhaps** the meeting will be canceled." (modal adjunct)

In a fully extended theme, these stack: **Textual ^ Interpersonal ^ Topical**.
> "**But** [textual] **honestly** [interpersonal] **I** [topical] don't understand why."

### 3.5 Marked vs. Unmarked Theme

**Unmarked Theme**: The most "expected" or typical choice for theme in a given clause type:
- In declarative clauses: the grammatical **Subject** is the unmarked theme. "**The cat** sat on the mat."
- In interrogative clauses (wh-questions): the **Wh-element** is the unmarked theme. "**Who** called?"
- In interrogative clauses (yes/no questions): the **Finite verb** is the unmarked theme. "**Did** she call?"
- In imperative clauses: the **Verb** (or "you" + verb) is the unmarked theme. "**Sit** down."

**Marked Theme**: A theme that deviates from these defaults — typically a Complement or Adjunct placed in clause-initial position instead of the Subject:
- "**That book** I really enjoyed." (Complement as theme — topicalization)
- "**On Tuesday** we had a meeting." (Circumstantial adjunct as theme)
- "**Slowly and carefully** she opened the box." (Manner adjunct as theme)
- "**Never before** had I seen such chaos." (Negative adjunct as theme, triggering subject-auxiliary inversion)

Marked themes are communicatively significant — they signal that the speaker has deliberately chosen a non-default starting point, typically for one of these reasons:
1. **Establishing a temporal or spatial frame**: "In 1945, the war ended." (framing the event in time)
2. **Contrasting with previous discourse**: "John passed the exam. **Mary**, on the other hand, failed." (shifting the topic for contrast)
3. **Connecting to the previous discourse topic**: When the non-subject element is what links most directly to the prior context
4. **Creating dramatic effect**: "**Into the room** walked a stranger." (scene-setting)

### 3.6 Thematic Progression

How Theme choices evolve across a sequence of clauses creates the texture and flow of a text. Frantisek Danes (1974) identified several major patterns of **thematic progression** — the way themes are connected from sentence to sentence:

**1. Constant Theme (Theme reiteration)**:
The same theme is maintained across multiple clauses.

> "**The president** announced the new policy. **He** said it would take effect immediately. **He** also noted that exceptions might be granted."

This pattern maintains a single "aboutness" — good for tracking a single participant or topic. Common in narrative when following one character's actions.

**2. Linear (Zigzag) Theme**:
The Rheme of one clause becomes the Theme of the next.

> "**A strange letter** arrived in the mail. **The letter** contained a map. **The map** showed the location of a buried chest."

This pattern creates a chain of interconnection — each sentence picks up where the last left off. It is effective for building logical or temporal sequences and introducing new entities progressively.

**3. Split (Derived) Theme**:
A "hypertheme" is divided into sub-themes that are developed in subsequent clauses.

> "**The plan** had two phases. **The first phase** involved reconnaissance. **The second phase** involved direct action."

This pattern is effective for organizing complex information with multiple subparts.

**4. Concentrating/Merging Theme**:
Multiple themes from separate clauses converge into a single theme.

> "**The wind** howled. **The rain** pounded. **The cold** crept in. **All three** made sleep impossible."

"All three" merges the separate themes of the previous clauses.

Understanding these patterns is crucial because they are how texts achieve coherence at the level of information flow. Deviation from expected thematic progression — random or unmotivated shifts in theme — is a major cause of texts feeling "choppy," "disconnected," or "hard to follow."

## 4. FOCUS STRUCTURE

### 4.1 What Focus Is

Focus is the dimension of information structure concerned with **which part of the sentence carries the informational prominence** — which element is the "point" of the utterance.

Krifka (2008), in his comprehensive synthesis "Basic Notions of Information Structure," defines focus as follows: "Focus indicates the presence of alternatives that are relevant for the interpretation of linguistic expressions." The focused element is that which is selected from a set of contextually available alternatives.

Focus is realized in English primarily through **prosodic prominence** — stress (pitch accent). The focused element receives the nuclear accent of the intonation phrase. This connection between focus and prosody is the primary bridge between this category and Category 15 (Prosodic Pragmatics), where the acoustic details of focus marking are treated in depth.

### 4.2 Types of Focus

**Presentational (Information) Focus**: Also called "new-information focus." The focused element introduces new information into the discourse, answering an explicit or implicit question. There is no element being contrasted or corrected — just information being provided.

- Q: "What happened?" A: "[John bought a car]_F" (the entire proposition is new — broad focus)
- Q: "What did John buy?" A: "John bought [a car]_F" (narrow focus on "a car")

**Contrastive Focus**: The focused element is explicitly contrasted with an alternative — either correcting a previous assertion, selecting among explicitly mentioned options, or rejecting one possibility in favor of another.

- "I didn't buy a CAR — I bought a TRUCK." (correction)
- "JOHN bought the car, not BILL." (identification by contrast)
- A: "Did Mary call?" B: "No, JOHN called." (correction of presupposed identity)

**Exhaustive (Identificational) Focus**: The focused element is identified as the *only* member of the alternative set for which the proposition holds. This is sometimes considered a subtype of contrastive focus.

- "It was JOHN who bought the car." (exhaustive — John and no one else)
- "Only JOHN bought the car." (explicitly exhaustive via "only")

Kiss (1998) argued for a categorical distinction between **information focus** (which merely marks new information) and **identificational focus** (which carries an exhaustive interpretation — "it is X and only X"). She showed that Hungarian grammatically distinguishes these two focus types through different syntactic positions, supporting the claim that they are distinct phenomena and not merely stronger/weaker versions of the same thing.

### 4.3 Broad Focus vs. Narrow Focus

Focus can span different amounts of the sentence:

**Broad focus** (wide focus): The entire sentence or a large constituent is in focus. This typically occurs in "all-new" contexts — answers to broad questions like "What happened?" The nuclear accent falls in its default position (on the last accentable element of the clause), and there is no deaccenting of material.

> Q: "What happened?"
> A: "John bought a CAR." (broad focus — everything is new)

**Narrow focus**: A single constituent is in focus. This occurs when a specific element is being highlighted, contrasted, or provided as the answer to a specific question. Material outside the focus is part of the "background" or "given" and may be deaccented.

> Q: "Who bought a car?"
> A: "JOHN bought a car." (narrow focus on John)

**The ambiguity problem**: In English, broad focus and narrow focus on a clause-final element are **prosodically identical**. "John bought a CAR" with default final accent could be either:
- Broad focus (everything is new) — answering "What happened?"
- Narrow focus on "car" — answering "What did John buy?"

Disambiguation relies on context. Non-final narrow focus is unambiguous: "JOHN bought a car" can only be narrow focus on John.

Selkirk (1984, 1995) proposed **Focus Projection** rules to explain this: F-marking on a word can "project" to its phrase and beyond, under specific syntactic conditions. Accent on a head can project to the phrase; accent on an internal argument can project to the head's phrase. This is why accent on the object ("car") is ambiguous — it can project to the VP or the entire clause.

### 4.4 Focus and Presupposition/Background

Every sentence with a focused element implicitly divides into:
- **Focus**: The highlighted, new, informative portion
- **Background (Open Proposition)**: The presupposed, given portion — what's taken for granted

Following Jackendoff (1972), the background can be represented as an **open proposition** — a proposition with a variable in the focus position:

> "JOHN called." → Focus: John. Background: x called. (Someone called — who?)
> "John CALLED." → Focus: called. Background: John did x. (John did something — what?)

This analysis connects focus to the **Question Under Discussion (QUD)** framework: the background corresponds to the question, and the focus provides the answer. The background "x called" corresponds to the question "Who called?" and the focus "John" provides the answer. (See section 5 on QUD.)

### 4.5 Givenness and Focus (Schwarzschild 1999)

Roger Schwarzschild (1999), in his paper "GIVENness, AvoidF, and Other Constraints on the Placement of Accent," provided an influential account that unifies the treatment of focus and givenness. His key idea: **deaccenting (not accenting) is driven by givenness — an expression is deaccented when its content is "given" (has a salient antecedent in the discourse or context)**. The accent that remains when everything given is deaccented is the focus accent.

Schwarzschild defined **GIVEN** as follows: An expression counts as GIVEN if it has an antecedent in the discourse that entails it (modulo a focus-appropriate transformation). This definition captures both discourse-old entities and entities that are inferrable from discourse-old content.

His system uses two constraints:
1. **GIVEN**: A constituent that is not F-marked (not focused) must be GIVEN.
2. **AvoidF**: Do not F-mark without necessity (i.e., don't focus elements unnecessarily).

These two constraints, working together, predict that accent falls on what is new and away from what is given — deriving the given-new accent pattern from principled constraints rather than stipulation. The account is significant because it shows that "novelty focus" (accenting new information) and "contrastive focus" (accenting a contrasted element) arise from the same underlying mechanism — both involve accenting the non-given element.

## 5. THE QUESTION UNDER DISCUSSION (QUD) FRAMEWORK

### 5.1 Roberts' Theory

Craige Roberts (1996/2012) proposed one of the most influential frameworks for understanding how information structure connects to discourse structure: the **Question Under Discussion (QUD)** framework.

The core idea: discourse is organized as a series of **implicit or explicit questions** that conversational participants are collaboratively trying to answer. At any point in a conversation, there is a **current QUD** — the question that is currently being addressed. Each assertion made by a speaker should be **relevant** to the current QUD — it should at least partially answer the question.

**The discourse structure** is modeled as a stack of QUDs. The topmost QUD is the one currently being addressed. Questions can be introduced explicitly ("Who's coming to the party?") or implicitly (when an assertion raises a follow-up question). Sub-questions can be pushed onto the stack to address a larger question incrementally.

### 5.2 Focus and QUD Congruence

The key link between QUD and information structure is **focus**: the focus of an assertion indicates which QUD it addresses.

> QUD: "Who called?"
> Answer: "JOHN called." (Focus on John — congruent with the QUD)

An answer is **congruent** with a QUD if the focus of the answer corresponds to the wh-element of the question, and the background of the answer matches the presupposition of the question.

Incongruent answers — where the focus doesn't match the QUD — cause pragmatic oddness:

> QUD: "Who called?"
> Answer: #"John CALLED." (Focus on "called" — incongruent; the question is about who, not what John did)

This incongruence doesn't make the answer *false* — it makes it *pragmatically infelicitous*. The information is correct, but the packaging doesn't fit the discourse context.

### 5.3 Implicit QUDs

Crucially, QUDs need not be explicitly asked. Much of discourse is organized around **implicit** QUDs that participants infer from context:

> [Context: You walk in late to a meeting]
> Colleague: "JOHN is presenting." (Implicit QUD: "What's happening?" or "Who's presenting?")

> [Context: Discussion about vacation plans]
> "We're going to SPAIN." (Implicit QUD: "Where are you going?")

Speakers constantly assess what implicit question is "on the table" and package their utterances to answer it. Listeners constantly try to infer what implicit question a speaker's utterance is addressing.

### 5.4 QUD and Discourse Coherence

The QUD framework provides a formal account of **discourse coherence**: a discourse is coherent to the extent that each utterance can be understood as relevant to some QUD (either the current one or a newly introduced sub-question). Utterances that don't seem to address any identifiable QUD feel **incoherent** or **off-topic** — the listener can't figure out what question the speaker is trying to answer.

This has direct implications for autistic communication: when neurotypical listeners perceive autistic speech as "tangential" or "off-topic," it may sometimes be because the speaker and listener are tracking different QUDs. The speaker may be addressing a QUD that is clear in their own reasoning but that the listener has not inferred. Making one's QUD explicit ("The thing I'm wondering about is...") can bridge this gap.

### 5.5 Büring's Contrastive Topic

Daniel Büring (2003, 2014) extended the QUD framework to account for **contrastive topics** — elements that are topical (already established) but also carry contrastive accent, signaling a partial answer to a larger question.

> Q: "Who ate what?"
> A: "JOHN ate the PASTA, and MARY ate the SALAD."

Here, "John" and "Mary" are contrastive topics — they are established entities being contrasted with each other, and each sub-answer addresses a sub-question of the larger question. Contrastive topics typically carry a **rise-fall** accent pattern (B-accent in Jackendoff's 1972 terms, L+H* in ToBI notation), distinct from the simple falling accent (A-accent, H*) of regular focus.

Büring formalized this by positing that contrastive topics indicate a **strategy of answering** — the speaker is addressing the larger question ("Who ate what?") by dividing it into sub-questions ("What did John eat?" "What did Mary eat?") and answering them one at a time. The contrastive topic marks which sub-question is currently being addressed.

## 6. TOPIC AND COMMENT

### 6.1 What "Topic" Means in Information Structure

The term "topic" in information structure refers to **what the sentence is about** — the entity about which the sentence provides information. This is distinct from the conversational notion of "topic" (what a stretch of discourse is about, covered in Category 13) and from the Hallidayan notion of "Theme" (what comes first in the clause, section 3).

**Topic**: The entity that the sentence is about — the entity about which new information is predicated. The topic is typically (but not always) given/accessible, definite, and in subject position.

**Comment**: What is said about the topic — the new information predicated of the topic entity. The comment typically contains the focus.

> "**John** [topic] bought a new car [comment]."
> "**My sister** [topic] is getting married in June [comment]."

### 6.2 Li and Thompson (1976): Topic-Prominent vs. Subject-Prominent Languages

Charles Li and Sandra Thompson (1976) proposed a typological distinction between languages based on whether topic or subject is the more fundamental organizing category:

**Subject-prominent languages** (e.g., English, French, German): Sentences are organized primarily around a grammatical subject that stands in a specific syntactic relationship to the verb (agreement, case marking). The subject may or may not be the topic.

**Topic-prominent languages** (e.g., Mandarin Chinese, Japanese, Korean): Sentences are organized primarily around a topic — an entity established in the discourse about which the sentence provides information. The topic is marked by a specific morphological marker and does not need to be the grammatical subject.

> Mandarin: "**Nèi-chang huǒ** [topic marker] xìngkuī xiāofáng-duì lái-de kuài."
> "That fire [TOPIC], fortunately the fire brigade came quickly."
> (The topic "that fire" has no grammatical role in the clause — it's not the subject, object, or anything else. It's simply what the sentence is about.)

**English as a compromise**: English is fundamentally subject-prominent, but it has **topic-marking constructions** that allow speakers to foreground a topic when needed:

- **Topicalization**: "**That book**, I really enjoyed." (fronting the object as topic)
- **Left-dislocation**: "**My brother**, he just got a new job." (fronting an entity and resuming it with a pronoun)
- **As for/As to constructions**: "**As for the budget**, we'll discuss it next week." (explicit topic-marking)
- **Existential there-constructions**: "**There**'s a problem with the report." (introducing a new entity into the discourse)

These constructions allow English speakers to separate topic from subject when needed — to talk about something that isn't the grammatical subject of the clause.

### 6.3 Lambrecht's Information Structure Framework

Knud Lambrecht (1994), in his comprehensive monograph *Information Structure and Sentence Form*, provided one of the most influential frameworks integrating topic, focus, and information structure. His key contributions:

**Three focus structure types**:

1. **Predicate focus** (topic-comment): The topic is given, and the predicate (what's said about the topic) is the focus. This is the most common, unmarked focus structure.
   > Q: "What did John do?" A: "John [topic] **bought a car** [focus]."

2. **Argument focus** (identificational): The predicate is given (part of the background), and an argument is the focus — identified or specified. This occurs when the listener knows something happened and the speaker identifies a participant.
   > Q: "Who bought a car?" A: "**JOHN** [focus] bought a car [background]."

3. **Sentence focus** (presentational): The entire sentence is the focus — there is no topic. This occurs when an entire event is presented as new.
   > Q: "What happened?" A: "**[John bought a car]** [all-focus]."

**Activation states**: Lambrecht adopted a version of Chafe's activation model:
- **Active**: Currently in the interlocutors' shared focus of attention. Active referents can be topics.
- **Accessible**: Not currently active but recoverable — "semi-active." Accessible referents may be activated as topics with some signaling.
- **Inactive**: Not in consciousness at all. Inactive referents **cannot** serve as topics — they must first be introduced (made active or accessible) before they can be topicalized.

This explains a fundamental constraint: **new referents cannot normally be topics**. You can't say "A man called" if you mean "I'm going to tell you about a man, and what I want to say about him is that he called." You'd need to first introduce the man ("There's this man I know...") and then make him the topic of subsequent predication. This constraint — **the topic accessibility constraint** — is one of the most important information-structural rules governing English sentence construction.

### 6.4 Aboutness Topic Tests

How do you determine what the topic of a sentence is? Several diagnostic tests have been proposed:

**The "as for" test (Gundel 1988)**: If you can paraphrase a sentence with "As for X, ..." where X is the topic, then X is functioning as the topic.
- "John bought a car" → "As for John, he bought a car." (John is the topic)
- "A car was bought by John." → "As for the car, it was bought by John." (The car is the topic — shifted via passive)

**The "What about X?" test (Reinhart 1981)**: The topic of a sentence is what could have prompted the sentence as an answer to "What about X?"
- Q: "What about John?" A: "John bought a car." → John is the topic.
- Q: "What about the car?" A: "The car was bought by John." → The car is the topic.

**The "tell me about X" test**: Similar to "what about" — the topic is what you'd be "telling about."

These tests confirm that topic is a **pragmatic** category, not a purely syntactic one. The same sentence can have different topics depending on discourse context.

## 7. SYNTACTIC CONSTRUCTIONS AS INFORMATION STRUCTURE TOOLS

English, as a relatively fixed word order language (SVO), has limited ability to rearrange constituents purely for information-structural purposes compared to free word order languages. Instead, English uses a repertoire of **syntactic constructions** that each serve specific information-structural functions. These constructions are not random stylistic variants — each has a specific discourse function.

### 7.1 It-Clefts

**Form**: "It + be + focused element + relative clause"
> "It was **John** who called."
> "It was **yesterday** that she arrived."
> "It was **a red dress** that she was wearing."

**Information-structural function**: It-clefts mark their focused element (the post-copular constituent) as **exhaustive focus** — it's John and no one else, yesterday and no other day. The relative clause contains the **presupposition** — information taken for granted.

- Focus: The post-copular element ("John")
- Presupposition: Someone called.
- Assertion: That someone is John (and only John).

**Discourse function**: Clefts are used when the speaker wants to:
1. Answer a question about identity — "Who called?" → "It was John who called."
2. Correct a mistaken assumption — "Bill called." → "No, it was JOHN who called."
3. Specify one element in a known frame — the listener knows someone called; the cleft specifies who.

**The exhaustivity debate**: Whether clefts semantically entail exhaustivity or merely pragmatically implicate it has been debated extensively. Horn (1981) argued for semantic exhaustivity; others (Percus 1997, Velleman et al. 2012) have argued for weaker exhaustivity. Experimental evidence suggests cleft exhaustivity is harder to cancel than focus exhaustivity (the exhaustivity associated with mere prosodic prominence), which may indicate that clefts encode exhaustivity more deeply than simple focus.

### 7.2 Wh-Clefts (Pseudo-Clefts)

**Form**: "What + clause + be + focused element"
> "What I need is **a vacation**."
> "What she did was **open the window**."
> "What happened was **the pipe burst**."

**Information-structural function**: The wh-clause establishes the presupposition (an open proposition — "I need something," "she did something"), and the post-copular element provides the focus — the specification that fills the variable.

**Discourse function**: Pseudo-clefts are used to:
1. Clarify or specify what fills a known slot — "I need something, and what I need is X"
2. Summarize and specify — "something happened, and what happened was X"
3. Create a framework and then fill it

**Reverse pseudo-clefts** place the focus first: "**A vacation** is what I need." This is less common and puts greater emphasis on the focused element by fronting it.

### 7.3 Topicalization

**Form**: Moving a non-subject constituent to clause-initial position without a resumptive pronoun.
> "**That book** I really enjoyed." (compare: "I really enjoyed that book.")
> "**Beans** I don't like." (compare: "I don't like beans.")
> "**To Paris** we drove." (compare: "We drove to Paris.")

**Information-structural function**: The fronted element becomes the **topic** — the element the sentence is about. It must be **discourse-old or accessible** (you can't topicalize a brand-new entity: #"A book I just found I really enjoyed" is odd as a discourse opener).

**Discourse function**: Topicalization is used to:
1. Shift the topic — "I liked the movie. **The book**, I wasn't so sure about."
2. Contrast with a previous topic — "I love coffee. **Tea**, on the other hand, I can't stand."
3. Resume a previously mentioned entity as the current topic

**Constraints**: Topicalization in English requires the fronted element to be given/accessible. It also typically requires a specific intonation contour — a rise on the topicalized element followed by a pause (the "comma intonation").

### 7.4 Left-Dislocation

**Form**: A constituent is placed at the left edge of the sentence and then **resumed with a pronoun** in the clause.
> "**My brother**, he just got a promotion."
> "**That restaurant on 5th street**, I've never been there."
> "**The thing about John**, he never listens."

**Information-structural function**: Left-dislocation establishes or re-establishes a topic. The dislocated element is the topic; the pronoun in the clause is an anaphoric link. Unlike topicalization, left-dislocation can introduce **new topics** (including hearer-new entities) because the resumptive pronoun provides a syntactic "place-holder" that integrates the referent into the clause.

**Discourse function**: Left-dislocation is used to:
1. Introduce a new topic — "You know, **this guy I met at the conference**, he had the most interesting theory."
2. Re-activate an inactive topic — (after a long digression) "**Our original plan**, it was actually pretty good."
3. Signal a topic shift — moving from one entity to another as the "aboutness" of the discourse

**Distinction from topicalization**: The resumptive pronoun is the key difference. Topicalization ("That book I really enjoyed") has no pronoun; left-dislocation ("That book, I really enjoyed **it**") does. They serve different functions: topicalization foregrounds a given entity; left-dislocation (re-)establishes an entity as a topic, especially when the entity is not fully active.

### 7.5 Right-Dislocation

**Form**: A referential expression is placed after the clause, with a pronoun anticipating it within the clause.
> "**He**'s always late, **John**."
> "I can't stand **it**, **the noise**."
> "**She**'s brilliant, **my daughter**."

**Information-structural function**: The pronoun in the clause is the topic; the right-dislocated noun phrase is an **afterthought** or **clarification** — specifying who or what the pronoun refers to.

**Discourse function**: Right-dislocation is used to:
1. Clarify a pronoun reference when the speaker realizes it might be ambiguous
2. Re-activate or specify a referent as an afterthought
3. Add an appositive identification

Right-dislocation signals that the referent is already **active** in the discourse (the speaker used a pronoun for it) but might need explicit identification for the listener. It often occurs in informal, spoken register.

### 7.6 Existential There-Constructions

**Form**: "There + be + indefinite NP (+ optional location/circumstance)"
> "**There**'s a cat on the roof."
> "**There** was a problem with the report."
> "Once upon a time, **there** was a beautiful princess."

**Information-structural function**: Existential constructions introduce **new entities** into the discourse. The post-verbal NP is almost always indefinite (new) and serves as **presentational focus** — the entire point of the sentence is to assert the existence of this entity and make it available for subsequent reference.

**The Definiteness Restriction**: Existential constructions in English strongly resist definite NPs:
- "There's a/some problem." (natural)
- #"There's the problem." (odd as an existential — though possible as a "discovery" statement, "Ah, there's the problem!")

This restriction follows from information structure: "the" signals givenness, but the whole point of an existential construction is to introduce something new.

**Discourse function**: Existential constructions are used to:
1. Introduce new referents into the discourse — "There was a man who lived alone in the mountains."
2. Present new information as the central point — "There's been an accident."
3. Open narratives — "There once was a king who had three daughters."

### 7.7 Passive Voice as Information Structure Tool

**Form**: Patient/theme promoted to subject; agent demoted to oblique (by-phrase) or omitted.
> Active: "John broke the window." → Passive: "The window was broken (by John)."

**Information-structural function**: Passivization moves the patient/theme to **subject position** (clause-initial, topic position) and can move the agent to **end position** (focus position) or remove it entirely. This allows information-structural rearrangement without changing the propositional content.

**When passive serves information structure**:

1. **When the patient is the topic** (given, accessible) and the agent is the focus (new):
   > "The Mona Lisa is one of the world's most famous paintings. **It** was painted by **Leonardo da Vinci** in the early 16th century."
   > (The Mona Lisa = topic/given → subject position. Leonardo da Vinci = focus/new → end position.)

2. **When the agent is unknown, irrelevant, or deliberately concealed**:
   > "The window was broken." (agent omitted — focus is on the event, not the agent)
   > "Mistakes were made." (deliberately agentless)

3. **When maintaining thematic continuity**:
   > "The bill was introduced in January. **It** was debated for three months. **It** was finally passed in April."
   > (Constant theme pattern — "it" (the bill) remains the topic throughout, which would be disrupted by active voice: "Congress debated it for three months. Congress passed it in April" — now Congress competes with the bill for topic status.)

### 7.8 Inversion Constructions

English has several inversion patterns that serve information-structural functions:

**Locative inversion**: "Into the room walked **a tall stranger**."
- The location is given/scene-setting (theme); the entity is new (focus). Inversion places the new entity in end-focus position.

**Quotative inversion**: "'I refuse,' said **the president**."
- The quote is the focus content; inversion allows the source attribution to be placed after the quote, maintaining the narrative flow.

**Negative inversion**: "Never had I seen **such a mess**."
- Fronting the negative adverb creates emphasis and triggers subject-auxiliary inversion. The fronted negative is a marked theme creating dramatic effect.

## 8. THE INTERACTION WITH PROSODY

### 8.1 The Division of Labor

English uses a **dual system** for marking information structure:
1. **Syntax**: Word order, clefts, passives, topicalization, etc. (the constructions in section 7)
2. **Prosody**: Pitch accent placement, deaccenting, phrasing. (treated in depth in Category 15)

These two systems complement each other. Syntax provides the structural framework; prosody provides the fine-grained marking of focus and givenness within that framework. The key prosodic mechanisms:

**Pitch accent** (nuclear accent): The main prosodic prominence in an intonation phrase. The word carrying the nuclear accent is interpreted as the focus (or as containing the focus). Accent placement directly maps to focus structure.

**Deaccenting**: Removing expected accent from a word signals that it is **given** — already established in the discourse. Deaccented words are produced with reduced pitch range, shorter duration, and reduced vowel quality.

**Accent type**: Different accent types (in the ToBI framework) correlate with different information-structural functions:
- **H*** (high target): Unmarked accent — compatible with new information focus
- **L+H*** (rise to high): Often associated with contrastive focus or contrastive topic
- **L*+H** (low with late rise): Associated with uncertainty, incredulity
- **H+!H*** (step-down): Mid-range accent

### 8.2 The Prosody-Syntax Interface

When syntax and prosody provide redundant information-structural marking, the result is strong, clear signaling:
> "It was JOHN who called." (Cleft syntax marks John as focus; accent on John confirms it.)

When they provide complementary marking:
> "JOHN bought the car." (Syntax leaves John in unmarked subject position; prosody does all the focus-marking work.)

When they conflict (rare, but possible), the result is processing difficulty or pragmatic oddness — the listener receives contradictory signals about what is given and what is new.

### 8.3 English vs. Free Word Order Languages

The relative weight of syntax vs. prosody for marking information structure varies across languages:

**English**: Relatively fixed word order (SVO), so prosody bears a heavy load for focus marking. Syntactic constructions (clefts, passives, etc.) provide additional tools but are more "marked" (require special motivation).

**German**: Somewhat freer word order than English, with the ability to move constituents to the prefield (Vorfeld) for topic/focus purposes. German uses a combination of word order and prosody.

**Czech, Russian, Turkish, Finnish**: Relatively free word order, allowing direct rearrangement of constituents for information-structural purposes. New/focused elements tend to appear clause-finally; given/topical elements tend to appear clause-initially. Word order does much of the work that prosody does in English.

**Japanese**: Uses morphological topic and focus markers (wa for topic, ga for focus/new subjects), providing an explicit, grammaticalized system. Word order is relatively free and also contributes to information structure.

**Hungarian**: Has a dedicated syntactic **focus position** — immediately preverbal. The focused element must move to this position, and occupying it signals exhaustive identification. This is one of the clearest cases of a grammaticalized focus position.

This cross-linguistic variation means that the specific information-structural strategies English speakers must learn are **language-specific**, not universal. The underlying dimensions (topic/focus, given/new) are universal, but how they are expressed varies dramatically.

## 9. INFORMATION STRUCTURE AND THE ARTICLE SYSTEM

### 9.1 Articles as Information-Status Markers

English articles provide lexicalized information-status marking:

**Definite "the"**: Signals that the speaker treats the referent as **uniquely identifiable** by the listener. This typically means the referent is given, accessible, or part of shared knowledge.

**Indefinite "a/an"**: Signals that the speaker does not expect the listener to uniquely identify the referent. This typically means the referent is new to the discourse.

**Zero article (bare plurals and mass nouns)**: Signals generic reference ("Dogs are loyal") or non-specific reference ("I need water").

**Demonstratives "this/that"**: Signal different degrees of activation and speaker involvement:
- "This" signals higher activation, greater speaker involvement, or referent-tracking ("There's this guy who...") — common in narrative introductions
- "That" signals emotional distance, previous mention, or shared knowledge ("Remember that restaurant we went to?")

### 9.2 The Definiteness-Givenness Connection

While the mapping between definiteness and givenness is imperfect (section 2.6), it is strong enough to create systematic expectations:

**Expected combinations**:
- Definite + discourse-old: "John walked in. **The man** sat down." (natural)
- Indefinite + discourse-new: "**A man** walked in." (natural)
- Definite + inferrable: "I went to a restaurant. **The waiter** was rude." (natural — bridging)

**Unexpected but possible combinations**:
- Indefinite + hearer-old: "**A friend of yours** called." (the listener knows who, but the identity is being presented as information)
- Definite + hearer-new: "Did you hear about **the earthquake in Chile**?" (the speaker assumes the listener can access this via the uniqueness of "earthquake in Chile")

**Genuinely anomalous combinations**:
- Definite + entirely new, uninferable: "I was walking along. **The spaceship** hovered overhead." (jarring — the listener has no basis for "the." Acceptable only if the speaker deliberately creates a vivid, in-medias-res effect.)

### 9.3 Prince's (1992) Taxonomy of Definites

Prince (1992), building on her earlier (1981) work, classified NP types by their discourse function:

- **Evoked**: Previously mentioned (discourse-old). "I saw a cat. **The cat/It** was orange."
- **Brand-new anchored**: Entirely new but anchored to something known. "**A friend of my sister's** called."
- **Brand-new unanchored**: Entirely new with no anchor. "**A cat** walked into the room."
- **Unused**: Hearer-old but not yet mentioned. "**The sun** was setting." (everyone knows about the sun)
- **Inferrable**: Not mentioned but deducible from context. "I walked into the classroom. **The teacher** was writing on **the board**."

This taxonomy is particularly useful for understanding why some sentences feel smooth and others feel bumpy — the smoothness depends on whether each NP's information status matches its grammatical encoding (article choice, position, accent).

## 10. PRAGMATIC CONSEQUENCES OF INFORMATION STRUCTURE

### 10.1 Processing Effects

Experimental research consistently shows that information structure affects **processing speed and accuracy**:

**Accent-givenness alignment**: Listeners process sentences faster when given information is deaccented and new information is accented. Mismatches — accenting given information or deaccenting new information — cause measurable processing delays (Terken & Nooteboom, 1987; Birch & Clifton, 1995; Delin & Oberlander, 1995).

**Given-before-new ordering**: English listeners prefer and process more easily sentences where given information precedes new information (Gernsbacher & Hargreaves, 1992; Arnold et al., 2000).

**Topic continuity**: Sentences that maintain the topic from the previous sentence are processed faster than those that shift to a new topic (Almor, 1999; Gordon, Grosz & Gilliom, 1993). This is why constant-theme progression (section 3.6) is the most "natural" feeling progression pattern.

**Cleft facilitation**: Cleft constructions facilitate the processing of focused information — the cleft structure sets up an expectation for the specific piece of information that will complete the presupposition, and when that information arrives, it is integrated more efficiently (Cowles et al., 2007).

### 10.2 Garden-Path Effects from Information Structure

Garden-path effects — where the listener initially parses a sentence one way and then has to reparse — can be triggered by information-structural mismatches:

> "The horse raced past the barn fell."

This is the classic garden-path sentence. Readers initially parse "raced" as the main verb (the horse raced past the barn), then encounter "fell" and must reparse "raced past the barn" as a reduced relative clause (the horse [that was] raced past the barn fell). The information-structural contribution: readers expect "the horse" (definite, subject-initial) to be the topic of a simple predication, and "raced" to be the main verb (the rheme). The reduced relative clause violates this expectation.

### 10.3 Mispackaging as a Communication Problem

When speakers mispackage information — when the focus, topic, or given-new mapping doesn't match the discourse context — the result is **communicative friction** that may not be attributable to any specific grammatical error:

**Wrong focus**:
> A: "Who called?" B: "John CALLED." (focus on "called" instead of "John")
> This is grammatically correct and propositionally responsive, but informationally mispackaged. The listener has to extra work to extract the relevant information.

**Absent anchor**:
> "So the meeting went well. The Blackburn proposal is going to be revised." (if "the Blackburn proposal" has no prior mention and is not inferrable, the listener is lost — where did this come from?)

**Premature topic**:
> [Beginning of conversation] "He's doing really well." (pronoun with no antecedent — who is "he"?)

These mispackaging errors do not violate grammar. They violate **information-structural expectations**, and they are one of the most common sources of feedback like "I don't follow you," "Wait, what are you talking about?", "That's a weird way to put it," or "You didn't answer my question."

### 10.4 The Manner Maxim Connection

Grice's Maxim of Manner — "Be perspicuous" — is essentially an instruction to package information well. Grice's sub-maxims under Manner include:

- "Avoid obscurity of expression" → Package information so the listener can process it
- "Avoid ambiguity" → Make focus and topic clear
- "Be brief" → Don't overmark given information
- "Be orderly" → Present information in a processable order (given before new)

Information structure is the primary mechanism through which speakers comply with the Manner maxim. A speaker who violates the Manner maxim is often a speaker who mispackages information — puts focus on the wrong element, fails to establish a topic before commenting on it, or presents information in an order that doesn't match the listener's expectations.

## 11. INFORMATION STRUCTURE AND AUTISM

### 11.1 Why Information Structure Matters for Autistic Communication

Information structure is a particularly important area for autistic individuals because:

1. **It operates through implicit expectations**: Neurotypical speakers unconsciously package information based on rapid assessment of what the listener knows, what question is "on the table," and what the discourse context demands. These assessments rely on moment-to-moment theory-of-mind computations that may not be automatic for autistic individuals.

2. **Violations create interpersonal friction without clear grammatical errors**: A sentence can be grammatically perfect, factually correct, and still feel "wrong" to the listener because of information-structural mispackaging. This kind of error is particularly frustrating because the speaker may not understand what they did wrong — "I said the right thing! Why are you confused?"

3. **It connects to prosody, which is independently challenging**: Prosodic focus marking — stressing the right word — is one of the key ways English marks focus. Prosodic differences in autism (sometimes described as "flat" or "unusual" prosody, but more accurately as different prosodic patterns) can affect the signaling of information structure even when the speaker has the right information-structural intent.

4. **It affects narrative and extended discourse**: Many of the communication difficulties that autistic individuals experience in extended speech — narratives that are hard to follow, explanations that seem to start in the middle, topic shifts that feel abrupt — may involve information-structural mispackaging rather than (or in addition to) other pragmatic issues.

### 11.2 Research on Information Structure in Autism

Research on information structure specifically in autism is relatively limited compared to research on other pragmatic areas (e.g., figurative language, speech acts), but what exists is illuminating:

**Prosodic focus marking**: Several studies have examined whether autistic individuals use and interpret prosodic focus marking differently. Peppé et al. (2007) found that children with ASD had difficulty both producing and comprehending focus accent — specifically, difficulty using stress to mark the contrastive element in a correction context. Subsequent studies have found that some autistic adults do use prosodic focus marking, but may use it less consistently or in atypical patterns (Diehl et al., 2009; McCann et al., 2007).

**Given-new reference**: Autistic individuals may have difficulty tracking what information is shared (given) vs. new from the listener's perspective. This connects to the broader finding that perspective-taking — modeling what the listener knows — is a challenge area. de Marchena & Eigsti (2010) found that autistic adolescents used fewer referential forms appropriate to given vs. new status (e.g., using full names when a pronoun would suffice for given entities, or using pronouns when the referent was not yet established as given).

**Narrative coherence**: Studies of narrative production in autism have found differences in information flow — specifically, less clear establishment of topics before commenting on them, less consistent given-before-new ordering, and more frequent use of full NPs where pronouns would be expected for given entities (Colle et al., 2008; Losh & Capps, 2003; Norbury & Bishop, 2003). These patterns are consistent with information-structural mispackaging rather than grammatical difficulty.

### 11.3 Information Structure as a Learnable System

The key insight for autistic individuals is that information structure is not "intuition" or "social instinct" — it is a **system with identifiable rules**:

1. **Given before new** (in English): Put what the listener already knows first; put the new information at the end.
2. **Establish before referring**: Don't use pronouns or definite descriptions for entities that haven't been established in the discourse.
3. **Match the question**: Focus (accent) should fall on the element that answers the question being addressed.
4. **One new thing at a time**: Don't try to introduce multiple new entities in a single sentence without anchoring each to something established.
5. **Signal topic shifts**: When changing what you're talking about, mark the shift explicitly ("Speaking of X," "On a different note," "Actually, about Y").

These rules can be made **explicit and systematic**, which suits pattern-based learning. The challenge is that neurotypical speakers apply them unconsciously and may not be able to articulate what went wrong when they're violated — they just know something feels "off." Making these rules visible converts an implicit, intuition-based system into an explicit, analyzable one.

### 11.4 The Double Empathy Dimension

Information-structural expectations are bidirectional. When a neurotypical listener finds an autistic speaker's information packaging "confusing" or "off," the difficulty may be mutual — the autistic speaker may find the neurotypical listener's expectations unclear or arbitrary. The double empathy problem (Milton, 2012) applies here: communication breakdowns due to information structure mismatches are jointly produced, not solely the autistic person's "deficit."

Furthermore, information-structural norms vary across discourse communities. Academic writing has different information-structural expectations than casual conversation. Online communication has different norms than face-to-face. Formal presentations have different requirements than informal storytelling. The challenge is not "learning the one right way to package information" but "learning which information-structural expectations apply in which context" — a metacognitive skill that benefits everyone.

## 12. THEORETICAL SYNTHESIS: HOW THE DIMENSIONS INTERACT

### 12.1 The Three Dimensions

Krifka (2008) provides a useful synthesis of the main dimensions of information structure, distinguishing them clearly while showing how they interact:

**Dimension 1: Focus-Background (Alternatives)**
- What part of the sentence introduces alternatives? (Focus)
- What part is presupposed/given? (Background)
- Marked by: prosodic prominence, syntactic focus constructions (clefts)

**Dimension 2: Topic-Comment (Aboutness)**
- What entity is the sentence about? (Topic)
- What is said about it? (Comment)
- Marked by: sentence position (initial), topicalization, left-dislocation, "as for" constructions

**Dimension 3: Given-New (Activation/Recoverability)**
- What information is already accessible to the listener? (Given)
- What information is being newly introduced? (New)
- Marked by: deaccenting (given), accent (new), articles (definite = given, indefinite = new), pronouns (given) vs. full NPs (new)

These three dimensions are **independent but correlated**:
- Topics tend to be given (you talk about what's already established), but they can be new (presentational constructions introduce new topics).
- Focus tends to be new (the informational point is usually new), but it can be given (contrastive focus on a given entity: "I said I wanted COFFEE, not tea").
- The comment tends to contain the focus, but not always (the focus can be the topic in identificational sentences).

### 12.2 A Working Model for Learners

For practical purposes, the information-structural system can be understood through a small set of questions that speakers (unconsciously) ask before producing each sentence:

1. **What does my listener already know?** (Given-New assessment)
   → This determines what I can treat as background and what I need to present as new.

2. **What am I talking about?** (Topic selection)
   → This determines what goes in the subject/initial position.

3. **What is my point?** (Focus determination)
   → This determines what receives prosodic prominence and may motivate special constructions (clefts, etc.).

4. **What question am I answering?** (QUD identification)
   → This determines how to align focus with the current discourse purpose.

5. **How does this connect to what came before?** (Coherence/thematic progression)
   → This determines thematic choices and given-new ordering across sentences.

Autistic individuals who find these assessments non-automatic can develop **explicit checking strategies** — running through these questions consciously before or during speech production. Over time, with practice, some of this checking becomes more automatic, but the explicit framework remains available as a fallback.

## 14. SUMMARY: KEY CONCEPTS FOR THE LEARNING HUB

### The Five Core Ideas

1. **Information structure is packaging, not content**: The same content can be packaged in many ways. The choice of packaging is determined by the discourse context — what the listener knows, what question is being addressed, what came before.

2. **Given before new**: In English, given information tends to come first and new information last. This is the default, unmarked order. Deviations are meaningful — they signal contrast, emphasis, or special discourse functions.

3. **Focus marks the point**: The focused element is what the sentence contributes to the discourse — the answer to the current question. Focus is marked by prosodic prominence (stress) and sometimes by syntactic construction (clefts).

4. **Topic is what you're talking about**: The topic is the entity that the sentence provides information about. It should be established (accessible) before it is commented on.

5. **English has a toolkit of constructions**: Clefts, pseudo-clefts, passives, topicalization, left/right-dislocation, existential constructions, and inversion are not random stylistic choices — each serves a specific information-structural function.

### The Practical Rule Set

| Rule | What It Means | Example of Violation |
|------|---------------|---------------------|
| Given before new | Put what the listener knows first | Starting with entirely new information before establishing context |
| Establish before referring | Introduce entities before using pronouns for them | "He called me yesterday" at the start of a conversation (who is "he"?) |
| Match the QUD | Focus should answer the question being asked | Q: "Who called?" A: "John CALLED." (focus on wrong element) |
| One new thing at a time | Don't overload with new information | "A man I'd never met from a company called Nexus brought a prototype of a device called the Q7." |
| Signal topic shifts | Mark when you change what you're talking about | Abrupt switch from one topic to another without a transition |
| Use end-focus | Put the most important new information at the end | "It was really interesting, the presentation about AI" (buried focus) |

### Cross-Category Connections

| Category | Connection to Information Structure |
|----------|-------------------------------------|
| Category 12 (Narrative) | Thematic progression, given-new flow in storytelling, information structure for suspense/surprise |
| Category 13 (Topic Management) | Conversational topic is related to but distinct from sentence topic; topic shifts involve information-structural resetting |
| Category 15 (Prosodic Pragmatics) | Prosody is the primary marker of focus in English; accent placement maps to information structure |
| Category 16 (Common Ground) | Given information is information in the common ground; the Given-New Contract assumes common ground tracking |
| Category 2 (Implicature) | The Manner maxim is about information packaging; focus triggers scalar implicatures ("only"-type readings) |
| Category 9 (Presupposition) | Background/presupposition in focus structure connects to presupposition in general; clefts carry presuppositions |
| Category 5 (Discourse Markers) | Many discourse markers function as textual themes (Halliday) or signal topic shifts/thematic connections |
| Category 7 (Register) | Information-structural norms vary by register (academic writing has strict given-new; casual speech is more flexible) |
