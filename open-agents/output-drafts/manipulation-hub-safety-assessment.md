# Safety Assessment: The First Page to Read

## Pragmatic Manipulation and Self-Protection Hub — Foundation Page

---

## Why This Page Exists Before Any Other

Every other page in this hub teaches you how to recognize a specific pattern of manipulation, or how to apply a specific strategy in a specific context. But those pages assume you have already answered a prior question:

**How dangerous is my situation right now?**

The answer to that question determines which strategies are safe to try. A script that defuses a workplace disagreement can escalate an intimate partner to violence. Documentation that protects you in a custody dispute can get you hurt if your abuser finds it. Calmly pointing out a gaslighting pattern to a therapist is different from pointing it out to someone with weapon access.

This is not a checklist you complete once. It is a lens you use every time you think about taking action. The core question is always: **what am I actually dealing with, and what is the realistic floor on how bad this could get?**

This page draws directly from validated safety assessment research (Campbell's Danger Assessment, Stark's coercive control framework, Herman's staged recovery model) and adapts it for autistic readers, with explicit notes about where the research does and does not apply.

## 1. The First Question: Fight vs. Control

Before anything else, you need to determine what kind of situation you are in. This is the single most useful concept from the manipulation literature, and it comes directly from Evan Stark's *Coercive Control* (2007) and Michael Johnson's typology of domestic violence (*A Typology of Domestic Violence*, 2008).

There are two fundamentally different patterns, and they require different responses:

| Pattern | What It Looks Like | What It Responds To |
|---------|-------------------|---------------------|
| **Fight** (Situational conflict) | Specific disagreements over specific things. Both people have voice. The pattern does not consistently advantage one person. The relationship is repairable through improved communication. Apologies are mutual. | Communication strategies, repair, negotiation, sometimes couples work with careful screening |
| **Control** (Coercive control) | A pattern — not a list of incidents — in which one person systematically restricts what the other can say, do, think, feel, or remember. The other person's voice is erased. The pattern consistently advantages the controller. | Safety assessment, advocacy, professional help, often some form of exit |

The distinction matters because **couples therapy is empirically contraindicated when coercive control is present**. This is a strong research consensus, not an opinion (Bograd & Mederos, 1999, *Journal of Marital and Family Therapy* 25(3); Stith et al., 2012, *Journal of Marital and Family Therapy* 38(1); Karakurt et al., 2016, meta-analysis, PMC5050084). Traditional couples therapy assumes approximately equal power and safety to speak honestly — assumptions that are violated by coercive control. In those situations, couples therapy can provide the abuser with new information about what the victim has shared and can collude with the minimization framing at the heart of the abuse.

**Why this matters for autistic readers specifically:** Autistic people are often told their relationship difficulties are "mutual communication problems" or "just the double empathy problem." Sometimes they are. But sometimes the "communication problem" framing is itself part of the pattern — a way of turning deliberate control into shared responsibility. Pearson et al. (2024, *Autism in Adulthood*) documented that autistic survivors often internalize this framing ("I thought I just wasn't trying hard enough"). The fight/control distinction gives you a way to check whether the framing fits the reality.

**See also:** [Manipulation vs. Miscommunication (decision framework page)] for the longer version of this distinction applied to specific incidents.

### How to tell which pattern you're in

This is not a diagnostic instrument. It is an orientation tool. If your answers consistently fall on the right-hand side, this is likely a control pattern, not a fight pattern.

| Question | Fight pattern | Control pattern |
|----------|--------------|-----------------|
| When you disagree, can you both speak? | Yes | No — one of you dominates, the other self-censors |
| When apologies happen, are they mutual? | Usually | No — one person never apologizes, or their apologies come with conditions |
| Does the pattern consistently benefit one person? | No | Yes |
| Can you name what you want without fearing the consequences? | Usually | No |
| Does raising concerns make things better or worse? | Eventually better | Consistently worse |
| Does the other person adjust when you explain the impact of something? | Yes, over time | No, or they say they will and don't |
| Are there topics that are not "allowed"? | Usually not | Yes — and the list grows |
| Are there people you are not "allowed" to see? | No | Yes |
| Has the other person told you that your perception of reality is wrong, repeatedly? | Rarely | Yes |
| Do you find yourself changing how you talk to avoid triggering something? | Sometimes | Constantly |

These questions are adapted from Stark's four elements of coercive control (violence, intimidation, isolation, control) and the Power and Control Wheel (Pence & Paymar, 1993; Domestic Abuse Intervention Programs). They are not a score — they are a pattern test.

### A critical note on autistic experience

If your answer to "Does raising concerns make things better or worse?" is "it makes things worse because the other person says I'm too literal / too sensitive / misunderstanding" — that *can* be genuine double empathy mismatch (Milton, 2012), and it *can* be coercive control using autism-specific language to deflect accountability. The difference is in the pattern over time. Genuine double empathy misunderstandings tend to resolve with explanation and adjustment. Control patterns tend to get worse the more you explain, because explanation is what's being controlled.

This is the "weaponized features" finding from Douglas & Sedgewick (2024, *Autism* 28(7)) — abusers specifically use autism as the reason to dismiss the autistic person's perceptions. *Evidence quality: preliminary (small qualitative sample), but converges with other qualitative work.*

## 2. Lethality Indicators: The Things That Change Everything

Most situations that involve pragmatic manipulation do not involve the risk of physical harm. But some do, and the research base here is unambiguous enough that we need a dedicated section. If any of the indicators below are present, **the strategies on the rest of the hub may not apply in the way they are presented, and professional advocacy becomes essential rather than optional.**

Everything in this section comes from the Campbell Danger Assessment (DA), which is the gold-standard, peer-reviewed, empirically validated risk assessment instrument in the intimate partner violence field (Campbell et al., 2003, *American Journal of Public Health* 93(7); Campbell, Webster & Glass, 2009, *Journal of Interpersonal Violence* 24(4)).

### The strongest individual predictor

**Prior non-fatal strangulation is associated with a >7x increase in odds of completed intimate partner femicide.** (Glass et al., 2008, *Journal of Emergency Medicine* 35(3); OR 7.48, 95% CI 4.53–12.35.)

If anyone in your current or past relationships has ever choked you, grabbed you by the throat, or put pressure on your neck — even once, even "playfully," even in a way they said was an accident — this is the single most predictive signal in the entire research literature. As of 2023, all 50 US states classify non-fatal strangulation as a felony largely because of this finding. Strangulation can also cause delayed medical harm (carotid artery damage, delayed stroke risk) that requires medical evaluation regardless of whether you are still in the relationship.

**If this applies to you, the most important thing on this page is this: call the National Domestic Violence Hotline (1-800-799-7233 in the US; see section 7 for other regions) and ask to be connected with a trained advocate who can perform a full Danger Assessment. This is not something to do alone.**

### Other validated high-risk indicators

The following factors appear on Campbell's Danger Assessment as independent risk factors for intimate partner femicide (Campbell et al., 2003):

- Access to firearms (single strongest gun-specific predictor)
- Prior threats with a weapon
- Prior threats to kill
- Escalating frequency or severity of physical violence over the past year
- Violent and constant jealousy
- Forced sex
- Abuser suicide threats or attempts
- Beating while pregnant
- Threats to kill the children
- Abuser belief that they could kill the victim
- Stalking: following, spying, leaving threatening messages
- Recent separation or attempted separation
- Controlling most of the victim's daily activities
- A child in the home who is not biologically the abuser's

**This is not a DIY tool.** The Danger Assessment's original case-control development (Campbell et al., 2003, *American Journal of Public Health* 93(7):1089-1097) used 220 attempted/completed femicide cases and 343 abused-but-not-killed controls across 11 cities. The subsequent Campbell, Webster & Glass (2009) *Journal of Interpersonal Violence* DA validation paper refined the instrument using a further case-control sample. Administering the instrument yourself does not give you the calibrated risk score. The value of knowing about it is that (a) if multiple items are present, you are in the population the instrument was designed for, and (b) advocates can administer it properly if you reach out.

### The separation paradox

This is the most counterintuitive finding in the entire safety literature, and it must be stated clearly:

**Leaving is the highest-risk period. 55.2% of women murdered by an intimate partner had separated or were in the process of leaving.** (Campbell et al., 2003; replicated in Wilson & Daly, 1993, *Violence and Victims* 8(1).)

This does not mean you should not leave. It means that **"just leave" is empirically unsafe as standalone advice.** Leaving without a safety plan, without advocacy, and without attention to the specific lethality indicators above, is when most intimate partner homicides happen. The strongest research consensus in the field is that leaving needs to be safety-planned, ideally with a trained advocate, ideally informed by a risk assessment.

If you are reading this and thinking about leaving an intimate partner: please call the National Domestic Violence Hotline before you do. They have advocates who are trained in this. This hub cannot replace that.

## 3. The Engage/Exit Decision Framework

Once you've oriented yourself with the fight/control distinction and scanned for lethality indicators, you can start thinking about what to actually do. The research supports a staged approach — not "always engage" or "always exit," but "engage when engagement is viable, exit when it is not."

### Is engagement viable?

Engagement strategies (boundary setting, direct communication, documentation, naming patterns) only work when certain conditions are present. From the research on what makes communication interventions succeed:

**Engagement is viable when:**
- The pattern is a fight pattern, not a control pattern (Section 1)
- There are no lethality indicators (Section 2)
- The other person has demonstrated the capacity to hear feedback and adjust, even imperfectly
- You have the energy and resources to maintain engagement over time
- The social environment supports your voice (you have witnesses, allies, professional relationships where your account will be heard)
- Disengagement is available if engagement fails

**Engagement is not viable when:**
- The pattern is a control pattern, not a fight pattern
- Any lethality indicators are present
- The other person has consistently weaponized your attempts to explain
- You are in a sustained state of depleted resources (burnout, crisis)
- The social environment discredits your voice in advance (professional settings where you're already labeled "difficult"; family systems where you're already the scapegoat)
- You cannot disengage if engagement fails (economic dependence, custody risk, housing, etc.)

### What the research tells us about choosing

From the IPV literature: "disclosure outcomes are asymmetric — negative reactions harm more than positive reactions help" (Ullman, 2010, *Talking About Sexual Assault*; systematic reviews). This means the choice of confidant, therapist, or advocate is itself a safety decision. Telling the wrong person can harm you more than not telling anyone. Telling the right person is protective.

From the workplace bullying literature: "mediation between target and bully" is not supported by the research (parallels couples therapy contraindication) because it assumes equal power. Zero-tolerance policies with enforcement work; training-only interventions and HR-only investigations without independent oversight do not (Salin, 2008; Leiter et al., 2011).

From the trauma literature: Herman (1992) argues that **safety must precede any meaningful trauma processing.** You cannot usefully "work through" an abusive situation while still in it. If the current situation is ongoing, the priority is establishing safety, not processing the trauma of the past.

### The Prochaska stages applied to leaving

Burke et al. (2004, *American Journal of Health Behavior* 28(2)) applied the transtheoretical model of behavior change to leaving abusive relationships. They found that approximately 95% of abused women in their sample were in **precontemplation or contemplation** stages at study entry — meaning they were not yet actively preparing to leave. Interventions that push someone from precontemplation directly to action tend to fail.

**The practical implication:** If you are reading this page and thinking "I'm not ready to do anything about this yet" — that is a real and valid stage, and it has a name. Precontemplation and contemplation are not failure states. They are where most people in abusive relationships are, most of the time. The right question is not "why haven't I left yet?" It is "what can I do at my current stage that moves me forward without putting me in more danger?"

*Evidence quality: moderate. The stages model has face validity and has been applied to IPV with some quantitative support, but it was designed for individual health behaviors and its fit with a socially embedded decision is debated.*

## 4. Why Your Safety Assessment Is Different

Standard safety assessment materials have been developed on populations that excluded autistic samples. The frameworks above (Stark, Campbell, Herman) transfer in principle, but several things work differently for autistic readers.

### The credibility discount is structural, not personal

Lim, Young & Brewer (2022, PMC8813809) found that autistic adults are perceived as more deceptive and less credible than neurotypical adults *when telling the truth*. Your prosody, eye contact, and body language trigger neurotypical "deception detectors" that were calibrated for neurotypical behavior. This is not a confidence problem you can solve by speaking up more forcefully. It is a structural fact about how your communication will land.

**Implications for safety assessment:**
- Your own assessment of the situation is likely more accurate than others will credit it for being. This is worth knowing.
- When you disclose to professionals, advocates, or trusted others, you may need written records to back up your account, because your verbal account may be under-weighted.
- Choose disclosure targets who have demonstrated they can hear you — not just anyone who is "supposed to" help.

### "Trust your gut" is poor advice when alexithymia is present

Approximately 50% of autistic adults also meet criteria for alexithymia — difficulty identifying and describing one's own emotional states (Hull et al. research; Kinnaird et al. 2019 meta-analysis). Standard safety advice relies heavily on "gut feelings" and "intuition." If your interoceptive signals are unreliable, this advice does not work for you.

**What to use instead: pattern recognition over time.** The research on autistic cognition suggests that detection of repeated patterns across time is a relative strength, while real-time nonverbal cue detection is a relative weakness (Williams et al., 2018, *Autism Research*; Rozenkrantz et al., 2021, *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* — the latter with the caveat that it's a review paper and the generalization from lab findings to real-world detection is not fully established). This is why every pattern page in this hub focuses on **patterns**, not on "trust your instincts." The instinct-level signal is less reliable for autistic cognition; the pattern-level signal is more reliable.

*Evidence quality: preliminary but converging. The specific claim that pattern-recognition-over-time substitutes for real-time detection is extrapolated from Baron-Cohen's hypersystemizing hypothesis and Williams et al. 2018; it has not been tested directly as a safety intervention.*

### Your memory works differently, and the research on gaslighting did not account for that

Autistic adults show relative differences in episodic memory (memory for specific events, contextualized in time and place), while semantic memory (general knowledge, facts) is relatively intact (multiple studies reviewed in the autism-specific foundation document). This matters for gaslighting, where the manipulator rewrites past events — "you never said that," "that's not what happened." If your episodic memory is less reliable *and* you are being told your memory is wrong, you have fewer internal resources to resist the rewriting.

**What to use instead: external written records.** Not because it's "good practice" but because it is a **mechanical necessity** given how your memory works under pressure. The documentation habit (covered on its own strategy page) is not optional for gaslighting contexts; it is the compensation for a specific cognitive difference.

*Evidence quality: extrapolated. The research on episodic memory differences in autism is solid; the specific application to gaslighting resistance has not been directly tested, but the mechanism is straightforward.*

### The compliance training problem

Many autistic adults grew up in environments (ABA, "social skills training," behavioral compliance programs, or just unrelenting pressure to mask) that trained them to suppress "no" responses, prioritize others' comfort, and treat natural boundaries as problems to be corrected. These patterns continue into adulthood and create specific vulnerability to coercive control. If someone has taught you for twenty years that your natural responses are wrong, you are pre-conditioned to defer to someone else's version of reality when they challenge yours.

**Implication for safety assessment:** If you find yourself thinking "maybe I'm overreacting" or "maybe I'm being too much" or "maybe I'm the problem here," **pause and notice that these thoughts are the exact shape of what you were trained to think.** They are not necessarily evidence about the current situation. They may be evidence that the compliance training is active.

*Evidence quality: The claim that compliance training creates adult vulnerability is community knowledge with strong experiential support and some clinical case literature but no rigorous empirical validation. The Kupferstein 2018 ABA-PTSD study is under Expression of Concern and cannot be cited as established. The underlying phenomenon may be real but the empirical status is preliminary.*

### The "weaponized features" framing

Rather than asking "what is wrong with me that this is happening?", Pearson et al.'s (2022, 2024) research on autistic victimization offers a better question: **"What specific features of my autism are being weaponized against me, and by whom?"** This reframe is not cosmetic. It correctly locates the problem in the perpetrator's behavior and the social environment, not in the autistic person's neurology. Douglas & Sedgewick (2024) documented this pattern in their qualitative interviews with autistic survivors: abusers learn which autistic traits can be exploited (sensory sensitivities, special interests, literal-mindedness, routine-dependence) and deploy them deliberately.

This is why this hub uses "situational vulnerability" rather than "personal vulnerability" — language that comes directly from Pearson's primary research.

## 5. Crisis Resources

If you are in immediate danger, the strategies on this hub are not the first thing you should be thinking about. The first thing is getting to safety.

### International

- **WHO directory of IPV resources**: who.int (search "intimate partner violence resources")

### For workplace harassment specifically

- **EEOC (US)**: 1-800-669-4000 — Equal Employment Opportunity Commission
- **Workplace Bullying Institute**: workplacebullying.org (not a crisis line; provides resources)
- **Your state/country's labor board or employment tribunal**

### Important notes on crisis resources

- **Call from a phone you control.** Abusers often monitor shared devices. Public library phones, trusted friends' phones, or work phones may be safer.
- **Delete your call history afterwards** if you are on a monitored device.
- **Most hotlines do not mandate reporting** except in narrow circumstances (child abuse, imminent harm). You can ask about confidentiality before sharing specifics.
- **Trained advocates vs. volunteers**: NDVH, RAINN, and the major hotlines staff trained advocates. Quality of local resources varies. If the first person you talk to is not helpful, try again. This is not unusual.
- **Reporting to police is a decision, not a default.** Advocates can walk you through the tradeoffs. In coercive control situations involving autism, police encounters carry their own risks (autistic behaviors can be misread as deception, non-compliance, or threat).

## 6. How to Use the Rest of This Hub

Once you have oriented yourself with this page, the rest of the hub is structured to let you move laterally rather than reading in sequence.

### The four parts

- **Pattern pages** — If you have a specific behavior you are trying to make sense of ("Is what just happened gaslighting? DARVO? Tone policing?"), start with the pattern pages. Each one describes what the pattern looks like, how to distinguish it from legitimate behavior, and which strategies apply.
- **Context pages** — If you have a specific setting you need strategies for (intimate relationships, workplace, medical, family, education), start with the context pages. Each one integrates the patterns and strategies that are most common in that setting.
- **Strategy pages** — If you already know what pattern you're dealing with and what setting you're in, and you want to know HOW to do something (document, set a boundary, de-escalate, leave), the strategy pages have the mechanics.
- **Cross-cutting framework pages** — This page, the manipulation-vs-miscommunication decision framework, and a few others provide the lenses you use across the whole hub.

### Cross-linking to pragmatics categories

Every page in this hub links back to the relevant pragmatics categories. When a manipulation pattern depends on understanding a pragmatic mechanism — hedging, face dynamics, presupposition, common ground, tone, register, hierarchy — you can click through to learn the mechanism, then come back. You do not have to read the entire pragmatics hub before using the manipulation hub. The pragmatics pages are a reference library, not a prerequisite course.

### What this hub cannot do

This hub is research-grounded content. It cannot:

- **Replace a trained advocate.** If lethality indicators are present, if you are considering leaving, if you are in crisis — call the hotline. The hub is a complement to advocacy, not a substitute.
- **Replace a trauma-informed therapist.** If you have symptoms of PTSD or complex PTSD (intrusive memories, hypervigilance, emotional numbing, avoidance, difficulty with relationships), please seek professional help. There are known-harmful therapies (Lilienfeld, 2007, *Perspectives on Psychological Science* 2(1)), so the choice of therapist matters, but the right therapy is substantially better than no therapy for moderate-to-severe trauma. The trauma foundation research document has detail on what's evidence-based.
- **Tell you what to do in your specific situation.** The hub provides frameworks. Your situation involves facts the hub cannot know — who else is involved, what resources you have, what the history is, what the realistic options are. You are the expert on your situation. The hub helps you ask better questions, not answers the questions for you.
- **Work for all autistic people equally.** The underlying research excludes non-speaking autistic people, autistic people with intellectual disability, Black autistic women, autistic people in the criminal justice system, and other intersectionally marginalized groups (scoping reviews; PMC9745006). Where the research base is thin, the hub should say so and you should weight the content accordingly.

## 7. Key Takeaways

1. **Fight vs. control is the first question.** Situational conflicts and coercive control are fundamentally different patterns requiring different responses. Couples therapy is contraindicated for coercive control (Bograd & Mederos, 1999).

2. **Lethality indicators change everything.** Prior strangulation is the single strongest predictor of intimate partner femicide (OR 7.48; Glass et al., 2008). If any lethality indicators are present, professional advocacy is essential, not optional.

3. **Leaving is the highest-risk period.** 55.2% of intimate partner femicide victims had separated or were in the process of leaving (Campbell et al., 2003). "Just leave" is empirically unsafe as standalone advice. Safety planning + advocacy is the evidence-based pathway.

4. **Your safety detection works differently.** Alexithymia makes "trust your gut" unreliable. Pattern recognition over time is your compensation. Written records are mechanical necessities for gaslighting resistance, not just good practice.

5. **The credibility discount is structural.** Your presentation will be read against you by people calibrated for neurotypical signals (Lim, Young & Brewer, 2022). This affects which disclosure targets are safe and how you document.

6. **The compliance training problem is a real factor.** "Maybe I'm overreacting" is the exact shape of what you were trained to think. It is not necessarily evidence about the current situation.

7. **Precontemplation is a valid stage.** If you are not ready to do anything yet, that is where 95% of people in abusive relationships are at any given moment (Burke et al., 2004). The right question is what you can do at your current stage, not why you haven't leveled up.

8. **The hub is not a substitute for advocates or therapists.** If lethality indicators are present, if you are considering leaving, if you are in crisis, call the hotline. 1-800-799-7233 in the US. Other regions listed in Section 5.
