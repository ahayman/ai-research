## Claim-by-Claim Verification

### 1. Ferguson 1959 "Diglossia" Paper — Arabic as Primary Example with Swiss German, Haitian Creole, and Greek
**Claim**: Charles Ferguson published "Diglossia" in 1959 using Arabic, Swiss German, Haitian Creole, and Greek as his four case studies.
**Rating**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: The original paper is available via Taylor & Francis (Word, 1959). Multiple scholarly sources confirm: "Ferguson picks out four speech communities (Arabic, Modern Greek, Swiss German, and Haitian Creole) and describes their features." The H/L terminology and functional distribution are confirmed exactly. The specific pairs are confirmed: Arabic (Fusha/ʿĀmmiyya), Swiss German (Schriftsprache/Schwyzerdütsch), Haitian Creole (français/Créole), Greek (Katharevousa/Dhimotiki).

### 2. MSA Is Nobody's Native Language
**Claim**: No child grows up speaking MSA at home; every Arabic speaker acquires a dialect first and learns MSA through education.
**Rating**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: This is one of Ferguson's defining features of diglossia and is universally confirmed in Arabic sociolinguistics. The Queen Rania Foundation, Wikipedia (Varieties of Arabic), and all diglossia scholarship confirm that MSA is acquired through formal education, never as a native language.

### 3. Major Dialect Groups: Egyptian (~100M), Levantine (~30-40M), Gulf (~30M), Maghrebi (~80-100M), Iraqi
**Claim**: Five major dialect groups with approximate speaker populations.
**Rating**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Wikipedia (Varieties of Arabic), Playaling, Middle East Eye, and multiple dialect guides confirm five major groups: Peninsular/Gulf, Mesopotamian/Iraqi, Levantine, Egyptian/Nile Valley, and Maghrebi. Speaker counts are approximate but within accepted ranges. Egyptian at ~100 million, Maghrebi at ~80-100 million, and the characterization of Maghrebi as "the most divergent" are all confirmed.

### 4. Egyptian Arabic: ج = /g/, ق = /ʔ/ (Glottal Stop)
**Claim**: Egyptian Arabic pronounces jīm as /g/ and qāf as a glottal stop.
**Rating**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Multiple dialect comparison sources confirm: "The Egyptian ج is pronounced like the English 'g' in game." The realization of qāf as glottal stop /ʔ/ in Cairene Egyptian is one of the most frequently cited dialect markers in Arabic sociolinguistics.

### 5. Maghrebi Arabic Is the LEAST Mutually Intelligible with Eastern Dialects
**Claim**: Maghrebi (North African) Arabic is the least mutually intelligible dialect group relative to other Arabic dialects.
**Rating**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Playaling's mutual intelligibility guide states: "Darija (Moroccan, Algerian, and Tunisian Arabic) is often considered the least mutually intelligible group of Arabic dialects." Multiple sources confirm: "Speakers of Levantine or Gulf Arabic would not understand the Maghrebi dialects, unless they have been previously exposed to them." The asymmetric intelligibility (Maghrebi speakers understand Mashriqi better than vice versa) due to media exposure is confirmed.

### 6. Egyptian Arabic's Outsized Intelligibility Due to Media Dominance
**Claim**: Egyptian Arabic is the most widely understood dialect due to Egypt's century-long dominance in Arabic-language entertainment.
**Rating**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Multiple sources confirm Egyptian Arabic's unique position. Playaling: "North Africans typically grow up with high exposure to Egyptian and Levantine content." The role of Egyptian cinema (since the 1930s), television, and music in creating passive intelligibility across the Arab world is universally documented.

### 7. ALL Dialects Lost the MSA Three-Case System
**Claim**: All Arabic dialects have completely lost the case system (nominative, accusative, genitive).
**Rating**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: This is one of the most well-established facts in Arabic dialectology. The loss of iʿrāb (case endings) is universal across all dialects and is frequently cited as the single most dramatic structural difference between MSA and colloquial Arabic.

### 8. Structural MSA-Dialect Differences: Negation, Future Markers, Vocabulary
**Claim**: Negation differs (MSA lā/lam vs. Egyptian ma...sh circumfix), future markers differ (MSA sawfa → Egyptian ha- → Levantine raḥ- → Gulf b-), vocabulary differs dramatically ("now" = al-ān/dilwaʾti/hallaʾ/al-ḥīn/dāba).
**Rating**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: All structural differences listed are confirmed by dialect comparison sources (Discover Discomfort, Middle East Eye, Alif Arabic, Kylian). The vocabulary table (now, what, want, good, bread, how, very) with MSA and four dialect variants is independently verified with matching forms.

### 9. MSA-Dialect Distance Comparable to Portuguese-Spanish or Italian-Romanian
**Claim**: The distance between MSA and some dialects (particularly Maghrebi) is comparable to the distance between related but distinct Romance languages.
**Rating**: UNCERTAIN (7/10) — Pedagogically useful but debated
**Evidence**: This analogy is commonly made in Arabic sociolinguistics and is pedagogically useful. The comparison is directionally correct for Maghrebi Arabic specifically. However, linguists debate exact equivalences. Some scholars argue the distance is even greater (Moroccan Arabic to Gulf Arabic compared to Portuguese to Italian). The classification of Arabic "dialects" vs. "languages" is politically charged, as the document's own research notes acknowledge.

### 10. Badawi 1973 Five-Level Continuum Model
**Claim**: Badawi (1973) proposed five levels for Egyptian Arabic, refining Ferguson's binary H/L model.
**Rating**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Multiple academic sources confirm Badawi's 1973 book "Mustawayāt al-ʿarabiyya al-muʿāṣira fī Miṣr" (The Levels of Contemporary Arabic in Egypt). The five levels are confirmed exactly: (1) fuṣḥā al-turāth (Classical Arabic), (2) fuṣḥā al-ʿaṣr (Modern Standard), (3) ʿāmmiyyat al-muthaqqafīn (educated colloquial), (4) ʿāmmiyyat al-mutanawwirīn (semi-literate colloquial), (5) ʿāmmiyyat al-ummiyyīn (illiterate colloquial). The refinement from binary to continuum is confirmed.

### 11. Dual Number Preserved in Nouns but Eliminated from Verb Conjugation in Dialects
**Claim**: Dialects retain dual mainly for nouns (especially time and body parts) but eliminated it from verbs.
**Rating**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: This is a well-documented feature of dialectal simplification. Examples like yōmēn (two days) and ʿēnēn (two eyes) surviving while dual verb forms are lost are standard observations in Arabic dialectology.

### 12. Social Media Disrupting Diglossic Boundaries
**Claim**: Written dialect use is exploding on social media; Arabizi (Latin script with numbers) was widespread.
**Rating**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: This is a well-documented contemporary phenomenon. The use of dialect in writing on social media platforms is widely discussed in sociolinguistic research. Arabizi (using numbers like 3 = ع, 7 = ح) is confirmed as a formerly widespread practice now declining with improved Arabic keyboard support.

### 13. Vocabulary Comparison Table (MSA vs. Egyptian vs. Levantine vs. Gulf vs. Moroccan)
**Claim**: Specific vocabulary forms for "now," "what," "want," "good," "bread," "how," "very" across five varieties.
**Rating**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Cross-checked against Discover Discomfort, Middle East Eye, and Kylian dialect comparison guides. All forms match: dilwaʾti (Egyptian "now"), hallaʾ (Levantine), al-ḥīn (Gulf), dāba (Moroccan). The Egyptian ʿēsh for "bread" (vs. MSA khubz) is widely documented.

### 14. Arabic Official Language of 26 Countries, One of Six UN Official Languages
**Claim**: Arabic is an official language of 26 countries and a UN official language.
**Rating**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: This is standard reference information. Arabic is indeed one of the six official languages of the United Nations and holds official status in 26 countries.
