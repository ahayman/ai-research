## 1. Why This Page Exists

Documentation is the most-referenced strategy in this entire hub. Almost every pattern page will link back here, because documentation is the mechanical compensation for several specific vulnerabilities that make autistic people disproportionately targeted for gaslighting and pattern-denial.

The standard framing of documentation is that it is "good practice" — a responsible thing to do in case of future need. For autistic readers, that framing is too weak. Documentation is not good practice; **it is mechanical necessity**, and the mechanism is specific:

1. **Episodic memory works differently in autistic cognition.** Multiple studies have shown that autistic adults show relative differences in episodic memory (memory for specific events contextualized in time and place) while semantic memory (general knowledge, facts) is relatively intact. This is not memory loss in any clinical sense — it is a specific shape of memory that makes you more vulnerable to "that didn't happen the way you remember it" than someone with typical episodic memory.

2. **Gaslighting directly exploits this.** The core gaslighting move is rewriting past events. If your memory of events is already less robustly anchored than the manipulator's version of events (from your own subjective experience, not from objective reality), you have fewer internal resources to resist the rewriting.

3. **The credibility discount amplifies the problem.** Lim, Young & Brewer (2022, PMC8813809) found that autistic adults are perceived as more deceptive and less credible than neurotypical adults when telling the truth. When your account is disputed by someone else, outside observers are predisposed to believe them. Verbal testimony alone will systematically underperform for you.

4. **Alexithymia complicates retrospective reporting.** About 50% of autistic adults meet criteria for alexithymia. If you cannot clearly identify and describe your emotional states in the moment, reconstructing them later is even harder. The specific words and feelings slip away; the outline remains; the outline is what the manipulator can reshape.

**Written, contemporaneous, external records solve all four problems at once.** They anchor what happened in a form that cannot be rewritten by gaslighting. They provide evidence that does not depend on your verbal testimony. They preserve the specific words and sequences that feelings alone cannot. They exist outside your cognition and therefore cannot be corrupted by it.

This is not a mental health recommendation. It is a mechanical necessity for a specific cognitive architecture operating in a specific pragmatic environment.

*Evidence quality note: The episodic memory differences finding is [VALIDATED] across multiple studies. The application to gaslighting resistance is [EXTRAPOLATED] — the mechanism is straightforward but has not been directly tested as a safety intervention. The credibility discount finding (Lim, Young & Brewer, 2022) is [VALIDATED]. The recommendation to compensate with documentation is consistent with the employment-law practitioner literature on harassment cases, which is an [ESTABLISHED LEGAL PRACTICE] rather than an [RCT-VALIDATED] recommendation.*

## 2. What Documentation Is and Isn't

### What documentation is

- **A contemporaneous written record of what happened, in enough specificity that the record carries evidentiary weight.**
- **An external anchor against memory corruption** — a place where the version you observed at the time is preserved.
- **Pattern evidence** — the accumulated record of multiple events that, taken together, show something that no single event could show.
- **A compensation for specific cognitive and social vulnerabilities** — episodic memory differences, the credibility discount, the power asymmetries that make verbal testimony unreliable.
- **A preparation for possible future action** — legal, workplace, medical, or interpersonal — that may or may not ever be needed but cannot be reconstructed if it is.

### What documentation isn't

- **It isn't a diary of your feelings.** Journaling about how you feel is a legitimate mental-health practice but it is not the kind of documentation that produces evidentiary weight or resists gaslighting. "I felt really bad today" is not the kind of record that tells you later what actually happened. Good documentation records **events**, not **emotions** — or records emotions only as part of recording events.
- **It isn't proof that will convince a skeptic.** Documentation strengthens your position but does not guarantee belief. A sufficiently hostile audience will dismiss any evidence. Documentation is most useful with neutral audiences (courts, HR if actually neutral, new therapists, your future self).
- **It isn't a substitute for action.** Documenting what is happening is not the same as addressing what is happening. Some people use documentation as a way to feel they are "doing something" when they are actually avoiding doing anything. This can be a valid coping strategy in resource-thin phases but should not be mistaken for resolution.
- **It isn't universally safe.** In some situations, the existence of documentation is itself a risk — specifically when the abuser has physical or digital access to your records and discovery would trigger escalation. This page addresses how to manage that risk, but the risk is real and the default of "always document everything" is not appropriate for every situation.
- **It isn't a one-time project.** Documentation is an ongoing practice, not a task you complete. A record from a single week in a pattern that has lasted years is much weaker than a record kept across the span of the pattern.

## 3. What to Document

Not everything needs to be documented. Documenting too much produces an unmanageable record that obscures rather than reveals the pattern. Documenting too little produces gaps that manipulators exploit. Both are failure modes.

The guidance from the employment-law practitioner literature on harassment cases, synthesized with the coercive control literature, identifies a specific set of details that make documentation evidentiarily strong:

### The six elements of usable documentation

1. **Date and time.** Not "last week" — specific dates, ideally with time of day when that matters. If you cannot be precise, note the range ("between approximately 2 and 4 PM on April 10, 2026").
2. **Location.** Where the event happened. Physical location for in-person events. Platform and channel for digital events ("in the #general Slack channel," "in a DM on Instagram," "on a work email to me and cc'd to my manager").
3. **People present.** Everyone who was there, not just the person causing harm. Witnesses matter enormously. If there were no witnesses, note that too — it's a feature of the event.
4. **What was said and done.** The specific actions and words. **Direct quotes are much stronger than paraphrases.** "He said 'you're being crazy and nobody will believe you'" is evidence; "he was dismissive of my concerns" is interpretation. Both have value, but the first carries weight the second does not.
5. **Your response, and their response to your response.** What you said or did at the time, and what they did next. The sequence is important because manipulation often operates in a specific structure (you raise a concern → they deflect → you push → they escalate). The structure is what distinguishes manipulation from disagreement.
6. **Physical evidence where available.** Screenshots, emails, texts, voicemails, photos (of damage, injuries, environmental conditions), receipts, call logs, delivery records. Preserve anything that was created by a system you do not control and that confirms elements of your written record.

### The pattern test, revisited

Individual documented events are useful but limited. **Pattern documentation is what prevails in hostile-environment workplace cases, coercive control proceedings, and other pattern-dependent contexts.** A single incident is rarely legally actionable; a pattern of incidents often is. This has a practical implication for what to document: **keep records of the small things that individually seem like "not a big deal" but collectively form the pattern.**

The small things include:
- Minor contradictions and retellings of previous events
- The exact language of dismissive comments
- The specific accommodations requested and denied
- The topics you have learned not to raise
- The times you have apologized for things you did not do
- The things you have had to document for your own sanity because you could tell you were going to doubt them later

Each of these, alone, may seem too minor to document. In pattern, they are the actual evidence.

### What not to document (or to document carefully)

- **Third parties' private information.** Do not record things about uninvolved people that could harm them if the document were disclosed. Focus on what affects you.
- **Speculation about what the other person "really thinks."** Stick to observable behavior. Inferences about motivation are weaker than records of what actually happened.
- **Emotional reactions that are not part of the event record.** "I felt crazy" is not evidence; "after the conversation I could not remember what was said and had to ask a friend to help me reconstruct it" is.
- **Material you cannot verify.** If someone else told you something happened, note that you were told, by whom, and when — do not record it as if you observed it directly.
- **Things that would hurt more if discovered than help if used.** The existence of documentation is a potential risk (see Section 6). Some things are safer to remember carefully than to record in writing.

## 4. How to Document

The practical mechanics of documentation vary by context, but some principles are general.

### Contemporaneous is dramatically stronger than reconstructed

**Write down what happened as close to the time it happened as you can — ideally the same day.** Courts, HR, therapists, and advocates all weight contemporaneous records more heavily than reconstructed accounts, because memory degrades predictably and contemporaneous records cannot be "adjusted" by later pressure. This is not just a legal convention; it is how the evidentiary weight actually works.

The implication: **a rough record made on the day is more valuable than a polished record made a week later.** If you have five minutes at the end of the day and can jot notes in a notebook, that is more useful than waiting until you have an hour to write a proper account. Get the words down while they are fresh; polish later if polishing is needed.

### Choose a format you will actually use

The best documentation system is the one you will maintain. A perfect system that you abandon after two weeks is worse than a mediocre system that you maintain for two years.

Factors to consider:
- **Accessibility under stress.** Can you use the system when you are tired, depleted, or in a sensory-overload state? Complex systems with many required fields tend to get abandoned; simple systems survive.
- **Search and retrieval.** Can you find things later? If your system is a pile of sticky notes, you have a documentation record with no searchability, which limits its usefulness.
- **Backup and durability.** Will the record survive a device failure, a house fire, a deliberate destruction? Single-location records are fragile.
- **Privacy and security.** Who can access the record, deliberately or accidentally? (See Section 5.)

### Specific format options

**Text file or document synced to cloud storage.** Simple, searchable, backed up, accessible from multiple devices. Appropriate for most situations where digital security is adequate. Formats: plain text, markdown, Word document, Google Doc.

**Email to yourself.** Underrated. You can email yourself a brief note of an event using a personal account, and the email has a timestamp you cannot alter. Easy, searchable, stored off your primary device. Works from any device with email access. Not suitable for situations where your email is monitored.

**Dedicated journaling app.** Some apps are designed for timestamped, secure documentation. Day One, Journey, and similar apps offer encryption, cloud sync, and organizational features. The investment of learning an app is worth it if it produces a documentation habit you can sustain.

**Physical notebook.** Old technology, still works. A dated notebook in which you write contemporaneous entries. Advantages: no digital surveillance, no battery, no password. Disadvantages: not searchable, not backed up, physically vulnerable to discovery or destruction. Consider a notebook stored off-site (at a friend's house, in a safe deposit box, at work if work is not the source of harm).

**Voice memos transcribed later.** If writing is harder than speaking, record voice memos and transcribe them later (or keep them as audio). Useful especially post-meltdown or post-crisis when writing is too demanding but speaking may still be possible.

**The "time-stamped photo of handwritten notes" method.** Write notes on paper, photograph them with your phone (the phone adds metadata timestamps), store the photos in a secure cloud folder. Combines the contemporaneousness of handwritten notes with the durability and searchability of digital storage.

### The follow-up email technique

This is one of the highest-value documentation strategies for workplace and interpersonal contexts:

**After any significant verbal conversation, send an email summarizing what was agreed.** Phrasing: "Per our conversation this afternoon, I wanted to confirm that we agreed on [X]. Let me know if I've captured this correctly."

This technique does three things at once:
1. Creates a written record with a timestamp
2. Gives the other person a chance to correct genuine misunderstandings (distinguishing miscommunication from manipulation — see the [Decision Framework](manipulation-hub-miscommunication-vs-manipulation.md))
3. Puts the other person in the position of either confirming, correcting, or silence — each of which is informative

Silence is particularly diagnostic. If you send a confirmation email and the person does not respond, that silence becomes evidence of their position, because "I did not object at the time" is much harder to argue against later.

**For autistic readers:** this technique is especially valuable because it plays to your strengths. Written communication allows time to compose carefully. Confirmation emails have a predictable, script-like structure you can reuse. And they convert verbal exchanges — where the credibility discount affects your testimony — into written records that speak for themselves.

### Pattern tracking: the log vs. the narrative

Over time, documentation takes two shapes:

**The log** — a chronological list of events with details (date, time, people, what happened). This is where individual events are recorded as they happen.

**The narrative** — a synthesized document that describes the pattern over time, pulling together individual log entries into a coherent picture.

Most people start with the log. The narrative comes later, either when you need to present the pattern to someone (advocate, lawyer, therapist) or when you need to see the pattern clearly for your own decision-making. Keep the log up to date; write the narrative only when needed.

## 5. Where to Store Documentation Safely

Storage is a safety issue. Documentation that can be discovered by the person causing harm is not protective — it is a potential trigger for escalation. The DV frameworks research and the workplace bullying literature both emphasize that safe storage is as important as the documentation itself.

### Threat model: who has access to your devices and records?

Before choosing storage, think concretely about who has access to:
- Your phone (physical access, knowledge of passcode, installation of monitoring apps)
- Your computer (shared accounts, proximity, monitoring software)
- Your email (shared passwords, monitoring tools, account recovery through shared devices)
- Your home (where physical records could be found)
- Your car (if records travel with you)
- Your work devices (employer surveillance, physical access by coworkers)
- Cloud storage (shared accounts, device sync)
- Your location (if someone can see where you go, they can see if you visit a storage location)

If any of these are accessible to the person causing harm, documentation stored there is at risk. This is not paranoia; monitoring apps and account surveillance are documented tactics in coercive control situations.

### Safer storage options

**Personal accounts they don't know about.** Create an email account and cloud storage account they have never heard of, accessed only from devices or sessions they cannot reach. This is the most useful single protective move you can make. The account should use a password they cannot guess and should not be linked to anything they can see.

**Off-site physical storage.** A notebook at a trusted friend's house, a safety deposit box, a locker at a gym, a desk drawer at work (if work is not the problem). The key is that the physical record is not in a location the person has access to.

**Work devices and accounts (for non-work situations only).** If the problem is not workplace-related, your work devices may actually be safer than your personal devices because a controlling partner may not have access to them. Use this carefully — employer surveillance is its own issue, and work devices can be seized by employers.

**Legal custody arrangements.** Some lawyers will hold documents in trust as part of legal representation. Some domestic violence advocates will hold documents in case files. These options require an ongoing professional relationship but provide very secure storage.

**Encryption.** If you use digital storage on devices that might be accessed, encrypt the files. Strong-password-protected zip archives, encrypted notes apps, and whole-disk encryption all help. The encryption only matters if the password cannot be guessed or extracted from you.

### Storage methods to avoid

- **Documents on a shared computer without encryption.** Trivially discovered.
- **Cloud storage synced to the monitored device.** Defeats the purpose — anything that syncs to the device is accessible from the device.
- **Email in the same inbox they can see.** Deleted emails can often be recovered; drafts folders are visible.
- **Physical documents in the shared home, even "hidden" ones.** People in shared living spaces usually find each other's "hidden" things eventually. Especially during arguments, when searches are often more thorough.
- **Conspicuous new accounts or apps.** A phone that suddenly has an encrypted notes app or a new email client is a signal. If you add tools, add them gradually or use tools that blend in.

### Browser history and digital footprints

If you are using shared devices to research this hub or similar resources, your browsing history is a record of what you have been thinking about. Private browsing modes hide history from casual inspection but do not defeat dedicated monitoring software or router-level logging.

**Safer alternatives:**
- A device your abuser does not know about
- Public computers (library, work if work is not the problem)
- A trusted friend's device
- Private networks they cannot monitor

If you must use a monitored device, clear browser history and cookies after each session. Be aware that abruptly cleared history can itself be a signal that something was being hidden.

## 6. The Risks of Documentation

Documentation is a powerful tool, but it has specific risks that must be weighed against the benefits. The honest framing is that documentation is net-protective in most situations, but not all situations, and not without cost.

### Risk 1: Discovery by the person causing harm

This is the primary physical safety risk. Documentation found by an abuser can trigger escalation — sometimes extreme escalation. Lethality indicators increase the risk (see [Safety Assessment](manipulation-hub-safety-assessment.md)). In situations where discovery is plausible and where lethality indicators are present, **selective documentation with extreme attention to storage** is more appropriate than comprehensive documentation. In the extreme case, a person may need to rely on memory plus off-site external support rather than writing things down at all.

**Mitigation:** secure storage (Section 5), gradual adoption, realistic threat modeling.

### Risk 2: Legal discovery

In legal proceedings — divorce, custody, employment lawsuits, criminal cases — documentation you have created can be subpoenaed and used, including against you. This is especially important in custody situations, where anything that can be characterized as "the parent being unstable" or "the parent being fixated on the other parent" can be used to argue against custody.

**Mitigation:** keep documentation focused on events (not speculation about the other person's motives), avoid inflammatory language, consult a lawyer before any legal proceeding to understand what your documentation will look like if disclosed. Do not write things you would not want a judge to read.

### Risk 3: Weaponization by employers

In workplace situations, documentation you create about a colleague or supervisor can become evidence in the employer's investigation — and the employer's interests may not align with yours. Employers sometimes use documentation against the complainant ("you were keeping records, so clearly this was your obsession").

**Mitigation:** store workplace documentation on non-work systems whenever possible. Keep the records factual and professional. Understand that HR is not your advocate in most situations; HR represents the employer, not the employees.

### Risk 4: Retraumatization from rereading

The act of documenting harmful events means reliving them — sometimes repeatedly, if you review what you wrote. For people with PTSD or complex trauma, this can produce real psychological harm. Documentation is not neutral emotionally; it has a cost.

**Mitigation:** limit rereading to when there is a specific purpose. Do not dwell in the records. Use the documentation as a tool when needed, then close the file. Consider discussing with a therapist how to manage the psychological load of keeping records of ongoing harm.

### Risk 5: Obsessive over-documentation

For some autistic readers specifically, documentation can tip from protective into consuming. The systematic thinking that makes autistic pattern recognition strong can also produce a tendency to document exhaustively, check repeatedly, and build increasingly detailed records that serve no additional protective function. This can drain capacity, sustain attention on the harm beyond what is useful, and begin to resemble the obsessive reviewing that characterizes some forms of PTSD.

**Mitigation:** set specific goals for documentation (what you are keeping records for, and what you will do with them). Set time limits on documentation sessions. If you notice yourself rereading compulsively or adding more and more detail that does not serve a purpose, that is a signal to step back.

### Risk 6: The "documentation as identity" trap

If you have been treated as unreliable for a long time, producing a comprehensive record can feel validating — finally, proof that you are not making it up. This is legitimate, but it can become a trap if your sense of self becomes dependent on the documentation. Being "the person who has the evidence" is a role, and roles can outlast their usefulness. The goal of documentation is protection, not identity.

## 7. Autism-Specific Considerations

### The memory anchor benefit is specific and significant

As discussed in Section 1, the research on autistic episodic memory shows relative differences that interact with gaslighting in specific ways. Documentation is not generic advice for autistic readers — it is compensation for a specific cognitive architecture. This means:

- **It is working with your grain, not against it.** Autistic cognition is systematic and rule-based. Documentation is a systematic, rule-based practice.
- **The systematic thinking that characterizes autistic cognition is an asset here.** The precision that sometimes gets labeled as "pedantic" or "obsessive" in social contexts is exactly the right tool for evidence preservation.
- **You may be better at this than neurotypical people.** Attention to detail, systematic recording, and memory for specifics are strengths that translate directly into high-quality documentation.

### Masking reduces your capacity for documentation

The masking research (Hull et al., 2017, PMC5509825) shows that camouflaging is associated with exhaustion, anxiety, depression, and suicidality. It also has a practical consequence that matters for this strategy: **masking eats the cognitive resources that documentation requires**. After a day of masking, you may not have the capacity to write a detailed account of events. You may have the will and not the capacity.

**Practical implication:** design your documentation practice for low-energy states. Short, rough notes are better than nothing. Voice memos when writing is too demanding. A template you can fill in without having to remember what fields matter. Lower the activation energy for documentation so it survives high-masking periods.

### Alexithymia and documentation

If you have difficulty identifying and naming your emotional states in the moment, you cannot document them with precision later. This has a specific implication for documentation style:

- **Focus on observable events rather than internal states.** "He said X, I said Y, he said Z" is possible to record even when "I felt Y" is not clear.
- **Note physical sensations, which are often more accessible than emotions.** "After the conversation my hands were shaking and I could not focus on my work for an hour" is concrete and reliable even when "I felt X emotion" is not.
- **Record behavioral changes over time.** "I stopped going to team lunches after the incident on [date]" is evidence of impact even when the emotional description is unclear.

### The autistic tendency to over-document and the scripted-defense trap

A specific risk for autistic readers: because the systematic-recording approach works with your strengths, you may produce records that are dramatically more detailed than what a normal observer would create. In most situations this is protective. In a specific situation — when you are later trying to explain your documentation to a neurotypical audience — it can backfire.

A neurotypical HR investigator, judge, or therapist who sees a 200-page detailed log of an ongoing situation may interpret it as "obsessive," "fixated," or "disproportionate to the events described." This is the credibility discount in a specific form — your diligence is read against you.

**Mitigation:**
- **Keep comprehensive records for yourself, but prepare selective summaries for external audiences.** The full log is your memory anchor; the summary is what you share.
- **Let an advocate or lawyer help you decide what to share from a large record.** Trained people know what will land with specific audiences.
- **Do not volunteer the full documentation to a hostile audience.** Share what is asked for, not more.

### The compliance training problem, again

Documentation is the kind of activity that can feel "wrong" to someone who has been trained to accommodate and defer. The internal script may say "I shouldn't be keeping records of a loved one" or "this is sneaky" or "if I document, I've already given up on the relationship." These are trained responses, not accurate information about the situation.

**Practical reframe:** you are not documenting "against" anyone. You are compensating for specific cognitive and social vulnerabilities that you would have regardless of the specific person causing harm. The documentation exists to protect you from memory corruption and credibility discount, which are structural facts, not acts of disloyalty.

## 8. Strategies by Context

### Intimate relationships (highest-stakes context)

- **Assess safety first.** If lethality indicators are present, default to extreme caution about storage. Documentation discovered can escalate the situation dramatically. Consult an advocate — they have specific guidance on documentation in high-risk DV situations.
- **Use accounts and devices your partner does not know about.** This is non-negotiable in coercive control situations. A new email account opened from a library computer and accessed only from work or public networks is one model.
- **Document to protect your memory first, legal usefulness second.** The primary value of documentation in an ongoing abusive relationship is maintaining your own sense of what is real. Legal applications come later if they come at all.
- **Save preserving digital evidence before it is destroyed.** Screenshot threatening texts, emails, social media. Forward emails to your secure account. Make copies of anything that lives on a device your partner could access.
- **Do not let documentation replace action.** If you are documenting for years without moving toward any kind of exit or safety planning, documentation is functioning as avoidance rather than protection. Return to the [Can This Be Repaired? page](manipulation-hub-can-this-be-repaired.md) and assess.

### Workplace

- **Store everything on non-work systems.** Personal email, personal cloud storage, personal notes. Work devices can be seized, surveilled, or wiped.
- **Preserve the legal chain of evidence.** Specific dates, exact quotes, witness names, exact email subject lines and timestamps. If you may eventually file an EEOC complaint, unemployment appeal, or lawsuit, the evidence needs to meet legal standards from the start.
- **The follow-up email is your friend.** After every significant verbal conversation, send a summary email. This creates a written record with a timestamp that the other person has implicitly confirmed by not objecting.
- **Document accommodation requests and denials meticulously.** ADA and equivalent protections depend on being able to show the employer knew about your needs and how they responded. Vague memory of "I asked for accommodations and they weren't great about it" is very different, legally, from "On [date] I sent [specific email] requesting [specific accommodation], and on [date] I received [specific response]."
- **Keep copies of performance reviews, employee handbooks, and policy documents.** Download everything while you still have access. Employers routinely revise policies after complaints are filed.
- **Be aware that your employer's interests are not your interests.** HR is not your advocate. Document accordingly.

### Medical settings

- **Write down symptoms and what you want to discuss before appointments.** You will not remember in the moment, especially under the stress of being in a medical setting.
- **Take notes during or immediately after appointments.** What the provider said, what tests were ordered, what medications were prescribed, what they recommended. Medical records do not always capture what was actually said.
- **Request your medical records.** Under HIPAA (US) and equivalent laws, you have the right to your own medical records. Request them regularly and keep copies. They are evidence of what has been diagnosed, investigated, and dismissed.
- **Document symptoms over time.** Medical gaslighting often relies on each visit being treated as an isolated encounter. A log of symptoms spanning months or years reveals patterns no single visit would show.
- **Document the specific language used when you were dismissed.** "The doctor said 'it's probably just anxiety'" is much more usable than "the doctor was dismissive." The specific phrase is what a patient advocate, second-opinion doctor, or malpractice attorney can work with.

### Family settings

- **Documentation is often less useful here than elsewhere.** Family disputes rarely reach legal systems in ways where documentation matters, and the psychological cost of documenting family harm is often high.
- **Consider selective documentation of specific patterns you need to see clearly.** You may not need a legal record, but you may benefit from being able to see the pattern of, say, how often a specific topic triggers a specific response.
- **Physical records in shared family homes are risky.** Digital records with secure storage are usually safer.
- **The follow-up email technique works for family too.** If an important conversation happened in person, an email summarizing your understanding can both create a record and surface genuine misunderstandings.

### Educational settings

- **Documentation for accommodation situations is critical.** Section 504 and IDEA in the US (and equivalents elsewhere) require that accommodations be formally requested and responded to. Keep every email about accommodations. Keep copies of IEPs, 504 plans, meeting notes.
- **For K-12 students, parents should usually be the primary documenters.** A student navigating educational discrimination without parental support is in a particularly vulnerable position.
- **For higher education, disability services offices and ombudspersons are the documented interlocutors.** Maintain email correspondence through official channels rather than informal conversations.

### Online and digital contexts

- **Screenshots with timestamps visible are the standard unit of evidence.** A screenshot without a timestamp is weaker than one that shows the date and time. Consider tools that add metadata automatically.
- **Save full conversation context, not just the offending message.** A quoted sentence out of context can be spun; a full conversation thread is harder to re-frame.
- **Platform-specific download tools.** Most major platforms (Twitter/X, Facebook, Instagram, Slack, Discord) allow you to download your account data. Do this before you are locked out, banned, or before the platform changes policies. The downloads contain information not visible in the interface.
- **Link rot is real.** Websites disappear. Archive important links using the Internet Archive's Wayback Machine, or by saving the page as a PDF, so the evidence survives the original source.

## 9. What This Page Cannot Do

- **Tell you whether documentation is safe in your specific situation.** The decision of whether and how much to document depends on your threat model, your resources, and your specific circumstances. This page provides the framework; you do the application.
- **Replace legal advice for legally-significant documentation.** If you may eventually be in a legal proceeding, a lawyer should be involved in how you document, not just this page.
- **Replace an advocate for high-stakes safety situations.** In coercive control contexts, an advocate will have specific guidance for your situation that generalizes poorly to a written resource.
- **Guarantee that documentation will protect you.** Documentation strengthens your position. It does not guarantee outcomes. People with good documentation have still lost custody cases, lost jobs, lost medical appeals, and stayed in unsafe situations. Documentation is one tool among several, not a complete solution.
- **Protect you from the costs of documenting.** Keeping records of ongoing harm is psychologically expensive. This page cannot make that cost go away; it can only explain why the cost is usually worth it in protective contexts.

## 10. Key Takeaways

1. **Documentation is mechanical necessity for autistic readers, not just good practice.** The episodic memory differences plus the credibility discount plus alexithymia plus the gaslighting vulnerability combine to make external records a specific compensation for a specific cognitive architecture.

2. **Contemporaneous is dramatically stronger than reconstructed.** A rough note written on the day beats a polished account written a week later.

3. **Specific details carry weight; generalizations don't.** Direct quotes, exact dates, named witnesses. "He was dismissive" is weak; "He said 'you're being ridiculous'" is strong.

4. **The pattern matters more than any single incident.** Keep records of the small things that individually seem too minor to matter. The pattern is where the evidence lives.

5. **The follow-up email technique is a force multiplier.** After verbal conversations, send a summary email. It creates a written record with a timestamp and gives the other person a chance to confirm or correct — both informative.

6. **Storage is as important as documentation itself.** Records discovered by the person causing harm can escalate the situation. Use storage options they cannot reach. Personal email accounts they don't know about are the single most useful protective move.

7. **Digital safety is part of documentation strategy.** Monitored devices, shared accounts, and synced cloud storage can defeat documentation before it protects you. Consider your threat model explicitly.

8. **Documentation has real risks.** Discovery, legal disclosure, employer weaponization, retraumatization, obsessive over-documentation. The benefits usually outweigh the risks in protective contexts, but not always, and not without cost.

9. **Design your practice for low-energy states.** Masking eats cognitive resources. Documentation systems that require high capacity will be abandoned. Simple, fast, habit-based systems survive.

10. **Do not let documentation replace action.** Keeping detailed records for years without moving toward any resolution can be a form of avoidance. Return to the [Can This Be Repaired? page](manipulation-hub-can-this-be-repaired.md) to reassess when needed.
