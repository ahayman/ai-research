# Category 13: Topic Management in Conversation — Theoretical Research

## Overview

Topic management is one of the most fundamental yet analytically elusive aspects of conversational pragmatics. It encompasses how speakers introduce, sustain, shift, and close topics in natural conversation — and who gets to do these things. Unlike many other aspects of conversation analysis (turn-taking, repair, adjacency pairs), topic has proven remarkably difficult to formalize precisely, because it operates not at the sentence level but as a collaboratively constructed, locally managed property of ongoing talk. For autistic individuals, topic management represents a particularly consequential area of pragmatic competence: knowing when a topic has been exhausted, recognizing that others want to shift topics, understanding how to smoothly introduce new topics, and navigating the implicit rules about what topics are appropriate in which contexts — these are all skills that depend heavily on reading subtle, often unspoken social signals.

## 1. What "Topic" Is in Conversation Analysis

### 1.1 The Surprisingly Difficult Definition

Topic — the "aboutness" of a stretch of conversation — seems intuitively obvious but has proven remarkably resistant to formal definition. Unlike a sentence, which can be analyzed for its grammatical subject, a conversation does not have a fixed, sentence-level topic. Topic in conversation is not a property of individual utterances; it is a property of stretches of talk that emerges from what participants are doing together.

The difficulty arises because:
- A single conversation can contain multiple topics, nested topics, and topics that blend into one another
- Two people can be "talking about" something without either of them ever explicitly naming it
- What counts as "the topic" depends on the level of abstraction: "we talked about vacation" vs. "we talked about the hotel pricing in Barcelona" are both valid descriptions of the same stretch of talk
- Topic is not determined by one speaker alone — it is a joint product of all participants

### 1.2 Keenan & Schieffelin (1976): Discourse Topic as Collaborative Achievement

Elinor Ochs Keenan and Bambi B. Schieffelin published the foundational paper "Topic as a Discourse Notion" (1976), which examined how topic functions in the conversations of both children and adults. Their key contributions:

- **Topic as a set of related propositions**: From a semantic perspective, they defined discourse topic not as a single sentence or idea but as a cluster of related propositions that participants treat as connected.
- **Collaborative construction**: Topic is not something one person "decides" — it is collaboratively achieved. One person may propose a topic, but it only becomes the discourse topic when the other participant(s) engage with it.
- **Topic as foregrounding**: Keenan and Schieffelin proposed that speakers use strategies to bring referents into the "foreground" of the listener's consciousness — making something the center of shared attention. This foregrounding process is a key part of how topics are established and maintained.
- **Developmental dimension**: They studied how children acquire the ability to establish and maintain discourse topics, showing that topic management is a skill that develops over time and requires understanding of what is currently in the listener's consciousness.

**Why this matters for autistic individuals**: The collaborative nature of topic means that unilateral topic control (talking at length about one's own interests without checking whether the other person has "bought in") constitutes a failure of the collaborative mechanism. Understanding that topic is jointly constructed — not individually determined — is a foundational insight.

### 1.3 Brown & Yule (1983): Topic Framework

Gillian Brown and George Yule, in their influential textbook *Discourse Analysis* (Cambridge University Press, 1983), developed the concept of the **topic framework** — the set of activated features, knowledge, and contextual information against which new talk is interpreted.

Key ideas:
- **Topic framework vs. topic**: The topic framework is broader than "what we're talking about." It includes all the background knowledge, assumptions, and contextual information that participants are currently treating as relevant. New utterances are interpreted against this activated framework.
- **Sentential topic vs. discourse topic**: Brown and Yule distinguished between what a single sentence is "about" (sentential topic) and what a stretch of discourse is "about" (discourse topic). These are different levels of analysis, and discourse topic cannot be reduced to sentential topic.
- **Topic as a descriptive convenience**: They argued that "topic" is partly an analyst's convenience — a way of describing what participants appear to be doing — rather than a precisely bounded linguistic unit. There are no clear, objective "topic boundaries" in the way there are sentence boundaries.

### 1.4 Schegloff (1990): "Speaking Topically" vs. "Talking About a Topic"

Emanuel Schegloff, in his 1990 paper "On the Organization of Sequences as a Source of 'Coherence' in Talk-in-Interaction," made a crucial distinction that reframes how we understand topic in conversation:

- **"Talking about a topic"**: This implies a pre-existing subject that speakers address, as if the topic were a container that speakers pour their utterances into. This view treats topic as external to the interaction.
- **"Speaking topically"**: This means that speakers orient to making their utterances relevant to what has just been said — they speak in ways that maintain topical coherence locally, turn by turn. Topic coherence emerges from this local orientation, not from a pre-determined plan.

This distinction is analytically powerful because it explains:
- Why conversations can "drift" smoothly from one subject to another without anyone noticing a "topic change" — each individual turn was locally relevant to the prior turn
- Why topic boundaries are so hard to identify — there often aren't sharp boundaries, just gradual shifts in what's being treated as relevant
- Why conversations rarely follow a predetermined topical agenda in everyday talk — topic is managed moment-by-moment, not planned in advance

### 1.5 Topic as Locally Managed and Collaboratively Constructed

The consensus in conversation analysis is that topic has the following properties:

1. **Locally managed**: Topic is handled turn by turn, not determined in advance. Each speaker's contribution shapes what comes next.
2. **Collaboratively constructed**: Both (or all) participants contribute to what the conversation is "about." One person's proffer of a topic succeeds only if others engage with it.
3. **Incrementally developed**: Topics evolve through successive contributions, with each turn potentially extending, narrowing, or redirecting the topical focus.
4. **Retrospectively recognizable**: You can often identify what a conversation was "about" better in hindsight than in real time.
5. **Multi-layered**: A conversation may have a "main topic" and various sub-topics, side sequences, and tangents occurring simultaneously or in rapid succession.

## 2. Topic Introduction — How Topics Enter Conversation

### 2.1 Button & Casey's Framework: Topic Initial Elicitors, Itemized News Inquiries, and News Announcements

Graham Button and Neil Casey (1984, 1985) produced foundational research on how topics are generated at the beginning of conversations. They identified three primary mechanisms:

#### Topic Initial Elicitors (TIEs)
These are broad, open questions that invite the other person to introduce a topic:
- "What's new?"
- "How's it going?"
- "What's up?"
- "How've you been?"
- "Anything happening?"

TIEs do **not** specify what should be talked about. They hand topic-selection rights to the respondent, who must choose from their mental inventory of "mentionables" — things worth reporting. The respondent selects something they project as conversation-worthy in the current context.

The sequence works as follows:
1. Speaker A produces a TIE ("What's new?")
2. Speaker B produces a newsworthy-event-report ("I got the job!")
3. Speaker A topicalizes the proffered report ("Oh wow, tell me about it!")

If Speaker B produces a **no-news report** ("Not much, same old"), the TIE has failed to generate topic, and Speaker A may try a different strategy.

#### Itemized News Inquiries
Unlike TIEs, these specify a particular item to ask about:
- "How did the interview go?"
- "Did your mom's surgery go okay?"
- "What happened with that noise complaint?"

These are more targeted and don't leave topic selection to the respondent. They demonstrate that Speaker A has been tracking Speaker B's life and has specific items in their mental inventory about B's affairs.

#### News Announcements
Here, the speaker introduces a topic by announcing news:
- "Guess what happened!"
- "I have something to tell you."
- "You'll never believe this."

These are **pre-announcements** that project a newsworthy telling and seek recipient alignment before the actual telling begins.

### 2.2 Topic Proffers

A **topic proffer** is an offer to discuss something — a bid that the other person can accept, decline, or redirect. Maynard (1980) and others have examined how proffers work:

- **Statements as proffers**: A speaker makes a statement that functions as an invitation for the listener to "buy in." Example: "I saw the craziest thing on the way to work today." The listener can accept the proffer ("Oh yeah? What happened?"), give a minimal response that doesn't develop the topic ("Hmm"), or redirect ("Yeah, traffic was awful today, speaking of which...").
- **Questions as proffers**: "Have you seen that new restaurant downtown?" proffers a topic (the restaurant) for joint discussion.
- **Topical pivots**: A specific idea or phrase in one person's talk that becomes the launching point for the next topic. If Speaker A says "I was stuck in traffic for an hour," Speaker B might use "traffic" as a pivot: "Traffic has been insane since they started that construction."

### 2.3 Stepwise Topic Transitions

The most common and socially smooth way a new topic enters conversation is through **stepwise transition** — a gradual drift from Topic A to Topic B via intermediate links. This was described by Jefferson (1984) and elaborated by many subsequent researchers.

The mechanism:
1. Speakers are discussing Topic A
2. Something in Topic A triggers an association with a related but different matter
3. A speaker picks up that associated element and develops it
4. This associated element becomes the new focus of talk
5. Without anyone announcing a "topic change," the conversation has moved to Topic B

**Example**:
- A: "The kids' school had a fire drill today." (Topic A: school events)
- B: "Oh, my office had one too last week. Took forever to get back in the building." (Still related: fire drills, but shifting to B's workplace)
- A: "Yeah, our building's elevators are so slow, it would be a nightmare." (Pivot to building issues)
- B: "Speaking of buildings, have you seen what they're doing to the one on Fifth Street?" (Now: construction/urban development)

Jefferson (1984) specifically analyzed "stepwise transition from talk about a trouble to inappropriately next-positioned matters" — how speakers move away from discussing someone's problem without appearing callous. The stepwise mechanism allows for a gradual, face-saving departure from trouble-talk.

### 2.4 Disjunctive Topic Shifts

In contrast to stepwise transitions, **disjunctive** (or **boundaried**) topic shifts involve an abrupt change where the speaker explicitly marks that they are departing from the current topic. These require explicit marking because the shift is not locally motivated by the preceding talk.

Common markers for disjunctive shifts:
- **"Oh, by the way..."** — introduces a tangentially related or unrelated topic
- **"Totally different subject, but..."** — announces a complete topic break
- **"Before I forget..."** — urgency-based shift, implies the new topic might be lost if not raised now
- **"That reminds me..."** — associative link, though the connection may be tenuous
- **"Incidentally..."** — marks a digression from the main thread
- **"One more thing..."** — often used before a conversation closing to insert a remaining mentionable
- **"You know what?"** — attention-getter that projects a new topic

Disjunctive shifts are **marked** — they carry social freight. Using them signals awareness that you are breaking conversational flow. The explicit markers serve as a kind of apology or warrant for the disruption: "I know this isn't what we were talking about, but..."

### 2.5 The "Touched-Off" Topic Shift

Sacks (1992) described **touched-off utterances** — where something in the prior talk triggers an associative memory or thought that the speaker then introduces. The speaker's new topic is "touched off" by the preceding talk, even though it may not be directly related. This is a form of topic transition that falls between stepwise (fully gradual) and disjunctive (fully abrupt):

- A: "I had the best pasta last night at that place on Main Street."
- B: "Oh, Main Street! I keep meaning to tell you — I saw your ex-boyfriend there last week."

The reference to "Main Street" touched off B's memory. The new topic (seeing the ex-boyfriend) is not about pasta or restaurants but was associatively triggered.

### 2.6 Anchor Position: Where the First Topic Lands

Maynard (1980), in "Placement of Topic Changes in Conversation," analyzed where topics are positioned in conversations, particularly phone calls. His key finding:

- **In phone calls**, the first topic after greetings/openings is typically the **reason for the call**. Sacks observed that calling someone (especially non-intimates) is an accountable action — you need a reason. That reason typically becomes the first topic.
- **In face-to-face encounters**, especially casual ones, the first topic often emerges from the situation itself: shared environment, recent events, or a TIE sequence.

### 2.7 Who Gets to Introduce Topics

Topic introduction rights are not equally distributed:
- In **casual conversation** among equals, all participants have roughly equal rights to introduce topics, though social norms still operate (e.g., the person who initiated the encounter may feel obligation to provide the first topic)
- In **institutional settings**, topic introduction is often constrained:
  - **Doctors** typically set the opening agenda ("What brings you in today?") and control which topics are pursued
  - **Teachers** control topic introduction through the IRF (Initiation-Response-Feedback) structure
  - **Interviewers** pre-determine topics through their question agenda
  - **Meeting chairs** control topic introduction through the meeting agenda

### 2.8 Topic Negotiation: Accept, Reject, Redirect

When someone proffers a topic, the other participant has three basic options:

1. **Accept**: Engage with the proffered topic, ask follow-up questions, contribute relevant information. This is the preferred response.
2. **Reject**: Fail to take up the topic — give a minimal response, change the subject, or explicitly decline ("I'd rather not talk about that").
3. **Redirect**: Take an element of the proffered topic and steer it in a different direction. "Oh, speaking of work, did I tell you about my new boss?"

Button and Casey (1985) showed that topic negotiation is an interactional achievement — both parties must be willing to sustain a topic for it to become the discourse topic. A topic that is proffered but not taken up by the recipient simply dies.

## 3. Topic Maintenance — How Topics Are Sustained

### 3.1 The Relevance Requirement

Grice's Cooperative Principle (1975) provides the theoretical foundation for understanding topic maintenance. The **Maxim of Relation** (often called the Maxim of Relevance) states: *Be relevant.* This means that conversational contributions should be relevant to the current topic and direction of talk.

However, Grice himself acknowledged that "relevance" is complex:
- What different kinds and focuses of relevance there may be
- How these shift in the course of a talk exchange
- How to allow for the fact that subjects of conversation are legitimately changed

Relevance in conversation is not a binary property (relevant/irrelevant) but a gradient. Contributions can be:
- **Directly relevant**: Responds to or extends what was just said
- **Tangentially relevant**: Connected to the current topic but shifts the focus
- **Associatively relevant**: Connected by personal association rather than logical relation
- **Irrelevant**: No discernible connection to the current topic

### 3.2 What Counts as "On Topic"

Crow (1983) classified talk relative to current topic:
- **Topic-maintaining**: Contributions that continue the current topic without adding significant new information (backchannels, agreements, repetitions)
- **Topic-extending**: Contributions that stay within the current topic but add new information, develop a sub-topic, or ask for elaboration
- **Topic-shading**: Gradually shifting the topical focus while maintaining some semantic connection to the current topic (a mechanism for stepwise transition)
- **Topic-shifting**: Moving to a different topic, either gradually or abruptly

### 3.3 How Speakers Signal They're On Topic

Speakers use various linguistic devices to explicitly mark their contributions as topically relevant:
- **"Speaking of X..."** — explicitly ties new contribution to current topic
- **"Going back to what you said about..."** — marks a return to an earlier point within the current topic
- **"Along those lines..."** — signals that the contribution extends the current topical direction
- **"On that note..."** — connects what follows to what was just said
- **"Related to that..."** — explicitly marks topical relevance

These markers are especially important when the relevance of a contribution might not be immediately obvious.

### 3.4 How Listeners Signal Topic Engagement

Topic maintenance is not just the speaker's job — listeners actively participate in sustaining topics through:

- **Backchannels**: "Mm-hm," "yeah," "right," "uh-huh" — these signal continued attention and encourage the speaker to continue on the current topic. Research confirms that minimal responses such as "mmm," "yeah," and "okay" carry no propositional content but invite the speaker to keep talking about the topic-in-progress.
- **Follow-up questions**: Asking for more detail signals interest in the current topic and actively maintains it: "What happened next?" "How did she react?"
- **Elaboration/personal contributions**: Adding one's own related experience: "That happened to me too, actually..."
- **Assessments**: Evaluative responses like "That's amazing," "How awful," "No way" signal engagement and provide emotional scaffolding for the topic
- **Nonverbal signals**: Nodding, eye contact, leaning in, facial expressions matching the emotional tone

### 3.5 Topic Exhaustion: When a Topic Runs Out of Steam

Topics do not last forever. They exhibit predictable patterns of decline:

1. **Decreasing turn length**: As a topic nears exhaustion, speakers' turns become shorter. The elaborate, detailed contributions of the topic's peak give way to brief responses.
2. **Decreasing specificity**: Talk becomes more general and summary-like rather than detailed and new.
3. **Summary assessments**: Speakers begin offering evaluative wrap-ups: "Yeah, it was really something," "Well, that's life," "So that was pretty wild."
4. **Formulations**: One speaker produces a summary or "upshot" of the discussion: "So basically, the whole thing was a disaster."
5. **Silences/lapses**: Pauses become longer and more frequent as participants run out of things to add.
6. **Declining engagement markers**: Backchannels become more perfunctory; follow-up questions stop coming.
7. **Repetition**: Speakers begin repeating points already made, signaling that no new information is available.

Heritage (2015) found that "well" frequently functions as a harbinger of topical shifts and closures — its appearance in turn-initial position can signal that the current topic is approaching its end.

### 3.6 How Long Topics Last

Research on natural conversation provides some data on topic duration, though precise measurement is difficult given the fuzzy boundaries of topics:

- Studies of casual conversation suggest that topics typically last between 1 and 5 minutes, though this varies enormously by context, relationship, and topic type
- In institutional settings (meetings, therapy sessions), topics may be sustained for much longer because they are externally structured by agendas or treatment goals
- Research by Mastromonaco et al. (published in PNAS) on conversation duration found that most conversations don't end when participants want them to — about 66% of participants identified a point during the conversation when they felt ready for it to end, and only about 2% of conversations ended when both people wanted them to. This suggests that both topic duration and conversation duration are subject to mismatched expectations between participants.

## 4. Topic Shifting — How Topics Change

### 4.1 Taxonomy of Topic Shifts

Crow (1983) provided an influential classification of topic shifts based on their semantic relatedness to the prior topic:

1. **Coherent shift**: The new topic is semantically related to the prior topic — there's a clear conceptual link
2. **Non-coherent (disjunctive) shift**: The new topic has no semantic relationship to the prior topic
3. **Renewal shift**: A previously discussed topic is revived or returned to
4. **Insert shift**: A brief tangential topic is introduced and then the conversation returns to the main topic (like a parenthetical remark)

### 4.2 Gradual/Stepwise Shift

This is the most natural and socially smooth form of topic change. Jefferson (1984) analyzed this mechanism in detail:

- **Mechanism**: Each successive turn maintains some connection to the prior turn, but the overall direction of talk shifts. No single turn is "off topic" relative to its predecessor, yet after several turns, the conversation has moved to a substantially different topic.
- **Assessment tokens as pivots**: Assessment tokens (evaluations, reactions) often serve as the pivot point in a topic shift. When a speaker produces an assessment of the current topic ("Yeah, that's crazy"), this can simultaneously close the current topic and open space for a new one.
- **Social function**: Stepwise transitions maintain cooperative social relationships by avoiding the interactional work of explicit topic closure. They allow speakers to resist presuppositions of an idea without explicit disagreement and to smoothly bypass evolving disagreements (Tiitinen & Lempiälä, 2022).

### 4.3 Marked Shifts with Explicit Transition Markers

When speakers need to shift topics without the luxury of stepwise drift, they deploy explicit **discourse markers** that signal the shift. These markers function at the discourse level — they don't change the propositional content of what follows but signal how the upcoming talk relates to the preceding talk.

#### "Anyway..."
- **Function**: Returns to the main thread of talk after a digression, or dismisses the current topic as exhausted/unproductive
- **Analysis**: Park (2010) analyzed "anyway" as a sequence-closing device. It marks a return to a prior topic or a move toward the conversational business at hand. "Anyway" effectively says: "the current tangent is over; let's get back on track."
- **Topic orientation**: "Anyway" is classified as a topic-return marker — it signals movement back to an earlier or more central topic

#### "Oh, by the way..."
- **Function**: Introduces a tangentially related or unrelated topic
- **Analysis**: The "oh" component signals a "change of state" — the speaker is marking the upcoming talk as something they just thought of or remembered. "By the way" further marks the new topic as peripheral or tangential to the main discussion.

#### "Speaking of which..."
- **Function**: Links the new topic to the current topic via an associative connection
- **Analysis**: This marker explicitly acknowledges the connection between old and new topics, making the transition appear natural even when the actual relationship is tenuous

#### "Totally different subject, but..."
- **Function**: Announces a complete break from the current topic
- **Analysis**: This is the most explicitly disjunctive marker — it warns the listener that no connection should be expected between what came before and what follows

#### "That reminds me..."
- **Function**: Introduces a new topic via personal association
- **Analysis**: Similar to touched-off utterances — something in the current talk triggered a memory. The marker acknowledges that the connection is associative/personal rather than logical

#### "Before I forget..."
- **Function**: Urgency-based shift — the new topic needs to be raised before it's lost
- **Analysis**: This marker warrants the disruption by invoking the risk of forgetting. It implies the new topic is important enough to justify interrupting the current one

#### "So..."
- **Function**: Returns to main business, launches a pending action, or introduces the reason-for-the-interaction
- **Analysis**: Bolden (2009) analyzed "so" as implementing "incipient actions" — actions that have been pending or projected. "So" characterizes the upcoming action as "emerging from incipiency" — something the speaker has been wanting to get to. It's particularly common when returning to the main business after small talk or after a digression.

#### "Well..."
- **Function**: Signals a topic boundary, transition, or pre-closing
- **Analysis**: Heritage (2015) identified "well" as having four distinct functions: frame (introducing new topic), qualifier (partial answer), face-threat mitigator (prefacing disagreement), and pause filler. As a frame, it marks a boundary between what came before and what follows, frequently serving as a "harbinger" of topical shifts and closures.

### 4.4 Unmarked/Abrupt Shifts

When a speaker changes topic without any transition marker, the shift is **unmarked** and can seem jarring or socially awkward. Unmarked shifts:
- Lack any discourse marker signaling the change
- May not orient to the current topic at all
- Can be perceived as ignoring or dismissing what the other person was saying
- Are socially marked — they draw attention precisely because they lack the expected softening

In natural conversation, truly unmarked topic shifts are relatively rare among neurotypical adults, precisely because they violate the expectation of topical coherence.

### 4.5 Topic Resumption

Topic resumption occurs when speakers return to a topic that was discussed earlier and then abandoned or suspended. This requires explicit signaling because listeners would otherwise assume a closed topic remains closed.

Common resumption markers:
- "Going back to what you were saying about..."
- "To return to the earlier point about..."
- "You know, about [earlier topic]..."
- "So, back to [earlier topic]..."

Research by Button and Casey (1985) and others shows that returns to prior topics are frequently used **as a counter-move against attempts at topic change** — if one speaker tries to shift topic and the other isn't ready, the other may explicitly return to the original topic.

The function of explicit resumption signals is crucial: without them, the listener might not realize which prior topic is being invoked, especially if many topics have intervened. Not signaling a topic resumption could cause misunderstandings since speakers are likely to consider a topic closed once it is no longer being discussed.

### 4.6 Topic Abandonment

Topics can be dropped without resolution when:
- A disjunctive event interrupts (someone arrives, phone rings)
- A more urgent topic supersedes it
- One or both parties find the topic uncomfortable and let it die
- The conversation reaches a natural closing point

Abandoned topics leave a kind of "conversational debt" — participants may feel the need to return to them later. This is related to the concept of "mentionables" — items participants carry in mental inventory that they want to raise at some point.

## 5. Topic Closure — How Topics End

### 5.1 Topic-Closing Signals

Topics close through a combination of signals that may be produced by one or both participants:

1. **Summary statements**: "So basically..." or "The bottom line is..." — these formulate the "gist" or "upshot" of the preceding talk
2. **Assessments**: Evaluative statements that wrap up the topic: "Well, that's quite a story," "Sounds like it worked out well," "That's really unfortunate"
3. **Decreasing specificity**: Talk becomes more general and less detailed
4. **Declining turn length**: Responses become shorter
5. **Longer pauses**: Silences between turns increase
6. **Pro-forma backchannels**: "Yeah..." "Right..." delivered with falling intonation and decreasing engagement

### 5.2 Formulation and Upshot

Research distinguishes two types of topic-closing formulations:

- **Gist formulation**: Summarizes what has been said — "So what you're saying is..." or "So basically, the whole thing fell apart." Gist formulations are **backward-looking** — they restate what has already been discussed.
- **Upshot formulation**: Draws out the implication or consequence of what has been discussed — "So I guess we should probably..." or "So the lesson is..." Upshots are both **backward-looking** (summarizing) and **forward-looking** (projecting a next action or conclusion).

Both types serve to close the current topic by demonstrating shared understanding of what was discussed and signaling that there is nothing more to add.

### 5.3 The Relationship Between Topic Closure and Conversation Closure

Schegloff and Sacks (1973), in their seminal paper "Opening Up Closings," established the fundamental relationship between topic organization and conversation closure:

- **You close topics before you close conversations**: A conversation cannot simply stop — it must be *brought to a close*. This requires first closing any active topics.
- **Pre-closing sequences**: Participants use pre-closing elements ("okay," "well," "alright," "so") to signal that they have no more topics to introduce. These elements occupy the conversational floor without producing new topical content.
- **Mentionables and pre-closing**: The pre-closing phase provides a last opportunity for either participant to introduce any remaining "mentionables" — topics they've been wanting to raise. If a new mentionable is introduced, the pre-closing is suspended and the conversation continues.
- **The "one more thing" phenomenon**: Speakers often introduce remaining mentionables during pre-closing with markers like "Oh, before you go..." or "One more thing..." This is because the pre-closing phase activates a mental check of "do I have anything else I need to mention?"

### 5.4 When Parties Disagree About Topic Closure

Conflict can arise when one party wants to close a topic and the other doesn't:

- **Speaker A wants to close, Speaker B doesn't**: A produces closing signals (assessments, summary statements, shorter turns), but B continues to add new information or ask questions. A may need to escalate with more explicit closing moves: "Anyway..." or "Well, moving on..."
- **Speaker A wants to continue, Speaker B wants to close**: B produces minimal responses, doesn't ask follow-up questions, allows silences. A may persist for a time but eventually reads these as disengagement signals.
- **The face problem**: Explicitly closing someone else's topic before they're ready is face-threatening — it implies their topic isn't interesting or important enough to continue. This is why topic closure is typically accomplished through indirect, gradual signals rather than explicit statements like "I'm done talking about that."

## 6. Topic Sensitivity — What Can Be Talked About When

### 6.1 The System of Taboo and Appropriate Topics

Every culture, every relationship, and every context has implicit rules about what topics are appropriate. This creates a complex, multi-layered system:

**Universally or near-universally sensitive topics** (in Western English-speaking cultures):
- Personal finances (income, debt, cost of possessions)
- Religion and religious beliefs (especially among strangers/acquaintances)
- Politics and political opinions (especially in mixed or unknown company)
- Sex and sexual behavior
- Death and dying
- Personal health problems (beyond minor ailments)
- Age and appearance (especially weight)
- Relationship problems

**The "safe topics" system**: Conversational cultures have evolved a set of topics that are generally safe for conversation with strangers, acquaintances, and in formal settings:
- Weather
- Traffic/commute
- Sports (in cultures where this is common ground)
- Food/restaurants
- Weekend plans
- Holidays/travel
- Current non-controversial events
- Shared environment (the venue, the event)

These safe topics share important properties:
- They are **impersonal** — they don't require revealing private information
- They are **non-controversial** — they don't invite strong disagreement
- They are **shared** — all parties can contribute from common experience
- They serve a **phatic function** — maintaining social connection rather than exchanging substantive information (Malinowski's "phatic communion")

### 6.2 How Topic Sensitivity Varies

Topic appropriateness is not fixed — it shifts along several dimensions:

**Relationship closeness**: Topics that are taboo with strangers become acceptable with close friends. Close friends can discuss money, relationships, health, and sex in ways that would be inappropriate with acquaintances. The progression from "safe topics" to "sensitive topics" is itself a marker of increasing intimacy.

**Setting/context**: The same topic may be appropriate in one setting and not another. Discussing health problems is appropriate with a doctor but potentially awkward at a dinner party. Discussing salary is taboo in casual conversation but necessary in a job negotiation.

**Cultural variation**: Topic taboos vary significantly across cultures. In some cultures, asking about age or income is routine; in others, it's deeply offensive. The "safe topics" system is culturally specific. Research on cross-cultural communication shows that in high-context cultures (many East Asian cultures), certain topics require even more indirect approach, and the failure to observe topic sensitivity norms is a more serious social offense. In low-context cultures (US, Northern Europe), topic boundaries are somewhat more flexible but still present.

**Power relationships**: People in positions of power can introduce topics that subordinates cannot. A boss can ask about your weekend plans, but a new employee asking probing questions about the boss's personal life would be inappropriate. (See Section 7 for detailed analysis.)

### 6.3 Face-Threatening Topics and Pre-Sequences

Some topics are inherently **face-threatening** — they risk embarrassing, offending, or putting pressure on the listener. Introducing face-threatening topics requires additional interactional work:

**Pre-sequences for delicate topics**:
- "Can I ask you something personal?" — Seeks permission before introducing a face-threatening topic. Gives the recipient the option to decline.
- "I hope you don't mind me asking, but..." — Acknowledges the face-threat in advance
- "This might be none of my business, but..." — Explicitly acknowledges potential overstepping
- "I don't know if this is appropriate to ask, but..." — Flags sensitivity
- "Don't feel like you have to answer this, but..." — Provides an explicit opt-out

These pre-sequences perform multiple functions:
1. They **warn** the listener that a sensitive topic is coming
2. They **request permission** to proceed
3. They **provide an out** — the listener can decline without losing face
4. They **demonstrate awareness** of social norms — the speaker shows they know this topic is delicate
5. They **mitigate** the face-threat by showing consideration

**Why this matters for autistic individuals**: The implicit system of topic sensitivity — knowing which topics require pre-sequences, which topics are "safe," and how sensitivity varies by context — relies heavily on social calibration and implicit cultural knowledge. For autistic individuals, this system can be opaque because the "rules" are rarely stated explicitly and require reading contextual cues about relationship closeness, setting appropriateness, and the other person's comfort level.

## 7. Topic and Power

### 7.1 The Core Principle: Who Controls Topics Controls the Conversation

Topic control is one of the primary mechanisms through which conversational power is exercised. The person who determines what is talked about, how long it is talked about, and when the topic changes holds disproportionate influence over the interaction. This principle operates across all types of interaction, from casual conversation to institutional settings.

### 7.2 Topic Control in Institutional Settings

#### Doctor-Patient Interaction
Research consistently demonstrates asymmetry in medical consultations:
- **Agenda setting**: Doctors typically control the opening agenda ("What brings you in today?") and determine which of the patient's concerns will be pursued.
- **Topic restriction**: Doctors can redirect patient talk back to medically relevant topics, limiting discussion of social or emotional concerns.
- **Unmet concerns**: When doctors control the agenda tightly, patient concerns can go unaddressed. Research identifies three categories of agenda items: primary (what the doctor focuses on), additional (what gets briefly addressed), and unmet (what the patient wanted to discuss but didn't get to).
- **Conversation analysis research**: Studies using CA methodology show that patients initiate agendas more often than doctors in terms of frequency, but doctors have more power over which agendas are actually developed and pursued. Both parties can resist expanding the agenda, but the doctor's resistance is typically more effective.
- **Persistence of asymmetry**: Research documents the "remarkable persistence of asymmetry in doctor-patient interaction," noting that despite decades of advocacy for patient-centered care, the fundamental power asymmetry in topic control remains a feature of medical encounters.

#### Teacher-Student Interaction
The classroom presents one of the clearest examples of institutionalized topic control:
- **The IRF/IRE structure**: Sinclair and Coulthard (1975) and Mehan (1979) described the dominant classroom pattern — Initiation (teacher asks question), Response (student answers), Feedback (teacher evaluates). Research shows that 82% of discourse sequences are initiated by the teacher.
- **Display questions**: The majority of teacher initiations consist of "display questions" — questions where the teacher already knows the answer and is testing the student's knowledge. These are not genuine information-seeking questions and they constrain the topical scope of student talk.
- **Limited student topic rights**: Students generally cannot introduce new topics except when explicitly invited to do so. They are "closed down when they try to" introduce or shift topic outside of teacher-sanctioned moments.
- **Power dynamics**: Teachers put constraints on students' participation rights and restrict what counts as acceptable contributions to the discourse.

#### Courtroom Discourse
The courtroom represents perhaps the most extreme case of institutionalized topic control:
- **Pre-allocated turn-taking**: Judges and lawyers ask questions; witnesses and defendants answer. The turn-taking system is not open — "defendants were not allowed to ask questions."
- **Question design**: Cross-examiners use questions designed to force specific types of responses, constraining what the witness can contribute topically.
- **Total topic control**: The judge controls the flow of conversation entirely — "judges' questions, responses, interruptions, and controlled dialogue reiterate their position of highest authority."

#### News Interviews
Clayman and Heritage (2002) analyzed the news interview as an institutional setting with specific topic control features:
- **Pre-allocated topic rights**: The interviewer sets the agenda, chooses topics, contexts, and questions "on behalf of the audience."
- **Question-answer format**: The interviewer asks, the interviewee answers. This structure gives the interviewer de facto topic control.
- **Interviewee resistance**: Politicians and public figures "struggle to stay 'on message' and pursue their own agendas" against the interviewer's topic control. The tension between interviewer topic control and interviewee agenda-pushing is a central dynamic of news interviews.
- **Topic restriction**: The interviewer determines when topics will be changed, when the interview will be interrupted, and when it will close.

#### Job Interviews
Structured interviews restrict topic control to the interviewer, with pre-determined questions and limited scope for the interviewee to introduce topics. Research on assessment interviews (including IELTS speaking tests) shows that "management of topic is almost entirely pre-determined by the examiner's script" and there are "asymmetrical rights to topic management between examiner and candidate."

### 7.3 Topic Control and Social Hierarchy

Topic control correlates with social status even in ostensibly "equal" conversations:
- Higher-status individuals are more likely to introduce topics
- Their topics are more likely to be taken up and developed
- They are more likely to successfully shift topics
- Lower-status individuals are more likely to have their topics dropped or overridden

Okamoto and Smith-Lovin (2001), in "Changing the Subject: Gender, Status, and the Dynamics of Topic Change" (American Sociological Review), developed a systematic coding system for topic shifts and used hypotheses from group processes literature to predict whose topics would be developed and whose would be lost. Their research confirmed that social status affects topic dynamics in task-oriented discussions.

### 7.4 Gendered Patterns in Topic Control

Research on gender and conversational power reveals systematic differences in topic management:

**The Dominance Perspective (West & Zimmerman, 1983; Fishman, 1983)**:
- Men use more mechanisms for controlling the topic, including both topic development and the introduction of new topics
- Men initiate significantly more interruptions in cross-sex conversations — in West and Zimmerman's study, men initiated 96% of interruptions in cross-sex conversations
- Interruptions function as bids to take back the floor and control the conversation, and thus to control topic
- Men try to maintain dominance over topic by showing more assertive behavior during topic development and maintenance

**Tannen's Difference Perspective (1990)**:
- Women engage in "rapport talk" — communication meant to promote social affiliation and emotional connection
- Men engage in "report talk" — communication focused on exchanging information and establishing status
- These different conversational goals lead to different topic preferences: women tend toward personal, relational topics; men tend toward informational, status-relevant topics
- Tannen argued this reflects different socialization (girls taught that "talk is the glue that holds relationships together") rather than dominance per se

**Key gendered patterns in topic management**:
- Men are more likely to have their topics taken up and developed by the group
- Women's topics are more frequently dropped or redirected
- Women do more "conversational maintenance work" — asking questions, providing backchannels, supporting others' topics
- Men are more likely to make disjunctive topic shifts; women are more likely to use stepwise transitions
- Women tend to show greater acceptance in conversation and a more "facilitative role"; men tend to show more assertive behavior

**Important caveat**: These findings describe tendencies in group averages and are heavily context-dependent. They are also contested — some researchers argue that status differences, not gender per se, drive these patterns, and that when status is controlled for, gender differences diminish.

## 8. Connections to Other Categories and Autism-Specific Considerations

### 8.1 Why Topic Management Is Particularly Challenging for Autistic Individuals

Topic management difficulties are among the most commonly noted pragmatic challenges for autistic people. Research identifies several specific areas of difficulty:

**Recognizing topic exhaustion signals**: The signals that a topic is winding down — shorter turns, declining engagement, summary assessments, longer pauses — are primarily conveyed through prosody, timing, and nonverbal behavior. For individuals who process these channels differently, the exhaustion signals may be missed, leading to continued talk on a topic that others consider finished.

**Perseverative topic focus**: Research on "perseverative speech" or "restricted topic focus" in autism shows that autistic individuals may return repeatedly to topics related to their intense interests. Importantly, newer research reframes this as "autistic focused interests" rather than pathology. Discourse analysis suggests that focused interest-driven communication serves important functions: self-regulation, comfort in social interaction, and genuine sharing of knowledge and enthusiasm. The challenge is not the interest itself but the mismatch between the speaker's desire to discuss the topic and the listener's engagement level.

**Failure to adapt to listener interest**: Research by Cola et al. (PMC9167260, 2022) found that neurotypical participants adapted their talkativeness based on conversation partner cues (talking more when their partner appeared interested, less when bored), but autistic participants did not differentially adapt their behavior to bored versus interested contexts, remaining consistently talkative in both.

**Difficulty with stepwise transitions**: Stepwise topic transitions — the most common and socially valued form of topic change — require real-time tracking of associative links and shared knowledge. Autistic individuals may find it easier to use disjunctive shifts (which are more explicit) but may not realize that disjunctive shifts are socially marked and can seem jarring to others.

**Reading topic proffers**: When someone proffers a topic (makes a statement inviting engagement), recognizing this as a proffer — and knowing how to respond — requires pragmatic inference. The difference between "I tried that new restaurant" as small talk vs. as a topic proffer depends on contextual cues.

**Knowing when to introduce face-threatening topics**: The system of pre-sequences for sensitive topics ("Can I ask you something personal?") is based on calibrating the social risk of a topic in a given context. This calibration depends on implicit knowledge about relationship closeness, setting appropriateness, and cultural norms that may not be intuitively available.

### 8.2 The Double Empathy Problem and Topic Management

Recent discourse research invokes the "double empathy problem" (Milton, 2012): communication barriers between autistic and neurotypical individuals arise from mutual misunderstanding, not just from autistic "deficits." Applied to topic management:

- Neurotypical individuals may fail to accommodate autistic communication styles just as much as autistic individuals may fail to read neurotypical signals
- Topic management "rules" are neurotypical conventions, not universal laws of communication
- When two autistic people converse about shared interests, topic management may work very differently — with extended, deep exploration of single topics being natural and welcome rather than "perseverative"

### 8.3 Cross-References to Other Categories

- **Category 4 (Conversation Structure)**: Topic management is embedded within the larger structure of conversation openings, middles, and closings. Category 4's supplemental on openings/closings covered topic introduction, shifting, and safe topics briefly — this document provides the comprehensive deep dive.
- **Category 12 (Narrative)**: Narrative telling involves extended topic holding — the narrator maintains the floor and develops a single topic at length. Topic management interacts with narrative in that listeners must suspend topic-shifting rights during storytelling.
- **Category 22 (Power and Status)**: Topic control is a primary mechanism of conversational power. Who introduces topics, whose topics get developed, and who gets to shift topics are all indexical of social hierarchy.
- **Category 3 (Turn-Taking)**: Topic management and turn-taking are deeply intertwined. Topic shifts often occur at turn boundaries, and the ability to hold the floor long enough to develop a topic depends on turn-taking rights.
- **Category 5 (Adjacency Pairs)**: Topic introduction often occurs through adjacency pair sequences (question-answer, news announcement-response).
- **Category 8 (Politeness and Face)**: Topic sensitivity and the need for pre-sequences before face-threatening topics connect directly to Brown and Levinson's politeness theory.
