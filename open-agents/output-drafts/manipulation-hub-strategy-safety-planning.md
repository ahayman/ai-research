# Safety Planning

## Pragmatic Manipulation and Self-Protection Hub — Strategy Page

**⚠️ Read first:** [Safety Assessment](manipulation-hub-safety-assessment.md) — **if lethality indicators are present, this page is orientation, not execution.** Safety planning research is unambiguous on one point: **safety planning is substantially more effective when paired with advocacy** (Sullivan & Bybee, 1999, RCT evidence). If you are in a situation where safety planning is needed, please also contact a trained advocate. The [Crisis Resources Directory](manipulation-hub-crisis-resources.md) lists NDVH (1-800-799-7233 in the US) and international equivalents. This page cannot substitute for an advocate who knows your jurisdiction, your options, and the specific risks you face.

---

## 1. Why This Page Exists (and What It Can and Cannot Do)

Safety planning is not a general strategy. It is a specific response to a specific situation: **you may need to leave, or you may need to protect yourself while staying, in a situation that includes credible risk of physical harm.**

If that description fits your situation, the research base is unambiguous about three things:

1. **Safety planning paired with advocacy reduces revictimization.** The landmark Sullivan & Bybee (1999) RCT in *Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology* demonstrated that community-based advocacy combined with safety planning reduced re-assault at 24-month follow-up. Advocacy is the active ingredient; plans alone are significantly weaker.

2. **Safety planning is most effective when informed by a structured risk assessment.** Glass et al. (2017) on the MyPlan decision aid found that tailored, danger-informed safety planning outperforms generic plans. The specific instrument is Campbell's Danger Assessment (Campbell et al., 2003, 2009), which is the gold standard but is not a DIY tool — it requires trained administration to produce calibrated risk scores.

3. **"Just leave" is empirically unsafe.** 55.2% of women murdered by an intimate partner had separated or were in the process of leaving (Campbell et al., 2003). The separation period is the single highest-risk window. Safety planning is specifically designed to reduce the risk of this period, not to eliminate it. This is why leaving without a plan is so dangerous and why "the research says you should leave" has to be paired with "the research says how to leave."

**What this page can do:**
- Orient you to the components of safety planning that the research base supports
- Help you understand what a safety plan is and is not
- Provide the vocabulary to have a productive conversation with an advocate
- Identify the autism-specific adaptations that the 2024 pilot RCT (eClinicalMedicine) found matter
- Give you something concrete to read and think about while you are in precontemplation or contemplation stages of exit (see [Can This Be Repaired?](manipulation-hub-can-this-be-repaired.md))

**What this page cannot do:**
- Substitute for a trained advocate who knows your specific situation
- Perform a Danger Assessment or other calibrated risk scoring
- Tell you when to leave
- Tell you how to leave safely in your specific circumstances
- Replace legal advice for custody, restraining orders, or protective proceedings
- Address the financial and practical logistics of your specific jurisdiction
- Guarantee safety if followed

**Safety planning is one of the areas of the hub where professional advocacy is most strongly indicated.** The reason is not that the content is secret or that only professionals can understand it — the reason is that the active ingredient in the research on safety planning effectiveness is the advocate, not the plan itself. A plan you made alone is qualitatively different, in its protective effect, from a plan you made with someone trained in exactly this work.

## 2. The Three Phases of Safety Planning

The NDVH framework, which has been the de facto standard for safety planning in US DV advocacy since its founding in 1996, organizes safety planning into three temporal contexts. Each phase has different considerations and different concrete actions.

### Phase 1: Safety while in the relationship (or situation)

This phase addresses: **how do I reduce the risk of harm in the period before I am able to leave, or during a period when I am not planning to leave?**

Many people stay in dangerous situations for extended periods — months or years — for legitimate reasons: inadequate resources, children, legal constraints, fear of retaliation, or because they have not yet decided whether leaving is the right choice (see the Prochaska stages discussion on the [Can This Be Repaired? page](manipulation-hub-can-this-be-repaired.md)). Phase 1 safety planning is for this period.

**Core Phase 1 elements:**

- **Identify a safe room and safe exits.** In a home you share with the person causing harm, know which rooms have multiple exits, which have locks, which have phones or access to a phone, and which are closest to weapons (both yours to avoid and theirs to know about). Bathrooms and kitchens are statistically more dangerous because of proximity to weapons (kitchen knives, bathroom hazards).

- **Memorize crisis numbers.** Phone numbers you can call without looking them up. Write them somewhere only you can access, or memorize them. The NDVH number (1-800-799-7233), a trusted friend's number, and local emergency (911/999/000/etc.). Phones can be taken; memory cannot.

- **Identify neighbors or other external people** who could respond if you signaled for help. Consider what signal you could use that would not escalate the situation but would bring help.

- **Teach children what to do if something happens.** This is advocate territory; children's safety involves specific considerations beyond the scope of this page.

- **Hide critical items.** Spare keys, a small amount of cash, important documents, a burner phone if possible. These should be in a location the person causing harm will not find. Options: with a trusted friend, in a vehicle trunk, in a workplace locker, in a safe deposit box. The location itself is part of the plan.

- **Digital safety.** If you are planning exit or communicating with advocates, use devices the person causing harm does not monitor. Personal email accounts they do not know about. Public library computers. A trusted friend's phone. See the [Documentation page](manipulation-hub-strategy-documentation.md) for more on digital security.

- **Substance use planning.** Alcohol and drugs reduce your ability to execute safety plans and increase the risk of escalation. This is not a moral statement; it is a practical planning consideration. If substance use is part of the dynamic, safety planning needs to account for it.

- **De-escalation knowledge.** Know what triggers escalation in your specific situation and what has historically de-escalated. This is highly individual and is best worked out with an advocate, but self-knowledge is a starting point. (See the [De-escalation strategy page](manipulation-hub-strategy-de-escalation.md) for the detailed treatment.)

### Phase 2: Safety when preparing to leave

This phase addresses: **how do I prepare for exit so that the exit itself is as safe as possible?**

This is the phase that is most commonly handled poorly in popular framings, which treat leaving as a single decision rather than a preparation process.

**Core Phase 2 elements:**

- **Document copies.** Copies of ID, passport, insurance cards, birth certificates, custody papers, marriage certificate, financial documents, medical records, and any legal paperwork. Stored where the person causing harm cannot find them. Given to a trusted person if necessary. Preparing to leave with no documentation is dramatically worse than preparing to leave with copies of everything.

- **Financial preparation.** A separate bank account they do not know about, if possible. Cash stored securely. Understanding of joint debts and what you are liable for. Credit check to see what is in your name. Money saved, even in small amounts, in a place they cannot access.

- **Housing planning.** Where will you go? For how long? What happens after that? This is where many exit plans fail — the first night is planned but the second week is not. Domestic violence shelters are a legitimate option; they typically provide temporary housing, immediate safety, and connection to longer-term resources. The NDVH can connect you to local shelter options.

- **Transportation planning.** How will you get there? What do you take with you? Is your primary vehicle accessible at the time you need it? Is it in a condition to travel? Public transit options? If you need to ask someone for a ride, who?

- **Timing.** When will you leave? What is the window of opportunity? Many exits are timed to when the person causing harm is absent (at work, traveling, at an event). The abuser's schedule becomes strategically important.

- **Emotional preparation for the exit window.** You will likely be exhausted, frightened, and disoriented during the actual exit. Plan for the execution at your best, then prepare for executing it at your worst.

- **What to bring.** An emergency bag (see Section 5) prepared in advance, stored where it can be grabbed quickly.

- **Communication plan with trusted others.** Who knows you are planning this? Who will you contact immediately after you leave? What will you tell them? Have the conversation in advance so the communication does not have to be improvised in the crisis moment.

- **Pet safety.** Many DV shelters do not accept pets, though this is changing. If you have pets, their safety is part of the plan. Some organizations specifically help with pet-safety during exit: Safe Havens for Pets (RedRover), PALS (Pet Alliance Lost and Stray), and state-specific pet shelters for DV survivors. Abusers sometimes threaten or harm pets as leverage; leaving a pet behind can be used against you.

### Phase 3: Safety after leaving

This phase addresses: **how do I stay safe in the period after separation, which the research identifies as the highest-risk period?**

The post-separation period is qualitatively different from the in-relationship period and requires its own planning.

**Core Phase 3 elements:**

- **Change locks and security systems.** If you are in a home the person causing harm has had keys to. Change them, change the alarm codes, change garage openers, check for spare keys that were hidden.

- **Route variation.** Do not take the same route to work, to children's schools, to the grocery store, every day. Predictable routines are exploitable by stalkers.

- **Workplace notification.** Inform trusted colleagues, supervisors, and security (where applicable) about the situation so they know who to stop at the door or how to respond to calls from the person causing harm. This is difficult and carries its own risks; work with an advocate on the framing.

- **Children's schools and daycares.** If children are involved, their school, daycare, or care providers need to know the situation and need explicit written instructions about who is and is not authorized to pick them up.

- **Protective orders.** A protective order (restraining order, order of protection, peace bond, depending on jurisdiction) is a legal document that provides law enforcement with a basis for action if the person causing harm contacts you. Protective orders have mixed empirical effectiveness — they help in many cases and are violated in a substantial minority (Logan & Walker, 2010). They are part of a safety plan, not a guarantee.

- **Digital security hardening.** Change passwords on every account. Check for stalkerware on devices. Change email addresses if needed. Review who has access to what. The person causing harm may have more digital access than you realize.

- **Stalking awareness.** Post-separation stalking is common and documented (Logan & Walker, 2017, *Trauma, Violence & Abuse*). Partner stalkers have specific patterns: greater variety and frequency of tactics, shared knowledge and networks, earlier onset, greater psychological toll. Stalking safety planning is a specific sub-area that requires dedicated attention.

- **Emotional preparation.** The post-separation period is often when the full trauma response becomes accessible (Herman, 1992 — safety precedes processing). You may be more symptomatic after leaving than before, even though you are physically safer. This is normal and expected; it does not mean leaving was the wrong choice.

- **Ongoing advocate contact.** Safety planning is not a one-time event. The situation evolves; the plan needs to evolve with it. An ongoing relationship with an advocate provides the continuity that makes long-term safety planning effective.

## 3. The Danger Assessment and Risk-Informed Planning

Safety planning is more effective when it is informed by a structured risk assessment. The research on the Campbell Danger Assessment (DA) is strong enough that advocates routinely use it to tailor safety plans to specific risk levels.

**Key points about the Danger Assessment:**

- **It is the gold standard** for IPV lethality risk assessment (Campbell et al., 2003, 2009).
- **It is not a DIY tool.** The scoring involves weighted multivariate risk factors that produce a calibrated risk score. Self-administration does not produce the same result as trained administration.
- **Some of the highest-risk indicators deserve awareness** even without a full DA:
 - Prior non-fatal strangulation (OR 7.48 for femicide; Glass et al., 2008 — see the [Safety Assessment page](manipulation-hub-safety-assessment.md))
 - Weapon access, especially firearms
 - Prior threats to kill
 - Escalating severity over time
 - Recent separation or attempted separation
 - Violent jealousy
 - Forced sex
 - Abuser suicide threats

If any of these indicators are present in your situation, **safety planning done alone is substantially worse than safety planning done with advocacy.** This is the clearest case for contacting NDVH or an equivalent resource: they can perform the Danger Assessment and use the result to calibrate your safety plan.

**The Lethality Assessment Program (LAP)** is a version of the DA used by first responders in Maryland and other jurisdictions. It has shown population-level reductions in intimate partner femicide in quasi-experimental evaluations (35–45% reduction reported by Maryland Network Against Domestic Violence, 2022). This demonstrates that risk-informed safety planning paired with advocate connection is one of the more empirically supported interventions in the DV field.

**If you want an advocate to perform the DA with you**, call NDVH or your local DV service and ask. This is standard practice; you do not have to have a specific crisis to request it.

## 4. Autism-Specific Safety Plan Adaptations

This is one of the few areas of the manipulation hub where autism-specific empirical research exists. The Autism Adapted Safety Plans pilot RCT (eClinicalMedicine, 2024) and the related *Autism in Adulthood* paper on adapting safety plans with involvement from the autism community identified specific adaptations that made standard safety plans more usable for autistic adults.

**These adaptations are [PRELIMINARY] — a pilot RCT, not a large validation study** — but they are the best evidence currently available, and they come directly from autism-community-involved research.

### Adaptation 1: Plan when not in distress

Standard safety planning advice often describes planning as something to do "when you need it." For autistic readers, this does not work well — when you are in distress, your executive function, working memory, and decision-making capacity are all reduced. Plans made in crisis are often poorer than plans made in calmer times.

**Practical application:** Do your safety planning when you are relatively calm and resourced, even if the situation does not feel urgent in that moment. Review the plan regularly. Update it as the situation changes. When the crisis moment comes, you do not have to plan — you just have to execute a plan that already exists.

### Adaptation 2: Involve a trusted supporter

The 2024 pilot found that adapted safety plans that explicitly incorporated a trusted supporter (from the reality-check network or equivalent) were more acceptable and feasible than standard plans made alone. The supporter is not a surrogate decision-maker; they are an external reference point who knows the plan exists and can help with execution.

**Practical application:** Identify one or more people from your [Reality-Check Network](manipulation-hub-strategy-reality-check-networks.md) who will know about your safety plan. Tell them what is in it. Ask them to do specific things (hold documents, be available by phone, provide a place to go, pick you up from somewhere). Having a supporter involved reduces the cognitive load of executing the plan alone.

### Adaptation 3: Flexibility within structure

Autistic cognition often benefits from clear structure, but safety planning also requires flexibility because situations evolve. The 2024 research found that plans which were structured but not rigid worked better than either fully improvisational or completely scripted plans.

**Practical application:** Write down the plan in structured form, but include alternative branches. "If X happens, do A. If X is not possible, do B. If neither is possible, do C." This gives you decision trees that can be followed without real-time improvisation under stress.

### Adaptation 4: Pictures, symbols, and concrete rather than abstract coping strategies

For many autistic adults, abstract guidance ("use coping strategies," "practice self-care") is less useful than concrete, specific instructions. The autism-adapted safety plan research specifically recommended the use of pictures, symbols, and concrete examples.

**Practical application:**
- Use lists and checklists rather than narrative paragraphs in your plan
- Include specific phone numbers rather than "call someone"
- Include specific addresses rather than "go somewhere safe"
- Include specific sentences to say rather than "communicate your needs"
- Consider visual formats (flowcharts, lists with boxes to check) that reduce the cognitive load of reading a plan under stress
- Include photos or images if that helps you — a photo of the trusted friend's house, a map screenshot, a picture of the bag you need to grab

### Adaptation 5: Sensory considerations

Standard safety planning rarely addresses sensory considerations, but for autistic readers they matter. A safe destination that is sensory-hostile (loud, bright, unpredictable) is less viable than one that is not. Plans that require sensory-demanding tasks (emergency room visits, police station encounters, courthouse appearances) may need explicit sensory preparation.

**Practical application:**
- Include sensory considerations in evaluating destinations and actions
- Pack sensory support items in your emergency bag (noise-canceling headphones, sunglasses, stim toys, comfort objects)
- Know which environments you can tolerate for how long
- Plan recovery time after sensory-demanding exit actions

### Adaptation 6: Pre-scripted communications

Under stress, autistic language production often becomes harder, not easier. Pre-composed phrases, scripts, and written documents reduce the load.

**Practical application:** Write down what you will say in key scenarios. "I need you to come get me. I cannot explain right now." "I am calling 911 to report a domestic violence situation at [address]. I am autistic and may need extra time to answer questions." "I need to check into the shelter. I was told I could arrive anytime." Have these available as written prompts or memorized phrases so you do not have to construct them under stress.

### Adaptation 7: Consider processing time

Many safety planning frameworks assume rapid decision-making. Autistic cognition often benefits from more processing time, but stressful situations compress that time. The adaptation is not to pretend you can process faster; it is to build plans that do not require rapid decisions.

**Practical application:**
- Pre-decide as much as possible so the execution does not require new decisions
- Where decisions are unavoidable, identify the decision criteria in advance ("if X, then Y")
- Include pauses in the plan — times to stop, check in with a supporter, regulate before continuing
- Accept that the execution may take longer than standard safety plans assume

### Adaptation 8: Masking awareness

Many autistic adults have extensive experience with masking in professional and public settings. In crisis execution of a safety plan, masking may or may not serve you. Sometimes it helps (passing as neurotypical to avoid drawing attention); sometimes it costs capacity you need for survival.

**Practical application:** Be aware of when you are masking during plan execution. If you are spending significant cognitive resources on appearing "fine" to strangers at a hotel check-in or a shelter intake, you are using resources that could otherwise go to safety execution. Where possible, choose destinations where you can unmask without danger (shelters with autism awareness, autistic friends, autism-informed advocates).

*Evidence quality: [PRELIMINARY]. These adaptations come from a single pilot RCT (eClinicalMedicine 2024) with small sample size, plus the related qualitative research from *Autism in Adulthood*. They represent the best autism-specific empirical evidence currently available, which is significant given the near-total absence of autism-specific safety planning research before 2024.*

## 5. The Emergency Bag

The emergency bag (sometimes called a "grab bag," "ready bag," or "go bag") is the concrete tool that makes rapid exit possible. The research supports having one in contexts where exit may be needed with little notice.

### What goes in the emergency bag

**Essential documents (copies, not originals):**
- Government ID (driver's license, passport, ID card)
- Birth certificate
- Social Security card (US) or equivalent
- Immigration papers
- Insurance cards (health, auto, home/renter)
- Medical records, current prescription list
- Children's documents (if applicable)
- Marriage certificate, divorce papers, custody papers
- Bank information
- Lease or mortgage documents
- Court papers from prior proceedings

**Financial:**
- Cash (denominations that will actually be useful — small bills for transit, taxis, tips)
- Checkbook or debit card from an account they cannot access
- Credit cards they cannot cancel or track

**Communication:**
- Charged phone (or burner phone, if your primary phone is monitored)
- Phone charger
- Written list of important phone numbers (in case phones fail)
- A second form of communication if possible (prepaid phone)

**Keys:**
- Spare house keys (for the destination you are going to)
- Car keys
- Safe deposit box keys if applicable

**Clothing and personal items:**
- A few days of clothing
- Toiletries (enough for a few days)
- Medications (at least a week's supply, preferably more, with prescription information)
- Glasses or contact lenses, backup if you have them
- Sensory support items (for autistic readers specifically: noise-canceling headphones, sunglasses, stim toys, comfort items)

**For children (if applicable):**
- Favorite toy or comfort item
- Clothing
- Diapers, formula, baby food as needed
- Children's medications
- Children's documents (birth certificate, immunization records, school records)

**For pets (if applicable):**
- Leash, carrier, collar with ID
- Food for several days
- Medical records and medications
- A recent photo (in case you are separated and need to identify them)

**Legal and evidence:**
- Copies of documentation you have been keeping (see the [Documentation page](manipulation-hub-strategy-documentation.md))
- Photographs of injuries, property damage, or relevant scenes
- Any existing protective orders

### Where to store the emergency bag

The bag has to be accessible when you need it and inaccessible to the person causing harm. Options:

- **With a trusted friend or family member who does not know the abuser.** The bag lives at their house.
- **In a vehicle.** Trunk or glove compartment, if the vehicle is accessible.
- **At work.** A locker or desk drawer, if work is not the source of harm and the bag can be stored securely.
- **At a safety deposit box or storage locker.** Requires access during the exit window, but is highly secure.
- **Hidden in your own home.** Riskier — abusers often search homes during arguments — but sometimes the only option.

### When to update it

- **Regularly** (every 3-6 months). Documents expire, medications change, clothing sizes change, financial situations change.
- **When the situation escalates.** Any significant change in the pattern should prompt a plan review.
- **When circumstances change.** New pet, new child, new job, new medical condition.

### The "scaled-down" emergency bag

Not everyone has the resources or opportunity to prepare a full emergency bag. A scaled-down version still has protective value:

- A digital version: copies of documents in a cloud account they do not know about
- A minimal physical version: just ID, cash, and phone charger in a location they do not search
- A "borrowed bag": a friend stores an emergency bag for you, and you only need to reach them

Even a minimal preparation is dramatically better than none.

## 6. What Safety Planning Cannot Do

The research on safety planning is unambiguous about its limits:

- **It cannot guarantee safety.** Even the best safety plan does not eliminate risk; it reduces it. Campbell et al. (2003) found that many femicide victims had taken protective actions. Protection is probabilistic, not absolute.
- **It is only as effective as its execution.** A perfect plan that cannot be executed under stress is less valuable than an imperfect plan that can. Planning for execution under the cognitive conditions of actual crisis is part of the plan.
- **It does not replace advocacy.** Sullivan & Bybee (1999) showed advocacy was the active ingredient. A plan made alone is weaker than a plan made with an advocate.
- **It is context-dependent.** A plan that makes sense in one jurisdiction, with one set of resources, with one specific person causing harm, may not translate to another situation. Individual tailoring is essential.
- **It is not a one-time event.** Situations evolve. Plans must evolve with them. An out-of-date plan can be worse than no plan.
- **It does not address the underlying cause.** Safety planning reduces harm; it does not end the situation causing the harm. The [Can This Be Repaired? page](manipulation-hub-can-this-be-repaired.md) addresses the broader decision about direction.

## 7. Strategies by Situation

### If you are in Phase 1 (still in the situation)

- **Contact an advocate even if you are not planning to leave.** The advocate can help you safety-plan for Phase 1 and provide continuity for when Phase 2 or 3 becomes relevant.
- **Build resources quietly.** Small amounts of cash, copies of documents, a trusted supporter — these build over time and become the foundation of Phase 2.
- **Prioritize digital security.** The person causing harm may have more digital access than you realize. Do your Phase 1 planning work on devices they cannot see.
- **Do not announce the planning.** Discovering a half-finished safety plan can escalate the situation dramatically.

### If you are in Phase 2 (preparing to leave)

- **Advocacy is not optional in this phase.** Contact NDVH or the equivalent. Tell them you are in the preparation phase. They have specific guidance on timing, logistics, and lethality-aware preparation.
- **Execute the plan at your calmest moment possible.** Not when the situation is most urgent; when you have the clearest head. Exit timing is about strategic opportunity, not acute crisis.
- **Have a backup plan.** If the primary exit opportunity falls through, what is the next option?
- **Do not leave without the emergency bag.** The few minutes saved by skipping it are rarely worth the cost of leaving without your documents, medications, and identification.
- **Communicate with your trusted supporter immediately after you are out.** The first post-exit contact reduces isolation and begins the Phase 3 planning.

### If you are in Phase 3 (after leaving)

- **The post-separation period is the highest risk.** Take the stalking and retaliation risks seriously even if the person causing harm seemed to accept the exit calmly.
- **Protective orders are worth considering but are not guarantees.** Work with an advocate or attorney on whether and how to pursue one.
- **Change routines that were predictable.** Work schedules, gym schedules, shopping schedules, children's pickup schedules.
- **Monitor for digital stalking.** Check devices for tracking software. Change passwords on every account. Assume that anything the person causing harm previously had access to may still be accessible.
- **Expect emotional and trauma symptoms.** The safety allows the processing that was not possible before. This is painful but it is also progress — it is the beginning of recovery, not a setback.
- **Maintain contact with advocacy.** Safety planning is not complete when you leave; it continues into Phase 3 and beyond.

### If you cannot leave

Some people, for legitimate reasons, cannot leave their situation in the foreseeable future. Economic dependency, immigration status, children, disability, caregiving responsibilities, and safety risks can all produce this situation. The research does not moralize about this; it addresses how to plan safely under these conditions.

- **Phase 1 safety planning is for you.** The entire phase is specifically for people who are not leaving in the short term.
- **Build resources slowly over long timescales.** Months and years, if that is what it takes. Incremental preparation is legitimate.
- **Regular check-ins with advocacy.** Ongoing advocate contact is a resource that compounds over time.
- **Do not let the long timescale justify no planning.** Even if you cannot leave for a year, plan for what is possible in that year. Small improvements compound.
- **Know what would change your calculation.** If specific events happened, would your answer about leaving change? Having pre-identified trigger events for reassessment prevents drift.

### If you are not sure whether safety planning applies to you

- **Call an advocate for a consultation.** You do not have to commit to anything. A conversation with NDVH can clarify whether your situation warrants safety planning.
- **Apply the [Safety Assessment](manipulation-hub-safety-assessment.md) framework.** If any lethality indicators are present, safety planning applies.
- **Err on the side of planning.** Preparing for something you may not need costs time and attention. Not preparing for something you do need costs much more. The asymmetry favors planning.

## 8. What This Page Cannot Do

- **Replace an advocate.** This cannot be repeated enough. The research identifies advocacy as the active ingredient in safety planning effectiveness. A plan made without an advocate is substantially weaker than one made with an advocate, even if the written content is identical. The difference is in the calibration, the jurisdiction-specific knowledge, and the ongoing relationship.
- **Tell you when to leave.** That decision is yours, informed by the [Can This Be Repaired?](manipulation-hub-can-this-be-repaired.md) framework and by your specific circumstances.
- **Calibrate your specific risk.** The Danger Assessment requires trained administration. General awareness of risk factors is not the same as calibrated risk assessment.
- **Address every possible situation.** Coercive control without physical violence, workplace stalking, familial abuse of adult children, elder abuse, and many other situations involve safety planning with different specifics than the IPV model this page is built on. The general principles apply; the specific tactics may not.
- **Guarantee safety if followed.** Safety planning reduces risk. It does not eliminate it. Even excellent safety planning paired with strong advocacy and good execution can fail. This is a limitation of the world, not of this page.

## 9. Key Takeaways

1. **Safety planning is most effective when paired with advocacy.** Sullivan & Bybee (1999) RCT evidence is clear. If safety planning applies to your situation, contact a trained advocate — NDVH (1-800-799-7233 in the US) or the equivalent. This is non-negotiable.

2. **"Just leave" is empirically unsafe.** 55.2% of IPV femicide victims were estranged or had separated. Safety planning exists to address this.

3. **The three phases are not interchangeable.** In-relationship, preparing to leave, and after leaving each have different considerations. A plan that addresses only one phase is incomplete.

4. **Lethality indicators change the calculation.** Prior strangulation, weapon access, threats to kill, and escalation over time all indicate elevated risk. In these situations, advocacy is essential rather than optional.

5. **Autism-specific adaptations exist and matter.** The 2024 pilot RCT provides the best autism-specific safety planning evidence: plan when not in distress, involve a trusted supporter, use concrete rather than abstract guidance, include sensory considerations, pre-script communications, and account for processing time.

6. **The emergency bag is a concrete tool that makes rapid exit possible.** Documents, financials, keys, clothing, medications, and personal items in a location the person causing harm cannot access.

7. **Digital security is part of safety planning.** Monitored devices, shared accounts, and synced cloud storage can defeat the plan before it executes. Personal accounts they do not know about are one of the most protective moves you can make.

8. **The post-separation period is the highest-risk window.** Phase 3 planning is not optional after exit; stalking and retaliation are common and documented.

9. **Staying is a legitimate choice.** Phase 1 safety planning is for people who are not leaving in the short term. Planning under constraints is still planning.

10. **You are allowed to contact an advocate before you are "in crisis."** The best time to build a safety plan is when you are calm and resourced, not when the emergency has already begun.
