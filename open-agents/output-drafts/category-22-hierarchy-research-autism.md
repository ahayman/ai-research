## 1. HIERARCHY PROCESSING DIFFERENCES IN AUTISM

### 1.1 The "Hierarchy Blindness" Framing: Community Term, Research Nuance

"Hierarchy blindness" is a term used widely within the autistic community to describe the common autistic experience of not intuitively perceiving, processing, or responding to social hierarchies the way neurotypical individuals do. The term appears across autistic-authored blogs, forums, and advocacy literature, including The Articulate Autistic, The Autistic Boss, NeuroClastic, Wrong Planet forums, and various Medium posts by autistic writers.

**Important caveat about the term**: The community-knowledge label "blindness" can be misleading. The strongest empirical work on autistic rank recognition (Ogawa et al. 2019, covered in §1.2) actually found that autistic children showed *more robust* rank recognition than typically developing peers — but via different neural pathways, relying more on concrete physical features than on implicit social-contextual cues. What the community calls "hierarchy blindness" is more accurately described as a difference in which channels carry the hierarchy signal, combined with reduced automatic weighting of the specific implicit cues non-autistic people use. This hub uses the community term for its experiential clarity while flagging the empirical nuance.

Jaime A. Heidel (The Articulate Autistic) frames it directly: "We have no intuitive concept of social hierarchy." This is described not as a cognitive incapacity but as the absence of an automatic, implicit processing channel. The intuitive concept of social hierarchy that neurotypical people seem to learn as children -- absorbed through social observation, environmental immersion, and implicit pattern extraction -- does not appear to develop the same way in autistic individuals. Instead, many autistic people need to learn the concept of hierarchy *explicitly*, by being directly taught.

Graham Webb (Medium, "Autism from the Inside") describes hierarchy blindness as a core autistic experience that shapes workplace interactions, social relationships, and encounters with authority throughout the lifespan.

The Neurodivergent Brain characterizes it as a fundamental orientation: autistic individuals tend to see other people as equals -- as peers -- and sometimes get into trouble for not showing enough deference to those designated as "superiors."

**Key distinction**: Hierarchy blindness does not mean autistic people *cannot understand* hierarchy. It means they do not *automatically detect and respond to* the implicit signals that communicate hierarchical position. This is a processing difference, not a comprehension failure.

### 1.2 Research: Atypical Social Rank Recognition in ASD

**Ogawa, S., Iriguchi, M., Lee, Y.A. et al. (2019). "Atypical Social Rank Recognition in Autism Spectrum Disorder." *Scientific Reports*, 9, 15657.**
- DOI: https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-019-52211-8
- PMC: PMC6821924

This study provides crucial empirical evidence for how social rank recognition differs in autism. Key findings:

1. **ASD children showed MORE robust social rank recognition than typically developing (TD) children**, not less -- but their recognition operated through atypical neural pathways.

2. **Study design**: 11 ASD children (ages 7-19) and 10 TD children (ages 11-16) were shown images depicting social rank through body language/gestures, physical characteristics (size differences), and conflicting signals between the two.

3. **The conflict condition is critical**: When social context cues (e.g., body language of dominance) conflicted with physical size cues, ASD children showed significantly higher difference-of-rating scores than TD children. This means ASD children were *more* decisively identifying rank in ambiguous situations.

4. **The compensatory mechanism**: In TD children, social rank recognition via social contexts was NOT correlated with recognition via physical characteristics -- these operated as separate neural systems. In ASD children, the two WERE correlated, suggesting ASD children developed a compensatory strategy: they processed social rank cues through the system that normally processes physical size differences.

5. **Interpretation**: The systems for processing social information (facial expressions, body language, social context) and physical characteristics (size, spatial position) appear to operate independently in TD individuals and can compete with each other when signals conflict. In ASD, deficits in the social information processing system may lead to compensatory reliance on the physical characteristics processing system -- meaning autistic people may analyze hierarchy through more concrete, observable features rather than subtle social-contextual cues.

**What this means for pragmatics**: Autistic individuals may need to process hierarchy through explicit, observable signals (titles, organizational charts, physical positioning, concrete status markers) rather than through the implicit social-contextual channels (tone of voice, deference patterns, subtle body language shifts) that neurotypical people use automatically.

### 1.3 The Detection Problem: Implicit, Multi-Channel, Fast

Social hierarchy in neurotypical interaction is communicated through an extraordinarily complex, multi-channel signaling system that operates below conscious awareness:

**Vocal channels**: Lower-status speakers use more hedging ("maybe," "I think," "sort of"), rising intonation on statements (seeking validation), softer volume, and more hesitations. Higher-status speakers use declarative intonation, greater volume, fewer hedges, and more interruptions.

**Body language channels**: Higher-status individuals take up more physical space, maintain more sustained eye contact, initiate touch, position themselves centrally, gesture more expansively. Lower-status individuals orient their bodies toward higher-status people, yield space, break eye contact first, and mirror the higher-status person's emotional displays.

**Spatial and environmental channels**: Office size, floor level, proximity to windows/corners, furniture quality, parking spots, seating position at tables, who enters rooms first or last, who stands while others sit.

**Behavioral channels**: Who speaks first in meetings, who interrupts whom, who is addressed by first name vs. title, who makes the final decision, who defers to whom, patterns of turn-taking, who asks questions vs. who provides answers.

**Temporal channels**: Whose time is treated as more valuable, who waits for whom, who sets the meeting schedule, response time expectations (immediate for messages from superiors, delayed acceptable from superiors).

For neurotypical individuals, these signals are processed automatically, in parallel, in real time, from early childhood. The processing is fast, intuitive, and largely unconscious -- people "just know" who is in charge, who defers to whom, and where they fit.

For autistic individuals, who tend to process information more deliberately and sequentially, and who may miss or misweight individual channels (particularly the fast, context-dependent social ones), this multi-channel real-time processing can fail entirely -- not because they cannot understand hierarchy, but because the detection system requires exactly the kind of fast, implicit, multi-channel social processing that autism makes difficult.

### 1.4 The Intuition Gap

NeuroClastic's article "Autism and Responding to Authority" provides a particularly vivid description of the gap:

> "When presented with an authority figure, autistic people seek out what makes us the same, obliterating the arbitrary (to us at least) difference of their 'authority.' We automatically seek to be on a level with *everyone* we meet. This is subconscious and not controllable, unless we really need to."

This describes an automatic *leveling* process -- the autistic default orientation is egalitarian, treating all interlocutors as peers. The neurotypical default orientation is *rank-sensitive*, automatically adjusting communication based on perceived relative status.

NT children absorb hierarchy through years of social observation:
- Watching how parents speak differently to their boss vs. their friend
- Observing classroom dynamics (teacher speaks differently than students)
- Playground social learning (popular kids, unpopular kids, who defers to whom)
- Media consumption (characters display status through behavior)

Autistic children, who may process social observation differently (reduced attention to faces, reduced implicit social learning, different attentional priorities), may not extract these hierarchical patterns during the critical developmental windows when NT children absorb them effortlessly.

### 1.5 The Value Mismatch

Beyond detection differences, there is a genuine value mismatch. Many autistic individuals, even when they *understand* hierarchy intellectually, find it philosophically problematic:

NeuroClastic frames this explicitly: "Many Autistic people have great difficulties thinking of the world in hierarchical ways... this inability to think hierarchically turns out to be the ability to think and live entirely relationally."

This is described as "a reflection of innate human collaborative cultural capabilities in combination with a much reduced capacity for maintaining cognitive dissonance on an ongoing basis, which in turn can be traced to uncommon sensitivity profiles that fall outside the bell curve of hypernormativity."

The Autistic Boss describes "one of the most striking aspects of the autistic outlook" as "the intrinsic value placed on fairness and integrity, often leading to a disregard for established social hierarchies. This isn't about challenging the status quo for rebellion but stems from a fundamental belief in equality and justice."

The "I See Autistic People" blog notes that autistic individuals will respect authority figures who have earned respect through competence, morality, or genuine expertise -- but will not automatically defer to positional authority alone. "They will bow to greater expertise, experience, morality, and creativity, but not just to authority alone."

### 1.6 Research on Reduced Interest in Competing for Social Status

**Caldwell-Harris, C.L. & Schwartz, A.M. (2024). "Listening to autistic voices regarding competing for social status." *Autism: International Journal of Research and Practice*.**
- DOI: https://doi.org/10.1177/13623613231217057
- PubMed: 38142445

This study and its companion paper are landmark investigations:

**Caldwell-Harris, C.L. (2023). "Why Autistic Sociality Is Different: Reduced Interest in Competing for Social Status." *OUGHT: The Journal of Autistic Culture*, 5(1), Article 12. Grand Valley State University.**
- Available: https://scholarworks.gvsu.edu/ought/vol5/iss1/12/

Key findings across both papers:

1. **Autistic people find status-seeking illogical** and prefer egalitarian relationships.

2. **Multiple evidence sources converge**: The researchers reviewed writings by autistic people, including advice books, memoirs, book reviews, online discussion posts, and mission statements of autistic-led organizations. The consistency of themes across all these sources was striking.

3. **Autistic individuals are less invested in reputation management**, more likely to resist conformity, favor moral systems that reject authority, and prefer egalitarian interactions.

4. **Three proposed mechanisms for reduced status-seeking**:
   - **Reduced social motivation** (the traditional deficit framing, which the authors partially reject)
   - **Monotropism and special interests**: Monotropic attention (deep focus on domains of high interest) is not ideal for extracting the fuzzy, diffuse social regularities needed for status competition
   - **Deliberative cognitive style**: Where neurotypical people process social status intuitively and rapidly, autistic individuals process it deliberately and consciously -- and when you subject status competition to deliberate analysis, it often looks irrational

5. **The authors explicitly reject a pure deficit model**: They acknowledge that social misunderstandings interfere in daily life but argue that reduced status-seeking is a genuine characteristic of autistic sociality, not merely a failure to succeed at something all humans want.

### 1.7 Insensitivity to Social Reputation

**Izuma, K., Matsumoto, K., Camerer, C.F., & Adolphs, R. (2011). "Insensitivity to social reputation in autism." *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences (PNAS)*, 108(42), 17302-17307.**
- DOI: https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1107038108
- PMC: PMC3198313

This PNAS study demonstrated a specific mechanism:

1. **Experimental design**: Participants made real charitable donations both when observed and when alone.
2. **NT controls donated significantly more when being observed** -- a classic reputation management behavior.
3. **Autistic participants were NOT influenced by the presence of an observer** in their donation behavior.
4. **Critical control**: Both groups performed better on a continuous performance task (non-social) when observed, demonstrating that autistic participants *were* responsive to being observed in general -- they just weren't engaging in reputation-enhancing behavior.
5. **Conclusion**: The results suggest autistic individuals lack the automatic drive to manage how others perceive them -- not a general insensitivity to observation, but a specific insensitivity to *social reputation effects*.

**Cage, E., Pellicano, E., Shah, P., & Bird, G. (2013). "Reputation management: evidence for ability but reduced propensity in autism." *Autism Research*, 6(5), 433-442.**
- DOI: https://doi.org/10.1002/aur.1313
- PubMed: 23836550

This follow-up study refined the picture:

1. Twenty neurotypical and 19 autistic adults donated to charity both alone and when observed.
2. When given a specific reason to expect reciprocal behavior from the observer ("motivation condition"), **both groups donated more when watched** -- though the effect was significantly smaller in autistic participants.
3. **Key finding**: Autistic individuals *have the ability* to think about reputation and engage in reputation management -- they just have a reduced *propensity* to do so spontaneously.
4. **The ability vs. propensity distinction** is crucial: it means hierarchy and reputation sensitivity can be learned and deployed strategically, even though they don't operate automatically.

## 2. THE DIRECTNESS TRAP

### 2.1 How Autistic Directness Violates Implicit Pragmatic Norms

In neurotypical communication, speakers are expected to calibrate their directness based on the relative status of their interlocutor. The pragmatic rules (largely implicit) include:

**When speaking to someone of higher status, NT norms expect**:
- More hedging: "I was wondering if maybe we could consider..." rather than "We should do X"
- More indirect requests: "Do you think it might be possible to..." rather than "I need you to..."
- Softened disagreement: "That's an interesting approach, but I was thinking..." rather than "That won't work because..."
- Deference markers: Use of titles, formal register, asking permission to speak, yielding the floor
- Strategic complimenting: Acknowledging the authority figure's expertise before offering alternatives
- Face-saving indirectness: Framing corrections as questions ("Could it be that...?") rather than statements ("That's wrong")

**When an autistic individual speaks to their boss the same way they speak to a peer, the following violations occur simultaneously**:
- No hedging (directness reads as presumption)
- No deference markers (reads as not recognizing authority)
- Corrections stated as facts (reads as challenging competence)
- Questions asked without softening (reads as demanding or interrogating)
- Tone not adjusted for status (reads as disrespectful)
- Equal turn-taking assumed (reads as not knowing one's place)

### 2.2 The "Argue" Misperception

The Articulate Autistic's article "Why Does My Autistic Employee Argue With Me?" addresses this directly:

- What managers perceive as "arguing" is typically an autistic employee seeking clarification
- Questions, corrections, and "buts" serve a fundamentally different function for autistic communicators: they are information-seeking and accuracy-ensuring behaviors, not challenges to authority
- The autistic employee has "no intent to challenge authority or question leadership ability -- what they need and are asking for is more information"
- The disconnect occurs because neurotypical communication norms treat questioning from a subordinate as inherently confrontational, while autistic communication norms treat questioning as a neutral information-exchange behavior

### 2.3 Specialisterne's Analysis: "Chain of Command May Be Confusing"

Specialisterne USA's detailed analysis of chain-of-command confusion identifies specific failure points:

1. **The invisible hierarchy**: "For many autistic people, there is no inherent difference between the CEO of a company and its janitor" -- they treat everyone the same regardless of social status because some autistic people aren't even aware that there IS a chain of command.

2. **The "going over the boss's head" scenario**: An autistic employee might email the Board of Directors with a question they couldn't get answered by their boss -- not as insubordination, but because "if never faced with such a situation before, they may have no idea that it's considered inappropriate to go over their boss's head when they need help."

3. **The explicit instruction solution**: Managers should clearly state: (a) who the employee reports to, (b) what to do if that person is unavailable, (c) what channels exist for concerns or complaints, (d) what conduct expectations exist beyond job duties. None of this should be left implicit.

### 2.4 The Correction Impulse

The Articulate Autistic's companion piece, "Why Is My Autistic Employee So Rude?", identifies the correction impulse as a major friction point:

- The autistic brain is "wired to think literally and prioritize accurate information exchange"
- When someone makes an error, "the inconsistency hits their brain like an electric jolt"
- The impulse to correct is driven by "the need to dispel anxiety" caused by encountering incorrect information
- This impulse may cause the autistic person to "interrupt with a hasty correction" -- which in hierarchical contexts reads as insubordination rather than helpfulness

### 2.5 Consequences: Perceived as Insubordinate, Arrogant, or Challenging

The consequences of hierarchy-blind directness are severe and well-documented:

**Employment termination data**:

**Pezzimenti, F., Durrani, S. R., Zheng, S., Adams, R. E., Bishop, S. L., & Taylor, J. L. (2023). "Perspectives on Employer-Initiated Terminations Among Young Adults on the Autism Spectrum." *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*. PMC9838300.**

- From 315 autistic individuals ages 18-35, 93 (29.5%) reported having been terminated from a job
- Social difficulties were the second most common reason for termination
- One participant reported: "On my first day, I apparently talked down to one of my supervisors when I really just had a question in regard to how to do my job. I made him cry and was fired."
- Many terminations appeared to reflect "poor fit of the job to the individual's skills and abilities" rather than pervasive deficits

**Workplace discrimination**:
A case was reported where a company fired a worker with autism over "unprofessional communication" -- the worker's direct communication style was interpreted as violating professional norms, despite being a manifestation of autism (Source: UNA1.org).

The Specialisterne article "Why Being Fired as an Autistic Person Has a More Harmful Impact" describes the cascading effects: autistic individuals who are fired for social reasons often develop workplace PTSD, increased masking behaviors, and a cycle of anxiety that makes future employment even more difficult.

## 3. THE COMPETENCE PARADOX

### 3.1 When Expertise Meets Hierarchy Norms

There is a specific pragmatic norm in most workplaces: **competence should be demonstrated through work, not declared through speech**. The unwritten rules include:

- Let your work speak for itself
- Display competence indirectly (let others notice your expertise)
- Frame contributions modestly ("I think this might work" rather than "This is the correct approach")
- Avoid correcting higher-status individuals publicly
- Present qualifications in response to questions, not proactively
- Attribute success to the team or to the guidance of superiors

Autistic individuals who ARE genuinely expert may violate every one of these norms:

### 3.2 Correcting Others Publicly

The autistic impulse to correct inaccurate information does not automatically calibrate for audience, context, or the relative status of the person being corrected. When a CEO makes a factual error in a company-wide meeting, the autistic employee who raises their hand to correct it is:
- **From their perspective**: Being helpful by ensuring accurate information is shared
- **From the NT social perspective**: Publicly humiliating a superior, challenging authority, threatening the social order

The "I See Autistic People" blog describes this as: autistic individuals are "human lie-detectors who can spot a liar nine times out of ten despite many having limited social skills." The ability to detect errors, combined with the impulse to correct them, combined with hierarchy blindness, creates a specific and recurring friction point.

### 3.3 Stating Qualifications Directly

When asked about their knowledge, autistic individuals may respond with precise, factual statements about their qualifications: "I have a PhD in this field and have published twelve papers on this topic." In NT pragmatic norms, this reads as arrogant self-promotion. The expected NT response is more like: "Well, I've done some work in this area" (understatement) or "I was fortunate to be involved in some research on this" (credit deflection).

Suffolk Family Carers published a piece titled "I'm Autistic NOT Arrogant" addressing this gap directly: the same behavior that reads as factual self-description to the autistic person reads as status-claiming to the neurotypical observer.

Research on perception of autistic behavior (Perceptions of Behaviors Associated with ASD, MDPI) found that when people understand the autistic diagnosis, they perceive the same behaviors more positively -- rated higher on warmth and competence. The same correction, the same direct statement, the same factual claim about qualifications reads as arrogant without context and competent with it.

### 3.4 The Gap Between "I'm Right and Being Helpful" and "They Think I'm Arrogant"

This gap is one of the most painful aspects of autistic workplace experience. Several factors converge:

1. **Autistic individuals often ARE right** -- high attention to detail, deep knowledge of special interest areas, and precise information processing mean their corrections are frequently factually accurate
2. **The social cost of being right is invisible to them** -- because they don't automatically process the hierarchical implications of correcting someone
3. **NT observers interpret the behavior through a status lens** -- "Who does this person think they are?" rather than "Is this person correct?"
4. **The feedback is often delayed and indirect** -- the autistic person may not realize their correction was socially costly until they receive a poor performance review or are terminated

### 3.5 When Autistic Expertise Is Valued vs. Creates Friction

**Valued in**:
- Technical roles with clear metrics (the code works or it doesn't)
- Environments that explicitly prioritize accuracy over social harmony
- Flat organizations where expertise is the primary currency
- Crisis situations where being right matters more than being diplomatic
- Written communication (where tone is absent and content dominates)
- Peer-to-peer interactions (where hierarchy is absent or minimal)

**Creates friction in**:
- Meetings with mixed hierarchy levels
- Client-facing roles where relationship management is paramount
- Traditional hierarchies where "speaking up" requires navigating chain of command
- Situations where the person being corrected is higher-status
- Team environments where social cohesion is prioritized over accuracy
- Contexts where "how you say it" matters as much as "what you say"

## 4. PDA AND AUTHORITY

### 4.1 Pathological/Persistent Demand Avoidance as a Distinct Profile

PDA (Pathological Demand Avoidance, or as increasingly reframed: Persistent Drive for Autonomy / Pervasive Drive for Autonomy) is a behavioral profile within the autism spectrum characterized by:

- An intense, anxiety-driven resistance to demands and expectations
- A strong need for autonomy and control
- Use of social strategies (charm, negotiation, distraction) to avoid demands
- Rapid mood changes
- Surface-level sociability that can mask deeper difficulties

**Diagnostic status**: PDA has never been included in the DSM or ICD and therefore has no formal diagnostic criteria. However, it is increasingly recognized in clinical practice, particularly in the UK. During autism assessment, a person can be identified as having a "PDA profile" or "demand avoidance traits."

Sources: Autism BC ("Pathological Demand Avoidance Explained"), PDA Society (UK). The original PDA formulation comes from Newson, Le Maréchal & David (2003) *Archives of Disease in Childhood* 88(7):595-600. The Extreme Demand Avoidance Questionnaire (EDA-Q) is a separate instrument developed by O'Nions, Christie, Gould, Viding & Happé (2014) *Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry* (PMC4820467). These two sources are sometimes conflated in summary writing; they are distinct contributions to the PDA literature.

### 4.2 How Demand Avoidance Interacts with Hierarchical Expectations

The PDA interaction with hierarchy is qualitatively different from "standard" autistic hierarchy blindness:

**Standard autistic hierarchy experience**: "I don't automatically detect hierarchy, so I treat everyone as a peer. When I learn hierarchy exists, I can adapt (with effort)."

**PDA hierarchy experience**: "I detect hierarchy (sometimes hyper-detect it), but my nervous system experiences hierarchical demands as threats to my autonomy, triggering fight-flight-freeze responses."

Key differences:
1. **PDA individuals may be HYPER-aware of social hierarchies** -- specifically because they perceive those hierarchies as threats to their autonomy (Neurodivergent Insights)
2. **The avoidance is not pragmatic blindness** -- it is an anxiety-driven nervous system response
3. **Demands from authority figures are processed as threats**: "For the PDA nervous system, threats appear to trigger a response similar to what other people feel when physically trapped or restrained" (Neurodivergent Insights)
4. **The response is automatic and involuntary**: The body goes into fight-or-flight in response to a demand, and the person becomes immobilized -- this is not a choice or a value judgment

### 4.3 When Authority Response Is Driven by Anxiety vs. Pragmatic Blindness

This distinction is crucial for practical strategies:

**Pragmatic blindness** (hierarchy not detected):
- Can be addressed with explicit information: "This is your supervisor. Here is the chain of command."
- Response improves with education and practice
- The autistic person can learn to mask/perform deference strategically
- Not driven by emotional distress

**PDA-driven authority resistance** (hierarchy detected but experienced as threat):
- Explicit information about hierarchy may INCREASE anxiety
- Traditional authority approaches (direct commands, rigid expectations) worsen the situation
- Requires reducing the *perception of threat*, not increasing awareness of hierarchy
- Support involves: offering choices, using indirect/collaborative language, reducing perceived power differential, building trust before making demands

### 4.4 The Distinction: Not RECOGNIZING vs. Not ACCEPTING Hierarchy

There is also a third category that is neither blindness nor PDA but principled rejection:

- **Not recognizing**: Does not detect hierarchical signals (hierarchy blindness proper)
- **Not accepting (PDA)**: Detects hierarchy but experiences it as a threat to autonomy (anxiety-driven)
- **Not accepting (principled)**: Detects hierarchy, understands the social expectations, but finds them morally objectionable and refuses to comply on ethical grounds

The third category -- principled rejection of unearned authority -- is related to the autistic moral profile discussed below and represents a conscious value judgment rather than a processing difference or an anxiety response.

## 5. THE EGALITARIAN PREFERENCE

### 5.1 Research on Autistic Egalitarian Orientation

Multiple converging lines of evidence support an autistic preference for egalitarian interactions:

**Caldwell-Harris & Schwartz (2024)**: Autistic people "find status-seeking illogical and prefer egalitarian relationships."

**NeuroClastic**: "Many Autistic people have great difficulties to think of the world in hierarchical ways. From what we know about our evolutionary path as humans, this is a reflection of innate human collaborative cultural capabilities."

**Autistic-led organizations**: The growing worldwide interest in egalitarian NeurodiVenture worker cooperatives and the Ecologies of Care support model demonstrates this preference translating into institutional design.

**Animal models**: Research on VPA-treated rats (an autism animal model) found that while typical rats formed social hierarchies and dominant individuals guarded limited resources, VPA-treated rats did not demonstrate hierarchical or guarding behavior (Research Outreach, "Autism spectrum disorder and social hierarchy in rats").

### 5.2 Moral Foundations Research

**Moral Foundations Theory** (Jonathan Haidt) provides a framework for understanding the autistic relationship to authority:

Haidt's theory posits five moral foundations grouped into two clusters:
- **Individualizing foundations**: Care, Fairness
- **Binding foundations**: Loyalty, Authority, Sanctity

**Key research findings in autism**:

**Dempsey et al. (2020). "Moral foundations theory in autism spectrum disorder: A qualitative investigation." *Autism*, 24(8).**
- DOI: https://doi.org/10.1177/1362361320939331

**Greenberg et al. et al. (2024). "Moral foundations in autistic people and people with systemizing minds." *Molecular Autism*. PMC11092219.**

**Dempsey, Moore, Johnson, Stewart & Smith (2022). "Moral Foundations Theory Among Autistic and Neurotypical Children." *Frontiers in Psychology*. PMC8795511.**

Findings across these studies:
1. **Autistic people score HIGHER on Fairness** than neurotypical controls -- particularly autistic females
2. **Autistic people score LOWER on binding foundations**: Loyalty, Authority, Sanctity
3. Autistic people were "more utilitarian and principle-based" in moral decision-making tasks
4. Autistic children consistently rejected unequal rewards even when it meant losing out themselves (inequity aversion)
5. Unlike neurotypical peers who often accept unfairness if it benefits them, autistic children adhered rigidly to fairness norms

**The Authority foundation specifically**: Haidt defines this as respect for tradition and legitimate authority. Autistic individuals consistently score lower on this foundation, suggesting a reduced moral weight placed on authority as a value *in itself*.

### 5.3 Justice Sensitivity and "Radical Fairness"

The Autistic Boss describes autism and a sense of justice:

"When autistic individuals insist on fairness, consistency or the correction of wrongs, it is often misunderstood as moral rigidity or a desire to control others. In reality, this insistence is not about self-righteousness or moral superiority -- it is an expression of a different kind of moral grammar."

The TSPI article "Fairness Is Not Optional" describes this as a cognitive architecture where "justice, fairness and truth are not negotiable ideals within this framework but are structural necessities, much like mathematical rules or physical laws. When others violate these principles without explanation or correction, it creates an unresolvable contradiction in how the world is understood."

### 5.4 When Egalitarianism Is a Strength

- **Collaborative environments**: Autistic tendency to treat everyone as equals fosters genuine collaboration without the status games that can undermine teamwork
- **Flat organizations**: Startups, cooperatives, and Scandinavian-style flat hierarchies align naturally with autistic social orientation
- **Innovation contexts**: Status hierarchies suppress dissent; autistic willingness to question anyone can drive innovation
- **Ethical leadership**: The principled resistance to authority can identify and challenge genuine ethical violations that hierarchy-respecting employees overlook
- **Mentoring relationships**: Treating a junior colleague as an equal can empower and develop talent
- **Cross-functional teams**: Hierarchy blindness means autistic team members may communicate more effectively across departmental silos

### 5.5 When Egalitarianism Creates Friction

- **Traditional corporate hierarchies**: "The way we've always done things" clashes with the autistic question "But why?"
- **Military or paramilitary organizations**: Chain of command is non-negotiable
- **Formal institutions** (academia, government, legal): Procedural hierarchy has functional purposes
- **Client relationships**: Clients expect deference even when the autistic professional has superior expertise
- **Performance reviews**: Managers may interpret lack of deference as "attitude problems"
- **Cultural contexts with high power distance**: Interactions with hierarchical cultures (many East Asian, Middle Eastern, and Latin American business cultures) can create acute friction

### 5.6 The Moral Dimension: Principled, Not Defiant

This is perhaps the most important reframe: autistic resistance to hierarchy is often *morally principled*, not socially defiant.

- When an autistic person questions a supervisor's decision, they may be applying consistent moral reasoning about fairness, not trying to undermine authority
- When they refuse to comply with a directive they find unethical, they are prioritizing their moral architecture (where fairness is structural, not optional) over social compliance
- When they treat a janitor and a CEO identically, they are enacting a genuine belief in human equality, not failing to notice status

The NeuroClastic framing is instructive: "Because autistic people do not see identity as a station on a collective identity's hierarchy, they do not automatically assign value to mainstream authority and social rank -- which is immediately regarded as disrespectful by those who benefit from those hierarchies being in place."

## 6. THE DOUBLE EMPATHY PERSPECTIVE

### 6.1 Milton's Double Empathy Problem Applied to Hierarchy

**Milton, D. (2012). "On the ontological status of autism: the 'double empathy problem.'" *Disability & Society*, 27(6), 883-887.**

The double empathy problem, coined by autistic autism researcher Damian Milton, proposes that communication difficulties between autistic and non-autistic people arise from mutual misunderstanding, not from a one-directional deficit in autistic individuals.

Applied to hierarchy:
1. **NT expectation**: Lower-status individuals should automatically adjust their communication (more hedging, more deference, softer tone). This is treated as "normal" and "correct."
2. **Autistic orientation**: All interlocutors should be treated as equals. Communication should be direct and honest regardless of status.
3. **The double empathy failure**: NT individuals fail to understand that the autistic person is not being *rude* -- they are operating from a different social framework. The autistic person fails to understand that the NT person experiences directness from a subordinate as a *status violation* rather than neutral communication.

**Workplace data supporting this**:

**Szechy, K. A., Turk, P. D., & O'Donnell, L. A. (2024). "Autism and employment challenges: The double empathy problem and perceptions of an autistic employee in the workplace." *Autism in Adulthood*, 6(2), 205-217. PMC11317796.**

- Autistic people who read workplace scenarios were more likely to accurately understand autistic employees (50.7% accurate) compared with non-autistic people (31.2% accurate)
- The phenomenon of misunderstanding is bilateral: autistic adults often misinterpret employer intentions, and employers often misinterpret autistic worker intentions
- Employers frequently falsely attribute selfish or defiant intentions to behaviors that are neurologically driven

### 6.2 Are NT Hierarchical Expectations Culturally Specific?

**Hofstede's Power Distance Index** demonstrates that hierarchical deference is far from universal:

- **Low Power Distance cultures** (Denmark, Israel, Ireland, Norway, Sweden, Netherlands): Value equality, minimize status differences, encourage questioning authority, decisions are made collectively. In Sweden, over 80% of companies feature flat hierarchies where leaders facilitate rather than command.
- **High Power Distance cultures** (Malaysia, Philippines, China, India): Social hierarchies deeply embedded, deference to authority expected, questioning superiors discouraged.

Historical note: "The countries that fell under the influence of the Vikings are some of the most consensual and egalitarian countries in the world, and the Vikings themselves managed in tribe by consensus. There are historical stories about Southern Europeans coming to Scandinavia to negotiate and having to come home early because they weren't able to figure out who was in charge."

**The implication**: The degree of hierarchical deference expected in communication is *culturally variable*, not a universal human norm. What reads as "insubordinate" in a high-power-distance workplace might read as "appropriately direct" in a Scandinavian workplace.

### 6.3 When Hierarchical Deference Is Excessive or Unnecessary

Some hierarchy-driven communication patterns serve no functional purpose:

- **Excessive hedging wastes time**: "I was just wondering if maybe you might consider the possibility that we could perhaps..." vs. "I have an idea for improving this."
- **Deference can suppress important information**: Employees who are afraid to correct a superior may allow errors to propagate
- **Status performance costs cognitive resources**: Both the performer and the audience expend mental energy on status management rather than task completion
- **Groupthink is enabled by hierarchy**: When lower-status people defer to higher-status people, the group converges on the dominant view regardless of its quality

### 6.4 Whether Autistic Directness Is More Honest and Efficient

A case can be made that autistic communication patterns, in many contexts, are *superior* to hierarchy-inflected communication:

- **More honest**: No strategic flattery, no face-saving indirectness that obscures real problems
- **More efficient**: Fewer words to reach the point, less cognitive overhead for encoding and decoding status markers
- **More equitable**: Everyone's ideas evaluated on merit rather than status
- **More accurate**: Errors corrected promptly regardless of who made them
- **More inclusive**: New team members treated as equals rather than having to "earn" the right to speak

The 2025 systematic review of autistic interpersonal relations found that "autistic-autistic interactions were generally associated with better quality of life (e.g., mental health and emotional well-being) across various domains" -- suggesting that the autistic communication style, including its directness and egalitarianism, works well when it is the shared norm.

## 7. PRACTICAL STRATEGIES

### 7.1 How to Detect Hierarchy in a New Environment

Since implicit processing of hierarchy may not be available, autistic individuals can use explicit, systematic observation:

**Observable hierarchy signals checklist**:

1. **Organizational chart**: Request or find the formal org chart on day one. This is the explicit map of who reports to whom.

2. **Physical space signals**:
   - Larger offices = higher status
   - Private offices > shared offices > open floor plan
   - Corner offices and window offices = higher status
   - Floor level (higher floors often = higher status)
   - Distance from the main entrance or elevator

3. **Meeting behavior**:
   - Who sits at the head of the table?
   - Who speaks first and last?
   - Who interrupts without consequence?
   - Who defers to whom? (Watch for patterns of "What do you think, [Name]?" directed upward)
   - Whose agenda items take priority?

4. **Communication patterns**:
   - Who uses first names vs. titles?
   - Who CCs whom on emails? (CC patterns reveal reporting lines)
   - Response time patterns (people respond faster to higher-status individuals)
   - Who sets meetings vs. who attends them?

5. **Decision-making patterns**:
   - Who has final say?
   - Who can override whom?
   - Who signs off on decisions?

6. **Social signals**:
   - Who is invited to which social events?
   - Seating arrangements at lunch
   - Who jokes with whom (peers joke more freely; cross-hierarchical humor is more cautious)

**The mentor strategy**: Ask a trusted colleague (or specifically request a workplace mentor) to explicitly map the hierarchy for you. Ask: "Who should I never go over? Who is it safe to approach directly? What are the unwritten rules about who talks to whom?"

### 7.2 How to Calibrate Pragmatics Once Hierarchy Is Detected

Once you've identified where people sit in the hierarchy relative to you, calibrate your language:

**Speaking to someone higher-status (your boss, their boss, clients)**:
- Add hedges: "I think..." "It might be worth considering..." "In my experience..."
- Frame corrections as questions: "Could it be that...?" "I'm curious whether..."
- Ask permission to share: "Would it be helpful if I shared what I've seen in this area?"
- Soften disagreement: "I see the logic in that approach. I wonder if we might also consider..."
- Acknowledge their authority: "You know this area better than I do, but..."

**Speaking to peers**:
- More natural directness is usually acceptable
- Corrections can be more direct but still benefit from framing: "I think the number might actually be..."
- Collaborative framing: "What if we..." rather than "We should..."

**Speaking to someone lower-status (if you're in a supervisory role)**:
- Be direct (they expect directives from you)
- But also be kind: explain reasoning, invite questions, express appreciation
- Autistic managers often naturally create psychologically safe environments because they treat everyone as equals

### 7.3 The "Default to More Deference" Rule

When uncertain about relative status, default to MORE deference rather than less:

- Use the more formal address until invited to do otherwise
- Hedge more rather than less
- Ask questions rather than make statements
- Let others speak first
- Observe before acting

**Why this works**: Over-deference is almost never punished (at worst, people think you're very polite). Under-deference can be career-ending. The asymmetry of consequences makes "default to deference" the pragmatically safe strategy.

### 7.4 When and How to Challenge Authority Appropriately

There ARE times when challenging authority is necessary and appropriate. The key is doing it in a way that achieves the goal without triggering hierarchy-defense mechanisms:

**The private conversation rule**: Challenge authority in private, never in public. Request a one-on-one meeting. Frame it as "I have some concerns I'd like to discuss" rather than raising the issue in a group setting.

**The question frame**: Instead of "You're wrong about X," ask "Can you help me understand the reasoning behind X? I'm seeing some data that seems to suggest a different conclusion."

**The "and" technique**: Instead of "but" (which negates what came before), use "and": "I understand the decision to proceed with Plan A, and I want to make sure we've considered the implications of..."

**The evidence approach**: Instead of "I think you're wrong," present evidence: "I found some data that might be relevant to this decision. Could we look at it together?"

**The timing strategy**: Raise concerns early in the decision process, not after a decision has been publicly announced. Once a superior has publicly committed to a course of action, changing their mind requires them to lose face.

### 7.5 Managing the Emotional Response to Arbitrary Hierarchy

For many autistic individuals, hierarchy feels not just confusing but actively wrong. This emotional response needs management strategies:

1. **Acknowledge the feeling**: "I find this hierarchy frustrating because I value fairness" is valid. The feeling doesn't need to be suppressed -- it needs to be managed so it doesn't drive impulsive behavior.

2. **Separate the moral from the pragmatic**: You can believe hierarchy is wrong AND still navigate it strategically. These are not contradictory. Playing the game doesn't mean endorsing it.

3. **Choose your battles**: Not every hierarchical expectation is worth fighting. Save your resistance for genuine ethical violations and let routine deference expectations pass without expending emotional energy.

4. **Find aligned environments**: Actively seek flat organizations, collaborative teams, and leaders who value directness. The environment matters enormously -- the same autistic communication style that gets you fired in a traditional hierarchy gets you promoted in a flat startup.

5. **Reframe masking as code-switching**: Rather than seeing hierarchical deference as "being fake," frame it as code-switching -- the same way a bilingual person shifts between languages depending on context. You're not changing who you are; you're translating your communication for a different audience.

6. **Build private spaces for authenticity**: Maintain relationships (with other autistic people, with trusted friends, with understanding colleagues) where you can communicate in your natural style without hierarchical filtering.

### 7.6 When Your Directness Is Valued and When It Needs Calibration

**Directness is usually valued when**:
- There's a safety issue (medical, engineering, operational)
- You've been explicitly asked for honest feedback
- You're in a technical review or code review
- The environment explicitly values candor (e.g., Ray Dalio's "radical transparency" at Bridgewater)
- You're communicating with other autistic people
- You're in writing (email, documentation, reports) where directness reads as clarity
- There's a genuine crisis requiring fast, accurate information

**Directness needs calibration when**:
- There are mixed hierarchy levels present (especially public settings)
- You're new to the organization (before you've built relational capital)
- The topic involves someone's ego, reputation, or public image
- You're dealing with clients or external stakeholders
- The organizational culture is explicitly hierarchical
- The person you're speaking to has demonstrated sensitivity to perceived challenges
- Social/political dynamics are in play (mergers, reorganizations, promotions)
