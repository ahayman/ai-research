# Detecting and Navigating Social Hierarchies: A Practical Guide

## For English Pragmatics -- Designed for Autistic Individuals

---

## Why This Guide Exists

Social hierarchies are present in virtually every human interaction, but they are rarely made explicit. Neurotypical individuals often detect and navigate them through intuition -- a fuzzy, automatic process that draws on pattern recognition built up from years of social immersion. For autistic individuals, this process may not be automatic. Research confirms that autistic adults frequently have difficulty "extrapolating a person's social standing because they will not piece together relevant information (e.g., getting talked over or cut-off, group members laughing at their jokes, etc.) and measure such information against the context" ([PMC, 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9977004/)).

The good news: hierarchy signals are **observable**. They are concrete, repeated, and learnable. This guide replaces intuition with a systematic checklist of things you can literally see and hear. No mind-reading required.

### What Hierarchy Actually Is

Before the checklist, a critical distinction. Research in evolutionary psychology identifies **two fundamental pathways** to social rank ([Cheng et al., 2013](https://www2.psych.ubc.ca/~henrich/pdfs/Cheng%20et%20al.%20(2013)%20-%20Two%20Ways%20to%20the%20Top.pdf)):

- **Dominance**: Status gained through force, intimidation, coercion, or control of resources. Others comply out of **fear**. The dominant person takes; others yield.
- **Prestige**: Status earned through demonstrated expertise, skill, generosity, or teaching ability. Others defer out of **respect and admiration**. The prestigious person is sought out; others approach willingly.

Most real-world hierarchies are a mix of both, but the distinction matters because:
- Dominance hierarchies feel threatening; prestige hierarchies feel collaborative.
- The same pragmatic strategies (hedging, deference) serve different emotional functions in each.
- Knowing which type you're in helps you calibrate your response more accurately.

A boss who leads through expertise operates a prestige hierarchy. A boss who leads through threat operates a dominance hierarchy. Your communication strategy should differ accordingly.

## SECTION 1: THE HIERARCHY DETECTION CHECKLIST

This is a systematic, observable-signals-based method for identifying hierarchy in any social situation. **No social intuition required -- only things you can see and hear.**

The core principle: **hierarchy is visible in who controls the conversational and physical space**. Research on institutional discourse shows "a distribution of roles and resources that is characteristically asymmetrical, with participant roles associated with rights and capacities to influence the activity" ([PMC, 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10282763/)).

### 1.1 Linguistic Signals (What You Can Hear)

#### WHO SPEAKS THE MOST?

**Signal**: The person who occupies the most airtime is often (not always) higher status.

**Why it works**: Research on power in conversation shows that higher-status individuals "control over the agenda of the interaction," often through "asymmetrical turn-taking systems that endow participants with unequal amounts of freedom in their talk" ([Cornell, Echoes of Power](https://www.cs.cornell.edu/~cristian//pdfs/echoes_of_power.pdf)).

**The exception**: In some prestige-based hierarchies, the highest-status person speaks *less* but more decisively. Google's research found that the best teams had roughly equal speaking time -- but the person who spoke least but most precisely often had the most influence. Watch for: does everyone go quiet when this person starts talking?

**Observation method**: In a meeting or group conversation, roughly estimate who has spoken the most after 10-15 minutes. Note whether others seem to be competing for the remaining time.

---

#### WHO INTRODUCES TOPICS?

**Signal**: The person who sets the conversational agenda -- who says "Let's talk about X" or who naturally steers the conversation to new subjects -- is likely higher status.

**Why it works**: Topic introduction is a form of conversational control. In institutional settings, the person who controls the agenda controls the interaction. In casual conversation, topic introduction signals social confidence and assumed authority.

**Observation method**: Notice who introduces new subjects vs. who responds to subjects others introduced. Count: in the last 30 minutes, who brought up the most new topics?

---

#### WHO HEDGES MORE?

**Signal**: The person who uses more hedges ("I think maybe...", "I'm not sure but...", "It could be that...", "sort of", "kind of") is likely lower status *relative to the person they're hedging toward*.

**Why it works**: Hedging is a pragmatic strategy for "mitigating the harshness or hostility of the force of one's actions" ([European Proceedings, 2018](https://www.europeanproceedings.com/article/10.15405/epsbs.2018.04.02.98)). When we hedge, we are softening our claim because we perceive risk in being too direct -- and that risk perception correlates with status differential. You hedge more when talking to someone who can penalize your directness.

**Common hedges to listen for**:
- Epistemic hedges: "I think," "I believe," "maybe," "perhaps," "it seems like"
- Approximators: "sort of," "kind of," "roughly," "around"
- Shields: "I could be wrong, but...," "This is just my opinion...," "I'm not the expert here, but..."
- Downtoners: "just," "a little," "slightly"

**Observation method**: Compare the hedging level of different speakers talking to each other. If Person A hedges when talking to Person B but not to Person C, Person B is likely higher status than Person A, and Person C is likely equal or lower.

---

#### WHO INTERRUPTS, AND WHOSE INTERRUPTIONS ARE TOLERATED?

**Signal**: The pattern of interruptions maps almost directly onto hierarchy. "Who speaks over whom maps almost exactly onto who holds authority over whom" ([Prof. RJ Starr](https://profrjstarr.com/the-psychology-of-us/the-psychology-of-interruptions-power-anxiety-and-disregard-in-everyday-talk)).

**Why it works**: Research shows that "people with higher perceived status interrupt more often and are interrupted less." Furthermore, "interrupters gained social status after they interrupted, while those who were interrupted lost social status" ([Wikipedia, Interruption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interruption_(speech))).

**Critical nuance**: Not all interruptions are dominance moves. Supportive interruptions ("Yes! And --", "Oh exactly, like when --") indicate **solidarity**, not hierarchy. The hierarchy signal is **competitive** interruption -- cutting someone off to redirect or override.

**Observation method**: Track three things:
1. Who interrupts whom? (Direction of interruption)
2. What happens after? (Does the interrupted person yield or push back?)
3. Does the group react? (Are some interruptions met with silence/acceptance while others are met with "Let them finish"?)

If Person A interrupts Person B and B yields -- and the group accepts this -- A is likely higher status. If A interrupts B and someone says "Wait, let B finish" -- the group is protecting B's status.

---

#### WHO IS ASKED FOR INPUT?

**Signal**: Being consulted = status. When the group turns to someone and says "What do you think?" -- that person is being treated as having relevant authority or expertise.

**Why it works**: Soliciting someone's opinion is a prestige marker. It signals that their judgment matters. The more people who seek your input, the higher your status in that context.

**Observation method**: Notice who gets asked "What do you think?" vs. who has to volunteer their opinion unsolicited. Notice whether some people's unsolicited input is welcomed while others' is ignored.

---

#### WHAT ADDRESS FORMS ARE USED?

**Signal**: Asymmetric address forms = hierarchy. If Person A calls Person B "Dr. Smith" but Person B calls Person A "Mike" -- B is higher status.

**Why it works**: Research on academic etiquette confirms: "The more junior you are and the more senior the person you are writing to, the more likely it is that a 'formal' form of address would be appropriate" ([Harzing, 2017](https://harzing.com/blog/2017/05/how-to-address-other-academics-by-email)). Title + last name signals deference; first name signals equality or higher status.

**Common patterns**:
- **Title + Last Name** → Deference (lower to higher): "Professor Chen," "Dr. Williams," "Mr. Nakamura"
- **First Name** → Equality or informality: "Sarah," "Tom"
- **Asymmetric** → Clear hierarchy: If A uses a title for B but B uses A's first name
- **Nickname or shortened first name** → Intimacy or established equality: "Mike," "Jen"

**Observation method**: Listen to what names/titles people use for each other. Asymmetry is the most reliable signal of hierarchy.

---

#### WHO SPEAKS LAST BEFORE A DECISION?

**Signal**: The person who makes the final statement before a group decision is enacted is often the actual authority -- regardless of their formal title.

**Why it works**: Research on meeting dynamics shows that "formal power [is] attributed to title or position, while others carry informal power through influence based on experience, force of personality, persuasion, or strong relationships with decision-makers" ([Interaction Institute for Social Change](https://interactioninstitute.org/power-dynamics-the-hidden-element-to-effective-meetings/)). The last word often signals the true decision-maker.

**Observation method**: When a decision is being made, watch the sequence: who speaks, then does the group look to someone specific for the final word? That person may be the informal authority, even if they don't hold the highest title.

---

#### WHO USES MORE DISCOURSE MARKERS AND SOFTENERS?

**Signal**: Discourse markers ("well," "you know," "I mean," "like," "so"), fillers ("um," "uh"), and softeners ("just a thought," "if that makes sense") tend to increase in lower-status speech, particularly when addressing someone higher.

**Why it works**: These markers perform social work: they signal non-threat, willingness to yield the floor, and deference. They are conversational lubricant, and the person doing more lubricating is typically the one managing more social risk.

**Observation method**: Compare the speech of two people talking to each other. If one uses notably more filler and softening language, they are likely lower status in that interaction.

---

### 1.2 Behavioral Signals (What You Can See)

#### WHO DEFERS WHEN THERE'S DISAGREEMENT?

**Signal**: When two people disagree and one backs down ("Actually, you're right" or "OK, let's go with your approach"), the person who defers is usually lower status.

**Observation method**: Watch disagreements. Track who concedes first. If the same person always concedes, they are consistently lower in the hierarchy. If concession patterns shift by topic, that tells you about expertise-based status.

---

#### WHOSE SUGGESTIONS GET FOLLOWED?

**Signal**: If Person A says "Let's try X" and the group does X -- but when Person B suggests Y, nothing happens -- A has more influence.

**Observation method**: Track which suggestions get enacted. This is one of the most reliable status signals because it measures *actual influence*, not just perceived confidence.

---

#### WHO CAN BREAK SMALL RULES WITHOUT CONSEQUENCE?

**Signal**: Arriving late, dressing more casually than the norm, checking their phone during meetings, taking longer breaks -- if someone does these things and *no one reacts*, that person has high status.

**Why it works**: This is the **idiosyncrasy credit** phenomenon. Psychologist Edwin Hollander found that "the higher one's social status, the more one is conferred 'idiosyncrasy credit' that permits greater latitude to deviate from norms" ([Hollander, 1958](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idiosyncrasy_credit)). A well-regarded senior employee who arrives late to a meeting is forgiven far more readily than a new junior colleague who does the same.

**Observation method**: Notice who breaks minor norms (dress code, punctuality, phone use) and whether anyone reacts. No reaction = high idiosyncrasy credit = high status. If the same behavior in someone else would draw a comment or look, you've identified a status differential.

---

#### WHO DO PEOPLE LOOK AT WHEN A DECISION NEEDS TO BE MADE?

**Signal**: When a question hangs in the air -- "So what should we do?" -- watch where eyes go. The person everyone looks at is the perceived authority, regardless of their official title.

**Why it works**: Research on visual attention and status confirms that "people grant more visual attention to those in high-status positions" ([Psychology Town](https://psychology.town/general/decoding-non-verbal-cues-social-interactions/)). Gaze direction during decision moments is an unconscious status indicator.

**Observation method**: When an unresolved question or decision point arises, briefly scan the room (if you can). Where are people looking? If multiple people look at the same person, that person is the informal authority.

---

#### WHO SITS AT THE HEAD OF THE TABLE / CENTER OF THE GROUP?

**Signal**: "Occupancy of the head position was associated with higher ratings on talkativeness, persuasiveness, dominance, leadership, self-confidence, and intelligence" ([Pellegrini, 1971](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.2466/pr0.1971.28.3.887)). Central seating positions also enhance leadership emergence because they allow maximum eye contact with all group members.

**Why it works**: Spatial positioning is a status signal. "Distance signals hierarchy. Who sits close to the head of the table, who sits centrally, who sits peripherally" ([The Thought Lounge](https://www.thethoughtlounge.com/post/spatial-dominance-the-non-verbal-mechanics-of-meeting-room-geography)). The brain interprets proximity to power as power.

**Observation method**: Note who sits where. Head of table or center of group = likely highest status. Periphery = likely lower status or newest to the group. If someone takes the head position and no one reacts, the group accepts their status claim. If someone takes it and there's tension, a status contest is occurring.

---

#### WHO ENTERS/EXITS FIRST?

**Signal**: In formal contexts, higher-status individuals often enter or are seated first. In informal contexts, the pattern is less consistent but still present -- the person who leads the group into a room or who arrives "fashionably late" and is still welcomed warmly may be signaling or holding higher status.

**Observation method**: This is most reliable in formal or semi-formal contexts (business dinners, scheduled meetings, ceremonies). Watch who walks through doorways first, who is gestured through doors, and who is waited for.

---

#### POSTURE AND SPATIAL EXPANSION

**Signal**: "An open, expansive posture signals confidence or dominance; a contracted, closed posture signals submission, anxiety, or discomfort" ([IJSES, 2024](https://ijses.org/index.php/ijses/article/download/355/330/1063)).

**Why it works**: Research distinguishes between prestige and dominance displays: prestige is communicated through "subtle chest expansion" and confident (but not aggressive) bearing, while dominance involves "more grandiose space-taking" and head tilted upward ([Witkower et al., 2023](https://anthropology.washington.edu/sites/anthropology/files/documents/research/Witkower%20et%20al.%202023%20J%20Exper%20Psychol%20Gen.pdf)).

**Observation method**: Compare how much physical space different people occupy. Who leans back and spreads out? Who sits compactly? This signal is most useful when read alongside other signals -- spatial expansion alone could mean many things, but spatial expansion + topic control + others deferring = strong status signal.

---

### 1.3 The Quick Hierarchy Scan

When you enter any social situation, you can run this abbreviated scan in 5-10 minutes:

| # | Observe | What It Tells You |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Who is talking the most? | Likely higher status (or seeking status) |
| 2 | Who introduces topics? | Controls the agenda = likely higher status |
| 3 | Who interrupts whom -- and is it tolerated? | Interruption rights map to hierarchy |
| 4 | Who hedges the most? | Most hedging = lowest relative status |
| 5 | Where do eyes go during decisions? | The person everyone looks at is the authority |
| 6 | Who sits centrally / at the head? | Spatial position correlates with status |
| 7 | Who can break small rules without consequence? | High idiosyncrasy credit = high status |
| 8 | Whose suggestions actually get followed? | Tracks actual influence, not just confidence |

**Rule of thumb**: If 5+ of these signals point to the same person, you can be confident about their position. If signals are scattered across multiple people, the hierarchy is either flat or contested.

## SECTION 2: THE PRAGMATIC CALIBRATION TABLE

Once you've identified the hierarchy, the next question is: **how do I adjust my communication?**

This is the pragmatic calibration table. It tells you exactly how to shift your language based on your position relative to the person you're talking to.

### 2.1 The Core Table

| Your Position | Hedging Level | Politeness Strategy | Directness | Topic Rights | Example Phrasing |
|--------------|---------------|--------------------|-----------:|-------------|-----------------|
| **Clearly lower status** | High -- hedge most assertions | Negative politeness (don't impose, show deference) | Indirect -- use questions, suggestions, not commands | Follow topics set by others; ask permission to introduce new ones | "I was wondering if maybe we could consider..." / "Would it be possible to..." / "I might be wrong, but..." |
| **Somewhat lower** | Moderate-to-high | Mix, leaning negative | Mostly indirect, with some direct statements on your expertise area | Can introduce topics but frame them as suggestions | "I think it might be worth looking at..." / "One thing I noticed was..." |
| **Roughly equal** | Standard -- some hedging, some direct | Mix of positive and negative politeness | Direct with hedging on sensitive topics | Shared topic control -- introduce freely | "I think we should..." / "Have you considered..." / "My take is..." |
| **Somewhat higher** | Can reduce hedging | More direct is acceptable | Direct, but use softeners for face-threatening acts | Can set topics and redirect | "Let's focus on..." / "I'd like us to consider..." / "Here's what I'm thinking..." |
| **Clearly higher status** | Minimal hedging needed | Direct is expected (BUT the power paradox applies -- see below) | Direct | Full topic control available | "We need to..." / "Here's the plan..." / "What's your take on this?" |
| **Unclear / New situation** | Default to MORE deference | Negative politeness as default | Hedged and indirect until you calibrate | Follow others' topics; observe before introducing your own | "I'm still getting oriented, but..." / "What's the usual approach here?" |

### 2.2 Brown and Levinson's Politeness Strategies -- Practical Summary

Brown and Levinson's politeness theory ([1987](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politeness_theory)) provides the theoretical backbone. Here is the practical version:

**Negative Politeness** (= "I respect your autonomy and don't want to impose"):
- Used when: talking UP the hierarchy, or in formal/unknown situations
- How: Be indirect. Frame requests as questions. Apologize for imposing. Minimize the request size.
- Examples:
  - "I'm sorry to bother you, but would it be possible to...?"
  - "I don't want to take up too much of your time, but..."
  - "If it's not too much trouble, could you...?"
  - "I was just wondering whether..."

**Positive Politeness** (= "We're in this together, I like you, we're on the same side"):
- Used when: talking to equals, building rapport, establishing in-group membership
- How: Use inclusive language. Show interest in them. Claim common ground. Use humor.
- Examples:
  - "We should totally do this together."
  - "I know you'd understand this..."
  - "Great idea -- and building on that..."
  - "You know how it is with..."

**Bald On-Record** (= direct, no softening):
- Used when: you have clear authority, urgency exists, or the relationship is so established that no face-work is needed
- How: Just say it directly.
- Examples:
  - "Send me the report by Friday."
  - "That won't work."
  - "We're going with Option A."

**Off-Record / Indirect** (= hinting):
- Used when: the face threat is very high, or you want to preserve deniability
- How: Hint at what you need. Let the listener draw the inference.
- Examples:
  - "It's really cold in here..." (= close the window)
  - "That deadline is coming up fast..." (= you need to hurry)
  - "I noticed the report still has the old numbers..." (= fix it)

### 2.3 The Power Paradox

A critical nuance for higher-status positions: **the most effective leaders use more politeness, not less**.

Research shows that prestige-based leaders who maintain politeness and approachability are more effective and more respected than dominance-based leaders who leverage their position for directness. This is the power paradox:

- You *can* be direct. You have the status for it.
- But being direct without softeners makes people fear you rather than respect you.
- Effective high-status communication often looks like: clear direction + softened delivery.

**Practical formula for higher-status communication**:
- State the decision clearly (don't hedge the decision itself)
- BUT add inclusive framing: "Here's what I'm thinking -- I want to make sure this works for everyone"
- Ask for input even when you've already decided: "Does anyone see a problem with this approach?"
- Thank people for their contributions even when you're redirecting: "Great point, and building on that..."

## SECTION 3: HIERARCHY IN SPECIFIC CONTEXTS

### 3.1 The Workplace

#### Reading the Org Chart vs. Reading the Room

Every workplace has two hierarchies:

**Formal Hierarchy** (the org chart):
- Explicit. You can look it up.
- Boss → Manager → Team Lead → Individual Contributor.
- Sets the baseline expectation for who has authority over what.

**Informal Hierarchy** (the room):
- Implicit. Discovered through observation.
- The person the CEO always asks for opinions. The longtime employee everyone defers to. The person who, without a management title, seems to influence every decision.
- Research confirms that "some people hold formal power attributed to their title or position, while others carry informal power through influence based on experience, force of personality, persuasion, or strong relationships with decision-makers" ([Interaction Institute](https://interactioninstitute.org/power-dynamics-the-hidden-element-to-effective-meetings/)).

**How to map both**: Start with the org chart (formal). Then use the detection checklist (Section 1) to map the informal hierarchy. The two often diverge. When they diverge, the informal hierarchy is usually more important for day-to-day communication.

#### How to Talk to Your Boss

**Default strategy**: Negative politeness + indirect + hedged.

| Situation | What to Say | Why |
|-----------|-------------|-----|
| Making a suggestion | "I was thinking it might be worth exploring..." / "What if we tried..." | Frames as a question, not a directive |
| Reporting a problem | "I wanted to flag something -- [problem]. I have a couple of ideas for how to handle it." | Shows initiative, gives them decision authority |
| Disagreeing | "I see the value in that approach. One concern I have is..." / "Can I push back gently on one thing?" | Acknowledges their position first, then hedges the disagreement |
| Requesting something | "Would it be possible to..." / "I was hoping to..." | Indirect request form, easy to deny without losing face |
| Delivering bad news | "I wanted to give you a heads up about..." / "There's a complication with..." | Neutral framing, no blame, forward-looking |

**Key principle**: Frame your contributions as **input** to their decision, not as decisions themselves. Even if you're right and they're wrong, the pragmatic move is to present your perspective as information for them to weigh, not as a correction of their judgment.

#### How to Talk to Peers

**Default strategy**: Mix of positive and negative politeness. Direct with hedging.

| Situation | What to Say | Why |
|-----------|-------------|-----|
| Suggesting something | "I think we should try..." / "What about..." | Direct enough for equals, but framed collaboratively |
| Disagreeing | "I see it differently -- here's my take..." / "I'm not sure about that -- what if..." | Direct disagreement is acceptable between equals, but soften with framing |
| Asking for help | "Could you help me with..." / "Do you have a minute to..." | Straightforward, minimal hedging needed |
| Giving feedback | "One thing I noticed..." / "Have you thought about..." | Frame as observation, not judgment |

#### How to Talk to Direct Reports (The Power Paradox in Action)

**Default strategy**: Direct on content + kind on delivery. The goal is clarity without creating fear.

| Situation | What to Say | Why |
|-----------|-------------|-----|
| Giving direction | "I need you to do X by Y" -- BUT add: "Let me know if that timeline doesn't work" | Clear direction + escape valve |
| Giving feedback | "I want to share some feedback on X. The thing that worked well was... The thing to work on is..." | Specific, balanced, no hedging on the substance |
| Correcting a mistake | "I noticed X happened. Can you walk me through what went wrong?" | Curious rather than punishing; gives them room to explain |
| Praising | "Great work on X -- specifically, [detail]" | Be specific; vague praise feels hollow |

#### Meeting Dynamics

**Who speaks when in meetings**:
- The meeting organizer usually opens (formal authority)
- Higher-status people tend to speak earlier and more
- Contributions from lower-status attendees often need to be "endorsed" by someone higher before they gain traction
- The final word before a decision often belongs to the true authority

**How to disagree with a superior in a meeting**:

This is one of the highest-risk pragmatic situations. The research is clear: "correcting someone in front of others -- especially superiors -- is a big no-no" ([Crucial Learning](https://cruciallearning.com/blog/how-to-change-social-norms-at-the-office/)). Public correction threatens their face and violates deference norms.

**Strategy hierarchy (from safest to riskiest)**:

1. **Private disagreement afterward** (safest): "Hey, I had a thought about what we discussed in the meeting -- could I share a different perspective?"
2. **Hedge heavily in the meeting**: "I might be looking at this wrong, but I wonder if we've considered..."
3. **Use questions, not statements**: "Could there be a risk with that approach in terms of X?" (instead of "That approach is risky because of X")
4. **Invoke external authority**: "The data seems to suggest..." / "In the Smith report, it said..." (redirects the challenge from you-vs-them to data-vs-them)
5. **Direct contradiction** (riskiest): Only do this if the stakes justify the social cost, and frame it as protecting the group: "I want to raise a concern because I think it could affect the outcome..."

#### Email Hierarchy Signals

Email is a rich source of hierarchy information. Research shows that email communication reflects and reinforces workplace power dynamics ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0747563220302302)).

**Signals to read**:

| Email Feature | Higher Status Signal | Lower Status Signal |
|--------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **To/CC field** | In the "To" field; others CC'd | CC'd (informed but not the primary recipient) |
| **Greeting** | Brief or absent ("Hi team,") | Formal ("Dear Mr. Chen,") |
| **Closing** | Brief ("Thanks," "Best,") or absent | More formal ("Best regards," "Sincerely,") |
| **Response time** | Can respond slowly | Expected to respond quickly |
| **Length** | Can be brief (even terse) | Usually longer (more explanation, more hedging) |
| **Directness** | Direct requests ("Send me X by Friday") | Hedged requests ("Would it be possible to get X?") |
| **CC patterns** | CCs others as FYI | CCs boss to show work or escalate |

**Practical email rules**:
- When emailing your boss: Put their email in the "To" field. Use their preferred address form. Be concise but not terse. Hedge requests.
- When emailing your boss and CCing others: Boss in "To" -- this "subtly acknowledges the hierarchy and shows respect" ([Indeed](https://www.indeed.com/career-advice/career-development/email-cc-vs-bcc)).
- When someone CCs your boss on an email to you: This is a power move -- they're escalating. Respond carefully and professionally.
- Tone: "It's always best to err on the side of formality" in professional email ([WiseStamp](https://www.wisestamp.com/blog/email-etiquette/)). You can always reduce formality later; increasing it after being too casual is socially costly.

### 3.2 Academic Settings

#### Professor-Student Hierarchy

This is one of the most formal hierarchies in everyday life. The asymmetry is large and explicit.

**Address forms**: Use "Professor [Last Name]" or "Dr. [Last Name]" unless explicitly told otherwise. "The more junior you are and the more senior the person you are writing to, the more likely it is that a 'formal' form of address would be appropriate" ([Harzing, 2017](https://harzing.com/blog/2017/05/how-to-address-other-academics-by-email)).

**Key rule**: Don't use first names for professors unless they've explicitly invited you to. Even then, some professors prefer titles in public settings while allowing first names in private. When in doubt: "How would you like me to address you?" works well ([Global Conference](https://globalconference.ca/how-to-address-people-in-a-conference/)).

**Gender parity note**: Research specifically warns: "Don't call male faculty members 'Professor' or 'Dr.' and then call female professors by their first names" ([Chronicle of Higher Ed](https://www.chronicle.com/article/a-scientists-guide-to-academic-etiquette/)). Apply the same formality level regardless of gender.

**Communication strategies**:
- Use negative politeness: "I was wondering if you could help me understand..."
- Frame requests as questions: "Would it be possible to get an extension?" not "I need an extension."
- Show you've done work first: "I've looked at X and Y but I'm stuck on Z" -- not just "I don't understand."
- Defer to their expertise: "I'd really value your perspective on..."

#### TA-Student Hierarchy

Somewhere between professor-student and peer-peer. TAs have some authority (grading, leading sections) but are also students themselves.

**Strategy**: Somewhat less formal than with professors. You can use first names if they introduce themselves that way. Still use hedging for requests and disagreements, but less than with professors.

#### Peer Dynamics in Study Groups

Study groups often start flat but develop informal hierarchies quickly based on **expertise prestige** -- whoever understands the material best tends to gain status within the group.

**Signals**: Who do others ask questions of? Who explains things? Who leads the structure of study sessions?

**Strategy**: Positive politeness and direct communication. If you have expertise, share it -- this earns prestige. If you need help, ask directly -- in a study group, asking is expected and does not lose you status.

#### Conference Hierarchies

Academic conferences have layered hierarchies: keynote speakers > panel presenters > poster presenters > attendees. Senior researchers > junior researchers > graduate students > undergraduates.

**Practical rules**:
- Use titles for senior researchers you haven't met: "Professor," "Dr."
- At Q&A, identify yourself briefly: "I'm [Name], a graduate student at [University]."
- Approaching a senior researcher at a poster session or coffee break: "Professor [Name], I really enjoyed your talk. I work on [related topic] -- could I ask you a question about [specific point]?"
- If you see someone already in conversation, wait for a natural pause rather than interrupting ([Chronicle](https://www.chronicle.com/article/a-scientists-guide-to-academic-etiquette/)).

### 3.3 Medical Settings

#### Doctor-Patient Power Asymmetry

The doctor-patient relationship involves "four dynamics inextricably linked with asymmetrical power positioning: social, economic, knowledge-based, and communication" ([EBSCO Research](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/communication-and-mass-media/doctor-patient-communication)). Doctors control the conversation, the information, and the decisions.

Research shows that doctors "typically assert control over communication by interrupting patients, excluding them by writing while they speak, and eliciting information without explaining why" ([SIT Digital Collections](https://digitalcollections.sit.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=4556&context=isp_collection)).

**This is one hierarchy where you should NOT simply defer**. You are a consumer of a service AND the expert on your own experience.

#### How to Advocate Within the Hierarchy

The key is to **work within the hierarchy's norms while asserting your needs**. This means using the right pragmatic framing.

| What You Need | Deferential (Too Passive) | Assertive Within Hierarchy (Goal) | Aggressive (Too Direct) |
|--------------|--------------------------|----------------------------------|------------------------|
| More information | "OK" (accepting without understanding) | "Could you help me understand what that means for me specifically?" | "That doesn't make sense." |
| A different treatment | Silence / compliance | "I've read about [alternative]. Could we discuss whether that might work for my situation?" | "I want [alternative] instead." |
| A second opinion | Not asking | "I'd like to get a second opinion on this -- can you help me with a referral?" | "I don't trust your diagnosis." |
| To be heard | Minimizing symptoms | "I want to make sure I'm communicating this clearly -- [symptom] is significantly affecting my daily life." | "You're not listening to me." |
| To push back | Going along | "I appreciate your recommendation. I have some concerns about [specific aspect] -- can we talk through those?" | "I'm not doing that." |

**Strategies that work within the hierarchy**:
- **Prepare in advance**: Write down your symptoms, questions, and concerns. This gives you a script to fall back on when the power differential makes real-time communication harder.
- **Use "I" statements**: "I've been experiencing..." / "I'm concerned about..." -- these are harder to dismiss than vague reports.
- **Invoke the medical framework**: "Could you note in my chart that I asked about X and the decision was Y?" -- this activates the doctor's professional accountability within their own system.
- **Bring an advocate**: Research shows that pre-consultation coaching and having a companion who can help advocate leads to better outcomes ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11892210/)).

**For autistic individuals specifically**: Medical settings can be especially challenging because sensory overload, processing time differences, and difficulty with the rapid-fire question format can all compound the power asymmetry. Consider:
- Explicitly stating your communication needs: "I process information better when I can read it. Could you write down the key points?"
- Requesting additional time: "I need a moment to think about that before I respond."
- Bringing written notes of your questions and history.

### 3.4 Social Gatherings

#### Host Privileges and Guest Obligations

A party or gathering has its own hierarchy: the **host** holds a privileged position.

**Host privileges** (what they can do that guests cannot):
- Set the agenda (activities, music, food, timing)
- Introduce people to each other
- Direct the flow of the event ("Let's move to the patio")
- End the event ("It's getting late -- this was great")
- Break norms (they can eat first, sit where they want, etc.)

**Guest obligations** (what is expected of you):
- RSVP promptly. "Delaying your reply could hinder the host's planning and make it seem as if you're waiting for something better to come along" ([Emily Post](https://emilypost.com/advice/party-etiquette-tips-for-hosts-and-guests)).
- Arrive within 15 minutes of the stated time (social gatherings) or exactly on time (dinner parties).
- Follow the host's lead on activities and flow.
- Contribute where appropriate (bring something if suggested, offer to help, engage with other guests).
- Leave at a reasonable time, or when the host signals (cleaning up, mentioning early mornings, becoming less conversational).

**Pragmatic strategy as a guest**: Positive politeness is the default. Be warm, inclusive, interested. Follow the host's conversational lead. Introduce yourself to people you don't know. Don't dominate conversations or redirect activities unless asked to.

#### Meeting a Partner's Family for the First Time

This is a high-stakes social hierarchy situation. You are a newcomer entering an established group, and you are being evaluated.

**Your position**: Clearly lower -- you are the newcomer seeking acceptance.

**Strategy**:
- **Default to formality**: Use "Mr./Mrs./Ms. [Last Name]" until told otherwise.
- **Negative politeness**: Don't impose. Ask before helping. Follow their house rules.
- **Show interest**: Ask questions about them, their home, their interests. Being asked about = giving prestige.
- **Defer on topics**: Let them lead conversations. Ask follow-up questions rather than steering to your topics.
- **Match the group's energy**: If they're reserved, be reserved. If they're loud and joking, you can relax -- but don't try to be the funniest person in the room.
- **Compliment the host specifically**: "This meal is wonderful" / "Your home is lovely" -- these acknowledge their effort and position.

#### Navigating Unfamiliar Social Groups

When you enter any new social group, you are temporarily at the bottom of the hierarchy -- regardless of your status elsewhere. You haven't earned standing in this specific group yet.

**The safe approach**:
1. **Observe first** (5-10 minutes): Run the quick hierarchy scan from Section 1.3.
2. **Start with questions**: "How do you all know each other?" / "What do you do?" -- this is non-threatening and shows interest.
3. **Follow conversational norms**: Match the group's volume, pace, topic range, and humor level.
4. **Don't correct or contradict** until you have standing: Even if someone says something wrong, the social cost of correcting a stranger in their own group is very high.
5. **Earn idiosyncrasy credits before spending them**: Conform first, differentiate later.

#### How to Read the Informal Hierarchy at a Party

Use the detection checklist, but adapted for informal settings:

| Signal | What It Looks Like at a Party |
|--------|-------------------------------|
| Topic control | Who tells the stories everyone listens to? Who can redirect the group's attention? |
| Spatial centrality | Who stands in the middle of the largest group? Who do people approach? |
| Deference | Who do people make room for? Whose jokes get the biggest laughs? |
| Rule-breaking | Who can be loud/rude and be laughed off? Who would be judged for the same? |
| Gaze direction | When something notable happens, who do people look at for their reaction? |
| Introductions | Who introduces other people? (Introducing = social power: "This is my friend X, they're amazing at Y.") |

## SECTION 4: COMMON HIERARCHY MISTAKES AND FIXES

These are the most frequent pragmatic errors related to hierarchy, with specific corrections.

### Mistake 1: Speaking to Your Boss Like a Peer

**What happens**: Using overly casual language, being too direct, making statements instead of suggestions, introducing topics without framing.

**Why it's a problem**: It signals that you don't recognize or respect the hierarchy. This can be interpreted as arrogance, obliviousness, or disrespect.

**The fix**:
- Add hedges: "I think..." → "I was thinking maybe..."
- Use questions instead of statements: "We should do X" → "What would you think about doing X?"
- Defer on topic control: "Let me tell you about..." → "Would it be helpful if I shared..."
- Match their formality level, or exceed it slightly.

**Example**:
- **Too casual**: "That plan won't work. Here's what we should do instead."
- **Calibrated**: "I see the direction you're going with that. One thing I'm wondering about is whether [concern]. Would it make sense to explore [alternative]?"

---

### Mistake 2: Not Acknowledging Someone's Expertise

**What happens**: Jumping into a topic without recognizing that someone in the room has more knowledge or experience.

**Why it's a problem**: In prestige-based hierarchies, failing to acknowledge expertise is a status violation. It's like skipping someone in line -- they notice.

**The fix**: Explicitly defer to their expertise before (or as) you speak:
- "I know you have way more experience with this, but..."
- "I'd love to get your perspective on this -- [your thought]"
- "Before I share my take, I'm curious what you think, since you've worked on this longer."

---

### Mistake 3: Correcting a Higher-Status Person Publicly

**What happens**: Pointing out that your boss, professor, or senior colleague is wrong in front of others.

**Why it's a problem**: Public correction threatens their face and violates deference norms. "Preserving one's own face and helping others save theirs is often more important than being 'right'" ([IJoC](https://ijoc.org/index.php/ijoc/article/viewFile/1284/749)).

**The fix** (in order of preference):
1. **Correct privately afterward**: "Hey, I noticed something in the meeting -- I think the number might actually be X. Wanted to flag it before it goes further."
2. **Hedge heavily if it must be in the meeting**: "I might be remembering this wrong, but I thought the figure was closer to X -- could we double-check?"
3. **Use external authority**: "The latest report has a different number, I think -- let me pull it up."
4. **Frame as a question**: "Was it X or Y? I want to make sure I have the right number in my notes."

**Never**: "Actually, that's wrong. It's X." (This is face-threatening regardless of hierarchy, and devastating when directed at someone above you.)

---

### Mistake 4: Not Deferring Enough in a New Environment

**What happens**: Walking into a new group, team, or social situation and communicating as if you've already earned standing.

**Why it's a problem**: You haven't accumulated idiosyncrasy credits yet. The group hasn't accepted you. Being too confident or directive before you've earned it reads as presumptuous.

**The fix**: Default to more formal, more hedged, more deferential communication until you've had time to:
1. Observe the group's norms
2. Identify the hierarchy
3. Earn initial acceptance through conformity
4. Gradually calibrate toward the actual norm

**Practical phrase**: "I'm still getting the lay of the land, so let me know if I'm off base here..."

---

### Mistake 5: Over-Deferring (Appearing Insecure)

**What happens**: Using so much hedging and deference that you undermine your own credibility. Apologizing for every contribution. Prefacing every statement with "Sorry, this might be a dumb question..." or "I don't know if this is helpful, but..."

**Why it's a problem**: Excessive deference doesn't just signal lower status -- it can signal incompetence or insecurity. There is a threshold below which hedging becomes self-sabotaging.

**The fix**: Calibrate by observing what others at your level do. Match, don't exceed, the hedging level of your peers.

**The test**: Ask yourself -- would other people at my level say it this way? If you're hedging noticeably more than your peers, pull back.

**Specific corrections**:
- "Sorry, this might be stupid, but..." → "One thing I noticed is..."
- "I don't know if this is helpful..." → "Here's another angle on this..."
- "Sorry to bother you, but..." → "Quick question for you..."
- "I could be totally wrong about this..." → "My understanding is... does that match yours?"

---

### Mistake 6: Challenging Unearned Authority Too Directly

**What happens**: Someone is asserting status they haven't earned (through expertise, position, or group acceptance), and you push back on it directly.

**Why it's a problem**: Even when authority is unearned, direct challenges are socially expensive. The group may not be ready to renegotiate the hierarchy, and your challenge may isolate you rather than correcting the situation.

**The fix**: Use **questions rather than statements** to challenge:
- "What's the basis for that approach?" (asks them to justify, without you asserting they're wrong)
- "Has anyone else had experience with this?" (opens the floor without directly targeting the person)
- "I want to make sure we're considering all angles -- what about X?" (adds your perspective without framing it as opposition)

**Choose battles**: Not every instance of unearned authority needs to be challenged. Ask: Will this matter in a week? Is the outcome important enough to justify the social cost?

## SECTION 5: THE FOUR-STEP NAVIGATION FRAMEWORK

For any social situation where hierarchy matters, use this framework.

### Step 1: Identify the Hierarchy Type

Ask: **What kind of hierarchy is this?**

| Type | Basis | How You Detect It | How It Feels |
|------|-------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Formal / Positional** | Title, role, institutional authority | Org chart, seating arrangement, introductions | Structured, predictable |
| **Informal / Influence** | Relationships, charisma, track record | Who people listen to, whose opinions shape decisions | Subtle, requires observation |
| **Expertise / Prestige** | Knowledge, skill, demonstrated competence | Who gets consulted, who explains things | Respectful, merit-based |
| **Social / In-group** | Familiarity, history, social connections | Who knows whom, inside jokes, shared references | Cliquish, can feel exclusionary |
| **Dominance** | Intimidation, resource control, aggression | Who people avoid contradicting, tension when they speak | Tense, fear-based |

**Most situations are a blend**, but one type usually dominates. Identify the primary type and you know which signals to watch for.

### Step 2: Detect Your Position

Use the observable signals checklist (Section 1) to determine where you sit in this specific hierarchy.

**Quick-assess questions**:
- Am I being consulted? (If yes → you have some status)
- Am I being addressed formally or informally? (Formal → you're new or lower; informal → you're accepted)
- Are my suggestions being followed? (If yes → you have influence)
- Am I hedging more than others at my level? (If yes → you may be over-deferring)
- Am I hedging less than others at my level? (If yes → you may be under-deferring)

### Step 3: Calibrate Your Pragmatics

Use the calibration table (Section 2) to adjust:
- **Hedging level**: Match to your position
- **Politeness strategy**: Negative politeness for up-hierarchy, positive for equals, direct (with kindness) for down-hierarchy
- **Directness**: Scale with your status and the relationship
- **Topic control**: Proportional to your position

### Step 4: Monitor for Shifts

Hierarchies are not static. They shift. The person who was at the top can lose status when the topic changes, the setting changes, or new people enter.

**Monitor for these shift triggers** (detailed in Section 6 below).

## SECTION 6: WHEN HIERARCHY SHIFTS

Hierarchies feel fixed, but they move. Recognizing shifts is critical because using the wrong calibration after a shift has occurred will either make you seem out of touch (still deferring to someone who lost status) or presumptuous (not deferring to someone who just gained it).

### 6.1 Topic Changes

**What happens**: The conversation or meeting moves to a new topic. The person who was the expert on the old topic may not be the expert on the new one.

**Example**: In a marketing meeting, the VP of Marketing holds the highest status. Then the conversation shifts to a technical implementation question. Now the senior engineer's expertise gives them topic-specific status. People start looking at *them* for answers, even though the VP is technically higher-ranked.

**How to detect**: Watch the signals shift. Who is being consulted now? Who defers on this topic? If the interruption pattern changes -- if someone who was quiet starts being asked for input -- expertise-based status has shifted.

**How to adapt**:
- If you gain topic-based status: Speak more confidently on your area of expertise, but maintain appropriate hedging toward formally higher-ranked people. ("I can speak to the technical side -- here's what I'd recommend...")
- If you lose topic-based status: Reduce your contributions on the new topic. Cede the floor. Ask questions rather than making statements. ("I'm not as close to this area -- what are the key considerations?")

### 6.2 Setting Changes

**What happens**: The same people interact in a different context. Your boss at work has no formal authority at a weekend barbecue.

**Example**: Your manager is your subordinate's peer at the company softball game. A professor is just another parent at the school pickup line.

**How to detect**: Context norms override workplace hierarchy. If you're at a social event and your boss is using first names, relaxed posture, and casual language -- the hierarchy has flattened. If they maintain formal language and posture, they're signaling that the hierarchy carries over.

**How to adapt**:
- Default to slightly more casual than work, but don't overcorrect. Your boss may be casual at a barbecue, but that doesn't make them your buddy on Monday.
- Mirror their level of formality. If they loosen up, you can too. If they don't, don't force it.
- Avoid work topics unless they bring them up first. Introducing work topics in a social setting reimports the work hierarchy.

### 6.3 New People Enter

**What happens**: When someone new joins a group, the hierarchy can shift. A new person may have higher status than existing members (new boss, expert guest), or lower status (intern, newcomer).

**How to detect**: Watch how the group responds to the new arrival:
- Does conversation pause? (High-status arrival)
- Do people adjust their posture or formality? (Hierarchy recalibration happening)
- Is the new person introduced formally or casually? (Formal = higher status; casual = equal or lower)
- Does someone yield their seat? (Status deference)

**How to adapt**:
- When a higher-status person enters: Increase your hedging and deference slightly. Observe how others adjust and match.
- When an equal enters: Maintain your current calibration but be welcoming (positive politeness).
- When a lower-status person enters: Be inclusive. Make space for them to contribute, since they're navigating the hierarchy blind, just as you once were.

### 6.4 Conflict Arises

**What happens**: Conflict can rapidly transform a prestige-based hierarchy into a dominance-based one. When people argue, the person who is louder, more aggressive, or more persistent may temporarily gain status -- not because they're right, but because others yield to avoid escalation.

**How to detect**:
- Someone raises their voice or uses more forceful language
- Others go quiet or look uncomfortable
- The conversation becomes about winning rather than collaborating
- People start taking sides

**How to adapt**:
- Recognize that conflict-generated hierarchy is often temporary and not legitimate.
- Don't automatically defer to the loudest person. Silence does not equal agreement.
- If you must engage: stay calm, use facts, frame your position as questions. ("I hear what you're saying. Can we look at the data on this?")
- If you're a bystander: you can defuse by redirecting to process. ("Maybe we should take a step back and hear from everyone before deciding.")

### 6.5 The Re-Calibration Checkpoint

After any shift, re-run the quick hierarchy scan (Section 1.3). Ask:

1. Has who is being consulted changed?
2. Has the deference pattern changed?
3. Has who controls topics changed?
4. Has the physical arrangement changed?

If the answers are "yes," the hierarchy has shifted and your pragmatics need to shift with it. Re-enter at Step 3 (Calibrate) of the four-step framework.

## APPENDIX A: KEY TERMINOLOGY

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Face** | Your public self-image; the persona you present to others. "Losing face" = being embarrassed or having your competence/status questioned publicly. |
| **Face-Threatening Act (FTA)** | Any communication act that potentially threatens someone's face: requests, criticisms, disagreements, corrections. |
| **Negative Politeness** | Strategies that respect the listener's autonomy and avoid imposition: hedging, indirectness, apologies. |
| **Positive Politeness** | Strategies that affirm the listener's sense of belonging and being liked: inclusive language, agreement, interest-showing. |
| **Hedging** | Words and phrases that soften the force of a statement: "maybe," "sort of," "I think," "it seems like." |
| **Idiosyncrasy Credit** | Accumulated social capital that allows someone to deviate from group norms without penalty. Earned through conformity and competence. |
| **Prestige Hierarchy** | Status hierarchy based on demonstrated competence, respect, and voluntary deference from others. |
| **Dominance Hierarchy** | Status hierarchy based on force, intimidation, or control of resources. Others comply out of fear. |
| **Pragmatic Calibration** | The process of adjusting your communication style (hedging, politeness, directness) to match the current social context and your position in it. |
| **Topic Control** | The ability to set and steer what the group talks about. A reliable marker of status. |

## APPENDIX B: QUICK REFERENCE CARDS

### Card 1: Entering Any New Social Situation

```
1. OBSERVE (5-10 minutes)
   - Who talks most?
   - Who gets consulted?
   - Who can break rules?
   - Where do eyes go during decisions?

2. DEFAULT TO DEFERENCE
   - More formal than you think necessary
   - More hedging than you think necessary
   - Follow topics, don't introduce

3. CALIBRATE
   - Match the hedging level of others at your apparent level
   - Mirror formality of people who seem similar to your position
   - Pull back on hedging gradually, not all at once

4. EARN BEFORE YOU SPEND
   - Conform first
   - Build idiosyncrasy credit
   - Then you can gradually differentiate
```

### Card 2: Speaking Up the Hierarchy

```
USE:
- Questions, not statements
- "I was wondering..." not "We should..."
- "Could we consider..." not "Let's do..."
- "I noticed..." not "The problem is..."

FRAME AS:
- Input for their decision (not your decision)
- Observation (not judgment)
- Curiosity (not correction)

ADD:
- Hedges before assertions
- Acknowledgment of their perspective first
- Escape routes ("...but you'd know better than me")
```

### Card 3: Speaking Across the Hierarchy (to Equals)

```
USE:
- Direct but collaborative language
- "I think we should..." / "What about..."
- "I see it differently..." / "Here's my take..."

FRAME AS:
- Shared exploration (not top-down)
- Your perspective among equals (not the final word)

BALANCE:
- Some hedging (you're not above them)
- Some directness (you're not below them)
- Positive politeness (warmth, inclusion)
```

### Card 4: Speaking Down the Hierarchy

```
USE:
- Clear direction ("I need X by Y")
- Specific feedback ("The part that worked was...")
- Invitation for input ("What's your take?")

REMEMBER THE POWER PARADOX:
- You CAN be direct. But kindness earns more respect.
- State decisions clearly + soften delivery
- Ask for input even when you've decided
- Thank contributions even when redirecting

AVOID:
- Hedging your actual decisions (makes you seem unsure)
- Being direct without warmth (creates fear)
- Confusing kindness with ambiguity
```
