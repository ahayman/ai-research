# Category: Logical Fallacies as Pragmatic Tactics

## The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

---

## How to Read This Document

This is not a logic textbook. Logic textbooks teach you to name fallacies. This document teaches you why naming them doesn't work -- and what to do instead.

The central insight: **logical fallacies are not primarily errors in reasoning. They are pragmatic tactics that exploit the social systems of language.** They hijack presupposition (Category 9), face dynamics (Category 3), hierarchy (Category 22), common ground (Category 16), relevance processing (Category 10), and topic management (Category 13). A fallacy is a conversational move that forces the listener into a disadvantageous position by leveraging the unwritten rules of social interaction.

This means the "correct" response -- naming the fallacy -- is often the worst pragmatic move. It violates politeness norms, threatens the speaker's face, and positions you as pedantic. The person who names the fallacy frequently pays a higher social cost than the person who committed it.

For autistic individuals, this creates a specific double-bind. Research shows autistic people have **enhanced rationality** -- reduced susceptibility to framing effects, sunk-cost bias, and conjunction fallacy (Rozenkrantz et al., 2021). You detect the error. But the social system punishes you for calling it out. Understanding fallacies as pragmatic phenomena -- not just logic errors -- gives you tools to choose when and how to respond, and when to conserve your energy.

## 1. Why Fallacies Are a Pragmatic Problem

### 1.1 The Logic Textbook Gets It Wrong

If fallacies were just logic errors, they'd be easy to spot and dismiss -- like a math mistake. But fallacies work on intelligent, educated people in high-stakes settings. Courtrooms, boardrooms, and academic debates are full of successful fallacies deployed against people who could name them.

The reason: fallacies don't primarily operate at the level of logic. They operate at the level of **pragmatics** -- the social systems through which language achieves its effects.

### 1.2 Fallacies Are Parasitic on Cooperation

Grice's Cooperative Principle (Category 2) describes the rational expectations that make conversation possible. Fallacies exploit these expectations:

| Gricean Maxim | What Listeners Expect | How Fallacies Exploit This |
|---|---|---|
| **Quality** (Be truthful) | The speaker has evidence for their claims | Cherry-picked data *looks like* truthfulness while hiding the full picture |
| **Quantity** (Be complete) | The options presented are exhaustive | **False dilemma** presents only two options; listeners infer there are no others |
| **Relation** (Be relevant) | Every contribution is relevant | **Red herrings** introduce irrelevant material; listeners try to find relevance and follow the deflection |
| **Manner** (Be clear) | Words are used consistently | **Equivocation** shifts word meaning mid-argument; listeners assume consistency |

Fallacies work because listeners are cooperating -- making charitable interpretations, assuming relevance, trusting truthfulness. The fallacy exploits this cooperative disposition.

### 1.3 The Pragma-Dialectical Reframe

Van Eemeren and Grootendorst (1984, 2004) reframed fallacies as violations of the rules governing **rational dialogue**. They identified 10 rules for critical discussion, and showed that every major informal fallacy violates at least one. Douglas Walton (1995) went further: whether an argument is fallacious depends on the **type of dialogue** -- persuasion, negotiation, inquiry, deliberation. An appeal to authority that's fallacious in a scientific inquiry may be perfectly reasonable in a practical decision-making context.

This is critical for autistic learners: **context determines whether something is a fallacy, not just its logical form.**

## 2. The Top Fallacies as Pragmatic Tactics

For each fallacy: what it is, what pragmatic system it exploits, and why correcting it is costly.

### 2.1 Fallacies That Exploit Face (Category 3)

| Fallacy | How It Works | Pragmatic Mechanism | The Correction Trap |
|---------|-------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Ad hominem** | Attacks the person instead of the argument | Forces target to defend their character before their argument; listeners process social threat before logic | Defending your character accepts the frame shift; naming the fallacy sounds academic |
| **Tone policing** | Criticizes delivery to avoid addressing content | Shifts evaluation from WHAT was said to HOW it was said; exploits politeness/register norms | Defending your tone accepts the frame; insisting on content sounds like you don't care about communication |
| **Poisoning the well** | Pre-loads bias against a speaker before they talk | Plants presuppositions that frame everything the target says as suspect | The target now has to defend themselves before making their actual point |

### 2.2 Fallacies That Exploit Presupposition (Category 9)

| Fallacy | How It Works | Pragmatic Mechanism | The Correction Trap |
|---------|-------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Loaded question** | "Have you stopped cheating?" | Any direct answer (yes or no) accepts the presupposition that you were cheating | Saying "That assumes I was cheating" sounds evasive to listeners who didn't notice the presupposition |
| **Begging the question** | The conclusion is hidden in the premise | Exploits presupposition accommodation -- listeners accept premises as given without noticing the circularity | Pointing out circular reasoning requires meta-analysis that sounds like "you're trying to avoid answering" |
| **No True Scotsman** | Redefines terms to exclude counterexamples | Manipulates the definitional common ground; the shifting definition isn't noticed | Challenging the definition shift sounds like you're quibbling over words |

### 2.3 Fallacies That Exploit Hierarchy (Category 22)

| Fallacy | How It Works | Pragmatic Mechanism | The Correction Trap |
|---------|-------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Appeal to authority** | Argues from status rather than evidence | Exploits hierarchy/prestige systems; challenging authority costs social capital | Questioning an authority figure looks like you think you know better |
| **Appeal to tradition** | "We've always done it this way" | Exploits historical common ground; traditions feel like established truth | Challenging tradition makes you look like you don't respect the group's history |
| **Bandwagon** | "Everyone agrees" / "Everyone knows" | Creates artificial common ground; social conformity pressure | Disagreeing with "everyone" positions you as the outsider |

### 2.4 Fallacies That Exploit Relevance (Category 10)

| Fallacy | How It Works | Pragmatic Mechanism | The Correction Trap |
|---------|-------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Red herring** | Introduces an irrelevant topic | The human relevance-processing system automatically tries to connect it; you follow the deflection | "That's not relevant" sounds dismissive; addressing the deflection validates it |
| **Tu quoque / Whataboutism** | "You did it too" | Exploits fairness norms; you feel compelled to address your own behavior | Refusing to address the counter-charge looks like you're dodging |
| **Gish gallop** | Overwhelms with too many arguments to address | Exploits turn-taking constraints; you can't respond to 12 points in one turn | Addressing some points lets the unaddressed ones stand; asking to slow down makes you look like you can't keep up |

### 2.5 Fallacies That Exploit Cognitive Architecture

| Fallacy | How It Works | Pragmatic Mechanism | The Correction Trap |
|---------|-------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **False dilemma** | Presents only two options | Simpler frames require less processing effort; listeners assume options are exhaustive | "There are other options" sounds vague unless you can name them instantly |
| **Slippery slope** | Claims an inevitable chain of bad consequences | Exploits narrative processing; each link feels plausible even if the chain is implausible | You end up arguing each link separately, which is exhausting and appears defensive |
| **Appeal to emotion** | Substitutes emotional reaction for reasoning | Emotionally arousing information achieves high cognitive effects with low processing effort | Challenging the emotion makes you look cold and uncaring |
| **Equivocation** | Shifts word meaning mid-argument | Listeners assume consistent word use (Manner maxim) and don't notice the shift | Pointing out the shift sounds like you're nitpicking definitions |

## 3. The Correction Trap: Why Naming Fallacies Backfires

This is the central pragmatic insight of this category. Correcting a fallacy triggers five simultaneous problems:

| Problem | What Happens |
|---------|-------------|
| **Pedantry** | You sound like you're lecturing from a logic textbook |
| **Meta-communication** | You shift the conversation from the topic to the process, which looks like avoidance |
| **Face threat** | Telling someone their reasoning is faulty attacks their positive face (Category 3) |
| **Evasion perception** | Listeners may think you're dodging the substance by attacking the form |
| **Double-bind** | Responding to the content validates the fallacious frame; refusing to respond looks like you have no answer |

The asymmetry is brutal: **deploying a fallacy takes 5 seconds. Correcting it takes 5 minutes and costs social capital.** This is why fallacies persist.

### 3.1 Intentionality Doesn't Matter

Whether someone uses a fallacy deliberately or accidentally, the pragmatic effect is the same. This is important for autistic learners because it **removes the mind-reading requirement**: you don't need to determine intent to recognize the pattern. The mechanism works the same regardless.

## 4. The Autistic Experience with Fallacies

### 4.1 Enhanced Rationality: The Advantage

Research (Rozenkrantz et al., 2021, MIT) found autistic individuals show reduced susceptibility to:

| Bias/Fallacy | Finding |
|-------------|---------|
| **Conjunction fallacy** | Less likely to judge a conjunction as more probable than either component |
| **Sunk-cost fallacy** | Significantly lower sunk-cost effect; better at cutting losses |
| **Framing effects** | 7.66% susceptibility vs. 14.24% in controls (De Martino et al., 2008) |
| **Anchoring** | Reduced influence of irrelevant initial information |

The mechanism: autistic cognition relies more on deliberative (System 2) processing and less on the intuitive (System 1) shortcuts that make neurotypical people vulnerable to heuristic-based fallacies (Brosnan et al., 2016).

### 4.2 The Double-Bind: The Problem

The autistic advantage (detecting fallacies) collides with the autistic challenge (navigating social dynamics). You see the error clearly but:

- **The correction reflex**: The drive to correct errors -- rooted in truth orientation and justice sensitivity -- pushes toward direct correction, which is the highest-cost pragmatic move
- **The social penalty is invisible**: The social cost of correction isn't obvious to someone who processes logical content more readily than social dynamics
- **Hierarchy amplifies the cost**: Correcting a boss's fallacy in a meeting is doubly penalized (Category 22)
- **The response targets your traits**: Instead of engaging with your correction, people may dismiss it with "You're being too literal" or "That's not how normal conversations work" -- ad hominem directed at autistic characteristics

### 4.3 Where Autistic People Are Specifically Vulnerable

Not all fallacies exploit logic. Some exploit social/pragmatic processing:

| Fallacy | Why Autistic People Are Vulnerable |
|---------|-----------------------------------|
| **Tone policing** | Direct communication style is weaponized; autistic delivery is the "problem" |
| **Appeal to emotion** | Difficulty reading emotional manipulation as a tactic rather than genuine feeling |
| **Ad hominem targeting autistic traits** | Autistic characteristics used as grounds for dismissal |
| **Bandwagon** (when belonging-driven) | Desire for social acceptance can override logical detection |
| **False dilemma** | Documented tendency toward dichotomous thinking (mediated by intolerance of uncertainty; Inoue et al., 2023) makes binary frames feel natural |
| **Gaslighting** (compound) | Lifetime of being told you're wrong primes self-doubt; alexithymia makes it harder to name your own experience |

### 4.4 The Autistic "Bullshit Detector"

A community-recognized autistic strength: many autistic individuals have an acute ability to detect dishonesty, inconsistency, and manipulation. The systemizing mechanism (Baron-Cohen, 2017) actively searches for rule violations -- and fallacies are rule violations. This isn't intuition; it's pattern recognition applied to argumentation.

The challenge is not detection. It's **what to do with the detection**.

## 5. Response Strategies: Five Levels

### The Framework

| Level | Strategy | When to Use | Social Cost |
|-------|----------|-------------|-------------|
| **0: Let it go** | Don't engage | Low stakes, depleted energy, person won't change, no audience | None |
| **1: Redirect** | Steer back to substance without naming the fallacy | Most everyday situations | Very low |
| **2: Gentle challenge** | Question the reasoning without fallacy terminology | When the relationship matters and the issue matters | Low |
| **3: Direct but hedged** | Name the pattern with softening | When you have standing and the stakes justify it | Moderate |
| **4: Explicit** | Name the fallacy directly | Debate, academic discussion, trusted allies only | High |

### Examples at Each Level

**When someone uses a straw man ("So you want to delay the entire project indefinitely?"):**

| Level | Response |
|-------|---------|
| 0 | [Internal: I see what happened there. Not worth it.] |
| 1 | "Not quite -- I'm suggesting a phased rollout. What do you think about that specifically?" |
| 2 | "There's a middle ground between what I said and what you're describing. My specific proposal is [X]." |
| 3 | "I want to make sure we're responding to my actual proposal, which is narrower than that. I'm suggesting [X], not [Y]." |
| 4 | "That's a more extreme version of what I said. My actual position is [X]. Can we discuss that?" |

### The Energy Cost Calculation

Before correcting a fallacy, ask:

1. **Does it matter?** (Will the fallacy affect a real decision?)
2. **Will correction be heard?** (Is the person open to feedback?)
3. **What's the social cost?** (Public or private? Peer or authority?)
4. **Do I have the energy?** (Will this drain me? Cause rumination?)
5. **Is there a lower-cost alternative?** (Later? Different channel? Different person?)

This is not suppressing truth. It's **strategic deployment of energy** -- a skill that connects directly to the burnout management principles in Category 21.

## 6. The Fallacy of Calling Out Fallacies

An important meta-lesson: **over-identifying fallacies is itself a conversational failure.**

| Trap | What Happens |
|------|-------------|
| **The Fallacy Fallacy** | Assuming that because an argument contains a fallacy, the conclusion is false. (An argument can be badly reasoned AND have a true conclusion.) |
| **Weaponized labels** | Using fallacy names as a form of ad hominem: "That's just an appeal to emotion" dismisses someone's genuine feelings |
| **Social alienation** | Constantly naming fallacies positions you as the person who can't have a normal conversation |
| **Understanding vs. deploying** | The goal is to understand how persuasion works (for self-protection and comprehension), not to deploy fallacy labels as weapons |

The most powerful use of fallacy knowledge is **internal**: recognizing what's happening, deciding how to respond, and protecting yourself from manipulation -- without necessarily ever saying the word "fallacy" out loud.

## 7. Key Takeaways

1. **Fallacies are pragmatic tactics, not just logic errors.** They work by exploiting the social systems of language -- presupposition, face, hierarchy, relevance, common ground. Understanding the mechanism is more useful than memorizing the name.

2. **The correction trap is real.** Naming a fallacy costs more social capital than committing one. This asymmetry is why fallacies persist and why the "just call it out" advice fails in practice.

3. **Autistic people are enhanced fallacy detectors.** Research shows reduced susceptibility to framing effects, sunk-cost bias, and conjunction fallacy. The challenge isn't detection -- it's the pragmatic response.

4. **Vulnerability is domain-specific.** Autistic individuals resist logic-exploiting fallacies but may be vulnerable to social-exploiting ones (tone policing, emotional manipulation, gaslighting, bandwagon in belonging-driven contexts).

5. **Five response levels provide flexibility.** From letting it go to explicit identification, having a graduated framework prevents the all-or-nothing approach (stay silent or call it out directly) that the correction trap creates.

6. **Intentionality doesn't matter for response.** Whether a fallacy is deliberate or accidental, the pragmatic dynamics are the same. This removes the mind-reading burden.

7. **The best fallacy knowledge is internal.** Understanding fallacies protects you from manipulation and helps you comprehend what's happening in conversations. You rarely need to say the word "fallacy" out loud.
