# Concept Research: Chinese Syntax — Topic-Prominence, Ba/Bei Constructions, and Sentence Architecture in Contrast with English

## Overview

Chinese syntax operates on a fundamentally different organizing principle from English. In their landmark 1976 typological study, linguists Charles Li and Sandra Thompson classified the world's languages into four types based on whether they organize sentences primarily around **subjects** or **topics**. They identified English as a quintessential **subject-prominent** language, where every sentence requires a grammatical subject that agrees with the verb and occupies a structurally defined position. Chinese, by contrast, was classified as a **topic-prominent** language, where the sentence is organized around a **topic** (what the sentence is about) followed by a **comment** (what is said about that topic) — and the topic need not be the grammatical subject, need not be an argument of the verb at all, and can be virtually anything that the speaker wants to foreground as the frame of reference for the statement.

This difference is not merely a stylistic preference — it produces sentence structures that have no natural English equivalent. The Chinese sentence 大象鼻子长 (dàxiàng bízi cháng) literally translates as "Elephants, noses are long" — the topic (elephants) sets the frame, and the comment (noses are long) makes a statement within that frame. There is no grammatical relationship between "elephants" and "long" — elephants are not the subject of "are long" (noses are). English cannot naturally express this without restructuring: "As for elephants, their noses are long" or "Elephants have long noses." The Chinese structure requires no "as for," no possessive "their," no restructuring — the topic simply sits at the front and the comment follows, connected by pragmatic relevance rather than syntactic agreement.

For the pattern-oriented learner, Chinese syntax rewards understanding a small number of powerful structural templates. The base word order is SVO (Subject-Verb-Object), just like English, which provides a familiar anchor. But layered on top of this are several distinctive constructions — the **topic-comment** frame, the **把 (bǎ) disposal construction** that brings objects before the verb, the **被 (bèi) passive construction**, **serial verb constructions** that chain multiple verbs together, an elaborate **complement system** (resultative, directional, potential), and ubiquitous **sentence-final particles** that modulate mood. Each of these is a learnable pattern, and together they form a coherent syntactic architecture that is elegant in its consistency: information flows from the known to the new, from the general frame to the specific assertion, from the topic to the comment.

## Core Components

### Component 1: Topic-Prominence — The Topic-Comment Structure

- **Purpose**: Organize sentences around "what we're talking about" (topic) + "what we say about it" (comment), rather than around "who does what" (subject-predicate)
- **Structure**: In a topic-prominent language, the topic occupies the sentence-initial position and establishes the frame of reference. The comment that follows provides information relevant to that frame:

  **Pattern**: TOPIC + COMMENT (where the topic may or may not be the grammatical subject)

  **Type 1 — Topic = Subject (overlaps with English SVO):**
  - 我很高 (wǒ hěn gāo) = "I am tall" — 我 (I) is both topic and subject
  - This feels natural to English speakers — the topic happens to be the grammatical subject

  **Type 2 — Topic ≠ Subject (distinctly Chinese):**
  - 大象鼻子长 (dàxiàng bízi cháng) = "[As for] elephants, noses are long"
  - Topic: 大象 (elephants) — not the subject of 长 (long)
  - Subject of comment: 鼻子 (noses) — grammatically, it is noses that are long
  - 这本书我已经看了 (zhè běn shū wǒ yǐjīng kàn le) = "[As for] this book, I have already read [it]"
  - Topic: 这本书 (this book) — the object, promoted to topic position
  - Subject: 我 (I) — the grammatical subject follows the topic
  - 中文语法不太难 (Zhōngwén yǔfǎ bú tài nán) = "[As for] Chinese, the grammar isn't too hard"
  - Topic: 中文 (Chinese) — a general frame
  - Subject of comment: 语法 (grammar) — what is actually "not hard"

  **Type 3 — Topic with no grammatical connection to comment:**
  - 那场火幸好没有人受伤 (nà chǎng huǒ xìnghǎo méiyǒu rén shòushāng) = "[As for] that fire, fortunately no one was injured"
  - The topic (fire) has no syntactic role in the comment — it merely sets the contextual frame

  **Li & Thompson's Four-Way Typology (1976):**
  - **Subject-prominent only** [+Sp, -Tp]: English, French, German — subjects are obligatory, topics are marked ("as for X...")
  - **Topic-prominent only** [-Sp, +Tp]: Chinese, Lisu — topics are primary, subjects are optional
  - **Both** [+Sp, +Tp]: Japanese, Korean — have both a subject marker (が) and a topic marker (は)
  - **Neither** [-Sp, -Tp]: Tagalog, Illocano — different organizing principles altogether

- **ENGLISH COMPARISON**: English sentences REQUIRE a grammatical subject, even a dummy one: "It is raining" (what is "it"?), "There are three books" ("there" is a placeholder, not a location). Chinese never needs dummy subjects — 下雨了 (xià yǔ le, "rain falling" = "It's raining") has no subject at all. English CAN topicalize, but it requires explicit marking: "As for elephants, their noses are long" — the "as for" signals departure from normal structure. In Chinese, topic-comment IS the normal structure; no marker is needed. The topic simply sits at the beginning, and the hearer understands it as the frame.

### Component 2: Basic Word Order — SVO with Flexibility

- **Purpose**: Define the default and variant word orders for Chinese sentences
- **Structure**: The basic order is Subject-Verb-Object (SVO), the same as English — but with important modifications:

  **Default SVO:**
  - 我吃苹果 (wǒ chī píngguǒ) = "I eat apples" — S(我) V(吃) O(苹果)
  - 她学中文 (tā xué Zhōngwén) = "She studies Chinese" — S(她) V(学) O(中文)

  **Time words precede the verb (before the VP or at sentence beginning):**
  - 我昨天去了商店 (wǒ zuótiān qù le shāngdiàn) = "I yesterday went to the store"
  - English: "I went to the store yesterday" — time word at the END
  - Chinese: time words typically appear BEFORE the verb, either after the subject or at the sentence beginning

  **Location phrases precede the verb:**
  - 我在家吃饭 (wǒ zài jiā chī fàn) = "I at-home eat-food" = "I eat at home"
  - English: "I eat at home" — location at the END
  - Chinese: location appears BEFORE the verb

  **Adverbs and auxiliary verbs precede the main verb:**
  - 我很喜欢吃苹果 (wǒ hěn xǐhuan chī píngguǒ) = "I very like eat apples" = "I really like eating apples"
  - 我不想去 (wǒ bù xiǎng qù) = "I not want go" = "I don't want to go"

  **General Chinese sentence template:**
  - (Time) + (Subject) + (Negation/Adverb) + (Auxiliary) + Verb + (Complement) + (Object) + (Sentence-final particle)

- **ENGLISH COMPARISON**: Both languages are SVO, which creates a familiar starting point. The major word-order difference is the position of TIME and PLACE phrases: English puts them at the end ("I went to the store yesterday"), Chinese puts them before the verb ("I yesterday went to the store"). Chinese follows a consistent principle: **modifiers precede what they modify** — this applies to time modifying verbs, adjectives modifying nouns, and adverbs modifying verbs. English is less consistent, sometimes pre-modifying (adjective + noun: "big house") and sometimes post-modifying (verb + adverb: "ran quickly").

### Component 3: The 把 (bǎ) Disposal Construction

- **Purpose**: Bring the object BEFORE the verb to emphasize what happens to it — marking the object as something being "disposed of," affected, or transformed by the action
- **Structure**: The 把 construction rearranges SVO to S-把-O-V(+complement):

  **Pattern**: Subject + 把 + Object + Verb + Result/Complement

  **Examples:**
  - 我把书放在桌子上了 (wǒ bǎ shū fàng zài zhuōzi shàng le) = "I 把 book placed on table" = "I put the book on the table"
  - 他把窗户打开了 (tā bǎ chuānghù dǎ kāi le) = "He 把 window hit-open" = "He opened the window"
  - 我把作业做完了 (wǒ bǎ zuòyè zuò wán le) = "I 把 homework do-finish" = "I finished (doing) the homework"
  - 请把门关上 (qǐng bǎ mén guān shàng) = "Please 把 door close-shut" = "Please close the door"

  **Key requirements:**
  - The object after 把 must be **specific/definite** — not "a book" but "the book" or "that book"
  - The verb MUST have a complement or result — bare verbs are not enough (*我把书看 is incomplete)
  - The construction emphasizes the EFFECT on the object, not just the action itself

  **When to use 把:**
  - When the action causes a change in the object's state, position, or existence
  - When the result or manner of the action is important (the verb needs a complement)
  - When you want to foreground the object as the thing being affected

- **ENGLISH COMPARISON**: English has no structural equivalent. The closest is simply using active voice with emphasis on the object: "I put the book on the table." English CAN topicalize ("The book, I put it on the table"), but this feels marked and requires a pronoun copy. The 把 construction is a regular, grammaticalized pattern in Chinese that moves the object to a pre-verbal position while maintaining active-voice agency — something English cannot do without sounding clumsy. It represents a distinctive "disposal" perspective on transitive actions that English simply does not grammaticalize.

### Component 4: The 被 (bèi) Passive Construction

- **Purpose**: Express passive voice — the subject is the receiver of the action rather than the performer
- **Structure**: The 被 construction reverses agency:

  **Pattern**: Subject (patient) + 被 + (Agent) + Verb + Result/Complement

  **Examples:**
  - 杯子被打破了 (bēizi bèi dǎ pò le) = "Cup 被 hit-broken" = "The cup was broken"
  - 他被老师批评了 (tā bèi lǎoshī pīpíng le) = "He 被 teacher criticized" = "He was criticized by the teacher"
  - 我的钱包被偷了 (wǒ de qiánbāo bèi tōu le) = "My wallet 被 stolen" = "My wallet was stolen"
  - 蛋糕被她吃了 (dàngāo bèi tā chī le) = "Cake 被 her eaten" = "The cake was eaten by her"

  **Key features:**
  - The agent (who did the action) is OPTIONAL — can be omitted when unknown or unimportant
  - Like the 把 construction, the verb typically requires a complement or result element
  - Traditionally, 被 carried a **negative connotation** — something bad happening to the subject ("was hit," "was stolen," "was criticized"). In modern Chinese, neutral/positive uses are becoming more common under English influence, but the negative flavor persists in many contexts

  **Relationship to 把:**
  - 把 and 被 are mirror images of the same event:
  - 他把杯子打破了 (He 把 cup broke) — active disposal: He broke the cup
  - 杯子被他打破了 (Cup 被 him broken) — passive: The cup was broken by him
  - Same event, different perspective — 把 foregrounds the agent's action on the object; 被 foregrounds the object's experience of the action

- **ENGLISH COMPARISON**: English passive voice uses "be + past participle": "The cup was broken," "He was criticized by the teacher." English passive is very frequent and pragmatically neutral — used for information structure, not just negative events. Chinese 被 passives are LESS frequent in natural speech than English passives, because Chinese has other strategies for foregrounding objects (topic-comment, 把 construction). When a Chinese speaker DOES use 被, it often carries more weight — historically signaling adversity or loss of control.

### Component 5: Serial Verb Constructions (连动句, liándòng jù)

- **Purpose**: Chain two or more verbs in sequence to describe actions that are temporally or logically connected — without conjunctions, subordination, or auxiliary verbs
- **Structure**: Verbs appear in sequence, sharing a subject, with the actions understood as sequential, purposive, or simultaneous:

  **Pattern**: Subject + Verb1 (+ Object1) + Verb2 (+ Object2)

  **Types and examples:**

  **Sequential actions (first...then...):**
  - 我去商店买东西 (wǒ qù shāngdiàn mǎi dōngxi) = "I go store buy things" = "I go to the store and buy things"
  - 她站起来说 (tā zhàn qǐlái shuō) = "She stand-up say" = "She stood up and spoke"

  **Purpose (go to...in order to...):**
  - 我来中国学中文 (wǒ lái Zhōngguó xué Zhōngwén) = "I come China study Chinese" = "I came to China to study Chinese"
  - Purpose is expressed simply by sequencing — no "in order to," no infinitive marker

  **Means (by doing X, achieve Y):**
  - 我坐飞机去北京 (wǒ zuò fēijī qù Běijīng) = "I sit airplane go Beijing" = "I go to Beijing by airplane"
  - 他用筷子吃饭 (tā yòng kuàizi chī fàn) = "He use chopsticks eat food" = "He eats with chopsticks"

  **Accompaniment (while doing X, also do Y):**
  - 他听音乐写作业 (tā tīng yīnyuè xiě zuòyè) = "He listen music write homework" = "He does homework while listening to music"

- **ENGLISH COMPARISON**: English requires conjunctions ("and," "then"), infinitives ("to study"), prepositions ("by airplane"), or subordination ("while listening") to connect multiple actions. Chinese simply sequences the verbs — the logical relationship is inferred from the order and semantic content. This is a fundamentally more streamlined approach: where English needs connective grammatical machinery, Chinese relies on semantic transparency and temporal iconicity (the first verb in the sequence represents the first action in time or logic).

### Component 6: The Complement System (补语, bǔyǔ)

- **Purpose**: Express the result, direction, degree, or possibility of an action — attached AFTER the verb, providing information that English typically expresses through adverbs, prepositions, or separate clauses
- **Structure**: Chinese has an elaborate complement system that is one of its most distinctive syntactic features:

  **Resultative Complements (结果补语):**
  - Verb + Result = the outcome of the action
  - 看完 (kàn wán) = "see-finish" = "finish reading/watching"
  - 听懂 (tīng dǒng) = "listen-understand" = "understand (by listening)"
  - 写错 (xiě cuò) = "write-wrong" = "write incorrectly"
  - 吃饱 (chī bǎo) = "eat-full" = "eat until full"
  - 打破 (dǎ pò) = "hit-broken" = "break (by hitting)"
  - 做好 (zuò hǎo) = "do-good/done" = "finish doing" or "do well"
  - **Pattern**: The result complement is a single syllable appended directly to the verb, creating a compact verb-result unit

  **Directional Complements (趋向补语):**
  - Verb + Direction = movement associated with the action
  - Simple: 上 (up), 下 (down), 进 (in), 出 (out), 回 (back), 过 (across), 起 (up/rise)
  - Compound: 上来 (come up), 下去 (go down), 进来 (come in), 出去 (go out), 回来 (come back)
  - 走进来 (zǒu jìn lái) = "walk-enter-come" = "walk in (toward speaker)"
  - 拿出去 (ná chū qù) = "take-exit-go" = "take out (away from speaker)"
  - 跑上去 (pǎo shàng qù) = "run-ascend-go" = "run up (away from speaker)"
  - **Pattern**: Direction stacks on verb, with 来 (toward speaker) / 去 (away from speaker) as the final element

  **Potential Complements (可能补语):**
  - Insert 得 (can) or 不 (cannot) between verb and complement to express ability/possibility
  - 看得懂 (kàn de dǒng) = "can understand (by reading)"
  - 看不懂 (kàn bu dǒng) = "cannot understand (by reading)"
  - 吃得完 (chī de wán) = "can finish eating"
  - 吃不完 (chī bu wán) = "cannot finish eating"
  - 听得见 (tīng de jiàn) = "can hear"
  - 听不见 (tīng bu jiàn) = "cannot hear"
  - **Pattern**: Verb + 得/不 + Complement — a perfectly regular, productive pattern

  **Degree Complements (程度补语):**
  - Verb/Adjective + 得 + Evaluation
  - 跑得快 (pǎo de kuài) = "run DE fast" = "run fast"
  - 说得很好 (shuō de hěn hǎo) = "speak DE very good" = "speak very well"
  - 忙得没时间 (máng de méi shíjiān) = "busy DE no time" = "so busy there's no time"

- **ENGLISH COMPARISON**: English does not have a grammaticalized complement system like Chinese. Where Chinese says 听懂 (listen-understand), English uses a separate word or clause: "understand by listening." Where Chinese says 看不完 (read-not-finish), English says "unable to finish reading" — a much longer construction. The Chinese complement system is extraordinarily compact, packing result, direction, ability, and degree into verb-adjacent slots. This is one area where Chinese is DENSER than English — more information per syllable in these constructions.

### Component 7: Pro-Drop — Omitting Subjects and Objects

- **Purpose**: Allow the omission of pronouns (and sometimes nouns) when the referent is clear from context
- **Structure**: Chinese is a **pro-drop** (or null-subject/null-object) language — subjects and objects can be omitted when recoverable from context:

  **Examples:**
  - A: 你吃了吗？ (nǐ chī le ma?) = "Have you eaten?"
  - B: 吃了。(chī le.) = "[I] ate." — subject "I" is dropped
  - A: 你看了那本书吗？ (nǐ kàn le nà běn shū ma?) = "Did you read that book?"
  - B: 看了。(kàn le.) = "[I] read [it]." — BOTH subject and object are dropped
  - 下雨了 (xià yǔ le) = "Raining." — no subject at all (no "it")

- **ENGLISH COMPARISON**: English generally REQUIRES explicit subjects — "It is raining" needs "it" even though "it" refers to nothing. English allows object dropping in informal speech ("Did you read it?" "Yeah, I read [it]"), but subject dropping is ungrammatical in standard English (*"Am going to the store" is not acceptable). Chinese drops both freely when context supplies the referent. This makes Chinese conversational exchanges very concise — short answers are normal and grammatical, not rude or elliptical.

## How It Works

### Step 1: Topic Establishment

The speaker determines the topic — what the sentence is "about" — and places it at the beginning:

- Trigger: Speaker wants to establish a frame of reference
- Action: Place the topic (which may be the subject, object, or a contextual frame) in sentence-initial position
- Result: The listener knows the frame within which to interpret the comment

### Step 2: Comment Construction

The comment is built using SVO order (or a variant construction) and attached to the topic:

- Trigger: Topic is established; now information about it must be provided
- Action: Construct the comment using appropriate word order, verbs, complements, and particles
- Result: A complete topic-comment sentence

### Step 3: Construction Selection

For transitive events, the speaker selects the appropriate construction based on communicative focus:

- **Default SVO**: Neutral presentation — 我打开了窗户 (I opened the window)
- **把 construction**: Emphasize disposal/effect on object — 我把窗户打开了 (I 把 window opened)
- **被 construction**: Emphasize object as patient/sufferer — 窗户被我打开了 (Window 被 me opened)
- **Topic-comment**: Foreground the object as topic — 窗户我已经打开了 (Window, I already opened)

- Trigger: Different communicative goals for the same event
- Action: Select the construction that matches the intended information structure
- Result: Same propositional content, different pragmatic emphasis

### Step 4: Complement and Particle Addition

Supplements the core proposition with result, direction, degree, aspect, and mood:

- Trigger: The bare proposition needs specification
- Action: Add complements after verbs; add aspect particles (了/着/过); add sentence-final particles (吗/吧/呢)
- Result: A fully specified, pragmatically nuanced sentence

## Key Relationships

| From | To | Relationship Type | Description |
|------|-----|------------------|-------------|
| Topic-prominence | Sentence-initial position | assigns-to | The topic occupies the first position in the sentence |
| Topic-comment structure | Subject optionality | enables | Topics need not be subjects, so subjects become less structurally central |
| SVO base order | 把 construction | transforms | 把 rearranges SVO to S-把-O-V to foreground the object's fate |
| 把 construction | 被 construction | mirrors | 把 = active disposal, 被 = passive experience — same event, opposite perspectives |
| Serial verb construction | English conjunctions | replaces | Verb chaining replaces "and," "to," "by," "while" |
| Complement system | English adverbs/clauses | condenses | Chinese V+complement encodes what English says in multiple words |
| Pro-drop | Context dependence | requires | Omitting pronouns works only because context supplies the referent |
| Sentence-final particles | Mood/pragmatics | encodes | 吗/吧/呢/啊 mark questions, suggestions, and emotional tone at the sentence boundary |
| Word order rigidity | Isolating typology | compensates-for | Without inflection, word order must be consistent to preserve meaning |

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: Topic-Comment vs. Subject-Predicate — 大象鼻子长
- **Scenario**: A sentence that has no natural English equivalent in the same structure
- **Illustration**: 大象鼻子长 (dàxiàng bízi cháng) = "Elephants, noses long" — The topic (elephants) has NO grammatical relationship to the predicate (noses are long). English forces: "Elephants have long noses" (restructured) or "As for elephants, their noses are long" (marked topicalization).
- **Key Insight**: This is the clearest demonstration that Chinese and English organize information differently at the sentence level. Chinese says "X — [something about X]" without requiring syntactic agreement between X and the predicate. This is natural in Chinese, not a marked or literary construction.

### Example 2: Four Constructions, One Event — The Broken Cup
- **Scenario**: Showing how construction choice encodes perspective
- **Illustration**:
  - 他打破了杯子 (SVO: He broke the cup — neutral)
  - 他把杯子打破了 (把: He disposed of the cup by breaking — emphasizes his action on the cup)
  - 杯子被他打破了 (被: The cup was broken by him — emphasizes the cup's experience)
  - 杯子他打破了 (Topic-comment: The cup, he broke — the cup is the topic of discussion)
- **Key Insight**: Chinese offers four distinct structural perspectives on the same event, each encoding a different communicative emphasis. English achieves similar effects through voice (active/passive) and intonation, but with less structural variety.

### Example 3: Serial Verb Economy — 我坐飞机去北京
- **Scenario**: Demonstrating Chinese verb-chaining efficiency
- **Illustration**: 我坐飞机去北京 (wǒ zuò fēijī qù Běijīng) = "I sit airplane go Beijing" = "I go to Beijing by airplane." Six syllables, no conjunctions, no prepositions. English needs "I go to Beijing by airplane" — seven words plus the preposition "by" and the directional "to."
- **Key Insight**: Serial verb constructions make Chinese syntactically compact. The logical relationships (means, purpose, sequence) are inferred from the order of verbs and their semantic content — no connecting grammar needed.

### Example 4: Potential Complements — 看得懂 vs. 看不懂
- **Scenario**: The elegant verb-得/不-complement pattern
- **Illustration**: 看懂 (kàn dǒng) = "read-understand" = "understand by reading." Insert 得: 看得懂 (kàn de dǒng) = "CAN understand by reading." Insert 不: 看不懂 (kàn bu dǒng) = "CANNOT understand by reading." This pattern is perfectly regular and infinitely productive: 吃得完/吃不完 (can/cannot finish eating), 听得见/听不见 (can/cannot hear), 做得到/做不到 (can/cannot achieve).
- **Key Insight**: This is one of the most pattern-rich structures in Chinese — a single template that generates ability statements for ANY verb-result combination. English requires entirely different constructions for each: "can understand," "can't finish," "able to hear."

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: "Chinese word order is free/flexible because it's an isolating language."
  - **Reality**: Chinese word order is STRICT within each construction type. SVO, topic-comment, 把, 被, and serial verb constructions each have defined word orders that cannot be freely rearranged. The flexibility lies in choosing WHICH construction to use for a given event, not in scrambling words within a construction.

- **Misconception**: "Chinese has no passive voice."
  - **Reality**: Chinese has the 被 construction, which IS a passive voice. However, it is used less frequently than English passive and historically carries a negative connotation (something undesirable happening to the subject). Chinese has alternative strategies (topic-comment, pro-drop) that reduce the need for passive.

- **Misconception**: "The 把 construction is just SOV word order."
  - **Reality**: 把 is not a simple SOV rearrangement. It is a specific construction with requirements: the object must be definite/specific, the verb must have a complement or result, and the construction encodes "disposal" — an agentive action affecting a specific patient. Not all SVO sentences can be rephrased with 把.

- **Misconception**: "Topic = Subject in Chinese, just with different terminology."
  - **Reality**: Topics and subjects are structurally and functionally different. A subject has a syntactic relationship with the verb (agreement, case assignment in some languages); a topic has a PRAGMATIC relationship with the comment (relevance, aboutness). In Chinese, topics can be things that have NO grammatical relationship with the verb in the comment — as in 大象鼻子长, where "elephants" is not a syntactic argument of "long."

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **Double-把 sentences**: Rare but possible — 他把这本书把封面撕了 (He 把 this book 把 cover tore) — nesting disposal constructions for complex events
- **Long-distance pro-drop**: Chinese can drop referents across many sentences in a narrative, far beyond what English allows — creating potential ambiguity that is resolved by discourse context
- **被...给 variant**: Some speakers add 给 (gěi) after 被 for emphasis: 杯子被他给打破了 — a colloquial intensifier in the passive construction
- **Verb-copying construction**: When a verb needs both an object and a degree complement, Chinese copies the verb: 她说中文说得很好 (she speak Chinese speak-DE very-well) — the verb 说 appears twice because 得 must immediately follow the verb
- **Objects that precede verbs without 把**: Certain verbs (especially those of cognition/speech) allow unmarked object-fronting: 中文我不会说 (Chinese I cannot speak) — without 把, but with the object topicalized

## Related Concepts

- **Chinese as an Isolating Language**: The syntax chapter builds on the morphological foundations — word order is critical BECAUSE there is no inflection
- **Chinese Phonology**: Tone sandhi interacts with syntactic phrasing — sandhi boundaries often align with syntactic boundaries
- **Chinese Classifier System**: Classifiers interact with the 把 construction (把 + [number + classifier + noun]) and the topic-comment structure
- **Chinese Semantics and Compound Formation**: Resultative complements are closely related to compound word formation — many V+R combinations lexicalize into fixed compound verbs
- **Japanese syntax**: Also topic-prominent (with the explicit topic marker は "wa"), but SOV base order — providing a cross-linguistic comparison with Chinese's SVO
- **Pragmatics and information structure**: Topic-prominence is fundamentally a pragmatic/discourse phenomenon — what the speaker chooses as "given" vs. "new" information
