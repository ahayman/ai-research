# Information Structure and Autism: Research Document

## For the English Pragmatics Learning Hub (Category: Information Structure)

**Scope**: How autistic individuals process, produce, and manage information structure -- the system by which speakers package what's "given" vs. "new," what's the topic vs. the focus, and how English uses word order, prosody, and syntactic constructions to signal this packaging.

**Frame**: Neurodiversity-affirming. Information structure is a learnable system. Where autistic individuals diverge from neurotypical patterns, the divergence is often systematic, frequently rational, and sometimes advantageous. The teaching gap is that nobody explains the system.

**Cross-references**: This document connects to Category 15 (Prosodic Pragmatics), Category 16 (Common Ground), Category 12 (Narrative and Discourse), Category 9 (Presupposition), and Category 10 (Relevance Theory) within the hub.

## 1. WHAT IS INFORMATION STRUCTURE AND WHY IT MATTERS

### 1.1 The Core Concept

Information structure is the system governing how speakers organize information within sentences and across discourse. Every sentence in English carries not just propositional content (what is said) but information-structural packaging (how it is presented relative to what the listener already knows).

The two foundational distinctions are:

1. **Given vs. New**: Information the speaker treats as already known to the listener (given) vs. information presented as not yet known (new). English marks this primarily through prosody -- new information receives stress, given information is deaccented.

2. **Topic vs. Focus**: The topic is what the sentence is about (the anchor); the focus is the new, informative, or contrastive part (the point). English marks this through word order, syntactic constructions (clefts, passives, topicalization), and prosody.

These distinctions interact with a third concept:

3. **Comment**: What is said about the topic. In "As for John, he passed the exam," "John" is the topic and "he passed the exam" is the comment containing the focus.

### 1.2 Why Information Structure Matters for Communication

Information structure is the sentence-level manifestation of common ground tracking (Category 16). Every time a speaker constructs a sentence, they must implicitly decide:

- What does the listener already know? (Mark it as given.)
- What is new to the listener? (Mark it as new.)
- What is the sentence about? (Establish the topic.)
- What is the point of saying this? (Mark the focus.)

Getting this packaging wrong doesn't change the propositional content of a sentence -- "John ate the cake" and "The cake was eaten by John" and "It was John who ate the cake" and "What John ate was the cake" all describe the same event. But they package the information differently for the listener. Using the wrong packaging creates processing difficulty, confuses listeners about what the point is, and can make speech sound either patronizing (re-stating what's known) or incomprehensible (assuming shared knowledge that isn't shared).

### 1.3 How English Marks Information Structure

English uses four primary mechanisms:

**Prosody (stress and intonation)**:
- New information receives nuclear stress (the main pitch accent in the intonation phrase)
- Given information is deaccented (reduced stress)
- Contrastive focus receives extra prominence
- Example: "I walked to YOUR house" (contrastive focus on "your" -- correcting an assumption about whose house)

**Word order**:
- English has a default Subject-Verb-Object order
- Departures from default order serve information-structural purposes
- Fronting/topicalization: "This book, I really enjoyed" (topic establishment)
- Right-dislocation: "She's amazing, your sister" (afterthought/clarification)

**Syntactic constructions**:
- It-clefts: "It was JOHN who ate the cake" (focus on John)
- Wh-clefts (pseudo-clefts): "What I need is a vacation" (focus on "a vacation")
- Passivization: "The cake was eaten by John" (topic shift to cake)
- Existential "there" constructions: "There's a cat on the roof" (introducing new referent)

**Referential choice**:
- Full noun phrases for new or reintroduced referents: "A woman walked in"
- Pronouns for given, activated referents: "She sat down"
- Definite articles for referents assumed to be identifiable: "The woman"
- Indefinite articles for new referents: "A woman"

## 2. AUTISTIC PROCESSING OF INFORMATION STRUCTURE: THE RESEARCH

### 2.1 The Prosodic Marking of Focus: The Paul et al. (2005/2008) Studies

The most directly relevant study on autism and information structure is Paul, Augustyn, Klin, and Volkmar's research on prosody and information structure in high-functioning adults with autism.

**Study design**: Adults with ASD and controls produced sentences where target words appeared in either topic (given) or focus (new) position, at both sentence-initial and sentence-final locations. Acoustic analysis measured pitch range (falls and rises) and duration.

**Key findings** (Paul et al., 2005, Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders; extended in Shriberg et al., 2001; reported in PMC3312270):

The study identified **two distinct subgroups** based on language ability:

**Group A-highL (high language functioning, including Asperger's profiles):**
- Used **larger pitch ranges than controls** across all conditions
- But did **not vary pitch as a function of information structure** -- they failed to differentiate focus words from topic words prosodically
- They applied wide pitch excursions indiscriminately, meaning their speech sounded expressive but did not direct listener attention to the informational point
- "Indiscriminant use of large pitch excursions does not direct the listener's attention to focus words"

**Group A-moderateL (moderate language functioning):**
- Used **smaller pitch ranges than controls** (the classic "monotone" pattern)
- But actually **marked information structure appropriately** in sentence-initial positions (larger pitch falls for focus than topic, p = 0.03)
- Their marking broke down in sentence-final positions

**Controls:**
- Consistently produced larger pitch variations for focus vs. topic words across all positions
- Effect sizes d = 0.96 to d = 1.2 -- robust and reliable marking

**Critical theoretical implication**: These findings reveal a paradox. The group that sounds more "normal" (A-highL, with wider pitch range) is actually worse at using prosody communicatively for information structure. The group that sounds more "autistic" (A-moderateL, with narrower pitch range) is actually using prosody more functionally. This underscores that what listeners perceive as "good prosody" and what constitutes functionally communicative prosody are not the same thing.

### 2.2 Contrastive Stress Production and Comprehension

Research on contrastive stress -- a specific type of focus marking -- reveals consistent patterns across multiple studies.

**Production deficits** (Patel et al., 2023, PMC10395513):
- ASD group: Mean accuracy 11.77/16 on contrastive stress expression vs. Control: 14.23/16 (F(1,50) = 8.96, p = 0.004, effect size eta-squared = .15)
- ASD parents also showed reduced accuracy (14.02/16 vs. 15.13/16; p = 0.007), suggesting a heritable component
- Prosodic imitation was the most impaired domain: ASD mean 9.33/16 vs. Control 14.79/16 (F(1,51) = 30.08, p < 0.001, effect size eta-squared = .37)

**Comprehension deficits** (same study):
- ASD group: 12.90/16 on understanding contrastive stress vs. Control: 14.90/16 (F(1,49) = 10.81, p = 0.002)
- Greater ASD symptom severity was associated with poorer contrastive stress understanding (r = -0.42, p = 0.002)

**The processing speed factor** (Su et al., 2025, MDPI Languages):
- Autistic children could discriminate prosodic forms and could interpret contrastive pitch accent
- But showed **significantly slower reaction times** during processing
- Speed of contrastive pitch accent processing was significantly associated with pragmatic language skills and autism symptom severity
- Implication: The issue may be a processing speed penalty rather than inability -- in real-time conversation, the meaning encoded by stress may arrive too late

**The development factor** (Segal et al., 2017, Folia Phoniatrica et Logopaedica):
- ASD adolescents showed reduced overall performance vs. age-matched peers
- But showed similar performance on psychoacoustic discrimination and basic contrastive contexts
- ASD participants actually performed **better** on correction contexts (where contrastive stress signals a mistake)
- Conclusion: "Comprehension of narrow focus in adolescents with ASD is related to their cognitive-linguistic abilities and not to the autistic condition itself or to its severity"

### 2.3 Neural Processing Differences

The neural underpinnings of prosody processing in autism reveal important mechanism-level insights (PMC3461129):

- Autistic individuals show **more generalized neural activation** when processing prosody -- recruiting additional brain regions including bilateral parahippocampal gyrus, medial frontal gyrus, and superior temporal gyrus
- Despite this expanded recruitment, behavioral accuracy was over 87% on comprehension items
- The broader activation pattern suggests "less automaticity in language processing" -- autistic brains achieve similar accuracy but require more cognitive resources to do so
- This has direct implications for information structure: if prosodic processing consumes more cognitive resources, fewer resources remain for the higher-level pragmatic computation of what the prosodic signal means for information packaging

### 2.4 The Syntax-Pragmatics Interface: Focus Constructions

Research on the interface between syntax and pragmatics in autism provides evidence about how information structure affects syntactic choices (PMC4938855, Arosio et al.):

**Key findings from Italian-speaking children with ASD:**
- ASD children performed similarly to controls on syntactically complex structures (clitic left dislocation, passives)
- But showed significant deficits specifically when pragmatic knowledge was required to select appropriate forms
- In focus structures, ASD children produced clitic pronouns (appropriate for given/topic information) in contexts that required full noun phrases (appropriate for focused/new information): 71.5% accuracy vs. TD 88.9% (F(1,38) = 4.252, p = 0.046, effect size eta-squared = 0.106)
- Critically, the difficulty was not with syntactic complexity -- it was with mapping between information status (focus vs. topic) and the appropriate syntactic form

**Theoretical interpretation**: The deficit is at the "interface of syntax with pragmatics and prosody," not within core syntax. Children with ASD can build complex syntactic structures. What they struggle with is recognizing that a particular information-structural context (this is focused/new) requires a particular syntactic choice (use a full NP, not a pronoun). This is exactly the kind of system-level rule that can be made explicit.

## 3. REFERENTIAL EXPRESSIONS: THE INFORMATION STRUCTURE OF REFERRING

### 3.1 The Given-New Problem in Reference

Choosing how to refer to entities is one of the most pervasive information-structure tasks in language. Every time a speaker mentions a person, object, or concept, they must choose from a hierarchy of referential expressions:

- **Full name or description** ("the tall woman in the red coat") -- for new or reintroduced referents
- **Definite NP** ("the woman") -- for referents assumed identifiable
- **Pronoun** ("she") -- for highly activated/given referents
- **Zero/ellipsis** (implied subject) -- for maximally given referents

The choice is governed by information status: how activated/accessible the referent is in the listener's mental model.

### 3.2 Autistic Over-Specification: The Research

Research consistently finds that autistic speakers tend toward **over-specification** -- using more explicit referential forms than the discourse context requires.

**Arnold, Bennetto & Diehl (2009, PMC3668432)** -- Landmark study:

- Younger autistic children (ages 9-12) used fewer pronouns and more explicit names/descriptions, particularly in moderately constraining discourse contexts (referents mentioned 2-3 clauses prior)
- Age positively predicted pronoun use in the autism group (r = .60, p = .003) but not in controls -- suggesting developmental delay rather than persistent deficit
- Crucially, **in highly constraining contexts** (recently mentioned subject-position referents), autistic speakers converged with controls at ~96% pronoun use
- The three-way interaction (Group x Age x Recency) was significant: F(3,126) = 4.94, p = .003

**Key nuance**: Over-specification occurred specifically in the "middle ground" -- contexts where discourse status was moderately but not overwhelmingly clear. This suggests autistic speakers require a higher threshold of activation/certainty before using a reduced form.

**Theoretical explanations**:
1. **Elevated conservatism threshold**: Autistic speakers may require higher confidence that the referent is sufficiently activated before using a pronoun -- a rational strategy that avoids ambiguity at the cost of redundancy
2. **Accelerated activation decay**: Referent activation may drop below the pronoun threshold more rapidly in autistic individuals
3. **Production-centered model**: The authors argue this pattern supports a model where discourse rules and speaker-internal activation states substantially determine reference choice, and mentalizing sets decision thresholds rather than driving all choices

### 3.3 The Article System: Definite/Indefinite Choice

Research on article choice (definite "the" vs. indefinite "a") reveals a specific information-structure vulnerability.

**Schaeffer (2015, BU Conference on Language Development)**:
- Subgroups of Dutch-speaking children with ASD overgenerated the indefinite article in definite contexts
- They used "a cat" when the cat had already been introduced and "the cat" was required
- Researchers proposed these children "fail to calculate the pragmatic scalar implicature for definiteness"

**Arnold et al. (2023, JSLHR, PMC10555458)**:
- Autistic children were more likely to use definite NPs to introduce novel characters -- saying "the boy" when the character hadn't been mentioned yet and "a boy" was required
- This provides evidence that autistic children "are less likely to use rules related to definiteness and/or their listener's previous knowledge"
- Notably, there was no over-use of definiteness in indefinite contexts -- the errors were asymmetric

**What this reveals about information structure**: The definite/indefinite distinction is a fundamental information-structure signal. "The" presupposes the referent is identifiable (given or accessible); "a" introduces a new referent. Errors in both directions (using "a" for given referents, using "the" for new referents) suggest that the mapping between information status and article choice is not fully automatized -- not that the grammar is impaired.

### 3.4 Referential Coherence in Narratives

The narrative domain reveals information-structure challenges at a broader level.

**Peristeri et al. (2023, PMC10123896)** -- Comprehensive analysis of linguistic markers in narratives:
- ASC group produced significantly more "failed specific reference" errors (p = .003, effect size r = .52) -- cases where pronouns or inflections referred to specific individuals but the reference was unclear
- Specifically, 69% of ASC reference errors were "exophoric scope errors" -- relying on shared visual context rather than building internally coherent discourse representations
- ASC participants produced significantly fewer embedded clauses (16.8% vs. 42.9% of utterances; p = .0001, effect size = .636) -- suggesting less complex information packaging
- Critically, ASC and TD groups did not differ in morphosyntactic errors, internal state vocabulary, or definite/indefinite NP ratios -- the deficit was specifically in coordinating referential devices across discourse

**Harvey, Spicer-Cain, Botting & Henry (2025, JCPP)** -- Recent study of everyday narratives:
- Autistic adolescents showed vulnerability in maintaining global coherence
- Reduced use of cohesive linguistic devices such as referential links and causal conjunctions
- Less capability to organize stories around gist events
- An increase in referential anomalies was among the most consistent findings across narrative studies

### 3.5 The Double Empathy Angle on Over-Specification

Over-specification has a crucial double-empathy dimension. When an autistic speaker says "John went to the store. John bought some milk. John came home" instead of "John went to the store. He bought some milk. He came home," the autistic speaker is being maximally clear. But neurotypical listeners may interpret the repeated full name as:

- **Condescending** ("Do they think I can't follow who we're talking about?")
- **Pedantic** ("Why are they being so formal?")
- **Robotic or unnatural** (because NT English relies heavily on pronoun reduction)

Conversely, when autistic speakers do use pronouns in complex multi-referent discourse, they may produce ambiguous pronouns that neurotypical listeners find confusing -- because the autistic speaker's threshold for "sufficiently activated" differs from the listener's.

This is not a unilateral communication failure. It is a mismatch in information-structure calibration between two speakers who have different assumptions about what's "given enough" for reduction.

## 4. COMMON GROUND AND AUDIENCE DESIGN: THE INFORMATION STRUCTURE CONNECTION

### 4.1 Common Ground as the Foundation of Information Structure

Information structure fundamentally depends on common ground -- the shared knowledge between interlocutors. To mark something as "given," a speaker must believe the listener already knows it. To mark something as "new," a speaker must believe the listener doesn't. Every information-structural decision is implicitly an audience design decision.

### 4.2 Research on Common Ground in Autism

**Nadig, Vivanti & Ozonoff (2009, PMC4764348)** -- The landmark study:

- TD adolescents shortened their narratives by ~12% when sharing knowledge with listeners (referential shortening)
- Autistic adolescents showed **no such effect at the group level**
- However, autistic participants over age 15 DID demonstrate referential shortening -- younger ones did not
- Social skills (not IQ or vocabulary) correlated with shortening ability (r = -0.56)
- Social skills and age together predicted 45% of variance in referential shortening within the autism group
- Crucially, despite absent implicit adjustment, autistic teens showed explicit awareness of common ground -- they made equally infrequent explicit references to shared content

**Interpretation**: The researchers propose that successful common ground use requires substantial information processing resources. Autistic individuals may possess awareness of what's shared but lack sufficient cognitive capacity for **implicit, automatic implementation** -- a complexity-dependent challenge rather than absent pragmatic understanding.

### 4.3 Working Memory and Information Structure

**Schuh, Eigsti & Mirman (2016, Autism Research)** -- Discourse comprehension study:
- Youth ages 8-17 with high-functioning ASD (n=13) and TD (n=22) completed common ground tasks under varying working memory loads
- All participants were slower when considering privileged (unshared) information
- This effect was greatest during high WM load trials
- The ASD group was more likely to fixate competing (non-target) shapes
- Groups did not differ in Theory of Mind per se
- Key conclusion: "WM capacity constrains common ground representation and impacts pragmatic skills in ASD" -- the difficulty is domain-general cognitive load, not social cognition alone

This finding is crucial for information structure. If working memory constrains the ability to track what's given and what's new -- and autistic individuals have higher cognitive load from processing social information (as the neural prosody studies suggest) -- then information-structure packaging becomes harder not because the system is unknown but because the computational demands of real-time implementation exceed available resources.

### 4.4 The Geelhand et al. (2025) Referential Communication Study

A groundbreaking study by Geelhand, Papastamou, Jaspard & Kissine (Autism, 2025) examined referential communication across neurotype-matched and mixed dyads:

- 134 adults in three dyad types: 23 autistic pairs, 23 non-autistic pairs, 21 mixed pairs
- Participants were unaware of partners' neurotype
- In mixed dyads, autistic Directors were **more verbose** than non-autistic Directors -- they provided more information, used longer descriptions
- This occurred in both written (chat) and oral (microphone, no video) conditions
- Same-neurotype dyads (both autistic or both non-autistic) showed more efficient communication

**Information-structure interpretation**: In mixed-neurotype interactions, autistic speakers appear to compensate for uncertainty about shared knowledge by increasing specification -- a rational information-structure strategy when you're unsure what the listener's model contains. This is not a deficit; it's an adaptive response to communicative uncertainty.

## 5. WEAK CENTRAL COHERENCE AND INFORMATION ORGANIZATION

### 5.1 The WCC Framework

Uta Frith's Weak Central Coherence theory (1989) proposes that autistic individuals show a cognitive style characterized by attention to local details at the expense of global integration. In language, this manifests as:

- Less spontaneous use of sentence context to disambiguate words
- Difficulty organizing information around a "gist" or main point
- Preserved or enhanced processing of individual elements with reduced integration across elements

### 5.2 WCC and Information Structure

WCC has direct implications for information structure:

**Hierarchical information packaging**: Information structure requires hierarchical organization -- some information is backgrounded (given/topic), some is foregrounded (new/focus), and the relationship between them creates the discourse structure. If WCC biases processing toward individual elements rather than hierarchical relationships, the result may be:

- Treating each piece of information as equally weighted rather than distinguishing given from new
- Difficulty maintaining a topic across multiple sentences while varying the focus
- Producing narratives that are a series of details rather than a structured argument with foregrounded and backgrounded information

**Context-sensitivity**: Research shows autistic individuals are less likely to use sentence context spontaneously to inform pronunciation of homographs (Frith & Snowling, 1983) or to select story-appropriate words. This suggests that the "given" status of information -- which depends on processing prior context -- may not be computed as automatically.

**However**: More recent research complicates the WCC picture. Studies show autistic individuals CAN process globally when instructed to do so -- they simply default to local processing when no instructions are provided. This suggests the issue is not inability but default processing mode, which has implications for teachability.

### 5.3 Monotropism as an Alternative Framework

Monotropism theory (Murray, Lesser & Lawson, 2005) offers a complementary perspective. If autistic cognition is characterized by intense focus on a narrow attentional field ("attention tunneling"), then:

- Within the tunnel, information processing may be deeper and more detailed than NT processing
- Outside the tunnel, information may not be tracked or integrated
- For information structure, this means the autistic speaker may have intensely detailed knowledge of their own topic/focus but less awareness of what the listener has been tracking

This could explain both over-specification (providing detailed information about what's in the attention tunnel without calibrating to the listener's knowledge state) and under-specification (failing to register that information outside the tunnel was not shared with the listener).

## 6. NARRATIVE COHERENCE: WHERE INFORMATION STRUCTURE MEETS DISCOURSE

### 6.1 The Information Structure of Narrative

Narratives are extended discourse structures that depend heavily on information management:
- Characters must be introduced as new, then tracked as given
- Events must be organized with appropriate foregrounding (main storyline) and backgrounding (context, elaboration)
- Causal and temporal connections must link events coherently
- The listener's evolving mental model must be anticipated and accommodated

### 6.2 Autistic Narrative Patterns

Research consistently identifies specific information-structure related challenges in autistic narrative production:

**Macrostructure vulnerabilities** (across multiple studies):
- Reduced causal connectivity between key story events (Sah & Torng, 2015)
- Omission of central story components -- the "gist" events that carry the main information
- Frequent inclusion of idiosyncratic or off-topic details that disrupt the information hierarchy
- Difficulty maintaining global coherence -- organizing the narrative around a point

**Microstructure patterns**:
- Referential anomalies are the most consistent finding across narrative studies (Peristeri et al., 2023)
- Both overproduction of ambiguous pronouns and reduction of pronouns in favor of full definite NPs
- Reduced use of cohesive devices (referential links, causal conjunctions) that signal information-structure relationships
- Reduced production of embedded clauses (which subordinate information, a key information-packaging operation)

**What is NOT impaired**:
- Lexical diversity and vocabulary are often comparable to controls
- Basic morphosyntactic accuracy is typically preserved
- Use of internal state language (mental state verbs, emotion words) is variable but not consistently reduced
- Individual cohesion strategies (when used) are the same as those used by controls (Fine et al., 1994)

### 6.3 The "Too Much" and "Too Little" Problem

Autistic narratives often exhibit a characteristic pattern that maps directly onto information structure:

**Too much given information** (over-specification):
- Re-introducing characters by full name when a pronoun would suffice
- Providing background details that the listener already has
- Failing to compress known information

**Too little given information** (under-specification):
- Introducing referents with pronouns ("he did it") without establishing who "he" is
- Using definite reference ("the man") for characters not yet introduced
- Assuming shared knowledge of visual context that the listener doesn't have (exophoric reference errors)

**Not enough new information structure**:
- Failing to foreground the "point" of the narrative
- Giving equal weight to main events and peripheral details
- Not marking which information is the focus vs. background

This pattern suggests not a single deficit but a calibration problem: the system for mapping information status onto linguistic form is present but not finely tuned to the listener's actual knowledge state.

## 7. THE DOUBLE EMPATHY PROBLEM AND INFORMATION STRUCTURE

### 7.1 Mutual Misreading of Information Packaging

The double empathy problem (Milton, 2012) is directly relevant to information structure. Research by Williams, Wharton & Jagoe (2021, Frontiers in Psychology) used relevance theory to reframe autistic pragmatic differences as mutual failures in establishing shared relevance.

**Key findings from their naturalistic conversation study**:

- In autistic-autistic conversations (even between strangers): significantly increased flow, rapport, and intersubjective attunement; shorter, more coherent turns; natural turn-taking
- In autistic-non-autistic conversations (even with familiar partners): long monologic turns, parallel dialogues, minimal shared enthusiasm
- One participant (A6) showed dramatically improved speech fluency when talking with another autistic person -- stumbles and restarts "almost entirely disappeared"

**Information-structure interpretation**: When both speakers share similar information-structure conventions -- similar thresholds for given/new marking, similar expectations about what needs to be specified -- communication flows. When conventions mismatch, both sides work harder and less effectively. This is not a unilateral deficit.

### 7.2 When Autistic Information Packaging Is Misread

Specific ways neurotypical listeners misinterpret autistic information structure:

**Over-specification read as condescension**:
- Autistic speaker provides detailed, fully specified information
- NT listener interprets: "They think I'm stupid" or "They're being pedantic"
- Actual autistic intention: maximizing clarity, treating listener's knowledge as uncertain, providing all relevant information
- Research supporting this: Neuroclastic (2023) documents autistic adults frequently being told they are "condescending" when they intend to be clear

**Under-specification read as incoherence**:
- Autistic speaker assumes shared knowledge that isn't shared (possibly from intense engagement with a topic)
- NT listener interprets: "They're not making sense" or "They jumped topics"
- Actual autistic process: within the monotropic attention tunnel, the connection between ideas is clear; outside it, the links are not signposted

**Flat prosody read as disengagement**:
- Autistic speaker communicates with reduced prosodic variation (the A-moderateL pattern)
- NT listener misses focus markers and interprets: "They don't care about this" or "They're bored"
- Actual autistic state: may be deeply engaged but not signaling engagement through prosodic means

**Excessive prosodic variation read as "weird"**:
- Autistic speaker communicates with wide but non-functional pitch variation (the A-highL pattern)
- NT listener: hears animated speech but can't extract the information-structural signal from it
- Result: the speaker sounds "off" despite appearing expressive

### 7.3 When NT Information Packaging Is Missed by Autistic Listeners

The reverse also occurs:

**Stress-marked focus missed**:
- NT speaker uses contrastive stress: "I said I'd come to YOUR house" (correcting an assumption)
- Autistic listener may not process the stress quickly enough (Su et al., 2025 processing speed penalty) and miss the correction
- Result: the autistic listener may continue operating under the wrong assumption

**Presupposition in articles missed**:
- NT speaker says "Did you talk to THE teacher?" (presupposes a specific, shared referent)
- Autistic listener may not compute the presuppositional content of "the" and be unsure which teacher

**Given-status deaccenting missed**:
- NT speaker deaccents given information and stresses only the new part
- Autistic listener may not register what was deaccented, losing track of which information was treated as shared background

## 8. EXECUTIVE FUNCTION AND INFORMATION STRUCTURE

### 8.1 The Cognitive Demands of Information Packaging

Information structure requires multiple executive function operations simultaneously:

- **Working memory**: Tracking what has been said, what the listener knows, and what still needs to be communicated
- **Cognitive flexibility**: Shifting between speaker perspective (what I know) and listener perspective (what they know)
- **Planning**: Organizing information hierarchically -- deciding what to foreground, what to background, and in what order
- **Inhibition**: Suppressing information that is irrelevant or already known to the listener

Studies estimate 35-90% of autistic individuals experience significant executive function impairments (PMC6859507). These impairments have direct consequences for information-structure management.

### 8.2 Research on EF and Discourse Planning

**Narrative requires that a speaker suppress potentially irrelevant or confusing information** while maintaining what has been said and planning what still needs to be explained (ScienceDirect, Kuijper et al., 2022). Working memory has been found to influence narrative performance, as speakers must manage discourse status of multiple referents simultaneously.

The EF demands of information structure help explain why:
- Written communication may be easier for autistic individuals (allows more planning time, removes prosody demands)
- Structured communication formats (emails with clear sections, numbered points) may be preferred
- Real-time conversation creates the greatest information-structure challenges (simultaneous tracking, planning, and producing)

## 9. PREDICTIVE PROCESSING AND INFORMATION STRUCTURE

### 9.1 The Prediction Framework

Recent theoretical accounts frame autism as involving differences in predictive processing (Sinha et al., 2014; Van de Cruys et al., 2014). From this perspective:

- Language comprehension normally relies heavily on prediction -- listeners generate expectations about upcoming words and structures based on context
- Autistic individuals may have **attenuated prior expectations** (less influenced by top-down predictions) or **indiscriminately high precision weighting** of prediction errors (treating all mismatches as equally important)

### 9.2 Implications for Information Structure

Predictive processing differences have direct consequences for information structure comprehension:

**Information status prediction**: NT listeners use context to predict what referents are likely to be mentioned and how they'll be marked. If an autistic listener generates weaker predictions, they may not pre-activate "given" referents, making pronoun resolution harder.

**Focus prediction**: NT listeners predict where focus will fall in a sentence based on discourse context. Reduced prediction may mean autistic listeners don't pre-orient to the focus position, requiring more bottom-up processing of stress cues.

**Relevant research** (PMC12323288, 2025):
- Autistic children exhibited reduced prediction efficiency compared to neurotypical peers
- Evidenced by significantly lower proportions of fixations to target objects
- Both groups demonstrated predictions during comprehension and employed similar processing strategies
- Critically: "Individuals with ASD may predict upcoming linguistic information by using verb semantics but not pragmatic prosody" -- suggesting that semantic prediction is intact but pragmatic-prosodic prediction is impaired

This finding maps directly onto information structure: autistic listeners may predict WHAT is being talked about (semantic level) but not HOW the information is being packaged (pragmatic-prosodic level).

## 10. WHERE AUTISTIC INDIVIDUALS MAY EXCEL

### 10.1 Systematic and Explicit Reference

The autistic tendency toward over-specification, while sometimes socially costly, has genuine communicative advantages:

- **Reduced ambiguity**: Full NPs are never ambiguous in the way pronouns can be. "John went to the store and John bought milk" is unambiguous; "John went to the store and he bought milk" could (in some contexts) refer to someone else
- **Accessibility for all listeners**: Over-specified speech is actually easier to process for listeners who missed earlier context, have hearing difficulties, or are joining mid-conversation
- **Written communication clarity**: In email, documentation, and technical writing, the autistic preference for explicit reference aligns with professional best practices

### 10.2 Logical Information Ordering

Autistic individuals often default to topic-first organization -- stating the topic explicitly before providing the comment. This maps onto the "topic-comment" structure that linguists identify as the most basic information-structural pattern. Example:

- Autistic preference: "Regarding the meeting next Tuesday, I have a conflict."
- NT preference (may vary): "I've got a conflict -- the meeting next Tuesday?"

The explicit topic establishment is more processing-efficient for listeners in many contexts and is the recommended structure for technical writing, presentations, and formal communication.

### 10.3 Pattern Recognition in Information Structure Rules

Information structure is fundamentally rule-governed. The rules can be stated explicitly:
- New referents get indefinite articles; given referents get definite articles
- New information receives nuclear stress; given information is deaccented
- The passive promotes the patient to topic position; the active keeps the agent as topic
- Cleft constructions mark focused/new information explicitly

These rules are the kind of systematic patterns that autistic learners often excel at recognizing and applying once they are made explicit. The challenge is not in learning the system -- it is that the system was never made visible.

### 10.4 Written Communication Advantages

Research consistently shows autistic individuals prefer and excel in written communication:

- Written communication removes the prosody burden entirely -- information structure is marked through word order, punctuation, and formatting rather than stress and intonation
- Email and messaging allow time for information-structure planning that real-time conversation does not
- The autistic preference for explicit, detailed communication aligns with professional writing standards
- Many autistic individuals report that written communication "lets them think" without the processing demands of face-to-face interaction (Adultandgeriatricautism.com, 2023)

## 11. PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS AND STRATEGIES

### 11.1 Making the System Visible

The single most important intervention for information structure is making the implicit system explicit. This means teaching:

1. **The given-new distinction**: Every sentence treats some information as already known and some as the point. Learning to identify which is which -- in both production and comprehension -- is a learnable skill.

2. **Prosodic focus marking**: The word with the most stress is typically the focus (the new/important information). Practicing listening for nuclear stress and producing it intentionally can be structured as a pattern-recognition exercise.

3. **Referential choice rules**: Full NP for new referents, pronoun for highly activated given referents, definite article for identifiable referents. These can be taught as explicit decision trees.

4. **Syntactic construction choices**: Passive vs. active, cleft vs. canonical order -- each serves a specific information-structural function that can be articulated as a rule.

### 11.2 Audience Calibration Strategies

Since audience design (calibrating information packaging to the listener) is a core challenge, practical strategies include:

**The "Knowledge Check" Protocol**:
- Before providing information, explicitly assess: "Do you already know about X?"
- This converts implicit audience design into explicit inquiry

**The "Newspaper Lead" Structure**:
- Start with the most important/new information first (the focus)
- Then provide supporting details
- Mark background/given information explicitly: "As you know..." or "Building on what we discussed..."

**The "Link Back" Technique**:
- When introducing new information, explicitly connect it to shared knowledge: "Remember when we talked about X? Well, Y relates to that because..."
- This makes the given-new bridge visible instead of implicit

### 11.3 Workplace Applications

Information structure knowledge directly applies to professional communication:

**Email structure**:
- Subject line = topic
- First sentence = focus (the point/action item)
- Body = comment (supporting details)
- Bullet points for multiple new-information items
- "As discussed..." or "Following up on..." to mark given information explicitly

**Presentations**:
- Each slide should have one topic (stated in the title) and one focus (the point)
- Use explicit topic sentences: "I want to talk about X"
- Signal transitions between topics explicitly: "Moving on to..."
- Deaccent/minimize given information; highlight new information visually and verbally

**Meetings**:
- When speaking, state your topic before your point: "About the deadline -- I think we need an extension"
- When asking questions, mark whether you're asking about given information ("Just to clarify...") or introducing new information ("I wanted to raise something new...")

### 11.4 The Masking Warning

A critical note: teaching information structure should NOT be about teaching autistic individuals to "perform" neurotypical information packaging. The goal is:

1. **Understanding the system**: So you can decode how others are packaging information for you
2. **Strategic deployment**: So you can choose when and how to package information for a specific audience
3. **Self-advocacy**: So you can explain your own information-packaging preferences ("I tend to be very explicit -- that's a clarity preference, not condescension")

Research on masking/camouflaging (Hull et al., 2017; PMC5509825; PMC6483965) documents serious mental health costs -- exhaustion, anxiety, depression, identity erosion -- when autistic individuals suppress their natural communication style to match neurotypical expectations. A pragmatics learning hub must teach the system without demanding constant performance of it.

## 12. CONNECTIONS TO OTHER HUB CATEGORIES

### 12.1 Category 15: Prosodic Pragmatics
Information structure is realized primarily through prosody in spoken English. The stress patterns covered in Category 15 (contrastive stress, nuclear stress, deaccenting) are the phonological implementation of information-structural distinctions (given/new, topic/focus). Category 15 covers the mechanics; this category covers the system those mechanics serve.

### 12.2 Category 16: Common Ground
Common ground is the foundation on which information structure operates. To mark something as "given," you must believe it's in the common ground. To mark something as "new," you must believe it isn't. Category 16 covers the general process of establishing and tracking shared knowledge; this category covers how that knowledge determines sentence-level packaging.

### 12.3 Category 12: Narrative and Discourse Coherence
Information structure extends beyond single sentences to organize entire discourses. Narrative coherence problems documented in autistic speakers -- reduced causal connectivity, referential anomalies, difficulty maintaining global organization -- are manifestations of information-structure management across multiple sentences. Category 12 covers the broader discourse patterns; this category covers the sentence-level mechanisms that produce them.

### 12.4 Category 9: Presupposition
Presuppositions mark information as given/assumed -- they are a specific type of information-status assignment. The definite article presupposes identifiability; factive verbs ("realize," "know") presuppose the truth of their complement; clefts presuppose the existence of someone who did the action. Category 9 covers presupposition generally; this category covers how presupposition fits into the broader information-structure system.

### 12.5 Category 10: Relevance Theory
Relevance theory provides the cognitive processing model for how listeners determine what the speaker's intended focus and intended information packaging are. The "path of least effort to optimal relevance" is the mechanism by which listeners compute information structure in real time. Category 10 provides the processing engine; this category provides the packaging system the engine operates on.

### 12.6 Category 8: Deixis
Deictic expressions (pronouns, demonstratives, spatial/temporal terms) are fundamentally information-structure devices -- they index given/accessible referents and orient the listener's attention. Category 8 covers the forms; this category covers the information-structural logic that governs when each form is appropriate.

## 14. SUMMARY: THE TEACHING OPPORTUNITY

Information structure is perhaps the most directly teachable pragmatic system for autistic learners. Unlike many pragmatic domains that depend on real-time social inference, information structure is:

1. **Rule-governed**: Clear, articulable rules map between information status and linguistic form
2. **Systematic**: The given-new distinction, topic-focus structure, and referential hierarchy operate with high regularity
3. **Pattern-based**: Autistic pattern-recognition strengths can be leveraged to identify information-structure patterns in input
4. **Transferable to writing**: Many information-structure principles apply across modalities, and written communication is often an autistic strength

The research shows:
- Autistic individuals have the underlying linguistic competence for information structure
- Difficulties emerge at the interface between pragmatic knowledge and real-time implementation
- Processing speed, working memory load, and audience-modeling demands create bottlenecks
- With development (age), explicit instruction, and reduced processing demands (writing), information-structure performance improves
- The system can be taught as explicit rules rather than relying on implicit acquisition

The goal is not to make autistic information packaging "sound neurotypical." The goal is to make the system visible so autistic individuals can:
- Understand what neurotypical speakers are signaling through their information packaging
- Make informed choices about their own information packaging in specific contexts
- Advocate for their communication preferences when their natural packaging style is misread
- Leverage their strengths (explicit reference, logical ordering, written communication) as assets
