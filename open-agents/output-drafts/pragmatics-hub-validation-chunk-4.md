### 2. Mehrabian "7-38-55" framing (gesture-gaze file)
The gesture-gaze research file correctly calls out that Mehrabian's famous rule is widely misapplied and only covers the narrow case of conflict between verbal and nonverbal channels about attitudes/feelings. This is correct and well-framed. However, the same file then says "When nonverbal signals contradict verbal messages, people almost always trust the nonverbal message. This is well-established in research." That second claim is much stronger than Mehrabian's actual finding and is repeatedly treated in the literature as overstated. The research supports a tendency (not "almost always"), particularly for affective messages. This is a **WATCHLIST** copy-edit: consider softening "almost always trust" to "tend to trust" to avoid echoing the myth the same paragraph is correcting. Not a factual error so much as a rhetorical inconsistency.

### 3. A small cluster of unsourced numerical claims
Several specific numbers have no immediate citation pointing at a primary source:
- "approximately 800 milliseconds slower" (idiom processing eye-tracking, cited in the hub and humor files). The autism research file anchors this to an unspecified "2024 eye-tracking study" (Springer, cited as [Online Metaphor Comprehension Eye-Tracking Study (2024) — link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10803-024-06562-5]). The 800-ms figure is plausibly consistent with that study but the number itself isn't tied to a quote. **UNCERTAIN 7/10.** (Note: the figure is repeated in the hub in a form that conflates idiom and metaphor, though the underlying study is specifically about metaphors — this is a category-crossing issue flagged under findings for Cat 11 below.)
- "approximately 80% of autistic individuals use cooperative overlap" (topic-autism file, Section 3.4). Source given is an "Embrace Autism" blog post, not a peer-reviewed study. The 80% figure is popular on autism-community sites but I could not find a peer-reviewed primary source that grounds this specific percentage. **UNCERTAIN 5/10.** Recommend either sourcing to a primary paper or softening to "many autistic individuals use cooperative overlap."
- "Autistic individuals report laughing less, deriving less enjoyment from laughter" (nonverbal-autism file, Section 4). The source is cited as Nature *Scientific Reports* 2024, which is a real study on autistic laughter perception. The quantitative claim is broadly supported but the prose implies a stronger group-level effect than the study likely supports. **UNCERTAIN 7/10.**
- "Protodeclarative pointing may be absent or delayed" and "Protoimperative pointing is relatively intact" (gesture-gaze-autism). The research literature broadly supports this contrast (seminal studies go back to Baron-Cohen, Mundy, and others); the current citation is to Ramos-Cabo et al. 2021 rather than the original work. The claim itself is CONFIRMED as an orientation in the field, but the specific citation used does not originate the claim. **WATCHLIST** (consider adding a foundational Mundy/Baron-Cohen citation for the protodeclarative/protoimperative distinction).

### 4. Visual Dominance Ratio (nonverbal-gesture-gaze file)
The gesture-gaze file cites Dovidio and Ellyson (1982) and attaches specific percentages (~55% looking while speaking for high-status; ~40% while listening). The Dovidio & Ellyson VDR paradigm is real and is the correct citation, and the directional pattern (high-status look more while speaking, less while listening) is well-established. The specific "~55%" and "~40%" percentages are not reliably attached to Dovidio & Ellyson in my quick verification; they appear to be popularised-version numbers. The direction is correct; the exact percentages are **UNCERTAIN 7/10** as originating from that specific paper. Recommend: soften to "substantially more" and "substantially less" unless the user can point at a Dovidio & Ellyson table.
