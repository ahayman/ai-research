# Category 20: Digital Pragmatics — Real-Time Modalities

## The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

**Supplement to Category 20.** This file extends the main Category 20 document (text, email, social media, asynchronous chat) by covering the real-time digital modalities that were under-represented there: **video calls, voice calls, synchronous chat, and gaming voice**. These are now daily contexts for most autistic adults, and the post-2020 research literature on them is substantial enough to treat carefully.

Cross-links at the end of the file point to Cat 14 (Nonverbal Pragmatics), Cat 04 (Conversation Structure), Cat 19 (Phatic Communication), Cat 20 main (text/async), and relevant Manipulation Hub pages where real-time digital contexts appear.

## How to Read This Document

A phone call, a Zoom meeting, a Discord voice channel, and a rapid Slack DM exchange are not "the same conversation over different pipes." Each strips some communication channels while preserving or amplifying others. The pragmatic conventions that emerge on each modality are adaptations to **which channels are available** and **which channels have been removed**.

If you have ever felt that Zoom is somehow harder than face-to-face, that phone calls are somehow harder than Zoom, or that Discord voice with friends is somehow *easier* than any of them — you are not wrong, and you are not imagining it. The difference is structural. The channels are not the same.

This document walks through each modality as its own pragmatic context.

## 1. The Real-Time Digital Landscape

### 1.1 Real-Time Digital Is a Spectrum, Not a Category

There is no single thing called "real-time digital communication." There is a spectrum, sorted by **which channels are present** and **which are stripped or degraded**.

| Modality | Verbal | Prosody | Face | Gaze | Gesture | Body | Typing |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Face-to-face** | full | full | full | full | full | full | — |
| **Video call (camera on)** | full | full | degraded | mediated | torso-only | mostly absent | chat pane |
| **Video call (camera off)** | full | full | absent | absent | absent | absent | chat pane |
| **Voice call (phone, Discord voice)** | full | full | absent | absent | absent | absent | — |
| **Voice note (async)** | full | full | absent | absent | absent | absent | — |
| **Synchronous text (Discord/Slack/IM)** | full (written) | signalled via punctuation/emoji | absent | absent | absent | absent | typing indicators |
| **Gaming voice + game state** | full | full | absent | absent | absent | absent | avatar body, in-game actions |
| **Asynchronous text (SMS, email)** | full (written) | signalled | absent | absent | absent | absent | read receipts |

Three observations matter for everything that follows:

1. **Each modality preserves some channels and strips others.** The pragmatic implications of *which* channels are available change the rules of the interaction. You cannot "just act natural" across modalities, because the channels available for "acting" are different each time.
2. **Stripping a channel does not just remove information — it redistributes load onto the remaining channels.** On a voice call, prosody has to do everything that facial expression, gesture, and gaze would have done face-to-face. On synchronous text, punctuation and timing have to carry all of it.
3. **When a channel is *degraded* rather than absent, it is often worse than when it is absent.** A flattened video of a face is more cognitively expensive than a black box, because your perceptual system keeps trying to read it and keeps getting unreliable data (Bailenson, 2021).

### 1.2 Herring's CMDA Framework

The foundational framework for thinking about this is Susan Herring's **computer-mediated discourse analysis (CMDA)**, especially her "faceted classification scheme" (Herring, 2007, *Language@Internet* 4, Article 1). Herring lists **medium factors** that distinguish one computer-mediated environment from another:

| Medium factor | What it asks |
|---|---|
| **Synchronicity** | Is it real-time (both parties present at once) or asynchronous? |
| **Message transmission** | Is the message sent character-by-character, or all at once when you hit "send"? |
| **Persistence of transcript** | Does the conversation leave a record you can scroll back through? |
| **Size of message buffer** | How long can a single message be? |
| **Channels of communication** | Text, voice, video, images, reactions, which combinations? |
| **Anonymous messaging** | Can participants be pseudonymous? |
| **Private messaging** | Can subgroups have side conversations? |
| **Filtering** | Can messages be filtered, muted, blocked? |
| **Quoting** | Can you quote earlier messages explicitly? |
| **Format of message** | Plain text, rich text, markdown, embedded media? |

Herring's point is that no two platforms share all of these properties, and **pragmatic norms follow the medium factors**. Discord voice is not "just like" Zoom is not "just like" a phone call, because the medium factors differ. When someone says "Zoom etiquette" or "Discord culture," they are pointing at emergent norms shaped by those factors.

Herring (2001, 2004) developed CMDA as a method for studying computer-mediated interaction systematically — treating it as a legitimate object of linguistic analysis rather than a degraded form of face-to-face talk.

**For autistic readers**, the Herring framework is useful because it gives you a vocabulary for saying *which specific feature* of a platform is difficult for you. "Video calls are hard" is less useful than "I find the degraded gaze channel on Zoom harder than the absent gaze channel on a voice call, because my perceptual system keeps trying to read eye contact that isn't really there." Once you can name the feature, you can often find workarounds.

## 2. Video Calls as a Distinct Pragmatic Context

Video calls are worth the most attention because (a) they are the modality that most dramatically changed daily life from 2020 onward, (b) they are uniquely cognitively expensive, and (c) the "add a camera, it'll be like being in the room" premise is **wrong**, and the ways it is wrong matter for autistic adults specifically.

### 2.1 What Changes When You Add Video

Adding a camera to a voice call does not restore face-to-face. It creates a new modality with its own distortions.

**Gaze is now mediated through a camera, not the other person's eyes.** On a video call, to appear to look someone in the eye, you must look *into the camera lens*, not at the image of their face on the screen. But looking into the lens means you cannot see their face. Looking at their face means they see you looking slightly downward, past them. There is **no gaze position** on a standard video call that allows mutual eye contact as it works face-to-face. This is a structural feature of the modality, not a personal failing of any participant.

The consequence: "eye contact" on video is theatre. It looks like eye contact only when both people carefully glance at their cameras at the same moment, and it is not satisfying to either party.

**Facial expression is present but flattened.** At typical video-call bandwidth and framerate, micro-expressions (the sub-second cues that carry a lot of affective information face-to-face) are degraded or lost entirely. Lighting changes — someone's lamp flickering, a window behind them — dominate the visual field in a way they would not in person. Compression artifacts make expressions stutter.

**Turn-taking cues are degraded or delayed.** In face-to-face talk, the 200-millisecond gap between turns is cued by a complex cluster of signals: breath intake, gaze shift, small head turns, decreasing gesture amplitude, terminal pitch contour, syntactic closure. On video, several of these are absent (you cannot see torso or hands), degraded (gaze is mediated), or delayed (the audio arrives ~150–400 ms later than the speaker produced it, and audio and video may not be synchronised).

**Self-view is a continuous signal.** You can see yourself. You cannot see yourself in face-to-face conversation. The presence of a live mirror of your own face during every moment of talk is a continuous, effortful monitoring load — whether or not you consciously attend to it.

**Background becomes a signalling channel.** Home office vs. bedroom vs. kitchen vs. virtual background communicates class, formality, preparation, and privacy norms. "Where you are" is now legible to people you may not have invited to know.

### 2.2 Zoom Fatigue: Bailenson's Four Mechanisms

The best-known theoretical account of why video calls are exhausting is **Bailenson, J. N. (2021). "Nonverbal Overload: A Theoretical Argument for the Causes of Zoom Fatigue." *Technology, Mind, and Behavior*, 2(1).** Bailenson, the founding director of the Stanford Virtual Human Interaction Lab, proposes four (sometimes stated as five) mechanisms:

1. **Excessive eye gaze at close range.** On a gallery-view video call, multiple faces stare at you, at a distance that would, in face-to-face interaction, signal intimate interpersonal space. Your nervous system interprets "multiple faces at intimate distance, all facing you" as a social event of unusual intensity — except it is happening during a routine meeting.
2. **Cognitive load of sending and receiving nonverbal cues.** Producing the nonverbal signals that will read correctly on camera (exaggerated nodding, thumbs up, careful facial expression) requires conscious effort. So does parsing them when they arrive degraded at the other end. Face-to-face, most of this is automatic and cheap; on video it is deliberate and expensive.
3. **Seeing yourself constantly.** The self-view window is, effectively, a full-length mirror running for the duration of the call. Humans have never before been in a social situation where they watched their own face while talking, for hours at a time.
4. **Reduced mobility.** To stay in frame, you have to remain roughly fixed in one place, roughly centred in the camera. Face-to-face conversation normally involves subtle postural shifts, walking, turning, pacing. The cage of the camera frame removes those.

(Bailenson sometimes presents these as five by separating "mirror anxiety from seeing self" from "feeling physically trapped"; the substance is the same.)

Bailenson emphasised that his 2021 paper was a **theoretical argument, not an empirical test**. Follow-up empirical work has since accumulated — including the Zoom Exhaustion and Fatigue (ZEF) scale developed by Fauville, Luo, Queiroz, Bailenson, and Hancock (2021) in *Computers in Human Behavior Reports* — and generally supports the basic shape of the theory: video calls are more exhausting than voice calls, which are more exhausting than face-to-face, with the gap widening over meeting duration and meeting frequency.

### 2.3 Turn-Taking on Video Calls

Face-to-face conversation runs on a ~200 millisecond gap between turns (Sacks, Schegloff & Jefferson, 1974). That gap is the product of exquisitely tuned prediction: listeners plan their next turn during the current speaker's final phrase, using prosodic, syntactic, and visual cues to predict when the speaker will finish, then start speaking at almost the instant the speaker stops.

Video calls break this system. **Network latency alone is usually 150–400 milliseconds each way**, and audio and video are often desynchronised. Even with "low latency" optimisations, the round-trip from your mouth to the other person's ears is typically longer than the 200 ms gap that natural turn-taking runs on. The result:

- **Overlap is more common.** Both parties start talking at once, because neither can hear the other's turn-start in time to yield.
- **Pauses are longer and more awkward.** To avoid overlap, people wait — but "wait longer than 200 ms" cues "they are done," which triggers the other person to start, which then overlaps with the first speaker's continuation.
- **Repair sequences** ("no, you go—" "no, you—" "sorry—") happen more often.
- **Turn-taking feels off in a way nobody can quite name.** The subjective experience is of mild, constant conversational friction.

**Schoenenberg, K., Raake, A., & Koeppe, J. (2014). "Why are you so slow? — Misattribution of transmission delay to attributes of the conversation partner at the far-end." *International Journal of Human-Computer Studies*, 72(5), 477–487** ran the critical experiment. They showed that when a conversation partner's audio arrives delayed — even by as little as 1.2 seconds — listeners do not attribute the delay to the technology. They attribute it to the other person's **personality**. Delayed-audio partners were rated as less attentive, less extraverted, less conscientious, and less friendly. The higher the transmission delay, the more the other interlocutor was perceived as interrupting, inattentive, or unwilling to wait — when, in fact, the delayed interlocutor was behaving normally.

This matters enormously for autistic readers:

- If you are on a video call with poor connection and your partner experiences you as slow, hesitant, or unfriendly, **the cause may be the network, not your communication style.** They are not lying when they report the impression. They are having a genuine perceptual experience of delay. But the delay is a transmission artifact, not a property of you.
- The reverse is also true: partners whose audio arrives late at your end may feel unfriendly or inattentive to *you*, and the cause may be their connection, not their attitude.
- Autistic readers are already often told they are "slow to respond" or "don't pick up on cues." On a video call, network-induced lag will be **added** to any baseline processing time, and the combined effect will be attributed entirely to the person.

Workarounds emerging from practice:

- **Explicit turn-taking protocols.** "Raise hand" buttons, "over to you" verbal handoffs, chat-pane cues.
- **Mute culture.** Muting by default when not speaking, unmuting to talk. Reduces cross-talk but adds a coordination cost ("you're on mute").
- **Moderator role.** Someone in the meeting who explicitly calls on speakers. Works well for structured meetings, less well for social calls.
- **Waiting longer than feels natural.** Training yourself to count 1-2 before assuming the other person has finished.

See also: Boland, Fonseca, Mermelstein & Williamson (2022), "Zoom disrupts the rhythm of conversation," *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General* 151(6), 1272–1282, which empirically demonstrates lengthened inter-turn gaps on Zoom compared to face-to-face.

### 2.4 Video Calls for Autistic Readers

The net effect of video calls on autistic adults is **neither uniformly better nor uniformly worse** than face-to-face. It depends on which features of face-to-face were hardest for you in the first place.

#### 2.4.1 Why Video Is Sometimes Easier

- **You can control your environment.** Lighting, temperature, seating, sensory load, snacks, fidgets, stim toys, pacing (camera off), standing desk. All available. None of this is available in someone else's conference room.
- **A transcript or chat pane can run alongside.** If your processing of real-time audio is slower than the average, the chat pane becomes a second channel you can glance at for clarification, link-sharing, and "quick question while they're talking."
- **You can step out of frame.** "Be right back, grabbing water" is socially legible and easy. Leaving a face-to-face meeting for two minutes to regulate is much harder.
- **You can mute when overwhelmed.** You cannot mute yourself in person.
- **Processing time is available between speaking turns.** Nobody is watching your face closely enough (because the image is flattened) to notice a two-second pause. Face-to-face, a two-second pause reads as a long silence.
- **Camera off is an option.** Camera-off calls function as voice calls, which (see §3) have their own tradeoffs but strip out the self-view and gaze problems entirely.
- **Scripts and notes can be open.** You can have a list of "things I want to say," a question list, or a partial script visible next to the call window, invisible to the other party.

#### 2.4.2 Why Video Is Sometimes Harder

- **Self-view is hard to escape.** Most platforms let you hide self-view, but the option is buried, you forget, you turn it back on because you want to check your face. Masking load compounds: you are performing a face *and* checking the performance continuously.
- **Camera-mediated gaze is not satisfying for anyone.** As established above, there is no physically possible gaze position that gives "mutual eye contact" on a standard video call. But neurotypical people still **ask for "more eye contact"** from autistic participants on video, which is technically impossible to give. The request is grounded in a face-to-face intuition that does not transfer. Responses: (a) you can glance at the camera lens periodically, which produces the *appearance* of eye contact from the other side; (b) you can name the problem out loud ("video eye contact is weird for everyone, I'll glance at the camera occasionally but I'm mostly looking at your face on my screen"); (c) in autistic-friendly contexts you can simply not do it.
- **Flattened facial channel means more effortful reading, not less.** You might think "face is smaller and less clear → less to process." In practice, your perceptual system **works harder** to extract expression from a degraded signal than from either a clear one or no signal at all. Bailenson's "cognitive load of nonverbal sending/receiving" point is especially load-bearing here.
- **Background monitoring of oneself is a constant drain.** Am I in frame? Is my lighting okay? Is my hair okay? Does my background give away something I didn't mean to share? Is my pet about to walk across the frame? This runs as a background process for the entire call.
- **Audio problems hit processing.** Auditory processing differences mean that compressed, artifacted, or echoey Zoom audio may be substantially harder to parse than clean face-to-face audio. Captions help when available.
- **Meeting-on-meeting density.** Video fatigue is dose-dependent. Three one-hour video meetings back-to-back is a different experience than three one-hour face-to-face meetings, because of the mechanisms in §2.2.

#### 2.4.3 Evidence on Autistic Adults and Video Calls

The post-2020 research literature is thin but growing. Key findings so far:

- **Zolyomi, Begel, Waldern, Tang, Barnett, Cutrell, McDuff, Andrist & Morris (2019). "Managing Stress: The Needs of Autistic Adults in Video Calling." *Proceedings of the ACM on Human-Computer Interaction*, 3(CSCW), Article 134** reports on qualitative interviews with autistic adults about video calling. Themes include: self-view as both useful (for monitoring masking) and distressing; preference for camera-off when stressed; difficulty with group video calls specifically; reported benefits of asynchronous alternatives.
- **Yuruki, H. & Inoue, S. (2023). "Stress and benefits of video calling for people with autism spectrum disorders." *PLOS ONE* 18(4), e0283526** reports that autistic participants were more likely than typically developing controls to experience stress from light stimuli emitted by the screen and to find it harder to concentrate due to visual stimuli on-screen. **Benefits** were also reported: video calling reduced certain travel-related and in-person social demands.
- **Wehrle, Grice, Vogeley & Schulze (2023). "Turn-timing in conversations between autistic adults: Typical short-gap transitions are preferred, but not achieved instantly." *PLOS ONE* 18(4), e0284029.** In conversations between autistic adult German speakers, the preferred gap was still the typical short-gap transition, and the authors found no clear overall difference in turn-timing between autistic and non-autistic dyads — both groups showed a preference for very short silent-gap transitions. The study is not specifically about video, but the finding is relevant to the latency problem: autistic speakers are targeting the same short-gap transitions that video-call latency disrupts.

Cross-reference: Cat 04 (Conversation Structure) on turn-taking.

### 2.5 Tone Indicators in the Chat Pane

Video meetings often have a text chat pane running alongside. The pragmatic conventions of that chat pane are closer to synchronous text (see §4) than to the voice channel — which means **tone indicators** (/s, /gen, /j, /srs, /lh) apply there. They are useful on video calls because:

- Chat-pane comments interleave with spoken conversation, with no prosody of their own.
- Sarcasm in a chat pane read by a hundred meeting participants cannot rely on the verbal speaker's prosody.
- Autistic participants who find the video voice channel effortful may rely on the chat pane for participation — which means the chat pane is often an autism-friendly zone, and tone indicators make it more so.

See the main Cat 20 file for the tone indicator inventory and the broader discussion of digital-first emotional expression.

## 3. Voice Calls as a Distinct Pragmatic Context

### 3.1 Voice-Only Pragmatics

Voice calls — phone, Discord voice channel, voice-only Zoom, voice notes — remove face, gaze, and gesture entirely. **Prosody carries all the nonverbal work**, plus some of what face and gaze would have carried.

| Channel | Phone call | Zoom (video on) | Discord voice | Voice note (async) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Synchronicity | real-time | real-time | real-time | async |
| Voice | yes | yes | yes | yes |
| Prosody | yes | yes | yes | yes |
| Face | no | degraded | no | no |
| Gaze | no | mediated | no | no |
| Gesture | no | partial | no | no |
| Pause-to-think | hard | hard | slightly easier | trivial |
| Self-monitoring load | moderate | high | moderate | low |
| Transcript | no (usually) | chat pane | chat pane | replay |

**Turn-taking** in voice-only contexts relies almost entirely on **prosodic** and **lexical-boundary** cues: terminal pitch contours (does the speaker's pitch drop at the end? then they are done), lengthening of final syllables, drop in volume, and discourse markers signalling close ("so yeah", "anyway", "that's it"). Since you cannot see gaze shifts or breath, prosody must do more work than face-to-face.

The implication for autistic readers: if prosodic processing is effortful for you (see Cat 14 and Cat 15), voice-only will be **harder** than face-to-face in some respects, not easier, despite the reduction in channels. The reduction in channels also reduces the *backup* signals you could have fallen back on.

### 3.2 "I'll Call You" vs. "I'll Text You": A Generational and Pragmatic Choice

For a substantial part of the pre-2000 adult population, "I'll call you" is the default and "I'll text you" signals something specific (quick, low-priority, non-urgent). For a substantial part of the under-40 adult population, "I'll text you" is the default and "I'll call you" signals something specific (urgent, emotional, requires a real conversation, bad news, someone has died).

This means the same sentence — **"can I call you?"** — reads as:
- *Older default*: "I want to talk to you soon, routine conversation" (neutral).
- *Younger default*: "Something is wrong and it is too big for text" (alarming).

Autistic adults often (though not universally) prefer text over voice. The generational drift toward text-default is largely congenial for autistic readers — *if* the people you are communicating with are also on the text-default side. When a text-default person asks a voice-default person "can I call you?", the voice-default person may receive it as routine. When a voice-default person calls a text-default person unexpectedly, the text-default person may receive it as alarming.

The cross-link to Cat 19 (Phatic Communication) matters: phone-based phatic talk ("just checking in") is a voice-default convention. Text-default communicators may experience an unexpected "just checking in" phone call as having an unstated agenda, because in their native convention, calls are only for high-stakes content.

### 3.3 Why Phone Calls Are Often the Hardest Modality for Autistic Adults

**Howard, P. L. & Sedgewick, F. (2021). "'Anything but the phone!': Communication mode preferences in the autism community." *Autism* 25(8), 2265–2278** is the landmark paper here. The authors surveyed 245 autistic adults on communication-mode preferences across seven scenarios. Key findings:

- **Phone calls ranked lowest** across virtually every scenario surveyed.
- **Email ranked highly** for accessing services, seeking customer support, and research-related communication — contexts where a written record and processing time matter most.
- **Face-to-face and text/email** were preferred for communication with family, friends, at work, and in education.
- Participants reported that phone calls combined the worst features of multiple modalities: real-time pressure (no time to compose), no visual cues, limited ability to check understanding (no chat pane, no facial signals), processing lag, auditory-only channel (hard if you have auditory processing differences), and often high social stakes (scheduling medical care, dealing with bureaucracy, negotiating with landlords).
- Participants reported **actively avoiding** phone contact even when it imposed material costs — missed appointments, unresolved billing problems, unclaimed benefits, delayed medical care.

The authors conclude that services should move away from reliance on phone calls and instead offer written options such as email and live messaging, which are more accessible.

**The "autism tax" of phone avoidance.** Essential services — banking, healthcare, legal, government, utilities, insurance — often still require phone contact for non-trivial transactions. "Call this number" is the gatekeeper for a lot of basic adult life. For an autistic adult who finds phone calls acutely difficult, the choice is between (a) doing the call (with the associated cost, including possible shutdown or meltdown afterward), (b) asking someone else to do it (losing autonomy, creating dependence), or (c) not doing it (losing the service, accruing late fees, going without medical care). All three options have costs. This is a structural accessibility failure, not a personal shortcoming.

**Strategies that help:**

- **Scripts.** Write down what you need to say before dialling. Include the opening, the middle, the likely questions, and the closing. "Hi, my name is X, I'm calling about Y, account number Z, I need to ask about W." Keep the script beside you during the call.
- **Practice in advance.** Say the opening out loud two or three times. Dial-and-speak feels different from reading silently.
- **Ask for email follow-up.** "Can you send me what we discussed in writing?" is a standard, expected request and many services comply.
- **Ask in advance for accommodation.** Some services (especially healthcare, government) have formal channels for requesting email or text contact on accessibility grounds. Filling out the form once often carries forward.
- **Use relay services.** Some countries have text-to-voice relay services for deaf/hard-of-hearing users that are also usable by anyone who prefers text on the incoming side.
- **Use apps that enable real-time or recorded transcription of calls.** Android and iOS both now offer call-transcription tools; some platforms (Google Voice, Skype, certain VoIP providers) can provide real-time transcription of an incoming phone call.
- **Use voicemail-to-text.** Most mobile operators offer this. It converts missed voicemails into readable text.
- **Callback timing.** If you have control over when the call happens, schedule it for a time when your auditory processing and general bandwidth are at their best (often not first-thing-in-the-morning-under-stress).
- **Post-call recovery built in.** Schedule nothing for the 30 minutes after a difficult phone call.

### 3.4 Voice Messages and Voice Notes

**Asynchronous voice** — voice notes on WhatsApp, iMessage audio messages, Discord voice notes, voice memos — is a different beast from real-time voice.

| Feature | Real-time voice call | Voice note (async) |
|---|---|---|
| Processing time to respond | seconds | as long as you need |
| Re-listening | usually impossible | replay at will |
| Pace control | no | pause, skip, speed up |
| Transcription | usually unavailable | increasingly available (WhatsApp and iMessage now offer this) |
| Turn-taking pressure | high | none |
| Pause-and-think | socially awkward | normal and invisible |

**Some autistic adults find voice notes easier than real-time voice** because the turn-taking pressure vanishes and they can rehearse, rerecord, and process at their own pace. Others find them harder than text because they still require real-time listening comprehension with no visible transcript.

**Cultural variation is large.** Voice notes are the dominant casual mode in some regions and communication networks (common in much of Latin America, parts of Europe, parts of Asia, and in many Gen-Z friend networks), and almost absent in others (older US professional culture, for example). Receiving a long voice note from a new friend can feel like a significant shift in modality that you were not expecting.

**Practical note:** Most major messaging apps now offer **voice-note transcription**. WhatsApp added automatic voice-note transcription in 2024; recent iOS versions of iMessage offer it; Telegram has it as a premium feature. If you find voice notes difficult, turning on transcription makes them functionally closer to text, with an optional listen.

## 4. Synchronous Text and Mixed Modalities

### 4.1 Synchronous Text as Its Own Modality

**Synchronous text** — Slack DMs during work hours, Discord text channels, iMessage when both parties are actively typing, IRC, rapid SMS exchange — is neither "like face-to-face" nor "like email." It is a hybrid, and it has its own pragmatic conventions.

Compared with async text:

- **Expected response time is seconds to minutes**, not hours to days.
- **Messages are shorter.** Rapid exchange favours short bursts over paragraphs.
- **Typing indicators ("...") are live.** Your conversational partner can see that you are composing a reply in real time.
- **Read receipts are live.** The interval between "message delivered" and "message read" is itself a signal.
- **Interruption norms are weaker.** In voice-based turn-taking, overlap is dispreferred. In synchronous text, parallel messages are fine — you and your interlocutor can both be typing at once.

Compared with voice:

- **No prosody channel** — tone is carried by punctuation, emoji, formatting, and capitalisation (all covered in the main Cat 20 file).
- **Transcript is persistent.** You can scroll back and check what was said.
- **Processing time is elastic.** You can take ten seconds to compose a reply and nobody will notice.

### 4.2 Typing Indicators, Read Receipts, and "..."

These are Herring's "channels of communication" — small new signalling affordances that have acquired pragmatic meaning.

| Signal | Conventional reading |
|---|---|
| **Typing indicator appears** | "They saw my message and are composing." |
| **Typing indicator appears, disappears, reappears** | "They started a reply, deleted it, and are reworking — probably the topic is sensitive or they are choosing words carefully." |
| **Typing indicator appears, disappears, no reply for a while** | "They started a reply and gave up — could mean anything from 'got distracted' to 'decided not to say what they first wrote'." |
| **Read receipt, no reply** | In casual contexts, mildly worrying or dismissive. In work contexts, routine. |
| **Read receipt, reply hours later** | Normal if the person is busy; possibly cold if the person is known to be on their phone. |
| **No read receipt, no reply** | Neutral — could simply mean they haven't picked up their phone. |

**For autistic readers**: these signals are often over-interpreted. If you are prone to anxious rumination on "they read my message and didn't reply, what did I do wrong," remember that on any given day, most people have 20+ unanswered messages sitting on their phones, and the overwhelming majority of delays are about the recipient's life, not about you. The signal-to-noise ratio on typing indicators and read receipts is **low**. They are genuinely informative only as aggregate patterns over time with people you know well, not as single-point readings in new relationships.

Also relevant: most platforms let you **turn read receipts off** on your end. If the pressure of "they will know I read this" is creating load, that is an option.

### 4.3 Message Granularity

Herring's "message granularity" facet matters a lot for synchronous text. Two common styles:

- **Many short messages.** "hey / quick question / do you have the doc / the one from yesterday / nvm found it". Each message is a turn; each sends a notification; the conversation feels rapid and informal.
- **One long message.** "Hey — quick question, do you have the doc from yesterday? Never mind, found it." Same information, one notification, feels more deliberate and formal.

Which style a platform favours depends on norms. Discord/Slack/iMessage casual tends to be short-bursty. Email and work Slack trend longer and more self-contained. Matching the granularity of your conversational partner is a subtle pragmatic convention — sending one long message into a rapid-short exchange reads as over-formal; sending many short messages into a long-message conversation reads as jittery or impatient.

### 4.4 Gaming Voice Chat

Gaming voice chat is a specific context with its own norms:

- **Callouts.** Terse, tactical, information-first. "Top left." "One behind." "Reloading." Brevity is a norm; full sentences are sometimes actively dispreferred.
- **Call-and-response.** "Copy." "Got it." "On me." Acknowledgement phrases maintain the shared situational picture.
- **Tactical vs. social register.** Many gaming groups alternate fluidly between tactical mode (during high-intensity play) and social mode (during downtime, lobby, between rounds). The same voice call carries both, and the register-switch is near-instant.
- **Chat overlay.** Most gaming voice platforms also have a text chat overlay for linking things, for pings, for "brb," for communication that would disrupt tactical talk.

For many autistic gamers, gaming voice chat is **the most comfortable voice modality** because: (a) the topic is fixed and external (the game), which removes the small-talk improvisation load; (b) callouts are explicit and scripted; (c) shared activity provides structure and timing; (d) the community often skews younger and more accommodating of atypical communication styles; (e) "mute when tired" is a norm.

Heasman & Gillespie (2019) — see §5 below — specifically studied **collaborative video gaming** among autistic adults and found distinctive features of intersubjectivity emerging in that context.

## 5. The Double Empathy Problem in Real-Time Digital

### 5.1 Milton 2012 Applied to Video and Voice

**Milton, D. E. M. (2012). "On the ontological status of autism: the 'double empathy problem'." *Disability & Society* 27(6), 883–887** introduced the double empathy problem: mismatches in mutual understanding between autistic and non-autistic people are **bidirectional**, not a one-sided "autistic deficit." Non-autistic people are as bad at reading autistic communication as autistic people are at reading non-autistic communication; the difficulty is interactional, not individual.

On video calls, this problem has a specific shape. The nonverbal cues that video degrades (gaze, micro-expression, subtle facial movement, gesture) are the same cues that non-autistic communicators most rely on for reading "rapport." When those cues are degraded for everyone, non-autistic communicators experience the problem as "there's less rapport" — but often **charge the missing rapport to their autistic conversation partner specifically**, because autistic participants are already scripted into the "bad at nonverbal" role.

Concretely: on a video call where *everybody's* facial expression is flattened and *everybody's* gaze is mediated, the autistic participant will often be singled out as "hard to read," "seems checked out," "doesn't engage" — even though, on a frame-by-frame basis, they are not producing fewer or weaker nonverbal signals than the non-autistic participants on the same call.

This is a specific instance of Milton's double empathy problem: the expectation that autistic participants will do the nonverbal work that is degraded for everyone and is charged to autistic participants specifically. It is not a problem that autistic people can solve by trying harder, because the degradation is in the medium.

Milton, Gurbuz, and López (2022), "The 'double empathy problem': Ten years on" (*Autism* 26(8), 1901–1903), revisited the concept and noted that it has become an increasingly well-supported research programme, with empirical findings (including Crompton et al., 2020, below) consistent with the original theoretical claim.

### 5.2 Crompton et al. 2020 and Whether It Extends to Video

**Crompton, C. J., Ropar, D., Evans-Williams, C. V. M., Flynn, E. G., & Fletcher-Watson, S. (2020). "Autistic peer-to-peer information transfer is highly effective." *Autism* 24(7), 1704–1712** demonstrated that autistic-to-autistic and non-autistic-to-non-autistic diffusion chains transfer information equally effectively, while mixed chains (autistic-to-non-autistic or vice versa) lose significantly more information and produce lower reported rapport. The finding is a central empirical support for Milton's double empathy thesis: the problem is the *match* between participants, not an autistic deficit.

The original Crompton et al. (2020) study was face-to-face. **Whether the same peer-effectiveness advantage appears in video is an empirical question that the field is actively pursuing.** Subsequent work by Crompton's group and others continues to find the basic peer-effectiveness finding in varied contexts. A clean replication in degraded-channel video contexts would help answer: *does the autistic-peer advantage survive channel degradation?* Or: *does it depend on shared physical presence?*

Provisional read: there is good reason to expect the peer-effectiveness advantage to survive video, because its mechanism is the **match between communicators**, and shared communication style survives changes in channel. Autistic Discord voice channels and autistic Zoom meetups certainly *feel* more pragmatically comfortable than mixed-neurotype calls of the same format, for reasons consistent with Crompton's findings. Systematic research on this is in progress.

### 5.3 Heasman & Gillespie 2019: Neurodivergent Intersubjectivity in Gaming

**Heasman, B. & Gillespie, A. (2019). "Neurodivergent intersubjectivity: Distinctive features of how autistic people create shared understanding." *Autism* 23(4), 910–921** studied 30 members of an autism-community charity during collaborative video gaming. They identified two distinctive features of how autistic participants built shared understanding:

1. **Generous assumption of common ground.** When understood, this produced rapid rapport. When not understood by a conversational partner, it could produce "disruptive" turns that an outside observer might read as off-topic.
2. **Low demand for coordination.** The interaction did not require constant alignment on topic, attention, or pace. "Low demand for coordination" turned out to *ameliorate* many of the social challenges that would otherwise arise from disruptive turns: if nobody expects tight coordination, disruption does not disrupt.

Heasman & Gillespie argue that these are features of a legitimate mode of social relating, not failures to perform a neurotypical one. The study is also notable for using **video gaming** as a naturalistic setting for autistic adult social interaction — one of the few modalities where autistic adults are studied *doing something they enjoy*, rather than being scored on a social-skills task.

### 5.4 The Practical Implication: Autistic-Friendly Real-Time Spaces

Autistic-only or autistic-led real-time digital spaces — autistic peer group video calls, autistic Discord servers, autistic Zoom meetups, autistic gaming clans — often feel more pragmatically comfortable than mixed-neurotype spaces of the same format. The reasons, cumulatively:

- Shared low demand for coordination (Heasman & Gillespie).
- Shared tolerance for monologue, info-dumping, and special-interest talk.
- Shared acceptance of camera-off, mute-when-overwhelmed, stim-on-camera, text-over-voice preferences.
- Shared realism about eye contact, turn-taking gaps, and "awkward" silences.
- Shared effectiveness in autistic peer-to-peer information transfer (Crompton).
- Shared willingness to name problems out loud rather than imply them.
- Shared readiness to use plain language, skip phatic layers when not wanted, and allow explicit negotiation of communication norms.

This is not about "avoiding neurotypical people." It is about having access to contexts where the match between communication styles makes real-time digital less effortful. For many autistic readers, having *some* autistic-only or autistic-led real-time digital spaces in their lives is protective — a place where the double empathy problem is running in a more favourable configuration.

## 6. Platform-Specific Conventions

Each real-time platform has its own set of norms — Herring's "medium factors" combined with community history produce distinct pragmatic ecosystems. Brief sketches of the major ones, with one or two autism-relevant features each.

### 6.1 Zoom

- **Register.** Business-formal by default, especially for work meetings. Social Zooms (family catch-ups, autistic peer groups, online events) run differently.
- **Meeting etiquette.** Mute when not speaking; unmute to talk; raise hand for queue; chat pane for side questions.
- **Chat sidebar** is a genuine second channel. Questions, links, "sorry I have to step out," and real-time reaction cluster there.
- **Waiting room** and host controls create a host/participant asymmetry that does not exist in most other modalities.
- **Autism-relevant:** (a) Camera-off is tolerated for "bandwidth reasons" in most professional Zooms, which doubles as cover for needing to stim, regulate, or look away. (b) The chat pane is a legitimate participation channel — you do not need to unmute to contribute. (c) Gallery view can be replaced with speaker view to reduce the "many faces at intimate distance" gaze load (Bailenson §2.2). (d) Hiding self-view is a setting most people never find; learning where it is and using it reduces one of the biggest sources of video-call load.

### 6.2 Microsoft Teams

- **Register.** Business-enterprise. The default audience is your colleagues.
- **Presence indicators** (available / busy / do not disturb / in a meeting / away) are a persistent ambient signal. People check your status before messaging you.
- **Threading** in chat channels creates a layered conversation structure; replies live within a parent message rather than in the main flow.
- **Autism-relevant:** (a) Using "do not disturb" is a legible, accepted signal that sets expectations. (b) Threaded chat reduces the "many parallel conversations" load of flat channels. (c) Teams' Together mode attempts to reduce Bailenson-style gaze overload by placing everyone in a shared virtual background; evidence on whether this actually helps is mixed but the intent is right.

### 6.3 FaceTime and iMessage Audio/Video

- **Register.** Personal, casual, often dyadic. Almost always initiated between people who already know each other well.
- **Expectation is lower formality** — camera angles may be handheld, backgrounds are usually home, pets and children may appear.
- **Apple ecosystem features** include live audio transcription and voice-message transcription on recent iOS.
- **Autism-relevant:** (a) The personal/casual register means less masking pressure than on business Zoom. (b) Dyadic is easier than group (no gaze-from-many-faces problem). (c) SharePlay and Memoji features can substitute for the face channel if that is preferred.

### 6.4 Discord

- **Register.** Community-casual. Highly variable by server.
- **Voice channels** are persistent, joinable rooms rather than scheduled meetings. You drop in and out.
- **Stage channels** are asymmetric (speakers and audience), useful for talks, readings, Q&A.
- **Text-voice hybrid.** Text channels run alongside voice channels in the same server, continuously. The chat pane is not a sidecar — it is a full, persistent, equally-weighted channel.
- **Autism-relevant:** (a) Discord's culture is generally tolerant of neurodivergent communication styles, particularly on autism-related or gaming servers. (b) The persistent voice-channel model means "lurking in voice with mic off" is an accepted low-load way to be present. (c) Many autistic communities have their largest real-time gatherings on Discord. (d) Custom server rules often explicitly name tone-indicator norms, content-warning conventions, and pronoun conventions, reducing pragmatic ambiguity.

### 6.5 Twitch and Other Livestream Platforms

- **Register.** Performer/audience asymmetry — one broadcaster, many viewers, live.
- **Chat runs in parallel** with the video, usually much faster than any single viewer can read.
- **Emotes** are platform-specific visual tokens that function partly as vocabulary, partly as reactions, partly as community signals. Using emotes correctly is a significant pragmatic competence.
- **Autism-relevant:** (a) Parasocial engagement with streamers can feel more comfortable than reciprocal conversation — the pragmatic load is asymmetric. (b) The chat can be overwhelming; most clients let you slow it down or filter. (c) Emote vocabularies are learnable as lexicons, which some autistic viewers find easier than informal language.

### 6.6 Gaming Voice Chat (In-Game)

- **Register.** Tactical + social, alternating in near-real-time.
- **Callouts** are terse and scripted (see §4.4).
- **Voice channels often toggle** (push-to-talk, team-only, proximity-voice, all-chat). Knowing which channel you are on is a pragmatic skill.
- **Autism-relevant:** (a) Shared external task (the game) removes most small-talk improvisation load. (b) Callouts are scripted and rule-governed — comfortable for pattern-oriented communicators. (c) "Mute when tired" is a default, non-judgemental norm. (d) See Heasman & Gillespie (2019) for the research base on autistic intersubjectivity in collaborative video gaming.

### 6.7 WhatsApp and Signal Voice/Video

- **Register.** Personal, end-to-end encrypted, often intimate or urgent.
- **Voice notes** are heavily used in WhatsApp culture globally.
- **Group voice/video** is supported but typically small (family, close friend groups).
- **Autism-relevant:** (a) Voice-note transcription on WhatsApp (rolled out in 2024) makes voice-dominant communication networks much more accessible. (b) End-to-end encryption and privacy make these platforms preferred for sensitive topics. (c) "Can I call you?" in WhatsApp culture carries the same generational-variance issue as in §3.2.

## 7. Summary and Cross-Links

### 7.1 Key Takeaways

1. **Real-time digital is a spectrum of modalities**, each stripping or degrading specific channels. "Just like being in the room" is wrong for all of them.
2. **Video calls are not a restoration of face-to-face**; they are a new modality with specific costs (Bailenson, 2021) — camera-mediated gaze, self-view load, latency-disrupted turn-taking (Schoenenberg et al., 2014), degraded facial channel.
3. **Phone calls are often the hardest modality for autistic adults** (Howard & Sedgewick, 2021), and the post-pandemic shift from phone to text, email, and chat is, on net, an accessibility win — where services allow it.
4. **Voice notes and asynchronous voice** are a meaningfully different modality from real-time voice and are often more accessible.
5. **Synchronous text** has its own pragmatics — tone indicators, typing indicators, message granularity — and is often autism-friendlier than voice.
6. **Gaming voice** is a distinct context with scripted callouts, shared external task, and (Heasman & Gillespie, 2019) a research base showing autistic adults build shared understanding effectively in it.
7. **The double empathy problem** (Milton, 2012) runs on video and voice as it does face-to-face, but with an extra twist: the degraded channels are charged disproportionately to autistic participants, even though everyone's channels are degraded equally.
8. **Autistic-only or autistic-led real-time digital spaces** are often pragmatically comfortable in ways that mirror Crompton et al. (2020) face-to-face peer-effectiveness findings, and are legitimate and protective.
9. **Platform-specific conventions matter** — Zoom, Teams, Discord, FaceTime, Twitch, gaming voice, and WhatsApp are all different pragmatic ecosystems with their own norms.
