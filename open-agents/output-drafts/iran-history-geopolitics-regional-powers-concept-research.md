# Regional Powers: Saudi Arabia, Gulf States, Russia, China & Turkey

## Part I: Saudi Arabia & the Gulf States

### 1. The Saudi-Iran Rivalry: Foundations

The Saudi-Iranian rivalry is one of the defining geopolitical contests of the modern Middle East, shaped by sectarian identity, competing visions of Islamic leadership, oil politics, and regional power projection. Understanding its dynamics requires recognizing that while often framed as a "Sunni vs. Shia" conflict, the rivalry is fundamentally political and strategic, with sectarianism instrumentalized by both sides.

#### Competing Claims to Islamic Leadership

Saudi Arabia, as custodian of Mecca and Medina (Islam's two holiest cities), claims leadership of the Sunni Muslim world — roughly 85% of the world's 1.8 billion Muslims. The Saudi state's legitimacy is built on its alliance with the Wahhabi religious establishment, which provides religious authority in exchange for political power and resources to propagate its conservative interpretation of Islam globally.

Iran, following the 1979 revolution, claimed leadership of a revolutionary Islamic movement that transcended sectarian lines. Khomeini explicitly challenged the Saudi monarchy's Islamic credentials, arguing that hereditary kingship was un-Islamic and that the Saudi royals were corrupt servants of American imperialism. Iran positioned itself as the champion of the "dispossessed" — particularly Shia populations living under Sunni-majority rule across the region.

This competition for Islamic legitimacy has been the driving force behind decades of proxy competition.

#### The 1979 Rupture

Before the revolution, Saudi-Iranian relations were managed within the framework of the Twin Pillars policy — both served as American allies maintaining Gulf security. The relationship was competitive but contained. The revolution shattered this framework. Khomeini called for the overthrow of the Saudi monarchy, incited Saudi Arabia's Shia minority (concentrated in the oil-rich Eastern Province) to revolt, and accused the Saudis of being American puppets unfit to control the holy cities.

The 1987 Hajj clash — in which over 400 people died, including 275 Iranians, during confrontations between Iranian pilgrims and Saudi security forces — brought the two countries to the brink of war and led to a diplomatic break.

### 2. Proxy Battlegrounds

The Saudi-Iranian rivalry has been fought through proxies across the region, with devastating consequences for the populations caught in between.

#### Yemen

Yemen has become the most destructive theater of Saudi-Iranian proxy competition. The Houthi movement (Ansar Allah), drawn from Yemen's Zaidi Shia minority, received Iranian weapons, training, and financial support. When the Houthis seized the capital Sanaa in 2014, Saudi Arabia launched a military intervention in March 2015, leading a coalition of Gulf states in a bombing campaign that has killed tens of thousands of civilians and created what the UN called the world's worst humanitarian crisis.

From Riyadh's perspective, the Houthis represented an Iranian beachhead on Saudi Arabia's southern border, threatening shipping through the Bab el-Mandeb strait and potentially destabilizing Saudi Arabia's own Shia population. From Tehran's perspective, the Houthis were an indigenous Yemeni movement resisting Saudi hegemony — supporting them was relatively cheap and tied down Saudi military resources far from Iran.

#### Bahrain

Bahrain's population is 70-85% Shia, ruled by the Sunni Al Khalifa dynasty. When Arab Spring protests erupted in February 2011, Saudi Arabia sent troops across the causeway to crush them, viewing the protests as an Iranian-inspired attempt to topple a fellow monarchy. Iran denied direct involvement but vocally supported the protesters and condemned the crackdown. Bahrain has continued to experience low-level insurgency, with Iran accused of supporting militant groups like the Al-Ashtar Brigades.

#### Iraq

After the 2003 US invasion toppled Saddam Hussein, Iraq became the primary arena of Saudi-Iranian competition. Iran cultivated deep relationships with Shia political parties, militias (organized into the Popular Mobilization Forces), and religious leaders. Saudi Arabia, having lost its Sunni Ba'athist ally, struggled to maintain influence in a country where Shia demographics (60-65% of the population) guaranteed Shia-led governments.

#### Lebanon

Lebanon's confessional political system made it a natural theater for Saudi-Iranian competition. Iran backed Hezbollah (Shia), while Saudi Arabia supported the March 14 Alliance and Sunni political figures, particularly the Hariri family. The 2017 bizarre episode in which Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman apparently forced Lebanese Prime Minister Saad Hariri to resign on Saudi television illustrated both the intensity and sometimes ham-fisted nature of Saudi involvement.

#### Syria

The Syrian Civil War became the most intense Saudi-Iranian proxy conflict. Iran committed billions of dollars, deployed IRGC forces and Hezbollah fighters, and organized Shia militias from Iraq, Afghanistan, and Pakistan to defend the Assad regime. Saudi Arabia (along with Qatar and Turkey) funded and armed various Sunni rebel groups.

Iran's intervention proved decisive: by 2016, a combination of Iranian-led ground forces and Russian airpower had turned the tide in Assad's favor. However, Assad's fall in late 2024/early 2025 represented a significant setback for Iran, eliminating a key node in its strategic corridor from Tehran to Beirut.

### 3. Oil Politics and OPEC

Saudi Arabia and Iran are both founding members of OPEC, and their rivalry extends to oil politics. Saudi Arabia, with the world's largest spare production capacity, has historically used its ability to flood or constrain oil markets as a geopolitical tool. Iran, under sanctions that limit its export capacity, has consistently pushed for production cuts to raise prices and maximize revenue from its limited exports.

This tension within OPEC has sometimes broken into the open. Saudi Arabia's willingness to maintain high production during periods of low prices has been perceived by Iran as economic warfare — an attempt to bankrupt the Iranian economy by keeping oil prices below Iran's fiscal breakeven point.

### 4. The China-Brokered Rapprochement (2023)

On March 10, 2023, in a diplomatic surprise, Saudi Arabia and Iran announced they would restore diplomatic relations in an agreement brokered by China. The deal was significant for several reasons:

**Why Saudi Arabia Agreed:** MBS's Vision 2030 economic transformation plan required regional stability to attract foreign investment. The Yemen war had become a quagmire, draining resources and generating international criticism. Saudi confidence in American security commitments had eroded, particularly after the tepid US response to the 2019 Abqaiq-Khurais oil facility attacks.

**Why Iran Agreed:** Iran sought to break its diplomatic isolation, exacerbated by US sanctions since the JCPOA withdrawal. Reducing tensions with Riyadh could lower regional temperature and create space for economic recovery. The deal also served as a hedge against US pressure.

**Why China Brokered It:** Beijing demonstrated its growing diplomatic capability and its interest in Middle Eastern stability (as the region's largest oil customer). The deal enhanced China's image as a constructive alternative to American confrontation.

**What Happened Next:** Both countries exchanged ambassadors and reopened embassies by mid-2023. However, further progress stalled. The October 7, 2023 Hamas attack and subsequent regional escalation complicated the rapprochement. Saudi Arabia's progress toward an Abraham Accords-style normalization with Israel — which had been advancing in parallel — was suspended. By 2025-2026, the Saudi-Iran deal appeared to be in a state of "suspended animation," with diplomatic relations maintained but deeper cooperation failing to materialize amid the intensifying US-Israel-Iran conflict.

### 5. MBS's Strategic Vision

Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman's approach to Iran reflects a generational shift in Saudi strategic thinking. MBS is less ideologically driven than his predecessors and more focused on economic modernization and consolidation of personal power. His strategic calculus includes:

- **Vision 2030:** Diversifying Saudi Arabia away from oil dependence requires massive foreign investment, which in turn requires regional stability
- **Military realism:** The Yemen war demonstrated the limits of Saudi military power; direct confrontation with Iran is not in Saudi Arabia's interest
- **Hedging:** MBS has diversified Saudi Arabia's relationships, deepening ties with China, Russia, and India while maintaining the US alliance — creating leverage and reducing dependence on any single great power
- **Abraham Accords:** Normalization with Israel, if achieved, would create a powerful regional counterbalance to Iran while deepening Saudi-US relations

### 6. The Abraham Accords and Gulf State Normalization

The 2020 Abraham Accords saw the UAE and Bahrain normalize relations with Israel, followed by Sudan and Morocco. The deals were explicitly motivated in part by the shared perception of Iran as a regional threat:

- The accords facilitated the creation of a US-led integrated regional air defense shield — first tested during Iran's April 2024 missile and drone attack on Israel
- Israeli defense exports to Abraham Accords countries surged from 3% to 12% of total exports between 2023 and 2024, reaching a record $14.8 billion
- Saudi normalization with Israel — the grand prize — was advancing before October 7, 2023 halted progress

The accords embodied Israel's "reverse periphery doctrine" — the mirror image of Ben-Gurion's original strategy. Where Israel once allied with non-Arab states against Arab enemies, it now allies with Arab states against their shared Iranian adversary.

## Part II: Russia

### 1. Historical Russian Interference in Iran

Russia's relationship with Iran stretches back centuries, marked by imperial conquest, territorial loss, and deep Iranian mistrust.

#### The Qajar Era: Treaties of Humiliation

The Russo-Persian Wars (1651-1828) resulted in catastrophic territorial losses for Iran. The Treaty of Gulistan (1813) and the Treaty of Turkmenchay (1828) stripped Iran of its Caucasian territories — modern-day Georgia, Armenia, Azerbaijan, and parts of Dagestan. Under the Treaty of Turkmenchay, Iran ceded sovereignty over vast territories, paid 20 million rubles in reparations, and conceded exclusive Russian rights to maintain a Caspian fleet.

These treaties — among the most humiliating in Iranian history — transformed Russia from a distant power into an existential threat occupying Iran's northern frontier. For the next century, Russian and British influence divided Iran into spheres of control, with the Anglo-Russian Convention of 1907 formalizing this arrangement without Iranian consent.

The memory of these losses remains vivid in Iranian national consciousness. The phrase "Turkmenchay" carries emotional weight comparable to "Versailles" in German history or "Century of Humiliation" in Chinese discourse.

#### Soviet Era

During World War II, the Soviet Union and Britain jointly occupied Iran, forcing Reza Shah's abdication. After the war, Stalin's refusal to withdraw Soviet forces from northern Iran (the Azerbaijan crisis of 1946) became one of the first confrontations of the Cold War. The Soviet-backed Republic of Mahabad (a Kurdish state) and the Azerbaijan People's Government both briefly existed under Soviet protection before collapsing when Soviet forces withdrew under US and British pressure.

During the Cold War, Iran under the Shah was firmly in the Western camp, making Soviet-Iranian relations antagonistic. The Tudeh (Communist) Party maintained ties to Moscow but was ruthlessly suppressed by SAVAK.

### 2. Post-Soviet Rapprochement

After the Soviet collapse, Russia and Iran found common ground as two powers resentful of American hegemony. Their relationship has been primarily transactional, built on arms sales, nuclear cooperation, and shared opposition to Western dominance.

#### Nuclear Cooperation: The Bushehr Reactor

The most visible symbol of Russian-Iranian cooperation is the Bushehr Nuclear Power Plant. Construction began under a German contract in 1975 but was abandoned after the revolution. In 1992, Russia signed an agreement to complete the reactor, which finally began producing power in 2010. In 2014, Iran and Russia signed an agreement to build two additional reactors at Bushehr, with an option for six more at other sites.

Russia's nuclear assistance has been controversial. While Russia insists Bushehr is a civilian project and has cooperated with IAEA safeguards, critics argue that Russia's nuclear relationship with Iran has provided diplomatic cover and technical knowledge that advances Iran's broader nuclear ambitions.

#### Arms Sales

Russia has been Iran's primary source of advanced conventional weapons. Key systems include:

- **S-300 air defense system:** Delivered in 2016 after years of delay (the original contract was signed in 2007 but frozen under international pressure)
- **Su-35 fighter jets:** Negotiations for their sale were reported in 2023-2024
- **Mi-28NE attack helicopters:** Deliveries began in January 2025, a significant upgrade over Iran's aging pre-revolution AH-1 Cobras
- **Verba MANPADS:** A deal worth approximately 495 million euros was reported in February 2026, providing shoulder-fired air defense systems

### 3. Syria: Fighting Together for Assad

The Syrian Civil War cemented the Russia-Iran strategic relationship. Both powers had pre-existing partnerships with Damascus, but the war forced them into active military cooperation.

In the summer of 2015, IRGC Quds Force commander Qasem Soleimani traveled to Moscow — in violation of UN sanctions — to coordinate a joint military campaign. Russia brought airpower; Iran provided ground forces through Hezbollah, IRGC advisors, and imported Shia militias from Iraq, Afghanistan, and Pakistan.

In an unprecedented move, Tehran granted Russia access to Hamadan Air Base in August 2016 to launch airstrikes in Syria — the first time Iran had allowed foreign military forces on its soil since its constitution prohibited such arrangements after the revolution.

The alliance saved Assad's regime but created tensions. Russia and Iran sometimes pursued conflicting objectives in Syria — Russia sought political stability and a diplomatic settlement, while Iran sought to maintain its military infrastructure and land corridor to Lebanon.

Assad's fall in late 2024/early 2025 dealt a blow to both powers. As one analysis noted, both Moscow and Tehran found themselves "grappling with the collapse of Assad's regime," weakening their position in the wider Middle East.

### 4. Ukraine War: Iranian Drones for Russia

The Russia-Ukraine war created a new dimension in the Iran-Russia relationship. Iran supplied Russia with Shahed-136 (redesignated "Geran-2" by Russia) kamikaze drones, which became a signature weapon of the war. Iran reportedly supplied approximately 6,000 drones and helped Russia localize production at its Alabuga drone manufacturing facility in Tatarstan, enabling Russia to mass-produce modified versions at several thousand per month.

This drone partnership transformed the bilateral relationship from buyer-seller to co-producers. Russia's deadly drone industry was significantly upgraded with Iran's technical assistance.

### 5. The 2025 Strategic Partnership Treaty

In January 2025, Russia and Iran signed a 20-year Comprehensive Strategic Partnership Treaty, described as covering "all spheres, including defense, counter-terrorism, energy, finance, transport, industry, agriculture, culture, science, and engineering."

However, the agreement notably lacked a mutual defense clause. This meant Russia had no obligation to defend Iran if attacked — a limitation that became acutely relevant during the June 2025 and February-March 2026 conflicts.

### 6. The Partnership's Limits (2025-2026)

The US-Israeli military campaigns against Iran in 2025 and 2026 exposed the severe limitations of Russia's partnership with Iran:

- Russia did not intervene militarily to defend Iran during either the 12-Day War (June 2025) or the 2026 strikes
- Russia provided intelligence on US military asset locations but stopped short of direct military engagement
- Russia evacuated its scientists from the Bushehr nuclear plant as Israeli strikes threatened the facility
- After the assassination of Khamenei, a member of Iran's Expediency Discernment Council was quoted as saying the "war proved the strategic alliance with Moscow is worthless"

The fundamental reality is that Russia, embroiled in its own war in Ukraine and under severe Western sanctions, lacked both the capability and willingness to confront the United States and Israel militarily on Iran's behalf. The relationship, as one Moscow Times analysis noted, remains "a strategic partnership, not a military alliance."

## Part III: China

### 1. The 25-Year Strategic Cooperation Agreement (2021)

On March 27, 2021, Chinese and Iranian foreign ministers signed a 25-year Comprehensive Strategic Partnership agreement in Tehran. While final details were never fully disclosed, leaked drafts indicated:

- **Investment:** China would invest up to $400 billion in Iran's economy over 25 years, targeting oil, gas, petrochemical, and infrastructure sectors
- **Oil:** China would receive Iranian oil at a guaranteed minimum discount of 12% below benchmark prices, plus an additional 6-8% for "risk-adjusted compensation"
- **Infrastructure:** Major investments in Iran's transportation, telecommunications, and industrial infrastructure as part of the Belt and Road Initiative
- **Security:** China would be permitted to deploy security personnel to protect Chinese projects in Iran, with additional personnel and materiel to protect energy transit through the Persian Gulf

#### Implementation Challenges

The agreement has proven more aspirational than operational. Cumulative Chinese investment in Iran over the previous 15 years had totaled only approximately $27 billion — making the $400 billion pledge appear highly unrealistic. Western sanctions remain the primary obstacle, as Chinese banks and companies risk secondary sanctions by doing business with Iran.

### 2. Oil: China as Iran's Economic Lifeline

China's oil purchases represent Iran's most important economic relationship. The numbers tell the story:

- China buys approximately 90% of Iran's oil exports
- Iran exported an average of 1.38 million barrels per day to China in 2025
- Iranian crude represented 13.4% of China's overall imports by sea
- Iran's oil is sold at significant discounts, often through complex mechanisms to circumvent sanctions (ship-to-ship transfers, falsified documentation, intermediary companies)

For Iran, China is literally an economic lifeline — without Chinese oil purchases, Iran's economy would face catastrophic revenue loss. For China, Iranian oil represents a discounted supply that diversifies its import portfolio, though it accounts for only a fraction of China's total energy imports.

This asymmetry is the defining feature of the relationship: China is far more important to Iran than Iran is to China. Beijing has leveraged this asymmetry to extract favorable terms while limiting its commitments.

### 3. Diplomatic Role: The Saudi-Iran Deal Broker

China's brokering of the March 2023 Saudi-Iran rapprochement was its most significant Middle Eastern diplomatic achievement. The deal served multiple Chinese interests:

- Demonstrated China's emergence as a global diplomatic power capable of mediating between regional rivals
- Promoted stability in a region that supplies the majority of China's oil imports
- Positioned China as a constructive alternative to American confrontation
- Strengthened relationships with both Saudi Arabia and Iran simultaneously

### 4. China's Response to the 2025-2026 Conflicts

China's response to the US-Israeli military campaigns against Iran revealed both its interests and its limitations:

- During the June 2025 12-Day War, China prioritized mediation and regional engagement over overt political alignment
- Following Khamenei's assassination in February 2026, China faced what analysts called a "strategic test" — two China-friendly leaders (Assad in Syria, Khamenei in Iran) had been eliminated in quick succession
- Beijing adopted a "cautious and diplomatically focused posture," condemning the strikes verbally while taking no concrete retaliatory action
- China's calculus reflected the reality that its economic relationship with the United States dwarfs its interests in Iran — confronting Washington over Iran would risk far more than it could gain

As one CNN analysis noted, China had done "very little" despite the elimination of its partners, reflecting the fundamental constraint: China's interests in Iran are "not existential or even critical."

### 5. Belt and Road Implications

Iran occupies a potentially strategic position in China's Belt and Road Initiative (BRI), sitting at the crossroads of routes connecting China to Europe, the Middle East, and Africa. The China-Central Asia-West Asia Economic Corridor could make Iran a key transit hub.

However, sanctions, political instability, and now military conflict have prevented this potential from being realized. Iran remains one of BRI's most prominent unfulfilled promises — significant on paper but limited in actual implementation.

## Part IV: Turkey

### 1. Ottoman-Persian Historical Rivalry

The Turkish-Iranian relationship stretches back five centuries to the Ottoman-Safavid rivalry, which shaped the modern map of the Middle East. The Battle of Chaldiran (1514) established the frontier between Sunni Ottoman and Shia Safavid domains — a line that roughly corresponds to the modern Turkey-Iran border.

Despite centuries of conflict, the two empires also developed deep commercial and cultural ties. The modern relationship inherits both the competitive and cooperative dimensions of this long history.

### 2. Modern Pragmatism

Republican Turkey and Iran — whether under the Shah or the Islamic Republic — have maintained remarkably stable relations despite fundamental ideological differences. Key factors driving this pragmatism include:

**Shared Kurdish Concern:** Both Turkey and Iran face Kurdish separatist movements (the PKK and its affiliates in Turkey, PJAK in Iran). This shared threat has created a strong bond of mutual interest — both countries view Kurdish independence aspirations as existential threats and have coordinated military operations and intelligence sharing to contain them.

**Trade and Energy:** Turkey is a significant trading partner, with bilateral trade reaching $5.5 billion in 2025. Iran has been a major gas supplier through a 25-year pipeline contract that is set to expire in 2026, though Ankara has been dragging its feet on renewal negotiations as it seeks better terms and diversifies its energy sources.

**Geographic Reality:** Turkey and Iran share a 534-kilometer border, and geography dictates cooperation on issues from smuggling and migration to water resources.

### 3. Erdogan's Positioning

President Erdogan has positioned Turkey as a bridge between Iran and the West, while pursuing Turkey's own regional ambitions. His approach involves several elements:

**NATO Member, Independent Actor:** Turkey is a NATO ally but has pursued an increasingly independent foreign policy, purchasing Russian S-400 air defense systems, maintaining economic ties with Iran despite US sanctions, and positioning itself as a mediator in regional conflicts.

**Syria Complicates Relations:** Turkey and Iran found themselves on opposite sides of the Syrian Civil War — Turkey supported Sunni rebels while Iran backed Assad. Relations were strained by Turkish accusations that Iran was supporting Kurdish militias. In March 2025, Turkish Foreign Minister Hakan Fidan accused Iran of supporting Kurdish militias, leading both countries to summon ambassadors.

**Competing Regional Visions:** Both Turkey and Iran aspire to regional leadership, creating friction in areas where their spheres of influence overlap — particularly Iraq, Syria, and the Caucasus.

### 4. Turkey and the 2025-2026 Iran Crisis

Turkey's response to the US-Israeli military campaigns against Iran has been shaped by competing pressures:

**Opposition to Strikes:** Turkey condemned the US-Israeli strikes on Iran, with Erdogan calling them "illegal." This position reflects both genuine opposition to military escalation in Turkey's immediate neighborhood and a desire to maintain relationships with all sides.

**Kurdish Anxieties:** Turkey views threats to Iranian stability with deep concern because a governing vacuum in Iran could strengthen Kurdish separatist actors along the shared border. The US plan to arm Iranian Kurds has put Erdogan in what has been described as an "impossible position" — his NATO ally is empowering the very groups Turkey considers its primary security threat.

**Refugee Crisis:** Turkey, which does not require visas for Iranian citizens, faces potential massive refugee flows from an unstable Iran. In January 2026, reports emerged that Turkey had agreed to arrest and potentially return Iranian protesters and refugees who challenged the Iranian government — a controversial arrangement that drew condemnation from human rights organizations.

**Balancing Act:** Turkey's approach has been characterized by "strategic ambiguity" — condemning attacks on both sides, prioritizing containment and de-escalation, and seeking to preserve trade and border stability while avoiding entanglement. Ankara has three strategic scenarios under consideration: maintaining the status quo through diplomacy, positioning itself as a reconstruction partner if the regime falls, or managing the fallout of prolonged regional instability.

## Part V: The European Union / E3

### 1. JCPOA Stakeholders

The European Union and particularly the E3 (UK, France, Germany) were critical stakeholders in the JCPOA negotiations and its aftermath. When Trump withdrew the US from the deal in 2018, Europe attempted to maintain the agreement and Iran's compliance by creating alternative economic mechanisms.

### 2. INSTEX: The Failed Workaround

In January 2019, the E3 established INSTEX (Instrument in Support of Trade Exchanges), a special purpose vehicle designed to facilitate legitimate trade with Iran outside the US-controlled financial system, thereby circumventing American sanctions. In practice, INSTEX was a failure — it processed only a handful of small transactions before being discontinued in 2023. European companies were unwilling to risk US secondary sanctions, and the mechanism lacked the scale or credibility to substitute for normal banking channels.

### 3. The Snapback (2025)

On August 28, 2025, the E3 activated the "snapback" mechanism under UN Security Council Resolution 2231, which reimposed all pre-JCPOA international sanctions on Iran. The E3 cited Iran's accumulation of enriched uranium at 48 times the JCPOA limit, enrichment to 60% purity (lacking "any credible civilian justification"), and systematic violations of its commitments.

The snapback was activated on September 27, 2025, after a 30-day cooling period. The EU Council reimposed all nuclear-related sanctions that had been suspended or terminated under the JCPOA.

### 4. Response to the 2026 Crisis

The EU's response to the 2026 Iranian protests and subsequent military strikes included:

- Strong condemnation of the regime's brutal repression of nationwide protests
- In January 2026, the Foreign Affairs Council reached political agreement to designate the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) as a terrorist organization — a step Europe had long resisted
- New restrictive measures targeting individuals and entities involved in repression
- Mixed responses to the US-Israeli strikes: while several European governments expressed concern about proportionality and legality, the UK House of Commons held debates on the strikes, and public opinion was divided

### 5. Europe's Structural Limitations

Europe's Iran policy has been constrained by several structural factors:

- **Dependence on US financial system:** European companies ultimately prioritize access to the US market over Iran trade, making sanctions resistance futile
- **Internal divisions:** E3 positions are not always aligned, with France sometimes more hawkish and Germany more trade-oriented
- **Limited leverage:** Without military or economic tools comparable to the US, Europe's influence on Iran has been primarily diplomatic
- **Transatlantic tensions:** European attempts to maintain the JCPOA after Trump's 2018 withdrawal created friction with Washington, and subsequent alignment on sanctions reflected European pragmatism about American dominance in global finance
