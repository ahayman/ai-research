# Information Structure in Everyday English: A Practical Guide

## Research Document -- Practical Focus for Autistic Learners

---

## Purpose

This document provides concrete, worked examples of how English speakers package information within sentences and across discourse. Information structure is the system governing what gets presented as "old" vs. "new," what counts as the "topic" vs. the "focus," and how word order, stress, and syntactic constructions signal these distinctions. It is one of the most powerful tools for making communication clear -- and one of the most common sources of miscommunication when it goes wrong.

**Why this matters for autistic learners:** Information structure is almost never taught explicitly. Neurotypical speakers learn it through thousands of hours of conversational feedback -- someone looks confused, you rephrase; someone interrupts to ask "wait, who?", you learn to introduce referents first. Autistic individuals, who may not always detect these subtle feedback signals or may process them differently, often don't get this calibration. The result: messages that are technically accurate but pragmatically disorganized -- and the listener blames the speaker for being "confusing" when the real issue is information packaging. Making the rules explicit solves this.

**Relationship to Category 15 (Prosodic Pragmatics):** Category 15 covers how stress placement marks focus. This document covers the broader system that stress serves -- the discourse-level organization of given/new, topic/focus, and the syntactic constructions (clefts, passives, topicalization) that manage it. Think of Category 15 as covering the *signal* (stress) and this category as covering the *system* (information structure) that the signal plugs into.

## 1. THE GIVEN-NEW CONTRACT

### 1.1 What the Contract Is

The Given-New Contract is an implicit agreement between speakers and listeners: **present what the listener already knows (given information) before introducing what they don't know (new information)**. This principle, identified by Halliday (1967) and elaborated by Clark and Haviland (1977) as the "Given-New Strategy," governs how competent English speakers construct sentences.

The logic is straightforward:

1. **Given information** acts as an anchor -- something the listener can locate in their existing mental model
2. **New information** attaches to that anchor -- extending the listener's mental model

If you deliver new information without an anchor, the listener has nowhere to put it. If you deliver only given information, you've wasted their time. The contract requires both, in that order.

### 1.2 How Given-New Works in Conversation: Referent Introduction and Tracking

One of the most concrete manifestations of the Given-New Contract is how speakers introduce and track people, objects, and events in conversation.

**The Referent Introduction System:**

| Construction | Information status | What it signals | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| "a/an + noun" | Brand new | "You don't know this referent yet" | "I met **a woman** at the conference." |
| "this + noun" (informal) | Brand new, highlighted | "You don't know this referent, but pay attention" | "So **this guy** comes up to me and..." |
| "the + noun" | Given / identifiable | "You already know which one I mean" | "**The woman** from the conference called me." |
| Pronoun (she, he, it) | Given / highly accessible | "This referent is active in your mind right now" | "**She** said the project was canceled." |
| Proper name | Given / identifiable | "You know who this person is" | "**Sarah** called about the meeting." |
| "the + noun + relative clause" | Bridging (new but anchored) | "You don't know this specific referent, but you can infer it from context" | "**The woman who organized the conference** called me." |

**The system in action -- a correctly packaged narrative:**

> "I went to a conference last week. [introduces new referent: conference]
> The keynote speaker was incredible. [bridges from conference → speaker]
> She talked about AI in education. [tracks speaker with pronoun]
> After her talk, I met a woman in the hallway. [introduces new referent: woman]
> She runs a startup in Berlin. [tracks woman with pronoun]
> She gave me her card. [continues tracking]
> I emailed her yesterday and she wants to collaborate." [continues tracking]

Notice how each new referent gets introduced with "a/an" and then tracked with pronouns. The listener builds a mental model step by step: conference → speaker → woman → potential collaboration.

**The same narrative with Given-New violations:**

> "She wants to collaborate with me. [WHO? No referent established]
> The conference was last week. [What conference? Feels disconnected from "she"]
> Her startup is based in Berlin. [Whose startup?]
> I met her in the hallway after the keynote. [Still unclear who "her" is]
> The speaker was incredible. [Which speaker? The one from the conference? How does this connect?]"

The second version contains the same facts as the first. But it is dramatically harder to follow because new information arrives before the anchors that would make it interpretable.

### 1.3 The "Too Much Background" vs. "Not Enough Background" Problem

The Given-New Contract can fail in two opposite directions:

**Failure Mode 1: Too much given, not enough new (the "getting to the point" problem)**

> "So you know how we were talking last week about the project, and I mentioned that the timeline was tight, and then you said you thought we should talk to Sarah about it, and Sarah's the one who manages the budget, and she's been really busy lately because of the quarterly review..."

**What's wrong:** The speaker keeps restacking given information without ever arriving at the new point. The listener is waiting for the payoff and getting impatient. Each "and" adds more given context but no new information.

**The fix:** State the new information sooner, then backfill context as needed.

> "Sarah approved the budget extension. [NEW -- the point] Remember we discussed the tight timeline last week? [GIVEN -- just enough context] She reviewed it during the quarterly process and signed off." [NEW -- supporting detail]

**Failure Mode 2: Too much new, not enough given (the "out of nowhere" problem)**

> "The Berlin startup rejected the Series B term sheet from Sequoia because of the liquidation preference clause."

If the listener doesn't know about the Berlin startup, the Series B round, the Sequoia offer, or what a liquidation preference clause is, this sentence contains five new referents and zero given anchors. It is technically a grammatical English sentence. It is pragmatically a disaster.

**The fix:** Introduce one new thing at a time, anchoring each to something the listener already knows.

> "Remember that startup I told you about? [GIVEN anchor] They've been trying to raise funding. [NEW, anchored] Sequoia made them an offer. [NEW, anchored to the funding] But the terms had a clause that was really unfavorable -- something about investor payback priority. [NEW, anchored to the offer] So they turned it down." [NEW, anchored to the clause]

### 1.4 The "One New Referent Per Sentence" Heuristic

This is not an absolute rule, but it is a powerful practical guideline: **each sentence should ideally introduce no more than one new referent or one new piece of information.** 

When a sentence introduces multiple new elements simultaneously, the listener's processing load spikes. Research in psycholinguistics confirms this -- sentences with multiple new referents take longer to read and are more likely to produce comprehension errors.

| Overloaded sentence | Unpacked version |
|---|---|
| "My colleague John from the marketing department introduced me to his friend Sarah who works at a startup called TechFlow that just raised $5M." | "I have a colleague named John -- he's in marketing. John introduced me to his friend Sarah. She works at a startup called TechFlow. They just raised $5M." |
| "The restaurant we went to on Saturday that your sister recommended was closed because of the health inspection that happened last Tuesday." | "Remember that restaurant your sister recommended? We tried to go on Saturday. But it was closed. Apparently there was a health inspection last Tuesday." |

**Why this matters for autistic individuals specifically:** Many autistic individuals describe being excellent at tracking detailed information once they have a framework to attach it to. The problem is not processing capacity -- it is the initial anchoring. If a listener has not yet established a mental model for "John" before "Sarah" arrives, both referents may fail to stick. The one-new-thing heuristic respects this by building the framework incrementally.

## 2. FOCUS AND STRESS IN EVERYDAY SPEECH

### 2.1 How Stress Placement Changes the Information Point

(This section complements Category 15; here the focus is on the information-structure function rather than the acoustic/prosodic mechanism.)

When you stress a word in a sentence, you are declaring: "THIS is the new, important, or contrastive information." Everything else in the sentence is treated as given/background. The same words can have radically different information points depending on which word carries the stress.

**The classic demonstration:**

| Utterance (caps = stress) | What's new/focused | What's given/presupposed | What the speaker is really saying |
|---|---|---|---|
| "**I** didn't say he stole the money" | The identity of the speaker | Someone said it, he stole money | "Someone else said it, not me." |
| "I **DIDN'T** say he stole the money" | The polarity (negation) | I said something about him and money | "Contrary to what you think, I did not say that." |
| "I didn't **SAY** he stole the money" | The speech act | I communicated about the theft somehow | "I didn't say it -- I implied/wrote/hinted it." |
| "I didn't say **HE** stole the money" | The agent of the theft | Someone stole money, I said something about it | "I said someone else stole it, not him." |
| "I didn't say he **STOLE** the money" | The nature of the action | He did something with money | "I said he borrowed/found/lost it, not stole." |
| "I didn't say he stole **THE** money" | The specificity of the money | He stole some money | "He stole some OTHER money, not the specific money you're thinking of." |
| "I didn't say he stole the **MONEY**" | The object stolen | He stole something | "I said he stole the documents/jewelry/car, not money." |

Every one of those seven versions is grammatically identical. The ONLY difference is where the stress falls. And each version communicates a completely different information point -- a different claim about what's new, what's being corrected, what's being contrasted with what.

### 2.2 How Questions Constrain Focus in Answers

Questions are powerful focus-setters. A question tells the listener exactly which slot is "new" (the thing being asked about) and which is "given" (everything else). Competent speakers match their answer's focus to the question's focus.

**Question-Answer Focus Alignment:**

| Question | What's "given" in the answer | What's "new/focused" in the answer | Correct answer (stress on focus) | Incorrect answer (stress misplaced) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| "Who called?" | Someone called | The identity of the caller | "**JOHN** called." | "John **CALLED**." (sounds like you're correcting whether he called, not answering who) |
| "What did John do?" | John did something | What he did | "John **CALLED**." | "**JOHN** called." (sounds like you're correcting who did it, not what happened) |
| "Did John call or email?" | John contacted someone | The method | "He **CALLED**." | "**HE** called." (sounds like you're correcting who did it) |
| "When did she arrive?" | She arrived | The time | "She arrived **YESTERDAY**." | "She **ARRIVED** yesterday." (sounds like you're confirming she arrived, not when) |

**Why this matters:** When your focus doesn't match the question's focus, you sound like you're answering a different question -- or correcting a claim the asker didn't make. This can create confusion or even perceived rudeness. The question pre-selects a focus domain, and matching it is a pragmatic obligation.

### 2.3 Contrastive Focus: Correction, Rejection, and Selection

Contrastive focus is used when you are explicitly replacing one item with another. It is one of the clearest uses of information structure in everyday speech.

**Pattern: "Not X, Y" with stress on both the rejected and the selected item:**

| Situation | Contrastive focus utterance | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| Someone assumes you want coffee | "I don't want **COFFEE**, I want **TEA**." | Rejects coffee, selects tea |
| Someone says the meeting is Monday | "The meeting isn't on **MONDAY**, it's on **TUESDAY**." | Corrects day |
| Someone says you look tired | "I'm not **TIRED**, I'm **HUNGRY**." | Rejects one attribution, substitutes another |
| Someone says you should call | "I **DID** call. **TWICE**." | Focuses on the polarity (confirming the action happened) and adds new info |

**Contrastive focus in implicit correction:** Sometimes the contrast is not stated but implied. The listener must infer the rejected alternative.

| Utterance | Explicit contrast | Implicit rejected alternative |
|---|---|---|
| "I thought the movie was **GREAT**." (strong stress on "great") | None stated | Someone expressed a negative opinion; you're countering it |
| "**I** liked it." (stress on "I") | None stated | Someone else said they didn't like it; you're differentiating your opinion |
| "That was **INTERESTING**." (stress on "interesting") | None stated | The expected reaction might have been "boring" or "bad"; you're marking your different assessment |

**Practical note for autistic learners:** Contrastive focus is one of the most consistent and rule-governed aspects of information structure. If you hear someone stress a word that doesn't seem to need emphasis, ask yourself: "What is this word being contrasted with?" The answer is usually recoverable from the immediately preceding context.

## 3. INFORMATION STRUCTURE IN WRITTEN COMMUNICATION

### 3.1 Email Structure as Information Structure

Good email writing follows information structure principles. Bad email writing violates them. The difference is often the difference between "got a reply in five minutes" and "confused silence for three days."

**The anatomy of a well-structured email:**

```
Subject: Budget approval for Q3 marketing campaign    [TOPIC: tells the reader what this is about]

Hi Sarah,                                               [Greeting]

Following up on our meeting Tuesday about the           [GIVEN: anchors to shared knowledge]
Q3 marketing budget —

The finance team approved the $50K allocation.          [NEW: the main point / BLUF]

They did flag two conditions:                           [TRANSITION: signals more new info coming]
1. We need to submit receipts monthly (not quarterly)   [NEW: specific detail]
2. The training budget line can't exceed $10K           [NEW: specific detail]

I've attached the approval form. Could you sign         [NEW: action required]
and return it by Friday?

Thanks,
Alex
```

**What makes this work in information-structure terms:**

| Email section | Information function | Why it works |
|---|---|---|
| Subject line | Sets the topic (theme) | Reader knows what to expect before opening |
| "Following up on our meeting Tuesday" | Given information anchor | Links to shared experience; establishes common ground |
| "The finance team approved the $50K allocation" | New information / focus | THE POINT -- arrives early, clearly stated |
| "They did flag two conditions" | Transition from given (approval) to new (conditions) | Prepares the reader for caveats |
| Numbered list | New information, structured | Each item is one new thing, separately processable |
| "Could you sign and return it by Friday?" | New information: action request | Clear, specific, last (so it sticks) |

**The same email with information structure violations:**

```
Subject: Question

Hi Sarah,

So I was talking to finance and they mentioned 
something about receipts needing to be monthly 
instead of quarterly which I thought was weird because 
we always did quarterly but apparently that's a new 
policy and also there's a cap on the training line 
which is $10K and by the way the budget was approved 
which is great news. Can you sign the form? It's 
attached. This is for the Q3 marketing campaign.
```

**What's wrong:**
- Subject line tells the reader nothing (no topic established)
- No given-information anchor to shared context
- The main point ("budget was approved") is buried in the middle
- Multiple new referents introduced per sentence
- The topic ("Q3 marketing campaign") arrives last, when it should be first
- Action request is tacked on without emphasis
- No structure to separate different types of information

### 3.2 The "Inverted Pyramid" as Information Structure

Journalism uses a structure that is entirely explained by information-structure principles:

```
MOST NEWSWORTHY INFO (the NEW, the FOCUS)     ← Lead / headline
    ↓
IMPORTANT DETAILS                                ← Supporting new information
    ↓
BACKGROUND/CONTEXT (the GIVEN)                   ← Given info for those who need it
    ↓
ADDITIONAL DETAILS                               ← Least critical information
```

This is the opposite of conversational storytelling (which builds up to the new information). In journalism, the reader may stop reading at any point, so the most important new information must come first.

**The principle:** The expected reading behavior determines the optimal information structure. If the reader will read everything (a novel), build up to the new. If the reader may stop at any point (a news article, an email to a busy executive), front-load the new.

### 3.3 The "Given-Before-New" Principle in Paragraph Construction

Within paragraphs, the Given-Before-New principle creates cohesion -- the feeling that sentences "flow." Here's how:

**Technique: End-weight / end-focus**

English sentences tend to place new, heavy, or important information at the end. This is called "end-focus" (Halliday, 1967) or "end-weight" (Quirk et al., 1985). The beginning of a sentence is where given/topic information sits. The end is where new/focus information lands.

**Example of good flow (each sentence begins with given, ends with new):**

> "The research team completed the experiment last month. [introduces: team, experiment, timing]
> The experiment tested a new drug for chronic pain. [given: experiment → new: drug, pain]
> This drug, called XR-7, works by blocking a specific neural pathway. [given: drug → new: mechanism]
> The pathway is responsible for transmitting pain signals to the brain. [given: pathway → new: function]
> If the drug succeeds, it could replace opioids for millions of patients." [given: drug succeeding → new: impact]

Notice the chain: each sentence begins by picking up something from the end of the previous sentence (where it was new) and treating it as given (putting it at the start). This is called a **thematic progression** -- specifically, a "linear thematic progression" where the rheme (new info) of one sentence becomes the theme (given info) of the next.

**Example of poor flow (no thematic progression):**

> "Millions of patients rely on opioids for chronic pain.
> The research team completed an experiment last month.
> A specific neural pathway transmits pain signals.
> XR-7 is a new drug.
> The experiment tested the drug."

Same facts. But no flow. Each sentence starts with a different, unconnected topic. The reader has to do all the connecting work themselves.

### 3.4 Why "Walls of Text" Are Hard to Read

The "wall of text" problem is fundamentally an information-structure problem. When text has no paragraph breaks, no headings, no topic sentences, and no structural hierarchy, the reader has no way to distinguish:

- Given from new
- Topic from comment
- Main point from supporting detail
- One topic from the next

**A wall of text treats all information as equally important and equally new.** Since human processing depends on distinguishing important from unimportant, new from given, this creates maximum cognitive load.

**The fix is information structure made visible:**

| Structure element | Information-structure function |
|---|---|
| Heading | Declares the topic for everything that follows |
| Topic sentence (first sentence of paragraph) | Establishes the given/new frame for that paragraph |
| Paragraph break | Signals a topic shift |
| Bold/italic | Marks focus within running text |
| Bullet points | Separates discrete new items so each gets its own anchor |
| "In summary..." / "The key point is..." | Explicitly marks what the writer considers the focus |

## 4. CLEFT AND PSEUDO-CLEFT CONSTRUCTIONS

### 4.1 What Clefts Are and Why Speakers Use Them

Cleft sentences are a syntactic tool for explicitly marking what the focus is. They split ("cleave") a simple sentence into two clauses, one containing the focus and one containing the given/presupposed information.

**The basic mechanism:**

| Simple sentence | Cleft version | What's focused | What's presupposed |
|---|---|---|---|
| "The deadline stressed me out." | "**It was the deadline** that stressed me out." | The deadline (not something else) | Something stressed me out |
| "John broke the window." | "**It was John** who broke the window." | John (not someone else) | Someone broke the window |
| "She quit on Tuesday." | "**It was on Tuesday** that she quit." | Tuesday (not another day) | She quit at some point |
| "We need more time." | "**What we need** is more time." (pseudo-cleft) | More time | We need something |
| "She said something hurtful." | "**What she said** was something hurtful." (pseudo-cleft) | Something hurtful | She said something |

### 4.2 It-Clefts: "It was X that..."

**Structure:** It + be + FOCUSED ELEMENT + relative clause (containing the given information)

**When people use it-clefts:**

1. **To answer an implicit or explicit question:**
   - Q: "What went wrong with the project?"
   - A: "It was the **timeline** that killed us." (focuses "timeline" as the answer)

2. **To correct a misconception:**
   - A: "I heard you're upset about the salary."
   - B: "It wasn't the **salary** that bothered me, it was the **lack of communication**." (corrects the assumed cause)

3. **To create emphasis or drama:**
   - "It was **that moment** that changed everything." (could just say "That moment changed everything" but the cleft adds weight)

4. **To isolate a specific element for contrast:**
   - "It was **Sarah**, not **John**, who made the decision." (pinpoints who is responsible)

**Real-world examples in everyday speech:**

| Context | Cleft utterance | Why a cleft and not a simple sentence |
|---|---|---|
| Someone blames the wrong person | "It was **Mark** who forgot the keys, not me." | Explicitly focuses the responsible person |
| Explaining what helped | "It was **the medication change** that made the difference." | Isolates the cause from other possible causes |
| Clarifying timing | "It was **after** the meeting that I found out." | Focuses when, distinguishing from "during" or "before" |
| Identifying the problem | "It was the **noise**, not the workload, that was overwhelming." | Corrects misattribution of the cause |

### 4.3 Wh-Clefts (Pseudo-Clefts): "What I need is..."

**Structure:** What-clause (given) + be + FOCUSED ELEMENT (new)

Pseudo-clefts are extremely common in informal speech and serve a slightly different function from it-clefts. They are often used to:

1. **Set up the framework before delivering the point:**
   - "What I'm trying to say is that I need help." (frames the speech act before delivering the content)
   - "What happened was, the whole system crashed." (frames "I'm about to tell you what happened" before the event)

2. **Make a request or suggestion sound less direct:**
   - "What I'd suggest is starting with the basics." (softer than "Start with the basics")
   - "What might help is taking a short break." (softer than "Take a break")

3. **Organize an explanation:**
   - "What causes the problem is a conflict between two settings." (frames before explaining)
   - "What you need to do is restart the router." (frames before instructing)

**Common pseudo-cleft patterns in everyday English:**

| Pattern | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| "What happened was..." | Introduces a narrative | "What happened was, my alarm didn't go off." |
| "What I mean is..." | Clarifies intent | "What I mean is, we should try a different approach." |
| "What I need is..." | Makes a request | "What I need is about twenty more minutes." |
| "What concerns me is..." | Raises an issue | "What concerns me is the lack of testing." |
| "What we should do is..." | Proposes a plan | "What we should do is split the work into phases." |
| "What's interesting is..." | Highlights noteworthy information | "What's interesting is that nobody complained." |
| "The thing is..." | Introduces a complication (informal cleft-like) | "The thing is, we don't have the budget for that." |
| "The reason is..." | Provides an explanation | "The reason is that the server was down." |

### 4.4 Why Clefts Matter for Clarity

Clefts are not fancy or literary. They are practical tools for disambiguating what your point is. Compare:

| Without cleft | With cleft | What the cleft adds |
|---|---|---|
| "The timeline was the problem." | "It was **the timeline** that was the problem." | Explicitly presupposes something was a problem; isolates timeline |
| "We need better documentation." | "What we need is **better documentation**." | Frames "we need something" as given; focuses the specific need |
| "Nobody told me about the change." | "**What nobody told me** was that the deadline moved." | Separates the communication failure from what was not communicated |

**Practical tip for autistic learners:** If someone seems confused about what your main point is, try rephrasing as a cleft. "What I'm trying to say is [X]" or "The thing that matters here is [X]" explicitly labels X as your focus. This removes the ambiguity that simple sentences can create.

## 5. PASSIVE VOICE AS INFORMATION STRUCTURE TOOL

### 5.1 Why the Passive Exists (It's Not About Laziness or Evasion)

The passive voice is often taught as something to avoid. This advice is misleading. The passive exists because English information structure sometimes requires it -- specifically, when the logical object (the thing acted upon) needs to be the topic/theme (the starting point of the sentence) and the logical subject (the agent) is new information, unimportant, or unknown.

**The active/passive choice is an information-packaging decision:**

| Active | Passive | When passive is better |
|---|---|---|
| "A drunk driver hit the car." | "The car was hit by a drunk driver." | When "the car" is the current topic (already being discussed) |
| "Someone stole my bike." | "My bike was stolen." | When the agent is unknown |
| "The committee approved the proposal." | "The proposal was approved." | When the agent is irrelevant to the point |
| "Lightning struck the building." | "The building was struck by lightning." | When you're telling a story about the building, not about lightning |

### 5.2 Passive Moves Given Information to Topic Position

English has a strong preference for placing the topic (what the sentence is about) in subject position -- the beginning of the sentence. When the logical object is the topic (because it's been mentioned before, or because it's the entity under discussion), passive voice is the tool that moves it there.

**Worked example -- a paragraph about a proposal:**

> "We submitted a proposal to the city council last month. [Active: "we" is the topic, "proposal" is new]
> **The proposal** was reviewed by three independent auditors. [Passive: "proposal" is now the topic (given), auditors are new]
> **It** was returned with minor revisions requested. [Passive: "it" (the proposal) continues as topic]
> **The revisions** were completed yesterday. [Passive: "revisions" (given from previous sentence) is topic]
> We'll resubmit **it** on Monday." [Active: returns to "we" as agent for the next action]

Now try the same paragraph forcing active voice throughout:

> "We submitted a proposal to the city council last month.
> Three independent auditors reviewed the proposal. [Topic shifts to "auditors" -- disruptive]
> Someone returned it with minor revisions requested. [Who? Awkward "someone"]
> We completed the revisions yesterday. [Topic shifts to "we" -- choppy]
> We'll resubmit it on Monday."

The all-active version requires the reader to track shifting topics (we → auditors → someone → we → we). The mixed version maintains "the proposal" as a consistent through-line, which is what the paragraph is actually about.

### 5.3 Strategic Uses of the Passive

| Strategy | Example | Information-structure function |
|---|---|---|
| **Hiding the agent** (deliberately) | "Mistakes were made." | Avoids assigning blame (famously used in political speech) |
| **Unknown agent** | "The package was delivered while I was out." | Agent genuinely not known |
| **Irrelevant agent** | "The building was constructed in 1923." | Who built it doesn't matter; the building's history is the point |
| **Maintaining topic continuity** | "She applied for the job. She was interviewed on Tuesday. She was offered the position on Friday." | "She" remains the topic throughout |
| **Creating end-focus on the agent** | "The theory was developed by Einstein." | Saves "Einstein" for the emphatic end position |
| **Scientific/technical register** | "The samples were heated to 100°C." | De-emphasizes the researcher; focuses on the procedure |

### 5.4 When Passive Creates Problems

| Problematic passive | Why it's problematic | Better alternative |
|---|---|---|
| "It has been decided that the project will be discontinued." | WHO decided? The agent is relevant and being hidden. | "The VP decided to discontinue the project." |
| "You will be contacted by a representative who will be assigned to your case by the department." | Triple passive creates a maze; the reader loses track of who's doing what. | "We'll assign a representative to your case. They'll contact you directly." |
| "The ball was thrown by the boy to the dog in the park on Saturday." | Active version is simpler and the boy is the natural topic. | "The boy threw the ball to the dog in the park on Saturday." |

**The decision rule:** Use passive when the logical object is your topic (the thing you're talking about) or when the agent is unknown/irrelevant. Use active when the agent is your topic or when the agent matters. This is not about style preferences -- it's about information structure.

## 6. TOPICALIZATION AND FRONTING

### 6.1 What Topicalization Is

Topicalization moves an element from its normal position in the sentence to the front -- the topic position -- to signal "this is what I'm about to say something about." English word order is relatively fixed (subject-verb-object), so moving something to the front is a strong signal.

**Standard word order vs. topicalized:**

| Standard order | Topicalized | What the topicalization signals |
|---|---|---|
| "I love that movie." | "**That movie**, I love." | "Let me tell you about that movie" -- the movie is being set up as the topic |
| "I can't stand his attitude." | "**His attitude**, I can't stand." | Marks "his attitude" as the topic of comment |
| "I understand the first part, but I don't get the second part." | "**The first part** I understand, but **the second part** I don't get." | Creates a parallel contrast between the two parts |
| "We should put the report on the top shelf." | "**The report**, put it on the top shelf." | In instructions, marks which item is being addressed |

### 6.2 When Topicalization Happens in Real Speech

Topicalization is more common in speech than in formal writing. It often occurs when:

1. **Shifting to a new topic that has been mentioned before:**
   - "So about the car, I finally got it fixed."
   - "Your email from yesterday -- I had a few questions."
   - "The budget issue, I think we need to table that."

2. **Creating contrast between items:**
   - "The food was amazing. **The service**, not so much."
   - "Math I can handle. **Chemistry** gives me trouble."
   - "His work ethic is great. **His communication**, though, needs improvement."

3. **Resuming a previously interrupted topic:**
   - "Anyway, **that thing I was saying earlier** -- so basically what happened was..."
   - "Going back to **the parking situation** -- did you find out if they're adding more spaces?"

### 6.3 Left Dislocation: The "Referent Reactivation" Construction

Left dislocation is a close relative of topicalization. It moves a noun phrase to the front AND leaves a pronoun behind in the original position.

**Pattern:** NOUN PHRASE, ... pronoun ...

| Example | What it does |
|---|---|
| "**My neighbor**, he never takes out his trash." | Reactivates "my neighbor" as the topic, then comments about him |
| "**That restaurant on Main Street**, have you been there?" | Introduces a referent and immediately asks about it |
| "**The new policy**, I don't understand it at all." | Marks "the new policy" as topic, then states the comment |

**When speakers use left dislocation:**
- When switching topics: "**Your sister**, how's she doing?"
- When reactivating a dormant referent: "**That book you lent me**, I finally finished it."
- When the topic might be ambiguous: "**The one I was telling you about**, she got promoted."

**Why this matters:** Left dislocation is stigmatized in formal writing (editors mark it as "colloquial" or "informal") but is extremely common and useful in speech. It explicitly separates the "what we're talking about" (the topic) from "what I'm saying about it" (the comment). For autistic speakers learning information structure, left dislocation is a powerful and transparent tool -- it literally puts the topic first, then tells you about it.

### 6.4 Negative Inversion

Negative inversion places a negative or restrictive element at the front of the sentence, which triggers subject-verb inversion (like a question). It signals strong emphasis and is more common in formal or dramatic registers.

| Standard | Negative inversion | Register/effect |
|---|---|---|
| "I have never seen such a thing." | "**Never have I** seen such a thing." | Formal, emphatic |
| "He not only lied, he stole." | "**Not only did he** lie, he stole." | Dramatic escalation |
| "She rarely complains." | "**Rarely does she** complain." | Emphasizes the rarity |
| "I didn't realize it until later." | "**Not until later did I** realize it." | Focuses the timing |

**Practical note:** Negative inversion is uncommon in casual conversation. Hearing it signals formality, drama, or rhetorical emphasis. You don't need to produce it often, but recognizing it helps you identify when someone is being deliberately emphatic or performative.

## 7. COMMON INFORMATION STRUCTURE FAILURES

This section catalogs the most frequent ways information structure goes wrong, with examples and fixes. These are patterns, not personality flaws -- anyone can produce them, and they are all fixable with awareness.

### 7.1 Failure: Pronoun Without Antecedent ("Who?")

**The problem:** Using a pronoun (he, she, they, it) before the listener has any way to know who or what it refers to.

| Failing version | What the listener thinks | Fixed version |
|---|---|---|
| "She's so annoying today." (said to someone who doesn't know who "she" is) | "Who is?" | "My coworker Anna is so annoying today." |
| "They rejected it." (in an email with no prior context) | "Who rejected what?" | "The admissions committee rejected my application." |
| "He told me about it." | "Who told you about what?" | "My boss told me about the restructuring." |
| "It's broken again." | "What is?" | "The printer is broken again." |

**The rule:** A pronoun requires an accessible antecedent -- something already active in the discourse that the pronoun can refer back to. If you're starting a new conversation, starting a new topic, or talking to someone who wasn't present for the earlier context, you need to use a full noun phrase, not a pronoun.

**Exception:** When the referent is physically present and obvious (pointing at the printer and saying "it's broken" is fine -- the shared perceptual context makes the referent accessible).

### 7.2 Failure: Burying the Point ("What Was the Point?")

**The problem:** The most important new information is not in a prominent position (sentence-initial or sentence-final) but is hidden in the middle of a longer stretch of discourse.

**Example -- buried point in a workplace update:**

> "So the client called this morning and we went over the timeline again and talked about the scope changes they want and the revised budget figures and apparently the contract needs to be extended by three months which I know is a big deal but they also mentioned that the user testing phase went really well."

**Where is the point?** There are actually two important pieces of news -- (1) contract extended by three months and (2) user testing went well -- but both are buried in a run-on. The listener may catch neither.

**Fixed version:**

> "Two updates from the client call this morning. [FRAME: tells the listener what's coming]
> First: they want to extend the contract by three months. [NEW POINT 1: prominent position]
> Second: the user testing phase went really well. [NEW POINT 2: prominent position]
> The scope and budget changes we already knew about." [GIVEN: confirms background is unchanged]

### 7.3 Failure: The Wall of Text (No Structure = No Information Structure)

**The problem:** A long message with no paragraphs, no headings, and no structural markers. Everything is at the same level, so the reader cannot distinguish:
- What's important from what's minor
- What's the topic from what's a digression
- Where one idea ends and another begins

This is the written equivalent of speaking in a monotone: all information is given equal prosodic weight, so the listener cannot identify focus.

**Why this is particularly relevant for autistic individuals:** Many autistic individuals are detail-oriented and may want to include all relevant information. The temptation to write a wall of text comes from thoroughness, not carelessness. But the wall of text fails because it forces the reader to do the information-structuring work that the writer should do. The fix is not "write less" but "structure what you write."

**Quick wall-of-text fix template:**

```
[TOPIC SENTENCE: What this message is about]

[MAIN POINT: The most important new information]

[SUPPORTING DETAILS: Organized in logical groups]
- Detail 1
- Detail 2  
- Detail 3

[ACTION ITEM: What you need from the reader, if anything]
```

### 7.4 Failure: Too Many New Referents at Once ("Overloaded")

**The problem:** A sentence or short passage introduces multiple new people, places, concepts, or events simultaneously without giving the listener time to absorb each one.

| Overloaded | Why it fails | Restructured |
|---|---|---|
| "My friend Karen's colleague from the Berlin office mentioned to the team lead that the vendor's compliance report flagged three issues." | Six new referents in one sentence: Karen, colleague, Berlin office, team lead, vendor, compliance report | "I have a friend named Karen who works in our Berlin office. Her colleague found some problems in a vendor's report -- specifically, the compliance check flagged three issues. The colleague escalated it to the team lead." |
| "Professor Williams assigned the Martinez-Goldberg paper from the 2019 Stanford symposium proceedings as a response to the Chen framework critique." | Five new referents: Williams, the paper, the symposium, Chen, the critique | "Professor Williams assigned a new reading. It's a paper by Martinez and Goldberg, published in 2019 from a Stanford symposium. The paper responds to a critique of the Chen framework." |

### 7.5 Failure: Topic Shift Without Signaling

**The problem:** The speaker or writer moves to a completely new topic without any transition marker, leaving the audience still trying to connect the new information to the old topic.

**In conversation:**
> A: "The restaurant was great. The pasta was fresh."
> A: "Did you hear back about the job?"

The listener is still mentally in "restaurant" mode. The abrupt shift to "job" creates a processing gap. 

**The fix -- use a topic-shift marker:**
> A: "The restaurant was great. The pasta was fresh."
> A: "Oh, **completely different topic** -- did you hear back about the job?"

Or: "**By the way**...", "**Switching gears**...", "**Unrelated, but**...", "**On a different note**..."

(See Category 5: Discourse Markers for a full catalog of topic-shift markers.)

### 7.6 Failure: Given Information Treated as New ("I Already Know That")

**The problem:** The speaker presents information the listener already knows as if it were news. This violates Grice's Maxim of Quantity (don't say more than needed) and feels redundant or condescending.

| Over-explanation | What the listener thinks | Right amount |
|---|---|---|
| "So as you know, we had a meeting on Tuesday, which was two days ago, at 3pm, in the conference room on the third floor..." | "Yes, I was THERE." | "Following up on Tuesday's meeting..." |
| "Email, which is a system for sending electronic messages over the internet..." | "I know what email is." | "I'll send you an email about it." |
| "Your mother -- that's my wife's mother, your grandmother on your dad's side..." | "I know who my grandmother is." | "Your grandmother called." |

**The reverse problem:** Presenting given information as if the listener couldn't possibly know it can also feel condescending. Skilled communicators calibrate how much given context to provide based on an assessment of the listener's knowledge (see Category 16: Common Ground).

## 8. INFORMATION STRUCTURE IN SPECIFIC CONTEXTS

### 8.1 Workplace Presentations

Effective presentations follow a predictable information structure at every level:

**Macro-level (whole presentation):**

| Section | Information function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Opening | Establish the topic and the audience's given knowledge | "As you all know, we've been working on improving customer retention." |
| Problem statement | Transition from given to new | "What we've found is that retention drops 40% after the first month." |
| Main content | New information, organized by subtopics | Data, analysis, proposals |
| Conclusion | Summarize new information; return to the topic | "So the key takeaway is: invest in the first-month experience." |
| Call to action | The newest and most important new information | "I'm asking for budget approval to pilot a new onboarding program." |

**Micro-level (individual slides):**

Each slide should follow Given → New:

| Slide element | Function |
|---|---|
| Title | The topic (given or framing) |
| First bullet/sentence | Anchor to what audience already knows |
| Subsequent bullets | Build toward the new information |
| Last bullet or highlighted point | The key new takeaway from this slide |

**Common presentation failure:** Starting with the most technical/detailed new information before establishing why the audience should care. This is the information-structure equivalent of answering a question nobody asked.

### 8.2 Academic Writing

Academic writing has an extremely formalized information structure:

| Section | Information function | Given-New balance |
|---|---|---|
| **Abstract** | Compressed version of the whole paper's information structure | Given (context) → New (findings) in ~200 words |
| **Introduction** | Establishes shared knowledge | Almost entirely GIVEN -- what the field already knows |
| **Literature Review** | Establishes detailed shared knowledge | GIVEN -- what specific researchers have found |
| **Research Gap** | The pivot point | The critical transition: "What is NOT yet known" = the NEW question |
| **Methods** | How you sought the new information | Procedural information (neither given nor new in the traditional sense) |
| **Results** | The new information | The NEW -- this is what the paper contributes |
| **Discussion** | Integrating new with given | Links new findings back to the given (existing literature) |
| **Conclusion** | Summary of new contribution | Final statement of what's now known that wasn't before |

**The information-structure insight:** An academic paper is essentially one huge Given-New sequence. The entire first half (intro, lit review) establishes what counts as "given" in the field. The entire second half (results, discussion) delivers the "new." The research gap is the hinge point -- it converts the given into a question that the new answers.

### 8.3 Medical Communication

Doctor-patient communication is a masterclass in using focus to extract specific new information.

**How doctors use questions to set up focus:**

| Doctor's question | What's given | What's new (what the doctor needs) | Patient's focused answer |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Where does it hurt?" | You're in pain (given) | The location (new/focus) | "In my **lower back**." |
| "When did it start?" | It started at some point (given) | The time (new/focus) | "**Two days ago**." |
| "Is it a sharp pain or a dull ache?" | You have pain (given) | The type (new/focus, constrained to two options) | "More of a **dull ache**." |
| "Does anything make it better?" | It hurts (given) | Mitigating factors (new/focus) | "**Lying down** helps." |
| "On a scale of 1-10, how severe is it?" | You have pain (given) | The severity (new/focus, numerically constrained) | "About a **six**." |

Each question progressively narrows the focus domain, extracting one specific new piece of information at a time. This is information structure as a diagnostic tool.

**Why this matters for autistic individuals:** In medical settings, autistic patients sometimes provide too much information (the whole history of their symptoms, associated experiences, related concerns) when the doctor's question is focused on one specific element. Understanding that each question creates a narrow focus domain helps: when the doctor asks "where does it hurt?" they want a location, not a narrative. You can provide the narrative later, but first match their focus.

### 8.4 Social Media and Character-Limited Communication

Character limits (tweets, text messages, headlines) force extreme information-packaging decisions. There is not room for both given and new -- so writers must choose.

**Twitter/X information structure strategies:**

| Strategy | Example | What's sacrificed |
|---|---|---|
| **All new, no given** (assumes shared context) | "Finally got approved!!" | The reader must already know what was applied for |
| **Topic + reaction** (minimal given + new) | "The new policy: absolutely terrible." | No detail about why; the opinion IS the new information |
| **Given anchor + new point** | "Remember the server crash last week? Found the root cause." | Balanced but uses characters on the anchor |
| **Focus only** (the answer without the question) | "42%." | Completely opaque without shared context; works only for in-group |

**Text message information structure:**

Texting often relies on the ongoing conversation thread to provide given context. Each message adds one new thing:

> A: "Want to do dinner tonight?" [NEW: proposes dinner]
> B: "Sure" [NEW: acceptance]
> A: "Thai?" [NEW: specific cuisine -- question uses minimal characters because "dinner tonight" is now given]
> B: "I had Thai yesterday" [NEW: relevant constraint]
> A: "Italian?" [NEW: alternative -- the whole frame "cuisine for dinner tonight" is given]
> B: "Perfect" [NEW: acceptance]

This exchange is radically compressed but perfectly clear because each message relies on the previous messages to establish the given context. If someone read only B's messages ("Sure" / "I had Thai yesterday" / "Perfect"), they would be incomprehensible -- because the given information lives in A's messages.

## 9. PRACTICAL STRATEGIES FOR MANAGING INFORMATION STRUCTURE

### 9.1 The "One New Thing Per Sentence" Rule

Already introduced in Section 1.4, this deserves emphasis as a top-level strategy. When in doubt about whether your message is clear, check: does any sentence introduce more than one brand-new referent or concept? If so, split it.

**Self-check process:**

1. Read your sentence/message
2. Underline everything that is NEW to the reader
3. If there are more than two underlined items in a single sentence, split the sentence
4. Make sure each piece of new information is anchored to something given

### 9.2 The BLUF Strategy (Bottom Line Up Front)

BLUF is a military communication principle that has become standard in business writing. It means: **state your main point in the first sentence.**

| Without BLUF | With BLUF |
|---|---|
| "I've been thinking about the quarterly numbers and looking at the trends from last year and comparing them to this year's performance, and after considering all the factors including seasonal variation and the new product launch..." (reader is still waiting for the point) | "We need to increase the marketing budget by 15%. Here's why: the quarterly numbers show..." (point delivered, context follows) |
| "So I went to the mechanic and he looked at the car and did some tests and called me back..." | "The car needs a new transmission. The mechanic found the problem today." |

**Why BLUF works in information-structure terms:** It treats the reader's need for the new information as more urgent than the speaker's desire to build up to it. Given information (background, context, reasoning) is still provided -- it just comes AFTER the focus, not before.

**When BLUF is wrong:** Storytelling, narratives, and emotional communication often require building up to the point. "Your grandmother died" is BLUF but is not how most people would deliver that news. Sensitive information typically benefits from a given-before-new approach that prepares the listener.

### 9.3 Transition Phrases as Information Structure Signals

Transition phrases explicitly tell the listener what's happening to the information flow. They are the road signs of information structure.

**Given-to-new transitions:**

| Transition | What it signals | Example |
|---|---|---|
| "Building on that..." | The next thing extends what was just said (given → new) | "Building on that, I'd also suggest we revisit the timeline." |
| "Relatedly..." | The next thing is connected to the current topic | "Relatedly, the budget needs adjustment too." |
| "This means that..." | The next thing is a consequence of what was just said | "This means that we'll need more time." |
| "In other words..." | Reformulation -- same information, new packaging | "In other words, we're behind schedule." |

**Topic-shift transitions:**

| Transition | What it signals | Example |
|---|---|---|
| "On a different note..." | Topic is changing completely | "On a different note, have you seen the new office layout?" |
| "Switching gears..." | Deliberate topic change | "Switching gears -- how's the hiring going?" |
| "Speaking of [X]..." | New topic is triggered by something in the old topic | "Speaking of timelines, when is the Q4 review?" |
| "By the way..." | New topic is secondary/incidental | "By the way, I'll be out next Friday." |
| "Anyway..." | Returning to a previous topic after digression | "Anyway, back to the budget question..." |

**Focus-marking transitions:**

| Transition | What it signals | Example |
|---|---|---|
| "The key point is..." | Explicitly marks what follows as focus | "The key point is that we can't afford another delay." |
| "What matters most is..." | Ranks information by importance | "What matters most is patient safety." |
| "Here's the thing..." | Signals the critical new information | "Here's the thing -- nobody actually approved this." |
| "The bottom line is..." | BLUF marker | "The bottom line is we need three more weeks." |
| "Most importantly..." | Explicit focus/importance marker | "Most importantly, test everything before launch." |

### 9.4 Topic Sentences as Information Structure Anchors

Every paragraph should begin with a sentence that establishes the topic -- what that paragraph is about. This is the written equivalent of declaring the topic at the start of a conversational turn.

**How topic sentences work:**

| Topic sentence | What it establishes | What the paragraph can then do |
|---|---|---|
| "The second issue is cost." | Topic: cost | Subsequent sentences provide new information about cost |
| "Not everyone agrees with this approach." | Topic: disagreement | Subsequent sentences present the objections |
| "The solution turned out to be simpler than expected." | Topic: the solution | Subsequent sentences explain what it was |

**When topic sentences are missing:** Each sentence in the paragraph can feel disconnected. The reader must infer the connecting theme. This is extra work for the reader and often leads to misunderstanding.

### 9.5 The "Known-Unknown-Discovery" Narrative Frame

For longer explanations or presentations, a powerful information-structure template is:

1. **Known:** What we/you already know (given)
2. **Unknown:** What we/you don't know (the gap -- transitions from given to new)
3. **Discovery:** What I found out / what I'm proposing (new / focus)

**Example:**

> "We've been tracking customer complaints for six months. [KNOWN]
> What we couldn't figure out was why complaints spiked every third week. [UNKNOWN -- the gap]
> It turns out the spike correlates with our billing cycle -- customers get confused by the invoice format. [DISCOVERY -- the new information]"

This frame is used in academic papers (lit review → research gap → findings), detective stories (the crime → the mystery → the solution), medical diagnoses (symptoms → unknown cause → diagnosis), and business presentations (current state → problem → proposed solution). It works because it perfectly follows the given-new progression: anchor the listener in what's known, create a gap that needs filling, then fill it.

## 10. CONNECTIONS TO OTHER PRAGMATIC CATEGORIES

### 10.1 Discourse Markers (Category 5) as Information Structure Signals

Discourse markers are the explicit signals of information structure transitions. They tell the listener whether information is given, new, a topic shift, a focus marker, or a return to a previous thread.

| Discourse marker | Information structure function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| "So..." | Signals a conclusion (given information → new inference) | "So, we should probably reschedule." |
| "Well..." | Signals that the answer may not match the expected focus | "Well, it's not exactly what happened..." |
| "Actually..." | Marks contrastive focus -- corrects a prior assumption | "Actually, it was Tuesday, not Monday." |
| "Anyway..." | Returns to a previous topic after a digression | "Anyway, about the project..." |
| "By the way..." | Introduces new topic as secondary | "By the way, I found your book." |
| "Speaking of which..." | Bridges from current topic to a related new topic | "Speaking of which, did you call the plumber?" |
| "Now..." | Marks a shift to a new section or phase | "Now, let's talk about the budget." |
| "Look..." | Marks upcoming focus -- "pay attention to what comes next" | "Look, the real problem is trust." |
| "Here's the thing..." | Explicitly marks the focused/important information | "Here's the thing -- nobody approved this." |
| "The point is..." | Labels the focus directly | "The point is we need to act now." |

### 10.2 Prosodic Stress (Category 15) as Focus Realization

As noted in the introduction, Category 15 covers the mechanism (how stress marks focus) while this category covers the system (what focus does in information structure). The key connections:

- **Nuclear stress** (the main stress in a clause) typically falls on the last major constituent of the clause -- this is the **default focus position** (the Nuclear Stress Rule). When everything in the sentence is new, stress falls at the end.
- **Contrastive stress** (stress shifted to a non-default position) signals that a specific element is being focused, typically for correction or contrast.
- **Deaccenting** (reducing stress on a word that would normally carry it) signals that the word is given information. "I brought the CAKE" vs. "I brought the cake" -- in the second version, "cake" is deaccented because it's already known; the stress falls elsewhere to mark what's new.

### 10.3 Narrative Structure (Category 12) and Information Packaging

Storytelling is information structure in action. Every narrative decision is an information-packaging decision:

- **Story prefaces** ("You'll never believe what happened...") set up the given/new frame
- **Orientation** (who, what, where, when) establishes given information before the plot begins
- **Complication** (what went wrong) is the first major new information
- **Evaluation** (why it matters) marks the focus of the narrative
- **Resolution** (how it ended) delivers the final new information

When someone is a "bad storyteller," the issue is often information structure: they bury the point, fail to establish who the characters are, jump between topics, or deliver the punchline before the setup.

### 10.4 Common Ground (Category 16) Determines What Counts as "Given"

The entire given/new distinction rests on an assessment of what the listener knows -- which is common ground (Category 16). This means information structure is always an act of **audience design**: what counts as "given" for one listener may be "new" for another.

**The calibration problem:**

| Listener | What's "given" for them | How you should package "The server crashed because of a memory leak in the authentication module" |
|---|---|---|
| Your tech lead (shared context) | The server, the crash, basic technical concepts | "It was a memory leak in the auth module." (Mostly given, one new element) |
| Your manager (partial context) | The server, maybe the crash | "The server crash yesterday was caused by a memory leak -- basically, one part of the software used up all the available memory." (Mixed given and new with explanation) |
| Your grandmother (no shared context) | None of this | "The computer system at work broke down. There was a technical problem where part of the software used too much of the computer's resources." (All new, simplified) |

The same event requires three completely different information packages depending on the listener's common ground. This is why "audience awareness" is not a vague social skill -- it is a concrete information-structure calibration based on the listener's existing knowledge.

### 10.5 Question Design (Category 17) and Focus

Questions are the primary tool for controlling information structure in conversation. Every question does two things:

1. **Establishes what's given** (the presupposition of the question)
2. **Opens a focus slot** (the variable that needs filling)

| Question | What it establishes as given | The focus slot it opens |
|---|---|---|
| "Who ate the cake?" | Someone ate the cake | The identity of the eater |
| "Did you eat the cake?" | There is cake; you might have eaten it | Yes or no (polarity focus) |
| "What did you eat?" | You ate something | The thing you ate |
| "Why did you eat the cake?" | You ate the cake | Your reason |
| "Did you eat the cake or the pie?" | You ate one of two things | Which one (alternative focus) |

This is why **leading questions** are manipulative -- they smuggle new assertions into the given slot. "When did you stop cheating?" presupposes that you cheated. "Why is this project behind schedule?" presupposes it IS behind schedule. The question format makes the presupposition hard to challenge because the listener's attention is directed to the focus slot (the answer), not the given slot (the assumption).

## 11. WORKED EXAMPLES: SAME CONTENT, DIFFERENT PACKAGING

This section presents the same underlying information packaged in multiple ways, demonstrating how information structure changes the pragmatic effect.

### 11.1 Delivering News About a Project Delay

**The facts:** The project deadline has been moved from March 15 to April 1. The reason is that the client requested additional features. You need the team to adjust their timelines.

**Packaging 1: BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front)**
> "The deadline has moved to April 1. The client requested additional features, and the PM approved the extension. Please adjust your individual timelines by end of day Friday."

**Effect:** Direct, efficient, respects the reader's time. Good for teams that value clarity. May feel abrupt for people who want context first.

**Packaging 2: Given-Before-New (Narrative Build)**
> "As you know, we've been working toward a March 15 deadline. Last week, the client came back with some additional feature requests. After discussion with the PM, we've agreed to extend the deadline to April 1. I'll need everyone to adjust their timelines by Friday."

**Effect:** Gentler, provides reasoning before the news. Good for teams that want to understand why, or when the news might be unwelcome.

**Packaging 3: Focus on the Opportunity (Cleft construction)**
> "What this gives us is two extra weeks. The client requested additional features, and the PM agreed to move the deadline from March 15 to April 1. Let's use the time wisely -- please send me updated timelines by Friday."

**Effect:** Reframes the delay as a positive. The pseudo-cleft ("What this gives us is...") focuses on the benefit. Same facts, different pragmatic framing.

**Packaging 4: Focus on the Action Required (End-focus)**
> "Due to additional client feature requests, the deadline has shifted from March 15 to April 1. The critical next step: **everyone needs to submit updated timelines by end of day Friday**."

**Effect:** Buries the context in a subordinate clause and puts the action item in the focus position (the end). Good when the most important thing is what people need to do, not why.

### 11.2 Explaining Why You're Late to a Meeting

**The facts:** You are 15 minutes late. Your previous meeting ran over. You are sorry.

**Packaging 1: Apology-focused (focus on the speech act)**
> "I'm so sorry I'm late."

**Effect:** Focuses on the apology itself. Does not explain why. Appropriate when the reason doesn't matter or when brief is best.

**Packaging 2: Cause-focused (focus on the reason)**
> "My previous meeting ran over -- sorry about that."

**Effect:** The reason comes first (new information in prominent position), apology comes second. Explains the lateness without dwelling on the apology.

**Packaging 3: Impact-focused (focus on what you're going to do about it)**
> "Sorry, I know we're short on time now. Let me catch up -- where are we?"

**Effect:** Acknowledges the impact and immediately pivots to moving forward. Focus is on recovery, not the cause.

**Packaging 4: Minimizing (backgrounding the lateness entirely)**
> "Sorry -- go ahead, don't let me interrupt."

**Effect:** The lateness is barely acknowledged. Focus is shifted entirely to the other participants. This works when you don't want to make the lateness a "thing."

### 11.3 Giving Feedback on Someone's Work

**The facts:** A colleague's report has good analysis but poor organization. You need them to restructure it.

**Packaging 1: Good-news focus first (focus on the positive)**
> "The analysis in this report is really strong. What would make it even better is reorganizing the sections so the key findings come first."

**Effect:** The praise is in the initial/topic position (what we're talking about). The suggestion is in the focus position (what's new). This is the "sandwich" approach, though here only the first and last layers are used.

**Packaging 2: Problem-focused (focus on what needs to change)**
> "The report needs to be restructured. The analysis is good, but the organization is making it hard to find the key findings."

**Effect:** The needed action is in topic position. The explanation follows. More direct -- appropriate when the urgency of the fix outweighs the need for face-saving.

**Packaging 3: Reader-impact focused (focus on the audience)**
> "When the executives read this, they'll want the key findings up front. The analysis is great -- it just needs to be reorganized so the conclusions are immediately visible."

**Effect:** Shifts the focus away from what the writer did wrong and onto what the reader needs. This externalizes the reason for the change, which can feel less personal.

**Packaging 4: Cleft construction (explicitly marking the issue)**
> "It's the **organization** that needs work, not the content. The analysis is solid. Can you restructure so the findings lead?"

**Effect:** The cleft explicitly isolates what needs to change ("organization") and what doesn't ("content"). This is maximally clear about the scope of the feedback.

## 12. INFORMATION STRUCTURE QUICK REFERENCE

### 12.1 The Five Core Principles

| Principle | What it means | Practical rule |
|---|---|---|
| **Given before new** | Anchor new information to what the listener already knows | Start sentences with known entities; introduce new ones with "a" or a descriptive phrase |
| **One new thing per sentence** | Don't overload any single sentence with multiple new referents | Split complex sentences; let each new element have its own sentence |
| **Focus goes at the end** (or is marked by stress/cleft) | The most important new information should be in a prominent position | Put the point at the end of the sentence, or use a cleft to mark it |
| **Signal your transitions** | Listeners need to know when topics change or when the focus shifts | Use discourse markers, topic sentences, paragraph breaks |
| **Match your audience's common ground** | What counts as "given" depends on the listener | Assess what they already know; don't over- or under-explain |

### 12.2 Self-Check Questions Before Sending a Message

Use these questions to audit your own messages for information structure:

1. **Is my main point clear?** Can the reader identify what's new/important within the first few sentences?
2. **Have I introduced my referents?** Does every "he," "she," "they," or "it" have a clear antecedent? Would someone reading this without my mental context know who/what I'm talking about?
3. **Am I overloading any sentences?** Does any sentence introduce more than one new person, concept, or event?
4. **Is there a logical flow from given to new?** Does each sentence connect to the previous one? Or do they feel like disconnected facts?
5. **Have I signaled my topic shifts?** If I change subjects mid-message, have I used a transition phrase?
6. **Am I calibrated to my audience?** Am I explaining things they already know (condescending) or assuming knowledge they don't have (confusing)?
7. **Is the structure visible?** Does the message have paragraph breaks, and does each paragraph have a clear topic?

### 12.3 Common Constructions and Their Information Functions

| Construction | Information function | When to use it |
|---|---|---|
| Active voice: "John broke the window" | Agent as topic | When you're talking about what John did |
| Passive voice: "The window was broken" | Patient/object as topic | When you're talking about the window, not who broke it |
| It-cleft: "It was John who broke it" | Isolates and focuses one element | When you need to pinpoint exactly who/what/when |
| Pseudo-cleft: "What I need is time" | Frames the question, then answers it | When you want to set up context before your point |
| Topicalization: "That movie, I loved" | Explicitly marks the topic | When you're shifting to discuss a particular thing |
| Left dislocation: "The report, I sent it yesterday" | Reactivates a referent as topic | When resuming discussion of something mentioned earlier |
| There-insertion: "There's a problem" | Introduces a brand-new referent | When presenting something entirely new to the discourse |
| Existential "it": "It's raining" | Background/ambient information | When there is no agent or specific topic |
| Fronted adverbial: "Yesterday, I went to the store" | Sets the temporal/spatial frame before the main information | When time or place is important context for what follows |

---

## 13. SUMMARY: WHY INFORMATION STRUCTURE MATTERS

Information structure is not a decorative feature of language. It is the **organizational system** that determines whether communication succeeds or fails. The same facts, packaged differently, can be:

- Crystal clear or hopelessly confusing
- Engaging or boring
- Persuasive or unconvincing
- Polite or rude
- Memorable or forgettable

The difference is not the content. It is the packaging.

For autistic learners, information structure is particularly valuable because it is **highly systematic**. There are clear rules (given before new, one new thing per sentence, focus goes at the end, signal your transitions, calibrate to your audience). These rules can be learned, practiced, and applied. Unlike many aspects of pragmatics that depend on reading subtle social cues in real time, information structure can be planned in advance -- especially in written communication, where you have time to review and restructure before sending.

The payoff is significant: mastering information structure makes you a clearer writer, a more organized speaker, and a more effective communicator in every context -- professional emails, presentations, social media, conversations, and academic writing. It is arguably the single highest-leverage pragmatic skill for written communication.
