# Conversational Implicature and Autism: Research Synthesis

## Purpose
Research synthesis for a knowledge base teaching English pragmatics to autistic individuals. This document covers what research shows about how autistic people process conversational implicature, what the real difficulties are (vs. stereotypes), and what strategies help.

---

## 1. How Autistic Individuals Process Conversational Implicature

### 1.1 The Emerging Nuanced Picture

The research literature has shifted dramatically from early claims of global pragmatic impairment toward a highly nuanced understanding. The key finding of the 2020s is that **pragmatic difficulties in autism are selective, not uniform**. Different types of implicature tap different cognitive mechanisms, and autistic individuals show preserved ability in some areas while facing genuine challenges in others.

**Andrés-Roqueta et al. (2025)** published a landmark study titled "Some (but not all) Pragmatic Inferences are Difficult for Autistic Children" in the *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*. The study compared 23 autistic children to 237 neurotypical children using innovative tablet-based scalar implicature and metaphor tasks. Key findings:
- Autistic children performed **comparably to neurotypical children on scalar implicatures** (the "some but not all" inference)
- Autistic children showed **greater difficulty with metaphor comprehension** 
- This dissociation within a single group of autistic children challenges the notion of a general pragmatic deficit
- Language demands (rather than pragmatic competence per se) may explain group differences on metaphor tasks

**Source**: [Andrés-Roqueta et al. 2025, Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10803-025-06838-4) | PubMed ID: 40246746

### 1.2 Scalar Implicatures: Relatively Preserved

Scalar implicatures involve inferring "some but not all" when a speaker says "some" (because if they meant "all," they would have said "all"). This is arguably the most-studied type of implicature in autism research.

**Key finding: Most studies show autistic individuals handle scalar implicatures at or near neurotypical levels.**

- **Pijnacker et al. (2009)** tested 11 adults with autistic disorder, 17 with Asperger syndrome, and 28 controls. The combined ASD group was **just as likely as controls to derive scalar implicatures**. However, there was a difference between the autistic disorder and Asperger syndrome subgroups, suggesting heterogeneity within the spectrum. Published in *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*, 39(4), 607-618. PubMed ID: 19052858

- **Schaeken, Van Haeren & Bambini (2018)** tested 22 children with ASD and matched controls (66 total, 10-year-olds, Flemish-speaking) on scalar implicature tasks. Using a binary judgment task, ASD children performed similarly to controls. When given a three-option response (including "I agree a bit"), the results revealed that scalar inference competence is **not a yes/no matter but a fine-grained dimension** -- autistic children showed more variable tolerance for underinformative statements. PMC: PMC6064869

- **Andrés-Roqueta & Katsos (2020)** reviewed the evidence and concluded that most attempts to find differences between autistic and neurotypical children on scalar implicatures have **failed to find significant group differences**, when language ability is controlled for.

**Why scalar implicatures are preserved**: Andrés-Roqueta & Katsos (2020) proposed the distinction between **linguistic-pragmatics** and **social-pragmatics**. Scalar implicatures are classified as *linguistic-pragmatic* -- they depend primarily on linguistic competence and pragmatic knowledge about scales (some/all, sometimes/always, etc.) rather than on reading the speaker's mental states. Verbal skills alone are a strong predictor of scalar implicature ability. This means autistic individuals with intact language abilities can perform these inferences well.

### 1.3 Ad-Hoc Implicatures: More Difficulty

Ad-hoc implicatures are context-dependent inferences that don't rely on established linguistic scales but require constructing a contextual scale on the fly. Example: "I wore gloves" in the context of a biology experiment implies sterile gloves, not winter gloves.

- **Mazzaggio, Foppolo, Job & Surian (2021)** tested 26 children with ASD (ages 4-10, mean 7.1) and 26 TD controls on both scalar and ad-hoc implicatures. Children with ASD showed difficulty with **both** types, but the predictors differed: **Theory of Mind predicted performance on scalar but not ad-hoc implicatures**, while **IQ modulated performance on both types**. This suggests different mechanisms underlie different implicature types. Published in *Journal of Communication Disorders*. PubMed ID: 33621945

- **Su & Zhao (2024)** studied Mandarin-speaking autistic children (ages 4-8) and found challenges with computing both scalar and ad-hoc implicatures, published in *Journal of Communication Disorders*.

### 1.4 Particularized Conversational Implicatures: A Surprising Finding

Particularized implicatures are one-off, highly context-dependent inferences (e.g., "Is John a good cook?" -- "He makes a great cup of coffee" implicates that John is NOT a particularly good cook).

- **Jary, Martín-González, Vicente & Castroviejo (2025)** conducted a groundbreaking study published in the *Journal of Communication Disorders* comparing autistic and neurotypical adults on two types of particularized implicatures:
  - **Material implicatures**: The inferential chain from what is said to the implied meaning can be reconstructed purely from the propositional content (no need to reason about the speaker's behavior)
  - **Behavioural implicatures**: Require reasoning about the speaker's behavior and the motivations behind it (greater Theory of Mind demands)
  
  **Hypothesis**: Autistic adults would match neurotypicals on material implicatures but perform worse on behavioural ones.
  
  **Actual finding**: Autistic participants' performance **mirrored neurotypicals in BOTH conditions**. Both groups found behavioural implicatures slightly more difficult, but there was no autism-specific deficit. The researchers concluded that "the reported difficulties that autistic people have with implicit communication may be due to the demands of real-time interaction, rather than underlying issues of inferential competence."

  **Source**: [Jary et al. 2025, ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0021992425000164)

### 1.5 Which Types Are Harder and Why

The research suggests a hierarchy of difficulty:

| Implicature Type | Difficulty Level | Primary Mechanism | Key Factor |
|---|---|---|---|
| Scalar (linguistic scale) | Low - preserved | Linguistic-pragmatic | Language ability |
| Ad-hoc (contextual) | Moderate | Mixed | IQ + context integration |
| Particularized (material) | Low-moderate | Propositional reasoning | Logic |
| Particularized (behavioural) | Moderate | Social-pragmatic | Theory of Mind |
| Irony/sarcasm | High | Social-pragmatic | ToM + speaker attitude |
| Indirect requests in context | Variable | Mixed | Real-time processing demands |

The **linguistic vs. social pragmatics distinction** (Andrés-Roqueta & Katsos, 2020) is the most influential framework:
- **Linguistic-pragmatics** (scalar implicatures, some ad-hoc inferences): Depend on language competence and pragmatic knowledge; relatively preserved in autism
- **Social-pragmatics** (irony, sarcasm, certain implicatures requiring speaker-attitude reasoning): Additionally require reasoning about the speaker's mental states (ToM); more challenging in autism

### 1.6 The Role of Theory of Mind vs. Language Ability vs. Working Memory

Multiple cognitive factors contribute to implicature processing, and their relative importance varies by implicature type:

**Theory of Mind (ToM)**:
- **Surian, Baron-Cohen & van der Lely (1996)** found that success on pragmatic tasks (detecting Gricean maxim violations) was **related to the ability to attribute false beliefs**, establishing the early ToM-pragmatics link. PubMed ID: 16571474
- **Cardillo et al. (2021)** studied children and adolescents with ASD and found that when the mediating roles of ToM and executive functions on pragmatic language were tested, **only ToM contributed significantly**. Published in *Autism Research*, 14(5), 932-945. PubMed ID: 33111475
- **Mazzaggio et al. (2021)**: ToM predicted scalar implicature performance but NOT ad-hoc implicature performance in autistic children

**Language Ability**:
- Verbal skills are the **strongest predictor of scalar implicature competence** (Andrés-Roqueta & Katsos, 2020)
- **Lampri et al. (2024)** reviewed the figurative language literature and found that **ToM and verbal skills** correlated most strongly with figurative language comprehension in ASD. Published in *Autism Research*, 17(4), 674-689. PubMed ID: 38071498

**Working Memory**:
- Weak working memory has been suggested as an explanation for errors in pragmatic article choice and scalar implicature derivation
- Working memory demands may explain why real-time conversational implicature is harder than written/offline tasks

**Executive Functions**:
- Cardillo et al. (2021) found executive functions did NOT independently contribute to pragmatic language once ToM was accounted for
- However, attentional control and cognitive flexibility likely contribute to real-time pragmatic processing

### 2.2 The Wilson & Bishop Studies (2021, 2022): Adult Implicature Processing

These two studies from the University of Oxford are particularly important for understanding adult implicature processing:

**Wilson & Bishop (2021)** "Second guessing yourself all the time about what they really mean..." Published in *Autism Research*. PubMed ID: 32686325
- N = 66 autistic adults, 118 non-autistic adults
- Used the novel Implicature Comprehension Test
- Controlling for core language ability, autistic participants were:
  - **Over 2x as likely** to endorse a non-normative (incorrect) interpretation
  - **Over 5x as likely** to select "do not know"
- The high "don't know" rate suggests **uncertainty about inferences rather than inability to make them**
- The researchers concluded that "a cognitive preference for certainty and explicit communication, and not simply reduced ability, may account for some of the differences"

**Wilson & Bishop (2022)** Stage 2 Registered Report. Published in *PeerJ*, 10:e13110. PMC: PMC8919847
- N = 102 autistic adults, 109 non-autistic adults
- Tested the "preference for certainty" hypothesis
- Found accuracy AND confidence were **both reduced on implied meaning tasks** (but not grammar tasks) in the autistic group
- Found relationships between **Intolerance of Uncertainty**, implied meaning performance, and self-reported social communication challenges
- Supports the idea that **discomfort with uncertainty** (not inability) plays a role in pragmatic language challenges

### 2.3 The Andrés-Roqueta & Katsos (2018) Study

Published in *Frontiers in Psychology*. PMC: PMC5859064
- Examined comprehension of three types of **generalized conversational implicatures** in children with and without ASD
- ASD group performed worse than other groups both overall and for each implicature type
- Children with Level 2 ASD showed more severe limitations
- Importantly, this study focused on *generalized* (not particularized) implicatures

## 3. The "Literal-Mindedness" Stereotype vs. Reality

### 3.1 Evidence Against Uniform Literalism

The stereotype that autistic individuals are "uniformly literal" is contradicted by substantial evidence:

1. **Scalar implicatures are preserved**: Multiple studies show autistic individuals derive "some but not all" inferences at similar rates to neurotypical people (Pijnacker et al. 2009; Andrés-Roqueta et al. 2025)

2. **Particularized implicatures can be intact**: Jary et al. (2025) found autistic adults performed comparably to neurotypicals on BOTH material and behavioural particularized implicatures

3. **Heterogeneity is massive**: The autistic population is highly diverse. Many high-functioning autistic individuals understand scalar implicatures, metaphors, and even irony (Vicente & Falkum, 2023)

4. **Offline vs. online performance**: When given time (offline tasks), autistic individuals often perform much better than in real-time conversation, suggesting the bottleneck is processing speed/demands rather than competence

5. **Autistic perspectives challenge the label**: Some autistic individuals report that "taking things literally" is not an accurate description of their experience -- they may notice the implied meaning exists but feel uncertain about which inference is correct

### 3.2 The Predictive Processing Account (Vicente, Michel & Petrolini, 2023)

This is the most sophisticated current theoretical account of literalism in autism:

**Paper**: "Literalism in Autistic People: A Predictive Processing Proposal" published in *Review of Philosophy and Psychology* (September 2023).

**Core argument**: Literalism results from an atypical functioning of the **predictive processing system**:
- The brain normally generates top-down predictions about likely meanings in context
- In autism, there is an atypical balance between predictions and error signals
- This leads to **reduced confidence in one's own predictions** about non-literal meaning
- When uncertain about predictions, the safest strategy is to **default to the literal interpretation** (which is unambiguous and doesn't require betting on a contextual inference)
- Predictions play a key role in everyday comprehension, and a low level of confidence in one's own predictions escalates comprehension difficulties

**Why this matters for teaching**: This account suggests that autistic individuals are NOT unable to compute non-literal meanings -- they are **less confident** in their pragmatic inferences. Strategies that increase confidence (explicit rules, pattern recognition, confirmation opportunities) should help.

**Source**: [Vicente et al. 2023, Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s13164-023-00704-x)

### 3.3 The Vicente & Falkum (2023) Account

**Paper**: "Accounting for the preference for literal meanings in autism spectrum conditions" published in *Mind & Language*.

Key points:
- Critiques both the **executive dysfunction** explanation (literalism = failure to inhibit literal meaning) and the **ToM deficit** explanation (literalism = failure to read speaker intentions), arguing both assume a prior literalist bias
- Notes that **neurotypical children also go through a literal stage**, and provides evidence linking this to **rule-following behavior** -- following linguistic "rules" strictly rather than pragmatically
- Suggests literalism in autism may be linked to the same tendency toward **rule-following/systemizing** that is widespread in autism
- This creates an important insight: **literal interpretation is itself a kind of pattern** -- just an overly rigid one

**Source**: [Vicente & Falkum 2023, Wiley](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/mila.12371)

### 3.4 When Literal Interpretation Happens vs. When Pragmatic Inference Succeeds

Based on the literature, literal interpretation is MORE likely when:
- The implicature requires real-time processing under time pressure
- The implicature requires reasoning about the speaker's mental states or attitudes (social-pragmatic)
- The context is ambiguous or unfamiliar
- There is high uncertainty (the person cannot confidently predict the intended meaning)
- The implicature involves irony, sarcasm, or speaker-attitude recognition

Pragmatic inference SUCCEEDS when:
- The implicature relies on linguistic scales (some/all, sometimes/always)
- There is sufficient processing time (written/offline tasks)
- The context is clear and familiar
- The inference follows from propositional logic (material implicatures)
- The person has strong verbal/language abilities
- Explicit rules or patterns can be applied

## 4. Gricean Maxim Violations in Autistic Communication

### 4.1 Detecting Gricean Violations

**Surian, Baron-Cohen & van der Lely (1996)** is the foundational study, using the Conversational Violations Test (CVT):
- Tested sensitivity to violations of: Relevance, Quality, Quantity I (informativeness), Quantity II (redundancy), Politeness
- Most children with autism performed **at chance** on detecting violations
- All children with SLI and normal controls performed **above chance**
- Success on the task was linked to false-belief attribution (ToM)
- Published in *Cognitive Neuropsychiatry*. PubMed: 16571474

### 4.2 Maxim Violations in Autistic Speech

Research on which maxims autistic individuals violate in their own speech reveals:

**The Maxim of Relevance is the most commonly violated maxim** in autistic speech. A study analyzing utterances found:
- 67.65% of utterances by children with autism violated 1 maxim
- 20.59% violated 2 maxims
- 2.94% violated 3 maxims
- 8.82% violated all 4 maxims

Language and social development challenges, as well as difficulty maintaining focus, were considered responsible for the relevance violations.

**Source**: [Grice's Cooperative Principles Violation in Autism, Universitas Padjadjaran](https://jurnal.unpad.ac.id/sosiohumaniora/article/download/24378/12965)

### 4.3 Developmental Trajectory

**Understanding of the Gricean Maxims in Children with ASD** (ScienceDirect, 2022):
- Typically developing children performed better than children with ASD on understanding conversational maxims
- However, children with ASD **progressively develop** this pragmatic language ability
- This is not a static deficit but a developmental difference with a slower trajectory

**Source**: [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S091160442200029X)

### 4.4 Genuine Inability vs. Different Conversational Priorities

This is a critical reframing question. Recent research suggests maxim violations may represent **different conversational priorities rather than pure inability**:

**Williams, Wharton & Jagoe (2021)** "Mutual (Mis)understanding: Reframing Autistic Pragmatic 'Impairments' Using Relevance Theory" -- *Frontiers in Psychology*. PMC: PMC8117104
- Used relevance theory as a framework for analyzing interactions
- Found that **mutual understanding was unexpectedly high** across conversation types
- In conversations between two autistic participants, flow, rapport, and intersubjective attunement were **significantly increased**
- Argues that misunderstanding is not a consequence of autistic "impairment" but a **mutual failure in reaching consensus** through bidirectional empathy

**Kissine (2021)** "Theory of autistic mind: A renewed relevance theoretic perspective on so-called autistic pragmatic 'impairment'" -- *Journal of Pragmatics*
- Challenges the traditional ToM-deficit view
- Reframes interactions between autistic and non-autistic interlocutors as **mutually-mediated endeavors**
- Argues relevance theory's concept of **mutual manifestness** offers crucial insights: what is "salient" or "relevant" may genuinely differ between neurotypes

**Bottema-Beutel's enactive framework** describes autistic social interaction in terms of different **interactional priorities** and **enactment of meaning**, rather than failed attempts at neurotypical-style communication.

The evidence suggests a middle position:
- Some maxim violations (especially of Relevance) reflect genuine difficulties with maintaining conversational topic and integrating context
- Some violations reflect **different communicative priorities** (e.g., prioritizing informational accuracy over social smoothness -- Quality over Manner)
- Some apparent violations are only violations from a neurotypical perspective -- they may be perfectly communicative within autistic interaction norms

## 5. The Double Empathy Angle

### 5.1 Milton's Double Empathy Problem (2012)

Damian Milton, an autistic autism researcher, proposed that communication difficulties between autistic and non-autistic people are **bidirectional**:
- Autistic people struggle to understand non-autistic communication styles
- Non-autistic people equally struggle to understand autistic communication styles
- The "problem" is a **mismatch between neurotypes**, not a deficit in one group

### 5.2 Do Neurotypical People Fail to Draw Correct Implicatures from Autistic Speech?

**Yes, the evidence shows that inference failure is bidirectional.**

**Geelhand, Papastamou, Jaspard & Kissine (2025)** "Autistic adults display different verbal behavior only in mixed-neurotype interactions" -- *Autism* journal
- 134 adults in autistic, non-autistic, and mixed-neurotype dyads
- In mixed pairs, autistic Directors produced **more words** than non-autistic Directors (in both typing and speaking)
- Non-autistic dyads completed tasks faster than dyads with at least one autistic partner
- Even without knowing each other's neurotype, **neurotype mismatch triggered different verbal behavior**
- PubMed ID: 39572980

**Key implication for implicature**: If autistic speakers produce more explicit/verbose communication (which can be seen as violating the Quantity maxim from a neurotypical perspective), neurotypical listeners may **fail to correctly interpret the communicative intent** behind this verbosity -- they may interpret it as anxious, pedantic, or confused rather than as a deliberate strategy for clarity.

### 5.3 The Integrated Model of Pragmatic Competence

**Zou (2023)** "Divergent social communication between autistic and non-autistic individuals revisited" -- *Frontiers in Psychology*. PMC: PMC10690413
- Adopts the Double Empathy Problem framework
- Proposes two components:
  1. **Internal Pragmatic Competence (IPC)**: How each group independently processes linguistic/communicative issues -- different mindsets lead to different pragmatic processing
  2. **Pragmatic Competence for External Communication (PCEC)**: How impaired coordination between internal modules and external contexts affects communication between groups
- Argues the field must stop exclusively focusing on autistic "deficits" and examine the bidirectional nature of cross-neurotype communication failure

### 5.4 Same-Neurotype Communication is Easier

Multiple studies now show that **autistic-autistic communication works better than mixed-neurotype communication**:

- Williams et al. (2021) found increased flow, rapport, and attunement in autistic-autistic conversation pairs
- A 2025 systematic review of 52 papers found that autistic people generally have **positive interpersonal relations and communication experiences when interacting with other autistic people**, and these interactions were associated with better quality of life
- Information transfer studies show more efficient communication within same-neurotype pairs

This suggests that the "pragmatic deficit" is at least partially a **cross-neurotype phenomenon** rather than an intrinsic property of autistic cognition.

## 6. Practical Implications: Strategies for Autistic Individuals

### 6.1 Explicit Teaching of the Gricean Framework

The research supports making pragmatic principles **explicit and rule-based**, which aligns with autistic cognitive strengths in systemizing:

- **The Gricean maxims can be taught as an explicit system**: Quality (be truthful), Quantity (be as informative as needed, no more), Relation (be relevant), Manner (be clear)
- Understanding these as *rules that speakers are expected to follow* allows autistic individuals to **predict when someone is violating a maxim** and then reason about why
- The key insight: when a cooperative speaker seems to violate a maxim, they are probably **communicating something beyond the literal words** (the implicature)

### 6.2 Pattern Recognition Approaches

Autistic individuals often have enhanced pattern recognition abilities. This can be leveraged:

- **Systemizing** has been defined as the drive to observe, analyze, or construct rule-based systems that function in an "if-then" manner, and it is clearly elevated in autism
- Teaching implicature as a **pattern recognition task** ("when someone says X in context Y, they usually mean Z") aligns with autistic cognitive strengths
- Explicit "if-then" rules for common implicature patterns can serve as scaffolding
- Example: "If someone asks 'Can you pass the salt?' they are not asking about your physical ability -- this follows the pattern of polite indirect requests"

**Evidence for explicit instruction**: Research shows that when explicit-implicit instruction was compared to implicit-only instruction, **significantly more children who received explicit-implicit instruction met criteria to be considered 'pattern-users'** (PMC6802863).

**Source**: [Indiana University IRCA](https://iidc.indiana.edu/irca/articles/meeting-the-challenge-of-social-pragmatics-with-students-on-the-autism-spectrum.html)

### 6.3 Relevance Theory as a Computational Alternative

Relevance Theory (Sperber & Wilson, 1986/1995) offers a potentially more useful framework than Gricean maxims for autistic learners because it reduces pragmatics to a single principle:

**The Principle of Relevance**: Every act of communication carries a presumption that it is worth the listener's processing effort, and the speaker has aimed for the most relevant interpretation available.

This is computationally simpler than tracking four maxims and can be applied as a **single heuristic**:
1. Ask: "Why did the speaker say THIS rather than something simpler/more direct?"
2. The answer will usually be: "Because they wanted to communicate something beyond the literal words"
3. Search for the interpretation that makes the utterance most relevant in context

**Source**: [Frontiers, Williams et al. 2021](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2021.616664/full)

### 6.4 Addressing the Confidence Problem

Given the predictive processing account (Vicente et al. 2023), strategies should target **confidence in pragmatic inferences**:

- Teach that **uncertainty about implied meaning is normal** -- even neurotypical people sometimes misinterpret implicatures
- Provide explicit confirmation strategies: "Did you mean...?" or "I'm not sure if you're saying X or Y"
- Teach the concept of **degrees of certainty** in inference-making -- it's okay to have a tentative interpretation
- Normalize the "I'm not sure what you mean" response as a valid social move
- Frame pragmatic inference as a **probability judgment** rather than a right/wrong answer

### 6.5 The Pragmatic Intervention Programme (PICP)

**Exploring the effectiveness of the Pragmatic Intervention Programme (PICP)** (2024). PubMed ID: 39412294

The PICP addresses 11 pragmatic skills:
1. Eye contact
2. Joint attention
3. Turn-taking
4. Communicative response
5. Communicative initiative
6. Communicative functions
7. Comprehension/expression in verbal and non-verbal contexts
8. Cohesion
9. Inferential comprehension
10. Conversation
11. Figurative language

Active inclusion of both the learner and parent/support person in intervention was found to be a **significant mediator of intervention effect**.

### 6.6 Key Teaching Principles from the Research

1. **Distinguish linguistic from social pragmatics**: Start with scalar/linguistic implicatures (where autistic individuals already have strength) and build toward social-pragmatic inferences
2. **Make the implicit explicit**: Convert unwritten pragmatic rules into explicit, learnable patterns
3. **Leverage systemizing**: Frame pragmatic inference as a rule-based system with predictable patterns
4. **Allow processing time**: Real-time demands are a major bottleneck; strategies should include permission to slow down
5. **Address uncertainty tolerance**: Build comfort with probabilistic rather than certain interpretations
6. **Validate the autistic communication style**: Frame this as learning a second pragmatic "dialect" rather than fixing a deficit

## 7. Research on Autistic Adults and Implicature Processing

### 7.1 Why Adult Research Matters

Most pragmatic communication studies in ASD have focused on children and adolescents. However, difficulties in pragmatic communication processing **persist into adulthood** (as documented by multiple studies), and autistic adults are actively navigating pragmatic challenges in workplace, social, and relationship contexts.

### 7.2 Key Adult Studies

**Wilson & Bishop (2021)** -- N = 66 autistic adults, 118 non-autistic adults
- Most comprehensive study of implied meaning in autistic adults
- Found the "second guessing" phenomenon: autistic adults are over 5x as likely to select "don't know" when asked about implied meanings
- Suggests a **cognitive preference for certainty** rather than inability
- PubMed: 32686325

**Wilson & Bishop (2022)** -- N = 102 autistic adults, 109 controls
- Registered report replicating and extending 2021 findings
- Confirmed that reduced accuracy and confidence on implied meaning tasks are specific to inference-making (not grammar)
- Found Intolerance of Uncertainty linked to pragmatic challenges
- PMC: PMC8919847

**Pijnacker et al. (2009)** -- N = 28 autistic adults
- Adults with ASD derive scalar implicatures at similar rates to controls
- PubMed: 19052858

**Jary et al. (2025)** -- Autistic adults
- Autistic adults perform comparably to neurotypicals on BOTH material and behavioural particularized implicatures
- Suggests that offline inferential competence may be intact
- [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0021992425000164)

**Geelhand et al. (2025)** -- 134 adults in three dyad types
- Autistic adults modify their verbal behavior in mixed-neurotype interactions even without knowing their partner's neurotype
- PubMed: 39572980

### 7.3 The "Processing vs. Competence" Distinction in Adults

A recurring theme in adult research is the distinction between:
- **Competence**: The underlying ability to derive implicatures (often intact in autistic adults)
- **Performance**: Actual success in real-time conversational contexts (where challenges are more apparent)

Factors that reduce adult performance despite intact competence:
- Time pressure in real-time conversation
- Ambiguity and uncertainty (especially for those with high Intolerance of Uncertainty)
- Need to simultaneously process verbal content, prosody, facial expressions, and context
- Anxiety about misinterpreting (the "second guessing" Wilson describes)
- Cross-neurotype communication dynamics (the double empathy problem)

### 7.4 Pragmatic Language as a Diagnostic Marker in Adults

Research shows that both structural and pragmatic language scores discriminate between diagnostic groups, with **stronger effects for pragmatic scores**. Pragmatic scores remain robust predictors of ASD diagnoses even after accounting for cognitive and structural linguistic differences. This confirms that pragmatic differences persist into adulthood and are not simply "grown out of."

## 8. Theoretical Frameworks Summary

### 8.1 Framework Comparison for Teaching Purposes

| Framework | Core Idea | Strength for Teaching | Limitation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Gricean Maxims | 4 rules of cooperative communication; violations signal implicature | Provides explicit, learnable rules | 4 maxims can feel arbitrary; hard to apply in real-time |
| Relevance Theory | Single principle: utterances are presumed relevant and worth processing effort | Computationally simpler; single heuristic | Abstract; harder to make concrete |
| Linguistic vs. Social Pragmatics | Two types: language-based vs. mind-reading-based | Explains the pattern of difficulty; reduces anxiety about areas of strength | Still developing; may oversimplify |
| Predictive Processing | Reduced confidence in top-down predictions leads to literal defaults | Explains WHY literal interpretation happens; suggests confidence-building strategies | Complex theoretical framework |
| Double Empathy | Communication failure is mutual, not one-sided | Validates autistic experience; reduces self-blame | May seem to excuse not learning NT conventions when living in NT-majority world |

### 8.2 An Integrated Teaching Approach

The research suggests that the most effective approach combines:
1. **Gricean framework** for explicit rules (this is what speakers do)
2. **Relevance Theory** for a simpler heuristic (ask "why did they say it this way?")
3. **Linguistic/social pragmatics distinction** for understanding one's own strengths
4. **Predictive processing insights** for building confidence in inference-making
5. **Double empathy awareness** for understanding that miscommunication is bidirectional
