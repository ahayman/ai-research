## 1. Why This Page Exists (Opening Reframe)

Gaslighting is the most consequential pattern in this hub, the most researched, and the most diluted by popular usage. It is also the one autistic readers are most often subjected to without having a clean name for it, because the cultural script of "autistic people misread social situations" is itself a ready-made gaslighting infrastructure.

The reframe this page offers is the one Paige Sweet (2019) brought into the academic literature: **gaslighting is not a private psychological pathology of one bad person.** It is a sociological process. It works by mobilizing a pre-existing cultural story about *what kind of person you are* — irrational, oversensitive, "too literal," "too much," not credible — and using that story as the leverage that lets the perpetrator define reality on your behalf. Without that scaffolding, gaslighting tactics fail. With it, they need very little effort to succeed.

For autistic readers, this matters in a specific way. The cultural scaffolding for gaslighting an autistic person already exists — it has existed since you were a child. "You're misreading the situation," "you're being too sensitive," "you took it the wrong way," "you're remembering it wrong," "nobody else has a problem with this," "that's just how you are" — you have heard these sentences your entire life, often from people who were not trying to harm you. By the time an actual abuser uses them, the trail has been worn smooth. **You are not unusually susceptible to gaslighting because of a flaw in your cognition. You are structurally targeted by it because of an environment that has been preparing the ground since before you could resist.**

This page is a recognition tool, a distinguishing tool, and a strategy index. It is not a diagnostic test. The decision about whether what you are experiencing is gaslighting is not something this page can make for you, and it is not a decision that can be made from a single incident. Gaslighting is a *pattern* that becomes visible over time, and the recognition tools below are tools for seeing the pattern, not for adjudicating any single conversation.

## 2. The Concept Explained

### Sweet's structural definition

Sweet (2019), drawing on interviews with women in domestic violence shelters, defines gaslighting as a process by which perpetrators **mobilize gender-based stereotypes and structural inequalities** to make their version of reality stick at the expense of the victim's. The tactics — denial, minimization, attribution of irrationality — work because they line up with cultural scripts the audience already accepts.

The key insight is that gaslighting is *power-laden*. It is not symmetrical. Two people in equal positions denying each other's accounts is a disagreement. One person, holding more credibility and more social backing, systematically rewriting another person's account, is gaslighting. The asymmetry is what makes the rewriting succeed.

For the autism hub, the structural fact is that *neurotype functions analogously to gender* in this framework. The cultural script "autistic people misread social situations, miss sarcasm, take things too literally, get overwhelmed and misperceive, can't read intent" is a pre-loaded credibility discount. It does not need to be invoked explicitly. It is there in the audience's head before any conversation begins. (See Lim, Young & Brewer, 2022, for the empirical demonstration that autistic adults are perceived as more deceptive and less credible than neurotypical adults *when telling the truth*.)

**Evidence quality:** Sweet's framework is [VALIDATED] as a sociological model, with strong uptake (299+ citations as of 2023) and convergent qualitative support. The application to neurotype as a structural axis is [EXTRAPOLATED], but it is a strong extrapolation supported by Lim, Young & Brewer's credibility discount findings and by the autistic community literature on diagnostic and pragmatic discrediting.

### Abramson's philosophical refinement

Kate Abramson (2014, *Philosophical Perspectives*) sharpens the concept further. For Abramson, gaslighting is distinctively *epistemic harm*: the goal is not merely to win an argument but to undermine the victim's standing as an independent knower. The gaslighter is not satisfied with the victim agreeing under pressure; they want the victim to *come to doubt the legitimacy of their own perceptions, memory, and judgment*. This is what distinguishes gaslighting from ordinary lying or denial.

This refinement matters for autistic readers in two ways:

1. **It explains why arguing harder does not work.** If the goal of the perpetrator is to corrode your standing as a knower, then "winning" any individual argument is irrelevant — every confrontation is another opportunity to extend the corrosion. Strategies that depend on out-arguing a gaslighter fail because they assume the gaslighter is trying to be right, when they are actually trying to dismantle your confidence in being right.
2. **It clarifies what recovery means.** Recovery from gaslighting is not about settling factual disputes about specific past events. It is about re-establishing yourself as a legitimate knower of your own experience. The strategies on this page are oriented toward that goal, not toward winning arguments.

**Evidence quality:** Abramson's analysis is [PHILOSOPHICAL/CONCEPTUAL] rather than empirical. It is widely respected within the philosophy of epistemic injustice literature but has not been operationalized into measured outcomes.

### The Duluth bridge

In the Domestic Abuse Intervention Programs Power and Control Wheel (Pence & Paymar, 1993), the wedge labeled **"Minimizing, Denying, and Blaming"** describes the same set of tactics that Sweet later named gaslighting. The Wheel was developed in 1984 from structured interviews with survivors; it predates the academic gaslighting literature by decades. The two literatures converge on the same pattern from different methodological starting points, and the convergence is part of why this is the most well-documented manipulation pattern in this hub.

The Duluth framing adds an important practical fact: gaslighting tactics rarely appear alone. In the Wheel's structure, the wedges *hold each other in place*. Minimizing/denying/blaming sits next to isolation, intimidation, emotional abuse, and economic abuse. When you see one wedge, look for the others. A pattern of "you're remembering it wrong" alongside cutting you off from autistic peers is a more dangerous configuration than either tactic alone, and it is the configuration that the coercive control literature treats as a unified phenomenon.

### What gaslighting is *not*

A category dilution problem. Sweet, Abramson, and the survivor research literature all agree that gaslighting is a *specific* pattern with specific structural conditions. It is not the same as:

- Disagreeing with you
- Remembering an event differently
- Being defensive
- Lying
- Refusing to apologize
- Being a bad listener
- Having a different interpretation

Each of these can occur within gaslighting, and gaslighting can be built out of these elements, but **none of them, alone, is gaslighting**. The defining features are (a) structural power asymmetry, (b) systematic rather than incidental denial of the target's reality, (c) the goal of undermining the target's standing as a knower, not just winning a particular point, and (d) social scaffolding that makes the rewriting plausible to third parties. When all four are present, you are in a gaslighting pattern. When only one or two are present, you may be in a difficult relationship, in a misunderstanding, or in something else entirely — but the strategies on this page are calibrated for the full pattern, not for the dilution.

Section 12 of the trauma framework document explicitly identifies pop-gaslighting overreach — the use of "gaslighting" to describe any disagreement or unflattering account — as a claim this hub does not endorse. This page commits to the strict definition.

## 3. How It Looks in Practice

Gaslighting unfolds across at least six recognizable channels for autistic readers. Some of these were identified in Sweet's general framework; others come from autistic community knowledge and the autism-specific research literature. The channels often co-occur and reinforce each other.

### 3.1 Memory gaslighting

The most-named form. The perpetrator denies or rewrites events you remember.

Examples:
- "That conversation never happened."
- "I never said that. You always twist what I say."
- "You're imagining things again."
- "We didn't agree to that. I would have remembered."
- "You're confusing two different times."

This form has its own dedicated page later in the sub-hub (Memory Gaslighting, Tier 3) because the autism-specific cognitive mechanism — relative episodic memory differences combined with intact semantic memory — makes it the form autistic readers are most structurally vulnerable to.

### 3.2 Sensory gaslighting

The denial of sensory experience. For autistic readers, this is often the *foundational* form, beginning in childhood: the denial of sensory differences that the autistic person was unambiguously experiencing.

Examples:
- "That can't possibly be too loud, no one else has a problem with it."
- "You're not actually in pain, you just think you are."
- "It doesn't smell, you're being dramatic."
- "The lights are fine. You always make a scene about lighting."
- "You can wear that shirt. It's fine. Everyone wears clothes."

The autism research literature on interoception and sensory processing supports the view that autistic sensory experience can differ substantially from typical experience (Marco et al., 2011; Robertson & Baron-Cohen, 2017). When these differences are denied across an entire childhood, the cumulative effect is a deep skepticism toward your own sensory data — exactly the epistemic dismantling Abramson describes, but installed before adulthood. Many autistic adults arrive at gaslighting recognition with this layer already in place, which is part of why later gaslighting works on them so efficiently.

**Evidence quality:** Sensory differences in autism are [VALIDATED]. The framing of childhood sensory invalidation as a foundational gaslighting pattern is [COMMUNITY KNOWLEDGE] strongly supported by the qualitative autism research, but it has not been formally operationalized as gaslighting in clinical research. The Sensory Gaslighting page later in this sub-hub develops this further.

### 3.3 Diagnostic gaslighting

"That's your autism talking." The use of the diagnosis itself as a discrediting frame, regardless of whether the autistic person's account is in fact related to autism.

Examples:
- "You're only upset because you can't read social cues."
- "That's your autism. You're misreading the situation."
- "You took it literally again. I obviously didn't mean it that way."
- "You can't tell when someone is joking. This is your problem."
- "If you weren't autistic, you'd see why this is normal."

Pearson et al. (2022) and Douglas & Sedgewick (2024) both document this pattern in qualitative studies of autistic adults' experiences of intimate partner abuse. Pearson's interviewees described "feeling like they deserved it because they were autistic" — the internal endpoint of diagnostic gaslighting, where the discrediting frame is taken inside and operates without external prompting. Diagnostic Weaponization gets its own page (Tier 3) because the "weaponized features" framing from Douglas & Sedgewick deserves its own treatment.

### 3.4 Emotional gaslighting

The denial of emotional states. For autistic readers, this interacts with alexithymia (the difficulty in identifying and naming one's own emotional states; about 50% comorbidity with autism per Kinnaird et al., 2019, meta-analysis) in a particularly damaging way.

Examples:
- "You're not actually angry, you just think you are."
- "That's not what you really feel."
- "You're being oversensitive. This isn't a big deal."
- "You're just upset because you didn't get your way."
- "I can tell you better than you can tell yourself."

If you already have a hard time identifying your own emotional states, having someone else confidently label them for you can feel like a relief — until the labels start being weaponized. The intersection of alexithymia and emotional gaslighting is one of the most efficient routes into the kind of self-doubt Abramson describes.

### 3.5 Social gaslighting

The "everyone agrees with me" move. Bringing in third parties (real or imagined) to back the perpetrator's account.

Examples:
- "Everybody thinks you're being unreasonable."
- "I talked to your sister and even she said you're overreacting."
- "Nobody else has a problem with how I act."
- "Ask anyone — they'll tell you the same thing."
- "Your friends are too polite to say it but they all see it too."

Sweet identifies this as one of the core structural mechanisms: the gaslighter recruits the social environment as a chorus, and the victim has no way to verify whether the chorus is real. For autistic readers who are already isolated from autistic peer networks, this is particularly effective because the perpetrator has effectively all the social verification channels in their hands. (The Bandwagon Isolation page in Tier 3 develops this further.)

### 3.6 Institutional gaslighting

Where the structural scaffolding is most explicit: institutions (medical, legal, educational, social services, religious) systematically dismiss the autistic person's account and treat the institution's framing as the authoritative one.

Examples:
- A medical provider repeatedly attributing physical symptoms to "anxiety" or "autism" without investigation.
- A school treating an autistic student's report of bullying as "social misunderstanding" while disciplining the student for "behavioral outbursts" that are actually meltdowns.
- A workplace HR process treating documented harassment as "communication style differences."
- A police interaction in which the autistic person's flat affect or unusual answers are read as evasion or deception.

Institutional gaslighting is not always intentional. It is often the product of trained scripts meeting an autistic person whose presentation does not match the script. But the *effect* is the same: the autistic person's account is overridden by institutional framing. The Medical Settings, Educational Settings, Legal/CJS, and Social Services context pages in Tier 4 each address the institutional version directly.

## 4. How to Distinguish Gaslighting from Legitimate Behavior or Miscommunication

This is the hardest section to write, because the entire structure of gaslighting is designed to make the distinction impossible from inside the situation. The tools below are not infallible. They are *accumulating evidence* that shifts the probability, not proofs that resolve the question.

For the full distinguishing framework, see [Manipulation vs. Miscommunication Decision Framework](manipulation-hub-miscommunication-vs-manipulation.md). The five tests there — Repair, Consistency, Trajectory, Three-Conversation, and Witnessed Behavior — apply to gaslighting specifically. This section adds the gaslighting-specific signals that augment those tests.

### Signals that shift the probability *toward* gaslighting

- **The denial is systematic, not occasional.** Everyone misremembers things sometimes. A pattern in which one specific person consistently denies events that you have evidence for is a different shape.
- **The denial is asymmetric.** The other person's memory is treated as authoritative; yours is treated as suspect. The asymmetry holds across many incidents and is not context-specific.
- **The denial extends to things you have written down or recorded.** This is one of the most diagnostic signals. When someone denies an event you can document, and continues to deny it after seeing the documentation, you are no longer in a memory dispute. (This is also why documentation is so central to the strategy section below.)
- **The denial is paired with character attribution.** "You misremember" plus "you always misremember" plus "this is what you do" plus "this is who you are." The escalation from event-denial to identity-denial is the move from disagreement to gaslighting.
- **You are encouraged to doubt yourself in general, not just on this point.** "Maybe you should see someone." "Maybe your medication needs adjusting." "Maybe you're more confused than you realize." The signal here is that the doubt is being *generalized*, not contained to a specific factual dispute.
- **Your social network shrinks as the pattern continues.** Sweet's structural framing predicts this directly. Gaslighting needs the absence of contradicting voices to maintain itself, which means the perpetrator has structural reasons to isolate you. If your access to autistic peers, old friends, or family who knew you before is steadily decreasing, the configuration is becoming more dangerous.
- **The other person's account becomes the authoritative version even with third parties.** When mutual friends, family, or institutions begin to refer to events using the other person's framing rather than yours, the social scaffolding Sweet describes is in place.
- **The pattern resists every diagnostic test in the Decision Framework.** Repair attempts fail. Consistency is asymmetric. Trajectory is downward. Three conversations show the same pattern. Witnessed behavior matches non-witnessed. When all five tests point the same direction, the probability is much higher than any single test would warrant.

### Signals that shift the probability *away* from gaslighting

- **The other person sometimes acknowledges they were wrong.** Genuine acknowledgment, not strategic concession. Gaslighters very rarely concede; people who are simply difficult sometimes do.
- **The disagreements are bidirectional.** Both people sometimes have to revise their account. Both people sometimes accept the other's version. The asymmetry is not consistent.
- **Other people who know both of you have a mixed view.** Not "everyone is on your side," not "everyone is on theirs" — a mixed view in which different people see different things. This is more consistent with a difficult relationship or differing perspectives than with a gaslighting pattern.
- **The disputes are about specific factual claims, not about your general standing as a knower.** "I think we agreed on Thursday, you think Friday" is different from "I'm telling you what you remember and felt and meant."
- **The pattern is recent and tied to specific stressors.** Patterns that began with a job loss, a medical crisis, or a major life change and that do not fit the pre-existing relationship may be situational rather than structural. (This does not make them safe; it makes them a different problem requiring different strategies.)
- **There is no isolation pressure.** Your social network is intact, you can talk to autistic peers and old friends, you have access to outside perspectives. Gaslighting is much harder to maintain in an environment with intact social scaffolding for the target.

### When the signals are mixed

If some of the gaslighting signals are present and some are absent, the responsible position is "I do not know yet." This is uncomfortable but accurate. The strategies in this hub are calibrated for situations where the pattern is established. In situations where it is unclear, the most useful moves are:

1. **Begin documentation.** Not as accusation, but as data collection. The pattern will become clearer or less clear with more data.
2. **Reach out to a reality-check network.** Outside perspectives are part of how you find out which way the pattern is going.
3. **Apply the Three-Conversation Test from the Decision Framework.** A pattern that resists three good-faith repair attempts is in different territory from one that responds to the first.
4. **Do not accept "I'm gaslighting myself" as a conclusion you can reach alone.** This is precisely the move gaslighting wants you to make. Outside input is structurally necessary.

## 5. Autism-Specific Considerations

### The pre-loaded credibility discount

Lim, Young & Brewer (2022, PMC8813809) found that autistic adults are perceived as more deceptive and less credible than neurotypical adults *when telling the truth*. This is not a hypothetical risk — it is an empirically demonstrated baseline against which any gaslighting account will be heard. Outside observers do not begin from neutrality. They begin from a position that already favors the perpetrator's version, and the autistic person has to overcome that gap before being believed.

The practical implication: the documentation, third-party witnesses, and external evidence covered in the strategy section are not optional polish. They are the things that close the gap between zero credibility and being heard at all. (See [Building External Evidence Beyond Documentation](manipulation-hub-strategy-external-evidence.md) for the broader strategy.)

### The episodic memory mechanism

The single most-cited cognitive vulnerability for gaslighting in autistic readers. Multiple studies (reviewed in the foundational autism research document, including Boucher & Mayes, 2012, and PMC6424931) show that autistic adults often have relative differences in *episodic memory* — memory for specific events anchored in their original spatial-temporal context — while *semantic memory* (general knowledge, facts, what you know) remains relatively intact.

What this means in practice:
- **Pattern memory is often available** even when specific-incident memory is fuzzy. You may be unable to remember the exact words of a conversation last Tuesday but remember that this kind of conversation has happened many times. **Trust the pattern memory.** It is reliable in a way the gaslighter cannot easily dispute, and it is precisely the kind of memory the credibility discount tries to discount.
- **External records function as prosthetic episodic memory.** Documentation is not a self-improvement practice; it is a mechanical compensation for a memory architecture that does not naturally retain the evidence needed to counter gaslighting. ([Documentation](manipulation-hub-strategy-documentation.md) treats this in detail.)
- **The "you don't remember it correctly" move is particularly effective on you.** Not because your memory is bad, but because the part of memory that gaslighting attacks happens to be the part where the differences exist. This is a structural mismatch between your cognition and the attack vector, not a personal weakness.

**Evidence quality:** Episodic memory differences in autism are [VALIDATED]. The application to gaslighting vulnerability is [EXTRAPOLATED] but supported by a clear mechanism.

### The compliance training installation

Many autistic adults were trained from childhood to override their own perceptions when they conflicted with what authority figures said. ABA, "indistinguishable from peers" goals, social skills programs that explicitly trained suppression of stimming, sensory accommodation refusals dressed up as "you'll get used to it" — the cumulative effect is a person who has been taught, by reward and punishment, that their internal data is unreliable and that external instruction is authoritative. This is a *gaslighting trainer*. By the time an actual abuser arrives, the override is automatic.

This does not mean ABA *is* gaslighting in the technical sense — that is a [CONTESTED] claim and the evidence around the long-term effects of ABA is mixed and should not be overstated. What can be said with more confidence is that environments which systematically dismiss an autistic child's reported experience prepare the ground for adult gaslighting, regardless of whether the original environments were "abusive" in any direct sense.

### The internalization trap

Pearson et al. (2022) describe interview participants who said things like "I felt like I deserved it because I was autistic." This is the endpoint of diagnostic gaslighting taken inside: the autistic person no longer needs the perpetrator to attribute the abuse to autism, because they are doing it themselves. Recovery from gaslighting almost always requires interrupting this internalization, which is not a self-help project — it is generally the work of trauma-informed therapy paired with reality-check networks. ([Recovery and Post-Traumatic Growth](manipulation-hub-strategy-recovery.md) addresses this.)

### Justice sensitivity as an asset

Multiple sources describe heightened justice sensitivity in autistic adults — a strong aversion to unfairness and deception, often combined with firm personal moral codes. Properly oriented, this is a *defense* against gaslighting: the impulse to insist on what actually happened, even at social cost, is exactly what the gaslighter is trying to corrode. **The instinct to keep saying "but that is not what happened" is not a problem to manage. It is a feature to protect.** The strategies below are about giving that instinct external supports so it can survive sustained pressure.

**Evidence quality:** Justice sensitivity in autism is [PRELIMINARY] — well-described qualitatively, with some quantitative support, but with less standardized measurement than other autism constructs.

### The double empathy problem cuts both ways

Milton (2012) describes the "double empathy problem": the difficulty in autistic-allistic communication is bidirectional, not a one-sided autistic deficit. This matters for gaslighting in a specific way: when an allistic person says "you misread me," the cultural script gives the misreading to you. But the empirical literature gives it to *both* of you. Their account of what they meant is not automatically more reliable than your account of what they said. When you are told "that's not what I meant" and you have evidence of what was said, you do not need to defer to their interpretation of their own intent.

## 6. Strategies (with Conditionals)

The strategies for gaslighting are nearly all in the Strategy tier of this sub-hub. This section maps each strategy to its application against gaslighting and notes the conditions under which each is appropriate.

### 6.1 Documentation (primary strategy)

[Documentation](manipulation-hub-strategy-documentation.md) is the central strategy for gaslighting. The mechanism is direct: gaslighting attacks your memory of events; external records preserve the events outside your memory; the records cannot be rewritten by the gaslighter the way your recollection can.

**Apply when:** the pattern is established or even suspected, AND storage is safe (the person you are documenting cannot discover the records).

**Do not apply when:** you are in a high-lethality DV situation in which discovery of documentation would trigger escalation, AND you do not yet have a secure storage option. In this case, the safety planning page and an advocate should come first; documentation can begin once a safe channel exists.

**Autism-specific note:** Treat documentation as mechanical compensation, not as accusation. You are not building a case against anyone; you are compensating for an episodic memory architecture that cannot natively retain what gaslighting attacks. Reframing it this way often unblocks the resistance many autistic readers feel about "keeping records on people I love."

### 6.2 Reality-Check Networks (primary social strategy)

[Reality-Check Networks](manipulation-hub-strategy-reality-check-networks.md) are the social complement to documentation. They are people who can corroborate your perception when the gaslighter is trying to corrode it.

**Apply when:** you have access to people who can witness or have witnessed the pattern, OR you have access to autistic peers who recognize the pattern from their own experience.

**Do not apply when:** the only people available are part of the perpetrator's social network and would disclose to them. In this case, building new reality-check relationships outside that network is the prior step.

**Autism-specific note:** Crompton et al. (2020) found that autistic peer-to-peer communication is more effective than mixed autistic-allistic communication. Autistic peers who have experienced similar patterns are an underweighted resource in most gaslighting situations and should be valued higher than their numbers suggest.

### 6.3 The Pattern Memory Move

A specific tactical move worth naming: **when the gaslighter denies a specific incident, do not argue about the incident.** Argue (or do not argue) about the *pattern*. "I do not need to settle whether you said exactly that on Tuesday. I notice that this kind of conversation happens often."

This works for two reasons. First, the autistic memory architecture is generally stronger on pattern than on specific incident, so you are arguing from your strength rather than from the shape of memory the gaslighter is attacking. Second, gaslighters are usually good at relitigating individual events but much less good at denying patterns, because pattern denial requires denying multiple events at once. The pivot from incident to pattern is one of the few rhetorical moves that works against a competent gaslighter.

**Caveat:** this is not a debate-winning move. It is a *self-orientation* move. The point is not to convince the gaslighter but to keep yourself anchored in what you know.

### 6.4 The Internal/External Distinction

A core principle from the foundational autism research doc: distinguish *assertion-as-internal-knowledge* from *assertion-as-public-performance*. You can know something without successfully convincing someone else of it. The instinct to keep arguing until the other person agrees is exactly what gaslighting exploits — the gaslighter's job is to refuse to agree, indefinitely, while you exhaust yourself.

The protective move is: maintain the internal assertion silently, in writing, with a reality-check network — and stop trying to make the gaslighter admit it. Their admission was never on the table. The thing you are protecting is *your* knowledge, not theirs.

This is closely related to gray rock and strategic disengagement (see [Strategic Disengagement](manipulation-hub-strategy-strategic-disengagement.md)) but operates at the cognitive level rather than the behavioral one.

### 6.5 Strategic Disengagement

[Strategic Disengagement](manipulation-hub-strategy-strategic-disengagement.md) applies to gaslighting in a specific way: the more you engage with the disputed-event content, the more openings you give the gaslighter to extend the corrosion. Disengaging from the content of disputes (while continuing to document them) often reduces the surface area on which gaslighting can operate.

**Apply when:** the relationship is being maintained for non-elective reasons (children, finances, shared work, family) and full exit is not currently possible.

**Do not apply when:** what you are calling "strategic disengagement" is actually compliance masking — silent capitulation to the gaslighter's reality without the protective documentation, reality-checking, and internal assertion that distinguish disengagement from giving up.

### 6.6 Refusal of the Internalization Frame

Closely related to internal assertion: refuse, internally, the move from "I was wrong" to "I am wrong as a person." Even if you concede a specific factual point, do not accept the generalization to your standing as a knower. Abramson's analysis is the conceptual ground for this distinction; the practical version is something like: *You may have caught me on a specific detail. That does not give you the authority to tell me what I generally know, what I generally feel, or who I generally am.*

### 6.7 Disclosure Decisions

[Disclosure Decisions](manipulation-hub-strategy-disclosure-decisions.md) matters specifically because of the Ullman (2010) disclosure asymmetry: negative disclosure responses harm more than positive responses help. If you disclose gaslighting to someone who responds with the gaslighter's framing — "well, both of you have your version" or "have you tried being less sensitive?" — the cost can be substantial. Choose your audiences carefully. The decision tree on the Disclosure page is built for this.

### 6.8 Safety Planning (when relevant)

If the gaslighting is occurring inside an intimate partner relationship and any lethality indicators are present, [Safety Planning](manipulation-hub-strategy-safety-planning.md) is the higher-priority strategy and the strategies above should be sequenced under safety planning, not parallel to it. Specifically:

- Strangulation history → contact a DV advocate before any other strategy. Strangulation is associated with an odds ratio of 7.48 for later attempted homicide (Glass et al., 2008). This is the single most important indicator.
- Separation contemplation → 55.2% of femicide victims were estranged or in the process of separating (Campbell, 2003). Safety planning needs to be in place before separation begins.
- Weapon access, escalating threats, isolation pressure → all elevate risk and all warrant advocate contact.

The Safety Assessment page is the first filter; this is its main practical use.

### 6.9 Professional Support

[Finding Professional Support](manipulation-hub-strategy-professional-support.md) addresses the harder question of who can actually help with gaslighting recovery. The autism-informed-trauma-specialist intersection is small. The page details the red flags for autism-uninformed clinicians and the indicators of suitable support.

**Specific red flag for gaslighting work:** any therapist who frames gaslighting as a "communication problem" or recommends "couples counseling" while gaslighting is active. Bograd & Mederos (1999) is the standard reference for the contraindication of couples therapy when coercive control (which gaslighting often accompanies) is present.

### 6.10 Recovery

[Recovery and Post-Traumatic Growth](manipulation-hub-strategy-recovery.md) addresses the longer arc. Recovery from gaslighting is not about settling factual disputes — it is about re-establishing yourself as a legitimate knower. The page frames Herman's three-stage model (Safety → Remembrance and Mourning → Reconnection) as a non-linear heuristic, with autism-specific notes on the burnout-trauma overlap, compliance recovery, and the rejection of forgiveness-as-requirement.

## 7. What This Page Cannot Do

- **It cannot tell you, from the outside, whether what you are experiencing is gaslighting.** The pattern is identified over time, with multiple data points, often with outside input. A page cannot make that determination from a single visit.
- **It cannot win the argument with the gaslighter.** Gaslighting is structured to be argument-proof. The strategies on this page protect *you* — they do not convert the perpetrator.
- **It cannot guarantee that your reality-check network will believe you.** Lim, Young & Brewer's credibility discount applies inside reality-check networks too. Choosing carefully and disclosing in graduated steps reduces this risk but cannot eliminate it.
- **It cannot replace an advocate in safety-critical situations.** If the gaslighting is part of a coercive control pattern with lethality indicators, an advocate has training and information this page does not.
- **It cannot reverse the long-term effects of childhood gaslighting on its own.** Sensory and diagnostic invalidation across a childhood is a developmental trauma that generally requires trauma-informed therapy to work through. Reading this page is not equivalent to that work.
- **It cannot adjudicate cases where you are uncertain whether you are the gaslighter.** Some readers will worry, correctly or incorrectly, that they are the ones rewriting reality. This page is not built for that question. If you are seriously asking it, the most useful next step is a trauma-informed therapist with experience in autistic clients, not a self-diagnosis from a webpage.

## 8. Key Takeaways

1. **Gaslighting is structural, not just personal.** Sweet's framing is the most useful single move: the gaslighter is not powerful because they are uniquely cunning, but because the cultural scaffolding (which already exists, in your case, around autism) is doing most of the work for them.

2. **The goal of gaslighting is epistemic dismantlement, not winning arguments.** Abramson's refinement explains why arguing harder fails. Strategies must target the dismantlement, not the individual arguments.

3. **Autistic readers face six recognizable channels:** memory, sensory, diagnostic, emotional, social, and institutional. Each has its own mechanism. Several may be operating at once.

4. **The episodic memory mechanism is your specific structural vulnerability.** Documentation and pattern memory are the mechanical compensations. Use both.

5. **Pre-loaded credibility discount is not a confidence problem.** Lim, Young & Brewer (2022) demonstrates the empirical baseline. External evidence and reality-check networks exist to close the gap, not to make you more confident.

6. **The strict definition matters.** Disagreement, defensiveness, and even ordinary lying are not gaslighting. The dilution of the term in popular use is an obstacle to recognizing the actual pattern. This page commits to the strict definition.

7. **Documentation is the primary strategy.** Reality-check networks are the social complement. Internal assertion preserved without trying to convert the gaslighter is the cognitive move. These three together do most of the protective work.

8. **Pattern memory is reliable even when episode memory is fuzzy.** Trust it. Argue from your strength, not from the shape of memory the gaslighter is attacking.

9. **Safety planning takes precedence when gaslighting is part of coercive control with lethality indicators.** Strangulation, separation contemplation, and weapon access are the indicators. The Safety Assessment page is the first filter.

10. **Recovery is about re-establishing yourself as a legitimate knower, not about winning past arguments.** This is a multi-year process for many people, generally supported by trauma-informed therapy and intact reality-check relationships. It is not a self-help project but it is also not impossible. The strategies on this page support it; they do not replace it.

## 10. Cross-Links

### Pragmatics categories referenced on this page

- **Common Ground (Category 16)** — gaslighting is fundamentally an attack on the shared common ground of a relationship. The perpetrator unilaterally rewrites what was understood to be mutually known, and the target loses the ability to rely on shared reference. Reading the common ground category gives the underlying pragmatic mechanism without having to read the entire curriculum first. [category-16-common-ground.md](category-16-common-ground.md)
- **Presupposition (Category 9)** — gaslighting often operates by introducing false presuppositions ("when you said X you meant…") that smuggle a contested claim into the structure of a sentence as if it were already accepted. Recognizing presupposition manipulation is one of the most useful direct defenses. [category-09-presupposition.md](category-09-presupposition.md)
- **Implicature (Category 2)** — what is *implied* but not said is where much gaslighting lives. "I'm just worried about you" implicates incompetence; "are you sure that happened?" implicates unreliability. The implicature category gives the technical machinery for naming what the surface text is doing. [category-02-implicature.md](category-02-implicature.md)
- **Speech Acts (Category 1)** — denying, claiming, asserting, accusing — gaslighting is composed of specific speech acts and recognizing them as acts (rather than as descriptions of reality) is part of the cognitive defense. [category-01-speech-acts.md](category-01-speech-acts.md)
- **Politeness (Category 3)** — much gaslighting is wrapped in politeness scaffolding so that the target has to "be rude" to refuse it. Understanding politeness theory makes the wrapper visible. [category-03-politeness.md](category-03-politeness.md)
- **Register (Category 7)** — the institutional version of gaslighting often relies on register asymmetry: clinical, legal, or administrative language overriding the autistic person's account in plainer language. [category-07-register.md](category-07-register.md)
- **Deixis (Category 8)** — memory gaslighting attacks the deictic anchors (when, where, who) that make events reconstructable. Documentation preserves deictic content. [category-08-deixis.md](category-08-deixis.md)
- **Implicature / Cooperative Principle (Category 2)** — gaslighting is a systematic violation of the cooperative principle that the target is being asked to honor unilaterally. Naming the asymmetry in cooperative effort is one of the more clarifying moves. [category-02-implicature.md](category-02-implicature.md)
