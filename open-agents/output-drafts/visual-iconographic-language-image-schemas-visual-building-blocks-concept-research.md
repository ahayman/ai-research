# Concept Research: Image Schemas as Visual Building Blocks

## Overview

If semantic primes are the atoms of meaning, image schemas are the spatial geometry those atoms live in. Mark Johnson introduced the term in his 1987 *The Body in the Mind* to describe the recurring spatial-temporal structures that arise from pre-linguistic bodily experience: the CONTAINER (in/out, boundary), the PATH (source, trajectory, goal), FORCE (compulsion, blockage, resistance, enablement), BALANCE (equilibrium around an axis), LINK (connection between entities), and others. These schemas are not abstract philosophical constructs — they are patterns that human infants learn from physical interaction with the world before language develops. A six-month-old who drops a toy into a cup and retrieves it is building the CONTAINER schema. A toddler who walks across a room to reach a parent is enacting the SOURCE-PATH-GOAL schema. These structures are the cognitive bedrock on which all later conceptual development — including language — is built.

What makes image schemas uniquely promising as the foundation for a visual language is that they are simultaneously pre-linguistic and inherently spatial. Unlike Wierzbicka's semantic primes (which are defined verbally and include many abstract concepts resistant to visualization), image schemas are naturally visual — Johnson himself used simple diagrams (lines, arrows, circles) to represent them in his original work. The Diagrammatic Image Schema Language (DISL), published by Maria Hedblom, Fabian Neuhaus, and Till Mossakowski in *Spatial Cognition and Computation* in 2024, has formalized this intuition into a complete visual representation system with defined symbols for conceptual primitives and composition rules for combining them. DISL draws on Mandler and Pagan Canovas's work on developmental conceptual primitives and uses comic-strip-like sequences of panels to represent temporally extended narratives.

The critical bridge from image schemas to a general-purpose visual language is conceptual metaphor. George Lakoff and Mark Johnson demonstrated in *Metaphors We Live By* (1980) that abstract thought is systematically structured by metaphorical mappings from embodied experience: MORE IS UP (the VERTICALITY schema grounds quantitative reasoning), KNOWING IS SEEING (the visual perception schema grounds epistemic concepts), PURPOSES ARE DESTINATIONS (the PATH schema grounds goal-directed reasoning), INTIMACY IS CLOSENESS (the PROXIMITY schema grounds emotional concepts), TIME IS MOTION (the PATH schema grounds temporal reasoning). Joseph Grady's Primary Metaphor Hypothesis (1997) identifies specific sensorimotor correlations that generate these mappings, arguing they are universal because the bodily experiences that produce them are universal. If this chain holds — image schemas ground primary metaphors, and primary metaphors structure abstract thought — then a system that visually represents image schemas could, through metaphorical extension, represent any concept that human thought can express.

## Core Components

### Component 1: Johnson's Image Schema Inventory

- **Purpose**: Identify the recurring spatial-temporal structures that arise from pre-linguistic bodily experience
- **Original Inventory (Johnson, 1987)**:
  - **CONTAINER**: Interior, boundary, exterior. The fundamental schema of containment — things are in, out, or at the boundary. Grounded in the body itself (the body is a container), in physical containers (cups, rooms, boxes), and in the experience of entering and leaving spaces.
  - **PATH / SOURCE-PATH-GOAL**: A starting point, a trajectory, and an endpoint. Grounded in every experience of movement from one place to another. The most fundamental schema for understanding goal-directed action.
  - **FORCE**: Compulsion (being pushed), blockage (being stopped), counterforce (pushing back), enablement (being freed), attraction (being pulled), diversion (being redirected). Grounded in every physical interaction with objects and other agents.
  - **BALANCE**: Equilibrium around an axis. Grounded in the experience of maintaining physical balance (standing, walking) and in perceiving balanced/unbalanced visual configurations.
  - **LINK**: Connection between two entities. Grounded in physical linking (holding hands, chains, bridges) and in the experience of social bonds.
  - **CENTER-PERIPHERY**: A focal center with less important surroundings. Grounded in visual attention (foveal vs. peripheral), spatial organization (town center), and social structure (central authority vs. margins).
  - **CYCLE**: A recurring path that returns to its starting point. Grounded in biological rhythms (heartbeat, breathing, circadian), seasonal patterns, and repetitive physical activities.
  - **PART-WHOLE**: Components that constitute a larger entity. Grounded in the body (hands are parts of the body), in objects (wheels are parts of cars), and in social groups (members of a family).
  - **UP-DOWN / VERTICALITY**: The vertical axis and its asymmetry (up is different from down). Grounded in gravity, in the body's vertical orientation, and in the experience that "up" correlates with more, active, conscious, and "down" with less, passive, unconscious.
  - **NEAR-FAR / PROXIMITY**: The distance between entities. Grounded in the body's relationship to objects and to other people — near things can be grasped, far things require movement.
  - **SCALE / GRADIENT**: Continuous variation along a dimension. Grounded in the perception of size, intensity, loudness, brightness as varying quantities.
  - **CONTACT**: Physical touching between surfaces. Grounded in the tactile experience of touching and being touched.
  - **SURFACE**: An exposed, extended area. Grounded in the perception of flat, open planes.
  - **FULL-EMPTY**: The state of a container relative to its capacity. Grounded in filling and emptying physical containers.
  - **ITERATION**: Repeated instances of the same action or event. Grounded in repetitive physical activities (hammering, walking, breathing).
  - **SPLITTING**: A single entity becoming multiple. Grounded in physical breaking, branching, and divergence.
  - **MERGING**: Multiple entities becoming one. Grounded in physical joining, convergence, and combination.
  - **OBJECT / COLLECTION**: Bounded entities in space. Grounded in the perception of distinct objects and groups of objects.
- **Key Properties**: Image schemas are (1) pre-linguistic (acquired before language), (2) embodied (arising from bodily interaction with the world), (3) spatial (inherently structured in space), (4) schematic (abstract patterns, not specific images), (5) productive (combine to form more complex structures).

### Component 2: Mandler's Developmental Theory of Image Schema Acquisition

- **Purpose**: Explain how infants acquire image schemas through perceptual analysis of their environment
- **Perceptual Meaning Analysis (PMA)**: Jean Mandler proposed that infants use a mechanism called Perceptual Meaning Analysis to "reformat" rich perceptual information into a much sparser, more abstract code. PMA is a data summarization device that takes complex sensory input and distills it into schematic spatial structures — the image schemas.
- **Spatial Primitives as First Concepts**: The earliest concepts infants form tend to be global categories (like "animal" and "container") composed of simplified redescriptions of innately salient spatial information. Examples of innately salient information include:
  - Paths of object motion that change spatial relations (into/out of containers)
  - Objects going into or out of sight
  - Contingencies between paths (one object chasing another)
  - Self-moving vs. caused-motion distinction (animacy detection)
- **Developmental Progression**:
  1. Pre-linguistic spatial analysis: infants detect paths, containers, agents, contacts
  2. Image schema formation: repeated experiences consolidate into schematic patterns
  3. Conceptual subdivision: initial broad concepts subdivide into more specific categories
  4. Language interface: image schemas provide the conceptual structure that language maps onto
  5. Metaphorical extension: spatial schemas extend to non-spatial domains through experience-based correlations
- **Key Insight for Visual Language**: If image schemas are indeed the first concepts that human cognition constructs — prior to and independent of any spoken language — then they represent the deepest possible level of cross-linguistic, cross-cultural cognitive universality. A visual language built on image schemas would be grounded in the same cognitive structures that every human develops in infancy.

### Component 3: DISL — The Diagrammatic Image Schema Language (2024)

- **Purpose**: Formalize visual symbols for image schema primitives and define composition rules for combining them
- **Authors**: Maria M. Hedblom, Fabian Neuhaus, and Till Mossakowski
- **Publication**: *Spatial Cognition and Computation*, 2024 (published 2024, volume 25, 2025 issue)
- **Core Design**:
  - DISL defines a set of visual symbols for conceptual primitives drawn primarily from developmental psychology (Mandler and Pagan Canovas)
  - Symbols represent: objects (bounded entities), trajectories (paths), regions (containers/areas), contacts, forces, and modifiers (iteration, negation, state change)
  - Composition rules define how primitives combine to form complete image schemas
  - A "strip" format (inspired by comic strip conventions) uses sequences of panels to depict temporally extended narratives
  - Each panel illustrates a specific image-schematic scene (objects, states, relationships)
  - The strip as a whole conveys a temporal narrative
- **Machine-Readable Exchange Format**: DISL includes a logical exchange format that makes diagrams machine-readable, enabling computational processing of image schema representations
- **What DISL Demonstrates**:
  - Image schemas *can* be given formal, unambiguous visual representations
  - A relatively small number of conceptual primitives can generate a wide range of image schemas through composition
  - The composition is not arbitrary — it follows cognitively motivated rules
  - The resulting visual representations are readable and interpretable
- **What DISL Does Not (Yet) Do**:
  - DISL is a descriptive/analytic notation, not a productive communication system
  - It represents spatial-physical scenarios effectively but does not address abstract domains (emotions, logic, social relationships)
  - It lacks a mechanism for metaphorical extension (mapping spatial schemas onto non-spatial meanings)
  - It is designed for researchers, not for everyday communication

### Component 4: Image Schemas as Groundwork for Conceptual Metaphor

- **Purpose**: Explain how image schemas serve as the source domains for primary metaphors that structure abstract thought
- **The Metaphorical Chain**:
  1. **Embodied experience**: Walking across a room to reach a toy
  2. **Image schema formation**: SOURCE-PATH-GOAL pattern consolidated
  3. **Experiential correlation**: During early development, the child repeatedly experiences that achieving goals involves traversing paths. Goal-achievement and spatial-path-traversal co-occur.
  4. **Primary metaphor**: PURPOSES ARE DESTINATIONS crystallizes from the correlation
  5. **Abstract reasoning**: The adult can now think "I'm on the path to success," "We've arrived at a conclusion," "She's lost her way in life" — all using PATH schema vocabulary for abstract goal-directed thinking
- **Key Primary Metaphors and Their Image Schema Sources**:
  - **MORE IS UP** ← VERTICALITY schema: Piling objects makes piles go up; more correlates with higher
  - **KNOWING IS SEEING** ← PERCEPTION schema: Seeing and learning co-occur in childhood
  - **PURPOSES ARE DESTINATIONS** ← PATH schema: Achieving goals requires moving toward them
  - **INTIMACY IS CLOSENESS** ← PROXIMITY schema: Emotionally close people are physically close
  - **AFFECTION IS WARMTH** ← TEMPERATURE-CONTACT schema: Being held by caregivers is both warm and affectionate
  - **DIFFICULTY IS HEAVINESS** ← FORCE schema: Heavy objects are hard to move
  - **IMPORTANCE IS SIZE** ← SCALE schema: Larger things are more salient
  - **EMOTIONAL STATES ARE LOCATIONS** ← CONTAINER schema: "I'm in trouble," "He's in a good mood"
  - **TIME IS MOTION** ← PATH schema: Events come toward us and recede behind us
  - **DESIRE IS HUNGER** ← FORCE (internal compulsion): Hunger and desire co-occur
  - **UNDERSTANDING IS GRASPING** ← CONTACT/FORCE schema: Physically grasping objects and cognitively "grasping" ideas
- **Universality Argument**: Joseph Grady's Primary Metaphor Hypothesis argues that primary metaphors are universal because the embodied experiences that generate them are universal. All humans have bodies that interact with gravity, containers, paths, and forces. If the metaphors are universal, then the image schemas that ground them are universal — providing a cross-cultural foundation for visual symbols.

### Component 5: DISL Primitives and Their Visual Representation

- **Purpose**: Catalog the specific visual symbols DISL uses to represent conceptual primitives
- **Object Primitives**: Bounded entities represented as circles or enclosed shapes. Different fill patterns or annotations distinguish types (animate vs. inanimate, agent vs. patient).
- **Path/Trajectory Primitives**: Arrows showing direction of movement. Solid arrows for actual motion, dashed for potential or unrealized motion.
- **Region Primitives**: Bounded areas (rectangles, circles) representing containers or defined spaces. The boundary line itself can be solid (impermeable), dashed (permeable), or absent (unbounded region).
- **Contact Primitives**: Two entities touching at a point or along a surface. Represents the CONTACT schema.
- **Force Primitives**: Thick arrows representing applied force. Different annotations for compulsion, blockage, counterforce, enablement, attraction.
- **State Primitives**: Annotations indicating conditions — filled (FULL), empty (EMPTY), divided (SPLIT), merged (MERGED).
- **Temporal Primitives**: Panel sequencing (left-to-right strip format) represents temporal progression. Repeated panels represent ITERATION.
- **Composition Rules**: Primitives combine within panels following spatial logic — objects inside regions represent containment, objects connected by paths represent traversal, objects linked by force arrows represent causal interaction.

### Component 6: Scaling Challenges — From Spatial to Abstract

- **Purpose**: Identify the gap between what image schemas can directly represent and what a general-purpose language requires
- **What Image Schemas Handle Well**:
  - Spatial relationships (in, on, under, between, through, across)
  - Physical actions (moving, containing, linking, balancing, splitting, merging)
  - Causal relationships (force dynamics: pushing, blocking, enabling)
  - Quantitative relationships (more/less through SCALE schema)
  - Temporal sequences (before/after through PATH schema)
- **What Image Schemas Handle Through Metaphor**:
  - Emotions (via CONTAINER: "full of joy"; via VERTICALITY: "feeling down")
  - Goals and purposes (via PATH: "on the road to recovery")
  - Knowledge and understanding (via PERCEPTION/CONTACT: "I see what you mean," "grasping the concept")
  - Social relationships (via LINK, PROXIMITY: "close friends," "connected to")
  - Morality and evaluation (via VERTICALITY, BALANCE: "upright citizen," "moral balance")
- **What Image Schemas Struggle With**:
  - **Logical operations**: NOT, IF, MAYBE — negation, conditionality, and possibility do not have obvious image schema groundings (though BLOCKAGE may ground negation, and BRANCHING PATH may ground conditionality)
  - **Metalinguistic reference**: Talking about language itself ("I didn't mean that literally")
  - **Propositional attitudes**: "I believe that she thinks that he knows..." — nested mental state attribution
  - **Specificity and precision**: The difference between "some" and "37%" is hard to encode through schemas alone
  - **Cultural and institutional concepts**: "Democracy," "copyright," "mortgage" — concepts that depend on social conventions rather than embodied experience
- **The Central Question**: Can the metaphorical extension of image schemas reach all the way to logical operators, propositional attitudes, and culturally constructed concepts? Or is there a ceiling beyond which image schemas cannot ground meaning, and conventional (non-schematic) symbols must take over?

### Component 7: Neural Basis of Image Schemas

- **Purpose**: Review neuroscientific evidence for image schemas as real cognitive structures
- **Sensorimotor Grounding**: Image schemas are theorized to be represented in the same sensorimotor cortical areas that produced them. The CONTAINER schema activates spatial processing regions used for actual container interactions. The FORCE schema activates motor cortex regions involved in physical force production.
- **Cross-Domain Activation**: When people process metaphorical uses of image schemas ("she's in trouble" — CONTAINER schema used metaphorically), neuroimaging studies show activation of both the relevant sensorimotor regions and abstract reasoning regions, consistent with the metaphorical mapping account.
- **Developmental Evidence**: Infant studies using looking-time paradigms confirm that pre-linguistic infants are sensitive to containment violations, path continuity violations, and force dynamic events — consistent with the claim that image schemas are acquired before language.
- **Implications for Visual Language**: If image schemas are neurally grounded in sensorimotor processing, then visual symbols that activate these schemas should engage the same cognitive machinery that processes real spatial experience. This suggests that image schema-based visual symbols could leverage deep, pre-linguistic cognitive resources rather than requiring purely conventional learning.

## How It Works

### Step 1: Image Schema Identification
- **Trigger**: Experiencing a spatial-physical pattern (containment, path traversal, force interaction, etc.)
- **Action**: Perceptual Meaning Analysis (Mandler) distills the complex sensory input into a schematic spatial pattern
- **Result**: An image schema (CONTAINER, PATH, FORCE, etc.) stored as an abstract cognitive structure

### Step 2: Schema Composition
- **Trigger**: Experiencing complex scenarios that involve multiple schemas simultaneously
- **Action**: Schemas combine according to spatial logic — a path INTO a container (PATH + CONTAINER), a force ALONG a path (FORCE + PATH), an object INSIDE a container that is PART OF a larger structure (CONTAINER + PART-WHOLE)
- **Result**: Complex image schema compositions that represent multi-faceted spatial-causal scenarios

### Step 3: Metaphorical Extension
- **Trigger**: Experiential correlation between a spatial-physical pattern and a non-spatial experience (e.g., achieving a goal co-occurs with reaching a destination)
- **Action**: The spatial schema is mapped onto the abstract domain as a primary metaphor (PURPOSES ARE DESTINATIONS)
- **Result**: The image schema now serves as cognitive infrastructure for understanding abstract concepts

### Step 4: Visual Encoding (DISL approach)
- **Trigger**: A researcher or language designer needs to represent an image schema visually
- **Action**: Select the appropriate DISL primitives (objects, paths, regions, forces, contacts) and compose them according to DISL rules within a panel or strip format
- **Result**: A formal, unambiguous, machine-readable visual representation of the image schema

### Step 5: Communication (proposed — not yet achieved)
- **Trigger**: A user of a hypothetical visual language wants to express a thought
- **Action**: Decompose the thought into its constituent image schemas and metaphorical mappings, select the corresponding visual primitives, compose them according to the visual grammar
- **Result**: A visual expression that communicates the thought to another user who knows the visual language

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: The CONTAINER Schema in Action
- **Scenario**: Expressing "she was full of anger but kept it inside"
- **Illustration**: The CONTAINER schema provides the structure: the person IS a container, the emotion IS a substance inside the container, the boundary (self-control) keeps the substance from escaping. Visually: a bounded region (person) with a filled interior (emotion substance), solid boundary (containment maintained).
- **Key Insight**: This is not just a colorful way of talking — it reflects the systematic metaphor EMOTIONAL STATES ARE SUBSTANCES IN CONTAINERS, grounded in the CONTAINER image schema. A visual language could represent this thought using exactly the visual structures that DISL already formalizes.

### Example 2: PATH Schema Grounding "Progress" and "Failure"
- **Scenario**: Expressing "the project is on track" vs. "the project has gone off the rails"
- **Illustration**: Both expressions use the PATH schema. "On track" = entity moving along the intended path toward the goal. "Off the rails" = entity deviating from the path. Visually: a path arrow with the entity positioned on or off the path line.
- **Key Insight**: Abstract project management concepts are being understood through the spatial structure of PATH. A visual language user could literally draw the situation — the entity (project), the path (intended trajectory), the goal (completion), and the current position (on-path or off-path). The drawing IS the thought.

### Example 3: FORCE Schema Grounding "Persuasion"
- **Scenario**: Expressing "she convinced him to change his mind"
- **Illustration**: The FORCE schema provides the structure: the persuader applies a force, the persuadee is the object of the force, the "change of mind" is the movement caused by the force. Visually: a force arrow from agent to patient, with the patient's trajectory altered by the force.
- **Key Insight**: Persuasion, a purely social-cognitive phenomenon, is understood through the same FORCE dynamics that govern physical interactions. The visual representation of FORCE (arrow, direction, magnitude) can represent both physical and social force.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: Image schemas are mental images — specific, detailed pictures in the mind.
  - **Reality**: Image schemas are *schematic* — they capture the abstract spatial structure of experiences, not their perceptual details. The CONTAINER schema is not a picture of any specific container; it is the abstract structure of inside/outside/boundary that applies to cups, rooms, bodies, and metaphorically to emotional states.

- **Misconception**: Image schemas are just spatial vocabulary — another way of saying "in," "on," "through."
  - **Reality**: Image schemas are pre-linguistic cognitive structures that exist before and independently of any language's spatial vocabulary. The fact that English happens to use "in" for containment is a consequence of the CONTAINER schema, not the other way around. Languages express schemas differently — some use case markers, some use postpositions, some use serial verbs — but the underlying schemas appear to be universal.

- **Misconception**: DISL already provides a visual language that could be used for communication.
  - **Reality**: DISL is an analytical notation for researchers, not a communication system. It can represent spatial-physical scenarios formally and precisely, but it lacks mechanisms for metaphorical extension, abstract logical operators, vocabulary for cultural concepts, or the pragmatic flexibility needed for everyday communication. DISL is a powerful foundation that needs several additional layers to become a language.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **Schemas that may not be universal**: While the core schemas (CONTAINER, PATH, FORCE) appear robustly cross-cultural, some proposed schemas (like CENTER-PERIPHERY or CYCLE) may be more culturally variable. Cultures with different spatial organization patterns (non-centralized societies, non-cyclical time concepts) may weight these schemas differently.

- **The "image schema list has never constituted a closed set"**: Unlike NSM's defined 65 primes, the image schema inventory is open-ended. Different researchers include different schemas, and the criteria for what counts as a "basic" schema (vs. a composition of simpler schemas) are debated. This openness is both a strength (flexibility) and a weakness (lack of standardization).

- **Complex metaphors that blend multiple schemas**: Many common conceptual metaphors involve simultaneous activation of multiple schemas. "I'm stuck in a dead-end job" uses CONTAINER (stuck in), PATH (dead-end = path with no exit), and FORCE (stuck = blocked). How a visual composition would handle such multi-schema blends without becoming visually overwhelming is an unsolved design challenge.

## Related Concepts

- **Semantic Primitives and NSM** (Category 3): The complementary framework providing the semantic inventory that image schemas would visually ground
- **Compositionality** (Category 5): The grammar rules needed to compose image schema symbols into complex expressions
- **Historical Visual Language Systems** (Category 1): DISL extends the tradition from Leibniz through Blissymbolics to formalized visual representation
- **Semasiographic vs. Glottographic** (Category 2): Image schema-based visual symbols would be inherently semasiographic — representing meaning through spatial structure, not speech sounds
- **The Phoneme Representation Problem** (Category 6): The gap between spatial-schematic representation and phonetic specificity that image schemas cannot bridge

## Research Gaps

- Can DISL be extended from a descriptive notation into a productive communication system? What additional layers would be needed?
- Has anyone empirically tested whether image schema-based visual symbols are recognized consistently across cultures?
- What is the full list of primary metaphors, and for each, what is the source image schema? Is there a comprehensive mapping?
- Can image schemas represent logical operators (NOT, IF, BECAUSE) through metaphorical extension? What specific visual representations would work?
- How many distinct image schema compositions are needed to cover the semantic territory of the ~300 core vocabulary words used in 80%+ of daily communication?
- What is the cognitive load of reading image schema compositions compared to reading conventional text? At what complexity level does visual parsing become slower than verbal processing?
