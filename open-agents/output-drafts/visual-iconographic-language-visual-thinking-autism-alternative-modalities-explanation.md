# Visual Thinking, Autism, and Alternative Communication Modalities

The "Why" Behind the Entire Project

---

## 1. The Wrong Question and the Right One

The standard framing: "How do we help nonverbal autistic people communicate?"

The reframe: "Why does the dominant communication infrastructure assume everyone thinks in words?"

That shift -- from deficit to design flaw -- is not a rhetorical gesture. It is grounded in converging evidence from cognitive science, neuroimaging, intelligence testing, and decades of lived experience. The evidence says, consistently and across multiple research programs, that a significant fraction of human beings process information most effectively through visual-spatial channels, and that the current communication infrastructure -- speech, phonetic writing, and the verbal encoding they demand -- imposes a systematic cost on those people.

This is not about "accommodating a disability." It is about recognizing that the communication tools most humans use were designed for a specific cognitive architecture, and that architecture is not universal. A visual iconographic language is not a workaround. It is a correction.

## 2. How Autistic People Think: Three Styles

Temple Grandin's taxonomy of thinking styles in autism, developed from her own experience as an object visualizer and refined through decades of observation and research, identifies three patterns:

**Object visualizers** (photo-realistic thinkers) think in vivid, detailed mental images. Grandin describes her own mind as "working like an Internet search engine set to access only images." When she thinks about a concept, she accesses a library of stored mental photographs that serve as templates for understanding and action. Object visualizers tend toward strengths in art, design, animal behavior, and concrete problem-solving -- domains where the ability to mentally simulate specific, detailed scenarios matters.

**Spatial visualizers** (pattern thinkers) think in abstract patterns, systems, and spatial relationships rather than photographic images. Where the object visualizer sees the specific image, the spatial visualizer sees the underlying structure -- the mathematical regularity, the systemic relationship, the pattern beneath the surface. Strengths cluster in mathematics, engineering, music, programming, and systems thinking. The spatial visualizer does not need to see the specific dog to understand "dog"; they understand "dog" through its relationships -- to other animals, to domestication, to evolutionary branching.

**Verbal thinkers** process primarily through language. Some autistic individuals are verbal thinkers, though this is less commonly discussed as a specifically autistic strength.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-2-grandin-taxonomy.html -->

Most individuals have mixed profiles, not pure types. But the distinction matters for design. A visual language built on image schemas and spatial relationships -- the fundamental spatial-relational structures like CONTAINER, PATH, FORCE, BALANCE -- would naturally align with spatial visualizers. Object visualizers might benefit more from pictorial/iconic detail in concrete referents. The design challenge is accommodation of both: a system whose base symbols are spatially and schematically organized (serving pattern thinkers) but can include iconic detail for concrete concepts (serving photo-realistic thinkers).

Research on cognitive styles in autistic adults supports the distinction between object and spatial visualization as meaningful dimensions, though the boundary between them is graded rather than categorical. Most people have elements of multiple styles. But the core observation holds: there is a population of human beings for whom visual-spatial processing is not a secondary skill but a primary cognitive mode -- and for whom verbal communication imposes a translation cost that verbal thinkers do not experience.

This is not a new insight. What is new is the proposal to build a communication system around it rather than simply documenting it. The typical response to Grandin's taxonomy has been: "Interesting. Autistic people think in pictures. How do we help them with language?" The right response is: "Interesting. We have been assuming everyone thinks in words. What would communication look like if we stopped assuming that?"

## 3. Enhanced Perception, Not Disordered Perception

Laurent Mottron's Enhanced Perceptual Functioning (EPF) model, published in 2006 with over 1,766 citations, is the most empirically supported reframing of autistic cognition. (The "most empirically validated" characterization reflects a specific perspective in the field -- other models such as weak central coherence and predictive coding also have substantial support. EPF's influence is undeniable.)

The core claim: autistic perception is genuinely enhanced, not disordered. This is not metaphor or euphemism. It is an empirical finding replicated across multiple perceptual domains.

Mottron and colleagues identified eight principles of autistic perception:

1. **Enhanced visual pattern detection** -- superior ability to identify visual patterns and regularities
2. **Greater perceptual discrimination** -- finer ability to distinguish similar stimuli
3. **Locally oriented default processing** -- processing defaults to fine detail rather than global overview
4. **Enhanced first-order pattern detection** -- stronger detection of basic structural regularities
5. **Possible increased autonomy from top-down influence** -- perception less shaped by prior expectations and more by actual stimulus
6. **Spatial task superiority** -- better performance on tasks requiring spatial reasoning
7. **Enhanced auditory processing** -- superior discrimination of auditory stimuli
8. **Possible enhanced tactile processing** -- better discrimination of tactile input

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-4-epf-radar.html -->

These are not deficits wearing a positive label. Enhanced Embedded Figures Task performance (finding a hidden shape within a complex pattern), faster visual search for targets in cluttered displays, better discrimination of similar tones -- these are measurable, replicable advantages across large sample sizes. The EPF model does not claim that everything about autistic perception is "better" -- it claims that specific dimensions of perceptual processing are genuinely enhanced, and that these enhancements are a physiological feature of neural organization, not a learned compensatory strategy.

The pattern of connectivity supporting these enhancements is documented through neuroimaging: autistic brains show increased short-range connectivity within certain regions (supporting detailed local processing) and decreased long-range connectivity between distant regions (potentially supporting processing independence from top-down conceptual influence). This neural architecture does not produce "deficient perception with some scattered abilities." It produces a different perceptual profile with specific, measurable advantages in the domains most relevant to visual symbol processing.

It is worth pausing on what "autonomy from top-down influence" means in practice. In neurotypical perception, what you expect to see shapes what you actually see -- prior knowledge, context, and expectation actively filter incoming visual information. If autistic perception is more autonomous from this top-down filtering, it means the stimulus itself has greater influence over what is perceived. For a visual symbol system, this is not a bug; it is a feature. Symbols designed to carry their meaning in their visible structure (bottom-up) rather than requiring contextual interpretation (top-down) would align perfectly with this perceptual profile.

The design implications are direct. If the target user population has enhanced visual pattern detection, the symbol system should reward pattern detection. If they have superior local detail processing, symbols should carry meaningful information at the component level. If their perception is more autonomous from top-down influence, meaning should be derivable from visible components rather than requiring contextual interpretation. If spatial reasoning is a strength, grammar should be encoded through spatial arrangement.

A visual language designed to leverage these enhancements would not merely be "accessible" to autistic users. It would be designed for their strengths. They might process it more efficiently than neurotypical users, not less.

## 4. The Raven's Revelation: Intelligence Hidden by Modality

In 2007, Michelle Dawson, Isabelle Soulieres, Morton Ann Gernsbacher, and Laurent Mottron published a finding that should have changed how the world understands autistic intelligence.

They gave the same autistic children two intelligence tests: the Wechsler Intelligence Scales (which rely heavily on verbal comprehension, verbal working memory, and verbally-mediated reasoning) and Raven's Progressive Matrices (which measure abstract spatial pattern reasoning without any verbal requirement).

The results were not a modest difference. They were a chasm.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-3-ravens-revelation.html -->

On the Wechsler: not a single autistic child scored in the "high intelligence" range. One-third scored in the "intellectually disabled" range.

On Raven's: one-third scored in the "high intelligence" range. Only 1 in 20 scored in the "intellectually disabled" range.

The same children. Thirty to seventy percentile points higher on Raven's than on the Wechsler. The intelligence was there all along. The measurement tool could not see it.

Neuroimaging added a physiological dimension. During Raven's tasks, autistic individuals showed increased activity in extrastriate visual areas (BA18) and decreased activity in prefrontal cortex (BA9) -- visual processing playing a more prominent role in reasoning than in neurotypical controls. They also showed a stronger preference for constructive matching -- building the answer from components rather than eliminating wrong answers. This is not a deficit-based strategy. It is a construction-based one, and it is more effective for them.

The parallel to communication is exact. If a verbal intelligence test systematically underestimates autistic intelligence by forcing visual-spatial reasoning through a verbal bottleneck, then verbal communication may systematically underestimate autistic communicative competence by forcing visual-spatial thought through the same bottleneck.

Consider what this means in practical terms. An autistic child who scores in the "intellectually disabled" range on the Wechsler may receive educational and communication support calibrated to that measured intelligence. The expectations set for that child -- the complexity of material presented, the communication goals established, the employment prospects projected -- will be shaped by a number that underestimates their cognitive capacity by 30 to 70 percentile points. The child is not intellectually disabled. The test is modality-disabled.

The same phenomenon plays out in communication settings. An autistic individual who struggles to articulate complex ideas verbally -- who pauses, who loses the thread, who simplifies, who gives up midway through an explanation -- may be perceived as having "limited communication skills" or even "limited cognitive complexity." But the limitation may be entirely in the channel. The thought may be rich, structured, and complete in visual-spatial form. What appears as communicative poverty may be a translation bottleneck presenting as a content deficit.

Change the measurement tool -- change the modality -- and competence becomes visible. A visual communication system could do for expression what Raven's does for assessment. The intelligence is already there. The communication capacity is already there. The infrastructure to reveal it is what is missing.

## 5. The Modality Mismatch: Not a Deficit, a Bandwidth Problem

This is where the evidence converges into a synthesis. It is important to be precise about what this synthesis is: it is the document's own integration of multiple findings into a coherent framing, not a named published theory with the label "modality mismatch hypothesis." The individual findings supporting it are each independently verified. The integration is ours.

The evidence:

**Autistic individuals perform better on semantic tasks when visual access is provided.** When matching pictures to words (visual access to meaning), autistic participants perform better than when matching words to words (verbal access to meaning). The semantic knowledge is intact. The bottleneck is in the access channel.

**Autistic thought modality is more stable.** Non-autistic adults shift fluidly between verbal and visual thinking modes depending on task demands. Autistic adults maintain a more consistent reliance on visual processing -- less likely to shift to verbal mode even when tasks seem to call for it. This is not inflexibility. It is a consistent cognitive architecture that happens to misalign with a verbal communication infrastructure.

**The translation cost is real and described by autistic people themselves.** Verbal autistic adults describe the experience: "I can see the entire concept in my mind as a spatial structure -- all the pieces, how they connect. But to tell you about it, I have to disassemble that structure, serialize it into a linear sequence of words, find the right words for each piece, and speak them one at a time. By the time I have said the third sentence, I have forgotten what I was going to say in the seventh, because the verbal serialization process has consumed the working memory I need to hold the overall structure."

This is not a language deficit. It is a bandwidth mismatch. The visual-spatial representation is rich, complete, and coherent. The verbal channel is the bottleneck. Cognitive energy that should be available for the content of communication is instead consumed by the process of translating between modalities.

A communication system that does not require this translation -- one that allows visual-spatial thought to be expressed directly in visual-spatial format -- could reduce or eliminate the translation cost. The thought structure stays spatial. The expression stays spatial. Nothing is lost in serialization.

This is the fundamental rationale for a visual iconographic language. Not as a communication aid for people who "can't" use words. As an alternative modality for people whose cognition works differently than the dominant communication infrastructure assumes.

## 6. Monotropism: Deep Focus as Design Partner

Monotropism, proposed by Dinah Murray, Mike Lesser, and Wendy Lawson in 2005, describes the autistic attention profile: intense focus on a smaller number of interests or stimuli at any given time, rather than broad, distributed attention across many channels.

The implications for learning are often framed negatively -- "difficulty transitioning," "rigid interests," "inability to multitask." Reframed through a design lens, monotropism is a learning superpower waiting for the right material.

A monotropic learner engaged with a visual symbol system during an interest-driven learning phase will process that system with extraordinary depth. Every sub-component will be inspected. Every pattern will be detected. Every compositional rule will be tested against examples. The depth of engagement that monotropism enables is exactly what a compositional visual system rewards.

The design principle that follows: the visual language should be learnable through interest-driven exploration, not mandatory sequential curriculum. An autistic teenager with an intense interest in marine biology should be able to enter the symbol system through marine biology concepts -- water, creature, deep, surface, predator, prey -- and expand outward through semantic connections. "Water" connects to "flow" and "container." "Predator" connects to "force" and "path." The monotropic attention that drives deep learning within the interest domain naturally extends to foundational symbols through compositional relationships.

Symbol design should reward close inspection with meaningful detail, not punish deep focus with overwhelming complexity. Each base symbol should have a reason for every visual element -- a visible logic that rewards the question "why is this part shaped like that?" A monotropic learner will find those reasons. If the reasons are not there -- if the symbol is arbitrary rather than grounded -- the system will feel unsatisfying at precisely the cognitive level where it needs to feel elegant.

There is a deeper connection here between monotropism and compositionality. A compositional symbol system -- one where complex meanings are built from simpler parts according to visible rules -- is inherently a system that rewards deep inspection. Every composed symbol is a puzzle whose solution is its meaning. A monotropic learner who becomes absorbed in working out compositional logic is not "stuck on details" -- they are learning the language through exactly the process that their attention profile supports.

The contrast with how AAC is typically taught is instructive. Standard AAC training often uses distributed practice: a little time on each of many symbols, spread across sessions, covering the curriculum in prescribed order. This approach fights monotropic attention rather than working with it. Interest-driven exploration -- entering the system through whatever domain captures attention, expanding outward through compositional connections -- aligns with the attention profile rather than opposing it.

## 7. An Estimated 25-30% Are Minimally Verbal

Between 25 and 30 percent of the autistic population is minimally verbal or nonspeaking -- producing fewer than 20-30 functional words, or no speech at all. This is a confirmed statistic across multiple sources.

For this population, the current communication landscape offers:

- **PECS and PCS-based AAC**: Functional but linguistically limited. Good for basic requesting and labeling. Insufficient for narrative, explanation, humor, or complex social exchange.
- **Blissymbolics**: More linguistically sophisticated, but harder to learn and less widely available.
- **Spelling-based AAC (typing + speech synthesis)**: Full expressiveness, but requires literacy -- and many minimally verbal individuals have not had the opportunity to develop full literacy.

Between the limited expressiveness of picture-based AAC and the full expressiveness of text lies a vast space. No system currently occupies it. That space -- more expressive than PECS, not dependent on phonetic encoding, designed for visual-spatial processing, usable as a primary communication medium rather than a supplement -- is where the visual iconographic language aims.

But the 25-30% figure understates the population that could benefit. Many verbal autistic individuals -- people who speak fluently and are perceived as "not having communication difficulties" -- experience significant processing costs when using spoken language. The modality mismatch applies to them too. Exhaustion after extended conversation. Difficulty translating complex spatial thoughts into linear verbal output. A persistent sense that what they said was less than what they thought. Loss of detail and structure in the serialization from spatial thought to sequential words. Situations where they know something deeply but cannot make the verbal channel carry the full weight of that knowledge.

This experience is often invisible to others. A verbal autistic person who speaks in complete sentences does not look like someone with a communication difficulty. But the cost is internal: cognitive resources spent on the translation process rather than on the content of the communication. After a long meeting, the exhaustion is not from the topic but from the channel.

For these individuals, a visual communication option would not replace speech. It would provide an additional channel -- one that works with their cognitive architecture instead of against it. Some situations would still call for speech. Others -- personal reflection, complex explanation, emotional expression, creative work -- might be better served by a modality that preserves spatial structure rather than forcing linearization.

The visual language is not, therefore, designed only for nonspeaking people. It is designed for anyone whose thought patterns are better served by visual-spatial expression than by verbal serialization. The nonspeaking population has the most acute need. The broader population of visual-spatial thinkers has a need that is less obvious but no less real.

## 8. The Communication Landscape and the Gap

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-5-communication-modality-landscape.html -->

When existing communication systems are mapped on two dimensions -- expressiveness (from basic requests to full natural language) and visual-spatial alignment (from purely verbal/phonetic to purely visual/spatial) -- a clear pattern emerges:

**Speech** occupies the upper-left: full expressiveness, zero visual-spatial alignment. All encoding is phonetic.

**Written text** sits nearby: full expressiveness, but still phonetically encoded. Spelling represents speech sounds. Written language is a visual medium, but its encoding is glottographic (representing language sounds), not semasiographic (representing meaning directly).

**Sign languages** occupy the upper-right: full expressiveness and full visual-spatial alignment. Sign languages prove that the visual-spatial modality can achieve everything spoken language can. But sign languages require 3D spatial production -- motor capabilities and spatial/temporal production that screen-based communication does not demand.

**PCS and PECS** occupy the lower-right: high visual-spatial alignment but limited expressiveness.

**Blissymbolics** sits in the center: moderate both.

The gap -- high expressiveness in a written/screen-based visual-spatial format -- is real and currently unoccupied. Sign languages prove it is possible in the visual-spatial modality. The visual iconographic language aims for the same quadrant via a written/digital medium.

## 8a. What People Get Wrong About Visual Communication and Autism

Several persistent misconceptions need direct address.

**"Thinking in pictures means photographic memory."** Grandin's taxonomy distinguishes object visualizers (photorealistic imagery) from spatial visualizers (abstract patterns and systems). Many autistic individuals think in abstract spatial patterns, not concrete photographs. And many have mixed profiles. "Thinking in pictures" is a useful shorthand that oversimplifies a diverse cognitive landscape.

**"Nonspeaking means nothing to say."** The inability to produce speech does not indicate absence of thought, emotion, or communicative intent. The Raven's finding alone demolishes this assumption: children who scored in the "intellectually disabled" range on a verbal test scored in the "high intelligence" range on a visual-spatial one. The same cognitive richness that is invisible through verbal measurement is invisible through verbal communication -- and for the same reason.

**"Visual supports are for children."** Visual processing preferences are lifelong. Many autistic adults prefer visual information presentation, use visual organizational tools, and process written/visual information more efficiently than spoken instructions. The infantilization of visual communication tools is a design failure, not a reflection of user needs. An adult who communicates through visual symbols is not using "baby tools" -- they are using a communication modality that their brain processes efficiently.

**"If someone can speak, they don't need alternatives."** Many verbal autistic individuals experience significant processing costs during speech. Verbal fluency does not mean verbal ease. Access to a visual communication modality would be valuable for verbal individuals who experience translation costs -- not just for nonspeaking individuals.

**"A system designed for autistic people would be limiting."** A well-designed visual-spatial communication system would be useful for anyone who processes information visually. Individuals with aphasia after stroke. Multilingual contexts where visual symbols bypass language barriers. Anyone who finds visual-spatial representation intuitive for certain types of content. Designing for the most demanding use case -- autistic visual-spatial thinkers with enhanced perceptual processing -- produces a system with high design quality that serves a broader population.

## 9. Neural Architecture and Symbol Design

The atypical neural connectivity patterns in autism -- increased short-range connectivity within certain regions, decreased long-range connectivity between distant brain areas -- have direct design implications.

Enhanced local connectivity supports superior local detail detection. This means symbols with rich local detail may be processed particularly well by autistic users. A base symbol should carry meaningful information at the component level -- not just at the gestalt level.

The pattern also suggests that bottom-up composition (build meaning from visible components) may work better than top-down interpretation (require context to disambiguate). Blissymbolics' indicator system, where a small mark above a symbol specifies its grammatical role, is a bottom-up mechanism: the information is visible in the symbol itself, not derived from context.

Processing with greater independence from top-down influence means symbols should carry their meaning as transparently as possible. Each composition should be interpretable from its visible parts without requiring the reader to know what conversation it appeared in. Context-dependent interpretation is a feature of natural language that may introduce unnecessary friction for autistic users.

## 10. Neurodiversity-Affirming Design: Not Optional

The ethical framework for this project is not a postscript. It is a design requirement.

**Design for cognitive difference, not deficit.** The visual language is not "fixing" a communication problem. It is providing an alternative modality that aligns with visual-spatial cognition. The problem being solved is infrastructure mismatch, not user malfunction.

**Center user agency and self-determination.** Autistic individuals must be co-designers, testers, and decision-makers in the language design process. Not consultants. Not test subjects. Partners with decision-making authority. Recent frameworks for neurodiversity-affirming interventions emphasize that until recently, nearly no research sought input from autistic people in designing interventions or prioritizing goals. This must change.

**Avoid the "cure" framing.** The language is not meant to replace speech or "normalize" autistic communication. It is an additional modality for those who find it useful. "I prefer to communicate this way" is the target user statement, not "I have been fixed."

**Recognize existing competence.** The Raven's finding demonstrates that competence is hidden by modality mismatch. The visual language recognizes and serves existing capabilities -- it does not create capabilities that were absent.

**Affirm autistic communication styles.** Direct, pattern-based, systematized communication is not "wrong" -- it may simply need a communication medium that matches its structure. A visual language based on systematic compositional rules is inherently compatible with a cognitive style that thrives on patterns and systems. The autistic preference for explicit rules over implicit social conventions, for systematic structure over contextual improvisation, for logical consistency over pragmatic flexibility -- these are features that a well-designed visual language would serve, not obstacles it would need to work around.

**Design for the most demanding use case.** A system designed for autistic visual-spatial thinkers -- with their enhanced perceptual processing, their demand for systematic structure, their sensitivity to arbitrary design -- will be a well-designed system for anyone who processes information visually. Designing for autistic users is designing for quality. The broader applicability -- to individuals with aphasia, multilingual contexts, visual learners generally -- follows from getting the design right for the most perceptive users.

## 11. Edge Cases and Honest Limits

Not all autistic people are visual thinkers. Some process primarily through language, and for them, text-based communication may always be preferable. The visual language is one option among several, not a universal solution.

Alexithymia -- difficulty identifying and describing emotions verbally -- affects a significant proportion of autistic individuals. A visual language with grounded emotion representations (image schema-based, like Blissymbolics' heart + direction for happy/sad) could provide alternative access to emotional vocabulary without requiring verbal labeling.

Intersecting disabilities -- visual impairment, motor impairment, intellectual disability co-occurring with autism -- require the system to support multiple input methods (touch, gaze, switch) and potentially tactile representation.

Cultural variation in visual processing is an open question. While enhanced perceptual functioning appears to be a consistent feature of autistic cognition across cultures, specific symbol interpretations may vary. An image schema like CONTAINER is claimed to be universal -- all humans experience containment through their bodies and the physical world. But the visual representation of CONTAINER (a bounded region on a surface) may carry different connotations in different cultures. A square boundary might evoke a room, a box, a prison, a garden, depending on cultural context. Cross-cultural testing is essential and has not been done.

Acquired communication disorders represent another population. Individuals who lose speech through stroke, traumatic brain injury, or progressive conditions like ALS may benefit from a visual communication system even without pre-existing visual processing preferences. Their learning pathway would differ fundamentally from lifelong visual thinkers -- they would be adapting to a new modality rather than finally receiving one that matches their existing cognition.

The system will not serve everyone. Some autistic individuals will find it unhelpful, irrelevant, or inferior to their existing communication tools. This is expected and acceptable. It is an option offered, not a solution imposed. The neurodiversity-affirming principle applies in both directions: just as the system should not be withheld from people who might benefit, it should not be pushed on people who do not want it.

## 12. What This Means for Design

The evidence converges on a set of design principles:

1. **Visual-spatial encoding, not phonetic.** The system must represent meaning directly in visual-spatial form, not encode the sounds of any spoken language.

2. **Bottom-up composition.** Meaning should be derivable from visible components, not requiring contextual interpretation.

3. **Pattern regularity.** Consistent compositional rules that reward pattern detection -- the cognitive strength most strongly documented in autistic processing.

4. **Spatial grammar.** Grammatical relationships encoded through position, proximity, and enclosure -- leveraging spatial reasoning strengths.

5. **Interest-driven learning.** The system should support monotropic engagement -- deep focused exploration from any entry point, expanding through semantic connections.

6. **Core vocabulary priority.** Start with the 200-400 most communicatively powerful words, not with easy-to-picture nouns.

7. **Multimodal compatibility.** Alongside speech and gesture, not instead of them. Triple coding (visual + verbal + gestural) is more effective than any single channel.

8. **Digital-first, accessible-always.** Touch, eye-tracking, switch scanning compatibility. SVG rendering. Screen-based composition.

9. **Autistic co-design.** From the first prototype to the governance structure. Not "designed for autistic people" but "designed with autistic people."

These are not aspirational values. They are requirements derived from evidence. Each one has a citation trail leading to verified research.

The convergence is worth emphasizing. These nine principles do not come from a single theoretical framework. They come from Grandin's taxonomy (visual thinking types), Mottron's EPF model (enhanced perception), the Raven's finding (modality affects measured competence), monotropism theory (attention profile), AAC core vocabulary research (functional word priority), Paivio's dual coding (multimodal memory), and neurodiversity-affirming design principles (co-design, agency). That all of these independently developed lines of research point toward the same set of design requirements is itself evidence that the requirements are sound.

The visual iconographic language does not need every element to work perfectly. Even partial success -- a system that achieves Tier 1 communication, serves some visual-spatial thinkers, and provides a useful thinking tool -- would represent a meaningful contribution. The evidence says the attempt is worth making. The design principles say how to make it. The rest is engineering, testing, and iteration.
