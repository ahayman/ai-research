# IFS and Alexithymia: How It Works (and Where the Mismatch Lives)

## Overview

There is a structural mismatch at the centre of Internal Family Systems and you should know what it is before you sit down with it again.

IFS asks a person to find parts in their body, hold inward attention on them, build them out into images or scenes, and notice the felt sense of their own emotional stance toward each one. Three of the four entry steps lean directly on interoception — the perception of internal bodily state. Alexithymia disrupts exactly that channel. Roughly half of autistic adults are alexithymic; about 5% of non-autistic adults are too. So a non-trivial fraction of the people who arrive at IFS — including most autistic adults seeking it — bring a perceptual profile that the standard protocol leans on least gracefully.

The question this page answers is: **does that mean IFS is a poor fit, a slow fit you train into, or a fit that requires structural modification?** The honest answer, drawing on the IFS Telehealth Collective's published practice, several autistic IFS clinicians, the longer-running Gendlin Focusing tradition, and the empirical interoception literature, is **structural modification with cautious openness to slow capacity-building where it emerges naturally — and never via "feel harder."** This page lays out why.

**Confidence overview**: Of fourteen substantive claims in this page, eleven are confirmed, three are uncertain (specific terminology, expressive-writing effect sizes, and the specific text where one IFS author uses a phrase). Zero are invalid. The empirical anchors — Shah/Bird's *Cortex* paper, Garfinkel's three-dimensional interoception model, the TAS-20 subscale structure, the anterior insula neural correlates — all hold up under independent verification. The clinical adaptation literature is clinician-experiential, not peer-reviewed RCT evidence; that distinction is preserved throughout.

**Key takeaways**:
- Alexithymia disrupts interoceptive accuracy specifically, and roughly half of autistic adults have it.
- Three of the six Fs are interoception-heavy; three are not. The protocol has a built-in bottleneck zone and a built-in escape route.
- IFS already permits substitute access channels — externalized, cognitive, written, visual — but tends to treat them as workarounds rather than primary modes. For low-interoception clients they are the primary modes.
- Pushing alexithymic clients to "feel into your body" can backfire. The harm risk is real and documented in the body-scan literature.
- The literature lands on structural modification, not "you'll feel it eventually." That's the honest answer.

## What Alexithymia Is, and What It Actually Disrupts

Alexithymia — literally "no words for feelings" — is a trait-level pattern of difficulty processing one's own emotional states. The most common measurement instrument is the Toronto Alexithymia Scale, 20 items, developed by Bagby, Parker, and Taylor in 1994 and validated extensively since. The scale yields a total score and three subscales:

- **DIF — Difficulty Identifying Feelings** (7 items). Trouble distinguishing emotional states from each other and from bodily arousal. "I am often confused about what emotion I am feeling."
- **DDF — Difficulty Describing Feelings** (5 items). Inability to put feelings into words once identified. "It is difficult for me to find the right words for my feelings."
- **EOT — Externally Oriented Thinking** (8 items). A thinking style that prefers concrete external detail over inner reflection. "I prefer talking to people about their daily activities rather than their feelings."

The three subscales are intercorrelated but separable. DIF and DDF share the most variance and tend to track together; EOT has weaker psychometric properties and is sometimes excluded or replaced by newer instruments like the Perth Alexithymia Questionnaire. The clinical implication is that alexithymia is not one thing — it includes a perceptual component (DIF), a labelling component (DDF), and a cognitive-style component (EOT), and a person can be high on one without being high on the others.

### Interoception is not one thing either

A key clarification before going further. Sarah Garfinkel and colleagues (2015, 2016) proposed that "interoception" decomposes into three empirically separable dimensions:

- **Interoceptive accuracy** — performance on objective tasks like heartbeat detection. How close are you to truth?
- **Interoceptive sensibility** — self-reported tendency to attend to internal signals. How much do you orient inward?
- **Interoceptive awareness** — metacognitive: how well does your confidence track your accuracy?

These dissociate. A person can have low accuracy with high sensibility (they think they notice things, but they don't pick up the signal) — or high accuracy with low sensibility (they perceive accurately but don't habitually orient inward) — or any combination. "I can't feel into my body" is at least three distinct problems.

The heartbeat-counting paradigm itself has methodological controversy (Zamariola et al. 2018; commentary and counter-commentary 2020–2025), with critics noting that scores are dominated by underreports and that knowledge-based estimation can produce artefactually good performance. The clinical implication is robust regardless: self-reported interoception aligns very tightly with alexithymia even when the behavioral tasks are noisy.

### The Shah, Hall, Catmur, Bird (2016) finding

In the 2016 *Cortex* paper "Alexithymia, not autism, is associated with impaired interoception," Punit Shah, Richard Hall, Caroline Catmur, and Geoffrey Bird ran two heartbeat-detection experiments comparing autistic and non-autistic adults stratified by alexithymia level. The result: reduced interoceptive accuracy tracked alexithymia, not autism. When alexithymia was statistically controlled, autism alone did not predict the impairment.

This matters for IFS because it places the interoception problem squarely on alexithymia, not on autism per se. About half of autistic adults have it; the other half have roughly typical interoception. Non-autistic populations — PTSD, eating disorders, somatic-symptom presentations, around 5% of neurotypicals — also show alexithymia. The Brewer/Bird group's separate 2016 *Royal Society Open Science* paper "Alexithymia: a general deficit of interoception" (Brewer, Cook, Bird) generalises the finding: alexithymia involves deficits not just in cardiac interoception but across modalities (gastric, respiratory, thermal). Both papers are real and load-bearing; do not conflate them.

(Note: earlier drafts of this material cited "Brewer et al." for the autism-vs-alexithymia finding. The correct citation is **Shah, Hall, Catmur, Bird (2016)**.)

### Neural correlates

The neural substrate of alexithymia centres on the **anterior insula** and the **anterior cingulate cortex**. Hogeveen and colleagues (2016) showed that lesions to the anterior insula produce *acquired* alexithymia in previously non-alexithymic adults — strong evidence that the AI is doing something causally relevant rather than just correlating with the trait. Structural neuroimaging meta-analyses (van der Velde et al.) find reduced gray matter volumes in the left insula, left amygdala, orbital frontal cortex, and striatum in high-alexithymia samples. Functional studies show reduced AI activation during emotional processing.

The clinical translation: when a therapist asks a high-DIF client "where in your body do you notice this?", the part of the brain that would normally generate the integrated answer is genuinely doing less work. This isn't avoidance, resistance, or insufficient practice. It's neuroanatomy.

### Emotional granularity — the labelling side

Lisa Feldman Barrett's emotional-granularity work intersects here. Granularity is the precision with which a person carves up emotional states — a high-granularity person distinguishes "irritated," "frustrated," "resentful," and "mildly furious" as different states; a low-granularity person uses "bad" or "upset" to cover all of them. Granularity is inversely related to alexithymia — they are essentially measuring nearby constructs from different research traditions.

This matters because it suggests an additional access route. Even when interoceptive perception is impaired, granularity-building work — affect-labelling exercises, structured emotion vocabularies, journaling that requires increasingly differentiated language — can sometimes improve emotional processing without going through the body-scan route at all. For some clients this is the cleaner path.

## Which IFS Steps Depend on Interoception

The 6 Fs are the standard sequence for accessing a part. Their interoception load is uneven, and once you see the pattern the protocol's bottleneck is obvious.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-1 -->

- **Find** asks "where in your body do you notice this part?" — the prompt itself names the body. **High interoception load.**
- **Focus** asks the client to sustain inward attention on what was found. If the target is somatic, sustaining attention on it is interoception-heavy. If the target is cognitive or visual, the load drops. **Medium-to-high.**
- **Flesh out** invites image, age, appearance — visual and sometimes somatic. Aphantasia (the absence of voluntary mental imagery, ~2–4% prevalence) is a separate disruption here. **Medium load**, with a visual bottleneck independent of interoception.
- **Feel toward** asks "how do you feel toward this part?" This is the gateway question — IFS treats the answer as the diagnostic for whether Self-energy is present. The question itself is interoceptive: it requires reading your own emotional stance, in the moment. **High interoception load.**
- **beFriend** is dialogic. It runs on relationship-building moves that don't require interoceptive perception. **Low load.**
- **Fears** asks the part what it's afraid would happen if it stopped doing its job. Cognitive and narrative. **Low load.**

The pattern is clean. **The first half of the protocol is the interoception zone. The second half runs on language.** A person with low interoceptive access faces the highest barrier in Find, Focus, and Feel toward — the steps that get you in the door. Once those are accomplished by any means, the back half of the protocol works substantially the same regardless of how the front half was done.

## What Clinicians Who Work with Alexithymic Clients Actually Do

The empirical literature on IFS modified for alexithymic clients is thin. There is no peer-reviewed RCT. What exists is a body of practice writing from clinicians who have worked through the problem in session and published their adaptations. Three sources are concrete enough to quote.

### The IFS Telehealth Collective

The IFS Telehealth Collective — a clinical group of IFS-trained therapists working specifically with neurodivergent clients online — published "Supporting Your Neurotype with Internal Family Systems" as a public statement of practice. They name the conditions they adapt for explicitly: "low interoception, alexithymia, aphantasia, and sensory sensitivities, as well as those challenged by executive functioning tasks."

Their stated approach has three concrete moves. First, they start with externalized parts work and let interoceptive noticing emerge later, if it emerges. "Some clients may work most effectively with externalized parts, and with guidance from their therapist, they begin paying mindful attention to any physical sensations they may experience. Over time, clients often make connections between their somatic experience and a particular part of themselves." Second, they reframe alexithymia as a feature the client has been criticized and ridiculed for — making the prior invalidation itself a target for IFS-style burden tending. Third, they describe sessions as menu-style rather than protocol-fixed: "mindfulness, imagery, identifying and feeling emotions, cognitive processing, verbal processing, sensing into your body, externalizing through creating art or using objects, and other outlets" — the therapist picks what works for this client, this part, today.

The Collective's "Creative Externalization" companion piece details art-based externalization specifically: parts mapping with drawn figures, sand tray with chosen miniatures, painting and clay work. The practice premise is that putting parts outside yourself — onto paper, onto the table — gives clients without strong interoception or imagery a representational system they can actually work with.

### Sarah Bergenfield

Bergenfield is a Level 3 IFS practitioner, a somatic psychologist, an autistic adult diagnosed at age 50, and the co-author with Martha Sweezy and Beth Ann Malow of *Wired to Feel: Autism as a Condition of Sensory Surplus* (2025). Her central reframing is that autistic emotional life is often *sensory surplus rather than absence* — what looks like alexithymia from the outside may be signal that is too overwhelming or too unparseable to convert into the standard categories, not signal that is missing. This reframing has clinical consequences. If alexithymia is a *parsing* problem rather than a *perception* problem in some autistic clients, granularity-building and labelling work may help where body-scan training would not. Her work integrates predictive processing, polyvagal theory, and IFS into a single frame.

### IFS with Sanni and IFS & Neurodiversity (Substack)

Sanni Lehtinen, an online practitioner under the name IFS with Sanni, writes that "you do not need to be visual to connect with your parts" — parts can show up as thoughts, energy patterns, or external behaviors. Her practice frames alexithymia as a feature of the system rather than a deficit, treating cognitive and externalized access as primary modes.

Emma's IFS & Neurodiversity Substack — the post "Can Self be Autistic?" and the resources page — collates adaptations from across the autistic IFS-clinician community and engages directly with whether the IFS canonical Self-qualities map onto autistic phenomenology. (The autism-specific intersection is treated in depth in Cat 7; what concerns us here is the alexithymia overlap.)

### The recurring concrete moves

Across these writers, the same adaptations recur:
- Externalized representation as the default — figurines, sand tray, drawings, written dialogue, cards
- Cognitive access treated as primary, not as fallback — recognizing parts as belief-clusters or thought-patterns
- Direct access (the IFS protocol move where the therapist speaks with parts directly through the client's voice) used from the outset rather than as a workaround
- Slowing the pace — checking that "no" answers actually mean "I don't experience that" rather than "I can't tell"
- Removing the body-noticing requirement explicitly when it isn't producing signal
- Reframing somatic surplus rather than somatic absence

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-3 -->

These adaptations have a clinical logic that deserves to be named directly. **They are not concessions to a deficit. They are different routes through the same protocol.** The 6 Fs still happen. They are routed through writing or drawing or recognition or behavioral observation rather than felt sense.

## The Gendlin Focusing Parallel

Eugene Gendlin developed Focusing in the 1960s and 1970s after his University of Chicago research found that successful psychotherapy clients across all orientations did *something* characteristic. They paused mid-sentence, attended inward in a specific way, and let words emerge from a bodily knowing rather than from cognition. Gendlin called that knowing the "felt sense" — the unclear, pre-verbal bodily sense of a situation that, when attended to, contains the seeds of its own resolution. Unsuccessful clients, his research found, did not do this.

The Focusing tradition has therefore been working on the alexithymia problem since well before IFS existed, and the literature is informative. Some clients build felt-sense access over time with patient training. Others never do, and pushing harder can produce dissociation or somatic dysregulation rather than the emergence of the signal.

Modern Focusing-oriented therapists — most notably **Jan Winhall**, whose Felt Sense Polyvagal Model (FSPM) integrates Gendlin's Focusing with Stephen Porges's polyvagal theory — have made an important corrective explicit. If the autonomic nervous system is collapsed (dorsal vagal shutdown) or in fight/flight (sympathetic activation), the felt sense is structurally inaccessible. Pushing inward attention before the system is regulated does not produce signal; it can deepen the shutdown or trigger flooding. The intervention, in Winhall's frame, is not "feel harder" but **regulate state first, then approach the felt sense if and when it becomes available**. Her two books — *Treating Trauma and Addiction with the Felt Sense Polyvagal Model* (Routledge, 2021) and *20 Embodied Practices for Healing Trauma and Addiction* (W.W. Norton, 2025, with a foreword by Porges) — articulate this in clinically usable form.

This polyvagal-informed view recasts alexithymia in some cases as a *protective* shutdown rather than a pure perceptual deficit, especially in trauma-origin alexithymia. The clinical implication for IFS is direct: forcing inward attention before regulating state can re-traumatize. The Cat 8 page treats complex trauma in depth; what concerns us here is that the literature in the parent tradition (Focusing) explicitly anticipates the harm risk and offers a cleaner pacing model than IFS materials typically provide.

> **Evidence note**: Winhall's books exist and Porges did write the Norton foreword. The clinical claim — that polyvagal-informed pacing makes felt-sense access safer — is articulated in book form and represents the working consensus among Focusing-oriented trauma therapists. It has not been tested as an empirical intervention against unmodified Focusing or against IFS in head-to-head trials.

## Can Interoceptive Capacity Be Built? An Honest Answer

The literature is genuinely mixed and worth reading carefully.

**Some evidence that body-based mindfulness reduces alexithymia scores.** Norman, Marzano, Coulson, and Oskis (2019) conducted a systematic review of mindfulness-based interventions for alexithymia, meta-analysing four RCTs. They found a statistically significant effect on TAS-20 scores (mean difference -5.28 on the standard scale, 95% CI -9.28 to -1.28, p=0.010). Heterogeneity reduced when restricted to interventions of three months or less. Khalsa and colleagues (2025) ran a much larger meta-analysis — 29 RCTs, N=2,191 — on mindfulness interventions and self-reported interoception, finding a small-to-medium positive effect. Both reviews indicate that interoceptive and alexithymia outcomes can improve with sustained mindfulness practice.

**But the relationship is non-linear.** Aaron and colleagues (2020), in a study of 76 young adults, found that the relationship between alexithymia and interoceptive accuracy was best described as quadratic. Both very high and very low interoceptive accuracy correlate with elevated alexithymia. The implication is non-trivial: more interoceptive signal is not always better. A person could become more accurate at registering signals they then can't parse — which may produce *more* distress rather than less. The pilot mindfulness intervention in the same study found that both meditation and control groups improved equivalently, with no group difference attributable to the intervention itself — meaning the strong "mindfulness fixes alexithymia" claim is not as robust as the brief reviews above might suggest.

**And the harm risk is real.** Norman et al. note in their 2019 review that "improving awareness and emotional release can be overwhelming for patients with psychiatric disorders." Body-scan practice in trauma populations has documented adverse-event rates (Lindahl et al. 2017, Britton et al. 2021 on meditation-related difficulties). For someone whose externalized processing already works — who can write, observe behavior, reason cognitively about their own patterns — the cost-benefit profile of pushing harder for body-felt access is not obviously favorable.

The honest synthesis: interoceptive capacity is partially trainable for some people some of the time, but (a) it isn't always desirable to build, (b) pushing it without state-regulation foundations can trigger dysregulation, and (c) for someone whose external access is functional, the case for retraining is genuinely unclear. **The "you'll feel it eventually with practice" reassurance is unsupported as a blanket prescription.**

## When Alexithymia Is a Contraindication vs. an Adaptation Challenge

Few sources frame alexithymia itself as an absolute contraindication for IFS. The published clinical concerns are about specific failure modes.

**Severe alexithymia + complex trauma** combined with insistent body-focused prompts can produce flooding or dissociation. The interoceptive entry-point pushes attention into a dysregulated channel before the system is regulated, and the result is not therapeutic processing but intensification of trauma signal. Winhall's polyvagal-first pacing addresses this directly; standard IFS materials address it less explicitly.

**The "performed parts work" risk.** A client who has learned what answer the protocol expects can produce the right shapes — locating a part "in my chest," feeling "curious" — without the internal access that would give the protocol its therapeutic action. The therapy becomes a script. Several of the autistic IFS clinicians flag this risk explicitly. It's particularly likely in autistic clients who have spent decades masking — performing expected emotional responses without their own internal access being engaged. The session looks like IFS. The internal work is not happening.

**The push to "feel harder."** When a therapist persistently returns to body-noticing prompts despite the client's accurate report that nothing is accessible there, the client's available channels — cognitive, written, externalized, behavioral observation — are never developed. The therapist has not adapted the modality to the client; the client must adapt to the modality. For low-interoception clients this can mean years of therapy that never reaches the deeper protective parts at all, because the entry door is always being held in a place the client can't open.

**What this means for the contraindication question.** The contraindication, when it exists, is not about alexithymia per se — it is about **mismatch and persistence in mismatch.** With structural adaptation (substitute access channels, direct access from the outset, externalized parts work, polyvagal-informed pacing), IFS appears to work. Without structural adaptation, the protocol can be actively counterproductive. This is the working consensus across the clinician writing reviewed here, and it matches what the empirical literature on body-scan harms predicts.

## Practical Phrasings — Substitutions for Interoception-Heavy Prompts

If you are bringing this page to a therapist, the operative content is here. These substitutions are drawn from the IFS Telehealth Collective, IFS with Sanni, Bergenfield's framing, and the externalization literature. The principle is **swap the access modality without swapping the underlying move.** Find, Focus, Flesh out, Feel toward, beFriend, and Fears are still happening — they are just being routed through writing, drawing, behavioral observation, or cognitive recognition rather than felt sense.

| Standard prompt | Adapted prompt |
|------|------|
| "Where do you notice this part in your body?" | "How would you draw this part? What shape, color, or image fits? Or — if it had a voice in your mind, what would it sound like?" |
| "How do you feel toward this part?" | "If you wrote this part a letter, what would the tone be? Or — describe your stance toward it logically. Protective? Suspicious? Friendly?" |
| "What's it like to be with this part?" | "What does this part do in your behavior? What patterns do you notice when this part is active? What do you do when it's running things?" |
| "Take a breath and notice what comes up." | "Take a moment and write down what's most present. Or — describe what you would be observing if you were watching yourself from outside." |
| "Let's invite the part to share more." | "Let's set up a written dialogue. You write its lines, then write back to it." |
| "Can you find Self?" | "Can you find a stance that feels grounded — calm, curious, not flooded? You don't have to feel anything specific; you can recognize it cognitively." |

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-4 -->

A few tactical notes that may be useful:

- **The state check comes first.** If you are flooded or shut down, no channel will work. The first move is regulating state. Polyvagal-informed pacing — Winhall's frame, but also any therapist trained in trauma-informed somatic work — is the entry point, not the bypass.
- **Direct access can be the default.** Frank Anderson's *Transcending Trauma* (2021) describes direct access as a routine clinical practice in IFS. It is canonical protocol. Asking your therapist to use direct access from the outset is not a workaround; it is a sanctioned move.
- **Behavioral observation is data.** What you DO when a part is active is information about that part's stance and your stance toward it, even when introspection produces nothing. "When this part runs, I avoid" is real. "When this part runs, I push back hard" is real. Use the observable pattern as the answer to "feel toward."
- **Writing-led parts work has its own page.** Cat 10 treats journaling-led IFS in depth; this page only flags it as a primary substitute. If externalized written dialogue is your strongest channel, that page is the next stop.
- **Aphantasia compounds without overlapping.** If you have low interoception *and* aphantasia (no mental imagery), the visual substitute is also unavailable. Cognitive and externalized channels are the remaining viable routes. Cat 7 treats aphantasia in depth.

## Where the Literature Lands

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-2 -->

Three positions on whether IFS fits alexithymic clients are worth distinguishing carefully.

**Position (a): poor fit.** The claim would be that IFS depends on interoception in ways that can't be substituted, and alexithymic clients should be referred to other modalities — CBT, narrative therapy, ACT, more cognitive approaches. The literature support for this position is essentially absent. No IFS clinician or critic publishes a recommendation against IFS for alexithymic clients specifically, and the protocol's existing accommodation (direct access) makes the strong-form claim hard to defend. Position (a) is not where the literature lands.

**Position (b): slow fit / training modality.** The claim is that IFS can build interoceptive access over time — that with sustained practice, alexithymia recedes and the standard protocol becomes accessible. This position has partial empirical support: Norman et al.'s mindfulness-on-alexithymia meta-analysis, Khalsa's 2025 meta-analysis on interoception, multiple body-scan trials. But the picture is complicated by Aaron 2020's quadratic finding (more interoception is not always better), the harm-risk literature (mindfulness-related difficulties in trauma populations), and the fact that the strong intervention in Aaron's pilot did not outperform control. Position (b) overpromises as a blanket prescription.

**Position (c): structural modification.** The claim is that the standard protocol's interoceptive entry doesn't fit, and the right move is to substitute access channels — cognitive, visual, externalized — and use direct access as the default rather than the fallback. The "feel into your body" requirement is removed explicitly. This is what the autistic-IFS-clinician community publishes (IFS Telehealth Collective, Bergenfield, IFS with Sanni, Emma at IFS & Neurodiversity). The evidence base is clinician-experiential rather than peer-reviewed RCT, and that distinction matters — but as practice guidance from people who have run the experiment in session, it represents the working consensus.

**The honest synthesis.** Position (c) with cautious openness to (b) where capacity emerges naturally — never imposed. The "you'll feel it eventually" reassurance is unsupported. The "this isn't for you" dismissal is also unsupported. What the literature actually supports is: substitute the interoceptive channels, run the protocol through whatever channels are available, regulate state before pushing inward attention, and let interoceptive capacity grow if it's going to grow without making it the goal of the work. That's the answer.

> **Evidence note**: This synthesis is interpretation, not a peer-reviewed conclusion. The peer-reviewed literature on IFS-with-alexithymia specifically does not exist; the synthesis here draws from clinician practice writing, the parent Focusing tradition, the empirical interoception literature, and the body-scan harms literature. A reasonable therapist who reviews this page should be able to engage with it, push back where they disagree, and adapt their approach with you. That is the page's job.

## Common Misconceptions

### Misconception: "Interoception can be trained, so alexithymic clients will catch up to standard protocol over time."

**Reality**: For some, slowly. For others, the felt-sense channel is structurally inaccessible — or unsafe to access for trauma reasons. The body-scan harms literature is real. Aaron et al. 2020's quadratic finding undermines the simple linear "more is better" model. A blanket "you'll get there with practice" is unsupported.

### Misconception: "Direct access and externalized parts work are inferior workarounds."

**Reality**: They are sanctioned IFS protocol moves. The autistic-IFS-clinician literature describes them as primary modes, not concessions. Frank Anderson's *Transcending Trauma* describes direct access as a routine clinical practice. The protocol explicitly permits these channels.

### Misconception: "Alexithymia is an autism-only issue."

**Reality**: About half of autistic adults are alexithymic; the other half have roughly typical interoception. Non-autistic populations — PTSD, eating disorders, somatic-symptom presentations, around 5% of neurotypicals — also show alexithymia. The Shah et al. 2016 finding establishes that alexithymia, not autism, drives the interoception disruption.

### Misconception: "If you don't feel the part, you're not doing IFS."

**Reality**: IFS protocol explicitly accommodates non-felt-sense access through direct access. Cognitive recognition of parts (as belief-clusters, voices in mind, behavior patterns) is documented across the autistic-IFS-clinician writing as a valid access route. The 6 Fs run regardless of which channel carries them.

> **Correction**: Earlier exploration drafts cited "Brewer et al." for the autism-vs-alexithymia interoception finding. The correct citation is Shah, Hall, Catmur, Bird (2016) in *Cortex*. The Brewer/Bird group did publish a related paper in 2016 ("Alexithymia: a general deficit of interoception" in *Royal Society Open Science*, by Brewer, Cook, Bird) — both papers are real but they are different papers with different first authors.

## Summary

### Key Points

1. **Alexithymia disrupts interoceptive accuracy specifically.** The TAS-20 has three subscales (DIF, DDF, EOT). Around half of autistic adults score above clinical cutoff. The Shah/Bird 2016 finding establishes that the interoception impairment tracks alexithymia, not autism per se.
2. **Three of the six Fs are interoception-heavy** (Find, Focus, Feel toward). Three are not (Flesh out has a separate visual bottleneck; beFriend and Fears run on dialogue and reasoning). The protocol has a built-in bottleneck zone and a built-in escape route.
3. **The IFS protocol already permits substitute access channels** — cognitive, visual, externalized, direct access — but typically frames them as workarounds. For low-interoception clients they are the primary modes.
4. **The harm risk of pushing into the body is real.** Body-scan practice in dysregulated populations has documented adverse-event rates. Polyvagal-informed pacing (Winhall) makes the order of operations explicit: regulate state first, then approach felt sense if and when it becomes available.
5. **The literature lands on structural modification** (Position c) with cautious openness to capacity-building (Position b) where it emerges naturally — never imposed. Position (a) "poor fit" is unsupported.
