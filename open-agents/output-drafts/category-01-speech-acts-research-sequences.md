# 1. Speech Act Sequences: How Speech Acts Chain Together

Speech acts rarely occur in isolation. They form **adjacency pairs** -- two-part exchanges where the first utterance (First Pair Part, or FPP) creates a conditional expectation for a specific type of response (Second Pair Part, or SPP). But real conversation goes well beyond simple pairs. Speech acts chain into predictable **sequences** with multiple decision points, expansions, and resolution phases.

## Core Principles of Speech Act Sequencing

### Conditional Relevance
When someone produces the first part of a pair (e.g., asks a question), the second part becomes **conditionally relevant** -- it is expected. If it doesn't come, its absence is noticeable and meaningful. Silence after a question is not neutral; it communicates something (usually reluctance, confusion, or refusal).

### Preferred vs. Dispreferred Responses
Every first pair part has a **preferred** response (structurally simpler, delivered quickly) and a **dispreferred** response (structurally complex, delivered with delay and mitigation). This is not about what the speaker wants to hear -- it is a structural property of conversation.

**Preferred responses are characterized by:**
- Minimal gap before responding (often less than 200ms)
- Immediate, direct delivery
- Simple declarative form
- No hedging or preface markers
- Short length

**Dispreferred responses are characterized by:**
- Noticeable delay or silence before responding (median ~561ms, significantly longer than preferred)
- Preface markers: "well...", "uh...", "um..."
- In-breath before speaking
- Hedging language: "I'm not sure, but..."
- Mitigating elements: appreciation ("That's really nice of you, but..."), apology ("I'm sorry, but...")
- An account or explanation for why the dispreferred response is being given
- Greater overall length and complexity
- Often includes partial agreement before the disagreement: "Yeah, but..."

**Critical pattern for recognition:** If you hear silence, an in-breath, "well...", or "uh..." before someone responds, chances are the response will be dispreferred (a refusal, disagreement, or decline). This is one of the most reliable signals in English conversation.

### The Account Obligation
When giving a dispreferred response, speakers feel socially obligated to provide an **account** -- a reason, excuse, or explanation. Giving a dispreferred response without an account is perceived as rude, hostile, or socially incompetent.

- Preferred: "Want to come to dinner?" --> "Sure!" (no account needed)
- Dispreferred: "Want to come to dinner?" --> "Oh... I'd love to, but I have a thing tonight. My sister is visiting and we already made plans. I'm really sorry. Maybe next week?" (delay + appreciation + account + apology + alternative)

## Sequence 1: Request --> Grant/Refuse --> (Account) --> Acknowledgment

### The Complete Request Sequence Structure

Requests are among the most studied speech acts. A full request sequence has multiple components:

#### Pre-Request Phase (Optional but Common)
- **Alerter/Attention-getter:** "Excuse me," "Hey," "Um..."
- **Preparator:** Checks availability or readiness. "Are you busy right now?" / "Do you have a minute?"
- **Grounder (pre-positioned):** Provides the reason before the request itself. "I'm running late and I left my phone at home..."

#### Head Act (The Actual Request)
The request itself can be realized at different levels of directness:

1. **Direct strategies** (most direct, lowest politeness for strangers):
   - Imperative: "Give me a pen."
   - Performative: "I'm asking you to help me move."
   - Want statement: "I want you to proofread this."

2. **Conventionally indirect strategies** (most common and most polite in American English):
   - Ability question: "Could you help me move this weekend?"
   - Willingness question: "Would you mind proofreading this?"
   - Permission question: "Can I borrow your charger?"
   - Suggestory formula: "How about lending me your car?"

3. **Non-conventionally indirect / Hints** (most indirect):
   - Strong hint: "I still haven't found anyone to help me move..." (implies request)
   - Mild hint: "Wow, moving is really hard to do alone." (very indirect)

#### Post-Request Phase
- **Supportive moves (post-positioned):** Grounder after the fact, promise of reward, minimizer ("It'll only take a second"), apology for asking

#### The Response: Grant (Preferred) or Refuse (Dispreferred)

**Grant (Preferred):**
```
A: Could you pass me the salt?
B: Sure, here you go.
A: Thanks.
```
- Delivered quickly, simply, directly
- May include compliance marker: "Sure," "Of course," "No problem"
- Followed by the actual compliance (doing the thing)

**Refuse (Dispreferred):**
```
A: Could you help me move this Saturday?
B: Oh... [delay] I would, but [partial agreement] I actually have my
   daughter's soccer tournament all day. [account/reason] I'm really
   sorry. [apology] Maybe I could help Sunday morning though? [alternative]
A: That's okay, I understand. Sunday might work actually.
```

**Full Refusal Structure (typical components in order):**
1. Delay/hesitation: silence, "um," "uh"
2. Preface: "Well..." or partial agreement "I'd love to, but..."
3. Expression of regret: "I'm sorry," "I wish I could"
4. Excuse/reason/explanation: "I have a prior commitment" (the account)
5. Alternative offer: "But maybe I could..." or "How about..."
6. Promise of future compliance: "Next time for sure"

#### Acknowledgment Phase
After the grant or refusal:
- **After grant:** "Thanks!" / "Thank you so much!" / "I really appreciate it." (gratitude)
- **After refusal with good account:** "No worries, I understand." / "That's okay." (acceptance of account)
- **After refusal, pushing back:** "Are you sure? It would really help." (re-request, potentially starting an insistence cycle)

#### The Complete Sequence Map:

```
PRE-REQUEST: "Are you busy?" -----> "Not really, what's up?" (go-ahead)
                                 \-> "Kind of, why?" (hedge - proceed with caution)
                                 \-> "Yeah, super busy" (block - may abandon request)
         |
    [go-ahead]
         |
REQUEST: "Could you help me move Saturday?"
         |
    +---------+-----------+
    |                     |
  GRANT                 REFUSE
  "Sure!"              "Oh... I can't, I have..."
    |                     |
    |              +------+-------+
    |              |              |
    |        Good account    Weak/no account
    |        (legitimate)    (feels rude)
    |              |              |
    |         Accept it     Push back?
    |         "No problem"  "Are you sure?"
    |              |              |

## Sequence 2: Offer --> Accept/Decline --> (Insistence Cycle) --> Resolution

### The Complete Offer Sequence

Offers differ from requests because the person initiating (the offerer) is proposing to do something *for* the other person. This creates a different politeness dynamic.

#### The Offer
```
"Can I get you something to drink?"
"Would you like me to drive you?"
"Let me help you with those bags."
"I'd be happy to pick that up for you."
```

#### Accept (Preferred)
```
A: Can I get you a coffee?
B: That would be great, thanks!
A: How do you take it?
B: Just black, please.
```
- Simple, direct, appreciative
- Often includes specification: "Yes please, with milk"

#### Decline (Dispreferred)
```
A: Can I get you a coffee?
B: Oh, no, I'm fine, thanks. [appreciation + decline + reassurance]
A: Are you sure? It's no trouble. [insistence/re-offer]
B: Really, I'm okay. I just had one. [reinforced decline + account]
A: Okay, well let me know if you change your mind. [acceptance + open door]
```

#### The Insistence Cycle (Critical Cultural Pattern)

In American English, there is a **mild insistence cycle** that is considered polite:

**Round 1:** Offer --> Decline
**Round 2:** Re-offer/Insist ("Are you sure?" / "It's no trouble") --> Decline again or Accept
**Round 3 (rare in American English):** Final insistence --> Final answer

**The American English norm:** One round of insistence is polite and expected. The offerer shows sincerity by insisting once. The decliner can either accept on the re-offer (this is socially comfortable -- "Well, if you're sure it's no trouble...") or decline again firmly, at which point the offerer should accept the decline.

**Key rule:** In American English, more than one round of re-offering after a firm decline can feel pushy or uncomfortable. The decliner saying "No, really, I'm fine" with firm intonation is a signal to stop insisting.

#### Cross-Cultural Note: The Taarof Pattern

In some cultures, particularly Iranian/Persian culture, the insistence cycle is much more elaborate and ritualized. This is called **taarof**:

- A host must offer anything a guest might want
- The guest is obligated to refuse initially
- This may repeat **three times** before the "real" answer emerges
- Declining a gift or offer typically requires saying "no" two or three times before accepting
- A shopkeeper may initially refuse to quote a price, saying the item is "unworthy" of the shopper
- The customer must insist on paying, typically three times, before real negotiation begins

This pattern also appears in Ethiopian, Middle Eastern, and some East Asian cultures. **The key point:** what counts as a "real" decline vs. a "polite" decline varies dramatically across cultures, and the number of expected insistence rounds is culture-specific.

## Sequence 3: Complaint --> Apology/Justification/Denial --> Resolution

### The Complete Complaint Sequence

Complaints are face-threatening acts -- they damage the positive face of the hearer by expressing criticism or dissatisfaction with their behavior. This makes complaint sequences particularly complex and socially loaded.

#### Severity Levels of Complaints (Olshtain & Weinbach's Five Strategies)

Complaints can be expressed at escalating levels of severity:

1. **Below the level of reproach:** General remarks expressing annoyance without holding the person responsible. "Traffic was terrible today, and I ended up waiting forever." (Implies: you were late and it affected me, but I'm not blaming you directly.)

2. **Expression of annoyance/disapproval:** Explicitly expressing negative feelings without directly stating a complaint. "I'm really frustrated right now." / "This isn't what I expected."

3. **Explicit complaint:** Directly stating the complaint and holding the person responsible. "You said you'd be here at 3, and it's now 4:30." / "This report has several errors in it."

4. **Accusation:** Direct attribution of blame. "You forgot to pick up the groceries again." / "You didn't follow the instructions I gave you."

5. **Warning or threat:** Indicating consequences. "If this happens again, I'll have to talk to your manager." / "I'm not going to keep covering for you."

#### Recipient's Response Options

After receiving a complaint, the recipient has several response paths:

**Apology (Preferred for maintaining relationship):**
The apology speech act set (Cohen & Olshtain, 1981) consists of up to five components:

1. **IFID (Illocutionary Force Indicating Device):** The explicit apology. "I'm sorry." / "I apologize." / "Forgive me."
2. **Expression of responsibility:** Acknowledging fault. "That was my mistake." / "I should have been more careful." / "You're right, I dropped the ball."
3. **Explanation/Account:** Describing what happened. "I got stuck in a meeting that ran over." / "I misunderstood the deadline."
4. **Offer of repair:** Proposing to fix it. "Let me redo this by tomorrow." / "I'll make it up to you."
5. **Promise of forbearance:** Committing to not repeat it. "It won't happen again." / "I'll set a reminder next time."

A full apology might include all five: "I'm so sorry [IFID]. I completely forgot [responsibility]. My week has been insane and it slipped my mind [account]. Let me run out and get them right now [repair]. I'll put it in my calendar so this doesn't happen again [forbearance]."

A minimal apology might include only one or two: "Sorry, my bad." (IFID + minimal responsibility acknowledgment)

**Justification (Defending without full apology):**
```
A: You're an hour late.
B: I know, but traffic was absolutely impossible. There was a major
   accident on the highway. I left at the usual time.
```
- Acknowledges the factual complaint but provides mitigating circumstances
- Implies: it happened but it wasn't really my fault

**Denial:**
```
A: You said you'd email me the file yesterday.
B: I did send it. Check your spam folder.
```
- Rejects the premise of the complaint
- Can escalate the sequence if the complainer doesn't accept the denial

**Counter-complaint:**
```
A: You never help with the dishes.
B: Well, you never take out the trash.
```
- Deflects by raising a reciprocal complaint
- Often escalatory

#### Resolution Patterns

If the complaint receives an apology with a good account, the typical resolution is:
```
A: You forgot to pick me up from the airport.
B: Oh no, I'm so sorry. I had your flight time wrong in my calendar.
   I feel terrible. Can I come get you now?
A: It's fine, I already got an Uber. Just... set a reminder next time.
B: Absolutely. I'm really sorry.
A: Don't worry about it.
```

If the complaint does NOT receive adequate acknowledgment, the complainant tends to **extend the sequence** -- restating, escalating, providing more evidence, or seeking stronger displays of acknowledgment. This can create a cycle:

```
COMPLAINT --> Inadequate response --> ESCALATED COMPLAINT --> Better response --> RESOLUTION
     or
COMPLAINT --> Inadequate response --> ESCALATED COMPLAINT --> Still inadequate --> FURTHER ESCALATION
```

```

## Sequence 4: Invitation --> Accept/Decline --> (Negotiation) --> Confirmation

### The Complete Invitation Sequence

Invitations in American English are notably complex because they are often **negotiated** rather than delivered as simple yes/no propositions. Research shows that unambiguous invitations (with specific time, place, and activity stated upfront) are actually the *least* common form. More typically, invitations emerge through a collaborative process.

#### Pre-Invitation Phase

Pre-invitations are extremely common and serve a critical function: they allow the inviter to test the waters without committing to an invitation that might be declined.

```
A: What are you doing Saturday? [pre-invitation: testing availability]
B: Nothing much, why? [go-ahead: signals availability]
A: Want to come over for a barbecue? [actual invitation]
```

vs.

```
A: What are you doing Saturday? [pre-invitation]
B: Oh, I have my in-laws visiting. [block: signals unavailability]
A: Oh, that sounds nice! [abandons invitation, saves face for both parties]
```

**Three possible responses to a pre-invitation:**
1. **Go-ahead:** "Nothing, why?" / "I'm free!" --> Inviter proceeds to actual invitation
2. **Hedge:** "Depends, what's up?" / "I might have something, why?" --> Inviter may proceed cautiously
3. **Block:** "I'm busy all weekend" / "I have plans" --> Inviter can abandon without either party losing face

#### The Invitation Itself

**Unambiguous invitation** (less common but clearest):
"We're having a barbecue Saturday at our place at 4pm. Would you like to come?"
- Specifies: activity, time, place, and requests response

**Ambiguous invitation** (more common, emerges through negotiation):
"We should get together sometime." --> "Yeah, totally!" --> "How about this weekend?" --> "Saturday works" --> "Want to come to ours? We could grill out." --> "Sounds great, what time?"

#### Accept (Preferred)
```
A: Want to come to our barbecue Saturday around 4?
B: I'd love to! Can I bring anything?
A: Just yourself! Maybe some chips or something if you want.
B: Perfect, see you then!
```
- Quick, enthusiastic acceptance
- Often includes offer to contribute: "Can I bring anything?" / "What can I bring?"
- Confirmation of details: time, place, what to bring

#### Decline (Dispreferred)
```
A: Want to come to our barbecue Saturday around 4?
B: Oh... [delay] that sounds really fun, [appreciation/partial agreement]
   but we already have plans with my parents that day. [account]
   I'm bummed to miss it. [expression of regret]
A: No worries! We'll do it again soon. [acceptance + future promise]
B: Definitely, we'd love to come next time! [reciprocal future promise]
```

**Decline structure typically includes:**
1. Delay/hesitation
2. Appreciation of the invitation: "That sounds amazing"
3. Expression of regret: "I wish I could" / "I'm bummed"
4. Account/reason: "I already have plans" / "I'll be out of town"
5. Alternative or future promise: "Next time for sure" / "Rain check?"

#### Negotiation Phase

When the decline is partial or conditional, negotiation can occur:

```
A: Want to come Saturday at 4?
B: We can't do Saturday, but we're free Sunday if that works?
A: Sunday works! Same time?
B: Could we do a little later, like 5?
A: Sure, 5 it is.
```

#### The Insincere/Ostensible Invitation

An important pattern to recognize: not all invitations are genuine. **Ostensible invitations** are social rituals that both parties understand are not real commitments:

- "We should get together sometime!" (when running into an acquaintance)
- "You should come visit!" (said at the end of a trip without specific plans)
- "Let's do lunch!" (said as a farewell)

**How to recognize ostensible vs. genuine invitations:**
- Genuine: includes specific time, place, or follow-up ("Let me check my calendar and text you")
- Ostensible: vague, no specifics, no follow-up mechanism, often said during leave-taking

#### Confirmation Phase

After acceptance, the sequence typically closes with confirmation:
```
A: So Saturday at 4, our place?
B: Perfect, see you then!
A: Great, looking forward to it!
```

## Sequence 5: Compliment --> Acceptance/Deflection --> (Return Compliment)

### The Complete Compliment Sequence

Compliments in American English create a peculiar tension that Herbert (1989) identified: the recipient faces a conflict between two conversational principles -- (1) agree with the speaker (accept the compliment) and (2) avoid self-praise (don't appear vain). This tension produces a wide variety of response strategies.

#### The Compliment

American English compliments are remarkably formulaic. Research shows:
- Two-thirds of compliments use just 5 adjectives: nice, good, beautiful, pretty, great
- About 90% use just two verbs: like and love
- Common structures: "I love your [noun]!" / "That's a really nice [noun]" / "You look great!"

#### Herbert's Taxonomy of Compliment Response Strategies

**Agreement/Acceptance strategies:**

1. **Appreciation token:** Simple thanks.
   - "Thanks!" / "Thank you!"
   - The most common response in American English

2. **Comment acceptance:** Agreement plus elaboration.
   - A: "I love your jacket!" B: "Thanks, I just got it at that new store downtown!"

3. **Praise upgrade:** Agrees with and increases the praise.
   - A: "That was a really good presentation." B: "Yeah, I think it was one of my best, honestly."
   - (Rare in American English; can sound arrogant)

**Non-Agreement/Deflection strategies:**

4. **Scale down/Downgrade:** Reduces the complimentary force.
   - A: "Your garden looks amazing!" B: "Oh, it's nothing special. It mostly just grows itself."

5. **Reassignment:** Redirects credit away from self.
   - A: "Great job on the project!" B: "Oh, it was really a team effort. Sarah did most of the heavy lifting."

6. **Return compliment:** Deflects by complimenting the complimenter back.
   - A: "You look great today!" B: "Thanks, so do you!"
   - Very common in American English, especially between women

7. **Comment history:** Shifts focus to the background of the complimented object.
   - A: "I love that painting!" B: "Oh, I picked it up at a flea market years ago. It was only $5."

8. **Qualification/Hedge:** Partially accepts but adds a downplaying qualifier.
   - A: "You're such a good cook!" B: "Thanks, though I burned the rice. I can never get the rice right."

9. **Disagreement:** Directly rejects the compliment.
   - A: "You look great!" B: "Ugh, no, I look terrible today."
   - (Note: In American English, this can make the complimenter feel awkward)

10. **No acknowledgment:** Ignores the compliment entirely and changes topic.
    - A: "Nice shirt!" B: "Did you finish the report?"

#### The Typical Sequence:

The most common compliment exchange in American English is:
```
A: I love your shoes!
B: Oh, thanks! I got them on sale. [appreciation token + comment history]
   Yours are cute too! [return compliment]
A: Thanks! [appreciation token for return compliment]
```

#### Gender and Context Patterns
- Women compliment other women most frequently
- Compliments between men are less common and tend to focus on possessions or skills rather than appearance
- Workplace compliments focus on performance: "Great presentation" / "Nice work on that"
- Close friends may use more extreme language: "Oh my god, you look AMAZING"

# 2. Pre-Sequences: The Preliminary Moves

Pre-sequences are adjacency pairs that come *before* the main speech act. They are themselves structured as their own mini-adjacency pairs. Understanding pre-sequences is essential because they are the **early warning system** of conversation -- they signal what kind of speech act is coming, and they give both parties an opportunity to manage the interaction before committing.

## Why Pre-Sequences Exist

Pre-sequences serve several critical functions:

1. **Face protection:** They allow the recipient to signal unavailability *before* a request or invitation is made, so neither party has to deal with the face-threatening act of an explicit refusal. If someone asks "Are you busy Saturday?" and you say "Yeah, packed schedule," the other person can simply not issue the invitation they were planning. No one has to refuse anything, and no one has to be refused.

2. **Gauging likelihood of success:** The initiator can test whether their upcoming action will succeed before committing to it.

3. **Preparing the recipient:** They give the listener mental preparation for what's coming.

4. **Establishing joint attention:** They ensure both parties are ready for the main interaction.

## Pre-Requests

**Function:** Check whether the conditions for a successful request are met before making the request.

**Common forms:**
- "Are you busy right now?"
- "Do you have a minute?"
- "Can I ask you something?"
- "Do you know anything about computers?"
- "Are you using that pen?"

**How they work:**

```
A: Do you have a sec? [pre-request: checking availability]
B: Sure, what's up? [go-ahead: "yes, proceed"]
A: Could you help me carry these boxes? [request]
```

vs.

```
A: Do you have a sec? [pre-request]
B: Actually, I'm heading into a meeting. [block: "no, don't proceed"]
A: Oh, no worries, it can wait. [abandons request]
```

**Possible responses to pre-requests:**
1. **Go-ahead:** "Sure!" / "Of course" / "What do you need?" --> Requester proceeds
2. **Hedge:** "Depends... what is it?" --> Requester may proceed cautiously or preview the request
3. **Block:** "I'm really busy right now" / "I can't talk" --> Requester typically abandons
4. **Pre-emptive offer:** "Sure, do you need help with those boxes?" --> The recipient figures out what's coming and offers before being asked (saves face for both)

**The pre-emptive offer is worth special attention:** Sometimes the pre-sequence is so transparent that the recipient can guess the upcoming request and grant it preemptively:
```
A: Do you know anything about taxes? [pre-request, transparent]
B: Yeah, do you need help with yours? [pre-emptive offer -- skips the request]
A: That would be amazing, actually! [accepts the pre-emptive offer]
```

## Pre-Invitations

**Function:** Check the potential invitee's availability and interest before committing to an invitation.

**Common forms:**
- "What are you doing this weekend?"
- "Are you free Saturday night?"
- "Do you have plans for the holiday?"
- "Have you been to that new restaurant downtown?"
- "Do you like Thai food?"

**How they work:**

```
A: Are you free Saturday night? [pre-invitation: testing availability]
B: I think so, why? [go-ahead + "tell me more"]
A: We're having a dinner party, want to come? [invitation]
```

vs.

```
A: Are you free Saturday night? [pre-invitation]
B: No, we're going to my mom's. [block: providing unavailability]
A: Oh nice, have fun! [abandons invitation without ever having to be refused]
```

**The beauty of pre-invitations:** They allow BOTH parties to avoid the discomfort of an explicit refusal. The potential invitee who says "I'm busy Saturday" may genuinely be busy, or may be strategically blocking an unwanted invitation -- and crucially, this ambiguity is useful. It protects everyone's face.

**Important pattern -- the "monitor space":** After issuing a pre-invitation, the inviter should pay attention to the response timing and manner:
- Quick, upbeat "I'm free!" = strong go-ahead
- Hesitant "Ummm... I'm not sure..." = possible reluctance (consider not inviting)
- "Why do you ask?" = neutral, needs more information before committing

## Pre-Announcements

**Function:** Signal that news or noteworthy information is coming, and create a slot for the recipient to display readiness to hear it.

**Common forms:**
- "Guess what!"
- "You'll never guess what happened!"
- "I have some news."
- "You know what I just heard?"
- "I have to tell you something."
- "So you're not going to believe this..."

**How they work:**

```
A: Guess what! [pre-announcement]
B: What? [go-ahead: "tell me"]
A: I got the job! [announcement/news delivery]
B: Oh my god, congratulations! [response to news]
```

**Valence signaling:** Pre-announcements often signal whether the news is good or bad:
- **Good news signals:** "Guess what!" (excited tone), "I have great news!", "You're not gonna believe this!" (positive tone)
- **Bad news signals:** "I need to tell you something" (serious tone), "I have some bad news," "So... something happened" (hedged, slow delivery)
- **Ambiguous:** "I have news" (could be either)

**The recipient's role:** After a pre-announcement, the recipient is expected to produce a "go-ahead" -- "What?" / "Tell me!" / "What happened?" This creates a collaborative sequence where the news-teller has been explicitly invited to share, rather than just launching into it unilaterally.

## Pre-Closings

**Function:** Signal the desire to end a conversation without abruptly cutting it off, giving the other party a chance to raise any final topics.

**Common forms:**
- "Well..."
- "So..."
- "Anyway..."
- "Well, I should probably get going."
- "I should let you go."
- "I don't want to keep you."
- "Okay, well..."
- "Alright, so..."

**How they work (Schegloff & Sacks, 1973):**

A conversation cannot just stop. Both parties need to coordinate the ending. The pre-closing is the mechanism for this coordination. It works in two phases:

**Phase 1: Pre-closing**
One participant produces a "possible pre-closing" -- an utterance that occupies a turn without introducing a new topic or continuing the current one. This signals: "I have nothing more to say; do you?"

```
A: Well... [pre-closing signal]
B: Yeah, so... [accepts pre-closing: "I have nothing more either"]
```

If B *does* have something more, they can override the pre-closing:
```
A: Well... [pre-closing signal]
B: Oh, before you go, I forgot to mention... [overrides pre-closing, raises new topic]
A: Oh yeah? [conversation continues]
```

**Phase 2: Terminal exchange**
Once both parties have agreed they're done (via the pre-closing), they proceed to the terminal exchange -- the actual goodbye:

```
A: Well, I should get going. [pre-closing]
B: Yeah, me too. It was great seeing you. [accepts pre-closing + positive assessment]
A: You too! Talk soon. [reciprocal + future reference]
B: Bye! [terminal]
A: Bye! [terminal]
```

**Common pre-closing sequences in American English:**

For phone calls:
```
"Okay, well, I'll let you go."
"Alright, I should get back to it."
"Well, it was good catching up!"
"Okay, I'll talk to you later then."
```

For in-person conversations:
```
"Well, I should probably head out."
"Anyway, I'll let you get back to what you were doing."
"I should get going."
"I better run."
```

For email/text:
```
"Anyway, just wanted to let you know."
"That's all for now!"
"I'll let you know how it goes."
```

**The phrase "I should let you go" is notable:** It frames the closing as being for the *other* person's benefit ("I don't want to take up your time"), which is a face-saving device -- even if the speaker is the one who wants to end the conversation.

## Pre-Sequence Summary Table

| Pre-Sequence Type | Typical Form | Go-Ahead Response | Block Response | Function |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-request | "Are you busy?" | "No, what's up?" | "Yeah, really busy" | Check availability for upcoming request |
| Pre-invitation | "Are you free Saturday?" | "Yeah, why?" | "No, I have plans" | Test availability for invitation |
| Pre-announcement | "Guess what!" | "What?" | (rarely blocked) | Signal news is coming; secure listener attention |
| Pre-offer | "Are you hungry?" | "Actually, yes!" | "No, I just ate" | Check if an offer would be wanted |
| Pre-closing | "Well, anyway..." | "Yeah, so..." | "Oh wait, one more thing" | Signal desire to end conversation |
| Pre-telling | "Did you hear about...?" | "No, what?" | "Yeah, I already heard" | Check if recipient already knows the story |

# 3. Speech Acts in Specific Everyday Scenarios

Each everyday scenario has a recognizable **script** -- a sequence of speech acts that participants follow. Knowing these scripts reduces cognitive load and allows you to predict what comes next. Deviating from the script is noticed and can cause confusion.

## Scenario 1: Ordering at a Restaurant

### The Complete Sequence

**Phase 1: Greeting / Seating**
```
Host: "Hi, welcome to [restaurant name]. How many in your party?"
You: "Just two, please."
Host: "Right this way."
[Host seats you]
Host: "Your server will be right with you."
You: "Thanks."
```

**Phase 2: Server Introduction**
```
Server: "Hi, I'm [name], I'll be taking care of you tonight.
         Can I start you off with something to drink?"
You: "I'll have a water, please." / "Could I get a Coke?"
Companion: "Water for me too, please."
Server: "Sure thing. I'll give you a minute to look at the menu."
```

**Phase 3: Taking the Order**
```
Server: "Are you ready to order, or do you need a few more minutes?"
   --> If not ready: "Could we have a few more minutes?" / Server: "Of course, take your time."
   --> If ready: "I think we're ready."
Server: "Great, what can I get for you?"
You: "I'll have the chicken Caesar salad, please."
Server: "And for you?" [turns to companion]
Companion: "The burger, medium, please."
Server: [May ask follow-up questions] "What kind of dressing?" / "Fries or salad with that?"
[You answer follow-ups]
Server: "Great, I'll get that right in for you."
You: "Thanks."
```

**Phase 4: Check-ins During Meal**
```
Server: "How's everything tasting?" / "Is everything okay here?"
You: "Great, thanks!" [standard phatic response -- even if it's merely adequate]
   --> If there's a problem: "Actually, could I get some more water?"
     / "This is a little cold, would you mind..."
```

**Phase 5: Offering Dessert / Wrapping Up**
```
Server: "Can I get you anything else? Dessert, coffee?"
You: "No, I think we're good. Could we get the check?"
Server: "Sure, I'll bring that right over."
```

**Phase 6: Payment**
```
Server: [brings check] "Here you go. No rush at all."
[You review, place card or cash in folder]
Server: [processes payment] "Here's your receipt. Thank you so much!"
You: "Thank you! Everything was great."
Server: "Thanks for coming in, have a great night!"
You: "You too!"
```

**Key script notes:**
- "I'll have..." is the standard ordering formula (not "Give me..." which sounds demanding, or "I want..." which sounds childish)
- "Please" is expected with every order
- The standard response to "How's everything?" is positive even if the food is just okay
- "No rush" from the server is a formulaic politeness marker
- Tipping is a non-verbal speech act of evaluation/gratitude in American culture

## Scenario 2: Making a Phone Call to a Business

### The Complete Sequence

**Phase 1: Opening**
```
Business: "Thank you for calling [business name], this is [name]. How can I help you?"
You: "Hi, I'm calling about [reason]."
```
OR with automated system:
```
Recording: "Thank you for calling [business]. Press 1 for..."
[Navigate menu]
Representative: "Thank you for calling [business], how can I help you?"
You: "Hi, yes, I'm calling because..."
```

**Phase 2: Stating Your Purpose**
```
You: "I'm calling to ask about [topic]." / "I need to [schedule/cancel/inquire about]..."
Rep: "Sure, I can help you with that. Can I get your [name/account number/phone number]?"
You: [Provide information]
```

**Phase 3: Transaction**
```
Rep: "Okay, I'm pulling up your account... [processing]... 
     So it looks like [information/options]."
You: "Great, I'd like to [choice]."
Rep: "Okay, I've got that set up for you. Is there anything else I can help with?"

You: "No, that's everything. Thank you!"

Rep: "Bye."
```

**Key script notes:**
- State your purpose clearly and early: "I'm calling about..."

- "No, that's all" is the go-ahead to close
- Both parties say bye; hanging up without a goodbye feels abrupt

## Scenario 3: Asking for Help from a Stranger

### The Complete Sequence

This is a heavily face-threatening interaction because you are imposing on someone with whom you have no relationship. Maximum politeness strategies are used.

**Phase 1: Attention + Pre-Request**
```
You: "Excuse me..." [alerter/attention-getter -- wait for them to make eye contact]
Stranger: [looks up, makes eye contact] [non-verbal go-ahead]
You: "Sorry to bother you, but..." [apology for imposing]
```

**Phase 2: Request (Highly Mitigated)**
```
You: "Do you happen to know where the nearest subway station is?"
     "Would you mind watching my bag for just one second?"
     "Could I possibly borrow your phone? Mine just died."
```

**Key mitigation strategies used with strangers:**
- "Sorry to bother you" (apology for the imposition)
- "Do you happen to..." (softens the assumption that they can help)
- "Would you mind..." (frames it as their choice)
- "just" / "really quickly" / "for one second" (minimizes the imposition)
- "Could I possibly..." (maximally indirect)
- Rising intonation (makes it sound more tentative)

**Phase 3: Response**
```
Grant: "Sure! It's two blocks that way, on the left."
You: "Thank you so much, I really appreciate it." [emphatic thanks -- higher level than with acquaintances]

Decline: "Sorry, I'm not from around here."
You: "No problem, thanks anyway!" [immediate acceptance, no pushing]
```

**Phase 4: Closing**
```
You: "Thanks again!" / "Have a good day!"
Stranger: "No problem!" / "You too!"
```

**Critical rules:**
- Always begin with "Excuse me" -- approaching a stranger without an alerter is startling
- The apology ("Sorry to bother you") is not a real apology; it is a ritualistic acknowledgment of the imposition
- With strangers, accept a refusal immediately. Do not insist or push back.
- Thank emphatically. Strangers have no obligation to help you, so gratitude should be proportionally greater.
- Do not use bare imperatives with strangers ("Tell me where the subway is") -- this is perceived as rude or aggressive

## Scenario 4: Job Interview Greeting and Small Talk

### The Complete Sequence

**Phase 1: Arrival and Reception**
```
Receptionist: "Hi, can I help you?"
You: "Hi, I'm [full name]. I have a [time] interview with [interviewer name]."
Receptionist: "Great, have a seat and I'll let them know you're here."
You: "Thank you."
```

**Phase 2: Interviewer Greeting**
```
Interviewer: [approaches] "Hi, you must be [name]. I'm [name], nice to meet you."
             [extends hand for handshake]
You: [stands up] "Hi [name], great to meet you. Thank you for having me."
     [firm handshake]
Interviewer: "Thanks for coming in. How was the drive / Did you find us okay?"
You: "Great, no trouble at all!" / "Yes, your directions were really helpful, thanks."
```

**Phase 3: Transitional Small Talk (Walking to Office)**
```
Interviewer: "Would you like some water or coffee?"
You: "Water would be great, thank you." / "I'm fine, but thank you."
[Brief small talk about weather, the office space, etc.]
Interviewer: "So, did you have any trouble finding parking?"
You: "No, it was easy. You have a really nice office here."
```

**Phase 4: Transition to Interview**
```
Interviewer: "Well, let's go ahead and get started. [transition marker]
             Thanks again for coming in. So tell me a little about yourself..."
```

**Key notes:**
- Use full name when introducing yourself
- Match the interviewer's level of formality
- Small talk topics should be safe: weather, commute, the building, local area
- "Thank you for having me" / "Thanks for the opportunity" signals professionalism
- Stand up when the interviewer approaches
- Mirror the interviewer's energy level
- The transition from small talk to interview is usually signaled by the interviewer with "So..." or "Well, let's get started"

## Scenario 5: Running into an Acquaintance Unexpectedly

### The Complete Sequence

This scenario varies dramatically based on relationship closeness.

**Close friend:**
```
A: "Oh my god, hey!! What are you doing here?!" [high energy, surprise]
B: "Hey!! I can't believe it, it's been forever!" [reciprocal high energy]
A: "I know, I haven't seen you in ages! How are you?"
B: "Good! Really good. How about you?" [genuine "how are you"]
A: "Great! What have you been up to?" [opens extended catch-up]
[May lead to extended conversation or plans]
A: "We should get together soon! Let me text you."
B: "Yes, definitely! I'll text you this week."
A: "So good to see you!" [pre-closing]
B: "You too! Talk soon!" [closing]
A: "Bye!" [terminal]
```

**Acquaintance (someone you know but aren't close to):**
```
A: "Oh, hey! [Name], right?" [recognition + possible name confirmation]
B: "Hey! Yeah, how are you?" [greeting + phatic "how are you"]
A: "Good, good. How about you?" [phatic response + reciprocal]
B: "Can't complain! What brings you here?" [phatic + context question]
A: "Just grabbing some groceries. You?"
B: "Same, same."
[Brief pause -- both sensing the conversation is complete]
A: "Well, good to see you!" [pre-closing]
B: "You too! Take care." [closing]
A: "Bye!" [terminal]
```

**Person you barely know (awkward encounter):**
```
A: "Oh, hey." [low-key greeting]
B: "Hey, how's it going?" [minimal phatic]
A: "Good, you?" [minimal phatic return]
B: "Good."
[Possible brief pause]
A: "Well, see you around!" [rapid pre-closing]
B: "Yeah, take care!" [closing]
```

**Key patterns:**
- The closer the relationship, the more extended the greeting sequence
- "How are you?" with acquaintances is phatic -- expected response is "Good!" not a detailed report
- "We should get together!" with acquaintances is often an ostensible (non-genuine) plan -- both parties recognize it
- The pre-closing "Good to see you!" is the polite escape mechanism
- If you cannot remember someone's name, you can try: "Hey! Good to see you!" (no name) or "I'm sorry, remind me of your name?" (honest but slightly awkward)
- These encounters are typically brief: 30 seconds to 2 minutes for acquaintances

## Scenario 6: Email Requests -- Colleague vs. Boss

### Email to a Colleague (Equal Status, Lower Social Distance)

```
Subject: Quick question about the Henderson report

Hey Sarah,

Hope your Monday's going well!

Quick question -- do you have the latest numbers for the Henderson account?
I need them for the presentation tomorrow. If you could send them over
today that would be great.

Thanks!
Alex
```

**Structure:**
1. Informal greeting: "Hey [name],"
2. Phatic opener (optional): "Hope you're doing well"
3. Pre-request/framing: "Quick question"
4. Request (conventionally indirect): "do you have..." / "could you..."
5. Grounder: reason for the request
6. Deadline/softened: "if you could... that would be great"
7. Thanks in advance
8. Casual sign-off

### Email to a Boss (Higher Status, Greater Power Distance)

```
Subject: Request for Henderson report data

Hi Dr. Martinez,

I hope this message finds you well.

I'm preparing the slides for tomorrow's client presentation and wanted
to check whether the latest Henderson account figures have been finalized.
If so, would it be possible to get a copy? I want to make sure I'm using
the most current numbers.

I appreciate your time, and I'm happy to work with whatever version
is available if the final numbers aren't ready yet.

Best regards,
Alex
```

**Structure:**
1. Formal greeting: "Hi Dr./Mr./Ms. [name],"
2. Polite opener: "I hope this message finds you well"
3. Contextual grounder: why you need this (justifies the request before making it)
4. Highly indirect request: "would it be possible to..."
5. Additional justification/face-saving
6. Offer of flexibility: reduces the imposition ("whatever version is available")
7. Gratitude: "I appreciate your time"
8. Formal sign-off: "Best regards,"

### Key Differences:

| Feature | To Colleague | To Boss |
|---|---|---|
| Greeting | "Hey Sarah," | "Hi Dr. Martinez," |
| Opener | "Hope Monday's going well!" | "I hope this message finds you well." |
| Request form | "Do you have..." | "Would it be possible to..." |
| Justification | Brief | Detailed |
| Flexibility shown | Minimal | Explicit |
| Sign-off | "Thanks!" | "Best regards," |
| Length | Shorter | Longer |
| Indirectness level | Moderate | High |

**Research finding:** In workplace emails, direct strategies are actually used 71% of the time, but indirectness increases significantly when writing to someone of higher status. The key variable is **power distance** -- the greater the power difference, the more indirect and elaborated the request.

## Scenario 7: Returning an Item to a Store

### The Complete Sequence

**Phase 1: Approach and Purpose Statement**
```
You: "Hi, I'd like to return this." [direct statement of purpose -- acceptable in this institutional context]
Clerk: "Sure, do you have your receipt?"
You: "Yes, here it is."
```

**Phase 2: Information Exchange**
```
Clerk: "And what was the reason for the return?"
You: "It doesn't fit." / "It stopped working." / "I changed my mind."
Clerk: "Okay, no problem. Would you like an exchange or a refund?"
You: "A refund, please."
```

**Phase 3: Processing**
```
Clerk: "Okay, I'll process that for you. It'll go back on the card you used."
You: "Great, thanks."
[Clerk processes return]
Clerk: "All set. You should see the refund in 3-5 business days."
You: "Perfect, thank you."
```

**Phase 4: Closing**
```
Clerk: "Is there anything else I can help you with?"
You: "No, that's it. Thanks!"
Clerk: "Have a great day!"
You: "You too!"
```

**If there's a problem (item isn't eligible, no receipt):**
```
Clerk: "I'm sorry, but this item is past the return window." / "Without a receipt,
        I can only offer store credit."
You: [Polite complaint approach] "Oh, I see. Is there any way to make an exception?
      I only bought it two weeks ago."
Clerk: "Let me check with my manager." / "Unfortunately, that's our policy."
You: [Escalation option] "Could I speak with a manager, please?" [request -- not demand]
     [Acceptance option] "Okay, store credit is fine then."
```

**Key notes:**
- Stating your purpose directly is appropriate in transactional contexts
- "I'd like to return this" is standard -- not "I want my money back" (too aggressive) or "Would it be possible to..." (too indirect for this context)
- Keep the reason brief -- you don't owe an elaborate explanation
- If denied, you can escalate politely: "Could I speak with a manager?" (conventionally indirect request)
- Maintaining a polite, calm tone gets better results than expressing anger
- Use a three-part complaint structure: state the facts, state your request (refund/exchange), state next step if needed

## Scenario 9: Greeting Sequences by Relationship Closeness

Greeting sequences are among the most ritualized speech acts. Research (based on Schegloff's work) identifies four core phases of conversation openings, though not all phases are present in every encounter.

### Schegloff's Four Opening Phases:
1. **Summons-Answer:** Getting attention and signaling availability
2. **Identification-Recognition:** Establishing who is speaking
3. **Greeting exchange:** Ritualized greeting pair
4. **"How-are-you" exchange:** Phatic health inquiry

### How Relationship Closeness Affects Greetings:

**Intimate/Very Close (spouse, best friend, family):**
```
A: "Hey."
B: "Hey. How was your day?"
```
- Minimal greeting required
- May skip greeting entirely and launch into a topic
- "How was your day?" is genuine, not phatic
- Can use nicknames, terms of endearment
- No self-identification needed (you recognize each other automatically)

**Close Friend:**
```
A: "Hey! What's up?"
B: "Not much! Just got home. What about you?"
A: "Same. Hey, so I was thinking..."
```
- Casual greeting: "Hey," "Yo," "What's up"
- "What's up?" is semi-phatic -- a brief real answer is okay ("Not much" / "Just working")
- Quick transition to main topic
- Phone: no self-identification needed

**Acquaintance / Colleague:**
```
A: "Hi, [name]! How are you?"
B: "Good, thanks! How about you?"
A: "Good, good. [Topic or purpose of interaction]"
```
- Use their name (shows recognition, builds rapport)
- "How are you?" is phatic -- expected response is "Good" or "Fine"
- A real answer ("Actually, not great, my cat is sick") is unexpected and can cause awkwardness
- Transition to purpose after the greeting ritual

**Professional / Formal:**
```
A: "Good morning, Dr. Smith."
B: "Good morning. Please, have a seat."
```
- Use title + last name unless invited to use first name
- "Good morning/afternoon" rather than "Hi" or "Hey"
- More structured; the higher-status person often controls the greeting format
- Physical greeting may include a handshake

**Stranger / Service Worker:**
```
A: "Hi there."
B: "Hello, how can I help you?"
```
- Brief, functional greeting
- Moves quickly to transactional purpose
- No personal names
- Smiling and eye contact do much of the social work

### The "How Are You?" Decision Tree

This is one of the most formulaic exchanges in English and a major source of confusion for non-native speakers and autistic individuals:

```
"How are you?" / "How's it going?" / "How are you doing?"
         |
    Is this person a close friend or family member
    who is genuinely asking?
         |
    +----+----+
    |         |
   YES        NO (most cases)
    |         |
  Give a     Give a PHATIC response:
  real       "Good, thanks! You?"
  answer     "Fine, how about you?"
             "Can't complain!"
             "Not bad!"
             "Doing well, thanks."
             "Living the dream!" (humorous)
```

**Rules for the phatic "How are you?":**
1. Respond positively (even if you're not feeling great)
2. Keep it brief (one to three words)
3. Reciprocate: "Good, thanks. You?" (the return question is expected)
4. Do NOT give a detailed medical or emotional report to an acquaintance or stranger
5. Negative responses are okay ONLY with close friends who are genuinely asking: "Honestly? Not great. [Topic]..."

# 4. Speech Act Rituals: Formulaic Exchanges

Speech act rituals are exchanges so routinized that they function almost like social reflexes. The actual semantic content matters less than the performance of the ritual. Deviation from the expected pattern is immediately noticed and can cause social discomfort.

## "Thank You" / "You're Welcome" (and Variants)

### The Basic Ritual

The thanking ritual is one of the most fundamental in English:

```
A: [does something for B]
B: "Thank you!" / "Thanks!" [expression of gratitude]
A: "You're welcome." / "No problem." [acknowledgment/minimizer]
```

The second part -- the response to thanks -- is technically called a **"thanks minimizer"** or **"imbalance reducer after thanks" (IRAT)**. Its function is to reduce the perceived social debt created by the thankable act.

### Modern Variants and Their Connotations

| Response | Connotation | Typical User |
|---|---|---|
| "You're welcome" | Formal, traditional, acknowledges the favor was real | All ages, more formal contexts |
| "No problem" | Casual, frames the favor as not burdensome | Younger speakers especially |
| "No worries" | Very casual, relaxed | Younger speakers, common in Australian/British English |
| "Of course" | Implies it was natural/obvious to help | All ages, warm tone |
| "Sure thing" | Casual, friendly | American English, informal |
| "Anytime" | Implies willingness to help again | All ages, warm |
| "My pleasure" | Formal, service-oriented | Service workers, formal settings |
| "Don't mention it" | Actively minimizes the favor | Somewhat old-fashioned |
| "You bet" | Casual, enthusiastic | American English, informal |
| "Happy to help" | Warm, service-oriented | Professional settings |
| "Mm-hmm" | Minimal acknowledgment | Very casual, sometimes perceived as dismissive |

### The Generational Divide

This is a well-documented source of generational misunderstanding:

**Older generations' framework (Baby Boomers, older Gen X):**
- "You're welcome" = standard, proper response
- "No problem" = implies that helping *could have been* a problem, which feels dismissive
- Worldview: Helping someone is going above and beyond, so gratitude should be acknowledged formally

**Younger generations' framework (Millennials, Gen Z):**
- "No problem" / "No worries" = humble, egalitarian, frames helping as a normal thing to do
- "You're welcome" = can feel overly formal or transactional, as if claiming credit for the help
- Worldview: Helping is a social baseline, not an exceptional act, so gratitude doesn't need formal acknowledgment

**Neither interpretation is wrong.** They reflect different frameworks for understanding social obligation and gratitude. The shift from "you're welcome" to "no problem" as the default mirrors a broader trend toward casual, egalitarian communication norms.

### Missing the Ritual

What happens when the thanks ritual is violated:

- **No thank you:** If someone holds a door for you and you walk through without thanking them, they may feel annoyed or say "You're welcome!" sarcastically (this is a social sanction for the violation).
- **No response to thank you:** If you say "Thank you" and the other person says nothing, it feels incomplete -- like a question without an answer.

## "Sorry" as a Speech Act vs. an Apology

### The Dual Function of "Sorry"

The word "sorry" performs two very different speech acts in English:

**1. Real apology (expressive speech act):**
Expresses genuine remorse for a genuine offense.
```
"I'm sorry I forgot your birthday. I feel terrible about it."
"I'm so sorry I broke your mug. Let me replace it."
```
- Involves genuine fault on the speaker's part
- Carries the full weight of the apology speech act set (IFID, responsibility, account, repair, forbearance)
- Requires a real response: "It's okay" / "I appreciate that"

**2. Phatic/ritual "sorry" (social lubricant):**
Does NOT express remorse. Functions as a politeness marker or acknowledgment.

```
[bumping into someone in a hallway]
A: "Oh, sorry!" [not: "I apologize for my carelessness in failing to notice you"]
B: "Sorry!" or "No worries!" [reciprocal -- often both parties say it]
```

```
[reaching across someone at dinner]
"Sorry, could I just grab the salt?" [pre-request softener, not a real apology]
```

```
[asking someone to repeat themselves]
"Sorry, what was that?" [not apologizing for anything; means "excuse me"]
```

```
[hearing someone's bad news]
"Oh, I'm sorry to hear that." [expression of sympathy, not apology]
```

### When "Sorry" Isn't an Apology -- Recognition Guide:

| Context | Function | What It Really Means |
|---|---|---|
| Bumping into someone | Social reflex | "I acknowledge the minor physical intrusion" |
| "Sorry, quick question" | Hedge/softener | "I acknowledge I'm about to impose on you" |
| "Sorry?" (rising intonation) | Request for repetition | "Could you say that again?" |
| "I'm sorry to hear that" | Sympathy expression | "That's unfortunate and I empathize" |
| "Sorry, but I disagree" | Politeness marker | "I'm about to say something potentially face-threatening" |
| "Sorry about the mess" | Social convention | "I feel socially obligated to acknowledge this" |
| Passing in front of someone | Right-of-way acknowledgment | "Excuse me" equivalent |

### The Expected Response to Phatic "Sorry"

When someone says a ritual "sorry" (like bumping into you), the expected response is:
- "No worries!"
- "You're fine!"
- "All good!"
- "Oh, sorry!" (reciprocal -- very common; both parties apologize)
- "No problem."

It is NOT expected to say: "I forgive you" or "Apology accepted" (these responses treat the phatic sorry as a real apology, which creates an awkward mismatch).

## "Bless You" After a Sneeze

### The Ritual

```
[Person sneezes]
Others: "Bless you!" / "God bless you!" / "Gesundheit!"
Sneezer: "Thank you." / "Thanks." / "Excuse me."
```

### Historical Origin

This ritual dates to at least 590 AD, when Pope Gregory I reportedly commanded that anyone who sneezed should receive an immediate blessing to protect them from the plague. Earlier, Hebrew scholars taught that the soul might depart through the nostrils during a sneeze. The practice has persisted for over 1,400 years, long after its original rationale disappeared.

### Modern Function

In contemporary use, "bless you" has shed its supernatural meaning entirely. It functions as:
- A social acknowledgment ("I noticed your sneeze")
- A display of basic politeness and attention
- A social bonding ritual (reinforcing community through shared conventions)
- Social lubricant (fills what would otherwise be an awkward silence after a bodily function)

### Expected Behavior

- **One sneeze:** "Bless you" from someone nearby; sneezer says "Thank you"
- **Multiple sneezes:** Some say "bless you" after each; others wait until the sneezing stops; some use humorous escalation: "Bless you... bless you... bless you! Are you okay?"
- **No one says it:** Can feel awkward; the sneezer may say "Excuse me" to fill the gap
- **Stranger sneezes:** Whether to say "bless you" to a stranger varies -- it's generally perceived as friendly in the US, but is more optional than with someone you know

### Cultural Variation

- **Germany:** "Gesundheit" (health) -- the health-wish framing
- **French:** "A tes souhaits" (to your wishes) for the first sneeze; "A tes amours" (to your loves) for the second
- **Spanish:** "Salud" (health); "Dinero" (money) for second; "Amor" (love) for third
- **Iceland:** Elaborate three-part sequence for three sneezes: "God help you," "Strengthen you," "And support you"
- **Japan:** Generally no verbal response; calling attention to a bodily function is considered rude
- **Scandinavia (Sweden, Norway, Denmark, Finland):** Often no response to strangers; a verbal response can feel like social overreach

## Birthday Greetings and Responses

### The Basic Ritual

```
A: "Happy birthday!" [the standard formula]
B: "Thank you!" / "Thanks!" [standard acknowledgment]
```

### Variations in Delivery

**In person:**
```
"Happy birthday! How does it feel to be [age]?"
--> "Thanks! Same as yesterday, honestly!" [humorous deflection]
--> "Thank you! I feel great." [sincere acceptance]
```

**Via text/social media:**
```
A: "Happy birthday!! Hope you have an amazing day! 🎂"
B: "Thank you so much!! ❤️" [reciprocal enthusiasm]
```

**Sung (the birthday song):**
- The recipient sits and accepts the singing, usually looking slightly uncomfortable (this is a known social convention)
- After the song: everyone says "Happy birthday!" or claps
- Recipient: "Thank you!" (often with a slightly embarrassed smile)
- This is immediately followed by the candle-blowing ritual

### Social Obligations

- **Reciprocity:** If someone wishes you happy birthday, you are expected to wish them happy birthday when theirs comes. Social media has made this tracking more visible.
- **Thanking:** The birthday person is expected to thank each person who wishes them -- on social media, this may be done individually or with a blanket thank-you post.
- **Initiating:** Friends and family are expected to remember and initiate. Forgetting a close person's birthday is a social transgression.

### Response Strategies

| Their greeting | Your response |
|---|---|
| "Happy birthday!" (simple) | "Thank you!" |
| "Happy birthday! Any fun plans?" | "Thanks! Yeah, we're going out to dinner." |
| "Happy birthday! You don't look a day over 30!" | "Ha! I'll take it, thanks!" |
| "Happy birthday!" (from someone you barely know) | "Thank you, that's so nice of you!" |
| Group singing "Happy Birthday" | Smile, look appreciative, say "Thank you, guys!" after |

## Condolence Expressions and Responses

### The Ritual

Condolence is one of the most delicate speech act rituals because it involves face-threatening territory: acknowledging someone's pain while trying not to increase it.

### Standard Condolence Expressions

```
"I'm so sorry for your loss."
"My condolences."
"I'm thinking of you / your family."
"If there's anything I can do, please let me know."
"[Name] was a wonderful person."
"They will be so missed."
```

### Condolence Strategy Categories

Research identifies several common condolence strategies:

1. **Acknowledging the death:** "I was so sorry to hear about [name]."
2. **Expressing sympathy:** "My heart goes out to you."
3. **Offering assistance:** "Please let me know if you need anything at all."
4. **Future-oriented remarks:** "They'll always be with you." / "Their legacy lives on."
5. **Expression of concern:** "How are you holding up?"
6. **Sharing positive memories:** "[Name] always made everyone laugh. I'll never forget..."
7. **Religious/spiritual comfort:** "They're in a better place now." / "May they rest in peace." (Note: this is appropriate only if you know the recipient shares this framework)

### The Response

The bereaved person is in a peculiar social position: they are receiving comfort, but they are also socially expected to respond, which requires effort during a time of grief.

**Standard responses:**
```
"Thank you, that means a lot."
"Thank you for saying that."
"I appreciate it."
"Thank you, yes, they were [wonderful/amazing/one of a kind]."
```

**Important social norm:** There is a very wide tolerance for how a bereaved person responds. Brevity, tears, silence, or topic-shifting are all acceptable. The social pressure is almost entirely on the *condoler* to get their expression right, not on the bereaved to respond "correctly."

### What NOT to Say

Certain well-intentioned statements often feel hurtful or dismissive:
- "Everything happens for a reason" (imposes meaning on someone's grief)
- "I know how you feel" (unless you have truly been through a comparable loss)
- "At least they're not suffering anymore" (can feel dismissive of the loss)
- "You'll get through this" (can feel minimizing)
- "They're in a better place" (only appropriate if you know the person's beliefs)

### The Condolence Sequence in Practice

```
A: "I heard about your mom. I'm so sorry." [acknowledging + sympathy]
B: "Thank you. It's been really hard." [acceptance + sharing state]
A: "She was such a kind person. I always loved visiting your house growing up." [sharing memory]
B: "She loved having you over. Thank you for saying that." [appreciation]
A: "If you need anything -- meals, help with anything -- I mean it, just call." [offer of assistance]
B: "I appreciate that so much."
A: [May give a hug if close; may add one more sentiment]
    "I'm thinking of you and your family."
B: "Thank you."
```

# 5. Cultural and Generational Variation in Speech Act Patterns

## Key Dimensions of Variation

Speech act patterns are not universal. They vary along several dimensions:

### 1. Directness Norms

Different cultures have very different norms for how directly speech acts should be performed:

- **High directness cultures (many Northern European, Israeli):** Direct requests and refusals are not inherently rude. "No" is a complete sentence. Excessive hedging may be perceived as evasive or insincere.
- **Medium directness cultures (American English):** Conventional indirectness is the default for requests and refusals. "Could you...?" is preferred over bare imperatives. Refusals require accounts.
- **High indirectness cultures (many East Asian, some Middle Eastern):** Direct refusals are strongly dispreferred. Hinting, silence, and topic-shifting may serve as refusals. Saying "That might be difficult" may function as a definitive "no."

### 2. Apology Frequency

- **High-frequency apology cultures (British English, Japanese, Canadian English):** "Sorry" is used very frequently as a social lubricant, far beyond genuine apologies. British speakers may apologize when *someone else* bumps into *them*.
- **Lower-frequency apology cultures (American English, German):** Apologies are more tied to actual fault-acknowledgment. Excessive apologizing may be perceived as weak or unconfident.

### 3. Compliment Norms

- **American English:** Compliments are very frequent and serve as social bonding tools. Not complimenting when one is expected can be noticed.
- **Chinese:** Compliments are traditionally deflected or rejected to display modesty. Accepting a compliment could historically imply arrogance (though this is shifting).
- **Middle Eastern Arabic:** Complimenting someone's possessions can create a social obligation for the owner to offer the item (in some traditional contexts).

### 4. Insistence and Hospitality Rituals

- **American English:** One round of polite insistence after a decline is standard. "Are you sure?" once is expected. More than that can feel pushy.
- **Persian (taarof):** Multiple rounds (typically three) of offer-decline-re-offer are ritually required. Accepting on the first offer can seem greedy.
- **Ethiopian:** Multiple rounds of invitation insistence are expected before acceptance.
- **Japanese:** Gifts may be initially refused, with the giver expected to insist.

### 5. Greeting Elaborateness

- **American English:** "Hi, how are you?" "Good, you?" "Good." (Brief, formulaic)
- **Arabic:** Extended greeting sequences with religious invocations, health inquiries for the person and their family, and may take several minutes.
- **French:** Greetings include physical contact (cheek kisses, the number of which varies by region).

## Generational Variation in American English

### Communication Channel Shifts

| Pattern | Older Generations | Younger Generations |
|---|---|---|
| Primary channel | Phone calls, in-person | Texting, messaging apps |
| Email formality | Full greetings, formal closings | May skip greeting; casual closings or none |
| Phone call comfort | High -- prefer voice | Lower -- phone calls feel intrusive |
| Voicemail | Expected and checked | Rarely left, rarely checked |
| Emoji use | Literal/decorative | Complex, ironic, layered meaning |

### Speech Act Pattern Shifts

**Thanking responses:**
- Older: "You're welcome" (default, formal)
- Younger: "No problem," "No worries," "Of course" (casual, minimizing)

**Greeting formality:**
- Older: "Good morning," use of Mr./Mrs./Dr.
- Younger: "Hey," first names for nearly everyone

**Email openings:**
- Older: "Dear [Name]," / "Dear Mr./Ms. [Name],"
- Younger: "Hey [Name]," / "Hi [Name]," / "[Name]," / or no greeting at all

**Apology frequency:**
- Appears to be increasing among younger speakers, especially in text-based communication
- "Sorry" used increasingly as a softener: "Sorry, but could you..." / "Sorry to bother you"

**Communication style:**
- Older: tends toward longer, more formal, complete sentences
- Younger: tends toward brevity, efficiency, directness (especially in digital communication)
- Gen Z social media posts average 13.4 words, with a median of 11; Generation X posts are notably longer with complete sentences

**Emoji pragmatics:**
- Millennials: emojis used at face value (conventional meaning)
- Gen Z: emojis used ironically, sarcastically, or with layered in-group meanings. A slightly smiling face (🙂) may signal passive-aggression rather than friendliness. Skull emoji (💀) means "I'm dying laughing," not death.

### The Period (".") Controversy

One of the most striking generational pragmatic differences:
- **Older texters:** A period ends a sentence. "Sounds good." is neutral.
- **Younger texters:** In text messages (not email), a period can signal coldness, finality, or passive-aggression. "Sounds good." feels different from "Sounds good" or "Sounds good!"
- This is because in casual texting, the message boundary itself serves as sentence-ending punctuation, making an explicit period feel intentional and marked.

## Digital Communication and Speech Act Adaptation

### Text Messages

Text messages have developed their own speech act conventions:

- **Opening:** Often skipped entirely between close friends (launch directly into topic). With less close contacts, may begin with "Hey" or the person's name.
- **Closing:** Often no explicit closing. Conversation may fade out rather than formally close. The "leave-on-read" phenomenon (not responding) can function as either a natural ending or a social sanction depending on context.
- **Response timing:** Faster expected response time than email. Delayed responses can be interpreted as disinterest or passive-aggression (especially by younger users).
- **Read receipts:** Function as an ambient social signal. Seeing "read" without a response is a modern form of noticeable absence (similar to silence after a question in face-to-face conversation).

### Video Calls

- Opening: More greeting ritual than phone calls (you can see each other, so visual greetings: wave, smile)
- Closing: More explicit pre-closing needed because there's no natural physical separation cue
- Turn-taking: More disrupted due to lag; requires more explicit turn-yielding signals

# Summary: Key Principles for Understanding Speech Act Sequences

1. **Speech acts chain in predictable patterns.** Learning these patterns reduces the need to figure out each interaction from scratch.

2. **Pre-sequences are warning signals.** When someone asks "Are you busy?" or "Are you free Saturday?", something bigger is coming. The pre-sequence tells you what type of speech act to expect.

3. **Preferred responses are simple; dispreferred responses are complex.** If someone hesitates, hedges, or starts with "well...", a refusal or disagreement is likely coming.

4. **Accounts are socially required for dispreferred responses.** Saying "no" without explanation is perceived as rude. The explanation doesn't even need to be detailed -- "I can't, I have plans" is sufficient.

5. **Many exchanges are rituals, not information exchanges.** "How are you?" doesn't want medical details. "Thank you" / "You're welcome" is a social handshake, not a transaction. "Sorry" after bumping into someone is a reflex, not a confession.

6. **Scripts reduce cognitive load.** Once you know the expected sequence for ordering food, making a phone call, or running into an acquaintance, you can follow the script rather than generating each response from first principles.

7. **Deviation from expected patterns is noticed.** Not saying "bless you" after a sneeze, not thanking someone who holds a door, or giving a detailed answer to "How are you?" from a stranger -- all these deviations are registered and may be interpreted negatively.

8. **Context determines which rules apply.** The same person might greet their boss formally and their friend casually. The same "sorry" means different things when you bump into someone vs. when you've genuinely hurt someone's feelings. Flexibility across contexts is the key pragmatic skill.

9. **Cultural and generational variation is real.** There is no single "correct" way to perform speech acts. What feels rude to one generation may feel natural to another. What counts as a real refusal in one culture may count as a polite first-round decline in another.

10. **When in doubt, match the other person's register.** If they're formal, be formal. If they're casual, be casual. Mirroring the other person's level of formality is one of the safest default strategies.
