# Register in Specific Everyday Contexts: A Practical Map

## Research Summary

This document maps specific everyday contexts to their expected registers, showing how the same message transforms across register levels. The goal is to provide autistic individuals with an explicit, learnable system for what is normally acquired through implicit social absorption. Register is not about "politeness" in the abstract -- it is about matching your language to a specific context so that communication flows without friction and your message is received as intended rather than distorted by register mismatch.

The five-register framework (frozen, formal, consultative, casual, intimate) provides the backbone, but real life is a gradient. Most everyday situations live in the consultative-to-casual range, and the skill is knowing exactly where on that gradient each context falls.

## 1. REGISTER IN WORKPLACE COMMUNICATION

The workplace is where register matters most consequentially. Using the wrong register can damage your reputation, create misunderstandings about your attitude, or signal that you don't understand the social dynamics of the organization. Every workplace has its own register culture, but the patterns below hold across most professional environments.

### 1.1 Email Register by Recipient

Email register is determined primarily by **power distance** (how far above or below you the recipient is in the hierarchy) and **relationship closeness** (how well you know them).

#### To CEO / Senior Management (FORMAL)

These emails are high-stakes performances. You are communicating across a large power distance to someone who has limited time and many demands on their attention.

**Characteristics:**
- Full salutation: "Dear Ms. Chen," or "Dear Dr. Patel,"
- No contractions ("I would like to" not "I'd like to")
- Complete sentences, no fragments
- Hedged language: "I would like to suggest" rather than "I think we should"
- No emoji, no exclamation marks (or at most one)
- Professional sign-off: "Sincerely," "Best regards," "Respectfully,"
- Clear subject line stating purpose
- Brief -- senior leaders scan, they don't read at leisure

**Example:**
```
Subject: Q3 Marketing Budget -- Approval Request

Dear Ms. Whitfield,

I am writing to request approval for the revised Q3 marketing budget, 
which reflects a 12% increase over the initial projection. The increase 
is driven primarily by the expanded digital campaign we discussed at the 
March strategy meeting.

I have attached the detailed breakdown for your review. I would be happy 
to discuss this further at your convenience.

Thank you for your time.

Best regards,
James Okonkwo
Marketing Manager
```

#### To Direct Manager (CONSULTATIVE)

Your manager knows you, works with you regularly, and the power distance is smaller. The register relaxes but remains professional.

**Characteristics:**
- Greeting: "Hi Sarah," or "Good morning, David,"
- Some contractions are fine ("I've attached" rather than "I have attached")
- Can be slightly more direct
- Light hedging still appropriate
- Can include brief personal touches ("Hope your weekend was good")
- Sign-off: "Thanks," "Best," "Cheers," (depending on manager's own style)
- Can use occasional exclamation marks

**Example:**
```
Subject: Q3 budget update

Hi Sarah,

I've put together the revised Q3 budget -- it's come in about 12% over 
the initial projection, mostly because of the expanded digital campaign. 
I've attached the breakdown.

Would you have time to look it over this week? Happy to walk through 
it whenever works for you.

Thanks,
James
```

#### To Peer / Colleague (CASUAL-CONSULTATIVE)

Peers share your power level. The register sits at the boundary between consultative and casual, leaning one way or the other depending on how well you know them.

**Characteristics:**
- Greeting: "Hey Alex," or even just "Alex --"
- Contractions throughout
- Can be direct and efficient
- Emoji acceptable in some workplaces (sparingly)
- Sentence fragments OK for efficiency
- Sign-off: "Thanks!" "Cheers," or just your name, or nothing at all

**Example:**
```
Subject: Q3 budget numbers

Hey Alex,

Quick heads up -- the Q3 budget is coming in about 12% over what we 
originally had. Mostly the digital campaign expansion. 

Can you check the social media line items when you get a chance? Want 
to make sure those numbers look right before I send it up.

Thanks!
James
```

#### To Direct Report (CONSULTATIVE-CASUAL)

You have more power, so the register question flips. Being too formal with someone you manage can feel cold or signal displeasure. Being too casual can undermine your authority or seem unprofessional.

**Characteristics:**
- Greeting: "Hi Priya," or "Hey Priya,"
- Warm but clear
- Direct without being curt
- Can include encouragement or acknowledgment
- Should still be clear about expectations and deadlines
- Sign-off: "Thanks," "Best," or your name

**Example:**
```
Subject: Q3 budget -- need your input

Hi Priya,

Nice work on those social media projections last week. I'm putting 
together the final Q3 budget and need a couple of things from you:

1. Updated cost estimates for the Instagram campaign
2. Final vendor quotes for the video production

Could you get these to me by Thursday? Let me know if that timeline 
is tight and we can figure something out.

Thanks,
James
```

### 1.2 Meeting Register vs. Hallway Chat vs. Lunch Conversation

The same two coworkers will use different registers in different physical/situational spaces within the same building on the same day:

| Context | Register | Example (same information) |
|---------|----------|--------------------------|
| **Formal meeting with leadership** | Formal-consultative | "I'd like to flag a concern regarding the timeline. Based on the current resource allocation, we may not be able to deliver the full scope by Q3." |
| **Team meeting (peers only)** | Consultative | "So the timeline's looking tight. With what we've got resource-wise, I'm not sure we can hit everything by Q3. We might need to cut scope." |
| **Hallway chat** | Casual-consultative | "Hey, between you and me, that Q3 deadline is going to be brutal. I don't think we can do everything they want." |
| **Lunch conversation** | Casual | "Dude, that Q3 thing is a disaster waiting to happen. There's no way we're getting all of it done." |
| **After-work drinks** | Casual-intimate | "Honestly? I think the whole Q3 plan is kind of insane. I'm already stressed about it." |

**Key pattern:** Notice how each step removes a layer of hedging, formality, and professional packaging, while the core message remains the same. The information doesn't change; the social wrapping does.

### 1.3 When Register Shifts Signal Meaning

**This is critically important for autistic individuals to understand:** A sudden, unexpected register shift from someone you interact with regularly is itself a message. The shift IS the meaning.

#### Boss Suddenly Becomes Formal = Warning Signal

If your manager normally emails you "Hey James, can you..." and suddenly writes "James, I would like to discuss your recent performance on the Henderson project. Please schedule a meeting at your earliest convenience," the shift from casual to formal IS the message. Before you even read the content, the register tells you: something is wrong.

**Common register-shift signals in the workplace:**

| Normal Register | Shifted Register | What It Signals |
|----------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| "Hey, got a minute?" | "Do you have time for a meeting this afternoon?" | This is serious, not casual |
| "Nice work on that!" | "I appreciate your effort on this project." | Distant praise = something else is coming |
| "James" | "Mr. Okonkwo" | Extreme formalization = formal complaint, legal territory, or very serious issue |
| Manager uses your first name | Manager CCs HR on an email | The CC itself shifts the register; the audience has changed |
| "No worries, we'll figure it out" | "I need this resolved by end of day." | Patience has run out |
| Casual Slack message | Formal email about the same topic | They want a paper trail = this matters officially |

**The reverse is also meaningful:** If someone who is normally very formal with you suddenly becomes casual, it can signal:
- They are trying to build rapport
- They want something from you (softening before a request)
- They genuinely feel closer to you now
- They are trying to defuse a tense situation

### 1.4 Slack/Teams vs. Email Register Differences

These two channels have fundamentally different register expectations, even when the sender, recipient, and message content are identical.

| Feature | Email | Slack/Teams |
|---------|-------|-------------|
| **Default register** | Consultative-formal | Casual-consultative |
| **Greeting expected?** | Yes ("Hi Sarah,") | Optional; often just start talking |
| **Contractions** | Depends on recipient | Almost always |
| **Emoji/GIFs** | Rare; can seem unprofessional | Normal and expected |
| **Sentence fragments** | Avoided in formal emails | Standard |
| **Response expectations** | Hours to a day | Minutes to hours |
| **Sign-off** | Expected | Rarely used |
| **Typos tolerated?** | Low tolerance | High tolerance |
| **Thread length** | Single message, fully composed | Back-and-forth conversation |

**The same message in email vs. Slack:**

Email:
```
Hi Sarah,

I wanted to let you know that the client meeting has been moved to 
Thursday at 2pm. I've updated the calendar invite. Please let me 
know if this creates any conflicts.

Best,
James
```

Slack:
```
hey heads up - client meeting moved to Thursday 2pm
updated the cal invite
lmk if that doesn't work for you
```

**Critical rule:** When someone moves a conversation FROM Slack TO email, it means they want more formality, more documentation, or both. This is a register-shift signal just like the boss-becoming-formal example above.

**Another critical rule:** Some messages should NEVER be Slack/Teams messages even if that's where most communication happens:
- Performance feedback (positive or negative)
- Salary/compensation discussions
- Complaints about coworkers
- Legally sensitive topics
- Resignation

These require email or in-person conversation precisely because the higher register of those channels signals the seriousness of the content.

### 1.5 Interview Register vs. Once-You're-Hired Register

The job interview is one of the most dramatic register contexts in professional life because it requires performing at a register level you will almost never use again once you get the job.

**Interview register (formal-consultative):**
- "I would welcome the opportunity to contribute to your team's goals."
- "In my previous role, I was responsible for managing a team of twelve."
- "I'm particularly drawn to your company's commitment to innovation."
- "Thank you for taking the time to meet with me today."
- Address interviewer as "Ms. Rodriguez" unless explicitly invited to use first name
- No slang, no contractions (or minimal), complete sentences
- Posture and body language also at "formal" setting

**First week on the job (consultative):**
- "Thanks for showing me the ropes. I really appreciate it."
- "I managed about twelve people in my last job."
- "I'm excited to be here."
- Start with "Ms. Rodriguez"; if she says "call me Maria," switch immediately
- Observe what register others use and match it

**After three months (casual-consultative, depending on workplace):**
- "Hey, can you show me how to do the expense reports again?"
- "Yeah, I had a decent-sized team at my old place."
- "This place is great."
- First names with everyone at your level and most people above
- Match the register of the workplace culture

**The adjustment period is real.** Many people (especially autistic individuals) struggle with the question: "When do I stop being interview-formal?" The answer: watch your peers. Within the first week, observe how people who have been there a while talk to each other, and calibrate to that. If everyone is casual, being persistently formal will mark you as stiff or distant. If the culture is formal, staying too casual will mark you as unprofessional.

## 2. REGISTER IN EDUCATIONAL SETTINGS

Educational settings have their own register rules, and these rules shift significantly between high school and university.

### 2.1 Student-to-Professor Register

The student-to-professor email is one of the most common register challenges for young adults. Professors are authority figures with earned titles, and the power distance is significant.

**Expected register: Formal to consultative-formal**

**Correct approach:**
```
Subject: Question about Essay 2 deadline -- ENGL 201

Dear Professor Nakamura,

My name is Taylor Chen, and I am a student in your Tuesday/Thursday 
English 201 section. I am writing to ask whether it would be possible 
to receive a brief extension on Essay 2. I have been dealing with a 
family emergency this week and have not been able to complete the 
assignment on time.

I understand if an extension is not possible, and I appreciate your 
consideration.

Sincerely,
Taylor Chen
Student ID: 4789321
```

**Common mistakes (too casual):**
```
hey prof,

can i get an extension on the essay? things have been crazy 
this week lol

thanks,
taylor
```

**What's wrong with the casual version:**
- "hey prof" -- too informal for someone with a doctoral degree
- No last name used for the professor
- No self-identification (which class? which section?)
- "things have been crazy" -- vague and casual
- "lol" -- completely inappropriate register for this context
- No closing formality

**Address rules for professors:**
- Default: "Professor [Last Name]" -- always safe
- "Dr. [Last Name]" -- safe if they have a doctorate
- "Mr./Ms. [Last Name]" -- can be perceived as demoting someone who has a higher title
- First name -- ONLY if the professor explicitly says "call me [First Name]," and even then, many students maintain the title in written communication

### 2.2 Student-to-Student Register

Students shift register constantly depending on context:

| Context | Register | Example |
|---------|----------|---------|
| Study group text message | Casual | "yo anyone understand the thing about market equilibrium? im lost" |
| Group project email | Casual-consultative | "Hey everyone, here's my section of the paper. Let me know if anything needs changing." |
| Peer review in class | Consultative | "I think your argument in paragraph 3 is really strong. One thing that might help is adding a specific example to support your point about economic inequality." |
| Student government meeting | Consultative-formal | "I'd like to propose that we allocate additional funding to the tutoring center based on the increased demand this semester." |

### 2.3 Classroom Participation Register

Speaking up in class requires a register that is:
- More formal than hallway conversation
- Less formal than written academic prose
- Respectful of the professor and other students
- Clear and on-topic

**Appropriate classroom contributions:**
- "I think what the author is getting at here is..." (hedged interpretation)
- "Could you clarify what you mean by [term]?" (polite question)
- "I agree with what Sarah said, and I'd add that..." (building on others)
- "I'm not sure I fully agree -- it seems like..." (hedged disagreement)

**Inappropriate classroom contributions (too casual):**
- "That's dumb." (too direct, no hedging)
- "Nah, I don't buy it." (too casual for classroom)
- "Whatever, I guess." (dismissive)

**Inappropriate classroom contributions (too formal):**
- "I would like to respectfully submit for the consideration of this assembly..." (this is a courtroom, not a seminar)

### 2.4 Academic Writing Register

Academic writing operates at the formal register, but it has its own specific features that distinguish it from other formal writing (like business formal or legal formal):

**Key features of academic register:**
- **Hedging:** "The data suggest that..." rather than "The data prove that..."
- **Nominalization:** "The investigation revealed..." rather than "When we investigated, we found..."
- **Passive voice (strategic):** "The samples were analyzed using..." rather than "I analyzed the samples using..."
- **Impersonal constructions:** "It can be argued that..." rather than "I think..."
- **Formal vocabulary:** "investigate" not "look into"; "demonstrate" not "show"; "utilize" not "use"; "approximately" not "about"
- **No contractions:** "does not" not "doesn't"
- **Citation and attribution:** Every claim supported by evidence
- **Complex sentence structures:** Subordinate clauses, relative clauses

**Example of the same idea at different registers:**

| Register | Expression |
|----------|-----------|
| Casual | "Social media makes people depressed." |
| Consultative | "There's growing evidence that heavy social media use is linked to increased rates of depression." |
| Academic formal | "A growing body of empirical research suggests a statistically significant correlation between intensive social media engagement and elevated prevalence of depressive symptomatology among adolescent populations (Smith et al., 2023; Patel & Wong, 2024)." |

### 2.5 The Shift from High School to College/University

This is one of the most disorienting register transitions because the rules change without anyone explicitly stating the new rules.

| Dimension | High School | College/University |
|-----------|------------|-------------------|
| **How you address teachers** | "Mr. Johnson" / "Mrs. Davis" | "Professor Nakamura" / "Dr. Chen" |
| **Asking for help** | Teacher checks on you proactively | You must seek help yourself |
| **Email to teacher** | Often not required or very informal | Formal email expected |
| **Excuses for late work** | Parents may advocate for you | You must advocate for yourself, formally |
| **Classroom speaking** | Raise hand, short answers | Expected to contribute substantive analysis |
| **Relationship with authority** | Paternalistic (they manage you) | Professional (you manage yourself) |
| **Writing register** | Five-paragraph essay, clear opinions | Academic register, hedged claims, citations |

**The core shift:** In high school, teachers often function in a quasi-parental role and the register reflects that -- protective, directive, personal. In college, professors function as professional experts and the register shifts to reflect that -- more formal, more independent, more like a junior colleague than a child being supervised.

## 3. REGISTER IN SERVICE ENCOUNTERS

Service encounters are transactions where register signals social identity, expected behavior, and the nature of the space you're in.

### 3.1 Customer-to-Server Register

The register you use with a server depends heavily on the type of establishment:

#### Fast Food / Quick Service

**Register: Casual**

The entire interaction is scripted, efficient, and informal. Both parties know this is transactional.

Typical exchange:
```
Server: "Hi, what can I get for you?"
You:    "Can I get a number 3 with a Coke?"
Server: "You want that medium or large?"
You:    "Medium's fine."
Server: "Anything else?"
You:    "Nah, that's it. Thanks."
Server: "Cool, that'll be $8.47."
```

**Features:** Short sentences, fragments, contractions, "nah," no titles, minimal pleasantries, focus on efficiency.

#### Casual Sit-Down Restaurant

**Register: Casual-consultative**

More social interaction is expected. The server may introduce themselves by first name. You have more time and more conversational exchange.

Typical exchange:
```
Server: "Hi there, I'm Kyle, I'll be taking care of you tonight.
         Can I start you off with something to drink?"
You:    "Hi Kyle. Yeah, could I get an iced tea?"
Server: "Absolutely! And for you?"
Partner:"I'll have a water with lemon, please."
Server: "Perfect. I'll give you a few minutes to look at the menu."
```

**Features:** First names, pleasantries, "please" and "thank you," slightly longer exchanges, server uses "absolutely" and "perfect" as affiliative responses.

#### Fine Dining

**Register: Consultative-formal**

The server's language is deliberately elevated, and your register should meet it partway. The server will never say "you guys" or "no problem." They say "certainly," "my pleasure," "may I suggest."

Typical exchange:
```
Server: "Good evening. Welcome to La Maison. May I offer you 
         something from the bar while you consider the menu?"
You:    "Good evening. Yes, could we see the wine list, please?"
Server: "Of course. I'd also like to mention that this evening's 
         special is a pan-seared branzino with a lemon-caper sauce."
You:    "That sounds lovely. We'll take a look."
Server: "Wonderful. Take your time."
```

**Features:** "Good evening" not "hi," "may I" not "can I," "of course" not "sure," no first name introduction from the server, formal vocabulary ("offer" not "get"), no slang, measured pace.

### 3.2 Customer Service Phone Calls

Phone calls to customer service lines operate in a semi-scripted consultative-formal register:

**The company's script (formal-consultative):**
```
"Thank you for calling TechCorp support. My name is Aisha. May I 
have your account number, please?"

"I understand how frustrating that must be. Let me look into this 
for you right away."

"Thank you for your patience. I've resolved the issue on our end. 
Is there anything else I can help you with today?"

"Thank you for calling TechCorp, Mr. Okonkwo. Have a wonderful day."
```

**What you as the customer should use (consultative):**
```
"Hi, I'm calling about a billing issue on my account."
"The charge on March 15th doesn't look right -- I was billed twice 
for the same service."
"Thank you, I appreciate your help."
```

**What NOT to do (register mismatch):**
- Too casual: "Yeah so basically my bill is screwed up and I need someone to fix it" -- sounds adversarial
- Too formal: "I wish to formally dispute the validity of a charge appearing on my account statement" -- this is a phone call, not a legal filing (save this register for a written complaint letter)

### 3.3 Register at Different Types of Establishments

The register of a service space signals who "belongs" there. This is a crucial insight: **register is a gatekeeping mechanism.** Using the wrong register in a space can make you feel out of place or make others treat you as an outsider.

| Establishment | Expected Customer Register | What Signals "I Belong Here" |
|--------------|--------------------------|------------------------------|
| **Fast food** | Casual | Quick, efficient ordering; not overthinking word choices |
| **Coffee shop** | Casual | Knowing the ordering conventions ("tall oat milk latte, please") |
| **Casual restaurant** | Casual-consultative | Friendly, relaxed, using please/thank you |
| **Fine dining** | Consultative-formal | Measured pace, formal vocabulary, not looking at prices audibly |
| **Casual clothing store** | Casual | "Just browsing, thanks" / "Hey, do you have this in a medium?" |
| **Luxury store (Gucci, Tiffany's)** | Consultative-formal | Measured language, confidence, not asking "how much is this?" in shock |
| **Hardware store** | Casual-consultative | Using some technical vocabulary shows competence |
| **Doctor's office** | Consultative | Clear description of symptoms, respectful of doctor's time |
| **Lawyer's office** | Consultative-formal | Precise language, organized presentation of your situation |
| **Government office (DMV, etc.)** | Consultative | Polite, patient, prepared with documents |

**The luxury store dynamic:** In high-end retail, language signals social class and purchasing intent. The staff use formal, elegant language ("May I show you our new collection?" not "Need help finding anything?"). Customers who match this register are treated as serious buyers. Customers who use very casual register may be treated with less attention -- not because they shouldn't be there, but because the register mismatch signals (accurately or inaccurately) that they may not be the target customer. This is a form of social gatekeeping through language.

### 3.4 How Register Signals You "Belong" in a Social Space

Register works as a membership card. When you walk into a space and use the "right" register, you signal:
- "I have been in places like this before"
- "I understand the norms here"
- "I am the kind of person who frequents this space"

When your register doesn't match the space, others may:
- Offer you extra guidance (assuming you're new)
- Treat you with less respect (assuming you don't belong)
- Be confused about how to interact with you

**This is not about being "fake."** Matching register to context is the same as wearing appropriate clothing to different events. You wear different things to a job interview, a beach, and a funeral -- not because you're being dishonest, but because different contexts call for different presentations.

## 4. REGISTER IN DIGITAL COMMUNICATION

Digital communication has created entirely new register contexts. The platform itself determines much of the register expectation.

### 4.1 Text Message Register by Relationship

Text messages vary dramatically by recipient:

#### To Your Romantic Partner (Intimate-Casual)
```
miss u
when r u coming home
can u grab milk
love u babe
ugh today was the worst
[string of emoji]
```
**Features:** Abbreviations, sentence fragments, emoji, terms of endearment, emotional vulnerability, minimal punctuation, no greeting or sign-off.

#### To a Close Friend (Casual)
```
dude
you will not believe what just happened at work
my boss literally just
ok so basically she called this meeting right
and then
TOLD EVERYONE the project is canceled
like what
im dead
```
**Features:** Fragmented storytelling, dramatic pauses (sending multiple short messages), all lowercase, no punctuation, slang ("literally," "im dead"), shared emotional reactions.

#### To a Parent (Casual-Consultative, varies by family)
```
Hi Mom
Yeah I'm doing fine, work is good
I'll be there for dinner Sunday
Do you need me to bring anything?
Love you
```
**Features:** More complete sentences than with friends, fewer abbreviations (parents may not understand them), greeting included, polite questions, may include "love you" sign-off. Note: Many parents use periods in texts, which reads as neutral to them but may read as "angry" or "curt" to younger texters.

#### To Your Boss (Consultative)
```
Hi Sarah, just wanted to let you know I'm running about 
10 minutes late this morning. Traffic is backed up on 
the highway. Sorry about that.
```
**Features:** Full sentences, proper capitalization and punctuation, greeting, explanation, apology, no abbreviations, no emoji (unless your workplace culture specifically allows it).

### 4.2 Email Register Spectrum

Email covers a wider register range than almost any other medium:

| Context | Register | Greeting | Closing | Body Features |
|---------|----------|----------|---------|---------------|
| Job application | Formal | "Dear Hiring Manager," | "Sincerely," | No contractions, hedged, complete sentences |
| Client communication | Formal-consultative | "Dear Mr. Park," | "Best regards," | Professional, clear, some contractions OK |
| Manager email | Consultative | "Hi Sarah," | "Thanks," / "Best," | Contractions OK, direct, efficient |
| Colleague email | Casual-consultative | "Hey Alex," | "Thanks!" / name / nothing | Fragments OK, emoji maybe, efficient |
| Close colleague | Casual | "Alex --" | "- J" | Very brief, may just be forwarding something with one-line comment |

### 4.3 Social Media Register by Platform

Each social media platform has developed its own register norms. Using the wrong register on a platform is like wearing a tuxedo to a barbecue -- technically not "wrong" but socially conspicuous.

#### LinkedIn (Formal-Consultative)
```
I'm thrilled to announce that I've joined Quantum Analytics as 
Senior Data Scientist. After five years at TechCorp, I'm excited 
to take on new challenges in the AI space. 

Thank you to everyone who has been part of my professional journey. 
Special thanks to @Maria_Santos for her mentorship.

#DataScience #NewBeginnings #AI
```
**Register features:** Professional vocabulary, career-focused content, named acknowledgments, hashtags as professional markers, positive/aspirational tone, no slang, complete sentences.

#### Twitter/X (Casual-Consultative to Casual)
```
just started at Quantum Analytics and honestly?? the vibes 
are immaculate

also their coffee machine makes actual good espresso which is 
really what matters
```
**Register features:** Lowercase, informal, humor, self-deprecation, sentence fragments, conversational tone, "vibes," "honestly??", focus on personality over professionalism.

#### Instagram (Casual, Visual-Primary)
```
new chapter [sparkle emoji] [rocket emoji]

#quantumanalytics #datascience #newjob #blessed
```
**Register features:** Very brief, emoji-heavy, hashtags as categorization, visual content does the heavy lifting, captions are secondary, casual vocabulary, no full sentences needed.

#### Reddit (Casual but Variable by Subreddit)
```
r/datascience:
"Just switched from TechCorp to a smaller AI firm. Anyone else 
made a similar move? Curious about the adjustment period for 
going from big company infrastructure to startup life."
```
**Register features:** Casual but substantive, question-oriented (community engagement), no personal branding, pseudonymous (so no professional posturing), specific and genuine.

**Important:** Reddit register varies dramatically by subreddit. r/AskHistorians requires formal, sourced answers. r/CasualConversation is deliberately informal. r/legaladvice is consultative. Each subreddit is its own register community.

### 4.4 How Platform Determines Register

The platform's design constraints shape register:

- **Character limits** (Twitter's 280 characters) force brevity and informality
- **Persistence** (LinkedIn posts visible to professional contacts) forces professionalism
- **Anonymity** (Reddit pseudonyms) allows more directness and informality
- **Visual primacy** (Instagram) reduces the importance of text register
- **Threaded conversation** (Slack) encourages rapid, fragment-style communication
- **Single-message format** (email) encourages composed, complete communication

**Rule of thumb:** The more a platform resembles a spoken conversation (real-time, short messages, back-and-forth), the more casual the register. The more it resembles a written document (composed, standalone, persistent), the more formal the register.

### 4.5 Generational Register Differences in Digital Communication

Different generations have different "default registers" for digital communication because they learned to communicate digitally at different life stages:

| Generation | Default Digital Register | Key Features |
|-----------|------------------------|--------------|
| **Baby Boomers** (born ~1946-1964) | Formal-consultative | Full sentences, proper punctuation, periods at end of every sentence, "Dear" greetings even in texts, may sign texts with their name |
| **Gen X** (born ~1965-1980) | Consultative | Proper grammar but more relaxed, can code-switch between formal email and casual text, adapted to each new platform |
| **Millennials** (born ~1981-1996) | Casual-consultative | Grew up with instant messaging, invented many texting conventions, emoji-literate, flexible register |
| **Gen Z** (born ~1997-2012) | Casual | Lowercase default, minimal punctuation, rapid-fire short messages, emoji and memes as communication, see periods as aggressive |

**The period problem:** This is a major generational register mismatch. For Boomers and Gen X, a period at the end of a text message is standard punctuation. For Gen Z, a period in a text signals anger, disappointment, or passive-aggression.

Compare:
- Boomer texting: "Sounds good." (neutral -- just standard punctuation)
- Gen Z reading it: "Sounds good." (Why are they mad? What did I do?)
- Gen Z version: "sounds good" or "sounds good!" (the absence of a period IS the neutral register)

This happens because in texting, hitting "send" already signals the end of a thought. Adding a period is redundant, so it must mean something extra -- and that extra meaning is read as formality, distance, or displeasure.

### 4.6 Intentional Register Violations in Digital Spaces

Sometimes people deliberately violate register expectations for humor, irony, or social bonding.

**Ironic formality (using formal register in casual context):**
- Friend texts: "wanna grab dinner?"
- Response: "I would be most delighted to accept your gracious invitation to partake in the evening meal."
- Effect: Humor through incongruity. The absurdly formal register in a casual context creates comedy.

**Deliberate casualness (using casual register in formal context):**
- LinkedIn post: "hot take: meetings could be emails and emails could be slack messages and slack messages could be nothing"
- Effect: The casual register on a formal platform signals authenticity, irreverence, or deliberate rejection of professional performance.

**Strategic informality (using casual register to build connection):**
- CEO sends company-wide Slack: "shipped the new feature! team absolutely crushed it [fire emoji] [rocket emoji]"
- Effect: The CEO's casual register signals accessibility and shared excitement, breaking down hierarchical distance.

**Warning for autistic individuals:** These intentional violations work BECAUSE the person using them clearly knows the "correct" register and is deliberately choosing not to use it. If you accidentally use the wrong register, it reads as ignorance rather than intentional humor. The key difference is whether the speaker demonstrates awareness of the norm they're violating.

## 5. REGISTER IN FAMILY AND SOCIAL CONTEXTS

### 5.1 How Register Shifts When a Friend's Parent Is Present

This is one of the most common and disorienting register shifts for many people. You're hanging out with your friend using casual-intimate register, and their parent walks in. The register must shift instantly.

**Before parent arrives (casual-intimate):**
```
You: "Oh my god, did you see what Jake posted? I'm literally 
      dying. He's such an idiot."
Friend: "I know! And then Megan was like 'excuse me??' and I 
         lost it."
```

**Parent walks in (shifts to casual-consultative):**
```
Parent: "Hi there! You two having fun?"
You: "Hi Mrs. Patterson! Yeah, we're just hanging out. How are 
      you doing?"
Parent: "Oh, I'm good. Do you kids want some snacks?"
You: "That would be great, thank you so much."
```

**What changed:**
- Greeting with title: "Mrs. Patterson" not "your mom"
- Removed profanity and harsh judgments ("idiot" → wouldn't say this)
- Added pleasantries: "How are you doing?"
- Added explicit gratitude: "thank you so much"
- Shifted from storytelling mode to polite-conversation mode
- Sentence structure more complete

**After parent leaves (back to casual-intimate):**
```
You: "OK anyway so Jake..."
```

The return is almost instant. The register acts like a switch that flips when the audience changes.

### 5.2 How Register Shifts When Meeting a Partner's Family

Meeting a partner's family for the first time is a high-stakes register performance. The expected register is **formal-consultative** -- more formal than your normal social interaction, because you are being evaluated.

**What to do:**
- Address parents as Mr./Mrs./Ms. [Last Name] until told otherwise
- "It's so nice to finally meet you. [Partner's name] has told me wonderful things about you."
- If they say "call me Karen," switch to first name: "Thank you, Karen."
- Compliment the home: "You have a lovely home."
- Respond to questions with complete answers, not fragments
- Use please and thank you consistently
- No profanity, no slang
- Listen actively and ask follow-up questions about them

**Over subsequent visits, the register will naturally relax:**
- First visit: "Thank you so much for having me, Mrs. Williams. Dinner was absolutely wonderful."
- Fifth visit: "Thanks for dinner, Karen! The chicken was amazing."
- Twentieth visit: "Hey Karen, we brought wine. What's for dinner? I'm starving."

**The timeline for register relaxation varies by family.** Some families are informal from the start ("Oh, call me Karen, none of that Mrs. stuff!"). Others maintain formality for much longer. Match the family's own register level -- don't relax faster than they do.

### 5.3 Register at Formal Events

#### Weddings (Formal-Consultative)

**During the ceremony (frozen-formal):**
- Silence, listening
- If participating: scripted vows, readings (frozen register)

**Reception, speaking to the couple:**
```
"Congratulations! The ceremony was beautiful. You both look 
absolutely wonderful. We're so happy for you."
```

**Reception, speaking to other guests:**
```
"How do you know the bride and groom?"
"What a lovely venue they chose."
"Are you enjoying the evening?"
```

**Reception, after drinks with friends (casual):**
```
"OK this DJ is killing it."
"Did you see the cake? It's massive."
"I need to get on that dance floor."
```

#### Funerals (Formal-Consultative)

Funerals require the most consistently formal social register because the emotional stakes are high and the cost of register mismatch is significant (you might seem disrespectful).

**Speaking to the bereaved family:**
```
"I'm so sorry for your loss."
"Your mother was a truly wonderful person. She will be deeply 
missed."
"Please let me know if there's anything I can do."
```

**Speaking to other attendees:**
```
"How are you holding up?"
"Did you know her well?"
```

**What NOT to say (register and content violations):**
- "She's in a better place now" -- potentially offensive depending on beliefs
- "I know how you feel" -- presumptuous
- "At least she lived a long life" -- minimizing
- "Hey, what's up?" -- far too casual for the setting

#### Graduations (Formal-Consultative to Casual)

Graduations are interesting because they have a formal ceremony followed by informal celebration, requiring a register shift.

**During ceremony:** Silence, applause at appropriate times (frozen-formal audience behavior)

**After ceremony, to the graduate:**
```
"Congratulations! We're so proud of you. You worked so hard for 
this."
```

**At the celebration party:** Shifts to casual register, matching the social gathering context.

### 5.4 Register at Informal Gatherings

#### Barbecues

**Register: Casual**
```
"Hey! Glad you could make it. Grab a beer from the cooler."
"Who's manning the grill?"
"These burgers are awesome."
"Has anyone seen the ketchup?"
```

**Features:** Short sentences, slang OK, first names or nicknames, physical comfort (sitting on the ground, standing around), no one is performing formality.

#### Game Nights

**Register: Casual-intimate (with established friends)**
```
"Whose turn is it?"
"Dude, you totally cheated."
"OK I'm calling BS on that move."
"Wait wait wait -- that's not how the rule works."
"Rematch. Right now."
```

**Features:** Competitive banter, teasing, interruptions, emotional expressiveness, in-group humor, potential for (playful) profanity.

**Important note:** If game night includes someone's new partner or a person you don't know well, you may need to temporarily shift UP in register to be welcoming:
```
"Hey, welcome! Have you played Settlers of Catan before? Don't 
worry, we'll walk you through it."
```

## 6. THE SAME MESSAGE IN FIVE REGISTERS -- Worked Examples

These examples demonstrate exactly how vocabulary, grammar, hedging, sentence structure, and address forms change across register levels. Study these like translation exercises -- the underlying meaning stays constant while the surface form transforms.

### 6.1 Asking Someone to Be Quiet

**Underlying message:** "Please reduce your volume."

| Register | Expression | Context |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| **Frozen** | "Silence in the court." / "Quiet, please — the ceremony is about to begin." | Courtroom, ceremony, announcement |
| **Formal** | "I would appreciate it if we could maintain a lower volume. Thank you." | Workplace meeting, library announcement |
| **Consultative** | "Excuse me, would you mind keeping it down a little? We're trying to concentrate over here." | Open office, shared space with acquaintances |
| **Casual** | "Hey, can you guys keep it down? I can't hear myself think." | Among friends, roommates, familiar coworkers |
| **Intimate** | "Babe, shh." / "Dude, shut up." [said with affection] | Partner, very close friend |

**What changes across registers:**

| Feature | Frozen→Formal | Consultative | Casual | Intimate |
|---------|-------------|--------------|--------|----------|
| **Vocabulary** | "silence," "maintain" | "would you mind," "keeping it down" | "keep it down," "can't hear" | "shh," "shut up" |
| **Hedging** | None (command) | "would you mind," "a little" | Minimal hedging | None |
| **Sentence length** | Short, ritualized | Medium, with polite phrasing | Medium, direct | Very short |
| **Address form** | None (broadcast) | "Excuse me" | "you guys" | "Babe" / "Dude" |
| **Directness** | Maximum (authority) | Indirect (requesting) | Direct (telling) | Maximum (intimacy) |

### 6.2 Requesting Information

**Underlying message:** "I need to know what time the event starts."

| Register | Expression |
|----------|-----------|
| **Frozen** | "For event details, please consult the official program." (written notice) |
| **Formal** | "I am writing to inquire about the start time for the annual gala. Could you kindly provide this information at your earliest convenience?" |
| **Consultative** | "Hi, could you tell me what time the event starts? I want to make sure I plan accordingly." |
| **Casual** | "Hey, what time does the thing start?" |
| **Intimate** | "Wait what time is it again?" / "When do we need to be there?" |

**What changes:**

| Feature | Formal | Consultative | Casual | Intimate |
|---------|--------|--------------|--------|----------|
| **"The event"** | "the annual gala" | "the event" | "the thing" | "it" |
| **Request form** | "I am writing to inquire" | "Could you tell me" | "What time does..." | "When do we need to be there?" |
| **Justification** | Implied (the formality itself justifies) | "I want to make sure I plan accordingly" | None needed | None needed |
| **Assumed knowledge** | No shared knowledge assumed | Some context assumed | Shared context assumed | Full shared context |

### 6.3 Expressing Disagreement

**Underlying message:** "I think your idea won't work."

| Register | Expression |
|----------|-----------|
| **Frozen** | "The motion is overruled." / "Objection." (legal/parliamentary) |
| **Formal** | "With all due respect, I have concerns about the feasibility of this approach. The evidence suggests that alternative strategies may yield more favorable outcomes." |
| **Consultative** | "I see where you're coming from, but I'm not sure that approach would work in practice. What if we considered a different angle?" |
| **Casual** | "Hmm, I don't know about that. I feel like that's going to cause more problems than it solves." |
| **Intimate** | "Nah, that's not gonna work." / "Sorry, but that's a terrible idea." [said with love] |

**What changes:**

| Feature | Formal | Consultative | Casual | Intimate |
|---------|--------|--------------|--------|----------|
| **Opening hedge** | "With all due respect" | "I see where you're coming from" | "Hmm, I don't know" | None |
| **Strength of disagreement** | "concerns about feasibility" (very softened) | "not sure that would work" (softened) | "going to cause problems" (moderate) | "terrible idea" (direct) |
| **Face-saving** | Extensive (deflects to "evidence") | Moderate (offers alternative) | Minimal | None needed (trust assumed) |
| **Solution offered** | "alternative strategies" (abstract) | "What if we considered...?" (collaborative) | Not required | Not required |

### 6.4 Apologizing

**Underlying message:** "I made a mistake and I'm sorry."

| Register | Expression |
|----------|-----------|
| **Frozen** | "The company formally retracts the statement issued on March 3rd and extends its sincerest apologies to all affected parties." |
| **Formal** | "I sincerely apologize for the oversight. It was entirely my responsibility, and I assure you it will not happen again. Please accept my apologies for any inconvenience this may have caused." |
| **Consultative** | "I'm really sorry about that. It was my mistake, and I should have caught it sooner. I'll make sure it's fixed right away." |
| **Casual** | "Oh man, I'm sorry. That was totally my fault. I'll fix it." |
| **Intimate** | "My bad." / "Ugh, sorry sorry sorry. I suck." |

**What changes:**

| Feature | Formal | Consultative | Casual | Intimate |
|---------|--------|--------------|--------|----------|
| **Apology word** | "sincerely apologize" | "really sorry" | "sorry" | "my bad" |
| **Responsibility** | "entirely my responsibility" | "my mistake" | "totally my fault" | Implied |
| **Promise** | "assure you it will not happen again" | "make sure it's fixed right away" | "I'll fix it" | Not needed |
| **Self-deprecation** | None (too unprofessional) | None | None | "I suck" (acceptable because intimacy) |
| **Formality markers** | "Please accept," "oversight," "inconvenience" | "should have caught it" | "oh man" | "ugh" |

### 6.5 Ending a Conversation

**Underlying message:** "I need to leave this conversation now."

| Register | Expression |
|----------|-----------|
| **Frozen** | "This meeting is adjourned." / "This concludes today's proceedings." |
| **Formal** | "Thank you so much for your time today. It's been a pleasure speaking with you. I look forward to continuing our discussion. Please don't hesitate to reach out if you need anything further." |
| **Consultative** | "Well, I should probably get going. It was really great talking with you. Let's catch up again soon. Have a good rest of your day!" |
| **Casual** | "Alright, I gotta head out. Good talking to you! See you around." |
| **Intimate** | "K I gotta go. Love you, bye." / "Later, dude." |

**What changes:**

| Feature | Formal | Consultative | Casual | Intimate |
|---------|--------|--------------|--------|----------|
| **Pre-closing signal** | "Thank you for your time" | "I should probably get going" | "Alright" | "K" |
| **Reason given** | Not needed (formal closes are self-sufficient) | Optional ("I should probably...") | Brief ("gotta head out") | None needed |
| **Future reference** | "I look forward to continuing" | "Let's catch up again soon" | "See you around" | None / implied |
| **Farewell** | "Please don't hesitate to reach out" | "Have a good rest of your day!" | "See you around" | "Later" / "bye" |
| **Length** | Long, multi-sentence | Medium | Short | Very short |

### 6.6 Requesting a Deadline Extension

**Underlying message:** "I need more time to finish this."

| Register | Expression |
|----------|-----------|
| **Frozen** | "A motion to extend the deadline for submissions has been filed pursuant to Section 4.2 of the bylaws." |
| **Formal** | "I am writing to respectfully request an extension on the submission deadline. Due to unforeseen circumstances, I have been unable to complete the deliverable within the original timeframe. I would be grateful for any additional time you might be able to grant." |
| **Consultative** | "Hi Professor Nakamura, I'm running behind on the essay and was wondering if it would be possible to get an extension. I've had some family issues this week that have set me back. I completely understand if that's not feasible." |
| **Casual** | "Hey, any chance I can get a couple extra days on that assignment? This week has been rough." |
| **Intimate** | "I'm so screwed -- there's no way I'm finishing this by Friday. Help??" |

### 6.7 Saying No to a Request

**Underlying message:** "I can't/won't do what you're asking."

| Register | Expression |
|----------|-----------|
| **Frozen** | "The application has been denied." / "Permission is not granted." |
| **Formal** | "Thank you for your proposal. After careful consideration, we are unfortunately unable to accommodate this request at this time. We appreciate your understanding." |
| **Consultative** | "I appreciate you thinking of me, but I'm not going to be able to take that on right now. My plate is pretty full with the current project deadlines." |
| **Casual** | "Ah, I can't. I'm swamped. Maybe next time?" |
| **Intimate** | "Nope." / "Can't. Sorry, love." / "Absolutely not." [laughing] |

## 7. ADDRESS FORMS AND REGISTER

Address forms -- what you call someone -- are one of the most reliable indicators of register. They are also one of the most anxiety-producing aspects of social interaction because getting them wrong is immediately noticeable.

### 7.1 The Address Form Hierarchy

From most formal to most informal:

| Address Form | Register | Example | When Used |
|-------------|----------|---------|-----------|
| **Full title + last name** | Frozen-formal | "The Honorable Justice Roberts" | Legal, governmental, ceremonial |
| **Professional title + last name** | Formal | "Dr. Patel," "Professor Chen," "Senator Williams" | Professional, academic, political |
| **Courtesy title + last name** | Formal-consultative | "Mr. Okonkwo," "Ms. Rodriguez," "Mrs. Kim" | Service encounters, new professional relationships, formal correspondence |
| **First name** | Consultative-casual | "Sarah," "James," "Priya" | Workplace peers, acquaintances, social equals |
| **Shortened first name** | Casual | "Sare" (Sarah), "Jim" (James), "Mike" (Michael) | Friends, close colleagues |
| **Nickname** | Casual-intimate | "Mikey," "Sare-bear," "J" | Close friends, family |
| **Terms of endearment** | Intimate | "honey," "babe," "sweetheart," "love" | Romantic partners, parent to child, very close family |
| **In-group terms** | Casual (within group) | "dude," "bro," "mate," "y'all," "man" | Peer solidarity markers |

### 7.2 Professional Title + Last Name

**When to use:** Default for people with earned professional titles, especially in first interactions.

- **Dr. [Last Name]:** Medical doctors, PhDs, EdDs. "Dr. Patel" is always safe for either a physician or a professor with a doctorate.
- **Professor [Last Name]:** University professors. This is the safest default in academic settings because it works whether they have a PhD or not.
- **Mr./Ms./Mrs. [Last Name]:** General courtesy titles. "Ms." is the safe default for women when you don't know marital status preference.

**Key rules:**
1. When in doubt, use the more formal title
2. Wait to be invited to use a less formal address
3. If someone says "please, call me [first name]," switch immediately -- continuing to use their title after they've invited first-name use can seem standoffish
4. In email, match the sign-off they use. If they sign "Sarah," you can address them as "Sarah" next time

### 7.3 First Name

First-name address signals that you and the other person are on roughly equal social footing, or at least that the relationship has moved past initial formality.

**When it's appropriate:**
- After being explicitly invited ("Call me Sarah")
- Among workplace peers at the same level
- In social settings among adults of similar age
- When introduced by first name ("This is Sarah, she works in marketing")

**When it's NOT appropriate:**
- With someone significantly senior to you (until invited)
- With professors (unless invited)
- With your partner's parents (until invited)
- In formal ceremonies or proceedings

### 7.4 Nicknames and Diminutives

Nicknames signal closeness and in-group membership. They come in several types:

| Type | Example | Signals |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Shortened name** | "Mike" (Michael), "Jen" (Jennifer), "Alex" (Alexander) | Casual familiarity; most people have one and it's widely used |
| **Affectionate diminutive** | "Mikey," "Jenny," "Lexi" | Closer relationship than the shortened form; often childhood-origin |
| **Unique nickname** | "Red" (for hair color), "Ace," "Doc" | In-group history; usually has a story behind it |
| **Family nickname** | "Buddy," "Peanut," "Munchkin" | Family intimacy; deeply inappropriate outside the family |

**Critical rule:** Do NOT use someone's nickname unless they (or people who know them well) use it first. Using a nickname you weren't invited to use presumes intimacy that doesn't exist.

**Example:**
- Someone introduces themselves: "Hi, I'm Michael."
- You can call them: "Michael"
- Later, others call them: "Mike"
- You can now call them: "Mike"
- Much later, close friends call them: "Mikey"
- You should NOT call them "Mikey" unless you're also a close friend and they've used it with you

### 7.5 Terms of Endearment

These are the most intimate address forms and are restricted to specific relationships:

| Term | Appropriate Context | Inappropriate Context |
|------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| "Honey," "babe," "sweetheart" | Romantic partner, parent to young child | Workplace (to anyone), stranger, acquaintance |
| "Dear" | Partner, elderly family member, some close friendships | Workplace (can sound condescending) |
| "Love" (British English) | Wide range -- shopkeepers, friends, family (UK) | Less common in American English outside intimate contexts |
| "Sweetie," "hun" | Close female friends (in some communities) | Cross-gender in workplace (reads as condescending or harassing) |

**The workplace problem:** Terms of endearment in the workplace are almost always inappropriate, regardless of intent. Research shows that when used in professional settings:
- They signal power imbalance (the person using them claims familiarity they haven't earned)
- Women who are called "honey" or "sweetie" at work report feeling that others perceive them as less competent
- They can constitute harassment, especially across gender lines or from supervisor to subordinate
- Even well-intentioned use ("honey" as warmth) is frequently experienced as condescending

**Regional and cultural exceptions:** In some parts of the American South and in British English, "dear," "love," "hun," and "sweetie" are used broadly in casual service encounters (a waitress calling everyone "hon"). These are regional register conventions, not personal intimacy markers. But they can still be misread across cultural contexts.

### 7.6 In-Group Terms

In-group address terms signal peer solidarity -- "we are the same kind of person, in the same social group."

| Term | Primary Context | Register | Notes |
|------|----------------|----------|-------|
| **"Dude"** | Peer solidarity, casual surprise | Casual | Originally male-to-male, now widely gender-neutral among younger speakers. "Dude!" as exclamation. Signals nonchalant solidarity. |
| **"Bro"** | Male friendship, gym/sports culture, casual address | Casual | Signals casual masculine-coded solidarity. "Bro" marks membership in peer-oriented groups. Increasingly used gender-neutrally. |
| **"Mate"** | British/Australian English, peer address | Casual | "Cheers, mate." Broad solidarity marker. Can be used with strangers in casual settings. |
| **"Y'all"** | American Southern English, increasingly nationwide | Casual | "Hey y'all." Group address, warm, inclusive. Now used nationally as a gender-neutral group address. |
| **"Guys"** | General group address | Casual-consultative | "Hey guys." Still dominant in many English varieties, though some view it as male-biased. |
| **"Folks"** | Inclusive group address | Consultative-casual | "Hey folks." More deliberately inclusive alternative to "guys." Slightly warmer than "everyone." |
| **"Man"** | Casual exclamation or address | Casual | "Man, that was intense." / "Come on, man." Can be used gender-neutrally in some contexts. |

**Key principle:** In-group terms signal that you see the other person as a peer. Using them with someone above you in hierarchy is risky -- "dude" to your CEO in a formal meeting would be a serious register violation. Using them with someone below you can signal warmth and break down hierarchy intentionally.

### 7.7 The Asymmetric Address Problem

**This is one of the most confusing address situations:** When someone addresses you by a more casual form than you can use for them.

**Examples:**
- Your professor calls you "Taylor" but you must call them "Professor Chen"
- Your boss's boss calls you "James" but you call them "Ms. Whitfield"
- A doctor calls you by first name but you call them "Dr. Patel"
- Your partner's father calls you "sweetie" but you call him "Mr. Williams"

**Why this happens:** The person with more power/status has the right to set the register level. They can choose to address you casually because their position of power means casual address doesn't undermine their authority. You can't make the same choice upward because it could be read as presumptuous or disrespectful.

**How to navigate it:**
1. Accept the asymmetry as normal -- it's not unfair, it's just how power distance works in language
2. If you're unsure, stay with the more formal form
3. If they explicitly say "call me [first name]," do so
4. If the asymmetry makes you uncomfortable, you can ask: "Would it be alright if I called you [first name]?" -- but be prepared for them to say no, and don't be offended

**When asymmetric address signals a problem:**

In equitable professional environments, many managers are moving toward symmetric first-name address with their teams. If someone insists on being called by their title while using your first name, and this creates discomfort, it might signal:
- Cultural background where hierarchy is strongly marked in language
- A personality that emphasizes authority
- An organizational culture that is very hierarchical

None of these are inherently problematic, but if the asymmetry is being used to belittle or intimidate, that's a power abuse issue.

**The equity dimension:** Research shows that in academic settings, women and people of color are more likely to have their titles dropped (students calling them by first name when they should use "Professor" or "Dr."), while their white male counterparts are more likely to receive the correct title. This makes the address form system not just a register issue but an equity issue. If you're unsure about someone's title, using the formal form (Professor, Dr.) is always the respectful choice.

## SYNTHESIS: Key Principles for Register Navigation

### The Register Decision Algorithm

When entering any communication situation, run through these questions:

1. **Who am I talking to?** (Power distance: above me, same level, below me?)
2. **Where am I?** (Setting: formal space, casual space, digital platform?)
3. **What is our relationship?** (Stranger, acquaintance, colleague, friend, intimate?)
4. **What is the purpose?** (Request, inform, socialize, persuade, comfort?)
5. **What channel am I using?** (Email, Slack, text, face-to-face, phone?)
6. **What register are they using?** (Match or go slightly more formal)

### The Most Important Rule

**When in doubt, go one register level more formal than you think is necessary.** It is always easier and less costly to relax from formal to casual than to recover from being too casual. Being "too formal" reads as polite and careful. Being "too casual" reads as rude, clueless, or disrespectful.

### The Second Most Important Rule

**Match the register of the person you're talking to, especially if they have more power.** If your boss sends casual Slack messages, you can be casual back. If your boss sends formal emails, match that formality. The person with more power sets the register, and you follow.

### Register Mismatch Costs

| You're too... | The cost |
|--------------|----------|
| **Too formal** for the context | You seem stiff, distant, cold, or nervous |
| **Too casual** for the context | You seem rude, disrespectful, clueless, or unprofessional |
| **Too formal** for the relationship | You seem like you're creating distance, or you don't feel close |
| **Too casual** for the relationship | You seem presumptuous, like you're claiming intimacy that doesn't exist |

Of these four, "too casual" is almost always the more costly error. This is why the "when in doubt, go more formal" rule is so important.
