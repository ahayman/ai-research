# Foundational Research: Trauma, Recovery, and Trauma-Informed Care Frameworks

**Purpose.** This document compiles the empirical bedrock for the "recovery" and "self-protection" strand of the Pragmatics Learning Hub's manipulation category. It is a research compilation, not a teaching document. Downstream pages will only recommend strategies/treatments that trace back to sources gathered here.

**Scope.** Validated frameworks for understanding trauma from interpersonal manipulation, coercive control, and emotional abuse; the established models of recovery; the evidence base (and limitations) of the major trauma treatments; the autism-specific research landscape; and the claims that are NOT well supported.

**Methodological stance.** For each framework, treatment, or claim, the document notes (a) the primary sources, (b) the strength of empirical support, (c) known critiques, and (d) autism-specific considerations where literature exists. Claims lacking empirical support are flagged explicitly in the final section so they are not carried forward into downstream content.

**Date of compilation.** 2026-04-09.

## 1. Judith Herman and the Foundations of Complex Trauma

### 1.1 Primary source

Herman, J. L. (1992). *Trauma and Recovery: The Aftermath of Violence—From Domestic Abuse to Political Terror*. New York: Basic Books. Revised editions 1997 and 2015 (the 2015 edition adds an epilogue addressing three decades of subsequent research).

Open-access PDF of the book text is available via the Complex Trauma UK archive (https://www.complextrauma.uk/uploads/2/3/9/4/23949705/trauma_and_recovery.pdf).

### 1.2 Core contributions

**(a) Naming captivity-based trauma.** Herman's central argument was that trauma arising from prolonged, repeated interpersonal subjugation (domestic abuse, prisoners of war, political prisoners, childhood abuse, trafficking, hostage-taking) differs categorically from acute single-incident trauma (motor vehicle accidents, natural disasters, one-off assaults). She argued that the diagnostic category of PTSD as it existed in DSM-III/IV failed to capture the full clinical picture of captivity survivors.

**(b) Proposing Complex PTSD (then "DESNOS").** Herman's 1992 work proposed a diagnostic extension she originally called "Complex PTSD." In the DSM-IV field trials this was labeled "Disorders of Extreme Stress, Not Otherwise Specified" (DESNOS). Though DESNOS was not adopted into DSM-IV as a standalone diagnosis, Herman's framing directly shaped the ICD-11 Complex PTSD diagnosis adopted in 2018 (see Section 3).

**(c) The three-stage recovery model.** Herman proposed that recovery from captivity-based trauma proceeds through three non-linear stages:

1. **Safety** — establishing physical safety, bodily integrity, a stable environment, control over self-care, reducing self-destructive behaviors. Herman argues safety must precede any meaningful trauma processing. This stage may take weeks, months, or years depending on circumstances.
2. **Remembrance and mourning** — reconstructing the trauma story in a safe relational context, putting words and affect to what happened, grieving what was lost. This corresponds to processing-phase therapy.
3. **Reconnection** — rebuilding a sense of self, re-engaging with ordinary life, reconstructing meaning, recommitting to relationships and purpose.

Herman emphasizes the model is cyclical, not linear: survivors often revisit earlier stages.

**(d) Linking manipulation/coercion to trauma.** Herman's sixth chapter ("Captivity") explicitly describes the psychological mechanisms of coercive control: isolation, monopolization of perception, induced debility, occasional indulgences, demonstrations of omnipotence, enforcement of trivial demands. She cites Amnesty International's torture literature and Biderman's chart of coercion from Korean War POW research (Biderman, A. D. (1957). Communist attempts to elicit false confessions from Air Force prisoners of war. *Bulletin of the New York Academy of Medicine*, 33(9), 616–625). This link between interrogation/torture psychology and domestic abuse is a load-bearing element of the framework.

**(e) Safety before processing.** Herman's insistence that trauma processing is contraindicated in ongoing unsafe conditions has become a cornerstone of trauma-informed practice. Premature processing while the survivor is still in danger (or dysregulated without stabilization skills) is associated with destabilization.

### 1.3 Influence on trauma-informed care

Herman's work is widely cited as the conceptual ancestor of the phase-based approach later codified by Cloitre and colleagues in the 2012 ISTSS expert consensus guidelines (see Section 3.3). The SAMHSA trauma-informed care principles (Section 4) also draw on Herman's emphasis on safety, trust, and empowerment.

### 1.4 Critiques and refinements

- **Phase-based debate.** The assumption that phase-based treatment (safety → processing → reconnection) is necessary has been challenged by RCT evidence showing that direct trauma-focused therapies (PE, CPT) can be effective for many complex trauma survivors without a prolonged stabilization phase. The updated 2018/2019 ISTSS PTSD Prevention and Treatment Guidelines do not explicitly mandate sequential phase-based treatment, moving instead to a more individualized framing (Bisson et al., 2019; see https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31283056/).
- **Some evidence suggests stabilization-only treatment does not outperform waitlist in terms of PTSD symptoms and has high dropout rates (~50%). See the systematic review and meta-analysis by Melton et al. on stabilisation/phase-oriented treatment: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2468749922000539.
- **Herman's framework was developed pre-neuroimaging** and makes few specific neurobiological claims, which is arguably a strength (it has aged well compared to more neurobiology-heavy popular works — see Section 2).

### 1.5 Related Herman sources

- Herman, J. L. (1998). Recovery from psychological trauma. *Psychiatry and Clinical Neurosciences*, 52(S1), S145–S150. https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1046/j.1440-1819.1998.0520s5S145.x — a condensed re-statement of the three-stage framework.
- Herman, J. L. (2023). *Truth and Repair: How Trauma Survivors Envision Justice*. Basic Books. — Herman's more recent work on survivor-defined justice.

**Evidence-quality note.** Herman's framework is a clinical-theoretical model. It has generative empirical descendants (the ICD-11 CPTSD diagnosis, phase-based treatment literature) but the three-stage model itself has not been directly tested as a unit in RCTs. It is best understood as a well-validated heuristic that has structured the field.

## 2. Bessel van der Kolk and the Neurobiological Turn

### 2.1 Primary source

Van der Kolk, B. A. (2014). *The Body Keeps the Score: Brain, Mind, and Body in the Healing of Trauma*. New York: Viking.

### 2.2 Van der Kolk's research contributions (peer-reviewed)

Separate from the popular book, van der Kolk is an author on legitimate peer-reviewed work:
- Van der Kolk, B. A. (1994). The body keeps the score: Memory and the evolving psychobiology of posttraumatic stress. *Harvard Review of Psychiatry*, 1(5), 253–265.
- Van der Kolk, B. A., et al. (2007). A randomized clinical trial of EMDR, fluoxetine, and pill placebo in the treatment of posttraumatic stress disorder. *Journal of Clinical Psychiatry*, 68(1), 37–46.
- Van der Kolk, B. A., et al. (2014). Yoga as an adjunctive treatment for posttraumatic stress disorder: A randomized controlled trial. *Journal of Clinical Psychiatry*, 75(6), e559–e565.

These contributions include RCT evidence that EMDR is effective for adult-onset trauma, and preliminary evidence that yoga may reduce PTSD symptoms as an adjunct.

### 2.3 Empirically supported elements in *The Body Keeps the Score*

- **The general observation that trauma has somatic correlates** (autonomic arousal, altered interoception, startle response) is uncontroversial.
- **The limitation of talk-only approaches for some trauma presentations** — e.g., re-experiencing phenomena that do not respond to narrative-only approaches — is consistent with the broader literature supporting multi-modal treatments.
- **The book helped popularize trauma-informed care** and move PTSD discourse out of the narrow Vietnam-veteran framing into a more general framework applicable to childhood trauma, domestic violence, and complex trauma.

### 2.4 Significant critiques (important to include in any recommendation)

The book's scientific accuracy has been challenged. Sources compiled below.

- **Cambridge editorial evaluating the book's claims.** A *BJPsych Bulletin* article, "Evaluating evidence behind popular trauma narratives: neurobiological and treatment claims in *The Body Keeps the Score*" (https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/bjpsych-bulletin/article/evaluating-evidence-behind-popular-trauma-narratives-neurobiological-and-treatment-claims-in-the-body-keeps-the-score/5DE000F0254747495B1CAFF4051C3B75), concluded that several central neurobiological and treatment claims exceed the available evidence.
- **Research on Social Work Practice (2023) editorial** criticized van der Kolk and Peter Levine for "regularly ignor[ing], misrepresent[ing], and sometimes veer[ing] into or close to pseudoscience when it comes to the scientific knowledge base of PTSD treatment," and for promoting therapies with limited to no evidence over evidence-based therapies (CBT, PE, CPT, EMDR).
- **Richard McNally (2005)** in *Canadian Journal of Psychiatry* described van der Kolk's 1994 paper's reasoning as "mistaken" and called the broader theory "plague[d] by conceptual and empirical problems."
- **Citation distortion.** Multiple researchers whose work is cited in *The Body Keeps the Score* have stated their findings are misrepresented in the book (see discussion summaries on https://theconversation.com/the-body-keeps-the-score-how-a-bestselling-book-helps-us-understand-trauma-but-inflates-the-definition-of-it-184735).
- **Specific claims flagged as pseudoscientific or oversold:** the triune brain model (neuroscientifically obsolete), certain polyvagal theory claims (contested), mirror neurons as applied to trauma (overinterpreted), and the idea that talking therapies are categorically inadequate (contradicted by the CPT/PE evidence base).
- **Institutional context.** Van der Kolk was dismissed in 2018 from the Trauma Center at Justice Resource Institute (which he founded) after an internal investigation into his management; this is relevant only insofar as it affects how authoritative his institutional voice is positioned.

### 2.5 Wikipedia overview (for basic orientation)

- Bessel van der Kolk: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bessel_van_der_Kolk
- The Body Keeps the Score: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Body_Keeps_the_Score

### 2.6 How this research compilation should treat van der Kolk

Use *The Body Keeps the Score* for what it is: a widely-read popular introduction to trauma that helped legitimize trauma-informed care and expanded lay understanding of somatic dimensions of trauma. Do **not** cite it as a scientific authority for specific neurobiological claims (polyvagal theory details, triune brain, specific somatic treatment efficacy). For those, cite primary literature. In downstream pages, where van der Kolk is cited, pair the citation with the caveat that his specific treatment recommendations exceed the peer-reviewed evidence base in several places.

## 3. Complex PTSD: The ICD-11 Diagnosis and Its Evidence Base

### 3.1 ICD-11 diagnostic criteria (effective 2018; WHO adopted 2019; enforceable 2022)

Complex PTSD (6B41) was added to the ICD-11 as a sibling diagnosis to PTSD (6B40). It is defined as a disorder that may develop following exposure to an event or series of events of an extremely threatening or horrific nature, most commonly prolonged or repetitive events from which escape is difficult or impossible (torture, slavery, genocide campaigns, prolonged domestic violence, repeated childhood sexual or physical abuse).

To meet criteria, the individual must have all three core PTSD symptom clusters:
1. **Re-experiencing** the trauma in the present (intrusive memories, flashbacks, nightmares).
2. **Avoidance** of trauma reminders.
3. **Persistent sense of current threat** (hypervigilance, exaggerated startle).

**Plus** all three "Disturbances in Self-Organization" (DSO) clusters:
4. **Affective dysregulation** — heightened emotional reactivity, violent outbursts, reckless or self-destructive behavior, dissociative symptoms under stress, or emotional numbing and inability to experience pleasure.
5. **Negative self-concept** — persistent beliefs of oneself as diminished, defeated, or worthless, accompanied by deep shame, guilt, or failure related to the stressor.
6. **Disturbances in relationships** — persistent difficulties in sustaining relationships and feeling close to others, avoidance or disinterest in relationships and social engagement.

**Key distinction from PTSD:** Only CPTSD requires the DSO cluster. A person with only the PTSD clusters (re-experiencing, avoidance, current threat) receives the PTSD diagnosis.

### 3.2 Supporting literature and measurement

- **Primary validation paper:** Karatzias, T., et al. (2017). Evidence of distinct profiles of Posttraumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) and Complex Posttraumatic Stress Disorder (CPTSD) based on the new ICD-11 Trauma Questionnaire (ICD-TQ). *Journal of Affective Disorders*, 207, 181–187.
- **International Trauma Questionnaire (ITQ).** Cloitre, M., et al. (2018). The International Trauma Questionnaire: Development of a self-report measure of ICD-11 PTSD and complex PTSD. *Acta Psychiatrica Scandinavica*, 138(6), 536–546. — the standard self-report measure.
- **ITQ DSO validation:** Hyland, P., et al. (2018). A psychometric assessment of Disturbances in Self-Organization symptom indicators for ICD-11 Complex PTSD using the International Trauma Questionnaire. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5774393/.
- **Latent class studies confirming CPTSD is empirically distinct from PTSD and BPD:** Brewin, C. R., et al. (2017). A review of current evidence regarding the ICD-11 proposals for diagnosing PTSD and complex PTSD. *Clinical Psychology Review*, 58, 1–15. And: Jowett, S., et al. (2019). Differentiating symptom profiles of ICD-11 PTSD, complex PTSD, and borderline personality disorder. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31259603/.
- **Cambridge review article:** Brewin, C. R., et al. (2022). Complex post-traumatic stress disorder: A new diagnosis in ICD-11. *BJPsych Advances*. https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/bjpsych-advances/article/complex-posttraumatic-stress-disorder-a-new-diagnosis-in-icd11/2977140CBDAAF402610715BB609F688C.
- **US VA National Center for PTSD overview:** https://www.ptsd.va.gov/professional/treat/essentials/complex_ptsd.asp.
- **Systematic review of CPTSD assessment:** Sarr et al. (2024). *Clinical Psychology & Psychotherapy*. https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/cpp.3012.

### 3.3 ISTSS 2012 expert consensus on CPTSD treatment

Cloitre, M., Courtois, C. A., Ford, J. D., Green, B. L., Alexander, P., Briere, J., Herman, J. L., Lanius, R., Stolbach, B. C., Spinazzola, J., Van der Kolk, B. A., & Van der Hart, O. (2012). *The ISTSS Expert Consensus Treatment Guidelines for Complex PTSD in Adults*. International Society for Traumatic Stress Studies. https://terrorvictimresponse.ca/wp-content/uploads/ISTSS-Expert-Concesnsus-Guidelines-for-Complex-PTSD-Updated-060315.pdf.

The 2012 consensus endorsed a **phase-based approach** (stabilisation → trauma memory processing → reintegration) by 84% expert agreement, explicitly echoing Herman's structure.

**Updated 2019 ISTSS guidelines.** Bisson, J. I., et al. (2019). The International Society for Traumatic Stress Studies New Guidelines for the Prevention and Treatment of Posttraumatic Stress Disorder. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31283056/. The 2019 guidelines take a more individualized position: phase-based treatment is one option, but the evidence does not mandate it for all CPTSD cases. See also the 2018 ISTSS Position Paper on Complex PTSD: https://aztrauma.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/01/ISTSS_CPTSD-Position-Paper-Adults_FNL.pdf.

### 3.4 CPTSD in autistic populations

See Section 8 for detailed coverage. In brief: research on CPTSD prevalence and presentation in autistic adults is thin; standard ITQ validation samples have not specifically reported autism prevalence within them; autism-specific treatment adaptations are under-researched.

## 4. SAMHSA Trauma-Informed Care

### 4.1 Primary document

Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA). (2014). *SAMHSA's Concept of Trauma and Guidance for a Trauma-Informed Approach*. HHS Publication No. (SMA) 14-4884. Rockville, MD: SAMHSA. https://library.samhsa.gov/product/samhsas-concept-trauma-and-guidance-trauma-informed-approach/sma14-4884.

Infographic version: https://www.samhsa.gov/resource/dbhis/infographic-6-guiding-principles-trauma-informed-approach.

### 4.2 The Four R's

A program, organization, or system that is trauma-informed:
1. **Realizes** the widespread impact of trauma and understands potential paths for recovery.
2. **Recognizes** the signs and symptoms of trauma in clients, families, staff, and others involved with the system.
3. **Responds** by fully integrating knowledge about trauma into policies, procedures, and practices.
4. **Resists re-traumatization** of clients and staff.

See: https://libguides.up.edu/tiep/four-rs.

### 4.3 The Six Key Principles

1. **Safety** — physical and psychological safety of clients and staff.
2. **Trustworthiness and transparency** — decisions are made transparently, with the goal of building and maintaining trust.
3. **Peer support** — using survivors' lived experience to promote recovery and healing.
4. **Collaboration and mutuality** — leveling power differences; partnership over hierarchy in decision-making.
5. **Empowerment, voice, and choice** — prioritizing client/staff agency, recognizing strengths, providing real choices.
6. **Cultural, historical, and gender issues** — actively moving past cultural stereotypes and biases, addressing historical trauma, offering gender-responsive services.

See: https://opentextbc.ca/peersupport/chapter/samhsas-six-principles-of-trauma-informed-care/.

### 4.4 2023 update

SAMHSA published the *Practical Guide for Implementing a Trauma-Informed Approach* in 2023: https://library.samhsa.gov/product/practical-guide-implementing-trauma-informed-approach/pep23-06-05-005. This provides organizational implementation guidance but does not revise the core Four R / Six Principles framework.

### 4.5 Evidence-quality note and critiques

The SAMHSA framework is an **organizational practice framework**, not a clinical treatment protocol. It is explicitly consensus-based, drawing on expert panels and survivor input, rather than derived from RCTs. Its strength is that it codifies widely-endorsed values (safety, choice, transparency) that are consistent with what trauma survivors report as helpful. Its limitation is that "trauma-informed care" as a construct has been criticized for being vague, non-specific, and sometimes invoked without operational implementation (see e.g., Hanson, R. F., & Lang, J. (2016). A critical look at trauma-informed care among agencies and systems serving maltreated youth and their families. *Child Maltreatment*, 21(2), 95–100).

For the Pragmatics Hub, SAMHSA's Six Principles are most useful as a **values framework** and as a way to describe what a survivor should look for in professional services, rather than as a set of evidence-based techniques per se.

### 4.6 How trauma-informed care differs from traditional approaches

Traditional: "What is wrong with you?" (symptom-focused, pathologizing)
Trauma-informed: "What happened to you?" (context-focused, recognizes symptoms as adaptations)

Traditional: expert-directed, compliance-oriented.
Trauma-informed: collaborative, choice-preserving, explicitly structured to avoid replicating the power dynamics of abuse.

## 5. Validated Trauma Treatments: Evidence Base and Limitations

### 5.1 Summary comparison table

| Treatment | Primary developer(s) | Evidence class | Best-evidenced for | Major guidelines endorsement | Autism-specific evidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT)** | Patricia Resick | **Strong (first-line)** — multiple RCTs, meta-analyses | Adult PTSD, including military and sexual assault survivors | APA, ISTSS, NICE, VA/DoD, Phoenix Australia | Very limited; case reports only |
| **Prolonged Exposure (PE)** | Edna Foa | **Strong (first-line)** — multiple RCTs, meta-analyses | Adult PTSD, single-incident and complex presentations | APA, ISTSS, NICE, VA/DoD, Phoenix Australia | Very limited; emerging case series |
| **EMDR** | Francine Shapiro | **Strong** — 30+ RCTs; 2024 IPD meta-analysis shows equivalence to other trauma-focused therapies | Adult PTSD | Most international guidelines as first-line (including WHO) | Emerging — some evidence EMDR may be tolerated in autistic adults |
| **TF-CBT** | Cohen, Mannarino, Deblinger | **Strong (first-line for children)** — 10+ RCTs, including independent replications | Children and adolescents with PTSD | APA, ISTSS, NICE, NCTSN | Some adapted case work; no RCTs specific to autism |
| **STAIR / STAIR-NT** | Marylène Cloitre | **Moderate-to-strong for complex trauma** — 3 RCTs | Complex PTSD, childhood abuse survivors, emotion regulation deficits | Supported in ISTSS CPTSD consensus | None specific to autism |
| **Somatic Experiencing** | Peter Levine | **Weak/emerging** — very few RCTs, small samples, methodological concerns | Proposed for PTSD + somatic complaints | Not included as first-line in major guidelines | None |
| **Internal Family Systems (IFS)** | Richard Schwartz | **Emerging** — 1 completed RCT (PARTS, 2023), pilot studies | Complex trauma, adjunct to trauma-processing work | Listed as "promising" — not endorsed as first-line | None specific to autism |
| **Yoga (trauma-sensitive)** | Van der Kolk et al. (adaptation) | **Weak/emerging adjunct** — one RCT | Adjunctive symptom reduction | Not first-line; adjunct only | None |

### 5.2 EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing)

**Primary sources:**
- Shapiro, F. (1989). Efficacy of the eye movement desensitization procedure in the treatment of traumatic memories. *Journal of Traumatic Stress*, 2(2), 199–223.
- Shapiro, F. (2001). *Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing: Basic Principles, Protocols, and Procedures* (2nd ed.). New York: Guilford Press.

**Current state-of-the-science review:**
- De Jongh, A., et al. (2024). State of the science: Eye movement desensitization and reprocessing (EMDR) therapy. *Journal of Traumatic Stress*. https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/jts.23012.

**Key meta-analyses:**
- Wright, S. L., et al. (2024). EMDR v. other psychological therapies for PTSD: A systematic review and individual participant data meta-analysis. *Psychological Medicine*. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38173121/. Conclusion: no significant difference between EMDR and other trauma-focused therapies in symptom reduction, response, remission, or dropout.
- Simpson, E., et al. (2025). Clinical and cost-effectiveness of EMDR for treatment and prevention of PTSD in adults. *British Journal of Psychology*. https://bpspsychub.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/bjop.70005. Finding: certainty of evidence vs. waitlist is rated low to very low; long-term effects unknown.

**Theoretical framework:** Shapiro's Adaptive Information Processing (AIP) model. The specific active mechanism (bilateral eye movements, working-memory taxation) remains contested; dismantling studies suggest much of the effect may be attributable to repeated exposure to the memory in a safe context.

**Contraindications:** active psychosis, uncontrolled dissociative disorders without stabilization, acute suicide risk, severe unmanaged substance use.

**Autism-specific considerations:** a small literature suggests EMDR may be tolerable for autistic individuals because it is less verbal than CPT or standard talk therapy. Formal efficacy data in autistic samples is limited; most support comes from case reports.

### 5.3 Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT)

**Primary sources:**
- Resick, P. A., & Schnicke, M. K. (1992). Cognitive processing therapy for sexual assault victims. *Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology*, 60(5), 748–756.
- Resick, P. A., Monson, C. M., & Chard, K. M. (2017). *Cognitive Processing Therapy for PTSD: A Comprehensive Manual*. New York: Guilford Press.

**State-of-the-science review:**
- Resick, P. A., et al. *State of the Science of Cognitive Processing Therapy*. https://www.ptsd.va.gov/professional/articles/article-pdf/id1634266.pdf.

**Key meta-analyses:**
- Asmundson, G. J. G., et al. (2019). A meta-analytic review of cognitive processing therapy for adults with posttraumatic stress disorder. *Cognitive Behaviour Therapy*, 48(1), 1–14. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30332919/.
- Sager, Z. S., DeJesus, B., Kearns, J. C., Thompson-Hollands, J., Trendel, S. L., Mark, T. L., & Sloan, D. M. (2025). A meta-analytic review of CPT with and without the written account. *Journal of Anxiety Disorders*, 110, 102976. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S088761852500012X. Finding: CPT+A and CPT-C produce large reductions in PTSD symptoms with no significant difference between protocol versions.
- Shearing, V., et al. (2024). Allegiance effects in CPT for PTSD: A systematic review and meta-analysis. *Clinical Psychologist*. https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/13284207.2024.2347643.
- Cochrane-style NCBI review: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK362346/.

**Content focus:** CPT addresses five core "stuck point" domains: safety, trust, power/control, esteem, and intimacy. It is cognitive-restructuring-focused with optional written trauma account.

**Concerns review:** Larsen, S. E., et al. (2022). Clinician concerns about cognitive processing therapy. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1077722922001377.

**First-line endorsement:** APA, ISTSS, NICE, VA/DoD, Phoenix Australia.

### 5.4 Prolonged Exposure (PE)

**Primary sources:**
- Foa, E. B., Rothbaum, B. O., Riggs, D. S., & Murdock, T. B. (1991). Treatment of posttraumatic stress disorder in rape victims: A comparison between cognitive-behavioral procedures and counseling. *Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology*, 59(5), 715–723. — the first RCT.
- Foa, E. B., Hembree, E. A., Rothbaum, B. O., & Rauch, S. A. M. (2019). *Prolonged Exposure Therapy for PTSD: Emotional Processing of Traumatic Experiences — Therapist Guide* (2nd ed.). Oxford University Press.

**VA evidence summary:** https://www.ptsd.va.gov/professional/treat/txessentials/prolonged_exposure_pro.asp.

**Key outcome data:**
- Intent-to-treat: on average 53% of participants who initiate PE no longer meet PTSD criteria post-treatment; 68% among completers.
- Dropout: ~1 in 3 in general populations; up to 35–62% in PTSD with co-occurring substance use; as low as 13.6% in massed (10 sessions / 2 weeks) format.
- Dissemination analysis: Foa, E. B., et al. (2013). https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4338436/.
- "Do You Need To Talk About It?" Rauch et al.: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2770710/.
- Massed format RCT: Foa et al. (2018). https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5833566/.

**Contraindications/cautions:** active suicide risk requiring stabilization first, acute psychosis, intoxication during sessions. Historically concerns about "overwhelming" complex trauma survivors, but direct evidence suggests PE can be safe and effective for complex presentations when delivered competently.

### 5.5 Trauma-Focused Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (TF-CBT)

**Primary sources:**
- Cohen, J. A., Mannarino, A. P., & Deblinger, E. (2017). *Treating Trauma and Traumatic Grief in Children and Adolescents* (2nd ed.). New York: Guilford Press.

**Evidence reviews:**
- de Arellano, M. A. R., et al. (2014). Trauma-focused cognitive-behavioral therapy for children and adolescents: Assessing the evidence. *Psychiatric Services*, 65(5), 591–602. https://psychiatryonline.org/doi/10.1176/appi.ps.201300255. Rating: level of evidence high based on 10+ RCTs including three independent replications.
- Cary, C. E., & McMillen, J. C. (2012). The data behind the dissemination: A systematic review of trauma-focused cognitive behavioral therapy for use with children and youth. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4396183/.

**Components (PRACTICE):** Psychoeducation and Parenting skills, Relaxation, Affect modulation, Cognitive coping and processing, Trauma narrative, In-vivo mastery, Conjoint child-caregiver sessions, Enhancing safety.

**First-line endorsement:** APA, ISTSS, NICE, NCTSN for children and adolescents.

### 5.6 STAIR / STAIR-Narrative Therapy

**Primary sources:**
- Cloitre, M., Koenen, K. C., Cohen, L. R., & Han, H. (2002). Skills training in affective and interpersonal regulation followed by exposure: A phase-based treatment for PTSD related to childhood abuse. *Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology*, 70(5), 1067–1074.
- Cloitre, M., et al. (2010). Treatment for PTSD related to childhood abuse: A randomized controlled trial. *American Journal of Psychiatry*, 167(8), 915–924.
- Cloitre, M., Cohen, L. R., & Koenen, K. C. (2020). *Treating Survivors of Childhood Abuse and Interpersonal Trauma: STAIR Narrative Therapy* (2nd ed.). New York: Guilford Press.

**Recent systematic review:**
- Stiles, B. J., et al. (2024). Skills training in affective and interpersonal regulation (STAIR) for treating complex trauma: A systematic review. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2468749924001169.

**ISTSS Clinician's Corner overview (Cloitre):** https://istss.org/clinicians-corner-skills-training-in-affective-and-interpersonal-regulation-stair-marylene-cloitre-phd/.

**Enhanced STAIR (ESTAIR) for ICD-11 CPTSD:** Karatzias et al. (2023). https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3425/13/9/1300.

**Structure:** 16 sessions; 8 sessions of skills training (emotion awareness, soothing, distress tolerance, interpersonal schemas, assertiveness, flexibility) followed by 8 sessions of narrative processing.

**Rationale:** explicitly designed for individuals whose childhood trauma disrupted emotional and interpersonal developmental processes — precisely the population most relevant to captivity-based / coercive-control trauma.

### 5.7 Somatic Experiencing (SE)

**Primary source:**
- Levine, P. A. (1997). *Waking the Tiger: Healing Trauma*. Berkeley: North Atlantic Books.
- Levine, P. A. (2010). *In an Unspoken Voice: How the Body Releases Trauma and Restores Goodness*. Berkeley: North Atlantic Books.

**Evidence base:**
- Scoping review: Kuhfuß, M., et al. (2021). Somatic experiencing — effectiveness and key factors of a body-oriented trauma therapy: A scoping literature review. *European Journal of Psychotraumatology*. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8276649/. Finding: "Only few studies meet rigorous methodological criteria such as RCT design necessary for robust proof of efficacy and effectiveness… The current evidence base is weak and does not (yet) fully accomplish the high standards for clinical effectiveness research."
- Brief SE RCT (back pain + PTSD): Andersen, T. E., et al. (2017). https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5489867/.
- Wikipedia summary: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somatic_experiencing.

**Critical concerns.** SE is built on polyvagal-theory-adjacent claims that are themselves contested in the neuroscience literature. The 2023 *Research on Social Work Practice* editorial grouped Levine with van der Kolk as popularizing treatments with limited-to-no evidence over evidence-based alternatives.

**Status:** Not included as first-line in major PTSD treatment guidelines. Classified here as "emerging/weak evidence." Should be presented honestly in any recommendation context.

### 5.8 Internal Family Systems (IFS)

**Primary source:**
- Schwartz, R. C. (1995). *Internal Family Systems Therapy*. New York: Guilford Press.
- Schwartz, R. C., & Sweezy, M. (2019). *Internal Family Systems Therapy* (2nd ed.). New York: Guilford Press.

**Evidence:**
- Hodgdon, H. B., Anderson, F. G., Southwell, E., Hrubec, W., & Schwartz, R. (2021). Internal Family Systems (IFS) therapy for PTSD among survivors of multiple childhood trauma: A pilot effectiveness study. *Journal of Aggression, Maltreatment & Trauma*. https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10926771.2021.2013375. N=17 pilot; 92% of completers no longer met PTSD criteria at 16 weeks.
- PARTS RCT (first RCT of group IFS-derived treatment for PTSD): Cambridge Health Alliance (Harvard Medical School affiliate). Initial RCT published 2023. Details: https://ifs-institute.com/resources/research.
- Scoping review: (2025). Exploring the evidence for Internal Family Systems therapy: A scoping review. *Clinical Psychologist*. https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/13284207.2025.2533127. Conclusion: "promising" but limited high-quality evidence.

**Critiques:** Society for the Advancement of Psychotherapy critical piece: https://societyforpsychotherapy.org/internal-family-systems-exploring-its-problematic-popularity/ — argues the theoretical model (literal inner "parts" with personalities) risks reifying dissociative phenomena and may appeal to confirmation bias in clients.

**Status:** Classified as "emerging/promising" with one completed RCT and multiple pilot studies. Not yet established as first-line.

### 5.9 Note on treatment equivalence

A critical finding from the 2024 Wright et al. individual-participant-data meta-analysis (https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38173121/) is that **among evidence-based trauma-focused therapies (EMDR, CPT, PE, TF-CBT), direct comparisons generally show roughly equivalent outcomes**. This means fit, preference, accessibility, and therapist competence often matter more than choice of modality among first-line options.

See also: Cuijpers, P., et al. (2025). Psychological interventions for adult posttraumatic stress disorder: A systematic review of published meta-analyses. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0887618525000532.

## 6. Recovery Process Research

### 6.1 Stages of recovery (validated models)

Herman's three-stage model (Section 1) remains the most cited clinical framework. The empirical research does not directly validate the three stages as discrete phases but does support several component claims:

- **Safety first is supported** by evidence that trauma processing when a survivor is actively unsafe (e.g., still in the abusive relationship) has poorer outcomes.
- **Emotion regulation skills precede or accompany processing** — STAIR RCT evidence (Cloitre 2002, 2010) specifically supports this for childhood-abuse survivors.
- **Reintegration / reconnection** is less well operationalized in outcome research; see the expert survey on reintegration interventions: Roberts et al. (2022). Expert international trauma clinicians' views on the definition, composition and delivery of reintegration interventions for complex PTSD. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9848321/.

### 6.2 Post-traumatic growth (Tedeschi & Calhoun)

**Primary sources:**
- Tedeschi, R. G., & Calhoun, L. G. (1996). The Posttraumatic Growth Inventory: Measuring the positive legacy of trauma. *Journal of Traumatic Stress*, 9(3), 455–471. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8827649/.
- Tedeschi, R. G., & Calhoun, L. G. (2004). Posttraumatic growth: Conceptual foundations and empirical evidence. *Psychological Inquiry*, 15(1), 1–18. https://psycnet.apa.org/record/2004-11807-003.
- Calhoun, L. G., & Tedeschi, R. G. (Eds.). (2006). *Handbook of Posttraumatic Growth: Research and Practice*. Mahwah, NJ: Lawrence Erlbaum.

**The PTGI measures 5 factors:** New Possibilities, Relating to Others, Personal Strength, Spiritual Change, Appreciation of Life.

**What the evidence supports:**
- Some survivors do report sustained positive changes after trauma, measurable on the PTGI.
- Growth and distress can co-occur; reports of growth are not simply denial.
- PTG is associated with meaning-making, social support, and active coping rather than avoidance.

**Legitimate criticisms (important to include):**
- **Self-report bias:** much PTG research relies on retrospective self-report of change, which is weakly correlated with actual measured change over time. See Frazier, P., et al. (2009). Does self-reported posttraumatic growth reflect genuine positive change? *Psychological Science*, 20(7), 912–919. — found self-reported PTG was only weakly related to actual pre-post improvement.
- **Illusory growth:** some reported PTG may reflect motivated cognitive restructuring rather than actual change in well-being.
- **"What doesn't kill you makes you stronger" is overstated.** Average people after trauma are not, on balance, better off. PTG is a real phenomenon for some; it is not a universal trajectory or something to be prescribed.
- **Risk of pressuring survivors** to display growth, which can function as a form of toxic positivity (e.g., "silver lining" narratives imposed on victims of prolonged abuse).

**For the Pragmatics Hub:** present PTG as a possible outcome, not an expected one. Never frame recovery as requiring the survivor to find meaning or "grow" from what happened.

### 6.3 Social support

**Meta-analyses:**
- Wang, Y., Chung, M. C., Wang, N., Yu, X., & Kenardy, J. (2021). Social support and posttraumatic stress disorder: A meta-analysis of longitudinal studies. *Clinical Psychology Review*. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0272735821000416. Moderate cross-sectional association (r ≈ −0.26 between social support and PTSD severity). Reciprocal effects: social support buffers PTSD; PTSD erodes social support.
- Wang, Y., et al. (2022). Social support and posttraumatic growth: A meta-analysis. *Journal of Affective Disorders*. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0165032722011193.
- 2023 review: Bryant, R. A., et al. The role of social support in coping with psychological trauma. *Psychiatric Quarterly*. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9534006/.

**Critical nuance — not all support is helpful:**
- Negative social reactions to disclosure (victim-blaming, minimization, disbelief) have a *larger* effect size predicting PTSD severity than positive reactions have in reducing it. See the bidirectional-effects study at https://www.mdpi.com/2076-328X/14/4/284.
- Quality of support > quantity.
- Isolation impedes recovery, but forcing social contact with an unsupportive network can worsen outcomes.

### 6.4 Meaning-making

**Primary framework:**
- Park, C. L. (2010). Making sense of the meaning literature: An integrative review of meaning making and its effects on adjustment to stressful life events. *Psychological Bulletin*, 136(2), 257–301. — the most cited model.
- Park, C. L., & Folkman, S. (1997). Meaning in the context of stress and coping. *Review of General Psychology*, 1(2), 115–144.

**Key findings:**
- Trauma disrupts the fit between "situational meaning" (how the specific event is appraised) and "global meaning" (core assumptions about self and world).
- Recovery often involves "meanings made" — changes in the person's global beliefs, reattributions, or integration of the experience into identity.
- Greater reductions in discrepancy between situational and global meanings over time predict reduced PTSD symptoms.
- "Meaning-making" is distinct from finding silver linings; it includes acknowledging loss and developing a coherent account of what happened.

**Narrative identity literature:**
- McAdams, D. P. (2001). The psychology of life stories. *Review of General Psychology*, 5(2), 100–122.
- Recent review: Meaning making following trauma: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8984472/.

### 6.5 Self-compassion (Neff)

**Primary sources:**
- Neff, K. D. (2003). Self-compassion: An alternative conceptualization of a healthy attitude toward oneself. *Self and Identity*, 2(2), 85–101.
- Neff, K. D. (2023). Self-compassion: Theory, method, research, and intervention. *Annual Review of Psychology*, 74, 193–218. https://self-compassion.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/01/Neff-2023.pdf.
- Germer, C. K., & Neff, K. D. (2015). Cultivating self-compassion in trauma survivors. In V. Follette et al. (Eds.), *Mindfulness-oriented interventions for trauma*.

**Components (three dyads):** self-kindness vs. self-judgment; common humanity vs. isolation; mindfulness vs. over-identification.

**Evidence:**
- Meta-analyses support self-compassion's protective association with depression, anxiety, and PTSD symptoms.
- Systematic review: self-compassion is negatively associated with suicidal ideation and self-harm.
- Mindful Self-Compassion (MSC) program has RCT support for general well-being; specifically-trauma-tailored trials are more limited.
- Research hub: https://self-compassion.org/the-research/.

**Relevance to manipulation recovery:** victims of gaslighting and coercive control typically internalize deeply negative self-appraisals. Self-compassion interventions target precisely this internalized self-judgment.

### 6.6 Psychoeducation as stabilization component

- Melton et al. systematic review of stabilisation-phase treatment: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2468749922000539. Pooled effect size d=0.59 (all studies) reducing to d=0.42 in RCTs only. Moderate-to-small effect.
- Psychoeducation provides symptom explanation, normalization, and a conceptual frame for what is happening.
- Caveat: stabilization-only without subsequent processing has high dropout (~50%) and does not consistently outperform waitlist on PTSD symptoms in some analyses. Stabilization is best understood as preparation for processing, not a complete treatment for most cases.

### 6.7 "Healing" vs. "moving on"

There is no evidence-based definition of "healed from trauma." The clinical literature typically operationalizes recovery as:
- Symptom reduction below diagnostic threshold for PTSD/CPTSD,
- Restored functioning (work, relationships, self-care),
- Reduced subjective distress,
- Preserved capacity for affect, engagement, and meaning.

Language like "getting over it" is explicitly rejected in trauma-informed care because it implies trauma is a memory to discard rather than an experience to integrate. Herman and most clinical frameworks emphasize integration, not erasure.

## 7. Frameworks Specific to Recovery from Coercive Control and Gaslighting

### 7.1 Robin Stern — *The Gaslight Effect*

**Source:**
- Stern, R. (2007). *The Gaslight Effect: How to Spot and Survive the Hidden Manipulation Others Use to Control Your Life*. New York: Morgan Road Books. (Revised 2018.)
- Stern, R. (2023). *The Gaslight Effect Recovery Guide*. New York: Harmony.

**Author credentials:** Robin Stern, PhD, is a licensed psychoanalyst and co-founder / associate director of the Yale Center for Emotional Intelligence. She is co-creator of RULER, an evidence-based emotional intelligence curriculum used in schools.

**Evidence base of the gaslighting framework specifically:**
- *The Gaslight Effect* is based on Stern's clinical experience, not randomized trials or systematic data collection. The book popularized the term "gaslighting" in therapeutic contexts and provided a three-stage descriptive model (disbelief, defense, depression) but has not been empirically validated as a recovery protocol.
- Peer-reviewed academic literature on gaslighting as a distinct phenomenon is growing but remains small relative to the volume of popular discussion. Key academic sources:
  - Sweet, P. L. (2019). The sociology of gaslighting. *American Sociological Review*, 84(5), 851–875. — first sociological formal treatment.
  - Abramson, K. (2014). Turning up the lights on gaslighting. *Philosophical Perspectives*, 28(1), 1–30. — philosophical analysis.

**For the Pragmatics Hub:** Stern's framework is useful as a descriptive vocabulary and for validating survivors' experiences, but should be presented as a clinically-informed popular framework, not as an evidence-based treatment. The general recovery literature (CPT, STAIR, self-compassion) should be treated as the evidence base; Stern as orientation.

### 7.2 Lundy Bancroft — *Why Does He Do That?*

**Source:**
- Bancroft, L. (2002). *Why Does He Do That? Inside the Minds of Angry and Controlling Men*. New York: Berkley.
- Bancroft, L., & Silverman, J. G. (2002). *The Batterer as Parent: Addressing the Impact of Domestic Violence on Family Dynamics*. Thousand Oaks, CA: Sage.

**Background:** Bancroft co-directed Emerge, one of the first batterer-intervention programs in the US, and draws on over 15 years of clinical work with abusive men and interviews with their partners.

**Central thesis:** Abuse is driven by entitlement and chosen tactics, not by anger issues, addiction, mental illness, or loss of control. Abusers' behavior is strategic and goal-directed.

**Evidence status:** Bancroft's book is based on clinical experience and qualitative synthesis, not peer-reviewed empirical research. Its thesis is broadly consistent with (and influential on) the feminist/structural domestic-violence literature (e.g., Evan Stark's coercive control framework — Section 7.3) but has also been criticized from within the DV field.

**Criticisms:**
- Ananias Foundation (a batterer-intervention organization) does not recommend the book for those working with perpetrators, arguing it does not accurately capture underlying motivations for most individuals causing harm.
- Bancroft has publicly endorsed positions outside the DV field (e.g., anti-vaccination views) which some commentators argue undermine his authority as a public intellectual.
- The book risks essentializing abusers as a fixed type rather than recognizing the range of perpetrator typologies identified in research (e.g., Holtzworth-Munroe & Stuart typology).

**For survivors, the book's clearest validated contribution** is the framing "this is not about anger or stress — it is a strategic pattern of control," which aligns with Evan Stark's empirically grounded coercive control literature and helps survivors name what happened. For the Pragmatics Hub, use Bancroft as a popularly-accessible entry point with a pointer to the more rigorous coercive-control literature.

### 7.3 Evan Stark — Coercive Control

**Primary source:**
- Stark, E. (2007). *Coercive Control: How Men Entrap Women in Personal Life*. Oxford: Oxford University Press. (Updated 2nd edition 2023.)
- Stark, E., & Hester, M. (2019). Coercive control: Update and review. *Violence Against Women*, 25(1), 81–104. https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/1077801218816191.
- Stark, E. (2009). Rethinking coercive control. *Violence Against Women*, 15(12), 1509–1525. https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1077801209347452.

**Academic stature:** Stark is a sociologist and forensic social worker. With his wife Anne Flitcraft, MD, he co-directed the Yale Trauma Studies, which demonstrated domestic violence was the leading cause of injury for which women sought medical attention, and established the health-system case for treating DV as a public health issue.

**Core argument:** Domestic abuse is better understood as a liberty crime than as a violence crime. The defining feature is a pattern of controlling behaviors (isolation, economic abuse, sexual coercion, surveillance, monopolization of perception, enforcement of trivial demands) that deprive the victim of autonomy, personhood, and resources. Many of the tactics are legal in isolation; their criminal character emerges from the pattern.

**Policy impact:** Stark's framework directly shaped the UK's 2015 Serious Crime Act, which criminalized "controlling or coercive behaviour" in intimate or family relationships as a standalone offense. Similar legislation has followed in Scotland (2018), Ireland, and multiple Australian jurisdictions.

**Evidence-quality note:** Stark's framework is an **empirically informed sociological and legal framework**, not a clinical treatment model. It is the most rigorously developed academic account of what people refer to informally as "coercive control" and should be the primary citation for any claim about the *structure* of abusive control (as opposed to recovery from it).

### 7.4 "Cognitive emancipation" as a recovery concept

The term "cognitive emancipation" has been used in popular survivor-recovery discussions to describe the process of re-establishing epistemic autonomy (trusting one's own perceptions, memories, and judgments after gaslighting). It does **not** correspond to a named, empirically validated framework in the peer-reviewed trauma literature.

**Closest empirically validated concepts:**
- **Epistemic injustice** (Miranda Fricker, 2007, *Epistemic Injustice: Power and the Ethics of Knowing*) — philosophical framework for understanding how gaslighting wrongs people in their capacity as knowers.
- **CPT stuck-point work on power/control and trust domains** — directly addresses distorted beliefs about one's own judgment.
- **IFS "Self"-leadership and unburdening of "parts" that carry internalized abuser voices** — conceptually similar but with the evidence-base limits noted in Section 5.8.

For the Pragmatics Hub: if "cognitive emancipation" language is used at all, it should be presented as informal survivor-community vocabulary, not as a clinical framework.

## 8. Trauma Recovery in Populations with Pre-Existing Conditions (with emphasis on autism)

### 8.1 Prevalence of trauma in autistic populations

- 75% of autistic adults report having experienced physical or sexual abuse in their lifetime in some survey samples.
- A widely-cited figure: approximately 84% of autistic people experience multiple forms of adverse childhood experiences (ACEs).
- Parent-report population study: 28% of parents of autistic children report two or more ACEs vs. 18% in the general population.

**Sources:**
- Rumball, F., Happé, F., & Grey, N. (2020). Experience of trauma and PTSD symptoms in autistic adults: Risk of PTSD development following DSM-5 and non-DSM-5 traumatic life events. *Autism Research*, 13(12), 2122–2132.
- NCBI Bookshelf summary: Autism, adverse events, and trauma. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK573608/.
- PMC: Trauma and social adversity in autism. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11997697/.
- Quinton et al. (2025). Autistic traits in childhood and PTSD as young adults: A cohort study. *JCPP*. https://acamh.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/jcpp.14163.

### 8.2 PTSD prevalence estimates

- One 2021 survey of 687 autistic adults found 44% met PTSD criteria.
- A youth sample found 28% reported maltreatment but only 2.6% had a PTSD diagnosis — indicating probable diagnostic overshadowing.
- Approximate figures commonly cited: 14% of autistic children/adolescents, up to 44% of autistic adults, compared to general population estimates of 6.8–9.2% (adults) and ~7.8% (youth by age 18).

**Sources:**
- Autism Research Institute overview: https://autism.org/autism-and-trauma-research-updates/.
- Haruvi-Lamdan, N., Horesh, D., & Golan, O. (2018). PTSD and autism spectrum disorder: Co-morbidity, gaps in research, and potential shared mechanisms. *Psychological Trauma: Theory, Research, Practice, and Policy*, 10(3), 290–299.
- Peterson, J. L., et al. (2019). Trauma and autism spectrum disorder: Review, proposed treatment adaptations and future directions. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6901292/.

### 8.3 Diagnostic overshadowing

- **The core problem:** PTSD hyperarousal overlaps with autistic sensory hyperreactivity; PTSD emotional numbing/detachment overlaps with autistic social-emotional presentation; PTSD avoidance overlaps with autistic routine preference and sensory avoidance.
- Clinicians unfamiliar with autism may attribute trauma symptoms to autism itself ("that's just how they are") and miss treatable PTSD.
- Conversely, trauma exposure can *mimic* new-onset autistic traits in non-autistic individuals, complicating differential diagnosis.

**Sources:**
- Kildahl et al. (2020). "If we do not look for it, we do not see it": Clinicians' experiences and understanding of identifying PTSD in adults with autism and intellectual disability. https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jar.12734.
- National Elf Service summary: https://www.nationalelfservice.net/mental-health/ptsd/diagnostic-overshadowing-in-ptsd-and-autism-what-do-we-know-about-trauma-in-asd/.
- ISSTD News: Co-occurring autism and trauma, posttraumatic stress and dissociation. https://news.isst-d.org/co-occurring-autism-and-trauma-posttraumatic-stress-and-dissociation/.

### 8.4 Treatment adaptation research (very thin)

**Systematic review:**
- Quinton, A. M. G., Ali, D., Danese, A., Happé, F., & Rumball, F. (2024). The assessment and treatment of post-traumatic stress disorder in autistic people: A systematic review. *Review Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders*. [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40489-024-00430-9)

**Current state:**
- EMDR: some case-study and small-sample evidence suggesting tolerability in autistic individuals, possibly because the protocol is less dependent on verbal processing than CPT or PE.
- Standard trauma-focused CBT approaches may require adaptation for: sensory environment, pacing, concrete/visual language, interoceptive awareness differences (alexithymia), executive-function demands.
- **No RCTs of any major trauma treatment in an autism-specific sample as of this compilation.**
- Consensus: autism-informed adaptations are urgent but empirically under-supported.

**Treatment adaptation recommendations (expert consensus, not RCT-validated):**
- Extended psychoeducation and orientation.
- Concrete, structured materials; reduced abstraction; visual supports.
- Explicit attention to alexithymia and interoception (naming emotions, body-signal education).
- Sensory-informed session environment.
- Longer sessions or more frequent breaks.
- Slower pacing with explicit transition cues.
- Caregiver/support-person involvement where appropriate.

### 8.5 Misdiagnosis in both directions (critical safety point)

- **Trauma symptoms misread as autism:** a person with complex trauma may show social withdrawal, affect flattening, repetitive self-soothing behaviors, sensory avoidance. These can be mistaken for autism, leading to inappropriate "autism interventions" that miss the trauma.
- **Autism masks trauma:** autistic adults may not display trauma in expected ways (may not report nightmares in the same narrative way; may show increased stimming or shutdown rather than typical hyperarousal). Clinicians screening only for canonical PTSD presentations will miss this.
- **Alexithymia compounds both problems.** Autistic individuals with alexithymia (estimated 50% of autistic people) may not be able to report internal emotional states in ways that match standard assessment instruments.

**Implications for the Pragmatics Hub:** self-assessment resources and referral pathways should explicitly acknowledge these overlaps. Directing an autistic survivor to a non-autism-informed trauma therapist carries real risk of misdiagnosis and poor outcome.

### 8.6 Related populations with pre-existing conditions

- **ADHD + trauma:** executive function impairments interact with concentration-demanding treatments (e.g., homework-heavy CPT). Brief or modified versions may be more feasible.
- **Dissociative disorders + trauma:** standard trauma-focused work requires careful pacing and stabilization. ISSTD guidelines (Guidelines for Treating Dissociative Identity Disorder in Adults, Third Revision, 2011) are the standard reference.
- **Alexithymia + trauma:** reduces the match with emotion-focused treatments that assume the person can identify and label feelings. Somatic and behavioral approaches may be more accessible in principle, though with the evidence-base caveats from Section 5.7.

## 9. The Role of Therapy: What Research Supports

### 9.1 Common factors research

- **Therapeutic alliance** (bond, goal agreement, task agreement) consistently predicts outcome across modalities. Lambert's classic attribution: ~30% of outcome variance from common factors including alliance; ~15% specific technique; ~40% extra-therapeutic; ~15% expectancy.
- **Norcross & Lambert** led the APA Division 29 Task Force on Evidence-Based Therapy Relationships.
  - Norcross, J. C., & Lambert, M. J. (2018). Psychotherapy relationships that work III. *Psychotherapy*, 55(4), 303–315.
  - Norcross, J. C., & Lambert, M. J. (Eds.). (2019). *Psychotherapy Relationships That Work: Volume 1: Evidence-Based Therapist Contributions* (3rd ed.). Oxford University Press.
- **Relationship factors demonstrably effective:** working alliance, collaboration, goal consensus, positive regard, therapist empathy, using client feedback.

### 9.2 Therapist factors

- Therapist training in the specific trauma modality matters. Poorly-delivered "EMDR-ish" or "CBT-ish" sessions do not equal EMDR or CBT.
- Therapist demographic/identity match can matter for some populations.
- "Therapist effect" (variability in outcome between therapists using the same method) is typically larger than differences between methods. (Wampold, B. E. (2015). How important are the common factors in psychotherapy? *World Psychiatry*, 14(3), 270–277.)

### 9.3 When therapy is contraindicated or harmful

- **Lilienfeld, S. O. (2007). Psychological treatments that cause harm.** *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, 2(1), 53–70. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26151919/. — seminal paper cataloguing Potentially Harmful Therapies (PHTs).
- **Example PHTs identified by Lilienfeld relevant to trauma contexts:**
  - Critical Incident Stress Debriefing (single-session, soon after trauma) — multiple trials show no benefit and possible worsening of outcomes.
  - Grief counseling for individuals with normal grief reactions — iatrogenic effects in up to ~50% of that subgroup.
  - Recovered-memory techniques (hypnotic age regression, guided imagery for "memory retrieval") — risk of false memory creation.
  - "Attachment therapies" involving coercive holding — associated with deaths.
  - Dissociative Identity Disorder-oriented therapy when conducted without proper training — can worsen dissociation.
- Estimated deterioration rates in psychotherapy: 3–10% in general; 10–15% in some substance-abuse populations.
- Iatrogenic-harm review: Berk, M., & Parker, G. (2009). The elephant on the couch: Side-effects of psychotherapy. *Australian and New Zealand Journal of Psychiatry*, 43(9), 787–794. See also: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26932481/.

### 9.4 Specific risks with trauma survivors

- **Pushing trauma processing before the survivor has stabilization skills** — can lead to destabilization, suicidality, or flight from treatment.
- **Therapists untrained in trauma treating complex-trauma survivors** — risk of re-traumatization through insensitive questioning, boundary violations, or misattribution of symptoms.
- **Therapists who reproduce the power dynamics of abuse** (controlling, dismissive, boundary-violating) — can replicate the original harm.
- **Over-disclosure of therapist personal trauma** — role confusion.
- **Bad-faith actors:** there is no universal licensing standard globally; some "trauma coaches" and unregulated practitioners market services without any accountability structure.

### 9.5 Finding autism-informed trauma therapists

- No accreditation exists for "autism-informed trauma therapy." Practical indicators of competence include: familiarity with adult-diagnosed autism, non-pathologizing language, willingness to discuss sensory accommodations, willingness to pace treatment, explicit acknowledgment of alexithymia.
- Resources: AASPIRE (Academic Autism Spectrum Partnership in Research and Education) Healthcare Toolkit; national autism advocacy organizations' practitioner directories (quality varies).

### 9.6 Self-help vs. professional help

- **Where self-help is best-evidenced:** mild-to-moderate depression and anxiety with CBT-based bibliotherapy (see Section 10).
- **Where self-help is insufficient alone:** moderate-to-severe PTSD, CPTSD with significant DSO symptoms, active suicidality, active abuse situations, dissociative disorders.
- **Where self-help can complement professional care:** psychoeducation, skills practice between sessions, peer community support.

### 9.7 Affordable alternatives when therapy is inaccessible

- **Crisis resources (US):** 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline; RAINN (Rape, Abuse & Incest National Network, 1-800-656-HOPE); Crisis Text Line (text HOME to 741741); National Domestic Violence Hotline (1-800-799-7233).
- **Support groups:** CPTSD Foundation; Out of the FOG (personality-disordered family members); NAMI peer support; online CPTSD communities (moderated, variable quality).
- **Community resources:** domestic violence shelters (offer counseling, safety planning regardless of shelter use); community mental health centers (sliding scale).
- **Low-cost therapy options:** training clinics at graduate psychology programs; Open Path Collective; telehealth providers with sliding scales.

## 10. Self-Help, Psychoeducation, and Bibliotherapy Evidence

### 10.1 Bibliotherapy

- **Meta-analysis (Cuijpers-style work on CBT bibliotherapy for depression):** 2004 meta-analysis of 17 studies yielded effect size d≈0.77 (95% CI 0.61–0.94) — large effect for mild-to-moderate depression.
- **Long-term effects review (2017):** Gualano et al. — bibliotherapy effects maintained 3 months to 3 years post. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0272735817302908.
- **Yuan et al. meta-analysis (children/adolescents):** SMD = −0.52 for depression/anxiety outcomes. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5788928/.
- **Equivalence to individual therapy** for unipolar depression found in some comparisons (small sample caveat). 
- **Adverse Childhood Experience bibliotherapy review (2025):** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2212958825000515.

**Key limitations:**
- Evidence is strongest for CBT-structured workbooks and mild-to-moderate depression/anxiety, not for severe PTSD or CPTSD.
- "Self-help book" is a heterogeneous category; very little direct evidence distinguishes effective from ineffective titles.
- Guided self-help (with therapist or coach check-ins) outperforms pure self-help.

### 10.2 Expressive writing (Pennebaker paradigm)

**Primary sources:**
- Pennebaker, J. W., & Beall, S. K. (1986). Confronting a traumatic event: Toward an understanding of inhibition and disease. *Journal of Abnormal Psychology*, 95(3), 274–281.
- Pennebaker, J. W., & Chung, C. K. (2011). Expressive writing and its links to mental and physical health. In H. S. Friedman (Ed.), *The Oxford Handbook of Health Psychology*.
- Pennebaker, J. W., & Smyth, J. M. (2016). *Opening Up by Writing It Down: How Expressive Writing Improves Health and Eases Emotional Pain* (3rd ed.). New York: Guilford Press.

**Meta-analytic effect sizes:**
- Cohen's d across 100+ studies ≈ 0.16 (small).
- Pavlacic et al. (2019). A meta-analysis of expressive writing on PTSD, PTG, and quality of life. https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/1089268019831645.
- Frattaroli, J. (2006). Experimental disclosure and its moderators: A meta-analysis. *Psychological Bulletin*, 132(6), 823–865.
- Network meta-analysis for adult trauma survivors: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9772920/.

**Key caveats:**
- Effect sizes are small and variable.
- Writing about ongoing abuse or while still unsafe can worsen distress.
- Some evidence favors instructions that combine disclosure with cognitive processing / meaning-making over pure emotional venting.

### 10.3 Peer support

**Meta-analyses:**
- Lyons, N., et al. (2021). A systematic review and meta-analysis of group peer support interventions. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34162340/.
- White, S., et al. (2020). The effectiveness of one-to-one peer support in mental health services. *BMC Psychiatry*. https://bmcpsychiatry.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12888-020-02923-3.
- Smit, D., Miguel, C., Vrijsen, J. N., Groeneweg, B., Spijker, J. & Cuijpers, P. (2023). The effectiveness of peer support for individuals with mental illness: systematic review and meta-analysis. *Psychological Medicine*. [PMC10476060](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10476060/)
- Effectiveness in personal/clinical recovery: https://psychiatryonline.org/doi/10.1176/appi.ps.202100138.

**Findings:**
- Small effects on personal recovery measures.
- Small effects on anxiety symptoms.
- Little/no effect on clinical recovery in severe mental illness.
- Evidence base rated weak overall due to study quality.
- Improvements in self-efficacy and self-advocacy in some contexts.

**Interpretation for trauma recovery:** peer support is endorsed as a SAMHSA principle and consistent with survivor preferences, but outcome effects are modest and it is not a substitute for evidence-based trauma treatment in clinical cases.

### 10.4 Psychoeducation as self-help

Psychoeducation (understanding the trauma response, the symptoms, and recovery principles) is a shared component across virtually every evidence-based trauma treatment. It is:
- A core component of the SAMHSA framework.
- The first PRACTICE component of TF-CBT.
- Part of Phase 1 in phase-based approaches.
- Shown to reduce distress modestly in isolation, with larger effects as part of a full treatment.

As a standalone intervention, psychoeducation is low-risk and widely used. The Pragmatics Hub content itself functions as psychoeducation.

## 11. Autism-Specific Gaps in Trauma Research

The following are *known unknowns* — areas where the field has acknowledged gaps but adequate research does not exist:

1. **No RCTs of major trauma treatments (CPT, PE, EMDR, STAIR) in autism-specific samples.** All current recommendations for autism-informed trauma treatment are expert consensus or case-series based.
2. **No validated trauma assessment instruments for autistic adults.** The ITQ, PCL-5, and CAPS were not developed or normed in autistic samples. Ceiling/floor effects, item interpretation, and alexithymia-related under-reporting are concerns.
3. **No validated screening tools for trauma in autistic children/adolescents.**
4. **Alexithymia + trauma interaction is under-researched** despite its obvious clinical importance.
5. **Sensory processing and trauma interaction** — the hypothesis that sensory hyperreactivity both increases vulnerability to trauma and complicates PTSD presentation is widely held but not well characterized empirically.
6. **Camouflaging/masking and trauma recovery** — late-diagnosed autistic adults often report decades of unrecognized autism producing chronic stress and coercion experiences; recovery research in this specific population is virtually absent.
7. **Therapist competency models** — no validated "autism-informed trauma therapist" certification or training standard.
8. **Autism + gaslighting / coercive control research is essentially absent from the peer-reviewed literature** despite substantial anecdotal and community reporting that autistic people may be disproportionately targeted (due to trusting communication style, difficulty detecting deception, social inexperience).
9. **Recovery trajectories in autism** — whether the stages, timelines, and markers of recovery from complex trauma look the same in autistic adults is not systematically studied.

## 12. Claims About Trauma Recovery That Are NOT Well-Supported

This section exists so downstream content does not inadvertently repeat popular claims that exceed the evidence. Any of these claims should be either removed, hedged heavily, or attributed to their popular (non-research) origin.

### 12.1 Neurobiological claims from *The Body Keeps the Score*

- "The triune brain (reptilian / limbic / neocortex) explains trauma responses." — The triune brain model is obsolete neuroscience.
- "Polyvagal theory explains trauma." — Polyvagal theory is contested; several of Porges' specific vertebrate-physiology claims have been challenged (see Grossman, P. (2023). Fundamental challenges and likely refutations of the five basic premises of the polyvagal theory. *Biological Psychology*, 180, 108589).
- "Trauma is stored in the body at the cellular/tissue level and must be released physically." — No mechanism supporting this literal claim exists in peer-reviewed literature.
- "Talk therapy cannot treat trauma." — Directly contradicted by RCT evidence for CPT, PE, and TF-CBT.

### 12.2 Somatic and energy-based claims

- "Tapping" / Thought Field Therapy / Emotional Freedom Technique — the specific proposed mechanisms (meridians, energy fields) lack support; any benefit seen in studies is attributable to exposure + relaxation elements shared with established treatments.
- "Releasing trauma from the nervous system" as a discrete event — not a validated clinical concept.
- Generic "somatic work" — an umbrella term for many practices of varying evidence quality; Somatic Experiencing specifically has weak evidence (Section 5.7).

### 12.3 Memory claims

- "Recovered memory" techniques (hypnotic regression, guided imagery for memory retrieval) — risk of false memory; explicitly flagged as harmful (Lilienfeld 2007).
- "The body remembers what the mind forgets" — poetic, not literal. Implicit memory and physiological conditioning exist, but the claim that discrete autobiographical memories are encoded in muscle tissue or fascia is not supported.
- "Trauma can be inherited genetically and cause symptoms in descendants" — epigenetic transmission of stress effects is an active research area with small effects; claims of discrete inherited trauma memories are pop-science embellishment.

### 12.4 Universal-trajectory claims

- "Everyone experiences post-traumatic growth." — No. PTG occurs in some survivors and is not predictable or prescribable.
- "Trauma makes you stronger." — No (Section 6.2). Average outcomes are not better than no-trauma baselines.
- "Time heals all wounds." — Untreated PTSD and CPTSD often persist for decades (Kessler et al. 1995 NCS; Kessler et al. 2005 NCS-R).
- "You need to fully forgive your abuser to heal." — No empirical support. Forgiveness is one possible outcome among several; recovery does not require it.
- "Confronting your abuser is a necessary step." — No empirical support; can be unsafe.

### 12.5 Treatment equivalence claims

- "All therapy modalities are equally effective." — Largely true among *first-line evidence-based trauma therapies* (CPT, PE, EMDR, TF-CBT for children), but NOT true across all modalities. Generic supportive counseling without trauma focus underperforms trauma-focused therapies in direct comparisons for PTSD.
- "Any therapist is better than no therapist." — False. Therapy can harm (Section 9.3).

### 12.6 "Gaslighting" definitional overreach

- The term "gaslighting" has broadened in popular usage to cover any disagreement or denial. Clinically, it refers specifically to the sustained pattern of inducing self-doubt about perception, memory, and sanity. Downstream content should use the specific sense and avoid reinforcing the broader pop-usage.

### 12.7 "Narcissistic abuse" as a clinical category

- "Narcissistic abuse" is a popular-literature term without peer-reviewed clinical status as a distinct trauma subtype. Where the term is used in downstream content, it should be translated to the more defensible "coercive control by a partner who may have narcissistic traits" or "emotional abuse involving patterns of entitlement and exploitation" — claims about a distinct trauma syndrome caused by narcissists specifically are not empirically supported.

### 12.8 "CPTSD is caused only by childhood abuse"

- The ICD-11 criteria specify prolonged, repeated, inescapable trauma — which *includes* but is not limited to childhood abuse. Adult-onset CPTSD from prolonged coercive control, trafficking, captivity, or sustained domestic violence is recognized.

### 12.9 "You cannot heal from trauma without therapy"

- Overstated. Many people recover with social support, time, meaning-making, and peer communities, especially for less severe presentations. However, for moderate-to-severe PTSD and CPTSD, the evidence base for professional trauma-focused treatment is much stronger than for unaided recovery.

### 12.10 "Boundaries" as trauma recovery slogan

- The concept of boundaries is clinically useful but has been stripped down in popular usage to mean "refuse any request you don't like," which misrepresents the clinical concept of relational limits and can become self-isolating. Cite the actual assertiveness literature (e.g., STAIR interpersonal module; CBT assertiveness training) rather than influencer "boundary" discourse.
