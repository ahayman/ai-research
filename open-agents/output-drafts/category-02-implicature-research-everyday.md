# Everyday Conversational Implicature: A Practical Catalog

## Purpose and Audience

This document is a reference catalog for teaching English pragmatics to autistic individuals. It maps common conversational situations to the implicatures they generate, organized by the Gricean mechanism involved.

**Why this matters for autistic individuals:** Research shows that autistic people may process implicatures differently -- not as a deficit, but as a difference in how implicit vs. explicit information is weighted. Metaphor interpretation and scalar implicature derivation appear largely preserved in autism, while particularized (context-dependent) implicatures present greater difficulty. The challenge is not understanding *that* people speak indirectly, but having a reliable catalog of *when and how* they do so. This document provides that catalog.

**How to use this document:** Each entry follows the pattern:
- **What is said** (the literal words)
- **What is implicated** (what the speaker actually means)
- **The mechanism** (which Gricean maxim is being flouted or exploited)
- **How to detect it** (contextual cues that signal the implicature is present)

---

## 1. QUANTITY IMPLICATURES: When People Say Less Than They Could

### The Principle

Grice's Maxim of Quantity states: "Make your contribution as informative as is required." When someone says *less* than you'd expect given what they know, the gap itself becomes the message. The listener reasons: "If they had better information, they would have shared it. Since they didn't, the information they're withholding must be negative."

This is the mechanism behind "damning with faint praise" -- praising someone for something minor implicates that you *can't* praise them for something major.

### The Core Pattern

**The Detection Rule:** When someone responds to a question with *technically true but conspicuously incomplete* information, the missing information is the real message.

---

### Example 1.1: The Exam Result

- **Context:** You ask a friend, "How was the exam?"
- **What is said:** "I passed."
- **What is implicated:** I barely passed. If I'd done well, I would have said so -- "I got an A!" or "I aced it!"
- **Mechanism:** The speaker gives less information than expected. Passing is the minimum positive outcome, and volunteering only the minimum implicates that the minimum is all there is.
- **Detection cue:** The response is technically positive but conspicuously lacking enthusiasm or specifics.

### Example 1.2: The Painting Opinion

- **Context:** Someone shows you their painting and asks, "Do you like it?"
- **What is said:** "It's very colorful."
- **What is implicated:** I don't think it's good, but I found *something* positive to say. If I liked the painting, I'd say "It's beautiful" or "I love it."
- **Mechanism:** Quantity violation -- praising an incidental feature (color) instead of the thing actually asked about (quality/appeal).
- **Detection cue:** The compliment addresses a peripheral rather than central quality. "Colorful" is an observation, not an evaluation.

### Example 1.3: The Suspiciously Brief Recommendation Letter

- **Context:** A professor writes a recommendation letter for a philosophy job candidate.
- **What is said:** "Dear Sir, Mr. X's command of English is excellent, and his attendance at tutorials has been regular."
- **What is implicated:** Mr. X is not a good philosopher. If he were, the letter would say so. The professor knows more is expected, cannot be ignorant of the candidate's abilities (being the candidate's supervisor), and is clearly cooperating (they wrote the letter at all) -- so the absence of substantive praise is the message.
- **Mechanism:** This is Grice's own classic example. The writer praises only irrelevant qualities (English proficiency, attendance) rather than the relevant one (philosophical ability). The omission implicates the negative.
- **Detection cue:** The letter is suspiciously short. The qualities praised are things anyone could have. The core competency being evaluated is never mentioned.

### Example 1.4: The Restaurant Review

- **Context:** You ask a friend about a restaurant.
- **What is said:** "The decor was nice."
- **What is implicated:** The food was not good. If the food were good, that's what they'd mention first -- it's a restaurant, after all.
- **Mechanism:** Same as 1.2 -- praising the peripheral feature (decor) while conspicuously not mentioning the central one (food).
- **Detection cue:** When asked about an experience, the response focuses on the least important aspect.

### Example 1.5: The Job Candidate Reference

- **Context:** A hiring manager calls a reference and asks about a candidate's performance.
- **What is said:** "She was always on time and very pleasant to work with."
- **What is implicated:** Her actual work quality was not noteworthy. Punctuality and pleasantness are baseline expectations, not achievements.
- **Mechanism:** Quantity -- listing only minimum-bar attributes implicates that higher-bar attributes (competence, initiative, results) are absent.
- **Detection cue:** The attributes praised would apply to virtually anyone who wasn't actively problematic.

### Example 1.6: The Date Report

- **Context:** You ask a friend, "How was your date?"
- **What is said:** "The restaurant was great."
- **What is implicated:** The date itself was not great. If the person had been wonderful, that's what they'd talk about.
- **Mechanism:** Quantity -- deflecting to an environmental detail instead of the thing actually being asked about (the person/connection).
- **Detection cue:** The response discusses the setting rather than the experience with the other person.

### Example 1.7: The Scalar Implicature -- "Some"

- **Context:** A teacher says, "Some of the students passed the exam."
- **What is said:** "Some of the students passed."
- **What is implicated:** Not all of them passed. If all had passed, the teacher would say "all" -- using the weaker term "some" when a stronger term was available implicates that the stronger term doesn't apply.
- **Mechanism:** This is a *scalar implicature* -- words exist on scales of strength (some < many < most < all). Using a weaker word implicates that the stronger word is not warranted.
- **Detection cue:** A weaker quantifier is used where a stronger one could be. Other scales: "or" implicates "not and"; "warm" implicates "not hot"; "possible" implicates "not certain."

### Example 1.8: The Scalar Implicature -- "Or"

- **Context:** A parent says, "You can have cake or ice cream."
- **What is said:** "Cake or ice cream."
- **What is implicated:** Not both. Logically, "or" includes "both," but conversationally, if both were available, the parent would say "and."
- **Mechanism:** Scalar implicature on the scale (or < and). "Or" implicates "not and."
- **Detection cue:** "Or" in a permission or offer context usually implicates exclusivity.

### Example 1.9: The Faint Praise Pattern -- "He's a Hard Worker"

- **Context:** Asked about a colleague's intelligence or skill.
- **What is said:** "He's a really hard worker."
- **What is implicated:** He's not particularly talented or smart. If he were, that would be mentioned. "Hard worker" is what you say about someone whose main virtue is effort, not ability.
- **Mechanism:** Quantity -- praising effort implicates a lack of natural ability to praise.
- **Detection cue:** The praised quality (effort) is the consolation prize for the absent quality (talent).

### Example 1.10: The Movie Review

- **Context:** You ask, "Was the movie good?"
- **What is said:** "The special effects were impressive."
- **What is implicated:** The story, acting, and other core elements were not impressive.
- **Mechanism:** Praising a technical aspect while omitting judgment on artistic quality.
- **Detection cue:** The praised element is something that can be good even when the movie as a whole is not.

### Example 1.11: The "It Was... Fine" Response

- **Context:** You ask someone about their vacation, job, meal, etc.
- **What is said:** "It was fine."
- **What is implicated:** It was mediocre or disappointing. "Fine" is the minimum positive assessment. If it were genuinely good, the speaker would use a stronger word.
- **Mechanism:** Quantity -- "fine" is informationally minimal. The lack of enthusiasm *is* the information.
- **Detection cue:** The word "fine" delivered without elaboration or enthusiasm. Compare: "It was fine, actually -- the beach was gorgeous" (genuine) vs. "It was... fine" with a pause and no follow-up (implicature: not fine).

### Example 1.12: The "At Least..." Qualifier

- **Context:** Someone describes a bad situation.
- **What is said:** "Well, at least nobody got hurt."
- **What is implicated:** Everything else about the situation was terrible. "At least" finds the one salvageable fact, which implicates that everything else is unsalvageable.
- **Mechanism:** Quantity -- marking the single positive element highlights the absence of other positives.
- **Detection cue:** "At least" introduces the lowest bar of acceptable outcome, implying everything above that bar was not met.

### Example 1.13: The Resume Gap

- **Context:** A candidate's resume lists five previous jobs with detailed descriptions but one job has only a title and dates.
- **What is said:** "Marketing Associate, Acme Corp, 2019-2020"
- **What is implicated:** That job did not go well. If there were accomplishments to report, they would be listed.
- **Mechanism:** Quantity -- the conspicuous absence of detail where detail is expected signals that the omitted information is negative.
- **Detection cue:** Inconsistency in the level of detail provided. When most items are elaborated but one is bare, the bare one draws attention.

### Example 1.14: "She Has Many Qualities"

- **Context:** Asked for your opinion of someone.
- **What is said:** "She has many qualities."
- **What is implicated:** None of them are good enough to name specifically. If there were a standout positive quality, you'd name it.
- **Mechanism:** Quantity -- vagueness where specificity would be normal implicates that specificity would be unflattering.
- **Detection cue:** Generic, non-specific praise that could apply to anyone.

### Example 1.15: The Number Game

- **Context:** You ask a friend how many people came to their party.
- **What is said:** "A few people showed up."
- **What is implicated:** Fewer people came than expected or hoped. If the turnout were good, they'd say "about twenty people" or "a great crowd."
- **Mechanism:** Scalar implicature -- "a few" on the scale (a few < several < many < a lot) implicates "not many."
- **Detection cue:** Vague quantifiers where a specific number could easily be given often signal the number is disappointingly low.

### Example 1.16: The Damning Omission in Introductions

- **Context:** A parent introduces their adult child to friends.
- **What is said:** "This is my son, David. He's very kind."
- **What is implicated:** David does not have a career, degree, or other conventional achievement worth mentioning. If he were a doctor, the parent would say so.
- **Mechanism:** Quantity -- in the cultural context of parental introductions, omitting conventional achievements while highlighting personality traits implicates the absence of those achievements.
- **Detection cue:** The introduction highlights character rather than accomplishment in a context where accomplishment would normally be mentioned.

### Example 1.17: "They Tried Their Best"

- **Context:** Evaluating someone's performance.
- **What is said:** "They really tried their best."
- **What is implicated:** Their best wasn't good enough. If the result were successful, you'd praise the result, not the effort.
- **Mechanism:** Quantity -- praising the input (effort) implicates poor output (result).
- **Detection cue:** Effort-focused praise in a results-focused context.

### Example 1.18: The Recruiter's "Competitive Salary"

- **Context:** A job listing describes compensation.
- **What is said:** "Competitive salary."
- **What is implicated:** The salary is probably not high enough to impress you. If it were genuinely impressive, they'd list the number. "Competitive" means "within the range that won't immediately disqualify us."
- **Mechanism:** Quantity -- withholding the specific number (which they know) in a context where specificity would be informative implicates that the specific number is not a selling point.
- **Detection cue:** Vagueness about a numeric value where precision would be easy and expected.

## 2. QUALITY IMPLICATURES: When People Say Something Obviously Untrue

### The Principle

Grice's Maxim of Quality states: "Do not say what you believe to be false." When someone *blatantly* violates this -- saying something so obviously untrue that both parties know it's false -- the falseness itself is the point. The listener must figure out what the speaker *actually* means by saying something they clearly don't believe.

Quality implicatures generate irony, sarcasm, metaphor, hyperbole, understatement (litotes), and rhetorical questions.

### The Core Pattern

**The Detection Rule:** When a statement is *so obviously false* that the speaker clearly knows it's false, the literal meaning is not the intended meaning. Look for the opposite meaning, an exaggerated version, or a comparison.

---

### Example 2.1: Irony -- Weather Complaint

- **Context:** It's pouring rain and freezing cold. Someone looks out the window.
- **What is said:** "What lovely weather we're having."
- **What is implicated:** The weather is terrible.
- **Mechanism:** Quality violation via irony -- the statement is obviously false, and the speaker knows the listener knows it's false. The meaning is the opposite of the literal words.
- **Detection cue:** The statement contradicts the observable reality that both speaker and listener can see. The tone is often flat or overly cheerful.

### Example 2.2: Sarcasm -- Meeting Fatigue

- **Context:** An employee checks their calendar and sees another meeting has been scheduled.
- **What is said:** "Oh great, another meeting. Just what I needed."
- **What is implicated:** I'm frustrated by too many meetings. This is the opposite of what I needed.
- **Mechanism:** Quality violation via sarcasm -- the positive words ("great," "just what I needed") are delivered with a tone that signals the opposite meaning.
- **Detection cue:** Exaggerated enthusiasm combined with a context where enthusiasm is implausible. Sarcasm often involves a mismatch between words and tone/context.

### Example 2.3: Sarcasm -- Obvious Statement

- **Context:** Someone states something painfully obvious.
- **What is said:** "Wow, what an incredibly original observation."
- **What is implicated:** Your observation was unoriginal and obvious. The praise is the opposite of sincere.
- **Mechanism:** Quality -- the extreme praise is so disproportionate to what was actually said that it signals the reverse.
- **Detection cue:** Disproportionately strong positive language applied to something trivial or obvious.

### Example 2.4: Metaphor -- "He's a Snake"

- **Context:** Describing someone's character.
- **What is said:** "He's a real snake."
- **What is implicated:** He is deceitful, untrustworthy, and potentially dangerous.
- **Mechanism:** Quality violation via metaphor -- the speaker says something literally false (the person is not a reptile) to draw a comparison. The listener must extract the relevant attributes of the comparison vehicle (snake: deceptive, slithering, venomous) and map them to the person.
- **Detection cue:** The statement is categorically false if taken literally but makes sense as a comparison. Common pattern: "[person] is a [animal/object]."

### Example 2.5: Metaphor -- "She Has a Heart of Gold"

- **Context:** Describing someone's character positively.
- **What is said:** "She has a heart of gold."
- **What is implicated:** She is exceptionally kind and generous.
- **Mechanism:** Quality violation -- hearts are not made of gold. The metaphor maps the properties of gold (valuable, pure, precious) onto the person's character. Note: this is a "dead metaphor" -- so conventionalized that most speakers no longer process the literal falseness.
- **Detection cue:** The statement is literally impossible but universally understood. Dead metaphors often go unnoticed as metaphors.

### Example 2.6: Hyperbole -- "I've Told You a Million Times"

- **Context:** A parent is frustrated with a child who keeps doing something they've been asked not to do.
- **What is said:** "I've told you a million times not to do that."
- **What is implicated:** I've told you many times -- far more than should be necessary. I'm very frustrated by the repetition.
- **Mechanism:** Quality violation via hyperbole -- the number is obviously false, and the exaggeration emphasizes the speaker's frustration with repetition.
- **Detection cue:** A specific number that is impossibly large. Common hyperbolic numbers: "a million," "a thousand," "a billion."

### Example 2.7: Hyperbole -- "I'm Dying"

- **Context:** A friend tells you something very funny.
- **What is said:** "I'm dying!" or "I'm literally dying!"
- **What is implicated:** This is extremely funny. (Not: I am actually experiencing death.)
- **Mechanism:** Quality -- the statement is obviously false. The extreme claim emphasizes the intensity of the emotional reaction.
- **Detection cue:** A claim about extreme physical states (dying, starving, freezing) in a context where the actual state is emotional, not physical. Note: "literally" is now commonly used as an intensifier that does NOT signal literal truth -- "I'm literally dying" is hyperbolic.

### Example 2.8: Hyperbole -- "I Could Eat a Horse"

- **Context:** Someone hasn't eaten in a while.
- **What is said:** "I'm so hungry I could eat a horse."
- **What is implicated:** I am extremely hungry. The exaggeration is proportional to the actual hunger.
- **Mechanism:** Quality -- the claim is obviously false (no one can eat a horse) and serves to emphasize the degree of hunger.
- **Detection cue:** "So [adjective] I could [impossible action]" is a recognizable hyperbolic template.

### Example 2.9: Hyperbole -- "I've Been Waiting Forever"

- **Context:** Someone has been waiting in a long line.
- **What is said:** "I've been waiting forever."
- **What is implicated:** I've been waiting a long time -- longer than I think is reasonable.
- **Mechanism:** Quality -- "forever" is obviously false and emphasizes perceived unreasonableness of the wait.
- **Detection cue:** Impossible time claims: "forever," "an eternity," "all day" (when it hasn't been all day).

### Example 2.10: Understatement/Litotes -- "Not Exactly the Brightest"

- **Context:** Describing someone's intelligence.
- **What is said:** "He's not exactly the brightest bulb in the box."
- **What is implicated:** He's quite unintelligent.
- **Mechanism:** Quality violation via understatement (litotes) -- negating the positive is a softer way of asserting the negative. The double negative structure (not + positive) allows the speaker to communicate a negative judgment while maintaining plausible deniability of rudeness.
- **Detection cue:** The pattern "not exactly [positive quality]" or "not the most [positive quality]." Litotes typically takes the form "not + positive" to mean "decidedly negative."

### Example 2.11: Litotes -- "Not Bad"

- **Context:** Someone tastes a dish you made.
- **What is said:** "Not bad."
- **What is implicated:** This is actually quite good. (British English especially -- "not bad" can mean "really excellent." Context and culture matter.)
- **Mechanism:** Quality/understatement -- negating the negative to express a positive, but deliberately understating the degree. The scale of understatement varies by culture: in British English, "not bad" can be high praise. In American English, "not bad" is more moderate.
- **Detection cue:** "Not + [negative adjective]" as a response to something positive. The meaning is positive but the degree depends on cultural context and tone.

### Example 2.12: Litotes -- "It's Not the Cheapest Option"

- **Context:** Discussing a product or service.
- **What is said:** "It's not the cheapest option."
- **What is implicated:** It's expensive. Possibly too expensive.
- **Mechanism:** Understatement -- "not the cheapest" avoids directly saying "expensive" or "overpriced." The indirect formulation softens the criticism.
- **Detection cue:** "Not the [most positive adjective]" = the opposite of that adjective. "Not the friendliest person" = unfriendly. "Not the easiest task" = difficult.

### Example 2.13: Rhetorical Question -- "Do I Look Like I Care?"

- **Context:** Someone is telling you information you find irrelevant.
- **What is said:** "Do I look like I care?"
- **What is implicated:** I don't care. (The question is not seeking information -- it's asserting the opposite of what a genuine answer would be.)
- **Mechanism:** Quality -- the question form is used not to seek information but to assert a proposition. The expected "answer" is "no," which is the speaker's actual message.
- **Detection cue:** Questions that have an obvious answer, where that obvious answer IS the message. The speaker does not pause for or expect a response.

### Example 2.14: Rhetorical Question -- "Is the Pope Catholic?"

- **Context:** Someone asks you a yes/no question with an obvious answer.
- **What is said:** "Is the Pope Catholic?" (Also: "Does a bear live in the woods?")
- **What is implicated:** Yes, obviously. The answer to your question is so obvious it's absurd to ask.
- **Mechanism:** Quality -- answering a question with another question whose answer is obviously "yes" is a way of saying "obviously yes" with added emphasis (and possibly mild contempt for the questioner).
- **Detection cue:** A question answered with a different question that has a universally known answer.

### Example 2.15: Rhetorical Question -- "Who Cares?"

- **Context:** Someone brings up a topic the speaker finds unimportant.
- **What is said:** "Who cares?"
- **What is implicated:** Nobody cares. This doesn't matter.
- **Mechanism:** Quality -- the question form asserts its own answer (nobody).
- **Detection cue:** "Who [verb]?" and "What does it matter?" are rhetorical questions that assert their own negative answers.

### Example 2.16: Sarcastic Gratitude -- "Thanks for Nothing"

- **Context:** Someone has failed to help or has actively made things worse.
- **What is said:** "Thanks a lot." or "Thanks for nothing."
- **What is implicated:** I'm angry at your lack of help. The "thanks" is the opposite of sincere.
- **Mechanism:** Quality -- the conventional politeness formula (thanks) is deployed in a context where nothing worthy of thanks has occurred, signaling the opposite of gratitude.
- **Detection cue:** "Thanks" applied to a situation where nothing helpful happened. Tone is often flat or exaggeratedly cheerful.

### Example 2.17: Ironic Competence Assessment

- **Context:** Someone does something clumsy (trips, drops something).
- **What is said:** "Smooth move." or "Nailed it." or "Real graceful."
- **What is implicated:** That was clumsy/embarrassing.
- **Mechanism:** Quality -- positive evaluation applied to an obviously negative event. The mismatch between the praise and the reality is the signal.
- **Detection cue:** Praise or compliment immediately following a failure or mishap.

### Example 2.18: The Impossible Wish

- **Context:** Someone has made a request you find excessive.
- **What is said:** "Yeah, and I'd like a pony for Christmas."
- **What is implicated:** Your request is unrealistic and will not be fulfilled.
- **Mechanism:** Quality -- equating the request with something obviously impossible signals that the speaker considers the request equally unrealistic.
- **Detection cue:** Responding to a request with an obviously impossible counter-request.

### Example 2.19: Hyperbolic Agreement

- **Context:** Someone asks if a task took long.
- **What is said:** "Only about ten thousand years."
- **What is implicated:** It took a very long time -- unreasonably so.
- **Mechanism:** Quality -- the obviously false time frame emphasizes the speaker's frustration with how long something took.
- **Detection cue:** Impossibly large quantities used as answers to "how much/how long" questions.

### Example 2.20: Understated Crisis

- **Context:** Something has gone seriously wrong.
- **What is said:** "Well, that's not ideal." or "We have a slight problem."
- **What is implicated:** This is a major problem. The understatement is either humorous or signals that the speaker is maintaining composure in a crisis.
- **Mechanism:** Quality via understatement -- describing a large problem with language appropriate for a small one. The gap between the description and the reality is the information.
- **Detection cue:** Mild language applied to a clearly serious situation. "Slight problem" when something is on fire. "Not ideal" when a project has catastrophically failed.

## 3. RELEVANCE IMPLICATURES: When People Say Something Seemingly Off-Topic

### The Principle

Grice's Maxim of Relation (Relevance) states: "Be relevant." When someone responds to a question with something that *seems* irrelevant, listeners don't conclude the speaker has lost track of the conversation. Instead, they search for a way to make the response relevant -- and the connection they find IS the implicature.

Relevance implicatures are often the most difficult for autistic individuals because they require the listener to bridge between what was said and what was asked, using contextual knowledge that may not be explicit.

### The Core Pattern

**The Detection Rule:** When a response doesn't directly answer the question, ask: "How would this information be relevant to what I asked?" The answer to that meta-question is the implicature. Often, the seemingly irrelevant response provides a *reason* that implicitly answers the original question.

---

### Example 3.1: The Movie Deflection

- **Context:** You ask a friend, "How was the movie?"
- **What is said:** "The popcorn was good."
- **What is implicated:** The movie was not good. If it were, the friend would talk about the movie. Pivoting to the popcorn means the movie itself is not worth discussing positively.
- **Mechanism:** Relevance -- the response appears to change the subject, but the listener infers that the *best* thing about the experience was the popcorn, which answers the question implicitly.
- **Detection cue:** Response discusses a peripheral element of the experience rather than the central one being asked about. (Note the overlap with Quantity -- this example could be analyzed either way.)

### Example 3.2: The Party Decline

- **Context:** You invite someone to a party Saturday night.
- **What is said:** "I have an early flight tomorrow."
- **What is implicated:** No, I can't come. The early flight is the reason.
- **Mechanism:** Relevance -- the response doesn't say "no" directly but provides information from which "no" can be inferred. Having an early flight is incompatible with attending a party, so the statement is relevant as a reason-giving rejection.
- **Detection cue:** The response provides a fact about the speaker's schedule or circumstances that, when combined with common knowledge, produces a "no."

### Example 3.3: The Gas Station

- **Context:** You tell a stranger, "I'm out of gas."
- **What is said:** "There's a gas station around the corner."
- **What is implicated:** That gas station is open and you can get gas there. (If the speaker knew it was closed, telling you about it would be irrelevant and unhelpful.)
- **Mechanism:** Relevance -- the information is only relevant if it can solve the problem. The speaker, being cooperative, would not mention a closed gas station.
- **Detection cue:** When someone offers a solution, the listener can infer that the speaker believes the solution will actually work.

### Example 3.4: The Eavesdropping Warning

- **Context:** You're about to say something critical about someone. A friend suddenly says:
- **What is said:** "Lovely weather for March, isn't it?"
- **What is implicated:** Someone who shouldn't hear this is nearby -- stop talking about that topic.
- **Mechanism:** Relevance -- the sudden irrelevant topic change is so conspicuously off-topic that it must be a signal. The listener infers that the topic change IS the message: "Don't continue with what you were saying."
- **Detection cue:** A sudden, conspicuous, uncharacteristic topic change, especially to something banal (weather, sports). The abruptness is the signal.

### Example 3.5: The Relationship Question Dodge

- **Context:** A parent asks their adult child, "Are you seeing anyone?"
- **What is said:** "Work has been really busy lately."
- **What is implicated:** No, and I don't want to talk about it. "Work is busy" provides an implicit excuse (too busy to date) and a topic redirect.
- **Mechanism:** Relevance -- the response is only relevant if interpreted as an excuse/explanation for the implied "no."
- **Detection cue:** The response addresses the *reason* behind the implied answer rather than the answer itself.

### Example 3.6: The Homework Check

- **Context:** A parent asks a child, "Have you done your homework?"
- **What is said:** "My favorite show is about to start."
- **What is implicated:** No, I haven't, and I want to watch TV instead.
- **Mechanism:** Relevance -- the response is relevant only as an excuse for why the homework hasn't been done and a counter-request to be allowed to watch TV.
- **Detection cue:** The response provides a competing activity rather than answering the question.

### Example 3.7: The Salary Question

- **Context:** During a job interview, the candidate asks about salary.
- **What is said:** "We offer an excellent benefits package."
- **What is implicated:** The salary is not our strongest selling point, but the benefits help compensate for that.
- **Mechanism:** Relevance -- responding to a salary question with information about benefits is relevant only if the salary itself isn't as attractive.
- **Detection cue:** Answering a specific question by pivoting to a related but different positive attribute.

### Example 3.8: The Plans Question

- **Context:** Someone you're not interested in asks, "Are you free Friday night?"
- **What is said:** "I think I'm coming down with something."
- **What is implicated:** No (and possibly: I don't want to go out with you). The illness provides a socially acceptable reason for declining without directly rejecting the person.
- **Mechanism:** Relevance -- the health information is relevant as a pre-emptive explanation for unavailability.
- **Detection cue:** A reason is offered before a direct answer. The reason makes the "no" implicit.

### Example 3.9: The Quality Assessment Dodge

- **Context:** A friend asks, "What do you think of my new boyfriend?"
- **What is said:** "You seem really happy."
- **What is implicated:** I don't approve of the boyfriend, but I acknowledge that you're happy, and I'll defer to your judgment. If I liked the boyfriend, I'd say so directly.
- **Mechanism:** Relevance -- the response addresses the friend's emotional state rather than the direct question about the boyfriend. The friend's happiness is the only positive the speaker can offer.
- **Detection cue:** The response evaluates the asker's state rather than the thing being asked about.

### Example 3.10: The Knowledge Disclaimer

- **Context:** You ask, "Is the new Thai place any good?"
- **What is said:** "Well, my friend got food poisoning there last week."
- **What is implicated:** No, don't go there. The anecdote makes the negative recommendation without stating it directly.
- **Mechanism:** Relevance -- the food poisoning anecdote is relevant as evidence supporting a negative answer to the question.
- **Detection cue:** An anecdote or fact offered in response to an opinion question, where the fact implies the opinion.

### Example 3.11: The Job Performance Answer

- **Context:** A manager asks an employee, "How is the project going?"
- **What is said:** "I've been putting in a lot of overtime."
- **What is implicated:** The project is behind schedule or in trouble. If it were on track, the employee would report on milestones and progress, not hours worked.
- **Mechanism:** Relevance -- reporting on effort (overtime) is relevant as a defense/explanation for why the project is not going as expected.
- **Detection cue:** Answering a results question with an effort/process answer.

### Example 3.12: The Cooking Deflection

- **Context:** A guest tastes a dish their host made and the host asks, "How is it?"
- **What is said:** "You always put so much effort into cooking."
- **What is implicated:** The dish isn't very good, but I appreciate the effort. If it were good, I'd say "This is delicious."
- **Mechanism:** Relevance -- praising the process (effort) instead of the product (taste) is relevant only as a deflection from the product's quality.
- **Detection cue:** Praising the effort or intention rather than the result.

### Example 3.13: The Vacation Non-Answer

- **Context:** Someone asks a couple, "How was your trip together?"
- **What is said:** "The scenery was beautiful."
- **What is implicated:** The trip was not great as a couple's experience. The scenery was the highlight, not the togetherness.
- **Mechanism:** Relevance -- "together" was the operative word in the question, and the answer pointedly doesn't address it.
- **Detection cue:** The response addresses a different aspect of the experience than what was emphasized in the question.

### Example 3.14: The Excuse Chain

- **Context:** You invite someone to multiple events over time.
- **What is said (event 1):** "I have a doctor's appointment."
- **What is said (event 2):** "I'm visiting my parents that weekend."
- **What is said (event 3):** "I have a work thing."
- **What is implicated (cumulatively):** I don't want to spend time with you. Any single excuse is perfectly normal, but the pattern of always having a reason -- without ever suggesting an alternative time -- implicates deliberate avoidance.
- **Mechanism:** Relevance -- each individual excuse is relevant, but the pattern reveals the deeper truth. The key absent element is: they never say "But I'm free next week!" or "How about another time?"
- **Detection cue:** Repeated excuses without counter-proposals. If someone wants to see you, they'll suggest alternatives. The absence of alternatives is the message.

### Example 3.15: The Resume Gap Question

- **Context:** An interviewer asks about a gap in employment.
- **What is said:** "I took some time to focus on personal development."
- **What is implicated:** Something happened (fired, health issue, burnout) that I don't want to discuss specifically. "Personal development" is a positive-sounding non-answer.
- **Mechanism:** Relevance -- the response provides a reason but one so vague that the listener infers the real reason is being concealed.
- **Detection cue:** A conspicuously vague positive framing where specifics would be expected.

### Example 3.16: The Implicit Warning

- **Context:** You mention you're thinking of taking a job at a particular company. A friend who works there says:
- **What is said:** "Make sure you negotiate your severance package."
- **What is implicated:** People get fired from that company frequently. Job security is poor.
- **Mechanism:** Relevance -- mentioning severance in response to a question about taking a job is only relevant if severance is likely to become relevant (i.e., you might get fired).
- **Detection cue:** Advice about a negative scenario offered in response to an optimistic statement implies the negative scenario is likely.

## 4. MANNER IMPLICATURES: When People Say Something in an Unusual Way

### The Principle

Grice's Maxim of Manner states: "Be perspicuous -- avoid obscurity, avoid ambiguity, be brief, be orderly." When someone says something in an unusually complicated, vague, formal, or roundabout way, the *way* they say it carries meaning beyond the words themselves. If there's a simple way to say something and the speaker chooses a complex way, the complexity is doing communicative work.

### The Core Pattern

**The Detection Rule:** When something is said in a noticeably unusual way -- more formal than expected, more vague than necessary, more elaborate than needed, or in a different order than normal -- the manner of expression itself is part of the message.

---

### Example 4.1: Euphemism -- Death

- **Context:** Informing someone about a death.
- **What is said:** "She passed away." / "He's no longer with us." / "We lost her."
- **What is implicated:** She died. The indirect phrasing softens the emotional impact.
- **Mechanism:** Manner -- the speaker avoids the direct word ("died") in favor of a less direct expression. The indirection signals that the topic is emotionally sensitive and the speaker is trying to be gentle.
- **Detection cue:** Conspicuous avoidance of a common, direct word. When someone uses three words where one would suffice, the extra words serve an emotional or social function.

### Example 4.2: Euphemism -- Termination

- **Context:** An employee is being fired.
- **What is said:** "We've decided to let you go." / "Your position has been eliminated." / "We're going in a different direction."
- **What is implicated:** You're fired.
- **Mechanism:** Manner -- the passive voice ("position has been eliminated") and indirect phrasing ("let you go" -- as if the employee wanted to leave) obscure the agent (the company) and soften the action (firing).
- **Detection cue:** Passive voice and indirect language about actions that could be stated directly. "We're letting you go" vs. "You're fired."

### Example 4.3: Euphemism -- "Between Jobs"

- **Context:** Describing unemployment.
- **What is said:** "I'm between jobs right now." / "I'm exploring new opportunities."
- **What is implicated:** I'm unemployed.
- **Mechanism:** Manner -- framing unemployment as a transition ("between") rather than a state softens the stigma.
- **Detection cue:** Temporal or transitional framing of a static situation.

### Example 4.4: Overly Formal Language to Signal Distance

- **Context:** Two friends have been in a fight. One says to the other:
- **What is said:** "I would appreciate it if you could return my belongings at your earliest convenience."
- **What is implicated:** I'm very angry with you and we are not on friendly terms. The formal, bureaucratic language signals emotional distance -- it's the register you'd use with a stranger or in a legal letter, not with a friend.
- **Mechanism:** Manner -- the mismatch between the expected register (casual, between friends) and the actual register (formal, distant) IS the message. The formality implicates that the relationship has been downgraded.
- **Detection cue:** Language that is more formal than the relationship normally warrants. When a friend suddenly sounds like a lawyer, they're communicating emotional distance.

### Example 4.5: Using Technical Jargon to Exclude

- **Context:** In a meeting with mixed technical and non-technical attendees, an engineer says:
- **What is said:** "The latency spike is likely due to a cache invalidation cascade triggered by the microservice's event-driven architecture."
- **What is implicated (beyond the technical content):** This is too complex for you to understand. I am the expert here. (When simpler explanations are available but not used.)
- **Mechanism:** Manner -- being unnecessarily obscure when clearer language is available signals that the obscurity itself is doing work -- either excluding non-experts, asserting expertise, or avoiding accountability.
- **Detection cue:** Jargon used where simpler explanations would serve. The question to ask: "Could they have said this more simply?" If yes, the complexity is intentional.

### Example 4.6: The Conspicuous Pause

- **Context:** Someone asks, "Is she a good manager?"
- **What is said:** "She's... [pause]... dedicated."
- **What is implicated:** The pause signals that the speaker is carefully choosing a word that is technically positive but not what they really think. The hesitation implicates that their first instinct was to say something negative.
- **Mechanism:** Manner -- the hesitation and careful word choice signal that a more direct answer would be unflattering.
- **Detection cue:** Noticeable pauses, restarts, or hedging before a compliment. "She's... hmm... you know, she's... very organized."

### Example 4.7: Changing the Order to Implicate Causation

- **Context:** Describing a sequence of events.
- **What is said:** "She drank five cocktails and drove into a tree."
- **What is implicated:** The drinking *caused* the accident. The speaker hasn't explicitly said so, but the order (drinking, then accident) and the conjunction ("and") invite the causal inference.
- **Mechanism:** Manner -- Grice's submaxim "be orderly" leads listeners to interpret sequential descriptions as causally connected. Saying "She drove into a tree and drank five cocktails" would NOT implicate that drinking caused the crash.
- **Detection cue:** When events are described in sequence with "and," listeners typically infer that the first event caused or led to the second.

### Example 4.8: The Elaborate Non-Answer

- **Context:** A politician is asked, "Did you authorize the wiretapping?"
- **What is said:** "I have always been committed to protecting the constitutional rights of all Americans and ensuring that our national security apparatus operates within the bounds of the law."
- **What is implicated:** The speaker is avoiding the question. The elaborate, general statement that doesn't actually answer "yes" or "no" implicates that a direct answer would be damaging.
- **Mechanism:** Manner -- being prolix (unnecessarily wordy) where brevity was expected. A simple "yes" or "no" was available and wasn't used.
- **Detection cue:** Long, general responses to specific yes/no questions. The ratio of words to information is the signal.

### Example 4.9: Using Someone's Full Name

- **Context:** A parent addresses a child.
- **What is said:** "Jonathan Michael Peterson, come here right now."
- **What is implicated:** You are in serious trouble. The full name signals formal authority and displeasure.
- **Mechanism:** Manner -- using a full name where a first name or nickname would be normal signals a shift to a more formal, authoritative register.
- **Detection cue:** Full names used by parents, partners, or close friends signal that something is wrong. The formality escalation is the message.

### Example 4.10: Scare Quotes in Speech

- **Context:** Describing someone's role.
- **What is said:** He's their "expert" on the subject. (with air quotes)
- **What is implicated:** He's not actually an expert. The scare quotes signal that the speaker is using someone else's word while distancing themselves from it.
- **Mechanism:** Manner -- the air quotes (or verbal emphasis on the word) signal that the word is being used non-standardly -- specifically, that the speaker disputes the accuracy of the term.
- **Detection cue:** Audible emphasis or visible air quotes around a key word. "She did a 'thorough' job" = she didn't do a thorough job.

### Example 4.11: The Passive-to-Active Shift

- **Context:** Discussing who was responsible for an error.
- **What is said (original report):** "Mistakes were made." (passive voice, no agent)
- **What is implicated:** Someone made mistakes, but I'm not going to say who -- possibly because it was me.
- **Mechanism:** Manner -- using passive voice where active voice would be more natural ("I made mistakes" or "The team made mistakes") obscures responsibility. The obscurity itself is the information.
- **Detection cue:** Passive voice in contexts where identifying the agent would be natural and relevant. "Mistakes were made" vs. "I made mistakes."

### Example 4.12: The Overly Specific Denial

- **Context:** Someone is asked, "Did you eat the last cookie?"
- **What is said:** "I did not eat the last cookie."
- **What is implicated (potentially):** If this is delivered with unusual emphasis on "last" -- "I did not eat the *last* cookie" -- the implicature may be "I ate other cookies, just not the last one." Unusual emphasis on a specific word narrows the denial.
- **Mechanism:** Manner -- placing emphasis on a particular word implicates that the emphasis is meaningful and the unemphasized parts may not hold.
- **Detection cue:** Suspiciously specific or narrow denials. "I didn't steal any money *from that account*" = I may have stolen from other accounts.

### Example 4.13: Hedging Language

- **Context:** Providing an opinion.
- **What is said:** "I sort of think that maybe it could potentially be somewhat of a problem."
- **What is implicated:** I think this is a problem but I'm softening my statement to avoid conflict or commitment.
- **Mechanism:** Manner -- excessive hedges ("sort of," "maybe," "could," "potentially," "somewhat") make a statement less clear than it needs to be, signaling the speaker's discomfort with being direct.
- **Detection cue:** Multiple hedging words stacked in one statement. Each hedge reduces the force of the claim, and the accumulation signals avoidance.

## 5. IMPLICATURE IN SPECIFIC CONTEXTS

### 5A. Workplace Implicatures

The workplace generates a rich set of implicatures because professional norms require politeness and indirectness even (especially) when delivering negative messages.

#### Performance Review Language

| What is written | What is implicated | Detection cue |
|---|---|---|
| "Meets expectations" | Does the minimum; not outstanding | Neutral phrasing in a context where positive phrasing is normal |
| "Room for improvement" | Currently underperforming | Euphemistic framing of a negative |
| "Has potential" | Is not currently performing well | Future-oriented praise = present-tense criticism |
| "Is a team player" | Doesn't stand out individually | Generic positive with no specific achievements |
| "Works well with supervision" | Cannot work independently | The qualification ("with supervision") is the real message |
| "Is very enthusiastic" | Enthusiasm exceeds competence | Energy-focused praise = ability gap |
| "Has made progress" | Started from a low baseline | Improvement framing implies the starting point was poor |
| "Adequate performance" | Barely acceptable | "Adequate" is the minimum passing grade |
| "Demonstrates creativity in problem-solving" | Doesn't follow standard procedures | Can be positive or negative depending on context |
| "Would benefit from additional training" | Is currently unable to do the job properly | Euphemistic framing of incompetence |

#### Email and Meeting Implicatures

| What is written/said | What is implicated | Detection cue |
|---|---|---|
| "Per my last email..." | I already told you this. Read more carefully. | Reference to a previous communication = frustration with repetition |
| "As previously discussed..." | We agreed on this. You're going back on the agreement. | Formal reminder of prior agreement |
| "Going forward, I'd prefer..." | You did something I didn't like. Don't do it again. | "Going forward" = criticizing past behavior through future-oriented language |
| "Just to clarify..." | You misunderstood (or are misrepresenting what was said). | "Clarify" = correct |
| "Correct me if I'm wrong, but..." | I'm confident I'm right and you're wrong. | False humility before an assertion |
| "I'm a little confused about..." | I think you made an error (and I'm annoyed). | "Confused" = you're wrong |
| "Thanks for your patience" | This took too long and I know it. | Thanking for patience acknowledges the imposition |
| "Let's take this offline" | This discussion is inappropriate for this audience/forum. | Redirecting = shutting down |
| "That's an interesting approach..." | I don't agree with your approach. | "Interesting" = wrong (see Section 6) |
| "I want to make sure we're aligned..." | I think we disagree and I want to resolve it. | "Aligned" = currently misaligned |

#### Meeting Dynamics

- **"Does anyone have anything else?"** (said while closing laptop) = This meeting is over.
- **"I think what [name] is trying to say is..."** = [Name] is communicating poorly and I'm taking over.
- **"That's a great question. Let me think about it."** = I don't have an answer / I don't want to answer that now.
- **"We should revisit this"** = We're not doing this now and probably won't later either.
- **"Let's table this for now"** = I want to stop discussing this topic.

### 5B. Dating and Romantic Interest Implicatures

#### Interest Signals

| What is said/done | What is implicated | Detection cue |
|---|---|---|
| "I'd love to do this again sometime" | I'm interested in seeing you again | Direct positive future reference |
| "It was nice meeting you" (without follow-up plans) | I'm not interested in seeing you again | Polite closure without continuation |
| "I'm really busy right now but..." (suggests specific alternative time) | I'm genuinely interested but actually busy | A counter-proposal signals genuine interest |
| "I'm really busy right now" (no alternative offered) | I'm not interested | Absence of counter-proposal = polite rejection |
| "You're such a good friend" | I don't have romantic interest | The "friend" classification is the message |
| "I'm not looking for anything serious right now" | I'm not interested in a serious relationship *with you* | The qualifier "right now" softens but the message is about the specific person |
| "Let's just see where things go" | I'm keeping my options open / low commitment | Refusal to define the relationship |
| "I need some space" | I want to reduce or end contact | "Space" is a euphemism for distance |
| "It's not you, it's me" | It's you. (Or: I'm ending this and trying to soften the blow.) | The classic deflection of blame |

#### Rejection Patterns

The key implicature in dating rejection: **the absence of enthusiasm IS the message.** If someone wants to see you, they make it happen. The specific excuses are usually not the real reason.

**Pattern to watch for:** Someone who:
1. Always has a reason they can't meet
2. Never suggests an alternative time
3. Responds to messages slowly and briefly
4. Uses polite but non-committal language

Each behavior individually is normal. The *pattern* implicates avoidance.

### 5C. Family Implicatures

#### Parental Hints

| What is said | What is implicated | Detection cue |
|---|---|---|
| "Your cousin just got promoted" | Why haven't you been promoted? You should be doing better. | Mentioning a peer's achievement in a context where your own achievement would be relevant |
| "Mrs. Johnson's daughter just got engaged" | When are you going to get engaged? | Same pattern -- bringing up a peer's milestone |
| "I'm not getting any younger" | I want grandchildren soon | Mentioning aging in response to questions about (or absence of) grandchildren |
| "We just want you to be happy" | We don't approve of your choice but won't say so directly | This phrase is often used when the parent's definition of "happy" differs from the child's |
| "That's nice, dear" | I'm not actually interested / I don't approve but I'm not going to argue | Dismissive minimization |
| "Do whatever you think is best" | I disagree with what you're planning to do | False deference that actually communicates disapproval |
| "I saw an article about [your career field/lifestyle]..." | I'm worried about your choices | Sending articles as proxy criticism |
| "How's your... friend?" (emphasis on "friend") | Are you dating this person? I suspect you're in a relationship you haven't told me about. | Marked emphasis or pause before a relationship term |

#### Sibling Dynamics

- **"That's SO you"** = That was a characteristic mistake/behavior (affectionate teasing or genuine criticism depending on tone).
- **"I'm not surprised"** = I expected you to fail/make that choice (can be affectionate or cutting).
- **"Ask Mom"** = I don't want to be involved in this decision / I don't agree but don't want to fight about it.

### 5D. Friendship Implicatures

#### Checking In Without Imposing

| What is said | What is implicated | Detection cue |
|---|---|---|
| "Just checking in -- no need to reply" | I'm worried about you but I don't want to pressure you | Explicit removal of response obligation = awareness of the person's distress |
| "I made too much food, do you want some?" | I think you might need help (with food, with company) and I'm giving you a face-saving way to accept | Framing help as the helper's excess rather than the recipient's need |
| "I'll be in your neighborhood on Saturday" | I want to see you / I'm available if you want company | Creating an opening without making a direct request |
| "Let me know if you need anything" | I want to help but I don't know what you need | This is genuine but non-specific. More committed: "I'm bringing dinner Tuesday." |
| "Hey, are you okay? You seemed off yesterday" | Something seemed wrong and I'm concerned. I want you to know I noticed. | Referencing specific observations rather than general inquiry |
| "Do you want to talk about it?" | I've noticed something is wrong and I'm available to listen | Offering without demanding |

#### The Friendship Decline Signal

Similar to dating, the pattern of declining without counter-proposing is the key implicature. A friend who consistently can't make plans but never initiates alternatives is communicating reduced interest in the friendship -- not through any single declination, but through the pattern.

### 5E. Service Encounter Implicatures

#### Customer Complaint Escalation

Service encounters have their own implicature ladder, where each level signals increasing dissatisfaction:

| What is said | What is implicated | Escalation level |
|---|---|---|
| "Excuse me, I think there might be an issue with..." | I've found a problem (low threat) | Level 1: Polite notification |
| "This isn't what I ordered" | Fix this mistake | Level 2: Direct complaint |
| "I've been waiting for quite a while" | This service is unacceptably slow | Level 3: Impatient but still engaging |
| "Is there someone else I could speak with?" | You're not resolving this and I want someone with more authority | Level 4: Implicit threat to escalate |
| "I'd like to speak to a manager" | The current service has failed and I'm escalating | Level 5: Formal escalation |
| "What's your name? I'd like to note who I spoke with" | I'm building a record for a formal complaint | Level 6: Documented complaint threat |
| "I'll be writing a review about this experience" | I'm going to cause reputational damage | Level 7: Public threat |
| "I'll be contacting corporate / my lawyer" | This will have consequences beyond this interaction | Level 8: Institutional escalation |

#### Service Provider Implicatures

| What is said | What is implicated |
|---|---|
| "I'll see what I can do" | I probably can't help, but I'll try (or: I'll go through the motions) |
| "That's our policy" | I'm hiding behind the rules to avoid responsibility |
| "I understand your frustration" | I'm acknowledging your emotion without agreeing to fix the problem |
| "Let me check with my manager" | I don't have authority to do what you're asking (or: I need a break from this interaction) |
| "Is there anything else I can help you with?" | This interaction is over / I've done what I'm going to do |

## 6. COMMON ENGLISH PHRASES THAT ARE ALMOST ENTIRELY IMPLICATURE

These phrases have literal meanings that almost nobody intends when they say them. The implicature has become the primary meaning, and the literal meaning is just a shell.

### Phrases That Signal Disagreement or Disapproval

| # | Phrase | Literal meaning | Implicated meaning | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | "With all due respect..." | I respect you | I'm about to say something disrespectful. "Due respect" may be zero. | British equivalent of Southern US "bless your heart." Some describe it as "silently following the clause with 'which is none.'" |
| 2 | "That's one way to look at it." | Your perspective is one of many | I disagree with your perspective. It's not a good way to look at it. | The emphasis on "one" implicates that better ways exist. |
| 3 | "That's... interesting." | That is interesting | I disapprove, disagree, or find this strange. | Delivered with a pause and flat or rising intonation. "Interesting" without enthusiasm = negative. |
| 4 | "I hear what you say" | I'm listening to you | I disagree and don't want to discuss it further. | British English especially. Distinguished from "I understand" which may signal agreement. |
| 5 | "That's a very brave decision." | You are courageous | You're making a terrible mistake. | British English. "Brave" = reckless. Also applies to "bold choice." |
| 6 | "I'm sure you know best." | I trust your judgment | I think you're wrong but I'm done arguing. | False deference. The emphasis on "you" distances the speaker from the decision. |
| 7 | "Correct me if I'm wrong, but..." | I may be wrong | I'm confident I'm right. I'm framing my correction of you as a question to be polite. | Pre-emptive disclaimer that actually signals confidence. |
| 8 | "Not to be rude, but..." | I don't want to be rude | I'm about to be rude. | Same pattern as "no offense, but..." -- the disclaimer signals awareness that what follows IS offensive. |
| 9 | "Fair enough." | That's fair | I disagree but I'm dropping it. | Concession without conviction. Signals the end of discussion, not agreement. |
| 10 | "If you say so." | I accept your statement | I don't believe you but I'm not going to argue. | Puts all responsibility for the claim on the other person. |

### Phrases That Signal "No" Without Saying It

| # | Phrase | Literal meaning | Implicated meaning | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 11 | "We'll see." | The outcome is uncertain | Probably no. | Parents to children especially. "We'll see" almost never becomes "yes." |
| 12 | "I'll think about it." | I will consider it | No, but I'm deferring the rejection. | In most contexts, if the answer were yes, they'd say yes now. |
| 13 | "Let me get back to you." | I'll respond later | I might not respond. I'm buying time. | Genuine if followed up; a soft no if never followed up. |
| 14 | "Maybe next time." | There may be a next time | There probably won't be a next time. | Softened rejection that avoids finality. |
| 15 | "I wish I could, but..." | I want to but I can't | I can't or don't want to. The "wish" may or may not be genuine. | The clause after "but" is the real message. |

### Phrases That Signal Emotional States

| # | Phrase | Literal meaning | Implicated meaning | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 16 | "I'm fine." | I'm doing well | I'm not fine, but I don't want to discuss it. (Or: I'm upset and you should notice.) | One of the most universally recognized implicatures. Tone is everything: flat "I'm fine" = not fine. |
| 17 | "It's fine." / "It doesn't matter." | The situation is acceptable | I'm upset but suppressing it. | Often delivered with a sigh, tight voice, or turned-away posture. |
| 18 | "Do what you want." / "Whatever you want." | I'm giving you freedom to choose | I disagree with what you're about to do but I'm withdrawing from the decision. | Often signals resentment or passive withdrawal from conflict. |
| 19 | "I'm not mad." | I'm not angry | I am very likely mad. | The unsolicited denial of an emotion often confirms the emotion. |
| 20 | "It's not a big deal." | This is minor | This IS a big deal but I'm pretending it's not (either to avoid conflict or because I feel my concern won't be taken seriously). | Context-dependent: sometimes genuine, sometimes the opposite. |

### Phrases That Are Social Lubricant

| # | Phrase | Literal meaning | Implicated meaning | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 21 | "No offense, but..." | I intend no offense | I'm about to say something offensive and this disclaimer doesn't prevent the offense. | The disclaimer signals awareness that what follows IS offensive -- which is why the disclaimer is needed. |
| 22 | "Bless your heart." | May God bless you | (Southern US) I think you're being foolish, naive, or pitiable. | Highly context-dependent: can be genuinely sympathetic, affectionately teasing, or a polite insult. Prosody (tone, speed, pitch) is the decoder. Slow, drawn-out = warm. Clipped and flat = dismissive. |
| 23 | "I don't want to bother you, but..." | I'm being considerate | I'm about to bother you. | The disclaimer pre-acknowledges the imposition without actually preventing it. |
| 24 | "I hate to ask, but..." | I'm reluctant to make this request | I'm going to ask anyway. My reluctance is performed, not actual (or is actual but overridden by need). | The reluctance display is a politeness strategy. |
| 25 | "Not to gossip, but..." | I don't want to gossip | I'm about to gossip. | Same "disclaimer + violation" pattern. |
| 26 | "I'm just saying..." | This is a neutral observation | I'm expressing a strong opinion and using "just saying" to reduce my accountability for it. | Allows the speaker to make a pointed comment while claiming they're merely observing. |
| 27 | "To be honest..." / "Honestly..." | I'm being truthful now | (a) What I'm about to say might be surprising or blunt, OR (b) What I said before may not have been fully honest. | Implicitly raises the question: were you not being honest before? |
| 28 | "I'll let you go." | I'm freeing you from this conversation | I want to end this conversation but I'm framing it as your need, not mine. | Common on phone calls. The speaker is the one who wants to stop, but attributes the desire to the listener. |
| 29 | "Sure." (flat tone) | Yes | Reluctant agreement. I'll comply but I'm not enthusiastic. | "Sure" with enthusiasm = genuine agreement. "Sure." with flat tone = grudging compliance. |
| 30 | "Asking for a friend." | My friend has this question | I have this question but I'm embarrassed to ask directly. | So widely recognized as a self-referencing joke that it's now often used self-consciously for humor. |

| # | Phrase | Literal meaning | Implicated meaning | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 31 | "I'm not going to lie..." | I won't deceive you | What I'm about to say might be surprising or unwelcome but it's true. | Variant of "to be honest." |
| 32 | "Good luck with that." | I wish you luck | (sarcastic) You'll need luck because this won't work. | Tone determines whether this is genuine or sarcastic. Flat or emphatic delivery = sarcasm. |
| 33 | "Isn't that nice." / "Isn't that special." | Expression of appreciation | (Southern US / sarcastic) I'm judging you. | Flat intonation + absence of question prosody = sarcastic. |
| 34 | "You do you." | Be yourself | I disagree with your choice but I'm not going to stop you. It's your problem. | Signals resignation or judgment disguised as acceptance. |
| 35 | "I could be wrong, but..." | I acknowledge uncertainty | I'm confident I'm right. | Same pattern as "correct me if I'm wrong" -- false humility before an assertion. |
| 36 | "That's above my pay grade." | This exceeds my authority | I don't want to deal with this / I'm refusing responsibility. | Can be genuine (authority issue) or evasive (responsibility avoidance). |
| 37 | "It is what it is." | The situation is as it appears | I'm frustrated but I've accepted this can't be changed. There's nothing to discuss. | Signals resignation. Conversation closer. |
| 38 | "No worries." | Don't worry about it | (In response to an apology) It was actually a problem but I'm being gracious. OR: It genuinely wasn't a problem. | Context-dependent. "No worries" after a serious problem = gracious forgiveness, not genuine unconcern. |
| 39 | "I'm sorry you feel that way." | I sympathize with your feelings | I'm not actually apologizing for what I did. I'm reframing the problem as your emotional reaction. | The classic non-apology. Acknowledges feelings without accepting responsibility. |
| 40 | "With all due respect, I think you'll find..." | Respectful correction | You're wrong and I'm about to prove it. | Stacking "with all due respect" on "I think you'll find" doubles the implied condescension. |

## 7. DETECTION FRAMEWORK: A Practical Decision Tree

For each utterance, an autistic individual can systematically check:

### Step 1: Is the response surprisingly incomplete?
- Does it answer a narrower question than was asked?
- Does it praise something peripheral while ignoring the central topic?
- Does it use a weaker word than expected ("some" vs. "all," "fine" vs. "great")?
- **If yes: Quantity implicature.** The missing information is negative.

### Step 2: Is the statement obviously false?
- Would both speaker and listener know this statement isn't literally true?
- Is the tone mismatched with the content (cheerful tone + bad situation)?
- Is the claim impossibly extreme (a million, forever, dying)?
- **If yes: Quality implicature.** Look for irony (opposite meaning), hyperbole (exaggerated degree), metaphor (comparison), or litotes (understatement via negation).

### Step 3: Does the response seem off-topic?
- Does the response not directly answer the question?
- Can you find a logical bridge between the response and the question? (e.g., "I have an early flight" bridges to "I can't come to the party")
- **If yes: Relevance implicature.** The bridge IS the implicated meaning.

### Step 4: Is the response oddly phrased?
- Is the language more formal, vague, elaborate, or indirect than the situation calls for?
- Did the speaker conspicuously avoid a direct word?
- Is the word order unusual?
- **If yes: Manner implicature.** The unusual phrasing itself carries meaning.

### Step 5: Is this a Known Phrase?
- Does the phrase appear in Section 6?
- Does it match a common pattern (disclaimer + violation, false deference, lukewarm agreement)?
- **If yes: Apply the documented implicature for that phrase.**

### Step 6: When in Doubt -- The Enthusiasm Test

**The single most reliable detector of implicature in English:** If someone could show more enthusiasm and doesn't, the lack of enthusiasm IS the message.

- Compare what was said to what *could* have been said.
- If the response is the minimum positive assessment, the real assessment is probably below positive.
- If the response avoids specifics when specifics would be easy, the specifics are probably negative.

## 8. CULTURAL AND CONTEXTUAL NOTES

### British vs. American English

British English uses more understatement and litotes. A British "quite good" can mean "mediocre" or even "terrible." American "quite good" more often means "really good." The famous British politeness chart:

| British phrase | British meaning | What foreigners think it means |
|---|---|---|
| "I hear what you say" | I disagree and don't want to discuss it | He accepted my point of view |
| "With the greatest respect" | I think you're an idiot | He's listening to me |
| "That's not bad" | That's good | That's not good |
| "Quite good" | A bit disappointing | Quite good |
| "That is a very brave proposal" | You are insane | He thinks I have courage |
| "I would suggest..." | Do it or face consequences | Think about it, but do what you like |
| "Oh, by the way..." | The primary purpose of this discussion | That wasn't important |
| "I was a bit disappointed that..." | I'm angry that... | It doesn't really matter |
| "Very interesting" | That is clearly nonsense | They are impressed |
| "I'll bear it in mind" | I've forgotten it already | They'll probably do it |
| "I'm sure it's my fault" | It's your fault | It was their fault |
| "You must come for dinner" | Not an invitation; a polite noise | I should expect an invitation soon |
| "I almost agree" | I don't agree at all | They're about to agree |
| "I only have a few minor comments" | I'm going to rewrite the whole thing | They made a few minor edits |

### Southern US English

"Bless your heart" operates as a multitool -- it can express genuine sympathy, affectionate teasing, or polite contempt. The decoder is prosody (rhythm, pitch, pacing):
- **Slow, drawn-out, rising intonation:** Genuine warmth or sympathy
- **Clipped, flat, after a brief pause:** Polite dismissal or veiled insult

Related Southern phrases:
- **"Isn't that nice/special/different"** = I'm judging you
- **"Well, I never"** = I'm scandalized (genuine or performed)
- **"She means well"** = She's doing something wrong but I'll be charitable about her intentions

### Gender Patterns

Research notes that some implicature patterns have gendered tendencies (not rules):
- Women are more likely to use indirect rejection (in dating contexts) due to safety concerns
- "I'm fine" as a non-fine signal is culturally associated with women (though used by all genders)
- Workplace implicature patterns may differ by gender, with women's direct communication sometimes perceived differently than men's

### Power Dynamics

Implicature use correlates with power asymmetry:
- **Lower power to higher power:** More hedging, more indirectness, more face-saving ("I was wondering if perhaps it might be possible to...")
- **Higher power to lower power:** Can be direct, but implicature is used for criticism ("There's room for improvement" rather than "Your work is poor")
- **Equal power:** Implicature use depends on relationship closeness and cultural norms

## 9. KEY CONCEPTS GLOSSARY

**Conversational Implicature:** Something a speaker means but doesn't literally say, which the listener infers based on the assumption that the speaker is being cooperative.

**Grice's Cooperative Principle:** The assumption that speakers in a conversation are trying to be helpful, truthful, relevant, and clear.

**Flouting a Maxim:** Deliberately and obviously violating a conversational maxim so that the listener will recognize the violation and search for the implied meaning.

**Scalar Implicature:** An implicature generated by using a weaker term from an ordered scale, which implicates that the stronger term doesn't apply. ("Some" implicates "not all.")

**Particularized Implicature:** An implicature that depends on specific contextual details and cannot be predicted from the words alone.

**Generalized Implicature:** An implicature that arises in most contexts regardless of specific details. ("Some students passed" almost always implicates "not all.")

**Litotes:** A figure of speech using negation of the opposite to express a positive (or negative). "Not bad" = good. "Not the brightest" = quite dim.

**Dead Metaphor:** A metaphor so conventionalized that speakers no longer process its literal falseness. "Heart of gold," "foot of the mountain."

**Face:** A person's public self-image. Much implicature exists to protect face -- the speaker's (avoiding being rude) or the listener's (avoiding making them feel bad).

**Non-apology Apology:** A statement structured like an apology that doesn't actually accept responsibility. "I'm sorry you feel that way" = your feelings are the problem, not my actions.
