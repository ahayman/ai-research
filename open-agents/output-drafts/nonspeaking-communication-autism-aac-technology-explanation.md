# AAC Technology: How It Works

## Overview

Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC) encompasses the tools, strategies, and techniques that supplement or replace spoken communication for individuals who cannot rely on speech alone. For non-speaking autistic individuals -- estimated at 25-30% of the autistic population -- AAC represents the most evidence-supported pathway to functional communication. The term "augmentative" refers to methods that supplement existing speech; "alternative" refers to methods that replace speech entirely. In practice, the distinction is fluid: the same individual may use AAC as augmentative in some contexts and alternative in others, depending on energy, stress level, environment, and communication partner.

**What is AAC?**
AAC is any communication method other than natural speech. It ranges from gestures and picture cards through simple voice-output buttons to sophisticated tablet-based apps and eye-gaze controlled speech-generating devices. Modern AAC for autism is dominated by high-tech solutions -- primarily iPad-based apps and dedicated speech-generating devices -- but effective AAC often involves multiple tools at different technology levels used simultaneously.

**Why does it exist?**
Approximately 25-30% of autistic individuals are non-speaking or minimally speaking. For decades, the absence of speech was conflated with the absence of thought, intelligence, or communicative intent. AAC exists to bridge the gap between what a person wants to communicate and what their motor system allows them to produce through speech. The critical insight driving modern AAC is that speech is a motor act, and the inability to perform that motor act does not imply the absence of the cognitive and linguistic capacity that speech typically conveys.

**Key takeaways:**
- AAC does not inhibit speech development -- decades of research confirm it may actually support speech emergence
- There are no cognitive or linguistic prerequisites for AAC -- the "readiness" myth has been thoroughly debunked
- AAC abandonment rates remain high (30-50%), driven primarily by inadequate training and attitudinal barriers
- Family engagement is the single strongest predictor of sustained AAC success
- Most AAC users benefit from tools at multiple technology levels (multimodal communication)

### Confidence Overview

The research underpinning this explanation was independently validated with an overall confidence rating of 9/10. All major claims were confirmed. Minor uncertainties exist around specific app pricing and language availability details (treated as approximate throughout this explanation).

## Core Components

### Speech-Generating Devices (SGDs)

A speech-generating device is any hardware that produces speech output from symbol or text selection. SGDs exist in two broad categories: dedicated devices built specifically for AAC, and consumer tablets (primarily iPads) running AAC software.

**Dedicated devices** -- such as the Tobii Dynavox I-Series (I-13, I-16) and PRC-Saltillo Accent series -- are purpose-built for communication. They offer multiple access methods on a single device (eye-gaze, touch, switch scanning), ruggedized construction for daily use, wheelchair mounting options, and environmental control integration (operating lights, doors, phones, and computers through the same device). Dedicated SGDs typically cost $5,000-$15,000 or more, but are often funded through Medicaid, Medicare, private insurance, or school districts under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).

**Consumer tablets** running AAC apps are significantly less expensive ($500-$1,500 including the app), more socially normalized (everyone uses iPads), and easier to replace. However, they offer fewer access method options, are less durable, and face more variable insurance coverage.

A 2025 systematic review of 20 studies (1998-2023) found that SGD interventions for autistic preschoolers produced small to high effects on requesting and social communication outcomes.

### High-Tech AAC Applications

Three major AAC apps serve the autism community, each implementing a different philosophy of vocabulary organization:

**Proloquo2Go** (AssistiveWare) uses the research-based Crescendo vocabulary system, which progressively reveals more words as competence grows. Three pre-programmed levels -- Basic Communication, Intermediate Core, and Advanced Core -- provide access to approximately 4,750 unique words. Core words maintain consistent screen positions across all levels to support motor learning. Available on iOS.

**TouchChat** (PRC-Saltillo) offers multiple vocabulary sets including WordPower. It supports multiple languages including English, Spanish, Canadian French, and Mandarin/Simplified Chinese. Priced at approximately $300.

**LAMP Words for Life** (PRC-Saltillo) is designed specifically for autism, based on Language Acquisition through Motor Planning principles. Every symbol occupies the same position on the screen regardless of vocabulary level, maximizing motor memory. One-time purchase at approximately $300.

**TD Snap** (Tobii Dynavox) is a communication platform with eye-gaze, touch, and switch access, designed to work with Tobii Dynavox dedicated hardware.

> **Evidence note**: The research stated TouchChat was "approximately $100 less than Proloquo2Go." Validation found both apps are priced in the approximately $300 range. Treat price comparisons as approximate.

### Core Vocabulary Systems

Modern AAC is built on a principle with robust research support: a small set of high-frequency words accounts for the vast majority of all communication. Approximately 200-400 core words -- function words like I, want, go, help, more, not, like, stop, that -- constitute roughly 80% of everything anyone says, regardless of age, language, or context. These are the words that make sentences work.

The remaining 20% of communication consists of fringe vocabulary: context-specific nouns, names, and specialized words (pizza, school, Mom, swimming). Fringe vocabulary is what makes communication personal and specific; core vocabulary is what makes it generative and flexible.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-2-core-fringe-vocabulary -->

The motor planning principle is fundamental to how core vocabulary systems work. Core words always occupy the same position on the communication display, so that selecting them becomes automatic through motor memory -- analogous to how a touch-typist does not look at the keyboard. When a user learns that "want" is always in a specific position, accessing that word eventually becomes as automatic as reaching for a light switch. This is why AAC apps like Proloquo2Go and LAMP maintain consistent button placement across vocabulary levels: changing the layout disrupts motor learning and forces the user to relearn the physical act of communication.

### Access Methods

The way a person physically interacts with their AAC device is called the access method, and selecting the right one is a critical clinical decision. The wrong access method can render an otherwise appropriate AAC system unusable.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-3-access-methods -->

**Direct touch** (touchscreen) is the most common access method -- the user touches symbols on a screen with a finger. It requires sufficient fine motor coordination to point accurately, which many but not all non-speaking autistic individuals possess.

**Eye-gaze tracking** allows users to select vocabulary items by looking at targets on the screen. Tobii Dynavox I-Series devices use infrared cameras to track corneal reflection and pupil position, working in varied lighting conditions including outdoors. Eye-gaze tracking bypasses hand motor control entirely, providing communication access to individuals with severe motor impairments. The same device enables environmental control -- operating lights, doors, phones, and computers through gaze alone.

**Switch scanning** serves individuals who can reliably activate at least one switch (a button, proximity sensor, or sip-and-puff device). The device highlights options sequentially, and the user activates the switch when their desired item is highlighted. Switch scanning is slower than direct selection methods but can provide access when no other method is available.

**Head tracking** uses a camera to track head position and movement to control an on-screen cursor.

**Partner-assisted scanning** is a low-tech fallback for individuals who cannot use any device-based access method. A communication partner verbally offers choices, and the individual signals (with an eye blink, vocalization, or movement) when the desired option is named.

**Brain-computer interfaces (BCIs)** represent emerging technology. In 2025, UC Davis and Berkeley researchers demonstrated real-time speech synthesis from brain activity for an ALS patient using neural implants. This technology is not yet clinically available for autism, but research is beginning to explore its potential.

Access method selection is iterative, not one-time. Motor profiles change. Energy levels fluctuate. Many users employ multiple access methods across different contexts -- touch when rested and regulated, switch scanning when fatigued, eye-gaze when motor access is unreliable. The assessment process involves trials of multiple methods with ongoing reassessment.

## How It Works

### Step 1: Assessment and System Selection

The AAC journey begins with assessment by a speech-language pathologist (SLP). The SLP evaluates motor capabilities, cognitive profile, vision, hearing, and existing communication strategies. Multiple access methods are trialed. Existing communication behaviors -- gestures, vocalizations, leading by the hand, challenging behavior used communicatively -- are documented, because these represent the individual's current communication system that AAC will augment, not replace.

A critical principle governs this step: **there are no cognitive or linguistic prerequisites for AAC**. The concept of "AAC readiness" -- the idea that a person must demonstrate certain cognitive milestones before they are "ready" for AAC -- has been thoroughly debunked in the clinical literature. AAC should be introduced as early as possible and should not wait for failed speech therapy attempts. AAC and speech therapy are complementary, not sequential.

### Step 2: Vocabulary Selection and Customization

Once a system is selected, the SLP and family customize vocabulary for the individual's life. Core vocabulary provides the base. Fringe vocabulary is personalized for home, school, community, and the individual's specific interests. Visual appearance, button size, and layout are adjusted for the user's motor and visual profile.

Customization is not a one-time event. Vocabulary must evolve as the individual grows, changes environments, develops new interests, and expands their communicative needs. An AAC system for a 4-year-old requesting snacks looks very different from one for a 16-year-old navigating social relationships.

### Step 3: Aided Language Modeling

Aided language modeling -- sometimes called aided language stimulation -- is the primary teaching strategy for AAC. Communication partners model AAC use by pointing to symbols on the device while speaking naturally. "Do you WANT [points to 'want' on device] to GO [points to 'go'] OUTSIDE [points to 'outside']?" This shows the AAC user how to construct messages using their device without requiring them to imitate immediately.

The research is clear: aided language modeling is more effective than drill-based instruction for building AAC competence. It teaches communication in context, demonstrates how symbols combine to express meaning, and respects the learner's own timeline for productive use.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-4-learning-trajectory -->

### Step 4: Functional Communication Training

As the user develops understanding of the symbol system, structured opportunities to use AAC are provided across contexts. Communication training progresses through communicative functions: requesting (getting needs met), rejecting (saying no), commenting (sharing observations), asking questions (requesting information), and social interaction (greetings, turn-taking, jokes).

Evidence shows that multicomponent interventions -- combining SGDs with modeling, time delay strategies, and peer education -- produce the strongest outcomes. The most robust evidence exists for requesting; evidence for higher communicative functions (commenting, questioning, narrating) is growing but less extensive.

### Step 5: Motor Learning and Automaticity

With consistent use over time, the physical act of accessing vocabulary becomes automatic through motor learning. The user develops muscle memory for high-frequency selections, just as a pianist develops muscle memory for scales. This is why consistent symbol placement is so critical -- and why disruptions to layout (system transitions, updates that change button positions) can temporarily regress communication fluency.

The Crescendo approach (used by Proloquo2Go) manages motor learning by progressively revealing more vocabulary while keeping existing buttons in their established positions. New words appear in new positions; old words never move.

### Step 6: Ongoing Support and System Evolution

AAC is not a device delivered in a box. It is a lifelong communication system that requires ongoing support: regular vocabulary updates, access method reassessment, technology upgrades, transition planning between educational settings, and coordination of adult services. The SLP, family, educators, peers, and the AAC user themselves all play ongoing roles.

## The Abandonment Problem

Across all AAC modalities, device abandonment or significant underuse is estimated at 30-50% of users. This is not a failure of the users. It is a failure of the system surrounding them.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-5-barriers-abandonment -->

Five categories of barriers drive abandonment:

**Device factors**: Poor customization, limited vocabulary, slow access speed, and inadequate durability. An AAC system that does not contain the words a person needs, or that takes too long to produce a message, will not be used.

**Training factors**: Inadequate training of communication partners is the most addressable barrier. If the people in a user's life do not know how to model AAC, respond to AAC-based communication, and create opportunities for AAC use, the device sits in a backpack. Staff turnover in schools compounds this problem -- new aides and teachers may not know how to support the user's AAC system.

**Attitudinal factors**: The myth that AAC inhibits speech development continues to delay AAC introduction. Low expectations ("they can't learn to use that"), stigma around visible device use, and the "readiness" gatekeeping that demands cognitive prerequisites all prevent timely AAC access.

**Systemic factors**: Funding barriers, insurance limitations, long wait times for evaluation, and gaps in support during school transitions (elementary to middle, middle to high school, high school to adult services) leave users without consistent AAC support during critical periods.

**Individual factors**: Motor challenges that change over time, sensory sensitivities to device sounds or screens, and the sheer energy cost of communication through AAC -- which is slower and more effortful than speech -- contribute to fatigue-based underuse.

**Family engagement is the strongest protective factor.** When families are actively involved in the AAC journey -- modeling at home, integrating the device into daily routines, advocating for school support -- abandonment rates drop significantly.

## The Speech Question

The single most persistent myth in AAC is that using a communication device will prevent a child from developing speech. This myth continues to delay AAC introduction for children who need it, sometimes by years.

The evidence is unambiguous: **AAC does not inhibit speech development.** A systematic review found that across 27 of 27 studies, AAC interventions showed an increase in speech production or no change. No studies reported a decrease. A separate systematic review specific to autistic children confirmed that AAC "does not hinder speech production" and "may also support speech production."

The mechanism likely involves reduced communicative frustration (when a person can get their needs met through AAC, the pressure and distress around communication decrease, creating conditions more favorable to speech emergence) and the provision of a visual-motor scaffold for language (the AAC system makes language visible and persistent, unlike transient speech).

## Practical Examples

### Jordyn Zimmerman: What Delayed AAC Costs

Jordyn Zimmerman is a non-speaking autistic woman who was not given access to AAC until age 18. For 18 years, she was placed in segregated educational settings and assessed as having intellectual disability. After receiving an iPad with Proloquo2Go, she demonstrated literacy, intelligence, and communicative competence that had been unrecognized for her entire life.

Zimmerman subsequently earned a Bachelor's degree in Education Policy from Ohio University, a Master's degree from Boston College (she is currently pursuing an MBA from Quantic), was named to the inaugural Forbes Accessibility 100 in 2025, serves as Board Chair of CommunicationFIRST, and is a two-time Presidential appointee and Apple Distinguished Educator. Every one of these accomplishments was enabled by access to AAC technology that should have been provided years earlier.

Her case illustrates the devastating cost of delayed AAC introduction -- and the catastrophic consequences of the "readiness" myth that denied her communication access for 18 years.

### The Speech Facilitation Effect

A 4-year-old autistic child with fewer than 5 spoken words begins using an AAC app with consistent aided language modeling at home and school. After 6 months of AAC use, the child has approximately 50 words on their device AND has begun producing 15-20 spoken words -- more than tripling their pre-AAC speech output. The AAC device did not replace speech; it created the conditions for speech to emerge.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

**Situational AAC use**: Some autistic individuals who can speak in many contexts lose reliable speech access during stress, overload, shutdown, or meltdown. AAC serves as a supplementary mode -- "part-time AAC" -- rather than a primary one. This is an underrecognized use case.

**Multilingual AAC**: Most AAC apps are designed for monolingual use, primarily in English. Families who speak languages not well-supported by major AAC platforms face significant barriers. Research on multilingual AAC is virtually nonexistent.

**Adult AAC introduction**: Most AAC research focuses on children. Adults who have spent decades without communication access face unique challenges: entrenched behavioral patterns, a lack of age-appropriate research, and service systems not designed for adult AAC introduction.

**System transitions**: Moving between AAC systems (from one app to another, from a dedicated device to a tablet) disrupts motor learning and can cause temporary communication regression. Careful transition planning is essential.

## Common Misconceptions

### Misconception: "A person needs to show they're ready before getting AAC"

**Reality**: There are no evidence-based prerequisites for AAC. The concept of "AAC readiness" has been thoroughly debunked. AAC should be introduced as early as possible, regardless of cognitive or linguistic assessment results.

**Why people think this**: The readiness myth persists because some professionals were trained in an era when AAC was considered a "last resort" after speech therapy failed, and because some assessments implicitly require demonstration of skills that the AAC is meant to provide.

### Misconception: "AAC is for people who will never speak"

**Reality**: AAC is for anyone who cannot rely on speech alone in any context. This includes individuals who speak in some situations but not others, those whose speech is unreliable under stress, and those who use both speech and AAC depending on context.

### Misconception: "Low-tech AAC is for lower-functioning people"

**Reality**: Technology level should match motor profile, communication needs, and context -- not presumed cognitive level. A person may use a high-tech SGD at school and a simple picture card in the swimming pool.

## Summary

### Key Points

1. AAC encompasses all communication methods beyond natural speech, from picture cards to eye-gaze controlled speech-generating devices, and most users benefit from multiple tools simultaneously
2. AAC does not inhibit speech -- decades of research show it may facilitate speech emergence -- and there are no prerequisites for AAC access
3. The 30-50% abandonment rate is driven by system failures (inadequate training, funding barriers, myths) rather than user failures; family engagement is the strongest protective factor

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| AAC | Augmentative and Alternative Communication -- tools and strategies supplementing or replacing speech |
| SGD | Speech-Generating Device -- hardware producing speech output from symbol/text selection (also: VOCA) |
| Core vocabulary | The ~200-400 highest-frequency words constituting ~80% of all utterances |
| Fringe vocabulary | Context-specific, lower-frequency words making up ~20% of utterances |
| Access method | The physical means by which a user selects items on an AAC device |
| Eye-gaze tracking | Technology tracking eye position to allow selection by looking |
| Switch scanning | Access method where items are highlighted sequentially and selected by switch activation |
| Aided language modeling | Teaching strategy where partners model AAC use while speaking naturally |
| Motor planning | The neurological process of planning and executing purposeful movements (praxis) |
| Abandonment | Discontinuation of AAC use, typically due to barriers rather than recovery of speech |
| Multimodal communication | Use of multiple communication modes by the same individual |
| LAMP | Language Acquisition through Motor Planning -- vocabulary approach emphasizing consistent icon placement |
| Crescendo | Proloquo2Go's vocabulary system that progressively reveals more words as competence grows |
