## 1. Why This Page Exists (Opening Reframe)

Performative allyship is the move where someone establishes themselves as an autism-affirming, autism-aware, autism-allied person — and uses that established identity as cover for manipulating, dismissing, or harming the autistic people they are supposedly allied with. *"I'm one of the good ones." "I'm not like other neurotypicals — I actually understand autism." "I read all the books." "I follow autistic creators." "I'm a parent of an autistic kid, I get it." "I work with autistic clients all day." "I used to be ableist, but I learned."* The credentials are real. The person did read the books. They do follow the creators. They do work with the clients. And the credentials are deployed, every time critique approaches, as the protective frame around behavior that is not actually autism-affirming at all.

This is the lowest-priority Tier 3 page in the build plan, and the page should be honest about why. **The empirical research base for performative allyship as a distinct manipulation pattern is thin.** Performative allyship is largely [COMMUNITY KNOWLEDGE] from autistic survivor accounts — it has been named, described, and cross-confirmed in autistic community spaces for years, but it has not been isolated as a research target in the way that diagnostic weaponization (Pearson, Douglas & Sedgewick), the credibility discount (Lim, Young & Brewer), or masking (Hull, Cook) have been. The pattern is real and recognizable; the peer-reviewed literature has not caught up. The page treats it because the autistic adults most active in autism community spaces are the ones most likely to encounter this pattern from people they initially trusted, and the recognition matters even when the evidence base is community rather than academic.

The reframe this page offers, in one sentence: **allyship is not a credential that exempts behavior from evaluation. It is a practice that is visible in behavior over time. When credentials are used to override the evaluation of behavior, the credentials are doing manipulation work regardless of how sincerely they were originally earned.** The strategies in section 6 are calibrated to hold that reframe without attacking legitimate allies, because attacking legitimate allies is the false positive this page most needs to avoid.

Section 4 is the center of the page for that reason. Real allies make mistakes. Real allies are sometimes clumsy. Real allies sometimes fall back on cultural scripts. Calling every clumsy ally *performative* is both unfair and counterproductive, and it would degrade the one resource — people whose allyship is real — that many autistic adults most need to keep in their lives. The distinguishing tools have to protect against false positives, and they are treated with appropriate care.

## 2. The Concept Explained

### The credentials wrapper

The foundational cognitive move of this page is the credentials/behavior distinction, which is structurally parallel to the wrapper/substance distinction used on the [Concern Trolling](manipulation-hub-pattern-concern-trolling.md) and [Weaponized Accommodations](manipulation-hub-pattern-weaponized-accommodations.md) pages. Performative allyship has two layers:

1. **The credentials wrapper.** The surface identity of the speaker as an autism ally. This can be built out of: extensive reading (they have actually read Devon Price, Steve Silberman, Dr. Luke Beardon, autistic bloggers); knowledge of vocabulary (they can say *neurodivergent*, *masking*, *sensory overload*, *double empathy*, *stimming*, *meltdown vs. shutdown*); public statements (social media posts, shared articles, stated positions); professional roles (therapist, educator, researcher, advocate, service provider); family connections to autism (a child, sibling, parent, spouse); community affiliations (organizations they belong to, people they follow, causes they publicly support); or simple repeated assertions (*"I'm autism-affirming," "I really get this stuff," "I'm not like other people"*). The credentials are usually *real* in the thin sense that the person did do the thing they claim to have done.

2. **The substantive behavior.** What the person actually does in relationship to the specific autistic person in front of them. How they respond to feedback. Whether they update when told something landed wrong. Whether they accommodate costs or offload them. Whether they believe the autistic person's account of their own experience. Whether they are willing to be wrong. The substance is what would be visible to a careful observer who ignored the credentials entirely and watched only the behavior.

The move works because **the wrapper is evaluated as a proxy for the substance.** When the autistic person, or a third-party witness, tries to assess whether the relationship is actually safe, the credentials are treated as evidence: *they read the books, they must be okay.* The behavior under the credentials is left unevaluated. The autistic person who tries to raise a concern about the behavior is then positioned as attacking an ally, missing the person's good intentions, or failing to notice how much the person has done to learn about autism.

The foundational cognitive move against the pattern is therefore to read the two layers separately and to evaluate each on its own merits. The credentials can be sincerely earned and the behavior can still be harmful. The person can really have read Devon Price and can still be being ableist in the specific interaction in front of them. Recognizing that the two layers can diverge is the entire scaffolding of the defense. It is also the move that, done without care, produces the false-positive error of reading imperfect allyship as performative. Section 4 is how that error is avoided.

### Evidence quality: be honest about the gap

This page should not overclaim. The direct peer-reviewed research base for performative allyship as a specific manipulation pattern against autistic adults is essentially nonexistent. The term itself comes from broader critical discourse about allyship practices in marginalized-community advocacy — most of that discourse is in race, gender, and queer theory rather than in autism research. Writers like Phillipe Leonard Fradet, the autistic-led community organizations ASAN, AWN, NeuroClastic, and Thinking Person's Guide to Autism, and commentators like Devon Price have named the pattern repeatedly in community contexts, but it has not been operationalized as an empirical construct in the peer-reviewed autism literature.

What the peer-reviewed literature *does* provide is converging structural support:

- **Pearson et al.'s (2023) internalization findings** (*"I felt like I deserved it because I was autistic"*) document that autistic adults who have been harmed by people they initially trusted frequently arrive at the conviction that they are misreading the harm, and one of the factors that makes the misreading stick is the presence of allyship-adjacent features in the harmer's self-presentation.
- **Douglas & Sedgewick's (2024) weaponized features framework** — *"autism used against you"* — applies structurally: the autistic person's willingness to extend trust to someone who has signaled autism-affirmation is itself a feature that can be weaponized. The credentials become a tool in the harmer's kit rather than a reliable indicator of safety.
- **Lim, Young & Brewer's (2022) credibility discount** documents that when an autistic person pushes back against a non-autistic interlocutor, third-party observers default to believing the interlocutor over the autistic person. When the interlocutor also carries publicly visible allyship credentials, the default is compounded: observers now have a second reason — *they're an ally, so surely they wouldn't* — to disbelieve the autistic person.
- **Sweet's (2019) sociology of gaslighting** treats manipulation as largely scaffolded by pre-existing cultural scripts. The cultural script of *allies as the good guys* is one of the available scaffolds. The manipulator does not have to convince anyone that they are trustworthy from scratch; the cultural script of allyship does the load-bearing work for them.
- **Lilienfeld's (2007) work on potentially harmful psychological treatments** is relevant to the autism-professional subtype. Lilienfeld documents that therapists whose self-understanding is *I use approaches that help* have been shown to sometimes use approaches that in fact harm, and that the self-understanding is not a reliable safeguard. The autism-professional subtype is a specific instance of this broader finding.
- **Milton's (2012) double empathy problem** is the theoretical frame that performative allyship most directly denies. Double empathy states that the communication gap between autistic and allistic people is *bidirectional* — both parties are missing something about the other. Performative allyship installs the unidirectional premise that the allistic ally has access to what autistic people are really like, and that the autistic person in front of them is therefore subject to the ally's reading rather than being the authoritative source on their own experience.
- **Crompton et al. (2020) on autistic peer-to-peer communication** establishes the empirical basis for why autistic peer reality-check is particularly useful for this pattern: autistic peers can often recognize the credentials/behavior gap more quickly than allistic friends can, because they have usually lived through it and because they do not process the credentials through the cultural script of *allies as the good guys.*

These sources underwrite the structural analysis in this page. They do not establish a prevalence estimate for performative allyship, a standardized definition, or an empirically validated intervention. **Everything in this page is either [EXTRAPOLATED] from the sources above or drawn from [COMMUNITY KNOWLEDGE] from autistic-led organizations and writers.** Readers should weight the strategies accordingly: they are grounded in recognizable patterns and in structural analogy to better-studied patterns, not in a research literature specific to performative allyship itself.

### The structural function: producing the internal contradiction

What the credentials wrapper does, structurally, is produce an internal contradiction in the autistic person between *I am being harmed* and *but they are supposed to be one of the good ones.* The two propositions are logically compatible — a person who has read the books can still be harmful — but they are socially and emotionally dissonant, and the dissonance is exhausting to sustain.

The path of least resistance, under the exhaustion, is to resolve the contradiction by doubting the first proposition. *Maybe I am misreading this. Maybe my autism is making me see it wrong. Maybe they are just having a bad day. They have read Devon Price; they wouldn't actually be ableist.* The wrapper is structurally designed to produce exactly this resolution, because the cultural script of allyship tells the autistic person that allies are rare and precious and that losing one by misreading their behavior would be a loss.

The structural function is the same as the function of concern trolling: both patterns produce an internal contradiction that is most easily resolved by the target doubting their own perception. Concern trolling does it through the wrapper of *the speaker loves me*; performative allyship does it through the wrapper of *the speaker is an ally*. In both cases, the exit from the contradiction is not internal. The autistic person cannot argue themselves out of it alone, because both propositions feel true. The exit is through external reality-check — someone outside the loop who can hold both *this person has allyship credentials* and *this person is harming you* at once, without collapsing either into the other.

### Stance versus identity: the pragmatic mechanism

The pragmatics category that underwrites this pattern most directly is **Identity and Stance (Category 24)**. The distinction between *identity* (a stable property of a person) and *stance* (a positioning move a person makes within a specific conversation) is load-bearing here.

The credentials wrapper works by letting the speaker claim an *identity* as an ally — a stable, persistent property that applies across situations. Identity claims are hard to refute with single-interaction evidence, because any single interaction can be dismissed as unrepresentative of the identity. *"I had a bad day; that is not who I am."*

The distinguishing tool that section 4 builds is the move from *identity* to *stance*. Allyship, treated accurately, is not an identity that exempts specific moves; it is a *stance that is taken, re-taken, and sometimes not taken within specific interactions.* In interaction A, the person took an allyship stance. In interaction B, with you, the person took a different stance — defensive, dismissive, controlling, disbelieving — and no amount of previous allyship stances in other interactions makes the current stance any less what it is. The pragmatic vocabulary of stance-taking gives the technical machinery for naming this: *in this specific interaction, with this specific autistic person, what stance is being taken?* The identity question is the wrong question; the stance question is the right one.

This is not a clever rhetorical trick. It is the technical structure of how stance is treated in discourse-analytic work on positioning (see the Identity and Stance category page for the underlying theory). Stance is a moment-by-moment move; identity is the aggregation of stances across time and audience. Performative allyship is the move of using aggregated-stance identity to pre-empt the evaluation of a specific current stance. Recognizing it at that level makes the pattern refusable in the way that surface-level identity disputes are not.

### What makes performative allyship *manipulation*, not imperfect allyship

This is the central distinguishing problem and it is the hardest part of the page. Real allies make mistakes. Real allies are sometimes clumsy. Real allies sometimes fall back on cultural scripts they have not fully worked through. Calling every clumsy ally *performative* would be both unfair and counterproductive, and it would also be corrosive to the real allyship that does exist and that the autistic community genuinely needs.

The pattern this page treats is not imperfect allyship. It is the *use of allyship credentials as cover* for behavior that (a) does not change in response to feedback, (b) consistently harms the specific autistic person in front of the ally, and (c) is held against any autistic person who tries to name the harm. The three features cluster. No single one establishes the pattern.

The distinguishing features that shift probability toward manipulation rather than imperfect allyship:

1. **Feedback is not received.** When the autistic person says *this landed wrong*, a real ally sits with it, asks what would have worked better, or updates their behavior going forward. A performative ally redirects to their own pain at being misread, cites their credentials as evidence they would never do what is being alleged, or reframes the feedback itself as the autistic person's autism distorting the interaction.

2. **The focus is on the abstract category, not the specific person.** Real allies care about the specific autistic person they are interacting with. Performative allies champion *autistic people* as a category while harming the specific person in their life. The asymmetry between the public championing and the private relationship is often the cleanest single signal of the pattern.

3. **Allyship costs nothing.** Real allyship costs something — effort, inconvenience, accommodation, sometimes social standing, sometimes professional risk. Performative allyship is structured to cost the speaker nothing while extracting credit for the appearance of cost. When the cost of a specific accommodation arrives, the performative ally discovers a reason it cannot be provided, while the credentials continue to be deployed.

4. **Behavior does not match stated commitments.** Stated commitments to autism-affirming values are visible; behavior is examined less closely. Performative allyship relies on this asymmetry by keeping the statements loud and the behavior invisible. A careful examination of behavior over time, weighted equally against the statements, is what reveals the divergence.

5. **Critique is deflected, not sat with.** When an autistic person says *this hurt me*, a real ally sits with it and does not immediately foreground their own experience of being misunderstood. A performative ally pivots the conversation onto their own pain, their own good intentions, their own allyship history, and their own hurt that the autistic person does not see how hard they have tried.

6. **Credentials are used as armor.** Real allies generally do not need to keep citing their credentials. When allyship credentials are invoked specifically in response to critique — *I literally read Devon Price last month, you cannot tell me I am being ableist* — the credentials are functioning as armor rather than as a good-faith indicator of underlying practice.

7. **The pattern co-occurs with other manipulation patterns.** Performative allyship is rarely the primary pattern; it is more often the wrapper under which other patterns operate. Gaslighting, diagnostic weaponization, tone policing, the compliance trap, concern trolling, and DARVO can all be delivered inside a credentials wrapper, and the co-occurrence is one of the strongest signals that the credentials are doing manipulation work.

When most or all of these features are present, you are looking at the manipulation pattern. When one or two are present, you may be looking at a real ally whose behavior has gone briefly off, or at a person who is doing the work imperfectly and is still worth being in relationship with. The distinguishing tools in section 4 operationalize the difference in more detail.

## 3. How It Looks in Practice

Performative allyship appears in several recognizable subtypes. The list below is not exhaustive, but it covers the forms that autistic community accounts most consistently describe. Each subtype has its own configuration, and the strategies in section 6 apply differently depending on which subtype is present.

### 3.1 The autism professional

A therapist, educator, researcher, advocate, or service provider whose professional identity is built around autism allyship. Their credentials are their job: *I work with autistic clients all day. I have been in this field for fifteen years. I trained under [name]. I have an autism specialty. I run groups for autistic adults.* When the autistic person raises a concern about the professional's actual behavior toward them, the credentials are cited as evidence the concern cannot be right.

- *"I work with autistic clients every day, so I know that what you're describing isn't actually how autism works."*
- *"I have been doing this for fifteen years. I think I know the difference between a meltdown and what you're describing."*
- *"I am trained in [approach]. If what I did had been harmful, I would know."*
- *"I have autistic clients who do not complain about this, so it must be your specific sensitivity."*

This is one of the more material forms because the autism professional usually holds institutional authority — clinical authority, educational authority, gatekeeping authority over services or accommodations — that amplifies the cost of the credentials wrapper. The autistic person who disagrees with the professional is not just pushing back against one person; they are pushing back against a professional identity, an institutional role, and sometimes a funded treatment program. The Lilienfeld (2007) work on potentially harmful psychological treatments is relevant here: the professional's confidence that they are *autism-affirming* is not evidence that the specific approach they are using is in fact affirming or helpful, and the field has documented repeatedly that clinician self-perception is not a reliable safeguard. The autism-professional subtype is where the authority vacuum described in the foundational autism research document (few clinicians are both autism-informed and trauma-specialized) hits hardest.

### 3.2 The parent of an autistic child

A parent who claims to understand autism through their child, and who uses that claim to override the perspectives of autistic adults — sometimes including their own child when the child grows up. The *warrior parent* or *autism mom* identity can function this way when it hardens into a credential that insulates the parent's behavior from critique.

- *"I have raised an autistic kid. I know how autism actually works. What you adult autistics talk about online is not what I see at home."*
- *"I am in the trenches. I have earned the right to have opinions about autism that armchair autistic adults do not get to have."*
- *"I read all the autistic creators, but my kid cannot even speak, so I think my lived experience counts for more than theirs."*
- *"I have spent twenty years advocating for my child. Do not tell me I am being ableist."*

This subtype intersects with generational conflicts in the autism community that are beyond the scope of this page. What this page treats is the specific move of using the parent-of-autistic-child identity as a credential that exempts the parent's actual behavior toward specific autistic people (sometimes their grown child, sometimes autistic adults who interact with them) from critique. The parenting credential is real — raising any child is real work and raising an autistic child often involves substantial work — but it does not confer expertise on the lived experience of the autistic adult in front of the parent, and it does not exempt any specific behavior from evaluation on its own merits.

### 3.3 The reader of autism content

A friend, family member, partner, or coworker who has read books, follows autistic creators on social media, knows the vocabulary, and uses that knowledge as evidence that they are an ally and therefore not subject to critique. This is probably the most common form in everyday life because the barrier to reading a few books is low and the resulting credential feels, to the reader, like sufficient proof of standing.

- *"I literally read Devon Price last month. You cannot tell me I am being ableist."*
- *"I follow [autistic creator] on Instagram. I know about double empathy. That is not what is happening here."*
- *"I know what masking is. I know what stimming is. I know what sensory overload is. So when I say you are overreacting, I am saying it from a place of understanding."*
- *"I have done more reading about autism than most people you know. That is worth something."*

The distinguishing feature of this subtype is that the reading is treated as a terminal achievement rather than as the beginning of ongoing practice. Real allies who read autistic writers tend to use the reading to inform their behavior and to be more responsive to the specific autistic person in front of them; performative allies of this type use the reading as proof of standing and then behave as if that proof is a substitute for the actual responsiveness.

### 3.4 The autism-affirming workplace or institution

An employer, school, clinic, agency, or service provider that publicly markets itself as autism-friendly and uses that public identity as cover for refusing accommodations, dismissing complaints, or weaponizing autism against employees, students, or service recipients. This is one of the more materially consequential subtypes because it can affect employment, education, medical care, housing, and legal standing.

- *"We are a neurodiversity-affirming workplace. Our commitment to inclusion is in the company handbook. So when you say you need [accommodation], we have to think carefully, because our other autistic employees do not need it."*
- *"This is an autism-friendly school. Our staff are all trained in [approach]. If your child is having problems, it cannot be the school — we have the certification."*
- *"This is a neurodiversity-affirming practice. Everything we do is evidence-based and autism-affirming. You must be misreading the session."*

The autistic person trying to disclose that an autism-friendly institution is actually hostile faces an audience pre-loaded with the institution's marketing. Outside observers — HR departments, oversight bodies, prospective employees, other parents — arrive at the situation with the credential already installed, and the autistic person's account is heard against that pre-loading. The Sweet (2019) structural-scaffolding analysis applies directly: the cultural script *this institution is autism-friendly, so any complaint must be the autistic person's misperception* is doing most of the work, and the institution only has to point at its own marketing.

### 3.5 The fellow autistic person positioning as the authority

Sometimes an autistic person uses their own diagnosis as a credential to override other autistic people's experiences. *As an autistic person myself, I can tell you this is not how autism works.* This is rarer than the other subtypes, but it is worth naming because the structural dynamic is similar even when both parties share the diagnosis.

- *"I am autistic and I do not have that problem, so it is not an autism problem."*
- *"As an autistic person, I can tell you that what you are describing is not autism, it is just [character flaw]."*
- *"I am autistic and I managed to do [X]. You should be able to too."*
- *"I am autistic and I say this with love: you are using your diagnosis as an excuse."*

The same diagnosis does not confer the same experience. Autistic people vary enormously in support needs, sensory profile, co-occurring conditions, life circumstances, and what they have had to do to survive. A fellow autistic person's account of their own experience is valuable; their use of their diagnosis as a credential to override another autistic person's account of *theirs* is structurally the same move as the allistic versions above. The *same diagnosis* wrapper is just a different wrapper around the same substance: *my standing overrides your account.* Naming the pattern in this form is difficult in autistic community spaces precisely because of the scarcity-of-allies effect (section 5), but it happens and it causes harm.

### 3.6 The social media ally

A public figure, community member, or content creator who builds a public platform around autism advocacy and uses the platform to deflect critique of their actual behavior toward specific autistic people in their life. The platform is the credential, and the visibility of the platform is treated as proof of good faith.

- *"I have spent years publicly advocating for autistic people. So when you accuse me of [harm], you are attacking an ally who has done more for your community than you realize."*
- *"I have [X] followers who will vouch for me. You are one voice against all of them."*
- *"Do you know how much I have done for this community? And this is how I am treated?"*

The social media ally subtype is distinguished by the asymmetry between the public-facing work and the private relationships. The public work may be genuinely valuable; the private relationships may still be the site of harm; and the public work is deployed, when critique arrives, to make the private critique socially unavailable. The structural parallel to institutional performative allyship is direct: in both cases, a visible record of good work is being used to pre-empt evaluation of specific behavior that is not visible to the same audience.

### 3.7 The "I'm not like the other [neurotypicals]" frame

A variant that can appear within any of the above subtypes. The speaker positions themselves as exceptionally autism-aware, more enlightened than other allistic people, and therefore exempt from the kinds of mistakes other allistic people make. The implicit claim is that their allyship is different in kind from ordinary allyship, which places them in a category that is supposed to be immune to the ordinary critiques of ordinary allies.

- *"I am not like other neurotypicals. I really get this. So you cannot apply the generic critiques of ableism to me."*
- *"Most people do not understand autism, but I am not most people."*
- *"I hate when other allistic people get autism wrong. That is why it is safe to tell me anything — I am different."*

The *not like the others* frame functions by establishing a protected category for the speaker in which critique cannot operate. If critiques of allistic behavior apply to other allistic people but not to the speaker, then any specific critique of the speaker's behavior has to be rebutted by appeal to the general category exemption rather than by examining the specific behavior on its own terms. Recognizing this move is the same as recognizing other category-exemption moves: the speaker has not actually answered the critique; they have just placed themselves outside the domain in which the critique applies.

### 3.8 The reformed-perpetrator frame

*"I used to be ableist about autism, but I learned."* The history of personal growth becomes a credential that prevents present behavior from being critiqued. The speaker's narrative of their own development functions as proof that whatever they are doing now cannot be what they used to do.

- *"I used to think autism was an excuse, but I read [book] and I completely changed. So what you are saying cannot be right, because I am not that person anymore."*
- *"I have done the work. I have examined my own ableism. You cannot accuse me of things I already confessed to in my past."*
- *"I grew up in a household that was very ableist about autism, and I have spent years unlearning that. If I were still like that, I would know."*

The reformed-perpetrator frame is particularly difficult to refute because the growth narrative is socially valued and because pushing back on it appears to deny the possibility of change — which is something many autistic readers do not want to deny, and which a performative ally can exploit by collapsing *we believe growth is possible* into *we must believe your growth is complete.* The two propositions are not the same. Growth is possible; a specific person's claim to have completed growth on a specific topic is an empirical question about their current behavior, not a credential.

## 4. How to Distinguish Performative Allyship from Imperfect Allyship

This is the hardest section of this page, and it is the one the page most needs to get right. The goal is to give tools that identify the manipulation pattern without weaponizing *performative allyship* against real allies who are doing the work imperfectly. The false-positive error — calling a clumsy-but-genuine ally *performative* — is not a harmless error. It costs the autistic person a real relationship and it corrodes the social conditions under which allyship can exist at all.

The tools below are designed to protect against the false positive as carefully as they are designed to identify the manipulation pattern. They accumulate evidence across interactions; no single feature establishes the pattern.

### Signals that shift the probability *toward* performative allyship

- **Credentials are cited specifically in response to critique.** Real allies do not usually need to keep citing their credentials in ordinary interaction. When the allyship history is deployed in response to feedback, the deployment is armor rather than context.
- **Feedback does not change behavior over time.** Real allies, told that something landed wrong, usually update their behavior going forward. Performative allies absorb the feedback into the allyship narrative — *I already know about that, I am already working on that, I am one of the good ones about that* — without the underlying behavior changing. Pattern memory across interactions is the reliable test.
- **The public record and the private record diverge.** The speaker is visibly autism-affirming in public contexts (social media, professional settings, group conversations) and less so in private interaction with the specific autistic person. The asymmetry between the public and private records is one of the cleanest single signals.
- **The autistic person's specific account is overridden by the speaker's general expertise.** *I know autism; you do not really know what is happening with you* is the underlying structure. Real allies treat the autistic person in front of them as the authoritative source on their own experience. Performative allies treat themselves as the authoritative source.
- **Critique is redirected to the speaker's pain at being misread.** When the autistic person raises a concern, the conversation pivots onto how much it hurts the speaker that their allyship is not being recognized, rather than onto the substance of the concern. The pivot itself is diagnostic.
- **The speaker's allyship comes at no cost.** When a real accommodation is needed and it would cost the speaker something — time, inconvenience, money, social standing, professional risk — the speaker discovers reasons it cannot be provided, while the credentials continue to be deployed. The cost-free pattern across accommodation opportunities is visible.
- **Other manipulation patterns are present.** Performative allyship is usually a wrapper, not a primary pattern. Diagnostic weaponization, gaslighting, tone policing, the compliance trap, concern trolling, or DARVO are present underneath the allyship wrapper, and the wrapper is what makes them harder to name.
- **The speaker claims exemption from ordinary critiques of allistic behavior.** *I am not like other neurotypicals; the standard critiques of ableism do not apply to me* is a structural exemption move. Real allies generally know that the critiques of allistic behavior apply to them too and do not claim exemption.
- **Other autistic people who have been around the speaker report similar experiences.** When a second and third autistic person who has interacted with the same speaker independently describe similar patterns, the pattern is real and the odds it is specific to you are vanishing. Autistic peer reality-check is particularly useful here (Crompton et al., 2020).
- **Credentials are held against the autistic person who tries to name the harm.** *How dare you accuse an ally like me* is a specific move. It recasts the autistic person's attempt to name the harm as an attack on the abstract category of allyship rather than as a description of specific behavior. Real allies, even when stung, do not usually frame the critique this way.

### Signals that shift the probability *away* from performative allyship

- **Feedback visibly changes behavior.** The single most reliable signal of real allyship is the update loop. The person is told something landed wrong; they sit with it; the next interaction is different. The update may be imperfect; the point is that the update happens.
- **Credentials are not deployed in response to critique.** The person may have read the books, worked in the field, raised an autistic child — but they do not bring up the fact in response to feedback. The credentials are background context for why they are trying, not armor against evaluation.
- **The speaker centers the specific autistic person in front of them.** They ask what works for you, specifically. They do not override your account of your own experience with their general knowledge. They treat the general knowledge as a starting point that needs to be calibrated to you.
- **The speaker accepts that they will make mistakes.** Real allies expect to mess up and are not devastated when told they have. They do not treat feedback as an attack on their identity; they treat it as information.
- **The speaker can be wrong about autism.** A person who can accept correction about a specific claim they made about autism — without retreating into their credentials or reframing the correction as the autistic person's autism — is probably not operating in the performative pattern.
- **Allyship costs them something.** They actually accommodate. They actually adjust. They actually advocate when it is inconvenient. They experience the cost and continue anyway. Real allyship is visible in the costs that are paid.
- **The speaker is curious about the double empathy problem.** A person who genuinely engages with Milton's (2012) double empathy idea — who accepts that they might be missing something that the autistic person is not missing — is usually not operating in the performative pattern, because the performative pattern depends on the speaker's unidirectional authority about autism.
- **Other autistic people who have been around the speaker report positive experiences.** The inverse of the bad-signal version. When autistic peers who have interacted with the speaker describe them as responsive, updateable, and trustworthy, that is evidence worth weighting.
- **The speaker does not need to be centered for their allyship to continue.** Real allies do not need to be thanked, praised, or reassured of their good standing in order to keep showing up. When the allyship is structured to extract reassurance from the autistic people the ally is supposedly allied with, the extraction is the diagnostic move.
- **Imperfect allyship that gets better.** The single best signal of real allyship is probably the trajectory: the person is clumsy, they are told, they are less clumsy next time, they are told, they are less clumsy still. Over weeks and months, the relationship is visibly working toward better calibration. The trajectory matters more than any single interaction.

### When you are uncertain

The hardest cases are the ones where some signals point each way. A structural diagnostic test for performative allyship specifically:

1. **Check the behavior-under-cost test.** Has the speaker ever done something that was costly to them, in accommodation or advocacy, specifically for you? If yes, that is evidence of substance. If no, the allyship may be real but it has not yet had the chance to be tested, or it may be structured to avoid testing.
2. **Check the feedback-update test.** In the last month, has any piece of feedback you gave the speaker visibly changed their behavior? If yes, you are probably not in the manipulation pattern, even if other signals are ambiguous. If no, and the feedback has been absorbed into the allyship narrative without changing anything, the wrapper is doing work the substance is not.
3. **Check the public-private divergence test.** Does the speaker's behavior with you, when no one else is watching, match their public commitments about autistic people? If yes, the allyship is probably real even if imperfect. If the divergence is large, the credentials are probably doing wrapper work.
4. **Check the credentials-as-armor test.** When you have raised concerns, how often have the credentials been cited back at you? Once or twice is probably fine; a pattern of credentials being deployed every time critique approaches is the armor configuration.
5. **Get an autistic peer reading.** This is the single most useful test. An autistic peer who knows you and who can hear the situation from outside the relationship is in the best position to identify the credentials-versus-behavior divergence, because they do not have the relationship's emotional weight and they do not process the credentials through the scarcity-of-allies frame.
6. **Look at the aggregate over months.** A single interaction is rarely enough. The question is what the relationship looks like over months: is it getting better, worse, or more ambiguous? The trajectory is the most reliable diagnostic, and the trajectory question is the question to take to a reality-check network.
7. **Resist the urge to label prematurely.** If the signals are genuinely mixed and you cannot tell, the appropriate frame is *I do not know yet,* not *they are performative.* Premature labeling creates its own costs. Documentation and continued observation are the protective moves under uncertainty.

The protective principle behind all of these tests: **the point of the distinguishing tools is to keep real allies and exit false ones, not to reach a verdict that fits either tool.** Where the signals point both ways, the appropriate move is usually to continue the relationship with increased documentation and reality-check, not to break it off on the basis of incomplete evidence.

## 5. Autism-Specific Considerations

### The scarcity-of-allies effect

The autism community is small enough that *people who claim to be allies* feels like a precious resource. Many autistic adults have spent years in environments where allyship was not just rare but actively absent — families that did not understand, schools that punished autistic traits, workplaces that required masking as a condition of continued employment, medical systems that pathologized every feature of autistic neurology. The appearance of an ally, after that background, can feel too valuable to lose to a critique. The calculus becomes: *if I am wrong about this person being performative, I lose one of the few people who has even claimed to be on my side.*

The scarcity-of-allies effect is not irrational. It is a reasonable response to real conditions. But it is also exactly the structural condition that makes performative allyship effective. The performative ally relies on the target's awareness of how few alternatives there are, and the credentials wrapper does some of its work by activating the autistic person's *I cannot afford to lose this.* Recognizing the scarcity effect as a factor does not require the autistic person to pretend the scarcity is not real. It only requires recognizing that the scarcity is being leveraged and that the protective move is to evaluate the specific relationship on its own merits rather than on its place in a scarcity calculus.

A secondary observation: when the scarcity is driving the assessment, the actual number of available real allies tends to be higher than it feels. The felt scarcity is real in local experience but less true at larger scales — autistic community organizations, online autistic communities, some autism-affirming clinicians, some autism-affirming employers and workplaces, autistic peers who have become reality-check partners. The protective move is to keep building the broader network so that no single claimed ally is structurally irreplaceable.

### The credibility discount, compounded

Lim, Young & Brewer (2022) documents that autistic adults are perceived as less credible and more deceptive by non-autistic observers, even when telling the truth. The effect is below conscious awareness and reflects how observers process affect, prosody, gaze, and fluency. For performative allyship specifically, the credibility discount has a compounded effect: when the autistic person tries to push back against a self-identified ally, third-party observers default to believing the ally over the autistic person, and the *they are an ally* frame gives them a second independent reason on top of the default credibility discount.

The compound effect is particularly damaging in institutional contexts. When an autistic employee reports that an autism-friendly workplace is actually hostile, the institutional audience processes the report through two layers of skepticism: the baseline credibility discount on the autistic person, and the pre-loaded cultural script that the institution is autism-friendly. The employee's account has to overcome both, and the institution's account has to overcome neither. The asymmetry is structural and it is one of the reasons performative allyship at institutional scale is so difficult to disclose through normal channels.

The implication for strategy is that naming performative allyship within the system where the credentials were established is generally the least productive move. The credentials are the system's currency; challenging them directly invites the full weight of the structural scaffolding. Reality-check networks and documentation that travels outside the system are generally more useful than attempts to persuade the system against its own marketing.

### Trained deference to authority

Many autistic adults were trained from childhood to defer to the authority of teachers, therapists, doctors, and behavioral interventionists — the very professional categories that produce the autism-professional subtype of performative allyship. The deference is not just a habit; it is often the thing that enabled survival in childhood environments where resistance to authority was punished. By adulthood, the default response to an autism professional who claims expertise over the autistic person's experience is already pre-installed: *they probably know better than I do.*

The compliance-training pipeline described in the Diagnostic Weaponization page applies directly here. When a performative-ally professional says *as a therapist who specializes in autism, I can tell you that what you are describing is not really autism*, the trained response is not the autonomous adult's *I am the authority on my own experience* but the child's *they probably know better*. The resulting compliance is not evidence that the professional is right; it is evidence that the pipeline worked on the autistic person decades earlier and is still operating.

Recognizing this is not the same as exiting it. Recovery from the compliance pipeline is slow work (see [Recovery and Post-Traumatic Growth](manipulation-hub-strategy-recovery.md) and the [Compliance Trap](manipulation-hub-pattern-compliance-trap.md) page). What the recognition does is give the autistic person a second interpretive frame — *maybe my default deference here is not because they are right; maybe it is because I was trained to defer to people in this role* — that can be held alongside the first frame long enough for a reality-check network to help resolve which one fits the specific situation.

### Justice sensitivity and the allyship contradiction

Justice sensitivity — the strong aversion to unfairness and deception documented repeatedly in autistic adult qualitative research — collides with performative allyship in a specific and exhausting way. The pattern's core is a contradiction between stated allyship and actual behavior, and the justice-sensitive instinct is to engage the contradiction directly: *but you said you were autism-aware; how can you also be doing this?* The instinct is correct; the contradiction is real; and naming it feels, to the justice-sensitive autistic adult, like the obvious and necessary move.

The performative ally, however, can deflect the contradiction indefinitely. Any attempt to name it is absorbed into the allyship narrative: *I know what you mean, I am working on that, I am not perfect but I am learning, you have to understand how hard this is for me too.* The contradiction does not resolve; it just produces more loops, and each loop consumes the autistic person's energy without changing anything. The justice-sensitive instinct to keep engaging becomes a vulnerability, because the engagement is what keeps the pattern live.

The protective move is the same as for Diagnostic Weaponization and DARVO: maintain the internal assertion silently, or in writing, or with a reality-check network, and stop trying to make the performative ally agree. The justice-sensitive impulse stays intact; the channel changes. You are not giving up on the recognition that the contradiction is real. You are giving up on the expectation that pointing it out to the person enacting it will make them stop. (See [Strategic Disengagement](manipulation-hub-strategy-strategic-disengagement.md).)

### The good-faith assumption

Autistic adults tend, on average, to take statements at face value. When someone says *I am an ally*, the autistic person tends to register the statement as information rather than as a claim to be independently verified. The good-faith assumption is not a deficit; it is one of the communication defaults documented in the foundational autism research document's discussion of autistic pragmatics. In many contexts — conversations with other autistic people, interactions with people who mean what they say — the default works. In interactions with performative allies, who rely on the default to shortcut the evaluation of their actual behavior, the default is exactly the thing they are leveraging.

The protective move is not to become a person who takes no statements at face value. That would be exhausting, corrosive to trust, and incompatible with functioning in ordinary autistic-to-autistic contexts where the good-faith default is both accurate and appropriate. The protective move is narrower: **for statements of ally identity specifically, hold the statement and the behavior in separate evaluation buckets, and weight the behavior more heavily over time.** The statement goes in the "what the person said about themselves" bucket. The behavior goes in the "what the person actually did" bucket. The second bucket is where the assessment gets made. This is not a loss of good faith; it is a refinement of which signals get evaluated against which.

### Autistic peer reality-check is particularly valuable here

Crompton et al. (2020) established that autistic peer-to-peer communication is highly effective, and the finding has a specific application here: autistic peers can recognize the credentials/behavior gap more quickly than allistic friends can. The reasons are structural. Autistic peers have usually lived through versions of the pattern themselves. They process the credentials without the cultural script of *allies as the good guys* pre-loading their assessment. They are not subject to the same good-faith assumption about allyship statements because they have usually been burned by the assumption before. And they are not processing the specific relationship through the scarcity-of-allies frame in the same way, because their own network of autistic peers usually provides the support that an allistic friend's claimed allyship would have to provide from scratch.

The practical implication is that autistic peer reality-check should be weighted particularly heavily for this pattern. An allistic friend who hears your account of a suspected performative ally may, in good faith, partially echo the performative ally's framing — not because they are being manipulative but because the cultural script about allyship is the easiest interpretive frame they have available. An autistic peer is much less likely to do this, because the script is not in their defaults. When the two readings differ, the autistic peer's reading is generally the better guide for this pattern.

## 6. Strategies (with Conditionals)

### 6.1 Read the two layers separately

The foundational cognitive strategy, parallel to the wrapper/substance distinction on the Concern Trolling and Weaponized Accommodations pages. Build the habit of evaluating the credentials and the behavior in separate buckets, and weighting the behavior more heavily. Statements of ally identity go in one bucket; concrete behavior toward you specifically goes in another. The assessment is made on the second bucket. The first bucket is context for why the person is trying, not evidence that the trying has succeeded in your specific case.

This is not a strategy for producing a verdict in a single conversation. It is a strategy for accumulating evidence over time, so that when the verdict eventually becomes clear, the two layers have been kept separate long enough to read the verdict accurately.

**Apply when:** any relationship where ally credentials are being established and you are trying to figure out whether the allyship is real.

**Do not apply when:** the two layers are converging in a good direction. If the behavior matches the stated commitments, and both are improving in response to feedback, the two-layer read has done its job and you can stop running it actively. Healthy relationships do not need perpetual wrapper/substance monitoring, and continuing to run it when the relationship is actually working is corrosive to trust.

### 6.2 Run the feedback-update test

Over the next several weeks, pay attention to whether feedback you give the speaker visibly changes their behavior. Pick a small, concrete item — something specific enough that you would notice an update. Raise it in whatever way is most natural for you. Then watch. Does the next relevant interaction show any change? Does the update reach the specific area you raised, or does it get absorbed into the general allyship narrative without changing anything concrete?

The feedback-update test is the single most reliable signal because real allies, almost by definition, update. A person who cannot take specific feedback without citing their credentials, redirecting to their own pain, or reframing the feedback as your autism is giving you the diagnostic information you need. A person who does update, even imperfectly, is probably not in the performative pattern.

**Apply when:** you have any stake in figuring out whether a claimed ally relationship is real.

**Do not apply when:** raising the feedback is unsafe — where there is a real risk of retaliation, escalation, or material loss if the speaker receives the feedback poorly. In those situations, documentation and reality-check networks come first, and the feedback test gets used only when safety allows.

### 6.3 Document the credentials-behavior divergence

[Documentation](manipulation-hub-strategy-documentation.md) is the primary written strategy for this pattern, specifically focused on the divergence layer. For each interaction that raises your concern, write down:

- The credentials the speaker cited (what they said about themselves, what autism content they referenced, what professional or personal history they invoked).
- The behavior that followed (what they actually did in the specific interaction).
- The gap between the two.
- Any feedback you gave and whether it produced a visible change in subsequent interactions.

Over weeks or months, the records show whether the credentials and the behavior are converging or diverging. A closing gap is evidence of real allyship doing the work; a persistent or widening gap is evidence of the credentials-as-wrapper pattern.

The written record is also what you take to a reality-check network. An outside reader who has not been inside the relationship cannot evaluate the pattern from your summary alone, because the credentials are the thing the relationship has been selling; the reader needs the concrete divergence in writing to see what you are seeing.

**Apply when:** you suspect the pattern and you have safe storage (the person being documented cannot access the records).

**Do not apply when:** the documentation itself would produce risk — in a coercive control context where discovery of the records would trigger escalation, or in an institutional context where the act of documenting could be characterized as paranoid or hostile. Safety planning and an advocate come first; documentation can begin once a safe channel exists.

### 6.4 Prioritize autistic peer reality-check

[Reality-Check Networks](manipulation-hub-strategy-reality-check-networks.md) with explicit priority on autistic peer relationships. Autistic peer reality-check is particularly useful for this pattern because autistic peers are not processing the credentials through the cultural script of *allies as the good guys* and because they have usually lived through versions of the pattern themselves.

Practical guidance:

- Bring the documentation (not just the summary) to an autistic peer who knows you and who is in a stable enough place to help.
- Ask the peer specifically about the credentials-behavior divergence, not whether the person is a "good person" in some general sense. The evaluation question is narrower than the character question.
- If the peer's reading differs from your own, weight theirs substantially. The whole reason to do reality-check is that your own read is subject to the scarcity-of-allies effect and the exhaustion of sustaining the internal contradiction; the peer is not.
- If you do not have access to autistic peer relationships, building them is itself a protective move. Online autistic communities (with the usual cautions about privacy and vetting), local autistic adult groups, autism-affirming organizations, and autistic-led advocacy organizations are starting points.

**Apply when:** you have any access to autistic peer relationships, and whenever credentials are being used to override your account of your own experience.

**Do not apply when:** the specific peer is themselves in crisis, in active manipulation, or otherwise not in a position to give the kind of reality-check you need. Peer support is not unconditional; it depends on the peer being in a position to give it.

### 6.5 Refuse the identity-exemption move internally

When credentials are cited as exemption from critique — *I am an ally, I read the books, I work with autistic clients, I am not like other allistic people* — the internal refusal is something like: *allyship is a practice visible in behavior, not an identity that exempts behavior. What you are doing in this interaction is what I am evaluating, not what you are claiming about who you are.*

This is the stance-versus-identity move from section 2, applied as an internal protective reframe. You are not trying to convince the speaker to accept the reframe; you are protecting your own ability to evaluate the current interaction on its own terms. The internal refusal is much harder to maintain alone, so pair it with reality-check support.

**Apply when:** credentials are being deployed as exemption and you find yourself being pulled into evaluating the credentials rather than the behavior.

**Do not apply when:** the credentials are being shared as context, not as armor. Real allies sometimes share relevant background about why they are engaged with autism, and sharing context is not the same as deploying armor. The distinguishing signal is usually whether the sharing happens in response to critique or happens in ordinary conversation.

### 6.6 Do not expect naming the pattern to the performative ally to work

Naming *performative allyship* to a person actively engaged in it almost always produces another iteration of the pattern. The speaker will either cite more credentials, absorb the naming into the allyship narrative (*I appreciate you bringing this up, I will work on it, this is exactly why I am committed to doing better*), or reframe the naming itself as a feature of your autism. The loop does not break from inside.

Save the framework for your own use and for your reality-check network. The framework is a tool for you, not a tool for persuading the speaker. Trying to get the speaker to agree that they are performative is a version of the same mistake the Diagnostic Weaponization page and the DARVO page both warn about: you are trying to change the manipulator's framing when the protective move is to protect your own knowledge of what is happening.

**Apply always** as a restraint on the instinct to engage.

**Do not apply when:** you actually want to exit the relationship and the naming is being used as part of the exit, not as an attempt to change the relationship from inside. In exit contexts, stating your read clearly may be part of the exit speech-act, not an attempt at persuasion.

### 6.7 Plan for institutional performative allyship separately

The institutional subtype (section 3.4) has a different strategy profile than the interpersonal subtypes. Individual behavior change is not generally achievable; the strategy is evidence-gathering that travels outside the institution.

Practical guidance:

- Assume the institution's marketing is what HR, oversight bodies, and new observers will hear first. Your account has to survive contact with the pre-loaded marketing.
- Gather documentation in forms the outside world can evaluate — written communications, timestamps, witnesses, specific incidents with specific dates.
- Find autism-specific disability-rights organizations where they exist. Disability rights legal organizations, ASAN, AWN, and in employment contexts the EEOC and the Job Accommodation Network are starting points. (See [Crisis Resources Directory](manipulation-hub-crisis-resources.md).)
- Consider the exit move seriously. In institutional contexts, the cost of trying to reform a performative-ally institution from within is often higher than the cost of exiting and documenting the exit. The institution's marketing will not change in response to one employee's complaint; your employment or educational outcome might.
- When exiting is not immediately possible, protect the inside period with documentation and external reality-check, so that when exit becomes possible the record is already in place.

**Apply when:** the performative ally is an employer, school, clinic, agency, or service provider whose autism-friendly marketing is not matching their behavior.

**Do not apply when:** you are in an institution that is doing the work imperfectly but really trying, where the evidence points to convergence rather than divergence. The strategies above are for the diagnosed pattern; they are corrosive if deployed against a real institutional ally.

### 6.8 Protect the autism-professional relationship specifically

The autism-professional subtype (section 3.1) deserves separate strategic attention because the cost of being performative-ally-trapped by a clinician is high and the authority vacuum makes alternatives scarce. [Finding Professional Support](manipulation-hub-strategy-professional-support.md) addresses the broader question of clinician selection; for performative allyship specifically, the red flags include:

- The clinician cites their autism specialty in response to your feedback about the session.
- The clinician treats your pushback about their approach as a feature of your autism rather than as substantive feedback about the approach.
- The clinician's actual practice does not match their stated autism-affirming orientation (e.g., they claim to be anti-compliance but are in practice demanding compliance).
- The clinician recommends couples counseling or family therapy while a manipulation pattern is active (see [Bograd & Mederos, 1999](manipulation-hub-strategy-professional-support.md) on couples therapy contraindication).
- The clinician treats their autism-affirming credentials as evidence they cannot be harming you, rather than as one input into an ongoing evaluation of whether the work is actually helping.
- Lilienfeld's (2007) criteria for potentially harmful psychological treatments are being met — the clinician's self-perception as autism-affirming is not preventing the specific approach from being harmful to you.

The strategic response is the same as for other professional-support questions: continue documentation, seek reality-check, and be willing to change clinicians even when the clinician presents as rare or specialized. The authority vacuum is real, but being stuck with a performative-ally clinician is not a solution to the vacuum; it is a particularly damaging form of the vacuum.

**Apply when:** you are in a clinical relationship with someone whose autism specialty is being deployed in ways that override your account of your own experience.

**Do not apply when:** you are in a clinical relationship that is doing the work imperfectly but is updating in response to feedback. Imperfect clinicians who update are valuable; the shortage is real, and not every clinician needs to be exited when the signals are mixed and the trajectory is improving.

### 6.9 Keep building real allyship, carefully

The recovery frame for this page is specific: **the goal is not to become a person who refuses all allyship. The goal is to recognize the pattern of credential-as-cover so that real allyship can be received and false allyship can be exited.** Performative allyship recovery is not *becoming more suspicious of allies*; it is *becoming more accurate about which allies are real.*

Real allies exist. They are sometimes hard to find. They make mistakes. They update. They do not keep citing their credentials. They pay costs. They center the specific autistic person in front of them. They accept that the autistic person is the authoritative source on their own experience. They engage the double empathy problem as a real bidirectional gap rather than as a gap the ally has already solved. When you find them, keep them. The protective move is to evaluate the relationships on their own terms, not to treat allyship itself as inherently suspect.

This distinction matters because the cost of refusing all allyship is high. Isolation is a significant risk factor for autistic adult mental health (see the Vulnerability Experiences Quotient work by Griffiths et al., 2019) and the scarcity-of-allies effect is not eliminated by refusing the category; it is made worse. Recovery is about quality, not quantity. A smaller number of real ally relationships, evaluated accurately, is more protective than a larger number of ambiguous ones, and far more protective than the refusal of the category entirely.

## 7. What This Page Cannot Do

- **It cannot tell you, from a single conversation, whether a specific person is performative or imperfect.** The pattern is identified across multiple interactions, with reality-check input, often over weeks or months. Single incidents are almost never enough.
- **It cannot give you a decisive test that will not produce false positives.** Every tool in section 4 is probabilistic. The tools are designed to accumulate evidence carefully, not to produce verdicts on thin data.
- **It cannot replace autistic peer reality-check.** Of all the patterns on this hub, this one is probably the one where an autistic peer's reading is most important, because the autistic peer is not running the credentials through the cultural script that is doing most of the manipulation work. The page is a starting point; the peer reading is where the actual assessment happens.
- **It cannot substitute for the broader research that has not been done.** The peer-reviewed literature specific to performative allyship is thin. This page has been honest about that. The strategies here are [EXTRAPOLATED] from better-studied patterns and from community knowledge; they are not empirically validated interventions.
- **It cannot tell you how to repair an institution.** Institutional performative allyship is structurally different from the interpersonal forms, and the strategy profile is exit-and-document rather than repair. The page points toward advocacy resources; the actual repair of institutions is not something this page can offer.
- **It cannot keep you from missing real allies if you misapply it.** The distinguishing tools in section 4 are designed to protect against false positives, but any diagnostic tool can be misapplied. The page's strongest warning is that the goal is not *refuse all allyship* but *evaluate allyship accurately,* and readers who use the page to justify refusing real relationships have used it against its own intent.
- **It cannot solve the scarcity of real allies.** The scarcity is real, and this page cannot conjure more allies into existence. It can only help you recognize which of the existing potential allies are real, and keep those relationships when you find them.

## 8. Key Takeaways

1. **Performative allyship has two layers — credentials and behavior — and the credentials are used as wrapper for behavior that does not match.** The foundational cognitive move is to read the two layers separately and weight the behavior more heavily.

2. **This is the page in the hub with the thinnest empirical base.** Performative allyship is largely [COMMUNITY KNOWLEDGE] from autistic survivor accounts. The pattern is real and recognizable, but the peer-reviewed literature specific to it is thin, and readers should weight the strategies accordingly.

3. **Real allies make real mistakes.** The pattern this page treats is not imperfect allyship; it is the *use of credentials as cover* for behavior that does not change in response to feedback, consistently harms the specific autistic person, and is held against any autistic person who tries to name the harm. Section 4 is designed to protect against the false-positive error of calling imperfect allyship performative.

4. **The feedback-update test is the most reliable diagnostic.** Real allies update in response to specific feedback. Performative allies absorb feedback into the allyship narrative without the underlying behavior changing. Pattern memory across multiple feedback loops is the clearest signal.

5. **The public-private divergence is one of the cleanest single signals.** When a person is visibly autism-affirming in public and less so in private interaction with the specific autistic person, the asymmetry is diagnostic. Real allies' public and private behavior converge; performative allies' diverge.

6. **The autism-professional subtype is particularly damaging.** The authority vacuum (few clinicians are both autism-informed and trauma-specialized) and the Lilienfeld (2007) findings on potentially harmful treatments combine to make performative-ally clinicians a concentrated risk. Red flags should be taken seriously even when alternatives are scarce.

7. **Autistic peer reality-check is the single most valuable strategy for this pattern.** Autistic peers are not processing the credentials through the *allies as the good guys* cultural script and they have usually lived through versions of the pattern themselves. Their reading deserves substantial weight.

8. **Naming the pattern to the performative ally almost never works.** The naming is absorbed into the allyship narrative. Save the framework for your own use and for your reality-check network; do not expect it to function as persuasion of the person it describes.

9. **The scarcity-of-allies effect is real, but it is being leveraged.** The performative ally relies on the target's awareness of how few alternatives there are. Recognizing the leverage does not require pretending the scarcity is not real; it requires continuing to evaluate the specific relationship on its own merits rather than on its place in a scarcity calculus.

10. **Recovery is not refusing all allyship. Recovery is becoming accurate about which allyship is real.** The goal is to be in relationships with people whose credentials and behavior align, not to treat allyship itself as inherently suspect. Real allies exist, they are worth keeping, and the distinguishing tools are for identifying them accurately — not for breaking the relationships off on thin evidence.

**Evidence quality statement for this page:**

The peer-reviewed research base specific to performative allyship against autistic adults is essentially nonexistent. The structural analogues — the Pearson research program, Douglas & Sedgewick's weaponized features framework, Lim, Young & Brewer's credibility discount, Sweet's sociology of gaslighting, Stark's coercive control, Milton's double empathy problem, and Lilienfeld's work on potentially harmful treatments — all support the structural analysis in this page. The specific pattern has not been isolated as a research target.

- [VALIDATED] for Lim, Young & Brewer's credibility discount, Sweet's sociology of gaslighting, Stark's coercive control framework, Milton's double empathy problem, Crompton et al.'s autistic peer-to-peer communication work, and Lilienfeld's potentially harmful treatments work.
- [EXTRAPOLATED] for the synthesis into a specific performative allyship profile.
- [COMMUNITY KNOWLEDGE] for the six or seven subtypes in section 3 and for most of the distinguishing tools in section 4, drawn from autistic-led community organizations and from the writing of autistic commentators cited above. ## 10. Cross-Links

### Pragmatics categories referenced on this page

- **Identity and Stance (Category 24)** — the most important pragmatics cross-link on this page. Performative allyship is fundamentally a stance move (claiming the position of *ally*), and the distinction between stance-as-identity-claim and stance-as-in-interaction-positioning is the underlying mechanism the page depends on. [category-24-identity-stance.md](category-24-identity-stance.md)
- **Framing and Frame Analysis (Category 23)** — performative allyship is a frame attack. It replaces the frame *we are evaluating specific behavior* with the frame *we are evaluating an ally's identity*. Naming the frame swap is part of the cognitive defense. [category-23-framing-frame-analysis.md](category-23-framing-frame-analysis.md)
- **Implicature (Category 2)** — much of the pattern operates through what is *implied* by citing credentials rather than through what is said directly. *I literally read Devon Price last month* implicates *so I cannot be doing what you are saying I am doing.* The implicature is doing the work. [category-02-implicature.md](category-02-implicature.md)
- **Speech Acts (Category 1)** — distinguishing the speech act of claiming allyship identity from the speech act of actually doing allyship work. The first is visible in statements; the second is visible only in behavior over time. [category-01-speech-acts.md](category-01-speech-acts.md)
- **Implicature / Cooperative Principle (Category 2)** — performative allyship exploits the cooperative principle by relying on the hearer to extend good-faith interpretation to the ally's statements about themselves. Grice's framework gives the technical vocabulary for naming when the cooperative principle is being leveraged asymmetrically. [category-02-implicature.md](category-02-implicature.md)
- **Presupposition (Category 9)** — citing credentials smuggles in the presupposition that allyship is an identity that exempts specific behavior from evaluation. The presupposition is doing load-bearing work; naming it is part of the defense. [category-09-presupposition.md](category-09-presupposition.md)
- **Common Ground (Category 16)** — performative allyship unilaterally rewrites the common ground of the relationship to install the ally's allyship identity as a shared given that does not have to be reestablished each interaction. [category-16-common-ground.md](category-16-common-ground.md)
- **Politeness (Category 3)** — performative allyship is often wrapped in politeness scaffolding (*I am telling you this because I care, I am one of the good ones*). Brown and Levinson's face theory gives the underlying mechanism. [category-03-politeness.md](category-03-politeness.md)
- **Register (Category 7)** — the clinical or community-advocacy register of autism vocabulary gives performative allyship an authority effect it would not have in plain language. Recognizing the register shift is part of the cognitive defense. [category-07-register.md](category-07-register.md)
