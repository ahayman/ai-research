# Category 16: Common Ground and Grounding in Conversation -- Research & Theory

## Overview

Common ground is one of the most fundamental concepts in pragmatics. It refers to the body of mutual knowledge, mutual beliefs, and mutual assumptions shared by participants in a conversation. Every act of communication depends on it: speakers design their utterances based on what they believe their listeners already know, and listeners interpret those utterances against the backdrop of what they believe is shared. The process of *grounding*---the collaborative, turn-by-turn work through which participants establish and maintain mutual understanding---is the mechanism by which common ground is both used and expanded during conversation.

This document provides a comprehensive theoretical account of common ground and grounding, drawing on the foundational work of Herbert H. Clark, Susan Brennan, Robert Stalnaker, David Lewis, and others. It is designed to support a knowledge base teaching English pragmatics to autistic individuals, for whom the implicit processes of tracking and building common ground may benefit from being made explicit and systematic.

**Relationship to other categories:** Category 9 (Presupposition) covers how speakers assume certain information is already shared. Category 10 (Relevance Theory) covers Sperber & Wilson's alternative concept of "mutual manifestness." Category 8 (Deixis) touches on shared physical/temporal context. This document treats common ground as its own system---the underlying infrastructure on which presupposition, implicature, audience design, and much of pragmatic competence depend.

## 1. WHAT COMMON GROUND IS

### 1.1 Foundational Work: Herbert H. Clark

The most influential account of common ground in language use comes from Herbert H. Clark, particularly his 1996 book *Using Language* (Cambridge University Press). Clark's central thesis is that language use is not merely a matter of speakers encoding messages and listeners decoding them. It is a *joint activity*---a collaborative endeavor in which participants coordinate their actions to achieve mutual understanding. Common ground is the foundation on which this coordination rests.

Clark defines common ground as **the sum of the mutual knowledge, mutual beliefs, and mutual assumptions that participants in a conversation share at any given moment**. It is not merely the overlap of what two people happen to know independently; it is what they *mutually recognize* as shared. This mutuality is crucial: common ground is not just "I know X and you know X" but "I know X, you know X, *and we both know that we both know X*."

Clark's work builds on earlier proposals by David Lewis (*Convention*, 1969), Stephen Schiffer (*Meaning*, 1972), and Paul Grice (William James Lectures, 1967). Grice is credited with introducing the term "common ground" in pragmatics, and Lewis developed the first rigorous analysis of "common knowledge" in the context of conventions and coordination games. Clark synthesized and extended these ideas into a comprehensive psycholinguistic framework.

**Key works:**
- Clark, H. H. (1996). *Using Language*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press.
- Clark, H. H., & Marshall, C. R. (1981). "Definite reference and mutual knowledge." In Joshi, Webber, & Sag (Eds.), *Elements of Discourse Understanding*.
- Clark, H. H., & Brennan, S. E. (1991). "Grounding in communication." In Resnick, Levine, & Teasley (Eds.), *Perspectives on Socially Shared Cognition*.

### 1.2 The Three Sources of Common Ground

Clark and Marshall (1981) identified three primary sources from which common ground is built. These correspond to three types of "copresence heuristics"---ways that participants can reasonably infer that something is mutually known:

#### 1.2.1 Community Membership (Cultural/Communal Co-presence)

This is the common ground that comes from belonging to the same cultural, linguistic, professional, or social communities. Members of a community can assume that other members share certain knowledge, norms, values, and conventions simply by virtue of their shared membership.

**Examples:**
- Two Americans can assume mutual knowledge that Thanksgiving involves turkey, that the president lives in the White House, that "911" is the emergency number.
- Two software engineers can assume shared knowledge of what a "pull request" is.
- Two speakers of English can assume shared knowledge of English vocabulary, grammar, and conversational conventions.
- Two Catholics can assume shared knowledge of sacraments and liturgical practices.

Community-based common ground is often called **communal common ground**. It is the broadest and most stable form. People belong to multiple overlapping communities simultaneously (national, linguistic, professional, generational, subcultural), and each membership contributes a layer of assumed shared knowledge.

**Important:** Community-based common ground is probabilistic, not absolute. Not every American knows who won the last Super Bowl; not every software engineer knows Rust. Speakers make educated guesses about what community knowledge their listeners possess, and these guesses can be wrong.

#### 1.2.2 Physical Co-presence (Perceptual Co-presence)

This is the common ground that arises from what both participants can currently perceive---what they can both see, hear, feel, smell, or otherwise experience in the immediate environment. If both participants can see a dog running across a park, the presence of that dog is part of their common ground without anyone needing to mention it.

**Examples:**
- In face-to-face conversation, pointing and saying "that one" works because both parties can see what is being pointed at.
- A waiter approaching a table where a glass has been knocked over can say "Let me clean that up" without specifying what "that" refers to.
- Two people standing in the rain can reference "this weather" without establishing what the weather is.

Physical co-presence is the most immediate and reliable source of common ground, but it is also the most fragile---it exists only as long as both participants are in the same perceptual environment. This is why telephone and text-based communication require more explicit verbal information: the physical co-presence channel is reduced or eliminated.

**Clark and Marshall's insight:** Deixis (Category 8) maps naturally onto physical co-presence. Deictic expressions like "this," "that," "here," "there," "now" rely on physically co-present context to be interpretable.

#### 1.2.3 Linguistic Co-presence (Discourse Co-presence)

This is the common ground that accumulates from what has already been said in the current conversation. Every utterance that is successfully communicated and accepted becomes part of the participants' shared conversational record. This is the most dynamic source of common ground---it grows with every turn.

**Examples:**
- If Speaker A says "I went to Paris last summer" and Speaker B acknowledges this, then "Speaker A went to Paris last summer" is now part of their common ground. Future references to "the trip" or "when you were in Paris" can draw on this.
- Pronouns often rely on linguistic co-presence: "She was amazing" makes sense only if "she" was previously introduced in the discourse.
- Anaphora (using a pronoun or reduced expression to refer back to something already mentioned) maps naturally onto linguistic co-presence.

**Clark and Marshall's mapping:** The three types of definite reference map onto the three sources of common ground:
- **Deixis** relies on *physical co-presence*
- **Anaphora** relies on *linguistic co-presence*
- **Proper names and general knowledge references** rely on *community membership*

### 1.3 Communal vs. Personal Common Ground

Clark further distinguished between two broad categories of common ground:

**Communal common ground** is based on community co-membership. It includes all the knowledge, beliefs, and norms that members of a community can reasonably assume other members share. It is relatively stable and does not require prior personal interaction. Examples: cultural knowledge, language knowledge, professional domain knowledge.

**Personal common ground** is based on personal acquaintance and joint experience. It includes everything two specific individuals have learned through their direct interactions---past conversations, shared experiences, jointly witnessed events, and the current discourse record. It is highly partner-specific.

**Example:** When going for a bike ride with a friend, you ride on the right side of the street because of communal common ground (a culturally shared traffic norm). Your friend takes the lead because your personal common ground includes the knowledge that she knows her way around the city better than you do.

Research has shown that speakers track personal common ground with remarkable specificity. Brennan and Clark (1996) demonstrated that when speakers establish a way of referring to something with one partner, they maintain that partner-specific "conceptual pact" even when talking to a different partner about the same thing. Studies have also shown that when a speaker has previously discussed the same referent with two different partners in two different modalities (e.g., seeing an image with one partner, hearing a description with another), subsequent references are tailored to the specific modality of experience shared with each partner.

### 1.4 How Common Ground Grows During Conversation

A critical property of common ground is that it is **dynamic**---it grows with every successfully communicated utterance. Each time a speaker says something and the listener provides evidence of understanding, the content of that utterance is added to the participants' common ground.

This is why conversations become progressively easier as they continue: each new contribution can build on an expanding base of shared information. Referring expressions become shorter (because more can be assumed as shared), explanations require less detail, and participants can use shorthand that would be opaque to someone who had not been part of the conversation.

**The tangram experiments (Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs, 1986):** In a landmark study, pairs of participants described abstract tangram shapes to each other over multiple rounds. On the first round, descriptions were long and elaborate: "the person who looks like they're ice skating, leaning forward with one leg behind them." By the sixth round, the same shape was referred to with a single word: "the skater." The descriptions shortened dramatically over time as each successful reference added to the pair's common ground, allowing progressively more abbreviated expressions. Crucially, this shortening was dramatically reduced when partners were not allowed to interact (i.e., when the grounding process was blocked), demonstrating that the shortening depends on collaborative confirmation, not merely on repetition.

### 1.5 The Difference Between Individual Knowledge and Mutual Knowledge

A persistent and important theoretical issue is the distinction between:
- **Shared knowledge**: I know X, and you know X (but neither of us necessarily knows that the other knows)
- **Mutual knowledge**: I know X, you know X, I know that you know X, you know that I know X, I know that you know that I know X... and so on, infinitely

The "infinite regress problem" was raised by Schiffer (1972): true mutual knowledge seems to require an infinite chain of meta-knowledge ("I know that you know that I know that..."), which appears psychologically impossible to compute in finite time.

Several solutions have been proposed:

1. **Schiffer's implicit knowledge solution**: Most mutual knowledge is implicit---we possess it without having explicitly considered each level. Just as you implicitly know that 37 + 45 = 82 without having previously computed it, you implicitly know that your conversation partner knows what you both can see, without needing to reason through each level.

2. **Lewis's common knowledge via shared basis**: Lewis (1969) argued that common knowledge arises from a shared public event or basis (like both seeing the same thing), combined with shared reasoning abilities. If we both see X, and we both know we both see X (because we can see each other seeing it), the entire infinite chain follows automatically from the shared basis.

3. **Clark's copresence heuristics**: Clark proposed that people do not actually compute infinite recursion. Instead, they use heuristics based on the three types of copresence. If something is physically co-present, participants assume it is mutually known without needing to reason through each level explicitly. Clark called this the **principle of justification**: "People take a proposition to be common ground in a community only when they believe they have a proper shared basis for the proposition in that community."

4. **Reflexive definition**: Common ground can be defined reflexively (self-referentially) rather than as an infinite list. If A takes a proposition as common ground with B, then A takes the following to be true: "A and B have information that the proposition is true *and that this entire statement is true.*" This single self-referential statement captures the infinite chain without requiring infinite computation.

In practice, for everyday communication, what matters is not theoretical mutual knowledge to infinite depth but **practical mutual belief sufficient for current purposes**. People operate on reasonable assumptions about what is shared, and they repair when those assumptions turn out to be wrong.

## 2. GROUNDING: The Process of Establishing Common Ground

### 2.1 Clark & Brennan's Model of Grounding

If common ground is the *product*, grounding is the *process*. Clark and Brennan (1991), in their seminal chapter "Grounding in Communication," defined grounding as **the collaborative process by which speakers and listeners work together to ensure that what the speaker meant is what the listener understood, to a degree sufficient for current purposes**.

This definition contains several crucial elements:
- **Collaborative**: Grounding is not something the speaker does alone or the listener does alone. It is a joint activity requiring coordinated effort from both parties.
- **Process**: It is ongoing throughout the conversation, not a one-time event.
- **What the speaker meant / what the listener understood**: The goal is alignment between speaker intention and listener interpretation.
- **Sufficient for current purposes**: The standard of understanding is not perfection---it is adequacy relative to the goals of the interaction.

### 2.2 The Grounding Criterion

The "grounding criterion" is the mutual belief that partners have understood what the contributor meant, to a criterion sufficient for current purposes. Technically, grounding is the collective process by which participants try to reach this mutual belief.

The "sufficient for current purposes" qualifier is essential. The level of understanding required varies enormously depending on context:
- Casual chat: A vague acknowledgment ("yeah, totally") may suffice.
- Giving directions: The listener may need to repeat the directions back.
- Relaying a secret code to deactivate a bomb: A simple "uh huh" from the listener would be wildly insufficient; reiteration of the code is needed.
- Medical instructions: The doctor may need the patient to demonstrate understanding of the medication regimen.

The grounding criterion flexibly adjusts to the stakes and goals of the interaction.

### 2.3 The Contribution Model: Presentation and Acceptance

Clark and Schaefer (1989), in "Contributing to Discourse," modeled conversation as a series of **contributions**---units that add to common ground. Each contribution has two phases:

**Phase 1: Presentation**
One participant presents an utterance (or other signal) for the other participants to consider. The speaker designs the utterance based on their current model of common ground, assuming that if evidence of understanding is given, they can believe the other understands what they meant.

**Phase 2: Acceptance**
The other participant(s) provide evidence that they have understood the presentation well enough for current purposes. Once both phases are completed, the content of the contribution becomes part of the participants' common ground.

In the simplest case, this creates an alternating pattern:
- A's Turn 1: Presentation of Contribution 1
- B's Turn 1: Acceptance of Contribution 1 + Presentation of Contribution 2
- A's Turn 2: Acceptance of Contribution 2 + Presentation of Contribution 3
- ...and so on

But contributions can also be more complex, involving **installments** (presenting information in pieces, with acceptance at each step), **embedded repairs** (pausing the main contribution to fix a misunderstanding), and **side sequences** (temporary departures to establish needed common ground before returning to the main thread).

### 2.4 Evidence of Understanding

Clark identified several types of evidence that listeners can provide to demonstrate understanding. These range from weakest to strongest:

#### 2.4.1 Continued Attention
The listener continues to pay attention without interrupting or signaling confusion. This is the *weakest* form of evidence---essentially, the absence of a signal of non-understanding. In face-to-face conversation, this includes maintaining eye contact, body orientation, and appropriate posture. The logic: by passing up the opportunity to request clarification, the listener implicitly signals adequate understanding.

#### 2.4.2 Acknowledgments (Back-Channel Responses)
The listener produces verbal or non-verbal signals that indicate continued understanding without taking over the conversational floor. These include:
- Verbal: "mm-hmm," "uh-huh," "yeah," "right," "OK," "I see"
- Non-verbal: head nods, eyebrow raises, brief smiles
- Paraverbal: intake of breath, "hm"

These are often called "back-channel responses" or "continuers" because they signal to the speaker: "I'm following; continue." They are stronger evidence than mere continued attention because they are active signals.

#### 2.4.3 Relevant Next Contribution
The listener initiates a response that is relevant to and builds on what the speaker said. This is stronger evidence of understanding than an acknowledgment because it demonstrates that the listener has processed the content well enough to respond to it meaningfully.

**Example:**
- A: "I'm thinking of going to that new Thai place on Main Street."
- B: "Oh, I heard their pad thai is amazing."

B's relevant response demonstrates that B understood A's statement---what the place is, where it is, and the general topic---without explicitly saying "I understand."

**Clark's insight:** In conversation analysis, this is related to the "next-turn proof procedure"---the idea that each turn in conversation provides evidence of how the *previous* turn was understood.

#### 2.4.4 Demonstration (Paraphrase or Action)
The listener demonstrates understanding by paraphrasing, summarizing, or acting on what was said. This is stronger still because it provides *specific* evidence about what the listener understood.

**Examples:**
- A: "Could you pick up some milk on your way home?"
- B: "Sure, I'll stop at the grocery store on Third Street."
  (B demonstrates understanding by specifying how they will fulfill the request.)

- A: "The meeting is at 3 PM in Conference Room B."
- B: "Three o'clock, Room B---got it."
  (B demonstrates understanding by repeating back the key details.)

#### 2.4.5 Display
The listener displays understanding through their subsequent behavior in a way that goes beyond paraphrase. For example, if A explains how to complete a task and B then performs the task correctly, B has displayed understanding more convincingly than any verbal acknowledgment could provide.

Clark notes that **behavioral demonstrations are more convincing evidence than verbal assertions**: someone who says "I understand" may or may not actually understand, but someone who acts correctly based on what they were told demonstrates understanding through action.

### 2.5 Evidence of Non-Understanding

Just as listeners can signal understanding, they can signal problems:

#### 2.5.1 Requests for Repair
Direct requests for clarification or repetition:
- "What?" / "Huh?" / "Sorry?" (signal of non-hearing)
- "What do you mean?" (signal of non-understanding)
- "The one on the left or the right?" (signal of ambiguity)
- "Wait, I thought you said Tuesday" (signal of inconsistency)

#### 2.5.2 Non-Response / Silence
Failing to respond when a response is expected can signal non-understanding. However, silence is ambiguous---it could mean the listener didn't hear, didn't understand, is thinking, or is distracted. This ambiguity makes silence a weak signal in face-to-face conversation but a particularly problematic one in mediated communication (see Section 5.4).

#### 2.5.3 Inappropriate Next Turns
If the listener's response reveals a misunderstanding, this triggers repair:
- A: "Can you pass me the salt?"
- B: *reaches for the pepper*
- A: "No, the salt---the white one."

The inappropriate action reveals a misunderstanding, which is then repaired.

### 2.6 The Principle of Least Collaborative Effort

Clark and Brennan proposed that participants in a conversation follow the **principle of least collaborative effort**: they try to minimize the total effort spent by all participants in the contribution---across both the presentation and acceptance phases.

This principle explains several observable features of conversation:

1. **Referring expressions shorten over time**: As demonstrated in the tangram experiments, initial descriptions are long and detailed, but with repeated reference they shrink to short labels. This is efficient: once an expression has been grounded, it can be reused with minimal effort.

2. **Speakers avoid unnecessary detail**: If a listener already knows something (it is in common ground), restating it wastes joint effort.

3. **Speakers use the simplest expression that will be understood**: Rather than saying "the person I was telling you about earlier who works at the coffee shop on the corner of Fifth and Main," a speaker might say "the coffee shop guy"---if they believe this is sufficient given the listener's common ground.

4. **Repair is triggered only when understanding falls below the threshold**: Participants tolerate minor ambiguities or imprecisions if they are unlikely to matter for current purposes.

However, the principle also predicts that participants will invest *more* effort when the stakes are high: a pharmacist explaining dosage instructions will speak more carefully and solicit more explicit evidence of understanding than someone making casual small talk.

### 2.7 Grounding Constraints: The Role of Communication Medium

Clark and Brennan identified eight properties of communication media that affect the grounding process. Different media provide different combinations of these properties, and the absence of any one increases the effort required to establish common ground:

| Constraint | Definition | Face-to-face | Phone | Video call | Email/Text | Letter |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Copresence** | Participants share the same physical environment | Yes | No | No | No | No |
| **Visibility** | Participants can see each other | Yes | No | Yes | No | No |
| **Audibility** | Participants can hear each other | Yes | Yes | Yes | No | No |
| **Cotemporality** | Messages are received approximately when sent | Yes | Yes | Yes | Partial | No |
| **Simultaneity** | Both can send and receive at the same time | Yes | Yes | Yes | No | No |
| **Sequentiality** | Contributions are received in order | Yes | Yes | Yes | Varies | Yes |
| **Reviewability** | Messages can be re-examined later | No | No | No | Yes | Yes |
| **Revisability** | Messages can be edited before sending | No | No | No | Yes | Yes |

**Key insight:** Face-to-face communication is the richest medium for grounding because it provides copresence, visibility, audibility, cotemporality, and simultaneity. Every channel removed increases the effort required to ground utterances. This is why misunderstandings are more common in text messages than in face-to-face conversation---the grounding signals (facial expressions, tone of voice, immediate back-channel responses) are absent, and participants must compensate with explicit verbal strategies.

## 3. AUDIENCE DESIGN AND COMMON GROUND

### 3.1 Designing Utterances for Specific Listeners

Audience design is the process by which speakers tailor their utterances to specific listeners based on their model of what those listeners know---their common ground. Clark and Murphy (1982), in "Audience Design in Meaning and Reference," argued that speakers design *each utterance* for specific listeners, and listeners make essential use of this fact in understanding.

Audience design pervades every level of language production:
- **Lexical choice**: Using "myocardial infarction" with a colleague vs. "heart attack" with a patient
- **Referring expressions**: "Sarah's place" to a mutual friend vs. "my friend Sarah's apartment on Fifth Street" to a stranger
- **Level of detail**: Giving a tourist detailed directions vs. saying "it's near the post office" to a local
- **Language variety**: Code-switching between formal and informal registers based on the listener
- **Topic selection**: Choosing topics relevant to the listener's interests and knowledge

### 3.2 The Refashioning of Referring Expressions

One of the most well-documented phenomena in common ground research is how referring expressions are "refashioned" (shortened and simplified) over the course of a conversation as common ground accumulates.

The classic demonstration comes from Clark and Wilkes-Gibbs (1986). In their tangram study:
- Round 1: "The one that looks like a person who's kind of leaning to the left, ice skating, with their arms out"
- Round 3: "The ice skater"
- Round 6: "Skater"

This shortening reflects the progressive addition of shared references to common ground. Each successful use of a referring expression creates a **referential precedent**---a mutually recognized way of referring to that entity.

Brennan and Clark (1996) extended this with the concept of **conceptual pacts**: when interlocutors agree on a name for an object, they are forming a temporary, partner-specific agreement about how to conceptualize that object. These pacts are:
- **Partner-specific**: The expression "the skater" is associated with the specific partner with whom it was established, and is part of their personal common ground
- **Flexible**: Pacts can be renegotiated if circumstances change
- **Persistent**: They tend to carry over within and sometimes across conversations with the same partner

### 3.3 Overspecification and Underspecification

A speaker's estimate of common ground determines how much information to include in an utterance. Two types of calibration errors are possible:

**Underspecification** (assuming too much common ground):
- The speaker leaves out information the listener actually needs
- Result: Confusion, requests for repair, or silent misunderstanding
- Example: Saying "Just do the thing we talked about" to someone who has forgotten the earlier conversation

**Overspecification** (assuming too little common ground):
- The speaker includes information the listener already has
- Result: The listener feels talked down to, patronized, or bored
- Example: Explaining to an experienced cook how to boil water

Finding the right balance requires:
1. **Accurate modeling of the listener's knowledge state**: What do they already know?
2. **Monitoring feedback**: Are they showing signs of confusion or impatience?
3. **Willingness to repair**: Being ready to add more information or cut back based on signals

### 3.4 The Curse of Knowledge

A major obstacle to accurate audience design is the **curse of knowledge** (Camerer, Loewenstein, & Weber, 1989)---the cognitive bias whereby once you know something, it is extremely difficult to imagine not knowing it. Your own knowledge "bleeds" into your estimate of others' knowledge.

**In communication, the curse of knowledge manifests as:**
- Experts who cannot simplify their explanations because they have forgotten what it is like not to understand the jargon
- Speakers who make references assuming the listener shares knowledge that they do not
- Writers who omit crucial context because it seems "obvious" from their perspective
- Teachers who move too quickly because the material seems self-evident to them

**Keysar and colleagues' research on egocentric processing:**
Boaz Keysar and colleagues (Keysar, Barr, Balin, & Brauner, 2000) demonstrated that listeners initially process referential expressions *egocentrically*---their first interpretation is based on their own knowledge, including information they know the speaker does *not* share. Common ground functions as a later monitoring and correction process, not as the initial filter.

This "egocentric anchoring and adjustment" model suggests that:
1. People start from their own perspective (anchoring)
2. They adjust toward the partner's perspective (adjustment)
3. The adjustment is often incomplete---people do not fully correct for the asymmetry

This has important implications: even neurotypical adults routinely make errors in common ground tracking. The process is effortful and imperfect for everyone.

## 4. THE COMMON GROUND PROBLEM IN COMMUNICATION

### 4.1 When Common Ground Assumptions Go Wrong

Communication failures related to common ground fall into several categories:

**4.1.1 False Assumption of Shared Knowledge**
The speaker assumes the listener knows something that the listener does not:
- "Did you hear what happened at the meeting?" (listener was not at the meeting and has not heard about it from anyone)
- "Just use the Henderson protocol" (listener does not know what the Henderson protocol is)
- Using an acronym the listener does not recognize

**4.1.2 False Assumption of Unshared Knowledge**
The speaker assumes the listener does *not* know something that they actually do:
- Over-explaining a concept to an expert
- Providing context that was already established earlier in the conversation
- Treating a colleague as a novice in their own area of expertise

**4.1.3 Ambiguous or Misaligned Common Ground**
Both parties believe they have common ground, but their understanding of the shared content differs:
- Both think they are talking about "the project," but each has a different project in mind
- Both understand the word "soon," but one means "within an hour" and the other means "within a week"
- Both assume a plan is in place, but they have different understandings of the details

### 4.2 Strategies for Calibrating Common Ground

Effective communicators employ several strategies:

1. **Checking understanding explicitly**: "Does that make sense?" "Do you know what I mean by X?"
2. **Reading feedback continuously**: Monitoring facial expressions, body language, back-channel responses, and silence for signs of confusion or understanding
3. **Adapting in real time**: Adding more detail when confusion is detected, streamlining when understanding is confirmed
4. **Using grounding probes**: Introducing a term or reference tentatively to see if the listener recognizes it: "You know the Henderson protocol---the one from last quarter?"
5. **Offering escape hatches**: "Stop me if I'm telling you things you already know"
6. **Soliciting demonstrations**: "Can you walk me through what you understood?"

## 5. COMMON GROUND IN DIFFERENT CONTEXTS

### 5.1 Between Strangers

When strangers communicate, their common ground is minimal and consists almost entirely of communal common ground---what they can infer from visible cues (age, dress, accent, setting) and assumed community memberships. They have no personal common ground (no shared history, no previous conversations).

**Consequences:**
- Referring expressions must be fully specified ("a friend of mine" rather than "Sarah")
- Assumptions about shared knowledge must be conservative
- More grounding effort is required for each contribution
- Cultural assumptions may be mismatched

Research shows that conversations between strangers involve more neural and linguistic convergence over time---strangers' patterns become more similar as they build common ground, while friends' patterns actually diverge as they explore more diverse topics from their shared base.

### 5.2 Between Friends and Intimates

Friends and long-term partners have extensive personal common ground: shared history, shared experiences, in-jokes, established ways of referring to people and places, and accumulated knowledge of each other's preferences, beliefs, and communication styles.

**Consequences:**
- Communication can be extremely efficient: "the usual place" can replace a full address
- In-jokes and shorthand function because they are backed by rich shared history
- Friends generate more topics in conversation and change topics more rapidly
- Less explicit grounding is needed because the base of common ground is large
- But friends may also *overestimate* their common ground, assuming shared knowledge or agreement that does not actually exist

### 5.3 In Professional and Expert-Novice Settings

Professional settings create an interesting common ground asymmetry:
- **Within-domain colleagues** may share extensive communal common ground about their profession (jargon, methods, norms) but little personal common ground
- **Expert-novice pairs** have deeply asymmetric knowledge: the expert knows the domain; the novice does not

The "curse of knowledge" is particularly dangerous in expert-novice communication. A physician immersed in medical jargon may fail to realize that the patient cannot follow explanations laden with technical terms. A programmer explaining code to a non-technical manager may skip over fundamental concepts that seem obvious from the programmer's perspective.

**Clark's framework predicts:** Speakers should adjust their language to the listener's community memberships. Within the same professional group, technical jargon is appropriate and efficient. Addressing someone outside the group requires translation into layperson terms. The speaker's challenge is accurately estimating which community memberships the listener holds.

### 5.4 In Digital and Asynchronous Communication

Digital communication media reduce or eliminate several of the grounding constraints that face-to-face communication provides:

**Text messaging and email:**
- No visibility (no facial expressions, gestures, or gaze)
- No audibility (no tone of voice, prosody, or paralinguistic cues)
- Limited or no cotemporality (messages may be read hours after sending)
- No simultaneity (no real-time back-channel feedback)
- BUT: reviewability (messages can be re-read) and revisability (messages can be edited before sending)

**Consequences for grounding:**
- Back-channel responses must be explicit ("Got it," "Makes sense," thumbs-up emoji)
- Silence is deeply ambiguous (the person may be busy, thinking, asleep, or confused)
- Emojis function as paralinguistic proxies for nonverbal signals
- Misunderstandings are more common because repair cycles are slower
- Humor and sarcasm are riskier because tone cannot be heard
- Participants must work harder to establish and verify common ground

**Compensatory strategies in digital communication:**
- Explicit confirmation messages ("Understood," "Will do")
- Emojis and reactions as grounding signals
- Screenshots and shared documents to create visual co-presence
- Read receipts and typing indicators as attention signals
- More careful message composition (leveraging revisability)

### 5.5 In Cross-Cultural Communication

Cross-cultural communication creates common ground challenges because the communal common ground that participants assume may differ dramatically. What is "obvious" or "everyone knows" in one culture may be unknown or carry different meaning in another.

**Examples of cross-cultural common ground failures:**
- **Directness norms**: In German culture, directness is valued and expected; in Japanese culture, indirectness preserves social harmony. Each party may interpret the other's communication style as rude or evasive based on their own cultural assumptions.
- **Politeness rituals**: Offering food to a guest may be a genuine offer in one culture and a ritualized exchange requiring refusal in another.
- **Conversational structure**: Turn-taking norms, acceptable pause lengths, and overlap conventions vary across cultures.
- **Shared references**: A cultural reference that is universal in one community ("like something from a Seinfeld episode") may be completely opaque in another.

The fundamental problem: each participant *assumes* their communal common ground is shared, but their communities overlap imperfectly. Effective cross-cultural communication requires recognizing this asymmetry and investing more grounding effort.

## 6. COMMON GROUND AND OTHER PRAGMATIC CATEGORIES

### 6.1 Presupposition (Category 9)

Presupposition is the pragmatic mechanism most directly dependent on common ground. When a speaker presupposes something, they treat it as already part of the common ground---as something that does not need to be asserted because it is already mutually accepted.

**Example:**
- "Have you stopped exercising?" presupposes that the listener was exercising previously. The speaker treats this as common ground.
- If the listener was *not* previously exercising, the presupposition fails---the speaker has incorrectly assumed something to be in common ground that is not there.

**Stalnaker's connection:** In Stalnaker's framework, presuppositions are propositions that the speaker takes to be part of the common ground. An assertion is appropriate only if its content is *not* already in the common ground (otherwise it would be redundant), but its presuppositions *are* (otherwise the assertion would be contextually defective).

**Accommodation (Lewis, 1979):** When a speaker presupposes something that is *not* in the common ground, listeners often **accommodate** the presupposition---they silently add it to the common ground to make the utterance interpretable. For example, if a stranger says "My sister lives in Boston," the listener accommodates the presupposition that the speaker has a sister, adding it to common ground without objection. Accommodation is a cooperative repair mechanism that allows conversation to proceed smoothly even when common ground assumptions are slightly off.

### 6.2 Deixis (Category 8)

Deictic expressions ("this," "that," "here," "there," "now," "then," "I," "you") rely fundamentally on common ground---specifically, on physical and temporal co-presence. The speaker's use of "this" assumes that the listener can perceive what is being indicated; the use of "now" assumes shared temporal reference.

When physical co-presence is reduced (e.g., in phone calls or text messages), deictic expressions become more ambiguous and require more explicit grounding: "this" must be replaced with specific descriptions, and "here" must be supplemented with location information.

### 6.3 Implicature (Category 2)

Gricean implicatures rely on common ground in at least two ways:

1. **The cooperative principle is assumed common ground**: Both speaker and listener must mutually know that they are following (or appear to be following) the cooperative principle and its maxims. This is itself a piece of communal common ground for speakers of a language.

2. **The content needed to derive implicatures must be in common ground**: If a speaker says "It's getting cold in here" as an indirect request to close the window, the listener must know (a) that the window is open, (b) that closing it would make the room warmer, and (c) that the speaker can feel the cold. All of this must be common ground for the implicature to be derivable.

### 6.4 Politeness (Category 3)

Face-work (Brown & Levinson, 1987) requires estimating the listener's values, sensitivities, and social expectations---all of which are matters of common ground. Choosing an appropriate politeness strategy requires the speaker to model:
- The listener's face wants (common ground about the person)
- The social distance between speaker and listener (personal common ground)
- The cultural norms governing the interaction (communal common ground)
- The relative power dynamics (communal and personal common ground)

### 6.5 Register (Category 7)

Register choice---the level of formality, the vocabulary, the syntactic complexity of one's speech---is fundamentally an audience design decision calibrated to estimated common ground. Speakers choose register based on:
- Who the listener is (community memberships, social role)
- What the relationship is (personal common ground about intimacy and hierarchy)
- What the setting demands (communal common ground about norms for the context)

Using the wrong register signals a common ground miscalculation: too formal suggests you do not recognize the intimacy of the relationship; too informal suggests you do not recognize the gravity of the situation or the listener's status.

### 6.6 Relevance Theory (Category 10)

Sperber and Wilson's Relevance Theory (1986, 1995) offers an alternative to the traditional common ground framework. They replace "common ground" with the concept of **mutual manifestness** and replace "mutual knowledge" with the notion of a **shared cognitive environment**.

**Key differences:**
- **Common ground** (Clark, Stalnaker) consists of propositions that participants mutually *know* or *accept* as shared.
- **Mutual manifestness** (Sperber & Wilson) consists of facts and assumptions that are *manifest* (capable of being inferred or perceived) in both participants' cognitive environments, and mutually manifest (both participants recognize that these facts are manifest to both of them).

**Why the distinction matters:** Sperber and Wilson argue that manifestness is weaker (and more psychologically realistic) than knowledge or belief. Something can be manifest to you without you actually having thought about it---it just needs to be perceivable or inferable given your cognitive resources and environment. Mutual manifestness avoids the infinite regress problem because it does not require explicit meta-knowledge at every level.

**Criticism:** Several scholars (Mey, Talbot) have argued that mutual manifestness is not really different from common ground---that Sperber and Wilson "send mutual knowledge out at the front door and let it back in at the back door, disguised as 'mutually manifest assumptions.'" The debate continues about whether mutual manifestness genuinely resolves the theoretical problems of common ground or merely relabels them.

**For practical purposes in pragmatic education:** Both frameworks point to the same underlying reality---that communication depends on participants' ability to model what their interlocutors know, believe, and can perceive. The difference is primarily theoretical.

## 7. STALNAKER'S COMMON GROUND: THE PHILOSOPHICAL FRAMEWORK

### 7.1 Overview

Robert Stalnaker's account of common ground, developed across several influential papers (1974, 1978, 1998, 2002), provides the most widely used *formal philosophical* framework for common ground, complementing Clark's psycholinguistic approach.

Where Clark focuses on the cognitive and interactive processes by which people build and use common ground in real conversations, Stalnaker focuses on the formal structure of common ground and its role in the semantics and pragmatics of assertion and presupposition.

### 7.2 The Context Set

Stalnaker's central construct is the **context set**: the set of possible worlds compatible with everything that the participants in a conversation currently take for granted.

**How it works:**
- Before a conversation begins, the context set is relatively large---many possible worlds are compatible with what the participants assume.
- Each assertion, if accepted, **reduces** the context set by eliminating possible worlds in which the asserted proposition is false.
- The common ground is the set of propositions that are true in *all* worlds in the context set---what all participants take for granted.

**Example:**
- Before the conversation: The context set includes worlds where it is raining and worlds where it is not.
- Speaker A asserts: "It's raining."
- If B accepts this assertion, the context set is reduced: worlds where it is not raining are eliminated.
- "It is raining" is now part of the common ground.

### 7.3 Assertion as Context Update

In Stalnaker's framework, the illocutionary point of assertion is **to reduce the context set**---to narrow the set of possible worlds compatible with what participants take for granted. A successful assertion eliminates some possibilities and thereby increases common ground.

This explains why it is pragmatically inappropriate to assert what is already presupposed (it would not change the context set) and why it is inappropriate to assert contradictions (it would try to eliminate all possible worlds, collapsing the context set to nothing).

### 7.4 Presupposition in Stalnaker's Framework

Presuppositions are propositions that the speaker takes to be already part of the common ground---already true in all worlds in the context set. They are the background against which assertions are evaluated, not new information being contributed.

A presupposition is appropriate when its content is indeed in the common ground. When it is not---when the speaker presupposes something the listener has not accepted---the context is **defective**. The listener can respond by:
1. **Accommodating**: Silently adding the presupposed content to the common ground
2. **Challenging**: "Wait, I didn't know you had a sister"
3. **Ignoring**: Proceeding without addressing the mismatch (which may cause later problems)

### 7.5 Acceptance vs. Belief

Stalnaker is careful to distinguish the attitude underlying common ground. He proposes that common ground is built on **acceptance** rather than belief. Acceptance is broader than belief: participants may accept things for the purposes of conversation that they do not genuinely believe (hypotheticals, fictional premises, provisional assumptions). This allows the framework to handle cases like:
- "Let's assume for the sake of argument that the earth is flat..."
- "In the world of Harry Potter, magic is real..."
- "Suppose we go with Option B..."

In each case, the participants add something to the common ground (and thus update the context set) without necessarily believing it to be true.

### 7.6 Clark vs. Stalnaker: Complementary Perspectives

Clark and Stalnaker approach common ground from different directions, but their accounts are largely complementary:

| Dimension | Clark | Stalnaker |
|---|---|---|
| **Discipline** | Psychology / Psycholinguistics | Philosophy / Formal Semantics |
| **Focus** | The *process* of building common ground in real-time interaction | The *structure* of common ground as a formal object |
| **Central question** | How do people collaboratively establish mutual understanding? | What is the logical structure of the shared information that constrains assertion and presupposition? |
| **Key construct** | Grounding (the collaborative process) | Context set (the set of possible worlds) |
| **Underlying attitude** | Mutual belief / mutual knowledge (via copresence heuristics) | Mutual acceptance (broader than belief) |
| **Granularity** | Fine-grained: tracks moment-to-moment negotiation, repair, acknowledgment | Abstract: tracks propositional content of common ground without modeling the interactive process |
| **Medium sensitivity** | Highly sensitive to communication medium (face-to-face vs. text, etc.) | Medium-neutral (the formal framework applies regardless of medium) |

Both frameworks agree on the fundamental insight: communication depends on a shared informational base that is mutually recognized, and this base is updated through the process of conversation.

## 8. COMMON GROUND AND AUTISM: RESEARCH CONSIDERATIONS

### 8.1 The Common Ground Challenge

Research has identified common ground tracking as an area where autistic individuals may face particular challenges. The process of building and maintaining common ground involves several skills that can be difficult:

1. **Theory of mind / Perspective taking**: Accurately modeling what the listener knows, believes, and can perceive requires perspective-taking abilities that may develop differently in autistic individuals.

2. **Implicit vs. explicit processing**: Much common ground tracking is implicit---speakers automatically adjust their language based on unconscious models of listener knowledge. Research on adolescents with ASD suggests that while they may be *aware* of common ground in explicit tasks, they may show delays in *implicitly* applying this knowledge in real-time interaction. One study found that adolescents with ASD did not show implicit reductions in narrative length when retelling to someone who had heard the story before (a typical common ground adjustment), even though they demonstrated awareness of the shared knowledge when asked explicitly.

3. **Real-time monitoring of feedback signals**: Tracking acknowledgments, facial expressions, and other grounding signals requires rapid processing of social cues.

4. **Working memory demands**: In addition to ToM knowledge, working memory capacity constrains common ground representation and impacts pragmatic skills.

### 8.2 Reframing: Difference, Not Deficit

Recent work (including contributions from autistic scholars) emphasizes that communication difficulties around common ground are bidirectional---they occur *between* neurotypes, not solely *within* one neurotype. This "double empathy problem" suggests that neurotypical individuals may be equally poor at modeling autistic people's common ground as autistic individuals are at modeling neurotypical common ground.

### 8.3 Why Explicit Teaching Helps

The fact that common ground processes are often implicit makes them particularly good candidates for explicit instruction. If the underlying system is made visible---if you can name the three sources of common ground, identify the types of grounding evidence, and recognize the signals of understanding and non-understanding---then you have a framework for performing consciously what others perform automatically. This is analogous to learning the rules of a game explicitly rather than absorbing them through observation.

## 9. KEY TERMINOLOGY SUMMARY

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Common ground** | The body of mutual knowledge, beliefs, and assumptions shared by conversation participants |
| **Grounding** | The collaborative process of establishing mutual understanding |
| **Grounding criterion** | The standard of understanding deemed sufficient for current purposes |
| **Communal common ground** | Common ground based on shared community membership |
| **Personal common ground** | Common ground based on shared personal history and joint experience |
| **Physical co-presence** | Common ground from shared perceptual environment |
| **Linguistic co-presence** | Common ground from what has been said in the conversation |
| **Community co-presence** | Common ground from shared cultural/social group membership |
| **Presentation phase** | The phase where a speaker offers content for the listener's consideration |
| **Acceptance phase** | The phase where listeners provide evidence of understanding |
| **Contribution** | A unit of conversation that adds to common ground (presentation + acceptance) |
| **Conceptual pact** | A partner-specific agreement about how to refer to or conceptualize something |
| **Referential precedent** | An established way of referring to an entity, created through prior use |
| **Audience design** | Tailoring utterances to specific listeners based on estimated common ground |
| **Curse of knowledge** | Cognitive bias making it hard to imagine not knowing what you know |
| **Accommodation** | Silently adding a presupposed proposition to common ground to make an utterance interpretable |
| **Context set** (Stalnaker) | The set of possible worlds compatible with common ground |
| **Mutual manifestness** (Sperber & Wilson) | Facts/assumptions perceivable/inferable by both participants and mutually recognized as such |
| **Least collaborative effort** | Principle that participants minimize total joint effort while maintaining adequate understanding |
| **Back-channel responses** | Listener signals (uh-huh, mm-hmm, nods) indicating continued understanding |
| **Repair** | The process of fixing misunderstandings when grounding fails |
| **Installment** | Presenting information in pieces, with acceptance at each step |
