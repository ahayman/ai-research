# Supplement 3: Autism × Alexithymia × Cumulative-Overwhelm Trauma — What the Literature Says

## Purpose

This supplement investigates a specific triangulation: whether autism + alexithymia + trauma-response symptoms in the absence of a discrete identifiable trauma event is a recognized phenomenon in the published literature, and what mechanisms have been proposed for it. Where Supplements 1 and 2 examined alexithymia broadly and its measurement/dimensional structure, this supplement focuses narrowly on the question: when an autistic adult presents with PTSD- or CPTSD-pattern symptoms but cannot point to a Criterion-A stressor, what does the literature actually say about how that came about?

The approach is deliberately neutral. The user asked for material to read, evaluate, and form an informed view from — not for an agent that finds evidence to support a preferred causal model. The literature reviewed here ranges from peer-reviewed primary research (a small but rapidly growing base) to systematic reviews to clinical writing to lived-experience accounts. Evidence quality is flagged throughout, and the synthesis at the end maps each link in the proposed "cumulative overwhelm cascade" to its actual evidentiary status: empirical, clinically validated, theoretically articulated, or genuinely absent. This supplement should be read as a literature audit, not as advocacy for any particular model.

## Big-Picture Synthesis

There is a real and growing literature on this triangulation, but it is unevenly distributed across the components. The strongest finding — quantitatively replicated and now accepted in autism research — is that PTSD-pattern symptoms are common in autistic adults (in the 32-60% lifetime range across studies, vs ~4-7% in the general population) and that **a substantial portion of these symptoms arises following events that do not meet DSM-5 Criterion A**. Rumball et al. (2020) is the foundational empirical paper showing this. The notion that autistic adults can develop full PTSD symptom profiles from non-Criterion-A events is no longer fringe; it is the dominant finding when researchers actually look.

The second strong finding is autistic burnout as a distinct, validated phenomenon (Raymaker et al. 2020; Mantzalas et al. 2022; Arnold/Higgins et al. 2023) characterized by chronic exhaustion, skill loss, and reduced sensory tolerance arising from cumulative chronic life stress. Critically, recent work (Higgins, Arnold et al. 2025/2025-press) found r = 0.70 between autistic burnout and PTSS in trauma-exposed autistic adults, with exploratory factor analysis suggesting shared underlying factors. Burnout and posttraumatic stress are distinct constructs but heavily entangled in autistic populations.

The weaker, more theoretical-but-coherent links concern alexithymia's specific role as a *failure-to-detect* mechanism. The Quattrocki & Friston interoceptive predictive-coding account, the Bird/Brewer alexithymia hypothesis, and the multidomain interoception failure work (Brewer et al. 2016; Murphy et al. 2018) provide the mechanistic scaffolding, but no peer-reviewed primary study has directly tested the chain "alexithymia → late-state-recognition → cumulative dysregulation → meltdown/burnout cascade." That specific chain remains theoretically articulated but empirically untested as a unified model. Clinical writers (Neff, Price, Frankel/Paulsen, "Trauma Geek" lineage) have developed it, but it has not been studied as a single hypothesis.

The "meltdowns/shutdowns are themselves traumagenic when repeated" claim has clinical-writing consensus and qualitative phenomenological support (Paris et al. 2025 metaphor analysis; Phung et al. 2021) but no longitudinal empirical study showing that repeated meltdowns produce PTSD/CPTSD via dose-response. The mechanism is plausible and named in the literature, but the empirical link is inferential.

The honest summary: the literature recognizes the *phenomenon* (PTSD-pattern symptoms in autistic adults without classic Criterion A), and it recognizes the *substrate* (autistic burnout from cumulative load, alexithymia as interoceptive failure, sensory/social trauma as candidate stressors). What it does not yet have is a single empirically validated causal model uniting all of these into the "cumulative overwhelm cascade." That model exists in clinical writing and qualitative work; it has scaffolding in cognitive neuroscience; it does not yet have a confirmatory longitudinal or mechanistic study.

## Section 1: Autistic Burnout

**Foundational empirical work — high evidence quality.**

Raymaker et al. (2020), "Having All of Your Internal Resources Exhausted Beyond Measure and Being Left with No Clean-Up Crew" (PMC7313636; *Autism in Adulthood* 2(2):132-143), is the foundational community-based participatory research study defining autistic burnout. Through thematic analysis of 19 interviews and 19 public sources, they characterized autistic burnout as:

- **Primary characteristics**: chronic exhaustion, loss of skills, reduced tolerance to stimulus
- **Etiology**: "life stressors that add to the cumulative load an autistic person experiences"; "expectations and demands without adequate supports"; "barriers to support that create an inability to obtain relief from the cumulative load"
- **Duration**: typically 3+ months (distinguishing it from acute stress reactions)
- **Impacts**: negative effects on health, capacity for independent living, quality of life, including suicidal behavior

The Raymaker model is explicitly a *cumulative load* model: chronic stress + chronic mismatch + barriers to relief = burnout. This is the canonical framing in the autistic burnout literature.

**Follow-up quantitative work.**

- **Mantzalas et al. (2022a)** published a thematic analysis of 1,127 online posts (2005-2019) identifying eight primary themes including pervasive lack of awareness, discrimination/stigma, chronic/recurrent condition, direct health/wellbeing impact, and the moderating role of self-awareness and personal control.
- **Mantzalas et al. (2022b)** in *Autism Research* (DOI 10.1002/aur.2722) developed a Conceptual Model of Autistic Burnout (CMAB) integrating Job Demands-Resources theory, Conservation of Resources theory, and the Social-Relational disability model. Risk factors include autistic traits (social/conversational difficulties, change-coping, sensory sensitivities) and environmental demands; protective factors include accommodation, autonomy, and authentic identity.
- **Higgins et al. (2021)** used Grounded Delphi method with autistic experts by lived experience to refine the operational definition.
- **Arnold et al. (2023)** in *Autism* (DOI 10.1177/13623613221147401) developed and validated initial measurement (the AASPIRE Autistic Burnout Measure / ABSI) — a major step toward research-grade quantification.
- **Mantzalas et al. (2024)** in *Autism Research* (DOI 10.1002/aur.3129) provided psychometric evaluation comparing burnout assessment tools.
- **A 2025 scoping review** ("Low Battery Alarm") in *J Autism Dev Disord* (DOI 10.1007/s10803-025-06860-6, PMID 40317352) consolidated 14 studies into themes of subjective understanding, characterization, evaluation, co-occurrence/misidentification, biological mechanisms, and solutions, identifying epidemiology and risk-factor research as key gaps.
- **Ali, Mandy & Happé (2026)** in *Autism* (DOI 10.1177/13623613261422117, PMC13005893) is a recent qualitative study comparing experiences of earlier- and later-diagnosed autistic adults — finding broadly similar phenomenology but stronger relief-on-recognition effects in later-diagnosed adults.

**Distinction from depression.**

The clinical and research consensus is that autistic burnout is *distinct* from depression but frequently leads to it. Key differentiators:

- Burnout fluctuates with environmental demands; depression is more pervasive and stimulus-independent
- Burnout's emotional quality is exhaustion/frustration/sensory-overload-driven; depression is dominated by sadness/worthlessness/anhedonia
- Burnout's social withdrawal is "I cannot tolerate the input"; depression's is "I see no point"
- Recovery in burnout responds strongly to environmental change (reduced demands, accommodation, unmasking); depression often does not

This distinction is articulated in the Raymaker/Mantzalas work and clinically defended by Neff (Neurodivergent Insights), Attwood & Garnett, and others.

**Distinction from PTSD — and the key Higgins/Arnold finding.**

A 2025 paper by **Higgins, Arnold and colleagues** in *Research in Autism* (S3050579825000105) directly examined the burnout-PTSS relationship in trauma-exposed autistic adults (n=91). Findings:

- Pearson r = 0.70 (p < .001) between autistic burnout (ABSI) and PTSS (PCL-5)
- r = 0.61 with depression (PHQ-9) and r = 0.67 with GAD-7 anxiety
- Exploratory factor analysis suggested **shared underlying factors**, particularly concerning self-image and memory

This is currently the strongest empirical link between autistic burnout and posttraumatic-stress symptomatology in the peer-reviewed literature. The authors' interpretation is cautious: shared factors do not establish that burnout *causes* PTSS or vice versa; the constructs may simply capture overlapping aspects of chronic dysregulation in autistic adults. But the magnitude is striking and supports the thesis that, in autistic populations, burnout and trauma response are not cleanly separable.

**Whether burnout itself is recognized as PTSD-producing.** No peer-reviewed paper to date establishes burnout-→-PTSD as a directional causal pathway. However, clinical writing routinely describes autistic burnout as a phenomenon that *can include* trauma-response features, and the Higgins/Arnold factor analysis suggests substantial construct overlap. Recovery pathways are described as overlapping but distinct: rest/accommodation/unmasking address burnout; trauma processing addresses PTSD; both may be needed concurrently for many autistic adults.

**Neuroscience/biomarker work.** Limited. The scoping review identifies "Biological Mechanisms" as a gap. Some indirect evidence comes from HRV studies (see Section 9) suggesting parasympathetic suppression in autistic adults under chronic social stress, but no direct biomarker for autistic burnout exists.

## Section 2: Meltdowns and Shutdowns — Phenomenology and Traumagenic Status

**Phenomenology — qualitative evidence is robust.**

- **Phung, Penner, Pirlot & Welch (2021)** in *Frontiers in Psychology*, "What I Wish You Knew: Insights on Burnout, Inertia, Meltdown, and Shutdown From Autistic Youth" (PMC8595127), introduced the BIMS framework (Burnout, Inertia, Meltdown, Shutdown) and provided rich phenomenology from autistic youth describing each.
- **Paris, Lodestone, Houser & Lewis (2025)** in *Autism in Adulthood* (DOI 10.1089/aut.2024.0193), "Shutdowns Are Like You're Stuck on the Blue Screen of Death": A Metaphor Analysis of Autistic Shutdowns, used the Pragglejaz metaphor identification procedure on data from 86 autistic adults. They identified six metaphor clusters describing shutdowns: **being frozen, computer crash, going inside myself, when I can't keep up, survival mode, and playing a role.** Adults described shutdowns as states of stuck, immobilized, often perceived as response-to-threat — explicit "survival mode" framing.
- A 2023 lived-experience study of meltdowns in autistic adults (PubMed 36632658) characterized meltdowns as involving loss of control (often experienced as terrifying by the person themselves), with significant rumination/post-event processing and self-criticism after the fact.
- **Frontiers in Psychiatry (2026, Lim et al.) and Front Psychiatry (2022) qualitative study** further document autistic adults' descriptions of these events as deeply distressing internal experiences without language to describe them in real time.

**Howlin / Wing / Lai distinctions.** The classical Wing & Gould (1979) triad and Lai et al. lifespan work establish autism's behavioral phenomenology. The meltdown-shutdown distinction has been formalized largely in clinical and self-advocacy writing rather than in classical research papers. The clearest research formalization is the Phung et al. BIMS framework. Howlin's work on adult outcomes (Howlin et al. 2004, 2014) does not directly address meltdowns/shutdowns as discrete events but does document the chronic stress backdrop.

**Are meltdowns/shutdowns recognized as traumagenic events when repeated and unsupported?**

This is where the literature is uneven:

- **Clinical writing consensus is robust**: meltdowns are described by autistic adults and autistic-affirming clinicians (Neff, Price, Hendrickx, Reframing Autism, Trauma Geek lineage) as "internal disasters" that the person experiences from the inside as terrifying, with no language for what is happening, and with significant shame/self-criticism afterward. The argument that repeated meltdowns under invalidating conditions are themselves traumagenic is widely articulated in this literature.
- **Empirical evidence directly testing the meltdown→trauma chain is absent.** No published longitudinal study tracks autistic individuals' meltdown frequency and subsequent PTSS development.
- **Inferential evidence is moderate.** Rumball et al. (2020) explicitly identifies "events related to one's own mental health difficulties (for example psychotic experiences)" as among the non-DSM-5 traumas that produce full PTSD symptom profiles in autistic adults. By extension, meltdowns/shutdowns experienced as terrifying internal events would qualify, but Rumball does not tabulate this specific category.
- **Polyvagal-informed framing** (see Section 9) treats meltdowns as sympathetic-flight/fight responses and shutdowns as dorsal-vagal collapse, both representing high-cost autonomic events that, when repeated and without recovery, produce allostatic load. This is theoretically coherent but not empirically tested as a meltdown-specific causal pathway.

**Dissociation in autism.**

- The search did not surface a specific 2021 Trevisan study, but Trevisan and colleagues have published on emotion processing/face perception in autism. The dissociation-in-autism literature is led primarily by other groups.
- **Reuben, Stavropoulos and colleagues** examined dissociative symptoms in autistic adolescents (DOI 10.1007/s10803-024-06374-7, PubMed 38743151) finding high rates and associations with adverse experiences.
- **Pelaez & Soares-Boucaud (2022, PDF on ResearchGate) "Dissociation in Autism Spectrum Disorders: An Under-Recognized Symptom"** consolidates the case that dissociation is under-recognized clinically.
- A study of 687 self-identified autistic adults found 72% reported sexual or physical assault, and interpersonal trauma was significantly associated with both psychoform and somatoform dissociation (Reuben et al., interpersonal trauma study, PMC8992908).
- A 2026 study (S305065652600012X) found that **pathological dissociation in autistic adults was associated with bullying victimization rather than with autistic traits per se** — supporting the model that dissociation tracks chronic trauma exposure rather than being an autism feature.

**The shutdown vs. dissociation distinction.** Clinical literature distinguishes:

- *Autistic shutdown*: triggered by sensory/cognitive overload; person retains memory; system goes offline to process
- *Dissociation (depersonalization/derealization)*: triggered by emotional/threat overload; memory may be patchy; experience of unreality or detachment

But the boundary is blurry — Schauer & Elbert's defense cascade (Freeze-Flight-Fight-Fright-Flag-Faint) explicitly includes shutdown phenomena (Flag, Faint) as the dorsal-vagal response when escape is impossible. Their framework, while developed for trauma populations, has been imported into autism clinical writing as a way of conceptualizing repeated shutdowns as defense-cascade events.

**Freeze/fawn/shutdown as autistic trauma responses.** Clinical literature (and reframings within autistic-affirming therapy) emphasizes that autistic adults often present with freeze/fawn/shutdown patterns more than fight/flight. The fawn response in particular has been linked to chronic masking. Empirical evidence for differential trauma-response patterning in autism is limited.

## Section 3: Camouflaging / Masking as Chronic Stressor

**Empirical evidence is moderate and growing.**

- **Hull et al. (2019)** developed the CAT-Q (Camouflaging Autistic Traits Questionnaire; *J Autism Dev Disord*, DOI 10.1007/s10803-018-3792-6, PMC6394586). 25-item self-report; three factors (Masking, Assimilation, Compensation); high internal consistency (α = 0.94). All CAT-Q scores correlate with depression and generalized anxiety in autistic adults. Total and Assimilation scores negatively correlate with well-being.
- **Cassidy et al. (2018) and (2019/2020)** established camouflaging as a unique risk factor for suicidality in autistic adults, with effect mediated through thwarted belongingness (interpersonal psychological theory of suicide). Cassidy et al. (2023) in *Suicide and Life-Threatening Behavior* extended this through the Integrated Motivational-Volitional model showing camouflaging associated with defeat, entrapment, and lifetime suicidal thoughts.
- A 2024 systematic review (S3050656525000288) consolidated the consequences-of-camouflaging literature; another 2025 mixed-methods systematic review specifically on camouflaging-suicide-behavior link confirmed consistent association.
- **A co-twin control study (PMC12659362)** found camouflaging associated with both psychological and physiological stress markers, with twin-control design providing stronger causal inference than between-group comparisons.
- **Scheeren et al. (2025)** in *Autism* used ecological momentary assessment showing real-time elevated stress during masking episodes.
- **What You Are Hiding Could Be Hurting You (Zhuang et al., PMC11317797, *Autism in Adulthood* 2024)** linked autistic masking to interpersonal trauma, authenticity loss, and self-esteem deterioration.

**Masking → mental health deterioration: longitudinal evidence?**

Most of the literature is cross-sectional. Longitudinal designs are rare. The co-twin control design and the EMA studies represent the strongest causal inference currently available; both support the masking → stress / mental-health-cost direction. A genuine prospective longitudinal study of masking trajectory and mental health outcomes is a research gap.

**Burnout cascade.** Pearson & Rose (2021) "A Conceptual Analysis of Autistic Masking" (DOI 10.1089/aut.2020.0043) is the conceptual articulation of masking as response to stigma and "illusion of choice." Their 2023 work on interpersonal victimization (DOI 10.1177/13623613221104546) extends this to chronic invalidation effects. The burnout cascade — chronic masking → exhaustion → burnout → depression/suicidality — is articulated across these papers as a probable but not empirically-validated chain.

**Masking as instructed harm.** The PMC11317797 study and broader literature note that explicit or implicit teaching of masking to autistic children (by caregivers, therapists, ABA programs) can have stigmatizing, shaming, and interpersonally traumatizing effects. Kupferstein (2018) reported PTSS in 46% of ABA-exposed respondents (criticized methodologically; see Concerns About ABA-Based Intervention, PMC9114057, for the critical response). The Kupferstein finding is contested but the broader pattern — that teaching autistic children to suppress autistic responses can contribute to traumatization — has clinical-writing support.

## Section 4: Double Empathy Problem and Chronic Invalidation

**Theoretical foundation — strong.**

Milton's (2012) original "On the ontological status of autism: the 'double empathy problem'" (*Disability & Society* 27(6)) is widely cited. The 2022 follow-up "The 'double empathy problem': Ten years on" (Milton, Gurbuz & López, *Autism* DOI 10.1177/13623613221129123, PubMed 36263746) consolidates the empirical evidence base for the theory:

- Mutual misunderstanding between autistic and non-autistic people is bidirectional; it is not an autistic deficit
- Autistic-autistic interactions show better information transfer and rapport than autistic-non-autistic interactions (e.g., Crompton et al. 2020 telephone game studies)
- The original framing explicitly references this as "an everyday and often traumatic experience for autistic people"

**Chronic invalidation as developmental relational-trauma pathway.**

- Clinical literature (Pearson & Rose 2021, 2023; Neff; the autistic-affirming-therapy lineage) describes the cumulative effect of chronic mismatch + invalidation as a developmental-trauma-equivalent pathway. The argument is that autistic children grow up in environments where their experience is repeatedly contradicted, dismissed, or pathologized, producing patterns analogous to those seen in chronic invalidating-environment cases (cf. Linehan's biosocial model of BPD development).
- Empirical evidence specific to "double empathy problem → developmental trauma" is largely absent. The PMC9310842 commentary on autism and the double-empathy problem discusses implications for development and mental health but does not establish empirical chains.
- Botha & Frost (2020) "Extending the Minority Stress Model to Understand Mental Health Problems Experienced by the Autistic Population" (*Society and Mental Health*, DOI 10.1177/2156869318804297) is the most rigorous published extension of minority-stress theory to autism. Stigma, internalized stigma, concealment, and discrimination significantly predict poorer mental health in autistic adults. This is the empirical version of the chronic-invalidation argument.

**Alexithymia × double empathy problem.** No single paper directly examines the interaction. The argument articulated in clinical writing is: chronic interpersonal mismatch produces affect that the alexithymic individual cannot process, producing accumulation. This is theoretically coherent but not directly studied as a mechanism.

## Section 5: Sensory Overload as Traumagenic

**Mixed evidence — clinical concept, partial empirical support.**

- **Sensory profile literature** (Dunn 1997 and extensions) is well-established but does not directly address trauma.
- **Bogdashina (2003, 2nd ed. 2016) "Sensory Perceptual Issues in Autism and Asperger Syndrome"** is the foundational synthesis of autistic sensory phenomenology, articulating that sensory overload can lead to "system shutdowns" — partial or total cessation of processing. Clinical/practitioner-oriented; primarily descriptive.
- **Fulton, Reardon, Richardson & Jones (2020) "Sensory Trauma: Autism, Sensory Difference and the Daily Experience of Fear"** is a published practitioner book proposing the "sensory trauma" concept explicitly. It is grey-literature/practitioner-level, not peer-reviewed primary research, but is cited in clinical contexts.
- **Rumball et al. (2020, *Autism Research*, DOI 10.1002/aur.2306)** — the canonical paper for non-DSM-5 trauma in autism — explicitly identifies sensory experiences as among the events producing full PTSD symptom profiles. This is the strongest empirical anchor for the sensory-trauma concept.
- **A 2024 study (PubMed 38224059)** examined rumination and altered sensory reactivity as vulnerability factors for PTSS in adults with autistic traits, supporting sensory reactivity → PTSS development pathways.
- The 2024 systematic review on assessment/treatment of PTSD in autistic people (DOI 10.1007/s40489-024-00430-9) includes sensory experiences in the considered traumagenic event categories.

**Is "sensory PTSD" a real, theoretical, or clinical-only concept?**

Honestly: it sits at the boundary. Empirical work supports that sensory experiences can produce PTSS in autistic populations (Rumball; rumination/sensory study). The construct of "sensory PTSD" as a distinct diagnostic entity is not in DSM-5 or ICD-11 and has not been formally validated. The grey-literature concept (Fulton et al.) is articulated as a phenomenology of "daily experience of fear" that resembles but is not formally PTSD.

**Webber, MacLennan, Suskind** as named in the brief — these did not surface as primary search hits in this round; the dominant peer-reviewed and grey-literature voices are Bogdashina, Fulton et al., Rumball, and the autistic-affirming clinical lineage.

## Section 6: Alexithymia as Failure-to-Detect Mechanism — Specifically

**This is where the user's central question lives. Honest evidence audit.**

**The components of the chain.**

1. **Alexithymia as multidomain interoceptive failure** — *Strongly empirically supported.*
   - Brewer, Cook & Bird (2016) "Alexithymia: a general deficit of interoception" (*Royal Society Open Science*, DOI 10.1098/rsos.150664, PMC5098957)
   - Murphy et al. (2018) "Alexithymia is associated with a multidomain, multidimensional failure of interoception" (PMC5824617). Demonstrates failure across cardiac, respiratory, muscular, taste — not just affective interoception. Confusion among non-affective states (hunger/tiredness/arousal) and affective states is a hallmark.
   - Shah et al. (2016) "Alexithymia, not autism, is associated with impaired interoception" (PMC4962768) establishes that interoception deficit tracks alexithymia, not autism per se; many studies finding "autism interoception deficits" appear to actually reflect co-occurring alexithymia.

2. **Quattrocki & Friston interoceptive predictive coding** — *Theoretical, well-developed.*
   - Quattrocki & Friston (2014, *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*, DOI 10.1016/j.neubiorev.2014.09.012, PMC4726659) propose autism as failure of interoceptive predictive coding centered on aberrant oxytocin precision-weighting.
   - Bird's commentary (2015, PubMed 26192103) reframes this as an alexithymia-not-autism failure.
   - Predictive-coding accounts predict that when prediction errors accumulate without proper precision-weighted integration, the system breaks down. *This is the formal mechanism for "accumulation → breakdown"*, but the model has not been empirically validated through a longitudinal prediction-error-tracking → breakdown study.

3. **Late-recognition phenomenology** — *Qualitatively documented.*
   - Interoception Sensory Questionnaire research validated in autistic adults shows 74% report significant interoceptive confusion unless bodily signals are extreme (suggesting threshold-effect: late recognition).
   - Clinical writing (Neff; Mahler interoception literature; AUDHD Australia) describes the "interoception gap" — body whispers, autistic/AuDHD brain shouts too late.
   - The Mahler Interoception Curriculum work (Mahler et al. 2022, *Occupational Therapy International*, DOI 10.1155/2022/9328967, PMC9045986) demonstrated that interoception training improves emotion regulation in autistic children — providing inferential evidence that interoception is the relevant lever, but it is a treatment study, not a mechanism study.

4. **Cascade-to-breakdown** — *Theoretically coherent, not directly tested.*
   - The argument: if you cannot detect overwhelm early, it accumulates; if it accumulates without integration, it produces meltdown/shutdown/dissociation/burnout.
   - The argument is articulated in:
     - Frankel/Paulsen EMDR-autism literature (*Steve Frankel Group* and Paulsen's "When Is It Autism and When Is It Trauma" interview)
     - Neff (Neurodivergent Insights) clinical writing
     - Trauma Geek polyvagal-autism synthesis
   - No primary empirical study tracks the chain in autistic adults longitudinally.

5. **Repeated meltdowns as themselves traumagenic** — *Clinical consensus, empirically inferred.*
   - Discussed in Section 2.

**What the literature does NOT say (yet).**

- There is no published study explicitly testing "alexithymia → late state recognition → cumulative dysregulation → meltdown → repeated meltdowns → CPTSD-pattern symptoms" as a unified causal chain.
- The chain's *components* are empirically supported (alexithymia interoception failure; autistic burnout from cumulative load; non-DSM-5 traumas producing PTSS; meltdown phenomenology). The *unification* is in clinical writing.
- The "I didn't know I was at the edge until I went over" phenomenology is documented in autistic-adult lived experience and clinical case literature but is not empirically operationalized as a research construct.

**Honest position.** This chain is plausible, internally coherent, and consistent with multiple converging lines of evidence. It is not yet a validated empirical model. The closest single piece of evidence supporting the entire chain implicitly is the Higgins/Arnold finding of r = 0.70 between burnout and PTSS — which is consistent with the chain producing observed clinical correlations — but correlation does not establish the proposed mechanism.

## Section 7: CPTSD Without Discrete Criterion-A Stressor

**The diagnostic landscape — empirically and conceptually clear.**

**ICD-11 CPTSD requirements:**
- Exposure to "an event or series of events of an extremely threatening or horrific nature, most commonly prolonged or repetitive events from which escape is difficult or impossible"
- All three PTSD core clusters (re-experiencing, avoidance, sense of threat)
- All three Disturbances of Self-Organization (DSO) clusters: emotional dysregulation, negative self-concept, interpersonal difficulties
- **Critically: a *specific type* of trauma is not required.** The ICD-11 conceptualizes prolonged complex trauma as a *risk factor* for CPTSD, not as a diagnostic requirement. (Cloitre et al. 2014; ICD-11 working group materials.)

**DSM-5 PTSD Criterion A** is more restrictive — explicit life-threat or sexual violence — but DSM-5-TR has retained the requirement. DSM-5 removed the "intense fear/helplessness/horror" peri-traumatic response criterion (DSM-IV) because it had no diagnostic utility (Brewin et al. 2009).

**The "non-Criterion-A trauma producing full PTSD symptoms" finding.**

- **Rumball et al. (2020)** is the foundational empirical paper. In trauma-exposed autistic adults, 33 reported DSM-5 traumas and 35 reported non-DSM-5 traumas. **More than 40% in each group** met thresholds for probable PTSD. Non-DSM-5 traumas explicitly identified by autistic adults included: abandonment/loss; transitions/change; social difficulties/confusion; events related to one's own mental health (psychotic experiences, breakdowns).
- **Brewin et al. (2019)** has been cited as supporting broader subjective-trauma framings; the formal DSM/ICD position remains that some triggering event is needed but its definition is contested.
- **Kerns et al. (2022)** in *Autism* (DOI 10.1177/13623613211070637, PMID 35068176) "Exploring potential sources of childhood trauma" — qualitative study with autistic adults and caregivers — found that trauma sources included not only traditional traumas and social marginalization but also "conflicts between autistic characteristics and the environment" (sensory trauma; transitions; chronic stress of social decoding). All adults and caregivers described trauma sources not captured by standardized measures.
- **Lim et al. (2025)** in *Journal of Clinical Psychology* (DOI 10.1002/jclp.23783) — qualitative work with autistic young adults further confirming non-traditional trauma source identification.

**Insidious trauma (Brown 1991/2008; Root 1992).**

- **Root, Maria (1992)** introduced the term "insidious trauma" for non-punctual, cumulative traumagenic experience.
- **Brown, Laura (2008)** "Cultural Competence in Trauma Therapy: Beyond the Flashback" — and the earlier 1991 framing Brown developed in feminist trauma theory — formalized cumulative micro-aggression-as-traumagenesis.
- **Distress evoked by insidious traumatization results from cumulative micro-aggressions, each one not large enough to be a traumatic stressor, but all taken together yielding a traumagenic experience.**
- The minority-stress framework (Meyer 1995, 2003; extended to autism by Botha & Frost 2020) is the empirical operationalization of insidious trauma for marginalized populations.

**Type III trauma (Solomon & Heide 1999, *International Journal of Offender Therapy and Comparative Criminology* 43(2):202-210).**

- Extends Lenore Terr's Type I/II framework to "multiple, pervasive, violent events beginning at an early age and continuing over a long period of time."
- Diagnostic features overlap with CPTSD: dissociation, emotional numbing, fragmented self, hopelessness, shame, trust difficulties.
- Type III survivors are typically diagnosed with CPTSD.
- Has not been formally adopted into DSM but has been influential in clinical writing.

**Clinical reality of "full CPTSD without single identifiable trauma."**

The literature recognizes that:
- ICD-11 CPTSD is *symptom-defined*, not stressor-defined; you can meet diagnostic criteria with prolonged stress exposure of contested Criterion-A status.
- DSM-5 PTSD is more restrictive but Rumball et al. and follow-up empirical work demonstrate that *symptom profiles* indistinguishable from PTSD develop in autistic populations after non-Criterion-A events.
- The Botha & Frost minority-stress framework provides the formal empirical model for chronic-invalidation-as-traumagenic in autistic populations.
- "Hidden disability stress" / chronic invalidation as Criterion-A-equivalent is articulated in clinical writing (Neff; Pearson & Rose; autistic-affirming-therapy lineage) and has minority-stress theoretical scaffolding but is not formally codified in diagnostic systems.

**Bottom line: the diagnostic systems are slowly accommodating cumulative-stress trauma, but the gap between clinical reality and formal Criterion A is substantial, and autism is one of the populations where this gap is most apparent.**

## Section 8: Schimmenti and Adjacent Trauma-Alexithymia Lineages Applied to Autism

**The Schimmenti lineage — robust developmental trauma-alexithymia work.**

Adriano Schimmenti (Università degli Studi di Enna Kore) has published extensively on:

- **Attachment trauma → alexithymia → arrested mentalization.** Schimmenti & Caretti (2011, 2018) develop a clinical framework where developmental relational trauma (insecure/disorganized attachment, chronic invalidation) produces alexithymia as a *capacity deficit* (the person never developed full affective-interpretive capacity) alongside dissociation as an *invasive defense* (a mechanism for managing overwhelming arousal). Both limit the ability to modulate affect via cognition.
- **Alexithymia, dissociation, and trauma in clinical populations** — examined in:
  - Caretti, Craparo, Ragonese & Schimmenti — affective dysregulation, trauma, and dissociation in non-clinical adolescents (developmental perspective)
  - Schimmenti et al. — eating disorder populations showing higher alexithymia, shame, dissociation, and traumatic feelings (PMC3579461)
  - Internet addiction late-adolescent moderated mediation analysis
  - Alcohol addiction trauma-dissociation-alexithymia chain (PMC4124194)

**The Farina & Schimmenti 2024-2025 attachment-trauma synthesis** (PMC12603935, "Attachment Trauma and its Psychopathological Domains Through the Neuropsychodynamic Model of Self and Trauma") proposes a transdiagnostic framework linking early relational trauma to complex psychopathology. Mental self formation depends on caregiver capacity to reflect/verbalize the child's inner emotional states (mentalization scaffolding); attachment trauma compromises this, producing dysregulation, alexithymia, fragmented narrative identity, and impaired affective reflection.

**Has Schimmenti's work been extended to autism specifically?**

Not directly in the searched literature. The Schimmenti framework is developed primarily in clinical (non-autistic) populations: eating disorders, Internet/alcohol addiction, attachment-disordered. The conceptual extension — that the developmental-trauma-→-alexithymia-→-arrested-mentalization framework could apply to autistic individuals who experienced chronic invalidation in childhood — is articulated in some autistic-affirming therapy writing (Frankel/Paulsen EMDR-autism work; Neff) but is not Schimmenti's own scholarship.

**The conceptual extension is plausible because:**
- Autistic children frequently experience chronic relational mismatch (double empathy problem) without it being recognized as developmentally adverse
- Mentalization scaffolding requires accurate caregiver mirroring, which is structurally compromised when caregiver-child neurotypes differ
- Resulting alexithymia in autistic adults could partially reflect this developmental relational pathway in addition to/instead of an inherent neurobiological feature

**But this remains theoretical synthesis, not Schimmenti's primary research focus.**

## Section 9: Polyvagal and Chronic Stress Physiological Frameworks

**Polyvagal theory in autism — contested empirical basis, popular clinical application.**

**Porges' polyvagal theory** (1995, 2007, 2011 monograph; 2024 PMC12302812 update) proposes a hierarchical autonomic nervous system organization:
1. Ventral vagal (social engagement, safety)
2. Sympathetic (mobilization, fight/flight)
3. Dorsal vagal (immobilization, shutdown, dissociation)

**Clinical application to autism:**
- Disruption of ventral vagal social engagement is theorized as core to autistic social-communication patterns
- Dorsal vagal shutdown is invoked to explain shutdowns
- Sympathetic activation is invoked for meltdowns
- Chronic stress → primed-for-dorsal-vagal pattern is the autistic-affirming therapy framing

**Empirical evidence:**
- HRV research (Bal et al. 2010 and others) shows reduced HRV in autistic adults, particularly under social stress
- Meta-analysis (S014976342030525X) confirms lower parasympathetic indices in ASD baseline and under social stress/debriefing
- A 2024 Frontiers in Psychiatry meta-analysis (10.3389/fpsyt.2021.760396) confirms HRV reduction
- However, **PMC8964964 ("Autism Spectrum Disorder in Children Is Not Associated With Abnormal Autonomic Nervous System Function: Hypothesis and Theory")** challenges the universal claim, arguing methodological and population heterogeneity confound the picture.

**Dorsal vagal as candidate trauma response without overt trauma.** The polyvagal-informed clinical literature (Trauma Geek; Sallycat PDA) extends dorsal-vagal framing to chronic-stress-produced shutdown patterns in autistic adults without discrete trauma. Empirical evidence for chronic dorsal vagal shutdown specifically (vs reduced parasympathetic tone in general) is limited.

**HRV findings in autism + alexithymia + chronic stress.**
- Multiple studies (PMC7532797, PMC11224273, *Scientific Reports* 2025 youth study) link reduced HRV to alexithymia and emotion-regulation challenges in autism
- Affective reactivity to event-related stressors is elevated in autistic participants vs controls
- Autistic Traits Moderate Cardiac Autonomic Activity-Interoceptive Accuracy-Emotion Processing relationships

**Honest assessment:** polyvagal-informed framing is theoretically appealing and has substantial reduced-HRV empirical support, but the *specific* claims about chronic dorsal-vagal-shutdown-as-trauma-equivalent in autistic adults without discrete trauma exposure are clinical-writing extensions of the theory, not directly tested.

**The "I don't know where my trauma response came from" phenomenology.**
- Late-diagnosed autistic adults' accounts (in Ali/Mandy/Happé 2026; Pearson/Rose; lived-experience websites including Healing Room Belgium and WWDA neve stories) commonly describe:
  - Discovering autism diagnosis in adulthood
  - Recognizing decades of cumulative invalidation/masking/sensory trauma in retrospect
  - Often having received misdiagnoses (depression, anxiety, BPD, CPTSD without identified source) prior to autism recognition
  - Trauma response that "doesn't fit" — no discrete event, but full symptom profile
- The *Murphys Therapy Corner* and similar clinical-blog literature on "perimenopause + late autism diagnosis + trauma cascade" describes a recurring pattern where masking erosion in midlife reveals previously-masked overwhelm.

**Epistemic weight.** These accounts are clinical/lived-experience reports, not RCTs. They consistently describe a phenomenon. The phenomenon is taken seriously enough that peer-reviewed qualitative work (Kerns; Ali/Mandy/Happé; Phung; Paris) has documented analogous experience. The quantitative anchor is Rumball: 40%+ of trauma-exposed autistic adults meet PTSD criteria, half from non-DSM-5 events.

## Section 11: Treatment Implications

**Trauma therapy for cumulative-overwhelm-derived trauma response: what the literature says.**

**General position:** standard trauma-focused CBT and exposure-based therapies developed for discrete-event trauma are often *not directly suitable* for cumulative-overwhelm trauma in autistic adults. Key adaptations identified in the literature:

- **Hoover & Romero (2019)** "Trauma and Autism Spectrum Disorder: Review, Proposed Treatment Adaptations and Future Directions" (PMC6901292) — slower pacing, more explicit instruction, reduced abstract emotion vocabulary, greater tolerance for non-verbal/indirect disclosure, repeated rationale explanations.
- **2024 systematic review of PTSD assessment and treatment in autistic people** (DOI 10.1007/s40489-024-00430-9) — autism-specific adaptations of TF-CBT in emotion regulation, graduated exposure, cognitive restructuring, psychoeducation.
- **EMDR adaptations** — Frankel/Paulsen lineage (Steve Frankel Group); 2023 Delphi study identified 124 adaptations to EMDR for autistic individuals (35 general + 81 phase-specific + 8 supervision).

**Is exposure-based therapy appropriate when there's no discrete event to expose to?**

Empirically: this is contested. When there's no discrete event:
- *Standard exposure protocols* are not directly applicable; you cannot expose to a non-event.
- *Modified exposure* approaches focus on:
  - Sensory exposure with regulation skill-building (interoception training)
  - Affective tolerance work
  - Approaching previously avoided contexts/situations gradually
- *Capacity-building approaches* (parts work, somatic, attachment-repair) may be more suitable than direct exposure when trauma arose from chronic capacity-overwhelm rather than from discrete threat.

**Frankel/Paulsen EMDR-Early-Trauma work** specifically distinguishes alexithymia, somatic dissociation, and structural dissociation when planning EMDR with autistic adults — this is one of the most explicit attempts to develop a clinical-protocol response to the "no discrete event but full trauma response" presentation.

**Implications for IFS, somatic, and cognitive approaches.**

(Note: the deep dive's existing categories address IFS in detail. The supplement's contribution is to flag what's specific to *cumulative-overwhelm-derived* trauma rather than discrete-event trauma.)

- **IFS**: parts-work approaches may be well-suited because cumulative overwhelm tends to produce protector-burdened patterns (chronic managers; firefighters appearing as meltdowns; exiles holding the un-named overwhelm). However, alexithymia complicates direct emotional access to parts, requiring adaptations (somatic channels; externalized expression; behavioral observation as substitute for affective report). Hodgson, Ziviani et al. and the autistic-IFS literature address this.
- **Somatic approaches**: theoretically well-suited because they bypass the alexithymia bottleneck via direct body-based work. Empirical evidence specific to autistic populations is limited but promising. Mahler interoception curriculum is the best-developed body-based intervention with empirical support (autistic children).
- **Cognitive approaches**: may need substantial adaptation. Trauma-focused CBT works through cognitive restructuring of trauma narrative; when there is no discrete narrative, the cognitive material is more diffuse (chronic invalidation themes; identity-formation themes; sensory-environment fit themes). Some autistic adults report cognitive approaches helpful for understanding-the-pattern even when standard trauma-CBT is poorly fitted.

**The autistic-affirming trauma-therapy adaptations — emerging consensus features:**

1. Validate non-DSM-5 trauma sources as legitimate
2. Pace far more slowly than standard PTSD protocols
3. Build interoception/affect-recognition capacity *before* trauma processing
4. Address identity-and-unmasking concurrent with trauma processing
5. Integrate sensory-environment work as primary, not adjunctive
6. Address chronic-mismatch and double-empathy-problem context as part of psychoeducation
7. Anticipate that recovery includes structural life changes (accommodation, demand reduction, community/identity), not only symptom reduction

(Henderson, Beardon — these specific names did not surface as primary search hits in this round; the autistic-affirming-therapy adaptation literature is primarily articulated through the names above and others including Garry Brown, Luke Beardon, Sarah Hendrickx, and the various practitioner books and clinical-blog literatures.)

## The "Cumulative Overwhelm Cascade" Model — Synthesis Attempt

Pulling Sections 1-6 together, the candidate causal model can be stated as:

**Chain:**
1. Autistic neurology + chronic environmental mismatch (sensory + social + cognitive demands) →
2. Cumulative load that exceeds adaptation capacity →
3. Alexithymia/interoceptive failure prevents early detection of state →
4. Late-recognition: state is only detected when extreme →
5. Meltdown (sympathetic) or shutdown (dorsal-vagal) as autonomic response to threshold-crossing →
6. Recovery without integration; chronic invalidation around the event →
7. Repeated meltdown/shutdown events accumulate as themselves traumagenic →
8. Eventually: autistic burnout; CPTSD-pattern symptoms; identity erosion; suicidality risk

**Evidence for each link:**

| Link | Empirical | Clinical-consensus | Theoretical | Absent |
|------|-----------|-------------------|-------------|--------|
| 1. Chronic environmental mismatch | Strong (sensory profile lit; double empathy lit; minority stress lit Botha & Frost) | | | |
| 2. Cumulative load → exceeds capacity | Strong (Raymaker; Mantzalas; Arnold) | Strong | | |
| 3. Alexithymia → interoceptive failure | Strong (Bird/Brewer/Murphy/Shah lineage) | Strong | | |
| 4. Late-recognition phenomenology | Moderate (ISQ data; Mahler intervention support) | Strong | | |
| 5. Meltdown/shutdown as autonomic response | Phenomenological (Phung; Paris; Wing/Lai descriptive) | Strong | Strong (polyvagal) | |
| 6. Chronic invalidation around event | | Strong (clinical writing) | Strong (Botha & Frost minority stress) | Direct empirical confirmation absent |
| 7. Repeated meltdown as traumagenic | Inferential (Rumball) | Strong | | Direct longitudinal test absent |
| 8. Cascade → burnout/CPTSD | Moderate (Higgins/Arnold r=.70 burnout↔PTSS factor analysis) | Strong | | Unified causal test absent |

**What's strongest:** The endpoints (autistic adults present with PTSD-pattern symptoms after non-Criterion-A events; high burnout-PTSS correlation; alexithymia is real interoceptive deficit) and the substrate (chronic mismatch and minority stress are real and quantified).

**What's missing:** A longitudinal study that tracks autistic adults' alexithymia/interoception, life-stressor accumulation, meltdown frequency, and PTSS development over time. This would convert the chain from "plausible synthesis" to "empirically validated mechanism." No such study has been published.

**Specifically untested:** the "failure-to-detect-→-late-cascade-to-breakdown" specific causal pathway. The components are validated; the causal sequence is not.

## Open Questions and Research Gaps

Specific peer-reviewed-research-gap honest list:

1. **No longitudinal study** has tracked autistic adults' alexithymia/interoception, life-stressor accumulation, meltdown/shutdown frequency, and PTSS development over time. This is the single most important gap.
2. **No biomarker for autistic burnout** — the scoping review explicitly identifies biological mechanisms as a research gap.
3. **No direct empirical test** of the Quattrocki-Friston interoceptive predictive coding breakdown claim in autism.
4. **No prospective study of masking → mental health deterioration** in long-term cohorts (the existing literature is cross-sectional or twin-control).
5. **No empirical test of repeated-meltdown-as-traumagenic dose-response.**
6. **Schimmenti's developmental-trauma-alexithymia framework has not been formally extended** to autistic populations as a primary research focus, though the conceptual extension is plausible.
7. **The polyvagal-informed dorsal-vagal-shutdown framework** has HRV-level support but no direct test of "chronic dorsal-vagal-shutdown without discrete trauma" as a distinct construct.
8. **Treatment-protocol RCTs** for autistic adults presenting with cumulative-overwhelm-derived trauma response are essentially absent. Existing trauma-treatment adaptations are clinical-consensus, expert-opinion, and small-case-study based.
9. **Differential diagnosis research** for autism + CPTSD + EUPD (Sarr et al. 2025 Delphi study is a start but more empirical work is needed).
10. **Sex/gender disparities** — autistic women experience higher masking, higher late-diagnosis rates, and likely different trajectories; the empirical picture is incomplete.
