# Category 22: Social Hierarchy and Power Pragmatics

## The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

---

## How to Read This Document

This is the **cross-cutting** category. Hierarchy doesn't sit alongside other pragmatic mechanisms -- it **modulates all of them**. Your politeness level (Category 3), hedging intensity (Category 6), register (Category 7), turn-taking rights (Category 4), topic control (Category 13), and even phatic greeting conventions (Category 19) all shift based on where you sit relative to the other person in a social hierarchy.

If the previous categories are instruments, hierarchy is the **conductor** -- it tells each instrument how loud to play, when to play, and when to stay silent.

For autistic individuals, this is one of the most consequential pragmatic domains. The autistic community has coined the term **"hierarchy blindness"** -- not an inability to understand hierarchy, but a failure to **automatically detect and respond** to implicit hierarchy cues. This document makes those cues explicit.

---

## 1. Hierarchy Is Encoded in Language

Every pragmatic system we've covered shifts based on relative status:

| Category | How Hierarchy Modulates It |
|----------|---------------------------|
| **Politeness (Cat 3)** | More power differential = more politeness needed upward (Brown & Levinson's P variable) |
| **Hedging (Cat 6)** | Lower-status speakers hedge more; higher-status speakers can be more direct |
| **Register (Cat 7)** | Register shifts with hierarchy -- more formal upward, more casual with equals |
| **Turn-taking (Cat 4)** | Higher-status speakers speak more, interrupt more, and control who speaks |
| **Topic control (Cat 13)** | Higher-status speakers introduce and change topics; others follow |
| **Questions (Cat 17)** | The questioner typically has more power (doctor, interviewer, teacher) |
| **Discourse markers (Cat 5)** | Lower-status speakers use more hedging markers; higher-status use fewer |
| **Phatic (Cat 19)** | Even greetings encode hierarchy -- who initiates, what formality level |
| **Prosody (Cat 15)** | Higher-status speakers speak more slowly with more pauses |

## 2. Two Pathways to Status

Research (Cheng et al., 2013) identifies two fundamental ways people achieve status:

| Pathway | How Status Is Gained | Pragmatic Style | Stability |
|---------|---------------------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Dominance** | Coercion, intimidation, resource control | Direct, assertive, interrupts freely, controls topics | Unstable -- maintained by threat |
| **Prestige** | Demonstrated competence, knowledge, skill | Earns speaking rights rather than seizing them | Stable -- maintained by continued competence |

Most real hierarchies involve **both** simultaneously. A company has a formal hierarchy (org chart = dominance-adjacent) and an informal one (who people actually go to = prestige-based). The person with the title and the person with the influence may not be the same.

---

## 3. Five Hierarchy Types

| Type | How It Forms | Pragmatic Rules | Autistic Difficulty |
|------|-------------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Formal/Institutional** | Explicitly assigned (titles, org charts, ranks) | Use titles, hedge upward, accept directives | Lowest -- structure is visible |
| **Informal/Emergent** | Forms spontaneously in any group | Must be inferred from behavior | Highest -- invisible |
| **Expertise/Competence** | Who knows most about the current topic | Shifts dynamically with topic | Often navigated well |
| **Social/Popularity** | Charisma, likability, network centrality | Based on social capital | Very high -- signals are opaque |
| **Situational/Contextual** | The host, the driver, the regular | Shifts with context | Moderate -- context clues available |

## 4. Detecting Hierarchy: The Observable Signals Checklist

**No social intuition required** -- only observable behaviors:

### 4.1 Linguistic Signals

| Signal | What to Observe | Higher Status = |
|--------|----------------|----------------|
| **Speaking time** | Who talks most? | Speaks more, longer turns |
| **Topic control** | Who introduces/changes subjects? | Sets the agenda |
| **Hedging** | Who softens their speech more? | Hedges LESS |
| **Interruptions** | Who interrupts, and whose interruptions are tolerated? | Interrupts freely; their interruptions accepted |
| **Questions** | Who asks questions of whom? | Asks more (questioner = power) |
| **Address forms** | Asymmetric? (They use your first name; you use their title) | Addressed more casually by others |
| **Last word** | Who speaks last before a decision? | Often the actual decision-maker |
| **Deference markers** | Who says "Sorry to bother you" / "If you don't mind"? | Receives deference, doesn't produce it |

### 4.2 Behavioral Signals

| Signal | Higher Status = |
|--------|----------------|
| **Who defers in disagreement?** | Others adjust their position toward theirs |
| **Whose suggestions get followed?** | Their proposals become action |
| **Who can break rules?** | They arrive late, dress casually without consequence ("idiosyncrasy credit") |
| **Who do people look at?** | Gaze directed toward them during decisions |
| **Spatial position** | Head of table, center of group |
| **Posture** | More expansive, takes up more space |

## 5. The Hierarchy Paradox: Competence vs. Claiming

In prestige hierarchies, competence earns status -- but **you're not supposed to claim it overtly**. The acceptable way is to **demonstrate**, not **declare**.

| Action | How It's Perceived |
|--------|-------------------|
| "I'm the expert here." | Arrogant -- LOWERS perceived status |
| Providing a well-timed, insightful contribution | Competent -- RAISES perceived status |
| Correcting someone publicly | Status challenge -- creates friction |
| Correcting someone privately with hedging | Helpful -- raises prestige |
| Stating your qualifications | Can read as insecure or boastful |
| Having others reference your qualifications | Prestige confirmed by third party |

**The autistic trap**: Autistic individuals who ARE expert may state qualifications directly, correct others publicly, or be overtly accurate -- all of which are socially penalized not because they're wrong, but because the pragmatic system demands that competence be **displayed** rather than **declared**.

## 6. The Autistic Experience with Hierarchy

### 6.1 "Hierarchy Blindness"

Research (Ogawa et al., 2019, *Scientific Reports*) found that autistic individuals process social rank through **different neural pathways** -- using physical-characteristic systems rather than social-status systems. This isn't inability; it's different processing.

The community term "hierarchy blindness" describes: not detecting the implicit signals that establish hierarchy in any group. Since most hierarchy signals are fast, multi-channel, and implicit, they require the kind of automatic social processing that autistic cognition handles differently.

### 6.2 The Egalitarian Preference

Research shows autistic individuals:
- Find status-seeking illogical
- Prefer egalitarian relationships
- Score higher on Fairness in Moral Foundations measures
- Score lower on Authority/respect for hierarchy
- Resist conformity
- Tend to ignore dominance structures unless they "make sense" (i.e., are competence-based)

This isn't defiance. It's a principled preference for merit-based systems over arbitrary ones.

### 6.3 The Directness Trap

When an autistic person speaks to their boss the same way they speak to a peer -- without adjusting hedging, register, or deference -- the boss reads it as a **status challenge**. The autistic person isn't challenging anyone; they're using one pragmatic register in a context that demands another.

## 7. The Four-Step Navigation Framework

### Step 1: Identify the Hierarchy Type

| If You See... | It's Probably... |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Titles, org chart, uniforms, ranks | Formal/institutional |
| No explicit structure but clear status patterns | Informal/emergent |
| Status shifts when topic changes | Expertise-based |
| Status seems based on likability/connections | Social/popularity |
| Status tied to the situation (host, driver) | Situational |

### Step 2: Detect Your Position (Use the Checklist)

Apply the observable signals from Section 4. Focus on: who hedges more (lower status), who controls topics (higher status), and who defers in disagreement (lower status).

### Step 3: Calibrate Your Pragmatics

| Your Position | Hedging | Politeness | Directness | Topics |
|-------------|---------|-----------|-----------|--------|
| **Lower** | More hedges, more softeners | Negative politeness | Indirect: "I was wondering if..." | Follow, don't lead |
| **Equal** | Standard hedging | Mix positive/negative | Direct with hedging: "I think we should..." | Shared control |
| **Higher** | Can reduce hedging | Can be more direct -- but humility is rewarded | Direct is available: "Let's do X" | Can lead |
| **Unclear** | Default to MORE hedging | Negative politeness | Hedged: safer to over-defer | Follow until calibrated |

### Step 4: Monitor for Shifts

Hierarchies are not static:
- **Topic changes**: Expertise hierarchies rotate
- **Setting changes**: Your boss at work ≠ your boss at a barbecue
- **New people enter**: Status may need renegotiating
- **Conflict**: Dominance dynamics can emerge in previously prestige-based groups

---

## 8. Key Takeaways

1. **Hierarchy modulates EVERYTHING.** Politeness, hedging, register, turn-taking, topic control, questions, and even phatic greetings all shift based on relative status.

2. **Two pathways: dominance and prestige.** Most real hierarchies involve both. The person with the title (dominance) and the person with the influence (prestige) may differ.

3. **Observable signals replace intuition.** Who speaks most? Who hedges less? Who controls topics? Whose interruptions are tolerated? These are detectable without social intuition.

4. **The competence paradox is real.** Demonstrate, don't declare. Being right AND delivering that rightness with appropriate hedging is the skill.

5. **Default to more deference when unsure.** Over-deferring reads as "formal but harmless." Under-deferring reads as "challenging authority." The asymmetry makes over-deferring the safer default.

6. **The egalitarian preference is principled, not defiant.** Autistic resistance to arbitrary hierarchy reflects a genuine moral framework -- one that values merit over position.

7. **Hierarchies shift.** The same person has different status in different contexts and on different topics. Monitor and recalibrate.
