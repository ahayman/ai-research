# Validation: French for Pattern Learners — Decision Trees, Systematic Rules, and the Meta-Pattern

## Validation Summary

The pattern learners document is the capstone synthesis of the deep dive and draws on claims validated in other categories. All major rule systems — gender prediction from endings (~80% accuracy, Lyster 2006), BAGS adjective placement, liaison obligatory/forbidden/optional categories, WEIRDO subjunctive triggers, etre verb list, and clitic pronoun ordering template — were independently confirmed. One date discrepancy was found (the document says "2005" for Lyster's study; publication was 2006). The decision-tree approach to French grammar is well-supported by the underlying linguistic facts.

## Claim-by-Claim Verification

### Claim 1: Gender prediction ~80% accuracy from noun endings — Lyster study
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED with date correction
- **Evidence**: Roy Lyster's corpus analysis examined 9,961 nouns from Le Robert Junior Illustre and found that "81 percent of all feminine nouns and 80 percent of all masculine nouns in the corpus are rule governed, having endings that systematically predict their gender." The study used endings that predict gender of at least 90% of nouns with that ending as "reliable predictors."
- **Date correction**: The document states "A 2005 corpus study by Roy Lyster." The actual publication date is 2006: Lyster, R. (2006). Predictability in French gender attribution: A corpus analysis. Journal of French Language Studies, 16, 69-92. (A conference presentation may have occurred earlier, but the published study is 2006.)
- **Assessment**: The 80% figure is accurately reported. The specific ending predictions listed in the document (e.g., -tion/-sion ~99% feminine, -ment ~95% masculine, -age ~90% masculine, -isme ~100% masculine) are consistent with Lyster's findings and widely cited in French pedagogy sources.

### Claim 2: Specific gender-predicting endings — feminine (-tion, -te, -ure, -ence, -ie, -ette, -ee) and masculine (-ment, -age, -eau, -isme, -eur, -al, -oir)
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: French Together, Polyglottist Language Academy, and Copycatcafe all confirm the ending-based gender prediction rules essentially as stated. The feminine endings (-tion/-sion, -te/-ite, -ure, -ence/-ance, -ie, -ette/-elle) and masculine endings (-ment, -age, -eau, -isme, -eur, -al/-ail/-eil, -oir, -et/-at/-ot) are consistently cited with similar accuracy percentages across sources.
- **Assessment**: The known exceptions are also accurately stated: image/page/plage as feminine -age words; fleur/couleur/peur/douleur/humeur as feminine -eur words; musee as a masculine -ee word. The decision tree algorithm presented is a legitimate and useful pedagogical tool.

### Claim 3: BAGS adjective placement rule — Beauty, Age, Goodness, Size
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Preply, CliffsNotes, Coffee Break Languages, and multiple French learning sources confirm BAGS as a standard mnemonic for prenominal adjective placement. The categories are confirmed: Beauty (beau, joli), Age (jeune, vieux, nouveau, ancien), Goodness (bon, mauvais, meilleur), Size (grand, petit, gros, long, court). Some sources use BANGS (adding Number: autre, meme).
- **Assessment**: The document's description of the deeper pattern (prenominal adjectives tend to be short, high-frequency, and subjective/evaluative) is well-supported. The position-dependent meaning changes (ancien = former vs. ancient, grand = great vs. tall, pauvre = pitiable vs. poor) are standard and confirmed. The special forms (bel, nouvel, vieil before vowel-initial masculine nouns) are confirmed.

### Claim 4: Liaison — three-category system (obligatory, forbidden, optional)
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Humanities LibreTexts, Lawless French, Master Your French, and multiple pronunciation guides confirm the three-category system exactly as described.
  - **Obligatory**: determiner + noun, pronoun + verb, prenominal adjective + noun, after tres/plus/moins/bien/trop, fixed expressions. Confirmed.
  - **Forbidden**: after "et" (never), before aspirate-h words, after singular nouns, before onze and oui. Confirmed.
  - **Optional**: after plural nouns, after verbs (except etre/avoir), after prepositions. Confirmed as register markers (more liaison = more formal).
- **Assessment**: The consonant pronunciation rules (-s -> /z/, -x -> /z/, -t -> /t/, -d -> /t/, -n -> /n/, -f -> /v/) are confirmed. The register insight (optional liaisons function as a formality dial) is well-attested in sociolinguistic literature.

### Claim 5: WEIRDO subjunctive triggers — Wishes, Emotions, Impersonal, Recommendations, Doubt, Opinions
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: French with Agnes, Lawless French, VerbForge, and Fiveable all confirm WEIRDO as a standard mnemonic for subjunctive triggers:
  - W: vouloir que, souhaiter que, desirer que, preferer que
  - E: etre content que, etre triste que, avoir peur que, regretter que
  - I: il faut que, il est possible que, il est important que
  - R: demander que, exiger que, ordonner que, suggerer que
  - D: douter que, ne pas croire que, ne pas penser que
  - O: il est bon que, il est dommage que, c'est etrange que
- **Assessment**: The document accurately presents the WEIRDO system. The two-subject rule (subjunctive requires different subjects; same subject -> infinitive) is confirmed. The exception for esperer (takes indicative in the affirmative despite being a "wish" verb) is a well-known pedagogical point.
- **Note on croire/penser**: The document correctly states that croire que and penser que in the affirmative take indicative, and only in the negative/interrogative do they trigger subjunctive. This is confirmed by multiple grammar sources.

### Claim 6: Etre verb list — DR MRS VANDERTRAMP
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: The French Post, FluentU, LingoPie, FrenchCrazy, and French Circles all confirm the DR MRS VANDERTRAMP mnemonic for the 17 verbs conjugated with etre in the passe compose: Descendre, Rentrer, Monter, Retourner, Sortir, Venir, Arriver, Naitre, Demeurer (or Devenir in some versions), Entrer, Revenir, Tomber, Rester, Aller, Mourir, Passer, Partir.
- **Assessment**: The document mentions the etre verb list in the context of the passe compose/imparfait decision tree but does not provide the full mnemonic. The underlying grammatical principle (intransitive verbs of motion/state change use etre; past participle agrees with subject in gender and number) is correctly described.

### Claim 7: Clitic pronoun ordering template — me/te/se/nous/vous + le/la/les + lui/leur + y + en
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Lawless French and Wikipedia's article on French personal pronouns confirm the exact ordering template: "Subject + (ne) + me/te/se/nous/vous + le/la/l'/les + lui/leur + y + en + verb + (pas)." The maximum two-pronoun-per-verb rule and the column-based selection system are confirmed.
- **Assessment**: The document's template is accurately stated. The imperative exception (affirmative imperatives reverse the order to: verb + le/la/les + moi/toi/lui/nous/vous/leur + y + en) is confirmed by Lawless French. The negative imperative returning to standard preverbal order is also confirmed.

### Claim 8: Group 1 (-er) verbs account for ~90% of all French verbs
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Multiple French conjugation sources confirm that -er verbs are by far the largest group, frequently cited as approximately 90% of all French verbs. French Today states the principle that if you learn to conjugate one verb in each group, you can conjugate hundreds of others.
- **Assessment**: The document's "90/10 principle" is a reasonable pedagogical simplification. The claim that Group 2 (-ir with -issant) accounts for ~300 verbs and Group 3 accounts for ~350 verbs is consistent with standard estimates.

### Claim 9: The conditional = future stem + imparfait endings
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: This is a standard and universally taught formation rule in French pedagogy. The future stem (infinitive for regular verbs, modified stem for irregular verbs) combined with imperfect endings (-ais, -ais, -ait, -ions, -iez, -aient) produces the conditional. The document's insight that "once you know the future and the imparfait, the conditional is free" is pedagogically accurate.

### Claim 10: Passe compose vs. imparfait — aspectual distinction, decision tree
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Lawless French and other grammar sources confirm the aspectual distinction: passe compose for completed, bounded events; imparfait for ongoing, habitual, or background situations. The trigger words listed (imparfait: souvent, toujours, d'habitude; passe compose: hier, soudain, un jour) are standard pedagogical tools. The "movie metaphor" (imparfait = background scenery, passe compose = plot events) is widely used in French teaching.
- **Assessment**: The eight-step decision tree presented in the document is a legitimate and useful systematization of the aspectual distinction. The edge case about states (normally imparfait, but passe compose when a state changes at a specific moment) is correctly identified.
