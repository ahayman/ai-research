# Category 4: Conversation Structure -- Openings, Closings, and Topic Management

## The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

## How to Read This Document

Categories 1-3 covered what sentences **do** (speech acts), what they **imply** (implicature), and **why** they are indirect (politeness/face). This category moves from the sentence level to the **conversation level**: how entire conversations are structured from first word to last.

Conversations are not random. They follow predictable structural patterns -- opening sequences, topic management rules, and closing sequences -- that are as rule-governed as grammar, but rarely taught explicitly. For autistic individuals who may find conversations confusing, exhausting, or difficult to navigate, understanding the **architecture** of conversation can be transformative. It turns "I never know when to start/stop/change the subject" into a system with identifiable parts and predictable sequences.

This document maps the full skeleton of conversation: how they begin, how topics are introduced and managed, and how they end. Each section includes the structural rules, the common scripts, and -- critically -- the specific phrases that signal each transition.

## 1. Conversation Openings

### 1.1 The Core Problem Openings Solve

You cannot simply start talking to someone about what you want to talk about. If you walk up to a colleague and say "The Henderson report has three errors in section four," you will get a strange look -- even if the information is perfectly accurate and relevant. Something is missing. That something is the **opening sequence**.

Openings solve a coordination problem: two people need to mutually establish that they are now **in a conversation together**. This requires checking that the other person is available, willing, and oriented toward interaction. The opening sequence does this work in a structured, predictable way.

### 1.2 The Summons-Answer Sequence: The Prototypical Opening

The most fundamental opening structure in conversation analysis is the **summons-answer sequence**, first described by Emanuel Schegloff (1968). It is the mechanism that transitions two people from "not in a conversation" to "in a conversation."

**Structure:**

| Position | Speaker A (Summoner) | Speaker B (Answerer) |
|----------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Turn 1 | Summons: "Hey!" / "Sarah?" / "Excuse me" / [phone rings] | |
| Turn 2 | | Answer: "Yeah?" / "What?" / "Hi" / "Hello?" |

**What each part does:**

- **The summons** is a bid for attention and interaction. It says: "I want to talk to you. Are you available?"
- **The answer** is an acceptance of that bid. It says: "I am available. Proceed."

**Critical properties of the summons-answer sequence:**

1. **The summons creates an obligation.** If someone says your name or says "Hey," you are socially obligated to respond. Not answering a summons is a noticeable absence -- it will be interpreted as ignoring, hostility, or unavailability.

2. **The answer is not yet the conversation.** When you answer "Yeah?" you have not yet started talking about anything. You have merely opened a channel. The summoner now has the floor to introduce whatever they wanted to say.

3. **The summons cannot be repeated indefinitely without consequences.** If you call someone's name and they don't answer, you can try again. Maybe twice. But a third or fourth repetition signals urgency, frustration, or potential conflict. The social meaning of repetition escalates.

4. **The summons constrains what comes next.** After a summons-answer, the summoner is expected to speak. If they remain silent after getting an answer, this is awkward -- the answerer will prompt: "What?" / "What's up?" / "Did you need something?"

**Examples across contexts:**

| Context | Summons | Answer |
|---------|---------|--------|
| Getting a friend's attention | "Hey, Mark!" | "Yeah?" |
| Getting a stranger's attention | "Excuse me?" | [turns to look] "Yes?" |
| Phone call | [ring ring] | "Hello?" |
| Knocking on office door | [knock knock] | "Come in" / "Yeah?" |
| Calling across a room | "Mom!" | "What?" |
| Getting a waiter's attention | [eye contact + slight hand raise] | [approaches] "What can I get you?" |

### 1.3 Schegloff's Four Core Opening Sequences (Telephone Calls)

Emanuel Schegloff's foundational research on telephone conversation openings (1967, 1968, 1979, 1986) identified four sequential components that participants routinely work through before reaching the main business of the call. These four sequences are the most thoroughly studied opening structure in conversation analysis.

**Sequence 1: Summons-Answer**

```
[phone rings]                    ← Summons
"Hello?"                         ← Answer
```

The phone ring is the summons. Picking up and saying "Hello?" is the answer. In modern mobile communication, the caller's identity is often visible on screen, which changes Sequence 2.

**Sequence 2: Identification-Recognition**

```
"Hi, it's Sarah."              ← Caller identifies self
"Oh, hi Sarah!"                ← Answerer recognizes caller
```

In the landline era, this was critical -- you couldn't know who was calling. With caller ID and mobile phones, this sequence is often compressed or skipped:
- **Caller ID era:** "Hey Sarah!" (answerer already knows who it is)
- **Close relationships:** Voice alone is sufficient -- no explicit identification needed

**Sequence 3: Greeting Exchange**

```
"Hey, how are you?"            ← Greeting (often combined with Sequence 4)
"Good, how are you?"           ← Return greeting
```

This is the mutual acknowledgment of the social relationship. Greetings are **reciprocal**: if one person greets, the other must return the greeting before the conversation can proceed.

**Sequence 4: "How Are You" (Initial Inquiries)**

```
"How's it going?"              ← First "how are you"
"Good, good. You?"             ← Response + reciprocation
"Yeah, not bad."               ← Response
```

This is the phatic communion component (see Section 1.5). In practice, Sequences 3 and 4 are often merged into a single exchange.

**The Anchor Position:**

After completion of these four sequences, Schegloff identified what he called the **anchor position** -- the point where the first topic of the conversation is introduced. Ordinarily, it is the **caller** (or the initiator of the contact) who introduces the first topic at the anchor position. This is because the caller presumably had a reason for calling, and the answerer expects to hear it.

```
"So listen, I was calling because..."   ← Anchor position: first topic introduced
```

### 1.4 Greeting Sequences by Relationship Type

The specific words, tone, and elaboration of greetings vary predictably based on the relationship between speakers. This is one of the most pattern-friendly aspects of openings.

#### Intimate Partners / Very Close Family

**Characteristics:** Maximum informality, terms of endearment, minimal ritual structure.

```
"Hey, babe."                    →  "Hey."
"Hi, love."                     →  "Hey, what's up?"
"Morning."                      →  "Morning."
```

- Greetings may be extremely minimal (single word)
- The "how are you" sequence is often skipped entirely (you already know how they are)
- Topic can be introduced immediately
- Terms of endearment signal the relationship: "babe," "love," "honey," "sweetheart"
- Tone does significant work -- the same word "Hey" can communicate very different things depending on intonation

#### Close Friends

**Characteristics:** Informal, enthusiastic, often includes display of pleasure at contact.

```
"Hey! How's it going?"          →  "Good, man. You?"
"Dude, what's up?"              →  "Not much, what's up with you?"
"Oh my god, hi!"                →  "Hey! I haven't seen you in forever!"
```

- Address terms signal friendship: "dude," "man," first name, nicknames
- Enthusiastic uptake ("Oh my god, hi!") is a token of extra attention that signals the friends are pleased to see/hear from each other
- The "how are you" exchange is brief but genuine -- friends may actually answer honestly
- Shared knowledge allows abbreviated openings: "Hey, so remember that thing?"

#### Acquaintances / Colleagues

**Characteristics:** Moderately formal, use of first names, full "how are you" ritual.

```
"Hi, David. How are you?"       →  "Good, thanks. How about you?"
"Hey, Karen. How's it going?"   →  "Pretty good. You?"
"Morning, Tom."                 →  "Morning."
```

- First names are standard (not titles)
- "How are you" is expected and must be reciprocated
- Responses are formulaic: "Good, thanks" / "Not bad" / "Can't complain"
- Slightly more ritual structure than with close friends
- Workplace context often abbreviates: "Morning" alone may suffice in hallways

#### Strangers

**Characteristics:** Formal opening, attention-getting device, apology for intrusion, reason for contact.

```
"Excuse me..."                  →  [attention] "Yes?"
"Hi there, sorry to bother you" →  "No problem."
"Pardon me..."                  →  [turns] "Yeah?"
```

- Openings with strangers almost always begin with an **attention-getter**: "Excuse me," "Pardon me," "Sorry"
- The attention-getter functions as both summons AND pre-apology for the intrusion
- The full sequence for approaching a stranger is covered in Section 1.8
- There is no "how are you" sequence with strangers
- The interaction moves quickly to the reason for contact

#### Professional / Institutional

**Characteristics:** Formal, title usage, full ritual structure.

```
"Good morning, Dr. Chen."       →  "Good morning."
"Good afternoon, Professor."    →  "Good afternoon. Please, come in."
"Hello, Mr. Williams."          →  "Hello. How can I help you?"
```

- Titles are used: Dr., Professor, Mr./Ms./Mrs., Officer
- Time-of-day greetings: "Good morning/afternoon/evening" (more formal than "Hi")
- "How can I help you?" immediately opens the reason-for-contact slot
- The higher-status person often controls the pace of the opening
- In service encounters, the professional typically provides the opening: "Welcome to [business]. How can I help you?"

### 1.5 The "How Are You" Ritual: Phatic Communion

#### What It Is

The anthropologist Bronisław Malinowski coined the term **phatic communion** in 1923 to describe language used not to communicate information but to **establish and maintain social bonds**. "How are you?" is the quintessential example.

When someone asks "How are you?", they are almost never requesting a medical or psychological status report. They are performing a **social ritual** that says: "I acknowledge you as a person, I recognize our social relationship, and I am willing to engage in interaction."

#### The Rule That Trips People Up

**The expected answer to "How are you?" is NOT an honest report of how you are.**

The expected answer is a brief, positive formula:
- "Good, thanks. You?" ← **Standard response**
- "Fine, thanks."
- "Pretty good."
- "Not bad."
- "Can't complain."
- "Doing well, thanks."

This is deeply counterintuitive for many autistic individuals. The question *sounds* like a request for information. It uses interrogative syntax. It *looks* like it wants an answer. But the social function is ritualistic, not informational.

#### When "How Are You" IS a Real Question

Context determines whether "How are you" is ritual or genuine:

| Context | Type | Expected Response |
|---------|------|-------------------|
| Passing someone in a hallway at work | Ritual | "Good, you?" (while continuing to walk) |
| Meeting a friend for coffee | Mostly ritual | "Good, good" (then transition to real topics) |
| Visiting someone in the hospital | Genuine | An actual report of how they are feeling |
| Knowing someone just went through a breakup | Genuine | An honest answer is welcome |
| Said with slow, concerned tone and eye contact | Genuine | "How are you... really?" invites honest response |
| Said quickly while walking past | Ritual | "Good!" (and keep walking) |

**How to tell the difference:**
- **Ritual "how are you"**: Said quickly, often while walking, with flat or rising intonation, in passing or at the very start of an interaction
- **Genuine "how are you"**: Said slowly, with paused delivery, sustained eye contact, often following known difficult circumstances, sometimes with emphasis: "How ARE you?"

#### What Happens If You Give an Honest Negative Answer

If someone says "How are you?" and you say "Terrible, actually. I haven't slept in three days and I think my cat is dying" -- the other person will be **socially caught**. They now must respond to your genuine distress, which they weren't prepared for. This doesn't mean you can never be honest -- it means you need to **read which version of the question you received**.

With close friends and in genuine-inquiry contexts, honest answers are welcome and expected. In ritual contexts, honest negative answers create an awkward situation that requires the other person to either (a) engage with your problems when they weren't prepared to, or (b) give an inadequate "Oh, that's too bad" that satisfies neither party.

**The compromise strategy**: "I'm okay" or "Hanging in there" -- signals that things aren't perfect without requiring a full discussion. The other person can then choose to probe ("Everything alright?") or accept the signal and move on.

### 1.6 How Topic Is Introduced After the Greeting Sequence

Once the greeting and "how are you" ritual are complete, someone needs to introduce what the conversation will actually be about. This is the **first topic position** -- Schegloff's "anchor position."

#### Who Introduces the Topic

The default rule: **the person who initiated the contact introduces the first topic.** This is because they presumably had a reason for making contact.

- If you called someone → you introduce the topic
- If you walked up to someone → you introduce the topic
- If you texted someone first → you introduce the topic

If the answerer introduces a topic first, it constitutes a kind of preemption -- the caller may feel their reason for calling was overridden.

#### Common First-Topic Introduction Formulas

| Formula | Function | Example |
|---------|----------|---------|
| "So..." | General topic launcher | "So, I wanted to ask you something." |
| "So listen..." | Signals upcoming important information | "So listen, I talked to the landlord." |
| "I was calling because..." | Explicit reason-for-call | "I was calling because the meeting got moved." |
| "Hey, did you hear about...?" | News announcement | "Hey, did you hear about Tom getting promoted?" |
| "I wanted to tell you..." | Announces upcoming information delivery | "I wanted to tell you what happened at work." |
| "Guess what?" | Pre-announcement (builds anticipation) | "Guess what?" → "What?" → "I got the job!" |
| "I have a question..." | Pre-request | "I have a question -- do you know anyone who..." |
| "You'll never believe..." | Dramatic pre-announcement | "You'll never believe what Sarah said to me." |

#### The "Reason for the Call" Slot

In institutional and transactional calls, there is a clearly defined slot for explaining why you are calling. This is expected and should be filled promptly:

```
Receptionist: "Good morning, Dr. Miller's office."
Caller: "Hi, I'm calling to schedule an appointment."

Customer service: "Thank you for calling TechCorp, how can I help you?"
Caller: "Hi, I have a question about my bill."
```

The "reason for the call" slot is particularly important in institutional contexts because the institutional representative needs this information to route you correctly. Not providing it -- saying "Hello" and waiting -- creates an awkward pause where the representative must prompt: "What can I help you with?"

### 1.7 Phone Call Openings vs. Face-to-Face vs. Digital

#### Phone Call Openings

Phone calls use the full Schegloff four-sequence structure (summons → identification → greeting → how-are-you → first topic). The phone ring is the summons. Because the caller chose to call, the caller is expected to have a reason, and the first topic position belongs to the caller.

**Modern mobile era changes:**
- Caller ID eliminates the identification sequence ("Hey Sarah!" replaces "Hello? Who is this?")
- Texting before calling has become a social norm: "Hey, can I call you?" functions as a pre-summons
- Unexpected phone calls are increasingly marked -- many people interpret an unannounced call as signaling urgency

#### Face-to-Face Openings

In face-to-face interaction, the summons may be verbal ("Hey") or non-verbal (eye contact, wave, approaching). Key differences from phone:

- **Mutual availability is visible.** You can see whether someone is busy, engaged in another conversation, wearing headphones, etc. This visual information replaces some of the work that the summons-answer sequence does on the phone.
- **Greetings often include embodied displays.** Hugs, handshakes, waves, nods -- the body contributes to the opening in ways that phone calls cannot.
- **Walk-by greetings exist.** In face-to-face, you can have a complete opening AND closing in a single exchange while walking past someone: "Hey!" → "Hey!" This is a greeting that is also a complete conversation. No topic is introduced because none is needed.

#### Text Message / Chat Openings

Text-based communication has developed its own opening norms that differ significantly from both phone and face-to-face:

**The "Don't Just Say Hey" Norm:**

A widely recognized norm in text and instant messaging is: do not send a message that only says "hey" or "hi" and then wait for a response. This is considered inefficient and anxiety-inducing because:
- The recipient gets a notification with zero information
- They don't know if you need something urgent, want to chat, or have bad news
- They now have to stop what they're doing to respond to a content-free message
- The actual content won't arrive until after they've already been interrupted

**Better approach:** Combine the greeting and the reason in one message:
```
BAD:  "Hey"
      [waiting]
      "Hey"
      [waiting]
      "So I was wondering if you could help me with something"

GOOD: "Hey! Quick question -- do you know what time the meeting is tomorrow?"
```

**Text openings by relationship:**

| Relationship | Opening Style | Example |
|-------------|---------------|---------|
| Close friend | Minimal greeting + content | "dude check this out [link]" |
| Friend | Brief greeting + reason | "Hey! Are you free Saturday?" |
| Colleague | Greeting + context + request | "Hi Sarah -- quick question about the Q3 report. Do you have the latest numbers?" |
| Professional contact | Formal greeting + self-ID + reason | "Hi Dr. Chen, this is Alex from the marketing team. I wanted to ask about..." |
| Stranger (cold outreach) | Self-ID + reason + softening | "Hi, I'm Alex -- I found your work through [source] and wondered if..." |

#### Email Openings

Email has the most codified opening conventions, existing on a clear formality spectrum:

**Salutations (from most formal to most casual):**

| Formality | Salutation | When to Use |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Very formal | "Dear Dr./Professor [Last Name]," | First contact with senior professionals, academic contexts |
| Formal | "Dear Mr./Ms. [Last Name]," | Business correspondence with unknown contacts |
| Standard professional | "Hello [First Name]," | Most workplace emails to known contacts |
| Friendly professional | "Hi [First Name]," | Colleagues, established professional relationships |
| Casual | "Hey [First Name]," | Close colleagues, informal workplace culture |
| Very casual | "Hey!" / first name only | Friends, very informal contexts |

**Rule of thumb:** Match or slightly exceed the formality of what you receive. If someone writes "Hi Alex," respond with "Hi Sarah" -- not "Dear Ms. Johnson."

#### Slack / Teams / Workplace Chat Openings

Workplace messaging platforms have developed distinct norms:

- **Don't just say "hi"** -- include your reason in the same message
- **Front-load context:** "Hi Annie! I'm a new product marketer. I heard from Heather that we'll be working closely together. Could I put some time on your calendar for next week?"
- **One message, not five:** Get your full point into a single message so the recipient gets one notification, not five
- Slightly more informal than email, but more professional than personal text
- Emoji are acceptable but context-dependent: 👋 as a greeting opener is common
- Thread replies keep conversations organized and reduce notification noise

### 1.8 How to Open a Conversation with a Stranger

Approaching a stranger to initiate conversation is one of the most anxiety-producing pragmatic tasks. There is a specific sequence that makes this work smoothly:

#### The Full Stranger-Approach Sequence

**Step 1: Attention-getter**

Signal that you want to interact. This is the summons.
```
"Excuse me..."
"Pardon me..."
"Sorry..."
"Hi there..."
```

**Step 2: Apology for intrusion (often combined with Step 1)**

Acknowledge that you are imposing on their autonomy (their negative face -- see Category 3).
```
"Sorry to bother you..."
"I don't mean to interrupt..."
"Sorry to trouble you, but..."
```

**Step 3: Reason for contact**

Explain why you are approaching. This legitimizes the interaction.
```
"...do you happen to know where the library is?"
"...I was wondering if you could help me with something."
"...I noticed you dropped this."
"...do you know if this bus goes to downtown?"
```

**Step 4: The request/question itself**

If the reason and request aren't combined in Step 3, the specific request follows.

**Full example sequences:**

```
"Excuse me, sorry to bother you -- do you know where the nearest ATM is?"

"Hi, I'm sorry to interrupt -- I'm looking for Building C. Do you happen to know which way it is?"

"Pardon me -- is this seat taken?"

"Hey, sorry -- could I ask you a quick question? Do you know what time this place closes?"
```

**What makes this work:**

- The apology component ("sorry to bother you") explicitly acknowledges the imposition -- this protects the stranger's negative face
- The reason ("I'm looking for...") provides a legitimate basis for the interaction -- without it, the approach seems random and potentially threatening
- Hedging language ("do you happen to," "could I possibly," "I was wondering if") further softens the imposition
- The whole sequence gives the stranger a clear framework: they know what you want, why you want it, and that you recognize you're imposing

#### Context-Specific Stranger Openings

| Context | Opening | Why This Works |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| Asking for directions | "Excuse me, do you happen to know..." | "Happen to" acknowledges they may not know, reducing imposition |
| Service encounter | "Hi, I'd like to..." | Direct because the context already provides the reason (they're there to serve) |
| Social event (party) | "Hi, I'm [name]. How do you know [host]?" | Self-identification + connection to shared context |
| Networking event | "Hi, I'm [name] from [company]. What brings you here?" | Self-ID + open question inviting them to share |
| Waiting room / queue | "Have you been waiting long?" | Shared situation provides the reason for contact |
| Comment on shared environment | "Is it always this busy here?" | Shared experience as opening gambit |

## 2. Conversation Closings

### 2.1 The Closing Problem

Why can't you just stop talking? Why can't you say what you need to say and then walk away?

The answer reveals a deep structural feature of conversation: **conversation is a joint activity that requires mutual coordination to start AND to end.** Just as you need a summons-answer sequence to open, you need a closing sequence to close. Unilateral termination -- simply stopping -- is a hostile or bizarre act.

Schegloff and Sacks (1973), in their landmark paper "Opening Up Closings," identified the fundamental **closing problem**: any turn in a conversation could potentially be followed by another turn. The turn-taking system (Sacks, Schegloff, & Jefferson, 1974) distributes turns continuously -- after every turn, someone can speak next. This means there is no natural endpoint built into the system. You have to **create** the endpoint through cooperative work.

The closing problem has two components:

1. **You can't just stop.** If you stop talking, the other person will think it's their turn. If they stop too, there's an awkward silence that suggests something is wrong.

2. **You can't close unilaterally.** Even "Goodbye" doesn't work alone. If you say "Goodbye" and the other person hasn't been part of the closing process, your "Goodbye" feels abrupt, rude, or angry.

The solution is the **pre-closing sequence**: a collaborative mechanism that both parties use to negotiate the end of the conversation before it actually ends.

### 2.2 Schegloff and Sacks' Model of Conversation Closing

Schegloff and Sacks (1973) described the two essential components of closing:

#### Component 1: The Pre-Closing

The pre-closing is an adjacency pair in which both parties signal that they have nothing more to say. It works like this:

**Turn 1 (Pre-closing initiation):** One speaker produces a token that:
- Is NOT a new topic
- Is NOT a topically coherent continuation
- IS a signal that they are ready to close

Common pre-closing tokens:
```
"Well..."
"So..."
"Anyway..."
"Okay..."
"Alright..."
"Right..."
```

**Turn 2 (Pre-closing acceptance):** The other speaker produces a matching token:
```
"Okay."
"Yeah."
"Alright."
"Mm-hm."
```

**What this pair does:** It creates a moment where both parties are "passing turns" -- neither is introducing new talk. This mutual passing is the signal that both parties are ready to close.

**Example:**
```
A: "Well..."                     ← Pre-closing initiation
B: "Yeah, okay."                 ← Pre-closing acceptance
[Both have now agreed that closing can proceed]
```

#### Component 2: The Terminal Exchange

Once the pre-closing is accepted, the **terminal exchange** is the final adjacency pair:

```
A: "Bye."                       ← Terminal first pair part
B: "Bye."                       ← Terminal second pair part
[Conversation ends]
```

Or more elaborated:
```
A: "Talk to you later."
B: "Yeah, see ya."
A: "Bye."
B: "Bye."
```

#### What Happens Between: The Leave-Taking Section

Between the pre-closing and the terminal exchange, various elements can appear:

- **Arrangement-making:** "We should do this again" / "Let's get lunch sometime"
- **Appreciations:** "Thanks for calling" / "This was really helpful"
- **Well-wishes:** "Have a good one" / "Enjoy your weekend"
- **Back-references:** "Oh, and tell Sarah I said hi"
- **Summary/recap:** "Okay, so I'll send you that file tomorrow"

These elements are part of the closing section -- they occur within the trajectory toward ending, not as new conversation.

#### Movements Out of Closings (Reopening)

A critical feature of the pre-closing: **either party can reject it.** If, after one person says "Well..." the other person says "Oh, before I forget..." -- the conversation has been **reopened**. The pre-closing attempt failed because the other party had more to say.

This can happen multiple times. There may be several pre-closing attempts before both speakers genuinely have nothing left to add:

```
A: "Well, I should let you go."       ← Pre-closing attempt 1
B: "Oh wait, one more thing --"       ← Reopening
   [new topic discussed]
A: "Okay, well anyway..."             ← Pre-closing attempt 2
B: "Okay, yeah."                      ← Pre-closing accepted
A: "Talk soon."                       ← Terminal exchange begins
B: "Bye."
A: "Bye."
```

### 2.3 The Pre-Closing Sequence in Detail

Pre-closing signals are among the most important conversational signals to recognize. They announce "I'm getting ready to end this conversation."

#### Verbal Pre-Closing Signals

| Signal | Example | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| "Well..." | "Well, it's been great talking to you." | The most common pre-closing token |
| "So..." | "So, I guess I should get going." | Signals transition to closing |
| "Anyway..." | "Anyway, I won't keep you." | Marks departure from current topic toward closing |
| "Alright..." | "Alright, I'll let you get back to it." | |
| "I should probably..." | "I should probably get going." | Provides a reason for closing |
| "I'd better..." | "I'd better let you go." | The polite fiction (see below) |
| "I'll let you go" | "Well, I'll let you go." | Actually means "I want to go" (see Section 2.8) |
| "I don't want to keep you" | "I don't want to keep you any longer." | Face-saving attribution |
| "I've got to run" | "Well, I've got to run." | External obligation as exit reason |

#### Non-Verbal Pre-Closing Signals (Face-to-Face)

- Shifting weight from one foot to another
- Gathering belongings (picking up bag, putting on coat)
- Looking at watch or phone
- Angling body toward exit/door
- Breaking sustained eye contact and looking around
- Slapping knees or thighs ("knee-slap" pre-closing)
- Standing up (if seated)
- Producing a big exhale or sigh

#### "Passing Turns": The Winding-Down Signal

Before the pre-closing proper, conversations often exhibit a **winding-down phase** where both parties produce increasingly minimal responses:

```
A: "Yeah, so that's basically it."
B: "Mm-hm."
A: "Yeah."
B: "Yeah."
[silence]
A: "Well..."                          ← Now the pre-closing begins
```

Those "Yeah" / "Mm-hm" turns are called **passing turns** -- the speakers are passing up their opportunity to introduce new material. When both parties are passing, the conversation is drifting toward closure. This mutual minimal responding is a collaborative signal: "I don't have anything new to add. Do you?"

### 2.4 Arrangement-Making as Pre-Closing

One of the most common elements in the closing section is making arrangements or affirming the relationship for the future. This serves a dual function:

1. **Practical function:** Actually making plans
2. **Social function:** Signaling that the relationship will continue -- "This conversation is ending, but our relationship is not"

**Common formulas:**

| Formula | Genuineness | Notes |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| "We should do this again." | Often genuine | Signals enjoyment of the interaction |
| "Let's get lunch sometime." | May be vague/non-committal | Often a polite closing formula rather than a real plan |
| "I'll text you about Saturday." | Genuine | Specific = more likely to be a real plan |
| "We should catch up soon." | Semi-genuine | Expresses desire without commitment |
| "Let me know how it goes." | Genuine interest | Signals continued engagement with the topic |
| "I'll send you that link." | Usually genuine | Specific future action |

**How to tell if "Let's get lunch sometime" is real or ritual:**
- If it includes a specific time or action ("How about Tuesday?"), it's real
- If it's vague and produced in the closing section with no follow-up, it's often ritual
- If the other person responds with specifics ("Yeah, how about next week?"), they're treating it as real
- If they respond with "Yeah, definitely!" and nothing more, they may be treating it as ritual

### 2.5 The Terminal Exchange

The terminal exchange is the final adjacency pair that actually ends the conversation. It consists of goodbye tokens:

**Common terminal exchanges:**

| First Part | Second Part | Register |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| "Bye." | "Bye." | Neutral |
| "Bye-bye." | "Bye." | Slightly warm/casual |
| "See you later." | "See ya." | Casual/friendly |
| "Talk to you later." | "Yeah, talk soon." | Phone closing |
| "Take care." | "You too." | Warm/genuine |
| "Have a good one." | "You too." | Casual/American |
| "Night." | "Night." | End of evening interaction |
| "Peace." | "Peace." | Very casual/youth |

**Note:** The terminal exchange is typically brief. Unlike openings (which can be elaborate), closings tend toward efficiency. The work of ending has already been done in the pre-closing; the terminal exchange just executes it.

### 2.6 Formulaic Closing Expressions by Context

#### Phone Calls

```

"I should probably get going."
"I don't want to keep you."

"Talk to you later."  →  "Bye."
```

#### Email

Email closings have their own formality spectrum:

| Formality | Sign-Off | When to Use |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| Very formal | "Yours sincerely," / "Respectfully," | Paired with "Dear [Title] [Last Name]" |
| Formal | "Kind regards," / "Best regards," | Standard professional |
| Standard | "Best," / "Regards," | Most workplace emails |

#### Slack / Teams / Chat

```

```

Digital chat closings are often minimal -- a thumbs-up emoji or a "thanks!" can serve as the entire closing sequence. The asynchronous nature of chat means closing is less fraught: the conversation simply stops receiving new messages.

### 2.7 How to End a Conversation You Don't Want to Be In

Sometimes you need to exit a conversation that isn't naturally winding down. Here are socially acceptable strategies, arranged from gentlest to most direct:

#### Strategy 1: Introduce a Pre-Closing Reason

Provide an external reason for needing to leave:
```
"Oh, I just realized I have to make a call."
"I should get back to work."
"I've got to pick up my kids soon."
"I need to grab some food before the break is over."
```

**Critical rule:** If you state a reason, you must follow through. If you say "I need to go to the restroom" and then walk to the bar instead, the other person will notice and feel deceived.

#### Strategy 2: The Bookend Technique

Set time expectations at the beginning:
```
"I only have about five minutes, but I wanted to say hi."
"I can chat for a sec, but I've got a meeting at 2."
```

This gives you a legitimate exit that was established before the conversation started.

#### Strategy 3: The Introduction Pivot

Redirect the person to someone else:
```
"Oh, have you met Sarah? You two would really get along. Sarah, this is..."
```

This transitions the conversation rather than terminating it -- the other person doesn't feel abandoned because they have a new conversation partner.

#### Strategy 4: The Summary Close

Summarize what they've said as a way to signal completion:
```
"That sounds like it was quite an experience. Well, it was great hearing about it."
```

The summary functions as a topic closer, and the appreciation ("great hearing about it") signals the pre-closing.

#### Strategy 5: Direct but Polite

```
"I'm sorry, I need to step away."
"I don't mean to cut this short, but I've got to run."
"Forgive me -- I promised I'd find [person] before they leave."
```

The apology component ("I'm sorry," "Forgive me") mitigates the face threat of cutting someone off.

### 2.8 How to End a Conversation the Other Person Doesn't Want to End

This is one of the most difficult pragmatic tasks. The other person is not accepting your pre-closing attempts -- they keep reopening the conversation. Strategies:

#### Name-and-Redirect

Research shows that saying someone's name gets their attention and signals a shift:
```
"David -- this has been really great, but I really do have to go."
```

Using their name first is particularly effective because the brain responds to hearing one's own name, causing a natural pause.

#### Summarize Their Point

Reflect back what they've said to show you listened, then close:
```
"So it sounds like the trip was amazing despite the weather. I'm glad you had a good time. I've really got to run though -- let's catch up more later."
```

This validates their contribution while firmly closing.

#### Escalating Directness

If gentle methods fail:
1. First attempt: "Well, I should get going..."
2. Second attempt: "I really do need to head out."
3. Third attempt: "I'm sorry, but I have to go now." + physical movement toward exit
4. Final: "It was great talking to you. Bye!" + actually leave

**The physical component matters.** Gathering your things, standing up, taking a step backward, putting your hand on the door handle -- these non-verbal signals reinforce the verbal closing.

#### Body Language Signals

Before escalating verbally, you can try non-verbal closing signals:
- Turn your shoulders 90 degrees (no longer facing them straight on)
- Angle your feet toward the exit
- Shift your weight to signal readiness to move
- Look toward where you need to go
- Begin gathering belongings

### 2.9 The Polite Fiction of "I'll Let You Go"

The phrase "I'll let you go" deserves special attention because it perfectly illustrates pragmatic meaning versus literal meaning.

**Literal meaning:** "I recognize that you are busy and I am keeping you. I will stop keeping you."

**Pragmatic meaning:** "I want to end this conversation."

The speaker frames their desire to leave as a *favor* to the other person -- as if the other person is the one who needs to go. This is a face-saving device: instead of saying "I want to stop talking to you" (which threatens positive face), you say "You probably have things to do" (which actually compliments their busy, important life).

Everyone knows this is a polite fiction. But the fiction is useful: it allows both parties to close the conversation without either one appearing to reject the other.

**Variants:**
- "I'll let you go" → "I want to go"
- "I don't want to keep you" → "I want to stop talking"
- "You must be busy" → "I'm busy"
- "I should let you get back to it" → "I want to get back to it"

## 3. Topic Management

### 3.1 How Topics Are Introduced

Topics don't appear from nowhere. They are **proffered** -- actively introduced through specific conversational devices. Understanding these devices helps you both recognize when someone is introducing a topic AND introduce your own topics smoothly.

#### Topic-Proffering Devices

**1. News Announcements**

The speaker announces that they have news to share:
```
"Guess what?"  →  "What?"  →  [news delivered]
"You'll never believe what happened."
"Did you hear about [event]?"
"So get this -- "
"I have some news."
"Oh my god, you guys."
```

"Guess what?" is a **pre-announcement** -- it's a preliminary that secures the listener's attention and creates a slot for the news. "What?" is the go-ahead that grants the floor. This is a pre-sequence: it comes before the main sequence (the actual telling of the news).

**2. Topic Initial Elicitors**

The speaker invites the other person to introduce a topic:
```
"What's new?"
"What have you been up to?"
"So, anything exciting happening?"
"What's going on with you?"
"Tell me everything."
```

These are invitations for the other person to proffer a topic. They are common after greetings, when neither party has a specific agenda, or when one person wants to be a listener.

**3. Pre-Topical Sequences**

The speaker signals that a topic is coming without delivering it yet:
```
"Can I ask you something?"
"I've been meaning to talk to you about something."
"There's something I wanted to bring up."
"Do you have a second? I wanted to run something by you."
```

These pre-topicals serve several functions: they check that the listener is available, they signal the general type of upcoming talk (serious, casual, a favor), and they prepare the listener for what's coming.

**4. Setting Talk / Scene-Setting**

The speaker sets the scene before introducing the topic:
```
"So I was at the grocery store yesterday, right? And..."
"You know how I've been looking for a new job?"
"Remember that guy I told you about from the party?"
```

This provides the listener with the context they need to understand the topic that's coming.

**5. Disjunctive Topic Introduction**

When a topic has no connection to prior talk, the speaker explicitly marks the shift:
```
"Totally random, but..."
"Completely different subject --"
"This has nothing to do with anything, but..."
"Oh, I just thought of something."
"Before I forget --"
```

The disjunction marker ("totally random") warns the listener that what's coming is unrelated, preventing confusion about how it connects to what was said before.

### 3.2 How Topics Are Maintained

Once a topic is introduced, contributions must be **relevantly connected** to the prior talk. This is topic maintenance, and it has rules:

#### The Relevance Requirement

Each new turn should connect to what came before. The connection can be:
- **Direct continuation:** "And then what happened?"
- **Related comment:** "That reminds me of when..."
- **Assessment:** "That's crazy" / "Wow, really?"
- **Elaboration request:** "Wait, what do you mean by...?"
- **Related personal experience:** "Something similar happened to me..."

#### What Counts as "On Topic"

"On topic" is more flexible than it might seem. You don't have to stick to the exact narrow subject -- you need to maintain a recognizable connection. If someone is talking about their vacation to Italy, you can:

✅ Ask about the food (connected to Italy)
✅ Share your own Italy experience (connected to Italy)
✅ Ask about travel logistics (connected to vacation)
✅ Comment on Italian culture (connected to Italy)
✅ Mention that you've been wanting to travel (connected to vacation concept)

❌ Suddenly ask about their car's oil change (no recognizable connection)

#### Topic Maintenance Signals

Speakers and listeners use signals to maintain topic flow:

| Signal | Function | Example |
|--------|----------|---------|
| Backchannels | "I'm listening, continue" | "Uh-huh" / "Mm-hm" / "Yeah" / "Right" |
| Assessments | "I have a reaction to this" | "Wow" / "That's great" / "No way" |
| Continuers | "Keep going" | "And then?" / "So what happened?" |
| Clarification requests | "I want to understand this better" | "Wait, who said that?" / "What do you mean?" |
| Collaborative completions | "I'm so engaged I can finish your thought" | A: "And he was just --" B: "Standing there?" A: "Exactly!" |

#### The Autistic Experience of Topic Maintenance

Research consistently identifies topic management as an area where autistic and neurotypical communication styles may differ. Some patterns identified in the literature:

- Autistic speakers may introduce topics with less "warm-up" -- getting to the point more quickly and directly
- Responses may be briefer or may not include the expected reciprocal elaboration
- Topic shifts may occur more abruptly, without the gradual transition devices that neurotypical speakers expect
- Deep engagement with a topic may result in extended development of a single subject beyond what neurotypical listeners expect
- The relevance of a topic connection may be clear to the autistic speaker but not transparent to the neurotypical listener (associative links that are internally logical but externally opaque)

These are differences in conversational style, not deficits. However, understanding the neurotypical expectations helps in navigating conversations where those expectations apply.

### 3.3 How Topics Shift

Topic shift is how conversations move from one subject to another. There are three main types:

#### Type 1: Gradual Topic Drift

The most natural and common form. Topics evolve incrementally through associative links:

```
Talking about → weekend plans
            → the restaurant they'll go to
            → food preferences
            → cooking at home
            → kitchen renovations
            → home improvement costs
            → real estate market
```

Each step connects to the previous one. No single transition is jarring, but after several steps, the conversation is far from where it started. This is **stepwise topic transition** -- and it's considered the most "natural" way topics change. No markers or announcements are needed because each step is recognizably connected to the last.

**Key insight:** In gradual topic drift, it's normal to end up far from where you started. No one considers this a problem. If someone said "Wait, how did we get from weekend plans to the real estate market?" -- it would be a humorous observation, not a genuine complaint.

#### Type 2: Marked Topic Shift

When a new topic is somewhat connected to the current one but the connection needs to be made explicit:

```
"Speaking of which..."
"That reminds me..."
"On a related note..."
"Oh, that makes me think of..."
"You know what else?"
"While we're on the subject..."
```

These markers **acknowledge that a shift is happening** while claiming a connection to the prior talk. "That reminds me" says "I'm changing the topic, but the new topic was triggered by the old one."

#### Type 3: Disjunctive Topic Shift

When the new topic has NO connection to the current one, the speaker must mark the shift explicitly:

```
"Totally different subject, but..."
"Changing the subject completely..."
"This has nothing to do with what we were talking about, but..."
"Random question --"
"Oh, by the way..."
"Before I forget..."
"I keep meaning to ask you..."
```

The disjunction marker serves several functions:
1. **Warns** the listener that what's coming is unrelated
2. **Apologizes** (implicitly) for the non-sequitur
3. **Prevents confusion** about how the new topic connects to the old one
4. **Requests permission** to shift (the listener can accept or defer: "Hold on, let me finish this first")

**Without the marker, disjunctive shifts are jarring.** If someone is talking about their vacation and you suddenly say "Do you have a screwdriver I could borrow?" -- without any transition marker -- the other person will be confused and potentially offended (it signals you weren't listening).

**With the marker:** "Oh, totally unrelated -- do you have a screwdriver I could borrow?" This works fine because the marker explains the non-sequitur.

### 3.4 Topic Closure Signals

Just as conversations need closing sequences, individual topics within a conversation need closure signals. These tell the listener "I'm done with this subject":

| Signal Type | Examples |
|------------|---------|
| Summary statements | "So basically, the whole thing was a mess." |
| Assessments | "It was pretty wild." / "What a day." |
| Upshot formulations | "So the point is, we need to be more careful." |
| Generalization | "That's just how things go sometimes." |
| Silence / pause | [a beat of silence after a story concludes] |
| Laughter | Laughter can mark the end of a humorous topic |
| "Anyway..." | "Anyway, that's the story." / "Anyway." |
| Diminishing contributions | Both speakers producing shorter and shorter turns |

After a topic closure signal, the next speaker has two options:
1. **Accept the closure** and introduce a new topic (or allow a silence)
2. **Reopen the topic** by asking a follow-up question or adding new information

### 3.5 Who Gets to Introduce Topics

Topic introduction is not equally distributed. It reflects and reinforces **social hierarchy and power**:

#### In Casual Conversation (Symmetrical)
- Both parties can introduce topics freely
- There's an informal norm of reciprocity: "I talked about my thing, now you talk about yours"
- Dominating topic introduction (only ever talking about your subjects) is perceived as selfish

#### In Institutional Settings (Asymmetrical)
- **Doctor-patient:** The doctor typically controls topic introduction. "What brings you in today?" opens the floor, but the doctor controls subsequent topic shifts (from symptoms → medical history → examination → diagnosis → treatment plan). The patient can introduce sub-topics but rarely controls the overall structure.
- **Teacher-student:** The teacher introduces topics, controls topic shifts, and decides when a topic is closed. Students can raise hands to introduce sub-topics but generally follow the teacher's agenda.
- **Job interview:** The interviewer introduces topics ("Tell me about your experience with..."). The candidate responds within the topic but doesn't typically introduce new topics unprompted.
- **Boss-subordinate:** In meetings, the higher-status person often controls the agenda and topic shifts. Lower-status participants may need to use pre-topicals: "Can I bring something up?" / "I wanted to mention..."

#### The Power of Topic Control

Controlling topics = controlling the conversation. This is why topic management is linked to power dynamics. When you can decide what gets talked about and what doesn't, you shape the interaction. Recognizing who has topic control in any given interaction helps you navigate it appropriately.

### 3.6 Topic Sensitivity: What's Appropriate Where

Not all topics are appropriate in all contexts. This is one of the areas where the rules are most culturally specific and context-dependent.

#### "Safe" Topics (Almost Always Appropriate)

These topics can be introduced in virtually any casual or professional setting without risk:

| Topic | Why It's Safe | Example Opener |
|-------|--------------|----------------|
| Weather | Universal, neutral, non-controversial | "Can you believe this rain?" |
| Weekend plans | Shows friendly interest, low stakes | "Got any plans for the weekend?" |
| Sports (major events) | Shared cultural event, opinion-safe | "Did you catch the game last night?" |
| Traffic/commute | Shared experience, neutral complaint | "How was the drive in?" |
| Food | Universal, pleasurable, non-threatening | "Have you tried that new place on Main?" |
| Shared environment | Relevant to both parties | "Is it always this busy here?" |
| Entertainment | Low stakes, invites sharing | "Watching anything good lately?" |
| Vacations/travel | Positive, invites storytelling | "Been anywhere fun recently?" |

#### Taboo or Sensitive Topics (Context-Dependent)

In American English culture, the following topics require careful judgment about when they're appropriate:

| Topic | Risk Level | When It Might Be Okay |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| Personal finances / salary | High | Very close friends, financial advisor, negotiation context |
| Politics | High | If you know the other person's views align, political events, close friends |
| Religion | High | With co-religionists, philosophical discussions, close friends |
| Health problems (detailed) | Medium | With close friends/family, medical professionals, support groups |
| Relationship problems | Medium | Close friends, therapists |
| Weight / physical appearance | High | Almost never appropriate to initiate |
| Age | Medium-High | Context-dependent; rude to ask women in many American contexts |
| Pregnancy | High | Only if the person has announced it themselves |
| Death / dying | Medium | If the person brings it up, or in contexts where it's expected |
| Sex / sexual topics | High | Intimate relationships, close same-gender friends, medical contexts |
| Race / ethnicity | High | Academic discussions, close cross-racial friendships, social justice contexts |
| Mental health | Medium | If the person discloses first, therapeutic contexts, close friends |

**The core principle:** When unsure, don't initiate the topic. If the other person introduces it, you can engage. Following someone else's lead is almost always safer than introducing a sensitive topic yourself.

**Consequences of violating topic norms:** Research shows that when social norms regarding appropriate topics are not followed, people are less satisfied with the interaction and may evaluate the norm-breaker more negatively.

### 3.7 The "Safe Topics" Inventory: A Quick Reference

For situations where you need to make conversation and aren't sure what to discuss, here is a ranked inventory:

**Tier 1: Always safe**
- Weather
- The immediate shared environment ("This place is nice")
- Weekend plans / recent weekend activities
- Food and restaurants

**Tier 2: Usually safe**
- Entertainment (movies, TV, books, music)
- Sports (if you know the person is a fan)
- Travel
- Pets
- Local events and happenings
- Holidays coming up

**Tier 3: Safe with acquaintances**
- Work (general -- not complaints or politics)
- Hobbies and interests
- Current non-controversial news
- Family (general and positive: "How are the kids?")

**Tier 4: Friends only**
- Relationship updates
- Work complaints
- Health concerns
- Life frustrations
- Personal opinions on politics/religion

**Tier 5: Close friends / appropriate context only**
- Finances
- Deeply personal struggles
- Controversial opinions
- Gossip

## 4. Everyday Conversation Scripts

This section maps the full structural skeleton of common conversation types. These are not scripts to memorize word-for-word -- they are **structural templates** that show the expected sequence of components.

### 4.1 A Phone Call to a Friend

```
PHASE 1: OPENING
[ring ring]                                    ← Summons
"Hey!"                                         ← Answer + greeting
"Hey! How's it going?"                         ← Greeting + how-are-you
"Good, good. You?"                             ← Response + reciprocation
"Yeah, not bad."                               ← Response

PHASE 2: FIRST TOPIC (REASON FOR CALL)
"So listen, I wanted to ask you something."    ← Anchor position: topic introduced
"Yeah, what's up?"                             ← Go-ahead

PHASE 3: BODY
[The actual conversation about the topic]
[May include topic shifts, stories, questions]
[Duration: anywhere from 1 minute to hours]

PHASE 4: PRE-CLOSING
"Well, anyway, I should let you go."           ← Pre-closing initiation
"Yeah, okay."                                  ← Pre-closing acceptance

PHASE 5: CLOSING ELEMENTS
"Thanks for the info."                         ← Appreciation
"No problem."                                  
"We should hang out this weekend."             ← Arrangement
"Yeah, let's do that."

PHASE 6: TERMINAL EXCHANGE
"Alright, talk to you later."                  ← Terminal first pair part
"Bye."                                         ← Terminal second pair part
"Bye."                                         ← (Optional third)
```

### 4.2 A Coffee Shop Encounter with an Acquaintance

This is a **chance encounter** -- neither person planned this interaction.

```
PHASE 1: RECOGNITION + GREETING
"Oh, hey! David!"                              ← Recognition + greeting
"Hey! Karen! How are you?"                     ← Return greeting + how-are-you
"Good! It's been forever!"                     ← Response + time-marking

PHASE 2: CATCH-UP (MUTUAL TOPIC ELICITATION)
"What have you been up to?"                    ← Topic elicitor
"Oh, same old. Working a lot. You?"            ← Brief update + reciprocation
"Yeah, pretty busy too. We just moved, so."    ← Brief update

PHASE 3: BODY (BRIEF)
[Short exchange about the move / work / shared acquaintances]
[This is typically brief -- 2-5 minutes for an unplanned encounter]
[Neither party expected this conversation, so neither has a prepared topic]

PHASE 4: PRE-CLOSING
"Well, it's great seeing you!"                 ← Pre-closing + appreciation
"Yeah, you too!"                               ← Reciprocation

PHASE 5: ARRANGEMENT (POSSIBLY RITUAL)
"We should grab coffee for real sometime."     ← Arrangement (may be ritual)
"Yeah, definitely! Text me."                   ← Acceptance

PHASE 6: TERMINAL EXCHANGE
"Take care!"                                   ← Terminal exchange
"You too. Bye!"                                ← Terminal exchange
```

**Key feature:** Chance encounters are expected to be brief. Both parties understand that neither anticipated this conversation. The "We should grab coffee" line may be genuine or may be ritual closing -- the ambiguity is socially useful because it allows both interpretations.

### 4.3 A Workplace Hallway Chat

This is the briefest common conversation type -- often under 60 seconds.

```
PHASE 1: GREETING (WALK-BY OR STOP)
"Morning, Paul."                               ← Greeting (may not stop walking)
"Hey, morning."                                ← Return greeting

IF WALKING PAST (COMPLETE):
[That's it. The exchange is complete. Both continue walking.]

IF STOPPING BRIEFLY:
"How's it going?"                              ← How-are-you
"Good, good. Busy week."                       ← Brief response
"Tell me about it."                            ← Empathetic acknowledgment

PHASE 2: BRIEF TOPIC (OPTIONAL)
"Hey, did you see the email about the meeting?" ← Brief topic
"Yeah, I'll be there."                          ← Brief response

PHASE 3: CLOSING (VERY QUICK)
"Cool. See you in there."                      ← Pre-closing + terminal in one
"See ya."                                      ← Terminal
```

**Key feature:** Hallway chats have compressed or absent pre-closing sequences. Because the interaction is already expected to be brief, a single turn can combine pre-closing and terminal: "Alright, catch you later." The physical context (both people are walking somewhere) provides the exit reason automatically.

### 4.4 A First Meeting at a Party or Social Event

```
PHASE 1: APPROACH + OPENING
"Hi, I'm Alex."                                ← Self-identification
"Hey, I'm Jordan. Nice to meet you."           ← Return identification + greeting
"Nice to meet you too."                        ← Reciprocation

PHASE 2: ESTABLISHING CONNECTION
"So how do you know [host/organizer]?"         ← Connection question
"We work together. You?"                       ← Response + reciprocation
"College friends, actually."                   ← Response

PHASE 3: BODY (GETTING TO KNOW)
[Topics drawn from safe inventory:]
"What do you do?"                              ← Occupation question
"So are you from around here?"                 ← Location question
"Been to any good restaurants lately?"         ← Interest exploration
[This phase can expand into genuine conversation or remain surface-level]

PHASE 4: NATURAL EXIT POINT
[Watch for signs of topic exhaustion or mutual readiness to circulate]

PHASE 5: CLOSING
"Well, it was really nice talking to you."     ← Pre-closing + appreciation
"Yeah, you too!"                               ← Reciprocation
"Maybe I'll see you around."                   ← Soft arrangement
"Yeah, definitely! Enjoy the rest of the party." ← Closing + well-wish
"Thanks, you too!"                             ← Terminal
```

**Key features:**
- Self-identification is required (you don't know each other)
- The "How do you know [host]?" question is the standard first topic at parties -- it establishes shared context
- "What do you do?" is the second-most common question (appropriate in American culture; may be less so in other cultures)
- Either party can leave at any time without offense -- parties are circulating environments
- "Nice meeting you" is the standard terminal formula for first meetings

### 4.5 A Doctor's Appointment

This is an **institutional conversation** with a highly structured, asymmetrical format. The doctor controls the conversation flow.

```
PHASE 1: OPENING
Doctor: "Hi, [Patient Name]. I'm Dr. Chen."   ← Greeting + identification
Patient: "Hi, nice to meet you."              ← Return greeting

PHASE 2: REASON FOR VISIT (Doctor elicits)
Doctor: "So, what brings you in today?"        ← Opening the presenting-complaint slot
Patient: "I've been having headaches."         ← Presenting complaint

PHASE 3: INFORMATION GATHERING (Doctor controls)
Doctor: "How long has this been going on?"     ← History-taking questions
Patient: "About two weeks."
Doctor: "Can you describe the pain?"
Doctor: "Any other symptoms?"
Doctor: "Are you taking any medications?"
[The doctor controls topic introduction and shift throughout this phase]
[The patient responds to questions -- they do not typically introduce new topics]

PHASE 4: EXAMINATION
Doctor: "Let me take a look. Can you..."       ← Transition to physical exam
[Instructions: "Look up," "Take a deep breath," etc.]

PHASE 5: DIAGNOSIS / ASSESSMENT
Doctor: "Based on what I'm seeing..."          ← Doctor delivers assessment
[This phase is controlled by the doctor]

PHASE 6: TREATMENT PLAN
Doctor: "Here's what I'd recommend..."         ← Treatment recommendation
Patient: "Okay" / [questions]

PHASE 7: CLOSING
Doctor: "Any other questions?"                 ← Pre-closing: offer for reopening
Patient: "No, I think that covers it."         ← Acceptance of closing
Doctor: "Great. Take care."                    ← Terminal
Patient: "Thank you. Bye."                     ← Terminal

[Note: "Any other questions?" is the doctor's pre-closing signal. If the patient
says "Actually, there is one more thing..." -- the conversation reopens.]
```

**Key features:**
- The doctor controls topic introduction and shift at every phase
- The patient's main opportunity to introduce topics is at the presenting-complaint slot ("What brings you in?") and the "any other questions?" slot at the end
- The conversation follows a predictable phase sequence: complaint → history → exam → diagnosis → treatment → closing
- Many patients find it helpful to prepare a list of topics/questions before the appointment, because the structure gives limited windows for patient-initiated topics
- The "doorknob question" phenomenon: patients sometimes introduce their most important concern just as the doctor is reaching for the doorknob to leave. This is the last possible moment to introduce a topic before closing. Doctors are trained to watch for this.

### 4.6 Starting and Ending a Video Call

Video calls blend elements of phone calls and face-to-face meetings, with unique additional components.

#### Opening

```
PHASE 1: TECHNICAL CHECK
"Can everyone hear me?"                        ← Audio verification
"Yes!" / "Sounds good" / [thumbs up]          ← Confirmation
"Can you see my screen?"                       ← Visual verification (if sharing)

PHASE 2: GATHERING PERIOD
[1-3 minutes of people joining]
"We'll give it another minute for people to join."  ← Waiting announcement
[Small talk during wait: "How is everyone?" / "How's the weather there?"]

PHASE 3: FORMAL OPENING (for meetings)
"Okay, let's get started."                    ← Meeting formally begins
"Thanks for joining, everyone."               ← Appreciation
"So today we're going to talk about..."       ← Agenda/first topic

PHASE 3 (ALTERNATIVE): INFORMAL OPENING (for personal calls)
"Hey! Good to see you!"                       ← Greeting
"Hey! How are you?"                           ← Return greeting + how-are-you
[Standard phone-call opening sequences follow]
```

#### Closing

```
PHASE 1: PRE-CLOSING
"I think that covers everything."              ← Pre-closing (meeting)
"I think we're good."                          ← Pre-closing (meeting)
"Well, it's been great talking."               ← Pre-closing (personal)

PHASE 2: FINAL BUSINESS
"I'll send out the notes."                     ← Action items
"Sound good to everyone?"                      ← Confirmation
"Any last questions?"                          ← Reopening offer

PHASE 3: TERMINAL
"Thanks, everyone. Have a good one."           ← Terminal (meeting)
"Bye! Talk soon!"                              ← Terminal (personal)
[Everyone waves at camera]                     ← Embodied closing gesture
[Clicks "Leave Meeting"]                       ← Physical termination
```

**Key features unique to video calls:**
- **The technical check** is a phase that doesn't exist in other conversation types
- **The awkward wave**: People often wave at the camera during the terminal exchange, then feel awkward about when to click "Leave"
- **"You're on mute"**: This has become a ubiquitous video-call phrase that functions as a repair initiation -- telling someone their turn didn't transmit
- **The "who goes first" problem**: When multiple people try to speak at once (because turn-taking cues are harder to read on video), the norm is to say "Sorry, go ahead" -- a brief repair sequence
- **Screen sharing changes the conversation**: When someone shares their screen, it creates a new focus of attention that structures the conversation around the visual content
- **The "still here?" problem**: In pauses, participants may wonder if the connection dropped. "Are you still there?" / "Can you hear me?" are unique video-call repair initiations

## 5. Structural Summary: The Architecture of Conversation

### 5.1 The Universal Template

Every conversation, regardless of type, follows this basic architecture:

```
1. OPENING
   ├── Summons-Answer (attention secured)
   ├── Identification-Recognition (who are we?)
   ├── Greeting Exchange (social acknowledgment)
   └── How-Are-You (phatic communion / ritual)

2. FIRST TOPIC INTRODUCTION
   └── Anchor Position (reason for contact / first topic)

3. BODY
   ├── Topic Maintenance (staying on topic)
   ├── Topic Shift (marked or gradual)
   ├── Multiple Topics (may cycle through several)
   └── Topic Closure (within-conversation)

4. PRE-CLOSING
   ├── Pre-Closing Signal ("Well..." / "So...")
   ├── Pre-Closing Acceptance ("Yeah, okay")
   └── OR: Reopening ("Oh, one more thing...")

5. CLOSING ELEMENTS
   ├── Arrangements ("Let's do this again")
   ├── Appreciations ("Thanks for calling")
   ├── Well-wishes ("Have a good one")
   └── Back-references ("Tell Sarah hi")

6. TERMINAL EXCHANGE
   └── "Bye" ↔ "Bye"
```

### 5.2 How Conversation Types Compress the Template

Different conversation types compress or expand different parts:

| Conversation Type | Opening | Body | Closing |
|------------------|---------|------|---------|
| Hallway chat | Compressed (1 turn) | Minimal or absent | Compressed (1 turn) |
| Phone call to friend | Full sequence | Extended | Full sequence |
| Chance encounter | Full but quick | Brief | Full but quick |
| Doctor visit | Formal | Highly structured | Quick |
| Video meeting | Extended (tech check) | Agenda-driven | Formal |
| First meeting at party | Extended (intro) | Exploratory | Moderate |
| Text message thread | Minimal/absent | Variable | Often absent (just stops) |
| Email | Formulaic | Self-contained | Formulaic |

### 5.3 The Key Insight for Autistic Individuals

Conversations have **parts**. They have a beginning, middle, and end, and each part has identifiable signals. The confusion many autistic people experience in conversation is not a failure to "be social" -- it's the difficulty of tracking a complex real-time system that neurotypical speakers navigate mostly unconsciously.

By learning the structural components explicitly, you gain what neurotypical speakers have implicitly: a map of where you are in the conversation, what's expected next, and what signals to watch for. The map doesn't make conversation effortless -- but it makes it navigable.
