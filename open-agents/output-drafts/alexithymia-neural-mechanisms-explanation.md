# Where Does Alexithymia Live in the Brain?

The question this category answers is the natural follow-on from the previous three. Categories 1 to 3 said what alexithymia is, how it fractures, and how to measure it. This one asks where in the brain the construct sits, and what mechanisms generate the phenotype that the measurement tools are detecting.

It matters in three concrete ways. First, mechanism conditions therapy. If a person's alexithymia is principally a failure of interoceptive *detection*, body-focused awareness training is plausibly indicated. If it is principally a failure of *interpretation* of signals already detected, the same intervention can amplify symptom intensity rather than help. Second, the behavioural distinctions in Category 2 — Type I versus Type II, primary versus secondary versus organic, parsing-deficit versus detection-deficit — gain or lose credibility depending on whether they map onto separable brain mechanisms. If the subtypes don't carve nature at the joints, the categories are clinically useful labels but not real things. Third, several theoretical frames make different mechanistic claims about alexithymia, and the user has been told different stories by different therapists. The neural evidence is the arena where those stories contest each other.

The honest answer that this category needs to deliver, and the one this page works toward, is that **alexithymia does not have a single neural address**. The most replicated single finding is anterior-insula reduction. Beyond that, the picture is multi-system and probably multiroute, in the Hobson, Brewer, Catmur and Bird (2019) sense. Different alexithymics may have different upstream causes converging on the same downstream phenotype. That conclusion is not a hedge or a cop-out. It's where the evidence currently sits. And it has direct implications for everything in Categories 5 through 11.

## The anterior insula is the single most replicated finding

Of all the brain regions implicated in alexithymia, the anterior insular cortex (AI) has the strongest evidence base. This is true on three independent fronts: structural imaging, functional imaging, and lesion studies.

Structurally, voxel-based morphometry studies have repeatedly shown reduced grey-matter volume in anterior insula in alexithymic populations. The meta-analytic synthesis by van der Velde and colleagues in 2013, which compiled functional imaging studies, identified the anterior insula and the dorsal anterior cingulate as the two most consistently implicated regions. A subsequent VBM meta-analysis by Xu and colleagues in 2018 reached convergent conclusions specifically for grey-matter reductions in the left anterior insula. This isn't a single-paper claim. It's been replicated across populations, methods, and decades.

Functionally, during emotional-stimulus processing — face viewing, emotional word processing, autobiographical recall — high-alexithymia participants show reduced AI activation compared to non-alexithymic controls. The signal is more reliable for negative-valence stimuli than for positive, partly because most paradigms in this literature use negative material. The reduced-activation finding fits naturally with the structural one: less tissue and less recruitment.

The strongest causal claim, though, comes from lesion work. **Hogeveen, Bird, Chau, Krueger and Grafman (2016)**, published in *Neuropsychologia* and available at PMC4752907, is the single most cited paper in this corner of the literature. Their sample was 200 Vietnam War veterans with focal penetrating brain injuries, plus 51 non-injured controls. Their method was voxel-based lesion-symptom mapping, regressing post-injury TAS-20 scores against quantified lesion location and volume. The findings were specific.

Loss of more than 15% of anterior-insula volume reliably produced acquired alexithymia in adults who had been non-alexithymic before their injuries. About 15% of brain-injury patients in the sample met alexithymia criteria post-injury, an elevated rate over the general-population baseline of around 10%. And critically, the dose-response held *after* controlling for ACC damage and total lesion volume. This is what makes the claim more than an association. It rules out the alternative that AI damage is just a marker for a generally damaged brain. The effect is specific to the anterior insula.

Lesion-symptom mapping bypasses the correlation-versus-causation problem of fMRI. If you damage a region and a phenotype appears, and the appearance is dose-dependent and survives controls for adjacent regions, you have something close to a causal claim. The Hogeveen study is therefore the cleanest piece of evidence the field has that anterior insula is causally involved in producing alexithymia.

It deserves two caveats. The 15% threshold is a discretisation of a continuous dose-response. It isn't a magic cutoff — it's a level at which the probability of meeting alexithymia criteria becomes high enough to be clinically reliable. And the study speaks to *acquired* alexithymia (Messina's third category from Cat 2), not to primary developmental alexithymia. It demonstrates that AI damage is *sufficient* to produce the phenotype. It does not demonstrate that primary developmental alexithymia originates from the same locus, even though convergent imaging evidence suggests AI involvement there too.

What is the AI doing functionally? The dominant story is that it is the cortical hub for **interoceptive integration**. Building on Bud Craig's work and on the more recent synthesis by Critchley and Garfinkel, AI receives ascending visceral and bodily inputs, integrates them with descending cognitive context, and is implicated in the subjective awareness of bodily state. The mechanistic claim is that AI dysfunction — reduced volume, reduced activation, dose-dependent lesion effects — impairs the integration step that converts raw bodily afferents into the conscious felt sense on which emotion-labelling depends. This is the architectural substrate for the interoceptive-deficit account that comes next.

## The anterior cingulate cortex shows up too — usually in compensatory hyperactivation

The second most reliably implicated region is the dorsal/supragenual anterior cingulate cortex. Across multiple imaging studies, high-alexithymia participants often show **hyperactivation** of supragenual ACC during emotional stimuli, alongside reduced AI activation. This pattern is somewhat counter-intuitive at first read — if alexithymia is reduced emotional processing, why would any region be working harder?

The interpretation in van der Velde 2013 and subsequent work is that the ACC is doing extra effortful work to compensate for impoverished interoceptive integration upstream. Salience-detection without the visceral signal that would normally accompany it. The ACC and AI together form the salience network, and the network is detecting motivationally relevant stimuli even when the AI's contribution is degraded. The ACC compensates by ramping up appraisal and conflict-monitoring activity.

Subgenual and perigenual ACC findings are more mixed than the dorsal/supragenual finding. Some studies report reduced activation in deeper ACC regions; others find no difference. The reliability is lower. Magnetic-resonance-spectroscopy studies of ACC GABA concentration in alexithymia are sparse, but a small literature reports reduced GABA, which would be consistent with reduced inhibitory tone and possibly with the hyperactivation finding. This isn't yet a large evidence base. Treat it as suggestive rather than established.

The substantive point is that alexithymia is plausibly a **salience-network dysfunction**, not just an AI dysfunction. A signal needs both visceral integration (AI) and salience tagging (ACC) to become a felt emotion accessible for naming. When the AI's contribution is degraded, the ACC's signature changes too, in ways that don't simply mirror the AI's reduction.

## Amygdala, OFC, mPFC: real findings, mixed replication

Beyond the salience network, the literature touches on three other regions, with weaker evidence than for AI or ACC.

Amygdala findings in alexithymia are mixed. Reduced activation to emotional faces has been reported in studies like Reker and colleagues in 2010 and Kano and colleagues in 2003, but **replication is mixed**. Some studies find reduced amygdala activation specifically for negative-valence faces; others find no difference; a minority find increased rather than decreased activation. Possible reasons for the inconsistency include high heterogeneity within "alexithymic" samples, different paradigms, different baseline contrasts, and small sample sizes. Amygdala-ACC coupling has been reported as reduced in alexithymia by Pohl and colleagues in 2018 using resting-state fMRI, which would be consistent with a reduced top-down regulatory loop, but this is also a small literature.

Orbitofrontal cortex (OFC) findings show reduced activation during emotional-decision tasks in some studies, including work by Bagby and colleagues on monetary-reward processing, and reduced grey matter in voxel-based morphometry. The OFC is implicated in valuation and reward learning, and the alexithymia phenotype's selective impairment under decision-ambiguity — Zhang and colleagues 2017, where alexithymics struggle on the Iowa Gambling Task but perform normally on the Game of Dice Task — is consistent with OFC-mediated somatic-marker dysfunction. This is theoretically interesting but the evidence base is much smaller than for AI.

The medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC) is part of the default-mode network and is implicated in self-referential processing and emotion-concept generation. Alexithymia is associated with altered mPFC activation in self-evaluation and emotion-attribution tasks, consistent with the constructed-emotion frame discussed below. Again, the evidence is suggestive rather than definitive.

The honest framing for the user is that the AI and ACC findings should be presented as well-replicated, while amygdala, OFC, and mPFC findings should be presented as plausible but mixed. Don't over-claim the reliability of these adjacent regions just because the overall mechanistic story sounds coherent.

## The Bird/Murphy/Brewer interoception lineage — and the asymmetric walk-back

Now the harder part of this category. There is a body of work running through Geoff Bird, Jennifer Murphy, Rebecca Brewer and their collaborators that has, since 2016, advanced the strongest theoretical claim about alexithymia mechanism: that alexithymia involves a **multidomain, multidimensional failure of interoception**. This claim has been simplified, mistold, and partially conflated with a separate claim about *autism*. It deserves a careful telling because it conditions everything in Categories 7 and 8.

The parent exploration's framing — "Bird walked back the interoception-deficit hypothesis" — was wrong. Two different claims got conflated. The Bird group did walk back one claim. They did not walk back the other. The walk-back is asymmetric.

What was walked back is the **autism-specific claim** — the claim that interoceptive deficits underlie autism. *That* claim Bird and colleagues retreated from. The 2016 paper by **Shah, Hall, Catmur and Bird** in *Cortex* — "Alexithymia, not autism, is associated with impaired interoception" — established that across autistic and non-autistic samples, interoceptive accuracy variance loads on alexithymia level rather than on autism diagnosis. Interoceptive deficits are a feature of *alexithymia*, on this account, not of autism per se. This is a real walk-back of the original autism interoception claim, and it has direct implications for the alexithymia hypothesis of autism (the Bird and Cook 2013 paper revisited in Cat 6).

What was *not* walked back is the **alexithymia claim**. In fact, the Bird group has *strengthened* this position over time.

The 2016 foundation was **Brewer, Cook and Bird**, in *Royal Society Open Science* (PMC5098957). N=208. Self-reported alexithymia was associated with poor non-affective interoception and increased perceived similarity between affective and non-affective body states. The interpretation was that alexithymia involves a *general* interoception failure, not just an emotional one. The methodological caveat is that this study used self-reported interoception measures, which are themselves susceptible to alexithymia-related self-report bias.

The 2018 strengthening was **Murphy, Brewer, Catmur and Bird** in the *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General* (PMC5824617), using novel non-cardiac interoception tasks across three modalities — respiratory output, muscular effort, taste sensitivity. High-alexithymia individuals showed reduced interoceptive accuracy and reduced *use* of interoceptive cues across all three domains, even after controlling for autism, depression, and anxiety. This is the multidomain claim, supported with behavioural data outside the cardiac heartbeat-counting task. It's the strongest empirical leg of the interoceptive-deficit account.

A second 2018 paper by **Murphy, Brewer, Hobson, Catmur and Bird** in *Biological Psychology* (PubMed 29803614) addressed the cardiac heartbeat-counting task (HCT) head-on. N=287. Initial bivariate analyses showed no relationship between alexithymia and HCT performance. *After* controlling for ten covariates — age, gender, time-estimation ability, BMI, knowledge of average HR, HRV, depression, anxiety, systolic BP, resting HR — the predicted negative relationship between alexithymia and HCT performance emerged. The paper has two messages. The first is that the association is real once confounds are controlled. The second is that the HCT itself is methodologically problematic. The methodological critique has sometimes been misread as a substantive walk-back. It isn't. It's a clarification that the cardiac HCT is a noisy instrument, and that effects need to be looked for in the right places.

Then comes the paper that has caused the most confusion. **Nicholson, Williams, Grainger, Christensen, Calvo-Merino and Gaigg (2018)**, "Interoceptive impairments do not lie at the heart of autism or alexithymia," published in the *Journal of Abnormal Psychology* and available at PMC6089261. This is the paper most easily mistaken for a Bird-internal walk-back. It is not. **The authors are an independent group at City University London / UCL**. Across multiple experiments using the cardiac HCT, with Bayesian analyses, they support the null hypothesis: neither autism nor alexithymia is associated with HCT performance. This is a third-party challenge to the Bird group, not an internal walk-back.

The Bird group's response to Nicholson 2018 has been that the cardiac HCT is a poor measurement tool — which is consistent with their own 2018b methodological paper. The Nicholson group treats the null Bayesian results as substantive evidence against the interoception-deficit account. This is a real, ongoing empirical disagreement, not a settled question.

The 2019 multiroute synthesis is **Hobson, Brewer, Catmur and Bird**, in *Emotion Review*. The Bird group's most current synthesis. The thesis is a qualified version of the earlier interoceptive-deficit position. Alexithymia is the common downstream phenotype of *multiple* possible upstream impairments. Interoception is one route. Language and labelling is another. Emotion-concept conceptualisation may be a third. Different alexithymics may sit at different upstream loci. This isn't a retreat from the interoception position — interoception is still affirmed as a route — it's an acknowledgement that interoception isn't the *only* route, and that some alexithymia presentations may have other primary upstream causes.

The 2021 authoritative current synthesis is **Brewer, Murphy and Bird** in *Neuroscience and Biobehavioral Reviews* (PMC8522807). Their direct quote: *"many individuals with alexithymia experience a generalised interoceptive impairment, rather than one specific to emotional processing."* They hypothesise impairment at multiple representational levels — conscious detection, recognition, and labelling — and individuals may differ in which level is affected. They honestly note that meta-analytic data on objectively measured interoceptive accuracy is mixed. They preserve the multidomain claim while qualifying it.

The bottom line for the user: the Bird group's published 2025 position is that alexithymia involves a multidomain, multidimensional interoception failure. The cardiac heartbeat-counting task is methodologically problematic; effects in HCT data emerge only after careful confound control. Autism per se is not associated with interoception deficits — variance loads on co-occurring alexithymia. The deficit may sit at multiple levels — detection, recognition, labelling — and different alexithymics may sit at different levels. Language and conceptualisation is a parallel route. The Nicholson 2018 paper is a live third-party challenge, not a Bird-internal retraction.

This matters because it conditions therapy reasoning. If interoception is a real route to alexithymia for some people, body-focused work has theoretical motivation for them. If it's not the route for others, body-focused work has no theoretical motivation and may even be contraindicated. The honest mechanistic answer doesn't license a single therapeutic protocol.

## The Garfinkel three-dimensional model — the architectural substrate for parsing-deficit

The most consequential single piece of architecture in this section is Sarah Garfinkel's three-dimensional interoception model, published with Anil Seth, Adam Barrett, Keisuke Suzuki and Hugo Critchley in *Biological Psychology* in 2015, "Knowing your own heart." The paper's empirical core is small — N=80 — but its conceptual architecture has now become standard in the field.

Three dissociable dimensions of interoception:

**Interoceptive accuracy** is objective performance on a behavioural task — for instance, counting heartbeats accurately during a fixed interval. The detection layer.

**Interoceptive sensibility** is self-reported tendency to attend to body signals. This is the dimension captured by questionnaires like MAIA. The attentional-disposition layer.

**Interoceptive awareness** is metacognitive correspondence between accuracy and confidence — does the person know whether they are accurate? The metacognitive layer.

The three dimensions dissociate empirically. A person can have any combination of high or low values across them. This means that the term "interoception" in the alexithymia literature has been doing too much work. When a paper says alexithymics have "impaired interoception," what it actually means depends on which of the three dimensions the paradigm is measuring.

The reason this matters for alexithymia is that the three dimensions correspond to three clinically distinct profiles.

A person can have **low IAcc + low ISen** — reduced upstream signal, body signals that are weak or absent. This is the detection-deficit profile. For this person, interoceptive amplification training has theoretical motivation: making weak signals more available could plausibly help.

A person can have **normal-to-high IAcc + low IAw** — signals are detected but the metacognitive labelling step fails. The body is generating data but the system isn't converting it into conscious affective categorisation. This is the parsing-deficit profile. For this person, body-scan training without concept-scaffolding may amplify already-loud signals without resolving the labelling failure.

A person can have **high ISen + low IAcc + low IAw** — lots of body attention, poor accuracy, poor metacognition. The somatic-symptom-disorder pattern. The person is hypervigilant to body signals but interprets them inaccurately or catastrophically. This is the hypervigilance-with-mislabelling profile. For this person, open-ended interoceptive training is most likely to backfire — they don't need more body attention; they need help with inference.

The Garfinkel three-dimensional model is therefore the architectural substrate for the differential-therapy thesis the user wants to take into Categories 7 and 8. It is what makes the parsing-deficit reading of alexithymia mechanistically coherent rather than rhetorically convenient.

## The Aaron 2020 quadratic finding

The most important single empirical paper for the parsing-deficit reading is **Aaron, Blain, Snodgress and Park (2020)**, "Quadratic relationship between alexithymia and interoceptive accuracy, and results from a pilot mindfulness intervention," published in *Frontiers in Psychiatry* and available at PMC7076086.

A note about attribution: this paper is sometimes misattributed to Trevisan, Mehling and McPartland. The actual authors are Aaron, Blain, Snodgress and Park. Trevisan does have a separate body of work on alexithymia and interoception — a 2019 meta-analysis in *Journal of Abnormal Psychology* and a 2021 paper in *Autism Research* — but the quadratic-relationship finding is from the Aaron group, and the mistaken citation has been propagated through the literature several times.

The empirical claim is that alexithymia and interoceptive accuracy show a U-shaped (quadratic) relationship. Both *low* IA and *high* IA can co-occur with high alexithymia. The deficit, by this reading, lies in interpretation of body signals rather than in detection per se.

Why does this matter mechanistically? If the relationship were strictly negative — alexithymia going up as IA goes down — the interpretation would be straightforward: alexithymia is detection failure, and amplifying detection should help. The quadratic finding rules this out. Two distinct alexithymic profiles are present in the same population: a low-IA profile consistent with detection failure, and a high-IA profile consistent with hypervigilance-with-mislabelling — body signals detected acutely but interpreted poorly.

The pilot mindfulness body-scan intervention in the same study showed effect-modification by baseline IA. The same intervention works differently in the two profiles. This is suggestive evidence that subtype-matched intervention is the right framework.

The Aaron 2020 finding is a single pilot study with N=76. It hasn't been directly replicated. But its architectural implication is consistent with the Garfinkel three-dimensional model and with the broader heterogeneity literature, which makes it the clearest single-paper architectural support for treating alexithymia as a parsing failure rather than a uniform detection failure.

## Quattrocki and Friston's predictive processing account

A different theoretical layer is offered by **Quattrocki and Friston (2014)**, "Autism, oxytocin and interoception," in *Neuroscience and Biobehavioral Reviews*. This is a predictive-processing account. It sits at a different abstraction level from the Bird, Brewer and Garfinkel work — it's a computational theory rather than an empirical paradigm.

The active-inference frame casts interoception as a top-down predictive system. The brain generates predictions about expected bodily states; afferent signals adjust those predictions only if they are *precision-weighted* — only if the brain assigns enough confidence to the incoming signal to allow it to update the prediction. Precision is a meta-parameter that determines how much weight prediction errors get.

Quattrocki and Friston propose that alexithymia involves a failure in precision-weighting of interoceptive prediction errors. Either the precision assigned to interoceptive afferents is chronically low — signals are detected but ignored as noise — or chronically miscalibrated — signals are over-weighted when they should be down-weighted. The phenotype is that detected signals fail to update emotion concepts, or detected signals over-update them in a destabilising way.

Oxytocin is hypothesised to modulate precision-weighting in the salience network. Quattrocki and Friston propose that alexithymia involves reduced or atypical oxytocinergic tone, which contributes to the precision-weighting failure. This is the mechanistic motivation for the small intranasal-oxytocin trials reporting larger effects in high-alexithymia participants — Luminet and colleagues 2011, and the Quirin and Carter 2014 review at PMC4172094.

Two caveats. First, this is a theoretical proposal, not an empirically established mechanism. Some convergent computational-modelling work exists, but no direct alexithymia-precision-weighting RCT has been published. Second, even if the theory is approximately right, it doesn't immediately license a specific therapeutic protocol — re-calibrating precision is more abstract than amplifying a signal.

The implication for the user, if Quattrocki-Friston is approximately right, is that interventions which simply amplify body-attention will not address the locus of impairment. What is theoretically needed is *re-calibration of precision* — work that helps the system assign appropriate confidence to interoceptive signals so they can update predictions. This is the mechanistic motivation for IFS-style scaffolded approaches and for psychoeducation-heavy approaches that build emotion concepts before deploying body-attention.

## Lisa Feldman Barrett's constructed-emotion frame

A third theoretical frame, and the most ambitious reformulation, is Barrett's theory of constructed emotion, published in 2017 (PMC5390700). On Barrett's account, emotions are not biologically given categories with dedicated neural circuits. They are *constructed* by the brain in real time using a population-coded conceptual system. The brain generates emotion concepts as predictive simulations integrating interoceptive signals, exteroceptive context, and learned conceptual schemas. The same physiological pattern can be experienced as anxiety in one context and excitement in another. What differs is the conceptual frame, not the body.

The implication for alexithymia is direct. Alexithymia is a state of impoverished emotion-concept system. The conceptual library used to construct emotions is sparse, undifferentiated, or poorly populated. Emotional granularity — the fineness of one's emotion concepts — is inversely related to alexithymia. High-granularity individuals can distinguish many emotional states; low-granularity individuals lump them into broad good/bad categories. The neural correlate is in default-mode-network and conceptual-system involvement — mPFC, posterior cingulate, and related regions — not just in the interoceptive network.

This is a different mechanistic story from Bird and Murphy's. Bird's account locates the deficit in *interoception* — the afferent signal. Barrett's account locates the deficit in *conceptualisation* — the categorisation of signals into emotion concepts. The Hobson 2019 multiroute model treats these as parallel routes to the same downstream phenotype. They aren't necessarily competing. They may both be true for different alexithymics or for the same alexithymic at different layers.

The practical implication is that if the deficit is conceptual rather than interoceptive, the intervention class is *conceptual training* — building richer emotion vocabulary, reading fiction, learning labels, expanding granularity. Lieberman's 2007 paper on putting feelings into words and the broader affect-labelling literature live in this frame. Affect-labelling reduces amygdala reactivity via a right-ventrolateral-PFC-to-mPFC-to-amygdala pathway. Putting feelings into words isn't just a post-hoc description of internal states — it does something downstream. The neural mechanism is plausible, well-mapped, and points away from interoception-first interventions.

## David Preece's attention-appraisal model

The CBT-shaped reformulation is **Preece, Becerra, Robinson, Dandy and Allan (2017)**, the attention-appraisal model. It's operationalised by the Perth Alexithymia Questionnaire (PAQ) discussed in Cat 3.

Preece reframes alexithymia at the conscious-process level. Emotion regulation requires (a) attention to the emotion when it occurs, (b) appraisal of the emotion (identifying, labelling, evaluating), (c) some response. Alexithymia is operationalised as a failure at the attention or appraisal stage. Difficulty Identifying Feelings is reframed as a failure of attention — the person doesn't deploy attention to the emotion when it arises — or of appraisal — the person attends but cannot categorise. The PAQ measures DIF and DDF separately for positive and negative emotions, plus a general externally-oriented thinking factor. The valence-separation operationalises Preece's frame: a person can be alexithymic for negative emotions but not positive, or vice versa.

Preece's frame is **complementary** rather than competing with Bird, Barrett, and Quattrocki-Friston. It's a cognitive-behavioural-level description of what's happening at the conscious-process level. Bird's frame is afferent-network-level. Barrett's is conceptual-system-level. Quattrocki-Friston is computational-precision-level. Preece's is attention-and-appraisal-level. They sit at different abstraction layers, and a comprehensive account probably needs all of them.

For therapy matching, Preece gives a CBT-shaped path. Train attention to emotional cues. Train appraisal granularity. Train labelling. This is theoretically friendly to CBT-derivative approaches and to externalised cognitive scaffolding — journaling, vocabulary work, mood-tracking apps with emotion-vocabulary scaffolding. It does not require interoceptive-detection training as a starting point.

It also has a competitor. Taylor and Bagby's 2024 *Defense of the Original Conceptualization* (PMC11555664) is an explicit critique of Preece's reformulation. Taylor and Bagby argue that Preece's frame flattens the construct into emotion-regulation-failure, losing the psychoanalytic core. This is a real, active dispute in 2025 about what alexithymia *is*, not just where it lives.

## The Sifneos psychoanalytic frame is still defended

Peter Sifneos coined "alexithymia" in 1972, with John Nemiah, in a psychosomatic-medicine context. Their definition was a four-feature construct: difficulty identifying feelings, difficulty describing feelings, externally-oriented thinking, and *restricted imaginative/fantasy life*. The construct was tied to a psychodynamic model of failed symbolic mentalisation. Emotions are unprocessed at the bodily level because they cannot be elaborated symbolically.

Taylor and Bagby in 2024 explicitly defend this original conceptualisation against Preece's attention-appraisal reformulation. They argue that the fantasy/imagination criterion is essential and was wrongly dropped from the TAS-20. Alexithymia is best understood as a developmental-psychosomatic phenomenon, not a CBT-process phenomenon. Preece's reformulation, in their reading, loses the construct's depth.

For the user, the take-home is that the original frame is alive in 2025 and continues to defend a different mechanistic picture — one in which alexithymia is a failure of *symbolic elaboration of affect*, not an interoceptive or conceptual or attentional deficit. This maps reasonably well onto the Hobson 2019 *language* route, even though Taylor and Bagby would not phrase it that way. The implied therapy class is long-term psychodynamic work, mentalisation-based therapy, and symbolic-elaboration interventions.

## The Hobson multiroute model — the integrative answer

The most useful synthesis, and the one this category lands on, is the Hobson, Brewer, Catmur and Bird (2019) multiroute model. The thesis is that alexithymia is the **common downstream phenotype of multiple possible upstream impairments**. The same TAS-20 score can be generated by:

An *interoception route* — failure in detection, integration, or representation of bodily signals (anterior insula, ACC, salience network).

A *language/labelling route* — failure in the verbal-symbolic system that maps physiological-conceptual states onto words (left lateral PFC, the affect-labelling pathway documented by Lieberman 2007).

An *emotion-concept route* — impoverished conceptual system in the Barrett sense (mPFC, default-mode network).

A *precision/predictive-coding route* — atypical precision-weighting in the Friston sense (salience network plus modulatory systems).

An *attention/appraisal route* — at the conscious-process level in the Preece sense (executive control networks).

A *symbolic-elaboration route* — failure of psychodynamic-style symbolic mentalisation of affect, in the Sifneos / Taylor-and-Bagby sense.

These aren't necessarily competing accounts. Different alexithymics may sit at different routes. The same alexithymic may have impairments at multiple routes. The therapeutic implication is that the *appropriate* intervention is route-specific — interoceptive training for the detection-deficit profile, granularity and affect-labelling work for the conceptual or language profile, attention-deployment work for the attentional profile, scaffolded inference for the precision-weighting profile, and so on.

The user's natural question — *where does my alexithymia live in the brain?* — has, on this account, no single answer. It depends on which upstream route is implicated. The user's profile (autism plus alexithymia plus the heterogeneity-aware self-classification work in Categories 2 and 3) is consistent with a multiroute presentation: some interoceptive component, some conceptual or language component, some attentional component, with the proportion varying.

This is not a hedge. It's the field's best current answer. And it leads directly to the Cat 7 and Cat 8 question of differential therapy by subtype — there is no one-size-fits-all alexithymia therapy because there is no one-size-fits-all alexithymia mechanism.

## Replication status — an honest table

A few of the findings discussed above are well-replicated. Others are single high-quality studies. Others are mixed. Others are theoretical proposals. The honest thing is to flag these distinctions clearly.

Anterior-insula grey-matter reduction is well-replicated across multiple meta-analyses. AI hypoactivation during emotional processing is well-replicated. The Hogeveen 2016 lesion dose-response is a single high-quality study with very strong internal design — large sample, lesion-symptom mapping, controls for ACC and total volume — but has not been directly replicated. It's the strongest single causal claim in the field, and that does not mean it's been re-tested independently.

Supragenual ACC hyperactivation is replicated across several studies. The compensatory-interpretation is theoretically motivated but not directly tested. ACC GABA findings are sparse — treat as suggestive only.

Reduced amygdala activation is mixed. Pohl 2018 amygdala-ACC reduced coupling is a single resting-state study. OFC reduced activation is suggestive with a smaller literature. mPFC altered self-referential processing is suggestive, consistent with the Barrett frame, but not specific to alexithymia.

Brewer 2016 self-reported general interoception failure was foundational but used self-report measures only. Murphy 2018 multidomain (respiration, muscle, taste) is a single high-quality study with strong design, replicated within the Bird group but not independently. Nicholson 2018 Bayesian-null is a real third-party paper from an independent group; the cardiac HCT it uses is methodologically contested. Murphy 2018b's confound-control HCT result is real but should be presented as a methodological clarification, not a clean substantive effect.

The Garfinkel 2015 three-dimensional model is widely adopted as architecture; the dimensional dissociation is well-supported. The Aaron 2020 quadratic relationship is a single pilot study with N=76, not yet directly replicated, consistent with the broader heterogeneity literature. Quattrocki-Friston is theoretical; Barrett constructed emotion is theoretical with broad conceptual support and one well-supported empirical leg in the granularity-alexithymia inverse relationship; Preece attention-appraisal is operationalised as PAQ with adequate psychometrics, but the mechanistic claim is theoretically contested.

This is the evidence base the user gets to hold up against any therapy claim. A therapist who says "your alexithymia is from interoceptive failure, do body-scan meditation" is making a claim about *which route* the user's alexithymia primarily lives on, plus a claim about *which intervention class* maps onto that route. Both claims can be examined against the evidence above.

## Where this leaves the user

The honest mechanistic answer for the user is multiroute. Anterior insula is the most replicated single locus, but the multiroute model says the user's particular alexithymia could plausibly involve interoception, language and labelling, emotion concepts, precision-weighting, attention and appraisal, or symbolic elaboration — any combination, with weights that vary across individuals. The neural address is not a single building. It's a network of partially independent systems, any of which can fail in ways that produce the same downstream phenotype.

The therapy-matching answer follows directly. Categories 7 and 8 will use the route mapping to ask which approaches plausibly help which profiles, and which are contraindicated for which. The Garfinkel three-dimensional model and the Aaron 2020 quadratic finding are the architectural substrate for distinguishing detection-deficit, parsing-deficit, and hypervigilance-with-mislabelling profiles — distinctions that map directly onto risk of iatrogenic harm in Cat 8.

The user's stated frame for this deep dive — *which subtype am I and which therapies help*  — is, in mechanism terms, a multiroute question. The user's job over Categories 5 through 11 is to triangulate which routes their own alexithymia most plausibly lives on, then evaluate therapy options against that profile. The neuroscience doesn't give a single answer. But it gives a structured set of questions, and a clear sense of what kind of evidence to ask for when therapists make claims.
