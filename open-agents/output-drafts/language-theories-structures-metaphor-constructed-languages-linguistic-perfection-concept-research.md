# Constructed Languages and the Dream of Linguistic Perfection

## Research Summary

This document compiles findings on constructed languages (conlangs) across their full history, from 17th-century philosophical languages through modern artistic creations. The research traces a persistent human dream: to build a language free of ambiguity, irregularity, and cultural bias. Every attempt, whether aimed at classifying all knowledge, enabling international communication, enforcing logical precision, or building fictional worlds, reveals something fundamental about why natural languages work the way they do. The recurring lesson is that the features designers most want to eliminate (ambiguity, redundancy, irregularity, polysemy) turn out to be essential, not accidental.

## 1. 17th-Century Philosophical Languages: Classifying the World in Symbols

### The Intellectual Context: The Scientific Revolution's Demand for Precision

The 17th-century philosophical language movement emerged directly from the Scientific Revolution. The old practice of hiding new discoveries in private jargon, obscure language, or even anagrams was giving way to an ideal of universal comprehensibility, with new canons of reporting devised so that experiments and discoveries could be reproduced by others. This required new precision in language. The Royal Society of London, founded in 1662 with the motto *Nullius in verba* ("take nobody's word for it"), embodied the conviction that knowledge should be verifiable, communicable, and systematically organized.

Philosophical languages were artificial constructed languages devised primarily in the 17th century to enable precise, universal communication of ideas by systematically classifying and labeling all human concepts in a hierarchical manner. The goal was to eliminate the ambiguities inherent in natural languages and facilitate philosophical and scientific discourse. The concept reflected a universalist belief that a single, rational system could encapsulate all existent things and promote international understanding.

### Francis Lodwick's "A Common Writing" (1647)

Francis Lodwick (1619-1694) was a London merchant, not an academic, yet he produced the first known attempt to construct a language using a universal character. His first work, *A Common Writing*, appeared in 1647, followed by *The Groundwork or Foundation Laid (or So Intended) for the Framing of a New Perfect Language and a Universal Common Writing* in 1652.

Lodwick's innovation was to reduce the number of terms by deriving them from a finite number of primitives expressing actions, using a conventional character with grammatical marks to express ideas like the actor, act, object, and place. George Dalgarno acknowledged Lodwick's pioneering work in his own *Ars Signorum*, while noting that Lodwick, as a merchant rather than a scholar, "did not possess adequate force for such an undertaking."

**Key insight**: Lodwick's approach of reducing language to primitive actions and relationships anticipated later attempts at semantic decomposition. The fact that a merchant, not a philosopher, initiated this tradition speaks to how widely the desire for linguistic precision was felt during the Scientific Revolution.

### George Dalgarno's "Ars Signorum" (1661)

George Dalgarno was a 17th-century Scottish intellectual and teacher whose *Ars Signorum -- the Art of Signs* (1661) was the first work in the century to present a fully elaborated universal language constructed on philosophical principles. Dalgarno's work contained an account of the previous history of the project, mentioning both Ward and Lodwick by name.

Dalgarno's system classified concepts into hierarchical categories and assigned systematic symbols based on their position in the taxonomy. His approach influenced John Wilkins, who would produce the most ambitious version of the same project seven years later.

### John Wilkins' "Real Character and Philosophical Language" (1668): The Most Ambitious Attempt

John Wilkins' *Essay towards a Real Character and a Philosophical Language* (1668) represents the most ambitious 17th-century attempt to create a universal language based on the systematic classification of all knowledge. Published under the imprimatur of the Royal Society, Wilkins was a founding member, and the Essay was intended to contribute to improved scientific practice, facilitate inter-linguistic communication, and ameliorate religious controversy.

#### The Taxonomical Structure

Wilkins designed his artificial language to produce "the shortest and plainest way for the attainment of real knowledge" by categorizing things and notions, dividing them into subgroups, and assigning them letters and symbols. The system works as follows:

- **40 Main Genera**: All concepts are divided into forty main genera, each assigned a two-letter syllable
- **Differences**: Each genus subdivides into differences, each adding another letter
- **Species**: Differences subdivide further into species, adding a fourth letter
- **Total scope**: Using 40 genera and 251 differences, Wilkins' tables define 2,030 species

**Example**: *Zi* identifies the genus of "beasts" (mammals); *Zit* gives the difference of "rapacious beasts of the dog kind"; *Zita* gives the species of dogs. Another example: *de* means an element; *deb* means the first element, fire; *deba* means a part of fire, a flame.

#### The "Real Character" vs. Spoken Language

The Real Character is emphatically not an orthography -- it is not a written representation of oral speech. Instead, each symbol represents a concept directly, without there being any way of vocalizing it at all (at least in the early parts of the Essay's presentation). This reflects the dream of a language of pure thought, unmediated by the contingencies of sound.

#### Structure of the Work

The work comprises five parts: the first two provide context and motivation; the third treats "philosophical grammar" (what we would now call universal grammar); the fourth contains the actual "real character" and "philosophical language"; and the fifth is an alphabetical dictionary.

#### Legacy

Wilkins' language was, and is, a fascinating failure. Yet its influence persists: library classification systems, Linnaeus' taxonomy of living things, and Roget's thesaurus all borrow ideas from his work. The attempt to organize all knowledge into a systematic hierarchy of categories anticipated modern ontologies and classification schemes, even though the language itself proved unusable.

Sources: [An Essay Towards a Real Character - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/An_Essay_Towards_a_Real_Character,_and_a_Philosophical_Language), [John Wilkins - History of Information](https://www.historyofinformation.com/detail.php?id=1553), [Atlas Obscura - 17th Century Language](https://www.atlasobscura.com/articles/the-17thcentury-language-that-divided-everything-in-the-universe-into-40-categories), [Royal Society Publishing](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsnr.2002.0174)

## 2. Leibniz's Characteristica Universalis: Reducing Thought to Calculation

### The Two-Part Vision

Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646-1716) conceived the most philosophically radical version of the universal language dream. His program comprised two interconnected parts:

1. **Characteristica Universalis**: A universal notation by which any item of information could be recorded in a natural and systematic way -- a formal language able to express mathematical, scientific, and metaphysical concepts
2. **Calculus Ratiocinator**: A means of manipulating knowledge thus recorded in a computational fashion, so as to reveal its logical interrelations and consequences

Together, these would form a complete system for encoding and reasoning about all human thought.

### "Calculemus!" -- Let Us Calculate

Leibniz's most famous formulation captures the audacity of his vision:

> "The only way to rectify our reasonings is to make them as tangible as those of the Mathematicians, so that we can find our error at a glance, and when there are disputes among persons, we can simply say: *Let us calculate* [calculemus], without further ado, to see who is right."

The dream was extraordinary: philosophical, political, and even theological disputes could be resolved through computation, as reliably as arithmetic problems. Disagreements would become errors in calculation rather than matters of opinion.

### Why It Remained Unfinished

Several fundamental obstacles prevented Leibniz from completing his project:

1. **Analytical impossibility**: The characteristica universalis presupposed "an analysis brought to an end and deepened down to the ground," but such complete analysis of all concepts into their primitive components could not be accomplished given the state of knowledge at the time -- or perhaps at any time
2. **Lack of operational detail**: Leibniz never described the characteristica universalis in sufficient operational detail to implement it, leading many philosophers to dismiss it as an "absurd fantasy"
3. **Vagueness and inconsistency**: The project evolved throughout Leibniz's career, and modern scholars find inconsistency and a lack of specifics across his various writings on the subject
4. **The fundamental problem**: Reducing thought to calculation requires knowing the complete set of primitive concepts -- the "alphabet of human thoughts" -- and there is no principled way to determine whether such a set exists or is complete

### The Connection to Formal Logic and Computer Science

Despite its failure as a realized project, Leibniz's vision proved prophetic:

- Leibniz was trying to encode the problem of reasoning about concepts into the simpler problem of reasoning about numbers, for which machines were being developed at his time -- an early precursor of today's encoding of all sorts of problems down to binary arithmetic
- The calculus ratiocinator prefigured modern computational logic and automated reasoning
- His characteristica universalis, calculus ratiocinator, and vision of a "community of minds" can be seen as distant, unwitting anticipations of artificial languages (such as Esperanto), symbolic logic, programming languages, and even the World Wide Web
- Although Leibniz's technical contributions to logic did not survive into mainstream modern logic, his dream inspired generations of logicians, from Frege to Russell to the creators of programming languages

**The pattern**: Leibniz imagined that all knowledge could be expressed in symbols and all reasoning could be mechanized. He was right about mathematics and formal logic (where programming languages succeed), but wrong about natural language and human thought (which resist such formalization). The boundary between what can and cannot be calculated turns out to be the same boundary between formal and natural language.

## 3. Why Taxonomical Languages Failed: The Impossibility of Fixed Categories

### The Instability of Categories: Borges' Devastating Critique

Jorge Luis Borges delivered the most famous critique of taxonomical languages in his 1942 essay "The Analytical Language of John Wilkins." To illustrate the arbitrariness and cultural specificity of any attempt to categorize the world, Borges invented a fictitious Chinese encyclopedia called the *Celestial Emporium of Benevolent Knowledge*, which supposedly divides animals into:

> (a) those that belong to the Emperor, (b) embalmed ones, (c) those that are trained, (d) suckling pigs, (e) mermaids, (f) fabulous ones, (g) stray dogs, (h) those that are included in this classification, (i) those that tremble as if they were mad, (j) innumerable ones, (k) those drawn with a very fine camel's hair brush, (l) others, (m) those that have just broken a flower vase, (n) those that resemble flies from a distance.

Borges' conclusion: "there is no description of the universe that isn't arbitrary and conjectural for a simple reason: we don't know what the universe is."

This classification is absurd -- but that is Borges' point. It exposes that *every* classification system, including Wilkins', reflects the prejudices and priorities of its creators. In the Soviet library system, Marxism-Leninism was a top-level category. In the Dewey Decimal system, religion is a top-level category, with 8 of its 9 subcategories devoted to Christianity and the last one labeled "Other religions." Every taxonomy encodes a worldview.

Michel Foucault was so struck by Borges' passage that he made it the opening of *The Order of Things* (1966), using it to explore how different cultures create fundamentally different systems of order.

Sources: [Celestial Emporium of Benevolent Knowledge - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_Emporium_of_Benevolent_Knowledge), [The Analytical Language of John Wilkins - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Analytical_Language_of_John_Wilkins)

### The Problem of Polysemy: Words NEED Multiple Meanings

Research on polysemy has increasingly demonstrated that the multiple meanings of words are not a defect to be engineered away but a functional property of efficient communication:

- **Efficiency**: Polysemy allows for fewer words to express a wide range of ideas, making everyday communication more efficient. One root in Esperanto, for comparison, is estimated to be the communicative equivalent of ten words in English -- precisely because English words carry multiple related meanings
- **Context-dependence**: Any efficient communication system will necessarily be ambiguous when context is informative about meaning. A well-designed system will be ambiguous when examined out of context because it does not redundantly specify information already provided by context
- **Processing advantage**: Modern investigations have found that many expressions in everyday interactions are ambiguous but surprisingly rarely lead to miscommunication. Speakers and listeners effectively manage polysemy through context

The theoretical conclusion is stark: removing polysemy from language does not improve it. It makes communication slower, more cumbersome, and less adapted to how human cognition actually works.

Sources: [Polysemy - Computational Linguistics / MIT Press](https://direct.mit.edu/coli/article/50/1/351/118497/Polysemy-Evidence-from-Linguistics-Behavioral), [The communicative function of ambiguity in language - ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0010027711002496)

### Eco's "The Search for the Perfect Language"

Umberto Eco's *The Search for the Perfect Language* (1995) provides the definitive historical survey of the attempt to create perfect languages, from the myth of Adamic language (the "original" tongue spoken before Babel) through medieval mystical alphabets, Renaissance combinatory arts, 17th-century philosophical languages, and into the modern era.

Eco's central argument is that the search for a perfect language has been driven by a persistent dream that language could transparently represent reality -- that the relationship between words and things could be made rational, systematic, and universal. Every attempt has failed because language is not merely a labeling system for pre-existing categories; it actively constructs the categories through which we understand the world. The search for the perfect language is, in Eco's analysis, the search for an impossible transparency.

Eco incorporated Borges' parodic classifications into his semiotic explorations, leveraging similar lists to probe the instability of meaning and interpretive frameworks.

Sources: [The Search for the Perfect Language - Goodreads](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/10513.The_Search_for_the_Perfect_Language), [Umberto Eco: Search for the Perfect Language - Samizdat](https://therealsamizdat.com/2016/04/23/umberto-eco-search-for-the-perfect-language/)

## 4. International Auxiliary Languages: The Dream of Universal Communication

### Volapuk (Johann Martin Schleyer, 1879-1880): The First Major Attempt

Volapuk was constructed in 1879-1880 by Johann Martin Schleyer, a German Catholic priest, and intended for use as an international second language. It experienced explosive early growth: by 1889, there were an estimated 283 clubs, 25 periodicals in or about Volapuk, and 316 textbooks in 25 languages, with nearly a million claimed adherents.

**Linguistic characteristics**: Although its vocabulary was based on English and Romance languages, the roots were modified to such a degree that they became virtually unrecognizable. For example: *lol* from English "rose," *nim* from "animal," *Melop* from "America." The grammar was complex: four cases for nouns, six pronouns with plural forms, six tenses for verbs, plus indicative, passive, infinitive, aorist, conditional-conjunctive, imperative, and participial forms, each with full or nearly full conjugation.

**Why it collapsed**: The critical failure was governance, not linguistics. Tensions arose between Auguste Kerckhoffs and others in the Academy, who wanted reforms, and Schleyer, who insisted on retaining proprietary rights over the language. The resulting schism led much of the Academy to abandon Volapuk in favor of Idiom Neutral and other projects. As one commentator summarized: "Volapukism as a movement extinguished Volapuk as a language." Many former Volapuk clubs simply converted to Esperanto.

**Lesson**: A constructed language cannot survive if its creator treats it as personal property. Language requires community ownership to evolve and survive. Schleyer's refusal to cede control demonstrated that even constructed languages obey the social dynamics of natural ones.

Sources: [Volapuk - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volap%C3%BCk), [Volapuk - Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Volapuk), [Truth, Beauty, and Volapuk - Public Domain Review](https://publicdomainreview.org/essay/truth-beauty-and-volapuk)

### Esperanto (L. L. Zamenhof, 1887): The Most Successful Constructed Language

#### Creation and Motivation

Esperanto was created by L. L. Zamenhof (1859-1917) in 1887 as "the International Language" (*la Lingvo Internacia*). The first textbook, *Unua Libro* ("First Book"), was published under the pseudonym *Doktoro Esperanto* ("one who hopes"). Zamenhof grew up in Bialystok (then part of the Russian Empire), a culturally diverse yet conflict-ridden city where Russians, Poles, Germans, and Jews lived side by side. He became convinced that language barriers contributed fundamentally to social strife and conceived Esperanto as a bridge language to foster world peace and international understanding.

#### Why It (Partially) Succeeded

Several design decisions contributed to Esperanto's relative success:

**Regular agglutinative morphology**: Esperanto is completely regular -- grammatical rules have no exceptions. All words consist of a stem plus a grammatical ending:
- `-o` for nouns (*telefon-o* = "telephone")
- `-a` for adjectives (*telefon-a* = "telephonic")
- `-i` for infinitive verbs (*telefon-i* = "to telephone")
- `-e` for adverbs (*rapid-e* = "quickly")
- `-j` for plural (*libro-j* = "books")
- `-n` for accusative (*libro-n* = "book" as direct object)

**Productive derivational morphology**: A large number of prefixes and suffixes allow a relatively small number of roots to expand into a large vocabulary. It has been estimated that on average one root in Esperanto is the communicative equivalent of ten words in English. The outer affixes modify the inner ones: *mal-san-ul-ej-o* = "mal" (opposite) + "san" (health) + "ul" (person) + "ej" (place) + "o" (noun) = hospital.

**Familiar European vocabulary**: Word roots drawn primarily from Romance, Germanic, and Slavic languages make the vocabulary immediately recognizable to many European language speakers.

**Community ownership**: Unlike Schleyer with Volapuk, Zamenhof claimed no proprietary rights to the language, making it freely available for modification and evolution.

#### Current Status

Esperanto is the world's most widely spoken constructed auxiliary language. Estimates put the number of speakers who know Esperanto at roughly 100,000, with around 2,000 native speakers (*denaskuloj*) who acquired it from birth. It is spoken by communities in over 100 countries, with an active literary tradition, music, online courses (including a popular Duolingo course), and annual international congresses (*Universala Kongreso*).

#### Historical Persecution

Esperanto faced significant persecution. Nazi Germany condemned the language, using Zamenhof's Jewish heritage as justification. Hitler's *Mein Kampf* specifically mentioned Esperanto as a potential tool of "international Jewry." The language and its speakers were targeted during the Holocaust. Stalin's Soviet Union also persecuted Esperantists.

### Criticisms of Esperanto

Despite its success, Esperanto has been criticized on several fronts:

- **European bias**: Nearly all vocabulary is drawn from Indo-European languages, specifically European ones. The language is significantly easier for speakers of European languages than for speakers of Chinese, Japanese, Arabic, or other non-Indo-European languages
- **Gender system**: All animate nouns were assumed male unless given the "ghettoizing" suffix *-in*. The word *studento* did not mean "student" but "male student"; a female student had to be marked as *studentino*. This has sparked ongoing reform movements
- **The accusative case**: The accusative marker *-n* is sometimes criticized as redundant given Esperanto's predominantly fixed SVO word order, and it creates difficulty for speakers of languages (like English and French) that lack nominal case marking
- **Underlying Indo-European grammar**: Despite claims of neutrality, the grammar reflects fundamentally Indo-European patterns

Sources: [Esperanto grammar - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Esperanto_grammar), [Esperanto - RationalWiki](https://rationalwiki.org/wiki/Esperanto)

### Other Notable Auxiliary Languages

#### Ido (c. 1907-1910)
A reformed offspring of Esperanto, designed to address some of Esperanto's perceived flaws. It emerged from a delegation of linguists who proposed modifications. Uses the modern Latin alphabet without diacritics. Never achieved Esperanto's community size.

#### Interlingua (1937-1951)
Developed by the American International Auxiliary Language Association (IALA). Takes a "naturalistic" approach -- designed to look like a Romance language and be immediately comprehensible (to some degree) to speakers of Romance languages without study. Has enjoyed greater institutional adoption than most auxiliary languages, used in some scientific publications.

#### Novial (1928)
Created by the Danish linguist Otto Jespersen (who had previously contributed to Ido). Based primarily on Germanic and Romance languages with English-influenced grammar. More analytic than Ido. The language largely disappeared after Jespersen's death in 1943, illustrating the dependence of constructed languages on their communities.

**Pattern across auxiliary languages**: Every auxiliary language faces the same tension between *schematic* design (systematic and easy to learn for speakers of any language) and *naturalistic* design (familiar-looking and easy to read for speakers of source languages). Ido leans schematic; Interlingua leans naturalistic. No design solves both problems simultaneously, because there is no culturally neutral starting point for a language.

Sources: [Comparison between Ido and Interlingua - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comparison_between_Ido_and_Interlingua), [Interlingua - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interlingua), [International auxiliary language - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_auxiliary_language)

## 5. Logical Languages: Encoding Predicate Logic in Speech

### Loglan (James Cooke Brown, 1955)

Loglan is a logical constructed language developed beginning in 1955 by Dr. James Cooke Brown with the explicit goal of testing the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis -- the idea that the language we speak constrains what we are able to think. Brown reasoned that if a language were designed to be maximally different from all natural languages in its structure, speakers who learned it might demonstrate measurably different thought patterns, thereby providing evidence for or against the hypothesis.

**Design principles**:
- Grammar based on predicate logic
- Completely regular and unambiguous: each sentence can be parsed in exactly one way
- Syllabic structure designed so that a sequence of syllables can be separated into words in only one way, even without pauses in speech
- Vocabulary drawn from the most widely spoken natural languages, blended algorithmically to avoid favoring any single source

### Lojban (1987-present): The Open-Source Fork

The Logical Language Group (LLG) began developing Lojban in 1987 as a fork of Loglan after disputes with Brown over intellectual property (echoing the Volapuk pattern). The LLG sought to realize Loglan's purposes, improve the language's usability, and make it freely available.

**Key features of Lojban**:
- Completely unambiguous grammar: every well-formed sentence has exactly one parse
- Based on predicate logic: the fundamental unit is the *bridi* (predication), composed of a *selbri* (predicate/relation) and its *sumti* (arguments)
- Culturally neutral vocabulary: root words (*gismu*) derived algorithmically from the six most widely spoken languages (Mandarin, English, Hindi, Spanish, Russian, Arabic)
- Formal, computer-parseable grammar (defined by a Parsing Expression Grammar)
- Attitudinal indicators: a systematic way to express emotions and attitudes toward propositions, far more precise than natural language interjections

### Does Learning Lojban Change Thought?

The Lojban community reports cognitive benefits:
- Learning Lojban is described as a "mind-expanding experience" that broadens perspectives beyond one's native language culture
- Lojbanists' discussions about language tend to be "more sophisticated as well as qualitatively different from the talk of students of second natural languages"
- The logical organization embedded in Lojban reportedly aids in organizing and clarifying thoughts, even when subsequently using natural languages

However, these claims come with important caveats. It is not known whether cognitive changes result from exposure to a different linguistic structure, exposure to a different culture, or simply the cognitive exercise of learning any new system. No formal, controlled experiments have been conducted to definitively establish that Lojban's specific structural features cause the reported cognitive effects.

**The deeper lesson**: Lojban demonstrates that it is possible to create a genuinely unambiguous language -- but at a steep cost. Lojban sentences are often significantly longer than their natural language equivalents, because disambiguation requires explicit marking that context normally provides for free. The language proves that ambiguity in natural language is not laziness; it is compression. Natural languages are ambiguous because context resolves most ambiguity, and encoding contextual information redundantly in the linguistic signal would be wasteful.

## 6. Artistic and Fictional Languages: Language as World-Building

### J.R.R. Tolkien's Quenya and Sindarin: Languages with Histories

Tolkien's constructed languages represent a fundamentally different approach from philosophical or auxiliary languages. Where Wilkins and Zamenhof tried to make language more rational, Tolkien aimed to make it more *natural* -- to create languages that looked and felt as though they had evolved over centuries, with all the irregularity and historical depth that implies.

**Key facts**:
- Tolkien stated: "The invention of languages is the foundation. The 'stories' were made rather to provide a world for the languages than the reverse." The languages came first; Middle-earth was built to house them
- Tolkien began working on Quenya around 1910, inspired by his encounter with Finnish. Finnish morphology (its rich system of inflection) gave rise to Quenya, while features of Welsh phonology found their way into Sindarin
- To achieve linguistic realism, Tolkien designed his languages to have histories, creating the proto-language Valarin and designing all Elvish dialects to believably descend from this single proto-language, just as the Romance languages descend from Latin
- Sindarin and Quenya were designed to be natural languages with their own irregularities, quirks, and oddities, but whose peculiarities would make sense when examined from a historical linguistic perspective

**What Tolkien reveals**: Tolkien understood, as a professional philologist, that real languages are *historical* objects. Their apparent irregularities are the fossils of past regularities. By building fictional language histories, Tolkien showed that irregularity is not noise -- it is signal, encoding a language's past. A language without history is a language without depth.

### Klingon (Marc Okrand, 1984): From Alien Sounds to Living Community

For *Star Trek III: The Search for Spock* (1984), director Leonard Nimoy commissioned a full language from Marc Okrand, a linguist who had studied Native American and Southeast Asian languages. Phonological and grammatical features of these languages "worked their way into Klingon, but for the most part, not by design." The language was deliberately designed to sound "alien."

**From fiction to community**: A community of Klingon speakers arose and expanded beyond the language's original context. No longer restricted to alien warriors in outer space, Klingon shifted to become a vehicle for composing original songs and stories, translating works of literature (including *Hamlet* and parts of the Bible), and discussing everyday concerns. Over 300,000 copies of *The Klingon Dictionary* were sold.

**Natural language dynamics in an artificial language**: The Klingon community has had to create words for concepts never imagined in Star Trek -- bicycles, pineapples, scarecrows, even the coronavirus. This expansion demonstrates that even a language designed to be alien is subject to the same pressures as natural languages: communities need to talk about their world, and language must grow to accommodate them.

### David J. Peterson and Modern Conlanging: A Profession Emerges

David J. Peterson (b. 1981) came to prominence after creating the Dothraki and Valyrian languages for *Game of Thrones* (2011-2019). In 2009, HBO turned to the Language Creation Society for help, resulting in a contest. Peterson won by submitting a 303-page proposal including the full grammar and dictionary of Dothraki, translations of all pilot material, cultural idioms, a 10-page summary, and a page of "fun facts."

**The professionalization of conlanging**: Peterson has since created over 40 languages for film and television, including Chakobsa for *Dune* and *Dune: Part Two*. He published *The Art of Language Invention* (2015) and created a free High Valyrian course on Duolingo that has become extremely popular. Moviemakers and TV producers have increasingly hired professional conlangers, transforming what was once a niche hobby into a recognized profession.

Peterson's path to conlanging is itself revealing: his first contact with constructed languages came through attending an Esperanto class at UC Berkeley in 2000. In 2006, he organized the inaugural Language Creation Conference, and in 2007 helped create the Language Creation Society.

Sources: [David J. Peterson - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_J._Peterson), [How I created Dothraki and Valyrian - OUP Blog](https://blog.oup.com/2014/07/dothraki-valyrian-language-game-of-thrones/)

### Toki Pona (Sonja Lang, 2001): Minimalism as Philosophy

Toki Pona represents the opposite extreme from Wilkins' attempt to classify everything: it is a philosophical and artistic language designed for its tiny vocabulary, simplicity, and ease of acquisition. Created by Canadian translator and polyglot Sonja Lang, it was first published online in 2001.

**Core design**:
- Approximately 120-137 "essential" words (Lang presents ~120 in *Toki Pona: The Language of Good*; the later *Toki Pona Dictionary* lists 137)
- Only 14 phonemes
- Isolating grammar (no inflection)
- Complex concepts must be expressed by combining simple elements

**Philosophical basis**: Partly inspired by Taoist philosophy, Toki Pona's goal is to help speakers focus on essentials by reducing complex concepts to basic elements. Lang has described it: "Toki Pona was my philosophical attempt to understand the meaning of life in 120 words." Based partly on the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis, it was designed to induce positive thinking by forcing speakers to reframe concepts in simple, direct terms.

**What Toki Pona reveals**: With only ~120 words, speakers must use extensive circumlocution and contextual interpretation. The word *jan* means person, people, human, humanity. The word *tomo* means building, house, room, indoor space. *tomo tawa* (moving building) means car. This extreme minimalism demonstrates that natural languages' large vocabularies are not excessive -- they enable precision. But Toki Pona also shows that communication is possible with remarkably few building blocks when speakers share context and are willing to negotiate meaning.

Sources: [Toki Pona - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toki_Pona), [Sonja Lang Roundtable - University of Colorado](https://www.colorado.edu/linguistics/2024/03/13/roundtable-sonja-lang-toki-pona-personal-art-project-small-world-language)

## 7. What Constructed Languages Reveal About Natural Language

### Redundancy Is Necessary (Not Waste)

Research comparing constructed and natural languages has shown that redundancy serves crucial cognitive purposes:

- The big visual and phonological difference between "I am" and "he is" makes them easy to tell apart quickly, despite being "irregular"
- Redundancy speeds up comprehension: redundant information makes processing faster, even in simple detection tasks
- Only common verbs "should" be irregular (and in most natural languages, they are -- with "to be" generally being the most common and most irregular verb). This strategic placement of irregularity suggests natural languages have evolved to optimize human processing
- As one researcher concluded: "Esperanto may be rational, but it is not a language for human beings, who are, after all, not rational." A language optimized for logical consistency is not optimized for human cognition

Sources: [What would a modern constructed language look like? - Cogsci.nl](https://cogsci.nl/blog/what-would-a-modern-constructed-language-look-like), [Linguistic Redundancy - University of Groningen](https://www.math.rug.nl/~ernst/linguistics/redundancy3.pdf)

---

### Irregularity Emerges Even in Designed Languages

Esperanto, despite being designed as completely regular, has developed irregularities through actual use:

- **Phonological change**: The near-loss of the sound *ĥ*, with the German-derived word *ĥino* ("Chinese") replaced by Italian/English-influenced *ĉino*, and most other instances of *ĥ* replaced with *k*
- **Nativization effects**: When children acquire Esperanto as a first language (there are approximately 2,000 native speakers), they show natural language acquisition effects including loss or modification of the accusative case, phonological reduction, attrition of the tense-aspect system, and pronominal cliticization
- **Creolization parallels**: The acquisition of Esperanto as a first language is comparable to creolization -- children acquiring a language that is by definition not the native language of their parents. These children introduce the same kinds of changes that occur in natural creolization processes

This finding is deeply significant: it demonstrates that linguistic irregularity is not a design flaw that can be engineered away. It emerges inevitably from the process of language use by real human beings. Regularity is an ideal of the drawing board; irregularity is a property of living language.

Sources: [Modern evolution of Esperanto - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_evolution_of_Esperanto), [Nativization processes in L1 Esperanto - UC San Diego](https://pages.ucsd.edu/~bkbergen/papers/NEJCL.pdf), [Native Esperanto as a test case for natural language - ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/49912635_Native_Esperanto_as_a_test_case_for_natural_language)

---

### Ambiguity Enables Efficiency

The research on polysemy and ambiguity in communication converges on a counterintuitive conclusion: ambiguity is not a bug but a feature of optimally efficient communication systems.

- Any efficient communication system will necessarily be ambiguous when context is informative about meaning
- Natural languages have evolved to leverage context for meaning disambiguation, making polysemy a rational feature rather than a design flaw
- Lojban demonstrates the cost of removing ambiguity: sentences must explicitly encode information that context would normally provide, making them significantly longer
- The theoretical basis: the units of an efficient communication system will not redundantly specify information already provided by context

**The information-theoretic perspective**: Natural language can be understood as a lossy compression algorithm. Ambiguity is the compression. Context is the decoder. Removing ambiguity is like transmitting uncompressed data -- technically possible, but wasteful of bandwidth. Human communication channels (speech, working memory, attention) have limited bandwidth, so compression (ambiguity) is not optional.

---

### Community Shapes Language Evolution Regardless of Design Intent

Every constructed language that gains a community of speakers begins to evolve in ways its creator did not intend and often tried to prevent:

- **Volapuk**: Schleyer's attempt to maintain control destroyed the community
- **Esperanto**: Despite Zamenhof's regular design, the language has evolved phonologically, morphologically, and syntactically through use
- **Klingon**: Designed for alien warriors, it expanded to cover bicycles, pineapples, and pandemics because its community needed those words
- **Toki Pona**: Although designed with ~120 words, the community has developed conventions and extended meanings that Lang later documented in an expanded dictionary

The pattern is universal: language belongs to its speakers, not its creator. Design intent is a starting condition, not a permanent constraint.

---

### You Cannot Separate Language from Culture

The deepest lesson of constructed languages is that language is not merely a tool for transmitting information -- it is a cultural artifact that encodes worldviews, social relationships, and shared history:

- Wilkins' taxonomy encoded 17th-century English assumptions about how knowledge should be organized
- Esperanto's vocabulary and grammar reflect European (specifically Indo-European) linguistic structures despite claims of universality
- Even Lojban's "culturally neutral" vocabulary, derived algorithmically from six source languages, still reflects the choice of which six languages to use
- Tolkien understood this most clearly: he built entire cultures to go with his languages, because languages without cultures are abstractions without life

---

## 8. The Recurring Pattern: "Perfection" Defeats Itself

Across the entire history of constructed languages, a single pattern recurs with striking consistency:

**Every attempt to make language "perfect" by removing its natural features fails, because those features are essential to how language actually works.**

| Feature Removed | Why It Was Removed | Why It's Actually Necessary |
|---|---|---|
| **Ambiguity** | Creates confusion, prevents precision | Enables efficient compression; context resolves most cases |
| **Irregularity** | Makes learning harder, seems arbitrary | Speeds processing of frequent forms; emerges naturally from use |
| **Polysemy** | One word should mean one thing | Reduces vocabulary burden; enables metaphorical extension |
| **Redundancy** | Wastes effort, adds no information | Provides error correction; speeds comprehension |
| **Cultural specificity** | Prevents universal adoption | Language without culture is a notation, not a means of thought |
| **Historical change** | Degrades the original design | Allows language to adapt to new needs and contexts |

The history of constructed languages is, paradoxically, the strongest argument for the design intelligence of natural languages. Natural languages are not poorly designed; they are brilliantly optimized for constraints (limited memory, noisy channels, cultural embeddedness, the need for change) that rational designers consistently underestimate.

## 11. Connections to Other Categories in This Deep Dive

- **Foundations of Linguistic Theory (Cat. 1)**: The philosophical language tradition directly engaged with questions about language universals and the relationship between language and thought
- **Semiotics and the Philosophy of Signs (Cat. 2)**: Wilkins' "Real Character" is a semiotic system where signs directly represent concepts without phonetic mediation -- pure ideography
- **Structural Typology (Cat. 5)**: Constructed languages experiment with typological features (agglutination in Esperanto, isolation in Toki Pona, predicate logic in Lojban) that illuminate the design space of possible language structures
- **Historical Linguistics and Language Change (Cat. 6)**: Tolkien deliberately simulated historical sound change in his languages; Esperanto's nativization shows real historical change happening to a designed language
- **The Sapir-Whorf Hypothesis (Cat. 10)**: Loglan was explicitly designed to test linguistic relativity; Toki Pona was designed to induce positive thinking through minimal vocabulary; Lojban's community reports cognitive effects
- **Pragmatics (Cat. 11)**: The failure of disambiguation in logical languages demonstrates that meaning always exceeds what is linguistically encoded -- pragmatic inference is not optional
