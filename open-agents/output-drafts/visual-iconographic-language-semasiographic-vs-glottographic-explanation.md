# Semasiographic vs. Glottographic Systems

Writing That Represents Meaning vs. Writing That Represents Speech -- and Why the Distinction Defines the Entire Visual Language Project

---

## 1. Introduction: The Fundamental Fork

Every writing system ever created by humans can be placed along a single axis: does it represent meaning directly, or does it encode the sounds of a specific spoken language?

This question, first systematized by Ignace Gelb in his 1952 *A Study of Writing*, is not a minor taxonomic detail. It defines the entire theoretical framework for any visual language project. A metaphor-based visual language that encodes image schemas and conceptual structures rather than the phonemes of English or Mandarin is, by definition, a **semasiographic** system -- writing that represents meaning directly, readable by speakers of any language without translation. The question is whether a semasiographic system can achieve the full expressive power of natural language. That has never been accomplished.

The term *semasiographic* derives from the Greek *semasia* (meaning) and *graphe* (writing): writing that speaks directly to the mind without detour through any spoken language. *Glottographic*, from *glotta* (tongue), is writing that encodes elements of a specific spoken language -- its words, syllables, or phonemes.

Understanding this distinction is the entry point to understanding what a visual language would be, what it would need to do, and why the problem is genuinely hard.

## 2. What Semasiographic Means

A semasiographic system represents meaning directly without encoding the sounds of any spoken language. Consider the expression:

**2 + 3 = 5**

A Japanese mathematician, a Brazilian engineer, and a Nigerian accountant all read this expression identically. No translation is needed. The notation is fully compositional -- novel expressions can be constructed freely. "2 + 3 = 5" is pure semasiography: it says what it means without passing through any particular language's sound system.

Mathematical notation is the most successful semasiographic system in human history. But it is domain-restricted. None of those three readers can use it to say "I'm feeling anxious about tomorrow's meeting."

Other functioning semasiographic systems include:

- **Musical notation**: A quarter note on the G line of a treble staff represents the same pitch and duration regardless of the reader's language. It is compositional -- notes combine into chords, measures, and phrases. But it is restricted to acoustic specification.
- **Chemical formulas**: H2O means the same thing in every language. Elements combine into compounds following bonding rules. But the domain is molecular structure.
- **Road signs**: The red octagon means STOP internationally (standardized through the Vienna Convention on Road Signs and Signals, 1968). Shape, color, and pictographic content encode meaning without words. But the vocabulary is fixed and closed.
- **Circuit diagrams**: Resistors, capacitors, and transistors are represented by universal symbols that combine into circuits. But the domain is electrical systems.

These systems succeed because they share four properties: (1) a restricted domain with clear boundaries, (2) shared context among users, (3) a finite or slowly growing vocabulary, and (4) precise, unambiguous semantics. The question driving the visual language project is whether these properties can be extended beyond domain restriction to general-purpose communication.

## 3. What Glottographic Means

A glottographic system represents elements of a specific spoken language. The Latin alphabet is glottographic: the letter sequence "c-a-t" encodes the English phonemes /k/, /ae/, /t/. You can only read it if you know English. The same three phonemes in French, German, or Swahili produce different written forms.

Glottographic systems come in several types, forming their own spectrum:

- **Logographic**: Each symbol represents a word or morpheme. Chinese characters, Sumerian cuneiform, and Egyptian hieroglyphs all have logographic elements -- though all three are actually mixed systems.
- **Syllabic**: Each symbol represents a syllable. Japanese kana, the Cherokee syllabary, and Linear B are syllabic systems.
- **Alphabetic**: Each symbol represents a phoneme. Latin, Greek, Cyrillic, and Hangul are alphabetic (though Hangul is sometimes classified as featural).
- **Abjad**: A consonant-only alphabet with optional vowel marking. Arabic and Hebrew are abjads.
- **Abugida**: A system where consonant-vowel units are the basic symbols, with vowels shown as modifications of the consonant base. Devanagari and Ethiopic are abugidas.

The key insight: all glottographic systems identify phonetic values. They are inherently tied to a specific spoken language or language family. A text in the Latin alphabet can only be "read" by someone who knows which spoken language it represents. But a semasiographic symbol -- 2, +, =, the red octagon, H2O, a quarter note -- can be read in any language.

## 4. The Spectrum, Not the Binary

The semasiographic-glottographic distinction is a continuum, not a binary switch. Most real writing systems blend both elements, and the most successful writing systems in history have been mixed.

### Chinese: The Master Case

Chinese characters are the most important example of a mixed system, and the most commonly misunderstood. Over 80% of Chinese characters are **semantic-phonetic compounds** -- each containing both a semasiographic element and a glottographic element within a single character.

Consider the character for "mother": **ma** (妈). It contains two components:

- **Semantic radical**: 女 (nu, "woman/female") -- this is the semasiographic component. It says "this character relates to women/femaleness." A Korean reader familiar with Chinese characters (hanja) can extract this meaning even though they pronounce the character completely differently. The semantic radical crosses language boundaries.
- **Phonetic radical**: 马 (ma, "horse") -- this is the glottographic component. It says "pronounce this character something like 'ma.'" This information is useless without knowing Mandarin pronunciation. The phonetic radical does not cross language boundaries.

The same pattern holds throughout: 河 (he, "river") = 氵(water, semasiographic) + 可 (ke, phonetic hint). 情 (qing, "emotion") = 忄(heart/mind, semasiographic) + 青 (qing, phonetic hint).

This mixed architecture evolved over millennia for good reason. The semantic radical provides cross-linguistic transparency -- anyone can guess that characters with the water radical relate to water. The phonetic radical provides the precision needed for a specific spoken language. Neither component alone is sufficient.

### Egyptian Hieroglyphs

Egyptian hieroglyphs combined logographic symbols, phonetic signs (uniliteral, biliteral, and triliteral), and **determinatives** -- purely semasiographic markers that specify a meaning domain without contributing to pronunciation. The "walking legs" determinative after a verb indicates physical movement. The "papyrus roll" determinative after a word indicates an abstract concept. These determinatives function exactly like Chinese semantic radicals.

### Pre-Columbian Mesoamerican Writing

Aztec and Maya notation systems blended semasiographic notation (pictographic scenes, calendric symbols, directional markers) with glottographic elements (phonetic signs representing syllables of Nahuatl or Mayan languages). Research on Teotihuacan notation has analyzed these systems as occupying a stable middle ground -- they were not "evolving toward" phonetic writing but deliberately maintaining both channels.

### Modern Emoji

Emoji function as a semasiographic overlay on glottographic text. "I'm going to the beach tomorrow" is glottographic. Adding a beach emoji creates a mixed expression. Emoji are the most widespread semasiographic system in modern use -- and also demonstrate the severe limitations of semasiography without grammar.

## 5. The Teleological Debate: Is Alphabetic Writing the "Goal"?

Gelb argued in 1952 that writing systems naturally evolve from semasiographic origins toward ever more precise phonetic encoding. In his model, semasiography was a primitive "forerunner" stage, and the Greek alphabet was the culmination -- the endpoint toward which all writing systems inevitably progressed. This is called the **teleological** view of writing development.

Modern scholarship has largely rejected this claim:

- **Chinese has not evolved toward an alphabet in over 3,000 years.** Despite extensive contact with alphabetic systems, Chinese remains logographic/mixed. If the teleological drive were real, Chinese would have adopted alphabetic writing long ago.
- **Mesoamerican mixed systems were stable, not transitional.** Pre-Columbian writing systems maintained both semasiographic and glottographic elements without "evolving" toward pure phonography.
- **Emoji represents movement in the reverse direction.** Modern digital communication is adding semasiographic elements to alphabetic text -- the opposite of Gelb's predicted trajectory.
- **Mathematical notation has expanded, not contracted.** The most powerful semasiographic system continues to grow in scope and precision without any pressure toward phonetic encoding.

The teleological view reflects a Western alphabetic bias that privileged the Greek alphabet as the "culmination" of writing development. The modern understanding recognizes multiple parallel stable states, with mixed systems as viable endpoints rather than intermediate stages.

This matters for visual language design because Gelb's teleology implies that semasiographic systems are inherently primitive and must eventually be replaced by phonetic writing. If that were true, designing a semasiographic visual language would be swimming against the current of history. But the modern view -- that semasiography, mixed systems, and phonography are all stable, viable architectures -- opens the design space.

## 6. The Generality Problem: Why No Semasiographic System Has Achieved Full Expressiveness

Natural language can express anything that can be thought. Novel propositions, counterfactual conditions, nested hypotheticals, emotional nuance, irony, poetry, metaphor, lies. This capacity is called **open-ended expressiveness**, and it depends on several properties that domain-restricted semasiographic systems lack.

### The Properties That Make Language Powerful

**Productivity**: The ability to create an unlimited number of novel expressions from a finite set of elements. This requires both compositionality (combining meaningful parts) and recursion (embedding structures within structures).

**Displacement**: The ability to refer to things not present -- past events, future possibilities, hypothetical scenarios, abstract concepts. "If Napoleon had won at Waterloo..." is displacement in action.

**Metalinguistic capacity**: The ability to talk about the language itself. "I didn't mean that literally" is a metalinguistic statement.

**Pragmatic flexibility**: The ability to mean different things with the same expression depending on context. "Nice weather we're having" means the opposite during a hurricane.

### Why Domain Restriction Enables Semasiographic Success

Mathematical notation achieves compositionality and productivity within its domain because the domain itself constrains interpretation. "2 + 3" can only mean arithmetic addition because the domain excludes other interpretations. General-purpose communication lacks this constraint -- the same visual symbol must function in contexts ranging from cooking instructions to philosophical arguments to love letters.

This is the fundamental asymmetry: domain-restricted semasiographic systems succeed precisely because they are restricted. Removing the restriction removes the constraint that makes unambiguous interpretation possible. A general-purpose semasiographic system must find a different source of interpretive constraint -- and the candidate proposed by this project is the systematic structure of conceptual metaphor and image schemas.

### The Counterfactual Test

A simple test exposes the expressiveness gap. Attempt to express this sentence in various semasiographic systems:

**"If it hadn't rained yesterday, we would have gone hiking."**

- Mathematical notation: impossible. Not in the domain.
- Road signs: impossible. Not in the domain.
- Emoji: hopelessly ambiguous. Rain-cloud + X + hiking-boot + mountain could mean "no rain, go hiking" or "rain stopped the hike" or "don't hike in rain."
- Blissymbolics: possible but extremely cumbersome, requiring multiple indicator stacks for counterfactual past conditional.
- Natural language: trivial.

This sentence requires temporal displacement (yesterday), negation (hadn't), conditionality (if), and irrealis mood (would have). Any system that cannot express these is not a general-purpose language.

## 7. The Sign Language Counter-Argument

A natural objection arises: sign languages are visual-spatial communication systems, and they achieve full expressiveness. Doesn't this prove that semasiographic systems can be fully expressive?

The answer requires a careful distinction.

Sign languages -- American Sign Language, British Sign Language, Japanese Sign Language, and hundreds of others -- are indeed full natural languages with complete grammars. They have syntax, morphology, pragmatics, and poetry, all encoded in the visual-spatial modality through handshape, movement, location, and facial expression. They achieve full displacement, productivity, metalinguistic capacity, and pragmatic flexibility.

But sign languages are **not** semasiographic systems. They are full natural languages that happen to use the visual-spatial modality instead of the vocal-auditory modality. They have phonology (formational parameters that function like phonemes). They are language-specific (ASL and BSL are mutually unintelligible, despite both being used in English-speaking countries). They are tied to communities rather than universally transparent.

A semasiographic visual writing system would need to be readable without prior knowledge of any specific sign language. It would need to represent meaning directly, not encode the formational parameters of a particular sign language.

**What sign languages prove** is not that semasiography can be fully expressive, but something more fundamental: that the visual-spatial modality has the bandwidth and structural resources for full linguistic expressiveness. The limitation of semasiographic writing is not a limitation of the modality. It is a design limitation -- a problem that has not yet been solved, not a problem that cannot be solved.

## 8. The Three Grounding Strategies

There are three fundamentally different ways a visual symbol can acquire meaning:

### Strategy 1: Convention

An arbitrary symbol is given meaning by social agreement. The dollar sign ($) means "dollar" not because it looks like a dollar but because everyone agrees it does. Musical sharps, mathematical operators, and traffic signs all rely on convention.

**Strength**: Can represent anything, including abstract concepts.
**Weakness**: Must be learned. Not cross-culturally transparent.

### Strategy 2: Iconicity

A visual resemblance links the symbol to its referent. A picture of a house means "house" because it looks like a house.

**Strength**: Immediately recognizable. Low learning cost for concrete objects.
**Weakness**: Fails for abstract concepts. "Justice," "if," "because" have no visual form to resemble.

### Strategy 3: Experiential Grounding

Image schemas from embodied experience provide the visual vocabulary, and metaphorical extension maps those schemas onto abstract meaning domains. The CONTAINER schema (interior, boundary, exterior) arises from the bodily experience of being in and out of enclosed spaces. Through primary metaphor, it extends to abstract domains: we are "in" trouble, "in" love, "in" a good mood. We "contain" our emotions. Ideas are "in" our heads.

**Strength**: Combines the language-independence of semasiography with the expressiveness potential of natural language. Image schemas are pre-linguistic, arising from embodied experience shared by all humans.
**Weakness**: The metaphorical mappings must be consistent and learnable. Not all abstract concepts have obvious experiential grounding.

The hypothesis driving the visual language project is that experiential grounding -- the third strategy -- can bridge the gap between domain-restricted semasiographic success and general-purpose expressiveness. Image schemas are both visual (they have spatial structure) and abstract (they extend metaphorically). If the metaphorical extensions are systematic and cross-culturally stable, a visual language built on image schemas could be both semasiographic and expressively general.

This remains a hypothesis. No one has built and tested such a system. But the theoretical framework -- combining NSM's semantic primitives, Johnson's image schemas, and Lakoff's conceptual metaphor theory -- provides the most principled approach to the problem that has ever been available.

## 9. Programming Languages: The Closest Existing Model

One category of semasiographic system deserves special attention: programming languages.

Consider the symbol `+` in the expression `x = a + b`. This symbol means addition in C, Python, Java, Ruby, JavaScript, and dozens of other languages. It is partially semasiographic -- the operator carries meaning independent of any spoken language. Variable names and keywords are more conventional, but the structural operations are language-independent.

Programming languages achieve enormous expressiveness within their domain through precisely the mechanism that general-purpose semasiographic systems lack: **formal grammar and compositionality**. A programming language has a complete grammar (syntax rules), semantic compositionality (the meaning of a complex expression is determined by its parts and how they combine), productivity (unlimited novel programs can be written), and even a form of displacement (programs can reference future events, conditional states, and hypothetical branches through control flow).

The parallel to visual language design is strong but underexplored:

- Programming languages prove that formal grammar enables semasiographic expressiveness far beyond what pictographic systems achieve.
- They demonstrate that arbitrary conventional symbols (curly braces, semicolons, parentheses) become completely natural to trained users.
- They show that compositionality can scale to enormous complexity when the grammar is well-designed.
- They also demonstrate the cost: programming languages require significant training, and their expressiveness is domain-restricted (they can compute anything but cannot express "I miss you" or "the sunset was beautiful").

The question for visual language design is whether the expressiveness model of programming languages -- formal grammar over conventional symbols -- can be adapted for general-purpose human communication by replacing arbitrary symbols with image-schema-grounded visual primitives.

## 10. Design Requirements Derived from the Framework

The semasiographic-glottographic distinction yields seven specific design requirements for a visual language:

1. **Compositionality**: Must combine primitives to produce unlimited novel expressions, as mathematics does for quantities.

2. **Grammar**: Must encode relationships between symbols -- agent, patient, action, time, condition, negation, scope -- not just list symbols side by side.

3. **Abstractability**: Must represent abstract concepts (justice, possibility, irony) not just concrete objects (house, tree, person). This is the critical gap between a picture dictionary and a language.

4. **Displacement**: Must reference past, future, hypothetical, and counterfactual situations. Without displacement, the system is stuck in the here-and-now.

5. **Productivity**: Must create new meanings for new situations without requiring new symbols for each concept. The combinatorial machinery must be generative.

6. **Phonetic bridge**: Must handle proper names, borrowed words, and technical terminology through some subsystem. This is the phoneme representation problem -- the point where semasiography must interface with the glottographic world. Chinese's mixed architecture (semantic radicals + phonetic radicals) evolved this solution over millennia. Any new visual language will need its own version.

7. **Learnability**: Must be acquirable in reasonable time. Chinese characters demand years of study. A new system must be more efficient -- and experiential grounding (image schemas that feel natural because they arise from shared bodily experience) may provide the key to rapid acquisition.

## 11. Edge Cases and Exceptions

### Emoji in Legal Contexts

Courts and employers increasingly treat emoji as communicative acts with real semantic content. A Canadian court ruled that a thumbs-up emoji constituted agreement to a contract. This demonstrates that even a limited semasiographic system can acquire pragmatic force in context -- but the meaning is always context-dependent and disputable, precisely because emoji lack grammar. The thumbs-up was interpreted as agreement only because the surrounding glottographic text established what was being agreed to.

### Aztec and Maya Mixed Notation

Pre-Columbian Mesoamerican writing systems combined semasiographic elements (pictographic scenes, calendric notation, directional symbols) with glottographic syllabic signs. Research on Teotihuacan notation has analyzed these as stable mixed systems that deliberately maintained both channels rather than "evolving" toward pure phonography. This provides historical evidence against Gelb's teleology and historical evidence for the viability of mixed semasiographic-glottographic architectures.

### The Curious Case of Musical Notation

Musical notation is semasiographic for pitch and duration but requires verbal annotation for expression and interpretation. "Allegro con brio" is glottographic Italian. "This passage should feel nostalgic" is ordinary language. Music notation thus demonstrates both the power of domain-restricted semasiography and the point where domain restriction forces a return to glottographic communication. The boundary between what can and cannot be semasiographically expressed is informative for visual language design: the things music notation cannot express (emotional quality, aesthetic intention, cultural context) are precisely the things that require the grammatical and metaphorical machinery of natural language.

## 12. Where the Proposed Visual Language Sits

The proposed visual language aims to sit near the semasiographic end of the spectrum but not at the extreme. It would be **primarily semasiographic** -- representing meaning through image schemas and conceptual structures rather than encoding any spoken language's phonemes. But it would include a **phonetic bridge subsystem** for proper names and borrowed terms, making it a mixed system -- exactly the architecture that Chinese evolved, but designed from scratch rather than accumulated over millennia.

The theoretical grounding comes from three complementary frameworks:

- **NSM's 65 semantic primes**: The irreducible atoms of meaning, validated across 30+ languages. These define what must be representable.
- **Image schemas**: The pre-linguistic spatial-bodily structures (CONTAINER, PATH, FORCE, BALANCE, UP-DOWN) that ground abstract thought in physical experience. These provide the visual vocabulary.
- **Conceptual metaphor theory**: The systematic mappings that extend spatial schemas to abstract domains (HAPPY IS UP, KNOWING IS SEEING, PURPOSES ARE DESTINATIONS). These bridge the gap between visual and abstract.

Whether this approach can succeed where all previous attempts have failed is an empirical question. But the semasiographic-glottographic framework makes the design space clear, the historical record identifies the specific challenges, and the theoretical tools are more powerful than any previous generation of visual language designers has had available.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Semasiography | Writing that represents meaning directly without encoding the sounds of a spoken language; also called thought writing or ideographic writing |
| Glottography | Writing that represents elements of a specific spoken language (words, syllables, phonemes); also called phonographic writing |
| Logographic | Glottographic writing where symbols represent words or morphemes |
| Syllabic | Glottographic writing where symbols represent syllables; also called syllabary |
| Alphabetic | Glottographic writing where symbols represent phonemes; also called segmental writing |
| Abjad | Consonant-only alphabet with optional vowel marking; also called consonantary (Arabic, Hebrew) |
| Abugida | Writing system where consonant-vowel units are the basic symbols, with vowels shown as modifications; also called alphasyllabary (Devanagari, Ethiopic) |
| Determinative | A semasiographic element within a glottographic system that specifies meaning domain without contributing to pronunciation |
| Semantic radical | In Chinese characters, the component that indicates the meaning domain of the character (semasiographic function) |
| Phonetic radical | In Chinese characters, the component that hints at pronunciation (glottographic function) |
| Gelb's teleology | The now-contested claim that writing systems inevitably evolve from semasiographic to phonographic representation |
| Productivity | The ability of a language system to create unlimited novel expressions from finite elements |
| Displacement | The ability to refer to things not present in the immediate context (past, future, hypothetical, counterfactual) |
| Open-ended expressiveness | The capacity to express anything that can be thought, including novel propositions never before expressed |
| Domain restriction | The property of a semasiographic system that limits its expressiveness to a particular subject area |
