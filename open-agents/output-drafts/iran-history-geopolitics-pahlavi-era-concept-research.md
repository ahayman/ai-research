# The Pahlavi Dynasty: Modernization, Oil & the 1953 Coup (1925–1979)

## 1. Reza Shah Pahlavi: The Founder (1925–1941)

### Rise to Power

Reza Khan, a commanding officer of the Persian Cossack Brigade, seized control of the government in a 1921 coup alongside journalist Seyyed Zia al-Din Tabatabaei. By 1923 he had become prime minister, and in 1925 the Constituent Assembly deposed the Qajar dynasty and crowned him Reza Shah Pahlavi. Iran was on the verge of bankruptcy when he took the throne; the country had experienced virtually no modern economic development.

### The Modernization Program

Reza Shah launched the most ambitious top-down modernization campaign in Iranian history, consciously modeled on Mustafa Kemal Ataturk's reforms in Turkey:

**Infrastructure**: The centerpiece was the Trans-Iranian Railway, a massive engineering project linking the Caspian Sea to the Persian Gulf. Highway mileage expanded from roughly 2,000 to 14,000 miles. Modern industrial plants increased 17-fold (excluding oil installations). Ports, telegraph lines, and new government buildings were constructed across the country.

**Education**: Reza Shah established free, compulsory education for both males and females. He shut down private religious schools — Islamic, Christian, Jewish, and others — replacing them with a secular state curriculum. The University of Tehran, Iran's first modern university, was founded in 1934. Hundreds of students were sent to European universities on government scholarships.

**Legal Reform**: Between 1925 and 1928, he replaced sharia (Islamic religious law) with civil codes modeled on French law. State courts were created, stripping the clergy of their traditional judicial authority. Registration of documents, marriages, and births was transferred from religious to secular authorities.

**Bureaucratic Centralization**: He founded a standing army of 100,000 men (previously, shahs relied on tribal forces rewarded with plunder) and a 90,000-person civil service. The goal was to forge primary loyalty to the Iranian nation-state rather than to tribe, clan, or ethnic community.

**Women's Rights**: In 1936, Reza Shah banned the chador and all forms of veiling in public — the so-called "Kashf-e Hijab" decree. Women were encouraged to enter the workforce and attend universities. While progressive in intent, the forced unveiling provoked deep resentment among devout families.

### Suppression of Tribal and Clerical Power

**Tribal Subjugation**: Reza Shah viewed nomadic tribes as the primary impediment to a modern centralized state. Beginning in 1933, he embarked on a policy of forced sedentarization: grazing lands were confiscated, tribal leaders were imprisoned or executed, and nomadic movements were restricted by military force. The Bakhtiari, Qashqai, Lur, Kurd, and Baluchi tribes bore the heaviest burdens. Many communities were devastated — deprived of their traditional livelihoods and forced into unfamiliar agricultural settlements.

**Clerical Marginalization**: The new school system, codified laws, and secular courts systematically undercut clerical influence. In a notorious 1928 incident, Reza Shah personally entered the sanctuary of Qom's Fatima Masumeh Shrine to beat a cleric who had admonished the Shah's wife for briefly exposing her face during a pilgrimage. This act of violence in Islam's holiest Iranian site symbolized the Shah's contempt for clerical authority. By the mid-1930s, intense dissatisfaction among the Shia clergy was widespread, though it remained largely suppressed.

### The Name Change: From Persia to Iran (1935)

On March 21, 1935 (Nowruz, the Persian New Year), Reza Shah officially requested that all foreign governments use the name "Iran" rather than "Persia" in diplomatic correspondence. The motivations were multiple:

- **National identity and modernization**: "Iran" (meaning "Land of the Aryans") was what Iranians had always called their country. "Persia" was a Greek-derived exonym referencing only one province (Pars/Fars). The change sought to align the country's international identity with its internal self-conception.
- **Multiethnic representation**: Scholar Mohammad Ali Foroughi and others argued that "Iranian" was a more neutral, unifying term for the country's diverse ethnic groups — Persians, Azeris, Kurds, Baluch, Arabs, Turkmen, and others — whereas "Persia" was geographically confusing and ethnically exclusive.
- **Break with the past**: The name change was part of Reza Shah's broader project of distancing the country from the weakness and perceived decadence of the Qajar era.
- **Aryanist ideology**: Some historians note the influence of contemporary European racial theories, though this was secondary to nationalist motivations.

## 2. World War II: The Anglo-Soviet Invasion and Forced Abdication (1941)

### Reza Shah's Neutrality and German Ties

When World War II erupted, Iran declared neutrality. However, Reza Shah had cultivated significant economic ties with Nazi Germany — German technicians and advisors worked throughout Iran, and trade between the two countries had grown substantially during the 1930s. This was partly a pragmatic counterbalance to British and Russian influence, but it alarmed the Allies.

### The Invasion (August 25, 1941)

Britain and the Soviet Union invaded Iran on August 25, 1941, in a coordinated assault: British and Australian naval forces attacked from the Persian Gulf while Soviet troops invaded from the north. The principal stated purposes were:

1. To secure the Trans-Iranian Railway as a supply corridor to the Soviet Union (the "Persian Corridor"), which was under intense pressure from the German invasion (Operation Barbarossa had begun in June 1941).
2. To expel German nationals residing in Iran who could potentially sabotage the strategically vital Abadan oil refinery.
3. To secure Iranian oil supplies for the Allied war effort.

Iran's military resistance collapsed within days. The invasion was a flagrant violation of Iranian sovereignty and neutrality.

### Forced Abdication (September 16, 1941)

The British forced Reza Shah to abdicate in favor of his 21-year-old son, Mohammad Reza Pahlavi. Reza Shah was taken to Mauritius by the British, then moved to Durban and finally to Johannesburg, South Africa, where he died on July 26, 1944, at age 66 of a heart ailment. The young Mohammad Reza Shah was installed on the throne on September 16, 1941 — a fact that would forever taint his legitimacy in the eyes of many Iranians, who saw him as a ruler imposed by foreign powers.

### Wartime Occupation and Its Legacy

During the occupation (1941–1946), Iran was effectively divided into British and Soviet zones of influence. The economy suffered severe inflation and food shortages. The occupation demonstrated Iran's vulnerability to great-power politics and deepened resentment of foreign interference — sentiments that would fuel both the oil nationalization movement and, eventually, the 1979 revolution.

## 3. Post-War Oil Politics and the Rise of Mossadegh

### The Anglo-Iranian Oil Company (AIOC)

The Anglo-Iranian Oil Company (AIOC, formerly the Anglo-Persian Oil Company, later British Petroleum/BP) had held a monopoly concession on Iranian oil since 1901 under the D'Arcy Concession. The terms were extraordinarily unfavorable to Iran:

- The British government received more revenue from taxing AIOC than the Iranian government received in royalties.
- By 1948, Britain's tax revenue from AIOC substantially exceeded Iran's share.
- AIOC refused to allow an audit of its accounts so Iran could verify whether it was receiving the contracted royalties.
- AIOC refused to offer the 50-50 profit-sharing arrangement that had become standard in other Persian Gulf concessions (notably the deal between ARAMCO and Saudi Arabia in 1950).
- Working conditions for Iranian laborers in the Abadan refinery were notoriously poor — workers lived in company towns with substandard housing while British employees enjoyed separate, privileged facilities.

### Mohammad Mossadegh and the Nationalization Movement

Mohammad Mossadegh, a Swiss-educated lawyer, aristocrat, and ardent nationalist, became the leader of the National Front (Jebhe-ye Melli), a coalition of nationalist parties. He articulated a simple but powerful thesis: no country could be politically independent unless it first achieved economic independence, and Iran could never be economically independent while a foreign company controlled its most valuable resource.

**The Nationalization Vote**: On March 15, 1951, the Iranian Majlis (parliament) voted unanimously to nationalize the oil industry. On April 28, 1951, Mossadegh was appointed prime minister by the Shah. On May 1, the nationalization law took effect, and the National Iranian Oil Company (NIOC) was created to replace AIOC.

**Mossadegh's Goals**:
- Establish Iranian sovereignty over Iranian natural resources
- Audit AIOC's books to verify royalty payments
- Achieve equitable revenue sharing
- End the neo-colonial relationship whereby a foreign company functioned as a state-within-a-state on Iranian soil
- Use oil revenues for Iranian development rather than enriching the British Treasury

## 4. The 1953 Coup: Operation Ajax/Boot — The Pivotal Event

### British Response to Nationalization

The British government, led by Prime Minister Winston Churchill and Foreign Secretary Anthony Eden, responded to nationalization with fury. Their countermeasures included:

- **Economic embargo**: A worldwide boycott of Iranian oil, enforced by the Royal Navy, which threatened any tanker that attempted to purchase Iranian crude.
- **Diplomatic pressure**: Britain took the case to the International Court of Justice and the UN Security Council (both ruled in Iran's favor or declined jurisdiction).
- **Covert propaganda**: MI6 launched a propaganda campaign inside Iran to portray Mossadegh as incompetent, mentally unstable, and dangerously sympathetic to communism.
- **Economic warfare**: The embargo devastated Iran's economy, as oil revenues collapsed almost entirely.

### US Involvement: Cold War Fears and the Dulles Brothers

Initially, the Truman administration was sympathetic to Iranian nationalism and tried to mediate between Britain and Iran. President Truman recognized the legitimacy of Iran's grievances and resisted British pressure for intervention.

Everything changed with Eisenhower's inauguration in January 1953. The new president appointed **John Foster Dulles** as Secretary of State and **Allen Dulles** as CIA Director — the first and only time in American history that two brothers simultaneously ran the overt and covert sides of U.S. foreign policy. Both were fervent anti-communists with deep connections to the international business establishment.

The British played masterfully on American Cold War anxieties, arguing that:
- Mossadegh was unstable and incapable of governing
- Iran's economy was collapsing under the oil embargo
- The Tudeh Party (Iran's communist party) would exploit the chaos to seize power
- Iran would become a Soviet satellite, giving Moscow control of Persian Gulf oil

By April 1953, Allen Dulles had authorized an initial $1 million for the CIA's Tehran station to use "in any way that would bring about the fall of Mossadegh." John Foster Dulles provided the diplomatic cover and political messaging.

### How the Coup Was Executed

**Operation Ajax** (CIA) and **Operation Boot** (MI6) were coordinated through CIA operative **Kermit Roosevelt Jr.** (grandson of Theodore Roosevelt), who entered Iran covertly and directed operations from Tehran.

**Preparatory Phase**:
- Roosevelt bribed newspaper editors and journalists to publish anti-Mossadegh propaganda
- He recruited allies among the Islamic clergy, paying mullahs to denounce Mossadegh from the pulpit
- He hired street gang leaders (lutis) to stage provocative demonstrations
- He convinced the reluctant Shah to sign firmans (royal decrees) dismissing Mossadegh and appointing **General Fazlollah Zahedi** as prime minister — it took nearly three weeks of pressure before the Shah agreed
- Networks of agents were established within the military

**The Failed First Attempt (August 15–16, 1953)**:
On the night of August 15, Colonel Nematollah Nassiri attempted to deliver the Shah's firman dismissing Mossadegh. But Mossadegh had been tipped off; Nassiri was arrested. The initial coup failed. The Shah panicked and fled the country — first to Baghdad, then to Rome. Most of the Iranian conspirators went into hiding. CIA headquarters in Washington cabled Roosevelt to abort the operation.

**The Improvised Success (August 19, 1953)**:
Roosevelt refused to give up. Over the next three days, he improvised:
- CIA agents distributed copies of the Shah's firman (dismissing Mossadegh) to newspapers and the public, framing the situation as Mossadegh defying a legitimate royal decree
- Paid agents spread fear that the Tudeh Party was about to seize power
- Street mobs were organized and paid to create chaos — some posed as Tudeh supporters, attacking mosques and public figures to provoke a backlash
- Pro-Shah demonstrations were organized, with crowds marching through Tehran chanting support for the monarchy
- Military units loyal to the conspirators were activated

On August 19, armored vehicles rolled through Tehran. Pro-Shah mobs, military units, and paid demonstrators converged on government buildings. By evening, Mossadegh's residence had been shelled and overrun. Approximately 300 people died in the street fighting. Mossadegh was arrested (he would be tried, convicted, and spend the rest of his life under house arrest until his death in 1967). General Zahedi was installed as prime minister, and the Shah returned triumphantly from Rome on August 22.

### Immediate Aftermath

- **The Shah restored with expanded power**: The young Shah, who had been largely a constitutional monarch, now became an absolute ruler. The experience of nearly losing his throne radicalized him — he would never again tolerate independent political forces.
- **The 1954 Oil Consortium Agreement**: The nationalization was effectively reversed. A new international consortium was created: British Petroleum retained 40%, five major American companies (Exxon, Mobil, Standard Oil of California, Texaco, and Gulf) received 40% collectively (8% each), Royal Dutch Shell got 14%, and Compagnie Francaise des Petroles received 6%. Iran now received a 50-50 profit split — ironically, what Mossadegh had initially sought before AIOC's intransigence drove him to full nationalization. The National Iranian Oil Company nominally owned the oil and facilities, but the consortium operated and managed production.
- **Mossadegh's fate**: Tried by a military tribunal, sentenced to three years in prison followed by house arrest for life. He died in 1967, never leaving his village of Ahmadabad.
- **Suppression of political parties**: The National Front was banned, the Tudeh Party was crushed, and independent political activity was systematically eliminated.

### Long-Term Consequences — Why the 1953 Coup Matters

The 1953 coup is arguably **the single most consequential event** in modern Iranian-American relations:

1. **Destroyed Iranian democracy**: Mossadegh's government, for all its flaws, represented a genuine experiment in parliamentary democracy. The coup extinguished it.
2. **Created the authoritarian Shah**: The frightened, insecure young monarch who returned from Rome would build one of the most repressive police states in the Middle East.
3. **Planted the seeds of 1979**: The memory of the coup — of American and British interference overthrowing a democratic government for oil — became a foundational grievance of the revolutionary movement 26 years later.
4. **Established the template**: The coup demonstrated that the West would prioritize its economic and strategic interests over Iranian sovereignty and democracy.
5. **Legitimized anti-Americanism**: For generations of Iranians, the coup proved that the United States was not a champion of freedom but an imperial power willing to destroy democracy to control resources.
6. **CIA acknowledgment**: In 2013, the CIA officially acknowledged its role in the coup for the first time, releasing declassified documents. In 2023, the National Security Archive at George Washington University published additional documents confirming the operational details.

## 5. Post-Coup Iran: The Shah's Authoritarian Modernization (1953–1979)

### SAVAK: The Instrument of Repression

In 1957, with direct assistance from the CIA and Israel's Mossad, the Shah established SAVAK (Sazeman-e Ettelaat va Amniyat-e Keshvar — Organization of Intelligence and National Security). SAVAK grew to over 5,300 full-time agents and an unknown but large network of part-time informers who penetrated every level of Iranian society.

SAVAK became synonymous with political repression:
- Monitored all opposition groups, from communists to religious conservatives
- Arrested, tortured, and murdered thousands of dissidents
- Used methods including electric shock, sleep deprivation, extraction of nails and teeth, and mock executions
- Censored the press, controlled universities, and infiltrated labor unions
- Time magazine described it at the Shah's overthrow (February 19, 1979) as "Iran's most hated and feared institution"

### The White Revolution (1963)

In January 1963, the Shah launched the White Revolution (Enqelab-e Sefid), a sweeping reform program with six initial principles that eventually expanded to nineteen. Key elements included:

- **Land reform**: Breaking up large feudal estates and redistributing land to peasant farmers. This was the most significant element — by 1971, roughly 2.5 million peasant families had received land.
- **Women's suffrage**: Women gained the right to vote and run for office.
- **Nationalization of forests and pastures**
- **Profit-sharing for industrial workers**
- **Literacy Corps**: Young volunteers sent to rural villages to teach reading and writing
- **Health Corps**: Medical workers deployed to underserved rural areas

**Consequences and Failures**:
- Land reform parcels were often too small and lacked water, credit, or technical support, making many new landowners unable to farm effectively
- Millions of dispossessed peasants migrated to urban slums, becoming an uprooted, alienated underclass that would later form the foot soldiers of the revolution
- The program alienated the clergy (who lost religious endowment lands), the landlord class, and the bazaar merchants
- The top-down nature of the reforms, combined with the absence of political participation, meant reforms were imposed without consent

### Khomeini's Exile (1964)

Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini, a senior Shia cleric based in Qom, emerged as the most vocal critic of the White Revolution. In June 1963, he delivered a fiery sermon attacking the Shah, calling him a "wretched miserable man" who had "embarked on the destruction of Islam in Iran." His arrest triggered three days of rioting (the 15 Khordad uprising) — the first mass religious-political protest against the Shah, in which hundreds were killed.

The decisive break came on October 26, 1964, when Khomeini publicly denounced the Shah for granting "capitulations" — diplomatic immunity to American military personnel stationed in Iran. This was a deeply resonant issue: the capitulations echoed the humiliating privileges that European powers had extracted from Iran in the 19th century. Khomeini thundered: "They have reduced the Iranian people to a level lower than that of an American dog."

Khomeini was arrested in November 1964 and sent into exile — first to Turkey (November 1964), then to Najaf, Iraq (October 1965), where he would remain for 13 years, developing his revolutionary political theology.

### Ali Shariati: The Intellectual Foundation

While Khomeini provided the clerical leadership, **Ali Shariati** (1933–1977) provided the intellectual framework that made the Islamic revolution appeal to educated, modern Iranians. Shariati was a Sorbonne-educated sociologist who synthesized Marxist revolutionary theory with Shia Islamic theology:

- He reinterpreted Shia history — particularly the martyrdom of Imam Hussain at Karbala — as a narrative of revolutionary resistance against tyranny
- He presented Islam as "the one true liberator of the Third World from oppressive colonialism, neo-colonialism, and capitalism"
- He argued that true Islam was inherently revolutionary and egalitarian, not the quietist faith of the traditional clergy
- His lectures at the Hosseiniyeh Ershad in Tehran drew thousands of young, educated Iranians
- He was arrested, imprisoned, and eventually fled to England, where he died under suspicious circumstances in 1977 (many Iranians blamed SAVAK)

Shariati's vision gave the revolution an intellectual legitimacy that pure clerical authority could not. Ironically, his ideas were later suppressed by the Islamic Republic he helped inspire — his democratic, pluralistic interpretation of Islam was incompatible with Khomeini's theocratic state.

### The Oil Boom and Its Discontents (1970s)

The 1973 oil crisis and the subsequent quadrupling of oil prices transformed Iran. The Shah — now styling himself "King of Kings, Light of the Aryans" — poured oil wealth into:

- A massive military buildup (Iran became the largest purchaser of American arms)
- Industrialization and petrochemical projects
- The lavish 1971 celebration of 2,500 years of Persian monarchy at Persepolis — a $200 million extravaganza that offended many Iranians
- Rapid urbanization and Western-style consumer culture

**Per capita income skyrocketed**, but the benefits were distributed extremely unevenly. A new wealthy elite — connected to the royal court, the military, and international business — flourished, while urban migrants from failed rural land reform lived in sprawling slums. Corruption was rampant. Inflation soared. The gap between the Westernized elite and the traditional majority widened into a chasm.

### The Growing Opposition Coalition

By the mid-1970s, an unusually broad coalition of opposition had formed:

- **Leftists**: The Tudeh Party (communist), Fedaiyan-e Khalq (Marxist guerrillas), and Mojahedin-e Khalq (Islamist-Marxist guerrillas) all conducted armed resistance
- **Nationalists**: Followers of the Mossadegh tradition, organized around the National Front and the Freedom Movement of Iran (led by Mehdi Bazargan)
- **Islamists**: Khomeini's clerical network, operating through mosques, seminaries, and religious networks that SAVAK could never fully penetrate
- **Bazaar merchants (Bazaaris)**: Traditionally the financial backbone of the clergy, they resented the Shah's economic policies, which favored modern corporations and Western businesses over the traditional bazaar economy
- **Students and intellectuals**: Radicalized by SAVAK repression and inspired by Shariati's ideas

### The Carter Factor (1977–1978)

President Jimmy Carter's inauguration in January 1977 introduced a new variable. Carter made human rights a centerpiece of American foreign policy, and his administration began pressing the Shah to liberalize. The Shah, who depended on American support, made partial concessions:

- Eased censorship
- Released some political prisoners
- Allowed limited political organizing

These concessions inadvertently opened the floodgates. Pent-up grievances that had been suppressed for decades erupted. But Carter's signals were also contradictory — he toasted the Shah at a 1977 state dinner as ruling "an island of stability" while simultaneously pressuring him on human rights. The Shah received mixed messages and became paralyzed by indecision.

### The Final Year: Revolution (1978–1979)

**January 1978**: A government-planted newspaper article attacking Khomeini by name (calling him a British agent and a closet homosexual) triggered protests in Qom. Security forces killed several seminary students — igniting a cycle of mourning and protest that would escalate throughout the year, following the Shia 40-day mourning cycle (arba'in).

**August 19, 1978**: The Cinema Rex fire in Abadan killed between 377 and 470 people when arsonists barred the doors of a movie theater and set it ablaze. The fire was later attributed to Islamic militants inspired by a local cleric who viewed the cinema as a symbol of Western decadence. However, the revolutionary movement blamed SAVAK, and the general atmosphere of mistrust made this accusation widely believed. The Cinema Rex fire became a powerful rallying point for the revolution.

**September 8, 1978 — Black Friday**: The Shah declared martial law. Thousands of protesters gathered in Tehran's Jaleh Square, many unaware of the martial law decree. Security forces opened fire, killing approximately 64 civilians and injuring 205 (opposition claims of "thousands dead" were widely believed at the time, though later investigations suggest lower figures). Black Friday destroyed any remaining possibility of compromise between the regime and the opposition. It was the point of no return.

**October–December 1978**: Strikes paralyzed the country. Oil workers shut down production — the ultimate weapon against a petrostate. The bazaars closed. The Shia mourning cycle of protests, funerals, and more protests created an inexorable momentum. Khomeini, now based in Paris (he had been expelled from Iraq under Shah's pressure in October 1978), gave interviews to the world's media, presenting himself as a moderate democratic figure who sought to end tyranny.

**The Shah's Cancer**: Unknown to most of the world, the Shah had been secretly diagnosed with lymphoma in 1974. By 1978 he was receiving chemotherapy. The disease contributed to his passivity and indecision during the crucial final months.

**The Huyser Mission (January 1979)**: In a last-ditch effort, President Carter sent Air Force General Robert Huyser to Tehran to assess whether the Iranian military could hold the situation together and, as a last resort, whether a military coup was feasible. Huyser spent 31 days meeting with Iranian generals ("Group Five"), but the mission revealed deep fractures: senior officers were loyal to the Shah but psychologically dependent on him; middle-ranking and junior officers came from the same social classes as the protesters and shared their grievances. The mission failed.

**January 16, 1979**: The Shah left Iran, ostensibly on "vacation." He would never return. He departed leaving behind a Regency Council and opposition politician Shapour Bakhtiar as prime minister — a last-ditch attempt at a constitutional transition that satisfied no one.

The Shah wandered in exile — Egypt, Morocco, the Bahamas, Mexico, the United States (for cancer treatment, which would trigger the hostage crisis), Panama, and finally Egypt again, where President Anwar Sadat offered him sanctuary. He died in Cairo on July 27, 1980, at age 60.

### Key Foreign Actors Throughout the Pahlavi Era

**United States**:
- **Eisenhower** (1953–1961): Authorized the coup; established the Shah as a Cold War ally
- **Kennedy/Johnson** (1961–1969): Pushed the Shah toward reform (White Revolution); tolerated growing authoritarianism
- **Nixon** (1969–1974): The "Twin Pillars" policy — Iran and Saudi Arabia as guarantors of Western interests in the Persian Gulf; massive arms sales; the "blank check" policy allowing the Shah to purchase any non-nuclear American weapons
- **Carter** (1977–1981): Human rights pressure created contradictory signals; ultimately unable to save the Shah or manage the transition

**United Kingdom**:
- **Churchill** (1951–1955): Drove the push for the 1953 coup; refused to negotiate with Mossadegh
- **Eden** (1955–1957): Continued post-coup relationship; managing the new oil consortium
- Britain remained deeply invested in Iranian oil throughout the period

**Soviet Union**:
- Occupied northern Iran during WWII; attempted to establish separatist republics in Azerbaijan and Kurdistan (1945–1946)
- Supported the Tudeh Party as an instrument of influence
- The Cold War competition between the US and USSR was the backdrop for much of Iranian politics during this era

**Israel**:
- Maintained an unofficial but extensive relationship with the Shah's Iran
- Helped establish SAVAK and provided intelligence training
- Iran supplied up to 60% of Israel's oil through a discreet pipeline
- The two countries collaborated on a joint missile development program
- Iran never established full diplomatic relations and officially supported Palestinian causes — a pragmatic ambiguity that allowed cooperation without alienating Arab states
