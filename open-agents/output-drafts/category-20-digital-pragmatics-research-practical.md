# Digital Pragmatics: A Practical Guide to Navigating Online Communication

## Research Summary

This guide provides a comprehensive, practical reference for understanding the unwritten rules of digital communication. It covers text messaging tone interpretation, emoji pragmatics, email templates, workplace messaging etiquette, video call conventions, social media navigation, and response time norms. All content is designed as an explicit, decodable reference for autistic individuals who may find these implicit social conventions challenging to navigate.

## 1. THE TEXT MESSAGE TONE DECODER

### Why Text Formatting Matters

In face-to-face conversation, tone of voice, facial expression, and gesture communicate whether someone is happy, annoyed, sarcastic, or sincere. In text messages, these cues are absent. Instead, **formatting choices become the tone of voice**: capitalization, punctuation, word length, and letter repetition all encode emotional and social meaning.

Research confirms that text message punctuation serves a **rhetorical** rather than grammatical function. A period at the end of a text message is not simply ending a sentence -- it is making a statement about the sender's emotional state. A 2016 study by psychologist Danielle Gunraj found that text messages ending with a period were perceived as **more insincere** than those without one. This effect did not occur in handwritten notes, proving it is specific to digital communication.

### The Core Principle

> **The more effort someone puts into formatting a text, the more deliberate the tone.**
> - ALL CAPS = shouting or extreme emphasis
> - all lowercase = casual, relaxed, low-effort
> - Standard capitalization = formal or deliberate
> - No punctuation = casual default
> - Period at end = formal, serious, possibly cold
> - Exclamation mark = enthusiasm, warmth
> - Ellipsis (...) = trailing off, hesitation, or ominous tone
> - Repeated letters (sooo, nooo) = emphasis, playfulness, or exasperation

### "OK" Variants

| Text Version | Tone | Why It Reads This Way |
|---|---|---|
| OK | Neutral, standard | Default capitalized form; no extra signals |
| OK! | Enthusiastic, positive | Exclamation adds energy and willingness |
| Ok | Casual, neutral | Mixed case signals low formality |
| ok | Very casual or slightly curt | All lowercase = minimal effort |
| OK. | Formal, cold, possibly angry | Period adds finality; feels deliberate and clipped |

| ok. | Cold and curt | Lowest effort + period = maximum terseness |
| Ok... | Hesitant, trailing off, reluctant | Ellipsis signals uncertainty or unspoken reservations |
| OK... | Dramatic hesitation | Caps + ellipsis = "I'll agree but I have concerns" |
| Okay | Slightly warmer than "OK" | Full word = more effort = more warmth |
| okay | Casual and soft | Lowercase full word = relaxed, gentle agreement |
| Okay! | Warm, agreeable, enthusiastic | Full word + exclamation = genuine willingness |
| okay! | Casually enthusiastic | Relaxed enthusiasm |
| Okayyy | Playful, drawn out, or reluctant | Extended letters = performative tone |
| Okie | Cute, friendly, playful | Informal variant signals closeness and warmth |
| K | Dismissive, curt, possibly annoyed | Absolute minimum effort; reads as "I don't care" |
| k | Dismissive, very curt | Even less effort than "K" |

| KK | Casual but slightly more emphatic | Caps version of the friendly "kk" |
| Kk | Standard casual acknowledgment | Mixed case, common and neutral-friendly |

**Rule of thumb**: "Okay" > "OK" > "Ok" > "ok" > "k" in terms of warmth. Adding "!" warms any variant up. Adding "." cools any variant down.

## 2. EMOJI AS TONE MARKERS -- A Practical Guide

### How Emoji Function

Emoji serve as **prosodic substitutes** in text -- they do the work that tone of voice, facial expressions, and gestures do in spoken conversation. Research identifies their primary functions as:

1. **Tone modification**: Changing how the accompanying text should be read
2. **Emotion signaling**: Showing how the sender feels
3. **Face-threat softening**: Making potentially rude or awkward messages feel gentler
4. **Illocutionary force modification**: Changing the strength or type of a speech act (e.g., turning a demand into a friendly request)
5. **Backchannel signaling**: Showing you're listening/engaged without words

### Critical Warning: Emoji Meaning Is Not Fixed

A single emoji can communicate irony, sincerity, or sarcasm depending on:
- **Who** is using it (generational differences are significant)
- **What platform** it's on (workplace vs. personal)
- **What relationship** exists between sender and receiver
- **What text** accompanies it

Always consider context before interpreting.

### Emoji Placement Rules

1. **End of message** (most common): "Sounds great 😊" -- modifies the tone of the entire message
2. **After a specific word**: "That cake 😍 was incredible" -- emphasizes one element
3. **Standalone reaction**: Just "😂" as an entire reply = strong amusement
4. **Multiple emoji**: "😂😂😂" = intensity multiplier; the more emoji, the stronger the feeling
5. **Emoji + text mismatch**: "I'm fine 🙂" -- when emoji contradicts text, the mismatch itself is the message (often sarcasm or hidden distress)

## 3. EMAIL TEMPLATES FOR COMMON SITUATIONS

### How to Read These Templates

Each template includes **annotations in [brackets]** explaining the pragmatic function of each component. The annotations are not part of the email -- they explain *why* each element is there.

---

### Template 1: First Email to Someone You Don't Know

```
Subject: [Specific, clear topic] -- [Your connection point]
Example: "Question about your research on urban planning -- referred by Dr. Chen"

[SUBJECT LINE: Be specific. Vague subjects like "Hello" or "Quick question" get 
ignored or filtered as spam. Including a connection point increases open rates.]

Hi [First Name],

[GREETING: "Hi" is safe for most professional contexts. "Dear" is more formal 
(government, academia, legal). Never use "Hey" for a first email to a stranger.]

My name is [Your Name], and I'm a [your role] at [organization]. 
I came across your [work/article/profile] on [specific topic/platform], 
and [one specific thing that impressed you or connects you].

[INTRODUCTION: 2-3 sentences maximum. Establish who you are and WHY you're 
reaching out. Lead with connection to THEM, not with yourself. "I came across 
your work" shows you've done research and aren't mass-emailing.]

I'm reaching out because [specific reason -- what you need and why they 
specifically can help]. [One sentence providing context for your request.]

[THE ASK: Be direct about what you want. Vagueness wastes their time and 
makes you seem unprepared. Being specific shows respect for their expertise.]

Would you be open to [specific, low-commitment ask]? I'd be happy to work 
around your schedule.

[CALL TO ACTION: Make it easy to say yes. "A 15-minute call" is better than 
"picking your brain." Offering schedule flexibility shows respect for their time.]

Thank you for your time, and I look forward to hearing from you.

[CLOSING: Express gratitude. "I look forward to hearing from you" is standard 
but sets a gentle expectation of response without being pushy.]

Best regards,
[Your Full Name]
[Your Title/Role]
[Your Organization]
[Your Contact Info -- phone optional, LinkedIn optional]

[SIGNATURE: Full professional signature on first contact. Gives them context 
about who you are. In subsequent replies, you can shorten this.]
```

---

### Template 2: Following Up on an Unanswered Email

```
Subject: Re: [Original Subject Line]

[SUBJECT LINE: Reply to the original thread so they can see the context. 
Do NOT start a new thread -- it makes you look disorganized.]

Hi [First Name],

[GREETING: Same level of formality as the original email.]

I hope you're doing well. I wanted to follow up on my email from 
[day/date -- e.g., "last Tuesday" or "March 15th"] regarding [brief topic].

[CONTEXT REMINDER: Assume they forgot, not that they're ignoring you. 
Reference the date and topic so they can quickly locate the original. 
"I hope you're doing well" is a social lubricant -- it softens the follow-up.]

I understand you're busy, and I don't want to take up too much of your time. 
[Restate your request in one sentence, slightly rephrased.]

[SOFTENER: "I understand you're busy" acknowledges their time is valuable. 
It removes any accusatory tone. Restating the request saves them from 
having to find your original email.]

If this isn't the right time, or if there's someone else I should contact, 
I completely understand.

[EXIT RAMP: Giving them a graceful way to decline or redirect actually makes 
them MORE likely to respond. It removes pressure and shows social awareness.]

Thank you again for your consideration.

Best regards,
[Your Name]

[TIMING: Send first follow-up 3-5 business days after original. 
Second follow-up 5-7 days after that. Maximum 2-3 follow-ups total. 
After that, silence IS the answer.]
```

### Template 3: Asking for Something (Request Email)

```
Subject: Request: [Specific thing you need] -- [Timeline if relevant]
Example: "Request: Review of Q3 report -- by Friday if possible"

[SUBJECT LINE: Starting with "Request:" flags the email type immediately. 
Including timeline helps them prioritize.]

Hi [Name],

[GREETING: Match the formality of your relationship.]

I hope this message finds you well.

[SOCIAL OPENER: Optional in ongoing relationships. Include for people 
you don't email frequently. Skip with close colleagues who email daily.]

I'm working on [project/task], and I could use your help with [specific need]. 
Specifically, I'm looking for [exact deliverable or information].

[THE CONTEXT + ASK: Lead with why (context), then what (the specific ask). 
Be as specific as possible. "Could you review pages 5-8 of the attached 
report?" is far better than "Could you look at this?"]

The timeline for this is [deadline]. If that doesn't work for you, 
I can be flexible -- just let me know what would work better.

[TIMELINE + FLEXIBILITY: State the real deadline, then offer flexibility. 
This shows respect for their workload while being clear about needs.]

For context, [one sentence about why this matters or how their contribution fits].

[MOTIVATION: Briefly explain why their help matters. People are more willing 
to help when they understand the purpose and their specific value.]

Please let me know if you have any questions, or if there's anything I can 
provide to make this easier.

[RECIPROCITY: Offering to reduce their burden increases likelihood of help.]

Thank you in advance -- I really appreciate it.

Best,
[Your Name]
```

### Template 4: Delivering Bad News

```
Subject: Update on [Topic] -- change of plans

[SUBJECT LINE: "Update" and "change of plans" gently signal that something 
has shifted without being alarming. Avoid subjects like "Bad news" or "Problem."]

Hi [Name],

Thank you for [relevant recent effort/context -- e.g., "your work on the 
Johnson proposal" or "your patience as we sorted this out"].

[GRATITUDE FIRST: Acknowledge their effort or investment before delivering 
bad news. This shows you value their contribution regardless of outcome.]

I wanted to let you know that [state the bad news directly and clearly, 
in one to two sentences]. [Brief factual explanation of why.]

[DIRECT DELIVERY: Do not bury bad news in paragraphs of padding. State it 
clearly in the first half of the email. Excessive cushioning feels evasive 
and actually makes the news worse because the reader dreads what's coming.]

I understand this is disappointing, and I want to be transparent about 
what happened: [brief, honest explanation without excessive blame or excuses].

[ACKNOWLEDGMENT: Name the emotion they're likely feeling. This shows empathy 
without being patronizing. Honesty about what went wrong builds trust.]

Here's what we're doing about it: [specific next steps, plan, or resolution].

[FORWARD PATH: Always pair bad news with a concrete plan. Bad news + no plan 
= panic. Bad news + clear next steps = manageable situation.]

I'm happy to discuss this further -- would [specific time] work for a call, 
or would you prefer to handle this over email?

[AVAILABILITY: Offer to discuss further and let them choose the medium. 
Some people process bad news better in writing; others need a conversation.]

[Your closing and signature]
```

### Template 5: Responding to Criticism

```
Subject: Re: [Original Subject]

[SUBJECT LINE: Keep the original thread. Do NOT start a new subject line 
that reframes the criticism -- it looks defensive.]

Hi [Name],

Thank you for sharing this feedback -- I appreciate you taking the time 
to bring it to my attention.

[GRATITUDE OPENER: Even if the criticism stings, thanking them disarms 
defensiveness and shows professional maturity. This is the hardest part 
and the most important. You don't have to agree -- you just have to 
acknowledge they took the time to communicate.]

I've taken some time to consider your points. [Then choose one of these 
approaches based on the situation:]

**If you agree with the criticism:**
You're right that [specific point]. I should have [what you'll do differently]. 
Going forward, I plan to [specific corrective action with timeline].

**If you partially agree:**
You make a fair point about [specific area you agree with]. I'd like to 
provide some context on [area where you have a different perspective]: 
[brief, non-defensive explanation]. That said, I can see how [their concern] 
and I'll [specific action] to address that.

**If you disagree but want to maintain the relationship:**
I appreciate your perspective on this. I see it somewhat differently -- 
[brief, factual explanation of your view]. I'd welcome the chance to discuss 
this further and find common ground. Would [meeting/call] work?

[RESPONSE STRATEGY: Never respond to criticism in the first 10 minutes. 
Write a draft, wait, then revise. The goal is to be professional, not to 
"win." Even if they're wrong, responding defensively damages the relationship.]

[Your closing and signature]
```

### Template 6: Professional Thank You

```
Subject: Thank you -- [what you're thanking them for]
Example: "Thank you -- incredible mentorship this quarter"

[SUBJECT LINE: Lead with "Thank you" -- it's pleasant to see in an inbox.
```

## 4. SLACK/TEAMS WORKPLACE MESSAGING ETIQUETTE

### Channel vs. DM: Decision Guide

| Use a Channel When... | Use a DM When... |
|---|---|
| The information could benefit others on the team | The topic is personal or sensitive |
| You're asking a question someone else might also have | You're giving individual feedback (positive or negative) |
| You're sharing a status update or announcement | You're discussing someone's private matter (salary, health, conflict) |
| You want a searchable record others can find | The message is truly only relevant to one person |
| You're unsure -- **default to channel** | You're having a quick 1:1 coordination that doesn't affect others |

**The key principle**: If you ask a question in a DM, only one person sees the answer. If you ask in a channel, everyone benefits. DMs create information silos. Default to transparency.

---

### Threading: When to Thread vs. Main Channel

| Thread | Main Channel |
|---|---|
| Replying to a specific message with discussion | New topics or announcements |
| Asking clarifying questions about someone's post | Time-sensitive updates everyone needs to see |
| Extended back-and-forth that would clutter the channel | Status updates and FYIs |
| Side conversations related to a main topic | Standalone questions that need broad visibility |

**Threading rule**: If your reply is going to spawn a conversation (more than 2-3 messages back and forth), it belongs in a thread. This keeps the main channel scannable and prevents important announcements from getting buried in chatter.

**Check "Also send to #channel"** when your threaded reply contains a conclusion or decision that the whole channel needs to see.

---

### @Mentions: When to Use Each

| Mention Type | What It Does | When to Use | When NOT to Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| @here | Notifies everyone **currently online** in the channel | Time-sensitive question during work hours that any available person could answer | After hours; for non-urgent things; when you need a specific person |
| @channel | Notifies **everyone** in the channel (even offline -- they'll see it when they return) | Important announcements everyone must see; urgent issues affecting the whole team | Casual questions; things only some people need; frequently (causes notification fatigue) |
| @[person] | Notifies one specific person | When you need a specific person's input, decision, or action | When anyone could answer; when you're assigning work without authority |
| No @mention | No push notification | General FYIs, sharing articles, casual discussion | When you need a response or action from someone |

**Key rule**: Every time you @mention someone, you're interrupting them. Use @here and @channel sparingly -- they're the digital equivalent of standing up and shouting across an office.

---

### Emoji Reactions as Speech Acts in Slack/Teams

Emoji reactions in workplace messaging have evolved into a standardized communication system. Using them **reduces unnecessary reply messages** and keeps channels cleaner.

| Reaction | Meaning | Replaces the Message... |
|---|---|---|
| 👍 | "Acknowledged" / "I agree" / "Looks good" | "Sounds good!" / "Got it" |
| ✅ | "Done" / "Completed" / "Confirmed" | "I've finished this" / "This is done" |
| 👀 | "I'm looking at this" / "I'll review it" | "I'll take a look" |
| 🎉 | "Congratulations" / "Great job" | "That's awesome, well done!" |
| ➕ or 👆 | "+1" / "I agree" / "Me too" | "I agree with this" / "Same" |
| 🙏 | "Thank you" | "Thanks for this!" |
| 📌 | "This is important" / "Pinned for reference" | "Everyone should note this" |
| ⏳ | "Working on it" / "In progress" | "I'm still working on this" |
| ❓ | "I have a question about this" | "Can you clarify?" |
| 🔥 | "This is great / impressive" | "Wow, nice work!" |

**When to react vs. reply**: If your response would be a single short sentence (like "Got it" or "Thanks!"), use a reaction emoji instead. If you have a question, need clarification, or have substantive input, reply in a thread.

---

### Status Messages and Their Social Function

Your Slack/Teams status communicates availability and context to colleagues without requiring you to announce it individually.

| Status | What It Signals | Social Effect |
|---|---|---|
| 🏠 Working remotely | "I'm available but not in the office" | Sets expectations for response medium (message, not walk-over) |
| 🤒 Out sick | "Don't expect responses today" | Signals you should only contact for emergencies |
| 📅 In a meeting | "I can't respond right now" | Explains delayed responses; sets expectation |
| 🎧 Focus time / Deep work | "I'm heads-down; don't interrupt unless urgent" | Legitimizes non-response; protects concentration |
| 🌴 Vacationing / PTO | "I am not working; do not contact" | Strong boundary; redirect to backup |
| 🍕 Lunch | "I'll be back in ~30 min-1 hour" | Explains brief absence |
| (No status set) | "I'm available (presumably)" | Default state; people expect normal response times |

**Key rule**: Set your status honestly. If you're in a meeting, say so. If you're done for the day, update it. Colleagues calibrate their expectations based on your status, and inaccurate statuses erode trust.

---

### When to Use "Quick Call?" vs. Typing It Out

| Suggest a Call When... | Type It Out When... |
|---|---|
| The topic is emotionally sensitive or could be misread | The question has a simple, factual answer |
| You've gone back and forth 5+ messages without resolution | You need a written record (decisions, approvals) |
| You need to brainstorm or discuss multiple options | The person is in a different time zone or marked as busy |
| The explanation would require a very long message | The person has indicated they prefer async communication |
| Tone matters and text might create misunderstanding | It's a status update or FYI that doesn't need discussion |

**How to ask for a call properly**: "Hey [Name], would you have 5-10 minutes for a quick call about [topic]? I think it would be faster than typing it out. No rush -- whenever works for you today."

**Why this works**: It states the topic, estimates the time, explains why a call is better, and gives them control over timing.

---

### The "Hey" DM Problem

**Don't do this:**
```
You: Hey
[5 minutes pass]
You: Are you there?
[10 minutes pass]
Them: Hey, what's up?
You: I had a question about the project deadline
```

**Do this instead:**
```
You: Hey! Quick question -- do you know if the project deadline 
was moved to Friday or is it still Thursday? No rush, just want 
to plan my week.
```

**Why the first version is problematic:**
1. "Hey" alone provides zero information -- the recipient can't assess urgency or prepare a response
2. It forces them to stop what they're doing just to find out what you want
3. It creates unnecessary back-and-forth that could have been one message
4. It generates multiple notifications when one would suffice
5. The recipient may feel anxious: "Hey" from a boss or coworker with no context often triggers worry ("Am I in trouble?")

**The rule**: Never send a bare greeting. Always include your question or topic in the first message. This is sometimes called "nohello" etiquette (nohello.net).

## 5. VIDEO CALL PRAGMATICS

### Camera On vs. Off: When Each Is Expected

| Situation | Camera Expectation | Why |
|---|---|---|
| 1:1 meeting | **On** (strongly expected) | Camera off in a 1:1 is like having a conversation with someone who won't look at you; it creates distance and can feel disrespectful |
| Small team meeting (2-8 people) | **On** (expected) | Small groups rely on visual feedback; your face helps with turn-taking and engagement |
| Client/external meeting | **On** (strongly expected) | Professionalism and trust-building; camera off signals disinterest to clients |
| Presentation (you're presenting) | **On** (expected) | Audience connects better with a visible speaker |
| Presentation (you're audience, <15 people) | **On** (expected) | Speaker needs visual feedback to gauge engagement |
| Large all-hands meeting (30+ people) | **Off** is generally acceptable | Too many video feeds; speaker can't see individual faces anyway |
| Webinar | **Off** (expected for attendees) | Audience cameras are usually disabled by default |
| Quick sync / standup | **On** (expected) | These are brief and benefit from face-to-face energy |
| Working session (parallel work, minimal talking) | **Off** is acceptable | You're not really interacting; camera adds fatigue |
| You're having a bad day / no bandwidth | **Off** is okay with a brief note | "Camera off today -- not feeling great but wanted to be here" is sufficient |

**Key principle**: When in doubt, turn your camera on. It's always safer to have it on. If you need it off, a brief explanation ("Camera off today, low bandwidth") removes any negative interpretation.

---

### Muting: The Complete Guide

| Situation | Mute Status | Why |
|---|---|---|
| You're not speaking | **Muted** | Background noise, breathing, typing all transmit; muting is the default courtesy |
| You're about to speak | **Unmute just before speaking** | Unmuting itself can signal "I want to say something" |
| Active small-group discussion | **Unmuted** is OK if your environment is quiet | Constant muting/unmuting disrupts natural conversation flow |
| You're the presenter | **Unmuted** | Obviously |
| Someone is presenting and you have a question | **Stay muted; use hand-raise or chat** | Unmuting and interrupting a presenter is rude |

**The "You're on mute" situation**: This happens to everyone. When someone says "you're on mute," simply unmute, give a brief smile or nod, and restart your point. Don't over-apologize. A simple "Sorry about that!" and continuing is the correct response.

**Pro tip**: Some people unmute and take a breath before speaking as a signal that they want to talk. Others use the hand-raise feature. Learn which convention your team uses.

---

### Turn-Taking on Video Calls

Video calls fundamentally change how turn-taking works compared to in-person conversation. The slight audio delay, inability to read subtle body language, and the "gallery view" format make natural conversation flow difficult.

**Available turn-taking signals** (from most to least disruptive):

1. **Unmute and speak** -- Most direct but risks talking over someone
2. **Raise physical hand** (visible on camera) -- Visual signal, works in small meetings
3. **Use the digital hand-raise feature** -- Formal but clear; best for large meetings
4. **Type in the chat** -- Parallel channel; good for questions without interrupting speaker
5. **Wait for a pause and lean forward** -- Subtle body language signal (only works if cameras are on)

**The chat as parallel channel**: Many teams use the meeting chat for:
- Questions for the presenter (addressed during Q&A or pauses)
- Links and references mentioned in discussion
- Side comments and reactions ("+1", "great point")
- Real-time note-taking and action items

**Rule**: The chat is not a private conversation -- assume everything in it is visible to all participants and may be reviewed later.

---

### Virtual Backgrounds: When Appropriate

| Situation | Virtual Background Appropriate? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Casual internal meeting | Yes | Common and accepted |
| Client/external meeting | Blurred background preferred over virtual | Virtual backgrounds can glitch and look unprofessional |
| Job interview | No -- use a real, tidy background | Signals preparedness and professionalism |
| Informal team social | Yes, even fun ones | Part of team culture |
| Your real background is messy/distracting | Yes -- blur or virtual | Better than a distracting real background |

**Key rule**: A simple blurred background is always safe and professional. Novelty virtual backgrounds (beaches, space, memes) are fine for casual internal calls but never for external-facing meetings.

### Screen Sharing Etiquette

**Before you share:**
1. Close all unnecessary tabs and applications
2. Turn on Do Not Disturb / Focus mode (this prevents notification pop-ups)
3. Close personal messaging apps (Slack DMs, personal texts, email)
4. Have the specific window/tab you need already open and ready
5. Share a **specific window** rather than your entire screen when possible
6. Check that your desktop background is appropriate

**While sharing:**
- Narrate what you're doing: "I'm going to scroll down to the data section"
- Pause to let viewers read before moving on
- If you need to navigate away briefly: "I'm going to pull up another tab for a moment"
- Use your cursor to point at what you're discussing

**After sharing:**
- Stop sharing immediately when done (don't leave it running while you chat)
- Return to gallery view so participants can see faces again

**The nightmare scenario**: Having personal messages, embarrassing tabs, or notifications pop up during screen share is a common and genuine fear. The solution is preparation (close everything first and enable Do Not Disturb), not avoidance of screen sharing.

### "Let's Take This Offline"

This phrase means: **"Let's stop discussing this in the current meeting and handle it separately."**

| What it literally means | What it often really means |
|---|---|
| "This deserves more time than we have right now" | Genuine -- the topic is important but we need to stay on agenda |
| "Let's discuss this in a smaller group" | The topic only concerns a few people; don't waste everyone's time |
| "This conversation is getting too detailed for this format" | The discussion has gone into the weeds and needs a focused session |
| "I want to stop this line of discussion" | Sometimes used to shut down uncomfortable topics -- watch if the follow-up actually happens |

**What to do when someone says it to you**: Agree, make a note to follow up, and actually schedule the follow-up meeting/conversation. If THEY said it, send them a message afterward: "Hey, you mentioned taking [topic] offline -- would [time] work to discuss?"

**What to do when you want to say it**: "This is a great discussion, and I want to give it the time it deserves. Could we take this offline and schedule a focused session? I'll set something up."

## 6. SOCIAL MEDIA NAVIGATION

### Platform Register Expectations

Every social media platform has an unspoken "register" -- a level of formality and type of content that is expected. Posting content in the wrong register is like wearing a swimsuit to a business meeting -- the content might be fine in another context, but it's wrong for the setting.

| Platform | Register | Expected Content | Tone |
|---|---|---|---|
| **LinkedIn** | Professional / formal | Career updates, industry insights, professional achievements, thought leadership, job announcements | Polished, measured, authoritative. Personal stories only if tied to professional lessons |
| **X (Twitter)** | Casual-opinionated / public | Quick takes, news commentary, humor, conversation threads, sharing links | Brief, witty, direct. Hot takes are welcome; lengthy earnestness is not |
| **Instagram** | Visual-personal / curated | Photos, Stories, Reels. Travel, food, lifestyle, creative work, personal milestones | Aesthetic, aspirational, casual. Captions range from minimal to storytelling |
| **Facebook** | Personal-social / community | Life updates, family photos, local events, group discussions, marketplace | Conversational, community-oriented. Longest acceptable post length of any platform |

### What to Post vs. What to Keep Private

| Generally Safe to Post | Think Twice / Risky | Do Not Post |
|---|---|---|
| Professional achievements | Strong political opinions (career risk) | Your home address or location in real-time |
| Travel photos (after you return) | Complaints about your employer | Financial details (salary, bank info) |
| Food, hobbies, creative work | Photos of others without permission | Other people's private information |
| Positive life milestones | Venting about specific people (even unnamed) | Passwords, tickets, boarding passes |
| Industry insights and articles | Content while intoxicated | Content showing illegal activity |
| Supportive comments on others' posts | Relationship conflicts | Work confidential information |

**The "employer test"**: Before posting, ask: "Would I be comfortable if my current or future employer saw this?" If no, don't post.

**The "permanence test"**: Everything posted online can be screenshot, saved, and shared even after deletion. Post as if it could be permanent.

### Comment Etiquette by Platform

| Platform | Comment Norms | What's Expected |
|---|---|---|
| **LinkedIn** | Professional, substantive | Add insight or genuine congratulations. "Congrats!" is fine. Avoid arguments. |
| **Instagram** | Brief, positive | "Love this!" / emoji reactions / compliments. Criticism is generally unwelcome on personal posts |
| **X (Twitter)** | Direct, conversational | Replies can disagree but should engage with the actual point. Sarcasm is common but risky |
| **Facebook** | Conversational, personal | Longer comments are fine. Engaging with friends and family. Community varies widely by group |
| **Reddit** | Substantive, evidence-based | Quality comments add information. Reddiquette: upvote good contributions, downvote off-topic (not disagreement). Different subreddits have different rules |
| **YouTube** | Wide range | Top comments are often jokes or timestamps. Constructive feedback is appreciated on educational content |

### How "Likes" and Reactions Function as Minimal Speech Acts

A "like" or reaction on social media is the digital equivalent of a nod, a smile, or a brief "mm-hmm" in conversation. It communicates:

| Action | What It Communicates |
|---|---|
| Liking a post | "I see this and I approve/support it" |
| Loving a post (❤️) | "I feel strongly positive about this" |
| Laughing reaction (😂) | "I found this amusing" |
| Sad reaction (😢) | "I acknowledge this is unfortunate" |
| Angry reaction (😡) | "This makes me upset" (usually about the content's topic, not the poster) |
| NOT reacting to someone's post | Usually means nothing. But if you consistently react to others' posts and never theirs, it can be noticed |
| Liking someone's comment on YOUR post | "I've seen your comment and appreciate it" -- often expected as acknowledgment |
| Liking an old post (from weeks/months ago) | Can signal you were looking through their profile -- some find this flattering, others find it intrusive ("deep-liking" or "stalking") |

**Reciprocity norm**: Many people feel a social obligation to like or react to posts from people who regularly engage with their content. This is not a hard rule but an observed social pattern.

---

### The Subtweet/Vague-Post Phenomenon

**What it is**: Posting about someone without naming them, usually to express frustration, criticism, or shade.

**Examples**:
- "Some people really need to learn how to communicate" (who? about what?)
- "Funny how people show their true colors" (vague, aimed at someone specific)
- "I guess loyalty means nothing to some people" (clearly about someone, but unnamed)

**Why people do it**:
- Express frustration without direct confrontation
- Seek validation from their audience
- Create plausible deniability ("I wasn't talking about YOU")

**Why it's generally considered poor form**:
- Audiences perceive subtweeters negatively (studies show people have worse impressions of indirect criticism)
- Creates anxiety for multiple people who wonder if it's about them
- Doesn't resolve the actual conflict
- Can escalate tensions if the subject recognizes themselves

**How to handle if you suspect a vague-post is about you**:
1. **Don't respond publicly** -- this escalates the situation
2. **Don't assume** -- it might genuinely not be about you
3. If you're quite sure, address it **privately** and directly: "Hey, I noticed your post and wondered if something was bothering you. I'd rather talk about it directly."
4. If they deny it, let it go. Pressing the point rarely helps.

**As a rule**: If you have a problem with someone, address them directly. Vague-posting is a conflict-avoidance strategy that typically makes conflicts worse.

---

### Blocking/Muting as Pragmatic Boundary-Setting

| Action | What It Does | When to Use It | Social Implications |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mute** | You stop seeing their content; they don't know | When someone's content annoys or upsets you but there's no conflict | No social consequence; they never know. Low-stakes boundary |
| **Unfollow** | You stop seeing their content; they may notice (on some platforms) | When you no longer want their content in your feed | On Instagram, they may notice the follower count drop. On X, less visible |
| **Block** | They cannot see or interact with your content; you can't see theirs | When someone is harassing you, making you uncomfortable, or you need a hard boundary | They WILL eventually notice if they try to find your profile. This is the "nuclear option" for social media |
| **Restrict** (Instagram) | Their comments on your posts are only visible to them; DMs go to requests | When blocking feels too aggressive but you need distance | They don't know they're restricted; subtle boundary |

**Key principle**: You do not owe anyone access to your online presence. Muting, blocking, and restricting are healthy boundary tools, not acts of aggression. You don't need to justify using them.

---

### When to Engage vs. When to Scroll Past

| Engage When... | Scroll Past When... |
|---|---|
| You have something substantive to add | You just want to express anger |
| The person seems open to genuine discussion | The person is clearly not looking for dialogue |
| The topic affects you directly and meaningfully | You'd be the 50th person making the same point |
| You can maintain calm, respectful tone | You're tired, stressed, or emotionally reactive |
| The platform/audience is appropriate for debate | It's a stranger's post and your input is unsolicited |
| You want to support/uplift someone's post | The thread is already a hostile argument |

**The engagement test**: Before commenting on a contentious post, ask: "Will this comment improve the conversation or just add noise?" If you're not adding new information, perspective, or genuine support, scrolling past is almost always the better choice.

**The cool-down rule**: If you feel a strong emotional urge to respond to something, wait 10 minutes. Most of the time, the urge to respond will decrease, and you'll realize engaging wouldn't have been productive.

## 7. THE RESPONSE TIME GUIDE

### Response Time Expectations by Context

Response times in digital communication carry social meaning. A fast response signals engagement and prioritization. A slow response may signal that you're busy, that the message is low-priority, or (in some interpretations) that you're upset.

**Important**: These are general norms, not rules. Individual relationships establish their own patterns. The most important thing is consistency -- a sudden change in someone's response pattern is more significant than absolute speed.

| Context | Expected Response Time | What a Delay Signals | What's Considered Rude |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Text from close friend** | Within a few hours | Busy, occupied. 1 day = totally fine. 2+ days without explanation = might seem like ignoring | Reading and never responding (leaving on "read") without eventual follow-up |
| **Text from partner** | Minutes to a few hours | Highly context-dependent. During work = busy. Evening = might be occupied. Pattern matters more than individual delays | Consistently ignoring texts while being active on social media |
| **Text from parent/family** | Within the day | Busy. Most family members understand delays | Days without response to a direct question, especially from an older relative who may worry |
| **Text from acquaintance** | Same day to next day | Normal; not close enough to demand immediate attention | Never responding at all |
| **Work email** | Within 24 hours (business days) | Standard professional norm. Within 4 hours is attentive. Same-day is good | Over 48 hours without acknowledgment or auto-reply |
| **Personal email** | 1-3 days | Email is understood to be asynchronous and less urgent than texting | Over a week without response to something that requires an answer |
| **Slack/Teams DM (during work hours)** | Within a few hours | Busy; in meetings; focused work. Status message provides context | Over 24 hours for a direct question without explanation |
| **Slack/Teams channel message** | Hours to next business day | Normal; channels are lower urgency than DMs | Ignoring a direct @mention for more than a day |
| **Social media DM** | Varies widely | No strong norm; some people rarely check DMs | Leaving someone on "seen" and never responding can feel rude; simply not seeing it is fine |
| **Social media comment** | Days are fine | Very low urgency; no immediate response expected | Generally no obligation to respond to comments unless they ask a direct question |
| **Dating app message** | Within a day | Interest wanes with delay. Same-day = interested. 2+ days = probably not interested | Matching and never messaging; leaving conversations mid-exchange without explanation |

---

### What Delayed Responses Signal

Research on response timing reveals important patterns:

**Speed and interest**: Fast responses generally signal interest and engagement. The quicker the response, the more connected and valued the sender feels. This applies to both personal and professional contexts.

**Delay and bad news**: When people need to deliver a "dispreferred response" (saying no, declining an invitation, disagreeing), they tend to delay. Studies found that when responses to requests were delayed by more than 1 minute, it was much more likely to be a "no" rather than a "yes." Preferred responses (yes, agreement) tend to be sent quickly.

**Delay and relationship concern**: When someone who usually responds quickly suddenly takes much longer, the sender may worry about the relationship -- "Are they upset with me?" This shows that response time is interpreted relative to the individual's baseline pattern, not in absolute terms.

**Read receipts amplify everything**: If a message has been opened (shown by read receipts), delayed response is perceived much more negatively than if the message status is unknown. A read-but-unanswered message signals deliberate non-response.

---

### Managing Your Own Response Times

**When you can't respond immediately but want to signal engagement:**
- "Hey, saw this -- will reply properly later today!" (takes 5 seconds, buys hours)
- React with an emoji (in Slack/Teams) to acknowledge receipt
- "Good question, let me think on this and get back to you"

**When you need to delay intentionally (not playing games -- legitimate reasons):**
- Complex questions that deserve thoughtful answers
- Emotional topics where you need time to process
- Professional decisions that require consultation

**When you've unintentionally left someone waiting:**
- "Sorry for the late reply -- [brief reason]" is sufficient
- Don't over-explain; a simple acknowledgment is fine
- Respond to the content of their message, not just the delay

---

### Response Time: The Anxiety Management Section

For autistic individuals, response time norms can be a significant source of anxiety. Some important reframes:

1. **A delayed response usually means "busy," not "angry."** Most people are juggling multiple conversations, tasks, and responsibilities. Their response time to you is almost never a deliberate comment on your value.

2. **Your response time doesn't have to match theirs.** If someone responds in 2 minutes, you are not obligated to match that speed. Respond when you're ready.

3. **It's okay to not respond immediately.** You are allowed to be unavailable. You don't need an excuse.

4. **"Seen" without immediate reply is normal, not hostile.** People often read messages when they can't respond (in meetings, commuting, busy with tasks) and intend to reply later. The "read receipt" feature creates anxiety out of normal behavior.

5. **If you're worried about seeming rude with a slow response, a brief acknowledgment message solves the problem.** "Got your message, will reply properly tonight" removes all ambiguity.

| /nsx | Non-sexual | "You look great /nsx" |
| /nbh | Nobody here (not directed at anyone in conversation) | "Some people are so rude /nbh" |
| /aff | Affectionate | "You're such a dork /aff" |

### Usage Notes

- Tone indicators are most common in casual online spaces (Discord, Twitter, Tumblr, group chats)
- They are less common in professional contexts (email, Slack, LinkedIn)
- Not everyone knows or uses them -- they're most recognized in neurodivergent and younger online communities
- You don't need to use them if you don't want to; they're a tool, not a requirement
- If someone asks you to use them, it's a reasonable accommodation that costs you nothing
- Don't overuse them -- 1-2 per conversation is typical; using them on every message can feel patronizing
