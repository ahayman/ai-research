# Off-Record Politeness Strategies and Criticisms of Brown & Levinson's Politeness Theory

## Research Document for English Pragmatics Knowledge Base (Autism-Focused)

## PART 1: OFF-RECORD (INDIRECT) STRATEGIES -- Brown & Levinson's Strategy 4

### Overview

Off-record strategies are the most indirect form of politeness in Brown and Levinson's (1987) framework. The core principle: **the speaker says something that could mean multiple things, relying on the hearer to figure out the intended meaning**. If challenged, the speaker can deny the face-threatening interpretation entirely.

This is the strategy of **plausible deniability**. The speaker performs a face-threatening act (FTA) in such a way that "it is not possible to attribute only one clear communicative intention to the act" (Brown & Levinson, 1987). The speaker violates one or more of Grice's Cooperative Principle maxims in order to generate a conversational implicature -- an implied meaning the hearer must work out from context.

**Why speakers use off-record strategies:**
- To avoid direct responsibility for the FTA
- To give the hearer the option to pretend not to understand
- To allow the hearer to be "helpful and generous" voluntarily rather than under compulsion
- To preserve both speaker's and hearer's face

**The fundamental risk:** The implication may be so vague or indirect that the hearer genuinely does not understand the intended meaning. The speaker sacrifices communicative clarity for social safety.

**Connection to Grice:** Off-record strategies work by deliberately flouting Gricean maxims. When a speaker says something obviously too little (understatement), too much (overstatement), obviously untrue (irony), or obviously uninformative (tautology), the hearer recognizes the violation and searches for an implied meaning that would make the utterance cooperative. This is how conversational implicature is generated.

Brown and Levinson identified **15 specific off-record strategies**, organized into two broad groups:

### Group A: Strategies That Invite Conversational Implicatures

These strategies work by violating Grice's maxims (Relevance, Quantity, Quality, or Manner) to trigger the hearer's inferential reasoning.

---

#### Strategy 1: Give Hints

**Definition:** The speaker mentions something associated with the desired act -- a reason, a condition, a consequence -- without stating the act itself. The hearer must infer the request or criticism from what is mentioned.

**Gricean basis:** Violates the maxim of Relevance. The speaker says something that seems tangential unless the hearer infers the real point.

**Examples:**
- "It's cold in here." (Implied: Please close the window / turn up the heat.)
- "I have a really early morning tomorrow." (Implied: I'd like to leave / I'd like you to leave.)
- "Oh, I forgot to bring cash." (Implied: Could you pay for me? / Is there an ATM nearby?)
- "This soup could use a little something." (Implied: Please pass the salt.)

**Why it's off-record:** The speaker has only stated a fact about the temperature, their schedule, their wallet, or the soup. They have not made a request. If the hearer says "OK, so?" the speaker cannot be accused of having asked for anything. The hearer can choose to act on the hint or ignore it.

**Autism note:** Hints are one of the most commonly used off-record strategies in everyday English conversation. They are also one of the most frequently missed by autistic individuals, because the literal content gives no indication that a request is being made. The gap between what is said ("It's cold in here") and what is meant ("Close the window") requires real-time pragmatic inference that draws on shared situational awareness.

---

#### Strategy 2: Give Association Clues

**Definition:** The speaker mentions something associated with the desired act through a chain of association, expecting the hearer to connect the dots. Similar to hints, but the connection is more indirect -- it works through associated knowledge rather than direct cause-and-effect.

**Gricean basis:** Violates the maxim of Relevance through associative rather than logical connection.

**Examples:**
- "My car is filthy. I've been so busy lately..." (Implied: Could you help wash it? / I haven't had time to wash it -- maybe you could.)
- "Oh, there's that new Italian place on Main Street..." (Implied: Let's go eat there / Would you like to go there?)
- "I hear they're hiring at the bookstore." (Said to an unemployed friend. Implied: You should apply there.)

**Why it's off-record:** The speaker has merely mentioned a fact or observation. The associative leap to a request or suggestion happens in the hearer's mind, not in the speaker's words. The speaker can always say "I was just mentioning it" if challenged.

---

#### Strategy 3: Presuppose

**Definition:** The speaker makes a statement that contains a hidden assumption (presupposition) that does the real communicative work. The face-threatening content is buried in what the sentence takes for granted, not in what it directly asserts.

**Gricean basis:** Violates the maxim of Relevance -- the surface statement seems informative, but the real message is in what it assumes.

**Examples:**
- "I washed the car again today." (Presupposition: I'm always the one who washes it. You never help. The word "again" carries the criticism.)
- "I managed to finish the report despite everything." (Presupposition: There were obstacles -- possibly caused by you -- that I had to overcome.)
- "It happened before you arrived." (Presupposition: You were late.)
- "Have you stopped leaving your dishes in the sink?" (Presupposition: You have been leaving dishes in the sink -- this is a criticism disguised as a question.)

**Why it's off-record:** The speaker has not directly accused the hearer of anything. The criticism lives in the presupposition, which the speaker can claim they didn't intend to highlight. If challenged ("Are you saying I never help?"), the speaker can respond, "I was just telling you about my day."

---

#### Strategy 4: Understate (Litotes)

**Definition:** The speaker says less than they mean, using a weaker expression than the situation warrants. Often accomplished through litotes -- affirming something by negating its opposite ("not bad" for "good"; "not exactly brilliant" for "stupid").

**Gricean basis:** Violates the maxim of Quantity -- the speaker provides less information than is clearly warranted by the situation.

**Examples:**
- "He's not exactly the brightest bulb." (Meaning: He's quite dim/unintelligent.)
- "The food was OK, I suppose." (Meaning: The food was bad.)
- "That's not a bad idea." (Meaning: That's actually a good idea.)
- "There's been a slight problem with the budget." (Meaning: There's been a major problem.)
- "I'm not entirely happy with the results." (Meaning: I'm quite unhappy / disappointed.)

**Why it's off-record:** The speaker has technically said something mild and qualified. The full force of the opinion is left for the hearer to infer. If challenged, the speaker can point to the literal, mild content: "I said it was OK! I didn't say it was bad."

**Pragmatic function:** Understatement allows speakers to deliver criticism, bad news, or strong opinions while maintaining a veneer of moderation. It is particularly common in British English culture, where direct criticism is often socially penalized.

---

#### Strategy 5: Overstate (Hyperbole)

**Definition:** The speaker says more than the situation warrants, using exaggeration to convey a point without being held to the literal content.

**Gricean basis:** Violates the maxim of Quantity (saying more than needed) and possibly Quality (saying something not literally true).

**Examples:**
- "I tried to call you a million times!" (Meaning: I called several times and you didn't answer. I'm frustrated.)
- "Everyone agrees with me on this." (Meaning: Several people do, and I want to bolster my position.)
- "That lecture went on forever." (Meaning: It was too long and boring.)
- "I've been waiting an eternity." (Meaning: I've been waiting too long.)
- "There were a thousand people in line." (Meaning: The line was very long.)

**Why it's off-record:** The speaker has not made a literal claim. If challenged on the specific content ("You did NOT call a million times"), the speaker can retreat to the intended meaning ("I just meant I called a lot"). The exaggeration signals that the utterance should not be taken at face value.

---

#### Strategy 6: Use Tautologies

**Definition:** The speaker utters a statement that is logically self-evident and therefore informationally empty on the surface ("A is A"). Because the statement is trivially true, the hearer must search for a deeper implied meaning.

**Gricean basis:** Violates the maxim of Quantity -- the statement provides zero new information, so the hearer must infer why it was said.

**Examples:**
- "War is war." (Implied: We must accept the harsh realities. Don't be naive about what's happening.)
- "Boys will be boys." (Implied: That behavior is expected/natural/excusable. Don't complain about it.)
- "A promise is a promise." (Implied: You made a promise and you must keep it.)
- "If I won't do it, I won't do it." (Implied: Stop asking. My decision is final.)
- "The law is the law." (Implied: We must obey it, regardless of personal feelings.)

**Why it's off-record:** The speaker has said nothing that can be literally disputed -- of course war is war, of course a promise is a promise. The evaluative content (acceptance, dismissal, insistence) is entirely implied. The speaker can claim they were merely stating a truism.

**Cultural note:** Tautologies often encode conservative or fatalistic attitudes. "Boys will be boys" has been extensively criticized for excusing problematic behavior by naturalizing it. Understanding the implied meaning of tautologies requires recognizing that their informational emptiness is itself the signal that something else is being communicated.

---

#### Strategy 7: Use Contradictions

**Definition:** The speaker makes a self-contradictory statement, signaling that they cannot or will not commit to a single position. This forces the hearer to search for a resolution or interpretation.

**Gricean basis:** Violates the maxim of Quality -- the speaker appears to be saying two incompatible things at once.

**Examples:**
- "Well, I do and I don't like it." (Meaning: I have mixed feelings. I don't want to commit to either position.)
- "It's a great plan and it's a terrible plan." (Meaning: It has both strengths and serious weaknesses.)
- "I'm here and I'm not here." (Meaning: I'm physically present but mentally checked out / I don't want to be here.)
- A: "Are you happy with the new arrangement?" B: "Yes and no." (Meaning: It's complicated. I don't want to give a simple answer that might offend you.)

**Why it's off-record:** The speaker has not committed to any clear position. By contradicting themselves, they avoid the face threat of a direct negative evaluation while signaling that one exists. The hearer must work out which aspect is positive and which is negative.

---

#### Strategy 8: Be Ironic (Use Sarcasm)

**Definition:** The speaker says the opposite of what they mean, relying on context, tone, or shared knowledge to signal that the literal meaning should be inverted.

**Gricean basis:** Violates the maxim of Quality -- the speaker says something obviously untrue. The hearer infers the opposite.

**Examples:**
- "What lovely weather!" (Said during a torrential downpour. Meaning: The weather is terrible.)
- "Oh, brilliant. Just brilliant." (Said when something goes wrong. Meaning: This is terrible.)
- "Thanks for all your help." (Said to someone who did nothing. Meaning: You were useless.)
- "Well, that went well." (Said after a disaster. Meaning: That went very badly.)
- "You're always so punctual." (Said to someone who is late. Meaning: You're always late.)

**Why it's off-record:** The speaker has technically said something positive or neutral. The critical meaning is generated by the contrast between the utterance and the obvious reality. If challenged, the speaker can (disingenuously) say "What? I said it was lovely weather!"

**Autism note:** Irony and sarcasm are among the most challenging pragmatic phenomena for autistic individuals. The literal content of sarcastic utterances is the direct opposite of the intended meaning, and detecting the reversal requires processing tone of voice, facial expression, shared context, and the pragmatic principle that a speaker who says something obviously false must mean something else. Research consistently identifies this as an area where explicit, pattern-based teaching can help.

---

#### Strategy 9: Use Metaphors

**Definition:** The speaker uses a figurative comparison to convey meaning indirectly, often making a comment about someone or something without stating it literally.

**Gricean basis:** Violates the maxim of Quality -- the statement is not literally true, prompting the hearer to find the figurative meaning.

**Examples:**
- "He's a real snake." (Meaning: He's deceptive and untrustworthy.)
- "She's got a heart of gold." (Meaning: She's very kind and generous.)
- "That meeting was a circus." (Meaning: It was chaotic and unproductive.)
- "John is the


 


foundation of this department." (Meaning: He's essential and holds everything together.)
- "Her words were daggers." (Meaning: What she said was hurtful.)

**Why it's off-record:** The speaker has not literally called anyone a snake or described the meeting as chaotic. The critical or evaluative content is carried by the figurative meaning, which the speaker can soften or deny. "I didn't say he was untrustworthy, I was using a figure of speech."

---

#### Strategy 10: Use Rhetorical Questions

**Definition:** The speaker asks a question that does not seek information but rather makes a point. The expected answer is obvious, and the question form serves to emphasize the speaker's position without stating it directly.

**Gricean basis:** Violates the maxim of Quality (the speaker does not genuinely seek an answer) and Manner (indirect way of asserting something).

**Examples:**
- "How was I supposed to know?" (Implied assertion: I had no way of knowing. It's not my fault.)
- "How many times do I have to tell you?" (Implied assertion: I've told you too many times already. You should have listened.)
- "Who cares?" (Implied assertion: No one cares. This doesn't matter.)
- "Is the Pope Catholic?" (Implied assertion: Obviously yes. The answer to whatever question prompted this is equally obvious.)
- "What's the point?" (Implied assertion: There is no point. This is futile.)
- "Do I look like I was born yesterday?" (Implied assertion: I'm not stupid/naive.)

**Why it's off-record:** The speaker has asked a question, not made an accusation or assertion. If challenged, they can say "I was just asking a question." The assertive force is carried by the implicature, not the literal form.

---

### Group B: Strategies That Create Vagueness or Ambiguity

These strategies work by being deliberately unclear about who, what, or how much is meant. Rather than triggering specific implicatures, they leave the intended meaning underspecified.

---

#### Strategy 11: Be Ambiguous

**Definition:** The speaker uses language that has more than one possible interpretation, leaving it unclear which meaning is intended.

**Gricean basis:** Violates the maxim of Manner (be clear, avoid ambiguity).

**Examples:**
- "Nice friends you've got there." (Ambiguous: Could be genuine praise or sarcastic criticism of the friend's behavior.)
- "John's a pretty sharp guy." (Ambiguous: Could mean intellectually sharp or sarcastically noting he's been rude/cutting.)
- "That was some performance." (Ambiguous: Could mean an impressive performance or a terrible one.)
- "I see you've been busy." (Ambiguous: Could acknowledge productive work or criticize making a mess.)

**Why it's off-record:** The speaker has not committed to either reading. If the hearer takes offense at the negative interpretation, the speaker can claim they meant the positive one. The ambiguity is the shield.

---

#### Strategy 12: Be Vague

**Definition:** The speaker is deliberately imprecise about the details -- who did what, when, how much, etc. -- so that the hearer cannot pin the speaker down.

**Gricean basis:** Violates the maxim of Manner (be clear, be specific).

**Examples:**
- "I heard someone did something they shouldn't have." (Vague about who, what, and when.)
- "Maybe someone should look into that." (Vague about who should act and what specifically needs attention.)
- "There have been some issues with your work." (Vague about which specific issues.)
- "Certain people in this office need to step up." (Vague about exactly who.)
- "Something happened at the party." (Vague about what.)

**Why it's off-record:** The speaker has not named anyone or specified anything. The vagueness means no one can be directly accused or criticized. The speaker can always say, "I wasn't talking about you" or "I didn't say what it was."

---

#### Strategy 13: Over-Generalize

**Definition:** The speaker uses a general rule, proverb, or universal statement instead of directly addressing the specific situation or person. The hearer is left to determine whether and how the general statement applies to them.

**Gricean basis:** Violates the maxim of Quantity -- the speaker provides a general statement when a specific one is called for.

**Examples:**
- "People who live in glass houses shouldn't throw stones." (Implied: You are criticizing others while being vulnerable to the same criticism yourself.)
- "Mature people don't hold grudges." (Implied: You're holding a grudge, and that's immature.)
- "Everyone makes mistakes." (Implied: You made a mistake, and I'm softening the criticism by universalizing it.)
- "You can't please everyone." (Implied: Don't worry about the specific person who is upset.)
- "Early bird catches the worm." (Implied: You should have been here earlier.)

**Why it's off-record:** The speaker has stated a general truth about the world, not a specific criticism of the hearer. If the hearer says "Are you saying I throw stones?", the speaker can say "No, I was just making a general observation."

---

#### Strategy 14: Displace H (Displace the Hearer)

**Definition:** The speaker addresses the face-threatening act to someone other than the actual intended target, while knowing that the real target is present and can overhear. The FTA is formally directed at a third party but pragmatically aimed at the actual hearer.

**Gricean basis:** Violates relevance -- why is the speaker telling person X something that is clearly relevant to person Y?

**Examples:**
- A mother, in front of her messy teenager, says to the family dog: "At least you don't leave your clothes on the floor." (The criticism is aimed at the teenager.)
- A teacher says to the whole class: "Some students haven't handed in their homework," while looking at a specific student. (The criticism is directed at that student.)
- A boss says to a colleague in a meeting: "I always appreciate people who come prepared," while a notoriously unprepared team member is present. (The praise of others is an indirect criticism of the unprepared person.)
- Speaking to a child present with their parent: "Do you know it's rude to shout in restaurants?" (The implied criticism is directed at the parent for not controlling the child.)

**Why it's off-record:** The speaker has not addressed the target directly. The face-threatening act is formally aimed elsewhere. The real target can recognize that the comment is meant for them, but the speaker maintains plausible deniability: "I was talking to [other person], not to you."

---

#### Strategy 15: Be Incomplete (Use Ellipsis)

**Definition:** The speaker begins an utterance but trails off before completing it, leaving the face-threatening part unsaid. The hearer must fill in the blank, which means the speaker never actually said the critical thing.

**Gricean basis:** Violates the maxim of Manner (be clear, be complete) and Quantity (say enough).

**Examples:**
- "Well, if you'd just..." (Implied completion: ...listened to me / done it properly / been on time, this wouldn't have happened.)
- "I'm not saying you're wrong, but..." (Implied: There's something seriously wrong with what you said.)
- "Well, I would have thought..." (Implied: ...that you'd know better / that it was obvious.)
- "If that's how you want to play it..." (Implied threat or warning left unspecified.)
- "It's just that, you know..." (Implied: Something awkward or critical that the speaker doesn't want to say.)

**Why it's off-record:** The speaker has literally said nothing complete. The FTA exists only in the implication created by the trailing off. If challenged, the speaker can say "I didn't actually say anything" or "I was going to say something else entirely."

**Autism note:** Incomplete utterances are particularly challenging because they require the hearer to (1) recognize that the utterance is deliberately incomplete (not just a pause or lost thought), (2) reconstruct the missing content from context, and (3) understand that the missing content is typically the most face-threatening part. The trailing-off pattern is a learned pragmatic signal that many neurotypical speakers recognize automatically.

---

### Summary: Why Off-Record Strategies Are Systematically Difficult for Autistic Individuals

Off-record strategies share a core feature: **the intended meaning is never actually said**. The literal content is always deniable, and the real message must be inferred through:

1. **Contextual reasoning** -- What is happening in the situation that would make this utterance relevant?
2. **Theory of Mind** -- What does the speaker want? What do they know that I know?
3. **Pragmatic conventions** -- Recognizing patterns like "it's cold in here" as a request format
4. **Prosodic cues** -- Tone of voice often signals irony, sarcasm, or trailing off
5. **Social scripts** -- Knowing what kinds of things people typically request indirectly

Each of these channels poses specific challenges for autistic individuals, making off-record strategies a concentrated cluster of pragmatic difficulty. However, many of these strategies follow **learnable patterns** -- once the pattern is identified (e.g., "a statement about a problem in the environment is often an indirect request to fix it"), it can be applied systematically.

## PART 2: CRITICISMS AND ALTERNATIVES TO BROWN & LEVINSON'S POLITENESS THEORY

### Overview of the Critiques

Brown and Levinson's (1987) politeness theory is arguably the most influential framework in pragmatics. It is also one of the most criticized. The critiques cluster around several major themes:

1. **Cultural bias** -- The theory reflects Western, individualistic assumptions
2. **Static model** -- It treats politeness as rule-governed rather than emergent
3. **Speaker-centric** -- It focuses on the speaker's strategies, not the hearer's interpretation
4. **Power-blind** -- It doesn't adequately address how politeness relates to power and inequality
5. **Gender-blind** -- It doesn't account for gendered norms around politeness

What follows are the major critiques and alternative frameworks, each offering insights that are particularly relevant for understanding English pragmatics from a neurodivergent perspective.

---

### 1. Cultural Criticisms: Is "Face" Universal?

#### Matsumoto (1988): Japanese Politeness Doesn't Fit

**The critique:** Yoshiko Matsumoto (1988, 1989) argued that Brown and Levinson's concept of **negative face** (the desire to be free from imposition) is fundamentally alien to Japanese culture. In Japanese society, the self is **interdependent** rather than independent -- people define themselves through their social relationships and roles, not through individual autonomy.

**Key arguments:**
- The desire to "defend one's territory" assumes an individual's right to autonomy, which is foreign to the Japanese interdependent self-concept
- Japanese honorifics (keigo) are not "redressive strategies" for face-threatening acts, as Brown and Levinson claim. They are **"relation-acknowledging devices"** that indicate status differences between interlocutors
- Honorific use is obligatory and socially prescribed, not a strategic choice by an individual speaker
- The Japanese language is inherently sensitive to social context in ways that Brown and Levinson's model cannot capture

**Impact:** Matsumoto's work was one of the first major challenges to the universality claims of Brown and Levinson, and it opened the door for extensive cross-cultural critique.

#### Ide (1989): Wakimae -- Politeness as Discernment, Not Strategy

**The critique:** Sachiko Ide (1989) proposed that there are **two fundamentally different types** of linguistic politeness:

1. **Volitional politeness** -- governed by individual intention, realized through verbal strategies (this is what Brown and Levinson describe)
2. **Discernment politeness (wakimae)** -- operated by social norms and expectations, expressed through linguistically encoded forms

**The wakimae concept:** In East Asian societies, politeness is mainly a matter of conforming to social norms -- recognizing one's place in the social hierarchy and speaking accordingly. This is **wakimae** (literally "discernment" or "knowing one's place"). Wakimae is not about individual strategic choice; it is about reading the social situation and selecting the appropriate register, honorific level, and speech style.

**Key distinction:** Brown and Levinson treat all politeness as strategic -- the speaker calculates the weight of the face threat and selects a mitigation strategy. Ide argues that much of politeness (especially in Japanese) is **automatic and socially determined**, not individually calculated.

**Why this matters for autism:** The distinction between volitional and discernment politeness is potentially very useful for autistic individuals. Discernment politeness follows **learnable, rule-based patterns** (e.g., "use this form with elders," "use this register in formal settings"). Volitional politeness requires **real-time social calculation** that is harder to systematize. Understanding which type of politeness is expected in a given situation can guide more effective communication strategies.

#### Chinese Mianzi and Lian vs. B&L's Positive/Negative Face

**The critique:** Chinese culture has two distinct face concepts that do not map neatly onto Brown and Levinson's positive face / negative face distinction:

- **Mianzi (面子)** -- reputation achieved through success, social standing, and ostentation. It is **other-oriented** and represents one's public self-image, capability, dignity, and reputation. Mianzi can be given, earned, lost, and fought for.
- **Lian (臉)** -- the confidence of society in the integrity of one's moral character. It is **self-oriented** in the sense of personal moral standing. Lian represents moral integrity and is lost through immoral or socially irresponsible behavior.

**How this challenges B&L:**
- Brown and Levinson define face as "the public self-image that everyone wants to claim for himself" -- but Chinese face is "too intricate to be explained by" this definition
- B&L's face is individualistic (addressed to desires of individual persons), while Chinese face is communal (addressed to expectations of society at large)
- Gu Yueguo (1990) argued that B&L's individual-based approach "does not address the normative constraints society imposes on its individuals"

**Gu's alternative:** Gu (1990) proposed four maxims for Chinese politeness (limao): **self-denigration, address, tact, and generosity**. These emphasize social obligation and hierarchy rather than individual face-want calculation. To be polite (you limao) in Chinese is to demonstrate with words one's knowledge of mianzi and lian norms.

#### Is Negative Face a Western (Anglo-Saxon) Concept?

**The argument:** Multiple scholars have argued that the entire concept of negative face -- the desire not to be imposed upon, to have one's autonomy respected -- reflects specifically **Western, Anglo-Saxon, individualistic** values:

- Independence as a characteristic of negative face was based on placing a high value on the individual in American and European cultures
- In collectivist cultures (much of East Asia, the Middle East, Latin America, Africa), social harmony, group solidarity, and hierarchical respect may take precedence over individual autonomy
- Members from individualist cultures prefer negative politeness strategies (distance, deference, apology for imposing), while members from collectivist cultures prefer positive politeness strategies (inclusion, solidarity, warmth)
- Brown and Levinson's "Model Person" -- a rational individual calculating face threats -- reflects a specifically Western philosophical tradition of rational individualism

**The broader point:** If negative face is culturally specific rather than universal, then the entire architecture of Brown and Levinson's theory (which treats negative and positive face as universal human needs) is called into question. The theory may describe Anglo-Saxon politeness well but fail to capture politeness systems organized around different principles (hierarchy, discernment, social harmony, moral reputation).

### 2. Leech's Politeness Principles: A Maxim-Based Alternative

#### Overview

Geoffrey Leech (1983) proposed the **Politeness Principle** as a complement to Grice's Cooperative Principle. Where Grice's maxims (Quality, Quantity, Relevance, Manner) explain how we communicate efficiently, Leech's maxims explain **why we often communicate inefficiently** -- because maintaining social relationships sometimes requires sacrificing informational clarity.

**The key insight:** The Politeness Principle has a **higher regulative role** than the Cooperative Principle. We can be cooperative in the first place only because politeness maintains the social relationships that make cooperation possible.

#### The Six Maxims

Leech's six maxims are organized in complementary pairs:

**Pair 1: Tact and Generosity** (about costs and benefits in actions)
- **Tact Maxim:** Minimize the cost to others; maximize the benefit to others. (Focus: what the act costs the hearer.)
  - Example: "Could you possibly lend me your car?" (framing a request as a great favor, minimizing the imposition)
  - Example: "Would you like another piece of cake?" (framing an offer that maximizes benefit to hearer)
- **Generosity Maxim:** Minimize the benefit to self; maximize the cost to self.
  - Example: "Let me do the washing up -- you relax." (taking on cost voluntarily)
  - Example: "I'll carry those bags for you." (imposing cost on self)

**Pair 2: Approbation and Modesty** (about praise and criticism)
- **Approbation Maxim:** Minimize dispraise of others; maximize praise of others.
  - Example: "That was a really interesting presentation" (even if it was mediocre)
  - Example: Avoiding direct criticism: "It had some good points" (when there were also many bad ones)
- **Modesty Maxim:** Minimize praise of self; maximize dispraise of self.
  - Example: "Oh, it was nothing really" (downplaying one's achievement)
  - Example: "I'm not that great at it" (self-deprecation, even when skilled)

**Pair 3: Agreement and Sympathy** (about alignment and emotional response)
- **Agreement Maxim:** Minimize disagreement between self and other; maximize agreement.
  - Example: "Yes, you're right, but maybe we could also consider..." (prefacing disagreement with agreement)
  - Example: "I see what you mean" (acknowledging the other's point before diverging)
- **Sympathy Maxim:** Minimize antipathy between self and other; maximize sympathy.
  - Example: "I'm sorry to hear about your loss" (expressing sympathy even with acquaintances)
  - Example: "That must have been really difficult for you" (validating emotional experience)

#### How Leech Complements Grice

Grice's Cooperative Principle explains why we follow rules of informational efficiency. But people constantly violate these rules (they are vague, they say too much, they change the subject, they lie politely). Leech's Politeness Principle explains many of these apparent violations: **people sacrifice informational efficiency to maintain social harmony**.

For example, when someone asks "How do you like my new haircut?" and you think it looks terrible, Grice's maxim of Quality says you should be truthful. But Leech's Approbation Maxim says you should minimize dispraise of others. The result is often a hedged, indirect, or mildly positive response ("It's different!" "It suits you!") that is pragmatically polite but informationally imprecise.

#### Strengths and Limitations

**Strengths:**
- Explains why Gricean maxims are routinely flouted
- Provides clear, teachable maxims
- Works well for English and many Western languages

**Limitations:**
- Like B&L, may be too Western-centric (the Modesty Maxim has different force in different cultures)
- Treats politeness as a set of norms rather than a negotiated process
- Criticized for being too static and not accounting for context

### 3. Watts' Relational Work: Politeness Is Not What You Think

#### Overview

Richard J. Watts (2003), building on the work of Locher and Watts (2005), proposed a fundamentally different approach to politeness -- one that treats it as **emergent, negotiated, and context-dependent** rather than formulaic and rule-governed.

#### First-Order vs. Second-Order Politeness

**The core distinction:**
- **First-order politeness (politeness1):** How ordinary people actually perceive, talk about, and evaluate polite behavior in their daily lives. This is folk understanding of politeness.
- **Second-order politeness (politeness2):** The theoretical construct used by researchers -- a technical term within a theory of social behavior and language usage.

**Watts' criticism:** Brown and Levinson (and others) confuse these two levels. They claim their theoretical model (politeness2) describes universal behavior, but actually it describes a very specific (and culturally limited) version of politeness1. What Brown and Levinson defend as universal is "their conceptualisation of an idealized concept of politeness2, not...the ways in which groups of participants struggle over politeness1 in social interaction."

#### Relational Work

**Definition:** Relational work is "the 'work' individuals invest in negotiating relationships with others." It encompasses the full range of interpersonal behavior, not just polite behavior.

**The spectrum of relational work:**
- **Negatively marked behavior:** Rudeness, impoliteness, over-politeness (behavior that participants judge as inappropriate)
- **Unmarked behavior (politic behavior):** Behavior that participants construct as appropriate and expected for the ongoing interaction. This is the baseline -- it's not noticed or remarked upon.
- **Positively marked behavior (politeness):** Behavior that goes above and beyond what is expected -- behavior that participants notice and evaluate as particularly polite.

**The crucial insight:** Most of what we call "appropriate" social behavior is actually **politic** -- unremarkable, expected, baseline. True politeness is behavior that **exceeds** expectations. A waiter saying "What can I get you?" is politic (expected). A waiter saying "What a lovely dress! Can I get you started with something special today?" might be polite (exceeding expectations) -- or might be marked as over-polite / intrusive depending on context.

#### Critique of Brown and Levinson

Locher and Watts assert that Brown and Levinson's work is "not a theory of politeness but a theory of facework, dealing only with the mitigation of face-threatening acts." It fails to account for:
- Situations where face-threat mitigation is not a priority
- The way politeness is dynamically negotiated by participants
- The distinction between expected (politic) behavior and genuinely polite behavior
- The role of the hearer's evaluation in determining what counts as polite

**Key criticism:** The Brown and Levinson model is **static** -- "represented by a rule-like system of strategies and their linguistic realizations, with no dynamic activity of the participants' evaluations and perceptions of politeness in specific contexts."

**Why this matters for autism:** Watts' distinction between politic (expected) and polite (above-and-beyond) behavior is genuinely useful for autistic individuals. It helps explain why some "polite" behaviors feel invisible (they're actually politic -- baseline expectations that neurotypical people perform automatically) while others get noticed. It also helps explain why doing "too much" (over-politeness) can be as socially marked as doing too little.

### 4. Spencer-Oatey's Rapport Management: Beyond Face

#### Overview

Helen Spencer-Oatey (2000, 2005, 2008) developed the **Rapport Management** framework as a broader alternative to Brown and Levinson. Rather than focusing narrowly on face, she identifies three bases of rapport, four rapport orientations, and multiple domains of interaction where rapport can be managed or mismanaged.

#### Three Bases of Rapport

**1. Face Management**

Spencer-Oatey expands face beyond B&L's positive/negative distinction:

- **Quality Face:** The fundamental desire for people to evaluate us positively in terms of our **personal qualities** (competence, ability, appearance). "I want people to think I'm good at what I do."
- **Social Identity Face:** The fundamental desire for people to acknowledge and uphold our **social identities and roles** -- as a group leader, valued customer, close friend, parent, professional. "I want people to respect my role."
- **Relational Face:** Concern for the relationship itself and how it is perceived.

**2. Sociality Rights and Obligations**

These go beyond face to include fundamental beliefs about social entitlements:

- **Equity Rights:** The belief that we are entitled to personal consideration and fair treatment from others, including:
  - Cost-benefit consideration (being treated fairly in terms of effort and reward)
  - Fairness and reciprocity (mutual exchange)
  - Autonomy-control (having appropriate control over one's own actions)
- **Association Rights:** The belief that we are entitled to social involvement with others in keeping with the type of relationship we have. This includes:
  - Involvement (appropriate amount of interaction)
  - Empathy (having feelings acknowledged)
  - Respect (being treated with appropriate deference)

**3. Interactional Goals**

People enter interactions with goals (transactional and relational), and failure to achieve these goals can damage rapport independently of face considerations.

#### Four Rapport Orientations

People approach interactions with different orientations:
1. **Rapport enhancement:** Actively trying to improve the relationship
2. **Rapport maintenance:** Trying to keep the relationship stable
3. **Rapport neglect:** Not caring about the relational impact of one's behavior
4. **Rapport challenge:** Deliberately threatening the relationship

#### Why This Might Be More Applicable to Autistic Communication

Spencer-Oatey's framework offers several advantages for understanding autistic communication:

1. **Separating face from rights:** Many autistic communication difficulties are not about "face" in the B&L sense but about **equity rights** (being treated fairly) and **association rights** (being appropriately included). The rapport management framework captures these concerns explicitly.

2. **Quality face and social identity face:** Autistic individuals often care deeply about being seen as competent (quality face) and having their social roles recognized (social identity face), even when they struggle with the performative aspects of positive face maintenance.

3. **Interactional goals:** Autistic communication often prioritizes **transactional goals** (getting information, completing a task) over relational goals. Spencer-Oatey's framework recognizes both types of goals and explains conflicts that arise when interlocutors have different goal priorities.

4. **Rapport orientations:** The concept of rapport neglect helps explain situations where autistic individuals are perceived as impolite not because of hostile intent but because of **different prioritization of relational work**.

5. **Cross-neurotype applicability:** The framework was originally developed for cross-cultural pragmatics, making it well-suited for cross-neurotype communication analysis. The "double empathy problem" (Milton, 2012) -- where communication difficulties between autistic and non-autistic people are bidirectional -- maps naturally onto Spencer-Oatey's framework of rapport management between groups with different interactional norms.

### 5. The Gender Critique: Who Gets to Be "Polite"?

#### Lakoff's Foundational Work (1975)

Robin Lakoff's *Language and Woman's Place* (1975) was one of the earliest works to identify gendered patterns in politeness:
- Women use more hedging (phrases like "sort of," "kind of," "I think")
- Women use more polite forms ("Would you mind...")
- Women use more tag questions ("It's nice, isn't it?")
- Women use more indirect requests and commands
- Women use more "empty" adjectives ("divine," "lovely," "adorable")
- Women use more intensifiers ("so," "very," "really")

**Lakoff's interpretation:** Women's speech is more polite because women occupy a subordinate social position and must use linguistic deference to navigate a male-dominated world. Politeness, in this view, is partly a marker of powerlessness.

**Criticism of Lakoff:** This "deficit model" portrays women's speech as weak and deficient. Critics argue it reinforces sexist stereotypes by treating male speech norms as the unmarked default.

#### Holmes' Research (1995)

Janet Holmes (*Women, Men and Politeness*, 1995) provided extensive empirical data on gendered politeness:

- **Compliments:** Women use more compliments as positive politeness devices to build rapport and strengthen social bonds. Women generally perceive compliments as positive acts; men may perceive them as face-threatening (evaluative).
- **Tag questions:** Women use more facilitative tags (designed to include others in conversation and invite participation). Men use more tags that express genuine uncertainty.
- **Hedges:** Women use more positive politeness hedges (softening assertions to be inclusive). Men's hedges tend to express epistemic uncertainty.
- **Interruptions:** In mixed-gender conversations, men interrupt more frequently and for longer. Even in professional settings where women hold authority, male patients interrupt female physicians.

**Holmes' key insight:** Gender differences in politeness are not absolute but statistical patterns that vary significantly by context. Gender alone does not determine politeness strategy -- power, context, and individual variation all play roles.

#### The Double Bind

Gendered politeness norms create a **double bind** for women (Burrow, 2010):
- If women use polite, deferential language, they are perceived as less authoritative, less competent, and lower in social status
- If women use direct, authoritative language, they are perceived as rude, aggressive, or unfeminine
- Men using the same direct language are perceived as confident, decisive, and competent

**The structural problem:** Politeness norms are not neutral descriptions of behavior -- they encode and reproduce gender hierarchies. When "polite" behavior (hedging, deferring, softening) is socially prescribed for women but not for men, politeness becomes a mechanism for maintaining unequal power relations.

#### The Dominance vs. Difference Debate

Two competing explanations for gendered politeness patterns:
- **Dominance approach** (Lakoff, Zimmerman & West): Differences reflect male power over women. Women are polite because they are subordinate.
- **Difference approach** (Tannen): Differences reflect different socialization. Women and men grow up in different "cultures" with different norms for interaction.
- **Critical approach** (Cameron): Both dominance and difference are too simple. Gender is performed, not fixed, and language use is part of how gender is constructed.

### 6. The Power Critique: Politeness as Social Control

#### Fairclough and Critical Discourse Analysis

Norman Fairclough's work in Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA) provides the most systematic critique of politeness as a mechanism of social control:

**Key argument:** "Politeness is based upon recognition of differences of power, degrees of social distance" -- and it is oriented to **reproduce** these differences without change. Politeness norms don't just reflect power relations; they actively maintain them.

**How politeness functions as social control:**
- Politeness norms define who must defer to whom
- "Being polite" often means performing subordination (using hedges, apologies, indirect requests) toward those with more power
- Violating politeness norms is punished socially (being labeled rude, aggressive, difficult)
- Those with more power can violate norms with fewer consequences

#### Eelen's Critique (2001)

Gino Eelen's *A Critique of Politeness Theories* (2001) is one of the most comprehensive critiques of the field:

**Key arguments:**
1. **Speaker bias:** All major politeness theories focus on the **speaker's** production of polite behavior, ignoring the **hearer's** evaluation. But whether something is polite is determined by the hearer, not the speaker.
2. **Politeness1 vs. Politeness2:** Theorists confuse everyday understanding of politeness (politeness1) with their theoretical constructs (politeness2), and then incorrectly claim their constructs describe everyday reality.
3. **Ideological blindness:** The field has a "coherent and consistent ideology underlying it" that causes it to miss important aspects of everyday social reality -- particularly how politeness relates to power, inequality, and social struggle.
4. **The habitus alternative:** Drawing on Bourdieu's concept of habitus, Eelen proposes a more dynamic view that takes account of: the hearer's position and evaluative role; both politeness and impoliteness; and a bidirectional relationship between social structures and individual behavior.

#### Politeness as a "Miniature Panopticon"

From a critical sociolinguistic perspective, politeness functions as what has been called a "miniature panopticon" (referencing Foucault's concept of surveillance-based social control):
- Because we are constantly under social observation, we internalize politeness norms
- We police our own behavior to avoid being labeled "rude" or "disruptive"
- We police others' behavior by negatively evaluating those who violate norms
- This self-surveillance maintains social hierarchies without requiring overt force

**Examples of politeness as control:**
- Employees must use polite, deferential language with bosses, but bosses can be relatively direct with employees
- Students must use formal, respectful language with professors, but professors can be casual or even brusque with students
- In customer service, workers are required to perform politeness regardless of how they are treated
- Women are expected to be more linguistically polite than men, and are penalized more harshly for "rudeness"

#### The Institutional Dimension

The power critique extends to how institutions use politeness norms:
- Corporate "politeness training" teaches employees to perform deference
- "Professional communication" standards often privilege the communication styles of dominant groups
- Academic discourse norms can exclude or penalize direct communication styles
- Medical, legal, and educational settings all have politeness hierarchies that serve institutional power

#### Why This Matters for Autistic Individuals

The power critique of politeness is particularly relevant for autistic people because:

1. **Autistic directness is penalized:** Autistic communication tends to be more direct, literal, and efficient. This is consistently misread as rude in neurotypical contexts -- not because it is rude, but because it violates politeness norms designed around neurotypical communication patterns.

2. **Double standards:** Autistic individuals are expected to learn and perform neurotypical politeness norms, but there is little reciprocal accommodation of autistic communication norms by neurotypical speakers.

3. **Masking costs:** Many autistic individuals engage in extensive "masking" (performing neurotypical social behaviors), which is exhausting and has documented mental health costs. Politeness performance is a significant component of masking.

4. **Recognizing the system:** Understanding that politeness norms are socially constructed (not natural laws) and that they serve power functions can help autistic individuals make more informed decisions about when and how to engage with these norms. The goal is not to blindly comply but to understand the system well enough to navigate it strategically.
