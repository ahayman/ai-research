# Research Validation: Symbol-Based Communication — PECS, Makaton, and Visual Support Systems

## Validation Summary

The Symbol-Based Communication concept research is accurate and well-sourced. All major factual claims were independently verified. The document appropriately notes evidence-base limitations for Makaton and Blissymbols relative to PECS. No significant errors were found.

**Overall Confidence Rating: 9/10** — High confidence. All core claims confirmed.

## Claim-by-Claim Validation

### Claim 1: PECS developed by Andy Bondy and Lori Frost in 1985 at the Delaware Autism Program
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Multiple sources including Pyramid Educational Consultants (official PECS organization), Wikipedia, and Indiana University IRCA confirm PECS was developed in 1985 by Andy Bondy, PhD, and Lori Frost, MS, CCC-SLP, at the Delaware Autism Program.
- **Note**: The first formal publication describing PECS appeared in 1994 (Bondy & Frost, 1994 in Focus on Autism and Other Developmental Disabilities), but the system was developed and implemented starting in 1985.
- **Sources**: [Pyramid Educational Consultants](https://pecsusa.com/pecs/), [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Picture_Exchange_Communication_System)

### Claim 2: PECS has six phases (I through VI as described)
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: The six phases are confirmed exactly as described in the research document: Phase I (How to Communicate/physical exchange), Phase II (Distance and Persistence), Phase III (Picture Discrimination), Phase IV (Sentence Structure), Phase V (Responding to Questions), Phase VI (Commenting). These match official PECS documentation.
- **Sources**: [Pyramid Educational Consultants](https://pecsusa.com/pecs/), [National Autism Resources](https://nationalautismresources.com/the-picture-exchange-communication-system-pecs/), [Indiana University IRCA](https://iidc.indiana.edu/irca/articles/what-is-the-picture-exchange-communication-system-or-pecs.html)

### Claim 3: Makaton has over 100,000 users in the UK
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Multiple sources confirm "more than 100,000 children and adults in the UK use Makaton." Some sources cite even higher numbers (over 1 million users including those who have been exposed to Makaton through media like Mr Tumble/Something Special). The 100,000 figure appears to refer to active primary users.
- **Sources**: [CPD Online](https://cpdonline.co.uk/knowledge-base/safeguarding/what-is-makaton/), [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Makaton)

### Claim 4: Makaton developed by Margaret Walker and colleagues
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED with minor correction
- **Evidence**: Makaton was developed at Botleys Park Hospital in Chertsey, Surrey. The name "Makaton" is derived from MArgaret Walker, KAtherine Johnston, and TONy Cornforth. The research document says "Royal Association for Deaf People" — this appears to be **inaccurate**. Sources consistently state Botleys Park Hospital, not the Royal Association for Deaf People. The Makaton Vocabulary Development Project was founded in 1976 by Margaret Walker.
- **Correction needed**: The institution should be Botleys Park Hospital, not "Royal Association for Deaf People."
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Makaton), [LD Network](https://ldnetwork.org/blog/the-power-of-makaton-communication-beyond-words/)

### Claim 5: Blissymbols has over 6,500 authorized symbols built from approximately 100-120 base elements
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: BCI's 2025 authorized vocabulary (BCI-AV 2025) contains 6,183 approved symbols. The claim of "over 6,500" is slightly overstated — the document itself corrects this later, noting "6,183 approved symbols." The base elements figure of "approximately 100-120" is confirmed by BCI documentation, which states learners need to master "approximately a maximum of 100 to 120 elements."
- **Note**: The "over 6,500" in the overview should read "over 6,100" or "approximately 6,200" to match the BCI-AV 2025 figure of 6,183. The document inconsistently uses "over 6,500" and "6,183" — the latter is more accurate.
- **Sources**: [BCI](https://www.blissymbolics.org/), [BCI-AV 2025](https://blissymbols.com/index.php/symbol-files-2025), [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blissymbols)

### Claim 6: Blissymbols developed by Charles K. Bliss (1897-1985), originally developed 1942-1949, inspired by Chinese characters
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Wikipedia and BCI confirm Charles K. Bliss developed the system while a refugee, inspired by Chinese characters. The system was originally called "Semantography."
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blissymbols), [BCI](https://www.blissymbolics.org/)

### Claim 7: Blissymbols pioneered for AAC by Shirley McNaughton in 1971 at the Ontario Crippled Children's Centre
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Sources confirm Shirley McNaughton introduced Blissymbols for AAC use with children with cerebral palsy at the Ontario Crippled Children's Centre (now Holland Bloorview). The year 1971 is consistent with available sources.
- **Sources**: [ISAAC](https://isaac-online.org/the-symbols-of-bliss/), [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blissymbols)

### Claim 8: ARASAAC has shown the best iconicity across grammatical categories in comparative studies
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Research confirms ARASAAC pictograms showed greater iconicity and ease of learning than PCS and Blissymbols across nouns, verbs, and adjectives. ARASAAC was significantly more transparent than SPC in these categories. A 2024 Frontiers in Psychology study provided transparency and translucency indices for 1,525 ARASAAC pictograms.
- **Sources**: [ARASAAC Research](https://aulaabierta.arasaac.org/en/empirical-analysis-of-the-formal-characteristics-of-the-symbols-pictographic-of-arasaac), [Frontiers 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2024.1467796/full)

### Claim 9: PECS meta-analysis (Flippin et al., 2010) found PECS effective for communication frequency but weaker evidence for speech gains
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: The Flippin et al. 2010 meta-analysis in AJSLP is a well-cited study confirming increased communication frequency through PECS, with more modest evidence for speech production gains.
- **Sources**: [ASHA/AJSLP 2010](https://pubs.asha.org/doi/10.1044/1058-0360(2010/09-0022))

### Claim 10: Social stories developed by Carol Gray, 1991
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Carol Gray developed Social Stories in 1991. This is widely cited and well-established in the autism literature.

## Issues Found

### Minor Issues
1. **Makaton institutional origin**: The research states Makaton was developed "at the Royal Association for Deaf People." Verified sources consistently state it was developed at Botleys Park Hospital, Chertsey, Surrey. The Makaton Charity is a separate entity from the Royal Association for Deaf People. **Correction recommended.**
2. **Blissymbols count inconsistency**: The document uses "over 6,500" in the overview and "6,183" when citing BCI-AV 2025. The 6,183 figure is the verified number from BCI. The overview should be corrected to approximately 6,200 or "over 6,100" to avoid overstating.

### No Major Issues Found
The document is substantively accurate and well-organized.

## Recommendations for Downstream Content

1. Correct the Makaton institutional origin from "Royal Association for Deaf People" to "Botleys Park Hospital, Chertsey, Surrey."
2. Standardize the Blissymbols count to the verified 6,183 figure from BCI-AV 2025.
3. The PECS six-phase structure is fully verified and suitable for detailed diagramming.
4. The ARASAAC iconicity finding is well-supported and notable — worth highlighting as it supports open-source AAC symbol availability.
