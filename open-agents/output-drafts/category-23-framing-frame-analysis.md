# Category 23: Framing and Frame Analysis

## The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

---

## How to Read This Document

Every utterance arrives inside a frame. Before you can read what a message *means*, you have to read what *kind of thing it is*: a joke, a request, a threat, a performance, a diagnosis, an ordinary question, a test, a ritual, an attack. That reading is not a detail added on top of the meaning. It is the structural precondition for the meaning being recoverable at all. The same sentence -- "Are you sure about that?" -- means completely different things inside the frames *collaborative check*, *parental concern*, *courtroom cross-examination*, *flirtation*, and *setup-for-humiliation*. Frames are not decoration. They are the organizing layer that tells every subsequent pragmatic system (implicature, politeness, register, common ground, turn-taking) what game is being played.

Framing is therefore the category that sits underneath almost everything else in this hub. It is also the category that matters most when things go wrong. Most serious pragmatic harm is not about individual words -- it is about which frame got installed around those words, and who got to install it. For autistic readers, who may weight contextual cues differently than the non-autistic speakers around them (Pellicano & Burr, 2012; Vermeulen, 2012), making framing explicit is a way of taking a process that is usually invisible and laying it out where it can be examined. This document gives you the vocabulary -- Goffman's *Frame Analysis*, Bateson's meta-communication, Fillmore's frame semantics, Lakoff's cognitive framing, Entman's framing functions, Tannen's interactive frames -- and shows how that vocabulary applies to the specific situations where framing becomes load-bearing: interactions, institutions, records, relationships, and the manipulation patterns catalogued in the Pragmatic Manipulation Sub-Hub.

This document is reference documentation, not a course. You do not need to read it linearly. Use the table of contents, the cross-links, and the diagnostic sections as entry points.

## 1. What a Frame Is

### 1.1 Definition

A **frame** is the answer to the question *"what is it that's going on here?"* (Goffman, 1974, p. 8). It is the interpretive schema that lets participants treat a stretch of experience as one *kind* of activity rather than another. Frames classify what counts as a move, what counts as relevant, what counts as normal, what counts as a problem, and what everyone is supposed to do next.

Frames operate at a level above individual meanings. A word has a meaning; a sentence has a meaning; but a frame tells you what *type of thing* the word and the sentence are part of. When a doctor says "this will just pinch a little" while holding a needle, the *frame* is "medical procedure with reassurance-talk." The frame makes the sentence readable as not-a-threat, even though the literal content ("I am about to cause you pain") would be alarming in most other frames.

### 1.2 The Bateson Origin: Meta-Communication

Gregory Bateson (1972) introduced the foundational idea in his essay "A Theory of Play and Fantasy." Watching monkeys at a zoo play-fighting, Bateson observed that the monkeys' bites were not bites the way a real bite would be. The animals had somehow communicated to each other: *this is play*. That meta-message -- the message *about the type of message* -- is the primal frame move. "This is play" is not a statement about the world; it is a statement about how to read everything else that is about to happen. Bateson called this meta-communication, and he argued that every interaction carries a meta-communicative layer specifying the frame.

The core insight: **the meta-communicative layer must be present, or the direct communication cannot be interpreted.** A bite without "this is play" is an attack. The same physical action means different things inside different frames. This is why frame analysis is not a philosophical luxury -- it is the infrastructure of interpretation.

### 1.3 The Goffman Elaboration: Primary Frameworks

Erving Goffman's *Frame Analysis: An Essay on the Organization of Experience* (1974) took Bateson's insight and built a comprehensive vocabulary for how frames structure everyday life. Goffman distinguished:

- **Natural frameworks** -- the schemata we use to read unguided physical events. "The rain is falling." "The rock fell." No agent, no intention, just physics.
- **Social frameworks** -- the schemata we use to read guided, intentional action by agents. "She is arguing." "He is apologizing." "They are negotiating."

Every situation is read through one of these primary frameworks (or a combination). The same event -- a man falling to the ground -- is read through a natural framework if we think he slipped, through a social framework if we think he is performing a stunt, and through a *different* social framework if we think he is being assaulted.

Goffman then showed that primary frameworks can be transformed in systematic ways -- we will return to these transformations in Section 3.

### 1.4 Frames as Vocabulary-Installed Structures

Charles Fillmore (1982, 1985) added a crucial piece from cognitive linguistics. In Fillmore's **frame semantics**, individual words evoke whole structured scenes. The verb *buy* pulls in the whole COMMERCIAL_TRANSACTION frame: a buyer, a seller, goods, money, an exchange. You cannot understand *buy* without the frame. Using the word installs the frame.

This matters because **vocabulary choice is frame-choice**. The same event can be labeled *argument*, *fight*, *discussion*, *disagreement*, *row*, *confrontation*, *dispute*, *difference of opinion*, or *misunderstanding* -- and each of those words pulls in a different frame with a different cast, a different set of expectations, and a different set of next-actions. The person who gets to pick the label gets to pick the frame. The frame then determines what is readable as normal and what is readable as a problem.

## 2. How Frames Work

### 2.1 Frame Selection: Which Frame Gets Chosen

For any given situation, several frames are usually *possible*. Frame selection is the process of settling on one. Selection happens through cues: the physical setting, the participants, the prior activity, the opening moves, the vocabulary, the tone, the timing. A room with desks and a person at the front cues the *lecture* frame. A raised voice and a flushed face cue the *conflict* frame. A white coat and a stethoscope cue the *medical visit* frame.

Frame selection is normally fast and non-conscious, which is part of why it is dangerous. When a frame has been selected without being noticed, it runs in the background and shapes every subsequent interpretation without anyone examining it. Making frame selection visible is one of the main protective moves this category offers.

### 2.2 Frame Installation: Getting a Frame Adopted

A frame becomes operative when participants adopt it. Adoption can happen in several ways:

- **Tacit uptake.** Someone begins behaving as if a frame is in effect, and nobody contests it. The frame is now installed by default.
- **Explicit proposal.** Someone says "let's talk about this as colleagues, not as friends" or "I want to be clear that this is a formal conversation." The frame is proposed and -- if not refused -- adopted.
- **Authority-backed imposition.** A person with institutional power declares the frame: "I'm going to need you to treat this as a disciplinary meeting." Refusing is possible but costly.
- **Vocabulary trigger.** The right word evokes the frame without anyone announcing it. Saying *assessment* triggers the assessment frame; saying *interview* triggers the interview frame; saying *intervention* triggers the intervention frame.
- **Environmental priming.** The setting, clothing, props, and preceding activity install a frame before a word is spoken.

### 2.3 Frame Maintenance: Keeping a Frame Running

Once a frame is installed, it has to be maintained. Participants maintain frames by behaving consistently with them, by using appropriate vocabulary, by sanctioning deviations, and by repairing frame slippages. A formal meeting is maintained by people not making casual jokes; a therapy session is maintained by the therapist redirecting small-talk back to therapy-talk; a classroom is maintained by the teacher treating off-task behavior as off-task. Maintenance work is constant and usually invisible, but it is what keeps the frame alive.

### 2.4 Frame Breaks

A **frame break** is a moment when the current frame suddenly fails and participants must re-find their footing. Frame breaks happen when the maintenance work fails, when a participant refuses to play along, when an outside event intrudes, or when a contradiction becomes unavoidable. Goffman (1974) describes the acute disorientation of frame breaks: for a few seconds, nobody knows what kind of thing is going on. That disorientation is diagnostic -- if you feel it, a frame just broke.

## 3. Types of Frames

Goffman (1974) identified several systematic ways in which primary frameworks get transformed into more complex frames. He called these transformations **keyings** and **fabrications**.

### 3.1 Keyings

A **keying** is a transformation of a primary framework that is done openly, with all participants in on the transformation. A play-fight is a keying of a fight. A rehearsal is a keying of a performance. A demonstration lesson is a keying of an actual classroom event. Everybody involved knows the keying is in effect. The key changes what the action *means*: the play-fighter who lands a punch has made a mistake, not committed an assault.

Keyings can be stacked -- Goffman calls this **lamination**. A play-fight being described in a story is a keying (the story) of a keying (the play-fight) of a primary event (a fight). Each layer transforms the one below. In conversation, people routinely laminate: "Remember when John said [imitated voice] 'I'm the manager now'?" -- a quotation (layer 3) of a performance (layer 2) of a claim (layer 1).

### 3.2 Fabrications

A **fabrication** is a frame transformation that is *not* shared by all participants -- where one or more participants are intentionally being kept in the wrong frame. A con is a fabrication. A sting operation is a fabrication. A staged event presented as real is a fabrication. Goffman distinguishes **benign fabrications** (surprise parties, April Fools' jokes, harmless pranks) from **exploitative fabrications** (scams, entrapment, gaslighting). The structure is the same: one party thinks the frame is X; the other party knows the frame is actually Y; the asymmetry is being used.

**This is the structural definition of much manipulation.** When the Pragmatic Manipulation Sub-Hub talks about "frame attacks," it is talking about exploitative fabrications in Goffman's sense. The target is being operated on from inside a frame they do not know is in effect.

### 3.3 Interactive Frames

Tannen and Wallat (1987) developed the concept of **interactive frames** in a landmark study of pediatric examinations. A pediatrician examining a child with cerebral palsy was simultaneously operating in multiple frames: the *examination* frame (with the child), the *consultation* frame (with the mother), the *teaching* frame (with observing students), and the *reporting* frame (dictating into a tape recorder). The pediatrician shifted frames constantly, cued by whom she was addressing, and each shift changed her vocabulary, tone, and stance.

Tannen and Wallat distinguished interactive frames (the *kind of activity* in progress -- examination, consultation, teaching) from **knowledge schemas** (the participants' expectations about the content). The two interact: what counts as relevant inside the examination frame differs from what counts as relevant inside the consultation frame, even when the content is identical.

### 3.4 Linguistic / Semantic Frames

Fillmore's frame-semantics frames (Section 1.4) are a different kind of frame. They are lexical structures -- the COMMERCIAL_TRANSACTION frame evoked by *buy*, the RISK frame evoked by *risk*, the REVENGE frame evoked by *avenge*. Each word has, built into its meaning, a whole cast of participants and a structured scenario. The vocabulary choice installs the frame.

### 3.5 Institutional Frames

Some frames are maintained by institutions rather than by moment-to-moment interaction. *Student*, *patient*, *employee*, *defendant*, *claimant*, *service user*, *applicant* -- each of these is an institutional frame that persists across encounters and pre-loads the next interaction. Institutional frames are particularly hard to contest because they are backed by structural authority, paperwork, and routine.

### 3.6 Media and Public Discourse Frames

Entman (1993) identified four functions of frames in media and public discourse, which apply equally to ordinary interaction:

1. **Problem definition** -- what is being identified as the problem?
2. **Causal diagnosis** -- what is being identified as the cause?
3. **Moral evaluation** -- who is being identified as good, bad, responsible, blameless?
4. **Treatment recommendation** -- what is being proposed as the solution?

A single framing move can accomplish all four at once. "She's just being difficult" defines the problem (difficulty), diagnoses the cause (her disposition), evaluates morally (she is at fault), and implies a treatment (manage her, override her, discount her). Entman's four functions are a diagnostic checklist: when you suspect a frame is being installed, ask which of these four it is doing.

## 4. Reframing

**Reframing** is the act of replacing one frame with another. The same event, the same facts, the same words -- but the frame changes, and with it everything that follows from the frame.

### 4.1 Reframing as Therapeutic Technique

Watzlawick, Weakland, and Fisch (1974) developed reframing into a core technique of brief therapy in their book *Change: Principles of Problem Formation and Problem Resolution*. Their insight was that many problems are sustained not by the underlying facts but by the frame that has been placed around the facts. If the frame changes, the problem either dissolves or becomes tractable. A child's "stubbornness" reframed as "persistence" is the same behavior evaluated from inside a different frame -- but the new frame makes different next-actions available. The therapeutic reframe does not deny the facts; it contests the frame in which the facts have been interpreted.

### 4.2 Reframing as Defensive Move

This is the use that matters most for autistic readers dealing with manipulation. When a frame is being installed on you that misrepresents your situation, the protective move is to refuse the frame and substitute a more accurate one. Every page of the Pragmatic Manipulation Sub-Hub opens with a one-sentence *protective reframe* -- a reformulation of the situation that displaces the frame the manipulation is trying to install. The reframe is not denial. It is frame-substitution, grounded in evidence.

Example, from the sub-hub: "a meltdown is a neurological response to overload, not a behavior, a choice, an attack, or a manipulation; the framing of meltdowns as any of these things is the manipulation, not the meltdown" (post-meltdown shaming page). The sentence contests the frame that has been installed around meltdowns and substitutes one that the evidence supports.

### 4.3 Reframing as Manipulative Move

Reframing is also what manipulators do. DARVO is a reframe ("this is about my mistreatment"). Gaslighting is a reframe ("your perception is the problem"). Concern trolling is a reframe ("I'm just worried about you"). The technique is morally neutral -- what matters is whether the new frame is a *more accurate* description of the situation or a *less accurate* one that serves the reframer's interests. Frame analysis is what lets you tell the difference.

## 5. Frame Attacks and Frame Swaps

A **frame attack** is the deliberate installation of a frame that misrepresents a situation in a way that harms one of its participants. A **frame swap** is the specific move of replacing one operative frame with another mid-interaction. These are the core technical moves inside manipulation.

### 5.1 DARVO as Frame Swap

DARVO (Deny, Attack, Reverse Victim and Offender) is structurally a frame swap. The initial frame is *we are discussing the perpetrator's behavior*. The DARVO move replaces that frame with *we are discussing the perpetrator's mistreatment*. The original topic has not been refuted; it has been *reframed out of relevance*. The new frame pulls in a new cast of characters (the perpetrator as victim), a new moral evaluation (the person raising the concern as the real wrongdoer), and a new proposed remedy (the person raising the concern should apologize). Entman's four functions have all been reassigned in one move.

Frame analysis gives you the technical machinery for naming the swap. When you can say "the frame has just been replaced," you have a foothold that naming the emotion alone (hurt, confusion, anger) does not give you. See the Pragmatic Manipulation Sub-Hub page on DARVO for the full treatment.

### 5.2 Records as Pre-Installed Frames

Educational records, medical records, social services assessments, HR files, and court documents frame people in ways that subsequent readers encounter *before they meet the person*. The record is a frame that has been installed in advance and is already running when the next interaction begins. The subsequent reader reads the person *through* the record, which means the person has to do interpretive work *against* the pre-installed frame rather than fresh work from scratch. This is especially consequential for autistic readers, because records written by non-autistic assessors often encode the assessor's frame of autism-as-deficit rather than a frame the autistic person would recognize as their own experience.

Frame analysis is the technical tool for recognizing the record as a frame rather than as a neutral description.

### 5.3 Assessment as Frame Attack

Assessment situations -- clinical assessments, eligibility assessments, capacity assessments, fitness assessments -- are structurally frame-impositions. The assessor arrives with a frame (the *assessment protocol*) and reads the person through it. The person being assessed has limited ability to contest the frame during the assessment itself, because contesting it is often read as non-cooperation, which is itself interpreted inside the assessor's frame. The assessment then produces a record (Section 5.2) that carries the frame forward.

The Pragmatic Manipulation Sub-Hub flags social services assessments of autistic adults as one of the most important contexts where frame analysis is the operative tool. The assessment frames the autistic adult in ways that subsequent decisions operate within. Recognizing the framing *as* a framing is the precondition for contesting it.

### 5.4 "Everyone Agrees" as Frame Installation

The bandwagon move -- *everyone thinks this*, *the whole family is worried*, *we all see it*, *your friends have noticed too* -- is a frame move. What is being installed is not the specific content but the *frame* of a unanimous chorus against which the target's perception looks like an outlier. Once the chorus frame is in place, the target has to argue against an entire group rather than a single person. The chorus may be real, partially real, or fabricated; frame analysis lets you see that the move is the same regardless, because the move is the *installation of a frame of consensus*. See the sub-hub page on bandwagon/isolation.

### 5.5 The Friendship Frame as Protective Scaffolding (and as Cover)

Friendship is itself a frame. When friendship is real, the frame provides protective scaffolding: people inside it extend trust, share vulnerabilities, and interpret ambiguous behavior charitably. But the same scaffolding makes exploitation possible when the frame is being held by someone who is not acting as a friend. The exploitation operates *inside* the friendship frame and is shielded by the frame's interpretive generosity. This is the mechanism behind mate-crime (see the sub-hub's friendships/mate-crime context page) and behind many workplace-bullying patterns as well. The friendship frame is not the problem; the frame is the scaffolding that makes the problem hard to see from inside.

### 5.6 "Fights" vs. "Control": The Relationship-Level Frame

Evan Stark's (2007) central distinction in *Coercive Control* between "fights" and "control" is fundamentally a frame question. Is this relationship being read through the frame *this couple fights sometimes* or through the frame *one person is subjecting the other to ongoing coercive control*? The behaviors look different from inside each frame. The interventions are different. The safety implications are different. Frame analysis is the diagnostic tool for the difference. See the sub-hub's intimate-partner-relationships context page.

## 6. Frame Analysis as Diagnostic Tool

This section distills the practical use. The practical-research file (*category-23-framing-research-practical.md*) contains the extended treatment.

### 6.1 The Core Diagnostic Questions

When you suspect a frame is operating on you or being installed around a situation, work through these questions:

1. **What frame is currently in effect?** Name it. "This is being read as X."
2. **Who installed it?** Did you choose it, or did someone else? If someone else, who?
3. **Does the frame fit the facts?** What does the frame predict you should be seeing? What are you actually seeing?
4. **What does the frame hide?** Frames both reveal and conceal. Which aspects of the situation is this frame making invisible?
5. **Whose interests does the frame serve?** Who benefits from this reading? Who pays?
6. **Is there a more accurate frame?** Can you name one?
7. **What happens if you substitute the more accurate frame?** Does the situation become more tractable?

### 6.2 The Four Entman Checks

For any frame you suspect is in play, apply Entman's (1993) four functions:

- **Problem definition.** What is this frame saying the problem *is*?
- **Causal diagnosis.** What is this frame saying caused it?
- **Moral evaluation.** Who is this frame saying is at fault or in the right?
- **Treatment recommendation.** What is this frame saying should be done next?

If any of these answers feel wrong, the frame is likely mis-fit. If *all* of them feel wrong, the frame is a frame attack and you need to refuse it.

### 6.3 Signals That a Frame Is Being Installed on You

- A situation suddenly has a confident name attached to it that you did not pick.
- The vocabulary has shifted in a way that assigns responsibility differently.
- A previously mutual situation is being described with you as the asymmetric party (the difficult one, the unwell one, the irrational one).
- You are being asked to agree to characterizations ("we both know that...", "you have to admit...") that do prep-work for a frame you would not have chosen.
- Your objections are being interpreted as *evidence for the frame* rather than as disagreements with it.
- You feel a sudden disorientation or footing-loss and don't know why -- a frame break or frame swap may have just happened.

### 6.4 The Protective Moves

- **Name the frame out loud.** "I think we're reading this differently -- you're treating this as X, and I'm treating it as Y."
- **Refuse implicit uptake.** Don't agree to characterizations you don't endorse, even in passing.
- **Substitute a better frame.** Offer a reformulation. "I'd describe this differently -- here's how I see it."
- **Document the frame.** Written records let you revisit later whether the frame that was installed in real-time still makes sense with distance.
- **Seek outside readers.** Someone outside the frame can often see it more clearly than you can from inside. This is why autistic peer networks are structurally valuable (Crompton et al., 2020).

## 7. Frames Across Categories

Framing interacts with essentially every other category in this hub.

- **Category 2 (Implicature).** Implicatures are calculated *inside* a frame. The same utterance implicates different things inside different frames.
- **Category 3 (Politeness).** Politeness strategies are selected based on the frame. What counts as polite in a *formal-meeting* frame is different from what counts as polite in an *intimate-conversation* frame.
- **Category 7 (Register).** Register is how you perform the current frame linguistically. Register choice installs and maintains the frame.
- **Category 9 (Presupposition).** Presuppositions are the assumptions baked into a frame. Questioning a presupposition is a way of contesting a frame.
- **Category 12 (Narrative).** Narratives install frames over whole stretches of experience. "The story of what happened" is always a frame.
- **Category 13 (Topic Management).** Topic shifts are often frame shifts in disguise. Changing the subject can change the frame.
- **Category 16 (Common Ground).** Frames specify what is supposed to be common ground. Installing a frame installs a whole schema of assumed-shared assumptions.
- **Category 22 (Social Hierarchy).** Who gets to install a frame is a power question. Authority backs framing.
- **Pragmatic Manipulation Sub-Hub.** Every pattern in the sub-hub can be described as a frame move. The sub-hub depends on this category as its technical machinery for naming frame attacks, frame swaps, and frame installations.
