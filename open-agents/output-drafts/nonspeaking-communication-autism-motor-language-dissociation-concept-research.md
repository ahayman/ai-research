# Concept Research: Motor-Language Dissociation — Apraxia, Motor Planning, and the Speech-Cognition Gap

## Overview

The motor-language dissociation hypothesis proposes that in many non-speaking autistic individuals, the inability to produce speech reflects a breakdown in motor planning and execution rather than a deficit in language comprehension, cognitive capacity, or communicative intent. This hypothesis has profound implications: if the primary barrier to communication is motor output rather than linguistic competence, the entire clinical approach to non-speaking autism must shift from trying to build language through behavioral drills and comprehension-focused speech therapy to providing motor-bypass routes to expression through AAC, typing, and alternative output channels. The hypothesis reframes non-speaking autism from a cognitive disorder to a motor access disorder.

The evidence for motor-language dissociation comes from multiple converging lines of research. Motor difficulties are extraordinarily prevalent in autism — present in an estimated 50-95% of autistic individuals regardless of IQ, with the most recent large-scale estimate at approximately 87%. Childhood apraxia of speech (CAS) — a motor speech planning disorder where the brain has difficulty coordinating the jaw, lip, tongue, and respiratory movements needed for speech — shows meaningful overlap with autism, though the exact prevalence is debated. One widely cited study (Tierney et al. 2015) reported 63.6% co-occurrence, but this came from a small convenience sample of only 30 children specifically referred for speech/autism concerns and should be treated as preliminary rather than definitive. Other research suggests more modest overlap, and a 2023 retrospective chart review found that children with comorbid ASD were "no more likely to have severe CAS than children without autism." The relationship between autism and apraxia is real but its precise dimensions remain uncertain.

Beyond apraxia specifically, the broader motor system in autism shows consistent dysfunction. The cerebellum — critical for motor coordination, timing, and speech production — is one of the most consistent sites of abnormality in autism, with reduced connectivity between cerebellar circuits and the cerebral cortical regions that underpin movement and language. Basal ganglia dysfunction, affecting motor planning and sequencing, has been associated with both repetitive behaviors and motor initiation difficulties in autism. Catatonia, affecting approximately 10% of autistic individuals (meta-analytic estimate: 10.4%, 95% CI: 5.8-18.0%), can produce motor initiation failure, extreme slowness, and mutism that overlaps substantially with the features attributed to "severe autism." Selective mutism — historically defined as anxiety-based speech loss — shows significant co-occurrence with autism, though prevalence estimates range widely from 11.7% in population-based studies to 63% in clinical samples. The most compelling evidence for motor-language dissociation comes from studies showing that fine motor skills predict expressive (but not receptive) language abilities in minimally verbal autistic adults, and that motor skills are the most significant predictor of the gap between what non-speaking autistic individuals understand and what they can express.

## Core Components

### Component 1: Childhood Apraxia of Speech (CAS) in Autism
- **Definition**: CAS is a motor speech disorder in which the brain has difficulty planning and coordinating the complex sequences of movements needed for speech production (jaw, lip, tongue, velum, larynx, respiratory muscles). The individual knows what they want to say but cannot reliably execute the motor plan to say it.
- **Prevalence in autism (range of findings)**:
  - Tierney et al. 2015: 63.6% (21 of 33 children) — but from a clinical convenience sample of only 30 children specifically referred for speech/language/autism concerns at Penn State Hershey Medical Center. This figure should be interpreted as preliminary evidence from a small, non-representative sample.
  - Other clinical samples: 64-65% in some studies, consistent with Tierney but from similarly selected populations
  - Contrasting evidence: A 2023 retrospective chart review (PMC10205100) found children with comorbid ASD (33.6% of CAS sample) were "no more likely to have severe CAS than children without autism"
  - Research suggesting CAS in autism may be similar to general population prevalence, meaning the apparent overlap could reflect referral bias
- **Diagnostic challenges**: CAS is already difficult to diagnose in neurotypical children; in autistic children, the diagnosis is further complicated by limited speech output (you need enough speech attempts to identify the characteristic inconsistency pattern), co-occurring oral-motor difficulties, and behaviors that may be misattributed to autism rather than CAS
- **Clinical significance**: If a substantial proportion of non-speaking autistic children have CAS, their lack of speech reflects a motor programming failure rather than a failure to understand or have language. Treatment implications differ dramatically: CAS requires motor-based speech therapy approaches (e.g., DTTC — Dynamic Temporal and Tactile Cueing) while language delays require vocabulary and comprehension interventions.

### Component 2: Broader Motor Planning Difficulties (Dyspraxia)
- **Prevalence**: Motor difficulties affect 50-95% of autistic individuals across the entire spectrum, regardless of IQ or language level; the most recent meta-estimate is approximately 87%
- **Types of motor difficulties**:
  - **Praxis deficits**: Difficulty with planning and executing purposeful movements (gestures, tool use, novel motor sequences); documented extensively in autism research (PMC3548336)
  - **Imitation difficulties**: Autistic individuals often have difficulty imitating motor actions, which directly affects learning through modeling
  - **Fine motor deficits**: Difficulty with precision movements including writing, pointing, and button pressing — all relevant to AAC access
  - **Gross motor coordination**: Difficulties with gait, balance, and whole-body coordination
  - **Oral-motor coordination**: Difficulties with the complex motor sequences required for speech production — jaw movement, tongue placement, lip rounding, velopharyngeal closure, respiratory coordination
- **Motor difficulties as feature vs. co-occurrence**: A 2023 review (PMC10725993) asks whether motor problems in autism are a co-occurring condition or a core feature of the autism phenotype itself. Growing evidence suggests motor difficulties are intrinsic to autism rather than an incidental accompaniment.
- **Relationship to speech**: The motor system required for speech is among the most complex motor systems in the human body — rapid, precisely timed coordination of approximately 100 muscles in the face, mouth, throat, chest, and abdomen. If autism involves widespread motor planning difficulties, the motor system for speech may be disproportionately affected because of its extreme complexity.

### Component 3: The Receptive-Expressive Language Gap
- **Key finding**: In minimally verbal autistic individuals, fine motor skills are significantly associated with expressive (but not receptive) language abilities. This is the most direct evidence for motor-language dissociation: the ability to understand language is independent of motor skills, but the ability to express language through speech depends on them.
- **Receptive-expressive discrepancy**: Motor skills are the most significant predictor of the gap between receptive and expressive language in minimally verbal autistic children and adolescents. The larger the motor deficit, the larger the gap between what the individual understands and what they can say.
- **Assessment implications**: Standard language assessments typically require motor responses (pointing, selecting, speaking), which means they systematically underestimate the receptive language abilities of individuals with motor planning difficulties. Eye-tracking and neural measurement methods that bypass motor output can reveal language comprehension that behavioral tests miss.
- **Neural evidence**: Neuroimaging studies of minimally verbal autistic children report intact neural markers of lexico-semantic processing (brain responses to word meaning) despite an absence of spoken language, suggesting that the language processing system may be functional even when the motor output system for speech is not.

### Component 4: Catatonia in Autism
- **Prevalence**: Meta-analysis of 7 studies (969 individuals with ASD) found pooled prevalence of 10.4% (95% CI: 5.8-18.0%); actual prevalence likely higher due to diagnostic overshadowing
- **Core features (Wing & Shah 2000)**: (1) Increased slowness in movement and verbal responses, (2) Difficulty initiating and completing actions, (3) Increased reliance on prompts, (4) Increased passivity and apparent lack of motivation
- **Motor features relevant to communication**: Freezing during actions, very slow voluntary movements, stopping midway through movements, requiring prompting to complete actions, mutism, posturing, negativism
- **Diagnostic overlap with autism**: Catatonic symptoms (motor stereotypies, mannerisms, rituals, mutism, echolalia, negativism) overlap substantially with behaviors commonly attributed to autism itself, making it challenging to distinguish exacerbation of autistic features from onset of catatonia. Some individuals diagnosed with "severe autism" and "intellectual disability" may have undiagnosed catatonia contributing to their apparent unresponsiveness and mutism.
- **Treatment implications**: Catatonia is treatable (benzodiazepines, ECT in severe cases), which means some cases of apparent non-speaking autism may include a treatable motor initiation component that is being missed

### Component 5: Selective Mutism and Autism
- **Definition**: Selective mutism (SM) is characterized by consistent failure to speak in specific social situations despite speaking in others, traditionally attributed to anxiety
- **Co-occurrence with autism (range)**:
  - Population-based studies: A Norwegian study (2008-2023, N=1,682) found 11.7% overlap between SM and autism diagnoses
  - Clinical samples: Steffenburg et al. 2018 found 63% of children with SM met criteria for ASD; other clinical studies report 63-80% overlap
  - The wide range (11.7% to 63%+) reflects fundamental differences between population-based and clinical sampling
- **Diagnostic exclusion controversy**: European diagnostic manuals list autism as an exclusion criterion for selective mutism, but research increasingly suggests this exclusion may not be empirically justified; the Norwegian population-based study specifically investigated this question
- **Relevance to motor-language dissociation**: SM is traditionally conceptualized as anxiety-based, but in autistic individuals, the mechanism may be different — involving motor initiation difficulty, sensory overload interfering with motor planning for speech, or demand avoidance rather than (or in addition to) social anxiety
- **Shared genetic basis**: The Norwegian study suggests a partially shared genetic basis between autism, selective mutism, and social anxiety disorder

### Component 6: Neurological Mechanisms
- **Cerebellar dysfunction**: The cerebellum is one of the most consistent sites of abnormality in ASD. It forms closed-loop circuits with cerebral cortical regions underpinning movement, language, and social processing. Cerebellar connections to Broca's area (the primary speech production region) mean that cerebellar dysfunction can directly impair speech planning and execution. Research shows decreased connectivity across the motor execution network in autistic children compared to neurotypical controls.
- **Basal ganglia dysfunction**: The basal ganglia control planning aspects of actions and regulate force production. In ASD, basal ganglia dysfunction has been associated with increased repetitive/stereotyped motor behaviors and may contribute to motor initiation difficulties that affect speech production.
- **Cortical-subcortical connectivity**: Reduced connectivity between frontal motor planning regions and subcortical motor execution structures (cerebellum, basal ganglia) may explain why autistic individuals can process language (cortical) but cannot reliably produce it (requiring cortical-subcortical coordination).
- **Complex information processing model (Minshew)**: Nancy Minshew proposed that autism involves a generalized deficit in complex information processing — any task requiring coordination across distributed brain networks is affected, while tasks that can be processed locally are preserved or enhanced. Speech requires coordination across multiple brain regions and motor systems simultaneously — making it particularly vulnerable to the connectivity disruptions seen in autism.

## How It Works

### Step 1: Language Comprehension (Intact in Many Non-Speaking Individuals)
- **Process**: Auditory language input is processed through temporal cortical regions (Wernicke's area and surrounding cortex); semantic meaning is extracted; syntactic structure is parsed
- **Evidence**: Neural markers of language processing (ERP components, fMRI activation) are present in some minimally verbal autistic individuals, suggesting intact receptive language processing
- **Motor independence**: Language comprehension does not require motor output — understanding can occur entirely within cortical language networks

### Step 2: Communicative Intent Formation (Often Intact)
- **Process**: The individual formulates a thought or communicative goal — something they want to say, request, or express
- **Evidence**: Behavioral indicators of communicative intent (reaching, pointing attempts, leading, vocalizations, eye gaze toward desired objects, frustration behavior) are present in many non-speaking autistic individuals
- **Motor independence**: Intent formation occurs in prefrontal and limbic circuits and does not require motor execution

### Step 3: Motor Planning for Speech (Where Breakdown Occurs)
- **Process**: Converting communicative intent into the motor plan for speech requires: selecting the correct phonemes (sound units), organizing them into syllables and words (phonological encoding), programming the precise timing and force of approximately 100 muscles (articulatory planning), and coordinating respiratory support (breathing)
- **Breakdown points**: Apraxia disrupts the programming of articulatory movements; cerebellar dysfunction disrupts timing and coordination; basal ganglia dysfunction disrupts motor initiation; general motor planning difficulties affect the sequencing of the complex motor chain
- **Result**: The individual has language, has intent, but cannot execute the motor plan for speech — or can execute it only unreliably, slowly, or partially

### Step 4: Motor Execution (Often Impaired Across Domains)
- **Process**: The motor plan is transmitted from cortical motor areas through subcortical structures (basal ganglia, cerebellum) to brainstem motor nuclei and cranial nerves that innervate the muscles of speech
- **Evidence of dysfunction**: Broader motor execution difficulties (gait, fine motor, imitation) are present in 50-95% of autistic individuals, suggesting that motor execution is broadly compromised, with speech as one of the most demanding motor tasks affected
- **Catatonic overlay**: In individuals with autism-related catatonia, motor initiation failure can add an additional layer of difficulty to an already compromised motor system

### Step 5: Alternative Output Pathways (AAC and Motor Bypass)
- **Process**: When the motor pathway for speech is compromised but language comprehension and communicative intent are intact, alternative motor pathways can be used: pointing (grosser motor than speech), typing (finger-to-key rather than 100-muscle coordination), eye-gaze selection (bypasses limb motor system entirely), or head/switch activation
- **Key insight**: AAC works because it provides a motor pathway to communication that is simpler than speech. The individual uses the same language system but outputs through a different motor channel — one that may be less affected by the specific motor planning difficulties that impair speech.

## Diagram Specifications

### Diagram 1: The Motor-Language Dissociation Model
- **Type**: Architecture/systems diagram
- **Purpose**: Illustrates the separation between language processing (intact) and motor output (impaired) in non-speaking autism
- **Elements**:
  - Box 1: Language Comprehension System (Wernicke's area, semantic networks) — labeled "Often Intact"
  - Box 2: Communicative Intent (prefrontal, limbic) — labeled "Often Intact"
  - Box 3: Motor Planning for Speech (Broca's area → articulatory planning) — labeled "BREAKDOWN POINT"
  - Box 4: Motor Execution (basal ganglia, cerebellum, cranial nerves, muscles) — labeled "Often Impaired Broadly"
  - Alternative path: AAC bypass route from Box 2 directly to simplified motor output (pointing, typing, eye-gaze)
- **Connections**: Normal speech pathway through all four boxes; AAC bypass from intent to alternative motor output
- **Notes**: The key visual message: the problem is in boxes 3-4 (motor), not boxes 1-2 (language/cognition)

### Diagram 2: The Range of Motor Difficulties in Autism
- **Type**: Spectrum/range diagram
- **Purpose**: Maps the breadth and prevalence of motor difficulties across the autism spectrum
- **Elements**:
  - Fine motor: 50-95% affected (pointing, writing, button pressing)
  - Gross motor: 50-95% affected (gait, balance, coordination)
  - Praxis/motor planning: Widespread (gesture imitation, tool use, novel sequences)
  - Oral-motor coordination: Variable (chewing, tongue movement, lip coordination)
  - Speech motor planning (CAS): Prevalence debated (one small study: 63.6%; other estimates lower)
  - Motor initiation (catatonia): ~10% affected
- **Connections**: All emanate from central motor system; speech highlighted as the most demanding motor task
- **Notes**: Present the CAS figure with explicit notation about the small sample size and preliminary nature

### Diagram 3: CAS (Childhood Apraxia of Speech) — What Goes Wrong
- **Type**: Process flow with breakdown annotation
- **Purpose**: Shows the specific motor planning steps that fail in CAS
- **Elements**:
  - Normal pathway: Thought → Phoneme selection → Syllable assembly → Articulatory motor plan → Coordinated muscle movement → Speech
  - CAS disruption: The articulatory motor plan stage fails — correct phonemes are selected but the motor programs for producing them are inconsistent, error-prone, or inaccessible
  - Characteristics of CAS speech: Inconsistent errors, difficulty with longer/more complex words, groping articulatory movements, prosodic abnormalities
- **Connections**: Normal flow with CAS disruption annotated at the motor planning stage
- **Notes**: Distinguish CAS from other speech disorders (dysarthria = muscle weakness; phonological disorder = rule-based sound errors)

### Diagram 4: The Receptive-Expressive Gap
- **Type**: Diverging bar chart or visual comparison
- **Purpose**: Illustrates the discrepancy between receptive and expressive language in non-speaking autism and its relationship to motor skills
- **Elements**:
  - Left bar: Receptive language level (what the individual understands) — higher
  - Right bar: Expressive language level (what the individual can say) — much lower
  - Gap between bars: Labeled "Motor-mediated gap"
  - Annotation: "Fine motor skills predict expressive but NOT receptive language" (MIT Media Lab finding)
  - Additional annotation: "Motor skills are the most significant predictor of the receptive-expressive gap"
- **Connections**: Arrow from motor skills to the gap between bars
- **Notes**: This is the single most direct piece of evidence for motor-language dissociation

### Diagram 5: Catatonia in Autism — Features and Overlap
- **Type**: Venn diagram or overlap diagram
- **Purpose**: Shows the overlap between catatonic features and features commonly attributed to "severe autism"
- **Elements**:
  - Circle 1 (Catatonia): Motor freezing, extreme slowness, motor initiation failure, mutism, posturing, negativism, increased prompt dependency
  - Circle 2 (Severe Autism Features): Limited speech/mutism, apparent lack of motivation, stereotyped movements, echolalia, resistance to demands, apparent intellectual disability
  - Overlap zone: Mutism, stereotypies, prompt dependency, apparent lack of motivation, negativism
- **Connections**: Overlap region highlighted; note that catatonia is treatable (benzodiazepines, ECT)
- **Notes**: The implication: some individuals diagnosed with "severe autism" and presumed intellectually disabled may have undiagnosed, treatable catatonia

### Diagram 6: Neural Architecture of Speech Production
- **Type**: Brain diagram with circuit annotations
- **Purpose**: Maps the neural circuits involved in speech production and their known dysfunction in autism
- **Elements**:
  - Broca's area (left frontal): Speech planning and programming
  - Wernicke's area (left temporal): Language comprehension
  - Cerebellum: Motor timing and coordination (abnormal in ASD)
  - Basal ganglia: Motor initiation and sequencing (associated with repetitive behaviors in ASD)
  - Primary motor cortex: Motor execution commands
  - Cranial nerve nuclei (brainstem): Final motor output to speech muscles
  - Connecting pathways: Arcuate fasciculus, cortical-cerebellar loops, cortical-basal ganglia loops
- **Connections**: Arrows showing circuit paths; highlight disrupted connections in autism (cerebellar-cortical, frontal-posterior)
- **Notes**: Emphasize that disruption at any point in this complex circuit can produce non-speaking autism even with intact language comprehension

### Diagram 7: Alternative Motor Pathways — Why AAC Works
- **Type**: Comparative pathway diagram
- **Purpose**: Compares the motor demands of speech vs. different AAC access methods
- **Elements**:
  - Speech: ~100 muscles, millisecond precision timing, bilateral coordination, respiratory support → HIGHEST motor demand
  - Pointing/touchscreen: Single limb, gross pointing movement → MODERATE motor demand
  - Typing: Finger-to-key, sequential rather than simultaneous → MODERATE motor demand
  - Switch activation: Single movement (press, blink, sip) → LOW motor demand
  - Eye-gaze: Sustain visual fixation → MINIMAL motor demand (bypasses limb motor system)
- **Connections**: Each method maps to a different motor complexity level
- **Notes**: The key insight: AAC succeeds because it replaces the most complex motor task (speech) with simpler motor tasks; the simpler the motor demand, the broader the population that can access it

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: The Locked-In Communicator
- **Scenario**: A non-speaking autistic adult has been assessed as having severe intellectual disability based on standard IQ tests (which require verbal and motor responses). They show little behavioral evidence of comprehension. When provided with an eye-gaze AAC system that requires only sustained visual fixation (bypassing the motor system), they begin selecting vocabulary items accurately, demonstrating receptive vocabulary knowledge far exceeding their assessed cognitive level.
- **Illustration**: Demonstrates how motor barriers can create a false appearance of cognitive impairment, and how removing the motor bottleneck reveals hidden competence
- **Key Insight**: The assessment created a tautological loop: the test required motor skills the individual lacked, producing low scores that confirmed the assumption of intellectual disability

### Example 2: Catatonia Masquerading as Severe Autism
- **Scenario**: A 17-year-old autistic individual who previously used some speech gradually becomes slower, less responsive, stops speaking, and appears increasingly withdrawn and "unmotivated." This is attributed to "regression" or "severe autism." Years later, a psychiatrist recognizes the presentation as catatonia and prescribes lorazepam. Within weeks, the individual's motor initiation improves, and they begin using speech again — suggesting the "loss of speech" was a treatable motor initiation failure, not a cognitive decline.
- **Illustration**: Shows how diagnostic overshadowing (attributing all symptoms to autism) can miss treatable conditions that contribute to non-speaking status
- **Key Insight**: Catatonia is treatable; some cases of apparent "severe autism with regression" may include a catatonic component that is being overlooked

### Example 3: The Motor Skills Study
- **Scenario**: Researchers at MIT Media Lab measure both motor skills and language abilities in minimally verbal autistic adults. They find that fine motor skills significantly predict expressive language scores but have no relationship to receptive language scores. Two individuals with identical receptive language levels (both understanding complex sentences) have dramatically different expressive language levels — and the difference is entirely predicted by their motor skill scores.
- **Illustration**: Provides the clearest evidence that the speech-cognition gap is motor-mediated
- **Key Insight**: If the barrier were cognitive (language processing), both receptive and expressive language would be affected equally. The selective impairment of expression (but not comprehension) points directly to the motor system.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: Non-speaking means non-thinking
  - **Reality**: The motor-language dissociation hypothesis (with growing empirical support) proposes that many non-speaking autistic individuals have intact or even advanced language comprehension and cognitive abilities that are masked by motor output difficulties.

- **Misconception**: 63.6% of autistic children have childhood apraxia of speech
  - **Reality**: This figure comes from a single study of only 30 children from a clinical convenience sample (children referred specifically for speech/autism concerns). It should be treated as preliminary data from a small, non-representative study, not as an established population prevalence rate. Other research suggests the co-occurrence may be lower when measured in representative populations.

- **Misconception**: If someone cannot speak, speech therapy should focus on building language comprehension
  - **Reality**: If the problem is motor planning rather than language comprehension, the appropriate intervention targets motor output (through motor-based speech approaches or AAC) rather than comprehension. Assuming the individual does not understand is both clinically incorrect (in many cases) and ethically harmful.

- **Misconception**: Motor difficulties in autism are a separate, co-occurring condition
  - **Reality**: Growing evidence suggests motor difficulties may be a core feature of the autism phenotype rather than an incidental accompaniment, given their near-universal prevalence and their consistent presence across the spectrum.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **Individuals with both motor and language difficulties**: Not all non-speaking autistic individuals have intact language comprehension — some genuinely have language processing difficulties alongside motor difficulties. The motor-language dissociation applies to a subset (possibly large, possibly not) of the non-speaking autistic population, not necessarily to all.
- **Fluctuating motor ability**: Some autistic individuals can produce speech some of the time but not others — speech access fluctuates with stress, fatigue, sensory load, and emotional state. This pattern is consistent with motor planning being a resource-dependent system that fails under load.
- **Late speech emergence**: Some individuals who are non-speaking in early childhood develop speech later (after age 5 or even into adolescence), suggesting that the motor system for speech may continue developing on a delayed trajectory in autism.
- **Motor difficulties without speech impact**: Some autistic individuals have significant motor difficulties (poor handwriting, clumsiness, motor coordination problems) but fluent speech, suggesting that the speech motor system may be relatively spared in some individuals even when other motor systems are affected.

## Related Concepts

- **AAC Technology**: Motor-language dissociation provides the scientific rationale for AAC — if the problem is motor output, the solution is alternative output pathways
- **Visual-Spatial Cognition**: Complements the motor-language dissociation by explaining what autistic cognition does well (visual-spatial processing) alongside what it struggles with (motor output for speech)
- **Facilitated Communication / Spelling-Based Methods**: Proponents invoke motor-language dissociation to justify assisted communication methods; critics note that the dissociation can be addressed through evidence-based AAC without facilitator involvement
- **Intelligence and Assessment**: Motor barriers cause systematic underestimation of cognitive abilities on standard tests that require motor responses
- **Gestalt Language Processing**: Motor planning difficulties may intersect with how language is acquired — if speech is effortful, acquiring language in pre-assembled chunks (gestalts) may be more motorically efficient than assembling novel sentences from individual words

## Research Gaps

- **Population-based CAS-autism prevalence**: The field urgently needs large, representative studies of CAS prevalence in autistic populations, not just clinical convenience samples
- **Motor-mediated communication outcomes**: Few studies directly test whether motor-focused interventions (rather than language-focused interventions) improve communication outcomes for non-speaking autistic individuals
- **Catatonia screening in non-speaking autism**: How many non-speaking autistic individuals have undiagnosed catatonia? Routine screening could identify treatable cases
- **Longitudinal motor-language trajectories**: Following non-speaking autistic individuals over years to track how motor abilities and language expression co-develop would provide critical evidence
- **Selective mutism mechanisms in autism**: Is SM in autistic individuals primarily anxiety-based, motor-based, or some combination? Different mechanisms imply different treatments
- **Disaggregating the non-speaking population**: Non-speaking autism likely encompasses multiple motor subtypes (primarily apraxic, primarily catatonic, primarily dyspraxic); identifying these subtypes would enable targeted intervention
