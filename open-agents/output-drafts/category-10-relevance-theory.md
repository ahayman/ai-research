# Category 10: Relevance Theory -- The Cognitive Processing Engine

## The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

---

## How to Read This Document

Categories 1-9 described individual pragmatic mechanisms -- speech acts, implicature, politeness, hedging, and so on. This category offers something different: a **unified framework** that can handle ALL of them through a single principle.

Relevance Theory (RT), developed by Dan Sperber and Deirdre Wilson, proposes that every aspect of pragmatic communication can be explained by one cognitive principle: **the search for relevance**. Instead of four maxims (Grice), fifteen politeness strategies (Brown & Levinson), and separate theories for each pragmatic phenomenon, RT offers one principle and one procedure.

For autistic learners who think in systems, this is potentially the most powerful tool in the entire hub. It converts "How do I figure out what they meant?" into an **algorithm** you can run consciously.

**Note**: The basics of RT (two principles, the comprehension procedure overview, and RT's account of irony/metaphor) were introduced in Category 2. This document expands into RT as a **complete pragmatic framework** and a **practical decision tool**.

---

## 1. The Single Principle That Replaces Everything

### 1.1 What RT Claims

RT's radical claim: a single cognitive principle -- the pursuit of **optimal relevance** -- underlies ALL pragmatic interpretation. You don't need separate theories for implicature, speech acts, politeness, presupposition, and figurative language. They all fall out of one principle.

**Relevance** is defined as a ratio:

> **Relevance = Cognitive Effects / Processing Effort**

An utterance is relevant to the degree that it gives you **useful new information** (effects) without requiring **too much mental work** (effort). More effects + less effort = more relevant.

### 1.2 The Two Principles (Recap from Category 2)

**Cognitive Principle**: Human cognition tends to be geared to the maximization of relevance. (This is how minds work in general.)

**Communicative Principle**: Every act of ostensive communication conveys a presumption of its own optimal relevance. (When someone speaks to you, they're implicitly guaranteeing it's worth processing.)

### 1.3 How One Principle Replaces Four Maxims

| Gricean Maxim | What It Governs | How RT Handles It |
|---------------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Quality** (be truthful) | Truthfulness | False information produces misleading effects → not optimally relevant |
| **Quantity** (say enough, not too much) | Information amount | Too little = insufficient effects; too much = unnecessary effort. Both reduce relevance |
| **Relation** (be relevant) | Topical relevance | This IS the entire theory |
| **Manner** (be clear) | Clarity of expression | Greater clarity = less processing effort = more relevant |

The key difference: Grice's maxims are norms that speakers **can violate** (and violations generate implicature). RT's principle is an **exceptionless generalization** about what happens when someone communicates. You can't "violate" relevance -- it's always presumed. The question is only what interpretation satisfies the presumption.

## 2. Explicature: Even "Literal" Meaning Requires Pragmatics

### 2.1 The Semantic Underdeterminacy Thesis

This is RT's most surprising claim: **what a sentence literally means massively underdetermines what the speaker explicitly communicates**. Even understanding the "literal" meaning of an utterance requires substantial pragmatic work.

Consider: "I've had breakfast."
- Linguistically encoded meaning: The speaker has eaten breakfast at some point in their life.
- What the speaker explicitly communicates: The speaker has had breakfast **this morning** (probably recently, probably fully, probably not wanting another breakfast).

The "this morning" part isn't in the words. It's **pragmatically enriched** by the listener using context. RT calls this enriched meaning the **explicature**.

### 2.2 Types of Enrichment

Every utterance requires multiple types of pragmatic enrichment to produce an explicature:

| Enrichment Type | What It Does | Example |
|----------------|-------------|---------|
| **Disambiguation** | Choosing between word meanings | "Bank" → river bank or financial institution? |
| **Reference assignment** | Determining who pronouns refer to | "He left" → who is "he"? |
| **Saturation** | Filling in unarticulated elements | "It's raining" → raining WHERE? |
| **Free enrichment** | Adding information not required by grammar | "I've had breakfast" → this morning |
| **Broadening/loosening** | Expanding a word's meaning | "The ATM swallowed my card" → swallowed* (took in, didn't literally eat) |
| **Narrowing** | Restricting a word's meaning | "I need a drink" → drink* (alcoholic, not just any liquid) |

### 2.3 Why This Matters

The implication is profound: **there is no bright line between "literal" and "non-literal" language**. ALL language use involves pragmatic processing. The difference between "literal" understanding and "metaphorical" understanding is one of **degree**, not kind:

```
Literal ←——————————————————————→ Metaphorical
"round" (perfectly circular) ... "round" (roughly circular) ... "round" (plumpish) ... "The earth is round" (spherical)
```

Each use of "round" involves some degree of pragmatic adjustment. The listener constructs an **ad hoc concept** (ROUND*) that fits the context. This is what RT calls **lexical pragmatics**.

**For autistic learners**: This reframes the challenge. It's not that "literal" language is easy and "figurative" language is hard. ALL language requires interpretation. The question is always: "What did the speaker mean by THIS word in THIS context?" -- and the RT procedure gives you an algorithm for answering it.

## 3. The Comprehension Procedure: A Usable Algorithm

### 3.1 The Five-Step Explicit Version

For neurotypical speakers, this runs automatically. For autistic learners, it can be made into a **conscious decision tool**:

**Step 1: DECODE** -- What did they literally say?
- Parse the words
- Identify the basic linguistic meaning
- Note ambiguities, unclear references, missing information

**Step 2: SCAN CONTEXT** -- What information is available right now?
- Physical environment: Where are we? What just happened?
- Conversational context: What were we just talking about?
- Speaker knowledge: What do I know about this person and their likely goals?
- Social/cultural norms: What's expected in this setting?

**Step 3: COMPUTE** -- What interpretation is most relevant?
- Ask: "Does the literal meaning give me useful new information here?"
- If yes → the literal meaning may be correct
- If no → the literal meaning fails to be relevant enough, so something more is going on
- Ask: "What interpretation would make this utterance **worth saying** right here, right now, to me?"

**Step 4: CHECK** -- Would a rational communicator intend this interpretation?
- Does it fit what I know about the speaker?
- Would they expect me to reach this interpretation?
- Is it the most accessible interpretation that satisfies relevance?

**Step 5: ACCEPT or ITERATE**
- If it passes Step 4 → accept and respond
- If it doesn't → go back to Step 3 and try the next most accessible interpretation
- If stuck → ask for clarification (this is always legitimate)

### 3.2 Worked Examples

**"Nice shirt." (said by a coworker)**

1. **Decode**: Statement about the quality of my shirt.
2. **Context**: I'm wearing a normal shirt. The coworker is looking at it. Their tone is... neutral? Possibly slightly flat?
3. **Compute**: If literal (genuine compliment), it's relevant as social bonding. If the tone is flat or the context makes a compliment surprising (e.g., I'm wearing an old t-shirt), the literal meaning doesn't achieve enough relevance → search for an alternative. RT's echoic theory: they may be **echoing** an expectation of a nice shirt and expressing **dissociation** → sarcasm.
4. **Check**: Would this person be sarcastic? Does the tone support it?
5. **Accept**: If tone is warm → compliment. If tone is flat/ironic → sarcasm.

**"I have a meeting at three." (in response to "Can you help me this afternoon?")**

1. **Decode**: Statement about the speaker's schedule.
2. **Context**: I asked for help. They responded with schedule information.
3. **Compute**: The literal meaning (schedule information) isn't directly relevant to my request. But if I assume they're being relevant, the meeting explains why they **can't help** → the utterance achieves relevance as an **indirect refusal** plus an **account** (the reason they can't help).
4. **Check**: This interpretation yields maximum effects (I understand both the refusal and the reason) for minimal effort.
5. **Accept**: They're saying no, with the meeting as the reason.

**"Well, that's one way to do it." (said by a colleague looking at your work)**

1. **Decode**: Statement that your method is one of multiple ways.
2. **Context**: They're looking at my work and commenting on my method.
3. **Compute**: The literal meaning (there are multiple methods) is trivially true and not very informative → low relevance. If I search for more effects: saying "one way" when they could say "a good way" is a Quantity-style understatement. The implication: there are **better** ways, and this is not one of them → hedged criticism.
4. **Check**: The discourse marker "well" supports this (Category 5: signals a non-straightforward response).
5. **Accept**: Hedged criticism of my approach.

**A boss saying: "I was hoping this would be done by now."**

1. **Decode**: Statement about the boss's past hopes and current reality.
2. **Context**: Boss is looking at my work. The work isn't done.
3. **Compute**: As a literal report of internal state, this is low-relevance (why would I need to know about their hopes?). As an indirect speech act, it's highly relevant: it functions as a **reprimand** (you should have finished) + a **directive** (finish it now). The hedged form ("I was hoping" = past tense distancing, Category 6) protects both faces while communicating the criticism.
4. **Check**: The boss has authority (Category 22) and the work IS late → reprimand interpretation is most relevant.
5. **Accept**: This is a reprimand wrapped in hedging.

### 3.3 The One-Line Heuristics

For real-time use when you can't run the full algorithm:

**Heuristic 1**: "What interpretation makes this **worth saying** right here, right now?"
- Use when: Someone says something that seems trivially true or irrelevant

**Heuristic 2**: "Why would a rational person say **THIS** to **ME** right **NOW**?"
- Use when: You can't figure out the point of what someone just said

**Heuristic 3**: "What's the most **useful** thing this could mean?"
- Use when: Multiple interpretations are available and you need to pick one quickly

## 4. How RT Handles Everything

### 4.1 RT and Speech Acts (Category 1)

RT doesn't need Searle's five-category taxonomy. It recognizes three basic speech-act types (saying that, telling to, asking whether) and derives everything else pragmatically:

"The boss is coming" can be an assertion, a warning, a promise, or a threat -- depending entirely on which interpretation achieves relevance in the context. The speech act emerges from the interpretation procedure, not from a separate classification system.

### 4.2 RT and Presupposition (Category 9)

RT handles presupposition through **manifest assumptions**: a presupposition is content the speaker treats as already manifest in the listener's cognitive environment. "John stopped smoking" achieves relevance only if "John used to smoke" is already part of the shared background. If it isn't, the listener **accommodates** it -- adds it to their model -- because that's the most accessible way to make the utterance relevant.

### 4.3 RT and Politeness (Category 3)

RT handles politeness through **clause (b)** of optimal relevance: the utterance is the most relevant one compatible with the speaker's **abilities and preferences**. "Preferences" includes face-management. An indirect request ("Could you possibly pass the salt?") requires more processing effort than "Pass the salt," but the extra effort is compensated by additional cognitive effects: the listener learns not just the request but that the speaker is considerate, respects their autonomy, and values the relationship.

### 4.4 RT and Register (Category 7)

Register choices affect **processing effort**. Using the expected register minimizes effort; using an unexpected register (too formal, too casual) increases effort but may yield additional effects (social distance, intimacy, humor, sarcasm). The listener asks: "Why did they use THAT form?" and the answer is the pragmatic meaning of the register choice.

## 5. Cognitive Environment: A Better Model Than "Common Ground"

### 5.1 The Problem with "Mutual Knowledge"

Traditional pragmatics relies on "mutual knowledge" or "common ground" -- what both parties KNOW that they both KNOW. But this creates an infinite regress: I know X, I know you know X, I know you know I know X, I know you know I know you know X...

### 5.2 RT's Solution: Mutual Manifestness

RT replaces "mutual knowledge" with **mutual manifestness**:

- An assumption is **manifest** to you if you're capable of accepting it as true or probably true at this moment
- A **cognitive environment** is the total set of assumptions manifest to someone at a given time
- A **mutual cognitive environment** is the set of assumptions manifest to both parties

The key difference: manifestness doesn't require certainty. You don't need to KNOW what someone knows. You just need assumptions to be AVAILABLE to both of you. This is a much weaker (and more psychologically realistic) requirement.

### 5.3 Why This Matters for Autistic Communication

The Williams, Wharton & Jagoe (2021) paper showed that RT's mutual manifestness concept **formally models Milton's double empathy problem**: autistic and NT individuals have different cognitive environments (different things are manifest to them), which means they generate different relevance expectations from the same utterance. Communication breaks down not because one party is "deficient" but because the cognitive environments don't overlap sufficiently.

This reframes the challenge: it's not "autistic people can't figure out what others know." It's "autistic and NT people have different things manifest to them, and neither side fully appreciates what's manifest to the other."

## 6. RT and the Autistic Experience

### 6.1 Happé's Three-Level Model (1993)

The first direct application of RT to autism. Happé proposed that autistic individuals process language for relevance but at different levels depending on Theory of Mind ability:

| ToM Level | Can Understand | Can't Understand | RT Mechanism |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|-------------|
| **No ToM** | Similes ("She's LIKE a bulldozer") | Metaphors, irony | Can decode but can't infer speaker intention |
| **First-order** | Similes + metaphors | Irony | Can recognize "speaker means something different from literal" |
| **Second-order** | Similes + metaphors + irony | Complex indirect language | Can recognize speaker's attitude toward what they're echoing |

Research supported this gradient: similes were easiest, then metaphors, then irony -- matching the prediction that each requires a deeper level of intention recognition.

### 6.2 Williams, Wharton & Jagoe (2021): RT Meets Double Empathy

The most important recent RT-autism paper. They showed that RT's framework can model **both sides** of the communication breakdown:

- When an autistic person says something that an NT listener misinterprets, it's because different things are **manifest** in their respective cognitive environments
- The NT listener's "first satisfactory interpretation" is satisfactory relative to THEIR cognitive environment -- which may not match the autistic speaker's intended interpretation
- And vice versa: the autistic listener's "first satisfactory interpretation" of an NT speaker's utterance is satisfactory relative to THEIR cognitive environment

This is not one party being "wrong." It's two cognitive systems generating different but internally valid interpretations.

### 6.3 Why RT Suits Autistic Cognition

| Feature | Why It Helps |
|---------|-------------|
| **One principle** | Simpler than four maxims + separate theories for each phenomenon |
| **Algorithmic** | A step-by-step procedure that can be run consciously |
| **Cognitive, not social** | Based on how minds process information, not on social norms |
| **No "violations"** | You don't need to detect which social rule is being broken -- just follow the relevance path |
| **Systemizing-compatible** | The procedure IS a system: input (utterance + context) → process (compute relevance) → output (interpretation) |
| **Literal-nonliteral continuum** | No binary switch between "literal" and "figurative" -- just degrees of pragmatic adjustment |
| **"First satisfactory" rule** | Gives you a clear stopping point rather than an open-ended search |

### 6.4 Connections to Autistic Cognitive Theories

| Theory | How It Connects to RT |
|--------|----------------------|
| **Weak central coherence** | Affects what's in the cognitive environment (local details more manifest than global patterns) |
| **Monotropism** | Intense focus may narrow the cognitive environment, affecting which assumptions are accessible |
| **Predictive processing** | Reduced top-down predictions may mean the "path of least effort" leads to literal interpretation more often |
| **Enhanced perceptual functioning** | Greater perceptual detail in the cognitive environment → different relevance computations |

RT may serve as a **unifying framework** that encompasses these theories: each one describes a different way the cognitive environment is structured, which in turn affects how the comprehension procedure runs.

## 7. When the Procedure Fails (and How to Recover)

### 7.1 Common Failure Modes

| Failure | What Happens | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **First interpretation is wrong** | You stop too early at a literal reading | "Nice shirt" taken as compliment when it's sarcasm |
| **Equal alternatives** | Two interpretations are equally accessible | "We should do lunch" -- genuine or phatic? |
| **Deliberate ambiguity** | Speaker intends the ambiguity | "I'll think about it" (= no, but deniable) |
| **Missing cultural knowledge** | Context needed for interpretation isn't manifest to you | Missing that "bless your heart" (Southern US) = criticism |
| **Processing overload** | Too many demands → procedure crashes | Noisy environment + unfamiliar person + complex topic |

### 7.2 Recovery Strategies

| Strategy | When to Use |
|----------|------------|
| **Ask for clarification** | "Sorry, do you mean X or Y?" -- always legitimate |
| **Check understanding** | "So you're saying...?" -- reflects your interpretation back |
| **Contextual probe** | "What made you think of that?" -- gathers more context |
| **Honest admission** | "I'm not sure I'm reading this right." -- disarming and effective |
| **Delayed processing** | "Let me think about that." -- buys time to run the procedure |
| **Post-hoc check** | After the conversation: "When they said X, did they mean...?" |

## 8. RT vs. Grice: When to Use Which

Both frameworks are tools. Use whichever gives you the better answer:

| Situation | Better Framework | Why |
|-----------|-----------------|-----|
| "Why did they say that?" | **RT** | The relevance heuristic ("What makes this worth saying?") is a single question |
| "Which maxim is being violated?" | **Grice** | When you can identify a specific maxim violation, Grice's framework labels it clearly |
| Irony/sarcasm detection | **RT** | Echoic theory (speaker echoes + dissociates) is more precise than "Quality violation" |
| Scalar implicature ("some" = "not all") | **Grice** | Horn scales map directly to Gricean Quantity |
| Complex, multi-layered utterance | **RT** | The procedure handles everything in one pass |
| Teaching someone what went wrong | **Grice** | "You violated the maxim of Quantity" is more specific feedback |
| Real-time processing | **RT** | One question ("What's most relevant?") is faster than four-maxim check |
| Post-hoc analysis | **Either** | Both work for analyzing conversations after the fact |

## 9. Key Takeaways

1. **One principle replaces four maxims.** "What interpretation of this utterance is most relevant given what I know?" -- that single question does the work of all of Grice's maxims and more.

2. **Even "literal" meaning requires pragmatics.** The explicature concept shows that understanding ANY utterance involves pragmatic enrichment. There's no "free pass" for literal language.

3. **The comprehension procedure is an algorithm.** Decode → Scan context → Compute most relevant interpretation → Check against speaker's likely intentions → Accept or try again. You can run this consciously.

4. **Ad hoc concepts mean words don't have fixed meanings.** "Drink" means DRINK* (adjusted for context). "Round" means ROUND*. Every word is pragmatically tuned in real-time. This is normal, not "figurative."

5. **Mutual manifestness replaces mutual knowledge.** You don't need to know what others know. You need assumptions to be available to both parties. This is a weaker, more realistic requirement.

6. **The double empathy problem has a formal RT model.** Different cognitive environments → different relevance computations → different interpretations of the same utterance. Both sides are doing valid reasoning; the inputs differ.

7. **RT suits autistic cognition.** Single principle, algorithmic, cognitive not social, systemizing-compatible, no violation detection needed, literal-nonliteral as continuum.

8. **When stuck, use the one-line heuristic.** "Why would a rational person say THIS to ME right NOW?" -- if you can answer that, you've found the intended meaning.
