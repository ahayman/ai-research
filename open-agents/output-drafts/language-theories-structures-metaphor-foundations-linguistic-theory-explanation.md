# Foundations of Linguistic Theory -- From Structuralism to Cognitive Linguistics

How a century of competing frameworks shaped our understanding of what language is

---

## 1. Why Linguistic Theory Matters

Every time you speak a sentence, you perform one of the most complex cognitive acts known to science. You select from tens of thousands of stored words, arrange them according to intricate structural rules you have never been taught explicitly, and produce an utterance that another person -- who has also never been taught those rules -- understands immediately. You do this multiple times per minute, effortlessly, and you have been doing it since roughly age four.

The question of *how* this works -- what language actually is, where its structure comes from, and how children acquire it -- has driven more than a century of theoretical debate. That debate is not merely academic. The answer determines how we teach languages, how we build translation systems, how we diagnose language disorders, and how we understand the relationship between language and thought.

What makes this field particularly satisfying to study systematically is that it is structured by a set of **recurring oppositions** -- binary tensions that reappear in every era, often under different names but with the same underlying logic. These oppositions form the skeleton of the entire field:

| Opposition | Pole A | Pole B |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| **Nature vs. Nurture** | Language is innate (Chomsky) | Language is learned (Skinner, Tomasello) |
| **Formal vs. Functional** | Grammar is autonomous formal rules | Grammar serves communicative function |
| **Modular vs. Distributed** | Language uses a dedicated brain module | Language uses general cognition |
| **Rule-based vs. Statistical** | Knowledge is discrete symbolic rules | Knowledge is distributional patterns |
| **System vs. Use** | Study the idealized system (langue, competence) | Study actual usage (parole, performance) |
| **Individual vs. Social** | Grammar lives in individual minds | Grammar is a shared social convention |

Every theory covered below takes a position on each of these axes. The pattern is not random: each new framework emerges as a reaction to the perceived failures of its predecessor, typically by inverting one or more of these polarities.

## 2. Saussure's Structuralism: Language as a System of Differences

### The Founding Insight

Ferdinand de Saussure (1857--1913), a Swiss linguist, never published the work that made him famous. His *Course in General Linguistics* appeared in 1916, three years after his death, compiled by his students Charles Bally and Albert Sechehaye (with the collaboration of Albert Riedlinger) from lecture notes taken at the University of Geneva between 1906 and 1911.

Before Saussure, linguistics was primarily **historical**: scholars traced how languages evolved over centuries, comparing Latin to French to Italian, reconstructing proto-languages, cataloguing sound changes. Saussure's revolution was to insist that language could and should be studied as a **synchronic system** -- a snapshot of its structure at a single moment in time, analyzed on its own terms.

This is the first appearance of a pattern that will repeat throughout the field: a shift in what counts as the proper object of study.

### The Linguistic Sign

Saussure argued that the basic unit of language is the **sign**, which consists of two inseparable components:

- **Signifier (signifiant)**: The sound pattern -- not the physical sound wave itself, but the psychological impression of the sound. A mental image of sound.
- **Signified (signifie)**: The concept associated with that sound pattern -- not the physical object in the world, but the mental idea.

The sign is the union of these two, "like two sides of a sheet of paper." You cannot separate one from the other.

The relationship between signifier and signified is **arbitrary**. There is no natural reason why the sound pattern "tree" corresponds to the concept of a tree. Evidence: different languages use entirely different sounds for the same concept (English "tree," French "arbre," German "Baum," Japanese "ki"). Saussure distinguished between **absolute arbitrariness** (most vocabulary -- no motivation at all) and **relative arbitrariness** (partial motivation -- "nineteen" is motivated by "nine" and "ten," while "twenty" is fully arbitrary).

### Langue vs. Parole

Saussure drew a distinction that set the terms for every debate that followed:

- **Langue**: The abstract, systematic code shared by a speech community. It is social, collective, and stable. Langue is what linguists should study.
- **Parole**: The actual utterances produced by individual speakers. It is variable, messy, and subject to errors, hesitations, and personal idiosyncrasies.

This is the first instance of the **system vs. use** opposition. Saussure chose the system side: parole is data, but langue is the proper object of science.

### Language as a System of Differences

Saussure's deepest insight: signs have **no positive content** of their own. They gain their value entirely through their differences from other signs in the system. The word "cat" means what it means partly because it is not "bat," not "cap," not "car," not "dog." Meaning is relational, not intrinsic.

This operates along two axes:

- **Paradigmatic relations** (vertical): The set of elements that could substitute for each other in a given slot. In "The ___ sat on the mat," the paradigmatic set includes "cat," "dog," "bird," and so on.
- **Syntagmatic relations** (horizontal): The sequential combinations of elements in a chain. In "The cat sat on the mat," each word's value is partly determined by what precedes and follows it.

### Structuralism After Saussure

Saussure's framework branched into several schools, each emphasizing different aspects:

**The Prague School (1920s--1930s)** -- Vilem Mathesius, Roman Jakobson, and Nikolai Trubetzkoy combined structuralism with functionalism. Trubetzkoy's *Principles of Phonology* (1939, published posthumously) formalized how speech sounds are defined by their oppositions. Jakobson extended this into binary distinctive features, a system where every speech sound is characterized by a set of yes/no values (voiced/voiceless, nasal/oral, etc.). This binary-opposition approach later influenced generative phonology.

**American Structuralism (1930s--1950s)** -- Leonard Bloomfield's *Language* (1933) established a rigorously empirical, behaviorist-leaning approach. Bloomfield insisted on **distributional analysis**: classify linguistic units by the environments in which they occur, not by appeals to meaning or intuition. His student Zellig Harris further formalized these methods. Harris's own student, Noam Chomsky, would overturn the entire paradigm.

**The Sapir-Whorf Hypothesis** -- Edward Sapir (1884--1939) and Benjamin Lee Whorf (1897--1941) proposed that language shapes thought. The strong version (language *determines* thought) is largely rejected. The weak version (language *influences* certain cognitive processes, such as color perception and spatial reasoning) has received substantial empirical support.

Notice the pattern: each branch of structuralism pushes toward a different pole of the key oppositions. Prague School: functional. American Structuralism: formal and empirical. Sapir-Whorf: social and cognitive.

## 3. Chomsky's Generative Revolution: Language as Biological Endowment

### The 1957 Paradigm Shift

In 1957, Noam Chomsky (b. 1928) published *Syntactic Structures*, a slim book that challenged both American structuralism and behaviorist psychology simultaneously. Chomsky argued three things:

1. Language is not a set of habits learned through conditioning.
2. Language is not adequately described by cataloguing surface patterns.
3. Language is a **generative** system -- a finite set of rules capable of producing an infinite number of well-formed sentences.

This inverted multiple oppositions at once. Where Bloomfield had treated language as behavior to be observed, Chomsky treated it as mental structure to be modeled. Where structuralists had catalogued surface patterns, Chomsky proposed abstract underlying rules. Where behaviorists located language in the environment, Chomsky located it in the biology of the individual mind.

### Universal Grammar

Chomsky's central theoretical claim: all humans are born with an innate **Universal Grammar (UG)** -- a biological endowment that constrains which forms human languages can take. UG is not a grammar of any specific language but a framework of principles and parameters:

- **Principles**: Universal constraints that hold across all languages (e.g., every clause has a head; certain structural dependencies cannot cross certain boundaries).
- **Parameters**: Binary switches that vary across languages. The **head-directionality parameter**, for example, determines whether a language places the head before its complement (English: "eat the apple") or after (Japanese: "ringo-o taberu" -- "apple eat"). A child acquiring language sets these parameters based on the input they receive.

This is a strong position on the **nature** side of the nature/nurture opposition: the child's brain comes pre-equipped with the architectural blueprint of language.

### Deep Structure and Surface Structure

In the Standard Theory (1965, *Aspects of the Theory of Syntax*), Chomsky proposed that every sentence has two levels of representation:

- **Deep structure**: The abstract, underlying representation that captures basic grammatical relationships and meaning. Generated by phrase structure rules.
- **Surface structure**: The actual linear order of words as spoken or written.

**Transformational rules** map deep structures to surface structures. They can move elements (wh-movement: "You saw what" becomes "What did you see?"), insert elements (do-support: "She not likes" becomes "She does not like"), or restructure them (passivization: "The cat chased the mouse" becomes "The mouse was chased by the cat").

The active and passive sentences share the same deep structure (same agent, same action, same patient) but differ in surface structure. The transformation explains how.

### Competence vs. Performance

Chomsky distinguished:

- **Competence**: A speaker's unconscious, internalized knowledge of their language -- the mental grammar that allows them to judge grammaticality, produce novel sentences, and understand sentences they have never heard before.
- **Performance**: The actual use of language in real situations, affected by memory limitations, fatigue, distractions, and errors.

This parallels Saussure's langue/parole distinction, but with a critical difference that reveals how the field has shifted. Saussure's **langue is social** -- a shared convention of a speech community. Chomsky's **competence is individual** -- a psychological reality inside a single mind. Same opposition (system vs. use), but the system itself has moved from the social to the individual pole.

### The Poverty of the Stimulus

This is one of the most debated arguments in cognitive science, and it is the linchpin of the nativist position:

1. Children acquire complex grammatical knowledge rapidly, typically by age 4--5.
2. The linguistic input they receive is impoverished: it contains no explicit instruction about what is ungrammatical (only "positive evidence"), includes speech errors and fragments, and does not exemplify many abstract principles children come to know.
3. Therefore, children must bring innate, language-specific knowledge to the task. That knowledge is Universal Grammar.

Supporting evidence includes the phenomenon of **homesign**: deaf children who are not exposed to sign language often spontaneously develop gestural communication systems with grammatical structure. Children also make systematic overgeneralization errors ("goed," "mouses") that suggest rule application rather than mere imitation.

Counterarguments: Philosopher Fiona Cowie has argued the poverty of the stimulus fails on both empirical and conceptual grounds. Usage-based theorists argue that children's input is richer than assumed and that general cognitive abilities (pattern-finding, analogy, statistical learning) are sufficient. This debate continues.

### The Minimalist Program (1993+)

Chomsky's framework has evolved substantially over the decades. The Minimalist Program, introduced in a 1993 paper and elaborated in *The Minimalist Program* (1995), represents a radical simplification:

- The core claim: the language faculty is "optimal in its design" -- it contains only what is strictly necessary.
- **Merge** replaces the elaborate system of transformations. Merge is a single operation: take two syntactic objects and combine them into a new, larger object. **External Merge** combines items from the lexicon; **Internal Merge** moves an element to a new position.
- In a landmark 2002 paper in *Science*, Hauser, Chomsky, and Fitch hypothesized that the **Faculty of Language in the Narrow sense (FLN)** -- the component unique to humans -- may contain only **recursion**: the ability to embed structures within structures indefinitely.

The evolution of Chomsky's framework itself follows a pattern: progressive simplification, from many rules to fewer rules to a single operation.

| Era | Framework | Core Mechanism |
|-----|-----------|---------------|
| 1957 | Syntactic Structures | Phrase structure rules + transformations |
| 1965 | Standard Theory | Deep/surface structure, competence/performance |
| 1970s | Extended Standard Theory | Trace theory, X-bar theory |
| 1981 | Government and Binding | Principles and parameters, modular subsystems |
| 1993+ | Minimalist Program | Merge (one operation), bare phrase structure |

## 4. The Chomsky-Skinner Debate: A Pivotal Moment

### Two Books in 1957

By coincidence, 1957 saw the publication of two books that embodied opposite poles of the nature/nurture axis:

- **B.F. Skinner's *Verbal Behavior***: An attempt to explain language entirely through behaviorist principles of operant conditioning.
- **Chomsky's *Syntactic Structures***: An argument that language requires positing innate mental rules.

Skinner defined verbal behavior as "behavior reinforced through the mediation of other persons" and proposed six functional categories of verbal operants:

| Operant | Function | Example |
|---------|----------|---------|
| **Mand** | Request/demand, reinforced by specific consequence | Child says "water," receives water |
| **Tact** | Label for an object/event, reinforced by social approval | Sees a duck, says "duck" |
| **Echoic** | Verbal imitation | Repeating what one hears |
| **Intraverbal** | Response controlled by other verbal behavior | "How are you?" → "Fine, thanks" |
| **Textual** | Reading aloud from written stimuli | Reading a sign |
| **Autoclitic** | Modifies or comments on other verbal behavior | "I think...," "it is not the case that..." |

Skinner explicitly rejected internal mental states, intentions, and rules as explanatory constructs. Language is shaped entirely by environmental contingencies.

### Chomsky's 1959 Review

Chomsky's review of *Verbal Behavior*, published in the journal *Language* (vol. 35, pp. 26--58), became arguably more famous and influential than the book it reviewed. Written in what scholars have described as an "aggressive debating style," the review advanced four main arguments:

1. **Stimulus control is vacuous**: Skinner's concepts of "stimulus" and "response" become so broad when applied to verbal behavior that they lose scientific precision. A person looking at a painting might say "Dutch," "clash of colors," "I thought you liked abstract work," or an infinite number of other things. What controls the response?

2. **The creativity problem**: Humans routinely produce and understand sentences they have never heard before. Stimulus-response associations cannot account for this generative capacity.

3. **The structure problem**: Behaviorism treats language as a linear chain of associations. But sentences have hierarchical structure. "The boy the girl the man saw liked ran" is grammatical but cannot be explained by left-to-right chaining.

4. **The acquisition problem**: Children acquire language too quickly, too uniformly, and with too little explicit instruction to be explained by operant conditioning alone.

### The Cognitive Revolution

Chomsky's review is widely credited as a catalyst for the **cognitive revolution** in psychology, though the foundations of behaviorism were already weakening by 1959. The review helped establish principles that reshaped the field:

- Mental representations and internal rules are legitimate objects of scientific study.
- Language cannot be reduced to behavior -- it requires positing abstract internal cognitive structures.
- The mind is not a blank slate; innate biological constraints shape cognitive development.

### The Counter-Narrative

The standard telling of this debate presents it as a one-sided victory. The fuller picture is more nuanced:

- Kenneth MacCorquodale published a detailed defense of Skinner's position in 1970, arguing that Chomsky had mischaracterized many of Skinner's claims.
- Some scholars argue that Chomsky's review succeeded more as rhetoric than as rigorous engagement with Skinner's actual framework.
- Modern applied behavior analysis (ABA) continues to use Skinner's verbal operant categories productively, particularly in clinical contexts.
- The either/or framing (nature vs. nurture, rules vs. associations) has largely given way to interactionist perspectives that reject the dichotomy.

The pattern here: a debate that appeared settled was not as settled as the standard narrative suggests. This is itself a recurring pattern in the field -- apparent resolutions that turn out to be temporary.

## 5. Cognitive Linguistics: The Counter-Revolution

### Origins

Cognitive linguistics emerged in the late 1970s and early 1980s from scholars who had been trained in generative grammar and rejected its foundational assumptions. The movement inverted several of Chomsky's core commitments:

| Chomsky's Position | Cognitive Linguistics Position |
|-------------------|-------------------------------|
| Syntax is autonomous (independent of meaning) | All grammar is meaningful |
| Language uses a dedicated innate module (UG) | Language uses general cognitive abilities |
| Competence (idealized system) is the proper object | Usage (actual language experience) is primary |
| Grammar is formal computation | Grammar is embodied conceptualization |

Three founding figures, all born within a year of each other, drove the movement:

**George Lakoff** (b. 1941) -- Originally one of the "generative semanticists," a group that included Haj Ross, Paul Postal, and James McCawley. They called themselves "The Four Horsemen of the Apocalypse" and argued that deep structure should be purely semantic. After the "linguistics wars" of the 1960s--70s, where generative semantics lost institutional ground to Chomsky's interpretive semantics, Lakoff turned to metaphor theory and embodied cognition. His 1987 book *Women, Fire, and Dangerous Things* became a foundational text.

**Ronald Langacker** (b. 1942) -- Developed Cognitive Grammar (originally called "Space Grammar") beginning in the mid-to-late 1970s. His two-volume *Foundations of Cognitive Grammar* (1987, 1991) provided the formal architecture.

**Leonard Talmy** (b. 1942) -- Developed cognitive semantics, focusing on how language structures conceptual content. His two-volume *Toward a Cognitive Semantics* (2000) systematized concepts including force dynamics, figure/ground organization, and motion event framing.

The first International Cognitive Linguistics Conference was held in Duisburg, Germany in 1989, and the journal *Cognitive Linguistics* began publication shortly after.

### Core Commitments

Cognitive linguistics rests on five linked commitments, each one a direct rejection of a Chomskyan assumption:

1. **No autonomy of syntax.** Grammar is not a self-contained formal system independent of meaning. Every grammatical structure carries meaning.

2. **No Universal Grammar module.** There is no language-specific innate module. Language draws on general cognitive abilities: categorization, attention, memory, analogy, pattern recognition.

3. **Language is embodied.** Linguistic meaning is grounded in bodily experience. We understand abstract concepts through metaphorical extension from concrete, physical experience -- "grasping an idea," "the argument fell apart," "time flies."

4. **Semantics drives grammar.** Meaning is primary. Grammatical form reflects conceptual structure rather than operating as an autonomous formal system.

5. **Usage-based.** Linguistic knowledge emerges from actual language use, not from an innate grammar merely "triggered" by input.

### Lakoff: Conceptual Metaphor Theory

George Lakoff, with philosopher Mark Johnson, argued in *Metaphors We Live By* (1980) that metaphor is not a literary ornament but a fundamental cognitive mechanism. We systematically understand abstract domains in terms of concrete, experiential ones:

- **ARGUMENT IS WAR**: "He *attacked* my position," "She *shot down* my argument"
- **TIME IS MONEY**: "Don't *waste* my time," "I *spent* three hours"
- **IDEAS ARE OBJECTS**: "Let me *give* you that idea," "I can't *grasp* the concept"
- **LIFE IS A JOURNEY**: "She's at a *crossroads*," "We've come a long *way*"

These are not just linguistic conventions. Lakoff and Johnson's claim is that they structure thought itself -- that the metaphors embedded in language shape how we reason about abstract domains. (This topic receives extensive treatment in a separate section of this deep dive.)

### Langacker: Cognitive Grammar

Ronald Langacker's framework treats grammar as a system of meaningful symbolic units, built on several key principles:

- **Symbolic units**: The basic units of grammar are pairings of semantic structure (meaning) with phonological structure (form). There are no meaningless formal rules -- all grammar is symbolic.
- **Profile and base**: Every expression profiles (highlights, brings into focus) a particular element against a larger base (background). "Uncle" profiles a male person against the base of a kinship network. "Arc" profiles a curved segment against the base of a full circle.
- **Schema and instance**: Abstract structures (schemas) relate to specific ones (instances) through categorization. The noun schema is instantiated by specific nouns; the transitive clause schema by specific transitive sentences.
- **Construal**: The same situation can be linguistically construed in different ways. "The glass is half full" vs. "The glass is half empty" describe the same physical reality but construe it differently.
- **Continuum of lexicon and grammar**: There is no sharp boundary between vocabulary and grammar. Both consist of symbolic units differing only in specificity and abstraction.

### Talmy: Cognitive Semantics

Leonard Talmy's work addresses how language packages conceptual content. His contributions include:

- **Force dynamics**: A generalized framework for how entities interact with respect to force -- pushing, resisting, blocking, overcoming blockage, helping, letting, hindering. This extends across physical, psychological, social, and inferential domains. It subsumes and refines the traditional notion of causation.
- **Figure and ground**: Adapted from Gestalt psychology. In spatial language, the Figure is the entity being located (often smaller, more mobile) and the Ground is the reference entity (often larger, more stationary). "The cat [Figure] is on the mat [Ground]."
- **Motion event typology**: Languages differ systematically in how they package motion event components. English typically encodes manner in the verb and path in particles ("run out," "swim across" -- **satellite-framed**). Spanish typically encodes path in the verb and manner in adjuncts ("salir corriendo" = "exit running" -- **verb-framed**).
- **Fictive motion**: Figurative expressions describing static scenes with motion language: "The road *runs* along the coast," "The fence *goes* from the house to the barn."

### Construction Grammar

Construction Grammar (CxG), closely aligned with cognitive linguistics, holds that the basic unit of language is the **construction**: a learned pairing of form and meaning at every level of linguistic organization:

| Level | Example |
|-------|---------|
| Morpheme | un-, -ing, -ness |
| Word | avocado, the, yesterday |
| Fixed expression | "by and large," "jog X's memory" |
| Partially filled pattern | "the Xer, the Yer" ("the bigger they are, the harder they fall") |
| Fully abstract pattern | Ditransitive [Subj V Obj1 Obj2] = "X causes Y to receive Z" |

Adele Goldberg's key insight (1995, *Constructions*): syntactic patterns are themselves meaningful, independent of the words that fill them. The ditransitive construction means "transfer" even when combined with verbs that do not inherently mean transfer. "She sneezed the napkin off the table" uses the caused-motion construction to give "sneeze" a causal meaning it does not have in isolation. The construction carries the meaning; the verb is recruited into it.

### Usage-Based Acquisition

Michael Tomasello's *Constructing a Language* (2003) synthesizes cognitive linguistics with developmental psychology, proposing that children learn language through two general cognitive capacities:

- **Intention-reading**: Understanding the communicative goals of others through shared attention.
- **Pattern-finding**: Extracting regularities from input through categorization, analogy, and statistical learning.

Children's early language is "item-based" -- tied to specific words and phrases they have heard. Only gradually do they abstract more general patterns. There is no need for a language-specific innate module.

## 6. Connectionism and Emergentism: The Latest Challenge

### Core Ideas

Connectionism (also called Parallel Distributed Processing, or PDP) and emergentism represent approaches that model language as an emergent property of neural-network-like computation:

- Language knowledge is not stored as discrete rules but as **patterns of connection strengths** between simple processing units.
- Learning consists of adjusting connection weights based on statistical regularities in the input.
- Complex, apparently rule-governed behavior **emerges** from the interaction of many simple processes without explicit rules being represented anywhere.

This challenges Chomsky's position from a different direction than cognitive linguistics does. Where cognitive linguistics argues that language uses general cognition rather than a dedicated module, connectionism argues that the very *form* of linguistic knowledge is different from what both Chomsky and cognitive linguists assume -- it is not rules or constructions but distributed patterns of activation.

### The Past Tense Debate (1986)

The landmark study that crystallized the debate:

Rumelhart and McClelland (1986) built a neural network that learned to produce English past tense forms from verb stems. The model contained no explicit rules -- only units representing phonetic features connected by weighted links adjusted during training. The model mimicked children's developmental stages: first producing correct irregular forms ("went," "came"), then over-regularizing ("goed," "comed"), then eventually mastering both patterns.

The provocative claim: "Linguistic rules may be merely convenient approximate fictions." The real mechanisms underlying language are patterns of activation in neural networks.

Pinker and Prince (1988) published a detailed critique:
- The model could not represent certain words correctly.
- It could learn "rules" found in no human language.
- It could not properly distinguish regular from irregular morphology.
- It failed on fundamental aspects of its assigned task.

Despite these flaws, the model shifted the field's attention toward the role of statistical structure in language acquisition and processing.

### The Competition Model

Elizabeth Bates and Brian MacWhinney developed a functionalist, connectionist model of language processing (1982 onward):

- Language processing involves **competition** among multiple cues -- word order, case marking, agreement, animacy, stress -- that point to different interpretations.
- Different languages weight these cues differently. English relies heavily on word order; Italian relies more on agreement and animacy; German uses case marking.
- The architecture is connectionist: processing consists of concurrent activation of competing interpretations, with the strongest winning.
- Bilingual speakers show **cue competition** between the weightings of their two languages.

### Statistical Learning in Infants

Research by Saffran, Aslin, and Newport (1996) demonstrated that 8-month-old infants can segment continuous speech into words based solely on transitional probabilities between syllables. This provided striking evidence that domain-general statistical learning mechanisms are available from very early in development -- a finding more consistent with emergentist than nativist accounts.

### The Rules vs. Statistics Debate

The past tense debate exemplifies a deeper tension in cognitive science:

| Dimension | Rules (Symbolic) | Statistics (Connectionist) |
|-----------|-----------------|--------------------------|
| Knowledge representation | Discrete rules and categories | Distributed patterns of activation |
| Learning mechanism | Parameter setting, hypothesis testing | Weight adjustment, statistical extraction |
| Regular/irregular distinction | Dual mechanism (rule + memorized exceptions) | Single mechanism (one network handles both) |
| View of grammar | Symbolic computation | Emergent pattern |

Modern views increasingly recognize that both rule-like behavior and statistical sensitivity are real, and that the debate may have been structured around a false dichotomy. The success of large language models since the late 2010s has reinvigorated connectionist arguments in new ways, demonstrating that statistical models trained on large corpora can produce fluent, grammatically structured language -- raising fresh questions about whether symbolic rules are necessary at all.

## 7. The Missing Piece: Systemic Functional Linguistics

No survey of linguistic theory is complete without acknowledging **M.A.K. Halliday's Systemic Functional Linguistics (SFL)**, which the original research omitted. Halliday (1925--2018) developed a comprehensive functional theory of language starting in the 1960s that treats language as a **social semiotic system** -- a resource for making meaning in social contexts.

SFL identifies three simultaneous **metafunctions** in every utterance:
- **Ideational**: Language represents experience (what is being talked about).
- **Interpersonal**: Language enacts social relationships (who is talking to whom, with what authority).
- **Textual**: Language creates coherent messages (how information is organized and flows).

SFL occupies a distinctive position on the key oppositions: it is functional (not formal), social (not individual), usage-oriented (not system-abstracted), and meaning-driven (not syntax-autonomous). It has had enormous influence in applied linguistics, discourse analysis, education, and computational linguistics, particularly outside the United States.

## 8. The Recurring Patterns

### The Pendulum

There is a discernible historical oscillation in what the field takes language to be:

1. **A social system** (Saussure: language as shared convention)
2. **Observable behavior** (Bloomfield/Skinner: language as stimulus-response)
3. **An individual mental organ** (Chomsky: language as innate grammar)
4. **Embodied cognition** (Cognitive Linguistics: language as grounded experience)
5. **Emergent network dynamics** (Connectionism: language as statistical pattern)

Each paradigm shift has been partly a reaction against the perceived failures of its predecessor. Each one inverts at least one of the key oppositions.

### What Connects Every Theory

Despite deep disagreements, several themes recur across all frameworks:

**Structure beyond the surface.** Every theory acknowledges that language has internal organization beyond surface sequences. Saussure's system of differences, Chomsky's deep structure, Langacker's symbolic units, and connectionist hidden layers all grapple with the non-obvious organization of language.

**The infinite from the finite.** How does a finite system (brain, grammar, vocabulary, network) produce and comprehend an infinite range of expressions? Saussure saw this in paradigmatic and syntagmatic combination; Chomsky in recursive rule application; Construction Grammar in productive constructions; connectionism in generalization from training data.

**The acquisition puzzle.** Every theory must explain how children learn language. The disagreements are about mechanism (innate module vs. general cognition vs. statistical learning), not about the remarkable nature of the achievement.

**Form-meaning pairing.** From Saussure's signifier/signified to Langacker's symbolic units to Goldberg's constructions, the idea that language fundamentally pairs form with meaning has never been abandoned -- only its implementation differs.

**The tension between system and use.** Saussure's langue/parole, Chomsky's competence/performance, the usage-based emphasis on actual utterances -- every framework must negotiate between the idealized system and the messy reality of language in action.

### How Each Theory Views the Fundamentals

| Framework | Language is... | The mind is... | Acquisition works by... | Grammar is... |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|------------------------|---------------|
| **Saussure** | A social system of differences | A repository of shared conventions | Internalizing the social code | Abstract relations |
| **Bloomfield** | Observable verbal behavior | A black box | Habit formation | Distributional patterns |
| **Chomsky** | A biological endowment | A rule-governed computer | Innate grammar + parameter setting | An autonomous formal system |
| **Skinner** | Operant verbal behavior | Stimulus-response associations | Reinforcement and shaping | Not a meaningful concept |
| **Cognitive Ling.** | Embodied conceptualization | Grounded in bodily experience | Usage, analogy, intention-reading | Meaningful symbolic assemblies |
| **Connectionism** | Emergent network patterns | A neural network | Statistical learning | Emergent regularities |
| **Halliday (SFL)** | A social semiotic resource | A meaning-making system | Learning through social function | Functional choices in context |

## 9. Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Arbitrariness** | The principle that there is no natural connection between a linguistic form and its meaning |
| **Competence** | A speaker's unconscious knowledge of their language (Chomsky); contrasts with performance |
| **Construction** | A learned pairing of form and meaning at any level of linguistic organization (morpheme to clause) |
| **Construal** | How a speaker chooses to linguistically portray a situation (Langacker) |
| **Deep structure** | The abstract underlying syntactic representation of a sentence (Chomsky, Standard Theory) |
| **Embodiment** | The principle that cognitive and linguistic categories are grounded in bodily experience |
| **Emergentism** | The view that linguistic structure emerges from the interaction of simpler, non-linguistic processes |
| **Force dynamics** | Talmy's framework for how entities interact with respect to force, resistance, and blockage |
| **Generative grammar** | A grammar using a finite set of rules to produce all and only the well-formed sentences of a language |
| **Langue** | The abstract, shared system of a language (Saussure); contrasts with parole |
| **Merge** | The fundamental structure-building operation in the Minimalist Program; combines two objects into one |
| **Minimalist Program** | Chomsky's research program (1993+) seeking the simplest possible characterization of the language faculty |
| **Paradigmatic** | Relations of substitution between elements that could fill the same slot in a structure |
| **Parole** | Individual, concrete instances of language use (Saussure); contrasts with langue |
| **Performance** | The actual use of language in real situations, subject to memory and processing limitations (Chomsky) |
| **Poverty of the stimulus** | The argument that linguistic input is too impoverished to explain acquisition without innate knowledge |
| **Profile / Base** | In Cognitive Grammar, the highlighted element (profile) against its background knowledge (base) |
| **Signified** | The concept or meaning component of a linguistic sign (Saussure) |
| **Signifier** | The sound-image or form component of a linguistic sign (Saussure) |
| **Surface structure** | The actual linear arrangement of words as spoken or written |
| **Syntagmatic** | Sequential, combinatorial relations between elements in a chain |
| **Symbolic unit** | A pairing of semantic and phonological structure; the basic unit in Cognitive Grammar (Langacker) |
| **Transformations** | Rules that map deep structures to surface structures by moving, inserting, or deleting elements |
| **Universal Grammar (UG)** | The hypothesized innate biological endowment that constrains possible human languages (Chomsky) |
| **Usage-based** | The view that linguistic knowledge is built from actual experience with language, not innate specification |
| **Verbal operant** | A functional unit of verbal behavior defined by its controlling variables (Skinner) |
