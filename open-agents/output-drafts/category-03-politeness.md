# Category 3: Politeness Theory -- The Face System

## The Pragmatics Learning Hub for Autistic Individuals

---

## How to Read This Document

Category 1 covered what sentences **do** (speech acts). Category 2 covered what sentences **mean beyond what they say** (implicature). This category explains **why** so much of English is indirect, hedged, and softened -- and provides the framework for predicting exactly *how much* indirectness any given situation demands.

Politeness theory is, in many ways, the Rosetta Stone of English pragmatics. Once you understand the face system, the behavior that previously seemed arbitrary ("Why can't I just ask directly?") reveals itself as a **consistent, predictable, calculable system**. It has variables, a formula, and clear outputs. It is one of the most pattern-friendly aspects of pragmatics.

## 1. The Core Concept: Face

### 1.1 What Face Is

The sociologist Erving Goffman (1955) defined **face** as:

> "The positive social value a person effectively claims for himself by the line others assume he has taken during a particular contact."

Translated: face is the **public self-image** that every person projects and maintains during social interaction. It is not a fixed property of the person -- it exists in the interaction, created moment by moment through what you say, how you say it, and how others respond.

The concept originated in Chinese culture, where two terms capture different dimensions:
- **Mianzi** (面子): prestige, social status, reputation earned through achievement
- **Lian** (臉): moral character, the community's respect for a person's integrity

Goffman universalized the concept: every person, in every culture, has face -- a self-image they work to maintain.

### 1.2 Why Face Matters So Much

People are **emotionally invested** in their face. When your face is sustained (others treat you as you want to be seen), you feel confident and secure. When your face is threatened (someone challenges your self-image), you feel anxiety, shame, or anger. When you lose face, the experience is painful -- embarrassment, humiliation.

This emotional investment creates a social system: people feel **morally obligated** to help maintain each other's faces. Not doing so -- allowing someone to lose face unnecessarily -- is one of the most severe social transgressions in English-speaking culture. Much of what we call "rudeness" is actually a failure to participate in mutual face maintenance.

### 1.3 Two Types of Face

Brown and Levinson (1987) took Goffman's unitary concept and split it into two complementary needs that every person has simultaneously:

**Positive face**: The desire to be **liked, approved of, appreciated, and valued**. You want others to see you as competent, interesting, worthy of attention, and belonging to the group.

**Negative face**: The desire to be **autonomous, unimpeded, and free from imposition**. You want your time, space, choices, and actions to be respected. You don't want to be told what to do, pushed into obligations, or controlled.

**"Negative" does NOT mean bad.** It means "freedom from" -- freedom from imposition. Just as "negative liberty" in political philosophy means freedom from interference, negative face means freedom from being imposed upon.

**Everyone has both faces simultaneously.** At any moment, you want to be both liked AND left alone. These two needs exist in tension -- and managing that tension is what politeness is for.

| Face Type | Core Need | Threatened By | Protected By |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|-------------|
| **Positive** | Being liked and valued | Criticism, insults, disagreement, being ignored, being excluded | Compliments, agreement, showing interest, including in the group |
| **Negative** | Being autonomous and unimpeded | Requests, orders, advice, offers, obligations, intrusions | Hedging, apologizing, minimizing impositions, giving choice |

## 2. Face-Threatening Acts (FTAs)

### 2.1 What They Are

A **face-threatening act** is any communicative act that inherently threatens someone's face -- either the speaker's or the hearer's, either positive or negative. Brown and Levinson argued that a huge number of everyday speech acts are inherently face-threatening, which is why politeness exists: to mitigate the threat.

### 2.2 The Four-Cell Matrix

| | **Threatens Positive Face** (desire to be liked) | **Threatens Negative Face** (desire for autonomy) |
|---|---|---|
| **Hearer's face** | Criticisms, insults, disagreements, complaints, accusations, corrections, contempt, raising taboo topics, ignoring the hearer | Requests, orders, suggestions, advice, reminders, offers, promises, compliments (can create obligation), threats, warnings |
| **Speaker's face** | Apologies, self-criticism, confessions, accepting compliments (risks seeming vain), losing composure | Expressing thanks (acknowledges debt), accepting offers (acknowledges need), making excuses, accepting compliments (acknowledges obligation) |

### 2.3 Examples That Make the System Click

**Requests threaten the hearer's negative face:**
When you ask someone to do something, you're imposing on their autonomy. "Pass the salt" imposes on what the hearer was doing (not passing salt). This is why requests in English are so heavily padded with mitigation -- the padding is face-protection.

**Criticism threatens the hearer's positive face:**
When you tell someone their work isn't good enough, you're attacking their self-image as competent. This is why criticism in English is so elaborately softened -- the softening protects the hearer's desire to be seen as capable.

**Apologies threaten the speaker's positive face:**
When you apologize, you admit you did something wrong -- damaging your own self-image. This is why people resist apologizing even when they know they should: the apology costs them face.

**Accepting a compliment is doubly threatening:**
It threatens the speaker's positive face (risking seeming vain) AND negative face (creating a social obligation to reciprocate). This is why compliment responses are so awkward in English -- "Oh, this old thing?" deflects both threats simultaneously.

## 3. The Five Super-Strategies

Brown and Levinson proposed that when a speaker needs to perform a face-threatening act, they choose from five strategies, arranged on a scale from **most direct/efficient** to **most face-protective**:

### 3.1 Strategy 1: Bald On-Record

**What it is**: Perform the FTA directly, without any mitigation. Say exactly what you mean.

**Examples**: "Close the door." / "You're wrong." / "Give me that."

**When it's used**:
- Emergencies: "Watch out!" (no time for face-work)
- Task-oriented urgency: "Pass the scalpel." (efficiency matters more than face)
- Great power difference: Officer to soldier, parent to small child
- Very close relationships where face threat is minimal: "Hand me the remote."
- When the FTA is trivially small: "Come in." (after someone knocks)
- When the speaker deliberately wants to be rude: "Get lost."

**What it communicates**: Maximum efficiency, zero face protection. In most contexts, this signals either high urgency, high power, high intimacy, or deliberate impoliteness.

**Why this matters for autistic learners**: Bald on-record is the autistic "default" communication style in many contexts -- saying what you mean, directly and efficiently. The issue is that English social convention expects this strategy only in specific contexts (emergencies, close relationships, high power). In most other contexts, some degree of mitigation is expected.

### 3.2 Strategy 2: Positive Politeness

**What it is**: Perform the FTA while simultaneously addressing the hearer's positive face -- making them feel liked, valued, and included.

**Core message**: "I like you, we're alike, we're on the same team."

**Examples**:
- "Hey, you're great at spreadsheets -- could you take a look at mine?" (compliment + request)
- "We should totally get together this weekend!" (inclusion)
- "I know you'll understand..." (claiming common ground)
- "Buddy, can you help me out?" (in-group marker + request)

**Brown and Levinson's 15 positive politeness strategies** (grouped by mechanism):

**Claim Common Ground (Strategies 1-8):**
1. Notice and attend to the hearer's interests/wants ("You look tired -- let me get you coffee")
2. Exaggerate interest/approval ("What a *brilliant* idea!")
3. Intensify interest to the hearer ("You'll never guess what happened...")
4. Use in-group identity markers ("mate," "dude," nicknames, shared jargon)
5. Seek agreement (safe topics, repetition, "right?")
6. Avoid disagreement (token agreement, white lies, hedging)
7. Presuppose/assert common ground ("As we all know...")
8. Joke

**Convey Cooperation (Strategies 9-14):**
9. Assert knowledge of hearer's wants ("I know how much this means to you")
10. Offer/promise ("I'll help you with that")
11. Be optimistic ("I'm sure you won't mind...")
12. Include both S and H ("Let's get this done")
13. Give or ask for reasons ("I need this because...")
14. Assume reciprocity ("I'll do X if you do Y")

**Fulfill Wants (Strategy 15):**
15. Give gifts (goods, sympathy, understanding, cooperation)

**When positive politeness is preferred**: Close relationships, peer interactions, solidarity contexts, minor FTAs, when building rapport.

**Potential pitfalls**: Can seem **insincere** ("flattery"), **presumptuous** (assuming closeness that doesn't exist), or **manipulative** (using friendliness to extract compliance). For autistic learners, the inauthenticity of strategic friendliness can feel deeply uncomfortable.

### 3.3 Strategy 3: Negative Politeness

**What it is**: Perform the FTA while simultaneously addressing the hearer's negative face -- acknowledging and minimizing the imposition on their autonomy.

**Core message**: "I know I'm imposing on you, I respect your right to say no, I'll make this as painless as possible."

**This is the dominant politeness strategy in English.** English is a **negative-politeness dominant** language. The elaborate hedging, softening, apologizing, and indirectness that characterizes polite English is primarily serving negative face.

**Brown and Levinson's 10 negative politeness strategies:**

1. **Be conventionally indirect**: "Could you...?" / "Would you mind...?"
2. **Question, hedge**: "I think," "sort of," "perhaps," "I wonder if..."
3. **Be pessimistic**: "I don't suppose you could...?" / "You wouldn't happen to...?"
4. **Minimize the imposition**: "I just need a tiny favor" / "It'll only take a second"
5. **Give deference**: Titles, "sir/ma'am," self-lowering, other-raising
6. **Apologize**: "I'm sorry to bother you" / "I hate to ask" / "Forgive me"
7. **Impersonalize**: "It would be appreciated if..." / Passive voice / "One doesn't..."
8. **State the FTA as a general rule**: "Regulations require..." / "Company policy says..."
9. **Nominalize**: "Your consideration is appreciated" (instead of "Please consider this")
10. **Go on record as incurring a debt**: "I'd be forever grateful" / "I'll owe you one"

**The stacking effect**: These strategies **compound**. A single request can stack multiple negative politeness strategies:

> "I'm really sorry to bother you [Strategy 6: apologize], but I was wondering [Strategy 2: hedge] if you might possibly [Strategy 3: be pessimistic] have just a minute [Strategy 4: minimize] to take a quick look at this [Strategy 4: minimize again]?"

That sentence contains **five** negative politeness strategies in a single request. It says "pass the salt" in 30 words. The extra 25 words are all face-protection.

**The annotated email**: Here's what a polite professional email looks like when you label the strategies:

> Hi Sarah, [positive politeness: in-group address]
> 
> Hope you're doing well! [positive politeness: attend to hearer's wants]
> 
> I'm sorry to bother you with this [NP6: apologize], but I was wondering [NP2: hedge] if you might [NP3: pessimistic] have a moment [NP4: minimize] to look over the Johnson report? [NP1: indirect]
> 
> No rush at all [NP4: minimize], and please don't worry if you're too busy [NP: acknowledge autonomy] -- I just thought [NP2: hedge] your expertise would really help. [PP: compliment hearer's competence]
> 
> I'd really appreciate it [NP10: incur debt], and I'm happy to return the favor anytime. [PP14: reciprocity]
> 
> Thanks so much!

That email makes one request: "Please review this report." Everything else is face-work.

### 3.4 Strategy 4: Off-Record (Indirect)

**What it is**: Perform the FTA so indirectly that you can plausibly deny having performed it at all. The hearer must infer your intention; if they don't, you haven't "officially" made the request, criticism, etc.

**Core message**: "I'm saying something related to what I want, but I'm not directly asking. You can choose to act on it or not."

**Brown and Levinson's 15 off-record strategies** (key ones):

| Strategy | Example | Mechanism |
|----------|---------|-----------|
| Give hints | "It's cold in here." (= close the window) | Relevance implicature |
| Give association clues | "My car is filthy..." (= help me wash it) | Associative inference |
| Understate | "He's not exactly a genius." | Quality flout |
| Overstate | "I've been waiting forever!" | Quality flout |
| Be ironic | "What lovely weather!" (in rain) | Quality flout |
| Use metaphor | "He's a real snake." | Quality flout |
| Use rhetorical questions | "How was I supposed to know?" | Relevance flout |
| Be vague | "I heard someone did something..." | Manner flout |
| Be incomplete | "Well, if you'd just..." | Manner flout |

**The plausible deniability advantage**: If you say "It's cold in here" and the other person ignores it, you haven't been refused -- because you never officially asked. If you'd said "Close the window" and they said no, both parties would lose face (you for being refused, them for refusing).

**Why off-record is systematically difficult for autistic learners**: These strategies require the listener to **infer the intended meaning from context**. The literal content ("It's cold in here") gives no indication that a request is being made. Research consistently shows this is the area of greatest pragmatic difficulty for autistic individuals -- not because of an inability to reason, but because the gap between what's said and what's meant is at its widest.

### 3.5 Strategy 5: Don't Perform the FTA

**What it is**: Stay silent. Don't make the request, don't give the criticism, don't raise the issue.

**When it's used**: When the face threat is too great relative to the benefit. You might want to tell your boss their presentation was bad, but the face threat (to both of you) outweighs the benefit.

**The trade-off**: Maximum face protection, zero communicative efficiency.

### 3.6 The Trade-Off Scale

The five strategies exist on a scale:

```
Most efficient,                                    Most face-protective,
least face-protective                              least efficient
        |                                                  |
  Bald on-record → Positive → Negative → Off-record → Don't do it
        |              |           |           |              |
    "Close the     "Hey buddy,  "Would you  "It's cold    [silence]
     door."        close the    mind closing  in here..."
                   door?"       the door?"
```

As you move right, you gain face protection but lose clarity. As you move left, you gain clarity but risk face damage. **The entire politeness system is a negotiation between these two pressures.**

## 4. The Weight Formula: Predicting Politeness Level

This is where the system becomes genuinely **calculable**. Brown and Levinson proposed that the degree of politeness (i.e., which strategy to use) can be predicted by a formula:

### 4.1 The Formula

**W = D + P + R**

Where:
- **W** = Weightiness of the FTA (how much politeness is needed)
- **D** = Social **Distance** between speaker and hearer
- **P** = Relative **Power** of the hearer over the speaker
- **R** = Absolute **Ranking** of the imposition in the culture

**Higher W → More politeness needed → Strategy further to the right on the scale.**

### 4.2 The Variables

**D (Social Distance):** How close are you socially?

| Low D (close) | High D (distant) |
|---------------|-----------------|
| Family, close friends | Strangers, acquaintances |
| Long-term colleagues | New colleagues |
| People in your in-group | People in out-groups |

**P (Power):** How much power does the hearer have over you?

| Low P (you're equal or above) | High P (hearer has authority) |
|-------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Peer, friend, subordinate | Boss, professor, judge, parent |
| Same professional rank | Senior professional rank |
| Customer to service worker | Employee to employer |

**R (Ranking of Imposition):** How big is the ask in this culture?

| Low R (trivial) | High R (major) |
|----------------|----------------|
| "Pass the salt" | "Lend me your car for a week" |
| "What time is it?" | "Can I stay at your house?" |
| "Hold the door" | "Will you co-sign my loan?" |

### 4.3 Worked Examples

**Scenario 1: Asking a close friend (low D) to pass the salt (low R).**
- D = low, P = low, R = low → W = very low
- Strategy: Bald on-record. "Pass the salt?"

**Scenario 2: Asking your boss (high P) for a day off (moderate R).**
- D = moderate, P = high, R = moderate → W = high
- Strategy: Negative politeness with stacking.
- "I was wondering if there's any way I might possibly take Friday off? I know it's short notice, and I completely understand if it's not possible."

**Scenario 3: Asking a stranger (high D) for the time (low R).**
- D = high, P = neutral, R = very low → W = moderate
- Strategy: Mild negative politeness.
- "Excuse me, sorry -- do you have the time?"

**Scenario 4: Telling a new colleague (high D) their work has errors (moderate R, threatens positive face).**
- D = high, P = neutral, R = moderate → W = high
- Strategy: Negative politeness + positive politeness combination.
- "I really like the direction you've taken here [PP: compliment]. I noticed a couple of things that might need a second look [NP: hedge + minimize] -- do you want me to flag them?"

**Scenario 5: Asking a stranger (high D) to lend you their phone (high R).**
- D = high, P = neutral, R = high → W = very high
- Strategy: Heavy negative politeness.
- "I'm so sorry to bother you [NP6], and I know this is a really strange request [NP: acknowledge imposition], but my phone just died and I need to make an urgent call [give reasons]. Is there any way I could possibly borrow yours for just one minute [NP1 + NP3 + NP4]? I'd really appreciate it [NP10]."

### 4.4 The Formula as a Practical Decision Tool

For autistic learners, the Weight Formula is enormously useful because it converts **social intuition into calculation**:

1. **Assess D**: How close am I to this person? (Strangers = high. Friends = low.)
2. **Assess P**: Do they have power over me? (Boss, professor = high. Peer = low.)
3. **Assess R**: How big is what I'm asking/saying? (Salt = low. Car = high. Criticism = moderate-high.)
4. **Sum them up**: High total → more hedging, softening, apologizing. Low total → more directness is acceptable.

**The default rule**: When in doubt, err on the side of MORE politeness (further right on the strategy scale). It is always easier to dial back excessive politeness than to recover from being perceived as rude. Over-politeness is read as "formal but harmless." Under-politeness is read as "rude or aggressive."

## 5. English: A Negative-Politeness Language

### 5.1 Why English Defaults to Negative Politeness

English-speaking cultures (particularly British, American, and Australian) are characterized by a strong orientation toward **negative face** -- the protection of individual autonomy. This means:

- Requests are almost always hedged, even between friends
- Impositions are apologized for before they're made
- Criticism is softened with elaborate face-saving
- Direct orders are reserved for emergencies and extreme power differentials
- The default assumption is that any request is an imposition

Compare English to a positive-politeness dominant culture (e.g., Mediterranean, Latin American):

| English (negative-politeness dominant) | Positive-politeness dominant culture |
|---------------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| "Would you mind if I...?" | "Hey, do me a favor." |
| "I'm sorry to bother you, but..." | "Listen, I need..." |
| Elaborate hedging signals respect | Directness signals trust/closeness |
| Over-familiarity is rude | Over-formality is cold |
| Autonomy is the highest social value | Solidarity is the highest social value |

### 5.2 The Hedging Ecosystem

English has developed an extraordinarily rich system of hedging devices, all serving negative politeness:

**Modal verbs as hedges:**
- "Could" (instead of "can")
- "Would" (instead of "will")
- "Might" (instead of "may")
- "Should" (instead of "must")

**Past tense distancing** (using past tense for present requests to create psychological distance):
- "I **wanted** to ask you..." (not "I want to ask you")
- "I was **wondering** if..." (not "I wonder if")
- "I **thought** maybe..." (not "I think maybe")

**The word "just"** -- the single most overused negative politeness marker in English:
- "I **just** wanted to check..."
- "Could you **just** take a quick look?"
- "I **just** have one small question."
"Just" minimizes the imposition. It says: "This is small, this won't take long, I'm barely asking."

**Stacking hedges**: English speakers routinely stack 3-5 hedging devices in a single utterance:
1. "Pass the salt." (0 hedges -- bald on-record)
2. "Could you pass the salt?" (1 hedge -- modal)
3. "Could you possibly pass the salt?" (2 hedges -- modal + adverb)
4. "Sorry, could you possibly pass the salt?" (3 hedges -- apology + modal + adverb)
5. "Sorry, I don't suppose you could possibly pass the salt?" (4 hedges)
6. "I'm so sorry, I don't suppose you could possibly just pass the salt? If it's not too much trouble." (6 hedges)

Levels 1-2 are normal for friends. Level 3 for acquaintances. Level 4+ for strangers, superiors, or large impositions. Level 6 is either a very large imposition or British.

## 6. Politeness and the Autistic Experience

### 6.1 What the Research Shows

**Autistic individuals use fewer negative politeness markers** -- specifically hedges, softeners, and deference markers. This finding is robust across studies (Butterbrod, Tilburg University; VET employment study; computational pragmatics study, PMC9633181).

However, **positive politeness capacity is more variable** -- some autistic individuals use positive politeness strategies (warmth, interest, humor) effectively in certain contexts, particularly with people they know well (Sirota, 2004).

**Comprehension of conventionalized indirect speech** is largely preserved ("Could you pass the salt?" is understood as a request). The difficulty is specifically with **novel, unconventionalized** indirect forms (hints, insinuations).

### 6.2 The "Rudeness" Misperception

The most consequential finding: **autistic directness is consistently misinterpreted as rudeness by neurotypical listeners.**

This is not a deficit in the autistic person -- it is a **mismatch between communication systems**:

- Autistic communication values: clarity, honesty, efficiency, accuracy
- NT politeness system values: indirectness, face-protection, social lubrication

When an autistic person says "Your presentation has three factual errors" (bald on-record assertive), they are prioritizing accuracy. A neurotypical listener hears a face-threatening act performed without mitigation and interprets it as hostile or contemptuous -- when it was intended as helpful.

**Workplace impact**: The Szechy et al. (2024) study found that non-autistic colleagues were *more likely* to misinterpret autistic behavior (31.2% accuracy) than autistic colleagues (50.7% accuracy). The unemployment rate among autistic adults is estimated at 85%, with social communication differences being a major barrier.

### 6.3 Face and Autistic Identity

**Positive face in autistic experience:**
For many autistic individuals, positive face centers on **authenticity** rather than approval. The autistic desire is not primarily "be liked by performing social norms" but "be accepted as you actually are." This creates a fundamental tension with the NT politeness system, which often requires performing emotions you don't feel (congratulating when you don't care, apologizing when you don't feel guilty).

**Negative face in autistic experience:**
The desire for autonomy may be **heightened** in autistic individuals. Sensory sensitivities, need for routine, demand avoidance (PDA), and the cognitive load of social navigation all increase the felt weight of impositions. This means autistic individuals may be more sensitive to threats to their own negative face while simultaneously producing fewer negative politeness markers that protect others'.

**Masking as forced politeness:**
Camouflaging research (Hull et al., 2017, PMC5509825) documents that many autistic individuals learn to perform NT politeness norms at significant cognitive cost. This "masking" is associated with burnout, depression, and suicidality. The learning hub's approach is therefore critical: teach the system for **understanding and choice**, not for mandatory performance.

### 6.4 Autistic-Autistic Politeness Norms

Research is beginning to document that autistic communities have their own politeness system:

- **Directness as respect**: In autistic-autistic interaction, saying what you mean is valued as a form of respect and trust. "Wrapping" a message in hedges can feel dishonest or condescending.
- **Explicit negotiation**: Rather than inferring boundaries, autistic interlocutors often negotiate them openly: "I need to leave in 20 minutes" rather than dropping hints.
- **Tolerance for silence**: Pauses are not awkward -- they're processing time. The NT obligation to fill silence doesn't apply.
- **Information over ritual**: Phatic exchanges may be reduced or skipped in favor of substantive content.

**Cross-cultural parallels**: Israeli *dugriut* (direct speech as a cultural value), Dutch *bespreekbaarheid* (everything should be discussable), and German *Direktheit* (directness as honesty) all parallel autistic communication norms. This reinforces the frame that autistic communication is a **cultural style**, not a deficit.

### 6.5 Politeness as a Weapon

An important critique from the autistic community (AutSide): politeness norms can be weaponized. When someone uses elaborate politeness to make a manipulative request, the recipient is socially penalized for refusing directly. The politeness creates a coercive framework where the "polite" response is compliance and the "rude" response is self-advocacy.

Autistic individuals, who may detect the manipulation but lack the "polite" credibility to name it without being seen as the aggressor, are disproportionately harmed by this dynamic.

Understanding politeness as a *system* -- including its potential for manipulation -- is itself a form of empowerment.

## 7. A Practical Politeness Calculator

### 7.1 The Quick Assessment

For any FTA you need to perform:

**Step 1**: What type of FTA is it?
- Request/advice/suggestion → threatens hearer's **negative face**
- Criticism/disagreement/complaint → threatens hearer's **positive face**

**Step 2**: Calculate W = D + P + R
- D: How distant is the relationship? (1 = close friend, 5 = stranger)
- P: How much power does the hearer have? (1 = peer/subordinate, 5 = authority figure)
- R: How big is the imposition? (1 = trivial, 5 = major)

**Step 3**: Select strategy based on W

| W Score | Strategy | Example |
|---------|----------|---------|
| 3-5 (low) | Bald on-record or light positive politeness | "Hey, pass me that." |
| 6-8 (moderate) | Positive or mild negative politeness | "Could you pass me that?" |
| 9-11 (high) | Full negative politeness | "I'm sorry, would you mind passing me that?" |
| 12-14 (very high) | Heavy negative politeness or off-record | "I'm so sorry to trouble you, but I was wondering if..." |
| 15 (maximum) | Consider not performing the FTA | [Silence, or find another way] |

### 7.2 The "When in Doubt" Rules

1. **Default to one level more polite than you think is necessary.** Over-politeness is forgiven; under-politeness is penalized.
2. **Match the other person's level.** If they're being casual, you can be casual. If they're being formal, be formal.
3. **Requests always need more hedging than you think.** Even between friends, "Pass the salt" is less common than "Could you pass the salt?"
4. **Criticism always needs more softening than you think.** The sandwich method (positive → negative → positive) exists because criticism is one of the highest-weight FTAs.
5. **Upward communication always needs more politeness.** When in doubt about power, assume the other person has more.

### 7.3 Reading Others' Politeness

The same system works in reverse -- you can decode what others' politeness level tells you about how they perceive the situation:

- **Suddenly more formal** → They're creating distance (possibly angry, hurt, or establishing authority)
- **Suddenly less formal** → They're reducing distance (possibly building rapport, or disrespecting you)
- **Heavy hedging on a small request** → They're unsure of the relationship, or the request is bigger than it seems
- **No hedging on a big request** → They either have significant power, feel very close to you, or are being deliberately rude
- **Unusually polite** → Either a large request is coming, or they're being passive-aggressive

## 8. Key Takeaways

1. **Face is the engine.** Almost everything in English politeness exists to protect positive face (the desire to be liked) or negative face (the desire for autonomy).

2. **The Weight Formula is your calculator.** W = D + P + R. Assess distance, power, and imposition size, then select a strategy. This converts social intuition into a systematic process.

3. **English defaults to negative politeness.** When in doubt, hedge, soften, and apologize. This is not because English speakers are insecure -- it's because the culture prioritizes individual autonomy, and every request is treated as an imposition to be mitigated.

4. **Your directness is not a deficit.** Autistic communication prioritizes different values (clarity, accuracy, authenticity) that are equally valid. But understanding the NT politeness system gives you the choice of when to use it.

5. **Over-politeness is safer than under-politeness.** You can always dial back formality. Recovering from being perceived as rude is much harder.

6. **Politeness strategies stack.** Learning the individual strategies (hedge, minimize, apologize, give deference) gives you a toolkit. You combine them based on the Weight Formula.

7. **Other people's politeness level is data.** When someone suddenly gets more formal, more hedging, more apologetic -- they're telling you something about how they perceive the social situation. Read it.
