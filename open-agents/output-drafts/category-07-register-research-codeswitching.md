# Code-Switching and Style-Shifting: Research for English Pragmatics Knowledge Base

## Research Context
This document provides comprehensive research on code-switching and style-shifting for a knowledge base teaching English pragmatics to autistic individuals. The target audience has strong analytical and pattern-recognition abilities but may find implicit social-linguistic rules opaque. The research covers theoretical frameworks, practical mechanisms, social signaling functions, and the specific challenges and insights relevant to autistic experience.

---

## 1. What Code-Switching IS

### 1.1 Traditional Linguistic Definition

Code-switching in its original, narrow linguistic sense refers to the alternation between two or more languages (or language varieties) within a single conversation or even a single utterance. The term was first used in linguistics to describe what bilingual and multilingual speakers do when they shift between languages mid-discourse.

**Formal definition:** "The use of more than one linguistic variety in a manner consistent with the syntax and phonology of each variety" (Wikipedia, synthesizing Poplack and others). More precisely, code-switching is "the alternation between two or more languages, dialects, or language registers in the course of discourse between people who have more than one language in common."

**Key point:** Code-switching is distinct from plurilingualism (the *ability* to use multiple languages). Code-switching is the *act* of using multiple varieties together in real interaction.

**Types of code-switching (traditional):**
- **Inter-sentential switching:** Switching languages between sentences or clauses
- **Intra-sentential switching:** Switching within a single sentence
- **Tag-switching:** Inserting a tag phrase from one language into an utterance in another ("You know," "n'est-ce pas," etc.)

### 1.2 Broader Sociolinguistic Definition

The term has expanded far beyond its bilingual origins. In sociolinguistics, code-switching now refers to shifting between *any* language varieties -- including registers, dialects, styles, and sociolects -- not just different languages.

**Critical insight for autistic learners:** Even monolingual English speakers "code-switch" constantly. Every English speaker operates with a repertoire of different ways of speaking and shifts between them throughout the day. A monolingual English speaker who uses casual slang with friends but formal language in a job interview is code-switching between registers, just as surely as a Spanish-English bilingual who switches between languages.

**Examples of monolingual code-switching:**
- A speaker of African American Vernacular English (AAVE) shifting to Standard American English in professional settings
- A person using technical jargon at work but plain language at home
- Anyone who speaks differently to their boss than to their best friend
- A doctor who uses medical terminology with colleagues but simplified explanations with patients

As one source puts it: "Monolinguals can communicate effects like friendliness or distance by varying the level of formality of their speech." This is functionally identical to what bilingual speakers do when they switch languages -- the mechanism is different, but the social purpose is the same.

### 1.3 The Cognitive Process of Register Selection

Code-switching (in the broad sense) is not random. It involves a real-time cognitive process with identifiable stages:

**Step 1: Context Assessment**
The speaker (usually unconsciously) scans the social situation. Key variables include:
- Who is present (audience/interlocutors)
- What is being discussed (topic)
- Where the interaction is happening (setting)
- What the communicative purpose is (goal)
- What the relationship dynamics are (power, solidarity, familiarity)

**Step 2: Register Selection**
Based on the context assessment, the speaker selects an appropriate language variety. Carol Myers-Scotton's **Markedness Model** proposes that speakers are "rational actors who use the linguistic form that is indexical of the social role they wish to present in a particular interaction." Speakers choose the *unmarked* (expected, normal) code for a given situation, or deliberately choose a *marked* (unexpected) code to create a specific social effect.

**Step 3: Production and Monitoring**
The speaker produces language in the selected register while monitoring the interaction for cues that might require adjustment. This monitoring is largely automatic in neurotypical speakers but may require more conscious effort for autistic speakers.

**Step 4: Dynamic Adjustment**
Throughout the conversation, speakers continuously reassess and may shift registers in response to changing conditions -- a new person enters, the topic shifts, the emotional temperature changes.

**The key insight:** For neurotypical speakers, steps 1-4 happen largely below conscious awareness, like driving a familiar route. For autistic speakers, the process may need to be more deliberate and conscious, more like navigating with a map. This does not mean the skill cannot be developed -- it means the learning pathway is different.

### 1.4 Theoretical Frameworks for Understanding Code-Switching

#### Gumperz: Situational vs. Metaphorical Code-Switching

John Gumperz, a pioneer of interactional sociolinguistics, distinguished two fundamental types:

- **Situational code-switching:** A direct, predictable relationship between code use and observable situational factors. You use one variety at home, another at school, another at work. The switch happens because the *situation* changes. Example: Speaking casually in the break room, then switching to formal language when the meeting starts.

- **Metaphorical code-switching:** The switch happens *within* a situation to signal something about the speaker's attitude, the nature of the topic, or the relationship. The situation hasn't externally changed, but the speaker imports associations from another context. Example: A teacher who normally speaks formally suddenly uses slang to connect with students about a sensitive topic -- importing the warmth and solidarity associated with casual speech into the formal classroom.

**Why this matters for autistic learners:** Situational code-switching follows clearer rules (setting X = register Y) and is generally easier to learn explicitly. Metaphorical code-switching is more subtle and context-dependent, carrying social meaning that needs to be decoded.

#### Gumperz: Contextualization Cues

Gumperz also introduced the concept of **contextualization cues** -- linguistic and paralinguistic features that signal how an utterance should be interpreted. These cues include prosody (intonation, stress), lexical choice, code-switching itself, and other stylistic features. A code-switch can itself function as a contextualization cue, telling the listener: "interpret what follows differently from what came before."

**Example:** A manager speaking casually to a team suddenly switches to formal register: "Alright, so -- *I need to inform you that the deadline has been moved to Friday.*" The shift to formal language is the contextualization cue that signals: this is serious, official, non-negotiable.

#### Myers-Scotton: The Markedness Model

Carol Myers-Scotton's framework revolves around the concept of **markedness** -- the degree to which a linguistic choice is expected or unexpected in a given context.

- **Unmarked choices:** The expected, default code for a situation. Using formal English in a business meeting is unmarked. Using casual English with friends is unmarked. These choices maintain the existing social equilibrium.

- **Marked choices:** Unexpected code choices that disrupt the social equilibrium and signal something specific. Using slang in a business meeting is marked. Using formal English with close friends is marked. These choices carry additional social meaning precisely because they are unexpected.

- **We-code vs. They-code:** Related to Gumperz's concept, Myers-Scotton describes how in any given community, certain varieties function as solidarity codes (we-codes, associated with in-group identity, informality, closeness) and others as distance codes (they-codes, associated with formality, authority, outsider status). Switching between them signals shifts in the speaker's social positioning.

#### Halliday: Field, Tenor, and Mode

M.A.K. Halliday's **Systemic Functional Linguistics** provides a framework for analyzing what determines register through three variables:

- **Field:** What is being talked about; the subject matter and social activity
- **Tenor:** Who is talking to whom; the social relationships and roles of participants
- **Mode:** How the communication is happening; the channel (spoken, written, digital) and rhetorical function

**These three variables collectively define a register.** When any of them changes, the register shifts. This provides a systematic framework for predicting when code-switching will occur:
- Field changes (topic shifts from weekend plans to budget review) --> register shift
- Tenor changes (peer leaves, supervisor arrives) --> register shift
- Mode changes (face-to-face conversation becomes email) --> register shift

#### Dell Hymes: Communicative Competence and the SPEAKING Model

Dell Hymes challenged Chomsky's narrow definition of linguistic competence (knowledge of grammar) by proposing the broader concept of **communicative competence** -- the knowledge necessary to use language appropriately in social context. Hymes argued that knowing *how to speak* in different contexts is just as fundamental as knowing grammar.

His SPEAKING model identifies the components that define any communicative event:
- **S**etting and Scene (physical and psychological context)
- **P**articipants (speaker, addressee, audience)
- **E**nds (purposes and goals)
- **A**ct sequence (form and content of message)
- **K**ey (tone, manner, spirit)
- **I**nstrumentalities (channel and forms of speech)
- **N**orms (of interaction and interpretation)
- **G**enres (types of speech events)

**For autistic learners:** The SPEAKING model provides a systematic checklist for analyzing any social situation. Rather than relying on intuition to know what register to use, one can consciously assess: Who is here? What is the purpose? What is the setting? What tone is expected? This converts an intuitive process into an analytical one.

## 2. Style-Shifting (Labov)

### 2.1 Labov's Foundational Research

William Labov, often called the father of variationist sociolinguistics, demonstrated through rigorous empirical research that linguistic variation is not random but systematically correlated with social factors. His work on style-shifting is foundational.

### 2.2 The Attention-to-Speech Continuum

Labov proposed that speech styles can be arranged along "a single dimension, measured by the amount of attention paid to speech." This creates a continuum:

**Casual speech** (least attention to speech)
- Occurs when speakers are relaxed, emotionally engaged, or talking to someone other than the interviewer
- Most reveals the speaker's natural, unmonitored language patterns
- Labov called this the **vernacular** -- "the style in which minimum attention is given to the monitoring of speech"
- The vernacular is, paradoxically, "the most regular in its structure" -- it is the most systematic, rule-governed form of a person's speech

**Careful speech** (moderate attention)
- Occurs when speakers know they are being observed or evaluated
- More conscious self-monitoring
- More standard or prestige forms appear

**Reading passage style** (more attention)
- Reading aloud a connected text
- Greater formality than spontaneous speech
- Speakers can "see" the standard forms and produce them

**Word list style** (still more attention)
- Reading individual words in isolation
- Even more focus on "correct" pronunciation
- More standard forms produced

**Minimal pairs** (maximum attention)
- Reading pairs of words that differ by only one sound (e.g., "pin" vs. "pen")
- Maximum focus on individual sounds
- Most standard/prestige pronunciation

### 2.3 The Observer's Paradox

Labov identified a fundamental methodological challenge: "The aim of linguistic research in the community must be to find out how people talk when they are not being systematically observed; yet we can only obtain this data by systematic observation."

This is the **Observer's Paradox** -- the act of studying speech changes the speech being studied. When people know they are being recorded or observed, they shift toward more formal registers. Labov developed techniques to elicit casual speech despite this paradox, including:
- Asking emotionally engaging questions ("Have you ever been in a situation where you thought you were going to die?")
- Noticing when speakers become absorbed in their stories and "forget" they are being recorded
- Recording peer group interactions rather than one-on-one interviews

### 2.4 The New York Department Store Study

Labov's most famous study (1966) examined style-shifting in real-world conditions. He posed as a customer in three New York department stores of different social prestige:
- **Saks Fifth Avenue** (high prestige)
- **Macy's** (middle prestige)
- **S. Klein** (low prestige)

He asked questions designed to elicit the response "fourth floor," then pretended not to hear, prompting the employee to repeat the phrase with more emphasis (and thus more attention to speech).

**Results:** The variable studied was post-vocalic /r/ (pronouncing the "r" in words like "fourth" and "floor"), which was a prestige feature in New York English. Saks employees used the most /r/ (62%), Macy's was intermediate (51%), and Klein's used the least (20%). Crucially, employees at all stores produced *more* /r/ in the emphatic (second) pronunciation than in the casual (first) -- demonstrating style-shifting in real time.

**Key insight:** This showed that the same person systematically shifts their pronunciation based on how much attention they are paying to their speech. This is style-shifting in action.

### 2.5 Style-Shifting vs. Register Shifting

Style-shifting (Labov's concept) and register shifting are related but distinct:

- **Style-shifting** specifically refers to changes along the attention-to-speech continuum -- how carefully someone monitors their pronunciation and grammar. It is primarily about the *degree of monitoring*.

- **Register shifting** is broader -- it encompasses changes in vocabulary, grammar, discourse patterns, politeness strategies, and overall communicative approach in response to situational variables (audience, topic, setting, channel).

- In practice, they often co-occur. When you shift to a more formal register (e.g., speaking to your boss), you typically also style-shift (paying more attention to your pronunciation). But they can be independent: you might use casual vocabulary while carefully monitoring your pronunciation, or use formal vocabulary while speaking rapidly and casually.

## 3. When and Why Speakers Switch Registers

### 3.1 Change of Audience

The most powerful trigger for register shifting is a change in who is listening. Allan Bell's **Audience Design** framework (1984) proposes that style-shifting occurs primarily in response to the speaker's audience, not just the topic or setting.

**Bell's audience types:**
- **Addressee:** The person directly being spoken to (strongest influence on style)
- **Auditor:** A known, ratified listener who is not directly addressed (moderate influence)
- **Overhearer:** A known but not ratified listener (lesser influence)
- **Eavesdropper:** An unknown listener (minimal direct influence, but awareness of potential eavesdroppers can shift register)

**Common audience-triggered shifts:**

| Scenario | Register Shift | Reason |
|----------|---------------|--------|
| Friend enters the room | May become more casual | Solidarity, comfort |
| Boss enters the room | Shift to more formal | Respect, professionalism, power dynamics |
| Child enters the room | Simplified vocabulary, warmer tone | Accommodation to comprehension level |
| Stranger overhears | May moderate slang, profanity | Social propriety, impression management |
| Parent calls | May shift to "child" register regardless of age | Relational identity, habit |

**Pattern for autistic learners:** The general rule is: *the more social distance or power difference between you and the listener, the more formal your register should be.* Close relationships = casual register. Professional/distant relationships = formal register. Authority figures = formal register.

### 3.2 Change of Topic

Topic shifts often trigger register shifts even when the audience stays the same.

**Examples:**
- Two colleagues chatting about weekend plans (casual) --> discussion turns to a project deadline (more formal, more precise)
- Friends gossiping casually (informal) --> one friend brings up a serious health concern (register shifts to more careful, supportive)
- A family dinner conversation (casual) --> discussion of finances or legal matters (more formal, more careful word choice)

**The mechanism:** Different topics carry different social weight, different expected levels of precision, and different emotional valences. Technical topics demand technical vocabulary. Serious topics demand more careful framing. Light topics permit more casual language.

### 3.3 Change of Setting

Physical environment acts as a powerful contextual cue for register selection.

**Examples:**
- Same two coworkers, same topic: the language used in the office is different from the language used at the bar after work
- A teacher at school vs. the same teacher at a social gathering
- A doctor in the clinic vs. the same doctor at a barbecue
- A church or courtroom vs. a backyard party

**The mechanism:** Settings carry institutional expectations and social norms that constrain register. A courtroom demands formal language regardless of who is present. A bar permits casual language regardless of topic.

### 3.4 Change of Channel (Mode)

The communication medium itself shapes register expectations.

**Channel-register associations in modern English:**

| Channel | Typical Register | Notes |
|---------|-----------------|-------|
| Face-to-face | Variable, context-dependent | Richest context cues (tone, gesture, expression) |
| Phone call | Slightly more formal than face-to-face | Lacks visual cues, compensates with verbal clarity |
| Video call | Between phone and face-to-face | Visual cues present but attenuated |
| Email | More formal than speech | Permanent record, wider potential audience, more time to compose |
| Text message | Casual to moderate | Informal conventions, abbreviations acceptable |
| Slack/Teams message | Casual to moderate | Workplace casual, but still professional |
| Formal letter | Highly formal | Institutional conventions, formulaic elements |
| Social media post | Varies by platform and audience | Public vs. private distinction matters |

**Key insight:** A project update might start with an email for detailed information (formal), followed by a Slack message for quick clarification (casual), and then a phone call for in-depth discussion (moderate). The same information is communicated differently across channels.

**For autistic learners:** Channel-register associations are among the most systematic and learnable register rules. "This is an email, therefore more formal" is a reliable heuristic.

### 3.5 Strategic Register Shifts

Beyond reactive shifts (responding to changes in audience, topic, setting, or channel), speakers also make *deliberate, strategic* register shifts to achieve specific social goals. This is where code-switching becomes most complex and most socially powerful.

#### Deliberately Going Formal to Signal Anger or Authority

When a speaker who normally uses casual language suddenly shifts to formal language, it signals that something has changed emotionally or in the power dynamic.

**Examples:**
- A parent who normally says "Hey, come here" shifting to "Come here. Now." or using the child's full name including middle name -- signals serious displeasure
- A friend who normally says "Don't worry about it" shifting to "I would prefer that you not do that again" -- signals genuine anger beneath controlled formality
- A manager who normally uses first names and casual language shifting to "I need to speak with you in my office" -- signals disciplinary context
- Dropping contractions: "I don't care" (casual, possibly throwaway) vs. "I do not care" (formal, emphatic, signals deliberate emotional weight)
- "Per my last email" -- a workplace phrase that has become widely recognized as a formal-register way of expressing frustration or irritation

**The mechanism:** Formality creates psychological distance. When someone who is normally close to you suddenly creates distance through formal language, the contrast itself carries the message. The shift *is* the message.

#### Deliberately Going Casual to Signal Solidarity or Defuse Tension

Conversely, a strategic shift toward informality can break down barriers and reduce tension.

**Examples:**
- A doctor who normally uses clinical language saying "Look, here's the deal..." -- signals they are being real, approachable, human
- A manager in a tense meeting saying "Okay, let's just take a breath here" or cracking a joke -- using informality to de-escalate
- A teacher shifting from lecture mode to "Alright, real talk..." -- signals a shift to honest, peer-like communication
- Someone offering to use first names after being addressed formally -- "Please, call me Sarah" -- explicitly lowering the register to invite closeness

**Research context:** Studies on humor and de-escalation show that "humor defuses conflict because of its coping function, which allows people to psychologically detach from their immediate situation." Casual language functions similarly -- it imports the emotional associations of relaxed, safe, intimate contexts into potentially threatening ones.

#### Using a Formal Register Ironically

Speakers sometimes deploy formal language in deliberately inappropriate contexts for humorous or ironic effect.

**Examples:**
- Texting a friend: "I hereby formally request your presence at Taco Bell this evening" -- the mismatch between frozen/formal register and casual context creates humor
- Responding to a minor inconvenience: "Well, this is most unfortunate" -- formal understatement applied to a trivial situation
- Among close friends, using overly formal address: "Greetings, esteemed colleague" instead of "Hey"
- British humor frequently relies on this: deadpan, formal delivery of absurd or trivial content

**The mechanism:** The humor comes from the *mismatch* between register and context. The speaker is performing a register that both parties know is inappropriate, and the shared recognition of the performance creates in-group bonding. This is a form of *play* with social conventions.

**Important note for autistic learners:** Ironic register use can be particularly difficult to detect because it requires recognizing that the speaker is *deliberately* mismatching register and context. The surface meaning (formal) contradicts the intended meaning (playful/ironic). Cues that someone is being ironic with register include: the context makes the formal register obviously inappropriate, the speaker's tone or facial expression contradicts the formality, and the relationship between speakers normally uses a very different register.

#### "Performing" a Register for Social Effect

Sometimes speakers adopt an entire register as a conscious performance -- putting on a character, imitating someone, or deliberately invoking the social associations of a particular way of speaking.

**Examples:**
- Imitating a stereotypical authority figure to mock bureaucracy
- Adopting "corporate speak" ironically to comment on workplace culture
- Using exaggerated informality or slang as a performance of coolness or rebellion
- Adopting a "teacher voice" when playfully correcting someone

## 4. Register-Shifting as Social Signal

### 4.1 The Signaling System

Register shifts are not just functional (adapting to communicate effectively). They are *semiotic* -- they carry social meaning beyond the literal content of speech. The shift itself is a signal.

**Core principle:** In any ongoing interaction, the *current* register establishes a baseline. Deviations from that baseline carry meaning. The meaning comes from the *direction* and *magnitude* of the shift, not from the absolute register level.

### 4.2 When Someone Suddenly Becomes More Formal

**Signals:**
- **Distance creation:** The speaker is pulling back emotionally or socially
- **Anger or displeasure:** Formality as a controlled expression of negative emotion (contrasted with shouting, which is informal anger)
- **Authority assertion:** The speaker is invoking their institutional role or power
- **Disapproval:** Creating formality where warmth previously existed signals withdrawal of approval
- **Boundary setting:** "I'm setting a limit here" communicated through register rather than explicit statement
- **Seriousness:** "This is not a joke; I need you to understand this matters"

**Specific markers of a shift toward formality:**
- Using someone's full name or title instead of nickname/first name
- Dropping contractions ("I will not" instead of "I won't")
- Using more complex sentence structures
- Eliminating slang, humor, or playful language
- Adopting institutional/professional vocabulary
- Speaking more slowly and deliberately
- Reducing physical proximity or casual body language

**Example scenario:** Two colleagues who normally joke around. Colleague A makes a comment that crosses a line. Colleague B's face goes neutral and they say: "I'd appreciate it if you didn't make comments like that." The shift from joking to formal is the primary signal of displeasure -- possibly more powerful than if B had said angrily, "Don't say that!"

### 4.3 When Someone Suddenly Becomes More Casual

**Signals:**
- **Rapport-building:** The speaker is inviting closeness, signaling trust
- **Inclusion:** "I'm treating you as an insider, a friend, one of us"
- **Relaxation:** "The formal part is over; we can be ourselves now"
- **De-escalation:** "I don't want this to be a conflict; let's be human about it"
- **Vulnerability:** Dropping formal armor to signal openness

**Specific markers of a shift toward informality:**
- Switching from title + last name to first name
- Using contractions, slang, or colloquialisms
- Using humor, especially self-deprecating humor
- Speaking faster, with more natural rhythm
- Relaxing physical posture
- Using in-group language or shared references

**Example scenario:** A new employee's manager has been using professional language throughout onboarding. After a few weeks, the manager says: "Hey, want to grab coffee? I should probably stop being so formal -- just call me Mike." The casual shift signals that the probationary social distance is being reduced.

### 4.4 When Someone Matches Your Register

**Signals:**
- **Alignment:** "I'm on the same page as you"
- **Acceptance:** "Your way of communicating is fine; I'm comfortable here"
- **Social harmony:** Register matching reduces friction and signals cooperative intent
- **Rapport:** Matching creates a sense of similarity and connection

**This is the default, expected behavior.** When it happens, nothing feels notable. It is the *absence* of matching -- the mismatch -- that is socially marked.

### 4.5 When Someone Mismatches Your Register

**Possible signals depending on context:**
- **Power move:** Using a more formal register than the other person asserts authority or superiority
- **Social awkwardness:** The person may not know the appropriate register (common for newcomers to a group, or for anyone who has difficulty reading social context)
- **Deliberate distance:** Refusing to match casual register can signal that someone doesn't want to be friends or peers
- **Conflict:** Maintaining formality when informality is offered (or vice versa) can signal disagreement with the relationship being proposed
- **Identity assertion:** Refusing to accommodate can signal "I am who I am, and I'm not going to change to fit your expectations"

**Example scenario:** At a casual social gathering, everyone is using first names and relaxed language. One person consistently speaks formally and uses titles. This might signal: social anxiety, cultural difference, that they don't feel they belong, or that they are deliberately maintaining distance.

**For autistic learners:** Register mismatch is one of the most common sources of social friction for autistic people. Research documents that "someone with Social Communication Disorder might use overly formal language in relaxed environments, creating a sense of distance, or speak too casually in professional settings, leading to misunderstandings about their competence or intent." Understanding that register mismatch sends unintended signals is a crucial first step.

## 5. Accommodation Theory (Giles)

### 5.1 Overview

Communication Accommodation Theory (CAT), developed by Howard Giles beginning in the 1970s (originally as "Speech Accommodation Theory"), explains how and why people adjust their communication behavior during interactions. It is one of the most influential frameworks in sociolinguistics.

**Core principle:** People adjust their speech (and other communication behaviors) to manage social distance. They can minimize differences (convergence) or maximize them (divergence).

### 5.2 Convergence

**Definition:** Adjusting your speech patterns to become more similar to your conversation partner's.

**What gets converged:**
- Pronunciation and accent
- Speaking rate and pause lengths
- Vocabulary and formality level
- Vocal intensity (volume)
- Non-verbal behaviors (posture, gestures)
- Intimacy of self-disclosures
- Topic selection
- Sentence length and complexity

**Why people converge:**
- To gain social approval
- To increase communicative efficiency
- To maintain a positive social identity
- To signal solidarity and rapport
- To reduce social distance
- Because it happens automatically -- people naturally synchronize with conversation partners

**Example:** A manager speaking with a new intern might slow down, use simpler vocabulary, and adopt a warmer tone. A person from the rural South visiting New York might unconsciously reduce their accent. Two people on a first date might gradually match each other's speaking pace and volume.

**Key research finding:** Both convergence and divergence "can occur either subconsciously (without the speaker recognizing what they are doing), or consciously, where the speaker intentionally makes nonverbal and verbal adjustments." For neurotypical speakers, convergence is often automatic -- they "immediately (and often unconsciously) begin to synchronize aspects of their verbal and nonverbal behavior."

### 5.3 Divergence

**Definition:** Adjusting your speech patterns to become *more different* from your conversation partner's.

**Why people diverge:**
- To assert group identity ("I'm not like you, and I'm not going to pretend I am")
- To mark social distance deliberately
- To express disapproval or hostility
- To maintain distinctiveness
- To assert power or authority

**Example:** A teenager who normally speaks Standard English at school might deliberately increase their use of slang or non-standard features when talking to parents -- asserting independence and youth identity. A Welsh speaker in England might pronounce Welsh names with exaggerated Welsh phonology to assert cultural identity.

### 5.4 Overaccommodation

**Definition:** Going too far in adjusting one's speech, exceeding what is necessary or appropriate for successful communication.

Giles defined overaccommodation as "an attempt to overdo efforts in regulating, modifying or responding to others" with the effect of making the target "feel worse."

**Forms of overaccommodation:**
- **Patronizing speech:** Speaking very simply to someone who doesn't need it (e.g., "elderspeak" -- addressing elderly people in baby talk regardless of their cognitive abilities)
- **Over-correction:** Exaggeratedly careful pronunciation or grammar that comes across as condescending
- **Sensory overaccommodation:** Shouting at a blind person, or speaking very slowly to someone with mild hearing difficulty
- **Dependency-related overaccommodation:** Being excessively directive or disciplinary

**Relevance to autism:** Autistic adults frequently experience overaccommodation. When neurotypical people learn someone is autistic, they may unconsciously simplify their language, slow their speech, or adopt a patronizing tone -- even if the autistic person is fully competent in the conversation. This is a form of overaccommodation that can feel infantilizing and signals that the speaker perceives the autistic person as less capable.

### 5.5 Underaccommodation

**Definition:** Not adjusting enough -- failing to adapt one's communication to meet the needs or expectations of the other person.

**Examples:**
- Using highly technical jargon with a layperson
- Speaking at the same rapid pace regardless of whether the listener is keeping up
- Failing to adjust formality when the social context calls for it

### 5.6 How Accommodation Operates Differently for Autistic Speakers

**For neurotypical speakers:** Convergence is largely automatic and unconscious. People naturally and effortlessly begin to mirror their conversation partner's speech patterns, rate, volume, and vocabulary level. This happens without deliberate thought.

**For autistic speakers:** The automatic component may be reduced or absent. This does not mean accommodation is impossible -- it means it may require *conscious, deliberate effort* rather than happening automatically. This has several implications:

1. **Higher cognitive load:** Actively monitoring and adjusting register requires mental energy that neurotypical speakers don't need to expend
2. **Delayed adjustment:** Conscious processing takes longer than automatic processing, so register shifts may come slightly later than expected
3. **Possible over- or under-shooting:** Without the automatic calibration that comes from unconscious processing, deliberate accommodation may sometimes be too much or too little
4. **Fatigue:** Sustained conscious accommodation is exhausting, contributing to the well-documented pattern of autistic burnout

**Important reframe:** The challenge is not that autistic people *cannot* accommodate -- many can and do, often skillfully. The challenge is that it costs more cognitive and emotional energy because it requires conscious processing rather than automatic processing. This is analogous to the difference between a native speaker producing grammar automatically and a language learner who must consciously apply grammatical rules.

## 6. Code-Switching and Identity

### 6.1 Register Choice as Group Membership Signal

Language variety is one of the most powerful markers of social group membership. The variety you speak signals:
- Where you are from (regional dialect)
- Your social class (sociolect)
- Your profession (professional register/jargon)
- Your age group (generational language)
- Your subculture (in-group language)
- Your educational background (formal vs. informal language)

**In-group language:** "A specialized form of communication used by specific groups to create and reinforce their social identity, often including jargon, slang, or unique expressions not easily understood by outsiders." When members use in-group language, they "strengthen their connections with one another while simultaneously delineating boundaries against outsiders."

**The dual function:** Every code choice simultaneously includes (signals belonging to one group) and excludes (signals distance from other groups). Using slang with friends says "I'm one of you" while also saying "I'm not performing for outsiders." Using professional jargon says "I'm a competent member of this profession" while also saying "I'm not speaking to laypeople right now."

### 6.2 Professional Identity vs. Personal Identity

Most adults maintain at least two registers:
- A **professional register** used at work (more formal, institutional vocabulary, regulated emotional expression, standard grammar)
- A **personal register** used with friends and family (more casual, in-group language, freer emotional expression, possibly non-standard features)

The transition between these registers is one of the most common daily code-switches. Many people describe the experience of "leaving work mode" or "putting on my work voice." Some professions require particularly dramatic register shifts -- a drill sergeant, for instance, uses a very different register on the parade ground than at the dinner table.

### 6.3 The Emotional Cost of Sustained Register Performance

Maintaining a register that is distant from one's "authentic" or "default" speech is emotionally and cognitively costly. This is well-documented in several populations:

**African American professionals:** Research shows that 61% of Black employees report having to "compromise their authenticity to conform to dominant (often white) workplace standards." Code-switching can "deplete cognitive resources and hinder performance" and "reduces authentic self-expression and contributes to burnout." W.E.B. Du Bois's concept of **double consciousness** -- "the peculiar sensation of always looking at oneself through the eyes of others" -- describes the psychological burden of constantly monitoring and adjusting one's language and behavior for a dominant culture.

**Anyone performing a high-effort register:** The more different the required register is from one's default, the more effortful and draining the performance. An introverted person performing an extroverted sales register, a working-class person performing an upper-class register, a non-native speaker performing a native-like register -- all experience this cost to varying degrees.

**The broader point:** All humans experience some cost from register performance. The cost increases with the magnitude of the gap between default and required register, the duration of the performance, and the social consequences of "slipping."

### 6.4 When Register Shifting Feels Like "Being Fake" -- A Key Autistic Concern

This is perhaps the most important section for the target audience of this knowledge base.

**The autistic experience of register shifting:**

Many autistic people report that register shifting feels deeply inauthentic -- like "being fake" or "lying about who I am." This is not a character flaw or a lack of understanding. It stems from several intersecting factors:

1. **Reduced automatic calibration:** Because register shifting requires more conscious effort for autistic speakers, it feels more deliberate and therefore more artificial. When neurotypical speakers shift registers, much of the process is unconscious, so it doesn't *feel* like they are doing anything different. When autistic speakers shift registers, the conscious effort makes the shift feel like a performance rather than a natural adaptation.

2. **Strong identity-values integration:** Research suggests that for autistic individuals, "values aren't just beliefs -- they are who they are, with no daylight between 'what I believe' and 'who I am.'" This means that adopting a communication style that doesn't align with core values feels like a fundamental identity violation, not just a social adjustment.

3. **Literal communication preference:** Many autistic people prefer direct, literal communication. The implicit, layered nature of register play -- where the same words can mean different things in different registers, where formal language can mean anger, where casual language can be a power move -- conflicts with a preference for straightforward meaning.

4. **Masking overlap:** For autistic people, register shifting often gets tangled up with **masking** (also called camouflaging) -- the broader practice of suppressing autistic traits to appear neurotypical. It can be difficult to separate "I'm adjusting my register for this context" (which all humans do) from "I'm hiding who I am to avoid rejection" (which is masking and is psychologically harmful).

**Critical distinction -- Code-Switching vs. Masking:**

| Feature | Code-Switching (Register Shifting) | Masking (Camouflaging) |
|---------|-----------------------------------|----------------------|
| **What changes** | Language style, vocabulary, formality level | Core behaviors, sensory responses, emotional expression, self-presentation |
| **Conscious/unconscious** | Can be either | Often becomes unconscious over time, starting as conscious |
| **Universal?** | Yes -- all humans do it | More specific to neurodivergent and marginalized populations |
| **Core identity** | Does not require changing who you fundamentally are | Requires suppressing fundamental aspects of self |
| **Energy cost** | Moderate | Often severe |
| **Health impact** | Generally low when voluntary | Associated with burnout, anxiety, depression, identity confusion |
| **Purpose** | Communicative effectiveness, social navigation | Survival, avoidance of rejection or punishment |
| **Analogy** | Changing clothes for different occasions | Wearing a costume that doesn't fit, all day, every day |

**The reframe for autistic learners:** Register shifting is *not* inherently being fake. It is more like being multilingual in styles -- the same way a bilingual person is still authentically themselves whether speaking English or Spanish, a person using a professional register is still authentically themselves. The key distinction is whether the shift is *volitional and situational* (I choose to use more formal language in this meeting) versus *survival-driven and identity-suppressing* (I must hide who I am to avoid harm).

That said, the line between code-switching and masking can genuinely be blurry for autistic people, and the discomfort with register shifting is *valid*. The goal is not to eliminate the discomfort but to develop the skill of register shifting as a *tool* that can be deployed *when the person chooses to*, rather than as an exhausting constant requirement.

## 7. Register Shifts That Carry Specific Pragmatic Meaning -- Extended Examples

### 7.1 Suddenly Using Someone's Full Name

**The shift:** From casual name use (first name, nickname) to full formal name (first + middle, first + last, or title + last name).

**What it signals:**
- **Anger or serious displeasure** (most common): A parent saying "Jonathan Michael Harris, come here RIGHT NOW" instead of "Johnny, come here" -- the full name signals that the speaker is genuinely upset, not just mildly annoyed. The formality of the full name imports the gravity of a formal context into what is normally a casual relationship.
- **Authority assertion:** A teacher who normally uses first names shifting to "Mr. Chen, please take your seat" -- signals that the teacher is invoking institutional authority rather than personal relationship.
- **Social reset:** When a relationship has become too casual and someone uses a more formal name to re-establish boundaries.

**Why it works:** Names exist on a formality continuum. "Johnny" < "John" < "Jonathan" < "Jonathan Harris" < "Mr. Harris." Movement up this continuum within an established relationship signals a shift toward formality, distance, and seriousness. The contrast with the usual naming pattern is what carries the meaning.

**The cultural universality:** Research confirms this is "a common parenting behavior across many households." Parents use their child's first and middle name when angry "to emphasize the gravity of the situation and to get their attention." The shift from intimate naming to formal naming is one of the most widely recognized register-shift signals in English.

### 7.2 Dropping Contractions

**The shift:** From contracted forms (don't, won't, can't, I'm, we're) to uncontracted forms (do not, will not, cannot, I am, we are).

**What it signals:**
- **Emphatic seriousness:** "I don't want to discuss this" (casual, possibly dismissive) vs. "I do not want to discuss this" (formal, emphatic, the speaker means this deeply)
- **Controlled anger:** "You can't do that" (normal, casual prohibition) vs. "You cannot do that" (formal, cold, signals the speaker is restraining stronger emotion)
- **Official/institutional speech:** "We will not be accepting further applications" -- the uncontracted form signals institutional authority
- **Deliberate precision:** In contexts where clarity matters (legal, medical, safety), uncontracted forms eliminate ambiguity

**The mechanism:** Contractions are a feature of casual, spoken English. They signal informality, ease, and conversational flow. Removing contractions adds a beat -- a pause -- that slows the speech down and makes it more deliberate. This deliberateness carries the pragmatic weight. The speaker is signaling: "I have chosen each word carefully."

**Research context:** "Using 'do not' instead of 'don't' signals formality, emphasis, clarity, or a particular tone." In everyday interactions, native English speakers "use contractions much more often than they articulate the two words that make up the contraction." Therefore, the *absence* of contraction is the marked, meaningful choice.

### 7.3 Switching from Casual to Formal Mid-Sentence

**The shift:** Beginning a statement in casual register and shifting to formal register partway through, or vice versa.

**What it signals:**
- **Correcting course:** The speaker realizes they should be more serious/formal and adjusts in real time. Example: "Yeah, so like -- *I mean, the proposal has several issues that need to be addressed before we can proceed.*"
- **Marking the important part:** The casual lead-in establishes rapport; the formal shift marks the content that really matters. Example: "Look, I hear you, but *the policy is clear on this matter.*"
- **Power shift:** The speaker is moving from peer mode to authority mode. Example: "Hey, that's funny, but -- *I need you to complete that report by end of day.*"
- **Emotional shift:** "Haha yeah -- *actually, that really hurt my feelings.*"

**The mechanism:** The mid-sentence shift creates a before/after contrast that draws attention to the shift point. Everything after the shift is marked as belonging to a different social frame than everything before it.

### 7.4 Using Slang or In-Group Language to Include Someone

**The shift:** Moving from standard/neutral language to in-group-specific language (slang, jargon, shared references, in-jokes).

**What it signals:**
- **Inclusion:** "I consider you one of us." Using in-group language with someone who isn't yet a member can be an act of *welcoming* them into the group.
- **Trust:** In-group language is a form of vulnerability -- it reveals the speaker's group affiliations and non-professional self.
- **Solidarity:** "We share something that outsiders don't."
- **Testing:** Sometimes in-group language is used to see if the other person *recognizes* it, functioning as a social litmus test.

**Research context:** "When individuals recognize the informal language (slang) of their fellow group members, they feel confident and included in their group; however, when individuals hear their peers communicate norms by using slang with which they are unfamiliar, it creates feelings of exclusion and threatens their identity as a group member."

**Examples:**
- A gamer using gaming terminology with a new acquaintance to see if they are also a gamer
- A colleague starting to use workplace-specific nicknames and inside jokes with a new team member -- signaling they are now "in"
- A person of the same cultural background using heritage-language phrases or culturally specific expressions to signal shared identity

### 7.5 Using Formal Language to Exclude Someone

**The shift:** Moving from accessible, casual language to formal, technical, or institutional language in a way that deliberately creates barriers to participation.

**What it signals:**
- **Exclusion:** "You are not qualified to participate in this conversation"
- **Power assertion:** "I have knowledge/authority that you do not"
- **Gate-keeping:** "This space belongs to insiders, and you have not earned entry"
- **Deliberate distancing:** "I do not wish to be on casual terms with you"

**Examples:**
- A doctor responding to a patient's question with dense medical jargon instead of plain language -- signals "you don't have the knowledge to question me"
- An academic using unnecessarily complex language in a conversation where simpler language would communicate more effectively -- signals intellectual status
- A bureaucrat responding to a complaint with institutional language: "Your inquiry has been noted and will be processed in accordance with standard procedures" -- formal language as a barrier to genuine engagement
- A group of friends who all use in-group slang then deliberately switching to standard English when an outsider approaches -- the absence of the usual in-group language signals "you're not one of us"

### 7.6 The "Professional Warmth" Register Shift

**The shift:** A distinctive shift common in service industries and professional settings where formal structure is combined with manufactured warmth.

**What it signals:**
- The speaker is performing a professional role that requires both competence (formal) and approachability (warm)
- The interaction is institutional, not personal, despite surface friendliness

**Examples:**
- "Hi! Welcome to [store]. How can I help you today?" -- the warmth is genuine in tone but formulaic in structure
- "I totally understand your frustration. Let me see what I can do." -- casual empathy language + professional problem-solving frame
- "That's a great question!" -- often a filler phrase that signals the speaker is processing, wrapped in warmth

**For autistic learners:** This register can be confusing because it sounds personal but is institutional. The warmth is real (the speaker may genuinely want to help) but bounded (they will not actually become your friend). It follows scripts and patterns, which makes it learnable: "This is Professional Warmth Register, which I will encounter in shops, customer service, and medical receptions."

### 7.7 The Passive-Aggressive Formality Shift

**The shift:** Using excessively formal, polite language in a context where it is clearly inappropriate -- the politeness itself becomes the weapon.

**What it signals:**
- Anger or frustration that the speaker cannot or chooses not to express directly
- The speaker is "on the record" -- using language that is technically beyond reproach while communicating hostility through register mismatch

**Examples:**
- "Per my last email..." -- formally referencing a previous message; pragmatically saying "I already told you this and I'm annoyed that you didn't read it or are ignoring it"
- "As previously discussed..." -- technically a reference marker; pragmatically a reprimand
- "I trust this will not be an ongoing issue." -- formally expressing confidence; pragmatically issuing a warning
- "Thank you for your feedback." -- technically expressing gratitude; pragmatically dismissing the feedback, particularly when said in a flat tone
- "Kind regards" at the end of an email that is clearly not kind -- the formulaic warmth contrasts with the cold content

**The mechanism:** The politeness is weaponized because: (a) it cannot be challenged ("But I was being polite!"), (b) it creates emotional distance at a moment of conflict, and (c) the mismatch between the emotional content and the formal packaging itself communicates contempt or frustration.

### 7.8 Additional Register-Shift Patterns

**The "disappointed parent" shift:**
- Shift from anger/shouting (informal, hot) to quiet, measured, formal speech: "I'm not angry. I'm disappointed."
- The *drop* in emotional intensity paired with an *increase* in formality signals that the situation has moved beyond casual anger to something more serious

**The "suddenly professional" shift:**
- When a personal conversation in a workplace accidentally strays into unprofessional territory, one speaker might suddenly shift to professional register to signal "we should stop this conversation"
- Example: gossip about a colleague --> "Anyway, do you have those quarterly numbers?"

**The conspiratorial register drop:**
- Shifting *down* in formality to signal shared confidentiality or trust
- Example: A formal meeting pauses and someone leans in and says quietly, "Okay, between us..." -- the informality signals "this is off the record, I'm trusting you"

**The "addressing the room" vs. "addressing you" shift:**
- When a speaker shifts from formal (addressing everyone) to informal (focusing on one person), it signals personal attention and connection
- Example: A teacher giving a lecture (formal) then leaning toward a struggling student and saying quietly, "Hey, you okay? Don't worry, we'll go over this" (informal)

## 8. Additional Frameworks and Concepts

### 8.1 Martin Joos: The Five Clocks (Five Register Levels)

Martin Joos (1962) identified five distinct register levels in English:

| Level | Name | Characteristics | Example Context |
|-------|------|----------------|-----------------|
| 1 | **Frozen** | Fixed, unchanging, often archaic; no words can be changed | Religious texts, Pledge of Allegiance, legal oaths, wedding vows |
| 2 | **Formal** | Personal detachment, complete grammar, explicit pronunciation, careful semantics, clear intent to inform | Academic lectures, official speeches, business presentations |
| 3 | **Consultative** | Still informative, but includes listener participation; supplies background information; complete grammar | Professional discussions, doctor-patient conversations, teacher-student |
| 4 | **Casual** | Marked by ellipsis and slang; speakers on first-name terms; background information not needed | Conversations between friends, informal chats with colleagues |
| 5 | **Intimate** | Almost nonverbal; private language of couples, very close friends, families | Between spouses, parent and small child, closest friends |

**Relevance for autistic learners:** This five-level system provides a concrete framework for categorizing social situations. Rather than a vague sense of "formal" or "informal," learners can ask: "Is this a frozen, formal, consultative, casual, or intimate situation?" and adjust accordingly. Most daily life operates at levels 3-4 (consultative and casual).

### 8.2 Allan Bell's Audience Design

Bell's framework emphasizes that the *audience* is the primary driver of style-shifting, more important than topic, setting, or other factors.

**Key principles:**
1. Style variation is derived from social variation -- the way you shift style mirrors the way different social groups differ from each other
2. Speakers shift *toward* the speech patterns of their audience (convergence) or *away from* them (divergence)
3. The addressee (person being directly spoken to) has the strongest influence on style
4. **Referee design:** Sometimes speakers shift style not toward their present audience but toward an *absent reference group* -- adopting the speech style of a group they identify with or aspire to join, even when no one from that group is present

### 8.3 Brown and Levinson's Politeness Theory (Connection to Register)

Brown and Levinson's theory of politeness connects directly to register shifting through the concept of **face:**

- **Positive face:** The desire to be liked, admired, and related to positively
- **Negative face:** The desire for autonomy, freedom from imposition, and control over one's own territory

Register choice is a fundamental politeness strategy:
- **Positive politeness** (attending to the hearer's positive face) often uses *informal* register: slang, in-group language, humor, casualness that signals "I like you, you're one of us"
- **Negative politeness** (attending to the hearer's negative face) often uses *formal* register: indirectness, deference, hedging, formality that signals "I respect your space and your right to refuse"

**The connection to register shifting:** When you shift to a more formal register, you are often (not always) shifting from positive politeness to negative politeness -- from "we're friends" to "I respect your autonomy/authority." When you shift to a more casual register, you are often shifting from negative to positive politeness -- from "I respect the social distance between us" to "let's be close."

### 8.4 Nikolas Coupland: Style as Identity Construction

Coupland moved beyond Labov's attention-to-speech model and Bell's audience design to argue that style-shifting is an *active, creative process* through which speakers construct social identities.

**Key insight:** Style-shifting is "not merely a passive adjustment to suit different audiences and contexts but an active process of shaping social identities, interactions, and cultural norms." Speakers "proactively use (para-)linguistic variations to construct their social identity, achieve communicative goals, and conform to cultural norms."

**Implication:** Register choice is not just reactive ("the situation requires formal language, so I use formal language"). It is also creative and agentive ("I use formal language to *create* a particular kind of social situation, to *become* a particular kind of social person in this interaction").

## 9. Synthesis: Key Patterns for Autistic Learners

### 9.1 The Register-Selection Algorithm

Based on the research above, here is an explicit algorithm for register selection that converts intuitive social processing into systematic analysis:

**Step 1: Assess the variables**
- Who am I talking to? (audience analysis: power, social distance, familiarity)
- What am I talking about? (topic: serious/light, professional/personal, sensitive/routine)
- Where am I? (setting: formal institution/casual space/mixed)
- How am I communicating? (channel: face-to-face/phone/email/text/social media)

**Step 2: Select baseline register**
- High power distance + serious topic + formal setting + written channel = **Formal register**
- Low power distance + light topic + casual setting + spoken channel = **Casual register**
- Mixed signals = **Consultative register** (the safe middle ground)

**Step 3: Monitor and adjust**
- Watch for signals that your register is mismatched (confusion, discomfort, laughter in others)
- If someone shifts their register, consider matching (convergence)
- If the situation changes (new person enters, topic shifts), reassess

**Step 4: Use strategic shifts when desired**
- Shift *up* (more formal) to signal seriousness, set boundaries, assert authority
- Shift *down* (more casual) to build rapport, include someone, defuse tension
- Keep in mind that the shift itself carries meaning

### 9.2 Common Autistic Challenges with Register Shifting

1. **Defaulting to one register:** Using formal language in all situations (or casual language in all situations) because shifting feels inauthentic or effortful
2. **Delayed shifting:** Recognizing that a shift is needed but implementing it too slowly, after the social moment has passed
3. **Binary thinking about register:** Seeing only "formal" and "informal" rather than the gradient of levels between them
4. **Missing register-shift cues from others:** Not noticing when someone else has shifted register and what that shift signals
5. **Confusing register shifting with masking:** Believing that any communication adjustment is inherently dishonest
6. **Over-reliance on explicit rules:** Applying register rules rigidly without accounting for the many exceptions and strategic uses

### 9.3 Strengths That Autistic Learners Can Leverage

1. **Pattern recognition:** The rules of register, while complex, ARE patterns -- and many autistic people excel at detecting and applying patterns once they are made explicit
2. **Systematic thinking:** The SPEAKING model, Halliday's field/tenor/mode, and Joos's five clocks all provide systematic frameworks that align well with analytical thinking styles
3. **Explicit learning:** While neurotypical speakers learn register rules through unconscious socialization, explicit instruction can be equally effective -- and sometimes results in more *precise* register control
4. **Honesty about effort:** Acknowledging that register shifting takes effort (rather than pretending it's effortless) can actually lead to more authentic social interactions
5. **Meta-awareness:** Being consciously aware of register as a system can provide insights that most neurotypical speakers never articulate
