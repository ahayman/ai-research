## Claim-by-Claim Verification

### Claim 1: English is an SVO language where word order became rigid due to loss of case inflections
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- **Evidence**: This is one of the most well-established narratives in English historical linguistics. Multiple sources confirm that Old English had relatively flexible word order because case endings marked grammatical roles. As inflectional endings eroded during the Middle English period (accelerated by Viking and Norman contact), word order became the primary signal of grammatical relationships, and SVO was established as canonical by approximately 1200-1300. The functional relationship between case loss and word-order rigidification is supported by typological research showing that absence of morphological marking correlates with fixed word order across languages.
- **Sources**: [Diva Portal - Decay of Case System](https://www.diva-portal.org/smash/get/diva2:1028178/FULLTEXT01.pdf), [Historical Linguist Channel](https://thehistoricallinguistchannel.com/the-history-of-the-english-language-middle-english-syntax/), [De Gruyter](https://www.degruyterbrill.com/document/doi/10.1515/ling-2022-0036/html?lang=en)

### Claim 2: OSASCOMP adjective order (Opinion-Size-Age-Shape-Color-Origin-Material-Purpose)
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- **Evidence**: The OSASCOMP mnemonic and the fixed adjective order it describes are widely confirmed across linguistic and ESL sources. The order is consistently reported as Opinion > Size > Age > Shape > Color > Origin > Material > Purpose, with some sources adding "Quantity/Number" before Opinion (Q+OSASCOMP). The gradient from subjective to objective is confirmed. The research document's example ("a lovely little old rectangular green French silver whittling knife") matches the pattern exactly. Sources confirm that native speakers follow this order unconsciously and that violations produce unnatural-sounding results, though the order is described as a strong preference rather than an exceptionless rule.
- **Sources**: [English Study Online](https://englishstudyonline.org/order-of-adjectives/), [Woodward English](https://www.woodwardenglish.com/lesson/adjectives-word-order/), [Englishan](https://englishan.com/order-of-adjectives/), [Break Into English](https://breakintoenglish.com/grammar-content/english-adjective-order-complete/)

### Claim 3: Do-support is typologically unique to English / "virtually unique among well-known languages"
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- **Evidence**: Multiple sources confirm that do-support (the insertion of auxiliary "do" for negation and questions when no other auxiliary is present) is a unique characteristic of English among the world's major languages. Wikipedia states it is "found uniquely in English." While other languages have do-periphrasis (periphrastic verb constructions involving "do"-type verbs), English-type do-support — where "do" is obligatorily inserted as a dummy tense carrier specifically for negation and question formation — is unparalleled. Related Germanic languages (German, Dutch) use verb-second movement instead; Romance languages use direct verb inversion or particles.
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia - Do-support](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Do-support), [KUL](https://www.kul.pl/files/165/history%20of%20english/18Do-periphrasis.pdf), [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/255582673_Grammaticalization_paths_of_periphrastic_'do'-_constructions)

### Claim 4: Do-support developed in Middle English and became obligatory in the 18th century
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- **Evidence**: Independent sources confirm the historical timeline. Periphrastic "do" first appeared in the 13th century. Shakespeare (1564-1616) uses both constructions (with and without do-support). The 1611 King James Bible never uses do-support, reflecting archaic style. By approximately 1700, do-support became standard, and it became fully obligatory during the 18th-19th centuries. The research document's claim that "Do-support developed during the Middle English period (14th-16th centuries) and became obligatory only in the 18th century" aligns well with the evidence.
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia - Early Modern English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Modern_English), [The History of English](https://www.thehistoryofenglish.com/early-modern-english), [KUL](https://www.kul.pl/files/165/history%20of%20english/18Do-periphrasis.pdf)

### Claim 5: English is head-initial with the exception that adjectives precede nouns
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- **Evidence**: English is consistently classified as a head-initial language: prepositions precede their objects, verbs precede their complements, and auxiliaries precede main verbs. The notable exception of pre-nominal adjectives (a head-final characteristic retained from Germanic) is well-documented. Sources confirm that attributive adjectives are typically analyzed as adjuncts rather than complements, which is why their pre-nominal position does not disqualify English from head-initial classification.
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia - Head-directionality parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head-directionality_parameter), [Duolingo Blog](https://blog.duolingo.com/verb-order-headedness/)

### Claim 6: Subject-auxiliary inversion for questions, negative inversion, conditional inversion
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- **Evidence**: All inversion patterns described in the research are standard syntactic analysis. Yes/no questions (Is she leaving?), wh-questions with inversion (When is she leaving?), negative inversion (Never have I seen...), conditional inversion (Had I known...), and tag questions (She's leaving, isn't she?) are all well-documented constructions. The rule that the FIRST auxiliary moves to pre-subject position, and that do-support supplies an auxiliary when none is present, is confirmed.
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia - Subject-auxiliary inversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subject%E2%80%93auxiliary_inversion), [Wikipedia - Do-support](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Do-support)

### Claim 7: Wh-movement with long-distance dependencies and island constraints
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- **Evidence**: The description of wh-movement is accurate and reflects standard generative syntax. The examples of long-distance movement ("What do you think she said ___?") and island constraints (complex NP islands, adjunct islands) are well-established in syntactic theory. The concept that the wh-word moves from its "deep" position to clause-initial position, leaving a gap (trace) that is interpreted semantically, is foundational to transformational grammar.
- **Sources**: [MIT Linguistics](http://web.mit.edu/norvin/www/24.902/phrasestructure.html), [Fiveable](https://fiveable.me/introduction-linguistics/unit-5), [Wikipedia - Subject-auxiliary inversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subject%E2%80%93auxiliary_inversion)

### Claim 8: Restrictive relative clauses use "that"; non-restrictive use "which" (in formal writing)
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- **Evidence**: The that/which distinction for restrictive vs. non-restrictive relative clauses is confirmed across multiple style guides and grammar references. The rule is: "that" introduces restrictive (defining) clauses; "which" introduces non-restrictive (non-defining) clauses with commas. This is especially enforced in American English formal/academic writing. The research correctly notes that this distinction is "often blurred" in spoken English and informal writing. The additional constraints (non-restrictive clauses cannot use "that" or zero relative; restrictive clauses can use "that," "which," "who," "whom," or zero) are all confirmed.
- **Sources**: [Grammarly](https://www.grammarly.com/blog/grammar/which-vs-that/), [Grammarly - Restrictive and Nonrestrictive](https://www.grammarly.com/blog/commonly-confused-words/using-that-and-which-is-all-about-restrictive-and-non-restrictive-clauses/), [Butte College](https://www.butte.edu/departments/cas/tipsheets/grammar/relative_pronouns.html), [MLA Style Center](https://style.mla.org/on-witches-and-whiches/)

### Claim 9: Passive construction types (be-passive, get-passive, middle voice)
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- **Evidence**: The three passive-related constructions described are all standard: be-passive ("The cake was eaten"), get-passive ("The window got broken" — more informal, often implying adverse effect), and middle voice ("This bread cuts easily" — syntactically active but semantically passive, requiring a manner adverb). The characterization of the get-passive as more informal and often implying subject involvement/adverse effect is confirmed.
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia - English passive voice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_passive_voice), [Oreate AI Blog](https://www.oreateai.com/blog/the-middle-voice-in-english-grammar)

### Claim 10: Preposition stranding is a natural feature of English; the "rule" against it is a prescriptive myth based on Latin grammar
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- **Evidence**: This is well-documented in linguistic literature. Preposition stranding ("Who did you give it to?") is a natural product of wh-movement in English. The prescriptive "rule" against sentence-final prepositions was likely first articulated by poet John Dryden in 1672 and was based on Latin grammar, where preposition stranding is impossible. Linguists consistently characterize this as a prescriptive myth rather than a genuine grammatical constraint. The pied-piping alternative ("To whom did you give it?") is formal/literary.
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia - Preposition stranding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preposition_stranding), [Language Log (UPenn)](http://itre.cis.upenn.edu/~myl/languagelog/archives/000743.html), [Historical Linguist Channel](https://thehistoricallinguistchannel.com/a-preposition-is-not-a-good-word-to-end-a-sentence-with/)

### Claim 11: Auxiliary stacking order is Modal + Perfect + Progressive + Passive (rigid order)
- **Rating: CONFIRMED (with minor nuance)**
- **Evidence**: The rigid ordering of auxiliaries is confirmed: Modal → have (perfect) → be (progressive) → be (passive). The example "She might have been being observed" is valid and correctly illustrates all four auxiliary positions. The claim that this order is rigid and cannot be rearranged is confirmed.
- **Nuance**: The research document states the order as "Modal + Perfect + Progressive + Passive" and gives the example "might have been being observed." This is correct. However, it is worth noting that some sources describe the order as Modal → Perfect → Passive → Progressive in certain interpretations, though the standard analysis (and the one the document uses) is correct: the progressive "being" is closest to the main verb in the full passive-progressive construction.
- **Sources**: [Linguistics Girl](https://linguisticsgirl.com/english-auxiliary-verbs/), [ELT Concourse](https://www.eltconcourse.com/training/inservice/modality/modality_and_aspect.html), [Wikipedia - Perfect progressive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perfect_progressive)

### Claim 12: The determiner system as a three-step decision tree (countability → number → definiteness)
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- **Evidence**: The description of the determiner system as governed by countability, number, and definiteness is standard in English grammar pedagogy and linguistic analysis. The decision tree described (Is it countable? → Is it singular? → Is it definite? → Select determiner) accurately captures the logic behind article selection. The characterization of the article system as one of the hardest aspects for speakers of article-less languages is well-supported by SLA research.
- **Sources**: [Cambridge Dictionary Grammar](https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/grammar/british-grammar/word-order-and-focus_2), [Fiveable](https://fiveable.me/introduction-linguistics/unit-5)

### Claim 13: Complementation patterns (intransitive, monotransitive, ditransitive, etc.)
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- **Evidence**: The verb complementation patterns described are standard in English syntax. The nine patterns listed (intransitive, monotransitive, ditransitive, complex transitive, copular, prepositional, clausal, infinitival, gerundial) cover the major complementation types. The ditransitive alternation ("gave him the book" vs. "gave the book to him") and its semantic distinction (double object implies successful transfer) are well-documented.
- **Sources**: [MIT Linguistics](http://web.mit.edu/norvin/www/24.902/phrasestructure.html), [Fiveable](https://fiveable.me/introduction-linguistics/unit-5), [Wikipedia - The Cambridge Grammar of the English Language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Cambridge_Grammar_of_the_English_Language)

### Claim 14: Cleft sentences, pseudo-clefts, fronting, existential "there," and extraposition
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- **Evidence**: All focus constructions described are standard syntactic analysis. It-clefts ("It was John who..."), wh-clefts/pseudo-clefts ("What I need is..."), topicalization/fronting ("This book, I really enjoyed"), existential "there" ("There is a cat on the mat"), and extraposition ("It is important that...") are all well-documented constructions that rearrange information structure without changing propositional content.
- **Sources**: [Wikipedia - Cleft sentence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cleft_sentence), [Cambridge Grammar](https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/grammar/british-grammar/word-order-and-focus_2)

### Claim 15: The end-weight principle — English prefers heavy constituents at sentence end
- **Rating: CONFIRMED**
- **Evidence**: The end-weight principle (also known as "weight" or the "heavy NP shift") is a well-documented tendency in English syntax. Heavy or complex phrases tend to be shifted to the end of the sentence for processing ease. This principle interacts with extraposition and heavy NP shift, both of which are correctly described in the research.
- **Sources**: [Cambridge Grammar](https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/grammar/british-grammar/word-order-and-focus_2)

## Issues Found

### No Major Issues Found

The syntax document is remarkably accurate across all its claims. Every major structural claim, historical narrative, and specific example checked out against independent sources.

### Very Minor Issues

1. **Comparison with German**: The document states German has "SVO in main clauses, SOV in subordinate clauses (verb-second language)." This is essentially correct but slightly simplified. German is more precisely described as a verb-second (V2) language in main clauses (the finite verb is always in second position, regardless of what fills first position, which is not always the subject) and verb-final (SOV) in subordinate clauses. The characterization as "SVO in main clauses" is a common simplification that works for the purpose of comparison with English.

2. **"Likes she tea?" described as "the older pattern"**: The document states that *"Likes she tea?" was the older pattern before do-support. This is accurate for the Middle English period, when verb-to-C movement (the main verb moving to clause-initial position for questions) was still productive. The framing is correct.

3. **Raising vs. control distinction**: The examples "She seems to be happy" (raising) vs. "She wants to be happy" (control) are correctly identified. This is a nuanced syntactic distinction that the document handles accurately.
