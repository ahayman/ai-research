## Category 15: Prosodic Pragmatics — Contrastive Stress

### 1. Coverage Completeness

**Core rule**: Present and clear (Section 1). "Whatever word you stress is the word you are contrasting with alternatives."

**Question-answer test**: Section 1.1 — excellent pedagogical tool. "What question is this sentence answering?" decodes stress patterns.

**Seven-meanings sentence**: Complete (Section 2). "I never said she stole my money" with all seven meanings, implicit questions, and alternatives for each. This is the canonical teaching example.

**Stress in questions**: Section 3 covers "Did you do this?" and "Why did you leave?" with different stress patterns and what each presupposes/asks.

**Stress and hedges**: Section 4 addresses the sarcasm flip (your original observation, now formalized). Stressing minimizers (slightly, just) in heavily hedged statements activates stronger alternatives → sarcasm.

**Stress and negation**: Section 5 — narrow vs. broad scope of negation depending on stress placement. "I didn't SAY you were stupid" vs. "I DIDN'T say you were stupid."

**Focus-sensitive particles**: Section 6 covers "only," "even," "just," "also/too." Shows how these words associate with whatever is stressed, changing meaning.

**Default stress rule**: Section 7 — Nuclear Stress Rule. Stress on last content word is unmarked; any other position is meaningful.

**Missing subtopics**:
- **Double-focus constructions**: Sentences with multiple stressed words and how they interact.
- **Deaccenting**: What happens when a word is de-emphasized (stressed word becomes unstressed).
- **Verum focus**: Stress on the verb specifically to emphasize the truth of the predicate ("He DID call you").
- **Focus projection**: How focus on one constituent can project to larger constituents.
- **Cleft constructions and focus**: "It WAS John who broke it" — interaction with clefts.
- **Information structure beyond focus**: Theme, rheme, given/new.

**Flags**: Note-for-website for double-focus, deaccenting, verum focus. These are in Rooth's theory but not in main document.

### 2. Depth Consistency

**Main document**: 296 lines. Focused and practical. Sections 1–8 are all core material. Section 9 (Practical Toolkit) and Section 10 (Quick Reference) are highly pedagogical.

**Research-theory**: 639 lines — Rooth's (1992) Alternative Semantics in detail, formal theory of focus, relationship between stress/focus/information structure, Nuclear Stress Rule, intonation and pragmatics.

**Research-examples**: 836 lines (!) — Worked examples on steroids. Seven-meanings sentence, stress in questions (6 sets), stress in responses, stress and hedges (6 sets), stress and negation, focus-sensitive particles, everyday contexts, quick-reference map.

**Research-autism**: 41,787 bytes — Su et al. (2025) processing speed, Jiang & Wang (2025) "unaffected by prosody," pitch paradox, grammatical vs. pragmatic prosody, teaching evidence.

**Observation**: Exceptional depth in research-examples. The 836 lines is more comprehensive than most pragmatics textbooks' chapters on focus. This is a **strength**.

### 3. Autism Coverage Specifics

**Key studies**:
- **Su et al. (2025)**: Autistic children CAN discriminate contrastive pitch accent and CAN interpret pragmatic function — but significantly slower. Real-time conversation impact not addressed.
- **Jiang & Wang (2025)**: For scalar quantifiers, autistic children's comprehension was unaffected by prosodic focus. They processed as though no word was stressed.
- **Chien et al. (2022)**: Hyper-sensitivity to simple pitch changes, hypo-sensitivity to prosodic MEANING. Can hear the difference but don't assign it pragmatic significance.

**Grammatical vs. pragmatic prosody**: Section 8.1 notes this distinction. Grammatical prosody (question intonation, lexical stress) is relatively preserved; pragmatic prosody (contrastive focus, attitude signaling) is the challenge.

**Why this is learnable**: Section 8.2 is excellent. Contrastive stress is rule-based (focus activates alternatives), the Question-Answer Test works reliably, it's decomposable, explicit instruction works, and the seven-meanings sentence is a perfect teaching tool.

**Formula (Section 8.3)**: "Focus = Variable; Everything Else = Constant." This is a learnable heuristic.

**Age range**: Research on autistic children (Su et al., Jiang & Wang, Chien et al.).

**Flag**: Strong coverage. The processing speed finding is key — not inability, but speed. Explicit knowledge can compensate (Section 8.2).

### 4. Cross-Category Connections

**Category 14 (Nonverbal)**: Brief reference at end of main document pointing to Cat 14 for broader prosodic context. Could be tighter. The modularity is explained in Cat 14's note about contrastive stress being handled separately in Cat 15.

**Presupposition (Cat 9)**: Stress placement affects presupposition scope (e.g., "**I** never said..." presupposes someone said it, not me).

**Implicature (Cat 2)**: Stress can trigger scalar implicatures (e.g., "I **might** come" with stress suggests more uncertainty than unmarked "I might come").

**Questions (Cat 17)**: Section 3 covers stress in questions in detail — could reference Cat 17 if it exists.

**Assessment**: Modularity with Cat 14 is clear but could be more prominent. Cross-references to presupposition and implicature could be more explicit.

### 5. Cultural / Variety Scope

**English default**: All examples in English. English Nuclear Stress Rule may not apply to other languages (e.g., Italian stress is lexical, not intonational; Mandarin uses particles for focus).

**No mention of cross-linguistic focus systems**: Other languages mark focus differently.

**Flag**: Note-for-website. The rule-based system is language-specific. Learners of other languages need different rules.

### 6. Age-Range Balance

**Child/adolescent**: All research cited is on children.

**Adult**: Limited. Theory papers assume adult speakers.

**Older adults**: Not mentioned.

**Reasonable** for the hub, but adult conversation data would strengthen claims about real-time processing challenges.
