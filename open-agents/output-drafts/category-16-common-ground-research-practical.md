## 1. THE GROUNDING PROCESS IN REAL CONVERSATIONS

### 1.1 What Grounding Is

Grounding is the process by which speakers and listeners collaborate, turn by turn, to establish mutual understanding (Clark & Brennan, 1991). It is not something the speaker does alone. Communication is a **joint activity**: the speaker presents information, the listener provides evidence that they understood (or didn't), and the speaker adjusts accordingly. This cycle repeats continuously throughout a conversation.

The key insight: **a message is not "communicated" just because someone said it.** It is communicated only when both parties have reached mutual belief that the listener understood what the speaker meant -- to a standard sufficient for current purposes. Clark and Brennan call this the **grounding criterion**.

This "sufficient for current purposes" part matters enormously. If someone asks you to pass the salt, a nod and reaching for it is enough evidence of understanding. If a surgeon is confirming which limb to operate on, the grounding criterion is much higher -- they need explicit verbal confirmation, written records, and physical markings.

### 1.2 The Two Phases: Presentation and Acceptance

Every conversational contribution unfolds in two phases (Clark & Schaefer, 1989):

**Phase 1 -- Presentation:** The speaker produces an utterance intended to convey a particular meaning. They design it for their specific listener (this is called **recipient design**), adjusting vocabulary, detail level, and assumed knowledge based on what they believe the listener knows.

**Phase 2 -- Acceptance:** The listener provides evidence that they have (or have not) understood. The speaker monitors for this evidence. If the evidence is positive, both parties treat the contribution as grounded, and the conversation moves forward. If the evidence is negative or absent, they enter a **repair** sequence.

The principle of **least collaborative effort** governs both phases: participants try to minimize the total work spent achieving mutual understanding. This is why people use shortcuts, abbreviations, and pronouns -- they reduce effort when understanding is going smoothly.

### 1.3 Evidence of Understanding: What Listeners Do to Show They Understood

Clark and Brennan identified a hierarchy of positive evidence, from weakest to strongest:

#### Level 1: Continued Attention (Weakest)
- The listener keeps looking at the speaker
- They don't walk away, check their phone, or turn away
- In a phone call: they don't hang up or go silent for too long
- **What it signals:** "I'm still here and I'm receiving your words"
- **What it does NOT signal:** "I understood what you meant"
- This is the default -- it's the *absence* of a problem signal, not a *presence* of understanding

#### Level 2: Backchannels / Acknowledgment Tokens
These are the small sounds and words listeners produce *without* taking a full conversational turn:

| Signal | What it communicates | Typical usage |
|--------|---------------------|---------------|
| "Mm-hmm" | "I'm following, continue" | During the other person's turn; doesn't claim the floor |
| "Yeah" | "I'm tracking what you're saying" | Slightly more active than mm-hmm; can also mean agreement |
| "Right" | "That makes sense to me" | Often signals logical agreement with what was just said |
| "OK" | "I've registered that information" | More about receipt than agreement |
| "Uh-huh" | "Go on" | Pure continuer; minimal commitment to understanding |
| "I see" | "I understand the point" | Slightly stronger claim of understanding |
| "Sure" | "That's clear/acceptable" | Can mean agreement OR acknowledgment depending on tone |
| Head nod | "I'm following" (in person) | Often accompanies verbal backchannels |

**Critical point for autistic learners:** These backchannels are *expected* by neurotypical speakers. If a listener provides NO backchannels, many speakers will interpret the silence as a problem -- they may stop and ask "Does that make sense?" or feel uneasy that they're not being understood. Producing occasional backchannels ("mm-hmm," "right," "yeah") at natural pause points signals that the conversation can continue.

Research on backchannels shows they serve as "continuous feedback signals, giving speakers feedback on how their utterance is received, without needing to explicitly check by asking" (Distribution and Timing of Verbal Backchannels in Conversational Speech, 2025). They are a core mechanism for real-time grounding.

#### Level 3: Relevant Next Turn
When the listener says something that is **appropriate as a response** to what the speaker just said, this is stronger evidence of understanding than backchannels.

Examples:
- Speaker: "The meeting got moved to Thursday." → Listener: "What time on Thursday?" (relevant follow-up question = understood the core message)
- Speaker: "I'm thinking of getting a dog." → Listener: "What breed are you considering?" (builds on what was said = understood and is contributing)
- Speaker: "Can you send me the file?" → Listener: "Sure, I'll email it after lunch." (agrees to act = understood the request)

Questions and answers form what linguists call **adjacency pairs** -- once one part appears, the other is expected. If A asks a question and B gives an appropriate answer, that answer is evidence B understood the question. If B answers a *different* question, or gives a non-sequitur, that's evidence of a problem.

#### Level 4: Paraphrase / Reformulation
When the listener restates what they think the speaker said in their own words. This is much stronger evidence because it reveals *what* they understood, not just *that* they understood.

Examples:
- Speaker: "The deadline's been pushed back because the client needs more time to review the specs." → Listener: "So we've got extra time because they're still looking at the requirements?" (paraphrase confirms understanding)
- Speaker: "I feel like people don't listen to me in meetings." → Listener: "You feel like your ideas get overlooked?" (emotional paraphrase)

This is the strategy behind the common advice to paraphrase what someone said: "So what you're saying is..." or "Let me make sure I understand..."

#### Level 5: Demonstration / Acting on It (Strongest)
The ultimate evidence of understanding is doing the right thing based on what was said.

Examples:
- Speaker: "Can you grab me a coffee? Black, no sugar." → Listener brings back a black coffee = perfect evidence of understanding
- Speaker: "Take a left at the light, then the second right." → Listener drives correctly = perfect evidence
- Speaker: "Can you grab me a coffee?" → Listener brings back a tea = evidence of MISunderstanding

### 1.4 Evidence of Non-Understanding: What Listeners Do When They DON'T Understand

#### Explicit Signals (Easiest to Recognize)
These are **other-initiated repair** sequences -- the listener explicitly flags a problem:

| Signal | What it targets | Example context |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| "What?" / "Huh?" / "Sorry?" | Hearing problem -- didn't catch the words | Speaker spoke too quietly or there's background noise |
| "What do you mean?" | Meaning problem -- heard the words but doesn't understand | "We need to leverage our synergies" → "What do you mean?" |
| "What do you mean by [X]?" | Specific word/phrase problem | "The repo needs rebasing" → "What do you mean by rebasing?" |
| "Wait, which [X]?" | Reference problem -- ambiguous reference | "He said he'd do it" → "Wait, which he?" |
| "Can you say that again?" | Repeat request -- processing failure | Speaker spoke too fast or listener wasn't ready |
| "I don't follow" / "I'm lost" | Global non-understanding | After a complex explanation |
| "Hang on, back up" | Lost the thread | Speaker went too far ahead |

#### Implicit Signals (Harder to Recognize)
| Signal | What it might mean | Why it's ambiguous |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Silence / no response | May not have understood; may be thinking; may not have heard | Could also mean they're processing or distracted |
| Inappropriate response | Evidence the message wasn't received correctly | They may have misheard or misinterpreted |
| Confused facial expression | Visual signal of non-understanding | Furrowed brow, squinting, head tilt |
| Change of subject | May be avoiding admitting non-understanding | Or may just have a different priority |
| Overly vague response | May be bluffing understanding | "Yeah, sure, totally" (without engaging with specifics) |

#### The "Inappropriate Response" Problem
Sometimes the strongest evidence of non-understanding is when the listener responds as if they understood, but their response reveals they didn't:

- Speaker: "We should meet at the place on Fifth Street." → Listener: "OK, I'll see you at the one on First Street." (misheard; needs correction)
- Speaker: "The project is due next Friday." → Listener starts working on a different project (acted on wrong interpretation)
- Speaker: "I was being sarcastic." → Listener: "Oh. I took that literally." (pragmatic non-understanding, now repaired)

### 1.5 How Speakers Monitor for Grounding Evidence and Adjust

Speakers don't just talk and hope for the best. They are continuously monitoring their listener for evidence of understanding. This monitoring happens through:

**Visual monitoring (in person):**
- Watching the listener's face for confusion, comprehension, or interest
- Checking if the listener is looking at the same thing (if discussing something visible)
- Noticing head nods, furrowed brows, or wandering gaze

**Auditory monitoring:**
- Listening for backchannels at expected points
- Noticing silence where a response was expected
- Hearing the listener's tone (uncertain? confident? confused?)

**Response monitoring:**
- Checking if the listener's response is relevant and appropriate
- Noting whether follow-up questions target what was said or something else
- Observing whether the listener acts correctly on instructions

**When evidence is negative or absent, speakers adjust:**
- **Repeat:** Say it again, possibly louder or slower
- **Rephrase:** Say the same thing differently: "What I mean is..." / "In other words..."
- **Elaborate:** Add more detail or context
- **Simplify:** Use simpler words or shorter sentences
- **Check explicitly:** "Does that make sense?" / "Am I being clear?"
- **Offer an example:** "Like, for instance..."
- **Back up:** "OK, let me start over."

This whole process -- present, monitor, adjust -- is the engine that keeps conversations on track. It's a continuous feedback loop, not a one-shot transmission.

## 2. REFERENCE AND COMMON GROUND

### 2.1 How We Choose What to Call Things

When you mention a person, place, or thing in conversation, you have to choose how to refer to it. That choice depends entirely on what you believe your listener already knows.

The fundamental principle: **use the minimum description necessary for the listener to identify what you're talking about.** This follows from the principle of least collaborative effort -- don't make the listener work harder than necessary, but also don't waste your own effort over-describing.

### 2.2 First Mention vs. Subsequent Mention

**First mention** (introducing something new to the conversation):
When something is **not yet in the common ground** -- the listener doesn't know about it yet -- you need to introduce it with enough information for them to create a mental representation.

- "I was talking to **a woman I met at the conference** last week..."
- "There's **this coffee shop on the corner of Main and 5th** that I love."
- "My neighbor -- **the one with the two dogs** -- came over yesterday."
- "Have you heard of **a show called Severance**?"

These use **indefinite reference** ("a woman," "this coffee shop," "a show") because the entity is *new to the listener's awareness.*

**Subsequent mention** (referencing something already established):
Once something is **in the common ground** -- both parties know what you're referring to -- you can use shorter, more efficient expressions.

- "...and **she** said the keynote was terrible." (pronoun)
- "I went back to **the coffee shop** yesterday." (definite article, shorter description)
- "**He** was complaining about the noise again." (pronoun)
- "Have you watched **it** yet?" (pronoun)

These use **definite reference** ("the," "she," "he," "it") because the entity is *already identified in shared awareness.*

Research confirms this pattern: "Directors tend to use an indefinite reference the first time they discuss [something] in order to establish with their partner how to view, categorize, and talk about it. Once such a shared perspective has been grounded, speakers use definite references, thereby marking the referent as co-present, i.e., part of their personal common ground" (Direct and indirect linguistic measures of common ground, 2024).

### 2.3 The Refashioning Phenomenon: Expressions Get Shorter Over Repeated Use

Brennan and Clark (1996) documented what they called **conceptual pacts** -- temporary agreements between conversational partners about how to refer to something. Once a pact is established, the reference gets progressively shorter.

**Example progression:**

| Mention | Expression | Why this length |
|---------|-----------|----------------|
| 1st | "the coffee shop on the corner of Main and 5th, the one with the blue awning" | Listener needs full identification |
| 2nd | "the coffee shop on Main" | Listener can now distinguish it |
| 3rd | "the coffee shop" | It's the only one they've been discussing |
| 4th | "there" | Fully established in common ground |

**Another example:**
| Mention | Expression | Context |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| 1st | "my colleague David from the marketing department" | Listener doesn't know David |
| 2nd | "David from marketing" | Already established who David is |
| 3rd | "David" | Only one David in this conversation |
| 4th | "he" | Obvious from context |

This shortening is **collaborative**: both parties participate. The speaker tries shorter forms; the listener accepts them (by not requesting clarification) or rejects them (by asking "which one?" or looking confused). If the shorter form works, it becomes the new standard for that conversation.

**Key insight:** This is partner-specific. The short form "there" or "David" only works with the person you established the reference with. If a third person joins the conversation, you need to re-establish the reference for them.

### 2.4 When to Use a Name vs. a Pronoun vs. a Description

**Use a name** when:
- Introducing someone for the first time: "My friend Sarah..."
- The listener knows the person but multiple people are in play: "Sarah called" (not "she called" when multiple women have been mentioned)
- Re-establishing reference after a topic change

**Use a pronoun** ("he," "she," "they," "it") when:
- There's only one possible referent: "My sister called. She said..."
- The referent was the most recent topic: "I saw David. He looked tired."
- It's obvious from the context who you mean

**Use a description** when:
- The listener doesn't know the person's name: "the woman at reception"
- The name alone isn't enough: "Sarah -- my college roommate, not my neighbor Sarah"
- You want to highlight a relevant property: "the tall one," "your boss"

### 2.5 The Problem of Ambiguous Reference

Ambiguous reference occurs when a pronoun or short expression could refer to more than one thing in the conversation.

**Classic example:**
- "John told David that **he** needed to leave early." (Who needs to leave -- John or David?)

**How people resolve this in practice:**
1. **Grammatical position bias:** Listeners tend to assume "he" refers to the *subject* of the sentence (John), not the object (David). But this isn't reliable.
2. **Semantic fit:** "John told David that **he** was fired" -- listeners use world knowledge (the person being fired is more likely the one being told).
3. **Context from prior talk:** If the previous sentences were about David's schedule, "he" probably means David.

**When resolution fails, speakers repair:**
- Listener: "Wait, who needed to leave? John or David?"
- Speaker: "Oh sorry -- David needed to leave."

**Prevention strategies speakers use:**
- Repeat the name instead of using a pronoun: "John told David that *David* needed to leave."
- Add clarification: "John told David that he -- John -- needed to leave."
- Restructure: "David needed to leave early, and John told him so."

## 3. CHECKING AND ESTABLISHING COMMON GROUND

### 3.1 Phrases That CHECK Whether Something Is in Common Ground

These phrases ask "Do you already know about X?" before proceeding to talk about it. They are **probes** -- the speaker is testing whether they need to explain or can assume knowledge.

**Direct checks:**
| Phrase | Function | When to use |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| "Do you know [person/place/thing]?" | Tests whether listener has any knowledge of X | Introducing a person, restaurant, concept |
| "Are you familiar with [concept]?" | Tests depth of knowledge (slightly more formal) | Technical topics, specialized knowledge |
| "Have you heard of [X]?" | Tests basic awareness (doesn't assume knowledge) | New products, events, people |
| "You know [X], right?" | Assumes they probably know; checks to be sure | When you expect they do know |
| "I'm not sure if you know this, but..." | Hedged introduction; doesn't assume either way | When you genuinely don't know if they know |
| "Have you seen / read / heard [X]?" | Tests specific experience | Movies, articles, news |

**How listeners respond to common ground checks:**
- "Yeah, I know [X]" → speaker can proceed without explanation
- "I've heard of it but don't know much" → speaker should give a brief overview
- "No, what is it?" → speaker needs to establish the common ground from scratch
- "Sort of" / "A little" → speaker should gauge how much to explain

### 3.2 Phrases That ESTABLISH Common Ground

These phrases **create** shared understanding rather than testing for it.

**Creating shared understanding:**
| Phrase | Function | Example |
|--------|----------|---------|
| "So basically, [explanation]..." | Summarizing to ensure shared understanding | "So basically, the app tracks your sleep patterns." |
| "Here's the thing about [X]..." | Adding important context to common ground | "Here's the thing about that restaurant -- it's cash only." |
| "The way it works is..." | Building technical common ground | "The way it works is you submit the form first, then wait for approval." |
| "Think of it like [analogy]..." | Creating common ground via comparison | "Think of it like a library, but for tools." |
| "What that means is..." | Translating jargon into shared terms | "What that means is the server is down." |

**Invoking EXISTING common ground:**
| Phrase | Function | Example |
|--------|----------|---------|
| "You know how [shared experience]?" | Connecting new info to shared knowledge | "You know how the printer always jams? Well, now the copier does too." |
| "Remember when [shared event]?" | Activating specific shared memory | "Remember when we got lost in Portland? This was worse." |
| "It's like that time [shared event]..." | Using shared history as explanatory frame | "It's like that time the Wi-Fi went down during the presentation." |
| "Like [reference both know]..." | Using shared cultural knowledge | "Like that episode of The Office where..." |

### 3.3 Phrases That CHECK Understanding

After explaining something, speakers often want to confirm the listener actually understood. These checking phrases appear at the boundaries of explanations:

**Speaker-initiated comprehension checks:**
| Phrase | Tone / Register | Notes |
|--------|----------------|-------|
| "Does that make sense?" | Neutral, common | Very widely used; risk: listeners often say "yes" even when confused |
| "Am I being clear?" | Slightly self-deprecating | Puts the responsibility on the speaker, not the listener |
| "Do you follow?" | Neutral to slightly formal | Common in sequential explanations |
| "Are you with me?" | Informal, friendly | Often used mid-explanation |
| "Make sense so far?" | Casual | Used at checkpoints during long explanations |
| "Sorry, am I going into too much detail?" | Apologetic, considerate | Shows awareness of potential information overload |
| "Is this making sense or am I losing you?" | Honest, direct | Gives explicit permission to say "no" |
| "Any questions?" | Formal | Common in presentations; rarely elicits honest confusion |

**The problem with "Does that make sense?":**
Research suggests that yes/no questions about understanding rarely elicit honest responses. People tend to say "yes" because admitting non-understanding feels socially costly. Better alternatives invite specific engagement:
- "What questions do you have?" (assumes they have questions)
- "What's your understanding of what I just said?" (asks for demonstration)
- "Can you walk me through how you'd do it?" (asks for demonstration via action)

**Listener-initiated understanding signals:**
| Phrase | What it does |
|--------|-------------|
| "So what you're saying is..." | Paraphrase check -- strongest form |
| "Let me make sure I understand..." | Signals careful processing |
| "Just to be clear..." | Flagging possible misunderstanding |
| "Wait, so does that mean...?" | Testing an inference |
| "OK so basically..." | Offering a summary for confirmation |
| "I want to make sure I've got this right." | Explicit framing of verification |

## 4. COMMON GROUND IN DIFFERENT RELATIONSHIP TYPES

### 4.1 The Architecture of Common Ground

Herbert Clark distinguished two types of common ground:

**Communal common ground:** Knowledge shared by virtue of belonging to the same community -- cultural knowledge, language, widely known facts, shared norms. You share communal common ground with anyone from the same culture, profession, age group, or community.

**Personal common ground:** Knowledge shared through direct interaction -- things you've experienced together, previous conversations, shared private jokes, knowledge of each other's lives. You share personal common ground only with people you've interacted with.

These two types combine differently depending on your relationship with the listener.

### 4.2 With STRANGERS

**What you share:** Almost exclusively communal common ground -- you can assume they speak the same language, know basic cultural facts, understand common social norms, and have general world knowledge.

**What you don't share:** No personal common ground. No shared history. No knowledge of what they know or care about.

**Communication consequences:**
- **Must explain more, assume less.** You can't say "you know that thing" because they probably don't.
- **Use generic common ground** -- what an average member of the relevant community would know.
- **More formal register.** Fewer shortcuts, more complete sentences, more explicit.
- **Reference requires full introduction.** "There's a restaurant I like called Mario's, on the corner of 3rd and Main..." (not "Mario's" alone).

**Practical example -- giving directions:**
- To a stranger/tourist: "Go down this street for about three blocks until you see a large church on the right. Turn right just after the church. The shop is about halfway down the next block, on the left side."
- To a local: "It's on Wilson, just past St. Michael's."

### 4.3 With ACQUAINTANCES

**What you share:** Some communal common ground (workplace, neighborhood, mutual friends) plus limited personal common ground (a few shared interactions, things they've told you).

**What you don't share:** Deep personal knowledge, detailed history, private references.

**Communication consequences:**
- **The tricky middle ground.** You know some things about them but not everything. You must constantly gauge what they know vs. what you're assuming.
- **Can reference shared context** but not assume deep knowledge: "You know how the parking lot floods when it rains?" is fine; "Remember that thing we talked about last year?" may be too vague.
- **Risk of both under- and over-estimating.** You might explain something they already know (condescending) or skip explaining something they don't know (confusing).

**Practical example -- mentioning a mutual friend:**
- "Do you know Chris from the accounting team?" (checks shared acquaintance)
- vs. "Chris said..." (assumes they know who Chris is, which they might not)

### 4.4 With CLOSE FRIENDS

**What you share:** Extensive personal common ground -- shared history, shared experiences, in-jokes, knowledge of each other's lives, families, preferences, and pet peeves. Plus all relevant communal common ground.

**What you can do:**
- Use abbreviated reference: "remember that thing?" (and they actually might)
- Use in-jokes and private language: references that only make sense to you two
- Use emotional shorthand: "I'm having a Tuesday" (meaning something specific between you)
- Skip background: "So she called AGAIN" (they know who "she" is and why it's annoying)

**The danger -- the closeness-communication bias:**

Research by Savitsky, Keysar, Epley, Carter, and Swanson (2011) discovered a counterintuitive finding: **people communicate LESS carefully with close friends than with strangers, and this leads to more miscommunication, not less.**

Key findings from their experiments:
- Participants were **more likely to interpret instructions egocentrically** (from their own perspective, ignoring the other's perspective) when the instructions came from a friend vs. a stranger
- Participants **overestimated how well they communicated** with friends compared to strangers
- "People engage in active monitoring of strangers' divergent perspectives because they know they must, but they 'let down their guard' and rely more on their own perspective when they communicate with a friend."

The researchers concluded: "Getting close to someone appears to create the **illusion of understanding** more than actual understanding."

**Why this happens:** With strangers, you *know* you don't share much common ground, so you work harder to be explicit. With friends, you *assume* you share common ground and stop checking -- but even close friends have separate lives, separate experiences, and separate interpretations.

**Practical lesson:** Even with close friends, it's worth checking: "Wait, did I tell you about X?" or "Do you know what happened with Y?" rather than assuming they already know.

### 4.5 With PROFESSIONAL COLLEAGUES

**What you share:** Domain-specific communal common ground (professional jargon, shared workflows, company knowledge) plus whatever personal common ground you've built through working together.

**The jargon calibration problem:**
Every profession develops specialized vocabulary. Within the team, jargon is efficient -- "Can you rebase the PR?" is faster and more precise than explaining git operations from scratch. But jargon creates an invisible barrier for anyone who doesn't share that technical common ground.

Research shows that **75% of employees say jargon affects their understanding of communications** and reduces overall clarity in their workplaces. This is a massive common ground failure hiding in plain sight.

**New team members** face a particular challenge:
- They don't have the team's **linguistic common ground** -- the shorthand, acronyms, nicknames for systems and processes
- They don't have the team's **experiential common ground** -- the history of past decisions, failed projects, and institutional knowledge
- They may have the *technical* common ground (same profession) but lack the *local* common ground (how THIS team does things)

**Calibration strategies for professional settings:**
- "How familiar are you with [X]?" (before launching into explanation)
- "I'll explain this as if you're new to it -- stop me if you already know this" (gives permission to skip ahead)
- "We call it [jargon] -- basically it means [plain language]" (defines terms inline)
- "For those who weren't in the meeting..." (explicitly addresses the CG gap in group emails)

## 5. THE CURSE OF KNOWLEDGE

### 5.1 What It Is

The **curse of knowledge** is a cognitive bias: once you know something, it is extremely difficult to imagine what it's like NOT to know it. Your knowledge feels so natural, so obvious, so self-evident that you unconsciously project it onto others.

The term was coined in economics (Camerer, Loewenstein, & Weber, 1989) and has been extensively studied in communication. MIT Sloan describes it: "When we see a pattern or recognize something or know something, we forget what it was like before we knew that thing... This is why experts struggle to communicate about their own domain to nonexperts."

### 5.2 How It Affects Communication

**In explanation and teaching:**
- Experts skip foundational steps they consider "obvious" -- but they're only obvious if you already know them
- Explanations are "logically complete but pedagogically useless" -- all the information is there, but it's not organized for someone who doesn't already understand the framework
- Jargon feels like plain language to the expert

**In everyday conversation:**
- "You know, the thing" -- spoken as if the listener should know, when they don't
- Giving directions using landmarks the listener can't recognize
- Telling a story and leaving out context that was obvious to you (because you were there)
- "As I mentioned in my email..." -- assuming someone read and retained your email

**In professional settings:**
- IT explaining a problem: "The DNS isn't resolving" (meaningless to a non-technical person)
- A doctor saying "the results were unremarkable" (the patient may not know "unremarkable" means "normal/good")
- A manager saying "we need to pivot" without explaining what that means practically

### 5.3 Why It's Hard to Fix

Research shows that the curse of knowledge is **remarkably resistant to correction:**
- Telling people about the bias doesn't reduce it
- Asking people to think about the other person's perspective doesn't reliably help
- Financial incentives don't reduce the bias
- Even people who study the bias still fall victim to it

The classic demonstration is the "tapper-listener" experiment (Newton, 1990): people tapped out well-known songs and estimated that listeners would identify 50% of them. In reality, listeners identified only 2.5%. The tappers *could hear the song in their heads* while tapping, so they couldn't imagine what the tapping sounded like without that mental soundtrack.

### 5.4 Practical Strategies for Overcoming the Curse

Since awareness alone doesn't fix it, you need *behavioral* strategies:

**Before explaining:**
1. **Ask what they already know:** "How familiar are you with X?" / "What do you already know about this?"
2. **Calibrate to their level:** "Are you looking for the quick version or the detailed explanation?"
3. **State your assumption:** "I'm going to assume you know the basics of X -- correct me if I'm wrong."

**During explanation:**
4. **Watch for confusion signals:** Furrowed brow, silence where response was expected, "uh-huh" without engagement, eyes glazing over
5. **Check in at intervals:** "Am I making sense so far?" / "Should I slow down?" / "Want more detail on that part?"
6. **Start with the big picture before details:** "The basic idea is [one sentence]. Now let me break that down..."
7. **Use analogies:** "Think of it like..." connects new information to something already in the listener's common ground
8. **Define terms inline:** "We use a CMS -- a content management system -- to update the website"

**After explaining:**
9. **Ask for paraphrase:** "Can you tell me what you took away from that?" (more reliable than "Did that make sense?")
10. **Invite questions without judgment:** "What questions do you have?" (not "Any questions?" which implies there shouldn't be any)

## 6. COMMON GROUND IN DIGITAL COMMUNICATION

### 6.1 What Changes When You Go Digital

Clark and Brennan (1991) identified key properties of face-to-face communication that support grounding. Different digital media remove or reduce these properties:

| Property | Face-to-face | Phone call | Video call | Text/chat | Email |
|----------|-------------|------------|------------|-----------|-------|
| **Co-presence** (share physical space) | Yes | No | No | No | No |
| **Visibility** (can see each other) | Yes | No | Yes | No | No |
| **Audibility** (can hear each other) | Yes | Yes | Yes | No | No |
| **Contemporality** (receive in real time) | Yes | Yes | Yes | Mostly | No |
| **Simultaneity** (both send at once) | Yes | Yes | Yes | Partially | No |
| **Sequentiality** (turns take sequence) | Yes | Yes | Yes | Disrupted | No |
| **Reviewability** (can re-examine) | No | No | No | Yes | Yes |
| **Revisability** (can edit before sending) | No | No | No | Yes | Yes |

Each missing property makes grounding harder in specific ways.

### 6.2 Specific Digital Grounding Challenges

**Reduced physical co-presence:**
- You can't point at things ("that one, right there")
- You can't see what the other person is looking at
- You can't rely on shared physical context ("it's cold in here" -- they might be somewhere warm)
- Deictic expressions (this, that, here, there) lose their anchoring

**Temporal displacement (especially email and async messaging):**
- "Today" in an email sent Monday might be read on Wednesday -- which day is "today"?
- "Just now" in a Slack message read hours later is misleading
- "As we discussed" -- the reader may not remember the discussion
- Reactions to shared events can be delayed, making "Did you see what just happened?" nonsensical by the time it's read

**Thread-based common ground (group chats and channels):**
- Not everyone has read every message in the thread
- Someone joining a conversation midway doesn't have the established common ground
- Side conversations may have happened in DMs that others don't know about
- "As I said above" -- the reader may have scrolled past it

**The forwarded message problem:**
- When a message is forwarded, the new recipient lacks the original conversation's common ground
- Inside jokes, abbreviations, and references that made sense in context become opaque
- The forwarded message includes the words but not the shared understanding behind them
- This is why people add "For context: ..." at the top of forwards

### 6.3 Strategies for Establishing Common Ground in Digital Settings

**Context setting:**
- "For those who weren't in the meeting: [summary of what was decided]"
- "Quick background: [relevant context before the actual message]"
- "Looping in [Name] -- here's a summary of where we are: ..."

**Explicit grounding in async communication:**
- "Got it" / "Understood" / "Makes sense" -- explicit acknowledgments replace backchannels
- "Just to confirm: you want me to [restatement of action]?" -- paraphrase for verification
- "Replying to your point about X: ..." -- explicit reference to specific parts of the message

**Handling temporal displacement:**
- Use specific dates instead of relative time: "Let's meet Monday March 15" not "Let's meet next Monday"
- Use time zones explicitly: "3pm Eastern"
- Don't assume the reader is reading in real time

**Managing thread-based CG:**
- Quote the specific message you're responding to
- Tag the specific person you're addressing in group chats
- Summarize key decisions at the end of long threads
- Create a pinned summary for ongoing discussions

## 7. COMMON GROUND FAILURES -- EXAMPLES AND FIXES

### 7.1 Failure Type: Assuming They Know Something They Don't

**What happens:**
Speaker: "So I used DALL-E to generate the images and then cleaned them up in Figma."
Listener: (has no idea what DALL-E or Figma are, but nods along to avoid looking ignorant)
Result: Listener is lost for the rest of the conversation. Speaker thinks they communicated successfully. Both are wrong.

**Why it happens:** The curse of knowledge. DALL-E and Figma are so familiar to the speaker that they feel like "common knowledge." They're not.

**The fix (for the speaker):** Check before assuming.
- "I used a tool called DALL-E -- have you heard of it? It's an AI that generates images from text descriptions."
- Or define inline: "I used DALL-E -- that's an AI image generator -- to create the images."

**The fix (for the listener):** Ask rather than fake it.
- "Sorry, what's DALL-E?"
- "Can you give me a quick overview of what Figma does?"
- "I'm not familiar with those tools -- can you give me some context?"

### 7.2 Failure Type: They Assume YOU Know Something You Don't

**What happens:**
Speaker: "So anyway, after the whole thing with Marcus, I decided to just quit."
Listener: (has no idea who Marcus is or what "the whole thing" was)
Result: Listener is missing critical context but may feel it's too late to ask.

**Why it happens:** The speaker has been living with "the whole thing with Marcus" for days or weeks. It's prominent in their mind, so they assume it's prominent in yours.

**The fix (for the listener):**
- "Wait, I don't think I know about this -- who's Marcus?"
- "Can you back up? What happened?"
- "I'm missing some context -- can you fill me in?"

**The fix (for the speaker):** Signal when you're introducing something new.
- "I don't think I've told you about this, but..." (explicitly marks new information)
- "So there's this guy at work, Marcus, who..." (provides enough context)

### 7.3 Failure Type: You Explain Too Much (They Already Know)

**What happens:**
Speaker: "So a podcast is like a radio show, but you can listen to it whenever you want, and you download episodes through--"
Listener: (who has been listening to podcasts for a decade) "...I know what a podcast is."
Result: The listener feels condescended to. The relationship takes a small hit.

**Why it happens:** Opposite of the curse of knowledge -- the speaker underestimates the listener's knowledge. This can happen with:
- New acquaintances whose expertise you haven't gauged
- People the speaker subconsciously underestimates (younger people, people from different backgrounds, etc.)
- Topics where the speaker is used to being the "expert"

**The fix:**
- "How much do you already know about [X]?" (calibration question)
- "I'm not sure how much you know about this, so stop me if I'm telling you things you already know." (gives permission to interrupt)
- "You probably already know this, but just in case: ..." (acknowledges their likely knowledge while still providing the info)

### 7.4 Failure Type: A Reference Falls Flat

**What happens:**
Speaker: "It's giving 'that scene from Fleabag where she looks at the camera.'"
Listener: (has never seen Fleabag) "...What?"
Result: Awkward silence. The speaker's clever reference lands with a thud. The listener may feel excluded.

This applies to:
- Cultural references (movies, TV shows, memes, books)
- In-jokes that not everyone was present for
- Generational references ("It's like Myspace but for X" -- means nothing to someone who never used Myspace)
- References to events the listener wasn't part of

**The fix (for the speaker):**
- Gauge your audience: Don't assume everyone has seen what you've seen
- Provide context for references: "Oh, it's from a show called Fleabag -- there's this running gag where the main character breaks the fourth wall"
- Self-correct gracefully: "Sorry, that's a reference to [X]. What I mean is..."

**The fix (for the listener):**
- "I haven't seen that -- what does that mean?"
- "I don't get the reference, but I think I get what you're saying."

### 7.5 Failure Type: Pronoun Ambiguity Leads to Misunderstanding

**What happens:**
Speaker: "I told Sarah that Emma wasn't happy with her work."
Listener: (interprets "her" as Sarah's work)
Speaker: (meant Emma wasn't happy with Emma's own work)
Result: Listener thinks Sarah is being criticized. Possible gossip chain based on misunderstanding.

**The fix:**
- Speaker: Use the name instead of the pronoun when it's ambiguous: "I told Sarah that Emma wasn't happy with Emma's own work."
- Listener: "Whose work -- Sarah's or Emma's?"

### 7.6 Failure Type: Grounding Failure Across Digital Channels

**What happens:**
Person A (in a group Slack channel, earlier that day): "Let's cancel the Thursday standup."
Person B (didn't read Slack, shows up to the standup)
Person A: "Didn't you see my message?"
Person B: "I had 200 unread messages."

Result: Person A assumed that posting in Slack constituted grounding. It didn't -- Person B never received the information, so it was never part of their common ground.

**The critical principle:** In digital communication, **sending is not grounding.** A message is only grounded when the recipient has demonstrably received and understood it. An unread Slack message, an unopened email, or a notification that was swiped away does not constitute common ground.

**The fix:**
- For critical information, seek explicit confirmation: "Can you confirm you saw this?"
- Use the right channel for the urgency level (time-sensitive = direct message or phone call, not a group channel)
- Don't assume silence means agreement or acknowledgment in async channels
- If it truly matters, follow up: "Just checking -- did you see my message about Thursday?"

### 7.7 Meta-Pattern: The Fix Is Almost Always "Check"

Across all these failure types, the underlying fix is the same: **check common ground before assuming it.**

- Before referencing something: "Do you know about X?"
- Before explaining something: "How familiar are you with X?"
- After explaining something: "Does that track?" / "What questions do you have?"
- When they reference something unfamiliar: "Can you give me some context on that?"
- When you're uncertain: "Just to make sure we're on the same page..."

The social cost of checking is almost always lower than the cost of a misunderstanding. A two-second "Do you know X?" prevents a five-minute confusion spiral.

## 8. SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS FOR AUTISTIC COMMUNICATORS

### 8.1 Why Common Ground Can Be Particularly Tricky

Research on autism and pragmatic communication highlights several relevant challenges:

**Modeling the listener's knowledge state:**
Common ground management requires constantly modeling what the other person knows, doesn't know, and is thinking. This involves what researchers call "conversational theory of mind" -- tracking the listener's mental state in real time. For autistic individuals who process social information differently, this can require more conscious effort than it does for neurotypical communicators.

However, recent research emphasizes the **double empathy problem** (Milton, 2012): communication difficulties between autistic and non-autistic people are bidirectional. Non-autistic people also struggle to model autistic perspectives. The communication gap is mutual, not one-sided.

**Backchannel production and reading:**
Neurotypical speakers rely heavily on backchannels ("mm-hmm," "right," nods) as evidence of understanding. If an autistic listener processes information deeply but produces fewer backchannels, the speaker may misinterpret this as non-understanding or disengagement. Conversely, autistic individuals may have difficulty reading subtle confusion signals in others.

**The explicit vs. implicit divide:**
Much of common ground management happens implicitly -- through facial expressions, tone shifts, silence patterns, and vague language ("you know what I mean"). Autistic communicators who process explicit information more readily than implicit signals may miss grounding cues that neurotypical speakers consider obvious.

### 8.2 Practical Strategies for Autistic Communicators

**As a listener:**
1. **If you're confused, say so.** "I didn't follow that" or "Can you explain what you mean by X?" is always socially acceptable. Most people would rather re-explain than discover later you didn't understand.
2. **Paraphrase when stakes are high.** "So what you're saying is..." confirms understanding and prevents errors downstream.
3. **Produce some backchannels at pause points.** Even a simple "mm-hmm" or "OK" at natural pauses tells the speaker you're tracking. This doesn't have to be constant -- just enough to signal continued engagement.
4. **It's fine to say "I need a moment to process that."** This is better than silence, which the speaker might misinterpret.

**As a speaker:**
1. **Check explicitly rather than relying on subtle cues.** Instead of trying to read facial micro-expressions for confusion, ask: "Does that make sense so far?"
2. **Give context generously.** "I don't think I've told you about this yet, but..." prevents the assumption problem.
3. **Define terms.** "We use Jira -- that's our project management tool -- to track tasks."
4. **Welcome questions.** "Feel free to stop me if I'm not being clear" gives permission.

**In both roles:**
1. **"Just to make sure we're on the same page"** is a universally useful phrase. It frames checking as collaboration, not deficit.
2. **Repair is normal, not failure.** Every conversation involves some misunderstanding and correction. The skill is not in avoiding misunderstanding (impossible) but in catching and repairing it quickly.
3. **Written follow-up for important verbal conversations** can compensate for real-time processing challenges: "Following up on our conversation -- here's what I understood we agreed to: ..."
