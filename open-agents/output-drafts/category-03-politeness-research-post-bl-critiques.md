# Category 03 Research: Post-Brown-and-Levinson Politeness Theory

## Companion Research File to Category 03: Politeness

---

## How to Read This Document

The main Category 03 file presents Brown and Levinson's (1987) face theory as the organizing framework for English politeness. That framework is genuinely useful -- it is pattern-friendly, it has variables, it makes predictions, and it is still the most widely taught model of politeness in English-language linguistics courses. The Category 03 research files on positive politeness, negative politeness, off-record strategies, and the four-cell face matrix all lean heavily on it.

But Brown and Levinson's book is from 1987. The field did not stop there. In the twenty-five years between Brown and Levinson (1987) and the period when the Category 03 material was first assembled, a sustained body of scholarship emerged that pushed back on the framework -- sometimes sharply. This research file covers that post-Brown-and-Levinson scholarship. It is not a rejection of Brown and Levinson. It is the acknowledgment that the 1987 model is one theoretical lens among several, that it has real limits, and that those limits matter especially for autistic readers.

The claims in this document are consequential. If the field has moved from "politeness is a speaker strategy" to "politeness is a judgment hearers make about speakers," then the practical question for autistic readers shifts accordingly. Instead of asking "did I use the right strategy?" the real question becomes "am I being judged as polite by this specific hearer in this specific context, using the norms of their speech community?" These are genuinely different questions, and the second one is both more accurate and more actionable.

This file covers seven major post-Brown-and-Levinson developments, then closes with a section on what they mean for autistic communication specifically.

## 1. Eelen (2001): A Critique of Politeness Theories

### 1.1 The Book and Its Core Argument

Gino Eelen's *A Critique of Politeness Theories* (2001, Manchester: St. Jerome Publishing) is the single most influential theoretical critique of the Brown-and-Levinson tradition. Eelen's central charge is that Brown and Levinson universalized a specific, historically situated view of politeness -- a view rooted in Western, academic, Anglo-centric intuitions about what "being polite" means -- and then presented it as if it were a theory of politeness in general.

Eelen argues that the Brown-and-Levinson framework (and the Leech, Lakoff, and Fraser frameworks alongside it) makes three interlocking moves that he considers problematic:

1. **It privileges "linguistic politeness" over "politeness as judgment."** Politeness is treated as something encoded in utterances -- in the choice of strategy, the presence of hedges, the degree of indirectness. The question of who is doing the judging, and by what standards, is pushed to the margins.
2. **It treats politeness as an individual speaker strategy.** The model imagines a speaker calculating a weighted risk (Power + Distance + Ranking of imposition) and selecting a strategy to minimize face threat. This frames politeness as a problem solved by the speaker alone, before the interaction unfolds.
3. **It assumes that the theorist's intuitions about politeness match the intuitions of actual language users.** When Brown and Levinson label a move "negative politeness," they assume the reader will recognize this label as corresponding to what ordinary speakers would also call polite. Eelen shows that this assumption is not defended and is often false.

### 1.2 First-Order and Second-Order Politeness

Eelen's most-cited contribution is the distinction between two senses of the word "politeness":

- **First-order politeness (politeness₁)** is politeness as judged by actual users in their own terms. It is the commonsense, folk, lived notion: the sense in which ordinary people call some behavior polite, other behavior rude, and argue about the edge cases. It varies by community, by context, by individual, and by the particular relationship in play. It is emic -- from within the perspective of the participants.
- **Second-order politeness (politeness₂)** is the technical theoretical construct used by linguists to model politeness. It is etic -- from the outside, imposed by the analyst. Brown and Levinson's "positive politeness strategies," "negative politeness strategies," and "bald on-record" are second-order categories.

Eelen's argument is that Brown and Levinson conflated the two. They built a second-order model and then assumed it captured first-order politeness. When actual language users disagree with the model -- when they call something polite that the model labels impolite, or vice versa -- the model has no way to accommodate that. It simply overwrites the users' judgments with the theorist's.

This distinction has been widely adopted in the field. Watts (2003), Mills (2003), and Locher and Watts (2005) all build on it. The first-order / second-order distinction is now a standard background assumption in politeness research (Eelen, 2001; Watts, 2003; see Haugh, 2007 for discussion).

### 1.3 Politeness as Judgment

Eelen's deepest move -- the one with the most practical consequences for autistic readers -- is his reconceptualization of politeness as **judgment** rather than **strategy**.

In Brown and Levinson's picture, politeness is something a speaker produces. The speaker estimates the weight of the face threat, chooses a strategy, and utters the result. Politeness is a property of the utterance. Whether the hearer perceives it as polite is largely an afterthought.

In Eelen's picture, politeness is something a hearer (and the broader community) judges. The speaker produces an utterance; the hearer evaluates that utterance against the norms they bring to the interaction; the label "polite" or "impolite" is applied as a result of that evaluation. Politeness is a property of the hearer's judgment about the speaker's behavior. The utterance is merely the evidence on which the judgment is made.

Eelen puts the point directly: "(im)politeness becomes not only a matter of speakers producing behaviour but also of hearers evaluating that behaviour" (Eelen, 2001). The verb "evaluating" is load-bearing. It means that two hearers can evaluate the same utterance differently -- one finding it polite, the other rude -- and both can be correct within their respective frames of judgment.

### 1.4 Why This Matters for Autistic Readers

The concept of politeness-as-judgment is load-bearing for autistic readers specifically, and the Category 03 material is incomplete without it. If politeness is strategy, then the question "Am I being polite?" is a question the speaker can answer: did I use the right strategy, at the right weight? If politeness is judgment, then the question "Am I being polite?" is unanswerable without reference to the specific hearer in the specific context. It becomes "Am I being judged as polite by this hearer, who brings their own norms, their own history, and their own expectations to this interaction?"

This is a harder question on its face, and it can feel worse. But it is also more accurate, and -- crucially -- it relocates the problem. The speaker is no longer solely responsible for producing politeness. The hearer is no longer a passive receiver. The judgment is a relational event. If the judgment comes back negative, there are more places to look for the cause than "I used the wrong strategy." Maybe the hearer brought unusually strict norms. Maybe the context triggered expectations the speaker did not know about. Maybe the hearer has already decided the speaker is rude for reasons unrelated to the utterance. These are all possibilities the strategic model does not surface.

For autistic readers specifically, the judgment frame helps in three ways:

1. It explains why the same behavior works with one person and fails with another. The behavior has not changed. The judging context has.
2. It makes visible the hearer's contribution to the judgment. Autistic communication can be judged rude when identical non-autistic communication would not be -- the judgment is not about the utterance alone but about who is uttering it.
3. It weakens the self-blaming inference "if my politeness failed, I must have gotten the strategy wrong." The strategy may have been fine. The judging context may have been hostile, inconsistent, or working from information the speaker did not have.

The judgment frame does not solve the problem, but it names it more accurately.

## 2. Spencer-Oatey: Rapport Management

### 2.1 The Framework

Helen Spencer-Oatey developed the rapport management framework across two major works: *Culturally Speaking: Managing Rapport Through Talk Across Cultures* (2000, first edition; London: Continuum) and its substantially revised second edition, *Culturally Speaking: Culture, Communication and Politeness Theory* (2008, London: Continuum). Her 2008 chapter "Face, (Im)Politeness and Rapport" (pp. 11-47) is the most frequently cited single statement of the framework.

Spencer-Oatey's central claim is that politeness, as Brown and Levinson conceived it, is too narrow. What actually matters in interaction is a broader process she calls **rapport management**: the ways people manage their relationships with one another -- maintaining harmony, repairing damage, negotiating power, preserving alignment -- across the full arc of an interaction. Politeness, in her view, is one component of rapport management, not the whole thing.

Rapport management in Spencer-Oatey's framework involves two motivational sources:
- **Face concerns** (related to but broader than Brown and Levinson's face)
- **Sociality rights and obligations** -- the participants' entitlements and duties to one another

### 2.2 The Three Rights

The sociality-rights dimension of Spencer-Oatey's model is organized around three kinds of rights that people expect to have honored in interaction. These overlap with but are distinct from Brown and Levinson's positive and negative face:

**Equity rights**: The right to be treated fairly -- not imposed upon unreasonably, not taken advantage of, not patronized, not treated as if one's time and autonomy count for less than others'. Spencer-Oatey identifies two components of equity rights: the cost-benefit component (costs and benefits should be roughly balanced through reciprocity) and the autonomy-imposition component (people should not be unduly controlled by others). Equity rights are about being treated as an equal participant.

**Association rights**: The right to an appropriate degree of involvement with others -- not too much, not too little. People have expectations about how much interactional attention they should receive, how much disclosure is appropriate, how much inclusion they are entitled to. Association rights are about being treated with the right amount of engagement. Too little involvement (being ignored, being shunned) violates association rights; too much (being intruded upon, being smothered) also violates them.

**Sociality obligations / role-consistency rights**: The right to be treated in ways consistent with one's role, status, and the ongoing relationship. A person in a professional role expects to be addressed in a manner appropriate to that role; a long-standing friend expects to be treated according to the accumulated history of the friendship. Role inconsistency -- being addressed as a stranger when you are a friend, being treated as a subordinate when you are a peer -- is a violation of this third right.

### 2.3 Why Rapport Management Is More Useful Than Face Theory for Some Contexts

Spencer-Oatey's framework was developed primarily in response to the needs of cross-cultural and institutional research. Brown and Levinson's face theory, she argues, struggles to capture what happens in contexts where participants do not share a single set of face expectations, or where the interaction involves more than a single face-threatening act and its mitigation.

Rapport management handles these cases by:
- Distinguishing between individual face and group/institutional face
- Treating the relationship as the object of management, not just individual utterances
- Making room for the possibility that what matters most is not the face of the speaker or hearer but the fairness of the exchange, the appropriateness of the involvement, or the consistency of the role performance
- Allowing the analyst to ask which of the three rights is being prioritized by whom, rather than collapsing everything into a single face calculus

### 2.4 Why This Matters for Autistic Readers

The rapport-management framework, and especially the equity-rights dimension, maps onto the autistic communicative experience more sharply than Brown-and-Levinson face in several ways.

Brown and Levinson's positive face (the desire to be liked) and negative face (the desire to be unimpeded) are both about the individual self-image. They do not have a dedicated vocabulary for fairness, and they do not have a dedicated vocabulary for being taken seriously as a contributor. Much autistic experience of pragmatic failure is not about being liked or left alone -- it is about being treated fairly: not patronized, not talked around, not dismissed, not addressed as if one is not there, not having one's contribution reframed or diluted before it is allowed to land.

Equity rights name exactly this. When an autistic person reports that a conversation felt "off" without being able to articulate why, the problem is often that equity rights were violated -- the exchange was imbalanced, the costs and benefits were asymmetric, the imposition ran one way. The Brown-and-Levinson framework has no place to put this experience. Rapport management does.

Association rights are also useful. Autistic people are often treated with the wrong amount of involvement: too little (excluded from the group, ignored in meetings, left out of decisions affecting them) or too much (micromanaged, intruded upon, subjected to unwanted disclosure demands). The term "association rights" gives a name to this misfit.

Sociality-rights / role-consistency also matters. Being addressed as a child when you are an adult, being addressed as a subordinate when you are a peer, or being addressed as a patient when you are a person -- these are role-inconsistency violations. Spencer-Oatey's framework treats them as a legitimate category of interactional harm, separate from (and in addition to) face threat.

Cross-link: the equity-rights concept links directly to the manipulation sub-hub page on **pragmatic-packaging-invalidation**, which analyzes how autistic communication is often reframed as rude or inappropriate in ways that allow the reframer to avoid engaging with the content. Pragmatic-packaging-invalidation is, in Spencer-Oatey's terms, a systematic violation of equity rights.

## 3. Leech: Politeness Maxims and the 2014 Update

### 3.1 The 1983 Framework

Geoffrey Leech's original politeness framework appears in *Principles of Pragmatics* (1983, London: Longman). Leech proposed politeness as a complement to Grice's Cooperative Principle: where Grice described the maxims speakers follow to be informative and cooperative (Quantity, Quality, Relation, Manner), Leech described the maxims speakers follow to be interpersonally appropriate. These were his six original **politeness maxims**:

1. **Tact maxim**: Minimize cost to others; maximize benefit to others. (Applies to directives and commissives: requests, offers.)
2. **Generosity maxim**: Minimize benefit to self; maximize cost to self. (The flip side of Tact.)
3. **Approbation maxim**: Minimize dispraise of others; maximize praise of others. (Applies to expressives and assertives.)
4. **Modesty maxim**: Minimize praise of self; maximize dispraise of self.
5. **Agreement maxim**: Minimize disagreement between self and other; maximize agreement.
6. **Sympathy maxim**: Minimize antipathy; maximize sympathy.

Each maxim has the same structure: minimize the socially costly pole, maximize the socially generous one. Together they form a system of default norms that speakers follow (and are judged by) in everyday talk.

### 3.2 The Critique of Leech's Original Framework

Leech's 1983 maxims were criticized on several fronts:
- **Proliferation**: Why six maxims? Why not more? Brown and Levinson (1987) themselves argued that Leech's approach threatened to generate an unlimited number of maxims, one for every kind of face work.
- **Cross-cultural fit**: The maxims as stated seem to work best for English-speaking Western contexts. In cultures where, for example, praising oneself is appropriate in certain situations, the Modesty maxim produces counterintuitive predictions.
- **Unclear status**: Are the maxims descriptive (reporting what speakers do) or normative (prescribing what they should do)? Leech was not always clear.

### 3.3 Leech (2014): The Pragmatics of Politeness

Leech returned to these issues in *The Pragmatics of Politeness* (2014, Oxford: Oxford University Press, Oxford Studies in Sociolinguistics series). The 2014 book substantially revises the framework while preserving its core intuition. Key changes:

1. **From six maxims to ten**. Leech retained the original six (Generosity, Tact, Approbation, Modesty, Agreement, Sympathy) and added four new ones: Obligation by speaker, Obligation by hearer, Opinion reticence, and Feeling reticence. The additions were intended to improve coverage, especially for phenomena like acknowledgment of debt, expression of restraint about one's own feelings, and the management of strong opinions.
2. **A General Strategy of Politeness (GSP)**. Leech argued that all ten maxims are specific instantiations of one underlying principle: the speaker gives communicative value to the hearer rather than to the speaker. The GSP unifies the maxims into a single theoretical principle, addressing the "too many maxims" critique.
3. **A pragmalinguistic focus**. Leech explicitly framed the 2014 book as returning to the pragmalinguistic side of politeness -- the side that looks at how linguistic forms encode politeness functions -- which he felt had been neglected in the post-2000 discursive turn.
4. **Politeness as communicative altruism**. Leech defends the controversial claim that politeness is a form of communicative altruism -- the speaker absorbs costs (in precision, in efficiency, in directness) in order to confer interpersonal benefits on the hearer. This is the normative core of the updated theory.

Reviews of Leech (2014) generally treat it as a serious and updated version of the maxim-based approach, not as a refutation of discursive critics like Eelen, Mills, and Watts. The book and the discursive-turn books occupy complementary positions: Leech is more pragmalinguistic and normative; the discursive-turn writers are more sociopragmatic and descriptive.

### 3.4 Why This Matters for Autistic Readers

Leech's maxim-based approach has one major advantage over Brown and Levinson's strategy-based approach for autistic learners: **the maxims are more rule-based and more learnable**. Brown and Levinson ask the speaker to perform a real-time weighted calculation (Power, Distance, Imposition) and select an output strategy. Leech asks the speaker to recognize when a given maxim applies and follow it as a default.

The maxims can be studied explicitly. They can be memorized, recognized, applied, and debugged -- even if their fluent real-time deployment is not automatic. For autistic learners who benefit from explicit rule-based frameworks, the Leech approach provides a second, parallel way to understand politeness phenomena.

Some concrete uses:
- **Approbation / Modesty pair**: Explains why English compliment exchanges are awkward (the Modesty maxim pushes the recipient to downplay the compliment even when the Approbation maxim urged the speaker to give it). This is a useful diagnostic for why "Oh, this old thing?" is not strange -- it is the Modesty maxim in action.
- **Tact / Generosity pair**: Explains why offers and requests in English are heavily padded. The pads are the maxims at work: making the cost to the other smaller, making the benefit to the self smaller.
- **Agreement maxim**: Explains the heavy use of "yes, but..." and the ritualistic softening of disagreement.
- **Opinion reticence and Feeling reticence** (the 2014 additions): Explain the expected restraint around strong opinions and strong feelings. These are especially useful for autistic readers, because the expectation of reticence is often not stated -- it is a silent default that non-autistic speakers absorb by osmosis. Leech (2014) makes it an explicit maxim that can be studied.

A word of caution: Leech's maxims are not a complete answer. They are normative defaults, and they do not capture the full judgmental, relational, hearer-involved dimension that the discursive-turn theorists emphasize. They are best used **alongside** the judgment frame from Eelen and the rapport-management frame from Spencer-Oatey, not instead of them.

## 4. Mills: The Feminist and Discursive Critique

### 4.1 The Book

Sara Mills' *Gender and Politeness* (2003, Cambridge: Cambridge University Press) is the major feminist intervention in politeness theory. Mills' project is twofold: first, to show that Brown and Levinson's framework systematically misreads women's politeness; second, to argue that this misreading reflects a deeper problem with treating politeness as a property of individual utterances rather than as a discursive, community-situated phenomenon.

### 4.2 The Misreading of Women's Politeness

The conventional wisdom -- both before and to some extent after Mills -- was that women's speech is "more polite" than men's, where "more polite" was glossed as "more deferential" and "more use of negative politeness." This was read as deficit (women are less direct, less assertive, less powerful) or as virtue (women are more considerate) depending on the commentator.

Mills argues that both readings are wrong, and that they are wrong in the same way. What looks like deference or negative politeness in women's speech is often something else entirely: solidarity, in-group positive politeness, mutual affiliative work, or collaborative floor management. The Brown-and-Levinson framework, which labels strategies based on surface features, is structurally unable to see this. It sees a hedge and labels it negative politeness. It sees a compliment and labels it positive politeness. It does not see -- because its framework does not allow it to see -- the discursive function of the move in its actual community of use.

The same logic runs in reverse. What looks like bald-on-record directness in men's speech (within, say, male friend groups) is often solidarity and in-group positive politeness, not impoliteness. Again, Brown and Levinson's labels do not capture this.

Mills' diagnosis: **the meaning of a politeness move depends on the speech community's norms, not on the universal B&L strategy labels**. The same linguistic form can be a positive-politeness move in one community and a negative-politeness move in another, or an impoliteness move in a third. The form alone does not determine the function.

### 4.3 Discursive Politeness

Mills' broader theoretical contribution is the concept of **discursive politeness**: the view that politeness emerges in discourse, across multi-turn interactions, through the joint work of participants, rather than being encoded in individual utterances.

The discursive view has several components:
- **Politeness is negotiated, not encoded.** An utterance is not polite or impolite on its own. It becomes polite or impolite through how it is received, taken up, ratified, or challenged by subsequent turns.
- **Politeness is community-relative.** What counts as polite varies by **community of practice** -- a term Mills borrows from Eckert and McConnell-Ginet. Communities develop their own norms, and those norms determine what moves land as polite.
- **Politeness is an assessment, not a property.** (Here Mills converges with Eelen.) The label "polite" is applied by participants in the act of judging each other. It is not a feature of the utterance itself.
- **Context matters constitutively.** Gender, class, race, profession, institutional setting, history, and power all shape what can count as polite in a given interaction. Removing these contextual factors produces a model that mislabels actual behavior.

### 4.4 Why This Matters for Autistic Readers

Mills' argument has a specific and painful implication for autistic readers: **what matters is not what you are doing, but how it is being read**. Being stance-read as rude is not the same as being rude, but the social consequences fall on the person being read, not on the reader.

This is worth stating carefully, because it is load-bearing and also because it is dangerous when misapplied. The lesson is NOT "it is all your fault for not managing the reading better." The lesson is: the reading is a real social fact, it has real consequences, and it is worth understanding what is being read because the reading is shaped by context and norms that the speaker may not share.

Three practical implications:

1. **Identical utterances produce non-identical readings in different communities.** An autistic person moving between communities (workplace, family, online, clinical settings) should not assume that a move that worked in one will work in another. The same words will be judged differently.
2. **Being read as rude is a distinct harm from being rude.** Mills' framework names this distinction. It means autistic readers can hold two thoughts simultaneously: "I did not do anything wrong in my own frame" and "in their frame, I was read as rude, and I am paying the social cost of that reading." Both are true.
3. **The reading can be corrected, in some contexts, through meta-communication.** Naming the mismatch directly ("I realize that came across as dismissive; I meant it as information") is itself a discursive move. Whether it succeeds depends on the community, the hearer, and the power dynamics in play.

There is a deep connection here with the Milton (2012) double empathy problem -- discussed below in §8 -- and with the manipulation sub-hub page on tone policing, which analyzes how the "read as rude" reading is weaponized in some interactions. Mills' discursive framework is what lets us diagnose tone policing as a specific kind of interactional abuse: it is the selective, power-laden application of a community-specific politeness judgment to silence the person being judged.

## 5. Watts: Politic Behavior vs. Politeness

### 5.1 The Distinction

Richard Watts' *Politeness* (2003, Cambridge: Cambridge University Press) introduces one of the most practically useful distinctions in the post-Brown-and-Levinson literature: **politic behavior** versus **politeness**.

- **Politic behavior** is linguistic behavior that is perceived to be appropriate to the social constraints of the ongoing interaction. It is expected. It is unmarked. It passes without notice. It is the baseline of what participants consider normal for this kind of exchange, between these kinds of people, in this kind of setting.
- **Politeness** is linguistic behavior that is perceived to be **beyond what is expectable** -- marked, noticed, salient, carrying extra interpersonal meaning. Politeness adds something above the baseline. It is not just meeting the norm; it is exceeding it.

Watts argues that most of what Brown and Levinson call "politeness" is actually politic behavior. When a speaker says "Excuse me, could you pass the salt?" in a restaurant setting, Brown and Levinson would label this as negative politeness. Watts would say: no, this is politic -- this is the unmarked, expected form of the request in this setting. It does not exceed the norm; it meets the norm. It is not a mitigation of face threat; it is the baseline.

Calling it "politeness" obscures this. It implies that the speaker has done something extra when in fact the speaker has merely done what was expected. And it implies, symmetrically, that not doing it would be merely un-polite, when in fact not doing it would be a violation of the politic norm and therefore noticeable, marked, and potentially classifiable as impolite.

### 5.2 Politeness₁ and Politeness₂

Watts also developed a version of Eelen's first-order / second-order distinction, which he labels **politeness₁** (the folk, everyday, lay use of the term by ordinary speakers) and **politeness₂** (the technical theoretical concept used by linguists). Watts argues that the proper object of study for politeness theory is politeness₁ -- what ordinary speakers actually mean when they call something polite -- not politeness₂, which is the analyst's imposed construct.

This dovetails with the politic/polite distinction: politic behavior often does not get called polite at all by ordinary speakers (politeness₁), because it is merely meeting baseline. The extra moves -- the gracious thank-you, the unusual deference, the going-out-of-one's-way -- are what ordinary speakers actually label "polite."

### 5.3 Why This Matters for Autistic Readers

The politic/polite distinction is, for practical purposes, one of the most useful theoretical tools in the post-Brown-and-Levinson literature for autistic readers. The reason: **most of what non-autistic interlocutors expect is politic (baseline normal), not polite (extra nice)**.

This reframes the autistic pragmatic task in a more accurate and more tractable way:

- **The task is meeting the politic baseline, not achieving extra politeness.** Autistic readers sometimes feel that they must be exceptionally polite in order to compensate for perceived awkwardness. This is often wrong, and sometimes counterproductive. The actual expectation is the politic baseline: the unmarked, expected form. Exceeding it can itself be marked and noticed in ways that are not always positive.
- **Failing to meet the politic baseline is judged more harshly than failing to achieve extra politeness.** Politic behavior is the baseline; falling below the baseline is noticed. Extra politeness is a bonus; not adding the bonus is not noticed in the same way.
- **The politic baseline is community-specific.** Different settings have different baselines. A workplace baseline is different from a family baseline, which is different from a close-friend baseline, which is different from a clinical-encounter baseline. Learning the baseline for each setting separately is usually more practical than trying to derive a universal politeness strategy.
- **Much of the apparent "politeness" in English is invisible to its users.** Native speakers often do not notice that they are using politic forms, because the forms are unmarked. When asked, they often describe the behavior as "just being normal" or "just being themselves." This is consistent with Watts' framework: politic behavior is, by definition, unnoticed by its users. Autistic readers who have been told "just be yourself" and then found that being themselves produced negative social consequences are encountering exactly this phenomenon: the advisor did not realize they were meeting a politic baseline that the autistic person was not aware existed.

A concrete diagnostic move: when an interaction went wrong, ask "was I below the politic baseline or above it?" Below-baseline misses feel one way (the hearer was irritated, withdrew, became curt). Above-baseline misses feel another way (the hearer was uncomfortable, found the interaction weird, pulled back). They are different failures and have different repairs.

## 6. Culpeper and Impoliteness Theory

### 6.1 Impoliteness as Its Own Category

For most of the Brown-and-Levinson era, impoliteness was treated as a derivative phenomenon: it was the absence of politeness, or the failure to mitigate face threat, or bald-on-record FTAs in contexts that did not license them. Jonathan Culpeper and others (e.g., Derek Bousfield, Miriam Locher) argued that this is wrong -- impoliteness is a distinct category, with its own mechanisms, its own strategies, its own social functions, and its own research literature.

Culpeper's major statement is *Impoliteness: Using Language to Cause Offence* (2011, Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, Studies in Interactional Sociolinguistics 28). The book develops impoliteness theory in three directions:

1. **Impoliteness has its own strategies.** Culpeper catalogues specific impoliteness moves (bald-on-record impoliteness, positive impoliteness, negative impoliteness, sarcasm / mock politeness, withhold politeness). These are not mirror images of Brown and Levinson's politeness strategies; they are distinct moves with their own forms.
2. **Impoliteness has its own intentionality structure.** Culpeper distinguishes between impoliteness that is produced intentionally (the speaker means to cause offense) and impoliteness that is produced unintentionally or accidentally. This distinction is crucial: the same surface utterance can be impoliteness or not, depending on whether the speaker intended face attack and whether the hearer perceives intention.
3. **Impoliteness has emotional and institutional dimensions.** Culpeper's 2011 book includes a cross-cultural study of offense types across British, Chinese, Finnish, German, and Turkish student populations, and it examines how impoliteness is institutionalized in certain contexts (military training, courtroom cross-examination, exploitative reality television). Impoliteness is not just an accidental failure -- it is, in some settings, a deliberate and systematic practice.

### 6.2 Why This Matters for Autistic Readers

The distinction between intentional and unintentional impoliteness is crucial for autistic readers, in two directions that both matter:

**Direction 1: Being misjudged as deliberate when not.** Autistic communication is sometimes read as deliberately impolite when the speaker had no such intention. The social cost falls on the speaker regardless of intention, which is why the reading matters even when it is wrong. Culpeper's framework lets this be named: the speaker produced an unintentional-impoliteness event that was then misread as intentional. Naming the misreading is a necessary first step to repairing it.

**Direction 2: Recognizing deliberate impoliteness that is being denied.** The other direction is at least as important. Some interlocutors deliberately produce impoliteness while claiming it was unintentional. The move "I was just joking / I didn't mean it / you're being too sensitive" attempts to convert intentional impoliteness into unintentional impoliteness after the fact, and to shift the cost of the face attack from the attacker to the attacked. Culpeper's framework gives autistic readers the vocabulary to name this pattern: the utterance was impolite, the claim of non-intentionality is a follow-up move, and the follow-up move is itself a further face attack (it adds the accusation that the hearer misread the speaker to the original offense).

This second direction is the explicit link to the manipulation sub-hub. Several manipulation patterns -- DARVO, tone policing, post-meltdown shaming, and gaslighting -- rely on the intentional/unintentional-impoliteness distinction being weaponized. Culpeper's framework is a useful diagnostic tool for these patterns because it makes the underlying move visible.

Cross-links: the **tone policing** page (manipulation-hub-pattern-tone-policing) analyzes one specific deployment of this weaponization -- the selective application of politeness standards in a way that makes the target's protest against impoliteness itself count as impolite. The **DARVO** page (manipulation-hub-pattern-darvo) analyzes the larger pattern in which an offender denies, attacks, and reverses victim-offender roles, and Culpeper's framework illuminates the role that impoliteness-intentionality claims play in the "reverse" step.

### 6.3 Mock Politeness and Sarcasm

One specific Culpeper strategy deserves its own note: **mock politeness**, where the surface form is polite but the intended function is face attack. "Oh, that's a *brilliant* idea" said with the right prosody and context is face attack, not compliment. Culpeper includes sarcastic mock politeness in his impoliteness taxonomy because its social function is impoliteness even though its surface form is polite.

For autistic readers, mock politeness is one of the most difficult categories to detect reliably. The surface form is polite; the detection requires reading prosody, facial expression, relationship history, and situational context to recognize that the politeness is inverted. The category's existence is, at minimum, worth naming explicitly: when the surface seems polite but the interaction feels worse afterward, one possibility is that the politeness was mocked rather than sincere. This does not solve the detection problem -- nothing does, reliably -- but it lets the possibility be considered and tested rather than dismissed.

## 7. The Discursive Turn More Broadly

### 7.1 What "Discursive" Means Here

The individual theorists covered above -- Eelen, Spencer-Oatey, Mills, Watts, Culpeper, and their collaborators (notably Miriam Locher) -- collectively represent what is now called the **discursive turn** in politeness research. Locher and Watts (2005), in their widely cited article "Politeness Theory and Relational Work" (Journal of Politeness Research, 1, 9-33), are an explicit statement of the discursive position.

"Discursive" here has a technical meaning. It does not mean "chatty" or "talking about things." It means that politeness is located in **discourse** -- in ongoing multi-turn interaction, in the joint work of participants, in the unfolding of social meaning over time -- rather than in isolated speaker-produced utterances.

The discursive view contrasts with what we can call the **speaker-intentional** view, which was the default assumption of Brown and Levinson (1987) and much of the framework that followed. On the speaker-intentional view:
- Politeness is produced by a speaker
- Before the utterance is made
- Through a calculation of face threat
- And encoded in the linguistic form of the utterance
- The hearer is a passive receiver

On the discursive view:
- Politeness is produced by a speaker *and* a hearer *and* the larger community
- During and after the utterance is made
- Through ongoing negotiation, uptake, and judgment
- Emerging across turns rather than encoded in any one of them
- The hearer is an active co-producer

### 7.2 Relational Work

Locher and Watts (2005) propose **relational work** as the umbrella concept: all the work that participants do to negotiate their relationships in interaction. Politeness is one part of relational work; impoliteness is another part; politic behavior is another; ritualized behavior is another. The analyst's job is to study relational work in its full range, not just the narrow slice that Brown and Levinson focused on.

Locher and Watts point out that Brown and Levinson's framework, despite being called a theory of politeness, is actually a theory of **face-threat mitigation** -- the narrower phenomenon of how speakers reduce the threat of face-threatening acts. This is an important phenomenon, but it is not the whole of relational work, and treating it as if it were distorts the field. The relational-work frame restores the full scope.

### 7.3 Why This Matters for Autistic Readers

The discursive turn matters for autistic readers because it **relocates politeness from the speaker's head to the interactional field**. This relocation has three consequences that are worth holding in mind:

1. **Politeness is not a skill one possesses.** It is a relational achievement that happens (or fails to happen) between people, across turns, over time. This means the question "am I good at politeness?" is not quite the right question. The better question is "am I participating in relational work with people who are also participating with me, and is the relational work succeeding?" The achievement is joint.
2. **Failure of politeness is not unilateral.** If the discursive view is right, then politeness failures are joint failures. The speaker, the hearer, and the surrounding community all contribute. This does not absolve the speaker of responsibility for their contribution, but it also does not place all the responsibility there. The deficit framing of autistic communication -- which treats failure as a one-sided autistic problem -- is inconsistent with the discursive view of politeness.
3. **Relational work can be learned, in part, by observing interactions.** Because politeness is discursive, it is visible in how turns unfold. Autistic readers who struggle with real-time production can still profitably study relational work as an observer: watching how politeness is jointly negotiated in specific interactions, noting which moves land and which do not, tracking the community-specific norms of the settings they move in. The discursive view licenses this observational approach in a way that the strategic view does not.

## 8. Implications for Autistic Readers

This section gathers the implications of the post-Brown-and-Levinson developments specifically for autistic communication. It is the section that makes the theoretical material above actionable.

### 8.1 The Discursive Turn Aligns With Milton's Double Empathy Problem

Damian Milton's 2012 paper "On the Ontological Status of Autism: The 'Double Empathy Problem'" (Disability & Society, 27(6), 883-887) proposes that communicative difficulties between autistic and non-autistic people are bidirectional: non-autistic people struggle to empathize with autistic people just as much as autistic people struggle to empathize with non-autistic people. The problem is not a one-sided autistic deficit; it is a two-sided mismatch of communicative frames.

Milton's argument converges with the discursive turn in politeness theory on a single key point: **communication (and politeness, as a special case of communication) is a joint achievement, not a one-sided performance**. When autistic and non-autistic people fail to communicate, the failure is relational. Neither party has "failed" unilaterally. The framework that treats autistic communication as a deficit to be remediated -- the one that animates much of the pragmatics-teaching literature -- is inconsistent with both Milton's model and the discursive turn in politeness.

Empirical support: Crompton, Ropar, Evans-Williams, Flynn, and Fletcher-Watson (2020), "Autistic Peer-to-Peer Information Transfer Is Highly Effective" (Autism, 24(7), 1704-1712), found that information transfer degrades selectively in mixed autistic/non-autistic pairs, while autistic-only pairs perform as well as non-autistic-only pairs. Rapport ratings followed the same pattern: lower rapport in mixed pairs, equivalent rapport in autistic pairs and in non-autistic pairs. This is the kind of result the discursive turn and the double empathy problem both predict and the deficit frame cannot explain.

**Takeaway**: if the discursive view of politeness is right, and if Milton's double empathy problem is right, then the question "why does my politeness fail?" should never be answered solely in terms of the autistic speaker's skill. Some of the failure is on the speaker; some is on the hearer; some is on the mismatch between them; some is on the community norms in play. The analysis can be done without shifting all the cost onto the autistic person.

### 8.2 Equity Rights Often Matter More Than Face

Spencer-Oatey's equity rights -- the right to be treated fairly, not patronized, not imposed upon, not taken advantage of -- often map onto autistic experience more sharply than Brown and Levinson's positive or negative face.

When an autistic person reports that an interaction "felt wrong" without being able to say why, and when the Brown-and-Levinson framework fails to identify a face threat, the analysis often works better in equity-rights terms:
- Was the interaction balanced in costs and benefits?
- Was autonomy preserved on both sides?
- Was the autistic person's contribution treated as a contribution, or as something to be reframed?
- Was the exchange reciprocal?

Equity-rights violations produce a characteristic affective signature: a sense of having been diminished, talked around, not taken seriously, or treated as if one's time and autonomy count for less. This signature is common in autistic experience of clinical encounters, workplace meetings, family gatherings with dismissive members, and interactions with authority figures who do not respect the autistic person as an equal participant.

Being able to name the problem as an equity-rights violation is useful for three reasons:
1. It distinguishes the harm from "rudeness" (which is often what the other party will counter-accuse).
2. It makes the harm recognizable to the autistic person themselves, who might otherwise have trouble naming what felt wrong.
3. It provides a vocabulary for describing the harm to third parties (therapists, advocates, sympathetic witnesses) who may be more familiar with face-based than equity-based analyses of interactional harm.

### 8.3 Focus on the Politic Baseline, Not Extra Politeness

Watts's politic / polite distinction reframes the autistic pragmatic task in a more tractable way: **meet the politic baseline in each setting, and don't worry about achieving extra politeness**.

Concretely:
- Identify the politic baseline for each recurring setting in the autistic person's life (workplace, specific family contexts, clinical encounters, online communities, friend groups). The baseline is the unmarked form -- what passes without notice, what is expected.
- Focus attention on matching the baseline. This is usually a manageable, learnable target: the baseline is a set of expected behaviors, not an infinite calculation.
- Treat "extra politeness" as optional. Going beyond the baseline is sometimes appropriate (for specific high-stakes interactions), but it is not the default task.
- Notice that different settings have different baselines. A move that is politic in a close-friend context may be above-baseline in a workplace, and vice versa. The skill is not "be polite everywhere" but "recognize which baseline is in play here."

This reframe reduces the implicit pressure toward exceptional performance ("I have to be extra polite to make up for being autistic") and focuses attention on the actually-expected norm. For autistic readers who have spent years in the exceptional-performance trap, the shift to baseline-matching can be a material improvement in both accuracy and sustainability.

### 8.4 The Judgment Frame Reframes the Practical Question

Eelen's reframe -- politeness as judgment, not strategy -- has the most far-reaching implication for day-to-day autistic communication. It changes the question being asked.

The old question was: "Am I being polite? Did I use the right strategy?"
The new question is: "Am I being judged as polite by this specific hearer in this specific context?"

The new question is harder on its face, and it can feel worse. But it is more accurate, and it opens up diagnostic moves that the old question does not:
- The hearer's judgment depends on the hearer's norms, not just the speaker's behavior.
- Different hearers apply different norms. Behavior that succeeds with one fails with another.
- Some hearers apply norms more generously than others. The same behavior that a fair-minded hearer judges polite can be judged rude by a hostile hearer. The hearer's disposition is part of the judgment.
- The judgment is sometimes unrecoverable even with perfect behavior. Some hearers have already decided the speaker is rude before the interaction begins; no utterance will change their judgment. Recognizing this is useful because it stops the autistic reader from treating every failed interaction as a personal strategy failure.

The judgment frame does not lift the burden, but it distributes it more accurately. Some of the burden belongs to the speaker's behavior. Some belongs to the hearer's disposition. Some belongs to the mismatch of norms between them. And some belongs to circumstances that no single party controls.

### 8.5 Recognizing Deliberate Impoliteness Disguised as Unintentional

Culpeper's intentionality distinction -- intentional versus unintentional impoliteness -- lets autistic readers name a specific and damaging pattern: **deliberate impoliteness followed by a claim of non-intentionality**.

The pattern: a speaker produces an impoliteness move (an insult, a dismissal, a face attack), and when challenged, claims it was unintentional ("I was just joking," "I didn't mean it that way," "you're reading too much into it," "you're being too sensitive"). The claim of non-intentionality is itself a further face attack: it accuses the hearer of misreading the speaker, and it shifts the social cost of the offense from the offender to the offended.

For autistic readers, this pattern is especially damaging because the deficit frame of autism is already primed to attribute "misreading" to the autistic party. The autistic hearer who tries to protest the impoliteness is told that they are misreading -- a claim that matches the deficit frame and is therefore taken seriously by third parties, even when it is false.

Culpeper's framework provides a theoretical handhold: the intentionality of an impoliteness move is not determined by the speaker's subsequent claim about it. It is determined by features of the original utterance and its surrounding context -- prosody, choice of words, timing, power dynamics, history with the hearer. An autistic reader who suspects a pattern of intentional impoliteness disguised as unintentional can, in principle, analyze these features and form an independent judgment about intentionality. The speaker's post-hoc denial does not settle the question.

This analytical move is a precondition for recognizing several manipulation patterns covered in the manipulation sub-hub -- especially tone policing, DARVO, and gaslighting. The discursive and impoliteness frameworks do not solve these patterns, but they give them names, and naming is a prerequisite to recognition.

### 8.6 The Overall Takeaway

The post-Brown-and-Levinson developments, taken together, do the following for autistic readers:

1. They locate politeness in the interaction, not in the speaker -- which aligns with Milton's double empathy problem and Crompton et al.'s empirical results on autistic peer-to-peer communication.
2. They provide vocabulary for interactional harms that face theory cannot name, especially through Spencer-Oatey's equity rights.
3. They reframe the practical task from "achieve politeness" to "meet the politic baseline for this setting" (Watts), which is more tractable and more accurate.
4. They reframe the diagnostic question from "did I use the right strategy?" to "am I being judged as polite in this specific context?" (Eelen), which is more accurate and distributes the responsibility more fairly.
5. They provide analytical handholds for recognizing weaponized impoliteness and the post-hoc denial of impoliteness (Culpeper), which is foundational for recognizing several common manipulation patterns.
6. They do not replace Brown and Levinson so much as situate B&L as one useful model among several, applicable in some contexts (especially for diagnosing face-threat mitigation in baseline-cooperative interactions) but incomplete as a general theory of politeness.

Brown and Levinson remains genuinely useful. Nothing in this file is meant to discard the face model. What the post-B&L developments do is expand the toolkit: in addition to Brown-and-Levinson face theory, autistic readers have access to Eelen's judgment frame, Spencer-Oatey's rapport management, Leech's maxims, Mills's discursive politeness, Watts's politic/polite distinction, and Culpeper's impoliteness theory. Each tool is appropriate for different analytical tasks. Having them all available produces a more complete account of what English politeness actually is.
