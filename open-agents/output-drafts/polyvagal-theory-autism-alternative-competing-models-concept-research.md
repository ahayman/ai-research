# Concept Research: Alternative & Competing Models — Is Polyvagal Even the Right Framework?

## Overview

Polyvagal Theory (PVT) is not the only — or even the dominant — framework in current cognitive and affective neuroscience for explaining the data the user cares about: autistic HRV differences, autonomic regulation, emotion construction, chronic-stress sequelae, and burnout. A theory's epistemic status depends partly on what its competitors can explain. If less contested models predict the same observations equally well — or better — then "the HRV/autism data fits polyvagal predictions" stops being strong evidence *for* polyvagal theory. It becomes evidence for *any* framework that links central nervous system regulation to peripheral autonomic output, which is the entire post-1990 autonomic neuroscience literature.

This chapter surveys five distinct frameworks that explain the same autonomic, interoceptive, and emotion-regulation data without invoking polyvagal-specific claims (three-tier vagal hierarchy, mammalian-only myelinated vagus, dorsal-vagal-as-shutdown, neuroception). It also covers interoception frameworks (Craig 2002/2009, Garfinkel 2015) and the Bird & Cook 2013 alexithymia hypothesis — both of which reframe autism-related interoception/emotion findings in ways that don't require PVT. The chapter closes with the **convergence/divergence map**: where PVT and its competitors predict the same things, and where PVT makes unique predictions that the others don't.

The honest summary across these five models is that PVT is not winning. Two frameworks — neurovisceral integration (NVI, Thayer & Lane) and predictive-processing/active-inference (Friston, Seth, Barrett) — explain everything PVT explains about HRV and emotion regulation, are mainstream in autonomic neuroscience and computational neuroscience respectively, and don't require contested phylogenetic claims. A third — allostasis and allostatic load (Sterling, McEwen) — is the framework that *Raymaker's empirical autistic burnout research actually uses*, making it the relevant model for the user's centrally-stated concern. The theory of constructed emotion (Barrett) explicitly contradicts PVT's hardwired-emotion-state architecture. The somatic marker hypothesis (Damasio) overlaps PVT but is older and doesn't require its specific vagal claims.

## Core Components — The Five Competing Models

### Model 1: Neurovisceral Integration Model (NVI) — Thayer & Lane

**Foundational citations**:
- Thayer JF, Lane RD. (2000). A model of neurovisceral integration in emotion regulation and dysregulation. *Journal of Affective Disorders*, 61(3): 201–216. doi:10.1016/S0165-0327(00)00338-4. [PubMed 11163422](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11163422/).
- Thayer JF, Lane RD. (2009). Claude Bernard and the heart–brain connection: further elaboration of a model of neurovisceral integration. *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*, 33(2): 81–88. [PubMed 18771686](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18771686/).
- Smith R, Thayer JF, Khalsa SS, Lane RD. (2017). The hierarchical basis of neurovisceral integration. *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*, 75: 274–296. [PubMed 28188890](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28188890/).

**Core mechanism**: A flexible central autonomic network (CAN) integrates cortical, subcortical, and brainstem regions to produce coordinated adaptive responses. The architecture: the **ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC)** tonically inhibits the **amygdala**, which gates autonomic output via brainstem projections to the **nucleus ambiguus and dorsal motor nucleus of the vagus**, which controls cardiac vagal tone (measured peripherally as HRV/RSA). Higher tonic vmPFC inhibition of the amygdala produces higher resting HRV. Higher HRV indexes greater capacity for top-down regulation of subcortical emotional/autonomic output.

**What NVI predicts**:
- High resting HRV correlates with better emotion regulation, executive function, working memory, attentional flexibility, and social cognition.
- Low resting HRV (and reduced HRV reactivity) indexes impaired top-down regulation and is observed in depression, anxiety, PTSD, alcohol use disorder, schizophrenia, and *autism*.
- HRV is a transdiagnostic biomarker of "functional integrity of the central autonomic network."
- Interventions that strengthen vmPFC function (psychotherapy, meditation, biofeedback, exercise) should raise HRV.

**Evidence base**: Very strong. Thayer & Lane (2000) has 2,679+ citations. The model is broadly accepted in autonomic neuroscience and has been validated in hundreds of studies across psychiatric, cardiovascular, and behavioral domains. The structural overlap between the central autonomic network and prefrontal-amygdala emotion-regulation circuits is well-replicated in neuroimaging (Beissner et al. 2013; Smith et al. 2017).

**How NVI explains the autism HRV findings**:
- Cheng 2020's meta-analysis (g = -0.52 baseline, g = -0.72 RSA reactivity to social stress) is *exactly* what NVI predicts for any population with reduced top-down regulatory capacity. Autistic individuals show atypical vmPFC structure/function (Hyde et al. 2010, multiple subsequent), atypical amygdala reactivity (Dichter 2012), and atypical resting state connectivity in regulatory networks.
- NVI provides a mechanistic story (vmPFC → amygdala → vagus) for the HRV finding without needing to invoke three vagal nuclei, phylogenetic claims, or neuroception.
- The autistic HRV data fits NVI *exactly as well as* it fits PVT.

**Why this matters for the user's "is PVT the right framework?" question**:
NVI is the established framework in autonomic neuroscience for what PVT claims to uniquely explain. NVI has higher citation impact, broader empirical support, and doesn't make the comparative-anatomy claims the 39-author 2026 Untenable consensus rejected. From a "best framework" standpoint, NVI dominates PVT for HRV/emotion-regulation interpretation.

### Model 2: Predictive Processing / Active Inference

**Foundational citations**:
- Friston K. (2010). The free-energy principle: a unified brain theory? *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 11(2): 127–138.
- Seth AK, Suzuki K, Critchley HD. (2012). An interoceptive predictive coding model of conscious presence. *Frontiers in Psychology*, 2: 395.
- Seth AK. (2013). Interoceptive inference, emotion, and the embodied self. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 17(11): 565–573. [PubMed 24126130](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24126130/).
- Seth AK, Critchley HD. (2013). Extending predictive processing to the body: emotion as interoceptive inference. *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 36(3): 227–228. [PubMed 23663284](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23663284/).
- Quattrocki E, Friston K. (2014). Autism, oxytocin and interoception. *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*, 47: 410–430. [PubMed 25277283](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25277283/).
- Barrett LF, Simmons WK. (2015). Interoceptive predictions in the brain. *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 16(7): 419–429.
- Pellicano E, Burr D. (2012). When the world becomes "too real": a Bayesian explanation of autistic perception. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 16(10): 504–510.

**Core mechanism**: The brain is a *prediction machine*. Rather than passively receiving sensory data, it generates probabilistic predictions ("priors") about what it will perceive next — both externally (sights, sounds, touch) and internally (interoception: heart rate, gut state, hunger, breath). Sensory input is compared to predictions; the discrepancy is **prediction error**. The brain minimizes prediction error by (a) updating its predictions (perceptual inference) or (b) acting on the world or body to make sensory data match predictions (active inference). Emotion, on this view, is not a hardwired output of evolved circuits but a *constructed inference* about the meaning of interoceptive prediction errors in context.

**For autonomic regulation specifically**: The brain *predicts* what physiological state the body will need before it needs it, and pre-emptively adjusts via descending autonomic outputs (this is also the allostatic story — see Model 3). HRV, on this view, isn't just "vagal tone" — it's the visible signature of a regulatory system that's anticipating, weighting predictions against incoming data, and adjusting both interoceptive predictions and bodily action.

**What predictive processing predicts**:
- Emotion regulation is a process of updating interoceptive predictions (Seth 2013).
- Atypical emotional experiences arise from atypical precision-weighting of priors vs. prediction errors (Pellicano & Burr 2012 for autism: "hypo-priors," sensory data weighted too heavily relative to priors).
- Autonomic dysregulation = failed predictive control; the system fails to anticipate needs and lurches reactively.
- Allostatic load (Model 3 below) is predictive-regulation gone wrong over time.

**Evidence base**: This is currently the *dominant* framework in computational and cognitive neuroscience for interoception and emotion. Karl Friston is one of the most-cited neuroscientists alive. Anil Seth's interoceptive predictive coding model has become foundational. Barrett & Simmons (2015) in *Nature Reviews Neuroscience* established interoceptive predictions as a unifying explanatory frame across emotion, body awareness, and psychiatric disorder.

**How predictive processing explains the autism + alexithymia + interoception data**:
- Quattrocki & Friston (2014) explicitly framed autism as an interoceptive prediction problem, with developmental oxytocin abnormalities producing atypical precision-weighting on interoceptive signals — leading to alexithymia and difficulties representing emotional states.
- Pellicano & Burr's (2012) "hypo-priors" account explains autistic perceptual differences without invoking PVT.
- Bird & Cook's (2013) alexithymia hypothesis fits cleanly within predictive processing: alexithymia = atypical interoceptive precision-weighting; co-occurring alexithymia (~50% of autistic adults) explains the interoceptive findings often attributed to autism per se.
- Autistic burnout, on this view, looks like *cumulative allostatic dysregulation* — the predictive-regulation system fails to anticipate cumulative demands and the body's reserves are exhausted (this connects to Model 3).

**Cross-reference to existing project work**: This is the framework the alexithymia deep dive already uses (see `alexithymia-deep-dive/neural-mechanisms/index.html`). Quattrocki & Friston 2014 is already validated in that project.

**Why this matters for the user's question**: Predictive processing is the *contemporary* framework for interoception and emotion in computational neuroscience. PVT pre-dates it (1994). Predictive processing absorbs everything PVT explains about body-brain communication and adds a far broader explanatory frame (perception, emotion, psychiatric disorder, autism, depression, anxiety). When predictive processing and PVT make the same predictions, the parsimony tie-breaker favors predictive processing. When they differ (e.g., on whether "neuroception" is a special non-conscious mechanism or just standard predictive inference), predictive processing wins because it's both more general and more empirically constrained.

### Model 3: Allostasis & Allostatic Load — Sterling & Eyer; McEwen

**Foundational citations**:
- Sterling P, Eyer J. (1988). Allostasis: A New Paradigm to Explain Arousal Pathology. In: Fisher S, Reason J (eds), *Handbook of Life Stress, Cognition and Health*. Wiley. [Chapter — original allostasis concept].
- McEwen BS. (1998). Protective and damaging effects of stress mediators. *New England Journal of Medicine*, 338(3): 171–179. doi:10.1056/NEJM199801153380307. [The foundational "allostatic load" paper].
- McEwen BS, Stellar E. (1993). Stress and the individual. Mechanisms leading to disease. *Archives of Internal Medicine*, 153(18): 2093–2101. [The 1993 paper that introduced "allostatic load" as a term].
- Sterling P. (2012). Allostasis: a model of predictive regulation. *Physiology & Behavior*, 106(1): 5–15. [PubMed 21684297](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21684297/).
- Sterling P. (2020). *What Is Health? Allostasis and the Evolution of Human Design*. MIT Press.

**Core mechanism**: Stability *through* change, not despite it. The brain predicts what the body will need (food, water, salt, glucose, oxygen, social safety, sleep) and *pre-emptively* adjusts physiology to meet anticipated demand. Heart rate, blood pressure, hormones, immune function — all are continuously re-set by the brain in anticipation of need, not held at fixed setpoints. This is allostasis. **Allostatic load** is the cumulative wear-and-tear that accumulates when (a) the system is chronically over-demanded, (b) it can't shut off after stress ends, (c) it under-responds and other systems compensate, or (d) it adapts in maladaptive ways. Allostatic load damages the cardiovascular system, immune system, brain (particularly hippocampus and prefrontal cortex), and metabolic system over time.

**What allostasis predicts**:
- Chronic stress → glucocorticoid dysregulation → cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, cognitive decline, depression, immune dysregulation.
- Allostatic load is *cumulative* and *biologically embedded*.
- Recovery from allostatic load requires reduction in demand, not just better coping with current demand.
- HRV is a sensitive index of allostatic load: chronically low HRV predicts cardiovascular and psychiatric morbidity (this overlaps with NVI).

**Evidence base**: Very strong. McEwen's NEJM 1998 paper is one of the most-cited papers in stress biology. The allostatic load construct has been operationalized via biomarker indices (cortisol, blood pressure, inflammatory markers, etc.) and validated in hundreds of studies linking chronic stress exposure to disease risk. Sterling's 2012 *Physiology & Behavior* paper reframes allostasis as predictive regulation — connecting it to predictive processing (Model 2).

**How allostasis explains autistic burnout**:
- **This is the key linkage**. Raymaker et al. (2020) defined autistic burnout as "chronic life stress and a mismatch of expectations and abilities without adequate supports... pervasive, long-term exhaustion, loss of function, and reduced tolerance to stimulus." That definition is functionally identical to allostatic-load-induced exhaustion.
- The Higgins/Arnold 2021 finding that autistic burnout correlates with PTSD symptoms at r ≈ 0.70 fits this: PTSD is paradigmatic allostatic dysregulation; chronic burnout-state autistic adults are showing the same biological signature.
- Masking, sensory overload, social demand mismatch, lack of accommodations — all are repeated allostatic challenges. Cumulative load over years produces the depleted, dysregulated state autistic adults report.
- *No polyvagal claims are needed to explain autistic burnout*. Allostasis is the framework the empirical literature actually uses.

**Cross-reference**: This is the framework that should anchor Category 5 (meltdown/shutdown/burnout) when discussing burnout specifically. The polyvagal "dorsal vagal shutdown" framing is metaphor; the allostatic load framing is the empirical alternative.

**Why this matters for the user's question**: For autistic burnout specifically, allostasis is not just an alternative to PVT — it is *the* alternative that empirical autism researchers (Raymaker, AASPIRE) actually use. PVT's "dorsal vagal shutdown" reframe is layered on top of an empirical literature that doesn't need it. Allostasis wins here, decisively, on grounds of both fit-with-evidence and parsimony.

### Model 4: Theory of Constructed Emotion — Lisa Feldman Barrett

**Foundational citations**:
- Barrett LF. (2017). *How Emotions Are Made: The Secret Life of the Brain*. Houghton Mifflin Harcourt. [Trade book — primary popular exposition].
- Barrett LF. (2017). The theory of constructed emotion: an active inference account of interoception and categorization. *Social Cognitive and Affective Neuroscience*, 12(1): 1–23. [The peer-reviewed primary paper].
- Lindquist KA, Wager TD, Kober H, Bliss-Moreau E, Barrett LF. (2012). The brain basis of emotion: a meta-analytic review. *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 35(3): 121–143. [The meta-analysis showing emotions don't map to discrete brain circuits].
- Barrett LF, Russell JA. (1998). Independence and bipolarity in the structure of current affect. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 74(4): 967–984. [Circumplex model of affect].

**Core mechanism**: Emotions are not hardwired biological programs. They are *constructed* in the moment by the brain from three ingredients: (1) interoceptive predictions and prediction errors (the body's current state), (2) concept knowledge (learned categories like "anger," "sadness," "fear" — which differ across cultures and individuals), and (3) context (what's happening, who's around, what's expected). Different brains construct different emotions from similar physiological states. There is no "fear circuit"; there are general-purpose prediction and categorization networks that produce *instances* of fear when interoceptive data + concept + context combine in a particular way.

**The direct anti-PVT implication**: PVT claims a hardwired evolutionary architecture where ventral vagal = "social engagement," sympathetic = "fight/flight," dorsal vagal = "freeze." These are presented as biological states with discrete neuroanatomical substrates. Constructed emotion theory says this is exactly wrong: there are no discrete biological emotion programs, no specific neural circuits for "social engagement state," and the brain doesn't have hardwired evolutionary modules that map onto vagal nuclei. Instead, what feels like "fight/flight" in one context might feel like "excitement" in another, depending on the brain's predictive construction.

**Evidence base**:
- Lindquist et al. 2012 meta-analysis of 91 studies and 1,829 subjects: no discrete brain region maps cleanly to any single emotion category. Emotions emerge from distributed networks active in many tasks, not from emotion-specific modules.
- Wager et al. 2015 used pattern classifier analysis on neuroimaging data: emotion-specific patterns exist but they're distributed across many brain regions, not localized to "emotion circuits."
- Behavioral evidence on **emotional granularity** (Barrett 2004, many subsequent): people who construct more differentiated emotion categories show better emotion regulation, fewer mental health problems, lower physical health risk.
- Cross-cultural research showing emotion categories vary substantially between cultures (Mesquita & Frijda 1992; Lutz 1988).

**How constructed emotion explains autistic emotional experience**:
- Autistic emotion may involve atypical interoceptive predictions + atypical concept-construction + atypical social context.
- Alexithymia (which co-occurs ~50% in autism per Bird & Cook 2013) fits cleanly: low emotional granularity = fewer differentiated emotion concepts to construct from = "I just feel bad" rather than "I feel anxious-about-X-because-Y."
- Sensory overload looks like cascading interoceptive prediction error that can't be efficiently categorized.
- Treatment implications: building emotional granularity (concept-learning interventions, emotion-labeling) should improve regulation independent of any vagal intervention.

**Why this matters for the user's question**: This is the framework that most directly *contradicts* PVT's architecture. PVT says specific biological circuits produce specific emotion-states; constructed emotion says no such mapping exists. The empirical data (meta-analyses of emotion brain mapping) supports constructed emotion. If constructed emotion is right, then PVT's "ladder" (ventral vagal → sympathetic → dorsal vagal as a state-hierarchy) is at best a clinically useful metaphor for a more complex underlying process and at worst a category error.

### Model 5: Somatic Marker Hypothesis — Damasio

**Foundational citations**:
- Damasio AR. (1994). *Descartes' Error: Emotion, Reason, and the Human Brain*. Putnam Publishing. [Primary trade book exposition].
- Damasio AR. (1996). The somatic marker hypothesis and the possible functions of the prefrontal cortex. *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B*, 351(1346): 1413–1420.
- Damasio AR, Carvalho GB. (2013). The nature of feelings: evolutionary and neurobiological origins. *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 14(2): 143–152. [Updated review].
- Bechara A, Damasio H, Damasio AR. (2000). Emotion, decision making and the orbitofrontal cortex. *Cerebral Cortex*, 10(3): 295–307.

**Core mechanism**: When a person faces a decision, the brain rapidly previews possible outcomes and the body responds with *somatic markers* — gut feelings, heart rate changes, skin conductance shifts — that signal whether each outcome is advantageous or risky based on past experience. These body-state signals are processed by the **ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC)** and bias decision-making toward advantageous options before conscious deliberation completes. Famous evidence base: Iowa Gambling Task studies showing that vmPFC-damaged patients fail to develop anticipatory skin conductance responses and continue making disadvantageous choices.

**What somatic marker hypothesis predicts**:
- Body-state signals guide decision-making and emotional learning.
- vmPFC damage impairs decision-making in ambiguous situations.
- Interoceptive sensitivity should correlate with decision-making quality in emotionally-charged contexts.
- Bodily feelings precede and shape conscious feeling-states.

**Where it overlaps PVT**: Both emphasize body-to-brain signals in emotion. PVT's "neuroception" — non-conscious detection of safety/threat cues — is conceptually adjacent to Damasio's "somatic markers as pre-conscious decision-guidance." Both share the broad framing: the body is not downstream of cognition; it's upstream.

**Where it differs from PVT**:
- No claims about three-tier vagal hierarchy.
- No claims about evolutionary phylogeny of vagal nuclei.
- No claim that "social engagement" has a dedicated brainstem substrate.
- Centered on vmPFC and the broader interoceptive cortical network (insula), not brainstem vagal nuclei.

**Evidence base**: Strong for the broad claim (body signals guide emotional and decision-making processes; vmPFC is a key integrator). Mixed for specific operationalizations (Iowa Gambling Task has been criticized; the precise role of somatic markers vs. other cognitive processes is debated). Updated in Damasio & Carvalho 2013, which reframes "feelings" as conscious mental representations of body states regulated by the interoceptive nervous system — a framing that converges with both predictive processing (Model 2) and Barrett's constructed emotion (Model 4).

**Why this matters for the user's question**: Damasio's framework is older but enduring. It captures the body-feels-it-first insight that makes PVT appealing while not requiring PVT's contested anatomical claims. For practical purposes, somatic marker hypothesis and PVT make similar high-level predictions about embodied emotion — but somatic marker hypothesis doesn't carry the comparative-anatomy baggage that 39 experts rejected in 2026.

## Bonus Frameworks: Interoception Models and the Bird & Cook Hypothesis

### Bud Craig's Insular Interoception Framework

**Foundational citations**:
- Craig AD. (2002). How do you feel? Interoception: the sense of the physiological condition of the body. *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 3(8): 655–666.
- Craig AD. (2009). How do you feel — now? The anterior insula and human awareness. *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 10(1): 59–70.

**Core claim**: The **insular cortex** (particularly the anterior insula) is the cortical substrate of interoception — the sense of the body's internal physiological condition. Body signals are first represented in the posterior insula, re-represented in the mid-insula, and integrated in the anterior insula, where activation corresponds to subjective feeling. The insula is the cortical hub of feeling.

**Evidence base**: Massive. Craig 2002 has 5,400+ citations; Craig 2009 has 6,200+. The insula's role in interoception is one of the best-replicated findings in cognitive neuroscience.

**Where it sits relative to PVT**: Compatible but cortical-centric. Where PVT focuses on brainstem nuclei and peripheral vagal output, Craig focuses on cortical representation. These can coexist in principle — but if you want to explain *conscious feeling* of body states, Craig's insular model is the established neuroscientific framework. PVT doesn't replace it.

### Garfinkel's Three-Dimensional Interoception Model

**Foundational citation**:
- Garfinkel SN, Seth AK, Barrett AB, Suzuki K, Critchley HD. (2015). Knowing your own heart: distinguishing interoceptive accuracy from interoceptive awareness. *Biological Psychology*, 104: 65–74. [PubMed 25451381](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25451381/).

**Core claim**: Interoception has three dissociable dimensions:
1. **Interoceptive Accuracy**: Objective performance on tasks like heartbeat detection — how well you can actually detect body signals.
2. **Interoceptive Sensibility**: Self-reported subjective awareness — how attentive you believe you are to body signals.
3. **Interoceptive Awareness** (metacognitive): Correspondence between accuracy and sensibility — do you actually know how well you can detect body signals?

**Why this matters**: These three dimensions can dissociate within individuals. A person can have high sensibility (thinks they're attuned to their body) but low accuracy (actually performs poorly on detection tasks). This nuance is invisible in PVT's framework, which collapses "interoception" into general body-awareness.

**Implication for autism**: Garfinkel et al. 2016 found autistic adults showed *higher* sensibility but *lower* accuracy than non-autistic adults — an interoceptive awareness mismatch, not simple deficit. This is the kind of nuance PVT can't represent.

### Bird & Cook's Alexithymia Hypothesis

**Foundational citations**:
- Bird G, Cook R. (2013). Mixed emotions: the contribution of alexithymia to the emotional symptoms of autism. *Translational Psychiatry*, 3(7): e285. [PubMed 23880881](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23880881/).
- Cook R, Brewer R, Shah P, Bird G. (2013). Alexithymia, not autism, predicts poor recognition of emotional facial expressions. *Psychological Science*, 24(5): 723–732. [PubMed 23528789](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23528789/).
- Shah P, Hall R, Catmur C, Bird G. (2016). Alexithymia, not autism, is associated with impaired interoception. *Cortex*, 81: 215–220. [PMC4962768](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4962768/).

**Core claim**: The interoceptive and emotional difficulties often attributed to autism are actually attributable to *co-occurring alexithymia*, which is present in roughly 50% of autistic adults but is conceptually distinct from autism. When alexithymia is controlled for, autistic vs. non-autistic interoceptive performance differences largely disappear.

**Why this matters for PVT**: A central appeal of PVT-for-autism is that it offers a story about *why* autistic people have emotional/regulatory differences (the ventral vagal social engagement system is "impaired"). If the relevant differences are alexithymia-driven rather than autism-per-se driven, then PVT's autism-specific story is targeting the wrong construct. The alexithymia deep dive in this project (`alexithymia-deep-dive/comorbidities-population-overlap/`) covers this in detail.

**Cross-reference**: This is already validated work in the project. See the alexithymia deep dive's comorbidities chapter.

## How It Works — The Convergence/Divergence Map (Central Analytical Anchor)

This is the chapter's analytical centerpiece. It answers: *for each data point the user cares about, which models predict it and which don't?*

### Phenomenon 1: Low baseline HRV in autistic adults (Cheng 2020, g = -0.52)

| Model | Predicts this? | Mechanism offered |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| **PVT** | Yes | Reduced ventral vagal social engagement system function |
| **NVI** | Yes | Reduced vmPFC inhibition of amygdala; reduced top-down regulatory capacity |
| **Predictive processing** | Yes | Atypical precision-weighting; impaired predictive regulation |
| **Allostasis** | Yes | Indicator of cumulative allostatic load |
| **Constructed emotion** | Indirectly | Atypical interoceptive predictions feeding emotion construction |
| **Somatic markers** | Indirectly | Body-state signaling difference |
| **Bird & Cook alexithymia** | Yes (with caveat) | The HRV effect may be alexithymia-driven, not autism-per-se |

**Verdict on Phenomenon 1**: PVT has *no unique explanatory advantage*. Five other frameworks predict the same finding with their own mechanisms. NVI does it with established neuroscience; predictive processing does it with the current dominant framework; allostasis does it with cumulative-stress framing. PVT does it with contested anatomical claims.

### Phenomenon 2: Reduced RSA reactivity to social stress in autism (Cheng 2020, g = -0.72)

| Model | Predicts this? | Mechanism offered |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| **PVT** | Yes (cleanest fit) | Vagal brake fails to engage during social-engagement demand |
| **NVI** | Yes | vmPFC fails to dynamically regulate amygdala during social challenge |
| **Predictive processing** | Yes | Failed predictive control of autonomic response under social demand |
| **Allostasis** | Yes | Insufficient anticipatory regulation under stress |
| **Constructed emotion** | Partial | Atypical emotion construction during social context |

**Verdict on Phenomenon 2**: PVT has the *most direct, ladder-shaped story* here ("vagal brake disengages"). NVI and predictive processing offer essentially the same prediction with different vocabulary. PVT's edge is interpretive clarity, not empirical specificity. None of these frameworks is *uniquely* supported by this data.

### Phenomenon 3: Autistic burnout — Raymaker 2020 (chronic exhaustion, loss of function, reduced stimulus tolerance)

| Model | Predicts this? | Mechanism offered |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| **PVT** | Marketed as "dorsal vagal shutdown" | Metaphorical; no direct in-vivo measurement of dorsal vagal "state" |
| **NVI** | Yes | Chronic regulatory failure; persistent low HRV |
| **Predictive processing** | Yes | Failed allostatic anticipation; depleted predictive reserves |
| **Allostasis** | **Yes — directly. This is the empirical framework Raymaker uses.** | Cumulative allostatic load → exhaustion → biological wear-and-tear |
| **Constructed emotion** | Partial | Atypical interoceptive predictions during burnout |
| **Bird & Cook** | Tangentially | Comorbid alexithymia may amplify burnout difficulty |

**Verdict on Phenomenon 3**: **Allostasis wins decisively**. It's the framework Raymaker and AASPIRE actually use. PVT's "dorsal vagal shutdown" framing is layered metaphor on top of an empirically-grounded allostatic-load construct. The user's centrally-stated concern (burnout) has allostasis as its native framework, not PVT.

### Phenomenon 4: Autistic emotion regulation difficulties

| Model | Predicts this? | Mechanism offered |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| **PVT** | Yes | Ventral vagal social engagement impairment |
| **NVI** | Yes | vmPFC-amygdala-vagus regulatory circuit atypicality |
| **Predictive processing** | Yes (with autism-specific account) | Atypical precision-weighting on interoceptive predictions |
| **Constructed emotion** | Yes | Atypical emotion-concept construction (low granularity, alexithymia) |
| **Bird & Cook** | Yes | Co-occurring alexithymia explains the emotion-regulation effects |

**Verdict on Phenomenon 4**: Four mainstream frameworks explain this without PVT. Bird & Cook adds that the *relevant* difference may be alexithymia, not autism. PVT here is *one of five competing accounts*, and arguably the least well-supported on grounds of mechanism specificity.

### Phenomenon 5: Sensory hypersensitivity in autism

| Model | Predicts this? | Mechanism offered |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| **PVT** | Yes (Listening Project Protocol motivation) | Defensive state biases auditory processing |
| **Pellicano & Burr (predictive processing)** | Yes | Hypo-priors: sensory data weighted too heavily |
| **NVI** | Partial | Reduced top-down regulation of sensory gating |
| **Allostasis** | Yes | Cumulative sensory overload → reduced threshold |

**Verdict on Phenomenon 5**: PVT and Pellicano & Burr both offer specific mechanisms here. Pellicano & Burr's account is more empirically constrained (testable with psychophysics paradigms) and connects to broader autism cognitive science.

### Where PVT Makes Unique Predictions That Others Don't

PVT *does* make some unique predictions:
1. **The dorsal motor nucleus mediates freezing/shutdown specifically** (rejected by Neuhuber & Berthoud 2022 and the 2026 Untenable consensus).
2. **Myelinated cardiac vagal fibers are mammalian-only** (rejected by Grossman 2023 with lungfish/reptile/bird evidence).
3. **Three discrete biological states ladder under threat (ventral → sympathetic → dorsal)** (not directly measurable in vivo; treated as clinical heuristic by most users).
4. **Neuroception is a distinct non-conscious detection mechanism separate from standard sensory/cognitive processing** (no clear empirical separation from ordinary predictive inference; predictive processing absorbs the construct).
5. **The social engagement system links face, ear, vocal, and cardiac muscles via a coordinated brainstem network** (anatomically the connections exist but the functional integration claim is contested).

**The pattern**: PVT's unique predictions are precisely the ones that the 2026 Untenable consensus rejected. The predictions PVT *shares* with other models are the ones supported by autism HRV data — but those don't privilege PVT over its competitors.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception 1**: "PVT is the main scientific framework for understanding HRV and emotion regulation."
  - **Reality**: NVI (Thayer & Lane) has far broader acceptance in autonomic neuroscience. PVT is the most-popular *clinical/wellness* framework but is not the academic standard.

- **Misconception 2**: "If autism HRV data fits polyvagal predictions, that supports polyvagal theory."
  - **Reality**: Five competing frameworks predict the same HRV findings. The data is consistent with PVT but doesn't privilege PVT over its competitors. PVT only "wins" on phenomena where it makes unique predictions — and those (dorsal vagal freezing, phylogenetic vagal split) have been empirically rejected.

- **Misconception 3**: "Autistic burnout is best understood through polyvagal theory."
  - **Reality**: The empirical autistic burnout literature (Raymaker 2020, AASPIRE) uses *allostatic load* framing, not PVT. PVT's "dorsal vagal shutdown" label is layered metaphor on top of an allostatic-load substrate.

- **Misconception 4**: "Emotions like fear, anger, and sadness are hardwired biological programs."
  - **Reality**: Meta-analyses (Lindquist et al. 2012) show emotions don't map to discrete brain circuits. Barrett's constructed emotion theory has substantial empirical backing for the view that emotions are constructed from interoceptive prediction + concept + context.

- **Misconception 5**: "Autistic interoception is impaired."
  - **Reality**: Bird & Cook (2013) and Shah et al. (2016) show that interoceptive impairment is alexithymia-driven, not autism-driven. When alexithymia is controlled for, autistic-vs-non-autistic interoceptive differences largely disappear. Garfinkel et al. 2016 also showed the picture is more complex: autistic adults show *higher* interoceptive sensibility but *lower* accuracy.

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: An autistic adult after a difficult social event experiences exhaustion, brain fog, sensory overload, and emotional shutdown.

- **PVT framing**: "You're in dorsal vagal shutdown."
- **NVI framing**: "Your top-down regulatory capacity (vmPFC → amygdala → vagus) is depleted; HRV is low."
- **Predictive processing framing**: "Your interoceptive prediction system has accumulated unresolvable prediction errors; predictive regulation has failed."
- **Allostasis framing**: "You're experiencing acute allostatic load that hasn't yet shut off."
- **Constructed emotion framing**: "Your brain is constructing 'shutdown' from accumulated interoceptive prediction errors + the concept 'I am overwhelmed' + the context of social demand exceeding capacity."

The phenomenology is the same. The mechanistic stories differ. NVI, predictive processing, and allostasis offer testable mechanisms; PVT offers an evocative label.

### Example 2: An autistic adult shows low resting HRV and reduced RSA reactivity to a public-speaking task.

- **PVT**: "Your ventral vagal social engagement system is impaired."
- **NVI**: "Your vmPFC isn't dynamically inhibiting your amygdala under social-stress demand."
- **Predictive processing**: "Your predictive autonomic control failed to anticipate the demand."
- **Allostasis**: "You're operating with chronic allostatic load; you have less reserve capacity for acute regulation."

Same HRV finding, four mechanistic stories. The question is which mechanism has the best independent evidence — and PVT is not the answer there.

### Example 3: An autistic adult wants to recover from burnout.

- **PVT-derived advice**: "Build ventral vagal tone through SSP, breathwork, social safety, co-regulation."
- **Allostatic-load-derived advice**: "Reduce cumulative demand. Identify the chronic stressors that have been adding load (masking, sensory environment, work demand, lack of accommodation). Reduce or eliminate them. Add restorative practices (sleep, low-demand recovery, sensory-safe environments). The biology will recover when demand is reduced."
- **Predictive processing / constructed emotion advice**: "Build emotional granularity through emotion-labeling practices. Reduce interoceptive prediction error by sleep, regular meals, sensory accommodation. Practice noticing and naming body states."
- **NVI-derived advice**: "Improve top-down regulation through psychotherapy targeting vmPFC function, HRV biofeedback, exercise, meditation."

The PVT-derived advice has overlap with allostatic load advice (rest, safety, co-regulation are reasonable in all frameworks). But the PVT-derived advice also pushes commercial interventions (SSP) and a "ventral vagal tone" metric that has no direct measurement. The allostatic-load advice is closer to what the empirical autistic-burnout literature actually recommends.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **Where PVT might still add value as a clinical heuristic**: For populations who find the "ladder" metaphor (ventral → sympathetic → dorsal) genuinely useful for naming and tracking their states, the framework provides a usable vocabulary even if its underlying anatomy is contested. This is the Journal of Psychiatry Reform 2023 stance: "Polyvagal Approaches: scientifically questionable but useful in practice." But this is heuristic value, not theoretical privilege.

- **Where PVT's "neuroception" might be defended as distinct from predictive inference**: Some defenders argue neuroception specifically targets evolutionary-relevant threat cues (predators, postural cues, vocal prosody) and is therefore a specialized non-conscious detection system rather than ordinary predictive inference. This is plausible but not separable empirically from standard threat-detection processing.

- **Where allostasis and PVT genuinely converge**: Sterling 2012's reformulation of allostasis as "predictive regulation" is broadly compatible with PVT's "neuroception" framing if neuroception is understood as predictive anticipation. The two can be partially reconciled — but predictive processing absorbs both more cleanly than either absorbs the other.

## Research Gaps

- **Direct head-to-head model comparisons in autistic populations**: I found no study that explicitly compares PVT-derived predictions against NVI-derived predictions against allostatic-load-derived predictions on the same dataset. This would be the cleanest way to test which framework wins empirically. As of 2026, the comparison is theoretical and based on which framework explains existing data more parsimoniously.
- **Adult-specific autistic data for allostatic load biomarkers**: Most allostatic-load biomarker work has been on general populations. Autism-specific allostatic-load biomarker work is just emerging.
- **Predictive-processing autism research**: This is a fast-developing field. The 10-year-review of Bayesian theories of autism (ScienceDirect 2023) is a useful entry point for the current state of the model.
