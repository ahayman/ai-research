# Fatigue, Energy and the ME/CFS Overlap

> The only trial of duloxetine in ME/CFS failed on its primary endpoint. Its adverse-event table is the most useful thing in this chapter, and eight research passes cited the trial without opening it.

There is one trial of duloxetine in ME/CFS. Arnold 2015 (PMID 25660434) randomised 29 patients to duloxetine at 60 to 120mg against 30 on placebo for twelve weeks. Its primary endpoint was the General Fatigue subscale of the Multidimensional Fatigue Inventory, and it was negative `[primary, verified]`. That is the headline and it should not be softened. Duloxetine is not a treatment for ME/CFS, and there is no evidence that it is. Whatever this drug is doing for you, it is not doing the thing this chapter is named after.

That single trial is also the entire literature. One trial, negative on its primary, at doses three to six times yours, never replicated `[primary, verified]`. Nobody has studied duloxetine at 20mg in ME/CFS. Nobody has studied duloxetine's autonomic effects in ME/CFS. Nobody has studied duloxetine in autistic adults at all, beyond a three-patient case series `[no evidence exists]`. So when you ask what this drug does to your fatigue, the honest answer begins with the fact that the question has essentially never been asked.

And yet the useful material in this chapter came out of that same trial, and it was sitting in a table nobody had opened. Eight research passes cited Arnold 2015 for its negative primary endpoint. None of them read its adverse-event list. When it was finally retrieved, from the ClinicalTrials.gov results posting for NCT00375973 because the journal's own table was not reachable, it contained four numbers worth having. Dizziness in 31.0% of duloxetine patients against 6.7% on placebo, which is roughly double the rate seen in fibromyalgia or depression populations `[primary, verified]`. Somnolence in 41.38%, against 10.00% on placebo `[primary, verified]`. Insomnia in 34.48%, against 13.33% `[primary, verified]`. And "vivid dreams" as a literal registry adverse-event term at 13.79% against 3.33% `[primary, verified]`, which is your exact reported symptom, catalogued as a side effect, in your exact condition. You are not unusual. You are in the 14%.

The fifth thing in that table is what is not in it. The only ME/CFS trial of duloxetine ever conducted measured no sleep outcome at all `[primary, verified]`. Its outcome list runs: fatigue, average pain, depression, clinical impression, and discontinuations. Nobody measured sleep in the population you belong to. So the effect you report as the drug's best is the effect its only trial in your illness did not look for.

## The thing you value, and the channel that cannot see its cost

You report less morning fatigue and you value it highly. That report is real, and it is better evidence about you than any group study on this page, because every other number here comes from someone else's body. But there is a problem with it that you identified yourself, and it is the most important thing in this chapter:

> "There are different types of exhaustion. Just feeling 'sleepy' isn't a bother. It's mostly that bone-weary, pain-laden exhaustion where my brain is filled with so much fog I can barely function... I'm not sure I would notice."

Read that against the 41% somnolence figure. The adverse effect you are most exposed to is daytime sleepiness. The channel you would use to detect it is a channel where sleepiness does not register as a complaint. Your fatigue does not answer the question "am I sleepy?"; it answers the question "am I wrecked?" The instrument is therefore not merely insensitive. It is answering a different question while appearing to answer yours. The channel is not buried. It is hijacked.

This is a solved problem, and the solution needs no introspection at all, which is exactly why it works. **Count events. Do not rate feelings.** Specifically: count two things separately, and never merge them into one number. The first is deliberate nap attempts that succeeded. The second is unintended dozes, meaning you fell asleep without deciding to, while reading or in conversation or as a passenger. Benefit looks like the first rising while the second stays at zero. Somnolence looks like the second rising, whatever the first does. Both can happen at once, and you should not expect a clean verdict `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

The reason that axis works is that "did I choose it?" is a question about an event, not about a sensation, so it routes around the compromised channel entirely. It also maps onto a real dissociation in sleep medicine. The Multiple Sleep Latency Test asks whether you can fall asleep when you try; the Maintenance of Wakefulness Test asks whether you can stay awake when you must. They correlate at only r = 0.41, which is under 17% shared variance, which means they are measuring two factors rather than one `[primary, verified]`. You cannot run an MWT at home, but "did I ever fight to stay awake and lose?" is its kitchen-table version.

One honest limit, because it matters more than the taxonomy does. The label may be less decision-relevant than it looks. If you can nap, and you function better, and nothing intrudes, then whether a physiologist would call it "reduced tonic arousal" or "mild somnolence" does not change what you should do. The question that actually bears on the dose decision is not "which is it?" but "does it cost me anything?", and that is answered by the unintended-doze count, the night-sleep log, and whether hours go missing. Those are countable. The mechanism label is not `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

And the discriminator that would settle it cleanly is not available to you in time. Somnolence is dose-dependent; reduced tonic arousal has no established dose direction at all. So watching what happens when the dose changes would separate them. But you are at one dose, and that discriminator only comes into existence after the decision it would inform `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

## You are braced for the wrong side effect, and so was this page

You are watching for insomnia. Insomnia does not rise across your step. In the only duloxetine trial with posted results containing a fixed 20mg arm, insomnia went from 10.17% at 20mg to 8.62% at 60mg, which is a fall, and the inflection is at 120mg `[primary, verified]`. So the thing you are braced for is the one thing that demonstrably does not happen at this step.

The obvious next move is to say that somnolence is what rises instead. An earlier version of this page said exactly that, and it was wrong, so here is the correction rather than a quiet edit. The figures usually quoted for a somnolence rise are 2.6% to 10.2% and 7.8% to 14.3%, and those are **30 to 60mg steps, in trials with no 20mg arm at all**. They cannot speak to your step. In the one trial that measured 20mg directly, somnolence across 20 to 60mg goes 5.08% to 4.31%, which is flat `[primary, verified]`.

The row that does move is fatigue. It runs 0% on placebo, 0% at 20mg, 6.03% at 60mg, and 8.93% at 120mg, and it is the only one of the three that rises monotonically across your step `[primary, verified]`. That row has been sitting in this project's own published table the whole time, underneath a paragraph written about somnolence. The data was not missing. It was printed, and unread.

And now the correction has to be corrected in its turn, because the obvious headline is not supportable either. **The fatigue rise across your step does not reach significance.** Zero events in 59 people at 20mg against 7 in 116 at 60mg gives a Fisher exact p of 0.0969, and the two confidence intervals overlap: 0 to 6.11% at 20mg, 2.95% to 11.93% at 60mg `[reasoning, not evidence — the counts are primary]`. The reason is simply that the 20mg arm is small and had no events, and by the rule of three alone a true rate of up to about 5% is compatible with observing zero. Writing "fatigue rises at your step" as a headline would be a confident claim laid over a non-significant result, which is precisely the failure this page catalogues other people committing three separate times. Committing it here would make four.

So the supportable form is narrower and it is worth having exactly as it stands. Of the three effects, insomnia demonstrably falls across your step, somnolence is flat across your step, and fatigue is the only one that moves at all. Fatigue is also the only one that clearly separates from placebo at 60mg, at zero out of 117 against 7 out of 116, p = 0.0069, so it is established that 60mg causes more fatigue than placebo `[primary, verified]`. Whether it rises specifically across 20 to 60mg is unresolved in 59 people. Not "no". Not "yes". Unresolved.

That is the irony this chapter turns on, and it is worth sitting with. Your primary disability is fatigue. Your valued gain is waking less tired. The one row with any signal at the step you are considering is the row that lands on both of those, and it is also the row your own instrument can least read, because you cannot tell drug-fatigue from disease-fatigue by feel. Two further limits belong right here rather than lower down: this is chronic low back pain and not your illness, and it is a spontaneously reported adverse-event term rather than a fatigue measurement. The 0% placebo rate is the tell, because fatigue is near-universal in chronic pain, so the term is only catching fatigue notable enough that someone volunteered it unprompted. That cuts both ways: it makes the 6% a higher bar than it looks, and it makes the row a blunt instrument.

None of this argues against going up, and it is not intended to. Insomnia falls at your step, somnolence is flat at your step, and the fatigue contrast is underpowered. What it argues is about measurement rather than dose: the row worth watching is the one you would find hardest to see, the way to see it is the event-counting from the previous section rather than a rating, and the baseline has to exist before the step rather than after it.

**The 41% is a different kind of claim and must be held separately.** The 41% somnolence figure from the ME/CFS trial is not a dose-step finding and it should not be used as one. It is one arm, **n=29**, at a **flexible 60 to 120mg, unstratified** `[primary, verified]`. What it says is that when duloxetine is given to people with your illness at those doses, two in five report daytime sleepiness. What it does not say is anything at all about what happens going from 20 to 60. **Conflating those two is exactly how the error above was made**, and the figure is worth more when it is not asked to do a job it cannot do.

Somnolence *is* dose-dependent somewhere above 30mg, and that much is real: duloxetine's own FDA label gives 10% against 4% (pooled MDD and GAD) and 12% against 3% (pooled DPNP, OA and CLBP), and names both insomnia and somnolence among the reactions with "a significant dose-dependent relationship in fixed-dose studies" `[primary, verified]`. The widely-quoted figure of 1.5% for somnolence is a **discontinuation** rate rather than an incidence rate, and repeating it understates the effect by roughly seven-fold. But "dose-dependent across the licensed range" is not the same claim as "rises at 20 to 60mg", and the trial data says the latter is exactly where it does not.

## The nap findings, and a retraction this project owes you

An earlier pass of this project told you that your lifelong inability to nap was the ME/CFS sleep-latency finding experienced from the inside. That is wrong, and I am retracting it rather than quietly dropping it.

Watson's CFS twins had a mean sleep latency of 8.9 minutes `[primary, verified]`. They fell asleep, in under nine minutes, on a nap test. That is not nap failure; that is normal-to-brisk sleep onset. Watson's actual finding was "feels sleepy, sleeps normally", because their Epworth score was elevated at 10.9 against 8.2 while their latency was unremarkable. Your report is "feels exhausted, cannot sleep at all". Those are different observations. One is a mismatch between a rating and a latency. The other is a long latency.

It is worse than that, because the dissociation the claim rested on is not merely unreplicated. It is contradicted. The entire MSLT-in-ME/CFS literature is fifteen papers, and it points three ways. Neu 2008 found CFS patients fall asleep **significantly faster** than matched controls `[primary, verified]`. Spitzer and Broadman 2010 found 73 of 92 patients, 80%, abnormal in the sleepy direction `[primary, verified]`. Le Bon 2000 found 30% objectively sleepy and concluded that subjective and objective sleepiness were "not associated with CFS" `[primary, verified]`. Watson, Reeves and Majer found no difference. The field's own systematic review, Maksoud 2021 across 20 studies, says multiple sleep latency testing was "inconsistent across the studies" and describes "non-replicated findings" `[primary, verified]`. The CDC's own paper, Decker 2009, says that "no reproducible perturbations of sleep architecture, multiple sleep latency times or Epworth Sleepiness Scores are found to be associated consistently with CFS" `[primary, verified]`. And the best meta-analysis, Mohamed 2023 across 24 studies and 801 adults, did not analyse the MSLT at all `[primary, verified]`.

Nothing in the ME/CFS sleep literature describes a group that cannot nap. Where the literature is directional at all, it points the other way. So your nap-failure is not the ME/CFS group finding lived from the inside; if anything it is the opposite of that finding, and it needs its own explanation `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

What it does resemble is the insomnia hyperarousal phenotype, where an objectively-verified inability to nap **is** the phenotype rather than an anomaly. Bonnet and Arand's psychophysiological insomnia patients had longer MSLT latencies, with a baseline nap latency of 13.4 minutes `[primary, verified]`. But I will not oversell that fit, because it is imperfect and the imperfection is obvious: you do not report insomnia. You report that your sleep improved and that it is the best thing about the drug. Daytime nap-failure alongside decent night sleep is not a described phenotype in either literature. You sit between them, and the field's own 2023 review of hyperarousal names the cross-diagnostic comparison as an open question rather than a settled overlap `[primary, verified]`.

## Gotts 2015: you are common, and your arrow may run backwards

There is exactly one number on nap-failure in ME/CFS, and you should have it. Gotts 2015 gave 101 CFS patients fourteen-day sleep diaries. Twenty-one of them, 20.8%, did not report any daytime napping across the whole two weeks `[primary, verified]`. About one in five. You are in that fifth. That makes you common rather than anomalous, and after a lifetime in which it has probably looked like a personal defect, that is worth knowing. The limit belongs at the claim rather than below it: Gotts measured reported napping, not nap **ability**, so it cannot separate "cannot nap" from "chooses not to nap" `[reasoning, not evidence]`. Your claim is about ability. This is the closest number that exists and it is not quite the number you want.

The same study runs against your own theory, and honesty requires me to hand it to you rather than file it somewhere you will not look. You wondered whether your inability to nap might be causing your poor sleep. Gotts' conclusion is verbatim: "Napping, particularly in the afternoon is associated with poorer cognitive functioning and more daytime sleepiness in CFS" `[primary, verified]`. In the only dataset on napping in your illness, napping is associated with worse outcomes rather than better ones. It is cross-sectional, so the direction is unresolvable, and it is confounded by severity. But the sign is the opposite of your assumption, and it converges with ordinary sleep medicine, where daytime napping lowers nocturnal sleep pressure and degrades night sleep.

The consequence is a live possibility you should hold before you treat learning to nap as a goal: your causal arrow may run backwards. Not napping may be **protecting** your night sleep rather than damaging it `[reasoning, not evidence]`. That is untested, and it is not a claim that you are wrong. It is a claim that the one relevant dataset points the other way and nobody has looked properly.

## Cortisol: you are not backwards, the question is malformed

You wondered about excess cortisol, or a fight-or-flight mechanism that will not turn off. The textbook answer is that you have it backwards, because ME/CFS is associated with mild hypocortisolism. The textbook answer does not survive its own meta-analysis.

Powell 2013 reviewed 19 studies and meta-analysed the case-control CFS subset. It found no statistically significant group difference on total cortisol output. The only significant finding was an attenuated cortisol awakening response, at d = −0.34, which is small `[primary, verified]`. And on the other side of the comparison, the study on which the "insomnia means high cortisol" claim rests, Vgontzas 2001, reports 24-hour cortisol at 218.0 against 190.4 nM with **P = 0.07** in eleven people `[primary, verified]`. Not significant. ACTH separated at p = 0.04; cortisol did not, under a title and a conclusion that assert an increase in both.

So you are not backwards. The question is malformed, and that is the finding. You asked how much cortisol there is. Powell's verdict, verbatim, is that total cortisol output "was rarely associated with fatigue in any population" and that outcomes reflecting within-day change "may be the most relevant to fatigue experience" `[primary, verified]`. The absolute level is not the variable. The shape is. If you want this tested, the thing to raise is a diurnal salivary panel taken at waking, at waking plus thirty minutes, and in the evening, so that the slope and the awakening response are visible, rather than a single number that the best available meta-analysis says is the wrong measurement `[reasoning, not evidence]`. That is a conversation with your doctor, not a conclusion.

## Orthostatic intolerance: real, dose-qualified, and pointing in an unknown direction

There is one more channel connecting duloxetine to your energy, and it is orthostatic. This one has flip-flopped twice in this project, so it is worth stating exactly where it landed.

The claim that duloxetine can produce a POTS-like phenotype is **real**, and an earlier pass wrongly dismissed it as AI-content-farm sourced. It appears verbatim in Mar 2014 (PMID 24227635) `[primary, verified]`, is corroborated by Raj 2013 (PMID 23753844) `[primary, verified]`, and the 2015 Heart Rhythm Society consensus statement carries a **Class III, Level B-R** recommendation that drugs blocking the norepinephrine reuptake transporter "can worsen symptoms in patients with POTS and should not be administered" `[primary, verified]`. Class III is the net-harm, do-not-administer class. An earlier pass searched the recommends-for list, found nothing, and concluded the recommends-against list was empty.

But it is dose-qualified, and the qualifier is most of the story. Both real sources say "high-dose" and "chronic", and the only duloxetine-specific study behind the claim used **80 to 240 mg/day** `[primary, verified on the sources; the underlying Vincent 2004 full text was not retrieved and this is the largest remaining gap in the safety picture]`. That is four to twelve times your dose. Any text asserting a POTS-like phenotype at 20mg is overreaching its own source. This is not a 20mg problem. It is worth a conversation at 60mg. And the adjacent claim you will meet in passing, that SSRIs are preferred over SNRIs in POTS, is **invalid**: no guideline says it, its only source is a speculative sentence in Mar 2014, and Mar 2014's own data refute it, because sertraline 50mg against placebo in 39 POTS patients left standing heart rate not significantly different while symptoms were **worse** on sertraline `[primary, verified]`.

The direction for you is genuinely unknown, and that is a stronger and more useful statement than "the risk is small". NET inhibition does two opposite things at once. It worsens orthostatic tachycardia, and it **prevents** vasovagal pre-syncope: tolerated tilt duration rose from 29 to 35 minutes under NET inhibition, p = 0.001, and in the reboxetine study vasovagal reactions occurred in 9 of 18 subjects on placebo against 1 of 18 on reboxetine `[primary, verified]`. Which effect you get depends on which kind of orthostatic intolerance you have. Most ME/CFS orthostatic intolerance is **not** POTS: in Garner and Baraniuk's cohort only 6 of 39 met POTS criteria while 72% had orthostatic symptoms on standing, and the authors' conclusion was that "orthostatic tachycardia did not account for OI symptoms in CFS" `[primary, verified]`. If your orthostatic intolerance is the hypotensive or cerebral-blood-flow kind rather than the tachycardic kind, a NET inhibitor may be neutral or even helpful.

Duloxetine's own measured cardiovascular effect is small. Heart rate rises 2.22 bpm (95% CI 1.53 to 2.91) across 17 trials; blood pressure rises 0.5 systolic and 0.8 diastolic against placebo; and across 42 placebo-controlled studies and 8,504 patients there was no significant difference in sustained blood pressure elevation `[primary, verified]`. Hold that alongside its opposite, because both are true. A population mean of +2 bpm is entirely compatible with most people at zero and a minority at +40, and there is a case report of a 26-year-old man on duloxetine **20 mg/day** with a heart rate of 110 to 120, a negative cardiac workup otherwise, a positive dechallenge and a positive rechallenge, graded "probable" on the Naranjo scale `[primary, verified]`. That is your exact dose. The mean is small. The tail is real.

This one is answerable before your appointment, which makes it unusual on this page. An active stand test is a poor man's tilt: lie down for ten minutes and record heart rate and blood pressure, then stand and record at one, three, five and ten minutes, noting symptoms at each. A sustained heart rate rise of 30 bpm or more without a blood pressure drop is the POTS pattern, which is the phenotype where duloxetine's mechanism is unfavourable. A systolic drop of 20 or a diastolic drop of 10 within three minutes is orthostatic hypotension. Symptoms with neither is the commonest ME/CFS pattern, and it is the one where duloxetine's effect is genuinely unknown. A cuff costs about £20 to £30. Nobody has ever characterised your phenotype, and two weeks is enough time to characterise it.

## Your hypothesis: what you got right, and where the evidence isn't

> "I suspect they [the crashes] aggravate the whole CFS and fibromyalgia."

The first half of that is not a hunch. It is a documented clinical phenomenon, and you rediscovered it independently, which is worth saying plainly before anything else.

The Canadian Consensus Criteria, published in 2003, list under Neurological and Cognitive Manifestations: "There may be **overload** phenomena: cognitive, sensory (e.g. photophobia and hypersensitivity to noise) and/or **emotional overload, which may lead to 'crash' periods and/or anxiety**" `[primary, verified]`. The body text is more explicit still: "Overload phenomena may trigger a 'crash' where the patient becomes temporarily immobilized by physical and/or mental fatigue and recovery is slow" `[primary, verified]`. And under Neuroendocrine Manifestations: "loss of adaptability and worsening of symptoms with stress" `[primary, verified]`.

Sensory overload, then crash, then anxiety. That is your exact sequence, written down in a primary ME/CFS clinical consensus document twenty-three years ago, using your own word, with "crash" defined in the document's own footnote as "a temporary period of immobilizing physical and/or cognitive fatigue". You arrived at it independently. Your phenomenology is a recognised pattern in your illness. It is not an idiosyncrasy, and it is not something you constructed after the fact to explain yourself.

Before going further I want to be explicit about what this section is and is not, because this territory has been used against people with your illness for decades. Graded exercise therapy and the deconditioning model were withdrawn. NICE guideline NG206 (2021) states, verbatim, "Do not offer people with ME/CFS... any programme that... uses fixed incremental increases in physical activity or exercise, for example, graded exercise therapy", and states that CBT "is not curative" and does not assume abnormal illness beliefs cause ME/CFS `[primary, verified]`. That is current, verified, binding guidance. Nothing here is a psychological model of your illness. The claim under examination is pharmacological: whether a drug that prevents a documented ME/CFS phenomenon would slowly reduce your pain. You raised it. And as it happens, the evidence below points **away** from a stress-reactivity account of chronic pain and **toward** a metabolic one, which is the opposite direction from psychologising.

Your hypothesis is three claims with wildly different evidential status, and conflating them is the main risk:

1. Sensory and emotional overload triggers crashes. **Established as clinical description.** That is the CCC, above.
2. Crashes aggravate the illness cumulatively. **Unstudied.**
3. Therefore preventing crashes improves pain at 20mg over months. **Never tested by anyone.**

Claim 1 is your genuine find. Claim 2 is where the model is load-bearing, and it is where the evidence is not. The CCC delivers Claim 1 and is silent on Claim 2: it asserts that crashes happen and that clinicians should recognise them, and it says nothing whatsoever about crashes causing lasting damage `[primary, verified]`. Anyone citing the CCC in support of the aggravation hypothesis is citing it for something it does not say.

And the adjacent evidence, where any exists, leans against. Five things, none decisive, all pointing the same way.

First, **allostatic load has never been measured in fibromyalgia at all.** A title-level search returns zero papers. A broader search returns nine, and not one of them measured an allostatic load index in fibromyalgia patients `[primary, verified — search-level]`. The word is borrowed.

Second, where it has been measured in ME/CFS, the 2006 study reports an odds ratio of 1.9 with a **confidence interval of 0.75 to 4.75**, which is not significant, sitting under an abstract that concludes CFS "was associated with a high level of allostatic load" `[primary, verified]`.

Third, and this one runs directly against you: in the 2009 population-based study, "among persons with CFS, the duration of fatigue was **inversely** correlated with ALI (r = −.26, p = .047)" `[primary, verified]`. Longer illness, lower allostatic load. A cumulative-damage model predicts the opposite.

Fourth, prospectively, the limb of allostatic load that predicts pain is the **metabolic** one. In MIDUS, 781 people, 27 biomarkers across seven systems, roughly 6.7 years: metabolic dysregulation predicted high-interference pain (RRR 2.00, 1.06 to 3.79) and three-or-more pain sites (RRR 2.03, 1.08 to 3.83), while **parasympathetic dysregulation predicted nothing at all** `[primary, verified]`. Your model runs entirely through the autonomic limb. The evidence points at the other one.

Fifth, at the highest resolution anyone has achieved in fibromyalgia, the direct path fails. Okifuji 2011 ran ecological momentary assessment in 81 women, three times a day for 30 days, with lagged mixed-effects models. Fatigue predicted pain, pain predicted distress, and distress predicted fatigue, all at p < .001. But **emotional distress did not predict subsequent pain**. The authors wrote: "The failure of emotional distress to predict subsequent pain may seem counterintuitive" `[primary, verified]`.

There is a sixth item that is really a ruler rather than a finding. Human stress-induced hyperalgesia is measured minutes to about ninety minutes after a stressor. Not one human experiment measures it weeks or months later `[no evidence exists]`. Your model lives on the timescale of a season. Citing stress-hyperalgesia in support of it requires an extrapolation across roughly three orders of magnitude in time, which is the same error that correctly killed the BNST story elsewhere in this project, in a different literature.

Now the part that matters most, and it deserves to be stated as forcefully as the objections were. **None of that is a refutation.** Unstudied is not false. Your exact intersection, autistic plus sensory-triggered crashes plus ME/CFS plus fibromyalgia, is the population these studies exclude or never assemble: a search for autistic burnout crossed with chronic fatigue or post-exertional malaise returns **one** result, and it mentions chronic fatigue only as something to rule out `[primary, verified — search-level]`. Most of this absence of evidence is absence of research. The honest verdict is three-part, and no part cancels the others: **descriptively supported, causally unstudied, adjacent evidence unfavourable.** What that adds up to is a hypothesis that should not bear weight in a dose decision, that is nevertheless not a crank idea, and that nobody has tested.

## The one observation that separates the models, and the trap underneath it

There is a single observation that tells your model apart from the standard one, and it costs nothing.

Your model has to run through the **fatigue limb**, because Okifuji's data says the only surviving prospective path from distress to pain in fibromyalgia is distress → fatigue → pain. The direct path was null `[primary, verified]`. So your model predicts that fatigue and ME/CFS burden improve first, or at least concurrently, and pain follows weeks later. The standard model, in which pain relief requires crossing a noradrenergic threshold at roughly 40 to 60mg, predicts that pain moves without any change in fatigue, and only after a dose increase.

The consequence is a free test: **track fatigue and pain as separate numbers.** If pain improves while fatigue does not, your model is dead, whatever else is helping. If fatigue improves and pain follows with a lag, your model gains real support. This is the sharpest and cheapest test available to you and it rests on the one high-quality within-person dataset that exists `[reasoning, not evidence — the design is not mine originally; Okifuji is primary]`.

And here is the trap, because it is the actual decision content. Your model and the standard model make **different** predictions right now, and **identical** predictions at 60mg. 60mg crosses the noradrenergic threshold and it also carries on preventing crashes. So if pain improves at 60mg, both models are satisfied at once and you learn nothing about your own hypothesis, permanently. The 20mg window is the only clean test of it that will ever exist for you. It is open now, and a dose increase closes it irreversibly `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

That is not an argument for staying at 20mg. The cost of waiting is months of unrelieved pain, and pain matters to you; you have said so explicitly, and you have corrected the framing that treats your pain as something you would trade away. It is an argument for knowing that the window exists before it shuts, so that whatever you and your doctor decide, you decide it with that on the table rather than discovering it afterwards.

## What this leaves you with

What survives all of this is short enough to carry. Duloxetine is not a treatment for your ME/CFS, and the one trial that asked said so; if your energy is better, that is downstream of something else, and it is not a reason to expect this drug to touch the illness itself. You are braced for insomnia, and insomnia is the one effect that demonstrably falls across your step. Somnolence is flat across your step too, in the only trial that measured your dose. The row that moves is fatigue, which is your disability and your valued gain, and the honest statement about it is smaller than it wants to be: fatigue clearly beats placebo at 60mg, and whether it rises across 20 to 60mg specifically is unresolved in 59 people at p = 0.0969. That is not a reason to do anything in particular about the dose. It is a reason to know which row to watch, and by your own account it is the row you might not feel arrive. That problem has a free solution requiring no introspection at all: count the deliberate naps that worked and the unintended dozes separately, never merge them, and let the second number do the talking.

Your nap-failure is not the ME/CFS finding an earlier pass told you it was, and the retraction is worth more to you than the claim was. About one in five people with your illness shares it, so you are common rather than broken. The one study of napping in ME/CFS suggests that not napping may be protecting your night sleep rather than wrecking it, which is the reverse of what you assumed and is untested in both directions. On cortisol you are not backwards; you asked how much, and the best evidence says the answer is the shape, so the panel to ask about is diurnal rather than single-point. On orthostatic intolerance the mean effect is trivial and the tail is real at your own dose, most ME/CFS orthostatic intolerance is not the kind duloxetine threatens, and nobody has ever checked which kind you have, which makes a blood pressure cuff and ten minutes the highest-yield thing on this page.

And your crash sequence is written down in a 2003 consensus document, in your own word, which means you rediscovered something real instead of inventing it. Whether those crashes damage you cumulatively is genuinely unknown, the nearby evidence leans against it, and the nearby evidence is not about you and never enrolled anyone like you. The single most useful thing you can do in the two weeks you have is not to change anything. It is to write fatigue and pain down as separate numbers, count the dozes you did not choose, and take a standing heart rate, so that whatever you and your doctor decide, you will be able to read the result.

**Dizziness in context** `[primary, verified]`: roughly 11.0% in fibromyalgia, ~14.5% in breast cancer, ~16.1% in depression at 60mg. **The CFS cohort's 31% is roughly double, against a 6.7% placebo rate.**

**Grading, stated at the claim** `[reasoning, not evidence]`: n=60 total, single trial, secondary safety data, no multiplicity control, doses 3 to 6× the reader's, not stratified by dose, and the confidence interval on 31% from n=29 is wide. **It is not proof.** It is the only direct evidence that exists on the question "what happens when you give duloxetine to people with ME/CFS", and it was in a table that eight research passes cited the trial without opening.

**Insomnia and somnolence occurred simultaneously in the same 29 people, at 34% and 41%.** `[primary, verified]` These are not opposite ends of one dose axis. They are two different effects happening to different people on the same drug, and duloxetine's FDA label lists **both** among reactions with "a significant dose-dependent relationship in fixed-dose studies" `[primary, verified — DailyMed]`.

**The standing brief said *"somnolence rises at the 20→60 step (2.6%→10.2%, 7.8%→14.3%)"*. Those are 30→60 steps, from trials with no 20mg arm.** They cannot speak to this step. **In the only trial that measured 20mg directly, somnolence across his step is 5.08% → 4.31%, which is flat (Fisher p = 1.00), and insomnia falls (p = 0.78).** `[primary, verified]`

**The row that moves across his step is fatigue**, and it is the only monotonic rise in the table. **That row was sitting in this project's own published table, under a spine written about somnolence. The data was not missing. It was printed, and unread.** `[reasoning, not evidence]`

### ⚠ AND THE CORRECTION HAS TO BE CORRECTED — the obvious next headline is not supportable

**"Fatigue rises at his step" does not reach significance.** 0/59 vs 7/116 gives **Fisher exact two-sided p = 0.0969**. The 20mg arm's 95% CI runs **0% to 6.11%**; the 60mg arm's runs **2.95% to 11.93%**. **They overlap.** The 20mg arm is **59 people with zero events**, and by the rule of three alone a true rate of up to about 5.1% is compatible with observing zero. `[reasoning, not evidence — the counts are primary]`

> **Writing it as a headline would be a confident claim over a non-significant result: the exact failure this project documents three times in its source literature** (Vgontzas' cortisol at p=0.07; Maloney's allostatic load at CI 0.75–4.75; Maier & Seligman's unmeasured "3 days"). **Committing it here, in the document that catalogues it, would make four.**

**What survives, in the form the data supports:**

1. **Insomnia does not rise across his step** (10.17% → 8.62%, p = 0.78). **He is braced for the one thing that demonstrably does not happen here.** `[primary, verified]`
2. **Somnolence does not rise across his step either**, in the only trial that measured it (5.08% → 4.31%, p = 1.00). `[primary, verified]`
3. **Fatigue is the only one of the three that moves at his step**, and the only one that clearly separates from placebo at 60mg: **0/117 vs 7/116, p = 0.0069. At 60mg, duloxetine causes more fatigue than placebo. That much is solid.** `[primary, verified]`
4. **Whether it rises specifically across 20→60 is underpowered and unresolved.** Not "no". Not "yes". **Unresolved, in 59 people.**

> **Where the inflection actually sits: across all three rows the step that moves everything is 60→120, not 20→60.** Insomnia 8.62%→18.75%; somnolence 4.31%→12.50%; fatigue 6.03%→8.93%. The defensible claim is *"no evidence the 20→60 step damages sleep"*. It is **not** *"60mg helps"*.

**Three more limits on the fatigue row, at the claim:** it is **chronic low back pain, not ME/CFS and not fibromyalgia**; it is a **spontaneously reported AE term, not a fatigue measurement** (the **0% placebo rate is the tell** — fatigue is near-universal in chronic pain, so the term only catches fatigue notable enough to volunteer unprompted, which cuts both ways: it makes 6% a higher bar than it looks, and it makes the row blunt); and **the 41% ME/CFS somnolence figure is a different kind of claim** (one arm, **n=29**, **flexible 60–120mg, unstratified**) that **says nothing about going from 20 to 60**. **Conflating them is how the original error was made.**

**The 30→60mg step, correctly labelled** `[primary, verified]`. Real, **not his step**, and from trials **with no 20mg arm**. They support *"somnolence is dose-dependent somewhere above 30mg"*. They do **not** support *"somnolence rises when he goes from 20 to 60"*.

| Trial | Population | 30mg | 60mg |
|---|---|---|---|
| NCT00849693 | MDD, children/adolescents | **2.6%** | **10.2%** |
| NCT00960986 | MDD, Korean adults | **0%** | **6.8%** |
| NCT00960986 (second cohort) | MDD, Korean adults | **7.8%** | **14.3%** |

**The correction that matters**: the widely-quoted **1.5%** somnolence figure is a **discontinuation** rate, not an incidence rate `[primary, verified — BINDING-CORRECTIONS §4]`. Quoting it understates incidence by roughly seven-fold.

**The largest dataset, and its complication** `[primary, verified]`: **Zhou 2023** (PMID 37422714), dose-effect network meta-analysis, 216 trials, 64,696 patients. Duloxetine somnolence OR **3.02 (2.31–3.95)**; insomnia OR **1.96 (1.60–2.42)**. The paper's own verbatim finding is that dose-effect curves *"included linear shape, inverted U-shape, and other shapes"* and that risks *"were not always increased linearly along with the dose increase for 21 antidepressants"*. Duloxetine's somnolence curve is modelled as an **inverted U**. `[model-based, not measured]`

> **The folk claim "low dose sedating, high dose activating" is NOT supported.** The actual finding is stranger: duloxetine causes insomnia **and** somnolence in the same population at the same time.

### The measurement instrument: count events, do not rate feelings

**The problem** `[reasoning, not evidence — the reader's own report is the premise]`: the reader's introspective sleepiness channel does not answer the question the adverse-event term asks. His fatigue is "bone-weary, pain-laden... brain filled with fog". Ordinary sleepiness "isn't a bother". So a 0–10 sleepiness rating from him is not a noisy measurement of somnolence. It is a measurement of something else.

**The supporting primary**, and the one the instrument advice should rest on `[primary, verified]`: **Olson LG, Cole MF, Ambrogetti A.** *J Sleep Res.* 1998;7(4):248-53. **PMID 9844851.** Verbatim: *"The ESS cannot be used to demonstrate or exclude sleepiness as it is measured by MSLT."*

> **This replaces the earlier justification.** Supplement 6 built the instrument advice on Watson's ESS-high/MSLT-normal group dissociation. That dissociation is contested (see below). **The instrument advice was right; the mechanism story under it was oversold.** It now rests on Olson, which is a statement about the instrument rather than about ME/CFS.

**The discriminator table** `[reasoning, not evidence — the premises are primary]`:

| Discriminator | Reduced tonic arousal (benefit) | Somnolence (adverse) | Strength |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Volitional vs intrusive** | Falls asleep **when he tries**, at a chosen time. **Unintended dozes stay at zero.** | Falls asleep **when not trying**: reading, in conversation, as a passenger. **Unintended dozes rise.** | **STRONGEST.** Opposite on "did I choose it?", and **requires no introspection.** |
| **MSLT/MWT dissociation** | Should move **MSLT** (can you sleep when you try), leave **MWT** (can you stay awake when you must) alone | Should move **both** | **Strong and principled.** Sangal 1992 (PMID 1555459): MSLT vs MWT **r = 0.41**, under 17% shared variance, **two factors** `[primary, verified]`. Home version of the MWT: *"did I ever fight to stay awake and lose?"* |
| **Time-locking to dose** | Tonic: **flat across the day** | Should track the plasma curve: a post-dose trough | **Moderate.** Requires fixed-time sampling and a dose-time log. |
| **Dose-dependence** | Direction unknown | **Label-confirmed dose-dependent** `[primary, verified]` | **UNAVAILABLE BEFORE THE APPOINTMENT.** He is at one dose. This discriminator only exists *after* the decision it would inform. |

**The rule:**

- Count **deliberate nap attempts that succeeded** (benefit channel).
- Count **unintended dozes** (harm channel).
- **Never merge them.**
- Benefit = (1) rises, (2) stays zero. Somnolence = (2) rises, whatever (1) does. **Both can happen at once.**

**The honest limit** `[reasoning, not evidence]`: the mechanism label may be less decision-relevant than the taxonomy implies. The question that bears on the dose decision is not *"which is it?"* but *"does it cost me anything?"*, which is answered by the unintended-doze count, the night-sleep log, and whether hours disappear. Those are countable. The label is not.

**The six-marker journal, carried from supplement 5** `[reasoning, not evidence — but it follows directly from the AE data]`. Nightly, 30 seconds:

1. Minutes to fall asleep
2. Number of awakenings
3. **Morning fatigue on waking, 0–10**
4. **Daytime sleepiness, 0–10, scored SEPARATELY from #3**
5. Vivid dreams: present / absent
6. RLS severity at bedtime, 0–10

> **#3 and #4 must never be collapsed.** For an ME/CFS patient they feel similar and mean opposite things: fatigue is the disease, somnolence would be the drug. Merging them makes the single most informative signal in this analysis unreadable.

**Add PAIN as a seventh item**, scored independently, because the somnolence instrument and the CFS-hypothesis test then become the same instrument (see "the separating observation" below).

**Baseline now, at 20mg, before the appointment.** Two weeks of baseline is achievable in the time available. Without a baseline, the dose test is uninterpretable.

**What is not useful** `[reasoning, not evidence]`: total sleep time from a consumer wearable. Wearables cannot measure sleep continuity or limb movements reliably; they would be measuring noise and giving it authority.

He quits at 10 to 20. **His lifelong "hard zero" may be a lifelong latency of 18 minutes he has never let run.** Running the full 20 converts a lifelong binary into a number, and numbers can move.

- **Protocol if he runs it**: four attempts; **run the full 20 minutes**; fixed early-afternoon time (~13:00–15:00); **no exertion for 30+ minutes before** (a 5-minute walk raised latency 6.9 → 11.9 min `[primary, verified]`); fixed conditions; no caffeine 8h; **log the previous night's sleep** (the dominant covariate); log dose time; **pre-register the prediction in writing**. **Now, because the 20mg window closes at the dose change.**

**The four-part answer** `[reasoning, not evidence — every premise is primary and verified]`:

1. **Against the textbook ME/CFS claim, yes, he is backwards.** The direction of the (weak) ME/CFS evidence is hypo, not hyper.

3. **If he indexes to the insomnia phenotype instead**, which his nap-failure actually resembles, **he is arguably right in direction**. But that finding is p = 0.07 in eleven people.
4. **The deepest answer: both literatures say the absolute level is not the variable.** He asked "how much?"; the best evidence says "the shape".

> **Verdict: he is not backwards. He is asking a question the evidence in both candidate literatures cannot answer, about a variable the best meta-analysis says is the wrong one.** If he wants it tested, the measurement is a **diurnal salivary panel** (waking, +30 min, evening) so the **slope and the awakening response** are visible, rather than a single number. **That is a conversation with his doctor, not a conclusion.** `[reasoning, not evidence]`

### Orthostatic intolerance: real, dose-qualified, direction unknown

**The claim is real. An earlier pass was wrong to call it AI-sourced.**

> **Mar PL, Raj V, Black BK, Biaggioni I, Shibao CA, Paranjape SY, Dupont WD, Robertson D.** *J Psychopharmacol.* 2014;28(2):155-61. **PMID 24227635.** `[primary, verified — verbatim from PMC]`

### The separating observation, and the trap

**Model A (his)**: 20mg prevents crashes → less cumulative load → **pain improves slowly over months, no threshold to cross.**
**Model B (the standing analysis)**: pain relief requires noradrenergic reuptake inhibition at **~40–60mg**. **20mg does approximately nothing for pain, ever.** (Below 40mg: **0-for-3 on pain primaries across ~350 patients, twice adequately powered.** Cochrane's own authors: *"doses of 60 mg and 120 mg daily are efficacious... but lower daily doses are not."*)

**"Just wait and see if pain improves at 20mg" is NOT sufficient** `[reasoning, not evidence]`, because placebo, expectancy and regression to the mean produce slow improvement under either model. **Four sharper discriminators:**

**1. ORDER OF EFFECTS — the strongest test, and it is free.**

Model A's causal chain **must** run through the fatigue limb, because Okifuji's lagged data says the only surviving prospective path from distress to pain in FM is **distress → fatigue → pain** `[primary, verified]`.

- **Model A predicts**: fatigue/ME-CFS burden improves **FIRST** (or concurrently); pain follows **weeks later**.
- **Model B predicts**: pain moves **WITHOUT** any change in fatigue, and **only after a dose increase**.

> **→ Track fatigue and pain as SEPARATE measures. If pain improves while fatigue does not, Model A is falsified, whatever is helping. If fatigue improves and pain follows with a lag, Model A gains real support.**
>
> **This integrates directly with the somnolence instrument**: the journal already needs to separate BODY from FOG from SLEEP PRESSURE. **Add PAIN as an independent item and the somnolence instrument and the Model A test become the same instrument.**

**2. CONTINGENCY ON EXPOSURE — unique to Model A.** Model A's benefit is purchased by crashes that did not happen, so it is contingent on exposure history. Model B's benefit is a pharmacological constant, indifferent to life events.

> **→ The killer within-subject test: if a crash DOES break through, does pain step up and STAY up for weeks?** If crashes truly aggravate, a breakthrough crash should leave a **durable pain signature**. **If pain returns to baseline within days, the "aggravation" limb is falsified even though crash-prevention is real** — the crashes would be unpleasant but not damaging, which is a completely different thing and changes what the crash benefit is worth. `[reasoning, not evidence]`

**3. SPEED OF ONSET.** Direct drug analgesia is fast; allostatic unloading is slow by construction. Pain drops within 2–4 weeks of a dose change → Model B. Pain drifts down over months with no dose change → Model A, or noise. **Bergenheim supplies the noise floor: 13% mean improvement over 10–12 years, only 16% achieving ≥50%.**

**4. ⚠ THE DOSE-DIRECTION TRAP — the actual decision content.**

> ### **Going UP to 60mg does not discriminate. It destroys the experiment.**
>
> **60mg crosses the noradrenergic threshold AND continues preventing crashes. If pain improves at 60mg, BOTH models are satisfied and he learns nothing about his own hypothesis, permanently.** `[reasoning, not evidence]`
>
> **The 20mg window is the ONLY clean test of Model A that will ever exist for this person, and it is available for free by waiting. A dose increase closes it irreversibly.**
>
> **Conversely**: if he holds 20mg for 3–6 months and pain is unchanged while crashes stay prevented, **Model A's pain limb is cleanly falsified and the dose decision becomes trivial.**
>
> **The information value is asymmetric in time. Cost of waiting: months of unrelieved pain. Benefit: a clean, unrepeatable answer.** **That is the real trade, and it is not "which model is right". It is "how much is the answer worth, against months of pain?"** **That is a question only he can answer, with his doctor. This page's job is to make sure he knows the window exists before it shuts.**

**5. Pre-commit, or the test is worthless** `[reasoning, not evidence]`. Model A must specify how long and how much, **in advance**. Without a pre-registered threshold, any outcome reads as confirmation ("it's working, just slowly"), which is precisely how an unfalsifiable belief forms. Given Bergenheim's base rate, something like: **"≥30% pain reduction by month 4, with ≥2 survived exposures and 0 crashes." Failing it must count.**

**Four variables are already in motion**: duloxetine, melatonin, iron (started this week, acts at ~12 weeks, cannot explain anything at the appointment), and a possible dose change. **Decide what to measure and how to tell causes apart BEFORE changing anything.**

**Somnolence, dose, and the label**
- **NCT00408876** (CLBP): the only duloxetine trial with posted results containing a fixed 20mg arm. `[primary, verified — registry]`
- **NCT00849693** (MDD, children/adolescents): 30 vs 60mg, somnolence 2.6% → 10.2%. `[primary, verified]`
- **NCT00960986** (MDD, Korean adults): 30 vs 60mg, insomnia flat; somnolence 0%→6.8% and 7.8%→14.3%. `[primary, verified]`
- **FDA / DailyMed duloxetine label.** Insomnia and somnolence both listed among reactions with "a significant dose-dependent relationship in fixed-dose studies". `[primary, verified]`
