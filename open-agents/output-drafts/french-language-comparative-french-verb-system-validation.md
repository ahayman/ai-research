# Validation Report: The French Verb System — Tense, Aspect, Mood in Contrast with English

## Summary

The concept research document on the French verb system is thorough and highly accurate. All major claims — 6 persons x 8+ tense/mood combinations, the passe compose vs. imparfait aspectual distinction, the etre/avoir auxiliary split, subjunctive triggers, and the si-clause system — are confirmed through independent verification. The document provides an excellent structural comparison showing how French achieves through morphological inflection what English achieves through periphrasis.

## Claim-by-Claim Verification

### Claim 1: 6 persons x 8+ tense/mood combinations producing 50+ distinct written forms
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: The document enumerates 5 indicative simple tenses (present, imperfect, passe simple, future, conditional) x 6 persons = 30 forms, plus 2 subjunctive tenses x 6 = 12, plus 3 imperative + 3 non-finite = 6, totaling ~48 simple forms. Adding compound tenses yields 50-60+ total written forms. Wikipedia's French conjugation article and Rosetta Stone confirm this count. The Reverso conjugator lists all forms for any given verb, confirming the numbers.
- **Sources**: [French conjugation - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_conjugation), [Reverso Conjugator](https://conjugator.reverso.net/conjugation-french.html)

### Claim 2: Passe compose encodes perfective (completed) aspect; imparfait encodes imperfective (ongoing/habitual) aspect
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Lawless French, UT Austin's Francais Interactif, and academic linguistic sources all confirm this aspectual distinction. The passe compose presents events as completed and bounded; the imparfait presents them as ongoing, habitual, or background. The narrative interplay (imparfait sets the scene, passe compose moves the story forward) is a standard description in French pedagogy and linguistics.
- **Sources**: [Lawless French - PC vs Imparfait](https://www.lawlessfrench.com/grammar/passe-compose-vs-imparfait/), [UT Austin - Narration](https://laits.utexas.edu/tex/gr/tap8.html)

### Claim 3: Same verb changes meaning between passe compose and imparfait (connaitre, savoir, vouloir, pouvoir)
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: This is a well-documented phenomenon in French linguistics. "Connaitre" in imparfait = "knew" (ongoing familiarity) vs. passe compose = "met" (moment of acquaintance). "Savoir" in imparfait = "knew" vs. passe compose = "found out." These meaning shifts are standard examples used across pedagogical and linguistic sources.
- **Sources**: [Lawless French - PC vs Imparfait](https://www.lawlessfrench.com/grammar/passe-compose-vs-imparfait/)

### Claim 4: ~17 verbs use etre as auxiliary (DR MRS VANDERTRAMP)
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED (with nuance on exact count)
- **Evidence**: The DR MRS VANDERTRAMP mnemonic encodes 17 verbs that take etre. FluentU, Lingopie, and FrenchCrazy all confirm the 17-verb list. However, different versions of the mnemonic include slightly different verbs (some include "demeurer" instead of "devenir," some add "passer"). The core set of ~14 verbs is universally agreed upon; the extended set of 16-17 is standard. The document's list (Devenir, Revenir, Monter, Rester, Sortir, Venir, Aller, Naitre, Descendre, Entrer, Rentrer, Tomber, Retourner, Arriver, Mourir, Partir) plus Passer = 17 verbs, which matches the standard mnemonic.
- **Sources**: [FluentU - DR MRS VANDERTRAMP](https://www.fluentu.com/blog/french/dr-mrs-vandertramp-verbs/), [Lingopie - DR MRS VANDERTRAMP](https://lingopie.com/blog/dr-mrs-vandertramp-guide/)

### Claim 5: All reflexive verbs use etre as auxiliary
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: This is a fundamental rule of French grammar confirmed across all sources. "Elle s'est levee" — all reflexive verbs form compound tenses with etre. The document correctly notes that reflexive verbs require past participle agreement with the reflexive pronoun when it functions as the direct object.
- **Sources**: Standard French grammar reference.

### Claim 6: Some verbs take either etre or avoir with meaning change (monter, descendre, sortir, rentrer, retourner, passer)
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: The transitive/intransitive split is well-documented: "monter" with avoir = to carry up ("J'ai monte les valises"), "monter" with etre = to go up ("Je suis monte"). This pattern applies to several verbs on the etre list when used transitively (with a direct object).
- **Sources**: [FluentU - DR MRS VANDERTRAMP](https://www.fluentu.com/blog/french/dr-mrs-vandertramp-verbs/)

### Claim 7: Past participle agrees with subject (etre) and with preceding direct object (avoir)
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Both agreement rules are universally confirmed: (1) with etre, the past participle agrees with the subject in gender and number; (2) with avoir, the past participle agrees with the preceding direct object if one exists. The avoir-preceding-DO agreement rule is widely noted as one of the most difficult French grammar rules, frequently violated by native speakers.
- **Sources**: Standard French grammar reference.

### Claim 8: The French subjunctive is actively used daily after specific triggers
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Lawless French, Kwiziq, and multiple pedagogical sources confirm that the subjunctive is mandatory after: il faut que (necessity), je veux que (desire), je doute que (doubt), je suis content que (emotion), and conjunctions like bien que, avant que, pour que, sans que, a moins que. The il faut que construction alone is used many times daily by French speakers, confirming the subjunctive is a living, productive part of the grammar.
- **Sources**: [Lawless French - Subjunctive](https://www.lawlessfrench.com/grammar/subjunctive/), [Kwiziq - Il faut que + subjunctive](https://french.kwiziq.com/revision/grammar/il-faut-que-is-always-followed-by-le-subjonctif-present-the-subjunctive-mood)

### Claim 9: The English subjunctive is nearly extinct, surviving only in formulaic expressions
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: The document's examples ("If I were you," "God save the King," "I suggest he leave") are the standard surviving subjunctive forms in English. The mandative subjunctive ("I suggest he leave") is more common in American English than British English. The overall decline of the English subjunctive is a well-documented linguistic fact.
- **Sources**: Standard English grammar reference.

### Claim 10: Si-clause system has three types with strict tense correspondence
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Lawless French, CliffsNotes, and multiple sources confirm the three-type system: (1) si + present -> future/present, (2) si + imperfect -> conditional, (3) si + plus-que-parfait -> conditional past. The critical rule that si + conditional is NEVER grammatical is confirmed across all sources as one of the strongest prescriptive rules in French.
- **Sources**: [Lawless French - Si Clauses](https://www.lawlessfrench.com/grammar/si-clauses-conditionals/), [CliffsNotes - Conditional Sentences](https://www.cliffsnotes.com/study-guides/french/french-ii/french-ii-conditional-mood-and-conditions/conditional-sentences)

### Claim 11: The passe simple is exclusively literary/written and never used in spoken French
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Wikipedia's passe simple article and multiple sources explicitly state that the passe simple "is never used in spoken French" and is "limited to formal writing, such as literature, journalism, and historical accounts." Lawless French confirms it is "a literary tense which is only used in formal written French."
- **Sources**: [Passe simple - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pass%C3%A9_simple), [Lawless French - Passe Simple](https://www.lawlessfrench.com/grammar/passe-simple/)

### Claim 12: French has NO progressive aspect ("je parle" covers both "I speak" and "I am speaking")
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: This is a well-established structural difference. French has no grammaticalized progressive construction equivalent to English "be + -ing." Context disambiguates between habitual and ongoing readings. The construction "etre en train de + infinitive" exists for emphasis but is not a systematic tense/aspect marker.
- **Sources**: Standard comparative linguistics reference.

### Claim 13: The conditional is debated as mood vs. tense
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: The document correctly notes this debate in its Research Notes. Traditional French grammar treats the conditional as a separate mood; modern analyses often classify it as a tense within the indicative (because its formation — future stem + imperfect endings — parallels other tense formations rather than mood formations). Both positions have scholarly support.
- **Sources**: Confirmed through French linguistics literature.

### Claim 14: The near-future "aller + infinitive" is increasingly replacing the future simple, paralleling English "going to"
- **Rating**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: This is a well-documented grammaticalization process in both languages. The periphrastic future (aller + infinitive / going to + infinitive) is gaining ground over the morphological future (future simple / will + verb) in both French and English, particularly in spoken registers.
- **Sources**: Standard grammaticalization literature.
