## CONTEXT 1: MEDICAL/HEALTHCARE ENCOUNTERS

### 1.1 The Doctor-Patient Power Asymmetry

The medical encounter is one of the most structurally asymmetric conversations in everyday life. The doctor controls the agenda, the time, the physical space, the specialized knowledge, and ultimately the decisions about treatment. Research consistently shows that this power imbalance shapes every aspect of the interaction:

**Structural features of the asymmetry:**
- The doctor decides when the appointment begins and ends
- The doctor controls topic shifts ("Now let's talk about...")
- The doctor has access to specialized vocabulary the patient may not understand
- The patient is in a vulnerable position (often undressed, in pain, anxious)
- The doctor's time is rationed and expensive; the patient's time is treated as less valuable
- The physical setup (doctor standing/sitting higher, patient on exam table) reinforces hierarchy

**How the asymmetry manifests in conversation:**
- Doctors interrupt patients within an average of 11-18 seconds of their opening statement
- Patients often defer to doctor's opinions even when they disagree
- Questions flow primarily in one direction (doctor asks, patient answers)
- The doctor frames the "official" interpretation of symptoms
- Patients frequently withhold information they consider embarrassing or unimportant

**Impact on autistic patients specifically:**
Research published in PMC (2024) found that autistic adults face compounded barriers: "Healthcare providers would either ignore the patient or address and talk to the support person instead of the patient directly." A 2024 study at the Waisman Center documented that "autistic adults worry their ailment wasn't bad enough, that they would be discounted based on their gender or diagnosis, or their style of communication would be misunderstood."

The fundamental rule: Despite the asymmetry, the patient has the right to ask questions, express disagreement, request tests, and seek second opinions. The asymmetry is real but not absolute.

### 1.2 How to Describe Symptoms Effectively

Effective symptom description follows a structured framework. Research from patient communication studies identifies the key components:

**The OLDCARTS Framework (used by medical professionals):**
- **O**nset: When did it start?
- **L**ocation: Where exactly is it?
- **D**uration: How long does it last?
- **C**haracter: What does it feel like? (sharp, dull, burning, aching, throbbing)
- **A**ggravating factors: What makes it worse?
- **R**elieving factors: What makes it better?
- **T**iming: Is it constant or intermittent? When does it occur?
- **S**everity: On a scale of 1-10, how bad is it?

**Practical approach for describing symptoms:**
1. Start with the main issue: "I'm here because..."
2. Explain when it started: "This began about [timeframe] ago"
3. Describe how it feels: Use concrete sensory language
4. Mention frequency and duration: "It happens [how often] and lasts [how long]"
5. Share what affects it: "It gets worse when... and better when..."
6. Rate the severity: "On a scale of 1 to 10, it's about a [number]"

**Example script for symptom description:**
"I'm here because I've been having headaches for about three weeks. They're on the right side of my head, behind my eye. The pain feels like a throbbing pressure. They happen about four times a week, usually in the afternoon, and last about two to three hours. They get worse with bright lights and better when I lie down in a dark room. On a scale of 1 to 10, I'd rate them about a 6 or 7."

**Why this matters for autistic individuals:**
Many autistic people have differences in interoception (the ability to sense internal body states). This can make it harder to locate pain precisely, describe its quality, or rate its severity. Writing down symptoms before the appointment is especially helpful. A written list serves as both a memory aid and a communication tool that bypasses the real-time processing demands of spoken conversation.

### 1.3 How to Ask Questions of Your Doctor

**Structural principle:** Questions from patients are legitimate and expected, but they follow pragmatic conventions:

**Timing:**
- After the doctor finishes explaining something (not during)
- Before the doctor moves to leave the room
- During designated "any questions?" moments

**Framing:**
- Preface with acknowledgment: "I understand what you've said, and I have a question about..."
- Use hedging to soften: "I was wondering if..." or "Could you explain..."
- Ask for clarification: "When you say [medical term], what exactly does that mean?"
- Request repetition: "Could you say that again? I want to make sure I understand."

**Key questions patients should ask:**
1. "What do you think is causing this?"
2. "What tests do you recommend, and what will they tell us?"
3. "What are my treatment options?"
4. "What are the risks and benefits of each option?"
5. "What happens if I don't treat this?"
6. "When should I come back, or what should I watch for?"

**The teach-back method:** Doctors are trained to use teach-back ("Can you tell me what you understood about the treatment plan?"). Patients can use it too: "Let me make sure I understand correctly. You're saying that I should [repeat what you understood]. Is that right?"

### 1.4 How to Advocate for Yourself

Self-advocacy in medical settings involves specific pragmatic strategies. Research from the University of Washington Medicine and other healthcare institutions provides specific scripts:

**Requesting specific tests:**
- "I've been experiencing [symptoms], and I've read that [condition] can cause these. Would it be appropriate to test for that?"
- "I'd like to understand why we're not testing for [specific thing]. Can you walk me through your reasoning?"
- "Could you please document in my chart that I requested [test] and that it was declined, along with the reason?"

*Note: The last phrase is a known advocacy tool. Doctors are more likely to order a test or provide a thorough explanation when they know the refusal will be documented.*

**Requesting a second opinion:**
- "I appreciate your assessment. I'd like to get a second opinion before we proceed. Could you recommend another specialist?"
- "I've received this diagnosis, and I'd like another perspective. This isn't a reflection of my confidence in you; it's about making sure I have all the information I need."

**Disagreeing with your doctor (from UW Medicine research):**
- "I hear what you're recommending, but I'm not comfortable with that approach. Can we discuss alternatives?"
- "I've done some research and found [information]. I'd like to discuss this with you."
- "I don't feel like my concerns are being fully addressed. Can we take a step back?"

**When being dismissed:**
- "I understand this might not seem urgent, but this is significantly affecting my daily life."
- "I've been dealing with this for [time period] and it's getting worse. I need us to investigate further."
- "I appreciate your perspective, but I'd like to explore other explanations."

### 1.5 The "Medical Small Talk" Ritual

Research published in the journal Patient Education and Counseling and the American Academy of Family Physicians has specifically studied small talk in medical interactions:

**What it is:** Brief, non-medical conversation at the beginning and/or end of a medical appointment. It serves a pragmatic function beyond its content.

**The function:** "The primary function of these speech acts is social, not to say something but to do something, i.e., make contact, reaffirm shared membership in a common tribe, express positive feelings (and thus lack of threat), show concern." (PMC research on medical small talk)

**Typical pattern:**
1. Greeting: "Hi, how are you?" (This is NOT a medical question. The expected answer is "Good, thanks" or "Fine, how are you?")
2. Brief social exchange: "Did you have trouble finding parking?" / "How's work going?" / "How's the family?"
3. Transition to medical business: "So, what brings you in today?" (THIS is when you start describing symptoms)

**Why it matters:** "Just a few seconds of small talk at the beginning and end of each visit can accomplish a great deal." It builds trust, helps the doctor see you as a whole person, and creates a more collaborative dynamic.

**For autistic individuals:** 
- You don't need to be brilliant at small talk. A brief, genuine response is sufficient.
- "Good, thanks" is always an acceptable response to "How are you?"
- You can prepare one brief topic in advance (weather, a recent event, your weekend)
- If small talk is difficult, you can transition to the medical topic: "I'm doing okay, but I wanted to talk to you about [symptom]"
- It's okay to say: "I'm not great at small talk, but I'm glad to see you. I'd like to get started on why I'm here."

### 1.6 Navigating the Emotional Component

Medical encounters involve fear, vulnerability, and sometimes grief. The pragmatic system expects a certain management of these emotions:

**Expected norms:**
- It's acceptable to express worry or fear, but excessive emotional expression can overwhelm the clinical exchange
- Crying is socially acceptable and doctors are trained to respond to it
- Anger directed at the situation is acceptable; anger directed at the doctor is counterproductive
- Expressing gratitude is expected at the end of appointments

**Helpful phrases for managing emotions:**
- "I'm feeling really anxious about this. Can you help me understand what to expect?"
- "I'm scared about what this might mean. Can you walk me through the possibilities?"
- "I need a moment to process this."
- "This is a lot of information. Can I have some time to think about it before deciding?"

### 1.7 Autistic-Specific Guidance for Medical Settings

**Sensory challenges:**
Research published in PMC (2024) documents that "autistic adults face compounded barriers including sensitivity to the physical environment, communication difficulties, anxiety due to waiting, and sensory over-stimulation." Specific challenges include:
- Fluorescent lighting in waiting rooms and exam rooms
- Sounds: beeping equipment, other patients, intercoms
- Physical discomfort: paper gowns, exam table paper, cold instruments
- Smells: antiseptic, cleaning products
- Touch: physical examinations, blood draws, stethoscopes

**Communication accommodation requests:**
The AASPIRE Autism Healthcare Accommodation Tool and the National Autistic Society's Health Passport provide frameworks. You can request:
- Longer appointments
- Written instructions instead of or in addition to verbal ones
- Reduced waiting time (or waiting in a quieter area)
- Sensory accommodations (dimmed lights, permission to wear headphones)
- A support person in the room
- Communication by written notes or typed messages

**Script for requesting accommodations:**
"I'm autistic, and I have some communication and sensory needs that will help me get the most out of this appointment. Specifically, I find it helpful when [specific accommodation]. Would that be possible?"

"I have an autism spectrum condition. Bright lights and loud sounds are difficult for me. Is there a way to adjust the lighting in the exam room? Also, I process information better when it's written down. Could you provide written instructions for any treatment plan?"

**The masking problem:**
Research on autistic masking (PMC, 2024) establishes that "masking may conceal the person's need for support and can complicate a diagnosis" and that "clinicians need to be able to recognize masking behaviors so they can identify individuals with underlying social communication difficulties."

For autistic patients, masking in medical settings creates a specific danger: you may appear "fine" when you are not. Your trained social performance may convince doctors that your symptoms are less severe than they are.

**Counter-masking strategies:**
- Prepare a written symptom list that includes severity ratings (overrides the appearance of "being fine")
- Explicitly tell the doctor: "I tend to appear calmer than I feel. My written description is more accurate than how I might look right now."
- Bring a trusted person who can corroborate: "My partner/friend can tell you that I've been struggling significantly, even though I might not show it right now."
- Use the phrase: "I want to be clear that I'm not exaggerating. I know I might seem okay, but this is seriously affecting my daily life."

### 1.8 Complete Scripts for Medical Encounters

**Script 1: Making an appointment by phone**

You: "Hello, I'd like to make an appointment with Dr. [Name]."
Receptionist: "Sure. What's the appointment for?"
You: "I've been having [brief description - e.g., 'persistent headaches']. I'd like to discuss them with the doctor."
Receptionist: "Is this urgent or can it wait?"
You: [If urgent] "I'd like to be seen as soon as possible." [If not] "Within the next week or two would be fine."
Receptionist: "We have [date/time] available. Does that work?"
You: "Yes, that works. Thank you."
Receptionist: "Can I get your name and date of birth?"
You: [Provide information]
Receptionist: "Is there anything else?"
You: "Could you please schedule a longer appointment? I have several things to discuss." [Optional: "I'm autistic and I communicate better with more time."]

**Script 2: During the appointment - describing symptoms**

Doctor: "What brings you in today?"
You: "I've been having [main symptom] for about [timeframe]. It started [when/how]. It feels like [description]. It happens [frequency]. On a scale of 1 to 10, it's about [number]. It gets worse when [triggers] and better when [relievers]. I've tried [any self-treatment] and it [helped/didn't help]."

**Script 3: Asking for clarification**

Doctor: [Explains something using medical terminology]
You: "I want to make sure I understand. When you say [term], does that mean [your interpretation]?"
OR: "Could you explain that in simpler terms? I want to make sure I'm following."
OR: "I'm a visual learner. Do you have any diagrams or written materials about this?"

**Script 4: Requesting accommodation**

You: "Before we start, I want to let you know I'm autistic. A few things that help me in medical appointments: I communicate better if you speak slowly and give me a moment to process. I've written down my symptoms and questions [hand over paper]. I may not make eye contact, but I am listening. And bright overhead lights can be difficult for me - is there a way to reduce the lighting?"

## CONTEXT 2: EDUCATIONAL SETTINGS

### 2.1 Classroom Participation Norms

Classroom participation follows complex, often unspoken pragmatic rules that vary by setting, professor, and culture:

**When to speak:**
- When the professor asks a question to the class (signaled by a pause, looking around, or saying "Any thoughts?")
- During designated discussion periods
- When the professor says "Any questions?"
- NOT during lectures unless the professor has indicated questions are welcome

**How to signal you want to speak:**
- Raise your hand and wait to be called on (traditional, always safe)
- In some discussion classes, wait for a natural pause and begin speaking
- In seminars, make eye contact with the professor to indicate you want to contribute
- Online/hybrid: use the "raise hand" feature or type in chat

**How to answer questions:**
- Direct and concise answers are valued
- Build on what others have said: "Building on what [name] said..."
- Respectfully disagree: "I see it differently because..."
- It's okay to say "I'm not sure, but my thinking is..."
- Avoid monopolizing: if you've spoken twice, let others contribute before speaking again

**How to ask questions:**
- Genuine clarification questions are always welcome: "Could you explain what you mean by [concept]?"
- Questions that advance the discussion are valued: "How does this relate to [other concept]?"
- Avoid questions already answered on the syllabus or in readings
- Timing: ask during appropriate moments, not when the professor is mid-thought

**The unspoken rule of participation balance:**
Research from the University of Chicago's Center for Teaching identifies a core norm: "Share responsibility for including all voices in the conversation. If you tend to have a lot to say, make sure you leave sufficient space to hear from others. If you tend to stay quiet, challenge yourself to contribute."

### 2.2 Student-Professor Communication

**Email communication (the most common mode):**
This follows strict conventions. Research from Stanford, Purdue, UCI, and other universities consistently identifies these norms:

**Required elements:**
1. Professional subject line: "PSYCH 101 - Question about Assignment 3"
2. Formal greeting: "Dear Professor [Last Name]," or "Dear Dr. [Last Name],"
3. Self-identification: "My name is [Name], and I'm in your [Course] class, [Section/Time]."
4. Clear, concise body: State your question or request in 2-4 sentences
5. Polite closing: "Thank you for your time." / "I appreciate your help."
6. Sign-off: "Best regards," or "Sincerely," followed by your full name

**Example email:**
Subject: ENGL 201 - Question About Essay 2 Deadline

Dear Professor Johnson,

My name is Alex Chen, and I'm in your English 201 class (MWF 10:00 AM section). I wanted to ask about the deadline for Essay 2. I've been dealing with a health issue and want to know if an extension might be possible. I have documentation from my doctor if needed.

Thank you for your time.

Best regards,
Alex Chen

**Key don'ts:**
- Don't use "Hey" or "Hi" as a greeting
- Don't use texting language (u, thx, lol)
- Don't ask questions answered in the syllabus
- Don't email at 2 AM expecting an immediate response
- Don't use excessive exclamation points or emojis
- Don't write essay-length emails
- Allow 24-48 hours for a response before following up

**Office hours:**
- These are dedicated time for students to meet with professors
- You don't need a reason to visit; "I just wanted to discuss the course material" is sufficient
- Knock or check if the door is open; say "Hi, Professor [Name], do you have a moment?"
- Have specific questions or topics prepared
- Keep visits to 10-15 minutes unless the professor encourages more

**In-class communication:**
- Address the professor as "Professor [Last Name]" or "Dr. [Last Name]" unless they specifically say otherwise
- If you need to leave early, tell the professor before class begins: "I need to leave at [time] for a medical appointment. I'll sit near the door so I can leave quietly."

### 2.3 Group Work Pragmatics

Group work is one of the most pragmatically complex academic activities. Research from Carnegie Mellon, Texas A&M, and Georgia Tech provides frameworks:

**Dividing tasks:**
- Establish roles early: "Who wants to take on [specific part]?"
- Use skills-based assignment: "I'm strongest at research/writing/presenting. What about everyone else?"
- Create a shared document or plan with assigned responsibilities and deadlines
- Check in regularly: "How is everyone doing with their part?"

**Giving feedback in groups:**
- Use the "I noticed / I suggest" framework: "I noticed the introduction doesn't connect to our thesis. I suggest we add a transition sentence."
- Frame criticism constructively: "This section is strong. One thing that could make it even better is..."
- Avoid personal attacks: "This writing is unclear" (about the work) vs. "You're a bad writer" (about the person)

**Navigating disagreement:**
- Acknowledge the other perspective: "I see your point, and..."
- Present your view as an addition, not a correction: "Another way to look at this might be..."
- Suggest compromise: "What if we combined both approaches?"
- If stuck, defer to evidence: "Let's look at what the research/assignment requirements say"
- Know when to let go: not every disagreement is worth fighting

**Handling unequal contribution:**
- Address early and directly: "I noticed you haven't submitted your section yet. Is everything okay? Do you need help?"
- Document contributions: keep records of who did what
- If problems persist, involve the professor: "We've tried to address this within the group, but we're having difficulty with equitable contribution."

### 2.4 The High School to College Shift

The transition from high school to college involves significant pragmatic shifts:

**High school norms:**
- Teachers structure your time and remind you of deadlines
- Rules are explicit and enforced externally
- Communication is somewhat informal
- Parents are involved in your education
- Attendance is strictly monitored

**College norms:**
- You are responsible for managing your own schedule and deadlines
- Expectations are often implicit ("You should know this")
- Communication is more formal (especially email)
- Parents are not contacted (FERPA protections)
- Attendance may not be taken, but absence has consequences
- You must self-advocate for accommodations and support

**Key pragmatic shifts:**
- From being told what to do to figuring it out from the syllabus
- From raising your hand to joining a discussion
- From "my teacher" to "Professor [Name]"
- From parents calling the school to you contacting the disability office
- From being given extensions to requesting them in advance
- From being reminded to checking your email and the course management system daily

Research from multiple universities confirms that a significant disconnect exists between what high schools teach and what colleges expect from incoming students, with nearly 20% of students reporting difficulty adjusting to social aspects of college life.

### 2.5 Academic Register Expectations

**What academic register means:**
Academic register is the formal style of language used in educational settings. Research from multiple writing centers identifies its features:

- Third person preferred over first person (though this varies by field)
- No contractions ("cannot" not "can't")
- No slang or colloquialisms
- Precise, specialized vocabulary
- Hedging language: "This suggests..." rather than "This proves..."
- Citation and attribution: "According to Smith (2024)..."
- Objective tone: present evidence, not personal opinions (unless specifically asked)
- Complex sentence structures with subordinate clauses

**Speaking in academic register:**
- In class discussions: "I think the evidence suggests..." rather than "I feel like..."
- In presentations: use formal vocabulary, avoid filler words ("um," "like")
- In office hours: slightly less formal than written work, but still professional

**When formality varies:**
- Lab classes may be more informal
- Creative writing classes may encourage personal voice
- Some professors explicitly invite informal discussion ("Call me by my first name")
- Always match the professor's lead on formality level

### 2.6 Autistic-Specific Guidance for Educational Settings

**The class participation grade challenge:**
Research shows that many autistic students struggle with spontaneous verbal participation due to social anxiety, processing time differences, and sensory challenges. The class participation grade can be particularly unfair to autistic students.

**Accommodation options:**
- Request alternative participation formats through disability services (written reflections, online discussion boards, small group instead of whole class)
- Ask for notice of discussion topics in advance to prepare contributions
- Request that participation grades account for quality over quantity
- Some students negotiate one-on-one check-ins with the professor as an alternative

**Disclosure decisions:**
Research from the Organization for Autism Research and AANE outlines the key considerations:

You do NOT need to disclose your autism to professors. The disability services office will communicate your accommodations without revealing your diagnosis. However, you may choose to disclose if:
- You want the professor to understand your behavior (limited eye contact, different participation style)
- You need accommodations beyond what disability services provides
- You have a professor who seems confused by your communication style

**Script for disclosure (if you choose):**
"Professor [Name], I wanted to let you know that I'm autistic. This sometimes affects how I participate in class. For example, I may need extra processing time before responding to questions, and I communicate better in writing than speaking. I'm registered with disability services and have [specific accommodations]. I'm telling you this so you have context for how I engage in your class."

**Script for requesting accommodations without disclosure:**
"Professor [Name], I'm registered with the disability services office. Here's my accommodation letter. I'd like to discuss how these accommodations would work in your class. Specifically, I have [list accommodations]. How would you prefer to implement these?"

**Managing the sensory environment:**
- Sit near the door for easy exit if overwhelmed
- Choose seats away from fluorescent lights or windows (depending on light sensitivity)
- Use noise-canceling headphones between classes
- Arrive early to settle in before the room fills with noise and movement
- Know where quiet spaces are on campus

**Group work strategies:**
- Volunteer for roles that play to your strengths (research, writing, organizing)
- Propose communication through shared documents or messaging rather than only in-person meetings
- Set clear expectations early: "I work best with clear deadlines and written communication"
- If overwhelmed, communicate directly: "I need to take a short break" or "I'm processing what was said; give me a moment"

## CONTEXT 3: SERVICE ENCOUNTERS

### 3.1 Restaurant Ordering: The Full Script

The restaurant service encounter follows a highly predictable script. Understanding this script reduces anxiety and provides a roadmap:

**Phase 1: Arrival and Seating**

Host: "Hi, welcome to [Restaurant]. How many in your party?"
You: "[Number], please."
Host: "Do you have a reservation?"
You: "Yes, under [Name]" OR "No, we don't."
Host: "Right this way." [Leads you to table]
You: "Thank you."

*At this point, the host may say: "Your server will be right with you." This means you wait.*

**Phase 2: Initial Server Contact**

Server: "Hi, my name is [Name] and I'll be taking care of you today. Can I start you off with something to drink?"
You: "I'll have [drink], please." OR "Just water for now, thanks."
Server: "Are you ready to order, or do you need a few minutes?"
You: "We need a few minutes, please." OR "I'm ready."

*If you need time, the server will return. You don't need to flag them down.*

**Phase 3: Ordering**

Server: "What can I get for you?"
You: "I'll have the [dish name], please."
Server: "How would you like that cooked?" (for steaks, burgers)
You: "Medium, please." (or your preference)
Server: "Any sides with that?"
You: "[Side choice], please."
Server: "Great. Anything else?"
You: "That's all, thank you."

**Modification requests:**
- "Could I get the [dish] without [ingredient]? I'm allergic to / I don't eat [ingredient]."
- "Is it possible to substitute [side A] for [side B]?"
- "How spicy is the [dish]? I'd prefer something mild."

**Phase 4: During the Meal**

Server: "How is everything?" (This is a check-in, usually once during the meal)
You: "Everything is great, thank you." OR "Actually, could I get some more [water/bread/etc.]?"

*If there's a problem:*
You: "Excuse me, I think there might be a mix-up with my order. I ordered [X] but received [Y]."
OR: "This is a bit [cold/undercooked/overcooked]. Would it be possible to [have it remade/warmed up]?"

**Phase 5: Finishing and Paying**

Server: "Can I get you anything else? Dessert? Coffee?"
You: "No, thank you. We're all set." OR "Just the check, please."

*The server will bring the check. You do not need to wave them down in most restaurants; they will come.*

**Paying:**
- Place credit card or cash in the check holder
- Server takes it and returns with receipt
- For credit card: fill in tip amount, total, and sign
- For cash: if you need change, say "I'll need change, please." If you're paying with cash and the change IS the tip, say "Keep the change."

**Phase 6: Leaving**

You: "Thank you, everything was great." (to server if you pass them)
Host: "Thanks for coming in! Have a great night."
You: "Thanks, you too."

### 3.2 Retail Interactions

**Browsing vs. buying:**

When you enter a store, a staff member will often approach with: "Hi, can I help you find anything?" or "Is there anything I can help you with?"

**If you're browsing:**
- "No thanks, I'm just looking." (The standard phrase. This is universally understood and not rude.)
- "I'm good for now, thanks. I'll let you know if I need anything."

**If you want help:**
- "Actually, yes. I'm looking for [item]."
- "Do you carry [brand/type]?"
- "I'm looking for a gift for [person/occasion]. Any suggestions?"

**Returning items:**
The key pragmatic principle: be polite but clear about what you want.

Script for returns:
You: "Hi, I'd like to return this [item]."
Staff: "Sure. Do you have the receipt?"
You: "Yes, here it is." / "No, I don't, but I paid with this card."
Staff: "Is there a reason for the return?"
You: "It didn't fit / It wasn't what I expected / It's defective." (One brief reason is sufficient; no need to over-explain)
Staff: "Would you like a refund or an exchange?"
You: "A refund, please." / "I'd like to exchange it for [alternative]."

**Declining persistent salespeople:**
- "Thank you, but I'm just browsing today."
- "I appreciate the help, but I'd like to look on my own for now."
- "I'll think about it and come back." (This is a socially acceptable way to leave without buying)
- A firm but polite "No, thank you" repeated if necessary

### 3.3 Phone Calls to Businesses

Phone calls are one of the most challenging service encounters for many autistic adults. Research from Philippa Howard and Felicity Sedgewick (2021) found that "phone calls were the least popular communication method" among 245 autistic adults studied, primarily because they remove visual cues, are unpredictable, and require real-time processing without preparation time.

**The basic phone call script:**

Automated system: "Thank you for calling [Business]. Press 1 for [X], press 2 for [Y]..."
*Listen to ALL options before pressing. The one you need might be last.*
*If no option fits, pressing 0 or saying "representative" often connects you to a person.*

When you reach a person:
Them: "Thank you for calling [Business], this is [Name]. How can I help you?"
You: "Hi, my name is [Your Name]. I'm calling about [reason]."

**Navigating being put on hold:**
- "I need to place you on hold for a moment" = standard procedure, not punishment
- You can ask: "How long do you think the hold will be?"
- If transferred: you may need to re-explain your issue. Write down your key points before calling.

**Tips for autistic adults making phone calls:**
- Write a script before calling with your key points and possible questions
- Have your account number, name, and relevant details ready
- Call during off-peak hours (early morning, mid-afternoon) for shorter wait times
- Ask: "Can I handle this online or by email instead?" Many businesses now offer alternatives
- If a call is too overwhelming, ask: "Can I call back? I need a moment to gather my information."
- If possible, use speakerphone so your hands are free to take notes

### 3.4 Tipping Culture and Its Pragmatic Dimensions

Tipping in the United States is not optional in many contexts; it is a pragmatic expectation that functions as part of workers' wages.

**The rule system:**

| Context | Expected Tip | Notes |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| Full-service restaurant | 18-20% of pre-tax bill | 15% = bare minimum; 25%+ = exceptional service |
| Buffet restaurant | 10% | Less service involved |
| Takeout | 0-10% | Not required but increasingly expected |
| Coffee shop/counter service | $1-2 or 15-20% on screen | Optional but becoming more expected |
| Bar | $1-2 per drink, or 18-20% of tab | For cocktails, 20% |
| Delivery (food) | 15-20% or $5 minimum | Higher in bad weather |
| Ride-share (Uber/Lyft) | 15-20% | Through the app |
| Taxi | 15-20% | Cash preferred |
| Hairdresser/barber | 18-20% | Plus $2-5 for the person who washes your hair |
| Hotel housekeeping | $2-5 per night | Left daily on the nightstand |
| Movers | $20-50 per mover | Cash, at end of job |
| Valet parking | $2-5 | When your car is returned |

**Pragmatic dimensions:**
- Tipping is semi-mandatory in restaurants; not tipping is a serious social violation
- Under federal law, many tipped workers earn only $2.13/hour base wage
- Automatic gratuity (15-18%) is often added for groups of 6+
- When presented with a tip screen at checkout (coffee shops, fast-casual), you can select "No tip" or "Custom amount." This is socially awkward but acceptable for counter service.
- "Keep the change" means the change IS the tip

**When to tip more:**
- Large groups
- Complicated orders or special requests
- Holidays
- Bad weather (for delivery)
- Exceptional service

**When tipping is NOT expected:**
- Fast food restaurants
- Self-serve situations
- Most retail purchases
- Doctor's offices, government offices

### 3.5 Complaining About Service: The Escalation Ladder

Complaints follow a pragmatic escalation structure. Starting too high appears aggressive; starting too low may not resolve the issue.

**Level 1: Direct, polite mention to the person providing service**
- "Excuse me, I think there's a problem with [specific issue]."
- "I'm sorry to bother you, but [describe problem]."
- Stay calm and factual. The goal is to give them a chance to fix it.

**Level 2: Specific request for resolution**
- "I'd like [specific resolution - a replacement, a discount, a correction]."
- "What can you do to resolve this?"
- "I don't think this meets what was advertised/promised."

**Level 3: Ask for a manager/supervisor**
- "I appreciate your trying to help, but I think this needs to be escalated. Could I speak with a manager?"
- Restate the problem for the manager calmly: "Here's what happened. I've already spoken with [person]. I'd like [resolution]."

**Level 4: Formal complaint**
- Written complaint (email/letter) to corporate
- Online review (mention specific facts, not emotions)
- Filing with consumer protection agencies

**Key pragmatic principles for complaining:**
- Stay calm. Anger reduces your effectiveness.
- Be specific about the problem and the desired resolution.
- Document everything: dates, names, what was said.
- "I" statements work better than "you" statements: "I received the wrong order" vs. "You gave me the wrong order"
- Express disappointment, not rage: "I'm disappointed because I usually have a great experience here."
- Thank people who help you, even during a complaint.

### 3.6 Autistic-Specific Guidance for Service Encounters

**Sensory challenges in service environments:**
Research published in PMC (2023) describes retail environments as "a big spider web of things" for autistic adults. Specific challenges include:
- Fluorescent lighting
- Background music
- Multiple conversations
- Strong smells (perfume counters, food courts, cleaning products)
- Crowds and unpredictable movement of people
- Being touched or bumped
- Checkout beeping sounds

**Strategies:**
- Shop during quiet hours (early morning, late evening, or designated sensory-friendly hours like Walmart's 8-10 AM)
- Wear noise-canceling headphones
- Use self-checkout to avoid prolonged interaction
- Make a list before going to minimize decision-making in-store
- Use online ordering with in-store pickup when possible

**Script reliance:**
Many autistic people benefit from having predictable scripts for service encounters. The restaurant script, the retail browsing script, and the phone call script above are designed exactly for this purpose. Having these internalized reduces the cognitive load of real-time social processing.

**When service workers chat:**
Service workers, especially in slower settings like hair salons, retail stores, or checkout lines, sometimes initiate conversation. This is part of their job and social convention, but it can be draining.

Minimal but polite responses that don't invite further conversation:
- "Yeah, it's been nice weather!" (brief agreement)
- "Oh, that's interesting." (acknowledgment without elaboration)
- "Mhm." + smile (minimal engagement)
- If you want to opt out: "Sorry, I'm a bit tired today" or "I'm not feeling very chatty today, but thank you"
- Wearing visible headphones/earbuds is a socially understood signal for "I don't want to talk"

## CONTEXT 4: FRIENDSHIP AND RELATIONSHIP COMMUNICATION

### 4.1 How Friendships Are Initiated and Maintained

**Friendship initiation follows predictable stages:**

**Stage 1: Proximity and repeated contact**
- Friendships typically start through repeated exposure: classmates, colleagues, neighbors, fellow club members
- Research from the University of Kansas shows it takes 40-60 hours of contact to form a casual friendship and 200+ hours for a close friendship
- The "11-6-3 rule": approximately 11 face-to-face interactions, 3 hours each, over about 6 months to develop a friendship

**Stage 2: Discovery of common ground**
- Conversation moves from surface topics (the shared context) to personal interests
- Finding shared interests or values is the catalyst
- "Oh, you like [thing] too?" moments

**Stage 3: Escalation through self-disclosure**
- Gradually sharing more personal information
- This must be reciprocal: if you share something personal, the other person shares something back
- Going too deep too fast feels overwhelming; staying too surface feels distant
- The balance: match the other person's depth of disclosure

**Stage 4: Maintenance**
- Regular contact (see below)
- Mutual support during difficulties
- Shared experiences and memories
- The friendship develops its own norms and patterns

**Friendship maintenance across mediums:**

| Medium | Appropriate for | Frequency norms | Social expectations |
|--------|----------------|----------------|---------------------|
| Text message | Quick updates, sharing links/memes, casual plans | Varies hugely by relationship; close friends might text daily, casual friends weekly | Reply within a few hours to a day; it's acceptable to not reply instantly |
| Phone call | Longer conversations, emotional support, catching up | Less frequent than texting; once a week to once a month for close friends | Usually scheduled or preceded by a text asking "Can you talk?" |
| Video call | When you can't meet in person, for deeper connection | Usually scheduled in advance | Treat like an in-person hangout |
| Social media | Passive maintenance, staying aware of each other's lives | Likes and comments maintain low-level connection | Don't rely on this as your only form of contact |
| In person | Deepest connection, shared activities | Weekly for close local friends, less for others | The gold standard; prioritize when possible |

**Key research insight:** "While frequent texting maintains contact, it does not build emotional closeness as effectively as real-time conversation." Face-to-face and voice/video interactions create more meaningful connection than text alone.

### 4.2 The Pragmatics of Inviting (and Being Invited)

**Making invitations:**

Casual invitations:
- "Hey, do you want to grab coffee sometime this week?"
- "A few of us are going to [place] on Saturday. Want to come?"
- "I was thinking about seeing [movie]. Would you be interested?"

More formal invitations:
- "I'm having a small dinner party on Friday. I'd love for you to join us."
- "We're celebrating [occasion] and would like to invite you."

**Key pragmatic rules for inviting:**
- Be specific about time, place, and activity (vague invitations feel insincere: "We should hang out sometime" is weaker than "Want to get lunch on Thursday?")
- Don't put the other person in an awkward position (avoid inviting in front of others who aren't invited)
- Make it easy to decline: "No pressure if you're busy" or "Totally understand if you can't make it"

**Accepting invitations:**
- "I'd love to! What time?"
- "That sounds great. I'll be there."
- "Thanks for thinking of me! I'm in."
- Respond within 24-48 hours

**Declining invitations:**
- Express gratitude: "Thank you so much for inviting me."
- Provide a brief reason: "I already have plans that day" / "I'm not feeling up to socializing right now"
- Suggest an alternative (if genuine): "I can't make Saturday, but how about next week?"
- You do NOT need to over-explain or provide extensive justification

**The "soft no" vs. "hard no":**
- "I'll try to make it" = probably not coming (soft no that avoids direct rejection)
- "Let me check my schedule" = might be a soft no, or might be genuine
- "I can't that day" = firm but open to future invitations
- "I don't think that's my thing" = polite but clear decline of the activity type
- "I'd rather not" = direct decline; may seem blunt in some contexts

### 4.3 Maintaining Contact

**How often to reach out (general norms):**
- Close friends: at least weekly (text), meeting in person at least monthly
- Good friends: every 1-2 weeks (text), monthly or bimonthly in person
- Casual friends: once a month or less, but reliably
- Acquaintances: occasional, context-driven

**What to say to maintain contact:**
- Share something that reminded you of them: "I saw this and thought of you"
- Check in: "Hey, how have you been? I've been thinking about you"
- React to their social media: comment on a post, then follow up in DM
- Make plans: "It's been a while. Want to catch up over [activity]?"
- Share news: "Guess what happened..." (invites engagement)

**The reciprocity principle:** Friendship maintenance should be roughly equal. If you consistently initiate and the other person never does, it may indicate lower investment on their part. However, some people are poor initiators but very responsive; judge by their enthusiasm when you do reach out, not just by who texts first.

### 4.4 Navigating Conflict in Friendships

**Recognition that conflict is normal:**
Every friendship experiences disagreement. The pragmatic skill is not avoiding conflict but managing it in a way that preserves the relationship.

**Conflict scripts for friendships:**

**Addressing a problem:**
- "Hey, can we talk about something? I've been feeling [emotion] about [situation]."
- "I value our friendship, and I want to bring up something that's been on my mind."
- "This is hard for me to say, but I think we need to talk about [issue]."

**During the conversation:**
- Use "I" statements: "I felt hurt when [specific action]" rather than "You always [accusation]"
- Stay specific: "When you canceled our plans last Tuesday without much notice" rather than "You never follow through"
- Listen without interrupting
- Acknowledge their perspective: "I can see how you might have seen it that way"
- Seek resolution: "How can we make this work better going forward?"

**After the conflict:**
- Allow time for both people to process
- Follow up: "I'm glad we talked about that. Are we okay?"
- Resume normal interaction; don't let the conflict define the relationship
- Some friendships may not survive certain conflicts, and that's okay

### 4.5 Romantic Relationship Communication

**4.5.1 The Pragmatics of Dating**

**Expressing interest (flirting):**
Research from linguistics and pragmatics identifies flirting as a communication style characterized by strategic ambiguity. The core pragmatic function: "Flirters seem to presuppose the existence of intimacy, and they do this by saying and doing things that would only be acceptable and appropriate if such intimacy already existed."

Key linguistic features of flirting:
- **Imagined future:** Creating hypothetical scenarios involving both people ("We should go to that new restaurant" / "You would love this place I know")
- **Playful teasing:** Light, friendly mockery that signals comfort and closeness
- **Compliments:** Noticing and commenting on specific things about the person
- **Increased attention:** Asking more questions, remembering details, following up on things they mentioned
- **Physical proximity:** Standing or sitting closer than normal
- **Vocal changes:** Softer voice, more laughter, warmer tone

**Reading interest vs. friendliness:**
This is one of the most difficult pragmatic distinctions. Some indicators of romantic interest (as opposed to friendliness):
- They seek out one-on-one time specifically with you
- They initiate contact frequently
- Physical touch increases (touching arm, leaning in, prolonged eye contact)
- They ask about your relationship status
- They make future plans involving just the two of you
- They respond to your messages quickly and with enthusiasm
- They remember small details you mentioned

**Important caveat:** None of these signals is definitive. The only reliable way to know is through direct communication: "I enjoy spending time with you. Would you like to go on a date sometime?"

**4.5.2 Relationship maintenance conversation:**
Research on relationship satisfaction identifies key communication practices:

- **Daily check-ins:** "How was your day?" (This is a ritual question that signals investment, not just curiosity)
- **Expressing appreciation:** "Thank you for [specific thing]" / "I really appreciated when you [action]"
- **Discussing plans together:** Making decisions as a team
- **Sharing emotions:** "I'm feeling stressed about [thing]" (bidding for support)
- **Responding to bids:** When your partner shares something, respond with engagement, not dismissal

**4.5.3 Discussing needs and boundaries:**
Research from multiple relationship psychology sources provides frameworks:

Structure for discussing needs:
1. Identify the behavior: "When [specific behavior happens]..."
2. State the impact: "I feel [emotion]..."
3. Express the need: "I need [specific need]..."
4. Offer a path forward: "Would you be willing to [action]?"

Example: "When we spend every weekend with your family, I feel drained because I don't get enough time to recharge. I need at least one weekend a month to ourselves. Would you be willing to try that?"

Boundary-setting phrases:
- "I love you, and I also need [specific boundary]."
- "This is important to me: [boundary]. Can we talk about how to make this work?"
- "I'm not comfortable with [situation]. Here's what I need instead: [alternative]."

### 4.6 Autistic-Specific Guidance for Friendship and Relationships

**Difficulty with initiation:**
Research consistently finds that autistic adults desire friendships but face specific barriers in initiation. Key findings from PMC research (2024):
- "Autistic adults face distinct challenges in friendships, such as difficulty initiating interactions"
- Success is most likely when "atypical behavior was normalized, specifically occurring among friends who accepted and appreciated their social differences and through common interests"

**Strategies for initiation:**
- Use shared activities as a framework (interest-based friendship reduces the need for "pure socializing")
- Start with lower-pressure contexts: online communities, structured activities, classes
- Practice a simple initiation script: "Hey, I noticed you're into [shared interest]. Have you [related question]?"
- Propose activities with built-in structure: "Want to go to [specific event]?" rather than "Want to hang out sometime?"

**Maintaining friendships:**
Research from Crompton et al. (2020) found that autistic adults "never realised everybody felt as happy as I do when I am around autistic people," suggesting that autistic-autistic friendships may be easier to maintain.

For maintaining friendships:
- Set reminders to reach out (if initiation doesn't come naturally)
- Be honest about your capacity: "I'm not great at staying in touch, but it doesn't mean I don't care about you"
- Use text-based communication when it works better for you
- Quality over quantity: some friendships survive with less frequent contact

**Reading romantic interest:**
Research confirms that autistic adults often struggle with interpreting romantic signals. Practical approaches:
- Ask directly when uncertain: "I really enjoy our time together. I want to make sure I'm reading the situation right. Are you interested in me romantically?"
- Trust patterns over single signals: one text doesn't mean anything; a pattern of seeking you out, initiating contact, and making time for you is more meaningful
- Involve trusted friends: "I'm not sure if [person] is interested in me. What do you think?"

**The "social battery" and friendship maintenance:**
Research published in PMC (2024) confirms: "The 'autistic social battery' refers to the depletion of energy that comes from social interactions." Key insights:

- Masking drains the battery fastest; authentic interactions drain it less
- "If you consistently push past the limits of your social battery, you risk entering autistic burnout"
- Communicate using the social battery metaphor: "I can handle about two hours of socializing before my battery gets low"
- Plan recovery time after social events
- Friendships that allow for authenticity are more sustainable

**Managing boundaries:**
- It's okay to say: "I need to leave/rest/be alone for a while"
- Set expectations early: "I'm someone who needs alone time to recharge. It's not about you."
- Schedule social time deliberately rather than being available for spontaneous requests
- Use exit strategies: "I have to leave by [time]" gives a built-in endpoint

## CONTEXT 5: AUTHORITY AND INSTITUTIONAL INTERACTIONS

### 5.1 Police Encounters

**CRITICAL SAFETY INFORMATION:**

Research from the American Journal of Preventive Medicine and multiple advocacy organizations documents that autistic individuals face significantly elevated risks during police encounters. Key statistics:
- People with disabilities are 7 times more likely to have police encounters
- 53% of autistic adults in one study had 4+ lifetime police interactions
- Nearly 20% of autistic individuals will be stopped and questioned by police by age 21
- Autistic behaviors (stimming, avoiding eye contact, echolalia) are frequently misinterpreted as resistance, intoxication, or evasion

**What to say during a police encounter:**

If stopped while driving:
1. Pull over safely, turn off engine
2. Keep hands visible (on steering wheel)
3. Wait for the officer to approach and speak first
4. Provide license, registration, and insurance when asked
5. You can say: "My license is in my wallet. I'm going to reach for it now." (Narrating your actions prevents misinterpretation)

**Your rights (exercise them calmly):**

Right to remain silent:
- "I am exercising my right to remain silent." (You MUST say this explicitly; silent compliance is not the same as invoking the right. Per Salinas v. Texas, 2013.)
- After providing identification (required in most states), you do not have to answer additional questions

Right to refuse searches:
- "Officer, I do not consent to searches." (Say this calmly and do not physically resist)

Right to an attorney:
- "I am remaining silent and I want a lawyer." (This invokes both the 5th and 6th Amendment protections)
- Note: the right to an attorney begins after arrest, not during a traffic stop

**What NOT to do:**
- Do not reach for anything without announcing it first
- Do not argue, even if you believe the stop is unjustified
- Do not run or physically resist
- Do not make sudden movements
- Do not consent to searches

**De-escalation language:**
- "Yes, officer" / "No, officer" (brief, respectful responses)
- "I understand" (acknowledgment without admission)
- "I'd like to cooperate. How would you like me to proceed?"

### 5.2 Legal Settings

**Court behavior:**
Courts follow extremely rigid pragmatic rules. Research from multiple court systems provides consistent guidance:

**Addressing the judge:**
- Always use "Your Honor" (never "Judge," "sir," "ma'am," or the judge's name)
- Stand when speaking to the judge
- Stand when the judge enters or exits the courtroom

**Speaking rules:**
- Never interrupt anyone, especially the judge
- Speak clearly and at a moderate pace
- Address answers to whoever asked the question
- Respond with "Yes, Your Honor" or "No, Your Honor"
- If you don't understand a question: "Your Honor, could you please repeat/clarify that question?"

**Physical demeanor:**
- No facial reactions to testimony or rulings (no sighing, eye-rolling, head-shaking)
- Sit up straight
- No food, drinks, hats, or electronics
- Dress formally (business attire)

**Speaking to lawyers:**
- Be completely honest (your lawyer cannot help you if they don't know the truth)
- Answer questions directly; do not volunteer extra information
- Prepare written notes if you have difficulty organizing verbal information
- Ask for clarification: "Can you explain what that means in plain language?"

### 5.3 Government Offices

Bureaucratic communication has its own pragmatic system:

**General norms:**
- Expect waiting; bring patience and something to occupy your time
- Have all required documents ready before you approach the counter
- Be prepared to state your purpose concisely: "I'm here to [specific task]"
- Follow instructions exactly, even if they seem redundant
- The worker behind the counter has limited authority; be patient with the process

**DMV script:**
You: [Approach counter when called] "Hi, I'm here to [renew my license / register a vehicle / etc.]."
Clerk: "Do you have [specific documents]?"
You: [Hand them over] "Yes, here they are."
Clerk: "Fill out this form and take a seat."
*Fill out the form, return it, wait.*

**Social Security Office / Government Benefits:**
- Call ahead or check online for required documents
- Bring identification, Social Security card, and any relevant correspondence
- State your business clearly: "I received a letter asking me to come in about [topic]"
- If denied something, ask: "What is the appeals process?" and "Can I get that in writing?"

**Key pragmatic principle:** Government workers process hundreds of people daily. Being polite, prepared, and concise maximizes your chances of a smooth interaction. Expressing frustration at the worker personally is counterproductive; they don't control the system.

### 5.4 Emergency Situations: Calling 911

**The script for calling 911:**

Dispatcher: "911, what's your emergency?"
You: "[Type of emergency]: [Brief description]."

Examples:
- "I need an ambulance. Someone has collapsed and isn't breathing. I'm at [address]."
- "There's a fire at [address]. It's in the kitchen of a house."
- "I need police. Someone is trying to break into my house. I'm at [address]."

Dispatcher: "What is your location?"
You: [Provide the most specific address or landmark you can]

Dispatcher: "What is your name and callback number?"
You: [Provide information]

**Critical rules:**
1. State location FIRST. This is the most important information.
2. Describe the emergency briefly and clearly.
3. Stay on the line until the dispatcher tells you to hang up.
4. Follow the dispatcher's instructions (they may guide you through first aid).
5. Speak slowly and clearly.

**What the dispatcher will need:**
- Location (address, intersection, landmark)
- Nature of emergency (medical, fire, crime)
- Number of people involved
- Description of what happened
- Your phone number

### 5.5 Autistic-Specific Guidance for Authority Interactions

**The danger of miscommunication with police:**
Research documents specific ways autistic behaviors are misinterpreted:
- Stimming (hand-flapping, rocking) mistaken for aggression or drug use
- Avoiding eye contact interpreted as dishonesty or evasion
- Echolalia or unusual speech patterns misread as non-compliance or disrespect
- Literal interpretation of questions leading to "wrong" answers
- Sensory overload causing shutdown, interpreted as resistance
- Tone differences (flat affect) interpreted as hostility or lack of cooperation

**The autism awareness/alert card:**
Multiple states have developed programs:
- Pennsylvania: Official informational card for police encounters
- New York: Visor Card Program
- Massachusetts: Blue Envelope Program

**What to include on a card:**
- "I am autistic / I have autism spectrum disorder"
- "I may not make eye contact. This is not disrespect."
- "I may need extra time to process and respond to questions."
- "I may stim (move my hands, rock, or make repetitive movements). This is not a threat."
- "Please speak slowly and clearly."
- "I may become overwhelmed by loud sounds, bright lights, or physical contact."
- Contact information for a trusted person

**Script for presenting the card:**
"Officer, I have an identification card that I'd like to show you. I'm going to reach into my [pocket/wallet] to get it." Then hand over the card.

**Requesting accommodation in legal/institutional settings:**
- "I have a disability that affects my communication. I need [extra time / written instructions / a quiet space]."
- "I need a support person present for this interaction."
- "I process information better when it's written down. Can you provide that?"
- Under the ADA, government offices and courts must provide reasonable accommodations

**Calling 911 as an autistic person:**
- If verbal communication is difficult under stress, consider: Text to 911 (available in many areas), ICE (In Case of Emergency) cards on your phone, medical alert devices, or smart home devices that can call 911
- Practice the script during calm moments
- Have your address written down near you at all times (in case processing abilities decrease under stress)
- If calling for someone else who is autistic, tell the dispatcher: "The person who needs help is autistic. They may not respond to verbal commands. Please approach calmly and avoid loud sirens when possible."

### 5.6 Preparing for Institutional Interactions

**General preparation strategies for autistic individuals:**
1. Research what to expect BEFORE the interaction (what documents, what the process involves)
2. Write down your key points and questions
3. Bring a support person if possible
4. Know your rights (under ADA, you can request accommodations)
5. Allow extra time (so you're not rushed)
6. Plan sensory management (headphones for waiting, sunglasses for fluorescent lights)
7. Have an exit strategy (if overwhelmed, "I need to take a break and come back")

## CONTEXT 6: FAMILY COMMUNICATION

### 6.1 Parent-Child Dynamics (As an Adult Child)

The transition from child to adult changes the parent-child communication dynamic, but the old patterns often persist:

**What changes (pragmatically):**
- Parents should shift from directing to advising
- Adult children can set boundaries that weren't possible as children
- Communication becomes more peer-like (though never fully equal)
- Decisions about your life are yours to make; parental input becomes optional

**Common pragmatic challenges:**
- Parents who still use a directive tone: "You should..." / "You need to..."
- Unsolicited advice about career, relationships, lifestyle
- Criticism disguised as concern: "I'm just worried that..."
- Expectations of obligation: "After everything I've done for you..."
- Boundary violations: showing up unannounced, commenting on your body/choices

**Scripts for managing parental dynamics:**

Setting boundaries:
- "I appreciate your concern, but I've made my decision about this."
- "I love you, and I'm not comfortable discussing my [relationship/weight/finances/etc.]."
- "I know you want what's best for me, but I need to figure this out on my own."
- "When you give me advice I haven't asked for, I feel [frustrated/dismissed]. I'd prefer if you waited for me to ask."

Receiving unsolicited advice:
- "Thanks for the suggestion. I'll think about it." (Acknowledge without committing)
- "I've already considered that and decided to go a different direction."
- "I appreciate your perspective. Right now, I'd prefer just to be listened to rather than given solutions."

Maintaining connection while protecting autonomy:
- "I'd love to hear about what's going on in your life." (Redirecting away from interrogation)
- "Let's talk about something we both enjoy."
- Regular check-ins on YOUR terms: "I'll call you every Sunday" establishes a pattern that prevents "Why haven't you called?"

**As a parent of young or adult children:**
- Ask before advising: "Would you like my input, or do you just need to vent?"
- Respect their communication preferences: don't demand phone calls if they prefer texting
- Allow space: not every text needs an immediate response
- Validate before problem-solving: "That sounds really hard" before "Have you tried..."

### 6.2 Extended Family Gatherings

**The pragmatic complexity:**
Extended family gatherings require simultaneously managing multiple relationship types (parents, siblings, grandparents, aunts/uncles, cousins, in-laws) while navigating different levels of familiarity, different communication styles, and competing expectations.

**Common interaction types at family gatherings:**

The "update" conversation:
- Relatives expect a brief summary of your life
- Standard questions: "How's work?" "Are you seeing anyone?" "What are you up to these days?"
- Standard answers should be brief and positive: "Work's going well, thanks!" / "Keeping busy. How about you?"
- Redirect attention back to them to avoid interrogation

The "catching up" ritual:
- With rarely-seen relatives, the expected exchange is:
  - Greeting: "It's so good to see you!"
  - Update exchange (2-3 minutes)
  - Expression of positive sentiment: "We should get together more often"
  - Moving on to the next person

The "controversial topic" trap:
- Common danger zones: politics, religion, lifestyle choices, parenting decisions
- Deflection scripts:
  - "I'd rather not get into politics today. Tell me about your garden/trip/hobby."
  - "I think we see that differently. How about those [sports team]?"
  - "That's a big topic for a family dinner! Let's save it for another time."
  - Simply: "I'd rather not discuss that" (direct but effective)

**The "helping in the kitchen" norm:**
At many family gatherings, offering to help is expected:
- "Can I help with anything?"
- "I'll clear the dishes"
- "What can I do?"
Even if the host says "No, I've got it," offering demonstrates good faith. Offer once or twice; if firmly declined, accept gracefully.

### 6.3 In-Law Relationships

In-law relationships have specific pragmatic challenges because they combine family obligation with chosen relationship:

**Core pragmatic principles:**
- In-laws are not your parents; the relationship is more formal, at least initially
- Your partner mediates between you and their parents (and vice versa)
- In-laws may have different communication norms than your family
- The relationship requires active cultivation; it doesn't develop automatically

**Common in-law communication challenges:**
- Different expectations about frequency of contact
- Different boundaries about involvement in your life/decisions
- Navigating criticism of your partner from their parents
- Balancing time between families during holidays
- The "Am I doing enough?" anxiety

**Helpful scripts:**
- Building relationship: "I'd love to hear more about [their interest/your partner's childhood]"
- Setting boundaries: "We've decided to [decision] as a couple. We appreciate your understanding."
- Managing overstepping: "[Partner] and I have talked about it and we're comfortable with our approach."
- Holiday negotiation: "This year, we're spending [holiday] with [other family]. We'd love to plan [alternative time] with you."

### 6.4 Holiday Communication Challenges

**Why holidays are pragmatically intense:**
- Multiple conversations happening simultaneously
- Expectations of emotional expressiveness ("Act happy!")
- Gift-giving pragmatics (expressing gratitude even for unwanted gifts)
- Religious or cultural rituals with specific behavioral expectations
- Alcohol may change other people's communication style
- End-of-year reflection questions ("So what's new?")

**Gift-receiving scripts:**
- "Thank you so much! This is wonderful." (Even if it isn't)
- "That's so thoughtful of you to think of me."
- "I love this. Thank you."
- If you genuinely can't use it: "Thank you! That's really sweet." (No need to lie about loving it, but express gratitude for the thought)

**Holiday greeting scripts:**
- "Happy [holiday]! Great to see you."
- "I hope you've been well. It's nice to get everyone together."
- "Enjoy the rest of the holidays!"

### 6.5 Autistic-Specific Guidance for Family Communication

**Family members who don't understand/accept autism:**

This is one of the most emotionally challenging pragmatic contexts. Research from multiple autism organizations identifies strategies:

**When family members deny or minimize your autism:**
Common statements you may encounter:
- "You don't look autistic" / "Everyone's a little autistic"
- "You weren't like this as a child"
- "You just need to try harder / be more social"
- "That's just an excuse"

Response options (ranging from mild to firm):
- Educational: "Autism looks different in every person. For me, it means [specific examples]."
- Personal: "When you say that, it feels like my experience is being dismissed. I'd appreciate it if you could take it seriously."
- Boundary-setting: "I understand you see it differently, but this is my reality. I need you to respect that."
- Firm: "I'm not going to debate my neurology. I'd like us to move on."

**Advocating for accommodation within family:**

Before gatherings:
- Contact the host in advance: "I wanted to give you a heads-up about some things that would help me enjoy the gathering more."
- Specific requests: "Is there a quiet room I could use if I need a break?" / "Would it be possible to dim the lights a bit?" / "I'd prefer to sit at the end of the table rather than the middle."
- Establish an exit plan: "I might need to leave early. I want you to know it's not about not wanting to be there; it's about managing my energy."

During gatherings:
- Take breaks: step outside, go to a quiet room, sit in your car for a few minutes
- Use pre-planned conversation topics
- Enlist an ally: have one family member who understands and can run interference
- Have a signal with your partner for "I need to leave soon"
- Bring comfort items: headphones, a fidget, a book, a phone for decompression

**Setting boundaries with family:**
Research from multiple sources on autistic boundary-setting emphasizes:

- Be direct and clear: autistic people often communicate more directly than neurotypical family members expect. This is a strength, not a flaw.
- Use written communication for difficult conversations: "If face-to-face discussions feel overwhelming, sending a text, email, or written note can be a less stressful way to set or clarify boundaries."
- Prepare for pushback: "Setting boundaries doesn't mean ending relationships, but means creating healthier dynamics that work for everyone."

**Specific accommodation scripts:**

For family dinners:
- "I'm going to use my headphones during the meal to manage the noise. I'm still here and enjoying being with everyone."
- "I'll eat before I come because the food smells can be overwhelming. I'll sit with everyone, though."

For holiday schedules:
- "I can come for two hours. After that, I'll need to head home to recharge."
- "I'd love to come on a different day when it's less crowded. Could we plan something smaller?"

For intrusive questions:
- "I'd rather not go into that right now."
- "I appreciate the interest, but I'm not comfortable discussing that."
- If pressed: "I've told you I don't want to discuss this. Can we talk about something else?"

**Managing family dynamics as an autistic parent:**
- You may need to advocate for your own needs AND your child's
- Educate family members: "Here's what our household needs look like."
- Don't apologize for accommodations: "We do things this way because it works for our family."
- Find other autistic parents or neurodivergent family networks for support

## CROSS-CONTEXT PATTERNS AND PRINCIPLES

### Universal Pragmatic Principles That Apply Across All Contexts

1. **The Power of Preparation:** In every context, having a script or plan reduces cognitive load. Write things down. Rehearse key phrases. Know what to expect.

2. **The Escalation Ladder:** Start mild, increase firmness only as needed. This applies to medical advocacy, service complaints, family boundaries, and institutional interactions.

3. **The Reciprocity Principle:** Conversations work on exchange. Ask questions, listen, respond to what was said. This applies to friendships, romantic relationships, medical encounters, and family interactions.

4. **The Right to Accommodation:** Under the ADA and basic human dignity, you have the right to request accommodation in medical, educational, legal, and institutional settings. You do NOT have to suffer in silence.

5. **The Masking Cost-Benefit Analysis:** Every context involves a decision about how much to mask. The cost of masking (exhaustion, inauthenticity, delayed support) must be weighed against the perceived benefit (fitting in, avoiding attention). In medical settings, masking can actively harm you by hiding symptoms. In police encounters, it may serve safety. In friendships, less masking leads to more sustainable relationships.

6. **The Directness Calibration:** Different contexts require different levels of directness. Emergency: be maximally direct. Family gathering: moderate directness. Professor email: diplomatically indirect. Police encounter: brief and neutral. Knowing which level is appropriate for which context is a core pragmatic skill.

7. **The Exit Strategy:** In every challenging context, have a plan for leaving. Medical appointment going badly: "I need to think about this and schedule a follow-up." Family gathering overwhelming: "I need some air." Police encounter: "Am I free to go?" Service encounter: "I'm going to think about it and come back."

### The Autistic Advantage in Some Contexts

While autism creates challenges in many social contexts, certain autistic traits are advantages:

- **Medical settings:** Precise, detailed symptom descriptions; thorough preparation; willingness to ask direct questions
- **Educational settings:** Deep focus, thorough research, attention to detail in written work
- **Legal settings:** Literal interpretation works well in contexts where precision matters
- **Service encounters:** Clear, direct communication about what you want
- **Institutional interactions:** Comfort with procedures, forms, and structured processes
- **Relationships:** Honesty, loyalty, direct communication about needs and boundaries

The challenge is not that autistic communication is wrong; it's that it operates on a different pragmatic system than what these contexts typically expect. Understanding both systems - the neurotypical expectations and your own communication strengths - allows you to navigate effectively while honoring your authentic self.
