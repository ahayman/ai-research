# Polyvagal Theory: What the Theory Actually Says

## Overview

Before you can evaluate whether polyvagal theory is good science or bad science — and that is what the rest of this deep dive is for — you have to know what the theory actually claims. Most popular treatments collapse it into the "ladder" metaphor and stop there. The technical theory is more specific, and the specifics are exactly what the scientific critique targets.

This page is the foundation. It tells the story of where the theory came from (Stephen Porges, 1994 SPR presidential address), walks through the puzzle that motivated it (the "vagal paradox" of bradycardia), explains the three-tier hierarchy of autonomic states, defines the key terms (neuroception, social engagement system, dissolution), and shows how the theory has evolved across thirty years of Porges's writing.

The tone here is descriptive, not evaluative. This is *what the theory says*. Whether it survives empirical scrutiny is the subject of Category 3 (The Scientific Critique). Whether the autism-specific claims hold up is Category 4. Whether the clinical practices built on the theory are useful regardless of whether the underlying mechanism is correct is Category 7. Whether the wellness industry has run away with the language is Category 8. The job of this page is to give you Porges's framework clean — so that when you read the criticism, you'll know exactly what's being criticized.

**Confidence overview.** Of 16 substantive claims in this page, 13 are confirmed against primary sources, 2 are uncertain at the level of attribution (whose framing is whose), and 1 was identified and corrected during validation (SSP commercial launch is 2016, not 2014; the 2014 paper is the predecessor Listening Project Protocol research). Zero claims are invalid. The chronological scaffolding (1994/1995/2004/2011/2022/2025), the cranial-nerve anatomy of the social engagement system, the citation of Jackson, and the Dana ladder metaphor all hold up under independent verification.

**Key Takeaways:**
- Polyvagal theory was introduced October 8, 1994 in Porges's presidential address to the Society for Psychophysiological Research and published in 1995.
- The theory rests on a puzzle ("the vagal paradox"): how can vagally-mediated RSA be a sign of health while vagally-mediated bradycardia is potentially lethal?
- Porges's answer: there are *two* functionally distinct vagal pathways — myelinated (NA-origin) and unmyelinated (DMNX-origin) — supporting different defensive and social functions.
- The theory proposes a three-tier autonomic hierarchy (ventral vagal → sympathetic → dorsal vagal) recruited in reverse order under threat (Jackson's "dissolution").
- "Neuroception" is the theory's most clinically useful contribution: the sub-conscious neural process distinct from perception that scans for safety vs. danger.
- The "social engagement system" is the integrated brainstem network of cranial nerves V, VII, IX, X, and XI per Porges.
- Deb Dana's 2018 "polyvagal ladder" is the popular teaching metaphor; the underlying technical theory is more specific (and more contestable).

## Prerequisites

Before reading this explanation, it helps to know:
- That the autonomic nervous system (ANS) regulates involuntary visceral functions (heart, gut, glands) outside conscious control
- The classical two-branch view: sympathetic (fight/flight) and parasympathetic (rest/digest)
- Heart rate variability (HRV) is the beat-to-beat variation in heart rate; respiratory sinus arrhythmia (RSA) is the breath-locked HRV component
- That the vagus nerve is the tenth cranial nerve (CN X) and is the largest source of parasympathetic input to the body's organs

## The Story of the Theory

The construct begins with a clinical puzzle and a sabbatical at NIH.

In the early 1990s, Stephen Porges — a developmental psychophysiologist, then at the University of Maryland and visiting scientist at the National Institutes of Health — spent two years (1992 to 1994) immersed in the comparative-neuroanatomy literature on the autonomic nervous system. He had access to the NIH library and the National Library of Medicine. He read hundreds of papers on vertebrate vagal physiology, on the brainstem nuclei that send fibers down the vagus, on the difference between myelinated and unmyelinated nerve fibers, and on how the vagus innervates the heart in fish, reptiles, birds, and mammals.

The puzzle that drove him was clinical. In obstetrics and neonatology, the same nerve — the vagus — was being interpreted in two opposite ways. **Respiratory sinus arrhythmia (RSA)**, the rhythmic speeding and slowing of heart rate with breathing, was treated as a sign of vagal *health*: a resilient infant has good RSA, indicating functional parasympathetic regulation. But **bradycardia**, severe vagally-mediated slowing of the heart, especially in preterm infants, was potentially *lethal*: the heart slowed so dramatically that not enough oxygenated blood reached the brain. Both are vagally driven. How can the same nerve do both?

Porges's hypothesis: there are not one but two vagal output systems. One — myelinated, fast, originating in the brainstem **nucleus ambiguus (NA)** — produces the rhythmic, breath-locked RSA pattern and supports social engagement. The other — unmyelinated, slow, originating in the **dorsal motor nucleus of the vagus (DMNX)** — produces severe bradycardia and is part of an older, reptile-shared defensive system. Calling these "the vagus" as a single thing was obscuring the clinical picture. The vagus had a *poly*-vagal structure.

He laid out this hypothesis in his **presidential address to the Society for Psychophysiological Research on October 8, 1994, in Atlanta**, and published the formal paper a year later in *Psychophysiology* under the title *"Orienting in a Defensive World: Mammalian Modifications of Our Evolutionary Heritage. A Polyvagal Theory"* (Volume 32, Issue 4, pages 301–318).

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-1-timeline -->

That paper began a thirty-year arc.

In 2001, Porges published *"The polyvagal theory: phylogenetic substrates of a social nervous system"* in the *International Journal of Psychophysiology*. The 2001 paper expanded the framework — introducing the "social engagement system" as a brainstem network integrating cardiac vagal regulation with the muscles of the face, voice, middle ear, and head, via cranial nerves V, VII, IX, X, and XI.

In 2004, in a *Zero to Three* article aimed at infant-development clinicians, Porges introduced the term **"neuroception"** — distinguishing the nervous system's sub-conscious safety scan from conscious perception. This is the term that has become most clinically useful to therapists.

In 2009, he published *"The polyvagal theory: New insights into adaptive reactions of the autonomic nervous system"* in the *Cleveland Clinic Journal of Medicine* — a consolidated, medical-audience statement of the theory.

In 2011, W. W. Norton published his book *The Polyvagal Theory: Neurophysiological Foundations of Emotions, Attachment, Communication, and Self-Regulation* (ISBN 9780393707007). That book became the standard reference. It was also the moment the theory began to spread beyond psychophysiology — into trauma therapy, into developmental psychology, into the broader wellness ecosystem.

In 2014, Porges et al. published preliminary findings on the **Listening Project Protocol (LPP)** in autism — an acoustic intervention designed to recruit the social engagement system via filtered music that emphasized the human-voice frequency band. In **2016**, the LPP's commercial successor — the **Safe and Sound Protocol (SSP)** — launched through Integrated Listening Systems (now Unyte Health). Porges holds patent rights and receives royalties; he serves as Chief Scientific Advisor at Unyte.

Also in 2014, Bessel van der Kolk's *The Body Keeps the Score* (Viking) drew heavily on polyvagal theory in its trauma chapters. The book became a global bestseller and remains the dominant popularization vector for PVT.

In 2018, the clinician **Deb Dana** published *The Polyvagal Theory in Therapy: Engaging the Rhythm of Regulation* (W. W. Norton). Dana introduced the **"polyvagal ladder"** — the three states color-coded green (ventral vagal), yellow (sympathetic), red (dorsal vagal) — as a clinical teaching metaphor. This is the form in which most people now encounter polyvagal theory: the ladder, not the original technical anatomy.

In 2020, Porges and Dana co-founded the **Polyvagal Institute (PVI)**, a 501(c)(3) non-profit that offers paid courses and certifications.

In 2022, Porges published *"Polyvagal Theory: A Science of Safety"* in *Frontiers in Integrative Neuroscience* (PMC9131189), reframing the theory as a "science of safety" with measurable physiological substrates.

In 2023, the first major formal critique landed: Paul Grossman's *"Fundamental challenges and likely refutations of the five basic premises of the polyvagal theory"* in *Biological Psychology*. Grossman, a psychophysiologist with decades of vagal-physiology work of his own, argued that all five core premises of the theory were inconsistent with established comparative neuroanatomy and had likely been falsified.

In 2025, Porges published his most recent update: *"Polyvagal Theory: Current Status, Clinical Applications, and Future Directions"* in *Clinical Neuropsychiatry* 22(3) (PMC12302812). This update introduced new biomarkers (vagal efficiency, weighted coherence), defended the theory with transcriptomic evidence about mammalian brainstem neurons, and explicitly addressed the falsifiability critique with four named testable predictions.

In February 2026, a consensus paper followed: *"Why The Polyvagal Theory Is Untenable"* in the same journal — co-authored by 39 researchers (38 invited international experts plus Grossman as first author), directly rebutting Porges's 2025 update. Porges responded in the same issue with *"When a Critique Becomes Untenable."* That exchange is the live state of the field as of mid-2026.

That's where the deep dive begins. The user needs Porges's framework clean before any of the critique is intelligible. Below: what the theory actually claims.

## The Vagal Paradox

The motivating puzzle is the first thing to understand.

"The vagus" — cranial nerve X — is the largest cranial nerve and the largest source of parasympathetic input to the body's organs. It travels from the brainstem down through the neck, sends branches to the heart, the lungs, the larynx and pharynx, and continues subdiaphragmatically to the stomach, liver, gallbladder, pancreas, and most of the intestines. Roughly 80% of vagal fibers are afferent (carrying sensory information *from* organs to the brain); about 20% are efferent (carrying motor commands *to* organs).

In the clinical population that Porges came from — developmental psychophysiology, especially neonatology — "the vagus" was being read in two contradictory ways.

**Reading One**: Vagal RSA = health. An infant whose heart rate oscillates rhythmically with breathing has a functional vagus modulating cardiac output beat-to-beat. Higher RSA correlates with better self-regulation, better attention, calmer behavioral states. This is the basis of treating "vagal tone" as a positive biomarker.

**Reading Two**: Vagal bradycardia = danger. A preterm infant whose heart slows dramatically, sometimes to the point of needing intervention, is also showing vagal activity — but a very different kind. This slowing can starve the brain of oxygen and is potentially fatal.

If both are "the vagus," then "high vagal activity" should be either uniformly good or uniformly bad. It isn't. So either the framing is wrong, or there are two different vagal systems being conflated.

Porges proposed the latter. The two clinical phenomena correspond to two anatomically and functionally distinct vagal pathways with two different evolutionary origins, two different brainstem source nuclei, two different fiber types, and two different effects on the heart. The myelinated, fast-conducting ventral vagus (from the nucleus ambiguus) produces RSA and the "vagal brake" that supports calm states. The unmyelinated, slow-conducting dorsal vagus (from the dorsal motor nucleus) produces severe bradycardia and is part of an older defensive system the theory traces back to reptilian ancestors.

The basic two-source anatomy here is uncontroversial. The vagus does have two main brainstem nuclei, and the fibers from them do have different myelination properties. What polyvagal theory adds is the *functional and phylogenetic interpretation* of that anatomy — and the interpretation is where the controversy lives.

## The Three-Tier Hierarchy

The theory's central organizational claim is that the autonomic nervous system is not a two-branch system (sympathetic vs. parasympathetic) but a three-tier hierarchy. Each tier emerged at a different evolutionary stage.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-2-three-tier-ladder -->

**Tier 1 — Ventral Vagal Complex (VVC).** The newest tier, claimed by Porges to be uniquely mammalian. It originates in the nucleus ambiguus, sends myelinated fast-conducting fibers to the heart (producing RSA and the vagal brake), and is anatomically integrated with the muscles of the face, jaw, voice, middle ear, and neck via cranial nerves V, VII, IX, X, and XI. When active, you are calm-but-alert, available for connection, prosodic in voice, open in facial expression, capable of co-regulation. This is the substrate, in PVT's framework, of human sociality.

**Tier 2 — Sympathetic Nervous System (SNS).** The intermediate tier. Spinal thoracolumbar outflow, adrenergic signaling, engagement of the adrenal medulla. When active, you are mobilized for fight or flight: heart racing, attention narrowed to threat, muscles primed, digestion suppressed. Phenomenologically, this is anxiety, hypervigilance, irritability, restlessness.

**Tier 3 — Dorsal Vagal Complex (DVC).** The oldest tier, shared per PVT with reptiles. Originates in the dorsal motor nucleus of the vagus, sends unmyelinated slow-conducting fibers primarily to subdiaphragmatic viscera (gut, liver, pancreas), and includes the cardiac fibers responsible for severe bradycardia and vasovagal syncope. When active, you are immobilized — feigned death, freeze, collapse, dissociation. Phenomenologically, this is shutdown: voice gone, face flat, mind blank, body numb.

Importantly, the relationships *between* these tiers are hierarchical. Under conditions of safety, the ventral vagal complex is active and *inhibits* the lower tiers. The sympathetic system is held in check by the vagal brake; the dorsal system is held in check by the active VVC. When neuroception detects threat, the ventral vagal brake releases, the sympathetic system mobilizes, and the lower tier becomes available. If sympathetic mobilization fails (or escape is impossible), the system can drop further to the dorsal vagal default.

Recovery passes through the same tiers in reverse: dorsal disengages, sympathetic activates transitionally, ventral comes back online. This is why Porges's writing — and Dana's clinical work — often emphasizes that someone stuck in dorsal shutdown may have to *move through sympathetic activation* (movement, trembling, breath, tears) to get back to ventral availability. Going directly from collapse to calm is not how the system is theorized to work.

## The Five Core Premises

Porges does not typically structure the theory as a numbered list of premises. His writing is more narrative-philosophical. But readers — especially critics — have parsed the theory into roughly five load-bearing claims, and this parsing has become the standard way of organizing the debate. The "five basic premises" framing was crystallized in Paul Grossman's 2023 *Biological Psychology* paper, which titled itself *"Fundamental challenges and likely refutations of the five basic premises of the polyvagal theory."*

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-3-five-premises -->

The five, as commonly enumerated:

**Premise 1: Hierarchical autonomic organization.** The autonomic nervous system is organized as a three-tier phylogenetically-ordered hierarchy, with newer tiers inhibiting older tiers under conditions of safety.

**Premise 2: Mammalian-specific myelinated ventral vagus.** Mammals uniquely possess a myelinated, fast-conducting cardiac vagal pathway originating in the nucleus ambiguus — an evolutionary innovation absent from reptiles, fish, and other non-mammalian vertebrates.

**Premise 3: Neuroception.** The nervous system continuously and sub-consciously evaluates environmental and visceral cues for safety, danger, or life-threat — a process distinct from conscious perception.

**Premise 4: Integrated social engagement system.** Cranial nerves V, VII, IX, X, and XI form a coordinated brainstem network — integrating heart, face, voice, middle ear, and head orientation — that supports mammalian social interaction.

**Premise 5: Physiological state as intervening variable.** Autonomic state, measurable via biomarkers like RSA, vagal efficiency, and weighted coherence, mediates between environmental cues and behavioral output.

> **Evidence note**: This enumeration has a validity rating of 7/10. The five themes are recognizable in Porges's writing, but the *enumeration as five* is a reader-imposed structuring (especially Grossman's). Porges's 2025 update, in defending the theory's falsifiability, lists *four* "falsifiable predictions" rather than five premises. The reader should hold this five-premise structure loosely — it's a useful way to organize the debate, not the canonical form Porges himself uses.

The five-premise structure matters because Categories 3 and 4 of this deep dive will examine which premises hold up under empirical scrutiny and which don't. Different premises have different epistemic statuses. Premise 1 is largely defensible at the anatomical level (two vagal nuclei, two fiber types, different functional profiles — uncontroversial). Premise 2 is the most contested (lungfish, sharks, and birds also have myelinated cardiac vagal fibers, contradicting "uniquely mammalian"). Premise 3 (neuroception) is more conceptual than empirical and is hard to falsify directly. Premise 4 is partly anatomy (the cranial nerves exist and serve those functions — uncontested) and partly theoretical claim about coordination (contested). Premise 5 depends on whether the biomarkers measure what Porges claims they measure.

For the user's purposes — disentangling science from pseudoscience — being able to attach the right epistemic status to each premise is what makes the rest of the deep dive navigable.

## The Evolutionary Phylogeny Claim

The most consequential and most contested premise is Premise 2 — the claim that the myelinated cardiac vagus is uniquely mammalian. It's worth examining what Porges actually says here, because critics often attack a stronger version of this claim than Porges has consistently defended.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-4-phylogeny -->

The basic story per PVT goes like this. In reptiles, the cardiac vagus is unmyelinated and originates in the dorsal motor nucleus. Slow, sufficient for ectothermic life, doesn't need to support sustained social engagement or fine cardiac modulation. The defensive system is dominated by *immobilization* — playing dead, freezing, slowing the heart dramatically to reduce metabolic demand and detection.

At some point in the mammalian lineage, a ventral migration of cardioinhibitory neurons occurred. A subset of vagal cardiac neurons moved from the dorsal motor nucleus to a more ventral position — the nucleus ambiguus — and acquired myelination. The result was a fast, finely-modulating cardiac vagal pathway that could rapidly engage and disengage the heart, producing respiratory sinus arrhythmia and the "vagal brake." This new pathway became integrated with the brainstem nuclei controlling face, voice, middle ear, and head orientation. The integrated system supported the suite of behaviors that defines mammalian sociality: nursing, vocalization, gaze, facial expressivity, prosodic communication.

The DMNX pathway didn't disappear. Mammals retained it. The two systems coexist, doing different jobs. The DMNX continues to handle subdiaphragmatic visceral functions and remains the substrate for severe defensive bradycardia (vasovagal syncope, dorsal-vagal shutdown). The NA pathway handles the rapid, social-engagement-related cardiac modulation.

That is the theory's framing.

What critics — especially Grossman and the 2026 "Untenable" consensus — argue is that the *uniquely mammalian* part of this is wrong. Lungfish have myelinated cardiorespiratory vagal fibers from a ventrolateral brainstem source (Monteiro et al. 2018, *Science Advances*). Sharks and elasmobranchs have myelinated cardioinhibitory vagal neurons conducting at 7–35 m/s (Taylor & Joyce 2018 and related comparative work). Birds also have myelinated cardiac vagal fibers. The pattern Porges describes as the mammalian innovation appears across multiple non-mammalian lineages.

The dispute about *how* mammalian the mammalian cardiac vagus actually is — and what that means for the theory as a whole — is the subject of Category 3. For now, what matters is what the theory *claims*, and the claim is what's diagrammed here: the cardioinhibitory-migration story from DMNX to NA, framed as the evolutionary substrate of mammalian sociality.

## Neuroception

This is the term polyvagal theory has contributed that has been most clinically useful, and it's worth slowing down on.

Porges coined "neuroception" in 2004 in a *Zero to Three* article aimed at infant-development clinicians. The original definition: *"a neural process, distinct from perception, that is capable of distinguishing environmental (and visceral) features that are safe, dangerous, or life-threatening."* That language has been carried forward almost verbatim in subsequent papers.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-5-neuroception-vs-perception -->

The distinction Porges is drawing is between two parallel streams of evaluation that the nervous system runs continuously.

**Perception** is cortical. It's conscious. It produces reportable thoughts: "this room is safe," "I trust this person," "nothing is wrong." It's slow, voluntary, narratable, available to deliberate revision.

**Neuroception** is subcortical. It runs through limbic and brainstem circuits. It's fast, involuntary, not directly reportable. It produces autonomic state shifts without any need for conscious recognition. The same sensory input enters both streams in parallel, and the two streams can produce *different* outputs.

This is the clinical insight: someone can intellectually know they're safe while their body is reading the situation as dangerous. The cortex says "this is fine"; the brainstem says "no." Telling someone in this state to "calm down" or "you're safe now" tries to use perception to override neuroception, and it typically doesn't work — because the two streams have different operating speeds, different inputs, and different control architectures.

For autistic adults who have spent a lifetime being told their bodies are overreacting to safe contexts — the meeting room, the office, the casual social gathering — this distinction can be revelatory. The mismatch between "I know I should be okay" and "my body is screaming" stops being a personal failure and starts being a recognizable mismatch between two real neural processes. That reframing alone is one of the most useful things polyvagal theory has given trauma-informed practice.

It is also one of the harder parts of the theory to test directly. "Neuroception" is a hypothesized process — the empirical evidence for it consists of the *outputs* (autonomic state shifts in response to cues that haven't been consciously processed) rather than direct measurement of the process itself. Whether neuroception is a single coherent mechanism or whether it's a label for a heterogeneous collection of subcortical safety-evaluating processes is an open question. The clinical utility of the concept doesn't depend on the underlying mechanism being unitary.

## The Social Engagement System

If the three-tier hierarchy is the theory's organizational claim and neuroception is its most useful clinical concept, the social engagement system is its most specific anatomical claim.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-6-social-engagement-system -->

Porges's claim is that five cranial nerves — V, VII, IX, X, and XI — form a coordinated brainstem network. Each nerve serves a different function, but they share embryological origin in the pharyngeal arches, they share brainstem proximity in the medulla, and (per PVT) they functionally coordinate as a single output system when the ventral vagal complex is active.

**Cranial Nerve V (trigeminal)** controls the muscles of mastication and the tensor tympani, one of the two small muscles in the middle ear that adjusts tension on the eardrum.

**Cranial Nerve VII (facial)** controls the muscles of facial expression — smile, frown, brow movement, eye widening — and the stapedius, the second middle-ear muscle, which specifically dampens loud low-frequency sounds and helps tune hearing to the human-voice frequency range.

**Cranial Nerve IX (glossopharyngeal)** controls the pharynx, the gag reflex, taste sensation, and contributes to swallowing.

**Cranial Nerve X (vagus)** — specifically the ventral branch — controls the heart (via the vagal brake), the bronchi, and the larynx (which shapes vocal prosody).

**Cranial Nerve XI (accessory)** controls the sternocleidomastoid and trapezius muscles, which govern head turn and neck orientation — the readiness to face a conversational partner.

The clinical extension that Porges built from this framework is the Listening Project Protocol, later commercialized as the Safe and Sound Protocol (SSP). The hypothesis: by playing acoustically filtered music that emphasizes the human-voice frequency range, you can exercise the middle-ear muscles (especially the stapedius, via CN VII) and thereby recruit the broader social engagement system. Recruiting the SES is theorized to shift autonomic state toward ventral vagal availability and reduce the defensive postures common in autism.

The cranial-nerve anatomy is real, well-documented, and uncontested. What polyvagal theory specifically claims is that these five nerves form a *coordinated, integrated system* orchestrated by the VVC, rather than five separate cranial-nerve outputs that happen to be anatomically nearby. That integration claim — and the SSP's specific theory of mechanism — are matters for Categories 6 (Interventions) and 4 (Autism Evidence) to evaluate.

## Dissolution: How the Hierarchy Disengages Under Threat

The last technical concept worth getting right is *dissolution* — the principle that gives the three-tier hierarchy its dynamic structure.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-7-dissolution -->

Porges borrowed the concept from John Hughlings Jackson, a 19th-century English neurologist (1835–1911) who proposed that brain damage and disease produce a "dissolution" of neural function — a reverse of evolution, in which newer, higher-order neural functions are lost first, revealing older, lower-order substrates that had been inhibited. Jackson's framework was diagnostic: a patient's symptoms could be read as a guide to which level of the hierarchy had been damaged.

Porges generalized the principle in two directions. First, he applied it to the autonomic system specifically — not just the cortex Jackson focused on. Second, he applied it to *adaptive* state shifts, not just brain damage. The autonomic hierarchy, in PVT, dissolves under threat in the same reverse-phylogenetic order Jackson described.

The dissolution sequence under increasing threat:

1. **Default state**: VVC active. The ventral vagal brake holds sympathetic and dorsal systems in check.
2. **Threat detected by neuroception**: VVC disengages. The vagal brake releases.
3. **Sympathetic mobilization**: The SNS, no longer inhibited, activates fight or flight.
4. **If SNS mobilization is unsustainable or escape impossible**: The DVC takes over. Immobilization, freeze, shutdown.

The recovery sequence reverses:

1. **From DVC shutdown**: Safety cues from outside (a regulated person nearby, a familiar voice, a settled environment) are detected by neuroception.
2. **Sympathetic re-engagement**: The system transitionally mobilizes — movement, trembling, tears, breath. This *passes through* the sympathetic tier rather than skipping it.
3. **VVC re-engagement**: As felt safety is re-established, the ventral vagal complex comes back online. Voice returns, facial expression softens, social availability is restored.

The clinical implication that has been most useful: recovery from dorsal-vagal shutdown is not "calm down." Going directly from collapse to calm bypasses the sympathetic tier the system is theorized to need to pass through. Practitioners trained in polyvagal-informed therapy will often encourage gentle mobilization — movement, breath, voice, expressive activity — as the transitional state out of shutdown, rather than asking a person in collapse to simply relax. Whether this clinical heuristic depends on the underlying anatomy being correct, or whether it would be useful regardless of the mechanism, is one of the questions Categories 7 and 11 take up.

## The Polyvagal Ladder — Deb Dana's Clinical Metaphor

Most people who have heard of polyvagal theory have heard of the ladder. Many have heard of *only* the ladder.

The ladder is Deb Dana's contribution. Dana, a clinical social worker who became Porges's primary clinical translator, published *The Polyvagal Theory in Therapy: Engaging the Rhythm of Regulation* in 2018 (W. W. Norton, ISBN 9780393712377). The book became the bridge between Porges's technical psychophysiology and the trauma-therapy community. Dana co-founded the Polyvagal Institute with Porges in 2020.

The ladder is a metaphor. Imagine the three autonomic states as rungs on a vertical ladder:

- **Top (green) — Ventral vagal**: "I am here, I am okay, I can engage." Calm, social, connected.
- **Middle (yellow) — Sympathetic**: "I am not safe, I need to do something." Mobilized, activated, anxious.
- **Bottom (red) — Dorsal vagal**: "I cannot escape, I will disappear." Shut down, collapsed, dissociated.

Movement up and down the ladder is not pathological — it's *normal lived rhythm*, what Dana calls "the rhythm of regulation." Throughout a day, you move up and down. You wake calm (ventral), get stressed in traffic (sympathetic), settle back into a good meeting (ventral), get overwhelmed by an email and start to shut down (dorsal), walk outside and feel yourself activating back (sympathetic), and eventually return to settled connection (ventral). The pattern is the rhythm.

Pathology, in this framing, is being *stuck* on one rung. Chronic dorsal-vagal collapse looks like depression-spectrum shutdown — flat, withdrawn, disconnected. Chronic sympathetic activation looks like anxiety-spectrum hypervigilance — restless, on edge, unable to settle. Chronic *inability to access ventral vagal* looks like trauma-related social impairment — unable to feel safe with others even when objectively safe. The therapy task, in Dana's framing, is to restore *flexibility* — being able to move through the ladder in response to actual conditions, rather than being locked at one state.

The ladder is what circulates on Instagram, TikTok, and trauma-therapy social media. Most popular polyvagal content is some form of the Dana ladder, often without the technical anatomy underneath. That's not necessarily a criticism — clinical teaching tools are supposed to compress complex frameworks into something a client can use. But it does mean that "polyvagal theory" in popular usage often refers to the ladder metaphor specifically, and the technical theory (with its anatomy, its phylogenetic claims, its falsifiable biomarkers) is sometimes left implicit or forgotten.

When you're reading polyvagal content, it's worth asking which version you're encountering. The ladder is a metaphor and is mostly downstream of the technical claims. The technical claims are what survive or don't survive scientific scrutiny. Both can be useful, but they're not the same thing.

## How the Theory Has Evolved

One final point worth marking. Polyvagal theory has not been a static target across thirty years. Porges has continuously refined, extended, and re-articulated the framework.

<!-- DIAGRAM: diagram-8-theory-evolution -->

The 1995 paper introduced the basic two-vagus framework and the vagal-paradox motivation. The 2001 paper introduced the social engagement system and cranial-nerve V/VII/IX/X/XI integration. The 2004 article introduced neuroception. The 2009 Cleveland Clinic paper consolidated the theory for a medical audience. The 2011 Norton book gave the theory its definitive textbook form. The 2022 Frontiers paper reframed it as a "science of safety." The 2025 Clinical Neuropsychiatry update added vagal efficiency and weighted coherence as new biomarkers, defended the theory's falsifiability with four explicit testable predictions, and deployed transcriptomic (single-nucleus RNA sequencing) evidence about mammalian brainstem neurons.

This matters because critics and defenders sometimes engage different versions of the theory. When Grossman 2023 critiques "the five basic premises," he is engaging the version that crystallized around the 2011 book. When Porges 2026 responds with "When a Critique Becomes Untenable," he is defending the 2025 articulation — which has accumulated additional specifications, biomarkers, and explicit responses to critics. The result is sometimes two parties talking past each other.

The honest picture: the theory is a moving target, the critique is also evolving, and the user's deep dive needs to track which version of which claim is being defended or attacked at any given point. The diagrams in this chapter are anchored to the 2025 update — Porges's most recent statement — but the references map back to the original articulations where relevant.

## Summary

### Key Points

1. Polyvagal theory was unveiled in Porges's October 8, 1994 SPR presidential address and formalized in the 1995 *Psychophysiology* paper. The 2011 Norton book is the standard reference; the 2025 *Clinical Neuropsychiatry* paper is the most recent update.
2. The motivating puzzle is the "vagal paradox": vagally-mediated RSA reads as healthy, vagally-mediated bradycardia is potentially lethal. Porges proposed two functionally distinct vagal pathways (NA-myelinated and DMNX-unmyelinated) to resolve it.
3. The three-tier hierarchy is the organizational claim: ventral vagal (newest, social), sympathetic (intermediate, mobilized), dorsal vagal (oldest, shutdown). Each tier emerged at a different phylogenetic stage and is recruited in reverse order under threat.
4. The "five basic premises" framing is largely reader-imposed (Grossman 2023) rather than Porges-imposed, but it usefully organizes the debate: (1) hierarchical organization, (2) mammalian-unique ventral vagus, (3) neuroception, (4) integrated social engagement system, (5) physiological state as intervening variable.
5. The mammalian-uniqueness claim is the most contested premise. PVT claims the migration of cardioinhibitory neurons from DMNX to NA is uniquely mammalian; critics argue lungfish, sharks, and birds also have myelinated cardiac vagal fibers.
6. Neuroception is the theory's most clinically useful contribution: the sub-conscious neural process distinct from perception that scans for safety vs. danger vs. life-threat. The two streams can disagree; this makes the "mind says safe, body says danger" experience legible.
7. The social engagement system is the integrated cranial-nerve V/VII/IX/X/XI network. The Listening Project Protocol → Safe and Sound Protocol acoustic interventions are built on this anatomical claim. SSP was commercialized in 2016.
8. Dissolution (from Hughlings Jackson) is the dynamic principle: under threat, the hierarchy disengages newest-tier-first. Recovery passes back through sympathetic mobilization before ventral availability returns.
9. Deb Dana's 2018 "polyvagal ladder" is the popular teaching metaphor. Most polyvagal content circulating publicly is the ladder, often without the underlying technical anatomy.
10. The theory has continuously evolved across thirty years. The 2025 update is the most recent restatement; the 2026 "Untenable" critique and Porges's response are the live state of the field.

### What This Page Did Not Do

This page presented the theory's claims. It did not evaluate them. The questions of whether the mammalian-uniqueness claim is correct, whether the dorsal vagal complex is actually the substrate of human freeze responses, whether RSA measures what Porges claims it measures, whether the SSP works through the proposed mechanism, and whether the clinical practices built on the theory require the theory to be correct — those are the work of the rest of the deep dive.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Polyvagal Theory (PVT) | Porges's three-tier hierarchical framework for the autonomic nervous system, with two functionally distinct vagal pathways. |
| Ventral Vagal Complex (VVC) | Per PVT: the myelinated, fast-conducting vagal pathway originating in the nucleus ambiguus, supporting calm and social engagement. |
| Dorsal Vagal Complex (DVC) | Per PVT: the unmyelinated, slow-conducting vagal pathway originating in the dorsal motor nucleus, supporting immobilization and shutdown. |
| Sympathetic Nervous System (SNS) | The thoracolumbar autonomic branch driving fight-or-flight mobilization; the middle tier of PVT's hierarchy. |
| Nucleus Ambiguus (NA) | Brainstem nucleus that PVT identifies as the source of the myelinated cardiac vagus and the coordinating hub of the social engagement system. |
| Dorsal Motor Nucleus of the Vagus (DMNX) | Brainstem nucleus, source of the unmyelinated dorsal vagus targeting subdiaphragmatic viscera. |
| Respiratory Sinus Arrhythmia (RSA) | Rhythmic variation in heart rate phase-locked to breathing; used as a non-invasive index of parasympathetic cardiac control. |
| Vagal Brake | PVT's term for the inhibitory effect of the myelinated ventral vagus on the heart's intrinsic pacemaker, allowing fine modulation of heart rate. |
| Neuroception | Porges's term for the non-conscious neural process that continuously evaluates cues for safety, danger, or life-threat — distinct from conscious perception. |
| Social Engagement System (SES) | PVT's integrated network of cranial nerves V, VII, IX, X, and XI plus brainstem nuclei that coordinates face, voice, middle ear, and heart for social interaction. |
| Dissolution | The principle (from Jackson) that under threat, the autonomic hierarchy disengages newest-tier-first. |
| Polyvagal Ladder | Deb Dana's clinical teaching metaphor representing the three states as rungs (ventral / sympathetic / dorsal). |
| Vagal Efficiency (VE) | Porges's biomarker measuring the relationship between RSA amplitude and heart rate over time; claimed to index central-peripheral integration. |
| Weighted Coherence | Porges's biomarker capturing phase consistency between respiratory and cardiac oscillations; claimed to index brainstem coordination. |
| Listening Project Protocol (LPP) | Porges's original research-stage acoustic intervention. |
| Safe and Sound Protocol (SSP) | The commercial successor to LPP, launched in 2016 through Integrated Listening Systems (now Unyte Health). |
| Polyvagal Institute (PVI) | Non-profit (501(c)(3)) co-founded by Porges and Dana in 2020 for PVT education and certification. |
| Co-regulation | The clinical concept that one nervous system can help regulate another via cues of safety. |
