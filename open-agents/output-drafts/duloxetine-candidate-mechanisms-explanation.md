# Why It Might Be Working: Candidate Mechanisms, Rated

> On one Saturday, the same drug moved two things in opposite directions in the same person. The acute sensory overwhelm got worse. The crash that always follows it never arrived. Everything on this page is an attempt to say what could do that, and nothing on this page succeeds.

## Narrative

The facts first, because they are the constraint that everything else has to satisfy. Five hours across two crowded festivals. Two panic attacks, with a heart rate above 140 beats per minute while standing still. In your history that combination is not a risk factor, it is a guarantee: one day of that kind of input, then one to two weeks of dissociation, severe anxiety, and intense depression. The acute experience on the drug was, by your own account, *worse* than it would have been without it. And then nothing happened. In your words: *"Its lack was very stark, not just to me but to my whole family. We all braced for it, but it never came."*

Read that as two measurements rather than one story, because two measurements is what it is. Measurement one: acute sensory reactivity went **up**. Measurement two: the delayed sequela went **down**, all the way to zero. Same drug, same person, same day, opposite directions.

That shape does useful work immediately, because it kills a whole class of explanation before any pharmacology is needed. If the drug were sedating you, blunting you, or globally muting your emotional range, then the acute overwhelm would have been *milder*, because that is what those words mean. It was not milder. And this is not the shape expectancy predicts either. A hopeful person starting a new medication before a hard day reports the hard day feeling *easier*. You reported it feeling worse, and you reported the absence of a thing your family had independently braced for. Expectancy does not usually predict the disconfirming half of its own observation, and it does not usually recruit witnesses. `[reasoning, not evidence]`

So the question this page has to answer is not "why did the episodes stop." It is narrower and stranger. **Why is the overwhelm decoupled from the crash?**

Here is the answer, stated at the top so that nothing below reads as a build-up to a reveal. **No single candidate explains you.** The best account available is three separate mechanisms, drawn from three separate literatures that have never been joined by anybody, and two of those three have never been tested in a human at all. One of them explains the acute half. One explains the crash half. Two explain the sleep half. Nothing explains all of it, and this page will not manufacture a winner by promoting the least-bad option.

### Your instruction, and why it was right

You wrote:

> *"Any reasonable hypothesis will always find a counter example in the population. That doesn't prove the hypothesis wrong; it just proves the hypothesis wrong for some of the population. The problem, to my mind, is you're trying to find a single explanation that can't be disproved instead of compiling a list of possibly valid explanations."*

That diagnosis is correct, and this project is the evidence for it. Eight research passes went into this deep dive. Each one crowned a mechanism, and the next one killed it, and the pass after that killed the killer. The pattern is not a chain of increasing accuracy. It is **alternating over-correction**: each pass correctly identifies its predecessor's methodological sin and then commits the same sin in reverse. Recency is not reliability, and this ninth pass has no special claim to being the one that finally got it right.

The number that makes your argument quantitative rather than philosophical is this. Abanades and colleagues, at a GlaxoSmithKline imaging centre in London, gave ten healthy men the same drug at the same dose on the same schedule, scanned them, and fitted an EC50 to each individual separately. The values ran from 0.54 to 4.61 ng/mL `[primary, verified]`. **That is an 8.5-fold spread across ten people who were treated identically.** Their plasma concentrations varied 5.7-fold on the same milligrams. The population average describes none of them.

This is the mechanism behind everything you already suspected. It is why online reports of the same drug read like reports of different drugs. It is why "try it and see" is correct clinical practice rather than laziness, because there is no measurement that would tell a prescriber where in that 8.5-fold band you sit. And it is why the fact that escitalopram failed you while duloxetine appears not to have is **expected rather than anomalous**. You do not need a special explanation for being different from a trial average. Being different from a trial average is the normal condition.

### The one distinction that shortens the list

If everything is individual, the list would be infinite, and it is not. There is a single distinction that shortens it, and it is the intellectual engine of this page.

**A mechanism killed by heterogeneity is not dead.** A trial that found no average effect is entirely compatible with a large effect in one person. A null average is a statement about a mean, and you are not a mean. Those candidates stay on the list, and the honest label for them is not "refuted" but "untested in anyone like you."

**A mechanism killed by logic or by measurement error is dead**, and no amount of individual variation revives it. If a model predicts the *opposite* of what you report, being unusual does not rescue it, because the model is not making a claim about averages, it is making a claim about direction. If its timescale is off by a factor of a thousand, being unusual does not rescue it. If it rests on comparing two studies that used different scanners on different brain regions, or on somebody misreading the paper they cited, then it was never a claim about people at all.

**This is why the graveyard at the bottom of this page is short.** Almost nothing here died because a trial came out negative. The things that died, died of arithmetic.

### The shape of the list

Ranked by fit-to-you multiplied by evidence quality, and explicitly not by elegance, the list comes out in an order that irritated the researchers who produced it.

**The dullest candidate ranks first.** Duloxetine reduced your anxiety and improved your sleep continuity, and the vivid dreams, the reduced morning fatigue and the minimal pain change all follow from those two things. It ranks first for one reason: it is the only candidate on the list with a positive randomised trial at your actual dose. Nicolini 2009 ran a fixed 20mg arm in generalised anxiety, and it separated from placebo on total anxiety and on the psychic factor `[primary, verified]`. It explains four of your ten features natively, including the three you told us you value most. And it **cannot** explain the acute worsening, and it **cannot** explain why escitalopram gave you nothing. Those are gaps in its scope rather than contradictions of it, which is exactly why it stays on the list rather than joining the graveyard. It is worth saying plainly that eight passes were so busy hunting for mechanism that nobody rated the obvious explanation until the ninth.

**The only candidate that predicts your acute worsening ranks second.** Same lab in Copenhagen, same paradigm, same population, sequential studies designed to extend each other: escitalopram at 10mg left P50 sensory gating **intact**; escitalopram at 15mg **disrupted** it `[primary, verified ×2]`. Change detection was elevated at both doses and habituation was delayed at both. Read the higher dose as a description of a person and it is you at a festival: detects more, filters less, stops responding more slowly. That is not a metaphor for sensory overwhelm, it is an electrophysiological description of one. The caveats travel with it and they are large. One lab, with no independent replication `[primary, verified — by absence]`. Every study a single acute dose in a drug-naive healthy volunteer, so whether the cost persists on chronic dosing is unknown `[no evidence exists]`. Around twenty men per study. Escitalopram rather than duloxetine. And it is a comparison across two studies rather than one dose-response experiment, which is this chain's single most repeated error, committed here in the open. The one independent lab that touched the question ran the *opposite* manipulation and found that depleting serotonin breaks a different gate and spares P50, which is a mirror image and argues that serotonin and gating is not one dial but something closer to an inverted U `[primary, verified]`.

**The most elegant candidate ranks third, and its elegance is the reason to distrust it.** Chattarji's lab has shown repeatedly that a single two-hour stressor produces a delayed amygdala state ten days later, and that blocking NMDA receptors *during* the stressor prevents it `[primary, verified]`. Musazzi showed that chronic antidepressants at fourteen days, with an SNRI among the four drugs, completely prevent the stress-induced glutamate surge while corticosterone rises normally in the same animals `[primary, verified]`. Join those two and you get: the drug dampens the glutamate surge during the crowd, so the delayed consolidation never happens, so the overwhelm occurs and the crash does not. It is the only candidate whose native timescale is your timescale. **The join has never been tested by anybody** `[no evidence exists]`. It crosses a brain region without warrant, because Musazzi measured prefrontal cortex and Chattarji's effect is in the amygdala. Chattarji's drug arm is confounded, because the vehicle gavage worked too `[primary, verified]`. It is animal-only. And it predicts your acute experience would be **intact**, not worse, because that is what "corticosterone rises normally" means. It is the best timescale match anyone has found, and it is a hypothesis with two verified halves and an unverified seam.

### The demotion, and why it is the most useful story here

Candidate 5 was the orchestrator's number one, and watching it fall is more instructive than any of the survivors, because it shows you what these arguments look like from the inside when they break.

The story went: duloxetine inhibits norepinephrine reuptake, which lowers locus coeruleus tonic firing through α2 autoreceptor feedback, which raises the phasic-to-tonic ratio. Read that off and you get baseline arousal down, acute reactivity up. That is your entire report from a single mechanism with nothing bolted on. It fit beautifully.

It fit beautifully because **the source had been misread**. The paper is Bari and Aston-Jones, 2013 `[primary, verified]`. Its abstract says that atomoxetine decreased tonic activity *"although leaving intact the sensory-evoked excitatory component of LC phasic response"*, and that the **inhibitory** component was **increased** in magnitude and **prolonged** in duration. So the ratio rose because the **denominator fell** and the brake grew, not because the numerator rose. "Acute reactivity up" is not in that paper. It is the opposite of what that paper reports.

Then the theory contradicts it as well. Aston-Jones and Cohen's adaptive gain theory, which the candidate invokes by name, defines high tonic firing as *"disengagement from the current task and a search for alternative behaviors (exploration)"* `[primary, verified]`. **Tonic is not "baseline arousal." It is a distractible mode.** So lowering it predicts *better* filtering, not worse. The sanity check settles it: atomoxetine is prescribed to *reduce* distractibility. If raising the phasic-to-tonic ratio made people more sensory-overwhelmed, atomoxetine would be a bad ADHD drug, and it is not.

Then the decisive study, which nobody in eight passes had found. Alba-Delgado 2012 `[primary, verified]`: duloxetine, 5 mg/kg/day, seven days, osmotic minipump, in vivo recording from the locus coeruleus itself. It is **the only chronic-duloxetine locus coeruleus recording in existence**. The sensory-evoked excitatory response went **down**. That is the "acute reactivity up" claim, tested in your own drug, at a chronic duration, and it came out the other way.

And then a counterexample sitting inside your own history. The tonic limb is not specific to norepinephrine at all. SSRIs lower locus coeruleus tonic firing too, by an entirely different route, because serotonin neurons tonically inhibit the locus coeruleus, and Szabo proposes exactly that as *the anxiolytic mechanism of SSRIs* `[primary, verified]`. **Escitalopram is an SSRI, and it gave you zero anxiolysis.** That is the kind of counterexample heterogeneity cannot rescue, because you are not a population the candidate is being generalised to. You are the single case it is being fitted to.

So candidate 5 went from first to fifth. What survives is worth having and is smaller than advertised: duloxetine may have lowered your baseline arousal, which is consistent with your sleep improving, and which a nap attempt might speak to. That is a decent theory of your sleep. It is not a theory of everything, and it never predicted the acute half at all.

### The graveyard, and the inversion at the centre of it

The dead list is short, and every entry earned its place by arithmetic rather than by a negative trial.

The star exhibit is the dorsal raphe 5-HT1A model, and it is worth understanding for what it reveals about the field rather than about you. Maier and Watkins' learned-helplessness model says that uncontrollable stress **desensitises** the inhibitory 5-HT1A autoreceptors on dorsal raphe serotonin neurons, which removes the brake, which sensitises those neurons, which produces the behavioural collapse `[primary, verified]`. Chronic fluoxetine, at fourteen days, does the same thing to the same receptor in the same nucleus: the neurons become resistant to autoinhibition and maintain their firing `[primary, verified]`.

**Same receptor. Same nucleus. Same end state.** One literature calls it learned helplessness. The other calls it the antidepressant mechanism. **Nobody has reconciled them.** Read as it was proposed, the model predicts that duloxetine *installs* your crash state rather than preventing it. Which means your Saturday is a **counterexample** to that model, not a confirmation of it. That is not a caveat to be filed at the bottom. That is a model running backwards.

The rest of the graveyard is the same species of error at smaller scale. A duration story borrowed from the bed nucleus of the stria terminalis, where "longer-lasting" means tens of minutes in a startle paradigm and your episodes last one to two weeks, so the analogy is off by roughly a thousandfold. A diagnostic match to non-fearful panic disorder, where panic peaks in minutes. A propranolol parallel that turns out to be an anti-parallel, because propranolol *blunts* acute reactivity and yours rose above 140 bpm. None of these are close calls.

### Why cognitive therapies never worked

One feature of your presentation does more diagnostic work than it looks like it should, and it is the **absence of negative cognitive content**. Your episodes do not come with negative thoughts attached. That is why cognitive therapies never worked on them, because there was never a thought pattern there to restructure.

Now notice what several candidates on this list predict. Candidate 2 predicts an electrophysiological change with no propositions anywhere in it. Candidate 3 predicts synaptic plasticity in the amygdala, which likewise contains no beliefs. Both describe **a state rather than a thought**, and a state is not something a person can be argued out of. That convergence is not evidence that either mechanism is correct, and it should not be read as any. What it is, is a reason that "cognitive therapy didn't work" was never a fact about your effort or your insight. It was a category mismatch between the tool and the thing.

One thing must not happen here, and it is worth naming because this chain has already made this exact error once. Duloxetine improves the HAM-A **psychic** factor and not the **somatic** factor, across eight trials and 2,399 patients `[primary, verified]`. It is tempting to map "somatic anxiety" onto "anxiety without cognitive content" and declare a match. **Do not.** The HAM-A somatic factor is a list of physical symptom items: cardiovascular, gastrointestinal, respiratory, autonomic. It is not your phenomenology. The chain previously matched you to non-fearful panic disorder because both involve an absence, when in fact non-fearful panic is an absence of *fear* and yours is an absence of *cognitions*, and those are different things. An absence is not a fingerprint, and a symptom checklist is not a description of what it is like to be you.

### The trap

Everything above is mechanism. There is one place where mechanism touches a decision, and it is worth knowing before your appointment rather than after it.

You have a hypothesis: the crashes aggravate your ME/CFS and fibromyalgia, so preventing crashes should improve pain slowly, at 20mg, with no threshold to cross. The standard model says pain relief requires crossing the noradrenergic threshold at roughly 40 to 60mg, and that 20mg does approximately nothing for pain. **Right now, those two models predict different things.** At 60mg they predict the same thing, because 60mg crosses the threshold *and* keeps preventing crashes.

**So if pain improves at 60mg, both models are satisfied, and you learn nothing about your own hypothesis, permanently.** The 20mg window is the only clean test of your idea that will ever exist for you, and it is open right now.

This is not an argument for staying at 20mg. Months of unrelieved pain is a real cost, and pain matters to you; you said so explicitly, and you corrected an earlier framing that pitted the pain benefit against the psychological one. You want both. This is an argument for knowing that the window exists before it shuts, because a decision looks different when you know you are spending something rather than nothing.

And there is a separating observation that costs nothing at all. Your model has to run through the fatigue limb, because the only prospective path from distress to pain in fibromyalgia that survives analysis runs distress to fatigue to pain; the direct distress-to-pain path was null `[primary, verified]`. **Therefore: track fatigue and pain as separate lines. If pain improves while fatigue does not, your model is dead**, whatever else is helping you.

### What nobody has studied

Finally, the claims on this page most likely to be true, because they are the ones that assert the least.

Nobody has studied duloxetine in autistic adults; there is one three-patient case series. Nobody has studied the overwhelm-to-crash cycle pharmacologically; the autistic burnout literature contains no pharmacology whatsoever, and its recovery advice is entirely environmental and behavioural. Nobody has studied whether prior dissociation predicts recurrence. And the fourth, which is the one that matters: **nobody has studied this decoupling in any human, for any drug.** Not weakly, not in a small sample, not in the wrong population. The question has never been asked.

Read that fourth absence against the list and it explains the list's shape. The human evidence explains the acute half and says nothing about any aftermath. The animal evidence explains the aftermath half and has never been given the drug. **No study, in any species, has both halves.** So a person who converted sensory overwhelm into a two-week collapse for decades, took a drug for two weeks, then absorbed five hours of two crowded festivals and two panic attacks and did not collapse, has produced an observation that the literature has no entry for. That does not make the observation right. **It makes it data**, and it is a reason to record it carefully rather than a reason to believe it.

### What to take from this

Not a mechanism. A shape.

The shape is that your report is currently better than the literature that is supposed to explain it. Three candidates each explain a different third of you, drawn from three fields that have never spoken to one another, and the seams between them are precisely where the explanations ought to be. The most attractive story on the list turned out to be the most misread. The most elegant one is animal-only with an untested join and predicts the wrong sign on your most distinctive observation. The one with the best evidence is the dullest one, and it cannot explain the thing that makes your case interesting. The one that does predict your acute worsening rests on twenty healthy men in Copenhagen who each took one pill, once. And not one candidate on the list explains your restless legs, which is worth noticing because it shows the list is not being stretched to cover everything.

None of that means you should distrust what you observed. It means the honest description of your situation is that you are running an experiment nobody has run, in a body nobody has studied, on a two-week observation window against a baseline of episodes that arrived several times a month. Two weeks is short. The confidence available here is genuinely low, and it is low for reasons that have nothing to do with whether the drug is working.

What is worth doing with that is small and specific. Watch for a breakthrough crash after an exposure no worse than the festivals, because that is the cleanest falsifier available to you and it costs nothing to watch for. Keep fatigue and pain as separate lines, because that one split is what separates your hypothesis from the standard one. Notice whether the sleep benefit holds on quiet weeks as well as eventful ones, because candidate 1 predicts it should and the crash-focused candidates predict nothing about it either way. And treat the whole list as what it actually is: not seven guesses at one answer, but seven partial answers to a question nobody has yet asked properly.

You said you did not think it was possible to get more certain than "possibly valid," because we do not understand the brain well enough to go beyond that. That is the correct read, and it is not a counsel of despair. It is the thing that lets you stop hunting for *the* explanation and start noticing which part of you each one covers.

## Deep Dive

### How the ratings work

Every candidate is scored against ten features of your presentation. Those ten are the complete set of things that need explaining, and they are drawn from your own reports rather than from what the literature finds convenient.

acute-worse · crash-absent · no-cognitive-content · sleep-better · vivid dreams · less morning fatigue · RLS-worse · pain-minimal · escitalopram-failed · nap-failure

The scores mean:

- **✓✓** the candidate predicts this natively, without an extra assumption
- **✓** the candidate is consistent with this
- **○** the candidate is silent on this
- **✗** the candidate predicts the **opposite** of this

Ranking is by fit-to-you multiplied by evidence quality. It is deliberately not by elegance, which is why the most elegant candidate ranks third and the dullest ranks first.

Three properties of the completed matrix are worth reading before the rows:

1. **No row is complete.** Not one candidate scores above ○ on more than five of the ten features.
2. **One column is empty.** Nothing on the list explains why your restless legs got worse. Category 8 handles that separately, and the honest statement here is that the candidate list does not reach it.
3. **The acute-worse column is where the list breaks.** One candidate predicts it. Four predict its opposite.

### The rated candidate list

#### #1 — Anxiolysis and sleep continuity ("the boring one")

**The claim.** Duloxetine 20mg reduced your anxiety and improved your sleep continuity, and everything else you report follows from those two things.

**Mechanism.** Serotonergic anxiolysis at roughly 75% striatal SERT occupancy `[primary, verified — Abanades 2011, PMID 20940733]`, plus reduced nocturnal awakenings. Nicolas and Ruby 2020 (PMID 33224081) identify reduced intra-sleep awakenings as the variable that best explains changed dream recall `[primary, verified]`, which would explain the vivid dreams **and** the reduced morning fatigue with a single mechanism rather than two.

**Evidence tier.** RCT-backed, and it is **the only candidate with a positive trial at your exact dose**. Nicolini 2009 (PMID 18485261): fixed 20mg in generalised anxiety, n=84 in that arm, HAM-A total and psychic factor separated at p=0.01–0.001 `[primary, verified]`. The somatic factor did not separate (p=0.07).

**Fit.** acute-worse **✗** · crash-absent **✓** · no-cognitive-content **○** · sleep-better **✓✓** · vivid dreams **✓✓** · less morning fatigue **✓✓** · RLS-worse **○** · pain-minimal **✓✓** · escitalopram-failed **✗** · nap-failure **○**

**What it predicts you would observe.** The benefit is **not contingent on exposure**. It should hold on quiet weeks exactly as well as on festival weeks. Dreams and sleep should track together as one thing rather than two.

**What would falsify it.** Anxiety returning while sleep stays good, or the reverse, because under this account they are the same mechanism and should move together. Or episodes recurring with no change in either sleep or anxiety.

**Dose implication.** This is the one candidate with real dose evidence, and it cuts against escalation on serotonergic grounds specifically. SERT occupancy plateaus at the minimum approved dose (Sørensen 2022) `[primary, verified]`, so at roughly 75% you are already on the plateau and going up buys very little additional serotonergic engagement. Nicolini's higher-dose arm was **slightly better, not worse** (−15.3 points and 55% response versus −14.7 and 45%), but non-inferiority of 20mg was never tested against it, and the 20mg arm was n=84 against n=158.

**Why it is not dead.** Untested against the alternatives rather than refuted. Its weaknesses are gaps in scope, not contradictions.

**The hole, stated plainly.** Escitalopram gave you zero anxiolysis. If duloxetine's benefit is simply "an antidepressant reduced his anxiety," then why did the last antidepressant not? This candidate needs candidate 4 or candidate 7 to explain its own biggest anomaly. `[reasoning, not evidence]`

#### #2 — Serotonergic sensory-gating dose ladder

**The claim.** Raising serotonin acutely degrades sensory gating and habituation. That is why the festival was worse.

**Mechanism.** Same lab, same paradigm, same population, sequential dose extension `[primary, verified ×2]`. Jensen 2008 (PMID 18000656): escitalopram 10mg, n=21, **P50 gating intact**. Oranje 2011 (PMID 20971512): escitalopram 15mg, n=20, **P50 gating disrupted**. Plus mismatch negativity increased at both doses (Oranje 2008, PMID 17971261; Wienberg 2010, PMID 19304862) and habituation delayed at both (Jensen 2007, PMID 17299503). The composite reads: detects more, filters less, stops responding more slowly.

**Evidence tier.** Human, dose-graded, blind, placebo-controlled, crossover, **and single-lab**. All four studies come from Copenhagen/Glostrup `[primary, verified — the author search was enumerated]`.

**Fit.** acute-worse **✓✓** and it is **the only candidate that predicts it** · crash-absent **○** · no-cognitive-content **✓** (it is electrophysiology; there are no propositions in it) · sleep-better **○** · vivid dreams **○** · less morning fatigue **○** · RLS-worse **○** · pain-minimal **○** · escitalopram-failed **✓✓** because the studies **are** escitalopram · nap-failure **○**

**What it predicts you would observe.** A dose increase should make the acute sensory cost **worse**. You are contemplating a 3× increase. The only human dose comparison that exists flipped gating at 1.5×.

**What would falsify it.** Acute overwhelm improving over months at a fixed dose, which is what Zohar 1988's adaptive-subsensitivity finding would predict (PMID 3276283) `[primary, verified]`. Or a 60mg increase that leaves the acute experience unchanged.

**Dose implication.** It is the strongest mechanistic argument against 60mg anywhere in this project, and it bears on the thing you are most wary of.

**Why it is not dead.** Untested in chronic dosing rather than refuted `[no evidence exists]`. Every one of the four studies is a single acute dose in a drug-naive volunteer, so whether the cost persists at two weeks or two years is simply unknown.

**Limits, at the claim.** Cross-study rather than within-study: two papers, not one dose-response experiment. No independent replication. The only other lab that touched it (Mann 2008, PMID 17895917) ran the **opposite** manipulation and found that serotonin **depletion** disrupts prepulse inhibition and spares P50, which is a mirror image and argues for an inverted-U rather than a single dial `[primary, verified]`. Escitalopram is not duloxetine. And the leap from a P50 waveform in twenty healthy men to a crowded art festival is enormous and unbridged.

#### #3 — Chattarji delayed amygdala plus Musazzi glutamate decoupling

**The claim.** One acute stressor consolidates into a delayed multi-day state via NMDA and glutamate signalling **during** the event. Chronic antidepressants block the glutamate surge while leaving the stress response itself intact. So the overwhelm happens and the crash does not.

**Mechanism.** Yasmin 2016 (PMID 27798355): one 2-hour stressor produces, **ten days later**, increased basolateral amygdala mEPSC frequency, increased spine density, and increased anxiety-like behaviour; **APV infused during the acute stress prevents all of it** `[primary, verified]`. Musazzi 2010 (PMID 20052403): chronic antidepressant treatment for **14 days**, including **venlafaxine**, prevents the stress-induced glutamate surge **while corticosterone rises normally in the same animals** `[primary, verified]`.

**Evidence tier.** Animal-only, and the join between the two halves is a synthesis with no experiment behind it.

**Fit.** acute-worse **✗** because it predicts acute-**intact**, which is what normal corticosterone means · crash-absent **✓✓** and it is the only candidate whose native timescale **is** your timescale · no-cognitive-content **✓✓** · sleep-better **○** · vivid dreams **○** · less morning fatigue **○** · RLS-worse **○** · pain-minimal **○** · escitalopram-failed **✗** · nap-failure **○**

**What it predicts you would observe.** Crash protection should be **all-or-nothing per exposure** and **not dose-graded**, because the glutamate surge is either dampened or it is not. It should hold for exposures of any intensity, because Chattarji's trigger is a single discrete stressor rather than a cumulative load.

**What would falsify it.** A breakthrough crash following an exposure no worse than the festivals. **That is the cleanest falsifier available to you and it costs nothing to watch for.**

**Dose implication.** None available. Musazzi used one dose per drug. **Whether the protection scales with dose is completely unknown** `[no evidence exists]`, and this is the thing that should worry you most about going up. If the protection is already maximal at 20mg, a dose increase buys only the cost. If the protection scales, the trade holds. There is no evidence either way and the two possibilities have opposite implications.

**Why it is not dead.** Untested rather than refuted. The underlying phenomenon is robust across five papers from Chattarji's lab.

**Limits, at the claim.** **The regions do not match.** Musazzi measured prefrontal and frontal cortex; Chattarji's effect is in the amygdala. **Nobody has shown that chronic antidepressants dampen the glutamate surge in the basolateral amygdala** `[no evidence exists]`, and joining a prefrontal drug effect to an amygdalar plasticity effect because both involve glutamate is exactly the cross-region move this chain has repeatedly condemned and then committed. **Chattarji's drug arm is vehicle-confounded**: Chakraborty 2019 (PMID 31193585) reports that *"gavage of only the vehicle also had a protective effect"* `[primary, verified]`, so the "drugs prevent it" limb is far weaker than the phenomenon itself. Musazzi discloses Servier consultancy, and agomelatine is one of the four drugs tested. Rats. Footshock and immobilisation. Not duloxetine, not a human.

#### #4 — Escitalopram-specific allosteric S2 mechanism

**The claim.** Escitalopram failed you because of something escitalopram does that duloxetine does not, rather than because of a quantity of serotonin.

**Mechanism.** Escitalopram binds an allosteric S2 site on the serotonin transporter and slows its own dissociation from the orthosteric site, which is a mechanism no other antidepressant has in the same form. The review is Zhong, Haddjeri and Sánchez 2012 (PMID 21901317) `[primary, verified]`. Its **relevance to your dissociation** is entirely unevidenced `[no evidence exists]`.

**Evidence tier.** Mechanistic inference.

**Fit.** escitalopram-failed **✓✓** and it is the only candidate that addresses it at all · everything else **○**

**What it predicts you would observe.** Nothing you can check. Other SSRIs without the S2 mechanism should not reproduce your escitalopram reaction, but that is not a test anybody should run.

**What would falsify it.** A future dissociative reaction to a non-allosteric serotonergic drug. Again, not a test to run.

**Dose implication.** None.

**Why it is not dead.** Never studied. It survives by never having been examined, which is a weak kind of survival and should be read as one.

**Why it matters anyway.** It rests on the strongest negative finding in the project. Matched on region, tracer, modality, dosing paradigm and population, duloxetine 20mg and escitalopram 10mg deliver **indistinguishable** SERT occupancy: escitalopram 10mg's own between-study range is 59–88%, which entirely contains duloxetine's 71–78% `[primary, verified — Sørensen 2022]`. **So occupancy is not the explanatory variable**, which means the explanation has to be something drug-specific. This candidate is the placeholder for that gap. It is a well-posed question rather than an answer.

#### #5 — Tonic/phasic locus coeruleus shift *(demoted from #1)*

**The claim.** Norepinephrine reuptake inhibition lowers locus coeruleus tonic firing via α2 autoreceptors, lowering baseline arousal.

**Mechanism, and it got stronger in places.** Duloxetine-specific support now exists: Arai 2025 (PMID 40481171) reports that duloxetine increased noradrenaline around the locus coeruleus and decreased pERK immunoreactivity `[primary, verified]`, which is the α2 mechanism in your own drug. The timescale limb holds and is fast: LC tonic suppression is present at 2 days and does not grow between day 2 and day 21 (Szabo 2000, PMID 11343573) `[primary, verified]`, so at 14 days you are well past the point where this effect would be fully expressed. The persistence limb holds: the somatodendritic α2 autoreceptors that control **LC firing** are **not** desensitised by chronic treatment, even though the ones controlling dendritic **release** are (Mateo 2001, PMID 11498523) `[primary, verified]`. The candidate needs the firing one, and the firing one survives.

**Evidence tier.** Animal-only for the tonic limb. **Contradicted for the phasic limb.**

**Fit.** acute-worse **✗** and it predicts acute-**better**, twice over · crash-absent **○** and silent, because "less arousal therefore no two-week collapse" is a hope with a receptor attached rather than an argument · no-cognitive-content **✓** · sleep-better **✓** · vivid dreams **○** · less morning fatigue **✓** · RLS-worse **○** · pain-minimal **○** · escitalopram-failed **✗** · **nap-failure ✓**, which is where it earns its place

**What it predicts you would observe.** The nap test. Lower tonic arousal should mean shorter nap latency. This is the one candidate a nap attempt speaks to, which is why the test is worth running and also why a null will not settle it: duloxetine improved your sleep, which reduces sleep drive, which makes napping **harder**. Bonnet and Arand measured that directly, and sleep deprivation drove nap latency from 13.4 minutes to 3.8 `[primary, verified]`. **The drug pushes the outcome both ways at once, so a negative result is uninterpretable.** A positive result is informative. Categories 6 and 7 carry the protocol.

**What would falsify it.** Already partly fired. The phasic limb is contradicted in duloxetine at 7 days (Alba-Delgado 2012, PMID 22591831) `[primary, verified]`.

**Dose implication.** **None. It is dose-silent.** Bari and Aston-Jones's own conclusion is that *"ATM effects on LC neuronal activity are dose-dependent, with different doses affecting different aspects of LC firing"* `[primary, verified]`. Three doses produced three qualitatively different patterns, which is a dose-*character* change rather than a dose-response curve. Anyone predicting the ratio at 60mg is extrapolating from an anaesthetised rat given one injection of a different drug.

**Why it is not dead.** The tonic limb is untested in humans rather than refuted, so it survives. The phasic limb is contradicted by direct duloxetine evidence, so that limb is dead. The candidate is not dead; the version of it that was ranked first is.

**The structural warning.** Its autism premise (Kim 2022, PMID 36164264: autistic children show increased tonic and reduced phasic LC-NE activity) is **the identical contested premise that an earlier pass correctly deleted**, revived with its sign flipped. It was deleted because de Vries 2021 (PMID 33172600) found the baseline pupil evidence *"conflicting"* at meta-analytic level. It is measured with an instrument that a 2022 eLife paper shows does not index the locus coeruleus (Megemont, PMID 35107419: *"pupil diameter is not an accurate real-time readout of locus coeruleus activity"*) `[primary, verified]`. And in Kim's own data the indices tracked **ADHD symptomatology, not ASD** `[primary, verified]`. **If the premise is too weak to support "duloxetine pushes the wrong way," it is exactly as too weak to support "duloxetine pushes the right way." It is unavailable to both sides.**

#### #6 — Your CFS-aggravation hypothesis

**The claim.** The crashes aggravate your ME/CFS and fibromyalgia, so preventing them should improve pain slowly at 20mg, with no threshold to cross.

**Mechanism, and it is three separable claims with wildly different status.** Claim 1, that sensory and emotional overload triggers crashes, is **established as clinical description**, and it is described in a primary consensus document using your own word: the Canadian Consensus Criteria 2003 state that *"Overload phenomena may trigger a 'crash' where the patient becomes temporarily immobilized"* `[primary, verified]`. Claim 2, that crashes aggravate the illness, is **unstudied** `[no evidence exists]`. Claim 3, that therefore preventing crashes improves pain, has never been tested.

**Evidence tier.** Claim 1 established. Claim 2 no evidence, and adjacent evidence leans against. Claim 3 speculation.

**Fit.** crash-absent **✓** (it is a theory *about* the crash rather than about the drug) · pain-minimal **✓✓** because it explains why pain has not moved yet: the mechanism is slow by construction · acute-worse **○** · everything else **○**

**What it predicts you would observe.** Your model must run through the fatigue limb, because Okifuji 2011 (PMID 20591745) found the **direct** distress-to-pain path was **null** and only distress→fatigue→pain survived `[primary, verified]`. **So track fatigue and pain separately. If pain improves while fatigue does not, this is falsified.** Second prediction: a breakthrough crash should leave a **durable multi-week pain signature**. If pain returns to baseline within days, the aggravation limb is falsified even though crash-prevention would still be real, and the crashes would then be unpleasant but not damaging, which is a completely different thing and changes what the crash benefit is worth.

**What would falsify it.** Pain improving without fatigue improving. Or a breakthrough crash whose pain resolves in days.

**Dose implication. This is the trap, and it is the real decision content.** 60mg crosses the noradrenergic threshold **and** keeps preventing crashes. If pain improves at 60mg, both models are satisfied and you learn nothing about your own hypothesis, permanently `[reasoning, not evidence]`. The 20mg window is the only clean test of your hypothesis that will ever exist, and a dose increase closes it irreversibly. Conversely, if you hold 20mg for three to six months and pain is unchanged while crashes stay prevented, your pain limb is cleanly falsified and the dose decision becomes trivial.

**Why it is not dead.** Killed by heterogeneity and by absence of research, **not by logic**, so it survives. Your exact intersection (autistic, sensory-triggered crashes, ME/CFS, fibromyalgia) is the population these studies exclude or never assemble. A PubMed search for `"autistic burnout" AND ("chronic fatigue" OR "ME/CFS" OR "post-exertional")` returns **one result** `[primary, verified — search-level]`.

**Three places adjacent evidence leans against it, stated because honesty requires it.** First, allostatic load in CFS: duration of fatigue is **inversely** correlated with the index (r=−0.26, p=0.047), so cumulative damage predicts the opposite of what your model needs `[primary abstract only — Maloney 2009]`. Second, prospectively, the allostatic-load limb that predicts pain is **metabolic**, and parasympathetic dysregulation predicted nothing `[primary, verified — Liang & Booker 2024]`, which means your model runs entirely through the limb that fails. Third, Okifuji's direct distress-to-pain path was null `[primary, verified]`.

**Why it must not be dropped.** It is your own hypothesis, it is not a crank idea, and Claim 1 is a genuine find: you independently rediscovered a documented clinical phenomenon and used the same word for it. But Claim 2 is the load-bearing limb and it is unsupported. On current evidence this should not be the reason to stay at 20mg. **The information-value argument is a much better reason to stay than the pain argument.**

#### #7 — Chronic 5-HT2C adaptation opposing acute action

**The claim.** Acute serotonin is anxiogenic and dissociogenic via 5-HT2C; chronic treatment desensitises 5-HT2C; so the benefit is the adaptation rather than the serotonin.

**Mechanism.** The human evidence for the desensitisation half is Zohar 1988 (PMID 3276283): n=9 OCD patients, m-CPP challenge before and after four months of clomipramine, showing *"adaptive subsensitivity to the serotonergic agonist mCPP"* `[primary, verified]`.

**Evidence tier.** Mechanistic inference, with one n=9 human study pointing the wrong way.

**Fit.** acute-worse **✗** because Zohar predicts acute-**better** · crash-absent **✓** · escitalopram-failed **✓** · everything else **○**

**Dose implication.** An earlier pass predicted that a dose increase should **transiently** re-create the acute state. The prediction's direction is right and **the word "transiently" is not supported**. It came from this account, which fails on other grounds, and every gating study is single-dose, so nothing in the literature speaks to duration `[no evidence exists]`.

**Why it is demoted but not dead, and the distinction matters because you asked for it.** It is a **single-mechanism** account: 5-HT2C does the acute badness and 5-HT2C desensitisation does the benefit. **A single mechanism cannot move two things in opposite directions.** If 5-HT2C is desensitised, both the acute overwhelm and the crash should improve together. If it is not yet desensitised at two weeks, both should still be bad. The account has **no state** in which the acute is worse and the crash is absent. That is a **logic** argument, not a heterogeneity argument, so it is **dead as an account of the dissociation**. But it is not dead as a partial account of the crash half, where it is redundant with candidate 3, which has a better timescale and an identified molecular link.

**Why it is not fully dead.** Its wrong-direction problem rests on Zohar: n=9, no control group, no counterbalancing possible, an uncontrolled order effect, four months, clomipramine, OCD. That is a heterogeneity-grade objection rather than a logic-grade one, so the direction claim survives as weak. The single-mechanism argument is what kills it, and it kills it only for the job it was hired to do.

### The DRN inversion, in full

This one deserves its own section, because it is the clearest example on the page of what "killed by logic" means, and because it says something about the field rather than about you.

**In the learned-helplessness model** (Rozeske 2011, PMID 21976495) `[primary, verified]`, verbatim: *"reduced 5-HT1A-R function would... sensitize these cells"*, and *"These data suggest that uncontrollable stressors produce a desensitization of DRN 5-HT1A autoreceptors and that this desensitization is responsible for the behavioral consequences of uncontrollable stress."* Desensitised autoreceptors mean the brake is off, the dorsal raphe is sensitised, and helplessness follows.

**In the antidepressant literature** (Zhang 2025, PMID 40309832) `[primary, verified]`, fluoxetine 10 mg/kg/day for 14 days *"downregulated the 5-HT activation of the inhibitory GirK current in 5-HT neurons"*. Hyperpolarisation fell from −26.03 mV to −8.7 mV. The neurons become resistant to autoinhibition and maintain their spiking.

**And independently**, from a different lab on a different continent, Laaris 1997 (PMID 9243251) `[primary, verified]` showed the stress limb in rats: 16 hours in novel uncontrolled conditions *"significantly reduced the cell response to ipsapirone, indicating a decreased sensitivity of somatodendritic 5-HT1A autoreceptors"*, and the change did not occur in adrenalectomised rats, so it is corticosterone-dependent.

| | **Uncontrollable stress** | **Chronic SSRI** |
|---|---|---|
| 5-HT1A somatodendritic autoreceptor | **Desensitised** | **Desensitised** |
| Autoinhibitory brake on the dorsal raphe | **Removed** | **Removed** |
| DRN firing to subsequent input | **Increased** | **Maintained / increased** |
| Serotonin output | **Increased** | **Increased** |
| **What the field calls it** | **"Sensitization" → helplessness** | **"The antidepressant mechanism"** |

**The two mechanisms are the same event.** One literature calls it the disease. The other calls it the cure. Nobody has reconciled them, and Rozeske's own authors flag the problem in their discussion, noting that *"acute fluoxetine is known to cause 5-HT1A-R internalization in the DRN"* `[primary, verified]`. They raise it precisely because it threatens their causal claim.

**Two caveats against the tidiness of that table** `[reasoning, not evidence]`. Rozeske and Laaris are rats and Zhang is mice, so the two halves are not the same species, and a within-species head-to-head does not exist. And Rozeske's own discussion concedes that the sufficiency test has never been run: *"it would also be desirable to determine whether manipulations that reduce DRN 5-HT1A-R inhibitory function, in the absence of IS treatment, also produce typical IS-induced behaviors"* `[primary, verified]`. **Nobody has checked whether desensitising these autoreceptors, on its own, produces helplessness.**

**What it means for you.** Read as it was proposed to explain your case, this model predicts that duloxetine installs your crash state rather than preventing it. So your Saturday is a counterexample to the model. It is dead, it is dead by logic rather than by heterogeneity, and it must not be resurrected.

### Candidate 5's demotion, in full

The most useful thing on this page, because it shows what a good fit looks like when it is wrong.

**What was read.** "NET inhibition lowers LC tonic firing via α2-autoreceptor feedback while raising terminal NE and raising the phasic:tonic ratio. Read what that predicts: baseline arousal DOWN, acute reactivity UP."

**What the source actually says.** Bari A, Aston-Jones G, *Neuropharmacology* 2013 Jan;64(1):53-64, PMID 22820275, abstract verbatim `[primary, verified]`:

> *"ATM caused a significant decrease of the tonic activity of LC single-units, **although leaving INTACT the sensory-evoked excitatory component of LC phasic response**. Moreover, **the magnitude of the INHIBITORY component of LC response to paw stimulation was INCREASED** after 1 mg/kg of ATM and its **duration was PROLONGED** at 0.3 mg/kg. **Together, these effects of ATM produced an increase in the phasic-to-tonic ratio** of LC phasic response to sensory stimulation."*

The excitatory phasic response was left **intact**, not increased. The ratio rose because the denominator fell and because the inhibitory component grew. **A ratio that rises because its denominator falls is not evidence that the numerator rose.** And the second half is worse for the reading: the *inhibitory* component of the LC's response to a sensory stimulus was increased in magnitude and prolonged in duration, which describes a system that damps down harder and for longer after each sensory event `[reasoning, not evidence — the measurements are primary]`. That is close to the opposite of a system that gets overwhelmed by a crowd.

**Four further problems with the source as used** `[primary, verified]`: it is **atomoxetine**, not duloxetine. It is a **single acute IP injection**, so it cannot speak to two-week dosing at all. It is **isoflurane-anaesthetised rats at 2–2.5%**, and you cannot measure sensory overwhelm in an unconscious animal. And the α2 mechanism is **cited from prior literature, not tested** in that paper.

**The theory contradicts the reading too.** Aston-Jones G, Cohen JD, *Annu Rev Neurosci* 2005;28:403-50, PMID 16022602, verbatim `[primary, verified]`:

> *"When utility in the task wanes, LC neurons exhibit a tonic activity mode, associated with **DISENGAGEMENT from the current task and a search for alternative behaviors (EXPLORATION)**."*

So `tonic → baseline arousal` is not the theory's mapping. Adaptive gain is a theory about a mode switch between exploitation and exploration, not about how much arousal there is. Run the candidate through the theory properly and lowering tonic moves the system **into** the focused, task-engaged mode, which predicts **better** filtering and **less** capture by irrelevant stimuli. **A crowded festival should be easier.** The sanity check: atomoxetine is prescribed to reduce distractibility. If NET inhibition made people more sensory-overwhelmed, atomoxetine would be a bad ADHD drug.

**The decisive study.** Alba-Delgado C, Mico JA, Sánchez-Blázquez P, Berrocoso E. *Pain.* 2012 Jul;153(7):1438-1449. PMID 22591831 `[primary, verified]`. Duloxetine 5 mg/kg/day, **7 days**, osmotic minipump, in vivo extracellular LC recording. **The only chronic-duloxetine LC recording in existence.** Verbatim: *"Antidepressant administration over 7 days... **DECREASED the excitatory firing rate of the early response**."*

The caveats belong at the claim `[reasoning, not evidence]`: the stimulus is noxious paw stimulation rather than a crowd, the model is neuropathic pain rather than autism, and the paper's own title says antidepressants *"promote the responsiveness"* of LC neurons, because the late C-fibre response was augmented and the inhibitory period shortened. So the picture is a **reshaping** rather than a simple suppression. But reshaping is not "acute reactivity up," and the specific component the reading needed went **down**. Note also that Alba-Delgado's inhibitory-period result runs **opposite** to Bari's: atomoxetine increased and prolonged the inhibitory component, duloxetine reduced it `[primary, verified ×2]`. Same claimed mechanism, two drugs, opposite signs on the same measurement, which is itself the strongest argument against extrapolating from atomoxetine to duloxetine on LC electrophysiology.

**The counterexample inside your own history.** Szabo ST, de Montigny C, Blier P. *Int J Neuropsychopharmacol.* 2000;3(1):1-11. PMID 11343573 `[primary, verified]`, verbatim:

> *"Sustained administration of the selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) citalopram... and paroxetine... produced a gradual DECREASE in spontaneous firing activity of locus coeruleus (LC) noradrenergic neurons."* And: *"given the hyperadrenergic state often observed in anxiogenic conditions the latter phenomenon is believed to **contribute more to the ANXIOLYTIC effect of SSRIs** than to their antidepressant action."*

So the tonic limb is **not NET-specific**. SSRIs lower LC tonic firing too, via serotonin's tonic inhibitory input, and Szabo proposes that as the anxiolytic mechanism of SSRIs. **Escitalopram is an SSRI and it gave you zero anxiolysis.** That damages the candidate three ways. It destroys its mechanistic specificity, because at 20mg duloxetine is predominantly serotonergic and the serotonergic route to lowered LC tonic firing is at least as available as the NET route the candidate is named after. It supplies a direct counterexample from your own history. And the best rescue available, which is that an SSRI lowers both limbs together while a NET inhibitor lowers tonic and spares phasic, leans entirely on the phasic limb, which is precisely the limb Alba-Delgado tested in duloxetine and found going the wrong way.

**And it is silent on the crash.** Nothing in Bari, Aston-Jones & Cohen, Szabo, Mateo, Rueter, Arai or Alba-Delgado concerns a multi-day delayed sequela `[primary, verified — by absence across seven retrieved papers]`. Every dependent variable in that literature is a firing rate, a pupil, a nap latency, a paw withdrawal or a molecular marker, measured within hours. The candidate's native timescale is seconds to days. Your crash is 7 to 14 days.

**And you cannot observe it.** There is no home measurement of LC tonic/phasic activity, and the pupil does not index it (Megemont 2022, PMID 35107419) `[primary, verified]`.

### The 8.5-fold spread, and what it licenses

Abanades S, et al. *J Cereb Blood Flow Metab.* 2011 Mar;31(3):944-52. PMID 20940733 `[primary, verified]`. Ten healthy men, duloxetine 20mg single dose then 20mg daily × 4 days, PET with [11C]DASB, scanned about 6 hours after the fourth dose.

| EC50 (direct fit) | 2.29 ± 1.11 ng/mL | **0.54 – 4.61** | **8.5-fold** |

Same drug. Same dose. Same protocol. Same laboratory. Same four days.

**What that licenses, stated carefully** `[reasoning, not evidence]`:

- **It licenses "the population curve does not describe any individual in it."** That is a direct reading of the table.
- **It licenses "try and see is correct clinical practice."** There is no measurement a prescriber could take that would place you within that 8.5-fold band. Titration is not laziness; it is the only instrument available.
- **It licenses "your Lexapro/duloxetine divergence is expected."** You do not need a special explanation for responding differently from a trial average, because responding differently from a trial average is the normal condition.
- **It does NOT license "therefore any mechanism might be true for you."** That is the move this page exists to refuse. Individual variation rescues a null average. It does not rescue a model that runs backwards. See the two kinds of wrong.

### What has never been studied

Six absences. The first four are the standing set. Do not read any of them as hedging; they are the highest-confidence claims on this page, because they assert the least.

1. **Nobody has studied duloxetine in autistic adults.** One three-patient case series exists.
2. **Nobody has studied the overwhelm-to-crash cycle pharmacologically.** The only pharmacological entry in the entire autistic-burnout literature is a single conference-abstract case report of a stimulant `[secondary — unverified]`. The recovery literature is entirely environmental and behavioural: rest, self-understanding, reduced masking, changed expectations. There is no pharmacology in it at all.
3. **Nobody has studied whether prior dissociation predicts recurrence.**
4. **Nobody has studied this decoupling in any human, for any drug** `[no evidence exists]`. Not "the evidence is weak." The question has never been asked.
5. **Nobody has measured what duloxetine, or any SNRI, does to daytime sleep propensity.** No MSLT study exists at any dose in any population `[primary, verified — search-level]`.
6. **Nobody has recorded a locus coeruleus neuron on chronic duloxetine in an awake animal**, let alone a human. The only chronic-duloxetine electrophysiology study (Rueter 1998, PMID 9580577) recorded the dorsal raphe and hippocampus and never touched the LC. The only chronic-duloxetine LC recording (Alba-Delgado 2012) is anaesthetised, in a neuropathic pain model, with noxious paw stimulation `[primary, verified ×2]`.

**Read absence 4 against the candidate list and it explains the list's shape.** The nearest thing that exists is Musazzi: a real acute-intact/downstream-blocked decoupling, in a rat. The human evidence (Oranje/Jensen) explains the acute half and says nothing about any aftermath. The animal evidence (Chattarji) explains the aftermath half and has never been given an antidepressant. The one human study of chronic serotonergic adaptation (Zohar) predicts acute-**better**. Propranolol prophylaxis is an anti-parallel. **No study, in any species, has both halves.**

### The trap, laid out

| | **Your model** | **The standard model** |
|---|---|---|
| The claim | 20mg prevents crashes → less cumulative load → pain improves slowly over months, no threshold | Pain relief requires noradrenergic reuptake inhibition at ~40–60mg. 20mg does approximately nothing for pain, ever |
| Prediction **now, at 20mg** | Pain drifts down over months; fatigue improves first | Pain stays flat indefinitely |
| Prediction **at 60mg** | **Pain improves** | **Pain improves** |
| What you learn at 60mg | **Nothing.** Both models satisfied | **Nothing.** Both models satisfied |

**Going to 60mg satisfies both models and destroys the experiment permanently** `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

The discriminators that remain available at 20mg, in descending order of strength:

1. **Order of effects (strongest, and free).** Your model must run through fatigue. So: fatigue improves first or concurrently, pain follows weeks later. The standard model predicts pain moves **without** any change in fatigue, and only after a dose increase. **Track them separately. If pain improves while fatigue does not, your model is dead.**
2. **Contingency on exposure (unique to your model).** Your benefit is purchased by crashes that did not happen, so it is contingent on exposure history and should be step-wise. The standard model's benefit is a pharmacological constant, indifferent to what your week contained. **The killer within-subject test: if a crash does break through, does pain step up and stay up for weeks?** If pain returns to baseline within days, the aggravation limb is falsified even though crash-prevention would still be real.
3. **Speed of onset.** Direct analgesia is fast; allostatic unloading is slow by construction. Pain dropping within 2–4 weeks of a dose change means direct pharmacology. Pain drifting down over months at a fixed dose means your model, or noise. Bergenheim supplies the noise floor `[primary, verified]`: 13% mean improvement over 10–12 years, with only 16% achieving ≥50%. **Spontaneous large improvement in fibromyalgia is rare**, so a substantial drift down at a fixed 20mg would be genuinely surprising and therefore informative.
4. **Pre-commit, or the test is worthless.** Specify how long and how much **in advance**. Without a pre-registered threshold, any outcome reads as confirmation ("it's working, just slowly"), which is exactly how an unfalsifiable belief forms.

**The sensory-gating dose warning, restated because it belongs next to the trap.** You have already accepted worse acute sensitivity at 20mg. The Jensen/Oranje ladder says a **1.5×** step flipped the filter from intact to disrupted. You are contemplating **3×**. The claim that the acute window would re-open only "transiently" is **unsupported**: every study in that literature is single-dose, so chronicity has never been measured `[no evidence exists]`.

**And the asymmetry that is the actual decision content** `[reasoning, not evidence]`: the cost of waiting is months of unrelieved pain, and the benefit is a clean, unrepeatable answer. That is a question only you can answer. This page's job is to make sure you know the window exists before it shuts. **Nothing here directs a dose.**

### What would tell you more

Four things, all free, none of which requires anybody's permission.

1. **A breakthrough crash after an exposure no worse than the festivals** would falsify candidate 3 cleanly. It is the sharpest single falsifier on the list.
2. **Fatigue and pain as separate journal lines** separate your model from the standard one, and they are the same instrument.
3. **Whether the sleep benefit holds on quiet weeks** separates candidate 1 (which predicts it should, because it is not contingent on exposure) from candidates 3 and 6 (which are silent about it).
4. **Whether dreams and sleep move together** tests candidate 1's internal coherence, because under that account they are one mechanism rather than two.

One thing that will **not** tell you more, and it is worth knowing before you spend effort on it: a **failed** nap attempt tells you nothing, because duloxetine improved your sleep, which reduces sleep drive, which makes napping harder, and the two effects cancel by an unknown amount. A **successful** nap on a lifelong hard-zero channel would be genuinely informative. The test is worth running for the positive and worthless for the negative, and you should know that before you run it rather than after. Categories 6 and 7 carry the protocol.
