# Why It Might Be Working: Candidate Mechanisms, Rated

> On one Saturday, the same drug moved two things in opposite directions in the same person. The acute sensory overwhelm got worse. The crash that always follows it never arrived. Everything on this page is an attempt to say what could do that, and nothing on this page succeeds.

## Narrative

The facts first, because they are the constraint that everything else has to satisfy. Five hours across two crowded festivals. Two panic attacks, with a heart rate above 140 beats per minute while standing still. In your history that combination is not a risk factor, it is a guarantee: one day of that kind of input, then one to two weeks of dissociation, severe anxiety, and intense depression. The acute experience on the drug was, by your own account, *worse* than it would have been without it. And then nothing happened. In your words: *"Its lack was very stark, not just to me but to my whole family. We all braced for it, but it never came."*

Read that as two measurements rather than one story, because two measurements is what it is. Measurement one: acute sensory reactivity went **up**. Measurement two: the delayed sequela went **down**, all the way to zero. Same drug, same person, same day, opposite directions.

That shape does useful work immediately, because it kills a whole class of explanation before any pharmacology is needed. If the drug were sedating you, blunting you, or globally muting your emotional range, then the acute overwhelm would have been *milder*, because that is what those words mean. It was not milder. And this is not the shape expectancy predicts either. A hopeful person starting a new medication before a hard day reports the hard day feeling *easier*. You reported it feeling worse, and you reported the absence of a thing your family had independently braced for. Expectancy does not usually predict the disconfirming half of its own observation, and it does not usually recruit witnesses. `[reasoning, not evidence]`

So the question this page has to answer is not "why did the episodes stop." It is narrower and stranger. **Why is the overwhelm decoupled from the crash?**

Here is the answer, stated at the top so that nothing below reads as a build-up to a reveal. **No single candidate explains you.** The best account available is three separate mechanisms, drawn from three separate literatures that have never been joined by anybody, and two of those three have never been tested in a human at all. One of them explains the acute half. One explains the crash half. Two explain the sleep half. Nothing explains all of it, and this page will not manufacture a winner by promoting the least-bad option.

### Your instruction, and why it was right

You wrote:

> *"Any reasonable hypothesis will always find a counter example in the population. That doesn't prove the hypothesis wrong; it just proves the hypothesis wrong for some of the population. The problem, to my mind, is you're trying to find a single explanation that can't be disproved instead of compiling a list of possibly valid explanations."*

That diagnosis is correct, and this project is the evidence for it. Eight research passes went into this deep dive. Each one crowned a mechanism, and the next one killed it, and the pass after that killed the killer. The pattern is not a chain of increasing accuracy. It is **alternating over-correction**: each pass correctly identifies its predecessor's methodological sin and then commits the same sin in reverse. Recency is not reliability, and this ninth pass has no special claim to being the one that finally got it right.

The number that makes your argument quantitative rather than philosophical is this. Abanades and colleagues, at a GlaxoSmithKline imaging centre in London, gave ten healthy men the same drug at the same dose on the same schedule, scanned them, and fitted an EC50 to each individual separately. The values ran from 0.54 to 4.61 ng/mL `[primary, verified]`. **That is an 8.5-fold spread across ten people who were treated identically.** Their plasma concentrations varied 5.7-fold on the same milligrams. The population average describes none of them.

This is the mechanism behind everything you already suspected. It is why online reports of the same drug read like reports of different drugs. It is why "try it and see" is correct clinical practice rather than laziness, because there is no measurement that would tell a prescriber where in that 8.5-fold band you sit. And it is why the fact that escitalopram failed you while duloxetine appears not to have is **expected rather than anomalous**. You do not need a special explanation for being different from a trial average. Being different from a trial average is the normal condition.

### The experiment you ran on yourself

Push that spread one step further and it says something more useful than "everyone is different." If the population curve describes nobody in it, then the only control that spread does not wreck is you, measured against yourself. **You ran that control. Twice, about a year apart, without intending to**, and nine passes missed it because half of it only arrived when you corrected your own history.

You took escitalopram at 10mg and lost a week to dissociation, badly enough that you could not drive or work. You took duloxetine at 20mg and got mild floatiness. The question nobody thought to ask is how much serotonin transporter each of those doses was actually occupying.

**Nothing anyone can measure tells them apart.** Ask it three ways and you get the same answer three times. In the three brain regions where both drugs have ever been imaged, the published values overlap `[primary, verified]`. Fit each drug's own published dose-response curve and escitalopram 10mg comes out as a **69.2**-point step up from zero while duloxetine 20mg comes out as **71.7** `[reasoning, not evidence]`, two independent laboratories' data landing within two and a half points of each other. And the best-matched single pair anyone has found, same tracer and same scanner type and same region and same single-dose design and the same population of healthy Japanese volunteers, reads **71.3%** thalamic for duloxetine 20mg against roughly **70%** for escitalopram 10mg.

That last comparison carries a warning that belongs beside it rather than under it: **this project's own binding record says the escitalopram half of that pair could not be found in the paper it is attributed to**, while the most recent pass says it re-checked it and it is there `[secondary — unverified]`. Two of this project's documents disagree about whether that number was ever verified. So do not lean on it. **You do not need to**, and that is the point of asking three ways.

**Same brain. Serotonin transporter engagement that no measurement can distinguish. Opposite outcomes.**

Now notice what that does, because it is sharper than it first looks. The claim that escitalopram dissociated you by delivering *more* serotonin is already dead on this page, and it died because the comparison between these two drugs cannot demonstrate a difference. **That same fact is what breaks the occupancy account, because you cannot explain a difference in outcome by appealing to a comparison that cannot show a difference.** To keep an occupancy story alive from here you have to posit that duloxetine delivered substantially less transporter engagement **to you specifically** than escitalopram did, despite every published estimate saying they match, and in exactly the direction that rescues the theory. That move is available. It is also unmeasured, unmotivated, and it needs you to sit at opposite ends of two different drugs' individual curves by coincidence.

So the honest verdict is not that occupancy has been refuted. It is that **occupancy is now unsupported as the explanation, and left standing on a rescue nobody can check.** The caveats that get it there are real and they belong in this paragraph rather than in an appendix, because this is the strongest claim on the page and the strongest claims are where this project has gone wrong every single time. What is within-person here is the **outcome**, not the exposure: you were never scanned, so both occupancy figures come from other people's brains, and the 8.5-fold spread that makes your own body the best available control is the same spread that makes those borrowed numbers loose when applied to you. The escitalopram side is retrospective, unblinded, and a year old. Indistinguishable is not identical.

And it does not stand alone, which matters more than the fact that it is n=1. There is a second leg with a completely different shape, and it is the next candidate on the list: a **3.7**-point occupancy step, in a blind placebo-controlled crossover, flipped the human sensory filter from working to broken `[primary, verified ×2 on the outcomes]`. Between-person where yours is within-person, controlled where yours is retrospective, prospective where yours is recalled. Two arguments that fail in opposite directions, reaching the same conclusion. **Serotonin transporter occupancy is not the variable that explains you.**

**You got there first, and that is worth saying plainly.** You wrote that duloxetine simply works differently from escitalopram and that this alone is enough to explain the divergence. The research had reached the same place from the literature and written it down as a hypothesis. Your corrected history is what turned it into a demonstration, and the evidence to do that had been sitting in the corpus, unassembled, for eight passes.

Which leaves your real question standing exactly where you left it. Something drug-specific did the work. What?

### Your own candidate, and the scan nobody has run

You answered that question yourself: the difference could be little more than one drug concentrating more in one part of the brain than another, and that alone could produce an outsized effect. That is now candidate 8 on the list. It earns its place, and it earns a straight answer rather than a flattering one, which is what you would want given that you proposed it.

**The form of it is legitimate, and a published paper makes the identical move.** Kim and colleagues scanned twelve people on escitalopram and found occupancy differs by region: higher in the dorsal raphe nucleus than in the putamen, p<0.001, with a differently shaped curve in each `[primary, verified]`. Their conclusion is the part that matters. They explain the delayed onset of every SSRI ever prescribed by appealing to that regional gradient. A second team, independently, states your assumption almost word for word as established preclinical fact, describing regionally varying transporter activity **and antidepressant drug concentration**, and found occupancy running highest in the subgenual cingulate, amygdala and raphe `[primary, verified]`. You arrived at the structure of a published pharmacology paper on your own.

**And the amplifier you intuited is real and has a name.** The dorsal raphe holds the autoreceptors. Occupy the transporter there and serotonin rises at those autoreceptors, which slows raphe firing, which lowers serotonin release everywhere downstream. A small difference at one small nucleus can therefore change the **sign** of a drug's effect across the entire projection field, not merely its size `[primary, verified on the mechanism; the inversion framing is inference]`. So "slight difference, outsized effect" is not hand-waving. It is the published explanation for why antidepressants take weeks to work.

**Now the part you are owed straight.** As you state it, it is not a hypothesis. It is a schema. It names no region and no direction, and with fourteen regions and two directions there are twenty-eight post-hoc stories available in it. Every result fits: if duloxetine helps it must concentrate somewhere good, and if it fails it must not. A story that cannot lose is not doing work, and this page does not award points for flexibility. That is why candidate 8 scores **silent** on your acute worsening rather than scoring a hit for being able to accommodate it.

**Made specific it becomes sharply falsifiable, and then the literature stops you dead.** Name the region and the direction and it predicts a number: duloxetine's dorsal-raphe-to-striatum occupancy ratio should differ from escitalopram's at matched exposure, which would mean a different autoreceptor brake, which would mean projection-field serotonin differing in direction rather than degree. One scan settles it. **That scan does not exist.** Escitalopram has been imaged in fourteen brain regions. Duloxetine has been imaged in three, which are thalamus, midbrain and striatum `[primary, verified]`. Every region the dissociation literature implicates, meaning the insula, the anterior cingulate and the prefrontal cortex, **has never been measured for duloxetine at any dose, by anyone, in twenty years** `[primary, verified]`. And in the three where both drugs have been measured, they match.

So the verdict is an unusual one and it is worth stating precisely. **Live, well-formed, and untestable, which is not the same thing as unfalsifiable.** Nothing contradicts it. One scan would test it. It cannot be tested today because duloxetine has been imaged in three brain regions in two decades and not one of them is anywhere that dissociation lives. **You guessed that this might not be answerable. You were right, and you were right for a citable reason, and the reason is a fact about funding priorities rather than about your reasoning.**

One thing it must be docked for, and the researcher who examined it caught himself in the act. The single regional comparison that **favours** your hypothesis is duloxetine's midbrain 85% against escitalopram's 75%, a ten-point gap in the raphe-containing region, pointing precisely the way you would want. It is worthless: one number is PET with one tracer and the other is SPECT with another, in different laboratories, and the modality difference alone is several times larger than the gap `[primary, verified]`. That is the identical artifact sitting in this page's graveyard. It was nearly written up as support and survived only a deliberate check. **Your hypothesis reproduces this project's signature error on contact**, which is real information about how carefully anyone has to handle it, including you.

### The one distinction that shortens the list

If everything is individual, the list would be infinite, and it is not. There is a single distinction that shortens it, and it is the intellectual engine of this page.

**A mechanism killed by heterogeneity is not dead.** A trial that found no average effect is entirely compatible with a large effect in one person. A null average is a statement about a mean, and you are not a mean. Those candidates stay on the list, and the honest label for them is not "refuted" but "untested in anyone like you."

**A mechanism killed by logic or by measurement error is dead**, and no amount of individual variation revives it. If a model predicts the *opposite* of what you report, being unusual does not rescue it, because the model is not making a claim about averages, it is making a claim about direction. If its timescale is off by a factor of a thousand, being unusual does not rescue it. If it rests on comparing two studies that used different scanners on different brain regions, or on somebody misreading the paper they cited, then it was never a claim about people at all.

**This is why the graveyard at the bottom of this page is short.** Almost nothing here died because a trial came out negative. The things that died, died of arithmetic.

### The shape of the list

Ranked by fit-to-you multiplied by evidence quality, and explicitly not by elegance, the list comes out in an order that irritated the researchers who produced it.

**The dullest candidate ranks first.** Duloxetine reduced your anxiety and improved your sleep continuity, and the vivid dreams, the reduced morning fatigue and the minimal pain change all follow from those two things. It ranks first for one reason: it is the only candidate on the list with a positive randomised trial at your actual dose. Nicolini 2009 ran a fixed 20mg arm in generalised anxiety, and it separated from placebo on total anxiety and on the psychic factor `[primary, verified]`. It explains four of your ten features natively, including the three you told us you value most. And it **cannot** explain the acute worsening, and it **cannot** explain why escitalopram gave you nothing. Those are gaps in its scope rather than contradictions of it, which is exactly why it stays on the list rather than joining the graveyard. It is worth saying plainly that eight passes were so busy hunting for mechanism that nobody rated the obvious explanation until the ninth.

**The only candidate that predicts your acute worsening ranks second, and it got stronger this pass.** Same lab in Copenhagen, same paradigm, same population: escitalopram at 10mg left P50 sensory gating **intact**; escitalopram at 15mg **disrupted** it `[primary, verified ×2]`. Change detection was elevated at both doses and habituation was delayed at both. Read the higher dose as a description of a person and it is you at a festival: detects more, filters less, stops responding more slowly. That is not a metaphor for sensory overwhelm, it is an electrophysiological description of one.

Two things about that pair had been undersold. The first is that it is **not two papers someone stapled together**, which is how this page previously described it. Oranje's own abstract says the 15mg study was designed as a dose extension of the 10mg one, with the directional hypothesis stated in advance and then confirmed `[primary, verified]`. A planned extension with a pre-stated prediction is a much better object than a post-hoc comparison, and calling it the chain's signature error was over-correction. It is still two separate cohorts rather than one within-subject dose-response, and that limit is real, but it is a different and smaller limit than the one previously claimed.

The second is the one that matters. **Convert that dose step into occupancy and it is almost nothing.** Ten milligrams to fifteen is a **3.7**-point move in serotonin transporter occupancy, on the flat part of a curve that has already plateaued by the minimum approved dose `[reasoning, not evidence on the conversion; the gating results are primary]`. And it flipped the filter. Which forces a choice: either the sensory filter is exquisitely sensitive to transporter occupancy in a range where nothing else is, or **the gating effect is not a transporter-occupancy effect at all** and is carried by something still unsaturated that keeps scaling with concentration. The second is far more parsimonious, and the exact conversion does not matter to the argument, because on any curve that plateaus near the minimum dose the 10-to-15 step is a small one. **So candidate 2 and the experiment you ran on yourself are the same finding arriving twice by different roads.** Occupancy is not the variable. Nobody has identified what is.

The caveats travel with it and they are large. One lab, with no independent replication `[primary, verified — by absence]`. Around twenty men per study. Escitalopram rather than duloxetine. The leap from a P50 waveform in healthy volunteers to a crowded art festival is enormous and unbridged. Every study is a single acute dose in a drug-naive volunteer, so whether the cost persists on chronic dosing has never been measured `[no evidence exists]`, and your own history cannot fill that gap however tempting it looks: your escitalopram dissociation faded within a week, but dissociation is not sensory gating, and on duloxetine your acute sensitivity is **still worse at two weeks on a steady dose**. If anything, the only datum you have on whether a gating cost habituates says it has not yet. And the one independent lab that touched the question ran the *opposite* manipulation and found that depleting serotonin breaks a different gate and spares P50, which is a mirror image and argues that serotonin and gating is not one dial but something closer to an inverted U `[primary, verified]`.

**The most elegant candidate ranks third, and its elegance is the reason to distrust it.** Chattarji's lab has shown repeatedly that a single two-hour stressor produces a delayed amygdala state ten days later, and that blocking NMDA receptors *during* the stressor prevents it `[primary, verified]`. Musazzi showed that chronic antidepressants at fourteen days, with an SNRI among the four drugs, completely prevent the stress-induced glutamate surge while corticosterone rises normally in the same animals `[primary, verified]`. Join those two and you get: the drug dampens the glutamate surge during the crowd, so the delayed consolidation never happens, so the overwhelm occurs and the crash does not. It is the only candidate whose native timescale is your timescale. **The join has never been tested by anybody** `[no evidence exists]`. It crosses a brain region without warrant, because Musazzi measured prefrontal cortex and Chattarji's effect is in the amygdala. Chattarji's drug arm is confounded, because the vehicle gavage worked too `[primary, verified]`. It is animal-only. And it predicts your acute experience would be **intact**, not worse, because that is what "corticosterone rises normally" means. It is the best timescale match anyone has found, and it is a hypothesis with two verified halves and an unverified seam.

Those three are the composite, and the composite is still three even though the list has grown to eight. **The bottom of the list is candidates that each address exactly one thing**, and they are on the list for that reason rather than despite it. Two of them, numbers 4 and 8, are hired to fill the same hole: the fact that escitalopram gave you nothing.

**Candidate 4 was the placeholder, and this pass it collapsed.** It says escitalopram failed you because of something escitalopram specifically does: it binds a second, allosteric site on the serotonin transporter and slows its own release from the first one. This page previously called it untested, never studied, surviving by never having been examined. **That was false, and it is the most consequential factual error the page carried.** The site has been tested twice, in living animals, with knockin mice purpose-built to answer exactly this question, and it failed both times `[primary, verified ×2]`. One team abolished the site and found no measurable difference in the serotonin response to escitalopram, concluding that the site "may not produce meaningful in vivo effects." The other, working at clinically relevant plasma levels and therapeutic occupancy, found no evidence the site matters for how escitalopram inhibits the transporter at all. Two things make that worse rather than better for the candidate. **The site is not escitalopram's**: it belongs to the transporter, and R-citalopram modulates it too `[primary, verified]`. What is escitalopram-specific is the therapeutic exploitation, not the possession. And the **positive** literature on that site is substantially a research programme run by escitalopram's own manufacturer `[primary, verified]`, which is the standard this page applies elsewhere and had never applied here. The one thing that cuts the other way is worth knowing: two authors of the second disconfirming study were employed by that manufacturer, and the paper concluded against their employer's own marketing differentiator anyway. **A finding against the sponsor's interest is the most credible category of sponsored finding.** Candidate 4 is not dead, because knockin mice with a partly-expressed transporter are a heterogeneity-grade objection and not a logic-grade one, and this page does not kill things by heterogeneity. But it has moved from "untested inference" to "tested twice in vivo and negative twice," which is a categorically worse place to stand.

**Candidate 8 is yours, and it is now the better placeholder for the same hole**, for one reason only: candidate 4 has been tested and failed, and candidate 8 has not been tested at all. That is a low bar and it should be read as one. It is treated in full above.

**Candidate 6 is also yours, and it is not a crank idea.** Your hypothesis is that the crashes aggravate the ME/CFS and the fibromyalgia, which would mean preventing them should improve pain slowly, at 20mg, with no threshold to cross. It splits into three claims with wildly different standing. The first, that sensory and emotional overload triggers crashes, is **established**, and it is described in a clinical consensus document using your own word: *"Overload phenomena may trigger a 'crash' where the patient becomes temporarily immobilized"* `[primary, verified]`. You independently rediscovered a documented clinical phenomenon and reached for the same word for it. The second claim, that the crashes *aggravate* the illness, is the load-bearing one, and it is unstudied `[no evidence exists]`. Adjacent evidence leans mildly against it in three places, and the sharpest is that the allostatic-load limb which actually predicts pain prospectively is the metabolic one, while your model runs entirely through the autonomic limb that predicted nothing `[primary, verified]`. On current evidence this should not be the reason to stay at 20mg. There is a better reason, and it is in the trap below.

**Candidate 7 is the instructive corpse**, because it shows both kinds of wrong meeting in one place. It says acute serotonin is what makes you dissociate, chronic treatment desensitises the receptor responsible, so the benefit is the adaptation rather than the serotonin. As an account of your dissociation it is **dead by logic**, and the argument is short: it is a single mechanism, and a single mechanism cannot move two things in opposite directions. If the receptor has adapted, both halves should improve together. If it has not adapted yet, both halves should still be bad. There is no state of that account in which the acute is worse and the crash is gone. But it is dead only for the job it was hired to do. As a partial account of the crash half it survives, weakly, and it is redundant with candidate 3, which has a better timescale and an identified molecular link.

Candidate 7 also generated the only prediction anybody in this project has ever tested, and the way that turned out is a lesson in how little a confirmed prediction is sometimes worth. An earlier pass predicted that raising the dose should **transiently** re-open the acute window. Your corrected history, supplied afterwards, reports exactly that: a dose increase transiently re-created the state, twice, and it faded. The prediction was written before the datum was known, it was specific and directional, and all three of its components match. **And it should move nothing.** It was a prediction about duloxetine confirmed by escitalopram, which assumes the very drug-difference that is the open question. It is n=1, retrospective, unblinded, about a year old. The theory that generated it was already dead on other grounds. And the deepest problem is the simplest: **the prediction is not discriminating.** Activation folklore predicts it, the gating ladder predicts it, and ordinary pharmacological intuition predicts it. A prediction that its rivals also make selects nothing when it comes true. The chain's own warning applies with full force here: a confirmed prediction plus an exact numerical match is when to be most suspicious, not least.

### The demotion, and why it is the most useful story here

Candidate 5 was the orchestrator's number one, and watching it fall is more instructive than any of the survivors, because it shows you what these arguments look like from the inside when they break.

The story went: duloxetine inhibits norepinephrine reuptake, which lowers locus coeruleus tonic firing through α2 autoreceptor feedback, which raises the phasic-to-tonic ratio. Read that off and you get baseline arousal down, acute reactivity up. That is your entire report from a single mechanism with nothing bolted on. It fit beautifully.

It fit beautifully because **the source had been misread**. The paper is Bari and Aston-Jones, 2013 `[primary, verified]`. Its abstract says that atomoxetine decreased tonic activity *"although leaving intact the sensory-evoked excitatory component of LC phasic response"*, and that the **inhibitory** component was **increased** in magnitude and **prolonged** in duration. So the ratio rose because the **denominator fell** and the brake grew, not because the numerator rose. "Acute reactivity up" is not in that paper. It is the opposite of what that paper reports.

Then the theory contradicts it as well. Aston-Jones and Cohen's adaptive gain theory, which the candidate invokes by name, defines high tonic firing as *"disengagement from the current task and a search for alternative behaviors (exploration)"* `[primary, verified]`. **Tonic is not "baseline arousal." It is a distractible mode.** So lowering it predicts *better* filtering, not worse. The sanity check settles it: atomoxetine is prescribed to *reduce* distractibility. If raising the phasic-to-tonic ratio made people more sensory-overwhelmed, atomoxetine would be a bad ADHD drug, and it is not.

And then a counterexample sitting inside your own history. The tonic limb is not specific to norepinephrine at all. SSRIs lower locus coeruleus tonic firing too, by an entirely different route, because serotonin neurons tonically inhibit the locus coeruleus, and Szabo proposes exactly that as *the anxiolytic mechanism of SSRIs* `[primary, verified]`. **Escitalopram is an SSRI, and it gave you zero anxiolysis.** That is the kind of counterexample heterogeneity cannot rescue, because you are not a population the candidate is being generalised to. You are the single case it is being fitted to.

So candidate 5 went from first to fifth on those grounds, and it stays at fifth. What survives is worth having and is smaller than advertised: duloxetine may have lowered your baseline arousal, which is consistent with your sleep improving, and which a nap attempt might speak to. That is a decent theory of your sleep. It is not a theory of everything, and it never predicted the acute half at all.

### The kill that got retracted

Everything above stands. What follows is the part this page got wrong, and it is worth more to you than any of the survivors, because it is the failure mode of this entire project caught in a single paragraph.

There was a fourth argument, and it was presented as the decisive one. Alba-Delgado 2012: duloxetine, seven days, osmotic minipump, in vivo recording from the locus coeruleus itself, and **the only chronic-duloxetine recording of those neurons that exists anywhere**. This page reported that the sensory-evoked excitatory response went **down**, and called that the "acute reactivity up" claim tested in your own drug and coming out the other way.

**The paper is titled "Analgesic antidepressants promote the responsiveness of locus coeruleus neurons to noxious stimulation."**

It reports three effects. The page quoted one and dropped the two that run the other way: the inhibitory period was **reduced** and the late response was **augmented** `[primary, verified]`. Both of those describe a neuron that brakes less and responds more. And the qualifier on the one finding the page did quote is the part that finishes it: **the decrease happened only in the nerve-injured animals**, while the two effects pointing the other way happened, in the authors' own words, *"in all animals."* So the single result the argument needed was the one confined to a group you are not in, and the results that survive in every animal both point away from it. The authors read their own net effect as increased responsiveness, and put it in the title.

Now notice what this page had already done, because it is worse than a simple misquote and more useful to know about. **The page did print the title and both opposing findings**, three sections below, in a subordinate clause, and then dismissed them and went on calling the paper "the decisive study." The caveat was filed where nobody would weigh it while the headline spent certainty it had not earned. **That is this project's signature defect exactly, in miniature, on this page, in the section about other people's misreadings.**

And there is a sharper joke in it. The pass that made this error is the same pass that caught the *identical* error in the Bari paper, in the paragraph above, and wrote the rule for detecting it: read what the ratio is made of, count all the components, do not quote the one you like. It then read one of three components and quoted the one it liked. **The chain's own diagnosis is that each pass correctly identifies its predecessor's sin and then commits it in reverse. Here it is committing it in the act of naming it.**

So: **candidate 5's phasic limb is unresolved, not refuted.** The other three arguments for the demotion are untouched by this, because they are independent of it: the source was misread, the theory predicts the opposite, and your own escitalopram history is a counterexample. **Candidate 5 stays at fifth.** What has been removed is a false certainty, not a candidate, and I would rather hand you an honest "unresolved" than a tidy corpse.

### The graveyard, and the inversion at the centre of it

The dead list is short, and every entry earned its place by arithmetic rather than by a negative trial.

The star exhibit is the dorsal raphe 5-HT1A model, and it is worth understanding for what it reveals about the field rather than about you. Maier and Watkins' learned-helplessness model says that uncontrollable stress **desensitises** the inhibitory 5-HT1A autoreceptors on dorsal raphe serotonin neurons, which removes the brake, which sensitises those neurons, which produces the behavioural collapse `[primary, verified]`. Chronic fluoxetine, at fourteen days, does the same thing to the same receptor in the same nucleus: the neurons become resistant to autoinhibition and maintain their firing `[primary, verified]`.

**Same receptor. Same nucleus. Same end state.** One literature calls it learned helplessness. The other calls it the antidepressant mechanism. **Nobody has reconciled them.** Read as it was proposed, the model predicts that duloxetine *installs* your crash state rather than preventing it. Which means your Saturday is a **counterexample** to that model, not a confirmation of it. That is not a caveat to be filed at the bottom. That is a model running backwards.

The rest of the graveyard is the same species of error at smaller scale. A duration story borrowed from the bed nucleus of the stria terminalis, where "longer-lasting" means tens of minutes in a startle paradigm and your episodes last one to two weeks, so the analogy is off by roughly a thousandfold. A diagnostic match to non-fearful panic disorder, where panic peaks in minutes. A propranolol parallel that turns out to be an anti-parallel, because propranolol *blunts* acute reactivity and yours rose above 140 bpm. None of these are close calls.

### Why cognitive therapies never worked

One feature of your presentation does more diagnostic work than it looks like it should, and it is the **absence of negative cognitive content**. Your episodes do not come with negative thoughts attached. That is why cognitive therapies never worked on them, because there was never a thought pattern there to restructure.

Now notice what several candidates on this list predict. Candidate 2 predicts an electrophysiological change with no propositions anywhere in it. Candidate 3 predicts synaptic plasticity in the amygdala, which likewise contains no beliefs. Both describe **a state rather than a thought**, and a state is not something a person can be argued out of. That convergence is not evidence that either mechanism is correct, and it should not be read as any. What it is, is a reason that "cognitive therapy didn't work" was never a fact about your effort or your insight. It was a category mismatch between the tool and the thing.

One thing must not happen here, and it is worth naming because this chain has already made this exact error once. Duloxetine improves the HAM-A **psychic** factor and not the **somatic** factor, across eight trials and 2,399 patients `[primary, verified]`. It is tempting to map "somatic anxiety" onto "anxiety without cognitive content" and declare a match. **Do not.** The HAM-A somatic factor is a list of physical symptom items: cardiovascular, gastrointestinal, respiratory, autonomic. It is not your phenomenology. The chain previously matched you to non-fearful panic disorder because both involve an absence, when in fact non-fearful panic is an absence of *fear* and yours is an absence of *cognitions*, and those are different things. An absence is not a fingerprint, and a symptom checklist is not a description of what it is like to be you.

### The trap

Everything above is mechanism. There is one place where mechanism touches a decision, and it is worth knowing before your appointment rather than after it.

You have a hypothesis: the crashes aggravate your ME/CFS and fibromyalgia, so preventing crashes should improve pain slowly, at 20mg, with no threshold to cross. The standard model says pain relief requires crossing the noradrenergic threshold at roughly 40 to 60mg, and that 20mg does approximately nothing for pain. **Right now, those two models predict different things.** At 60mg they predict the same thing, because 60mg crosses the threshold *and* keeps preventing crashes.

**So if pain improves at 60mg, both models are satisfied, and you learn nothing about your own hypothesis, permanently.** The 20mg window is the only clean test of your idea that will ever exist for you, and it is open right now.

This is not an argument for staying at 20mg. Months of unrelieved pain is a real cost, and pain matters to you; you said so explicitly, and you corrected an earlier framing that pitted the pain benefit against the psychological one. You want both. This is an argument for knowing that the window exists before it shuts, because a decision looks different when you know you are spending something rather than nothing.

And there is a separating observation that costs nothing at all. Your model has to run through the fatigue limb, because the only prospective path from distress to pain in fibromyalgia that survives analysis runs distress to fatigue to pain; the direct distress-to-pain path was null `[primary, verified]`. **Therefore: track fatigue and pain as separate lines. If pain improves while fatigue does not, your model is dead**, whatever else is helping you.

### What nobody has studied

Finally, the claims on this page most likely to be true, because they are the ones that assert the least.

Nobody has studied duloxetine in autistic adults; there is one three-patient case series. Nobody has studied the overwhelm-to-crash cycle pharmacologically; the autistic burnout literature contains no pharmacology whatsoever, and its recovery advice is entirely environmental and behavioural. Nobody has studied whether prior dissociation predicts recurrence. And the fourth, which is the one that matters: **nobody has studied this decoupling in any human, for any drug.** Not weakly, not in a small sample, not in the wrong population. The question has never been asked.

There is a seventh now, and it is the one most specifically about you. **The literature on antidepressant-induced dissociation is empty** `[no evidence exists]`. Not thin: empty. A PubMed search returns forty-four papers, and most of them are burnout studies that match only because the Maslach Burnout Inventory happens to contain a subscale called "depersonalisation," which is an unrelated construct wearing the same word. Narrow it properly to *induced* dissociation and four papers remain, none of them on the topic. **There is no prevalence figure. There is no time course. There is no habituation data.** The single most disabling drug reaction you have ever had is a phenomenon the field has never named, and the reason your question has no answer is that nobody has asked it.

It is worth blocking one escape route from that, because it is the obvious one and it is a trap. There **is** a named syndrome that occurs at antidepressant dose transitions, and the sentence you would want exists nearly verbatim, describing an adverse effect seen "during the initiation or change of dose" `[primary abstract only]`. It is called activation or jitteriness syndrome, and every study that operationalises it uses the same eleven-item list: anxiety, agitation, panic, insomnia, irritability, hostility, aggressiveness, impulsivity, akathisia, hypomania, mania `[primary abstract only]`. **Dissociation is not on that list. Depersonalisation is not on that list. Derealisation is not on that list.** It is a syndrome of over-arousal and you report the opposite pole. Reaching for it to explain you would repeat exactly the error this page already made once, when it matched you to non-fearful panic disorder because both involve an absence. **An absence is not a fingerprint.** And the syndrome's dose-change half is asserted rather than measured in any case: the one prospective study that put a time course on it enrolled only people starting from no antidepressant at all, so it contains no dose-increase data whatsoever `[primary, verified]`.

Read that fourth absence against the list and it explains the list's shape. The human evidence explains the acute half and says nothing about any aftermath. The animal evidence explains the aftermath half and has never been given the drug. **No study, in any species, has both halves.** So a person who converted sensory overwhelm into a two-week collapse for decades, took a drug for two weeks, then absorbed five hours of two crowded festivals and two panic attacks and did not collapse, has produced an observation that the literature has no entry for. That does not make the observation right. **It makes it data**, and it is a reason to record it carefully rather than a reason to believe it.

### What to take from this

Not a mechanism. A shape.

The shape is that your report is currently better than the literature that is supposed to explain it. Three candidates each explain a different third of you, drawn from three fields that have never spoken to one another, and the seams between them are precisely where the explanations ought to be. The most attractive story on the list turned out to be the most misread, and the study that was supposed to have killed it turned out to say the opposite of what it was quoted for. The most elegant one is animal-only with an untested join and predicts the wrong sign on your most distinctive observation. The one with the best evidence is the dullest one, and it cannot explain the thing that makes your case interesting. The one that does predict your acute worsening rests on twenty healthy men in Copenhagen who each took one pill, once. The one that was hired to explain why escitalopram failed you has been tested twice and failed twice. Your own candidate is well-formed, has published precedent, and cannot be checked, because duloxetine has been imaged in three brain regions in twenty years. And not one candidate on the list explains your restless legs, which is worth noticing because it shows the list is not being stretched to cover everything.

One thing did get settled this pass, and it is yours. **Serotonin transporter occupancy is not the variable.** Your two initiations delivered engagement nothing can tell apart and produced opposite outcomes, and a blind crossover in a Copenhagen lab flipped a sensory filter on an occupancy step of under four points. Those two arguments have nothing in common except their conclusion. That is not a mechanism, and it does not tell you what duloxetine is doing. It tells you what it is not doing, which is a smaller result and a much more solid one.

None of that means you should distrust what you observed. It means the honest description of your situation is that you are running an experiment nobody has run, in a body nobody has studied, on a two-week observation window against a baseline of episodes that arrived several times a month. Two weeks is short. The confidence available here is genuinely low, and it is low for reasons that have nothing to do with whether the drug is working.

What is worth doing with that is small and specific. Watch for a breakthrough crash after an exposure no worse than the festivals, because that is the cleanest falsifier available to you and it costs nothing to watch for. Keep fatigue and pain as separate lines, because that one split is what separates your hypothesis from the standard one. Notice whether the sleep benefit holds on quiet weeks as well as eventful ones, because candidate 1 predicts it should and the crash-focused candidates predict nothing about it either way. And treat the whole list as what it actually is: not eight guesses at one answer, but eight partial answers to a question nobody has yet asked properly.

You said you did not think it was possible to get more certain than "possibly valid," because we do not understand the brain well enough to go beyond that. That is the correct read, and it is not a counsel of despair. It is the thing that lets you stop hunting for *the* explanation and start noticing which part of you each one covers.

### The rated candidate list

#### #1 — Anxiolysis and sleep continuity ("the boring one")

**The claim.** Duloxetine 20mg reduced your anxiety and improved your sleep continuity, and everything else you report follows from those two things.

**Mechanism.** Serotonergic anxiolysis at roughly 75% striatal SERT occupancy `[primary, verified — Abanades 2011, PMID 20940733]`, plus reduced nocturnal awakenings. Nicolas and Ruby 2020 (PMID 33224081) identify reduced intra-sleep awakenings as the variable that best explains changed dream recall `[primary, verified]`, which would explain the vivid dreams **and** the reduced morning fatigue with a single mechanism rather than two.

**Evidence tier.** RCT-backed, and it is **the only candidate with a positive trial at your exact dose**. Nicolini 2009 (PMID 18485261): fixed 20mg in generalised anxiety, n=84 in that arm, HAM-A total and psychic factor separated at p=0.01–0.001 `[primary, verified]`. The somatic factor did not separate (p=0.07).

**Fit.** acute-worse **✗** · crash-absent **✓** · no-cognitive-content **○** · sleep-better **✓✓** · vivid dreams **✓✓** · less morning fatigue **✓✓** · RLS-worse **○** · pain-minimal **✓✓** · escitalopram-failed **✗** · nap-failure **○**

**What it predicts you would observe.** The benefit is **not contingent on exposure**. It should hold on quiet weeks exactly as well as on festival weeks. Dreams and sleep should track together as one thing rather than two.

**What would falsify it.** Anxiety returning while sleep stays good, or the reverse, because under this account they are the same mechanism and should move together. Or episodes recurring with no change in either sleep or anxiety.

**Dose implication.** This is the one candidate with real dose evidence. SERT occupancy plateaus at the minimum approved dose (Sørensen 2022) `[primary, verified]`, so at roughly 75% you are already on the plateau. **But the inference this page previously drew from that is invalid and is withdrawn.** "You are on the plateau, therefore going up buys very little additional serotonergic engagement" does not follow: occupancy is not a linear proxy for serotonergic engagement, and the plateau is exactly where the two come apart `[reasoning, not evidence]`. The empirical demonstration is candidate 2: a 3.7-point occupancy step at the plateau flipped a measured human outcome. Whatever is going on there, "at the plateau, more dose buys almost nothing" is false for at least one thing anyone has measured. What survives is narrower and still worth having: Nicolini's higher-dose arm was **slightly better, not worse** (−15.3 points and 55% response versus −14.7 and 45%), non-inferiority of 20mg was never tested against it, and the 20mg arm was n=84 against n=158.

**Why it is not dead.** Untested against the alternatives rather than refuted. Its weaknesses are gaps in scope, not contradictions.

**The hole, and it got deeper this pass.** Escitalopram gave you zero anxiolysis. If duloxetine's benefit is simply "an antidepressant reduced his anxiety," then why did the last antidepressant not? **The previously available answer was that the escitalopram trial had not been a fair test, and your corrected history removes it.** Six months. Titrated to a full 20mg over a month and held there for months. That is an adequate trial by any standard, so "wrong dose" and "not long enough" are both gone, and what remains is a clean, fair negative. **That makes the escitalopram datum more valuable and this candidate's problem worse, not better.** It is not that the drug was never given a chance. It is that the drug does not work in you. This candidate needs candidate 8 to explain its own biggest anomaly, and candidate 8 predicts nothing you can check. `[reasoning, not evidence]`

#### #2 — Serotonergic sensory-gating dose ladder

**The claim.** Raising serotonin acutely degrades sensory gating and habituation. That is why the festival was worse.

**Mechanism.** Same lab, same paradigm, same population, sequential dose extension `[primary, verified ×2]`. Jensen 2008 (PMID 18000656): escitalopram 10mg, n=21, **P50 gating intact**. Oranje 2011 (PMID 20971512): escitalopram 15mg, n=20, **P50 gating disrupted**. Plus mismatch negativity increased at both doses (Oranje 2008, PMID 17971261; Wienberg 2010, PMID 19304862) and habituation delayed at both (Jensen 2007, PMID 17299503). The composite reads: detects more, filters less, stops responding more slowly.

**Evidence tier.** Human, dose-graded, blind, placebo-controlled, crossover, **and single-lab**. All four studies come from Copenhagen/Glostrup `[primary, verified — the author search was enumerated]`. **Upgraded this pass on one axis**: the 15mg study was a *planned* dose extension of the 10mg study, with the directional hypothesis stated in advance and then confirmed `[primary, verified]`. This page previously filed it as a post-hoc cross-study comparison and called it the chain's signature error committed in the open. That was over-correction. It is still two cohorts rather than one within-subject dose-response, which is a real limit, but it is a smaller and different limit than the one claimed.

**The occupancy conversion, and it is the most important thing in this row.** Fit escitalopram's published single-dose curve and Jensen's 10mg is **69.2%** occupancy while Oranje's 15mg is **73.0%** `[reasoning, not evidence on the conversion; the gating outcomes are primary ×2]`. **The step that flipped the human sensory filter from intact to disrupted is 3.7 percentage points, on a curve that has already plateaued.** Either the filter is exquisitely sensitive to occupancy in a range where nothing else is, or the gating effect is carried by something that is **not** saturated and still scales with concentration. The second is far more parsimonious. **Either way, this is not a SERT-occupancy effect**, and the target has never been identified `[no evidence exists]`. Note that the argument does not depend on the exact fit: on any curve that plateaus near the minimum approved dose, 10 to 15mg is a small occupancy step. **This is the same conclusion the within-person control reaches by a completely different route.**

**Fit.** acute-worse **✓✓** and it is **the only candidate that predicts it** · crash-absent **○** · no-cognitive-content **✓** (it is electrophysiology; there are no propositions in it) · sleep-better **○** · vivid dreams **○** · less morning fatigue **○** · RLS-worse **○** · pain-minimal **○** · escitalopram-failed **✓✓** because the studies **are** escitalopram · nap-failure **○**

**What it predicts you would observe.** A dose increase should make the acute sensory cost **worse**. You are contemplating a 3× increase. The only human dose comparison that exists flipped gating at 1.5×.

**What would falsify it.** Acute overwhelm improving over months at a fixed dose, which is what Zohar 1988's adaptive-subsensitivity finding would predict (PMID 3276283) `[primary, verified]`. Or a 60mg increase that leaves the acute experience unchanged.

**Dose implication.** It is the strongest mechanistic argument against 60mg anywhere in this project, and it bears on the thing you are most wary of.

**Why it is not dead.** Untested in chronic dosing rather than refuted `[no evidence exists]`. Every one of the four studies is a single acute dose in a drug-naive volunteer, so whether the cost persists at two weeks or two years is simply unknown.

**The transience question, and why your history does not settle it.** The corrected history says your escitalopram dissociation was transient: about a week at each dose step, then it faded. It is tempting to convert that into support here, on the reasoning that a single acute dose in a naive volunteer is a model of the first day of a dose change, so the studies' chief limitation would turn out to match the phenomenon's real timescale. **Do not take that trade.** It swaps two constructs for each other: *dissociation* is not *sensory gating*, nobody measured your P50, and the page has already been burned once by matching you to a thing on the strength of a shared word. Worse, your own duloxetine report runs against it. **Your acute sensory sensitivity is still worse at two weeks on a steady dose.** So the only datum anyone has on whether a gating cost habituates says that so far it has not, in the drug that actually matters, in you. Two weeks is short and this may change. The honest statement is that the corrected history neither helps nor hurts this candidate, and it was the tempting synthesis that had to be refused.

**Limits, at the claim.** Two cohorts, not one within-subject dose-response. No independent replication. The only other lab that touched it (Mann 2008, PMID 17895917) ran the **opposite** manipulation and found that serotonin **depletion** disrupts prepulse inhibition and spares P50, which is a mirror image and argues for an inverted-U rather than a single dial `[primary, verified]`. Escitalopram is not duloxetine. And the leap from a P50 waveform in twenty healthy men to a crowded art festival is enormous and unbridged.

#### #3 — Chattarji delayed amygdala plus Musazzi glutamate decoupling

**The claim.** One acute stressor consolidates into a delayed multi-day state via NMDA and glutamate signalling **during** the event. Chronic antidepressants block the glutamate surge while leaving the stress response itself intact. So the overwhelm happens and the crash does not.

**Mechanism.** Yasmin 2016 (PMID 27798355): one 2-hour stressor produces, **ten days later**, increased basolateral amygdala mEPSC frequency, increased spine density, and increased anxiety-like behaviour; **APV infused during the acute stress prevents all of it** `[primary, verified]`. Musazzi 2010 (PMID 20052403): chronic antidepressant treatment for **14 days**, including **venlafaxine**, prevents the stress-induced glutamate surge **while corticosterone rises normally in the same animals** `[primary, verified]`.

**Evidence tier.** Animal-only, and the join between the two halves is a synthesis with no experiment behind it.

**Fit.** acute-worse **✗** because it predicts acute-**intact**, which is what normal corticosterone means · crash-absent **✓✓** and it is the only candidate whose native timescale **is** your timescale · no-cognitive-content **✓✓** · sleep-better **○** · vivid dreams **○** · less morning fatigue **○** · RLS-worse **○** · pain-minimal **○** · escitalopram-failed **✗** · nap-failure **○**

**What it predicts you would observe.** Crash protection should be **all-or-nothing per exposure** and **not dose-graded**, because the glutamate surge is either dampened or it is not. It should hold for exposures of any intensity, because Chattarji's trigger is a single discrete stressor rather than a cumulative load.

**What would falsify it.** A breakthrough crash following an exposure no worse than the festivals. **That is the cleanest falsifier available to you and it costs nothing to watch for.**

**Dose implication.** None available. Musazzi used one dose per drug. **Whether the protection scales with dose is completely unknown** `[no evidence exists]`, and this is the thing that should worry you most about going up. If the protection is already maximal at 20mg, a dose increase buys only the cost. If the protection scales, the trade holds. **Why it is not dead.** Untested rather than refuted. The underlying phenomenon is robust across five papers from Chattarji's lab.

**Limits, at the claim.** **The regions do not match.** Musazzi measured prefrontal and frontal cortex; Chattarji's effect is in the amygdala. **Nobody has shown that chronic antidepressants dampen the glutamate surge in the basolateral amygdala** `[no evidence exists]`, and joining a prefrontal drug effect to an amygdalar plasticity effect because both involve glutamate is exactly the cross-region move this chain has repeatedly condemned and then committed. **Chattarji's drug arm is vehicle-confounded**: Chakraborty 2019 (PMID 31193585) reports that *"gavage of only the vehicle also had a protective effect"* `[primary, verified]`, so the "drugs prevent it" limb is far weaker than the phenomenon itself. Musazzi discloses Servier consultancy, and agomelatine is one of the four drugs tested. Rats. Footshock and immobilisation. Not duloxetine, not a human.

#### #4 — Escitalopram-specific allosteric S2 mechanism

**The claim.** Escitalopram failed you because of something escitalopram does that duloxetine does not, rather than because of a quantity of serotonin.

**Mechanism.** Escitalopram binds an allosteric S2 site on the serotonin transporter and slows its own dissociation from the orthosteric site. The review is Zhong, Haddjeri and Sánchez 2012 (PMID 21901317) `[primary, verified]`. Its **relevance to your dissociation** is entirely unevidenced `[no evidence exists]`.

**Evidence tier. Corrected, and this is the largest single correction on the page. It is no longer "mechanistic inference." It is *directly disconfirmed in vivo, twice.*** This page previously said the candidate was untested, never studied, surviving by never having been examined. **That was false.** Two groups built knockin mice with the allosteric site genetically abolished, specifically to answer this question, and both found essentially nothing:

- **Murray, Ressler and Owens 2016 (PMID 26621784)** `[primary, verified — all three authors Emory, no manufacturer involvement]`. Microdialysis in ventral hippocampus: **no measurable decrease** in the extracellular serotonin response to escitalopram in mice lacking the site, **p=0.297**. No effect in the tail suspension test. Their conclusion: the role of the allosteric site *"may not produce meaningful in vivo effects."*
- **Jacobsen and colleagues 2014 (PMID 24810106)** `[primary, verified]`. Humanised transporter mice with the site abolished, **at clinically relevant plasma levels and therapeutic occupancy**. Verbatim: *"We find no evidence that R-citalopram directly antagonizes escitalopram or that the putative allosteric site is important for hSERT inhibition by escitalopram."*

**The conflict of interest, both ways, because it cuts both ways.** The **positive** allosteric-site literature is substantially a manufacturer research programme: the 2012 review is Lundbeck, escitalopram's maker, and so is the 2009 primary establishing the site `[primary, verified ×2]`. This page applies exactly that standard to an industry-funded pregabalin meta-analysis elsewhere in the deep dive and had never applied it here. **But the disconfirming literature is partly the manufacturer's own and negative anyway**: Jacobsen's conflict statement reads *"Lundbeck Research, USA. MGC has consulted for Lundbeck and received compensation"* `[primary, verified]`, and the paper concludes against Lundbeck's own marketing differentiator. **A finding against the sponsor's interest is the most credible category of sponsored finding**, so that disclosure argues *for* the result rather than against it.

**And the premise is wrong in a way nobody noticed.** "Escitalopram is the only SSRI with that site" is false. **The site belongs to the transporter**, and R-citalopram is also an allosteric modulator of it `[primary, verified]`. What is escitalopram-specific is the therapeutic *exploitation* of the site, not the possession of it. So even the uniqueness the candidate was built on is narrower than advertised.

**Fit.** escitalopram-failed **✓✓** and it is one of two candidates that address it at all · everything else **○**. Read that double tick carefully: it is earned **by construction**, because the candidate was invented to fill this hole, and a hit you were built for is not a prediction.

**What it predicts you would observe.** Nothing you can check. Other SSRIs without the S2 mechanism should not reproduce your escitalopram reaction, but that is not a test anybody should run.

**What would falsify it.** A future dissociative reaction to a non-allosteric serotonergic drug. Again, not a test to run. **In the ordinary sense it has already been falsified twice**, in mice, on the mechanism itself rather than on your phenotype.

**Dose implication.** None.

**Why it is not dead.** Both disconfirmations are mouse studies, and Murray's knockin expressed the transporter at only **10–30% of endogenous density**, which is a genuine power confound `[primary, verified]`. **A mouse null with an expression confound is a heterogeneity-grade objection, not a logic-grade one**, and this page does not kill things by heterogeneity. So it survives, at the bottom of the live list, and its survival is now the thinnest on the page. One thread does run its way and it is included because omitting it would be the selective reading this project keeps committing: Murray's own authors speculate that *"there may be a regional specificity in the role of the allosteric site"* after seeing a modest effect in one behavioural test and none in another `[primary, verified]`. That is a mouse behavioural dissociation rather than an occupancy measurement, it is the weakest possible support, and it points at candidate 8 rather than at this one.

**What it used to rest on, and where that went.** This candidate's real justification was never the S2 site. It was the negative finding underneath it: **occupancy is not the explanatory variable, therefore the explanation must be drug-specific, therefore something like this has to exist.** That argument is now stronger than ever and has moved to its own section, because it turns out to be about you rather than about escitalopram's chemistry. The gap is real. This candidate is a worse placeholder for it than it was, and candidate 8 is a better one.

#### #5 — Tonic/phasic locus coeruleus shift *(demoted from #1)*

**The claim.** Norepinephrine reuptake inhibition lowers locus coeruleus tonic firing via α2 autoreceptors, lowering baseline arousal.

**Mechanism, and it got stronger in places.** Duloxetine-specific support now exists: Arai 2025 (PMID 40481171) reports that duloxetine increased noradrenaline around the locus coeruleus and decreased pERK immunoreactivity `[primary, verified]`, which is the α2 mechanism in your own drug. The timescale limb holds and is fast: LC tonic suppression is present at 2 days and does not grow between day 2 and day 21 (Szabo 2000, PMID 11343573) `[primary, verified]`, so at 14 days you are well past the point where this effect would be fully expressed. The persistence limb holds: the somatodendritic α2 autoreceptors that control **LC firing** are **not** desensitised by chronic treatment, even though the ones controlling dendritic **release** are (Mateo 2001, PMID 11498523) `[primary, verified]`. The candidate needs the firing one, and the firing one survives.

**Evidence tier.** Animal-only for the tonic limb. **Unresolved for the phasic limb, corrected this pass from "contradicted."** The paper that was supposed to have settled it says something different from what it was quoted for, and the correction is set out in full below. The demotion does not depend on it.

**Fit.** acute-worse **✗** and it predicts acute-**better**, twice over · crash-absent **○** and silent, because "less arousal therefore no two-week collapse" is a hope with a receptor attached rather than an argument · no-cognitive-content **✓** · sleep-better **✓** · vivid dreams **○** · less morning fatigue **✓** · RLS-worse **○** · pain-minimal **○** · escitalopram-failed **✗** · **nap-failure ✓**, which is where it earns its place

**What it predicts you would observe.** The nap test. Lower tonic arousal should mean shorter nap latency. This is the one candidate a nap attempt speaks to, which is why the test is worth running and also why a null will not settle it: duloxetine improved your sleep, which reduces sleep drive, which makes napping **harder**. Bonnet and Arand measured that directly, and sleep deprivation drove nap latency from 13.4 minutes to 3.8 `[primary, verified]`. **The drug pushes the outcome both ways at once, so a negative result is uninterpretable.** A positive result is informative. Categories 6 and 7 carry the protocol.

#### #6 — Your CFS-aggravation hypothesis

**The claim.** The crashes aggravate your ME/CFS and fibromyalgia, so preventing them should improve pain slowly at 20mg, with no threshold to cross.

**Mechanism, and it is three separable claims with wildly different status.** Claim 1, that sensory and emotional overload triggers crashes, is **established as clinical description**, and it is described in a primary consensus document using your own word: the Canadian Consensus Criteria 2003 state that *"Overload phenomena may trigger a 'crash' where the patient becomes temporarily immobilized"* `[primary, verified]`. Claim 2, that crashes aggravate the illness, is **unstudied** `[no evidence exists]`. Claim 3, that therefore preventing crashes improves pain, has never been tested.

**Evidence tier.** Claim 1 established. Claim 2 no evidence, and adjacent evidence leans against. Claim 3 speculation.

**Fit.** crash-absent **✓** (it is a theory *about* the crash rather than about the drug) · pain-minimal **✓✓** because it explains why pain has not moved yet: the mechanism is slow by construction · acute-worse **○** · everything else **○**

**What it predicts you would observe.** Your model must run through the fatigue limb, because Okifuji 2011 (PMID 20591745) found the **direct** distress-to-pain path was **null** and only distress→fatigue→pain survived `[primary, verified]`. **So track fatigue and pain separately. If pain improves while fatigue does not, this is falsified.** Second prediction: a breakthrough crash should leave a **durable multi-week pain signature**. If pain returns to baseline within days, the aggravation limb is falsified even though crash-prevention would still be real, and the crashes would then be unpleasant but not damaging, which is a completely different thing and changes what the crash benefit is worth.

**What would falsify it.** Pain improving without fatigue improving. Or a breakthrough crash whose pain resolves in days.

**Dose implication. This is the trap, and it is the real decision content.** 60mg crosses the noradrenergic threshold **and** keeps preventing crashes. If pain improves at 60mg, both models are satisfied and you learn nothing about your own hypothesis, permanently `[reasoning, not evidence]`. The 20mg window is the only clean test of your hypothesis that will ever exist, and a dose increase closes it irreversibly. Conversely, if you hold 20mg for three to six months and pain is unchanged while crashes stay prevented, your pain limb is cleanly falsified and the dose decision becomes trivial.

**Why it is not dead.** Killed by heterogeneity and by absence of research, **not by logic**, so it survives. Your exact intersection (autistic, sensory-triggered crashes, ME/CFS, fibromyalgia) is the population these studies exclude or never assemble. A PubMed search for `"autistic burnout" AND ("chronic fatigue" OR "ME/CFS" OR "post-exertional")` returns **one result** `[primary, verified — search-level]`.

Second, prospectively, the allostatic-load limb that predicts pain is **metabolic**, and parasympathetic dysregulation predicted nothing `[primary, verified — Liang & Booker 2024]`, which means your model runs entirely through the limb that fails. **Why it must not be dropped.** It is your own hypothesis, it is not a crank idea, and Claim 1 is a genuine find: you independently rediscovered a documented clinical phenomenon and used the same word for it. But Claim 2 is the load-bearing limb and it is unsupported. On current evidence this should not be the reason to stay at 20mg. **The information-value argument is a much better reason to stay than the pain argument.**

The single-mechanism argument is what kills it, and it kills it only for the job it was hired to do.

#### #8 — Regional occupancy and distribution *(yours; ranks above #4)*

**The claim.** The two drugs deliver the same global transporter engagement but distribute it differently across brain regions, and a modest regional difference is enough to produce opposite-valence outcomes. In your words: it *"could be little more than one drug concentrating more in one part of the brain than another"* and still produce an outsized effect.

**Mechanism, and it is real, measured and published.** Regional SERT occupancy is **systematic and significant**: higher in the dorsal raphe nucleus than in the putamen, **p<0.001**, with different Hill coefficients by region (0.817 versus 0.459) `[primary, verified — Kim 2017, PMID 27557550]`. Occupancy runs **above the whole-brain mean in the subgenual cingulate, amygdala and raphe** `[primary, verified — Baldinger 2014, PMID 24121201]`, which is to say it is elevated exactly where behaviour lives. **The small-to-large amplifier is named and published**: a gradient at the dorsal raphe modulates autoreceptor feedback and therefore changes net serotonin in the entire projection field, which is Kim's own proposed explanation for the SSRI onset delay `[primary, verified]`. There is outcome-level support that the brake can win: at clinically relevant single doses in humans, cortical serotonin may go **down** on initiation rather than up `[primary, verified — Finnema 2015, PMID 25980484]`. So a regional account can flip the **sign** of a drug's effect, not merely its size, and no other candidate on this list can do that natively.

**Evidence tier. Premise established; application to you, no data exists.** The premise is human, within-subject, primary, and replicated across two independent labs in Seoul and Vienna. The application to you has never been measured and cannot currently be.

**Fit.** escitalopram-failed **✓✓** but earned **by construction**, exactly as candidate 4's is · acute-worse **○** · crash-absent **○** · no-cognitive-content **○** · sleep-better **○** · vivid dreams **○** · less morning fatigue **○** · RLS-worse **○** · pain-minimal **○** · nap-failure **○**

### The DRN inversion, in full

This one deserves its own section, because it is the clearest example on the page of what "killed by logic" means, and because it says something about the field rather than about you.

**In the learned-helplessness model** (Rozeske 2011, PMID 21976495) `[primary, verified]`, verbatim: *"reduced 5-HT1A-R function would... sensitize these cells"*, and *"These data suggest that uncontrollable stressors produce a desensitization of DRN 5-HT1A autoreceptors and that this desensitization is responsible for the behavioral consequences of uncontrollable stress."* Desensitised autoreceptors mean the brake is off, the dorsal raphe is sensitised, and helplessness follows.

**In the antidepressant literature** (Zhang 2025, PMID 40309832) `[primary, verified]`, fluoxetine 10 mg/kg/day for 14 days *"downregulated the 5-HT activation of the inhibitory GirK current in 5-HT neurons"*. Hyperpolarisation fell from −26.03 mV to −8.7 mV. The neurons become resistant to autoinhibition and maintain their spiking.

**And independently**, from a different lab on a different continent, Laaris 1997 (PMID 9243251) `[primary, verified]` showed the stress limb in rats: 16 hours in novel uncontrolled conditions *"significantly reduced the cell response to ipsapirone, indicating a decreased sensitivity of somatodendritic 5-HT1A autoreceptors"*, and the change did not occur in adrenalectomised rats, so it is corticosterone-dependent.

| | **Uncontrollable stress** | **Chronic SSRI** |
|---|---|---|
| 5-HT1A somatodendritic autoreceptor | **Desensitised** | **Desensitised** |
| Autoinhibitory brake on the dorsal raphe | **Removed** | **Removed** |
| DRN firing to subsequent input | **Increased** | **Maintained / increased** |
| Serotonin output | **Increased** | **Increased** |
| **What the field calls it** | **"Sensitization" → helplessness** | **"The antidepressant mechanism"** |

**The two mechanisms are the same event.** One literature calls it the disease. The other calls it the cure. Nobody has reconciled them, and Rozeske's own authors flag the problem in their discussion, noting that *"acute fluoxetine is known to cause 5-HT1A-R internalization in the DRN"* `[primary, verified]`. They raise it precisely because it threatens their causal claim.

**Two caveats against the tidiness of that table** `[reasoning, not evidence]`. Rozeske and Laaris are rats and Zhang is mice, so the two halves are not the same species, and a within-species head-to-head does not exist. And Rozeske's own discussion concedes that the sufficiency test has never been run: *"it would also be desirable to determine whether manipulations that reduce DRN 5-HT1A-R inhibitory function, in the absence of IS treatment, also produce typical IS-induced behaviors"* `[primary, verified]`. **Nobody has checked whether desensitising these autoreceptors, on its own, produces helplessness.**

**What it means for you.** Read as it was proposed to explain your case, this model predicts that duloxetine installs your crash state rather than preventing it. So your Saturday is a counterexample to the model. It is dead, it is dead by logic rather than by heterogeneity, and it must not be resurrected.

### Candidate 5's demotion, in full

The most useful thing on this page, because it shows what a good fit looks like when it is wrong.

**What was read.** "NET inhibition lowers LC tonic firing via α2-autoreceptor feedback while raising terminal NE and raising the phasic:tonic ratio. Read what that predicts: baseline arousal DOWN, acute reactivity UP."

**What the source actually says.** Bari A, Aston-Jones G, *Neuropharmacology* 2013 Jan;64(1):53-64, PMID 22820275, abstract verbatim `[primary, verified]`:

> *"ATM caused a significant decrease of the tonic activity of LC single-units, **although leaving INTACT the sensory-evoked excitatory component of LC phasic response**. Moreover, **the magnitude of the INHIBITORY component of LC response to paw stimulation was INCREASED** after 1 mg/kg of ATM and its **duration was PROLONGED** at 0.3 mg/kg. **Together, these effects of ATM produced an increase in the phasic-to-tonic ratio** of LC phasic response to sensory stimulation."*

The excitatory phasic response was left **intact**, not increased. The ratio rose because the denominator fell and because the inhibitory component grew. **A ratio that rises because its denominator falls is not evidence that the numerator rose.** And the second half is worse for the reading: the *inhibitory* component of the LC's response to a sensory stimulus was increased in magnitude and prolonged in duration, which describes a system that damps down harder and for longer after each sensory event `[reasoning, not evidence — the measurements are primary]`. That is close to the opposite of a system that gets overwhelmed by a crowd.

**Four further problems with the source as used** `[primary, verified]`: it is **atomoxetine**, not duloxetine. It is a **single acute IP injection**, so it cannot speak to two-week dosing at all. It is **isoflurane-anaesthetised rats at 2–2.5%**, and you cannot measure sensory overwhelm in an unconscious animal. And the α2 mechanism is **cited from prior literature, not tested** in that paper.

**The theory contradicts the reading too.** Aston-Jones G, Cohen JD, *Annu Rev Neurosci* 2005;28:403-50, PMID 16022602, verbatim `[primary, verified]`:

> *"When utility in the task wanes, LC neurons exhibit a tonic activity mode, associated with **DISENGAGEMENT from the current task and a search for alternative behaviors (EXPLORATION)**."*

So `tonic → baseline arousal` is not the theory's mapping. Adaptive gain is a theory about a mode switch between exploitation and exploration, not about how much arousal there is. Run the candidate through the theory properly and lowering tonic moves the system **into** the focused, task-engaged mode, which predicts **better** filtering and **less** capture by irrelevant stimuli. **A crowded festival should be easier.** The sanity check: atomoxetine is prescribed to reduce distractibility. If NET inhibition made people more sensory-overwhelmed, atomoxetine would be a bad ADHD drug.

**What still stands from the paper**, with its caveats at the claim `[reasoning, not evidence]`: the stimulus is noxious paw stimulation rather than a crowd, and the model is neuropathic pain rather than autism, so it is thin evidence for anything about you either way. And its inhibitory-period result runs **opposite** to Bari's: atomoxetine increased and prolonged the inhibitory component, duloxetine reduced it `[primary, verified ×2]`. Same claimed mechanism, two drugs, opposite signs on the same measurement, which remains the strongest argument against extrapolating from atomoxetine to duloxetine on LC electrophysiology.

**The counterexample inside your own history.** Szabo ST, de Montigny C, Blier P. *Int J Neuropsychopharmacol.* 2000;3(1):1-11. PMID 11343573 `[primary, verified]`, verbatim:

> *"Sustained administration of the selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) citalopram... and paroxetine... produced a gradual DECREASE in spontaneous firing activity of locus coeruleus (LC) noradrenergic neurons."* And: *"given the hyperadrenergic state often observed in anxiogenic conditions the latter phenomenon is believed to **contribute more to the ANXIOLYTIC effect of SSRIs** than to their antidepressant action."*

So the tonic limb is **not NET-specific**. SSRIs lower LC tonic firing too, via serotonin's tonic inhibitory input, and Szabo proposes that as the anxiolytic mechanism of SSRIs. **Escitalopram is an SSRI and it gave you zero anxiolysis.** That damages the candidate three ways. It destroys its mechanistic specificity, because at 20mg duloxetine is predominantly serotonergic and the serotonergic route to lowered LC tonic firing is at least as available as the NET route the candidate is named after. It supplies a direct counterexample from your own history. And the best rescue available, which is that an SSRI lowers both limbs together while a NET inhibitor lowers tonic and spares phasic, leans entirely on the phasic limb, which is precisely the limb Alba-Delgado tested in duloxetine and found going the wrong way.

**And it is silent on the crash.** Nothing in Bari, Aston-Jones & Cohen, Szabo, Mateo, Rueter, Arai or Alba-Delgado concerns a multi-day delayed sequela `[primary, verified — by absence across seven retrieved papers]`. Every dependent variable in that literature is a firing rate, a pupil, a nap latency, a paw withdrawal or a molecular marker, measured within hours. The candidate's native timescale is seconds to days. Your crash is 7 to 14 days.

**And you cannot observe it.** There is no home measurement of LC tonic/phasic activity, and the pupil does not index it (Megemont 2022, PMID 35107419) `[primary, verified]`.

### The within-person control, in full

The strongest finding in this project, and the only thing on this page that resembles a controlled experiment. It was assembled from material that had been sitting in the corpus for eight passes, and it became visible only when you corrected your own drug history.

**The design, such as it is.** One person. Two serotonergic drugs, about a year apart. Two initiations, both from zero. The outcomes are categorically different and both are well documented by you: escitalopram 10mg produced a week of dissociation severe enough that you could not drive or work; duloxetine 20mg produced mild floatiness and nothing else.

3. **What is within-person is the outcome, not the exposure.** You were never scanned. Every occupancy figure above comes from other people's brains, and the 8.5-fold individual spread documented in the next section applies to those figures as much as to anything else. The escitalopram side is also retrospective, unblinded, and about a year old.

**What survives all three, and why it is enough.**

The claim that escitalopram dissociated you by delivering *more* serotonin is already dead on this page. It died because **this comparison cannot demonstrate a difference between these two drugs.** That is the finding, and it is the same finding read forwards: you cannot explain a difference in outcome by appealing to a comparison that cannot show a difference `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

So an occupancy-magnitude account of your dissociation now requires positing that duloxetine delivered substantially **less** transporter engagement to you specifically than escitalopram did, despite every published estimate saying they match, and in exactly the direction that saves the theory. That rescue is available, because individual variation is real and large. It is also unmeasured, unmotivated, and it needs you to sit at opposite extremes of two different drugs' individual curves by coincidence.

**Verdict, stated at the strength the evidence supports and no higher.** Not "occupancy is definitively refuted." **Occupancy is unsupported as the explanation, and left resting entirely on a rescue nobody can check.** Indistinguishable is not identical, and n=1 with imputed exposure cannot deliver "definitively." What makes it strong is not certainty; it is that the rival account has nowhere left to stand.

**And it does not stand alone**, which is what actually settles it. Candidate 2 reaches the same conclusion from a completely different design: a **3.7**-point occupancy step, blind, placebo-controlled, prospective, between-subject, flipped the human sensory filter from intact to disrupted `[primary, verified ×2 on the outcomes]`. Your control is within-person and imprecise; that one is between-person and precise. They fail in opposite directions and agree on the answer. **That convergence, and not either leg by itself, is why "SERT occupancy is not the explanatory variable" is on this page as a finding rather than a hypothesis.**

**Whose finding it is.** You reasoned to the premise from first principles, and wrote that duloxetine simply works differently from escitalopram and that this alone was enough. An earlier research pass had reached the same place from the literature and recorded it as a hypothesis. **Your corrected history is what turned it from a hypothesis into a demonstration**, and the evidence needed to do that was in the corpus the whole time, unassembled.

### The 8.5-fold spread, and what it licenses

Abanades S, et al. *J Cereb Blood Flow Metab.* 2011 Mar;31(3):944-52. PMID 20940733 `[primary, verified]`. Ten healthy men, duloxetine 20mg single dose then 20mg daily × 4 days, PET with [11C]DASB, scanned about 6 hours after the fourth dose.

| Measure | Group value | **Individual range** | Spread |
|---|---|---|---|
| EC50 (direct fit) | 2.29 ± 1.11 ng/mL | **0.54 – 4.61** | **8.5-fold** |
| Plasma concentration at scan | 19.74 ± 7.8 ng/mL | **5.98 – 34.17** | **5.7-fold** |
| Striatal SERT occupancy | 75.2% | **59% – 86%** | — |

Same drug. Same dose. Same protocol. Same laboratory. Same four days.

**What that licenses, stated carefully** `[reasoning, not evidence]`:

- **It licenses "the population curve does not describe any individual in it."** That is a direct reading of the table.
- **It licenses "try and see is correct clinical practice."** There is no measurement a prescriber could take that would place you within that 8.5-fold band. Titration is not laziness; it is the only instrument available.
- **It licenses "your Lexapro/duloxetine divergence is expected."** You do not need a special explanation for responding differently from a trial average, because responding differently from a trial average is the normal condition.
- **It does NOT license "therefore any mechanism might be true for you."** That is the move this page exists to refuse. Individual variation rescues a null average. It does not rescue a model that runs backwards. See the two kinds of wrong.

### What has never been studied

Seven absences. The first four are the standing set. Do not read any of them as hedging; they are the highest-confidence claims on this page, because they assert the least.

1. **Nobody has studied duloxetine in autistic adults.** One three-patient case series exists.
2. **Nobody has studied the overwhelm-to-crash cycle pharmacologically.** The only pharmacological entry in the entire autistic-burnout literature is a single conference-abstract case report of a stimulant `[secondary — unverified]`. The recovery literature is entirely environmental and behavioural: rest, self-understanding, reduced masking, changed expectations. There is no pharmacology in it at all.
3. **Nobody has studied whether prior dissociation predicts recurrence.**
4. **Nobody has studied this decoupling in any human, for any drug** `[no evidence exists]`. Not "the evidence is weak." The question has never been asked.
5. **Nobody has measured what duloxetine, or any SNRI, does to daytime sleep propensity.** No MSLT study exists at any dose in any population `[primary, verified — search-level]`.
6. **Nobody has recorded a locus coeruleus neuron on chronic duloxetine in an awake animal**, let alone a human. The only chronic-duloxetine electrophysiology study (Rueter 1998, PMID 9580577) recorded the dorsal raphe and hippocampus and never touched the LC. The only chronic-duloxetine LC recording (Alba-Delgado 2012) is anaesthetised, in a neuropathic pain model, with noxious paw stimulation `[primary, verified ×2]`.
7. **The literature on antidepressant-induced dissociation is empty** `[no evidence exists]`. Not thin. Empty. PubMed returns **44** papers for depersonalisation or derealisation crossed with SSRIs or antidepressants, and most of them are **burnout studies** matching only because the Maslach Burnout Inventory contains a subscale called "depersonalisation," which is an unrelated construct sharing a word. The rest are depersonalisation *treated with* SSRIs, Cotard's syndrome, and dissociative-disorder pharmacotherapy reviews. Narrowed to **induced** dissociation: **4 papers, none on topic** `[primary, verified — search-level]`. **No prevalence figure exists. No time course exists. No habituation data exists.** Nobody has asked the question. This is the absence most specific to you: your single most disabling drug reaction is a phenomenon the field has never named.

**The escape route from absence 7, and why it is a trap.** There **is** a named dose-transition syndrome, and the sentence anyone would reach for exists nearly verbatim: an adverse effect recognised *"during the initiation or change of dose"* of an antidepressant `[primary abstract only — Arai 2023, PMID 38868737]`. It is **activation** or **jitteriness syndrome**, and every study that operationalises it uses the same eleven items: anxiety, agitation, panic attacks, insomnia, irritability, hostility, aggressiveness, impulsivity, akathisia, hypomania, mania `[primary abstract only — Harada 2008, PMID 18781664; identical list in the prospective replication, Harada 2014, PMID 25419134]`.

**Dissociation is not on that list. Depersonalisation is not on that list. Derealisation is not on that list.** It is a syndrome of **over-arousal** and you report the opposite pole. **Mapping yourself onto it would repeat the non-fearful-panic error exactly**: matching a construct to your experience on the strength of an absence, when the construct excludes the thing you actually have. An absence is not a fingerprint.

Three further reasons not to reach for it, none of which required leaving the abstracts `[primary abstract only]`. Its own systematic review of 107 articles found **no validated rating scale**, an incidence range of **4% to 65%**, no robust evidence of any difference between drug classes, and concluded the syndrome *"remains poorly characterised."* A 4-to-65% range is a confession rather than an estimate, and it is the same shape as the tachyphylaxis range this project already called out. The review set out to establish a time course and its results section reports none. And the one prospective study that did measure a time course **required participants to have taken no antidepressant for at least a month before enrolling**, so it studied **initiation only and contains no dose-increase data at all** `[primary, verified]`. **The dose-change half of the only named dose-change syndrome is asserted, not measured.**

**Read absence 4 against the candidate list and it explains the list's shape.** The nearest thing that exists is Musazzi: a real acute-intact/downstream-blocked decoupling, in a rat. The human evidence (Oranje/Jensen) explains the acute half and says nothing about any aftermath. The animal evidence (Chattarji) explains the aftermath half and has never been given an antidepressant. The one human study of chronic serotonergic adaptation (Zohar) predicts acute-**better**. Propranolol prophylaxis is an anti-parallel. **No study, in any species, has both halves.**

### The trap, laid out

| | **Your model** | **The standard model** |
|---|---|---|
| The claim | 20mg prevents crashes → less cumulative load → pain improves slowly over months, no threshold | Pain relief requires noradrenergic reuptake inhibition at ~40–60mg. 20mg does approximately nothing for pain, ever |
| Prediction **now, at 20mg** | Pain drifts down over months; fatigue improves first | Pain stays flat indefinitely |
| Prediction **at 60mg** | **Pain improves** | **Pain improves** |
| What you learn at 60mg | **Nothing.** Both models satisfied | **Nothing.** Both models satisfied |

**Going to 60mg satisfies both models and destroys the experiment permanently** `[reasoning, not evidence]`.

The discriminators that remain available at 20mg, in descending order of strength:

1. **Order of effects (strongest, and free).** Your model must run through fatigue. So: fatigue improves first or concurrently, pain follows weeks later. The standard model predicts pain moves **without** any change in fatigue, and only after a dose increase. **Track them separately. If pain improves while fatigue does not, your model is dead.**
2. **Contingency on exposure (unique to your model).** Your benefit is purchased by crashes that did not happen, so it is contingent on exposure history and should be step-wise. The standard model's benefit is a pharmacological constant, indifferent to what your week contained. **The killer within-subject test: if a crash does break through, does pain step up and stay up for weeks?** If pain returns to baseline within days, the aggravation limb is falsified even though crash-prevention would still be real.
3. **Speed of onset.** Direct analgesia is fast; allostatic unloading is slow by construction. Pain dropping within 2–4 weeks of a dose change means direct pharmacology. Pain drifting down over months at a fixed dose means your model, or noise. Bergenheim supplies the noise floor `[primary, verified]`: 13% mean improvement over 10–12 years, with only 16% achieving ≥50%. **Spontaneous large improvement in fibromyalgia is rare**, so a substantial drift down at a fixed 20mg would be genuinely surprising and therefore informative.
4. **Pre-commit, or the test is worthless.** Specify how long and how much **in advance**. Without a pre-registered threshold, any outcome reads as confirmation ("it's working, just slowly"), which is exactly how an unfalsifiable belief forms.

**The sensory-gating dose warning, restated because it belongs next to the trap.** You have already accepted worse acute sensitivity at 20mg. The Jensen/Oranje ladder says a **1.5×** step flipped the filter from intact to disrupted. You are contemplating **3×**. The claim that the acute window would re-open only "transiently" is **unsupported**: every study in that literature is single-dose, so chronicity has never been measured `[no evidence exists]`.

**And the asymmetry that is the actual decision content** `[reasoning, not evidence]`: the cost of waiting is months of unrelieved pain, and the benefit is a clean, unrepeatable answer. That is a question only you can answer. This page's job is to make sure you know the window exists before it shuts. **Nothing here directs a dose.**

### What would tell you more

Four things, all free, none of which requires anybody's permission.

1. **A breakthrough crash after an exposure no worse than the festivals** would falsify candidate 3 cleanly. It is the sharpest single falsifier on the list.
2. **Fatigue and pain as separate journal lines** separate your model from the standard one, and they are the same instrument.
3. **Whether the sleep benefit holds on quiet weeks** separates candidate 1 (which predicts it should, because it is not contingent on exposure) from candidates 3 and 6 (which are silent about it).
4. **Whether dreams and sleep move together** tests candidate 1's internal coherence, because under that account they are one mechanism rather than two.

One thing that will **not** tell you more, and it is worth knowing before you spend effort on it: a **failed** nap attempt tells you nothing, because duloxetine improved your sleep, which reduces sleep drive, which makes napping harder, and the two effects cancel by an unknown amount. A **successful** nap on a lifelong hard-zero channel would be genuinely informative. The test is worth running for the positive and worthless for the negative, and you should know that before you run it rather than after. Categories 6 and 7 carry the protocol.
