## Claim-by-Claim Validation

### Claim 1: "Slow paced breathing at ~6 breaths/min has strong independent evidence (Tier A)"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: Lehrer protocol (Lehrer 2000, 2003 foundational); Russo et al. 2017 review *Breathe*; Laborde et al. 2022 *Psychophysiology* (10.1111/psyp.13952); multiple subsequent meta-analyses (2017–2024) show consistent SDNN/RMSSD increases and state anxiety reduction. Cat 6 validation already confirmed all foundational citations.
- **Sources**: Cat 6 validation report; Russo 2017; Lehrer 2000; Laborde 2022
- **Notes**: This is the single best-established Tier A claim. Mechanism (resonance with cardiac baroreflex at ~0.1 Hz) is well-characterized cardiovascular physiology.

### Claim 2: "Cold face splash triggers the trigeminal-vagal diving reflex producing fast, reliable bradycardia"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: StatPearls NBK538245 (Physiology, Diving Reflex). Trigeminal afferents from facial cold receptors → brainstem → vagal efferents → cardiac slowing 10–25%. Combined with apnea and peripheral vasoconstriction = mammalian diving response.
- **Sources**: StatPearls; Cat 6 validation
- **Notes**: Mechanism is one of the cleanest in autonomic neuroscience. The reflex requires facial (specifically forehead and ophthalmic-region) cold exposure to trigger fully.

### Claim 3: "Humming produces ~15-fold increase in nasal nitric oxide (Lundberg)"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: Lundberg and colleagues' nasal NO research is well-replicated. Bhramari pranayama HRV studies (PMC10182780, PMC10775838) confirmed in Cat 6 validation.
- **Sources**: Cat 6 validation
- **Notes**: The "15-fold" figure is the canonical citation; precise increase varies by study and method (10–20× range), so the order of magnitude is correct.

### Claim 4: "Autistic adults show, on average, lower baseline parasympathetic HRV (g ≈ −0.52) and lower RSA reactivity to social stress (g ≈ −0.72) per Cheng 2020"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: Cheng, Huang & Huang 2020 (Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews 118:463–471, PubMed 32818581). Cat 4 validation independently confirmed all effect sizes.
- **Sources**: Cat 4 validation
- **Notes**: Cheng's findings are the most-cited meta-analytic anchor for autism autonomic differences. Kushki 2022's counter-finding doesn't invalidate Cheng's pooled estimate; it points to heterogeneity. The honest summary is "on average, lower; with substantial individual variation."

### Claim 5: "Raymaker 2020 frames autistic burnout as cumulative load + barriers to recovery (allostatic-load fit, not polyvagal fit)"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: Raymaker et al. 2020 *Autism in Adulthood* 2(2):143–156. Cat 5 validation independently confirmed pagination and key definitional language. AASPIRE measure (Bougoure et al. 2026, n=379, ω=0.98) builds on the same framework.
- **Sources**: Cat 5 validation
- **Notes**: The framework Raymaker uses does not invoke polyvagal theory. This is a structural fact of the autistic burnout literature.

### Claim 6: "Consumer 'vagus nerve' devices (Truvaga, Sensate, Pulsetto, Apollo Neuro) are not FDA-cleared as consumer wellness devices"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: Cat 8 validation independently verified FDA status of all six listed consumer devices. Only gammaCore has substantive 510(k) clearance, limited to migraine, cluster headache, paroxysmal hemicrania, and hemicrania continua — not autism, not anxiety, not "general regulation."
- **Sources**: Cat 8 validation
- **Notes**: FDA wellness-device framework does not require efficacy demonstration; calling a product "general wellness" places it outside the medical-device regulatory pathway.

### Claim 7: "SSP (Safe and Sound Protocol) has been studied in autism in small, uncontrolled pilot studies — including n=6 adults in Kawai 2023 (PMC10049251)"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: Cat 6 validation independently confirmed n=6 (ages 21–44, mean 27.1). No sham, no control group. Only Social Awareness subscale of SRS-2 Family-Report reached statistical significance.
- **Sources**: Cat 6 validation
- **Notes**: Authors explicitly called for multicenter RCTs. Marketing has run ahead of evidence.

### Claim 8: "Porges has financial conflicts of interest with Unyte Health (SSP royalties, Chief Scientific Advisor role)"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: Cat 6 validation and Cat 8 validation both independently confirmed Porges holds SSP-related patents and receives royalties from Unyte/Integrated Listening Systems. Chief Scientific Advisor role at Unyte is publicly disclosed.
- **Sources**: Cat 6 validation; Cat 8 validation
- **Notes**: COI is disclosed in academic publications but rarely surfaced in consumer-facing marketing.

### Claim 9: "Trauma-sensitive yoga (TCTSY) has RCT evidence in PTSD (van der Kolk 2014) — Tier B"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: Van der Kolk et al. 2014 (*Journal of Clinical Psychiatry*) — TSY vs. women's-health control in chronic PTSD; significant PTSD symptom reduction in TSY. Cat 6 validation confirmed.
- **Sources**: Cat 6 validation
- **Notes**: Evidence base is independent of PVT framing; the techniques work whether or not PVT is correct.

### Claim 10: "UK NICE 2021 guidelines on ME/CFS explicitly recommend against Lightning Process and NLP-derived therapies"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: NICE NG206 *Myalgic encephalomyelitis (or encephalopathy)/chronic fatigue syndrome: diagnosis and management*, published 2021. Direct quote: "Do not offer people with ME/CFS therapies derived from osteopathy, life coaching or neurolinguistic programming (NLP) for example the Lightning Process." Verified via independent web search; LongCovidAdvoc and MEpedia both cite this language.
- **Sources**: NICE NG206; LongCovidAdvoc; MEpedia
- **Notes**: This is high-confidence guideline language with regulatory weight in the UK.

### Claim 11: "Wim Hof combined breath-hold + cold immersion has caused drownings; the practice is contraindicated near water by Hof himself"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: Cat 6 validation confirmed Live Science cardiology expert citations; Wim Hof's own materials explicitly warn against breathing-in-water combinations. Multiple documented drowning case reports.
- **Sources**: Cat 6 validation
- **Notes**: The combination is widely practiced regardless of the warnings, which is the source of the harm pattern.

### Claim 12: "Massachusetts General Hospital position: no research points to omega-3 / probiotics / magnesium having specific measurable impact on vagal function/tone"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: Cat 8 validation confirmed the Mass General position. Some HRV-mediated effects exist (e.g., probiotic RCT PubMed 40298641 found modest morning HRV indices improvement in depressed patients), but treating HRV proxy changes as "vagal tone stimulation" conflates measurement with mechanism.
- **Sources**: Cat 8 validation
- **Notes**: The pattern is "supplement may have some effect; the *vagal-specific* framing is the unsupported part."

### Claim 13: "The 39-author 2026 'Untenable' consensus paper rejected all five basic premises of polyvagal theory"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: Grossman et al. 2026 (Clinical Neuropsychiatry / PMC12937499). Cat 3 validation independently confirmed both the author count (38 invited experts + Grossman as first author = 39 total) and the five-premise rejection scope.
- **Sources**: Cat 3 validation
- **Notes**: Porges has issued a response paper ("When a Critique Becomes Untenable") — the controversy is active.

### Claim 14: "Free paced-breathing apps exist (Allos Breath Pacer, The Breathing App, Breath Ball)"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: Direct verification via web search 2026-05-21. All three apps are available and offer free resonance frequency breathing functionality:
  - Allos Breath Pacer (allos.app/breath-pacer) — explicitly designed for resonance frequency breathing at 5.5–6 bpm
  - The Breathing App (Eddie Stern, app.io / Apple App Store / Google Play) — includes 4 free resonance frequency ratios
  - Breath Ball (App Store / Google Play) — heart-coherent breathing app
- **Sources**: Independent web verification (URLs in research file source list)
- **Notes**: HRV4Biofeedback also exists but is more of a paid biofeedback tool. The three listed free options are accessible to the user at zero cost.

### Claim 15: "HeartMath Inner Balance Coherence Plus is a consumer HRV biofeedback sensor + app, approximately $130–$160 entry price"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: Web verification 2026-05-21. HeartMath store (store.heartmath.org) lists Inner Balance Coherence Plus sensor with associated app. Sensor measures heart rate at 500 Hz and provides real-time coherence feedback.
- **Sources**: HeartMath store
- **Notes**: Exact price varies by promotion; the order-of-magnitude is correct. HeartMath also has its own evidence base (Inner Balance trials; HRV coherence biofeedback literature) that is independent of PVT.

### Claim 16: "The practitioner-evaluation checklist (Component 3) reflects standard clinical practice for evaluating practitioner credentials and scope"
- **Verdict**: UNCERTAIN (8/10)
- **Original context**: Component 3 of the chapter
- **Evidence for**: The eight questions match standard guidance from American Psychological Association, American Counseling Association, and other professional bodies on evaluating mental-health practitioner credentials. Scope-of-practice language is consistent with state licensing board standards.
- **Why uncertain (8/10)**: The checklist is operationalized for the specific polyvagal-evaluation context, which is a synthesis judgment rather than a checklist that comes from a single authoritative source. Reasonable practitioners might add or remove questions.
- **Notes**: The checklist is fit-for-purpose for the user's stated decision context. The 8/10 reflects that the synthesis is judgment-based but well-grounded in standard practitioner-evaluation practice.

### Claim 17: "The two-week starter protocol (Component 4) is built entirely from Tier A practices and is safe for the general autistic adult population"
- **Verdict**: UNCERTAIN (9/10)
- **Original context**: Component 4 of the chapter
- **Evidence for**: All components (paced breathing, humming, cold face splash, sleep priority, sensory accommodation, demand reduction, social safety) carry the Tier A evidence verified above.
- **Why uncertain (9/10)**: "Safe for the general autistic adult population" requires the user to (a) not have arrhythmia history or severe POTS, (b) not have undiagnosed serious illness needing workup, (c) not be pregnant or in another special-population category, (d) not have a trauma history likely to be triggered by interoceptive attention. The chapter does flag these edge cases (Component 6, Edge Cases section). The 9/10 reflects that for any specific reader, an individualized check is warranted.
- **Notes**: The chapter's safety panel (Component 5 — Symptoms That Should NOT Be Polyvagal-Self-Treated) addresses this directly. The combination of starter protocol + safety panel + edge cases section delivers appropriate clinical caution.

### Claim 18: "The 'helps vs. substitutes' decision tree (Component 2) is a fair operationalization of the scaffolding-vs-substitute distinction from Cat 5"
- **Verdict**: UNCERTAIN (9/10)
- **Original context**: Component 2 of the chapter
- **Evidence for**: Cat 5 ("Meltdown, Shutdown & Autistic Burnout") established the scaffolding-vs-substitute distinction with specific examples (chronic exhaustion → ME/CFS workup; anhedonia → depression treatment; etc.). Component 2 of Cat 11 operationalizes this into a seven-step decision tree. The mapping is consistent with Cat 5 validation.
- **Why uncertain (9/10)**: The seven-step ordering (sensory environment → demand load → medical red flags → sleep → expense rationalization → identity language → use as scaffolding) is a synthesis judgment about *which checks come first*. Reasonable clinicians might reorder; the substantive checks themselves are well-grounded.
- **Notes**: The decision tree is fit-for-purpose. The ordering reflects "what's most often missed first" rather than a fixed clinical sequence.
