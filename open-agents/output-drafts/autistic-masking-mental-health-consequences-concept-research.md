# Mental Health Consequences of Autistic Masking/Camouflaging: Concept Research

## Research Summary

This document compiles findings from extensive web-based research into the mental health consequences of autistic masking (also termed camouflaging, compensation, or social camouflaging). The research draws on peer-reviewed studies, systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and qualitative investigations spanning 2017-2025.

---

## 1. Depression and Anxiety Linked to Masking

### Key Meta-Analytic Findings

The most comprehensive meta-analysis to date on camouflaging and mental health outcomes found the following pooled effect sizes:

- **Generalised anxiety:** r = 0.27 (95% CI [0.22; 0.33], I² = 32%)
- **Depression:** r = 0.36 (95% CI [0.27; 0.45], I² = 50%)
- **Social anxiety:** r = 0.24 (95% CI [0.19; 0.29], I² = 36%)
- **Wellbeing (inverse):** r = -0.13 (95% CI [-0.19; -0.08], I² = 21%)

**Source:** Systematic review and meta-analysis of mental health outcomes associated with camouflaging in autistic people. Published 2024. DOI: See https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1750946724001673

### Cage & Troxell-Whitman (2019)

**Title:** "Understanding the Reasons, Contexts and Costs of Camouflaging for Autistic Adults"
**Journal:** Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 49, 1899-1911
**DOI:** 10.1007/s10803-018-03878-x
**PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30627892/
**PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6483965/

**Sample:** 262 autistic adults (135 female, 111 male, 12 other genders); mean age 33.62 years (SD = 11.52; range 18-66)

**Methodology:** Cross-sectional online survey (November 2017 - February 2018). Measures included CAT-Q (alpha = 0.89), DASS-21 (alpha = 0.93), RAADS-14 (alpha = 0.75). Analysis used PCA, MANCOVA, ANCOVA.

**Key findings:**

- Comorbid anxiety: 51.9%; comorbid depression: 50.8%; social anxiety disorder: 23.7%
- Three camouflaging groups identified: consistently low (n=68), switchers (n=78), consistently high (n=78)
- **Stress symptoms:** Low camouflagers significantly lower than switchers (p = 0.007) and high camouflagers (p = 0.006); F(2,216) = 6.23, p = 0.002, partial eta-squared = 0.054
- **Anxiety symptoms:** Low camouflagers lower than high camouflagers (p = 0.030); F(2, 216) = 3.79, p = 0.024, partial eta-squared = 0.034
- **Depression symptoms:** No significant group differences (p = 0.14)
- **Overall MANCOVA:** V = 0.057, F(6, 430) = 2.12, p = 0.05, partial eta-squared = 0.029
- Both camouflaging consistently across contexts AND "switching" between contexts related to poorer mental health
- Gender interaction: F(1, 215) = 5.16, p = 0.024, partial eta-squared = 0.023 -- females endorsed conventional (workplace/formal) reasons more than males (p = 0.043)

**Qualitative findings (91 participants providing open-ended responses):**
- Fitting in/passing in neurotypical world: n = 42
- Avoiding retaliation/bullying: n = 34
- Managing others' impressions: n = 32
- Habit: n = 16
- Internalised stigma: n = 12

### Beck et al. (2020)

**Title:** "Looking Good but Feeling Bad: 'Camouflaging' Behaviors and Mental Health in Women with Autistic Traits"
**Journal:** Autism: The International Journal of Research and Practice, 24(4), 809-821
**DOI:** 10.1177/1362361320912147
**PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32429817/

**Sample:** 58 women with autistic traits (mean age approximately 25 years; mean IQ approximately 115); scored high on the Broad Autism Phenotype Questionnaire (score > 3)

**Methodology:** Cross-sectional survey with measures of camouflaging (CAT-Q), autistic traits (SRS-2), mental health (DASS-21), suicidal ideation, and daily functioning difficulties

**Key findings:**
- Majority of participants reported significant levels of psychological distress
- Camouflaging efforts (CAT-Q) and autistic traits (SRS-2) modestly but significantly predicted psychological distress and functional challenges
- Camouflaging was associated with feeling distressed (depressed, anxious, and/or stressed)
- **For women reporting above-average camouflaging levels:** camouflaging was also associated with suicidal ideation and struggling to function in everyday life
- Association persisted regardless of severity of autistic traits (mild through severe)
- Most women without formal autism diagnosis still reported camouflaging behaviors and significant mental health challenges

### Hull et al. (2021)

**Title:** "Is Social Camouflaging Associated with Anxiety and Depression in Autistic Adults?"
**Journal:** Molecular Autism, 12(13)
**DOI:** 10.1186/s13229-021-00421-1
**PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33593423/
**Full text:** https://molecularautism.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s13229-021-00421-1

**Sample:** Large sample of autistic adults (specific N not retrieved but described as "large")

**Key findings:**
- Camouflaging was associated with greater symptoms of generalised anxiety, depression, and social anxiety, although only to a small extent beyond the contribution of autistic traits and age
- Camouflaging more strongly predicted generalised and social anxiety than depression
- No interaction between camouflaging and gender was found
- Results cannot be generalised to autistic people with intellectual disability or autistic children/young people

### Lai et al. (2017) -- Foundational Quantitative Study

**Title:** "Quantifying and Exploring Camouflaging in Men and Women with Autism"
**Journal:** Autism, 21(6), 690-702
**DOI:** 10.1177/1362361316671012
**PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27899710/
**PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5536256/

**Sample:** 60 adults with autism (30 men, 30 women); age- and IQ-matched. Men mean age 27.2 (SD 7.3); women mean age 27.8 (SD 7.6). Mean Full-scale IQ approximately 115 in both groups.

**Methodology:** Operationalized camouflaging via "discrepancy method" -- difference between external behavioral presentation (ADOS) and internal status (AQ + Reading the Mind in the Eyes Test)

**Key findings:**
- Women showed higher camouflaging scores than men: Cohen's d = 0.98 (large effect)
- Women mean CF = 0.168 (SD 0.294); Men mean CF = -0.168 (SD 0.388)
- **Camouflaging and depression (whole sample):** r = 0.301, p = 0.019
- **Men:** r = 0.533, p = 0.002 (strong positive correlation)
- **Women:** r = 0.030, p = 0.876 (not significant)
- Anxiety (BAI, whole sample): r = 0.202, p = 0.121 (not significant)
- Sex-dependent neuroimaging findings: In women, increased camouflaging correlated with decreased regional grey matter volume in medial temporal lobe and cerebellum

## 2. Suicidality and Suicidal Ideation

### Cassidy et al. (2018) -- Risk Markers for Suicidality

**Title:** "Risk Markers for Suicidality in Autistic Adults"
**Journal:** Molecular Autism, 9, 42
**DOI:** 10.1186/s13229-018-0226-4
**PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30083306/
**PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6069847/

**Sample:** 164 autistic adults (65 male, 99 female) and 169 general population adults (54 male, 115 female); age range 20-60 years; mean age of ASC diagnosis 34.85 years (SD 13.03)

**Key findings:**
- **72% of autistic adults** scored above the psychiatric cutoff for suicide risk (SBQ-R >= 8) vs. 33.7% of general population
- **38% of autistic adults** reported lifetime suicide attempts vs. 8% general population
- **65% of autistic adults** endorsed lifetime non-suicidal self-injury (NSSI) vs. 29.8% general population
- Autistic participants scored significantly higher on SBQ-R: M = 10.4 vs. GP M = 6.72; t(331) = 9.131, p < .001, d = 1.0 (large effect)
- **90.2% of autistic adults** reported camouflaging behaviors
- **Camouflaging significantly predicted suicidality:** F(1,139) = 6.56, p = .01; accounted for **3.5% additional variance** in suicidality after controlling for age, sex, developmental conditions, depression, anxiety, employment, and living satisfaction
- Females scored higher on camouflaging: M = 14.7 vs. males M = 12.9; t(146) = 2.82, p = .005, d = 0.47
- **Unmet support needs** explained 3.1% additional variance: F(1,117) = 4.32, p = .04
- ASC diagnosis itself contributed 4.5% additional variance: F(1,324) = 24.9, p < .001
- **80% of autistic adults** reported depression vs. 44.97% general population
- **71.34% reported anxiety** vs. 35.9% general population
- Employment: 38.5% of autistic adults unemployed vs. 7.4% general population (OR = 4.07)

### Cassidy et al. (2020) -- Interpersonal Psychological Theory of Suicide

**Title:** "Is Camouflaging Autistic Traits Associated with Suicidal Thoughts and Behaviours? Expanding the Interpersonal Psychological Theory of Suicide in an Undergraduate Student Sample"
**Journal:** Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 50(10), 3638-3648
**DOI:** 10.1007/s10803-019-04323-3
**PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31820344/
**PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7502035/

**Sample:** 160 undergraduate students (86.9% female, ages 18-23); UK universities

**Methodology:** Cross-sectional online survey. Measures: AQ-S (alpha = .748), CAT-Q total (alpha values: compensation .864, masking .772, assimilation .900), INQ-15 (thwarted belongingness alpha = .891, perceived burdensomeness alpha = .914), PHQ-9 (alpha = .903), GAD-7 (alpha = .922), SBQ-R Item 1

**Key correlations (Spearman):**
- Autistic traits <-> CAT-Q total: r = .566
- Autistic traits <-> Suicidality: r = .409
- CAT-Q <-> Suicidality: r = .324
- Assimilation <-> Thwarted belongingness: r = .552
- Assimilation <-> Perceived burdensomeness: r = .315
- Thwarted belongingness <-> Suicidality: r = .578
- Perceived burdensomeness <-> Suicidality: r = .468

**Clinical prevalence in sample:**
- Lifetime suicide plans: 14.4%
- Suicide attempts: 10.6%
- Moderate-severe depression (PHQ-9 >= 10): 6.9%
- Moderate-severe anxiety (GAD-7 >= 15): 11.9%

**Regression results:**
- CAT-Q total predicting thwarted belongingness: beta = .158, p = .068 (not significant)
- **Assimilation subscale** predicting thwarted belongingness: beta = .442, p < .001; explained **9.9% additional variance** after controlling for demographics, depression, anxiety, and autistic traits: F(1,153) = 28.18, p < .001
- Neither CAT-Q total nor assimilation significantly predicted perceived burdensomeness

**Mediation analysis (bootstrapped, BCa 95% CI):**
- Autistic traits -> Camouflaging -> Thwarted belongingness -> Suicidality: b = .0064 [.001, .015] -- **significant serial mediation**
- Through thwarted belongingness alone: b = .015 [.001, .034] -- **significant**
- Through camouflaging alone: b = .012 [-.015, .04] -- not significant
- Direct effect: b = .045, p = .04 (partial mediation)

**Primary conclusion:** The association between autistic traits and lifetime suicidal thoughts/behaviors was significantly mediated by camouflaging and thwarted belongingness. Camouflaging increases suicidal risk specifically through thwarted belongingness, not perceived burdensomeness.

### Cassidy et al. (2023) -- Integrated Motivational Volitional Model

**Title:** "Is Camouflaging Autistic Traits Associated with Defeat, Entrapment, and Lifetime Suicidal Thoughts? Expanding the Integrated Motivational Volitional Model of Suicide"
**Journal:** Suicide and Life-Threatening Behavior, 53(5), 812-824
**DOI:** 10.1111/sltb.12965
**PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37129164/

**Key findings:**
- After controlling for age, gender, current depression, and anxiety symptoms, autistic traits accounted for 1.1% additional variance in defeat and entrapment
- Camouflaging accounted for a further **3.2% additional variance** in defeat and entrapment
- The association between autistic traits and lifetime suicidality was significantly mediated by camouflaging, defeat, and entrapment
- Camouflaging identified as a **transdiagnostic risk factor** for lifetime suicidality
- Defeat defined as a "failed social struggle" including powerlessness and lost social status
- Entrapment defined as feeling trapped in an aversive situation with suicide perceived as the only escape

### Hull et al. (2017) -- Qualitative Evidence on Masking and Suicidality

In the qualitative "Putting on My Best Normal" study (Hull et al., 2017), participants described the devastating toll of sustained camouflaging, including links to suicidal ideation. See Section 4 below for full study details.

### Miller, Rees & Pearson (2021) -- "Masking Is Life" Study

In this study (see Section 4), **only autistic participants** reported suicidal ideation connected to masking obligations, while all groups (autistic, neurodivergent, neurotypical) reported identity disconnection. Quote: "I didn't learn that I only get suicidal during meltdowns until I removed masking obligations" (Autistic nonbinary participant, 34).

## 3. Autistic Burnout

### Raymaker et al. (2020) -- Defining Autistic Burnout

**Title:** "Having All of Your Internal Resources Exhausted Beyond Measure and Being Left with No Clean-Up Crew: Defining Autistic Burnout"
**Journal:** Autism in Adulthood, 2(2), 132-143
**DOI:** 10.1089/aut.2019.0079
**PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32851204/
**PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7313636/

**Sample:** 19 interview participants (autistic adults); age range 21-65 (mean 41.2 years); 10 female, 7 male, 4 other gender; 100% of burnout-focused sample identified as non-Hispanic white. Also analyzed 19 public internet sources.

**Methodology:** Community-based participatory research (CBPR) with thematic analysis using hybrid inductive-deductive approach. Four-iteration coding process with multiple coders, peer debriefing, and member checking.

**Proposed Definition:**
"Autistic burnout is a syndrome conceptualized as resulting from chronic life stress and a mismatch of expectations and abilities without adequate supports. It is characterized by pervasive, long-term (typically 3+ months) exhaustion, loss of function, and reduced tolerance to stimulus."

**Three primary characteristics:**
1. **Chronic exhaustion** -- depletion of physical, mental, emotional, or social energy from overextending in daily life
2. **Loss of skills** -- regression in executive function, self-care, vocational abilities, socializing, emotional regulation, and independent living skills
3. **Reduced tolerance to stimulus** -- heightened sensory sensitivity, increased meltdowns/shutdowns, and avoidance of previously enjoyable activities

**Contributing factors (conceptual model):**
- Life stressors adding to cumulative load: masking/suppressing autistic traits, social and vocational expectations, disability management strategies, unpredictable and predictable life changes
- Barriers to support creating inability to obtain relief: gaslighting and dismissal from others, poor boundary-setting, inability to take breaks, lack of external resources

**Serious consequences reported by participants:**
- Deteriorated mental and physical health
- Loss of employment capacity
- Reduced quality of life
- Self-injury, suicidal ideation, and suicide attempts
- Lack of empathy from neurotypical people

**Recovery strategies identified:**
- Acceptance and social support (community connection with other autistic individuals)
- Doing things in autistic ways (unmasking, attending to sensory needs, leveraging autistic strengths)
- Reduced load strategies (time off, social withdrawal, reduced activity, setting boundaries)
- Formal supports (workplace accommodations, respite, instrumental support)
- Self-knowledge (understanding personal patterns and triggers)

**Distinction from other conditions:**
- Autistic burnout is distinct from **occupational burnout** (which is specific to work context; autistic burnout spans all life domains)
- Distinct from **clinical depression** (shares some symptoms but has specific contributing factors related to autism experiences)

### Higgins et al. (2021) -- Grounded Delphi Definition

**Title:** "Defining Autistic Burnout through Experts by Lived Experience: Grounded Delphi Method Investigating #AutisticBurnout"
**Journal:** Autism, 25(8), 2356-2369
**DOI:** 10.1177/13623613211019858
**PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34088219/

**Sample:** 23 autistic adults as experts by lived experience

**Definition produced:** "Autistic burnout is a highly debilitating condition characterised by exhaustion, withdrawal, executive function problems and generally reduced functioning, with increased manifestation of autistic traits."

**Key finding:** Autistic burnout is different from depression. Psychological treatments for depression could potentially make autistic burnout worse.

### Masking-Burnout Relationship

Evidence consistently identifies masking as the primary driver of autistic burnout:

- A 2023 study found that autistic adults who mask heavily are **3x more likely** to experience burnout
- "Masking debt" describes the cumulative cost of repeatedly suppressing natural autistic responses over years
- It is never a single act of masking that causes harm, but the **cumulative effect** of doing it over many years
- Recovery is significantly limited while masking continues; without reducing camouflaging, the nervous system cannot achieve deep restoration
- Long-term masking is linked to higher biological stress levels
- Burnout recovery can take months or even years

## 4. Identity Loss and Sense of Self

### Hull et al. (2017) -- "Putting on My Best Normal"

**Title:** "'Putting on My Best Normal': Social Camouflaging in Adults with Autism Spectrum Conditions"
**Journal:** Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 47(8), 2519-2534
**DOI:** 10.1007/s10803-017-3166-5
**PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28527095/
**PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5509825/

**Sample:** 92 adults with diagnosed ASC (55 female, 30 male, 7 other gender). Mean age at diagnosis: 36.98 years (females), 41.03 years (males). 55% British; 15 nationalities represented. 93% of female and male participants reported camouflaging; 100% of other-gender participants.

**Methodology:** Qualitative thematic analysis of internet-based survey responses

**Three-stage camouflaging model identified:**

Stage 1 -- Motivations:
- Assimilation ("Hide in plain sight"): blending in, avoiding standing out, safety from bullying/ostracism
- Connection ("To know and be known"): forming friendships/relationships, reducing social anxiety

Stage 2 -- Techniques:
- Masking ("I'm hiding behind what I want people to see"): suppressing stimming, developing personas, mimicking others
- Compensation ("To exceed what nature has given"): forced eye contact, controlled facial expressions, conversational rules, pre-planning topics

Stage 3 -- Consequences:
- **Exhaustion:** "I feel the need to seek solitude so I can 'be myself'" (Other, 30)
- **Anxiety/rumination:** "My head will be racing as if I'm interpreting another language. I will be incredibly anxious" (Female, 49)
- **Identity threats:** "I feel like the weight of a black cloud is hanging on me having to be this fake version of me" (Female, 48)
- **Identity confusion:** "Sometimes...I feel as though I've lost track of who I really am, and that my actual self is floating somewhere above me like a balloon" (Female, 22)
- **Delayed/questioned diagnoses** due to appearing "too normal"
- **Unmet support needs**

### Miller, Rees & Pearson (2021) -- "Masking Is Life"

**Title:** "'Masking Is Life': Experiences of Masking in Autistic and Nonautistic Adults"
**Journal:** Autism in Adulthood, 3(4), 330-338
**DOI:** 10.1089/aut.2020.0083
**PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36601640/
**PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8992921/

**Sample:** 238 participants providing qualitative data (from 461 recruits): 144 autistic (101F, 28M, 15NB; mean age 36.3), 49 neurodivergent without autism diagnosis (42F, 4M, 3NB; mean age 33.9), 45 neurotypical (38F, 3M, 4NB; mean age 37.3)

**Methodology:** Inductive thematic analysis using semantic coding at critical realist paradigm; online survey with open-ended prompt

**Seven themes identified:**
1. External manifestations -- mirroring facial expressions, vocal patterns, dress
2. **Identity loss** -- internal disconnection from authentic self
3. **Resource drain** -- emotional and physical exhaustion
4. Neurotype nuances -- autism-specific sensory suppression and stimming inhibition
5. External consequences -- invalidation and relationship disconnect
6. External influences -- stigma avoidance and social pressure
7. Time -- developmental trajectory and unconscious masking

**Key identity loss findings:**
- All three groups reported feeling disconnected from their true selves
- "I have a lower sense of self than most seem to. I feel foreign to myself" (NT, age 23)
- "I feel as though I am wearing a different person. I don't know where I went" (NT woman, 48)
- "Life is masking, masking is life" (Autistic woman, 35)
- Some participants camouflaged so extensively that it eroded their sense of self, causing them to question their autistic diagnosis and identity
- Others reported difficulty "dropping the mask" as they could no longer differentiate which aspects of themselves were real vs. part of the mask

**Autism-specific findings:**
- Suicidality linked to masking (only in autistic group)
- Dangerous coping mechanisms (substance use, restricted eating)
- Sensory suppression as a masking component

### Pearson & Rose (2021) -- Conceptual Analysis

**Title:** "A Conceptual Analysis of Autistic Masking: Understanding the Narrative of Stigma and the Illusion of Choice"
**Journal:** Autism in Adulthood, 3(1), 52-60
**DOI:** 10.1089/aut.2020.0043
**PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36601266/

This conceptual paper argued that masking involves having to suppress aspects of one's identity and seeing oneself through the lens of another, reflecting broader identity struggles studied in marginalized communities. The nature of masking as identity suppression was framed as systematic rather than voluntary.

### Broader Evidence on Identity

A meta-ethnography by Field et al. (2024) synthesized 13 qualitative studies and found that **19 studies** reported how camouflaging led to identity conflict, confusion, and loss. Autistic and non-autistic participants described how camouflaging often resulted in feelings of inauthenticity and disconnectedness from one's true self.

**Source:** Field, S.L., Williams, M.O., Jones, C.R.G., & Fox, J.R.E. (2024). "A meta-ethnography of autistic people's experiences of social camouflaging and its relationship with mental health." Autism. DOI: 10.1177/13623613231223036. PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11134975/

## 5. Executive Function Depletion and Cognitive Load

### Cognitive Mechanisms of Camouflaging

Autistic masking involves intense cognitive multitasking -- simultaneously attempting to focus on conversations, formulate thoughts, analyze others' speech, gauge timing, evaluate group dynamics, and manage internal distress signals. This makes it fundamentally an executive function challenge.

### Livingston et al. (2019) -- Compensatory Strategies

**Title:** "Compensatory Strategies Below the Behavioural Surface in Autism: A Qualitative Study"
**Journal:** The Lancet Psychiatry, 6(9), 766-777
**DOI:** See https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanpsy/article/PIIS2215-0366(19)30224-X/fulltext

Compensation refers to "the use of alternative cognitive routes to demonstrate a less autistic behavioural presentation despite persisting autism-related difficulties or differences in cognition."

Three types of compensatory strategies identified:
1. **Masking** -- increasing/dampening pre-existing social behaviors to hide autistic characteristics fairly superficially
2. **Shallow compensation** -- circumventing social cognition using learned "rules" instead
3. **Deep compensation** -- finding alternative ways to actually emulate good social-cognitive ability

### Livingston et al. (2020) -- Quantifying Compensation

**Title:** "Quantifying Compensatory Strategies in Adults with and Without Diagnosed Autism"
**Sample:** 117 adults (58 with autism, 59 without)

**Key findings:**
- Higher compensation scores (greater repertoire of strategies) were associated with having an autism diagnosis, more autistic traits, and higher education level
- IQ and executive functioning have been hypothesized as supporting camouflaging via the facilitation of compensatory cognitive strategies
- Greater camouflaging (discrepancy method) was related to higher Full-Scale and Verbal IQ
- Data indicate a link between compensation and anxiety, depression, and suicidal ideation, potentially due to the difficulty in successful compensation affecting self-esteem and wellbeing

### Cognitive Depletion Model

The research redefines masking from a social difficulty to a **complex executive function challenge** occurring in social situations. Key aspects:

- Masking exhaustion represents the **cumulative fatigue and depletion of cognitive resources** from sustained effort to suppress/conceal autistic traits
- Loss of skills in autistic individuals represents a critical manifestation of **depleted functional capacity**
- The process is significantly more labor-intensive and cognitively demanding for autistic people compared to universal human social masking behaviors
- Chronic cognitive overload contributes to the development of autistic burnout

## 6. Chronic Stress and Physiological Impacts

### Cortisol Dysregulation in Autism

#### Gao et al. (2022) -- Meta-Analysis

**Title:** "Alteration of Peripheral Cortisol and Autism Spectrum Disorder: A Meta-Analysis"
**Journal:** Frontiers in Psychiatry, 13, 928188
**DOI:** 10.3389/fpsyt.2022.928188
**PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9334910/

**Sample:** 12 studies; 375 ASD patients, 335 controls

**Key findings:**
- Overall Hedges' g = -0.278 (95% CI: -1.026 to 0.470, p = 0.467) -- no significant overall difference due to substantial heterogeneity (I² = 95.198%)
- **After removing 3 outlier studies (9 remaining):** Hedges' g = 0.389 (95% CI: 0.190 to 0.588, p < .001) with reduced heterogeneity (I² = 33.897%) -- **significantly elevated cortisol in ASD**
- Serum samples (n=7): Hedges' g = -0.340; Plasma (n=5): Hedges' g = -0.192
- Age, gender proportion, sample size, and publication year did not significantly moderate

#### Ilen et al. (2024) -- Diurnal Cortisol in Autistic Youth

**Title:** "Diurnal Cortisol Profiles in Autistic Adolescents and Young Adults"
**Journal:** Autism Research
**DOI:** 10.1002/aur.3184

**Sample:** 48 autistic participants (mean age 17.6) and 51 non-autistic participants (mean age 18.4); salivary cortisol collected 6 times/day for 2 days

**Key findings:**
- Autistic participants showed **higher total cortisol output** (AUCG) during the day
- Similar diurnal cortisol slopes and cortisol awakening responses between groups
- More severe **social difficulties** associated with **flatter diurnal cortisol slope** in autistic group
- Cortisol alterations associated with self-reported mental health symptoms, especially in autistic females
- Autism-related characteristics such as social difficulties may contribute to high perceived stress and increased exposure to stressful events, leading to **HPA-axis dysfunction** and vulnerability to mental health difficulties

### Physical Health Consequences

#### Grant et al. (2022) -- Central Sensitivity Syndromes

**Title:** "Autism and Chronic Ill Health: An Observational Study of Symptoms and Diagnoses of Central Sensitivity Syndromes in Autistic Adults"
**Journal:** Molecular Autism, 13, 7
**DOI:** 10.1186/s13229-022-00486-6
**PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8842858/

**Sample:** 973 autistic adults (410 men, 563 women); mean age 44.6 years; Netherlands Autism Register

**Key findings:**
- **21% reported one or more CSS diagnosis** (fibromyalgia, ME/CFS, IBS, RLS, TMJD)
- **60% scored at or above the clinical cutoff** on the Central Sensitization Inventory (CSI score >= 40)
- Autistic participants without formal CSS diagnoses still scored 40.6 on CSI -- substantially higher than non-CSS chronic pain controls (30.9)
- Autistic women significantly over-represented in both diagnosed CSS and high symptom groups
- **Sensory sensitivity and anxiety fully mediated** the relationship between autistic traits and CSS symptoms, accounting for **50.9% of variance** in CSI scores
- Most commonly reported somatic symptoms: fatigue (72.8%) and menstrual problems (61.4% of females)
- Autistic people have higher rates of IBS, fibromyalgia, chronic fatigue syndrome, temporomandibular joint syndrome, migraine, and tinnitus

#### Physiological Stress Cascade from Masking

Research describes the following pathway:
- Sustained masking activates chronic stress response via the HPA axis
- Prolonged psychological distress contributes to inflammation, fatigue, and weakened immune response
- Physical illness exacerbates emotional strain, creating a feedback loop
- Common stress-related symptoms include headaches, muscle tension, stomach issues, and insomnia
- Constant "fight-or-flight" state from performing socially leads to physical exhaustion

### Scheeren et al. (2025) -- Ecological Momentary Assessment

**Title:** "Masking, Social Context and Perceived Stress in Autistic Adults: An Ecological Momentary Assessment Study"
**Journal:** Autism
**DOI:** 10.1177/13623613251353358
**PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40635406/

**Sample:** 87 autistic individuals (58 females; mean age 48; range 17-68)

**Methodology:** Smartphone-based ecological momentary assessment measuring real-time associations between social context, masking, and perceived stress

**Key findings:**
- Participants reported masking **significantly less when alone** vs. when others were present
- Masked **significantly more with non-autistic others** vs. autistic others
- **More masking was associated with concurrent higher levels of perceived stress**
- Autistic adults reported they could be more themselves among autistic peers
- Reduced masking associated with reduced stress
- Provides ecological validity to the stressful impact of masking in daily life

## 7. Psychological Distress Outcomes

### Loneliness Despite Social Competence

Research consistently shows that camouflaging can paradoxically increase loneliness:

- Masking creates relationships "based on deception" that increase loneliness even when appearing socially successful (Hull et al., 2017)
- **Assimilation** (behavioral strategies to blend in) is associated with identity loss, loneliness, and heightened depression and anxiety
- Loneliness is a "particularly strong predictor" of camouflaging in diagnosed autistic adults
- Loneliness **mediated the relation** between being autistic and having poorer mental health (Botha & Frost, 2020)

### Minority Stress Framework Applied to Autism

#### Botha & Frost (2020)

**Title:** "Extending the Minority Stress Model to Understand Mental Health Problems Experienced by the Autistic Population"
**Journal:** Society and Mental Health, 10(1), 20-34
**DOI:** 10.1177/2156869318804297

**Sample:** 111 high-functioning autistic individuals

**Key findings:**
- Autistic individuals constitute an **identity-based minority** exposed to excess social stress from disadvantaged and stigmatized social status
- Three minority stressors identified: **everyday discrimination**, **internalized stigma**, and **concealment**
- All three significantly predicted poorer mental health, **even after controlling for general stress exposure**
- Concealment (analogous to masking) was identified as a proximal minority stressor
- The model provides an alternative framework for understanding mental health disparities beyond biological/deficit-based perspectives
- Parallels drawn to minority stress research in LGBTQ+ populations

### Perry et al. (2022) -- Stigma and Camouflaging

Higher perceived autism stigma predicted higher levels of self-reported camouflaging in autistic adults, after controlling for age, age at diagnosis, gender, and autistic traits. While autism-related stigma was associated with lower wellbeing, this was not mediated by camouflaging, suggesting that stigma's impact on wellbeing operates through additional pathways beyond camouflaging alone.

### Bradley et al. (2021) -- Time Spent Camouflaging

**Title:** "Autistic Adults' Experiences of Camouflaging and Its Perceived Impact on Mental Health"
**Source:** https://docs.autismresearchcentre.com/papers/2021_Bradley_Autistic-adults'-experiences-of-camouflaging.pdf

Found that **time spent camouflaging** is what seems to be most damaging for participants' mental health. The main reason reported for needing to spend so much time camouflaging is **society's lack of awareness and acceptance of autism**.

### Van der Putten et al. (2025) -- Cause vs. Consequence (Longitudinal)

**Title:** "Camouflaging in Autism: A Cause or a Consequence of Mental Health Difficulties?"
**Journal:** Autism
**DOI:** 10.1177/13623613251347104

**Key findings -- longitudinal data:**
- Cross-sectional: camouflaging was associated with baseline mental health difficulties (consistent with previous studies)
- **Longitudinal (2-year follow-up):** Higher baseline camouflaging was linked to a **small but significant decrease** in mental health difficulties over time -- opposite to what was expected
- The association between camouflaging and mental health **differs in strength across subgroups** of autistic adults
- Suggests the relationship may be more nuanced than simple causation, and some individuals may develop more effective coping over time while subgroups remain highly vulnerable

### Cook et al. (2021) -- Systematic Review

**Title:** "Camouflaging in Autism: A Systematic Review"
**Journal:** Clinical Psychology Review, 89, 102080
**DOI:** See https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0272735821001239
**PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34563942/

**Sample:** 29 studies reviewed quantifying camouflaging in children and adults

**Three preliminary findings:**
1. Adults with more self-reported autistic traits report greater engagement in camouflaging
2. Sex and gender differences exist in camouflaging
3. Higher self-reported camouflaging is associated with worse mental health outcomes
