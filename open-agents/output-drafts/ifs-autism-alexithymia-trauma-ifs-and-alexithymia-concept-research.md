# Concept Research: IFS and Alexithymia — The Interoception Problem

## Overview

Internal Family Systems is a method that asks people to **find** parts in the body, **focus** on them, **flesh** them out (often visually), and **feel toward** them with curiosity. Three of those four core access steps lean directly on interoceptive perception — the felt sense of what is happening inside one's own body. Alexithymia, which is present in roughly half of autistic adults and a non-trivial minority of non-autistic adults, disrupts exactly that channel.

The literature on alexithymia + IFS specifically is thin. There is no peer-reviewed empirical study of IFS modified for alexithymic clients. What exists is a body of clinician writing (IFS Telehealth Collective; Sarah Bergenfield; IFS with Sanni; Emma at IFS & Neurodiversity Substack), an adjacent and longer-running literature in Gendlin's Focusing tradition (which faced the same problem starting in the 1970s, with adaptations by Jan Winhall and others), the experimental interoception literature (Shah/Bird/Catmur, Garfinkel, Khalsa), and the expressive-writing tradition (Pennebaker), which has produced mixed but interesting findings on alexithymia outcomes.

This research note maps the structural mismatch between IFS protocol and alexithymic perception, surfaces the named clinicians who have written usable adaptations, and lays out three positions on the (a) poor-fit / (b) slow-fit-with-training / (c) structural-modification question. The literature, where it speaks, leans toward (c) with cautious elements of (b).

## Core Components

### Component 1: Alexithymia (the construct)

- **Purpose**: A trait-level pattern of difficulty processing one's own emotional states.
- **Operationalization**: Most often the Toronto Alexithymia Scale (TAS-20), which produces a total score plus three subscales:
  - **DIF** — Difficulty Identifying Feelings (7 items): trouble distinguishing emotional states from each other and from bodily arousal
  - **DDF** — Difficulty Describing Feelings (5 items): inability to put feelings into words once identified
  - **EOT** — Externally Oriented Thinking (8 items): a thinking style that prefers concrete external detail to inner reflection (this subscale has weaker psychometric properties than the others and is sometimes excluded)
- **Inputs**: Emotional events; bodily arousal; relational signals
- **Outputs**: Reduced emotional differentiation; difficulty naming; reliance on cognitive/external cues to infer state
- **Relationships**: Strongly correlated with reduced interoceptive accuracy (Brewer/Bird group meta-analytic claim — the Royal Society Open Science 2016 paper "Alexithymia: a general deficit of interoception"). High comorbidity with autism (~50%), but also present in PTSD, eating disorders, somatic-symptom presentations, and a baseline ~5% of neurotypicals.

### Component 2: Interoception (the perceptual channel)

- **Purpose**: Sensing internal bodily state — heartbeat, breath, hunger, thirst, gut, temperature, fatigue, arousal.
- **Three dimensions** (Garfinkel, Seth, Suzuki, Critchley 2015 *Biological Psychology*; clarified in Garfinkel & Critchley 2016):
  - **Interoceptive accuracy** — objective performance on tasks like heartbeat counting (how close are you to truth?)
  - **Interoceptive sensibility** — self-reported tendency to attend to internal signals (questionnaire-based)
  - **Interoceptive awareness** — metacognitive: how well your confidence tracks your accuracy
- **Why this matters for IFS**: A person can have low accuracy with normal sensibility (they think they notice things, but they don't pick up the signal accurately), or high sensibility but disorganized awareness, or any other combination. "I can't feel into my body" is several different problems. Most studies find alexithymic people show reduced accuracy specifically; sensibility is more variable.
- **Neural correlates**: Anterior insula damage produces acquired alexithymia (Hogeveen et al. 2016 *Neuropsychologia*); meta-analyses find reduced gray matter in the AI in alexithymic samples; the anterior cingulate cortex is also implicated in conscious access to interoceptive signal.

### Component 3: The Brewer/Shah/Bird finding (the load-bearing empirical anchor)

- **Citation (corrected from exploration draft)**: Shah P, Hall R, Catmur C, Bird G (2016). "Alexithymia, not autism, is associated with impaired interoception." *Cortex*, 81, 215–220.
- **What the study did**: Two heartbeat-detection experiments comparing autistic and non-autistic adults stratified by alexithymia level.
- **What it found**: Reduced interoceptive accuracy tracked alexithymia, not autism. When you statistically remove alexithymia, autism alone does not predict the impairment.
- **What it implies for IFS**: The interoception problem is not "an autism problem." It is an alexithymia problem. About half of autistic adults have it, but autistic adults without elevated alexithymia have roughly typical interoception, and non-autistic alexithymic adults have the same disruption.
- **Caveat to the caveat**: The heartbeat-detection paradigm itself has been critiqued for potential confounds (Zamariola et al. 2018; Desmedt et al. 2020). The robust finding is that *self-reported* interoception aligns with alexithymia very tightly across measures; the *behavioral* heartbeat task has methodological controversy. The clinical implication still holds.

### Component 4: IFS access channels

IFS, in practice, can be entered through several modalities. The standard protocol assumes interoceptive entry, but the manual permits alternatives:

- **Interoceptive access** — felt-sense, "where in your body" (the default in most IFS materials)
- **Visual access** — seeing a part as a figure, color, or image (separable from interoception; relies on mental imagery, which aphantasia disrupts independently)
- **Cognitive access** — "hearing" a part's voice in mind, or recognizing it as a thought-cluster or belief-pattern (described as "in-sight" without somatic anchor)
- **Externalized access** — representing parts on paper, as figurines, in sand tray, in journaling dialogue, or in "two-chair" enactment

The IFS Institute typically frames externalized and direct-access methods as workarounds. The clinical writing reviewed here reframes them as primary modes for alexithymic and aphantasic clients — not detours, but the actual route in.

### Component 5: Direct access (the IFS protocol's escape hatch)

- **Purpose**: A protocol move where the therapist speaks directly with a part (often through the client's voice), rather than asking the client's Self to speak with the part internally.
- **Two variants**:
  - **Explicit direct access** — the therapist names parts and speaks to them directly: "Can I talk to the part that's worried right now?"
  - **Implicit direct access** — the therapist works with parts without ever naming the framework or using the words "Self" or "parts" (Frank Anderson, *Transcending Trauma*, 2021)
- **Why it matters here**: Direct access is the protocol's existing accommodation for clients who can't easily access Self-to-part dialogue internally. For low-interoception clients, it may be the default rather than the fallback.

## How It Works (the structural mismatch)

### Step 1: The 6 Fs and their interoception load

The 6 Fs are the standard IFS sequence for accessing a part. Each step has a different relationship to interoceptive perception:

| Step | What happens | Interoception load |
|------|--------------|---------------------|
| **Find** | Locate the part — typically "where in your body do you notice this?" | **High** — the prompt itself names the body |
| **Focus** | Sustain attention on it | **High** for somatic targets; medium for cognitive/visual targets |
| **Flesh out** | Build out detail — visual, somatic, narrative | **Medium** — visualization can substitute, but standard prompts mix modalities |
| **Feel toward** | Notice your stance toward the part — curious? annoyed? afraid? | **High** — this is the gateway to Self-energy detection; the question itself is interoceptive |
| **beFriend** | Build relationship through dialogue | **Low** — relational/cognitive |
| **Fears** | Ask what the part is afraid would happen if it stopped | **Low** — primarily cognitive/narrative |

The pattern is clean: **the front half of the protocol is interoception-heavy; the back half is dialogic and largely independent.** A person with low interoceptive access faces the largest barrier in the first three steps.

### Step 2: What clinicians who work with alexithymic clients actually do

The IFS Telehealth Collective's published guidance ("Supporting Your Neurotype with Internal Family Systems") is the most concrete public statement from an IFS-aligned clinical group:

- They explicitly adapt for "low interoception, alexithymia, aphantasia, and sensory sensitivities."
- They recommend **starting with externalized parts** (objects, drawings, figurines) and only moving toward somatic noticing if and when it emerges naturally.
- They reframe alexithymia as something a client has been criticized for, rather than a deficit the therapy must correct — and explicitly include burden-tending around past invalidation as part of the work.
- They describe sessions that may include "mindfulness, imagery, identifying and feeling emotions, cognitive processing, verbal processing, sensing into your body, externalizing through creating art or using objects, and other outlets" — explicitly menu-style rather than protocol-fixed.

**Sarah Bergenfield** (Level 3 IFS practitioner, autism-specialized, somatic psychologist; co-author with Martha Sweezy of *Wired to Feel: Autism as a Condition of Sensory Surplus*, 2025) writes from the position that autistic people experience emotions as *sensory surplus* rather than absence — reframing the alexithymic profile not as missing data but as overwhelming or unparseable data. Her work integrates predictive processing, polyvagal theory, and IFS, with an emphasis on adapting interoceptive language to the client's actual perceptual profile.

**IFS with Sanni** (Sanni Lehtinen, online practitioner serving European/Asian clients) writes about working with alexithymia as a feature of the system rather than a bug. Her stated approach: "you do not need to be visual to connect with your parts" — parts can show up as thoughts, energy patterns, or external behaviors as readily as as body sensations or images.

**Emma at IFS & Neurodiversity Substack** writes a series including "Can Self be Autistic?" engaging directly with whether the IFS canonical Self-qualities map onto autistic phenomenology. Her resources page collates adaptations from across the autistic IFS-clinician community.

The recurring concrete adaptations across these writers:
- **Externalized representation** — cards, figurines, sand tray, drawings, written dialogue
- **Cognitive access as primary** — "hearing in mind," recognizing parts as thought-patterns or belief-clusters
- **Permission not to feel into the body** — explicitly removing the requirement
- **Direct access from the outset** rather than as a fallback
- **Reframing somatic surplus** — recognizing that what looks like "no signal" may be "too much signal that can't be parsed"
- **Slowing the pace** — checking that "no" answers actually mean "no" rather than "I can't tell"

### Step 3: The Gendlin Focusing parallel — the field that's been wrestling with this longer

Eugene Gendlin developed Focusing in the 1960s–70s as an explicit attempt to teach the "felt sense" — Gendlin's term for the unclear, pre-verbal bodily sense of a situation that, when attended to, contains the seeds of its own resolution. Focusing directly anticipates the alexithymia problem:

- Gendlin's research at the University of Chicago found that successful psychotherapy clients across orientations did *something* characteristic — they paused, attended inward, and let words emerge from a bodily sense rather than from cognition. Unsuccessful clients did not.
- The implication: people who can't access the felt sense were systematically failing in conventional therapy.
- Focusing's training material was explicitly designed to teach the felt-sense skill, on the assumption it could be built.

The empirical and clinical literature on Focusing + alexithymia is mixed:
- Some clients build felt-sense access over time with patient training.
- Some never do — and pushing harder produces dissociation or somatic dysregulation rather than emergence of the signal.
- Modern Focusing-oriented therapists (notably **Jan Winhall**'s *Felt Sense Polyvagal Model*, 2021/2024 — Routledge and Norton books) have integrated polyvagal theory to make explicit what Gendlin left implicit: if the autonomic state is collapsed (dorsal vagal) or in fight/flight (sympathetic), the felt sense is structurally inaccessible. The intervention is therefore not "feel harder" but "build autonomic safety first."
- This polyvagal-informed view recasts alexithymia in some cases as a *protective* shutdown (especially in trauma-origin alexithymia) rather than a pure perceptual deficit. The clinical implication: forcing inward attention before regulating state can re-traumatize.

### Step 4: Can interoceptive capacity be built? An honest answer

The literature here is genuinely mixed:

**Evidence that body-based mindfulness can reduce alexithymia scores**:
- Norman et al. 2019 systematic review — small-to-medium effect of mindfulness-based interventions on alexithymia.
- Khalsa et al. 2025 meta-analysis (2,191 participants, 29 RCTs) — small-to-medium positive effect of mindfulness on self-reported interoception.
- Multiple trials of body-scan meditation show reductions in TAS-20 scores.

**Evidence the relationship is non-linear**:
- Murphy et al. 2020 *Frontiers in Psychiatry* — found a *quadratic* relationship between alexithymia and interoceptive accuracy. Both very high and very low interoceptive accuracy correlate with elevated alexithymia. Implication: more interoceptive signal is not always better — a person could become more accurate at registering signals they then can't parse.
- Pilot mindfulness intervention in the same paper produced mixed effects.

**Cautions about harm**:
- The mindfulness-for-alexithymia literature explicitly notes risk: "improving awareness and emotional release can be overwhelming for patients with psychiatric disorders" (Norman et al.).
- Body scan practice in trauma populations has documented adverse-event rates (Lindahl et al. 2017, Britton et al. 2021 on meditation-related difficulties).
- For someone whose externalized processing already works, the question "should I push to feel more?" has a non-trivial cost-benefit profile.

**The honest synthesis**: Interoceptive capacity is partially trainable for some people some of the time, but (a) it isn't always desirable to build, (b) pushing it without state-regulation foundations can trigger dysregulation, and (c) for someone whose externalized access is functional, the case for retraining is unclear.

### Step 5: When alexithymia is contraindication vs. adaptation challenge

Few sources frame alexithymia as an absolute contraindication for IFS. The more common framing, when negative outcomes are flagged:

- **Severe alexithymia + complex trauma** combined with insistent body-focused prompts can produce flooding or dissociation — not because IFS itself is wrong, but because the protocol's interoceptive entry-point pushes into a dysregulated channel.
- **"Performed parts work"** — a client who has learned what answer the protocol expects can produce the right shapes (locating a part "in my chest," feeling "curious") without the internal access that gives the protocol its therapeutic action. The therapy becomes a script. Several of the autistic IFS clinicians flag this risk explicitly.
- **The push to "feel harder"** — when a therapist persistently returns to body-noticing prompts despite the client's accurate report that nothing is accessible there, the client's available channels (cognitive, written, externalized) are never developed.

The contraindication, when it exists, is therefore about **mismatch and persistence in mismatch**, not about alexithymia per se. With structural adaptation, IFS appears to work. Without it, the protocol can be actively counterproductive.

### Step 6: Practical phrasings — substitutions for interoception-heavy prompts

Drawn from the IFS Telehealth Collective, IFS with Sanni, Bergenfield, and the externalization literature:

| Standard prompt | Adapted prompt |
|------|------|
| "Where do you notice this part in your body?" | "How would you draw this part? What shape, color, or image fits? Or — if it had a voice in your mind, what would it sound like?" |
| "How do you feel toward this part?" | "If you wrote this part a letter, what would the tone be? Or — what's your stance toward it logically — protective, suspicious, friendly?" |
| "What's it like to be with this part?" | "What does this part do in your behavior? What patterns do you notice when this part is active?" |
| "Take a breath and notice what comes up" | "Take a moment and write down what's most present. Or — describe what you'd be observing if you were watching yourself from outside." |
| "Let's invite the part to share more" | "Let's set up a written dialogue. You write its lines, then write back to it." |

The principle: **swap the access modality without swapping the underlying move.** Find, Focus, Flesh out, Feel toward, beFriend, and Fears are still happening — they're just being routed through writing, drawing, behavioral observation, or cognitive recognition rather than felt sense.

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: Standard protocol fails on the first F
A therapist asks "where do you notice this part in your body?" The client says "I don't really feel anything in my body about this." The therapist tries again with a longer pause. The client says "I think it might be in my chest? Maybe?" — but the answer is performed, not perceived. The session proceeds with the therapist treating "chest" as data. The protocol runs, but the client's actual access to the part is missing. The internal work is not happening.

### Example 2: Externalized access produces real movement
The same client, asked to draw the part — or to choose a figurine, or to write a paragraph in the part's voice — produces concrete output that they recognize. They can now answer "what's its stance?" "what does it want?" "what is it afraid of?" because the externalized representation is something they can look at and reason about. The work proceeds via cognitive and visual channels. The therapist never returns to the body-noticing prompt for this part.

### Example 3: Direct access from the outset
Rather than working with the client's Self-to-part inner dialogue, the therapist uses explicit direct access from session one: "Can I talk with the part that's worried right now? — Hi worried part, can you tell me what you're afraid of?" The client speaks for the part, and through the part, without ever needing to first interoceptively locate it. This is entirely within sanctioned IFS protocol; the only departure is that it is the default rather than the fallback.

### Example 4: Building interoception cautiously, in someone for whom it might emerge
A client has elevated TAS-20 and complex trauma. The therapist does not push body-noticing. They begin with externalized work, build autonomic safety (Winhall-style), and only after months — when the client spontaneously notices a sensation — do they invite gentle interoceptive language as one channel among several. Capacity emerges slowly, in people for whom it can; it is never forced.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: "Interoception can be trained, full stop, so alexithymic clients will catch up to standard protocol over time."
  - **Reality**: For some, yes — slowly. For others, the felt-sense channel is structurally inaccessible (or unsafe to access for trauma reasons). The literature on body-scan harms is real. A blanket "you'll get there with practice" is not supported.

- **Misconception**: "Direct access and externalized parts work are inferior workarounds."
  - **Reality**: They are sanctioned IFS protocol moves. The literature on autistic and alexithymic IFS describes them as primary modes, not concessions. Frank Anderson's *Transcending Trauma* describes implicit direct access as a routine clinical practice.

- **Misconception**: "Alexithymia is an autism-only issue."
  - **Reality**: Roughly half of autistic adults are alexithymic; the other half have roughly typical interoception. Non-autistic populations (PTSD, eating disorders, somatic-symptom presentations, ~5% of neurotypicals) also show alexithymia. The Brewer/Bird finding establishes that alexithymia (not autism) is what drives the interoception disruption.

- **Misconception**: "If you don't 'feel' the part, you're not really doing IFS."
  - **Reality**: IFS protocol explicitly accommodates non-felt-sense access through direct access. Cognitive recognition of parts (as belief-clusters, voices in mind, behavior patterns) is a valid access route documented across the autistic-IFS-clinician writing.

- **Misconception**: "The Brewer paper" is the citation.
  - **Reality**: The lead author of the 2016 *Cortex* paper is **Punit Shah**, with Geoffrey Bird as senior author. The cited paper is Shah, Hall, Catmur, Bird (2016). The Brewer/Bird group did related interoception work, but the load-bearing autism-vs-alexithymia paper is Shah et al.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **Trauma-origin alexithymia**: When alexithymia is a protective shutdown rather than a pure perceptual deficit, Winhall-style polyvagal-informed pacing (regulating state before pursuing felt sense) is the indicated path. Pushing inward attention before the autonomic system is regulated risks re-traumatization.
- **Aphantasia comorbid with alexithymia**: Visual access (one of the standard substitutes) is also unavailable. Externalized and cognitive channels are the remaining viable routes. Aphantasia gets full treatment in Cat 7; this category notes the compounding.
- **Quadratic interoception relationship**: Some clients have elevated alexithymia *with* normal or even high interoceptive accuracy — they pick up signals, they just can't parse them. For these clients, the issue is conceptual/labeling, not perceptual; affect-labeling work and granularity-building (Barrett tradition) may help where body-scan training would not.

## Related Concepts

- **Aphantasia** (Cat 7): Absence of voluntary visual imagery. Compounds the interoception problem when comorbid with alexithymia by removing the visual substitute. ~2–4% of the general population.
- **Self-led journaling and externalized parts work** (Cat 10): The full treatment of writing-led IFS. This category notes externalized access as one option; Cat 10 details the practice.
- **Mechanics — the 6 Fs** (Cat 2): The protocol whose interoception-dependence is examined here. Cat 2 includes a brief interoception-matrix; this category is the deep treatment.
- **Complex trauma and IFS** (Cat 8): Alexithymia is comorbid with complex trauma; the Winhall-style polyvagal pacing intersects with both.
- **Affect labeling and emotional granularity** (Barrett, Lieberman): A different route into emotional-processing work that does not require interoceptive accuracy. May help in cases of "high accuracy, low parsing" alexithymia.

## Research Gaps

- **No peer-reviewed empirical study of IFS adapted for alexithymic clients exists.** All clinical writing on this topic is from clinician blogs, training materials, and trade books. The evidence base for the adaptations themselves is clinical-experiential, not empirical.
- **Direct comparison of access modalities in IFS** — interoceptive vs. cognitive vs. visual vs. externalized — has not been done. Whether outcomes are equivalent across channels is unknown.
- **The "performed parts work" risk** is named in clinical writing but has not been operationalized or measured. It is therefore conjectural at present, however clinically plausible.
- **Long-term outcomes for alexithymic clients in IFS** — whether interoceptive capacity actually builds with sustained IFS work, whether externalized methods produce equivalent therapeutic results, whether there are unique adverse events — is open territory.
- **Integration of IFS with the Focusing-tradition adaptations (Winhall, FOT-trained therapists)** is happening clinically but has not been documented in a research-quality way.
