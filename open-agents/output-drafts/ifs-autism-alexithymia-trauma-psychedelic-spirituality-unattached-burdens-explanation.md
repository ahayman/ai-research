# Psychedelic-Assisted IFS, Spirituality, and the "Unattached Burdens" Turn

## Why this category exists at this point in the deep dive

By 2026, the answer to "what is IFS?" depends substantially on which practitioner, training, or text you reach. The clinical-modality version of IFS — what *No Bad Parts* (2021) lays out, what most "IFS-Informed" therapists practice, and what the small RCT base addresses — is a structured psychotherapy with a defined parts taxonomy and procedural sequence. The version Schwartz himself is currently teaching at Sounds True (the ten-week course *The Spirituality of Internal Family Systems*), endorsing in his foreword to Robert Falconer's 2023 book *The Others Within Us: Internal Family Systems, Porous Mind, and Spirit Possession*, and articulating on podcasts (Tim Ferriss, Edge of Mind, Living Medicine Institute, Psychedelics Today), is an explicitly transpersonal practice that frames Self as connected to "the compassionate, unifying consciousness known as the Divine, True Nature, or God across the world's wisdom traditions" and that treats some clinical material as originating from non-personal — discarnate, transpersonal, or "guide" — sources.

These are not separate movements with separate vocabulary. They use the same Self/parts/burden language, often the same training certifications, and many of the same authors. A reader who picks up *No Bad Parts* and a reader who takes *The Spirituality of IFS* will both think they are doing "IFS." Reading order in this deep dive places this category after the critiques baseline because what's covered here cannot be evaluated without the falsifiability and commercial-dynamics framings already in hand. It comes before the intersection block because what "IFS" *is* in 2026 determines what it means to consider it as a clinical option for an autistic / alexithymic / complex-trauma reader.

This page covers four developments that reinforce each other: psychedelic-assisted IFS; the unattached-burdens doctrine; Schwartz's spiritual reframing; and legacy / generational unburdening. It steel-mans what intelligent practitioners actually claim, then concretizes the safety, falsifiability, and iatrogenic-harm concerns. The reader can decide what to do with both registers.

## Where this places on the spectrum

<!-- DIAGRAM: ifs-spectrum -->

A useful first move is to see IFS as occupying a spectrum rather than a single position. Three positions are practically meaningful:

**Clinical-modality IFS** treats parts and Self as useful clinical metaphors — functional descriptions of mental life. The goal is symptom reduction, trauma processing, improved daily functioning. Evidence is the small published RCT/pilot literature (Shadick 2013 RA RCT; Haddock 2016 depression RCT; the 2025 scoping review's two-RCT count). Metaphysical commitments are minimal.

**Contemplative-practice IFS** treats parts work as a sustained contemplative practice with cross-tradition resonance — Buddhist mindfulness, somatic awareness, contemplative Christianity. The goal is psychotherapy outcomes plus "Self-leadership" as ongoing practice. Metaphysical commitments are soft: "Self has qualities like the divine in many traditions" without specific ontological claim.

**Transpersonal / parapsychological IFS** treats parts as real entities with autonomous voices, some not even from the client's own history. Self is the divine. Spirit guides exist. Psychedelic experience reveals genuine non-physical realities. The goal is healing across personal, ancestral, and possibly past-life dimensions.

All three use the same vocabulary. A reader cannot tell from the words "parts," "Self," "burden," or "unblending" which position the speaker holds. The point of the spectrum is to make registers visible so a reader can choose deliberately rather than drift.

## The empirical context for psychedelic-assisted IFS

The most-cited empirical anchor for IFS-Institute marketing of psychedelic-assisted therapy is **Mithoefer's "Parts-work" finding**: in a Phase 2 dose-response trial of MDMA-assisted psychotherapy for PTSD in 26 veterans, firefighters, and police officers (published *Lancet Psychiatry*, 2018), Mithoefer used a "Parts-work" measure he developed for an internal pilot and found that **awareness of parts emerged in 78% of active-dose MDMA-assisted sessions** without prompting. Therapists rated marked increases in qualities indicating Self-energy in **92% of active-dose sessions** versus 29% of low-dose sessions. The trial manual was non-directive, but allowed therapists to switch to IFS if clients spontaneously generated parts language. Mithoefer has reported clinically that MDMA "facilitates unblending" and often allows a client to "condense or even skip the first 6 steps of IFS" — moving directly to witnessing and unburdening exiles.

This is a real and important phenomenon. It is not an RCT confirmation that "MDMA + IFS" outperforms an alternative framework.

> **Evidence note (validity 9/10)**: The 78% figure is from Mithoefer's Phase 2 pilot Parts-work measure (n=26). It is widely cited in IFS-Institute marketing as "~80%." Phase 3 MAPP1/MAPP2 trials did not replicate the Parts-work measure. Therapist non-blinding to dose creates rating-bias risk for the Self-energy ratings.

The **Phase 3 program (MAPP1, MAPP2)** ran later and was designed differently. MAPP1 (Mitchell et al., *Nature Medicine*, 2021, n=90) showed MDMA-assisted therapy reduced CAPS-5 scores significantly more than placebo-with-therapy (mean change −24.4 vs −13.9; Cohen's d=0.91; 67% of MDMA-AT participants no longer met PTSD criteria vs 32% of placebo-with-therapy). MAPP2 (Mitchell et al., *Nature Medicine*, 2023) confirmed the result. Both trials used a non-directive inner-directed psychotherapy protocol — *not* IFS specifically.

And then, in **August 2024**, the FDA *rejected* the Lykos NDA for MDMA-assisted therapy. The Complete Response Letter cited functional unblinding (more than 90% of the MDMA arm and 75% of the placebo arm correctly guessed their assignment in MAPP2 — a problem when the drug's psychoactive effects are unmistakable), durability concerns, and prior MDMA use among participants. **One day later**, the journal *Psychopharmacology* retracted three earlier Phase 2 papers over "protocol violations amounting to unethical conduct" at the **MP4 study site in Canada**, where an unlicensed therapist had been accused of inappropriate physical contact during dosing, followed by a post-trial sexual relationship and alleged assault by the same therapist. MAPS founder Rick Doblin disagreed with the retractions; one author (Allison Feduccia) agreed.

> **Evidence note**: The empirical status of MDMA-assisted therapy as of April 2026 is: not FDA-approved, three Phase 2 papers retracted, a serious therapist-misconduct case unresolved, IFS-Institute marketing materials largely unchanged. The "MDMA validates IFS" framing predates these events and has not been retrofitted.

**Ketamine-assisted IFS** (KAP-IFS or K-IFS) is the most accessible psychedelic-IFS practice in 2026. Ketamine is FDA-approved as an anesthetic; sub-anesthetic use for depression is off-label. Many state-level ketamine clinics now advertise IFS as their preparation/integration framework. Nick Brüss, the Sapience KIFS Retreat, and the Tucson Counseling Associates KAP Provider Training are widely cited. **There is no published RCT of K-IFS as a discrete intervention.** Existing ketamine-plus-psychotherapy literature uses non-IFS frameworks. K-IFS is widely *practiced*, not yet *validated*.

**Psilocybin-assisted IFS** sits in a similar place. Psilocybin has FDA breakthrough therapy designation (2018 for treatment-resistant depression; 2019 for major depressive disorder) but no marketing approval. Oregon (Measure 109, regulated services since 2023) and Colorado (Proposition 122, healing centers active 2025) offer state-regulated access; Oregon had served roughly 16,000 people legally as of late 2025. Many Oregon and Colorado practitioners use IFS as their integration framework, but no published RCT of psilocybin + IFS as a specific intervention exists.

<!-- DIAGRAM: psychedelic-mapping -->

The conceptual fit is real and worth taking seriously. Psychedelics produce ego softening — a relaxation of the protective parts that, in IFS terms, *blend* with the system and prevent Self-led work. In ketamine, MDMA, and psilocybin sessions, many clients describe spontaneous awareness of inner figures or aspects of self that feel distinct from the everyday "I." The IFS framework gives this experience a structured language. What's contested is not whether the phenomenon happens (it does) but whether the IFS framework specifically — versus, say, ACT, motivational, or non-directive support — is the validated psychotherapy partner for psychedelic medicines. As of April 2026, that question has not been answered by RCT-level evidence.

## The "unattached burdens" doctrine

In 2023, Robert Falconer — a senior IFS practitioner and trainer — published *The Others Within Us: Internal Family Systems, Porous Mind, and Spirit Possession*, with a foreword by Richard Schwartz. The book's central thesis: a class of clinical material — voices, intrusive thoughts, characterological structures clients describe as "not me" — does not respond to ordinary IFS unburdening because, on Falconer's account, it does not originate in the client's own history. Falconer calls these "unattached burdens" (UBs). Other cultures call them "spirit possession," "guides," or analogous categories. The book explicitly reframes a cross-cultural anthropology of possession in IFS vocabulary.

Falconer's framing of the mechanism: UBs "often enter into a person's system during trauma when defenses are weak and the system is vulnerable." The mind is conceptualized as **porous**, not closed. A client may carry a UB without ever knowing — until parts work brings it forward. Chapters 6 and 7 lay out a step-by-step protocol: identify the UB as distinct from the client's own parts; approach with curiosity from Self; engage and negotiate with the UB; help it find what it needs (often: somewhere else to go); release or send the UB on. The vocabulary borrows from anthropology of possession ("eviction") and from IFS ("unburdening").

Schwartz's foreword endorses this work. The key passage: "This book can help many people. Bob has done an impressive amount of research on how the Unattached Burden phenomenon is understood and dealt with around the world and lays out clear guidelines and examples for using IFS to unload them." He frames the book as marking "a landmark for helping those in Western cultures reorient their perspective in meaningful ways in the paradigm shift from physicalism to the primacy of consciousness." He notes some reservations given psychotherapy's history but explicitly endorses the work. This is meaningful endorsement, not arms-length acknowledgment.

<!-- DIAGRAM: burden-taxonomy -->

To see what claim is being made it helps to lay the three categories side by side. **Personal burdens** originate in the client's own history; mechanism is trauma absorption; evidence is the broader clinical trauma literature; status is uncontroversial. **Legacy burdens** (covered in their own section below) originate from family / ancestors / culture; mechanism is some combination of social transmission, internalized cultural messages, and possibly epigenetics; evidence is strong for social transmission and weaker for biological epigenetic transmission in humans; status is a useful clinical metaphor that becomes pseudoscientific if framed as literal biological transmission. **Unattached burdens** originate, on Falconer's account, from transpersonal / discarnate / external sources; mechanism is porous mind plus trauma vulnerability; evidence in peer-reviewed clinical literature is *none*; status is an explicitly transpersonal claim.

Reception within IFS circles has been mixed but substantially favorable. Sympathetic engagement appears in *Mad in America* ("Unattached Burdens: IFS Helps Us Understand What Psychiatry Ignores," September 2023), the Parts & Self book review, and Natalie Deering's *Intuitive Self* podcast. Some IFS-aligned voices express concern about ontological commitments and clinical risk. **Reception outside IFS** has been thin in clinical psychology — there are no published peer-reviewed clinical-psychology engagements with the doctrine that surfaced in standard searches as of April 2026. The most substantial outside engagement has been from the rationalist blogger Scott Alexander (Astral Codex Ten), who treated the book seriously without endorsing its ontology. The clinical-psychology silence is itself notable. It is not active rejection. It is also not tacit agreement.

## Schwartz's spiritual reframings

Schwartz's public framing of IFS has shifted noticeably between roughly 2015 and 2025. The 1995 *Internal Family Systems Therapy* clinical text describes Self in operational, clinical terms — eight C-qualities (curiosity, calm, compassion, clarity, courage, confidence, creativity, connectedness) as therapeutic markers. The 2021 *No Bad Parts* extends this with the "5 Ps" (presence, patience, perspective, persistence, playfulness) and introduces a more spiritual register, but holds the clinical voice. The 2024 Sounds True course **The Spirituality of Internal Family Systems** is explicitly transpersonal. Course marketing describes Self as "more than just a mental state" and discusses "nonphysical guides distinct from inner parts." The course's ten weekly modules cover — verbatim from Sounds True — "the spiritual and energetic dimensions of Self and its relationship to helpful nonphysical guides, shamanic and psychedelic methods, and healing multi-generational and societal burdens." Eight experiential exercises help participants encounter these dimensions.

The drift is clearest in how Schwartz describes Self. In clinical writing: "the seat of compassionate awareness," characterized by 8 Cs and 5 Ps. In transpersonal writing and interviews (Sounds True, Edge of Mind, Living Medicine Institute, Tim Ferriss #492): "the compassionate, unifying consciousness known as the Divine, True Nature, or God across the world's wisdom traditions." The same word — Self — denotes a clinical-functional construct in one register and a transpersonal-metaphysical claim in another.

Schwartz's Sounds True course marketing includes the line that his "clinical research has repeatedly demonstrated undeniable spiritual phenomena that cannot be explained by conventional science." This is not a paraphrase. It is the text Sounds True publishes to promote the course. A reader trying to figure out what Schwartz currently believes about IFS as of 2026 has the founder's own word here.

## Legacy burdens and generational unburdening

A separate but adjacent expansion concerns **legacy burdens** — burdens inherited from family of origin, ancestors, or cultural systems rather than acquired in the client's own life. The IFS-Informed protocol for "legacy unburdening" typically follows: target the legacy burden; unblend; witness; pass back to the source (ancestor, family system, culture); release to elements (light, water, fire, earth, wind); bring forward inherited "gifts" rather than burdens.

The clinical metaphor is defensible. Family-of-origin patterns, internalized cultural messages, and lineage-specific dynamics demonstrably shape adult functioning. Naming "this isn't yours, it was handed to you" is therapeutically useful and not committed to any particular biological mechanism.

The trouble appears when the protocol is presented with a literal-biological framing. IFS-Informed materials frequently invoke epigenetic-trauma research (Yehuda's work with Holocaust survivor offspring) as a scientific scaffold. The honest scientific picture is more constrained. Yehuda & Lehrner's 2018 review in *World Psychiatry* documents intergenerational stress effects in human samples but openly notes methodological limits — small sample sizes (often n=32 or fewer in Holocaust offspring studies), difficulty separating epigenetic from social/environmental transmission, the strongest evidence sitting in animal models. The authors themselves write that "it is not possible to attribute intergenerational effects in humans to a single set of biological or other determinants at this time."

The clinical metaphor "legacy burdens" is good. The literal claim that ancestral material is *biologically inherited via epigenetics* and can be *passed back to ancestors* through ritualized imagery is overstated science. Some IFS-Informed teachers further merge legacy unburdening with soul-retrieval and shamanic practices. At that point the practice has crossed from clinical psychology into spiritual direction. It is a legitimate practice category for those who want it; it should not be marketed as evidence-based clinical psychology.

## Where the model crosses into spiritual direction or parapsychology

The structural ambiguity in IFS has always allowed both clinical-modality and contemplative-practice readings. The 2020–2026 trajectory has tilted toward the second and now in places toward the third. Several markers identify the crossing:

When Self is described as "the compassionate unifying consciousness behind all things" rather than "the seat of compassionate awareness with eight C-qualities" — that is the clinical-to-transpersonal step. When clinical material is treated as originating from non-personal sources ("unattached burdens," "spirit guides") that the protocol "evicts" — that is parapsychology in IFS vocabulary. When healing is claimed to operate across past lives, ancestral lines, and societal systems via ritualized imagery — that is spiritual direction with a psychotherapy interface.

None of these are illegitimate practices in their own categories. They are not validated as clinical psychology. The question is not "is IFS a real thing?" but "which version of IFS is the one in front of you?" And that depends on which book, which training, which practitioner.

<!-- DIAGRAM: version-flowchart -->

A practical schema helps. Question 1: Are they IFS-Trained or IFS-Certified through the Institute? This is a floor for clinical competence; positioning still varies. Question 2: Do they cite *The Others Within Us*, *The Spirituality of IFS*, or shamanic frames? Heavy citation indicates transpersonal-end positioning. Question 3: Do they invoke "unattached burdens," spirit guides, or past lives in their work? Routine invocation marks transpersonal positioning. Question 4: How do they describe Self — as 8 Cs and therapeutic markers (clinical), as 8 Cs with cross-tradition resonance (contemplative), or as divine consciousness (transpersonal)? Question 5: What is the venue — licensed clinical setting with continuing-education credit (clinical/contemplative), or retreat center with mixed audience (often contemplative or transpersonal)?

These markers are not absolute. A practitioner can drift across positions in different settings. A book can be read at different registers by different readers. The point of the schema is informed selection rather than blind drift into a register the reader didn't intend to enter.

## Why mainstream clinical psychology has largely declined to engage

The silence from peer-reviewed clinical psychology around the unattached-burdens doctrine and Schwartz's transpersonal turn is structurally interesting. Several forces compound:

**Falsifiability concerns** are foundational. How would one disprove the existence of unattached burdens? A successful UB-eviction session looks the same regardless of whether the UB was a real discarnate entity, a dissociated fragment, or a clinically useful fiction. The doctrine is not framed in a way that admits empirical refutation. The same critique applies — though more weakly — to "Self" and "parts" in their original clinical-modality form, which is why Cat 5 raised falsifiability as a general IFS concern. The transpersonal expansion sharpens that concern, not softens it.

**Suggestibility under altered states** is a recognized risk constellation in the false-memory literature. Loftus's "lost in the mall" research and successors demonstrate that suggestion plus imagination plus authority can produce confidently held memories of events that never occurred. Approximately 25% of adult subjects in the original study adopted a false childhood memory after suggestion. Extensions push this to roughly one-third for various scenarios. The recovered-memory therapy lawsuits of the 1990s (Ramona v. Isabella, Castlewood Treatment Center, Burgus v. Braun) involved exactly this combination of suggestion, altered states, and authority.

**The recovered-memory parallel** is sharper than abstract risk. Patihis & Pendergrast's 2019 study in *Clinical Psychological Science* surveyed 2,326 U.S. adults. Those whose therapists "discussed the possibility of repressed memories of abuse" were **20 times more likely** to report recovering abuse memories than those whose therapists did not. The "discovered" content of such sessions correlated strongly with what the therapist signaled as discoverable. This is the load-bearing structural parallel: when a therapist's framework signals that "unattached burdens" or "ancestral material" or "past lives" are discoverable categories, the rate at which clients discover them rises sharply — and there is no independent way, after the fact, to know whether what was discovered was pre-existing content or framework-implanted content.

**The Lykos MP4 site case** is a 2024 instance of the failure mode in real time: an unlicensed therapist accused of inappropriate physical contact during MDMA dosing, post-trial sexual relationship, and alleged assault. Three Phase 2 papers were retracted by *Psychopharmacology* over the broader pattern of protocol violations at that site. This is not an abstract risk. It is what the constellation produced once.

**Reputational risk for critical clinicians** suppresses published engagement. A clinician who critiques IFS's spiritual extensions in a peer-reviewed journal incurs the cost of being framed as "closed-minded" or "physicalist." A clinician who ignores the extensions inadvertently signals tacit acceptance via silence. Both registers suppress published critical engagement. The Society for the Advancement of Psychotherapy's 2024 article ("IFS: Exploring Its Problematic Popularity") was a notable but rare exception.

**Iatrogenic risk in vulnerable populations** raises ethical concerns. Implanting transpersonal frameworks in clients who are dissociative, suggestible, or in crisis is structurally similar to recovered-memory-era practices. The IFS Institute names some safeguards (informed consent, pacing, working from Self) but does not formally engage the false-memory literature in its training materials or public statements.

## Implications for safety

<!-- DIAGRAM: risk-constellation -->

The safety picture in psychedelic-assisted IFS rests on three factors that combine into a recognized risk pattern. Factor one: parts framing allows for autonomous voices — a part can speak as "not me," and the framework gives that experience credibility. Factor two: altered states (psychedelics, deep visualization, somatic trance, hypnosis) increase suggestibility and reduce the client's ability to ground "discovered" content against everyday reality testing. Factor three: therapist suggestion — even unintentional — shapes what "emerges." When the therapist's framework includes unattached burdens, ancestral material, or past lives as discoverable categories, those categories are statistically more likely to be discovered in altered-state sessions.

This is the false-memory / confabulation risk pattern. It does not mean psychedelic-assisted IFS is uniformly harmful. It does mean the safeguards must be more robust than for clinical-modality IFS alone:

- **Informed consent that covers psychological risks**, not just pharmacological ones — including the specific risk that altered-state content may be framework-implanted rather than discovered
- **Pre-session screening** for dissociative-disorder history, psychosis history, and suggestibility — not as exclusion criteria automatically, but as factors that raise the bar for safeguards
- **Therapist non-leading stance** — no implanting of content the client did not generate; avoiding "do you sense an unattached burden here?" framing
- **Phenomenological holding before biographical claim** — during integration, "discovered" content should be held as experienced material, not yet treated as historical fact, until the client and therapist can together evaluate what it does and doesn't claim
- **Documentation that distinguishes** phenomenological content (what the client experienced) from claimed historical content (what they claim happened)

For complex-trauma clients, the dissociation risk under psychedelics is real but not categorical. C-PTSD is *not* an automatic exclusion from psychedelic-assisted therapy; many of the strongest therapeutic results have been in this population. The bar is set higher: more careful screening, slower pacing, more integration sessions, and explicit attention to the suggestibility / confabulation risk constellation. Practitioners who cannot articulate these safeguards or who frame them as anti-spiritual gatekeeping are not the right partners for this work in this profile.

## What this means for the user's profile

For an autistic, suspected-alexithymic, complex-trauma reader, the practical decisions cluster in three areas.

**If you want to engage IFS as a clinical modality without being drawn into the spiritual or psychedelic wing**:
- Choose practitioners who position at the clinical end of the spectrum. Use the Figure 4 schema. Ask the differentiating questions before the first session — "How do you describe Self?", "Do you work with unattached burdens?", "What books do you most often recommend?"
- Use *No Bad Parts*, the Sweezy/Schwartz clinical workbook, and licensed-therapist-authored material rather than *The Spirituality of IFS* or *The Others Within Us* for self-led reading
- If "unattached burdens" or "spirit guides" come up unprompted in session, name explicitly that you'd prefer to work in clinical register. A practitioner who can't accommodate that is the wrong fit for this profile
- Recognize that "IFS-Trained" certification is a floor, not a ceiling. Ask directly about positioning rather than relying on credentials alone

**If you are interested in psychedelic-assisted therapy** (the user is not currently asking, but the question may arise):
- Ketamine + IFS is the most accessible. Vet the clinic for clinical (not "shamanic") framing. Look at what safeguards they articulate, not just what experiences they promise
- Screen carefully for dissociative-disorder history with the prescribing clinician. Complex trauma is not a contraindication; it is a screening / pacing / integration matter
- Insist that "discovered" content under altered states be treated phenomenologically until integration sessions establish what it does and doesn't claim
- Be aware that the FDA rejected Lykos's MDMA-AT NDA in 2024 over methodological and safety concerns. That doesn't make MDMA-AT useless; it does mean current practice is on weaker regulatory ground than IFS-Institute marketing materials suggest

**If the spiritual / contemplative register appeals**:
- That is a legitimate choice. The point is to make it a deliberate choice rather than a drift
- Recognize that the contemplative-practice and transpersonal registers do not have the same evidence base as clinical IFS, and adjust expectations accordingly
- Hold "discovered" content in altered states with epistemic humility regardless of how vivid the experience is. Vividness is not evidence

The reader who has worked through this category understands that "IFS" in 2026 is not a single practice but a family of practices sharing vocabulary. Clinical-modality IFS is what most licensed practitioners do and what the small RCT base addresses. Contemplative-practice IFS is what many books, courses, and senior trainers articulate. Transpersonal IFS is where Schwartz's current public voice substantially sits and where the most contested expansions live. None of these is automatically the wrong choice. The wrong choice is *not knowing which one you're in*.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: "MAPP1/MAPP2 validated MDMA + IFS"
  - **Reality**: The trials validated MDMA-assisted therapy with non-directive inner-directed support. IFS was a framework therapists could switch into if clients spontaneously generated parts language. The intervention validated is "MDMA + non-directive psychotherapy," not "MDMA + IFS." And the FDA rejected the NDA in August 2024.
- **Misconception**: "Unattached burdens are a fringe addition Schwartz disagrees with"
  - **Reality**: Schwartz wrote the foreword to Falconer's book and runs his own *Spirituality of IFS* course. The transpersonal direction is the founder's current position, not a deviation.
- **Misconception**: "Mainstream clinical psychology has examined and rejected the spiritual claims"
  - **Reality**: Mainstream clinical psychology has *not engaged* in published peer-reviewed literature. The silence is the finding, not a verdict.
- **Misconception**: "Legacy burdens are scientifically validated through epigenetics"
  - **Reality**: Intergenerational stress effects are documented. Specific epigenetic mechanisms in humans are constrained by methodological limits. The clinical metaphor is useful; the literal biological claim is overstated.
- **Misconception**: "If the spirituality material doesn't appeal, you can ignore it and it won't affect clinical IFS"
  - **Reality**: It affects which texts to read, which trainings to choose, which practitioners are likely to operate in which register, and what to expect when you walk into an IFS workshop in 2026.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Unattached burden (UB) | In Falconer/Schwartz, clinical material framed as not originating from the client's own system; reframing of cross-cultural "spirit possession" in IFS vocabulary |
| Legacy burden | Burden inherited from family / ancestors / culture rather than from client's own life |
| Generational unburdening | Protocol for releasing legacy burdens, often involving "passing back" imagery to ancestors or family system |
| Porous mind | Falconer's framing — the mind as not a closed system, capable of being entered by external content |
| Spirit guides | In transpersonal IFS, helpful non-physical figures distinct from inner parts |
| KAP / KAP-IFS / K-IFS | Ketamine-Assisted Psychotherapy, often paired with IFS as the integration framework |
| MAPP1, MAPP2 | The two MAPS Phase 3 trials of MDMA-assisted therapy for PTSD (Mitchell et al., *Nature Medicine*, 2021 and 2023) |
| Lykos | Lykos Therapeutics, formerly MAPS PBC; the entity whose MDMA-AT NDA was rejected by FDA in August 2024 |
| Spontaneous parts language | The phenomenon, observed in 78% of Mithoefer's Phase 2 active-dose MDMA sessions, of clients spontaneously using "parts" framing without prompting |
| Self-energy | IFS framing for the felt qualities (8 Cs, 5 Ps) indicating the client is operating from Self rather than from a part |
| Functional unblinding | When a drug's psychoactive effects make participants able to correctly guess assignment despite blinding procedures — a major reason for the FDA's 2024 rejection |
| Non-dual | A contemplative / philosophical framing that consciousness is fundamentally unified rather than divided into subject and object |
| Primacy of consciousness | The metaphysical thesis that consciousness is fundamental rather than derivative of matter; the framing Schwartz endorses in his foreword to Falconer |
