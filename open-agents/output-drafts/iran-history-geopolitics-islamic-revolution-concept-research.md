# The Islamic Revolution (1979)

## 1. The Revolutionary Coalition

The Iranian Revolution was remarkable for the breadth of the coalition that brought down the Pahlavi monarchy. Shared antipathy toward the Shah was the only glue binding together groups with fundamentally incompatible visions for Iran's future:

### Islamists
- **Khomeini's clerical network**: The core revolutionary force, operating through Iran's thousands of mosques, seminaries (howzehs), and religious associations. The mosques provided an organizing infrastructure that SAVAK could never fully penetrate or control.
- **Islamic Republican Party (IRP)**: Founded in 1979 by Khomeini's closest allies — Mohammad Beheshti, Ali Khamenei, Akbar Hashemi Rafsanjani, and others — to institutionalize clerical control.

### Leftists
- **Tudeh Party**: Iran's communist party, aligned with the Soviet Union. Initially supported the revolution but was eventually crushed.
- **Fedaiyan-e Khalq (Organization of Iranian People's Fadai Guerrillas)**: Marxist guerrillas who had conducted armed operations against the Shah since the 1970s.
- **Mojahedin-e Khalq (MEK/PMOI)**: A unique Islamist-Marxist synthesis — they combined Shia theology with revolutionary socialism. Led by Massoud Rajavi, they were the most organized armed opposition group and played a significant military role in the revolution.

### Nationalists and Liberals
- **National Front**: Heirs of Mossadegh's nationalist movement, advocating parliamentary democracy and constitutional government.
- **Freedom Movement of Iran (Nehzat-e Azadi)**: Founded by Mehdi Bazargan and Ayatollah Mahmoud Taleghani, blending Islamic values with democratic principles. Bazargan would become the first post-revolutionary prime minister.

### Bazaar Merchants (Bazaaris)
The traditional merchant class had deep, centuries-old ties to the Shia clergy. They financed mosques, seminaries, and clerical activities. The Shah's economic modernization — favoring Western corporations, supermarkets, and modern retail over the traditional bazaar — had alienated this powerful class. The bazaaris provided the financial backbone of the revolution, funding strike activities and supporting the families of those killed or imprisoned.

### Students and Intellectuals
University students were among the most active participants, inspired by Ali Shariati's revolutionary reinterpretation of Islam and by Third World liberation movements. Many had been radicalized by SAVAK repression on campuses.

## 2. Khomeini's Return (February 1, 1979)

### The Buildup

After the Shah's departure on January 16, 1979, the situation in Iran was volatile. Prime Minister Shapour Bakhtiar — appointed by the Shah in a last-ditch attempt at compromise — attempted to establish a constitutional transition. He offered concessions: dissolving SAVAK, freeing political prisoners, promising free elections. But the revolutionary momentum was unstoppable.

Khomeini had spent 14 years in exile — in Turkey (1964–1965), Iraq (1965–1978), and France (October 1978–February 1979). From his base in Neauphle-le-Chateau outside Paris, he had conducted an extraordinarily effective media campaign, giving interviews to the world's press, recording messages distributed on cassette tapes throughout Iran, and presenting himself as a moderate figure seeking to end tyranny and restore dignity.

### The Return

On February 1, 1979, Khomeini boarded an Air France Boeing 747 chartered by the revolutionary movement and landed at Tehran's Mehrabad Airport. An estimated three to five million people lined the streets of Tehran to greet him — one of the largest public gatherings in human history. When asked on the plane by a journalist what he felt about returning to Iran after 14 years, Khomeini famously replied: "Nothing" (hichi) — a statement that conveyed either Sufi-like detachment or cold political calculation, depending on interpretation.

### The Dual Government

For ten days, Iran had two governments: Bakhtiar's appointed government and Khomeini's parallel revolutionary authority. On February 4, Khomeini appointed **Mehdi Bazargan** as prime minister of a provisional revolutionary government. Bakhtiar refused to resign.

### The Final Collapse (February 9–11, 1979)

On February 9, Air Force technicians (homafars) at Doshan Tappeh Air Base clashed with Imperial Guard units loyal to the Shah. Guerrillas from the Fedaiyan and Mojahedin joined the fighting. Over the next two days, the revolution became an armed insurrection. Police stations, military bases, and government buildings were seized. Arms were distributed to the public. On February 11, the Supreme Military Council declared the armed forces "neutral" — effectively abandoning the regime. Bakhtiar went into hiding and eventually fled to Paris.

February 11, 1979, became the official date of the revolution's triumph (celebrated annually as "22 Bahman").

## 3. Why the Military Did Not Intervene Effectively

The failure of the 450,000-strong Iranian military to save the monarchy remains one of the most analyzed aspects of the revolution:

### Structural Dependencies on the Shah
The military was designed as a personal instrument of the monarch, not an autonomous institution. Senior generals had been selected for loyalty rather than competence. They earned the equivalent of roughly $70,000 annually with extensive fringe benefits and were psychologically incapable of acting without the Shah's orders. When the Shah left, they were paralyzed — the command structure had no mechanism for independent decision-making.

### Class Divisions Within the Military
Senior officers lived affluent lives tied to the regime. But middle-ranking and junior officers came overwhelmingly from the politically disaffected middle and lower-middle classes. Unlike generals who lived in insulated privilege, these officers interacted daily with Iranian society and absorbed the same frustrations. Many were sympathetic to the revolution. Conscript soldiers — drawn from the same social classes as the protesters — were even less reliable.

### The Huyser Mission's Contradictions
U.S. General Robert Huyser spent 31 days (January 3 – February 3, 1979) meeting with Iran's top military commanders ("Group Five"), ostensibly to stiffen their resolve but also to explore the feasibility of a military coup. The mission sent contradictory signals: was the U.S. backing the Shah? Bakhtiar? A military government? The generals received no clear directive and were privately embittered — the Shah himself believed that Huyser's mission proved America no longer wanted him in power.

### The February 11 Declaration of Neutrality
On February 11, two dozen senior officers met in Tehran and instructed the Chief of Staff that the armed forces should withdraw from the conflict. Deputy Chief of Staff General Houshang Hatam issued an order proclaiming military neutrality. The army simply dissolved — soldiers walked away from their posts, many joining the revolutionary crowds.

## 4. Establishment of the Islamic Republic

### The March 1979 Referendum

On March 30–31, 1979, a referendum was held with a single question: "Should the form of government be an Islamic Republic?" Voters were offered two choices: yes or no. There was no option for a secular republic, a constitutional monarchy, or any other form of government. The result was 98.2% in favor. Critics pointed out that the referendum offered no real alternative, that there was enormous social pressure to vote yes, and that "Islamic Republic" was never clearly defined before the vote.

### Velayat-e Faqih: Khomeini's Political Theory

The foundational political doctrine of the Islamic Republic is **velayat-e faqih** (Guardianship of the Islamic Jurist). Khomeini first articulated this theory in a series of lectures in Najaf, Iraq, in 1970, later published as the book *Hokumat-e Eslami* (Islamic Government):

- **Core argument**: In the absence of the Twelfth (Hidden) Imam — whom Twelver Shia Muslims believe went into occultation in 874 CE and will return at the end of time — a qualified Islamic jurist (faqih) must exercise political authority over the Muslim community.
- **Unprecedented claim**: This was a revolutionary innovation in Shia political thought. Traditional Shia theology held that all political authority was illegitimate during the occultation of the Hidden Imam. Khomeini reversed this, arguing that a supreme jurist had both the right and the obligation to rule.
- **Platonic echoes**: Khomeini's faqih resembled Plato's philosopher-king — a ruler whose proven moral and intellectual commitment would ensure just governance.
- **Clerical opposition**: Of the dozen Grand Ayatollahs alive at the time of the revolution, only one besides Khomeini — **Ayatollah Hussein-Ali Montazeri** — supported velayat-e faqih. Grand Ayatollah Shariatmadari, Grand Ayatollah Khoei, and others opposed it, viewing it as a dangerous innovation that conflated religious authority with political power. Shariatmadari was later placed under house arrest, and his clerical rank was effectively stripped — unprecedented in Shia history.

### The New Constitution (December 1979)

The Assembly of Experts for the Constitution, elected in August 1979, drafted the constitution. Despite Bazargan's provisional government hoping for a French-style republic with Islamic values, Khomeini's supporters dominated the Assembly and enshrined velayat-e faqih as the governing principle. The constitution was ratified by referendum on December 2–3, 1979, with 99.5% approval. Key institutions:

**Supreme Leader (Rahbar)**: The most powerful figure in the system. Appointed for life by the Assembly of Experts. Powers include:
- Delineating general policies of the Islamic Republic
- Supervising the legislature, judiciary, and executive branches
- Commanding the armed forces
- Appointing the head of the judiciary, heads of state media, and half the Guardian Council
- Power to dismiss the president

**Guardian Council (Shura-ye Negahban)**: Twelve members — six Islamic jurists appointed by the Supreme Leader and six civil jurists nominated by the Chief Justice and approved by the Majlis. Powers include:
- Veto power over all legislation passed by parliament (must conform to Islamic law and the constitution)
- Approving or disqualifying candidates for all elections (local, parliamentary, presidential, and Assembly of Experts)
- This veto power makes the Guardian Council a de facto filter on Iranian democracy

**Assembly of Experts (Majles-e Khobregan)**: 88 mujtahids (senior Islamic scholars) elected by popular vote for eight-year terms (though candidates are vetted by the Guardian Council). Sole constitutional power: electing and, theoretically, dismissing the Supreme Leader. In practice, it has never challenged the Supreme Leader.

**President**: Head of government (below the Supreme Leader), elected by popular vote for four-year terms, limited to two consecutive terms.

**Majlis (Islamic Consultative Assembly)**: The parliament, with 290 seats elected by popular vote. Passes legislation, but all laws must be approved by the Guardian Council.

## 5. The Iran Hostage Crisis (November 4, 1979 – January 20, 1981)

### The Trigger

On October 22, 1979, President Carter allowed the deposed Shah to enter the United States for cancer treatment at a New York hospital. For many Iranians, this was an echo of the 1953 coup — the fear that America would again use Iranian exile to plot a restoration of the monarchy.

### The Embassy Seizure (November 4, 1979)

A group calling itself the **Muslim Student Followers of the Imam's Line** — approximately 300–500 students — stormed and occupied the U.S. Embassy in Tehran. Sixty-six Americans were taken hostage (later reduced to 52 after the release of women, African Americans, and one ill hostage).

**Student Motivations**:
- Demanded the extradition of the Shah to face trial in Iran
- Sought to expose what they believed were CIA operations being run from the embassy (documents were later published as the "Documents from the Den of Spies" series)
- Intended to depose the moderate provisional government of Prime Minister Bazargan, which they suspected of plotting to normalize relations with the United States
- Twenty-five years of accumulated rage over the 1953 coup, SAVAK repression, and American support for the Shah

**The initial plan was to hold the embassy for only a short time** — perhaps 48 to 72 hours. But the takeover proved wildly popular among the Iranian public, and Khomeini gave it his full support, transforming it from a student protest into a state-sanctioned act.

### Khomeini's Decision to Support the Crisis

Khomeini's endorsement was strategic:
- It outflanked and destroyed Bazargan's moderate government — Bazargan resigned on November 4, the day of the seizure, recognizing he was powerless
- It consolidated revolutionary fervor and united the public against a foreign enemy
- It provided a permanent justification for anti-American ideology
- Khomeini called the embassy seizure "the second revolution" — greater even than the first

### Carter's Response and Operation Eagle Claw

President Carter imposed economic sanctions, froze Iranian assets (approximately $12 billion), broke diplomatic relations, and attempted negotiation through multiple intermediaries. When diplomacy stalled, Carter authorized **Operation Eagle Claw** — a military rescue mission launched on April 24, 1980.

**The Operation**:
- The plan involved elements from all four military branches converging at a desert staging area code-named "Desert One," approximately 200 miles southeast of Tehran
- Eight Navy helicopters launched from the aircraft carrier USS Nimitz
- Two helicopters suffered mechanical failures en route; a third was damaged at Desert One
- With only five operational helicopters (six were deemed the minimum), the mission was aborted
- During the withdrawal, a helicopter collided with a C-130 transport plane and exploded, killing eight American servicemen (five Air Force, three Marines)
- The failed mission humiliated the Carter administration and contributed to his election defeat
- The disaster led to the creation of the United States Special Operations Command (USSOCOM) in 1987

### The October Surprise Controversy

The hostages were released on January 20, 1981 — minutes after Ronald Reagan was inaugurated, 444 days after the seizure. The timing fueled one of the most enduring controversies in American political history: the **October Surprise** allegation.

**The Core Allegation**: Members of Reagan's presidential campaign, led by campaign manager **William Casey** (later CIA Director), secretly negotiated with Iranian officials to delay the release of the hostages until after the November 1980 election, thereby undermining Carter's re-election chances.

**Evidence and Claims**:
- In March 2023, former Texas Governor John Connally's associate **Ben Barnes** publicly confirmed that Casey sent Connally on a secret mission to Middle Eastern leaders in the summer of 1980, asking them to urge Iran not to release the hostages before the election
- Former Iranian President **Abolhassan Banisadr** wrote in his 1991 memoir that "Ayatollah Khomeini and Ronald Reagan had organized a clandestine negotiation" to prevent the hostages' release
- Former Israeli Prime Minister **Yitzhak Shamir**, when asked in 1993 whether the hostage delay was part of a deal with Iran, answered: "Of course it was"
- Congressional investigations in the early 1990s concluded there was insufficient evidence to confirm the allegations

**Historical Assessment**: While official investigations found the evidence "insufficient," multiple participants, historians, and subsequent revelations have lent increasing credibility to the allegation. The controversy remains unresolved, though many historians now believe some form of back-channel communication between the Reagan campaign and Iranian officials did occur.

### Impact on U.S.-Iran Relations

The hostage crisis severed U.S.-Iran relations completely. Diplomatic ties have never been restored. The crisis:
- Created a permanent framework of mutual hostility
- Made "Death to America" a ritual chant of the Islamic Republic
- Ensured that any future U.S. president who attempted rapprochement with Iran would face enormous domestic political costs
- Established the U.S. Embassy compound as a propaganda museum ("Den of Spies")

## 6. Consolidation of Theocratic Power: Purges and Repression

### Destruction of the Liberal-Nationalist Opposition

The elimination of non-Islamist revolutionary allies was systematic and methodical:

- **August 1979**: The National Democratic Front was banned
- **November 1979**: Bazargan's provisional government was effectively dissolved (he resigned on the day of the embassy seizure)
- **January 1980**: The Muslim People's Republic Party was banned
- **February 1980**: Supporters of the Mojahedin-e Khalq came under attack
- **March 1980**: The Cultural Revolution was launched against universities
- **June 1981**: President **Abolhassan Banisadr** (Iran's first elected president) was impeached

**Banisadr's Fall**: Banisadr, elected president in January 1980, increasingly clashed with the clerical establishment. He opposed the continuation of the hostage crisis, criticized the growing power of the Islamic Republican Party, and warned against theocratic dictatorship. The Majlis filed articles of impeachment on June 21, 1981, with only one legislator speaking in his defense. Khomeini signed off on his removal the next day. Banisadr fled Iran disguised as a woman and found asylum in France.

By the end of 1979, as one historian noted, "the secular middle class and liberals had been vanquished and the only surviving players on the Iranian political stage were the Islamist intellectuals, the young urban poor, and the devout bourgeoisie."

### Crushing the Left

The armed left was suppressed with extreme violence:

- The Mojahedin-e Khalq (MEK) launched an armed insurgency in June 1981 after Banisadr's ouster, carrying out bombings that killed dozens of senior officials, including IRP leader Ayatollah Beheshti (June 28, 1981), President Rajai and Prime Minister Bahonar (August 30, 1981)
- The regime's response was massive: thousands of leftists were arrested, tortured, and executed
- In the **1988 prison massacres**, Khomeini issued a fatwa ordering the execution of imprisoned political dissidents who refused to recant. An estimated 4,000–5,000 prisoners (some estimates go higher) were executed in a matter of weeks — primarily MEK members but also Tudeh and Fedaiyan prisoners
- The Tudeh Party, despite having supported the revolution and the Islamic Republic, was crushed in 1983 when its entire leadership was arrested

### Ethnic Minority Suppression

The revolutionary government faced immediate challenges from ethnic minorities seeking autonomy:

**The Kurdish Rebellion (March 1979 onward)**:
- Kurdish groups, led by the Democratic Party of Iranian Kurdistan (KDPI), initially campaigned for political autonomy within Iran, not separatism
- Negotiations were attempted but collapsed. On August 19, 1979, Khomeini declared a "jihad" against Iran's Kurds, labeling them infidels
- The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) launched a major offensive in spring 1980
- Approximately 10,000 people were killed, including 1,200 Kurdish political prisoners executed by the government
- The suppression of the Kurdish uprising was critical in establishing the IRGC's legitimacy and operational capability

**Arab Uprising in Khuzestan**:
- The Ahwazi Arabs, concentrated in oil-rich Khuzestan province, demanded cultural and political rights
- Despite Khuzestan holding approximately 80% of Iran's hydrocarbon resources, its Arab population lived in extreme poverty
- Uprisings were met with military force; the IRGC established permanent control over the region

**Baloch Resistance in Sistan-Balochestan**:
- Sunni Baloch communities faced religious as well as ethnic discrimination
- Sporadic armed resistance was suppressed; the province remains one of Iran's least developed regions

**Khomeini's Ideological Framework**: In a Radio Iran speech on December 17, 1979, Khomeini declared that ethnic identities were contrary to Islamic doctrines — nationalism within minority groups was a plot by Islam's enemies to divide the Muslim community. This ideological stance securitized all ethnic demands, treating calls for cultural rights or autonomy as existential threats to the state.

## 7. The Cultural Revolution (1980–1983)

### Closing the Universities

On June 14, 1980, Khomeini ordered the "Islamification" of the higher education system, calling it the Cultural Revolution (Enqelab-e Farhangi). Universities were closed for approximately three years (1980–1983). The explicit goal was to "purify" higher education by removing Western, liberal, and leftist elements.

Khomeini justified the closure by asserting that "all the major problems of the last fifty years" could be traced back to the universities, which he claimed had been breeding grounds for the "gharbzadegi" (Westoxification) virus spread by academics, liberals, and intellectuals.

### Violence on Campuses

In 1981, mobs — often organized by Hezbollahi (Party of God) militias — attacked university campuses. Students were beaten, leftist organizations were forcibly expelled from their offices, and campuses were occupied. The violence was systematic, not spontaneous.

### Purges and Screening

When universities reopened in 1982–1983:
- Students were screened by committees; those deemed ideologically unfit were barred from continuing their studies
- 20,000 teachers were dismissed as too "Westernized"
- Nearly 8,000 military officers were purged
- Curricula were rewritten to conform with Islamic values
- Books were banned; Western philosophical and social science texts were removed from libraries
- Student unions were banned and replaced with Islamic associations
- Purges continued for five more years after reopening, with special focus on "Islam's enemies"

## 8. Women's Rights Rollback

### The Speed of Regression

The rollback of women's rights was one of the most immediate and visible consequences of the revolution:

- **March 7, 1979**: Just weeks after the revolution, Khomeini announced a mandatory Islamic dress code for women
- **March 8, 1979**: International Women's Day — more than 100,000 women gathered in Tehran's streets to protest the mandatory hijab ruling. This was one of the first mass demonstrations against the new government
- The protests resulted in a temporary retraction of mandatory veiling — a tactical retreat, not a genuine concession

### Gradual Enforcement

Once the leftists and liberals were eliminated and the conservatives secured sole control:
- **July 1980**: "Islamification of offices" — unveiled women were refused entry to government buildings and public offices; women could be fired for not wearing hijab at work
- **1980**: Hijab became mandatory in government and public offices
- **1983**: Hijab became mandatory for all women in all public spaces, enforced by the newly established morality police (Gasht-e Ershad)

### Broader Legal Rollback

- The 1967 Family Protection Law was repealed — this had given women rights in divorce, child custody, and limited polygamy
- Women were barred from becoming judges
- Beaches, sports facilities, and public transportation were sex-segregated
- The marriage age for girls was lowered to 9 (later raised to 13)
- Inheritance and testimony laws reverted to traditional Islamic jurisprudence, with women's testimony valued at half a man's
- Women's dress code violations became criminal offenses punishable by fines, imprisonment, or flogging

## 9. Economic Restructuring: Bonyads and the IRGC

### Bonyads (Revolutionary Foundations)

In the immediate aftermath of the revolution, the new government seized vast assets — properties, businesses, and financial holdings of the Pahlavi royal family, their associates, and anyone deemed connected to the old regime. These assets were placed under the control of **bonyads** (charitable foundations):

- **Bonyad-e Mostazafan va Janbazan** (Foundation of the Oppressed and War Veterans): The largest, managing billions in assets across manufacturing, agriculture, construction, and real estate. Originally intended to redistribute wealth to the "mostazafan" (oppressed masses).
- **Astan-e Quds-e Razavi** (Imam Reza Shrine Foundation): Controls vast holdings in Mashhad and across Iran.

**Scale**: Bonyads encompass an estimated 30–40% of Iran's total GDP, with approximately 5 million Iranians working in bonyad-affiliated enterprises. They control 20–30% of Iran's non-oil economy.

**Accountability**: Bonyads operate with exemptions from taxes, antitrust laws, and standard governmental oversight. Their leadership is appointed directly by the Supreme Leader. They function as parallel power structures — a state within the state.

**Original Intent vs. Reality**: Created to embody Islamic economic justice and redistribute wealth to the poor, bonyads have in practice become instruments of patronage, enriching those connected to the clerical establishment. They represent one of the most significant examples of the revolution's transformation from populist movement to entrenched elite.

### The IRGC's Economic Empire

The **Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps** (Sepah-e Pasdaran-e Enqelab-e Eslami), founded in April 1979 as a parallel military force to protect the revolution from internal enemies, rapidly expanded into economic activities:

- The IRGC capitalized on wartime reconstruction (during and after the Iran-Iraq War, 1980–1988) to expand into construction, engineering, oil and gas, manufacturing, telecommunications, and agriculture
- By 2026, the IRGC controls a multibillion-dollar business empire reaching virtually all sectors of the Iranian economy
- IRGC-affiliated companies receive preferential access to government contracts, import licenses, and financial resources
- This economic power serves dual purposes: generating revenue for military and strategic programs, and building a loyal base among Iran's poorer, rural populations through patronage and employment

### The Bazaar's Continued Role

The clerical leadership maintained and deepened the historic bazaari-clergy alliance:
- Bazaar merchants continued to provide financial support for mosques, seminaries, and clerical institutions
- In return, the new economic order protected bazaari interests from modern corporate competition
- Many clerics were themselves bazaari families — the social networks overlapped extensively
- The bazaar served as an informal financial system, handling remittances, trade financing, and capital movements outside the formal banking sector

## 10. How the Revolution Hardened Into Theocracy

### The Bait-and-Switch

Many members of the revolutionary coalition believed Khomeini would serve as a spiritual guide rather than a political ruler. Before the revolution, Khomeini made statements suggesting he would not seek political power directly. In Paris, he spoke of freedom, democracy, and a government that represented all Iranians.

However, as one historian observed, "what began as an authentic and anti-dictatorial popular revolution based on a broad coalition of all anti-Shah forces was soon transformed into an Islamic fundamentalist power-grab." The transformation followed a pattern:

1. **Eliminate the moderates**: Bazargan's government was sidelined within months
2. **Use external crises to consolidate**: The hostage crisis destroyed liberal opposition and made anti-Americanism the state religion
3. **Close the universities**: Remove the institutional base of leftist and secular thought
4. **Purge the military and bureaucracy**: Replace professional expertise with ideological loyalty
5. **Destroy the armed left**: Use the MEK insurgency as justification for mass repression
6. **Constitutional entrenchment**: Lock velayat-e faqih into the constitution so that clerical supremacy became legally unassailable
7. **War mobilization**: The Iran-Iraq War (1980–1988) provided the ultimate justification for suppressing dissent in the name of national survival

### Structural Mechanisms of Control

The constitution created interlocking mechanisms that made reform virtually impossible:
- The Guardian Council vetoes legislation and disqualifies candidates, ensuring only approved figures enter the political system
- The Assembly of Experts theoretically oversees the Supreme Leader but is itself vetted by the Guardian Council (appointed by the Supreme Leader) — a circular system of self-reinforcing control
- The IRGC operates as a parallel military, intelligence, and economic power center, answerable only to the Supreme Leader
- The judiciary is headed by an appointee of the Supreme Leader
- State media is controlled by an appointee of the Supreme Leader

## 11. Foreign Reactions to the Revolution

### United States

The revolution represented a catastrophic strategic loss. Iran had been the cornerstone of America's security architecture in the Persian Gulf since the British withdrawal east of Suez in 1971. The Nixon Doctrine had designated Iran as America's regional policeman — the "Twin Pillar" alongside Saudi Arabia. The loss of Iran:
- Eliminated America's most powerful regional ally
- Created a hostile regime controlling the Strait of Hormuz
- Led to the hostage crisis and permanent severing of diplomatic relations
- Prompted the Carter Doctrine (January 1980): any attempt by an outside force to gain control of the Persian Gulf region would be regarded as an assault on vital U.S. interests, to be repelled by military force
- Led to the creation of the Rapid Deployment Joint Task Force, which later became U.S. Central Command (CENTCOM)

### Soviet Union

Moscow was surprised by the revolution to an even greater degree than Washington. The Soviets had expected their ally, the Tudeh Party, to benefit from the Shah's fall. Instead:
- Khomeini designated the Soviet Union as the "Lesser Satan" (the U.S. being the "Great Satan")
- The new regime suppressed the Tudeh Party and executed its members
- Soviet ideology was as anathema to Khomeini's Islamic vision as American capitalism
- The Soviet invasion of Afghanistan in December 1979 further poisoned relations
- Moscow declared "neutrality" in the Iran-Iraq War but became Iraq's primary arms supplier

### Israel

The revolution created one of the most dramatic foreign policy reversals in the modern Middle East:
- Under the Shah, Iran and Israel had extensive but unofficial cooperation: military ties, intelligence sharing, oil trade (Iran supplied up to 60% of Israel's oil), and joint missile development programs
- Israel had helped establish SAVAK and maintained a significant presence in Iran
- Within weeks of the revolution, Khomeini severed all ties, recognized the PLO, and handed over the Israeli mission in Tehran to Yasser Arafat's representatives
- The elimination of Israel as a Jewish state became a core foreign policy principle of the Islamic Republic
- Despite this public hostility, the Iran-Contra affair of the 1980s would later reveal continued covert dealings

### Arab States

Iran's revolutionary rhetoric alarmed its neighbors:
- The Persian Gulf monarchies (Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Bahrain, UAE, Qatar, Oman) feared that Iran would export its revolution to their Shia populations
- Saudi Arabia, as the leader of the Sunni Arab world and guardian of Mecca and Medina, saw Khomeini's claim to lead all Muslims as a direct challenge
- Iran's revolutionary message explicitly threatened the legitimacy of monarchies — "Islam is the religion of the oppressed, not the oppressors"
- When the Iran-Iraq War began in September 1980, the Gulf monarchies provided Iraq with tens of billions of dollars in financial support
- The Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) was founded in 1981 largely in response to the perceived Iranian threat
- The revolution intensified the Sunni-Shia sectarian divide across the region, a division that continues to shape Middle Eastern politics

## 12. The Revolution's Immediate Legacy

### What Was Gained

- End of the Pahlavi dictatorship and SAVAK's reign of terror
- Iranian sovereignty and independence from American domination
- A political system that incorporated elements of popular participation (elections, parliament) — however constrained
- A sense of national dignity and agency, particularly resonant after the humiliations of the 1953 coup and decades of perceived subservience to Washington

### What Was Lost

- The democratic aspirations of the revolution — the broad coalition's diverse visions were crushed in favor of one
- Women's rights and legal equality
- Freedom of expression, association, and the press
- Academic freedom and intellectual diversity
- Ethnic minorities' hopes for cultural recognition and political autonomy
- Iran's international relationships and economic integration
- Thousands of lives — in the revolution itself, the purges, the 1988 massacres, and the Iran-Iraq War that followed

### The Central Paradox

The Islamic Revolution remains one of the defining events of the 20th century. It demonstrated that a mass popular movement could overthrow a heavily armed, Western-backed regime without conventional military force. It inspired Islamist movements worldwide and fundamentally reshaped the politics of the Middle East.

Yet it also illustrated the pattern by which revolutions devour their children. The broad, inclusive coalition that made the revolution possible was systematically dismantled by its most organized faction. The revolution against tyranny produced a new form of authoritarianism — one clothed in religious legitimacy rather than royal prerogative, but no less determined to monopolize power.

As former president Banisadr reflected from exile: the revolution that was meant to free Iran from one form of despotism had merely replaced it with another.
