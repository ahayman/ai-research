# Practical Decision Aid: What to Try, What to Skip, How to Self-Assess

## Overview — Where the Deep Dive Lands

Across the ten preceding chapters, the deep dive has assembled a structured argument. Polyvagal theory as proposed by Stephen Porges is contested at the level of its evolutionary and anatomical claims; a 2026 international consensus of 39 experts in vagal physiology and vertebrate evolution rejected all five of its basic premises. The data that motivated the theory is real; autistic adults show, on average, lower baseline parasympathetic heart-rate variability and substantially reduced respiratory sinus arrhythmia reactivity to social stress. Less-contested frameworks — Neurovisceral Integration, predictive processing, allostatic load, constructed emotion — explain the same data without requiring the contested anatomical claims. Several practices marketed under polyvagal language work for reasons that predate or are independent of polyvagal theory: slow breathing produces vagally-mediated HRV effects established in cardiovascular physiology by the 1990s; humming produces measurable nasal nitric oxide increases; cold facial exposure triggers the well-characterized trigeminal-vagal diving reflex; attuned therapeutic presence is grounded in attachment theory from Bowlby onward. And the wellness industry has overreached significantly — consumer "vagus nerve" devices, most of which are not FDA-cleared and have no independent sham-controlled trials; supplements marketed with vagal framing for which Mass General Hospital explicitly states there is no specific vagal-tone evidence; brain-retraining programs sold as cures for ME/CFS that UK NICE guidance specifically recommends against.

This chapter is the synthesis. Its job is to convert that body of work into something you can use. If you have read or scanned the ten chapters before this one, the reasonable expectation is a clear answer to the original question that motivated the whole deep dive: **should I try polyvagal-adjacent approaches, and if so, which ones, and how do I evaluate practitioners, products, and my own results?**

The chapter answers in five operational tools: a five-tier evidence framework with concrete recommendations in each tier; a decision tree that distinguishes when polyvagal framing helps from when it substitutes for something that needs a different answer; a practitioner-evaluation checklist you can apply in real time; a two-week starter protocol built entirely from the strongest-evidence practices; and a safety panel listing symptoms that should not be polyvagal-self-treated.

**Key takeaways:**

- **Yes, try the Tier A practices.** They are mostly free, mostly old, and mostly work for non-polyvagal reasons.
- **Treat Tier B with structured experimentation** and clear endpoints.
- **Skip Tier C.** The cost-benefit math is bad.
- **Avoid Tier D.** Cure claims, pseudo-credentials, courses without outcomes data.
- **Be especially careful with Tier E.** Brain-retraining for diagnosed ME/CFS, Wim Hof + water, unscreened intense breathwork — these have documented harm in specific populations.
- **Use polyvagal language as scaffolding, not as mechanism, not as identity.**

## The Five-Tier Evidence Framework (A–E)

The framework is portable. It applies to any wellness or nervous-system claim — not just polyvagal-branded ones. After this chapter, you have a tool for evaluating any future claim that comes across your feed, regardless of the framework it invokes.

### Tier A — High-value, low-cost, do these now

Tier A contains practices with strong independent evidence, low cost, minimal risk, and mechanisms that don't depend on whether polyvagal theory is correct. Every practice on this list predates polyvagal theory and would keep working if the theory were definitively overturned tomorrow.

**Slow paced breathing at approximately 6 breaths per minute** is the single most evidence-supported intervention in the entire deep dive. The Lehrer protocol (Lehrer 2000, 2003) established the methodology; Russo et al. 2017 in *Breathe* summarized the physiology; Laborde et al. 2022 in *Psychophysiology* confirmed effects with and without HRV biofeedback; multiple meta-analyses through 2024 show consistent increases in SDNN, RMSSD, and decreases in state anxiety. The mechanism is straightforward cardiovascular physiology: at approximately 6 breaths per minute (roughly 5 seconds inhale, 5 seconds exhale), heart rate and breathing synchronize at resonance with the cardiac baroreflex, producing large respiratory sinus arrhythmia. Free apps (Allos Breath Pacer, The Breathing App, Breath Ball) provide visual pacers at no cost. If you are practicing supine and have a POTS diagnosis, this is safe; if you are practicing standing, the orthostatic component matters less than the breathing pattern itself.

**Humming, or Bhramari pranayama in its yogic form,** produces measurable physiological effects. Lundberg and colleagues' research showed that humming during exhalation increases nasal nitric oxide output approximately fifteen-fold compared with quiet exhalation. Bhramari pranayama Holter studies (PMC10182780) show measurable HRV effects. The mechanism is concrete: the vagus nerve innervates the larynx via the recurrent laryngeal branch, and vibration of the vocal folds provides afferent stimulation. Practically: hum on the exhale, comfortable pitch, can be combined with the paced breathing protocol above. Cost: zero.

**Cold face splash** triggers the mammalian diving reflex, one of the cleanest mechanisms in autonomic neuroscience (StatPearls NBK538245). Cold water on the face stimulates the trigeminal nerve (cranial nerve V), which projects to brainstem nuclei, which send efferent signals via the vagus nerve, producing a 10–25% reduction in heart rate combined with peripheral vasoconstriction. The reflex is fast and reliable, useful as a "circuit breaker" when acute over-arousal hits — for instance in the bathroom at work when you need a discreet reset. If you have an arrhythmia history, check with a cardiologist; otherwise the risk is minimal. Distinct from cold plunge: the diving reflex specifically requires *facial* immersion, particularly the forehead and ophthalmic region.

**Adequate sleep** is foundational rather than additive. Sleep loss alone can produce burnout-pattern symptoms; no nervous-system practice will compensate for chronic sleep deficit. If you are sleeping fewer than seven hours, or your sleep is broken, address that before trying to optimize other layers. The project's existing deep-sleep work directly applies.

**Sensory accommodation** has the strongest evidence base for autistic adults specifically. Raymaker 2020's community-based participatory research on autistic burnout identifies "expectations and demands without adequate supports" as a primary causal factor. Noise-canceling headphones, lighting control (lamp instead of overhead fluorescents), fabric choices, reducing visual clutter, scheduling sensory-quiet recovery time — these are interventions, not luxuries. They have the strongest evidence base for the specific population the chapter is written for.

**Demand reduction** is, in the literal sense, the autistic-burnout intervention. The Raymaker 2020 framework treats autistic burnout as cumulative chronic stress combined with barriers to recovery. Reducing demands is not a precursor to treatment; it *is* the treatment. The cumulative-overwhelm pattern is exactly what the project's `alexithymia-autism-cumulative-overwhelm` deep dive describes in detail.

**Social safety with attuned others** has its evidence base in attachment theory — Bowlby (1969), Ainsworth (1969–1978), Sroufe (1996) — well before Porges's 1994 presidential address. Sroufe explicitly defined attachment as "the dyadic regulation of emotion." The practice of being with regulated, attuned people supports your own regulation; this is well-established. What's contested is the polyvagal-specific *explanation* for why co-regulation works. The practice works regardless.

### Tier B — Worth a structured trial with clear endpoints

Tier B contains practices with moderate evidence — at least one well-controlled RCT, or multiple smaller controlled trials, with replicated effects across independent labs. Mechanisms are plausible but not fully established. Conflict-of-interest exposure is manageable but worth knowing about. If you try a Tier B practice, set pre-specified endpoints and a defined trial window. Treat it as an n=1 experiment.

**HRV biofeedback** via consumer device and app (HeartMath Inner Balance Coherence Plus, Polar H10 plus Elite HRV, etc.) has independent evidence — the Lehrer protocol is the foundational methodology; the practice has been shown to improve various outcomes across multiple populations. The interpretation of "vagal tone" claims is contested, but the underlying HRV measurement is real, the methodology has been replicated, and consumer-grade implementations are affordable ($130–$160 for sensor, free or subscription app). An 8-week trial with weekly self-report tracking is a reasonable design.

**Transcutaneous auricular vagus nerve stimulation (taVNS)**, if your clinician offers it, has emerging evidence in autism. The at-home feasibility study PMC10568329 showed 88.5% completion across 132 sessions with mild adverse events; anxiety measures improved at two weeks. The broader taVNS literature in depression has stronger evidence. The mechanism is anatomically grounded (the auricular branch of the vagus nerve does carry vagal afferents). A 4–6 week structured trial with a pre-registered endpoint is reasonable.

**Trauma-sensitive yoga (TCTSY)**, specifically the Trauma Center Trauma-Sensitive Yoga developed by David Emerson and colleagues at the Trauma Center, has the strongest RCT evidence in the trauma-yoga space. Van der Kolk et al. 2014 in the *Journal of Clinical Psychiatry* compared TSY to a women's-health education control in chronic PTSD predominantly from interpersonal trauma and found significantly greater PTSD symptom reduction with TSY. Veterans-population feasibility trials have replicated the basic signal. The evidence is independent of polyvagal framing — the practice's mechanism involves interoceptive learning and titrated arousal, not specifically polyvagal-theory mechanisms. Importantly, generic yoga is not TCTSY; the training and structure matter.

**The Safe and Sound Protocol (SSP)** is the chapter's most COI-laden Tier B entry. Developer Stephen Porges holds the patents, receives royalties from Unyte Health (which sells SSP), and serves as Chief Scientific Advisor at Unyte. The autism evidence base is preliminary: the 2023 Kawai adult-ASD pilot was n=6, with no sham control, and only the Social Awareness subscale of the SRS-2 Family-Report reached statistical significance (PMC10049251). Sound-based interventions systematic reviews have rated the evidence mixed. *If* you try SSP, pre-specify a single concrete outcome — for instance, "less sensory overwhelm in supermarkets over four weeks" — and use a self-rated baseline and post-measure. Treat it as a 4–6 week n=1 trial with realistic expectations.

> **Evidence note**: The SSP entry is intentionally Tier B–C rather than B. The developer COI plus the lack of sham-controlled adequately-powered RCTs in autism warrants the lower placement than mechanism plausibility alone would suggest. This is a judgment call consistent with Cat 6 validation.

**Polyvagal-informed therapy with a licensed clinician** sits at Tier B with the understanding that the therapy relationship itself has independent evidence — decades of alliance research show the therapeutic relationship is one of the most consistent predictors of treatment outcome. The polyvagal framework layered on top may or may not be load-bearing depending on the clinician. Use the practitioner-evaluation checklist below to assess whether the framework is one tool among many or whether it has become ideology.

**HRV monitoring wearables** (Oura, Whoop, Garmin) provide real biological data. The numbers are real, the underlying RMSSD and HF-HRV measurements are valid. What's contested is the interpretation — calling HRV "vagal tone" conflates a measurement with a mechanism. Use the data for pattern-noticing across weeks and months, correlate with subjective state, but don't make the device the center of your practice. There is a documented pattern of HRV-tracking becoming its own source of stress; if your wearable is making you anxious, take it off.

### Tier C — Probably unhelpful or weak evidence, be skeptical

Tier C contains products and approaches with manufacturer-funded studies, no sham control, mechanism claims that invoke contested or speculative pathways, and marketing claims that outrun the evidence. Not necessarily *harmful*, but the cost-benefit math is bad. Money spent here is money not spent on Tier A and B practices.

The four leading consumer "vagus nerve" devices — Truvaga, Sensate, Pulsetto, and Apollo Neuro — are all sold under the FDA general wellness device framework, which does not require clinical efficacy demonstration. None has substantive FDA clearance (only gammaCore does, and only for migraine and cluster headache). Apollo Neuro has more independent-looking pilot data than the others — a University of Pittsburgh trial and a 2025 *American Journal of Medicine* student RCT — but no autism-specific RCT exists, and the mechanism (wrist haptic vibration via touch receptors) doesn't map directly onto vagal afferents. The "vagus nerve" branding on these devices is loose at best.

"Vagus nerve" supplements — typically omega-3, magnesium, B-complex, L-theanine, probiotics with vagal-tone framing — have a documented Mass General Hospital position: although omega-3 and probiotics have been described as vagus-nerve stimulators, "there is no research that points to them as having a specific or measurable impact on the function or tone of the vagus nerve." Some products may have small independent effects through other mechanisms; the *vagal-specific* framing is what's unsupported.

Gua sha and "vagus nerve massage" tools rest on an anatomical claim that doesn't hold. The cervical vagus is deep; superficial neck massage cannot reach it meaningfully. Northwell Health and McGill Office for Science and Society have both noted that the mechanism claim is inconsistent with established anatomy. Massage may produce relaxation through other mechanisms; the "vagal stimulation" branding is the unsupported part.

"Polyvagal" coaching from non-licensed practitioners occupies an ambiguous zone. The Polyvagal Institute's own scope-of-practice language distinguishes "Applied Polyvagal Trauma Practitioner" (for licensed clinicians) from language for non-licensed practitioners ("I integrate applied polyvagal trauma interventions into my practice"). The distinction is real and important. Coaching may be useful for accountability and structure; it is not therapy, and the scope-of-practice difference matters when you have actual clinical needs.

Practitioners who diagnose autonomic states ("you're in dorsal vagal collapse," "your ventral vagal is offline") without measurement are using descriptive language, not assessment. No clinical tool exists for non-invasive in-vivo measurement of dorsal vagal state. The framing may be heuristically useful; treating it as diagnosis is a category error.

### Tier D — Active red flags

Tier D is where the framework asks you to walk away. The red-flag patterns are concrete and recognizable.

Autism "cure" claims via polyvagal interventions are red flags. No polyvagal-adjacent intervention has reversed autism in any peer-reviewed trial. Claims to do so target a desperate population. The autistic community has pushed back specifically on the framing of autism as a deficit of social engagement function that can be remediated through nervous-system intervention — this framing is contested both empirically (Cat 4 evidence) and from an autistic-affirming position (Trauma Geek's neurodiversity-paradigm critique among others).

"Rewire your nervous system in [short time]" content compresses what may be years of pattern change into a five-day or four-week marketing claim. Marketing language that promises rapid total transformation, particularly when paired with high price points and no published outcomes data, is a red flag pattern.

Expensive courses with no published outcomes data — typically in the $2,000–$5,000 range, often labeled as "certifications" or "programs" — that cannot point to peer-reviewed efficacy data are bad cost-evidence ratios. If a program cannot produce its outcomes, the absence is the data.

Pseudo-credentials standing in for clinical licenses is the structural red flag. "Polyvagal-informed practitioner," "certified somatic coach," "trauma-informed coach" are not licenses to provide mental-health treatment. They are certifications. If the practitioner is also licensed (LCSW, LMFT, PsyD, PhD psychology, MD, RN, OT, PT), the certificate is one tool the licensed clinician uses. If the practitioner is *only* a certified-coach, they cannot legally provide mental-health treatment in most US jurisdictions regardless of how impressive the certificate appears.

Refusal to disclose conflicts of interest combined with strong efficacy claims is a structural pattern across the consumer-device industry. Porges has disclosed his Unyte/SSP royalties in academic publications — those disclosures rarely appear in consumer marketing. When a practitioner cannot or will not articulate their financial relationships with products they recommend, treat the recommendation as commercial advice rather than clinical guidance.

### Tier E — Potentially harmful in specific contexts

Tier E contains practices and approaches with documented adverse events in specific populations. The harm is real, documented, and population-specific. Match your risk profile to the practice carefully.

**Brain-retraining programs for diagnosed ME/CFS-spectrum fatigue** — Gupta Program, DNRS (Dynamic Neural Retraining System), Primal Trust, Lightning Process — are the clearest Tier E entries. UK NICE 2021 guidelines on ME/CFS (NG206) explicitly state: "Do not offer people with ME/CFS therapies derived from osteopathy, life coaching or neurolinguistic programming (NLP) for example the Lightning Process." Up to 89% of people with Long COVID have post-exertional malaise; brain-retraining programs that disregard pacing pose particular harm risk for this population. The ME/CFS advocacy community (MEAction, Goudsmit, others) has documented harm cases over more than a decade. If you have diagnosed ME/CFS, Long COVID with PEM, or fibromyalgia spectrum with PEM, the evidence-based approach is pacing-based management rather than retraining. Cat 10 covers this in detail.

**Wim Hof breath-hold + cold combination** for cardiac-vulnerable populations has documented drowning case reports. Cyclic hyperventilation produces hypocapnia, which can drop blood oxygen; combining this with cold-water immersion is the specific harm pattern. Even Wim Hof's own materials warn never to do the breathing in or near water; the combination is widely practiced regardless. Live Science has cited cardiology experts on potentially dangerous arrhythmias from the practice.

**Intense breathwork** — holotropic breathwork, breath of fire, similar high-intensity protocols — produces hypocapnia leading to tetany, paresthesias, dizziness; in vulnerable individuals can precipitate panic, dissociation, in rare cases hallucinations or seizure. The 1993 Findhorn Foundation report led to suspension of their breathwork program after psychosis and seizure case reports. Trauma survivors without skilled facilitation are in the highest-risk category. The chapter is not saying "never do breathwork"; it is saying "screen and facilitate properly."

**Substitution of polyvagal language for medical or mental-health workup** is the Tier E concern that doesn't look like Tier E. It looks like self-care. The cost of substitution is delayed treatment of treatable conditions. If your "dorsal vagal" symptoms turn out to be hypothyroidism, sleep apnea, ME/CFS, untreated ADHD, depression, perimenopause, B12 deficiency, or iron-deficiency anemia, the months or years of polyvagal self-treatment are months or years of delayed diagnosis. The chapter's safety panel (below) lists the specific symptom patterns that should trigger clinical evaluation rather than self-care.

## What to Try First — A Decision Tree for Autistic Burnout

The order of operations matters. Many readers will recognize themselves in a familiar pattern: feeling overwhelmed, reaching for the nervous-system regulation toolkit, layering breathing techniques and supplements and devices on top of an environment that is itself the proximal cause of the dysregulation. The decision tree below front-loads the highest-leverage interventions.

The default position for an autistic adult with overwhelm and burnout is: the highest-leverage interventions are demand reduction, sensory accommodation, sleep, and the Tier A practices. Most people skip these and go straight to expensive Tier B and C tools because the foundational interventions are harder — they require renegotiating with employers, partners, family, and one's own self-image. The nervous-system regulation toolkit is easier to buy than to ask for accommodations. ## When the Framework Helps vs. When It Substitutes

This is the centrally important distinction the deep dive arrives at. Polyvagal language can be both genuinely useful (scaffolding) and genuinely problematic (substitute), depending on how it's used. The same vocabulary can serve either function.

<!-- DIAGRAM: helps-vs-substitutes -->

The framework helps when it gives you a way to communicate internal states, normalizes dysregulation as a body-state rather than a moral failing, scaffolds self-compassion, and supports asking for accommodations. "I notice I'm in shutdown" is more communicable than no language at all. The polyvagal ladder works as a self-tracking tool regardless of whether the underlying neuroanatomical claim is biologically accurate. These benefits are real and don't require the theory to be correct.

The framework substitutes when it distracts from unaccommodated sensory environments, masks comorbid mental-health conditions that respond to evidence-based treatment, replaces medical workup for chronic illness, justifies expensive interventions that have no demonstrated efficacy, or becomes identity language that closes off the possibility of updating self-understanding. "I'm a person who lives in dorsal vagal" stops being a description and starts being a fixed frame.

The seven-step operational check distinguishes the two uses. For any internal state you might describe with polyvagal language, ask: is there a sensory input I could reduce? Is there a demand I could reduce? Are there medical red flags? Have I been sleeping inadequately? Am I using this language to justify expense? Am I using it as identity rather than description? Only after all of those — use the language as scaffolding for state-tracking and self-regulation practice.

## Autistic-Specific Considerations

The framework above is designed for an autistic adult dealing with overwhelm and burnout. There are several places where the autistic-specific considerations matter more than the general framework.

**Sensory accommodation is treatment, not luxury.** The Raymaker 2020 / AASPIRE framework treats autistic burnout as cumulative load + barriers to recovery + loss of access to coping mechanisms. In that frame, noise-canceling headphones aren't an accommodation request, they are a clinical intervention. The autistic adult's sensory environment is often the single largest unaddressed source of allostatic load. Modifying it has direct, measurable effects on regulatory capacity over time.

**Demand reduction is the autistic-burnout intervention.** This is the key empirical finding from Cat 5. The autistic burnout literature does not support a framing in which intensive nervous-system regulation work substitutes for environmental and demand modification. The literature explicitly identifies demand load and barriers to recovery as the causal factors. The intervention is not "improve your regulation capacity"; the intervention is "reduce the demands on your regulation capacity."

**Comorbidity awareness matters.** Approximately 70% of autistic adults carry at least one comorbid psychiatric diagnosis, and approximately 40% carry two or more. Lifetime prevalence ranges in the literature: anxiety 28–42%, depression 23–37%, PTSD 11–60% depending on population. The polyvagal framework's RSA-based predictions are transdiagnostic — low resting RSA shows up across internalizing disorders, externalizing disorders, schizophrenia, autism, and nonsuicidal self-injury. This means that "improve your HRV" is not specific to any one condition, and that PVT-informed therapies positioned as treating "the nervous system" may help across comorbidities but typically not better than gold-standard treatments for each specific condition (CBT for anxiety, behavioral activation or CBT for depression, PE/EMDR/CPT for PTSD).

**Heterogeneity matters.** Cat 4's Kushki vs. Cheng disagreement reconciles through autistic heterogeneity: hyper-arousal and hypo-arousal subgroups exist, and your individual autonomic profile matters more than the group average. A practitioner who treats every autistic client as needing "ventral vagal activation" is missing the underlying variation. Self-tracking your own patterns over time matters more than applying a generic template.

**The chronic-illness overlap matters.** Many autistic adults also have POTS, hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, ME/CFS-spectrum fatigue, or other dysautonomia/connective-tissue conditions. The brain-retraining industry — Gupta, DNRS, Primal Trust, Lightning Process — has been a particular harm vector in this overlap because the marketing targets exactly the population that has multiple co-occurring conditions. If you have an actual chronic-illness diagnosis with post-exertional malaise, pacing-based management is the evidence-based approach; brain-retraining is contraindicated.

## The Practitioner-Evaluation Checklist

When considering a practitioner offering "polyvagal-informed" services, the eight-question checklist below operationalizes Cat 7 and Cat 8 into a portable tool.

A practitioner who scores well welcomes these questions. The framework is one tool among many; they use it as vocabulary and case-conceptualization aid; they distinguish framework claims from mechanism claims; they cite specific studies; they disclose financial relationships; they would update beliefs in light of evidence. The underlying clinical license is doing the legally-relevant work.

A practitioner who scores poorly treats these questions as adversarial. The framework has become branding or belief system rather than tool. The certificate may be standing in for a license; the mechanism story may be doing load-bearing work that should be done by a clinical license. Consider another practitioner.

The most common red flag in practice is question seven: "Would you update your beliefs if a definitive study tomorrow showed polyvagal theory is wrong?" A practitioner who answers "yes — the practices I use have independent evidence bases" is using the framework correctly. A practitioner who answers "polyvagal theory has helped so many people, it must be true" has converted an empirical question into a faith claim. That conversion is the structural problem the deep dive has tracked across eleven chapters.

## The Two-Week Starter Protocol

The chapter's most concrete offering is a two-week starter protocol built entirely from Tier A practices. Free. Low-risk. Strong evidence. You could start tonight.

<!-- DIAGRAM: two-week-protocol -->

The Week 1 / Week 2 structure layers progressively. Week 1 establishes the foundations: morning and evening paced breathing, cold face splash as needed, sensory audit, demand audit, sleep priority. Week 2 adds: pre-emptive paced breathing before known stressors, a glimmers list (Dana's coinage; the noticing practice is universal), one social safety contact, a scheduled sensory recovery block.

After two weeks, assess honestly. Did you do the breathing most days? If not, what got in the way? Did sensory modifications change your baseline? Did you actually defer demands? What single change had the largest effect?

If consistent two-week practice has produced no shift, the proximal cause is probably outside the polyvagal layer entirely. Revisit medical workup, demand levels, sleep, accommodations. Don't escalate to Tier B practices yet — Tier B works best layered on top of consistent Tier A foundation, and skipping the foundation to chase paid solutions is the wellness-industry capture pattern.

If consistent two-week practice has produced a modest shift — say, fewer meltdowns, faster recovery from over-arousal, more sustainable energy through the workday — continue Tier A as your floor and consider whether to add one Tier B practice with a pre-specified endpoint. Don't add multiple new things at once; you won't be able to attribute changes.

## Symptoms That Should NOT Be Polyvagal-Self-Treated

The safety chapter within the synthesis. Wellness self-care belongs in the maintenance and regulation layer of your life. It does not belong in the diagnostic-workup layer. When symptoms suggest a treatable medical or mental-health condition, the polyvagal framing should be a complement to clinical evaluation, not a substitute for it.

<!-- DIAGRAM: not-polyvagal-self-treated -->

The pattern is repeatable: each of these symptoms or symptom clusters could be the focus of polyvagal-style self-treatment, and for many readers it has been. The cost of substitution is delayed treatment of treatable conditions. Chronic fatigue with post-exertional malaise that turns out to be ME/CFS responds to pacing-based management, not retraining. Syncope and postural lightheadedness that turn out to be POTS need cardiology workup. Persistent anhedonia and hopelessness that turn out to be major depressive disorder respond to evidence-based depression treatment. Sleep disturbance that turns out to be sleep apnea needs a sleep study. Untreated ADHD with executive function collapse has specific evidence-based treatments. Perimenopause, thyroid disorders, B12 deficiency, and iron-deficiency anemia all have lab workups and specific treatments.

The pattern: get evaluated for the medical and mental-health considerations first, then apply wellness self-care on top of (not instead of) the evaluation. If a clinician dismisses your concerns and you suspect substitution is happening, get a second opinion.

## "What I'd Do If I Were You" — Concrete First Steps

The chapter has done its analytical work. Here is the concrete recommendation pattern for an autistic adult with overwhelm and burnout who has read this far.

**Tonight, before going to sleep**: Install one of the free paced-breathing apps (Allos Breath Pacer, The Breathing App, or Breath Ball). Set it to five to six breaths per minute. Breathe for five minutes. Note any difference, however small. Plan to repeat in the morning. This is Tier A; cost zero.

**Tomorrow**: Do the morning paced breathing. Identify one sensory input you can modify (most likely candidates: lighting, sound, fabric, scent, visual clutter). Modify it before the end of the week. Identify one demand you can defer, decline, or renegotiate. Defer, decline, or renegotiate it before the end of the week. Confirm you are sleeping at least seven hours; if not, address that first.

**This week**: Continue morning and evening paced breathing. Add cold face splash as a circuit breaker for acute over-arousal. Add humming on the exhale if it's comfortable. Notice the sensory and demand changes. Don't add anything new beyond this list. Tier A foundation only.

**Next week**: Continue Week 1 practices. Add pre-emptive paced breathing before known stressors. Write a glimmers list — five small things that move you toward feeling regulated. Identify one social safety contact and schedule time with them. Schedule one explicit sensory-quiet recovery block of at least 60 minutes.

**After two weeks**: Assess honestly. If consistent practice produced a modest shift, continue and consider whether to add one Tier B practice with a pre-specified endpoint. If consistent practice produced no shift, the proximal cause is probably outside this layer — revisit medical workup, demand levels, sleep, accommodations.

**If you are considering a Tier B practice**: Pre-specify a single outcome metric and a defined trial window (4–8 weeks typical). Treat it as an n=1 experiment. Return within the return window if the outcome metric doesn't move.

**If you are considering buying a Tier C device**: Don't, until Tier A is consistently in place. If after consistent Tier A you still want to try a device, treat it as a Tier B trial with pre-specified endpoints, and use the return window aggressively if it doesn't deliver.

**If you encounter a practitioner using polyvagal language**: Apply the practitioner-evaluation checklist. The license under the framework is what matters. Pseudo-credentials are not clinical licenses.

**If you encounter any symptom on the safety panel**: See a clinician. Wellness self-care is a complement, not a substitute.

**If you find yourself using polyvagal language as identity rather than as description**: Pause. Run the seven-step operational check. Hold the framework lightly.

## Summary

### Key Points

1. **Polyvagal theory as proposed is contested at its evolutionary and anatomical claims.** The 2026 international consensus paper (39 experts) rejected all five basic premises.
2. **The data that motivated the theory is real.** Autistic adults show lower baseline parasympathetic HRV and reduced RSA reactivity to social stress on average.
3. **Alternative frameworks explain the same data.** Neurovisceral Integration, predictive processing, allostatic load, and constructed emotion are less contested and account for the findings.
4. **Several practices marketed under polyvagal language work for non-PVT reasons.** Slow breathing, humming, cold face splash, attuned therapeutic presence, sensory accommodation, demand reduction — none require PVT to be correct.
5. **The wellness industry has overreached significantly.** Consumer devices without FDA clearance, supplements without specific vagal evidence, brain-retraining programs sold as cures for ME/CFS that NICE specifically advises against.
6. **For autistic burnout specifically, allostatic load is the empirically anchored framework.** Raymaker 2020 and the AASPIRE measure use cumulative-load framing, not polyvagal.
7. **The five-tier framework (A–E) is the portable decision tool.** It applies to any wellness claim, not just polyvagal-branded ones.
8. **Tier A tonight. Tier B with structured trial. Skip Tier C. Avoid Tier D. Especially careful with Tier E.**
9. **Use polyvagal language as scaffolding, not as mechanism, not as identity.** Hold it lightly.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Tier A | Strong evidence, low cost, do these now (free practices like slow breathing, humming, cold face splash) |
| Tier B | Worth a structured trial with pre-specified endpoints (HRV biofeedback, taVNS, TCTSY, SSP) |
| Tier C | Probably unhelpful or weak evidence, be skeptical (consumer "vagus nerve" devices, supplements) |
| Tier D | Active red flags (cure claims, pseudo-credentials, courses without outcomes data) |
| Tier E | Potentially harmful in specific contexts (brain-retraining for ME/CFS, Wim Hof + water, unscreened intense breathwork) |
| Scaffolding | Using polyvagal language as a self-tracking tool — helpful |
| Substitute | Using polyvagal language to replace medical workup, mental-health diagnosis, or accommodation work — risky |
| PEM | Post-exertional malaise — hallmark symptom of ME/CFS |
| Allostatic load | Cumulative wear-and-tear from chronic dysregulated stress |
| Co-regulation | Dyadic emotion regulation; attachment-theory-grounded; predates PVT |
| Demand reduction | The autistic-burnout intervention; reducing cumulative demand |
| Sensory accommodation | Environmental modification to reduce sensory input |
| Pre-specified endpoint | A defined outcome metric set before starting an intervention |
