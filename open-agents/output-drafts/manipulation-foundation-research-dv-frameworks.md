# Foundational Research Compilation: Domestic Violence, Intimate Partner Violence, and Coercive Control Frameworks

**Purpose.** This document maps the validated empirical frameworks in domestic violence, intimate partner violence (IPV), and coercive control research. It is intended as a citable bibliography for downstream pages of an English pragmatics learning hub for autistic individuals, specifically for the highest-stakes category: self-protection from manipulation and abuse. Every claim below is traceable to a source. The document distinguishes *validated frameworks* (peer-reviewed empirical support) from *established practice* (widely used but less rigorously tested) and notes where evidence is weak, contested, or missing for marginalized populations.

**Date compiled.** April 2026

**Critical framing.** This is a compilation, not a teaching document. Downstream pages should not publish a self-protection strategy unless it is traceable to a source listed here or in supplemental research. For autistic readers, add the caveat that most underlying research did not include autistic samples — the frameworks are transferable in principle but the strategies require adaptation and individual safety planning.

## Section 1: The Duluth Model and the Power and Control Wheel

### 1.1 Origin and Development

The Power and Control Wheel was created in 1982 in Duluth, Minnesota by Ellen Pence, Coral McDonnell, and Michael Paymar, working through the Domestic Abuse Intervention Project (DAIP). It was developed as part of curriculum for a court-ordered program for men who battered their partners and emerged from focus groups of battered women who were asked what they wanted judges and prosecutors to understand about the non-episodic, pattern-based aspects of their abusers' behavior (theduluthmodel.org; DAIP archival materials).

In 1984, DAIP conducted structured interviews with survivors to identify what they described as the eight most universal tactics abusers used to control them. These tactics became the spokes of the Wheel, with "power and control" at the hub and "physical and sexual violence" forming the outer rim — the tactics inside the wheel are what hold the violence in place even when physical violence is infrequent.

**Primary sources:**
- Pence, E., & Paymar, M. (1993). *Education Groups for Men Who Batter: The Duluth Model.* Springer.
- Domestic Abuse Intervention Programs. "The Development of the Power and Control and Equality Wheels." (theduluthmodel.org)
- Paymar, M. (2018). *The Duluth Model.* Battered Women's Justice Project / BISCMI.

### 1.2 The Eight Spokes of the Wheel

The eight categories of abusive tactics in the original (heterosexual, male-perpetrator) Power and Control Wheel are:

1. **Coercion and Threats** — threats to hurt, leave, commit suicide, report to authorities, take the children; forcing compliance with illegal acts.
2. **Intimidation** — creating fear via looks, gestures, actions, smashing things, destroying property, displaying weapons.
3. **Emotional Abuse** — put-downs, name-calling, making the partner feel crazy, playing mind games, humiliation, inducing guilt.
4. **Isolation** — controlling what the partner does, sees, reads; whom they see and talk to; limiting outside involvement; using jealousy as justification.
5. **Minimizing, Denying, and Blaming** — denying the abuse happened, shifting responsibility, saying the partner caused it, minimizing severity.
6. **Using Children** — making the partner feel guilty about children, using children to relay messages, using visitation to harass, threatening to take children.
7. **Economic Abuse** — preventing work, making partner ask for money, giving an allowance, hiding income, preventing access to family finances.
8. **Male Privilege** — treating the partner as a servant, making all the "big decisions," acting as "master of the castle," defining roles.

Source: Domestic Abuse Intervention Programs, "Understanding the Power and Control Wheel."

### 1.3 The Equality Wheel (Contrasting Model)

The Equality Wheel, also created by DAIP, visualizes what replaces the power-and-control tactics in a non-abusive relationship. Its spokes are: Non-threatening Behavior, Respect, Trust and Support, Honesty and Accountability, Responsible Parenting, Shared Responsibility, Economic Partnership, and Negotiation and Fairness. The outer ring is "Nonviolence" rather than "Physical and Sexual Violence." It functions as a pedagogical tool in batterer-intervention programs and as an assessment tool for what a healthy relationship looks like (theduluthmodel.org; Raft Cares; Next Door Solutions).

### 1.4 Validation and Critique

**Evidence quality: MIXED / CONTESTED as a treatment model; STRONG as a descriptive taxonomy.**

The Wheel itself — as a descriptive taxonomy of abuser tactics — is widely accepted and has become the de facto ontology used by shelters, law enforcement training, and courts in the US and internationally. As a *treatment model* for batterer intervention, the evidence is weaker:

- An early NIJ-sponsored evaluation found modest reductions in recidivism for completers vs. non-completers (Office of Justice Programs).
- A 2003 National Institute of Justice study found "little or no effect" of Duluth-model batterer intervention on recidivism.
- Babcock, Green, & Robie (2004), *Clinical Psychology Review*, meta-analysis of batterer treatment: effect sizes in the "small" range, with no significant difference between Duluth-style and CBT-style programs beyond the effect of arrest itself. (DOI: 10.1016/S0272-7358(02)00174-5)
- Ellen Pence herself later wrote that the "power motive" framing did not fit the lived experience of all the men and women they worked with — an important concession from the Wheel's co-creator (Pence, 1999, "Some Thoughts on Philosophy").

**Critique:** The Wheel was developed from a heterosexual, cisgender, predominantly white working-class sample, which has been criticized as underrepresenting LGBTQ+ and racial-minority experiences. Adaptations (the LGBT Power and Control Wheel by The Network/La Red; workplace adaptations by Scott, 2018) address some gaps but have not been rigorously validated.

**Workplace adaptation source:** Scott, H. S. (2018). "Extending the Duluth Model to Workplace Bullying: A Modification and Adaptation of the Workplace Power-Control Wheel." *Workplace Health & Safety* (SAGE). DOI: 10.1177/2165079917750934.

### 1.5 Mapping the Wheel to Pragmatic Manipulation (for the Autism Hub)

This mapping is **analytical, not independently validated** — it is an extrapolation for the downstream pragmatics hub from established Wheel categories to autism-specific manipulation patterns. Mark clearly as "framework application" rather than "evidence."

| Duluth Wheel category | Pragmatic / autism-context mechanism |
|---|---|
| Intimidation | Tone policing framed as threat; implicit "if you don't mask, consequences follow" |
| Emotional abuse | "You're too literal"; "nobody likes the way you talk"; weaponized labels |
| Isolation | Cutting you off from other autistics; framing your support network as "enabling" |
| Minimizing/denying/blaming | The core gaslighting trio — "that didn't happen," "you misread it," "you caused it" |
| Using children / using diagnosis | "I didn't say that because of your autism"; weaponizing diagnosis as excuse for their behavior |
| Coercion and threats | Threats to out the person's diagnosis, withdraw accommodation, report to employers |
| Economic abuse | Controlling disability benefits, SSI, supported-employment income |
| Privilege | "Neurotypical privilege" framing — "normal people don't need this explained" |

## Section 2: Lenore Walker's Cycle of Abuse and Battered Woman Syndrome

### 2.1 The Four Phases

Lenore E. Walker, a psychologist, first published the cycle of violence theory in *The Battered Woman* (1979), based on interviews with approximately 1,500 women in abusive relationships. The original model had three phases; Walker later refined it to four:

1. **Tension-building** — stressors accumulate, communication breaks down, victim attempts to placate and control the environment; fear rises.
2. **Acute battering incident** — discharge of tension via physical, sexual, or severe psychological attack. Often brief relative to the other phases.
3. **Reconciliation / "honeymoon"** — abuser expresses remorse, gives gifts, makes promises, shifts blame to external causes; loving contrition reinforces attachment.
4. **Calm** — period of relative peace in which the abuse is rationalized or denied by both parties; eventually yields to renewed tension-building.

**Primary sources:**
- Walker, L. E. A. (1979). *The Battered Woman.* Harper & Row.
- Walker, L. E. A. (2016). *The Battered Woman Syndrome* (4th ed.). Springer Publishing.

### 2.2 Learned Helplessness and Battered Woman Syndrome (BWS)

Walker adapted Martin Seligman's learned-helplessness research (originally from animal studies) to explain why battered women do not leave: repeated exposure to uncontrollable aversive events produces a cognitive/motivational state in which the person stops trying to escape even when escape becomes possible. From this, Walker developed "Battered Woman Syndrome" as a posited psychological profile that she later argued to be a subtype of PTSD.

**Primary sources:**
- Walker, L. E. A. (1977–78). "Battered Women and Learned Helplessness." *Victimology* 2: 525–534. (Office of Justice Programs abstract)
- Seligman, M. E. P. (1975). *Helplessness: On Depression, Development, and Death.* Freeman.

### 2.3 Validation and Critiques

**Evidence quality: CONTESTED. Foundational and widely used but methodologically flawed; partially superseded by trauma and coercive-control theories.**

Major critiques documented in the literature:

- **Sampling:** Walker's original 1,500-woman sample was non-random and self-referred. Walker herself acknowledged the sample "cannot be considered a legitimate data base from which to make specific generalizations" (Walker, 1984).
- **Incomplete fit:** The cycle does not describe many abuse patterns — particularly those dominated by coercive control without cyclical physical violence, and situational couple violence (Michael P. Johnson's typology, below).
- **Victim-blaming concern:** BWS framing can be read as pathologizing the victim rather than the perpetrator; advocates have criticized it as placing the "syndrome" inside the woman rather than the abuse inside the relationship.
- **Seligman's disagreement:** The learned-helplessness researcher himself reportedly did not endorse Walker's extension of his animal-model findings to battered women (see BWS critiques collected at VAWnet).
- **Contemporary status:** The field has substantially moved toward framing what Walker called BWS as complex PTSD or trauma adaptation, and has absorbed the descriptive insight of the cycle into pattern-based frameworks (coercive control, entrapment) that do not require the honeymoon phase.

**Key critique source:** Dutton, M. A. (2009). "Update of the 'Battered Woman Syndrome' Critique." VAWnet (National Resource Center on Domestic Violence) Applied Research Forum.

### 2.4 Why the Cycle Still Matters for Safety Planning

Even with methodological limitations, the cycle remains operationally useful because:
- It names the **reconciliation phase** as part of the abuse pattern, not a reprieve from it — critical for survivors trying to interpret "good times" as meaningful rather than as a predictable phase of entrapment.
- It gives language for the **predictable escalation** in the tension-building phase, supporting anticipatory safety planning.
- Non-physical ("purely pragmatic") abuse appears to follow an analogous tension → eruption → apologies → calm cycle, though this extension is primarily clinical wisdom rather than empirical fact.

## Section 3: Evan Stark's Coercive Control Theory

### 3.1 Core Work

**Stark, E. (2007).** *Coercive Control: How Men Entrap Women in Personal Life.* Oxford University Press. ISBN: 978-0195384048.

Stark's thesis reframes intimate partner abuse: "domestic violence" is misnamed because it is neither necessarily primarily violent nor confined to episodes. Instead, it is a pattern of **entrapment** more closely analogous to hostage-taking or kidnapping than to assault. The pattern operates through four interwoven elements:

1. **Violence** — physical and sexual, but often low-level and sporadic, functioning as periodic reinforcement rather than the primary mechanism.
2. **Intimidation** — looks, gestures, threats, displays of destructive power, that produce constant fear and behavioral modification without requiring new violence each time.
3. **Isolation** — systematic severing of the victim's social, family, work, and communication ties, so that the abuser becomes the only authoritative source of information and validation.
4. **Control** — the granular, day-to-day regulation of behavior: what she wears, eats, reads, says, does with her time and money — "micro-regulation" that Stark likens to the rule-books of prisons.

Stark defines coercion as "the use of force or threats to compel or dispel a particular response" and control as "structural forms of deprivation, exploitation, and command that compel obedience indirectly." When coercion and control occur together, the product is a "condition of unfreedom" experienced as entrapment.

**Primary sources:**
- Stark, E. (2007). *Coercive Control: How Men Entrap Women in Personal Life.* OUP.
- Stark, E. (2009). "Rethinking Coercive Control." *Violence Against Women* 15(12): 1509–1525. DOI: 10.1177/1077801209347452.
- Stark, E., & Hester, M. (2019). "Coercive Control: Update and Review." *Violence Against Women* 25(1): 81–104. DOI: 10.1177/1077801218816191.

### 3.2 The Incident-Based → Pattern-Based Shift

Stark's most consequential theoretical move: **the field must stop counting incidents and start mapping patterns.** Incident-based framing (how many times did he hit you, how hard) systematically underestimates abuse dominated by control rather than assault, and mismatches the survivor's experience of *living under a regime* rather than *being in a series of fights.*

This reframing is cited as the empirical and conceptual basis for criminalizing coercive control in the UK, Scotland, Ireland, and parts of Australia. (See Section 3.4.)

### 3.3 Stark's "Fights vs. Control" Distinction — Critical for the Manipulation vs. Miscommunication Question

One of Stark's most valuable contributions for an autism-focused pragmatics hub is his distinction between **fights** (bidirectional conflicts between people with comparable power, in which both parties use aggressive tactics to win a specific dispute) and **control** (unidirectional regulation in which one party systematically narrows the other's autonomy across domains). This distinction is crucial because autistic readers are often told — and may themselves fear — that their difficulties are "miscommunication." Stark's framework gives them a criterion: *miscommunication is bidirectional and issue-specific; control is unidirectional and total-life-spanning.*

**Key citation:** Stark, E. (2007), Chapter 8 on the entrapment model; and Johnson, M. P. (2008). *A Typology of Domestic Violence: Intimate Terrorism, Violent Resistance, and Situational Couple Violence.* Northeastern University Press. Johnson's typology is the complementary empirical framework that operationalizes the "fight vs. control" distinction into four types: intimate terrorism (coercive control with violence), violent resistance, situational couple violence, and mutual violent control.

### 3.4 Criminalization of Coercive Control

The legal codification of Stark's framework is a major empirical and legal development:

- **England and Wales:** *Serious Crime Act 2015*, Section 76, created the offence of "controlling or coercive behaviour in an intimate or family relationship." Maximum penalty: 5 years imprisonment and/or fine. (legislation.gov.uk; GOV.UK statutory guidance framework.)
- **Scotland:** *Domestic Abuse (Scotland) Act 2018*, in force April 2019. Widely regarded as the most comprehensive of the CCB laws — it allows a single offense covering a course of physical and psychological abuse together.
- **Ireland:** *Domestic Violence Act 2018*, Section 39. Creates a similar offense, limited to current or former intimate partners.
- **Northern Ireland:** *Domestic Abuse and Civil Proceedings Act (Northern Ireland) 2021*.
- **Australia (various states)**, **Hawaii**, **California (SB 1141, 2020)**, and **Connecticut (Jennifer's Law, 2021)** have adopted or are adapting similar definitions, though mostly in family-court rather than criminal-code contexts.

**Primary sources:**
- Home Office. *Controlling or Coercive Behaviour: Statutory Guidance Framework.* GOV.UK.
- Sheley, E. (2024). "Criminalizing Coercive Control Within the Limits of Due Process." *Duke Law Journal.*
- Bettinson, V., & Bishop, C., eds. (2024). *Criminalizing Coercive Control: Cross-Jurisdictional Lessons.* Oxford Academic, *Statute Law Review.*
- Time Magazine (2019). "Why These Nations Took the Lead in Criminalizing Controlling Behavior in Relationships."

### 3.5 How Coercive Control Operates Through Pragmatic Mechanisms

Stark's work explicitly identifies pragmatic-linguistic control as a primary vector of coercive control. Examples documented in *Coercive Control* and subsequent literature:

- **Controlling what you can say:** scripting of responses, forbidden topics, required phrases, "approved" friends to discuss "approved" topics with.
- **Controlling whom you can talk to:** isolation via monitoring, framing other relationships as threats, rewriting the history of friendships.
- **Controlling how reality is defined:** gaslighting proper — the abuser as sole authoritative interpreter of events, emotions, and intentions.
- **Controlling the moment-to-moment rules of conversation:** tone policing, interruption monopoly, forced apologies, demand for particular emotional displays.

This section is the *direct bridge* from Stark's framework to the autism-pragmatics hub — coercive control *is*, in large part, the weaponization of pragmatic power over someone who is already at a pragmatic disadvantage.

## Section 4: Safety Planning Frameworks

### 4.1 National Domestic Violence Hotline (NDVH) Safety Planning

The NDVH safety planning framework — established practice since the hotline's founding in 1996 — organizes safety planning into three temporal contexts:

1. **Safety while in the relationship** — signal codes with children/neighbors, memorizing crisis numbers, identifying safe rooms and exits, hiding weapons, "grab and go" bags.
2. **Safety when preparing to leave** — document copies (ID, insurance, legal papers) hidden or given to trusted person, financial reserves, destination planning, timing around abuser's absence.
3. **Safety after leaving** — changing locks, route variation, workplace notification, protective orders, digital security, new phone numbers, restraining order logistics.

**Primary sources:**
- The Hotline. "Create Your Personal Safety Plan." thehotline.org.
- The Hotline. "Relationship Abuse Safety Planning." thehotline.org.
- Baylor College of Medicine, Center for Research on Women with Disabilities. "Interpersonal Violence: Safety Planning."
- SAMHSA. *Substance Abuse Treatment and Domestic Violence* (NCBI Bookshelf NBK64443), Appendix D: Sample Personalized Safety Plan.

**Evidence quality: ESTABLISHED PRACTICE. Most safety planning elements have face validity and are consensus-based among advocates, but component-level randomized testing is limited. The strongest empirical evidence is for safety planning when paired with advocacy (see Sullivan & Bybee, 1999, below).**

- Sullivan, C. M., & Bybee, D. I. (1999). "Reducing Violence Using Community-Based Advocacy for Women With Abusive Partners." *Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology* 67(1): 43–53. — Seminal RCT showing that advocacy-plus-safety-planning reduced re-assault at 24-month follow-up.

### 4.2 The Danger Assessment (DA) — Jacquelyn Campbell

The Danger Assessment is the **gold-standard, peer-reviewed, empirically validated** risk-assessment instrument in the IPV field. It was developed by Jacquelyn C. Campbell, PhD, RN, FAAN (Anna D. Wolf Chair, Johns Hopkins School of Nursing).

**Development.** Campbell constructed the DA by comparing risk factors present in intimate-partner homicide cases against those present in non-lethal IPV cases. The 11-city case-control study of intimate partner femicide (N=310 femicide cases, 324 abused controls) produced multivariate odds-ratio-weighted risk factors that became the scoring algorithm.

**Key risk factors on the DA include:**
- Increasing frequency or severity of physical violence over the past year
- Perpetrator ownership of or access to a gun
- Prior use of a weapon
- Prior threats with a weapon
- Prior threats to kill
- Avoided killing
- Beaten while pregnant
- Violent and constant jealousy
- Control of most of her daily activities
- Choked / strangled (see Section 5.1 below)
- Violent outside the home
- Forced sex
- Threats of suicide by the abuser
- Threats to kill the children
- Believes he is capable of killing her
- Abuser follows/spies/leaves threatening messages
- Recent separation or attempted separation
- Abuser unemployed
- Child in home who is not biologically his

**Primary sources:**
- Campbell, J. C., Webster, D. W., & Glass, N. (2009). "The Danger Assessment: Validation of a Lethality Risk Assessment Instrument for Intimate Partner Femicide." *Journal of Interpersonal Violence* 24(4): 653–674. DOI: 10.1177/0886260508317180. (PMC7878014)
- Campbell, J. C., Webster, D., Koziol-McLain, J., Block, C. R., Campbell, D., Curry, M. A., et al. (2003). "Risk Factors for Femicide in Abusive Relationships: Results From a Multisite Case Control Study." *American Journal of Public Health* 93(7): 1089–1097. (PMC1447915)
- Williams, K. R., Stansfield, R., & Campbell, J. (2022). "Persistence and Potential Lethality in Intimate Partner Violence: Evaluating the Concurrent and Predictive Validity of a Dual Risk Assessment Protocol." *Violence Against Women.*
- Danger Assessment website: dangerassessment.org

**Evidence quality: STRONG.** Convergent construct validity supported across multiple studies against the Index of Spouse Abuse, Conflict Tactics Scale, and injury measures. At least three independent predictive-validity studies partially support the DA's ability to predict re-assault.

### 4.3 The Lethality Assessment Program (LAP) — Maryland Model

The LAP is a first-responder–delivered, protocol-linked **implementation** of Campbell's DA. Created by the Maryland Network Against Domestic Violence in 2005. The LAP uses an 11-item Lethality Screen that officers administer at the scene; if the victim screens "high danger," the officer immediately connects the victim by phone to a trained DV advocate on-scene.

**Evaluation outcomes:**
- **35–45% reduction** in female homicide victimization by male partners in Maryland following successful LAP implementation (Messing et al. program evaluations; MNADV position paper, June 2022).
- **34% drop** in intimate-partner DV homicides in Maryland between July 2007 and June 2012.
- The Lethality Screen correctly identified **93% of women** who experienced severe violence between baseline and 7-month follow-up.
- LAP participants showed increased protective actions (hiding weapons, accessing DV services, seeking protection orders, establishing help codes) compared to controls.

**Primary sources:**
- Messing, J. T., Campbell, J., Wilson, J. S., Brown, S., & Patchell, B. (2017). "The Lethality Screen: The Predictive Validity of an Intimate Partner Violence Risk Assessment for Use by First Responders." *Journal of Interpersonal Violence* 32(2): 205–226.
- Messing, J. T., Campbell, J., et al. (2015). "Police Departments' Use of the Lethality Assessment Program: A Quasi-Experimental Evaluation." NIJ grant report, via VAWnet.
- Maryland Network Against Domestic Violence. *Position Paper: Effectiveness of the Lethality Assessment Program* (June 2022).
- Office of Justice Programs. "Lethality Assessment Program Maryland Model For First Responders."

**Evidence quality: STRONG. One of the few IPV interventions with population-level homicide-reduction signal in quasi-experimental evaluation.**

### 4.4 When Safety Planning Works and When It Fails

The research base is clear on several boundary conditions:

- Safety planning is **most effective when combined with advocacy** — individual-level safety planning without connection to resources is significantly less effective (Sullivan & Bybee, 1999; Bell & Goodman, 2001).
- Safety planning is **less effective or counterproductive without confidentiality** — if the plan is discovered by the abuser, it can precipitate escalation. Hence the emphasis on safe storage, code words, and separation of document copies.
- Safety planning **does not eliminate lethality risk** during the separation window (see Section 5), and in some cases the plan itself triggers the highest-risk escalation.
- The empirical work on **tailored, danger-informed safety planning** (Glass et al., 2017, on the MyPlan app) shows that matching the plan to the DA risk level produces better outcomes than generic plans.

**Key citations:**
- Bell, M. E., & Goodman, L. A. (2001). "Supporting Battered Women Involved in the Court System: An Evaluation of a Law School–Based Advocacy Intervention." *Violence Against Women* 7(12): 1377–1404.
- Glass, N. E., Perrin, N. A., Hanson, G. C., Bloom, T. L., Messing, J. T., Clough, A. S., et al. (2017). "The Longitudinal Impact of an Internet Safety Decision Aid for Abused Women." *American Journal of Preventive Medicine* 52(5): 606–615.

## Section 5: Lethality and Escalation Indicators

### 5.1 Non-Fatal Strangulation as the Highest-Predictive Single Indicator

**The landmark study:** Glass, N., Laughon, K., Campbell, J., Block, C. R., Hanson, G., Sharps, P. W., & Taliaferro, E. (2008). "Non-Fatal Strangulation Is an Important Risk Factor for Homicide of Women." *Journal of Emergency Medicine* 35(3): 329–335. DOI: 10.1016/j.jemermed.2007.02.065. (PMC2573025)

**Key findings:**
- Non-fatal strangulation was reported in **10% of abused controls, 45% of attempted homicides, and 43% of completed homicides.**
- Prior non-fatal strangulation was associated with **>6× odds (OR 6.70, 95% CI 3.91–11.49)** of becoming an attempted homicide case.
- Prior non-fatal strangulation was associated with **>7× odds (OR 7.48, 95% CI 4.53–12.35)** of becoming a completed homicide case.

**Policy consequence:** As of 2023, all 50 US states have criminalized non-fatal strangulation as a felony (or equivalent aggravated assault), largely on the basis of this research. Clinical consensus: any report of strangulation triggers immediate high-risk safety planning and medical evaluation (carotid artery damage, delayed mortality risk).

**Additional citations:**
- Training Institute on Strangulation Prevention (strangulationtraininginstitute.com) — clinical protocols.
- Pritchard, A. J., Reckdenwald, A., & Nordham, C. (2017). "Nonfatal Strangulation as Part of Domestic Violence: A Review of Research." *Trauma, Violence, & Abuse* 18(4): 407–424.

### 5.2 Separation as the Highest-Risk Period

The research consensus is unambiguous: **leaving is the single most dangerous act a survivor can perform**, in terms of near-term lethality risk.

- **Campbell et al. (2003)** found that **44% of women murdered by an intimate partner had separated or were in the process of leaving**.
- Daly, M., Wiseman, K. A., & Wilson, M. I. (1997). "Women with Children Sired by Previous Partners Incur Excess Risk of Uxoricide." *Homicide Studies* 1(1): 61–71. Establishes separation and presence of stepchildren as co-elevating risk factors.
- Wilson, M., & Daly, M. (1993). "Spousal Homicide Risk and Estrangement." *Violence and Victims* 8(1): 3–16. Canadian homicide data show the estrangement effect clearly.
- **Adjusted odds ratio of 4.07** for femicide under the combination of high control × separation (Campbell et al., 2003).

**Why this matters for downstream guidance:** The research requires that any resource directing a survivor to "leave" also direct them to safety-plan the leaving, ideally with an advocate, ideally informed by a DA-based risk assessment. "Just leave" is empirically unsafe advice. The post-separation period is also associated with stalking, legal-system abuse, and post-separation control (Spearman, Hardesty, Campbell, 2023; post-separation abuse literature review PMC11114442).

### 5.3 Stalking as IPV-Adjacent and IPV-Embedded

**Primary work:** Logan, T. K., & Walker, R. Kentucky stalking research program.

- Logan, T. K., & Walker, R. (2017). "Stalking: A Multidimensional Framework for Assessment and Safety Planning." *Trauma, Violence, & Abuse* 18(2): 200–222. DOI: 10.1177/1524838015603210.
- Logan, T. K., & Walker, R. (2009). "Partner Stalking: Psychological Dominance or 'Business as Usual'?" *Trauma, Violence, & Abuse* 10(3): 247–270.
- Logan, T. K., Cole, J., Shannon, L., & Walker, R. (2006). *Partner Stalking: How Women Respond, Cope, and Survive.* Springer.

**Key findings:**
- **Over half (61%) of female stalking victims** and **44% of male stalking victims** report that the stalker is a current or former intimate partner (NISVS data).
- Logan and Walker's five dimensions distinguishing partner stalking: (1) prior history between perpetrator and victim, (2) greater variety and frequency of available tactics (shared knowledge, shared networks), (3) increased likelihood of threats and violence, (4) earlier onset (often before separation), (5) greater psychological toll.
- Women reporting partner stalking have more severe violence histories, more distress, more fear, and more protective-order violations.

### 5.4 Other Validated Risk Factors

From the IPV risk assessment literature (Campbell 2003; ODARA; SARA):

- Access to firearms (single strongest gun-specific predictor)
- Prior threats with weapon
- Threats to kill
- Escalating severity/frequency
- Violent jealousy
- Forced sex
- Abuser suicide threats or attempts
- Unemployment of the abuser
- Abuse during pregnancy
- Child in home not biologically the abuser's
- Controlling most of the victim's daily activities

**Risk assessment instruments in the validated literature:**
- **Danger Assessment (DA)** — Campbell (gold standard for IPV femicide prediction)
- **Ontario Domestic Assault Risk Assessment (ODARA)** — Hilton et al. (actuarial, brief)
- **Spousal Assault Risk Assessment (SARA)** — Kropp, Hart, Webster, Eaves (structured professional judgment)
- **Lethality Screen (LS)** — Messing et al. (derived from DA, for first responders)

## Section 6: The "Should I Stay or Should I Leave" Question

### 6.1 Why Leaving Is Hard: Documented Barriers

The research literature documents the barriers to leaving explicitly and empirically. The commonly cited statistic — **a survivor makes on average 7 attempts to leave before finally leaving for good** — is attributed to the National Domestic Violence Hotline and repeated in secondary literature. It should be cited with care: the "7 attempts" figure appears to originate in NDVH practitioner data rather than a single peer-reviewed study, though the underlying phenomenon (repeated attempts before final separation) is robustly documented.

- 66.3% of women in one IPV shelter sample reported at least one prior separation-and-return, and 97.1% of those reported multiple (Khaw & Hardesty, 2007, and related research).

**Documented barrier categories** (NDVH "50 Obstacles to Leaving"; also summarized in Kim & Gray 2008, Anderson & Saunders 2003):
- Financial dependence / economic abuse
- Housing insecurity
- Immigration status (deportation as a weapon)
- Children / custody fears
- Religious and cultural pressure
- Love and attachment (especially during honeymoon phase)
- Fear of lethal retaliation (empirically justified — see Section 5.2)
- Isolation from support network
- Disability / dependence
- Pets (abuser threats to harm pets)
- Legal complexity and cost
- Shame and self-blame

**Evidence quality: STRONG for barriers list; MODERATE for the specific "7 attempts" number.**

### 6.2 Couples Therapy: Contraindicated When Coercive Control Is Present

**This is one of the strongest research consensus positions in the field.** Traditional couples/marital therapy is widely regarded as **contraindicated** in relationships involving coercive control or severe intimate partner violence, for the following reasons:

- Couples therapy assumes approximately equal power and equal safety to speak honestly — assumptions violated by coercive control.
- The victim cannot truthfully disclose in session without later retaliation.
- The therapeutic framing of "communication problems" or "both contribute" can collude with the abuser's minimization/blaming tactics (Duluth wheel category 5).
- Sessions can provide the abuser with new information about what the victim has shared with the therapist.

**Primary sources:**
- Bograd, M., & Mederos, F. (1999). "Battering and Couples Therapy: Universal Screening and Selection of Treatment Modality." *Journal of Marital and Family Therapy* 25(3): 291–312. DOI: 10.1111/j.1752-0606.1999.tb00249.x. — Landmark framework; advocates universal screening and treatment-modality gating.
- Stith, S. M., McCollum, E. E., Amanor-Boadu, Y., & Smith, D. (2012). "Systemic Perspectives on Intimate Partner Violence Treatment." *Journal of Marital and Family Therapy* 38(1): 220–240. — Notes that some specialized conjoint treatments (e.g., Domestic Violence–Focused Couple Treatment) may be appropriate for carefully-screened situational violence cases but not for intimate terrorism / coercive control.
- Karakurt, G., Whiting, K., van Esch, C., Bolen, S. D., & Calabrese, J. R. (2016). "Couples Therapy for Intimate Partner Violence: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis." *Journal of Marital and Family Therapy* 42(4): 567–583. (PMC5050084).

**Practical consensus:** Couples therapy is generally contraindicated when coercive control is present. Specialized, screened, safety-focused conjoint approaches exist for situational violence but require advanced training and explicit screening protocols.

### 6.3 Stages of Change Applied to Leaving Abuse

**Prochaska, J. O., & DiClemente, C. C. (1983).** "Stages and Processes of Self-Change in Smoking: Toward an Integrative Model of Change." *Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology* 51(3): 390–395.

The transtheoretical model (TTM) — precontemplation → contemplation → preparation → action → maintenance (plus relapse) — has been applied to leaving abusive relationships by:

- Brown, J. (1997). "Working Toward Freedom From Violence: The Process of Change in Battered Women." *Violence Against Women* 3(1): 5–26.
- Burke, J. G., Denison, J. A., Gielen, A. C., McDonnell, K. A., & O'Campo, P. (2004). "Ending Intimate Partner Violence: An Application of the Transtheoretical Model." *American Journal of Health Behavior* 28(2): 122–133.
- Shurman, L. A., & Rodriguez, C. M. (2006). "Cognitive-Behavioral Stages of Change Related to Partner Abuse." *Journal of Interpersonal Violence*.

**Key empirical finding:** Burke et al. reported that ~95% of abused women in their sample were in precontemplation or contemplation stages at study entry — meaning interventions should match the stage, and attempts to push someone in precontemplation straight to action are likely to fail.

**Evidence quality: MODERATE. TTM application to IPV has face validity and some quantitative support, but the model was designed for individual health behaviors and its fit with a dyadic, socially embedded decision is debated.**

## Section 7: Workplace Bullying Research

### 7.1 The Workplace Bullying Institute and the Namies' Work

**Founders:** Gary Namie, PhD, and Ruth Namie, PhD. WBI founded 1997.

**Core definition (WBI):** Workplace bullying is "repeated, health-harming mistreatment of one or more persons (the targets) by one or more perpetrators that takes one or more of the following forms: verbal abuse; offensive conduct/behaviors (including nonverbal) which are threatening, humiliating, or intimidating; work interference — sabotage — which prevents work from getting done."

**Prevalence findings (WBI US Workplace Bullying Surveys, 2007–2024):**
- Approximately **19% of Americans report having been bullied at work**; another 19% have witnessed it; ~63–75% are aware workplace bullying exists.
- **70% of bullies are men; 30% are women.** Targets are disproportionately women (~66%).

**Primary sources:**
- Namie, G., & Namie, R. F. (2009). *The Bully at Work: What You Can Do to Stop the Hurt and Reclaim Your Dignity on the Job* (2nd ed.). Sourcebooks.
- Namie, G. (2021, 2024). *WBI U.S. Workplace Bullying Survey.* Workplace Bullying Institute.
- Workplace Bullying Institute research page: workplacebullying.org/wbi-research.

### 7.2 Health Impacts

Documented in peer-reviewed literature:

- **Cardiovascular:** Hypertension (associated with ~60% of sustained targets in some samples), coronary heart disease, increased stroke risk, and all-cause mortality effects in long-term cohort studies (Kivimäki et al., 2003, *Occupational and Environmental Medicine*).
- **Mental health:** Elevated PTSD symptoms, major depression, anxiety disorders, suicidal ideation (Nielsen & Einarsen, 2012, *Work & Stress*, meta-analysis).
- **Cognitive/occupational:** Reduced job performance, sleep disruption, cognitive impairment, increased errors.
- **Patient safety (healthcare context):** Bullying is linked to measurable degradation of patient care (Rosenstein & O'Daniel, 2008).

**Key citations:**
- Nielsen, M. B., & Einarsen, S. (2012). "Outcomes of Exposure to Workplace Bullying: A Meta-Analytic Review." *Work & Stress* 26(4): 309–332.
- Kivimäki, M., Virtanen, M., Vartia, M., Elovainio, M., Vahtera, J., & Keltikangas-Järvinen, L. (2003). "Workplace Bullying and the Risk of Cardiovascular Disease and Depression." *Occupational and Environmental Medicine* 60(10): 779–783.
- Mikkelsen, E. G., Hansen, Å. M., Persson, R., Byrgesen, M. F., & Hogh, A. (2020). "Individual Consequences of Being Exposed to Workplace Bullying." In *Bullying and Harassment in the Workplace* (3rd ed.), CRC Press.
- PMC article: "Workplace Bullying: A Tale of Adverse Consequences" (PMC4382139).

### 7.3 "5% Who Do 95% of the Bullying"

This figure is a **clinical/practitioner claim** associated with workplace bullying advocacy, not a rigorously validated finding. Empirical prevalence studies generally find 4–9% of workers identified as perpetrators in any given window (self-label method) or 8–10% (behavioral method). The "5% who do 95%" framing captures the heavy-tailed distribution in which a small number of chronic perpetrators account for most reported incidents, but the specific 5/95 numbers should be cited as **practitioner framing, not peer-reviewed prevalence.**

### 7.4 Effective vs. Ineffective Workplace Interventions

**What the research supports:**
- Zero-tolerance policies **with enforcement** (policy alone without enforcement has no effect).
- Leadership modeling and training.
- Clear reporting channels with protection from retaliation.
- Early intervention by third parties (not just HR, which is structurally conflicted).
- Target support resources (EAP, counseling, documentation assistance).

**What the research does not support:**
- Mediation between target and bully (parallel to couples therapy contraindication — assumes equal power).
- Training-only interventions without structural change.
- HR-only investigations without independent oversight.

**Key citations:**
- Salin, D. (2008). "The Prevention of Workplace Bullying as a Question of Human Resource Management." *Scandinavian Journal of Management* 24(3): 221–231.
- Escartín, J. (2016). "Insights into Workplace Bullying: Psychosocial Drivers and Effective Interventions." *Psychology Research and Behavior Management* 9: 157–169.
- Leiter, M. P., Laschinger, H. K., Day, A., & Oore, D. G. (2011). "The Impact of Civility Interventions on Employee Social Behavior, Distress, and Attitudes." *Journal of Applied Psychology* 96(6): 1258–1274.

### 7.5 Documentation in Workplace Harassment Contexts

Legal and practitioner literature converges on the following principles (as synthesized from employment-law practitioner guides; not randomized trials, but the legal-evidence standards are robust):

1. **Contemporaneous documentation** carries substantially more weight than reconstructed memory.
2. **Specificity matters:** date, time, location, people present, exact words/actions. Generalizations ("he was hostile") are weak; quotes are strong.
3. **Preserve multiple evidence types:** emails, texts, Slack, voicemails, meeting recordings where legal, written communications from the perpetrator.
4. **Store off-work systems** (personal email/cloud) — employer-owned systems can be wiped or surveilled.
5. **Pattern documentation** is what prevails in hostile-environment cases — single incidents rarely suffice legally.
6. **Witness identification** at time of incident, not retroactively.

**Evidence quality: ESTABLISHED LEGAL PRACTICE, not randomized trials. The recommendations are grounded in evidentiary law rather than outcome RCTs.**

### 9.1 WHO Guidelines — Key Recommendations

**Source:** WHO (2013). *Responding to Intimate Partner Violence and Sexual Violence Against Women: WHO Clinical and Policy Guidelines.* ISBN 9789241548595. Updated recommendations through 2021.

- **Do not conduct universal screening** for IPV in healthcare settings (WHO position; contrasts with some US/USPSTF recommendations, which were updated to support screening of women of reproductive age). Instead, conduct **case-finding** when presenting conditions are associated with possible IPV.
- Survivors should be offered **cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT)** or **eye movement desensitization and reprocessing (EMDR)** for PTSD.
- Healthcare providers should deliver **"LIVES" first-line support** (Listen, Inquire about needs, Validate, Enhance safety, Support through referrals).
- Do not mandate reporting by healthcare workers against the survivor's wishes, except for child abuse and specified safety contexts.

### 9.2 CDC NISVS — Methodology and Use

**Source:** CDC NISVS 2016/2017 Report; 2023/2024 methodological redesign.

- NISVS is the US federal standard for IPV/SV/stalking surveillance.
- 2023/2024 cycle shifted to Address-Based Sampling (ABS) with push-to-web plus optional telephone mode; collected September 2023 – September 2024; N = 15,609 (8,842 women, 6,767 men); 16.5% response rate, 72.1% cooperation rate.
- Provides lifetime and 12-month prevalence for contact sexual violence, stalking, psychological aggression, physical violence, and control of reproductive/sexual health by intimate partners.
- Key finding: nearly half of women and more than 1 in 6 men in the US report some form of contact sexual violence in their lifetimes.

**Citable source:** CDC NISVS documentation pages and methodological reports at cdc.gov/nisvs.

### 9.3 SAMHSA Trauma-Informed Care: Six Principles

**Source:** SAMHSA (2014). *SAMHSA's Concept of Trauma and Guidance for a Trauma-Informed Approach.* HHS Publication SMA14-4884.

The six principles:
1. **Safety**
2. **Trustworthiness and Transparency**
3. **Peer Support**
4. **Collaboration and Mutuality**
5. **Empowerment, Voice, and Choice**
6. **Cultural, Historical, and Gender Issues**

These are **framework-level** (how to organize a system), not strategy-level (what to do in a specific case). For downstream hub pages, treat SAMHSA six-principles as the ethical/organizational frame around any specific tactic drawn from Sections 1–8.

## Section 10: Gaslighting as a Specific Pragmatic Mechanism

### 10.1 Sweet's Sociological Reframe

**Sweet, P. L. (2019).** "The Sociology of Gaslighting." *American Sociological Review* 84(5): 851–875. DOI: 10.1177/0003122419874843.

Sweet's central argument: gaslighting is not a purely psychological phenomenon residing in one person's pathology; it is a *sociological* phenomenon that becomes effective when perpetrators mobilize **gender-based stereotypes and structural inequalities** against the victim. The perpetrator's ability to successfully define reality depends on external social scaffolding (cultural scripts about female irrationality, psychiatric labels, etc.).

**For the autism hub, this is directly applicable:** structural inequalities based on **neurotype** function analogously to gender. The cultural script of "autistic people misread social situations, miss sarcasm, and take things too literally" is a ready-made scaffolding for gaslighting. Sweet's framework explains *why* autistic people are structurally vulnerable to gaslighting even before any individual abuser appears.

**Key characteristics of gaslighting per Sweet:**
- Tactics rely on associating the victim's identity (woman, autistic, trauma survivor, etc.) with irrationality.
- Gaslighting is power-laden and executed in intimate or asymmetric relationships.
- Targets experience loss of trust in their own memory and perception — the pragmatic infrastructure of selfhood.

**Evidence quality: STRONG conceptually; FOUNDATIONAL for a pragmatics-of-manipulation framework. Sweet's article has 299+ citations (as of 2023) and is widely adopted.**

### 10.2 Gaslighting ↔ Duluth Wheel Category 5

Sweet's framework makes explicit what Duluth's "Minimizing, Denying, and Blaming" wedge had already observed: the abuser's denial of events, attribution of blame to the victim, and minimization of severity *are* gaslighting. The two literatures converge.

## Section 11: What's Known, What's Contested, What's Missing

### 11.1 What the Field Considers ESTABLISHED

- Coercive control is a **pattern-based phenomenon**, not merely a sum of incidents (Stark; legislative adoption in UK/Scotland/Ireland).
- **Non-fatal strangulation** is the single highest-risk individual behavioral predictor of intimate-partner femicide (Glass et al.).
- **Separation** is the highest-risk period; separation + prior control multiplicatively increases femicide risk (Campbell et al., Wilson & Daly).
- **Safety planning paired with advocacy** reduces revictimization (Sullivan & Bybee; LAP evaluations).
- **Couples therapy is contraindicated** when coercive control is present (research consensus).
- **Workplace bullying causes measurable health harm** (Nielsen & Einarsen meta-analysis; cardiovascular cohort studies).
- **Gaslighting operates through social-structural scaffolding**, not merely individual psychopathology (Sweet).
- **Contemporaneous documentation** is the legal-evidentiary standard for pattern cases.
- **Disclosure outcomes are asymmetric** — negative reactions harm more than positive reactions help (Ullman; systematic reviews).

### 11.2 What the Field Considers CONTESTED

- The **treatment-model effectiveness** of the Duluth Model for batterer rehabilitation (meta-analyses show small effects).
- The **scientific status** of Battered Woman Syndrome as a distinct diagnosis (largely superseded by trauma frameworks).
- **Universal screening** for IPV in healthcare (WHO says no; USPSTF says yes for some groups).
- Whether **specialized conjoint treatment** can ever safely be used for situational couple violence, or whether all IPV should be excluded from couples work.
- The "**7 attempts to leave**" statistic (widely cited; empirical origin less robust than the underlying phenomenon).
- The "**5% do 95% of workplace bullying**" claim (practitioner framing; empirical distribution is heavy-tailed but not rigorously 5/95).

### 11.3 What Is MISSING from the Research Base

- **Autistic samples:** The overwhelming majority of IPV, coercive control, and gaslighting research was conducted on neurotypical samples. Autistic-specific prevalence and vulnerability research is beginning to emerge (Douglas & Sedgewick 2024; Weir et al. 2021; Pecora et al. systematic reviews 2023) but strategy-level research is nearly nonexistent. Autistic adults appear to face **40–90% lifetime victimization rates** across categories, with cis-female and gender-minority autistics at highest risk.
- **LGBTQ+ samples**, particularly for coercive control specifically. A 2024 scoping review (PMC11545122) flagged the paucity of research. Legal help-seeking research is newer (Ball et al. 2023, *Journal of Family Violence*).
- **Racial and immigrant populations:** The Duluth Model's original sample was disproportionately white and working-class; adaptations exist (e.g., Incite!, Creative Interventions) but have less formal validation.
- **Disability-specific IPV research** beyond Baylor College of Medicine's Center for Research on Women with Disabilities.
- **Pure-pragmatic-abuse research:** There is essentially no dedicated empirical literature on coercive control operating exclusively through verbal/pragmatic mechanisms without physical violence at all. Stark's framework *accommodates* such cases theoretically, but most empirical samples have physical violence in the history.
- **Strategy-level RCTs:** Many recommended self-protection strategies (reality-check networks, journaling, boundary-scripts) have never been tested in a randomized trial specific to abuse contexts. They rest on adjacent literatures (social support and trauma recovery; clinical-boundary literature; crisis-intervention training) from which their application to abuse is inferred but not directly validated.
- **Outcomes of the coercive control laws:** Early UK/Scotland evaluations are beginning to appear, but the long-term protective effects of criminalization remain under study.

### 11.4 Implications for the Autism Pragmatics Hub

Given the gaps:

1. **Every strategy page should carry a "research base" note** indicating which evidence tier the strategy sits in (strong empirical / moderate / contested / framework-level / practitioner wisdom).
2. **Autistic readers should be explicitly told** that the underlying research rarely includes autistic samples and that adaptation is necessary and individual.
3. **Lethality indicators should be prominent** — strangulation, separation-as-risk, weapon access — regardless of how "minor" the rest of the pattern seems.
4. **The Danger Assessment itself is not a DIY tool** — directing autistic readers to a trained advocate for DA-informed risk assessment is the only responsible path. The hub can point to the assessment's existence and validate its legitimacy; it should not replace advocate contact.
5. **"Leave" is empirically unsafe as standalone advice.** Any page that raises leaving must pair it with safety planning and an advocate referral.
6. **Gaslighting content should lean on Sweet's structural framing** — it lets autistic readers understand why they are disproportionately targeted without pathologizing their cognitive style.
7. **Coercive control's "fights vs. control" distinction** (Stark) may be the single most useful concept for autistic readers who have been told that their difficulties are "mutual communication problems."
