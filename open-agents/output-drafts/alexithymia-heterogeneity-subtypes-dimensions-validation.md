# Self-Validation Notes — Heterogeneity Category

## Summary

This category synthesizes the seven heterogeneity axes identified in the parent exploration, with corrections applied per the validation supplement. The validator-flagged YELLOW status concerns the framing of:
1. LPA cluster subtypes (must reframe as severity continuum, not validated four-class)
2. Type II / psychosis vulnerability (real-as-published from one Dutch lineage; no prospective transition data)
3. Type I / Type II categorical structure (dimensions robust; categorical types weak)
4. Parsing-deficit vs detection-deficit attribution (corrected per validator)

This document flags every contested claim and how it is framed in the concept research, plus citation-accuracy checkpoints for the build phase.

## Contested-claim flags by axis

### Axis 1 (TAS-20 facets)
- **Claim**: DIF and DDF are correlated; EOT dissociates.
  - **Strength**: Strong. Replicated across many factor analyses.
  - **Source**: Schroeders 2022 meta-analytic CFA (PMC9597132).
- **Claim**: EOT factor is unstable cross-culturally.
  - **Strength**: Strong. Schroeders meta-CFA documents poor cross-cultural invariance for EOT specifically.
- **Claim**: TAS-20 covers only negative emotion.
  - **Strength**: Strong. Item content review confirms.

### Axis 2 (Bermond Type I/II)
- **Claim**: The two-dimensional cognitive-affective BVAQ structure replicates.
  - **Strength**: Strong. Vorst & Bermond 2001; multiple replications.
- **Claim**: The categorical Type I/II distinction has weaker empirical support than the underlying dimensions.
  - **Strength**: Strong. A five-factor BVAQ model fits better than higher-order two-factor.
  - **Framing in research**: Presented as "dimensions robust, categorical types weak" — accurate.
- **Claim**: Type II → psychosis vulnerability.
  - **Strength**: MODERATE — real-as-published. **YELLOW FLAG**.
  - **Sources**: Larøi 2008; Cedro 2001; van der Velde 2015 (PMC4451258, the strongest single piece — parametric-cohort gradient cog-alex 48.8 → 55.2 → 68.0 → 64.6 across controls/siblings/UHR/patients); Ozdemir 2025 (PMC12050105) systematic review.
  - **Hedges applied**:
    - "from one research lineage (Polish, Dutch)"
    - "no prospective ultra-high-risk transition data"
    - "the categorical Type II distinction itself is weakly supported"
    - "Cedro 2001 not 2015"
    - The cognitive-alexithymia *dimension* is the more defensible claim, not the Type II categorical typology.

### Axis 3 (Primary/Secondary/Organic)
- **Claim**: Messina et al. 2014 framework.
  - **Strength**: Useful clinical heuristic, NOT large empirical study. Framing in research correctly notes this.
- **Claim**: Heritability ~30% (Danish) to ~42% (Italian).
  - **Strength**: Strong. Two large twin studies.
- **Claim**: Anterior insula damage >15% reliably produces acquired alexithymia.
  - **Strength**: Strong. Hogeveen 2016 (PMC4752907), Vietnam-veteran cohort.

### Axis 4 (LPA / cluster subtypes) — **YELLOW FLAG**
- **Claim**: Severity continuum is better-supported than the Chen four-class scheme.
  - **Strength**: Moderate-to-strong. Multiple recent LPA/FMM studies (Schaeuffele 2021, Hogrefe FMM 2025, 2025 nursing students, 2025 elderly) all yield 2-3 class severity-distinguished solutions, not the four-class profile differentiation.
  - **Framing in research**: Reframed as severity continuum throughout. Chen labels presented as "descriptive language people sometimes find helpful" but explicitly NOT validated discrete entities.
  - **Hogrefe 2025 specific framing**: 2-class Chinese-only finding (High-EOT 18.2% / Non-alex 81.8%). NOT presented as Chinese-vs-German comparison.

### Axis 5 (State vs Trait)
- **Claim**: Strong relative stability over 11 years.
  - **Strength**: Strong. Tolmunen 2011 (PubMed 21081227) and 2017 (PubMed 28314554) — Finnish Health 2000/2011 follow-up, N=3,083, mean TAS-20=44.2 in both years.
- **Claim**: State component fluctuates with depression severity.
  - **Strength**: Strong. Documented in multiple studies of depression-alexithymia comorbidity.
- **Claim**: Inpatient psychotherapy can produce measurable change.
  - **Strength**: Strong. Cameron 2024 systematic review (PMC11673933) — medium effect sizes across 21 RCT arms.

### Axis 6 (Interoceptive split) — **YELLOW FLAG**
- **Claim**: Three positions in the literature (detection / parsing / hypervigilance).
  - **Strength**: Descriptive frame; widely used. Accurate as a categorization of positions.
- **Claim**: Bird/Murphy/Brewer lineage continues to argue multidomain interoception failure.
  - **Strength**: Strong. Brewer 2016, Murphy 2018ab, Brewer Murphy Bird 2021 (PMC8522807) all affirm.
  - **Critical correction applied**: NOT a "Bird walked back" framing. The autism-specific claim was walked back; the alexithymia-multidomain claim was strengthened.
- **Claim**: PMC6089261 is the strongest no-association challenge.
  - **Strength**: Strong, but **must be correctly attributed to Nicholson, Williams, Grainger, Christensen, Calvo-Merino, Gaigg (2018) — INDEPENDENT group, NOT Bird/Murphy/Catmur**.
  - **Framing in research**: Explicitly noted as third-party challenge, NOT internal walk-back.
- **Claim**: Parsing-deficit reading from Garfinkel-3D + Aaron 2020 quadratic.
  - **Strength**: Real findings.
  - **Critical correction applied**: Aaron, Blain, Snodgress, Park 2020 (NOT Trevisan/Mehling/McPartland) — verified.
- **Claim**: Hobson 2019 multiroute model is the most defensible synthesis.
  - **Strength**: Strong as the most recent synthesis from the Bird group.
  - **Source**: Hobson, Brewer, Catmur, Bird (2019) Emotion Review.

### Axis 7 (Cognitive vs Affective BVAQ)
- **Claim**: The two-dimensional structure is the most replicated structural BVAQ finding.
  - **Strength**: Strong. Multiple language replications.
- **Claim**: TAS-20 measures mainly cognitive dimension.
  - **Strength**: Strong. Item content overlap supports.

## Framing checkpoints

The category page must, throughout:
1. **Present all sides** — interoception positions, Type II evidence strength, LPA replication status all framed even-handedly.
2. **Weight proportional to evidence** — strong findings as strong; weak/contested as such.
3. **Hold alexithymia as a trait with trade-offs where applicable** — the page is the spine, so user-selected framings (especially the trait-with-trade-offs frame) must be available, though the trade-off content lives more in Category 11.
4. **Conversational, not clinical-textbook** — present complex distinctions in accessible language, anchor with patient phenomenology where useful.
5. **Decision-tree as navigation, not diagnosis** — user requested.

## Open questions deferred

These open questions appear in the category but are not resolved here:
- Whether the multiroute model (Hobson 2019) admits a third route (autistic perceptual/interoceptive architecture distinct from alexithymia-as-language and alexithymia-as-interoception).
- Whether the parsing-deficit framework actually carves alexithymia at a useful joint vs the multiroute model's carving.
- Cross-cultural construct validity of TAS-20 in non-Western populations — continues unresolved.
- Whether elevated alexithymia in first responders is protective or sequela — contested.
