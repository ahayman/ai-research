# Category 23: Framing and Frame Analysis -- Autism-Specific Research

## Overview

Framing is load-bearing for autistic readers in ways that it is not for most non-autistic readers. Three reasons converge. First, autistic cognition appears to weight bottom-up perceptual detail and top-down prior context differently from non-autistic cognition (Pellicano & Burr, 2012; Vermeulen, 2012; Palmer, Lawson, & Hohwy, 2017), which means that ambient frame cues may be processed less automatically and more effortfully. Second, the dominant non-autistic culture frames autism in ways that are often inaccurate and that get installed on autistic people via records, assessments, diagnoses, and institutional encounters -- which means that autistic adults frequently find themselves inside pre-installed frames they did not choose and did not endorse. Third, the manipulation patterns catalogued in the Pragmatic Manipulation Sub-Hub operate disproportionately through frame moves against autistic targets, because the non-autistic norms that frame autism as a deficit provide ready-made scaffolding for exploitation (Douglas & Sedgewick, 2024; Pearson et al., 2024).

This file takes the theoretical and practical material from the other two research files and applies it specifically to autistic experience. The default theoretical stance is Milton's (2012) *double empathy problem*: frame mismatches between autistic and non-autistic interlocutors are bidirectional, not one-sided. Neither group is "bad at framing"; the two groups are operating with different frame-repertoires, and the mismatch belongs to the mismatch, not to either party alone. This reframe is foundational and is the default across the entire Pragmatics Learning Hub.

The file is organized around several intersecting questions: what the neurocognitive research says about autistic frame-processing (without treating any of that research as a deficit story); how masking relates to frame-performance; how the non-autistic culture's frames of autism get installed on autistic people through records and assessments; how frame attacks operate in autism-specific abuse and manipulation contexts; and what protective use autistic readers can make of frame analysis.

## 1. THE DOUBLE EMPATHY PROBLEM AND FRAMING

### 1.1 Milton's Reframe

Damian Milton's 2012 paper "On the ontological status of autism: the 'double empathy problem'" is the foundational reframe for how the hub discusses autism and communication. Milton's argument is that interactions between autistic and non-autistic people involve bidirectional mismatch, not unidirectional deficit. The non-autistic person misreads the autistic person just as the autistic person misreads the non-autistic person. Each is operating with a set of expectations that the other does not share. The mismatch is symmetric, and calling it a symptom of autism is a one-sided description of a two-sided phenomenon.

Milton's (2012) original formulation: "A 'double problem of empathy' occurs then, when people of different dispositional outlooks and personal conceptual understandings attempt to interact" (p. 884). The problem is dispositional and conceptual -- which is to say, at the level of the frames each party brings to the interaction. The autistic person's frames of what the interaction is, what is being communicated, what counts as appropriate, what the next action should be, may differ systematically from the non-autistic person's frames. Neither set of frames is wrong. Both are internally coherent and each works well within its own group. The mismatch is where the trouble is.

This is exactly a frame-analytic claim. What Milton is describing is a sustained frame dispute (Goffman, 1974) between two groups that each think their frame is the default. The asymmetry in how this frame dispute has been treated in the clinical literature -- with the autistic frame being pathologized and the non-autistic frame being treated as neutral ground -- is itself a framing choice, one that the double-empathy reframe refuses.

### 1.2 Empirical Support for Double Empathy

The double empathy problem has accumulated substantial empirical support since 2012.

**Crompton et al. (2020)** provided the key demonstration that autistic-to-autistic information transfer is highly effective -- in fact, as effective as non-autistic-to-non-autistic transfer, and more effective than mixed-group transfer. Participants played a "telephone game" (chained retellings of a story) in autistic-only chains, non-autistic-only chains, and mixed chains. Information loss was similar in the two same-group conditions and significantly higher in the mixed condition. The finding is structurally incompatible with an autistic-deficit account: if autism were a deficit in communication, autistic-to-autistic communication should be the worst of the three conditions. Instead, it is tied for best. The communication breakdown is a mismatch phenomenon, not a one-sided failure.

**Morrison, DeBrabander, Jones, Faso, Ackerman, and Sasson (2020)**, "Outcomes of real-world social interaction for autistic adults paired with autistic compared to typically developing partners" (*Autism* 24(5):1067-1080), showed that autistic adults prefer autistic to non-autistic social partners on a range of measures after brief interaction, and that the preference goes both ways for the autistic participants -- they find autistic interaction more comfortable and lower-effort than mixed interaction. In frame-analytic terms, this matters because it shows that autistic-autistic interaction runs a different set of frame dynamics than autistic-non-autistic interaction: same-group pairs converge on a shared operative frame more quickly and with less effort; cross-group pairs do frame-negotiation that is effortful on both sides. The usual clinical reading -- that autistic people have a deficit in building rapport -- survives only if one ignores the same-group half of the comparison, which shows autistic adults building rapport efficiently with each other. The Morrison data is the empirical backbone of the double-empathy framing of frame mismatch: the mismatch belongs to the pair, not to one member of the pair.

**DeBrabander, Morrison, Jones, Faso, Chmielewski, and Sasson (2019)** showed that first-impression biases against autistic people (based on thin-slice video) are driven by the *non-autistic* raters' judgments; autistic raters do not show the same bias. The frame that "autistic people are hard to read" is itself a frame held disproportionately by non-autistic observers, and it is based on mismatch effects rather than on absolute qualities of the autistic people being rated.

The DeBrabander work builds on **Sasson, Faso, Nugent, Lovell, Kennedy, and Grossman (2017)**, "Neurotypical peers are less willing to interact with those with autism based on thin slice judgments" (*Scientific Reports* 7:40700), which is the seminal finding. Sasson et al. showed that non-autistic observers form less favorable first impressions of autistic adults within seconds of thin-slice exposure -- before any specific content has been exchanged -- and that these negative impressions predicted reduced willingness to engage in further interaction. Crucially, the bias disappeared when the observers were given only the *content* of what the autistic adults said (in text transcript form) without the audio-visual presentation: on content alone, autistic adults were rated at least as favorably as non-autistic controls. The driver of the negative first impression was the presentation style, not the substance. Style is exactly the channel through which a frame is installed in the first seconds of an encounter.

For the autism-specific application of frame analysis, the Sasson finding is foundational. It means that the frame in which an autistic adult will be read by a non-autistic stranger is typically installed *before* the autistic adult has had any opportunity to contribute information that could affect the frame. The installation is complete within the first seconds, it is driven by presentation style rather than content, and it is robustly negative. Subsequent interaction is then interpreted through that pre-installed frame. The autistic adult is not in a neutral frame-negotiation with the observer; they are already inside a frame, and the frame has been installed faster than any deliberate response could have contested it.

This is a specifically frame-analytic phenomenon: a frame installed on a person, without their participation, before the interaction has properly begun. Frame analysis gives vocabulary for what is happening, but the installation itself is too fast to be prevented in real time. What frame analysis does offer is retrospective clarity (the subsequent difficulty is being driven by a pre-installed frame, not by anything the autistic adult did), prospective orientation (disclosure, diagnostic information, and content-first communication channels can partially counter the frame), and structural framing (Sasson and Morrison's follow-up work (2019) showed that disclosure of the autism diagnosis and improved autism knowledge in the audience shifts the thin-slice impression toward more favorable ratings -- the frame is modifiable through audience-side intervention). The implication is that the interventions that would actually make the largest difference to autistic adults' social outcomes are not interventions on the autistic adults; they are interventions on the non-autistic audience's frame-repertoire.

The ethics of frame-installation on strangers belong to the installer, not to the installed-on. The Sasson finding is, in the language of this category, evidence that a frame attack -- an unchosen, inaccurate, consequence-bearing frame -- is happening, and that the autistic person's "failure to make a good first impression" is not a failure but a consequence of the attack.

**Mitchell, Sheppard, and Cassidy (2021)**, in "Autism and the double empathy problem: Implications for development and mental health" (*British Journal of Developmental Psychology* 39(1):1-18), review the evidence linking this systematic mis-framing of autistic adults by non-autistic observers to mental health outcomes. Their argument: being chronically misperceived is associated with poorer mental health and wellbeing in autistic adults, and the misperception is not a failing of autistic people but a consequence of the double empathy problem. The paper is explicit that the frame of autism held by the non-autistic majority is itself contributing to mental health harm, and that attempting to address the mental health outcomes without addressing the frame misses the causal structure.

**Sheppard, Pillai, Wong, Ropar, and Mitchell (2016)** showed that non-autistic participants are worse at reading autistic participants' mental states than they are at reading other non-autistic participants' mental states. The "theory of mind" asymmetry runs in both directions.

**Heasman and Gillespie (2019)** used conversation analysis to show that autistic-autistic interactions display high levels of mutual accommodation and genuine back-and-forth, contrary to stereotypes of autistic talk as one-sided or interview-like.

Taken together, these findings support the double-empathy reframe and the frame-dispute analysis: the communication difficulties that have been clinically attributed to autism are better described as mismatches between two groups, each with its own frame-repertoire.

### 1.3 Implications for This Category

What this means for frame analysis specifically: when an autistic reader and a non-autistic interlocutor are operating with different frames, the research supports an even-handed description in which neither frame is privileged as the default. In practice, of course, social power and institutional authority usually back the non-autistic frame. The autistic reader is often being asked to adopt a frame that is not native to them, while the non-autistic reader is rarely asked to adopt a frame that is not native to them. This asymmetry is not a fact about frames -- it is a fact about power. Frame analysis is the technical tool for seeing the asymmetry and for reclaiming the ground the autistic reader's native frames are entitled to.

## 2. AUTISTIC COGNITION AND FRAME-PROCESSING

### 2.1 Vermeulen's Context-Sensitivity Account

Peter Vermeulen's *Autism as Context Blindness* (2012) offers a non-deficit-framed account of autistic cognition that maps directly onto frame analysis. Vermeulen argues that autistic cognition processes individual elements excellently -- often more accurately than non-autistic cognition -- but has difficulty automatically weighting context. The result is that ambient context cues that non-autistic cognition uses to pre-select an interpretation may be less automatically integrated in autistic cognition.

In frame-analytic terms, what Vermeulen is describing is difficulty with the *automatic frame-selection* step. Frames are normally selected rapidly and non-consciously, based on a combination of cues from the physical setting, the participants, the prior activity, the opening moves, and the vocabulary. Vermeulen's account suggests that autistic cognition does this automatic selection less, and has to do more of the frame-selection work explicitly.

Crucially, Vermeulen's account is not a deficit story. He is at pains to distinguish difficulty with context-*weighting* from failure of context-*perception*. Autistic people perceive context just fine -- often more accurately than non-autistic people. What they do differently is how they weight context relative to individual elements in forming an interpretation. This is a feature of the processing style, not a broken module. It produces systematic strengths (accurate perception, resistance to certain kinds of priming-based errors, better performance in detail-critical tasks) as well as the difficulties that have been more widely discussed.

Vermeulen's book predates most of the later Bayesian-priors literature but is conceptually aligned with it.

### 2.2 Pellicano and Burr: Attenuated Priors

Elizabeth Pellicano and David Burr's 2012 paper in *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* -- "When the world becomes 'too real': A Bayesian explanation of autistic perception" -- gave a formal computational account that is now central in the autism cognitive-science literature. Their core claim: autistic perception may involve **attenuated priors** -- "hypo-priors" -- meaning that autistic cognition weights bottom-up sensory input more heavily and top-down prior expectation more lightly, relative to non-autistic cognition. The consequence, in their phrase, is that the world becomes "too real": perceptions are less modulated by prior experience, more driven by the raw input.

This is the Bayesian translation of Vermeulen's context-blindness account. In a Bayesian model of perception, beliefs about the world are updated by combining prior expectations with new evidence. If the priors are weighted less, the evidence dominates. The perceiver sees what is there more accurately but is more sensitive to unexpected or inconsistent input and less able to predict ahead.

Pellicano and Burr are careful that this is not a deficit account. Attenuated priors produce better perception in many domains, including more accurate color discrimination, better detection of subtle stimulus changes, and resistance to certain visual illusions that depend on top-down influence. The difficulty appears when top-down prediction would have made interpretation easier -- when context is supposed to do heavy lifting that the priors are not doing as heavily.

Subsequent work has extended the framework in several directions. Lawson, Rees, and Friston (2014), in *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience* 8:302, developed the "aberrant precision" account, which formalizes the intuition that autism involves a different weighting of prediction errors relative to priors in the Bayesian update. Palmer, Lawson, and Hohwy (2017), in *Psychological Bulletin* 143(5):521-542, reviewed the full family of Bayesian approaches to autism and argued for a more nuanced reading in which the imbalance varies with task, domain, and volatility of the environment rather than being a single global "hypo-priors" setting. Van de Cruys et al. (2014) proposed that autism involves inflexibly precise prediction errors, a closely related claim. The empirical picture is not fully settled -- recent work suggests the story is more nuanced than a single "hypo-priors" knob -- but the broad framework continues to gain support.

### 2.2.1 Attenuated Priors in Depth: The Frame-Resistance Implication

If the Pellicano-Burr framework is approximately correct, the implication for framing is not trivial. Frames are, computationally, priors about what kind of situation this is and what interpretive schema applies. Frame installation is the act of handing the perceiver a new prior and asking them to let it shape their subsequent processing. A perceiver whose priors are weighted less will be harder to install a frame on unilaterally -- because the perceiver's processing is less dominated by whatever prior is currently at the top of the stack, and more dominated by the actual evidence of what is happening.

This has a non-obvious consequence: autism may confer partial resistance to certain frame attacks specifically because the attack requires top-down acceptance. A frame attack that depends on the target just *taking up* the manipulator's framing and running the rest of the interpretation inside it will work less well when the target is, by default, running more of the interpretation bottom-up. The manipulator has to install the frame repeatedly, explicitly, and with more effort; the target keeps checking the frame against the ongoing input; the frame does not settle into the invisible default state that makes frame attacks most effective.

This is not a claim that autistic people are invulnerable to frame attacks. They clearly are not -- the manipulation sub-hub is full of patterns that operate against autistic targets, and the autistic population is disproportionately victimized in many of them. The claim is more specific: the *default* frame-installation mechanism (the one that works by exploiting automatic, unconscious uptake of top-down priors) is less efficient against autistic cognition. Manipulators adapted to non-autistic targets may find that their usual moves are less sticky, and may respond by amplifying the force of the moves, repeating them, or moving to more blatant forms that operate through coercion rather than through subtle priming. This is consistent with the clinical and community observation that autistic targets of coercive control often describe a manipulation that feels unusually overt, clumsy, or heavy-handed compared to the "gaslit into confusion" experience described in the non-autistic literature.

The cost side is also real: because the top-down-resistance is not selective, it also applies to legitimate ambient context. Autistic cognition processes situational cues effortfully in exactly the contexts in which non-autistic cognition processes them automatically. Social situations, which are saturated with frame cues, exact a processing toll. Bottom-up frame-checking is protective but it is also exhausting.

### 2.2.2 Reframing "Context Blindness"

Vermeulen's phrase "context blindness" has been read in several directions, and at least one of the readings is wrong. The wrong reading is: *autistic people do not see context*. The research does not support this, and Vermeulen himself was at pains to deny it. Autistic people see context; what is different is how it is automatically weighted in frame selection.

A more accurate phrase, and one that is more consistent with both Vermeulen's text and the subsequent Bayesian literature, is that autistic cognition has difficulty with the *automatic top-down weighting* of context in real-time frame selection. This is not "not seeing context" but something closer to "seeing all the contexts at once and not weighting them automatically in favor of the dominant one." Every context that is physically present in the environment is processed. What does not happen as automatically is the rapid pre-selection that picks one of those contexts as the operative frame and down-weights the others.

The practical experience of this, as many autistic adults describe it, is not blindness. It is more like having several contexts simultaneously available and needing to decide, effortfully and usually under time pressure, which one the current interaction "belongs to." A social occasion at a restaurant contains a business frame, a friendship frame, a service-transaction frame, a public-behavior frame, and several others, all overlapping. A non-autistic participant pre-selects (typically friendship or business) in the opening moments and the other frames fade into the background. An autistic participant may be tracking all of them, checking each one against the ongoing cues, and updating with effort when the active frame changes. The result is accurate but costly. This is not a deficit; it is a different distribution of the processing effort.

### 2.2.3 Monotropism and the Deep Channel

A distinct and complementary framework is **monotropism**, developed by Dinah Murray, Mike Lesser, and Wendy Lawson in "Attention, monotropism and the diagnostic criteria for autism" (Murray, Lesser, & Lawson, 2005, *Autism* 9(2):139-156). Monotropism proposes that autistic attention is characterized by deep focus on a narrow attentional channel, with a correspondingly high cost to switching between channels. Where non-autistic attention is commonly described as polytropic -- distributed across multiple simultaneous concerns -- autistic attention is monotropic, engaged deeply with one concern at a time.

For framing specifically, the monotropism framework adds an independent reason for the phenomenology of frame shifts. When an autistic person is deeply engaged with the substance of a conversation -- attending to the words, the logical structure, the implications, the accuracy of the claims -- they are not, in parallel, running a background process that monitors and updates the operative frame. The attentional channel is committed to content; frame monitoring would require either dividing the channel or switching it. Both are expensive under a monotropic attention regime.

This explains part of why frame shifts feel abrupt and disruptive to autistic participants. A frame shift is not just new interpretive work; it is a demand to suspend the current deep-engagement channel, switch to frame-tracking, select a new frame, and then switch back to a content channel that is now operating inside a different frame. The non-autistic participant doing the shift has been running frame-monitoring as a lightweight background process the whole time. The autistic participant has not. What the non-autistic participant experiences as a smooth transition, the autistic participant experiences as a hard context switch with a measurable cost.

Monotropism is not a competing account to the Pellicano-Burr framework; it is complementary. Both describe different facets of the same broad pattern: autistic cognition trades reduced automatic-background processing for increased depth and accuracy in the channel that is currently active. For framing, both implications matter: frame installation is harder to achieve automatically (Pellicano-Burr), and frame shifting is more costly to perform (Murray, Lesser, & Lawson).

### 2.2.4 Enhanced Rationality and Reduced Framing Effects

A recent and highly relevant line of research bears directly on framing specifically. **Rozenkrantz, D'Mello, and Gabrieli (2021)**, in their review "Enhanced rationality in autism spectrum disorder" in *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* 25(8):685-696, synthesize evidence that autistic adults are reduced-susceptibility to a range of classic cognitive biases -- including the framing effect itself. The review covers empirical work showing that autistic participants are less susceptible to:

- **Classic framing effects** (Kahneman and Tversky 1981-style problems, where the same risk is accepted or rejected depending on gain- versus loss-framing). Autistic participants make more frame-consistent decisions regardless of which frame the problem is presented in.
- **The conjunction fallacy** (the Linda problem). Autistic participants are more likely to produce the logically correct answer.
- **Sunk-cost bias.** Autistic participants are less likely to throw good resources after bad ones just because prior investment has been made.
- **Anchoring.** Autistic participants are less swayed by irrelevant initial numerical reference points.
- **Context-dependent preference reversals.** Autistic participants produce more internally consistent rankings of options across contexts.

The pattern is striking enough that the Rozenkrantz-D'Mello-Gabrieli review proposes the umbrella term "enhanced rationality" for it. The causal story they offer connects this to the attenuated-priors account: if autistic cognition is less dominated by top-down priors, then the cognitive shortcuts that produce bias (most of which operate through exactly the kinds of rapid, automatic, prior-driven inference that the Pellicano-Burr framework predicts will be weaker in autism) will fire less.

For frame analysis, this has complex implications. The good news is that autistic readers may, on average, be *less susceptible* to framing manipulations that operate through classic cognitive biases. A gain/loss reframe that would move a non-autistic audience's decision may move an autistic audience's decision less. An emotional appeal that exploits anchoring may anchor less firmly. A chorus-frame that exploits social proof may generate less automatic uptake. Autistic readers may notice frame attacks that non-autistic readers miss.

The complication is that the noticing is cognitively effortful. The Rozenkrantz review is careful that "enhanced rationality" is not costless: it reflects a processing style in which more of the interpretive work is done explicitly and effortfully rather than automatically and cheaply. The same resistance to automatic frame uptake that protects autistic readers from some frame attacks is the same resistance that makes ordinary social interaction exhausting. There is no free lunch in the cognitive trade-off.

A second complication: reduced susceptibility to classic cognitive biases does not mean reduced susceptibility to all frame attacks. Attacks that operate through institutional authority, through physical coercion, through sustained exposure, through relational captivity, or through exploitation of sensory and processing vulnerabilities do not work by hijacking cognitive biases, and nothing in the enhanced-rationality literature implies autistic protection against these. The autism-specific abuse literature (Douglas & Sedgewick, 2024; Pearson et al., 2024) documents the specific manipulation patterns that do work against autistic targets, and they are typically not of the gain/loss-framing-effect type. They are of the engineered-overload type, the weaponized-feature type, the credibility-discount type -- none of which are cognitive-bias attacks in the Rozenkrantz sense.

The useful read: *enhanced rationality is a real protective asset for autistic readers against a specific class of frame attacks, namely the ones that work through classic cognitive biases; it does not generalize to the structural and coercive frame attacks that dominate the autism-specific manipulation literature, and it is paid for in processing effort.* The asset is worth recognizing and worth using; it is not sufficient by itself.

### 2.3 Frame Selection as Prior-Driven

What does this have to do with framing? Frame selection is, in Bayesian terms, a prior-driven process. You walk into a room and your brain rapidly pre-selects a frame based on prior expectations about what kinds of events happen in rooms of that type. The pre-selection is automatic and is rarely noticed. When it is correct, it saves enormous processing effort: the frame pre-loads the relevant vocabulary, relevant expectations, relevant next-actions. When it is wrong, it has to be discarded and replaced, which is the subjective experience of a frame break.

If autistic cognition weights priors less heavily, then automatic frame-selection runs less automatically. Frames may be selected more deliberately, more slowly, or more accurately (when the automatic selection would have been wrong). This is neither better nor worse than the non-autistic pattern in general; it is different, and it produces different strengths and different costs.

The strengths: when a situation is misleading -- when the ambient cues point toward a frame that is not actually the right one -- autistic cognition may be more accurate, because it is less dominated by the priming. When the environment is novel, when cultural scripts do not apply, when attention to detail matters more than top-down expectation, the attenuated-priors style is an advantage. Many autistic people describe the experience of seeing things that other people miss because the others were running a script that the situation did not actually fit. This is frame-selection accuracy, earned by doing the work bottom-up instead of trusting the priors.

The costs: when a situation is standard, when cultural scripts do apply, when the ambient cues are accurate, the extra work of explicit frame-selection is effortful, slower, and more tiring. Social situations, which are saturated in culturally-specific frame cues, often produce this cost. Masking -- the topic of the next section -- is largely the work of manually maintaining frame-performances that are not native.

### 2.4 What the Research Does Not Support

Before proceeding it is worth explicitly stating what the Pellicano-Burr, Rozenkrantz-D'Mello-Gabrieli, Murray-Lesser-Lawson, and Palmer-Lawson-Hohwy frameworks do *not* collectively establish, because the autism cognitive-science literature has a history of being mis-read and the miss-reads cause harm.

The research does not establish that autistic people cannot process context. They can, and they often do, more accurately than non-autistic people when the situation allows for explicit rather than automatic processing. What the research suggests is that the *automatic, implicit, prior-driven* form of context processing is differently weighted.

The research does not establish that autistic people cannot understand frames. Understanding a frame is a cognitive task that autistic cognition is often very good at; it can be done explicitly and analytically, and autistic adults are routinely the most frame-analytic participants in conversations about social dynamics. What the research suggests is that the rapid, implicit, automatic uptake of ambient frame cues is less efficient in autism.

The research does not establish that autistic people are emotionally unresponsive, cognitively rigid, or socially uninterested. These are popular stereotypes; they are not findings. The double-empathy literature (Milton, 2012; Crompton et al., 2020; Morrison et al., 2020; Mitchell, Sheppard, & Cassidy, 2021) is specifically structured around disconfirming them.

The research does not establish that autistic cognition is a deficit relative to non-autistic cognition. It establishes that autistic cognition is different in specifiable ways, that the differences produce different strengths and different costs, that the strengths and costs are distributed across tasks in ways that do not favor one group globally, and that the cultural expectation of automatic frame-tracking is one of the specific contexts in which the autistic processing style is at a cost. The same processing style is at an advantage in many other contexts.

Readers who take the research seriously should treat it as describing a cognitive-style variation, not as describing a disorder in autistic processing. The "disorder" framing is, itself, a framing choice, and one that the autistic community and much of the contemporary research literature have rejected.

### 2.5 The Generous Read

All of this should be read in the generous direction. The research on autistic cognition has historically been framed in deficit terms, and the Pellicano-Burr account has sometimes been misread as another deficit story. The accurate read is that autistic cognition has a distinct processing profile that works excellently for some tasks, less well for others, and that the "less well" cases include some of the same social situations in which the dominant culture expects automatic frame-tracking. This is not a failure of autistic people; it is a feature of their cognition that interacts poorly with a specific set of cultural expectations. Change the cultural expectations (as autistic-affirming environments do) and the "difficulty" largely disappears.

## 3. MASKING AS FRAME-PERFORMANCE WORK

### 3.1 What Masking Is

Masking (also called camouflaging) is the conscious or semi-conscious suppression of autistic behaviors and the performance of non-autistic behaviors in social contexts. The phenomenon has been studied extensively in the past decade, most notably by Hull and colleagues in a qualitative study that gave the concept its most widely-cited articulation: "Putting on my best normal: social camouflaging in adults with autism spectrum conditions" (Hull et al., 2017). Subsequent work (Cage & Troxell-Whitman, 2019; Hull, Petrides, & Mandy, 2020; Lai et al., 2017; Pearson & Rose, 2021; Miller, Rees, & Pearson, 2021) has extended the description and begun to document the costs.

Masking is not a cosmetic adjustment. It involves suppressing comfortable stims, suppressing natural facial expressions, suppressing natural speech patterns, performing scripted social behaviors, monitoring one's own presentation in real time, rehearsing conversations in advance, and constantly checking for the cues that would indicate a frame mismatch or social failure. Hull et al. (2017) found that their participants reported masking being exhausting, identity-damaging, and associated with mental health difficulties. Raymaker et al. (2020), in their study of autistic burnout, identified masking as one of the principal contributors to burnout.

### 3.2 Masking in Frame-Analytic Terms

Framed in the vocabulary of this category, masking is the work of manually maintaining a *non-native frame-performance*. The autistic person is not simply behaving differently; they are running a frame -- typically a non-autistic frame of the current interaction -- that is not their own, while also tracking the underlying interaction, while also monitoring their performance of the frame for slippages. It is three layers of simultaneous cognitive work where the non-autistic person is running one layer.

The non-autistic observer sees a person who is, to some degree, "passing." They do not see the three-layer cognitive process that is producing the passing. The observer then forms the frame that *this person communicates normally*, which becomes the baseline against which any later visible autistic behavior is read as regression, as acting out, or as manipulation.

This is one of the places where frame analysis is most directly useful for autistic readers. The frame the observer has formed (based on masked performance) is not the accurate frame. The accurate frame would include the cost of the performance, the effort required, and the fact that the "normal" presentation was a manual production, not a native one. The Pragmatic Manipulation Sub-Hub's treatment of masking-related harm draws on this: masking is sometimes specifically exploited by people who prefer the masked presentation and who treat the unmasked presentation as a defect that needs to be suppressed.

### 3.3 Masking and the Authenticity Frame

A specific frame harm that falls out of masking: the *authenticity frame* is sometimes installed by non-autistic observers in a way that penalizes both masking and unmasking. When the autistic person masks, they are performing inauthenticity, which (in certain frames) marks them as untrustworthy. When they unmask, they are behaving in ways that the observer finds confusing or off-putting, which (in other frames) marks them as socially deficient. Either way, the autistic person is inside a frame they did not choose, and the frame is installed by the observer.

The protective move is the same as elsewhere: recognize the frame as a frame, substitute a more accurate one, and refuse to be operated on by the authenticity frame as if it were the neutral ground. The accurate frame is: *masking is what I am doing to accommodate non-autistic norms; unmasking is what I am doing when those norms are not the operative ones; neither is inauthentic in any morally significant sense; both are legitimate responses to social context*.

### 3.4 The Three-Layer Cognitive Load

It is worth spelling out the three-layer cognitive process that masking imposes, because the description makes the cost visible in a way that casual use of the word "masking" does not. When an autistic person is masking in a social interaction, they are simultaneously running:

**Layer 1: the interaction itself.** This is what the non-autistic interlocutor is also running. The content of the conversation, the turn-taking, the topic management, the responses to what is being said. This layer is shared across both participants.

**Layer 2: the frame-performance.** The autistic person is running a non-native frame of what the interaction "should look like" from the outside and is performing behaviors (eye contact, facial expressions, prosody, pacing, vocabulary choices, stim suppression, posture management) that match that frame. The frame is non-native in the technical sense: it is not the frame the autistic person would default to if the performance were not required. Performing a non-native frame requires constant reference to the frame specification, which is itself maintained explicitly in working memory rather than as an automatic background process.

**Layer 3: the monitoring of the performance.** The autistic person is watching themselves in something like real time, checking whether the performance is working, looking for micro-signs of social failure (a puzzled look on the interlocutor's face, a pause that runs too long, a topic shift that did not land), and correcting the performance when the monitoring detects an error. This layer is what the clinical literature sometimes calls "camouflage effort" and is the proximal driver of the documented costs.

The non-autistic interlocutor is running Layer 1 only. The autistic person is running all three, simultaneously, in real time. The cost is the cost of doing three cognitive tasks at once in a cognition that is already processing context in ways that are more effortful than the non-autistic default (per the Pellicano-Burr and monotropism frameworks). Hull et al. (2017), Cage and Troxell-Whitman (2019), and Miller et al. (2021) all document the subjective phenomenology of this three-layer process from the autistic perspective. The phenomenology is remarkably consistent across studies: exhausting, identity-eroding, performatively-successful-but-privately-costly.

For frame analysis, the key insight is that the three-layer process is not separable from the non-autistic observer's perception of the interaction. The observer sees Layer 1 and forms a frame of the autistic person as someone who "communicates normally" or "functions well" or "does fine in social situations." This frame is then carried forward into subsequent encounters. The observer does not see Layers 2 and 3, does not know they are happening, and does not budget for their cost. When the autistic person later struggles, burns out, or stops being able to sustain the performance, the subsequent struggle reads as "regression" or "change" or "something wrong now" rather than as the predictable cost of sustained three-layer cognitive labor. The frame the observer formed at first encounter (*this person communicates normally*) was not wrong in the sense of being a false report; it was wrong in the sense of being a radically incomplete report. Frame analysis names the incompleteness.

### 3.5 The Cost of Frame-Performance

Raymaker et al. (2020) on autistic burnout document what sustaining non-native frame-performance costs over time. The participants in the Raymaker study described burnout as resulting from *chronic life stress and a mismatch of expectations and abilities without adequate supports* -- and masking was one of the primary stressors. The costs include exhaustion, loss of skills, worsening physical and mental health, identity erosion, and, in severe cases, inability to sustain ordinary daily functioning. The costs are not metaphorical. They are well-documented empirical outcomes.

This matters for the frame-analytic practical file (*category-23-framing-research-practical.md*, Section 5 on the protective toolkit) because it sets a realistic expectation for what sustained frame work can cost. The recommendation to "refuse the frame" is reasonable only in contexts where refusal is sustainable. In contexts where refusal is not safe, the work of holding the frame internally while performing compliance externally is itself draining, and the draining is itself evidence that the frame attack is doing damage even when it cannot be openly contested.

## 4. HOW AUTISM GETS FRAMED BY NON-AUTISTIC INSTITUTIONS

### 4.1 The Clinical Frame of Autism

The dominant clinical frame of autism is encoded in the DSM-5 (American Psychiatric Association, 2013) and ICD-11 (World Health Organization, 2019). Both treat autism as a disorder characterized by deficits in social communication and restricted, repetitive behaviors. The clinical frame is the official frame of most institutions that interact with autistic people: schools, mental health services, social services, disability services, courts, medical systems. When an autistic person encounters any of these institutions, the clinical frame is the frame they are being read through, whether it is accurate for their specific case or not.

The critical literature on this clinical frame is substantial. Kapp, Gillespie-Lynch, Sherman, and Hutman (2013) demonstrated that autistic people's preferences for identity-first language and their rejection of deficit framing are themselves empirical findings that deserve recognition. Pellicano and Stears (2011), Pellicano, Dinsmore, and Charman (2014), and Fletcher-Watson et al. (2019) have argued for community-driven research priorities and for frameworks that center autistic perspectives rather than clinical pathology. Botha and colleagues (Botha, Hanlon, & Williams, 2023; Botha, Dibb, & Frost, 2022) have written extensively on the psychological and social costs of the stigmatizing clinical frame.

For frame-analytic purposes, the important observation is that the clinical frame is a frame -- it is not a neutral description. It is a particular interpretive schema with particular problem-definitions, causal diagnoses, moral evaluations, and treatment recommendations (Entman's four functions). The schema has clinical utility in some contexts (it enables access to support, it provides diagnostic recognition, it organizes research) and harms in others (it pathologizes normal variation, it installs deficit expectations, it shapes records in ways that follow the autistic person across contexts). Treating the clinical frame as a frame -- rather than as the truth -- lets you make use of its utility without being wholly contained by it.

### 4.2 Educational Records

Educational records of autistic children often contain the clinical frame in written form. Incident reports, teacher observations, IEP notes, and behavioral plans typically describe autistic behaviors in deficit language and install a frame in which those behaviors are problems to be managed. A subsequent teacher reading the record encounters the child pre-framed: difficult, disruptive, unable to focus, resistant to transitions, inappropriate socially. The frame runs before the teacher meets the child.

The Pragmatic Manipulation Sub-Hub flags educational settings as one of the contexts where frame installation has lifelong consequences. A child framed as difficult at age six carries that framing into every subsequent school encounter; the framing shapes expectations, interventions, and opportunities. By the time the child is an adolescent, the record contains years of frame-consistent entries, and contesting the frame requires contesting a whole paper trail.

Frame analysis does not by itself solve this. What it gives is the orientation: the record is a frame that has been written about the child, and the child is entitled to a different frame. Advocating for re-framing in educational records is a real and sometimes successful strategy, particularly when combined with documented alternative framings from autistic-affirming professionals, from parents who are using accurate vocabulary, and from autistic self-advocates. The sub-hub's context-educational-settings page covers the specific strategies.

### 4.3 Social Services Assessments

Social services assessments of autistic adults -- for benefits, for disability services, for housing, for capacity, for custody, for safeguarding -- are frame-installation events with substantial downstream consequences. The assessor approaches the assessment through a protocol, which is a frame. The autistic adult's presentation is interpreted inside the protocol's frame. The resulting assessment installs a frame that subsequent decisions operate within.

The frame analysis insight here is the one the sub-hub's social-services context page foregrounds: the assessment frame is often inaccurate in specifiable ways. Assessment protocols were typically developed on samples that did not include or adequately represent autistic adults. They measure for traits the autistic adult may not present in the ways the protocol expects; they may mis-code masking as absence of need, or may code accurate self-report as non-cooperation, or may treat pattern-recognition of the protocol itself as gaming. The protocol's frame and the accurate frame of the autistic adult's situation are often misaligned, and the assessment will nevertheless produce a record that encodes the protocol's frame.

Recognizing the assessment as a frame -- rather than as an objective measurement -- is the precondition for any response. The response options are extensively covered in the sub-hub (context-social-services page, strategy pages on documentation and on external evidence). The frame-analytic contribution is just the recognition.

### 4.4 How the Clinical Frame Constitutes Institutional Encounters

A complication worth drawing out: the clinical frame does not just describe institutional encounters; it partly constitutes them. When an autistic adult walks into a benefits assessment, the assessment is *designed* around the clinical frame of autism. The questions the assessor asks, the criteria being applied, the categories on the form, the training the assessor has received, the institutional rules about what counts as "cooperation" or "evidence" or "capacity" -- all of these are themselves expressions of the clinical frame. The frame is not an overlay on a neutral encounter; it is the architecture of the encounter.

This matters because "recognizing the frame as a frame" sounds, at first, like the kind of cognitive move that should be sufficient to step outside it. It is not. You can recognize that the assessment is operating inside a clinical frame and still be subject to the assessment, because the frame has institutional teeth that do not go away when you recognize them. Recognition is the precondition for several kinds of response (advocacy, documentation, appeal, external framing), but recognition is not itself a response. The sub-hub's strategy pages on documentation and on external evidence are specifically about what *else* you need to do after the frame has been recognized.

For autistic readers it is worth naming the gap between intellectual recognition of the frame and practical escape from the frame. The gap is real, and the sub-hub is clear that cognitive tools alone are insufficient when the frame is backed by institutional enforcement. Frame analysis provides orientation; it does not provide leverage. Leverage has to come from elsewhere -- from legal protection, from advocacy networks, from documentation, from supportive professionals, from peer communities that can corroborate an alternative frame. None of these removes the original frame's institutional standing; what they do is install a competing frame in parallel, and the competition between the two frames is the arena in which the actual outcome is decided.

### 4.5 Medical and Psychiatric Records

Medical and psychiatric records carry similar frames forward. A psychiatric diagnosis applied to an autistic adult in a crisis (often misattributed as bipolar, borderline, anxiety disorder, or others) becomes part of the record that subsequent clinicians read. The record's frame then shapes the subsequent encounters in ways the autistic adult may not see until years later, when they are trying to get the record corrected. The Pearson et al. (2022) work on autism and interpersonal victimization is relevant here: autistic adults often find that their own accounts of their experience are discounted relative to the record's account, particularly in clinical and forensic settings.

The specific misdiagnosis pattern is worth describing because it is consistent enough across autism community accounts to have become a recognized phenomenon. An autistic adult presents in a crisis (burnout, shutdown, meltdown, sustained masking collapse, sensory overload in a stressful environment) and is read through the diagnostic categories that the clinician is trained to recognize. If the clinician is not trained in adult autism presentation, the presentation is typically coded as borderline personality disorder (the emotional reactivity reads as "instability"), as bipolar disorder (the energy variation reads as "mood swings"), as an anxiety disorder (the autonomic activation reads as "panic"), as psychosis (the sensory differences read as "perceptual disturbances"), or as a personality disorder more generally. None of these diagnoses is necessarily wrong on its own terms, but each of them installs a frame that is incompatible with the autism frame that would actually fit the presentation. The subsequent record carries the misattribution forward, and the autism underneath it may not be recognized for years.

Frame analysis does not, on its own, prevent misdiagnosis -- the remedy for that is clinical training and autism-informed care. What it does is provide the autistic adult (or their advocate) with a vocabulary for what has happened. The misdiagnosis is a frame attack in the structural sense: a frame has been installed that is not accurate to the situation, and the frame has institutional durability. Correcting the frame requires the same tools as correcting any other institutional frame -- advocacy, documentation, appeal, and external validation from autism-informed professionals.

### 4.6 HR and Workplace Records

HR records occupy a specific category that deserves separate treatment because they are simultaneously quasi-legal (actionable in disputes, subject to data-protection law) and quasi-clinical (used to make decisions about fit, about accommodations, about continued employment). An autistic employee who has requested accommodations has typically produced a record that describes their autism to the employer, and the record becomes the frame through which subsequent workplace interactions are interpreted.

Two patterns are frequently documented in the autism and workplace literature. First, the pattern in which the accommodation request itself becomes the frame: the autistic employee is thereafter "the one who asked for accommodations," and their subsequent workplace behavior is read through the frame of "the accommodation case." Second, the pattern in which the autism disclosure becomes a frame that operates alongside, and sometimes displaces, the employee's actual performance. Performance that would otherwise be evaluated on its merits is instead evaluated through the disclosure frame. Both patterns are versions of the same structural move: a record has installed a frame, and subsequent encounters are being read through it.

The sub-hub's context-workplace page treats the specific strategies. The frame-analytic contribution is the recognition that the HR record is doing the frame work, and that the employee's response options are shaped by what the record has already installed.

### 4.7 The Record-Reading Pattern

A pattern worth naming, because it is consistent across record types: the reader of a record about you has usually formed their frame of you before you walk in. The moment of first encounter is not a moment of frame-building; it is a moment of frame-verification. The reader is looking at you to confirm what the record has told them. Deviations from what the record predicted are often interpreted as deceptive presentation rather than as evidence that the record was wrong.

This makes the first-encounter moment especially vulnerable for autistic adults, because the record frame and the Sasson-et-al. thin-slice frame are both installed, simultaneously, in the first seconds of the encounter. The reader has the record's pre-loaded frame and the thin-slice impression frame both running, and the two frames typically reinforce each other: the record says "this person is autistic" and the thin-slice says "this person presents as socially off," and both frames converge on a conclusion that looks like independent corroboration but is actually two instances of the same cultural frame of autism operating in parallel.

The protective move in this kind of encounter is to introduce content that both frames fail to predict: specific evidence, specific capability, specific clarity of reasoning. Content that is strong enough to disrupt the pre-installed frame can force the reader to update, but the content has to be delivered in a way that the reader can receive -- which often means written documentation, prepared statements, or supporters who can speak for you. The sub-hub's strategy pages on documentation and on external evidence treat the specific moves. The frame-analytic recognition is that the first encounter is pre-framed and that the protective work has to include unframing as well as content delivery.

## 5. FRAME ATTACKS IN AUTISM-SPECIFIC ABUSE CONTEXTS

### 5.1 The Weaponized-Features Pattern

The Douglas and Sedgewick (2024) paper -- "Experiences of interpersonal victimization and abuse among autistic people" in *Autism* -- identified what they called the **weaponised features** pattern: autistic traits being used against the autistic person by abusers, including the deliberate engineering of overload conditions and the subsequent reframing of autistic responses as evidence of autistic instability. The pattern is specifically frame-analytic: the abuser installs a frame on the autistic person's autistic features, and the frame reassigns moral and causal responsibility from the abusing behavior to the autistic person's presentation.

Pearson et al. (2024) -- "It's Not a Physical Prison but You Can't Get Out.' How Autistic Adults Make Sense of the Experience of Intimate Violence and Abuse" -- documented similar patterns and extended the analysis. Both studies are qualitative, with modest samples, but they are the first peer-reviewed descriptions of dynamics that have been extensively reported in autistic community accounts. The research is early, and both papers are careful about the limits of what qualitative work can establish, but the patterns they describe are structurally clear and consistent with the broader coercive-control literature.

The specific frame moves include:

- **Engineering sensory overload and then framing the resulting meltdown as directed aggression.** The sub-hub's sensory gaslighting and post-meltdown shaming pages treat this in detail.
- **Using the autism diagnosis to discredit the autistic person's accounts.** "She's autistic, she doesn't understand relationships." "He's on the spectrum, he doesn't know how he comes across." The diagnostic frame is used to overwrite the autistic person's own frame of their experience.
- **Isolating the autistic person from autistic peers and reframing the peers as enablers.** The sub-hub's bandwagon isolation page treats this; the specific autism inflection is that autistic peers are the highest-leverage reality-check network and are therefore the highest-value target.
- **Exploiting the authenticity frame.** Masking is called fake and deceptive; unmasking is called inappropriate and concerning. The autistic person is pre-installed inside a frame where neither of the available options is safe.
- **Exploiting the care frame.** Support is offered in ways that install dependency, and the autistic person is then framed as ungrateful if the support is questioned or as helpless if the support is accepted.
- **Credibility discount as pre-installed frame.** Before the autistic person reports abuse, the autistic-as-unreliable-narrator frame is already running in the minds of the people who would be the reporting audience. The frame attack has been completed before the report is made.

### 5.2 The Credibility-Discount Frame

Pearson et al. (2023) and related work on autism and interpersonal victimization document what autistic community accounts have long reported: autistic adults reporting abuse, harassment, or misconduct are often not believed, or are believed less credibly than non-autistic reporters of similar reports. The pattern is not universal but it is well enough documented that it has to be treated as a structural feature of many institutional response contexts rather than as individual bad luck.

In frame-analytic terms, the credibility-discount is a frame that is already running before the report is made. The autistic person is being read, in advance, as an unreliable witness -- not because of anything they have said, but because the clinical frame of autism has been pre-installed in the audience's cognition. When the autistic person then speaks, the pre-installed frame decides what to do with their words before the words can do the work on their own.

This is why the Pragmatic Manipulation Sub-Hub's disclosure-decisions and documentation strategy pages are structured the way they are: they assume that the reporting environment has a pre-installed frame that needs to be accounted for, not that the environment is neutral ground.

### 5.3 What Frame Analysis Offers as a Protective Tool

For autistic readers dealing with frame attacks in abuse contexts, frame analysis offers several concrete protective uses:

1. **It names the move.** Being able to say "this is a frame attack -- specifically, the engineering-and-reframing pattern" gives you a foothold that a vaguer naming (hurt, confused, gaslit) does not give.
2. **It gives you an alternative frame to substitute.** The sub-hub's opening protective reframes are worked examples of this. Each page gives you a one-sentence alternative frame that you can hold internally even when you cannot assert it externally.
3. **It validates the work of holding a frame against pressure.** Sustained frame-refusal is cognitively expensive. Knowing that the work is real and purposeful, rather than a sign of stubbornness or paranoia, makes it sustainable.
4. **It lets you use peer-network reality-checks more effectively.** When you can describe the frame that is being installed, peers can tell you whether it matches anything they have seen. Crompton et al. (2020) support the use of autistic peer networks as reality-check resources; frame-analytic vocabulary makes the peer conversation more efficient.
5. **It sets realistic expectations.** Frame analysis does not defeat frame attacks on its own. The sub-hub is clear that cognitive tools are not sufficient in dangerous situations. Frame analysis works best in combination with documentation, peer support, legal advocacy, and safety planning when those are needed.

### 5.4 A Taxonomy of Autism-Specific Frame Attacks

The preceding sections mention frame attacks in general terms. This section names specific patterns that are documented in the autism literature or in community accounts, and that operate through frame-level moves rather than through content disputes. Each pattern is a frame that gets installed on an autistic person or on an interaction, and each installs Entman's four functions in a way that misallocates problem, cause, moral evaluation, and treatment.

**The "behavior is choice" frame.** This is the autism-specific version of intentionalism (see Category 22 on attribution). Autistic features -- stimming, meltdowns, literal interpretation, eye-contact patterns, sensory responses -- are framed as voluntary decisions rather than as neurological features of autistic functioning. Once installed, the frame licenses the treatment recommendation that the autistic person should "just stop" or "just try harder," and makes failure to do so evidence of bad faith rather than of the frame's inaccuracy. The accurate reframe is that autistic features are features of autistic cognition and sensory processing; they are not the outputs of a decision process that can be redirected by effort. The Pellicano-Burr and monotropism frameworks support this. Cross-link: the manipulation sub-hub's compliance-trap page treats the variant where this frame is installed in service of extracting compliance with demands that are incompatible with the target's actual processing needs.

**The "not really autistic" frame.** This frame is installed to undermine identity claims and deny accommodations. It takes several forms: *you don't look autistic*, *you're too articulate to be autistic*, *autism is overdiagnosed*, *you're just self-diagnosing*, *you managed fine as a child so you can't be autistic*, *real autism looks different*. The frame reassigns the problem from "this person needs autism-appropriate accommodations" to "this person is making dubious identity claims." Once installed, the frame licenses the denial of accommodations, the withdrawal of support, and the interpretation of subsequent autistic features as evidence of manipulation rather than as evidence that the original identity claim was correct. Cross-link: the manipulation sub-hub's diagnostic-weaponization and performative-allyship pages treat the specific variants.

**The "too literal" frame.** This frame positions the autistic person's literal interpretation of a speaker's words as the autistic person's own fault, rather than as a legitimate reading of ambiguous or under-specified speech. *You're being too literal* does work by implying that there is a non-literal reading everyone else would have gotten and only the autistic person has missed. In many actual cases, the speaker's utterance was genuinely ambiguous, and the non-literal reading the speaker now claims to have intended is a post-hoc rationalization that gives the speaker the benefit of whichever reading is more convenient at the moment. The frame attack is the move of installing "the literal reading was a failure on your part" rather than "the utterance was ambiguous and I owe you clarification." The accurate reframe is that literal interpretation is a legitimate response to under-specified speech, and the burden of disambiguation is on the speaker when the stakes are real. Cross-link: the manipulation sub-hub's pragmatic-packaging-invalidation page treats this as a sub-pattern.

**The "lacking empathy" frame.** This is the most widely propagated frame of autism in the popular and older clinical literature: that autism is characterized by a deficit in empathy or theory of mind. The frame is empirically contested in ways the popular literature has not caught up with. Mitchell, Sheppard, and Cassidy (2021) review the evidence: the "empathy deficit" frame treats a mismatch phenomenon as a one-sided deficit, and the mismatch has been documented in both directions. Sheppard et al. (2016) showed that non-autistic participants are worse at reading autistic participants' mental states than they are at reading other non-autistic participants'. The deficit is symmetric: each group is worse at reading the other than at reading its own. Calling this an autistic empathy deficit is a framing choice, not a neutral description.

The "lacking empathy" frame persists despite the contrary evidence because the frame serves functions beyond description: it licenses dismissal of autistic perspectives, it relieves non-autistic observers of the obligation to extend empathy toward autistic targets (you cannot be expected to empathize with someone who cannot empathize), and it organizes treatment around "teaching autistic people empathy" rather than around addressing the bidirectional mismatch. Frame analysis makes the functions of the frame visible; it does not, on its own, dislodge the frame from public discourse. But recognizing the frame as a frame with functions is the precondition for refusing it internally. Cross-link: the manipulation sub-hub's tone-policing and concern-trolling pages treat the variants where this frame scaffolds specific attacks.

**The "high-functioning/low-functioning" frame.** Functioning labels have been extensively critiqued by the autistic community and are increasingly deprecated in the research literature, but they continue to circulate and continue to do frame-analytic work that is worth naming. The frame installs a one-dimensional scale from "high-functioning" (talks, works, seems okay) to "low-functioning" (non-speaking, higher support needs). The frame allows two frame attacks to be deployed against the same autistic person simultaneously: *you're too high-functioning to need accommodations* and *you're too low-functioning to know what you need*. Which attack deploys depends on which the framer needs at the moment. Neither attack is responsive to the person's actual support profile, because the frame has replaced the actual support profile with a single label.

The accurate reframe, endorsed by the autistic community and by much of the current research, is that autistic people have support profiles that vary across domains, across time, across contexts, and across developmental stages. A person may need high support in one domain (executive function, sensory regulation) and low support in another (verbal communication, narrow expertise). Functioning labels conceal the actual profile. The disability-rights community rejects the labels on grounds that include both their inaccuracy and their exploitative uses. See Kapp et al. (2013) and related self-advocacy literature.

**The "burden" frame.** The autistic person is framed as a burden on family, partner, or employer. This is often installed without explicit statement, through sighs, martyrdom talk, references to how hard things are, and narratives of sacrifice. Once installed, the frame licenses the family member, partner, or employer to feel virtuous for enduring the autistic person, which in turn licenses a range of subsequent moves: *I've put up with so much*, *after everything I've done for you*, *you should be grateful*. The autistic person is placed in a debt-frame they did not accept and cannot discharge. Cross-link: the manipulation sub-hub's information-asymmetry-exploitation and compliance-trap pages treat specific uses.

**The "savant" frame.** The positive-sounding version of othering. The autistic person is framed as an interesting curiosity -- a walking encyclopedia, a prodigy, a mascot -- rather than as a person. This frame often reads as admiration, which makes refusing it socially costly, but it performs the same distancing work as the deficit frames: the autistic person is not being related to as a peer whose interior life matters, but as an exhibit. The frame also commonly installs a specific vulnerability: if the autistic person is valued for a specific capacity, the framer can withdraw the valuation whenever the capacity is not being displayed, leaving the person with no relational ground to stand on. Cross-link: the manipulation sub-hub's performative-allyship page treats the variants that involve an ally who presents the autistic person as their interesting project.

These seven patterns do not exhaust the autism-specific frame-attack repertoire, but they cover the most frequent ones documented in community accounts and the most cross-referenced ones in the manipulation sub-hub. Each of them operates at the frame level: each reassigns Entman's four functions in a way that misallocates problem, cause, moral evaluation, and treatment; each is refusable in principle but often not refusable in practice without cost; each has a more accurate substitute frame available.

### 5.5 Pattern-Specific Cross-Links to the Manipulation Sub-Hub

This section explicitly maps each major manipulation pattern in the Pragmatic Manipulation Sub-Hub to the frame-analytic vocabulary developed in this category, so that readers coming from the sub-hub pages can locate the theoretical apparatus quickly and readers coming from here can find the applied treatment.

**DARVO (sub-hub pattern: manipulation-hub-pattern-darvo).** DARVO is the paradigm frame swap: the operative frame *we are discussing X's behavior* is substituted, in three micro-moves, with the operative frame *we are discussing how I have been mistreated*. Entman's four functions are reassigned wholesale. Frame analysis gives the structural description: the move is a swap, not a content dispute, and content-level response to the new accusation is an inside-the-frame move that concedes the swap. The protective response is frame refusal plus reassertion of the original frame.

**Gaslighting (sub-hub pattern: manipulation-hub-pattern-gaslighting).** Gaslighting is the sustained installation of a perceptual-frame attack: *the target's perception is unreliable*. The attack operates cumulatively. For autistic targets, the attack intersects with pre-existing frames of autistic people as unreliable narrators, which is why the attack is often faster and stickier against autistic targets than against non-autistic ones even though the attack itself is the same move.

**Memory gaslighting (sub-hub pattern: manipulation-hub-pattern-memory-gaslighting).** A specific variant of the above in which the frame attack targets the target's memory rather than their perception of the present. For autistic targets with exceptional verbal or episodic memory, this attack is often especially stark because the target's memory is actually more accurate than the framer's, which makes the frame attack both more contestable and more exhausting to contest.

**Sensory gaslighting (sub-hub pattern: manipulation-hub-pattern-sensory-gaslighting).** A frame swap from *this environment is overstimulating* to *you are being difficult, oversensitive, or dramatic*. Specifically autism-relevant because sensory differences are empirically well-established and diagnostic, which means the frame being refused by the attacker is the same frame the diagnostic criteria endorse. The reframe is that the sensation is a neurological fact, not a personality flaw.

**Post-meltdown shaming (sub-hub pattern: manipulation-hub-pattern-post-meltdown-shaming).** A frame installation on a meltdown as *behavior*, *choice*, *attack*, or *manipulation*. The accurate frame is *a neurological response to overload*. The sub-hub page offers the one-sentence reframe; this category explains why the reframe works: it changes Entman's four functions so that problem, cause, moral evaluation, and treatment all move to the environmental conditions that produced the overload rather than to the autistic person.

**Diagnostic weaponization (sub-hub pattern: manipulation-hub-pattern-diagnostic-weaponization).** Over-application of the diagnostic frame to contexts where it is not accurate, with the effect of reassigning responsibility or discounting the autistic person's account. *"That's just your autism"* applied to an ordinary disagreement is an over-application. Frame analysis shows the move as a misapplication of a frame that has legitimate uses in other contexts.

**Compliance trap (sub-hub pattern: manipulation-hub-pattern-compliance-trap).** The frame being installed is *cooperation is the neutral default; non-cooperation requires justification*. Once installed, any refusal reads as transgression. For autistic targets, the frame is especially sticky because autistic adults are often explicitly trained through childhood compliance-based interventions to accept this frame as the default. The reframe is that cooperation is a choice, not the default, and that refusal does not require justification beyond the refusal itself.

**Concern trolling (sub-hub pattern: manipulation-hub-pattern-concern-trolling).** A frame wrapping that pre-legitimates criticism as care. The frame is structurally separate from the content, which means the concern can be acknowledged without the content being accepted. *"I hear that you're worried; that doesn't change my position on this."*

**Performative allyship (sub-hub pattern: manipulation-hub-pattern-performative-allyship).** A frame installation of *I am your ally*, which pre-legitimates interpretive authority that the behavior may not earn. Frame analysis allows the target to refuse the frame without refusing the relationship, by checking whether behavior matches the frame the ally has installed.

**Tone policing (sub-hub pattern: manipulation-hub-pattern-tone-policing).** A frame reassignment from content to delivery. For autistic targets this is especially load-bearing because autistic delivery features are read as "wrong tone" through frames that are not native to the autistic speaker. The reframe is that delivery is not the operative subject of the conversation, and that the content should be addressed regardless of delivery.

**Pragmatic packaging invalidation (sub-hub pattern: manipulation-hub-pattern-pragmatic-packaging-invalidation).** A frame overwriting in which the communication packaging becomes the object of attention rather than the content being communicated. The autistic inflection is the same as tone policing but applied across more channels: literal interpretation, directness, information density, topic focus.

**Emotional invalidation / double bind (sub-hub pattern: manipulation-hub-pattern-emotional-invalidation-double-bind).** A frame attack in which the target is placed inside a frame where neither of the available responses is safe. Bateson's original double-bind analysis is a frame-theoretic analysis; the sub-hub treatment applies it to contemporary manipulation patterns.

**Bandwagon isolation (sub-hub pattern: manipulation-hub-pattern-bandwagon-isolation).** The installation of a chorus frame: *everyone agrees with me; you are the outlier*. The chorus may be real, partial, or fabricated; the frame move is the same. Frame analysis shows that the move depends on the target accepting frame-level authority for an unverifiable chorus, and the refusal move is declining that authority.

**Information asymmetry exploitation (sub-hub pattern: manipulation-hub-pattern-information-asymmetry-exploitation).** A frame attack in which the target is kept in a frame that the attacker knows is not the operative one, or in which information that would change the frame is withheld. For autistic targets this often intersects with the literal-interpretation dynamics described above: the attacker can rely on the target's literal reading of under-specified speech to produce a frame mismatch that can later be exploited.

**Weaponized accommodations (sub-hub pattern: manipulation-hub-pattern-weaponized-accommodations).** Accommodations are offered in a frame that installs debt or dependency, and the frame then licenses subsequent coercion. The attack is autism-specific in its targets even though the structural form is general.

**"Can this be repaired?" (sub-hub decision: manipulation-hub-can-this-be-repaired).** The sub-hub's decision framework depends on recognizing whether the operative frames on both sides of a relationship are compatible with repair. Frame analysis is the diagnostic instrument: if the frames are genuinely incompatible and one party is unwilling to revisit their frame, repair is not available regardless of goodwill on the other side.

**Miscommunication vs. manipulation (sub-hub decision: manipulation-hub-miscommunication-vs-manipulation).** The diagnostic question is whether the frame mismatch is a good-faith mismatch (miscommunication) or a deliberately installed frame attack (manipulation). Frame analysis provides the technical apparatus for making the call. Good-faith mismatches are characterized by mutual adjustment in response to explicit naming; attacks are characterized by escalation or by self-sealing responses to explicit naming.

Each of these cross-links allows the sub-hub reader to locate the theoretical treatment and the theoretical reader to locate the applied cases. The integration is deliberate: the sub-hub was built on the assumption that a frame-analytic treatment of each pattern would be available, and this category provides it.

### 5.6 A Note on Scope

The autism-specific abuse literature is still small. Douglas and Sedgewick (2024) and Pearson et al. (2024) are the most directly relevant recent studies. Community accounts go back much further. The structural claims in this section are consistent across both sources, but the formal evidence base is not yet large. Readers should treat specific claims about frequency, severity, and typical patterns as [EXTRAPOLATED] from the available qualitative research, not as [VALIDATED] across large quantitative samples. The broader coercive-control frameworks (Stark, 2007; Johnson, 2008) transfer structurally but were developed without autism samples and should be read as general frameworks adapted to autistic contexts rather than as autism-specific research.

## 6. THE PROTECTIVE USE OF FRAME ANALYSIS FOR AUTISTIC READERS

### 6.1 What Frame Analysis Is Good For

Frame analysis is good for:

- **Seeing that a frame is in play at all.** The first protective move is noticing that interpretation is happening above the word-meaning level, and that the interpretation is not settled by the facts alone.
- **Naming the frame.** Having vocabulary -- DARVO as frame swap, gaslighting as perceptual-frame attack, bandwagon isolation as chorus frame -- lets you describe what is happening in terms that are precise enough to be useful.
- **Recognizing a frame attack as distinct from ordinary disagreement.** Not every objection to your view is a frame attack; many are ordinary content disagreements. Frame analysis helps you tell the difference.
- **Substituting a more accurate frame.** Reframing is a well-studied technique in cognitive therapy and in conflict resolution. Applied to specific situations, it is often protective.
- **Using peer networks for frame-checking.** Autistic peers who are not inside the frame being installed on you are often better positioned to see it than you are.

### 6.2 What Frame Analysis Is Not Good For

Frame analysis is not good for:

- **Protecting you in contexts where the frame is backed by physical danger or severe institutional power.** In these contexts, cognitive tools are insufficient and should be combined with external resources. See the sub-hub safety assessment.
- **Winning arguments inside the frame that is being installed.** The point of frame analysis is to avoid the inside-the-frame argument, not to win it.
- **Replacing the evidence of autistic peers, advocates, lawyers, or clinicians.** Frame analysis is a lens, not a substitute for the specific expertise that specific situations require.
- **Protecting you from exhaustion.** Holding a frame against pressure is effortful. Frame analysis tells you why the effort is worthwhile but does not reduce the effort itself.
- **Guaranteeing that you will read the frame correctly.** Frames are sometimes genuinely ambiguous, and autistic readers in particular may find frame-selection effortful (Pellicano & Burr, 2012; Vermeulen, 2012). When uncertain, consult with people you trust -- especially autistic peers, who are more likely to be operating with frames that match yours.

### 6.3 The Autism-Affirming Use

The most important use of frame analysis for autistic readers is the use that recovers the autistic frame from the clinical frame that has been written over it. The clinical frame of autism -- the deficit model, the pathology vocabulary, the treatment orientation -- has been installed on you in ways large and small across your entire life. Recognizing that frame as a frame, rather than as the truth, is what makes it possible to substitute a more accurate one. The accurate frame, the one that autistic community work has been building for decades, treats autism as a form of human variation with its own strengths, its own costs, and its own cultural patterns -- not as a deficit relative to a non-autistic norm.

This is not denial of the real difficulties autistic people experience. Difficulties with sensory input, with masking costs, with frame-mismatch exhaustion, with institutional encounters, with the manipulation patterns in the sub-hub -- all of these are real, and all of them are extensively documented. But the difficulties do not add up to "autism is a deficit"; they add up to "autism is a form of variation that interacts poorly with specific cultural expectations." The distinction matters because the two frames recommend very different responses. The deficit frame recommends changing the autistic person. The variation frame recommends changing the environment, or accepting the cost of working within an environment that is not built for you, or finding environments that are. Frame analysis is the tool for seeing the difference.

### 6.4 Frame Resistance Strategies for Autistic Readers

This section collects specific strategies that autistic readers can use to notice and resist frame installation. None of them is a cure, and none of them removes the cost; each of them is a tool whose use is appropriate in some contexts and not in others.

**Name the frame.** The foundational Lakoff insight (Lakoff, 2004): naming a frame activates it, but it also makes it visible, and visibility is the precondition for contesting it. Using the word "frame" explicitly in response to a frame attack is itself a move. *"I don't accept the frame you're putting me in. You're framing this as [X] and I'd describe it as [Y]."* The sentence does not win the argument inside the frame, but it ejects the conversation from the inside-the-frame position in which the argument would have been lost. For autistic readers with an analytical vocabulary, this can feel more tractable than the alternative of arguing the content inside a frame that has already decided the conclusion. Naming the frame makes it a legitimate object of conversation rather than an invisible precondition of the conversation.

**Separate content from frame.** A practice that autistic readers may find particularly useful: pause mid-interaction and ask two questions in sequence. First, *what would I think of this content if it came from someone I trust?* Second, *what frame is this content being presented in right now?* The gap between the two answers is diagnostic. If the content is reasonable and only the frame is doing the harm, the response is frame refusal plus content engagement. If both the content and the frame are problematic, the response is refusal at both levels. If the content is fine and the frame is fine, there is nothing to contest. The practice is slower than ordinary conversation and may feel clinical, but it is exactly the kind of explicit sequential processing that autistic cognition is often good at, and it produces more accurate diagnoses than the gut-level "something is wrong here" that is sometimes all a frame attack leaves as a trace.

**Use external frame-checks.** The reality-check network (cross-link to the manipulation sub-hub's reality-check-networks strategy page) is, structurally, a mechanism for external frame verification. Someone outside the frame being installed on you can usually see the frame more clearly than you can from inside it. The Crompton et al. (2020) finding on autistic peer-to-peer information transfer applies specifically here: autistic peers may be especially good at diagnosing frame attacks on other autistic people because they are likely to be running a similar frame-repertoire to the target and less likely to import the non-autistic frame being installed. The protective move is to present the situation to the reality-check network in content-first terms (what actually happened, what was said, what the specific words were) rather than in frame-first terms (what it felt like, what the target thinks is going on). Presenting it content-first lets the network form its own frame, which can then be compared with the target's.

**Refuse the frame without refusing engagement.** Frame refusal is not the same as disengagement. You can engage with the content of an interaction while refusing the frame in which the content is being presented. *"I'm happy to discuss the specific situation. I'm not willing to do it inside the frame of 'my behavior is the problem.' Let's talk about the specific event."* The move separates frame refusal from interaction refusal, which matters because interaction refusal is sometimes read (correctly or incorrectly) as evidence for the frame being refused. Frame refusal plus content engagement is often more defensible and more sustainable than a blanket refusal of the whole conversation.

**Use the bottom-up advantage.** The attenuated-priors style that the Pellicano-Burr and Rozenkrantz-D'Mello-Gabrieli frameworks describe is an advantage specifically for spotting frame inconsistencies. A frame that does not fit the actual content of the interaction will produce mismatches that are easier to notice when the perceiver is not top-down-papering-over them in service of the dominant frame. Autistic readers should trust the "something is wrong here" signal when it fires, and should use it as a cue to check the frame explicitly rather than dismissing it as confusion on their part. The signal is often more accurate than it feels, because it is being generated by a cognitive style that is less willing to ignore discrepancies.

**Document contemporaneously.** A written record of what actually happened, written close to the event in your own words, preserves the frame you were operating in at the time. Subsequent frame-installation attempts have to contest the written record, which is harder than contesting a memory that has no external anchor. The sub-hub's documentation strategy page treats this in detail. For frame analysis specifically, the point is that documentation is itself a frame-preservation move: you are installing the accurate frame in writing, in advance, so that it cannot be easily overwritten by later reinterpretation.

**Allow for frame-checking latency.** If your cognitive style processes frame selection more slowly and more explicitly, then you are entitled to take more time. "I need to think about this before I respond" is a legitimate move, not an admission of inadequacy. The sub-hub's de-escalation and refusal pages both treat the general form. For frame attacks specifically, latency is protective because it interrupts the automatic-uptake mechanism the attack depends on. A frame that has to wait for your considered response is much less likely to install itself at the level of reflex than a frame that receives immediate uptake or immediate defensive pushback (either of which lets the frame operate unchallenged at the frame level).

**Treat the exhaustion as information.** Sustained frame-resistance is cognitively expensive, and the cost is real. If you notice that a specific relationship, job, or context produces frame-resistance exhaustion day after day, the exhaustion is telling you something about the density of frame attacks in that context, not about your adequacy. The Raymaker et al. (2020) autistic-burnout literature supports this read: burnout is, in part, a consequence of sustained mismatch with expectations, and frame-resistance is one of the forms that mismatch takes. The protective move is to take the exhaustion seriously as a signal about the environment, not to interpret it as a personal failing.

**Use content-first communication channels when available.** The Sasson et al. (2017) finding that thin-slice bias against autistic adults disappears when observers see content-only (text) presentations is a usable asset. For stakes-bearing communication with unfamiliar non-autistic audiences, written channels can often deliver the same content without the presentation-style first-impression frame that tends to install against autistic speakers. This is not a recommendation that autistic people should hide behind text; it is a recognition that text delivers content with less frame-installation noise, and that using text for high-stakes first communications is a legitimate strategic choice.

### 6.4b The "Everything Is a Frame" Trap

A caveat that is important enough to state explicitly. Frame analysis is a powerful interpretive tool, and like any powerful tool it has a failure mode: over-application. Once you become fluent in reading frames, the temptation is to read every interaction as a frame attack in progress and every disagreement as a frame dispute in disguise. That reading is sometimes correct. It is also sometimes paranoid, and the paranoid version of frame analysis can cause harm both to the reader (by turning ordinary social life into a continuous threat assessment) and to the reader's relationships (by treating good-faith interlocutors as manipulators).

The calibration is not obvious. A few practical heuristics help.

**Frequency check.** If you are reading frame attacks into more than about ten percent of your interactions, you are probably over-applying. Ordinary interaction does contain framing -- that is what frame analysis tells us -- but the vast majority of ordinary framing is cooperative, good-faith, mutually constructed work. Most conversations are not frame attacks. If your diagnostic is producing a high rate of attack-detection, the first move is to double-check the diagnostic itself rather than trust the output.

**Consistency check.** A frame attack typically persists across multiple interactions with the same person or context. A one-off ambiguous move is rarely an attack; a pattern of similar moves from the same source across time is more likely to be one. The Pragmatic Manipulation Sub-Hub's miscommunication-vs-manipulation page is structured around exactly this distinction, and it is worth revisiting when you are uncertain.

**Self-sealing check.** Real frame attacks tend to self-seal: they interpret objections to them as evidence for them (Section 6.3 in the practical research file). Ordinary framing does not. If your objection produces a good-faith adjustment or a curious question, the frame was probably not an attack. If your objection produces escalation, reinterpretation-of-you-as-the-problem, or doubling-down, the self-sealing signature is there.

**External-reader check.** Someone outside the interaction can often tell you whether your reading is calibrated or over-applied. Autistic peer networks are particularly valuable here (Crompton et al., 2020), because peers with similar frame-repertoires can compare notes. But non-autistic readers you trust can also be calibrated readers, and their input matters precisely because they are not inside your frame-repertoire.

**The opportunity cost.** Frame analysis has a cost. Running frame diagnostics on every interaction is exhausting, socially alienating, and not free. The point of having the tool is that it is available when you need it, not that it must run continuously. The protective move in low-stakes situations is usually to not run the diagnostic at all, reserve it for moments where the signal is strong, and let ordinary interactions proceed under ordinary good-faith assumptions. This is not naive; it is resource management. The pathological version is running frame diagnostics on everything, which burns resources that would be better spent elsewhere and tends to produce false positives.

The autism-specific risk. For autistic readers who have been through sustained manipulation -- which is disproportionately common given the autism-specific abuse literature -- the pattern-recognition that protected them during the manipulation can continue running afterward in contexts where it is no longer needed. Over-application of frame analysis can be a trauma response, and treating it as such is more accurate than treating it as a failure of calibration. The appropriate response is the same as for other hypervigilance patterns: recognize that the pattern is serving a protective function, notice when the function is no longer needed, and allow the pattern to quiet down in contexts where it can. This is long-term work, not immediate calibration, and it is appropriate to do with therapeutic support when that is available.

### 6.4c Autistic-Autistic Frame Coordination

A complementary observation to the double-empathy mismatch literature: autistic-autistic interaction often does not show the same frame mismatch difficulties that characterize autistic-non-autistic interaction. Crompton et al.'s (2020) telephone-game study is the most famous demonstration -- information transfer in autistic-only chains was as efficient as in non-autistic-only chains, and both were significantly more efficient than in mixed chains -- but the finding fits into a broader picture that is worth naming explicitly.

Heasman and Gillespie's (2019) conversation-analytic study of autistic-autistic interaction documented high levels of mutual accommodation, genuine back-and-forth, and turn-taking patterns that matched what the participants themselves described as comfortable and generative. The finding contradicts stereotypes of autistic interaction as one-sided or interview-like. What Heasman and Gillespie actually observed was cooperative, frame-coordinated interaction in which both participants were evidently reading each other's frames successfully.

Morrison et al.'s (2020) work on real-world interaction outcomes extended the finding beyond the laboratory: autistic adults paired with other autistic adults rated interactions more positively, reported lower social effort, and showed behavioral measures consistent with easier interaction than in mixed pairings. The non-autistic participants in the mixed pairings reported no clear difference, which is itself diagnostic: the asymmetry of effort is being absorbed by the autistic participant in mixed settings.

DeBrabander et al.'s (2019) thin-slice work ran the study in the opposite direction: when autistic raters evaluated thin-slice videos of autistic speakers, they did not show the negative first-impression bias that non-autistic raters showed. The autistic raters were rating the same speakers more accurately, or at least more charitably, because they were not bringing the non-autistic frame-repertoire to the task.

**What does this mean for frame analysis specifically?** Several things. First, frame mismatches are not a property of autism; they are a property of mismatch between frame-repertoires, and the mismatch disappears when the repertoires are more similar. Second, autistic peer networks are structurally valuable as frame-check resources because they are less likely to import the non-autistic default frames that may be doing the misreading. Third, autistic-autistic interaction is a legitimate communicative environment, not a degraded approximation of non-autistic interaction, and autistic readers do not need to "practice" frame-tracking by immersing in difficult non-autistic contexts. They already have a context where frame-coordination works well, and that context is worth investing in on its own terms.

A caveat. "Autistic-autistic interaction goes smoothly" is a generalization across studies; individual autistic pairings can also have frame mismatches, personality conflicts, communication style clashes, and the ordinary difficulties of any human interaction. The claim is not that autistic people are uniformly easy for each other; it is that the systematic mismatch effects that appear in mixed interactions do not appear at the same rate in same-group interactions. This is consistent with the broader picture that mismatch is what produces the difficulty, and mismatch is symmetrical across pairings -- not unique to autism.

### 6.4d Masking and the Pearson & Rose (2021) Frame

Pearson and Rose's (2021) conceptual analysis of autistic masking in *Autism in Adulthood* extends the masking literature in a direction that is particularly relevant to frame analysis. They argue that masking is often described as a "choice" autistic people make, and they show that the "choice" framing is empirically inaccurate and functionally harmful. Masking is, on their analysis, a response to a stigma environment in which unmasked autistic presentation is punished. Calling it a choice installs a frame in which the autistic person is responsible for the costs of masking, rather than a frame in which the environment is responsible for creating the conditions under which masking was necessary.

The Pearson-and-Rose frame analysis of masking-as-choice versus masking-as-forced-adaptation is itself an example of frame analysis doing protective work. The "choice" frame places moral and causal responsibility on the autistic person (they chose to mask, they could stop); the "forced adaptation" frame places it on the environment (the environment punishes authentic presentation, forcing adaptation). The two frames are not just different interpretations of the same facts -- they generate different prescriptions, different support needs, and different measures of justice.

Cage and Troxell-Whitman's (2019) empirical work on masking contexts and costs is complementary. They documented that masking motivations cluster around assimilation pressures (conforming to non-autistic norms to access resources or avoid punishment) and protection pressures (avoiding specific negative consequences of being read as autistic). Both clusters are responses to environmental pressure, not free choices. The protective reframe -- masking is work done under pressure, not a choice freely made -- is a frame substitution in the Lakoff sense. It changes how the reader interprets the same behavior.

For autistic readers, the practical consequence is that when someone (a clinician, a partner, an employer) installs a frame in which masking is your "normal" presentation and unmasking is a regression or a failure, you are looking at a frame attack. The accurate frame is that masking is costly adaptive work, that the cost is documented (Hull et al., 2017; Raymaker et al., 2020; Miller, Rees, & Pearson, 2021), and that being able to unmask in a given context is a feature of that context, not a failure of the person. The sub-hub's pages on post-meltdown shaming, weaponized accommodations, and authenticity-frame attacks draw on this analysis.

### 6.4e Preparing Frame Maps for High-Stakes Interactions

A concrete practice that autistic readers can adopt for high-stakes encounters: building a *frame map* in advance. A frame map is a written inventory of the frames that might plausibly be in effect during an upcoming interaction, with each frame's answers to Entman's four functions spelled out, and with the autistic reader's preferred frame explicitly stated.

**How to build a frame map.** For an upcoming meeting, appointment, assessment, interview, or conversation:

1. Write down the ostensible purpose of the interaction in the other party's vocabulary. *"Performance review"; "capacity assessment"; "intake interview"; "family meeting about arrangements."*
2. List the frames you expect the other party to bring. There are usually several. A performance review might include the "evaluation" frame, the "development conversation" frame, the "disciplinary prelude" frame, or the "fit" frame (see the practical research file, Section 2.6).
3. For each expected frame, work through Entman's four functions. What would this frame identify as the problem? The cause? The moral assignment? The treatment recommendation?
4. Write down the frame you would prefer the interaction to run in. Apply Entman's four functions to the preferred frame. Make sure the preferred frame is coherent, defensible, and achievable.
5. Identify the specific frame-moves you might need to make during the interaction: naming a frame, refusing a frame, offering the preferred frame, requesting explicit clarification of which frame is in effect.
6. Prepare the specific sentences you would use for each move. Rehearsed sentences are much easier to deploy under pressure than improvised ones, especially when the interaction is cognitively taxing.

**Why this helps.** The preparation moves the cognitive work of frame analysis out of the real-time window where it is most expensive, and into the pre-interaction window where it can be done with full processing resources. It also allows consultation with peers, advocates, or support people before the encounter rather than after. The sub-hub's disclosure-decisions and professional-support strategy pages extend this preparation to specific high-stakes contexts (court, medical encounters, benefits assessments).

**Requesting explicit frame statements.** One direct move available in many high-stakes encounters is asking the other party to state the frame explicitly. *"Can you help me understand what kind of conversation we're having today? Is this an assessment, an intake, an interview, or something else?"* The question is neutral in tone, legitimate in most institutional contexts, and produces information that can be checked against your frame map. If the other party's answer matches the frame you expected, you can proceed with more confidence. If it does not match, you have early warning that the interaction is going somewhere you did not plan for, and you can decide whether to proceed or to request clarification.

**Documenting the frames in play for later review.** A standard preparation move is to write down, after the encounter, what frames were actually in effect, how they shifted, and what moves the other party made at the frame level. The contemporaneous record is both a frame-preservation tool for your own review and a useful document if the encounter later becomes contested. The sub-hub's strategy-documentation page covers the mechanics of contemporaneous documentation in more detail.

These practices do not eliminate the cost or guarantee a good outcome. They do shift the ground: from reactive processing under pressure, to preparation, to informed response. For autistic readers who have often been caught flat-footed by frame moves they did not recognize until later, the shift is often protective.

### 6.5 Double Empathy as the Default Stance

Across every page of the hub, the default stance on autistic-non-autistic interaction is Milton's (2012) double empathy. Neither side is wrong; the mismatch is between them. When you feel yourself being framed as the one who is "bad at communication" or "hard to understand" or "not picking up on social cues," the double empathy reframe is the protective substitute: *we are two people with different frame-repertoires, and the mismatch is the mismatch; neither of us is the problem alone*. This is not a denial that difficulties are happening; it is a refusal of the asymmetric frame that puts the difficulty on one side.

## 7. THE NON-AUTISTIC FRAME REPERTOIRE OF AUTISM

This section complements the autism-specific frame-attacks taxonomy by describing the background frames of autism that circulate in the non-autistic majority culture. These are not attacks in the narrow sense of deliberate manipulation by an individual. They are the frames that an autistic person encounters everywhere -- in popular media, in casual conversation, in professional training, in parenting advice, in charitable appeals -- and that pre-install default interpretive schemas in the minds of the people the autistic person is about to meet. Reading this section as "what the other person is probably already thinking" is not the most charitable read, but it is often the most accurate one.

**The tragedy frame.** Autism as loss: a child has been "taken" by autism; a family's hopes have been "stolen"; a future has been "lost." This frame is associated with advocacy organizations that position themselves as helping families recover from the tragedy of autism. The autistic community has consistently rejected the frame, most pointedly in the Kapp et al. (2013) response to deficit framing, but the frame continues to circulate in charitable appeals and in some parenting literature. The autistic person inside this frame is pre-positioned as a source of family suffering rather than as a person with their own experience.

**The puzzle frame.** Autism as something to be solved, deciphered, figured out. The puzzle-piece logo that has circulated widely is the visual summary. The frame positions the autistic person as an object of interpretation rather than as a communicator, and it positions the non-autistic observer as the interpreter. The autistic community has also rejected this frame; it has been replaced in autistic-led advocacy by variants of the infinity symbol and identity-first language.

**The cure frame.** Autism as a disease with a potential cure, and the autistic person's life as improved if the cure is found. This is sometimes presented as compassionate; autistic community responses have argued that the cure frame is functionally an erasure frame -- what would be cured is not "autism" but "the autistic person." The frame persists in some parts of the biomedical autism research literature and has been critiqued extensively (Kapp et al., 2013; Pellicano & Stears, 2011; Pellicano et al., 2014).

**The inspiration frame.** The autistic person as inspiring for simply existing. This frame reads as positive but performs the same distancing work as the deficit frames: the autistic person is an exhibit, not a peer. Inspiration framing is especially common around autistic children in educational and fundraising contexts.

**The burden-on-society frame.** A macro-scale version of the burden frame from the attack taxonomy: autistic people, collectively, framed as a cost to society, a burden on services, a drag on productivity. This frame underwrites austerity narratives around disability benefits and around special education funding. It is almost never challenged when it appears in policy discussions.

**The "spectrum" frame that invisibilizes needs.** The phrase "everyone is a little bit autistic" and its relatives. The frame reads as inclusive but its function is to dilute the diagnostic category to the point where specific needs cannot be asserted. When every trait is "a bit autistic," nothing is autistic, and no accommodations are owed.

**The genius frame.** The cultural narrative that autism comes with extraordinary giftedness. This is a variant of the savant frame from the attack taxonomy, but it operates at the background-cultural level rather than the individual-encounter level. It raises the bar for autistic adults who are not visibly extraordinary, who then fail to match the frame and lose even the distorted recognition the frame offered.

**The clinical frame as described in Section 4.** The deficit-model clinical frame as encoded in DSM-5 and ICD-11. This is the frame with institutional teeth.

The point of cataloging these is not to make autistic readers cynical about the people they interact with. Most non-autistic people carry some of these frames without having chosen them; the frames are the default inheritance of growing up inside a culture that has circulated them for decades. The point is that the autistic reader approaching any new interaction with a non-autistic stranger is approaching a person whose cognition has been partially pre-loaded with one or more of these frames, and whose first-pass interpretation of the autistic person will be shaped accordingly. The Sasson et al. (2017) thin-slice finding is the empirical demonstration that this pre-loading is consequential within seconds. The protective move is not to defeat the frames (that is cultural work, not individual work) but to recognize when they are operating, to budget for their effects, to use content-first channels when possible, and to seek out the contexts and people for whom the frames are less dominant.

The specific applications to individual situations should be held more lightly and combined with the specific supports appropriate to the specific situation.

## 9. SYNTHESIS: WHY FRAME ANALYSIS IS LOAD-BEARING FOR AUTISTIC READERS

The material in this file has covered substantial ground: the double-empathy framing of autistic-non-autistic interaction, the cognitive-science literature on autistic processing, monotropism and the deep-channel attentional style, masking as frame-performance work, the clinical and institutional frames that are written about autistic people, the autism-specific frame-attack taxonomy, the non-autistic cultural frame repertoire of autism, and the protective strategies that frame analysis makes available. This concluding section synthesizes the reasons frame analysis is specifically load-bearing for autistic readers, as distinct from its general utility.

**First, frames are already operating on autistic readers whether or not the readers recognize them.** The Sasson et al. (2017) thin-slice finding, the clinical frame of autism, the non-autistic cultural frames catalogued in Section 7, and the institutional records of Section 4 all install frames on autistic readers without their consent and often without their awareness. Frame analysis does not create the frames; it makes the already-existing frames visible. The cost of not having frame analysis is not "no frames"; it is "invisible frames you are operating inside." Making the frames visible is the minimum protective move, even if no further action is possible.

**Second, autistic cognition has specific strengths for frame-analytic work.** The attenuated-priors style (Pellicano & Burr, 2012), the enhanced rationality pattern (Rozenkrantz, D'Mello, & Gabrieli, 2021), and the explicit-sequential processing that autistic cognition often favors are all aligned with the deliberate, analytical work that frame analysis requires. The kinds of reading that frame analysis calls for -- breaking apart content and frame, applying Entman's four functions, checking for self-sealing, separating what is being claimed from how it is being framed -- are exactly the kinds of analytical moves that autistic cognition is often excellent at. The category is not asking autistic readers to do cognitive work that is unnatural to them; it is asking them to apply cognitive strengths they already have to a domain where those strengths have specific protective value.

**Third, the cost of frame work is real and should be budgeted.** The same analytical style that makes autistic readers good at frame analysis is expensive to sustain. Masking is frame-performance work (Section 3), burnout is partly the cost of sustained frame mismatch (Raymaker et al., 2020), and everyday social interaction imposes frame-tracking demands that autistic cognition runs explicitly rather than automatically. Frame analysis is a tool, but using it continuously is not sustainable. The appropriate use is selective: apply it when the stakes warrant the cost, and conserve the effort when the stakes are low.

**Fourth, frame analysis is compatible with -- and requires -- the other tools in the hub.** The protective moves in Section 6 are not complete protective responses on their own. Documentation, peer networks, legal advocacy, professional support, and safety planning are all required complements in serious situations. Frame analysis provides the orientation that makes the other tools more effective; it does not replace them. Readers who try to rely on frame analysis alone in high-stakes situations are at risk; readers who combine frame analysis with the appropriate external resources are making the best use of all the tools available.

**Fifth, the double-empathy framing is the correct default and should be maintained across all applications.** Milton (2012) and the empirical work that has followed are explicit that the communication difficulty between autistic and non-autistic people is a mismatch between groups, not a deficit in one of them. Every application of frame analysis in this file should be read through that default. When the file describes a frame attack on an autistic person, it is not saying autistic people are uniquely vulnerable because of something wrong with them; it is saying that the frame repertoire of the dominant culture produces specific attacks that work specifically well against people whose frames are different. The asymmetry is a feature of power and of majority status, not of autistic cognition.

**Sixth, the research supports autistic self-trust in frame perception.** When autistic readers notice that "something is off" in an interaction, the signal is often tracking accurate perception of a frame mismatch that non-autistic observers are papering over. The Pellicano-Burr framework, the monotropism framework, and the enhanced-rationality literature all converge on this: autistic cognition is often picking up on real discrepancies that top-down-dominated processing would smooth away. Autistic readers should take their frame-level suspicions seriously and should resist the meta-frame that their noticing is itself a deficit. The noticing is often accurate; the culture's framing of the noticing as paranoid or pedantic or over-analytical is itself a frame attack.

**Seventh, the aim is not to win inside the frames but to escape from being unilaterally framed.** Frame analysis is not a rhetorical technique for winning arguments. It is a technique for avoiding being operated on by frames you have not consented to. Sometimes the appropriate outcome is escape from the interaction rather than victory within it; sometimes the appropriate outcome is recognition that no resolution is available and that the protective move is distance, documentation, and external advocacy. Frame analysis is the diagnostic that tells you what is available, not the guarantee of a favorable outcome.

**Finally, the category itself is an autism-affirming intervention.** By taking the autistic reader seriously as an analyst of their own situation, by providing technical vocabulary for what is happening, by trusting the autistic reader's perceptions, and by refusing the deficit framings that the clinical tradition has applied to autistic cognition, the category's treatment of framing is itself a frame. The frame is: *autistic readers are capable analysts, their perceptions are reliable, their cognitive style is a strength, and the work of understanding their own experience belongs to them*. This is not a neutral frame; it is a deliberate reframe against the deficit-framed tradition. The category's existence is an instance of the protective work it describes.
