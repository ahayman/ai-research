## 1. How to Use This Page

This is a directory. It is not a substitute for the strategy pages on the rest of the hub — it is the backup when a situation exceeds what those strategies can handle. There are four reasons to use this directory instead of the strategy pages:

1. **Lethality indicators are present** (see [Safety Assessment, Section 2](manipulation-hub-safety-assessment.md)). Any of the indicators from Campbell's Danger Assessment means you should be talking to a trained advocate, not applying hub strategies alone.
2. **You are in immediate crisis** — in danger right now, or so depleted that the decision frameworks on other pages are not usable.
3. **You need specialized expertise** the hub does not provide — legal, medical, immigration, criminal-justice, or context-specific advocacy.
4. **You want an external reality-check** from a trained advocate who can hear your account and help you assess it, which is one of the most effective applications of these resources even when you are not in immediate danger.

Most of the resources listed below are **free, confidential, and staffed 24/7** by trained professionals or volunteers. Many are specifically designed for situations where you cannot speak openly. Almost all of them can help you safety-plan, assess risk, and connect you with local services. **Contacting a hotline is not a commitment to any action.** You can call, get information, and hang up. You can text, receive information, and close the conversation. You do not have to tell them your name, location, or full story. You control what you share.

## 2. Critical Safety Notes Before Contacting Any Resource

These apply to every resource listed on this page. Read them before you call, text, or chat.

### Device safety

- **Your devices may be monitored.** Abusers frequently install tracking apps, monitor browser history, read messages, or check call logs. Before contacting a crisis resource from your own phone or computer, consider whether your abuser has access to it.
- **Safer alternatives:** Public library computers, a trusted friend's phone, a work phone (if not shared), or a separate device your abuser does not know about. Some domestic violence organizations provide free prepaid phones.
- **Private browsing does not always work.** Incognito mode hides browser history from casual inspection but does not defeat dedicated monitoring software or network-level logging.
- **Clear your call log and browser history** after contacting a resource if you suspect monitoring — but be aware that abruptly cleared logs can themselves signal that something was being hidden.
- **Text-based resources may be safer than voice** in situations where you cannot speak privately. Most major hotlines offer text or chat options.

### Confidentiality and mandatory reporting

- **Most hotlines are confidential.** They do not share your information without your consent, with narrow exceptions.
- **Mandatory reporting exceptions** apply in most jurisdictions to: immediate child abuse, elder abuse, imminent harm to self or others, and specific legal situations. The advocate you speak with can tell you what the exceptions are in their jurisdiction, and you can ask before sharing specific information.
- **You can ask about confidentiality before sharing details.** "Before I tell you what's happening, can you walk me through your confidentiality policy and what circumstances would require you to report something?" Any legitimate advocate will answer this question clearly.
- **Some helplines are more confidential than others.** Government-funded lines may have reporting obligations that independent non-profits do not. This is worth knowing.

### You are allowed to change your mind

- **You can hang up.** If the person on the other end is not helpful, or says something that makes you uncomfortable, you can end the contact. This is not rude. The person taking the call expects this.
- **You can contact the same resource again.** Different advocates have different styles. If your first experience was not good, the second may be different.
- **You can contact multiple resources.** There is no rule against getting information from several sources and comparing.
- **Contacting a hotline does not commit you to action.** Getting information is not the same as making a report, filing a restraining order, or leaving a relationship. You can gather information and decide later what to do with it.

### Reporting to police is a decision, not a default

- Most hotlines will not force you to report to police. Good advocates will tell you the tradeoffs of reporting and let you decide.
- For autistic readers specifically, police encounters carry their own risks. Autistic behaviors (gaze aversion, flat affect, literal responses to questions, delayed responses under stress) can be misread as deception, non-compliance, or threat. If you are deciding whether to involve police, an advocate who is aware of this dynamic can help you think through it.

## 3. Immediate Danger

If you are in immediate physical danger right now:

- **United States, Canada:** 911
- **United Kingdom:** 999 (emergency) or 101 (non-emergency)
- **Australia:** 000
- **New Zealand:** 111
- **Ireland:** 999 or 112
- **European Union:** 112 (works across all EU countries)

### What to say to a 911 / emergency operator

- Your location (address if you know it, nearest cross-street or landmark if you don't)
- Whether you need police, ambulance, fire, or more than one
- What is happening in the simplest terms ("My partner is hurting me." "Someone broke in." "I need help.")
- If you cannot speak freely, some jurisdictions support silent calls, tapping signals, or the "Silent Solution" system (UK). You can also try calling and leaving the line open.

### Autism-specific considerations for emergency calls

- **You may need extra processing time under stress.** Emergency operators are trained to repeat questions; you are allowed to ask them to repeat.
- **If you are able to say "I am autistic, I may need extra time to respond,"** this can help. But do not worry about saying it if the situation does not allow.
- **Literal questions get literal answers.** An operator asking "Are you safe?" is asking about your current physical state, not about whether you feel emotionally secure. Answer what they are asking.
- **Some autistic people carry autism alert cards** — a small card stating "I am autistic. I may communicate differently under stress. I am not being evasive." These can be useful with first responders if you can present one without escalating risk.

## 9. Medical Advocacy and Patient Rights

Medical gaslighting and diagnostic dismissal are common experiences for autistic people. These resources are specific to healthcare contexts.

- **Patient advocates / ombudspersons** — most hospitals and large clinics have patient advocates or ombudspersons. Ask at the front desk, call the hospital's main number, or search the hospital's website for "patient advocacy" or "patient relations."
- **State medical boards** (US) — handle complaints against individual physicians for specific issues; do not typically address interpersonal mistreatment unless severe.
- **The Joint Commission** (US hospital accreditation) — jointcommission.org — accepts complaints about US hospitals that may affect accreditation.
- **CMS (Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services)** (US) — for complaints about Medicare/Medicaid providers
- **General Medical Council** (UK) — gmc-uk.org — for complaints about UK doctors
- **Patient Advice and Liaison Service (PALS)** (UK, NHS hospitals) — each trust has one
- **Australian Commission on Safety and Quality in Health Care** — has state-level complaint mechanisms

**For autistic patients specifically:** consider bringing a written symptom list, a support person, or both. If you tend to "look calmer than you feel" or mask during appointments, say so explicitly. Sample phrasing: "I may look calmer than I am. My pain/distress is actually at [level]. I need this taken seriously."

## 12. A Note on Autism-Specific Resources

**There are currently no 24/7 autism-specific crisis hotlines in most countries.** This is a documented gap, not an oversight.

The major autism-led advocacy organizations provide extensive resources but are not crisis lines:

- **Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN)** (US) — autisticadvocacy.org — policy advocacy, resources, position papers. Not crisis-oriented.
- **Autistic Women & Nonbinary Network (AWN)** (US) — awnnetwork.org — community, resources, "Divergent" initiative on IPV and trauma. Not crisis-oriented.
- **National Autistic Society** (UK) — autism.org.uk — has a helpline (0808 800 4104, Monday–Friday) but is not a 24/7 crisis line
- **Autism New Zealand** — autismnz.org.nz
- **Amaze** (Australia) — amaze.org.au — autism helpline 1300 308 699 (business hours)

**What this means in practice:** if you need immediate crisis help, you will most likely be contacting a resource that is not specifically trained in autism. The advocate you speak with may or may not know much about autistic communication, sensory needs, masking, or the specific ways autism creates vulnerability. You may need to explain things.

**Strategies for calling non-autism-specific hotlines:**

- **Lead with what you need, not with your diagnosis.** "I am experiencing [specific situation]" is usually more useful than "I am autistic" as an opener. You can disclose later if it becomes relevant.
- **If you disclose autism, name what you need from them.** "I am autistic. Please give me extra time to respond, and please be literal with me." This is more useful than disclosure alone.
- **Text-based crisis services (Crisis Text Line, Shout, some NDVH options) may be more accessible** if verbal communication under stress is difficult for you.
- **If the first advocate is not helpful, try again.** Some advocates are more familiar with autism than others, and quality varies within organizations.
- **Autistic-led organizations can help you prepare** even if they are not crisis-response. An ASAN or AWN resource can help you understand your rights and options before you need them.

## 13. How to Vet a Resource Before Using It

Not every organization that claims to help is actually helpful. Before trusting a resource with significant information:

**Signs of a legitimate resource:**
- Clear confidentiality policy that explains mandatory reporting exceptions
- Free or clearly-disclosed fees
- Referrals to other services (rather than "we are the only solution")
- Trained staff (not just well-meaning volunteers for complex situations)
- Accreditation or funding from established bodies (government, major foundations, etc.)
- Named in trusted directories (WHO, NDVH, etc.)
- Long operating history
- Transparent about limits ("we can help with X; we cannot help with Y")

**Warning signs of a questionable resource:**
- Pressure to make immediate decisions
- Pushing religious or ideological frames you did not ask for
- Asking for payment or donations before providing help
- Claims of exclusive expertise ("only we really understand")
- Hostility toward other resources or toward professional help
- No clear confidentiality statement
- Unclear who is staffing the line and what their training is

**If a resource turns out to be unhelpful or harmful:** disengage, try a different resource, and consider reporting your experience to an organization you trust (e.g., an umbrella body or a journalist who covers the issue).

## 14. What to Do If the First Contact Is Unhelpful

This happens. Crisis line quality varies. The person you reach may be new, overworked, unfamiliar with your situation, or a poor match for you. **It is not a commentary on you if the first call does not go well.**

Options:

1. **Call back.** Most hotlines staff multiple advocates; the next person may be different.
2. **Try a different resource.** If you called the general DV hotline, try a specialized one (LGBTQ+, Native, trans-specific, etc.).
3. **Use a different channel.** If the call was frustrating, try text or chat. The pace is different and sometimes easier.
4. **Contact an autistic-led organization** for support in navigating the crisis resources. ASAN and AWN cannot provide crisis response but can help you prepare.
5. **Reach out to your reality-check network** (see the [Reality-Check Networks strategy page] — to be built) — a trusted friend, family member, or peer who can sit with you while you try again.
6. **Take a break.** You are not obligated to get crisis help on any specific timeline. If the first attempt was depleting, rest and try again later.

If the first contact was actively harmful (the advocate blamed you, dismissed you, or made things worse), that is worth naming: "The response I got was not helpful and it made the situation harder." You can report this to the organization's supervisory structure, but you are also allowed to simply disengage.

## 15. What This Page Cannot Do

- **Guarantee any specific resource will help.** Quality varies. Staff training varies. Individual advocates vary. This page provides the best-known options; it cannot promise outcomes.
- **Keep up with changes in real time.** Phone numbers change. Organizations close or merge. Before relying on a specific resource, verify it is still active (a quick search or test call).
- **Replace local knowledge.** A trained local advocate familiar with your specific jurisdiction, community, and legal system will usually be more helpful than a national line. National lines can refer you to local resources.
- **Work for every population equally.** Resources for non-speaking autistic people, autistic people with intellectual disability, autistic people in the criminal justice system, and several intersectionally marginalized groups are notably thin. Where the research base is thin, the resource base is also thin (foundational research document, Section 8).
- **Tell you what to do with the information.** Calling a hotline gives you information and options. What you do with them is your decision. Advocates can help you think through the decision; they should not make it for you.

## 16. Key Takeaways

1. **Contacting a hotline is not a commitment.** You can call, get information, and hang up. Most resources are confidential with narrow exceptions.
2. **Device safety matters.** Before calling from your own phone, consider whether your abuser has access to it. Text and chat options exist and are often safer.
3. **Ask about confidentiality before sharing.** Any legitimate advocate will answer clearly.
4. **If the first call is unhelpful, try again.** Quality varies within and between organizations.
5. **Crisis lines can be used even when you are not in crisis.** Trained advocates can provide an external reality-check and help you think through a situation before it becomes a crisis.
6. **Reporting to police is a decision, not a default.** For autistic readers, police encounters carry specific risks; an advocate can help you weigh them.
7. **There are no 24/7 autism-specific crisis hotlines.** You will most likely be contacting a resource not trained in autism. Lead with what you need, not your diagnosis.
8. **Workplace, medical, legal, and educational situations have dedicated resources.** The general DV lines are not the right first stop for these contexts.
9. **Specialized populations have dedicated lines.** LGBTQ+, Native, trans, Deaf/HOH, men, youth, elder, and trafficking resources exist and are worth using over general lines when they apply.
10. **This page is a starting point, not a substitute for research into your specific situation.** Verify resources before relying on them. Local advocates are often more helpful than national lines.
