# The Iran-Iraq War (1980-1988)

## Overview

The Iran-Iraq War (September 22, 1980 - August 20, 1988) was one of the longest and deadliest conventional wars of the twentieth century. Lasting eight years, it produced an estimated 500,000 to 1,000,000 deaths on both sides, devastated the economies of two oil-rich nations, reshaped Middle Eastern geopolitics, and entrenched the Islamic Republic's revolutionary institutions in ways that continue to define Iran's domestic politics and foreign policy to this day. The conflict has been compared to World War I in its brutality, featuring trench warfare, chemical weapons attacks, human-wave assaults, and deliberate targeting of civilian populations.

---

## 1. Causes and Saddam Hussein's Motivations

### 1.1 The Shatt al-Arab Waterway Dispute

The Shatt al-Arab (known as Arvand Rud in Persian), formed by the confluence of the Tigris and Euphrates rivers, was Iraq's only navigable access to the Persian Gulf. Sovereignty over this waterway had been contested for centuries. In the 1975 Algiers Agreement, a weakened Iraq under Saddam Hussein (then vice president) conceded to Iran's demand to share sovereignty along the thalweg (the deepest channel line), splitting control of the waterway. Saddam viewed this agreement as a humiliation forced upon him by the Shah's military superiority.

When the Iranian Revolution toppled the Shah in 1979, Saddam saw an opportunity to overturn the Algiers Agreement and reassert full Iraqi sovereignty over both banks of the Shatt al-Arab. On September 17, 1980, Saddam publicly tore up the Algiers Agreement on television and declared full Iraqi sovereignty over the waterway. Five days later, Iraqi forces invaded Iran.

### 1.2 Fear of the Shia Revolution Spreading

The 1979 Iranian Revolution, led by Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini, established a Shia theocratic state that explicitly called for the export of its revolutionary ideology throughout the Muslim world. This posed an existential threat to Saddam's Sunni-minority Ba'athist government. Iraq's population was approximately 55-60% Shia, and Khomeini directly called on Iraqi Shia to overthrow Saddam's regime.

Khomeini had lived in exile in the Iraqi Shia holy city of Najaf for 14 years before being expelled in 1978. He had deep connections with Iraqi Shia clerical networks, and Iranian revolutionary rhetoric specifically targeted Iraq's Shia majority as a potential revolutionary base. In 1979-1980, Iran-linked groups attempted assassinations of senior Iraqi officials, including Deputy Prime Minister Tariq Aziz. Saddam responded with mass arrests and deportations of Iraqi Shia with alleged Iranian ties.

### 1.3 Territorial Ambitions and Arab-Persian Rivalry

Saddam harbored broader geopolitical ambitions. He sought to replace Egypt (which had been ostracized from the Arab world following the 1979 Camp David Accords with Israel) as the leader of the Arab world. Conquering Iran's oil-rich, Arabic-speaking Khuzestan province (which Iraq called "Arabistan") would bolster these claims and significantly increase Iraq's oil reserves and strategic depth.

The Arab-Persian ethnic rivalry provided an additional ideological dimension. Ba'athist ideology emphasized pan-Arab nationalism, and Saddam framed the war as a defense of the Arab nation against Persian expansionism, invoking the seventh-century Arab-Islamic conquest of Persia and the Battle of Qadisiyyah (636 CE). The conflict was officially dubbed "Qadisiyyat Saddam" by Iraqi propaganda.

### 1.4 Perceived Iranian Weakness

The Iranian Revolution had thrown the country into chaos. The once-powerful Iranian military, built by the Shah with massive American arms purchases, was in disarray. The revolution had purged thousands of experienced officers suspected of royalist sympathies. Iran's international isolation, including the U.S. hostage crisis and arms embargo, left it unable to acquire spare parts for its American-made weapons systems (F-14 Tomcats, F-4 Phantoms, Chieftain tanks). Saddam calculated that a swift military strike would result in rapid territorial gains, the collapse of the Khomeini regime, or both.

## 2. The Course of the War

### 2.1 Iraqi Invasion (September 1980)

On September 22, 1980, Iraqi forces launched a massive ground invasion along a 644-kilometer front, with approximately 200,000 troops, 2,000 tanks, and supporting air power. The initial Iraqi air strikes, modeled on Israel's 1967 preemptive attack, targeted Iranian airfields but failed to destroy Iran's air force on the ground—a critical strategic failure.

Iraqi forces advanced rapidly in the northern and central sectors, seizing border towns and territory. However, Saddam's primary objective was Khuzestan, where he expected the Arab-speaking population to welcome Iraqi forces as liberators. This expectation proved catastrophically wrong.

### 2.2 The Battle of Khorramshahr (October-November 1980)

The Battle of Khorramshahr became the defining early engagement of the war and demonstrated the unexpected ferocity of Iranian resistance. When Iraqi forces pushed into the port city of 70,000 people on September 28, they encountered approximately 2,000 Iranian regular soldiers and a similar number of Revolutionary Guards (Pasdaran) armed with rocket-propelled grenades and Molotov cocktails, ready to wage street-by-street urban warfare.

The battle lasted 34 days and became known as "Khuninshahr" (City of Blood). The Iranians fought tenaciously despite being outnumbered and outgunned, turning every building into a strongpoint. Approximately 7,000 soldiers died on each side before the city finally fell to Iraq on November 10, 1980. The fierce resistance at Khorramshahr stalled the Iraqi offensive, consumed Iraqi reserves, and gave Iran time to mobilize its larger population for a protracted war.

### 2.3 Iranian Counterattack and Liberation of Khorramshahr (1981-1982)

By 1981, Iran had reorganized its military forces, combining the regular army (Artesh) with the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC/Pasdaran) and the Basij volunteer militia. In a series of major offensives in late 1981 and 1982, Iran pushed Iraqi forces back.

The pivotal Operation Beit ol-Moqaddas (April-May 1982) liberated Khorramshahr. On May 24, 1982, Iranian forces spearheaded by the 77th Khorasan Division, Revolutionary Guards, and Basij volunteers crossed the Karun River under devastating air strikes and artillery barrages, overwhelmed Iraqi defenses, and recaptured the city. An estimated 19,000 Iraqi soldiers were captured. The liberation of Khorramshahr was a defining moment: it restored Iranian morale, humiliated Iraq, and set the stage for Iran's fateful decision to carry the war into Iraqi territory.

### 2.4 Human Wave Tactics and the Basij Militia

Iran's military strategy relied heavily on infantry-intensive assaults that Western observers termed "human wave" attacks. The Basij Mobilization Resistance Force, established by Khomeini in November 1979, recruited hundreds of thousands of volunteers, many of them teenagers and elderly men, motivated by revolutionary fervor and religious zeal. Basij volunteers were promised martyrdom and paradise if they died fighting.

These mass infantry assaults sought to overwhelm Iraqi defensive positions through sheer force of numbers. Thousands of Basij and Pasdaran would advance across minefields and into fortified Iraqi positions, often suffering catastrophic casualties. During operations such as Operation Ramadan (July 1982) and Operation Karbala-5 (January 1987), Iranian forces launched repeated assaults against entrenched Iraqi positions near Basra, with casualty ratios sometimes reaching 5:1 or higher in Iraq's favor. Despite their enormous human cost, these tactics achieved mixed results—occasionally breaking through Iraqi lines but never achieving a decisive strategic breakthrough.

### 2.5 The War of Attrition (1984-1987)

After the initial Iranian advances into Iraqi territory stalled in 1982-1983, the war settled into a grueling stalemate reminiscent of World War I trench warfare. Both sides constructed elaborate defensive positions featuring trenches, minefields, barbed wire, and fortified bunkers stretching across hundreds of kilometers of front line.

## 3. The War of the Cities

Beginning in February 1984, Saddam Hussein initiated a systematic campaign of aerial bombardment and missile attacks against Iranian cities—the "War of the Cities." The aim was to break Iranian civilian morale and pressure the government into accepting a ceasefire.

### Phases and Escalation

The campaign unfolded across five major phases between 1984 and 1988:
- **Phase 1 (February 1984):** Iraq launched indiscriminate attacks against 11 Iranian cities, killing at least 1,200 civilians in the initial cluster of raids.
- **Phase 2 (January 1987):** Iraq struck 35 Iranian towns and cities using aircraft, missiles, and artillery.
- **Final Phase (January-February 1988):** Iraq fired modified Scud missiles (the "Al-Hussein" variant with extended range) directly at Tehran, causing panic and mass evacuations.

Over the course of the war, Iraq fired 533 ballistic missiles against Iran, of which 414 (77.7%) struck 27 Iranian cities. These attacks killed 2,312 civilians and injured 11,625. In total, approximately 13,000 Iranian civilians became casualties from the War of the Cities exchanges.

### Impact on Iran's Missile Program

The War of the Cities exposed a critical vulnerability in Iran's defenses. Iran initially had no surface-to-surface missile capability but acquired SS-1 Scud-B missiles from Libya in 1985 and from Syria in 1986. The Revolutionary Guard Corps took charge of these weapons and used them against Iraqi cities, particularly Baghdad, Kirkuk, and Basra. This experience planted the seed for Iran's indigenous ballistic missile development program, which became a cornerstone of its military doctrine in subsequent decades.

## 4. The Tanker War

### 4.1 Attacks on Oil Shipping

In 1984, Iraq expanded the war by attacking Iranian oil tankers and oil export facilities in the Persian Gulf, aiming to strangle Iran's economy and internationalize the conflict. Iran retaliated by attacking ships carrying oil from Iraq's Gulf Arab allies, particularly Kuwait and Saudi Arabia. Over the course of the Tanker War (1984-1988), Iraq was responsible for 283 attacks on merchant vessels while Iran accounted for 168.

### 4.2 Operation Earnest Will and U.S. Naval Intervention

In December 1986, Kuwait's government requested American naval protection for its oil tankers. After internal debate, the Reagan administration agreed in March 1987 to reflag eleven Kuwaiti tankers with American flags and provide them with U.S. Navy escorts. Operation Earnest Will (July 24, 1987 - September 26, 1988) became the largest naval convoy operation since World War II.

The U.S. naval presence led to direct confrontations with Iran. In April 1988, after the frigate USS Samuel B. Roberts struck an Iranian mine, the U.S. launched Operation Praying Mantis—the largest American naval engagement since World War II—destroying two Iranian oil platforms, two naval vessels, and six armed speedboats, and severely damaging a frigate.

### 4.3 Iran Air Flight 655

On July 3, 1988, the USS Vincennes, a guided-missile cruiser operating in the Persian Gulf, shot down Iran Air Flight 655, an Airbus A300 commercial airliner on a scheduled flight from Tehran to Dubai via Bandar Abbas. All 290 people on board—including 66 children—were killed. The Vincennes crew mistook the civilian aircraft for an attacking Iranian F-14 Tomcat fighter jet, despite the plane being on a normal flight path, in an assigned airway, squawking a civilian transponder code, and continuously ascending.

The U.S. Navy initially claimed the Vincennes was in international waters, but three years later Admiral William Crowe admitted on ABC's Nightline that the ship had been operating inside Iranian territorial waters. The United States never formally apologized for the incident, though it eventually agreed to pay $131.8 million in compensation through the International Court of Justice in 1996.

For Iranians, the shootdown of Flight 655 remains a defining symbol of American hostility and callousness. Many Iranians believe the attack was deliberate, and it significantly hardened anti-American sentiment. The incident is widely regarded as a factor in Khomeini's subsequent decision to accept the ceasefire.

## 5. International Involvement

### 5.1 United States: From Neutrality to Tilt Toward Iraq

The U.S. initially adopted a position of declared neutrality, but from 1982 onward—when Iran's counteroffensives threatened to overrun Iraq—Washington increasingly tilted toward Baghdad, driven by the imperative to prevent an Iranian victory that could destabilize the Persian Gulf oil supply.

Key steps in the U.S. tilt:
- **November 1982:** Iraq was removed from the State Department's list of state sponsors of terrorism, enabling economic and military assistance.
- **1984:** Full diplomatic relations with Iraq were restored (they had been severed since 1967).
- **1984-1988:** The U.S. provided Iraq with intelligence on Iranian troop movements, satellite imagery, and battlefield intelligence through the Defense Intelligence Agency. This intelligence was used to plan operations, including attacks where Iraq employed chemical weapons.
- **By 1987:** The U.S. had authorized approximately $5 billion in agricultural credits (Commodity Credit Corporation) to Iraq, which freed Iraqi funds for military purchases.
- The U.S. also provided dual-use technology, including precursor chemicals that contributed to Iraq's chemical and biological weapons programs.

### 5.2 The Iran-Contra Scandal

In a stunning contradiction of its pro-Iraq tilt, the Reagan administration simultaneously conducted secret arms sales to Iran. Beginning in August 1985, the U.S. authorized Israel to sell American-made weapons to Iran, including TOW anti-tank missiles and HAWK anti-aircraft missile parts. In early 1986, the operation shifted to direct U.S. sales to Iran.

The stated objective was to secure the release of American hostages held by Iran-backed Hezbollah in Lebanon, though some officials also hoped to cultivate moderate elements within the Iranian government. Lieutenant Colonel Oliver North, operating from the National Security Council, diverted profits from the Iranian arms sales to fund the Contra rebels in Nicaragua, circumventing a congressional ban (the Boland Amendment).

When the scandal was exposed in November 1986 (first reported by the Lebanese magazine Ash-Shiraa), it created a major political crisis. Of the $30 million Iran reportedly paid, only $12 million reached government accounts. Fourteen officials were charged with crimes. The affair revealed the deep contradictions in U.S. Middle East policy: Washington was simultaneously arming both sides of the war while publicly supporting Iraq.

### 5.3 Soviet Union

The Soviet Union was Iraq's largest arms supplier, providing an estimated $30-40 billion in military equipment over the course of the war—accounting for more than half of Iraq's total military expenditures. Soviet deliveries between 1986-1988 alone included 800 T-72 tanks, 300 fighter aircraft, and hundreds of Scud-B missiles.

Soviet policy was not consistently pro-Iraq, however. Moscow initially tilted toward Iran from 1980-1982, hoping to exploit post-revolutionary anti-American sentiment. After 1982, when Iran's counteroffensives threatened to topple the Iraqi government, the Soviets shifted decisively toward Iraq. The USSR's primary strategic interest was preventing either side from achieving a decisive victory that might destabilize the region and draw in NATO forces.

### 5.4 France

France was the second-largest arms supplier to Iraq after the Soviet Union, providing approximately $17 billion in military equipment. French arms sales included cutting-edge technology that Iraq's other suppliers could not match:

- **Mirage F-1 fighters:** France sold 133 Mirage F-1EQ fighters to Iraq between 1977 and 1987, which became the backbone of the Iraqi Air Force.
- **Super Etendard attack aircraft:** In October 1983, France "loaned" five Super Etendard naval attack aircraft from its own inventory, equipped with Exocet AM-39 anti-ship missiles. These were used to devastating effect in the Tanker War against Iranian oil tankers and shipping. The aircraft were returned in 1985 once Iraq's Mirage F-1s were configured to carry Exocet missiles.
- **Exocet missiles:** Iraq acquired approximately 812 Exocet anti-ship missiles from France over a ten-year period, including more than 400 AM-39 air-launched variants.
- **Roland surface-to-air missiles, AMX-30 tanks, and various artillery systems.**

France's motivations were partly economic (Iraq was a lucrative customer paying in oil) and partly strategic (containing Iranian revolutionary influence).

### 5.5 Gulf States: Massive Financial Support for Iraq

The Arab Gulf states, despite historically uneasy relations with Ba'athist Iraq, feared Iranian revolutionary ideology far more and provided massive financial support:

- **Saudi Arabia:** Provided an estimated $25-30.9 billion in aid, including approximately $1 billion per month from mid-1982 onward. Saudi Arabia also sold oil on Iraq's behalf from the neutral zone.
- **Kuwait:** Contributed approximately $6-8.2 billion, with nearly half in the form of oil sales on Iraq's behalf.
- **United Arab Emirates:** Provided approximately $8 billion.
- **Total Gulf state support:** Estimated at $37-60 billion in loans and grants.

Ironically, Iraq's massive postwar debt to Kuwait (approximately $14 billion) and Saddam's belief that Kuwait was overproducing oil to keep prices low became key factors in Iraq's invasion of Kuwait in 1990, leading to the Gulf War.

### 5.6 Israel: The Enemy's Enemy

In one of the war's most paradoxical dimensions, Israel secretly sold weapons and spare parts to the Islamic Republic of Iran throughout the conflict. Despite Khomeini's virulent anti-Israel rhetoric, both nations shared a common enemy in Saddam Hussein's Iraq.

Israel's strategic rationale was straightforward:
- Iraq, which had sent troops to fight Israel in multiple Arab-Israeli wars, was considered the more immediate military threat, especially after revelations about Iraq's nuclear weapons program (Israel had destroyed Iraq's Osirak reactor in 1981).
- A prolonged Iran-Iraq war that bled both nations served Israel's security interests. As one Israeli defense minister reportedly stated: "It is better for the two to kill each other than to unite against us."
- Israel wanted to maintain contacts with Iran in the hope that the revolutionary regime might eventually moderate.

Israeli arms sales to Iran began within months of the 1979 revolution and continued throughout the war. Shipments included U.S.-origin spare parts for F-4 Phantom jets, radar components, anti-tank TOW missiles, and HAWK missile parts, often routed through intermediaries, shell companies, and third-country ports to conceal their origin. The estimated value of Israeli arms sales to Iran reached $500 million between 1981 and 1983 alone, according to the Jaffee Center for Strategic Studies at Tel Aviv University.

## 6. Chemical Weapons

### 6.1 Iraq's Chemical Weapons Program

Iraq's use of chemical weapons during the Iran-Iraq War represented the most extensive employment of chemical warfare since World War I and included the first verified combat use of nerve agents in history. Iraq initially deployed mustard gas in 1983, then escalated to tabun (a nerve agent) in 1984, followed by sarin and, according to some reports, VX nerve agent.

Iraq conducted more than 30 targeted chemical attacks against Iranian military forces and civilians over the course of the war. Chemical weapons were used both tactically—to break up Iranian human-wave assaults—and strategically, to terrorize civilian populations.

### 6.2 The Halabja Massacre (March 16, 1988)

The most notorious chemical attack of the war was the March 16, 1988 bombardment of the Kurdish city of Halabja in northeastern Iraq. Iraqi aircraft and artillery struck the city with a mixture of mustard gas, tabun, sarin, and possibly VX nerve agent, killing at least 3,200 Kurdish civilians—men, women, and children. Thousands more suffered long-term health effects including cancers, respiratory diseases, and birth defects.

Halabja was part of the broader Anfal campaign (1986-1989), a systematic Iraqi military operation against Kurdish civilians that killed an estimated 50,000-100,000 Kurds and was later classified as genocide by several countries and international courts.

### 6.3 Western Complicity

The international community's response to Iraq's chemical weapons use was, by most accounts, shamefully inadequate. The Reagan administration continued to provide intelligence and economic support to Iraq despite knowing that Saddam was carrying out the worst chemical attacks in history. Iraq's chemical weapons program relied on foreign suppliers for precursor chemicals and technology:

- Dutch businessman Frans van Anraat was convicted in 2005 of complicity in war crimes for shipping more than 1,100 tons of thiodiglycol (a mustard gas precursor) from a U.S. company to Iraq.
- German, British, and other Western companies supplied dual-use equipment and chemicals.
- Iraq was not severely criticized internationally for its chemical weapons use until after Halabja in 1988; the previous five to six years of chemical warfare against Iran received minimal international attention because Western governments prioritized containing Iranian revolutionary influence.

The United Nations investigated and confirmed Iraq's use of chemical weapons on multiple occasions but took no enforcement action. For Iranians, the world's silence in the face of chemical attacks remains a powerful source of grievance and distrust of international institutions. It also directly influenced Iran's later insistence on self-reliance in military matters—if the international community would not protect them from chemical weapons, they would have to develop their own deterrent capabilities.

## 7. Iran's Proxy Development: The Creation of Hezbollah

The Iran-Iraq War catalyzed Iran's development of its most important and enduring proxy organization: Lebanon's Hezbollah.

### 7.1 Origins

In June 1982, Israel invaded Lebanon. Iran responded by deploying approximately 1,500 Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) advisers to Lebanon's Bekaa Valley, with Syrian permission. Their mission was to organize, train, and arm a new Shia militia. An additional 800 Revolutionary Guards provided political and religious indoctrination, military training, and instruction in guerrilla and terrorist tactics.

### 7.2 Formation and Development

Under IRGC supervision, various existing Lebanese Shia militant factions were consolidated into a single organization: Hezbollah (Party of God), formally established between 1982 and 1985. Iran provided comprehensive support including:
- Financial backing (eventually reaching hundreds of millions of dollars annually)
- Weapons and military equipment
- Training in guerrilla warfare, intelligence operations, and terrorist tactics
- Political and ideological guidance
- Organizational structure modeled on Iran's revolutionary institutions

### 7.3 Strategic Significance

Hezbollah's creation during the Iran-Iraq War established the template for Iran's "forward defense" doctrine—projecting power through trained, ideologically aligned proxy forces rather than through direct conventional military action. This model was later replicated with Iraqi Shia militias, Palestinian groups (Hamas and Islamic Jihad), the Houthis in Yemen, and Afghan and Pakistani Shia militias (the Fatemiyoun and Zainabiyoun brigades).

## 8. Khomeini's "Drinking Poison" — Accepting the Ceasefire

### 8.1 Military Reverses of 1988

By mid-1988, Iran's military position had deteriorated dramatically. Iraq's use of chemical weapons, combined with superior air power, Western intelligence support, and Gulf state financing, had tipped the balance. In April 1988, Iraq recaptured the Fao Peninsula, which Iran had seized in 1986 in one of its few successful offensive operations. Further Iraqi offensives in June and July 1988 recaptured additional territory.

The Tanker War, Operation Praying Mantis, and the USS Vincennes shootdown demonstrated Iran's vulnerability to American military power. Iran's economy was shattered, its people were demoralized, and Iranian military commanders informed Khomeini that victory was impossible within the next five years.

### 8.2 UN Security Council Resolution 598

Resolution 598, adopted on July 20, 1987, demanded an immediate ceasefire. Iran had rejected it for a year, insisting that Iraq be identified as the aggressor and that reparations be paid. Under mounting military pressure, Khomeini convened a meeting on July 15, 1988, and announced his acceptance of the resolution.

On July 20, 1988, Khomeini made his famous statement that accepting the ceasefire was "more deadly than drinking from a poisoned chalice." He stated that he had made his decision "solely on the basis of expediency" and "in the interest of the revolution." The ceasefire became effective on August 20, 1988.

### 8.3 The 1988 Prison Massacres

In the immediate aftermath of accepting the ceasefire, the Iranian regime carried out mass executions of political prisoners, primarily members of the Mojahedin-e Khalq (MEK) and leftist groups. Estimates of those killed range from several thousand to 30,000. These executions, ordered by Khomeini himself through a fatwa, represented a final act of ideological consolidation as the regime, humiliated by accepting the "poisoned chalice," moved to eliminate internal dissent.

## 9. Casualties and Economic Devastation

### 9.1 Human Cost

The Iran-Iraq War produced staggering casualties:
- **Total deaths:** Estimated at 500,000 to 1,000,000 on both sides, making it the deadliest conventional war between developing nations in modern history.
- **Iranian casualties:** Iran bore the heaviest losses, with estimates of 200,000-600,000 killed and 500,000+ wounded. An estimated 40,000 Iranians were affected by Iraqi chemical weapons attacks.
- **Iraqi casualties:** Estimated at 105,000-375,000 killed and 400,000+ wounded.
- **Kurdish casualties:** An estimated 50,000-100,000 Kurds were killed during the Anfal campaign (1987-1989).
- **Prisoners of war:** Both sides held tens of thousands of POWs, some for over a decade after the war ended. An estimated 19,000 Iraqi soldiers were captured in the liberation of Khorramshahr alone.

### 9.2 Economic Destruction

The war devastated both economies:
- **Iraq's oil production** fell from 3.4 million barrels per day to under 1 million in the first year of the war. Oil revenues in 1988 totaled $11 billion—less than half the pre-war amount. Iraq's foreign debt soared to over $80 billion by 1988, with $37-60 billion owed to Gulf states.
- **Iran's economy** suffered an estimated $1 trillion in direct and indirect costs. Oil export infrastructure was repeatedly targeted, reducing revenue. Foreign exchange reserves were depleted, development projects were abandoned, and the population endured severe shortages and rationing.
- Both nations' oil-exporting capacity was severely reduced at various points, and the consequent loss of income brought their economic development programs to a near standstill.

## 10. Legacy: How the War Shaped Iran

### 10.1 Military Doctrine: Self-Reliance and Asymmetric Warfare

The war fundamentally shaped Iran's military doctrine in several enduring ways:

- **Self-reliance (khod-kafai):** The international arms embargo and the failure of any country to defend Iran against chemical weapons attacks instilled a deep commitment to indigenous arms production. Concepts such as self-reliance, "holy defense," and export of the revolution entered the military lexicon during the war and were codified as doctrine in the early 1990s.

- **Asymmetric warfare:** The IRGC's experience using small boats, mines, and anti-ship cruise missiles against the U.S. Navy during the Tanker War placed asymmetric warfare concepts at the center of Iran's offensive and deterrence doctrine. Rather than attempting to match American conventional military superiority, Iran developed strategies emphasizing irregular warfare, area-denial capabilities, and proxy forces.

- **Ballistic missile program:** Iraq's devastating missile attacks on Iranian cities spurred the development of Iran's indigenous ballistic missile program. Beginning with reverse-engineered Scud technology to produce the Shahab-1 and Shahab-2, Iran eventually developed a diverse missile arsenal including the Shahab-3 (medium-range), Sejjil (solid-fuel medium-range), and various short-range rockets (Zelzal, Fateh, and their successors). The missile program became a cornerstone of Iran's deterrence strategy—compensating for its lack of modern air power.

- **Proxy warfare doctrine:** The successful creation of Hezbollah during the war established the model for Iran's "forward defense" strategy—projecting influence and deterrence through proxy forces across the region.

### 10.2 Entrenchment of Revolutionary Institutions

The war profoundly strengthened the Islamic Republic's revolutionary institutions:

- **The IRGC** was transformed from a loosely organized revolutionary militia into a parallel military establishment with a conventional army, navy, air force, and special operations capability. Its economic role expanded massively during the war as it handled engineering, logistics, and reconstruction. By war's end, the IRGC had established itself as a permanent power center within the Iranian state, with deep political influence and eventually a vast economic empire controlling significant portions of Iran's GDP.

- **The Basij** evolved from a wartime volunteer militia into a permanent internal security and social control organization. The massive sacrifices of Basij volunteers during the war gave the organization enormous political legitimacy. After the war, the Basij was placed under IRGC control and became a primary instrument for suppressing domestic dissent, including during the 1999 student protests, the 2009 Green Movement, and the 2022 Mahsa Amini protests.

- **The war generation** of IRGC and Basij veterans became the backbone of Iran's conservative political establishment. Many senior Iranian political and military leaders in subsequent decades—including the future Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, President Mahmoud Ahmadinejad, IRGC Quds Force commander Qassem Soleimani, and numerous members of parliament—were war veterans whose worldview was permanently shaped by the conflict.

- **The "Sacred Defense" narrative** became central to the Islamic Republic's legitimacy. The war was framed as a existential struggle against foreign aggression, justifying authoritarian governance, military spending, and revolutionary ideology. Museums, memorials, and annual commemorations kept the war's memory alive as a tool of political mobilization.

### 10.3 Regional and Global Consequences

- The war's legacy directly precipitated the 1990 Gulf War: Saddam invaded Kuwait partly to seize resources to pay off war debts and punish Kuwait for allegedly overproducing oil.
- The proliferation of weapons of mass destruction accelerated, with both nations pursuing missile, chemical, and (in the case of Iraq) biological and nuclear weapons programs.
- The war deepened Sunni-Shia sectarian tensions across the Middle East, establishing a pattern of proxy rivalry between Iran and the Arab Gulf states that continues to shape regional conflicts.
- For Iran, the war validated the revolutionary government's narrative of resistance against foreign aggression and Western betrayal, reinforcing the fortress mentality that defines Iranian foreign policy to this day.
