# Concept Research: Research Methodology, Ethics, and the Politics of Communication Access

## Overview

The study of non-speaking communication in autism exists at the intersection of science, ethics, and politics in a way that is unusual even for a field as contested as autism research. The methodological question — "how do we determine whether a communication method actually works?" — is inseparable from the ethical question — "who has the right to communicate, and who decides?" — which is inseparable from the political question — "whose voices count in debates about non-speaking people's lives?" These three strands are so tightly woven that pulling on any one of them moves the others.

At the methodological level, the gold standard for verifying authorship in assisted communication is the message-passing test: the communicator is shown something the facilitator cannot see, and is asked to communicate what they saw. If the output matches what the communicator saw (not what the facilitator saw), authorship is established. If it matches what the facilitator saw, facilitator influence is indicated. Approximately 40-50 controlled studies using this and related paradigms in the 1990s found overwhelming evidence of facilitator influence in facilitated communication, with correct responses under blinded conditions ranging from 0% to approximately 1.8%. Proponents argue these tests are ecologically invalid — that communication requires trust, familiar partners, and low-stress conditions that laboratory settings cannot provide. Critics respond that this objection would invalidate all controlled research and that the tests revealed facilitator influence, not communicator failure.

At the ethical level, informed consent presents a paradox: how do you ethically study the communication of people whose communicative capacity is the very thing you are investigating? If a person cannot reliably communicate through verified methods, how do they consent to participate in research? And if their communication method is itself under study, using that method to obtain consent is circular. The 2025 ethical listening framework (Aidonopoulou-Read) proposes combining video recordings, behavior checklists, and autism-affirming theories as alternatives to traditional consent processes — but the field has not yet converged on a standard approach.

At the political level, the debate over non-speaking communication is inseparable from the disability rights movement's central principle: "Nothing about us without us." This principle demands that non-speaking autistic people be included in decisions about their own lives, their communication methods, and the research that studies them. But this demand encounters a recursion problem: if a person's communication method is contested (as FC and S2C are), then including their "voice" in the debate may actually be including the facilitator's voice. Conversely, excluding non-speaking individuals from decisions about their own communication methods because their communication is contested reproduces exactly the gatekeeping the principle was designed to prevent. This paradox has no clean resolution, and any honest treatment of the topic must sit with that discomfort rather than pretending to resolve it.

## Core Components

### Component 1: Message-Passing Tests — The Gold Standard and Its Limitations
- **Purpose**: The primary experimental paradigm for determining whether a communicator or a facilitator is authoring a message
- **How it works**:
  1. The communicator is shown a stimulus (an object, picture, word, or asked a question) that the facilitator cannot see or hear
  2. The facilitator is either given no information, different information, or the same information as the communicator
  3. The communicator attempts to communicate about the stimulus using the method being tested
  4. Correct responses when the facilitator does not know the answer = evidence for communicator authorship
  5. Responses matching the facilitator's (incorrect) information = evidence for facilitator influence
- **Results from controlled studies**: When facilitators did not observe or got false information, correct responses were minimal (0% to 1.8%). In false-information trials, 66% (for pictures) to 80% (for activities) of the facilitated output matched what the facilitator was told, not what the participant experienced.
- **Scale**: Approximately 40-50 controlled studies conducted primarily in the 1990s, with systematic reviews (Mostert 2001, 2010; Schlosser et al. 2014; Hemsley et al. 2018) consistently finding no evidence for communicator authorship under blinded conditions.
- **Ecological validity critique (proponent argument)**:
  - Communication requires trust and familiar partners; laboratory settings are stressful and artificial
  - Performance anxiety may prevent communicators from demonstrating abilities they possess in comfortable settings
  - The test assumes ability to communicate on demand about assigned topics, which may not reflect natural communication capacity
  - The emotional disruption of being separated from a trusted facilitator may itself impair communication
- **Response to ecological validity critique (skeptic argument)**:
  - If a communication method cannot demonstrate authorship under ANY controlled conditions, the default explanation remains facilitator influence
  - The "stress" objection would invalidate all controlled research on any topic
  - The test does not merely fail to find communication — it actively finds facilitator influence (output matching facilitator's, not communicator's, information), which is a positive finding, not an absence of evidence
  - Proponents advance "the unsubstantiated claim that individuals' ability to message-pass decreases under controlled or 'stressful' situations"
- **Key publication**: "Can message-passing anecdotes tell us anything about the validity of RPM and S2C?" (Evidence-Based Communication Assessment and Intervention, 2023) argues that even informal message-passing anecdotes promoted by S2C/RPM supporters do not withstand methodological scrutiny
- **S2C/RPM authorship evidence**: A compilation at facilitatedcommunication.org documents that published authorship results for S2C/RPM are also negative

### Component 2: Eye-Tracking as Alternative Methodology
- **Purpose**: Studying communication agency through gaze behavior rather than message-passing
- **Jaswal et al. 2020 study**: Used head-mounted eye-tracking with 9 nonspeaking autistic letterboard users. Found that participants looked at and pointed to letters quickly, with patterns the authors interpret as evidence of planning and agency.
- **Published critiques**:
  - Katharine Beals (Catherine & Katharine blog): The study is based on faulty assumptions about "test performance, prerequisites for linguistic skills, a disconnect between speech-based vs. typing-based communication, what it would take for someone to point to letters based on cues from the board-holder, and the study's central premise: the agency of eye gaze"
  - Skeptical Inquirer: Detailed methodological concerns, including no control condition without a facilitator present, no blinding, and failure to explain why participants could hunt-and-peck type lengthy responses but could not articulate words through speech
  - Small sample size (N=9) with no comparison group
  - The study measured eye-gaze patterns but did not include a message-passing component that would directly test authorship
- **Jaswal et al. 2024 literacy study**: Used a tapping paradigm rather than message-passing. 17 of 31 participants showed patterns consistent with orthographic knowledge. Published rebuttal ("Illusions of literacy") raised concerns about participants' letterboard experience as a confound and absence of key control conditions.
- **Evidence status**: Eye-tracking provides interesting observational data but has not been accepted by the broader scientific community as a substitute for controlled authorship verification. The methodological critiques are substantial and have not been fully addressed.

### Component 3: Informed Consent Challenges
- **Purpose**: Examines the ethical problem of obtaining consent from people whose communication is the subject of study
- **The paradox**: If a person communicates only through a method whose validity is being tested (e.g., FC or S2C), then using that method to obtain their consent to study the method is circular. The consent itself may be facilitator-influenced.
- **Current approaches**:
  - Proxy consent (parent/guardian consenting on behalf): Widely used but raises autonomy concerns — the person being studied does not consent for themselves
  - Assent indicators: Observing behavioral indicators of willingness to participate (approaching the research setting, engaging with materials, not showing distress). The 2025 ethical listening framework recommends combining video recordings and behavior checklists as assent evidence.
  - Accessible consent materials: A 2025 study (PMC12448064) provides guidelines for creating consent materials that accommodate processing and communication differences, including visual formats, plain language, and extended processing time
  - Multi-modal consent: Allowing consent through any available communication method, with verification through behavioral observation
- **Unresolved tensions**:
  - The standard for "informed" consent in research typically requires demonstrated understanding of risks, benefits, and the right to withdraw. How this standard can be met by someone with uncertain communicative capacity remains contested.
  - Some argue that excluding non-speaking individuals from research to protect them from consent risks actually harms them by ensuring research never addresses their needs
  - The 2025 ethical listening framework proposes that ethical listening "means respectfully attending to multiple representations of human experience and knowledge with all of our senses, and refraining from judging a person or the merit of their communication based on difference"

### Component 4: The ASHA-Community Conflict
- **Purpose**: Documents the clash between professional organization recommendations and disability advocacy positions
- **ASHA's position**: In 2018, ASHA approved position statements discouraging use of both FC and RPM/S2C, following a systematic literature review, expert panel, and public comment period. ASHA warns that "there is no evidence that messages produced using RPM reflect the communication of the person with a disability." ASHA's stated goal: "The primary goal of speech-language intervention is independent communication, which is a basic human right, ensuring that words, thoughts, and feelings expressed are indeed their own."
- **ASAN's response**: In 2018, ASAN wrote a letter to ASHA stating they are "committed to ensuring access to a wide range of effective communication supports" and "do not take positions on individual communication support methods." ASAN raised concerns about the process: when ASAN attempted to learn about ASHA's review committee and offer input, they were "denied the opportunity to speak directly to the Committee members or even learn the identities of Committee members."
- **Parent community response**: Many parents of non-speaking autistic individuals who use S2C or RPM have vocally opposed ASHA's position, arguing it denies their children a voice. Some have organized through groups like United for Communication Choice.
- **Practitioner divide**: SLPs are divided. Some follow ASHA's guidance strictly. Others continue to use RPM/S2C approaches or work with clients who do. Some have faced professional consequences for deviating from ASHA's position.
- **The tension**: ASHA prioritizes the scientific evidence (controlled studies showing facilitator influence) and the protection of vulnerable individuals from potential facilitator manipulation. The autism community prioritizes communication access and the right of non-speaking individuals to use the methods they prefer. Both positions contain genuine moral weight. The conflict cannot be resolved by declaring one side correct because they are making different kinds of claims (empirical vs. rights-based) that operate in different domains.

### Component 5: CommunicationFIRST and Cross-Disability Advocacy
- **Purpose**: Documents the role of CommunicationFIRST and the cross-disability context
- **Organization**: CommunicationFIRST is "the only nonprofit organization dedicated to protecting and advancing the civil rights of the more than 5 million children and adults in the United States who, due to disability or other condition, cannot rely on speech alone to be heard and understood."
- **Critical clarification**: CommunicationFIRST is a cross-disability organization. The "5+ million" figure refers to ALL people with speech-related communication disabilities across ALL conditions — ALS, cerebral palsy, stroke, traumatic brain injury, intellectual disability, AND autism. This is not an autism-specific number. With approximately 5.4 million autistic adults in the US and 25-30% non-speaking, the autism-specific estimate would be roughly 1.3-1.6 million — significant, but substantially lower than 5 million.
- **Leadership**: Jordyn Zimmerman serves as Board Chair. Grant Blasko serves on the Advisory Council.
- **Advocacy focus**: Communication access as a civil right. Policy reform at federal and state levels. The "Communication for All" campaign. Legal advocacy for ADA compliance.
- **Relationship to FC/S2C debate**: CommunicationFIRST does not take an explicit position on FC/S2C. Their focus is on ensuring all people have access to effective communication, which they define broadly. Their leadership (Zimmerman, Blasko) uses standard AAC methods.

### Component 6: Legal Framework for Communication Rights
- **Purpose**: Documents the laws that establish communication access as a right
- **ADA (Americans with Disabilities Act)**:
  - Title II: People with disabilities have the right to equal access to state and local public services, including communication accommodations
  - ADA.gov specifies that covered entities must provide "appropriate auxiliary aids and services where necessary to ensure effective communication"
  - This includes AAC devices, interpreters, and other communication supports
- **IDEA (Individuals with Disabilities Education Act)**:
  - Guarantees a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE) for all children with disabilities
  - IEPs must address communication needs, including AAC provision
  - Mandates education in the Least Restrictive Environment (LRE)
  - An IEP team must consider "whether the child needs assistive technology devices and services" — including communication technology
- **Implementation gaps**: Despite legal mandates, non-speaking autistic individuals routinely lack communication access:
  - AAC devices are expensive ($5,000-$15,000 for dedicated devices; less for tablet-based apps)
  - Insurance coverage varies by state and plan
  - Many schools lack staff trained in AAC implementation
  - The "assessment-placement" loop (see Category 9) can result in children being assessed as unable to benefit from AAC, creating a circular denial of access
  - Non-speaking individuals in the legal system face additional barriers: their communication forms "may be viewed as eccentric or suspicious, leading to misunderstandings and negative outcomes"

### Component 7: The Politics of "Nothing About Us Without Us"
- **Purpose**: Examines how the disability rights movement's central principle applies to non-speaking communication
- **The principle**: "Nothing About Us Without Us" — that disabled people must be centrally involved in all decisions that affect their lives. ASAN's founding principle.
- **The recursion problem**: Applied to non-speaking communication, the principle encounters a logical recursion:
  - Non-speaking autistic people should be included in decisions about their communication → But their inclusion requires a communication method → If the communication method is contested (FC, S2C), their "inclusion" may actually be facilitator inclusion → Excluding them because their method is contested reproduces the gatekeeping the principle opposes → The principle cannot be applied without first resolving the communication method question, which is the very issue under debate
- **Attempted resolutions**:
  - Prioritize non-speaking individuals who use uncontested methods (standard AAC): Zimmerman, Blasko, and others can speak to the experience of being non-speaking using verified communication. But they may not represent the full population.
  - Accept all communication methods and include all voices: Respects autonomy but risks including facilitator-authored input alongside authentic voices.
  - Conduct verification studies and include only verified communicators: Scientifically rigorous but may exclude individuals whose communication is genuine but not yet verified.
  - None of these resolutions is fully satisfactory. The honest position is to acknowledge the paradox while prioritizing the inclusion of individuals whose communication methods are uncontested.

### Component 8: The Tension Between Evidence-Based Practice and Communication Access
- **Purpose**: Examines the fundamental tension between scientific rigor and the human right to communicate
- **The evidence-based practice position**: Communication interventions should be supported by controlled research before being widely adopted. Methods that have failed controlled authorship tests (FC) or that have no controlled evidence (RPM, S2C) should not be promoted as evidence-based. Promoting unvalidated methods risks harm (false hope, facilitator-authored communication mistaken for authentic).
- **The communication access position**: Non-speaking autistic people have waited decades for the scientific community to provide proven methods. The absence of evidence for newer methods (RPM, S2C) may reflect the absence of research rather than the absence of efficacy. Denying access to methods that some families report as effective based on "insufficient evidence" may itself cause harm — leaving non-speaking individuals without any communication.
- **The presuming competence framework**: Reframing facilitated communication argues that FC research should be "reconsidered and reconducted using current best practice autism research approaches, including coproduction and a presumption of autistic communication competence" (PMC8960292, 2022). This perspective holds that the FC studies of the 1990s used outdated, ableist methods.
- **The skeptical response**: The existing evidence does not merely show "insufficient evidence" — it actively shows facilitator influence. Approximately 40-50 controlled studies found that when facilitators were blinded, output matched the facilitator's knowledge, not the communicator's. This is a positive finding of facilitator control, not merely an absence of evidence for communicator authorship. Proposing to discard this evidence and start over with a "presumption of competence" is proposing to ignore the most relevant evidence available.
- **Where both sides have a point**:
  - Evidence-based practice correctly insists that unvalidated methods carry risks and should be tested before adoption
  - Communication access advocates correctly note that non-speaking individuals cannot wait for perfect evidence before receiving communication tools
  - The resolution may lie in distinguishing between evidence-based AAC (which should be the first-line intervention) and contested methods (which should be available under informed consent while rigorous research is conducted) — but this resolution is itself contested

### Component 9: Emerging Ethical Listening Frameworks
- **Purpose**: Documents newer approaches to including non-speaking individuals in research ethically
- **Aidonopoulou-Read (2025)**: "Empowering non-speaking autistic participants and neurotypical researchers in qualitative research through ethical listening: A framework." Published in SAGE, 2025.
- **Core principles**:
  - Ethical listening means "respectfully attending to multiple representations of human experience and knowledge with all of our senses, and refraining from judging a person or the merit of their communication based on difference"
  - Combines video recordings, behavior checklists, and autism-affirming theories as "ethical listening enablers"
  - Aims to include non-speaking individuals' perspectives without requiring traditional speech-based consent or communication
- **AAC user inclusion in research**: A 2025 Taylor & Francis article calls for "meaningful inclusion" of AAC users in "research, technology development, and professional training" — "Nothing about AAC users without AAC users"
- **Participatory research principles**: Community-based participatory research (CBPR) involves researchers and community members working together as equal partners. For non-speaking autism research, this requires developing methodologies that accommodate diverse communication methods while maintaining scientific rigor.
- **Challenges remaining**:
  - How to ensure that "ethical listening" does not become a way to bypass the empirical question of whether a communication method actually transmits the communicator's thoughts
  - How to balance the inclusion imperative with the need for methodological rigor
  - How to avoid "ethics-washing" — using the language of ethics and inclusion to avoid difficult empirical questions

## How It Works

### Step 1: A Communication Method Emerges
- **Trigger**: A new approach to non-speaking communication is developed or becomes popular (FC in the 1980s; RPM in the 1990s; S2C in the 2010s; letterboard communication broadly)
- **Action**: Families and practitioners adopt the method based on apparent results. Non-speaking individuals appear to produce sophisticated communication. Testimonials and case reports accumulate.
- **Result**: Widespread adoption based on clinical enthusiasm and family testimonials, without controlled research

### Step 2: Calls for Scientific Verification
- **Trigger**: Scientific community notes the absence of controlled evidence; cases of apparent facilitator influence emerge; false allegations occur (FC in the 1990s)
- **Action**: Controlled studies are conducted. Message-passing tests reveal facilitator influence. Systematic reviews find no evidence of communicator authorship under blinded conditions.
- **Result**: Professional organizations (ASHA, AAP, APA) issue position statements against the method

### Step 3: The Political Backlash
- **Trigger**: Professional organizations' warnings are perceived as silencing non-speaking individuals
- **Action**: Disability advocacy groups (ASAN, CommunicationFIRST, parent organizations) push back. They argue that controlled studies use inappropriate methods, that ecological validity is violated, and that non-speaking individuals have a right to their preferred communication method regardless of scientific consensus.
- **Result**: The debate becomes polarized between "follow the evidence" and "respect the person"

### Step 4: Methodological Stalemate
- **Trigger**: Neither side can convince the other
- **Action**: New methods emerge (eye-tracking, tapping paradigms) that attempt to study communication agency without traditional message-passing tests. These studies are immediately contested by both sides.
- **Result**: A field stuck between rigorous but potentially exclusionary science and inclusive but potentially uncritical acceptance

### Step 5: The Emerging Resolution Attempt
- **Trigger**: Growing recognition that the stalemate harms non-speaking individuals most of all
- **Action**: Ethical listening frameworks attempt to include non-speaking perspectives. Calls for new research designs that are both rigorous and accessible. Focus on evidence-based AAC as uncontested common ground while contested methods are studied further.
- **Result**: Partial progress — more non-speaking individuals using standard AAC in advocacy roles (Zimmerman, Blasko); ongoing dispute about FC/S2C variants; no resolution of the fundamental tension

## Diagram Specifications

### Diagram 1: The Message-Passing Test Paradigm
- **Type**: Experimental design diagram / flowchart
- **Purpose**: Shows exactly how message-passing tests work and what the results mean
- **Elements**:
  - Condition 1: Facilitator and communicator see SAME stimulus → facilitated output matches stimulus (ambiguous — both could be the source)
  - Condition 2: Only communicator sees stimulus (facilitator blind) → facilitated output does NOT match stimulus (indicates communicator is NOT authoring)
  - Condition 3: Facilitator sees DIFFERENT stimulus from communicator → facilitated output matches FACILITATOR's stimulus (demonstrates facilitator influence)
  - Results: Conditions 2 & 3 consistently show facilitator influence
- **Connections**: Decision tree showing what each outcome means
- **Notes**: Include the actual data: 0-1.8% correct under blinded conditions; 66-80% matching facilitator's false information

### Diagram 2: The Ecological Validity Debate
- **Type**: Argument/counterargument flow diagram
- **Purpose**: Maps the two sides of the ecological validity debate
- **Elements**:
  - Proponent argument chain: Lab settings are stressful → Stress impairs communication → Low performance reflects stress, not lack of ability → Tests are ecologically invalid
  - Skeptic counterargument chain: Tests don't merely show low performance → They show facilitator-matched output → This is a positive finding of influence, not an absence of communication → Stress cannot explain why output matches what the facilitator knows
- **Connections**: Point-counterpoint mapping
- **Notes**: Clearly mark where each argument is stronger or weaker; the facilitator-matched-output finding is the strongest skeptic evidence because stress cannot explain it

### Diagram 3: The Informed Consent Paradox
- **Type**: Circular/recursive diagram
- **Purpose**: Visualizes the logical circularity of consent in communication research
- **Elements**:
  - Step 1: Researcher wants to study whether communication method X works
  - Step 2: Researcher needs consent from participant
  - Step 3: Participant can only consent through communication method X
  - Step 4: If method X is invalid, consent obtained through it is invalid
  - Step 5: But we cannot know if method X is valid without studying it (requiring consent)
  - Return to Step 1
- **Connections**: Circular arrows showing the recursive loop
- **Notes**: Include escape routes: proxy consent, behavioral assent indicators, ethical listening framework

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: The Message-Passing Test in Action
- **Scenario**: A researcher shows a picture of a dog to a non-speaking participant. The facilitator, behind a screen, is shown a picture of a house. The participant is asked to type what they saw.
- **Illustration**: Through facilitated communication, the output reads "HOUSE" — matching what the facilitator saw, not what the communicator saw. This is replicated across dozens of similar trials with different stimuli. In trials where both see the same image, the output is correct. In trials where only the communicator sees the stimulus and the facilitator sees nothing, the output is nonsensical or no output is produced.
- **Key Insight**: This result cannot be explained by stress, ecological invalidity, or communicator anxiety. The output specifically and consistently matches the facilitator's knowledge. This is the strongest evidence against FC authorship and the most difficult for proponents to explain.

### Example 2: The Consent Paradox in Practice
- **Scenario**: A researcher wants to conduct a validation study of letterboard communication. The proposed participants communicate exclusively through letterboards held by trained facilitators.
- **Illustration**: The IRB (Institutional Review Board) requires informed consent. The researcher proposes obtaining consent through the participants' letterboard communication. The IRB objects: if the study's purpose is to determine whether letterboard communication reflects the communicator's thoughts, using letterboard communication to obtain consent is circular. The researcher proposes proxy consent from parents. Disability advocates object: proxy consent denies the participants' autonomy. The study is stalled.
- **Key Insight**: This is not a hypothetical — it reflects actual barriers to conducting research on contested communication methods. The consent paradox contributes to the evidence vacuum that perpetuates the debate.

### Example 3: The ASHA Warning and a Family's Choice
- **Scenario**: A family discovers that their non-speaking autistic teenager has begun producing text through S2C with a trained communication partner. The text includes emotional content, personal preferences, and academic-level vocabulary. ASHA warns that S2C is not evidence-based and may involve facilitator influence.
- **Illustration**: The family is faced with an impossible choice: believe their child is communicating for the first time (which aligns with their observations but conflicts with scientific evidence) or accept that the communication may not be their child's (which protects against facilitator influence but potentially takes away their child's voice). There is no guidance that adequately addresses this dilemma. Evidence-based AAC may or may not be effective for this particular teenager. The family's lived experience and the scientific consensus point in different directions.
- **Key Insight**: The political and personal dimensions of this debate are not academic abstractions — they are daily realities for families navigating communication decisions for non-speaking family members. Any honest treatment must acknowledge that these decisions are genuinely difficult, that the evidence points in one direction, and that families' motivations (wanting to hear their child's voice) are entirely sympathetic even when the method may be compromised.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: The FC debate is about whether non-speaking autistic people can think or communicate
  - **Reality**: The debate is specifically about whether a particular method (FC and its variants) transmits the communicator's thoughts or the facilitator's thoughts. No one disputes that non-speaking autistic people have thoughts, deserve communication access, and can benefit from evidence-based AAC.

- **Misconception**: CommunicationFIRST's "5+ million" figure represents the number of non-speaking autistic Americans
  - **Reality**: CommunicationFIRST is a cross-disability organization. The 5+ million figure includes everyone who cannot rely on speech due to ANY disability — ALS, cerebral palsy, stroke, TBI, intellectual disability, AND autism. The autism-specific estimate is approximately 1.3-1.6 million non-speaking Americans.

- **Misconception**: The message-passing test merely shows that non-speaking people cannot communicate under stress
  - **Reality**: The test does not merely show absence of communication — it shows positive evidence of facilitator influence. When facilitators are given incorrect information, the output matches the incorrect information, not the communicator's experience. Stress cannot explain why the output specifically and consistently matches what the facilitator (incorrectly) believes.

- **Misconception**: "Evidence-based practice" and "communication access" are opposed goals
  - **Reality**: Evidence-based AAC (Proloquo2Go, TouchChat, LAMP, eye-gaze systems) IS communication access. The conflict is specifically about unvalidated methods (FC, RPM, S2C), not about communication access in general. Every major professional organization, including ASHA, strongly supports AAC access for non-speaking individuals. The debate is about which specific methods are safe and effective, not about whether non-speaking people deserve communication tools.

- **Misconception**: Scientists who question FC/S2C want to silence non-speaking autistic people
  - **Reality**: Scientists who question FC/S2C are concerned that these methods may silence non-speaking people by substituting the facilitator's voice for theirs. The scientific motivation is the same as the advocacy motivation: ensuring that the non-speaking person's actual thoughts are what is communicated, not someone else's thoughts attributed to them.

## Edge Cases and Exceptions

- **Mixed-method communicators**: Some individuals use both standard AAC and supported letterboard methods in different contexts. How to evaluate their output when it may be authentic in one modality and potentially influenced in another is an unsolved problem.
- **Individuals who have "graduated" from supported to independent communication**: Some people (like Ido Kedar) began with RPM and transitioned to typing without physical facilitator involvement. Their earlier RPM output remains unverified, but their later independent typing may be genuine. How to treat the transition period is methodologically unclear.
- **Countries without professional regulatory bodies**: The ASHA-community debate is primarily an American phenomenon. In countries without equivalent professional organizations, there may be less institutional pushback against unvalidated methods — but also less protection against facilitator influence.
- **When families report observing clear authorship evidence**: Some families describe situations that strongly suggest communicator authorship (e.g., the non-speaking person communicating something the facilitator could not have known). These anecdotal observations are real but do not constitute controlled evidence. They may reflect genuine communication, facilitator cueing from subtle behavioral cues, or confirmation bias. Their status is irreducibly ambiguous without controlled verification.

## Related Concepts

- **Facilitated Communication Controversy (Category 3)**: The FC history provides the empirical foundation for the methodological concerns discussed here — the message-passing test results are the core evidence
- **Spelling-Based Methods (Category 4)**: S2C and RPM are the current focal points of the methodology debate — they are the methods ASHA warns against and advocates defend
- **Intelligence and Assessment (Category 9)**: The assessment problem is a specific instance of the broader methodology challenge — how to measure abilities when the measurement tools are confounded by the disabilities they are assessing
- **First-Person Accounts (Category 10)**: The authorship verification challenge directly affects how first-person accounts are evaluated
- **Neuroscience (Category 7)**: Neuroimaging offers the promise of objective evidence for language processing that bypasses the communication channel entirely — but the methodological barriers to scanning non-speaking participants create their own methodological challenges

## Research Gaps

- **No resolution mechanism for the ecological validity debate**: There is no agreed-upon standard for what constitutes an ecologically valid test of communication authorship. Until this is resolved, proponents and skeptics will continue to talk past each other about whether existing evidence is relevant.
- **Absence of large-scale, well-designed S2C/RPM studies**: The "empty systematic review" finding — that no S2C/RPM studies meet inclusion criteria — persists. Regardless of one's position, this evidence vacuum harms everyone: it prevents validation of methods that might work and prevents definitive rejection of methods that do not.
- **Informed consent standards for non-speaking research participants**: No consensus exists on how to ethically obtain consent from individuals whose communication is the subject of study. The 2025 ethical listening framework is a starting point, not a solution.
- **Cross-cultural research methodology**: The FC/S2C debate and the ASHA-community conflict are predominantly American. How non-speaking communication research and practice operate in other countries (UK, Australia, Europe, Global South) is underexplored.
- **Impact assessment of ASHA position statements**: What effect have ASHA's warnings actually had on clinical practice? Have they reduced FC/S2C use, or driven it underground? Have they increased or decreased communication access for non-speaking individuals? These questions have not been empirically studied.
- **The resolution pathway**: What specific research design could satisfy both sides? A study that is rigorous enough to detect facilitator influence, ecologically valid enough to allow natural communication, ethically designed to respect participant autonomy, and sufficiently large to produce generalizable results. Designing this study would be the single most valuable contribution to the field — but it has not yet been designed, let alone conducted.
