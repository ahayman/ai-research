# Research Validation Report: Co-Regulation, Social Engagement & Polyvagal-Informed Therapy

## Validation Summary

**Overall Reliability**: HIGH

- **Confirmed**: 14 claims (82%)
- **Uncertain**: 3 claims (18%)
- **Invalid**: 0 claims (0%)

**Key Findings**: The factual scaffolding of this chapter — publication dates, ISBNs, authors, sample sizes, effect sizes, foundational lineages — is well-supported by independent verification. The three uncertain claims involve population-level prevalence estimates that legitimately vary by study/sample and one quote attribution that is widely paraphrased but not directly verifiable to a single source quotation. No claim in the research was found to be invalid. The chapter's central analytical framework — that most polyvagal-informed therapy practices have independent evidence bases that don't require PVT being correct — is consistent with the historical record (attachment theory, somatic therapy lineages, EMDR's evidence base, breathing physiology) and with the published critique literature (Beauchaine's transdiagnostic biomarker work directly undermines PVT-specificity claims).

**Systemic Issues**: None. The research is careful to distinguish "polyvagal-vocabulary use" from "polyvagal-mechanism claims" throughout, which is the analytically correct framing.

### Claim 6: "Brom 2017 SE RCT enrolled n=63 (33 SE / 30 waitlist), 15 sessions, large effect sizes (d=0.94–1.26 PTSD, d=0.70–1.08 depression), 44.1% lost PTSD diagnosis."
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence for**: Brom, Stokar, Lawi, et al. (2017) *Journal of Traumatic Stress*, 30(3):304-312. PubMed/Wiley confirm sample n=63 (33 SE, 30 waitlist), 15 weekly SE sessions, effect sizes consistent with research draft (d=0.94-1.26 for PTSD; d=0.70-1.08 for depression), and 44.1% loss-of-diagnosis figure.
- **Sources**: [PubMed 28585761](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28585761/), [Wiley](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/jts.22189)
- **Notes**: Confirmed. The study was conducted in Israel during ongoing terrorist attacks; sample included variety of traumatic events.

### Claim 15: "Autistic adults carry psychiatric comorbidities at high rates: ~70% with at least one diagnosis; ~28-42% anxiety, ~23-37% depression."
- **Verdict**: UNCERTAIN (8/10) — ranges are appropriate but population-dependent
- **Evidence for**: Frontiers and other autism comorbidity meta-analyses confirm ranges in this general band. Pooled lifetime prevalence for depression ~37% and anxiety ~42% in adult ASD populations per meta-analytic estimates. The "70% with at least one" figure is also widely reported. ADHD pool prevalence ~28%; anxiety pool ~20% in some studies.
- **Why uncertain**: The exact ranges depend heavily on sample (clinical vs community), diagnostic instrument, age, gender, and inclusion criteria for autism. Different meta-analyses give different point estimates and ranges. The research draft's ranges are reasonable but should be treated as approximate.
- **Sources**: [Frontiers — Population-Based Psychiatric Comorbidity meta-analysis](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2022.856208/full), [Frontiers — Psychiatric Comorbidities in ASD](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2021.673169/full)
- **Notes**: Accurate as ranges; the explanation chapter should hedge specific percentages.

### Claim 16: "Sroufe formalised attachment as 'dyadic regulation of emotion' in 1996 *Emotional Development* textbook."
- **Verdict**: UNCERTAIN (7/10) — direction is correct, exact paraphrase may vary
- **Evidence for**: Sroufe LA (1996). *Emotional Development: The Organization of Emotional Life in the Early Years*. Cambridge University Press. The "dyadic regulation" framing is widely cited as a Sroufe contribution to attachment theory. Modern coregulation reviews cite the 1996 book as the central source.
- **Why uncertain**: The exact wording "dyadic regulation of emotion" is widely paraphrased and may not be a direct verbatim quote in that exact form. The conceptual claim is well-attributed; the specific phrasing may be a community paraphrase rather than verbatim.
- **Sources**: Multiple secondary references in attachment-theory and coregulation literatures, including PMC10453544 coregulation multilevel review.
- **Notes**: Direction is correct; exact wording should be hedged in the explanation. Suggest framing as "Sroufe formalized attachment as the dyadic regulation of emotion" (consistent with field consensus on his contribution) rather than placing in direct quotation marks.

### Claim 17: "Slow-paced breathing is FDA-cleared (e.g., RESPeRATE) for relaxation and is recognized by the AHA as adjunctive treatment for hypertension."
- **Verdict**: UNCERTAIN (8/10) — substantively correct; AHA wording may be qualified
- **Evidence for**: Device-guided slow breathing is FDA-cleared and the AHA has issued statements acknowledging slow breathing as adjunctive blood-pressure intervention. Lehrer & Gevirtz 2014, Russo 2017, and numerous slow-breathing meta-analyses confirm the cardiovascular physiology.
- **Why uncertain**: The exact AHA recommendation language has been revised across statement updates (2013, 2019); current AHA framing may be more or less strongly supportive than the research draft implies. The general claim is correct; the specific phrasing should be checked against current AHA statements.
- **Sources**: Slow-paced breathing meta-analyses (Springer 2023, Frontiers 2018); FDA RESPeRATE clearance documents; AHA scientific statements on lifestyle treatments for hypertension.
- **Notes**: The claim should be framed as "FDA-cleared device-guided slow breathing exists, and slow breathing has been recognized as adjunctive in cardiovascular health" rather than as a strong specific AHA recommendation, to remain safe.

## Data Validation

### Statistic 1: "Brom 2017 SE RCT: d = 0.94–1.26 for PTSD symptoms"
- **Verdict**: CONFIRMED
- **Primary source check**: Verified against PubMed abstract and Wiley full text descriptions.
- **Recency**: 2017; remains the principal SE-specific RCT through 2026.

## Research Opportunities

### Opportunity 1: Direct head-to-head RCT comparison (polyvagal-informed therapy vs matched active control)
- **Priority**: MEDIUM
- **Driven by**: The chapter's central claim that PVT-informed practices likely help for reasons other than PVT-specific mechanism. A direct comparison study would either confirm or constrain this claim.
- **Why**: Existing PVT-informed therapy evidence is modality-internal (compared to baseline or waitlist, not to matched active alternatives).
- **Suggested queries**: "polyvagal-informed therapy randomized controlled trial active comparator," "Rhythm of Regulation outcomes RCT versus CBT"
- **Expected impact**: If found, would directly test the chapter's thesis. Likely finding: such a study does not yet exist; the absence is itself informative.

### Opportunity 2: Autistic-adult-specific outcomes for polyvagal-informed therapy
- **Priority**: MEDIUM
- **Driven by**: User profile (autistic adult); most published PVT-informed therapy data is from trauma populations broadly, not autism-specific.
- **Why**: The user needs to know whether the available evidence speaks to their specific situation.
- **Suggested queries**: "polyvagal-informed therapy autistic adults outcomes," "Rhythm of Regulation autism case series"
- **Expected impact**: Likely finding: very limited; mostly case-study level. This gap should be acknowledged in the explanation.

### Opportunity 3: Long-term outcomes of polyvagal-informed therapy
- **Priority**: LOW
- **Driven by**: Concern about durability of effects.
- **Why**: Most pre-post studies follow short timeframes (weeks to months). Long-term (12+ month) outcomes are scarce.
- **Suggested queries**: "polyvagal-informed therapy 12-month follow-up," "long-term efficacy somatic therapy"
- **Expected impact**: Modest; would strengthen the evidence base assessment.

### Opportunity 4: The iatrogenic potential of polyvagal language to scaffold avoidance
- **Priority**: LOW
- **Driven by**: Anecdotal concern flagged in research draft.
- **Why**: No systematic study of cases where "my nervous system isn't ready" became a justification for indefinitely deferring necessary therapeutic work.
- **Suggested queries**: "polyvagal informed therapy iatrogenic," "somatic therapy avoidance scaffolding"
- **Expected impact**: Likely finding: anecdotal only, no systematic study. The chapter should mention this is an unstudied concern.
