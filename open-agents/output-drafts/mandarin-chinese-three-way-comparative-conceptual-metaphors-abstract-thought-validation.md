## Claim-by-Claim Verification

### 1. Lakoff & Johnson — Conceptual Metaphor Theory (1980)
**Claim**: CMT was developed by George Lakoff and Mark Johnson in *Metaphors We Live By* (1980), proposing that abstract thought is structured by metaphorical mappings from concrete experience.
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: This is foundational cognitive linguistics — *Metaphors We Live By* (University of Chicago Press, 1980) is accurately described.

### 2. Chinese Vertical Time Expressions (上/下)
**Claim**: Chinese uses 上 (shàng, up) for earlier/past and 下 (xià, down) for later/future: 上个星期 (last week), 下个月 (next month), 上午 (morning), 下午 (afternoon).
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Multiple sources confirm: "In Mandarin, earlier events are called 'Shang' (meaning up) and later events are called 'Xia' (meaning down)." Specific expressions are standard Mandarin and confirmed across all Chinese language references. (ScienceDirect 2023; SCIRP 2017)

### 3. Vertical Time as Pan-Chinese (Not Mandarin-Specific)
**Claim**: The vertical time metaphor exists in Cantonese and other Chinese varieties, not just Mandarin.
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Cantonese uses 上個禮拜 (soeng5 go3 lai5 baai3, last week) and 下個月 (haa6 go3 jyut6, next month) with the same up/down temporal mapping. This is a pan-Chinese feature inherited from Classical Chinese.

### 4. Horizontal Time More Frequent Than Vertical in Chinese
**Claim**: Chinese speakers use horizontal spatial metaphors for time MORE FREQUENTLY than vertical ones; approximately 64% horizontal vs. 36% vertical in corpus data.
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Chen (2007) established this frequency distribution. The primacy of 前/后 (front/back) over 上/下 (up/down) for temporal reference is confirmed across multiple studies.

### 5. Boroditsky (2001) Original Claim
**Claim**: Boroditsky (2001) argued Chinese-English bilinguals showed a vertical cognitive bias even when tested in English, as evidence for linguistic relativity.
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: PubMed and ScienceDirect confirm the original experimental design and claims.

### 6. Chen (2007) Failed Replication
**Claim**: Chen (2007) published "Failure of Replicating Boroditsky (2001)" with four experimental conditions failing to reproduce the vertical time bias.
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: PubMed confirms: "Do Chinese and English speakers think about time differently? Failure of replicating Boroditsky (2001)." (PubMed ID: 17070793)

### 7. January & Kako (2007) Failed Replication
**Claim**: January & Kako (2007) also failed to replicate Boroditsky's findings.
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Cited in multiple review articles alongside Chen (2007) and Tse & Altarriba (2008) as part of the replication failure pattern.

### 8. Current Consensus on Vertical Time
**Claim**: The vertical time metaphor is "real as a linguistic pattern" but "contested as a cognitive influence."
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: This nuanced framing matches the current state of the field as summarized in Frontiers in Psychology (2022) and multiple review articles.

### 9. 心 (Xīn) as Unified Heart-Mind
**Claim**: 心 refers simultaneously to the heart and mind; it is the seat of both cognition and emotion in Chinese philosophy.
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy confirms: "The xin is the nearest equivalent to the English term for mind in classical Chinese philosophy" and is "frequently translated as 'heart-mind.'"

### 10. Heart Radical in Cognitive AND Emotional Characters
**Claim**: The heart radical (心/忄) appears in both cognitive characters (想, 思, 忆, 忘, 悟) and emotional characters (情, 怒, 恐, 悲, 愁, 恨).
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: All listed characters correctly contain the heart radical 心 or its variant 忄. This is verifiable through any Chinese character dictionary.

### 11. TCM Five Organ-Emotion Mappings
**Claim**: Heart = Joy, Liver = Anger, Spleen = Worry, Lungs = Grief, Kidneys = Fear.
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Multiple TCM sources confirm these exact mappings: "anger (liver), joy (heart), thought (spleen), sorrow (lungs), and fear (kidneys)." (PMC: PMC5585554; TCM World Foundation)

### 12. Gallbladder = Courage
**Claim**: 胆 (dǎn, gallbladder) maps to courage: 胆大 = courageous, 胆小 = cowardly.
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Standard Chinese vocabulary confirmed across multiple language references. The gallbladder-courage mapping is well-established in Chinese and TCM tradition.

### 13. Compound Ideograph Examples
**Claim**: 明 (sun+moon=bright), 好 (woman+child=good), 安 (roof+woman=peace), 家 (roof+pig=home), 信 (person+speech=trust).
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED WITH NUANCE
**Evidence**: The component analyses are standard for traditional Chinese character pedagogy. The research correctly notes that many are folk etymologies rather than verified historical derivations. Modern scholarship (particularly Boltz 1994, Qiu 2000) has questioned several traditional huìyì classifications, but the document's caveat about this is appropriate and accurate.

### 14. Simplification Reducing Metaphorical Transparency (愛→爱)
**Claim**: The simplified character 爱 (love) removed the 心 (heart) component present in the traditional 愛.
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: This is a well-known example in discussions of Chinese character simplification. Traditional 愛 contains 心 at its center; simplified 爱 does not. This is verifiable through any traditional-simplified comparison resource.

### 15. Arabic Qalb (Heart) as Parallel to Chinese Xin
**Claim**: Arabic's قلب (qalb) serves a similar unifying function as heart-mind, particularly in Quranic/Classical usage.
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Well-established in Islamic philosophy. The Quran treats the qalb as the seat of reason, understanding, and spiritual perception.

### 16. Universal Metaphors: ANGER IS HEAT, HAPPINESS IS UP, SADNESS IS DOWN
**Claim**: These metaphors are found in Chinese, English, and most other languages studied.
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: These are among the best-established findings in cognitive linguistics (Lakoff & Johnson 1980; Kovecses 2000, 2005; Grady 1997). Cross-linguistic surveys confirm broad distribution.

### 17. 吃 (Eat) Metaphorical Extensions
**Claim**: 吃苦 (eat bitter = hardship), 吃亏 (eat loss), 吃惊 (eat surprise = startled), 吃醋 (eat vinegar = jealous), 吃力 (eat strength = strenuous), 吃香 (eat fragrance = popular).
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: All listed expressions are standard Mandarin vocabulary. 吃 is indeed one of the most metaphorically productive verbs in Chinese, confirmed across language learning resources and linguistic analyses.

### 18. English "Eat" Having Far Fewer Metaphorical Extensions
**Claim**: English has very limited metaphorical extensions of "eat" compared to Chinese 吃.
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: English "eat" extends to "eat your words," "eat humble pie," "what's eating you," "eat the cost" — a substantially smaller set than Chinese 吃.

### 19. Cantonese Sharing Organ-Emotion Mappings
**Claim**: Cantonese uses the same TCM-grounded organ-emotion metaphors as Mandarin.
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: Cantonese 肝火盛, 膽大, 脾氣 are standard Cantonese expressions with the same organ-emotion mappings.

### 20. Three Levels of Metaphorical Encoding
**Claim**: Chinese operates with metaphor at three levels: cognitive, lexical, and graphemic — the third being unique to logographic scripts.
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED
**Evidence**: This framework is well-supported in Chinese linguistics. The graphemic encoding layer (characters visually encoding metaphor) is genuinely absent from alphabetic scripts.

### 21. "Face" (面子) as Chinese Cultural Metaphor Borrowed into English
**Claim**: The English "save face" / "lose face" was borrowed from Chinese.
**Verdict**: CONFIRMED WITH NUANCE
**Evidence**: The OED traces "lose face" to an 1876 translation from Chinese. However, the concept of "face" in social interaction exists independently in English (and other cultures) — what was borrowed was the specific formulation "lose face" / "save face" and the heightened conceptual elaboration of face as a social resource. The Chinese concept of 面子 (miànzi) and 脸 (liǎn) — two different types of face — is substantially more elaborate than the English borrowed version.

### 22. Traditional Chinese Having MORE Graphemic Metaphorical Transparency Than Simplified
**Claim**: Traditional Chinese > Mandarin (simplified) > English in terms of metaphorical encoding depth, with simplification reducing the graphemic layer.
**Verdict**: UNCERTAIN
**Evidence**: The 愛/爱 example is valid, but this is a generalization that would require systematic analysis across all simplified characters. Many simplifications preserve the original semantic components. The claim is directionally plausible (some loss of graphemic metaphor) but the gradient framing (Traditional > Simplified > English) is somewhat oversimplified. The loss is real but applies to a subset of characters, not the system as a whole.

## Additional Notes

### Strengths of the Three-Way Frame
The research successfully identifies features that fall into three categories:
1. **Pan-Chinese** (shared by Mandarin, Cantonese, Traditional): heart-mind, organ-emotion mappings, vertical time, compound ideographs
2. **Mandarin/PRC-specific**: simplified character effects on visual metaphor, some modern compound coinages
3. **English-contrasting**: no vertical time axis, heart/mind split, sparse organ-emotion system

This triangulation is the key value-add over the previous Chinese vs. English treatment.

### Potential Additions
- The research could mention 气 (qì, qi/breath/energy) as another productive metaphorical concept that pervades Chinese expression (生气 = angry, literally "generate qi"; 气氛 = atmosphere, literally "qi-portion")
- The 吃 section could note parallel productive verbs in Chinese: 打 (dǎ, hit/strike) is similarly hyper-extended (打电话 = make a phone call, 打工 = do manual labor, 打算 = plan)
