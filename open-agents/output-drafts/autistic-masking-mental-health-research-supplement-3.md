# Research Supplement 3: Statistical Corrections and Updated Data

This supplement addresses 12 specific statistical claims, citation errors, and data points flagged by validators as incorrect, unverifiable, or needing update. Each section provides the corrected information with full citations and DOIs.

## Correction 1: "3x More Likely Burnout" Claim

### Original Claim
"A 2023 study found that autistic adults who mask heavily are 3x more likely to experience burnout."

### Correction
**This specific "3x" statistic is unverifiable.** No 2023 study was found reporting a 3x odds ratio for masking and burnout.

The most relevant 2023 study is Arnold et al. (2023), which found that masking was *statistically significantly associated* with autistic burnout severity scores (as measured by the Autistic Burnout Severity Items), after controlling for alexithymia, interoception, repetitive behaviours, sensory sensitivities, and autism severity. However, the study reported regression coefficients from a linear model of 141 autistic adults, not an odds ratio or relative risk of "3x."

Notably, the study found a *divergence* between two burnout measures: masking was associated with the Autistic Burnout Severity Items but *not* with the AASPIRE Autistic Burnout Measure (which showed association with depression only).

### What to Use Instead
The correct statement is: "Arnold et al. (2023) found that masking was a significant independent predictor of autistic burnout severity after controlling for multiple confounders, though the magnitude of association was reported as a regression coefficient rather than a risk ratio." For qualitative context, the Raymaker et al. (2020) thematic analysis identified masking as the single most commonly cited cause of autistic burnout.

### Full Citation
- Arnold, S. R. C., Higgins, J. M., Weise, J., Desai, A., Pellicano, E., & Trollor, J. N. (2023). Towards the measurement of autistic burnout. *Autism*, 27(7), 1933-1948. https://doi.org/10.1177/13623613221147401
- Arnold, S. R. C., Higgins, J. M., Weise, J., Desai, A., Pellicano, E., & Trollor, J. N. (2023). Confirming the nature of autistic burnout. *Autism*, 27(7), 1949-1960. https://doi.org/10.1177/13623613221147410

## Correction 2: "52% of Autistic Women Are LGBTQ+"

### Original Claim
"52% of autistic women are LGBTQ+"

### Correction
**The 52% figure is not supported by the major studies.** The best available data shows approximately 40-41% from the largest samples, not 52%.

Key findings from the SPARK cohort studies:
- **41%** of 651 independent autistic adults in one SPARK cohort identified as LGBQ+ (lesbian, gay, bisexual, queer, or another minority sexual orientation).
- **40%** of 980 autistic adults aged 18-46 in a second SPARK cohort identified as LGBTQ+.
- For comparison, a 2022 Gallup poll found only 7% of U.S. adults overall identify as LGBTQ+.

George & Stokes (2018) reported a higher figure of 69.7% non-heterosexual in their ASD sample (N=309), but this used the Sell Scale of Sexual Orientation in an online convenience sample, which is methodologically distinct from the SPARK population-based studies and likely inflated by self-selection bias. The comparison TD group in that study also showed an unusually high 30.3% non-heterosexual rate.

Broader systematic estimates place the LGB rate among autistic people at 15-35% among those without intellectual disability, with some studies reporting 2-3x the general population rate.

### What to Use Instead
"Large cohort studies (SPARK) find approximately 40-41% of autistic adults identify as LGBTQ+, compared to ~7% of the general population" -- this is the best-sourced figure.

### Full Citations
- SPARK for Autism. "Autistic People More Likely to Identify as LGBTQ." https://sparkforautism.org/discover_article/autism-lgbtq-identity/
- George, R., & Stokes, M. A. (2018). Sexual orientation in autism spectrum disorder. *Autism Research*, 11(1), 133-141. https://doi.org/10.1002/aur.1892
- George, R., & Stokes, M. A. (2018). Gender identity and sexual orientation in autism spectrum disorder. *Autism*, 22(8), 970-982. https://doi.org/10.1177/1362361317714587
- Warrier, V., Greenberg, D. M., Weir, E., Buckingham, C., Smith, P., Lai, M.-C., Allison, C., & Baron-Cohen, S. (2020). Elevated rates of autism, other neurodevelopmental and psychiatric diagnoses, and autistic traits in transgender and gender-diverse individuals. *Nature Communications*, 11, 3959. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-020-17794-1

## Correction 3: "8x Suicide Risk for Autistic Women"

### Original Claim
"Autistic women face 8x the suicide risk of neurotypical women."

### Correction
**The actual figure is approximately 13x for autistic women, not 8x.** The "8x" figure appears to be a conflation of the overall autism suicide OR with the sex-stratified figure.

Hirvikoski et al. (2016) found:
- **Overall suicide OR for autism: ~7.55** (combining all autistic individuals vs. matched controls)
- **Females with ASD: OR 13.05** for suicide mortality compared to females in the general population
- Specifically: 0.32% (27) of autistic women died by suicide vs. 0.03% (213) of non-autistic women
- **ASD without intellectual disability: OR 9.40** (95% CI: 7.43-11.9) for suicide

The "8x" figure likely arose from rounding the overall OR of 7.55 up to 8, then incorrectly attributing it to women specifically. The sex-stratified data actually shows a *higher* risk for autistic women (OR 13.05) than the overall average.

Kirby et al. (2019) in a 20-year Utah population study found females with ASD were over 3 times more likely to die by suicide than females without ASD (RR 3.42, p < 0.01). The difference from Hirvikoski is due to different study designs, populations, and time periods.

### What to Use Instead
"Hirvikoski et al. (2016) found autistic women were over 13 times more likely to die by suicide than women in the general population (OR 13.05), while the overall suicide risk for autistic individuals was approximately 7.5 times higher than matched controls."

### Full Citations
- Hirvikoski, T., Mittendorfer-Rutz, E., Boman, M., Larsson, H., Lichtenstein, P., & Bolte, S. (2016). Premature mortality in autism spectrum disorder. *British Journal of Psychiatry*, 208(3), 232-238. https://doi.org/10.1192/bjp.bp.114.160192
- Kirby, A. V., Bakian, A. V., Zhang, Y., Bilder, D. A., Keeshin, B. R., & Coon, H. (2019). A 20-year study of suicide death in a statewide autism population. *Autism Research*, 12(4), 658-666. https://doi.org/10.1002/aur.2076

## Correction 4: "$17+ Billion" ABA Industry

### Original Claim
"The ABA industry is worth $17+ billion."

### Correction
**The $17+ billion figure significantly overstates the ABA market.** This figure likely conflates the broader autism services/therapeutics market with ABA specifically, or uses projected future values rather than current actuals.

Multiple market research firms provide converging estimates for the ABA-specific market:

- **GM Insights**: U.S. ABA market was **USD $4 billion in 2023**, projected to grow at 4.8% CAGR through 2032.
- **Credence Research**: Global ABA market was **USD $7.6 billion in 2024**, projected to reach $10.7 billion by 2032.
- **Spherical Insights**: U.S. ABA market estimated at approximately **$7.97 billion in 2025**.

For context on the broader market:
- **Grand View Research**: The global autism spectrum disorder *treatment* market (all modalities, not just ABA) was USD $2.16 billion in 2024.
- The broader **behavioral health** market is much larger but includes all behavioral health services, not just ABA.

The discrepancy between different sources ($4B vs $7.6B) stems from whether they measure U.S.-only vs. global, and whether they include ABA software and related services or only direct ABA therapy.

### What to Use Instead
"The U.S. ABA therapy market was approximately $4-8 billion as of 2023-2025, depending on how broadly ABA-related services are defined (source: GM Insights, Credence Research, Spherical Insights market reports)."

### Sources
- GM Insights. (2024). U.S. Applied Behavior Analysis Market Size | Growth Outlook 2032. https://www.gminsights.com/industry-analysis/us-applied-behavior-analysis-market
- Credence Research. (2024). Applied Behavior Analysis Market Size, Growth and Forecast 2032. https://www.credenceresearch.com/report/applied-behavior-analysis-market

## Correction 5: "All 50 States by 2014" for ABA Insurance Mandates

### Original Claim
"All 50 states had mandated ABA insurance coverage by 2014."

### Correction
**The correct year is 2019, not 2014.** Tennessee was the final state, with coverage enacted in August 2019.

Timeline:
- The parent-led movement to extend insurance coverage for autism throughout the U.S. started in the early 2000s.
- Indiana and Maryland already had mandates by 2003.
- Between 2003 and 2017, 44 states introduced insurance mandates.
- Idaho, North Dakota, Washington, and Wyoming were among the last holdouts.
- **Tennessee enacted its rule in August 2019**, becoming the final state. The Tennessee Department of Commerce and Insurance enacted a new rule requiring all individual, small, and large group insurance plans to cover treatment for autism that is "medically necessary and appropriate and is not experimental," including ABA.

### What to Use Instead
"By October 2019, all 50 states and Washington, D.C. had mandates requiring some level of insurance coverage for autism treatment, including ABA. Tennessee was the final state, enacting its mandate in August 2019."

### Sources
- Disability Scoop. (2019, October 1). Autism Insurance Coverage Now Required In All 50 States. https://www.disabilityscoop.com/2019/10/01/autism-insurance-coverage-now-required-50-states/27223/
- NCSL. Autism and Insurance Coverage State Laws. https://www.ncsl.org/health/autism-and-insurance-coverage-state-laws

## Correction 8: Bottema-Beutel et al. (2021) vs. Dawson & Fletcher-Watson (2022)

### Original Confusion
The 93%/150 adverse events finding was attributed ambiguously between these two papers.

### Clarification

**Bottema-Beutel et al. (2021)** is the *primary data paper*. This team conducted the systematic review of 150 group-design, non-pharmacological intervention studies for young autistic children to assess adverse event reporting practices.

Key findings from Bottema-Beutel et al.:
- Of 150 studies examined, only **11 mentioned adverse events at all** (~7%).
- Of those 11, only **4 actually reported adverse effects or adverse events** from the study.
- Of 54 studies that reported reasons for withdrawal, 10 reported reasons categorizable as adverse events, 8 as adverse effects, and 12 were too vague to determine.
- This means **approximately 93% of studies failed to mention adverse events at all**.

**Dawson & Fletcher-Watson (2022)** is a *commentary* responding to these findings. Their commentary discussed the implications of this pervasive failure to consider harms in autism intervention research, citing the Bottema-Beutel findings and extending the critique to early intensive ABA-based interventions specifically.

### Correct Citations

**Data paper:**
Bottema-Beutel, K., Crowley, S., Sandbank, M., & Woynaroski, T. G. (2021). Adverse event reporting in intervention research for young autistic children. *Autism*, 25(2), 322-335. https://doi.org/10.1177/1362361320965331

**Commentary:**
Dawson, M., & Fletcher-Watson, S. (2022). When autism researchers disregard harms: A commentary. *Autism*, 26(2), 564-566. https://doi.org/10.1177/13623613211031403

## Correction 9: Trevisan, Bowering & Birmingham (2016) — Misattributed to Brewer et al.

### Original Error
The Molecular Autism paper on alexithymia and facial expression production was incorrectly attributed to "Brewer et al."

### Correction
The correct attribution is **Trevisan, Bowering & Birmingham (2016)**, not Brewer et al. The confusion likely arose because Brewer and colleagues published related work on alexithymia and emotion recognition (e.g., Cook, Brewer, Shah, & Bird, 2013, in *Psychological Science*), which explored a similar theme -- that alexithymia, not autism, explains emotional processing differences.

### Correct Citation
Trevisan, D. A., Bowering, M., & Birmingham, E. (2016). Alexithymia, but not autism spectrum disorder, may be related to the production of emotional facial expressions. *Molecular Autism*, 7, 46. https://doi.org/10.1186/s13229-016-0108-6

### Key Finding
The study showed children emotionally charged video clips and found that alexithymic traits -- but not ASD traits, IQ, or sex -- were associated with the quantity of facial expression production. This supports the broader "alexithymia hypothesis": that many emotion-processing differences traditionally attributed to autism may actually be driven by co-occurring alexithymia.

### Related (but distinct) Brewer/Cook Work
Cook, R., Brewer, R., Shah, P., & Bird, G. (2013). Alexithymia, not autism, predicts poor recognition of emotional facial expressions. *Psychological Science*, 24(5), 723-732. https://doi.org/10.1177/0956797612463582

## Correction 10: PTSD Prevalence in Autistic Adults

### Original Claim
"Rumball (2020) found 45%/61% PTSD rates in trauma-exposed autistic adults" -- presented without context that these were screening-tool-based estimates from a small sample, and validators identified a 2025 meta-analysis showing much lower clinically-diagnosed rates.

### Reconciliation

Both figures are real but measure fundamentally different things:

**Rumball et al. (2020)** found in a small sample of trauma-exposed autistic adults:
- **45% current probable PTSD** (using self-report screening tools)
- **61% lifetime probable PTSD** (using self-report screening tools)
These are the highest rates reported in the literature and come from a specifically *trauma-exposed* subsample using *screening instruments*.

**Mansour et al. (2025)** conducted a comprehensive meta-analysis (13 studies, N=142,081 autistic adults) and found:
- **Point prevalence: 2.06%** (95% CI: 0.00-11.97) using clinical diagnostic criteria
- **Lifetime prevalence: 2.72%** (95% CI: 2.54-2.90) using clinical diagnostic criteria

These rates are similar to general population estimates, which appears contradictory.

**How to reconcile:**
1. The Rumball study used screening tools on a trauma-exposed subsample; the meta-analysis used formal diagnostic criteria on broader populations.
2. The 2025 meta-analysis authors explicitly note the discrepancy, suggesting current PTSD diagnostic criteria "may not fully capture how trauma is experienced and expressed by autistic individuals, leading to underdiagnosis."
3. The very wide confidence interval (0.00-11.97%) for point prevalence reflects enormous heterogeneity across studies.

### What to Use Instead
"PTSD prevalence estimates in autistic adults vary dramatically by measurement method: screening tools in trauma-exposed samples detect probable PTSD in 45-61% (Rumball et al., 2020), while formal clinical diagnoses yield point prevalence of ~2% (Mansour et al., 2025 meta-analysis, k=13, N=142,081). This gap likely reflects underdiagnosis due to atypical presentation of trauma responses in autistic people."

### Full Citations
- Rumball, F., Happe, F., & Grey, N. (2020). Experience of trauma and PTSD symptoms in autistic adults: Risk of PTSD development following DSM-5 and non-DSM-5 traumatic life events. *Autism Research*, 13(12), 2122-2132. https://doi.org/10.1002/aur.2306
- Mansour, H., Kurana, S., Eshetu, A., Hoare, S., El Baou, C., Arnold, I., Halys, C., Stewart, G. R., Desai, R., John, A., Mandy, W., O'Nions, E., & Stott, J. (2025). Prevalence of Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) in autistic children or young people (CYP) and adults: A systematic review and meta-analysis. *Clinical Psychology Review*. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cpr.2025.102621

## Correction 11: Alexithymia General Population Prevalence

### Original Claim
"Alexithymia affects 5-10% of the general population."

### Correction
**The 5-10% range understates the prevalence.** The most commonly cited figure is approximately **10%**, but the full evidence supports a range of **approximately 10-13%** in Western populations, with wider variation globally.

Key epidemiological data:

**Salminen et al. (1999)** -- the landmark Finnish population study:
- **Overall prevalence: 13%** (using the TAS-20 in a representative population sample adjusted for psychological distress)
- **Men: 17%**, **Women: 10%**
- Associated with male gender, advanced age, low educational level, and low socioeconomic status

**Cross-national estimates:**
- Multiple systematic reviews cite approximately **10%** as the general population prevalence
- The range across different studies and countries is approximately **10-13%** in Western populations (Finland 13%, Germany ~10%)
- Some Middle Eastern studies report much higher rates (20-49%), but methodological differences complicate comparison
- Gender breakdown across studies: men 9-17%, women 5-10%

### What to Use Instead
"Alexithymia affects approximately 10-13% of the general population, with higher rates in men (~17%) than women (~10%) (Salminen et al., 1999). This compares to approximately 50% of autistic adults."

### Full Citation
- Salminen, J. K., Saarijarvi, S., Aarela, E., Toikka, T., & Kauhanen, J. (1999). Prevalence of alexithymia and its association with sociodemographic variables in the general population of Finland. *Journal of Psychosomatic Research*, 46(1), 75-82. https://doi.org/10.1016/S0022-3999(98)00053-1

## Correction 12: Weir et al. (2021) "9x" Substance Use Finding

### Original Claim
Needs clarification on what exactly the OR 8.89 was for, what overall substance use rates were, and what the confidence interval was.

### Detailed Findings

**The OR 8.89 was specifically for using recreational substances "to manage behaviour":**
- Among participants who reported a *specific motivation* for drug use, autistic individuals were **OR 8.89** (95% CI: 2.05-81.12; p=0.0017) more likely to report using recreational substances **to manage behaviour** compared to non-autistic individuals.
- The extremely wide confidence interval (2.05-81.12) reflects the small subsample size of those endorsing this specific motivation, meaning while statistically significant, the precision of the point estimate is low.

**Overall substance use rates -- autistic people use LESS, not more:**
- Autistic individuals were **less likely** to drink alcohol regularly: **16.0%** vs. 22.2% of non-autistic individuals
- Autistic individuals were **less likely** to binge-drink: **3.8%** vs. 8.2% of non-autistic individuals
- This is a critical contextual paradox: lower overall use, but those who do use are more likely to self-medicate

**Additional vulnerability findings:**
- Autistic individuals were more likely to report childhood use of drugs
- Autistic individuals were more likely to report being forced or tricked into using drugs
- Autistic individuals were more likely to use substances to manage mental health symptoms

### What to Use Instead
"Weir et al. (2021) found that while autistic adults use alcohol and drugs *less* overall than non-autistic peers (16% vs. 22% regular alcohol use; 4% vs. 8% binge-drinking), those who do use substances are nearly 9 times more likely to report using drugs specifically to manage behaviour (OR 8.89; 95% CI: 2.05-81.12). The very wide confidence interval reflects the small subsample endorsing this specific motivation."

### Full Citation
- Weir, E., Allison, C., & Baron-Cohen, S. (2021). Understanding the substance use of autistic adolescents and adults: a mixed-methods approach. *The Lancet Psychiatry*, 8(8), 673-685. https://doi.org/10.1016/S2215-0366(21)00160-7
