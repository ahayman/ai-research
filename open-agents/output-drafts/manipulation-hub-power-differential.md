## 1. Why This Page Exists

The strategies on this hub only work under certain conditions. A strategy that is safe and effective when you have power, resources, and alternatives can be dangerous when you don't. A strategy that is appropriate against a peer can be catastrophic against an authority figure with institutional backing.

The standard framing — "stand up for yourself," "set boundaries," "leave if it's bad enough" — assumes everyone has roughly the same power and resources. Most people don't. Autistic people especially often don't, for specific documented reasons: elevated unemployment, economic dependency, social isolation, credibility discount, compliance training, and the structural effects of masking on career and relationship capital.

**This page exists to help you answer a question that has to come before strategy selection: what power and resources do I actually have in this situation, and what does the other person have?** Without answering this, you will pick strategies based on an idealized version of your position rather than the real one, and the strategies will fail or backfire.

This is not about deciding whether your situation is "bad enough" to justify action. Your situation is as bad as your experience of it. This is about choosing strategies that match the actual terrain.

## 2. The Concept: Two Questions, Not One

Most advice in this space treats power as a single axis: "do you have power or not?" The real picture is two related but distinct questions:

1. **Power differential** — how does your power in this relationship compare to the other person's power? Is it balanced, tilted toward them, or tilted toward you?
2. **Resource availability** — do you personally have access to resources (financial, social, legal, emotional, informational) that let you implement strategies and absorb consequences?

A person can have a favorable power differential in a specific relationship but no resources to act on it (you can walk away from a friendship, but if the friend is your only support, walking away leaves you isolated). A person can have strong personal resources but face an unfavorable power differential (you can have savings and legal knowledge but still be at the mercy of a manager who controls your livelihood in a thin job market).

Strategy selection depends on both. This page gives you a structured way to assess them.

### Why this framing comes from the research

The framework draws directly on Evan Stark's analysis of coercive control, which argues that control is established through the **asymmetric accumulation of power and constraint of resources** (Stark, 2007, *Coercive Control*, Chapter 8 on entrapment). The Power and Control Wheel (Pence & Paymar, 1993) explicitly includes economic abuse as one of its eight categories — the deliberate disruption of a victim's resource base is recognized as a core coercive control tactic. Campbell's Danger Assessment includes "controlling most of her daily activities" and "abuser unemployed" as risk factors — both are resource and power indicators (Campbell et al., 2003).

For workplace situations, the Workplace Bullying Institute's prevalence research and the legal-evidentiary literature both recognize that "what you can do about bullying depends on what you have" — specifically what documentation, witnesses, union protections, legal standing, and alternative employment options you have (Namie & Namie, 2009).

For autism-specific considerations, Pearson et al. (2022, 2024) documented that autistic survivors often remain in abusive situations because of the compound effect of economic dependence, social isolation, masking fatigue, and the credibility discount. The "situational vulnerability" framing (Pearson et al., 2022) is specifically about resource and power constraints, not about something being wrong with the autistic person.

## 3. Assessing Power Differential

Power in a relationship comes from several sources, each of which can tilt toward either party. The goal of this section is not to produce a score — it is to make the dimensions visible so you can see which ones are operating in your situation.

### The eight dimensions of power

**1. Formal / institutional authority.** Does one of you have a title, role, or position that gives them authority over the other? (Manager over employee, parent over minor child, doctor over patient, teacher over student, spouse with sole account access.)

**2. Economic / financial power.** Who controls money, employment, housing, transportation, benefits, debt? Can one of you cut the other off from income, housing, or basic resources?

**3. Social capital.** Who has more friends, family, allies, professional networks, community standing? If there were a public dispute about what was happening between you, whose version would be believed first?

**4. Physical / bodily capacity.** Is there a physical size, strength, mobility, or ability difference? (This applies regardless of whether physical violence has ever occurred — the potential for physical harm shifts power even when it is not used.)

**5. Information asymmetry.** Who has information the other doesn't? Who controls access to information (medical, legal, financial, relational)? In many manipulation patterns, the controller deliberately limits the other person's access to information about their own situation.

**6. Control over alternatives.** If the relationship or situation ended, who has more options? (A partner with savings, marketable skills, supportive family, and housing alternatives has alternatives. A partner who gave up career for caregiving, is socially isolated by the relationship, and has no independent resources does not.)

**7. Credibility / testimonial weight.** In any dispute involving testimony — with professionals, authorities, family — whose account will be believed? This is where the autistic credibility discount applies directly. (Lim, Young & Brewer, 2022, PMC8813809 — autistic adults are perceived as more deceptive and less credible *when telling the truth*.)

**8. Ability to harm without consequence.** Can one of you hurt the other (emotionally, financially, professionally, reputationally, physically) without facing meaningful consequences? This is often asymmetric in ways that are invisible until tested.

### Running the assessment

For each dimension, ask: is this balanced, tilted toward me, or tilted toward them? You do not need to score this. You need to see the pattern.

A quick heuristic: **the relationship is power-asymmetric if three or more dimensions are tilted in the same direction.** A relationship in which your partner has formal authority (you work together), controls finances, has the stronger social network, is physically larger, and has better credibility with shared friends is not a "marriage between equals who are having communication problems." It is a power-asymmetric relationship, and the strategies that work in balanced relationships may not work in it.

### The dimensions that are easiest to miss

- **Credibility.** You may not notice that when you and another person disagree in front of a third party, the third party believes them first — until it happens in a situation that matters. For autistic readers, this dimension is almost always tilted against you in encounters with strangers or non-autism-informed professionals. Assume it until proven otherwise.
- **Control over alternatives.** You may not notice how constrained your options are until you try to use them. A person with formal authority who says "I'm flexible, you have lots of options" is often just untested. Think concretely: if this relationship ended tomorrow, where would you live? What would you live on? Who would support you? If the answers are thin, your alternatives are thin, which means your power is thin, regardless of how things feel day-to-day.
- **Information asymmetry.** In coercive control situations, the controller often deliberately limits the victim's access to information about finances, legal rights, alternatives, or even what the controller is saying about them to third parties. If you cannot answer basic questions about your own situation — how much money there is, what bills are due, what the landlord was told, what the school was told — that itself is a power signal.

## 4. Assessing Resource Availability

Resources are what let you implement strategies and absorb consequences. Having power in a relationship does not help you if you do not have the resources to act on it.

### Six resource categories

**1. Financial resources.**
- Do you have your own income? Is it stable?
- Do you have independent access to money (accounts in your name, not monitored)?
- Do you have emergency savings you could access?
- Are your basic costs (housing, food, medical, transportation) dependent on someone else?
- If you had to leave suddenly, could you afford the first month?

**2. Social resources.**
- Do you have people outside the situation who know what is happening?
- Do you have people you could stay with if needed, even briefly?
- Do you have people who could help you think through a decision without pressuring you toward their preferred outcome?
- Do you have an autistic community or peer network? (This is frequently underweighted in mainstream advice but is often one of the most useful resources autistic adults have.)

**3. Legal resources.**
- Do you know your basic legal rights in this situation? (Employment, housing, relationship, medical, educational — the relevant domain.)
- Do you have access to legal advice? (Legal aid, lawyer referral, bar association, trusted attorney, or even just Womens Law and similar online resources.)
- Are there legal protections that could apply to your situation if you invoked them? (Restraining order, ADA accommodation, Title IX, protected class, etc.)
- Do you have the documentation that legal action would require?

**4. Informational resources.**
- Do you understand what is happening to you well enough to describe it?
- Do you have access to reliable information about your situation? (This hub, books, trustworthy online resources, consultation with advocates.)
- Do you have the capacity to distinguish reliable information from unreliable? (Not a trivial resource under gaslighting.)

**5. Professional support.**
- Do you have a therapist, counselor, or advocate who knows your situation?
- If not a therapist specifically, do you have any professional (doctor, caseworker, advisor, clergy) who could be a starting point?
- **For autistic readers specifically:** do you have a professional who knows you are autistic? The authority vacuum matters here — most clinicians in DV/trauma are not autism-informed, and most clinicians in autism are not DV-informed. A professional in either specialty can still be useful; a professional in neither may be more risk than help.

**6. Cognitive and physical capacity.**
- Are you currently in a state where you can apply strategies? (Burnout, meltdown, depression, physical illness, and crisis all reduce available capacity.)
- Do you have time and mental space to plan and act?
- Are you getting enough sleep and food to think clearly?

This last category is often the one autistic readers underestimate. **Capacity is a resource.** When you are in a burnout state or in sensory overload or post-meltdown, you do not have the cognitive resources to implement complex strategies. This is not a character flaw; it is a fact about your available resources at that moment. Strategies should be chosen for the capacity you actually have, not the capacity you wish you had.

### The resource thin zone

A specific cluster of resource deficits defines what I will call the "resource thin zone":

- Financially dependent (no independent income or savings)
- Socially isolated (no trusted people outside the situation)
- Legal knowledge thin (don't know your rights or can't access legal advice)
- No professional support
- Currently depleted (burnout, crisis, post-meltdown)

**When you are in the resource thin zone, almost all of the strategies on this hub are inapplicable or dangerous.** The hub's strategies assume you can do things — document, disclose, disengage, plan — and the resource thin zone is defined by not being able to do those things effectively.

**What to do when you are in the resource thin zone:**
1. **Do not make consequential decisions.** If you do not have the resources to act on a decision, making the decision commits you to consequences you cannot handle.
2. **Focus on resource acquisition first.** The next strategy is not "confront the manipulator" but "get one more trusted person in my corner," "find out what my legal rights are," "build the smallest possible emergency fund," "find a therapist even if it's not ideal."
3. **Contact a crisis line for help thinking.** Advocates can help you identify resources you do not know exist. The [Crisis Resources Directory](manipulation-hub-crisis-resources.md) lists options.
4. **Use time as your ally.** Gaslighting and coercive control are designed to make you feel you have to act right now. Usually you don't. Waiting until you have one more resource is rarely the wrong answer.
5. **Accept that this is slow work.** Moving out of the resource thin zone is not a weekend project. It can take months or years. That is a feature of the situation, not a failure of your effort.

## 5. What the Combination Tells You

Power differential and resource availability produce a 2×2 that can be useful for orientation, even though real situations are more complex than any grid suggests:

| Your power | Your resources | What's usually viable |
|-----------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Balanced or favorable** | **Strong** | Most strategies on this hub. Engagement, direct communication, setting limits, and even naming patterns are often available. |
| **Balanced or favorable** | **Thin** | Engagement is possible but fragile. Don't commit to consequences you can't carry out. Build resources before major moves. |
| **Unfavorable** | **Strong** | Engagement is risky but survivable. Documentation becomes critical. Disengagement and exit are viable if needed. Pattern-based strategies work. |
| **Unfavorable** | **Thin** | The resource thin zone. Confrontation strategies are usually inappropriate. Focus on resource acquisition, reality anchoring, and waiting for a window. Contact an advocate. |

This grid is a rough orientation, not a prescription. You can have strong resources in some areas and thin ones in others. The power differential can shift over time. A situation that looks favorable in one dimension can be unfavorable in another. Use the grid to notice the combination, not to produce a verdict.

## 6. Autism-Specific Considerations

Several things work differently for autistic readers when assessing power and resources.

### Credibility is almost always tilted against you

Lim, Young & Brewer (2022) found that autistic adults are perceived as more deceptive and less credible than neurotypical adults when telling the truth. This is a structural finding, not something you can fix by being more confident or communicating more clearly. In any dispute where testimony matters — workplace grievance, medical disagreement, legal proceeding, family argument in front of relatives — you start with a credibility discount.

**Practical implication:** Assume the credibility dimension is tilted against you unless you have specific evidence that a particular audience gives you equal weight. This affects strategy selection: strategies that rely on "making your case" without written evidence will tend to underperform; strategies that rely on documentation and pattern evidence will tend to outperform.

*Evidence quality: [VALIDATED].*

### Your social resources may be thinner than expected — and denser than expected

The common finding is that autistic adults have smaller social networks than neurotypical adults on average. This is real, and it affects the resource availability assessment.

But there is a complicating factor: **autistic community can be much more useful than its size suggests.** Peer networks of other autistic people tend to produce higher-quality reality-checking, better understanding of the specific dynamics of autism-weaponization, and less credibility discount within the network itself. A small autistic peer network can sometimes function more effectively as a resource than a larger neurotypical network in which the individual autistic person is isolated within the group.

**Practical implication:** When assessing social resources, count autistic friends, autistic community (online or in-person), and autistic-informed professionals at something like their actual value — which may be higher than mainstream framings acknowledge — rather than heavily discounting them because they are "just online" or "just a few people."

*Evidence quality: [COMMUNITY KNOWLEDGE] with some preliminary research convergence. Crompton et al. (2020) on autistic peer information transfer supports the general point that autistic-to-autistic communication is more effective than autistic-to-neurotypical.*

### Economic vulnerability is elevated and not your fault

Autism-related unemployment and underemployment are documented at high rates. The VEQ study (Griffiths et al., 2019) found that employment difficulties and financial exploitation are among the areas where autistic adults report significantly higher rates of negative experiences than non-autistic controls. This means:

- Your financial resources may genuinely be thin, even if you have skills and capacity
- Your economic dependence on others (partner, family) may be more common and harder to escape than mainstream advice acknowledges
- Benefit systems, disability support, and supported employment programs are legitimate resources that do not reflect personal failure to use them

**Practical implication:** Do not discount economic resources you have access to through disability programs, family support, or public benefits just because mainstream framings treat "independent income" as the only legitimate form. These are resources, and using them is resource strategy, not weakness.

### Masking depletes capacity that the assessment framework needs

Every hour you spend masking is an hour you are not spending on the cognitive work of pattern recognition, strategy evaluation, and self-protection. Hull et al. research on camouflaging costs documents that masking is associated with anxiety, depression, suicidality, and exhaustion. This has a specific implication for the power and resource assessment:

**If you are in a high-masking environment, your capacity is lower than it would otherwise be.** Your ability to run the decision frameworks, track patterns, document incidents, and plan strategies is reduced by the cognitive work of masking. This is not "laziness" or "weakness" — it is a real resource constraint.

**Practical implication:** When you do this assessment, do it in a low-masking environment if possible. After a long work day in which you have been masking, you are not in the cognitive state to do this well. Your assessment will be more accurate when you are unmasked, rested, and in a physical environment that is not demanding sensory compensation from you.

### The compliance training problem, again

As noted on the [Safety Assessment page](manipulation-hub-safety-assessment.md) and the [Manipulation vs. Miscommunication page](manipulation-hub-miscommunication-vs-manipulation.md), the compliance training pipeline many autistic adults have been through creates specific distortions. One distortion matters especially for power assessment:

**Trained compliance produces the illusion of equal power where the reality is asymmetric.** If you have been trained your whole life to accommodate others, defer to authority, and suppress the impulse to protest unfairness, you will systematically underestimate power differentials because your learned behavior in those differentials is indistinguishable, to you, from behavior in balanced relationships. You do what the other person wants because you have been trained to; then when you assess the relationship, you see yourself "getting along" with them.

**Practical implication:** If your initial assessment is "it's pretty balanced, I just have some communication problems," test that by asking what would happen if you acted differently. What if you said no to something they asked? What if you took a position they did not share? What if you raised a concern about them? If the honest answer is "it would go badly," the relationship is not balanced — you are managing asymmetry with compliance, not experiencing balance.

## 7. Strategies by Conditional

The power and resource assessment leads to different strategic postures:

### If power is balanced and resources are strong

- **Engagement strategies are viable.** You can use the full range of approaches on this hub — direct communication, boundary setting, naming patterns, even explicit confrontation if the decision framework points that way.
- **You can afford to experiment.** If one strategy doesn't work, you can try another without catastrophic consequences.
- **Repair is realistic.** If the situation turns out to be miscommunication rather than manipulation, you have the relational capital to repair and continue.
- **This is the best case for applying most of the hub.**

### If power is balanced but resources are thin

- **Engagement is possible but fragile.** You can still use communication strategies, but be careful about committing to consequences you cannot enforce.
- **Prioritize resource-building.** The most effective next move is often not a communication move but a resource move — another trusted person, a little savings, a professional contact.
- **Avoid high-commitment strategies.** Don't issue ultimatums you can't follow through on. Don't set boundaries you can't enforce. The strategies that work in this quadrant are softer, slower, more exploratory.
- **Time is an ally.** When resources are thin, time that is spent building resources is directly improving your strategic position.

### If power is unfavorable but resources are strong

- **Documentation becomes critical.** When power is tilted against you, "making your case verbally" is unlikely to work. Evidence that exists outside your testimony — written records, witnesses, patterns over time — is how you compensate.
- **Disengagement is viable if needed.** Strong resources mean you have an exit. This changes the calculus of engagement: you can engage knowing that if it fails, you can leave.
- **Pattern-based strategies outperform incident-based ones.** Don't try to win any single argument. Build the pattern record. Pattern evidence works against power differentials in ways that single incidents do not.
- **Strategic disengagement from specific interactions while maintaining the relationship is often the most effective move.** You don't have to win every confrontation. You can pick your battles.
- **Professional advocacy amplifies your position.** Lawyers, therapists, union reps, HR (carefully), medical advocates — anyone with standing to speak on your behalf — is resource multiplication in unfavorable-power situations.

### If power is unfavorable and resources are thin (the resource thin zone)

- **Confrontation strategies are usually inappropriate.** Standing up for yourself in ways that would be reasonable in other quadrants can produce catastrophic consequences here. This is not because you should not stand up for yourself — it is because the conditions for safely doing so are not present.
- **Reality anchoring is essential.** Even when you cannot act on your assessment, you can preserve your own sense of what is happening. Journal privately. Read this hub and others. Build your own internal record even if you cannot yet externalize it.
- **Resource acquisition is the primary strategy.** Every action should be asking "does this move me toward more resources?" One more trusted person. One more hidden dollar. One more piece of information. One piece of documentation stored safely. Small moves compound.
- **Contact an advocate.** The [Crisis Resources Directory](manipulation-hub-crisis-resources.md) lists advocates trained in exactly this situation. They can help you identify resources you do not know exist and strategies for building them.
- **Do not make consequential decisions unless forced.** If you do not have the resources to act on a decision, making it commits you to consequences you cannot handle. Waiting is a strategy.
- **Capacity triage.** When capacity is thin, focus on what is actually necessary for survival and plan nothing else. This is not a failure of effort. It is appropriate allocation of scarce resources.

### When you cannot assess

Sometimes the situation is confusing enough that you cannot confidently run the assessment. This happens especially when gaslighting has corrupted your sense of what is real or when cognitive capacity is temporarily depleted.

- **Default to caution.** Assume power is less balanced and resources thinner than they feel. Acting on a conservative assessment will rarely be catastrophic; acting on an overconfident assessment can be.
- **Ask a trusted person to run it with you.** An external view can often see things you cannot see from inside.
- **Wait and observe.** As noted throughout this hub, time clarifies. Patterns become visible with repeated observation. Do not force a decision you cannot make clearly.
- **Read this page again in a different state.** If you did the assessment in a state of high stress or depletion, try again when you are rested and calmer. The answers may be different.

## 8. What This Page Cannot Do

- **Produce a numeric score.** This is a framework for seeing the dimensions of your situation, not a measurement instrument. Real situations are more complex than a grid.
- **Tell you what to do.** The assessment points toward categories of strategy, but the specific choice within a category depends on your situation, your values, and factors this page cannot know.
- **Replace an advocate or therapist.** When power is unfavorable and resources are thin, the most useful next step is often professional support. This page can describe the situation; it cannot walk you through it.
- **Work when you are in crisis.** Assessment frameworks are cognitively demanding. When you are depleted, this page may not be usable. That is normal. Return to it when you have more capacity.
- **Account for intersectional marginalization in depth.** Autistic people who are also Black, LGBTQ+, disabled in other ways, non-citizens, non-speaking, or otherwise marginalized face compounded power and resource constraints that are not well-captured by the general framework. Where the research base is thin (and for these populations it is very thin), the framework's accuracy diminishes. If you are in one of these intersections, organizations like AWN, ASAN, and population-specific advocacy organizations may be more helpful than the general framework.

## 9. Key Takeaways

1. **Strategies depend on conditions.** The strategies on this hub assume certain power and resource conditions. Pick strategies that match your actual conditions, not your idealized ones.

2. **Power has eight dimensions.** Formal authority, financial power, social capital, physical capacity, information asymmetry, control over alternatives, credibility, and ability to harm without consequence. The relationship is asymmetric if three or more dimensions tilt in the same direction.

3. **Resources have six categories.** Financial, social, legal, informational, professional, and cognitive/physical capacity. Capacity is a resource.

4. **The credibility dimension is almost always tilted against autistic readers** in encounters with non-autism-informed audiences. Plan accordingly.

5. **The resource thin zone calls for different strategies.** When power is unfavorable and resources are thin, confrontation strategies are usually inappropriate. Focus on resource acquisition, reality anchoring, and contacting advocacy.

6. **Time is often an ally.** Resource building is slow work. Gaslighting is designed to make you feel you must act now; usually you don't. Waiting for better conditions is often the correct strategy.

7. **Compliance training distorts your assessment.** If your initial read is "it's pretty balanced," test that by asking what would happen if you acted differently. Managed asymmetry can feel like balance when you have been trained to manage it.

8. **Autistic peer networks can be more valuable than their size suggests.** Do not heavily discount them because they are small or online.

9. **Masking depletes the capacity the assessment framework requires.** Do this assessment in an unmasked, rested state when possible.

10. **This is not a judgment of whether your situation is "bad enough."** Your situation is as bad as your experience of it. This is a tool for picking strategies that fit the terrain.
